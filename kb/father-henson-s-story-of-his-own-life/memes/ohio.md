# Ohio

Ohio was the free state that [[Josiah Henson]] encountered immediately upon crossing the [[Ohio River]] — a place of both temptation and transformation in his narrative. It was here that he was first told he could be a free man, here that he earned the money to attempt to purchase his [[freedom|liberty]], and here that he later gathered intelligence and allies for [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] missions back into [[Kentucky]].

## Location & Geography

Ohio lies north of [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River, bordered by [[kentucky|Kentucky]] to the south and [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]] to the north. In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s era, it was a free state whose towns along [[mississippi-river|the river]] — [[Cincinnati]], [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]], [[kentucky|Maysville]] — were vital crossing points for the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]]. The state stretches from the Appalachian foothills in the east to the flat plains near [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]] in the northwest, terrain that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] traversed on foot and horseback during his preaching tours and rescue missions.

## Role in the Narrative

Ohio appears at pivotal moments throughout [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s autobiography. In 1825, while leading eighteen enslaved people from [[Maryland]] to [[kentucky|Kentucky]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was confronted on [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] shore by free black residents who insisted he and his charges "were no longer slaves, but free men, if we chose to be so" (006-013). This was a test of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s honor — he had given his word to deliver his companions, and he resisted the temptation to defect, though the encounter planted seeds of resolve.

Ohio was also the theater of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s most hopeful period as a still-enslaved man. During a preaching tour through the state, he attended "the session of [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] Conference of the [[methodist-episcopal-church|Methodist Episcopal Church]]" where his oratory earned him $160 to put toward self-purchase (007-010). He recalled "the bright and hopeful days I spent in Ohio, while away on my preaching tour" as the time when he "heard much of the course pursued by fugitives from slavery, and became acquainted with a number of benevolent men" who would later assist his escapes (012-001).

After his [[escape-to-canada|1830 escape]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s rescue missions brought him back through Ohio repeatedly. He passed "through the States of [[new-york|New York]], [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]], and Ohio — free States, so called — and crossed [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River into [[kentucky|Kentucky]]" to rescue the family of a fellow fugitive (016-003). On a later mass escape with some thirty recruits, "we succeeded in crossing [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River in safety, and arrived in [[cincinnati|Cincinnati]] the third night after our departure" (016-006). His party traveled "through the wilderness" to reach "[[toledo|Toledo]], Ohio, a town on the south-western shore of [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]]," where they took passage for [[canada|Canada]] (016-006).

## Historical Context

Ohio was the first free state created from the Northwest Territory (admitted 1803) and remained a crucial node in the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] throughout the antebellum period. With over 400 miles of [[ohio-river|Ohio River]] frontage, it was the most accessible free territory for enslaved people escaping [[kentucky|Kentucky]] and Virginia. Cities like [[cincinnati|Cincinnati]], Ripley, and [[toledo|Toledo]] served as hubs where abolitionist networks — [[quaker|Quakers]], free black communities, and white sympathizers — funneled fugitives north toward [[canada|Canada]]. The state was also home to Oberlin College, a center of abolitionist thought and one of the first [[us-america|America]]n colleges to admit black students.
