# Quaker Assistance

The network of [[Quaker]] families who provided shelter, food, and transport to [[Josiah Henson]], his family, and other fugitive slaves during their escape journeys — acts of moral courage that risked fines, imprisonment, and social ostracism under the laws of the slaveholding republic.

## Background

During his [[escape-to-canada|1830 escape]] from [[Kentucky]] to [[Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and his family traveled primarily by night through [[Indiana]] and [[Ohio]], hiding by day in forests and depending on the charity of strangers for food. The region's population was "bitterly hostile to the fugitive," and discovery meant arrest and return to slavery (012-008).

Yet among this hostile population, a distinctive community stood apart: the [[quaker|Society of Friends]], or [[quaker|Quakers]], whose religious conviction that all persons possess an inner divine light led them to actively assist escaping slaves despite the legal penalties.

## Richmond, Indiana — A Quaker Haven

The town of [[richmond-indiana|Richmond, Indiana]], had been "settled by [[quaker|Quakers]]," and it became a reliable waypoint on the [[Underground Railroad]]. When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] arrived there with his family in the later stages of their escape:

> "This is a town which had been settled by Quakers, and there we found friends indeed, who at once helped us on our way, without loss of time; and after a difficult journey of two weeks, through the wilderness, we reached Toledo, Ohio..." (016-006)

The [[quaker|Quakers]] of [[indiana|Richmond]] did not merely offer sympathy; they provided material assistance — food, shelter, and guidance to the next safe haven — organized swiftly and without hesitation. This same network would later assist [[josiah-henson|Henson]] on multiple return journeys as he guided other fugitives north.

## The Quaker Wagon Rescue

On one of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s return journeys — escorting a party of escaped slaves northward through the [[indiana|Indiana]] wilderness — a young man in the group fell dangerously ill. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s party was compelled to carry him on a makeshift litter of poles and shirts. Eventually, "it appeared, both to himself and to us all, that death would soon release him from his sufferings," and the man begged to be left behind (017-009).

Tearfully, the party left him in a sheltered spot and continued. But within two miles, the dying man's brother could bear it no longer, and the group turned back. At this moment of desperation, a wagon appeared on the road:

> "I at length circumvented the road, so as to make it appear that I had been journeying in an opposite direction... When I came up with the driver, I bade him good day. He said, 'Where is thee going?'... I saw his coat, heard his _thee_ and _thou_, and set him down for a Quaker." (017-011)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] confessed their situation. The driver "at once stopped his horses" — though he had been heading to a distant market with a load of produce to sell — and "without delay turned his horses' heads, to proceed in the direction of his home" (017-011).

> "Immediately on viewing the sufferer he was moved to tears... The reception we met with from the Quaker's family overjoyed our hearts, and the transports with which the poor men looked upon their brother, now so favorably circumstanced, cannot be described." (017-011)

## James C. Fuller and Transatlantic Advocacy

The [[quaker|Quaker]] connection extended beyond the escape routes. In [[Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] met the Congregational missionary [[Hiram Wilson]], who wrote to his "[[quaker|Quaker]] friend... [[james-c-fuller|James C. Fuller]], residing at [[new-york|Skeneateles]], [[New York]]" (018-003). Fuller, an English-born [[quaker|Quaker]], took up the cause of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s settlement community, traveling to [[England]] and returning with $1,500 in subscriptions raised from British supporters (018-004).

This transatlantic [[quaker|Quaker]] network thus linked the fugitives huddled in Canadian forests to the drawing rooms of English philanthropy — a chain of conscience that would eventually help fund the [[Dawn Settlement]] and its manual-labor school.

## Significance

[[quaker|Quaker]] assistance was not incidental to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mission — it was infrastructural. The [[quaker|Quaker]] network:

1. **Provided safe passage** — Without [[indiana|Richmond]] and other [[quaker|Quaker]] settlements, the journey from [[kentucky|Kentucky]] to [[canada|Canada]] would have been nearly impossible for a family with young children.
2. **Modeled racial equality** — [[quaker|Quaker]] families treated black fugitives as guests, not charity cases, in an era when most whites viewed African [[us-america|America]]ns as property.
3. **Funded permanent settlement** — Fuller's fundraising in [[england|England]], rooted in [[quaker|Quaker]] networks, provided the capital for the [[Dawn Settlement]] school and sawmill.

## Henson's Tribute

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] never forgot the debt. Reflecting on the men and women who "voluntarily exposed themselves to hatred, fines, and imprisonment" to assist fugitives, he wrote:

> "If there be a God who will have mercy on the merciful, great shall be their reward. In the great day when men shall stand in judgment before the Divine Master, crowds of the outcast and forsaken of earth shall gather around them, and in joyful tones bear witness, 'We were hungry and ye gave us meat, thirsty and ye gave us drink, naked and ye clothed us, sick and ye visited us.'" (013-002)
