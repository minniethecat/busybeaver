Reverend William Brock was a [[london|London]] clergyman and [[abolition|abolitionist]] who supported [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] during his fundraising tours of [[visit-to-england|England]]. As one of the prominent [[london|London]] ministers who opened their pulpits to the formerly enslaved preacher, Brock helped provide [[josiah-henson|Henson]] with the platform and credibility needed to raise funds for the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]].

## London Pulpit and Abolitionist Network

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] arrived in [[england|England]] to raise money for the manual labor school and sawmill at [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]], he carried letters of introduction from his [[amos-lawrence|Boston supporters]]. In [[london|London]], he was received by a network of influential clergymen who lent him their pulpits — a crucial endorsement that signaled to British congregations that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was a legitimate religious leader, not merely a supplicant:

> I had been cordially received by the men above mentioned, and had preached in the pulpits of such men as Rev. Messrs. Thomas Binney, Baptist Noel, William Brock, James Sherman, George Smith, Dr. Burns, in London. (020-007)

The willingness of these ministers to share their pulpits with a Black preacher from [[canada|Canada]] was significant in an era when racial equality in religious leadership was far from accepted, even in abolitionist circles.

## The Celebratory Event

Brock also participated in a public celebration honoring [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s work — an occasion [[josiah-henson|Henson]] described as "one of the pleasantest days of my life":

> Among the distinguished persons who made speeches on this joyous occasion, I will mention the names of Rev. William Brock, Hon. Samuel M. Peto, and a Mr. Bess, brother-in-law of Mr. Peto, with his accomplished and beautiful lady.

Brock's presence alongside [[sir-samuel-morton-peto|Samuel Morton Peto]], a prominent British industrialist and Member of Parliament, illustrates the cross-class coalition — clerical, commercial, and political — that supported [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s Canadian mission.

## British Clerical Abolitionism

Brock belonged to a generation of British Nonconformist ministers who had been shaped by the successful campaign to abolish slavery in the [[england|British Empire]] (1833). For these clergymen, supporting fugitive slaves and Black-led initiatives in [[canada|Canada]] was a natural extension of their abolitionist convictions — a way of demonstrating that [[freedom|emancipation]] could succeed in practice, not merely in law.

The [[london|London]] pulpits [[josiah-henson|Henson]] occupied represented more than fundraising venues; they were spaces where British [[christianity|Christian]]s encountered firsthand a Black preacher whose dignity, eloquence, and religious conviction challenged prevailing racial assumptions.
