Thomas Binney was a prominent [[london|London]] minister and abolitionist who was among the British religious leaders that received [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] during his 1851 visit to [[england|England]]. He opened his pulpit to [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and served on the committee of twelve appointed to oversee [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s fundraising for the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] manual labor school.

## Role in Henson's England Visit

Upon [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s arrival in [[england|England]] in 1851, he carried letters of introduction to leading British clergy and abolitionists, including Thomas Binney. Binney was among those who welcomed [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and gave him access to [[london|London]] pulpits:

> Much to my astonishment, therefore, when I had arrived in England, and had been cordially received by the men above mentioned, and had preached in the pulpits of such men as Rev. Messrs. Thomas Binney, Baptist Noel, William Brock, James Sherman, George Smith, Dr. Burns, in London... [020-007]

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was publicly accused of being an impostor, he requested that his supporters form a committee to oversee the financial management of his fundraising. Binney served on this committee alongside other prominent figures including [[samuel-gurney|Samuel Gurney]], [[james-sherman|James Sherman]], [[john-branch|John Branch]], [[john-scobell|John Scobell]], and Lord Ashley:

> This committee had been appointed, and consisted of Samuel Gurney, Samuel Gurney, Junior, Samuel Marley, Esq., George Hitchcock, Esq., Rev. James Sherman, Rev. Thomas Binney, Rev. John Branch, Eusebius Smith, Esq., John Scobell, Secretary of the British and Foreign Anti-slavery Society... [020-007]
