# Indiana

Indiana was a free state that bordered the [[ohio-river|Ohio River]] and served as a critical entry point for [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] and other fugitive slaves escaping from [[kentucky|Kentucky]] on their way to [[canada|Canada]].

## Role in Henson's Escapes

During [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s [[escape-to-canada|first escape in 1830]], he crossed [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River and landed on Indiana soil under cover of darkness:

> "In due time we landed on the Indiana shore. A hearty, grateful farewell, such as none but companions in danger can know, and I heard the oars of the skiff propelling him home. There I stood in the darkness, my dear ones with me, and the all unknown future before us." (012-008)

On a later rescue mission in Chapter XVI, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] led a much larger party — about thirty fugitives collected from different states in [[kentucky|Kentucky]] — across [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River into free territory. The group succeeded in crossing [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River in safety and arrived in [[cincinnati|Cincinnati]] on the third night after their departure (016-005).

## Quaker Settlement at Richmond

Upon reaching Indiana, the party made for [[Richmond, Indiana]], a town settled by [[quakers|Quakers]], who provided immediate and decisive assistance:

> "Here we procured assistance; and, after stopping a short time to rest, we started for Richmond, Indiana. This is a town which had been settled by Quakers, and there we found friends indeed, who at once helped us on our way, without loss of time." (016-006)

The [[quaker|Quaker]] communities of Indiana were central to the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] network. Their religious convictions against slavery led them to offer material aid — food, shelter, and guidance — to fugitives passing through the state. For [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s party, the [[quaker|Quaker]] assistance at Richmond was the difference between recapture and continued progress toward freedom.

## Travel Conditions

The terrain of Indiana allowed fugitives to travel by day so long as they remained concealed in the woods. During one journey recorded in Chapter XVII, a companion fell gravely ill and had to be carried on a makeshift litter:

> "By this time we got into the State of Indiana, so that we could travel by day as long as we kept to the woods." (017-001)

Though Indiana was technically a free state, the presence of slave catchers and the general hostility toward people of color meant that even within its borders, caution was essential. Travel by night remained the safer option, and movement through wooded areas provided the best concealment from those who might report fugitives to authorities.

## The Wilderness Route

From Richmond, the party continued through the wilderness to [[toledo|Toledo, Ohio]], on the shore of [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]]. The journey through Indiana was part of a larger two-week passage through wilderness terrain:

> "After a difficult journey of two weeks, through the wilderness, we reached Toledo, Ohio." (016-006)

The "wilderness" described by [[josiah-henson|Henson]] reflects the still largely unsettled character of the Midwestern interior in the 1830s. The route from Richmond to [[toledo|Toledo]] passed through forests and sparsely populated areas, offering both concealment and hardship.
