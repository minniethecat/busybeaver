# Convention of Blacks (London, 1838)

The [[Convention of Blacks (London, 1838)|Convention of Blacks]] was a gathering of delegates from Black settlements across [[canada|Upper Canada]], held in [[london|London]] in June 1838. Called by [[Josiah Henson]] and [[Hiram Wilson]], it was a landmark exercise in community governance that led to the establishment of the manual-labor school at the [[Dawn Settlement]].

## Background

The convention was prompted by the arrival of fifteen hundred dollars raised in [[england|England]] by a friend of the settlement, intended for the benefit of the Black community. Rather than distribute the funds immediately — a sum that "sounded vast" to many — [[josiah-henson|Henson]] believed the money should be preserved for a purpose of permanent utility:

> "But, in order to come to a satisfactory conclusion, the first thing to be done was to call a convention of delegates from every settlement of blacks that was within reach; that all might see that whatever was decided on, was sanctioned by the disinterested votes of those who were thought by their companions, best able to judge what was expedient." (018-004)

"Mr. Wilson and myself called such a convention, therefore, to meet in [[london|London]], [[canada|Upper Canada]], and it was held in June, 1838." (018-004)

## The Debate and Decision

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] proposed using the donated funds to establish a manual-labor school. There was initial opposition, and the discussion continued for three days. Ultimately, however, the proposal was adopted unanimously. The convention also appointed a committee of three to select and purchase a site for the establishment, with [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and Wilson as the active members.

## Significance

The convention represented an important moment of collective decision-making. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s insistence on convening delegates from every settlement — rather than acting unilaterally — demonstrated a commitment to democratic governance. The convention's unanimous approval gave the manual-labor school project a legitimacy that individual action could not have provided.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] advocated for education over immediate distribution of funds:

> I urged the appropriation of the money to the establishment of a manual-labor school, where our children could be taught those elements of knowledge which are usually the occupations of a grammar-school; and where the boys could be taught, in addition, the practice of some mechanic art, and the girls could be instructed in those domestic arts which are the proper occupation and ornament of their sex. Such an establishment would train up those who would afterwards instruct others; and we should thus gradually become independent of the white man for our intellectual progress.
>
