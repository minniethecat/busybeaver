# Methodist Episcopal Church

The [[Methodist Episcopal Church]] was the first organized Methodist denomination in the [[us-america|United States]] (established 1784), and [[slavery|the institution]] that shaped [[Josiah Henson]]'s religious life — providing his conversion experience at a [[Camp Meeting]], his preaching credentials, and the network that enabled his [[Escape to Canada|escape from slavery]].

## Structure and Early History

The Methodist Episcopal Church was organized at the 1784 Christmas Conference in Baltimore, [[Maryland]] — the same state where [[josiah-henson|Henson]] would be born five years later. Its circuit rider system, camp meetings, and decentralized conference structure made it particularly influential on the frontier and in the South. The denomination was notable for its early opposition to [[slavery]] — John Wesley, [[methodism|Methodism]]'s founder, had condemned [[slavery|the institution]] in forceful terms — though the church would tragically split over the issue in 1844.

## Role in Henson's Life

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s connection to the Methodist Episcopal Church began with his conversion at a Methodist [[Camp Meeting]] where a preacher expounded Hebrews 2:9 — "that Jesus Christ tasted death for every man" (004-002). The emphasis on "for *every* man" — including an enslaved man — was "a new revelation" that fundamentally altered [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s self-understanding and set him on the path to becoming a preacher.

> "He preached from the text, 'that Jesus Christ tasted death for every man.' The emphasis he laid on the word *every* man startled me. It was a new revelation to me. Did Christ taste death for *me*? For *every* man meant for me." (004-002)

After his conversion, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] began preaching to fellow enslaved people and became "quite an esteemed preacher among them" (004-001). His Methodist credentials gave him a degree of mobility and respect unusual for an enslaved person — a fact that proved crucial to his eventual escape.

## The Ohio Conference Connection

The church network proved decisive for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s [[freedom]]. In 1828, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was invited by a [[methodism|Methodist preacher]] to accompany him to an Ohio Conference meeting (009-001). This journey provided [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s first exposure to free territory and introduced him to networks of people "engaged in helping fugitives on their way" to [[Canada]] (012-001).

> "I obtained an introduction to several benevolent men, who were engaged in helping fugitives on their way. From them I learned that Canada was often spoken of as the only sure refuge from pursuit." (012-001)

The Ohio Conference — part of the Methodist Episcopal Church's western jurisdiction — thus served as the information conduit through which [[josiah-henson|Henson]] learned about [[canada|Canada]] and the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]]. Without this church network, he might never have known where to flee or how to plan his family's escape.

## Historical Complexity

The Methodist Episcopal Church's relationship with slavery was deeply conflicted. While early [[methodism|Methodism]] opposed slaveholding — and individual Methodists like John McKenny refused to employ slave labor (004-001) — the church as an institution had largely accommodated slavery in the South by the early 19th century. The denomination's 1844 split into northern and southern branches over the slavery question reflected the same national division that would erupt into the Civil War.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative navigates this complexity. He acknowledges the church's failings while emphasizing the transformative power of its gospel message and the practical assistance provided by Methodist individuals and networks.
