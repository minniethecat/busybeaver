# Death of Charlotte Henson

The death of [[Charlotte Henson]] (née [[isaac-riley|Riley]]) in 1852 at the [[Dawn Settlement]], [[Canada]], shortly after her husband [[Josiah Henson]]'s return from [[England]]. Her passing ended a marriage of over forty years that had begun in [[slavery]], survived a perilous [[Escape to Canada|escape]], and endured the hardships of building a new life in freedom.

## The Marriage

[[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] was enslaved on the [[Isaac Riley]] [[plantation]] in [[Maryland]] when she and Josiah were married. Their union endured the brutal separations imposed by slavery: when [[isaac-riley|Riley]] fell into debt and determined to break up his [[plantation]], the [[josiah-henson|Henson]] family was nearly sold apart (002-003).

> "Our term of happy union as one family was now, alas! at an end. Mournful as was the Doctor's death to his friends it was a far greater calamity to us." (002-003)

[[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] bore twelve children and followed Josiah on the harrowing [[Escape to Canada]] in 1830, carrying their youngest children through wilderness and freezing rivers (013-009).

## Her Final Illness

[[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] fell gravely ill while [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was in [[England]] raising funds for the [[Dawn Settlement]]'s school and sawmill. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] received word of her condition and rushed home:

> "The ground was now prepared for me, and I reaped an abundant harvest. The whole debt of the institution was cancelled in a few months, when I was recalled to Canada by the fatal illness of my wife." (020-013)

He crossed the Atlantic with desperate hope. When he reached the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]], [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] was near death — but the reunion itself seemed to give her a brief resurgence:

> "The day following she revived; my return seeming to inspire her with the hope that possibly she might again be restored to health. It was not, however, so to be; but God in his mercy granted her a reprieve, and we were permitted to spend a little time with each other." (023-003)

## Her Character

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s tribute to his wife is one of the most personal and moving passages in his narrative:

> "I can truly and from an overflowing heart say, that she was a sincere and devoted Christian, and a faithful and kind wife to me, even up to the day of her death arranging all our domestic matters in such a way as to contribute as largely as possible to my comfort and happiness." (023-004)

She had been his partner through every trial: the [[Slave Auction]] that threatened to separate them, the failed attempt to purchase freedom from [[Isaac Riley]], the desperate [[Escape to Canada]] with children in arms, the settlement at [[Dawn]] where they built a home from wilderness. Her [[religion|Christian faith]], like his, was central to her identity.

## Aftermath

[[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] was buried at the [[Dawn Settlement]], in the free soil of [[Canada]] that she and Josiah had risked everything to reach. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s grief was profound but shaped by his faith:

> "Rest in peace, dear wife. If I am faithful to the end, as thou wert, we shall ere long meet again in that world where the sorrows of life shall not be remembered or brought into mind." (023-005)

Josiah would live another three decades, remarrying and continuing his work as preacher, community leader, and antislavery advocate. But [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]]'s death marked the end of an era — the closing chapter of a partnership forged under the lash and sanctified by shared sacrifice.
