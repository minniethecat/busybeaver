# Detroit

Detroit was the Michigan border city situated on the Detroit River — the waterway connecting Lake St. Clair to [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]] — that [[Josiah Henson]] explored in 1834 while scouting land for a Black settlement colony, and that later served as a landmark and transit point on [[Underground Railroad]] escape routes from the [[us-america|United States]] into [[Canada]].

## Location & Geography

Detroit lies on the north bank of the Detroit River, which forms the international boundary between the [[us-america|United States]] and [[canada|Canada]]. In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s day, the territory east of Lake St. Clair and the Detroit River was fertile, accessible, and well-positioned — he traveled there "on foot all over the extensive region between lakes [[canada|Ontario]], Erie, and Huron" and was "strongly impressed with its fertility, its convenience, and, indeed, its superiority, for our purposes, to any other spot I had seen" (015-002). The river was a natural corridor not only for commerce but also for fugitives seeking freedom: crossing it meant reaching Canadian soil.

## Role in the Narrative

Detroit figures in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative in three distinct ways — as an exploration destination, as an [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] landmark, and as a source of institutional support.

In the autumn of 1834, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] undertook a scouting expedition on behalf of a group of Black settlers in [[canada|Canada]] who sought land where they could "colonize, and raise our own crops, eat our own bread, and be, in short, our own masters." He traveled extensively between Lakes [[canada|Ontario]], Erie, and Huron, and when he reached the territory near the Detroit River, he "determined this should be the place" (015-002). Though the group eventually settled near the head of [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]] on McCormick's lands before moving to [[Dawn Settlement]], the Detroit region represented their first choice and the promise of independent Black agriculture.

Detroit also figured as a waypoint in the geography of escape. During [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s second journey on the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]], when slave hunters arrived at a tavern where fugitives were hiding, their white ally deflected the pursuers by telling them the escapees were "proceeding in the direction of Detroit" (017-015). The mention of Detroit as a plausible destination for freedom seekers confirms its known role in the clandestine geography of the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] — a northern city whose proximity to [[canada|Canada]] made it a natural crossing point.

Finally, Detroit appears as a source of establishment support for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s later British mission. When he traveled to [[England]] to exhibit black walnut lumber at the [[Great Exhibition]] of 1851, he carried letters of introduction from prominent figures including "Rev. Dr. Duffield, of Detroit, Michigan, Judge Conant, of the same city, Hon. Ross Wilkinson, U. S. Judge, residing also in Detroit" (020-004). These connections demonstrate that by mid-century, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had earned the support of respected civic and religious leaders in Detroit, a city whose proximity to his Canadian operations made it a natural source of trans-border alliances.

## Historical Context

Founded by the French in 1701, Detroit grew through the early nineteenth century into a major Great Lakes port and an important node on the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]]. Its location directly across [[mississippi-river|the river]] from [[canada|Canada]] made it one of the most significant crossing points for fugitive slaves — [[mississippi-river|the river]] was narrow enough to cross by small boat, and the Canadian shore, once reached, placed escapees beyond the reach of [[us-america|America]]n slave catchers (particularly before the Webster-Ashburton Treaty of 1842 and the [[fugitive-slave-law|Fugitive Slave Act]] of 1850 tightened cross-border enforcement). Detroit's Black community, which included both free-born African [[us-america|America]]ns and fugitives who had chosen to remain, was a vital part of the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] network, providing shelter, information, and material support to those continuing north. The city's abolitionist churches and civic leaders — including those who wrote letters for [[josiah-henson|Henson]] — represented [[slavery|the institution]]al infrastructure of [[abolition|anti-slavery]] sentiment in the upper Midwest.
