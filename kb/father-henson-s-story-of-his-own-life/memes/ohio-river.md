# Ohio River

The Ohio River was the geographic and psychological boundary between slavery and freedom in [[Josiah Henson]]'s world. It appears repeatedly in his narrative — first crossed in 1825 as he drove eighteen enslaved people from [[Maryland]] into [[Kentucky]], and later crossed in 1830 in a desperate escape with his own family, and again in later years as a conductor spiriting others to freedom.

## Location & Geography

The Ohio River flows nearly 1,000 miles from Pittsburgh, [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] to Cairo, Illinois, forming the southern borders of [[ohio|Ohio]], [[indiana|Indiana]], and Illinois. In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative, [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]]'s [[kentucky|Kentucky]] [[plantation]] lay "about five miles south of the Ohio River" (007-001), making [[mississippi-river|the river]] a constant physical presence and a perpetual symbolic reminder of freedom just beyond reach.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] first encountered [[mississippi-river|the river]] in mid-winter 1825, leading eighteen enslaved people from [[maryland|Maryland]] to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] under a pass from his master [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]]. At [[wheeling|Wheeling]], Virginia (now West Virginia), the party crossed [[mississippi-river|the river]] on the National Road — one of the major arteries of the internal slave trade (006-010).

The river was also the critical barrier during [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s [[escape-to-canada|1830 escape]]. Traveling by night through [[kentucky|Kentucky]] woods with his wife [[Charlotte Henson|Charlotte]] and their four children, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] reached [[mississippi-river|the river]] bank only to find his boat too small: "The [[ohio|Ohio]] river presented an impassable barrier" (012-007). After a desperate struggle in a leaky skiff, they reached the [[indiana|Indiana]] shore — and freedom.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] returned across the Ohio River as a conductor, leading parties of fugitives to safety. On one mission, "we succeeded in crossing the Ohio River in safety, and arrived in [[cincinnati|Cincinnati]] the third night after our departure" with a party of about thirty — one of the larger [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] operations described in the narrative (016-006).

## Historical Context

For the thousands of enslaved people who fled the upper South, the Ohio River was the single most significant geographic threshold of the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]]. The phrase "sold down [[mississippi-river|the river]]" entered [[us-america|America]]n English as a slave's worst fear — being sold from the border states into the Deep South. To "cross the Ohio" was its opposite: to reach nominal safety in a free state, though after the [[fugitive-slave-law|Fugitive Slave Act]] of 1850, even this was not truly secure.

## Key Passages

> "I arrived at Davis county, Kentucky, about the middle of April, 1825, and delivered myself and my companions to Mr. Amos Riley, the brother of my owner, who had a large plantation... His house was situated about five miles south of the Ohio River." (007-001)

> "We succeeded in crossing the Ohio River in safety, and arrived in Cincinnati the third night after our departure." (016-006)
