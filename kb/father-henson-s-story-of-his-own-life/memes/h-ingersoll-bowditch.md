Henry Ingersoll Bowditch was a [[boston|Boston]] physician and prominent abolitionist who provided crucial financial support to [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s industrial enterprise at the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]]. He was among the [[boston|Boston]] benefactors who vouched for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s honesty and character.

## Benefaction to the Dawn Settlement

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s funds for building a sawmill at [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]] were exhausted after completing the frame-work, he returned to [[boston|Boston]] seeking additional support:

> I immediately returned to my Boston friends. Amos Lawrence, H. Ingersoll Bowditch, and Samuel A. Eliot, Esqs., listened to me again, and gave me to understand that they deemed me an honest man. They encouraged me in my business enterprise, and the approval of such men was like balm to my soul. They endorsed a note for me and put it into the bank, by which I was enabled to borrow, on my own responsibility, about eighteen hundred dollars more. [019-005]

With this additional capital, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] completed the mill, stocked it with machinery, and "had the pleasure of seeing it in successful operation" (019-006). Bowditch's endorsement — alongside [[amos-lawrence|Amos Lawrence]] and [[samuel-eliot|Samuel Eliot]] — was instrumental in establishing the industrial foundation of the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]].

## Historical Context

Henry Ingersoll Bowditch was a leading figure in [[boston|Boston]]'s antislavery movement. The son of mathematician Nathaniel Bowditch, he became a respected physician and was among the founders of the [[boston|Boston]] Vigilance Committee, which aided fugitive slaves. His support for [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was part of a broader pattern of [[new-england|New England]] abolitionist philanthropy directed toward [[canada|Canadian]] freedom settlements, which provided economic independence and dignity for former slaves who had escaped via the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]].

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] found a willing supporter in Bowditch after his enterprise nearly failed:

> I immediately returned to my Boston friends. Amos Lawrence, H. Ingersoll Bowditch, and Samuel A. Elliot, Esqs., listened to me again, and gave me to understand that they deemed me an honest man. They encouraged me in my business enterprise, and the approval of such men was like balm to my soul.
