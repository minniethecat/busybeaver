# British Committee of Twelve

The [[British Committee of Twelve]] was an oversight committee of prominent British public figures appointed in 1851 to supervise [[Josiah Henson]]'s fundraising mission in [[england|England]] on behalf of the [[Dawn Settlement]] and its manual-labor school.

## Formation

The committee was formed after [[josiah-henson|Henson]] arrived in [[england|England]] to raise funds and was confronted by an anonymous printed circular accusing him of being an impostor. In response, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] requested that his supporters form an independent oversight body:

> "Fortunately I had already requested my friends to appoint a committee of twelve persons to examine carefully into the merits of my enterprise, which committee should appoint a sub-committee of three, and a treasurer, to receive every farthing contributed to me by the public, and to appropriate it only as they should deem proper." (020-007)

## Sub-Committee of Three

The sub-committee of three — [[john-scobell|John Scobell]], [[john-branch|Rev. John Branch]], and [[eusebius-smith|Eusebius Smith]] — had direct authority over fund disbursement, with [[samuel-gurney|Samuel Gurney]], Junior, serving as treasurer. This structure ensured complete financial transparency and protected [[josiah-henson|Henson]] from further accusations of impropriety.

## Effectiveness

The committee's existence successfully neutralized the circular's allegations. When a convention of Black delegates in [[canada|Canada]] was later convened, it "unanimously repudiated the false charges" (020-008), and [[john-scobell|John Scobell]] himself traveled to [[canada|Canada]] to investigate and report back.
