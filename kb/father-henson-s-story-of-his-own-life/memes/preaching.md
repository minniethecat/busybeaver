# Preaching

Preaching and exhortation were central to the religious experience of enslaved African [[us-america|America]]ns in the antebellum period. For [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]], preaching became both a spiritual calling and a path to leadership, respect, and ultimately freedom.

## Henson's Call to Preach

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s first encounter with [[christianity|Christian]] preaching came through a sermon he heard from a white minister, which profoundly transformed his life. Though he had grown up without [[christianity|Christian]] teaching, a single sermon was sufficient to ignite his faith and set him on the path to becoming a preacher himself (001-001).

After his [[camp-meeting|religious conversion]] at a camp meeting, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] felt called to share his faith with others. He became a [[methodism|Methodist preacher]], ministering to enslaved communities. He acknowledged that his theological knowledge was limited, but believed that speaking "from the fulness of a heart deeply impressed with its own sinfulness and imperfection" was sufficient for effective ministry (011-003).

> No great amount of theological knowledge is requisite for the purpose. If it had been, it is manifest enough that preaching never could have been my vocation; but I am persuaded that, speaking from the fulness of a heart deeply impressed with its own sinfulness and imperfection, and with the mercy of God, in Jesus Christ, I was listened to with attention and even approval by those whose intellectual and moral culture were far superior to my own.
>
> (007-002)


## Preaching as a Path to Freedom

In 1828, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] met a white [[methodism|Methodist preacher]] who became interested in his case and encouraged him to seek freedom. This preacher gave [[josiah-henson|Henson]] a letter of recommendation to a fellow preacher in [[cincinnati|Cincinnati]], which opened doors for him in the free states (011-005).

During his journey east, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was given opportunities to preach in [[cincinnati|Cincinnati]] pulpits. He made appeals for funds to purchase his freedom with "that eloquence which spontaneously breaks forth from a man who is pleading for his dearest rights" (012-003). Following advice, he purchased a decent suit of clothes and a horse, traveling from town to town preaching as he went, meeting with [[kindness]] and respect that contrasted sharply with the treatment he received on the [[plantation]] (012-004).

## Preaching in Canada

After escaping to [[canada|Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] resumed preaching among the Black community. Despite being unable to read, he was able to preach acceptably to educated audiences. He attributed this ability to divine grace, comparing himself to the kingdom of heaven which grows from small beginnings (016-001).

His son Tom learned to read, and would read the [[bible|Bible]] to [[josiah-henson|Henson]] on [[sabbath|Sunday]] mornings, enriching his preaching with scriptural knowledge. On one particularly memorable [[sabbath|Sabbath]], Tom read Psalm 103, which deeply moved [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and shaped his understanding of gratitude to God (017-001).

## Significance

Preaching in the enslaved community served multiple functions: it provided spiritual sustenance, created opportunities for leadership development, built networks across communities, and in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s case, provided a practical avenue toward freedom. The oral tradition of preaching allowed even those denied [[education|literacy]]—like [[josiah-henson|Henson]] himself—to develop sophisticated religious expression and moral authority.
