# Lancaster

Lancaster, [[ohio|Ohio]], was a waypoint on [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s second rescue mission into [[kentucky|Kentucky]], reached during the night of one of the most spectacular astronomical events of the nineteenth century — the great Leonid meteor shower of November 1833.

## A Night of Falling Stars

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative records a striking celestial phenomenon observed during the approach to Lancaster:

> "On my way, that strange occurrence happened, called the great meteoric shower. The heavens seemed broken up into streaks of light and falling stars. I reached Lancaster, Ohio, about three o'clock in the morning, and found the village aroused, and the bells ringing, and the people exclaiming, 'The day of judgment is come!'" (017-002)

The Leonid meteor shower of November 12–13, 1833, was one of the most extraordinary astronomical displays in recorded history. Across North [[us-america|America]], observers reported stars falling at a rate of tens of thousands per hour. Many interpreted the event in religious terms — as a sign of the Second Coming or the Day of Judgment. The phenomenon was so remarkable that it became a landmark date in [[us-america|America]]n memory, used to mark other events by reference: "the year the stars fell."

## Henson's Reaction

Unlike the terrified villagers, [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s response to the celestial display was strikingly calm. His sense of purpose — he was engaged in the work of liberating enslaved people — gave him equanimity even in the face of what many believed to be the end of the world:

> "I thought it was probably so; but felt that I was in the right business, and walked on through the village, leaving the terrified people behind. The stars continued to fall till the light of the sun appeared." (017-002)

This passage is one of the most vivid and frequently quoted in the entire narrative. It captures both the historical reality of the 1833 meteor shower and [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s characteristic determination: even the apocalypse would not distract him from his mission of rescue.

## The Second Rescue Mission

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s passage through Lancaster occurred during his second journey to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] to assist fugitive slaves. He had promised to return for the family of [[james-lightfoot|James Lightfoot]], a freed man living in [[canada|Canada]] whose relatives remained enslaved on the [[ohio-river|Ohio River]] near [[maysville|Maysville]] (017-001). After the Lancaster passage, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] continued to [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]], [[ohio|Ohio]], where he used a clever stratagem — wrapping his face in cloth and pretending to have severe toothache — to avoid questioning by suspicious Kentuckians (017-003).

## Location and Role

Lancaster, located in Fairfield County in south-central [[ohio|Ohio]], was on the overland route between [[ohio-river|Ohio River]] crossings and [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]]. As a free-state town, it offered relative safety for travelers like [[josiah-henson|Henson]], though the constant threat of slave catchers made even free-state travel dangerous, particularly after the [[fugitive-slave-act|Fugitive Slave Act of 1850]].
