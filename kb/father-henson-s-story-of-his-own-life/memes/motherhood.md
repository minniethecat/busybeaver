# Motherhood under Slavery

The experience of motherhood under slavery was marked by profound contradiction: enslaved women bore and raised children who belonged not to them, but to their enslavers. [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s narrative offers poignant glimpses into the strength and suffering of enslaved mothers, including his own.

## Henson's Mother

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother appears throughout his narrative as a figure of resilience and [[prayer|devotion]]. She was enslaved by Dr. Josiah McPherson but hired out to the Newman farm where [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was born in [[maryland|Charles County]], [[maryland|Maryland]] (001-001). Despite the [[kindness|brutality]] of the system, she maintained her connection to her children and provided what comfort she could.

The most wrenching scene involving [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother occurred at the auction block. When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was sold as a young child, he witnessed her agony at being forcibly separated from him. Her cries remained etched in his memory as a testament to the [[kindness|cruelty]] of a system that treated human bonds as irrelevant to commercial transactions (002-002).

## Maternal Labor

Enslaved mothers faced the double burden of forced labor and childcare. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] described how women were "compelled to perform unfit labor, sick, suffering, and bearing the peculiar burdens of her own sex unpitied and unaided." The physical demands of [[plantation]] work did not pause for pregnancy, childbirth, or nursing (003-006).

## His Wife as Mother

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s own wife, [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]], embodied the struggles of enslaved motherhood. When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] planned their escape to [[canada|Canada]], she was responsible for their young children during the dangerous journey. The children were placed in a wagon while the adults walked, and [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] alternated between riding to nurse them and walking alongside her husband through the night (013-001).

After reaching freedom in [[canada|Canada]], [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] raised their children and managed the household while [[josiah-henson|Henson]] traveled for preaching and fundraising. Her labor—both physical and emotional—was essential to the family's survival and success.

## Significance

The narrative's portrayal of motherhood underscores the particular cruelties slavery inflicted on women. The inability to protect one's children from sale, to provide for them adequately, or to determine their future constituted a unique form of suffering. Yet [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s account also testifies to the strength of maternal love that persisted despite all obstacles.
> She was a good mother to us, a woman of deep piety, anxious above all things to touch our hearts with a sense of religion. How or where she acquired her knowledge of God, or her acquaintance with the Lord's Prayer, which she taught us to repeat, I do not know.
>
> (002-002)
