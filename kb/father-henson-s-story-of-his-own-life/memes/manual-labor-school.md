# Manual Labor School

The Manual Labor School was an educational institution established by [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] at the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] in [[canada|Canada]]. It represented [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s vision for Black self-sufficiency — combining practical vocational training with academic and moral instruction to prepare formerly enslaved people for independent, productive lives.

## Origins at the Dawn Settlement

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] helped establish the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] in 1841, education was a central priority. He believed that freedom alone was insufficient — the formerly enslaved needed skills, knowledge, and discipline to build sustainable communities:

> We bought two hundred acres of land, and laid it out into farm lots, and roads, and began the erection of a school-house. This was the beginning of the Manual Labor School, which has since grown into an institution of considerable importance. [041-003]

## Educational Philosophy

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s educational philosophy was deeply practical. The Manual Labor School taught academic subjects alongside trades — farming, carpentry, blacksmithing, and domestic skills. This combination reflected [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s belief that dignity came through productive labor and self-sufficiency:

> The object was to teach the children, and the adults too, to work as well as to read; to train them to habits of industry, and to qualify them for the duties of life. [041-005]

## Challenges and Legacy

The Manual Labor School faced significant challenges, including funding shortages and conflicts over its management. Despite these difficulties, it educated hundreds of Black children and adults and served as a model for other educational initiatives in Black settlements across [[canada|Canada]] West.
