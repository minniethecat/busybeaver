# Prayer

Prayer was the foundational spiritual practice of [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s life, from the bedside teachings of his enslaved mother through his conversion experience, his ministry among fellow slaves, and his public leadership in [[canada|Canada]]. It was, for [[josiah-henson|Henson]], the direct line of communication with a God he [[trust]]ed absolutely — the source of his resilience in [[slavery|bondage]], his comfort in uncertainty, and his authority as a preacher.

## Definition

In the context of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative, prayer refers to the act of communicating with God — through spoken words, silent meditation, or communal worship — as understood within 19th-century evangelical [[christianity|Christianity]]. For [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and his community, prayer required no book, no building, and no formal training; it was accessible to the illiterate and the learned alike. It was both a personal discipline and a public expression of faith, central to his identity as a preacher and community leader.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother was his first teacher of prayer. Though she herself was enslaved and could not read, she taught him to recite the Lord's Prayer and regularly knelt in supplication: "I remember seeing her often on her knees, trying to arrange her thoughts in prayer appropriate to her situation" (002-004). This maternal instruction remained with [[josiah-henson|Henson]] throughout his life and established prayer as the bedrock of his spiritual existence.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s own religious awakening was crystallized through prayer. After hearing his first sermon — delivered by John McKenny, who "first taught me the blessedness of religion" (004-001) — [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was so moved that he turned aside on his way home:

> "Revolving the things which I had heard in my mind as I went home, I became so excited that I turned aside from the road into the woods, and prayed to God for light and for aid with an earnestness, which, however unenlightened, was at least sincere." (004-003)

He dated his conversion to this period of solitary prayer, an intensely personal encounter that required no intermediary — no church, no minister, no book.

During his years in [[canada|Canada]], prayer remained central. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] described a day spent entirely in meditation and prayer, seeking divine guidance about his position as a fugitive and his responsibility to those still in [[slavery|bondage]]: "I spent the day in prayer to God, that he would direct me. I told him I had nothing to look to but him" (014-009, paraphrased from the full passage on the day of prayer in the woods in Chapter XIV).

## Henson's Perspective

Prayer sustained [[josiah-henson|Henson]] through his most desperate moments. During his [[escape-to-canada|escape from slavery]], traveling through the darkness with his family, it was his only resource: "In God was our only hope. Fervently did I pray to him as we trudged on cautiously" (012-004).

Yet the loss of prayer was equally devastating. During the journey down [[mississippi-river|the Mississippi]] toward the [[new-orleans|New Orleans slave market]] — the darkest chapter of his life — [[josiah-henson|Henson]] confessed that his capacity to pray had left him. This moment reveals how completely prayer was integrated into [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s identity: its loss was equivalent to the loss of God himself.

On a [[sabbath|Sabbath]] morning in [[canada|Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] asked his son Tom to read to him from the [[bible|Bible]]. The experience moved him so deeply that he could not preach that day:

> "I was greatly moved by the conversation I had with Tom, so much so that I could not undertake to preach that day. The congregation were disappointed, and I passed the Sunday without attempting anything more than to say a few words." (014-007)

This episode shows the fluid boundary between private prayer and public preaching in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s spirituality — both were forms of response to the divine, and a deeply personal encounter with scripture through his son's reading was as powerful as any sermon delivery.

## Historical Context

In 19th-century [[us-america|America]]n evangelicalism, prayer was understood as both a personal duty and a mark of genuine conversion. [[methodism|Methodism]] in particular emphasized extemporaneous prayer — spoken from the heart rather than read from a book — which made it accessible to the illiterate. For enslaved people, prayer offered one of the few permitted forms of spiritual expression, though even this was sometimes restricted by enslavers who feared that gatherings for prayer might facilitate conspiracy or resistance. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s public praying and exhortation among fellow slaves represented an assertion of spiritual authority that transcended his legal status as property.

The "day of prayer in the woods" described in Chapter XIV is a significant motif in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative, echoing the biblical tradition of solitary retreat for spiritual discernment — from Moses on Sinai to Christ in the wilderness. For an enslaved man who had never read these biblical precedents directly, the instinct to withdraw for solitary prayer demonstrates how deeply the practice was embedded in the oral religious culture he inherited.
