# Christianity

Christianity serves as the central religious and moral framework of [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s narrative, shaping his worldview, his sense of identity, and his aspirations for freedom. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s Christianity was deeply experiential — rooted in a transformative conversion moment — and profoundly practical, informing his relationships with both the enslaved and their enslavers. It also provided the theological basis for his critique of slavery as incompatible with the gospel.

## Henson's Conversion Experience

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s religious awakening occurred during a [[camp-meeting|Methodist camp meeting]], an event he described in vivid, emotional terms. The experience transformed him from a state of spiritual distress — "I was all confusion, and lost in darkness and pain" — to one of rapturous assurance: "I was transported with delicious joy. I seemed to see a glorious being, in a cloud of splendor, smiling down from on high" (004-002). This ecstatic encounter with divine love became the foundation of his faith and his sense of personal worth, countering the dehumanizing messages of the slave system. The conversion marked a decisive turning point: from that moment, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] understood himself as a child of God with intrinsic dignity, regardless of his legal status as property.

## Forgiveness and Love of Enemies

A defining mark of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s Christianity was its emphasis on forgiveness and love of enemies — a radical ethic in the context of slavery. After his conversion, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] described a profound transformation: "swallowed up in the beauty of the divine love, I loved my enemies, and [[prayer|pray]]ed for them that did despitefully use and entreat me" (004-002). This posture of forgiving love became the hallmark of his Christian character, shaping his relations with both enslavers and fellow fugitives. He attributed this capacity not to his own virtue but to "that divine spirit of love I had learned to worship in the woods" (009-005).

## The Call to Preach

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s [[camp-meeting|religious conversion]] was immediately followed by a call to preach. Within days of his camp-meeting experience, he began exhorting fellow enslaved people to embrace Christianity. "I felt an irresistible desire to tell my fellow-sufferers what I had experienced" (009-007). He held [[prayer|prayer meetings]] in the [[slave-cabin|slave quarters]], often at night after the day's labor, adapting his message to the conditions and understanding of his listeners. His gift for extemporaneous preaching — "I did not know a letter of the alphabet, but I poured out my soul in exhortation" (010-003) — attracted attention beyond the [[plantation]].

## Methodism and the Circuit System

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s Christianity was shaped by the Methodist tradition, with its emphasis on personal conversion, camp meetings, itinerant preaching, and the possibility of spiritual equality across racial lines. The [[methodist-circuit-preaching|Methodist circuit]] system provided a structure in which an illiterate but gifted exhorter like [[josiah-henson|Henson]] could exercise a public ministry (010-008). Methodist theology's [[abolition|anti-slavery]] sentiment — articulated by figures like Bishop Francis Asbury — gave [[josiah-henson|Henson]] a language for condemning [[slavery|the institution]] from within a Christian framework. The [[ohio-conference-methodist-episcopal|Ohio Conference of the Methodist Episcopal Church]], which [[josiah-henson|Henson]] later addressed, represented [[slavery|the institution]]al dimension of this tradition (010-010).

## Christianity as Critique of Slavery

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s faith gave him a moral vocabulary for critiquing slavery that transcended mere grievance. He framed slavery not primarily as a political or economic injustice but as a sin against God: "It is a sin against God to hold your fellow-man in [[slavery|bondage]]" (010-012). This theological framing carried weight with Christian audiences in both the [[us-america|United States]] and [[england|England]], for whom biblical authority was paramount. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] emphasized the contradiction between professed [[religion|Christian faith]] and the practice of slaveholding, noting that "men who called themselves Christians were buying and selling the image of God" (010-006).

## Practical Christianity and Community

Beyond doctrine, [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s Christianity was expressed in practical service. At the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]], he established a church and school, viewing education and economic self-sufficiency as extensions of Christian duty (015-004). His famous statement — "the only real Christianity is practical Christianity" (015-005) — encapsulated his conviction that faith must manifest in tangible care for the vulnerable. This practical emphasis resonated with the British evangelical philanthropists who supported his work, including [[samuel-gurney|Samuel Gurney]] and [[lord-john-russell|Lord John Russell]].
