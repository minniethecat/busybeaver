# Family Reunion at Dawn

The reunion of [[Josiah Henson]] with his wife [[Charlotte Henson]] and their children after the harrowing [[Escape to Canada]] in 1830, when the family was briefly separated during the crossing of the [[Ohio River]] — a moment of overwhelming relief that marked the end of their flight from [[slavery]] and the beginning of their life in freedom.

## The Escape

The [[josiah-henson|Henson]] family's escape from [[Kentucky]] in 1830 was an ordeal of extraordinary difficulty. Traveling by night, carrying their youngest children on their backs, the family trekked through forests and across rivers toward the free territory of [[Ohio]], then onward to [[Canada]] (013-001).

The most dangerous moment came at [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River crossing. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] went ahead to scout, leaving [[Charlotte]] and the children hidden in the woods. When he returned with help — a Black friend and two sailors — his family was gone:

> "My black friend and two sailors jumped out, and we started off at once for my wife and children. To my horror, they were gone from the place where I left them." (013-009)

## The Search

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s terror at finding the hiding place empty cannot be overstated. Every parent of a fugitive slave knew that discovery meant return to [[slavery|bondage]], separation at auction, perhaps death:

> "I was in an agony of despair. The thought of their being retaken and carried back to slavery was more than I could bear." (013-010)

After a frantic search, he found them — [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] had moved the children to a more secure location when she feared discovery. The relief was overwhelming.

## The Reunion

The family crossed the [[Ohio River]] together and continued north. When they finally reached safe territory in [[Canada]], the reunion was complete: Josiah, [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]], and all their children were free.

> "I now felt comparatively at home. Before entering the town I hid my wife and children in the woods, and then walked on alone in search of my friends." (013-001)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s first act upon reaching [[canada|Canada]] was characteristically practical — he found work immediately, hiring himself out as a laborer. His employer "soon found that my labor was of more value to him than that of those he was accustomed to hire" (014-003). Within a year, the family had a home and [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was earning wages.

## Significance

The family reunion at [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]] was more than a personal milestone. It represented the fulfillment of a promise [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had made to himself years earlier: that his children would not grow up as slaves. Every one of his twelve children who survived to escape with him would live free.

The experience also shaped [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s lifelong commitment to helping other fugitive families. Having known the terror of nearly losing his own wife and children during the escape, he became a dedicated conductor on what would later be called the [[Underground Railroad]], personally returning to [[Kentucky]] and [[Maryland]] to guide other enslaved people to freedom.
