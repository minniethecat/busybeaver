# Lake Erie

Great Lake forming the border between the [[us-america|United States]] and [[canada|Canada]]. Lake Erie was a critical geographic feature in [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s narrative — his family reached its shores during their escape, he explored its northern territories for settlement, and he repeatedly crossed its waters conducting fugitive slaves to freedom.

## Location & Geography

Lake Erie is the fourth-largest of the five Great Lakes by surface area, lying between Lake Huron to the north and Lake [[canada|Ontario]] to the northeast. During [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s time, its southern shore touched [[ohio|Ohio]] ([[us-america|United States]]), while its northern shore belonged to [[canada|Upper Canada]] (present-day [[canada|Ontario]]). Key locations in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s story along or near Lake Erie include [[toledo|Toledo]], [[ohio|Ohio]]; [[buffalo|Buffalo]] and [[sandusky|Sandusky]] on the [[us-america|America]]n side; and [[fort-erie|Fort Erie]] and the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] on the Canadian side.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] first surveyed the lands near Lake Erie in 1834, travelling "on foot all over the extensive region between lakes [[canada|Ontario]], Erie, and Huron" in search of a settlement site for the Black community he hoped to establish [015-002]. He later discovered government land "towards the head of Lake Erie" that was already cleared, offering immediate farming advantages for the settlers [015-002].

During his second rescue journey, [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s party reached "[[toledo|Toledo]], [[ohio|Ohio]], a town on the south-western shore of Lake Erie," where they took passage for [[canada|Canada]] [016-006]. On yet another expedition, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and a companion "once more set our faces in the direction of Lake Erie" as they guided fugitives northward [017-012].

The Lake Erie crossing represented the final stage of the [[escape-to-canada|journey to freedom]] for many of the people [[josiah-henson|Henson]] assisted. The towns along its shores — [[buffalo|Buffalo]], [[toledo|Toledo]], [[sandusky|Sandusky]], and [[fort-erie|Fort Erie]] — formed a network of transit points on the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]].

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] recorded his exploration of the region in search of a settlement site:

> Upon going farther towards the head of Lake Erie, I discovered an extensive tract of government land, which, for some years, had been granted to a Mr. McCormick upon certain conditions, and which he had rented out to settlers upon such terms as he could obtain. This land being already cleared, offered some advantages for the immediate raising of crops, which were not to be overlooked by persons whose resources were so limited as ours.
>
> (015-002)
