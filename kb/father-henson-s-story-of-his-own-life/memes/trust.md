# Trust and Betrayal

The relationship between trust and betrayal forms a central moral thread in [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s narrative. As an enslaved man entrusted with significant responsibilities — overseeing [[plantation]]s, managing property, conducting business — [[josiah-henson|Henson]] repeatedly demonstrated his faithfulness, only to be betrayed by those he served. His story exposes the fundamental asymmetry of trust under slavery: the enslaved were expected to be faithful, while enslavers could break every promise with impunity.

## Definition

In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative, "trust" operates on two levels: the personal trust between individuals, and the structural trust that slavery systematically violated. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] took seriously the [[christianity|Christian]] duty of faithfulness — "He that is faithful over a little, will alone be faithful over much" (007-007) — but discovered that his fidelity to his masters was not reciprocated. The concept thus illuminates the moral contradictions at the heart of a system that demanded loyalty from the powerless while offering none in return.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] was placed in an unusual position of trust for an enslaved man. As [[overseer-system|overseer]] on [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]]'s [[plantation]], he was "entrusted with the care" of the estate's operations (005-008). When [[isaac-riley|Riley]] fell into financial distress, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was the person in whom he confided: "He had confidence in my fidelity and judgment" (006-005). [[josiah-henson|Henson]] took this trust seriously: "it was my duty to be faithful to him in the position in which he placed me" (005-008).

Yet this trust was one-sided. After years of faithful service, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] discovered that his reward was to be sale and separation from his family:

> "As I surveyed this scene, and listened to the groans and outcries of my companions in misery... this was to be the reward and end of all my faithfulness to my master." (007-004)

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] later attempted to purchase his freedom, the betrayal was even sharper: the chapter heading "CHEATED AND BETRAYED" (008-002) announces the climactic moment when [[isaac-riley|Riley]] — whom [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had served faithfully — took his money and refused to honor the agreement for his freedom.

## Henson's Perspective

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s response to betrayal was not bitterness but a deepening reliance on divine rather than human fidelity. When his trust in his master was shattered, he turned to God: "I went about my work with as quiet a mind as I could, resolved to trust in God" (008-010). This theological reframing — from trusting men to trusting God — was a survival strategy that allowed [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to maintain his moral integrity in a system designed to destroy it.

## Historical Context

The asymmetry of trust was a structural feature of [[us-america|America]]n slavery. Enslaved people were legally incapable of making contracts; any promise made to them by a master was unenforceable. The common practice of "hiring out" enslaved workers — where trust was placed in the enslaved person to deliver earnings to the master — exemplified this one-sided arrangement. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s experience of being cheated out of his self-purchase money was not unique; countless enslaved people saved for years only to have their masters renege, sometimes on their deathbeds.

This systematic betrayal had profound effects on both enslaved and enslaver. As [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative shows, the master's power to break promises corrupted not only the relationship but the master's own character — a theme that runs through his portrayal of both [[isaac-riley]] and [[amos-riley]].
