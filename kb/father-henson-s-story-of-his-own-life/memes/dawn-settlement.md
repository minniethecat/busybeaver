# Dawn Settlement

The Dawn Settlement, established by [[Josiah Henson]] near Dresden in what is now [[canada|Ontario]], [[Canada]], was a community for fugitive slaves that combined economic enterprise with educational opportunity through its [[manual-labor-school|Manual Labor School]] (also known as the British American Institute).

## Founding and Vision

After establishing himself in [[Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] sought to create more than a personal refuge. He envisioned a community where fugitive slaves could achieve economic independence through skilled labor and agriculture, demonstrating that formerly enslaved people could "do something for themselves." The land was "covered with a beautiful forest of noble trees" (019-001) that the settlers cleared through organized lumbering operations.

The centerpiece of the settlement was the [[manual-labor-school|Manual Labor School]], combining academic education (reading, writing, arithmetic) with vocational training in agriculture, carpentry, and other trades. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s vision aligned with the manual labor school movement of the antebellum period, which emphasized practical skills alongside academic [[education|learning]].

## Economic Foundation

The settlement's economic base was lumbering. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] directed the clearing of the forest, built a sawmill, and exported black walnut lumber — a valuable commodity — via the St. Lawrence River (019-008). The enterprise provided employment for settlers and generated revenue to support the school and community.

> "The land on which the settlement was made was covered with a beautiful forest of noble trees, and the work of clearing it was the first thing to be done. We went to work with a will, and soon had a number of acres cleared and ready for cultivation." (019-001)

The lumbering enterprise was central to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s philosophy of self-sufficiency. Rather than depending on charity, the settlers would earn their own way through productive labor — a powerful argument against racist assumptions about the capabilities of formerly enslaved people.

## Challenges and Fundraising

The settlement faced significant challenges including financial pressures and "personal difficulties" with "certain individuals" who sought to undermine [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s leadership (020-006). [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s fundraising trips to [[england|England]] — including his attendance at the 1851 [[london|Great Exhibition]] in [[london|London]] — were essential to the settlement's survival (021-001).

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s interest in the school "was the means of my visiting [[boston|Boston]]" and other eastern cities to raise support (020-001). These fundraising journeys connected the Dawn Settlement to a transatlantic network of abolitionists and philanthropists, securing the resources that kept the community viable through difficult periods.

## Historical Significance

The Dawn Settlement was part of a broader network of Black Canadian communities established by [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] refugees in 19th-century [[canada|Ontario]]. Alongside settlements like the Elgin Settlement (Buxton) and the Refugee Home Society lands, Dawn demonstrated that formerly enslaved people could build thriving, self-governing communities when given the opportunity.

> "I had now been in Canada nearly three years, and had made some progress in the world. I had built a house, had a little stock of cattle, and was getting on quite comfortably. I felt that I was a man among men." (016-001)
