# Escape to Canada

[[Josiah Henson]]'s daring escape from [[slavery]] in [[Kentucky]] with his wife [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] and their children, culminating in their arrival on free soil in [[Canada]] on October 28, 1830. The escape is the narrative's dramatic climax — the moment when [[josiah-henson|Henson]] transformed from an enslaved man into a free one.

## Background

By 1830, despite years of faithful service — including a remarkable overland journey transporting enslaved people from [[maryland|Maryland]] to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] on behalf of [[Isaac Riley]] — [[josiah-henson|Henson]] learned he was to be sold south. The threat of permanent separation from his family galvanized him. He had already passed through the moral crisis of his "[[conversion-experience|terrible temptation]]" on the journey to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] (010-001), and was now determined to act.

> "One absorbing purpose occupied my soul — freedom, self-assertion, deliverance from the cruel caprices and fortunes of dissolute tyrants. Once to get away, with my wife and children, to some spot where I could feel that they were indeed *mine* — where no grasping master could stand between me and them, as arbiter of their destiny — was a heaven yearned after with insatiable longing." (010-002)

## The Escape Route

|-------|----------|---------|-----------|
| 1. Departure | [[kentucky|Kentucky]] river landing | Departed at night in small boat, crossing to [[indiana|Indiana]] shore | (012-006) |
| 2. Overland | [[ohio|Ohio]] | Two weeks traveling by night, following North Star | (012-009) |
| 3. Approach | [[buffalo|Buffalo]], [[new-york|New York]] | [[josiah-henson|Henson]] feigned rheumatism; sympathetic carriage driver assisted | (013-005) |
| 4. Crossing | Niagara River | Helped by Black sailor and two crewmen | (013-012) |
| 5. Arrival | [[canada|Canada]] | October 28, 1830 — "[[canada|land of freedom]]" | (013-012) |

The escape plan was carefully devised. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s [[slave-cabin|cabin]] was near [[mississippi-river|the river]] landing, enabling a nighttime departure. The family traveled by night and rested by day, following the North Star — the celestial guide used by countless freedom seekers. For two weeks they pressed steadily northward through [[ohio|Ohio]] (012-009).

The most critical moment came near [[buffalo|Buffalo]], where [[josiah-henson|Henson]], pretending to be ill with rheumatism, arranged passage in a carriage driven by a local man who was sympathetic to fugitive slaves (013-005). At the Niagara River crossing, a Black sailor and two crewmen facilitated the family's ferry to Canadian soil.

## Arrival

> "It was the 28th of October, 1830, in the morning, when my feet first touched the soil of a land of freedom. I was then about forty-one years of age, having lived more than forty years in bondage. I can truly say I know what slavery is, and what freedom is." (013-012)

The emotional impact was overwhelming. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] "thanked God with all the fervor of a full heart for his great mercy" (013-012). The precise date — October 28, 1830 — is one of the few exact dates in the narrative, underscoring its singular importance to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s life story.

## Aftermath and Legacy

The escape was not merely [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s personal liberation — it established a pattern. He would later return multiple times to guide other enslaved people to freedom, becoming an [[Underground Railroad]] conductor of considerable effectiveness (016-001). His escape narrative, embedded within his larger autobiography, became one of the compelling testimonies of the [[abolition]] movement.

The escape also made possible everything that followed: the founding of the [[Dawn Settlement]], the fundraising trips to [[england|England]], and the publication of the narrative itself. Without this moment of daring and [[freedom|deliverance]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] would likely have been sold to the Deep South and disappeared from the historical record — one more casualty of the domestic slave trade.
