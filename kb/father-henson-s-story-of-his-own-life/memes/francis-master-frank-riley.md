Francis "Master Frank" Riley was the brother of [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]]'s wife and a rare ally to [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] within the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] family orbit. Having established himself in business at [[washington|Washington]], D.C., he served as [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s confidant, freedom negotiator, and — crucially — the only disinterested witness to the true terms of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s manumission agreement, later betrayed by [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]].

## Early Relationship

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s bond with Frank [[isaac-riley|Riley]] dated to the latter's boyhood in the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] household. Frank had been "an abused and harshly-treated boy in the house," and [[josiah-henson|Henson]] — then an enslaved man on the same property — had done much to lighten his sorrows (008-003). This early [[kindness]] established a foundation of [[trust]] that would prove decisive years later.

## The Appeal at Washington

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] returned from [[kentucky|Kentucky]] to [[maryland|Maryland]] and found his former home in a degraded condition, he resolved to seek Frank's help. Frank was now of age and established in [[washington|Washington]], making him independent of [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]]'s authority:

> I knew of but one friend to whom I could appeal — "Master Frank," the brother of Riley's wife, before mentioned, who was now of age, and had established himself in business in Washington. I knew he would take an interest in me, for I had done much to lighten his sorrows when he was an abused and harshly-treated boy in the house. (008-003)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] obtained a pass from [[mrs-riley|Mrs. Riley]] under the pretext of a social visit and rode to [[washington|Washington]], where his reception by Frank confirmed all his hopes:

> My reception by Master Frank was all I expected, as kind and hearty as possible. He was delighted at my appearance, and I immediately told him all my plans and hopes. He entered cordially into them, and expressed, as he felt, I doubt not, a strong sympathy for me. (008-004)

## Negotiating the Freedom Purchase

Frank [[isaac-riley|Riley]] proved to be an effective advocate. He detested [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] — whom he charged with defrauding him of property held in guardianship — but was not openly at war with him. This allowed him to negotiate diplomatically:

> I found that he thoroughly detested Riley, whom he charged with having defrauded him of a large proportion of his property which he had held as guardian, though, as he was not at warfare with him, he readily agreed to negotiate for my freedom, and bring him to the most favorable terms. (008-004)

Frank rode to [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s house and presented a persuasive case: [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had money and a pass, was determined to gain his freedom, had served the family for many years, and — if not treated fairly — might simply disappear with his resources. His leverage proved effective:

> By such arguments as these, Mr. Frank not only induced him to think of the thing, but before long brought him to an actual bargain, by which he agreed to give me my manumission papers for four hundred and fifty dollars, of which three hundred and fifty dollars were to be in cash, and the remainder in my note. (008-004)

The manumission papers were thereafter secured on March 9, 1829.

## The Only Witness to the Truth

When [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] later defrauded [[josiah-henson|Henson]] by having the manumission papers sent in a sealed envelope to his brother Amos in [[kentucky|Kentucky]] — where Amos claimed [[josiah-henson|Henson]] still owed six hundred and fifty dollars more — Frank [[isaac-riley|Riley]] stood as the sole person who knew the real terms:

> The only witness to the truth, Master Frank, was a thousand miles away. I could neither write to him, or get any one else to write. Every man about me who could write was a slaveholder. (008-007)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s isolation from his only corroborating witness left him powerless to contest the fraud, and he was forced to labor additional years to pay the fictitious balance. The episode illustrates both the value of Frank's advocacy and the structural vulnerability that even a well-connected enslaved person faced without accessible legal testimony.
