# London

London was the center of [[Josiah Henson]]'s two fundraising and abolitionist tours of [[England]] in the early 1850s. It was here, at the Crystal Palace, that he exhibited Canadian black walnut lumber at the Great Exhibition; here, at [[exeter-hall|Exeter Hall]], that he addressed crowds on the condition of [[us-america|America]]n slaves; and here that he met with the highest dignitaries of British church and state in pursuit of support for the [[Dawn Settlement]].

## Location & Geography

London, the capital of the [[england|British Empire]] on the River Thames, was in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s era the largest city in the world and the nexus of global commerce, politics, and culture. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s London was concentrated in the districts around the Crystal Palace (Hyde Park), [[exeter-hall|Exeter Hall]] (the Strand), and the palace of the Archbishop of Canterbury, as well as the "elegant grounds" of [[lord-john-russell|Lord John Russell]]'s estate outside the city (022-005).

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] first arrived in London in 1850–51 with a practical commercial mission: "to go to [[england|England]], carrying with me some of the best specimens of black walnut boards our farm would produce, and to exhibit them in the great World's Industrial Exhibition, then in session at London" (020-004). He carried letters of introduction from prominent Canadian and [[us-america|America]]n figures including Chief Justice Robinson, [[john-rolfe|Rev. John Rolfe]], and [[amos-lawrence|Amos Lawrence]], which gained him "immediate introduction to the very best society in the kingdom" (020-004).

The exhibition at the Crystal Palace became a minor international incident when the [[us-america|America]]n superintendent, Mr. Riddle of [[boston|Boston]], insisted that [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s lumber display in the [[us-america|America]]n department. [[josiah-henson|Henson]], a Canadian citizen, successfully fought to have his boards moved to the Canadian section (021-002). His walnut planks, "about seven feet in length and four feet in width, of beautiful grain and texture," were "planed and perfectly polished, in French style, so that they actually shone like a mirror"(021-001). The display won a medal.

While in London, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] became a sought-after speaker on the abolitionist circuit. He "was frequently called upon to speak at public meetings," addressing the [[ragged-schools-visit|Ragged School enterprise]] and many "great anniversaries" (022-001). His most powerful platform was [[exeter-hall|Exeter Hall]], the great abolitionist venue on the Strand, where he spoke "on several occasions... to make known the true condition of slaves" (022-001).

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s London encounters brought him face to face with British eminence. [[samuel-gurney|Samuel Gurney]], the noted philanthropist, introduced him to the Archbishop of Canterbury at the archbishop's palace, where the prelate famously asked: "At what university, Sir, did you graduate?" — to which [[josiah-henson|Henson]] replied: "I graduated, your grace, at the university of adversity" (022-003). On a later visit, in 1852, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] dined with [[lord-john-russell|Lord John Russell]], the Prime Minister, in "a spacious dining hall, whose dimensions could not have been less than one hundred feet by sixty" alongside three hundred [[sabbath|Sabbath]] School teachers (022-005).

## Historical Context

Victorian London was the nexus of British abolitionism, a movement that had achieved [[freedom|emancipation]] throughout the [[england|British Empire]] in 1834. [[exeter-hall|Exeter Hall]], opened in 1831, was the principal venue for abolitionist, missionary, and reform meetings, drawing thousands. The [[worlds-fair-london|Great Exhibition of 1851]] was a showcase of imperial industry attended by six million people, including many abolitionist delegates from abroad. London's philanthropic societies raised large sums for antislavery causes, and [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s visits exemplified the transatlantic network that sustained the Canadian fugitive settlements.

## Key Passages

> "My project was to go to England, carrying with me some of the best specimens of black walnut boards our farm would produce, and to exhibit them in the great World's Industrial Exhibition, then in session at London, and perhaps negotiate for the sale of lumber." (020-004)

> "While in England I was frequently called upon to speak at public meetings of various kinds... On several occasions I did what I could to make known the true condition of slaves, in Exeter Hall and other places." (022-001)

> "He expressed the strongest interest in me, and after about a half hour's conversation he inquired, 'At what university, Sir, did you graduate?' 'I graduated, your grace,' said I in reply, 'at the university of adversity.'" (022-003)
