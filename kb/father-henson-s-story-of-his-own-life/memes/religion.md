# Religion

Religion was the central organizing force of [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s inner life and public identity. His narrative traces a journey from the rudimentary religious instruction of his enslaved mother through a dramatic conversion experience to a life of preaching, exhortation, and spiritual leadership among both Black and white communities. "My heart exults with gratitude," [[josiah-henson|Henson]] wrote, "when I mention the name of a good man who first taught me the blessedness of religion" (004-001).

## Definition

In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s context, religion meant evangelical Protestant [[christianity|Christianity]] — characterized by personal conversion, biblical authority, [[prayer|pray]]er, preaching, and active participation in a church community. It was the dominant cultural framework of 19th-century [[us-america|America]], yet it was selectively applied: enslaved people were often encouraged to accept [[christianity|Christianity]]'s promises of heavenly reward while being denied its implications of human dignity and freedom.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother was his first religious teacher. Though she could not read, she imparted a deep sense of faith: "How or where she acquired her knowledge of God... I am unable to say" (corpus text, Chapter 2). She taught him the Lord's [[prayer|Prayer]] and modeled a life of [[prayer|devotion]] despite her [[slavery|bondage]].

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s formal religious awakening came through a man named John McKenny, "who first taught me the blessedness of religion" (004-001). After hearing a sermon that presented Jesus Christ's "tender love for mankind, his forgiving grace, his mercy" (004-002), [[josiah-henson|Henson]] experienced a profound conversion:

> "The divine character of Jesus Christ, his tender love for mankind, his forgiving spirit his compassion for the outcast and despised, his cruel crucifixion and glorious ascension, were all depicted, and some of the hymns were descriptive of his death, which opened for me a new world of thought." (004-002)

This experience dated the turning point of his life. He became a preacher, exhorting fellow slaves and eventually traveling as a [[methodism|Methodist preacher]] (007-006).

## Henson's Perspective

Reflecting on his unlikely path from illiterate slave to internationally known preacher, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] acknowledged the paradox: "It may seem strange to many, that a man so ignorant as myself, unable to read, and having heard so little as I had of religion, natural or revealed, should be able to preach" (014-004). His answer was experiential rather than theological: "Religion is not so much knowledge, as a refuge" — a shelter for the soul drawn not from books but from lived suffering and divine encounter.

On a [[sabbath|Sabbath]] morning in [[canada|Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] asked his son Tom to read to him from the [[bible|Bible]], and the experience moved him so deeply that he could not preach that day:

> "I was greatly moved by the conversation I had with Tom, so much so that I could not undertake to preach that day. The congregation were disappointed, and I passed the Sunday without attempting anything more than to say a few words." (014-007)

During his visit to [[england|England]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had a memorable interview with the Archbishop of Canterbury, who questioned him closely about his religious knowledge. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] later reflected that "perhaps the archbishop would give me an interview of a quarter of an hour; I glanced at the clock and found that I had already been there an hour and a half" (022-004). This meeting demonstrated that a formerly enslaved man, without formal theological training, could engage at the highest levels of the Church of [[england|England]] on equal spiritual terms.

## Historical Context

The Second Great Awakening (c. 1790–1840) transformed [[us-america|America]]n religious life, spreading evangelical fervor across all social classes. [[methodism|Methodism]], in particular, grew rapidly among both white and Black [[us-america|America]]ns thanks to its emphasis on personal conversion, itinerant preaching, and camp meetings. For enslaved people, [[christianity|Christianity]] offered a language of dignity, hope, and liberation — even as enslavers selectively deployed scripture to justify [[slavery|the institution]]. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s religious story mirrors the broader story of African [[us-america|America]]n [[christianity|Christianity]]: born in oppression, sustained by faith, and expressed in a distinctive voice of prophetic witness.

The camp meeting, a frontier revival institution central to Methodist practice, was the setting for [[camp-meeting|Henson's conversion]]. These large outdoor gatherings drew thousands and were among the few spaces where enslaved and free people could worship together, making them crucibles of both spiritual and social transformation.
