# Sabbath

The Sabbath was the weekly day of rest and worship in [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s world — a day set apart from labor for religious observance, family time, and community gathering. For [[josiah-henson|Henson]], the Sabbath marked moments of spiritual renewal, family bonding, and public ministry. It was both a refuge from the demands of [[slavery|enslavement]] and, later, the cornerstone of community life at the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]].

## Definition

In the 19th-century evangelical [[christianity|Christian]] tradition in which [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was raised, the Sabbath (observed on Sunday) was a day of rest from physical labor, dedicated to church attendance, [[prayer|pray]]er, and religious instruction. For enslaved people, the Sabbath often provided the only respite from forced labor and a rare opportunity for independent religious gathering. The keeping of the Sabbath was sometimes permitted or even encouraged by enslavers who viewed religious instruction as a means of promoting docility (004-001, context of [[josiah-henson|Henson]] hearing preaching on Sundays). At other times it was contested ground — a day when enslaved people might seize rare moments of autonomy: "Saturday or Sunday, which were their holidays" (007-001).

## Role in the Narrative

The Sabbath appears at several significant moments in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative. As a child, he found solace in the quiet of the day: "the quiet of the Sabbath when there was no assembly" (014-006). Later, as a father in [[canada|Canada]], he recalled one particular Sabbath morning: "One beautiful summer Sabbath I rose early, and called him to come and read to me" (014-006). This scene — a father asking his newly literate son to read scripture aloud, "especially on Sunday mornings, when I was indisposed" (014-005) — symbolizes the transformation from illiterate slave to literate free man, enacted through the Sabbath ritual of family [[bible|Bible]] reading.

After settling in [[canada|Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] experienced another joyous Sabbath reunion: "After the lapse of a few months, on one joyous Sabbath morning, I had the happiness of clasping the poor boy we had left" (017-016), describing the reunion with a fugitive child who had been left in the care of [[quaker|Quakers]] during the escape journey. The Sabbath here is not only a day of rest but a day of restoration — of family bonds broken by slavery now mended in freedom.

As a preacher and community leader, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] describes churches that were "well filled from Sabbath to Sabbath with attentive listeners" (016-002), indicating the vitality of Sabbath worship in the Canadian settlements. His own preaching ministry reached its fullest expression on the Sabbath day.

## Henson's Perspective

For [[josiah-henson|Henson]], the Sabbath represented both religious obligation and personal liberation. As an enslaved man, his access to Sabbath worship was limited and controlled — the day offered spiritual sustenance but within boundaries set by enslavers. As a free man in [[canada|Canada]], the Sabbath became a day of public preaching and community leadership in which he exercised full autonomy. His narrative reveals a man for whom the Sabbath was not merely observed but inhabited — a day of genuine spiritual experience rather than dutiful formality.

The Sabbath also became the day when [[josiah-henson|Henson]], still illiterate, received access to [[bible|Scripture]] through his son's reading: "especially on Sunday mornings, when I was indisposed, I found him very useful" (014-005, context). This weekly ritual of listening to the [[bible|Bible]] read aloud was both a spiritual discipline and an intimate family practice.

## Historical Context

In antebellum [[us-america|America]], the Sabbath was a contested institution. Many enslavers permitted or even encouraged religious instruction on Sundays, believing it promoted obedience and contentment. Others restricted Sabbath gatherings, fearing they could become occasions for conspiracy or escape planning. The tension between these two impulses shaped the religious experience of enslaved communities.

For free Black communities in [[canada|Canada]], the Sabbath was central to community life — a day for worship, education (Sabbath Schools), and the reinforcement of shared values and identity. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s own involvement with Sabbath Schools is evident in the narrative's account of his speaking engagements. At one Sunday School anniversary in [[england|England]], he addressed a gathering of teachers and spoke candidly about the conditions of the enslaved: "I was at a meeting at the Sunday School Union... where the speaker was boasting of the great benefits of Sunday Schools" (022-008), using the occasion to advocate for the enslaved. The Sabbath, in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s hands, was never merely a day of rest — it was a platform for witness and advocacy.
