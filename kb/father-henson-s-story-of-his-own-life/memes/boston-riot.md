# Boston Mob Attack

A violent mob attack on [[Josiah Henson]] at the [[Boston]] Custom House around 1851, during the tense years following the [[Fugitive Slave Law]] of 1850. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] — a free Black man engaged in the lumber trade between [[Canada]] and [[Massachusetts]] — was set upon by a mob, but successfully defended his reputation through a public meeting backed by [[boston|Boston]]'s leading abolitionist citizens.

## Context

After the [[Escape to Canada]] and the founding of the [[Dawn Settlement]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] built a sawmill and began shipping black walnut lumber to [[Boston]]. His trade flourished: he brought down "eighty thousand feet" of lumber down the St. Lawrence River to [[boston|Boston]], paying his own duties and handling the business independently (019-008).

> "The next season I brought a large cargo by the river St. Lawrence, which came direct to Boston, where, without the aid of any agent or third party whatever, I paid my own duties, got the lumber through the Custom House, and sold it." (019-008)

However, the political climate in [[boston|Boston]] was dangerous. The [[Fugitive Slave Law]] of 1850 had empowered slave-catchers and emboldened anti-Black mobs in Northern cities. Any Black man — free or fugitive — could be targeted.

## The Attack

According to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s account, the attack was publicly known and discussed:

> "When the above attack was made upon me, a meeting of those interested in my cause was called, and my accuser, who was in the country, was requested to meet me face to face." (020-008)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] does not describe the mob violence in detail, but the context is clear: during one of his [[boston|Boston]] visits, an organized group confronted him at or near the Custom House, motivated by the anti-Black sentiment that had intensified after the 1850 law (019-003).

## The Vindication Meeting

In response to the attack, [[Amos Lawrence]], [[H. Ingersoll Bowditch]], and other [[boston|Boston]] philanthropists who knew [[josiah-henson|Henson]] personally called a public meeting. The atmosphere was charged — [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s reputation and physical safety were both at stake.

> "They were pleased to assure me of their entire satisfaction; but to give perfect quiet to the public they determined, at their own expense, to send an agent to Canada, to make a full inquiry into the whole matter." (020-011)

The meeting was presided over by [[John Rolfe|Rev. John Rolfe]] of [[toronto|Toronto]]. A thorough investigation cleared [[josiah-henson|Henson]] entirely. The agent sent to [[Dawn Settlement]] confirmed [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s character and business integrity, and the attack was exposed as motivated by sectarian jealousy and racial animus (020-012).

## Significance

The [[boston|Boston]] mob attack is one of the few episodes in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative where he directly experienced racial violence in a Northern city — the very region that abolitionists portrayed as a refuge from Southern [[kindness|brutality]]. It demonstrates that:

- Even free, accomplished Black men were vulnerable to mob violence in antebellum [[boston|Boston]]
- [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s network of white abolitionist allies — [[Amos Lawrence]], [[Samuel Eliot]], [[Rev. Ephraim Peabody]] — provided crucial protection
- The incident strengthened rather than weakened [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s standing, as the subsequent investigation proved his character beyond doubt
