## Henson's Journey of Observation

During his travels through the Northern states, [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] passed through New England on a journey of observation. He kept his purposes to himself, not revealing his intentions to anyone, as he studied the economic and agricultural conditions of the region. (019-002)

In [[new-york|New York]] and New England, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] found mills where logs similar to those available at the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] in [[canada|Canada]] were being sawed into lumber, which commanded large prices. These observations informed his plans for developing the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]] sawmill enterprise. (019-002)

## Economic Observations

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s travels through the Northern states served a practical purpose beyond personal curiosity. As the leader of the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]], he was actively scouting for economic models that could be applied to the Canadian community of formerly enslaved people. What he observed in the mills of New England provided a direct template for the industrial development of [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]].

> I kept my purposes to myself, and gave no utterance to my plans. I visited the mills and factories in the towns, and studied the economic conditions of the people. In New York and New England, I found logs being sawed into lumber — the same logs that could be had in abundance at Dawn — and commanding large prices.
>
> (019-002)

## Agricultural Comparison

In later reflections on Canadian settlement, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] drew comparisons between the agricultural potential of [[canada|Canada]] and that of a "Northern New England or [[new-york|New York]] farm." He noted that the Canadian lands could ripen corn, wheat, rye, oats, and various other productions, and that fruit trees — apples, cherries, plums, peaches, quinces, currants, gooseberries, and strawberries — were doing well. (024-001)
