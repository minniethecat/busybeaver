# Faith and Hope

Faith and hope were not merely theological abstractions for [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] and his community—they were essential resources for survival under the brutal conditions of slavery. Throughout his narrative, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] returns repeatedly to the sustaining power of [[christianity|Christian]] belief and the hope it provided for a better future, both spiritual and temporal.

## Faith as Moral Foundation

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s [[religion|religious faith]] was awakened by a single sermon he heard as a young man. Though he had grown up "without [[christianity|Christian]] light or knowledge," the message of Christ's love for the outcast and despised resonated deeply with his experience as an enslaved person. He responded with heartfelt [[prayer|pray]]er—"however unenlightened," yet "sincere and heartfelt"—that he believed was "acceptable to Him" (010-001).

The [[christianity|Christian]] doctrine of forgiveness became a defining element of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s character. Even when provoked to the point of contemplating violence against his oppressors, his faith intervened. In one dramatic moment, he raised an axe to kill his sleeping companions on a boat, intending to escape north, but was stopped by conscience and what he understood as [[providence|divine intervention]]. "God be praised!" became his refrain through the darkest moments (013-001).

## Hope as a Motive Force

Hope propelled [[josiah-henson|Henson]] forward through each stage of his journey toward freedom. When finally aboard the steamboat leaving the slave states, his heart "sang jubilees of praise to Thee, as the steamboat swung loose from the levee and breasted the mighty tide of [[mississippi-river|the Mississippi]]!" He cried: "Away from this land of [[slavery|bondage]] and death! Away from misery and despair! Once more exulting hope possessed me" (011-005).

In [[canada|Canada]], faith and hope remained central. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] described the immense gratitude he felt for [[freedom|deliverance]], and his conviction that God's providence had guided his entire journey. The faith that sustained him in slavery became the foundation for his work as a preacher and community leader.

## Corporate Faith

Faith was not only individual but communal. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] described days of [[prayer|pray]]er in the woods, [[sabbath|Sabbath]] worship, and the shared religious life of the fugitive slave settlements in [[canada|Canada]]. The church served as a center of community organization, mutual support, and collective hope for the future.

> I have no desire to represent the life of slavery as an experience of nothing but misery. God be praised, that however hedged in by circumstances, the joyful exuberance of youth will bound at times over them all. Ours is a light-hearted race. The sternest and most covetous master cannot frighten or whip the fun out of us.
>
> (003-006)
