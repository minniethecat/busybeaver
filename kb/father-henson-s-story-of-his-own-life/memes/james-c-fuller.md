James C. Fuller was an English-born [[quaker|Quaker]] residing at [[new-york|Skeneateles]], [[new-york|New York]], who served as a crucial transatlantic fundraiser for the Canadian black settlements in the late 1830s. At the request of [[hiram-wilson|Hiram Wilson]], Fuller traveled to [[england|England]] and returned with fifteen hundred dollars in subscriptions — a sum that catalyzed the first convention of black settlement delegates in [[canada|Upper Canada]].

## Connection to Hiram Wilson

Fuller's involvement with the Canadian settlements began through his friendship with [[hiram-wilson|Hiram Wilson]], a Congregational missionary from [[boston|Massachusetts]] working among the fugitive slave population in [[canada|Upper Canada]]:

> Among other things which he did for us then, he wrote to a Quaker friend of his, an Englishman, by the name of James C. Fuller, residing at Skeneateles, New York, and endeavored to interest him in the welfare of our struggling population. (018-003)

Wilson's letter to Fuller was part of a broader effort to secure resources for the growing communities of formerly enslaved people who had fled to [[canada|Canada]]. Fuller, as a [[quaker|Quaker]], belonged to a religious tradition already engaged in [[abolition|abolitionist]] work on both sides of the Atlantic.

## Fundraising Journey to England

Fuller's most significant contribution was a fundraising trip to [[england|England]] undertaken around 1837–1838. According to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative:

> He succeeded so far, that Mr. Fuller, who was going on a visit to England, promised to do what he could among his friends there, to induce them to aid us. He came back with fifteen hundred dollars which had been subscribed for our benefit. (018-004)

The sum of fifteen hundred dollars — a considerable amount in the 1830s — was raised entirely through Fuller's personal network of English [[quaker|Quakers]] and sympathizers. The funds were designated for the general welfare of the black settlements, without restriction to any particular project.

## The 1838 London Convention

The arrival of Fuller's fifteen hundred dollars prompted a significant organizational moment in the history of the settlements. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and Wilson recognized that the appropriation of such a sum required broad community consent:

> It was a great question how this sum, which sounded vast to many of my brethren, should be appropriated. I had my own opinion pretty decidedly as to what it was best for us all to do with it. But, in order to come to a satisfactory conclusion, the first thing to be done was to call a convention of delegates from every settlement of blacks that was within reach; that all might see that whatever was decided on, was sanctioned by the disinterested votes of those who were thought by their companions, best able to judge what was expedient. (018-004)

Wilson and [[josiah-henson|Henson]] called the convention to meet in [[london|London]], [[canada|Upper Canada]], in June 1838. This gathering — made possible by Fuller's fundraising — represented a milestone in the self-governance of the fugitive slave settlements.
