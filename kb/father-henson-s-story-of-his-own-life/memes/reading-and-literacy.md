# Reading and Literacy

Literacy was both a forbidden fruit and a gateway to freedom for enslaved African [[us-america|America]]ns. [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s narrative offers a firsthand account of how one man—denied [[education|literacy]] himself—came to value reading and ultimately devoted himself to establishing schools for his community.

## Henson's Illiteracy

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] was never taught to read or write during his years of [[slavery|enslavement]]. As he frankly acknowledged, he was "unable to read" and had "heard so little" of religion beyond what he absorbed orally. Yet he was still able to preach effectively, which he attributed to divine grace rather than formal education (016-001).

## Learning Through His Son

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s eldest son, Tom, was given two quarters of [[education|schooling]] by their employer [[mr-hibbard|Mr. Hibbard]] in [[canada|Canada]], with the schoolmaster adding more of his own [[kindness]]. Tom "learned to read fluently and well," which proved invaluable to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]. On [[sabbath|Sunday]] mornings before preaching, Tom would read the [[bible|Bible]] to his father, who could "easily commit to memory a few verses" and incorporate them into his sermons (017-001).

On one memorable [[sabbath|Sabbath]], Tom read Psalm 103 aloud: "Bless the Lord, O my soul: and all that is within me, bless his holy name." This passage moved [[josiah-henson|Henson]] deeply and strengthened his faith and gratitude (017-001).

## The Manual Labor School at Dawn

Recognizing the transformative power of education, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] became a driving force behind the establishment of a manual-labor school at the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]] settlement in [[canada|Canada]]. He advocated for a school where children could receive a grammar-school education while boys learned mechanic arts and girls were taught domestic skills (019-001).

The school faced significant financial challenges, accumulating a debt of approximately seven thousand five hundred dollars. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] traveled to [[england|England]] in 1849 to raise funds, an effort that ultimately succeeded in clearing the debt and securing [[slavery|the institution]] (020-001).

## Broader Impact

By the time [[josiah-henson|Henson]] wrote his narrative, he could report that the fugitive slave settlements in [[canada|Canada]] had schools "at which our children are being taught the ordinary branches of an English education." He expressed optimism that the Black community would "take a very respectable rank among the subjects of her majesty" (023-001).

## Significance

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s experience illustrates the central role of [[education|literacy]] in the struggle for freedom and dignity. Despite his own lack of formal education, he recognized its value and worked to ensure the next generation would not share his disadvantage. The story of his son reading the [[bible|Bible]] to him captures both the deprivation of slavery and the resourcefulness of those who overcame it.

> Mr. Hibbard was good enough to give my eldest boy, Tom, two quarters' schooling, to which the schoolmaster added more, of his own kindness, so that my boy learned to read fluently and well. It was a great advantage, not only to him, but to me; for I used to get him to read much to me in the Bible, especially on Sunday mornings, when I was going to preach; and I could easily commit to memory a few verses, or a chapter, from hearing him read it over.
>
> (014-005)
