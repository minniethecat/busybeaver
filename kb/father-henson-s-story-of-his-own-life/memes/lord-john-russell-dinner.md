# Dinner with Lord John Russell

On a June afternoon in 1852, [[Josiah Henson]] — born enslaved on a [[maryland|Maryland]] [[plantation]], self-taught preacher, and fugitive — was seated at the head of the table in the mansion of [[Lord John Russell]], Prime Minister of [[England]]. It was, he wrote, "one of the pleasantest days of my life" (022-009).

## The Invitation

[[josiah-henson|Henson]], in [[england|England]] to raise funds for the [[Dawn Settlement]] in [[Canada]], had become well known in [[london|London]] philanthropic circles through his speeches at [[Exeter Hall]], his visits to the Ragged Schools, and his interview with the Archbishop of Canterbury. [[Samuel Gurney]] and other evangelical leaders had opened doors throughout English society.

The invitation came during [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s second visit to [[england|England]]: he was asked to join "a large number of [[sabbath|Sabbath]] School Teachers" for a day at [[lord-john-russell|Lord John Russell]]'s country estate in [[Richmond Park]] (022-005). Russell, a Whig reformer who had served as Prime Minister from 1846 to 1852, was one of the most powerful men in Britain — and a patron of educational and religious causes.

## The Grounds

Russell's estate was a revelation to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]. The park was filled with "deer, of all colors, and from all climes, and sleek hares... with numberless birds, whose plumage rivalled the rainbow in gorgeous colors, together with the choicest specimens of the finny tribe, sporting in their native element" (022-005).

The contrast with his own past was immediate and overwhelming:

> "O, how different the condition of these happy, sportive, joyful, creatures, from what was once my own condition, and what is now the lot of millions of my colored brethren in America!" (022-005)

## The Banquet

At five o'clock, the three hundred guests were "most unexpectedly" summoned to the mansion. They were ushered into a dining hall "whose dimensions could not have been less than one hundred feet by sixty" — tables "groaning under every article of luxury for the palate, which [[england|England]] could supply" (022-005).

[[josiah-henson|Henson]], the fugitive slave, was invited to take the head of the table:

> "I was invited to take the head of the table; I never felt so highly honored." (022-005)

The blessing was sung, and after dinner the toasts began. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] rose and offered his own, which was received with "cheers and laughter" and the traditional English call of "_Up, up, up again!_" (022-007):

> "First to England. Honor to the brave, freedom to the Slave, success to British emancipation. God bless the Queen!" (022-006)

He followed with toasts to Queen Victoria — "May she have a long life, and a happy death. May she reign in righteousness, and rule in love!" — and to Prince Albert (022-007 through 022-008).

## Distinguished Company

Among the speakers that evening were the [[Reverend William Brock]], a prominent Baptist minister, and [[Sir Samuel Morton Peto]], the railway magnate and philanthropist who was one of the wealthiest men in Victorian [[england|England]]. Peto and his brother-in-law, a Mr. Bess, attended with "his accomplished and beautiful lady" (022-009).

The banquet was not a private dinner but a large-scale affair — part picnic, part state occasion — organized for the benefit of [[sabbath|Sabbath]] School teachers. That [[josiah-henson|Henson]], a black man who had been property under [[us-america|America]]n law only two decades earlier, was placed at the head of the table in this company was a deliberate gesture by Russell, whose Whig politics were aligned with the antislavery cause.

## Aftermath and Significance

The dinner at [[richmond-park|Richmond Park]] represented the apex of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s social recognition in [[england|England]]. From this platform, he completed the publication of his narrative — an edition of two thousand copies, "urgently requested... by some of the noblest men and women in [[england|England]]" (023-001) — just before receiving the devastating news that his wife [[Charlotte Henson|Charlotte]] lay dying in [[canada|Canada]].

The experience crystalized [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s understanding of what freedom meant in practice: not merely the absence of chains, but the capacity to sit as an equal at the tables of power and to speak in one's own voice before those who governed empires.
