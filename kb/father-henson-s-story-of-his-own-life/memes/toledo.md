# Toledo

Toledo, [[ohio|Ohio]], was a port town on the southwestern shore of [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]] that served as the final [[us-america|America]]n waypoint for [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] and a party of fugitive slaves before their passage to freedom in [[canada|Canada]].

## Henson's Arrival

After receiving aid from [[quakers|Quaker]] settlers at [[indiana|Richmond, Indiana]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s party endured a difficult two-week journey through the wilderness before reaching Toledo:

> "After a difficult journey of two weeks, through the wilderness, we reached Toledo, Ohio, a town on the south-western shore of Lake Erie, and there we took passage for Canada, which we reached in safety." (016-006)

The party that arrived at Toledo was substantial — about thirty fugitives collected from different states in [[kentucky|Kentucky]], whom [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had led across the [[ohio-river|Ohio River]] and through the wilderness of [[indiana|Indiana]] (016-005).

## The Rescue Mission

The journey that brought [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to Toledo was his first organized rescue mission after his own escape to freedom. The mission was prompted by [[james-lightfoot|James Lightfoot]], a freed man living in [[canada|Canada]], who appealed to [[josiah-henson|Henson]] at a large meeting at [[fort-erie|Fort Erie]]. [[james-lightfoot|Lightfoot]] had been free for five years but had left behind his family on [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River near [[maysville|Maysville]]. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] agreed to undertake the dangerous task, leaving his own family and walking four hundred miles alone through [[new-york|New York]], [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]], and [[ohio|Ohio]] to reach [[kentucky|Kentucky]] (016-001–016-003).

The return journey with the thirty fugitives was perilous. The group crossed [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River and reached [[cincinnati|Cincinnati]] on the third night, where they received assistance before pushing on to [[richmond-indiana|Richmond, Indiana]] (016-005).

## Strategic Significance

Toledo's location on [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]] made it a natural embarkation point for fugitive slaves heading to [[canada|Canada]] by water. The lake route was often safer and faster than overland travel through potentially hostile territory. From Toledo, vessels crossed [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]] to Canadian ports, completing the final leg of the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] journey. For [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s party, reaching Toledo meant the boundary between [[us-america|America]]n danger and Canadian safety had narrowed to a single body of water.
