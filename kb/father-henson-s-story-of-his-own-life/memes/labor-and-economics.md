# Labor and Economics of Slavery

The economic dimension of [[us-america|America]]n slavery pervades [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s narrative. From his earliest memories of [[plantation]] labor to his later efforts to build an economically self-sufficient Black settlement in [[canada|Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s life was shaped by the material realities of an economic system built on enslaved labor.

## Plantation Labor

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s earliest memories were of labor on a [[maryland|Maryland]] [[plantation]]. He described carrying buckets of water to field hands and performing tasks appropriate to a young enslaved child. The daily routine of the [[plantation]] was organized entirely around maximizing labor output — two meals a day, log huts with bare ground floors, and relentless work from sunrise to sunset (003-001, 003-003, 003-004).

The [[plantation]] system treated enslaved people as economic assets whose value was measured in their productive capacity. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] noted that despite this harsh system, he grew "robust and active," a physical constitution that made him more valuable as a laborer but also more aware of the injustice of his condition (003-005).

## The Economics of Tobacco Cultivation

After the [[josiah-henson|Henson]] family's escape to [[canada|Canada]], the Black settlers faced harsh economic realities. They were raising "nothing but [[tobacco-cultivation|tobacco]]," drawn by its high price. But the cultivation of [[tobacco-cultivation|tobacco]] was "a monopoly in their hands, as no white man understood it, or could compete with them at all" (018-004).

This specialized knowledge proved a curse rather than a blessing. The settlers had nothing but [[tobacco-cultivation|tobacco]] to sell, and the market became glutted. Meanwhile, the price of wheat rose while [[tobacco-cultivation|tobacco]] prices fell, and they "lost all they should have saved, in the profit they gave the trader for his corn and stores" (018-004). [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s analysis of this economic trap demonstrated his sophisticated understanding of market dynamics.

## Henson's Labor and Value

Throughout his [[slavery|enslavement]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s labor was systematically undervalued by those who claimed to own it. He worked as an [[overseer-system|overseer]], managed farming operations, and sold produce in the markets of [[washington|Washington]] and [[georgetown|Georgetown]] (006-002). His labor enriched his enslaver while he received nothing beyond bare subsistence.

Yet [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s skills as a manager and laborer became the foundation of his post-[[slavery|enslavement]] success. When he reached [[canada|Canada]], "my employer soon found that my labor was of more value to him than that of those he was accustomed to hire" (014-003). This recognition of his economic worth — now freely given and freely compensated — was a crucial element of his experience of freedom.

## Economic Independence as Freedom

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s vision of freedom went beyond legal [[freedom|emancipation]]. He understood that true freedom required economic independence. In [[canada|Canada]], he "insisted on the necessity of their raising their own crops, saving their own wages, and securing the profits of their own labor" (018-004).

His advocacy for economic self-sufficiency brought him into conflict with traders who profited from the dependency of Black settlers. He spoke plainly about the exploitation, addressing audiences that sometimes included "the very traders whose inordinate profits" he was criticizing (018-004).

## The Manual-Labor School

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s economic philosophy culminated in his proposal for a manual-labor school at [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]]. He urged "the appropriation of the money to the establishment of a manual-labor school, where our children could be taught those elements of knowledge which are usually the occupations of a grammar-school; and where the boys could be taught, in addition, the practice of some mechanical trade" (018-005).

This vision combined education with practical skills training. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] understood that [[education|literacy]] alone was insufficient — the next generation needed both knowledge and the economic skills to achieve genuine independence. The manual-labor school represented his integrated vision of freedom as both intellectual and economic liberation.

## The Cost of Freedom

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative repeatedly underscores the economic dimension of freedom-seeking. He purchased a decent suit of clothes and a horse to travel and preach while raising funds for his freedom (007-010). The attempt to purchase his legal freedom — and the subsequent [[trust|betrayal]] by his owner's agents — highlighted the cruel irony of a system that forced enslaved people to purchase what was rightfully theirs (011-003).

Money, in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s world, was simultaneously a tool of oppression and a means of liberation. The same economic system that commodified human beings also provided, through diligent labor and prudent management, a path toward autonomy and community development.

In harvest season we had three. Our dress was of tow-cloth; for the children nothing but a shirt; for the older ones a pair of pantaloons or a gown in addition, according to the sex.
