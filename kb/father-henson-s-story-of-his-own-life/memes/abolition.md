## Definition

The abolition movement was the multi-decade campaign to end [[slavery|the institution]] of [[slavery|chattel slavery]], taking distinct forms in the [[england|British Empire]] (culminating in the [[slavery|Slavery]] Abolition Act of 1833) and the [[us-america|United States]] (culminating in the Thirteenth Amendment of 1865). In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative, abolition appears both as the political movement that made his freedom possible in [[canada|Canada]] and as the moral cause to which he dedicated his public life as a speaker and writer.

## Role in the Narrative

Abolition is woven throughout [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s story at multiple levels. In the early chapters, the domestic slave trade serves as the negative backdrop — "the droves of negroes continually passing along under the system of the internal slave trade" (006-010) — against which [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s eventual escape gains its moral force. The middle chapters show [[josiah-henson|Henson]] negotiating his own [[freedom|emancipation]] through intermediaries, with his master Frank agreeing to "negotiate for my freedom" and "bring him to the most favorable terms" (008-004).

After his escape, [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s relationship to abolition transformed from personal quest to public mission. His 1851 trip to [[england|England]] brought him into direct contact with the British abolitionist establishment, including the [[british-and-foreign-anti-slavery-society|British and Foreign Anti-Slavery Society]] and prominent figures such as Lord Shaftesbury and Thomas Sturge. At a public dinner he toasted "success to British emancipation" (022-006), acknowledging Britain's 1833 abolition as the model toward which [[us-america|America]]n reformers should work.

## Henson's Perspective

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] understood abolition not as abstract political philosophy but as the lived difference between [[slavery|bondage]] and [[freedom|liberty]]. His perspective is that of a man who had experienced slavery and freedom directly, giving his testimony unique authority within the abolitionist movement. The narrative frames his personal [[freedom|emancipation]] attempt — through negotiation and purchase — as illustrative of the injustice built into the system: even when an enslaved person earned enough to buy freedom, the master's word was unenforceable without legal abolition.

> We met before a company of English gentlemen, who heard all that my accuser had to say. I simply re-stated to them all the facts I had previously made known. (Chapter XXII, British abolitionist investigation)

His narrative, published in 1858, functioned as an abolitionist document in its own right — providing first-person testimony of slavery's [[kindness|brutality]] at a moment when the [[us-america|America]]n debate had reached its pre-Civil War crisis.

## Historical Context

British abolition proceeded earlier than [[us-america|America]]n: the slave trade was abolished in 1807 and slavery itself in 1833, with full [[freedom|emancipation]] by 1838. This made [[canada|Canada]] — and by extension Britain — a genuine safe haven for fugitives, unlike the Northern [[us-america|United States]] after the [[fugitive-slave-law|Fugitive Slave Act]] of 1850. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s fundraising tours in [[england|England]] leveraged British abolitionist networks to support the [[Dawn Settlement]] and the broader Canadian refuge mission.

[[us-america|America]]n abolition was more fragmented, divided between gradualist approaches (colonization, compensated [[freedom|emancipation]]), immediate abolitionism (William Lloyd Garrison's *Liberator*), and political abolitionism (the Liberty Party, Free Soil Party, and eventually the Republican Party). [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative contributed primarily to the moral and religious wing of the movement, using personal testimony and [[christianity|Christian]] framing to argue against slavery.
