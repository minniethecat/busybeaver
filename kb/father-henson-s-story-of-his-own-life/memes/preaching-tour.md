# Ohio Preaching Tour

[[Josiah Henson]]'s preaching tour through [[Ohio]] and [[Kentucky]] in the summer of 1828 — a period of remarkable independence for an enslaved man, during which he earned money, gained [[trust|confidence]] as a preacher, and first learned about the network of abolitionists and fugitive slaves that would later enable his own [[Escape to Canada]].

## Background

In the summer of 1828, a white [[methodism|Methodist preacher]] visited the neighborhood of [[Amos Riley]]'s [[plantation]] in [[Kentucky]] and befriended [[josiah-henson|Henson]]. Impressed by [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s evident gifts as an exhorter, he encouraged him to seek a license to preach (007-006).

[[josiah-henson|Henson]], who could not read, was self-conscious about his qualifications:

> "No great amount of theological knowledge is requisite for the purpose. If it had been, it is manifest enough that preaching never could have been my vocation; but I am persuaded that, speaking from the heart, and on texts derived from the Bible, I have never failed to interest my hearers." (007-002)

The [[methodism|Methodist preacher]] provided [[josiah-henson|Henson]] with a certificate allowing him to travel between [[Kentucky]] and [[Maryland]] as [[Amos Riley]]'s servant — a travel pass that also served as protection against being seized as a runaway (007-008).

## The Tour

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] purchased a decent suit of clothes and an excellent horse, and set out:

> "By his advice I then purchased a decent suit of clothes and an excellent horse, and travelled from town to town preaching as I went. Everywhere I met with kindness. The contrast between the respect with which I was treated, and the ordinary demeanor towards the colored people, was very grateful to me." (007-010)

He preached in white congregations — a remarkable fact given the racial norms of the 1820s. His earnest, unlettered style resonated with frontier audiences. By the end of the tour, he had earned $275 — a substantial sum for an enslaved man — along with his horse and clothes (008-001).

## Discovering Freedom

While passing along the [[Ohio River]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] repeatedly heard from local residents that "we were no longer slaves, but free men, if we chose to be so" (006-013). This was his first exposure to the idea that [[Canada]] offered genuine freedom — a seed that would bear fruit two years later in the [[Escape to Canada]].

> "During the bright and hopeful days I spent in Ohio, while away on my preaching tour, I had heard much of the course pursued by fugitives from slavery, and became acquainted with a number of benevolent men engaged in helping them." (012-001)

He also heard of the hardships fugitives faced — families broken in the crossing, children lost to freezing rivers — sobering knowledge that shaped his own escape plan.

## Significance

The [[ohio|Ohio]] preaching tour was a watershed in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s life. For the first time, he experienced treatment based on his character and gifts rather than his legal status as property. White congregations listened to him preach; white ministers treated him as a colleague. The $275 he earned represented not merely money but proof of his capacity for economic independence.

Crucially, the tour planted the idea of freedom. Before 1828, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had submitted to slavery as an unchangeable condition. After the tour — having tasted respect, earned wages, and learned of the [[Underground Railroad]] — he began planning the escape that would change everything.
