# Richmond Park

Country estate of [[lord-john-russell|Lord John Russell]] in Richmond Park, [[london|London]]. During [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s second visit to [[england|England]], he was invited to a gathering of [[sabbath|Sabbath]] School teachers at Russell's estate — an occasion that marked the apex of his social recognition among British reformers and abolitionists.

## Location & Geography

Richmond Park is located in [[indiana|Richmond]] upon Thames, approximately eight miles southwest of central [[london|London]]. The park, originally a royal deer park created by Charles I in the 17th century, encompassed the country estate of [[lord-john-russell|Lord John Russell]], a prominent Whig politician who served as Prime Minister of the [[england|United Kingdom]] from 1846 to 1852.

## Role in the Narrative

During his 1851–1852 visit to [[england|England]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] received an invitation to join "a large number of [[sabbath|Sabbath]] School Teachers" for a day at [[lord-john-russell|Lord John Russell]]'s country estate in Richmond Park [022-005]. Russell, a Whig reformer and former Prime Minister, was one of the most influential men in Britain and a patron of educational and religious causes.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s own account captured the splendor of the Prime Minister's estate:

> His magnificent park, filled with deer, of all colors, and from all climes, and sleek hares, which the poet Cowper would have envied, with numberless birds, whose plumage rivalled the rainbow in gorgeous colors, together with the choicest specimens of the finny tribe, sporting in their native element, drew from me the involuntary exclamation: "O, how different the condition of these happy, sportive, joyful, creatures, from what was once my own condition, and what is now the lot of millions of my colored brethren in America!"
>
> (022-005)

The dinner at Richmond Park represented the highest level of social recognition [[josiah-henson|Henson]] achieved during his [[england|England]] tours. From this platform of respect and visibility, he completed work on the publication of his narrative — an edition of two thousand copies "urgently requested... by some of the noblest men and women in [[england|England]]" [023-001].
