# Legal Freedom Purchase

[[Josiah Henson]]'s attempt to legally purchase his own freedom from [[Isaac Riley]] in March 1829 — a bargain struck through the intercession of [[Francis "Master Frank" Riley]], only to be betrayed by a scheme that left [[josiah-henson|Henson]] still enslaved despite having paid $350 in cash.

## Background

After [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s preaching tour through [[Ohio]] — during which he had earned $275 and acquired a horse — he returned to [[Maryland]] in December 1828, proudly displaying his prosperity. The reception was cold. [[Isaac Riley]] examined [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s pass and ordered his wife to lock it in his desk — "I heard the old prison gate clang, and the bolt shoot into the socket once more" (008-002).

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] realized he could not remain. He appealed to Francis [[isaac-riley|Riley]] — "[[francis-master-frank-riley|Master Frank]]" — the brother of [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s wife, whom [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had befriended and protected during Frank's harsh boyhood in the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] household. Frank, now a young man established in [[washington|Washington]], agreed to negotiate [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s freedom.

## The Bargain

> "Accordingly, in a few days he rode over to the house, and had a long conversation with him on the subject of my emancipation. He disclosed to him the facts that I had got some money, and _my pass_..." (008-004)

[[francis-master-frank-riley|Master Frank]] argued that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had "really paid for myself a hundred times over" through his skill and influence on the [[plantation]], and warned [[isaac-riley|Riley]] that if he refused a fair offer, he would "find some day that I had the means to do without his help." On **March 9, 1829**, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] received manumission papers in due form of law, having paid $350 in cash (including his horse) and signed a note for the remaining $100 — a total of $450 (008-004 through 008-005).

## The Betrayal

As [[josiah-henson|Henson]] prepared to return to [[Kentucky]], [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] approached him with an offer of "friendly advice." He warned that showing the manumission certificate on the road would invite slave-traders to tear it up and kidnap him. Instead, [[isaac-riley|Riley]] proposed to seal the papers under cover, addressed to his brother [[Amos Riley]] in [[kentucky|Kentucky]]:

> "Let me enclose your papers for you under cover to my brother. Nobody will dare to break a seal, for that is a state-prison matter; and when you arrive in Kentucky you will have it with you all safe and sound." (008-005)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]], [[trust]]ing and relieved, agreed. [[isaac-riley|Riley]] sealed the precious papers in multiple wrappers, addressed the envelope to his brother Amos, and handed them to [[josiah-henson|Henson]] — who delivered his own [[slavery|enslavement]] documents unknowingly into the hands of the man who would use them against him.

## Discovery

> "But how are you going to raise enough to pay the remainder of the thousand dollars?" "What thousand dollars?" "The thousand dollars you were to give for your freedom." (008-007)

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] arrived in [[kentucky|Kentucky]], his wife [[Charlotte Henson|Charlotte]] revealed the truth: letters had arrived from [[maryland|Maryland]] stating that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had paid $350 down and still owed $650 more before he would receive his free papers. [[Amos Riley]] greeted [[josiah-henson|Henson]] the next morning: "Well, boy, how's your master? Isaac says you want to be free. Want to be free, eh! I think your master treats you pretty hard, though. Six hundred and fifty dollars don't come so easy in old Kentuck" (008-008).

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] had been doubly cheated: the sealed papers — which he dared not open — contained no manumission at all, or if they did, Amos [[isaac-riley|Riley]] was instructed to conceal them until [[josiah-henson|Henson]] paid a phantom balance.

> "Indignation is a faint word to express my deep sense of such villainy. I was alternately beside myself with rage, and paralyzed with despair." (008-007)

## Aftermath

The [[trust|betrayal]] had profound consequences. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s "dream of bliss was over." He could neither contact [[francis-master-frank-riley|Master Frank]] (a thousand miles away, and he could not write) nor appeal to any magistrate (every literate man nearby was a slaveholder who might seize him). His wife hid the sealed papers rather than surrender them to Amos [[isaac-riley|Riley]] (008-007).

The incident destroyed [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s remaining faith in the legal path to freedom. Within two years, he would abandon all hope of purchasing his [[freedom|liberty]] and instead plan the dramatic escape that carried his family to [[Canada]] (012-001). The episode also taught [[josiah-henson|Henson]] a lasting lesson about the legal system of slavery: even a signed contract, witnessed by a friendly white intermediary, was worthless when the power to enforce it lay entirely in the hands of the enslaver.
