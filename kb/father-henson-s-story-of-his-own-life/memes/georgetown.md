# Georgetown

Georgetown was the settlement in [[montgomery-county|Montgomery County]], [[Maryland]], a few miles from the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] [[plantation]], where [[Josiah Henson]] first heard a [[christianity|Christian]] sermon from the baker-preacher John McKenny — the man who introduced him to [[christianity|Christianity]] — and where, together with [[Washington]], he regularly sold the produce of his master's farm as [[plantation]] superintendent.

## Location and Geography

Georgetown was situated in [[montgomery-county|Montgomery County]], [[maryland|Maryland]], a few miles from the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] [[plantation]] and within trading distance of the federal capital at [[washington|Washington]]. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] identifies it alongside [[washington|Washington]] as one of the "neighboring markets" where he sold the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] farm's produce, indicating it was a town of sufficient size to sustain commercial activity (006-002). In the early nineteenth century, this Georgetown (not to be confused with the more famous Georgetown in the District of Columbia, which was a separate municipality until 1871) was a rural crossroads settlement typical of [[maryland|Maryland]]'s [[plantation]] economy.

## Role in the Narrative

Georgetown is inseparable in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative from the figure of John McKenny, the baker and lay preacher who lived there. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] writes with deep gratitude: "My heart exults with gratitude when I mention the name of a good man who first taught me the blessedness of religion. His name was John McKenny. He lived at Georgetown, a few miles only from [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s [[plantation]]; his business was that of a baker, and his character was that of an upright, benevolent [[christianity|Christian]]" (004-001). McKenny was notable for "his detestation of slavery, and his resolute avoidance of the employment of slave labor in his business" — he "would not even hire a slave, the price of whose toil must be paid to his master, but contented himself with the work of his own hands" (004-001).

It was to hear McKenny preach that [[josiah-henson|Henson]], at his mother's urging, asked his master for permission to attend a [[sabbath|Sunday]] meeting three or four miles from the [[plantation]]. Though [[josiah-henson|Henson]] "had so often been beaten for making such a request," his master relented on this occasion, and [[josiah-henson|Henson]] "hurried off, pleased with the opportunity" (004-001). This was the first sermon [[josiah-henson|Henson]] ever heard — he was then eighteen years old — and it proved to be the beginning of his [[Conversion Experience]] and lifelong Methodist faith. The biblical text that McKenny preached from was Hebrews 2:9, and its message of Christ's sacrificial love moved [[josiah-henson|Henson]] so deeply that on the way home "I became so excited that I turned aside from the road into the woods, and [[prayer|pray]]ed to God for light and for aid" (004-003).

Later, as superintendent of the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] [[plantation]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s "occupations being to superintend the farming operations, and to sell the produce in the neighboring markets of [[washington|Washington]] and Georgetown" made the town a routine part of his working life (006-002). The dual reference to [[washington|Washington]] and Georgetown as paired markets suggests that both were within a day's travel of the [[plantation]] and formed the commercial circuit within which [[josiah-henson|Henson]] moved as a [[trust]]ed enslaved [[overseer-system|overseer]].

## Historical Context

Georgetown in [[montgomery-county|Montgomery County]], [[maryland|Maryland]], was a small rural settlement in a county dominated by [[plantation]] agriculture. In the early 1800s, [[montgomery-county|Montgomery County]]'s economy was based on [[tobacco-cultivation|tobacco]] and wheat production, worked by enslaved labor. The county lay just north of the District of Columbia, and its proximity to the capital meant that its enslaved population lived at the intersection of the [[plantation]] South and the urban, free-labor North. John McKenny's principled refusal to use slave labor, which [[josiah-henson|Henson]] describes as "almost singular abstinence from what no one about him thought wrong," places Georgetown within a small but significant network of early abolitionist sentiment in the upper South (004-001). Religious gatherings like the one [[josiah-henson|Henson]] attended were often the only settings in which enslaved people could encounter [[abolition|anti-slavery]] ideas, and itinerant or lay preachers like McKenny played a crucial role in spreading both [[christianity|Christianity]] and, implicitly, the notion of human equality before God.

## Key Passages

> "My heart exults with gratitude when I mention the name of a good man who first taught me the blessedness of religion. His name was John McKenny. He lived at Georgetown, a few miles only from Riley's plantation; his business was that of a baker, and his character was that of an upright, benevolent Christian. He was noted especially for his detestation of slavery, and his resolute avoidance of the employment of slave labor in his business." (004-001)

> "Things remained in this condition for a considerable period; my occupations being to superintend the farming operations, and to sell the produce in the neighboring markets of Washington and Georgetown. Many respectable people, yet living there, may possibly have some recollection of 'Siah,' or 'Sie,' (as they used to call me,) as their market-man." (006-002)
