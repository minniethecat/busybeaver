# Canada

Canada was the destination of [[Josiah Henson]]'s [[Escape to Canada|escape from slavery]] in 1830, and the country where he established the [[Dawn Settlement]], a community for fugitive slaves near Dresden, Ontario. For the thousands of [[Underground Railroad]] refugees, Canada represented "the only sure refuge from pursuit" (012-001).

## Location & Geography

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] crossed into Canada via the Niagara River at [[buffalo|Buffalo]], [[new-york|New York]], arriving on the morning of October 28, 1830 (013-012). The [[Dawn Settlement]] was established near Dresden, in what is now Ontario, on land covered with "a beautiful forest of noble trees" that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and other settlers cleared through lumbering operations (019-001).

Southwestern Ontario — then called Upper Canada — was the primary terminus for the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]]'s eastern routes. Communities like the [[Dawn Settlement]], the Elgin Settlement (Buxton), and others in Essex and Kent counties formed a network of Black Canadian settlements that persisted through the 19th century.

## As a Freedom Destination

Canada's significance in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative is as the physical realization of [[freedom]]. During his preaching tour in [[ohio|Ohio]], he learned that Canada was "often spoken of as the only sure refuge from pursuit" and that "the unknown land of the North" had led "thousands of my poor, hunted brethren to freedom and security" (012-001).

> "It was the 28th of October, 1830, in the morning, when my feet first touched the soil of a land of freedom. I was then about forty-one years of age, having lived more than forty years in bondage. I can truly say I know what slavery is, and what freedom is." (013-012)

The abolition of [[slavery]] throughout the [[england|British Empire]] in 1834 made all of British North [[us-america|America]] free soil — a legal status that distinguished Canada from the Northern [[us-america|United States]], where the [[fugitive-slave-law|Fugitive Slave Act]] of 1850 made even free states dangerous for escaped slaves.

## Economic Opportunity

Upon reaching Canada, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] found that his labor was valued differently than under slavery. His employer soon found that "my labor was of more value to him than that of those who worked for mere wages" (014-003). Within a few years, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had improved his condition sufficiently to begin the work of establishing a settlement for fellow fugitives.

## Role in the Narrative

Canada functions in the narrative as the antithesis of the slave states. Where [[Maryland]] and [[Kentucky]] are associated with [[slavery|bondage]], violence, and the constant threat of family separation, Canada represents safety, dignity, and the ability to earn wages, own property, and educate children. This stark contrast structures the entire arc of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s story.

> "I had now been in Canada nearly three years, and had made some progress in the world. I had built a house, had a little stock of cattle, and was getting on quite comfortably. I felt that I was a man among men." (016-001)

## Historical Context

Following the [[fugitive-slave-law|Fugitive Slave Act]] of 1850, the flow of freedom seekers to Canada increased dramatically. An estimated 30,000 to 40,000 African [[us-america|America]]ns reached Canada via the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]]. Communities like the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] provided not just refuge but institutions — schools, churches, businesses — that enabled lasting community formation.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s role as a leader and fundraiser made him one of the most prominent figures in Black Canadian history. His multiple trips to [[england|England]], including attendance at the 1851 [[london|Great Exhibition]], generated international support for the Canadian refugee communities.
