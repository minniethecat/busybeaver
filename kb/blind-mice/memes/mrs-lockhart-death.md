## Overview

The death of [[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]] is a decisive turning point in the novel. Her passing removes a formidable matriarchal presence from the lives of [[lucy-winter|Lucy Winter]] and [[nannie-merwent|Nannie Merwent]], while simultaneously creating a power vacuum that [[miss-brennan|Cousin Minnie Sheldon]] moves to fill.

## Narrative Context

Mrs. Lockhart had been the dominant figure in the Lockhart family for decades. She presided over Nannie's marriage to [[arthur-merwent|Arthur Merwent]], managed the family's social standing in Russellville, and exercised a controlling influence over Lucy's marriage and household. Her death after "four years of this regime" [009-026] comes as a shock that reconfigures the family's power structure.

> Mrs. Lockhart died, and Nannie was left in "Cousin Minnie's" care [009-026]

The event is narrated economically — the death itself occurs offstage — but its consequences ripple through every subsequent chapter of the novel.

## Key Participants

- **[[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]]** — The deceased matriarch, whose legacy and example continue to shape events after her death
- **[[nannie-merwent|Nannie Merwent]]** — Left without her protector and thrust into Cousin Minnie's control
- **[[lucy-winter|Lucy Winter]]** — Mrs. Lockhart's daughter, who must navigate her mother's absence and her own complicated grief
- **[[miss-brennan|Cousin Minnie Sheldon]]** — Steps into the authority void, claiming guardianship over Nannie

## Aftermath

The death precipitates [[nannie-cousin-minnie-confrontation|Nannie's confrontation with Cousin Minnie]], as Nannie resists being treated as property to be managed. For Lucy, her mother's death removes a source of constant pressure on her marriage but also severs a defining relationship. The event marks the transition from the older generation's control to the younger generation's struggle for autonomy.

> Abruptly one day not long after Mrs. Lockhart's death. [009-027]
