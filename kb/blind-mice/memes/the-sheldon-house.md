# The Sheldon House

## Overview

The Sheldon house — referred to throughout the novel as "the big house" — is the former Lockhart ancestral mansion in Russellville, lost to Nannie's detested cousin Minnie Sheldon when [[arthur-merwent|Arthur Merwent]] could not afford to purchase it after Mr. Lockhart's financial ruin. More symbol than setting, the house appears in the narrative primarily through memory and resentment: it is the home [[nannie-merwent]] was raised to believe was her birthright, the monument to a class identity that collapsed before she could inherit it, and the wound that never heals.

> Nannie's bitterness was accentuated by the fact that Arthur could not afford to buy the Lockhart residence which had to be sold to clear her own father's estate, and she was forced to see the property pass into the hands of her cousin, Minnie Sheldon, whom she detested.
> (005-011)

## The Lockhart Ancestral Home

### Description in Memory

The Lockhart mansion appears in the novel's backstory as a vision of lost grandeur. Nannie's earliest memories center on "a large, square, white house with a front porch supported by Corinthian pillars, a long driveway lined with great trees on either side, and much display of lavish hospitality" (004-002). It was a house designed for performance — the Corinthian pillars, the tree-lined drive, the lavish entertaining — and it taught Nannie that a house is not a home but a stage.

The Lockharts "had never accomplished anything in particular except to be aristocratic" (004-001), and the house was the material proof of that aristocracy. When Mr. Lockhart's financial mismanagement brought the whole edifice down, the house passed to Cousin Minnie — a transfer that Nannie experienced not as an economic transaction but as a theft of identity.

### Nannie's Dependence

After Mrs. Lockhart's death, Nannie was left in the Sheldon house as a dependent:

> After four years of this regime Mrs. Lockhart died, and Nannie was left in "Cousin Minnie's" house with no one upon whom she could persuade herself she had the semblance of a claim.
> (009-026)

Living as a poor relation in the house she once expected to own, Nannie's bitterness curdled into the comprehensive resentment that she would eventually bring to [[the-winter-house|the Winter household]]. The Sheldon house, in this sense, is the origin point of Nannie's destructive trajectory: it is where she learned that class identity, once lost, cannot be recovered through charm or performance alone.

## Symbolic Function

| Dimension | The Sheldon House | The Winter House |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| Architecture | Grand: Corinthian pillars, long driveway | Modest: "pretty yard," suburban home |
| Class association | Declined plantation aristocracy | Professional middle class |
| Owner's relation to it | Lost birthright (Nannie) | Earned domesticity (Lucy) |
| Social function | Display and performance | Family and warmth |
| Narrative role | Symbol of what Nannie lost | Symbol of what Nannie destroys |

The contrast between the two houses is the novel's argument about class and domesticity. The Sheldon house — grand, performative, aristocratic — produces Nannie: a woman who values display over substance and who cannot conceive of a home as anything but a stage. The Winter house — modest, warm, middle-class — produces Lucy: a woman who values substance over display and whose domestic authority is grounded in care rather than performance. When Nannie enters the Winter house, she brings the values of the Sheldon house with her, and the collision is catastrophic.

## Role in the Novel

The Sheldon house appears only in flashback and in characters' memories, but its influence is pervasive:

1. **Nannie's psychology**: The loss of the house is the originating trauma of Nannie's adulthood — the proof that the world has denied her what she was promised.
2. **Class resentment**: Nannie's inability to accept middle-class domesticity is rooted in her memory of the "big house" and what it represented.
3. **The Lockhart-Merwent marriage**: Arthur's failure to buy back the house (005-011) is one of the grievances Nannie never forgives.
4. **Lucy's inheritance**: As Nannie's daughter, Lucy inherits the shadow of the lost house without its substance — a burden she carries into her own domestic life.
