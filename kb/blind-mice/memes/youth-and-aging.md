## Overview

Youth in *Blind Mice* is a currency whose value everyone recognizes and whose depletion everyone fears. John Winter's "boyish" face and manner — his way of "removing his hat and running his fingers through his hair" [001-002] — are presented as natural endowments, signs of an unforced vitality. But for the novel's women, youth is a discipline: Nannie Merwent appears in "a traveling costume of a cut and shade too youthful for her" [002-030], fighting a rearguard action against time with powder, rouge, and pencil. The novel tracks the different ways that men and women experience the passage of time, and the different stakes that attach to looking — and being — young.

## Role in the Novel

The novel's treatment of youth and aging is gendered from the start. Men's youth is described as an attribute of character: John is "boyish" in manner, Jim is "young" in his ambitions. These descriptions carry positive valence — youth is energy, promise, potential. For women, by contrast, youth is a performance and a vulnerability. **Nannie Merwent's** "conscientiously powdered, rouged, and penciled" face [002-030] is a battlefield on which she fights a losing war. Her advice to Lucy — that rest will make "a difference in your looks" [003-062] — frames aging as a problem of maintenance, a duty the conscientious woman must not neglect.

**Lucy Winter** occupies an ambiguous position in this economy. Her "nondescript shade of brown" hair and "generous figure" [001-018] place her outside the regime of performed youthfulness that Nannie represents. She is neither aging badly (like Nannie) nor conspicuously youthful (like the "pretty girl" on the train [001-005]). Her relationship to time is measured in work rather than looks — she ages, if she ages at all, from the labor of running a household, not from the failure of cosmetics.

The novel's older generation — **Mrs. Lockhart**, "rapidly growing deaf" [005-029] — represents aging as social diminishment. The grandmother who once ruled the Lockhart social world is reduced to a figure whose hearing loss makes her increasingly peripheral, her authority eroded by the very passage of time she once commanded.

**Jim Sprague's** observation that "parents are in secret competition with their children" [012-034] captures the generational dimension of the youth/aging dynamic — the sense that the rising generation's youth is purchased at the expense of the elders' decline.

## Key Passages

> "He had a somewhat boisterous and affectionate manner, and a rather loud voice. When excited or interested he had a boyish way of removing his hat and running his fingers through his hair." [001-002] — John's youth as natural, unperformed vitality.

> "She was attired in a faultless traveling costume of a cut and shade too youthful for her, and she was conscientiously powdered, rouged, and penciled." [002-030] — Nannie's youth as performance, the adverb "conscientiously" converting beauty into discipline.

> "You have no idea what a difference it would make in your looks, Lucy. You ought to make that a rule which nothing should interfere with." [003-062] — Aging as a problem of inadequate maintenance; rest as cosmetic strategy.

> "Parents being in secret competition with their children." [012-034] — The generational zero-sum game: youth and age as rival claimants on vitality.

## Thematic Connections

Youth and aging is inseparable from **[[appearance-and-looks]]**, for the visible signs of age are the primary site at which the novel's women wage their campaign against time. It connects to **[[gender-roles]]**, as the burden of maintaining youthfulness falls disproportionately on women — men may age into distinction, but women age into invisibility. It intersects with **[[marriage]]**, where the fear of losing youthful attractiveness shadows the security of the marital bond. And it relates to **[[pride-and-vanity]]**, for Nannie's refusal to dress her age is of a piece with her larger refusal to accept the social position to which her circumstances have assigned her.
