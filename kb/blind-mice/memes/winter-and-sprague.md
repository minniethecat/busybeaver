# Winter & Sprague

## Overview

Winter & Sprague, the architecture firm jointly operated by [[john-winter|John Winter]] and [[james-sprague|Jim Sprague]], is the commercial institution at the center of _Blind Mice_'s exploration of art, commerce, and male friendship. Though the firm's physical offices appear only occasionally in the narrative, its presence is pervasive: John's dissatisfaction with commercial architecture, the financial realities that shape the Winter household, and the partnership's ultimate dissolution are all mediated through this institution.

The firm embodies the novel's central economic tension — the uneasy alliance between John's artistic idealism and Jim's commercial pragmatism — and its trajectory from successful partnership to Jim's generous withdrawal mirrors the novel's larger arc from domestic harmony to fracture.

## Structure and Dynamics

### Founding

The firm was apparently established before the novel's opening. John Winter is the creative talent — an architect who "draws beautiful plans for pretty houses" — while Jim Sprague is the business engine: the partner who finds clients, negotiates contracts, and ensures that the artistic vision translates into economic survival. The partnership's division of labor reflects the gendered economy of the novel: the artistic (associated with feeling, beauty, Europe) and the commercial (associated with practicality, profit, America) are split between two men who need each other but whose values are fundamentally misaligned.

### The Unequal Partnership

The economic structure of the firm — a fifty-fifty split despite John doing the creative work — is a recurring source of tension. [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] sharpens this observation with characteristic precision:

> "I don't believe in women interfering in business, but if you went into something for yourself, don't you think you could make more? As it is, you work yourself nearly to death, and Mr. Sprague gets half the profits."
> (018-157)

Nannie's observation is factually accurate but strategically deployed — another wedge driven between John and the people who genuinely care for him. Yet the question she raises is real: is the partnership genuinely equitable, or does Jim benefit disproportionately from John's artistic labor?

### The Resolution

The novel's resolution of the partnership question is characteristically complex. Jim, having arranged to leave Chicago for another city, offers John his share of the business:

> "He has offered me his share of the business — at a low price — in fact almost nothing — and on liberal terms: time payments. He has an offer from another firm, but he wants me to have it if I can manage it."
> (036-015)

This gesture transforms the commercial relationship into something else entirely — an act of friendship that transcends the economic logic that has governed the partnership. Jim's offer is, in effect, a gift: he could sell to another firm for more money, but he chooses to give John control of the business they built together. The novel's final economic transaction is thus not a transaction at all but a renunciation — Jim's acknowledgment that some things matter more than money.

## The Office as Setting

The firm's physical offices serve as a significant secondary setting. It is where John works, where Jim manages, and — in the novel's final scene — where [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] comes to visit:

> Lucy rose again and, walking rather unsteadily to Jim's desk, laid her hands softly upon it. Then she looked across the rooftops.
> (036-022)

Lucy's gesture at Jim's empty desk is one of the novel's most powerful images — a farewell not only to Jim but to an entire configuration of work, friendship, and domestic life that the firm once sustained.

## Thematic Dimensions

| Theme | Expression in Winter & Sprague |
|-------|-------------------------------|
| Art vs. Commerce | John's idealism versus Jim's pragmatism; the architect as artist in a commercial market |
| Male Friendship | The partnership is also a friendship, tested by economic inequality and Nannie's manipulations |
| Class and Profession | The firm represents professional middle-class respectability — a step above manual labor, a step below inherited wealth |
| Gift and Sacrifice | Jim's final offer reframes the commercial relationship as an act of personal generosity |
| Domestic Economy | The firm's fortunes directly determine the Winter household's circumstances |
