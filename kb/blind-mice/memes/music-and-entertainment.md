## Overview

Music and entertainment form the social soundtrack of _Blind Mice_. From the domestic piano that [[john-winter|John Winter]] plays in the opening chapter to the theatre parties that become flashpoints of marital tension between John and [[lucy-winter|Lucy]], the novel's scenes of entertainment are never merely decorative. Each performance, each evening out, each song at the piano registers the state of the household's relationships and the economic pressures that shape them.

> John came in from the living room, where he had been playing "The Evening Star" from Tannhäuser.
> (001-033)

## The Piano as Domestic Barometer

The Winter household's piano is both a prized possession and a site of contested meaning. John and Lucy "got the piano over a year ago, but Lucy never plays it" (003-097) — the instrument belongs to John's aesthetic world, not Lucy's practical one. When [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] arrives, she immediately recognizes the piano's strategic value as a point of connection with John, praising his playing and using musical appreciation as a bond that excludes Lucy.

In the novel's later chapters, John's piano playing takes on a melancholy cast. He "seated himself at the piano and attempted to play the accompaniment to 'Ouvrez tes yeux'" (034-012) — a gesture of reaching for beauty in a home that has lost its harmony. The piano, once a symbol of domestic contentment, becomes an instrument of solitary consolation.

## The Theatre Party Incident

The novel's most significant entertainment episode occurs when John purchases box seats for the theatre without consulting Lucy. Her response is sharp and revealing:

> "We've spent twice as much on theatres already this month as we ought to in half a year!"
> (017-032)

The theatre party crystallizes the economic dynamic that Nannie has exacerbated. John's purchase of box seats — an extravagant gesture that Nannie has encouraged — represents the collision between his desire for social display and Lucy's insistence on fiscal responsibility. When John dismisses Lucy's protest as "her monthly fret over money matters" (018-129), the entertainment has ceased to be entertainment and become a proxy battle for control of the household's values.

## Music and Social Class

The novel's treatment of music is subtly class-inflected. John's repertoire — Wagner, French art songs — signals his aspirations to cultured refinement. Nannie's appreciation of music is performative: she values it as a marker of cultivation rather than for its intrinsic beauty. Lucy's non-participation in the household's musical life is not a failure of taste but a different relationship to the arts — one grounded in practical domesticity rather than aesthetic display.

The contrast illuminates one of the novel's recurring concerns: the difference between genuine aesthetic sensibility and the performance of refinement. John has the former but is susceptible to the latter; Nannie masters the latter without possessing the former; and Lucy, who is immune to both, is punished for it.

## Entertainment as Escape and Entrapment

Throughout the novel, entertainment functions as a temporary escape from domestic tensions that ultimately deepens them. The theatre party that John arranges leads to a marital argument. The dinner parties and social calls that fill the latter chapters mark John's progressive estrangement from his home. Entertainment, in the novel's vision, is not a solution to domestic discord but one of its symptoms — a way of avoiding the conversations that might actually repair what is broken.
