## Overview

The wedding of [[lucy-winter|Lucy Lockhart]] to [[john-winter|John Winter]] marks the founding of the Winter family at the center of the novel. Their marriage establishes the domestic world of Rosedene and creates the partnership that defines the novel's emotional and economic stakes. The wedding, recalled in flashback, represents a decisive break from the Lockhart family's expectations.

## Narrative Context

Lucy Lockhart grew up under the formidable influence of her mother, [[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]], whose social ambitions and domineering personality shaped the Lockhart household. John Winter, a young architect with modest means, represented a departure from the kind of match Mrs. Lockhart would have preferred for her daughter.

> John and Lucy were married and it was a quiet affair [013-035]

The partnership of Winter & Sprague was formed shortly before the wedding [013-035], establishing the professional foundation for the young couple's future at Rosedene.

## Key Participants

- **[[lucy-winter|Lucy Lockhart Winter]]** — The bride, breaking from her mother's social expectations to marry for love
- **[[john-winter|John Winter]]** — The groom, a young architect beginning his career with [[winter-and-sprague|Winter & Sprague]]
- **[[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]]** — Lucy's mother, whose approval of the match was grudging at best
- **[[nannie-merwent|Nannie Merwent]]** — Present at the wedding, her own recent marriage to Arthur Merwent provides a parallel and contrast

## Aftermath

The wedding establishes the Winter household at Rosedene, where Lucy and John begin their married life. The tensions between Lucy and Mrs. Lockhart that surfaced around the wedding continue to ripple through the narrative, manifesting in Mrs. Lockhart's intrusive visits and her disapproval of the couple's choices. The birth of [[dimmie-winter|Dimmie Winter]] and the purchase of the Rosedene home represent the fruition of the life John and Lucy envisioned at their wedding.
