## Overview

Manners serve as a visible social currency in *Blind Mice* — a system of signs by which characters signal their class position, judge the worth of others, and police the boundaries of acceptable behavior. The novel is attentive to the smallest details of social performance: how one wears a hat, when one rises to offer a seat, what one says at a dinner party, how one receives a guest. C. Kay Scott depicts manners not as empty forms but as a language charged with real social consequences, one that the upwardly mobile must learn to speak fluently and whose violations carry genuine penalties.

## Role in the Novel

The novel's attention to manners is established from its opening scene, where John Winter's "boisterous and affectionate manner" is contrasted with Jim Sprague's "fastidiously selected clothing" and "exaggeratedly quiet taste" [001-003]. Each man's demeanor is a social statement: John's warmth signals democratic ease, Jim's precision signals aspirational seriousness.

**Nannie Merwent** is the novel's most acute student and critic of manners. She judges women by how they wear their hats — "I could never like a woman who wore her hat like that" [014-116] — and Miss Powell earns her approval because "the way she put her hat on had real dash to it." For Nannie, the minutiae of dress and deportment are moral indices, and she is quick to pronounce on what "a woman's duty" requires in matters of appearance [014-116]. Her own manners, however, are shown to be performative rather than genuine: she adopts "a pertness and flippancy of speech that was described as 'smart'" and "assumed a domineering manner toward the servants" [005-014].

**Mrs. Lockhart** embodies an older generation's investment in manners as class boundary. Her social world is governed by rigid protocols of calling, receiving, and marrying appropriately. She sees her granddaughter's future in terms of social positioning — "If you marry your daughter to the heir of Judge Dodd it isn't too late to get back where you belong" [006-039].

For **Lucy Winter**, manners are a burden rather than a ladder. Her indifferent housekeeping and unadorned appearance are read by Nannie as failures of social duty, yet Lucy's simplicity also represents a quiet resistance to the performance culture her mother embodies.

## Key Passages

> "He wore fastidiously selected clothing that displayed an exaggeratedly quiet taste; the cravat chosen in stripes too minute for good general effect, and the cuffs, shoes, and other details of attire immaculate and suggestive of extreme thought for appearance." [001-003] — Scott's meticulous description of Jim Sprague's self-presentation as a study in aspirational manners.

> "She adopted a pertness and flippancy of speech that was described as 'smart' and assumed a domineering manner toward the servants." [005-014] — Nannie's social performance exposed as artificial construction.

> "I could never like a woman who wore her hat like that. Now that Miss Powell was the smartest one present at your tea. The way she put her hat on had real dash to it." [014-116] — Manners reduced to millinery judgment; Nannie's characteristic conflation of style with character.

> "I think that it's a woman's duty to look as well as she can." [014-116] — The language of moral obligation applied to personal grooming.

> "You really must have a sideboard in your dining room, Lucy. It looks so bare." [003-029] — Nannie's running critique of Lucy's housekeeping as a failure of social performance.

## Thematic Connections

The discourse of manners in *Blind Mice* is inseparable from **[[money-and-class]]** — manners are the visible language through which class anxiety speaks. Nannie's obsession with hats, sideboards, and social advancement is fundamentally an anxiety about class position. The theme also connects to **[[appearance-and-looks]]**, as physical appearance is the primary medium through which manners are performed and judged. It intersects with **[[marriage]]**, where the rituals of courtship and the protocols of married life are governed by unspoken rules of propriety. And it stands in tension with the novel's interest in **[[gender-roles]]**, for the burden of manners falls disproportionately on women — it is women's hats, women's dresses, women's sideboards, and women's "duty" to look well that preoccupy the novel's social criticism.
