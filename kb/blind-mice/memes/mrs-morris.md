## Overview

Mrs. Morris is a minor figure in the [[the-winter-house|Winter household]]'s social world, appearing in [[john-winter|John Winter]]'s party planning and later at the [[food-and-dining|dinner]] itself. Her brief remark to [[lucy-winter]] — "It's a good thing to get out occasionally" [035-048] — captures the quiet domestic confinement of married [[gender-roles|women]] in the novel's world and the social rituals that provide their sanctioned escapes.

## Biography

Mrs. Morris first appears as a name on [[john-winter]]'s [[food-and-dining|dinner]]-party guest list. [[john-winter|John]] runs through the names: "Let's see — There's [[miss-lyle]], and Mrs. Morris, too" [034-059]. Unlike [[miss-lyle|Miss Lyle]], who presents a logistical challenge (she needs "a man" paired with her), Mrs. Morris comes as p[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] of a unit: "We must invite Mrs. Morris's husband, too," [[john-winter|John]] adds [034-059], confirming her married status.

At the [[food-and-dining|dinner]] party, Mrs. Morris approaches [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] and exclaims, "It's a good thing to get out occasionally" [035-048]. The remark is casual but revealing — a married woman's acknowledgment that domestic life can be confining, and that social events provide a necessary release. [[lucy-winter|Lucy]], whose own domestic confinement is a central theme of the novel's later chapters, would understand the sentiment.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Morris is a minor figure whose single spoken line carries disproportionate weight. "It's a good thing to get out occasionally" [035-048] is the kind of remark that passes unnoticed in polite conversation — a pleasantry, a shared recognition of the evening's enjoyments. But in the context of a novel so preoccupied with the constraints of domestic life for [[gender-roles|women]], the line resonates.

Mrs. Morris is a married woman — her husband must be invited alongside her — and her remark implies that "getting out" is not a given but an occasion, something that requires planning and justification. The word "occasionally" is key: it suggests that most of her life is spent at home, and that the [[food-and-dining|dinner]] party represents a temporary release from domestic routine.

She has no described [[appearance-and-looks|appearance]], no backstory, and no role in the plot beyond this single line. But like several of the novel's background figures, she serves as a mirror for [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s own condition — a married woman for whom social events are the only sanctioned escape from the house that is also, in some measure, a c[[youth-and-aging|age]].
