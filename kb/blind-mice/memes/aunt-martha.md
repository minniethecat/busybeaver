## Overview

Aunt Martha is the elderly Black nurse who tends to [[lucy-winter]] during her childhood on the Merwent plantation. Described as "the old negress" in the novel's period langu[[youth-and-aging|age]] [004-006], she appears briefly but poignantly in the narrative: a figure of loyalty and endurance who stays with the family through declining fortunes, partly from "habit of allegiance" and partly from "real devotion to little [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]" [005-013]. Her presence in the novel, though limited, registers the racial and social hierarchies of the post-Reconstruction South that form the background of the Merwent family's world.

## Biography

Aunt Martha first appears in the novel's account of [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s childhood. She serves as the child's nurse, enduring rough treatment: the young [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] "stuck pins into the arms of Aunt Martha, her nurse, while the old negress was [[appearance-and-looks|dress]]ing her" [004-006]. This vignette, narrated without explicit commentary, reveals both the casual cruelty of the plantation [[domestic-life|household]] and Aunt Martha's stoic endurance.

When the Merwent family's fortunes decline, the [[domestic-life|household]] is forced to economize. "[[arthur-merwent|Arthur]]'s law practice did not increase and, as there were no more expectations from his father's estate, it was decided to discharge Aunt Martha" [005-018]. Yet despite the discharge, Aunt Martha does not entirely disappear: "[[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] continually quarreled with the old negress but Aunt Martha stayed on, partly from a habit of allegiance to the family and partly from real devotion to little [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]" [005-013].

The novel notes that the young [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] "preferred Aunt Martha and even [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]]" to her [[motherhood|mother]] [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]], as [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] "often held his daughter during the evenings and sang lullabies to her" [005-014]. This preference for the nurse and father over the [[motherhood|mother]] subtly critiques [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s [[motherhood|maternal]] shortcomings.

Aunt Martha reappears briefly during the preparations for [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s departure from Russellville. She is "called in to help with the arrangements for [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s departure" [007-071], and "went home at the same time" [007-072] — a final, fleeting [[appearance-and-looks|appearance]] that closes her connection to the family's story.

## Relationships

**[[lucy-winter]]** — The child she nurses and for whom she feels "real devotion" [005-013]. [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] prefers Aunt Martha's company to her [[motherhood|mother]]'s, suggesting the emotional bond between nurse and child.

**[[nannie-merwent]]** — A tense relationship marked by [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s childhood cruelty ("stuck pins into the arms of Aunt Martha" [004-006]) and adult quarrels [005-013]. Yet Aunt Martha's continued presence in the [[domestic-life|household]] reflects a bond that transcends these conflicts.

**[[arthur-merwent]]** — Her employer, who, together with Aunt Martha, forms the emotional foundation of [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s childhood — the father who sings lullabies and the nurse who offers devotion [005-014].

## Character Analysis

Aunt Martha is one of the novel's few Black characters, and her treatment reflects the racial conventions and limitations of early twentieth-century American domestic fiction. She appears only through the perspective of the white characters she serves, described with the period's unselfconscious racial langu[[youth-and-aging|age]] ("the old negress"), and granted no dialogue or interiority in the text.

Within these constraints, however, Aunt Martha emerges as a figure of quiet dignity. Her decision to stay with the family after her discharge — "from a habit of allegiance" and "real devotion to little [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]" [005-013] — suggests a loyalty that transcends the economic transaction of employment. The narrative implicitly contrasts her steadfastness with [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s self-absorption: while [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] quarrels and schemes, Aunt Martha simply endures and cares.

The detail that [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] "preferred Aunt Martha and even [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]]" [005-014] is telling. In the novel's emotional economy, the nurse's devotion places her alongside the father as a source of genuine nurture — a quiet rebuke to [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s inadequacies as a [[motherhood|mother]]. Aunt Martha's final [[appearance-and-looks|appearance]], "called in to help" with [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s departure [007-071] and then going home, closes her arc with the same unobtrusive service that defines her role.
