## Overview

The domestic world of *Blind Mice* is built as much on what is concealed as on what is spoken. Secrets run through the novel at every level: Nannie Merwent harbors "secret" hopes of inheritance that color her marital calculations [005-009]; parents are discovered to be "in secret competition with their children" [012-034]; Lucy conceals her agitation behind composed silence [002-008]; and the central crisis of the Winter marriage unfolds through what John does not say and Lucy does not ask. C. Kay Scott treats secrecy not as melodramatic intrigue but as the ordinary medium of domestic life — the things people cannot or will not say to one another, the hidden motives that drive visible actions.

## Role in the Novel

**Nannie Merwent** is the character most associated with secrecy, though her secrets are less dark conspiracies than self-serving concealments. Her "secret resolve" to show no interest in Lucy's future [007-036], her secret fury when her daughter prefers Arthur's company [005-009], and her strategic deployment of information all mark her as someone for whom transparency is never an option. Her secrets are the tools of a woman whose social position depends on managing what others know.

The novel's attention to secrecy extends to its structural observation about family life. Jim Sprague's provocative remark — "parents being in secret competition with their children" [012-034] — suggests that secrecy is built into the architecture of the family itself, a hidden dimension of the relationships the novel takes as its subject.

**Lucy Winter** practices secrecy as self-protection. She conceals her agitation from her mother during Nannie's first visit [002-008], and her emotional state from John as their marriage comes under strain. Her secrecy is not manipulative but defensive — the habit of a woman who has learned that expressing her feelings only invites Nannie's interference.

The novel's treatment of secrecy culminates in the scene where Miss Storms attempts to draw John into confidence, and he responds, "There is nothing to confide" [022-137]. This refusal to share — the deliberate withholding that is the reverse of secrecy — marks the point at which silence ceases to be protective and becomes destructive.

## Key Passages

> "In order to conceal her agitation Mrs. Merwent gave Lucy's hair and shoulders meaningless pats." [002-008] — The physical gesture of concealment, where affection masks anxiety.

> "Nannie resented this deeply, and it made her secretly furious to see Lucy toddle toward him as he came into the room." [005-009] — Secrecy as the hidden dimension of domestic rivalry; a mother jealous of her daughter's affection for her father.

> "When it became apparent that both Arthur and her daughter must be taken at their word, curiosity got the better of the secret resolve Nannie had made to show no interest in the matter of Lucy's future." [007-036] — Nannie's resolution to appear indifferent collides with her hunger for information.

> "Do you mean you didn't notice what he said after dinner about parents being in secret competition with their children?" [012-034] — Jim's observation frames secrecy as a structural condition of family life.

> "There is nothing to confide, Miss Storms." [022-137] — John's refusal to share marks the dangerous endpoint of secrecy: the foreclosure of communication itself.

## Thematic Connections

Secrecy is entangled with **[[gossip-and-reputation]]**, for gossip is the mechanism by which private secrets become public currency. It connects to **[[trust-and-betrayal]]**, as the breach of confidence — telling a secret one was trusted to keep — is a form of betrayal the novel repeatedly dramatizes. It intersects with **[[marriage]]**, where the question of what spouses may legitimately keep from one another is a persistent source of tension. And it relates to **[[shame-and-guilt]]**, for secrets are often the wounded hiding place of shame — what cannot be spoken because it cannot be borne.
