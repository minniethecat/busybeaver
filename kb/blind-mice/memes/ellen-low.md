## Character Analysis

Ellen Low is one of the novel's most ghostly presences — a name that surfaces three times across the full span of the narrative, each [[gender-roles|men]]tion adding a fragment to an implied story that is never directly told. She exists in the novel not as a character with [[youth-and-aging|age]]ncy or voice but as a token of the world beyond the [[the-winter-house|Winter household]]: a world of New York [[gender-roles|women]], [[art-and-aesthetics|artist]]ic circles, marital entanglements, and whispered [[gossip-and-reputation|scandal]]s.

The allegation that [[grace-storms|Miss Storms]] "aided and abetted Ellen Low in stealing her husband" [030-075] is the most significant detail attached to her name. Whether the accusation is true, exaggerated, or entirely false, the novel never resolves. What matters for the narrative is not the truth about Ellen Low but the fact of the accusation itself — and what it reveals about [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s readiness to believe the worst of other [[gender-roles|women]], and about the ease with which a woman's [[gossip-and-reputation|reputation]] can be weaponized.

[[grace-storms|Miss Storms]]'s possession of "old [[letter-writing|letters]] of Ellen Low's" [030-108] adds a further layer: Ellen Low's [[letter-writing|correspondence]] becomes a source of social intelligence, a written record of [[friendship|friends]]hips and connections that can be mined for useful information. In a novel so concerned with the social networks that sustain and constrain its characters, Ellen Low's [[letter-writing|letters]] represent the material infrastructure of [[gossip-and-reputation|reputation]] — ink and paper that can be produced as evidence, just as [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s own [[letter-writing|letters]] can be deployed as threats.
