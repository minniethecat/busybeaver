## Overview

The architectural office of [[winter-and-sprague|Winter & Sprague]] is the professional domain of [[john-winter|John Winter]] and [[james-sprague|James Sprague]], the economic engine that supports the Winter household at [[rosedene|Rosedene]]. It represents the world of masculine professional endeavor that complements and enables the domestic sphere.

## Description

The office appears in the novel primarily through the rhythm of the workday — John and Jim's departures on the morning train and returns in the evening. Its physical details are minimal, reflecting the novel's focus on domestic rather than professional space. It is located in [[chicago|Chicago]], reached by the commuter line from Rosedene.

## Significance in the Narrative

The partnership of Winter & Sprague, established shortly before [[lucy-john-wedding|John and Lucy's wedding]] [013-035], is the financial foundation of the Winter household. The office's fortunes directly affect the family — when business is good, domestic life is secure; when [[arthur-merwent|Arthur Merwent]]'s scandals threaten the firm's reputation, the reverberations are felt at Rosedene.

> John Winter, although farthest from the aisle, rose promptly. [001-005]

The office also serves as the site of male camaraderie and professional identity. Jim's devotion to the firm and to John is absolute — he is "Sprague" at the office and "Jim" at home, his identity split between professional and domestic spheres. The office is where crucial decisions about the family's future are made, decisions that Lucy is excluded from by virtue of her domestic role.

## Associated Characters

- [[james-sprague|James Sprague]] — Partner, whose loyalty to John defines both his professional and personal life
