# Motherhood

## Overview

Motherhood in *Blind Mice* is not a single idealized concept but a spectrum of maternal experience, ranging from Lucy Winter's steady, affectionate care for her son Dimmie to Nannie Merwent's histrionic, self-centered treatment of her daughter Lucy. The novel presents motherhood as a relationship shaped by character, circumstance, and generational inheritance -- Nannie's own inadequacies as a mother have roots in her upbringing by the pragmatic, status-conscious Mrs. Lockhart. Motherhood thus serves as a lens through which the novel examines emotional inheritance, domestic duty, and the capacity for genuine love versus performative affection.

## Role in the Novel

Motherhood serves as the emotional and structural axis around which much of the novel's tension revolves. The impending arrival of Lucy's mother Nannie at the Winter household sets the narrative in motion from chapter one (001-012), and the fraught mother-daughter reunion that follows -- a kiss where "their lips barely brushed" (002-035) -- establishes the estrangement that will define their interactions throughout the story.

The novel contrasts two models of motherhood. Lucy embodies maternal competence and warmth: she teaches Dimmie table manners (001-054), leads him through household chores (001-125, 003-115), and remains with him at bedtime "to tell stories and sing lullabies until he was sound asleep" (003-115). Her motherhood is practical, patient, and woven into the fabric of daily life. The physical inheritance of motherhood is underscored in the novel's first portrait of Dimmie, who "had his mother's fine grey eyes" (001-019).

Nannie represents the opposite: an emotionally volatile, self-dramatizing mother who treated her own pregnancy as "a crime and a calamity" (005-004). Her affection, though sometimes effusive -- Lucy "on her return, was covered with kisses" -- coexisted with cruelty, for "Nannie spoke crossly and often cruelly to the child when they were alone, and not infrequently struck her" (005-015). She delegated the practical work of motherhood, refusing to nurse her baby (005-013), and left much of Lucy's care to the family servant Aunt Martha. This inconsistency produces a daughter who is self-sufficient, emotionally guarded, and deeply ambivalent about her mother.

The third generation -- Dimmie -- benefits from Lucy's corrective approach to mothering. Where Nannie saw motherhood as a burden imposed upon her, Lucy embraces it as a central part of her identity. The difference is crystallized when Lucy, exhausted by her mother's accusations, addresses her as "Mother" rather than "Mamma" -- and Nannie pounces: "Yes, 'Mother!' You always used to call me 'Mamma'" (024-125), treating the linguistic shift as evidence of emotional distance. The novel ultimately suggests that Lucy's devoted motherhood is in part a conscious reaction against her own mother's example.

## Key Passages

> "Nannie afterward in a thousand ways suggested that the coming of the child was in the nature of a crime and a calamity, and that Arthur was to blame for it." (005-004)

Nannie's response to pregnancy establishes her fundamental rejection of motherhood as a welcome or natural role. From the novel's first extended glimpse into her past, she is presented as a woman who resents the very existence of her child, foreshadowing the dysfunctional relationship that will shape Lucy's entire childhood.

> "Lucy did not begin to go to school until she was eight years old. Even then her mother objected to the separation, and in the little girl's absence wandered restlessly about the house. Lucy, on her return, was covered with kisses. Acquaintances remarked on the unusual affection between the two, although Nannie spoke crossly and often cruelly to the child when they were alone, and not infrequently struck her." (005-015)

This passage captures the duality of Nannie's motherhood: the public performance of affection contrasted with private cruelty. The image of Lucy returning from school to be "covered with kisses" while enduring cross words and physical blows in private reveals the emotional instability and manipulation that shaped Lucy's childhood.

> "Lucy kissed her mother silently. Their lips barely brushed. In order to conceal her agitation Mrs. Merwent gave Lucy's hair and shoulders meaningless pats." (002-035)

The reunion between mother and daughter after years of separation is rendered with devastating restraint. The kiss that barely connects and the "meaningless pats" convey a relationship hollowed out by history, where physical gestures no longer carry emotional weight.

> "Dimmie helped his mother clear the table and wash the dishes, and when the tasks were done these two joined John and Nannie. However, Dimmie soon grew sleepy and Lucy led him away to bed, remaining with him to tell stories and sing lullabies until he was sound asleep." (003-115)

Lucy's motherhood is shown in the quiet rituals of domestic life. Her willingness to stay with Dimmie, telling stories and singing lullabies until he sleeps, models a kind of maternal patience entirely absent from Nannie's repertoire. The contrast is sharpened by the narrative's placement of this scene directly alongside Nannie's idle chatter with John in the next room.

> "Yes, 'Mother!' You always used to call me 'Mamma.'" (024-125)

This deceptively simple exchange crystallizes the emotional distance between Lucy and Nannie. For Nannie, the vocabulary of motherhood is a barometer of affection -- a weapon to guilt her daughter with. For Lucy, the shift from "Mamma" to "Mother" signals exhaustion with her mother's endless demands and accusations.

> "Oh, Lucy, I want to be loved! I want to be loved!" Nannie protested suddenly, dissolving into tears again. (031-026)

Nannie's climactic cry reveals the emotional vacuum at the heart of her character. Her need for love is presented as bottomless and almost infantile, and her failures as a mother are inextricable from her own unfulfilled emotional needs. Lucy's whispered response -- "So do I" (031-028) -- spoken almost to herself with her eyes on Dimmie across her mother's bent head, suggests that Lucy has found in her own motherhood the love that Nannie could never provide.

> "I know Mamma'll want me to blame Papa, Jim, and I can't." (002-021)

Lucy's quiet admission to Jim Sprague before her mother's arrival reveals the moral clarity that her difficult childhood has produced. Unlike Nannie, who assigned blame freely and dramatically, Lucy refuses to make her father the scapegoat for her mother's unhappiness. Her capacity for independent moral judgment is one legacy -- perhaps the only constructive one -- of growing up under Nannie's chaotic mothering.

## Thematic Connections

Motherhood intersects with the novel's treatment of emotional inheritance, where Nannie's inadequacies as a mother are rooted in her own upbringing by the status-driven Mrs. Lockhart, and Lucy's corrective mothering of Dimmie represents a deliberate break from that generational pattern. The theme also connects to the novel's portrayal of marriage: Nannie's rejection of motherhood is inseparable from her resentment of Arthur, while Lucy's embrace of the maternal role is intertwined with her stable partnership with John. Motherhood illuminates the novel's exploration of performance versus authenticity -- Nannie's public demonstrations of affection and her strategic, self-serving kisses stand in sharp contrast to Lucy's genuine, unselfconscious care for Dimmie. Finally, motherhood is linked to the theme of domestic life, as the daily rituals of meals, bedtimes, and household labor become the arena in which maternal competence or its absence is most vividly displayed.
