## Overview

Miss Brennan is a "pretty Irish girl" [013-041] employed as accountant and stenographer at the [[architecture-profession|architect]]ural firm of [[winter-and-sprague|Winter & Sprague]]. Her presence in the office catalyzes a crisis in the Winters' marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] when [[john-winter]] becomes infatuated with her, praising her "wonderful lot of grit" [013-055] and raising her salary at her own suggestion. [[james-sprague]], loyal to [[lucy-winter]], intervenes by dismissing Miss Brennan, an act that precipitates the [[business-and-commerce|business]] partnership's rupture and foreshadows the novel's broader themes of male loyalty, temptation, and the fragility of domestic happiness.

## Biography

Miss Brennan is hired by [[john-winter]] and [[james-sprague]] as their "accountant and stenographer" [013-041]. The narrator introduces her as "the involuntary arbiter of [[james-sprague|Jim]]'s spiritual destiny, according to his own interpretation" [013-041] — a framing that positions her as a test of character for the [[gender-roles|men]] around her rather than an [[youth-and-aging|age]]nt in her own right.

[[john-winter|John Winter]] quickly becomes taken with her. He describes her to [[james-sprague|Jim]] as "so beautifully simple" [013-043] and recounts following her into her Catholic [[religion-and-morality|church]] "just for curiosity" [013-043]. The religious element carries weight in the novel's Protestant social world — [[john-winter|John]]'s willingness to enter a Catholic [[religion-and-morality|church]] with Miss Brennan signals a fascination that crosses boundaries of [[money-and-class|class]] and [[religion-and-morality|faith]].

The first concrete sign of [[john-winter|John]]'s compromised judgment comes when he "insisted on raising Miss Brennan's salary" [013-046]. When [[james-sprague|Jim]] asks the proposed amount, [[john-winter|John]] "hesitated, and, glancing away as he spoke, named the sum Miss Brennan had suggested, which almost doubled the amount she was receiving" [013-049]. That Miss Brennan herself proposed the figure and [[john-winter|John]] accepted it reveals his loss of professional objectivity.

The situation reaches a crisis when [[james-sprague|Jim]], "entering the office at an unanticipated moment, surprised Miss Brennan in tears and [[john-winter|John]] soothing her with unconventional tenderness" [013-052]. [[john-winter|John]] defends her passionately, launching into "a eulogy of Miss Brennan which embraced all of the adjectives which [[james-sprague|Jim]], on other occasions, had heard him apply to [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]" [013-055] — a detail that makes explicit the threat Miss Brennan represents to the Winters' marri[[youth-and-aging|age]].

[[james-sprague|Jim]] acts decisively. Waiting until [[john-winter|John]] has left the office, he tells Miss Brennan directly: "I want to tell you that your services are not required any longer" [013-057]. Miss Brennan "opened her lips to speak" [013-058] but the moment is interrupted by [[john-winter|John]]'s return. [[james-sprague|Jim]]'s authority as partner makes the dismissal stick, and "Miss Brennan outside had on her hat and coat" [013-062], ready to leave. In the corridor, she is "close to the corridor entrance" [013-069] when [[john-winter|John]] attempts a private farewell, but [[james-sprague|Jim]] physically prevents them from [[loneliness-and-isolation|being alone]] together.

After Miss Brennan's departure, the partners "tacitly avoided employing a successor," with [[james-sprague|Jim]] doing the accounts himself each evening [013-072]. The episode leaves a lasting wound in the partnership and in [[john-winter|John]]'s marri[[youth-and-aging|age]], contributing to the tensions that eventually lead to [[winter-and-sprague|the firm]]'s dissolution.

He praises her with the same langu[[youth-and-aging|age]] he uses for [[lucy-winter]], raises her salary at her direction, and is caught comforting her with "unconventional tenderness" [013-052]. His behavior represents a threat not just to his marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] but to the integrity of the [[business-and-commerce|business]] partnership.

**[[james-sprague]]** — [[james-sprague|Jim]] acts as the guardian of both the partnership's professionalism and the Winters' marri[[youth-and-aging|age]], dismissing Miss Brennan to protect [[john-winter|John]] from his own poor judgment. ## Character Analysis

Miss Brennan exists in the novel primarily as a narrative function rather than a developed character. She is "pretty," "Irish," "so beautifully simple" [013-043] — descriptors that mark her as a type: the appealing young working woman whose presence tests the boundaries of male professional and domestic loyalty. The narrator's description of her as "the involuntary arbiter of [[james-sprague|Jim]]'s spiritual destiny" [013-041] is revealing: she is a moral test, not a moral [[youth-and-aging|age]]nt. The novel grants her no speech of substance, no interiority, and no future after her dismissal.

Her Irishness and Catholicism are significant but understated markers of difference in the novel's Anglo-Protestant social world. [[john-winter|John]] following her into a Catholic [[religion-and-morality|church]] "just for curiosity" [013-043] crosses an unspoken boundary, suggesting an attraction that operates outside the norms of his [[money-and-class|social class]]. Her w[[youth-and-aging|age]] negotiation — she "suggested" a figure that "almost doubled" her salary [013-049] — hints at a practical intelligence that [[john-winter|John]] misreads as simplicity.

The speed and impersonality of [[james-sprague|Jim]]'s dismissal — "your services are not required any longer" [013-057] — underscores Miss Brennan's vulnerability. She may be a threat to the Winters' marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] from within the narrative's moral framework, but she is also a young working woman with no power to contest the decisions made about her by the [[gender-roles|men]] who employ her. The novel's treatment of her departure is notably cold: she leaves, is not [[gender-roles|men]]tioned again, and the partners tacitly agree never to hire another woman for the position.
