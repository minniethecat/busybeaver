## Overview

Class consciousness pervades *Blind Mice*, shaping marriage prospects, social invitations, and personal identity. The novel's characters measure themselves and others against a hierarchy rooted in family name, wealth, and access to what the novel calls "society." Scott anatomizes this hierarchy with unusual precision, showing how financial precarity and social ambition intersect — and how the two are never quite the same thing.

## The Lockhart Decline

The Lockhart family's financial trajectory establishes the novel's class dynamics in miniature. Nannie's father "had inherited money which he invested and spent with equal display and absence of judgment" (004-014), a sentence whose balanced structure mirrors the symmetry of the disaster: the inheritance and its dissipation are two halves of the same act. The year Nannie leaves school is described as "a disastrous year for her father" (004-014), and the consequences radiate outward through every subsequent chapter.

Nannie's response to this decline is not acceptance but intensified entitlement. She is described as having "absorbed from those around her what seemed a tacit recognition of divine right as regarded the members of her family" (004-014). The phrase "divine right" is carefully chosen: Nannie's sense of class position is theological in its absoluteness, immune to empirical correction. Her marriage to Arthur Merwent — "a young lawyer from the north" (004-017) — is described as seeming "almost a divine intervention to insure the Lockharts' future" (004-017), a rescue that preserves the family's social position while papering over its financial hollowing-out.

## The Economics of Marriage

When Arthur's own financial difficulties emerge, Nannie's response strips away whatever romantic pretense the marriage once possessed: "Now we are penniless and you don't seem to care!" (005-005). The first-person plural "we" is tactical — the condition is shared but the grievance is entirely hers. The line reveals both the transactional foundation of the marriage and Nannie's expectation that her husband's primary obligation is to maintain her economic position.

Lucy Winter represents a different relationship to class and marriage. Her refusal to participate in social climbing — despite her mother's pressure — marks her as the novel's moral center on class questions. Her position is clear: "We none of us care for society, Mamma, even if we could afford it" — a sentence that redefines the problem from one of means to one of values.

## Lineage versus Wealth

The novel's small-town setting produces a class system in which lineage and wealth operate as distinct, sometimes competing currencies. The formulation is explicit:

> "Well, at home children of the first families move in the best society, even if they are poor." (014-022)

The qualifier "at home" is significant — it marks Russellville's class logic as local, perhaps provincial, distinct from the harsher wealth-based hierarchies of Chicago or New York. Yet the novel repeatedly demonstrates the limits of name without money. The Lockharts' "first family" status does not pay bills; the Merwents' gentility does not attract clients.

Jim Sprague represents the antithesis of this inherited-status model. His rise from impoverished childhood to professional success is the novel's argument for earned over inherited position. His fastidious self-presentation — "exaggeratedly quiet taste" (001-004) — is class aspiration rendered visible, a performance of belonging that, unlike Nannie's, is backed by genuine accomplishment.

## Mrs. Lockhart's Social Ambition

Mrs. Lockhart serves as the novel's most explicit voice of class anxiety. Her campaign to launch Lucy into society is conducted with an urgency that reveals how precarious she feels her own position to be. She frames social advancement as obligation — "It's high time you were introduced to society" (006-089) — and treats Lucy's indifference as a form of filial negligence. Her own dependence on family connections for social access — "All the society or notice I get is through my own people" — exposes the fragility of a class position maintained entirely through relationships rather than resources.
