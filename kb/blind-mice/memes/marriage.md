# Marriage

## Overview

Marriage functions as both narrative engine and thematic lens in *Blind Mice*, structuring the novel's central conflicts and defining the social world in which its characters move. Far from being treated as a simple romantic resolution, marriage is examined as a complex institution shaped by economics, family ambition, class anxiety, and the often irreconcilable demands of personal fulfillment and social duty. The novel presents marriage as an arrangement that can sustain or erode individual identity, and the quality of a marriage emerges as one of the novel's most reliable measures of character.

## Role in the Novel

Marriage in *Blind Mice* is explored through three primary unions, each offering a distinct model of conjugal life. The marriage of [[Lucy-Winter]] and [[John-Winter]] represents the novel's ideal: a partnership grounded in mutual affection, shared hardship, and the steady work of building a home together. Their union is tested by external pressures — financial strain, Nannie's interference, John's growing professional ambition — but it endures because both partners are fundamentally committed to each other's wellbeing.

The marriage of [[Nannie-Merwent]] and [[Arthur-Merwent]] serves as the novel's cautionary tale. Begun amid Nannie's class anxiety and her mother's financial calculations (004-016), the union deteriorates almost immediately after the wedding (005-003) and ends in divorce. Nannie's understanding of marriage as a vehicle for social position and material comfort, rather than as a reciprocal relationship, dooms the union from its outset. Her mother [[Mrs. Lockhart]] had explicitly framed marriage as financial salvation (004-016), and this transactional view poisons Nannie's capacity for genuine partnership.

Arthur Merwent's remarriage to [[Mrs. Low]] (009-021) and Mrs. Merwent's own later remarriage (034-047) demonstrate that the novel does not condemn second marriages but rather judges each union by the character of those who enter it. Arthur, freed from Nannie, finds a companion whom the narrative treats with quiet respect. Mrs. Merwent's remarriage late in the novel, announced by Lucy with simple directness (033-041), signals that even a woman whose first marriage was catastrophic may find happiness in a later union.

The marriage of Jim Sprague and Delia remains an unresolved thread, with Jim's desire to marry colliding against the economic realities that Delia articulates: "We can't get married now, Jim. You haven't anything to support us and if I marry I'll lose all the money Johnson left me" (012-153). This subplot underscores the economic dimension of marriage that the novel refuses to sentimentalize away.

## Key Passages

> "She had determined that Nannie should find salvation for the family by attracting a husband whose pretensions to that distinction should rest on a solid financial basis." (004-016)

Mrs. Lockhart's explicit framing of marriage as financial "salvation" establishes the mercenary logic that will shape Nannie's disastrous first marriage. The passage exposes the economic anxiety underlying middle-class courtship in the novel's social world.

> "Toward the end of their wedding journey, over some slight misunderstanding at a hotel, Nannie had given way to a hysterical fit of passion that amazed and dismayed her husband, but this was nothing compared with the scene when she discovered that she was pregnant. Tears, screams, striking Arthur in the face, with threats of killing herself, him, and the child were only the beginnings of the drama." (005-003)

The collapse of the Merwent marriage begins almost immediately, signaled by Nannie's violent emotional outbursts. The passage is remarkable for its unflinching depiction of domestic disorder and establishes the pattern of manipulation and emotional warfare that will characterize their married life.

> "The scene which followed the actual filing of Arthur's suit for divorce, therefore, was no worse than numerous other scenes to which she had treated her husband at frequent intervals throughout their married life, many of them precipitated by trifles." (008-043)

This summary judgment on the Merwent marriage reveals that Nannie's outbursts were not isolated incidents but the defining texture of the relationship. The use of "treated" is sharply ironic: Nannie's rages are framed as something she did *to* her husband, not something that happened to her.

> "John is only beginning business and we shall have to be very economical for a while at least," the letter said. "Miss Storms has asked us to be married at her flat, but we shall have a very simple wedding." (009-005)

Lucy's letter announcing her marriage plans to Nannie emphasizes modesty, practicality, and a quiet beginning — values that Nannie's elaborate and expensive wedding (004-028) conspicuously lacked. The contrast between the two weddings mirrors the contrast between the two marriages.

> "A married woman ought to be satisfied with the friendship of her husband." (024-116)

Nannie's taunt, delivered to Lucy, encapsulates the novel's central debate about the nature of marriage. The statement is meant as an accusation — that Lucy's friendship with Jim Sprague exceeds appropriate marital boundaries — but it also reveals Nannie's impoverished understanding of what marriage can be. For Nannie, marriage is about possession and social standing; for Lucy, it is about partnership that does not preclude other meaningful relationships.

> "It used to be that a woman had to turn all her money over to her husband when she married. Now she can hold on to at least some of it. Is she any less attractive? If she has a voice in the making of the laws that govern her and her children, will she become coarsened by it?" (026-164)

Jim Sprague's defense of women's legal and economic rights articulates a progressive vision of marriage as partnership between equals, directly challenging the patriarchal assumptions that underlie much of the novel's social world. His argument with Nannie about "masculine" women dramatizes the era's contested boundaries around gender roles within marriage.

## Related Characters

- [[Lucy-Winter]] — The novel's protagonist, whose marriage to John represents the ideal of loving partnership grounded in mutual respect and shared domestic labor
- [[John-Winter]] — Lucy's husband, whose professional ambition and occasional emotional blindness test but do not break their marriage
- [[Nannie-Merwent]] — Lucy's mother, whose two failed marriages illustrate the destructive consequences of treating marriage as a vehicle for social and financial gain
- [[Arthur-Merwent]] — Nannie's first husband, whose divorce from Nannie and subsequent remarriage demonstrate that marriage can be redeemed when entered into with genuine affection
- [[James-Sprague]] — The intimate family friend whose relationship with Lucy tests the boundaries of marital friendship and whose own thwarted marriage to Delia underscores the economic constraints on working-class unions
- [[Mrs. Lockhart]] — The architect of Nannie's mercenary approach to marriage, whose judgment that Nannie "made a mess of your own marriage" (006-039) is both accurate and hypocritical

## Thematic Connections

Marriage in *Blind Mice* is inseparable from the novel's broader thematic concerns. It intersects with **social class** — the Lockhart family's desperate attempt to marry upward, the Winter family's quiet middle-class respectability, and the economic constraints that delay Jim's marriage all demonstrate how marriage serves as a mechanism for class mobility or entrenchment. It connects to **domestic life** — the novel measures the health of a marriage by the quality of the home it produces, from the warmth of the Winter household to the dysfunction of the Merwent establishment. And it engages with **gender roles** — the novel dramatizes the era's contested ideas about what a wife owes her husband, what a husband owes his wife, and whether marriage must subsume a woman's independent identity. Through the parallel stories of Nannie's spectacular failure and Lucy's quiet endurance, the novel argues that marriage is not a destiny but a practice — one whose success depends on the character of those who undertake it.
