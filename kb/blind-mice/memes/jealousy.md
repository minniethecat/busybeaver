# Jealousy

## Overview

Jealousy in *Blind Mice* operates as one of the novel's most pervasive and destructive emotional forces. It takes multiple forms -- romantic possessiveness between spouses and lovers, rivalry between parents for a child's affection, social envy among women competing for status and male attention, and the simmering resentment of those who feel themselves perpetually second-best. C. Kay Scott traces jealousy not as an isolated passion but as a relational toxin that reshapes the bonds between nearly every major character in the novel. From the opening chapter to the final reconciliation, jealousy -- whether openly acknowledged or denied, whether grounded or imagined -- drives the novel's central conflicts and tests the moral constitution of its characters.

## Role in the Novel

Jealousy functions as both a personal flaw and a structural principle in the narrative. The novel's plot is propelled by overlapping triangles of desire, each one charged with its own form of jealous energy: Nannie's lifelong rivalry with Cousin Minnie Sheldon, Lucy's early jealousy of her mother's demands on John's attention, Jim Sprague's long-suppressed envy of John's position as Lucy's husband, John's growing suspicion of his wife's friendship with Jim, and Nannie's manipulative cultivation of jealousy in others.

The novel distinguishes between the innocent jealousy of new love and the weaponized jealousy of those who use suspicion to control others. When Lucy weeps because John lingered downstairs with Nannie on their wedding day, John's laughing reassurance -- "You dear, sweet, foolish, jealous little thing" (003-158) -- frames her jealousy as endearing, the natural possessiveness of a bride. But when Nannie later tells John that Lucy "had always been jealous of her, ever since you grew up" (022-056), jealousy has become a strategic accusation wielded by a character whose own life is defined by competitive envy.

Nannie Merwent is the novel's primary embodiment of jealousy as a way of life. Her character is established through a history of rivalry: the "childhood antipathy" with Cousin Minnie that culminated in a "contest for the affections" of the man Minnie married (005-011). This early defeat -- losing both her suitor and her ancestral home to her cousin -- leaves Nannie with a permanent sense of injury that she carries into every subsequent relationship. She resents her infant daughter for preferring Aunt Martha and Arthur over herself (005-014), resents Lucy's growing independence as an adolescent (005-017), and gazes "with envy at Lucy's charming color and clear eyes" even as she devotes hours to beauty rituals meant to preserve her own fading attractions (006-007).

Jim Sprague embodies a quieter, more self-aware form of jealousy. His childhood admission -- "While he lay there he admitted to himself that he was jealous of John" (012-009) -- establishes a pattern of painful self-honesty that persists into adulthood. His confession "I've always been jealous of John" (009-020) comes with a flush of shame, and his climactic declaration to Lucy in chapter 28 reveals years of suppressed resentment: "I've resented seeing your youth slipping by, and John not noticing it. I've tried not to covet his wife" (028-019). Jim's jealousy is the novel's most sympathetic because it is accompanied by genuine effort to transcend it.

John Winter's jealousy, by contrast, grows steadily less sympathetic as the novel progresses. What begins as discomfort with Jim's devotion to Lucy sharpens under Nannie's influence into outright suspicion. His resentful look at Lucy (025-008), his jealous comment about Nannie's shopping -- "Anyone would think you were making a trousseau" (030-124) -- and finally his explosive accusation that Lucy's "suspicions and jealousy" have been driving him "half crazy" (031-095) reveal a man who has become what Nannie wanted him to be: suspicious, mistrustful, and alienated from his wife.

## Key Passages

> "Nannie's bitterness was accentuated by the fact that Arthur could not afford to buy the Lockhart residence which had to be sold to clear her own father's estate, and she was forced to see the property pass into the hands of 'Cousin Minnie Sheldon' whom she cordially hated. The spirit of rivalry between Nannie and her cousin, the fruit of a childhood antipathy, had reached its climax in a contest for the affections of the well-to-do young business man who afterward became Minnie's husband, and when he and his wife took formal possession of Nannie's ancestral home she felt it as the cruel affirmation of her first defeat." (005-011)

This passage establishes the biographical roots of Nannie's lifelong jealous disposition. The language of rivalry, contest, and defeat frames her emotional life as a zero-sum competition in which another woman's gain is necessarily her loss. The phrase "cruel affirmation of her first defeat" implies a pattern: Nannie will spend the rest of the novel seeking victories to compensate for this originating loss.

> "Nannie gave scant attention to her baby until other people began to notice and praise the child, when she promptly asserted her proprietorship, pointing out with great pride the little thing's remarkable feats and insisting that Lucy be given to her whenever visitors were present. Nevertheless, the child preferred Aunt Martha and even Arthur ... Nannie resented this deeply, and it made her secretly furious to see Lucy toddle toward him as he came into the room. She often said things to drive Arthur out of the house so that he need not divide the child's affections." (005-014)

Nannie's maternal jealousy reveals her understanding of love as ownership. She values Lucy only when the child serves as a prop for her own vanity before visitors; in private, she cannot tolerate the child's spontaneous affection for anyone else. The phrase "divide the child's affections" treats love as a finite resource that must be monopolized rather than shared.

> "Nannie spent a great deal of time in beauty culture. She tried all sorts of exercises and devices for preserving her figure and warding off wrinkles. Every recipe she saw in the women's columns of the newspapers she tested carefully. Each day a large part of the morning was devoted to a faithful observance of these rites, and she never neglected to take her 'beauty nap' during the afternoon. Often she would gaze with envy at Lucy's charming color and clear eyes." (006-007)

The juxtaposition of Nannie's elaborate beauty rituals with her envious gaze at Lucy's natural vitality captures the futility of competitive jealousy. Nannie's discipline cannot compete with what Lucy possesses effortlessly -- youth. The passage also reveals the dark undercurrent of mother-daughter rivalry that Nannie projects onto Lucy even as she later accuses Lucy of being jealous of her.

> "'I'm jealous, child,' the older woman declared." (009-016) / "'I've always been jealous of John,' Jim answered, flushing a little as he spoke." (009-020)

These two confessions, spoken in quick succession during the family gathering after Lucy's wedding announcement, present contrasting models of acknowledged jealousy. Miss Storms's declaration is affectionate and self-aware -- she is jealous of Lucy's happiness because she loves her. Jim's confession, delivered with a flush of embarrassment, acknowledges a deeper and more threatening jealousy that has shaped his entire life beside John. The scene establishes jealousy as a feeling that can be spoken aloud among intimates without destroying the relationships it complicates -- provided it is owned rather than projected.

> "'I've stood all I can.' His tone grew more intense with each word, 'I've never been too honest with myself about you, Lucy, but I knew I loved you a long time ago. I've resented seeing your youth slipping by, and John not noticing it. I've tried not to covet his wife, because I thought he wanted her. But now I've got to tell you, Lucy. I want you.'" (028-019)

Jim's declaration is the novel's most concentrated expression of jealousy transformed into action. The verbs accumulate with devastating precision: resented, covet, want. Jim has spent years suppressing his envy of John and his desire for Lucy, but Nannie's destruction of the Winter household creates conditions in which suppression becomes impossible. The tragedy of this speech is that Jim's jealousy, however long controlled, finally erupts at precisely the moment when acting on it would make him complicit in Nannie's campaign.

> "Oh, I know, Nannie! 'Lucy didn't mean!' She don't want you out of the house, I suppose! She hasn't been driving you and me half crazy ever since you came with her suspicions and jealousy!" (031-095)

John's outburst marks the completion of Nannie's project: she has convinced him that the jealousy in the house belongs to Lucy, not to herself. The irony is comprehensive. Nannie, whose life has been defined by jealousy and rivalry, has successfully projected her own defining emotion onto her daughter, and John -- who once laughed at Lucy's jealousy as the "foolish" possessiveness of a bride -- now experiences it as a persecutory force.

## Related Characters

- [[Nannie-Merwent]] -- The character most thoroughly defined by jealousy in all its forms: rivalry with Cousin Minnie, possessiveness toward Lucy's affections, envy of Lucy's youth and beauty, and the strategic cultivation of suspicion between John and Lucy
- [[Lucy-Winter]] -- Accused of jealousy by both Nannie and John, though her early jealousy (003-158) is the innocent possessiveness of a bride and her later "suspicions" (031-095) are the rational response to Nannie's actual campaign against her marriage
- [[James-Sprague]] -- Whose lifelong jealousy of John -- first for his material advantages (012-008--009), later for his possession of Lucy (009-020, 028-019) -- is the novel's most self-aware and painfully suppressed form of the emotion
- [[John-Winter]] -- The character most vulnerable to Nannie's jealous manipulations; his trajectory from laughing at Lucy's jealousy (003-158) to accusing her of it (022-056, 031-095) traces his progressive estrangement from his wife
- [[Arthur-Merwent]] -- The object of Nannie's early possessiveness, driven from his own house by her resentment of his bond with Lucy (005-014), and later the target of her jealousy-tinged bitterness over the failed marriage

## Thematic Connections

Jealousy in *Blind Mice* is inseparable from the novel's other central themes. It intersects with **marriage** -- the novel tests each union by the jealous energies that surround and penetrate it, from Nannie's poisonous possessiveness to John's misplaced suspicion of Lucy's friendship with Jim. It connects to **friendship** -- Jim's lifelong friendship with John is shadowed by envy from childhood, and his friendship with Lucy is haunted by the question of whether it can remain platonic or must become romantic. It engages with **gossip and reputation** -- Nannie weaponizes suspicion and insinuation as instruments of social control, planting jealous thoughts in John's mind as deliberately as she once cultivated Professor Walsh's attentions as a counter to her envy of the other teachers (008-015). And it is bound up with the novel's treatment of **appearances versus reality**: nearly every accusation of jealousy in the novel is misdirected -- Nannie accuses Lucy of the emotion that defines Nannie herself, John suspects Lucy of jealous designs that are actually Nannie's, and the novel's deepest jealousy (Jim's) remains largely invisible to the characters who should see it most clearly.

Jealousy also functions as the novel's most reliable index of character. Those who acknowledge their jealousy -- John in his early laughter at Lucy, Miss Storms in her affectionate confession, Jim in his pained self-honesty -- are portrayed as morally redeemable. Those who deny their jealousy while projecting it onto others -- Nannie above all -- are the novel's true antagonists. The difference between feeling jealousy and weaponizing it is, for C. Kay Scott, the difference between being human and being destructive.
