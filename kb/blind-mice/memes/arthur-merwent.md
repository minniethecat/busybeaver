## Overview

Arthur Merwent is a northern lawyer who enters the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] family's world as a young man seeking to establish a law practice in their southern town. His marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] to [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]], undertaken with grave sincerity, devolves into decades of domestic misery from which he ultimately extricates himself through a strategically executed divorce. Remarried to [[ellen-low|Ellen Low]], he builds a flourishing legal career at the state capital, becoming a figure of quiet strength whose dignity contrasts sharply with the chaotic emotional landscape of the wife he left behind. Though physically absent from much of the novel's present-day action, Arthur's decisions and their consequences reverberate through [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s life and the central conflict of the narrative.

## Biography

### Arrival and Courtship

Arthur Merwent arrived in the Lockharts' home town as a young lawyer from the north, a circumstance described by the narrator as seeming "almost a divine intervention to insure the Lockharts' future" (004-017). He rented an office, purchased steel engravings of famous jurists for his walls, and hung a sign over his door, establishing himself with methodical professionalism (004-018). [[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]], aware through hearsay that Merwent held expectations of inheriting [[money-and-class|money]], arranged for him to board at the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] home (004-019).

Arthur proved "agreeable but uncommunicative," a reserve that pleased [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s father, who declared, "That young man can keep his mouth shut. He'll rise" (004-020). [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] assumed a saucy and capricious attitude toward him, demanding services and attentions calculated to impress upon him her superior birth and position (004-022). Thrown into continuous semi-[[friendship|intimacy]], "the imperturbable Arthur gravely declared himself" (004-023). The Lockharts, having investigated the Merwent family and finding their prosperity satisfactory, gave their encour[[youth-and-aging|age]][[gender-roles|men]]t. [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]], sharing the common conviction that Arthur was "a rising young lawyer and a desirable catch, consented to become engaged" (004-024).

### An Ill-Starred Marriage

The eng[[youth-and-aging|age]][[gender-roles|men]]t was tumultuous. [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] engaged in coquettish tricks that disconcerted her fiance until Arthur "refused to be played with longer, and, after exhausting her ingenuity in the endeavor to gain more delay, she fixed the wedding day" (004-026). Twice during wedding preparations, [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] and Arthur quarrelled and she returned his ring (004-027).

On the day before the wedding, [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] descended into "a state of extraordinary depression" and fled from Arthur's presence to weep on a sofa. When he approached her, she turned on him with a storm of accusations: "You've wrecked my life! I don't know what's to become of me!" (004-031). Arthur, his voice slightly unsteady but his manner quiet, replied, "It's not too late yet, [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]" (004-032). He was left alone, sitting with his head in his hands before lighting a cigar (004-034). [[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]] comforted him with eggnog, dismissing [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s outburst as "the perfectly natural result of her high strung state" (004-035).

The wedding proceeded. Arthur entered the [[religion-and-morality|church]] "gravely, his head bent" and did not lift his eyes even as [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] approached him at the chancel rail (004-038). The ceremony was marred when his heel caught in a loop of satin at the carri[[youth-and-aging|age]] steps, tearing [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s gown and provoking her to fling herself from his grasp and sit far from him in frosty silence [004-041, 004-043]. On the Pullman that night, however, [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] touched his arm with a softened expression, and they kissed stealthily [004-048, 004-051].

### The Disintegration of Family Life

[[lucy-winter|Lucy]] was born about a year after the wedding (005-001). The pregnancy and birth exacerbated [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s volatility. She struck Arthur in the face during hysterical fits and suggested "in a thousand ways that the coming of the child was in the nature of a crime and a calamity, and that Arthur was to blame for it" [005-003, 005-004]. She even implied that Arthur was responsible for her father's [[death-and-mortality|death]], which occurred a month before her confinement (005-004).

Arthur developed his characteristic defense: "his attitude of non-reaction to the stimuli she employed" (005-003). When confronted with reproaches after the [[death-and-mortality|death]]s of both fathers, Arthur answered quietly, "I don't see just what there is to say," and left the room (005-010). His law practice did not increase, and there were no more expectations from his father's estate, which forced the family into genteel poverty (005-018).

Yet Arthur formed a deep bond with his daughter. He "often held his daughter during the evenings and sang lullabies to her," and little [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] toddled toward him when he came into the room -- a fact that made [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] "secretly furious" (005-014). When [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s jealousy drove her to say "things to drive Arthur out of the house so that he need not divide the child's affections" (005-014), and when she prevented him from helping [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] with her [[education|school]] lessons (005-016), their family became a [[domestic-life|household]] divided. During [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s serious childhood [[illness-and-medicine|illness]], Arthur returned on the first train, seated himself beside her bed, and sat with her through the crisis [005-023, 005-026]. His quiet, emotionless manner infuriated [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]], whose chief [[jealousy|resentment]] arose from the fact "that he did not comfort her but only concerned himself with [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]" (005-023).

Speaking to [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] about her [[motherhood|mother]], Arthur revealed his unsparing clarity: "Your [[motherhood|mother]] has every defect of character that can be [[gender-roles|men]]tioned, so I suppose she shouldn't be blamed for being sensitive" (006-005).

### The Divorce and Its Aftermath

After [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] departed for the Art Institute of [[chicago|Chicago]] under Arthur's arrangement, the Merwent home "went from bad to worse." [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s presence had formerly "sufficed to prevent the more sordid expressions of the disgust and contempt which Arthur felt for [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]," and he now avoided being at home until he became "almost a stranger in the house" (008-001). Arthur's name became linked in [[gossip-and-reputation|scandal]] with that of [[ellen-low|Mrs. Ellen Low]] (008-002).

Meanwhile, [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] became increasingly dependent on [[professor-walsh|Professor Walsh]]. Arthur met Walsh at a reception and had a brief, outwardly cordial exchange in which Walsh promised him a letter of introduction to a [[chicago|Chicago]] lawyer [008-018, 008-024]. Shortly afterward, [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] sneeringly alluded to Arthur's attentions to Mrs. Low (008-025).

The divorce was engineered with lawyerly precision. When [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] emerged from an evening visit to [[professor-walsh|Professor Walsh]] at a [[gossip-and-reputation|scandal]]ous hour, she was confronted at the gate by a strange man asking if she were [[nannie-merwent|Mrs. Merwent]]. From the shadow of the Academy walls stepped [[mr-blair|Mr. Blair]], Arthur's legal colleague, "followed by Arthur himself and another strange man" (008-038). Arthur coldly declared he would bring suit on a graver charge, would not shrink from publicity, and would use the incident of the night to prove his case, as the two unknown [[gender-roles|men]] were "reputable witnesses" (008-040).

When [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] perceived "that r[[youth-and-aging|age]], abuse, and sarcasms all failed to affect Arthur's calm, she grew really frightened and began to weep hysterically" (008-044). She pleaded, "You can't mean that you will carry this thing through, Arthur! For [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s sake you can't do such awful things" (008-047), and "Arthur, I can't believe that you hate me that much! You don't hate me, do you?" (008-050). Arthur turned on his heel and went out (008-054).

After [[the-divorce-scandal|the divorce]], Arthur never entered his house again. He established residence at the state capital and, when compelled to visit Russellville, slept in his office and ate at the local hotel (009-001). He could not attend [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s wedding to [[john-winter|John Winter]] because he was "still engaged in an important case at the capital" (009-008), though his letter to her offered a single piece of advice: "don't let your [[motherhood|mother]] intrude on your happiness" (009-009).

### Second Marriage and Later Years

Arthur and [[ellen-low|Ellen Low]] were married at the state capital, not in [[chicago|Chicago]] as expected, at a time when Arthur's new practice had begun to flourish (009-021). He made two visits to [[chicago|Chicago]], and on one of these spent a night at [[rosedene|Rosedene]], where he met [[james-sprague|Jim Sprague]], "for whom he afterward expressed a strong liking" (009-021).

In the novel's later timeline, Arthur and Ellen visit [[chicago|Chicago]], where [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] brings [[dimmie-winter|Dimmie]] to meet them. Arthur wishes to see his grandson before leaving and promises him a watch (026-093). The visit becomes a point of conflict between [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] and [[john-winter|John]], who erupts with fury at her "tagging around after your father and the woman he deserted [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] for" (025-092). When [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] fails to see them again before their departure, Arthur says slowly, "Well, I'm sorry. It means that I have lost my daughter. I don't wonder that she's sick, poor girl. I could see that she was nearly frantic yesterday. I'll write to her." Then his voice grows bitter: "Of all the fiends in human form--!" Ellen lays her hand on his arm and he falls silent (026-112).

## Relationships

**[[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] Merwent (ex-wife):** Arthur's marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] to [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] defines the tragic arc of his early life. From their fraught [[courtship-and-romance|courtship]] through decades of domestic warfare to the climactic divorce, Arthur responds to [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s emotional storms with quiet restraint that hardens into steel. By the time of [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s departure, he tells his daughter, "I wouldn't ask her for a drink of water if I were [[death-and-mortality|dying]]," with "such suppressed hatred and vehemence" in his tone that [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] had never heard the like (007-101). Yet his method of exit -- a lawfully witnessed divorce rather than abandonment -- reflects the dignity he maintains even in severance.

**[[lucy-winter|Lucy Winter]] (daughter):** Arthur's relationship with [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] is the novel's most consistent bond of quiet devotion. He sings lullabies to her as a child, rushes home when she is gravely ill (005-023), arranges her escape to [[art-and-aesthetics|art]] [[education|school]] in [[chicago|Chicago]] (007-017), and pins a white carnation on her jacket as they say goodbye at the station (007-102). His succinct wedding advice -- "don't let your [[motherhood|mother]] intrude on your happiness" (009-009) -- encapsulates both his protective instinct and his understanding of the family's central problem. His grief at potentially "losing" [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] in the novel's later chapters (026-112) reveals how deeply he values their connection, even from a geographical and emotional distance.

**[[ellen-low|Ellen Low]] Merwent (wife):** Arthur's second marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] represents a conscious choice toward a life of mutual respect and quiet competence. Ellen is described as "too big a woman for her environment" with "too much self-respect to be understood by the people in this place" (007-032). The fact that they marry quietly at the state capital rather than in [[chicago|Chicago]] (009-021) suggests a shared preference for privacy and substance over display.

**[[james-sprague|Jim Sprague]]:** Arthur takes an immediate liking to [[james-sprague|Jim]] during a visit to [[rosedene|Rosedene]], and [[james-sprague|Jim]] later becomes an ally in the effort to counter [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] and [[professor-walsh|Professor Walsh]]'s machinations [009-021, 030-108].

**[[john-winter|John Winter]]:** Arthur's relationship with his son-in-law is strained by [[john-winter|John]]'s defense of [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] and hostility toward Ellen. Arthur is "terribly hurt by [[john-winter|John]]'s attitude" (027-072), and [[grace-storms|Miss Storms]] notes that Arthur's [[jealousy|resentment]] of [[john-winter|John]]'s position regarding Ellen is natural, though Arthur never mistakes the source of it or holds it against [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] herself (027-072).

## Character Analysis

Arthur Merwent is defined by the tension between deep feeling and surface calm. The narrator repeatedly emphasizes his imperturbable quality -- "the imperturbable Arthur gravely declared himself" (004-023), his "attitude of non-reaction to the stimuli she employed" (005-003) -- yet this exterior masks genuine emotion. When [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] attacks him on the day before their wedding, his voice is "slightly unsteady and his eyes shone dangerously, but his manner was quiet" (004-032). His quiet retreats to his office, his cigar-lighting, and his laconic replies are not signs of indifference but of a man who has chosen dignity over display and calculation over impulse.

His profession as a lawyer is integral to his character. He decorates his office with "steel engravings of famous jurists" (004-018), his colleague [[mr-blair|Mr. Blair]] assists in [[the-divorce-scandal|the divorce]] ambush (008-038), and he builds a flourishing practice at the state capital (009-021). Even [[the-divorce-scandal|the divorce]] itself is conducted with the methodical precision of a legal case: witnesses, documentation, and a calculated willingness to pursue a "graver charge" if necessary (008-040). He wields the law as both a profession and a shield against [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s chaos.

His dignity carries a cost. Years of emotional withdrawal leave him unable or unwilling to eng[[youth-and-aging|age]] in open emotional expression. His parting from [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] at the train station is tender but [[business-and-commerce|business]]like, focused on practical details -- tickets, [[money-and-class|money]], a carnation, the person who will meet her (007-102). His final words in the novel, "I'll write to her" (026-112), suggest a man who remains most comfortable communicating at a remove.

## Thematic Significance

Arthur embodies the novel's exploration of dignity under duress and the cost of silence. In a narrative populated by characters who externalize every emotional impulse -- [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]], [[john-winter|John]] in his rages, even [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] in her later confrontations -- Arthur represents a contrasting model: the man who absorbs, contains, and ultimately acts rather than declaims. His divorce from [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] is not a dramatic explosion but a legal procedure, witnessed and documented, the culmination of decades of patient endurance.

The North/South cultural tension runs through Arthur's characterization. He is "a Yankee" (004-023), an outsider whose northern reserve contrasts with the performative emotionality of the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] family. [[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]]'s initial reluctance to accept him stems partly from "a grave doubt as to the aristocracy of his connections" (004-023) -- he represents meritocratic professionalism rather than inherited status, a threat to the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] self-conception.

His second marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] to [[ellen-low|Ellen Low]] represents the novel's argument for character over station. Ellen is criticized in Russellville because she is "too bighearted to be prudent" (007-032), but Arthur sees in her the self-respect and competence that [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] entirely lacks. Their quiet life at the state capital, away from small-town [[gossip-and-reputation|gossip]], is the life Arthur should always have had.
