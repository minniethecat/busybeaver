# Nannie Merwent

## Overview

Nannie Merwent (nee Anna Lockhart) is the primary antagonist of *Blind Mice*. A petite, restless woman of diminished southern aristocratic pretensions, [[nannie-arrival|Nannie arrives]] at [[rosedene|Rosedene]] as the long-estranged [[motherhood|mother]] of [[Lucy-Winter]] and proceeds to methodically destabilize the [[the-winter-house|Winter household]] through a campaign of manipulation, emotional [[music-and-entertainment|theater]], and strategic victimhood. Self-absorbed to the point of blindness, obsessed with [[appearance-and-looks|appearance]]s and [[money-and-class|social standing]], Nannie is simultaneously the novel's most destructive force and its most fully realized portrait of a woman whose ambitions and energies have found no constructive outlet. Her four-month visit -- from chapter 2's warm welcome to chapter 33's [[shame-and-guilt|guilt]]-laden departure -- transforms the [[the-winter-house|Winter home]] from a domestic sanctuary into an arena of psychological warfare, exposing and exploiting every weakness in [[John-Winter]]'s character and driving a wedge between husband and wife that proves, in the end, impossible to remove.

## Biography

Anna Lockhart was born in Russellville, "in a southern state, of a family that was considered to be aristocratic and distinguished" (004-001). Her earliest memories centered on "a large, square, white house with a front porch supported by Corinthian pillars, a long driveway lined with great trees on either side, and much display of lavish hospitality" (004-002). From childhood, "her whims were gratified" and she was raised to believe that "the clothes, the pony, the negress who nursed her, the rings and brooches, were not a p[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] of her own charm and importance" (004-003).

As a child, Nannie "delighted in showing her power, and was relentless in exacting notice and recognition of her position" (004-006). She tortured the family dog Troupe, stuck pins into her nurse [[aunt-martha|Aunt Martha]] (004-007), and "was called stingy, as she seldom divided her sweetmeats or other good things with her playmates, and would not allow them to touch her toys" (004-008). "No attempt was made to teach Nannie any useful occupation, and, as she cared neither for stories nor for books, the task of amusing her became no sinecure" (004-009).

The family's fortunes collapsed when Nannie was eighteen. Her father "had inherited [[money-and-class|money]] which he invested and spent with equal display and absence of judgment, but he awoke one day to find that creditors were impervious both to the dignity of the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] name and the impressiveness of the ancestral mansion" (004-014). [[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]], a practical woman, "had determined that Nannie should find salvation for the family by attracting a husband whose pretensions to that distinction should rest on a solid financial basis" (004-016).

"It seemed almost a divine intervention to insure the Lockharts' future when [[arthur-merwent|Arthur Merwent]], a young lawyer from the north, came to the home town" (004-017). Nannie regarded [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] with some reservation: "True, his financial prospects were inclined to soften her, but with all that he was a Yankee and there existed a grave doubt as to the aristocracy of his connections" (004-023). Nevertheless, she consented to become engaged. The [[courtship-and-romance|courtship]] was stormy: Nannie "was saucy and capricious, demanding services and attentions calculated to convince him of her superior birth and position" (004-022), and on the eve of the wedding she was "discovered face downwards on an old horsehair sofa in a violent paroxysm of weeping" (004-029), turning on [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] with "a storm of accusations" (004-030). Yet the next day "Nannie was radiant" (004-037), and the wedding proceeded with the elaborate display she had insisted upon.

[[lucy-winter|Lucy]] was born about a year after the wedding (005-001). Nannie "declared that she could not nurse a [[dimmie-winter|baby]]" (005-013), and "gave scant attention to her [[dimmie-winter|baby]] until other people began to notice and praise the child, when she promptly asserted her proprietorship" (005-014). She resented [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s affection for [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]], and "it made her secretly furious to see [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] toddle toward him as he came into the room" (005-014). Nannie struck [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] when the girl expressed a wish to attend [[art-and-aesthetics|art]] [[education|school]] (005-015), and drove [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] from the house when he tried to help the child with her lessons (005-016).

The marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] deteriorated steadily. "The disgust and contempt which [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] felt for Nannie, and of Nannie's feeling of fear and [[trust-and-betrayal|distrust]] toward him" (008-001) deepened after [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] departed for [[art-and-aesthetics|art]] [[education|school]] in [[chicago|Chicago]]. [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] became linked in [[gossip-and-reputation|gossip]] with a [[ellen-low|Mrs. Ellen Low]] (008-002), and the couple eventually divorced. Nannie, always "panic stricken when alone, less from fear than from lack of resources within herself" (009-002), drifted without purpose until, having exhausted the patience of her relatives, she announced her intention to visit [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] and [[john-winter|John]] at [[rosedene|Rosedene]].

Nannie arrived at [[rosedene|Rosedene]] in chapter 2, and her presence immediately began to unsettle the [[domestic-life|household]]. She insinuated herself into [[john-winter|John]]'s affections by flattering his [[art-and-aesthetics|artist]]ic sensibilities and lamenting his financial constraints: "It's a pity you haven't [[money-and-class|money]] so you could follow your natural bent and be an [[art-and-aesthetics|artist]]" (003-088). She questioned the [[manners-and-etiquette|propriety]] of [[james-sprague|Jim Sprague]]'s constant presence: "This [[james-sprague|Mr. Sprague]] seems to be quite at home here. Aren't you afraid people will say things?" (003-053). And she systematically worked to displace [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] in the domestic order, taking over meal planning, redecorating, and cultivating a private alliance with [[john-winter|John]] that excluded his wife.

The conflict escalated across the summer. Nannie extracted [[money-and-class|money]] from [[john-winter|John]] behind [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s back (014-069), turned [[john-winter|John]] against [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] with insinuations about [[james-sprague|Jim]] (024-201), bribed [[dimmie-winter|Dimmie]] with candy to purchase his affection (025-029), and cultivated a servant named [[grace-storms|Grace]] as a [[domestic-life|household]] ally (chapter 21). When [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s father [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] visited [[chicago|Chicago]], Nannie weaponized the event: "Well, you are either on Nannie's side, or you are on your father's side" (024-069), forcing [[john-winter|John]] into a position where any loyalty to [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] felt like [[trust-and-betrayal|betrayal]] of Nannie.

The novel's climactic crisis was precipitated when [[james-sprague|Jim Sprague]] confronted Nannie in the park and threatened to expose her to her Russellville acquaintances: [[professor-walsh|Professor Walsh]], the Sheldons, and [[judge-dodd|Judge Dodd]] (030-164:030-197). Cornered, Nannie orchestrated her exit for maximum dramatic effect. A telegram from [[professor-walsh|Professor Walsh]] arrived at [[rosedene|Rosedene]] (031-074), and Nannie, playing the wounded innocent, announced she must leave: "I was driven to this because [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] has made no other course possible!" (031-116). [[john-winter|John]], by now wholly under her spell, raged against [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] for driving Nannie away. Nannie's departure in chapter 33 was carefully staged: "Nannie gazed back long and solemnly at the house" as the automobile pulled away (033-024), and she accepted from [[john-winter|John]] a box of bonbons "three or four times the size of the pack[[youth-and-aging|age]] she had given [[dimmie-winter|Dimmie]]" (033-038).

More than a month after Nannie's departure, the [[domestic-life|household]] remained fractured (036-001). Her influence persisted in [[john-winter|John]]'s lingering [[jealousy|resentment]], in [[james-sprague|Jim]]'s estrangement, and in the shadow her presence had cast over the marri[[youth-and-aging|age]]. Nannie's last indirect [[appearance-and-looks|appearance]] in the novel is a character at a party remarking, "Mrs. Merwent told me you were responsible for these [[courtship-and-romance|love]]ly pictures" (035-029) -- a final, faint echo of her talent for insinuation.

## Relationships

### Lucy Winter

Nannie's relationship with [[Lucy-Winter]] is the novel's most dam[[youth-and-aging|aging]] bond. From [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s infancy, Nannie alternated between neglect and possessive assertion of proprietorship, withdrawing affection when the child displeased her and demanding it when others praised the child (005-014). She physically struck [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] as a girl, opposed her education, and when [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] left for [[chicago|Chicago]], Nannie descended into "a state of hysteria bordering on madness" (007-091). At [[rosedene|Rosedene]], Nannie systematically undermined [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s position: usurping her role as hostess, turning [[john-winter|John]] against her, bribing [[dimmie-winter|Dimmie]] away from her, and insinuating im[[manners-and-etiquette|propriety]] in her [[friendship|friends]]hip with [[james-sprague|Jim]]. The shift from "Mamma" to "Mother" (024-125) marks [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s painful recognition of emotional distance. Yet Nannie's power over her daughter is real: [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] cannot simply expel her, and her presence forces [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] into increasingly desperate measures to protect her home.

### John Winter

Nannie's relationship with [[John-Winter]] is her most successful manipulation. She recognizes his weaknesses -- his [[pride-and-vanity|vanity]] about his [[art-and-aesthetics|art]], his [[jealousy|resentment]] of financial constraints, his need for flattery -- and exploits them with surgical precision. "They're simply exquisite" (003-092), she says of his [[art-and-aesthetics|watercolor]]s. "It's a pity you haven't [[money-and-class|money]] so you could follow your natural bent and be an [[art-and-aesthetics|artist]]" (003-088), planting the seed that [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] and domestic [[duty-and-obligation|responsibility]] are holding him back. She makes him feel like a protector and a man of importance, and he responds with fierce loyalty, defending her against [[lucy-winter|Lucy]], against [[grace-storms|Miss Storms]], against anyone who questions her. [[john-winter|John]]'s enraged comparison -- "She evidently thought when she married me that I was a man without any backbone, exactly the opposite of [[james-sprague|Jim Sprague]]!" (031-122) -- reveals how completely Nannie has reconfigured his emotional landscape.

### Arthur Merwent

Nannie's marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] to [[arthur-merwent|Arthur Merwent]] was a calculated transaction that turned sour. She "assumed a light and jesting attitude toward [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]]" from the beginning (004-022), alternating coquettish tricks with stormy quarrels. She returned his ring twice during the eng[[youth-and-aging|age]][[gender-roles|men]]t (004-027). After the wedding, she "gave way to a hysterical fit of passion that amazed and dismayed her husband" over a slight misunderstanding on the honeymoon (005-003). [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]]'s failure to become [[money-and-class|wealth]]y -- his inability to buy the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] mansion, the family's reduced circumstances -- fueled Nannie's bitterness: "Nannie began to feel the pinch of poverty more keenly, especially in the matter of her wardrobe" (008-003). She took [[music-and-entertainment|piano]] teaching work at [[professor-walsh|Professor Walsh]]'s [[education|school]], which "allowed her to remind [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] that he could not support her" (008-013). The divorce, when it came, was followed by [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]]'s eventual remarri[[youth-and-aging|age]] to [[ellen-low|Ellen Low]], a fact Nannie cannot forgive and uses as perpetual grievance.

### Jim Sprague

Nannie immediately identifies [[James-Sprague]] as a threat. His calm perceptiveness makes her uneasy; his immunity to her charm enrages her. She probes him about [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]: "It isn't every man who enjoys having his wife admired too much" (010-109). She complains to [[john-winter|John]] that [[james-sprague|Jim]] "hasn't even treated me with common ordinary courtesy since I've been here" (032-073). She insinuates to [[john-winter|John]] that [[james-sprague|Jim]] and [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] are conspiring against her: "If you think I haven't seen through the scheme you and your [[james-sprague|Mr. Sprague]] have worked from the very beginning, you're greatly mistaken" (032-025). Her parting jab -- "I suppose [[james-sprague|Mr. Sprague]] will begin to come out here twice a week to spend the night again, as soon as I've left" (032-030) -- is designed to poison the well. [[james-sprague|Jim]], for his p[[art-and-aesthetics|art]], assesses her with devastating accuracy: she has "a display of mingled clumsiness and cunning" (013-077). His confrontation in the park is the one moment in the novel when Nannie's manipulation fails entirely -- and she concedes, retreating from [[rosedene|Rosedene]] within the week.

### Dimmie Winter

Nannie's relationship with her grandson [[Dimmie-Winter]] reveals her fundamental inability to [[courtship-and-romance|love]] unselfishly. She forgets about him at their first meeting: "How stupid of me! Come and kiss your Nannie now" (002-057). She never uses his preferred nickname, calling him "[[dimmie-winter|Jimmie]]" instead (010-021). Her affection is transactional: chocolates for kisses, candy for declarations of [[courtship-and-romance|love]]. The most disturbing episode occurs in chapter 25, where she pressures the child repeatedly: "Don't you [[courtship-and-romance|love]] Nannie as much as you do Mamma?" (025-059), using a four-year-old's affection as a weapon in her war against her daughter. She gets him punished unjustly and then bribes him with candy to restore his compliance with her (chapter 18). Her parting words -- "Don't forget Nannie, will you, darling?" (033-034) -- capture the emotional manipulation that defines all her relationships.

## Character Analysis

Nannie Merwent is a study in the toxic combination of entitlement and insecurity. Raised to believe in her own superiority, she was given no skills, no purpose, and no internal resources to sustain that belief when external validation was withdrawn. The family's financial collapse left her with aristocratic tastes, no means to satisfy them, and no capacity for productive work. Her "desire to attract notice increased with the years" (004-011), and she channeled this need into the only arena left to her: manipulation of those closest to her.

Nannie's intelligence is considerable but narrowly applied. She reads people with an almost predatory acuteness, identifying vulnerabilities and exploiting them. Her cultivation of [[john-winter|John]] is a masterpiece of applied psychology: she tells him exactly what he wants to hear about his [[art-and-aesthetics|art]], his marri[[youth-and-aging|age]], his importance. Her flirtatiousness is not sexual but strategic -- she uses [[gender-roles|femininity]] as a weapon, understanding that [[gender-roles|men]] like [[john-winter|John]] are susceptible to a woman who makes them feel powerful. "Nannie never opened the back door to him without first looking into the mirror" (005-019) -- she knows that her [[appearance-and-looks|appearance]] is her currency, and she maintains it obsessively.

Yet Nannie is also a figure of pathos. She is "panic stricken when alone, less from fear than from lack of resources within herself" (009-002). Stripped of an audience, she has no self. Her conversation "consisted mostly of beginnings" (003-051) because she has nothing to say -- only performances to deliver. Her demand for attention is insatiable because no amount of external validation can fill the void where a self should be. The novel withholds easy condemnation even as it charts the wreck[[youth-and-aging|age]] she leaves behind: Nannie is what happens when a woman is taught that her value lies entirely in being admired, and then is stripped of the means to command that admiration.

Her signature technique is the performance of injury. When confronted, Nannie immediately assumes the role of victim: "I'm sure I didn't mean to interfere, [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]. I was only trying to help" (003-045); "I didn't say there was anything wrong about it" (024-119); "I'm all -- alone -- in the world" (032-043). This reflex protects her from accountability while placing her accuser in the position of aggressor. It is a tactic so effective that it ensnares even the otherwise perceptive [[john-winter|John]], who becomes her most ardent defender.

## Thematic Significance

Nannie Merwent is the novel's incarnation of the destructive power of thwarted [[gender-roles|femininity]]. Raised in a culture that taught [[gender-roles|women]] to value themselves only through the eyes of others, Nannie has become a predator of attention, devouring the emotional resources of everyone around her. Her presence at [[rosedene|Rosedene]] exposes the fragility of the domestic ideal: a marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] built on [[john-winter|John]]'s [[art-and-aesthetics|artist]]ic pretensions and [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s self-sacrifice cannot withstand the pressure she applies.

Nannie also serves as the novel's sharpest critique of southern aristocratic culture. The Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] "aristocracy" is revealed as hollow -- a family that "had never accomplished anything noteworthy" but "had usually raised sugar and cotton instead of corn and tobacco" (004-001). The great house with Corinthian pillars, the lavish hospitality, the sense of "divine right" -- all of it is faccade, propped up by inherited [[money-and-class|money]] that evaporates the moment it meets economic reality. Nannie's obsession with [[appearance-and-looks|appearance]]s and her inability to eng[[youth-and-aging|age]] with the world as it actually is are the direct products of this culture. Her character is the novel's argument that the old southern order, for all its surface graces, produced [[gender-roles|women]] who were emotionally deformed and morally bankrupt.

Finally, Nannie is the "farmer's wife" of the nursery rhyme that gives the novel its title. "Three blind mice, see how they run. They all run after the farmer's wife. She cut off their tails with a carving knife" (030-093:030-098). [[james-sprague|Jim Sprague]]'s bitter assessment -- "She's cut more than our tails off" (030-103) -- names Nannie as the [[youth-and-aging|age]]nt of the novel's destruction. The mice are blind, but they are not innocent; their blindness is complicity. Nannie is the knife, but the [[domestic-life|household]]'s vulnerabilities -- [[john-winter|John]]'s weakness, [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s passivity, [[james-sprague|Jim]]'s suppressed desire -- are what make the cutting possible.
