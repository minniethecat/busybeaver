## Overview

Duty and obligation form a pervasive moral language in *Blind Mice*, one that characters of every social station deploy to justify their conduct, to critique others, and to navigate the tensions between personal desire and social expectation. The novel presents duty not as a single coherent ethic but as a contested vocabulary: Nannie Merwent invokes a woman's duty to advance herself socially; Jim Sprague speaks of the citizen's duty to think and act; John Winter feels the obligation of a husband to protect his family; and Lucy Winter wrestles with the question of what she owes to herself versus what she owes to others. Across these competing claims, C. Kay Scott maps the gap between duty as professed principle and duty as lived burden.

## Role in the Novel

The language of obligation threads through every major relationship in the novel. **Nannie Merwent** is the character most vocally associated with duty — she lectures Lucy on "a woman's duty to advance herself" [014-026] and declares that "it's a woman's duty to look as well as she can" [014-116], always invoking duty to justify her own agenda of social climbing. Nannie also weaponizes obligation against others, as when she tells Professor Walsh that his accidental role in her embarrassment "constituted a sort of obligation on his part" [008-055].

**Jim Sprague** offers a contrasting, more civic-minded vision of duty. In a rare moment of political passion, he insists that "it's the duty of citizens to think and act" and laments that "the good men stay away from the polls and let things slide" [012-076]. This vision of duty as public responsibility stands in sharp relief against the novel's otherwise predominantly domestic frame.

For **Lucy Winter**, duty is a trap. Mrs. Lockhart tells her granddaughter she "ought to take your place as a young lady" [006-091] and pressures her toward marriage with Kingsley Dodd. Lucy's rebellion — "I don't think I ought to be dependent on anyone any longer" [007-010] — redefines duty as independence from the very social obligations her family insists upon.

**John Winter** shoulders duty as the material burden of provision. He works relentlessly, prompting Nannie's observation that "no matter how tired you are you are so cheerful," adding that "it ought to make Lucy and me ashamed of ourselves" [014-071]. John seldom speaks in the language of duty himself; instead, others invoke it on his behalf or to critique him, as when Jim tells him "if Lucy can overlook things you ought to be able to" [001-102].

## Key Passages

> "It's the duty of citizens to think and act. The good men stay away from the polls and let things slide, and the bad element gets in its dirty work, and the poor suffer." [012-076] — Jim Sprague articulates a civic ideal of duty that stands apart from the novel's domestic preoccupations.

> "It's a woman's duty to advance herself all she can." [014-026] — Nannie Merwent's most characteristic pronouncement, converting social ambition into moral imperative.

> "I think that it's a woman's duty to look as well as she can." [014-116] — Nannie extends the logic of duty to appearance, revealing how the vocabulary of obligation can be stretched to cover vanity.

> "I don't think I ought to be dependent on anyone any longer." [007-010] — Lucy Winter redefines duty as self-reliance, rejecting the filial and marital obligations that her mother and grandmother press upon her.

> "I certainly will not be stopped from doing my duty in protecting Lucy no matter if you do misunderstand me." [010-124] — Mrs. Merwent casts her interference in Lucy's life as maternal duty, illustrating how the word can mask intrusion.

> "No matter how tired you are you are so cheerful, John. It ought to make Lucy and me ashamed of ourselves." [014-071] — Nannie uses the language of obligation to praise John while subtly criticizing Lucy.

## Thematic Connections

The discourse of duty in *Blind Mice* intersects with several other major themes. It is intimately connected to **[[marriage]]**, where the obligations of husband and wife are contested ground between John, Lucy, Nannie, and Arthur. It overlaps with **[[gender-roles]]**, as women's duties (to look well, to advance socially, to obey) are prescribed with a specificity absent from men's more diffuse obligations. It stands in tension with **[[money-and-class]]**, for the duties the characters invoke so readily are in practice circumscribed by their financial means — Jim's civic duty requires leisure Nannie's social duty requires money. And it is shadowed by **[[shame-and-guilt]]**, as the failure to meet duty's demands is a recurring source of humiliation and self-reproach throughout the novel.
