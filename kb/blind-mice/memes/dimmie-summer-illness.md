## Overview

Dimmie Winter, the four-year-old son of [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] and [[john-winter|John Winter]], falls gravely ill during the summer season. The illness creates a crisis that draws the extended family together while exposing underlying tensions between Lucy and her mother, [[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]].

## Narrative Context

Dimmie is introduced as a bright, active child — "a slim child of four" [001-019] who charms everyone with his innocent questions. Named for Sprague Winter, John's business partner, Dimmie represents the future of the Winter family. When he falls ill, the household's routines are disrupted and long-simmering family tensions surface.

Throughout the novel, Dimmie's presence anchors domestic scenes — he plays under the old elm tree [002-002], accompanies Lucy and Nannie on walks, and provides moments of lightness amid heavier adult concerns.

## Key Participants

- **[[dimmie-winter|Dimmie Winter]]** — The four-year-old son of John and Lucy, whose illness is the central crisis
- **[[lucy-winter|Lucy Winter]]** — Dimmie's mother, who bears the primary burden of worry and care
- **[[john-winter|John Winter]]** — Dimmie's father, a young architect at [[winter-and-sprague|Winter & Sprague]]
- **[[nannie-merwent|Nannie Merwent]]** — Staying at Rosedene, assists Lucy during the crisis
- **[[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]]** — Lucy's mother, whose presence adds tension to the domestic crisis

## Aftermath

The illness crisis deepens tensions between Lucy and Mrs. Lockhart, whose domineering presence at Rosedene has been a source of friction. It also strengthens the bond between Lucy and Nannie, who proves herself a reliable support during the emergency. The episode underscores the vulnerability of the young Winter family's carefully built domestic life.
