## Overview

Mrs. Lockhart is [[nannie-merwent|Nannie Merwent]]'s [[motherhood|mother]] and the matriarch of the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] [[domestic-life|household]] in Russellville. A woman of forceful personality and unyielding opinions, she manages the practical affairs of her family after her husband's financial decline, arranging [[arthur-merwent|Arthur Merwent]]'s entry into the [[domestic-life|household]] and later overseeing [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s failed marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] and divorce with a "grim neutrality" [004-023]. After the Sheldons acquire the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] home, she negotiates to remain there and becomes an ally of [[professor-walsh]] in man[[youth-and-aging|aging]] — and delaying — his [[courtship-and-romance|courtship]] of [[nannie-merwent]]. Her [[death-and-mortality|death]] after "four years of this regime" [009-026] leaves [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] without her primary source of authority and direction.

## Biography

Mrs. Lockhart first appears as the practical counterweight to her husband's financial ineptitude. When her husband's "insignificant fortune" dwindles, she takes charge: "Mrs. Lockhart was an efficient person, however, and brought to bear upon the situation many of the practical qualities in which her husband was lacking" [004-014]. She "exercised her authority as decisively as was her custom" [004-014] and imposed financial discipline on the [[domestic-life|household]], giving her daughter [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] "to understand that, for the time being at least, she might enjoy few [[appearance-and-looks|dress]]es and fewer parties" [004-014].

When [[arthur-merwent]] arrives in Russellville, Mrs. Lockhart sees an opportunity. Learning that "Merwent held expectations of inheriting some [[money-and-class|money]]," and finding him "attractive and a stranger, it was soon arranged that [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] should live at the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] home" [004-019]. She initially preserves "a grim neutrality" regarding the developing [[courtship-and-romance|romance]] between [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] and [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] [004-023], but relents after a friend's enthusiastic report on the Merwent family, and her "negative aloofness became encour[[youth-and-aging|age]][[gender-roles|men]]t" [004-023].

On [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s wedding day, when the bride is discovered in distress, Mrs. Lockhart "was unable to elicit any explanation" and summons [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] [004-030]. She attributes [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s tears to a "perfectly natural result of her high strung state" and invokes the occasion's significance: "You must remember this is the last day of her girlhood" [004-035]. At the ceremony, she "shed a few impressive tears, glancing somewhat apprehensively from time to time at her daughter's unresponsive features" [004-045].

When the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] home is lost and acquired by "[[cousin-minnie|Cousin Minnie]]" Sheldon, Mrs. Lockhart — "who on previous occasions had not hesitated to express her own disapproval of '[[cousin-minnie|Cousin Minnie]]'" — makes "feeble and unconvincing protestations" before accepting "a grudging offer which allowed her to remain in the old place on the bounty of its new owner" [005-012].

As [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] to [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] deteriorates, Mrs. Lockhart becomes "bitter toward her daughter and made no effort toward consolation" [008-003]. She is pragmatic about [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s situation, at one point suggesting that [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] could claim her [[appearance-and-looks|dress]]es were new arrivals when she had finished them: "She could put it to them that it was a kind of favor, and if you'd keep your mouth shut nobody would be the worse for it" [008-005].

Mrs. Lockhart takes an active interest in her granddaughter [[lucy-winter]]'s social prospects. She brings [[kingsley-dodd]] to call on [[lucy-winter|Lucy]], explaining to [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] that "Young Kingsley is greatly taken with [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]" and noting that "all [[judge-dodd|Judge Dodd]]'s [[money-and-class|money]] will go to Kingsley" [006-036]. She presses [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] to use "sense and firmness" [006-039] in man[[youth-and-aging|aging]] [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s romantic life. When [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] visits her, Mrs. Lockhart arranges a dance in her honor, though [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] quietly resists [006-099].

In [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s post-divorce years, Mrs. Lockhart becomes, along with [[professor-walsh]], one of the two poles around which [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s dependent existence revolves. She forbids "any further relation with such an undutiful daughter" after [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] to [[john-winter]], and [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]], "overawed by the old lady and more or less dependent on her, dared not disobey" [009-023].

Mrs. Lockhart forms an unspoken alliance with [[professor-walsh|Professor Walsh]], who pays her "much special and flattering attention" as she grows "rapidly deaf and would otherwise have been lonely" [009-024]. She "employed all the ingenuity of a jealous and neglected old woman to curb impatience on the p[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] of her niece and nephew, while [[professor-walsh|Professor Walsh]], who was already receiving for nothing the benefits that [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] represented, constantly abetted the old lady's wiles in so subtle a manner that [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] hardly realized her own position" [009-025].

After "four years of this regime," Mrs. Lockhart dies [009-026], leaving [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] alone in the Sheldon house "with no one upon whom she could persuade herself she had the semblance of a claim" [009-026].

## Relationships

**[[nannie-merwent]]** — Mrs. Lockhart's daughter and primary object of her managerial instincts. Mrs. Lockhart arranges [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] to [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]], watches its dissolution with bitter detachment, and in [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s post-divorce years exercises authority over her daughter through financial and emotional dependency. Their relationship is characterized by [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s fear of her [[motherhood|mother]]'s judgment. When [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] rebels, [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] grows "afraid of Mrs. Lockhart's comments on family difficulties" [008-055].

**[[lucy-winter]]** — Mrs. Lockhart's granddaughter. Their relationship is marked by Mrs. Lockhart's attempts to control [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s social life and marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] prospects, and [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s quiet resistance. Mrs. Lockhart surveys [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s "reddened cheeks and well developed figure with approval" [006-015] and attempts to arrange a match with [[kingsley-dodd|Kingsley Dodd]], but [[lucy-winter|Lucy]] refuses to be managed.

**[[professor-walsh]]** — A strategic alliance. Walsh pays Mrs. Lockhart "much special and flattering attention" [009-024], while she uses her influence to delay any resolution of his relationship with [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]], "curbing impatience on the p[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] of her niece and nephew" [009-025]. Together they maintain [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] in a state of suspended dependency.

**[[arthur-merwent]]** — Mrs. Lockhart arranges for [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] to live in the Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] home and encourages his [[courtship-and-romance|courtship]] of [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] after hearing favorable reports of his family's prospects [004-019].

**Mr. Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]]** — Mrs. Lockhart's husband, a man of poor financial judgment. "Mrs. Lockhart was an efficient person, however, and brought to bear upon the situation many of the practical qualities in which her husband was lacking" [004-014]. She is "not accustomed to take his point of view seriously" [004-023].

**[[cousin-minnie]]** — A grudging relationship. Despite having previously expressed disapproval of [[cousin-minnie|Cousin Minnie]], Mrs. Lockhart negotiates to remain in the old Lockh[[art-and-aesthetics|art]] home after [[cousin-minnie|Cousin Minnie]] acquires it [005-012].

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Lockhart embodies the practical, domineering matriarch of the Southern small-town social order. Her defining characteristic is her decisiveness — she "exercised her authority as decisively as was her custom" [004-014] — and her man[[youth-and-aging|age]][[gender-roles|men]]t of family affairs reflects a worldview in which [[money-and-class|social standing]] and financial security are paramount. She sees [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] to [[arthur-merwent|Arthur]] as a financial arrangement ("Merwent held expectations of inheriting some [[money-and-class|money]]" [004-019]), [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s potential match with [[kingsley-dodd|Kingsley Dodd]] as an inheritance opportunity ("all [[judge-dodd|Judge Dodd]]'s [[money-and-class|money]] will go to Kingsley" [006-036]), and her own living situation as a negotiation to be managed with pragmatic self-interest.

Her relationship with [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] reveals the novel's concern with the emotional costs of dependency. Mrs. Lockhart's "grim neutrality" [004-023] during her daughter's marital crisis, her bitter withdrawal of consolation after [[the-divorce-scandal|the divorce]], and her manipulation of [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s dependent position all contribute to [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s chronic inability to act independently. Mrs. Lockhart's [[death-and-mortality|death]] after "four years of this regime" [009-026] does not liberate [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] but rather transfers her dependency to new figures — [[professor-walsh|Professor Walsh]], the Sheldons — who fill the void of authority Mrs. Lockhart leaves behind.

Her alliance with [[professor-walsh|Professor Walsh]] is the novel's most explicit depiction of how self-interest operates within family structures. Walsh and Mrs. Lockhart, without explicit coordination, maintain a system in which Walsh receives "for nothing the benefits that [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] represented" [009-025] while Mrs. Lockhart prevents her daughter from forcing the marri[[youth-and-aging|age]] question. The arrangement serves both parties at [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]]'s expense.
