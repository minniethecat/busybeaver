## Overview

After [[lucy-winter|Lucy Winter]] leaves for Chicago, [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] writes a furious letter accusing Lucy of conspiring against her. The letter reveals Nannie's deteriorating emotional state under [[miss-brennan|Cousin Minnie]]'s control and marks a low point in the relationship between the two women.

## Narrative Context

Following [[mrs-lockhart-death|Mrs. Lockhart's death]], Nannie found herself trapped in Cousin Minnie's household, isolated from the one person — Lucy — she trusted. When Lucy departed for Chicago for "an indefinite stay," Nannie interpreted the departure as abandonment and betrayal:

> Nannie at once wrote furiously to Lucy accusing her of plotting [008-027]

The letter reflects Nannie's paranoia, born of genuine vulnerability. Having lost her husband, her mother, and her autonomy, Nannie's accusation against Lucy is less about actual grievance than about the terror of being left alone with Cousin Minnie.

## Key Participants

- **[[nannie-merwent|Nannie Merwent]]** — Isolated and desperate, lashing out at the person she most depends on
- **[[lucy-winter|Lucy Winter]]** — The recipient of the letter, whose departure to Chicago triggers Nannie's panic
- **[[miss-brennan|Cousin Minnie Sheldon]]** — The oppressive presence whose control drives Nannie to desperation

## Aftermath

The letter strains but does not break the bond between Nannie and Lucy. Lucy understands that the fury is misdirected — Nannie's real enemy is Cousin Minnie and the system of female dependence that leaves her without recourse. The episode strengthens Lucy's resolve to offer Nannie refuge at Rosedene, setting the stage for [[nannie-arrival|Nannie's Arrival at Rosedene]].
