## Overview

Pride and vanity form the psychological foundation of _Blind Mice_'s central conflict. [[nannie-merwent]] is the novel's primary vehicle for exploring these qualities: her obsessive attention to appearance, her need for admiration, and her conviction of her own superiority are the engines that drive the narrative's destruction. But pride is not confined to Nannie — [[john-winter|John Winter]]'s artistic vanity makes him susceptible to her flattery, and the novel traces how pride, in its various expressions, becomes the solvent that dissolves the bonds of marriage, family, and friendship.

## Nannie Merwent: A Study in Vanity

### The Mirror and the Self

Nannie's relationship to her own appearance is the novel's most sustained portrait of vanity. From childhood, "her first pleasure was to attract attention" (004-004), and this need for external validation persists unchanged into adulthood. The most telling detail is her habitual gesture: "Nannie never opened the back door to him without first looking into the mirror" (005-019). The mirror is not an instrument of vanity — it is the source of self. Nannie exists only when she is being seen, and her reflection is the one audience that never leaves.

As an adult, impoverished but still performing gentility, "she exerted all her ingenuity in making an appearance that belied her pocket book" (008-004). Appearance precedes substance; display precedes truth. Nannie's vanity is not superficial in the ordinary sense — it is existential. Without the performance of refinement, there is no self beneath.

### Pride of Birth

Nannie's vanity is rooted in pride of birth — the conviction, absorbed from childhood, that the Lockhart name conferred an inherent superiority. She was raised in a family "considered to be aristocratic and distinguished" (004-001), and though the money that supported that distinction has vanished, the pride remains. She was "saucy to white children supposed not to be her social equals whom, in imitation of her elders, she designated as 'trash'" (004-005).

This inherited pride is the source of Nannie's most destructive qualities: her inability to accept middle-class domesticity, her contempt for the servants she depends upon, and her conviction that the world has denied her what she was promised. The Lockhart pride, stripped of its economic foundation, becomes a comprehensive resentment that Nannie directs at everyone who has what she has lost.

## John Winter: Artistic Vanity

John Winter's vanity takes a different form — the pride of the misunderstood artist. He resents "grubbing for bread" and "designing ugly cottages for fools" (001-057) because these activities conflict with his self-image as a creative sensibility trapped in a commercial world. This artistic pride makes him lethally susceptible to Nannie's flattery: when she praises his watercolors as "simply exquisite" (003-092) and laments that he lacks "money so you could follow your natural bent and be an artist" (003-088), she is feeding the vanity that Lucy's practicality cannot satisfy.

The novel is unsparing in its diagnosis: John's artistic pride is not a strength but a weakness — the point at which his character can be manipulated. His vanity makes him see Nannie as a kindred spirit rather than a predator, and by the time he recognizes the destruction she has wrought, it is too late.

## Pride and Its Costs

| Character | Form of Pride | Consequence |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| [[nannie-merwent]] | Pride of birth, vanity of appearance | Inability to form genuine relationships; comprehensive resentment |
| [[john-winter]] | Artistic pride, vanity of sensibility | Susceptibility to flattery; estrangement from Lucy |
| [[lucy-winter]] | Self-respect (the positive form of pride) | Refusal to be broken, even as her marriage crumbles |

Lucy Winter's pride is the novel's counter-example — not vanity but self-respect, acknowledged even by her father: "You seem to have all the self-respect in the family, Lucy" (007-011). This is pride as dignity, and the novel suggests it is the only form of pride that can survive the corrosion of domestic warfare.
