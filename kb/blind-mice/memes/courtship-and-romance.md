# Courtship and Romance

## Overview

Romantic love and its pursuit — courtship — form the emotional engine of *Blind Mice*, propelling its characters into marriages both happy and disastrous, and generating the novel's deepest tensions. C. Kay Scott examines courtship not as a single ideal but as a spectrum of behaviors ranging from the mercenary to the idealized, from the impulsive to the long-suffering. The novel presents courtship as a test of character: each character's approach to romantic love reveals the quality of their heart, the nature of their ambitions, and their capacity for genuine devotion. Across two generations — the Lockhart-Merwent courtship that produced Lucy, and the Winter-Merwent-Sprague triangle that defines Lucy's adult life — the novel traces how romantic choices echo through decades, binding or breaking the lives they touch.

## Role in the Novel

Courtship and romance in *Blind Mice* unfold across several distinct narrative arcs, each illuminating a different dimension of romantic love.

### Nannie Lockhart and Arthur Merwent: The Mercenary Courtship

The novel's earliest courtship — that of Nannie Lockhart and Arthur Merwent — is presented as a calculated transaction on one side and a quietly determined pursuit on the other. Mrs. Lockhart "had determined that Nannie should find salvation for the family by attracting a husband whose pretensions to that distinction should rest on a solid financial basis" (004-016). When the young lawyer Arthur Merwent arrives in Russellville, the courtship machinery activates: Nannie deploys "saucy and capricious" strategies, "demanding services and attentions calculated to convince him of her superior birth and position" (004-022), while Arthur's "imperturbable" gravity masks a steady advance (004-023).

Nannie, "who shared the common conviction that Arthur was a rising young lawyer and a desirable catch, consented to become engaged" (004-024) — but the word "consented" is significant. This is not passion but calculation. Nannie immediately "indulged in coquettish tricks of a number and variety that her fiance found disconcerting" (004-025), treating the engagement as a game to be prolonged. Arthur, with his characteristic directness, "refused to be played with longer, and, after exhausting her ingenuity in the endeavor to gain more delay, she fixed the wedding day" (004-026). The pattern established here — Nannie's manipulative charm, Arthur's cold resolve — predicts the marriage's failure. The wedding journey reveals the truth: "over some slight misunderstanding at a hotel, Nannie had given way to a hysterical fit of passion that amazed and dismayed her husband" (005-003). The romance ends almost before it begins.

### Lucy Merwent and John Winter: The Idealistic Romance

Lucy and John's courtship represents the novel's central example of romantic love that is genuine but tested by the realities of life. John, studying at the Art School in Chicago, announces to Jim "that he had fallen in love, and by raving in a school boy fashion over the object of his devotion" (013-027). His enthusiasm is endearingly innocent — Jim, the more practical man, immediately asks "what are you going to do to support a wife?" (013-029), and John's earnest reply — "Jim, I'm through with experiments. I'm going to work" (013-030) — marks his entry into adult responsibility.

Their wedding is simple and modest — "Miss Storms has asked us to be married at her flat, but we shall have a very simple wedding" (009-005) — a deliberate contrast to the elaborate ceremony Nannie had demanded. John's parents cannot attend, though "Mrs. Winter, a kindly sentimental lady...beamed the fact that John and Lucy did not come to the home town and so make it possible for Dr. Winter himself to officiate at the wedding of his only son" (009-010). The early years of their marriage are marked by the warmth of genuine affection: John's tender reassurance to Lucy — "Why, sweetheart, I love you. I only like other people" (003-158) — captures the quality of a romance grounded in devotion rather than display.

Yet the novel does not sentimentalize even its most sympathetic romance. John's "hazy outline of a woman seen through a mist of sex idealism" (028-035), as Jim later describes it, means that John loves an image of Lucy rather than the full reality of her. His susceptibility to flattery from Nannie and Miss Brennan reveals a weakness in his romantic character: he responds to admiration wherever it comes, and his love for Lucy, however genuine, lacks the steel of full understanding.

### Jim Sprague and Lucy Winter: The Long-Burning Devotion

The novel's most powerful romantic current runs beneath the surface of the Winter marriage: Jim Sprague's growing love for Lucy. From their first meeting, Jim recognized "you were the most desirable woman I had ever seen" (028-035), but his devotion was self-disciplined: "I was proud to give you this honor in my heart, and I was prouder that I gave it to you as you really were" (028-035). Unlike John's idealized affections, Jim's love is rooted in clear-eyed perception — he sees Lucy fully, without the "rose-colored spectacles" (013-039) through which John views the world.

This long-suppressed feeling builds across years of friendship and shared domestic life until it finally erupts in chapter 28: "I've stood all I can... I've never been too honest with myself about you, Lucy, but I knew I loved you a long time ago. I've resented seeing your youth slipping by, and John not noticing it" (028-019). Jim's declaration — "I want you. And I don't want just a mystical idea of you either" (028-019) — is both a confession and a critique, contrasting his own embodied love with John's disembodied idealism.

Lucy's response is less rejection than deflection: "Stop, Jim. I've trusted you, too, and you have no right to talk this way" (028-024). The appeal to trust, rather than to the sanctity of marriage or the absence of feeling, leaves the romantic tension unresolved. Jim's love remains the novel's great unanswered question — a devotion that cannot be consummated without destroying the domestic world it has helped sustain.

Earlier, in chapter 26, Jim had come closer to speaking: "Lucy, if you knew how much I — prize your friendship — how much I —" (026-206), and Lucy's breathless "Don't — Jim —" (026-207) confirms that she understands what remains unspoken. The novel traces a romance of what cannot be said, a courtship conducted in silences and unfinished sentences.

### Jim Sprague and Delia: The Thwarted Romance

A secondary romantic thread involves Jim's relationship with Delia, a working-class woman with whom he becomes involved. Jim, characteristically earnest, insists: "Delia, we must be married at once" (012-142). But Delia, more pragmatic about the economic realities of marriage, demurs: "We can't get married now, Jim. You haven't anything to support us and if I marry I'll lose all the money Johnson left me" (012-153). She offers a different model of romantic life: "Let's just love each other, Jim, and everything will come out all right" (012-155).

This subplot highlights the economic constraints on courtship for those without middle-class security, and Jim's eventual discovery of his father visiting Delia's house (012-165) adds a layer of betrayal that poisons the romance. The Delia subplot serves as a dark mirror to Jim's idealized love for Lucy — it is messy, compromised, and entangled with questions of class, money, and respectability that the novel's more ethereal romanticism cannot escape.

### Nannie Merwent's Later Romances

Nannie's romantic life after divorce continues the pattern of manipulation and self-deception. Her strategic pursuit of Professor Walsh — visiting his office at night, relying on "his devotion as a weapon of defense against the continual disapprobation" of her relatives (008-034) — repeats the courtship patterns of her youth. Later, her announcement through Lucy that "Mother is going to be married" (033-041), followed by John's horrified reaction — "Married to that —!" (028-126) — suggests that Nannie's romantic choices continue to be judged harshly even by those sympathetic to her.

Nannie's earlier attempt to arrange a financially advantageous match for Lucy with Kingsley, the heir of Judge Dodd — "If you marry your daughter to the heir of Judge Dodd it isn't too late to get back where you ought to be" (006-039) — demonstrates that she has learned nothing from her own disastrous experience. She seeks to replicate the mercenary courtship in the next generation, and Lucy's resistance marks a crucial break in the mother-daughter relationship.

## Key Passages

> "She had determined that Nannie should find salvation for the family by attracting a husband whose pretensions to that distinction should rest on a solid financial basis." (004-016)

Mrs. Lockhart's explicit framing of courtship as financial strategy establishes the mercenary logic that will govern Nannie's approach to romance. The word "salvation" is bitterly ironic: the salvation sought is entirely material, and the spiritual bankruptcy of this approach reveals itself across the subsequent narrative.

> "Why, sweetheart, I love you. I only like other people." (003-158)

John's tender reassurance to the jealous Lucy — spoken on the first day of Nannie's visit to their home — is the novel's most direct expression of romantic devotion within marriage. The simple distinction between "love" and "like" encapsulates a view of romance as exclusive, committed, and fundamentally different in kind from all other affections.

> "When I saw you I gave you the devotion of my soul that — I needed somebody, Lucy — that I had been trying to give him, because you were worthy of it. I knew you were the most desirable woman I had ever seen and I acknowledged this to myself without cant." (028-035)

Jim's confession to Lucy in chapter 28 reveals the architecture of his romantic feeling. His devotion, originally directed toward John in boyhood friendship, was transferred to Lucy when he recognized her "worthiness." The phrase "without cant" signals the honesty and self-knowledge that distinguish Jim's love from John's sentimentalism.

> "I've stood all I can... I want you. And I don't want just a mystical idea of you either. I love you, dear, and you won't have to be afraid of my ever stopping." (028-019)

The climactic romantic confession of the novel. Jim's insistence on the physical, embodied reality of his desire — "I don't want just a mystical idea of you" — directly challenges John's disembodied romantic idealism. The promise "you won't have to be afraid of my ever stopping" is both assurance and declaration: Jim's love, unlike John's wavering affections, is permanent.

> "We can't get married now, Jim. You haven't anything to support us and if I marry I'll lose all the money Johnson left me." (012-153)

Delia's frank acknowledgment of the economic constraints on marriage brings the novel's romantic idealism down to earth. Her alternative — "Let's just love each other, Jim, and everything will come out all right" (012-155) — proposes a romance outside the institution of marriage, a possibility the novel explores but ultimately cannot endorse as a viable path for its male protagonist.

> "Lucy, if you knew how much I — prize your friendship — how much I —" "Don't — Jim —" [026-206:026-207]

This exchange in chapter 26 is the novel's most compressed expression of the romantic tension between Jim and Lucy. The broken syntax, the unfinished declarations, and Lucy's swift interruption convey the unspeakable — a love that cannot be named without destroying the domestic peace it has protected for years.

## Related Characters

- [[Lucy-Winter]] — The object of two very different kinds of romantic devotion: John's idealized affection and Jim's clear-eyed, long-suffering love. Her own romantic choices — marrying John, remaining loyal to him despite his failures — define the novel's moral center
- [[John-Winter]] — Whose "school boy" (013-027) romantic enthusiasm for Lucy represents a sincere but limited form of love, susceptible to distraction and flattery, and ultimately tested by his susceptibility to Nannie's influence
- [[James-Sprague]] — The novel's most devoted lover, whose love for Lucy begins at first sight, grows through years of suppressed longing, and finally erupts in a confession that threatens the domestic world it would save
- [[Nannie-Merwent]] — The novel's cautionary example of courtship as manipulation, whose romantic life cycles repeatedly through the same pattern of strategic charm, escalating demands, and spectacular collapse
- [[Arthur-Merwent]] — The quiet suitor who "gravely declared himself" (004-023) and whose courtship of Nannie, conducted with the same methodical precision as his legal practice, ends in divorce and a happier remarriage
- [[Mrs. Lockhart]] — The architect of the mercenary approach to courtship that dooms her daughter's first marriage and echoes into the next generation through her attempt to arrange Lucy's match with Kingsley
- [[Miss-Storms]] — The facilitator of John and Lucy's wedding (009-005), whose steady friendship and good judgment provide a counterpoint to the novel's more turbulent romantic plots

## Thematic Connections

Courtship and romance in *Blind Mice* intersect with the novel's broader thematic architecture at multiple points. The way characters court reveals their relationship to **social class**: Nannie and her mother pursue wealthy suitors as a means of class mobility, while Lucy and John's modest wedding and early poverty reflect a romance that prioritizes character over status. The novel's treatment of romance also illuminates its concern with **appearance versus reality**: John's idealized love for Lucy cannot withstand the pressure of Nannie's flattery, while Jim's clear-eyed devotion — founded on seeing Lucy "as you really were" (028-035) — proves more durable.

The theme connects to **marriage** as both outcome and test of courtship: the novel argues implicitly that the character of a courtship predicts the quality of the marriage it produces. Nannie's manipulative engagement leads to a marriage of emotional warfare; Lucy and John's earnest romance leads to a marriage that, while tested, endures. Jim's unconsummated courtship of Lucy — a romance conducted in the space between friendship and confession — remains the novel's most powerful romantic current precisely because it cannot find resolution within the institution of marriage.

Finally, courtship intersects with **gender and power**: Nannie's coquettish tricks, Delia's economic pragmatism, John's romantic idealism, and Jim's disciplined desire each represent different positions within the gender economy of early 20th century America. The novel exposes the constraints — economic, social, and moral — that shape who can court whom, on what terms, and with what consequences.
