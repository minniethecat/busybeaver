Bob White, born Roberta White, is a minor background character in *Blind Mice*,
appearing only in the extended flashback of Chapter IV that recounts Nannie Merwent's
adolescence in Russellville. Her narrative function is twofold: as an early object
of Nannie's manipulative friendship and as a mirror for Nannie's competitive social instincts.

## Introduction and Friendship

Roberta White is introduced as "an admiring girl friend at the private school which
they both attended" whom Nannie "picked out for condescending notice" and "made a chum of her"
(004-013). Scott notes that Roberta "was far from pretty, and could be patronized, but was
not unintelligent, and possessed considerable personality" (004-013). The friendship dynamic
is established entirely on Nannie's terms: Roberta serves as an audience and foil, her plainness
highlighting Nannie's charms by contrast.

## The Rivalry

The relationship sours when "Nannie's first boy admirer soon transferred his callow devotion
to 'Bob White,' as Nannie had dubbed Roberta" (004-013). The nickname itself — masculine,
breezy, decidedly unromantic — is a small act of diminishment on Nannie's part. Scott records
that "there was a curious scene in which Bob White was forever disowned, and Nannie ever after,
in referring to it, spoke of Roberta's 'ingratitude'" (004-013). The word "ingratitude"
is revealing: Nannie frames the defection not as a romantic disappointment but as a breach of
feudal loyalty, casting herself as the betrayed patron.

Nannie's response to Bob White's social success is characteristically adaptive. "Not to be cut
out of the gaieties in which the once envious Bob White was participating, Nannie astonished no
one more than her mother by displaying considerable taste and talent in the improvization of very
effective frocks" (004-015). The rivalry spurs Nannie toward the sartorial creativity that will
become one of her defining skills — dressing above her means through instinct rather than
craftsmanship.

## Later Appearance

Bob White reappears at the Lockhart reception following Arthur Merwent's death, "being herself
engaged" (004-045). Nannie greets her "with effusive sadness and clasped her in an embrace that
was like a despairing renewal of devotion" (004-045). The performance is pitched perfectly —
public, emotional, impossible to refuse — and Scott's narrator dryly notes that moments later
Nannie "went off to prepare for travel and he [Arthur, in context, another guest] was able to
excuse himself" (004-045). The embrace, like the original friendship, lasts exactly as long as
Nannie requires it to.

## Narrative Significance

Bob White serves an early demonstration of a pattern that recurs throughout the novel: Nannie
cultivates relationships as instruments, discards them when they cease to serve, and performs
emotional intensity when an audience is present. With only three appearances — confined to a
single chapter — Bob White is among the novel's most minor figures. Her thin presence in the
text is itself informative: like most of Nannie's "friends," she exists only in relation to
Nannie and vanishes from the narrative the moment Nannie no longer needs her.
