## Overview

Following the death of [[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]], [[nannie-merwent|Nannie Merwent]] finds herself under the guardianship of "Cousin Minnie" Sheldon, a distant relative who assumes control of the Lockhart household. The confrontation between Nannie and Cousin Minnie marks Nannie's assertion of independence against a domineering surrogate authority figure.

## Narrative Context

Mrs. Lockhart's death leaves Nannie in a precarious position. Having already suffered through the collapse of her marriage to [[arthur-merwent|Arthur Merwent]], Nannie now faces the loss of her primary protector and the imposition of Cousin Minnie's control:

> After four years of this regime Mrs. Lockhart died, and Nannie was left in "Cousin Minnie's" care [009-026]

Cousin Minnie Sheldon, a distant relation, steps into the vacuum left by Mrs. Lockhart's death with a proprietary attitude toward both the Lockhart name and Nannie herself. The confrontation crystallizes around Nannie's refusal to accept Cousin Minnie's authority over her life and decisions.

## Key Participants

- **[[nannie-merwent|Nannie Merwent]]** — The young widow, grieving and vulnerable but determined to assert her autonomy
- **[[miss-brennan|Cousin Minnie Sheldon]]** — A distant Lockhart relation who seizes authority after Mrs. Lockhart's death
- **[[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]]** — Deceased, but her legacy and the precedents she set shape the confrontation
- **[[lucy-winter|Lucy Winter]]** — Nannie's sister-in-law, whose own struggles with Mrs. Lockhart provide a parallel

## Aftermath

> "Why, Cousin Minnie—" began Nannie, tearfully. "Why—why—" Nannie faltered. [009-030, 009-032]

The confrontation marks a turning point in Nannie's emotional development. Having been dominated first by her late husband and then by Mrs. Lockhart, Nannie begins to find her voice. The event foreshadows her later independence and her growing bond with [[lucy-winter|Lucy Winter]], who shares her experience of resisting Lockhart family control.
