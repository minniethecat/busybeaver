## Overview

The Merwent house in Russellville is the domestic setting for [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] and [[arthur-merwent|Arthur Merwent]]'s marriage. Unlike [[the-winter-house|the Winter house]] at Rosedene, which represents domestic harmony and refuge, the Merwent house embodies marital failure, financial decline, and the weight of [[mrs-lockhart|Mrs. Lockhart]]'s thwarted social ambitions.

## Description

> The windows of the Merwents' house were open and as the visitor approached she could hear voices raised in dispute. [006-106]

The house is located in Russellville, part of the Lockhart family's diminishing property. Arthur lived there with the Lockharts after the marriage [004-019] — an arrangement that surrendered his autonomy and fed the couple's conflicts. After Mrs. Lockhart's death and the divorce, the house passes out of the family's control.

## Significance in the Narrative

The Merwent house is a space defined by what it lacks: privacy from Mrs. Lockhart's oversight, the warmth that characterizes the Winter household, and the financial security to maintain appearances. It is the stage for [[nannie-arthur-quarrel|Nannie and Arthur's quarrels]] and the suffocating atmosphere that drives Nannie first to desperation and then to flight.

After [[mrs-lockhart-death|Mrs. Lockhart's death]], the house falls under [[miss-brennan|Cousin Minnie]]'s control, becoming a place of confinement rather than home. Nannie's eventual departure for Rosedene marks her escape from the house's oppressive associations.
