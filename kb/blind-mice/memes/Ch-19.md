##### XIX

[019-001] “[[john-winter|John]] said he was going to Benton Harbor today,” [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] observed to [[lucy-winter|Lucy]], who was seated near a window, darning [[dimmie-winter|Dimmie]]’s socks. She had just washed her hair and come down to the sunny dining room to dry it.

[019-002] “Did he?” Lucy raised her eyes in involuntary surprise, but glanced quickly down at her sewing.

[019-003] “Yes,” continued Nannie, seating herself in a comfortable rocker. “He is going to see somebody about a house decoration contract.”

[019-004] “Well, he’ll be back for dinner, won’t he?” Lucy inquired.

[019-005] “Oh, yes. But he’ll be late, he said. He wanted me to go with him but I was afraid of getting seasick. It’s a beautiful day after all, though!” and she gazed out the window regretfully. “I have to look through a trunk, anyway,” she added, as if comforting herself.

[019-006] “I didn’t know he was going,” said Lucy.

[019-007] “He was worried last night and I guess he forgot to tell you.”

[019-008] Lucy began to sew with renewed determination.

[019-009] “I was right sorry for John last night,” Mrs. Merwent declared. “You should be more careful what you say, Lucy.”

[019-010] “What do you mean, Mamma? What have I said?”

[019-011] “Why, things that make John feel that—that—why, about Mr. [[james-sprague|Sprague]].”

[019-012] “What about him?”

[019-013] Nannie’s gaze fell before Lucy’s.

[019-014] “Nothing in particular, except that you show so plainly that you—”

[019-015] “That I what?” Lucy demanded almost fiercely.

[019-016] “Well, that you are interested in him,” murmured Nannie, with attempted calmness, adding at once, “of course I know that there is nothing between you and Mr. Sprague, but you should consider—” She stopped abruptly.

[019-017] “Consider what my husband might think, I suppose,” interrupted Lucy hotly.

[019-018] “Well, when you take his part against John and me you must admit that you give people a right to think things.”

[019-019] “You, and not John, were the one who tried to queer Jim, and I would have taken the part of anyone under the circumstances,” returned Lucy indignantly, “and if you want to think evil of it, you can do so.”

[019-020] “Well, John agreed with everything I said,” defended Nannie.

[019-021] “Then, if he wants to think badly of me, he can too!” Lucy exclaimed angrily. “It would evidently suit you if he did.”

[019-022] “How can you say that, Lucy, when I was trying to save you from just such things! That’s the gratitude a [[motherhood|mother]] gets—especially if she’s forgiven a lot in her child,” complained Nannie in a tremulous voice.

[019-023] “Most of the evil you are always so anxious to save me from only exists in your own mind, Mamma. I would be much more grateful if you would quit thinking of nasty things to forgive me for,” retorted Lucy bitterly.

[019-024] “Well,” replied Mrs. Merwent, “I’m sure I’ve done my duty. I didn’t want John to get suspicious, but—”

[019-025] “That’s why you keep suggesting vile interpretations of innocent things to him!” interpolated Lucy disgustedly, springing to her feet and gathering up her mending.

[019-026] “Why, Lucy,” began Nannie, on the verge of tears. But Lucy went into the hall, and ascended the stairs.

[019-027] A few moments later she returned with her hat and coat on.

[019-028] “I’m going out,” she announced. Her voice was harsh. She paused in the doorway.

[019-029] Nannie looked up and met her daughter’s eyes uncomfortably.

[019-030] “Where are you going?” she asked finally, pulling at the handkerchief in her lap with uncertain fingers.

[019-031] “I’m going to see Jim.”

[019-032] “Lucy!” gasped Mrs. Merwent as the front door closed after her daughter.

[019-033] Jim was alone and hard at work when Lucy reached the office. The windows were open and a breeze fluttered the papers on his desk.

[019-034] “Sit down,” he invited, leaning back in his chair and reaching for his pipe. “How are you all getting along at your house? Where’s the kid?”

[019-035] “Dimmie’s at kindergarten.”

[019-036] “Didn’t know he’d started to go,” said Jim.

[019-037] “Lots of things have happened since you were out,” remarked Lucy quietly.

[019-038] “Yes?” Jim lighted his pipe.

[019-039] Lucy did not say more and Jim smoked a moment in silence.

[019-040] “What is it, Lucy? Are you worried about something?” he inquired at last, blowing out a cloud of smoke.

[019-041] Lucy nodded her head without speaking.

[019-042] “Well, [[gender-roles|women]] always worry about one of two things, [[money-and-class|money]], or another woman,” pursued Jim in a bantering tone. “Which is it?”

[019-043] “Both,” said Lucy seriously.

[019-044] Jim looked grave, and when he spoke again it was with a different manner.

[019-045] “Can I help, Lucy?”

[019-046] “I think you can, Jim. That’s why I’ve come.”

[019-047] He waited for her to go on.

[019-048] “John is spending too much, Jim, and Mamma encourages him in it,” Lucy began, rather suddenly, “and things are—are so I can’t do anything. I thought maybe if you—suggested to John—it might—” Lucy’s voice trailed off in embarrassment, and she opened and shut her purse nervously.

[019-049] “I will,” Jim promised earnestly. “You can depend on me, Lucy.”

[019-050] “I know I can, Jim.”

[019-051] “And, Lucy—”

[019-052] “Yes?”

[019-053] “[[About]] the money. If your expenses have swamped you—you know I have something laid up. My living costs me very little, and you are awfully welcome to what I have.”

[019-054] “Thank you, Jim, but you know I couldn’t take any money. Besides, we don’t need it yet, but I am frightened at the amount we are spending.”

[019-055] “I didn’t mean—I mean you could pay it back if you wanted to.” Jim was very red.

[019-056] “I understand, Jim, but it isn’t necessary.”

[019-057] “I don’t see what’s gotten into John!” he ejaculated with feeling.

[019-058] “It isn’t John,” said Lucy, “it’s Mamma.”

[019-059] “Well, he ought to know—” Jim stopped speaking and glanced at Lucy.

[019-060] “Yes, Jim, but so had she.”

[019-061] Jim thought.

[019-062] “Do you know yet if she’s going to stay—permanently?” he inquired after a pause.

[019-063] “I don’t know, Jim. I shouldn’t be surprised. She talks sometimes as though she were eventually going to marry a Professor Walsh, back in Russellville. But I don’t know.”

[019-064] Jim smoked hard for a while. When he spoke again his voice was low.

[019-065] “Lucy, you know I would do anything in the world for you,” he said. “Anything,” he repeated vehemently.

[019-066] “Yes, Jim. You are the best friend that ever was.” Lucy smiled at him frankly. “But I don’t see just how you can help in anything else.”

[019-067] “I’d like to help,” he offered, almost wistfully.

[019-068] “You have,” she declared, still smiling. “I feel a lot better already.”

[019-069] “Do you, Lucy?” he asked eagerly. “Do I—do you—am I—” he stammered, growing red again.

[019-070] “Why, of course you do,” she responded heartily and naturally. “It always does me good to [[gossip-and-reputation|talk]] to you.” Then she added rather plaintively, “Oh, Jim, why can’t people be honest with themselves and with others?”

[019-071] “I’m damned if I know!” he declared savagely, not looking at her.

[019-072] “I’m sure I don’t either,” she said, regarding him affectionately. “Well, goodbye. I’m going home.” She rose and shook hands.

[019-073] “Goodbye, Lucy. You can depend on me.” Jim held her hand.

[019-074] “Thank you, Jim,” she answered, pressing his hand.

[019-075] And she was gone.

[019-076] Mrs. Merwent was at the piano, practising, when Lucy reached home.

[019-077] “Well, did you see Mr. Sprague?” Nannie asked sarcastically, going into the hall when she heard the front door open.

[019-078] “Yes,” returned Lucy calmly.

[019-079] “You ought to be careful, Lucy,” reminded Nannie. “Of course *I* wouldn’t say anything to John, but others might.”

[019-080] “You can say anything you like to him,” retorted Lucy in a disgusted tone. “I’m going to tell him myself.”

[019-081] That night John returned late. Nannie, Lucy, and Dimmie had finished their evening meal, and Lucy had tucked Dimmie safely into bed, when the front door clicked and a cheerful voice was heard in the hall.

[019-082] “Now, Lucy, your croaking was all for nothing,” were John’s first words. “Look there!” he commanded, rushing into the living room and throwing a pile of bills on the table, his manner almost hilarious.

[019-083] “How nice!” murmured Nannie.

[019-084] “Where did that come from?” Lucy inquired quickly.

[019-085] “Why, Jim put over a deal today while I was away, and this is my share of the lucre,” and John put his hands in his pockets and began to whistle.

[019-086] “What kind of a deal was it?” persisted Lucy.

[019-087] “Oh, some private scheme of his. Sold building materials or something on commission,” answered John.

[019-088] Lucy looked nonplussed.

[019-089] “Didn’t Mr. Sprague say anything to you about it?” Nannie inquired of Lucy in an innocent voice.

[019-090] “Why, was Jim out here today?” asked John in surprise.

[019-091] “No. I was at the office,” Lucy explained without hesitation.

[019-092] “Why, he didn’t tell me,” said John, as though mystified. Then, apologetically, “I was only there a minute, Lucy. Went to Benton Harbor on [[business-and-commerce|business]]. I forgot to say anything about going last night.”

[019-093] “Your dinner’s getting cold,” was all Lucy’s reply.

[019-094] As John seated himself at the table a faint cry was heard from the upper floor and Lucy, exclaiming, “That’s Dimmie!” left the room quickly.

[019-095] “John, I wish you would ask Mr. Sprague out again,” requested Nannie, when she thought her daughter was safely upstairs.

[019-096] John looked teasingly at her.

[019-097] “What’s the matter? Are you getting lonesome, or just yearning for more of Jim’s society?”

[019-098] “Will you ask him out?” she repeated.

[019-099] “Yes. Of course I will. Let’s see. Today is Tuesday. I’ll bring him out Thursday night. He’s going to work late tomorrow, I heard him say.”

[019-100] “Now, don’t forget,” warned Nannie.
