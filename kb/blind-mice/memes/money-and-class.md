## Overview

Money and social class form an undercurrent that runs beneath nearly every relationship in _Blind Mice_. The novel examines economic anxiety not as abstract social commentary but as a lived texture of daily life: the [[the-winter-house|Winter household]]'s monthly budgets, the Lockhart family's decline from plantation aristocracy into genteel poverty, and the compromises that every character makes between aspiration and financial reality.

> "Well," insisted Jim, "there's money in such things, and that's what we're after."
> (001-053)

From the opening chapter, where [[james-sprague]] declares his commercial ambition, to the closing pages, where Jim offers [[john-winter|John]] his share of [[winter-and-sprague|the business]] "at a low price -- in fact almost nothing" (036-015), the novel tracks how money both enables and constrains the pursuit of art, love, and domestic happiness.

## Role in the Novel

### The Lockhart-Merwent Decline

The novel's most sustained portrait of class consciousness is the backstory of [[nannie-merwent]], née Anna Lockhart. Born into a southern family "considered to be aristocratic and distinguished," whose members "had usually raised sugar and cotton instead of corn and tobacco" (004-001), Nannie was raised to believe in her own superiority.

> Mrs. Merwent's maiden name was Anna Lockhart. She was born in a southern state, of a family that was considered to be aristocratic and distinguished. None of its members had ever accomplished anything in particular except to be aristocratic, and they had usually raised sugar and cotton instead of corn and tobacco.
> (004-001)

From childhood her whims were gratified, and "it never occurred to her that the numerous things given to her, the clothes, the pony, the negress who nursed her, the rings and brooches, were not a part of her own charm and importance" (004-003). She was "saucy to white children supposed not to be her social equals whom, in imitation of her elders, she designated as 'trash'" (004-005).

> Her first pleasure was to attract attention; whether by doing things that earned her the name of "Tomboy," by being able to show dresses and ribbons finer than her playmates, or by any other device her ingenuity could discover.
> (004-004)

The Lockhart family's fall is swift and complete. Nannie's father "had inherited money which he invested and spent with equal display and absence of judgment" and awoke one day "to find that creditors were impervious both to the dignity of the Lockhart name and the impressiveness of the ancestral mansion" (004-014). Her mother's strategy for family recovery was bluntly transactional:

> She had determined that Nannie should find salvation for the family by attracting a husband whose pretensions to that distinction should rest on a solid financial basis.
> (004-016)

Nannie's marriage to [[arthur-merwent]] was built on this foundation -- Mrs. Lockhart knew "by hearsay at least, that Merwent held expectations of inheriting some money" (004-019).

These expectations collapsed. Arthur's father died "leaving an involved estate, and the hope that Nannie had secretly treasured of inheriting his money died with him" (005-004). Nannie's bitterness was compounded when Arthur "could not afford to buy the Lockhart residence which had to be sold to clear her own father's estate" and the property passed to her hated cousin Minnie Sheldon, becoming [[the-sheldon-house]] (005-011).

After [[lucy-winter|Lucy]]'s departure, "Arthur's law practice, now that he was put to extra expense for Lucy's maintenance and education, barely supported the family, and Nannie began to feel the pinch of poverty more keenly, especially in the matter of her wardrobe" (008-003). Yet Nannie's class pride survived the collapse: she "exerted all her ingenuity, however, in making an appearance that belied her pocket book" (008-004).

### The Winter Middle-Class Stability

The Winters inhabit an economic position that is neither aristocratic nor impoverished but precarious in its own way. John and Lucy live in a "pretty yard" in suburban [[rosedene]] (001-016); they own "a good piano" but cannot afford to upgrade to a player piano (003-114); they can go to the theater but not to a box, as Lucy sharply observes: "We've spent twice as much on theatres already this month as we ought to in half a year!" (017-032).

When Lucy protests the expense of box seats, John dismisses it:

> "Oh, Lucy is having her monthly fret over money matters," John explained with uneasy lightness. "She has us in the poor house about once in so often."
> (018-129)

The difference between the Winters' economic anxiety and the Lockharts' is qualitative: the Winters worry about making do, while the Lockharts worried about maintaining appearances. Lucy's practical management -- tracking expenditures, dismissing a servant accused of theft when "the money disappeared. So did a dollar bill I purposely placed on the table" (021-139) -- is grounded in middle-class values of thrift and accountability. She can say "we can't afford travelling" (003-018) without the shame that such an admission would carry for Nannie, who has internalized the Lockhart cult of aristocratic display.

### John Winter: The Artist and the Dollar

The novel's central economic tension is embodied in [[john-winter|John Winter]]'s divided soul. He is an architect who sees himself as an artist, and he feels the pressure of commerce as a personal degradation:

> "That's the reason I wanted to go to Europe -- to drink in an atmosphere. To live and study where the almighty dollar isn't supreme! I've tried to hang on to ideals, but grubbing for bread first drags you down to designing ugly cottages for fools and ends by offering you an incinerator."
> (001-057)

Lucy shares John's aspirations but grounds them in family obligation: "Yes, money means opportunity to do the things we want to -- for us and for Dimmie" (001-060). For her, money is not an end but a means to the family's flourishing.

[[james-sprague|Jim Sprague]], by contrast, is the novel's unabashed materialist: "I'd run a fertilizer plant if there was money in it" (001-062). His pragmatism complements John's idealism, but it also creates an imbalance in their partnership. [[nannie-merwent|Nannie]] later observes that John "works himself nearly to death, and Mr. Sprague gets half the profits" (018-157). Yet the novel's ending reveals Jim's genuine friendship when he offers John his share of the business "at a low price" and on "liberal terms" (036-015).

### Nannie's Class Performance

Nannie's relationship to money is inseparable from her relationship to class identity. She values things not for their utility but for what they signify. As a child, her "first pleasure was to attract attention" (004-004). As an adult, impoverished but still performing gentility, she "exerted all her ingenuity in making an appearance that belied her pocket book" (008-004). Her approach to money is fundamentally aesthetic rather than economic: display matters more than substance, appearance more than solvency.

This class performance extends to her relationships. She "admires rich people," as [[grace-storms|Miss Storm]] observes, and her fondness for society is well-known (022-145). She treats servants with the hauteur of her plantation upbringing, greeting the Winters' second servant at the front door with "Go 'round to the back door. What is your name?" (022-002). At the same time, her class anxiety makes her vulnerable: left in Cousin Minnie's house after her mother's death "with no one upon whom she could persuade herself she had the semblance of a claim," she is soon disabused of "any doubts that Nannie may have had regarding her status in the 'big house'" (009-026).

## Thematic Connections

- **Class as Performance**: Nannie's wardrobe improvisations, her insistence on social recognition, and her cultivation of wealthy acquaintances all suggest that class is a role one plays rather than a simple economic fact. The Lockhart "name" survives long after the Lockhart money has vanished.

- **Art versus Commerce**: Through John Winter, the novel explores the American artist's predicament -- condemned to serve a market he despises, yet dependent on that market for survival. The tension between his artistic ideals and the "ugly cottages for fools" he designs is never resolved.

- **Inheritance and Decline**: Two inheritances fail in the novel: the Lockhart money (squandered by Mr. Lockhart) and the Merwent money (lost in an involved estate). The failure of intergenerational wealth shapes the circumstances of every major character and drives Nannie's lifelong bitterness.

- **Middle-Class Morality**: Lucy's careful household management, her accountability about money, and her willingness to work hard contrast with Nannie's sense of entitlement. The novel implicitly values the middle-class virtues of thrift, work, and honesty over the aristocratic values of display, leisure, and inherited status.
