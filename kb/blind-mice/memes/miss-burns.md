## Overview

Miss Burns is a public stenographer whose office is located "only a few doors down the hall" from [[james-sprague]]'s place of work [026-014]. She appears in a single brief scene when [[james-sprague|Jim]] goes to dictate a letter but finds her occupied, pacing the corridor while he waits. Though her role is minimal, she represents the novel's quiet acknowledgment of the working [[gender-roles|women]] who populate the professional world beyond the domestic sphere.

## Biography

Miss Burns appears only once, in a brief but vivid vignette. [[james-sprague]] announces, "I'm going to dictate a letter to Miss Burns," and the narrator explains she is "the public stenographer whose office was only a few doors down the hall" [026-014]. [[james-sprague|Jim]] proceeds to her office but finds her already occupied: "[[james-sprague|Jim]] went to Miss Burns' room but, finding her occupied, paced the corridor for a quarter of an hour or more" [026-015].

This is the entirety of Miss Burns's presence in the novel — a working woman, busy at her [[business-and-commerce|trade]], unavailable when a male professional calls on her. The im[[youth-and-aging|age]] of [[james-sprague|Jim]] pacing the corridor while she works serves as a small but telling inversion of the novel's usual [[gender-roles|gender]] dynamics, in which [[gender-roles|women]] wait for [[gender-roles|men]].

## Character Analysis

Miss Burns is one of the novel's most economical character sketches — two sentences that nonetheless convey a complete social portrait. She is a "public stenographer," a working woman in an era when clerical work was one of the few respectable occupations open to [[gender-roles|women]]. Her "office" is not a grand suite but a room "only a few doors down the hall," suggesting the modest scale of her enterprise. That she is "occupied" when [[james-sprague|Jim]] arrives — busy, earning her living, unavailable to drop everything for a male client — is a detail that quietly asserts her professional reality.

In a novel dominated by [[gender-roles|women]] who do not work for wages — wives, [[motherhood|mother]]s, socialites, and widows living on settlements — Miss Burns is a rare glimpse of female economic independence. She has no narrative arc, no relationships beyond the transactional, no inner life revealed. But her presence, however brief, serves as a reminder that the world of *Blind Mice* contains [[gender-roles|women]] whose lives are defined by labor rather than marri[[youth-and-aging|age]].
