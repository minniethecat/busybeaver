# Gallows

The *gallows* is a structure used for execution by [[Hanging|hanging]], consisting of a wooden frame with a rope suspended from a crossbeam. In *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*, the gallows is both a literal instrument of death and a pervasive symbol of the violence that courses through the novel — from the London of the opening chapter to the Paris of the Terror.

## Historical Context

In eighteenth-century England, hanging was the prescribed punishment for a vast range of offences — from murder and [[Treason|treason]] to pickpocketing and forgery. The so-called "Bloody Code" made over two hundred crimes capital, and public executions were a common spectacle. London's principal execution site was Tyburn, near the modern Marble Arch, where crowds gathered to watch the [[Condemned|condemned]] die. The gallows was, for Dickens's original readers, a familiar and unremarkable fixture of urban life — which is precisely why the novel's opening chapter can invoke it as a shared cultural reference without explanation.

The French equivalent — the gallows and the breaking-wheel — was equally familiar, though France's adoption of the guillotine in 1792 marked a deliberate departure. The guillotine was designed to be more humane (instant death by decapitation rather than the prolonged agony of hanging) and more democratic (the same method of execution for all, regardless of rank). The novel registers this historical transition through its imagery: the gallows belongs to the old world, the guillotine to the new.

## The Gallows in the Novel

Dickens introduces the gallows in the novel's very first chapter. Describing the age's contradictions, the narrator notes "foreheads knitted into the likeness of the gallows-rope they mused about enduring, or inflicting" (005-007) — the image of the noose already shaping the imaginations of both victims and executioners before any character has been named.

The gallows imagery intensifies throughout the narrative. In the Paris sequences, the child run down by the Marquis's carriage is described with a face "pointed as if he saw the gallows somewhere in the sky" (021-057) — a premonition that consigns the innocent victim to the same visual field as the instrument of justice that will never come for his killer. The revolutionary crowd later erects "a gallows forty feet high, poisoning the water" (021-056) at the [[Fountain|fountain]] — the instrument of popular justice literally contaminating the source of life.

The altitude of the gallows becomes a grim obsession in the chapter that describes the burning of the Marquis's [[Chateau|chateau]]: "The altitude of the gallows that would turn to water and quench [the fire]" (029-046). In this extraordinary image, the gallows is imagined as a firefighting device — if only it were high enough, it might douse the flames consuming the old order. The passage captures the paradoxical yearning that runs beneath the novel's violence: the hope that destruction, if sufficiently absolute, might somehow produce peace.

The novel draws a careful distinction between the English gallows and the French guillotine. In England, execution by hanging was the standard punishment, and "the gallows and the wheel — the axe was a rarity" (013-010). The French adoption of the guillotine as a supposedly more humane and egalitarian instrument of death is one of the Revolution's ironies that the novel explores — the gallows, at least, was a familiar horror; the guillotine was mass death made systematic.

## The Gallows as Psychological Image

Beyond its literal presence, the gallows functions in the novel as a psychological image — something that characters see even when it is not before them. The "foreheads knitted into the likeness of the gallows-rope" (005-007) are the foreheads of an entire society living under [[III. The Shadow|the shadow]] of execution. The phrase "pointed as if he saw the gallows somewhere in the sky" (021-057) transforms a dying child's expression into a vision of the apparatus of death.

This psychological dimension is essential to understanding the novel's treatment of violence. The gallows is not merely a punishment that some characters suffer; it is a presence that haunts the imagination of the entire age. Dickens suggests that a society built on the threat of violent death — whether the old regime's gallows or the Revolution's guillotine — corrupts the minds of all who live within it, accustoming them to cruelty and making the infliction of suffering seem ordinary.

## From Gallows to Guillotine

The movement from gallows to guillotine is one of the novel's implicit historical arcs. The gallows belongs to the old regime — a crude, individual instrument of punishment. The guillotine, by contrast, is industrial in its efficiency and democratic in its application: the same blade falls on king and [[Peasant|peasant]] alike. The novel charts this transition without nostalgia, recognizing that both instruments serve the same human appetite for violent retribution. The question that lingers at the novel's end is whether any society can be built without such instruments — or whether the gallows, by whatever name, will always be with us.
