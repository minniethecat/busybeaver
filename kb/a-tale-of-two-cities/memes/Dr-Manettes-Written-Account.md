# Dr. Manette's Written Account

Dr. Manette's Written Account is the document composed by [[Doctor-Manette|Dr. Alexandre Manette]] in secret during the last ten years of his imprisonment in the Bastille. Hidden in a chimney in Cell 105, North Tower, the manuscript recounts the crimes of the Evremonde brothers — the twin aristocrats whose actions destroyed Manette's life and, indirectly, sealed the fate of his own son-in-law [[Charles-Darnay]] a generation later. The document is discovered by [[Monsieur-Defarge]] during the storming of the Bastille and is read aloud at Darnay's second trial before the Revolutionary Tribunal, where it becomes the instrument of his condemnation.

## Origin and Discovery

The account was written during the tenth year of Manette's imprisonment. He describes himself as composing "this melancholy paper in my doleful cell in the Bastille, during the last month of the tenth year of my captivity" (040-001). The document was hidden inside the chimney of Cell 105, where Manette had concealed it using rusted iron scraped from the cell bars, mixed with soot and charcoal to form a crude ink.

When the Bastille falls, Defarge searches Cell 105 thoroughly, knowing it to be "One Hundred and Five, North Tower" — the cell once occupied by Dr. Manette (027-037). He finds the paper concealed in the chimney and takes it among the "captured Bastille memorials borne in procession" (040-121).

## Content and Narrative

The manuscript is a first-person denunciation composed in the measured prose of a physician, detailing how the Evremonde brothers — referred to as "the elder" and "the younger" — summoned Manette to attend a dying peasant woman and her mortally wounded brother. The peasant woman, whom the elder Evremonde had abducted and assaulted, was dying of a fever; the brother, who had hidden his younger sister in his cottage, lay dying of a sword wound inflicted by the younger Evremonde.

Manette records the clinical facts with the dispassionate precision of a medical observer: the fever's progress, the brother's wound, the death of both patients within hours of each other. But beneath the clinical register falls a heavier judgment: the Evremonde brothers were murderers. The elder offered Manette gold to forget what he had seen; Manette refused and, within days, was seized by the brothers and imprisoned by *lettre de cachet* in the Bastille, where he remained for eighteen years.

The account closes with a solemn curse upon "the Evremonde brothers, and their descendants, to the last of their race" (040-060).

## Role in the Trial

At Darnay's second trial, the document is read by Defarge as evidence that Darnay — the nephew and heir of the Marquis St. Evremonde — belongs to the accursed line. Madame Defarge has preserved the paper, and her husband reads it to the tribunal as the supreme proof of the Evremonde blood guilt (042-031).

The reading of the document produces an overwhelming effect upon the tribunal and spectators. Dr. Manette himself, who had no knowledge that the paper survived the destruction of the Bastille, is devastated by its public reading — the document he composed in solitary anguish has returned, after so many years, to destroy his own daughter's husband. The irony is complete: Manette's account, written as an act of witness against aristocratic tyranny, becomes the instrument by which Revolutionary justice condemns an innocent man.
