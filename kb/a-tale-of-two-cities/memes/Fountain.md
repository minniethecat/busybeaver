# Fountain

The public fountain was a central feature of eighteenth-century Parisian street life, providing water for drinking, washing, and daily domestic use in the city's working-class districts. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, the fountain in the [[Saint-Antoine]] district becomes the setting for one of the novel's most vivid symbolic episodes — the breaking of the wine cask — and recurs as a point of communal assembly where revolutionary sentiment gathers and spills over.

## Definition

In the pre-Haussmann Paris of the eighteenth century, public fountains were the primary source of potable water for most of the urban population. They were typically stone structures with a basin and one or more spouts, fed by municipal water systems. The fountain was both a practical necessity and a social hub — the place where neighbors met, exchanged news, and formed the informal networks that would become the infrastructure of revolutionary organization.

## Historical Context

Paris in the 1770s–1780s had approximately eighty public fountains, many dating from the seventeenth century. The supply was unevenly distributed, with wealthier neighborhoods better served than the crowded eastern faubourgs. For residents of [[Saint-Antoine]], the neighborhood fountain was a daily destination and a site where hunger, thirst, and grievance became visible. The scarcity of clean water, like the scarcity of bread, was experienced as a political failure long before it was articulated as a Revolutionary demand.

## Role in the Novel

The fountain at Saint-Antoine is the stage for the novel's opening symbolic tableau. In the famous wine-cask scene (013-015), a cask of wine breaks open in the street outside the Defarges' wine-shop, and the starving residents rush to drink the wine from the cobblestones:

> "A large cask of wine had been dropped and broken, in the street... All the people within reach had suspended their business, or their idleness, to run to the spot and drink the wine" (013-015).

The fountain itself enters the scene moments later (013-018), when the wine-spattered residents wash the red stains from their faces and hands — a gesture that prefigures the blood that will soon flow from the [[Guillotine]]:

> "Those who had been greedy with the staves of the cask, had acquired a tigerish smear about the mouth; and one tall joker so besmirched, his head more out of a long squalid bag of a night-cap than in it, scrawled upon a wall with his finger dipped in muddy wine-lees — BLOOD" (013-022).

The fountain marks the boundary between the mundane and the prophetic. The wine that looks like blood, the fountain that washes it away, and the word "BLOOD" scrawled on the wall — together they transform a street accident into a premonition of the Revolution itself.

Later in the novel, the [[Saint-Antoine]] fountain reappears as the site where the Defarges' wine-shop patrons gather (023-002), now transformed from a passive crowd into an organized revolutionary cell. The fountain remains what it has always been — a communal meeting point — but the nature of the community has changed.
