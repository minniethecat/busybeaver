# Postilion

A **postilion** is a rider who guides the lead horses of a horse-drawn coach or carriage, riding the left-hand horse of the leading pair rather than sitting on a coach box. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, postilions appear in scenes of long-distance travel, most prominently in the Dover Mail journey and in the passage of Monseigneur through the French countryside.

## Role in the Novel

Postilions appear in two key travel sequences. During the Dover Mail's journey to the Barrier, a postilion cracks his whip as the coach approaches the city gate (006-103). In Book the Second, Chapter VII ("Monseigneur in Town"), Monseigneur's carriage is drawn by four post-horses with two postilions, their whips cracking as they ascend a steep hill (014-002). The postilions are described as figures of the road, surrounded by the dust and noise of travel.

> "To the Barrier!" The postilion cracked his whip, and they clattered away under the feeble gas-lamps of the town. (006-103)

> Monseigneur's carriage was drawn by four post-horses and two postilions, fagged up a steep hill. A blush of the day's heat still lingered in the air... (014-002)

The postilions' whips become a recurring sensory detail in the Monseigneur chapter. They "twined snake-like about their heads in the evening air" as the carriage passed the village fountain (014-008), and later, surrounded by "a thousand gossamer gnats," they pressed onward through the summer night (014-042). The pace quickens as they descend the far side of the hill: "the postilions had quickened the pace, she was advancing as if by magic" (014-060) — a description that captures the transformation from grinding ascent to smooth descent.

In the novel's closing vision, Sydney Carton imagines the postilions of the future Paris: "Forward, my postilions! A good journey!" (043-123). This image of postilions mounted and departing — "At length the new postilions are in their saddles, and the old postilions follow" (043-132, 043-133) — becomes part of Carton's prophetic vision of renewal, where the everyday rhythms of travel continue in a world redeemed by his sacrifice.

## Dickens's Use of the Postilion

For Dickens, the postilion is more than a period detail. The figure serves several narrative functions. First, postilions mark transitions between spaces — the Dover Mail crossing from England toward the Paris Barrier, Monseigneur's carriage moving from town to country estate. They are threshold-figures, appearing at moments when the novel shifts location.

Second, the postilion's whip-cracking provides auditory punctuation. In the Monseigneur chapter, the rhythm of the whips — slow on the ascent, quick on the descent — mirrors the chapter's own pacing, which accelerates toward the Marquis's confrontation with the village. The postilions' instruments are not merely practical but musical, setting the tempo of aristocratic travel against the silence of the watching peasants.

Third, Carton's final vision of postilions transforms these figures of the road into emblems of continuity. The postilions who mount and depart in the novel's last paragraphs belong to a world that has survived revolution. They represent the ordinary life — journeys, arrivals, departures — that outlasts both tyranny and terror.

## Historical Context

Postilions were common in eighteenth-century Europe, particularly for long-distance mail coaches and aristocratic carriages. Unlike a coachman who sat on a box seat, the postilion rode one of the horses, providing greater control on difficult terrain. The occupation required skilled horsemanship and was physically demanding — postilions were exposed to weather and road hazards that coachmen inside the vehicle avoided.

The postilion system was integral to the postal networks of both England and France. The Dover Mail, which opens the novel's action, relied on postilions to maintain the speed and regularity that made the mail-coach system the backbone of eighteenth-century communication. By the time Dickens wrote *A Tale of Two Cities* in 1859, the postilion had been largely replaced by railways, and the figure carried a nostalgic charge — a reminder of a slower but more dramatic age of travel.
