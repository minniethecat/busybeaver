## Overview

Wine and intoxication form a linked symbolic pair in *A Tale of Two Cities*. Wine — the spilled wine of Saint Antoine, the wine of the Defarges' shop, the metaphorical wine of revolutionary blood — is the novel's central symbol of hunger and its satisfaction (005-001). Intoxication — both literal and metaphorical — represents the altered state of consciousness produced by revolutionary fervour, a condition in which ordinary moral restraints dissolve (021-087).

## The Spilled Wine

The breaking of the wine cask in the street of Saint Antoine is the novel's most concentrated symbolic scene (005-001). A large cask of red wine falls from a cart and shatters, and the starving residents rush to drink it from the cobblestones. The scene transforms wine from a commodity into a bodily need and from a bodily need into a political symbol:

> The wine was red wine, and had stained the ground of the narrow street in the suburb of Saint Antoine, in Paris, where it was spilled. It had stained many hands, too, and many faces, and many naked feet, and many wooden shoes. (005-004)

The red wine staining hands, faces, and feet prefigures the blood that will stain the same stones during the September Massacres and the Terror. The word "BLOOD" scrawled on a wall by a "tall joker" with wine-stained fingers makes the identification explicit (005-004). From this moment, wine and blood are interchangeable in the novel's symbolic vocabulary.

## Intoxication and Revolutionary Fervour

Dickens repeatedly uses the language of intoxication to describe the psychological state produced by revolutionary participation. The revolutionary crowd is "drunk" — not on wine but on the excitement of destruction, the thrill of overturned hierarchies, the vertigo of power:

> He cried Long live the Devil! — so much to his temporary intoxication, that he cried Long live the Devil! (021-087, adapted)

This metaphorical intoxication is more dangerous than literal drunkenness because it carries the sanction of political ideology. The revolutionary who is "drunk" on the people's justice cannot be sobered by the ordinary remedies — time, food, sleep — that cure wine-intoxication (036-006). The intoxication of revolution is self-renewing: each act of violence produces the appetite for more.

## The Defarge Wine-Shop

The wine-shop of Ernest and Madame Defarge is the novel's central site of revolutionary conspiracy. It is both a literal place — a shop where wine is sold and consumed — and a symbolic space in which the boundary between drinking and plotting, between intoxication and insurrection, dissolves:

- The wine-shop is where Jacques One, Two, and Three meet to plan the revolution
- Madame Defarge's knitting — the register of the doomed — proceeds alongside the pouring of wine
- The spilled wine outside the shop's door (005-001) connects the commerce of the shop to the hunger of the street

## Drunkenness and Degradation

Alongside the metaphorical use of intoxication, Dickens also depicts literal drunkenness as a form of social degradation. Characters like Jerry Cruncher — whose drinking is part of a broader pattern of coarse behaviour — illustrate the coarsening effect of poverty and insecurity. The "intoxication" of the revolutionary crowd is, in this reading, the same impulse that drives the London mob at Cly's funeral: the desire to escape the self through collective action, whether through drink or through violence.

## Key Scenes

- **The wine cask breaks** (005-001 to 005-004): Wine spills in the street; the starving drink it; the word BLOOD appears on a wall
- **The Defarge wine-shop**: Revolutionary conspiracy conducted over wine; Madame Defarge's knitting proceeds
- **The Carmagnole** (021-087): The revolutionary dance as intoxication — bodies moving in unison, identities dissolved in the collective
