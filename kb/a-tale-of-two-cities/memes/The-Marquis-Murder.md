# The Marquis's Murder

## Overview

The murder of the Marquis St. Evrémonde is an act of peasant vengeance that marks a turning point in *A Tale of Two Cities*. [[Gaspard]], whose young child was killed when the Marquis's carriage recklessly ran over him in the streets of Paris, tracks the Marquis to his country chateau and assassinates him in his bed. The event is discovered in the morning when the chateau servants find the Marquis dead with a knife driven through his heart, and Gaspard is subsequently captured, imprisoned, and executed by hanging at the village fountain.

## The Carriage Accident

The chain of events begins in Book 2, Chapter 7, "Monseigneur in Town," when the Marquis St. Evrémonde's carriage speeds through the narrow streets of Paris:

> "Pardon, [[Monsieur]] the Marquis!" said a ragged and submissive man. (013-024)

The carriage runs over and kills a child — the son of Gaspard. The Marquis's response reveals his complete indifference to the lives of the common people. He tosses a gold coin to the grieving father, inquiring whether the horses are injured, and remarks:

> "How do they call you?" (013-035)

When told the man is called Gaspard, the Marquis tosses another gold coin and drives on, dismissing the crowd as "dogs" and "accident people" who would "howl over anything" (013-043). The Defarges witness this scene — Madame Defarge knits the Marquis's name into her register, and Monsieur Defarge comforts Gaspard: "Be a brave man, my Gaspard! It is better for the poor little plaything to die so, than to live" (013-034).

## The Murder at the Chateau

In Book 2, Chapter 9, "The Gorgon's Head," the Marquis retires to his country chateau. The chapter describes it as both a physical building and the seat of aristocratic power:

> The chateau awoke later, as became its quality. These morning hours were always sluggish. (015-108)

The narrative cuts away to Paris where Charles Darnay renounces his inheritance — and when it returns to the chateau, the murder has already occurred:

> At the point of the compass where the chateau of Monsieur the Marquis, now in his grave, stood in solitary state... (022-001)

The discovery scene in Book 2, Chapter 22 reveals the aftermath: the Marquis was found dead in his bed with a knife driven through his heart. Gaspard had climbed into the chateau at night, motivated by the image of his child crushed beneath the carriage wheels.

> "...the polished Marquis who was murdered," said one. (030-033)

Two fine dints from the knife were found in the bed-chamber wall where the stabbing occurred (022-001), suggesting the violence of the act.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The murder sets off a cascade of consequences:

1. **Gaspard's Capture and Execution**: Gaspard is tracked down, imprisoned for a year, and then brought back to the village where he is hanged at the fountain — a brutal public spectacle meant to deter further peasant violence. The Defarges witness the execution, and Madame Defarge records it.

2. **The [[Chateau]] Fire**: The murder enrages the revolutionary sentiment among the peasants, and the chateau is later set ablaze:

   > The chateau and all the race. (021-070)

3. **Darnay's Inheritance**: The murder accelerates the chain of events that bring Charles Darnay back to France, where his association with the Evrémonde name becomes a death sentence.

## Significance

The Marquis's murder is one of the narrative fulcrums of the novel. It acts out the retributive logic that drives the French Revolution — the violent answer of the oppressed to the casual cruelty of the aristocracy. The Marquis's death at Gaspard's hands prefigures the larger violence of the Terror and the eventual fate of the Evrémonde name. As an event of private vengeance that reverberates publicly, it connects the intimate suffering of the peasant class to the tectonic political upheaval reshaping France.

Dickens portrays the murder with moral ambiguity: the Marquis's death is both retribution for the child and an act that itself invites further retribution in the cycle of revolutionary violence. The event's narrative function is to make the abstract injustice of aristocracy concrete — through the death of one child and the revenge of one father.
