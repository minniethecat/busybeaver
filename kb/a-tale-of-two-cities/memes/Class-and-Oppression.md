## Overview

Class oppression is the historical foundation upon which *A Tale of Two Cities* is built. Dickens presents the French Revolution not as a sudden eruption but as the inevitable consequence of centuries of aristocratic exploitation. The novel traces the causal chain from Monseigneur's indifference through peasant starvation to revolutionary vengeance, creating a moral argument about the relationship between systemic injustice and violent response. The novel's treatment of class is notably ambivalent: Dickens condemns aristocratic cruelty with savage satire — the Monseigneur who requires four men to serve his chocolate is emblematic — but he also recoils from the mob violence that answers it. The novel occupies a complex position, sympathetic to the oppressed yet horrified by what their oppression produces. This ambivalence is not a failure of nerve but a deliberate moral stance: Dickens insists that to understand the Revolution is not to excuse its excesses, and to condemn the Terror is not to absolve the aristocracy. The class struggle in *A Tale of Two Cities* admits of no innocent parties and no easy resolutions.

## Manifestations

### The Monseigneur's World

Dickens's portrait of the aristocracy centers on the figure of Monseigneur, "one of the great lords in power at the Court" (029-003). The description of his morning ritual — requiring four attendants "all of the Monseigneur class" before he can drink chocolate — is a masterpiece of satirical compression. The aristocracy's fatal flaw is not individual cruelty but structural indifference: "Monseigneur, as a class, had dissociated himself from the system" (030-003). This dissociation is the novel's most damning indictment — the rich have so thoroughly insulated themselves from the consequences of their privilege that they no longer see the people whose labor sustains them.

### Saint Antoine: Hunger as Identity

The Parisian district of Saint Antoine embodies the human cost of class oppression. Its inhabitants exist on "black bread and death" (005-024). Dickens describes their condition not as poverty but as a state of being:

> "The darkness of it was heavy — cold, dirt, sickness, ignorance, and want, were the lords in waiting on the saintly presence."
> — (005-006)

The spilled wine cask scene (005-001) dramatizes a community so starved that wine in the street becomes a communal event — hunger has made them less than human in the eyes of the powerful. The people of Saint Antoine are not characters but a condition: they are what happens when systematic oppression is sustained across generations.

### The Evrémonde Crimes

The Marquis St. Evrémonde's casual murder of Gaspard's child — compensated with a tossed coin — epitomizes the aristocracy's moral bankruptcy. The Marquis's philosophy is explicit: "Repression is the only lasting philosophy" (015-080). This belief in the necessity of violence to maintain class hierarchy makes the Revolution not merely political but morally inevitable. When the Marquis's carriage rolls over the child, it literalizes the metaphor — the aristocracy's wheels crush the poor without slowing down, and the only recompense offered is a coin tossed into the mud.

### Revolutionary Justice as Mirror

The novel's most uncomfortable insight is that revolutionary justice reproduces the structures of oppression it replaces:

> "The wives and mothers we have been used to see, since we were as little as this child, and much less, have not been greatly considered."
> — (033-047), Madame Defarge

Madame Defarge's knitting register, the Revolutionary Tribunal's summary judgments, the "inveterate hatred of a class" (044-036) — all mirror the aristocratic system they oppose. Oppression, Dickens suggests, is not a political position but a habit of mind that survives regime change.

## Key Passages

> "To the eye it is fair enough, here; but seen in its integrity, under the sky, and by the daylight, it is a crumbling tower of waste, mismanagement, extortion, debt, mortgage, oppression, hunger, nakedness, and suffering."
> — (015-080)

This litany of peasant experience catalogs the systematic nature of pre-revolutionary oppression. Each term names a different mechanism by which the aristocracy extracts life from the people. The "crumbling tower" metaphor captures the decay at the heart of apparent magnificence.

> "Charles Evrémonde, called Darnay. Released yesterday. Reaccused and retaken yesterday. Indictment delivered to him last night. Suspected and Denounced enemy of the Republic, Aristocrat, one of a family of tyrants."
> — (039-103)

The formal indictment links Darnay's family name directly to "the oppression of the people" — oppression so famous that it requires no elaboration before the Revolutionary Tribunal. The word "Aristocrat" functions as a death sentence in itself.

> "Two score and twelve were told off. From the farmer-general of seventy, whose riches could not buy his life, to the seamstress of twenty, whose poverty and obscurity could not save her."
> — (043-002)

Dickens's pairing of the richest and poorest among the condemned is the novel's final word on class. Oppression and revolution alike destroy indiscriminately. Class is not a protection but a target — and no one, rich or poor, is safe when the guillotine is the instrument of justice.
