# Barrier

In Revolutionary France, the barrier (*la barrière*) was a fortified checkpoint at the gates of Paris where all persons and goods entering or leaving the city were examined by armed patriots. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, the barrier represents the tightening grip of Revolutionary authority — the point at which a traveller's identity is scrutinized and their fate decided.

## Definition

The barriers of Paris were a ring of customs posts and guardhouses encircling the city. Originally built in the 1780s as tax-collection points under the Ferme Générale, they were repurposed during the Revolution as security checkpoints. Every person passing through a barrier was required to present papers identifying themselves and stating their business. Failure to satisfy the guards could result in arrest, imprisonment, or immediate condemnation as a suspected counter-revolutionary.

## Historical Context

The barriers took on their most ominous function during the Reign of Terror (1793–1794), when the Committee of Public Safety used them to control the movement of citizens, prevent the escape of suspects, and monitor the influx of provincial visitors to the capital. The phrase "to the Barrier" became synonymous with the journey toward Revolutionary justice — a passage from the relative safety of the provinces into the jurisdiction of the Revolutionary Tribunal.

## Role in the Novel

The barrier appears at critical moments of transit and transformation. When [[Jarvis-Lorry]] and [[Lucie-Manette]] travel to Paris to rescue [[Charles-Darnay]], Defarge gives the postilion the command: "To the Barrier!" (006-103). The carriage clatters "under the over-swinging lamps" toward one of the city gates, marking the passage from ordinary travel into the domain of Revolutionary danger.

The barrier's most significant appearance comes in Book the Third, when [[Charles-Darnay]] returns to France. After his arrest on the country roads, he is brought before the barrier, which "was closed and strongly guarded when they rode up to it" (031-036). A functionary examines his papers and declares the damning verdict: "You are an aristocrat, and must have an escort — and must pay for it" (031-009). At the barrier, Darnay is transformed from a free English gentleman into a prisoner of the Republic, his noble birth outweighing his renunciation of the Evrémonde name.

Dickens also uses the barrier metaphorically. Defarge "stood with his hand on the back of his wife's chair, looking over that barrier at the person to whom they were both opposed" (022-085), transforming the word from a physical checkpoint into a symbol of fixed antagonism.

## Related Terms

- **[[Charles-Darnay]]**: The returning aristocrat whose papers fail to satisfy the barrier guards
- **[[Conciergerie]]**: The prison to which suspects are sent after being detained at the barrier
- **Emigrant**: Those who fled France without passing through the barriers — condemned in absentia
- **[[Revolutionary-Tribunal]]**: The court that ultimately judged those detained at the barriers
