# Patriot

In the lexicon of the French Revolution, a *patriot* was a supporter of the republican cause — a citizen who demonstrated active loyalty to the Revolution and the new order it sought to establish. Yet in *A Tale of Two Cities*, Dickens exposes the slippage between the term's ideal meaning and its practical deployment. The "patriot" is as often a figure of menace as of virtue, and the word becomes a means by which the Revolutionary regime justifies its cruelties.

## Definition

The revolutionary patriot was defined by loyalty to the Nation rather than to the Crown. Patriots were expected to demonstrate their commitment through public conduct: serving in the National Guard, attending sectional assemblies, denouncing suspects, and using the prescribed forms of Revolutionary speech. The Patriot was the ideal citizen made militant — the defender of liberty against its external and internal enemies.

## Historical Context

The dichotomy between "patriots" and "aristocrats" structured Revolutionary political discourse. Patriots claimed to represent the will of the sovereign people; aristocrats represented the forces of reaction. As the Revolution radicalized through 1792–1794, the patriot identity became increasingly performative. Public denunciations, attendance at the guillotine, and rhetorical extremism were all markers of patriotic virtue. Failure to meet these standards could result in denunciation as a "false patriot" — a charge punishable by death.

## Role in the Novel

Dickens deploys the word "patriot" with a sardonic edge throughout Book the Third. The functionary who processes [[Charles-Darnay]] is described as speaking "in the conciliatory tone of a good patriot" (031-012) — conciliatory, that is, while informing a prisoner that he must pay for his own armed escort. The patriots who guard the road to Paris sit in their "most explosive state of readiness" (031-001), their patriotic vigilance indistinguishable from petty tyranny over travelers.

The most devastating ironies attach to the Defarges. [[Monsieur-Defarge]] is a patriot in the full Revolutionary sense: he was among the attackers of the [[Bastille]], and he serves on the Revolutionary committee. Yet the same patriotism that made him an enemy of aristocratic oppression also makes him an instrument of indiscriminate vengeance. When he argues against the extermination of Darnay's family, he proves to be the exception; [[Madame-Defarge]], the more implacable "patriot," dismisses mercy as a weakness.

The "wild excitement, patriotic enthusiasm" (040-122) that greets the sentencing of Darnay is indistinguishable from bloodlust. Dickens's patriots are not hypocrites — they genuinely believe in their cause — but the cause has become a machine that no longer distinguishes justice from cruelty.
