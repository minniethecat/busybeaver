# Hanging

**Hanging** — execution by the rope — appears in *A Tale of Two Cities* primarily as the English counterpart to the French guillotine. Dickens opens the novel with a sustained comparison between the two nations' methods of state killing, and hanging recurs through the London scenes as a reminder that England's claim to superior order and protection coexists with a brutal system of capital punishment.

## England's "Punishment of Death"

In the novel's opening chapter, Dickens names hanging as one of the defining facts of England in 1775, alongside daring burglaries, highway robberies, and the death penalty applied to "a vast number of petty offenders" (001-005). The punishment of death awaited not only murderers and traitors but also forgers, thieves, and those who stole a handkerchief — a penal code so severe that juries often acquitted the guilty rather than send them to the gallows for trivial offences. The hangman was, as Dickens observes, "ever ready" and "ever stringing up" — a figure as busy as his French counterpart the executioner, though the instrument differed.

## Hanging in the Imagination and Conscience

The novel's imagery of hanging extends beyond the literal gallows. During the Dover Mail journey, the passenger Lorry's troubled dreams conjure the image of a man who has been "dug out" of a long burial — "Got out at last, with earth hanging about his face and hair" (003-021). The word "hanging" here carries its broader meaning of suspension and clinging — the earth clings to the buried-alive figure as the rope clings to the condemned. The dream collapses the novel's central metaphor of resurrection with the shadow of the gallows: to be recalled to life is also to have been close to death.

## The Old Bailey and the Death Sentence

When Charles Darnay stands trial for treason at the Old Bailey, the charge carries the death penalty — and the chapter makes clear that execution in England meant hanging, drawing, and quartering for traitors. The spectators who gather to watch the trial treat the prospect of a hanging as a form of entertainment, indistinguishable in their appetite from the crowd that would gather at the guillotine in Paris. The courtroom scene establishes the death penalty context for the treason charge; the specific hanging method is implied by the English setting and the period's penal practices (008-046).

## A Mirror to the Guillotine

Throughout the novel, hanging serves as a structural mirror to the guillotine. Where the guillotine is swift, mechanical, and egalitarian — the "national razor" that levels all necks — hanging is older, more theatrical, and retains the personal touch of the hangman's hands. Yet Dickens's point is that both systems share the same moral logic: the state's right to kill in the name of order. England boasts of its protections while hanging petty criminals; France unleashes the Terror while denouncing aristocratic tyranny. The two nations, the novel's opening insists, are "not so different."
