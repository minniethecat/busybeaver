# Jacques Three

**Jacques Three** is a member of the innermost revolutionary circle in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*, serving alongside [[Monsieur-Defarge]], [[Madame-Defarge]], and [[The-Vengeance]] as a juryman of the Revolutionary Tribunal. Distinguished by his croaking voice and perpetually restless fingers, he is the most unambiguously savage of the Saint-Antoine revolutionaries — a figure in whom the Revolution's ideals of liberty and justice have curdled entirely into a craving for blood.

## The Revolutionary "Jacques"

The name "Jacques" is a collective revolutionary alias adopted by the Defarges' network in [[Saint-Antoine]]. Where Jacques One and Jacques Two remain largely undifferentiated voices in the crowd, Jacques Three emerges as a distinct and menacing personality. Dickens numbers his revolutionaries like interchangeable parts of a machine, but Jacques Three refuses to remain anonymous:

> "Jacques One, Jacques Two, Jacques Three! This is the witness encountered by the accused." (021-021)

He is singled out by number, yet his behavior — the croaking, the greedy fingers, the insatiable bloodlust — marks him as anything but interchangeable.

## The Croaking Juryman

Jacques Three's most distinctive trait is his voice. Dickens repeatedly uses the verb "croaked" to describe his speech, a choice that transforms him from a man into something amphibian and inhuman:

> "Kill him!" croaked Jacques Three, who had come close up. (027-056)

> "Magnificent!" croaked Jacques Three. [[The-Vengeance]], also, highly approved. (042-020)

The croaking recurs across multiple chapters, fixing Jacques Three as a creature of appetite rather than conviction. He does not argue for the killing — he croaks for it, as instinctively as a frog croaks for its prey.

## The Restless Fingers

If Madame Defarge's knitting fingers encode the death register, Jacques Three's fingers enact a different kind of anticipation — not the patient recording of names but the impatient craving to begin the slaughter. Dickens describes his "usual craving" (027-060) and "restless fingers" (042-005), a physical restlessness that betrays his hunger for the next condemnation, the next execution, the next head to fall.

This is most vividly dramatized when Doctor Manette's hidden letter is discovered in the Bastille. Jacques Three reads it with undisguised greed:

> "A. M.!" croaked Jacques Three, as he read greedily. (027-068)

The letter — which will condemn Charles Darnay — is not a document of justice to Jacques Three but a feast. He consumes it.

## Role in the Tribunal

By the climax of the Terror, Jacques Three has secured a position as a juryman:

> ...the jury, a specially selected jury, including the savage, bloody-minded juryman, the Jacques Three of St. Antoine. (039-101, adapted)

The apposition — "bloody-minded juryman" — leaves no ambiguity. He is not a juryman who happens to be revolutionary; he is a revolutionary who uses the jury box as a platform for vengeance. His verdicts are preordained.

## The Final Council

In the novel's closing chapters, Jacques Three participates in the dark council where Madame Defarge plots the destruction of [[Lucie-Manette]] and her child. When Monsieur Defarge shows reluctance, Jacques Three joins [[The-Vengeance]] in overruling him:

> "But our Defarge," said Jacques Three, "is undoubtedly a good Republican? Is he not?" (044-002)

The question, dripping with menace, makes clear that even a founding revolutionary like Defarge is not safe from suspicion. Jacques Three polices ideological purity with the same croaking enthusiasm he brings to the guillotine.

## Symbolism

Jacques Three represents the **institutionalization of vengeance**. Unlike Madame Defarge, who has a discoverable personal grievance, Jacques Three has no backstory and no motivation beyond the pleasure of the kill. He is what the Revolution becomes when its ideals rot from within — not justice, not even revenge, but appetite. His "restless fingers" are the fidgeting of a predator between meals.
