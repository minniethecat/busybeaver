# Dover Mail

The Dover Mail was a horse-drawn mail coach that ran between London and the port of Dover in late eighteenth-century England. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, the Dover mail is the setting of the novel's first dramatic scene — carrying Mr. Jarvis Lorry through a foggy November night toward a meeting that will set the entire plot in motion.

## Definition

A mail coach was a public carriage, operated under contract to the Royal Mail, carrying both letters and paying passengers along fixed routes. Unlike slower stagecoaches, mail coaches had priority on the roads and were permitted to travel at higher speeds. The Dover road was one of the principal coaching routes in England, connecting London to the Channel crossing at [[Dover]] and thence to France.

## Historical Context

Mail coaches were introduced in England in 1784 and rapidly became the backbone of long-distance communication. By the period of the French Revolution — the setting of the novel's opening — the mail-coach network was at its peak. A journey from London to Dover typically took between ten and twelve hours, depending on road conditions and weather. The coach carried an armed guard as protection against highwaymen, a feature Dickens exploits to create the atmosphere of suspicion and mistrust that pervades the opening chapters.

## Role in the Novel

The Dover mail appears in the novel's second chapter, where it provides the physical and atmospheric setting for the introduction of [[Jarvis-Lorry]]. The mail is "in its usual genial position" of mutual suspicion: "the guard suspected the passengers, the passengers suspected one another and the guard, they all suspected everybody else, and the coachman was sure of nothing but the horses" (002-005). This fog of distrust — both literal and metaphorical — establishes the novel's preoccupation with secrecy, identity, and hidden connections.

The mail's progress up Shooter's Hill is interrupted by a messenger on horseback: [[Jerry-Cruncher]], who delivers a cryptic message to Lorry: "Wait at Dover for Mam'selle" (002-048). Lorry's reply — "Recalled to Life" — introduces the novel's central motif. The mail "lumbered, jolted, rattled, and bumped upon its tedious way" (003-007) through the night, delivering not just letters and passengers but the first thread of a plot that will stretch from London to Paris.

When the mail arrives at [[The-Royal-George-Hotel]] in Dover the following morning (004-001), Lorry disembarks to meet [[Lucie-Manette]] — a meeting that begins the process of recalling her father, [[Dr-Alexandre-Manette]], from his living death in the [[Bastille]].

## Related Terms

- **[[Jerry-Cruncher]]**: The messenger who intercepts the mail at Shooter's Hill
- **[[Dover]]**: The port town where the mail terminates and where Lorry meets Lucie
- **Recalled to Life**: The cryptic message carried by the Dover mail, the novel's central motif
