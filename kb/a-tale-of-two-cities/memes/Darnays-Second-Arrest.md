## Overview

The second arrest of Charles Darnay is one of the most dramatically concentrated sequences in *A Tale of Two Cities*. Immediately after being acquitted by the Revolutionary Tribunal — thanks to the testimony of Doctor Manette, whose suffering at the hands of the Evrémondes has made him a revolutionary hero — Darnay is re-arrested on the spot. The denunciation comes from the Defarges and a third accuser whose identity is withheld until the following day. The event turns the novel's final section toward its inexorable conclusion and exposes the limits of even the most compelling personal testimony against the machinery of revolutionary justice.

The structure of the episode — acquittal, celebration, re-arrest — is a miniature of the novel's larger rhythm of hope and catastrophe. Dickens repeatedly allows his characters, and his readers, to believe that deliverance has come, only to snatch it away. This pattern is not mere melodrama; it enacts the novel's central philosophical claim that in revolutionary times, no individual fate is secure. The wheel of history turns, and those it lifts up in one moment it crushes in the next.


## The Acquittal

Darnay's first trial before the Revolutionary Tribunal ends in triumph. Doctor Manette's testimony — grounded in his years of unjust imprisonment in the Bastille — carries such moral weight that even the revolutionary court cannot ignore it. Darnay is carried from the courtroom on the shoulders of the crowd, celebrated as a citizen vindicated, and reunited with his wife Lucie. The reader, and the characters, are permitted to believe, for a few pages, that the danger has passed.

This moment of apparent deliverance is essential to the scene's dramatic effect. Dickens allows the joy to be felt before he destroys it. Lucie's reunion with Darnay — the embrace, the tears, the gratitude — is one of the novel's few moments of unalloyed happiness. And it is precisely because the happiness is so complete that the subsequent arrest is so devastating. Dickens understands that tragedy is not the absence of joy but joy's sudden and violent cancellation.


## The Re-Arrest

The arrest comes without warning. As the family gathers in their lodgings, four armed men of the Republic enter and take Darnay into custody again. Doctor Manette asks the essential question:

> "Tell me how and why am I again a prisoner?" (037-040)

The answer is delivered with the cold formality of revolutionary procedure. Darnay has been denounced to the Section of Saint Antoine:

> "Citizen Doctor," said the first, reluctantly, "he has been denounced to the Section of Saint Antoine." (037-048)

When Doctor Manette presses to know who has denounced his son-in-law, he receives an answer that radically alters the stakes:

> "Well! Truly it is against rule. But he is denounced — and gravely — by the Citizen and Citizeness Defarge. And by one other." (037-056)

The mention of "one other" — an unnamed, mysterious third accuser — creates an immediate dramatic tension. The reader knows the Defarges; their enmity toward the Evrémonde name is established. But the third accuser is a blank, and the withholding of their identity charges the subsequent trial with suspense.

The procedural language of the arrest — "against rule," "denounced to the Section," "gravely" — underscores the bureaucratic nature of revolutionary terror. Darnay is not seized by a mob but arrested by armed men acting under the formal authority of the Republic. The Revolution has institutionalised its violence, and the formality of the transaction is, in its own way, more chilling than spontaneous fury would be. The men who arrest Darnay are not angry; they are "reluctant." They are functionaries, not fanatics, and their reluctance makes the machinery they serve all the more inexorable.


## The Defarges' Denunciation

The Defarges' role as accusers is not surprising — they have been tracking the Evrémondes since the earliest chapters of the novel — but the timing is crucial. They do not denounce Darnay before his first trial; they wait until after his acquittal, when his release seems most secure. This is not mercy delayed but strategy executed: the public spectacle of acquittal followed by re-arrest maximises the demonstration of revolutionary power over individual fortune.

[[Madame-Defarge|Madame Defarge]] in particular has been knitting Darnay's name into her register since long before he returned to France. Her denunciation is the climax of a process that began when her family was destroyed by the Evrémondes — a process in which personal vengeance and political justice are indistinguishable:

> It was nothing to her, that an innocent man was to die for the sins of his forefathers; she saw, not him, but them. 

This sentence contains the novel's most concentrated critique of revolutionary justice. Madame Defarge's gaze, which the novel has trained us to respect — she is the witness, the recorder, the keeper of the register — is revealed here as a form of blindness. She sees "not him, but them": she sees the Evrémonde name, the class, the historical category, but not the individual human being standing before her. The Revolution, in Dickens's analysis, has reproduced the very crime it set out to punish — the reduction of human beings to abstractions.


## The Unnamed Third

The "one other" is revealed the following day to be Doctor Manette himself — specifically, the written account he composed during his imprisonment in the Bastille, denouncing the Evrémonde family and all their descendants. This document, discovered by Defarge in the Doctor's old cell during [[Storming-of-the-Bastille|the storming of the Bastille]], is read aloud at the second trial:

> I, Alexandre Manette, unfortunate physician, native of Beauvais, and afterwards resident in Paris, write this melancholy paper in my doleful cell in the Bastille, during the last month of the year, 1767. (044-001)

The letter — [[Dr-Manettes-Letter-from-the-Bastille|Dr. Manette's Letter from the Bastille]] — is the novel's most devastating irony: the testimony that saved Darnay (Manette's personal authority as a victim of the old régime) is also the testimony that condemns him (Manette's written curse against the Evrémonde line "to the last of their race").

| Accuser | Relationship to Darnay | Nature of Denunciation |
|---------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Citizen Defarge | Former servant of Doctor Manette; revolutionary leader | Political — Evrémonde aristocracy |
| Citizeness Defarge | Survivor of Evrémonde atrocity | Personal vengeance — family destroyed by Darnay's father and uncle |
| The Third (Manette's letter) | Darnay's father-in-law | Historical curse — written in the Bastille, discovered after the storming |

The three accusations form a kind of temporal progression: Defarge represents the present (revolutionary politics), Madame Defarge represents the past (inherited grievance), and Manette's letter represents the past-become-present — a voice from 1767, sealed in a prison wall, that reaches across decades to destroy the life of the man's own son-in-law. The Revolution does not transcend history; it is history, made irresistible.


## Doctor Manette's Collapse

The revelation of the third accuser precipitates Doctor Manette's psychological collapse. The man who has been the novel's figure of restorative reason — the physician who heals himself and others, the patriarch who presides over the Soho household — is reduced to his former state of helplessness:

> As it was evident that Doctor Manette had become a mere blank, passive, and helpless creature, the President of the Tribunal addressed him particularly. (044-015)

The word "blank" is precise and terrible. Manette, whose identity was painstakingly reconstructed over the course of Book the Second, has been erased by his own past. The letter he wrote in the Bastille is not merely a document; it is a revenant — a ghost that returns to possess the living. And the Revolution, which Manette had momentarily believed he could influence through his moral authority, reveals itself as a force that can consume even its heroes.

This collapse is the immediate precondition for [[Sydney-Carton|Sydney Carton]]'s intervention. With Manette incapacitated, the last barrier between Darnay and the guillotine is removed. Carton's sacrifice, when it comes, is not merely an act of love for Lucie but an act of substitution for the father who can no longer protect his family.


## Significance

The second arrest is the hinge on which the novel's final movement turns. It transforms Darnay from a free man to a condemned one, sets Sydney Carton's sacrificial plan in motion, and reveals the full tragic dimensions of the Evrémonde inheritance. The Revolution, which promised to liberate the oppressed, has instead become a mechanism by which the crimes of the past are perpetuated into the future. Darnay is condemned not for anything he has done but for what he is — and what he is, the son of an Evrémonde, is something he can never stop being.

In its formal architecture, the second arrest mirrors and inverts the novel's opening scene of resurrection — [[Recalled-to-Life|Dr. Manette's "recall to life"]] from the Bastille. Where that scene offered the possibility of renewal (the prisoner released, the mind restored), the second arrest forecloses it. Darnay is recalled not to life but to death. The two scenes, placed at opposite ends of the novel's narrative, trace the arc of the Revolution itself: from liberation to terror, from hope to the scaffold. The journey from Book 1's "Recalled to Life" to Book 3's "The Footsteps Die Out For Ever" is the journey from resurrection to execution, and Darnay's second arrest is the point of no return.

The second arrest enacts the novel's deepest structural irony: the past is inescapable. Darnay has renounced his family name, his inheritance, and his class; he has married the daughter of a man his family destroyed; he has returned to France to save a former servant. None of these virtuous actions, however, can cancel the fact of his birth. The Evrémonde name, like the blood on the grindstone at [[The-Grindstone|Tellson's courtyard]], is a stain "that the sun had never given, and would never take away."

Doctor Manette, the man who most loves Darnay and most desires his safety, is — without his knowledge or consent — the instrument of his destruction. The letter Manette wrote in the depths of his imprisonment, cursing the Evrémondes "to the last of their race," is a document that the Revolution appropriates for its own purposes, indifferent to the Doctor's subsequent forgiveness and love for his son-in-law. The written word, once committed to paper, acquires an autonomy that its author cannot revoke. Manette wrote the letter as an act of private desperation; the Revolution reads it as a public indictment. The gap between the two contexts — private anguish and political evidence — is the space in which Darnay's fate is sealed.
