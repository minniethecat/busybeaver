## Overview

Madame Defarge's knitting is one of the most famous symbols in Victorian fiction, and its significance extends far beyond its immediate narrative function. The knitting operates simultaneously as a death list, a historical record, an act of political resistance, and a meditation on the relationship between memory and violence. In a novel concerned with the recording of history—from Dr. Manette's Bastille manuscript to the Revolutionary Tribunal's judgments—the knitting register represents an alternative form of documentation: one maintained by a woman, in code, in plain sight, utterly inexorable.

Dickens's choice of knitting as the vehicle for revolutionary vengeance is inspired. Knitting is domestic, feminine, repetitive, apparently harmless—and yet, in Madame Defarge's hands, it becomes the most dangerous object in the novel. The tension between the activity's surface benignity and its lethal content is the source of the symbol's enduring power.

## Manifestations

### Knitting as Record

Madame Defarge's knitting functions as a register of the condemned: "Her knitting was before her, but she had laid it down to pick her teeth with a toothpick" (005-017). Each stitch encodes a name, a crime, a sentence. The register is comprehensive and irreversible—once a name is knitted in, that person's fate is sealed. The knitting represents an alternative judicial system, parallel to and more absolute than the official courts.

### Knitting as Political Action

The knitting is Madame Defarge's form of political participation in a world that excludes women from formal power. She attends executions, observes aristocrats, gathers intelligence—and records it all in her knitting. The activity is so innocuous that no one suspects its true purpose. This is covert resistance of the most sophisticated kind: visible to all, legible to none but the initiated.

### Knitting and Memory

The knitting register is a form of collective memory. Unlike written records, which can be destroyed (Dr. Manette's manuscript is nearly lost forever), the knitting exists in the minds of the women who maintain it. It is distributed, embodied, indestructible. The knitted register represents memory that cannot be erased, grievances that cannot be forgotten, debts that must be paid.

### The Inexorability of the Register

Madame Defarge "leaned against the door-post, knitting, and saw nothing" (006-101)—or appears to see nothing. In fact, she sees everything and forgets nothing. The knitting embodies a specific kind of attention: patient, unwavering, unforgiving. It is the antithesis of aristocratic indifference, and equally terrifying in its completeness.

## Key Passages

> Her knitting was before her, but she had laid it down to pick her teeth with a toothpick. (005-017)

Madame Defarge's first appearance establishes the knitting as an extension of her person. She is never without it; her hands are always in motion, encoding the fates of her enemies even in moments of apparent leisure.

> …leaned against the door-post, knitting, and saw nothing. (006-101)

The irony is devastating: Madame Defarge sees everything while appearing to see nothing. Her knitting is a mode of attention so acute that it registers what others overlook.

> …the figure of a dark stout woman, knitting. (013-042)

This recurring image—Madame Defarge, dark, stout, knitting—becomes a leitmotif of the novel's revolutionary sections. She is less a character than a force, less a woman than Fate made flesh.

## Character Connections

- **Madame Defarge** — The knitter; her identity is inseparable from her craft. She is the register made human.
- **The Vengeance** — Madame Defarge's lieutenant; the knitting circle is a political organization disguised as a sewing circle
- **Lucie Manette** — The antitype of the knitter: where Madame Defarge weaves death, Lucie spins a golden thread of life
- **Charles Darnay** — His name enters the register through no fault of his own; he is condemned by ancestry, not action

## Symbolic Elements

- **The knitting needles** — Instruments of fate, clicking like the Guillotine's blade
- **The register** — The invisible text that determines life and death
- **The knitting circle** — Women's domestic space transformed into revolutionary cell
- **The golden thread** — Lucie's counter-knitting, weaving life rather than death
