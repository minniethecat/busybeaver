# Turnkey

A *turnkey* is a prison guard or warder — the person who holds the keys to the cells and controls access to prisoners. In *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*, turnkeys appear throughout the novel's prison scenes, from [[The-Bastille|the Bastille]] to [[La-Force|La Force]] to the [[Conciergerie]], embodying the petty power and moral ambiguity of the carceral system.

## Definition

The term "turnkey" originates from the literal function of turning a key to lock or unlock a cell door. By the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, it had become the standard English term for a prison warder of the lower ranks — distinct from a governor or warden, but wielding direct physical control over prisoners' daily lives.

## Turnkeys in the Novel

Turnkeys appear at every prison in the novel. In the opening chapter, even in England, "prisoners in London gaols fought battles with their turnkeys" (001-005) — the antagonism between prisoner and jailer established as a universal condition before the French setting is introduced.

In the Bastille sequences, Defarge interacts with a turnkey during his search of Dr. Manette's old cell — an unnamed, menacing gatekeeper of the prison's secrets: "Defarge, the turnkey, and [[Jacques-Three|Jacques Three]], linked hand and arm, went with all the speed they could make" (027-061). This turnkey is complicit in the Revolution's attempt to exhume evidence from the old regime's prisons, holding the light while Defarge searches the chimney (027-076).

The Conciergerie turnkeys during Darnay's [[Imprisonment|imprisonment]] are a different breed — functionaries of the [[Republic]] rather than the monarchy, but no less indifferent to individual suffering. When Lucie visits, "Three turnkeys who entered responsive to a bell she rang, echoed the sentiment" (031-089) — a chorus of bureaucratic inhumanity, adding their mechanical sympathy to hers.

The most complex turnkey figure appears in the shadowy world of Revolutionary prison politics, where roles blur: a man described as "emissary of Republican committees, now turnkey, now prisoner, always spy and secret informer" (038-078). In the Revolution's accelerated moral universe, the distinctions between jailer, prisoner, and informer collapse — the turnkey can become the prisoner and the prisoner the turnkey with a shift of political fortune.

## The Turnkey and Revolutionary Transformation

The Bastille turnkey's role in Defarge's search of 105 North Tower marks a pivotal moment in the novel's symbolic architecture. When Defarge demands access to Dr. Manette's former cell, the turnkey leads the way down "dreadful staircases" (027-061) and stops at "a low door" (027-062) — the threshold between the buried past and the Revolution's appetite for evidence. His complicity is not ideological but occupational: he holds the keys regardless of who gives the orders. When Defarge commands him to "look among those fragments" (027-071), the turnkey fires the little pile of kindling that illuminates the chimney's hidden document (027-076). Without the turnkey's act — lighting the fire that reveals Manette's buried manuscript — the entire denouement of the novel, [[Darnay's Second Arrest|Darnay's second arrest]] and condemnation, would be impossible. The turnkey thus becomes, unwittingly, an agent of fate, his small act of obedience unlocking not just a cell door but the novel's climactic reversal.

## Related Terms

- **Bastille**: The prison where Defarge's turnkey holds the keys to Dr. Manette's old cell
- **Conciergerie**: The Revolutionary prison where turnkeys guard Darnay
- **La Force**: Another Paris prison with its own complement of turnkeys
