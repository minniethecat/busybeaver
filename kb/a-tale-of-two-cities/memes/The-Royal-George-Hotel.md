## Overview

The Royal George Hotel is the Dover coaching inn that serves as the English gateway in *A Tale of Two Cities*. It is here that the London mail coach delivers its passengers after the arduous journey described in the novel's opening chapters; here that Lucie Manette first encounters Mr. Jarvis Lorry; and here that the novel's English world briefly crystallizes before the narrative plunges into revolutionary Paris.

## Description

Dickens introduces the Royal George with the arrival of the mail coach in Dover:

> When the mail got successfully to Dover, in the course of the forenoon, the head drawer at the Royal George Hotel opened the coach-door as his custom was. He did it with some flourish of ceremony, for a mail journey from London in winter was an achievement to congratulate an adventurous traveller upon. (004-001)

The "flourish of ceremony" with which the head drawer opens the coach door establishes the Royal George as a place of orderly English custom — a world away from the chaos that awaits in Paris. The hotel's most distinctive feature is the "Concord bed-chamber," a room permanently reserved for mail-coach passengers. Dickens notes with characteristic humor that "although but one kind of man was seen to go into it, all kinds and varieties of men came out of it" (004-007) — the heavy winter wrappings that conceal travelers' identities create the comic illusion that the room itself transforms its occupants.

The Royal George is also where Miss Pross first meets Mr. Lorry. Their acquaintance, which proves crucial to the novel's climactic escape from Paris, began "at the Royal George Hotel at Dover" (012-019). Years later, sitting before his fire in Tellson's Paris branch, Mr. Lorry recalls "that younger elderly gentleman from Tellson's, who had looked into the red coals at the Royal George at Dover, now a good many years ago" (038-057). The hotel thus becomes a point of nostalgic return — a symbol of the safe, ordered English world that the novel's characters have left behind.

## Historical Context: Coaching Inns in Dickens's England

The coaching inn was an indispensable institution of pre-railway England. In the early nineteenth century, before the railway network had spread beyond its first lines, the mail coach was the fastest means of travel between London and the provinces. Coaching inns like the Royal George functioned as the hubs of this network: they provided fresh horses, meals, and beds for travellers, and they maintained the "Concord" rooms — named after the Concord coach, a lighter and faster design introduced in the 1780s — that were permanently reserved for passengers of the regular mail routes.

Dickens knew coaching inns intimately. As a young reporter, he travelled extensively by stage and mail coach, and the coaching inn appears throughout his fiction as a site of transition and revelation. The Pickwick Papers opens at a coaching inn; Nicholas Nickleby begins his adventures from one. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, the Royal George serves a particularly compressed narrative function: it is the last English interior space before the Channel crossing, and its ordered routine — the drawers, the Concord bed-chamber, the predictable arrival of the mail — constitutes a kind of architectural counter-argument to the revolutionary disorder that awaits across the water.

By the time Dickens wrote *A Tale of Two Cities* in 1859, the railway age was well advanced and the coaching inn was already a remnant of an earlier England. Dickens's depiction of the Royal George is thus tinged with nostalgia — it memorializes a mode of travel and a quality of English life that were already vanishing.

## Symbolic Function: The Threshold of Two Worlds

The Royal George Hotel operates as the novel's liminal English space — the last point of domestic order before the Channel crossing. Its function is structural as well as geographical: Dickens uses the Royal George to establish a baseline of English normality against which the subsequent French scenes register as increasingly alien and frightening.

The Concord bed-chamber, with its comic predictability, is the architectural emblem of this function. The room's name — "Concord" — is itself significant. Concord means harmony, agreement, peace; it is everything that revolutionary France is not. That the room seems to transform its occupants (all kinds of men emerging where only one kind entered) is Dickens's gentle joke about the English capacity for orderly metamorphosis — change without violence, transformation within established forms. It is the opposite of the Revolution's violent remaking of identity, where the aristocrat becomes the condemned, the banker becomes the fugitive, and the wastrel becomes the saviour.

The hotel's location in Dover — the closest English point to France, visible from the French coast on a clear day — makes it the geographical hinge of the novel's two-world structure. The journey from London to Dover (Book the First, Chapters 2–4) is the novel's first movement, and the Royal George is its terminus. What follows — the Channel crossing, the arrival in Calais, the road to Paris — takes place in an increasingly unfamiliar world. The Royal George is the last place where things make sense in the English way.

## Significance

The Royal George Hotel functions as a threshold space — the last point of English stability before the crossing to France and the revolutionary chaos beyond. Its "Concord bed-chamber," with its reassuringly predictable routine, is the architectural embodiment of English order. That the novel circles back to the Royal George in Mr. Lorry's memories years later underscores its symbolic weight: it represents the world the characters have lost, and the world to which they desperately hope to return.
