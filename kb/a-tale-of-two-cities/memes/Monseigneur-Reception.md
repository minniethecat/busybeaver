# Monseigneur's Reception

## Overview

"[[VII. Monseigneur in Town|Monseigneur in Town]]," the opening scene of Book 2, Chapter 7 of *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*, presents one of the most pointed satirical tableaux in the novel: the morning reception of an unnamed Monseigneur, a high-ranking French [[Aristocrat|aristocrat]], whose principal daily occupation is the elaborate ritual of drinking chocolate. The scene's power lies in its juxtaposition of grotesque aristocratic luxury against the starvation of the masses in [[Saint-Antoine|Saint Antoine]], less than three miles away.

## The Chocolate Ritual

Dickens devotes an extended passage to what becomes a devastating emblem of pre-Revolutionary decadence:

> Monseigneur was about to take his chocolate. Monseigneur could swallow a great many things with ease, and was by some few sullen minds supposed to be rather rapidly swallowing France; but, his morning's chocolate could not so much as get into the throat of Monseigneur, without the aid of four strong men besides the Cook.

The chocolate requires a retinue of four attendants — one to carry the chocolate-pot, a second to mill and froth the chocolate with a "little instrument," a third to present the napkin, and a fourth to pour out the chocolate. The entire apparatus serves to reinforce the Monseigneur's status:

> It was impossible for Monseigneur to dispense with one of these attendants and hold up his own chocolate. One lacquey would have put him out of existence in the very next world; he must have expired upon the spot with the assistance of two; three would have saved his life for something more than an hour; but four was the number requisite to convey the chocolate nobly into his august, much-enduring stomach.

The passage plays on the disproportion between the trivial action — drinking a cup of chocolate — and the elaborate human machinery required to perform it. The mock-solemn tone undercuts the pretensions of nobility and implicitly critiques a system that apportions labor and luxury so unevenly.

## The Levée: A Parade of Aristocratic Types

Following the chocolate ceremony, the Monseigneur holds his levée — a morning reception — attended by a parade of titled supplicants and hangers-on. Dickens populates the scene with a gallery of types that collectively indict the old régime:

- **The military officers** who hold sinecures and have never seen a battlefield
- **The clergy** whose "unbelieving" lives mock their holy offices
- **The courtiers** whose entire existence revolves around attendance at Versailles
- **The creditors** who dare not press their claims on men of rank
- **The Farmers-General**, tax-collectors whose very names excite dread among the populace

> Military officers destitute of military knowledge; naval officers with no idea of a ship; civil officers without a notion of affairs...

The levée is an economy of favor and display, disconnected from any productive purpose. Those who attend do so not to seek justice or conduct business, but to be seen, to petition for sinecures and pensions, and to maintain their position within a system that rewards birth over merit.

## The Marquis St. Evrémonde's Entrance

The Marquis St. Evrémonde — [[Charles-Darnay|Charles Darnay]]'s uncle — arrives at the reception. His treatment of the Monseigneur and the company emphasizes his own standing within the aristocratic hierarchy. He exits the reception and orders his [[Coachman|coachman]] to drive furiously through the narrow Paris streets — a decision that leads directly to the death of [[Gaspard]]'s child under the carriage wheels:

> ...the sun and the Marquis going down together, there was no warmth nor light. (014-004)

The juxtaposition is deliberate: the Marquis departs from a scene of hollow ceremony directly into an act of lethal negligence. Dickens connects the abstract corruption of the reception chamber with the concrete violence of the street.

## The Satirical Method

Dickens's technique in this chapter is notably different from his usual narrative approach. He employs direct authorial address and heavy irony, speaking in the voice of an omniscient moral commentator. The opening description of hunger in Saint Antoine frames the entire chapter — the reader is reminded, before any chocolate is poured or any bow exchanged, that people are starving:

> Saint Antoine was to give the name and number of its streets, and to know no other...

The placement of the reception scene immediately after the descriptions of Saint Antoine's hunger creates a powerful structural irony: every gesture of aristocratic excess in the chapter is measured against the invisible masses dying of want just beyond the hôtel's walls. The Monseigneur's chocolate becomes, in this light, not merely absurd but obscene.

## Significance

The Monseigneur's reception serves as the novel's most concentrated indictment of pre-Revolutionary aristocracy. By rendering the chocolate ritual in meticulous, deadpan detail, Dickens creates an image of a class so insulated by privilege that it has lost all connection to the human realities of the nation it governs. The scene functions as both comedy and moral anatomy — the laughter it provokes is edged with anger, and its exaggerations carry the force of historical judgment.
