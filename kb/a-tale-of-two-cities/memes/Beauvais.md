## Overview

Beauvais is a town in northern France, approximately fifty miles from Paris, that figures in *A Tale of Two Cities* principally as the birthplace of [[Doctor-Manette|Dr. Alexandre Manette]]. It is to the "Doctor of Beauvais" that the revolutionary citizens appeal when Charles Darnay attempts to return to Paris, and it is in Beauvais that Dr. Manette's reputation as a former Bastille prisoner grants him — and by extension his son-in-law — an authority that briefly shields them from the Revolution's indiscriminate violence.

## Description

Dickens depicts Beauvais as a town stirred by revolutionary fervour. When Darnay's party arrives there at eventide on the road to Paris, they find the town already transformed by the news from the capital — the postmaster has been deposed, and the citizenry are organized according to revolutionary principles (031-017). The town's recognition of "the Doctor of Beauvais" (004-069) provides a powerful demonstration of how Dr. Manette's past suffering has been converted into revolutionary currency: his status as a former Bastille prisoner makes him a figure of immense moral and political authority.

Yet Dickens also depicts the fragility of this authority. The townspeople's deference to Dr. Manette — "See! said the Doctor of Beauvais, raising his hand" (023-019) — rests on the unstable foundation of revolutionary sentiment, which can shift from adulation to accusation with terrifying speed. The "Doctor of Beauvais" epithet itself encapsulates the duality of Manette's identity: he is both the respected physician and the irreparably damaged relic of the ancien régime's injustice.

## Significance

Beauvais represents the reach of revolutionary consciousness beyond Paris into the French provinces. The town's rapid transformation — from an ordinary provincial settlement to a community governed by revolutionary committees — illustrates the speed and totality of the Revolution's penetration into French life. The scene at Beauvais, where the postmaster has been replaced and citizens move with a new, purposeful organization, serves as a warning to Darnay of what awaits him in Paris.

The town is also significant as an element of Dr. Manette's identity. The epithet "Doctor of Beauvais" follows him through the novel, linking his professional reputation to his birthplace and, by extension, to his French past — a past he has tried to escape through his exile in London. The revolutionary insistence on referring to him by this title is a form of reclamation, pulling him back into the French identity he had shed.

## Key Events

- **Darnay's Arrest at Beauvais** (Book 3, Chapter 1): On the road to Paris, Darnay's party is stopped at Beauvais. The revolutionary citizens, recognizing Dr. Manette, insist on escorting Darnay to Paris under guard — ostensibly as an honour, but effectively as an arrest (031-017).

- **The Doctor's Authority** (Book 3, Chapter 1): At Beauvais, Dr. Manette's reputation as a Bastille survivor exerts a temporary, talismanic power over the revolutionary crowd. They chant his name and demand protection for his son-in-law, unaware that this very protection will prove hollow upon their arrival in Paris (031-035).
