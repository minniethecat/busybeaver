# Condemned (Theme)

The state of being **condemned** — sentenced to death by the Revolutionary Tribunal — is one of the most pervasive psychological themes in *A Tale of Two Cities*. Multiple characters face condemnation, and each responds differently: some with terror, others with resignation, and one — Sydney Carton — with a transcendent act of sacrifice that transforms condemnation into redemption.

## Varieties of Condemnation

Dickens presents condemnation in multiple registers. Gaspard, the peasant, is condemned to death for the murder of the Marquis — yet the fountain-side crowd believes that "although condemned to death he will not be executed" because of his symbolic status (021-046). This is political condemnation: the crime is real but the punishment is contested by the community that sees Gaspard as a hero, not a criminal.

The Revolutionary Tribunal routinises condemnation into bureaucratic procedure. "All the fifteen were condemned, and the trials of the whole had gone through in less than an hour" (036-007). The mathematics of condemnation — fifteen lives in under sixty minutes — makes the point with terrible clarity. Condemnation at scale ceases to be justice and becomes production.

> All the fifteen were condemned, and the trials of the whole had gone through in less than an hour. (036-007)

Charles Darnay experiences the suddenness of revolutionary condemnation: he is "condemned to die within twenty-four hours" of his second arrest (036-036). The speed is itself part of the terror — no time for appeal, no time for reflection, barely time for farewell. The condemned man's time collapses from a lifetime to a single day.

The wood-sawyer, that cheerfully ghoulish figure, swings his hammer and announces: "Ay! and condemned as a traitor" (031-022). For him, condemnation is entertainment — proof that the Revolution is working, that enemies of the people are being discovered and dispatched. His attitude reveals how condemnation, repeated often enough, loses its moral gravity and becomes spectacle.

> One may be condemned tomorrow. Now, the stake I have is in this world. (038-074)

The condemned man in Carton's Parisian recollection speaks a truth that applies to every character in the novel: "One may be condemned tomorrow" (038-074). Condemnation is not a fixed sentence but a permanent possibility — the condition of living under revolutionary justice is the knowledge that anyone, at any moment, may be condemned. This ambient terror is the psychological atmosphere of Book the Third.

The seamstress, "again taken and condemned" (043-093), embodies the inexorable logic of the Terror. Having once been arrested and released, she is seized again — the second condemnation proving that acquittal under revolutionary justice is merely a postponement.

## The Condemned as a Shared Condition

The condemned in Dickens's novel form an unlikely fellowship. Darnay, the innocent aristocrat; the seamstress, a young woman caught in the sweep of revolutionary justice; and Carton, who voluntarily takes Darnay's place — all face the same scaffold. Their shared condition creates bonds across class and circumstance. The seamstress, meeting Carton in the Conciergerie, finds comfort in recognising a fellow condemned soul.

It is through this shared condition that the novel's deepest theme of resurrection emerges. Carton's acceptance of condemnation becomes the vehicle for his moral redemption. By choosing condemnation — by walking into it rather than being dragged — Carton transforms the condemned state from passivity to action, from victimhood to sacrifice. His prophetic vision imagines a future where the condemned are remembered not with horror but with honour.

## Condemnation and the Problem of Justice

The novel repeatedly asks: who has the right to condemn? The Old Bailey jury condemns on the basis of a spy's testimony. The Revolutionary Tribunal condemns on the basis of political identity. Dr. Manette's prison manuscript condemns the Evremonde line "to the last of their race." Each act of condemnation claims the authority of justice, yet each is compromised by the interests of those who judge.

Dickens offers no easy resolution to this problem. Even Carton's willing condemnation — the novel's most morally elevated act — is a kind of injustice: an innocent man dying for a guilty system. The theme of condemnation thus remains one of the novel's darkest threads, illuminated only by the fragile hope that individual sacrifice can redeem what institutional justice cannot.
