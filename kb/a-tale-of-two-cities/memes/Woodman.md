# Woodman

The Woodman is one of the novel's two great opening allegorical figures, paired
with the Farmer, Death. Introduced in the first chapter, "It is likely enough
that, rooted in the woods of France and Norway, there were growing trees, when
that sufferer was put to death, already marked by the Woodman, Fate, to come
down and be sawn into boards" (001-004). The Woodman does not act in haste; he
works in silence across generations, marking trees long before the Revolution
arrives to fell them.

## An Allegory of Inevitability

Dickens's Woodman is not a character but a presence — a personification of the
forces that make revolution unavoidable. The trees he marks are destined not for
building or for fuel but for the guillotine, the "boards" that will become the
platform on which thousands die. By naming the Woodman "Fate," Dickens insists
that the Revolution was not a random eruption but the working-out of a long,
silent logic. The wood has been growing and the Woodman has been marking for
generations before anyone hears the axe.

## The Silent Worker

The crucial quality of the Woodman, shared with the Farmer, is silence: "But
that Woodman and that Farmer, though they work unceasingly, work silently, and
no one heard them as they went about with muffled tread: the rather, forasmuch
as to entertain any suspicion that they were awake, was to be atheistical and
traitorous" (001-004). The phrase "no one heard them" is the indictment of the
ancien régime: not that the warnings were absent but that the ruling class
refused to hear them. To suspect the Woodman was awake — to recognise the
approaching storm — was to be "atheistical and traitorous," a formulation that
captures how deeply the blindness was institutionalised.

## The Woodman and the Farmer: A Pair

The Woodman and the Farmer, Fate and Death, work as a pair throughout the
opening paragraph. "Environed by them, while the Woodman and the Farmer worked
unheeded, those two of the large jaws, and those other two of the plain and the
fair faces, trod with stir enough" (001-006) — the kings and queens of England
and France go about their business, oblivious to the allegorical figures who
surround them. The phrase "worked unheeded" is the novel's thesis in miniature:
the real forces shaping history are not the crowned heads but the silent,
patient agencies of consequence.

## The Woodman's Harvest

The Woodman's work bears its fruit in Book the Third, when the guillotine —
built from the trees he marked — becomes the novel's central image. The
"woodman" transforms from allegorical figure to literal carpenter: the
guillotine's frame is wood, its blade is steel, and the union of the two is what
Fate has been preparing since the novel's first page. Dickens never returns to
the Woodman by name after the opening chapter, but his presence is felt in every
scene at the scaffold.
