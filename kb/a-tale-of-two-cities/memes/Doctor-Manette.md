# Doctor Manette

**Doctor Alexandre Manette** is the moral and emotional anchor of Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. His eighteen-year imprisonment in the Bastille — for the crime of witnessing the Evrémonde brothers' atrocities — and his subsequent "recall to life" form the novel's opening movement. A figure of profound suffering and equally profound resilience, Doctor Manette embodies the possibility of healing from trauma, even as the buried record of his past threatens to destroy everything he has rebuilt.

## Appearance and Manner

Doctor Manette's physical appearance shifts dramatically across the novel — one of the clearest external markers of his psychological state. When first encountered in the Paris garret, he is a hollowed shell: "a white-haired man, with a face of a deadly pallor," bent over a shoemaker's bench, answering to a number rather than a name. His voice is "the faintness of solitude and disuse," barely audible, as if speech itself has atrophied during eighteen years of isolation.

Restored to London life, he regains the bearing of a gentleman — erect, attentive, his white hair lending him a distinguished rather than spectral aspect. But traces of the Bastille never fully disappear. In moments of extreme stress, his face can revert to the "vacant" expression of a prisoner, and his hands, accustomed to the repetitive motions of shoemaking, betray a skill he can never entirely unlearn. The body remembers what the mind works to forget.

## The Prisoner of the Bastille

Before his imprisonment, Doctor Manette was a young physician of rising reputation in Paris. In 1757, he was summoned by the Evrémonde brothers to attend a dying peasant woman and her wounded brother — victims of the Marquis's violence. When Manette refused to be silenced about what he had witnessed, the Evrémondes had him arrested by lettre de cachet and imprisoned without trial in the Bastille. He was 25 years old. He would not emerge until he was 43.

In the Bastille, Manette survived by retreating into a narrow, repetitive craft — shoemaking. When [[Jarvis-Lorry]] and [[Lucie-Manette]] find him in the garret of Monsieur Defarge's wine-shop, he has become "One Hundred and Five, North Tower" — a man who no longer remembers his own name, who answers only to his prison number, who asks for nothing but his shoemaker's bench:

> The faintness of the voice was pitiable and dreadful. It was not the faintness of physical weakness, though confinement and hard fare no doubt had their part in it. Its deplorable peculiarity was, that it was the faintness of solitude and disuse. (010-001, adapted)

## Recalled to Life

The motif of resurrection — the novel's dominant theme — is first and most literally embodied in Doctor Manette. Lucie's love, [[Jarvis-Lorry]]'s practical kindness, and the passage of time in London gradually restore him. He resumes his medical practice; he receives patients in his quiet corner-house in Soho; he becomes, once again, a father:

> Doctor Manette received such patients here as his old reputation, and its revival in the floating whispers of his story, brought him. (012-006)

But the recovery is never complete. In moments of extreme stress, Manette retreats to his shoemaker's bench — the prison habit reasserting itself as the mind's last defense against overwhelming emotion. Dickens understood trauma before the language of trauma existed: Manette is not "cured" but managing, and the management can fail.

## The Father

Doctor Manette's relationship with his daughter Lucie is among the most tenderly drawn in Victorian fiction. When [[Charles-Darnay]] comes to confess his love for Lucie — and, more dangerously, his true identity as an Evrémonde — Manette's response reveals the depth of his paternal love. He consents to the marriage knowing full well who Darnay is:

> "I have had the happiness, Doctor Manette, of being so intimate here," so far at last as to suggest my deep regard — my true homage, and deep love, Doctor Manette!" he said deferentially. (016-021, adapted)

Manette's decision to bury his grievance against the Evrémonde name for his daughter's happiness is the novel's supreme act of forgiveness — and the source of its greatest irony.

## The Manuscript

The instrument of tragedy lies buried in the Bastille itself. During his imprisonment, Manette wrote a detailed account of the Evrémonde brothers' crimes, concealing it in a chimney of his cell. Years later, during the storming of the Bastille, the Defarges discover this manuscript:

> "A. M.!" croaked [[Jacques-Three]], as he read greedily. (027-068)

The initials — Alexandre Manette — become the signature on his own family's death warrant. When the letter is read aloud at [[Charles-Darnay]]'s second trial in Paris, it condemns Darnay not for anything he has done but for who his father and uncle were. Manette, who wrote the letter as an act of witness and justice, watches it become the means by which the Revolution claims his son-in-law. It is the novel's cruelest irony: the testimony of the victim destroys the victim's own family.

## The Breakdown

The trial's aftermath reveals the fragility of Manette's recovery. In his desperation to save Darnay, he throws himself into medical service among the prisoners — hoping his status as a former Bastille inmate will grant him influence. When Darnay is condemned nonetheless, Manette's mind fractures. He retreats to his shoemaker's bench, calling for his tools, his bench, his prison identity:

> The faintness of the voice was pitiable and dreadful. It was not the faintness of physical weakness... It was the faintness of solitude and disuse. (010-001, adapted)

The symmetry is devastating: the man who was "recalled to life" in Book the First is, in the final crisis, lost again to the living death of the Bastille.

## Symbolism

Doctor Manette is the novel's **wounded healer** — the physician who cannot heal himself. His shoemaker's bench is the physical correlative of his trauma: the narrow, repetitive craft that saved his sanity in prison becomes the symptom of his ongoing imprisonment. He represents the possibility of redemption through love and the limit of that redemption; he can be healed, but not completely, and never permanently. In this, Dickens refuses the easy consolation of a full recovery, offering instead something truer: the ongoing work of living with damage.

Manette also functions as the novel's living archive of aristocratic crime. His body — broken by eighteen years of unjust imprisonment — is the physical evidence of everything the Revolution claims to oppose. Yet his manuscript, written as an act of witness, becomes the weapon that the Revolution turns against the innocent. Manette thus embodies the novel's deepest tragic insight: that testimony to injustice, however righteous, can be conscripted into new cycles of injustice when extracted from its human context.
