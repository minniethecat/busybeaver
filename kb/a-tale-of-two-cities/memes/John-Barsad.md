# John Barsad

**John Barsad** is an English government spy and a recurring minor antagonist in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. He first appears as a prosecution witness at [[Charles-Darnay]]'s treason trial at the Old Bailey, where his testimony is discredited. He reappears in Paris during the Terror as a turnkey at the Conciergerie prison — and it is there that [[Sydney-Carton]] recognizes him and exposes his true identity as **Solomon Pross**, the estranged brother of [[Miss-Pross]]. This discovery gives Carton the leverage he needs to execute his plan to save Darnay from the guillotine.

## The Old Bailey Witness

Barsad first enters the novel as a witness for the Crown against Charles Darnay, who stands accused of treason. Barsad describes himself as "a gentleman" and testifies to having observed Darnay engaged in suspicious activities. But under cross-examination by C.J. Stryver, his character is systematically demolished:

> The story of his pure and virtuous life was shown to be a fabrication. (009-003, paraphrased)

The exposure of Barsad as a perjurer — and the revelation that his associate [[Roger-Cly]] is equally corrupt — is one of the novel's early demonstrations that the line between "gentleman" and "criminal" is thinner than appearances suggest.

## Solomon Pross

Carton's recognition scene in Paris is one of the novel's most satisfying reversals. He encounters Barsad at the Conciergerie and, through careful observation, realizes the spy is none other than Solomon Pross — the brother who has been the secret grief of Miss Pross's life. Solomon abandoned his family, stole from his sister, and has been living under a false name ever since:

> Mr. Lorry's inquiries into Miss Pross's personal history had established the fact that her brother Solomon was a heartless scoundrel who had stripped her of everything she possessed. (012-045, adapted)

Carton uses this information ruthlessly: if Barsad does not cooperate in the substitution plan, Carton will denounce him to the Revolutionary Tribunal as an English spy. The threat works. Barsad, a man who has lived his entire life by betrayal, is himself cornered by the threat of exposure.

## Role in the Substitution

Under Carton's coercion, Barsad facilitates the prison visit during which Carton drugs Darnay and exchanges clothes with him. It is a small but essential piece of the escape plot — without Barsad's access to the prison, Carton cannot reach Darnay. The irony is layered: the brother who betrayed Miss Pross now serves, unwillingly, as the instrument of her mistress's salvation.

## Character as Pattern

Barsad belongs to a Dickensian type: the false gentleman whose respectability masks corruption. Like [[Roger-Cly]] — his partner in espionage — he represents the moral ambiguity of the state's agents, who are as dishonest as the criminals they pursue. His exposure and coercion by Carton invert the power dynamic: the spy becomes the tool, and the man he once helped prosecute becomes his master.
