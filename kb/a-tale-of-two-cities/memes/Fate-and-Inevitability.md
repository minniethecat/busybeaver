## Overview

Fate and inevitability form one of the central structural devices of *A Tale of Two Cities*. From the novel's famous opening — "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times" (001-001) — Dickens establishes a world in which historical forces sweep individuals toward predetermined ends. The Woodman (Fate) and the Farmer (Death) introduced in the first chapter frame the entire narrative as an unfolding of forces beyond individual control. Characters repeatedly find themselves caught in currents they can neither resist nor redirect: Darnay's return to France, Dr. Manette's manuscript surfacing at the trial, Carton's long-prophesied sacrifice. The theme operates on two levels: the personal fate of characters caught in historical currents, and the broader inevitability of revolutionary justice. Dickens portrays the French Revolution not as a contingent political event but as an inexorable reckoning for centuries of aristocratic oppression — a storm that was always going to break, and whose breaking no individual could prevent.

## Manifestations

### The Opening Prophecy

The novel's first chapter establishes fate as an active force. The Woodman and Farmer are allegorical figures who "work unheeded" — their labor of preparing "tumbrils of the Revolution" precedes any individual human action in the story. This framing suggests that the entire plot unfolds within a predetermined structure: every scaffold, every cart, every execution has already been "marked" by cosmic forces before any human agent lifts a finger.

### The Knitting Register

Madame Defarge's knitting serves as the most powerful symbol of fate made material. Each stitch encodes a name destined for the guillotine, and the register is immutable — once a name is knitted in, that person's fate is sealed:

> "Madame Defarge held darkly ominous with the Fate of the Fifty-Two."
> — (044-001)

Madame Defarge does not merely predict deaths; she actively determines them through the register she maintains. She becomes the human embodiment of Fate in the novel — "darkly ominous," inexorable, incapable of making an exception. Her knitting transcribes abstract historical inevitability into a concrete, physical ledger of death.

### "Recalled to Life"

The motif of resurrection — Dr. Manette's release from the Bastille, Charles Darnay's reprieves, Sydney Carton's final transcendence — exists in constant tension with fate. Each "recall" is both a defiance of fate and, paradoxically, part of a larger pattern. Carton's sacrifice is simultaneously his freely chosen act and the fulfillment of his earlier prophecy: "I care for no man on earth, and no man on earth cares for me" (012-028). The novel suggests that freedom and fate are not opposites but intertwined dimensions of the same narrative arc.

### The Sea Rises

Dickens repeatedly uses the metaphor of the rising sea to describe the revolutionary mob:

> "The raging Revolution that was running so fearfully wild."
> — (030-070)

The sea, like fate, is impersonal, unstoppable, and indifferent to individual merit. Individual characters are tossed upon these waters; even the most morally upright — Dr. Manette, Lucie, Darnay — cannot escape its currents. The storming of the Bastille carries Defarge forward with "the force of the ocean bearing him on" (027-044), a force that no individual will can resist.

## Key Passages

> "Under the guidance of her Christian pastors, she entertained herself, besides, with such other achievements as the Woodman, Fate, and the Farmer, Death, had already marked to be their tumbrils of the Revolution."
> — (001-004)

This sentence, appearing in the novel's first paragraphs, announces the theme with mythic grandeur. Fate and Death are personified as laborers preparing for a harvest — the Revolution — that is already "marked" for certain individuals. The tumbrils have been chosen before the first crime has been committed.

> "The Fifty-Two awaited their fate."
> — (043-001)

The fifty-two prisoners awaiting execution in the Conciergerie embody the theme at its starkest. Each individual fate is subsumed into a collective destiny. Dickens's choice to number them rather than name them underscores the impersonal machinery of historical process — the guillotine does not care who you are, only that you are on the list.

> "Two score and twelve were told off. From the farmer-general of seventy, whose riches could not buy his life, to the seamstress of twenty, whose poverty and obscurity could not save her."
> — (043-002)

This passage enlarges the theme of fate beyond political allegory. Rich and poor, old and young, guilty and innocent — fate, under the Revolution, makes no distinctions. The guillotine is the great equalizer, and the equality it enforces is the equality of death.
