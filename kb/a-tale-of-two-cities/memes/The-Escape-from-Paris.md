# The Escape from Paris

## Overview

The escape of the Manette-Darnay family from Paris in the final chapters of *A Tale of Two Cities* is the novel's culminating action sequence — a desperate flight from revolutionary justice that succeeds only through Sydney Carton's deliberate self-sacrifice. The event spans three chapters and involves a series of nested substitutions and deceptions: Carton's blackmail of the spy Barsad, Carton's drugging and replacement of Darnay in his prison cell, and the family's passage through the gates of Paris as the guillotine claims the man who has taken Darnay's place.

## The Plot

### Carton's Bargain with Barsad

Carton learns through his acquaintance with the [[Old Bailey]] that John Barsad — the spy who testified against Darnay in England — is now in Paris as a turnkey at the [[Conciergerie]] prison. Barsad is also the brother of Miss Pross, and his connection to the Manette household gives Carton leverage. He confronts Barsad and reveals his knowledge of Barsad's identity as Solomon Pross, a double agent who has served both the English Crown and the French [[Republic]]:

> "I know you to be a spy, and a double spy..." (034)

Carton forces Barsad to cooperate in the escape plan by threatening to expose his duplicity to the Revolutionary Tribunal — a threat equivalent to a death sentence.

### The Substitution in the Conciergerie

On the day of Darnay's scheduled execution, Carton visits him in prison. He drugs Darnay with a chemical compound he has prepared, then exchanges clothes with him. Barsad, as the turnkey, assists by carrying the unconscious Darnay out of the cell — claiming that the prisoner has fainted — and delivering him to a waiting carriage where Lucie, Dr. Manette, Jarvis Lorry, and the faithful Miss Pross wait.

> "There is no time to lose! Bring him at once to the carriage!" (034)

Carton remains in the cell, awaiting the guillotine in Darnay's place.

### The Flight Through the Gates

The family's carriage passes through the Paris gates without difficulty. Darnay, dressed in Carton's clothes, is identified as "Sydney Carton, an English lawyer" rather than as "Charles Evrémonde, condemned aristocrat." The identity papers Carton has prepared — which Barsad has been compelled to verify — are sufficient for safe passage.

The carriage leaves Paris "at a furious rate," bearing the sleeping Darnay, the terrified Lucie, the shattered Dr. Manette, and the imperturbable Jarvis Lorry out of revolutionary France toward safety in England.

### Miss Pross and Madame Defarge

The escape is enabled in part by a parallel action occurring simultaneously: Miss Pross's confrontation with Madame Defarge in the Manette apartment. Madame Defarge, having discovered the family's flight, arrives to denounce them. Miss Pross bars the door and, in the ensuing struggle, Madame Defarge's pistol discharges, killing the revolutionary woman and deafening Miss Pross permanently.

This subplot serves both thematic and practical functions: it removes the single most implacable threat to the family's escape, and it demonstrates that loyalty and love — embodied in the spinster Miss Pross — can defeat even the implacable force of revolutionary vengeance.

## The Machinery of Escape

The escape functions as a complex mechanism of substitutions:

| Element | Original | Substitute |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| The condemned man | Charles Darnay | Sydney Carton |
| The identity papers | Evrémonde | Carton |
| The turnkey | Jailer | Barsad (compromised spy) |
| The threat | Madame Defarge | Miss Pross (her mortal enemy) |

Each substitution depends on a prior substitution, creating a chain of deceptions that extends from the Conciergerie cell to the gates of Paris. The mechanism is Dickens at his most plot-driven — a system of interlocking parts that functions with the precision of a stage farce, though the stakes are mortal.

## Dr. Manette's Collapse

The escape is shadowed by Dr. Manette's psychological collapse. The revelation at the trial — that his own letter condemned his son-in-law — has broken something in him, and he reverts to his Bastille identity, calling again for his shoemaker's bench. His condition during the escape is described in terms that echo his earlier "resurrection":

> ...he had sunk again into the state from which he had been recalled...

This relapse means that Jarvis Lorry must manage both the escape logistics and Manette's care, adding urgency to an already desperate situation. Manette's regression also serves as a reminder of the fragility of the recoveries the novel depicts: being "recalled to life" is not a permanent transformation but an ongoing struggle against the gravitational pull of trauma.

## Significance

The escape from Paris is the novel's most concentrated sequence of plot action, and it demonstrates the thematic principle that governs the novel's resolution: the substitution of one life for another. Sydney Carton's self-sacrifice is the moral climax toward which the entire plot has been building, and the escape — the physical movement of the surviving characters toward safety — is the narrative mechanism through which that sacrifice is actualised.

The event also functions as a repudiation of the novel's earlier pattern of imprisonment and trial. Where the first half of the book is structured around captivity and accusation, the final chapters are structured around flight and deliverance. The escape does not undo the guilt of the Evrémonde past or the crimes of the Revolution, but it preserves the possibility of a future beyond both — a future earned, paradoxically, by the death of the man who had seemed least capable of heroism.
