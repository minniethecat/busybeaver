# The Mail

The Dover mail-coach is the novel's opening vehicle — literally and
thematically. The first chapter of the story proper is titled "The Mail," and
the coach's progress up Shooter's Hill on a foggy November night in 1775
establishes the atmosphere of secrecy, suspicion, and urgent purpose that will
drive the entire narrative. It is inside this mail-coach that Mr. Lorry receives
the cryptic message — "RECALLED TO LIFE" — that sets the plot in motion.

## The Coach as World

Dickens makes the mail-coach a sealed world. "So the guard of the Dover mail
thought to himself" (002-004) — the guard's perspective gives the coach a
collective awareness of danger. Inside, the three passengers "were mysteries to
one another, as complete as if each had been in his own coach and six" (003-002).
The mail-coach is a paradox: it forces intimacy (the passengers sit pressed
together in "the narrow compass of one lumbering old mail coach") while enforcing
isolation (each is "a mystery" to the others). This tension between proximity and
estrangement is the novel's social vision in miniature.

## Suspicion and the Night

The atmosphere of the mail-coach is saturated with mistrust: "The Dover mail was
in its usual genial position that the guard suspected the passengers, the
passengers suspected one another and the guard, they all suspected everybody
else, and the coachman was sure of nothing but the horses" (002-005). The
sentence's chain of suspicion linking every person in and around the coach enacts
the social psychology of 1775. Highwaymen are real, but the deeper suggestion is
that this is a world where trust is structurally impossible.

The darkness compounds the menace. "Once more, the Dover mail struggled on, with
the jack-boots of its passengers squashing along by its side" (002-011). The
passengers walk beside the coach up the hill, their boots squelching in the mud
— a detail that roots the scene in unmistakable physical reality while the fog
turns the Surrey road into something alien and threatening. "The last burst
carried the mail to the summit of the hill" (002-012) — the coach's struggles
are rendered with epic effort, as if the climb up Shooter's Hill were a trial by
elements.

## The Message

The mail's dramatic function is to deliver the message. Jerry Cruncher appears
out of the night — "a figure on horseback came on through the mud, at a foot
pace" (002-012) — and gives Mr. Lorry the paper bearing the words "Wait at Dover
for Mam'selle." Lorry's spoken reply, "RECALLED TO LIFE," is the novel's first
great utterance, and it is spoken to the empty air — Jerry has already vanished
into the darkness. The mail-coach, which has laboured so mightily to carry
Lorry to this moment, continues on while the message ricochets through the rest
of the novel.

## The Mail as Threshold

The mail-coach is the novel's threshold between England and France. It carries
Mr. Lorry from London to Dover, where he will cross the Channel to retrieve Dr.
Manette. "What time, the mail-coach lumbered, jolted, rattled, and bumped upon
its tedious way" (003-007) — the alliteration of "lumbered, jolted, rattled, and
bumped" is pure Dickens, making the coach's physical motion felt in the reader's
body. The mail is cumbersome, old-fashioned, and dangerous — the very image of
the late eighteenth century stumbling toward revolution.

Inside the rocking coach, Mr. Lorry dozes and dreams: "Tellson's Bank had a run
upon it in the mail" (003-008). The coach becomes a dream-space where past and
present blur, and the banker's orderly mind is invaded by visions of being dug
out of a grave. The mail carries not only passengers and letters but the
unconscious anxieties of an age.
