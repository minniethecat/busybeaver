# Tocsin

The tocsin (from Old French *toquesain*, "touch the bell") was an alarm bell rung to summon a community to arms or to warn of imminent danger. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, the sound of the tocsin — along with drums and the roar of crowds — forms the auditory landscape of revolution, marking the moments when the collective overwhelms the individual.

## Definition

A tocsin is distinct from ordinary church bells in its function: it is not a call to worship but a call to action. Historically, the ringing of the tocsin signalled fire, invasion, or insurrection. During the French Revolution, the tocsin became a political instrument — its ringing announced the outbreak of popular violence, summoned citizens to the defence of the Republic, and marked the beginning of *journées*, the great revolutionary days that shaped the course of events.

## Historical Context

The most famous tocsin in Revolutionary history rang on 10 August 1792, when the alarm bells of Paris summoned the *sans-culottes* to march on the Tuileries Palace — the event that effectively ended the French monarchy. Throughout the Terror, the tocsin was rung by local revolutionary committees to mobilize the population against perceived internal and external enemies. The tocsin blurred the line between alarm and incitement; to hear it was to know that violence was imminent and that neutrality was no longer an option.

## Role in the Novel

Dickens deploys the tocsin at the novel's most volatile moment: the storming of the Bastille. As the Defarges lead the crowd toward the fortress, "alarm-bells ringing, drums beating, the sea raging and thundering on its new beach, the assault began" (027-038). The bell is not merely background noise — it is a participant in the action, amplifying the fury of the mob and marking the point at which revolutionary energy becomes an unstoppable force.

The tocsin also sounds in subtler registers. In the Defarges' wine-shop, as [[Madame-Defarge]] knits her register of the doomed, "the ringing of church bells and the distant beating of the military drums" (022-121) accompanies her work. The bells here are a harbinger — each stroke of the bell foreshadows a name added to the register and a head destined for the [[Guillotine]].

After the burning of the [[Chateau]], the tocsin spreads from village to village through messengers on horseback, "spurring and splashing through the darkness" (029-039), carrying the signal of insurrection across the countryside. The bell's sound is contagious; once rung in one place, it cannot be silenced until every village has taken up the call.

## Symbolic Significance

The tocsin in Dickens's novel is the auditory counterpart to the visual symbols of revolution — the red cap, the tricolour [[Cockade]], the [[Guillotine]]. It is the sound that transforms private grievances into public action, the signal that releases individuals from the restraints of ordinary morality into the anonymity of the crowd.
