## Overview

The Bastille, the medieval fortress and state prison of Paris, stands at the symbolic centre of *A Tale of Two Cities*. It is the place of Dr. Manette's eighteen-year confinement — a living death from which he is "recalled to life" in the novel's opening. The Bastille's fall on 14 July 1789 marks the dramatic climax of the novel's first revolutionary phase, but its true power in Dickens's narrative lies not in the moment of its destruction but in the long shadow it casts across every subsequent event. The Bastille is not merely a building; it is the physical embodiment of the ancien régime's capacity for arbitrary cruelty, and its ghost haunts even those who helped to destroy it.

## Description

Dickens's Bastille is rendered primarily through its effects on Dr. Manette rather than through direct architectural description. The prison appears in the narrative as a void — a "doleful cell" (040-001) that exists in memory and nightmare rather than in bricks and mortar. The reader sees the Bastille's interior most vividly through Dr. Manette's hidden manuscript, written during his imprisonment on scraps of paper using "a rusty iron nib, with soot and charcoal for ink" (040-001). This document, which recounts the crimes of the Evrémonde brothers and Dr. Manette's own wrongful imprisonment, becomes the instrument of his son-in-law's condemnation.

The Bastille's physical presence in the novel is always oblique — a "shadow" thrown upon Dr. Manette even in his London garden (010-002), a memory that returns in moments of stress. When the revolutionaries storm the Bastille in Book 2, Chapter 21, the description is vivid but swift: the outer courtyard, the cannon, the governor's surrender and execution. The Bastille's destruction is complete and cathartic, yet Dickens insists that the prison's true walls were never made of stone alone.

## Significance

The Bastille functions as the novel's master metaphor for unjust imprisonment — both literal and psychological. Dr. Manette's eighteen years in the "living grave" of the Bastille transform him from a promising young physician into a broken shoemaker who has forgotten his own name. His "recall to life" is the novel's central redemptive arc, but the Bastille's shadow never fully lifts: even years later in Soho, the trauma returns in moments of stress, and Manette's compulsive shoemaking signals the persistence of the prisoner within the freed man.

The Bastille also serves as the narrative linchpin of the plot. The hidden manuscript that Manette wrote in his cell — denouncing the Evrémonde brothers "and their descendants, to the last of their race" (040-121) — is discovered by Defarge during the prison's sack and later used by Madame Defarge to condemn Charles Darnay to death. The Bastille, even in ruins, reaches forward to seize the innocent. This is Dickens's deepest political insight in the novel: tyranny is not destroyed by the destruction of its symbols; it is merely transformed.

## Key Events

- **Dr. Manette's Imprisonment** (Book 3, Chapter 10): The hidden manuscript reveals how the young Dr. Manette was abducted by the Evrémonde brothers and secretly imprisoned in the Bastille for eighteen years, without charge or trial, for attempting to report their crimes (040-001).

- **Dr. Manette's Release** (Book 1, Chapter 1): The novel opens with the news that Dr. Manette has been "recalled to life" — released from the Bastille after nearly two decades, though his mind remains imprisoned.

- **The Storming of the Bastille** (Book 2, Chapter 21): The Defarges lead the revolutionary assault on the Bastille. Defarge searches Dr. Manette's old cell, finding the hidden manuscript that will later condemn Darnay. The governor is executed, and the Bastille falls (027-037).

- **The Discovery of the Manuscript** (Book 3, Chapter 10): At Darnay's trial before the Revolutionary Tribunal, Defarge produces Dr. Manette's Bastille manuscript. Its final words — denouncing the Evrémonde line — condemn Darnay to death despite Manette's desperate protests (040-121).
