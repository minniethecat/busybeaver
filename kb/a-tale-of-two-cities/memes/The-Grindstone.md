## Overview

The grindstone at Tellson's Bank in Paris is one of the most compressed and visually potent images in *A Tale of Two Cities*. During the September Massacres of 1792, a large grindstone is set up in the courtyard of the bank, and revolutionaries — their faces smeared with blood and sweat — take turns sharpening their swords upon it. Lucie Manette, trapped inside the bank with her father and Mr. Lorry, witnesses the scene through a window, and it becomes a defining moment in her encounter with the violence of the Revolution.

The grindstone scene belongs to a distinct category of Dickensian imagery: the domestic object repurposed as an instrument of terror. Throughout the novel, ordinary things — a wine cask, a knitting needle, a shoemaker's bench — are freighted with political menace. The [[The-Wine-Cask|wine cask]] that breaks in Saint Antoine is the first of these transformations; the grindstone is the most complete, for it takes a tool of peaceful labour and converts it, without alteration, into a tool of massacre.

## The Scene

The grindstone appears abruptly in the narrative, described as a utilitarian object transformed into an instrument of terror:

> ...in the open air, was a large grindstone: a roughly mounted thing which had been set up in the courtyard for the purpose of sharpening tools employed in the repairs and alterations of the house. (032-005)

The parenthetical detail — that the grindstone was originally used for house repairs — is essential. It underscores the improvisational character of revolutionary violence: the Revolution does not produce new instruments of terror but repurposes what is already at hand. The grindstone was there to sharpen chisels for construction; now it sharpens swords for killing. The continuity between the two uses suggests that violence is not an alien intrusion into ordinary life but a latent potential within it.

Men and women rush into the courtyard, and within moments the grindstone is in furious use. The workers at the grindstone are described with grotesque, almost subhuman imagery:

> The grindstone had a double handle, and, turning at it madly were two men, whose faces, as their long hair flapped back when the whirlings of the grindstone brought their faces up, were more horrible and cruel than the visages of the wildest savages in their most barbarous disguise. False eyebrows and false moustaches were stuck upon them, and their hideous countenances were all bloody and sweaty, and all awry with howling... (032-031)

These are not professional soldiers but ordinary citizens — the patriots of Saint Antoine — who have taken up arms and now prepare them for the killing to come. The false eyebrows and moustaches suggest a carnivalesque element: the violence has an improvised, theatrical quality. The participants are in costume, as if the massacre were a performance, and the false facial hair underscores the sense that the men are playing roles rather than acting as themselves.

Dickens's comparison to "the wildest savages in their most barbarous disguise" is deliberately provocative. It enacts the very dehumanisation that the novel elsewhere critiques — the reduction of political actors to subhuman forces. But Dickens's point is precisely that the Revolution has made savages of ordinary men, and that this transformation is itself the horror. The men at the grindstone were not born "savages"; they have been made so by poverty, oppression, and the logic of insurrection.

## Lucie's Witness

Lucie Manette, recently reunited with her husband Charles Darnay (who has been released from prison), watches the scene from the window of Tellson's. Her father, Doctor Manette, has gone out to intervene in the violence, leaving her in a state of terror. When the bell at the gate repeatedly sounds and the grindstone whirs again, Lucie cries out in fear:

> "What is it?" cried Lucie, affrighted. "Hush! The soldiers' swords are sharpened there," said Mr. Lorry. "The place is national property now, and used as a kind of armoury, my love." (032-038)

Mr. Lorry's attempt to reassure Lucie by naming the activity — it is only soldiers sharpening swords — is itself revealing: the domestic and the military have collapsed into one another, and the courtyard of a bank has become an armoury. The phrase "national property" carries a bitter irony: the Revolution has nationalised the bank's courtyard, converting Tellson's — that bastion of English commercial order — into a workshop of revolutionary killing. The boundary between England and France, between commerce and violence, has dissolved.

Lucie's terror reaches its peak when she sees her father among the crowd:

> She could not see her father, but she saw the grindstone, and the crowd of people round it. She saw the red upon the grindstone, and the red upon the faces of the men, and the red upon their arms, and the red — she could not look, she could not look, cried Mr. Lorry — she fainted. (032-037)

The repetition of "red" is one of Dickens's most effective uses of anaphora in the novel. The word is repeated until it loses its descriptive force and becomes a pure sensory assault — a verbal equivalent of the visual horror Lucie experiences. And her faint is not a failure of courage but the body's natural defence against what the mind cannot process. It is the same mechanism that causes Dr. Manette to retreat into shoemaking: when reality becomes unbearable, consciousness withdraws.

## Doctor Manette at the Grindstone

Doctor Manette's presence at the scene adds a layer of complexity to the event. Having been released from the Bastille and restored to his identity, he now ventures into the revolutionary mob to exert his moral authority — and, remarkably, succeeds:

> The Doctor, with his white hair and his quiet face, was among them — was walking among them — was speaking to them — was touching them. (032-033)

The anaphoric "was" constructions convey a sense of steady, deliberate action amid chaos. Dr. Manette moves through the crowd not as a combatant but as a physician moving among patients — touching, speaking, calming. His authority derives not from force but from his history as a Bastille prisoner, which gives him a kind of revolutionary immunity. He is the one figure in the novel who can move between the private and the public, the domestic and the political, without being destroyed by either.

And yet his success at the grindstone is ambiguous. He calms the mob momentarily, but the grindstone keeps turning, and the killings continue. His intervention is local, temporary, and contingent — a reminder that even the most compelling moral authority has limits against the machinery of mass violence.

## The Blood-Stained Wheel

The grindstone operates as a multi-layered symbol. On the most literal level, it is a tool of war preparation. On a deeper level, it represents the grinding, dehumanising machinery of revolutionary violence — a force that consumes individuals and reduces them to blood-smeared components of a larger process.

The morning-after image seals this reading:

> The great grindstone, Earth, had turned when Mr. Lorry looked out again, and the sun was red on the courtyard. But, the lesser grindstone stood alone there in the calm morning air, with a red upon it that the sun had never given, and would never take away. (032-040)

Dickens draws an explicit parallel between the human grindstone and the Earth itself — both turn, both are stained with blood that cannot be removed. The phrase "a red upon it that the sun had never given, and would never take away" suggests an irrevocable contamination: the Revolution has left a permanent stain on the world. The sun, which gives light and life, cannot rival the redness of human violence; nor can it erase it.

## The Earth Metaphor

The phrase "the great grindstone, Earth" elevates the image from a specific historical moment to a universal condition. Dickens implies that history itself is a grindstone: a great turning mechanism that sharpens some instruments and crushes others, indifferent to the human lives caught in its motion.

The contrast between the "great grindstone" (the Earth) and the "lesser grindstone" (the courtyard tool) enacts a kind of allegorical zoom: from the cosmic to the particular, from the eternal to the historical. The Earth turns regardless of human events — "had turned when Mr. Lorry looked out again" — but the human grindstone bears a stain that nature does not. Violence is a human signature, a mark that distinguishes the lesser grindstone from the greater.

| Element | Literal Meaning | Symbolic Meaning |
|---------|----------------|------------------|
| The grindstone | Tool for sharpening blades | Machinery of revolutionary violence |
| The blood | Physical residue of killing | Irreversible guilt and contamination |
| The turning | Rotational motion of sharpening | Historical process, indifferent and relentless |
| The morning sun | Natural light revealing the scene | Moral exposure — what was hidden is revealed |
| The false eyebrows | Improvised disguise | The theatricality of revolutionary identity |

## Connection to Other Images

The grindstone is one of several circular, rotating images in the novel that represent revolutionary process. [[The-Carmagnole|The Carmagnole]] dance is a human grindstone — a rotating mass of bodies that crushes individuality. The [[The-Knitting-Register|knitting]] of Madame Defarge is another form of rotation — the needles looping endlessly, recording names for the guillotine. And the guillotine itself, with its rising and falling blade, is the final expression of this circular, repetitive motion of killing. Dickens weaves these images together to suggest that the Revolution is not a linear progression toward liberty but a closed circle — a mechanism that turns endlessly upon itself.

## Significance

The grindstone scene condenses Dickens's ambivalent attitude toward revolutionary violence into a single, unforgettable image. The men at the grindstone are at once absurd (in their false moustaches), terrifying (in their beastly howling), and pitiable (they are worn-out murderers creeping back to consciousness). Dickens refuses to sentimentalise them — they are "horrible and cruel" — but he also refuses to dehumanise them entirely: they are men, not monsters, and their violence is a product of historical forces as much as individual choice.

The scene also marks Lucie's most direct exposure to revolutionary violence, and her terror in this moment — her dependence on Mr. Lorry's steadying presence — underscores the novel's gender dynamics: feminine domesticity is besieged by masculine political violence, and the home (or the bank that substitutes for it) offers only fragile protection. Dr. Manette's ambiguous success among the crowd suggests that the only effective response to revolutionary violence is not force but moral witness — a theme that will return in the novel's final scenes, when [[Sydney-Carton|Sydney Carton]] takes Charles Darnay's place at the guillotine.
