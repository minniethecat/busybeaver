# The Revolutionary Tribunal

## Overview

The Revolutionary Tribunal is the judicial engine of the Reign of Terror in the final book of *A Tale of Two Cities*. Presided over by a jury of revolutionary citizens that includes Jacques Three among their number, the tribunal dispenses a form of revolutionary justice that is swift, merciless, and ultimately indifferent to individual guilt or innocence (036-001). It is before this tribunal that Charles Darnay is twice tried — first acquitted through the influence of Dr. Manette, then condemned to death by the same man's long-buried letter.

## The Tribunal as Institution

Dickens presents the Revolutionary Tribunal as an inversion of justice — a court where the forms of legal process are preserved but emptied of the substance of fairness. The tribunal's proceedings are characterised by:

- **Rapid verdicts**: Trials are dispatched in minutes; the guillotine's appetite for victims demands efficiency (036-001)
- **Presumption of guilt**: The accused are assumed enemies of the [[Republic]] until proven otherwise
- **Denunciation as evidence**: Private grudges and political denunciations substitute for documented proof (040-121)
- **Popular participation**: The courtroom is packed with tricoteuses — the knitting women who attend each session as spectators and judges

The tribunal sits in the Palais de Justice, the same building that once housed the courts of the old régime. Dickens notes the grim continuity in the atmosphere and the proceedings (034-011):

> A revolutionary tribunal in the capital, and forty or fifty thousand revolutionary committees all over the land... (034-011)

## Darnay's First Appearance

Darnay's first appearance before the tribunal occurs after his arrest upon returning to France. He is charged as an émigré and former aristocrat — an Evrémonde — whose very name is a capital offence under revolutionary law (030-057).

Dr. Manette, now revered among the revolutionaries as a former Bastille prisoner, speaks on his son-in-law's behalf. His testimony carries such moral weight that the tribunal acquits Darnay. But Manette's influence is not sufficient to protect Darnay from further denunciation: the machinery of revolutionary justice is designed to resist individual intercession.

## The Second Trial

Darnay's second trial, held in Book 3, Chapter 10, is the novel's climactic courtroom scene. This time he is denounced not by one but by three accusers:

1. **[[Monsieur]] Defarge** — as the representative of revolutionary Saint Antoine
2. **Madame Defarge** — whose knitting register has recorded the Evrémonde name since Book 1
3. **Dr. Manette** — whose Bastille letter, discovered by Defarge in the cell of 105 North Tower, is read aloud in court

The reading of Manette's letter transforms the proceedings. The jury, which had seemed inclined to clemency, is swept by a wave of revolutionary fury:

> The dread tribunal of five Judges, Public Prosecutor, and determined [[Jury]], sat every day (036-001)

Darnay is condemned to death by guillotine within twenty-four hours (040-121).

## Madame Defarge's Role

Madame Defarge's testimony at the tribunal demonstrates her role as the tribunal's most implacable agent. She reveals that she is the surviving sister of the peasant woman and her brother whom the Evrémonde brothers destroyed — a fact she has concealed through the entire novel. Her question is rhetorical: the tribunal exists precisely to deny such desires. The revelation of her personal connection to the Evrémonde crimes re-frames her knitting register: it is not merely a political instrument but a record of personal vengeance (021-044).

## The Tribunal as Theatrical Space

The courtroom scenes at the tribunal are among the novel's most theatrical. Dickens describes the spectators as a "jury of the audience," and the proceedings function as both legal hearing and public spectacle:

| Theatrical Element | Function |
|-------------------|----------|
| The tricoteuses in the galleries | A Greek chorus of revolutionary women, knitting death sentences into wool |
| The president's bell | Punctuates verdicts like a stage direction |
| The crowd's roaring | Functions as both jury and audience, delivering verdicts through volume |
| The raised platform | Physical elevation that mimics both a dock and a stage |

The theatricality is not incidental but essential: revolutionary justice requires an audience, and the tribunal's legitimacy rests on the visible participation of the people (040-121). Justice must be seen to be done, and "seen" means "seen to condemn."

## The Irony of Manette's Testimony

The structural irony of the tribunal scenes centres on Dr. Manette. In the first trial, his reputation as a Bastille martyr secures Darnay's acquittal. In the second, his own letter — written in the same Bastille cell, preserved by the same revolutionary fervour that honours him — secures Darnay's condemnation (036-001). The tribunal thus enacts the novel's central paradox: the same revolutionary logic that liberates the innocent Manette destroys the innocent Darnay.

Manette's desperate attempt to undo the effect of his own words is his most agonised moment in the novel. But the tribunal's verdict, once pronounced, is final; revolutionary justice admits no appeal.

## Significance

The Revolutionary Tribunal represents the novel's profoundest engagement with the problem of revolutionary justice. Dickens does not argue that the old régime's courts were fair — they were instruments of class power. Nor does he argue that the revolutionary court was uniquely evil — it dispensed a form of rough justice that reflected the anger of the oppressed. Rather, the tribunal demonstrates that a system in which evidence is replaced by denunciation, and acquittal is foreclosed by hereditary guilt, can produce outcomes as unjust as the tyranny it replaced (040-121). The tribunal scenes are the novel's warning: that the logic of vengeance, however historically justified, cannot produce justice.
