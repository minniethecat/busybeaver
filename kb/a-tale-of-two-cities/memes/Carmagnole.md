# Carmagnole

## Definition

[[The Carmagnole]] is a revolutionary song and dance that originated during the French Revolution. Its name derives from the short jacket worn by the *sans-culottes* — the working-class revolutionaries of Paris — and the dance itself was a form of popular celebration performed in the streets, often around the [[Guillotine|guillotine]] or the Liberty Tree. In *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*, Dickens presents the Carmagnole as an emblem of revolutionary frenzy — a dance in which the energy of liberation becomes indistinguishable from the energy of destruction.

## The Dance in A Tale of Two Cities

Dickens's description of the Carmagnole occurs in Book 2, Chapter 21, in the aftermath of the [[Storming-of-the-Bastille|storming of the Bastille]]. The scene is renderered with a hallucinatory intensity:

> ...they danced to the popular Revolution song, keeping a ferocious time that was like a sort of demon's dance. (035-032)

The dancers are the citizens of [[Saint-Antoine|Saint Antoine]], and their dance is both celebration and threat:

- **The rhythm**: The Carmagnole is described as having a "ferocious time" — the word "ferocious" converting the dance from an expression of joy to an expression of barely contained violence
- **The movement**: Dancers link arms, form chains, and whirl through the streets with a momentum that seems to possess them rather than being directed by them
- **The numbers**: The dance swells as more and more citizens join it, transforming from individual expression to collective force
- **The sound**: The song's lyrics — political and often obscene — are shouted rather than sung, the volume itself an assertion of popular power

Dickens describes the Carmagnole as a form of mass possession — "a struggle made for the dance, under the circumstances, of being a demoniacal insanity" — rather than a voluntary social activity. The dancers are not choosing to dance; they are seized by a force larger than themselves.

## The Carmagnole as Revolutionary Expression

The Carmagnole serves several functions in the novel's representation of revolutionary culture:

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Communal bonding | The dance unites the citizens of Saint Antoine in a shared physical experience |
| Class defiance | The Carmagnole mocks the formal dances of the aristocracy with its raw, street-level energy |
| Political education | The lyrics of the Carmagnole celebrate revolutionary victories and denounce enemies of the [[Republic]] |
| Terroristic threat | The dance is performed around the guillotine, and its energy is indistinguishable from the energy of execution |

The Carmagnole is, in this sense, the Revolution made visible — a physical manifestation of the collective energy that has overthrown the monarchy, stormed [[The-Bastille|the Bastille]], and now dances around the [[Scaffold|scaffold]].

## The Dance and the Guillotine

Dickens's most disturbing image of the Carmagnole associates it directly with the guillotine. The dance is performed in [[III. The Shadow|the shadow]] of the scaffold, and the rhythm of the dance echoes the rhythm of the falling blade:

> They danced to the popular Revolution song, keeping a ferocious time... (035-032)

The guillotine and the Carmagnole are presented as two expressions of the same revolutionary energy — one lethal, the other festive, but both driven by the same implacable rhythm. The dance does not oppose or critique the executions; it celebrates them. The Carmagnole is the guillotine's music.

## Lucie's Encounter with the Carmagnole

The Carmagnole appears at a crucial moment in [[Lucie-Manette|Lucie Manette]]'s experience of Paris. Standing at a window, she watches the dancers whirl past in the street below — a spectacle of revolutionary energy that is at once mesmerising and horrifying:

> Lucie looked at her father, who had come to the window and was looking down. She saw the tears that ran silently down his face...

Dr. Manette's tears, as he watches the Carmagnole, are among the most ambiguous moments in the novel. Are they tears of joy at the liberation of the people from the tyranny that imprisoned him? Tears of horror at the violence of the liberation? Or tears of recognition — the recognition that the energy he helped unleash is now beyond his control?

Dickens does not tell us, and the ambiguity is deliberate. The Carmagnole, like the Revolution itself, is irreducible to a single moral judgment.

## The Ambivalence of Popular Energy

The Carmagnole scene encapsulates Dickens's characteristic ambivalence about revolutionary crowds. The dance is:

- **Energetic but destructive**: The same energy that liberates also kills
- **Democratising but anonymising**: The dance includes everyone but erases individuality
- **Joyful but violent**: The celebration occurs around the guillotine
- **Natural but possessed**: The dancers are not acting but are being acted upon

This ambivalence is not evasion but the novel's central political insight: revolutionary energy is a force that exceeds moral categories. It cannot be judged as simply good or simply evil, because it is both at once — and because the people who embody it have been shaped by centuries of oppression that the novel has spent hundreds of pages documenting.

## Historical Note

The historical Carmagnole song was composed in 1792 and became one of the most popular revolutionary anthems. Its refrain — "Dansons la Carmagnole, vive le son du canon!" ("Let us dance the Carmagnole, long live the sound of the cannon!") — captured the Revolution's fusion of festivity and violence. The Carmagnole was banned after the Thermidorian Reaction of 1794, when the fall of Robespierre brought the Terror to an end, but the song and dance survived in popular memory as emblems of revolutionary Paris.
