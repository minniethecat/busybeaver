# Carton's Prophetic Vision

Carton's Prophetic Vision is the imagined speech that closes *A Tale of Two Cities*, spoken not by the narrator but by [[Sydney-Carton]] himself as he stands at the foot of the guillotine awaiting execution. The passage — culminating in "It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done" (045-051) — is the novel's most famous literary artifact, a work within the work that has entered the common inheritance of English literature independent of its narrative context.

> "It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done; it is a far, far better rest that I go to than I have ever known." (045-051)

## Structure

The vision unfolds as a series of prophecies about the future Carton will not live to see: that [[Lucie-Manette|Lucie]] and [[Charles-Darnay]] will live long and happily; that their child, named Sydney, will grow to be a just and honoured man; that the Revolution will eventually burn itself out and "a beautiful city and a brilliant people will rise" from the abyss; that [[Doctor-Manette|Dr. Manette]] will die peacefully, old and beloved; and that Carton himself will be remembered in the hearts of those for whom he died. Each prophecy is a consolation for a specific grief, and together they form a vision of redemption achieved through sacrifice.

The vision is structured as a chain of "I see" clauses, each one a window into a different aspect of the future. This anaphoric structure gives the passage its liturgical quality — it reads less like fiction and more like scripture. Carton, who has spoken little and felt much throughout the novel, finally finds words adequate to his inner life at the moment he loses it.

## Rhetorical Form

The passage employs anaphoric repetition — each sentence begins with "I see" — to create the effect of prophetic utterance. The rhetoric is deliberately elevated, moving from the conversational register of the novel's dialogue into a cadenced, almost liturgical prose. This shift in register signals that Carton, at the moment of his death, has transcended the broken, dissolute man he was through the novel's first two books. His final vision is the proof of his transformation.

Dickens's choice to render the vision as Carton's internal speech rather than as narrated summary is crucial. By letting Carton speak — even if only in his mind — Dickens grants him an authority he has never possessed in life. The man who described himself as "a disappointed drudge" who cares "for no man on earth, and no man on earth cares for me" becomes the novel's prophet, its voice of ultimate meaning.

## The Closing Sentence

The vision concludes with what is arguably the most famous closing line in the English novel: "It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done; it is a far, far better rest that I go to than I have ever known." The sentence's structure — the repeated intensifier "far, far," the comparative form "better," the parallelism of "thing" and "rest" — creates the effect of a man making peace with death by converting it into an act. What could be a despairing end becomes, through Carton's rhetoric, a triumph of will over circumstance.

The grammar of the sentence is worth close attention. "Thing that I do" is present tense — the sacrifice is ongoing, not completed. "Rest that I go to" is future-oriented — death as destination, not cessation. The doubling of "far, far" is the only place in the entire novel where Dickens uses this construction, making it a deliberate rhetorical intensification rather than a habitual tic. And the word "better" — repeated, comparative, open-ended — refuses the absolutism of "best." Carton does not claim perfection; he claims improvement. His final act is better than his previous life. That is enough.

## The Vision as Theological Statement

Though Dickens was not a conventionally religious novelist, Carton's vision draws heavily on Christian imagery and rhetoric. The "I see" construction echoes the prophetic books of the Old Testament. The vision of a redeemed city recalls the Book of Revelation. And Carton's substitutionary death — the innocent dying for the guilty — is unmistakably Christological.

Yet Dickens secularises these patterns. Carton is no Christ; he is a failed lawyer, a drunkard, a man who has wasted his gifts. His sacrifice does not redeem the world — it redeems a single family. The vision imagines no heaven, only a better earthly future. And Carton does not rise from the dead; he is remembered, which in Dickens's moral universe is a form of resurrection.

## Literary Legacy

The passage has had an extraordinary afterlife. It has been quoted in political speeches, woven into other literary works, adapted for film and television, and taught to generations of schoolchildren as a touchstone of English prose. Its separation from the novel — many who know the final line have never read the book — is itself a mark of its status as a work within the work, a free-standing literary artifact generated by but not dependent upon its narrative source.

The closing line has become, in popular culture, shorthand for noble self-sacrifice — quoted at funerals, memorials, and moments of public mourning. This cultural absorption has paradoxically dulled the line's force for some readers, who encounter it as cliché before they encounter it as literature. Reading it in context — as the culmination of 45 chapters of carefully built character and theme — restores its power.
