# Drawbridge

A **drawbridge** is a movable bridge at the entrance of a fortified structure, typically hinged at one end and capable of being raised or lowered to control access across a surrounding ditch or moat. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, drawbridges appear as defining features of the [[The-Bastille|Bastille]] fortress, symbolizing the impenetrable old-regime architecture that the Revolution ultimately breaches.

## The Bastille's Drawbridges

Charles Dickens describes the Bastille with precise architectural language, emphasizing its defensive features. The fortress is repeatedly characterized by its "deep ditches" and "double drawbridge," along with "massive stone walls" and "eight great towers" (027-039). A variation of the description — "deep ditch, single drawbridge" — appears in the same passage (027-040), suggesting multiple fortified entry points. The drawbridges represent the physical barriers of the Ancien Régime: lowered to admit prisoners and supplies during normal operations, they become symbols of the fortress's oppressive isolation.

> Deep ditches, double drawbridge, massive stone walls, eight great towers... (027-039)

The storming of the [[The-Bastille|Bastille]] on 14 July 1789 involved breaching these very drawbridges — an act that transformed them from symbols of absolute power into monuments of revolutionary triumph. The drawbridge, once lowered only at the command of the governor, was forced down by the crowd, reversing the direction of power.

## Broader Fortification Imagery

The drawbridge motif extends beyond the Bastille. In Book the Second, the [[Tellsons-Bank|Tellson's Bank]] Paris branch is described in language that evokes a defended fortress "in expectation of a drawbridge" (006-100). The mender of roads, traveling toward Paris, observes a landscape of "guard-houses, gates, trenches, and drawbridges" that seem to him like preparations for an immense military campaign (029-031). This recurring imagery links drawbridges to the broader theme of defense, imprisonment, and the architecture of control that pervades the novel.

> ...guard-houses, gates, trenches, and drawbridges, seemed to the mender of roads to be making ready to resist an immense assault. (029-031)

## Symbolic Function

The drawbridge operates as a threshold figure in Dickens's symbolic vocabulary. It is the point of crossing between two states — inside and outside, imprisonment and freedom, old order and new. When [[Monsieur-Defarge|Defarge]] leads the revolutionaries across the Bastille's lowered drawbridge, the crossing is irreversible: the fortress that had swallowed prisoners for centuries is now devoured by the people. The drawbridge thus enacts the novel's central [[Revolution|revolutionary]] inversion — the barrier that once kept the people out now lets them in.

The architectural precision of Dickens's descriptions — "double drawbridge," "deep ditches," "eight great towers" — is not incidental decoration. It grounds the novel's symbolic action in material reality, making the Bastille not an abstract emblem of tyranny but a specific, physical place whose walls and bridges can be touched, breached, and crossed.
