# Monsieur

*Monsieur* (French for "my lord" or "sir") was the standard honorific applied to men of rank in pre-Revolutionary France. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, the term functions as both a title of aristocratic privilege and, through its eventual suppression, a marker of the social transformation wrought by the Revolution.

## Definition

The word derives from *mon sieur*, literally "my lord." By the eighteenth century it had become the standard polite form of address for any gentleman, but in the context of the novel it carries a specific class weight. The term distinguishes those entitled to deference from those obliged to give it.

## Monsieur in the Ancien Régime

Before the Revolution, "Monsieur" was part of a finely calibrated system of honorifics that encoded the entire social hierarchy of France. At its apex stood the royal family: the King was "Sa Majesté," the Dauphin was "Monseigneur," and the King's eldest brother was distinguished by the capitalised, article-free "Monsieur" — a title so elevated that it needed no further qualification. Below this uppermost tier, "Monsieur" combined with a title ("Monsieur le Marquis," "Monsieur le Comte," "Monsieur l'Évêque") to mark every rung of the aristocratic ladder.

For the bourgeoisie and the professions, "Monsieur" attached to the surname — "Monsieur [[Gabelle]]," "Monsieur Defarge" — indicated respectability without nobility. The distance between "Monsieur le Marquis" and "Monsieur Gabelle" was the distance between two orders of society, and the shared honorific only made the gap more visible.

Dickens understood that titles do not merely describe social reality — they create it. In the world of Saint Antoine before the Revolution, the word "Monsieur" is a daily reminder of who may command and who must obey. The child killed by the Marquis's carriage in Chapter 7 of Book the Second is the child of a father who would never be called "Monsieur" — and it is precisely this asymmetry that the Revolution exists to overturn.

## Usage in the Novel

The novel deploys "Monsieur" in two distinct registers. The first is deferential — functionaries, peasants, and servants address their social superiors with the title, as when a postmaster addresses the guard: "See here then, Monsieur the Officer" (006-104). The second is ironic — the narrator often uses "Monsieur" before a title to underscore the absurdity of aristocratic pretension, most prominently in the recurring figure of "Monsieur the Marquis" (015-002, 015-004).

The Marquis St. Evrémonde is the novel's most concentrated example of the title's meaning. He is invariably "Monsieur the Marquis" — the honorific and the title fused into a single assertion of station. When he orders his carriage driven at reckless speed through the streets of Saint Antoine, killing a child, the word "Monsieur" becomes inseparable from the arrogance it denotes:

> With a wild rattle and clatter, and an inhuman abandonment of consideration not easy to be understood in these days, the carriage dashed through the streets and swept round corners, with women screaming before it, and men clutching each other and clutching children out of its way. (015-001)

The Marquis's response to the death — tossing a coin to the grieving father as though the matter were settled — completes the portrait of a man for whom "Monsieur" is not courtesy but armour.

Beyond the Marquis, the title attaches to characters whose social function is defined by service or middle-class respectability: "Monsieur Gabelle" (029-036, 029-044, 030-066), the functionary who manages the Evrémonde estate; and "Monsieur Lorry" (036-033), the English banker whose testimony at Darnay's trial in Paris carries weight precisely because of his foreign neutrality. These characters occupy the intermediate space of the honorific — above the mass of the unnamed poor, far below the titled aristocracy — and their "Monsieur" marks the boundary between deference owed and deference received.

## Revolutionary Suppression

The Revolution's rejection of the honorific is one of the novel's most telling details. The Revolutionary tribunals replace "Monsieur" and "Madame" with the egalitarian "[[Citizen]]" and "Citizeness" — a linguistic revolution that parallels the political one. The man who was "Monsieur heretofore the Marquis" (030-057) is now merely a prisoner before the tribunal, stripped of the title that was once his identity.

The linguistic shift was historically accurate. In October 1792, the National Convention formally abolished the use of "Monsieur" and "Madame" in official documents, replacing them with "Citoyen" and "Citoyenne." The reform reached into every corner of daily life: letters, legal documents, shop signs, and even the theatre were required to adopt the new forms. To address someone as "Monsieur" after 1792 was to mark oneself as a counter-revolutionary.

Dickens captures the coercive force of this linguistic revolution in the scene where Charles Darnay, newly arrived in Paris, is stopped at the barrier and told he must be conducted to Paris "as an emigrant" — the new vocabulary of the [[Republic]] already determining his fate before any trial has taken place. The old titles persist only in memory and in the speech of those outside the Revolutionary apparatus. Monsieur Defarge, though a committed revolutionary, retains the title in the narrative's address — a reminder that language changes more slowly than politics, and that the Revolution's linguistic ambitions always exceeded its actual reach.
