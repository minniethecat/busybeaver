## Overview

The Seine is the river that flows through the heart of Paris. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, it appears in the novel's final chapters as an atmospheric presence — its rising mist and quiet quays forming the backdrop to Sydney Carton's final nocturnal wanderings before his sacrifice.

## In the Novel

The Seine is first evoked atmospherically as evening falls on Paris: a rising mist from the river signals the approach of darkness (035-050). In the hours before his execution, Sydney Carton crosses the Seine for the lighter streets of the Left Bank, then crosses back again toward the darker, more obscure quarters where he has concealed his plan (039-089). He finds brief refreshment at a quiet spot on the quay beside the river, gathering strength for his final acts (040-003).

## Significance

The Seine in these closing chapters serves as a geographical and symbolic boundary. Carton's crossings of the river mirror his internal movement between despair and resolve, darkness and light. The river's mist and flow evoke the passage of time and the approach of dawn — and with it, death. It is a quiet witness to the novel's climactic transformation.
