# Foulon's Death

## Overview

The killing of Foulon is one of the most savage mob executions in *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*, occurring during the revolutionary upheaval of Book 2, Chapter 22 ("[[XXII. The Sea Still Rises|The Sea Still Rises]]"). Foulon, a wealthy official who had advised hungry peasants to eat grass, becomes a symbol of aristocratic contempt for the poor. His death — dragged through the streets, beaten, strung up from a lamp-post, his mouth stuffed with the grass he once prescribed — represents the violent culmination of the revolution's logic in [[Saint-Antoine|Saint Antoine]].

The event serves as a dark companion to the storming of [[The-Bastille|the Bastille]] (027-037). Where the Bastille's fall was a strategic victory against a symbol of royal power, Foulon's death is personal: a cry of [[Vengeance|vengeance]] against a specific man whose words and deeds the people remember. It marks the point at which revolutionary justice becomes indistinguishable from revolutionary cruelty.

## Trigger: The Memory of an Insult

The event begins not with Foulon himself but with the collective memory of his infamous remark. In the Defarges' wine-shop, the revolutionary Jacquerie have gathered when [[Madame-Defarge|Madame Defarge]] raises the question:

> "Does everybody here recall old Foulon, who told the famished people that they might eat grass, and who died, and went to Hell?" (028-011)

> "Everybody!" from all throats.

The unanimous response is telling. Foulon had been believed dead — "who died, and went to Hell" — yet the memory of his contempt for the poor has not died with him. The revelation that he is alive (having faked his death to escape revolutionary justice) transforms [[The-Crowd|the crowd]] from a gathering into a mob. The insult of "let them eat grass" becomes the unifying cry that propels the pursuit.

## The Hunt

The pursuit of Foulon is instantaneous and total. Saint Antoine empties itself in minutes as men, women, and children pour into the streets:

> Armed men and women flocked out of the Quarter so fast, and drew even these last dregs after them with such a force of suction, that within a quarter of an hour there was not a human creature in Saint Antoine's bosom but a few old crones and the wailing children. (028-023)

The women of Saint Antoine are depicted as particularly terrifying in their rage, weaving personal grief into political action. Dickens renders their cries as a choral lament:

> Villain Foulon taken, my sister! Old Foulon taken, my mother! Miscreant Foulon taken, my daughter! ... Foulon who told my old father that he might eat grass, when I had no bread to give him! Foulon who told my baby it might suck grass, when these breasts were dry with want! (028-022)

This passage is one of the novel's most powerful uses of the female revolutionary voice. Unlike Madame Defarge's cold, strategic [[XV. Knitting|knitting]], these women are driven by starving children and withered parents — their fury is not political theory but bodily deprivation made audible.

## The Execution

Foulon is seized at the Hôtel de Ville and dragged through the streets. The execution is a protracted ordeal — the mob does not kill him quickly but makes his death a public theatre of retribution:

> Down, and up, and head foremost on the steps of the building; now, on his knees; now, on his feet; now, on his back; dragged, and struck at, and stifled by the bunches of grass and straw that were thrust into his face by hundreds of hands; torn, bruised, panting, bleeding, yet always entreating and beseeching for mercy. (028-030)

Madame Defarge presides over the scene with a chilling detachment. When Foulon is bound with ropes and a bunch of grass tied to his back, she applauds the theatrical irony:

> "See!" cried madame, pointing with her knife. "See the old villain bound with ropes. That was well done to tie a bunch of grass upon his back. Ha, ha! That was well done. Let him eat it now!" (028-030)

The ritual of the lamp-post — already established by the earlier scene of the broken wine-cask and the writing of "[[Blood|BLOOD]]" on the wall (005-004) — completes its grim trajectory here. Foulon is hauled to the fatal lamp where Madame Defarge releases him "as a cat might have done to a mouse" (028-030). The rope breaks twice before holding on the third attempt, and finally:

> his head was soon upon a pike, with grass enough in the mouth for all Saint Antoine to dance at the sight of. (028-030)

## Aftermath: The Son-in-Law

The day's violence does not end with Foulon. Before nightfall, the mob learns that Foulon's son-in-law is approaching Paris under an armed guard of five hundred cavalry. The crowd seizes him too:

> Saint Antoine wrote his crimes on flaring sheets of paper, seized him — would have torn him out of the breast of an army to bear Foulon company — set his head and heart on pikes, and carried the three spoils of the day, in Wolf-procession through the streets. (028-030)

The image of the "Wolf-procession" — severed heads and hearts carried on pikes — recalls the classical [[VI. Triumph|triumph]], perverted into a cannibal feast. The revolution has acquired momentum that no single death can satisfy.

## Significance

Foulon's death occupies a distinct place in the novel's portrayal of revolutionary violence. Where the [[Storming-of-the-Bastille]] is a strategic act against an institution and [[The-Gaspard-Execution]] is the state's brutal suppression of a lone [[Peasant|peasant]], Foulon's killing is the first time the mob itself becomes [[Executioner|executioner]]. The episode introduces the rhetoric of grass-as-insult — an [[Aristocrat|aristocrat]]'s contempt for the starving — and transforms it into the literal instrument of his death.

The scene also develops Madame Defarge's character from observer to director. Her clapping and her cat-and-mouse metaphor (028-030) foreshadow her later role in [[The Revolutionary Tribunal|the Revolutionary Tribunal]] and her pursuit of the Evrémonde family. The lamp-post, which would claim many more victims in the coming months, here receives its first named victim in the novel's narrative.

The chapter title, "[[The-Sea|The Sea]] Still Rises," reinforces the event's meaning. Foulon is not a cause but a symptom — the sea of revolutionary anger was already rising; his death is a wave, one of many that will follow.
