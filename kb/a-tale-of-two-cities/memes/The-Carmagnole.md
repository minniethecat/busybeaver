## Overview

The Carmagnole is a revolutionary street dance that appears twice in *A Tale of Two Cities*, each time at moments of intense dramatic tension. The dance — named after a song and costume of the French Revolution — sweeps through the streets of Paris as a manifestation of popular revolutionary energy, at once celebratory and menacing. For Lucie Manette, the Carmagnole represents the incomprehensible, overwhelming force of the revolutionary crowd: a force that can erupt without warning and sweep away everything in its path.

The Carmagnole belongs to a series of crowd scenes that Dickens deploys throughout the novel — from [[The-Wine-Cask|the wine cask in Saint Antoine]] to [[Storming-of-the-Bastille|the storming of the Bastille]] and later the massed spectators of [[The-Revolutionary-Tribunal|the Revolutionary Tribunal]]. In each case, the crowd is not a backdrop but an actor in its own right, possessed of a collective psychology that Dickens renders with forensic precision. The Carmagnole is the purest expression of this theme: a dance in which the individual is wholly absorbed into the mass.


## The Carmagnole in the Novel

The Carmagnole first appears as Lucie stands in the doorway of the wood-sawyer's house, waiting for her husband's fate to be decided. The dance bursts upon her without warning:

> This was the Carmagnole. As it passed, leaving Lucie frightened and bewildered in the doorway of the wood-sawyer's house, the feathery snow fell as quietly and lay as white and soft, as if it had never been. (035-033)

The contrast between the violence of the dance and the quietness of the falling snow is characteristically Dickensian. The snow — a recurring image throughout the novel, associated with purity, burial, and the erasure of identity — falls "as if it had never been" disturbed, as if the Carmagnole, for all its noise and fury, leaves no mark on the natural world. This indifference of nature to human passion is one of the novel's quiet themes.

Earlier in the same passage, Dickens describes the dance with a density of sensory detail that makes the reader feel its physical impact:

> There could not be fewer than five hundred people, and they were dancing like five thousand demons. There was no other music than their own singing. They danced to the popular Revolution song, keeping a ferocious time that was like a sort of demon's dance. (035-032)

The word "ferocious" is carefully chosen: it converts the dance from an expression of joy to an expression of barely contained violence. And the comparison to demons — "five thousand demons," "a demon's dance" — strips the crowd of its humanity, transforming the citizens of Saint Antoine into something infernal. Dickens is not simply describing a street festival; he is painting a scene of mass possession.

The Carmagnole reappears at the trial of Charles Darnay before the Revolutionary Tribunal. After Darnay embraces his wife, the courtroom crowd erupts into the dance:

> a few of the people fell to dancing. Instantly, all the rest fell to dancing, and the courtyard overflowed with the Carmagnole. Then, they elevated into the vacant chair a young woman from the crowd to be carried as the Goddess of Liberty... (036-040)

The scene is both grotesque and ecstatic. The crowd does not merely watch the trial — it participates in a mass ritual that transforms the judicial proceeding into a street festival. The elevation of the "Goddess of Liberty" — a young woman plucked from the crowd and placed in a chair — is an improvised, democratic parody of the old régime's ritual forms. The Revolution has its own liturgy, and the Carmagnole is its central rite.

The speed of the contagion is key: "a few of the people fell to dancing. Instantly, all the rest fell to dancing." There is no decision, no hesitation. The dance spreads through suggestion, bypassing reason and will — a form of revolutionary epidemiology that Dickens tracks with the precision of a naturalist observing a phenomenon.

After the trial, Mr. Lorry is described as having fought his way through the crowd:

> After grasping the hand of Mr. Lorry, who came panting in breathless from his struggle against the waterspout of the Carmagnole... (036-041)

The "waterspout" metaphor captures the dance's elemental, uncontrollable quality: it is a natural force, not a reasoned action, and resisting it is as futile as fighting the sea. Dickens uses similar hydrological language throughout the novel's revolutionary passages — the [[The-Wine-Cask|wine cask]] spills like blood, the crowd flows through the streets like a river — and the Carmagnole is the culmination of this imagery: the Revolution as flood, sweeping away everything in its path.

## The Revolutionary Crowd

The Carmagnole is the novel's most vivid representation of the revolutionary crowd as a distinct entity — not a collection of individuals but a single organism with its own will and momentum.

| Crowd Attribute | Expression in the Carmagnole |
|----------------|------------------------------|
| Spontaneity | The dance erupts instantly, without leadership or planning |
| Contagion | "a few fell to dancing. Instantly, all the rest fell to dancing" |
| Inclusivity | Men, women, children all participate |
| Ritualism | The elevation of the Goddess of Liberty mimics religious ceremony |
| Indifference to individuals | Lucie is "frightened and bewildered" — the crowd takes no notice of her |

Dickens's crowd theory, as expressed through the Carmagnole, is fundamentally psychological rather than political. The crowd is not driven by ideas or grievances but by a kind of emotional contagion — a "ferocious time" that seizes each participant and merges them into a single, undifferentiated mass. This is not an argument about the justice or injustice of the Revolution; it is an observation about how human beings behave when they cease to be individuals.

The Carmagnole also serves as a structural counterpoint to the novel's earlier crowd scenes. In [[Storming-of-the-Bastille|the storming of the Bastille]], the crowd is purposeful — it has a specific target and a specific grievance. By the time of the Carmagnole, purpose has dissolved into pure energy. The dance is the Revolution stripped of its political content, reduced to its raw emotional core.


## Lucie and the Carmagnole

For Lucie, the Carmagnole represents the outer limit of her capacity to engage with the Revolution. She can endure the abstract threat to her husband, but the physical, sensory assault of the dancing crowd — the noise, the movement, the proximity of bodies — overwhelms her. The Carmagnole, more than any speech or political argument, communicates to her the Revolution's utter indifference to domestic feeling.

Yet Lucie is not merely a passive victim. Her daily walks to the street corner where Darnay might see her from his prison window are themselves a form of participation in revolutionary Paris — a quiet, personal counter-ritual to the collective frenzy of the Carmagnole. Dickens sets these two dances against each other: the public, violent, masculine Carmagnole and the private, faithful, feminine vigil.

There is a further layer of pathos in Lucie's encounter with the dance. She has come to Paris to save her husband, armed only with her domestic virtues — love, patience, fidelity. The Carmagnole is the negation of everything she represents: it is public where she is private, collective where she is individual, violent where she is gentle. Her "frightened and bewildered" response is not weakness but the natural reaction of a private person confronted by a force that has no category for the private.


## The Carmagnole and the Guillotine

Though Dickens does not explicitly stage the Carmagnole at the foot of the guillotine, the dance and the scaffold are linked throughout the novel as twin expressions of revolutionary energy. Both operate through rhythm — the rising and falling of the blade, the "ferocious time" of the dance — and both dissolve the boundary between spectator and participant. The crowd that dances the Carmagnole is the same crowd that watches the executions, and Dickens implies that the two activities are driven by the same psychological impulse.

> They danced to the popular Revolution song, keeping a ferocious time... (035-032)

The word "time" here carries a double meaning: it refers to the musical beat of the dance but also to the historical moment — a "ferocious time" in which dancing and killing have become indistinguishable expressions of the same revolutionary fervor. The Carmagnole is the guillotine's music; the guillotine is the Carmagnole's final figure.


## Significance

The Carmagnole serves Dickens's broader purpose of rendering the Revolution as a sensory and psychological experience rather than a political abstraction. By focusing on the dance — its sound, its movement, its effect on bystanders — he makes the Revolution tangible. The Carmagnole is the Revolution as pure event: unplanned, irresistible, frightening, and, in its own terms, joyful. It is the sound of a world being unmade and remade in real time.

In the novel's architecture, the Carmagnole occupies a pivotal position between the purposeful violence of [[Storming-of-the-Bastille|the Bastille]] and the mechanised terror of [[The-Revolutionary-Tribunal|the Tribunal]]. It is the moment when revolutionary energy ceases to be directed at specific targets and becomes a self-sustaining force — a dance that dances itself, a crowd that needs no reason beyond its own momentum. In this sense, the Carmagnole is not merely an episode in the novel but its central metaphor: the Revolution as a dance from which no one can abstain.
