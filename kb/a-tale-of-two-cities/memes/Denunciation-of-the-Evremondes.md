# Denunciation of the Evremondes

The Denunciation of the Evremondes is the formal accusation submitted by [[Monsieur-Defarge]] and [[Madame-Defarge]] to the Revolutionary Tribunal, charging [[Charles-Darnay]] — the nephew and heir of the Marquis St. Evremonde — with crimes against the Republic. Along with Dr. Manette's Written Account, it constitutes the documentary foundation for Darnay's second trial and death sentence.

## Composition and Signatories

The denunciation carries three signatures: Defarge, his wife, and Dr. Alexandre Manette. Manette's inclusion is grimly ironic — his signature, which he affixed years earlier during his revolutionary enthusiasm, is now read back to condemn his own son-in-law. When Manette protests that he denounced the Evremonde *brothers*, not their descendants, the tribunal is unmoved. The denunciation, once written, has a life of its own, independent of its authors' later intentions (040-121).

The timing of the denunciation reflects the Revolution's acceleration: what was once a measured act of justice against two aristocratic criminals has become, in the Tribunal's hands, a weapon against an entire family line. The denunciation names Darnay as "at heart and by descent an Aristocrat, an enemy of the Republic, a notorious oppressor of the People" (040-125).

## The Culture of Denunciation in Revolutionary France

The denunciation was not merely a legal instrument in Revolutionary France — it was a defining social practice. The Law of Suspects (17 September 1793) created a legal framework in which any citizen could denounce any other as a suspect, and the accused bore the burden of proving their innocence. Denunciation became both a civic duty and a weapon of personal score-settling; neighbourhood committees and revolutionary surveillance committees actively solicited denunciations as proof of patriotic vigilance.

Dickens captures this culture in the scenes surrounding Darnay's arrest. The Defarges' denunciation is not an impulsive act but a carefully prepared document, reflecting the bureaucratic rationality that coexisted with Revolutionary violence. Madame Defarge's knitting — her encoded register of the condemned — is the domestic counterpart of the formal denunciation: one woman's private archive of grievances, converted through revolutionary procedure into a death sentence.

The denunciation of the Evremondes also illustrates what the historian François Furet called the Revolution's "logic of the worst" — the tendency for each phase of the Revolution to radicalise the accusations of the previous one. The Defarges' denunciation of the elder Evremonde brothers was originally an act of justice against specific criminals. In the hands of the Tribunal of 1793, it becomes a condemnation of an entire bloodline, then of a whole class, and finally — in the shadow of the guillotine — of anyone whose name can be connected to the old order.

## Content and Structure

The denunciation rests on two evidentiary pillars. The first is Darnay's Evremonde blood: the Defarges argue that aristocratic descent is itself a crime, regardless of individual conduct, because the crimes of the *ancien régime* inhere in the bloodline. The second is Dr. Manette's Bastille manuscript, which the denunciation presents as an eyewitness account of the elder Evremondes' atrocities — atrocities for which the nephew, as heir, bears inherited guilt.

The document is a masterpiece of Revolutionary logic: it converts genealogy into guilt, transforms legal inheritance into moral complicity, and makes Darnay's renunciation of his title into evidence of concealment rather than virtue. It is the textual mechanism by which the personal becomes political — Madame Defarge's private grief over her murdered family, encoded into revolutionary language, becomes a charge of capital crimes.

## The Denunciation as Gothic Document

The denunciation belongs to a distinct literary tradition: the Gothic document, the written text that emerges from the past to determine the present. Just as Dr. Manette's Bastille manuscript is discovered in a hidden cell and read aloud years after its composition, the denunciation acquires its fatal power at a temporal remove. Both documents share the Gothic logic of the "manuscript found" — the text that survives its author and returns to haunt the living.

But the denunciation differs from Manette's manuscript in a crucial respect. Manette's document is a personal testimony, written in extremis, addressed to posterity. The denunciation, by contrast, is a public document, written for an institutional audience, calibrated to produce a specific legal outcome. It is the Revolution's own Gothic form: not the ghost of the past returning unbidden, but the past deliberately summoned and weaponized by the living.

Dickens's handling of this distinction is one of the novel's most sophisticated political insights. The Revolution does not simply repeat the injustices of the ancien régime — it systematises them, gives them documentary form, and calls the result justice. The denunciation is the textual embodiment of that transformation.

## Role in the Narrative

The denunciation drives the novel's climactic trial. Without it, Darnay could be condemned only for returning to France — a charge he could answer by proving his Republican sympathies. With it, he is condemned for what he was born, not what he has done. The denunciation represents the Revolution's darkest transformation: from a movement against injustice into a system that reproduces injustice under a new name.
