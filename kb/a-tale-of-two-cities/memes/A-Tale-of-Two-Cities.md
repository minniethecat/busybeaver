# A Tale of Two Cities

**A Tale of Two Cities** — Charles Dickens' twelfth novel, published in weekly installments in *All the Year Round* from April to November 1859 — is a work of historical fiction set in London and Paris before and during the French Revolution. With estimated sales exceeding 200 million copies, it ranks among the best-selling novels ever written. Its famous opening — "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times" — is one of the most recognized passages in English literature, and its closing line — "It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done" (045-051) — is among the most quoted.

## Publication and Reception

Dickens serialized the novel in his own weekly journal, *All the Year Round*, without illustrations — an unusual choice for a Victorian novelist of his stature. The first installment appeared on 30 April 1859; the final, on 26 November of the same year. Unlike his earlier serials, which were published in monthly numbers, the weekly format imposed a tighter pacing: chapters are shorter, scenes more compressed, and the three-book structure imposes a classical architecture on what might otherwise have sprawled.

Contemporary reception was strong but not universally enthusiastic. Some critics found the novel thinner in humor and richer in melodrama than Dickens' previous work. Posterity has disagreed: *A Tale of Two Cities* has become Dickens' most assigned novel in schools and one of the most frequently adapted — for stage, screen, radio, and opera.

## Structure

The novel's three-book design maps theme onto form:

| Book | Title | Chapters | Period Covered | Thematic Key |
|------|-------|----------|---------------|-------------|
| Book the First | Recalled to Life | 1–6 | 1775–1780 | **Resurrection** established |
| Book the Second | The Golden Thread | 1–24 | 1780–1789 | Love and conspiracy intertwine |
| Book the Third | The Track of a Storm | 1–15 | 1789–1793 | The Revolution consumes everything |

Each book's title names a controlling metaphor: the grave from which the living are dug; the thread of love that holds human lives together; and the storm of history that sweeps away both guilty and innocent.

## Plot Architecture

The plot unfolds as a system of **doublings and substitutions**:

- **Two cities**: London and Paris — the one a refuge, the other a furnace
- **Two men**: [[Charles-Darnay]] and [[Sydney-Carton]] — physically identical, morally opposite, ultimately exchanged
- **Two trials**: one in London (1780) that acquits Darnay; one in Paris (1792) that condemns him
- **Two prisons**: the Bastille (old regime) and the Conciergerie (revolutionary)
- **Two resurrections**: [[Doctor-Manette]]'s release from the Bastille, and Carton's self-chosen death at the guillotine

Every major event in the novel's final third has been prepared by a detail planted in its first third. Dickens' plotting in *A Tale of Two Cities* is among the tightest in Victorian fiction.

| Theme | Manifestation | Climax |
|-------|--------------|--------|
| **[[Resurrection]]** | "Recalled to Life" — Manette released, Carton redeemed | Carton's final vision at the scaffold |

| **Doubles and Duality** | Carton/Darnay; London/Paris; oppression/revolution | The substitution that resolves all doublings |

## Historical Method

Dickens relied heavily on Thomas Carlyle's *The French Revolution* (1837) for historical detail. He carried a copy of Carlyle's book with him while writing and claimed to have read it "five hundred times." The novel's treatment of revolutionary violence reflects Carlyle's influence — sympathetic to the suffering that produced the Revolution, horrified by the Terror that followed.

Published in 1859 — the year of John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry and on the eve of the American Civil War — the novel spoke to contemporary Victorian anxieties about class conflict. Its political argument, insofar as it has one, is not revolutionary nor counter-revolutionary but moral: individuals, not systems, are the agents of redemption.

## Adaptations

The novel has been adapted for film (1935, 1958, 1980), television (multiple BBC productions), stage (a long-running Broadway adaptation, *The Only Way*), and radio. The 1935 MGM film, starring Ronald Colman as Carton, remains the most celebrated screen version.

## The Famous Opening

> "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness, it was the epoch of belief, it was the epoch of incredulity, it was the season of Light, it was the season of Darkness, it was the spring of hope, it was the winter of despair..." (001-001)

The sentence that follows — "we had everything before us, we had nothing before us" — completes the antithesis. Dickens' point is not that the age was contradictory but that contradiction is the condition of every age: every era is simultaneously the best and the worst, depending on where one stands.
