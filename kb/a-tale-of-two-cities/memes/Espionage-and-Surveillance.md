## Overview

Espionage and surveillance form a hidden infrastructure beneath the visible plot of *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. From the [[Old-Bailey]] courtroom to the [[Saint-Antoine]] wine-shop, from [[Tellsons-Bank]] to the revolutionary tribunal, characters are watched, reported upon, and registered. Dickens presents this surveillance as a feature of both the ancien régime and the Revolution: the spy is a figure who adapts to whichever power holds the upper hand.

The novel's surveillance network operates through three principal mechanisms: the professional spy ([[John-Barsad]], Roger Cly), the revolutionary register ([[Madame-Defarge]]'s knitting), and the informal informer system that pervades both London and Paris. Together they create an atmosphere of mutual suspicion in which no character is truly safe from observation.

> The spy, well used to his business, did not allow his baffled state to express itself in his sinister face. (022-086)

This moment in [[Defarges-Wine-Shop]] crystallises the spy's defining quality: professional impassivity. Barsad's face is a mask, his trade the manipulation of appearance. But the passage also reveals his limitation — Madame Defarge has already knitted his description into her register, rendering his professional skill useless against the collective surveillance of the Revolution.

## The Professional Spy: John Barsad

John Barsad is the novel's archetypal spy. He first appears as a prosecution witness at [[Charles-Darnay]]'s treason trial at the Old Bailey, where his testimony is discredited. Barsad's defining characteristic is his adaptability: he serves the English Crown, then the French Republic, always positioning himself on the side of power. [[Sydney-Carton]] exposes Barsad's past as "a hired spy and traitor, an unblushing trafficker in blood" (009-101), and later blackmails him into providing access to Darnay's prison cell at [[La-Force]].

> ...he knew that he had crossed the Channel, and accepted service in France: first, as a tempter and an eavesdropper among his own countrymen there: gradually, as a tempter and an eavesdropper among the natives. He knew that under the overthrown government he had been a spy upon Saint Antoine and Defarge's wine-shop. (043-001)

This passage lays bare the spy's transnational career. Barsad serves no cause but his own survival; his profession is the extraction and sale of information across borders and regimes. The repetition of "tempter and eavesdropper" emphasises the moral degradation inherent in the spy's work.

## Roger Cly and the Spy Network

Roger Cly, Barsad's associate and fellow informer, is described as an "Old Bailey spy" (020-019). His staged funeral — an empty coffin buried while he escapes to France — demonstrates the spy's talent for disappearance and reappearance. Together, Barsad and Cly represent a professional class that survives by trading information to whichever regime will pay.

> "Old Bailey spy," returned his informant. "Yah! T'other side's way is the spy business." (020-019)

[[Jerry-Cruncher]]'s informant identifies Cly's profession with contempt, but the novel's treatment of espionage is more complex than simple condemnation. The spy is a symptom of state power, not its cause; Barsad and Cly flourish because both England and France need informers.

The empty coffin — Cly's staged death — represents the spy's ultimate disappearance act: identity as a thing that can be shed and reassumed. This fungibility of identity connects the espionage theme to the novel's broader exploration of doubles and substitutions, from Carton's resemblance to Darnay to the interchangeable faces of the revolutionary mob.

## Madame Defarge's Knitted Register

The most sinister form of surveillance in the novel is Madame Defarge's knitting. Beneath the innocuous domestic activity, she stitches a coded register of names "doomed to destruction" (025-067). The register is both surveillance tool and death sentence: those whose names appear in its stitches are marked for the guillotine. Unlike Barsad's mercenary spying, Madame Defarge's surveillance is ideological — driven by a lifelong hunger for vengeance against the Evrémonde family and their class.

> "Jacques," returned Defarge, drawing himself up, "if madame my wife undertook to keep the register in her memory alone, she would not lose a word of it — not a syllable of it. Knitted, in her own stitches and her own symbols, it will always be as plain to her as the sun." (025-071)

Defarge's tribute to his wife's register reveals it as both surveillance technology and historical record — a counter-archive maintained by the oppressed against their oppressors. The knitting is memory made material, indelible and inerasable.

The knitted register embodies the theme of surveillance disguised as domestic labour — the transformation of women's work into revolutionary weapon. The rose in Madame Defarge's headdress functions as a counter-surveillance signal, warning the wine-shop's patrons when a spy is present. Surveillance and counter-surveillance operate in a continuous arms race, each side adapting to the other's methods.

## The Spy in the Wine-Shop

The convergence of professional and revolutionary surveillance occurs in the Defarges' wine-shop. When Barsad visits Saint Antoine to gather intelligence, he finds himself outmatched by Madame Defarge, who knits his own description into her register even as he attempts to extract information. The scene dramatizes the Revolution's superior intelligence apparatus: Barsad, the professional, is rendered amateur by the collective surveillance of the Section.

> "There is another spy commissioned for our quarter. There are many spies in our quarter, but he is the one of whom I am most in dread." (022-006)

Defarge's warning to his fellow revolutionaries reveals that surveillance is not one-sided. The Revolution surveils the state even as the state surveils the Revolution — an arms race of information in which Madame Defarge's knitting proves the more lethal weapon.

> The spy, well used to his business, did not allow his baffled state to express itself in his sinister face. (022-086)

The spy's face is a professional mask. But Madame Defarge has already seen through it — her knitting has recorded him before he speaks a word.

## Surveillance and the State

Dickens extends the theme beyond individual spies to encompass institutional surveillance. The French revolutionary tribunals rely on denunciation; the "watchful police" maintain files on Doctor Manette's imprisonment. In England, the treason trial of Charles Darnay demonstrates how the state employs informers to secure convictions. The novel suggests that surveillance is inseparable from state power, regardless of the regime's political character.

The pervasiveness of surveillance across both nations and both political systems is one of Dickens's darkest insights. Neither the ancien régime nor the Revolution, neither England nor France, offers its citizens freedom from being watched. The spy adapts; the register accumulates; and the individual — whether Darnay, Lucie, or the Wood-Sawyer — learns to perform for an audience that may at any moment become a tribunal.
