# Roger Cly

**Roger Cly** is a minor antagonist in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*, a former servant who becomes a government spy and co-conspirator of [[John-Barsad]]. He testifies against [[Charles-Darnay]] at the Old Bailey treason trial, and his later "death" — exposed as a sham when [[Jerry-Cruncher]] discovers his coffin is filled with paving stones — provides one of the novel's deftest comic-revelatory moments.

## The Perjurer

Cly appears in the witness box at the Old Bailey alongside Barsad, both presented as upstanding citizens whose testimony will seal Darnay's fate:

> The virtuous servant, Roger Cly, swore his way through the case. (009-005, paraphrased)

Like Barsad, Cly's testimony collapses under cross-examination. His pretensions to respectability — "the virtuous servant" — are shown to be a calculated performance. The exposure of both spies as perjurers is an early signal of the novel's concern with the gap between public reputation and private truth.

## The Empty Coffin

Cly's most memorable contribution to the novel occurs after his reported death. His funeral procession passes through the London streets, and Jerry Cruncher — the Tellson's messenger and part-time "resurrection man" — joins the mob of mourners. Later, on a nocturnal "fishing" expedition, Cruncher discovers the truth: Cly's coffin contains nothing but paving stones. The "death" was a fabrication, presumably to allow Cly to escape some threat or obligation. Cruncher files this knowledge away — and it later becomes part of the intelligence that [[Sydney-Carton]] uses to pressure Barsad, Cly's old partner.

## Partnership with Barsad

Cly and Barsad are presented as a matched pair: both are former servants, both have reinvented themselves as "gentlemen," and both sell their testimony to the highest bidder. Their partnership is one of convenience rather than loyalty, and each would presumably betray the other if the price were right. Together, they represent the **corruption of the state's instruments** — the men who enforce the law are as dishonest as the men they pursue.

## Narrative Function

Cly is a minor character who punches above his narrative weight. His empty coffin literalizes the novel's theme of false appearances and hidden truths; his exposure contributes to the leverage Carton needs; and his partnership with Barsad illustrates the network of espionage and informants that undergirds the legal system on both sides of the Channel.
