# Blood

Blood runs through *A Tale of Two Cities* as a transforming symbol, marking the
novel's journey from the personal to the political, from oppression to
revolutionary terror, and finally to redemptive sacrifice.

## Blood as Complaint and Injury

The novel's first blood is personal and petty. The Dover mail coachman, vexed by
a passenger's impatience, ejaculates "My blood!" — a commonplace exclamation
that foreshadows nothing of the torrent to come (002-010). In Paris, the
wine-cask shatters in Saint Antoine and the desperate poor dip their fingers in
the red liquid, scrawling "Blood" upon a wall with wine-dregs (005-004).

When injury enters the narrative, blood carries the stain of aristocratic
cruelty. The child run down by the Marquis's carriage leaves a scene of
bloodshed that the nobleman dismisses with a coin. The fountain in the
courtyard of the chateau "seemed to turn to blood" as the stone faces looked
upon the scene (015-106).

## Blood-Money and the Old Regime

Dickens connects blood to commerce through the figure of the Farmer-General and
the system of tax-farming that bled the peasantry. Tellson's Bank conducts
"extensive transactions in blood-money" — the wealth extracted from the
suffering of the poor (008-023). The blood of Monseigneur's class is imagined
as superior — "superior mankind of the blood of Monseigneur" (013-005).

## Revolutionary Bloodshed

When the Bastille falls, blood transforms from metaphor to material reality.
Saint Antoine's "blood was up, and the blood of tyranny and domination by the
iron hand was down" (027-079). The revolutionaries become "terrible, in the
bloody-minded anger with which they looked" (028-022). The September Massacres
bring bloodshed to its moral climax — the grindstone turns as men sharpen
weapons, their faces marked with blood, and "the last drop of blood having been
extracted from the flints" (029-003).

## The Tribunal and Blood-Thirst

The Revolutionary Tribunal formalises bloodshed into legal procedure. The
"blood-thirsting, cannibal-looking, bloody-minded juryman, the Jacques Three"
embodies a justice system indistinguishable from murder (039-101). "In the last
month of the ten, blood from the chimney mixed with blood" in the prison
(040-002) — the physical environment itself seems to bleed.

## Redemptive Blood

The novel's ultimate act transforms blood from a mark of violence to a sign of
love. Sydney Carton offers his blood as a willing sacrifice at the guillotine.
This redemptive blood echoes Dr. Manette's prison manuscript, written "mixed
with blood" in the Bastille (040-002), and the "cross of blood" that his
narrative marks upon the Evremonde line: "I mark this cross of blood upon you,
as a sign that I do it" (040-071). Carton inverts this curse — his blood
becomes a cross not of condemnation but of salvation.

## Blood and Hereditary Guilt

Beyond its function as a marker of violence and redemption, blood in Dickens's novel carries the weight of hereditary identity — the idea that guilt, like nobility, flows through bloodlines. The Evremonde family's crimes are imagined as a taint transmitted through generations: Darnay, though personally innocent, is condemned by the "blood of the Evremondes" that runs in his veins. Dr. Manette's prison manuscript, written with his own blood mixed into the ink (040-002), literalizes the connection between blood and testimony — the accusation against the Evremondes is written in the substance it condemns. The Revolution's logic mirrors the aristocracy's own: just as Monseigneur's class claimed superiority by blood (013-005), the Revolution condemns by blood, making no distinction between Darnay the renunciant and his uncle the rapist. This hereditary taint is what Carton's sacrifice finally breaks: his blood, spilled in Darnay's place, pays a debt that Darnay's own blood could never discharge — the debt not of personal guilt but of inherited wrong.
