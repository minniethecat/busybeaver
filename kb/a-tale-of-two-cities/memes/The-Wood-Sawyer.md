# The Wood-Sawyer

## Overview

The Wood-Sawyer is a minor but thematically potent character who appears in Book 3 of *A Tale of Two Cities*. Once a mender of roads — the same blue-capped peasant who witnessed the Marquis's crime and travelled to Paris with Defarge — he has transformed himself into an urban tradesman, setting up a wood-sawing shop against the very wall of La Force prison. His work of cutting wood into billets for domestic use becomes an involuntary symbol of the guillotine's work — the daily sawing of lives — and his shop serves as a surveillance post from which he reports on Lucie Manette's visits to her imprisoned husband.

Dickens uses the Wood-Sawyer to explore the corruption of ordinary people by revolutionary power. He is not evil by nature — he is a small man with small fears, eager to please whichever authority holds the knife. His trajectory from rural witness to urban informer charts the moral degradation that the Revolution inflicts on those it supposedly liberates.

## From Mender of Roads to Wood-Sawyer

The Wood-Sawyer's former identity is revealed early: "he had once been a mender of roads" (035-015). The transformation from repairing roads to sawing wood is not merely a change of trade but a change of world. The mender of roads belonged to the countryside, to the slow rhythms of peasant labour under the open sky. The wood-sawyer belongs to the prison wall, to the closed-in city where every sound is overheard and every face is watched.

His physical description reinforces his diminishment — he is "a little man with a redundancy of gesture" (035-015), a figure whose nervous, excessive movements suggest both anxiety and eagerness to be seen complying. Unlike Defarge's controlled authority or Madame Defarge's implacable stillness, the Wood-Sawyer is all twitch and obeisance, a small dog performing for his masters.

## The Shop Against the Prison Wall

The location of the Wood-Sawyer's shop is Dickens's darkest spatial joke. He works against the wall of La Force — the prison where Darnay is held and where the September massacres occurred. When Lucie Manette comes to stand at the appointed spot where Darnay can see her from his window, she must share the wall with the sawyer's operation:

> The wood-sawyer, who was a little man with a redundancy of gesture (he had once been a mender of roads), cast a glance at the prison, pointed at the prison, and putting his ten fingers before his face to represent bars, peeped through them jocosely. (035-015)

The gesture — fingers as prison bars — is a mockery, a pantomime of imprisonment performed for the benefit of a woman whose husband is inside. The sawyer finds entertainment in the suffering that Lucie endures daily.

His shop is decorated with revolutionary emblems: "On his house-top, he displayed pike and cap, as a good citizen must, and in a window he had stationed his saw inscribed as his 'Little Sainte [[Guillotine]]'" (035-031). The naming of his saw after the guillotine — "the great sharp female" recently canonised by the Revolution — collapses the distinction between his honest trade and the state's killing machine. Every billet he cuts is a rehearsal for the blade's descent.

In one of the novel's most harrowing scenes, Lucie watches from the sawyer's doorway as the [[Carmagnole]] — the dance of the revolutionaries — sweeps through the street:

> a throng of people came pouring round the corner by the prison wall, in the midst of whom was the wood-sawyer hand in hand with The [[Vengeance]]. (035-032)

The image of the wood-sawyer dancing hand in hand with The Vengeance — the Revolution's most fanatical female figure — cements his complicity. He is no longer a witness but a participant in the frenzy. The dance, with its "gnashing of teeth in unison," its "storm of coarse red caps and coarse woollen rags," is a demonic inversion of communal celebration:

> No fight could have been half so terrible as this dance. It was so emphatically a fallen sport — a something, once innocent, delivered over to all devilry. (035-032)

## The Informer

The Wood-Sawyer's most consequential role is as informer. Madame Defarge has "rehearsed" him to testify that Lucie makes "signs and signals to prisoners" during her daily vigils at the prison wall:

> He knows that a wood-sawyer, living by the prison wall, is under the control of the Defarges, and has been rehearsed by Madame Defarge as to his having seen Her making signs and signals to prisoners. (042-068)

The charge is fabricated — Lucie stands at the wall only so that Darnay can see her, a silent act of love — but the sawyer, under Madame Defarge's control, will swear to whatever she requires. His obedience is not ideological conviction but mortal fear. When questioned, he "hurriedly replied in the affirmative: seizing the occasion to add that he was the most ardent of Republicans" (044-025). The redundancy of his protest — "the most ardent of Republicans" — betrays its insincerity. He is not ardent; he is afraid.

The Wood-Sawyer's shed becomes the site of Madame Defarge's final conspiracy — the plan to denounce Lucie, her father, and her child:

> Not in the wine-shop did Madame Defarge confer with these ministers, but in the shed of the wood-sawyer, erst a mender of roads. (044-001)

The setting is significant. Madame Defarge chooses the sawyer's shed — a place of labour, of honest work — for her most dishonest plot. The wood-sawyer himself is excluded from the conference, standing "at a little distance, like an outer satellite who was not to speak until required, or to offer an opinion until invited" (044-001). He is simultaneously essential to the plot (as its false witness) and peripheral to its decision-making. He is a tool, not an agent.

When Madame Defarge's gaze falls upon him, the sawyer's bravado crumbles:

> The wood-sawyer, who held her in the respect, and himself in the submission, of mortal fear, advanced with his hand to his red cap. (044-013)

The gesture — hand to red cap — is the same gesture of obeisance that peasants once made to aristocrats. The revolution has changed the colour of the cap but not the structure of power. Small men still cower before their masters.

## Symbolism of the Saw

The Wood-Sawyer's trade is Dickens's most concentrated metaphor for the Terror. The saw divides wood into uniform lengths — billets — just as the guillotine divides bodies from heads. The rhythmic, repetitive motion of sawing mirrors the daily rhythm of the tumbrils. And the sawyer's self-naming — "Little Sainte Guillotine" — makes explicit what the reader already feels: that every sound of sawing against the prison wall prefigures the blade's fall.

The irony cuts deeper because the sawyer was once a road-mender — a man who repaired what was broken. Now he cuts things apart. The revolution has inverted his function: from mender to destroyer, from peasant to informer, from witness to tool.
