# Resurrection

**Resurrection** — being "Recalled to Life" — is the master theme that Charles Dickens weaves through every book, character, and symbolic image of *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. It operates on three distinct but interlocking levels: **physical** (release from literal imprisonment), **moral** (redemption from a wasted life), and **spiritual** (salvation through self-giving love). The novel's three-book structure mirrors these three registers, and its final passage — [[Sydney-Carton]]'s prophetic vision at the guillotine — binds them into a single transformative moment.

## The Motif Established

Dickens plants the resurrection theme in the novel's opening movement through the figure of Jarvis Lorry, the Tellson's Bank clerk. Riding in the Dover mail coach on a secret mission, Lorry is haunted by a dream:

> "After such a strange effort of the spirit, the banker dozed, and dreamed of digging a man out of a grave." (002-052)

The dream proves prophetic. The message Lorry carries — the response he is to send back to Tellson's — is a single, cryptic phrase:

> "RECALLED TO LIFE" (002-048)

What begins as a coded business communication becomes the novel's organizing metaphor. The man being "dug out of a grave" is [[Doctor-Manette]], who has spent eighteen years buried alive in the Bastille. His release and gradual restoration — accomplished through the love of his daughter [[Lucie-Manette]] — is the novel's first and most literal resurrection.

## Three Resurrections

| Level | Character | Mechanism | Book |
|-------|-----------|-----------|------|
| **Physical** | Doctor Manette | Released from the Bastille; restored to identity through Lucie's love | Book the First — "Recalled to Life" |
| **Moral** | Sydney Carton | Transformed from dissipated cynic to self-sacrificing hero through love for Lucie | Book the Second — "The Golden Thread" |
| **Spiritual** | Carton's final vision | Achieves immortality through death; his memory "recalled to life" in the generations that follow | Book the Third — "The Track of a Storm" |

### Doctor Manette: Physical Resurrection

After eighteen years of solitary confinement, Manette is a "living dead" — physically present but psychologically entombed. When Lorry and Lucie find him in the Defarges' garret, he has shrunk to a single activity: cobbling shoes, the mechanical task that preserved a thread of sanity during his imprisonment. He does not recognize his own name. He "had no real knowledge of what was going on" and appears to inhabit a world "which had no existence for him" (003-015).

Lucie's love "recalls him to life" not through dramatic intervention but through steady, patient presence. Her golden hair — which reminds Manette of his wife's — becomes the sensory bridge between his prison past and his reclaimed future. This resurrection is not instantaneous; throughout the novel, Manette relapses into shoemaking during moments of extreme stress, a reminder that being "recalled to life" is a precarious, ongoing process rather than a single event.

### Sydney Carton: Moral Resurrection

Carton's resurrection inverts Manette's. Where Manette is passive — released by others, restored by others — Carton actively chooses his own rebirth. He transforms from a man who "cared for no man on earth" (011-036) into one who willingly dies for another. His confession to Lucie is simultaneously a declaration of love and a self-diagnosis of despair:

> "For you, and for any dear to you, I would do anything." (012-100)

The resurrection is completed at the scaffold. Carton's silent recitation of the biblical passage — "I am the resurrection and the life, saith the Lord: he that believeth in me, though he were dead, yet shall he live" (039-087) — frames his death not as an ending but as a passage into a fuller existence.

### The Prophetic Vision: Spiritual Resurrection

Carton's final, unspoken meditation at the guillotine projects resurrection into the future. He foresees Lucie and Darnay naming a son after him — a child who will grow to be "a man, winning his way up in that path of life which once was mine" — and a world in which "the blot upon his name" is erased:

> "It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done; it is a far, far better rest that I go to than I have ever known." (045-051)

This is resurrection understood as legacy: the dead live on not literally but in the lives and memories of those who survive them. Carton exchanges his biological death for a kind of moral immortality.

## The Revolution as Counterfeit Resurrection

Dickens constructs the French Revolution as a dark parody of the resurrection theme. The people of [[Saint-Antoine]] rise from hunger and oppression into revolutionary fury — a genuine "recalling to life" of collective political agency. But this resurrection corrupts: instead of healing the world, the guillotine becomes an engine of death that "recalls" its victims only to the grave. "La Guillotine" — personified as "the sharp female" (035-026) — is the thematic antithesis of Lucie Manette: where Lucie's love restores life, the guillotine's "justice" extinguishes it.

The irony cuts deep: the Revolution that liberated Doctor Manette from the Bastille would, if it could, have executed his son-in-law [[Charles-Darnay]] — a man guilty of nothing beyond his family name.

## Christian Imagery

Dickens draws on Christian resurrection language without making the novel explicitly religious. Carton's final journey echoes the Stations of the Cross; his substitutionary death evokes the Christ narrative; and the biblical verse he recalls — "I am the resurrection and the life" — is the central claim of the Christian faith. But Dickens universalizes the trope: resurrection is available to anyone who loves deeply enough, irrespective of creed. The seamstress who rides with Carton to the guillotine understands his sacrifice without needing it explained in doctrinal terms.
