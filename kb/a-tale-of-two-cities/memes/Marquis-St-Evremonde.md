# Marquis St. Evrémonde

The **Marquis St. Evrémonde** is the principal embodiment of aristocratic cruelty in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. The uncle of [[Charles-Darnay]], he represents everything his nephew has renounced: the unchecked power, casual brutality, and moral blindness of the *ancien régime*. Introduced in the famous carriage scene of Book the Second, Chapter 7, the Marquis dominates only a handful of chapters before his assassination at the hands of [[Gaspard]] — yet his shadow falls across the entire novel. The written accusation he provoked from [[Doctor-Manette]] in the Bastille becomes the instrument of Darnay's condemnation decades later, and his crimes form the genealogical curse that the Revolution's logic of inherited guilt exploits to its fullest. Dickens draws him not as a psychologically complex villain but as a representative distillation of a dying class: proud, indifferent, and unable to perceive the forces massing against it.

## Appearance and Manner

Dickens presents the Marquis through a series of carefully controlled physical details that emphasize his distance from ordinary humanity. His face is described as "a fine mask" that "seemed to look down on everything" (014-009), suggesting a man for whom expression has been replaced by performance. His eyes are "very dark and very handsome, with a look of pride and ill-humour" (013-024). The morning ritual of drinking chocolate — requiring four attendants to prepare and serve — is a miniature of the parasitic economy that the Revolution will sweep away, a display of refinement so rarefied that it crosses into absurdity:

> "It took four men, all four ablaze with gorgeous decoration, and the Chief of them unable to exist with fewer than two gold watches in his pocket, emulative of the noble and chaste fashion set by Monseigneur, to conduct the happy chocolate to Monseigneur's lips." (015-001)

## Background

The Marquis belongs to the twin sons of the elder Marquis St. Evrémonde, and together with his brother committed the crimes that [[Doctor-Manette]] witnessed in December 1757: the abduction and rape of a peasant woman, the murder of her husband, and the fatal wounding of her brother. When Manette attempted to report these crimes to the Minister of State, the elder brother intercepted the letter and had Manette imprisoned in [[The-Bastille]] without trial — a living death that lasted eighteen years. The Marquis represents the second generation of Evrémonde cruelty; his father was the original perpetrator, but the Marquis inherited and perpetuated the family's sense of absolute entitlement.

## The Carriage Scene

The Marquis makes his most indelible impression during his journey through the streets of Paris, where his carriage — driven at a reckless speed — strikes and kills a child. His response, among the most damning moments in Victorian fiction, distills the moral logic of the aristocracy:

> "It is extraordinary to me," said he, "that you people cannot take care of yourselves and your children. One or the other of you is for ever in the way. How do I know what injury you have done my horses?" (013-041)

He tosses a gold coin to the grieving father, Gaspard, as though the death of a child were a commercial transaction, and drives on. The coin, thrown "as if it had been a box to a dog," becomes a symbol of the chasm between the classes — a life reduced to currency, grief dismissed as inconvenience.

## The Château and the Night Visitor

The Marquis retires to his country château, where his conversation with his nephew Charles Darnay reveals the ideological gulf between them. Darnay, who has renounced his family title and estates, speaks of the "fear and slave" basis of aristocratic rule; the Marquis responds with chilling candor:

> "Detestation of the high," said the Marquis, glancing at his hands, "is the involuntary homage of the low." (014-003)

That night, the Marquis is murdered in his bed by Gaspard, the father of the killed child, who has tracked him from Paris. The assassination is swift and silent, and the stone face carved above the château seems to look down in frozen approval. The murder sets in motion a chain of retribution: Gaspard is captured, hanged, and left on a gibbet; the village is terrorized; and the revolutionary fuse, already smoldering, burns faster.

## Symbolism and Themes

Dickens draws the Marquis not as a psychologically complex villain but as a representative figure — the aristocracy distilled to its essence. His characterization serves the novel's thematic exploration of [[Class-and-Oppression]] and [[Fate-and-Inevitability]]. The stone face on the château that mirrors his own links him to the theme of petrification: the *ancien régime* has become lifeless, incapable of change, and will shatter rather than bend.

| Trait | Manifestation | Narrative Function |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| Pride | "Detestation of the high is the involuntary homage of the low" | Articulates the ideology Darnay has rejected |
| Cruelty | Tosses a coin for a dead child, drives on | Catalyzes Gaspard's vengeance and the revolutionary spiral |
| Moral blindness | Genuinely cannot see why the peasants are ungrateful | Represents the self-justifying logic of oppression |
| Isolation | His château is surrounded by starvation, his bedchamber by stone faces | The aristocracy is literally surrounded by what it refuses to see |

## Role in the Story

The Marquis appears in only a few chapters and dies before the novel's midpoint, yet his narrative function extends to the final pages. He is the mechanism by which the past enters the present: his crimes generate [[Dr-Manettes-Letter-from-the-Bastille]], the document read aloud at Darnay's second trial. The Revolution, personified by [[Madame-Defarge]] — who is herself the younger sister of the peasant woman the Evrémondes destroyed — preserves his family's guilt across decades and visits it upon his innocent nephew. The Marquis thus operates as a structural pivot between the novel's two halves: his actions in Book the Second explain the catastrophe of Book the Third.

## Legacy in the Novel

Though the Marquis dies early — before the halfway point of the novel — his presence dominates the trial sequence in Book the Third. The written denunciation that [[Doctor-Manette]] composed in the Bastille, hidden in the chimney of Cell 105 North Tower and discovered by [[Monsieur-Defarge]] during the [[Storming-of-the-Bastille]], narrates the crimes of the Evrémonde brothers in full. One brother abducted a young peasant woman and killed her husband; the other (the Marquis) buried the dying woman's father alive in a coach. The document ends:

> "Them and their descendants, to the last of their race, I, Alexandre Manette, denounce to Heaven and to earth." (030-029, paraphrased)

It is this denunciation, read aloud at Darnay's trial, that condemns him to death — not for anything he has done, but for the crimes of his uncle and father. The Marquis thus functions as a structural pivot: his crimes create the accusation that sends his innocent nephew, whose only sin was blood relation, to the guillotine.
