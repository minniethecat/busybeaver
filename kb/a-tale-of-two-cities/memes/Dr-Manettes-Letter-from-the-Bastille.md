# Dr. Manette's Letter from the Bastille

## Overview

Dr. Manette's letter from the [[The-Bastille|Bastille]] is the narrative bomb buried at the heart of *A Tale of Two Cities* — a document that transforms the entire preceding story and drives the novel toward its catastrophic climax. Hidden in a chimney cavity in Cell 105, North Tower, for nearly twenty-five years, the manuscript is discovered by [[Monsieur-Defarge|Ernest Defarge]] during the storming of the Bastille and held in reserve as the Defarges' ultimate weapon. When read aloud at [[Charles-Darnay|Darnay's]] second trial before the Revolutionary Tribunal, it proves devastating: the letter documents the crimes of the Evrémonde brothers in December 1757 — the abduction and rape of a peasant woman, the murder of her husband and brother, and Manette's own imprisonment without trial — and concludes with [[Doctor-Manette|Manette's]] curse upon "them and their descendants, to the last of their race." The letter condemns Darnay not for anything he has done, but for the name he was born to, and secures his death sentence within twenty-four hours. As a structural device, the letter functions simultaneously as origin story, withheld evidence, and engine of destruction: it explains Manette's eighteen-year psychological oblivion while undoing everything he has rebuilt since his release.

## Context

Darnay has returned to Paris and been arrested as an emigrant aristocrat. In his first trial, [[Doctor-Manette|Dr. Manette's]] testimony as a beloved former Bastille prisoner secures Darnay's acquittal. But hours later, Darnay is re-arrested on a new denunciation — this time naming three accusers: [[Monsieur-Defarge|Ernest Defarge]], [[Madame-Defarge|Thérèse Defarge]], and "Alexandre Manette, physician" (039-111). The revelation that Darnay's own father-in-law is among his denouncers shocks the court and devastates [[Lucie-Manette]].

Defarge explains that he found a written paper in Dr. Manette's old cell during the storming of the [[The-Bastille|Bastille]], concealed in "a hole in the chimney, where a stone has been worked out and replaced" (039-125). He verifies the handwriting as Manette's own, and the paper is read aloud in a court that has already condemned Darnay in its heart.

## The Letter's Contents

The manuscript is written in Manette's own voice from the year 1767, the tenth year of his imprisonment. He introduces himself: "I, Alexandre Manette, unfortunate physician, native of Beauvais, and afterwards resident in Paris, write this melancholy paper in my doleful cell in the Bastille" (040-001). The text is composed in "scrapings of soot and charcoal from the chimney, mixed with blood" (040-002), scratched with a rusty iron point — a physical testament to the degradation of his captivity.

The letter recounts events of December 1757. Manette, walking by the Seine, is stopped by a carriage carrying two aristocratic brothers whom he later identifies as the twin sons of the [[Marquis-St-Evremonde|Marquis St. Evrémonde]]. They compel him at gunpoint to accompany them to a remote house, where he finds two patients.

### The First Patient

A young woman of "great beauty, and young; assuredly not much past twenty" (040-020), bound with sashes bearing the armorial bearings of a Noble and the letter E. She raves in a fixed pattern: crying "My husband, my father, and my brother!", counting to twelve, and whispering "Hush!" (040-022). She has been drugged and sexually assaulted by the younger Evrémonde brother. When Manette later discovers that she is pregnant, he knows her case is hopeless. She dies at midnight, "her forlorn young head drooped gently on one side, and all her earthly wrongs and sorrows ended" (040-093).

### The Second Patient

In a loft over a stable, Manette finds a handsome peasant boy of no more than seventeen, dying of a sword wound (040-044). The boy is the woman's brother. In the letter's most powerful passage, he delivers his dying testimony against the Evrémondes:

> "We were so robbed by that man who stands there, as all we common dogs are by those superior Beings — taxed by him without mercy, obliged to work for him without pay, obliged to grind our corn at his mill, obliged to feed scores of his tame birds on our wretched crops, and forbidden for our lives to keep a single tame bird of our own... our father told us it was a dreadful thing to bring a child into the world" (040-057).

The boy explains that his sister's husband was harnessed to a cart and driven like an animal until he died — "he sobbed twelve times, once for every stroke of the bell, and died on her bosom" (040-061). The younger Evrémonde then took the woman for his pleasure. The boy tracked the brothers to the house, climbed in with a sword, and was run through by the younger brother's blade.

Manette records that he had "never before seen the sense of being oppressed, bursting forth like a fire" (040-058). The boy's dying act is a curse, traced in blood with his finger:

> "Marquis, in the days when all these things are to be answered for, I summon you and yours, to the last of your bad race, to answer for them. I mark this cross of blood upon you, as a sign that I do it." (040-071)

### Manette's Own Fate

Manette writes a private letter to the Minister of State describing what he has witnessed (040-103), but the letter is intercepted. The elder Evrémonde brother visits Manette that night, shows him the letter he has burnt, and has him abducted and taken to the Bastille — "I was brought to my living grave" (040-118).

The manuscript concludes with Manette's own curse, written in his "unbearable agony" on the last night of 1767:

> "Them and their descendants, to the last of their race, I, Alexandre Manette, unhappy prisoner, do this last night of the year 1767, in my unbearable agony, denounce to the times when all these things shall be answered for. I denounce them to Heaven and to earth." (040-119)

## Aftermath

The reading of the letter has an instantaneous and catastrophic effect. "A terrible sound arose when the reading of this document was done. A sound of craving and eagerness that had nothing articulate in it but blood" (040-120). The jury votes unanimously: Darnay is condemned to death within twenty-four hours (040-125). The irony is absolute — Darnay's own father-in-law, the man whose freedom he helped secure, is the instrument of his doom.

The sentence triggers the novel's climactic sequence: [[Sydney-Carton]]'s decision to trade places with Darnay, the substitution at the Conciergerie, and Carton's execution at the guillotine. The letter thus functions as the direct cause of the novel's final act of sacrifice.

## Narrative Architecture

The Bastille letter is a masterpiece of delayed revelation. Dickens plants the seed in Book 1 — [[Jarvis-Lorry]] asks [[Doctor-Manette|Manette]] about his shoemaking bench, and Manette answers with the cryptic number "One Hundred and Five, North Tower" (006-041) — yet the full significance of that number is withheld until the novel's penultimate chapter. The letter thus functions as both origin story and engine of destruction: it explains Manette's eighteen-year psychological oblivion while simultaneously undoing everything he has rebuilt.

The structural parallel between the two courtroom scenes reinforces the novel's symmetry. At the [[Old-Bailey]] in Book 2, Darnay is saved by mistaken identity; at the [[Conciergerie]] in Book 3, he is destroyed by the very document that reveals his true identity. The [[Trial-of-Charles-Darnay]] in Paris mirrors and inverts the [[Darnays-Treason-Trial|Old Bailey trial]] at every turn.

## The Letter as Weapon

The manuscript embodies the Revolution's logic of documentary terror. [[Madame-Defarge]] — who was the younger sister of the violated peasant woman — has waited decades for this evidence to surface. Her knitting, the secret register in which she encodes the names of those marked for death, is the shadow counterpart to Manette's letter: both are texts of doom, one written in wool and the other in soot and blood.

> Defarge put this provender into the jury's sack, and they voted Darnay guilty of the crimes of his ancestors. (040-123)

The letter's transformation from private testimony to public weapon encapsulates the Revolution's relationship to history: past crimes are not forgiven, only preserved in new forms until the moment of reckoning arrives.

## Significance

The Bastille letter is the novel's masterstroke of narrative architecture. It transforms the entire preceding story: [[Doctor-Manette|Dr. Manette's]] long imprisonment, his relapse into shoemaking, his terror of revisiting memories of France — all of these are reframed as symptoms of a specific, documented trauma. The letter crystallises the novel's central theme of inherited guilt: [[Charles-Darnay|Darnay]], who has done nothing but renounce his family and its crimes, is condemned for the sins of his ancestors because the Revolution makes no distinction between the individual and the name. [[Madame-Defarge|Madame Defarge's]] knitting, the secret register of the doomed, finds its ultimate justification in this document — a text written by the Revolution's most celebrated victim and turned, with terrible irony, into the Revolution's deadliest weapon. The [[Resurrection]] theme is here inverted: the letter that resurrects Manette's memory also seals his son-in-law's fate.
