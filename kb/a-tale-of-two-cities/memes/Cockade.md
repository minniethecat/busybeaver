# Cockade

The cockade (*la cocarde*) was a knot or rosette of coloured ribbons worn on the hat or cap as a badge of political allegiance. In Revolutionary France, the tricolour cockade — combining blue, white, and red — was the most visible emblem of revolutionary patriotism. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, the cockade appears on the caps of the armed patriots who arrest [[Charles-Darnay]] and sit in judgment at his trial, marking its wearers as agents of Revolutionary authority.

## Definition

A cockade is a decorative knot of ribbon, traditionally worn on military headgear to denote regimental affiliation or national identity. During the French Revolution, the cockade evolved from a military accessory into a universal political symbol. To wear the tricolour cockade was to declare loyalty to the Republic; to refuse it was to invite suspicion, denunciation, and arrest.

## Historical Context

The tricolour cockade emerged in the summer of 1789, shortly after the storming of the Bastille. According to revolutionary legend, the Marquis de Lafayette presented a tricolour cockade to Louis XVI at the Hôtel de Ville on 17 July 1789, combining the blue and red of Paris with the white of the Bourbon monarchy. The cockade was mandated by law for all citizens; by 1793, failure to wear one was treated as evidence of counter-revolutionary sentiment. In the logic of the Terror, the absence of a cockade was as damning as the presence of an aristocratic title.

## Role in the Novel

The cockade appears in Dickens's novel as a visual shorthand for revolutionary power. When [[Charles-Darnay]] is arrested on his return to France, his escort consists of "two mounted patriots in red caps and tri-coloured cockades, armed with national muskets and sabres" (031-014). The cockade transforms ordinary men into agents of the state — their weapons give them force, but the cockade gives them legitimacy.

The cockade reappears at Darnay's trial before the Revolutionary Tribunal, where "the rough red cap and tricoloured cockade was the head-dress otherwise prevailing" (036-009). The judges themselves may wear feathered hats, but it is the cockade — the badge of the people — that dominates the courtroom. The visual contrast between judicial formality and popular costume reflects the novel's broader theme: that revolutionary justice is not justice at all, but the will of the crowd dressed in legal robes.

The cockade, like the red cap and the [[Tocsin]], is part of a system of revolutionary signs that Dickens presents as simultaneously inspiring and terrifying. They are the outward markers of an inward transformation — the point at which the individual disappears into the collective, and personal conscience yields to ideological obedience.

## Related Terms

- **[[Tocsin]]**: The alarm bell that summons patriots wearing the cockade to arms
- **[[Charles-Darnay]]**: The aristocrat judged by those wearing the cockade
- **[[Guillotine]]**: The destination to which the cockade-wearing patriots escort their prisoners
- **Red Cap**: The companion emblem to the cockade, worn together as the uniform of revolution
