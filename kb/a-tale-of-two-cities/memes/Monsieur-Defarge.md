# Monsieur Defarge

**Monsieur Defarge** is the proprietor of a wine-shop in the [[Saint-Antoine]] district of Paris and a key revolutionary leader in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. A former servant of [[Doctor-Manette]] during the doctor's imprisonment in the Bastille, Defarge combines personal loyalty — demonstrated in his care for the freed prisoner — with revolutionary conviction. His arc traces the transformation of legitimate grievance into the machinery of impersonal retribution, as the compassionate jailer of Book the First becomes the unyielding functionary of the [[Trial-of-Charles-Darnay|Revolutionary Tribunal]].

## Appearance and Manner

Defarge is described as a man of "a dark, short, bull-necked" build, with a face that is "good-humoured" in his dealings with customers and compatriots but can harden into something formidable when the occasion demands. His voice shifts register depending on his audience — warm and quiet when speaking to the broken Doctor Manette in the garret, sharp and commanding when directing the mob during the storming of the Bastille. This chameleon quality is his signature: Defarge navigates between worlds — between the public wine-shop and the secret revolutionary cell, between the tenderness of personal memory and the ruthlessness of political necessity — with a fluency that makes him indispensable to the Revolution and, ultimately, morally illegible.

## The Wine-Shop

Defarge's establishment in the faubourg Saint Antoine serves as both a commercial front and a nerve center for revolutionary conspiracy. The wine-shop is where the "Jacques" network of insurgents convenes, where Madame Defarge knits her fatal register, and where the grinding poverty of the Parisian poor is most vividly juxtaposed with the coming vengeance. Dickens first introduces Defarge through the eyes of the passerby:

> "Is all the spilt wine swallowed?" said one of these three to Monsieur Defarge. "Every drop, Jacques," answered Monsieur Defarge. (005-021–022)

The spilled wine cask that opens the chapter — staining the hands, faces, and feet of the starving inhabitants who scramble to drink it from the mud — is the novel's most concentrated symbol of the hunger that will become blood-lust. Defarge, standing above the scene, is positioned as both observer and orchestrator.

## Past in the Bastille

Defarge's revolutionary fervor is rooted in personal history. He was a servant to Doctor Manette during the latter's eighteen-year imprisonment in the Bastille, and it was in the doctor's old cell — 105 North Tower — that Defarge discovered the written accusation against the Evrémonde family. When [[Jarvis-Lorry]] and Lucie Manette arrive to retrieve the freed doctor, Defarge's care reveals the humanity that still coexists with his revolutionary zeal:

> Almost at the first word, Monsieur Defarge started and became deeply attentive. It had not been a very long speech, but he had a quick eye, and he had seen in Mr. Lorry's face something that made him change his manner. (005-036)

He leads Lorry and Lucie to the garret where the doctor sits at his shoemaker's bench — a scene of extraordinary pathos — and delivers one of the novel's gentlest lines: "You have a visitor, you see" (006-021). The Defarge of Book the First is capable of tenderness; the Defarge of Book the Third is not.

## Role in the Revolution

By the time of the [[Storming-of-the-Bastille]], Defarge has emerged as a commanding figure in the insurrection. He personally leads the mob to his former master's old cell in the liberated fortress — searching, with symbolic weight, for something in the chimney. When the Revolution hardens into the Terror, Defarge becomes a functionary of the Tribunal, the authority to whom [[Charles-Darnay]] must report upon returning to France. His wine-shop remains the hub:

> There was a greater crowd than usual in the wine-shop of Monsieur Defarge. As early as six o'clock in the morning, sallow faces peeping through its barred windows had descried other faces within. (021-001)

## Relationship with Madame Defarge

Defarge's relationship with his wife is one of the novel's most complex dynamics. [[Madame-Defarge]] is the more implacable of the two — her knitting encodes a death register from which no name can be removed. Defarge, by contrast, retains flickers of mercy. When he suggests that the destruction of the Evrémonde line might end with Charles Darnay, sparing Lucie and her child, Madame Defarge's response is categorical and chilling. Defarge's inability to oppose his wife signals the defeat of his remaining humanity by the Revolution's logic of total eradication.

| Aspect | Monsieur Defarge | Madame Defarge |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| Motive | Justice for past suffering | Absolute retribution |
| Method | Organization, leadership | Surveillance, the knitting register |
| Mercy | Present but weakening | None |
| Fate | Survives the novel | Killed by Miss Pross |

## Character Arc

Defarge's trajectory — from protective servant to revolutionary leader to impersonal agent of the Terror — is one of the novel's most politically sophisticated arcs. Dickens does not present him as villainous; his grievances are real, his courage is genuine, and his initial mercy toward Manette is moving. What happens to him is not a revelation of hidden evil but a demonstration of how systems of retribution can erode individual conscience. By the novel's end, the man who once whispered reassurance to a broken prisoner has become a functionary who processes death sentences without reflection.

This arc makes Defarge the novel's most troubling revolutionary — more troubling, in some ways, than Madame Defarge, whose implacability is at least consistent. Defarge's mercy is real but insufficient; he sees the moral problem of extending punishment to the innocent but lacks the will to resist it. He is, in microcosm, the tragedy of the Revolution itself: a movement born of genuine grievance that becomes, through the logic of escalation, indistinguishable from the oppression it was formed to oppose.
