## Overview

Imprisonment is the foundational theme of *A Tale of Two Cities*, operating on literal, psychological, and social levels throughout the novel. Dr. Manette's eighteen-year confinement in the Bastille is the original wound from which the entire plot unfolds — a "long imprisonment" (009-085) without trial or accusation that exemplifies the ancien régime's arbitrary cruelty. But Dickens extends the metaphor of imprisonment far beyond prison walls: Charles Darnay is imprisoned by his family name, Sydney Carton by his own self-loathing, Madame Defarge by her thirst for vengeance, and the people of Saint Antoine by hunger and oppression. The novel maps an entire geography of captivity — the Bastille, La Force, the Conciergerie, Newgate, the Old Bailey — each representing a different form of carceral logic. The Bastille imprisons the innocent without cause; La Force imprisons the guilty-by-birth; the Conciergerie processes the condemned with bureaucratic efficiency. The novel's central question is whether anyone can truly escape the prison that the world — and the self — constructs around them.

## Manifestations

### Literal Imprisonment

The novel is structured around a series of prisons, each representing a different form of incarceration. The Bastille, where Dr. Manette was buried alive for eighteen years, is the archetype — a "living grave" (009-088) that transforms a promising physician into a broken shoemaker who has forgotten his own name. When Manette is first asked about his imprisonment, he answers in a "tone that went to every heart" with just three words: "A long imprisonment" (009-088).

La Force, where Darnay is held during the Revolution, inverts the Bastille's logic: where the monarchy imprisoned the innocent, the Republic now imprisons the guilty-by-birth:

> "The prison of La Force was a gloomy prison, dark and filthy, and with a horrible smell of foul sleep in it."
> — (031-090)

The Conciergerie, the antechamber to the guillotine, reduces imprisonment to its barest bureaucratic function. Prisoners are processed, listed, and dispatched with industrial efficiency — "the passage to the Conciergerie was short and dark; the night in its vermin-haunted cells was long and cold" (036-007). Even the Old Bailey and Newgate in London represent the English variant of carceral injustice — less ideological but equally arbitrary (008-023).

### Psychological Imprisonment

Dickens's deepest insight is that the most durable prisons are internal. Dr. Manette, though physically freed from the Bastille, remains imprisoned by his traumatic memories — his compulsive shoemaking at moments of stress is the prisoner within him reasserting control. Sydney Carton is imprisoned by his own conviction of worthlessness, "careless and slovenly if not debauched" (009-100), unable to imagine himself as anything other than a wasted life. Charles Darnay is imprisoned by his inheritance — the Evrémonde name that he has renounced but cannot escape. Each character's arc is defined by whether and how they achieve liberation from these internal prisons.

### Social Imprisonment

The novel also depicts imprisonment as a collective condition. The inhabitants of Saint Antoine are imprisoned by poverty and the crushing weight of aristocratic privilege:

> "The darkness of it was heavy — cold, dirt, sickness, ignorance, and want."
> — (005-006)

Their "imprisonment" in the social order of the ancien régime is what fuels the revolutionary fury — and paradoxically, the Revolution itself becomes a new form of imprisonment, as the ideological certainties of the Terror imprison both its perpetrators and its victims. The knitting women who sit around the guillotine are as trapped in their ritual of vengeance as any prisoner in the Bastille.

## Key Passages

> "He had been afflicted with a great and unexpected misfortune — the misfortune to undergo a long imprisonment, without trial, or even accusation, in the Bastille."
> — (009-085)

This passage, describing the public narrative around Dr. Manette, underscores the arbitrariness of ancien régime justice. The phrase "long imprisonment" reverberates through the novel, connecting Manette's suffering to the broader injustice suffered by the French people. The word "misfortune" is bitterly ironic — it was not misfortune but systematic tyranny that destroyed Manette's life.

> "There was a time in my imprisonment, when my desire for vengeance was unbearable."
> — (023-021), Dr. Manette

Dr. Manette's confession reveals the moral cost of imprisonment: the transformation of the victim into one who dreams of vengeance. He describes having pictured his daughter "as perfectly forgetful of me — rather, altogether ignorant of me, and unconscious of me" (023-023), revealing how imprisonment does not merely confine the body but poisons every human connection.

> "Up to that time, his imprisonment had been associated in the minds of the people with the Bastille alone."
> — (034-008)

This observation, made after the September Massacres, marks the moment when the Revolution's promise of liberation curdles into a new form of imprisonment. The Bastille has fallen, but the impulse to imprison endures. The Revolution, Dickens suggests, did not abolish imprisonment — it merely democratized it.

## Symbolic Elements

The novel's architecture of imprisonment extends beyond buildings. The knitting of Madame Defarge is a form of imprisonment-by-register — each stitch binding a name to a death sentence (021-073). The echoing footsteps that Lucie hears in Soho are the footfalls of the imprisoned multitude, the sound of the prisons about to burst. Dr. Manette's shoemaking bench — the tool of his prison labour — becomes the symbol of his psychological imprisonment, reappearing whenever trauma overwhelms him.

The Bastille itself, even after its physical destruction, persists as a symbolic prison. Defarge preserves Manette's cell as a site of memory, and the discovery of the hidden manuscript in "the wall of the chimney" (040-001) demonstrates that the Bastille's power to imprison extends beyond its walls and beyond its existence.
