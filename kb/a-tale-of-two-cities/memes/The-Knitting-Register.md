# The Knitting Register

## Overview

[[Madame-Defarge|Madame Defarge]]'s [[XV. Knitting|knitting]] is among the most famous and chilling motifs in Victorian fiction. Throughout *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*, she knits — in her wine-shop, at public executions, during the [[Storming-of-the-Bastille|storming of the Bastille]] — and every stitch records a name destined for the [[Guillotine|guillotine]]. The knitting is at once a domestic act and a deadly political archive: a register of the [[Condemned|condemned]] written in wool and carried in the hands of a woman who never speaks openly of what she records.

## The First Appearance: The Wine-Shop

Madame Defarge is introduced in Book 1, Chapter 5, "[[V. The Wine-shop|The Wine-shop]]," and the knitting is immediately presented as inseparable from her character:

> Madame Defarge, his wife, sat in the shop behind the counter as he entered it, and knitted with nimble fingers and an unvarying composure. She said not a word.

The juxtaposition is striking: her husband welcomes strangers, runs the shop, and speaks freely; Madame Defarge sits, knits, and watches. Her silence is not passivity but concentration — a form of vigilant attention that misses nothing:

> ...her knitting was before her, but she had laid her head aside to look on. There was an aged man in the corner...

From her first scene, the knitting is established as continuous and unbroken. It is her occupation, her weapon, and her record.

## The Code

The register operates as a cipher, legible only to those who hold the key. Each stitch in the knitted fabric corresponds to a name, and the pattern of stitches encodes the fate of the person named — death by the guillotine. The act of knitting is thus transformed from women's domestic labour into an instrument of revolutionary justice:

> "Knitted, in her own stitches and her own symbols, it will always be as plain to her as the sun. It would be easier for the weakest poltroon to erase himself from existence, than to erase one letter of his name or his crimes from the knitted register of Madame Defarge."

The register's symbolism operates on multiple levels:

| Level | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| Domestic | The traditional image of a woman knitting is subverted — the feminine domestic sphere becomes a site of revolutionary power |
| Archival | The knitting is a historical record, preserving names that the official histories of the old régime would erase |
| Fatalistic | Each stitch is a sentence already passed; the knitting embodies the inexorability of revolutionary justice |
| Gendered | Madame Defarge's power is exercised through a specifically female medium — she wields wool as men wield swords |

## Key Knitting Scenes

### The Marquis's Carriage Accident (Book 2, Chapter 7)

When the Marquis's carriage kills [[Gaspard]]'s child, Madame Defarge knits the Marquis's name into the register. She does not speak, does not cry out, does not move to comfort the grieving father — she knits:

> ...and Madame Defarge knitted on, looking at the father of the crushed child.

This moment is crucial: the register first records the Marquis, whose name will subsequently be erased from existence by Gaspard's knife. The knitting is not merely reactive but prophetic — it pronounces a sentence that events will later execute.

### The Spy's Visit (Book 2, Chapter 16)

When [[John-Barsad|John Barsad]], the English spy, visits the wine-shop, Madame Defarge knits his name into the register as well. She recognises him immediately:

> ...she knitted on with the utmost composure.

The spy's visit demonstrates the register's function as counter-surveillance. While the state sends Barsad to spy on the revolutionaries, Madame Defarge's knitting spies back — recording the recorders, watching the watchers.

### The Storming of the Bastille (Book 2, Chapter 21)

During the storming of [[The-Bastille|the Bastille]], Madame Defarge is present at the front of the mob, [[XVI. Still Knitting|still knitting]]:

> ...Madame Defarge, still knitted on with a steady hand.

She does not carry a weapon. Her knitting is her weapon.

## The Register and Historical Memory

The knitting register is Dickens's narrative device for representing the pre-literate record-keeping of revolutionary conspiracy. In an era when printed records belonged to the state and church, Madame Defarge's wool register represents an alternative archive — a people's history inscribed in a medium the authorities would never think to search. The knitting passes as innocent female domesticity while encoding the death sentences of the aristocracy.

The register also embodies a distinctive temporality: it records the past (who has committed crimes against the people), judges the present (who must be watched), and sentences the future (who will die). For Madame Defarge, these three tenses are collapsed into the single continuous action of knitting — past, present, and future stitched together in a seamless fabric.

## Significance

The knitting register is the novel's master metaphor for revolutionary memory. Where the aristocratic regime is characterised by forgetting — the Marquis forgets the child he killed before his carriage has reached the next street — Madame Defarge's knitting remembers everything. It is the conscience of the Revolution rendered in wool: implacable, patient, and complete.
