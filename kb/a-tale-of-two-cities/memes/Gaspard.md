# Gaspard

**Gaspard** is a minor but pivotal character in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*, whose child is run down and killed by the carriage of the [[Marquis-St-Evremonde]] in the streets of Paris. His grief, and the Marquis's callous response — tossing a gold coin as though a child's life were a debt discharged — crystallizes the novel's indictment of aristocratic indifference. Gaspard's subsequent assassination of the Marquis, and his own capture and execution, set in motion the chain of retribution that drives the novel's revolutionary plot.

## The Carriage and the Coin

Gaspard first appears as a shadowy figure in the crowd of the Saint Antoine district, where the wine cask has just shattered in the street. But his defining moment comes in Book the Second, Chapter 7, when the Marquis's speeding carriage strikes his child:

> "Hold!" said Monsieur the Marquis. "Hold the horses! Who threw that?" (013-041)

The Marquis tosses a gold coin to Gaspard — compensation for a life — and drives on, complaining that "you people cannot take care of yourselves and your children." Gaspard does not speak; he watches the carriage depart. The coin, thrown as casually as if to a dog, becomes the novel's emblem of the moral chasm between the classes.

## The Assassination

Gaspard follows the Marquis to his country château. That night, he climbs to the Marquis's bedchamber and kills him in his sleep, leaving the body with a knife driven through the heart. The assassination is swift, silent, and portrayed by Dickens as an act of almost impersonal justice — the stone faces carved on the château walls seem to look on in frozen approval. Gaspard then vanishes into the countryside.

## Capture and Execution

Gaspard is eventually captured by the authorities. His punishment is designed as a warning: he is hanged on a forty-foot gibbet erected in the village square, his body left to rot in full view of the château. The spectacle is intended to terrorize the peasantry; instead, it radicalizes them. The gibbet becomes a rallying point, and the village's sullen silence is more dangerous than open defiance. Gaspard's corpse, swinging above the village, is one of the novel's most powerful images of the brutality that fuels revolution.

## Narrative Function

Gaspard himself is barely individualized — Dickens gives him almost no dialogue, no backstory, no interiority. He functions as a **representative figure**: the peasant reduced to absolute extremity by a system that values his child's life at a gold coin. His arc — grief, vengeance, capture, execution, martyrdom — traces the cycle of violence that the Revolution both continues and accelerates. He is the face of the anonymous suffering that the aristocracy has refused to see.
