# Emigrant

In the context of the French Revolution, an *emigrant* (French: *émigré*) was a person — typically a member of the aristocracy — who had fled France during the revolutionary upheaval. Under the Revolutionary laws passed from 1791 onward, emigrants were classified as traitors to the Republic, subject to execution if they returned to French soil and to the confiscation of their property.

## Definition

An emigrant was legally defined as any French citizen who had left the territory of the Republic after a specified date and had not returned under conditions prescribed by law. The designation carried immediate legal consequences, including civil death — the loss of all property and citizenship rights — and, for those who returned without authorization, the penalty of execution.

## Historical Context

The emigration of the French nobility began shortly after the fall of the Bastille in July 1789 and accelerated through 1791–1792. Many *émigrés* gathered in neighboring states, particularly in the Austrian Netherlands and the German principalities along the Rhine, where they formed armed contingents and sought foreign intervention to restore the monarchy. The Revolutionary government responded with increasingly severe legislation, culminating in the Law of Suspects (1793), which made emigration itself prima facie evidence of treason against the Republic.

## Role in the Novel

The charge of being an emigrant is central to [[Charles-Darnay]]'s legal peril in *A Tale of Two Cities*. Darnay, having renounced his aristocratic inheritance and emigrated to England, returns to France during the Revolution to aid an imprisoned former servant. He is immediately identified by his old family name, Evrémonde, and arrested as an emigrant aristocrat (031-006):

> "Emigrant," said the functionary, "I am going to send you on to Paris, under an escort."

The label follows Darnay through every stage of his journey. The functionary reminds him repeatedly: "You are an aristocrat, and must have an escort — and must pay for it" (031-009). In the towns through which he passes, crowds cry "Down with the emigrant!" (031-017). The term functions not merely as a legal category but as a social stigma — the emigrant is the enemy of the people by definition, and Darnay's actual innocence is irrelevant to the machinery of the Revolutionary courts.

[[Gabelle]]'s desperate letter — "where is that emigrant? I cry in my sleep where is he?" (030-058) — confirms the legal reality: any association with an emigrant, however indirect, is grounds for imprisonment and execution. Darnay's decision to return to France, though motivated by conscience, is legally suicidal precisely because of the emigrant laws.

## Related Terms

- **Aristocrat**: The social class most commonly associated with emigration
- **Evrémonde**: Darnay's family name, which condemns him under the emigrant laws
- **[[Lettre-de-Cachet]]**: The pre-Revolutionary instrument of arbitrary imprisonment, counterpart to Revolutionary justice
