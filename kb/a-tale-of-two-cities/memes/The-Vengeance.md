# The Vengeance

**The Vengeance** is a formidable figure among the revolutionary women of the [[Saint-Antoine]] district in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. She serves as the principal lieutenant and closest companion of [[Madame-Defarge]], acting as her eyes, her voice among the crowd, and her enforcer. Unlike many characters in the novel who bear personal names, she is known only by her revolutionary title — "The Vengeance" — a naming choice that strips her of individual identity and transforms her into a walking embodiment of the Revolution's retributive impulse.

## The Complimentary Name

The Vengeance's title is not a family name but a revolutionary honorific bestowed by her peers. Dickens introduces this with characteristic irony:

> ...the short, rather plump wife of a starved grocer, and the mother of two children withal, this lieutenant had already earned the complimentary name of The Vengeance. (028-003)

The juxtaposition is deliberate: a "rather plump" wife and mother, whose husband has been "starved" by the economic injustices of the ancien régime, becomes the human vessel of collective rage. Her domestic identity is subsumed entirely by her revolutionary function.

## Role in the Revolutionary Circle

The Vengeance occupies a position of considerable informal authority. She is not a member of the Tribunal, nor does she hold any official office, but her proximity to Madame Defarge grants her influence over the rhythms of revolutionary justice in Saint-Antoine. She appears at every significant gathering of the Defarge inner circle:

> The Defarges, husband and wife, The Vengeance, and Jacques Three, were in the front rank of the revolutionary jury, looking on with folded arms. (028-024)

Alongside [[Monsieur-Defarge]], Madame Defarge, and [[Jacques-Three]], The Vengeance forms the nucleus of the district's revolutionary machinery. She participates in the councils that decide life and death, her approval carrying weight in the grim deliberations of the Terror.

## Companion to Madame Defarge

The relationship between The Vengeance and Madame Defarge is one of the novel's most unsettling partnerships. Where Madame Defarge is still, watchful, and unnervingly silent, The Vengeance is vocal, demonstrative, and physically expressive — two faces of revolutionary fury:

> "She is an Angel!" said The Vengeance, and embraced her. (042-026)

This embrace, bestowed on Madame Defarge, reveals The Vengeance's total moral alignment with her leader. She does not merely obey Madame Defarge; she venerates her. The two women travel together, sit in council together, and prosecute the Revolution's enemies in tandem:

> Madame Defarge, smiling to The Vengeance. "Save him now, my Doctor, save him!" (040-123, adapted)

## The Wood-Sawyer and the Carmagnole

The Vengeance appears in several of the novel's most vivid revolutionary tableaux. She dances the Carmagnole — the frenzied revolutionary dance — hand in hand with the wood-sawyer, a former road-mender who has become a minor functionary of the Terror:

> The wood-sawyer hand in hand with The Vengeance. There could not be fewer than five hundred people, and they were dancing like five thousand demons. (035-032, adapted)

This image — the wood-sawyer and The Vengeance leading the demonic dance — captures the collective madness that Dickens saw in the Revolution's excess. The Vengeance is not a leader in the sense of giving orders; she is a leader in the sense of embodying and amplifying the crowd's most destructive impulses.

## The Final Council

In the novel's climactic sequence, The Vengeance participates in the dark council where Madame Defarge resolves to denounce [[Lucie-Manette]] and her child — extending the Evrémonde vendetta to the innocent:

> There was a darkly ominous council with The Vengeance and Jacques Three of the Revolutionary Committee. (044-001, adapted)

The Vengeance raises no objection to the plan to destroy an innocent woman and child. Her moral imagination, like Madame Defarge's, has been wholly consumed by the logic of retribution — guilt by association, death by lineage, justice without mercy.

## Symbolism

The Vengeance represents the Revolution's **collective fury made flesh**. She has no private life, no inner conflict, no moment of doubt or hesitation. Dickens gives her no backstory of personal suffering (unlike Madame Defarge) because she does not need one — her vengeance is not personal but societal, the accumulated rage of a class rather than the grief of an individual. In this sense, she is more terrifying than Madame Defarge: she kills not because she was wronged but because killing has become her identity.
