# Peasant

The peasant is the anonymous foundation of the novel's social world. Before any
named character speaks, the peasant class is present — as hunger, as labour, as
the silent suffering out of which revolution grows. Dickens gives the peasant
no single representative but scatters the condition across the novel like the
wine-dregs in the Saint Antoine street.

## The Peasant at Work

The first peasants we see are suspended in labour: "the peasants suspended
their operations to look at the carriage" (014-008) as the Monseigneur's
carriage passes. The word "suspended" captures the peasant's relationship to
time — their labour is ceaseless, interrupted only by the passage of noble
power. The "peasants at the fountain" in the village (015-100) are a recurring
image — the fountain as the meeting-point of peasant life, where water and news
flow together.

The riding-whips of the nobility fall upon "many a peasant, gone to his
benefactor Death" (015-003) — the peasant body is the canvas upon which noble
power writes its authority. Death, in this world, is a benefactor because it
ends suffering that life cannot relieve.

## Peasant Hunger

The novel's political economy is written in the peasant's stomach. "Two or
three ragged peasants emerged from the crowd to take a greedy share" of the
spilled wine (022-001) — the word "greedy" applied not to avarice but to
desperation. When "peasants' carts bringing in supplies" (031-041) enter Paris,
they carry not abundance but the bare minimum that separates life from
starvation.

Dickens contrasts the peasant's condition with the noble's through physical
detail. In Dr. Manette's manuscript, two bodies lie side by side: the noble
brothers with their "negligent indifference," the peasant boy with his
"all trodden-down sentiment" (040-060). The peasant body is not merely killed
but erased — the noble disregard extends beyond death to the extinction of the
peasant's humanity.

## The Peasant as Revolutionary

The peasant becomes the revolutionary. "Gentle born and peasant born; all red
wine for La Guillotine" (035-001) — the guillotine is the great leveller. The
peasant's long starvation finds its answer in the blade that falls equally on
noble and common neck. Gaspard, the peasant father whose child is killed by the
Marquis's carriage, enacts the first act of revolutionary justice: he murders
the Marquis and is himself executed, becoming a martyr whose body hangs as a
warning — and an inspiration.

The "peasant family so injured by the two brothers" (042-035) — the girl
violated, the boy killed — becomes the moral core of Dr. Manette's accusation.
The peasant's suffering is the novel's ultimate evidence against the old order.

## The Peasant and History

In the novel's closing vision, Dickens imagines the future of those "millions
of starving peasants" (045-002). The "great magician" of revolution will sweep
away the huts of the poor along with the palaces of the rich — a prophecy that
is also an elegy. The peasant, having suffered under the old regime and fought
in the revolution, will inherit a world still marked by hunger but animated
now by hope.
