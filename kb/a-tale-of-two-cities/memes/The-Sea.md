# The Sea

The sea is the novel's master metaphor for the revolutionary crowd — a living,
elemental force that rises without warning and drowns everything in its path.
Unlike the Woodman and the Farmer, who work silently and slowly, the sea is
sudden and deafening. It is the sound the Revolution makes, and Dickens returns
to it again and again in Book the Third to convey the scale and terror of the
mob.

## The Sound Before the Storm

The sea metaphor first appears as a premonition. As the storm of revolution
gathers in France, "they began to have an awful sound, as of a great storm in
France with a dreadful sea rising" (027-012). The words "awful" and "dreadful"
carry their full weight — the sea is not beautiful or sublime; it is terrifying.
What is rising in France is beyond human control, and those who hear it from
London can only listen and wait.

Dickens carefully distinguishes the sea from mere weather. A storm passes; the
sea is permanent. By choosing the sea as his central metaphor for the mob, he
insists that the revolutionary crowd is not a temporary disturbance but a
geological reality — as deep and old as the ocean itself, and as indifferent to
human intentions.

## The Sea Rises

The metaphor achieves its full power in the chapter describing the fall of the
Bastille: "With a roar that sounded as if all the breath in France had been
shaped into the detested word, the living sea rose, wave on wave, depth on
depth, and overflowed the city to that point" (027-038). The phrase "living
sea" is the key — Dickens fuses the natural and the human, the sea and the mob,
until they are indistinguishable. The sea "rose, wave on wave" — the mob does
not attack the Bastille in a single rush but in successive surges, each wave
breaking and being replaced by another.

The sea has its own geography: "Alarm-bells ringing, drums beating, the sea
raging and thundering on its new beach, the attack began" (027-038). The
"beach" is the Bastille's walls; the sea has found its shore. And once the sea
has found a shore, it will not retreat.

## A Sea of Cries

The sea speaks with a single voice composed of thousands. "Of all these cries,
and ten thousand incoherences, 'The Prisoners!' was the cry most taken up by the
sea that rushed in, as if there were an eternity of people, as well as of time
and space" (027-050). The sea cries "The Prisoners!" — a demand for liberation
— but it is a voice that dissolves individual identity into the roar of the
waves. Each person in the crowd is a drop of water; together they are an ocean.
And an ocean has no conscience, no memory, no pity.

This paradoxical quality — unity and dissolution at once — is what makes the sea
metaphor so effective. The mob acts as one, but that oneness is achieved at the
cost of every individual human quality that makes judgment possible.

## The Sea and the Guillotine

The sea metaphor persists through the Terror. The crowd at the guillotine is
described as "the great rough ocean of the people" and "the sea of black and
threatening waters" (040-002, 040-065). The colour has darkened — from "living
sea" to "black and threatening waters" — as the Revolution has darkened from
liberation to retribution. The sea that freed the Bastille's prisoners is the
same sea that watches the tumbrils roll, and it is no less voracious for having
changed targets.

## The Sea in Early Hints

Dickens also uses sea imagery in quieter passages long before the Revolution
arrives. The fog in the opening chapter moves "in ripples that visibly followed
and overspread one another, as the waves of an unwholesome sea might do"
(002-003) — the sea of revolution hinted at even in the English mist. And the
atmosphere of Tellson's Bank is described as having so strong a "piscatory
flavour that one might have supposed sick fish went up to be dipped in it"
(004-024) — a comic variation that anticipates the darker use of the metaphor.
These early touches show Dickens laying the groundwork for the sea image that
will dominate Book the Third.
