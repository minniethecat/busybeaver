# Noble

The French nobility stands at the centre of the novel's political argument. Not
merely a class but a system of inherited privilege, the "noble" world of
pre-revolutionary France is presented by Dickens as a machine for producing
suffering — glittering at court, brutal in the countryside, and ultimately
swept away by the very forces it has provoked.

## The World of the Noble

Dickens introduces the nobility through ironic juxtaposition. The opening
chapter notes that "noble lords at Court drawing-rooms" wore crosses at their
necks — even as the poor starved in the streets (001-005). The distance between
the courtly noble and the suffering peasant is measured not in miles but in
moral blindness.

The archetype of noble indifference is Monseigneur, a great lord who appears in
a single devastating chapter. "Monseigneur had one truly noble idea of general
public business, which was, to let everything go on in its own way" (013-004).
His morning ritual — the chocolate that requires four attendants — is the
novel's definitive image of noble excess. The "noble and chaste fashion set by
Monseigneur" (013-002) is the fashion of total disconnection from the world
beyond the palace gates.

## The Noble in the Countryside

The Marquis St. Evremonde brings the noble code to the French countryside with
murderous clarity. When his carriage runs down a child in the street, the
Marquis's concern is for his horses, not the dead peasant. "How do I know what
injury you have done my horses?" (013-031) — the noble's first thought in the
face of a child's death is property, not humanity.

When the Revolution comes, the nobles flee. "Those nobles who had seen the
coming storm" took "to their noble heels" (030-003, 030-006) — the adjective
"noble" repeated with savage irony. Their flight creates a vacuum that the
Jacquerie fills. The "great nobleman" whose Paris house becomes the Defarges'
stronghold "had lived in it until he had made himself a chimney-sweep and gone
across the water" (032-001) — the noble disguise as escape, the mansion turned
revolutionary headquarters.

## The Noble Name

Charles Darnay's entire story is shaped by his relationship to the noble name
of Evremonde. He renounces it, yet it pursues him across the Channel.
Gabelle's desperate appeal invokes "the honour of your noble name" (030-059,
030-088) — the very name Darnay has shed. And in Dr. Manette's Bastille
manuscript, the accusation falls on "the noble sleep" of the Evremonde
brothers, undisturbed by the cries of the peasant girl they have destroyed
(040-061).

The novel's final irony is that the nobility's most articulate defender is a
prisoner. Darnay, a noble by birth who has rejected nobility, is condemned by
his blood — a twist that makes the point: in the Revolutionary Tribunal, noble
birth is guilt, regardless of the life lived.

## Noble as a Moral Quality

Dickens reserves the word "noble" for one genuinely positive use — not to
describe a class but a character. Lucie tells Sydney Carton, "You are a noble
heart" (042-075). In this single line, Dickens separates the word from its
inherited meaning and restores it to its moral sense: nobility as generosity,
not genealogy.
