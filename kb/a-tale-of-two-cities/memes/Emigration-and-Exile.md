## Overview

Emigration and exile form the geographic and political framework of *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. The novel's title itself announces its dual setting, and movement between France and England drives the plot at every critical juncture. Characters cross the Channel to escape death, to answer calls for help, or to flee the consequences of their past. In the Revolution's logic, emigration becomes a crime punishable by death—a decree that ensnares [[Charles-Darnay]] and threatens everyone connected to him.

Dickens uses emigration to explore questions of identity, loyalty, and belonging. Is Darnay French or English? Is his renunciation of the Evrémonde name an act of moral integrity or aristocratic cowardice? The Channel becomes a moral boundary as much as a physical one: England represents refuge and the rule of law; France, under the Terror, represents the collapse of legal protection into arbitrary violence.

> "Truly, a decree for selling the property of emigrants... Everybody says it is but one of several, and that there will be others—if there are not already—banishing all emigrants, and condemning all to death who return." (036-033)

This passage articulates the escalating logic of revolutionary law. Each decree generates the next; what begins with property confiscation ends with the guillotine. The word "emigrant" transforms from a description of circumstance into a death sentence.

## Manifestations

### Charles Darnay: The "Emigrant" Evrémonde

Charles Darnay is the novel's central emigrant figure. Having renounced his aristocratic title and inheritance, he lives in England as a French tutor, having "voluntarily relinquished a title that was distasteful to him" (041-007). His return to France to save his former steward [[Gabelle]] triggers the novel's final crisis. Upon arrival, he is immediately arrested under the revolutionary decree "banishing all emigrants, and condemning all to death who return" (036-033). Throughout his imprisonment, he is addressed simply as "The Emigrant Evrémonde"—his identity reduced to a legal category that carries a death sentence.

Darnay's trial dramatizes the emigrant's dilemma. His defence—that he left France before the word "emigrant" acquired its revolutionary meaning, that he worked for his living in England, that he returned only to save a citizen's life—is legally sound but politically irrelevant. "Emigrants have no rights, Evrémonde," the officer tells him (036-035).

> "Rise and dress yourself, emigrant." (036-012)

The guard's command, with its curt imperative and its substitution of a legal category for a name, enacts the emigrant's reduction. Darnay is no longer a man with a history, a wife, a child; he is a legal status, and that status is capital.

> At Beauvais, "an ominous crowd gathered to see him dismount of the posting-yard, and many voices called out loudly, 'Down with the emigrant!'" (036-017)

The crowd's cry collapses all distinction: aristocrat and commoner, guilty and innocent, are alike condemned by their absence from France during the Revolution. The epithet "emigrant" does the work of the guillotine before the blade falls.

### Doctor Manette's Exile

Doctor Manette's exile is psychological rather than geographical. His eighteen-year imprisonment in [[The-Bastille]] is a form of internal exile—buried alive, removed from the world, forgotten. Upon his release, he becomes a "voluntary exile from France," rebuilding his life in London. Unlike Darnay, who chooses exile as a moral act, Manette's exile is inflicted upon him; his recovery in England, under [[Lucie-Manette]]'s care, represents the slow healing of trauma through displacement.

### The Émigré Community in London

Dickens sketches a community of French exiles in London: [[Tellsons-Bank]] serves as their financial and social hub. [[Jarvis-Lorry]], the quintessential English man of business, becomes the intermediary between the French émigrés and English institutions. The bank's Paris branch and London headquarters mirror the divided lives of the exiles themselves, who exist in both countries while belonging fully to neither.

> "I have acted against them for an emigrant. It is in vain I represent that I have acted for an emigrant who was not an emigrant in the sense of their laws." (030-057)

Lorry's frustration captures the emigrant's impossible legal position. The law does not recognise the distinction between an Evrémonde who fled his crimes and a Manette who was imprisoned without trial; both are "emigrants," and both are condemned.

### The Decree and Its Consequences

The revolutionary decree against emigrants transforms emigration from a personal choice into a political crime. Property is confiscated; return is punished by death. This legal framework creates the novel's central crisis: Darnay cannot abandon Gabelle without moral failure, but answering Gabelle's call means walking into a legal trap. The decree represents the Revolution's tendency to replace the ancien régime's arbitrary power with its own brand of absolute law.

> "You are a cursed emigrant," cried a farrier, making at him through the crowd. (036-020)

The farrier's curse is personal where the guard's command was bureaucratic. Together they represent the two faces of revolutionary justice: the impersonal machinery of law and the personal hatred of the crowd. Between them, the emigrant has no refuge.

## Key Passages

> "Truly, a decree for selling the property of emigrants... Everybody says it is but one of several, and that there will be others—if there are not already—banishing all emigrants, and condemning all to death who return." (036-033)

This passage articulates the escalating logic of revolutionary law. Each decree generates the next; what begins with property confiscation ends with the guillotine.

> "Emigrants have no rights, Evrémonde," was the stolid reply. (036-035)

The officer's words distill the emigrant's legal condition: outside the protection of law, subject to the state's absolute power. The phrase echoes the ancien régime's lettres de cachet, under which Manette was imprisoned without trial.

## Character Connections

- **[[Charles-Darnay]]** — The central emigrant; his return to France triggers the novel's climactic sequence
- **[[Doctor-Manette]]** — An exile of a different kind: the Bastille's internal exile, then a voluntary exile in London
- **[[Jarvis-Lorry]]** — Tellson's man, the bridge between French exiles and English institutions
- **[[Madame-Defarge]]** — For her, emigration is not escape but evidence of guilt; the Evrémondes' departure proves their crimes
