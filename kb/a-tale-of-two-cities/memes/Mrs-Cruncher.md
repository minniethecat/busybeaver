## Overview

Mrs. Cruncher is the wife of [[Jerry-Cruncher]] and mother of [[Young-Jerry|Young Jerry]]. She is a quiet, pious woman whose religious devotion manifests in prayer, a practice her husband derisively calls "flopping." Throughout the novel, Jerry Cruncher subjects her to verbal abuse for her faith, blaming her prayers for his misfortunes. Despite her limited page-time, Mrs. Cruncher plays a pivotal role in one of the novel's most unexpected redemptive arcs: her husband's eventual renunciation of grave-robbing and his grudging acknowledgment that her "flopping" may have had value after all.

## The Domestic Sphere of the Crunchers

The Cruncher household, located in Hanging-sword-alley near Whitefriars, is one of the novel's few sustained depictions of lower-class London domestic life. It is a home defined by fear and noise: Jerry Cruncher's boots on the stairs, his roaring demands for breakfast, and his wife's anxious silence punctuated by whispered prayers. The narrator describes the Cruncher breakfast ritual as a scene of domestic tyranny: "Mr. Cruncher was soothed, but shook his head in a gloomy and prophetic manner, and having taken a contemptuous survey of his wife" (007-016).

What makes this domestic portrait distinctive is Dickens's refusal to sentimentalise it. Mrs. Cruncher is neither a martyr nor a saint; she is a woman living with a bully, and her prayers are her only form of resistance. The "flopping" that Jerry so despises is, in fact, an act of quiet defiance — a refusal to accept her husband's worldview as the only reality. Each time she kneels to pray, she asserts the existence of a moral order that Jerry's grave-robbing and domestic tyranny cannot touch.

## The "Flopping" Motif

The word "flopping" — Jerry Cruncher's coinage — is one of the novel's most memorable verbal inventions. It reduces prayer from a spiritual act to a physical gesture, draining it of meaning and dignity. Jerry uses the word as a weapon, and its recurrence through the novel tracks his spiritual trajectory.

In the early chapters, "flopping" is pure contempt: "What do you mean by flopping yourself down and praying against me?" (007-018). The accusation is that Mrs. Cruncher's prayers are selfish — that she prays against her husband's prosperity, as though her piety were a form of sabotage. The irony, which the reader grasps but Jerry does not, is that his "prosperity" consists of robbing graves and selling the bodies to surgeons. Mrs. Cruncher's prayers against this are, in fact, prayers for his moral reclamation.

In the middle of the novel, the pattern continues unchanged. Jerry berates his wife with mechanical regularity, and she endures with the same mechanical patience. The monotony of the abuse is part of its horror: it is not dramatic cruelty but the dull erosion of daily life.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Jerry-Cruncher]]** — Husband. Jerry Cruncher's relationship with his wife is defined by domestic tyranny. He berates her for praying, accusing her of "flopping yourself down and praying against" his prosperity (007-016). His verbal abuse continues through most of the novel until a late conversion.
- **[[Young-Jerry]]** — Son. Mrs. Cruncher's son witnesses his father's harsh treatment of her, and the family dynamic forms the domestic backdrop to the Cruncher subplot. Young Jerry's ambivalent relationship with his father — admiring his strength, fearing his temper, pitying his mother — is one of the novel's nuanced portraits of filial loyalty under strain.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Cruncher first appears in the Cruncher household scenes of Book the Second, where Jerry Cruncher's morning routine includes abusing her for her prayers. The pattern recurs: "What do you mean by flopping yourself down and praying against me?" (007-018) he demands. In Book the Third, after the Crunchers are in Paris during the Terror, Jerry experiences a change of heart.

The conversion moment is rendered with Dickens's characteristic blend of comedy and moral seriousness. Following [[Sydney-Carton]]'s sacrifice and his own brush with revolutionary justice, Jerry announces: "Never no more will I interfere with Mrs. Cruncher's flopping, never no more!" (044-048). The vow is comic in its phrasing — "flopping" remains "flopping," even in renunciation — but genuine in its intent. Jerry has seen, in Carton's willing death for another, a sacrifice that dwarfs his own petty cruelties, and he returns to London determined to be a better man.

Mrs. Cruncher, through her patient endurance and quiet faith, becomes the silent agent of her husband's reformation. She does not argue, does not fight back, does not leave; she simply persists in her prayers, and in the end, her persistence is vindicated. Her role embodies the novel's theme of [[Resurrection]] in the domestic sphere — the idea that even the most hardened sinner can be redeemed, and that redemption often comes through the quiet witness of those who refuse to stop believing in it.

## A Counterpoint to Madame Defarge

Though the two characters never meet, Mrs. Cruncher and [[Madame-Defarge]] form a striking structural opposition in the novel. Madame Defarge is a woman driven by implacable vengeance, her knitting a register of those marked for death; Mrs. Cruncher is a woman sustained by patient prayer, her "flopping" a register of those she hopes will be saved. One seeks to destroy; the other endures in the hope of redemption. The contrast illuminates Dickens's broader argument about the nature of justice — that the cycle of revenge leads only to the guillotine, while the cycle of forgiveness, however slow and unglamorous, leads toward renewal.
