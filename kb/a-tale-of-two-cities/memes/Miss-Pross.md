# Miss Pross

**Miss Pross** is the devoted English servant and companion of Lucie Manette in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. Wild-haired, red-faced, and possessed of an almost feral protectiveness toward "Ladybird" (her pet name for Lucie), Miss Pross functions as both comic relief and, in the novel's climactic sequence, an unexpected agent of heroic violence. Her lethal struggle with [[Madame-Defarge]] in a locked Paris apartment — a battle between two formidable and opposing women — secures the escape of Lucie, her child, and [[Jarvis-Lorry]] from revolutionary France. Miss Pross represents Dickens' ideal of English domestic loyalty: a love so absolute and untheoretical that it is willing to kill and to be destroyed for the person it protects. Her permanent deafness from the pistol shot that kills Madame Defarge mirrors [[Sydney-Carton]]'s death as a sacrifice rendered in a minor key — one life traded for another, one form of damage accepted willingly for love.

## Appearance and Manner

Dickens introduces Miss Pross through the door of the Manette household in Soho, where her suspicious guardianship extends to screening every visitor:

> It proceeded from Miss Pross, the wild red woman, strong of hand, whose acquaintance with a certain expression of her bright eyes had been connected with the words "I'll pulling your head off" — spoken, it was understood, in a figurative sense. (012-019)

Her speech is a torrent of grievances, and her habit of addressing inanimate objects — a practice she calls "answering herself" — provides a running comic counterpoint to the novel's darker passages. Yet beneath the bluster is a capacity for love that equals any character's: she has given her entire life to Lucie, whom she has raised since the girl was ten.

Miss Pross is also fiercely patriotic in a comic register. She despises all things French, referring to "foreigners" with a contempt that Dickens employs for both humor and thematic contrast: her English prejudice is presented as comic and harmless, while the French Revolution's ideological certainties are lethal.

## Background

Miss Pross's past is revealed in fragments. She comes from a family of modest means and has a single brother, Solomon Pross, on whom she has lavished affection and money despite his persistent failures. She entered the service of [[Lucie-Manette]] when Lucie was a child of ten, following the death of Lucie's mother, and has served as a combination of nurse, guardian, and surrogate mother ever since. Her life has been defined by this devotion: she has no husband, no family of her own, and no apparent ambition beyond the protection of "Ladybird."

The one shadow over her life is her brother Solomon, who has repeatedly exploited her love, stolen her savings, and disappeared. The revelation in Book the Third that Solomon is none other than [[John-Barsad]], the spy and turnkey, forces Miss Pross to confront the reality that the brother she loved never existed — or, if he did, was transformed long ago into something she cannot recognize.

## The Prodigal Brother

Miss Pross's one vulnerability is her brother Solomon — a wastrel who has broken her heart and stolen her money. Dickens reveals, with characteristic irony, that Solomon Pross is none other than **John Barsad**, the spy who testified against [[Charles-Darnay]] at the Old Bailey and who later serves as a turnkey at the Conciergerie. The recognition scene, in which Barsad is exposed before [[Sydney-Carton]] and Jarvis Lorry, is one of the novel's most economical reversals: the brother who shamed Miss Pross becomes the instrument through which Carton's substitution plan succeeds.

## The Confrontation with Madame Defarge

Miss Pross's finest hour comes in Book the Third, when she remains behind in the Paris apartment to delay Madame Defarge while the Manette-Darnay family escapes. The confrontation is a masterstroke of Dickens' plotting: the two most determined women in the novel — one driven by revolutionary vengeance, the other by protective love — meet in a locked room. Neither speaks the other's language, and their mutual incomprehension intensifies the scene's tension. When Madame Defarge draws a pistol, Miss Pross seizes it:

> The two hands met, and there was a struggle. Madame Defarge's hands were at her bosom. Miss Pross looked up, saw what was there, struck at it, struck out a flash and a crash, and stood alone — blinded with smoke. (022-028, adapted)

Madame Defarge is killed by her own weapon. Miss Pross, permanently deafened by the pistol's report, escapes to rejoin the family — a sacrifice of her hearing that mirrors Carton's sacrifice of his life, rendered in a minor key.

## Role in the Story

Miss Pross serves three overlapping functions in the novel. As a comic character, she provides relief through her verbal eccentricities and her running battle with inanimate objects. As a guardian figure, she anchors the domestic world of the Manette household in Soho, acting as gatekeeper against all threats. And as an agent of the climax, she performs the single most physically decisive action by any character not named Carton: killing Madame Defarge and enabling the family's escape.

Her arc is one of hidden heroism revealed. Throughout the novel, her protectiveness is treated as comic excess — a fussy, exaggerated form of Englishness. In the final act, Dickens reveals that the comedy was always the mask of a ferocious commitment: Miss Pross was never ridiculous, only waiting for the moment when her particular form of love would be tested to its limit.

## Symbolism

Miss Pross embodies an ideal of **English domestic loyalty** that Dickens opposes to French revolutionary fanaticism. She has no interest in politics, no capacity for abstraction — only an absolute, bodily commitment to the person she loves. Her final confrontation literalizes this opposition: [[Love-and-Devotion]] versus [[Vengeance]], protection versus destruction, the personal versus the ideological. Her permanent deafness, like Carton's death, is a price willingly paid — and Dickens ensures that neither character regrets it.

The locked-room confrontation also serves as the novel's symbolic collision of two forms of female power. Madame Defarge represents political vengeance organized into a system; Miss Pross represents love organized into a reflex. Dickens frames Miss Pross's victory as a moral one: the personal triumphs over the ideological, not because it is stronger, but because it is truer to the human scale at which the novel insists life must be lived.
