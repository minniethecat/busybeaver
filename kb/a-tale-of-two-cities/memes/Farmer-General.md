# Farmer-General

## Definition

A Farmer-General (*Fermier-Général*) was a member of the *Ferme Générale*, the private corporation that contracted with the French Crown to collect indirect taxes — principally the *[[Gabelle|gabelle]]* (salt tax), customs duties, and various consumption taxes — on behalf of the royal treasury. In exchange for an upfront payment to the Crown, the Farmers-General were granted the right to collect taxes and keep the difference between what they collected and what they had paid. The system created a class of immensely wealthy financiers whose fortunes were built on the extraction of revenue from the poorest members of French society.

## The Tax-Farming System

The *Ferme Générale* was among the most hated institutions of the Ancien Régime. Its agents — known as *gabelous* in the case of the salt tax — had the power to search private homes, seize goods, and imprison those who evaded payment. The system was characterised by:

- **Regressive taxation**: The heaviest taxes fell on the most essential commodities — salt was a necessity for food preservation — and thus on the poorest consumers
- **Private enforcement**: The Farmers-General maintained their own armed force to suppress smuggling and enforce collection
- **No public accountability**: The Farmers-General were private contractors, answerable only to the Crown for the sums they paid, not to the public for how they extracted them
- **Immense personal wealth**: The leading Farmers-General lived in lavish hôtels in Paris, their fortunes a visible contrast to the poverty of the peasants who paid the taxes

The financial and moral logic of the system was stark: the Crown received immediate cash; the Farmers-General extracted far more from the population than they paid to the Crown; the poor bore the burden.

## The Farmer-General in A Tale of Two Cities

In *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*, Dickens uses the figure of the Farmer-General to dramatise the connection between aristocratic luxury and popular suffering. In the chapter "[[VII. Monseigneur in Town|Monseigneur in Town]]" — which opens with the Monseigneur's chocolate ceremony — a Farmer-General appears among the guests:

> ...a Farmer-General, of seventy years of age, whose wealth, enormous as it was, was not half so great as the poverty that produced it...

The sentence is Dickens at his most economically devastating. The Farmer-General's wealth and the people's poverty are presented not merely as co-existing but as causally connected: the wealth *is produced by* the poverty. The tax farmer's fine clothes, his house, his retinue of servants — all of it is extracted from the labour and consumption of the starving peasants of [[Saint-Antoine|Saint Antoine]].

Dickens describes the Farmer-General as old, rich, and surrounded by "a vast quantity of fantastically dressed women" — a detail that emphasises the relationship between financial exploitation and sexual luxury, the conversion of [[Peasant|peasant]] hunger into courtly excess.

## The Moral Economy of the Tax Farm

The Farmer-General serves as a narrative shorthand for the structural injustice of the Ancien Régime. Unlike the Marquis, who kills [[Gaspard]]'s child through active negligence, the Farmer-General kills no one directly — yet his wealth is the material expression of a system that starves thousands:

> ...his great wealth was a very small monster beside the monster of the taxes that created it, but it was a monster still...

Dickens refuses to let the Farmer-General's age, his physical frailty, or his cultivated manners obscure the nature of his wealth. The old man is not personally cruel — he does not beat his servants or run over children in his carriage — but his entire existence is sustained by a system of legalised theft that Dickens presents as morally indistinguishable from violence.

## The Farmer-General and the Revolution

The tax-farming system was among the primary grievances that fuelled the French Revolution. The *cahiers de doléances* (lists of grievances) drawn up in 1789 repeatedly demanded the abolition of the *Ferme Générale* and the punishment of its agents. When the Revolution came, the Farmers-General were among its earliest and most conspicuous victims:

- The *Ferme Générale* was abolished in 1791
- Many former Farmers-General were arrested and tried before [[The Revolutionary Tribunal|the Revolutionary Tribunal]]
- The most famous of the Farmers-General, the chemist Antoine Lavoisier — who had used his tax-farming wealth to finance his scientific research — was guillotined in 1794

The fate of the Farmers-General exemplifies the Revolution's ambivalent attitude toward economic crime: the system that enriched them was legal under the old régime, but the Revolution judged by a different standard of justice — one in which the extraction of wealth from the poor was itself a capital offence.

## Significance

The Farmer-General is a minor character in *A Tale of Two Cities* — he appears in a single paragraph — but he represents a major thematic node. His presence at the [[Monseigneur's Reception|Monseigneur's reception]] ties the personal luxury of the aristocracy to the systemic exploitation of the tax farm, connecting the chocolate-drinking Monseigneur to the starving peasant through the mediating figure of the financier. The Farmer-General is, in Dickens's moral economy, the missing link between the private vice of aristocratic excess and the public crime of popular immiseration.
