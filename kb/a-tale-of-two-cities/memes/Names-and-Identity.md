## Overview

Names in *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]* are never merely labels. They are instruments of power: to name someone is to define them, and to rename them is to transform their social and legal identity. [[Charles-Darnay]] renounces the name Evrémonde, trading an inheritance of guilt for a life of honest labour. [[Doctor-Manette]] is known only as "One Hundred and Five, North Tower" during his imprisonment—his name, and with it his identity, erased by the Bastille's walls. [[Sydney-Carton]] dies under Darnay's name, and in doing so, transforms both his own identity and Darnay's fate.

Dickens uses names to explore the relationship between self and society. A name can be an inheritance one cannot escape (Evrémonde), a legal category that carries a death sentence ("emigrant"), or a gift of love (Carton's name given to Lucie's child). Throughout the novel, the act of naming—and unnaming—is a locus of moral and political struggle.

> "Emigrants have no rights, Evrémonde." (036-035)

The officer's words reduce Darnay to a single identity—Evrémonde, emigrant—and with it, to a single fate: death. The legal power of naming is absolute, and this moment crystallises the novel's central insight: to be named by the state is to be at the state's mercy.

## Manifestations

### Charles Darnay: The Renounced Name

Charles Darnay's defining act occurs before the novel begins: he renounces the name Evrémonde and the aristocratic inheritance that accompanies it. "He had voluntarily relinquished a title that was distasteful to him" (041-007). This renunciation is both moral and practical—he earns his living as a tutor rather than living off peasant labour. But the novel is unsentimental about the power of renunciation: when Darnay returns to France, he is arrested as "The Emigrant Evrémonde," his chosen name irrelevant before revolutionary law.

> ...he had voluntarily relinquished a title that was distasteful to him, and a station that was distasteful to him, and had left his country... to live by his own industry in England. (041-007)

Darnay's self-renaming is an act of moral reconstruction. He does not simply flee France; he remakes himself as a different kind of man—one who works rather than exploits. The repetition of "distasteful" emphasises that Darnay's renunciation is a matter of moral taste, not political calculation.

### Doctor Manette: The Forgotten Name

Doctor Manette's identity is systematically destroyed by his imprisonment. He is known only by his cell number—"One Hundred and Five, North Tower"—and, upon release, answers only to that designation. The recovery of his name is coterminous with the recovery of his self: as [[Lucie-Manette]]'s care draws him back from catatonia, he reclaims his identity as "Doctor Manette." But the novel is honest about the limits of this recovery; his shoemaking fugues testify that some damage cannot be renamed away.

### Sydney Carton: The Substituted Name

Carton's final act is an act of naming. By taking Darnay's place at the guillotine, he substitutes his name—and his life—for Darnay's. His vision of the future includes a child who bears his name: "I see that child who lay upon her bosom and who bore my name, a man winning his way up in that path of life which once was mine." The name that Carton thought worthless becomes, through sacrifice, "illustrious."

> "I see that child who lay upon her bosom and who bore my name, a man winning his way up in that path of life which once was mine." (045-051)

Carton's prophecy transforms naming from an act of power into an act of love. The child who bears his name is not doomed by inheritance but blessed by sacrifice—a deliberate inversion of the Evrémonde name's toxic legacy.

### Revolutionary Naming and Unnaming

The Revolution deploys naming as a weapon. To call someone "aristocrat" or "emigrant" is to condemn them. [[Madame-Defarge]]'s knitted register is an act of naming: by stitching names into her fabric, she marks individuals for death. The power to name is the power to destroy—a dark mirror of the creative power of naming that Lucie exercises in restoring her father's identity.

> "Truly, a decree for selling the property of emigrants... banishing all emigrants, and condemning all to death who return." (036-033)

The revolutionary decree demonstrates how naming becomes legislative violence. The word "emigrant" is not merely descriptive but performative: it enacts the death sentence that it names. The decree's escalating logic—from property confiscation to banishment to death—mirrors the novel's broader argument about the dangerous power of legal categories.

### The Power of the Unnamed

Some characters resist naming. The "Wood-Sawyer" is known only by his occupation; "[[The-Vengeance]]" is named for the abstract force she embodies; "Jacques" is a collective name that conceals individual identity. These unnamed and collectively-named figures represent the anonymity of the revolutionary crowd—a force that derives power from its resistance to individual identification.

The contrast between individual naming (Lucie, Darnay, Carton) and collective naming (Jacques, the mob, the Vengeance) maps onto the novel's larger opposition between personal morality and collective violence. To have a name is to be human; to lose it is to become, like Manette in the Bastille, a number—or, like the revolutionary crowd, a force of nature.

## Character Connections

- **[[Charles-Darnay]]** — The novel's central figure of renaming: Evrémonde becomes Darnay, "emigrant" becomes condemned
- **[[Doctor-Manette]]** — A man whose name—and self—was buried in the Bastille and painfully recovered
- **[[Sydney-Carton]]** — His name, worthless in life, becomes illustrious through sacrificial death
- **[[Madame-Defarge]]** — The keeper of names; her knitted register names the doomed
- **[[Jerry-Cruncher]]** — His dual identity as "Resurrection-Man" and Tellson's messenger embodies the novel's preoccupation with double identity
