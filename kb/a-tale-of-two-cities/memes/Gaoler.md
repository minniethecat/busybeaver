# Gaoler

The **gaoler** (an archaic spelling of *jailer*) appears throughout *A Tale of Two Cities* as a figure of institutional authority within the prisons that punctuate the novel's action. From the Bastille to La Force, the gaoler represents the human face of the carceral system — sometimes cruel, sometimes indifferent, occasionally capable of a flicker of recognition or mercy.

## The Bastille Gaoler and Dr. Manette

The gaoler is first invoked through negation. When Mr. Lorry and Lucie visit the Defarges' wine-shop in search of Dr. Manette, Defarge asks the old man a series of questions meant to confirm his identity. "You are not the gaoler's daughter?" (006-059) — the question, directed at Lucie, links the figure of the gaoler to Dr. Manette's eighteen years of imprisonment. The gaoler here is unnamed and unseen — a shadow-figure who held the keys to Manette's cell and who likely witnessed, day after day, the prisoner's descent into shoemaking and silence.

## The Old Bailey Gaolers

In the London scenes, gaolers appear in their official capacity during Charles Darnay's treason trial. As the court settles and the Judge enters, "two gaolers, who had been standing there, went out, and the prisoner was brought in, and put to the bar" (008-039). The gaolers are functionaries — their movements scripted by courtroom procedure, their presence a reminder that the accused has come from a cell and will return to one. Dickens presents them without commentary, allowing their silent efficiency to convey the machinery of justice.

## The Prison Inscriptions: A Gaoler's Legacy

The most significant gaoler in the novel is one who never appears directly. When Defarge shows the liberated Bastille to the crowd, the visitors discover a dungeon wall "covered by inscriptions which had been carved by prisoners — dates, names, complaints, and prayers" (012-084). On a corner stone, one prisoner had carved three letters as his last work before execution. The gaoler who presided over this dungeon is absent from the scene — but his presence is felt in every chiselled word. The inscriptions exist because gaolers permitted them, or because gaolers did not care enough to prevent them. In either case, the gaoler's indifference is the condition of the prisoner's last act of self-assertion.

## The Gaoler as System

Across the novel, the gaoler is less an individual character than an embodiment of the prison system itself. In the Bastille, the gaoler is the ancien régime's jailer — keeper of lettres de cachet and secret prisoners. In La Force during the Terror, the gaoler serves the Revolutionary Tribunal — keeper of the aristocrats and counter-revolutionaries who await the tumbril. The gaoler's key turns for whichever regime holds power; his function remains constant. As Dickens traces the Revolution's descent from liberation to new tyranny, the gaoler's unchanged role becomes a quiet indictment: the prison outlasts every government, and the gaoler's keys pass from one master to the next without a sound.
