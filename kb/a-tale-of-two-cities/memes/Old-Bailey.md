# Old Bailey

## Definition

The Old Bailey, formally the Central Criminal Court of England and Wales, stands on the street of Old Bailey in the City of London, near St. Paul's Cathedral and [[Newgate]] Prison (008-001). In the eighteenth century — [[I. The Period|the period]] of *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*' English scenes — the Old Bailey was the venue for the most serious criminal trials, including cases of high [[Treason|treason]]. Dickens, who worked as a court reporter at the Old Bailey as a young man, knew the courtroom intimately, and his depiction of it in the novel draws on direct observation.

## The Old Bailey as Theatre

Dickens's rendering of the Old Bailey emphasises its character as a theatre of public spectacle. The courtroom is arranged to maximise the visibility of the accused — and the entertainment value of the proceedings:

> The Old Bailey was famous as a kind of deadly inn-yard, from which pale travellers set out continually, in carts and coaches, on a violent passage into the other world (008-023)

Several aspects of Dickens's description underscore the theatricality:

- **The public galleries**: Spectators crowd the galleries to watch trials, much as they would attend a play. The atmosphere is one of eager anticipation rather than sober attention to justice (008-024)
- **The accused as spectacle**: The prisoner is placed in a dock designed to make him visible from every angle — the architecture of the court itself enforces the theatrical function
- **The lawyers as performers**: Barristers like Stryver play to the gallery, their eloquence directed as much at the audience as at the [[Jury|jury]] (011-002)
- **The death sentence**: The most eagerly awaited moment in any trial is the pronouncement of the death penalty — the climax of the performance

The Old Bailey is, in Dickens's satire, a place where the forms of justice are preserved while its substance is subordinated to the appetites of [[The-Crowd|the crowd]].

## Darnay's Trial

[[Charles-Darnay|Charles Darnay]]'s trial for high treason takes place at the Old Bailey in 1780. He is accused of passing English military secrets to the French king — a capital charge. The courtroom is packed with spectators who have come to see a French spy [[Condemned|condemned]] to death:

> They paid to see the play at the Old Bailey, just as they paid to see the play in Bedlam (008-024)

The trial's atmosphere is febrile: the crowd has already convicted Darnay in its collective imagination before a word of evidence has been presented. The Old Bailey's culture of spectacle has pre-judged the prisoner.

## The Identification and Acquittal

The trial turns on a single dramatic moment: [[Sydney-Carton|Sydney Carton]] removes his wig, and the [[Witness|witness]] [[John-Barsad|John Barsad]] is unable to distinguish the prisoner from the barrister's assistant sitting in plain view. The crowd came pouring out after the acquittal (009-133):

> Mr. Carton, who had so long sat looking at the ceiling of the court, here looked at Mr. Stryver and then at the witness. In that moment an extraordinary change came over the witness's face.

The acquittal — when it comes — is almost an anticlimax. The crowd, cheated of its spectacle of death, disperses in disappointment. The Old Bailey has failed to deliver the entertainment it had promised.

## The Old Bailey as Counterpart to the Revolutionary Tribunal

Dickens structures the novel so that the Old Bailey (London) and [[The Revolutionary Tribunal|the Revolutionary Tribunal]] (Paris) mirror each other:

| Feature | Old Bailey | Revolutionary Tribunal |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| Purpose | Try serious crimes, including treason | Try enemies of the [[Republic]] |
| Audience | London public in galleries (008-024) | Tricoteuses and revolutionary crowd |
| Spectacle | The condemned as public entertainment | The condemned as revolutionary ritual |
| Vulnerability | Corrupt witnesses (Barsad, Cly) | Denunciation replacing evidence |
| Outcome in Darnay's case | Acquittal (lucky escape) (009-133) | Condemnation (doomed by Manette's letter) |

Both courts are shown to be flawed institutions in which the forms of justice are vulnerable to manipulation — by perjured witnesses in London, by revolutionary ideology in Paris. Darnay's contrasting fates in the two courts suggest that justice is contingent on context rather than absolute: the same man can be acquitted or condemned depending on which side of the Channel he stands.

## The Spy in the Dock

A detail Dickens draws from his own experience as a court reporter is the presence of pickpockets and thieves in the courtroom galleries — criminals who attend trials to study the technique of their fellows or to pick the pockets of spectators absorbed in the proceedings. The Old Bailey is, in this sense, not merely a theatre but a marketplace — a venue in which the boundaries between law, crime, and entertainment are thoroughly porous.

## Historical Note

The Old Bailey Sessions House that Dickens knew was rebuilt in 1774 and demolished in 1902. The present Old Bailey building, with its iconic dome and statue of Justice, opened in 1907. In Dickens's time, trials were held in open courtrooms that were often so cold in winter that judges, barristers, and jurors wore heavy coats and gloves. The proximity of Newgate Prison — where condemned prisoners were held — to the Old Bailey meant that the journey from sentencing to execution was, literally, a walk of a few hundred yards.
