## Overview

Treason — the crime of betraying one's sovereign or nation — is the legal charge that bookends Charles Darnay's story in *A Tale of Two Cities*. He is tried for treason twice: once in England, accused of passing state secrets to the French; and once in Revolutionary France, where his aristocratic birth is deemed a capital offence against the Republic.

## In the Novel

At Darnay's Old Bailey trial in London, the prosecution alleges "treasonable practices" in his communications with the French (009-001). The charge of treason is formally laid against him, though the Attorney-General's case collapses when Sydney Carton's resemblance to the prisoner undermines the identification evidence (009-100). Public interest in the treason trial is intense, and the proceedings roll over into wider notoriety (012-001). The word reappears in Revolutionary France, where helping an aristocrat is declared "treason against the majesty of the people" — a charge that threatens even those who show ordinary human compassion (030-057).

## Significance

Treason is the legal instrument through which the novel explores its central theme of loyalty and betrayal. In England, the charge is a tool of state security; in France, it becomes a weapon of revolutionary ideology. Darnay's two treason trials mirror each other across the Channel, each reflecting the political paranoia of its respective regime. The charge of treason, in both contexts, reveals how justice can be bent by the fears of the age.
