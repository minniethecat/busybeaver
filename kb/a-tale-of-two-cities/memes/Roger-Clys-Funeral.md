# Roger Cly's Funeral

## Overview

The funeral of Roger Cly, depicted in Book 2, Chapter 14 of *A Tale of Two Cities*, is one of the novel's most darkly comic set-pieces. Cly, a professional spy who testified against Charles Darnay at the [[Old Bailey]] treason trial, is reported dead. His funeral becomes a public spectacle in London, with a vast crowd assembling to hurl abuse at the coffin of a man they despise as an informer (020-004). The event is eventually revealed to have been an elaborate sham: Cly is not dead at all, and his coffin contains only paving-stones — a deception engineered to allow the spy to escape to France, where he will reappear as a turnkey during the revolutionary trials.

## The Funeral Procession

Dickens renders the procession with the grotesque energy characteristic of his crowd scenes. The populace of London turns out not to mourn but to execrate:

> The populace — moved by an extraordinary unanimity of sentiment — had resolved to see the body of the spy Roger Cly to its last home... (020-004, paraphrased)

The crowd is described as a single organism with a single purpose: the expression of collective hatred for a man who has betrayed his countrymen (020-012). The scene is London's answer to the revolutionary crowds of Saint Antoine — a display of mob fury that differs from its French counterpart in its object (a single despised spy rather than an entire class) and its outcome (stone-throwing rather than guillotining) but shares its fundamental character:

- **The anonymity of the crowd**: Individual faces are lost in the mass
- **The performative cruelty**: The crowd chants, jeers, and throws objects at the coffin
- **The carnival atmosphere**: What should be sombre becomes festive and violent

## The Riot

The funeral degenerates into a riot as the crowd's aggression escalates beyond verbal abuse:

> The crowd came pouring out at the gate at which the hearse had entered, and among them was Jerry Cruncher — a flag in one hand and a speaking-trumpet in the other — with the mob at his heels. (020-012, paraphrased)

Jerry Cruncher, the odd-job man and body-snatcher from Tellson's Bank, participates enthusiastically in the riot. His presence at the funeral foreshadows the later revelation about the coffin's contents: Cruncher, who supplements his income by digging up corpses and selling them to medical schools, is the one character in a position to know whether a grave truly contains a body.

The riot is put down by the authorities, but the funeral has achieved its purpose — the spectacle of the mob's hatred has been sufficiently convincing that no one questions whether Cly is actually dead.

## The Coffin's Secret

The truth about Cly's funeral is revealed only in Book 3, when Jerry Cruncher, now in Paris with the Tellson's party, confesses his grave-robbing activities. He admits to having dug up Cly's grave and discovered that the coffin contained only paving-stones and earth:

> "You'd be surprised to know what that there gentleman's coffin was filled with... Paving-stones and earth." (Book 3, Chapter 9)

The revelation has multiple implications:

1. **Cly is alive**: The spy faked his own death to escape England and resume his career in revolutionary France
2. **The funeral was theatre**: The entire public spectacle — the procession, the riot, the burial — was a performance staged for an audience that wanted to believe in the spy's death (020-017)
3. **Cruncher's secret is exposed**: The confession that enables this discovery also reveals Cruncher's grave-robbing to his wife and employer

## The Spy Network

Cly's fake funeral connects to the novel's broader depiction of espionage as a shadow economy that spans both England and France. The spies Barsad and Cly operate as a pair — both testify against Darnay at the Old Bailey, both serve whichever government pays best, and both end up in Paris during the Terror:

| Spy | English Identity | French Identity | Fate |
|-----|-----------------|-----------------|------|
| John Barsad | Crown witness | [[Turnkey]] at the [[Conciergerie]] | Blackmailed by Carton into facilitating Darnay's escape |
| Roger Cly | Supposedly dead (020-017) | Barsad's fellow turnkey | Exposed as alive by Cruncher's confession |

The revelation that Cly faked his death also retrospectively explains Barsad's cooperation with Carton: Barsad knows that his own position in Paris is as precarious as Cly's, and that exposure as a double agent would mean instant death at the guillotine.

## The Funeral as Social Commentary

The Cly funeral scene serves Dickens's satirical purpose by demonstrating that English mobs are as susceptible to manipulated spectacle as French ones (020-012). The London crowd that riots at Cly's funeral is not morally superior to the Paris crowd that storms the Bastille — it is merely less deadly. The scene also exposes the porous boundary between official justice and popular violence: the mob acts as an unofficial enforcer of the community's judgment, much as the revolutionary crowds in France act as an unofficial arm of the tribunal.

## Significance

Roger Cly's funeral is a minor event in the novel's plot structure but a significant one in its thematic economy. It establishes the unreliability of appearances — a coffin is not a body, a funeral is not a death — in a novel fundamentally concerned with the gap between surface and substance. It also connects the English and French strands of the narrative through the figure of the double agent, suggesting that the "two cities" of the title are linked not only by commerce and history but also by a shared underworld of espionage, deception, and violence.
