# Citizen

During the French Revolution, the term *Citizen* (*Citoyen* / *Citoyenne*) replaced all traditional forms of address such as *Monsieur*, *Madame*, and *Mademoiselle*. This linguistic transformation was not cosmetic: it encoded the Revolution's central ideological claim — that all persons were equal members of the sovereign nation, bound to one another by civic obligation rather than deference. In *A Tale of Two Cities*, Dickens uses the title "Citizen" as a marker of Revolutionary Paris, where the old social order has been swept away and replaced by a new vocabulary of republican virtue.

## Definition

The revolutionary concept of citizenship was defined in the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen (1789) and codified in the Constitution of 1791. A citizen was a rights-bearing member of the political community, entitled to vote, to hold office, and to the protection of the laws. The mandatory use of the title "Citizen" was decreed by the National Convention in 1792 and enforced by the Revolutionary Committees; failure to use it was a sign of counter-revolutionary sentiment.

## Historical Context

The linguistic revolution reflected the broader assault on hierarchy. The old honorifics — *Monsieur* (my lord), *Madame* (my lady) — implied a chain of dependence and subordination. "Citizen," by contrast, implied horizontal solidarity. However, as the Revolution radicalized, the term became a test of loyalty: to address someone by the old title was to expose oneself as a suspect. The same word that signified inclusion became a gatekeeping mechanism, distinguishing the virtuous patriot from the hidden aristocrat.

## Role in the Novel

Dickens introduces "Citizen" as a pervasive marker of the Revolutionary milieu in Book the Third. [[Monsieur-Defarge|Defarge]] is addressed as "Citizen Defarge" by the functionaries who process [[Charles-Darnay]]'s arrest (031-044), and the phrase recurs throughout the trial scenes. The insistence on the title signals a world in which all traditional forms have been inverted: the wine-shop keeper is now the authority, and the aristocrat is the prisoner.

The term also carries an ironic charge. The Revolutionary patriots who call themselves "Citizens" pride themselves on their virtue, yet the novel shows them as participants in — or acquiescent spectators to — judicial murder. The gap between the egalitarian ideal of "Citizen" and the brutal reality of the [[Guillotine]] is one of Dickens's most powerful critiques of the Revolution's descent from justice into tyranny.

"Citizen Defarge," [[John-Barsad|Barsad]] says, as if invoking the title is a password, a charm against suspicion (033-029). But the title protects no one — not even the Defarges would be safe from its withdrawal.

## Related Terms

- **Patriot**: A partisan of the Revolution, often used interchangeably with "good citizen"
- **[[Monsieur-Defarge]]**: The Revolutionary wine-shop keeper, addressed as "Citizen"
- **[[Madame-Defarge]]**: "Citizeness" whose knitting encodes the names of those to be executed
- **Aristocrat**: The antithesis of "Citizen" in Revolutionary rhetoric
