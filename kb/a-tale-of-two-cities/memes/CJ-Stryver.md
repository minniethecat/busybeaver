# C.J. Stryver

**C.J. Stryver** is a successful London barrister in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*, whose courtroom triumphs are built entirely on the unacknowledged labor of [[Sydney-Carton]]. Ambitious, florid, and intellectually mediocre, Stryver regards his exploitation of Carton as the natural order of things. His failed courtship of [[Lucie-Manette]] — which he approaches as though it were a legal brief to be won — reveals the emotional poverty beneath his professional prosperity.

## The Lion and the Jackal

Dickens introduces Stryver through the extended metaphor that defines his character:

> "Sydney Carton, idlest and most unpromising of men, was Stryver's great ally. What the two drank together, between Hilary Term and Michaelmas, might have floated a king's ship." (011-004)

The metaphor is precise: Stryver is the **lion** who claims the kill in court; Carton is the **jackal** who does the actual work of legal research, fact-gathering, and argument construction. Stryver presents the arguments that Carton devises; Carton, drunk and self-loathing, works through the night while Stryver sleeps. The arrangement perfectly suits both men — Stryver because he reaps the rewards, Carton because he believes he deserves nothing better.

## The Darnay Trial

Stryver's most notable professional moment is his defense of Charles Darnay at the Old Bailey treason trial. It is Carton who notices and points out the physical resemblance between Darnay and himself — the observation that destroys the prosecution's case by creating reasonable doubt about witness identification. Stryver takes full credit for the acquittal, and the legal world congratulates him on his brilliance.

## Courtship of Lucie Manette

Stryver's decision to marry Lucie Manette is presented as a business calculation. He informs Carton that he has decided to "make an offer" to the young lady, describing the proposition in terms that would not be out of place in a property transaction. When he visits the Manette household to propose, he is gently but firmly discouraged by [[Jarvis-Lorry]], who understands that Lucie would never accept him. Stryver's reaction is not heartbreak but wounded vanity — he cannot conceive that a woman of Lucie's modest circumstances would refuse a man of his professional standing. Within weeks, he has transferred his matrimonial ambitions elsewhere, marrying "a florid widow with property."

## Function in the Novel

Stryver is Dickens's portrait of **bourgeois complacency**. Unlike the aristocratic [[Marquis-St-Evremonde]], he is not cruel; unlike the revolutionary [[Madame-Defarge]], he is not vengeful. He is simply incapable of imagining that the world might be ordered differently than it is, or that his success might depend on anyone else's labor or suffering. His exploitation of Carton is not malicious — it is structural, the unexamined privilege of the "lion" who has never questioned why the "jackal" does all the work.
