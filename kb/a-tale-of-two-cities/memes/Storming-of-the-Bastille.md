# Storming of the Bastille

The **Storming of the Bastille** — depicted in Book the Second, Chapters 21–22 of Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]* — is the novel's explosive pivot from simmering oppression to revolutionary conflagration. On 14 July 1789, the citizens of [[Saint-Antoine]], led by the wine-shop keeper Monsieur Defarge, surge against the medieval fortress-prison that stands as the ultimate emblem of Bourbon tyranny. Dickens renders the event not as a dry historical chronicle but as a seething, oceanic convulsion — the long-suppressed fury of a people finally unleashed.

## Historical Context

The Bastille was a fourteenth-century fortress converted into a state prison, notorious for holding prisoners detained by *lettres de cachet* — sealed royal orders requiring no judicial process. Though by 1789 it housed only seven prisoners, its symbolic weight was immense. Dickens establishes this symbolism early in the novel, describing the Saint Antoine district as a place where "the heavy lands adjacent to Paris" sheltered "from the farmers-general and the farmers of the public lands" the seed-beds of future revolution (001-004).

By mid-1789, Saint Antoine is "a narrow street in the suburb of Saint Antoine, in Paris, where it was spilled" (005-004) — a cauldron of hunger, wine-stained cobblestones, and whispered conspiracy. The Defarges, husband and wife, have been organizing secretly for years, their wine shop serving as revolutionary headquarters.

## The Assault

Dickens stages the storming as a tidal event, the crowd moving with the elemental force of the sea:

> "Come, then!" cried Defarge, in a resounding voice. "Patriots and friends, we are ready! The Bastille!" (027-037)

The mob surges toward the fortress "with the fierce pat of the sea itself" (027-038). Dickens' oceanic imagery is deliberate: the revolution is a force of nature, neither entirely rational nor entirely irrational, but inexorable once set in motion.

Defarge forces his way into the inner courtyard and demands to be taken to "One Hundred and Five, North Tower" — the cell where [[Doctor-Manette]] had been buried alive for eighteen years. His search of the cell is methodical: he examines the walls for markings, finds the initials "A. M." scratched into the stone, and retrieves something concealed within the chimney — the hidden manuscript that will later condemn [[Charles-Darnay]] to death.

The governor is killed, his head paraded on a pike. Madame Defarge — "a tigress" in Dickens' rendering — decapitates the corpse with her own knife, an act of personal vengeance that prefigures the institutionalized violence of the Terror.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The fall of the Bastille is simultaneously liberation and damnation. It breaks the old regime's grip on power and heralds the possibility of justice. But it also inaugurates a cycle of retributive violence that will consume the innocent alongside the guilty. The same mob that tears down the symbol of tyranny will, in time, demand the head of Charles Darnay — a man who renounced everything the Bastille stood for.

The Defarges' roles are emblematic of this ambiguity. Monsieur Defarge's search of Cell 105 is motivated by loyalty to his former master; yet the evidence he uncovers there — Dr. Manette's written denunciation of the Evrémonde family — becomes the instrument of Darnay's condemnation. The past, once unearthed, cannot be reburied.

## In the Novel's Structure

The storming of the Bastille is the hinge between the novel's two worlds — the "two cities" of the title. Before this event, London is the primary stage and the plot moves at the pace of domestic drama. After it, Paris becomes the gravitational center, and the pace accelerates toward tragedy. The Bastille chapters mark the point where history ceases to be background and becomes an active, destroying force in the lives of the characters.
