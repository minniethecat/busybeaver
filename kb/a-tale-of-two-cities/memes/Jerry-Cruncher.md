# Jerry Cruncher

**Jerry Cruncher** is the vigorous and disreputable messenger of [[Tellsons-Bank|Tellson's Bank]] in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. By day, he runs errands between the bank's London premises and the courts at the Old Bailey; by night, he practices the illegal trade of "fishing" — body-snatching from graveyards to supply the medical schools. Uneducated, superstitious, and comically abusive toward his wife, Cruncher nonetheless embodies a rough vitality that cuts through the novel's more somber passages. His arc from grave-robber to would-be grave-digger — volunteering to accompany the Paris expedition in Book the Third — signals a genuine, if limited, moral growth. As a working-class Londoner navigating the edges of the law, Cruncher provides Dickens with a vehicle for dark comedy, social commentary on the medical economy of the era, and an oblique reflection of the novel's central [[Resurrection]] motif in its most literal and disreputable form.

## Appearance and Domestic Life

Dickens introduces Cruncher in his Hanging-sword-alley lodgings, where he shares cramped quarters with his wife and young son. The domestic scene is one of Dickens' most sustained comic performances:

> Mr. Cruncher reposed under a patchwork counterpane, like a Harlequin at home. (007-009)

His relationship with his wife is defined by his conviction that she is "flopping" (praying) against him — every time things go wrong, he blames her religious devotions. His son, Young Jerry, serves as a miniature double, observing his father's mysterious nocturnal activities with a child's mixture of awe and incomprehension. The family's poverty, the wife's silent endurance, and the father's blustering tyranny sketch a working-class London household in miniature.

Cruncher's physical description emphasizes his roughness: he has "spiky hair" that "looked as if it were made of iron" (007-006), and his hands are "always rusty" (007-007) from his nocturnal work. His habitual truculence extends to everyone around him, including his own boot, which he berates for its poor condition as though it had conspired against him.

## Background

Cruncher's life is defined by the gap between his daytime respectability and his nighttime criminality. He is employed as the outside messenger of Tellson's Bank — an institution so old-fashioned and conservative that keeping a man like Cruncher on staff is one of its few concessions to irregularity. The bank tolerates his rough edges because he is effective: he delivers messages reliably, knows the streets of London intimately, and asks no questions about the contents of the communications he carries.

His nocturnal trade as a "resurrection man" places him in a shadow economy that supplied the anatomy schools of eighteenth- and nineteenth-century London. The demand for cadavers for medical dissection far outstripped the legal supply (which was limited to the bodies of executed criminals), creating a thriving black market in which men like Cruncher dug up freshly buried bodies and sold them to surgeons. Dickens treats this trade with grim humor, but the historical reality was brutal and dangerous.

## "Fishing" — The Resurrection Man

Cruncher's night trade supplies the novel's most macabre comedy. He is a "resurrection man" — a grave-robber who digs up freshly buried bodies and sells them to surgeons for anatomical study. Dickens treats this with characteristic irony:

> On this post of his, Mr. Cruncher was as well known to Fleet-street and the Temple as the Bar itself. (007-029)

The grave-robbing expeditions — always referred to euphemistically as "fishing" — serve a double function in the novel. They provide comic relief through Cruncher's terror of being caught and his attempts to conceal the trade from his son. But they also establish the novel's master trope of **[[Resurrection]]** in its most literal and disreputable form. Cruncher's "recalled to life" is physical, clandestine, and mercenary — the shadow side of the spiritual resurrections experienced by [[Doctor-Manette]] and [[Sydney-Carton]].

The euphemism "fishing" creates a sustained comic irony: Cruncher goes out at night with a sack, a crowbar, and a rope, accompanied by his young son, to "fish" — but the catch is not what anyone suspects. His terror at hearing footsteps in a graveyard, his running battles with rusted coffin nails, and his determination that his wife's prayers are the cause of every failed expedition combine to produce some of the novel's darkest and funniest passages.

## The Tellson's Messenger

Cruncher's day job at Tellson's Bank, located in the shadow of Temple Bar, makes him a witness to — and occasional participant in — the novel's major London events. He is present at Darnay's Old Bailey trial, where he squeezes into court:

> They got a little way, and allowed Mr. Jerry Cruncher to squeeze himself into court. (008-025)

His rough commentary on the proceedings — he finds the legal spectacle baffling and the lawyers contemptible — provides a ground-level perspective on the novel's institutions. He also delivers the crucial message that summons [[Jarvis-Lorry]] to Paris in Book the Third, volunteering himself for the hazardous journey:

> "I'm a going," said Jerry, "to your lot, Mr. Lorry, if you'll have me. I'm a poor man, but I've got a spirit." (020-023, adapted)

## Moral Awakening

Cruncher undergoes a modest but significant transformation in the novel's final act. In Paris, confronted with the revolutionary mob and the guillotine — a different order of "resurrection" altogether — he begins to reconsider his nocturnal trade. His resolution to give up grave-robbing, prompted partly by superstitious terror and partly by genuine moral reflection, is one of the novel's small redemptions.

The turning point comes when Cruncher witnesses the fate of [[Roger-Cly]], a spy whose staged funeral he himself had desecrated. The discovery that Cly faked his death and that Cruncher's "fishing" was in vain forces a reckoning. Cruncher's promise to reform is delivered in his own idiom: "I won't do it no more" (037-005). Dickens signals this change through the character's treatment of his wife, too — the "Aggerawayter" becomes worthy of something better than abuse, and Cruncher himself becomes something closer to the "honest tradesman" he has always claimed to be.

## Symbolism and Themes

Cruncher is the novel's embodiment of [[Resurrection]] in its lowest register. Where [[Doctor-Manette]] is "recalled to life" spiritually, and [[Sydney-Carton]] achieves resurrection through sacrifice, Cruncher's version is physical, illicit, and absurd. Yet Dickens treats it with moral seriousness beneath the comedy: the society that taught Cruncher that dead bodies are commodities has laid the groundwork for the Revolution's casual disposal of the living.

Cruncher also functions as a commentary on [[Class-and-Oppression]] from below: he is an oppressor on a small scale (his wife) and an exploited worker on a large scale (the economy that forces him into grave-robbing). His eventual decision to give up "fishing" aligns him, in a minor key, with the novel's broader movement from exploitation toward moral responsibility.

## Verbal Tics

Cruncher's speech is distinguished by several comic distortions that Dickens uses to signal his uneducated but vigorous character:

- **"Aggerawation"** — his rendering of "aggravation," used to describe any inconvenience
- **"Flopping"** — his term for his wife's praying, which he regards as a personal curse upon his enterprises
- **"Aggerawayter"** — his nickname for his wife, from "aggravator"

These mannerisms, sustained across the novel, make Cruncher one of Dickens' most memorable minor characters and provide a running verbal counterpoint to the novel's elevated register.

## Role in the Story

Jerry Cruncher operates as the novel's primary source of comic relief in the London chapters, but his narrative function extends beyond humor. He serves as a witness and occasional participant in the novel's key London events — the Old Bailey trial, the Dover mail, the Paris expedition — providing a working-class perspective on the institutions and characters that dominate the plot. His grave-robbing trade literalizes the [[Resurrection]] motif, grounding the novel's spiritual theme in the dirt and danger of London's nocturnal economy. His modest moral reformation in Book the Third, prompted by his exposure to revolutionary violence, mirrors the novel's broader movement from exploitation toward responsibility. Dickens uses Cruncher to demonstrate that moral growth is possible at every social level, even for a man whose primary virtues are vitality and persistence.
