## Overview

The image of being "buried alive" recurs throughout *A Tale of Two Cities* with the persistence of a nightmare. Dr. Manette's eighteen-year imprisonment in the Bastille is described as live burial; Jerry Cruncher's nocturnal profession as a "Resurrection-Man" literalizes the theme as dark comedy; and Sydney Carton's famous final words imagine his execution as a form of burial that leads to resurrection. Dickens uses the motif to explore the relationship between trauma, memory, and the possibility of renewal.

The burial theme is the dark twin of the novel's resurrection motif. If resurrection represents hope and restoration, burial represents the condition from which one must be "recalled to life." The novel is populated by characters who are, in various ways, buried: in prisons, in secrets, in old identities, in the past. The drama of the plot concerns who can be dug out and who must remain entombed.

## Manifestations

### Dr. Manette: Buried Alive

Dr. Manette's imprisonment in the Bastille is the novel's central case of live burial. He is "buried" for eighteen years, during which time he ceases to be a person and becomes a number—"One Hundred and Five, North Tower." When he is released, he does not emerge into the world but into the psychological prison of his shoemaking fugue. His "recall to life" is a slow excavation, requiring years of Lucie's patient care, from the grave of his trauma.

### Jerry Cruncher: Grave Robber

Jerry Cruncher's profession as a "Resurrection-Man" provides the novel's grotesque comic treatment of burial and exhumation. He digs up freshly buried bodies to sell to medical schools, calling it "fishing." His son's gradual discovery of this secret profession—and his wife's prayers against "flopping"—create a domestic subplot that mirrors the novel's larger themes. Cruncher's eventual moral reformation (promising to be a "grave-digger" rather than a grave-robber) offers a small-scale version of the novel's resurrection pattern.

### Carton's Burial and Rebirth

Sydney Carton's final vision explicitly invokes burial and resurrection: "I see the lives for which I lay down my life, peaceful, useful, prosperous and happy." His death is a burial that produces new life for those he loves. The famous closing lines—"It is a far, far better thing that I do"—transform execution into sacrifice and burial into transcendence.

### The Bastille as Tomb

The Bastille itself functions as a vast tomb at the novel's imaginative center. Its destruction during the Revolution opens the grave and releases its secrets—including Dr. Manette's hidden manuscript. But the novel is ambivalent about what exhumation produces: the discovered document condemns Darnay, suggesting that some buried things should perhaps remain buried.

## Key Passages

> …it would have carried me to death I hope it would have sustained me (015-034)

Darnay's reflection on duty and death connects the burial theme to the novel's stoic ethic: the willingness to be "buried" for a cause, sustained by the hope that the burial has meaning.

## Character Connections

- **Dr. Manette** — The novel's central buried man; his excavation from trauma is the plot's emotional arc
- **Jerry Cruncher** — The literal grave-robber whose profession darkly parodies the resurrection theme
- **Sydney Carton** — He buries his wasted self to give birth to a redeemed memory
- **Madame Defarge** — She keeps her family's memory "buried" in her knitting, letting it mature until the moment of exhumation and revenge

## Symbolic Elements

- **The Bastille** — The great tomb from which Dr. Manette is exhumed
- **The shoemaking bench** — The portable tomb of Manette's trauma
- **Cruncher's tools** — The implements of exhumation, both grotesque and redemptive
- **Carton's final vision** — Burial transformed into prophecy
