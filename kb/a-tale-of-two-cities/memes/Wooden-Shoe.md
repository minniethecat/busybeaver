# Wooden Shoe

The **wooden shoe** (*sabot* in French) appears in *A Tale of Two Cities* as a persistent emblem of the poverty that defined pre-revolutionary French peasant life. Worn by the poorest inhabitants of Saint Antoine and the countryside, the wooden shoe marks its wearers as those who have nothing — and therefore have nothing to lose in the coming revolution.

## In the Streets of Saint Antoine

The wooden shoe first appears during the wine-cask scene in Saint Antoine, where the spilled wine stains "many naked feet, and many wooden shoes" (005-004). The detail places the wooden shoe alongside bare feet — a gradation of poverty in which the sabot represents the barest minimum of protection against the rough, irregular stones of the Paris streets. The wine — red as blood — soaks into the wood of the shoes as it soaks into the hands, faces, and feet of the people.

## The Mender of Roads and the Bound Prisoner

In Book the Second, the wooden shoe becomes linked to the figure of the mender of roads — the rural peasant whose perspective on the coming revolution Dickens develops across multiple chapters. When a prisoner is marched through the village, the mender of roads observes: "his wooden shoes are large and clumsy, and he is lame" (021-039). The wooden shoes here are not merely a mark of poverty but an instrument of suffering — ill-fitting, they contribute to the prisoner's physical degradation.

The mender of roads himself wears wooden shoes. As he works solitary in the dust, he sees approaching figures — the same rough company that will later appear at the storming of the Bastille (029-005). His wooden shoes root him in the landscape of deprivation from which revolutionary violence will spring.

## The Wayfarer's Rest

In a detail that captures both the material hardship and the casual acceptance of peasant life, a wayfarer "slipped off his great wooden shoes, and lay down on his back on the heap of stones" (029-030). The wooden shoes are large enough to be slipped off, heavy enough to merit the adjective "great," and worn by a man who makes his bed on a pile of stones. The image distills the condition of the French peasantry that Dickens sought to convey: a life so stripped of comfort that wooden shoes and a stone heap constitute rest.

## Dickens's Use of the Wooden Shoe

Dickens employs the wooden shoe as what might be called a documentary detail — a small, concrete object that carries the weight of social observation. The *sabot* was historically associated with the French peasantry; the word *sabotage* itself may derive from the practice of striking workers throwing their wooden shoes into machinery. By weaving the wooden shoe through the Saint Antoine scenes and the mender of roads sequence, Dickens gives the reader a tactile sense of the poverty that the novel's opening chapter names as one of the conditions of the age: "it was the age of foolishness, it was the epoch of incredulity."

The wooden shoe is also a quiet counterpoint to the aristocratic footwear of Monseigneur's world — the polished boots of the Marquis, the delicate shoes of the Parisian salon. Where the aristocrat's shoe strikes down a child and continues on its way, the peasant's wooden shoe stumbles, wears, and marks its owner as a "provincial slave" (021-079).
