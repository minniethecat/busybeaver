# Garret

A *garret* is a habitable attic or top-floor room, typically the cheapest and poorest lodging in a building. In *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*, the garret in the Defarges' wine-shop in [[Saint-Antoine|Saint Antoine]] is one of the novel's most resonant spaces — the room where Dr. Manette spent his first days of freedom after eighteen years in [[The-Bastille|the Bastille]], and the image to which he returns in his moments of relapse.

## Definition

The garret was historically the lodging of the poorest urban dwellers — servants, laborers, and the indigent — occupying the topmost floor of a building, farthest from the street and subject to extremes of heat and cold. Its literary associations, which Dickens exploits fully, are with poverty, obscurity, madness, and creative isolation.

## Dr. Manette's Garret

The novel introduces the garret in [[Book-the-First|Book the First]], when Mr. Lorry and [[Lucie-Manette|Lucie Manette]] climb the narrow stairs of the Defarges' wine-shop to find Dr. Manette. The room "was dim and dark: for, the window of dormer shape was in truth a door in the roof" (005-079). It had been "built to be a depository for firewood and the like" — a space not meant for human habitation.

Within this cramped space, Dr. Manette sits at his shoemaker's bench, bent over his work, oblivious to the world. "A broad ray of light fell into the garret, and showed the workman with an unfinished shoe upon his lap, pausing in his labour" (006-013). The image is one of the novel's most powerful: the brilliant physician reduced to mechanical labor in a near-lightless room, his mind preserved only by the repetitive work that both sustains and traps him.

The garret's [[XII. Darkness|darkness]] mirrors Dr. Manette's psychological state. He has been "recalled to life" from the Bastille, but life in the garret is barely distinguishable from the prison cell. When Lucie's face and voice begin to draw him back toward sanity, it is symbolically in this poorest of rooms that the recovery begins.

## The Garret as Recurring Image

Dickens returns to the garret imagery at key moments. When Dr. Manette suffers a relapse after Lucie's marriage, "Mr. Lorry saw him shrink into the exact figure that he had been in the garret" (042-053) — [[VI. The Shoemaker|the shoemaker]]'s posture returning as the mind retreats. The garret becomes the shorthand for Dr. Manette's trauma: whenever the world is too much, he returns, psychologically, to that dark room and his bench.

Yet the garret is also the site of loyalty and love. Lucie "took the white head to her fresh young bosom in the garret of Saint Antoine" (035-002), and the narrator declares she "had been true to her duties" from that moment forward. The poorest room in Paris becomes the birthplace of the novel's deepest devotion.

## Related Terms

- **Bastille**: The prison from which Dr. Manette was released, the garret's predecessor as his cell
- **Shoemaker**: Dr. Manette's trade in captivity, practiced in both the Bastille and the garret
- **[[Recalled to Life]]**: The novel's central metaphor, first enacted in the garret
