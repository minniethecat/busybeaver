## Overview

Violence in *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]* is not an aberration but a structural feature of the world Dickens depicts. The novel traces violence through three phases: the ancien régime's casual brutality against the poor, the revolutionary crowd's retaliatory fury, and the institutionalised killing of the Terror. Dickens refuses to sentimentalise any of these phases, presenting violence as simultaneously inevitable and dehumanising.

The mob is the novel's instrument of collective violence. Dickens's crowd scenes are among the most powerful in English literature, rendering the transformation of individuals into a single, destructive organism. The wine-cask scene in [[Saint-Antoine]], the Grindstone chapter, and the Carmagnole dance all depict the same process: the dissolution of individual moral agency into collective frenzy.

> The murmuring of many voices, the upturning of many faces, the pressing on of many footsteps in the outskirts of the crowd, so that it swells forward in a mass, like one great heave of water, all flashes away.

The crowd at the guillotine is a force of nature, as indifferent as the sea. The repetition of "many" empties the crowd of individual identity; it is a mass, not a collection of persons. Dickens's metaphor of water—swelling, heaving, flashing—renders the crowd as elemental, beyond moral judgment.

## Manifestations

### The Wine-Cask: Violence as Appetite

The novel's first major crowd scene is the bursting of the wine cask outside [[Defarges-Wine-Shop]]. The people of Saint Antoine "suspended their business, or their idleness, to run to the spot and drink the wine" (005-004). A man writes "Blood" on the wall with his wine-dipped finger. The scene establishes the crowd's desperate hunger—for sustenance, for sensation, for vengeance—and prefigures the blood that will flow when the Revolution begins.

The substitution of blood for wine is the novel's governing metaphor for revolutionary violence. What begins as desperate consumption becomes ritualised killing; the thirst that drove peasants to lap wine from the cobblestones will drive them to count heads at the guillotine.

### The Grindstone: Violence as Spectacle

The Grindstone chapter (037-001) is the novel's most concentrated depiction of mob violence. In the courtyard of what was once Monseigneur's townhouse, a grindstone has been set up for sharpening weapons. The scene is a vision of hell: men "more horrible and cruel than the visages of the wildest savages" turn the wheel while women hold wine to their mouths; "what with dropping blood, and what with dropping wine, and what with the stream of sparks struck out of the stone, all their wicked atmosphere seemed gore and fire."

> The eye could not detect one creature in the group free from the smear of blood. Shouldering one another to get next at the sharpening-stone, were men stripped to the waist, with the stain all over their limbs and bodies. (037-001)

The Grindstone passage universalises blood-guilt. No one is clean; everyone is complicit. The stain spreads from weapons to clothes to skin to eyes—violence is not an action but an environment. The men stripped to the waist, their bodies covered in blood, have been transformed from individuals into components of a killing machine.

> As these ruffians turned and turned, their matted locks now flung forward over their eyes, now flung backward over their necks, some women held wine to their mouths that they might drink; and what with dropping blood, and what with dropping wine, and what with the stream of sparks struck out of the stone, all their wicked atmosphere seemed gore and fire. (037-001)

Dickens's prose accelerates to match the frenzy it describes. The sentence's accumulating clauses—"and what with... and what with... and what with"—reproduce the grindstone's relentless turning. Blood, wine, and fire merge into a single element; the distinction between sustenance, weapon, and destruction collapses.

### The Carmagnole: Violence as Dance

The Carmagnole, the revolutionary dance, represents mob violence transformed into ritual. The dancers whirl through the streets in a frenzy that blurs the line between celebration and murder. The dance is both an expression of revolutionary joy and a rehearsal for the guillotine's rhythm. [[Lucie-Manette]], watching from a window, sees "the dreadful spectacle of these madly dancing figures, all bloody and all terrible" (038-001).

### The Marquis's Carriage: Aristocratic Violence

Dickens is careful to show that mob violence has its origins in aristocratic violence. The [[Marquis-St-Evremonde]]'s carriage drives through the streets "as if he were charging an enemy," and "the furious recklessness of the man brought no check into the face, or to the lips, of the master" (017-010). The carriage kills a child, and the Marquis's response—tossing a coin as compensation—epitomises the ancien régime's lethal indifference to common life.

### "Twenty-Three": The Guillotine Crowd

By the novel's end, violence has become routine. The crowd at the guillotine counts heads with mechanical precision: "Twenty-Two... Twenty-Three." The mob that once rioted for wine now assembles daily to watch killing as entertainment. The counting represents the ultimate dehumanisation: death reduced to arithmetic.

> There was no pause, no pity, no peace, no interval of relenting rest. (039-010)

The narrator's description of the guillotine's rhythm captures the mechanisation of violence. The repeated negatives—"no pause, no pity, no peace"—create a rhythm as relentless as the blade itself. Violence has become not merely routine but structural, built into the tempo of revolutionary time.

## Character Connections

- **[[Madame-Defarge]]** — The mob's moral centre; she transforms personal vengeance into collective action
- **[[The-Vengeance]]** — Madame Defarge's lieutenant, a personification of the mob's hunger for retribution
- **[[Marquis-St-Evremonde]]** — Embodiment of aristocratic violence; his murder by Gaspard is the Revolution in miniature
- **[[Sydney-Carton]]** — The one character who steps outside the mob, observing it from within ("only as one of the crowd")
