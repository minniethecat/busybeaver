# Trial of Charles Darnay (1792)

Charles Darnay faces **two trials** in Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]* — one in London that acquits him, and one in Paris that condemns him. The second trial, held before the Revolutionary Tribunal in 1792, is the crux of the novel's final crisis. Despite Darnay's renunciation of his aristocratic heritage and the impassioned testimony of [[Doctor-Manette]], the tribunal sentences him to death by guillotine — a verdict that makes [[Sydney-Carton]]'s substitution both necessary and possible.

## Context

In 1792, revolutionary France is in the grip of the Terror. The monarchy has fallen, the September Massacres have stained the streets of Paris, and the Revolutionary Tribunal dispenses a pitiless form of justice in which class membership is itself evidence of guilt. Darnay, who returned to France to aid an imprisoned former servant, is arrested as an *émigré* and aristocrat — an Evrémonde, the name he renounced years before.

## The Charge

Darnay is denounced not by one accuser but by three: Monsieur Defarge, Madame Defarge, and — most devastatingly — [[Doctor-Manette]] himself, through the written testimony he composed during his eighteen-year imprisonment in the Bastille. The manuscript, hidden in the chimney of Cell 105, North Tower, and recovered by Defarge during the [[Storming-of-the-Bastille]], recounts in harrowing detail the crimes of the Evrémonde brothers against a young peasant woman and her family.

The prosecution presents this document as damning evidence. Darnay, though personally innocent, bears the name of the family that committed the atrocities. Under revolutionary law, blood guilt is hereditary — and inescapable.

## The Trial

The setting is stark. The tribunal dispenses with formal legal procedure:

> "He was removed to the strong prison of La Force, and the next day was brought before the Tribunal." (035-044)

Doctor Manette, whose years of suffering at the hands of the Evrémondes have earned him the reverence of the revolutionaries, testifies on Darnay's behalf. His standing is so high that the crowd cheers him and the tribunal seems momentarily swayed. For a brief interval, acquittal appears possible.

Then the prosecution produces the manuscript. The revelation that the man who pleads for Darnay's life is the same man who — in his madness — wrote a curse upon "the last of the race" of Evrémonde is devastating. The contradiction is unresolvable: the Doctor's past self condemns the man his present self seeks to save. The tribunal votes unanimously for death.

## The Verdict and Its Irony

The irony of the verdict is multileveled. Darnay — who rejected his family's name, who lived honestly as a tutor in London, who returned to France only to save an innocent man — is condemned for crimes he did not commit and a heritage he disowned. The Revolution, which promised to judge men by their deeds rather than their birth, judges Darnay by birth alone.

The deeper irony concerns Doctor Manette. His eighteen-year imprisonment was itself a crime of the Evrémondes — an act of tyranny that the Revolution claims to avenge. Yet the written record of that suffering, created in madness and hidden in a prison cell, becomes the instrument of tyranny in the Revolution's hands. The victim's cry for justice is transformed into a weapon against an innocent man.

## Significance

The trial is the structural pivot of the novel's final movement. It transforms Darnay from an active agent (the man who chooses to return to France) into a passive victim (the condemned prisoner awaiting execution). It forces Sydney Carton — who has been watching from the shadows — to act upon the covenant he made with Lucie Manette years earlier. And it completes the thematic circuit: the Revolution, born of just grievance, now dispenses the same arbitrary violence it rose to destroy.

The scaffold that awaits Darnay is the same scaffold that will receive Carton. In substituting himself for the condemned man, Carton dies not only for a friend but as a rebuke to the logic of revolutionary vengeance — a single life freely given in place of another, breaking the endless chain of retribution.
