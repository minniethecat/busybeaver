# Recalled to Life

## Overview

"Recalled to Life" is the phrase that opens the narrative action of *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*. Uttered by [[Jarvis-Lorry|Jarvis Lorry]] in the [[XII. Darkness|darkness]] of the [[Dover]] mail coach as a cryptic message — "RECALLED TO LIFE" — it announces the novel's central theme of [[Resurrection|resurrection]] and sets in motion the plot that will reunite [[Lucie-Manette|Lucie Manette]] with her father, Dr. Alexandre Manette, after eighteen years of wrongful [[Imprisonment|imprisonment]] in [[The-Bastille|the Bastille]]. The phrase recurs throughout the novel, expanding in meaning from Dr. Manette's literal release to encompass the moral and spiritual resurrections of [[Charles-Darnay|Charles Darnay]] and [[Sydney-Carton|Sydney Carton]].

## The Message on the Dover Road

The event is introduced in the opening chapters as the night mail coach travels from London to Dover. Jarvis Lorry, an elderly clerk of Tellson's Bank, receives an urgent dispatch:

> "Wait at Dover for Mam'selle."

Lorry's reply — "RECALLED TO LIFE" — is delivered to the mounted messenger [[Jerry-Cruncher|Jerry Cruncher]], who can make no sense of it:

> "That's a blazing strange message. Recall to life another man out of this world..." mused the puzzled Jerry Cruncher. (002-048)

The phrase is deliberately mysterious, functioning as both a plot device — the reader does not yet know whom it refers to — and a thematic announcement. The novel's action begins not with a physical journey but with an idea: a man who has been dead to the world is to be restored to it.

## Lucie's Journey

At Dover, Lorry meets Lucie Manette, a young woman who has believed her father dead since infancy. He reveals the truth with characteristic delicacy and professional detachment — presenting Dr. Manette's existence as a "business matter" to soften the emotional blow.

Lucie's response transforms the abstract phrase "recalled to life" into a human commitment:

> "I am going to see his Ghost! It will be his Ghost — not him!"

The journey to Paris — through the streets of [[Saint-Antoine|Saint Antoine]] to [[V. The Wine-shop|the wine-shop]] of Ernest Defarge, and finally to the [[Garret|garret]] where Dr. Manette crouches over his shoemaker's bench — is a descent into a form of living death. [[VI. The Shoemaker|The shoemaker]], who has forgotten his own name and answers only to "One Hundred and Five, North Tower," represents what prolonged imprisonment does to a human soul.

## The Reunion

The recognition scene in the garret is the emotional core of the event. Dr. Manette at first shows no awareness of his daughter's presence. But as Lucie's golden hair catches the light, it triggers a buried memory — of his wife's hair, of a life before the Bastille:

> He had sunk to the ground, and she with him, that she might hold him and sustain him. (006-010)

The reunion is not an instant restoration but the beginning of a slow process of recovery — the first step in Manette's being "recalled to life." Dickens is careful to show that resurrection, in the moral sense, is gradual and painful, involving many relapses into the darkness of the shoemaker's bench.

## The Expanding Meaning

The phrase "recalled to life" expands its meaning across the novel:

| Character | How "Recalled" | The Form It Takes |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Dr. Manette | Released from the Bastille | Literal freedom + slow psychological recovery |
| Charles Darnay | Acquitted in his English [[Treason|treason]] trial | Legal salvation, initially anonymous |
| Charles Darnay (second trial) | Spirited out of Paris by Carton | Escape from the [[Guillotine|guillotine]] |
| Sydney Carton | His sacrificial death | Moral resurrection through self-[[Sacrifice|sacrifice]] |

By the novel's end, the phrase has been fully invested with its Christian resonance: Carton's death is a resurrection not of the body but of the soul, and his final words explicitly invoke the language of the Gospel.

## Significance

"Recalled to Life" is the novel's structural and thematic keynote. It establishes the pattern of imprisonment-and-release that governs all the major plotlines; it introduces the idea that identity can be lost and recovered; and it anchors the novel's central moral claim — that even in a world of revolutionary violence and aristocratic cruelty, individual acts of love and sacrifice can recall the dead to life. The phrase's journey from a cryptic message on a dark road to the final words of the novel mirrors the reader's own journey from mystery to understanding.
