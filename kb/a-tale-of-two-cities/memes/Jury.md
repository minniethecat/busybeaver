# Jury

The jury appears at the two great trials of the novel — Charles Darnay's trial
for treason at the Old Bailey, and his later trial before the Revolutionary
Tribunal in Paris. These two juries embody the contrasting legal cultures of
London and revolutionary Paris, yet Dickens suggests a deeper continuity: in
both cities, the jury responds not to evidence but to passion.

## The Old Bailey Jury

Darnay's English trial opens with the ceremonial swearing of the jury. Jerry
Cruncher reports "that the jury were swearing in; and that Mr. Attorney-General
was making a speech" (008-043). The Attorney-General "had to inform the jury,
that the prisoner before them was a young gentleman" — the ritual language of
the English bar masking the deadly stakes of a treason charge (009-001).

The jury becomes a canvas upon which the lawyers paint. Mr. Stryver's skill is
described as fitting the prosecution's case "on the jury, like a compact suit
of clothes," then turning it "inside out" (009-101, 009-102). The jury is not a
body of independent reasoners but a collective sensibility to be dressed and
redressed by rhetoric. When "the jury turned to consider," the spectators
"glanced anxiously at the jury" (009-103, 009-104), and "it began to be
rumoured that the jury would be out a long while" (009-107) — the suspense of
jury deliberation becoming the trial's dramatic engine.

The Old Bailey jury ultimately acquits Darnay, but Dickens makes clear that the
verdict turns on Carton's physical resemblance to the prisoner, not on evidence.
The jury system, for all its procedural dignity, is as susceptible to
appearances as the mob.

## The Revolutionary Jury

The Paris jury in Book the Third is a transformed institution. "Every eye was
turned to the jury. The same determined patriots and good republicans as
yesterday and the day before" (039-101) — this is not a randomly selected body
of peers but a permanent revolutionary panel, "the same determined patriots"
sitting day after day. Among them sits "the craving man on the jury" who
"rubbed his hands together" (039-118) — a juror who anticipates death as a
gourmand anticipates a meal.

The revolutionary jury dispenses with deliberation. "At every juryman's vote,
there was a roar" (040-124) — the jury's verdict is indistinguishable from the
crowd's appetite. Where the Old Bailey jury at least went through the motions of
private consideration, the Revolutionary Tribunal's jury votes publicly, each
man's decision greeted by the mob. Justice has become spectacle, and the jury
has become the audience turned executioner.

## Two Juries, One Truth

Dickens draws no simple contrast between English fairness and French barbarism.
The Old Bailey jury, after all, is prepared to convict an innocent man on the
testimony of a paid spy. Mr. Stryver "argued with the jury on substantial
worldly grounds — the only grounds ever worth taking into account" (018-002)
— a cynical admission that English justice, too, is a matter of performance
rather than truth.

The novel's deepest insight is that the jury — whether English or French,
whether swearing oaths on the Bible or voting to the roar of a mob — is a human
institution, and human institutions are vulnerable to the same forces that drive
the novel's larger drama: fear, prejudice, self-interest, and the hunger for
blood.
