# Guillotine

The guillotine is the mechanized execution device that dominates the second half of *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*, exerting a gravitational pull over every character and plotline from the moment Charles Darnay returns to revolutionary France. Dickens transforms the machine from a mere instrument of death into a fully personified presence — "La Guillotine," a "sharp female newly-born" (031-067) who becomes the dark deity of the Revolution's civic religion. The blade falls with a rhythm that the crowd counts aloud, and the tumbrils roll through the streets of [[Saint-Antoine]] as reliably as the market carts once brought bread. Yet the guillotine is not merely a symbol of terror: in the novel's culminating paradox, the same device that the Revolution designed as an engine of indiscriminate justice becomes, through [[Sydney-Carton]]'s willing substitution, the instrument of personal [[Resurrection]]. Carton's final words — "It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done" (045-051) — reconsecrate the scaffold as an altar of [[Sacrifice]].

## Definition

Invented as a humane method of execution, the guillotine employed a weighted, angled blade dropped from a height to sever the head cleanly from the body. During the French Revolution, it became the official instrument of capital punishment and the visible symbol of the Terror. Dickens calls it "the National Razor which shaved close" (034-012), capturing both its mechanical efficiency and the grim domesticity with which revolutionary Paris regarded it.

## Historical Context

The guillotine was adopted by the French National Assembly in 1792, partly on the recommendation of Dr. Joseph-Ignace Guillotin, who advocated for a more egalitarian and less painful method of execution than the sword or axe — previously reserved for the nobility — or the gallows and breaking wheel used for commoners. The irony that haunted the guillotine from its inception was its transformation from an instrument of egalitarian reform into the engine of the Terror. During the Reign of Terror (1793–1794), it was used to execute thousands, including King Louis XVI, Marie Antoinette, and eventually revolutionary leaders like Danton and Robespierre themselves.

## Role in the Novel

Dickens introduces the guillotine as an omnipresent force in revolutionary Paris through a cascade of darkly comic personifications:

> "It was the popular theme for jests; it was the best cure for the headache, it infallibly prevented the hair from turning grey, it imparted a peculiar delicacy to the complexion, it was the National Razor which shaved close: who kissed La Guillotine, looked through the little window and sneezed into the sack." (034-012)

The passage distills the guillotine's grip on the revolutionary imagination: it is at once a joke, a cosmetic treatment, and an object of perverse devotion. The jests are not incidental — they demonstrate how terror is normalized through the language of everyday life.

The guillotine's shadow lengthens across the entire third book. The tumbrils — the carts that carry the condemned through the streets — become a daily sight in the [[Saint-Antoine]] district, and the wood-sawyer who works near the Conciergerie reenacts executions with his saw, calling himself "the Samson of the firewood guillotine":

> "I call myself the Samson of the firewood guillotine. See here again! Loo, loo, loo; Loo, loo, loo! And off her head comes! Now, a child. Tickle, tickle; Pickle, pickle! And off its head comes. All the family!" (035-026)

This grotesque street performance — a man miming decapitation as entertainment — reveals how completely revolutionary violence has penetrated the fabric of daily life. The wood-sawyer's saw becomes a "Little Guillotine" (035-024), collapsing the distance between labor and execution.

In [[Charles-Darnay]]'s second trial ([[Trial-of-Charles-Darnay]]), the guillotine is the explicit destination for those condemned by the revolutionary tribunal. Defarge invokes "that sharp female newly-born, and called La Guillotine" (031-067) as though appealing to a higher authority. Carton himself, holding leverage over the spy [[John-Barsad|Barsad]], speaks of "a certain Guillotine card" (035-034) — the guillotine has become a currency of power, a threat that settles all arguments.

The blade's rhythmic fall is counted aloud by the crowd at the novel's climax. The knitting women "counted One... Two... Three" as each head fell (035-001), their needles never pausing. The guillotine and [[Madame-Defarge]]'s [[Knitting-and-the-Register|knitting register]] are twin instruments of revolutionary accounting — one keeps the ledger, the other collects the debt.

In the novel's final movement, the guillotine undergoes its symbolic transformation. Carton mounts the scaffold not as a victim but as a willing sacrifice, and the machine that was designed to destroy indiscriminately becomes, in that moment, the instrument through which one man's wasted life acquires transcendent meaning. The guillotine thus embodies the Revolution's double nature: it is the logical endpoint of the ancien regime's brutality (the same violence, now mechanized and democratized) and the paradoxical site of the novel's most redemptive act.
