# Coachman

The **coachman** appears in the opening pages of *A Tale of Two Cities* as the driver of the Dover mail coach — a figure who embodies the hazards, discomforts, and grim determination of eighteenth-century travel. In a novel structured around journeys (from England to France, from life to death, from despair to redemption), the coachman is the first guide — gruff, suspicious, and entirely focused on the task of getting his passengers and horses safely through the night.

## "Sure of Nothing but the Horses"

The coachman's first appearance establishes the atmosphere of mutual suspicion that defines the Dover Mail's journey up Shooter's Hill. "The coachman was sure of nothing but the horses; as to which cattle he could with a clear conscience have taken his oath on the two Testaments that they were not fit for the journey" (002-005). The sentence is pure Dickens — comic in its exaggeration, precise in its social observation. The coachman trusts no one: not the guard, who suspects the passengers; not the passengers, who suspect one another and the guard; and certainly not the horses, whose unfitness he knows with professional certainty.

This web of suspicion is the novel's England in miniature — a society where every person mistrusts every other, bound together only by the shared necessity of the journey. The coachman, at the centre of this arrangement, is neither hero nor villain but a professional doing his job under conditions that require constant vigilance.

## The Voice of Authority

When the coach reaches the crest of Shooter's Hill, the coachman's voice rings out with the authority of one who has coaxed exhausted animals to their limit: "'Wo-ho!' said the coachman. 'So, then! One more pull and you're at the top and be damned to you, for I have had trouble enough to get you to it! — Joe!'" (002-006). The speech is addressed to the horses but meant for the passengers — a performance of competence that reassures even as it complains. The coachman's "Wo-ho" and his curse are the sounds of a man who has done this climb many times before and will do it many times again.

## The Coachman as Threshold Figure

The coachman drives the novel's first journey — the passage from London to Dover that will carry Mr. Lorry toward his meeting with Lucie Manette and the "recalled to life" mission that sets the entire plot in motion. In this sense, the coachman is a threshold figure: he presides over the moment of departure, the crossing from the known world of England into the unknown that awaits across the Channel. He delivers his passengers to the Dover road and disappears from the narrative, having served his function. His brief appearance — a handful of paragraphs at the novel's beginning — is enough to establish the world the characters inhabit: a world of mud, darkness, mistrust, and the grinding labour of keeping things moving.

## Coaching in Dickens's England

For Dickens's original readers, the coachman was a familiar and increasingly nostalgic figure. By 1859, when *A Tale of Two Cities* was published, the railway had largely replaced the stagecoach and mail coach as the primary means of long-distance travel. The coachman belonged to a world that was passing away — a world of horse-drawn speed, highwaymen, and inn-yard departures. Dickens, who was a lifelong enthusiast of coaching and who made several journeys by stagecoach in his youth, invests the coachman with the affection of memory. The figure is not sentimentalised — the coachman is too grumpy and too practical for sentiment — but he is rendered with the vividness that comes from intimate knowledge of the type.
