# Madame Defarge's Death

## Overview

The death of Madame Defarge is the climactic action sequence of Book the Third. Arriving at the Manettes' deserted Paris lodging to denounce Lucie and her child to the Revolutionary Tribunal, Madame Defarge is confronted by Miss Pross — the family's fiercely loyal English servant. In a desperate physical struggle, Miss Pross seizes Madame Defarge and, when the Frenchwoman reaches for the loaded pistol hidden in her bosom, strikes out and discharges the weapon. Madame Defarge dies instantly, killed by her own gun. The confrontation costs Miss Pross her hearing — the pistol shot's concussion permanently deafens her — but it buys the Manette family the time they need to escape Paris.

## Context

The events follow immediately upon Sydney Carton's substitution for Charles Darnay in the [[Conciergerie]]. Carton has drugged Darnay, exchanged clothes with him, and had him carried out to Mr. Lorry's waiting carriage, where Lucie, her father, and her child are waiting to flee Paris. Miss Pross and Jerry Cruncher remain behind to close the lodging and follow separately in a lighter conveyance, a plan designed to minimise the delay at the city barriers (044-039).

## Madame Defarge's Intent

Earlier that day, Madame Defarge has conferred with The [[Vengeance]] and Jacques Three at the wood-sawyer's shed, revealing her plan to exterminate the entire Evrémonde line: "the Evrémonde people are to be exterminated, and the wife and child must follow the husband and father" (044-005). Unlike her husband, who has shown some reluctance toward Dr. Manette, Madame Defarge is implacable: "I cannot trust my husband in this matter... if I delay, there is danger of his giving warning, and then they might escape" (044-009).

Her motivation is rooted in personal history — the Evrémonde brothers were responsible for the deaths of her siblings and the destruction of her family. "It was nothing to her, that an innocent man was to die for the sins of his forefathers; she saw, not him, but them" (044-037).

The narrative underscores Madame Defarge's menace with the image of her concealed weapons:

> Lying hidden in her bosom, was a loaded pistol. Lying hidden at her waist, was a sharpened dagger. (044-038)

## The Arrival

Dickens intercuts between Miss Pross's preparations and Madame Defarge's approach through the Paris streets: "And still Madame Defarge, pursuing her way along the streets, came nearer and nearer" (044-053, repeated at 044-055, 044-059). The suspense builds with each repetition.

Miss Pross, alone in the lodging, is washing her swollen eyes with cold water when she looks up to see a figure — and recoils in shock. "The basin fell to the ground broken, and the water flowed to the feet of Madame Defarge. By strange stern ways, and through much staining blood, those feet had come to meet that water" (044-068).

Madame Defarge demands to see "The wife of Evrémonde" (044-069). Miss Pross immediately grasps the situation: the open doors might suggest flight, so she shuts all four of them and places herself before the door of Lucie's chamber.

## The Confrontation

The two women face each other across a linguistic and cultural divide — each speaking her own language, neither understanding the other's words, yet both "very watchful, and intent to deduce from look and manner, what the unintelligible words meant" (044-076).

Miss Pross declares her defiance in her distinctive English idiom:

> "You might, from your appearance, be the wife of Lucifer," said Miss Pross, in her breathing. "Nevertheless, you shall not get the better of me. I am an Englishwoman." (044-072)

When Madame Defarge realises the rooms are in disorder and suspects the family has fled, she rushes at Lucie's chamber door. Miss Pross seizes her:

> Miss Pross, on the instinct of the moment, seized her round the waist in both her arms, and held her tight. It was in vain for Madame Defarge to struggle and to strike; Miss Pross, with the vigorous tenacity of love, always so much stronger than hate, clasped her tight, and even lifted her from the floor in the struggle that they had. (044-093)

## The Death

Madame Defarge's hands cease striking and reach for her concealed pistol. Miss Pross feels the movement and fights for it: "'It is under my arm,' said Miss Pross, in smothered tones, 'you shall not draw it. I am stronger than you, I bless Heaven for it. I hold you till one or other of us faints or dies!'" (044-094).

The fatal moment is instantaneous:

> Madame Defarge's hands were at her bosom. Miss Pross looked up, saw what it was, struck at it, struck out a flash and a crash, and stood alone — blinded with smoke. (044-095)

> All this was in a second. As the smoke cleared, leaving an awful stillness, it passed out on the air, like the soul of the furious woman whose body lay lifeless on the ground. (044-096)

Madame Defarge dies by her own weapon — the pistol she carried to kill Lucie instead kills her. It is a death thick with poetic justice: the architect of the knitting register is undone by her own violence.

## Aftermath

Miss Pross, in shock, flees the room, then forces herself to return for her bonnet and possessions, locking the door and taking the key. "The marks of gripping fingers were deep in her face, and her hair was torn, and her dress... was clutched and dragged a hundred ways" (044-098). She drops the key into the [[Seine]] from the bridge, eliminating the evidence.

The pistol shot has a permanent consequence: Miss Pross is deafened. When she meets Cruncher at the cathedral, she tells him she hears "nothing" (044-109): "I feel as if there had been a flash and a crash, and that crash was the last thing I should ever hear in this life" (044-108). The narrative confirms this permanence: "And indeed she never did" (044-112).

The death of Madame Defarge is the price the novel exacts for the Manette family's deliverance. It is a victory of love over hatred — as Dickens makes explicit: "the vigorous tenacity of love, always so much stronger than hate" (044-093) — but it is a victory that leaves its mark, on Miss Pross's body and on the reader's sense of the moral cost of survival.
