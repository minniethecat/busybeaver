# Gaspard's Execution

## Overview

The execution of [[Gaspard]] — the [[Peasant|peasant]] who avenged his child's death by murdering the Marquis St. Evrémonde — is a pivotal event in the chain of revolutionary violence in *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*. Captured after a year in hiding, Gaspard is brought back to [[The-Village|the village]] near the Evrémonde [[Chateau|chateau]] and hanged from a gibbet erected above the village [[Fountain|fountain]], where his body is left to rot as a warning to the peasantry. The execution is witnessed by the Defarges, and it transforms [[Madame-Defarge|Madame Defarge]]'s [[XV. Knitting|knitting]] register from a record of private grievances into an instrument of collective revolutionary memory.

## The Capture

After the murder of the Marquis, Gaspard disappears into the countryside. For a year he evades capture, while the authorities of the old régime search for him with increasing urgency — the murder of a nobleman cannot go unpunished without undermining the entire structure of aristocratic power:

> ...the man who had been arrested more than a year ago, and was still enduring his solitary [[Imprisonment|imprisonment]], waiting for his trial — or rather, for his execution, without a trial.

Gaspard's capture is inevitable. The machinery of royal justice, however slow, is relentless, and the peasant cannot hide forever from a state that controls every village, every road, and every informant. He is imprisoned for a year — a year of waiting in a cell while the judicial process grinds toward its predetermined conclusion.

## The Gibbet at the Fountain

The execution site is chosen for maximum symbolic impact. The fountain is the centre of village life — the place where women draw water, where gossip is exchanged, where the rhythms of daily existence converge. By erecting the gibbet above the fountain, the authorities transform the centre of communal life into a theatre of state violence:

> ...a [[Gallows|gallows]] forty feet high, erected at the fountain in the village...

The fountain's water, which should sustain life, flows beneath the body of the hanged man. The image is one of Dickens's most compressed visual metaphors: life and death, sustenance and punishment, the natural and the political, superimposed in a single tableau.

Gaspard's body is left to hang — "poised" forty feet above the ground — until it rots. The purpose is not merely punitive but pedagogical: the peasants must see, every day, what becomes of those who raise a hand against their masters.

## The Defarges as Witnesses

The execution of Gaspard is a turning point in the political education of the Defarges. Ernest and Madame Defarge journey from Paris to the village specifically to [[Witness|witness]] the [[Hanging|hanging]]:

> "Ah, the poor Gaspard!" sighed Defarge. (022-083)

The journey to the execution site is an act of revolutionary solidarity. The Defarges do not know Gaspard personally — he was not a member of their Jacquerie — but they recognise his fate as a common one. Every peasant who resists dies at the hands of the state, and every such death must be remembered, recorded, and avenged.

Madame Defarge's knitting absorbs the spectacle. The hanging body at the fountain becomes another stitch in her register, another name added to the list of the [[Condemned|condemned]]. Her knitting in this scene is not passive observation but active documentation — a counter-archive of revolutionary memory that will outlast the rotted body at the fountain:

> ...and speaking of Gaspard (ah, poor Gaspard! It was cruel, cruel!)... (022-106)

## The Execution as a Miscalculation

The authorities intend Gaspard's execution as a deterrent — a demonstration that the state's power to punish is absolute and inescapable. But the execution produces the opposite of its intended effect. Rather than cowering the peasantry into submission, it deepens their hatred:

| Intended Effect | Actual Effect |
|----------------|---------------|
| Deter peasant violence | Proves that aristocratic power depends on terror |
| Demonstrate state authority | Reveals the state as a mechanism of class [[Vengeance|vengeance]] |
| Erase Gaspard from memory | Makes Gaspard a martyr in the revolutionary register |
| Isolate the act of resistance | Connects Gaspard's murder to a chain of revolutionary acts |

The execution is, in this sense, an instance of the dialectic of revolutionary violence that structures the novel: every act of state repression generates more resistance, which in turn provokes more repression, until the cycle can be broken only by total revolution.

## The Village Fountain as Recurring Image

The fountain that serves as the site of Gaspard's execution recurs throughout the novel in different contexts:

| Appearance | Context |
|------------|---------|
| The chateau fountain | Luxury and aristocratic excess, running unseen and unheard |
| The village fountain | Site of communal life — and state execution |
| The fountain runs dry | Symbol of the old order's exhaustion during the chateau fire |

The fountain thus accumulates meaning through repetition: by the time of the chateau fire, the image of the fountain has absorbed the memory of Gaspard's death, and the water that turns to [[Blood|blood]] in the firelight (015-106) carries the resonance of the executed peasant's body suspended above the water.

## Significance

Gaspard's execution is the moment when the abstract injustice of the old régime becomes, for the revolutionaries of [[Saint-Antoine|Saint Antoine]], a specific and unforgettable image. The body at the fountain is the answer to the question that haunts the novel — why does the Revolution become so violent? — and it is an answer that Dickens, despite his horror at revolutionary excess, refuses to dismiss. The execution of Gaspard is unjust, brutal, and calculated to humiliate; the Revolution's violence, when it comes, does not emerge from nowhere but from a history of such spectacles.
