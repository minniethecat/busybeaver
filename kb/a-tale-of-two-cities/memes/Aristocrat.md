# Aristocrat

In pre-Revolutionary France, an *aristocrat* was a member of the hereditary nobility, a class defined by birthright and legal privilege. During the French Revolution, the term was transformed from a social designation into an accusation. To be called an aristocrat — or, as Dickens renders it in *A Tale of Two Cities*, to have the epithet hurled at one by a Revolutionary crowd — was to be marked for the guillotine.

## Definition

The French aristocracy (*la noblesse*) constituted the Second Estate of the Ancien Régime, below the clergy (First Estate) and above the commoners (Third Estate). Aristocrats enjoyed exclusive privileges, including exemption from certain taxes, the right to bear arms, and access to high offices of state. By 1789, the aristocracy numbered approximately 1–2% of the French population but controlled a disproportionate share of the nation's wealth and political power.

## Historical Context

The abolition of feudal privileges on the night of 4 August 1789 formally ended the legal distinctiveness of the aristocracy, but the social and political stigma attached to noble birth intensified rather than diminished. The Revolutionary Tribunal, established in 1793, treated aristocratic status as presumptive evidence of counter-revolutionary intent. Aristocrats who remained in France faced denunciation, imprisonment, and execution; those who fled were classified as emigrants and condemned in absentia.

## Role in the Novel

The charge of being an aristocrat haunts [[Charles-Darnay]] from the moment he sets foot in Revolutionary France. The functionary at the town gate tells him plainly: "You are an aristocrat, and must have an escort — and must pay for it" (031-009). In the streets of [[Beauvais]], the blacksmith cries "you are a cursed aristocrat!" (031-020), and the crowd takes up the cry.

The accusation follows Darnay to Paris and into the [[Conciergerie]], where his formal indictment reads: "At heart and by descent an Aristocrat, an enemy of the Republic, a notorious oppressor of the People" (040-125). Dickens shows how the legal category becomes a machine for condemning individuals: the mere fact of noble birth, regardless of personal conduct, is sufficient for the death sentence.

The [[Marquis-St-Evremonde]] embodies the pre-Revolutionary aristocrat in his most callous form — the nobleman who rides through the streets indifferent to the children his carriage kills (013-023) and who scorns the common people as "the rats" who "come out of their holes" (015-018). The Marquis is the aristocrat whom the Revolution was ostensibly designed to destroy, but the Revolutionary Tribunal makes no distinction between the cruel Evrémonde uncle and the self-renouncing Evrémonde nephew.

The crowd's cry at the climax — "Down, Evrémonde! To the Guillotine all aristocrats! Down, Evrémonde!" (045-011) — collapses the category into a sentence. In the novel's moral economy, [[Sydney-Carton]]'s sacrifice is all the more remarkable because he dies in place of an aristocrat — the very class the Revolution exists to exterminate.
