# Jarvis Lorry

Mr. Jarvis Lorry is a senior clerk at [[Tellsons-Bank|Tellson's Bank]] in London, and one of the most steadfast and reliable characters in *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. A lifelong bachelor wholly devoted to his business, Lorry nevertheless becomes deeply entangled in the lives of the Manette family, serving as their trusted advisor, protector, and friend across both London and revolutionary Paris.

## Overview

Mr. Lorry is introduced in the novel's second chapter as the mysterious passenger on the Dover mail coach, carrying a cryptic message: "Recalled to Life" (003-015). His defining characteristic is his devotion to Tellson's Bank — "I belong to Tellson's Bank" (002-046) — which he treats almost as a religion. Yet beneath his prim, businesslike exterior lies a deep well of loyalty and compassion that makes him indispensable to the novel's central characters.

Dickens portrays Lorry as a model of English commercial reliability: orderly, methodical, and unfailingly discreet. He measures his life not in personal milestones but in the rhythms of the banking house, and he often retreats into his professional identity when confronted with emotional complexity. Despite this, he repeatedly proves himself capable of genuine warmth and courage when those he cares for are threatened.

## Appearance and Manner

Lorry is a gentleman of about sixty, meticulously dressed in the subdued fashion of a banking-house clerk. Dickens describes him as "very orderly and methodical," with a "healthy colour" in his cheeks and a manner that is precise without being officious. His speech reflects a lifelong habit of subordinating personal feeling to professional duty — he speaks of emotions as if they were entries in a ledger, to be acknowledged and filed but never permitted to disrupt the orderly conduct of business.

His most telling physical detail is his wig — "a little flaxen wig" — which he adjusts in moments of agitation, as if straightening his professional exterior can restore order to his inner life. This tic becomes a signature gesture, a visible trace of the humanity that his banker's discipline struggles to contain.

## Background

Jarvis Lorry has spent his entire adult life in the service of Tellson's Bank. By the time the novel opens, he is approximately sixty years old and has risen to a position of considerable trust and responsibility. The bank dispatches him on sensitive missions, including the journey to Paris that sets the novel's plot in motion.

His long tenure at Tellson's means he was involved in the bank's affairs during Dr. Manette's original imprisonment — a connection that gives him unique knowledge of the Manette family's tragic history. He was present when the bank managed the scattered remnants of Dr. Manette's estate, and it is this institutional memory that brings him back into the Manettes' orbit eighteen years later.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Lucie-Manette|Lucie Manette]]**: Lorry acts as a surrogate uncle and protector to Lucie from the moment he escorts her to Paris to recover her father. He is unfailingly gentle with her, and she in turn treats him with affectionate respect.

- **[[Doctor-Manette|Dr. Alexandre Manette]]**: Lorry knew of Manette before his imprisonment and becomes one of his most trusted friends after his release. He is instrumental in managing the practical aspects of Manette's recovery and later serves as a steadying presence during Manette's relapses.

- **[[Charles-Darnay]]**: Lorry respects Darnay but maintains a banker's measured distance, particularly during Darnay's trial for treason and his later imprisonment in Paris.

- **[[Sydney-Carton]]**: Though their interactions are limited, Lorry witnesses Carton's transformation and is present for the final stages of the novel's climax. He represents the institutional order that Carton ultimately transcends.

## Role in the Story

Lorry's role spans the entire novel. He is the messenger who brings Lucie Manette the news that her father is alive (004-011), the steady companion who witnesses Dr. Manette's fragile recovery, and the loyal employee who risks his own safety in revolutionary Paris to protect the bank's interests and his friends.

During the Terror, Lorry travels to Paris and remains there throughout the most dangerous period, managing the bank's French branch from within the eye of the revolutionary storm. It is in the rooms above Tellson's Paris office that many of the novel's final scenes unfold, including Carton's fateful exchange of clothes with Darnay.

Lorry's ultimate function is as a witness: he represents the continuity of ordinary decency and professional duty in a world convulsed by revolutionary violence. His survival at the novel's end — "I have done my best" — affirms the endurance of the values he embodies.

## Symbolism

Jarvis Lorry represents the **continuity of ordinary decency** in the novel's moral universe. He is neither a revolutionary nor an aristocrat, neither a hero nor a villain — he is a banker who does his job, keeps his promises, and refuses to abandon his friends. In a novel structured around extremes — imprisonment and freedom, love and hatred, sacrifice and survival — Lorry occupies the steady middle ground. His very unremarkableness is his significance: Dickens suggests that the survival of civilization depends not on grand gestures but on the quiet, persistent decency of people like Jarvis Lorry, who simply do what they are asked to do and refuse to look away when others suffer.
