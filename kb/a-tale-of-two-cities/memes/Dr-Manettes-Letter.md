# Dr. Manette's Letter

## Overview

Dr. Manette's letter — his memoir written [[I. In Secret|in secret]] during eighteen years of [[Imprisonment|imprisonment]] in [[The-Bastille|the Bastille]] — is the narrative keystone of *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities|A Tale of Two Cities]]*. Discovered by Ernest Defarge in the cell of 105 North Tower during the [[Storming-of-the-Bastille|storming of the Bastille]], the letter resurfaces at [[Charles-Darnay|Charles Darnay]]'s second trial before [[The Revolutionary Tribunal|the Revolutionary Tribunal]]. Read aloud by Defarge, it documents the crimes of the Evrémonde brothers — the rape of a [[Peasant|peasant]] woman, the murder of her husband and brother, and the wrongful imprisonment of Dr. Manette himself — and functions as a piece of evidence so damning that it condemns Darnay to death, despite his complete innocence of the crimes described.

## The Writing of the Letter

Dr. Manette wrote the letter during his long imprisonment, using a rusty nail for a pen and soot mixed with [[Blood|blood]] for ink. The conditions of its composition underscore both his desperation and the moral urgency of the testimony he recorded:

> It was written in the last year of his imprisonment, as he lay in the straw in the dim light of his grated window, and traced its characters — when and as he could — with a rusty nail and scrapings of soot and charcoal from the chimney, mixed with blood.

The material details are central to the letter's emotional force. This is not a document of official record-keeping but of existential [[Witness|witness]] — a man who has been erased from the world insisting, with the last resources of his body and his cell, that the truth of what he saw be preserved.

## The Events Recorded

The letter recounts an episode from December 1757, when Dr. Manette — then a young physician in Paris — was summoned by the Evrémonde brothers, the Marquis and his twin, to attend to two dying peasants on their estate.

### The Crimes of the Evrémonde Brothers

The elder brother (Charles Darnay's father) had desired the wife of a peasant tenant. When he could not have her, he exercised his seigneurial rights: he compelled the husband to work himself to death by harnessing him to a cart like an animal, and then raped the wife. The woman's brother came to her defence and was mortally wounded by the Evrémonde brothers' swords.

When Dr. Manette arrived, he found:

- The young woman, dying, raving in fever, repeating the words of the older Evrémonde brother — "a young and beautiful woman, lying on a bed, her arms bound to her sides with sashes and with handkerchiefs"
- The young woman's brother, dying of a sword wound through the chest, who with his last breath revealed the older brother as the perpetrator
- A young boy — the brother of the dying woman — who had already been killed

Dr. Manette treated the survivors as best he could, but both died. A week later, the younger Evrémonde brother (Charles Darnay's uncle, the future Marquis) visited him with an offer of gold in exchange for silence. When Dr. Manette refused, the brothers had him taken from his home and secretly imprisoned in the Bastille — without charge, without trial, without record.

### The Curse

The letter concludes with what it calls "the last words of Alexander Manette, prisoner in the Bastille," and it is from this passage that the document derives its lethal power:

> "I, Alexandre Manette, unfortunate physician, native of [[Beauvais]], and afterwards resident in Paris, write this melancholy history in my doleful cell in the Bastille, during the last month of the year 1767. I write it at long intervals, under every possible difficulty... I denounce them and their descendants, to the last of their race. I, Alexandre Manette, do this night, not only renounce the Evrémonde name and all connection with that accursed line, but devote them and their posterity to the day when these things shall be answered for. I call upon Heaven and earth to bear witness against them, and to bring down retribution, not only upon their own heads, but on the heads of those who shall tolerate their crimes. To the last of the human race, I devote them and theirs."

The curse is total: it extends to the "last of their race" and to subsequent generations. Charles Darnay, born after the crimes were committed and innocent of them, is nonetheless captured by the curse's language of hereditary guilt.

## The Letter at Darnay's Trial

At Darnay's second trial, when the [[Jury|jury]] seems inclined toward acquittal, Ernest Defarge produces the letter — found in Manette's old cell during the storming of the Bastille — and reads it aloud. The effect is electric. The courtroom, which had been murmuring sympathetically toward Darnay, is transformed:

> A murmur of acclamation and applause ran through the audience.

The fact that the letter was written by Manette himself — Darnay's own father-in-law, a man revered as a martyr of the Revolution — makes the evidence unanswerable. Manette's desperate attempt to preserve a record of the Evrémonde crimes, written in a dungeon decades before Darnay was born, kills the very son-in-law he now loves.

## The Dramatic Irony

The letter represents the novel's most concentrated instance of dramatic irony. Dr. Manette wrote it in despair, seeking justice against the brothers who had destroyed his life. Decades later, having been "[[Recalled to Life|recalled to life]]" by his daughter Lucie, he has become the beloved father-in-law of Charles Darnay — the Evrémonde heir. The letter, preserved by Defarge as a weapon against the aristocracy, now threatens to destroy the family Manette has rebuilt:

- **Manette wrote to condemn the Evrémonde brothers** → His letter condemns his own son-in-law
- **Manette wrote to seek justice** → His letter produces an injustice (Darnay is innocent)
- **Manette wrote in isolation** → His letter is read in a public tribunal
- **Manette wrote for the future** → His letter destroys his own future happiness with Lucie and her family

The irony is tragic rather than merely clever: it emerges from the structural logic of hereditary guilt, class [[Vengeance|vengeance]], and the gap between individual intention and historical consequence.

## Significance

Dr. Manette's letter is the novel's clearest statement on the fatal logic of revolutionary justice. The letter demonstrates that the past cannot be simply buried or repudiated — Darnay's renunciation of his family name, his emigration to England, his virtuous life as a tutor, none of it can erase what his father and uncle did. The letter returns, like the repressed, to destroy the present.

It also functions as a narrative hinge: before the reading of the letter, Darnay seems likely to be acquitted; after it, his death appears inevitable, and only [[Sydney-Carton|Sydney Carton]]'s [[Sacrifice|sacrifice]] can alter the outcome. The letter thus creates the moral and narrative conditions for the novel's climactic resolution.
