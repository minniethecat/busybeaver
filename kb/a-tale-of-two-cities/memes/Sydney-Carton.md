# Sydney Carton

**Sydney Carton** is a central character — arguably the moral center — of Charles Dickens' *[[A-Tale-of-Two-Cities]]*. A brilliant but dissipated English barrister, he serves as the "jackal" to the lion-like barrister C.J. Stryver, supplying the legal acumen that Stryver takes credit for in court. Cynical, alcoholic, and consumed by self-loathing, Carton nonetheless possesses an extraordinary capacity for love, which ultimately drives him to exchange his life for that of [[Charles-Darnay]] at the guillotine. His trajectory from wasted potential to transcendent [[Sacrifice]] embodies the novel's master theme of **[[Resurrection]]**.

## Appearance and Manner

Carton is introduced to the reader at Darnay's treason trial at the Old Bailey, where his physical resemblance to the defendant proves decisive. Dickens paints him as listless and disheveled, a man who has ceased to care how the world perceives him:

> "Sydney Carton, idlest and most unpromising of men, was Stryver's great ally. What the two drank together, between Hilary Term and Michaelmas, might have floated a king's ship." (011-004)

His slouched posture, careless dress, and habitual drinking are the outward signs of inward despair. Yet within minutes of appearing, he demonstrates the penetrating intelligence — "an amazingly good jackal" (011-004) — that makes him indispensable to Stryver's courtroom triumphs.

## The Jackal and the Lion

Carton's relationship with his employer C.J. Stryver is a study in exploitation and self-abnegation. Stryver — ambitious, florid, and intellectually mediocre — presents the courtroom arguments; Carton, working through the night in their shared chambers, researches the law, marshals the facts, and devises the strategy. The metaphor is Dickens' own:

- **Stryver** = the lion, who claims the kill
- **Carton** = the jackal, who makes the kill possible

This arrangement suits Carton's self-image: he believes he deserves nothing better. His drinking and indolence are both symptom and cause of a deeper conviction that he has "fallen into his rankest resignation" and can no longer imagine a different life.

## Love for Lucie Manette

Carton's emotional awakening centers on Lucie Manette, the daughter of [[Doctor-Manette]]. During a visit to her home in Soho, Carton drops his sardonic mask and confesses his feelings in one of the novel's most intimate speeches:

> "For you, and for any dear to you, I would do anything. If my career were of that better kind that there was any opportunity or capacity of sacrifice in it, I would embrace any sacrifice for you and for those dear to you." (012-100)

He does not ask for her love in return — he knows himself too well to believe that possible. Instead, he offers a kind of covenant: that his life, however worthless it appears, will be held in reserve for her, to be spent when the moment demands it. This pledge, offered quietly in a Soho parlor, is the seed of everything that follows.

## The Sacrifice

Carton's covenant is redeemed in the novel's final act. When Charles Darnay is condemned to the guillotine by the Revolutionary Tribunal, Carton — exploiting their physical resemblance — visits Darnay in the Conciergerie prison, drugs him, and exchanges clothes with him. Darnay is carried out to freedom; Carton remains to face the blade.

The substitution is not impulsive. Carton has planned it with methodical care, securing the cooperation of the English spy John Barsad (now a turnkey at the prison) and timing the drugging and exchange with precision. His composure during his final hours — comforting a young seamstress also condemned to die — reveals a man who has, at last, found his purpose.

His final, unspoken words are among the most famous in English literature:

> "It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done; it is a far, far better rest that I go to than I have ever known." (045-051)

## Symbolism

Carton is the novel's most complete embodiment of the **Resurrection** theme. He is "recalled to life" not literally — like [[Doctor-Manette]], freed from the Bastille — but spiritually. His sacrifice transforms a wasted, self-lacerating existence into one of supreme meaning. The novel's closing lines, spoken in Carton's imagined prophetic voice, extend this resurrection beyond the personal to the historical and the eternal.

His physical doubling with Darnay is also structurally significant: Darnay represents the virtuous life Carton might have lived; Carton represents the sacrificial death that makes Darnay's continued life possible. Together they form a composite portrait of Dickens' moral vision — in which individual virtue and redemptive sacrifice are two faces of the same truth.
