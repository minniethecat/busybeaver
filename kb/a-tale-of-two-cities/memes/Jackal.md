# Jackal

The jackal is Sydney Carton's metaphor for himself — an animal that does the
hunting and the killing while the lion takes the credit and the meal. When
Carton works through the night preparing legal briefs while Stryver sleeps, he
is being the jackal; when Stryver presents those briefs in court the next
morning as if they were his own, he is being the lion. The metaphor is central
to understanding Carton's self-image and the novel's analysis of how talent is
recognised — or not — in the world.

## The Lion and the Jackal

The partnership is introduced with casual brutality: "although Sydney Carton
would never be a lion, he was an amazingly good jackal, and that he rendered
suit and service to Stryver in that humble capacity" (011-004). The phrase
"would never be a lion" is the judgment Carton has internalised — a verdict
passed not by Stryver but by the world, and repeated by Carton himself until it
becomes identity. Yet the qualifying phrase "amazingly good" is Dickens's
counter-verdict: Carton's brilliance is real, only his direction is wrong.

## The Nightly Ritual

The most extended treatment of the jackal metaphor occurs in the chapter "The
Jackal," which details a night of work in Stryver's chambers. "Sullenly enough,
the jackal loosened his dress, went into an adjoining room, and came back with a
large jug of cold water, a basin, and a towel or two" (011-022). The adverb
"sullenly" carries the entire emotional weight of the scene — Carton does the
work not from loyalty or ambition but from a kind of mechanical compulsion, the
jackal who has accepted his role and despises himself for it.

The division of labour is spatial as well as professional: "the lion then
composed himself on his back on a sofa on one side of the drinking-table, while
the jackal sat at his own paper-bestrewn table proper, on the other side of it,
with the bottles and glasses ready to his hand" (011-028). Stryver reclines;
Carton works. Stryver drinks and dozes; Carton drinks and labours. The two men
share the same room and the same bottles, but they occupy different worlds.

## The Meal and the Credit

When the work is done, Carton presents it to Stryver: "At length the jackal had
got together a compact repast for the lion, and proceeded to offer it to him"
(011-029). The word "repast" is chosen with care — the legal work is literally
what the lion will consume. Stryver "made his selections from it, and his
remarks upon it, and the jackal assisted both" (011-029). The credit belongs to
Stryver; Carton's name appears nowhere in the courtroom. He is the unseen labour
behind the visible success.

This arrangement continues for years. Carton knows exactly what he is doing —
knows that Stryver is a "lion" only because Carton does the jackal's work — and
yet he cannot bring himself to break the pattern. The jackal metaphor is not
about exploitation; it is about self-exploitation, a man who knows his worth and
chooses to bury it.

## Jackal as Identity

The jackal metaphor extends beyond the legal partnership. When Carton describes
himself as "a disappointed drudge" who cares "for no man on earth, and no man on
earth cares for me" (011-038), he is the jackal speaking — the animal who works
in darkness and receives neither recognition nor affection. He "would never be a
lion" — but the tragedy is that this is a self-fulfilling prophecy. Carton
chooses the jackal role, and the novel's final act is his transcendence of it:
the jackal who, at last, takes the lion's place on the scaffold.
