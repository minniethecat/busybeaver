## Overview

Alonso, King of [[naples|Naples]], enters the play as an antagonist — the monarch who, twelve years before the opening storm, supplied the military force that enabled [[antonio|Antonio]] to depose [[prospero|Prospero]]. But unlike [[antonio|Antonio]], Alonso is capable of [[guilt|remorse]], and his journey through guilt, grief (over the supposed death of his son [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]), and ultimately repentance is the play's central moral arc. By Act 5, Alonso resigns his claim on [[milan|Milan]] voluntarily, asks [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[forgiveness|pardon]], and is reunited with [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] in the play's most emotionally charged recognition scene.

## Role in the Play

Alonso is introduced in the play's opening scene as a king under duress: the [[storm|shipwreck]] reduces him from sovereign to supplicant, begging the [[boatswain|Boatswain]] for information. His [[power|authority]], like the ship itself, is being dismantled by [[the-shipwreck|the storm]]. The [[boatswain|Boatswain]]'s retort — "What cares these roarers for the name of king?" (002-015) — announces Alonso's thematic role: he is a king who must learn that kingship is no protection against the forces of [[nature|nature]] or [[guilt|conscience]].

The guilt Alonso carries is specific. He acknowledges his complicity in [[prospero|Prospero]]'s overthrow, and the weight of that crime becomes unbearable after the (apparent) loss of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]. "My son is lost," he says in (005-128), and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] reminds him unkindly that "the fault's your own" (005-129). Alonso's grief is the mechanism through which guilt becomes [[guilt|remorse]]: the death of his son feels like retribution for his own political sin.

Alonso's capacity for genuine [[guilt|remorse]] distinguishes him sharply from [[antonio|Antonio]]. Where [[antonio|Antonio]] is silent and unrepentant in the final act, Alonso immediately acknowledges [[prospero|Prospero]]'s accusation: "Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat / Thou [[forgiveness|pardon]] me my wrongs" (014-135–136). His repentance is both personal and political — he gives back what he helped take, and asks forgiveness for the taking.

## Key Relationships

- **[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]**: Alonso's love for his son is the emotional centre of his character. He believes [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] drowned ("O thou mine heir / Of [[naples|Naples]] and of [[milan|Milan]], what strange fish / Hath made his meal on thee?" (005-136–138)), and his grief is the catalyst for his moral transformation. The reunion with [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] in (014-168) — "My dear son [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]" — is the play's most powerful moment of restoration.

- **[[sebastian|Sebastian]]**: [[sebastian|Alonso's brother]], who plots to murder him in his [[sleep|sleep]]. The [[sebastian|Sebastian]]-[[antonio|Antonio]] conspiracy (Act 2, Scene 1) mirrors the original usurpation: [[sebastian|Sebastian]] would do to Alonso what [[antonio|Antonio]] did to [[prospero|Prospero]], with [[antonio|Antonio]] as the instigator. Alonso is saved only by [[ariel|Ariel]]'s intervention.

- **[[antonio|Antonio]]**: The Duke of [[milan|Milan]] who drew Alonso into the original crime. [[antonio|Antonio]] continues his manipulations on the [[island|island]], tempting [[sebastian|Sebastian]] to fratricide. Alonso's complicity with [[antonio|Antonio]] is the source of his guilt, and his break with [[antonio|Antonio]] — symbolised by the separate [[forgiveness|pardon]]s [[prospero|Prospero]] grants each — is his moral rehabilitation.

- **[[prospero|Prospero]]**: The wronged duke whom Alonso helped depose. Their [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] in (014-084–160) is the play's political climax: [[prospero|Prospero]] accuses, Alonso repents, and the dukedom is returned. The warmth of their [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] — "Welcome, my friends all!" (014-093) — contrasts with the cold silence between [[prospero|Prospero]] and [[antonio|Antonio]].

- **[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]**: The honest councillor who saved [[prospero|Prospero]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] from death by supplying their boat with pro[[dream|vision]]s. Alonso's relationship with [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] shows his capacity for loyalty: even in grief, he defends [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] against [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s mockery.

## Key Speeches

> You cram these words into mine ears against
> The stomach of my sense. Would I had never
> Married my daughter there! for, coming thence,
> My son is lost, and, in my rate, she too.
> Who is so far from Italy removed
> I ne'er again shall see her. O thou mine heir
> Of Naples and of Milan, what strange fish
> Hath made his meal on thee?
> — (005-082–089)

Alonso's first extended speech in the play condenses his entire tragic situation into eight lines. Irritated by [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s consolations ("You cram these words"), he catalogues his losses: the daughter married into geographical and emotional distance, the son apparently devoured by the sea. The image of "what strange fish / Hath made his meal on thee" is one of the play's most intimate moments of despair — a king reduced to imagining his heir as carrion, his dynasty consumed by the element that answers to no monarch. The speech establishes grief as the solvent of royal identity: Alonso speaks not as a king but as a father who has lost everything.

> The affliction of my mind amends, with which,
> I fear, a madness held me: this must crave —
> An if this be at all — a most strange story.
> Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat
> Thou pardon me my wrongs. — But how should Prospero
> Be living and be here?
> — (014-132–137)

Alonso's repentance is the moral climax of Act 5 — and arguably of the entire play. The broken syntax registers the disorientation of a man emerging from enchanted guilt into the presence of the man he wronged: two dashes fracture the thought, and "An if this be at all" suspends the moment between dream and reality. "Thy dukedom I resign" is simultaneously a political act (abdicating a claim he should never have held) and a spiritual one (surrendering the identity built on usurpation). The question that follows — "But how should [[prospero|Prospero]] / Be living and be here?" — reveals the confusion of a man for whom [[forgiveness|forgiveness]] is as bewildering as [[magic|magic]]. Unlike [[antonio|Antonio]], who meets [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[forgiveness|pardon]] with silence, Alonso speaks — and in speaking, heals the political breach his complicity created.

> If thou be'st Prospero,
> Give us particulars of thy preservation;
> How thou hast met us here, who three hours since
> Were wreck'd upon this shore; where I have lost —
> How sharp the point of this remembrance is! —
> My dear son Ferdinand.
> — (014-162–168)

This speech traces Alonso's arc in miniature. It begins in the language of state — a king demanding an accounting from the man he confronts — then fractures into grief with the dash and exclamation ("How sharp the point of this remembrance is!"), only to resolve in the simplest, most affecting declaration in the play: "My dear son [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]." The word "dear" does enormous dramatic work: it is the vocabulary of a father, not a king, and marks Alonso's final movement from political identity to human love. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s response — drawing the curtain to reveal [[ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] playing chess — turns Alonso's cry of grief into the prelude to reunion.

## Character Arc

Alonso moves through three distinct strippings on his way to restoration. The [[storm|storm]] strips him of royal [[power|authority]]: the [[boatswain|Boatswain]]'s defiance in (002-001–065) reduces a king to a frightened passenger, establishing in the play's first minutes that [[nature|nature]] recognises no hierarchy. [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s apparent death strips him of hope: the grief that saturates Alonso's speeches in Act 2 (005-082–089, 005-128–138) is not merely paternal but dynastic — with his son gone, his line ends. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s [[harpy|Harpy]] speech at [[the-banquet|the banquet]] (010-001–110) strips him of self-justification: "the powers, delaying, not forgetting, have / Incensed the seas and shores" (010-099–100) renames what Alonso thought was accident as judgment, forcing him to see [[the-shipwreck|the storm]] as a consequence of his own sin.

These three strippings prepare Alonso for the restoration that follows. By Act 5 he is a broken man — and it is precisely this brokenness that makes him capable of receiving [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness. Unlike [[antonio|Antonio]], whose silence in the final act (014-084–160) signals an unrepentant will, Alonso speaks, and his speech is surrender: "Thy dukedom I resign" (014-135). The arc demonstrates the play's central moral thesis: guilt acknowledged and repented becomes the ground of restoration, not destruction. Alonso does not merely recover his son; he recovers himself — the king who wakes from enchanted [[sleep|sleep]] in Act 5 is a different man from the king who demanded the [[boatswain|Boatswain]]'s attention in Act 1. His restoration to [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] in (014-162–180) is the play's emotional climax: the father who imagined his son as "strange fish" meal now holds him alive, and the political order — [[naples|Naples]] united with [[milan|Milan]] through [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s marriage to [[miranda|Miranda]] — is healed in the same moment.

Alonso's arc is also the arc of the court p[[magic|art]]y as a whole. He is the highest-ranking figure among the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed nobles, and his moral reclamation pulls the entire company toward resolution. Where [[sebastian|Sebastian]] and [[antonio|Antonio]] resist the [[island|island]]'s transformative power — remaining sarcastic, conspiratorial, unreachable — Alonso yields to it. His arc is therefore structurally essential: without Alonso's repentance, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness would have no object, and the play's final concord would remain merely magical rather than moral.

## Critical Reception

Frank Kermode, in his landmark Arden edition of *The Tempest* (1954), identifies Alonso as the moral centre of the play's courtly world. Kermode reads Alonso's guilt as representative of a broader pattern of "courtly sin" that the pastoral [[island|island]] setting both exposes and heals: the king's complicity in [[antonio|Antonio]]'s usurpation is not an isolated crime but a symptom of the political corruption that the play's magic works to undo. For Kermode, Alonso's capacity for [[guilt|remorse]] — his willingness to acknowledge his wrong and seek [[forgiveness|pardon]] — is what distinguishes the restorable from the irredeemable in Shakespeare's late romances. The Arden commentary draws p[[magic|art]]icular attention to the banquet scene (010-001–110), where [[ariel|Ariel]]'s [[harpy|Harpy]] confronts Alonso not as a king but as a sinner, and Alonso's response — "Oh, it is monstrous, monstrous!" (010-106) — marks the moment [[guilt|conscience]] overtakes self-defence.

Stephen Greenblatt, approaching the play through the lens of political theology and Renaissance self-fashioning, has examined Alonso's resignation of [[milan|Milan]] as a rare instance of political [[power|authority]] voluntarily surrendered. In Greenblatt's reading, Alonso's "Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat / Thou [[forgiveness|pardon]] me my wrongs" (014-135–136) is not merely a personal apology but a theatrical undoing of the original usurpation: the king who once enabled a coup now performs its reversal. This reading situates Alonso within the play's larger meditation on the fragility of political [[authority|legitimacy]] — a theme that resonates through [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own abdication of magic and return to mere dukedom. Greenblatt notes that Alonso's repentance is structurally unusual: no other Shakespearean king surrenders territory and asks forgiveness in a single breath, making Alonso a unique figure in the canon of royal characters.

G. Wilson Knight, in *The Crown of Life* (1947), reads *The Tempest* as a mystical drama in which each character's journey traces a spiritual itinerary. For Knight, Alonso's passage through the [[island|island]] is a purgatorial experience: the grief that overwhelms him at [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s apparent death is a form of spiritual cleansing, a "[[storm|tempest]] of the soul" that parallels the literal storm of the opening scene. Knight emphasises the banquet scene's theological resonances — [[ariel|Ariel]]'s [[harpy|Harpy]] is a figure of divine judgment, and Alonso's guilt before it is not merely political but existential — and reads the reunion with [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] as a resurrection moment, a rebirth that completes the purgatorial arc. Alonso, in this reading, is the court p[[magic|art]]y's representative soul: his suffering, repentance, and restoration model the play's Christian pattern of sin, judgment, and grace.
