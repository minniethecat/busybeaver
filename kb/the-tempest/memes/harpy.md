## Overview

The harpy is a creature from classical mythology — a winged female monster, often associated with divine vengeance and the snatching away of food or people. In *The Tempest*, [[ariel|Ariel]] assumes the form of a harpy in Act 3, Scene 3, to confront [[alonso|Alonso]], [[antonio|Antonio]], and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] with their guilt and to pronounce [[prospero|Prospero]]'s judgment. The harpy is the most terrifying of [[ariel|Ariel]]'s disguises in the play, marking a shift from the playful, musical spirit of earlier scenes to an agent of supernatural justice.

## In the Play

The harpy episode occurs at the climax of Act 3, Scene 3. [[alonso|Alonso]] and his courtiers, exhausted and despairing, encounter a mysterious banquet that appears and vanishes. The stage direction reads: "Thunder and lightning. Enter [[ariel|Ariel]], like a harpy; claps his wings upon the table; and, with a quaint device, the banquet vanishes" (010-080). [[ariel|Ariel]]-as-harpy then delivers a withering address, accusing the "three men of sin" of having deposed [[prospero|Prospero]] and exposed him and the infant [[miranda|Miranda]] to the sea. The harpy declares that the powers "delay'd, not lost" have now brought them to this desolate [[island|island]] for judgment.

The harpy figure is carefully chosen. In classical tradition — most famously Virgil's *Aeneid*, Book III — harpies are agents of divine punishment who defile feasts and speak prophecy. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s harpy invokes this tradition directly, telling the courtiers that "I and my fellows / Are ministers of Fate" (010-113). The speech transforms the [[island|island]] from a place of random misfortune into a theater of moral reckoning.

[[alonso|Alonso]]'s response confirms [[ariel|the harpy]]'s effectiveness: he hears the name "[[prospero|Prospero]]" in the winds and thunder, and his guilt drives him toward suicide — "I'll seek him deeper than e'er plummet sounded, / And with him there lie mudded" (010-145).
