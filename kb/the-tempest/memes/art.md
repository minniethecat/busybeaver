## Overview

"Art" is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s preferred term for his magic, and the word carries a precise charge: it is learned, book-derived, and rational, as opposed to the malefic [[magic|sorcery]] of [[sycorax|Sycorax]] or the folk superstition of [[caliban|Caliban]]'s inherited lore. [[prospero|Prospero]] was drawn away from governance by "the liberal arts / Without a parallel; those being all my [[knowledge|study]]" (003-103–104), and his library became both the source of his power and the cause of his downfall. The play distinguishes [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] from witchcraft through a consistent set of markers: it is exercised through books and a staff, mediated by a spirit ([[ariel|Ariel]]), and ultimately renounceable. Art is knowledge made operative—a technology of the supernatural that, unlike [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s [[magic|sorcery]], can be laid down.

## In the Play

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s backstory establishes [[magic|art]] as the antagonist of governance. "The government I cast upon my brother, / And to my state grew stranger, being transported / And rapt in secret studies" (003-105–107). The opposition is structural: [[magic|art]] and [[authority|rule]] cannot coexist. [[prospero|Prospero]] chose [[magic|art]]; [[antonio|Antonio]] chose power. The play's resolution requires [[prospero|Prospero]] to reverse that choice.

His [[magic|art]] is located in his physical instruments: the magic garment ("pluck my magic garment from me" (003-035)), the staff, and the book. When he abjures his magic, he breaks the staff and drowns the book—renunciation is a physical destruction of the instruments of [[magic|art]] (014-062). The garment, staff, and book are the externalised forms of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s knowledge, and destroying them returns that knowledge to the realm of the merely intellectual, where it can no longer act upon the world.

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s grudging recognition of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]]—"I must obey: his [[magic|art]] is of such power" (003-504)—acknowledges its superiority even to the god [[setebos|Setebos]]. Art here is not merely powerful but ontologically overwhelming: it subordinates even divinity to its [[authority|command]]s.

The play's Epilogue extends the metaphor: having renounced his [[magic|art]], [[prospero|Prospero]] stands on stage as "a weak and sickly old man," asking the audience's applause to fill the space that magic once occupied. Art gives way to grace.

## Key Passages

> And Prospero the prime duke, being so reputed
> In dignity, and for the liberal arts
> Without a parallel; those being all my study.
> — (003-102–104)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s self-definition through [[magic|art]]. "Without a parallel" claims uniqueness; "being all my [[knowledge|study]]" claims total devotion. The liberal arts are the foundation of his identity, preceding and outlasting his political title.

> I must obey: his art is of such power,
> It would control my dam's god, Setebos,
> And make a vassal of him.
> — (003-504–505)

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s assessment of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] as a force that surpasses even divine [[power|authority]] (003-504). The word "control" is precise: [[magic|art]] does not merely compete with divinity but subordinates it.

> Lie there, my art.
> — (003-036)

[[prospero|Prospero]], laying down his magic garment. Four words that encapsulate the relationship between [[prospero|Prospero]] and his power: [[magic|art]] is an object that can be put down, a garment that can be shed—not an intrinsic identity.
