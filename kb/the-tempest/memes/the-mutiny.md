## Overview

The Mutiny is the comic subplot of *The Tempest*, running in parallel with [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s more serious conspiracy against [[alonso|Alonso]]. [[caliban|Caliban]], mistaking [[stephano|the drunken butler]] [[stephano|Stephano]] for a god who has "dropped from heaven" (006-062), swears allegiance to him and persuades him to murder [[prospero|Prospero]] and seize the island. [[trinculo|Trinculo]], [[trinculo|Alonso's jester]], joins opportunistically, lured by the promise of a viceroyalty. The three spend Acts 2 through 4 plotting, drinking, and stumbling toward [[prosperos-cell|Prospero's cell]] — only to be thw[[magic|art]]ed at every turn by [[ariel|Ariel]]'s invisible interventions, culminating in a spirit-hunt that drives them through briars and into a "foul lake" (012-224) before their humiliating exposure in Act 5.

The mutiny operates on multiple dramatic registers simultaneously. On the surface, it is the play's broadest physical comedy: a four-legged monster with two voices ([[trinculo|Trinculo]] sheltering under [[caliban|Caliban]]'s gaberdine), a butler who crowns himself king by bottle, and a conspiracy defeated by the irresistible allure of fine clothes. Beneath the farce, however, the subplot replays the play's deepest concerns — usurpation, colonial [[servitude|mastery]], legitimate [[authority|rule]], and the psychology of servitude — in a grotesque minor key. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s willingness to ex[[transformation|change]] [[prospero|Prospero]]'s rigorous [[authority|rule]] for [[stephano|Stephano]]'s drunken tyranny ("I'll kiss thy foot: I prithee, be my god," 009-037) renders the structure of colonial dependency visible as both tragedy and absurdity.

## Context

The mutiny begins in Act 2, Scene 2 (006), immediately after [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s assassination conspiracy in Act 2, Scene 1. Shakespeare structures the two conspiracies as mirror images: the courtiers plot in civilised whispers over [[sleep|sleep]]ing bodies; the drunkards plot in raucous shouts over a bottle. Both conspiracies target legitimate [[power|authority]] ([[alonso|Alonso]] in one case, [[prospero|Prospero]] in the other), and both are thw[[magic|art]]ed by [[ariel|Ariel]] — who wakes [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] to foil the court plot, and who torments the drunkards with ventriloquism and spirit-hounds to foil the island plot.

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s motivation is rooted in the history established in Act 1, Scene 2: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s arrival, his [[knowledge|education]] of [[caliban|Caliban]], and [[caliban|Caliban]]'s subsequent enslavement after attempting to rape [[miranda|Miranda]]. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s claim — "this island's mine, by [[sycorax|Sycorax]] my mother, / Which thou takest from me" (009-029) — frames the mutiny not merely as comic rebellion but as an anti-colonial insurgency, however debased its execution. [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]], for their p[[magic|art]], are survivors of the [[storm|shipwreck]] [[prospero|Prospero]] engineered in Act 1. Displaced from [[court-of-naples|the Neapolitan court]] hierarchy, they find on the island an apparent opportunity to invert their stations: a butler who would be king, a jester who would be viceroy.

## Dramatic Function

The mutiny serves as a grotesque mirror to the main conspiracy plot, operating through systematic inversion. Where [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] plot in civilised whispers over the [[sleep|sleep]]ing [[alonso|Alonso]] and [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]], [[caliban|Caliban]], [[stephano|Stephano]], and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] do so in drunken shouts, with [[caliban|Caliban]] repeatedly silenced by his co-conspirators' irreverent interruptions. Where the court conspiracy is motivated by ambition of Machiavellian sophistication — [[antonio|Antonio]]'s administrative coup in [[milan|Milan]], [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s contemplated regicide — the island mutiny is motivated by wine, resentment, and the absurd fantasy of a butler-king. The parallel structure insists that the distance between the two conspiracies is one of degree and style rather than kind: usurpation is usurpation, whether dressed in courtly rhetoric or drunken bravado.

The subplot also provides the play's most sustained comic relief while simultaneously deepening its most serious themes. The physical comedy — [[trinculo|Trinculo]] under the gaberdine (006-016–048), [[stephano|Stephano]]'s drunken coronation, the "steal by line and level" (012-274) garment-squabble — gives the audience permission to laugh at rebellion. Yet [[caliban|Caliban]]'s genuine lyricism in the same scenes ("[[island|the isle]] is full of noises," 009-098) prevents the laughter from becoming simple contempt. The conspiracy is farcical, but the conspirator is not merely a fool: [[caliban|Caliban]]'s desire for [[freedom|freedom]], however misdirected, registers as authentic.

Structurally, the mutiny provides [[ariel|Ariel]] with a stage for his most inventive interventions. Unlike the solemn [[harpy|harpy]]-apparition he performs for the court p[[magic|art]]y (010-080–087), his work against the drunkards is playful, mischievous, and [[music|music]]ally driven. He speaks in [[trinculo|Trinculo]]'s voice to sow discord (009-021), leads the trio with tabor and pipe, and ultimately directs [[ariel|the spirit]]-hounds in a hunt that is both punishment and theatrical spectacle. These episodes establish [[ariel|Ariel]]'s range as [[prospero|Prospero]]'s agent: capable of cosmic accusation and low comedy alike, and increasingly autonomous in his execution of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s designs.

Finally, the mutiny's failure provides the condition for [[prospero|Prospero]]'s most morally freighted acknowledgment. When [[caliban|Caliban]] is brought before the royal p[[magic|art]]y in stolen apparel, [[prospero|Prospero]] says: "this thing of darkness I / Acknowledge mine" (014-350–351). The pronoun "mine" can mean "my servant," "my responsibility," or "my own darkness made external." The ambiguity is the point: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s acknowledgment of [[caliban|Caliban]] is inseparable from his acknowledgment of what [[caliban|Caliban]] represents — the violence, coercion, and unresolved [[guilt|guilt]] that underwrite his [[authority|rule]] of the island.

## Key Passages

> "I'll swear, upon that bottle, to be thy true subject; for the liquor is not earthly." (006-056)

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s first oath of allegiance to [[stephano|Stephano]] is sworn not on a sword, a crown, or a sacred text, but on a bottle of wine. The gesture is both comic — reducing feudal oath-taking to drunken farce — and structurally precise: [[caliban|Caliban]], who has known only [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magical books as instruments of power, misrecognises wine as another form of supernatural [[power|authority]]. The phrase "not e[[magic|art]]hly" echoes the language of religious awe, and [[caliban|Caliban]]'s mistake — worshipping a butler because his liquor tastes divine — becomes the engine of the entire subplot. It is the play's most economical demonstration that the structures of colonial reverence can attach to any object of power, however unworthy.

> "Monster, I will kill this man: his daughter and I will be king and queen,—save our Graces! —and Trinculo and thyself shall be viceroys." (009-075)

[[stephano|Stephano]]'s acceptance of [[caliban|Caliban]]'s murder proposal lays bare the conspiracy's logic of social inversion. A butler imagines himself a king; a jester imagines himself a viceroy; a slave imagines himself a liberator — and all three are, in different ways, deluded. [[stephano|Stephano]]'s "save our Graces" is the language of courtly deference turned to self-congratulation, and his casual promise to distribute viceroyalties reveals that he understands [[authority|sovereignty]] as nothing more than the power to redistribute spoils among drinking companions. The line is comedy and critique in equal measure: this is what ambition looks like when stripped of its ideological justifications.

> "Why, as I told thee, 'tis a custom with him / I' th' afternoon to sleep: there thou mayst brain him, / Having first seized his books; or with a log / Batter his skull, or paunch him with a stake, / Or cut his wezand with thy knife. Remember / First to possess his books; for without them / He's but a sot, as I am, nor hath not / One spirit to command" (009-054–009-061)

This is the most strategically intelligent speech in the mutiny, and [[caliban|Caliban]] delivers it. His tactical specificity — the afternoon [[sleep|sleep]], the multiple murder methods, the primacy of the books — demonstrates a knowledge of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s habits and vulnerabilities that no other character possesses. The insistence on seizing the books before striking is critical: [[caliban|Caliban]] understands that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s power is not inherent but technological, mediated through texts. "Without them he's but a sot, as I am" is a moment of st[[magic|art]]ling self-awareness: [[caliban|Caliban]] sees the structural equivalence between himself and his [[master|master]], differentiated only by access to knowledge. That this insight leads to nothing — the conspirators are too drunk, too distractible, too comically incompetent to execute it — is the subplot's tragic dimension.

> "Do that good mischief which may make this island / Thine own for ever, and I, thy Caliban, / For aye thy foot-licker." (012-258–012-260)

As [[ariel|Ariel]] hangs the "glistering apparel" (012-239) that will doom the conspiracy, [[caliban|Caliban]] makes his final appeal to [[stephano|Stephano]]. The oxymoron "good mischief" captures the subplot's moral ambiguity: from [[caliban|Caliban]]'s perspective, the murder of [[prospero|Prospero]] is liberation, and what [[prospero|Prospero]] would call evil, [[caliban|Caliban]] calls good. The self-designation "thy foot-licker" is devastating — [[caliban|Caliban]], who resents [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[servitude|mastery]] with every breath, voluntarily offers [[stephano|Stephano]] the same abjection he claims to be fleeing. He has learned nothing from his enslavement except how to reproduce its terms. The line marks the exact point where the rebellion's political potential collapses into farce.

> "Let it alone, thou fool; it is but trash." (012-263)

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s furious dismissal of the glittering garments that have mesmerised [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] is the mutiny's turning point. Throughout the conspiracy, [[caliban|Caliban]] has been the strategist and the poet; here, for a single line, he becomes the voice of reason — and reason is powerless. The garments are [[prospero|Prospero]]'s perfect trap precisely because they exploit the difference between [[caliban|Caliban]]'s motivation ([[freedom|freedom]]) and his co-conspirators' (greed). [[caliban|Caliban]] sees "trash"; [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] see treasure. The line is the subplot in miniature: a serious rebellion undone by trivial appetites.

> *Re-enter Ariel, driving in Caliban, Stephano, and Trinculo, in their stolen apparel.* (014-329)

This stage direction is the mutiny's visual epitaph. The conspirators enter not as rebels but as thieves — "in their stolen apparel" — their political ambitions reduced to petty larceny. The image of [[ariel|Ariel]] "driving" them like livestock completes the reversal: the would-be king, viceroy, and liberator are herded before the court like animals. The silence of [[caliban|Caliban]]'s humiliation ("O [[setebos|Setebos]], these be brave spirits indeed! / How fine my [[master|master]] is! I am afraid / He will chastise me," 014-332–334) contrasts painfully with his earlier lyricism, and [[prospero|Prospero]]'s subsequent acknowledgment — "this thing of darkness I / Acknowledge mine" (014-350–351) — carries the weight of everything the stage direction has shown us.

| **[[stephano|Stephano]]** | Would-be king | Drunken ambition elevated by [[caliban|Caliban]]'s worship; the fantasy of [[authority|sovereignty]] without the burden of competence |

[[caliban|Caliban]] is the mutiny's intelligence, [[stephano|Stephano]] its figurehead, [[trinculo|Trinculo]] its sarcastic chorus, [[ariel|Ariel]] its invisible antagonist, and [[prospero|Prospero]] the watchful god whose interventions ensure the rebellion never graduates from farce to genuine threat. The five roles form a miniature political ecosystem: leader, puppet, opportunist, spy, and sovereign — with the sovereign never visible to the conspirators until the moment of judgment.

## Aftermath

The mutiny's unraveling proceeds through four stages, each more humiliating than the last.

First, [[ariel|Ariel]] sows discord among the conspirators by ventriloquising [[trinculo|Trinculo]]'s voice. When [[trinculo|Trinculo]] denies having spoken, [[stephano|Stephano]] threatens him: "[[trinculo|Trinculo]], keep a good tongue in your head: if you prove a mutineer,—the next tree!" (009-016). The would-be king's first act of [[authority|sovereignty]] is to threaten his own co-conspirator with execution — a dark joke about the [[nature|nature]] of power.

Second, [[ariel|Ariel]] leads the trio toward [[prosperos-cell|Prospero's cell]] with tabor and pipe, only to divert them through "toothed briers, sharp furzes, pricking gorse, and thorns" (012-253–254) and into the "foul lake" (012-224) where they stand "dancing up to the chins." The physical punishment is comic but also disciplinary: bodies that would rebel are subjected to the island's punishing geography.

Third, the garment trap. [[ariel|Ariel]] hangs "glistering apparel" (012-239) near [[prosperos-cell|Prospero's cell]]. [[caliban|Caliban]] urges the assassination; [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] are immediately mesmerised by the fine clothes. "Let it alone, thou fool; it is but trash" (012-263), [[caliban|Caliban]] pleads, but the two Europeans cannot resist — their greed overwhelms their political ambition, and the conspiracy collapses into a squabble over wardrobe. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s trap exploits the fault line within the rebellion itself: [[caliban|Caliban]] wants [[freedom|freedom]], [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] want status, and the latter appetite is far easier to manipulate.

Fourth, [[ariel|the spirit]]-hunt. "Divers Spirits, in shape of dogs and hounds" (012-282) are set upon the conspirators, driving them offstage in a cacophony of hunting cries — the play's most spectacularly physical piece of stage business. [[prospero|Prospero]] orders his goblins to "grind their joints with dry convulsions, / Shorten up their sinews with aged cramps" (012-283+), a punishment whose violence exceeds anything the mutiny itself threatened.

In Act 5, the conspirators are brought before the royal p[[magic|art]]y (014-329), bedraggled, wet, and wearing stolen clothes. [[alonso|Alonso]] recognises [[stephano|Stephano]] ("my drunken butler") and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] ("reeling ripe"). [[stephano|Stephano]] physically cringes from touch ("O, touch me not;—I am not [[stephano|Stephano]], but a cramp"), his would-be kingship reduced to bodily pain. [[caliban|Caliban]], seeing [[prospero|Prospero]] in his ducal robes, finally perceives his error: "What a thrice-double ass / Was I, to take this drunkard for a god, / And worship this dull fool!" (014-370–372). [[prospero|Prospero]] orders the three to [[prosperos-cell|his cell]] to "trim it handsomely" in ex[[transformation|change]] for [[forgiveness|pardon]] — a sentence of domestic [[servitude|service]] that restores the hierarchy the mutiny sought to overturn. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s final words in the play — "I'll be wise hereafter, / And seek for grace" (014-369–370) — close the mutiny on a note of ambiguous submission: repentance, strategic compliance, or both.

## Critical Notes

**Frank Kermode** (Arden Shakespeare, 1954) identifies the comic subplot as a structural parody of the main action, arguing that "the low plot of [[caliban|Caliban]], [[stephano|Stephano]], and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] is a characteristic Shakespearian device, reducing the main theme of usurpation to the level of farce." Kermode notes that [[caliban|Caliban]]'s conspiracy "reflects the main theme of usurpation" while operating in a register that makes it "grotesquely comic" rather than genuinely threatening. The drunken butler's ambition to be king exposes the arbitrariness of [[power|authority]]: if a butler can imagine himself a sovereign, what distinguishes legitimate [[authority|rule]] from successful usurpation? Kermode reads the mutiny as Shakespeare's demonstration that political order rests on performance and recognition rather than inherent right — a theme the play's romance resolution conspicuously declines to resolve.

**Stephen Greenblatt** ("Learning to Curse," 1990) reads the mutiny through the lens of colonial discourse, arguing that [[caliban|Caliban]]'s rebellion is a parody of indigenous resistance as imagined by European colonisers. For Greenblatt, the conspiracy's comic incompetence — the worship of alcohol, the distraction by trinkets (the "glistering apparel"), the inability to distinguish between a god and a drunkard — reproduces colonial stereotypes of native irrationality while simultaneously exposing the cynicism of the European interlopers, whose own ambitions are equally base. Greenblatt notes that the garment trap is p[[magic|art]]icularly revealing: [[caliban|Caliban]] alone recognises the clothes as "trash," while [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] — the Europeans — are the ones mesmerised by material display. The "colonial rebellion" is thus a double parody: [[caliban|Caliban]]'s resistance is debased by the form it takes, but the Europeans' greed is debased by comparison with [[caliban|Caliban]]'s clarity.

**Paul Brown** ("'This thing of darkness I acknowledge mine': *The Tempest* and the discourse of colonialism," 1985) argues that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s final acknowledgment of [[caliban|Caliban]] is the play's most important political moment. The phrase "acknowledge mine" performs a triple function: it claims [[caliban|Caliban]] as property, accepts responsibility for him, and — most troublingly — identifies [[caliban|Caliban]] as a projection of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own darkness. Brown reads the mutiny's failure as structurally necessary: the colonial project cannot permit successful native rebellion, but it cannot wholly disown the rebel either, since the rebel's existence is what justifies the colonial presence. [[caliban|Caliban]] must be punished, humiliated, and finally acknowledged — but never liberated — for [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|rule]] to remain coherent.
