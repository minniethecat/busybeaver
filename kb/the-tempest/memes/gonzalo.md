## Overview

Gonzalo is the moral centre of [[court-of-naples|the Neapolitan court]] (003-217). The Dramatis Personae describes him as "an honest old Counsellor," and every one of his appearances confirms the epithet. When [[prospero|Prospero]] was deposed and cast adrift, it was Gonzalo who secretly supplied the boat with "rich garments, linens, stuffs, and necessaries" and—most vitally—the books from [[prospero|Prospero]]'s library "which I prize above my dukedom." In a play structured around usurpation and betrayal, Gonzalo stands alone as the figure who never conspires, never mocks the defenceless, and never falls into despair—even when [[storm|shipwreck]]ed on a strange island.

His temperament is a fusion of kindness and absurd optimism, qualities that make him the target of [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s relentless mockery in Act 2. His famous "commonwealth" speech, in which he imagines a golden-age society without [[authority|sovereignty]], trade, or labour, is simultaneously a philosophical set-piece and a character revelation: this is a man whose instinct, even in crisis, is to imagine the best possible world.

## Role in the Play

Gonzalo first appears among the court p[[magic|art]]y in the opening [[storm|shipwreck]], where his famous gallows-humour—"I'll warrant him for drowning, though the ship were no stronger than a nutshell"—provides the play's first note of absurd comfort (002-028–002-032). His optimism, however unfounded, serves as a counterweight to the [[boatswain|Boatswain]]'s pragmatic fury and the nobles' panic.

In Act 2, Scene 1, Gonzalo is at his most exposed. He tries to console the grieving [[alonso|Alonso]]—who believes [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] drowned—and is met with savage mockery from [[sebastian|Sebastian]] and [[antonio|Antonio]]. When Gonzalo wearies and falls asleep under [[ariel|Ariel]]'s spell, the two conspirators plot to murder [[alonso|Alonso]] (005-337–005-339). [[ariel|Ariel]] wakes Gonzalo just in time, and his cry—"Preserve the King!"—thw[[magic|art]]s the assassination. In Act 5, Gonzalo is the emotional witness to the great [[forgiveness|reconciliation]], weeping openly and providing [[prospero|Prospero]] with the language of redemption: "I have inly wept, / Or should have spoke ere this."

Gonzalo is the character who bridges the play's two moral worlds: the court of [[milan|Milan]]/[[naples|Naples]] (defined by political betrayal) and the island (defined by [[prospero|Prospero]]'s long memory of that betrayal). His goodness is the thread that connects the pre-play past to the reconciled future.

## Key Relationships

- **[[alonso|Alonso]]**: Gonzalo is [[alonso|Alonso]]'s most loyal servant and counsellor. He comforts the king when [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] is presumed drowned—"Beseech you, sir, be merry" (005-001–005-002)—and physically protects him from [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s blade. His unwavering loyalty stands in sharp contrast to [[sebastian|Sebastian]], the king's own brother, who conspires to kill him.

> "The good old lord, Gonzalo; / His tears run down his beard, like winter's drops / From eaves of reeds." — Prospero, (014-020)

- **[[prospero|Prospero]]**: Gonzalo is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s secret benefactor. Without Gonzalo's pro[[dream|vision]] of books and supplies, neither [[prospero|Prospero]] nor [[miranda|Miranda]] would have survived the sea journey. [[prospero|Prospero]] acknowledges this in Act 5:

> "Holy Gonzalo, honourable man, / Mine eyes, even sociable to the show of thine, / Fall fellowly drops." — (014-074)

- **[[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]**: Gonzalo is the constant target of their cruelty. They mock his verbosity, his optimism, and his age—[[sebastian|Sebastian]] scornfully dismisses him as a chatterer "as this Gonzalo" (005-296–005-298). Their plot to murder [[alonso|Alonso]] is enabled only because Gonzalo—exhausted by their abuse—falls asleep. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s intervention through Gonzalo marks the play's moral pivot.

- **[[miranda|Miranda]]**: Though they share no direct scene dialogue, Gonzalo's pro[[dream|vision]]ing of the exile boat saved [[miranda|Miranda]]'s life. [[prospero|Prospero]] recounts this debt in Act 1, Scene 2.

## Key Speeches

> "I' the commonwealth I would by contraries / Execute all things; for no kind of traffic / Would I admit; no name of magistrate; / Letters should not be known; riches, poverty, / And use of service, none; contract, succession, / Bourn, bound of land, tilth, vineyard, none; / No use of metal, corn, or wine, or oil; / No occupation; all men idle, all; / And women too, but innocent and pure; / No sovereignty;—" — (005-150–005-170)

This speech is Gonzalo at his most characteristic: a [[dream|vision]] of utopia so naive that it invites mockery, yet so generous in spirit that it discloses the man's essential goodness. Shakespeare draws on Montaigne's essay "Of Cannibals" for the source material, but the speech functions dramatically as a moral counterpoint to [[antonio|Antonio]]'s cynical realpolitik.

The speech is more than comic relief. Gonzalo's commonwealth is a mirror held up to the corrupt court of [[naples|Naples]], where [[authority|sovereignty]] has become a weapon and traffic a means of exploitation. By imagining a world without magistrates, contracts, or property, Gonzalo [[magic|art]]iculates—however unwittingly—the play's deepest longing for a restored, innocent order. The irony is double: [[sebastian|Sebastian]] and [[antonio|Antonio]] mock Gonzalo for being naive, yet they themselves are plotting regicide while he dreams of peace. The speech thus functions as a structural hinge: Gonzalo's [[dream|vision]] of the ideal commonwealth is interrupted by the intrusion of the real conspiracy, and the collision between the two registers—utopian dream and murderous reality—is one of the play's most concentrated moments of dramatic irony.

## Critical Reception

Frank Kermode, in his Arden edition of the play, identifies Gonzalo as the moral centre of *The Tempest*, arguing that his persistent goodness—even when mocked and imperilled—provides the ethical standard against which all other characters are measured. For Kermode, Gonzalo is not merely a comic old man but the play's [[guilt|conscience]]: the figure whose unshakeable decency makes [[prospero|Prospero]]'s turn toward [[forgiveness|mercy]] both intelligible and earned. Without Gonzalo's prior act of charity in pro[[dream|vision]]ing the exile boat, the entire architecture of the play would collapse; he is, in Kermode's reading, the hidden foundation upon which the drama of redemption rests.

Northrop Frye situates Gonzalo within the utopian tradition that runs from More's *Utopia* through Montaigne to the Renaissance pastoral. In Frye's analysis of Shakespearean comedy, Gonzalo's commonwealth speech represents what he terms the "green world" impulse—the recurrent comic movement toward a transformed society beyond the corruptions of court and city. Frye notes that Gonzalo's [[dream|vision]], though parodied by [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]], is structurally identical to the comic resolution the play ultimately delivers: a return to [[milan|Milan]] where [[authority|sovereignty]] is restored not by force but by [[forgiveness|reconciliation]]. The old counsellor who is mocked for dreaming of an ideal commonwealth turns out, in the play's moral economy, to have been speaking the play's own deepest truth.

Stephen Greenblatt reads Gonzalo's commonwealth speech in the context of early modern European political thought, p[[magic|art]]icularly the discourse surrounding New World colonisation. For Greenblatt, Gonzalo's fantasy of a society without [[authority|sovereignty]], labour, or property is not merely a literary allusion to Montaigne but a substantive engagement with the period's political philosophy—a radical imagining that exposes the coercive foundations of European governance even as it is voiced by a loyal servant of that very order. Greenblatt observes that the speech's cancellation by the conspiracy plot enacts, in miniature, the play's larger argument about the fragility of utopian thought in a world governed by [[power|power]] and self-interest.
