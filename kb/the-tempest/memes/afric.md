# Afric

## Overview

Afric (Africa) is referenced in Act II, Scene I of *The Tempest* as the geographical setting for the marriage of [[claribel|Claribel]], [[alonso|Alonso]]'s daughter, to [[tunis|the King of Tunis]]. The reference anchors the play's action in the Mediterranean world and provides the backstory for the court p[[magic|art]]y's [[fortune|fate]]ful sea journey.

## The Marriage in Afric

[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] recalls the circumstances of the voyage, noting that the court p[[magic|art]]y's garments are "now as fresh as when we put them on first in Afric, at the marriage of the king's fair daughter [[claribel|Claribel]] to [[tunis|the King of Tunis]]" (005-059). This line establishes that the ship was returning from a diplomatic wedding on the African continent when it encountered [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[storm|tempest]].

## Political and Thematic Significance

The marriage in Afric represents [[alonso|Alonso]]'s Mediterranean diplomacy — an attempt to secure political alliances through family bonds. However, the journey home from this African wedding proves catastrophic, leading to the apparent loss of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and the crisis of succession that [[sebastian|Sebastian]] attempts to exploit. The reference to Afric thus serves as the geographical origin point of the play's political subplot.

## Claribel's African Fate

[[sebastian|Sebastian]] cruelly remarks that [[alonso|Alonso]] "would not bless our Europe with your daughter, / But rather lose her to an African" (005-105), using the geographical distance to emphasise [[claribel|Claribel]]'s removal from European dynastic politics. This racialised language — contrasting "Europe" with "African" — reflects the period's cultural attitudes and contributes to the play's exploration of the relationship between the Mediterranean centre and its perceived peripheries.
