## Overview

Prospero's Renunciation of Magic is the emotional and moral climax of *The Tempest*. It occurs in Act 5, Scene 1, immediately after [[ariel|Ariel]] reports that the royal p[[magic|art]]y is "brimful of sorrow and dismay" (014-029) and prompts [[prospero|Prospero]] toward compassion: "Your charm so strongly works 'em, / That if you now beheld them, your affections / Would become tender" (014-030–031). [[prospero|Prospero]]'s response — "Hast thou, which [[magic|art]] but air, a touch, a feeling / Of their affections?" (014-032–033) — acknowledges [[ariel|the spirit]]'s moral perception while holding firm to the hierarchy of man and elemental being. Yet [[ariel|Ariel]]'s prompting works. [[prospero|Prospero]] declares "the rarer action is / In virtue than in vengeance" (014-036–037) and, in a long, incantatory speech that echoes Ovid and gestures toward the metaphysical architecture of Renaissance magic, announces that he will "abjure" his "rough magic" (014-062), break his staff, and drown his book "deeper than did ever plummet sound" (014-067–068).

The stakes of the renunciation extend far beyond the plot. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic has been the engine of the entire play: the [[the-shipwreck|storm]], the invisible [[music|music]], [[ariel|the harpy]]'s accusation, the [[the-masque|masque]] — every spectacular moment has depended on his [[magic|art]]. To renounce that [[magic|art]] is to surrender not merely a set of technical capacities but an entire identity. [[prospero|Prospero]] has been the magus-king of the [[island|island]], [[authority|command]]ing [[ariel|Ariel]], disciplining [[caliban|Caliban]], and orchestrating the movements of every other character. By drowning his book and breaking his staff, he chooses to become, once again, merely the Duke of [[milan|Milan]] — a mortal man among mortals, his [[power|authority]] now political rather than supernatural. This choice is the most consequential action in the play: it enables the forgiveness of [[alonso|Alonso]], the restoration of the dukedom, the freeing of [[ariel|Ariel]], and ultimately the Epilogue's direct appeal to the audience.

The renunciation has also, for over four centuries, been read as Shakespeare's own farewell to the stage. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "rough magic" — the power to conjure storms and [[dream|vision]]s, to make spirits speak and vanish — is also the power of the theatre. When Prospero drowns his book, he performs, in miniature, what Shakespeare himself would do shortly after completing *The Tempest*: retire from the Globe, leave London, and return to Stratford as a private citizen. The biographical reading is not necessary to the play's integrity, but it is nearly impossible to resist: the play's final speech, spoken by [[prospero|Prospero]] in the Epilogue, is also an actor's plea for applause, blurring the boundary between magician and playwright, [[island|island]] and stage, character and man.

## Context

The renunciation is prepared by the entire architecture of the play, but its immediate catalysts are concentrated in the opening moments of Act 5. [[prospero|Prospero]] enters "in his magic robes" (014-001) — the costume that signifies his identity as magus — and receives [[ariel|Ariel]]'s account of the royal p[[magic|art]]y's condition. [[alonso|Alonso]], [[antonio|Antonio]], and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] are "distracted" and "cannot budge" (014-016–017); [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] weeps; "[[gonzalo|the good old lord]]" is the one figure whose innocence has earned him exemption from punishment. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s observation that [[prospero|Prospero]] "would become tender" if he saw them (014-031) is the moral pivot of the scene — and, arguably, of the entire play. It is [[ariel|the spirit]], not the [[master|master]], who first [[magic|art]]iculates the possibility of [[forgiveness|mercy]].

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s decision has been building across the play's trajectory. The successful execution of his plan — the [[storm|shipwreck]] that brought his enemies to the [[island|island]], the separation of the survivors, the torment of [[alonso|Alonso]] by guilt, the foiling of the assassination plots, the betrothal of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] — leaves him at a crossroads. He has achieved everything he set out to achieve. What remains is the question of what to do with his power now that its purpose is fulfilled. The renunciation is his answer: power that has served its purpose must be surrendered. The choice recapitulates, in reverse, the original error that cost him his dukedom. Twelve years earlier, [[prospero|Prospero]] lost [[milan|Milan]] because he was "transported / And rapt in [[art|secret studies]]" (003-106–107): his absorption in magical knowledge created the vacuum his brother [[antonio|Antonio]] filled. By renouncing magic at the moment of political restoration, [[prospero|Prospero]] corrects the imbalance that defined his life — he chooses governance over [[knowledge|study]], community over solitude, the human over the supernatural.

## Dramatic Function

The renunciation occupies the structural position of greatest dramatic weight in Act 5. It is the hinge between the play's two halves: before it, [[prospero|Prospero]] acts through magic; after it, he acts as a man. Every subsequent event — the revelation of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]], the forgiveness of the court p[[magic|art]]y, the freeing of [[ariel|Ariel]], the Epilogue — depends on and follows from this act of surrender. Shakespeare places the speech at the centre of the final act, surrounding it with the solemn stage direction "Solemn [[music|music]]" (014-068) and the re-entry of the charmed courtiers, whose frozen en[[dream|trance]] into [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic circle marks the boundary between the world of [[magic|enchantment]] and the world of restored humanity.

The intellectual background of the renunciation is the Renaissance distinction between two kinds of magic: *magia* (natural or white magic, grounded in Neoplatonic philosophy) and *goetia* (black magic, demonic invocation). Marsilio Ficino and Pico della Mirandola had argued, in the fifteenth century, that the learned magus does not [[authority|command]] demons but exercises a purified understanding of [[nature|nature]]'s hidden sympathies — a discipline as much intellectual as operational, a kind of applied philosophy that ascends through knowledge toward divine understanding. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "[[magic|art]]" has been consistently presented in these terms: his magic is book-learned ("my library was dukedom large enough"), his spirits are bound by knowledge rather than demonic pact, and his ends — restoration, [[forgiveness|reconciliation]], justice — are morally defensible. But the play's profound move is to suggest that even *magia*, even white magic, is incompatible with full humanity. [[prospero|Prospero]] does not renounce his [[magic|art]] because it is evil; he renounces it because it has become a barrier between himself and the ordinary human life he must now reclaim. This is a more radical claim than the simple opposition of white and black magic: it suggests that all supernatural power, however benevolent, is a form of exile from the human condition.

The Epilogue extends the renunciation into the theatrical space and, in doing so, completes its dramatic function. [[prospero|Prospero]], now stripped of magic, steps forward and addresses the audience directly: "Now my charms are all o'erthrown, / And what strength I have's mine own, / Which is most faint" (015-002–004). The magus who [[authority|command]]ed spirits is now a man who must rely on "prayer" and "indulgence" (015-017, 015-021). This is the logical terminus of the renunciation: the surrender of power leads not to weakness but to a different kind of strength — the strength of vulnerability, of asking rather than [[authority|command]]ing. The Epilogue's final couplet — "As you from crimes would [[forgiveness|pardon]]'d be, / Let your indulgence set me free" (015-020–021) — converts the theatrical convention of the epilogue into a theological gesture: the audience's applause becomes an act of grace, and the actor, like his character, is [[freedom|release]]d through the [[forgiveness|mercy]] of others.

The biographical reading of the renunciation — the identification of [[prospero|Prospero]] with Shakespeare — is almost as old as the play's criticism and has been remarkably durable. In this interpretation, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "rough magic" is theatrical [[magic|art]], his staff is the playwright's pen, his book is the collected works, and his return to [[milan|Milan]] is Shakespeare's retirement to Stratford around 1611. *The Tempest* was among the last plays Shakespeare wrote alone, and the sense of an ending — of a career reaching its culmination and its close — pervades the final act. Whether or not Shakespeare consciously intended the identification, the structural parallels are compelling: a man who has spent his life creating worlds through [[magic|art]] prepares to set that [[magic|art]] aside and re-enter the ordinary world. The renunciation speech is, on this reading, the most personal passage Shakespeare ever wrote — not a confession, but a valediction.

## Key Passages

> "My charms I'll break, their senses I'll restore, / And they shall be themselves." (014-040–041)

This couplet is the first declaration of intent in the renunciation sequence. [[prospero|Prospero]] announces two distinct actions — breaking his charms and restoring his enemies' senses — that are bound together as cause and effect. The phrase "they shall be themselves" carries enormous weight: for the duration of the play, the court p[[magic|art]]y have been under [[prospero|Prospero]]'s spell, their perceptions controlled, their emotions manipulated. To restore them to themselves is to restore them to moral agency — to make them capable, for the first time since [[the-shipwreck|the storm]], of genuine repentance rather than magically-induced terror. The line also glances at [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own condition: in renouncing magic, he too will become "himself" — not the magus but the duke, not the [[magic|art]]ist but the man.

> "But this rough magic / I here abjure." (014-062)

The word "abjure" is the most precise verb in the renunciation speech and carries a specific theological weight. To abjure is not merely to give up but to renounce under oath — it is the language of heresy trials, of formal recantation, of binding legal and spiritual commitment. By choosing this word, [[prospero|Prospero]] frames his renunciation not as a casual decision but as a solemn act with binding force. The epithet "rough" complicates the gesture: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic has been anything but crude — it has produced the exquisite [[music|music]] of [[ariel|Ariel]], the cosmic spectacle of [[ariel|the harpy]], the intricate pageantry of the masque. Calling it "rough" acknowledges that even the most refined [[magic|art]] operates on the materials of the world with a kind of violence, imposing form on what would otherwise be formless. It is an admission, in a single word, that [[magic|art]] is not innocent.

> "I'll break my staff, / Bury it certain fathoms in the earth, / And deeper than did ever plummet sound / I'll drown my book." (014-066–068)

These four lines constitute the most famous image of renunciation in Shakespeare — and one of the most famous in all of drama. The staff and the book are the two instruments of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]]: the staff directs, [[authority|command]]s, and channels power; the book stores, preserves, and transmits knowledge. Destroying them is not symbolic but absolute — the staff is broken, the book is drowned "deeper than did ever plummet sound," beyond any possibility of recovery. The hyperbole of the final image ("deeper than did ever plummet sound") is deliberate: [[prospero|Prospero]] is not merely putting magic aside for a time but annihilating its instruments entirely. There will be no going back. The staff and the book are [[prospero|Prospero]]'s counterp[[magic|art]]s to [[sycorax|the witch]]'s cauldron or the sorcerer's ring — the material objects in which supernatural power resides — and their destruction is the irreducible physical act at the he[[magic|art]] of the renunciation.

> "Now my charms are all o'erthrown, / And what strength I have's mine own, / Which is most faint." (015-002–004)

Spoken at the opening of the Epilogue, these lines complete the arc of the renunciation. [[prospero|Prospero]], who once boasted that " graves at my [[authority|command]] / Have waked their sleepers" (014-059–060), now confesses that his own strength is "most faint." This is not false modesty but theological precision: in the world of the play, the power to [[authority|command]] spirits and work charms was never [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own — it was lent to him by his [[magic|art]], and when the [[magic|art]] is surrendered, what remains is ordinary human frailty. The word "faint" carries connotations of weakness, weariness, and fading — the magus is dimming like the "insubstantial pageant" he described in Act 4. But the line also contains an implicit claim: the strength that is "mine own," however faint, is more real, more human, more valuable than the borrowed power it replaces.

> "As you from crimes would pardon'd be, / Let your indulgence set me free." (015-020–021)

The final couplet of the Epilogue, and thus the final lines [[prospero|Prospero]] speaks in the play. They draw on the Lord's Prayer ("forgive us our trespasses, as we forgive those who trespass against us") to collapse the distance between theatre and congregation, actor and penitent. [[prospero|Prospero]], having [[forgiveness|pardon]]ed everyone in the play, now asks the audience to [[forgiveness|pardon]] him — and the [[forgiveness|pardon]] takes the material form of applause. "Indulgence" is a word dense with theological resonance: in the Catholic tradition, an indulgence is a remission of temporal punishment for sin, granted by the Church. By asking the audience for "indulgence," [[prospero|Prospero]] invests the theatrical convention of the curtain call with the weight of a sacramental act. The renunciation of magic becomes, in this final gesture, a transaction of [[forgiveness|mercy]] between performer and spectator.

## Characters Involved

- **[[prospero]]** — The former sorcerer who voluntarily surrenders his power. His journey from magus to mortal is the moral spine of Act 5 and the precondition for every [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] that follows.
- **[[ariel]]** — [[prospero|Prospero]]'s spirit servant, whose moral prompting — "your affections / Would become tender" (014-030–031) — triggers the renunciation. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s freedom, long promised, is finally granted after the staff is broken and the book drowned.
- **[[alonso]]** — The King of [[naples|Naples]], chief among the captives restored to their senses. His [[guilt|penitence]] makes him the principal beneficiary of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness, and his restoration of [[milan|Milan]] to [[prospero|Prospero]] completes the political arc of the renunciation.
- **[[gonzalo]]** — The honest old councillor, whose unwavering goodness through the play's trials is vindicated when the spell is lifted. He is the moral witness to the renunciation's effects.

## Critical Notes

The most influential reading of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s renunciation was [[magic|art]]iculated by **Frank Kermode** in his landmark Arden edition of *The Tempest* (1954). For Kermode, the abjuration speech is the culmination of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s career as a Renaissance magus in the Neoplatonic tradition: the learned practitioner of *magia naturalis* who has ascended through knowledge toward divine understanding and now, having reached the summit of his [[magic|art]], voluntarily lays it aside. Kermode reads the renunciation not as a repudiation of evil but as a graduation — the student completing his curriculum and outgrowing his instruments. Central to Kermode's reading is the famous identification of [[prospero|Prospero]] with Shakespeare: the "rough magic" that [[prospero|Prospero]] abjures is also theatrical [[magic|art]], and the speech is Shakespeare's own farewell to the stage. Kermode noted the speech's deep echoes of Ovid (Medea's incantation in *Metamorphoses* Book 7, filtered through Golding's translation), arguing that Shakespeare uses the classical source to elevate [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic into a tradition as old as poetry itself — only to have [[prospero|Prospero]] transcend that tradition in the act of renouncing it. For Kermode, the play's final movement — from magic to prayer, from [[authority|command]] to request, from [[magic|art]] to grace — is not a diminishment but a paradoxically triumphant conclusion: [[prospero|Prospero]] gains more by surrendering power than he ever did by wielding it.

**Stephen Greenblatt**, writing in the context of new historicism (*Renaissance Self-Fashioning*, 1980), approaches the renunciation from a different angle. For Greenblatt, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s abjuration is not a spiritual graduation but an act of political self-fashioning — a strategic performance that enables the magus to re-enter the structures of legitimate [[power|authority]] from which his exile and his [[magic|art]] have excluded him. [[magic|Magic]], in Greenblatt's analysis, is a technology of power that operates through spectacle, manipulation, and coercion — and it is, in the end, incompatible with the forms of social and political order [[prospero|Prospero]] seeks to reclaim. The renunciation is therefore a transfer rather than a surrender: [[prospero|Prospero]] ex[[transformation|change]]s magical [[power|authority]] for political [[power|authority]], trading the absolute but illegitimate power of the [[island|island]] for the constrained but recognized [[authority|legitimacy]] of the dukedom. Greenblatt also connects the renunciation to the colonial dimensions of the play: just as European colonial power often represented itself as renouncing overt force in favour of "civilized" governance — while retaining the structures of domination that force had established — [[prospero|Prospero]]'s theatrical abjuration masks the continuity of his power. The duke who breaks his staff in Act 5 is the same man who threatened [[ariel|Ariel]] with imprisonment in an oak tree and who holds [[caliban|Caliban]] in perpetual [[servitude|bondage]]; the renunciation, in this reading, is a performance of virtue that leaves the underlying structures of domination intact.

## Aftermath

With his magic renounced, [[prospero|Prospero]] draws the charmed courtiers into his circle, forgives [[alonso|Alonso]] warmly and [[antonio|Antonio]] coldly, reveals [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] alive and betrothed, and [[authority|command]]s [[ariel|Ariel]] to calm the seas for the voyage home. He gives [[ariel|Ariel]] his long-promised freedom — "Then to the elements / Be free, and fare thou well!" (014-316–317) — and, in the Epilogue, stands before the audience as a powerless mortal, asking for their applause to set him free. He leaves his magical powers behind on the [[island|island]], buried and drowned beyond recovery. The gesture blurs the boundary between character and actor, magic and theatre, [[magic|art]] and life — a farewell that has resonated for more than four centuries as Shakespeare's own.
