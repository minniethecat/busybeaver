## Overview

Antonio is the play's agent of original wrong — the brother who deposed [[prospero|Prospero]], seized [[milan|Milan]], and set the exiled duke adrift with his infant daughter. His usurpation, narrated by [[prospero|Prospero]] in the long exposition of the second scene, is the founding crime from which all subsequent action flows. "In my false brother / Awaked an evil [[nature|nature]]," [[prospero|Prospero]] recalls (003-093–094), describing how Antonio, entrusted with "the manage of my state" (003-072), grew to "believe / He was indeed the duke" (003-105–106). Confiding "the inmost workings of his breast" (003-003) in Antonio was [[prospero|Prospero]]'s fatal error — the seed of betrayal planted in trust.

Unlike [[alonso|Alonso]], whose guilt enables his redemption, Antonio never expresses [[guilt|remorse]]. [[prospero|Prospero]] forgives him — "I do forgive thee, / Unnatural though thou [[magic|art]]" (014-090–091) — and Antonio says nothing. This silence is the play's most unsettling moral fact: forgiveness offered and refused, or at least unacknowledged. Antonio represents the possibility that some wrongs cannot be repaired, some [[nature|nature]]s cannot be [[transformation|change]]d — the limit case of the play's therapeutic optimism. Where [[caliban|Caliban]] learns to "seek for grace" (015-018) and [[alonso|Alonso]] weeps in [[guilt|penitence]], Antonio stands unmoved, a figure of radical im[[guilt|penitence]] who exposes the fragility of the comic ending.

## Role in the Play

Antonio appears in the opening [[storm|shipwreck]] scene, where he is already established as a voice of arrogance — mocking the [[boatswain|Boatswain]] with "Hang, cur! hang, you whoreson, insolent noise-maker" (002-023). His aggression under pressure contrasts with [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s calm and [[alonso|Alonso]]'s despair, establishing from the first lines a character defined by contempt for those beneath him.

In Act 2, Scene 1, Antonio reveals his [[nature|nature]] fully. Once the courtiers sleep under [[ariel|Ariel]]'s spell — all except [[alonso|Alonso]], [[sebastian|Sebastian]], and Antonio — the usurper begins his most dangerous work. With the king sleeping, Antonio persuades [[sebastian|Sebastian]] to kill [[alonso|Alonso]] and seize [[naples|Naples]], replaying his own usurpation of [[milan|Milan]] in a new key. His argument is chillingly rational: "My strong imagination sees a crown / Dropping upon thy head" (005-208–209). Where [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|power]] is magical, Antonio's is rhetorical — he needs no [[ariel|spirit]], only words, to corrupt. The seduction scene is a masterclass in political manipulation: Antonio neutralises [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s scruples by dismissing [[guilt|conscience]] as a phantom — "Ay, sir; where lies that? if 'twere a kibe, / 'Twould put me to my slipper: but I feel not / This deity in my bosom" (005-314–316) — and offers the cold practicality of his own success as precedent. "Look how well my garments sit upon me; / Much feater than before" (005-310–311), he preens, equating usurpation with s[[magic|art]]orial improvement. Only [[ariel|Ariel]]'s timely intervention — [[sleep|waking]] [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] with a warning [[music|song]] — prevents the murder.

Antonio is largely silent through Acts 3 and 4, present among the courtiers but inert, his conspiracy thw[[magic|art]]ed. His silence in these acts is strategic: the failed plotter withdraws, watching and waiting. In Act 3, Scene 3, he is among the "three men of sin" whom [[ariel|Ariel]]-as-harpy condemns — "you 'mongst men / Being most unfit to live" (010-085–086) — yet even this cosmic accusation draws no response from him.

His return to prominence in Act 5 is marked only by [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness and Antonio's lack of response. [[prospero|Prospero]] names him directly: "You, brother mine, that entertain'd ambition, / Expell'd [[guilt|remorse]] and [[nature|nature]]" (014-087–088) — then forgives: "I do forgive thee, / Unnatural though thou [[magic|art]]" (014-090–091). The stage direction records nothing; the text offers no reply. He does not repent, does not explain, does not even deny. The play ends with Antonio still standing there, unforgiven because unforgiving. His silence has been the subject of extensive critical debate: is it defiance, shame, indifference, or the truest sign that some [[nature|nature]]s lie beyond the reach of [[magic|art]]?

## Key Speeches

> **Ant.** Noble Sebastian,
> Thou let'st thy fortune sleep — die, rather; wink'st
> Whiles thou art waking.
> ...
> O, out of that "no hope"
> What great hope have you! no hope that way is
> Another way so high a hope that even
> Ambition cannot pierce a wink beyond,
> But doubt discovery there. Will you grant with me
> That Ferdinand is drown'd? ...
> Then, tell me,
> Who's the next heir of Naples? ...
> She that is queen of Tunis; she that dwells
> Ten leagues beyond man's life; she that from Naples
> Can have no note, unless the sun were post —
> The man i' the moon's too slow — till new-born chins
> Be rough and razorable ...
> And by that [[fortune|destiny]], to perform an act
> Whereof what's past is prologue; what to come,
> In yours and my discharge.
> ...
> Ay, sir; where lies that? if 'twere a kibe,
> 'Twould put me to my slipper: but I feel not
> This deity in my bosom: twenty consciences,
> That stand 'twixt me and Milan, candied be they,
> And melt, ere they molest!
> — (005-221–223, 261–270, 274–282, 314–318)

This is the play's most dangerous speech — more dangerous than any incantation — because it deploys pure reason in the [[servitude|service]] of evil. Antonio begins by [[sleep|waking]] [[sebastian|Sebastian]] to ambition: "Thou let'st thy fortune sleep — die, rather." He then dismantles the practical obstacle ([[alonso|Alonso]]'s daughter [[claribel|Claribel]], too far away to contest the succession) with the dismissive geography of "ten leagues beyond man's life." The phrase "what's past is prologue" (005-280) — which has entered the language as an aphorism — is here not an assertion of [[fortune|destiny]] but a seduction to crime: Antonio reframes his own usurpation as a precedent [[sebastian|Sebastian]] need only follow. Most chilling is the [[guilt|conscience]] speech, in which Antonio denies the very existence of moral sensation — "I feel not / This deity in my bosom" — and reduces the theological concept of [[guilt|conscience]] to a physical ailment, a chilblain ("kibe") that one can simply remove one's shoe to avoid. The metaphor of "candied" [[guilt|conscience]]s that "melt" before reaching him completes the dehumanisation: Antonio is the man to whom moral scruple is literally inconceivable.

## Relationships

- **[[prospero|Prospero]]**: His brother, whom he betrayed. Their relationship is the play's founding wrong. Antonio's usurpation is narrated by [[prospero|Prospero]] at length in the second scene, establishing the backstory of trust abused and [[power|authority]] seized
- **[[sebastian|Sebastian]]**: His mirror — a younger brother tempted to usurp an older brother's throne, just as Antonio did. The seduction scene in Act 2 replays Antonio's original crime and reveals his talent for corruption
- **[[alonso|Alonso]]**: The King of [[naples|Naples]] whom Antonio persuaded to help overthrow [[prospero|Prospero]]; now Antonio plots against him too, demonstrating that political alliance is, for him, merely a temporary convenience

## Critical Reception

Frank Kermode, in his introduction to the Arden Shakespeare edition of *The Tempest* (1954), identifies Antonio's silence as the play's most deliberately unresolved element. Kermode argues that Antonio represents a "secular, political evil" that neither [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic nor the play's movement toward [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] can accommodate. While the romance genre demands restoration and forgiveness, Antonio's refusal to speak — to repent, to acknowledge, even to deny — refuses the closure the form requires. For Kermode, Antonio is the figure who stands outside the comic arc, exposing the limits of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "project" and the fragility of the happy ending. His silence is not passive but actively resistant: "the one character who cannot be assimilated into the general [[music|harmony]]."

Stephen Greenblatt, in *Renaissance Self-Fashioning* (1980) and subsequent essays on *The Tempest*, reads Antonio as an exemplar of Machiavellian ambition — a "self-fashioned" political operator who achieves [[power|power]] not through inherited [[authority|legitimacy]] or learned [[magic|art]] but through cunning, manipulation, and the ruthless exploitation of opportunity. Greenblatt contrasts Antonio's mode of usurpation with [[prospero|Prospero]]'s: where [[prospero|Prospero]] retreats into [[knowledge|study]] and loses his dukedom through neglect, Antonio acts — seizing the machinery of state, buying allies, and reframing treason as necessity. The temptation of [[sebastian|Sebastian]] thus becomes, in Greenblatt's analysis, a compressed re-enactment of Renaissance political theory's central anxiety: that [[power|authority]], once detached from divine sanction or hereditary right, is merely a function of will and opportunity. Antonio's p[[magic|art]]ing boast — "look how well my garments sit upon me" — is the credo of the successful usurper: [[authority|legitimacy]] is costume, and costume fits whoever dares to wear it.
