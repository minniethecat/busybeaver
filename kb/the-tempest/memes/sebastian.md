## Overview

Sebastian is [[alonso|Alonso]]'s younger brother and the play's second usurper-in-waiting. If [[antonio|Antonio]] is the play's original sinner, Sebastian is his willing disciple—a man whose latent cruelty is catalysed by [[antonio|Antonio]]'s persuasion into active treason. He enters the play as a voice of sarcasm, mocking the [[boatswain|Boatswain]] in [[the-shipwreck|the storm]], [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s optimism in the calm, and his own brother's grief over the presumed death of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]].

His defining act is the conspiracy of Act 2, Scene 1: persuaded by [[antonio|Antonio]] that [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s death places Sebastian next in line for the throne of [[naples|Naples]], he agrees to murder the sleeping [[alonso|Alonso]]. The assassination is thw[[magic|art]]ed only by [[ariel|Ariel]], who wakes [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] in time. The conspiracy mirrors [[antonio|Antonio]]'s own usurpation of [[prospero|Prospero]] twelve years earlier, and Sebastian's willingness to repeat the crime exposes the contagious [[nature|nature]] of political ambition within the play's moral universe.

## Role in the Play

Sebastian first appears in the [[storm|shipwreck]] (002-007), where his sharp tongue is already on display—he tells the labouring [[boatswain|Boatswain]] "A pox o' your throat, you bawling, blasphemous, incharitable dog!" This aggression under crisis is his baseline: Sebastian responds to stress with attack, not reflection.

In Act 2, Scene 1 (005-001–005-370), Sebastian reveals his cruelty in its fullest form. When [[alonso|Alonso]] mourns [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]], Sebastian offers not comfort but blame: "Sir, you may thank yourself for this great loss." He and [[antonio|Antonio]] mock [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] relentlessly, twisting every one of the old counsellor's words into absurdity. The dynamic between the two mockers is itself revealing: [[antonio|Antonio]] leads and Sebastian follows, establishing a hierarchy of wit that prefigures the hierarchy of evil to come. Once [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] falls asleep, [[antonio|Antonio]] whispers the temptation: "My strong imagination sees a crown / Dropping upon thy head" (005-209). Sebastian is initially hesitant—he asks "But, for your [[guilt|conscience]]?" (005-221)—yet his resistance collapses with astonishing speed. His moral descent is shockingly rapid: from scepticism to enthusiastic agreement within a single scene. The conspiratorial dynamic between the two men is one of mutual recognition. [[antonio|Antonio]] sees in Sebastian the same ambition that drove his own usurpation, and Sebastian sees in [[antonio|Antonio]] a model of successful treachery. Their dialogue in this scene is the play's most concentrated anatomy of political evil: [[antonio|Antonio]] provides the precedent ("I did it, and I prospered"), Sebastian supplies the will, and together they draw their swords over the sleeping king (005-285).

In Act 3, Scene 3 (010-001–010-120), Sebastian is among the court p[[magic|art]]y who encounter [[ariel|Ariel]]'s [[harpy|harpy]]-[[dream|vision]] and the disappearing banquet. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s accusation—"You are three men of sin"—includes him equally, and his reaction is notably different from [[alonso|Alonso]]'s: where the king responds with guilt-stricken despair, Sebastian's response is described as one of "fury" rather than [[guilt|remorse]] (010-105). This distinction is crucial to understanding his character: Sebastian does not so much repent as he resents being caught. In Act 5 (014-069–014-088), Sebastian is revealed as "penitent," though his [[guilt|penitence]] is less convincing than [[alonso|Alonso]]'s. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s comment "I do forgive / Thy rankest fault—all of them" (014-131) suggests a forgiveness extended more in hope than in confidence. Sebastian's one-line reply to [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness—"The devil speaks in him" (014-129), muttered about [[antonio|Antonio]]—is perhaps his most characteristic moment: even in the scene of [[forgiveness|reconciliation]], he deflects blame rather than owning it.

## Key Relationships

- **[[alonso|Alonso]]**: As Alonso's brother, Sebastian owes him loyalty; instead he plots to murder him. Their relationship is the play's clearest mirror of the [[prospero|Prospero]]-[[antonio|Antonio]] fraternal betrayal.

> "Sir, you may thank yourself for this great loss, / That would not bless our Europe with your daughter, / But rather loose her to an African." — Sebastian to the grieving Alonso, (005-120)

- **[[antonio|Antonio]]**: The tempter and the tempted. [[antonio|Antonio]] recognises Sebastian as a kindred spirit and provides the rationale, the precedent, and the nerve. Their dynamic echoes Iago and Othello's, though here the seduction is wholly political:

> "Worthy Sebastian?—O, what might?—No more:— / And yet, methinks, I see it in thy face, / What thou shouldst be." — Antonio to Sebastian, (005-205)

- **[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]**: Sebastian treats [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] with unmasked contempt. He sees the old counsellor's kindness as weakness and his optimism as foolishness. The mockery is pointed and personal, and it continues in every scene they share—even after the conspiracy is exposed, Sebastian cannot resist a final sneer at [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s expense in Act 5 (014-015).

- **[[prospero|Prospero]]**: Though they share no direct history, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness of Sebastian in Act 5 is a test of the play's redemptive logic. Sebastian is the character least unequivocally redeemed, and the play leaves open the question of whether [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[forgiveness|mercy]] extends grace or merely restraint.

## Key Speeches

Sebastian has no extended soliloquies, but his dialogue ex[[transformation|change]]s with [[antonio|Antonio]] in Act 2 capture his character through action rather than introspection. His acceptance of the conspiracy is chilling for its brevity:

> "But, for your conscience?" — Sebastian, pressing Antonio to confirm that guilt will not trouble him, (005-221)

The question is not "is this wrong?" but "will I feel bad about it?"—and [[antonio|Antonio]]'s assurance that he will not is sufficient. Sebastian is a character defined by what he lacks: independent moral conviction, original thought, the courage to refuse. His earlier contribution to the conspiracy dialogue—"What stuff is this! How say you? / 'Tis true, my brother's daughter's queen of [[tunis|Tunis]]" (005-178)—reveals the same quality from a different angle: he parrots [[antonio|Antonio]]'s logic back to him almost verbatim, performing understanding rather than arriving at it. This is not a man who is persuaded; it is a man who allows himself to be colonised by another's will. Even his most active moment—drawing his sword alongside [[antonio|Antonio]] over the sleeping [[alonso|Alonso]] (005-285)—is done in silence, as if the act of murder, like the decision to commit it, requires no language of his own.

## Critical Reception

Frank Kermode, the Arden editor of the play, identifies Sebastian as an "unfinished [[antonio|Antonio]]"—a figure whose villainy mirrors the original usurper's but lacks [[antonio|Antonio]]'s self-possession and intellectual independence. For Kermode, the asymmetry between the two conspirators is thematically essential: Sebastian is not so much corrupted as he is annexed, his will absorbed into [[antonio|Antonio]]'s with an ease that reveals the void at his moral centre. This reading places Sebastian within the play's larger meditation on the contagion of evil—the way political ambition replicates itself from one soul to the next, degrading as it spreads.

Stephen Greenblatt approaches the fraternal dynamics of *The Tempest* through the lens of political ambition as repetition compulsion. In Greenblatt's analysis, Sebastian's readiness to re-enact [[antonio|Antonio]]'s crime against [[alonso|Alonso]] demonstrates that the forces driving usurpation are not individual pathologies but structural features of dynastic [[power|power]]. The conspiracy scene is not merely a test of character but an exposure of political logic: once [[antonio|Antonio]] has demonstrated that the unnatural act can be committed with impunity, Sebastian's p[[magic|art]]icipation becomes, in this view, almost structurally inevitable. The playwright, Greenblatt suggests, is less interested in Sebastian's psychology than in the mechanism by which one act of treason generates the next.

Janet Adelman, writing on sibling rivalry in Shakespeare's romances, reads Sebastian within a pattern of fraternal violence that runs through the late plays. Adelman notes that Sebastian occupies a liminal position between the bad brother ([[antonio|Antonio]]) and the good brother ([[prospero|Prospero]]), a position that forces the audience to confront the uncomfortable proximity between legitimate succession and murderous ambition. Sebastian's conspiracy, in Adelman's reading, is less an aberration than an unveiling: it reveals what is always latent in a system where [[power|power]] passes between brothers. The forgiveness scene in Act 5, in which Sebastian's silence is its own form of resistance to redemption, becomes for Adelman the play's most honest moment about the limits of [[forgiveness|reconciliation]].
