## Overview

Knowledge in *The Tempest* is the foundation on which nearly every relationship and power structure rests. It is the source of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]] — his "[[art|secret studies]]" (003-107) gave him [[authority|command]] over [[ariel|Ariel]], the elements, and the very plot of the play — but it is also the cause of his original downfall, as his absorption in those same studies created the vacuum of [[authority|rule]] that [[antonio|Antonio]] filled. Knowledge is what [[prospero|Prospero]] possesses and [[caliban|Caliban]] is taught; it is the currency of gift-ex[[transformation|change]] between [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] and [[prospero|Prospero]] and the contested ground of colonial encounter between European and [[island|island]]er. The play interrogates whether knowledge is inherently liberating — the Renaissance ideal of the contemplative life — or merely another instrument of domination, as postcolonial criticism has argued. Through its treatment of books, language, and education, *The Tempest* stages the central paradox of early modern epistemology: to know is to possess power, but to know deeply is also to invite the betrayal that power attracts.

## In the Play

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s knowledge is book-derived and solitary. "Me, poor man, my library / Was dukedom large enough" (003-145) — the library replaces the state as the object of his devotion. Before his exile, [[prospero|Prospero]] "being transported / And rapt in [[art|secret studies]]" (003-106–107) grew estranged from the political world he was supposed to govern. His knowledge is power, but it is a power that isolates: he loses [[milan|Milan]] through neglect and [[authority|rule]]s the [[island|island]] through magic, in both cases separated from the human community he is supposed to serve. The trajectory from duke to scholar to magician to renunciant traces a lifelong negotiation between knowledge-as-vocation and knowledge-as-retreat-from-the-world.

### The Book as Physical Object

One of the play's most distinctive features is its insistent materiality about knowledge. Books in *The Tempest* are not metaphors; they are physical objects with weight, presence, and vulnerability. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic is not an innate gift but a technology dependent on physical volumes — "Knowing I loved my books, he furnish'd me / From mine own library with volumes that / I prize above my dukedom" (003-222–224). The books can be seized, stolen, or destroyed. [[caliban|Caliban]] understands this perfectly: his instructions to [[stephano|Stephano]] are brutally practical: "Remember / First to possess his books; for without them / He's but a sot, as I am, nor hath not / One spirit to [[authority|command]]" (009-059–061). The sequence of verbs [[caliban|Caliban]] offers — "brain him," "batter his skull," "paunch him with a stake," "cut his wezand" — culminates in the most lethal of all: "Burn but his books" (009-062). For [[caliban|Caliban]], the books are [[prospero|Prospero]]'s true body; the physical man is secondary. And at the play's climax, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s renunciation is itself a physical act directed at material objects: "I'll break my staff, / Bury it certain fathoms in the e[[magic|art]]h, / And deeper than did ever plummet sound / I'll drown my book" (014-065–068). The book is submerged in the sea that brought [[prospero|Prospero]] to the [[island|island]] — knowledge returned to the element from which his exile began.

### Miranda's Education and Caliban's Education: Two Models of Knowledge Transfer

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s claims about education serve as his moral self-vindication, yet the play systematically exposes the asymmetries in how knowledge is distributed. To [[miranda|Miranda]], he declares: "Here in this [[island|island]] we arrived; and here / Have I, thy schoolmaster, made thee more profit / Than other princesses can" (003-231–233). The claim is one of benevolent paternal transmission — the father-scholar passing wisdom to the daughter-pupil. But the curriculum has been entirely under [[prospero|Prospero]]'s control, and [[miranda|Miranda]]'s knowledge serves [[prospero|Prospero]]'s narrative: she knows her father's version of their history, his version of [[antonio|Antonio]]'s betrayal, his version of [[caliban|Caliban]]'s depravity. Her education is a closed circuit.

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s education follows the same model — [[prospero|Prospero]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] boast of having taught him language, "I pitied thee, / Took pains to make thee speak" — but the outcome is radically different. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s reply redefines the gift as a weapon: "You taught me language; and my profit on't / Is, I know how to curse" (003-492). Where [[miranda|Miranda]]'s education produces grateful compliance, [[caliban|Caliban]]'s produces resistance. The same pedagogical method yields opposite results because the power relations are opposite: [[miranda|Miranda]] is the beloved daughter of the coloniser; [[caliban|Caliban]] is the dispossessed native whose [[island|island]] has been taken. Knowledge here is not liberation but a technology of colonial domination — and, paradoxically, of anti-colonial resistance. The colonised subject learns to use the coloniser's tools against him.

### Gonzalo's Provision: Knowledge as Gift and Survival

The counter-example to knowledge-as-domination is [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]. Alone among [[court-of-naples|the Neapolitan court]], [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] acts out of disinterested charity. When [[prospero|Prospero]] and the infant [[miranda|Miranda]] were cast adrift, [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] secretly supplied their boat not only with "rich garments, linens, stuffs and necessaries" (003-220) but — crucially — with books from [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own library: "Knowing I loved my books, he furnish'd me / From mine own library with volumes that / I prize above my dukedom" (003-222–224). The act is doubly significant. It is an act of intimate knowledge — [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] understood what [[prospero|Prospero]] valued most — and an act of material survival, since without those books [[prospero|Prospero]] would have had no magic, no power over [[ariel|Ariel]], and no means of engineering the [[storm|tempest]] that brings his enemies to the [[island|island]]. [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s gift of knowledge is the play's hidden foundation: every subsequent event depends on it. In Act 5, [[prospero|Prospero]] acknowledges this debt: "Holy [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]], honourable man" (014-074). Knowledge given freely, without the will to dominate, becomes the condition of possibility for the play's entire action — and ultimately for [[forgiveness|reconciliation]].

### Ariel's Instrumental Knowledge

[[ariel|Ariel]]'s knowledge is operational — he knows how to execute [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|command]]s, how to manipulate the elements, how to appear as a [[harpy|harpy]] or a [[nymph|nymph]], how to sing sailors into [[sleep|sleep]] and nobles into madness. His knowledge is entirely in [[servitude|service]]; he has no independent intellectual life, no books of his own, no claim to autonomous thought. This instrumentalisation of knowledge mirrors [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own trajectory from scholar to magician: knowledge in *The Tempest*, even at its most powerful, tends toward instrumentality. It is always knowledge *for* something — for control, for resistance, for survival — never knowledge for its own sake, despite [[prospero|Prospero]]'s nostalgia for the library that was his "dukedom large enough."

## Key Passages

> Me, poor man, my library
> Was dukedom large enough.
> — (003-145)

The library as substitute kingdom. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s confession is both proud ("large enough") and self-indicting — the knowledge that empowered him also impoverished his [[authority|rule]]. The word "poor" carries a complex irony: he is poor in political wisdom even as he is rich in book-learning.

> Knowing I loved my books, he furnish'd me
> From mine own library with volumes that
> I prize above my dukedom.
> — (003-222–224)

[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s gift of books is the play's foundational act of grace. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s declaration that he prizes these volumes "above my dukedom" redefines value itself: the books are worth more than the political entity whose loss set the entire plot in motion. Knowledge, in this formulation, is literally more precious than [[authority|sovereignty]].

> You taught me language; and my profit on't
> Is, I know how to curse.
> — (003-492)

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s retort reframes knowledge as a transfer that benefits the giver, not the receiver — except insofar as the receiver can repurpose the gift for resistance. This is the most politically charged moment in the play's treatment of knowledge: language, the supposed marker of civilisation, is revealed as a weapon that can be turned against its wielder.

> Remember
> First to possess his books; for without them
> He's but a sot, as I am, nor hath not
> One spirit to command.
> — (009-059–061)

[[caliban|Caliban]] demonstrates a precise understanding of the material basis of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s power. The books are not symbols; they are the physical source of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s ability to [[authority|command]] spirits. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s strategic intelligence here — identifying the critical vulnerability in the enemy's power structure — complicates any simple reading of him as unthinking brute. He knows exactly what must be destroyed.

> I'll break my staff,
> Bury it certain fathoms in the earth,
> And deeper than did ever plummet sound
> I'll drown my book.
> — (014-065–068)

The renunciation of knowledge is a physical act. The staff is buried in e[[magic|art]]h, the book drowned in the sea — the two elements that bookend the play's geography. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic, which depended on material objects, must be unmade through material gestures. The drowning of the book is a kind of funeral: knowledge that has served its purpose is returned to the depths, making way for [[prospero|Prospero]]'s re-entry into ordinary human life as the restored Duke of [[milan|Milan]].

## Critical Context

Frank Kermode, in his landmark Arden edition of *The Tempest* (1954), reads [[prospero|Prospero]]'s knowledge as an embodiment of Renaissance learning in its highest form — the ideal of the *vita contemplativa*, the [[music|harmony]] between natural philosophy and moral wisdom that the humanist tradition prized. For Kermode, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "[[art|secret studies]]" represent not merely magic but the full scope of liberal knowledge: the [[magic|art]]s of grammar, rhetoric, logic, and natural philosophy that constituted the educated mind of the period. Kermode's [[prospero|Prospero]] is a tragic scholar whose absorption in learning is both his greatness and his flaw — a figure who embodies the Renaissance tension between the active and contemplative lives, and whose final drowning of the book represents not a rejection of knowledge but a recognition that knowledge must ultimately serve human community. The play, in Kermode's reading, is a meditation on the proper ends of learning: knowledge pursued for its own sake leads to exile, while knowledge placed in the [[servitude|service]] of justice and [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] leads to restoration.

Stephen Greenblatt, writing from the New Historicist perspective in *Learning to Curse* (1990) and *Marvelous Possessions* (1991), radically reframes [[prospero|Prospero]]'s knowledge as a technology of colonial power. Greenblatt takes [[caliban|Caliban]]'s "you taught me language" speech as the key to the play's epistemology: language and education, far from being neutral gifts of civilisation, function as instruments of domination — "the coloniser's gift that is no gift but an invitation to p[[magic|art]]icipate in one's own subjugation." [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "[[art|secret studies]]," in this reading, are the intellectual counterp[[magic|art]] to European colonial expropriation: the [[servitude|mastery]] of [[nature|nature]] through knowledge parallels the [[servitude|mastery]] of peoples through conquest. Greenblatt notes the play's insistent linking of knowledge with physical coercion — [[prospero|Prospero]] teaches [[caliban|Caliban]] language while keeping him penned in a rock; the books that represent learning are the same books [[caliban|Caliban]] must destroy to be free. Knowledge, for Greenblatt, is never innocent in *The Tempest*: it is always situated within structures of power, always deployed by someone against someone else.

Jonathan Bate, in *The Genius of Shakespeare* (1997) and *Soul of the Age* (2008), approaches the question from the angle of Shakespeare's own bibliographic imagination. Bate observes that *The Tempest* is Shakespeare's most bookish play — the only one in which a physical book functions as a central prop, the source of the protagonist's power, and the object of the antagonist's destructive desire. For Bate, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s relationship to his library reflects Shakespeare's own relationship to his sources: [[prospero|the magician]] who conjures worlds from volumes is a figure for the playwright who transforms chronicles, romances, and travel narratives into dramatic [[magic|art]]. Bate reads [[prospero|Prospero]]'s drowning of his book as an act of authorial valediction — Shakespeare, through [[prospero|Prospero]], bids farewell not only to magic but to the book-learning that had sustained his entire career. The gesture is simultaneously an ending (the book is destroyed) and a liberation (the book's power is relinquished), modelling a relationship to knowledge in which wisdom means knowing when to let the book go.
