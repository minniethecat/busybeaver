## Overview

Caliban is the most fiercely debated character in *The Tempest* — simultaneously monster and poet, slave and native, comic butt and tragic figure. Son of [[sycorax|the witch]] [[sycorax|Sycorax]], he claims the island as his inheritance: "this island's mine, by [[sycorax|Sycorax]] my mother, / Which thou takest from me" (003-455–456). [[prospero|Prospero]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] describe him as irredeemably base — "a devil, a born devil, on whose nature / Nurture can never stick" (012-233–234) — yet Caliban is given some of the play's most beautiful verse: "[[island|the isle]] is full of noises, / Sounds and sweet airs, that give delight and hurt not" (009-098–099). This doubleness — brute and aesthete, rebel and dupe — makes Caliban irreducible to any single reading. He is the colonised subject, the repressed id, the unassimilable other, and the voice of the island itself.

No character in Shakespeare divides criticism more sharply. For the Romantics, Caliban was a "creature of the elements" whose language testified to a soul trapped in a misshapen body. For postcolonial critics, he is the paradigmatic native — displaced, enslaved, taught the master's language only to curse in it. Performance history traces an arc from comic grotesque (Restoration farce) through Darwinian primitivism (Victorian) to dignified revolutionary (postcolonial productions). Caliban's capacity to absorb radically contradictory interpretations without being exhausted by any of them is the measure of his complexity. He is the figure through which *The Tempest* asks its hardest questions: about [[nature]] and [[nature|nurture]], [[servitude]] and [[freedom|liberty]], [[knowledge|language]] and power.

## Role in the Play

Caliban enters in Act 1, Scene 2 (003-330), cursing [[prospero|Prospero]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] for his enslavement. His backstory — educated by [[prospero|Prospero]], then condemned to servitude after attempting to rape [[miranda|Miranda]] — establishes the play's central colonial dynamic, but the testimony is contradictory. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s version casts Caliban as ungrateful savage: "I have used thee, / Filth as thou [[magic|art]], with human care" (003-471–472), and the attempted rape is the justification for enslavement. Caliban's counter-narrative insists the island was stolen and that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s kindness was a prelude to dispossession: "When thou camest first, / Thou strokedst me, and madest much of me … and then I loved thee" (003-456–460). The language Caliban was given becomes his weapon: "You taught me language; and my profit on't / Is, I know how to curse" (003-491–492). This ex[[transformation|change]] is the play's colonial encounter in miniature — a struggle over land, language, and the [[power|authority]] to narrate.

In Act 2 (006-001–006-100), Caliban encounters [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] and transfers his allegiance. [[trinculo|Trinculo]]'s first sight of Caliban — "a strange fish" (006-023) — begins a sequence of dehumanising descriptions. Caliban's willingness to worship [[stephano|the drunken butler]] — "I'll kiss thy foot: I prithee, be my god" (006-068) — is both comic and pitiable: he ex[[transformation|change]]s one master for another because the structure of [[servitude|mastery]] is all he knows.

The conspiracy plot (Act 3–4) sees Caliban urging [[stephano|Stephano]] to kill [[prospero|Prospero]]. His instructions are precise and strategic: "Remember / First to possess his books; for without them / He's but a sot, as I am" (009-058–060). Caliban understands [[prospero|Prospero]]'s power better than his fellow conspirators do — the books are the source of the magic, and seizing them is the precondition of freedom. The failure of this rebellion — the conspirators are chased by spirit-dogs in (012-220–280) — is the play's most savage comedy, but Caliban's desire for freedom is not itself ridiculous.

In Act 5 (014-330–372), [[prospero|Prospero]] acknowledges Caliban with the morally freighted phrase: "this thing of darkness I / Acknowledge mine" (014-350–351). The word "thing" denies humanity; the possessive "mine" accepts responsibility. Caliban's final speech — "I'll be wise hereafter, / And seek for grace" (014-369–370) — has been read as repentance, as strategic submission, and as the moment a colonised subject learns to dissemble before power. The play does not resolve which.

## Key Speeches

> This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother,
> Which thou takest from me. When thou camest first,
> Thou strokedst me, and madest much of me; wouldst give me
> Water with berries in't; and teach me how
> To name the bigger light, and how the less,
> That burn by day and night: and then I loved thee,
> And show'd thee all the qualities o' th' isle,
> The fresh springs, brine-pits, barren place and fertile:
> Curs'd be I that did so! All the charms
> Of Sycorax, toads, beetles, bats, light on you!
> For I am all the subjects that you have,
> Which first was mine own king: and here you sty me
> In this hard rock, whiles you do keep from me
> The rest o' th' island.
> — (003-455–468)

Caliban's first sustained speech is an act of testimony — the counter-narrative to [[prospero|Prospero]]'s version of events. The possessive "mine" opens it; the catalogue of the island's features ("fresh springs, brine-pits, barren place and fertile") establishes his intimate knowledge of a land he claims by inheritance. The turn from love ("I loved thee") to curse ("Curs'd be I that did so!") is the emotional arc of the colonial encounter: trust extended, betrayed, and converted into rage. The final image — "here you sty me / In this hard rock" — reduces Caliban to a penned animal, the "sty" both a physical confinement and a metaphor for the condition of enslavement.

> Be not afeard; the isle is full of noises,
> Sounds and sweet airs, that give delight, and hurt not.
> Sometimes a thousand twangling instruments
> Will hum about mine ears; and sometime voices,
> That, if I then had waked after long sleep,
> Will make me sleep again: and then, in dreaming,
> The clouds methought would open, and show riches
> Ready to drop upon me; that, when I waked,
> I cried to dream again.
> — (009-098–106)

The most beautiful passage in *The Tempest* is spoken by its supposed monster. The speech describes an island that is itself [[music|music]]al — a soundscape of "twangling instruments" and voices that precede and exceed [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic. Caliban's dream-[[dream|vision]] of clouds p[[magic|art]]ing to reveal riches is a fantasy of plenitude and gentleness that stands in radical contrast to the violence he is simultaneously plotting. The final line — "I cried to dream again" — is among the most poignant in Shakespeare: a creature defined by others as subhuman longs for a world more beautiful than the one he inhabits. The speech has been read as evidence of Caliban's innate sensibility (the Romantic view), as a symptom of his susceptibility to colonial fantasy (the postcolonial view), and as the play's deepest expression of the human desire for transcendence.

> Ay, that I will; and I'll be wise hereafter,
> And seek for grace. What a thrice-double ass
> Was I, to take this drunkard for a god,
> And worship this dull fool!
> — (014-369–372)

Caliban's final speech is four lines, and its ambiguity is the ambiguity of the character entire. "I'll be wise hereafter" can be heard as genuine repentance — the monster who would murder his master now seeks grace — or as the strategic accommodation of a defeated rebel who has learned that open resistance fails. The self-reproach ("What a thrice-double ass / Was I") is directed not at his attempt on [[prospero|Prospero]]'s life but at his choice of co-conspirators — a moral calculus that complicates the repentance reading. Caliban does not say he was wrong to rebel; he says he was foolish in his choice of allies. The play refuses to resolve whether this is conversion or survival.

## Critical Reception

Caliban criticism is a map of shifting Western attitudes toward race, colonialism, and humanity. The character has been interpreted as a missing link (Darwinian), a noble savage (Romantic), a soul trapped in flesh (Coleridge), the colonial subject (postcolonial), and an e[[magic|art]]h-spirit (mythopoeic). No single reading exhausts him, and the history of Caliban criticism is itself a primary document in the history of ideas.

**Frank Kermode**, in the introduction to the second Arden edition (1954), identified the central interpretive problem: Caliban is "a being whose very humanity is contested — by [[prospero|Prospero]], by the other characters, and by the play's own language." Kermode noted that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s description of Caliban as "a born devil" whose "vile race … had that in't which good natures / Could not abide to be with" (003-487–488) employs the vocabulary of innate depravity, yet Caliban's lyrical speech about the island's [[music|music]] contradicts the verdict of a fixed, debased nature. For Kermode, this doubleness was not an inconsistency but the point: Caliban is "the figure through which the play interrogates the boundary between the human and the bestial, and finds it unstable."

**Stephen Greenblatt**, in "Learning to Curse" (1976) and later in *Marvelous Possessions* (1991), read Caliban as a figure of the colonised native whose linguistic situation mirrors that of indigenous peoples in the Americas. Caliban's "You taught me language; and my profit on't / Is, I know how to curse" (003-491–492) became, for Greenblatt, the emblematic expression of colonial linguistic violence: the coloniser gives the colonised a language, but the gift is a form of theft — Caliban speaks [[prospero|Prospero]]'s tongue and has lost his own. Greenblatt argued that Caliban's claim to the island — "this island's mine, by [[sycorax|Sycorax]] my mother" (003-455) — [[magic|art]]iculates a notion of inheritance that the play systematically delegitimates, replacing indigenous title with European political [[power|authority]].

**Ania Loomba**, in *Gender, Race, Renaissance Drama* (1989) and *Shakespeare, Race, and Colonialism* (2002), extended the postcolonial reading by attending to the intersection of race and gender in Caliban's representation. Loomba argued that Caliban's attempted rape of [[miranda|Miranda]] is the linchpin of his racialisation — the threat of sexual violence by the dark-skinned native against the white woman justifies the colonial order — and that the play's language consistently conflates racial difference with sexual danger. She also noted that [[sycorax|Caliban's mother]], [[sycorax|Sycorax]], is herself a colonised figure (banished from [[argier|Algiers]]), making Caliban's claim to the island a claim of indigeneity produced by an earlier colonial expulsion.

**G. Wilson Knight**, in *The Crown of Life* (1947), offered a mythopoeic reading that set Caliban not as colonial subject but as chthonic e[[magic|art]]h-spirit. For Knight, Caliban represents "the e[[magic|art]]h itself — its fertility, its darkness, its resistance to the civilising project." His knowledge of the island's "fresh springs, brine-pits, barren place and fertile" (003-462) is not the knowledge of a guide but of an embodiment: Caliban is the island's genius loci. Knight's reading influenced subsequent ecological criticism that sees in Caliban a figure for the more-than-human world that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s rational magic must master, exploit, and finally relinquish.

The Cambridge edition's Critical Apparatus records several textual cruces relevant to Caliban: the Folio's speech prefixes alternate between "Cal." and "Caliban," producing inconsistencies that editors have interpreted as evidence of re[[dream|vision]]; the stage direction at (014-330) — "Enter [[ariel|Ariel]], driving in Caliban, [[stephano|Stephano]], and [[trinculo|Trinculo]]" — raises questions about Caliban's physical presentation in the final scene that bear directly on the moral reading of his submission.
