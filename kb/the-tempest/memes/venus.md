## Overview

Venus, the Roman goddess of love, does not appear as a character in *The Tempest* — but her deliberate exclusion from [[prospero|Prospero]]'s betrothal masque in Act 4 carries dramatic weight. She is the deity summoned only to be banished, the erotic principle that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s pedagogical [[magic|art]] must exclude from the celebration of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]]'s union (012-107).

## Role in the Play

Venus enters the play negatively, through [[ceres|Ceres]]'s anxious interrogation of [[iris|Iris]] at the st[[magic|art]] of the masque (012-077): are Venus and her son Cupid present? [[ceres|Ceres]] has an ancient grievance against Venus, whose lustful influence enabled Pluto to abduct [[ceres|Ceres]]'s daughter Proserpina. [[iris|Iris]] reassures her: Venus has been kept away — "I met her Deity / Cutting the clouds towards Paphos, and her son / Dove-drawn with her" (012-101–103). [[ceres|Ceres]] then enters the celebration, assured of Venus's absence (012-093).

[[ceres|Ceres]] then explicitly links her willingness to p[[magic|art]]icipate to Venus's absence: "If Venus or her son, as thou dost know, / Do now attend the queen" (012-107–108) would be intolerable. The masque's moral economy is clear: the betrothal blessing is chaste, the deities of erotic desire are banished — Venus is headed toward Paphos, her cult centre on Cyprus (012-115) — and what remains — [[juno|Juno]] (marriage), [[ceres|Ceres]] (fertility), [[iris|Iris]] (the rainbow messenger) — is a sanitised, patriarchal version of love over which [[prospero|Prospero]] exerts total control.

The banishment of Venus is one of the masque's most telling details. It signals that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic — his "[[magic|art]]" — is not merely spectacular but pedagogical: he is teaching [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] what kind of love he expects them to practise, and what kind he will not permit.

## Key Scenes

Venus is referenced exclusively in Act 4, Scene 1, during the masque section. The invocation and banishment take place in a brief ex[[transformation|change]] between [[iris|Iris]] and [[ceres|Ceres]] (approximately lines 012-086 to 012-117) before the goddesses proceed to bless the betrothed couple.

## Relationships

- **Cupid** (son): The god of desire, banished alongside his mother. Dove-drawn, headed toward Paphos — Venus's cult centre on Cyprus.
- **[[ceres|Ceres]]** (enemy): The goddess of agriculture and fertility, whose daughter Proserpina was abducted by Pluto under Venus's influence (012-077). [[ceres|Ceres]] refuses to p[[magic|art]]icipate in any celebration where Venus is present.
- **[[prospero|Prospero]]** (summoner/excluder): Though Venus and [[prospero|Prospero]] never interact directly, it is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic — exercised through [[ariel|Ariel]] and his "meaner fellows" — that both stages the masque and ensures Venus's exclusion from it.

## Key Passages

> *Cer.* Tell me, heavenly bow,
> If Venus or her son, as thou dost know,
> Do now attend the queen? Since they did plot
> The means that dusky Dis my daughter got,
> Her and her blind boy's scandal'd company
> I have forsworn.
>
> (012-107)
