## Overview

The island is the play's sole setting — an "uninhabited island" (P02-002) in the Mediterranean that becomes a stage for competing claims to [[authority|sovereignty]], knowledge, and belonging. It is simultaneously [[prospero|Prospero]]'s prison and his kingdom, [[caliban|Caliban]]'s stolen inheritance, [[ariel|Ariel]]'s place of servitude, and a "brave new world" (014-231) to the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed court of [[naples|Naples]]. The island's ambiguous status — neither fully natural nor entirely under [[prospero|Prospero]]'s control, peopled by spirits and echoes, its boundaries contested by at least four different claimants ([[prospero|Prospero]], [[caliban|Caliban]], [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s legacy, and the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed would-be [[authority|rule]]rs) — makes it the ideal setting for a play about power, illusion, and transformation.

The island is also the most symbolically layered location in Shakespeare's late romances. It functions as a microcosm of the political world (a laboratory of [[authority|rule]]), a figure for the theatre itself (a place of illusion where identities are remade), and a colonial space whose contested ownership anticipates the political dynamics of European expansion. Unlike the forest of Arden or the coast of Bohemia — settings that provide temporary liberation before a return to normal order — the island is both origin and destination: every character either arrives from elsewhere or was born there, and the question of who has the right to stay, leave, or [[authority|rule]] is the play's unresolved political problem.

## In the Play

The island's history is narrated through competing voices, each with a different claim to truth. [[caliban|Caliban]] insists "this island's mine, by [[sycorax|Sycorax]] my mother" (009-029), grounding his claim in inheritance and prior occupation. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s counter-narrative frames [[sycorax|Sycorax]] as a usurper herself — she was "banish'd" from [[argier|Argier]] and "left … here" (003-265–267), spared execution only because of her pregnancy — making his own arrival a reclamation rather than a conquest. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s account of his imprisonment in the "cloven pine" (003-278) adds a third layer: the island before [[prospero|Prospero]] was a place of suffering, not innocence.

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s control over the island is exercised through [[ariel|Ariel]], who can "fly … to fetch dew / From the still-vexed Bermoothes" (003-231) and [[authority|command]] the island's spirits. The geography is detailed and domestic: [[prosperos-cell|Prospero's cell]], [[caliban|Caliban]]'s cave, the "fresh springs, brine-pits, barren place and fertile" (003-376) that [[caliban|Caliban]] showed [[prospero|Prospero]] when he first arrived. This mapping of the island — naming its features, claiming knowledge of its terrain — is itself an act of colonisation.

The island's magic is not [[prospero|Prospero]]'s alone. "The isle is full of noises, / Sounds and sweet airs, that give delight and hurt not" (009-098–099) — [[caliban|Caliban]]'s most lyrical speech describes an enchanted soundscape that predates and exceeds [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]]. The island speaks, sings, and dreams independently of its current [[master|master]]. This native [[magic|enchantment]] is what makes the island valuable to [[prospero|Prospero]] (he needs its spirits to work his magic) and what makes [[caliban|Caliban]]'s dispossession so total: even the island's sounds are claimed and repurposed by the coloniser's [[magic|art]].

For the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed court, the island is first a place of loss ([[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] believes his father drowned), then of conspiracy ([[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] plot regicide on its "desert" ground), and finally of revelation and [[forgiveness|reconciliation]]. Its strangeness — "though this island seem to be desert … / … this is a most majestic [[dream|vision]]" (010-045–046) — disorients the visitors sufficiently to make transformation possible. The island strips away the structures of court society and exposes its inhabitants to themselves.

At the play's end, [[prospero|Prospero]] leaves the island to [[caliban|Caliban]]: "this thing of darkness I / Acknowledge mine" (014-261–262). The acknowledgement is morally ambiguous — [[prospero|Prospero]] claims [[caliban|Caliban]] as his own "thing" even in the act of relinquishing the island — and the island's future after [[prospero|Prospero]]'s dep[[magic|art]]ure is left entirely unimagined. The island reverts to silence, its [[magic|enchantment]]s dissolved with [[prospero|Prospero]]'s staff, its original inhabitant left to inherit what the coloniser has depleted.

## Key Passages

> this island's mine, by Sycorax my mother,
> Which thou takest from me. When thou camest first,
> Thou strokedst me and madest much of me, wouldst give me
> Water with berries in't, and teach me how
> To name the bigger light, and how the less,
> That burn by day and night: and then I loved thee
> And show'd thee all the qualities o' the isle,
> The fresh springs, brine-pits, barren place and fertile.
> Cursed be I that did so!
> — (009-029–037)

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s claim asserts a right of inheritance against [[prospero|Prospero]]'s right of conquest. The speech is an archaeology of the colonial encounter: initial kindness, the teaching of language, the sharing of local knowledge, and then dispossession. "Cursed be I that did so" is the colonised subject's lament at having collaborated in his own dispossession.

> Be not afeard; the isle is full of noises,
> Sounds and sweet airs, that give delight and hurt not.
> Sometimes a thousand twangling instruments
> Will hum about mine ears; and sometime voices,
> That, if I then had waked after long sleep,
> Will make me sleep again: and then, in dreaming,
> The clouds methought would open and show riches
> Ready to drop upon me; that, when I waked,
> I cried to dream again.
> — (009-098–106)

The island speaks for itself through [[caliban|Caliban]]'s words. Its [[music|music]] is neither [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] nor [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s [[magic|sorcery]] but something older — a presence that exceeds all attempts to [[master|master]] it. The final revelation ("I cried to dream again") is one of the most poignant lines in the play: [[caliban|Caliban]]'s capacity for wonder undercuts every colonial justification for his subjugation.

> Here in this island we arrived; and here
> Have I, thy schoolmaster, made thee more profit
> Than other princesses can, that have more time
> For vainer hours, and tutors not so careful.
> — (003-231–234)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s version of the island as classroom. The island is the site of [[miranda|Miranda]]'s [[knowledge|education]] — and, by extension, the audience's. But the metaphor of school[[master|master]] elides the political: [[prospero|Prospero]] can educate because he first conquered.

> I'll break my staff,
> Bury it certain fathoms in the earth,
> And deeper than did ever plummet sound
> I'll drown my book.
> — (014-065–068)

The renunciation of magic is also the renunciation of the island as a site of supernatural power. Breaking the staff and drowning the book sever [[prospero|Prospero]]'s connection to the island's spirits. What remains is the island as mere land — to be left to its original inhabitant, whatever that may mean.

## Critical Context

The island has been the central site of postcolonial readings of *The Tempest* since the mid-20th century. Octave Mannoni's *Psychologie de la colonisation* (1950) used the [[prospero|Prospero]]-[[caliban|Caliban]] relationship as a model for the colonial dynamic, coining the "[[prospero|Prospero]] complex" to describe the coloniser's need to be loved by those he dominates. Aimé Césaire's *Une Tempête* (1969) — a radical rewriting of Shakespeare's play — reimagines [[caliban|Caliban]] as a Black revolutionary and [[ariel|Ariel]] as a mulatto intermediary, making the island explicitly a Caribbean space and the play's politics explicitly anti-colonial. These readings have been so influential that for many contemporary critics, the island *is* the play's political unconscious — the space where early modern European anxieties about New World colonisation find theatrical expression.

New Historicist critics, including Stephen Greenblatt, have connected the island to the Virginia Company's accounts of Bermuda, p[[magic|art]]icularly William Strachey's *True Reportory of the Wrack* (1610), which describes a [[storm|shipwreck]] on an enchanted-seeming island that shares details with Shakespeare's play. The island is thus not a pure fantasy but a refraction of contemporary colonial discourse: the "Bermoothes" that [[ariel|Ariel]] references (003-231) were a real place of [[storm|shipwreck]] and survival, and the play's preoccupation with [[authority|sovereignty]], labour, and indigenous resistance maps onto the early colonial experience with uncanny precision.

Feminist and ecocritical readings have drawn attention to the island's gendering. The island is repeatedly figured as maternal — [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s domain, [[caliban|Caliban]]'s inheritance through his mother, the "fresh springs" and fertile ground that [[nature|nurture]] and resist cultivation. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic is a form of masculine [[servitude|mastery]] over a feminised landscape, and his renunciation in Act 5 can be read as the exhaustion of that [[servitude|mastery]] rather than its transcendence. The island survives [[prospero|Prospero]]'s dep[[magic|art]]ure; what it becomes after him is the play's unanswerable question.
