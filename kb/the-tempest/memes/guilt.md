## Overview

Guilt is the invisible engine of the play's moral drama. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s guilt over his role in [[prospero|Prospero]]'s usurpation drives his grief and makes him receptive to the [[island|island]]'s supernatural interventions. [[antonio|Antonio]]—in pointed contrast—shows no guilt and therefore undergoes no transformation. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own relationship to guilt is complex: he prosecutes the guilt of others while his own treatment of [[caliban|Caliban]] hovers uneasily in the background. [[sebastian|Sebastian]] occupies an ambiguous middle ground, touched by guilt but never fully transformed by it. The harpy scene in (010-080–010-115) is the play's most explicit staging of guilt as a theatrical spectacle—[[ariel|Ariel]], as harpy, confronts the "three men of sin" with their crime and threatens divine punishment (010-081–010-086). The play's moral architecture depends on guilt as the precondition for all genuine [[forgiveness|reconciliation]]: without it, forgiveness has no purchase, and the comic resolution at which the play aims remains incomplete.

## In the Play

### Alonso's Deep Guilt

[[alonso|Alonso]] is the play's principal case [[knowledge|study]] in guilt. From his first appearance in Act 2, he is already burdened: his son [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] is apparently drowned, and he cannot separate this loss from his own political crimes. "My son is lost" (005-085), he says, and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s cruel rebuke—"the fault's your own" (005-117)—names the logic that [[alonso|Alonso]] himself already feels. Guilt here operates as what the Renaissance called "conscience," a faculty both cognitive and affective: [[alonso|Alonso]] knows his fault and suffers it simultaneously. The suffering intensifies in [[ariel|the harpy]] scene, where he hears the accusation directly. "O, it is monstrous, monstrous! / Methought the billows spoke and told me of it" (010-127–010-128). The natural world becomes a moral accuser, and [[alonso|Alonso]] immediately recognises the name behind the charge: "The name of Prosper: it did bass my trespass" (010-131). His guilt drives him to the verge of suicide—"I'll seek him deeper than e'er plummet sounded, / And with him there lie mudded" (010-133–010-134)—a response that, however extreme, qualifies him for the forgiveness that follows.

[[alonso|Alonso]]'s repentance in the final act confirms the sincerity of his guilt. When [[prospero|Prospero]] reveals himself, [[alonso|Alonso]] does not equivocate: "Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat / Thou [[forgiveness|pardon]] me my wrongs" (014-135–014-136). The language is direct and political—he returns what he took, and asks forgiveness for the taking. His guilt is the mechanism that converts a usurping ally into a penitent king, and his restoration at the play's end is contingent on this conversion.

### Antonio's Guiltless Silence

[[antonio|Antonio]]'s silence in the face of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness is the play's most chilling moral datum. [[prospero|Prospero]] confronts him directly—"For you, most wicked sir, whom to call brother / Would even infect my mouth" (014-157–014-158)—and yet offers [[forgiveness|pardon]]: "I do forgive / Thy rankest fault—all of them" (014-158–014-159). [[antonio|Antonio]] says nothing. No apology, no plea, no acknowledgment. His guiltlessness is not innocence but impenitence: he cannot be forgiven because he will not be guilty.

This silence has troubled critics because it exposes a limit in the play's moral scheme. The Tempest is structured as a romance, a form that promises restoration. But [[antonio|Antonio]]'s unrepentant presence at the finale—still alive, still silent, still presumably plotting—means the comic resolution is not total. Guilt, the play has insisted, is the door through which grace enters; [[antonio|Antonio]] keeps the door shut. His silence performs what Stanley Cavell would call the "avoidance of acknowledgment": a refusal to recognise the other's claim upon oneself that is the very essence of tragedy. Shakespeare's refusal to give [[antonio|Antonio]] a repentance speech is a deliberate dramatic choice, one that shadows the play's affirmations with an unresolved darkness.

### Sebastian's Shallow Guilt

[[sebastian|Sebastian]] is the play's middle case: capable of guilt, but never submerged in it as [[alonso|Alonso]] is. In Act 2, [[antonio|Antonio]] tempts him to murder [[alonso|Alonso]], and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] hesitates only briefly—"But for your conscience" (005-313)—before [[antonio|Antonio]] dismisses conscience as a mere "deity" inhabiting the bosom, a superstition to be overcome. The speed of [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s capitulation reveals the thinness of his moral resistance. His guilt, when it comes, is a surface phenomenon; it lacks the depth of [[alonso|Alonso]]'s grief.

In the final scene, [[sebastian|Sebastian]] is revealed alongside [[antonio|Antonio]] and described as one who "Expelled remorse and [[nature|nature]]" (014-088). Yet by the play's end he is categorised among the penitent, with [[prospero|Prospero]] noting "they being penitent" (014-037). The [[forgiveness|pardon]] offered to [[sebastian|Sebastian]] is less earned than extended—[[prospero|Prospero]] says "I do forgive thee, / Unnatural though thou [[magic|art]]" (014-090–014-091)—and critics have noted that [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s penitence seems performative rather than transformative. He occupies the grey zone between [[alonso|Alonso]]'s genuine remorse and [[antonio|Antonio]]'s absolute impenitence, demonstrating the spectrum of guilt-responses that Shakespeare maps across the court p[[magic|art]]y.

### Prospero's Unacknowledged Guilt

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s own guilt is only obliquely addressed, but it haunts the margins of the play. His acknowledgment of [[caliban|Caliban]]—"this thing of darkness I / Acknowledge mine" (014-350–014-351)—is the nearest the play comes to having [[prospero|Prospero]] admit fault. To "acknowledge" is not quite to confess: the verb hovers between acceptance of responsibility and a claim of ownership. [[caliban|Caliban]] is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s creature in both senses—his subject and his creation—and the moral ambiguity of that relationship remains unresolved.

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s treatment of [[caliban|Caliban]] throughout the play raises questions the text does not answer. He enslaved [[caliban|Caliban]], taught him language only to curse him, and tortures him with cramps and pinches. Before he can forgive his European enemies, [[prospero|Prospero]] must first renounce his magic—"I'll break my staff … I'll drown my book" (014-065, 014-068)—a surrender that suggests the instruments of his [[power|power]] are themselves morally compromised. But he never apologises to [[caliban|Caliban]]. The play stops short of requiring [[prospero|Prospero]] to feel guilt for his colonial relationship; that silence may be the play's own most significant unacknowledged guilt.

### The Harpy Scene as Guilt-Machine

The harpy scene (010-080–010-115) is the play's theatrical apparatus for producing guilt where none was felt. A table appears, laid with a banquet; the courtiers approach; "Thunder and lightning. Enter [[ariel|Ariel]], like a harpy; claps his wings upon the table; and, with a quaint device, the banquet vanishes" (010-080). [[ariel|Ariel]]'s speech names the three—[[alonso|Alonso]], [[antonio|Antonio]], and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]—as "three men of sin" (010-081) and frames their crime in cosmic terms: "the never-surfeited sea / Hath caused to belch up you" (010-082–010-083). The accusation is ontological: they are guilt made flesh, rejected by [[nature|nature]] itself. [[ariel|Ariel]] further specifies the charge: "thee of thy son, [[alonso|Alonso]], / They have bereft" (010-104–010-105)—linking [[alonso|Alonso]]'s political sin to his personal loss in a causal chain that makes the guilt inescapable.

The scene is designed as a test that differentiates the guilty. [[alonso|Alonso]] breaks; [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] draw their swords—the reflex of men who respond to moral accusation with violence. The harpy's machinery works perfectly on [[alonso|Alonso]], producing the anguish that will become repentance; on [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]], it produces only defiance. The scene thus functions as the play's guilt-laboratory, revealing the moral constitution of each character through their response to the same supernatural stimulus.

## Key Passages

> You are three men of sin, whom Destiny—
> That hath to instrument this lower world
> And what is in't—the never-surfeited sea
> Hath caused to belch up you; and on this island
> Where man doth not inhabit—you 'mongst men
> Being most unfit to live. I have made you mad.
> — (010-081–010-086, Ariel as harpy)

The accusation is cosmic in scope: the sea itself has rejected the guilty. The [[harpy]]'s speech transforms the [[storm|shipwreck]] from accident into judgment. The final clause—"I have made you mad"—marks the psychological dimension of guilt: it is not merely a legal category but a state of mind, a derangement that the guilty bring upon themselves.

> O, it is monstrous, monstrous!
> Methought the billows spoke and told me of it;
> The winds did sing it to me; and the thunder,
> That deep and dreadful organ-pipe, pronounced
> The name of Prosper: it did bass my trespass.
> — (010-127–010-131, Alonso)

[[alonso|Alonso]]'s confession is remarkable for the way it turns the natural world into a moral agent. Billows, winds, and thunder all bear the same message: his guilt is legible in creation itself. The musical metaphor—thunder as an organ-pipe "bassing" his trespass—suggests that guilt transforms the world into a text that can be read and heard. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s guilt makes him the ideal audience for [[prospero|Prospero]]'s theatrical justice.

> this thing of darkness I
> Acknowledge mine.
> — (014-350–014-351, Prospero)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s most morally complex lines. To "acknowledge" [[caliban|Caliban]] is to accept responsibility without quite confessing guilt. The phrase hovers between admission and evasion. "This thing of darkness" both names [[caliban|Caliban]]'s otherness and implicates [[prospero|Prospero]] as the source of that darkness. The line is the closest the play comes to a colonial apology, and its studied ambiguity is arguably more honest than a full-throated confession would be.

> Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat
> Thou pardon me my wrongs.
> — (014-135–014-136, Alonso)

The political expression of guilt. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s repentance takes the form of restitution—he returns the stolen dukedom—and a direct request for forgiveness. The economy is precise: what was taken is given back; what was wrong is named and offered for [[forgiveness|pardon]]. This is guilt as a political act, the mechanism by which the usurped state is restored.

## Critical Context

Guilt in The Tempest has attracted sustained critical attention, in p[[magic|art]] because the play's treatment of the theme is so uneven: some characters are flooded with remorse, others remain untouched, and the protagonist's own guilt is left strategically ambiguous.

Frank Kermode, in his foundational Arden edition of the play, reads guilt and repentance as essential structural elements of Shakespearean romance. For Kermode, the movement from sin through penitence to restoration is the genre's governing pattern, and The Tempest follows this arc with unusual precision—until [[antonio|Antonio]]'s silence disrupts it. Kermode notes that the romance form requires all wrongs to be acknowledged and forgiven for the final [[music|harmony]] to be genuine; [[antonio|Antonio]]'s impenitence introduces a "discord" that keeps the play from the unclouded resolution of, say, Pericles or The Winter's Tale. This unresolved element is, for Kermode, p[[magic|art]] of the play's peculiar gravity: it acknowledges that not all guilt yields to the pressure of grace.

G. Wilson Knight, in his influential essay on Shakespeare's last plays, reads guilt as a stage in what he calls "spiritual transformation." For Knight, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s abjuration of magic and his forgiveness of his enemies represent a movement from the natural to the supernatural, from justice to [[forgiveness|mercy]]—and guilt is the pivot on which this movement turns. The harpy scene is, in Knight's reading, not merely a theatrical spectacle but a theophany, a direct intervention of the divine into human affairs. The guilt it produces in [[alonso|Alonso]] is therefore not merely psychological but spiritual: it opens the king to a redemptive transformation that political justice alone could never achieve.

Stanley Cavell, writing from a philosophical perspective, approaches the problem through the linked concepts of acknowledgment and avoidance. In Cavell's reading of Shakespearean tragedy and romance, the refusal to acknowledge another's claim—what he calls "avoidance"—is the root of tragic impasse. [[antonio|Antonio]]'s silence in the final scene is the paradigm case: he avoids [[prospero|Prospero]]'s claim upon him, and in doing so excludes himself from the community of [[forgiveness|reconciliation]]. Cavell's framework illuminates the ethical structure of the play's ending: forgiveness is offered universally, but it can only be received by those who acknowledge their need for it. Guilt, on this account, is not a feeling but a form of recognition—the recognition that one stands in a relationship of indebtedness to another, a relationship that [[antonio|Antonio]] refuses and [[alonso|Alonso]] embraces.

Stephen Greenblatt approaches guilt from the angle of [[power|power]] and theatricality. In his work on Renaissance self-fashioning, Greenblatt argues that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s production of guilt in others is an exercise of [[power|power]] disguised as moral instruction: [[ariel|the harpy]] scene, the vanishing banquet, [[the-shipwreck|the tempest]] itself—all are theatrical displays designed to produce a specific affective response in their audience. Guilt, in this reading, is something [[prospero|Prospero]] manufactures in others while remaining largely exempt from it himself. Greenblatt's analysis exposes the asymmetry at the he[[magic|art]] of the play's moral economy: [[prospero|Prospero]] stages guilt for everyone else but never fully submits to his own.
