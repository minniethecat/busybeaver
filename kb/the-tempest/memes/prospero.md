## Overview

Prospero is the central figure of *The Tempest* — duke, magician, father, and playwright-surrogate. Deposed as Duke of [[milan|Milan]] by his brother [[antonio|Antonio]] and set adrift with his infant daughter, he has spent twelve years on an island mastering the "[[art|liberal arts]]" (003-103) that enable him to [[authority|command]] spirits and shape [[nature|nature]]. The play opens with Prospero at the height of his power: he has conjured [[the-shipwreck|the storm]] that brings his enemies within reach. But power is not the same as resolution, and the play's dramatic arc traces Prospero's movement from vengeance to forgiveness, from magic to mortality, from isolation to community. His final act — drowning his book and breaking his staff — is a renunciation that redefines him not as a diminished figure but as one who has chosen humanity over omnipotence.

Prospero's unique position in Shakespeare's canon — a magician who is also a father, a [[authority|rule]]r who is also an [[magic|art]]ist — makes him one of the most complex protagonists in the late romances. He is simultaneously the author of the play's action and a character within it, a figure whose theatrical self-consciousness — "Our revels now are ended" (012-184) — has invited centuries of identification with Shakespeare himself.

## Role in the Play

Prospero appears in every act, and his presence structures the entire dramatic action. Act 1, Scene 2 is dominated by his long narrative to [[miranda|Miranda]], recounting the usurpation and establishing his triple identity: wronged duke, devoted father, and [[authority|command]]ing magician. His control over [[ariel|Ariel]] and [[caliban|Caliban]] is demonstrated through a series of confrontations that establish the island's hierarchy.

In Act 3, Scene 1, Prospero watches unseen as [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] declare their love — "Fair encounter / Of two most rare affections!" (008-098–099) — revealing his role as stage-manager of the play's emotional action. Act 4 presents the masque he has devised to bless the betrothal, followed by his sudden recollection of [[caliban|Caliban]]'s conspiracy and the famous "Our revels now are ended" meditation.

Act 5 is Prospero's transformation. He enters "in his magic robes" (014-001) and delivers the abjuration speech, renouncing the [[magic|art]] that has defined him. His forgiveness of [[antonio|Antonio]] — "I do forgive thee, / Unnatural though thou [[magic|art]]" (014-119–120) — is offered but not accepted; his acknowledgment of [[caliban|Caliban]] — "this thing of darkness I / Acknowledge mine" (014-261–262) — is morally complex and deliberately unresolved. The Epilogue extends Prospero's renunciation into the theatrical space: stripped of magic, he asks the audience to [[freedom|release]] him with their applause.

## Key Speeches

> My brother and thy uncle, call'd Antonio —
> I pray thee, mark me — that a brother should
> Be so perfidious! — he whom next thyself
> Of all the world I loved, and to him put
> The manage of my state; as at that time
> Through all the signories it was the first,
> And Prospero the prime duke, being so reputed
> In dignity, and for the liberal arts
> Without a parallel; those being all my study,
> The government I cast upon my brother
> And to my state grew stranger, being transported
> And rapt in secret studies.
> — (003-095–107)

The exposition that sets the entire play in motion. Prospero's self-description balances pride ("the prime duke," "without a parallel") with self-indictment: his absorption in books created the vacuum [[antonio|Antonio]] filled. The interrupted syntax — dashes, qualifying clauses — dramatises a man still struggling to narrate his own trauma coherently after twelve years.

> Our revels now are ended. These our actors,
> As I foretold you, were all spirits and
> Are melted into air, into thin air:
> And, like the baseless fabric of this vision,
> The cloud-capp'd towers, the gorgeous palaces,
> The solemn temples, the great globe itself,
> Yea, all which it inherit, shall dissolve,
> And, like this insubstantial pageant faded,
> Leave not a rack behind. We are such stuff
> As dreams are made on, and our little life
> Is rounded with a sleep.
> — (012-184–194)

The play's philosophical he[[magic|art]]. Prospero moves from consoling [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] (the masque was only a show) to contemplating the impermanence of all things — palaces, temples, "the great globe itself" (the Globe Theatre, and the world). The speech enacts its own argument: the grand images dissolve into "thin air," leaving only the dream-stuff of human existence.

> Ye elves of hills, brooks, standing lakes, and groves;
> And ye that on the sands with printless foot
> Do chase the ebbing Neptune, and do fly him
> When he comes back; you demi-puppets that
> By moonshine do the green sour ringlets make,
> Whereof the ewe not bites; and you whose pastime
> Is to make midnight mushrooms, that rejoice
> To hear the solemn curfew; by whose aid,
> Weak masters though ye be, I have bedimm'd
> The noontide sun, call'd forth the mutinous winds,
> And 'twixt the green sea and the azured vault
> Set roaring war: … But this rough magic
> I here abjure … I'll break my staff,
> Bury it certain fathoms in the earth,
> And deeper than did ever plummet sound
> I'll drown my book.
> — (014-033–068)

The abjuration speech is Prospero's farewell to magic and, by extension, to the [[magic|art]] that made him who he is. The catalogue of spirits — elves of hills, brooks, standing lakes — is the most elaborate poetic enumeration in the play, a catalogue of wonders the audience has never seen (Prospero's magic has always been mediated through [[ariel|Ariel]]). The breaking of the staff and drowning of the book mark the end of his magical identity and the beginning of his return to mere dukedom — and to mortality.

## Critical Reception

The identification of Prospero with Shakespeare — the magician as playwright, the abjuration speech as Shakespeare's farewell to the stage — has been the dominant critical tradition since the 19th century. Coleridge first advanced the reading; Edward Dowden's *Shakspere: A Critical Study of His Mind and [[art|Art]]* (1875) codified it, treating *The Tempest* as Shakespeare's "poetic last will and testament." This biographical reading has been powerfully challenged by historicist and postcolonial critics, who note that Shakespeare continued to collaborate on plays after *The Tempest* (making the "farewell" reading chronologically suspect), and who draw attention to what the Prospero-Shakespeare identification occludes: the play's colonial politics, its representation of [[servitude|slavery]], and its fantasy of benign autocracy.

The postcolonial turn, inaugurated by Octave Mannoni's *Psychologie de la colonisation* (1950) — which coined the term "Prospero complex" to describe the colonial fantasy of the grateful native — and extended by critics such as Stephen Greenblatt, has made [[caliban|Caliban]] the interpretive centre of the play and Prospero the object of scrutiny rather than sympathetic identification. The Cambridge edition's Critical Apparatus contains numerous textual cruces related to Prospero: the stage direction "Prospero … invisible" (042-054), which editors have debated since the Folio; Capell's emendation of Prospero's final gesture ("advancing," 044-001); and the Folio variants that complicate the moral reading of Prospero's forgiveness.

Recent ecological criticism has returned to Prospero's magic as a figure for the human relationship to [[nature|nature]]. The abjuration speech's catalogue of natural spirits — elves of hills, brooks, standing lakes — is not merely pastoral ornament but a map of an animistic cosmos that Prospero's [[magic|art]] has mastered and must now [[freedom|release]]. In this reading, the renunciation of magic is not a diminishment but an ecological ethic: the recognition that [[servitude|mastery]] must give way to coexistence.
