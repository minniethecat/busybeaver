## Overview

Authority in *The Tempest* is perpetually contested. The opening scene stages a crisis of [[power|authority]] aboard the sinking ship: the [[boatswain|Boatswain]] commands the king — "You mar our labour: keep your cabins" (002-011) — and [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]], clinging to hierarchy, protests that "yet remember whom thou hast aboard." The [[boatswain|Boatswain]]'s retort — "What cares these roarers for the name of king?" (002-015) — demolishes the premise that rank confers [[power|authority]] in the face of [[nature|nature]]. This moment crystallises the play's central political question: what makes [[power|authority]] legitimate? Birth, virtue, knowledge, power, or consent?

Shakespeare anatomises [[power|authority]] across a spectrum that ranges from the divinely-sanctioned kingship of [[alonso|Alonso]] to the raw usurpation of [[antonio|Antonio]], from the [[art|learned magic]] of [[prospero|Prospero]] to the inherited claim of [[caliban|Caliban]], and from the competence-based command of the [[boatswain|Boatswain]] to the hollow pretensions of [[stephano|Stephano]]. The play's genius is to place these competing models in collision on an [[island]] that lacks the institutional scaffolding — courts, armies, law — that normally upholds [[power|authority]], forcing each claimant to demonstrate legitimacy through action rather than title. The resolution of Act 5 does not simply restore the pre-play political order; it redefines [[power|authority]] itself as something that must be earned through renunciation, forgiveness, and the acknowledgement of limits.

## In the Play

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]] is triple: as duke of [[milan|Milan]] (political), as father (domestic), and as magician (supernatural). Each is challenged over the course of the play and each requires a different mode of restoration. His political [[power|authority]] was usurped by [[antonio|Antonio]], who "being once perfected how to grant suits, / How to deny them, whom to advance" (003-111–112) [[master|master]]ed the [[magic|art]]s of governance that [[prospero|Prospero]] neglected — "[a]nd rapt in [[art|secret studies]]" (003-107), the duke ceded the daily exercise of rule and discovered too late that [[power|authority]] not exercised is [[power|authority]] lost. His domestic [[power|authority]] over [[miranda|Miranda]] is exercised absolutely — he puts her to [[sleep|sleep]] at will, controls her access to knowledge, and presents her to [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] as a reward — but it is shadowed by the memory of [[caliban|Caliban]]'s attempted violation, a threat that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]] as father could not prevent and can only punish. His magical [[power|authority]], supreme on the island, is challenged by [[ariel|Ariel]]'s demand for [[freedom|liberty]] and by his own recognition that [[magic|sorcery]] is incompatible with the obligations of a Christian ruler. The restoration of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s legitimate [[power|authority]] requires the surrender of one register — "But this rough magic / I here abjure" (014-061–062) — to reclaim another (014-062), while the domestic [[power|authority]] of the father is naturalised and never interrogated. In this asymmetry the play reveals its deepest assumption: that some forms of [[power|authority]] are subject to political contest and others are simply given.

[[caliban|Caliban]] mounts the play's most radical challenge to [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]]. His claim — "This island's mine, by [[sycorax|Sycorax]] my mother, / Which thou tak'st from me" (009-029–030) — asserts a right of inheritance that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s colonial occupation cannot answer within its own logic. [[caliban|Caliban]] is not merely a rebellious servant; he is a dispossessed sovereign whose language of legitimate succession mirrors [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own claim to [[milan|Milan]]. The play never resolves this contradiction: [[prospero|Prospero]] regains his dukedom through the exposure of usurpation while maintaining his own rule over the island by the same logic he condemns in [[antonio|Antonio]]. The colonial dimension of the play's treatment of [[power|authority]] is made explicit in [[caliban|Caliban]]'s bitter summary of his [[knowledge|education]] — "You taught me language, and my profit on't / Is, I know how to curse" (009-030–031) — which recasts [[prospero|Prospero]]'s civilising mission as a technology of subjugation. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s final speech — "I'll be wise hereafter, / And seek for grace" (014-369–370) — is an ambiguous submission: is it genuine conversion or strategic accommodation? The play leaves the colonial question open even as it closes the political one.

The [[boatswain|Boatswain]]'s [[power|authority]] in Act 1 represents an alternative model that the play proposes but cannot sustain. In [[the-shipwreck|the storm]], his knowledge of seamanship gives him the right to command anyone aboard, regardless of rank. [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] recognises this implicitly — "I have great comfort from this fellow" — acknowledging that [[power|authority]] can flow from expertise rather than title. This meritocratic [[dream|vision]] is the play's most radical political suggestion, but it is contained within [[the-shipwreck|the storm]]'s exceptional circumstances. Once the court reaches the island, hierarchy reasserts itself, and the [[boatswain|Boatswain]] disappears until his brief, amazed reappearance in Act 5. The competence-based [[power|authority]] that governed the ship could not govern the commonwealth; the play knows this, even as it lets us glimpse the alternative. The [[storm|storm]] thus functions as a temporary zone of exception — a miniature state of [[nature|nature]] in which the normal rules of political order are suspended and a different measure of [[power|authority]] briefly prevails, only to be reabsorbed into the restored hierarchy of the final act.

[[antonio|Antonio]] represents [[power|authority]] without legitimacy. His advice to [[sebastian|Sebastian]] — to kill [[alonso|Alonso]] and seize [[naples|Naples]] — replays his own usurpation, and his silence in Act 5, when [[prospero|Prospero]] confronts him, is the play's most eloquent admission of [[guilt|guilt]]. He never repents; he is simply outmaneuvered. [[antonio|Antonio]]'s silence is in fact the play's darkest political moment: the one character who refuses the cycle of forgiveness and restoration is also the one who most thoroughly understands the mechanics of power. His [[servitude|mastery]] of "how to grant suits, / How to deny them, whom to advance" (003-111–112) is a chillingly accurate description of governance reduced to technique, and his ongoing refusal to speak in Act 5 — "I do forgive thee, / Unnatural though thou [[magic|art]]" (014-094–095), [[prospero|Prospero]] says, and [[antonio|Antonio]] says nothing — suggests that [[power|authority]] founded on competence alone, without moral transformation, is a permanent threat that the play's comic resolution cannot neutralise.

[[alonso|Alonso]]'s [[power|authority]] as king is weakened by [[guilt|guilt]]. He believes his son is dead — a punishment, he feels, for his role in [[prospero|Prospero]]'s overthrow — and his grief makes him vulnerable to [[antonio|Antonio]]'s conspiracy. His restoration at the play's end is also a moral recovery: he receives his son back and acknowledges [[milan|Prospero's dukedom]]. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s trajectory illustrates the play's thesis that legitimate [[power|authority]] depends on moral self-awareness: a king who cannot face his own crimes cannot govern, and [[alonso|Alonso]]'s acknowledgment — "Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat / Thou [[forgiveness|pardon]] me my wrongs" (014-110–111) — is the act that makes the restoration of political order possible.

## Key Passages

> Do you not hear him? You mar our labour: keep your cabins: you do assist the [[storm|storm]].
> — (002-011)

The [[boatswain|Boatswain]] asserts the [[power|authority]] of competence over the [[power|authority]] of rank. In [[the-shipwreck|the storm]]'s democracy, a king is just another obstacle to saving the ship.

> Being once perfected how to grant suits,
> How to deny them, whom to advance, and whom
> To trash for over-topping …
> — (003-111–113)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s description of [[antonio|Antonio]]'s political skill. The verbs — grant, deny, advance, trash — describe the machinery of governance as a set of techniques that [[prospero|Prospero]] himself failed to [[master|master]]. Authority, in this cold anatomy, is not a sacred trust but a repertoire of moves.

> This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother,
> Which thou tak'st from me.
> — (009-029–030)

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s assertion of inherited sovereignty is the play's most politically charged moment. The claim is structurally identical to [[prospero|Prospero]]'s claim to [[milan|Milan]] — legitimate rule by birthright, seized by a usurper. That [[prospero|Prospero]] refuses to recognise the parallel exposes the limits of his own understanding of [[power|authority]]: legitimacy, it seems, is a principle that applies only to Europeans. The coloniser's narrative of rightful rule is unmasked by the colonised subject who speaks it back in the coloniser's own language.

> Now I want
> Spirits to enforce, art to enchant;
> And my ending is despair,
> Unless I be relieved by prayer …
> As you from crimes would pardoned be,
> Let your indulgence set me free.
> — (015-014–021)

Having renounced his magic, broken his staff, and drowned his book, [[prospero|Prospero]] stands before the audience stripped of every form of [[power|authority]] he once possessed. His final appeal is not a command but a request — he asks the audience to do for him what he himself has just done for his enemies: [[forgiveness|pardon]]. Authority, in the play's last word on the subject, dissolves into the mutual dependence of forgiveness. The epilogue thus completes the play's anatomy of [[power|authority]]: from the [[boatswain|Boatswain]]'s competence, through [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic, to [[antonio|Antonio]]'s technique, and finally to the audience's consent — the only [[power|authority]] that can free a character who has surrendered every other kind.

## Critical Context

The critical tradition on [[power|authority]] in *The Tempest* has been shaped by debates about the play's politics, its relationship to Jacobean ideologies of kingship, and its representation of colonial power. Three scholars have been p[[magic|art]]icularly influential in framing these questions, and their competing readings continue to structure discussion of the play's treatment of [[power|authority]].

**Frank Kermode**, in his landmark Arden edition of the play (1954), situated [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]] within the political theology of Jacobean England. For Kermode, [[prospero|Prospero]] is a figure of the Renaissance prince whose [[magic|art]] — the [[art|learned magic]] of the Neoplatonic magus — is an extension of royal power rather than a substitute for it. The play's movement from usurpation to restoration mirrors the providential pattern of romance, in which legitimate [[power|authority]] is tested, temporarily displaced, and ultimately reaffirmed. Kermode's reading established the influential view that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s rule, for all its ambivalence, represents a [[dream|vision]] of [[power|authority]] grounded in knowledge, tempered by suffering, and completed by renunciation. Kermode read [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]] as a mirror of the Jacobean ideal of kingship — a burden of [[knowledge|wisdom]] and self-discipline rather than a privilege of birth — and argued that the abjuration of magic in Act 5 is not a rejection of power but its purification, a graduation from supernatural command to properly political rule.

**Stephen Greenblatt**, in "The Improvisation of [[power|Power]]" and later in *Shakespearean Negotiations* (1988), shifted the critical emphasis from [[prospero|Prospero]]'s legitimacy to the techniques by which [[power|authority]] is constructed and maintained in performance. For Greenblatt, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]] is not a given but an "improvisation" — a continuous act of self-fashioning that depends on the manipulation of narratives, the staging of spectacular displays, and the strategic alternation of threat and [[forgiveness|mercy]]. The play exposes what Greenblatt calls the "circulation of social energy": [[power|authority]] is not possessed but enacted, and its power derives from the audience's — both on-stage and in the theatre — willingness to credit the performance. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s resistance — his insistence on an alternative narrative of the island's history — represents a counter-performance that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]] must constantly suppress. In Greenblatt's analysis, the meta-theatrical dimension of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] — the fact that his [[power|authority]] *looks like theatre* — is the play's most radical insight: power works by staging belief, and the subjects of [[power|authority]] are complicit in the [[magic|enchantment]]s they witness.

**Jonathan Dollimore**, in *Radical Tragedy: Religion, Ideology and [[power|Power]] in the Drama of Shakespeare and His Contemporaries* (1984), read *The Tempest* against the grain of the humanist tradition that treated [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]] as benign or providential. Drawing on the Marxist tradition of ideology critique, Dollimore argued that the play's resolution — the restoration of [[prospero|Prospero]] to his dukedom, the marriage of [[miranda|Miranda]] and [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]], the submission of [[caliban|Caliban]] — is not a transcendence of power but its consolidation. Authority, in Dollimore's reading, is never genuinely challenged in *The Tempest*; it is only redistributed among the same class of rulers. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s claim to the island is dismissed, [[antonio|Antonio]]'s usurpation is forgiven without cost, and the patriarchal order is reaffirmed under the guise of [[forgiveness|reconciliation]]. What the play's apparent generosity masks, Dollimore contends, is a "radical subordination" of all challenges to established [[power|authority]]: the play permits dissent only to contain it, making *The Tempest* not a celebration of forgiveness but an ideological management of subversion.

These three readings — Kermode's providential prince, Greenblatt's performative improviser, and Dollimore's ideological consolidation — have structured subsequent critical debate on [[power|authority]] in the play. Later scholars, including Paul Brown and Ania Loomba, have extended these frameworks to address the intersections of [[power|authority]] with race, gender, and colonial discourse, making *The Tempest* a central text in the development of postcolonial Shakespeare criticism and ensuring that the question of [[power|authority]] — what legitimises it, how it is performed, and whom it serves — remains at the centre of the play's critical reception.
