## Overview

The Betrothal of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s most carefully orchestrated achievement. In Act 3, Scene 1, [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] labours under [[prospero|Prospero]]'s imposed [[servitude|servitude]], carrying logs (008-001). [[miranda|Miranda]], moved by love, offers to help him and declares her affection. [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] proposes marriage, and [[prospero|Prospero]], watching invisibly, approves (012-044). In Act 4, [[prospero|Prospero]] gives [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] "a rich gift" (012-009) — his daughter — but with a stern warning: if [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] "dost break her virgin-knot before / All sanctimonious ceremonies may / With full and holy rite be minister'd" (012-018–020), their marriage will be cursed. The betrothal is celebrated with a supernatural masque performed by [[ariel|Ariel]]'s spirits.

## Dramatic Function

The betrothal is the structural centre of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s project. Where the play begins with [[storm|shipwreck]] and apparent disaster, the betrothal marks the turning point toward [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] and restoration. It is simultaneously a love story and a political transaction — [[miranda|Miranda]] is both a beloved daughter and the key to [[prospero|Prospero]]'s dynastic rehabilitation. The masque that celebrates the betrothal is the most spectacular display of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magical [[power|power]], but its abrupt ending (when [[prospero|Prospero]] remembers [[the-mutiny|Caliban's plot]]) reminds the audience that his control is not absolute.

## Key Passages

> "I am your wife, if you will marry me; / If not, I'll die your maid." (008-115–116)

> "I tender to thy hand: all thy vexations / Were but my trials of thy love." (012-006–007)

> "If thou dost break her virgin-knot before / All sanctimonious ceremonies may / With full and holy rite be minister'd, / No sweet aspersion shall the heavens let fall / To make this contract grow." (012-018–022)

> "A contract of true love to celebrate." (012-102)

## Characters Involved

- **[[ferdinand]]** — [[ferdinand|Prince of Naples]], who endures [[prospero|Prospero]]'s trials to win [[miranda|Miranda]]
- **[[miranda]]** — [[miranda|Prospero's daughter]], who has never seen a young man before [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]
- **[[prospero]]** — The orchestrator, who tests [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and gives the betrothal his blessing

## Aftermath

The betrothal leads directly to the masque, and the discovery of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] playing chess in Act 5 provides the climactic reveal for [[alonso|Alonso]]: his son is alive, and his future daughter-in-law is [[miranda|Prospero's daughter]]. The marriage alliance heals the breach between [[milan|Milan]] and [[naples|Naples]] that began with [[antonio|Antonio]]'s usurpation.
