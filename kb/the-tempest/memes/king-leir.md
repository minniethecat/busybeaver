## Overview

*The True Chronicle History of King Leir* is an anonymous Elizabethan play, first performed around 1594 and published in 1605, that tells the story of the legendary British king Leir and his three daughters. It is the principal dramatic source for Shakespeare's *King Lear* (c. 1605–1606). In the critical apparatus of [[the-tempest|*The Tempest*]], the play is cited by editors — not as a source for *The Tempest* itself, but as a parallel text for a p[[magic|art]]icular phrase in Act 3, Scene 3 (019-001–019-002).

## In the Play

The connection between *King Leir* and *The Tempest* is purely editorial and circumstantial. In the note apparatus for *The Tempest*, an editor identifies "King Leir" as a reference point for the metaphor "each putter out of five for one" (010-073) — a venture-capital phrase that appears both in *The Tempest* and in another play cited in the same note, [[john-fletcher|Beaumont and Fletcher]]'s *The Noble Gentleman* (031-001).

The editorial note does not suggest that Shakespeare drew on *King Leir* for *The Tempest*; it simply records that the phrase or a related one appears in the source play for *King Lear*. The connection is one of scholarly cross-reference rather than direct influence. However, the editorial apparatus of early modern drama — the network of notes, parallels, and sources that scholarly editions construct — is itself a form of [[knowledge]] that enriches the reading of any single play.

## Key Passages

> The form "princesses," the use of which in Shakespeare's time was doubted by one of our correspondents, is found in the *History of King Leir*.
>
> (019-002)

The note records an orthographic usage — the plural form "princesses" in *The Tempest* — and cites *King Leir* as a parallel instance (019-001). The citation functions not as a source claim but as a textual corroboration: if the form appears in another play of the period, it strengthens the case for retaining the Folio reading.
