## Overview

Music pervades *The Tempest* more thoroughly than any other Shakespeare play. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s songs guide characters across the [[island|island]], solemn music attends [[prospero|Prospero]]'s most powerful magic, and even [[caliban|Caliban]]--the play's supposed brute--speaks of the [[island|island]]'s "sounds and sweet airs, that give delight and hurt not" (009-098). Music functions as an agent of [[magic|enchantment]] (luring [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]], putting the court to [[sleep|sleep]]), a marker of civility ([[caliban|Caliban]]'s surprising sensitivity), and a figure for cosmic harmony restored. The play's movement from dissonance to resolution is scored musically at every turn, from the opening storm's cacophony through [[ariel|Ariel]]'s songs and [[the-masque|the betrothal masque]] to the final silence of the Epilogue, where [[prospero|Prospero]], having renounced his [[magic|art]], stands before the audience without accompaniment.

## In the Play

[[ariel|Ariel]]'s first song--"Come unto these yellow sands" (003-511)--lures [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] from the shore to [[miranda|Miranda]]. [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s response captures the uncanny quality of the [[island|island]]'s music: "Where should this music be? i' th' air or th' e[[magic|art]]h? / ... This music crept by me upon the waters" (003-524, 003-528). Music here is disembodied, sourceless--a voice without a body, a sound that "creeps" rather than announces itself.

[[ariel|Ariel]]'s second song--"Full fathom five thy father lies" (003-534)--tells [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] his father has drowned, transmuting death into a [[transformation|sea-change]] of "something rich and strange." The beauty of the music makes the false news bearable, perhaps even seductive.

The drunken conspirators' scene (Act 2, Scene 2) deploys music as mockery. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s tabor and pipe lead [[stephano|Stephano]], [[trinculo|Trinculo]], and [[caliban|Caliban]] through "toothed briars, sharp furzes, prickly gorse, and thorns" (012-221), while [[caliban|Caliban]]'s own speech about the [[island|island]]'s music--"Sometimes a thousand twangling instruments / Will hum about mine ears" (009-135-136)--elevates him momentarily above his companions' coarse comedy.

### The Banquet and the Harpy: "Solemn and Strange Music"

The banquet scene in Act 3, Scene 3 is the play's most theatrically intricate fusion of music and supernatural spectacle. As [[alonso|Alonso]] and his court, exhausted and despairing, encounter a mysterious banquet laid out by "several strange Shapes," the stage direction calls for "Solemn and strange music" (010-027). [[alonso|Alonso]]'s immediate response--"What harmony is this?--My good friends, hark!"--suggests wonder rather than fear. The music promises hospitality, even grace, and the famished courtiers move to eat.

But the promise is a trap. "Thunder and lightning" erupt, and [[ariel|Ariel]] enters "like a [[harpy|harpy]]," claps his wings upon the table, and "with a quaint device, the banquet vanishes" (010-080). The music that lured them to the table becomes the prelude to accusation: [[ariel|Ariel]], as "minister of Fate," confronts the "three men of sin" with their crime against [[prospero|Prospero]]. When [[ariel|Ariel]] vanishes "in thunder" and "to soft music" the Shapes return to dance mockingly and carry out the table (010-112), the music's function has shifted from invitation to judgment. The banquet scene thus dramatises music's double nature in the play: it can heal or harm, seduce or accuse, depending on the moral condition of those who hear it.

### Stephano's Drunken Songs: The Parody of Ariel's Music

The play's high musical register finds its deliberate comic parody in [[stephano|Stephano]]'s drunken songs. Where [[ariel|Ariel]] sings of [[transformation|sea-change]] and enchanted landscapes, [[stephano|Stephano]] enters Act 2, Scene 2 "singing: a bottle in his hand" and delivers a raucous sea-shanty: "I shall no more to sea, to sea, / Here shall I die a-shore--" (006-023). His second verse--"The [[master|master]], the swabber, the [[boatswain|boatswain]], and I, / The gunner, and his mate" (006-024-025)--reduces the ship's hierarchy to a drinking song, a burlesque of the nautical language that opened the play in Act 1, Scene 1.

The parody is structural. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s music compels obedience to a metaphysical order; [[stephano|Stephano]]'s songs compel only [[caliban|Caliban]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] to drink deeper. Where [[ariel|Ariel]]'s "Full fathom five" transforms death into [[magic|art]], [[stephano|Stephano]]'s "scurvy tune" transforms a funeral into a joke. The contrast sharpens the play's hierarchy of sound: from heavenly harmony ([[ariel|Ariel]], the masque) through human disorder ([[stephano|Stephano]], the conspiracy) to the brute noise of the opening [[storm|tempest]]. Music, the play insists, is an index of moral intelligence--and [[stephano|Stephano]]'s inability to hear beyond his own drunken noise marks him as irredeemably e[[magic|art]]hbound.

### The Epilogue's Silence: Prospero Without His Art

The play's closing movement traces music's gradual withdrawal. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s abjuration of magic is accompanied by "solemn music" (014-068) as he vows to "drown my book." The "heavenly music" he invokes to charm the court p[[magic|art]]y (014-062-064) is the last music he [[authority|command]]s. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s final song--"Where the bee sucks, there suck I" (014-101)--is a fantasy of pure freedom, sung as [[ariel|the spirit]] prepares to leave [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[servitude|service]]. The images are miniature and delicate--a bee's diet, a flower's bell for a bed, a bat's back for flight--worlds away from the cosmic scale of [[the-shipwreck|the storm]] and [[ariel|the harpy]]. After five acts of spectacular labour, [[ariel|Ariel]]'s [[freedom|liberty]] is imagined as smallness.

Then comes the Epilogue--and with it, silence. [[prospero|Prospero]] stands on stage alone, without spirits, without music, without magic: "Now my charms are all o'erthrown, / And what strength I have's mine own, / Which is most faint" (015-002-004). The play that began in the noise of a storm and moved through [[ariel|Ariel]]'s songs, the masque's harmonies, and the solemn music of abjuration ends with a man speaking in his own unaided voice. He asks the audience's applause--"Let your indulgence set me free" (015-020)--as if human hands, clapping, can replace the heavenly music he has surrendered. The Epilogue completes the play's sonic arc: from chaos through harmony to the vulnerable silence of mortality.

## Key Passages

> Where should this music be? i' th' air or th' earth?
> It sounds no more: and sure, it waits upon
> Some god o' th' island.
> -- (003-524-526)

[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s bewilderment at the sourceless music captures its supernatural quality. The suggestion that it "waits upon / Some god" anticipates the play's recurrent equation of music with divine order.

> Be not afeard; the isle is full of noises,
> Sounds and sweet airs, that give delight and hurt not.
> Sometimes a thousand twangling instruments
> Will hum about mine ears ...
> -- (009-098-136)

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s speech is the play's most surprising testament to music's power. The "savage and deformed slave" reveals the most refined aesthetic sensitivity, complicating every claim about his nature.

> ... when I have required
> Some heavenly music,--which even now I do,--
> To work mine end upon their senses ...
> -- (014-062-064)

Music as [[prospero|Prospero]]'s final instrument. The shift from "rough magic" to "heavenly music" (014-061-064) marks the transformation of his [[magic|art]] from coercion to grace.

> *Solemn and strange music. And let several strange Shapes bring in a banquet and dance about it ...*
> *Thunder and lightning. Enter Ariel, like a harpy; claps his wings upon the table; and, with a quaint device, the banquet vanishes.*
> -- (010-027, 010-080)

The banquet scene's two stage directions--the first an invitation, the second an ambush--frame music as the threshold between hospitality and judgment. The "solemn and strange" music that opens the scene dissolves into thunder; the music that promised comfort becomes the prelude to cosmic accusation.

> *Sings.* The master, the swabber, the boatswain, and I,
> The gunner, and his mate,
> Loved Mall, Meg, and Marian, and Margery,
> But none of us cared for Kate ...
> -- (006-024-028)

[[stephano|Stephano]]'s shanty is the play's deliberate comic antiphony to [[ariel|Ariel]]'s songs. Where [[ariel|Ariel]]'s music opens onto wonder and transformation, [[stephano|Stephano]]'s reduces the world to drink and the lowest registers of appetite. The two kinds of music map the play's moral universe: those who hear [[ariel|Ariel]] and those who can only hear themselves.

## Critical Context

Music in *The Tempest* has attracted sustained scholarly attention, p[[magic|art]]icularly for the way it fuses Renaissance neo-Platonic ideas of cosmic harmony with the practical theatrical resources of the Jacobean stage.

David Lindley, in his [[knowledge|study]] of music in *The Tempest* and the masque tradition, argues that the play's musical moments draw directly on the conventions of the Stu[[magic|art]] [[masque-form|court masque]], where music functioned as an audible sign of divine order and royal [[power|authority]]. The betrothal masque in Act 4, with its goddesses and its "majestic [[dream|vision]]," represents the play's most explicit engagement with the genre; when it dissolves--"Our revels now are ended" (012-184)--Lindley suggests we witness not merely the end of a performance but the exhaustion of an entire aesthetic and political form. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s subsequent meditation on impermanence ("the great globe itself ... shall dissolve") extends the masque's dissolution to the scale of the world itself, making music the medium through which the play contemplates its own theatricality.

Frank Kermode, in his influential Arden edition, reads the play's music through the Renaissance doctrine of cosmic harmony. For Kermode, the "heavenly music" [[prospero|Prospero]] invokes in Act 5 is not metaphor but a literal appeal to the music of the spheres--the inaudible harmony that, in neo-Platonic thought, governed the universe's order. When [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic can "require" such music, he occupies a position between the e[[magic|art]]hly and the divine; when he abjures it, he accepts mortality. Kermode's reading establishes music as the audible register of the play's metaphysical stakes: to hear music is to be aligned with cosmic order; to be deaf to it (like [[stephano|Stephano]] or [[antonio|Antonio]]) is to be exiled from grace.

John Hollander, in his wide-ranging treatment of music in Shakespeare, observes that *The Tempest* contains more songs and stage directions for music than any other Shakespeare play, and that this density is structural rather than decorative. Hollander argues that Shakespeare uses music to organise the play's emotional and narrative rhythms: each act contains at least one significant musical moment, and the quality of that music--from [[the-shipwreck|the storm]]'s noise to [[ariel|Ariel]]'s lyric sweetness to the Epilogue's silence--corresponds to the moral trajectory of the action. For Hollander, the Epilogue's unaccompanied speech is the play's most radical musical gesture: the withdrawal of music itself becomes the final aesthetic statement.

Catherine Dunn, examining [[ariel|Ariel]]'s songs in relation to Renaissance music theory, demonstrates that Shakespeare embedded specific musical forms--the round, the catch, the courtly air--into the play's dramatic structure, each carrying distinct cultural associations. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s "Full fathom five" draws on the tradition of the funeral elegy, while [[stephano|Stephano]]'s songs parody the popular catch. Dunn's analysis reveals that the play's musical vocabulary was legible to its original audience as a system of generic signals, mapping character and moral worth through musical style alone.
