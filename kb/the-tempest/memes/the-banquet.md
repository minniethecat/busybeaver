## Overview

The Banquet, often called "The Harpy's Banquet," is the supernatural turning point of Act 3 in Shakespeare's [[the-tempest|The Tempest]]. After their exhausting search for the supposedly drowned [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]], [[alonso|Alonso]] and his courtiers encounter a magical feast only to have it snatched away by [[ariel|Ariel]] disguised as a [[harpy|Harpy]], who delivers an accusation of damnation against "three men of sin." The scene serves as [[prospero|Prospero]]'s orchestrated judgment -- a theatrical trial staging justice, guilt, and the beginning of repentance before the [[forgiveness|forgiveness]] of Act 5.

## Events

The scene opens with the court weary and despairing. [[alonso|Alonso]] declares: "Even here I will put off my hope" (010-010), having accepted that [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] is drowned. Meanwhile, [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] plot to murder [[alonso|Alonso]] that very night, whispering aside: "I say, to-night: no more" (010-026).

Suddenly, "solemn and strange music" sounds (010-027), and several "strange Shapes" enter, "bringing in a banquet" (010-030). They dance with "gentle actions of salutation" and gesture for the famished courtiers to eat before dep[[magic|art]]ing. [[sebastian|Sebastian]] marvels at the apparition, but [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] notes their gentle manners: "though they are of monstrous shape, yet, note, / Their manners are more gentle-kind than of / Our human generation you shall find / Many, nay, almost any" (010-046-010-049).

Just as the courtiers prepare to eat, "thunder and lightning" erupt and [[ariel|Ariel]] enters "like a harpy," claps his wings upon the table, and "with a quaint device, the banquet vanishes" (010-080). The men draw their swords, but [[ariel|Ariel]]'s opening words paralyze them:

> "You are three men of sin, whom Destiny, -- / That hath to instrument this lower world / And what is in't, -- the never-surfeited sea / Hath caused to belch up you; and on this island, / Where man doth not inhabit, -- you 'mongst men / Being most unfit to live. I have made you mad" (010-081-010-086)

[[ariel|Ariel]] mocks their drawn swords: "You fools! I and my fellows / Are ministers of Fate: the elements, / Of whom your swords are temper'd, may as well / Wound the loud winds" (010-089-010-092). The [[harpy|Harpy]] then delivers the core accusation -- that [[alonso|Alonso]], [[sebastian|Sebastian]], and [[antonio|Antonio]] "From [[milan|Milan]] did supplant good [[prospero|Prospero]]" and "Exposed unto the sea, which hath requit it, / Him and his innocent child" (010-099-010-100). For this sin, the "[[power|power]]s" have bereft [[alonso|Alonso]] of his son, and "lingering perdition" awaits unless they feel "he[[magic|art]]-sorrow / And a clear life ensuing."

[[prospero|Prospero]], watching invisibly from above, exults: "My high charms work, / And these mine enemies are all knit up / In their distractions; they now are in my [[power|power]]" (010-109-010-111).

The three guilty men are driven to distraction. [[alonso|Alonso]], consumed by guilt, hears the sea and winds speak [[prospero|Prospero]]'s name, and declares: "Therefore my son i' the ooze is bedded; and / I'll seek him deeper than e'er plummet sounded, / And with him there lie mudded" (010-123-010-125).

## Aftermath

The Banquet marks [[prospero|Prospero]]'s most theatrical use of [[magic|magical power]] -- a psychodrama staged explicitly for moral reckoning. Its immediate effect is to plunge [[alonso|Alonso]] into suicidal [[guilt]]. [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] reports that the three guilty men are "all three distracted, / And the remainder mourning over them" (014-016-014-018), their madness lasting into Act 5.

The Banquet is the bridge between [[prospero|Prospero]]'s punitive torments of his enemies and the [[forgiveness|forgiveness]] he ultimately extends. By externalizing [[alonso|Alonso]]'s guilt as a supernatural judgment, the spectacle breaks [[alonso|Alonso]]'s spirit, making possible his genuine [[guilt|penitence]] and the [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] at [[the-reconciliation|the play's end]]. It also demonstrates that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] can "catch the [[guilt|conscience]] of a king" through theater -- an [[magic|art]] that wounds, convicts, and prepares for [[forgiveness|mercy]].
