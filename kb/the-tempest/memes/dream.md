## Overview

Dreaming and [[sleep|waking]] form the ontological ground of *The Tempest*. "We are such stuff / As dreams are made on; and our little life / Is rounded with a sleep" (Act 4, Scene 1)—[[prospero|Prospero]]'s most famous lines collapse the distinction between reality and illusion, theatre and life. The play repeatedly stages moments where characters cannot tell whether they are dreaming: [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] hears [[ariel|Ariel]]'s [[music|song]] and wonders if it belongs to "i' th' air or th' e[[magic|art]]h" (003-524); [[miranda|Miranda]] sees the court and exclaims "O brave new world" (014-231) as if [[sleep|waking]] from a twelve-year sleep; [[alonso|Alonso]] moves through the action in a grief-stricken trance. The masque in Act 4 is the play's dream within a dream—a vision that dissolves even as it is beheld. Yet the dream motif cuts in two directions: sleep can be a medium of healing and wonder, but it can also be the accomplice of treachery when exploited to render men unconscious. The dream-world of the island is never merely benign; it shadows as much as it illuminates.

## In the Play

[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] is the first to experience the island as a dream-state. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s [[music|song]]—"Full fathom five thy father lies"—reaches him across the water, and he follows it "with hair up-staring" into [[miranda|Miranda]]'s presence. [[prospero|Prospero]] tells him that what he has undergone—[[the-shipwreck|the wreck]], the music, the meeting with [[miranda|Miranda]]—is "but a dream" designed to test him (Act 1, Scene 2).

### Sleep and Treachery: Sebastian and Antonio

If the [[island]]'s dreams are framed as instruments of restoration, the play also shows the darker face of sleep. In Act 2, Scene 1, [[ariel|Ariel]] puts the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed courtiers to sleep with [[music|solemn music]]—all except [[sebastian|Sebastian]] and [[antonio|Antonio]], who remain "unwonted" in their [[sleep|waking]] (005-195). The sleep that overtakes [[alonso|Alonso]] and [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] is a literal vulnerability: it is the condition that enables [[antonio|Antonio]] to whisper usurpation into [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s ear, urging him to murder his sleeping brother just as [[antonio|Antonio]] once deposed [[prospero|Prospero]]. This is the dream motif inverted—not the redemptive reverie of the masque but the weaponised unconsciousness that makes treachery possible. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s return to wake the sleepers with a [[music|song]] (005-350) at the critical moment restores the moral order, but the scene leaves its residue: sleep in this play is morally neutral, a void that can host either grace or [[guilt|guilt]] depending on who stands watch.

The Act 4 masque is the dream's fullest expression. [[juno|Juno]], [[ceres|Ceres]], and [[iris|Iris]] appear in "solemn and strange music" (010-027), performing a betrothal blessing. But the dream is interrupted—[[prospero|Prospero]] "st[[magic|art]]s suddenly, and speaks" (Act 4, Scene 1), remembering [[caliban|Caliban]]'s plot—and [[the-masque|the vision]] dissolves: "Our revels now are ended. These our actors … / Are melted into air, into thin air" (012-184–186). The meta-theatrical gesture is unmistakable: the masque was a play within a play, and its dissolution reflects the fragility of all performed reality. [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s response—"This is a most majestic vision, and / Harmonious charmingly" (012-143)—marks him as the ideal audience, one who receives the dream without grasping at it, and whose wonder persists even after [[the-masque|the vision]] fades.

### Caliban's Dream of Riches

The most poignant dream in the play belongs to its least regarded character. In Act 3, Scene 2, [[caliban|Caliban]] describes the island's nocturnal music to [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]], then confesses his own dream-life: "in dreaming, / The clouds methought would open, and show riches / Ready to drop upon me, that when I waked / I cried to dream again" (009-103–106). The speech is remarkable for its tenderness. [[caliban|Caliban]], the would-be rapist and rebel, is revealed as a creature capable of visionary longing. His dream is not of [[power|power]] or revenge but of abundance—a sky that opens and yields. That he weeps upon [[sleep|waking]], and that the dream makes him cry to return to it, aligns him unexpectedly with the play's deepest metaphysical current: life itself is a [[sleep|waking]] from which we long to return to the dream. Shakespeare gives the most lyrical dream-speech not to [[prospero|Prospero]] or [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] but to the "monster," complicating any simple moral schema.

[[miranda|Miranda]]'s wonder at the court in Act 5—"How beauteous mankind is! O brave new world, / That has such people in't!" (014-231)—is dream-like in its intensity. She has known only her father, [[caliban|Caliban]], and [[ariel|the spirit]]s. The sudden appearance of so many humans feels to her like a new creation.

### The Epilogue as Dream-Frame

The Epilogue extends the dream metaphor to the audience and completes the play's architecture of illusion. [[prospero|Prospero]], now without [[magic|art]], stands alone on stage and addresses the spectators directly: "As you from crimes would [[forgiveness|pardon]]ed be, / Let your indulgence set me free" (015-026–027). His plea for applause—to "[[freedom|release]] me from my bands"—reframes the entire play as a shared dream from which the audience's collective [[sleep|waking]] (their hands, their breath, their dep[[magic|art]]ure from the theatre) is the only exit. The island, [[the-shipwreck|the storm]], the masque, the [[forgiveness|reconciliation]]: all were a vision sustained by the audience's imaginative complicity. When the applause comes, the dream dissolves, and the actor who was [[prospero|Prospero]] returns to being a man. The effect is both humble—[[prospero|Prospero]] renounces the very [[magic|art]] that made him godlike—and audacious: Shakespeare asks his audience to recognise that they, too, are "such stuff as dreams are made on."

## Key Passages

> Our revels now are ended. These our actors,
> As I foretold you, were all spirits, and
> Are melted into air, into thin air:
> And, like the baseless fabric of this vision …
> We are such stuff
> As dreams are made on; and our little life
> Is rounded with a sleep.
> — (012-184–189, context)

The play's philosophical centre. The masque's dissolution becomes a meditation on mortality, illusion, and the common substance of dreams and life.

> How beauteous mankind is! O brave new world,
> That has such people in't!
> — (014-231)

[[miranda|Miranda]]'s exclamation treats reality as dream. Her innocence transforms the ordinary into the miraculous—a mode of perception the play both celebrates and gently ironises.

> Be not afeard. The isle is full of noises,
> Sounds and sweet airs that give delight and hurt not.
> Sometimes a thousand twangling instruments
> Will hum about mine ears; and sometimes voices,
> That if I then had waked after long sleep
> Will make me sleep again; and then, in dreaming,
> The clouds methought would open, and show riches
> Ready to drop upon me, that when I waked
> I cried to dream again.
> — (009-098–106, context)

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s dream-speech is one of the most lyrical passages in the play, and it comes from the character least expected to produce it. The dream of falling riches is Edenic—a world where the sky itself provides—and the grief of [[sleep|waking]] ("I cried to dream again") resonates with [[prospero|Prospero]]'s later meditation on the dissolution of visions. [[caliban|Caliban]], no less than the courtiers, is caught in the island's dream-web; his sensitivity to [[music|music]] and dream reveals a register of experience that the play's colonial reading cannot easily dismiss. The passage repositions [[caliban|Caliban]] not merely as monster but as dreamer—one for whom the [[sleep|waking]] world is the true deprivation.

> This is a most majestic vision, and
> Harmonious charmingly. May I be bold
> To think these spirits?
> — (012-143–145)

[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s response to the masque is the play's model of proper wonder. Unlike [[prospero|Prospero]], who breaks [[the-masque|the vision]] with anxious recollection, [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] receives it with unguarded reverence, asking only "May I be bold / To think these spirits?" His question is epistemological—are these real beings?—but it carries no demand for certainty. The dream is permitted to exist on its own terms, and [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s willingness to dwell within the illusion without testing its edges is precisely what [[prospero|Prospero]] has been trying to teach: the capacity to trust what you see, to accept vision without suspicion, is the condition of wonder. [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] passes the test that the audience is also asked to pass.

## Critical Context

Marjorie Garber, in *Dream in Shakespeare* (1974), reads the dream motif in *The Tempest* as the culminating expression of Shakespeare's career-long interest in the relationship between theatrical illusion and the unconscious. For Garber, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "our revels now are ended" speech is not merely a meditation on transience but a theory of drama itself: the play, like the dream, is a "baseless fabric"—a structure with no material foundation that nonetheless shapes the experience of those who inhabit it. Garber's framework illuminates why the play's dream-language intensifies precisely at moments of meta-theatrical self-reference: the masque, the Epilogue, and [[ariel|Ariel]]'s invisibility all call attention to the dream-like contract between performer and spectator. When [[prospero|Prospero]] tells [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] that [[the-shipwreck|the wreck]] and music were "but a dream," he is describing not a deception but the ontology of the stage, where everything we see is, in Garber's terms, "neither true nor false but presented."

C.L. Barber, in *Shakespeare's Festive Comedy* (1959), provides a complementary reading by situating the dream within the tradition of festive [[freedom|release]]. Barber argues that Shakespearean comedy typically moves through a "green world" of inverted norms and liberated desire before returning to a restored social order. *The Tempest* compresses this festive arc into a single day on the island, and dream becomes the mechanism of [[freedom|release]]: characters are put to sleep, awakened, entranced, and disenchanted in a rhythm that mirrors the festive pattern of mis[[authority|rule]] and return. For Barber, the Epilogue's direct appeal to the audience is the final festive gesture—the community of spectators is asked to complete the ritual by releasing the performer, dissolving the boundary between the world of the play and the world of the audience just as dreams dissolve the boundary between self and other. Anne B[[magic|art]]on deepens this reading by linking the masque to the court entertainment tradition: [[the-masque|the betrothal masque]] of [[juno|Juno]], [[ceres|Ceres]], and [[iris|Iris]], she argues, is a dream of [[music|harmony]] that [[prospero|Prospero]] cannot sustain within the world of the play precisely because real [[music|harmony]] requires the agency of the p[[magic|art]]icipants, not the coercion of [[prospero|the magician]]. The masque's interruption is thus not a failure of [[magic|art]] but a necessary turn toward the moral work of forgiveness, which must happen outside the dream.

Frank Kermode, in his Arden edition introduction, observes that the play's dream imagery functions as a sustained metaphor for the creative imagination itself. The phrase "such stuff as dreams are made on" (012-189) identifies the material of dreams not as nothing but as "stuff"—a substance, however insubstantial, that has ontological weight. Kermode connects this to Renaissance theories of the imagination, which held that the faculty of *phantasia* could produce real effects in the body and the world. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]], in this reading, is the disciplined imagination made operative: he dreams with intent, and the island reshapes itself accordingly. The difference between [[prospero|Prospero]] and the other characters is not that he alone is awake but that he alone knows he is dreaming—a gnosis he finally surrenders in the Epilogue, when he steps out of the dream-frame and asks the audience to do the same.
