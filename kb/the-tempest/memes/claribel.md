## Overview

Claribel is [[alonso|Alonso]]'s daughter and [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s sister, a figure who never appears on stage but whose recent marriage to [[tunis|the King of Tunis]] provides the occasion for the sea voyage that opens the play. She exists at the margins of the drama — spoken of but never speaking — yet her off-stage presence anchors the play's Mediterranean geography and its themes of loss, distance, and political alliance.

## Role in the Play

Claribel enters the play through [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s observation in Act 2, Scene 1, that the court p[[magic|art]]y's garments are miraculously fresh despite the [[storm|shipwreck]], "as when we put them on first in [[afric|Afric]], at the marriage of the king's fair daughter Claribel to [[tunis|the King of Tunis]]" (005-059). This establishes that the Neapolitan fleet was returning from Claribel's wedding when [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[storm|tempest]] struck.

The marriage itself becomes a point of tension. [[sebastian|Sebastian]] reproaches [[alonso|Alonso]] for having married his daughter so far from [[naples|Naples]] — "Sir, you may thank yourself for this great loss, / That would not bless our Europe with your daughter, / But rather lose her to an [[afric|Africa]]n" (005-273) — blaming the king's political choice for the present disaster. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s grief over [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] compounds this [[guilt|guilt]]: marrying one child far away has seemingly cost him the other.

Claribel briefly resurfaces in the final act, when the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed p[[magic|art]]y's restoration is confirmed: "Did Claribel her husband find at [[tunis|Tunis]]" (014-269) confirms her safe arrival and marriage, closing the geographic arc that [[the-shipwreck|the tempest]] interrupted.

## Key Scenes

Claribel is discussed rather than seen. Her presence is felt in three key passages: the extended political debate in Act 2, Scene 1 (where [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]], [[adrian|Adrian]], [[sebastian|Sebastian]], and [[antonio|Antonio]] discuss [[tunis|Tunis]], [[carthage|Carthage]], [[widow-dido|Dido]], and the [[knowledge|wisdom]] of [[alonso|Alonso]]'s diplomatic marriage), [[antonio|Antonio]]'s rhetorical deployment of her distance — "A space whose every cubit / Seems to cry out, 'How shall that Claribel / Measure us back to [[naples|Naples]]?'" (005-290) — and the brief restoration notice in Act 5. In both instances she functions as a narrative device — her wedding voyage is the play's point of dep[[magic|art]]ure, and her safe arrival at [[tunis|Tunis]] is p[[magic|art]] of the general comic resolution.

## Relationships

- **[[alonso|Alonso]]** (father): The King of [[naples|Naples]], who arranged her diplomatic marriage to [[tunis|the King of Tunis]]. His decision to marry her "to an [[afric|Africa]]n" becomes a source of recrimination from [[sebastian|Sebastian]].
- **[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]** (brother): The [[ferdinand|Prince of Naples]], separated from his sister by her marriage. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s grief for the supposedly drowned [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] is intertwined with his sense of having already "lost" Claribel to a distant marriage.
- **The King of [[tunis|Tunis]]** (husband): An unseen political figure whose marriage to Claribel occasions the Mediterranean voyage.
