## Overview

The masque was a distinctive genre of Jacobean court entertainment — an extravagant fusion of poetry, [[music|music]], dance, costume, and scenic spectacle performed before the monarch at Whitehall Palace. Shakespeare embeds a masque within *The Tempest* in Act 4, Scene 1, where [[prospero|Prospero]] stages a betrothal pageant for [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]]. The embedded masque draws on all the conventions of the courtly form — allegorical deities, sung blessings, pastoral dances — only to have [[prospero|Prospero]] abruptly dissolve it with the famous speech "Our revels now are ended."

## In the Play

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s masque occupies approximately 90 lines of Act 4, Scene 1. [[iris|Iris]] (012-076) summons [[ceres|Ceres]] (012-077); [[juno|Juno]] descends (012-126); [[ceres|Ceres]] inquires after [[venus|Venus]] and is reassured of her absence; [[juno|Juno]] sings a blessing of "honour, riches, marriage-blessing" (012-131); [[ceres|Ceres]] adds agricultural abundance; [[iris|Iris]] summons the [[nymph|Naiads]] (012-160), and [[nymph|nymphs]] dance with reapers. The sequence is a compressed but complete court masque, complete with its characteristic anti-masque gesture — here displaced onto [[venus|Venus]] and Cupid, who are mentioned only to be banished.

The masque's abrupt termination is the scene's most significant gesture. [[prospero|Prospero]], "st[[magic|art]]ing suddenly," remembers "that foul conspiracy / Of the beast [[caliban|Caliban]] and his confederates / Against my life" and dissolves [[the-masque|the pageant]] mid-celebration. The moment is meta-theatrical — [[prospero|Prospero]] dismantles the fiction he himself has constructed — and it precipitates the play's most famous speech on the transience of [[magic|art]] and life:

> "Our revels now are ended. These our actors, / As I foretold you, were all spirits, and / Are melted into air, into thin air."
> — (012-197)

The dissolution of the masque recapitulates the play's central tension: [[magic|art]] is [[power|power]]ful, but it is also temporary. The magician who [[authority|command]]s spirits and stages pageants must remember that the "baseless fabric of this [[dream|vision]]" will fade — "We are such stuff / As dreams are made on; and our little life / Is rounded with a [[sleep|sleep]]."

## Critical Context

Critics have noted that the embedded masque functions as a figure for Shakespeare's own theatrical [[magic|art]]. The Jacobean court masque, with its elaborate scenic machinery designed by Inigo Jones, represented the most technologically sophisticated performance form of the period — a rival to the public theatre's more language-driven aesthetic. By embedding and then dissolving a masque within his play, Shakespeare simultaneously acknowledges the form's [[power|power]] and asserts the superiority of his own dramatic medium: the masque is beautiful, but it cannot incorporate [[caliban|Caliban]]'s conspiracy, cannot sustain the complexity of human experience that the play itself contains.
