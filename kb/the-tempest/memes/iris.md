## Overview

Iris is one of [[ariel|the spirit]] figures conjured by [[prospero]] for [[the-masque|the betrothal masque]] in Act 4, Scene 1. She is the classical goddess of the rainbow and serves as messenger to [[juno]], queen of the gods. In the masque, Iris summons [[ceres]] to the celebration of the betrothal between [[ferdinand]] and [[miranda]] and presides over the pastoral blessing. As the rainbow deity who bridges the celestial and terrestrial realms, Iris embodies the masque's thematic movement between the divine order of marriage and the fertile abundance of the natural world. Iris is listed among [[ariel|the spirit]]s in the Dramatis Personae (P02-001).

## Character and Role

Iris opens the masque by calling upon [[ceres|Ceres]], the goddess of agriculture, to join the proceedings and bless the young couple. Her speech is richly poetic, filled with imagery of the natural world — "rich leas, wheat, rye, barley, vetches, oats, and pease" — befitting her role as the rainbow goddess who bridges heaven and e[[magic|art]]h.

> "Ceres, most bounteous lady, thy rich leas / Of wheat, rye, barley, vetches, oats, and pease..."
> — (012-077)

After [[juno]] descends and delivers her blessing, Iris is sent on employment by [[juno|Juno]] and [[ceres|Ceres]], who whisper instructions to her (012-154). She then summons the [[nymph|Naiads]] — the [[nymph|nymphs]] of streams and rivers — to join the celebration (012-160), linking the pastoral and aquatic dimensions of the masque.

> "You nymphs, call'd Naiads, of the windring brooks, / With your sedg'd crowns and ever-harmless looks..."
> — (012-160)

Iris also speaks the line that names the masque's purpose: "A contract of true love to celebrate" (012-102). Her function is structurally crucial: she is the summoner, the intermediary who calls each deity into [[the-masque|the pageant]] and dispatches the pastoral dancers. Without her mediating voice, the masque's supernatural hierarchy — Iris to [[ceres|Ceres]], Iris to [[juno|Juno]], Iris to the [[nymph|Naiads]] — would collapse into a series of unconnected en[[dream|trance]]s. She is the thread that stitches [[the-masque|the pageant]] together.

## Key Scenes

Iris appears exclusively within [[the-masque|the betrothal masque]] (Act 4, Scene 1). Her en[[dream|trance]] is marked by the stage direction "Enter Iris" (012-076). She serves as the [[master|master]] of ceremonies: summoning [[ceres|Ceres]], welcoming [[juno|Juno]], and marshalling the [[nymph|nymphs]] for the celebratory dance. Her role is entirely within the enchanted pageant that [[prospero|Prospero]] stages and abruptly dissolves when he remembers [[caliban]]'s conspiracy.

## Critical Reception

Critics have noted Iris's role as p[[magic|art]] of the masque's carefully structured hierarchy of divine [[power|authority]]. As the rainbow — a natural phenomenon that arches between heaven and e[[magic|art]]h — Iris embodies the mediating function that the entire masque performs: connecting the divine realm of [[juno|Juno]]'s [[authority|command]]ments to the terrestrial realm of [[ceres|Ceres]]'s agricultural blessings. Frank Kermode observed that the masque's goddesses are arranged in a descending order of celestial rank: [[juno|Juno]] (queen of heaven), Iris (her messenger), [[ceres|Ceres]] (an e[[magic|art]]h-goddess). Iris occupies the pivotal middle position — neither fully celestial nor fully terrestrial, she is the conduit through which divine will reaches the natural world.

The whispered conference between [[juno|Juno]], [[ceres|Ceres]], and Iris — a conspiratorial aside within an already [[magic|art]]ificial pageant — has drawn attention from scholars interested in the masque's self-conscious theatricality. The stage direction "[[juno|Juno]] and [[ceres|Ceres]] whisper, and send Iris on employment" (012-154) creates a nested performance: the goddesses perform for [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]], while Iris performs for the goddesses, while [[the-spirits|Prospero's spirits]] perform for [[prospero|Prospero]], while Shakespeare's actors perform for the audience. This layering of spectatorial relationships is characteristic of late Shakespearean romance, where the boundary between watcher and watched is perpetually unstable.
