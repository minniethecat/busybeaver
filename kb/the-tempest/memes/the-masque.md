## Overview

The Masque in Act 4, Scene 1 is the play's most elaborate spectacle and its most explicit meta-theatrical moment. At [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|command]], [[ariel|Ariel]] conjures the goddesses [[iris|Iris]], [[ceres|Ceres]], and [[juno|Juno]], who perform a betrothal blessing for [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]]. The pageant celebrates fertility, marriage, and chaste love — the values [[prospero|Prospero]] wishes to consecrate. But the masque is shattered when [[prospero|Prospero]] "st[[magic|art]]s suddenly, and speaks" (stage direction, 012-172), remembering [[caliban|Caliban]]'s conspiracy against his life. The dissolution of the vision prompts the play's most famous meditation on impermanence: "Our revels now are ended … We are such stuff / As dreams are made on; and our little life / Is rounded with a [[sleep|sleep]]" (012-184–194).

The masque is a play within the play — an instance of [[prospero|Prospero]] exercising the same [[magic|art]] that created the [[storm|shipwreck]], but this time the performance is displayed openly to its audience ([[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]]) and to the theatre audience simultaneously. Its abrupt dissolution is the pivot on which the play turns from magic to mortality, from [[magic|art]] to life, from the [[island|island]] to [[milan|Milan]].

## Context

The masque is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s gift to [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] — what he calls "some vanity of mine [[magic|art]]" (012-044), presented as a "revels" to celebrate their betrothal. But it is also a test and an instruction. Before the masque begins, [[prospero|Prospero]] delivers a stern warning to [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] about premarital chastity: "If thou dost break her virgin-knot before / All sanctimonious ceremonies may / With full and holy rite be minister'd … barren hate, / Sour-eyed disdain and discord shall bestrew / The union of your bed" (012-014–023). The masque that follows is a dramatisation of this warning: [[juno|Juno]] presides over lawful marriage, [[ceres|Ceres]] over fertility, while [[venus|Venus]] and Cupid — the deities of illicit desire — are explicitly banished from the pageant.

The [[masque-form|masque form]] itself carried specific Jacobean associations. Court masques, such as those designed by Inigo Jones and Ben Jonson, were elaborate spectacles of royal [[power|power]] in which the monarch was both audience and symbolic centre. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s masque inverts this tradition: the monarch ([[prospero|Prospero]]) is also the [[magic|art]]ist, and the spectacle is not a display of [[power|power]] but a gift offered in love — and a gift that fails.

## Dramatic Function

The masque serves three interlocking dramatic functions. First, it is the culminating expression of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] — the most beautiful and controlled thing he creates. The goddesses' speeches are stately and harmonious; the dance is "a graceful dance" (stage direction); the vision seems to achieve exactly what [[prospero|Prospero]] intends. For the only time in the play, magic produces not terror or confusion but pure aesthetic pleasure.

Second, the masque's dissolution dramatises the limit of [[magic|art]]. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] cannot sustain itself against the intrusion of reality. The conspiracy of [[caliban|Caliban]], [[stephano|Stephano]], and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] — drunken, clumsy, murderous — is the antithesis of the goddesses' grace, and its eruption into [[prospero|Prospero]]'s consciousness shatters the vision. The "strange, hollow, and confused noise" to which [[ariel|the spirit]]s vanish is the sound of the real world breaking through the aesthetic frame.

Third, the dissolution prompts [[prospero|Prospero]]'s great meditation on impermanence — "Our revels now are ended" — which extends the masque's fragility into a universal principle: all things dissolve, all [[dream|vision]]s fade, all lives end. The speech transforms a theatrical accident (the actor playing [[prospero|Prospero]] forgot his next line? the character [[prospero|Prospero]] remembered [[caliban|Caliban]]?) into a philosophical [[dream|vision]] that encompasses "the great globe itself" — the theatre, the world, and everything in it.

## Key Passages

> Ceres: … Venus and her son,
> As they did plot to take from me my daughter,
> Are both dispersèd; here they shall not come,
> To plot against the honour of the bride.
> Tell me, heavenly bow,
> If Venus or her son, as thou dost know,
> Do now attend the queen?
> — (012-071–077)

[[ceres|Ceres]]'s interrogation of [[iris|Iris]] — are [[venus|Venus]] and Cupid present? — dramatises the masque's moral programme. The betrothal blessing is explicitly a chaste one, from which the deities of erotic love are banished. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] here is pedagogical: he is teaching [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] what kind of love he expects them to practise.

> Ferdinand: Let me live here ever;
> So rare a wonder'd father and a wife
> Makes this place Paradise.
> — (012-119–121)

[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s response to the masque — the [[island|island]] as Paradise — captures the utopian dimension of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] at its most beautiful. But the word "rare" (extraordinary and fragile) and the wish to "live here ever" (an impossibility) already contain the dissolution to come. Paradise in *The Tempest* is always about to end.

> Prospero: … I had forgot that foul conspiracy
> Of the beast Caliban and his confederates
> Against my life: the minute of their plot
> Is almost come.
> — (012-173–176)

The stage direction preceding this is stark: "[[prospero|Prospero]] st[[magic|art]]s suddenly, and speaks; after which, to a strange, hollow, and confused noise, they heavily vanish." The interruption is physical — a st[[magic|art]], a noise, a vanishing — before it is verbal. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s recollection of [[caliban|Caliban]] is the intrusion of the political (conspiracy) and the physical (the beast, the body, the threat of death) into the aesthetic space of the masque.

> Our revels now are ended. These our actors,
> As I foretold you, were all spirits and
> Are melted into air, into thin air:
> And, like the baseless fabric of this vision,
> The cloud-capp'd towers, the gorgeous palaces,
> The solemn temples, the great globe itself,
> Yea, all which it inherit, shall dissolve,
> And, like this insubstantial pageant faded,
> Leave not a rack behind. We are such stuff
> As dreams are made on, and our little life
> Is rounded with a sleep.
> — (012-184–194)

The play's philosophical centre. The movement from the masque's dissolution to the dissolution of "the great globe itself" — the Globe Theatre, the e[[magic|art]]h, everything — compresses the play's entire meditation on impermanence into eleven lines. "We are such stuff / As dreams are made on" identifies human beings with the vanished spirits; the difference between actor and audience, spirit and human, dissolves in the shared condition of mortality.

## Aftermath

The dissolution of the masque is immediate and complete. The spirits "heavily vanish" — heavily, not gracefully, because the vision is not concluded but broken. [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] are bewildered; [[prospero|Prospero]] must console them with the speech about dreams and [[sleep|sleep]].

But the interruption also recalls [[prospero|Prospero]] to his unfinished business. He summons [[ariel|Ariel]], who reports that [[caliban|Caliban]], [[stephano|Stephano]], and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] are approaching, "all … red-hot with drinking" and "lost … in the line-grove" (012-211–212). [[prospero|Prospero]] and [[ariel|Ariel]] set spirit-hounds upon them — a violent counter-magic that drives the conspirators through briars and into the "foul lake" (012-252). The masque's dissolution thus triggers the play's movement from [[magic|art]] to action: [[prospero|Prospero]] stops being the playwright and becomes the duke again, preparing to confront his enemies in Act 5.

The masque leaves one lasting trace. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s "glistering apparel" (012-239), which lured [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] off the path of conspiracy into the path of theft and punishment, is p[[magic|art]] of the masque's costume store — the detritus of [[magic|art]] repurposed as a tool of justice. The beautiful and the practical, the aesthetic and the political, are never fully separable in *The Tempest*.

## Critical Notes

The masque has been a central text for interpretations of *The Tempest* as Shakespeare's farewell to the stage. Edward Dowden, in *Shakspere: A Critical Study of His Mind and [[art|Art]]* (1875), read "Our revels now are ended" as Shakespeare's own valediction, spoken through [[prospero|Prospero]] — an identification that has been both influential and controversial. The Cambridge editors of the 1863 edition, whose textual notes form our Critical Apparatus, were more concerned with staging questions: whether the masque was "solemn" or "strange" music (030-001), the placement of [[prospero|Prospero]] "on the top (invisible)" (030-001), and the disputed attribution of certain speeches.

The connection between [[prospero|Prospero]]'s masque and the Jacobean [[masque-form|court masque]] form has been explored in detail by scholars of Renaissance drama. Inigo Jones and Ben Jonson's masques at the court of James I were spectacular displays of royal [[power|power]] in which the monarch was both audience and symbolic centre of the performance. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s masque — a spectacle created by a deposed [[authority|rule]]r on a deserted [[island|island]], performed for an audience of two young lovers — simultaneously invokes and subverts this tradition. The [[power|power]] on display is not political but personal: a father's blessing, an [[magic|art]]ist's gift. And its dissolution marks the exhaustion of that [[power|power]], not its apotheosis.

More recent criticism has drawn attention to the masque's exclusion of [[venus|Venus]] and Cupid. The banishment of erotic love from the betrothal celebration has been read as [[prospero|Prospero]]'s attempt to control [[miranda|Miranda]]'s sexuality, to ensure that the marriage he has arranged proceeds according to his script. The goddesses who appear — [[ceres|Ceres]] (fertility without desire), [[juno|Juno]] (marriage without passion), [[iris|Iris]] (messenger without agency) — are instruments of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s patriarchal design. The masque, in this reading, is not a celebration of love but a display of control.
