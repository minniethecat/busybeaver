## Overview

Freedom is the aspiration that drives the play's subordinated characters and structures its resolution. [[ariel|Ariel]] serves [[prospero|Prospero]] in hope of liberty; [[caliban|Caliban]] resists his [[master|master]] and seeks a new one who promises better terms; [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] accepts [[servitude|bondage]] as the price of [[miranda|Miranda]]'s love; and even [[prospero|Prospero]] ultimately frees himself from the [[island]] and from the magic that isolated him. The play distinguishes between freedom *from* (servitude, imprisonment, exile) and freedom *to* (return, [[authority|rule]], [[forgiveness|reconciliation]]), tracing how each character's understanding of freedom shapes their choices.

The play's most radical insight is that freedom is never absolute but always relational: one character's liberty depends on another's grant. [[prospero|Prospero]] holds the keys to everyone's freedom — [[ariel|Ariel]]'s release from [[servitude|service]], [[caliban|Caliban]]'s continued subjection, the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed nobles' return to [[naples|Naples]] — and exercises that power with the capriciousness of a [[authority|rule]]r who understands that freedom is, finally, a political instrument rather than an unalienable right. The [[island]] itself functions as a laboratory for testing what freedom means when the normal constraints of European society — law, hierarchy, property — are suspended. By the play's end, every character's relationship to freedom has been transformed, yet the transformations are profoundly unequal: [[ariel|Ariel]] gains the liberty he was promised, [[caliban|Caliban]] is merely [[forgiveness|pardon]]ed and re-enrolled in servitude, and [[prospero|Prospero]] trades magical omnipotence for ducal [[authority|authority]].

## In the Play

[[ariel|Ariel]]'s demand for freedom is the play's most consistent refrain. Having been imprisoned in a pine tree by [[sycorax|Sycorax]] for twelve years, [[ariel|Ariel]] was freed by [[prospero|Prospero]]—but only into a new form of servitude. Throughout the play, [[ariel|Ariel]] reminds [[prospero|Prospero]] of his promise: "Is there more toil? … Let me remember thee what thou hast promised, / Which is not yet performed me … my liberty" (Act 1, Scene 2). When freedom finally comes at the play's end—"But yet thou shalt have freedom" (014-109)—it is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s last act of [[authority|command]].

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s pursuit of freedom takes a different form. He professes loyalty to [[stephano|Stephano]]—"I'll show thee every fertile inch o' th' island; / And I will kiss thy foot: I prithee, be my god" (Act 2, Scene 2)—trading one [[master|master]] for another. His rebellion is a parody of political revolution: he mistakes the butler for a king and wine for liberty.

[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] transforms the meaning of freedom by accepting servitude willingly. When [[miranda|Miranda]] offers to help him carry logs, he replies that "one word more / Shall make me chide thee, if not hate thee" (Act 3, Scene 1)—his freely chosen labour elevates him above compulsion. His declaration—"with a he[[magic|art]] as willing / As [[servitude|bondage]] e'er of freedom" (008-124–125)—redefines [[servitude|bondage]] as a form of devotion rather than degradation.

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s freedom, paradoxically, comes through renunciation. By breaking his staff and drowning his book, he liberates himself from the magic that gave him power at the cost of his humanity. The epilogue extends this logic: [[prospero|Prospero]], now powerless on the stage, asks the audience to "release me from my bands / With the help of your good hands" (Epilogue), making freedom a gift that only others can bestow.

### The Two Freedoms of Prospero

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s relationship to freedom is marked by a profound asymmetry between the political and the personal. The political freedom he reclaims — his restoration as Duke of [[milan|Milan]] — is the freedom to govern, to inherit, to resume his place in the hierarchy from which he was exiled. It is a freedom of return, of restitution. But the personal freedom he achieves — the breaking of his staff, the drowning of his book — is a freedom *from*: from obsession, from the addictive power of [[magic|art]], from the identity of the wronged man that has defined him for twelve years. "But this rough [[magic|magic]] / I here abjure" (014-061–062) is not a triumphant declaration but a relinquishment. [[prospero|Prospero]] trades the absolute freedom of the island-god — the power to [[authority|command]] spirits, raise storms, and bend enemies to his will — for the constrained freedom of a mortal duke who must answer to political realities. The Epilogue makes this cost explicit: stripped of "Spirits to enforce, [[magic|art]] to enchant" (015-015), [[prospero|Prospero]] becomes "a weak and sickly old man" who can only achieve freedom through the audience's indulgence — "Unless I be relieved by prayer" (015-017). Political freedom is restored; magical freedom is renounced. Neither, in the end, is sufficient on its own.

### Ariel's Liberty and Caliban's Subjection

The [[fortune|fate]]s of [[ariel|Ariel]] and [[caliban|Caliban]] at the play's close reveal the racial and colonial logic that governs freedom in *The Tempest*. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s freedom — "But yet thou shalt have freedom: so, so, so" (014-109) — is the last [[authority|command]] [[prospero|Prospero]] issues before the Epilogue, a reward for obedient [[servitude|service]] rendered by an airy spirit who can be assimilated into the order of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s world. [[caliban|Caliban]], by contrast, receives no such liberation. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s final words about his slave are "Set [[caliban|Caliban]] and his companions free" (014-325) — but this "free" is not emancipation; it is release from the immediate torments of the scene (the stolen garments, the hounding by spirits) into a custody that will continue. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s earlier acknowledgment — "This thing of darkness I / Acknowledge mine" (014-261–262) — is not an admission of [[guilt|guilt]] but an assertion of ownership: [[caliban|Caliban]] remains "mine." The asymmetry is structural. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s freedom is earned through compliance; [[caliban|Caliban]]'s subjection is perpetuated through biology. The play closes with one freed spirit disappearing into the elements and one captive body returned to [[milan|Milan]], where his otherness will presumably continue to be managed rather than liberated.

### The Island as a Space of Suspended Constraints

The unnamed island is the enabling condition for every freedom explored in the play. Removed from the legal and political frameworks of Europe, it functions as what Michel Foucault would call a heterotopia — a counter-space where normal [[authority|rule]]s are suspended and alternative orders become possible. Here [[prospero|Prospero]] can practice a magic that would be unthinkable in [[milan|Milan]]; here [[caliban|Caliban]] can lay claim to a territory — "This island's mine, by [[sycorax|Sycorax]] my mother" — that European property law would never recognize; here the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed nobles can plot murder and usurpation without the normal consequences of treason. The island's freedom is its lawlessness, and every character exploits this condition differently. For [[prospero|Prospero]], it enables total [[power|dominion]]; for [[ariel|Ariel]], it is a prison disguised as a workplace; for [[caliban|Caliban]], it is the stolen inheritance around which his entire claim to freedom revolves. The play's ending — the dep[[magic|art]]ure from the island — is also the end of this experimental space. What "freedom" means once everyone returns to [[naples|Naples]] is left deliberately open, as if the island's magic cannot survive translation back into European politics.

## Key Passages

> But yet thou shalt have freedom: so, so, so.
> — (014-109)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s final words to [[ariel|Ariel]] before releasing him. The triple "so, so, so" suggests the casualness of a [[master|master]] who has always held the key—and the long delay before turning it.

> As bondage e'er of freedom: here's my hand.
> — (008-125)

[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s paradox. He finds more freedom in chosen [[servitude|service]] than in unconstrained liberty, a transformation that the play presents as the foundation of love.

> Freedom, hey-day! hey-day! freedom, freedom, hey-day, freedom!
> — (006-096)

[[stephano|Stephano]]'s drunken celebration after surviving the [[storm|shipwreck]]. The comic repetition exposes the gap between claiming and possessing freedom—[[stephano|Stephano]] is free only in his own imagination.

> But release me from my bands / With the help of your good hands.
> — (015-010–011)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s Epilogue appeal transforms the audience into the agents of his freedom. Having spent the entire play as the sole grantor of liberty to others, he now stands powerless and must beg for his own release. The theatrical metaphor — applause as emancipation — makes freedom a collective act, a gift that the audience must choose to bestow. The metatheatrical gesture collapses the distinction between character and actor, between the fictional island and the real playhouse. Freedom, in this final reframing, is not seized but received.

> This thing of darkness I / Acknowledge mine.
> — (014-261–262)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s admission encapsulates the play's most troubling insights about freedom and ownership. To "acknowledge" [[caliban|Caliban]] as "mine" is simultaneously to accept responsibility and to assert possession. [[caliban|Caliban]] is not freed by this acknowledgment; he is reclassified from enemy to property. The word "thing" — dehumanising and absolute — closes off any possibility that [[caliban|Caliban]] might achieve the liberty granted to [[ariel|Ariel]]. The line has become a touchstone for postcolonial criticism precisely because it reveals the way colonial power frames subjection as a form of belonging.

> I'll break my staff, / Bury it certain fathoms in the earth, / And deeper than did ever plummet sound / I'll drown my book.
> — (014-066–068)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s abjuration of his [[magic|art]] is the pivot on which all the play's freedoms turn. Before he can grant liberty to [[ariel|Ariel]], [[forgiveness|pardon]] his enemies, or return to [[milan|Milan]], he must first free himself from the instruments of his own power. The verbs — break, bury, drown — are acts of deliberate self-diminishment, each one more absolute than the last. The staff ([[authority|command]] over spirits) is broken; the book ([[knowledge|knowledge]], the source of the [[magic|art]]) is drowned beyond recovery. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s freedom is purchased through destruction, a pattern the play extends to the very concept of freedom itself: no one in *The Tempest* becomes free without losing something that defined them.

## Critical Context

Scholarly engagement with freedom in *The Tempest* has long recognised that the play's treatment of liberty cannot be separated from its colonial and theatrical contexts. **Stephen Greenblatt**, in *Shakespearean Negotiations* (1988) and subsequent work, reads [[prospero|Prospero]]'s power as an analogue for European colonial [[power|authority]], where the freedom of the coloniser depends on the unfreedom of the colonised. Greenblatt argues that the play registers — even as it aestheticises — the violence by which one culture claims the right to determine another's liberty, and that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s renunciation of magic is less an act of liberation than a strategic withdrawal that leaves the structures of colonial power intact. For Greenblatt, the freedom promised at the play's end is shadowed by the forms of subjection the play cannot imagine undoing.

**Paul Brown**, in his influential essay "'This thing of darkness I acknowledge mine': *The Tempest* and the Discourse of Colonialism" (1985), focuses on the line that has become the crux of materialist and postcolonial readings. Brown argues that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "acknowledgment" of [[caliban|Caliban]] is not an egalitarian recognition but a discursive strategy that naturalises colonial [[servitude|mastery]]: to "acknowledge" the "thing of darkness" as "mine" is to absorb the colonised subject into the coloniser's domain while denying that subject any claim to autonomy. The limits of freedom in the play, for Brown, are drawn at the colour line — [[ariel|Ariel]]'s airy insubstantiality can be freed, but [[caliban|Caliban]]'s embodied darkness must remain possessed.

**Frank Kermode**, the editor of the Arden second series edition of *The Tempest* (1954), offers a reading that places freedom in a metatheatrical frame. For Kermode, [[ariel|Ariel]]'s desire for liberty is a figure for the actor's relationship to the playwright: [[ariel|the spirit]]-performer serves at the [[master|master]]'s pleasure, his freedom deferred until the performance is complete. The Epilogue's appeal for release — "let your indulgence set me free" — extends this trope to the audience, making the theatre itself a space in which freedom is negotiated between those who perform and those who watch. Kermode's reading reminds us that the play's obsession with freedom operates not only at the level of character and politics but at the level of dramatic form itself.

**Ania Loomba** deepens the postcolonial critique by insisting that freedom in *The Tempest* must be read through the intersecting lenses of race and gender. In *Gender, Race, Renaissance Drama* (1989), Loomba argues that [[caliban|Caliban]]'s exclusion from the freedom granted to [[ariel|Ariel]] is not accidental but structural: the play's hierarchy of liberation maps onto early modern racial taxonomies, where the airy, compliant servant can be assimilated but the dark, recalcitrant one must be contained. Loomba also notes that [[miranda|Miranda]]'s freedom — her marriage to [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]], her passage from daughter to wife — is the one form of liberty the play naturalises completely, effacing its own patriarchal logic by presenting it as love. For Loomba, *The Tempest* reveals that freedom, in the colonial imagination, is never universal but always apportioned along lines of race, gender, and class.
