## Overview

Naples is the political counterweight to [[milan|Milan]] in the play's geography of power. [[alonso|Alonso]], King of Naples, aided [[antonio|Antonio]]'s usurpation of [[prospero|Prospero]] in ex[[transformation|change]] for [[milan|Milan]] becoming tributary to Naples — a transaction that entangled both states in guilt. The ship that founders in the opening storm is returning from [[alonso|Alonso]]'s daughter [[claribel|Claribel]]'s wedding in [[tunis|Tunis]] to Naples — a journey across the Mediterranean that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] intercepts. Naples represents the world of normal political relations that the island suspends and ultimately restores: at the play's end, all characters will sail there for the wedding of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] before [[prospero|Prospero]] retires to [[milan|Milan]].

Naples is never seen on stage, but its shadow falls across the entire play. The court that wanders the island is [[court-of-naples|the Neapolitan court]]; the king who grieves for his son is [[alonso|the King of Naples]]; the prince who falls in love with [[miranda|Miranda]] is the heir to the Neapolitan throne. The play's resolution — marriage between the heir of Naples and the daughter of [[milan|Milan]] — is a dynastic solution to a dynastic crime: the tributary relationship that [[antonio|Antonio]] imposed is replaced by a marital alliance that [[prospero|Prospero]] sanctions.

## In the Play

Naples enters the play as a political fact, established in [[prospero|Prospero]]'s narration. [[antonio|Antonio]], "new-created / The creatures that were mine … or [[transformation|change]]d 'em, / Or else new-form'd 'em" (003-114) within [[milan|Milan]]'s administration, and then "extort from [[milan|Milan]] … / Homage and … annual tribute" to Naples. This was the price of [[alonso|Alonso]]'s support: [[milan|Milan]] became a client state of Naples. The political crime is not only usurpation ([[antonio|Antonio]] against [[prospero|Prospero]]) but also subjugation (Naples against [[milan|Milan]]).

[[alonso|Alonso]]'s guilt is structurally different from [[antonio|Antonio]]'s. He is complicit — he agreed to the deal — but he is also capable of [[guilt|remorse]]. His grief for [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]], his recognition of his own guilt, and his eventual restoration make him the play's model of penitent [[power|authority]]. This reaches its climax in Act 5 when [[alonso|Alonso]] resigns the dukedom and entreats [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[forgiveness|pardon]] (014-110). [[antonio|Antonio]], by contrast, never speaks after Act 2 and does not reply to [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness. Naples, in the person of its king, is redeemable; [[milan|Milan]], in the person of its usurper, may not be.

The ship's journey from [[tunis|Tunis]] to Naples frames the play temporally. [[prospero|Prospero]] explicitly names the voyage: the court was "returning from the marriage of the king's fair daughter [[claribel|Claribel]] to [[tunis|the King of Tunis]]" (003-163), placing Naples at the terminus of a Mediterranean itinerary. [[claribel|Claribel]]'s wedding — "the marriage of the king's fair daughter [[claribel|Claribel]] to [[tunis|the King of Tunis]]" (005-064) — is the event the court is returning from when [[the-shipwreck|the storm]] hits. [[tunis|Tunis]] is at the opposite end of the Mediterranean from Naples; the return journey is long enough to make interception plausible and to give the play's events their compressed, dreamlike temporality. [[claribel|Claribel]]'s absence from the play — she is married and gone, beyond the reach of either guilt or restoration — is a reminder that the political world exceeds the frame of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]].

At the play's end, Naples is the destination for all. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s final speech announces: "in the morn / I'll bring you to your ship, and so to Naples, / Where I have hope to see the nuptial / Of these our dear-beloved solemnized" (014-287). The wedding will be in Naples, not [[milan|Milan]] — a gesture of [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] that reverses the tributary humiliation. [[milan|Milan]] and Naples are now equals, united by marriage rather than domination.

## Symbolic Significance

Naples in *The Tempest* functions as the site of the political future. [[milan|Milan]] is the past — the place of loss and betrayal to which [[prospero|Prospero]] returns to die. Naples is the future — the place where [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] will marry, reign, and produce heirs. The dynastic resolution of the play is worked out on the axis between these two Italian states, and the marriage that links them transforms a relationship of subjugation (tribute) into a relationship of alliance (marriage).

Naples also represents the possibility of political redemption. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s arc — from guilt to grief to repentance to restoration — is the play's most fully realised moral trajectory. When [[alonso|Alonso]] declares "Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat / Thou [[forgiveness|pardon]] me my wrongs" (014-110), he performs the act of political surrender that makes [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] possible. Unlike [[antonio|Antonio]] (who is silent), [[sebastian|Sebastian]] (who is shamed), or [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] (who are punished), [[alonso|Alonso]] is transformed. Naples, in the person of its king, shows that political guilt can be acknowledged and forgiven — that the past does not have to determine the future.

The geographical irony of Naples as the site of restoration is that it was the instrument of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s dispossession. The play does not punish Naples for this; instead, it transforms Naples into the site of the marriage that heals the wound Naples helped inflict. This is the logic of forgiveness at the political level: not to forget the wrong but to reconfigure the relationship so that the wrong ceases to define it.

## Key References

> This King of Naples, being an enemy
> To me inveterate, hearkens my brother's suit;
> … in lieu o' the premises
> Of homage and I know not how much tribute …
> — (003-121–124)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s account of the Naples-[[milan|Milan]] transaction. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s enmity is characterised as "inveterate" — long-standing, habitual — but also as secondary: he "hearkens" to [[antonio|Antonio]]'s suit rather than initiating it. The vagueness of "I know not how much tribute" suggests [[prospero|Prospero]]'s characteristic detachment from the details of the political transaction that cost him his dukedom.

> Alonso: Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat
> Thou pardon me my wrongs.
> — (014-110–111)

The political climax of [[alonso|Alonso]]'s arc: the voluntary surrender of [[milan|Milan]] and the request for forgiveness. "Entreat" is a strong word — to beg, to implore — and it marks the distance [[alonso|Alonso]] has travelled from the "inveterate" enemy who "hearkened" to [[antonio|Antonio]]'s suit.

> Prospero: … in the morn
> I'll bring you to your ship, and so to Naples,
> Where I have hope to see the nuptial
> Of these our dear-beloved solemnized …
> — (014-287–290)

The destination. Naples is the site of the wedding — and, implicitly, of the dynastic future that the marriage will produce. [[prospero|Prospero]] will "retire" to [[milan|Milan]]; [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] will remain in Naples. The di[[dream|vision]] of destinations — father to [[milan|Milan]], daughter to Naples — is the spatial expression of the generational transition.

## Critical Notes

Naples has received less critical attention than [[milan|Milan]] or the island, but its role in the play's political resolution is structurally essential. The Naples-[[milan|Milan]] axis maps onto the political geography of Renaissance Italy with a precision unusual in Shakespeare's Italian plays. Frank Kermode, in the Arden Shakespeare edition of *The Tempest* (1954), notes that the tributary relationship between [[milan|Milan]] and Naples was a historical possibility in the period — [[court-of-naples|the Kingdom of Naples]] was, until 1504, an independent Aragonese kingdom with its own sphere of influence in Italian politics, and its absorption into the Spanish crown created precisely the kind of dynastic instability the play dramatises. Kermode observes that the play's resolution — marriage as the instrument of political [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] — reflects early modern diplomatic practice, in which dynastic unions regularly sealed treaties between rival states.

Stephen Greenblatt, in *Renaissance Self-Fashioning* (1980), provides a broader framework for understanding the Naples-[[milan|Milan]] power relationship. Greenblatt argues that Renaissance political [[power|authority]] was constituted through performances of power — submission, tribute, marriage — rather than through abstract [[authority|sovereignty]] alone. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s voluntary resignation of [[milan|Milan]] (014-110) exemplifies what Greenblatt calls the "improvisation of power": the [[authority|rule]]r who can theatrically surrender what he has taken demonstrates his [[power|authority]] more effectively than the one who merely holds territory. The Naples-[[milan|Milan]] axis thus enacts a larger Renaissance pattern in which political identity is negotiated through symbolic gesture rather than military force.

The dynastic resolution has been read critically by feminist scholars who note that [[miranda|Miranda]] is given no voice in the arrangements that determine her future. She will go to Naples as [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s wife, not return to [[milan|Milan]] as [[miranda|Prospero's daughter]] — a transition from one form of patriarchal [[power|authority]] to another that the play presents as a happy ending. The marriage that heals the political wound is also the marriage that transfers [[miranda|Miranda]] from father to husband without her consent being sought or given in the public resolutions of Act 5.

Naples's association with dynastic marriage — through [[claribel|Claribel]] (married to [[tunis|Tunis]]) and [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] (betrothed to [[miranda|Miranda]]) — makes it the play's site of female [[fortune|destiny]]. [[claribel|Claribel]] is sent to [[afric|Africa]]; [[miranda|Miranda]] will be sent to Naples. The women of the play are moved across the political map by men making treaties, and Naples is the destination to which the men will sail while [[prospero|Prospero]] goes home alone to die.
