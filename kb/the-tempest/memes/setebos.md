## Overview

Setebos is the god of [[sycorax|Sycorax]], mentioned twice in *The Tempest* — both times by [[caliban|Caliban]]. The name derives from early modern accounts of Patagonian peoples, most likely from [[antonio|Antonio]] Pigafetta's narrative of Magellan's voyage or Robert Eden's English translation, where "Setebos" appears as a devil-god worshipped by the indigenous inhabitants of the Strait of Magellan. Shakespeare's appropriation of the name for [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s deity grafts New World ethnography onto the play's Mediterranean-imagined [[island|island]].

## In the Play

Setebos is first invoked in Act 1, Scene 2, during [[caliban|Caliban]]'s defiant ex[[transformation|change]] with [[prospero|Prospero]]. After [[prospero|Prospero]] threatens him with torments, [[caliban|Caliban]] retorts with bitter sarcasm: "His [[magic|art]] is of such power, / It would control my dam's god, Setebos, / And make a vassal of him" (003-505). The line performs several operations at once: it establishes Setebos as [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s deity, it mocks [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magical pretensions, and it asserts [[caliban|Caliban]]'s maternal lineage — [[sycorax|Sycorax]], Setebos, and himself — against [[prospero|Prospero]]'s imposed [[power|authority]].

The second mention comes near the play's end, when [[caliban|Caliban]] encounters [[prospero|Prospero]] dressed in his [[milan|Milan]]ese ducal robes: "O Setebos, these be brave spirits indeed!" (014-332). Here Setebos is invoked as an exclamation — a reflexive appeal to the only divine power [[caliban|Caliban]] knows. The moment is comic ([[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] are also present, drunk and gawking at the "brave spirits") but also poignant: [[caliban|Caliban]]'s god, the god of his mother, is no match for [[prospero|Prospero]]'s glittering spectacle of European power.

Scholars have debated whether Setebos represents a genuine theological presence in the play or merely a marker of alterity — a "savage god" invoked to underscore [[caliban|Caliban]]'s otherness. The name itself travelled from Patagonia to London via Italian and English travel accounts, a material trace of the colonial encounters that shadow the play's [[island|island]] fictions.
