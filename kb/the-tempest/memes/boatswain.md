## Overview

The Boatswain appears in only two scenes — the opening storm and the final reunion — but his opening ex[[transformation|change]] with the royal passengers is one of the play's most memorable passages. When [[alonso|Alonso]], [[sebastian|Sebastian]], and [[antonio|Antonio]] demand information during [[the-shipwreck|the tempest]], the Boatswain's retort cuts through hierarchy: "You mar our labour; keep your cabins: you do assist [[the-shipwreck|the storm]]" (002-011–012). When [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] reminds him to be patient, the name he invokes is the king's — and the Boatswain's reply is elemental: "What cares these roarers for the name of king?" (002-015–016). In a single line, the Boatswain announces the play's central theme: [[nature|nature]] does not respect social rank, and all human [[power|authority]] is contingent.

## Character and Role

The Boatswain is defined entirely by his competence. In the opening scene, he is the only character who knows what to do — [[authority|command]] [[mariners|the mariners]], adjust the sails, try to save the ship. The nobles, by contrast, are helpless; their [[power|authority]] is irrelevant to the work of survival. [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s famous observation — "I have great comfort from this fellow: methinks he hath no drowning mark upon him" (002-026–027) — acknowledges the Boatswain's practical value while missing the point: the Boatswain's "mark" is not immunity to drowning but competence in crisis.

His reappearance in Act 5 is brief but significant. Led in by [[ariel|Ariel]], "amazedly following" (014-280), he reports that the ship is "tight and yare and bravely rigged" (014-282) — [[the-shipwreck|the storm]] that seemed fatal was an illusion, and [[the-ship|the vessel]] was never truly in danger. The Boatswain's report is the factual ground note beneath the play's supernatural climax: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic was spectacle, not destruction.

## Key Relationships

- **[[alonso|Alonso]], [[sebastian|Sebastian]], [[antonio|Antonio]]**: The Boatswain defies all three in the opening scene, addressing them with the same rough [[power|authority]] he uses with his crew. "To cabin: silence! trouble us not" (002-014) — the imperatives that should flow from king to subject are reversed.

- **[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]**: The only noble who treats the Boatswain with respect rather than indignation. [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s "I have great comfort from this fellow" (002-026) and his joke about the Boatswain's hanging-[[fortune|destiny]] (002-028–032) are rare moments of humour in the terrifying storm.

- **The [[master|Master]]**: The Boatswain's superior, who appears only briefly in the opening scene. The [[master|Master]] defers to the Boatswain's judgement: the hierarchy of the ship is one of expertise, not birth.

## Key Passages

> What cares these roarers for the name of king? To cabin! silence! trouble us not.
> — (002-015–016)

The Boatswain's most famous line. "These roarers" are the waves, and the question is rhetorical — [[the-shipwreck|the storm]] does not recognise [[authority|sovereignty]]. The line encapsulates the leveling power of [[nature|nature]] that is the play's st[[magic|art]]ing condition.


> *Alon.* Good boatswain, have care. Where's the master? Play the men.
>
> *Boats.* I pray now, keep below.
>
> *Ant.* Where is the master, boatswain?
>
> *Boats.* Do you not hear him? You mar our labour: keep your cabins: you do assist the storm.
>
> *Gon.* Nay, good, be patient.
>
> *Boats.* When the sea is. Hence! What cares these roarers for the name of king? To cabin: silence! trouble us not.
>
> (002-008)
