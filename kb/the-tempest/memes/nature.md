## Overview

Nature in *The Tempest* is a contested concept, deployed in arguments about character, governance, and cosmic order. When [[prospero|Prospero]] says [[antonio|Antonio]]'s betrayal "awaked an evil nature" (003-127), he suggests nature is latent disposition—something that can be dormant or stirred. [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s utopian speech imagines a commonwealth where "all things in common nature should produce" (005-152) and "nature should bring forth / Of its own kind, all foison, all abundance" (005-155–156), invoking a Golden Age where nature requires no cultivation. Against this stands [[caliban|Caliban]], "a devil, a born devil, on whose nature / Nurture can never stick" (012-233)—the claim that some natures resist civilising. The play interrogates whether nature is fixed or malleable, and whether "natural" [[power|authority]] exists at all.

## In the Play

The nature debate crystallises around [[caliban|Caliban]]. [[prospero|Prospero]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] describe him as irredeemably base by nature: "a born devil" whose "vile race … had that in't which good natures / Could not abide to be with" (012-233–234, 003-487). But the play also supplies [[caliban|Caliban]] with some of its most lyrical verse—"[[island|the isle]] is full of noises, / Sounds and sweet airs, that give delight and hurt not" (009-098)—complicating the verdict of a fixed, debased nature.

[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s commonwealth speech (Act 2, Scene 1) offers the philosophical counterpoint: nature as abundance and equality, recalling Montaigne's essay "Of Cannibals." His [[dream|vision]] of a society without "name of magistrate," "riches, poverty, / And use of [[servitude|service]]" imagines nature as the solvent of hierarchy.

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic intervenes in nature—he claims to have "bedimm'd / The noontide sun, call'd forth the mutinous winds" (014-052–053)—but his [[magic|art]] is consistently framed as working *through* nature rather than against it. The spirits he [[authority|command]]s—"ye elves of hills, brooks, standing lakes, and groves" (014-044)—are nature spirits, and his renunciation of magic is also a return to the natural order of [[milan|Milan]].

[[miranda|Miranda]]'s famous exclamation—"O brave new world, / That has such people in't!" (014-231)—treats human variety as natural wonder. Her naivety is also a moral position: she sees nature in others as inherently good until proven otherwise.

## Key Passages

> All things in common nature should produce
> Without sweat or endeavour: treason, felony,
> Sword, pike, knife, gun, or need of any engine,
> Would I not have; but nature should bring forth
> Of its own kind, all foison, all abundance,
> To feed my innocent people.
> — (005-152–157)

[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s utopia. Nature uncoerced produces abundance and peace; human institutions (weapons, laws, labour) are the source of scarcity and violence.

> A devil, a born devil, on whose nature
> Nurture can never stick; on whom my pains,
> Humanely taken, all, all lost, quite lost.
> — (012-233–234)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s verdict on [[caliban|Caliban]]. The "nature vs. nurture" antithesis—Shakespeare's original formulation—is stated here as a dilemma: if nature is fixed, then nurture ([[knowledge|education]], kindness, civilisation) is futile.

> Ye elves of hills, brooks, standing lakes, and groves …
> by whose aid—
> Weak masters though ye be—I have bedimm'd
> The noontide sun, call'd forth the mutinous winds.
> — (014-044–053)

The catalogue of nature spirits reveals that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic is not anti-nature but hyper-nature: it intensifies and redirects natural forces rather than replacing them.
