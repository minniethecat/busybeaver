## Overview

John Fletcher (1579–1625) was a Jacobean playwright who, with his collaborator Francis Beaumont, produced some of the most successful comedies and tragicomedies of the period. He succeeded Shakespeare as the principal playwright for the King's Men around 1613 and is believed by some scholars to have collaborated with Shakespeare on *Henry VIII*, *The Two Noble Kinsmen*, and possibly the lost play *Cardenio*. In *The Tempest* critical apparatus, Fletcher appears not as an influence on the play but as a parallel source: the editors cite Beaumont and Fletcher's *The Noble Gentleman* (031-001) to gloss a financial metaphor in Act 3 (010-073).

## Role in the Play

Fletcher's connection to *The Tempest* is purely editorial. In Note XV, the annotator cites a passage from Beaumont and Fletcher's *The Noble Gentleman*, I. 1: "The return will give you five for one" — used to gloss the phrase "each putter out of five for one" in Act 3, Scene 3 (031-001). The citation illustrates the practice of venture investment in early modern London, where travellers might deposit money with a broker before a voyage and collect a multiple (typically five to one) upon safe return. The parallel suggests a shared vocabulary of mercantile risk between the two plays, but does not imply direct influence.

## Source Passage

> See Beaumont and Fletcher, *The Noble Gentleman*, I. 1. (Vol. II. p. 261, ed. Moxon): "The return will give you five for one."
>
> (031-001)

The editorial note connects Fletcher to *The Tempest* through a shared mercantile metaphor. The phrase "five for one" — the venture-capital rate for travellers depositing money before a voyage (010-073) — appears both in Shakespeare's play and in Beaumont and Fletcher's comedy, suggesting a common vocabulary of speculative risk in early modern London theatre.

## Key Scenes

Fletcher does not appear in the play. His sole connection is through the editorial apparatus of Note XV, which is a scholarly annotation rather than a dramatic scene.
