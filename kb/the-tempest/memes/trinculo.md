## Overview

Trinculo is Alonso's jester—a professional fool who finds himself [[storm|shipwreck]]ed, alone, and terrified on [[prospero|Prospero]]'s island. He is one of the play's principal comic figures, alongside [[stephano|Stephano]], [[stephano|the drunken butler]]. His first appearance in Act 2, Scene 2—stumbling upon the prone [[caliban|Caliban]] during a thunderstorm—produces one of Shakespeare's most absurd physical tableaux: Trinculo crawling under [[caliban|Caliban]]'s gaberdine for shelter (006-021), mistaken by the approaching [[stephano|Stephano]] for a four-legged monster with two mouths.

Trinculo's cowardice is matched by his wit. He comments on the action with a jester's cynical detachment, puncturing pretension and noting absurdity. Yet his p[[magic|art]]icipation in [[caliban|Caliban]] and [[stephano|Stephano]]'s drunken plot against [[prospero|Prospero]] shows the limits of comic detachment: Trinculo is not merely a commentator but an actor, and his greed for status—"viceroy"—implicates him in the play's politics of ambition.

## Role in the Play

Trinculo enters in Act 2, Scene 2, alone on the island during a storm. Spotting [[caliban|Caliban]], he mistakes him first for a fish—"What have we here? a man or a fish?"—and then, upon hearing thunder, pragmatically crawls beneath [[caliban|Caliban]]'s gaberdine for shelter. When [[stephano|Stephano]] arrives singing drunkenly, he discovers the "monster" and Trinculo's voice emerging from beneath it, creating a farcical scene of mistaken identity.

In Act 3, Scene 2, Trinculo becomes the third member of [[caliban|Caliban]]'s conspiracy against [[prospero|Prospero]]. [[caliban|Caliban]] offers [[stephano|Stephano]] [[authority|sovereignty]] over the island in ex[[transformation|change]] for killing [[prospero|Prospero]], and Trinculo is promised the role of viceroy. Trinculo's contribution to the plot is largely insolent commentary—he repeatedly undercuts [[caliban|Caliban]]'s speeches with sarcastic interjections—but he is complicit nonetheless. His greed over the "glistering apparel" in Act 4 dooms the rebellion to farcical failure.

In Act 5, Trinculo appears among the captured conspirators, bedraggled and humiliated, still reeling from the butler's wine. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s recognition—"Trinculo is reeling ripe" (014-356)—restores him to his proper station: a fool, not a viceroy.

## Key Relationships

- **[[stephano|Stephano]]**: Trinculo's p[[magic|art]]ner in folly. [[stephano|Stephano]] is the would-be king, Trinculo the would-be viceroy. Their dynamic is defined by petty squabbles, drunken camaraderie, and a shared gift for mistaking [[caliban|Caliban]]'s desperation for loyalty.

> "Trinculo, keep a good tongue in your head: if you prove a mutineer,—the next tree! The poor monster's my subject, and he shall not suffer indignity." — Stephano, (009-016)

- **[[caliban|Caliban]]**: Trinculo's first reaction to [[caliban|Caliban]] is revulsion and curiosity; his second is to exploit him. He shares in [[stephano|Stephano]]'s "conquest" of [[caliban|Caliban]] but lacks even [[stephano|Stephano]]'s dim affection for the "poor monster."

> "I shall laugh myself to death at this puppy-headed monster. A most scurvy monster! I could find in my heart to beat him—" — Trinculo on Caliban, (009-026)

- **[[prospero|Prospero]]**: Trinculo never meets [[prospero|Prospero]] directly, but is his unwitting pawn. The "glistering apparel" that tempts Trinculo and [[stephano|Stephano]] away from murder is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s trap, and Trinculo stumbles through it exactly as designed.

- **[[alonso|Alonso]]**: Trinculo is Alonso's jester, though their relationship is never dramatised on stage. The fact that Trinculo is in the king's [[servitude|service]] adds irony to his p[[magic|art]]icipation in a plot against courtly order.

## Key Speeches

Trinculo delivers no soliloquies, but his comic monologue upon discovering [[caliban|Caliban]] is a set-piece of physical comedy:

> "What have we here? a man or a fish? dead or alive? A fish: he smells like a fish; a very ancient and fish-like smell... A strange fish! Were I in England now, as once I was, and had but this fish painted, not a holiday fool there but would give a piece of silver." — (006-016–048)

This passage reveals Trinculo's mentality: even amid [[storm|shipwreck]] terror, he is mentally calculating profit. The reference to England—a rare geographic specificity in the play—also hints that Trinculo, like [[stephano|Stephano]], may have an independent history beyond the court of [[naples|Naples]].
