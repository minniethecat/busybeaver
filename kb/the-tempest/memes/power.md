## Overview

Power is the central political theme of *The Tempest*, manifesting in three interlocking forms: legitimate political authority, usurped [[authority|rule]], and supernatural [[authority|command]]. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s identity as "[[prospero|the prime duke]]" (003-102) and "a prince of power" (003-077) anchors one pole; [[antonio|Antonio]]'s betrayal, which "awaked an evil nature" (003-127), represents the corruption of power; and [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magical [[magic|art]] — "of such power" that even [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] "must obey" (003-504) — introduces a supernatural dimension that complicates any simple political reading. The play ultimately asks whether power can be exercised justly, what happens to those who are subjected to it, and what must be surrendered for [[music|harmony]] to be restored.

The island setting intensifies the question of power by stripping away the institutional structures that normally mediate it. There are no courts, no armies, no laws on the island — only the competing claims of [[prospero|the magician]], the native, [[ariel|the spirit]], and the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed nobles. Power on the island is naked force and persuasion, magic and rhetoric, and the play's exploration of how these forms of power interact, conflict, and resolve is its deepest political meditation.

## In the Play

Power is established in the backstory of Act 1, Scene 2, where [[prospero|Prospero]] recounts to [[miranda|Miranda]] how he lost [[milan|Milan]]. As duke, he was "[[prospero|the prime duke]], being so reputed / In dignity" (003-102–103), but his absorption in "[[art|secret studies]]" (003-107) created a vacuum that his brother [[antonio|Antonio]] filled. [[antonio|Antonio]] "being once perfected how to grant suits, / How to deny them, whom to advance" (003-111–112) mastered the machinery of governance that [[prospero|Prospero]] had neglected. The irony is sharp: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s power as a scholar cost him his power as a [[authority|rule]]r.

The island becomes a laboratory for competing claims to power. [[prospero|Prospero]] [[authority|rule]]s through magic — a power that compels obedience from [[ariel|Ariel]] (freed from [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s tree) and [[caliban|Caliban]] (who insists "this island's mine, by [[sycorax|Sycorax]] my mother" (009-029)). The [[storm|shipwreck]]ed court replays the [[milan|Milan]] coup in miniature: [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] plot to kill [[alonso|Alonso]] and seize [[naples|Naples]] (Act 2, Scene 1), while [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] imagine themselves kings of the island with [[caliban|Caliban]] as their subject (Act 2, Scene 2). Each mini-plot repeats the same pattern: a subordinate sees an opportunity to seize power through violence, and the island's lack of institutional constraints makes every ambition thinkable.

[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s willing submission is the counterpoint to all these coercive power relations. He accepts servitude "with a he[[magic|art]] as willing / As [[servitude|bondage]] e'er of freedom" (008-124–125), transforming a [[authority|command]] relationship into a voluntary one. This models the play's resolution: [[prospero|Prospero]] reclaims his dukedom not by force but by renouncing the magical power that set him ap[[magic|art]], breaking his staff and drowning his book (014-065–068). The restoration of political power requires the surrender of supernatural power.

The play's treatment of power is complicated by the gender dynamics of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s control over [[miranda|Miranda]]. His power as father — putting her to [[sleep|sleep]] at will, arranging her marriage, displaying her as a reward for [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s labour — is naturalised in a way that his magical and political power are not. The play never interrogates this dimension of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s authority, even as it subjects every other form of power to rigorous examination.

## Key Passages

> Twelve year since, Miranda, twelve year since,
> Thy father was the Duke of Milan, and
> A prince of power.
> — (003-075–077)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s self-definition. The repetition of "twelve year since" emphasises the duration of his dispossession. "Prince of power" is deliberately ambiguous — it can mean a powerful prince, or a prince whose [[authority|legitimacy]] derives from power itself. The phrase encapsulates the play's central question: is power the source of authority, or the other way around?

> I must obey: his art is of such power,
> It would control my dam's god, Setebos,
> And make a vassal of him.
> — (003-504–505)

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s aside captures the hierarchy of power on the island. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] surpasses even the god [[caliban|Caliban]] inherited from [[sycorax|Sycorax]] — magical power that colonises [[ariel|the spirit]]ual domain of the colonised. The word "vassal" registers the political dimension: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic doesn't just overpower [[setebos|Setebos]]; it subordinates him within a feudal relationship of lord and servant.

> Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat
> Thou pardon me my wrongs.
> — (014-110–111)

[[alonso|Alonso]]'s surrender of [[milan|Milan]] — the political power he acquired through [[antonio|Antonio]]'s coup — is the condition of his own restoration. Power, in this transaction, is not taken but returned, and the return is voluntary. The play imagines justice not as punishment but as the voluntary unwinding of unjust power relations.

> But this rough magic
> I here abjure … I'll break my staff …
> I'll drown my book.
> — (014-061–068)

The renunciation of magical power is the precondition for the restoration of political power. [[prospero|Prospero]] cannot re-enter [[milan|Milan]] as duke while wielding the [[magic|art]] that made him a god on the island. The speech is an acknowledgement that some forms of power are incompatible with the obligations of ordinary political life.

## Critical Context

The political philosophy of *The Tempest* has been read through multiple critical lenses. Traditional humanist criticism, following Frank Kermode's Arden edition (1954), treats [[prospero|Prospero]]'s renunciation of magic as the triumph of Christian [[forgiveness|forgiveness]] over pagan vengeance — a reading that aligns the play with the romance genre's movement from disorder to [[music|harmony]]. In this framework, power is a temptation that must be overcome, and [[prospero|Prospero]]'s greatness lies in his willingness to surrender it.

Marxist and materialist critics have challenged this reading by drawing attention to the economic and social structures of power that the play leaves intact. [[prospero|Prospero]] renounces magic but reclaims his dukedom; [[caliban|Caliban]] is acknowledged ("this thing of darkness I / Acknowledge mine") but not freed or compensated; the marriage of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] restores the political order of [[milan|Milan]]-[[naples|Naples]] without altering its hierarchical structure. The play's resolution, in this reading, is a restoration of the *status quo ante* disguised as transformation.

Postcolonial criticism, notably Stephen Greenblatt's influential reading of *The Tempest* as a document of early modern colonial encounter, has made the play's treatment of power inseparable from its representation of colonialism. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s claim to the island by inheritance — a claim of indigenous [[authority|sovereignty]] — is never answered, only overmastered; [[ariel|Ariel]]'s promised freedom is granted on [[prospero|Prospero]]'s terms and timeline. The play's exploration of power, in this reading, is not universal but historically specific: it reflects and interrogates the power dynamics of early modern European expansion, even as it ultimately endorses [[prospero|Prospero]]'s right to [[authority|rule]].

Feminist criticism has drawn attention to the gendered dimensions of power that the play's political framework occludes. [[miranda|Miranda]] is the object of every power transaction — given by [[prospero|Prospero]] to [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]], displayed as a reward, silenced in the political resolutions of Act 5 — and [[sycorax|Sycorax]], the only other woman mentioned in the play, exists only as a dead absence whose power ([[magic|sorcery]], maternity, resistance to male authority) is invoked only to be condemned. The play's [[dream|vision]] of restored political order is also a [[dream|vision]] of restored patriarchal order.
