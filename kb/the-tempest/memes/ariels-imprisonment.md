## Overview

[[ariel|Ariel]]'s imprisonment is a key backstory event recounted in Act 1, Scene 2. The spirit [[ariel|Ariel]] explains to [[prospero|Prospero]] that twelve years earlier, [[sycorax|the witch]] [[sycorax|Sycorax]] imprisoned him inside a "cloven pine" — a split pine tree — as punishment for refusing to carry out her "e[[magic|art]]hy and abhorr'd [[authority|command]]s" (003-373). [[ariel|Ariel]] remained trapped for twelve years, "howling away twelve winters" (003-399), until [[prospero|Prospero]] arrived on the island and freed him using his magical [[magic|art]]. In return, [[ariel|Ariel]] agreed to serve [[prospero|Prospero]] for a set period.

## Dramatic Function

The imprisonment serves multiple dramatic purposes. It establishes [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]] over [[ariel|Ariel]] through gratitude rather than conquest. It introduces [[sycorax|Sycorax]] as the island's previous [[authority|rule]]r, setting up the colonial frame of the play. And it mirrors the other confinements in the play — [[prospero|Prospero]]'s exile, [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s servitude, [[mariners|the mariners]]' [[sleep|sleep]] below deck — creating a thematic web around freedom and [[servitude|bondage]].

## The Account in Act 1 Scene 2

The story of [[ariel|Ariel]]'s imprisonment unfolds across a tense ex[[transformation|change]] between [[prospero|Prospero]] and [[ariel|Ariel]] (003-332–003-406). When [[ariel|Ariel]] reminds [[prospero|Prospero]] of his promised [[freedom|liberty]], [[prospero|Prospero]]'s response is abrupt and threatening: "Before the time be out? no more!" (003-333). He then forces [[ariel|Ariel]] to recount the trauma of the cloven pine — a rhetorical strategy that reasserts [[prospero|Prospero]]'s dominance by making [[ariel|the spirit]] relive his past agony. [[prospero|Prospero]] describes [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s punishment in graphic detail: the "rift" of the pine, [[ariel|Ariel]]'s groans that "did make wolves howl, and penetrate the breasts / Of ever-angry bears" (003-388–389), and [[sycorax|the witch]]'s inability to undo her own spell: "it was a torment / To lay upon the damn'd, which [[sycorax|Sycorax]] / Could not again undo" (003-390–391). [[prospero|Prospero]] casts himself as liberator — "it was mine [[magic|art]], / When I arrived and heard thee, that made gape / The pine, and let thee out" (003-391–394) — but immediately follows liberation with a new threat: "If thou more murmur'st, I will rend an oak, / And peg thee in his knotty entrails, till / Thou hast howl'd away twelve winters" (003-397–399). The oak threat mirrors the pine imprisonment with uncanny precision, revealing that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s power operates on the same logic as [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s: obedience or torment.

## Aftermath

[[prospero|Prospero]] freed [[ariel|Ariel]] and bound [[ariel|the spirit]] to his own [[servitude|service]]. This relationship drives the entire action of the play, as [[ariel|Ariel]] executes nearly all of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|command]]s. The imprisonment also underpins [[caliban|Caliban]]'s claim to the island — as [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s son, he argues the island is rightfully his.

## Thematic Significance

[[ariel|Ariel]]'s imprisonment establishes the moral economy of [[servitude|service]] and freedom that structures the entire play. The twelve-year duration parallels [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own twelve-year exile (003-075), creating a symmetry between [[prospero|the magician]] and his spirit: both were displaced by a more powerful figure, both owe their liberation to external intervention. Yet the parallel is asymmetrical — [[prospero|Prospero]] chose to free [[ariel|Ariel]]; [[miranda|Miranda]], [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]], and Providence freed [[prospero|Prospero]].

The cloven pine itself carries symbolic weight. A split tree — living wood riven open — figures the di[[dream|vision]] between freedom and [[servitude|bondage]], spirit and matter, air and e[[magic|art]]h. [[ariel|Ariel]], a creature "too delicate / To act her e[[magic|art]]hy and abhorr'd [[authority|command]]s" (003-372–373), is trapped inside the most e[[magic|art]]hbound of substances: the trunk of a pine. The imprisonment literalises the conflict between [[ariel|Ariel]]'s aerial [[nature|nature]] and [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s "e[[magic|art]]hy" demands.

The account also exposes the play's logic of intergenerational [[guilt|guilt]]. [[caliban|Caliban]] inherits [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s claim to the island — "This island's mine, by [[sycorax|Sycorax]] my mother" (003-455) — while [[prospero|Prospero]] inherits [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s [[power|dominion]] over [[ariel|Ariel]] through the act of liberation. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s power, the play quietly concedes, is built on foundations laid by [[sycorax|the witch]] he condemns.
