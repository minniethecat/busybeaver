## Overview

Miranda is the play's moral centre — a figure of natural goodness raised in enforced isolation. Having known only her father, [[caliban|Caliban]], and the [[island|island]]'s spirits, she encounters the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed court with a freshness of perception that the play treats as both naive and ethically potent. "O brave new world, / That has such people in't!" (014-231) captures her defining quality: an instinctive inclination to see the best in others. Yet Miranda is not merely passive innocence. She proposes to [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] — "I am your wife, if you will marry me" (008-068) — with a directness that defies Jacobean convention, and her compassion for the suffering sailors opens the play: "O, I have suffered / With those that I saw suffer" (003-006–007). Miranda is the character through whom the play's regenerative impulses flow: her love for [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] heals the dynastic wound between [[milan|Milan]] and [[naples|Naples]], her compassion models the emotional response the play asks of its audience, and her wonder at the "brave new world" crystallises the romance genre's investment in renewal and second chances.

Her name — from the Latin *mirandus*, "worthy of admiration" — signals her dramatic function: she is the object of the audience's admiring gaze, but also the subject whose own gaze transforms what it beholds. The court p[[magic|art]]y, seen through her eyes, becomes "beauteous mankind" despite including [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]. Miranda's innocence is thus not ignorance but a mode of perception that the play invites us to share even as it ironises.

## Role in the Play

Miranda appears in the play's first scene and its last, framing the dramatic action with her presence. In (003-001–003-230), she listens to [[prospero|Prospero]]'s long narrative and serves as the audience's surrogate — [[knowledge|learning]] the backstory as we do. Her compassion for the [[storm|shipwreck]] victims ("O, the cry did knock / Against my very he[[magic|art]]!" (003-010–011)) establishes her as the play's emotional compass. The verb "knock" is significant: Miranda's empathy is physical, involuntary, a blow she feels in her body rather than a sentiment she chooses. This somatic quality of her compassion distinguishes her from [[prospero|Prospero]], whose emotions are always mediated by calculation and [[magic|art]].

In Act 3, Scene 1, she meets [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]. [[prospero|Prospero]] has arranged their encounter, but their love develops with a speed and sincerity that exceed his scripting. Miranda's proposal — "I am your wife, if you will marry me" (008-068) — reverses the expected gender roles and asserts a moral autonomy that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s control over her has not extinguished. The conditional "if you will marry me" is crucial: Miranda does not [[authority|command]] but offers, preserving [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s agency even as she claims her own. In a play structured around coercion and obedience, this moment of mutual consent is the ethical counterweight to the hierarchies of [[servitude|mastery]] that govern the rest of the [[island|island]].

Act 4 presents Miranda as the audience for [[the-masque|the betrothal masque]]. Her silence during [[the-masque|the pageant]] is notable — she observes, receives, and wonders, but does not speak. In Act 5, her exclamation at the sight of the court — "How beauteous mankind is! O brave new world, / That has such people in't!" (014-230–231) — is the play's most-quoted expression of wonder, its irony (she is praising [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] among others) acknowledged by [[prospero|Prospero]]'s dry reply: "'Tis new to thee" (014-232). The ex[[transformation|change]] encapsulates the play's treatment of Miranda's innocence as both gift and limitation: she sees truly that the world is brave and new, but she does not yet see fully what the world contains.

## Key Speeches

> O, I have suffered
> With those that I saw suffer: a brave vessel,
> Who had, no doubt, some noble creature in her,
> Dash'd all to pieces. O, the cry did knock
> Against my very heart! Poor souls, they perish'd.
> Had I been any god of power, I would
> Have sunk the sea within the earth, or ere
> It should the good ship so have swallow'd and
> The fraughting souls within her.
> — (003-006–014)

Miranda's first speech establishes her defining trait: compassion so intense it becomes physical. "The cry did knock / Against my very he[[magic|art]]" makes empathy a blow, an involuntary convulsion of the body. The conditional — "Had I been any god of power" — contrasts her instinctive [[forgiveness|mercy]] with [[prospero|Prospero]]'s calculated use of power: where he raises storms to test and punish, she would sink the sea itself to save. The speech also introduces Miranda's characteristic rhetorical pattern of qualification and intensification: "no doubt," "poor souls," "or ere" — the syntax of someone feeling her way toward an emotion that exceeds available language.

> I am your wife, if you will marry me;
> If not, I'll die your maid: to be your fellow
> You may deny me; but I'll be your servant,
> Whether you will or no.
> — (008-068–071)

The most radical speech in the play, measured by Jacobean gender norms. Miranda proposes marriage — a reversal of every social script available to a Renaissance woman. The chiastic structure ("wife...maid...fellow...servant") maps the possible configurations of their relationship and claims all of them as her choice. "Whether you will or no" is the key phrase: Miranda asserts a will that is not contingent on [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s response. She will love him regardless of his answer, and this independence within devotion is what distinguishes her from the play's other servants and subjects. [[prospero|Prospero]], watching unseen, has staged this encounter — but Miranda's words exceed his script.

> How beauteous mankind is! O brave new world,
> That has such people in't!
> — (014-230–231)

The play's most famous lines, and its most ironic. Miranda beholds the court p[[magic|art]]y — [[alonso|Alonso]] (repentant), [[sebastian|Sebastian]] (shamed), [[antonio|Antonio]] (silent and unforgiven) — and sees only beauty. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s reply — "'Tis new to thee" (014-232) — is tender, rueful, and faintly patronising: the father who has kept his daughter ignorant of the world now notes the consequence of that ignorance. But the play does not simply undercut Miranda's wonder; it asks us to share it. The "brave new world" is brave and new precisely because Miranda sees it so, and her [[dream|vision]] — however naïve — is the condition of the forgiveness and renewal that close the play.

## Critical Reception

Frank Kermode, in his Arden edition introduction (1954), identifies Miranda as the embodiment of the regenerative principle essential to Shakespearean romance. For Kermode, Miranda's innocence is not merely a character trait but a structural requirement of the genre: romance depends on a figure of uncorrupted goodness through whom the broken world can be renewed. Her marriage to [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] is the mechanism by which the play's political wounds are healed, and her wonder at the "brave new world" is the emotional register in which the audience is invited to receive the play's resolution. Kermode reads [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "'Tis new to thee" as Shakespeare's acknowledgment of the gap between Miranda's [[dream|vision]] and the audience's knowledge — but insists that the play asks us to close that gap, to see with Miranda's eyes even as we know what she does not.

Stephen Greenblatt, in *Shakespearean Negotiations* (1988), offers a more skeptical reading of Miranda's role in the play's political economy. For Greenblatt, Miranda is not merely an innocent but a piece in [[prospero|Prospero]]'s dynastic strategy — the daughter whose marriage to [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] transforms the political relationship between [[milan|Milan]] and [[naples|Naples]] from subjugation to alliance. Her celebrated "brave new world" speech, in this reading, is deeply ironised: the "beauteous mankind" she beholds includes the man who usurped her father and the man who tried to murder his own brother. Greenblatt notes that Miranda's wonder, however genuine, serves [[prospero|Prospero]]'s purposes: it is the emotional counterp[[magic|art]] to the political theatre he has staged. The feminist critique extends this argument: Miranda is given no voice in the arrangements that determine her future. She will go to [[naples|Naples]] as [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s wife — a transition from one form of patriarchal [[power|authority]] to another that the play presents as a happy ending. Her proposal to [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] in Act 3 is the one moment where she speaks and acts independently, and it is significant that this moment of agency occurs under [[prospero|Prospero]]'s unseen surveillance.

Lorie Jerrell Leininger, in a pioneering feminist reading ("The Miranda Trap," 1980), argues that Miranda is caught in a patriarchal structure that the play both exposes and naturalises. Her [[knowledge|education]] by [[prospero|Prospero]] — "Have I, thy schoolmaster, made thee more profit / Than other princesses can" (003-230–231) — is a curriculum designed to produce an ideal wife for the political marriage [[prospero|Prospero]] has already arranged. Leininger reads Miranda's "brave new world" as genuinely moving but also as the culmination of a programme of controlled innocence: Miranda sees only beauty because she has been trained to see only what [[prospero|Prospero]] permits. The play's greatness, in this reading, lies p[[magic|art]]ly in its capacity to sustain both Miranda's wonder and its critique simultaneously.
