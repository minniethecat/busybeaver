## Overview

Stephano, the drunken butler of [[court-of-naples|Alonso's court]], provides the play's broadest comedy and its most absurd plot: [[caliban|Caliban]]'s plan to make him king of the [[island|island]] in place of [[prospero|Prospero]]. Stephano arrives on the [[island|island]] with a bottle of wine — "that's a brave god, and bears celestial liquor" (006-075), [[caliban|Caliban]] declares — and his drunkenness transforms him from servant to would-be sovereign. The "rebellion" he leads is a parody of the political usurpation that drives the main plot: where [[antonio|Antonio]] seized a dukedom through administrative cunning, Stephano attempts to seize an [[island|island]] through wine, noise, and [[caliban|Caliban]]'s misplaced worship. The comic subplot functions as a distorted mirror that reduces the play's grand themes of power, [[authority|rule]], and [[authority|sovereignty]] to their absurd essentials — a butler with a bottle, a monster on his knees, and a kingdom claimed by a drunkard's boast.

Stephano's trajectory is a miniature farce of the colonial encounter: a European of the lowest social rank washes ashore and is immediately mistaken for a god by the [[island|island]]'s native inhabitant. His "[[authority|rule]]" is sustained entirely by alcohol — the bottle is his sceptre — and his collapse is equally swift when [[prospero|Prospero]]'s spirits intervene. Yet for all its absurdity, Stephano's rebellion echoes the play's deepest anxieties about the fragility of political order and the ease with which [[power|authority]] can be seized by anyone with sufficient arrogance and a willing follower.

## Character and Role

Stephano enters singing a sea shanty: "I shall no more to sea, to sea, / Here shall I die ashore" (006-022–023). He has survived [[the-shipwreck|the wreck]], found his bottle intact, and is cheerfully drunk. His entry is the first note of unalloyed comedy after the terror of the opening [[storm|storm]] and the political tension of the court scenes. Stephano's defining characteristic is a drunken self-regard that inflates his status beyond all proportion: a butler, the lowest-ranking member of the court p[[magic|art]]y, he immediately assumes the manner of a conqueror. His first encounter with [[caliban|Caliban]] establishes the comic hierarchy: [[caliban|Caliban]], tasting the celestial liquor, kneels and worships Stephano as a god — "Hast thou not dropped from heaven?" (006-083) — and offers to serve him. Stephano's response — "He's a present for any emperor that ever trod on neat's-leather" (006-094) — reveals the scale of his ambition: a butler who now imagines himself an emperor.

The drinking that defines Stephano is both his power and his limitation. Wine makes him bold enough to claim a kingdom but too stupefied to plan its seizure. When [[caliban|Caliban]] lays out a detailed strategy for murdering [[prospero|Prospero]] — "thou mayst brain him, / Having first seized his books" (009-092–093) — Stephano can only respond with drunken distraction: "Monster, I will kill this man. His daughter and I will be king and queen — save our graces! — and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] and thyself shall be viceroys" (009-103–106). The comedy lies in the gap between the gravity of the conspiracy and the frivolity of the conspirator. Stephano is playing at tyranny the way a child plays at being a soldier: the language of power is there, but the understanding is absent.

The rebellion plot, narrated by [[caliban|Caliban]] in (009-001–110), is the play's most farcical sequence. [[caliban|Caliban]] urges Stephano to "possess his books" (009-105) — the source of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s power — and kill him while he sleeps. Stephano agrees with drunken gravity: "Monster, I will kill this man" (009-103). The conspiracy is incompetent from the st[[magic|art]]: Stephano is more interested in his bottle than in strategy, and the trio is easily distracted by the "glistering apparel" (012-241) [[prospero|Prospero]] leaves as a trap. The garments — "a frippery," as [[trinculo|Trinculo]] calls them — expose the rebels' true priorities: not freedom or justice, but the trappings of status. Stephano, the butler who would be king, cannot resist the costume of the role he covets.

Stephano's punishment, like [[caliban|Caliban]]'s, is humiliating rather than severe: he is chased by spirits in the shape of dogs, driven through "toothed briers, sharp furzes, pricking goss, and thorns" (012-253–254). The "rebel" who swaggered toward a crown is reduced to a fleeing beast, his pretensions scattered by the very [[island|island]] he sought to [[authority|rule]]. In the final scene (014-317–325), he is led in with [[caliban|Caliban]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]], wet and miserable — "O, touch me not; I am not Stephano, but a cramp" (014-322) — a defeated usurper whose rebellion was always more of a drunken fantasy than a real threat. [[prospero|Prospero]] dismisses him with a single line of contemptuous [[forgiveness|forgiveness]]: "Go, sirrah, to [[prosperos-cell|my cell]]; / Take with you your companions" (014-325–326). The butler returns to servitude, his brief reign forgotten.

Stephano's dramatic function extends beyond comic relief. His rebellion is a structural counterpoint to [[antonio|Antonio]]'s: where [[antonio|Antonio]]'s usurpation was cold, calculated, and successful (for twelve years), Stephano's is hot-headed, drunken, and foiled before it begins. The contrast sharpens the play's argument about true power: [[antonio|Antonio]]'s administrative competence makes his betrayal genuinely dangerous; Stephano's incompetence makes his rebellion merely ridiculous. Shakespeare uses the comic plot to suggest that political evil requires not just ambition but the competence to execute it — and that the incompetent aspirant, however harmless, is still a moral problem, a soul degraded by the fantasy of [[power|dominion]].

## Key Relationships

- **[[caliban|Caliban]]**: The "servant-monster" who worships Stephano as a god and proposes the murder of [[prospero|Prospero]]. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s "worship" of Stephano — "I'll kiss thy foot; I'll swear myself thy subject" (009-037) — is both comic and pathetic: the creature who resents [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|rule]] willingly submits to a drunk butler. The relationship reverses the colonial hierarchy in perverse form: the European servant becomes the native's [[master|master]], not through superior knowledge or force, but through the accidental gift of alcohol. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s "[[freedom|Freedom]], high-day!" — his exultation at having found a new [[master|master]] — is the saddest line in the comic subplot, a cry of liberation that is really an announcement of deeper enslavement. [[caliban|Caliban]] provides the plot's intelligence (he knows [[prospero|Prospero]]'s vulnerabilities, he knows the [[island|island]]'s geography), while Stephano provides only the alcohol that sustains [[caliban|Caliban]]'s misplaced devotion. The p[[magic|art]]nership is a [[knowledge|study]] in asymmetrical dependence: [[caliban|Caliban]] needs Stephano's wine and the illusion of a liberator; Stephano needs [[caliban|Caliban]]'s knowledge and labour. Neither gets what he seeks.

- **[[trinculo|Trinculo]]**: Stephano's companion and fellow castaway. Their reunion in (006-048–095) — [[trinculo|Trinculo]] inside [[caliban|Caliban]]'s gaberdine, creating a four-legged monster — is the play's most explicitly physical comedy. Stephano's discovery of this "most delicate monster" (006-089) and his attempt to understand its anatomy ("Have we not here a sort of tongue in this head?" — referring to [[trinculo|Trinculo]]'s voice from under the gaberdine) creates a scene of pure farce that Shakespeare sustains for nearly fifty lines. Stephano's relationship with [[trinculo|Trinculo]] is one of easy comradeship punctuated by petty squabbles over the bottle and precedence in the would-be new kingdom. Their quarrel over the "viceroyship" in (009-025–035) — each insisting on primacy in the rebellion's spoils — is a miniature of courtly factionalism, reduced to the absurd scale of two drunkards arguing over an unconquered [[island|island]]. Where [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s conspiracy is cloaked in courtly decorum, Stephano and [[trinculo|Trinculo]]'s is conducted at the volume of a tavern brawl.

- **[[prospero|Prospero]]**: Stephano never directly confronts [[prospero|Prospero]] — the rebellion is foiled before it reaches him. This structural separation is significant: the rebel and the [[authority|rule]]r never share a scene of conflict, never ex[[transformation|change]] a word of antagonism. [[prospero|Prospero]] monitors the conspiracy through [[ariel|Ariel]]'s reports (009-080–105) and dispatches it with contemptuous ease: "These three have robbed me, and this demi-devil … / Had plotted with them / To take my life" (014-318–320). The butler's rebellion is a problem solved by spirits and clothes, not by magic. The asymmetry between conspirator and target is absolute: Stephano never understands the forces arrayed against him, never realises he has been watched from the st[[magic|art]], never comprehends that his "kingdom" was a stage managed by the very man he sought to overthrow.

- **[[ariel|Ariel]]**: Though they rarely interact directly, [[ariel|Ariel]] is the invisible architect of Stephano's downfall. It is [[ariel|Ariel]] who leads the trio through "toothed briers" with tabor and pipe (009-060–080), who whispers contradictions to provoke the quarrel between Stephano and [[trinculo|Trinculo]], and who reports the conspiracy's progress to [[prospero|Prospero]]. Stephano's inability to perceive [[ariel|Ariel]] — he hears the music but cannot see the musician — dramatises his limitation: he is a creature of the material world (wine, garments, physical appetite) confronting a world of invisible intelligence. The spirit's manipulation of the butler is almost cruel in its ease; [[ariel|Ariel]] plays Stephano the way a musician plays an instrument.

## Key Speeches

> "I shall no more to sea, to sea; / Here shall I die ashore — / This is a very scurvy tune to sing at a man's funeral. / Well, here's my comfort." — (006-022–025)

Stephano's entry [[music|song]] is the first extended comic passage after the terror of the opening [[storm|storm]]. The sea shanty, a work-[[music|song]] of sailors, is repurposed as a drinking [[music|song]] — the "comfort" of line 25 is the bottle he carries. The speech establishes Stephano's mode: he transforms every situation, even his own supposed funeral, into an occasion for drinking. The dissonance between his imagined death at sea and his actual survival on land is the play's first comic deflation of its own tragic potential.

> "He's a present for any emperor that ever trod on neat's-leather. ... Come on your ways; open your mouth: here is that which will give language to you, cat. Open your mouth: this will shake your shaking, I can tell you, and that soundly." — (006-094–098)

Stephano's response to discovering [[caliban|Caliban]] (still believed to be a four-legged monster with [[trinculo|Trinculo]] inside the gaberdine) exemplifies his instinct for imperial language. [[caliban|Caliban]] is a "present," a gift to be offered to an emperor — Stephano is already thinking in terms of patronage and power. The wine, which Stephano forces into [[caliban|Caliban]]'s mouth, is the instrument of colonisation: it purchases loyalty, silences dissent, and creates dependence. The entire colonial dynamic of the subplot is compressed into these five lines.

> "Monster, I will kill this man: his daughter and I will be king and queen, — save our graces! — and Trinculo and thyself shall be viceroys." — (009-103–106)

Stephano's plan for the new regime is a [[master|master]]piece of self-serving distribution: the crown goes to him (and [[miranda|Miranda]], whom he has never met), while his co-conspirators get the subordinate offices. The parenthetical "save our graces" — a mock-courtly formula inserted into a murder plot — captures the essence of Stephano's character: he imagines kingship as a costume drama, a performance of titles and styles rather than an exercise of power. The speech is a miniature of incompetent ambition, the would-be tyrant who dreams of protocol rather than policy.

## Critical Reception

Frank Kermode, in his Arden edition of *The Tempest* (1954), reads the comic subplot of Stephano, [[trinculo|Trinculo]], and [[caliban|Caliban]] as a deliberate parody of the play's main political action. For Kermode, the drunken butler's "reign" functions as a comic reduction of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|rule]] and [[antonio|Antonio]]'s usurpation alike: the same mechanisms of power, ambition, and betrayal that govern the court of [[milan|Milan]] are replayed in the register of farce, with a bottle standing in for a sceptre and a fool for a counsellor. Kermode argues that Shakespeare uses the low plot to expose the essential absurdity of political ambition — if a butler with a bottle can claim a kingdom with the same language [[antonio|Antonio]] used to claim a dukedom, then what distinguishes legitimate [[power|authority]] from successful theft? The comic subplot thus serves a serious structural purpose: it deflates the pretensions of power by demonstrating that the same ambitions that shape kingdoms also animate tavern drunks. For Kermode, Stephano is not merely a clown but a "reductive mirror" in which the play's political themes are reflected at their most naked.

Northrop Frye, in his analysis of Shakespearean comedy, situates the Stephano-[[trinculo|Trinculo]]-[[caliban|Caliban]] trio within the tradition of the comic "blocking characters" — figures whose ambitions threaten the movement toward comic resolution but whose incompetence guarantees their failure. Frye notes that Stephano's rebellion, unlike [[antonio|Antonio]]'s, poses no genuine threat because it is conducted at the level of appetite rather than intellect: wine, not strategy, drives the conspiracy. The comic subplot thus enacts what Frye terms the "safety valve" function of farce in Shakespearean romance — it allows the audience to experience the idea of rebellion without the anxiety of real danger, containing the subversive energy of the play within a frame of absurdity. Frye observes that the structural parallelism between the two conspiracies — [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] plotting regicide in Act 2, Stephano and his companions plotting tyrannicide in Act 3 — creates a choric commentary: the low plot judges the high plot by revealing how similar they are when stripped of courtly decorum.

Stephen Greenblatt, in "Learning to Curse" (1990) and related essays, reads Stephano's rebellion through the lens of early modern colonial discourse. For Greenblatt, the encounter between Stephano and [[caliban|Caliban]] is a farcical dramatisation of the colonial dynamic documented in New World travel narratives: the ordinary European who, by virtue of arriving with unfamiliar technology (in this case, alcohol), is mistaken for a god by indigenous peoples and elevated to a position of unearned [[power|authority]]. Greenblatt notes that Shakespeare inverts the class hierarchy of the colonial encounter — here it is not a gentleman-adventurer but a butler, a member of the serving class, who becomes the native's "god" — and in doing so exposes the arbitrary foundation of colonial [[power|authority]]. Any European, however low, could be worshipped; any technology, however destructive (and Greenblatt emphasises the parallel between Stephano's wine and the historical "firewater" of colonial trade), could purchase [[power|dominion]]. The rebellion's collapse — [[caliban|Caliban]] finally recognising Stephano as a "drunken" fool rather than a god in Act 5 — is, for Greenblatt, the play's most pointed colonial critique: the native awakens from his worship, but too late, and his "freedom" consists only in returning to the servitude he sought to escape. Stephano's brief, absurd reign is thus a parable of colonial il[[authority|legitimacy]], rendered as farce because the truth it tells is too dark for tragedy.
