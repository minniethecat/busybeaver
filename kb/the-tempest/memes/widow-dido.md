## Overview

"Widow Dido" is not a character in the play but a classical reference that surfaces repeatedly in Act 2, Scene 1, as the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed Neapolitan courtiers banter about [[tunis|Tunis]] and [[carthage|Carthage]]. The joke — how did a "widow" come to be associated with Dido, who in Virgil's *Aeneid* was widowed before Aeneas arrived? — becomes a vehicle for the courtiers' competitive erudition and [[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s mockery of [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]].

## Role in the Play

The Dido ex[[transformation|change]] begins when [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]], describing the freshness of the courtiers' clothing, compares their situation to "widow Dido's time" (005-062). [[antonio|Antonio]] immediately seizes on the phrase: "Widow! a pox o' that! How came that widow in? widow Dido!" (005-063). [[sebastian|Sebastian]] joins in — "Bate, I beseech you, widow Dido" (005-077) — and [[antonio|Antonio]] echoes, "O, widow Dido! ay, widow Dido" (005-078).

The ex[[transformation|change]] is layered. On the surface it is pure mockery: [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s rambling erudition is a target for the younger courtiers' wit. But the Dido reference also enriches the play's geographic imagination. Dido, Queen of Carthage, connects [[tunis|Tunis]] to the Virgilian Mediterranean — Aeneas's wanderings, the tragedy of abandoned love, the founding of empires. For an audience familiar with the *Aeneid*, [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s observation that "This [[tunis|Tunis]], sir, was [[carthage|Carthage]]" (005-066) transforms a diplomatic wedding voyage into an echo of epic.

> *Gon.* Not since widow Dido's time.
> *Ant.* Widow! a pox o' that! How came that widow in? widow Dido!
> *Seb.* What if he had said 'widower Æneas' too? Good Lord, how you take it!
> *Adr.* 'Widow Dido' said you? you make me study of that: she was of Carthage, not of Tunis.
> *Gon.* This Tunis, sir, was Carthage.
>
> (005-062)

## Key Scenes

The Dido references are concentrated entirely in Act 2, Scene 1. The passage is p[[magic|art]] of a larger set-piece in which [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]], [[adrian|Adrian]], [[antonio|Antonio]], and [[sebastian|Sebastian]] engage in a battle of wits while [[alonso|Alonso]] grieves. The Dido joke is one of several learned references — alongside [[claribel|Claribel]], [[tunis|Tunis]], and [[carthage|Carthage]] — that mark the courtiers as educated Renaissance noblemen and distinguish their modes of speech from the [[island|island]]'s other inhabitants.

## Relationships

- **[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]**: The target of the mockery; he introduces the Dido reference earnestly and is mocked for it.
- **[[antonio|Antonio]] and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]**: The mockers, who use the "widow Dido" refrain as a weapon of courtly wit.
- **[[adrian|Adrian]]**: P[[magic|art]]icipates in the geographic discussion, serving as a foil to [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s erudition.
