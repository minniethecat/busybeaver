## Overview

Forgiveness is the culminating moral action of *The Tempest*, the choice [[prospero|Prospero]] makes in Act 5 when he holds his enemies entirely at his mercy and elects to pardon them rather than punish (014-035). The play's entire architecture — [[the-shipwreck|the storm]], the separation, the trials — is a mechanism designed not for revenge but for the possibility of forgiveness. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s decision to forgive transforms the play from a revenge tragedy into a romance: the wronged duke reclaims his dukedom not through force but through grace, and in doing so sets all p[[magic|art]]ies — including himself — free (014-119). Shakespeare's treatment of forgiveness draws deeply on Christian traditions of mercy and reconciliation, p[[magic|art]]icularly the Lord's Prayer ("forgive us our debts, as we forgive our debtors") and the Sermon on the Mount's injunction to love one's enemies. Yet the play complicates any simple Christian allegory: forgiveness is granted without repentance in [[antonio|Antonio]]'s case, rendered in secular and political terms, and framed as much by Renaissance Neoplatonism — with its emphasis on reason's triumph over passion — as by scripture.

## In the Play

The forgiveness theme crystallises in Act 5, when [[prospero|Prospero]] enters "in his magic robes" and confronts the men who deposed him. His speech to them traces the arc from rage to pardon. He begins by enumerating [[alonso|Alonso]]'s crime: "Most cruelly / Didst thou, [[alonso|Alonso]], use me and my daughter" (014-088–089), yet the accusation is a prelude to embrace. To [[antonio|Antonio]], the unrepentant usurper, he says "I do forgive thee, / Unnatural though thou [[magic|art]]" (014-119–120) — the pardon is granted without any sign of [[guilt|remorse]] from its recipient.

The language of forgiveness is intertwined with the language of mercy. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s report of the prisoners' distress moves [[prospero|Prospero]] to declare: "Though with their high wrongs I am struck to th' quick, / Yet with my nobler reason 'gainst my fury / Do I take p[[magic|art]]. The rarer action is / In virtue than in vengeance" (014-035–038). The spirit's compassion shames the man into his own — [[ariel|Ariel]] becomes the moral mirror in which [[prospero|Prospero]] sees his obligation to forgive. "Hast thou, which [[magic|art]] but air, a touch, a feeling / Of their afflictions, and shall not myself, / One of their kind … be kindlier moved than thou [[magic|art]]?" (014-029–033).

The pardon is not merely personal but political. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness extends even to acknowledging his own debt: "Must ask my child forgiveness!" (014-252) he exclaims, recognising that in his single-minded pursuit of justice he has wronged [[miranda|Miranda]] herself. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s "Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat / Thou pardon me my wrongs" (014-135–136) links forgiveness to restitution. Forgiveness restores not only relationships but the political order that [[antonio|Antonio]]'s usurpation shattered. The marriage of [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] and [[miranda|Miranda]], revealed in the play's final scene, is the dynastic seal on the pardon.

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness of [[caliban|Caliban]] is more grudging but no less significant. [[caliban|Caliban]], plotting murder to the end, is met with "He is as disproportioned in his manners / As in his shape. Go, sirrah, to [[prosperos-cell|my cell]]" (014-368–369). It is not a warm pardon, but [[prospero|Prospero]] acknowledges [[caliban|Caliban]] as "this thing of darkness I / Acknowledge mine" (014-367–368), accepting responsibility for the creature he has tormented and failed to civilise.

## Key Passages

> Though with their high wrongs I am struck to th' quick,
> Yet with my nobler reason 'gainst my fury
> Do I take part. The rarer action is
> In virtue than in vengeance.
> — (014-035–038)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s moral choice, spoken to [[ariel|Ariel]] before he confronts his enemies. "The rarer action is / In virtue than in vengeance" is the ethical thesis of the entire play (014-035). [[prospero|Prospero]] acknowledges his fury — he is "struck to th' quick" — but chooses reason over passion.

> For you, most wicked sir, whom to call brother
> Would even infect my mouth, I do forgive
> Thy rankest fault — all of them.
> — (014-157–159)

To [[antonio|Antonio]]: the hardest forgiveness (014-157). [[prospero|Prospero]] does not pretend affection — [[antonio|Antonio]] is "most wicked," his brotherhood a source of contamination. Yet the pardon is unconditional: "all of them." [[antonio|Antonio]], notably, offers no reply; his silence is the play's darkest note, a forgiveness accepted or rejected in a void.

> As you from crimes would pardon'd be,
> Let your indulgence set me free.
> — (015-019–020)

The Epilogue's appeal to the audience (015-019). [[prospero|Prospero]], having pardoned everyone in the play, now asks the audience to pardon him. The forgiveness theme extends beyond the fiction into the theatre itself: the actor playing [[prospero|Prospero]] asks for the spectators' "indulgence" as the duke asked for [[alonso|Alonso]]'s. [[art|Art]] and moral action merge in the final couplet.

## Critical Context

Frank Kermode, in his landmark Arden edition of *The Tempest*, identifies forgiveness as the structural keystone of the play's romance pattern. For Kermode, the movement from wrong to pardon is not merely a thematic concern but the genre's defining rhythm: romance as a form insists on reconciliation, and [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness is the culminating gesture that converts the play's accumulated wrongs into the materials of restoration. Kermode reads the pardon as inseparable from [[prospero|Prospero]]'s renunciation of magic — both are acts of surrender that make genuine reconciliation possible, and together they constitute what Kermode calls the "great scene of forgiveness" that the entire play has been building toward.

Stephen Greenblatt approaches [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness with greater scepticism, reading it as an act of political theatre rather than unalloyed virtue. In Greenblatt's analysis, forgiveness is the final instrument of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s power, not its abdication: the duke stages a display of mercy that reasserts his moral [[power|authority]] over the very men he pardons, binding them more securely through gratitude than he ever could through coercion. The conditional [[nature|nature]] of the pardons — [[alonso|Alonso]] receives warmth, [[antonio|Antonio]] cold silence, [[caliban|Caliban]] grudging acknowledgment — reveals, for Greenblatt, the political calculus behind the gesture. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s forgiveness does not transcend the politics of the [[island|island]] so much as consummate them.

Sarah Beckwith, in *Shakespeare and the Grammar of Forgiveness*, situates the play within post-Reformation debates about [[guilt|penitence]], absolution, and the [[nature|nature]] of forgiveness itself. Beckwith argues that Shakespeare's late plays are laboratories for exploring what it means to forgive in a world where the traditional sacramental frameworks have been shattered — where forgiveness can no longer be mediated by the Church and must be worked out between human beings in ordinary language. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s struggle to forgive — his acknowledged fury, his need for [[ariel|Ariel]]'s prompting, his coldness toward [[antonio|Antonio]] — is, for Beckwith, a dramatisation of the difficulty of forgiveness as a human act rather than a divine one. The Epilogue's direct appeal to the audience extends this grammar of forgiveness into the theatre itself, making the play an enactment of the very thing it describes.

Robert Bechtold Heilman, writing on the play's moral architecture, identifies "the rarer action" as one of Shakespeare's most compressed ethical formulations. In Heilman's reading, the line distils a central Renaissance debate between the claims of passion and reason, vengeance and virtue. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s choice is not instinctive — the play insists on the effort it costs him — and Heilman sees in that effort the play's most profound claim: that forgiveness is not a feeling but a discipline, an action chosen against the grain of natural impulse.

## Thematic Analysis

Forgiveness in *The Tempest* operates on three levels. First, the **personal**: [[prospero|Prospero]] forgives [[alonso|Alonso]] (warmly), [[antonio|Antonio]] (coldly), and [[caliban|Caliban]] (grudgingly). Each pardon is different in tone but identical in effect — the wrongdoer is [[freedom|release]]d. Second, the **political**: forgiveness is the mechanism by which the play's disrupted order is restored. [[alonso|Alonso]] resigns [[milan|Milan]], [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] weds [[miranda|Miranda]], and the Italian courts are reconciled — all made possible by [[prospero|Prospero]]'s initial act of pardon. Third, the **meta-theatrical**: the Epilogue extends the forgiveness theme to the audience, making the play itself an act of pardon-seeking.

The cost of forgiveness is as significant as its reach. [[prospero|Prospero]] gives up his magic to reach the point of pardon — "I'll break my staff … I'll drown my book" (014-065, 014-068). The [[magic|art]] that gave him power over his enemies is renounced before he can pardon them. Forgiveness, the play suggests, requires surrender: the surrender of the right to punish, of the instruments of coercion, of the identity of the wronged p[[magic|art]]y.
