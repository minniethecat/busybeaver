## Overview

Ariel is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s spirit-servant — an "airy spirit" capable of flying, shape-shifting, [[authority|command]]ing the elements, and enchanting with music. Rescued by [[prospero|Prospero]] from twelve years of imprisonment in a "cloven pine" where [[sycorax|the witch]] [[sycorax|Sycorax]] had confined him, Ariel serves in hope of the [[freedom|liberty]] [[prospero|Prospero]] has promised. Unlike [[caliban|Caliban]]'s resentful servitude, Ariel's is negotiated and temporary — a debt to be repaid with labour. Ariel executes the play's most spectacular moments: conjuring [[the-shipwreck|the storm]], appearing as a [[harpy|harpy]] to accuse the "three men of sin," leading the drunken conspirators through briars with tabor and pipe. Yet Ariel is never merely an instrument; the longing for freedom that surfaces in his every interaction with [[prospero|Prospero]] gives him a pathos that complicates his role as magical functionary, and his capacity for moral perception — prompting [[prospero|Prospero]] toward [[forgiveness|mercy]] in Act 5 — elevates him beyond the conventional spirit-servant of Jacobean drama.

## Role in the Play

Ariel first appears in Act 1, Scene 2, reporting the successful execution of [[the-shipwreck|the storm]] with vivid detail — "I flamed amazement: sometime I'ld divide, / And burn in many places" (003-197–198) — and receiving [[prospero|Prospero]]'s praise. But immediately Ariel presses his master: "Is there more toil? … Let me remember thee what thou hast promised, / Which is not yet performed me … my [[freedom|liberty]]" (003-243–246). [[prospero|Prospero]]'s response — threatening to imprison Ariel again in an oak tree for twelve years — reveals the coercive foundation of their relationship.

Ariel's agency grows through the play. He puts [[alonso|Alonso]] and [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] to sleep with "solemn music" (005-174), then wakes [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] in time to foil [[sebastian|Sebastian]] and [[antonio|Antonio]]'s assassination plot. In Act 3, Scene 2, he torments [[caliban|Caliban]], [[stephano|Stephano]], and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] by speaking in [[caliban|Caliban]]'s voice, sowing dissension among the conspirators. In Act 3, Scene 3, he appears as a [[harpy|harpy]] to confront [[alonso|Alonso]], [[antonio|Antonio]], and [[sebastian|Sebastian]]:

> You are three men of sin, whom Destiny —
> That hath to instrument this lower world
> And what is in't — the never-surfeited sea
> Hath caused to belch up you; and on this island,
> Where man doth not inhabit — you 'mongst men
> Being most unfit to live. I have made you mad;
> And even with such-like valour men hang and drown
> Their proper selves.
> — (010-080–087)

This cosmic accusation is the play's most direct moral judgement, and it is delivered not by [[prospero|Prospero]] but by his spirit-actor. [[prospero|Prospero]] calls it "of my instruction … bravely done" (010-114), but Ariel's performance exceeds mere execution: it carries theatrical conviction that suggests the spirit is more than a tool.

In Act 4, Ariel conjures the masque of goddesses — [[iris|Iris]], [[ceres|Ceres]], [[juno|Juno]] — and appears in the "glistering apparel" that lures [[caliban|Caliban]]'s conspirators. In Act 5, the moral arc of Ariel's character reaches its culmination when he prompts [[prospero|Prospero]] toward [[forgiveness|mercy]]: "Your charm so strongly works 'em, / That if you now beheld them, your affections / Would become tender" (014-029–031). [[prospero|Prospero]]'s reply — "Hast thou, which [[magic|art]] but air, a touch, a feeling / Of their affections?" (014-032–033) — acknowledges Ariel's moral perception while maintaining the hierarchy of spirit and man. The ex[[transformation|change]] marks the play's emotional turning point: the spirit of air teaches the master of magic what it means to be human.

Ariel's final liberation — "But yet thou shalt have freedom: so, so, so" (014-109) — is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s last [[authority|command]] before the Epilogue. The spirit who began the play as a bound servant ends it as a free being, singing his way back to the elements.

## Key Speeches

> I boarded the king's ship; now on the beak,
> Now in the waist, the deck, in every cabin,
> I flamed amazement: sometime I'ld divide,
> And burn in many places; on the topmast,
> The yards and bowsprit, would I flame distinctly,
> Then meet and join. Jove's lightnings, the precursors
> O' the dreadful thunder-claps, more momentary
> And sight-outrunning were not; the fire and cracks
> Of sulphurous roaring the most mighty Neptune
> Seem to besiege and make his bold waves tremble,
> Yea, his dread trident shake.
> — (003-197–208)

Ariel's first speech is a bravura performance — a description of [[the-shipwreck|the storm]] so vivid that it makes the opening scene's chaos visible to [[miranda|Miranda]] (and the audience) for the first time. The verb "flamed" and the iterative p[[magic|art]]iciples ("dividing," "burning," "flaming") capture the kinetic energy of Ariel's [[magic|art]]. The comparison to Jove's lightning elevates the spirit's [[power|power]] to the mythological register, while the image of Neptune trembling before [[the-shipwreck|the storm]] establishes the hierarchy of forces that [[prospero|Prospero]], through Ariel, [[authority|command]]s.

> Full fathom five thy father lies;
> Of his bones are coral made;
> Those are pearls that were his eyes:
> Nothing of him that doth fade
> But doth suffer a sea-change
> Into something rich and strange.
> Sea-nymphs hourly ring his knell:
> Hark! now I hear them — Ding-dong, bell.
> — (003-510–517)

The most famous [[music|song]] in the play is Ariel's elegy for a man who is not dead. The transformation of death into beauty — bones to coral, eyes to pearls — is the play's central transformation metaphor in miniature. [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] hears his father's funeral; the audience knows [[alonso|Alonso]] lives. Ariel's [[magic|art]] creates truth by making fiction feel more real than reality.

> Where the bee sucks, there suck I:
> In a cowslip's bell I lie;
> There I couch when owls do cry.
> On the bat's back I do fly
> After summer merrily.
> Merrily, merrily shall I live now
> Under the blossom that hangs on the bough.
> — (014-100–107)

Ariel's final [[music|song]], sung as he prepares to leave [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[servitude|service]], is a fantasy of pure freedom. The images — a bee's diet, a flower's bell for a bed, a bat's back for flight — are miniature and delicate, worlds away from the cosmic scale of [[the-shipwreck|the storm]] and the [[harpy|harpy]]. After five acts of spectacular labour, Ariel's [[freedom|liberty]] is imagined as smallness: the right to be inconsequential.

## Critical Reception

Scholarly attention to Ariel has centred on questions of agency, gender, and the colonial dynamics of servitude. The 19th-century editorial tradition — represented in the Critical Apparatus of the Cambridge edition and Frank Kermode's Arden edition (1954) — focused on textual and staging details: the disputed attribution of certain lines to Ariel rather than [[prospero|Prospero]] in the later folios (043-060), Theobald's emendation of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "I thank thee, Ariel: come" to "I thank you:—Ariel, come" (042-046), and the placement of stage directions for Ariel's en[[dream|trance]]s and exits (037-027, 037-030, 037-061). These textual cruces reflect the interpretive difficulty of a character whose material presence on stage — invisible to the other characters, visible to the audience — is inherently ambiguous.

Postcolonial criticism has read Ariel and [[caliban|Caliban]] as contrasting models of the colonised subject. Where [[caliban|Caliban]] resists and is punished, Ariel complies and is eventually freed — a narrative that has been critiqued as a fantasy of the "grateful native" who serves the coloniser willingly and earns [[freedom|emancipation]] on the coloniser's terms. Feminist criticism has explored the gender indeterminacy of Ariel, who is referred to with male pronouns throughout the Folio text but has been played by both men and women in performance, and whose "delicate" and "dainty" epithets — together with the non-gendered "chick" (014-397) — resist easy categorisation.

Ariel's moral autonomy — p[[magic|art]]icularly the [[forgiveness|mercy]]-prompting speech in Act 5 — has been the subject of theological readings that see in the spirit a figure of grace: an external force that enables the movement from vengeance to forgiveness that the human characters cannot achieve alone. In this reading, Ariel is not merely [[prospero|Prospero]]'s instrument but the agent of a larger moral order that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic can [[authority|command]] but not create.
