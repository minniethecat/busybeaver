## Overview

Fortune in *The Tempest* hovers between classical fate and Christian providence. [[prospero|Prospero]] describes the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed court as having been brought to the [[island|island]] "by accident most strange, bountiful Fortune … hath mine enemies / Brought to this shore" (003-240). The dual agency—"accident" and "Fortune" working together—captures the play's theological ambiguity: events are simultaneously chance and design. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic orchestrates what appears to be luck, but he insists that a "most auspicious star" (003-244) governs his actions. The play never resolves whether Fortune is an independent force or merely the name [[prospero|Prospero]] gives to his own manipulation of events.

## In the Play

Fortune is first invoked by [[prospero|Prospero]] to explain the arrival of his enemies. The [[storm|tempest]] that wrecks the ship appears to be an accident of weather, but [[prospero|Prospero]] reveals it as a calculated operation. Yet he still credits Fortune—as though his [[magic|art]] and Fortune collaborate, each working through the other. This aligns with Renaissance commonplaces about fortune as the sphere of human action: virtù (skill, [[magic|art]]) must seize the opportunity that fortuna (chance, luck) provides. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own metaphor — "If now I court not, but omit, my fortunes / Will ever after droop" (003-245) — casts fortune as a suitor who must be wooed at the right moment or lost forever.

[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s philosophy is the play's most sustained meditation on fortune. His speech on the commonwealth—"all things in common nature should produce"—imagines a world from which fortune, in the sense of unequal distribution, has been eliminated. His comfort to [[alonso|Alonso]]—"Beseech you, sir, be merry; you have cause … / … our hint of woe / Is common" (Act 2, Scene 1)—treats suffering as a leveller, a kind of negative fortune that equalises all.

[[alonso|Alonso]]'s experience of the [[storm|shipwreck]] is one of catastrophic fortune: he believes his son is dead, his kingdom in peril. His cry—"O, it is monstrous, monstrous!" (Act 3, Scene 3)—is the voice of a man who feels fortune has turned against him utterly. His restoration at the play's end is therefore experienced as a miraculous reversal—fortune redeemed (014-330).

## Key Passages

> By accident most strange, bountiful Fortune,
> Now my dear lady, hath mine enemies
> Brought to this shore.
>
> (003-240)

The duality is the point: "accident most strange" and "bountiful Fortune" name the same event from different perspectives—chance from below, providence from above. The passage is immediately followed by [[prospero|Prospero]]'s recognition of "a most auspicious star" (003-244), reinforcing the providential reading.

> *Ari.* You are three men of sin, whom Destiny,—
> That hath to instrument this lower world
> And what is in't,—the never-surfeited sea
> Hath caused to belch up you; and on this island,
> Where man doth not inhabit,—you 'mongst men
> Being most unfit to live.
>
> (010-081)

The [[harpy|harpy]] speech reframes fortune as divine instrument. Destiny here is not chance but moral agency — the sea "belches up" the sinners onto the [[island|island]] for judgment. The speech marks the play's most explicit alignment of fortune with providence.
