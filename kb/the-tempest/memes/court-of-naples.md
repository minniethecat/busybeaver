## Overview

The Court of Naples is the political entity that shadows the entire play. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s ship carries the Neapolitan court from [[tunis|Tunis]] (where his daughter [[claribel|Claribel]] was married to [[tunis|the King of Tunis]]) back to [[naples|Naples]] — a journey [[prospero|Prospero]]'s storm intercepts. The court arrives on the [[island|island]] as a microcosm of the political world: the king, his brother, his son, his councillor, and the [[milan|Milan]]ese usurper who made [[naples|Naples]] complicit in his crime. Over the course of the play, the Neapolitan court is subjected to [[prospero|Prospero]]'s testing: [[alonso|Alonso]] is driven to despair and then to repentance, [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s ambition is exposed and thw[[magic|art]]ed, [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] is separated from his father and led to [[miranda|Miranda]]. At the play's end, the court is restored, purged of its worst impulses, and bound to [[milan|Milan]] by marriage rather than tribute.

## Political Role

The Court of Naples is the play's vehicle for exploring political guilt and redemption. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s complicity in [[prospero|Prospero]]'s overthrow makes him an object of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s project; his grief for [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] (whom he believes drowned) is both punishment and preparation for repentance. The court's internal dynamics — [[sebastian|Sebastian]]'s ambition, [[antonio|Antonio]]'s manipulation, [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s steadfastness — replay the [[milan|Milan]]-usurpation pattern in miniature, suggesting that the dynamics of [[power|power]] and betrayal are not peculiar to [[milan|Milan]] but inherent in court life itself.

> This King of Naples, being an enemy
> To me inveterate, hearkens my brother's suit;
> Which was, that he, in lieu o' the premises,
> Of homage and I know not how much tribute,
> Should presently extirpate me and mine
> Out of the dukedom, and confer fair Milan,
> With all the honours, on my brother: whereon,
> A treacherous army levied, one midnight
> Fated to the purpose, did Antonio open
> The gates of Milan
>
> (003-163)

The resolution of the Neapolitan court's arc is dynastic. [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] will marry [[miranda|Miranda]]; [[naples|Naples]] and [[milan|Milan]] will be united through marriage rather than bound through tribute. [[alonso|Alonso]]'s guilt is expiated not by punishment but by the transformation of the political relationship his guilt created. The court that was an instrument of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s dispossession becomes [[the-ship|the vessel]] of his dynasty's future.

## Related Characters

- **[[alonso|Alonso]]**: The king whose guilt and grief drive the court's moral arc. His restoration — to his son, his throne, and his moral sense — is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s greatest achievement.
- **[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]**: The heir whose love for [[miranda|Miranda]] transforms the political relationship between [[naples|Naples]] and [[milan|Milan]].
- **[[sebastian|Sebastian]]**: The king's brother who nearly repeats [[antonio|Antonio]]'s crime. His silence in Act 5 leaves his moral status unresolved.
- **[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]**: The councillor whose goodness — "holy [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]" (014-254), [[alonso|Alonso]] calls him — is the court's moral touchstone. He gave [[prospero|Prospero]] the books that made the [[island|island]]'s magic possible.
- **[[antonio|Antonio]]**: The [[milan|Milan]]ese usurper travelling with the Neapolitan court. His silence in Act 5 is the play's most unsettling political fact.
