## Overview

Argier (Algiers) appears in *The Tempest* only in [[prospero|Prospero]]'s narration of [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s banishment in Act 1, Scene 2. It is the city from which the "damn'd witch [[sycorax|Sycorax]]" was exiled — spared execution only because of her pregnancy — and sent to the island that [[prospero|Prospero]] would later claim (003-362). Argier never appears on stage and has no independent existence in the play; its entire dramatic function is to provide [[sycorax|Sycorax]] with an origin, a crime, and a sentence (003-358). Yet Argier is thematically crucial: it is the dark counter-image to [[milan|Milan]], the [[afric|Africa]]n city of [[magic|sorcery]] and exile that stands opposite [[prospero|Prospero]]'s Italian city-state of [[knowledge|learning]] and [[authority|rule]]. Between these two cities — Argier and [[milan|Milan]] — the island's history of contested ownership is mapped.

## Description

Argier enters the play through [[ariel|Ariel]]'s single-word answer. [[prospero|Prospero]], testing [[ariel|Ariel]]'s memory, asks where [[sycorax|Sycorax]] was born: "Where was she born? speak; tell me" (003-357). [[ariel|Ariel]] replies: "Sir, in Argier" (003-358). The brevity of the answer — two syllables — does the work of locating [[sycorax|Sycorax]] in a specific, real geography. Argier was Shakespeare's name for Algiers, the North [[afric|Africa]]n port city that was, in the early seventeenth century, a centre of Barbary piracy and an object of English anxiety and fascination.

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s response confirms and elaborates: "This damn'd witch [[sycorax|Sycorax]], / For mischiefs manifold, and sorceries terrible / To enter human hearing, from Argier, / Thou know'st, was banish'd" (003-362–365). The syntax is significant: "from Argier" is delayed to the end of the sentence, the geographical origin functioning almost as a judgement (003-364). To be from Argier is to be associated with a specific kind of otherness — [[afric|Africa]]n, Muslim, magical, piratical — that early modern English audiences would have recognised.

The nature of [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s crime is left unspecified — "for mischiefs manifold, and sorceries terrible" — but the crucial detail is that "for one thing she did / They would not take her life" (003-365–366). That "one thing" was her pregnancy with [[caliban|Caliban]]. Argier's justice, unlike the island's later regime, was tempered by [[forgiveness|mercy]]: the pregnant witch was not executed but exiled. This act of conditional [[forgiveness|mercy]] — sparing the mother for the sake of the unborn child — is the act that sets the entire history of the island in motion. Without Argier's leniency, [[sycorax|Sycorax]] would have died, [[caliban|Caliban]] would never have been born, and [[prospero|Prospero]] would have arrived at an uninhabited island with no prior claimant.

## Significance

Argier serves as [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s [[milan|Milan]] — the political world she lost, the place from which she was exiled. The symmetry between [[prospero|Prospero]] and [[sycorax|Sycorax]] is structural: both are banished from their home cities ([[milan|Milan]] and Argier); both are sent to the island with a child ([[miranda|Miranda]] and [[caliban|Caliban]]); both exercise forms of power on the island ([[magic|art]] and [[magic|sorcery]]). But the asymmetry is ideological: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[milan|Milan]] is a European city-state of [[knowledge|learning]] and legitimate [[authority|rule]]; [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s Argier is an [[afric|Africa]]n port of [[magic|sorcery]] and piracy. The contrast between the two cities of origin encodes the play's racial and colonial assumptions: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s exile is unjust usurpation; [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s exile is just punishment.

However, the play itself provides the materials to question this encoding. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s narration is not neutral reportage but a self-serving account designed to justify his own [[power|authority]] over [[ariel|Ariel]] and the island. We have no independent access to [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s story, no testimony from Argier's magistrates, no evidence beyond [[prospero|Prospero]]'s word. Argier exists in the play only as [[prospero|Prospero]] narrates it — a city whose only function is to produce a witch, whose only act of note is to exile her, and whose subsequent history is silence. The colonial logic is transparent: Argier is constructed as [[milan|Milan]]'s dark double so that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|rule]] can appear legitimate by contrast.
