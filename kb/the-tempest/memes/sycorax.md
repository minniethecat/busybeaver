## Overview

Sycorax never appears on stage, yet her presence permeates the play. Banished from [[argier|Algiers]] "for mischiefs manifold and sorceries terrible" (003-363), she was marooned on the island while pregnant with [[caliban|Caliban]]. She imprisoned [[ariel|Ariel]] in a "cloven pine" (003-371) for refusing to execute her "e[[magic|art]]hy and abhorred [[authority|command]]s" (003-369), and died before [[prospero|Prospero]]'s arrival, leaving [[caliban|Caliban]] as the island's sole inhabitant. Sycorax functions as the dark double of [[prospero|Prospero]] — a witch where he is a magus, a tyrant where he is (in his own telling) a just [[authority|rule]]r — and her offstage existence creates the moral contrast by which [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "[[magic|art]]" is defined as legitimate.

Sycorax is the play's structuring absence: a figure whose power must be disavowed for the colonial order of the island to cohere. She is [[afric|Africa]]n where [[prospero|Prospero]] is European, female where he is male, practitioner of natural [[magic|sorcery]] where he wields book-learned [[magic|art]], and dead where he is alive — each opposition reinforcing the hierarchy that positions [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|rule]] as civilised and hers as monstrous. Yet the very energy with which [[prospero|Prospero]] denounces her betrays dependence: without Sycorax as foil, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s claim to moral superiority has no ground.

## Character and Role

Everything we know of Sycorax comes from [[prospero|Prospero]]'s account to [[ariel|Ariel]] (003-353–400). She is "the foul witch Sycorax" (003-353), "this damned witch Sycorax" (003-362), banished from [[argier|Algiers]] — saved from execution only because she was pregnant: "for one thing she did / They would not take her life" (003-366–367). The narrative is entirely one-sided; Sycorax has no speech, no advocate, and no presence beyond the words of the man who replaced her. This structural silencing is the point. Sycorax is not a character in any conventional sense — she is a rhetorical construction, the demonised predecessor against whom [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|rule]] defines itself.

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s description of Sycorax's power emphasises its natural, bodily, malefic character. Her magic is not learned from books but innate: "she was / A witch, and one so strong / That [she] could control the moon" (003-353–355). Unlike [[prospero|Prospero]]'s reasoned "[[magic|art]]," Sycorax's [[magic|sorcery]] is a force of [[nature|nature]] — female, [[afric|Africa]]n, bodily, and described in terms of deformity ("with age and envy / Was grown into a hoop" (003-353–354)). [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic derives from [[knowledge|study]], from the books [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] provided, from the rational discipline of Renaissance Neoplatonism; Sycorax's power, by contrast, is figured as archaic, uterine, and terrifying. The opposition is ideological: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] is legitimate because it is learned and European; her [[magic|sorcery]] is illegitimate because it is innate and [[afric|Africa]]n.

The crucial detail is Sycorax's treatment of [[ariel|Ariel]]. She imprisoned [[ariel|the spirit]] in a pine tree for twelve years for refusing to carry out her [[authority|command]]s. [[prospero|Prospero]] frees [[ariel|Ariel]] from this imprisonment — but immediately puts [[ariel|Ariel]] to work on his own errands. The parallel is pointed: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|rule]] over [[ariel|Ariel]] is gentler than Sycorax's, but it is [[authority|rule]] nonetheless. The distinction [[prospero|Prospero]] insists upon — between his benevolent [[servitude|mastery]] and her cruel tyranny — is real in degree but collapses in kind the moment [[ariel|Ariel]] threatens to remind him of his promises: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s rage in response ("Thou liest, malignant thing!" (003-381)) reveals the coercive foundation beneath the patriarchal civility.

Sycorax also functions as [[prospero|Prospero]]'s constructed "other" in the postcolonial sense: the figure whose supposed monstrosity authorises the coloniser's presence. By narrating Sycorax as a foul witch who imprisoned spirits and bred "a freckled whelp, hag-born" (003-382), [[prospero|Prospero]] establishes the moral narrative that legitimates his own seizure of the island. His [[authority|rule]] is not usurpation — so the logic runs — because the previous regime was illegitimate; he is not a coloniser but a liberator (of [[ariel|Ariel]]) and a civiliser (of [[caliban|Caliban]]). The play never allows Sycorax to answer this charge, but it does permit [[caliban|Caliban]] to contest it: "This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother" (003-455). [[caliban|Caliban]]'s claim, dismissed by [[prospero|Prospero]] as the ingratitude of a "born devil" (003-480), belongs to a counter-narrative the play gestures toward but does not endorse — a narrative in which Sycorax was not a monster but a sovereign, and [[prospero|Prospero]] not a saviour but a usurper.

## Key Relationships

- **[[caliban|Caliban]]**: Sycorax's son, born on the island after her banishment. [[caliban|Caliban]]'s claim to the island derives entirely from her: "This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother" (003-455). His invocation of Sycorax is his strongest argument against [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]] — an argument grounded in matrilineal inheritance and prior occupation. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s counter-narrative, which figures [[caliban|Caliban]] as the offspring of a witch and a demon ("got by the devil himself / Upon thy wicked dam" (003-440–441)), works to delegitimise that inheritance by delegitimising the mother.

- **[[ariel|Ariel]]**: Imprisoned by Sycorax for twelve years in a pine tree for refusing her "e[[magic|art]]hy and abhorred [[authority|command]]s" (003-369). [[prospero|Prospero]]'s freeing of [[ariel|Ariel]] is the act that distinguishes his [[authority|rule]] from Sycorax's, though the distinction is complicated by [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own demands on [[ariel|the spirit]]. [[ariel|Ariel]], notably, never speaks of Sycorax directly — all information comes through [[prospero|Prospero]] — which means [[ariel|Ariel]]'s twelve years of torment are available to [[prospero|Prospero]] as a rhetorical weapon precisely because [[ariel|Ariel]] cannot describe them independently.

- **[[prospero|Prospero]]**: Sycorax's successor as [[master|master]] of the island and [[caliban|Caliban]]'s [[authority|rule]]r. [[prospero|Prospero]] constructs his moral identity in opposition to Sycorax: where she practised malefic [[magic|sorcery]], he practises learned [[magic|art]] (003-353–400). The structure of this opposition — [[prospero|Prospero]]'s repeated insistence that his magic is different in kind, not merely degree — betrays an anxiety that the distinction may not hold. Sycorax is [[prospero|Prospero]]'s dark double: the figure who embodies everything he must not be, and who therefore enables everything he claims to be.

- **[[caliban|Caliban]] and [[stephano|Stephano]]**: In Act 3, Scene 2, [[caliban|Caliban]] invokes Sycorax in praise of [[miranda|Miranda]], asserting that "she as far surpasseth Sycorax / As great'st does least" (009-068–069). The comparison, offered as compliment, inadvertently reveals the depth of [[caliban|Caliban]]'s attachment to his mother's memory: even in praising another woman, he measures her against Sycorax.

## Key Speeches

Sycorax never speaks a single line — she is dead before the play begins. Her presence is entirely ventriloquised: every word we have about her is filtered through [[prospero|Prospero]], the man who supplanted her. This silence is itself the most significant fact about her character, and critical readings attentive to gender and empire have made it central to their interpretations.

The speech in which [[prospero|Prospero]] narrates Sycorax to [[ariel|Ariel]] (003-353–400) is the play's longest passage about any offstage character. [[prospero|Prospero]] recounts her banishment from [[argier|Algiers]], her pregnancy, her arrival on the island, her imprisonment of [[ariel|Ariel]], and her death — a complete biography delivered by the man who profited from her absence. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s language throughout is vehement, repetitive, and insistent: "foul witch," "damned witch," "the damn'd witch Sycorax" (003-353, 003-362). The insistent repetition of "witch" — three times in fewer than fifty lines — performs the rhetorical labour of fixing Sycorax's identity before [[ariel|Ariel]] (or the audience) can question it. That [[prospero|Prospero]], the island's current [[master|master]], should be so energetically invested in discrediting his dead predecessor invites suspicion rather than assent.

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s counter-speech — "This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother" (003-455) — is briefer but no less significant. Where [[prospero|Prospero]]'s discourse constructs Sycorax as a warrant for his own [[authority|rule]], [[caliban|Caliban]] invokes her as a warrant for inheritance. The two uses of Sycorax are symmetrical and irreconcilable: for [[prospero|Prospero]], she is [[caliban|the monster]] who justifies occupation; for [[caliban|Caliban]], she is the sovereign who authorises resistance. The play never adjudicates between these accounts, but their coexistence makes Sycorax the absent centre around which the island's contested [[authority|sovereignty]] turns.

## Critical Reception

**Ania Loomba** reads Sycorax through the lens of postcolonial and feminist theory. In *Gender, Race, Renaissance Drama* (1989), Loomba argues that Sycorax is the racialised and gendered Other whose silencing is constitutive of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[power|authority]]. For Loomba, Sycorax's absence from the stage is not incidental but structural: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|rule]] over the island requires that the prior female sovereign be dead, demonised, and unrepresented. Sycorax embodies the intersection of racial and sexual difference that early modern colonial discourse deployed to naturalise European domination — she is [[afric|Africa]]n, female, and figured as sexually transgressive (her pregnancy outside marriage, her coupling with a demon). Loomba's reading insists that Sycorax is not merely a plot device but the repressed foundation of the play's colonial logic: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s civility depends upon her constructed savagery.

**Stephen Greenblatt**, in *Shakespearean Negotiations* (1988) and subsequent essays, identifies Sycorax as [[prospero|Prospero]]'s dark double — the figure whose demonised [[magic|sorcery]] allows [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "[[magic|art]]" to present itself as legitimate and civilised. Greenblatt situates this doubling within the broader early modern discourse of witchcraft and [[art|learned magic]], arguing that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s anxious insistence on the difference between his books and her charms reveals the instability of the distinction. For Greenblatt, Sycorax is a figure of the "marvellous" that Renaissance culture needed to contain: her power, like [[prospero|Prospero]]'s, derives from control over [[nature|nature]], but where [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[magic|art]] is channelled through text and reason, Sycorax's [[magic|sorcery]] is figured as bodily, intuitive, and feminine. The opposition serves to legitimise masculine, European rationality at the expense of its feminine, [[afric|Africa]]n other.

**Marina Warner**, in her extensive work on witches and female power in early modern culture — p[[magic|art]]icularly *From the Beast to the Blonde* (1994) and *Phantasmagoria* (2006) — situates Sycorax within the long cultural tradition of the witch as a figure of female autonomy and threat. Warner notes that the early modern witch, like Sycorax, is typically defined by what she is not: not Christian, not married, not subordinated to male [[power|authority]]. Sycorax's power is fundamentally maternal and generative — she gives birth to [[caliban|Caliban]] on the island, and her [[magic|sorcery]] is described in terms of natural force rather than book [[knowledge|learning]]. For Warner, this association of female power with [[nature|nature]] and body (as against male culture and intellect) is a recurring pattern in patriarchal mythology, and Sycorax exemplifies it with p[[magic|art]]icular force: her very name, possibly derived from the Greek *sys* (sow) and *korax* (raven), associates her with animality.

**Frank Kermode**, in his Arden edition of *The Tempest* (1954, revised), provides the foundational textual analysis of Sycorax's function. Kermode notes that Sycorax is a dramatic necessity: [[prospero|Prospero]] requires a predecessor against whom his own [[authority|rule]] can be measured and found superior. Her function is to supply the backstory that makes [[prospero|Prospero]]'s liberation of [[ariel|Ariel]] meaningful and his enslavement of [[caliban|Caliban]] defensible. Kermode observes that Shakespeare draws on multiple sources — classical accounts of Circe and Medea, travel narratives of [[afric|Africa]]n [[magic|sorcery]], contemporary witch-lore — to construct a composite figure whose very hybridity reinforces her status as alien. For Kermode, Sycorax is the most important character never to appear in Shakespeare: the shadow whose absence gives [[prospero|Prospero]]'s presence its moral shape.
