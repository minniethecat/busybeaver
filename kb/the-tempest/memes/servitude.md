## Overview

Servitude is the prevailing social relation on [[prospero|Prospero]]'s island. [[ariel|Ariel]] is bound to [[prospero|Prospero]] by debt and promise; [[caliban|Caliban]] is enslaved by conquest; [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] accepts voluntary servitude as proof of love; and [[stephano|Stephano]] and [[trinculo|Trinculo]] imagine themselves [[master|master]]s of a kingdom won through drink. Each variant of servitude illuminates a different dimension of the [[master|master]]-servant bond: gratitude and resentment, coercion and consent, loyalty and rebellion. The play's resolution requires that every bond of servitude be acknowledged and either honoured or dissolved.

## In the Play

[[ariel|Ariel]]'s servitude is contractual and temporary — a debt incurred when [[prospero|Prospero]] freed him from [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s pine tree. "Once in a month recount what thou hast been," [[prospero|Prospero]] demands (003-361), keeping the terms of [[ariel|Ariel]]'s service always present. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s obedience is punctuated by reminders of the promise: "Is there more toil? … my [[freedom|liberty]]" (003-325). The relationship is closer to indentured labour than slavery, but its coercive edge is unmistakable — [[prospero|Prospero]] threatens to "rend an oak / And peg thee in his knotty entrails" (003-397–003-398) for disobedience, and reminds [[ariel|Ariel]] that he could be re-imprisoned for another "twelve winters" (003-399). The threat hangs over every ex[[transformation|change]] between them, transforming [[ariel|Ariel]]'s obedience from gratitude freely given into a calculation of survival. [[ariel|Ariel]] performs his tasks brilliantly — [[the-shipwreck|the storm]], the [[sleep|sleep]]-spell, [[ariel|the harpy]] spectacle — but always with an eye on the calendar. His freedom is not a gift but a contract with an expiry date that [[prospero|Prospero]] alone controls.

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s servitude is presented as conquest. [[prospero|Prospero]] and [[miranda|Miranda]] claim to have educated him — "I pitied thee, / Took pains to make thee speak" (003-481–003-482) — but [[caliban|Caliban]]'s retort redefines their relationship as colonial exploitation: "You taught me language; and my profit on't / Is, I know how to curse" (003-491). His attempted rape of [[miranda|Miranda]] is cited as the justification for his enslavement, but he insists the island was stolen: "this island's mine, by [[sycorax|Sycorax]] my mother" (009-029). Unlike [[ariel|Ariel]], [[caliban|Caliban]] is offered no promise of freedom; his servitude is permanent, enforced not by contract but by the threat of cramps, pinches, and the magic that keeps him confined to a "hard rock." His resistance is correspondingly absolute: where [[ariel|Ariel]] negotiates, [[caliban|Caliban]] curses. Where [[ariel|Ariel]] counts the days, [[caliban|Caliban]] dreams of murder.

[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s servitude is voluntary and romantic. He accepts log-bearing as a trial of worth: "This my mean task / Would be as heavy to me as odious, but / The mistress which I serve quickens what's dead" (008-005). His willing submission transforms the meaning of service — "with a he[[magic|art]] as willing / As bondage e'er of freedom" (008-124–125). [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s labour is real — he hauls "thousands of these logs" (008-011) — but unlike [[caliban|Caliban]]'s, it is freely chosen and temporally bounded. His servitude is a courtship ritual disguised as punishment; the log-pile is not a prison but a proving ground.

[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s utopian commonwealth speech offers the play's most radical counterpoint to the island's reality of servitude. In his imagined "plantation of this [[island|isle]]," he would "by contraries / Execute all things" — no trade, no magistrates, no [[knowledge|learning]], no weapons, and crucially "riches, poverty, / And use of service, none" (005-139–005-150). The [[dream|vision]] imagines a society without hierarchy or labour, where [[nature|nature]] provides all and every person is idle, innocent, and equal. [[sebastian|Sebastian]] and [[antonio|Antonio]] mock it mercilessly — "The latter end of his commonwealth forgets the beginning" (005-151) — but the speech functions as a philosophical benchmark against which every [[master|master]]-servant relationship on the island can be measured. [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s dream of a world without service is precisely what [[prospero|Prospero]]'s island conspicuously is not, and the very fact that the speech draws on Montaigne's essay "Of Cannibals" links it to the play's colonial subtext: the European fantasy of a prelapsarian new world is also a fantasy of labour without [[master|master]]s.

Servitude on the island has its own internal hierarchies. [[ariel|Ariel]] stands above [[caliban|Caliban]] in [[prospero|Prospero]]'s estimation — an airy spirit versus an e[[magic|art]]hy "tortoise" (003-434), a willing servant promised [[freedom|liberty]] versus a resentful one condemned to permanent enslavement. The two servants never interact directly on stage; their relationship to each other is defined entirely by their differential proximity to the [[master|master]]. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s tasks involve [[music|music]], spectacle, and the control of others; [[caliban|Caliban]]'s involve fetching firewood and scrubbing the cave. The hierarchy is not incidental — it is the mechanism by which [[prospero|Prospero]] maintains control, ensuring that no collective servant solidarity can form. In the comic subplot, this ladder of servitude is reproduced as farce. [[stephano|Stephano]], a butler in [[court-of-naples|the Neapolitan court]], declares himself king of the island and immediately establishes his own chain of [[authority|command]]: [[caliban|Caliban]] offers to serve him — "I'll kiss thy foot; I'll swear myself thy subject" (006-070) — and [[trinculo|Trinculo]], a jester, is subordinated beneath [[caliban|Caliban]]: "thou liest, most ignorant monster: I am in case to justle a constable" (009-012). The drunken rebellion thus replicates the very structure of [[master|master]]y and servitude it pretends to overthrow. Servitude, the play suggests, is not merely a relationship between [[master|master]] and servant but a ladder on which every figure occupies a rung — each above someone and below someone else — and even the fantasy of revolution imagines not the abolition of [[master|master]]y but merely a [[transformation|change]] of personnel.

## Key Passages

> As bondage e'er of freedom: here's my hand.
> — (008-125)

[[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] transforms servitude from compulsion into gift. The paradox — bondage as the form freedom takes when freely chosen — is the play's most hopeful [[dream|vision]] of service. The line is offered as a vow, and the gesture of the hand seals it: willing servitude, for [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]], is indistinguishable from love.

> You taught me language; and my profit on't
> Is, I know how to curse. The red plague rid you
> For learning me your language!
> — (003-491)

[[caliban|Caliban]]'s reply to the civilising mission. Language, the gift [[prospero|Prospero]] claims as the basis of [[master|master]]y, becomes [[caliban|Caliban]]'s weapon against his [[master|master]]. The word "profit" is the bitterest irony in the play: the only return on [[prospero|Prospero]]'s pedagogical investment is a curse. The passage has become one of the most quoted in postcolonial criticism, a concise dramatisation of the colonised subject's refusal to be grateful for the tools of his own subjugation.

> If thou more murmur'st, I will rend an oak,
> And peg thee in his knotty entrails, till
> Thou hast howl'd away twelve winters.
> — (003-397–003-399)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s threat to [[ariel|Ariel]] reveals the violence that undergirds even the most "benevolent" [[master|master]]-servant relationship. The image is visceral — being pegged inside the living tissue of a tree, howling for twelve years — and it is a precise reminder of what [[ariel|Ariel]] was rescued from. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s threat reframes his own liberation of [[ariel|Ariel]] as conditional: freedom was given, and freedom can be revoked. The threat of renewed bondage shadows every subsequent moment of [[ariel|Ariel]]'s service and transforms "I thank thee, [[master|master]]" from gratitude into compulsion.

> I' the commonwealth I would by contraries
> Execute all things: for no kind of traffic
> Would I admit; no name of magistrate;
> Letters should not be known; riches, poverty,
> And use of service, none …
> No sovereignty; —
> — (005-139–005-161)

[[gonzalo|Gonzalo]]'s utopian [[dream|vision]] imagines a world without the categories that enable servitude — no property ("riches, poverty"), no contract ("use of service"), no political [[power|authority]] ("[[authority|sovereignty]]"). The speech draws on Montaigne's essay "Of Cannibals" and stands as the play's only sustained imagining of human society without [[master|master]]y. That [[sebastian|Sebastian]] and [[antonio|Antonio]] mock it does not refute it; their cynicism indicts them, not the ideal. The broken-off sentence — "No [[authority|sovereignty]]; —" — marks the limit of the utopian imagination: even in fantasy, political power is easier to abolish than to replace.

> But yet thou shalt have freedom: so, so, so.
> — (014-109)

[[prospero|Prospero]] [[freedom|release]]s [[ariel|Ariel]]. The repeated "so" — casual, almost dismissive — contrasts with the twelve years of servitude it concludes. [[freedom|Freedom]] is both a gift and a demonstration of the [[master|master]]'s continuing power to grant or withhold. The line is not a celebration but a [[freedom|release]], and its offhand quality captures the asymmetry at the he[[magic|art]] of the [[master|master]]-servant relation: what for [[ariel|Ariel]] is the consummation of years of longing is for [[prospero|Prospero]] merely the final item on a checklist.

## Critical Context

Modern criticism has read the play's representations of servitude through the lenses of colonialism, political theory, and early modern social relations. Paul Brown, in his influential essay "'This thing of darkness I acknowledge mine': The Tempest and the Discourse of Colonialism" (1985), argues that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[master|master]]y over [[caliban|Caliban]] and [[ariel|Ariel]] enacts a specifically colonial mode of power. The European [[master|master]] "civilises" the native, extracting labour and offering language in return, while reserving the right to define the native as ungrateful when that "gift" is rejected. For Brown, [[caliban|Caliban]]'s servitude is not an aberration but the logical endpoint of a discourse of othering that constructs the colonised subject as a "thing of darkness" — [[prospero|Prospero]]'s own phrase, spoken of [[caliban|Caliban]] — requiring external governance. The play's language of monstrosity, deformity, and darkness, Brown contends, provides the ideological justification for servitude even as it registers, in [[caliban|Caliban]]'s eloquence, the resistance that colonial discourse cannot fully contain.

Ania Loomba extends this postcolonial reading by attending to the gendered and racialised dimensions of [[caliban|Caliban]]'s servitude. In *Gender, Race, Renaissance Drama* (1989), Loomba argues that [[caliban|Caliban]]'s attempted rape of [[miranda|Miranda]] is central to the play's justification of his enslavement: the threat of miscegenation transforms [[caliban|Caliban]] from native inhabitant into an irredeemable "born devil" whose labour can be extracted without moral complication. Servitude, in Loomba's reading, is not merely an economic relation but a mechanism for managing the anxieties — sexual, racial, political — that the colonial encounter provokes. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s obsessive control over [[miranda|Miranda]]'s body and [[caliban|Caliban]]'s labour are two faces of the same colonial project: the regulation of reproduction and the extraction of work.

Frank Kermode, in his landmark Arden edition of *The Tempest* (1954), identifies the tension between service and freedom as the play's central organising conflict. For Kermode, every character is defined by their relationship to some form of service or [[master|master]]y: [[ariel|Ariel]] seeks freedom from it, [[caliban|Caliban]] contests its [[authority|legitimacy]], [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] embraces it as a discipline of love, and [[prospero|Prospero]] ultimately discovers that his own [[master|master]]y is itself a form of bondage. When [[prospero|Prospero]] says of [[caliban|Caliban]], "this thing of darkness I acknowledge mine," Kermode reads the line as a recognition that the [[master|master]] is bound to the servant as surely as the servant is bound to the [[master|master]] — a mutual entanglement that only the renunciation of magic and the return to [[milan|Milan]] can dissolve. Stephen Greenblatt, in *Shakespearean Negotiations* (1988), situates [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[master|master]]y within Renaissance theories of [[authority|sovereignty]], arguing that [[master|master]]y and servitude in the play function as "political forms" — not merely personal relationships but models of governance. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s [[authority|rule]] over the island is a microcosm of early modern statecraft: the magus-prince who deploys knowledge, spectacle, and coercion to extract obedience from spirits, natives, and aristocrats alike. Servitude, in Greenblatt's analysis, is the political relation that makes the state visible: the [[master|master]]'s [[power|authority]] is constituted by the servant's submission, and the servant's resistance reveals the contingency of every claim to [[master|master]]y.
