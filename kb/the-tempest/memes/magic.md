## Overview

Magic is the central supernatural force in *The Tempest*, embodied in [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "art" — a learned, book-derived power that distinguishes him from the malefic sorcery of [[sycorax|Sycorax]]. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic governs every major event in the play: the opening storm, the separation and testing of the [[storm|shipwreck]]ed parties, [[the-masque|the pageant]] of spirits, and ultimately [[the-reconciliation|the resolution]] in Act 5. Unlike the crude witchcraft of [[sycorax|Sycorax]], [[prospero|Prospero]]'s art is portrayed as rational, scholarly, and ultimately renounceable — a tool of justice rather than malice.

The meta-theatrical resonance of magic is one of the play's most profound dimensions. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s art — the ability to summon storms, stage pageants, [[authority|command]] spirits, and dissolve illusions at will — mirrors the playwright's own craft. When [[prospero|Prospero]] declares that his actors "were all spirits, and / Are melted into air, into thin air" (012-185–186), the audience is reminded that the same is true of the actors on Shakespeare's stage. The magician and the dramatist both conjure worlds from words, both depend on the willing suspension of disbelief, and both must ultimately acknowledge the insubstantiality of their creations. This parallel makes *The Tempest*, perhaps more than any other Shakespeare play, a sustained meditation on the nature of art itself — what it means to make, to believe, and to let go. Frank Kermode, in his landmark Arden edition, identified this fusion of magic and meta-theatre as the play's governing conceit: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "art" is simultaneously the Renaissance magus's learned discipline and the dramatist's representational craft, and the abjuration of one is also, famously, read as Shakespeare's own farewell to the stage.

## In the Play

Magic first manifests through [[the-shipwreck|the tempest]] itself, conjured by [[prospero|Prospero]] with [[ariel|Ariel]]'s aid to bring his enemies to the [[island|island]]. In (003-002), [[miranda|Miranda]] entreats her father: "If by your art, my dearest father, you have / Put the wild waters in this roar, allay them." This establishes magic as [[prospero|Prospero]]'s defining power and the engine of the plot.

The nature of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic is clarified through his backstory. He was once [[prospero|the Duke of Milan]], but his absorption in "the [[art|liberal arts]]" (003-103) and "[[art|secret studies]]" (003-107) allowed his brother [[antonio|Antonio]] to usurp him. His library — "Me, poor man, my library / Was dukedom large enough" (003-145) — was both the source of his power and the cause of his political downfall.

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic is contrasted with [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s throughout. She was "a witch" banished from [[argier|Argier]] "for mischiefs manifold, and sorceries terrible" (003-363), and [[caliban|Caliban]] calls [[prospero|Prospero]] "a sorcerer, that by his cunning hath cheated me of the [[island|island]]" (009-020). The distinction between [[prospero|Prospero]]'s learned art and [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s malefic sorcery is central to the play's moral framework. Crucially, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic is textual — derived from books, mediated through [[knowledge|study]] — while [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s is innate and bodily: she imprisoned [[ariel|Ariel]] in a "cloven pine" by direct force, without intermediary or incantation. This distinction maps onto Renaissance debates about *magia naturalis* (natural magic, a legitimate branch of natural philosophy) versus demonic witchcraft. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s art operates through the learned [[authority|command]] of spirits who exercise their own agency — [[ariel|Ariel]] is bound but not coerced — whereas [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s sorcery was a raw imposition of will. The moral architecture of the play rests on this difference: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic can be renounced and redeemed because it was never essentially evil; [[sycorax|Sycorax]]'s, incapable of renunciation, is simply left behind in the [[island|island]]'s dark past.

The geographical dimension of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic is essential to understanding its trajectory. His art was incubated in [[milan|Milan]] — "transported hither" to the [[island|island]] through the volumes [[gonzalo|Gonzalo]] furnished from his library, "knowing I loved my books" (003-222–223) — but it only reaches full expression in the [[island|island]]'s charged atmosphere. [[milan|Milan]] was the site of [[knowledge|study]] and political failure; the [[island|island]] is the site of power and moral trial. On the [[island|island]], [[prospero|Prospero]]'s book-[[knowledge|learning]] becomes operational: he can [[authority|command]] the elements, paralyze enemies, summon a banquet, stage a marriage masque, and compel confession. This geographical displacement — from the Renaissance city-state to the enchanted periphery — mirrors the play's larger rhythm of exile and return. Magic belongs to the [[island|island]], not to [[milan|Milan]], and [[prospero|Prospero]]'s return to civilization at the play's end is contingent on leaving his art behind in the sea that separates the two worlds.

[[music|Music]] is the principal medium through which magic operates on the senses in *The Tempest*. [[ariel|Ariel]]'s agency is expressed almost entirely through [[music|song]]: "Come unto these yellow sands" (003-511) lures [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]] from the shore toward [[miranda|Miranda]], and "Full fathom five" (003-534) transmutes the false news of [[alonso|Alonso]]'s death into the play's most haunting image of transformation — "something rich and strange." [[ferdinand|Ferdinand]]'s response captures the uncanny quality of magical music: "Where should this music be? i' th' air or th' earth? / … This music crept by me upon the waters" (003-524, 003-528). The sourcelessness of the sound — a voice without a body, creeping rather than announcing — is itself a figure for the way magic permeates the [[island|island]]. In the banquet scene of Act 3, "solemn and strange music" (010-001–010-115) accompanies the phantom feast before [[ariel|Ariel]]'s [[harpy|harpy]]-accusation of the "three men of sin"; the music disarms the court party, making them receptive to judgment. The marriage masque of Act 4 is a fully musical pageant, with [[music|song]]s by [[juno|Juno]] and [[ceres|Ceres]]. And [[ariel|Ariel]]'s final [[music|song]] — "Where the bee sucks, there suck I" (014-101) — imagines [[freedom|freedom]] itself as [[music|melody]]: a life of pure, unburdened singing among the flowers. Across the play, music is the sensory signature of the supernatural, the mode through which magic enters the ear before it [[authority|command]]s belief.

The pageant in Act 4 ("Our revels now are ended" (012-184)) represents the peak of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magical display, followed immediately by his reminder that these [[dream|vision]]s "were all spirits, and / Are melted into air, into thin air" (012-185–186) — a meta-theatrical moment that links magic to the art of theatre itself.

The culmination of the magic theme occurs in Act 5, when [[prospero|Prospero]] enters "in his magic robes" (014-001) and delivers his abjuration: "But this rough magic / I here abjure" (014-061–062). He vows to "break my staff" (014-065) and "drown my book" (014-068), renouncing the art that has defined him. His farewell to magic — addressing "ye elves of hills, brooks, standing lakes, and groves" (014-044) — is one of Shakespeare's most powerful invocations, recounting feats from dimming the sun to [[sleep|waking]] the dead (014-052–060).

## Key Passages

> Ye elves of hills, brooks, standing lakes, and groves;
> And ye that on the sands with printless foot
> Do chase the ebbing Neptune … by whose aid —
> Weak masters though ye be — I have bedimm'd
> The noontide sun, call'd forth the mutinous winds,
> And 'twixt the green sea and the azured vault
> Set roaring war …
> graves at my command
> Have waked their sleepers, oped, and let 'em forth
> By my so potent art.
> — (014-044–061)

[[prospero|Prospero]]'s farewell to his art. He invokes [[ariel|the spirit]]s of nature as his "weak masters" — the magic is theirs, channelled through his [[authority|command]]. The catalogue of cosmic feats (eclipsing the sun, raising storms, shaking mountains, opening graves) establishes the scale of his power at the very moment he chooses to surrender it.

> But this rough magic
> I here abjure; and, when I have required
> Some heavenly music …
> I'll break my staff,
> Bury it certain fathoms in the earth,
> And deeper than did ever plummet sound
> I'll drown my book.
> — (014-061–068)

The abjuration proper. "Rough magic" acknowledges the limitation of his art — it can [[authority|command]] spirits and nature, but cannot achieve the "heavenly music" of grace and [[forgiveness|forgiveness]] without renunciation. The burial of the staff and drowning of the book are symbolic acts: magic must be returned to the depths from which it came before [[prospero|Prospero]] can re-enter the human world as the restored Duke of [[milan|Milan]].

> Our revels now are ended. These our actors,
> As I foretold you, were all spirits, and
> Are melted into air, into thin air:
> And, like the baseless fabric of this vision,
> The cloud-capp'd towers, the gorgeous palaces,
> The solemn temples, the great globe itself,
> Yea, all which it inherit, shall dissolve …
> — (012-184–189)

The meta-theatrical heart of the play. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s description of his magical pageant dissolving "into thin air" doubles as Shakespeare's meditation on the ephemerality of theatre — and of life itself. Magic here is a figure for art: both conjure worlds that vanish when the performance ends.

## Critical Context

The critical tradition on magic in *The Tempest* is rich and contested, shaped by shifting understandings of Renaissance occultism, colonial power, and theatrical self-consciousness. Two scholars in particular have defined the terms of the debate.

**Frank Kermode**, whose Arden edition of *The Tempest* (1954) remains a touchstone, argued that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic must be understood within the Renaissance tradition of "white" or natural magic — the *magia naturalis* articulated by Marsilio Ficino, Pico della Mirandola, and later by Cornelius Agrippa. In this framework, the learned magus does not [[authority|command]] demons but exercises a purified understanding of nature's hidden sympathies, a discipline as much intellectual as operational. Kermode reads [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "art" as fundamentally Neoplatonic: the magus ascends through knowledge toward divine understanding, and his renunciation of magic in Act 5 is not a repudiation of evil but a graduation — the student completing his curriculum and outgrowing his instruments. This reading established the influential view that [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic is morally coherent within its own terms, a system the play both celebrates and ultimately transcends.

**Stephen Greenblatt**, writing in the context of new historicism (*Renaissance Self-Fashioning*, 1980), shifted the emphasis from philosophical coherence to the social and theatrical functions of magical power. For Greenblatt, [[prospero|Prospero]]'s magic is an instance of Renaissance self-fashioning: the magus constructs his identity through the exercise of art, and that construction is inseparable from colonial domination (of [[caliban|Caliban]]) and theatrical manipulation (of the court party). Magic, in this reading, is not an escape from politics but the most intense form of it — a technology of control that produces both public spectacles and private subjections. The meta-theatrical dimension of [[prospero|Prospero]]'s art — the fact that his magic *looks like theatre* — becomes, in Greenblatt's analysis, the play's most radical insight: power works by staging belief, and the audience (both on-stage and in the theatre) are complicit in the enchantments they witness. [[prospero|Prospero]]'s abjuration, far from being a simple farewell, is a strategic transfer of power from magical to political [[power|authority]], from the [[island|island]] back to [[milan|Milan]].

These two readings — Kermode's philosophically-grounded magus and Greenblatt's politically-embedded manipulator — have structured much subsequent criticism. Barbara Mowat, in "[[prospero|Prospero]]'s Book" (*Shakespeare Quarterly*, 2001), proposed a mediating view: [[prospero|Prospero]]'s art is irreducibly textual, grounded in the material book that he prizes "above his dukedom," and the play's treatment of magic is therefore a treatment of reading itself — what it means to absorb, wield, and ultimately close a book. David Lindley, in his work on music and magic in the play, has shown how the pervasive presence of [[ariel|Ariel]]'s [[music|song]]s, the masque music, and [[prospero|Prospero]]'s "heavenly music" ties the magical to the auditory, suggesting that the play's deepest enchantments enter not through the eye but the ear. Together, these critical perspectives affirm that magic in *The Tempest* is not a mere plot device but the play's central intellectual problem — the site where questions of knowledge, power, art, and renunciation converge.
