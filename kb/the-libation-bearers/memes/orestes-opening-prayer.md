# Orestes' Opening Prayer

The opening lines of *The Libation Bearers* (Χοηφόροι), spoken by Orestes at the grave mound of his father Agamemnon. Accompanied by his companion Pylades, Orestes invokes Hermes as psychopomp and guardian of the dead, lays a lock of his hair as a funeral offering, and declares his return from exile.

## Text

> O Warder Hermês of the world beneath,
> Son of the Father who is Lord of Death;
> Saviour, be thou my saviour; Help in War,
> Help me! I am returned from lands afar
> To claim mine own. And on this headland steep
> Of death, I call my Father o'er the deep
> To hearken, to give ear. — Behold, I bring
> Out of my poverty one little thing,
> To adorn thy grave, though who can touch the dead
> Or wake from sleep that unuplifted head?
> Yet long ago in Phôkis, where I lay
> With Strophius in the hills, being cast away
> In childhood, plundered by mine enemies,
> And friendless, save for this man, Pylades,
> I sware an oath which should for ever set
> In memory those they taught me to forget:
> If once I came to manhood, so I sware,
> In tresses twain I would divide mine hair,
> One tress for Inachos river, by whose grace
> I live, and one for mourning at this place.
> Which oath I here fulfil.
> (PN 001-021)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Orestes]], son of Agamemnon and Clytemnestra
- **Addressee**: Hermes Chthonios (Hermes of the Underworld)
- **Dramatic moment**: The prologue of the play. Orestes and Pylades, in the garb of travellers, stand at Agamemnon's grave mound in a desolate expanse. Orestes has returned from exile in Phokis. His hair is cut short in mourning.
- **Form**: rhessis (extended speech)

## Analysis

The prayer opens the play with three interlocking divine invocations that establish the religious framework of the tragedy. First, Orestes calls on Hermes as "Warder of the world beneath," invoking the god's role as psychopomp who escorts souls between the living and the dead (PN 001-003). This positions the entire action under divine sanction — the vengeance to come is not merely human retribution but a sacred duty.

Second, Orestes appeals to his dead father, calling across "the deep" that separates the living from the dead. The image of the grave mound as a "headland steep of death" (PN 005-006) transforms the tomb into a liminal space where communication with the dead becomes possible. Third, the lock of hair — a traditional Greek mourning offering — becomes the physical token of Orestes' identity and his claim to his father's legacy.

The speech also establishes the play's geography of exile and return. Orestes names Phokis, the Inachos river, and his childhood exile with Strophius (PN 010-018) — details that ground the mythological action in specific locations of the Greek world. The divided hair — one tress for Inachos, one for the dead — symbolizes the divided self of the exile who must reclaim his place through the very act of mourning he was denied at his father's death.

## In Performance

The opening tableau — Orestes with shorn hair beside a grave mound — communicates "half the story in a flash" (Murray). The visual contrast between Orestes' cropped mourning hair and Pylades' long flowing locks immediately sets the tone of grief and purpose. The physical act of laying the hair on the grave mound gives the prayer a ritual dimension that a spoken prayer alone would lack. When Orestes suddenly breaks off upon seeing the approaching Chorus of Libation-Bearers, the shift from solitary prayer to public ritual marks the play's transition from prologue to dramatic action.
