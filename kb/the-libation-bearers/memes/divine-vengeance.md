# Divine Vengeance

In [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* (The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers), divine vengeance — the blood-for-blood [[dike|justice]] administered under the gods' authority — is the central moral engine of the drama, governing [[orestes|Orestes]]' killing of his mother [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and her lover [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]].

## Definition

Divine vengeance (*Dikē*) in the world of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* is the principle by which the gods, particularly Zeus and the chthonic powers, demand that spilled kindred blood be repaid in kind. It is not personal revenge but a sacred obligation — "the slayer shall meet the slayer, wrong smite with wrong" (001-002). The Erinyes (Furies) are its supernatural agents, pursuing those who shed familial blood, while Apollo's oracle at Delphi commands [[orestes|Orestes]] to carry out the killing (001-002).

## Greek Religious Context

In fifth-century BCE Athenian religious thought, the blood-feud was an archaic institution being gradually replaced by the law-court. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] dramatizes this transition across the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]. The commutation of the blood-feud for a money payment had "of course, a softening of primitive manners" and "is elaborately provided for in various codes of early law" (002-038). But in the *Choephoroe*, [[orestes|Orestes]] cannot pay his way out of the obligation — he must kill or be killed, as Apollo's oracle threatens that disobedience means madness, [[exile-and-return|exile]], and the Furies' pursuit (001-002).

## Role in The Libation Bearers

The play is structured around the working-out of divine vengeance. The dead [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] is summoned by the [[libation-ritual|libations]] and the great *[[kommos]]* (lamentation) at his tomb to awaken and aid his children's vengeance (P01-004). The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] invokes this principle explicitly: "For tongue of hate be tongue of hate made perfect... For the blow of death the blow of death atone" (001-002). The dream of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] — giving birth to a serpent that draws blood from her breast — is interpreted by [[orestes|Orestes]] as divine confirmation that he is the instrument of vengeance (001-002).

[[orestes|Orestes]] himself frames the act as multi-causal: "The God's command; grief for my father slain; And mine own beggary urgeth me amain" (001-002). Divine vengeance operates through human motives but ultimately answers to a higher law.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The *Choephoroe* represents the middle term of the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s arc from vengeance to law:

- In the **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]**, [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] kills her husband, claiming she acts as the agent of [[justice-and-vengeance|divine justice]] for [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s sacrifice of Iphigenia — vengeance begetting vengeance.
- In the **Choephoroe**, [[orestes|Orestes]] executes the gods' command to avenge his father, but the act is "a sin; a sin to be expiated by unfathomable suffering. Yet, since the god cannot have commanded evil, it is a duty also. It is a sin that must be committed" (P01-002).
- In the **Eumenides**, the cycle is broken by Athena's establishment of the Areopagus court, transforming the Erinyes from Furies to "Beneficent Beings, from 'Erinyes' to 'Eumenides'" (P01-001).
