# The Kommos

The "marvellous invocation scene" at the play's heart — a ritual lamentation at Agamemnon's tomb that is simultaneously an act of mourning, a summons to the dead, and a war council for the living.

## Structure and Function

The kommos (Greek: κομμός) is a formal lamentation combining sung lyrics and spoken dialogue, performed at the tomb of the dead. In *The Libation Bearers*, it is the longest sustained passage in the play and occupies a central position structurally and emotionally.

The Preface describes it as "the marvellous invocation scene itself" — a passage of unique power in Greek drama.

## Dramatic Arc

The kommos traces an emotional arc from passive grief to active resolve. It begins with individual laments — Orestes' prayer, Electra's grief, the Chorus's songs — and builds toward the united cry in which all three voices join. By its end, Orestes has been transformed from a mourning son into an agent of Apollo's command.

## Ritual Function

As ritual, the kommos performs a specific function: it calls the dead king's spirit to witness and aid the living. The tomb is not merely a setting but a participant — Agamemnon's spirit is summoned, and the act of lamentation makes him present in a way that transforms the living mourners.
