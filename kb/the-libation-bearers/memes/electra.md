# Electra

Electra, daughter of Agamemnon and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], is the emotional centre of the first half of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* — a woman reduced to slavery in her own house, who transforms her mother's appeasement offerings into a summons for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] and whose [[recognition-scene|recognition]] of her [[exile-and-return|exile]]d brother galvanises the play's central action.

## Identity

- **Play**: The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers (Choephoroe), second play of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* (458 BCE)
- **Family**: Daughter of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]; sister of [[orestes|Orestes]]; granddaughter of Atreus
- **Status**: Mortal — living as a virtual slave in the usurped [[house-of-atreus|palace of Argos]]
- **First appearance**: Entering with the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of [[the-chorus|Libation-Bearers]] to pour offerings at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb (001-002)

## Role in the Play

Electra is the play's initiator — she transforms [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s attempt at appeasement into an act of [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. When confronted with the question of what prayer to speak at the tomb, she hesitates: "What speech have I for utterance, when I sue / With offerings to the dead? What word of love, / What prayer to reach my father from above?" (001-002). The Leader guides her through the dilemma, and Electra's prayer becomes an explicit call for her brother's [[exile-and-return|return]] and her mother's death: "Guide thou [[orestes|Orestes]] homeward... And, oh, give me a heart to understand / More than my mother, and a cleaner hand!" (001-002).

[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' Electra is distinguished from her Sophoclean and Euripidean counterparts by her integration into the religious framework of the play. "In [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], amid all the strained emotions of the scene, there is a certain high courtesy between Electra and the Leader" (P01-007). "Of the other characters, Electra in [[euripides|Euripides]] bears the main burden of the tragedy; she has a kind of tenderness even in her prayers of hate. She hates her oppressors fiercely, but she hates evil" (P01-007). [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' Electra is less psychologically individuated but more firmly embedded in the ritual structure of the *[[kommos]]*.

## Key Scenes

### The Prayer at the Tomb
Electra's dilemma — whether it is impious to use her mother's offerings to pray for her mother's death — is the play's first moral crisis. The Leader's counsel is pragmatic: "How else? With hate thine hater to repay" (001-002). Electra mounts the grave mound and pours the [[libation-ritual|libations]], explicitly calling upon her dead father: "O Father, pity me; pity thine own / [[orestes|Orestes]], and restore us to thy throne" (001-002). She also prays for the death of her enemies: "The slayer in just recompense be slain" (001-002).

### The Recognition
Electra discovers the lock of hair, the [[recognition-and-tokens|footprints]], and finally [[orestes|Orestes]] himself. Her response is ecstatic — "O best beloved, O dreamed of long ago, / Seed of [[deliverance-and-salvation|deliverance]] washed with tears as rain" (001-002) — and she enumerates the four roles [[orestes|Orestes]] fills in her heart: father, mother (whom she hates), sister (whom she mourns), and faithful brother.

### The Great Kommos
In the extended lamentation at the tomb, Electra takes the lead in recounting the dishonours done to their father: "Ho, Mother! Ho, thou, Mother, / Mine enemy, daring all! / What burial made ye here? / His people followed not, / Mourned him not, knew him not: / Enemies bare his pall: / His wife shed no tear!" (001-002). After the *[[kommos]]*, Electra withdraws into the palace and does not reappear — her dramatic function is complete once the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] plot is set in motion.

## Dramatic Function

Electra serves as the bridge between the domestic world of the palace (from which she emerges as a slave) and the chthonic world of the tomb (where she summons the dead). She is the keeper of memory — she remembers the dishonours inflicted on [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s body, the details of his murder, and the family's fallen state — and her memory provides the emotional fuel for the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] that follows. Her disappearance from the play after the *[[kommos]]* reflects the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s larger movement: the domestic world of women and lamentation gives way to the male world of action and killing, which in turn gives way to the civic world of law.
