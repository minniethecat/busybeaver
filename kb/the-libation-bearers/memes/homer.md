## Identity

Homer, the legendary poet of the *Iliad* and *Odyssey*, is not a character in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* but a pervasive presence in its scholarly framework. Murray's Preface to the play repeatedly invokes Homer as the archaic baseline against which [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' tragic innovation must be measured. Homer is the poet who "would not elude the horror of this story by simply not mentioning it" — a judgment that establishes the moral seriousness of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' treatment (P01-002).

## Homer and the Oresteia Tradition

The *Odyssey* contains the earliest surviving narrative of [[orestes|Orestes]]' [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. In Homer's account, [[orestes|Orestes]] kills [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] to avenge his father [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], and this act is presented as straightforwardly praiseworthy — a model for Telemachus to emulate. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] transforms this simple tale of heroic [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] into a religious and moral problem of extraordinary complexity (P01-002).

Murray articulates the contrast with precision: "He would not elude the horror of this story by simply not mentioning it, like Homer, or by pretending that an evil act was a good one, like [[sophocles|Sophocles]]. He faces the horror; realizes it; and tries to surmount it on the sweep of a great wave of religious emotion" (P01-002).

## The Homeric Ghost

A key Homeric motif that [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] transforms is the relationship between the living and the dead. In Homer, "the ghosts... could not speak till they had drunk the blood of sacrifice" (P01-004). [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] adapts this archaic conception in the Invocation Scene: the dead [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] cannot gather his dim senses until the [[libation-ritual|drink-offering]]s have sunk into his grave. The wine and milk and honey reach his parched lips, and in that moment of hesitating consciousness, all the appeals that have power to rouse and sting are crowded upon him (P01-004).

This is, Murray argues, a union found nowhere else in literature: "Where in all literature, except [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], could one find this union of primitive ghostliness with high intellectual passion? One hand seems to reach out to the African or Polynesian, while the other clasps that of Milton or Goethe" (P01-004).

## Homeric Recognition Tokens

[[recognition-scene|The Recognition Scene]] in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* draws on traditional Homeric motifs. The [[recognition-and-tokens|tokens]] by which [[electra|Electra]] recognizes [[orestes|Orestes]] — a lock of hair, a footprint, and a piece of weaving — are referenced in the *Odyssey* (IV.148 ff., XIX.358, 381). These [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] were part of the traditional story, already ancient by the time of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] (002-029).

Murray notes that both Arab and Australian "trackers" have been cited as ethnographic parallels for the footprint [[recognition-scene|recognition]], and the *Odyssey* itself provides the literary precedent for [[recognition-scene|recognition]] by physical [[recognition-and-tokens|tokens]]. What [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] does with these traditional [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] — embedding them in a scene of mounting emotional intensity that culminates in the great Kommos — is characteristically transformative (002-029).

## Homer, Sophocles, and Euripides

The comparison between Homeric and tragic treatments of the [[orestes|Orestes]] story is central to Murray's critical framework. The Preface distinguishes three approaches:

1. **Homer**: "simply not mentioning" the horror — the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] is elided; only the killing of [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] is narrated
2. **[[sophocles|Sophocles]]**: "pretending that an evil act was a good one" — suppressing the religious problem and concentrating on direct passion
3. **[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]**: facing the horror, realizing it, and trying to surmount it (P01-002)

This tripartite comparison is the organising principle of Murray's entire introduction, and Homer serves as the archaic anchor point for understanding what is distinctively Aeschylean about *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* (P01-002).

## Relationships

- [[aeschylus]] — the tragic poet who transforms Homeric narrative into religious drama
- [[orestes]] — the avenger whose story originates in the *Odyssey*
- [[agamemnon]] — the murdered king whose ghost, in Homeric fashion, must drink blood to speak
- [[sophocles]] — the competing tragedian whose "Homeric tone" suppresses the religious problem

## In the Trilogy Context

Homer is not mentioned within the play text of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* itself. His presence is entirely within the critical apparatus — Murray's Preface and endnotes. However, the Homeric subtext is essential for understanding what [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] achieves: the transformation of a straightforward revenge story into a theological meditation on crime, punishment, and [[deliverance-and-salvation|deliverance]] (P01-002).
