## Definition

In the religious world of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]*, the gods communicate with mortals through a dense network of [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]]: dreams sent in sleep, oracles spoken at [[delphi|Delphi]], and omens read from the natural world. These divine messages are never merely decorative — they drive the plot, compel action, and frame the moral choices of every major character (001-002).

The *[[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is particularly rich in prophetic material, structured around three layers of divine communication: [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s dream (a private omen of guilt), Apollo's [[delphi|Delphi]]c command (an institutional oracle), and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s invocations (communal prophetic utterance) (P01-004).

## Clytemnestra's Serpent Dream

The most famous omen in the play is [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s dream, in which she gives birth to a serpent that draws blood from her breast as it nurses. The Endnotes observe that this dream "is traditional" — it appears in Sophocles and is attributed to [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] across the mythic tradition — but [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] gives it a uniquely structural role (002-068).

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s response to the dream reveals the logic of Aeschylean omen-reading: she does not consult a professional interpreter but sends [[libation-ritual|libations]] to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb, attempting to placate the dead rather than understand the sign. The Endnotes note that "if she had gone at once to a skilled interpreter, he might possibly have explained the dream away" — her failure is not in receiving the omen but in misreading it (002-017).

## Apollo's Command at Delphi

Above the private dream stands the institutional oracle: Apollo's command, delivered at [[delphi|Delphi]], that [[orestes|Orestes]] must avenge his father by killing his mother. [[orestes|Orestes]] invokes this command repeatedly — "as Lord Apollo hath foretold, Loxias the Seer" — as both warrant and compulsion (001-002).

The prophetic command creates the central moral tension of the play: it is divinely authorized, yet the act it authorises is [[the-mother-murder|kin-murder]]. The Endnotes clarify that "there is a mention of [[delphi|Delphi]] and Apollo's command" as the theological framework within which the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] must be understood — not as personal revenge but as divinely mandated [[dike|justice]] (002-035).

## The Chorus as Prophetic Voice

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of slave women functions as a third layer of prophetic utterance — not delivering formal oracles but shaping the interpretation of events through song. Their odes repeatedly invoke "the day of Fate," "the prayers of the Lost," and "the Doom from the deep" — framing the action within a cosmic scheme of impending judgment (001-004).

This choral prophecy differs from the dream and the oracle in being collective rather than individual, interpretive rather than declarative. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] does not receive [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]]; they *read* [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] already given, teaching the audience how to understand the omen-logic that governs the play (001-002).

## The Serpent Imagery System

[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] weaves the serpent omen through the entire play as a recurring image. [[orestes|Orestes]] compares himself to a serpent ("the serpent that I bare and fed," in [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s own words), the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] describes the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] as a serpent's strike, and the two serpents are visually recalled in the final scene (001-004). The Endnotes note "she mentions the 'two serpents' heads'" as a cross-reference to the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*'s imagery, confirming that [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] constructed a deliberate serpent-symbolism across the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] (002-127).

This systematic deployment of a single prophetic image across three layers of the play — dream, self-identification, and choral commentary — is characteristically Aeschylean: the omen is not a one-time portent but the symbolic vocabulary through which the entire action is articulated.

## Omen and Moral Agency

A distinctive feature of Aeschylean omen-theology is that divine signs do not remove human moral agency. The dream warns [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]; she chooses to send [[libation-ritual|libations]] rather than understand. The oracle commands [[orestes|Orestes]]; he still must decide to obey. The omens press upon the characters, but they do not relieve them of responsibility — a crucial distinction from later, more deterministic models of [[moira|fate]] (P01-008).
