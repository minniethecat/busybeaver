# O Zeus, look down on our estate — the eagle's brood

Among the most resonant images in Aeschylus' *The Libation Bearers*, Orestes' appeal to Zeus using the metaphor of the orphaned eagle marks the thematic heart of the play: the bond between father and son, the desolation of the usurped house, and the divine sanction sought for vengeance.

## 原文与来源

> O Zeus, O Zeus, look down on our estate!
> Hast seen thine eagle's brood left desolate,
> The father in the fell toils of the snake
> Dead, and his younglings perishing for sake
> Of food? And we, his children, are as they,
> Starved, broodless, bearing not our sire's array
> Of glory to his rest.
> (001-002)

## 说话人与语境

Orestes speaks these lines at the tomb of Agamemnon (001-002), near the close of his opening prayer. He has just dedicated a lock of hair to Inachus and to his dead father, and has seen the approaching Chorus of Libation Bearers with Electra. Before stepping aside to observe, he lifts his voice in a final invocation to Zeus (001-002).

The metaphor draws on a Homeric and Aeschylean pattern: the king as eagle, the rightful heir as eaglet. In Aeschylus' *Agamemnon*, the two Atreidae were described as "eagles" bereft of their young (Ag. 49–59). Here, Orestes reverses the image: the father-eagle is dead, and the young are starving, unable to fulfill their inherited role (001-002).

## 含义解析

The eagle metaphor operates on three levels. First, it is a **political image**: the king as eagle, his children as the natural heirs to power — now usurped by Clytemnestra and Aegisthus, the "snake" that trapped and killed the father (001-002). Second, it is a **ritual appeal**: by invoking Zeus, the god most closely associated with eagles and kingship, Orestes frames his vengeance as divinely ordained justice (001-002). Third, it is a **personal cry of desolation**: the word "starved" and "broodless" convey not just political disinheritance but emotional and spiritual deprivation (001-002).

## 戏剧功能

This prayer immediately precedes the entrance of the Chorus and Electra. It establishes Orestes' motivation — not simple revenge but the restoration of a broken natural order — and introduces the central tension of the play: whether divinely sanctioned vengeance can truly restore justice (001-002).
