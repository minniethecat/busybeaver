# The Chorus's Justice Ode

The Chorus of Libation Bearers delivers one of the most powerful choral odes in the Oresteia — a meditation on justice, the Erinyes, and the inexorable law that blood demands blood. Sung at the pivotal moment between the recognition of Orestes and the execution of the revenge plot, the ode provides the theological and cosmic framework within which the matricide must be understood.

## Text

> 'Tis written: the shed drop doth crave / For new blood. Yea, the murdered cry / Of dead men shrieketh from the grave (PN: 001-002)
>
> No fire ravening red, O Son, subdueth quite / The deep life of the dead; / His wrath breaks from the night (PN: 001-002)
>
> When they weep for one who dies / His Avenger doth arise, / Yea, for father and life-giver / There is Justice (PN: 001-002)
>
> Host on host, breedeth Earth / Things of fear and ghastly birth (PN: 001-002)

## Context

- **Speaker**: The Chorus of Libation Bearers (captive Trojan women)
- **Addressee**: The audience and the broader cosmos — the ode is a reflection rather than a direct address
- **Dramatic moment**: After Orestes and Electra have agreed on the plan for vengeance, the Chorus steps back to provide cosmic perspective on the action to come
- **Form**: choral ode (strophe and antistrophe structure)

## Analysis

The Justice Ode articulates what might be called the "old justice" of the Oresteia — the pre-Olympian law of blood-for-blood that governs the House of Atreus. The Chorus invokes a chain of cosmic forces: Earth breeds monsters, the sea teems with horrors, fire falls from the sky, and Justice (*Dike*) stands as an avenging force that cannot be appeased except through retribution.

The ode is notable for its catalogue of terrors — serpents, sea monsters, fire from heaven — that frame human violence as part of a larger cosmic pattern. This is not a comfortable justice; it is terrifying and absolute. The Chorus, as enslaved women who have witnessed the destruction of Troy, speak with the authority of those who know what violence costs. Their endorsement of the revenge plot carries weight precisely because they understand its horror.

## In Performance

The Justice Ode is a moment of stillness in the midst of mounting dramatic tension. After the rapid plotting of the revenge, the Chorus's song pulls the audience back to contemplate the cosmic scale of what is about to happen. The strophe-antistrophe structure creates a sense of inexorable rhythm — like the tide, like fate, like the returning avenger. The ode is typically performed with choreographed movement that mirrors the catalogue of terrors: sweeping gestures for the sea, upward gazes for fire from heaven, inward focus for the "heart of man."
