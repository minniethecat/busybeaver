# Athena

The Olympian goddess whose intervention in the *Eumenides* — the final play of the Oresteia — transforms the cycle of blood-vengeance into the institution of civic justice. Though she does not appear in *The Libation Bearers*, her impending role shapes the trilogy's arc.

## Role in the Trilogy

Athena presides over the trial of Orestes at the Areopagus, the first homicide court in Athenian legend. When the jury is evenly divided, she casts the deciding vote for acquittal. More importantly, she persuades the Furies to accept their transformation into the *Eumenides* — "the Kindly Ones" — guardians of Athens rather than avengers of blood.

The Areopagus court in Athens, the real-world fifth-century institution, was the audience's contemporary reference: Aeschylus' audience would have understood the myth as a foundation legend for their own legal system.

## Dramatic Significance

Athena represents the Oresteia's ultimate answer to the problem of vengeance. Her court at the Areopagus replaces the archaic law of retribution ("on him that doeth shall be done" [001-002]) with deliberative justice. The goddess herself embodies the principle that crime requires not more crime but judgment — a revolutionary idea in a world governed by the Furies' ancient claims.

The transformation of the Furies to Eumenides under Athena's guidance (P01-001) is the trilogy's central religious argument: that the archaic powers of blood-vengeance can be transmuted into guardians of civic order, that justice need not be indistinguishable from revenge.
