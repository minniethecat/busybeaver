## Identity

Sophocles (Σοφοκλῆς, c. 497–406 BCE) was an Athenian tragedian, the second of the three great masters of Greek tragedy after [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] and before [[euripides|Euripides]]. He composed approximately 120 plays, of which seven survive complete, including his *[[electra|Electra]]* — the most important comparative text for understanding [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' treatment of the [[orestes|Orestes]] myth in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* (P01-002).

Murray's Introduction sets Sophocles in a developmental triad: "the development of Greek tragedy moves almost straight from [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] to [[euripides|Euripides]], with Sophocles standing aside" (P01-006). This characterisation — Sophocles as the master of form who deliberately avoided the deep religious problems that drove [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] — shapes the entire comparative framework of the edition.

## Treatment of the Orestes Myth

Sophocles' *[[electra|Electra]]* is the crucial comparator for *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*. Murray identifies the essential difference: Sophocles "suppresses the religious problem and concentrates on the elements of direct passion" (P01-002). Where [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] builds the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] into a [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]-spanning theological argument — crime, punishment, [[catharsis|purification]], transformation — Sophocles treats it as a drama of character and emotion.

The Endnotes catalogue specific Sophoclean variations:
- Sophocles omits the traditional dream entirely — "it is interesting to note that Sophocles in his [[electra|Electra]] omits the tradition" of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s serpent dream (002-029).
- The invocation scene at the tomb is absent in Sophocles; "there is a brief one in [[euripides|Euripides]]" but Sophocles dispenses with the ritual summoning of the dead (002-044).
- The dream-motif is attributed to [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] "by Sophocles ([[electra|Electra]] 445)" in a different context (002-050).

## Dramatic Method

The Endnotes provide a striking comparison of Sophocles' dramaturgy. In Sophocles' *[[electra|Electra]]*, [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] "bursts in upon" [[electra|Electra]] rather than confronting [[orestes|Orestes]] — "a vivid picture of the ruined queen, with her wild, distracted words" (002-091). This is a character study; [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], by contrast, is a theological figure — the agent and victim of cosmic [[dike|justice]].

Murray's judgment is that "neither Sophocles nor [[euripides|Euripides]] has left any scene comparable" to [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' Great Kommos — the extended choral invocation at the tomb that is the structural and theological centre of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* (002-097). Sophocles' greatness lies in character and pathos; [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' in cosmic architecture.

## Key Contributions

For readers of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, Sophocles matters primarily as the contrasting voice — the poet who demonstrates, by his different choices, what is distinctively Aeschylean. Where [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] sees [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] as a religious problem, Sophocles sees it as a human tragedy. Where [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] invokes [[zeus|Zeus]] and the Furies, Sophocles invokes character and situation (P01-008).

## In Other Versions

The *[[electra|Electra]]* plays of all three tragedians survive — a unique situation in Greek literature. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* (the middle play of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]*, 458 BCE) presents the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] as divinely commanded; Sophocles' *[[electra|Electra]]* (date uncertain) presents it as psychologically motivated; [[euripides|Euripides]]' *[[electra|Electra]]* (c. 420 BCE) presents it as morally grotesque. The three treatments form a natural curriculum in the development of Greek tragic sensibility (P01-008).
