# The Chorus's Prayer to Zeus

The most urgent prayer in *The Libation Bearers* — the Chorus of slave women, alone outside the palace doors, lift their voices to Zeus at the moment of greatest peril. Aegisthus has entered the palace to question the "Phocian stranger." Orestes is inside, alone against two enemies. The women can only pray. And then, mid-prayer, the death-cry of Aegisthus answers them (001-004).

## Text

> Zeus, Zeus, how shall I speak, and how
> Begin to pray thee and beseech?
> How shall I ever mate with speech
> This longing, and obtain my vow?
>
> The edges of the blades that slay
> Creep forth to battle: shall it be
> Death, death for all eternity,
> On Agamemnon's House this day;
> Or sudden a new light of morn,
> A beacon fire for freedom won,
> The old sweet rule from sire to son,
> And golden Argolis reborn?
>
> Against two conquerors all alone,
> His last death-grapple, deep in blood,
> Orestes joineth.… O great God,
> Give victory!
> (001-004)

The prayer breaks off mid-line — and the stage direction reads: "*Death-cry of Aigisthos within.*" The divine response is immediate, violent, and irrevocable (001-004).

## Speaker and Dramatic Context

**Speaker**: The Chorus of slave women in the House of Clytemnestra and Aegisthus. They are foreign captives, not Argive citizens — a detail that heightens the irony of their role as the moral compass of the play. These women, who have the least stake in the dynastic politics of Argos, are the ones who conspire with Orestes, alter the Nurse's message to ensure Aegisthus comes alone and unarmed, and now pray for the success of the conspiracy (001-004).

**Addressee**: Zeus, the supreme Olympian, invoked not as the abstract principle of justice but as the personal guarantor of Orestes' victory. The repetition of "Zeus, Zeus" in the opening line is a feature of Greek prayer language — the doubled name intensifies the supplication and establishes the urgency of the address (001-004).

**Dramatic moment**: Aegisthus has just entered the palace to question Orestes (disguised as the Phocian stranger). The Chorus is alone on stage, knowing that Orestes faces two enemies — Aegisthus and, soon, Clytemnestra — inside the palace. The tension is unbearable because the Chorus has done everything it can: it has lied to the Nurse, ensured Aegisthus comes without his bodyguard, and now can only wait and pray as the killing happens offstage (001-004).

**Form**: choral ode, with an intense, irregular rhythm that mirrors the racing heartbeat of the moment. The short lines, the repeated invocations, the alternation between hope and fear — all create a sense of breathlessness that is unique in the extant tragic corpus (001-004).

## Analysis

### The Prayer as Structural Hinge

This prayer is structurally the hinge of the play's action. Everything that precedes it — the recognition scene, the kommos at the tomb, Orestes' entry into the palace, the Nurse's manipulated message — has been building toward this moment. Everything that follows — the death of Aegisthus, the confrontation with Clytemnestra, the matricide, the Furies' apparition — flows from it. The prayer is the still point at the center of the turning wheel (001-004).

The Chorus has shifted from passive lamentation to active conspiracy. In the *Agamemnon*, the Chorus of Argive elders was impotent — they heard Agamemnon's death-cry and debated what to do, achieving nothing. The Chorus of *Libation Bearers*, by contrast, intervenes directly in the action: they tell the Nurse to alter her message, they pray for Orestes' victory, and when Aegisthus' death-cry is heard, they exult. This shift from the first play's paralysis to the second play's complicity is one of the trilogy's most important structural developments (001-004).

### The Binary Question

The central question the Chorus poses — "shall it be death, death for all eternity, on Agamemnon's House this day; or sudden a new light of morn, a beacon fire for freedom won?" (001-004) — frames the entire action as a binary with no middle term. Either the House is destroyed forever, or it is reborn. There is no compromise, no partial outcome. This is the theology of Aeschylean justice: the crisis admits no gradualism; the stakes are absolute (001-004).

The imagery of the two alternatives is carefully balanced. "Death, death for all eternity" echoes the language of the curse — the chain of retribution that has no end. "A new light of morn, a beacon fire for freedom won" echoes the beacon-fire sequence that opens the *Agamemnon*, the signal that announced the fall of Troy and (ironically) set in motion Clytemnestra's plot. The beacon of hope in *Libation Bearers* is shadowed by the beacon of doom in *Agamemnon* — Aeschylus will not let us forget that every "new light" in this family has turned out to be another fire of destruction (001-004).

### The Personification of the Blades

The image of "the edges of the blades that slay creep forth to battle" (001-004) personifies the weapons themselves, as though the swords have their own agency independent of the men who wield them. This is typical of Aeschylus' tendency to animate the instruments of fate — the "edged" justice that cannot be turned aside once set in motion. The blades "creep" (ἕρπει, *herpei*), a verb associated with snakes and other chthonic creatures, linking the murder weapons to the underworld powers that will soon manifest as the Furies (001-004).

The personification also distances the human agents from their acts. If the blades themselves "creep forth to battle," then Orestes is merely their instrument, not their master. This is the theological logic of the trilogy: human beings are agents of forces they do not fully control, and their choices are always already shaped by the curse, the gods, and the inherited patterns of the House (001-004).

### "Against Two Conquerors All Alone"

The Chorus's description of Orestes as "all alone" against "two conquerors" (001-004) emphasizes the asymmetry of the confrontation. Aegisthus and Clytemnestra are the "conquerors" — they have held power in Argos for years, they control the palace, and they have a household of armed retainers. Orestes has only Pylades (who speaks exactly once in the entire trilogy) and the support of a chorus of foreign slave women. The odds are overwhelmingly against him.

This asymmetry makes the divine intervention — Zeus' granting of the prayer — all the more dramatic. Orestes does not win through superior force or strategy; he wins because Zeus wills it. The prayer is answered because the cosmic order has aligned behind the avenger, at least for this moment. The question the trilogy will pose — and that the *Eumenides* will adjudicate — is whether this alignment is just or merely another turn of the wheel of vengeance (001-004).

### The Interrupted Prayer

The prayer is answered mid-line. Murray's translation gives the stage direction: "*Death-cry of Aigisthos within.* Ha! The deed is done!" The divine response is immediate and violent — Zeus has heard, and the blade has fallen. The interruption of the prayer by the death-cry is one of the most effective theatrical moments in the extant tragic corpus: the word "victory" is still echoing in the air when Aegisthus' scream replaces it (001-004).

This interruption also has theological implications. The Chorus prays to Zeus, and the answer comes not as a sign or an oracle but as a human scream. The god works through human agents and human violence. Zeus' justice is not abstract; it is enacted through the sword in Orestes' hand. The prayer does not bring divine intervention *instead* of human action; it sanctifies the human action that is already in progress (001-004).

### Comparison with Other Prayers in the Oresteia

The Chorus's prayer contrasts with other moments of divine invocation in the trilogy. In the *Agamemnon*, Clytemnestra's prayer to Zeus Teleios ("Zeus the Fulfiller") as she strikes Agamemnon is a perversion of prayer — she invokes the god to bless her murder. In the *Eumenides*, Athena's prayer to the Furies to accept their new role is a prayer of persuasion and transformation. The Chorus's prayer in *Libation Bearers* stands between these extremes: it is a genuine prayer for justice, but the justice it seeks is indistinguishable from vengeance. The trilogy's moral arc moves from perverted prayer (Clytemnestra) through ambiguous prayer (the Chorus) to transformative prayer (Athena) (001-004).

## Theatrical Staging

In performance, this moment presents a unique challenge. The Chorus — typically twelve or fifteen members in Aeschylus' theatre — must convey both the urgency of prayer and the stillness of waiting. The choreography would likely have involved the chorus members turning toward the palace doors, their bodies tense with anticipation, their voices rising in supplication. The death-cry from within would shatter this stillness, and the chorus's movement would shift instantly from supplication to exultation — a transition that, in a skilled production, can be electrifying (001-004).

The spatial dynamics are also significant. The palace door — the *skēnē*'s central opening — is the focal point of the entire scene. The Chorus faces it, praying toward the unseen space where Orestes and Aegisthus are locked in combat. When the death-cry emerges from that door, the boundary between visible and invisible action is pierced by sound. The audience imagines what they cannot see, and the imagination is almost certainly more powerful than any staged violence could be (001-004).
