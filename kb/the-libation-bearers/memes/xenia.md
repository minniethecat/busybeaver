## Overview

Xenia (ξενία) — guest-friendship, the sacred bond between host and guest — is the Greek social and religious institution that governs the reception of strangers. In *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, xenia operates as a structuring irony: [[orestes|Orestes]] [[exile-and-return|return]]s to his own house in the guise of a stranger, weaponizing the very institution that his enemies have already violated, to gain entry and execute Apollo's command.

## Xenia in Greek Religion and Society

In archaic and classical Greece, xenia was a ritualized relationship protected by Zeus Xenios (Zeus the Guardian of Strangers). The bond created mutual obligations: the host provided shelter, food, and protection; the guest offered respect, news, and reciprocal hospitality in the future. The obligations were hereditary — "guest and battle-guest of yore" (001-002) bound not only individuals but their descendants.

The Introduction to *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* notes that xenia "is elaborately provided for in various codes" (P01-002), reflecting its central place in the moral framework of Greek society. To violate xenia was to commit an offense against Zeus himself.

The related institution of **supplication** (ἱκεσία, *hiketeia*) extended similar protection to those who approached an altar or hearth as formal suppliants. [[electra|Electra]]'s prayer at the tomb — "Ye thrallèd women, tirers of the bower, since ye are with me in this suppliant hour" (001-002) — draws on this tradition, casting her ritual libation as an act of supplication before the dead.

## Xenia in The Libation Bearers

### The Violated Bond

The [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]] is marked by a history of violated xenia. Atreus's original crime — serving [[thyestes|Thyestes]] his own children at a feast — was a perversion of the host's duty to provide nourishment. [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] compounds this violation by seizing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s throne and wife while [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] was away at war, exploiting the trust inherent in the household.

The bond is already broken when the play begins. [[orestes|Orestes]] [[exile-and-return|return]]s not as a rightful heir but as "a beggar and outcast" (001-002), the very figure whom xenia was designed to protect. His "beggary" — "mine own beggary urgeth me amain" (001-002) — is both a real condition ([[exile-and-return|exile]]d, impoverished) and a strategic disguise.

### The Deception Scene

The central dramatic use of xenia occurs in the [[guile-and-justice|deception]] scene, where [[orestes|Orestes]], disguised as a Phocian stranger, approaches the palace gates. His speech to the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] explains the strategy:

> First, I array me in a stranger's guise, / With all the gear of travel, and likewise / This man — their guest and battle-guest of yore! (001-002)

The irony is doubled: [[orestes|Orestes]] presents himself as a stranger to his own house, while [[pylades|Pylades]] is genuinely recognized under the ancient bond of xenia. The "guest and battle-guest of yore" is a xenos in the true sense — connected by inherited obligation — while the "stranger" [[orestes|Orestes]] is a counterfeit guest, using the institution's protections to breach the house's defenses.

When [[orestes|Orestes]] faces [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], he deploys the language of xenia with devastating irony:

> For me, in a great House and favoured thus / By fortune, 'tis by tidings prosperous / I fain were known and welcomed. Pleasantest / Of all ties is the tie of host and guest. (001-002)

The praise of xenia — "pleasantest of all ties" — comes from a man who has come to kill his hostess. The sacred bond becomes the instrument of murder, a perversion that mirrors [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s own violation of the marriage bond.

### Clytemnestra's Response

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], unaware of the stranger's identity, performs the duties of the host with elaborate courtesy:

> 'Tis time that strangers who / Have spent long hours in travel should have due / Refreshment. Ho, there! Lead him to our broad / Guest-chambers, and these comrades of his road / Who follow. (001-002)

She offers "guest-chambers" and "all comfort there to assuage their way-worn bodies" — the full ritual of xenia. The irony reaches its peak: [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], who violated every obligation of kinship and marriage, now scrupulously observes the obligations of hospitality toward the very man who will kill her.

### The Orestes-as-Beggar Motif

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s final ode celebrates the triumph in the language of xenia inverted. [[orestes|Orestes]] is:

> A walker in Pytho's way / On the neck of her kings hath trod, / A beggar and outcast, yea, / But led by God. (001-003)

The "beggar and outcast" — the figure most vulnerable within the xenia system — becomes the agent of [[justice-and-vengeance|divine justice]]. The paradox is deliberate: the violated guest ([[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], murdered at his own hearth) [[exile-and-return|return]]s through the form of the guest ([[orestes|Orestes]] as stranger) to punish the violators.

After the murders, [[orestes|Orestes]] declares himself "a wanderer and outcast for ever" (001-003), fulfilling his own prophecy. The man who weaponized xenia now must flee its protections entirely — no host will receive the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]], no altar will shelter him. This is the cost of using the sacred bond as a weapon: the user becomes untouchable.

## Xenia and the Trilogy's Resolution

The tension between violated xenia and its ironic deployment is resolved only in *Eumenides*. There, Athena herself receives [[orestes|Orestes]] as a suppliant at her altar in Athens, granting him the protection that xenia demands but that his [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] has forfeited everywhere else. The Areopagus — a civic institution — replaces the personal bond of xenia with the impersonal bond of law.

The translator frames this transformation in the language of the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s central triad: xenia, properly honored, is a form of [[deliverance-and-salvation|soteria]] ([[deliverance-and-salvation|deliverance]]); xenia violated is [[hubris]]; and xenia perverted (as [[orestes|Orestes]] perverts it) requires [[dike]] ([[dike|justice]]) before [[deliverance-and-salvation|deliverance]] can be achieved.
