# Aigisthos

Aigisthos (also *Aegisthus*), son of Thyestes, is the usurper-[[agamemnon|king of Argos]] — the lover of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and the co-murderer of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]. In [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe*, he is the first to die, killed by [[orestes|Orestes]] before the climactic confrontation with [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]].

## Identity

- **Play**: The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers (Choephoroe), second play of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* (458 BCE)
- **Family**: Son of [[thyestes|Thyestes]], whose children were slaughtered and served to him by his brother Atreus — Aigisthos inherits this [[divine-vengeance|blood-feud]]. He is "blood-foe to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]" (P02-011).
- **Status**: Mortal — usurper, co-ruler of [[argos|Argos]] with [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]
- **First appearance**: Returning to the palace to investigate the news of [[orestes|Orestes]]' supposed death (001-004)

## Role in the Play

Aigisthos is the play's secondary antagonist — less compelling than [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], less sympathetically drawn, and dispatched with less dramatic fanfare. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] gives him no great speech, no moment of self-justification, no final confrontation of moral weight. He enters, hears the (false) news of [[orestes|Orestes]]' death, investigates, and is killed — offstage — within a single scene.

This structural choice is deliberate. Aigisthos is not the moral problem of the *Choephoroe*; [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] is. His death is necessary but not interesting — a clearing of the board before the real confrontation. [[orestes|Orestes]] himself notes: "he bears, I know, / A woman's heart" (001-002) — a dismissive judgment that reflects [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' dramatic priorities.

## Key Scenes

### The Messenger Scene
Aigisthos enters having heard that travellers have arrived with news of [[orestes|Orestes]]' death. His response is calculated: he questions the messenger, expresses neither joy nor relief, and decides to investigate personally. His caution is reasonable — but it leads him directly into [[orestes|Orestes]]' trap.

### The Death
Aigisthos is killed inside the palace, offstage. A servant rushes out screaming: "Dead, dead! The master is dead! ... Aigisthos is no more!" (001-004). There is no dying speech, no final curse, no dramatic confrontation. His death is reported, not shown — a deliberate anticlimax that shifts the dramatic weight to the coming encounter with [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]].

## Dramatic Function

Aigisthos serves three functions in the *Choephoroe*:

1. **Political**: He is the usurper whose presence prevents [[orestes|Orestes]] from simply reclaiming his throne. The political dimension of the revenge required his removal.
2. **Structural**: His death provides the "first kill" that builds tension toward the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]]. The audience sees the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] mechanism work perfectly against him — then watch it go terribly wrong against [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]].
3. **Mythological**: As the son of Thyestes, Aigisthos embodies the inherited [[divine-vengeance|blood-feud]] between the two branches of the [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]]. His death closes one cycle of the curse even as [[orestes|Orestes]]' [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] opens another.

## Across the Trilogy

- **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]**: Aigisthos appears in the final scene as the triumphant avenger of his father's wrongs, claiming full credit for the plot against [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] — though the audience knows [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] was the driving force.
- **Choephoroe**: He dies quickly and without dignity — the avenger avenged, the plotter outplotted.
- **Eumenides**: He does not appear. The moral question has moved beyond him.
