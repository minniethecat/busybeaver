# The Mother-Murder

The mother-murder (*mētroktonia*) is the defining moral crisis of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* — the act around which the entire [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] turns. Unlike Sophocles and [[euripides|Euripides]], who soften or psychologize the horror, [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] "faces the horror; realizes it; and tries to surmount it on the sweep of a great wave of religious emotion" (P01-002).

## Definition

In the *Choephoroe*, the mother-murder is not a simple crime but a tragic knot of contradictory obligations. [[orestes|Orestes]] must kill his mother because Apollo commands it, because his dead father demands [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]], and because he swore an oath to do so. Yet the act is "a sin; a sin to be expiated by unfathomable suffering. Yet, since the god cannot have commanded evil, it is a duty also. It is a sin that must be committed" (P01-002). [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] refuses to resolve the paradox within the *Choephoroe* itself — the play ends with [[orestes|Orestes]] fleeing, pursued by the Furies, his sanity fracturing.

## Greek Context

Matricide was among the most abhorrent crimes in Greek thought, worse even than the murder of a father because it violated the most fundamental biological bond. The Erinyes (Furies) were especially associated with the punishment of those who shed kindred blood, and they pursued [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]]s with particular ferocity. [[orestes|Orestes]]' case was unusual in having divine sanction — Apollo himself commanded the killing — which set up the theological crisis the *Eumenides* would resolve. "In [[orestes|Orestes]]' days men had to do [[dike|justice]] on the wicked with their own hands, or else leave them unpunished and triumphant" (002-038).

## Role in The Libation Bearers

The play builds inexorably toward the killing. [[orestes|Orestes]]' hesitation is genuine — he does not kill eagerly. "The thought that he himself hates his mother is what pierces [[orestes|Orestes]]' heart" (002-047). When [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] bares her breast and cries "Stay, O my child! Have pity, O my son, / On this breast where thy babyhood was laid" (001-004), [[orestes|Orestes]] wavers and asks [[pylades|Pylades]] whether he should spare her. [[pylades|Pylades]]' single speech in the play — "Where then shall Apollo's word, / The Pythian oath, be kept?" (001-004) — recalls [[orestes|Orestes]] to his duty.

After the killing, the horror manifests immediately. [[orestes|Orestes]] displays [[the-net-and-the-robe|the bloodstained robe]] and begins to lose his reason: "the amazing scene at the end with [[the-net-and-the-robe|the bloodstained robe]], the gathering of the unseen Furies, the last struggle of [[orestes|Orestes]]' reason, and the flight of the would-be Saviour as one Accursed" (P01-008).

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The mother-murder is the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s moral fulcrum:

- The **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** establishes [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] as murderer and mother — the audience knows her as both.
- The **Choephoroe** forces the issue — [[orestes|Orestes]] must kill the woman who bore him. The play ends in crisis: "the final words of the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] ask the question which is to be answered, or at least attempted, in the Eumenides" (P01-008).
- The **Eumenides** resolves the crisis through divine and civic intervention: Apollo purifies [[orestes|Orestes]], Athena establishes the court, and the deadlock of the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] is broken by Athena's deciding vote — not because the act was right, but because the cycle must end.
