# The Trilogy Storm Finale

The final words spoken in *The Libation Bearers* are not Orestes' — he has already fled, pursued by the Furies. They belong to the Chorus, who close the play with a sweeping summary of the three great "storms" of the House of Atreus. This passage is both epitaph for the first two plays and prologue to the third, explicitly bridging the *Oresteia*'s narrative arc and posing the question that only the *Eumenides* can answer (001-004).

## Text

> Behold a third great storm made wild
> By winds of wrath within the race,
> Hath shook this castle from its place.
>
> The ravin of the murdered child
> First broke Thyestes in his pride:
>
> Second, a warrior and a King,
> Chief of Achaia's warfaring,
> Was smitten in the bath and died.
>
> And Third, this Saviour or this last
> Doom from the deep. What end shall fall,
> Or peace, or death outsweeping all,
> When night comes and the Wrath is past?
> (001-004)

## Speaker and Dramatic Context

- **Speaker**: The Chorus of slave women, possibly joined by all on stage (the text is ambiguous about whether this is the Chorus alone or a communal closing)
- **Addressee**: The audience, the departing Orestes, and the city of Argos — the closing words of a Greek tragedy traditionally address the *polis* as much as the individual characters
- **Dramatic moment**: Orestes, pursued by the Furies visible only to him, has just fled toward Delphi to seek purification at Apollo's shrine. The Chorus remains, surveying the bodies of Clytemnestra and Aegisthus, and speaks these closing lines as the *exodos* (final choral passage) of the tragedy (001-004).
- **Form**: choral ode (*exodos*), the conventional closing movement of a Greek tragedy. The *exodos* typically provides resolution or moral summation; Aeschylus subverts this convention by offering not resolution but an open question (001-004).

## Analysis

### The Three-Storm Structure as Trilogy Architecture

This passage functions simultaneously as epitaph and prophecy. The Chorus enumerates the three storms in strict chronological order, compressing three generations of atrocity into a single lyric movement that mirrors the trilogy's own three-play structure (001-004):

**First storm — Thyestes**: "The ravin of the murdered child / First broke Thyestes in his pride" (001-004). This refers to Atreus's killing and cooking of Thyestes' children — the primordial crime of the House that set the curse in motion generations before the play's action. Aeschylus names Thyestes (not Atreus, the perpetrator) as the initial victim, emphasizing the chain of suffering rather than the chain of crime. The word "ravin" (predatory violence, from Latin *rapina*) characterizes the act as bestial rather than human — the children are devoured like prey (001-004).

The choice to begin with Thyestes rather than with Tantalus (the House's founder, who served his son Pelops to the gods) or with Atreus (who committed the child-murder) is significant. By focusing on the victim of the first storm rather than its perpetrator, Aeschylus establishes the pattern that will govern the trilogy's moral logic: every crime produces a victim who demands justice, and every act of justice for one victim creates a new victim who demands justice in turn. The storm metaphor captures this recursion — each storm is both the consequence of the previous one and the cause of the next (001-004).

**Second storm — Agamemnon**: "Second, a warrior and a King, / Chief of Achaia's warfaring, / Was smitten in the bath and died" (001-004). The murder of Agamemnon by Clytemnestra, the subject of the first play of the trilogy, is summarized in three lines of devastating compression. The contrast between Agamemnon's heroic stature — "a warrior and a King, chief of Achaia's warfaring" — and the ignominy of his death — "smitten in the bath" — encapsulates the tragic reversal (*peripeteia*) at the heart of the *Agamemnon* (001-004).

The word "smitten" (from Old English *smitan*, "to strike") reduces the conqueror of Troy to a passive victim. The bath — a place of cleansing, vulnerability, and domestic intimacy — becomes the site of his murder, subverting every association of the space. Clytemnestra transformed the most private room of the palace into an abattoir, and the Chorus's choice of the bath as the sole detail of Agamemnon's death emphasizes the violation of domestic sanctuary that makes the crime particularly horrific (001-004).

**Third storm — Orestes**: Described as "this Saviour or this last / Doom from the deep" (001-004). The Chorus cannot yet say whether Orestes' matricide is salvation or final destruction — the ambiguity is deliberate and points directly to the *Eumenides*, where this question will be adjudicated by Athena's court. The word "Saviour" (Σωτήρ, *Sōtēr*) is a cult title of Zeus and other protective deities; to apply it to Orestes is to claim divine sanction for the matricide. But the alternative — "last Doom from the deep" — acknowledges the possibility that Orestes is not a saviour but the final agent of the curse, the wave that destroys the ship rather than bringing it to harbour (001-004).

### The Storm Metaphor and Greek Poetic Tradition

The storm (*cheimōn*, χειμών) is one of the most ancient and enduring metaphors in Greek poetry. In Homer, storms at sea represent divine wrath and the chaos that threatens human order (*Odyssey* Book 5, the storm that destroys Odysseus' raft). In Pindar, the storm represents political turmoil and the fragility of *polis* life (*Pythian* 4). Aeschylus draws on this entire tradition but transforms it: his storms are not external events that happen to the House but internal forces — "winds of wrath within the race" — generated by the House's own history of violence (001-004).

The metaphor "shook this castle from its place" (001-004) literalizes the storm's destructive power. The House of Atreus is a physical structure — a *castle*, a *dōma* (δῶμα) — that can be shaken to its foundations. The architectural metaphor reinforces the trilogy's central concern with the *oikos* (household) as both a physical space and a genealogical line. When the *oikos* is shaken, both the building and the bloodline are threatened with collapse (001-004).

The progression from "wind" to "storm" to "castle shaken from its place" traces an escalating sequence: internal passions (wrath) generate atmospheric disturbance (wind), which intensifies into cataclysmic force (storm), which destroys the physical structure that should have contained and controlled those passions (the castle). The House that was meant to be a refuge becomes the site of its own destruction — a paradox at the heart of Greek tragedy's conception of the *oikos* (001-004).

### The Open Question

The closing question — "What end shall fall, or peace, or death outsweeping all, when night comes and the Wrath is past?" (001-004) — is the open question of the entire trilogy. The trilogy form itself demands that this question not be answered here; the audience must wait for the third play. Aeschylus' structural genius is most visible in this refusal of closure: the *Choephoroe* ends not with resolution but with a question that makes the *Eumenides* structurally necessary (001-004).

The alternatives — "peace, or death outsweeping all" — reproduce the binary logic of the Chorus's earlier prayer to Zeus ("shall it be death, death for all eternity... or sudden a new light of morn?" — 001-004). But the closing question introduces a temporal dimension that the earlier prayer lacked: "when night comes and the Wrath is past." The storm will end; the question is what will remain when it does. This temporal framing transforms the binary from a static opposition into a narrative arc — the trilogy's three-play structure is itself the form that this narrative takes, moving from crime (*Agamemnon*) through retribution (*Libation Bearers*) to resolution (*Eumenides*) (001-004).

### The Exodos Convention Subverted

In conventional Greek tragedy, the *exodos* provides a moral summary, a restoration of order, and a transition back to the ordinary world. Euripides would later mock this convention with his formulaic closing tag: "Many are the shapes of things divine, and many things the gods accomplish beyond expectation." Aeschylus, writing at an earlier stage of the genre's development, is not bound by this convention — and in *Libation Bearers*, he deliberately violates it (001-004).

Instead of restoration, the *exodos* offers exhaustion. Instead of a moral, it offers a question. Instead of a return to normalcy, it offers the promise — or threat — of a third storm whose nature remains unknown. The audience leaves the theatre not satisfied but suspended, their need for resolution channeled directly into the expectation of the trilogy's third play. This is dramaturgy of the highest order: the play's incompleteness is not a flaw but a structural feature, a deliberate withholding that generates the energy that will carry through to the *Eumenides* (001-004).

### The Wrath (Μῆνις) and Its Transformation

The capitalized "Wrath" (Μῆνις, *Mēnis*) in the closing line is a word with specific epic resonance. It is the first word of the *Iliad* — "Μῆνιν ἄειδε, θεά" ("Sing, goddess, of the wrath") — and it refers specifically to the destructive anger of Achilles that drives the epic's action. By closing *Libation Bearers* with the same word, Aeschylus positions the *Oresteia* as the successor to the *Iliad*'s exploration of wrath — but with a crucial difference (001-004).

The *Iliad*'s wrath is personal (Achilles' anger at Agamemnon) and ends in a moment of human connection (Achilles' pity for Priam). The *Oresteia*'s wrath is transpersonal — it belongs not to an individual but to the *genos* (γένος, the family line), and it passes from generation to generation like an inheritance. The question the trilogy poses — and that the *Eumenides* answers — is whether this transpersonal wrath can be brought to an end through institutions (the law court, the *polis*) rather than through the exhaustion of the human agents caught in its cycle (001-004).

The closing question — "when night comes and the Wrath is past?" — imagines a time after wrath, a darkness that is not the darkness of the Furies' gowns but the darkness of rest after the storm. Whether such a rest is possible — and what form it might take — is the question that the *Oresteia* was written to answer (001-004).

## Theatrical Staging of the Exodos

The staging of this closing passage would have been both simple and powerful. The Chorus, having witnessed Orestes' flight and the revelation of the bodies, turns to face the audience directly. The *orchēstra* — the dancing floor — is now a space of aftermath: the bodies are visible (on the *ekkyklema*), the great red robe is still spread across the stage, and the Chorus, the only figures left who can speak, address the audience in the formal, measured language of lyric closure (001-004).

The final question hangs in the air. In the Theatre of Dionysus, with the Athenian acropolis visible on the horizon and the Areopagus — the very hill where the *Eumenides* trial will take place — a short walk away, the question would have resonated with specific topographical and political force. The audience members were also citizens of the *polis* that would, in the trilogy's conclusion, be offered as the solution to the cycle of wrath. The *exodos* of *Libation Bearers* is thus not only a bridge to the next play but an invitation to the audience to see their own city as the answer to the question the Chorus cannot yet resolve (001-004).
