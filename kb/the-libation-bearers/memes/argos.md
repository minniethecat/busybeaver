# Argos

Argos is the entire visible world of the *Choephoroe* — the city where [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] ruled, where [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] now governs, and to which [[orestes|Orestes]] [[exile-and-return|return]]s from [[exile-and-return|exile]] to reclaim his inheritance through blood.

## Location & Identity

- **Region**: The Argolid, in the northeastern Peloponnese
- **Type**: City — the seat of the [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]]
- **Mythological associations**: Kingdom of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]; the [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]]; the setting of much of the Trojan War cycle's aftermath

## Role in The Libation Bearers

The [[house-of-atreus|palace of Argos]] dominates the play's spatial imagination. [[orestes|Orestes]] [[exile-and-return|return]]s to "cross the threshold of the courtyard gate" (001-002) — the threshold that separates [[exile-and-return|exile]] from home, stranger from kin, and living from dead. The palace is simultaneously a place of rightful inheritance and a house of horrors: it contains [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s bloodstained bath, the usurpers' bed, and the weapons that will kill them.

## Mythological Significance

Argos in myth is one of the great cities of the Heroic Age — the kingdom of Perseus, then of Atreus, then of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]. Its royal house is the most cursed in Greek mythology, and the *Choephoroe* dramatizes the moment when the curse reaches its peak intensity before (in the *Eumenides*) being broken.

## Dramatic Function

The palace at Argos functions as a spatial paradox: it is [[orestes|Orestes]]' home, to which he has every right, but he can only enter it through [[guile-and-justice|deception]]. He must become a stranger to reclaim his inheritance, and the act of reclaiming it — the killing of the usurpers — makes him an [[exile-and-return|exile]] once more.
