# Guile and Justice

Guile (*dolos*) in [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* is not merely tactical deception but a principle of [[justice-and-vengeance|divine justice]]: "they who slew by guile a man most rare, / By guile, and snarèd in the self-same snare, / May die" (001-002). The play transforms deception from a morally suspect means into the instrument through which divine [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] achieves perfect symmetry.

## Definition

*Dolos* in Greek thought encompassed cunning, trickery, deception, and craft — qualities associated with figures like Odysseus and, in tragedy, with women. The *Choephoroe* deploys *dolos* as a principle of retributive symmetry: the manner of the killing must mirror the original crime. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] was killed by deception — trapped in a robe-net in his bath — therefore his killers must die by deception. [[orestes|Orestes]] disguises himself as a traveller bearing news of his own death, enters the palace under false pretences, and strikes when the victims are off-guard.

## Greek Context

[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' treatment of *dolos* reflects a broader Greek ambivalence about cunning. In Homer, Odysseus' *mētis* (cunning intelligence) is celebrated; in tragedy, *dolos* is often associated with female characters and with the violation of *xenia* (guest-friendship). [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s original murder of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] employed *dolos* — she welcomed him home and killed him in his bath. [[orestes|Orestes]]' use of the same method creates a poetic [[dike|justice]]: "He of the laughing lure, / The guile and the secret blow, / (O Vengeance, keep him sure!)" (001-004).

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] explicitly sanctions guile as a legitimate weapon of the weak against the strong: "a woman's trembling spear" (001-002) — deception is the only weapon available to the disempowered.

## Role in The Libation Bearers

[[orestes|Orestes]]' entire plan depends on *dolos*. He instructs [[pylades|Pylades]]: "I array me in a stranger's guise, / With all the gear of travel" (001-002). They will adopt "an accent of Parnassian speech" (001-002) to pass as Phocians. When admitted to the palace, [[orestes|Orestes]] will kill [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] before his identity is known. The plan is knowingly deceptive — and knowingly just.

The play links guile to the broader theme of hidden truth becoming visible. The dream, the lock of hair, the [[recognition-and-tokens|footprints]] — all are "[[recognition-and-tokens|signs]]" that must be interpreted, revealing what *dolos* conceals. The play's movement is from the hidden ([[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s crime, [[orestes|Orestes]]' identity, the meaning of the dream) to the revealed (the [[recognition-scene|recognition]], the killing, [[the-net-and-the-robe|the bloodstained robe]] displayed to all).

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

Guile's role changes across the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]:

- In the **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]**, *dolos* belongs to [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] — it is the weapon of the murderer, associated with female cunning and the violation of sacred bonds.
- In the **Choephoroe**, *dolos* is reclaimed by [[orestes|Orestes]] as an instrument of [[justice-and-vengeance|divine justice]] — still deceptive, but now authorized by Apollo.
- In the **Eumenides**, *dolos* is absent — the trial at the Areopagus proceeds through open argument, persuasion, and voting. The replacement of guile with transparency marks the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s advance from archaic to civic [[dike|justice]].

The persistence of *dolos* as a dramatic device even in a play about [[dike|justice]] suggests [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' [[recognition-scene|recognition]] that in a fallen world — a world where the rightful king has been murdered and usurpers rule — the just must sometimes use the tools of the unjust.
