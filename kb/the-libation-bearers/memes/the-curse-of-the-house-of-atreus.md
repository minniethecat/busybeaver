# The Curse of the House of Atreus

The dark chain of inherited guilt and retribution that binds the royal house of Argos across three generations. Each act of violence in the Oresteia is both a punishment for the previous crime and a new crime demanding fresh punishment — an unending cycle that only Athena's intervention can break.

## Origin

The curse began with Atreus' sin against his brother Thyestes: "First came the sin of Atreus against the children of Thyestes" (002-130). Atreus killed Thyestes' sons and served them to their father at a feast — an act of such horror that it stained the entire lineage with divine wrath.

## Chain of Transmission

The curse passes through the generations not as passive inheritance but as active repetition:

**Atreus → Agamemnon**: Agamemnon inherits the curse and compounds it by sacrificing his daughter Iphigenia at Aulis.

**Agamemnon → Clytemnestra**: Clytemnestra murders Agamemnon, claiming the justice of a mother avenging her daughter — but her act is also a fulfilment of the curse.

**Clytemnestra → Orestes**: Orestes kills his mother, commanded by Apollo but also driven by the curse's logic — "On him that doeth shall be done" (001-002).

## Theological Problem

The curse embodies the trilogy's theological crisis: how can justice be achieved when every act of retribution is itself a crime? The ancient law — "the blow of death the blow of death atone" (001-002) — creates an infinite regress. Only the transformation of private vengeance into civic justice, achieved in the *Eumenides*, can break the chain.
