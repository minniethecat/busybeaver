# Orestes as Saviour

The title "Saviour" (Soter) echoes through *The Libation Bearers* with mounting irony. Orestes is the saviour who saves through destruction, the redeemer whose redemption demands the most terrible of crimes.

## In the Opening Prayer

Orestes' first words invoke Hermes as "Saviour, be thou my saviour; Help in War, Help me!" (001-001). The triple invocation establishes the saviour motif as central to his self-understanding: he returns not as a son but as a divine agent, his personal identity subsumed in his salvific mission.

## The Chorus's Hope

The Chorus, the captive women of Troy, sees in Orestes their own hope of liberation. They call for "some man of might / To aid this land, some high and visible lord / Of battle, shining bright / Against Death" (001-005) — a saviour who will free Argos from tyranny.

## The Final Irony

The play's closing image is devastating: Orestes, the Saviour, flees "as one Accursed, never to rest again." The Preface describes the final scene as "the last struggle of Orestes' reason, and the flight of the would-be Saviour as one Accursed." The saviour motif completes its tragic arc — from hope to horror, from divine mission to divine punishment.
