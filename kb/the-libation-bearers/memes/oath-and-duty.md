# Oath and Duty

In [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe*, oath and duty form the moral-religious framework that transforms personal revenge into sacred obligation. [[orestes|Orestes]] does not kill [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] from hatred or ambition but because he is bound by oath to his dead father and commanded by Apollo's oracle — a conflict of duties that [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] presents as genuinely tragic.

## Definition

The Greek concept of *horkos* (oath) was not merely a promise but a religious act invoking the gods as witnesses and guarantors of punishment for oath-breakers. [[orestes|Orestes]]' obligation is triple-bound: he swore an oath to his father's memory while in [[exile-and-return|exile]] in Phocis (001-002), Apollo's oracle at [[delphi|Delphi]] commanded the killing under threat of horrific punishment (001-002), and the dead themselves cry out for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] through the invocatory ritual at the tomb. The duty is simultaneously filial, religious, and contractual — no escape is possible.

## Greek Religious Context

In fifth-century Athens, oaths structured public and private life — jurors, magistrates, and citizens swore binding oaths to the gods. The Erinyes (Furies) were specifically the enforcers of oaths and the punishers of perjury, particularly within families. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] portrays [[orestes|Orestes]]' dilemma in terms his audience would recognize: duty to one's father versus duty to one's mother, with the gods giving contradictory commands. "The mother-murder, even if done by a god's command, is a sin; a sin to be expiated by unfathomable suffering. Yet, since the god cannot have commanded evil, it is a duty also. It is a sin that must be committed" (P01-002).

## Role in The Libation Bearers

[[orestes|Orestes]] explicitly enumerates his motivations: "The God's command; grief for my father slain; / And mine own beggary urgeth me amain" (001-002). He presents himself not as a free agent but as one compelled by forces beyond his control. [[electra|Electra]] too frames her prayer as a matter of duty — she hesitates: "But dare I? Is it no sin thus to pray?" (001-002), and the Leader's pragmatic answer confirms the tragic bind: "How else? With hate thine hater to repay" (001-002).

The play explores the cost of duty fulfilled. [[orestes|Orestes]] performs the killing in obedience to divine command but is immediately pursued by the Furies — the same powers that would have pursued him for *disobedience*. The tragedy lies in the fact that moral clarity is impossible: "[[orestes|Orestes]] should think of his duty to his father and forget all else" (002-103), but the consequences of that singular focus are devastating.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The duty-conflict evolves across the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]:

- The **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** presents duty as ambiguous — [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] claims the right (and duty) to avenge Iphigenia.
- The **Choephoroe** presents duty as tragic — [[orestes|Orestes]] must act, but his act generates new guilt.
- The **Eumenides** resolves the conflict by institutionalizing judgment: the Areopagus court replaces personal oath-duty with civic [[dike|justice]]. The Furies, oath-enforcers, become guardians of the city's oaths rather than the family's.
