# Clytemnestra

Clytemnestra (*Klytaimēstra*) enters the *Choephoroe* as the queen of Argos and the murderer of her husband [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] — a figure already established in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* as a woman of formidable intelligence and will. In the *Choephoroe*, she appears only briefly, but her presence haunts every scene: the [[libation-ritual|libations]] she sends, the dream that terrifies her, and the killing that ends her life.

## Identity

- **Play**: The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers (Choephoroe), second play of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* (458 BCE); also appears in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*
- **Family**: Daughter of Tyndareus and Leda; sister of Helen; wife of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]; mother of [[orestes|Orestes]], [[electra|Electra]], and Iphigenia (sacrificed by [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] at Aulis)
- **Status**: Mortal — queen and usurper of [[argos|Argos]]
- **First appearance in this play**: Emerging from the palace after [[orestes|Orestes]]' entrance, facing her son in the climactic confrontation (001-004)

## Role in the Play

Though physically present only in the final scenes, Clytemnestra dominates the *Choephoroe* from its opening. It is her dream of the serpent that drives her to send the [[libation-ritual|libations]] — the act that brings [[electra|Electra]] and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] to the tomb, enables the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] with [[orestes|Orestes]], and sets the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] plot in motion. Her attempt to appease the dead is simultaneously the mechanism of her destruction.

When she finally appears, Clytemnestra remains formidable. Confronted by [[orestes|Orestes]], she does not cower but deploys every rhetorical weapon: she reminds him of her maternal care, she bares the breast that nursed him, she threatens him with the curse that attends [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]]. "Stay, O my child! Have pity, O my son, / On this breast where thy babyhood was laid" (001-004). Her plea almost succeeds — [[orestes|Orestes]] wavers — but [[pylades|Pylades]]' intervention recalls him to his duty.

## Key Scenes

### The Dream
Though Clytemnestra does not appear in the dream scene itself, her terror is reported by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]]: "appalled, came shrieking out of sleep; / And many a torch, long blinded in the deep / Of darkness, in our chambers burst afire / To cheer the Queen" (001-002). The dream — bearing a serpent that draws blood from her breast — is interpreted by [[orestes|Orestes]] as divine confirmation of his mission (001-002).

### The Confrontation
Clytemnestra's final scene is a tour de force of rhetorical self-defence. She claims the right to have killed [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], citing his sacrifice of Iphigenia and his infidelities. When argument fails, she turns to pathos: baring her breast, invoking the bond of nursing, and threatening [[orestes|Orestes]] with the Furies' pursuit. Her death is not shown onstage but reported — the climax of the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] she set in motion herself.

## Dramatic Function

Clytemnestra is the play's absent-present antagonist — absent from the stage for most of the action, present in every character's thoughts, prayers, and fears. She is the object of [[electra|Electra]]'s hatred, the target of [[orestes|Orestes]]' mission, and the source of the [[blood-guilt|pollution]] that must be cleansed. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' treatment is notable for its restraint: she is not a monster but a queen, a mother, and a murderer — the complexity established in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* is preserved, making her death more tragic than triumphant.

## Across the Trilogy

- **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]**: Clytemnestra is the dominant figure — the intellectual and rhetorical equal of any male character, she orchestrates her husband's murder with deliberation and triumph.
- **Choephoroe**: She appears briefly, already diminished by fear and guilt, but still formidable in her final confrontation.
- **Eumenides**: She appears only as a ghost, summoned by the Furies to testify against [[orestes|Orestes]] — a spectral presence whose claims must be adjudicated by Athena's court.

## In Other Versions

[[sophocles|Sophocles]]' *Electra* presents Clytemnestra as a more straightforward tyrant — her dream is reported but the psychological complexity of Aeschylus' version is diminished. [[euripides|Euripides]]' *Electra* shows her visiting the countryside, aged and somewhat pitiable, her murder rendered more squalid than tragic. Only Aeschylus preserves her full stature: a queen whose intelligence and will make her equally formidable as murderer and as victim.
