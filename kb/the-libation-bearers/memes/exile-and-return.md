# Exile and Return

The pattern of exile and return (*nostos*) is the narrative armature of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* — [[orestes|Orestes]] was driven from [[argos|Argos]] as a child after [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s murder, grew to manhood in Phocis under Strophius' protection, and returns at the play's opening to reclaim his inheritance through blood.

## Definition

*Nostos* (return, homecoming) is one of the fundamental narrative patterns of Greek literature, most famously in the *Odyssey*. But where Odysseus' return restores order, [[orestes|Orestes]]' return unleashes violence. He is "returned from lands afar / To claim mine own" (001-002), but what he claims is not a kingdom but [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. The return is not a homecoming in any conventional sense — [[orestes|Orestes]] comes as a stranger, disguised, and his "claim" is to blood, not welcome.

## Greek Context

Exile was a common [[moira|fate]] in Greek myth and history — political exiles, suppliants, and displaced persons populate Greek literature. [[orestes|Orestes]]' exile is particularly charged because it is the exile of a rightful heir. He grew up "in Phôkis, where I lay / With Strophius in the hills, being cast away / In childhood, plundered by mine enemies, / And friendless" (001-002). His return is both politically and religiously motivated: he must reclaim his throne and avenge his father. The two goals are inseparable — without [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]], there can be no rightful restoration.

## Role in The Libation Bearers

The play opens with [[orestes|Orestes]] already returned, standing at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb. His first words are a prayer to Hermes and his dead father, and his first action is to cut a lock of hair as an offering (001-002). He is simultaneously present and hidden — he watches the approach of the [[the-chorus|Libation-Bearers]] from concealment, not yet ready to reveal himself.

The return is achieved through [[guile-and-justice|deception]]: [[orestes|Orestes]] enters the palace not as the returning prince but as a traveller bearing false news of [[orestes|Orestes]]' death. The exile returns as the bearer of his own death-notice — a grim irony that underscores the cost of *nostos* in this play. He will "cross the threshold of the courtyard gate" (001-002) not in triumph but in guile, and the return will lead not to celebration but to [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] and madness.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The exile-return pattern structures the entire [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]:

- The **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** shows the perversion of return — [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s *nostos* from [[troy|Troy]] ends in his murder.
- The **Choephoroe** shows return as [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] — [[orestes|Orestes]] comes home to kill.
- In the **Eumenides**, after the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]], [[orestes|Orestes]] becomes an exile once more, pursued by the Furies across Greece until he reaches Athens. This second exile is purificatory — through it, he earns the right to a true return, this time as a free man.

The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] thus traces a double *nostos*: the first return (to [[argos|Argos]]) results in crime; the second return (from the Furies' pursuit, through the Areopagus trial) results in [[deliverance-and-salvation|deliverance]].
