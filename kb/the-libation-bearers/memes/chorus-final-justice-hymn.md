# The Chorus's Final Justice Hymn

The Chorus of bondmaids closes *The Libation Bearers* with a triumphant hymn celebrating Orestes' double slaying of Aigisthos and Clytemnestra, immediately followed by the darker recognition that this deliverance is but the "third great storm" in the history of the House — the cycle of violence may not yet be over.

## Text

> Cry, Ho for the perils fled,
> For the end of the long dismay!
> Cry, Ho for peace and bread;
> For the Castle's lifted head,
> For the two defilers dead,
> And the winding of Fortune's way! (PN: 001-004)

> Even as Apollo gave
> His charge on the Mountain, He
> Who holdeth the Earth-heart Cave,
> Hast thou wrought innocently
> Great evil, hindered long,
> Tracking thy mother's sin… (PN: 001-004)

> And soon shall the Perfect Hour
> O'er the castle's threshold stone
> Pass with his foot of power,
> When out to the dark is thrown
> The sin thereof and the stain
> By waters that purify. (PN: 001-004)

And the play's closing words:

> Behold a third great storm made wild
> By winds of wrath within the race,
> Hath shook this castle from its place.
> The ravin of the murdered child
> First broke Thyestes in his pride:
> Second, a warrior and a King,
> Chief of Achaia's warfaring,
> Was smitten in the bath and died.
> And Third, this Saviour or this last
> Doom from the deep. What end shall fall,
> Or peace, or death outsweeping all,
> When night comes and the Wrath is past? (PN: 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: Chorus of bondmaids in the House of Clytemnestra and Aigisthos
- **Addressee**: The audience, the city of Argos, and Orestes (implicitly)
- **Dramatic moment**: Immediately after Orestes, overcome by the vision of the Furies, flees the palace. The doors stand open, revealing the bodies of Clytemnestra and Aigisthos. The Chorus pronounces the final verdict on the action.
- **Form**: Choral ode (strophe/antistrophe pairs) concluding with a collective epode that surveys the full arc of the Atreid curse

## Analysis

The final chorus moves through three emotional registers: first, unalloyed celebration of deliverance ("Cry, Ho for the perils fled"); second, theological justification of the matricide as Apollo's command ("Even as Apollo gave / His charge"); and third, a sobering recognition that this is only the latest in a trilogy of disasters.

The enumeration of the "three storms" — Thyestes' feast, Agamemnon's murder, and now the matricide — situates Orestes' act within the full history of the House. The closing question "What end shall fall, / Or peace, or death outsweeping all, / When night comes and the Wrath is past?" refuses closure, pointing directly toward the *Eumenides* where these questions must be answered (PN: 002-109).

The word "Saviour" (*soter*) applied to Orestes is deeply ironic: he is both deliverer and matricide, and the ambiguity of whether he represents "peace or death" is the theological crux of the entire Oresteia.

## In Performance

The Chorus delivers these lines standing before the open palace doors with the bodies visible, creating a visual tableau of triumph and horror. The shift from exultant tone to the somber "third storm" enumeration marks one of the most dramatic tonal pivots in Greek tragedy.
