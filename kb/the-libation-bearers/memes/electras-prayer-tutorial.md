# Electra's Prayer Tutorial

An exquisite passage of dramatic instruction near the opening of *The Libation Bearers*. Electra, standing at her father's tomb with libation offerings sent by Clytemnestra, does not know how to pray. The Chorus Leader guides her, line by line, toward the forbidden prayer for vengeance — culminating in one of the most chilling instructions in Greek tragedy.

## Text

> **Electra:** For thee and me alone am I to pray?
> **Leader:** Ask thine own understanding. It will say.
> **Electra:** Who else? What heart that with our sorrow grieves?
> **Leader:** Forget not that—far off—Orestes lives.
> **Electra:** Oh, bravely spoke! Thou counsellest not in vain.
> **Leader:** Next; on the sinners pray, their sin made plain…
> **Electra:** Pray what? I know not. Oh, make clear my road!
> **Leader:** Pray that there come to them or man or god…
> **Electra:** A judge? Or an avenger? Speak thy prayer.
> **Leader:** Plain be thy word: one who shall slay the slayer.
> **Electra:** But dare I? Is it no sin thus to pray?
> **Leader:** How else? With hate thine hater to repay.
> (PN 001-002)

## Context

- **Speakers**: [[Electra]], daughter of Agamemnon, and the Chorus Leader
- **Addressee**: Each other — this is a rapid stichomythic exchange
- **Dramatic moment**: Electra has brought Clytemnestra's propitiatory offerings to Agamemnon's tomb. She asks the Chorus of slave women how she should pray, uncertain whether to follow the conventional formula or speak the truth of her heart. The Leader guides her through her hesitation.
- **Form**: stichomythia (line-by-line exchange)

## Analysis

This stichomythia is a pedagogical drama in miniature. Electra, young and uncertain, represents the tension between piety and justice — can one pray for murder without sinning? The Leader, older and wise in the ways of suffering, tutors her step by step.

The progression is carefully calibrated. First, the Leader expands the circle of prayer beyond Electra alone: "Forget not that—far off—Orestes lives" (PN 001-002). This introduces the hope of return without yet naming vengeance. Only when Electra accepts this does the Leader advance: "Next; on the sinners pray" (PN 001-002). Even then, Electra hesitates — "Pray what? I know not" — forcing the Leader to articulate the thought Electra cannot yet speak.

The climactic line — "Plain be thy word: one who shall slay the slayer" (PN 001-002) — is the verbal equivalent of a drawn sword. The Leader strips away euphemism. There is to be no ambiguity: the prayer is for a killer. Electra's final question — "But dare I? Is it no sin thus to pray?" — articulates the central religious problem of the Oresteia, the question that will not be resolved until the *Eumenides*. The Leader's answer is practical, not theological: "How else? With hate thine hater to repay."

This exchange establishes the play's moral framework: in a world before the Areopagus and civic justice, the only response to murder is counter-murder. The prayer-tutorial is the seed from which the entire revenge plot grows.
