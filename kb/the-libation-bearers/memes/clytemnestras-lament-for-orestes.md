# Clytemnestra's Lament for Orestes

A remarkable passage of layered meaning in the middle of *The Libation Bearers*. Orestes, disguised as a Phocian merchant, has just delivered the fabricated news of his own death to Clytemnestra. Her response, spoken directly to the man she does not recognize as her son, is a tour de force of dramatic irony — a mother's grief that is also a queen's relief, and ultimately her own death sentence.

## Text

> Ah me,
> So cometh the last wreck in spite of all!
> Curse of this House, thou foe that fear'st no fall,
> How dost thou spy my hidden things and mar
> Their peace with keen-eyed arrows from afar,
> Till all who might have loved me, all, are gone!
> And now Orestes; whom I had thought upon
> So wisely, walking in free ways, his gait
> Unsnarèd in this poison-marsh of hate!
> The one last hope, the healing and the prayer
> Of this old House, 'twas writ on empty air!
> (PN 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Clytemnestra]], queen of Argos and murderer of Agamemnon
- **Addressee**: Orestes (disguised as a Phocian stranger) and attendants
- **Dramatic moment**: Orestes has just delivered his fabricated account of his own death in Phocis. Clytemnestra, believing the news, responds with this speech before ordering the "strangers" to be given hospitality. She then withdraws to consult with Aegisthus.
- **Form**: rhesis (extended speech)

## Analysis

This speech is a masterclass in Aeschylean dramatic irony. Every line operates on at least two levels. For Clytemnestra, the words express what she presents as maternal grief — Orestes was "the one last hope, the healing and the prayer of this old House" (PN 001-004). For the audience, who knows Orestes stands before her, the lament is a confession: the House *is* cursed, and she herself is the "foe that fear'st no fall" who has marred its peace.

The phrase "curse of this House" (PN 001-004) is particularly layered. Clytemnestra means the family curse that has pursued the Atreidae across generations. Yet the curse has manifested most recently through *her own* murder of Agamemnon. She speaks of the curse as an external force — "thou foe" — disclaiming responsibility even as she names the very dynamic she set in motion.

The image of "keen-eyed arrows from afar" (PN 001-004) evokes Apollo, the far-shooting god whose oracle at Delphi has commanded Orestes' return. Unknowingly, Clytemnestra identifies the divine agency behind the revenge plot she cannot see.

Her description of Orestes' supposed safe removal — "walking in free ways, his gait unsnarèd in this poison-marsh of hate" (PN 001-004) — is bitterly ironic. She sent him away as a child to Strophius in Phocis; now a "Phocian" stranger brings news of his death. The "poison-marsh" she imagined him free of has, in fact, produced the avenger who stands before her.

The closing line — "'twas writ on empty air" (PN 001-004) — carries the weight of the entire Oresteia's theology. For Aeschylus, nothing is writ on empty air. The gods record all. Orestes has come, and the "last hope" has become the instrument of her destruction.
