# Clytemnestra's Death

the climactic murder of Clytemnestra by her son Orestes — the matricide that is simultaneously the fulfilment of Apollo's command and the most abhorrent crime in Greek thought.

## Context in the Play

This event occurs at a pivotal moment in *The Libation Bearers*. It is not simply a plot point but a carefully staged dramatic moment whose significance resonates through the remainder of the play and across the trilogy.

## Dramatic Presentation

Aeschylus stages this event with characteristic starkness. His characters speak with what the Preface calls "an archaic beauty and a stark grimness of speech." The theatrical choices — what is shown on stage, what is reported, what is heard from within — are deliberate and powerful.

## Aftermath

The consequences of this event drive the play's final movement. The Furies' appearance, Orestes' flight, and the Chorus's closing questions all flow directly from what has transpired.
