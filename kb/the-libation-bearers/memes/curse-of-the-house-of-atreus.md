# Curse of the House of Atreus

The curse upon the [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]] is the inherited doom that drives successive generations of the [[argos|Argive]] royal family to commit [[the-mother-murder|kin-murder]]. In [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe*, the curse is both a mythological inheritance and a living force that [[orestes|Orestes]] must confront and, through his actions, either perpetuate or end.

## Definition

The *Ara* (curse) in Greek belief was not merely a spoken wish for harm but a supernatural force set in motion by a grievous transgression — particularly the violation of kinship bonds. Once invoked, it pursued the family line across generations. The [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]] carried a curse originating with Atreus' slaughter of his brother [[thyestes|Thyestes]]' children, compounded by [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s sacrifice of Iphigenia and his killing by [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]. In the *Choephoroe*, [[orestes|Orestes]] stands within this chain, summoned by the dead and by Apollo to perform the next act of the curse's unfolding.

## Greek Context

[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] treats the ancestral curse not as blind [[moira|fate]] but as a moral-religious reality. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of the *Choephoroe* cries out: "Alas, alas, for the travail born in the race, / Alas for the harp of Atê, whose strings run blood" (001-002). The curse operates through human choices — each generation chooses violence — yet those choices are shaped by the inherited debt of blood. The key theological innovation of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* is that the curse can be broken not by more killing but by law, [[catharsis|purification]], and divine intervention.

## Role in The Libation Bearers

[[orestes|Orestes]] explicitly invokes the Curse as one of his motivations: "Then shall the Curse have drunken of our gore / Her third, last, burning cup, and thirst no more" (001-002). He hopes his act will be the curse's final satisfaction — though the immediate aftermath shows the Furies pursuing him, suggesting the curse's appetite is not so easily sated.

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] describes the curse's mechanism in the language of [[blood-guilt|pollution]] and obsession: "Craving to fly that curse / With graceless gift hither she urgeth me" (001-002) — [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s offerings are a futile attempt to escape what she has inherited and renewed. The play's central dramatic question is whether [[orestes|Orestes]] can be the end-point of the curse or merely its latest victim.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

Across the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]], the curse moves from background inheritance to foreground crisis to resolution:

- The **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** establishes the curse's history and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s role as its agent.
- The **Choephoroe** makes the curse the explicit dramatic engine, with [[orestes|Orestes]] confronting the full weight of his inheritance.
- In the **Eumenides**, the curse is broken when the Erinyes — the curse's supernatural embodiments — are persuaded to become guardians of Athens rather than pursuers of blood. "The spirits of Punishment" are converted "from Furies to Beneficent Beings" (P01-001).

[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] describes this as the great problem at the centre of Greek religion: "Hubris, [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]], Soteria or Crime, Punishment and Deliverance" (P01-002).
