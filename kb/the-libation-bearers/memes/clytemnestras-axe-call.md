# Ho, there, mine axe of battle!

When Clytemnestra hears the Slave's cry that Aegisthus is dead, her first instinct is not to flee or to plead — it is to arm herself. The axe-call is a flash of the same formidable queen who, in *Agamemnon*, wielded the weapon that killed her husband. In *The Libation Bearers*, it reveals a woman who, even at the edge of doom, refuses to go quietly.

## 原文与来源

> Ho, there, mine axe of battle! Let us try
> Who conquereth and who falleth! I am she,
> And here I stand, and here my axe shall be.
> (001-004)

Clytemnestra speaks these lines immediately after the Household Slave's alarm cry, summoning a servant to bring her weapon (001-004).

## 说话人与语境

Clytemnestra has just learned that Aegisthus — her lover, co-conspirator, and co-ruler — has been killed inside the palace (001-004). She does not yet know the identity of the killer, though the audience suspects she must realize it is Orestes. Her response is swift and physical: she calls for the very axe with which, in some versions of the myth, she killed Agamemnon (001-004).

This moment echoes the *Agamemnon* where Clytemnestra stands over her husband's body holding the weapon. The axe is the symbol of her agency — the tool that made her a killer and a queen (P01-007). Now, facing her own death, she reaches for it again, as though the weapon itself can restore her power (001-004).

## 含义解析

The axe-call reveals three facets of Clytemnestra's character. First, it shows her **warrior instinct** — unlike Aegisthus, who dies offstage without a fight, Clytemnestra prepares for combat (001-004). Second, it underscores her **refusal of the feminine victim role** — she will not be the pleading, weeping woman but the armed combatant (001-004). Third, it is tragically **futile**: the axe that killed one king cannot save her from his avenging son; the weapon that was her power has become the symbol of the cycle of violence that now consumes her (001-004).

In the larger arc of the Oresteia, this moment also prefigures the *Eumenides*, where the Furies — the embodiment of the old law of blood-for-blood — will pursue Orestes. Clytemnestra's axe, like the Erinyes, belongs to the ancient order of retribution that the trilogy ultimately seeks to transcend (001-004).
