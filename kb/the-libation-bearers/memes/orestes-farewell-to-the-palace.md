# Orestes' Farewell to the Palace

The final speech of [[Orestes]] in *The Libation Bearers*, spoken moments after the matricide and the killing of [[Aigisthos]]. Having fulfilled [[Apollo]]'s command to avenge his father [[Agamemnon]], Orestes now faces the consequences of his act: the [[Erinyes]] (Furies) rise to pursue him, and he must flee the palace of the Atreidae into exile. The speech forms the bridge to the third play of the Oresteia, *The Eumenides*, where Orestes will seek purification at Delphi and ultimately stand trial at the Areopagus in Athens.

## Text

> [Orestes stands before the palace, the bodies of Clytemnestra and Aigisthos behind him. The Chorus watches in mingled hope and dread.]
>
> I grieve for the slain, and for myself that killed,
> And for the whole sad race. A victor still,
> Yet poisoned with the victory I must go,
> A wanderer and an exile, and must know
> The bitter bread of them I know not of,
> And be their bondman. ...
> But, while I have some reason left, I vow
> To all this people, and will make it good,
> That he who freed his father and withstood
> The pollution of his house, shall not be spurned
> Nor left unpitied ... I must go hence, and learn
> To mate with madness. ...
> See where they come, the Gorgon shapes, the brood
> Of the great Night, with robes of black arrayed,
> And in their matted hair serpents displayed
> For diadems! Here is no resting-place!
> (PN 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Orestes]], son of [[Agamemnon]] and [[Clytemnestra]]
- **Addressee**: The Chorus of slave women, and the people of Argos
- **Dramatic moment**: After the double killing — Aigisthos and Clytemnestra lie dead inside the palace. Orestes emerges to display the robe in which Agamemnon was killed, then begins to feel the onset of the Furies' pursuit. This is his last speech before fleeing into exile.
- **Form**: rhesis (extended speech, increasingly fragmented as madness encroaches)

## Analysis

The farewell speech marks the tragic culmination of the revenge plot. Orestes has done what Apollo commanded — avenged his father — but the act that was mandated as justice now manifests as pollution (*miasma*). The speech dramatises the central paradox of the Oresteia: divine command and moral horror are inseparable in the world of blood-vengeance.

The speech's psychological power lies in its trajectory. It begins with Orestes' attempt at rational self-justification — he freed his father, he withstood the pollution — and descends into hallucinatory terror as the Furies become visible to him alone. The Chorus cannot see them; they try to reassure him that he is "safe in Argos." But Orestes sees the Gorgon-faced, serpent-crowned pursuers, and his reason breaks. This moment of "subjective vision" — where only one character perceives what the audience must imagine — is one of the most theatrically potent devices in Greek tragedy (PN 001-004).

Structurally, the farewell looks forward to *The Eumenides*, where the Furies will be transformed from private avengers into civic guardians. But at this moment, there is no civic resolution — only flight, madness, and the terror of the unseen.

## In Performance

The farewell speech demands an actor capable of charting a rapid descent from rhetorical control to hallucinatory terror. The transition point — when Orestes first "sees" the Furies — is a theatrical coup: the Chorus looks at empty air while Orestes recoils from visible horrors. Modern productions often use lighting, sound design, or movement to suggest the Furies' presence that only Orestes (and the audience's imagination) can perceive.
