# Killing of Clytemnestra

The killing of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] by her son [[orestes|Orestes]] is the climactic event of the *Choephoroe* — the act toward which the entire play has been building and the act whose consequences drive the remainder of the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]].

## Summary

After killing [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]], [[orestes|Orestes]] confronts his mother. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] deploys every rhetorical weapon: she reminds him of her maternal care, bares the breast that nursed him, and threatens the curse of matricide. [[orestes|Orestes]] wavers and asks [[pylades|Pylades]]: "What shall I do? Shall I spare my mother?" [[pylades|Pylades]]' single speech — "Where then shall [[apollo|Apollo]]'s word, / The Pythian oath, be kept?" (001-004) — recalls [[orestes|Orestes]] to his duty. He drags his mother into the palace and kills her. The act is reported, not shown — but its aftermath is the play's final, devastating scene.

## Dramatic Context

- **Position in the play**: Climax
- **Preceding event**: [[death-of-aigisthos|Killing of Aigisthos]] (offstage)
- **Following event**: Display of the bodies; [[orestes|Orestes]]' madness and flight
- **Offstage**: The killing itself occurs inside the palace

## Participants

- **[[orestes|Orestes]]** — the killer, acting under divine command but wavering at the crucial moment
- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]** — the victim, who fights with words when weapons fail
- **[[pylades|Pylades]]** — speaks once, recalling [[orestes|Orestes]] to his oath

## Dramatic Significance

The killing of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] is the moral crisis of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]*. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] refuses to simplify it: "The mother-murder, even if done by a god's command, is a sin; a sin to be expiated by unfathomable suffering. Yet, since the god cannot have commanded evil, it is a duty also. It is a sin that must be committed" (P01-002). [[orestes|Orestes]] is simultaneously executing [[justice-and-vengeance|divine justice]] and committing the most abhorrent crime in Greek thought. His immediate descent into madness — seeing the Furies gather, his reason fracturing — confirms that the act is both necessary and unbearable.
