## Identity

Hermes is the Olympian messenger god, guide of souls to the underworld (Psychopompos), and patron of travellers, boundaries, and communication. In *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, he appears in the play's very first moments: [[orestes|Orestes]] stands at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb and addresses his opening prayer to "Hermes of the Underworld," establishing the god as the mediator between the realm of the living and the realm of the dead.

The opening invocation addresses Hermes in his chthonic aspect — "Son of [[zeus|Zeus Chthonios]]" ([[zeus|Zeus of the Underworld]]) — a theological identification noted as obscure even to [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' contemporaries. The scholiast records that this phrase was discussed in Aristophanes' *Frogs* (1126 ff.), suggesting its theological density was already recognised in antiquity (002-029).

## Hermes Psychopompos in the Play

Hermes' role as Guide of the Dead (Psychopompos) is intimately connected to the play's central dramatic action: the awakening of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s ghost. The [[libation-ritual|libations]] poured by [[electra|Electra]] and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] must reach the dead king; Hermes is the divine agent who conducts them to their destination. [[orestes|Orestes]]' opening prayer thus does more than invoke divine favour — it names the mechanism by which the communication between living and dead will occur.

## Titles and Functions

The endnote records that "Saviour" and "Help in War" are other titles of Hermes, alongside his primary function as Psychopompos (002-029). This multiplicity of titles reflects Hermes' complex divine portfolio:

- **Psychopompos**: Guide of souls — the primary function in *Choephoroe*
- **Chthonios**: Of the Underworld — invoked as son of the chthonic [[zeus|Zeus]]
- **Saviour**: A protective aspect relevant to [[orestes|Orestes]]' dangerous mission
- **Help in War**: The martial aspect, appropriate to the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] plot

## Hermes and the Opening Scene

The prologue of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* opens with [[orestes|Orestes]] at the tomb. His first words are a prayer to Hermes, and this choice is dramatically significant. Before the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] plot can begin, communication with the dead must be established. Hermes, as the god who crosses boundaries — between Olympus and Earth, between the living and the dead — is the necessary divine sponsor of this communication.

The prayer to Hermes thus establishes the play's theological geography: the action will move between the chthonic and Olympian realms, between the tomb and the palace, between the dead father's command and the living son's obedience. Hermes presides over all these transitions.

## Relationships

- [[zeus]] — father; Hermes is invoked as "Son of [[zeus|Zeus Chthonios]]" in the play's opening
- [[orestes]] — the mortal who invokes Hermes at the tomb
- [[agamemnon]] — the dead king whose ghost Hermes must guide
- [[apollo]] — the Olympian half-brother whose command [[orestes|Orestes]] carries out

## In Greek Religion

Hermes Psychopompos was a significant figure in Greek funerary practice. [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]]s at tombs — [[libation-ritual|choai]], from which the play takes its name — were poured to Hermes as well as to the dead, acknowledging his role in conducting offerings to the underworld. [[the-chorus|The Chorus of Libation-Bearers]], in performing their ritual office, are thus acting within Hermes' sphere of divine influence.
