# Clytemnestra's False Grief

When the disguised Orestes brings news of his own death to the palace, Clytemnestra responds with a public lament whose every line is charged with double meaning. The audience knows Orestes stands before her alive; the Chorus knows; only Clytemnestra — and her hidden relief — are on display.

## Text

> Ah me,
> So cometh the last wreck in spite of all!
> Curse of this House, thou foe that fear'st no fall,
> How dost thou spy my hidden things and mar
> Their peace with keen-eyed arrows from afar,
> Till all who might have loved me, all, are gone!
> And now Orestes; whom I had thought upon
> So wisely, walking in free ways, his gait
> Unsnarèd in this poison-marsh of hate!
> The one last hope, the healing and the prayer
> Of this old House, 'twas writ on empty air! (PN: 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: Clytemnestra, queen of Argos and murderer of Agamemnon
- **Addressee**: Orestes (disguised as a Phocian merchant) and the assembled household
- **Dramatic moment**: Orestes has just delivered the false report of his own death. Clytemnestra comes to the gate to receive the news. She does not yet know she is speaking to her son.
- **Form**: Rhesis (extended speech) with rhetorical lamentation

## Analysis

Clytemnestra's speech is a masterpiece of layered meaning. On the surface, it is a mother's grief at the death of her son. Beneath the surface, every line can be read as true in ways she does not intend: the "Curse of this House" has indeed found her; those who "might have loved" her are indeed gone; and the "one last hope" of the House is not dead but standing before her with a sword.

The phrase "walking in free ways, his gait / Unsnarèd in this poison-marsh of hate" is bitterly ironic — Clytemnestra imagines Orestes free from the House's corruption, when in fact he has returned specifically to enact its bloodiest chapter. Her description of the news as "writ on empty air" inverts the truth: the words she hears are the plot that will kill her (PN: 002-091).

The speech also reveals Clytemnestra's characteristic grandeur. Even in deception, her language has a tragic weight that commands attention. The "keen-eyed arrows from afar" evoke the divine agents of doom that haunt the House — an image she deploys for rhetorical effect but which describes her actual situation with uncanny precision.

## In Performance

The scene depends on the audience's awareness of the discrepancy between Clytemnestra's performed grief and her private relief. An actor playing Clytemnestra must convey both the public lament and the subtle signs of relief beneath it — perhaps a controlled breathing, a flicker in the eyes, a pause too carefully measured.
