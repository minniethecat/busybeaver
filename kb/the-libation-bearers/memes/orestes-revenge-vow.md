# Orestes' Revenge Vow

At the climax of the great kommos — the extended ritual lamentation at Agamemnon's tomb — Orestes transforms the gathering from an act of mourning into a pact of vengeance. Invoking Zeus, Apollo, and the spirit of his dead father, he binds himself, Electra, and the Chorus to the sacred task of retribution. This is the turning point of the play: the moment when grief becomes action.

## Text

Orestes begins his invocation:

> O Warder Hermês of the world beneath, Son of the Father who is Lord of Death; Saviour, be thou my saviour; Help in War, Help me! I am returned from lands afar To claim mine own. (PN: 001-002)

And turns to Zeus:

> Zeus, Oh, grant to me this day My vengeance, and be near me in the fray! (PN: 001-002)

The Chorus echoes the call for divine justice:

> Thou holy Earth, thou holy shore Beyond the grave, where rests his head The Lord of Ships, the King, the Dead, Now list, now aid, or never more! The hour is full. The Guileful Word Descends to wrestle for the right, And Hermês guards the hour of night For him that smiteth with the sword. (PN: 001-002)

From the kommos, Orestes' direct address to his father:

> O Father, in the darkness of the grave, Thy children call on thee. Despise us not. Look on this nest of eaglets, and this plot Of ours to snare the eagles who have wrought Thy death. (PN: 001-002)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Orestes]], son of Agamemnon
- **Addressee**: Agamemnon (the dead), Zeus, Hermes, and the Chorus
- **Dramatic moment**: At Agamemnon's tomb, after the recognition scene between Orestes and Electra. The libations have been poured, the lock of hair identified, and the siblings reunited. Now the lamentation at the tomb — the great kommos that forms the play's emotional and ritual centre — builds toward Orestes' oath of vengeance.
- **Form**: Kommos (a lyric dialogue between actor and Chorus, mixing spoken and sung lines) transitioning to rhesis

## Analysis

### From Lament to Oath

The revenge vow marks a structural pivot in the play. Before this moment, the action at the tomb has been retrospective — mourning what was lost, identifying tokens of Orestes' return, remembering Agamemnon's murder. After the vow, the action becomes prospective: the planning of the murder, the journey to the palace, the execution of vengeance. The vow is the hinge, transforming the tomb from a place of grief into a place of empowerment (PN: 001-002).

### The Divine Chain of Authority

Orestes' invocation follows a careful theological hierarchy. He begins with Hermes, the guide of souls and messenger between worlds — the appropriate god for communication with the dead. He then ascends to Zeus, the supreme deity who sanctions justice. The structure establishes a chain of divine authorization: Hermes conveys the prayer, Zeus commands the vengeance, and Orestes becomes the instrument. This is not personal revenge; it is divinely mandated justice (PN: 001-002).

### The Chorus as Co-Conspirator

The Chorus's response — "Now list, now aid, or never more! / The hour is full" — transforms them from mourners into conspirators. Their words carry a military urgency: the "Guileful Word" (δόλος) that will "wrestle for the right," Hermes guarding "the hour of night / For him that smiteth with the sword." The language of wrestling and sword-stroke makes the forthcoming murder a sanctioned combat rather than a stealthy assassination (PN: 001-002).

### The "Eaglets" and the "Eagles"

Orestes' metaphor of the "nest of eaglets" — himself and Electra — plotting against the "eagles" who killed their father draws on the natural imagery that pervades the *Oresteia*. Eagles are the royal birds of Zeus, and Agamemnon was described in the *Agamemnon* as an eagle robbed of its young. Orestes reclaims the image: the eaglets, now grown, will snare the eagles who usurped the nest. The metaphor transforms the revenge from a sordid family murder into a restoration of the natural order (PN: 001-002).

## In Performance

The kommos is one of the longest and most demanding passages in Greek tragedy for its performers. The actor playing Orestes must move from the controlled grief of the recognition scene to the mounting intensity of the oath, while the Chorus's sung responses create a wave-like rhythm of emotional escalation. The physical setting — the bare tomb mound in a desolate landscape — concentrates all attention on the voices rising toward heaven.

The invocation of Zeus ("grant to me this day / My vengeance") is the moment when the play's moral stakes become explicit. A modern audience may feel the tension between the language of justice and the reality of what is being plotted: the murder of a mother. Aeschylus does not resolve this tension; he intensifies it, allowing the audience to feel both the righteousness of Orestes' cause and the horror of its consequence.
