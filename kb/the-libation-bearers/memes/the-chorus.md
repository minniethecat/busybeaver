# The Chorus of Libation-Bearers

The Chorus of the *Choephoroe* — the "Libation-Bearers" who give the play its Greek name (*Choephoroi*) — are foreign slave-women, captives of war now serving in the [[house-of-atreus|palace of Argos]]. They are the play's collective consciousness: mourners, ritual officiants, moral commentators, and at the crucial moment, active participants in the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] plot.

## Identity

- **Play**: The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers (Choephoroe), second play of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* (458 BCE)
- **Family**: Captive women — "God in far days / Laid hand upon my city, and herded me / From my old home to the House of Slavery" (001-002)
- **Status**: Choral — mortal slave-women, neither free citizens nor divine observers
- **First appearance**: Entering with [[electra|Electra]], bearing [[libation-ritual|libations]] for [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb (001-002)

## Role in the Play

The Chorus is unusually integrated into the *Choephoroe*'s action. Unlike some tragic choruses that comment from the sidelines, these women actively shape events: they guide [[electra|Electra]] through her moral dilemma ("How else? With hate thine hater to repay" [001-002]), participate in the great *[[kommos]]* as equal partners with [[orestes|Orestes]] and [[electra|Electra]], and at the crucial moment, they instruct the [[the-nurse|Nurse]] to alter [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s message — ensuring [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] comes alone and unguarded to his death.

The Chorus's moral stance evolves through the play. Initially they are cautious and fearful: "I needs must praise, / Just or unjust, / The pleasure of them that rule" (001-002). But as the ritual at the tomb intensifies, they become fierce advocates of [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]: "It hideth in my bosom's core, / It beats its wings for death, for death, / A bitter wind that blows before / The prow, a hate that festereth, / A thing of horror, yet divine!" (001-002).

## Key Scenes

### Guiding Electra's Prayer
When [[electra|Electra]] hesitates over what prayer to speak at the tomb, the Chorus's Leader guides her step by step — from acknowledging [[orestes|Orestes]]' existence to explicitly praying for the death of the murderers. The Chorus provides the ritual and moral framework within which [[electra|Electra]]'s private grief becomes public, religious action.

### The Great Kommos
The Chorus alternates with [[orestes|Orestes]] and [[electra|Electra]] in the extended ritual lamentation. Their strophes provide the mythological and cosmic context — the stories of Althaea and Scylla, the Lemnian crime — that place the [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]]'s suffering within a larger pattern of transgression and punishment.

### The Nurse Scene
In a crucial intervention, the Chorus instructs [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s [[the-nurse|Nurse]] — who has been sent to summon [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] with his bodyguard — to deliver a different message: that [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] should come alone. This alteration of the message ensures that [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] is unguarded when [[orestes|Orestes]] strikes.

## Dramatic Function

The Chorus serves as the play's ritual and ethical infrastructure. They are the officiants who make the invocation of the dead ritually effective; they are the moral commentators who articulate the play's theological framework ("'Tis written: the shed drop doth crave / For new blood" [001-002]); and they are the practical agents who alter events to ensure the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] succeeds. Their triple role — ritual, moral, practical — makes them the most functionally versatile chorus in surviving Greek tragedy.

## In Other Versions

The *Choephoroe*'s chorus of slave-women contrasts with the old men of Argos who form the chorus of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* — where that chorus is passive and retrospective, this one is active and forward-looking. [[sophocles|Sophocles]]' *Electra* uses a chorus of free Argive women who are sympathetic but less directly involved in the action. [[euripides|Euripides]]' *Electra* uses a chorus of Argive maidens whose detachment from the central violence reflects that play's more ironic, anti-heroic tone.
