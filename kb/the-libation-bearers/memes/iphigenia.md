# Iphigenia

The daughter whose death at her father's hands set in motion the chain of vengeance that culminates in *The Libation Bearers*. Though Iphigenia never appears in the play, her absence is everywhere present.

## The Sacrifice at Aulis

When the Greek fleet was becalmed at Aulis, unable to sail for Troy, the seer Calchas declared that Agamemnon must sacrifice his daughter Iphigenia to appease Artemis. Agamemnon lured Iphigenia to Aulis under the pretense of marriage to Achilles, then killed her on the altar. This act provided Clytemnestra's justification for murdering Agamemnon upon his return from Troy ten years later.

## In the Oresteia

Clytemnestra invokes Iphigenia's death as justification for her own murder of Agamemnon in the first play of the trilogy. By *The Libation Bearers*, Iphigenia is no longer mentioned by name, but the audience understands that Clytemnestra's claim — that she acted as an avenging mother — complicates Orestes' righteous vengeance. If Clytemnestra was herself an agent of justice, then Orestes' matricide becomes not pure justice but another link in an unending chain of retribution.

## Dramatic Significance

Iphigenia embodies the trilogy's central moral problem: every act of vengeance claims the mantle of justice, making it impossible to distinguish between the two. Her death — an act of parental violence — mirrors and prefigures the parental violence (mother-killing) that Orestes must commit, linking victim and perpetrator across generations.
