# Law of Retribution

The law of [[dike|retribution]] — "on him that doeth shall be done" (001-002) — is the organizing ethical principle of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe*, an archaic moral code that the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] both dramatizes and questions, ultimately moving beyond it toward the civic [[dike|justice]] of the law-court.

## Definition

The law of [[dike|retribution]] (*lex talionis* in Latin, *Dikē* in Greek) holds that [[dike|justice]] requires exact equivalence between crime and punishment: blood for blood, blow for blow. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] states it as divine ordinance: "as falls her rod, God's [[dike|justice]] crieth: 'For the blow / Of death the blow of death atone'" (001-002). This is not savage impulse but a religious and moral system — "Speaks a grey word of long ago" (001-002) — rooted in the conviction that the cosmic order (*Dikē*) demands balance.

## Greek Context

The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] was produced in 458 BCE, at a time when Athens was developing its democratic legal institutions. The old [[divine-vengeance|blood-feud]] system, in which families privately avenged their dead, was being replaced by public courts. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] dramatizes this transition: the law of [[dike|retribution]] is presented as real and valid — the dead do cry out for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]], and the gods do demand it — but also as ultimately insufficient. "To us, and in this special case to [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], the rule of [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] seems savage," yet in [[orestes|Orestes]]' world "men had to do [[dike|justice]] on the wicked with their own hands, or else leave them unpunished and triumphant" (002-038).

## Role in The Libation Bearers

The play operates entirely within the law of [[dike|retribution]]. [[electra|Electra]] prays explicitly: "The slayer in just recompense be slain" (001-002). [[orestes|Orestes]] declares: "The slayer shall meet the slayer, wrong smite with wrong" (001-002). The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] affirms: "'Tis written: the shed drop doth crave / For new blood" (001-002).

Yet [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] already seeds the critique. [[electra|Electra]] hesitates before praying for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]: "But dare I? Is it no sin thus to pray?" (001-002). The Leader's answer — "How else? With hate thine hater to repay" (001-002) — acknowledges the law while hinting at its moral cost. The play's final image of [[orestes|Orestes]] as "the would-be Saviour as one Accursed, never to rest again" (P01-008) shows the law of [[dike|retribution]] consuming its own agent.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] traces the movement from [[dike|retribution]] to law:

- The **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** shows [[dike|retribution]] in its pure form: [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] kills [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], claiming the right of *Dikē*.
- The **Choephoroe** intensifies the paradox: [[orestes|Orestes]] obeys the same law and becomes both agent of [[dike|justice]] and object of pursuit. The problem is stated: how can [[dike|justice]] be served without perpetuating violence?
- The **Eumenides** provides the answer: Athena's establishment of the Areopagus transforms [[dike|retribution]] into judgment, and the Erinyes into guardians of civic order. "The spirits of Punishment" become "Beneficent Beings" (P01-001).
