## Overview

[[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] (δίκη, *dikē*) — justice, righteous judgment, and retributive order — occupies the central position in the [[oresteia|Oresteia]]'s religious triad. Identified in the translator's Introduction as the second term after Hubris and before Soteria (P01-002), [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] is both the inevitable consequence of crime and the divine mechanism that restores balance to a violated cosmos. In *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] is not an abstract principle but an active, personified force — called upon in prayer, invoked in oaths, and recognized in the bloody resolution of the drama.

## Dike in the Libation Bearers

### Personification and Invocation

[[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] appears in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* not merely as a concept but as a divine presence. [[electra|Electra]] concludes her prayer at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb with a direct appeal:

> O Victory, be our own this day, With Justice who doth hold us fast, And Zeus most high, who saveth at the last! (001-002)

Justice ([[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]]) is paired with Victory (Nike) and Zeus — a trinity of divine forces aligned on the side of the rightful avengers. The phrase "who doth hold us fast" (συνέχουσα) suggests [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] as an active, sustaining power, not a passive standard.

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] deepens this personification in the great [[kommos]]:

> Came He of the laughing lure, The guile and the secret blow, (O Vengeance, subtle to slay!) But there held his hand that day The Daughter of Zeus, the pure, Justice yclept below. Justice they called her name, For where is a goodlier? And her breath is a sword of flame On the foes of her. (001-004)

Here [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] is named explicitly as the "Daughter of Zeus" (Διὸς κόρη), pure and uncompromising. The image of her "breath" as a "sword of flame" (πνέουσα φλόγα ξίφους) collapses the distinction between abstract justice and material retribution — [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] does not merely command punishment; she is punishment.

### Dike as Lex Talionis

The principle that governs [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is the *[[law-of-retribution|lex talionis]]* — the law of retribution in kind. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] articulates this with the force of ancient proverb:

> "For tongue of hate be tongue of hate Made perfect": thus, as falls her rod, God's justice crieth: "For the blow Of death the blow of death atone." "On him that doeth shall be done": Speaks a grey word of long ago. (001-002)

This "grey word of long ago" (τριγέρων μῦθος) is the foundational saying of archaic Greek justice: [[on-him-that-doeth-shall-be-done|δράσαντι παθεῖν]] (*[[on-him-that-doeth-shall-be-done|drasanti pathein]]*) — "to the doer, suffering." The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s moral architecture is built upon this principle. Every act of [[hubris]] generates a pro[[moira|portion]]ate claim of dike; every crime demands its answering punishment.

### The Double-Edged Nature of Dike

The tragic complexity of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* lies in [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]]'s double edge. The same justice that demands [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s death also condemns [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]]. [[orestes|Orestes]] himself acknowledges this after the killing:

> Behold, all ye who hear this tale of rue, This robe, this trap that did my father greet ... And bear me witness, when they seek some day To judge me, that in justice I did slay This woman. (001-004)

[[orestes|Orestes]] claims [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] as his justification, yet the very need for future witnesses and judgment undermines the certainty of his claim. The endnote commentary sharpens the point: "Justice has come both to [[troy|Troy]] and to [[argos|Argos]]," yet the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] immediately asks, "Is the power of God hemmed in so strangely to work with wrong?" (002-121)

## The Triadic Structure

| Element | Role in Trilogy | Manifestation in *[[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* |
|---------|----------------|--------------------------------------|
| [[hubris|Hubris (ὕβρις)]] | The originating crime | [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s murder of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], her and [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]]'s usurpation |
| [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] (δίκη) | The answering justice | [[orestes|Orestes]]'s divinely-commanded revenge |
| Soteria (σωτηρία) | The final [[deliverance-and-salvation|deliverance]] | Deferred to *Eumenides* — the [[catharsis|purification]] of [[orestes|Orestes]] |

[[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] is the hinge of the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]: it answers the first play's crime and precipitates the third play's crisis. Without [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]], [[hubris]] would stand unchallenged; with [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] alone, the cycle of violence continues indefinitely. The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s resolution requires a transformation of [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] itself — from the bloody [[law-of-retribution|lex talionis]] of the Furies to the civic justice of Athena's court.

## Dike in Choral Theology

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of slave women — captives from [[troy|Troy]], witnesses to the fall of a great house — serve as the play's primary theological interpreters. Their understanding of [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] encompasses the cosmic scale:

> Lo, the sword hovereth at the throat For Justice' sake. It scorneth not What the proud man to earth has trod. Its edge is bitter to the bone; It stabbeth on, thro' iron, thro' stone, Till it reach him who hath forgot That Ruth which is the law of God. (001-004)

Three features of this theology are noteworthy: (1) [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] is relentless — it pursues through all obstacles ("thro' iron, thro' stone"); (2) [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] is retributive — "the proud man" (the [[hubris]]tic transgressor) is its target; and (3) [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] is divine law — "the law of God" is specifically the law that the proud have "forgot," the law of Ruth (ἔλεος, mercy or pity) that tempers pure retribution.

The final chorus crystallizes the open question of the play: "Is [[orestes|Orestes]] the Third Saviour, or is his act only another link in the interminable chain of crime?" (002-121). [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] alone cannot answer this question; only the transformation accomplished in *Eumenides* — where [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] is institutionalized as civic justice rather than personal [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] — can break the chain.
