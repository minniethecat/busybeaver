## Identity

Cassandra (Κασσάνδρα), daughter of King Priam of [[troy|Troy]], is the most tragic of Greek mythology's prophetic figures — gifted with true vision by [[apollo|Apollo]] but cursed never to be believed. She appears as a central character in [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, where she foresees her own death and [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s with terrifying clarity, and is referenced in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* through the symbolism of the robe and [[the-net-and-the-robe|the net]] (002-090).

Cassandra does not appear in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* — she died before the play begins — but her prophetic voice echoes through the Endnotes, where her description of [[the-net-and-the-robe|the robe-net]] that killed [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] is cited as a crucial intertextual reference (002-124).

## The Agamemnon's Cassandra

In the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, Cassandra delivers one of the most powerful scenes in Greek tragedy. Brought to [[argos|Argos]] as [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s war prize, she stands silent before the palace, then breaks into prophetic frenzy — describing in vivid detail the crimes that have stained the [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]] and the murder about to be committed within. Her curse is that no one believes her: the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of [[argos|Argive]] elders hears her words but cannot understand them ([[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], 1072–1330).

She walks into the palace knowing she will die — the clearest-eyed character in the play, and the most powerless.

## Echoes in The Libation Bearers

Cassandra's presence in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is spectral — felt through the object that links the two plays. The robe-net displayed by [[orestes|Orestes]] at the climax is the same object Cassandra described: "a snare, a net" (002-124). The Endnotes explicitly connect [[orestes|Orestes]]' catalogue of names for the robe — "a winding-sheet, a snare, a net" — to "Cassandra in [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] 114," who calls it "a hunting-net, a snare" (002-124).

This intertextual echo is characteristically Aeschylean: the dead prophetess's words are confirmed by the live avenger's display, and the audience who heard Cassandra's unheeded warning now sees its truth laid bare.

## The Cassandra-Serpent Parallel

The Endnotes draw a further connection between Cassandra and the serpent imagery that pervades *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s words to Cassandra — "Thou, likewise, come Within" — are described as "hardly less sinister" than the serpent dream itself (002-090). Both women are summoned to their deaths by a voice whose menace they perceive but cannot escape.

## Thematic Significance

Cassandra matters for *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* because she represents the pattern of ignored prophecy that the play inherits and transforms. In the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, true prophecy is spoken and disbelieved. In *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, true prophecy — [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s dream — is received and misread. The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] traces a progression from rejected truth (Cassandra) through misinterpreted truth ([[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s dream) to institutionally validated truth (Athena's court in the *Eumenides*) (P01-001).

## In Other Versions

In [[euripides|Euripides]]' *Trojan Women*, Cassandra appears as a frenzied bride, celebrating her "marriage" to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] in the knowledge that it will lead to both their deaths — a characteristically Euripidean treatment that psychologises what [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] treats as theological mystery. In [[homer|Homer]], Cassandra is mentioned only briefly in the *Odyssey* as [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s unfortunate captive (P01-002).
