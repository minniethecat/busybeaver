Euripides (c. 480–406 BCE) was the youngest of the three canonical Athenian tragedians and the author of *[[electra|Electra]]* (413 BCE), a radical re-imagining of the [[orestes|Orestes]] myth. His treatment forms the most sustained ancient counterpoint to [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s *[[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*.

## Identity

Euripides was born on Salamis and died in Macedonia. He wrote approximately ninety-two plays, of which eighteen or nineteen survive — more than [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] and [[sophocles|Sophocles]] combined. Known for his psychological realism, rhetorical sophistication, and willingness to question traditional religion, Euripides won only four first prizes at the City Dionysia during his lifetime (compared to [[sophocles|Sophocles]]' reported eighteen), but became the most performed and influential tragedian after his death.

## Role in the Play

Although Euripides lived a generation after [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] and is not directly referenced in the *Choephoroe* text, he features prominently in Gilbert Murray's Preface as the crucial point of comparison. Murray argues that "the development of Greek tragedy moves almost straight from [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] to Euripides, with [[sophocles|Sophocles]] standing aside" (P01-006). Euripides's *[[electra|Electra]]* represents the terminus of this trajectory — a fully psychologized treatment that contrasts with [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s archaic grandeur.

Murray's comparison of the two treatments is specific and illuminating:

> "In Euripides a whole scene is given up to it. Orestes is shaken by the first sight of his mother in the distance, and actually rebels against the god or devil who has commanded him to kill her, till he is overborne by the scorn and passion of Electra's more bitter nature." (P01-006)

The contrast with [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] is stark: in [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], [[orestes|Orestes]]' scruple is swept away by "the whole great swelling rhythm of that litany of revenge"; in Euripides, it is overcome through interpersonal psychological pressure.

## Key Scenes

Euripides's *[[electra|Electra]]* (413 BCE) treats the same myth but relocates the action to a peasant's cottage, demythologizing the story in ways that highlight [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s very different aesthetic:

- **The [[anagnorisis|Recognition]]**: "Euripides uses them, but uses them in an ironic, half-mocking way" (002-029), noting that [[sophocles|Sophocles]] in his *[[electra|Electra]]* "omits the traditional [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] altogether" (002-029).

- **The Invocation**: "there is no invocation scene in [[sophocles|Sophocles]]; there is a brief one in Euripides ([[electra|Electra]] 671–685). It has great beauty" (002-044). Euripides's version is compressed and lyrical where [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s is monumental.

- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s Confrontation**: "In Euripides ([[electra|Electra]] 998 ff.), when we have some right to expect it, she bursts in upon [[orestes|Orestes]] and [[electra|Electra]] in a sudden agony of rage" (002-091), contrasting with [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] who preserves "almost superhuman grandeur" (P01-007).

## Relationships

- **[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]**: Euripides inherits and transforms [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s [[orestes|Orestes]]: "The first study is grander, tenderer, and more heroic; the second, of course, more detailed and varied and more finely poignant" (P01-006). Murray identifies "a typical difference between the two poets in the way in which [[orestes|Orestes]]' last scruples are overcome" (P01-006).

- **[[sophocles|Sophocles]]**: The forty-five years separating the *Choephoroe* (458 BCE) from Euripides's *[[electra|Electra]]* (413 BCE) were "years of very rapid artistic development" (P01-008). Euripides, not [[sophocles|Sophocles]], is the true successor to [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s theological project.

- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]**: Euripides made "a [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] deliberately and utterly different" (P01-007) from [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s — arguably a more human but less monumental figure.

## Dramatic Analysis

Euripides's technique represents the triumph of psychological realism over archaic grandeur. Murray acknowledges that "at point after point of the action it is easy to show how the two later poets refined and developed the plain lines of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], and exerted themselves to make the story more human and more probable" (P01-008). Yet Murray's judgement is ultimately weighted toward [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]: "The comparison tempts one to reflect how little such technical improvements really matter, and how dangerously near to nothingness, in the last resort, are the ingenuities of realism" (P01-008).

## In Other Versions

Euripides wrote at least two plays treating the [[orestes|Orestes]] myth: *[[electra|Electra]]* (c. 413 BCE) and *[[orestes|Orestes]]* (408 BCE). The *[[electra|Electra]]* is a systematic demythologization: [[electra|Electra]] is married to a peasant, the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene is handled with ironic self-consciousness, and the murder of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] is presented as a squalid and morally indefensible act. The Endnotes record Euripides's influence even on the text of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], suggesting that "the ideas deepened and elaborated by Euripides has been suggested by [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]" (002-032).
