# Agamemnon

Agamemnon, son of Atreus and king of Argos, is the dead father whose unquiet spirit is the invisible centre of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe*. Though he never appears onstage, every action in the play is directed toward him or motivated by him: the [[libation-ritual|libations]] poured at his tomb, the prayers spoken over his grave, the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] enacted in his name.

## Identity

- **Play**: The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers (Choephoroe), second play of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* (458 BCE); his murder is the subject of the *Agamemnon*
- **Family**: Son of Atreus; husband of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]; father of [[orestes|Orestes]], [[electra|Electra]], and Iphigenia (whom he sacrificed at Aulis to gain favourable winds for the Greek fleet)
- **Status**: Dead — murdered by [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] upon his [[exile-and-return|return]] from the Trojan War
- **Burial**: Dishonourable — his body was mutilated (hands and feet hacked away), and he was buried without mourners or proper rites (001-002)

## Role in the Play

Agamemnon is the play's absent protagonist — dead before the action begins yet more powerful in death than any living character. The *Choephoroe* is, in a sense, his play: the [[libation-ritual|libations]] that give the play its name are poured into his grave; the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene takes place at his tomb; the great *[[kommos]]* is addressed to him; and the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] that drives the plot is vengeance for his murder.

[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] treats Agamemnon's ghost with extraordinary imaginative sympathy. "The ghosts in [[homer|Homer]] could not speak till they had drunk the blood of sacrifice. Somewhat in the same way the dead Agamemnon here cannot gather his dim senses till the [[libation-ritual|drink-offering]]s have sunk into his grave. The wine and milk and honey reach his parched lips. He stirs in his sleep" (P01-004). The play's central paradox is that the dead man is the most vital presence in it.

## Key Aspects

### The Dishonoured Body
The *[[kommos]]* dwells at length on the mutilation of Agamemnon's corpse. "His hands and feet, they were hacked away from him! / Yea, she that buried him, she wrought it so" (001-002). The dishonourable burial — without mourners, without proper rites, with the body mutilated — is the wound that festers in the play's imagination and demands redress.

### The Summoning
The [[libation-ritual|libations]] do not merely honour Agamemnon — they awaken him. The extended ritual at the tomb proceeds in stages from grief to remembrance to summoning: "O Father, hearken and save, / For my sore sorrow's sake! / Children twain are above thy grave / Seeking for thee: Oh, wake!" (001-002). By the *[[kommos]]*' end, the dead king has been roused — not to visible apparition but to spiritual presence, authorizing his children's [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]].

### The Silence
Perhaps the most powerful aspect of Agamemnon in the *Choephoroe* is his silence. He never speaks — not as a ghost, not as a voice, not as a sign. He is the dead father who cannot answer his children's prayers directly, and this silence drives them deeper into grief, rage, and finally action. The play is, in part, about what the living must do when the dead cannot speak.

## Dramatic Function

Agamemnon's function is to be the object of every prayer and the justification for every killing. His unavenged death is the moral and ritual crisis that the play confronts; his awakened spirit is the authority that sanctions the violence. He is simultaneously victim, father, king, and presence — the dead centre around which the living orbit.

## Across the Trilogy

- **Agamemnon**: The living king, [[exile-and-return|return]]ed from Troy in triumph, walks the purple carpet to his death. His murder is the crime that sets the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] in motion.
- **Choephoroe**: The dead king, silent but powerful, is summoned from the underworld by his children's prayers. His unavenged spirit drives the action.
- **Eumenides**: Agamemnon is largely absent — the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] has moved beyond him to the larger questions of [[dike|justice]] and civic order that his death raised.
