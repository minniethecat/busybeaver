# Blood-Guilt

Blood-guilt (*[[blood-and-ritual-impurity|miasma]]*) is the ritual pollution that adheres to one who has shed kindred blood. In [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe*, it is the invisible stain that both demands [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] and results from it, creating the self-perpetuating cycle the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] seeks to resolve.

## Definition

In Greek religious thought, the spilling of blood — especially the blood of a family member — created a state of ritual impurity that was contagious and inherited. The *Choephoroe*'s [[the-chorus|Chorus]] describes it in visceral terms: "Has Earth once drunk withal / The blood of her child, Man, the avenging stain / Hardens, nor flows again. / A blind pain draweth the slayer" (001-002). The pollution is not merely symbolic — it physically defiles the killer, who must be excluded from temples, altars, and human fellowship until purified (001-002).

## Greek Religious Context

The concept of *[[blood-and-ritual-impurity|miasma]]* was central to Greek religious practice and law. Before entering a temple, worshippers were sprinkled with purifying water. Murderers were [[exile-and-return|exile]]d not merely as punishment but because their presence polluted the community. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] intensifies this convention: in the *Choephoroe*, Apollo's oracle threatens [[orestes|Orestes]] that if he fails to avenge his father, the Furies — "shapen of his father's blood" — will pursue him, and he will be barred from "every altar fire" and denied "harbourage" under any roof (001-002). Blood-guilt is simultaneously the spur to action and its consequence.

## Role in The Libation Bearers

The imagery of blood pervades the play. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] dreams of nursing a serpent that draws "that gout of gore" from her breast (001-002). The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] warns: "When blood is spilt, how shall a gift set free?" (001-002) — a direct challenge to [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s attempt to buy off the dead with offerings. [[orestes|Orestes]], after the killing, is shown clutching [[the-net-and-the-robe|the bloodstained robe]], his mind unravelling as he sees the Furies gathering (P01-008).

The play's central paradox is articulated by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]]: "The House hath healing for its own bitterness... It is here within" (002-056) — meaning the House can "cure bloodshed by bloodshed, sin by [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]" (002-056). The remedy for blood-guilt is more blood, a paradox the *Eumenides* will finally resolve.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

Across the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]], blood-guilt escalates and then is transformed:

- The **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** shows the blood of Iphigenia and [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] staining the [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]].
- The **Choephoroe** adds [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s and [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]]' blood to the tally, with the Furies visibly appearing to [[orestes|Orestes]].
- The **Eumenides** provides the resolution: through Athena's court, the Erinyes are transformed from avengers of blood into guardians of civic order, and [[orestes|Orestes]] is purified at [[delphi|Delphi]].
