Aeschylus (c. 525–456 BCE) was the earliest of the three great Athenian tragedians and the author of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] — *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* (*Choephoroe*), and *Eumenides* — the only complete [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] preserved from the Athenian stage (P01-001).

## Identity

Aeschylus was born at Eleusis near Athens and fought at the Battle of Marathon (490 BCE). He wrote approximately seventy to ninety plays, of which seven survive complete. The *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]*, produced in 458 BCE, was his last great work and won first prize at the City Dionysia. He died in Gela, Sicily, around 456 BCE.

## Role in the Play

Aeschylus is not a character in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* but is present throughout as the shaping intelligence behind its unique tone. Gilbert Murray, in his Preface, provides the most sustained characterization of Aeschylus's distinctive approach to the material:

> "Aeschylus, as I have said elsewhere, 'though steeped in the glory of the world of legend, would not lightly accept its judgment upon religious and moral questions, and above all would not, in that region, play at make-believe. He would not elude the horror of this story by simply not mentioning it, like Homer, or by pretending that an evil act was a good one, like Sophocles. He faces the horror; realizes it; and tries to surmount it on the sweep of a great wave of religious emotion.'" (P01-002)

This distinguishes Aeschylus fundamentally from his successors in treating the [[orestes|Orestes]] story. Where [[euripides|Euripides]] "realizes and psychologizes the horror" and [[sophocles|Sophocles]] "suppresses the religious problem and concentrates on the elements of direct passion" (P01-002), Aeschylus alone confronts the full theological weight of [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] commanded by a god.

## Key Scenes

Aeschylus's construction of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* demonstrates remarkable theatrical instinct:

- **The Opening at the Tomb**: Murray praises the prologue: "With the last lines of the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* still ringing in our minds we see, as the play opens, a young man standing with shorn hair beside a grave mound; and half the story is told in a flash" (P01-008).

- **The Kommos (Invocation Scene)**: The great antiphonal lament between [[orestes|Orestes]], [[electra|Electra]], and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] is the play's structural centre. Murray calls it "marvellous" and notes that in Aeschylus, "[[orestes|Orestes]]' scruple breaks out in the midst of the Invocation and is swept away, not specially by [[electra|Electra]], but by the whole great swelling rhythm of that litany of revenge" (P01-006).

- **[[the-nurse|The Nurse]] Scene**: A moment of tonal diversity — "the entry of the poor, loving, half-ridiculous [[the-nurse|Nurse]]" (P01-008) provides the only relief before the final horror.

- **The Final Revelation**: "the amazing scene at the end with [[the-net-and-the-robe|the bloodstained robe]], the gathering of the unseen Furies, the last struggle of [[orestes|Orestes]]' reason, and the flight of the would-be Saviour as one Accursed, never to rest again" (P01-008).

## Relationships

- **[[euripides|Euripides]]**: Aeschylus's treatment of [[orestes|Orestes]] "contains here in germ just the ideas that are so subtly developed in [[euripides|Euripides]]" (P01-006). The relationship is one of evolution: "The first study is grander, tenderer, and more heroic; the second, of course, more detailed and varied and more finely poignant" (P01-006).

- **[[sophocles|Sophocles]]**: The development of Greek tragedy "moves almost straight from Aeschylus to [[euripides|Euripides]], with [[sophocles|Sophocles]] standing aside" (P01-006) — [[sophocles|Sophocles]]' *[[electra|Electra]]* deliberately avoids the religious problem that Aeschylus centres.

- **[[homer|Homer]]**: The ghost-lore of the play draws from [[homer|Homer]] — Murray observes that "The ghosts in [[homer|Homer]] could not speak till they had drunk the blood of sacrifice. Somewhat in the same way the dead [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] here cannot gather his dim senses till the [[libation-ritual|drink-offering]]s have sunk into his grave" (P01-004).

## Dramatic Analysis

Aeschylus's play is remarkable for its fusion of the archaic and the sophisticated. Murray captures this: "Where in all literature, except Aeschylus, could one find this union of primitive ghostliness with high intellectual passion? One hand seems to reach out to the African or Polynesian, while the other clasps that of Milton or Goethe" (P01-004).

The play "produces its illusion quite sufficiently by its mere grandeur and intensity. Yet, judged on its own archaic level, it shows remarkable skill in construction, just as, amid its story of relentless revenge, it conveys a great sense of compassion" (P01-008).

## In Other Versions

Aeschylus's *Choephoroe* stands as the middle panel of the only complete tragic [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]. The [[orestes|Orestes]] myth was also treated by [[euripides|Euripides]] (*[[electra|Electra]]*, 413 BCE) and [[sophocles|Sophocles]] (*[[electra|Electra]]*, date unknown), each representing a fundamentally different approach to the same material. Murray notes "how little such technical improvements really matter, and how dangerously near to nothingness, in the last resort, are the ingenuities of realism" (P01-008) — a judgement that places Aeschylus's archaic grandeur above later refinements.
