[[euripides|Euripides]]' *[[electra|Electra]]*, produced around 413 BCE, is the defining late-fifth-century treatment of the [[orestes|Orestes]]-[[electra|Electra]] revenge myth. Written forty-five years after [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s *Choephoroe* (458 BCE), it represents a radically different approach to the same material and is the indispensable comparative text for understanding [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s distinctive achievement.

## Dramatic Conception

[[euripides|Euripides]] systematically demythologizes the story. Where [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] sets his play at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb in a "desolate expanse," [[euripides|Euripides]] relocates the action to a peasant's cottage outside [[argos|Argos]]. [[electra|Electra]] has been married off to a poor farmer — a political move by [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] to prevent her bearing noble sons who might seek revenge. The grandeur of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s ritual setting is replaced by the squalor of enforced domesticity. As Gilbert Murray notes, "The comparison tempts one to reflect how little such technical improvements really matter" (P01-008).

The [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene — central to both plays — is handled with deliberate irony in [[euripides|Euripides]]. The Endnotes observe: "[[euripides|Euripides]] uses them, but uses them in an ironic, half-mocking way" (002-029). The lock of hair, the [[recognition-and-tokens|footprints]], the woven cloth — the [[recognition-and-tokens|tokens]] that in [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] carry ritual weight — in [[euripides|Euripides]] become objects of self-conscious literary gamesmanship. The old retainer who proposes these [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] is gently mocked; [[electra|Electra]] dismisses them as absurd.

## Character Treatment

[[euripides|Euripides]]'s characterisation is consistently more psychological and less monumental:

- **[[orestes|Orestes]]**: "[[orestes|Orestes]] is shaken by the first sight of his mother in the distance, and actually rebels against the god or devil who has commanded him to kill her, till he is overborne by the scorn and passion of [[electra|Electra]]'s more bitter nature" (P01-006). [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s [[orestes|Orestes]] is swept away by the Kommos's collective rhythm; [[euripides|Euripides]]'s [[orestes|Orestes]] has a modern conscience.

- **[[electra|Electra]]**: In [[euripides|Euripides]], [[electra|Electra]] "bears the main weight of the tragedy on her own shoulders" (P01-007). [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s [[electra|Electra]] is "much less minutely and without doubt less cruelly studied" (P01-007) — she "keeps a kind of tenderness even in her prayers of hate" (P01-007). [[euripides|Euripides]]'s [[electra|Electra]] is harder, more bitter, more recognizably damaged.

- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]**: The two poets' [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]s are "deliberately and utterly different" (P01-007). "In [[euripides|Euripides]] ([[electra|Electra]] 998 ff.), when we have some right to expect it, she bursts in upon [[orestes|Orestes]] and [[electra|Electra]] in a sudden agony of rage" (002-091). [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] "preserves still the almost superhuman grandeur which was hers in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*" (P01-007). Murray's judgment is clear: "this heroine was a figure not in [[euripides|Euripides]]' style and perhaps not within his range" (P01-007).

## The Invocation Scene

[[the-great-kommos|The Great Kommos]] — the two-hundred-line antiphonal lament that is the structural apex of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s play — has no equivalent in [[euripides|Euripides]]. "There is no invocation scene in [[sophocles|Sophocles]]; there is a brief one in [[euripides|Euripides]] ([[electra|Electra]] 671–685). It has great beauty" (002-044), but it is compact, almost lyrical, and lacks the ritual accumulation that makes [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s version a "great litany growing in intensity of longing" (P01-004).

## Relationship to Aeschylus

Murray traces a direct line of influence: "The development of Greek tragedy moves almost straight from [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] to [[euripides|Euripides]], with [[sophocles|Sophocles]] standing aside" (P01-006). He compares the two treatments explicitly: "The first study is grander, tenderer, and more heroic; the second, of course, more detailed and varied and more finely poignant" (P01-006). [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] gives us a religious problem; [[euripides|Euripides]] gives us a psychological case study. Both are masterpieces, but they are masterpieces of fundamentally different kinds.
