# The Kommos United Cry

The climactic moment of the great *kommos* (lamentation) at Agamemnon's tomb, when the three parties — Orestes, Electra, and the Chorus — join their voices to summon the dead king's aid. It is the point at which mourning transforms into resolve.

## The Kommos Form

The kommos is a ritual lament performed at a tomb, combining sung lyric passages (by the Chorus and actors) with spoken dialogue. In *The Libation Bearers*, the kommos occupies a central structural position — it is the longest sustained passage in the play and the emotional engine that drives Orestes from mourning to action.

The Preface describes it as "the marvellous invocation scene" — the dramatic and ritual center of the play.

## The United Cry

At the kommos' climax, Orestes and Electra, who have been grieving separately, join their voices with the Chorus's in a single cry to the dead king. The cry is simultaneously a lament ("O great King, hear us") and a battle summons ("Be with us against thy foe!"). The living and the dead, the male and female, the royal and the servile — all divisions dissolve in the unified purpose of vengeance.

## Dramatic Transformation

The kommos performs a ritual transformation: it turns grief into anger, passive mourning into active resolve. When Orestes rises from the tomb at the kommos' end, he is no longer a grieving son but an agent of vengeance — a transformation made possible by the ritual's collective power.
