# The Nurse's Speech

A remarkable monologue spoken by Cilissa (Kilissa), the old Nurse of Orestes, in the middle of *The Libation Bearers*. Summoned by Clytemnestra to fetch Aigisthos after the (false) news of Orestes' death, the Nurse pauses to grieve for the child she raised. The speech is one of the most unconventional scenes in extant Greek tragedy — a servant's earthy, loving, comic-tinged lament that breaks the stately tragic decorum.

## Text

> The mistress bids me call Aigisthos here
> Quickly, to see these two, and learn more clear,
> As man from man, the truth of what they tell.
> Oh, to us slaves she makes it pitiable
> And grievous, and keeps hid behind her eyes
> The leaping laughter. Aye, 'tis a rich prize
> For her, and for the House stark misery,
> This news the travellers tell so trippingly.
> And, Oh, Aigisthos, he, you may be sure,
> Will laugh to hear it! … Ah, I am a poor
> Old woman! Such a tangle as they were,
> The troubles in this House, and hard to bear,
> Long years back, and all aching in my breast!
> But none that hurt like this! Through all the rest …
> Well, I was sore, but lived them down and smiled.
> But little Orestes, my heart's care, the child
> I took straight from his mother; and save me
> He had no other nurse! And, Oh, but he
> Could scream and order me to tramp the dark!
> Aye, times enough, and trouble enough, and stark
> Wasted at that! A small thing at the breast,
> That has no sense, you tend it like a beast,
> By guesswork. For he never speaks, not he,
> A babe in swaddling clothes, if thirst maybe
> Or hunger comes, or any natural need.
> The little belly takes its way. Indeed,
> 'Twas oft a prophet he wanted, not a nurse;
> And often enough my prophecies, of course,
> Came late, and then 'twas clothes to wash and dry,
> And fuller's work as much as nurse's. Aye,
> I followed both trades, from the day when first
> His father gave me Orestes to be nursed. …
> And now he is dead; and strangers come and tell
> The news to me. And this poor miserable
> Old woman must go tell the plunderer
> Who shames this house! Oh, glad he will be to hear!
> (PN 750-770)

## Context

- **Speaker**: Cilissa (the Old Nurse of [[Orestes]])
- **Addressee**: The Chorus of slave women
- **Dramatic moment**: After Orestes and Pylades have entered the palace with their false report of Orestes' death, Clytemnestra sends the Nurse to summon Aigisthos. On her way, the Nurse speaks to the Chorus, unburdening her grief.
- **Form**: rhessis (extended speech, with colloquial, exclamatory diction)

## Analysis

This speech is a striking departure from the stately conventions of Greek tragedy. Neither Sophocles nor Euripides has left any scene like it (Murray, commentary on line 750). The Nurse — a Cilician slave woman, as her name "Kilissa" suggests — speaks in a looser, more exclamatory grammar than the formal tragic register, though her metre remains correct.

The speech operates on multiple dramatic levels. At the surface, it is a servant's simple grief: the woman who nursed Orestes from infancy mourns his reported death. But beneath this, the Nurse's earthy recollections — the screaming infant who "needs a prophet, not a nurse," the endless washing of soiled clothes — create a sharp contrast with the elevated blood-vengeance that drives the main action. Her domestic world of feeding, cleaning, and tending is the anti-world of the palace's cycles of murder.

The Chorus, recognizing that the Nurse's genuine grief could jeopardize the revenge plot, intercepts her with a message that alters Clytemnestra's instructions — ensuring Aigisthos arrives without his bodyguard. The Nurse, in her simplicity, becomes an unwitting instrument of the vengeance she mourns.

Structurally, the speech provides a moment of human warmth before the matricide, making the violence to come more terrible by reminding the audience of the domestic bonds it will sever. The detail that Orestes was given to the Nurse directly by Agamemnon ("His father gave me Orestes to be nursed") links her love to the slain king, reinforcing the legitimacy of the revenge while complicating its human cost.

## In Performance

The Nurse's speech offers the actor a rare opportunity for tonal range within tragedy — moving from the urgency of her errand, to sardonic observation of Clytemnestra's hidden laughter, to the affectionate exasperation of remembering infant care, and finally to raw grief. The physical comedy of her descriptions (the infant's "little belly," the "prophet" needed to interpret wordless needs) would have provided the Athenian audience a moment of earthy relief before the horror of the coming murders. Her exit — the old woman trudging off to deliver news that will lead to Aigisthos' death — carries a dramatic irony that deepens the play's exploration of how the innocent are caught in cycles of retribution.
