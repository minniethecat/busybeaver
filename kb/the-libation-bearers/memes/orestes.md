# Orestes

Orestes, son of Agamemnon and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], is the protagonist of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* — an [[exile-and-return|exile]] returning to [[argos|Argos]] to fulfil [[apollo|Apollo]]'s command, avenge his father's death, and reclaim his throne, only to find that fulfilling his duty makes him the object of divine pursuit.

## Identity

- **Play**: The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers (Choephoroe), second play of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* (458 BCE)
- **Family**: Son of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] (king of Argos, leader of the Greek expedition to Troy) and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]; brother of [[electra|Electra]]; descendant of Pelops and Atreus, inheritor of the [[curse-of-the-house-of-atreus|family curse]]
- **Status**: Mortal — the last surviving male heir of the [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]]
- **First appearance**: Standing at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb with [[pylades|Pylades]], cutting a lock of hair as an offering (P01-004) (001-002)

## Role in the Play

Orestes is the play's avenger-protagonist, driven by a triple obligation: [[apollo|Apollo]]'s oracle at Delphi commanded him to kill the murderers under threat of horrific punishment; he swore an oath as a child in Phocis to avenge his father; and the dead [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] himself, awakened by the libations and the *[[kommos]]* at his tomb, demands [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. Orestes frames his motives precisely: "The God's command; grief for my father slain; / And mine own beggary urgeth me amain" (001-002).

Unlike the Orestes of [[sophocles|Sophocles]] or [[euripides|Euripides]], [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' character is not a psychological study but a figure caught in a religious and moral paradox. "He faces the horror; realizes it; and tries to surmount it on the sweep of a great wave of religious emotion" (P01-002). He does not kill eagerly — he wavers when [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] bares her breast — but [[pylades|Pylades]]' single speech recalls him to his oath. After the killing, his reason fractures and he flees, pursued by the Furies visible only to him (P01-008).

## Key Scenes

### The Recognition at the Tomb
Orestes watches from hiding as [[electra|Electra]] discovers his lock of hair and [[recognition-and-tokens|footprints]] at the grave. When he reveals himself, [[electra|Electra]]'s joy is immediate, but Orestes is guarded: "Hold! Ah, be not all dismayed / With joy! Our nearest is our deadliest foe" (001-002). The [[recognition-scene|recognition]] is not an end but a beginning — it authorizes the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] that follows.

### The Great Kommos
Orestes, [[electra|Electra]], and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] alternate in an extended ritual lamentation at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb, calling the dead king to wakefulness and aid. Orestes' strophes move from grief ("thee, thee I call. Father, be near thine own") to rage ("All, all dishonour, so thy story telleth it! / And for that dishonour shall the woman pay") to resolution ("Then Death may take me, let me only slay!") (001-002).

### The Killing of Aigisthos and Clytemnestra
Orestes enters the palace disguised as a Phocian traveller bearing news of his own death. He kills [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] first, then faces his mother. When [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] bares her breast and pleads, Orestes hesitates — "[[pylades|Pylades]], what shall I do? Shall I spare my mother?" (001-004) — and [[pylades|Pylades]] speaks his only lines in the play: "Where then shall [[apollo|Apollo]]'s word, / The Pythian oath, be kept?" (001-004). Orestes kills her.

### The Madness
After the killing, Orestes displays [[the-net-and-the-robe|the bloodstained robe]] and begins to lose his reason: "the amazing scene at the end with [[the-net-and-the-robe|the bloodstained robe]], the gathering of the unseen Furies, the last struggle of Orestes' reason, and the flight of the would-be Saviour as one Accursed, never to rest again" (P01-008).

## Dramatic Function

Orestes is simultaneously saviour and destroyer — the agent who cleanses the [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]] of its usurpers and the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] who incurs new [[blood-guilt|pollution]]. His dramatic function is to be the point at which the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s competing moral claims converge: obedience to [[apollo|Apollo]] versus the prohibition on [[the-mother-murder|kin-murder]], filial duty to the father versus filial duty to the mother, divine command versus human compassion. The *Choephoroe* does not resolve these tensions — it intensifies them until the *Eumenides* can provide the resolution through civic and divine institutions.

## In Other Versions

[[sophocles|Sophocles]]' *Electra* shifts the dramatic centre from Orestes to [[electra|Electra]], making Orestes a more straightforward avenger whose matricide is treated as an unambiguous triumph rather than a source of religious pollution. The recognition scene is prolonged and the moral complexity of the Aeschylean version is diminished.

[[euripides|Euripides]]' *Electra* goes further, presenting Orestes as hesitant, even cowardly — he refuses to enter the palace, delegates the killing of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] to [[electra|Electra]], and is consumed by guilt after the act. Euripides' Orestes is a psychological case study rather than a religious figure, reflecting the later fifth century's more skeptical attitude toward divine motivation.

[[homer|Homer]]'s *Odyssey* (Book 1, 3) treats Orestes' vengeance as a positive exemplum — the modelo of filial piety that Telemachus should emulate — without any hint of the matricidal pollution that dominates Aeschylus' treatment.
