# Pylades

Pylades, son of Strophius of Phocis, is the most economical character in Greek tragedy — he appears alongside [[orestes|Orestes]] throughout [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe*, speaks exactly once, and with those few words determines the play's outcome. His single speech, recalling [[orestes|Orestes]] to his oath when he wavers before killing [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], is one of the most consequential interventions in surviving Greek drama.

## Identity

- **Play**: The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers (Choephoroe), second play of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* (458 BCE)
- **Family**: Son of Strophius, king of Phocis, who sheltered the [[exile-and-return|exile]]d [[orestes|Orestes]] in childhood
- **Status**: Mortal — companion and "battle-guest" of [[orestes|Orestes]]
- **First appearance**: Standing with [[orestes|Orestes]] at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb as the play opens (001-002)

## Role in the Play

Pylades is present from the play's opening — [[orestes|Orestes]] refers to him as "this man, Pylades" (001-002) and later as "their guest and battle-guest of yore" (001-002). But he remains silent through the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene, the great *[[kommos]]*, and the planning of the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. His silence is a dramatic choice: he is the witness, not the actor — until the moment when his intervention becomes necessary.

That moment comes in the climactic confrontation with [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]. [[orestes|Orestes]], faced with his mother baring her breast and pleading for mercy, wavers and turns to Pylades: "Pylades, what shall I do? Shall I spare my mother?" (001-004). Pylades' reply is the only speech he makes in the entire play: "Where then shall [[apollo|Apollo]]'s word, / The Pythian oath, be kept? Let all men hate / Thee, rather than the gods" (001-004). This intervention recalls [[orestes|Orestes]] to his duty and seals [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s [[moira|fate]].

## Key Scenes

### The Opening
Pylades stands silently beside [[orestes|Orestes]] at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb. He is identified as the companion who shared [[orestes|Orestes]]' [[exile-and-return|exile]] in Phocis, and his presence establishes that [[orestes|Orestes]] does not act alone — he has a witness, a friend, and a representative of the world beyond [[argos|Argos]].

### The Intervention
Pylades' single speech is arguably the most important three lines in the play. It resolves the moral crisis that [[orestes|Orestes]] cannot resolve himself, recalling him to the religious obligation ([[apollo|Apollo]]'s command, the Pythian oath) that requires the killing. The speech is not argument but assertion — it does not debate the morality of [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] but declares the priority of divine command over human compassion.

## Dramatic Function

Pylades is the play's moral backstop — the external voice that recalls the protagonist to his duty when internal conviction wavers. His function is structurally identical to that of a deus ex machina but dramatically more satisfying: he is not a god descending from the machine but a human friend whose loyalty to the divine command is unwavering. His silence before and after his single speech makes the intervention more powerful — when the man who never speaks finally speaks, everyone listens.

## Across the Trilogy

- **Choephoroe**: Pylades appears as [[orestes|Orestes]]' silent companion and speaks once.
- **Eumenides**: Pylades does not appear — his dramatic function is complete once the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] is accomplished.
- **Later tradition**: In [[euripides|Euripides]]' *[[electra|Electra]]* and *[[orestes|Orestes]]*, Pylades becomes a speaking character and [[orestes|Orestes]]' co-conspirator in more developed ways — but [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' minimalist treatment remains the most dramatically effective.
