## Definition

Peripeteia (Greek περιπέτεια, "a turning about" or "reversal") is a key concept in Greek dramatic theory, most famously defined by Aristotle in the *Poetics* as "a change by which the action veers round to its opposite" (Poetics 1452a). It describes the moment in a tragedy when the protagonist's fortune suddenly and decisively reverses — typically from prosperity to adversity (001-001–005).

In [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, peripeteia is not merely a single dramatic beat but the structural principle governing the entire second half of the play, embodied in the swift and violent reversal of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s position (P01-007).

## Aeschylean Dramatic Architecture

[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] constructs the reversal across a sustained dramatic arc rather than a single moment. The play opens with [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] as [[clytemnestra|queen of Argos]], secure in power alongside [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] after the murder of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]. The arrival of [[orestes|Orestes]] — disguised as a Phôkian merchant bearing news of his own death — initiates the mechanism of reversal (001-003).

The peripeteia reaches its climax in the confrontation scene. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], who moments earlier believed herself safe and her dangerous son dead, suddenly finds herself facing the living [[orestes|Orestes]] with a sword at her throat. The reversal is compressed into a handful of lines: from "To kill thy mother, Child: is that thy thought?" to her death and public display (001-004).

## The Clytemnestra Reversal

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s peripeteia is particularly potent because [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] has engineered a complete inversion of the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*'s power dynamic. In the first play of the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]], [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] stood over the body of her slain husband, triumphant. In *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, she lies dead beside [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]], and [[orestes|Orestes]] displays the same robe-net in which [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] was trapped — a visual token that seals the reversal (P01-007).

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] marks this turning point with explicit language: "The prayers of the Lost have found their answer / The dead in the earth are awake" — the reversal is simultaneously human action and cosmic realignment (001-004).

## The Nurse's Unwitting Role

A subtler dimension of the peripeteia unfolds through the [[the-nurse|Nurse]]'s scene. Sent by [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] to summon [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]], the [[the-nurse|Nurse]] is persuaded by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] to alter the message — instructing [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] to come without his bodyguard. This small [[guile-and-justice|deception]] transforms a minor domestic errand into the hinge upon which the entire reversal turns: [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] arrives unguarded and is killed, leaving [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] exposed (001-004).

[[the-nurse|The Nurse]], a figure of pathos who "loved him [[[orestes|Orestes]]] and his father too," becomes an unwitting instrument of peripeteia — a characteristically [[tragic-irony|Aeschylean irony]] in which the most powerless figure in the house sets the reversal in motion (001-004).

## Fortune's Wheel in Aeschylean Theology

For [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], peripeteia is never merely dramatic mechanics but carries theological weight. The reversal of fortune is the visible expression of [[justice-and-vengeance|divine justice]] ([[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]]). The wind that "shivers to its [[moira|fate]]" embodies a cosmos in which excess (Hubris) generates its own reversal — the balance that Zeus maintains (001-004).

The Endnotes underscore this theological dimension: "the wind is cold, but Zeus may change the wind" (002-017). Fortune's reversal is not random — it is the mechanism through which the moral order reasserts itself.

## Relation to the Oresteia Trilogy

The peripeteia of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is structurally linked to the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s overarching arc. The *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* ends with [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] triumphant but morally polluted; the reversal of *Choephoroe* destroys her but leaves [[orestes|Orestes]] in turn polluted by [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] (P01-001). This double reversal — victor becomes victim, avenger becomes hunted — is the engine that drives the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] toward its resolution in the *Eumenides*, where the cycle of reversal is finally broken by the institution of civic law (P01-006).
