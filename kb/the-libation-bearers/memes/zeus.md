## Identity

Zeus, son of Cronos, is the king of the Olympian gods and the supreme deity of the Greek pantheon. In [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]*, he holds a distinctive theological role: not merely the wielder of the thunderbolt but the cosmic guarantor of [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] — the moral order that balances crime with punishment and ultimately transforms [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] into [[dike|justice]] (001-002).

The *[[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* invokes Zeus at every critical juncture: in [[electra|Electra]]'s prayer at the tomb, in the [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s odes, and in [[orestes|Orestes]]' justification of the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]]. Zeus is not a character who appears on stage but the divine presence whose moral logic structures the entire action (001-004).

## Role in The Libation Bearers

Zeus appears in the play through three primary modes of invocation:

**[[electra|Electra]]'s Prayer**: At [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb, [[electra|Electra]] appeals directly to Zeus: "Zeus, Oh, grant to me this day My [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]" (001-002). This prayer frames the entire [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] as divinely sanctioned — not personal revenge but the execution of Zeus's cosmic [[dike|justice]].

**The [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s Theological Commentary**: The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] articulates a developed Zeus-theology. "The wind is cold, but Zeus may change the wind" — this line, spoken at the moment of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s approach, crystallises the Aeschylean conception of Zeus as the power who can reverse fortune, turning the wind of [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] into the calm of [[dike|justice]] (001-004).

**[[orestes|Orestes]]' Justification**: When [[orestes|Orestes]] stands over his mother's body, he invokes [[apollo|Apollo]]'s command — but [[apollo|Apollo]]'s authority derives from Zeus. "[[apollo|Loxias]], [[apollo|Lord of Pytho]], who decreed" the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] acts as Zeus's prophetic agent at [[delphi|Delphi]] (001-004).

## Zeus Chthonios

A distinctive feature of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is the invocation of Zeus Chthonios — Zeus of the Underworld. The Endnotes explain that [[hermes|Hermes]], the [[hermes|Guide of the Dead]], "is son of Zeus Chthonios" — the aspect of Zeus that rules beneath the earth as well as above it (002-008).

This dual aspect — Zeus above and Zeus below — is essential to the play's theology. The dead are not beyond Zeus's jurisdiction; the prayers at the tomb reach both the Olympian and the Chthonic Zeus, uniting the realms of the living and the dead in a single cosmic order (001-002).

## Key Scenes

- **The tomb prayer (001-002)**: [[electra|Electra]]'s invocation of Zeus opens the ritual action and establishes the divine framework for the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]].
- **[[the-nurse|The Nurse]]'s wisdom (001-004)**: "The wind is cold, but Zeus may change the wind" — a moment of unexpected theological insight from the humblest character.
- **[[orestes|Orestes]]' defence (001-004)**: The appeal to [[apollo|Loxias]] ([[apollo|Apollo]]) as Zeus's prophetic agent.

## Relationships

- [[apollo]] — son and prophetic agent of Zeus, delivers the [[delphi|Delphi]]c command to [[orestes|Orestes]]
- [[hermes]] — son of Zeus Chthonios, guide of souls and warder of the underworld
- [[athena]] — daughter of Zeus, casts the deciding vote in the Eumenides
- [[agamemnon]] — the murdered king whose cause Zeus is invoked to champion
- [[orestes]] — the avenger who acts under Zeus's cosmic sanction

## In Other Versions

In [[sophocles|Sophocles]]' *[[electra|Electra]]*, Zeus is invoked but does not carry the same systematic theological weight — [[sophocles|Sophocles]] suppresses the religious problem in favour of direct passion. In [[euripides|Euripides]]' *[[electra|Electra]]*, the divine apparatus is treated with characteristic scepticism. Only [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] builds the Zeus-theology as the structural foundation of the entire [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] (P01-002).
