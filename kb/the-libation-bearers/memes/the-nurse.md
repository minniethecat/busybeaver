# The Nurse

The Nurse (*Cilissa*), [[orestes|Orestes]]' childhood nurse, appears in a single scene of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* — yet that scene is, as the preface notes, one of the play's "varied moments": "the entry of the poor, loving, half-ridiculous Nurse" (P01-008). Her genuine, uncalculated grief at the false report of [[orestes|Orestes]]' death provides the play's only moment of unfeigned human emotion, and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s alteration of her message to [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] is a crucial plot pivot.

## Identity

- **Play**: The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers (Choephoroe), second play of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* (458 BCE)
- **Family**: Servant of the [[house-of-atreus|House of Atreus]]; she nursed [[orestes|Orestes]] from infancy — "the child I took straight from his mother; and save me / He had no other nurse" (001-004)
- **Status**: Mortal — a household slave
- **First appearance**: Emerging from the palace in tears, sent by [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] to summon [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] (001-004)

## Role in the Play

The Nurse's scene serves both an emotional and a structural function. Emotionally, her grief for the (supposedly) dead [[orestes|Orestes]] is the play's only moment of love untainted by [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]], hatred, or duty. She recalls his infancy with tender specificity: his crying for food, his soiling his swaddling clothes — the unglamorous realities of childcare that no other character in Greek tragedy mentions. "The poor, loving, half-ridiculous Nurse" (P01-008) is [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' most human creation in this play.

Structurally, she is the unwitting instrument of [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]]' death. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] sends her to summon [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] with his bodyguard, but the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] persuades her to alter the message: "Tell him to come alone... that the bearer of good news may be well received" (001-004). The Nurse, simple and trusting, agrees — and [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] arrives unguarded to meet [[orestes|Orestes]]' sword.

## Key Scenes

### The Nurse's Lament
The Nurse's speech is unique in the *Choephoroe* for its unstudied, almost comic quality. She does not speak in elevated tragic diction but in the language of daily life: nappies, feeding, sleepless nights. Her grief is not theatrical but genuine — and the contrast with the ritualised lamentation of the *[[kommos]]* is deliberate. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] shows that grief can be simple and human, not only cosmic and ritual.

### The Altered Message
The [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s manipulation of the Nurse is one of the play's most morally complex moments. The Nurse is a simple woman, loyal to her masters and genuinely grieving — and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] exploits her simplicity to ensure [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]]' death. The scene complicates the audience's sympathy: the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] is just, but its instruments include the [[guile-and-justice|deception]] of the innocent.
