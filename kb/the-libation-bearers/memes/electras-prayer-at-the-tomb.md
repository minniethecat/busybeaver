[[electra|Electra]]'s prayer at the tomb of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], delivered as she prepares to pour the [[libation-ritual|libations]] sent by [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]. It is a speech caught between ritual obligation and moral horror — a woman unsure what words are safe or right to speak over the grave of a murdered father.

## Text

> Ye thrallèd women, tirers of the bower,
> Since ye are with me in this suppliant hour,
> Your escort giving, give your counsel too.
> What speech have I for utterance, when I sue
> With offerings to the dead? What word of love,
> What prayer to reach my father from above?
> "To dear Lord," shall I say, "due gifts I bear
> From loving mistress" … when they come from her?
> I dare not. And I cannot find the word
> To speak, when offerings like these are poured …
> Or shall I pray him, as men's custom is,
> To send to them who pay these offices
> Requital due … for murder and for pride?
> Or, as in silence and in shame he died,
> In shame and silence shall I pour this urn
> Of offering to the dust, and pouring turn,
> As men cast out some foulness they abhor,
> And fling the cup, and fly, and look no more?
> Share with me, Friends, this burden of strange thought.
> One hate doth make us one. Oh, hide not aught
> For fear of what may fall us! Destiny
> Waiteth alike for them that men call free,
> And them by others mastered. At thine ease
> Speak, if thou knowest of wiser words than these. (001-002)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[electra|Electra]], [[electra|daughter of Agamemnon]] and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]
- **Addressee**: The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of bondmaids (primary); [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s ghost (implied)
- **Dramatic moment**: [[electra|Electra]] stands at her father's tomb, holding the [[libation-ritual|libations]] sent by the very woman who murdered him. The offerings are a propitiatory gesture from [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], who has been troubled by a nightmare and seeks to appease the dead. [[electra|Electra]] must decide how to use these offerings — as her mother intends, or as a weapon.
- **Form**: rhessis (extended speech) with stichomythic exchange with the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] Leader following

## Analysis

This speech is remarkable for its dramatic honesty. [[electra|Electra]] does not know what to say, and she says so. The speech moves through a series of rejected possibilities — each one articulated and dismissed — in a rhetorical structure that exposes the character's paralysis. Murray's comment that in [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] "almost all her words are beautiful, and she keeps a kind of tenderness even in her prayers of hate" (P01-007) is perfectly illustrated here. Her hate "is based on love, and it has not eaten into her nor left her poisoned" (P01-007).

The speech is structured as three rejected options and one plea for help:

1. Praying as though the gifts come from a "loving mistress" — impossible, given who sent them
2. Praying for "requital due … for murder and for [[hubris|pride]]" — the honest prayer, but politically dangerous
3. Pouring the offering "in shame and silence" and fleeing — a physical gesture of rejection without words
4. Asking the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] for counsel — the only honest option, turning the private dilemma into collective deliberation

The final lines — "Destiny / Waiteth alike for them that men call free, / And them by others mastered" — contain a democratic theology: the gods' [[dike|justice]] applies equally, regardless of social station. This is a characteristically Aeschylean insistence on cosmic impartiality.

## In Performance

On stage, [[electra|Electra]] stands before the grave mound, the libation vessel in her hands. The speech's physicality is important: the "pouring turn" and "fling the cup" gestures are rejected but vividly imagined, and an actor might mime them. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] surrounds her — "since ye are with me in this suppliant hour" — creating a tableau of collective female mourning. The speech ends not in resolution but in the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] Leader's terse, practical reply: "Pray as thou pourest: To all hearts of love …" The ritual proceeds, but the dilemma has been fully aired.
