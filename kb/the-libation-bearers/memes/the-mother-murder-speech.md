# The Mother-Murder Speech

The central moral argument of *The Libation Bearers*, in which [[Orestes]] justifies the killing of his mother [[Clytemnestra]] to the Chorus. The speech is not a single monologue but a sequence of declarations spanning the play — from the tomb invocation through the post-matricide display of the bodies — in which Orestes repeatedly invokes [[Apollo]]'s divine command as the warrant for the matricide. The speech dramatises the Oresteia's deepest ethical problem: can an act of kin-killing ever be righteous, even when commanded by a god?

## Text

> [The speech unfolds across two key moments: first at the tomb, as Orestes reveals Apollo's oracle; then after the killings, as he displays the bodies.]
>
> Oh, Loxias shall not mock my great desire,
> Who spoke his divine promise, charging me
> To thread this peril to the extremity:
> Yea, raised his awful voice and surging told
> To my hot heart of horrors stormy-cold
> Till I seek out those murderers, brow for brow,
> And slay them. ...
> Behold your linkèd conquerors! Behold
> My Father's foes, the spoilers of the fold!
> Oh, lordly were these twain, when thronèd high,
> And lovely now, to lovers' dreaming eye,
> Even in death, these twain! ...
> 'Twas she devised this treason 'gainst her own,
> Against the man whose children she had borne.
> What shall I call her, though she were not known?
> An adder? A vile crawling thing, that stings
> And rots the flesh whereto its slime but clings?
> A snake? No snake, but something that defies
> All names! ...
> (PN 001-002-001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Orestes]], son of [[Agamemnon]] and [[Clytemnestra]]
- **Addressee**: The Chorus of slave women of the palace
- **Dramatic moment**: The speech spans two critical junctures. At the tomb (PN 001-002), Orestes reveals that Apollo himself commanded the revenge, threatening him with "horrors stormy-cold" if he failed. After the killings (PN 001-004), Orestes displays the bodies of Aigisthos and Clytemnestra, along with the robe in which Agamemnon was trapped, making his case to the people of Argos.
- **Form**: rhesis (extended speech), with moments of increasing incoherence as the Furies' approach begins

## Analysis

The speech is the moral pivot of the Oresteia. Orestes' defense operates on two registers simultaneously: the religious (Apollo commanded it) and the evidentiary (look at the robe, look at what she did). The tension between these two registers — divine mandate versus physical evidence — reflects the trilogy's larger movement from archaic blood-vengeance toward civic jurisprudence.

The invocation of Apollo ("Loxias shall not mock my great desire") is crucial. Loxias is an epithet meaning "the Oblique One" or "the Ambiguous One" — Apollo in his aspect as giver of oracles that are true but difficult to interpret. Orestes' confidence that the command was clear will be tested in *The Eumenides* when the Furies challenge the justice of that same command. The speech's "increasing incoherence," noted by scholars (PN 002-035), is not textual corruption but psychological realism: the horror of what Orestes has done already begins to fracture his reason, even as he defends it.

The display of the robe — the "nets of Agamemnon" — functions as physical evidence in a proto-legal sense. Orestes is not merely avenging; he is building a case. This forensic impulse looks forward to the trial at the Areopagus in *The Eumenides*, where Athena will transform the cycle of vengeance into a legal proceeding.

## In Performance

The speech's dramatic power lies in the contrast between Orestes' rhetorical certainty and his visible psychological disintegration. The actor must convey a man who is simultaneously vindicated and undone. The display of the bodies and the robe is a coup de théâtre — the palace doors open to reveal the corpses, and Orestes gestures to them as evidence while the Chorus and audience take in the visual horror of what justice looks like.
