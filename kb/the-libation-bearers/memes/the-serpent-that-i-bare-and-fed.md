# The Serpent That I Bare and Fed

At the climactic moment of *The Libation Bearers*, as Orestes forces his mother toward the palace to kill her, Clytemnestra suddenly understands: the serpent of her dream was her own son. Her cry of recognition is one of the most charged lines in Greek tragedy, collapsing the symbolic and the real into a single moment of terrible clarity.

## Text

> Ah God! The serpent that I bare and fed!
> (PN 283)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Clytemnestra]], queen of Argos, wife (now widow) of Agamemnon and mother of Orestes
- **Addressee**: [[Orestes]] (and herself — the line has the quality of a horrified realization spoken aloud)
- **Dramatic moment**: Orestes has revealed his identity and his purpose. He has driven Clytemnestra into the palace. She has bared her breast, appealed to their mother-son bond. Orestes hesitates, turns to Pylades. Pylades speaks his three lines. Orestes' resolve returns. At this moment, Clytemnestra realizes that her prophetic dream has come true.
- **Form**: rhessis (single line of horrified recognition within the stichomythic exchange)

## Analysis

This single line is the dramatic fulcrum on which the matricide turns. Earlier in the play, Clytemnestra's dream — that she gave birth to a serpent which drew blood along with milk when she nursed it — was reported by the Chorus. Orestes interpreted the dream as a prophecy of his own role: "I, the beast, the serpent, even I shall slay her" (PN 209). Now, in the moment before death, Clytemnestra sees the same truth.

The line works on multiple registers. Literally, it describes the biological fact of motherhood — she bore and nursed this child. Metaphorically, it acknowledges that the child she raised has become her destroyer. Mythologically, it connects to the traditional belief that certain serpents were "matricides" — at birth they tore and killed their mother (Herodotus 3.109, Euripides' *Orestes* 479). The serpent imagery also links Clytemnestra's death to the broader symbolism of the House of Atreus, where the line between human and monstrous, parent and child, is repeatedly violated.

Murray notes a fascinating piece of Aeschylean technique: in the original Greek, Orestes' words immediately before this line are arranged to produce almost exactly the word "hisses" (σοὐρίζει for σοι ὁρίζει = συρίζει). The hissing serpent is not just a metaphor — it is sounded in the very texture of the verse.

The line also marks the psychological collapse of a character who has maintained "almost superhuman grandeur" (Murray) throughout both *Agamemnon* and her scenes in this play. Until this moment, Clytemnestra has controlled every situation she enters. The recognition of the dream's meaning — and her inability to have prevented its fulfillment — is the crack through which her power drains away.

## In Performance

The line requires a shift from Clytemnestra's earlier defiance to something more primal — the horror of a parent who realizes she has nurtured her own destroyer. The actor must convey not just fear but the special horror of recognition: this was foretold, she was warned, and she did not — could not — prevent it. The single line may be followed by a physical collapse, as the queen who earlier called for "an axe to slay or be slain" (PN 889) is reduced to a mother facing death at her son's hands.
