# The Libation Ritual

The libations that give the *Choephoroe* its name are poured at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of [[the-chorus|slave-women]], accompanied by [[electra|Electra]], in the play's opening scene. Sent by [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] after her dream of the serpent, the offerings become — through [[electra|Electra]]'s subversive prayer — the mechanism of her destruction.

## Summary

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], terrified by her dream, sends the [[the-chorus|slave-women]] with offerings to appease [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s angry spirit. But [[electra|Electra]], guided by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] Leader, transforms the ritual: instead of appeasement, she prays for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. "The offerings as an appeal for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]" (002-023) invert the ritual's intended purpose. What was meant to protect the murderess becomes the instrument that summons the avenger.

## Dramatic Significance

The libation ritual establishes the play's central dynamic: the inversion of ritual. Throughout the *Choephoroe*, religious forms are turned against their intended purposes — appeasement becomes summons, lamentation becomes war-cry, and [[catharsis|purification]] generates new [[blood-guilt|pollution]]. The play's title names not a character but an act, underscoring that ritual — its power and its perversion — is the true subject of the drama.
