# Clytemnestra's Dream Speech

The dream that Clytemnestra recounts — giving birth to a serpent that draws blood along with milk — is one of the most powerful prophetic images in Greek tragedy. Though Clytemnestra herself never speaks the dream directly on stage, it is reported by the Chorus Leader and interpreted by Orestes, making it a dramatic pivot point that transforms the ritual lamentation at the tomb into a plan for vengeance.

## Text

> She bore to life, she said, a Serpent Thing. (PN: 001-002)
>
> In swathing clothes she lapt it like a child. (PN: 001-002)
>
> It craved for meat, that dragon of the wild? (PN: 001-002)
>
> Yes; in the dream she gave it her own breast. (PN: 001-002)
>
> And took no scathing from the evil beast? (PN: 001-002)
>
> The milk ran into blood. So deep it bit. (PN: 001-002)

And Clytemnestra's response:

> And she, appalled, came shrieking out of sleep;
> And many a torch, long blinded in the deep
> Of darkness, in our chambers burst afire
> To cheer the Queen. Then spake she her desire,
> To send, as a swift medicine for the dread
> That held her, these peace offerings to the dead.
> (PN: 001-002)

Orestes' interpretation:

> Behold, I pray this everlasting Earth,
> I pray my father's grave, they bring to birth
> In fullness all this dream. And here am I
> To read its heart and message flawlessly.
> Seeing that this serpent, born whence I was born,
> Wore the same swathing-bands these limbs had worn,
> Fanged the same breast that suckled me of yore,
> And through the sweet milk drew that gout of gore;
> And seeing she understood, and sore afeared
> Shrieked: therefore it must be that, having reared
> A birth most ghastly, she in wrath shall die:
> And I, the beast, the serpent, even I
> Shall slay her! Be it so. The dream speaks clear.
> (PN: 001-002)

## Context

- **Speaker**: The Chorus Leader reports Clytemnestra's words; Orestes interprets the dream
- **Addressee**: Orestes, Electra, and the Chorus
- **Dramatic moment**: At Agamemnon's tomb, immediately after the great kommos (invocation of the dead) and the recognition scene. Orestes pauses in his planning to ask why Clytemnestra sent the offerings — "Why sent she this drink-offering? Hoped she by late atonement to undo / That wrong eternal?" (PN: 001-002). The Leader's answer — the dream — transforms the scene from ritual mourning into active plotting.
- **Form**: Stichomythia (the dream is reported in rapid line-by-line exchange) transitioning to rhesis (Orestes' extended interpretation)

## Analysis

The serpent dream functions as a divine sanction for the matricide. Orestes seizes upon it immediately — "The dream is come. The man shall follow it" — and provides an extended interpretation identifying himself with the serpent: "Seeing that this serpent, born whence I was born, / Wore the same swathing-bands, had the same mouth / That loved the breast that nourished me to youth" (PN: 001-002).

The image of the serpent drawing blood along with milk is a masterful compression of the play's central conflict. The nurturing breast and the venomous bite coexist in a single image, just as Orestes is simultaneously Clytemnestra's beloved son and her destined killer. The dream makes the matricide a fulfillment of prophecy rather than a simple act of revenge — Orestes is the serpent by birth, not by choice.

### Clytemnestra's Shriek as Self-Interpretation

A crucial dramatic detail often overlooked: Clytemnestra does not send the dream to an interpreter. She shrieks. As the Chorus reports, "she, appalled, came shrieking out of sleep" (PN: 001-002). This shriek is itself an act of interpretation — a recognition, at the pre-rational level, of what the dream means — and it is this spontaneous terror, not any priestly reading, that sets the libation ritual in motion. Had she taken the dream to a skilled interpreter who might have read it favourably, the consequences might have been averted (PN: 002-017). But the shriek has already named the serpent for what it is: her killer.

### The Serpent as Matricide in Greek Folklore

The serpent is not an arbitrary dream-symbol. In Greek and North African folklore, certain serpents — the asps of Libya and "divers other serpent things" — were known as "matricides": at birth they tore and killed their mother (PN: 002-068). Clytemnestra's dream draws on this deep folkloric tradition. The serpent she births and nurses is, by its very nature, the creature that kills the mother who bore it. Orestes' interpretation is not merely clever; it is, in the play's symbolic logic, inevitable.

### The Two Recognizing Moments

The dream scene mirrors and inverts the recognition scene that precedes it. In the recognition, tokens of identity — hair, footprints, weaving — prove that Orestes is who he claims to be. In the dream scene, the symbolic token — the serpent — proves what Orestes must become. The first recognition restores family bonds; the second recognition demands their violent dissolution. The play's tragic structure turns on this pivot: recognition leads not to reunion but to murder.

## In Performance

The Leader's report of the dream shifts the play's emotional register from the ecstatic joy of recognition to the grim determination of plotting murder. The stichomythic exchange between Orestes and the Leader — short, urgent lines — creates a sense of mounting momentum. Orestes' extended interpretation of the dream is a rhesis that builds rhetorical intensity, culminating in his declaration that he will "fulfill this dream" by becoming the serpent.

At the play's climax, when Orestes drives Clytemnestra toward death, she cries out "Ah God! The serpent that I bare and fed!" (PN: 001-004). Orestes answers: "Surely of truth prophetic is the dread / That walketh among dreams." The circle closes: the dream, the shriek, the interpretation, and the killing become one continuous action, each moment pre-contained in the one before.
