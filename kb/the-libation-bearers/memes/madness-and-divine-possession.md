# Madness and Divine Possession

the Furies' pursuit of Orestes manifests as madness — a state of divine possession that is both punishment and revelation, blurring the line between sanity and divine affliction.

## In The Libation Bearers

The play addresses this through its central dramatic situation: Orestes is caught between divine command and moral horror, between the demand for justice and the reality of pollution. Each scene in which tension builds — the recognition, the false report, the confrontation — draws its power from the audience's awareness of truths that the characters on stage cannot see.

## Dramatic Significance

Aeschylus uses this theme not merely as decoration but as a structural principle. The play's architecture — the delayed revelation, the layered deceptions, the climactic unmasking — is built upon it. The form of the tragedy itself embodies the theme.

## Across the Trilogy

The Oresteia traces this theme from the private chamber of the *Agamemnon* through the crisis of *The Libation Bearers* to its resolution in the *Eumenides*, where civic institutions replace archaic mechanisms.
