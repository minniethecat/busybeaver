# Orestes the Broken Charioteer

After killing Clytemnestra, Orestes displays the bodies and the bloodstained robe to the people of Argos, justifying his act as obedience to Apollo's command. But as he speaks, his reason begins to fracture. The image of the broken charioteer — a mind losing control of its own course — marks the first visible onset of the madness that will drive him through the remainder of the trilogy.

## Text

> Yet wait: for I would have you understand.
> The end I know not. But methinks I steer
> Unseeing, like some broken charioteer,
> By curbless visions borne. And at my heart
> A thing of terror knocketh, that will start
> Sudden a-song, and she must dance to hear.
>
> But while I am still not mad, I here declare
> To all who love me, and confess, that I
> Have slain my mother, not unrighteously;
> Who with my father's blood hath stained the sod
> Of Argos and drawn down the wrath of God.
>
> And the chief spell that wrought me to the deed
> Is Loxias, Lord of Pytho, who decreed
> His high commandment: if this thing I dare,
> He lays on me no sin: if I forbear …
> I cannot speak his judgment: none can know
> The deeps thereof, no arrow from the bow
> Out-top it.
> (PN 293)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Orestes]], having just killed his mother Clytemnestra
- **Addressee**: The people of Argos (and the gathered household)
- **Dramatic moment**: The palace doors have been thrown open. Orestes stands over the bodies of Aigisthos and Clytemnestra while attendants hold the great red robe in which Agamemnon was murdered. He addresses the crowd in what he intends as a formal justification of the matricide. But the speech veers from legal argument into something darker — a dawning awareness that his mind is slipping from his control.
- **Form**: rhessis (extended speech that fractures into pre-madness)

## Analysis

The broken charioteer image is one of Aeschylus' most powerful metaphors for the loss of reason. The charioteer — the rational mind that guides — has been "broken," and the horses (the "curbless visions") now run uncontrolled. The image draws on the Platonic/ Homeric metaphor of reason as charioteer of the passions, but inverts it: Orestes is not giving in to passion; he is being driven by forces — divine and psychological — that overwhelm his capacity for control.

The speech has a distinctive dramatic rhythm. It begins as a formal legal defense: Orestes wants the Argives to "understand" and "bear witness" when Menelaus returns. He confesses to the killing but claims it was "not unrighteously" — a careful legal distinction. He invokes Apollo (Loxias) as the authority behind the act, framing the matricide as obedience rather than murder.

But the formal argument is interrupted by a different voice — the "thing of terror" knocking at his heart, "that will start sudden a-song, and she must dance to hear." The "she" is ambiguous: is it the heart itself, a Fury, or madness personified? Whatever it is, Orestes is already becoming a spectator to his own disintegration. The phrase "while I am still not mad" acknowledges what he and the audience both know: the madness is coming.

The speech ends with an unfinished sentence — "if I forbear … I cannot speak his judgment." Orestes cannot articulate what Apollo's punishment would have been for disobedience. The broken syntax mirrors the broken mind. Moments later, the Furies become visible to Orestes alone: "You cannot see them; I alone can see." The broken charioteer has lost his course entirely.

## In Performance

This speech demands a performance that moves from control toward its loss. The opening lines should have the measured quality of a legal defense. But as the broken charioteer image emerges, the actor's physical control should begin to fracture — perhaps a hand finding its way to the temple, eyes losing focus on the audience, breath becoming irregular. The line about the "thing of terror" that "will start sudden a-song" may be accompanied by a physical start — Orestes reacting to something the audience cannot see. The final unfinished sentence should trail into silence, leaving the Chorus to attempt comfort with words that cannot reach him. In the original staging, this moment bridges the display of the bodies (a scene of triumph) and the first appearance of the Furies (a scene of terror), making the speech the pivot between victory and its terrible cost.
