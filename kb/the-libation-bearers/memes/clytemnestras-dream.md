# Clytemnestra's Dream

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s dream of the serpent is the turning-point of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* — a divinely sent omen that drives the Queen to send [[libation-ritual|libations]] to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb, unwittingly setting in motion the very [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] she seeks to avert.

## Definition

In Greek religious thought, dreams were not psychological phenomena but divine visitations (*oneiroi*) sent by the gods or the dead. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s dream belongs to the category of symbolic or allegorical dreams requiring interpretation — a serpent born from her body, wrapped in swaddling clothes, drawing blood instead of milk from her breast (001-002). The dream is both a revelation of her crime and a prophecy of her punishment: "The dream is come. The man shall follow it" (001-002).

## Greek Religious Context

Dream interpretation was a recognized religious practice in ancient Greece, with professional interpreters and dedicated oracular sites. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] uses the dream convention throughout his works — in the *Persae*, Atossa's dream foretells Persian defeat; in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] claims to receive dream-warnings. But in the *Choephoroe*, the dream is treated with unusual theological weight: it is not merely a warning but a direct communication from the chthonic powers, "a voice of terror, blasting / The midnight, up from the inmost place it grew" (001-002).

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] reports that professional interpreters confirmed its supernatural origin: "the Seer, / Being sworn of God, made answer, there is here / Anger of dead men wronged" (001-002). The dream is thus authenticated as divine rather than merely psychological.

## Role in The Libation Bearers

The dream is the catalyst for the play's entire action. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], "appalled, came shrieking out of sleep" and orders the [[libation-ritual|libations]] sent (001-002). Had she not done so, [[electra|Electra]] and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] would not have come to the tomb, and the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene with [[orestes|Orestes]] might never have occurred. "If she had gone at once to a temple or to some wise interpreter, she might have been told the meaning and taken steps to protect herself" (002-017). Instead, her attempt at appeasement creates the conditions for her destruction.

[[orestes|Orestes]] interprets the dream as divine confirmation of his mission: "I, the beast, the serpent, even I / Shall slay her!" (001-002). The serpent is simultaneously the child she bore ([[orestes|Orestes]]) and the instrument of divine [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] — the dream collapses the distinction between son and avenger into a single terrifying image.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The dream functions as a pivot across the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]:

- In the **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]**, [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] is the agent of [[moira|fate]], confident and unafraid.
- In the **Choephoroe**, the dream reveals her vulnerability — the dead have power over her sleep, and her guilt has caught up with her. The dream is "a dangerous dream" (002-017) because it shows the dead are not silent.
- In the **Eumenides**, the dream logic gives way to the rational order of the law-court, but the Furies themselves are described in terms that recall the dream's terror — chthonic powers that haunt the guilty.

The dream is a crucial stage in the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s movement from the hidden to the revealed: what [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] has repressed (the murder, the curse) [[exile-and-return|return]]s in the form of the dream-serpent.
