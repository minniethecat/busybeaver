# The Chorus's Opening Ode (Parodos)

The Chorus of Libation Bearers enters chanting one of the most theologically dense odes in Greek tragedy. Their song moves from their own condition as enslaved women to the cosmic principles governing blood guilt and retribution, establishing the framework through which the entire play will be understood.

## Text

> Driven, yea, driven I come: I bear
> Peace-offering to the dead,
> Mine hands as blades that tear, my tresses riven,
> And cheek ploughed red.
> But all my years, before this day as after,
> Have been fed full with weeping as with bread. (PN: 001-002)

> Dread, very dread,
> And hair upstarting and the wrath that streams
> From the heart of sleep, have first interpreted
> What manner of dreams
> This house hath dreamed; a voice of terror, blasting
> The midnight, up from the inmost place it grew,
> Shaking the women's chambers; and the Seer,
> Being sworn of God, made answer, there is here
> Anger of dead men wronged, and hate outlasting
> Death, against them that slew. (PN: 001-002)

> When blood is spilt, how shall a gift set free?
> O hearthstone wet with tears!
> O pillars of a house broken in twain! (PN: 001-002)

> Has Earth once drunk withal
> The blood of her child, Man, the avenging stain
> Hardens, nor flows again.
> A blind pain draweth the slayer, draweth him,
> On, on, till he is filled even to the brim
> With sickness of the soul to atone for all. (PN: 001-002)

> For me, God in far days
> Laid hand upon my city, and herded me
> From my old home to the House of Slavery,
> Where all is violence, and I needs must praise,
> Just or unjust,
> The pleasure of them that rule, and speechless hold
> The ache of a heart that rageth in the dust. (PN: 001-002)

## Context

- **Speaker**: Chorus of enslaved women, captured in war and serving in Clytemnestra's household
- **Addressee**: The audience, and implicitly the gods and the dead
- **Dramatic moment**: The Chorus enters accompanying Electra, bearing libations to pour at Agamemnon's tomb. They have been sent by Clytemnestra, who was terrified by a dream.
- **Form**: Choral ode (strophe/antistrophe pairs) functioning as the play's parodos (entry song)

## Analysis

The parodos establishes three theological principles that govern the entire play. First, blood once spilled cannot be washed away — "the avenging stain / Hardens, nor flows again" — a statement that makes the libation ritual itself tragically futile as atonement. Second, the dead retain agency: the "anger of dead men wronged" is a real force that enters the world through dreams and demands satisfaction. Third, the social condition of the speakers — enslaved women — gives their theological reflections a political edge: the cosmic disorder of the House is mirrored in their personal experience of tyranny.

The image of blood hardening in the earth draws on archaic Greek beliefs about miasma (pollution), but Aeschylus transforms the ritual concept into a psychological one: the "blind pain" that "draweth the slayer" is internal compulsion as much as external curse (PN: 002-020).

## In Performance

The Chorus enters in procession, bearing vessels of wine, milk, and honey for the libation. Their movements — hands tearing at hair, cheeks bloodied from ritual mourning — enact the grief their words describe. The shift from personal lament to cosmic theology mirrors the physical movement from the palace to the tomb.
