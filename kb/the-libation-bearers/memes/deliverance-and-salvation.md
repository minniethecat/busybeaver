# Deliverance and Salvation

Deliverance (*sōtēria*) is the theological end-point of the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* — the resolution toward which the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s movement from crime through punishment to salvation is directed. In the *Choephoroe*, deliverance is promised but not yet achieved, leaving [[orestes|Orestes]] as "the would-be Saviour as one Accursed, never to rest again" (P01-008).

## Definition

*Sōtēria* in Greek religion meant deliverance, preservation, or salvation — not in the Christian sense of eternal salvation, but as rescue from immediate peril, [[blood-guilt|pollution]], or divine wrath. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] structures the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* around the sequence identified in the Preface: "Hubris, [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]], Soteria or Crime, Punishment and Deliverance" (P01-002). The *Choephoroe* represents the *Dikē* (punishment/[[dike|justice]]) stage, in which the crime (*[[hubris]]*) of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] is punished — but *sōtēria* remains ahead, in the *Eumenides*.

## Greek Religious Context

The concept of a *sōtēr* (saviour) was applied in Greek religion to gods who intervened to rescue mortals — Zeus *Sōtēr*, Apollo, the Dioscuri, and in later periods, Hellenistic rulers. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' innovation is to present the saviour-figure as a mortal — [[orestes|Orestes]] — who is simultaneously an agent of punishment and in need of salvation himself. "Whether [[orestes|Orestes]] is a saviour or a final caster-down of the House" (002-130) remains an open question within the *Choephoroe* itself.

## Role in The Libation Bearers

[[orestes|Orestes]] is repeatedly figured as a potential saviour. He prays to Hermes: "Saviour, be thou my saviour; Help in War" (001-002). [[electra|Electra]] hails him as "Seed of deliverance washed with tears as rain" (001-002). The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] invokes "some man of might / To aid this land, some high and visible lord / Of battle, shining bright / Against Death; the great lance / Bearing deliverance" (001-002).

But the play refuses to deliver the salvation it promises. The final image is of [[orestes|Orestes]] as "the would-be Saviour as one Accursed" (P01-008) — the saviour who has become himself accursed through the act of saving. This paradox is the theological engine driving the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] toward its resolution.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] maps the progression from crime through punishment to deliverance:

- The **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** stages the crime (*[[hubris]]*): [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s murder of the king.
- The **Choephoroe** stages the punishment (*[[dike|dikē]]*): [[orestes|Orestes]]' [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. But the punishment generates new crime, and deliverance is deferred.
- The **Eumenides** achieves deliverance (*sōtēria*): "the [[catharsis|purification]] of [[orestes|Orestes]] from the murder of his mother and the conversion of the spirits of Punishment from Furies to Beneficent Beings, from 'Erinyes' to 'Eumenides'" (P01-001).

This is "the great problem which lies at the centre of Greek religion" (P01-002) — and [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' answer is that deliverance requires both divine intervention and civic institution.
