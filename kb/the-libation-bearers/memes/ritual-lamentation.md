# Ritual Lamentation

The ritual lamentation (*[[kommos]]*) is the formal structure through which the central scene of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* — the invocation at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb — is organized, transforming private grief into a collective, religious act that summons the dead and authorizes [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]].

## Definition

The *[[kommos]]* is a technical term in Greek tragedy for an antiphonal (responsive) lamentation exchanged between actors and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]], characterized by lyric metres and intense emotional expression. It is distinguished from spoken dialogue (*rhesis*) and from [[choral-ode|choral ode]]s sung by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] alone. In the *Choephoroe*, the great *[[kommos]]* at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb occupies nearly half the play's length, with [[orestes|Orestes]], [[electra|Electra]], and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] alternating in strophes and antistrophes that grow in intensity from grief to rage to resolution.

## Greek Dramatic Context

The *[[kommos]]* was a standard element of Greek tragedy, deriving from pre-dramatic [[grief-and-lamentation|funeral lament]]s (*threnoi*). But [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' use of the form in the *Choephoroe* is exceptional in scale and function. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] instructs: "Do ye set lamentation like a wreath / Round them, and cry the triumph-song of death" (001-002). The lamentation is simultaneously a funeral rite, a legal argument (rehearsing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s wrongs), and a battle-cry. "It hideth in my bosom's core, / It beats its wings for death, for death" (001-002).

The *[[kommos]]* in this play also incorporates elements of other ritual forms — the [[kommos|dirge]] of Agbatana, the keening of Kissia — suggesting its archaic, cross-cultural origins: "With the [[kommos|dirge]] of Agbatana / I beat my breast: / Like the Keeners of Kissia, / I make songs of pain" (001-002).

## Role in The Libation Bearers

The *[[kommos]]* is structurally the play's centre of gravity. It proceeds through stages:

1. **Grief**: [[electra|Electra]] and [[orestes|Orestes]] mourn their father's dishonourable death and burial (001-002)
2. **Remembrance**: They recount the details of his murder — the hacked limbs, the shameful burial without mourners (001-002)
3. **Summoning**: They call upon [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] to awaken and aid them — "O Father, Father of Doom, / What word, what deed from me, / Can waft afar to the silent room / Where thy sleep holdeth thee?" (001-002)
4. **Resolution**: By the *[[kommos]]*' end, [[orestes|Orestes]] is resolved: "The dream is come. The man shall follow it" (001-002)

The *[[kommos]]* thus does dramatic work — it transforms [[orestes|Orestes]] from a hesitant [[exile-and-return|exile]] into a committed avenger — while remaining formally a ritual act.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The *[[kommos]]* form evolves across the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]:

- The **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** contains extended choral laments but no full *[[kommos]]* — Cassandra's scene with the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] approaches the form but remains prophetic rather than invocatory.
- The **Choephoroe** contains the longest and most elaborate *[[kommos]]* in extant Greek tragedy, placing ritual lament at the dramatic heart of the play.
- The **Eumenides** largely abandons the *[[kommos]]* — the Furies' "binding song" over [[orestes|Orestes]] is a perversion of the form, and the trial scene replaces ritual emotion with rhetorical argument. This formal shift mirrors the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s thematic movement from archaic religion to civic institutions.
