## Definition

The robe — simultaneously a winding-sheet, a hunting net, a snare, and a trap — is the central material symbol of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]*. It is the object in which [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] ensnared and murdered [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] in the first play, and its public display by [[orestes|Orestes]] at the climax of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* constitutes one of the most powerful visual moments in extant Greek tragedy (001-004).

The Endnotes capture the object's symbolic density: "his mind and he calls her 'a winding-sheet, a snare, a net,' and so on" (002-124). The robe is never merely a piece of cloth — it accumulates meanings across the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]: murder weapon, evidence, shroud, symbol of domestic treachery, and ultimately the visual proof that transforms [[orestes|Orestes]] from avenger to just executioner.

## The Robe in the *Agamemnon*

To understand the robe's power in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, one must recall its origin in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] trapped her husband in "a robe with no way out" — a garment woven to entangle, a domestic object transformed into a weapon of war. The robe blurred the boundary between home and battlefield, between the female sphere of weaving and the male sphere of killing (P01-008).

The robe was also the object on which [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s blood was spilled, making it simultaneously murder weapon and ritual [[blood-guilt|pollution]] object — a *[[blood-and-ritual-impurity|miasma]]*-stained artifact whose very display risks contagion.

## The Display Scene

At the climax of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, [[orestes|Orestes]] orders the great red robe to be displayed before the palace: "Attendants hold the great red robe in which [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] was murdered" (001-004). This is a moment of visual theatre — the object is held up for the audience to see, and [[orestes|Orestes]]'s speech catalogues its identities: "A dead man's winding-sheet? A hunter's snare? A trap, a toil, a tangling of the feet?" (001-004).

The rhetorical accumulation of names for the robe is not mere verbal display. Each name frames the murder in a different moral light: the "winding-sheet" evokes burial and piety, the "snare" evokes hunting and predation, the "net" evokes entrapment without honour (002-124). [[orestes|Orestes]]'s inability to settle on a single name for the object reflects the moral ambiguity of the act it witnessed.

## Symbolic Functions

The robe serves four interconnected symbolic functions in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*:

1. **Evidence**: The displayed robe proves [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s crime — it is the material fact that justifies [[orestes|Orestes]]' [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] before the city and the audience (001-004).

2. **Token of Transmission**: The robe passes from [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] (murderer) to [[orestes|Orestes]] (avenger), transferring guilt and the obligation of [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] across generations — a visual representation of the [[curse-of-the-house-of-atreus|inherited curse]] (P01-008).

3. **Gender Inversion**: The robe is a product of women's work (weaving) used for a man's death, and its display by a son against his mother inverts the normal relations of the *oikos* — the household order that the robe both represents and destroys (001-004).

4. **Ritual Object**: Stained with [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s blood, the robe is a *[[blood-and-ritual-impurity|miasma]]*-bearing artifact — its display risks [[blood-guilt|pollution]], and its handling requires the [[catharsis|purification]] that only the *Eumenides* can provide (P01-006).

## From Robe to Law

The display of the robe marks a crucial transition in the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s movement from archaic [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] to civic [[dike|justice]]. In the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, the robe was the instrument of private murder within the household. In *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, [[orestes|Orestes]] displays it as public evidence — transforming a domestic object into a forensic exhibit. In the *Eumenides*, the robe is superseded entirely by the institutions of law, its bloodstained logic replaced by the jury's vote (P01-006).

The robe is thus the material trace of the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s central argument: that the cycle of [[divine-vengeance|blood-vengeance]] must be visible — must be displayed, examined, and judged — before it can be transcended.
