# Pylades' Intervention

Throughout the entire Oresteia trilogy, Pylades — Orestes' companion and friend — speaks only once: three lines at the decisive moment when Orestes wavers before killing his mother. These three lines change the course of the action and, arguably, the entire trilogy.

## Text

> Where is God's voice from out the golden cloud
> At Pytho? Where the plighted troth we vowed?
> Count all the world thy foe, save God on high.
> (PN 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Pylades]], son of Strophios, King of Phôkis, friend to Orestes
- **Addressee**: [[Orestes]]
- **Dramatic moment**: The climactic confrontation between Orestes and Clytemnestra. Orestes, sword drawn, has driven his mother into the palace. She bares her breast and appeals to their bond: "I nursed thee. I would fain grow old with thee." Orestes hesitates, turning to Pylades: "Pylades, what shall I do? Shall I spare my mother?" Pylades, silent throughout the entire trilogy until this moment, speaks.
- **Form**: stichomythia (single speech within the rapid exchange)

## Analysis

Pylades' three lines are among the most consequential in Greek tragedy. He has been a silent presence since the prologue — standing beside Orestes at the tomb, witness to the recognition scene, comrade in the plotting. His silence is traditional: Pylades is the ideal friend, the *philos* whose presence needs no speech. When he finally breaks silence, the dramatic weight is immense.

The speech deploys three rhetorical strategies in rapid succession. First, Pylades invokes Apollo's oracle at Delphi — "God's voice from out the golden cloud at Pytho" — reminding Orestes that the command to kill Clytemnestra comes from divine authority, not human will. Second, he appeals to their shared oath: "the plighted troth we vowed." This transforms the murder from an individual act into a sacred covenant between the two men. Third, the final line — "Count all the world thy foe, save God on high" — frames the choice in absolute terms: obedience to Apollo outweighs every human bond, including the bond to one's mother.

The intervention resolves the central moral tension of the play. Orestes' hesitation is not cowardice — it is the correct human response to the horror of matricide. Pylades' words override that horror by asserting the priority of divine command. The trilogy will spend its third play (*Eumenides*) interrogating whether this assertion was justified.

In Aeschylus' handling, unlike Euripides' later version, Orestes' scruples are overcome not by another character's scorn or passion but by the voice of religious obligation speaking through the mouth of the truest friend. This makes the matricide a theological act rather than a psychological collapse — fitting the trilogy's cosmic scope.

## In Performance

The staging of this moment is freighted with silence. Pylades has stood on stage for hundreds of lines without speaking — the audience may have forgotten he can speak at all. When his voice finally sounds, the effect is electric. The transition from Clytemnestra's naked appeal to motherhood to Pylades' invocation of Apollo moves the scene from the domestic to the divine in three lines. Orestes' immediate shift from doubt to resolve demonstrates that the question was never really about courage — it was about which authority to obey.
