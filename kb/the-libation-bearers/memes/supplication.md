## Overview

[[xenia|Supplication]] (ἱκεσία, *hiketeia*) is the formal ritual of entreaty in Greek religion — a suppliant approaches a sacred person, altar, or tomb, adopts a posture of humility (kneeling, touching the knees or chin, holding a suppliant branch), and requests protection, aid, or mercy. In *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, supplication structures the play's central ritual actions: the [[libation-ritual|libations]] at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb, [[orestes|Orestes]]'s appeal to Apollo, and his final flight to seek [[catharsis|purification]] at [[delphi|Delphi]].

## The Ritual Form

Greek supplication followed a recognizable ritual pattern. The suppliant (ἱκέτης, *hiketēs*) would approach the person or sacred site, adopt a posture of submission — typically kneeling and clasping the knees or touching the chin of the person addressed — and make a formal request. The recipient of supplication was bound by religious obligation (αἰδώς, *aidōs*, reverent shame) to grant the request or at minimum to offer protection from immediate harm.

The Introduction to *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* notes that supplication was "elaborately provided for in various codes" (P01-002) of Greek customary and religious law. To reject a suppliant, or worse, to harm one, was to commit an offense against Zeus Hikesios (Zeus the Protector of Suppliants).

The ritual was closely related to xenia (guest-friendship) — both involved the sacred reception of an outsider — but supplication was the more intense form, implying urgent need and a stronger claim on divine protection.

## Supplication in The Libation Bearers

### The Tomb Libations

The play opens with an act of collective supplication. [[electra|Electra]], accompanied by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of captive women, approaches [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb bearing offerings. Her opening words frame the ritual:

> Ye thrallèd women, tirers of the bower, / Since ye are with me in this suppliant hour, / Your escort giving, give your counsel too. (001-002)

[[electra|Electra]] explicitly names this a "suppliant hour" — the tomb becomes an altar, the dead king becomes the recipient of entreaty, and the offerings become the [[recognition-and-tokens|tokens]] of supplication's formal request. But [[electra|Electra]] hesitates: what prayer is appropriate when the offerings come from the murderer? "I dare not. And I cannot find the word / To speak, when offerings like these are poured" (001-002).

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s advice transforms the supplication: rather than pray for the dead, [[electra|Electra]] should pray for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. The ritual of submission becomes the ritual of demanding [[dike|justice]] — supplication inverted, the suppliant speaking not as a petitioner for mercy but as an agent of [[dike|retribution]].

### Orestes's Flight

After the murders, [[orestes|Orestes]] adopts the posture of a formal suppliant for his departure:

> Therefore here ye see me, how / I go prepared, with wreaths and olive bough, / To kneel in supplication on the floor / Of Loxias, touch the fire that evermore / Men call the undying, and the midmost stone / Of Earth, flying this blood which is mine own. (001-003)

The "wreaths and olive bough" are the traditional [[recognition-and-tokens|tokens]] of supplication. [[orestes|Orestes]] will approach Apollo's temple at [[delphi|Delphi]] — the omphalos, "the midmost stone of Earth" — as a hiketēs, claiming the protection that supplication guarantees even to a [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]].

This supplication is the bridge between *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* and *Eumenides*. In the third play, [[orestes|Orestes]] appears at Athena's altar in Athens, having been purified at [[delphi|Delphi]] but still pursued by the Furies. His final supplication — to Athena herself — becomes the occasion for the founding of the Areopagus and the transformation of the Erinyes into the Eumenides.

### The Pattern of Sacred Entreaty

Throughout the play, characters adopt the language and postures of supplication even outside formal ritual contexts:

- **[[orestes|Orestes]] at the tomb**: "I call my Father o'er the deep / To hearken, to give ear" (001-001) — filial supplication to the dead
- **The [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s plea**: "As at God's altar, since so fain thou art, / Before this Tomb I will unveil my heart" (001-002)
- **[[electra|Electra]]'s prayer**: "O Zeus, bless thou the murder to be this day" (001-002) — supplication for divine sanction

Each instance draws on the grammar of supplication — approach, humility, formal request, divine witness — to authorize actions that might otherwise appear sacrilegious.

## Supplication and the Trilogy's Resolution

The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] traces supplication's evolution from the tomb to the law court. The tomb-supplication of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is private, familial, and vengeful — the dead are summoned to authorize murder. The altar-supplication of *Eumenides* is public, civic, and judicial — Athena receives [[orestes|Orestes]]'s plea and transforms it into a legal proceeding.

The translator's Introduction frames this movement in terms of the central triad: supplication in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is [[dike]] ([[dike|justice]]-seeking through ritual), but the [[dike|justice]] it seeks is indistinguishable from [[hubris]] (crime). Only in *Eumenides* does supplication find its proper institutional form, and only then does [[deliverance-and-salvation|soteria]] ([[deliverance-and-salvation|deliverance]]) become possible.
