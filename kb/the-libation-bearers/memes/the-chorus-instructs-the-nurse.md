# The Chorus Instructs the Nurse

A brief but pivotal scene in which the Chorus — usually a passive commentator — becomes an active agent in the plot. By altering the message carried by Cilissa, Orestes' old nurse, the Chorus ensures that Aegisthus walks into the palace unguarded.

## Dramatic Context

Clytemnestra has sent the Nurse with a message to Aegisthus: come to the palace with his bodyguard to hear the news that Orestes is dead. The Chorus intercepts the Nurse and persuades her to deliver a different message: come alone, quickly, without guards. This small alteration of the message is the hinge upon which the entire revenge plot turns.

## Significance

The scene is remarkable for several reasons. First, the Nurse (Cilissa) is one of Greek tragedy's earliest "ordinary" characters — not royal, not divine, but a household servant whose grief for the infant Orestes is tenderly drawn. Second, the Chorus steps out of its conventional role as commentator to become a co-conspirator. And third, the scene demonstrates how the "little people" — a slave woman, a group of foreign serving women — are essential agents in the working out of the divine plan.

The Nurse enters with her message "poor, loving, half-ridiculous" (as the Preface describes the moment) and leaves as an unwitting instrument of fate.
