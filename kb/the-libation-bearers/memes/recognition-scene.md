# The Recognition Scene

The recognition scene at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb — [[electra|Electra]] discovers a lock of hair, [[recognition-and-tokens|footprints]] matching her own, and finally [[orestes|Orestes]] himself — reunites the [[exile-and-return|exile]]d siblings and transforms [[electra|Electra]]'s passive grief into active alliance.

## Summary

[[electra|Electra]], having poured the [[libation-ritual|libations]], discovers on the tomb a lock of hair — "strange bird, but of one feather to mine eye" (001-002). She finds [[recognition-and-tokens|footprints]] matching her own — "the print of feet, a second sign! / The same feet: surely they are shaped like mine" (001-002). Still uncertain, she is overcome when [[orestes|Orestes]] reveals himself and the woven cloak she made for him in childhood. Her recognition is ecstatic: "O best beloved, O dreamed of long ago, / Seed of [[deliverance-and-salvation|deliverance]] washed with tears as rain" (001-002).

## Dramatic Significance

The recognition scene is the turning-point of the play's first half. Before it, [[electra|Electra]] and [[orestes|Orestes]] are isolated mourners; after it, they are co-conspirators. The scene also showcases [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' handling of the recognition [[recognition-and-tokens|tokens]] — the lock of hair, [[recognition-and-tokens|footprints]], and cloak — which [[sophocles|Sophocles]] would omit and [[euripides|Euripides]] would critique, but which [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] presents with archaic directness as valid proofs of identity.
