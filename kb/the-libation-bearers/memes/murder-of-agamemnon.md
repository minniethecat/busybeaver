The Murder of Agamemnon is the originating crime of the [[oresteia|Oresteia]] [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]. Though it occurs offstage and before the action of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* begins, it is the gravitational centre around which all the play's events orbit — the deed that must be answered.

## Background

[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], [[agamemnon|king of Argos]] and commander of the Greek forces at [[troy|Troy]], [[exile-and-return|return]]ed from the ten-year Trojan War to be murdered by his wife [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and her lover [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]]. The murder is the subject of the first play of the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]], *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*. In that play, [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] kills [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] in the bath, entangling him in a robe before striking — a method that combines ritual and treachery. [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]], [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s cousin and hereditary enemy (as [[aigisthos|son of Thyestes]], whom [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s father Atreus had wronged), is the co-conspirator.

## Significance in The Libation Bearers

The murder is the wound that will not close. The entire action of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is an attempt to answer it. [[orestes|Orestes]] opens the play at his father's tomb, declaring: "I am [[exile-and-return|return]]ed from lands afar / To claim mine own" (001-002). His [[exile-and-return|return]] is explicitly [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]: "I call my Father o'er the deep / To hearken, to give ear" (001-002). The ghost of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] must be awakened; the murder must be answered with murder.

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] captures the [[blood-guilt|pollution]] that murder leaves behind: "O hearthstone wet with tears! / O pillars of a house broken in twain! / Without sun, without love, / Murk in the heart thereof and mist above, / For a lord slain!" (001-002). The dead king's absence haunts every line of the early scenes.

Gilbert Murray notes that the very title of the play is determined by the attempt to reach the murdered king: "the dead [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] here cannot gather his dim senses till the [[libation-ritual|drink-offering]]s have sunk into his grave. The wine and milk and honey reach his parched lips. He stirs in his sleep" (P01-004).

## Perverse Consequences

The murder's consequences twist far beyond simple revenge. [[orestes|Orestes]], commanded by [[apollo|Apollo]] to kill his mother in [[dike|retribution]], enacts a deed that is simultaneously a duty and a sin. "The mother-murder, even if done by a god's command, is a sin; a sin to be expiated by unfathomable suffering. Yet, since the god cannot have commanded evil, it is a duty also. It is a sin that must be committed" (P01-002). This paradox — that responding to one murder with another creates only more [[blood-guilt|pollution]] — is the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s central religious problem.

The Endnotes observe that "the rule of [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] seems to the actors, and in this special case to [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], a natural and divine law" (002-038), yet the play's resolution lies not in more bloodshed but in the transformation of the Furies in *Eumenides* — the civic solution that breaks the cycle [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s murder set in motion.

## Participants

- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]**: The primary agent. Murray describes her as preserving "almost superhuman grandeur" (P01-007) in this play, even as she faces her son's [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]].
- **[[aigisthos|Aigisthos]]**: "[[aigisthos|son of Thyestes]], blood-foe to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], and now Tyrant of [[argos|Argos]]" (P02-011). His claim to [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] is ancestral but his position is usurpation.
- **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]**: The victim, whose ghost powers the play's central ritual. "He who was once King of Kings is in power among them" (P01-004) — dead, yet the most potent force in the drama.
