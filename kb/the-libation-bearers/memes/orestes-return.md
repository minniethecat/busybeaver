Orestes' Return from [[exile-and-return|exile]] in Phokis is the initiating event of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*. It transforms the static situation of mourning and oppression into active movement toward [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. The play opens with [[orestes|Orestes]] already standing at his father's tomb, hair shorn, having fulfilled his vow.

## The Prologue

The play's first moments establish the [[exile-and-return|return]] with extraordinary economy. "With the last lines of the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* still ringing in our minds we see, as the play opens, a young man standing with shorn hair beside a grave mound; and half the story is told in a flash" (P01-008). [[orestes|Orestes]] announces his [[exile-and-return|return]] in language that fuses supplication, self-assertion, and grief: "O Warder Hermês of the world beneath, / Son of the Father who is Lord of Death; / Saviour, be thou my saviour; Help in War, / Help me! I am [[exile-and-return|return]]ed from lands afar / To claim mine own" (001-002).

## The Vow Fulfilled

[[orestes|Orestes]]' [[exile-and-return|return]] is marked by the physical act of laying a lock of hair on the tomb. He explains his history: "long ago in Phôkis, where I lay / With Strophius in the hills, being cast away / In childhood, plundered by mine enemies, / And friendless, save for this man, [[pylades|Pylades]], / I sware an oath" (001-002). The vow was to divide his hair in two tresses — one for the river Inachos who sustained his life, and "one for mourning at this place" (001-002). The offering of hair transforms the anonymous mound into a site of filial duty.

## The Recognition

The [[exile-and-return|return]] leads directly into the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene with [[electra|Electra]], who approaches the tomb with the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] bearing [[libation-ritual|libations]]. [[orestes|Orestes]] sees her from hiding: "Surely 'tis [[electra|Electra]] there, / My sister, moves alone, none like to her / In sorrow" (001-002). The [[recognition-scene|recognition]] — through the lock of hair, [[recognition-and-tokens|footprints]], and a woven cloth — unites the siblings and transforms individual grief into shared purpose. This [[recognition-scene|recognition]] is one of the most debated scenes in Greek tragedy; the Endnotes observe that "[[euripides|Euripides]] uses them, but uses them in an ironic, half-mocking way" while "[[sophocles|Sophocles]] in his [[electra|Electra]] omits the traditional [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] altogether" (002-029).

## Dramatic Function

Murray captures the [[exile-and-return|return]]'s dramatic function: the ghost of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] was dormant, unreachable — "The dead [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] here cannot gather his dim senses till the [[libation-ritual|drink-offering]]s have sunk into his grave" (P01-004). [[orestes|Orestes]]' physical [[exile-and-return|return]] is what makes the ritual effective. The mourners, the offerings, and the [[exile-and-return|return]]ed son together "awaken" the dead king to action. Murray describes how "the first words spoken in prayer at his neglected tomb; the call for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] sent, as it were, unknowingly by the murderess; the repeated story of his old wrongs… above all, the voices of his desolate children crying to him" (P01-004) combine to rouse the ghost.
