# Clytemnestra's Confrontation

The confrontation between Orestes and Clytemnestra in the *Libation Bearers* represents the dramatic and moral climax of the play. In a rapid stichomythic exchange, mother and son face each other — she pleading for her life, he advancing inexorably toward the killing stroke. The scene distills the play's central conflict into a duel of words before the duel of the sword.

## Text

> Ah me, / So cometh the last wreck in spite of all! / Curse of this House, thou foe that fear'st no fall, / How dost thou spy my hidden things and mar / Their peace with keen-eyed arrows from afar (PN: 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: Clytemnestra and Orestes, in rapid alternating lines
- **Addressee**: Each addresses the other directly
- **Dramatic moment**: After the killing of Aegisthus, Orestes confronts Clytemnestra inside the palace. She has seen Aegisthus' body and knows her own death approaches
- **Form**: stichomythia (rapid line-by-line exchange) transitioning to rhesis

## Analysis

The stichomythic confrontation is structured as a legal debate disguised as a family quarrel. Clytemnestra attempts multiple rhetorical strategies in quick succession: she appeals to motherhood ("I nursed you"), to justice ("Fate had some part in that"), to pity ("Beware the hounds of a mother's curse"), and finally to self-preservation. Orestes counters each with reference to Apollo's command and the justice of retribution.

What makes this scene uniquely powerful is its inversion of the supplication convention. In Greek tragedy, a suppliant typically grasps the knees of the person whose mercy they seek. But Clytemnestra bares her breast — the breast that nursed the infant Orestes — and asks him to strike there. This gesture transforms the supplication into a moral trap: to kill her is to violate the most fundamental bond of nature.

## In Performance

The confrontation scene demands extraordinary control from both actors. The stichomythia — single lines exchanged back and forth at accelerating pace — builds tension through its rhythm. The moment when Clytemnestra bares her breast is one of the most physically vulnerable and emotionally charged gestures in Greek tragedy. The scene is typically played with Orestes hesitating at this moment, requiring Pylades' intervention ([[pylades-intervention]]) to remind him of Apollo's command.
