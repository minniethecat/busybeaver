# Aegisthus' Death Speech

Aegisthus' entrance and death are handled with startling swiftness. The man who has been the play's absent tyrant — present in the Chorus's curses, in Electra's hatred, in Orestes' determination — appears only to be dispatched almost immediately. His brief scene is a study in the collapse of power: the usurper who ruled Argos for seven years is reduced, in fewer than twenty lines, from commanding inquiry to a death-cry offstage.

## Text

Aegisthus enters:

> A message called me; else I scarce had thought To have come so quick. 'Tis a strange rumour, brought, They tell me, by some Phocian wayfarers In passing: strange, nor grateful to our ears. Orestes dead! A galling load it were And dripping blood for this poor House to bear, Still scored and festerous with its ancient wound. How shall I deem it? Living truth and sound? Or tales of women, born to terrify, That wildly leap, and up in mid-air die? What know ye further? I would have this clear. (PN: 001-004)

The Chorus Leader directs him inside:

> We heard the tale; but go within and hear With thine own ears. A rumoured word hath weak Force, when the man himself is there to speak. (PN: 001-004)

Aegisthus' final words:

> Hear him I will, and question him beside. Was this man with Orestes when he died, Or speaks he too from rumour? If he lies … He cannot cheat a mind that is all eyes. (PN: 001-004)

As he enters the house:

> Death-cry of Aigisthos within. Ha! The deed is done! (PN: 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Aegisthus]], usurper-king of Argos
- **Addressee**: The Chorus Leader, then (offstage) Orestes
- **Dramatic moment**: After Clytemnestra's false welcome and the Nurse's manipulated message, Aegisthus arrives at the palace alone and unarmed — the Chorus having persuaded the Nurse to alter Clytemnestra's instructions. He enters through the country gate, questions the Chorus about the rumour of Orestes' death, and walks into the house to his own.
- **Form**: Brief rhesis transitioning to stichomythia with the Chorus Leader

## Analysis

### The Speed of Justice

Aegisthus' death is the most efficient killing in the *Oresteia*. Agamemnon's murder is prefigured through an entire play; Clytemnestra's death is preceded by an extended stichomythic confrontation. But Aegisthus walks in, exchanges a few lines with the Chorus, and is dead. The speed is deliberate: Aegisthus, for all his seven years of rule, is a secondary figure. He is not the object of the play's moral gravity — Clytemnestra is. Aeschylus dispatches the usurper quickly to clear the stage for the confrontation that matters (PN: 001-004).

### Dramatic Irony of the "Mind That Is All Eyes"

Aegisthus' boast — "He cannot cheat a mind that is all eyes" — is the play's final, most concentrated instance of dramatic irony. The man who claims to see through all deception is walking, at that very moment, into the simplest trap imaginable: a closed room with a man holding a sword. The "mind that is all eyes" sees nothing. His confidence in his own intelligence is the instrument of his death, a classic tragic pattern in miniature (PN: 001-004).

### "Tales of Women, Born to Terrify"

Aegisthus' contempt for "tales of women, born to terrify, / That wildly leap, and up in mid-air die" reveals the gendered structure of power in the play. He dismisses women's speech as inherently unreliable — yet it is the Chorus of women, not any man, who lures him to his death. His misogyny, like his overconfidence in his own intelligence, is a fatal flaw exploited by those he underestimates.

### The Death-Cry as Structural Echo

The death-cry of Aegisthus — "Ha! The deed is done!" — echoes the death-cry of Agamemnon in the first play of the trilogy. In the *Agamemnon*, the king's cry from within the palace ("I am struck! A deep, a mortal blow!") announced the collapse of legitimate rule. Aegisthus' cry announces the collapse of illegitimate rule. The structural parallel establishes the symmetry of revenge: as Agamemnon fell to the usurper, so the usurper falls to Agamemnon's son (PN: 001-004).

## In Performance

The staging of Aegisthus' death is typically handled through the palace door (the *skene*). He enters through one door (the country entrance), delivers his lines, and exits through the central door into the palace. Moments later, his death-cry is heard from within. The audience never sees the killing — like Agamemnon's death, it is concealed behind the palace walls, reported only through sound.

The Chorus's reaction — "Ha! The deed is done!" — is a moment of theatrical shock that must register even with an audience that knows the myth. The cry transforms the Chorus from passive mourners into active participants in the killing. They have not wielded the sword, but they have directed Aegisthus to it.
