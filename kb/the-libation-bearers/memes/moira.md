## Overview

Moira (μοῖρα, *moira*) — fate, destiny, the allotted portion — is the cosmic force that governs the distribution of fortune and misfortune in the Aeschylean universe. Neither purely impersonal mechanism nor fully personified deity, Moira represents the boundary between divine will and cosmic necessity. In *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, Moira appears as an inescapable reality acknowledged by both characters and [[the-chorus|Chorus]]: the "day of Fate" that "is old" yet ever-renewed, the apportionment that each actor in the drama must ultimately receive.

## Moira in the Libation Bearers

### The Day of Fate

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] invokes Moira at a pivotal moment, as the revenge plot nears its climax:

> The day of Fate is old, it hath lingered long; It cometh to them that pray. (001-002)

This couplet condenses the Aeschylean understanding of Moira: it is ancient (the cosmic order predates and transcends any particular human action), it is patient (it "lingers"), and it is responsive to human invocation ("it cometh to them that pray"). Fate is not blind mechanism but intelligent apportionment — it arrives at the appointed time for those who call upon it rightly.

### Fate and Human Agency

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], facing her son's sword, attempts to displace responsibility onto Moira:

> Child, if I sinned, Fate had her part therein. (001-002)

[[orestes|Orestes]]'s reply is immediate and devastating:

> Then Fate is here, with the reward of sin. (001-002)

This exchange — one of the play's most concentrated moments — crystallizes the problem of fate and free will in Greek tragedy. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] invokes Moira as exculpation ("I am not fully responsible; Fate shares the blame"), but [[orestes|Orestes]] turns the logic against her: if Fate shares responsibility for the crime, then Fate also delivers the punishment. Moira is not partial; it apportions to each their due, whether in crime or in consequence.

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] reinforces this understanding earlier in the play:

> Ye great Apportionments of God, The road of Righteousness make straight. (001-002)

The "Apportionments of God" (θεοῦ μοῖραι) link Moira directly to divine will. Fate and the gods are not competitors but collaborators — the gods work through Moira, and Moira expresses divine intention.

### The Fates as Personified Agents

The endnote on Althaea's myth provides the mythological background for the personified Moirai:

> Althaea: When her son Meleâger was born she saw in the room the three Fates, one of whom foretold that Meleâger should die when a red brand then burning in the fire was consumed. Althaea leapt out of bed and saved the brand. Afterwards, when Meleâger fell in love with Atalanta, and in a feud on her behalf killed his mother's two brethren, she threw the brand into the fire. (002-081)

This myth — which the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] uses as one of its examples of feminine passion — reveals several features of the Greek understanding of Moira:

| Feature | Manifestation |
|---------|---------------|
| Moira as allotted span | Meleâger's life is bound to the brand — a concrete "portion" |
| Moira as conditional | Fate is foretold but not inevitable; human action can delay it |
| Moira as inescapable | Despite Althaea's initial rescue, fate is fulfilled |
| Moira and human passion | Althaea's rage becomes the instrument of fate's fulfillment |

The myth also illuminates [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s position: like Althaea, she is both agent and instrument, exercising choice within the framework of an apportioned destiny.

## Moira in the Cosmic Order

### Moira, Dike, and Zeus

The Aeschylean cosmos is hierarchically ordered, with Moira occupying a position that both constrains and enables divine action:

| Force | Function | Relationship |
|-------|----------|--------------|
| Moira (Μοῖρα) | Apportions destiny | The fundamental cosmic boundary |
| Zeus (Ζεύς) | Executes divine will | Works through Moira, does not override it |
| [[dike|Dike (Δίκη)]] | Delivers [[dike|justice]] | The mechanism by which Moira's apportionment is realized |

When [[electra|Electra]] prays to "Zeus most high, who saveth at the last" (001-002) alongside "Justice who doth hold us fast," she invokes this integrated cosmic order. Zeus saves, but only through the channels that Moira has apportioned and [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]] enforces.

### The Third Saviour Pattern

The endnote on the "Third Saviour" concept connects Moira to the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s triadic structure:

> First comes this year with its pride and its pollution, then the winter that kills it, then the clean spring. First comes the crime, then the punishment, which is only another crime, then perhaps the redemption. (002-121)

This seasonal metaphor is itself a vision of Moira — an allotted rhythm of decay and renewal that operates at every scale, from individual lives to dynastic histories. The question that *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* leaves open is whether [[orestes|Orestes]] is the "winter that kills" (another link in the chain) or the "clean spring" (the Third Saviour who breaks it).

## Moira in the Choral Odes

The great [[choral-ode|choral ode]] narrating feminine crimes places each myth within a framework of fate. Althaea "wrought all her thought to one counsel, fiery-blind" — human passion and divine Moira converge in the act that destroys her son. Scylla, "dyed with blood and [[hubris|pride]]," fulfills a destiny of betrayal. The Lemnian women's collective crime becomes "an evil song" that echoes through time — fate transmuted into story.

In each case, Moira operates not as external compulsion but as the inner logic of character and circumstance: the "apportionment" that each receives is the consequence of who they are and what they have done. This is the deepest Aeschylean insight into fate — it is not something that happens *to* us but the shape our actions make when viewed from the perspective of cosmic order.
