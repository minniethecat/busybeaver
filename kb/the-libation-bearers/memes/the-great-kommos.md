# The Great Kommos

The Great Kommos is the central scene of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe* — an extended antiphonal lamentation at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb that occupies approximately half the play's length, transforming private grief into collective ritual and passive mourning into active resolve.

## Summary

The *[[kommos]]* begins after the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene — [[orestes|Orestes]] and [[electra|Electra]] have been reunited, and together with the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] they turn to the tomb to summon their dead father. The scene proceeds through alternating strophes and antistrophes: grief for the dishonoured dead ([[orestes|Orestes]]), recounting of the murder's details ([[electra|Electra]]), invocation of cosmic [[dike|justice]] ([[the-chorus|Chorus]]), and gradual building of resolve (all three). By its end, the mourners have been transformed into conspirators, and the dead [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] is felt to be present among them.

## Dramatic Context

- **Position in the play**: Middle — the structural centre between the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] and the killing
- **Preceding event**: The [[anagnorisis|Recognition]] at the Tomb ([[orestes|Orestes]] reveals his identity to [[electra|Electra]])
- **Following event**: [[orestes|Orestes]]' plan to enter the palace in disguise
- **Onstage / offstage**: Onstage at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb
- **Dramatic form**: Antiphonal *[[kommos]]* (actor-chorus exchange in lyric metres)

## Participants

- **[[orestes|Orestes]]** — leads the invocation; his strophes move from grief to rage to resolution: "The slayer shall meet the slayer, wrong smite with wrong" (001-002)
- **[[electra|Electra]]** — recounts the dishonours inflicted on their father's body: "His hands and feet, they were hacked away from him!" (001-002)
- **The [[the-chorus|Chorus]]** — provides the ritual framework and mythological context, invoking the stories of Althaea, Scylla, and the Lemnian crime

## Dramatic Significance

The *[[kommos]]* is the engine of the play — it does what Greek tragedy often leaves to offstage action: it transforms the protagonists' emotional state from paralysis to action. When the scene begins, [[orestes|Orestes]] and [[electra|Electra]] are mourners; when it ends, they are avengers. The ritual is effective: "as the great litany grows in intensity of longing, the dead seem to draw nearer to the living" (P01-004).

The scene also establishes the play's theological framework. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] articulates the law of [[dike|retribution]] in explicitly religious terms: "'Tis written: the shed drop doth crave / For new blood" (001-002). The invocation of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] proceeds on the understanding that the dead can be awakened, can aid the living, and can demand action — a fusion of [[homer|Homeric]] [[invocation-of-the-dead|necromancy]] with Aeschylean theology that is unique in surviving Greek drama.
