# Clytemnestra's False Welcome

The scene in which Clytemnestra receives the disguised Orestes at the palace gate is one of the most concentrated passages of dramatic irony in Greek tragedy. Orestes, accompanied by Pylades, knocks three times at the gate, announces himself as a stranger bearing news of Orestes' death, and is welcomed with full hospitality by the very woman he has come to kill.

## Text

Orestes knocks at the palace gate:

> Ho, Warder! Hear! One knocketh at your gate! … Ho, Warder, yet again! I knock and wait. … A third time, ye within! I call ye forth; Or counts your lord the stranger nothing worth? (PN: 001-004)

Clytemnestra's welcome:

> Strangers, your pleasure? If ye have need of aught All that beseems this House is yours to-day, Warm bathing and the couch that soothes away Toil, and the tendering of righteous eyes. (PN: 001-004)

Clytemnestra's response to the news of Orestes' death:

> Ah me, So cometh the last wreck in spite of all! Curse of this House, thou foe that fear'st no fall, How dost thou spy my hidden things and mar Their peace with keen-eyed arrows from afar, Till all who might have loved me, all, are gone! And now Orestes; whom I had thought upon So wisely, walking in free ways, his gait Unsnarèd in this poison-marsh of hate! The one last hope, the healing and the prayer Of this old House, 'twas writ on empty air! (PN: 001-004)

She then orders hospitality for the strangers:

> But 'tis time that strangers who Have spent long hours in travel should have due Refreshment. Ho, there! Lead him to our broad Guest-chambers, and these comrades of his road Who follow. See they find all comfort there To assuage their way-worn bodies. And have care That in their tendance naught be found amiss. (PN: 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Clytemnestra]], Queen of Argos
- **Addressee**: [[Orestes]] (disguised as a Phocian stranger) and [[Pylades]]
- **Dramatic moment**: Orestes arrives at the palace after the recognition scene at the tomb, disguised as a traveller bearing news of his own death. He knocks three times — a ritualistic number that underscores the ceremonial quality of the impending violence. Clytemnestra emerges to greet the strangers with full ceremonial hospitality, unaware that she is welcoming her own killer.
- **Form**: Rhesis (extended speech), transitioning from formal hospitality formula to emotional lament to practical household command

## Analysis

### The Triple Knock as Ritual Threshold

Orestes' three knocks at the gate mark a ritual transition from the exterior world of the tomb and the dead to the interior world of the palace — the realm of the living, the usurpers, and imminent bloodshed. Each knock escalates the dramatic tension: the first announces presence, the second insists on urgency, and the third — "A third time, ye within! I call ye forth" — transforms the stranger's knock into a herald's summons, as if Orestes is not requesting entry but demanding it by right (PN: 001-004).

### Dramatic Irony and Double Meaning

Every line Clytemnestra speaks vibrates with double meaning. When she offers "warm bathing and the couch that soothes away toil," the audience knows that the "bath" awaiting her is the bath of death in which Agamemnon was slain, and the "couch" will be the marriage-bed of death beside Aigisthos (PN: 001-004). Her offer of "the tendering of righteous eyes" — meaning honest, respectful attention — ironically invokes the very standard of justice by which she will be judged and condemned.

### The Lament as Self-Indictment

Clytemnestra's response to the false news of Orestes' death is remarkable for its ambiguity. She calls the news "the last wreck in spite of all" and speaks of the "Curse of this House." On the surface, she mourns the loss of her last hope for the dynasty. But the language of wreckage and curse is also the language of guilt — the House is cursed because of her own act of murder, and the "wreck" is of her own making. Her claim that Orestes walked "in free ways, his gait / Unsnarèd in this poison-marsh of hate" is a self-serving fiction: the marsh of hate into which she plunged the House at Agamemnon's murder could never leave her son unsnared (PN: 001-004).

### Hospitality as the Mask of Power

The elaborate hospitality Clytemnestra extends — warm baths, comfortable chambers, attentive servants — is a demonstration of queenly authority. By playing the gracious hostess, she asserts her legitimacy as the ruler of the House. Yet the very formality of the welcome exposes the hollowness of that legitimacy: genuine hospitality belongs to the rightful master of the house, and Clytemnestra is a usurper playing a role. Orestes accepts the hospitality in silence, allowing the irony to accumulate until the moment of revelation (PN: 001-004).

## In Performance

The scene demands an actress capable of conveying multiple layers simultaneously. Every gesture of welcome — the open arms, the nod to servants, the gracious smile — must carry the shadow of its opposite meaning. The triple knock is a powerful sound cue: three percussive beats that break the silence after the tomb's ritual lamentations, announcing the shift from the world of the dead to the world of the living — and death.

The Chorus's silent presence throughout adds to the tension: they know who the stranger truly is, and their restraint becomes an act of complicity. When the Leader urges, "Ye handmaidens, arise, be bold: / See if our moving lips have power / To aid Orestes in his hour," the audience understands that the welcome scene is not merely a prelude to action — it is the action, the moment when the trap is set and the prey walks willingly inside (PN: 001-004).
