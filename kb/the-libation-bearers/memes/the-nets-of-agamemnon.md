# The Nets of Agamemnon

The final scene of *The Libation Bearers* culminates in one of the most powerful stage images in Greek tragedy: Orestes standing over the bodies of Clytemnestra and Aegisthus while attendants hold aloft the great red robe in which Agamemnon was trapped and murdered. The robe — called variously a net, a snare, a trap, a winding-sheet — is the physical evidence that transforms Orestes from avenger into prosecutor, and the scene from revenge killing into a public act of justice (001-004).

## Text

The palace doors open to reveal Orestes standing over the two bodies:

> Behold your linkèd conquerors! Behold My Father's foes, the spoilers of the fold! Oh, lordly were these twain, when thronèd high, And lovely now, as he who sees them lie Can read, two lovers faithful to their troth! They vowed to slay my father, or that both As one should die, and both the vows were true! (001-004)

Orestes displays the robe:

> And mark, all ye who hear this tale of rue, This robe, this trap that did my father greet, Irons of the hand and shackling of the feet! Outstretch it north and south: cast wide for me This man-entangler, that our Sire may see — Not mine, but He who watcheth all deeds done, Yea, all my mother's wickedness, the Sun — And bear me witness, when they seek some day To judge me, that in justice I did slay This woman. (001-004)

He struggles to name what the robe is:

> A dead man's winding-sheet? A hunter's snare? A trap, a toil, a tangling of the feet. … I think a thief would get him this, a cheat That robs the stranger. He would snare them so, And kill them, kill them, and his heart would glow. … Not in my flesh, not in my house, O God, May this thing live! Ere that, Oh, lift thy rod And smiting blast me, dead without a child! (001-004)

The bloodstains tell their story:

> Did she the deed or no? This robe defiled Doth bear me witness, where its web is gored, How deep the dye was of Aigisthos' sword; And blood hath joined with the old years, to spoil The many tinctures of the broidered coil. (001-004)

## Speaker and Dramatic Context

- **Speaker**: [[Orestes]], son of Agamemnon, standing over the bodies of Clytemnestra and Aegisthus. He speaks not only to the Chorus and the household but to the Sun itself — the "all-seeing witness" — transforming a private act of vengeance into a public prosecution (001-004).
- **Addressee**: The Chorus, the Household, and (by extension) the people of Argos — and the Sun, invoked as the divine witness who "watcheth all deeds done" (001-004).
- **Dramatic moment**: Immediately after driving Clytemnestra into the palace to her death. The palace doors open — likely via the *ekkyklema* (a wheeled platform) — to reveal the tableau: Orestes with his sword, the two bodies, and attendants holding the vast red fabric. This is the play's final scene before the Furies manifest (001-004).
- **Form**: Rhesis (extended speech), with an increasingly agitated, almost manic quality as the speech progresses and the Furies begin to gather at the edges of Orestes' perception (001-004).

## Analysis

### The Robe as Physical Evidence

The display of the robe is Orestes' attempt to transform an act of revenge into an act of justice. By presenting the physical instrument of Agamemnon's murder — "This robe, this trap that did my father greet, / Irons of the hand and shackling of the feet!" — he makes the crime visible and undeniable. The robe is not merely a stage prop; it is the central piece of evidence in a prosecution that anticipates the courtroom of the Areopagus in *Eumenides* (001-004).

Orestes' invocation of the Sun as witness — "that our Sire may see — Not mine, but He who watcheth all deeds done, Yea, all my mother's wickedness, the Sun" — frames the matricide as a public execution rather than a private killing. The Sun, as the all-seeing deity, replaces the absent human jury. But this appeal to divine witness also reveals Orestes' insecurity: if his act were unambiguously just, he would not need to call the cosmos into the courtroom. The very vehemence of his self-justification betrays the doubt that will soon consume him (001-004).

The robe functions simultaneously as murder weapon, evidence, and sacred object. In displaying it, Orestes is performing a ritual of public accusation that has no exact parallel in Greek legal practice but resonates with the Athenian democratic ideal of transparency — make the evidence visible, let the *demos* judge (001-004).

### The Failure of Language

One of the most striking features of Orestes' speech is his struggle to name what the robe actually is. He runs through a catalogue of inadequate terms: "A dead man's winding-sheet? A hunter's snare? / A trap, a toil, a tangling of the feet." None of these names suffices because the robe is fundamentally unnameable — an object that is simultaneously a garment, a weapon, a shroud, a hunting net, a fetter, and a monument to treachery (001-004).

This rhetorical struggle — what classicists call *aporia* (ἀπορία, "impasse" or "perplexity") — prefigures Orestes' impending mental collapse. Language itself begins to fail as the Furies draw near. The man who moments ago was a confident prosecutor, marshaling evidence and calling witnesses, is reduced to a stuttering attempt to name an object that defies all categories. The robe exceeds language because the crime it embodies exceeds the capacity of human justice to contain it (001-004).

The accumulation of nouns — "winding-sheet," "snare," "trap," "toil," "tangler," "entangler" — is a rhetorical figure called *congeries* (accumulation), and Aeschylus uses it here to dramatize the inadequacy of any single category. Each term captures one aspect of the robe's function but misses the others. The object is overdetermined: it means too many things at once, and the excess of meaning threatens to overwhelm the speaker (001-004).

### The Weaver Slain

The robe's embroidery — "the many tinctures of the broidered coil" now spoiled with blood — carries a particular horror that resonates throughout the *Oresteia*. In the *Agamemnon*, Clytemnestra described the robe she wove as an instrument of justice; its rich embroidery symbolized the wealth and artistry of the House of Atreus. She boasted of its creation and of the skill with which she trapped her husband in its folds (001-004).

Now, in *Libation Bearers*, blood has merged with those tinctures, and Orestes cannot separate the woven pattern from the stain. The woman who wove the robe is dead, killed by the son she bore, and the robe bears witness to the entire cycle of creation and destruction. The imagery collapses the distinction between weaving (a female-coded domestic art) and killing (a male-coded martial act), implicating both genders in the cycle of violence (001-004).

The blood on the embroidery also literalizes the metaphor of the "bloodstained house." The tinctures (dyes) of the weaving are interpenetrated with the "dye" of Aegisthus' sword-blood, so that the artifact itself becomes a palimpsest of the family's violence. Each generation adds its layer of stain, and the pattern becomes progressively darker and more illegible (001-004).

### The Sun as Witness

Orestes' appeal to the Sun — "that our Sire may see... the Sun — And bear me witness" — is one of the most theologically charged moments in the play. The Sun (Helios) is invoked not merely as a passive observer but as an active participant in the justice process. In Greek thought, the Sun sees all things because its light reaches everywhere; it is the natural ally of truth against deception (001-004).

But the Sun's witness is double-edged. The same Sun that saw Clytemnestra's crime also sees Orestes' matricide. The all-seeing witness cannot be selective: it testifies to everything, including the horror of the act Orestes has just committed. Orestes invokes the Sun to validate his killing of Clytemnestra, but the Sun's impartial gaze will also illuminate his own guilt. This is why the invocation, for all its rhetorical power, cannot protect Orestes from the Furies: the cosmic witness that condemns Clytemnestra also condemns her killer (001-004).

The Sun imagery also connects to the trilogy's larger pattern of light and darkness. The *Agamemnon* opens with the Watchman waiting for the beacon-fire; the *Libation Bearers* ends with Orestes fleeing into darkness pursued by the dark-gowned Furies; the *Eumenides* will culminate in a torchlit procession that transforms darkness into civic light. Orestes' appeal to the Sun is a plea for his act to be placed on the side of light rather than darkness — a plea whose answer must await the third play (001-004).

### The Eumenides Foreshadowed

This scene directly sets up the trial in *Eumenides* in ways both explicit and implicit. Orestes' appeal to the Sun as witness — "bear me witness, when they seek some day / To judge me, that in justice I did slay / This woman" — anticipates the courtroom of the Areopagus with startling precision. He speaks as though already standing before a jury, already making the case that will be adjudicated in the trilogy's final play (001-004).

The robe itself will reappear in *Eumenides* as a piece of evidence in Orestes' defense. But the crucial shift is this: in *Libation Bearers*, Orestes displays the robe himself, controlling its meaning; in *Eumenides*, the meaning of the robe will be contested by opposing advocates (Apollo for the defense, the Furies for the prosecution). The object that Orestes presents as unambiguous proof of Clytemnestra's guilt will be re-framed by the Furies as proof of Orestes' own crime — the son who displays his mother's bloodstained robe is himself stained with that blood (001-004).

But here, in the moment of triumph, the Furies are already gathering at the edges of Orestes' perception. The speech that begins with confident prosecution ends with panicked flight: "Ah! Ah! Ye bondmaids! They are here: like Gorgons, gowned / In darkness." The robe that proves Clytemnestra's guilt cannot protect Orestes from his mother's avenging spirits. The evidence that secures one verdict becomes the evidence that demands another (001-004).

### The Ekkyklema and Theatrical Spectacle

The robe-display scene is one of the most visually demanding moments in Greek tragedy. The *ekkyklema* (a wheeled platform) would roll out from the central doors of the *skēnē* to reveal the tableau: Orestes with his sword, the two bodies, and attendants holding the vast red fabric. The robe itself — traditionally a deep crimson cloth large enough to fill the acting space — becomes the dominant visual element, its colour echoing the blood that has been spilled across three generations of the House of Atreus (001-004).

Orestes' command to "outstretch it north and south: cast wide for me / This man-entangler" is a directorial cue embedded in the text. The attendants spread the fabric across the full width of the playing space, making the hidden crime visible to every spectator in the Theatre of Dionysus. The moment transforms the *orchēstra* into a courtroom exhibit, the audience into a jury, and the tragedy into a trial (001-004).

The moment when Orestes' triumphant rhetoric shifts to terror — "Ah! Ah! Ye bondmaids! They are here!" — is the play's final theatrical shock. The avenger who displayed the evidence of his mother's crime becomes the pursued who flees the evidence of his own. The *ekkyklema*, which revealed the tableau of justice, now becomes the platform from which Orestes flees in terror. The theatrical machine that exposed the crime cannot contain its consequences (001-004).
