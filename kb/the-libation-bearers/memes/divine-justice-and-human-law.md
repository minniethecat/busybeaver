# Divine Justice and Human Law

the conflict between the ancient chthonic law of blood-vengeance (the Furies' domain) and the Olympian civic justice (Apollo and Athena's domain) — the central theological argument of the Oresteia.

## In The Libation Bearers

The play explores this theme through its central dramatic situation: Orestes is commanded by Apollo to kill his mother — an act that is simultaneously the fulfilment of filial duty and the most terrible violation of kinship. The Chorus of captive Trojan women, who are both slaves and the play's moral conscience, embody the paradoxical status of those caught between freedom and servitude.

## Dramatic Manifestation

The theme runs through the play's key scenes — the kommos at the tomb, the recognition between Orestes and Electra, the confrontation with Clytemnestra, and the final flight from the Furies. In each, the play refuses to resolve the ambiguity: the demands of justice and the bonds of blood cannot be separated.

## Across the Trilogy

The Oresteia traces the transformation of this theme across three plays: from the private violence of the *Agamemnon* through the crisis of *The Libation Bearers* to the civic resolution of the *Eumenides*.
