[[orestes|Orestes]]' revelation of [[apollo|Apollo]]'s command, spoken at the grave of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] as the Kommos gathers intensity. The speech moves from divine mission to cosmic terror — the god's promise of what awaits the disobedient — in language of mounting horror.

## Text

> *He speaks with increasing horror as he proceeds.*
> Oh, Loxias shall not mock my great desire,
> Who spoke his divine promise, charging me
> To thread this peril to the extremity:
> Yea, raised his awful voice and surging told
> To my hot heart of horrors stormy-cold
> Till I seek out those murderers, by the road
> Themselves have shown — so spake he — blood for blood,
> In gold-rejecting rage, the wild bull's way!
> If not, for their offending I must pay
> With mine own life, in torment manifold.
> Of many things that rise from earth he told,
> To appease the angry dead: yea, and strange forms,
> On thee and me, of savage-fangèd worms,
> Climbing the flesh; lichens, which eat away
> Even unto nothingness our natural clay.
> And when they leave him, a man's hair is white.
> For him that disobeys, he said, the night
> Hath Furies, shapen of his father's blood;
> Clear-seen, with eyeball straining through the hood
> Of darkness. The blind arrows of dead men
> Who cried their kin for mercy and were slain,
> And madness, and wild fear out of the night,
> Shall spur him, rack him, till from all men's sight
> Alone he goes, out to the desert dim,
> And that bronze horror clanging after him!
> For such as he there is no mixing bowl,
> No dear libation that binds soul to soul:
> From every altar fire the unseen rage
> Outbars him: none shall give him harbourage,
> Nor rest beneath one roof with such an one;
> Till, without worship, without love, alone
> He crawls to his death, a carcase to the core
> Through-rotted, and embalmed to suffer more.
> *Collecting himself.*
> So spake he … God, and is one to believe
> Such oracles as these? Nay, though I give
> No credence, the deed now must needs be done.
> So many things of power work here as one:
> The God's command; grief for my father slain;
> And mine own beggary urgeth me amain,
> That never shall these Argives, famed afar,
> High conquerors of Troy in joyous war
> Cower to … two women. For he bears, I know,
> A woman's heart. … If not, this day will show. (001-002)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[orestes|Orestes]], [[orestes|son of Agamemnon]] and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]
- **Addressee**: [[electra|Electra]] and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] (kneeling at the Grave during the Kommos)
- **Dramatic moment**: The invocation scene is reaching its peak. [[orestes|Orestes]], pressed by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] and [[electra|Electra]]'s grief, reveals the full content of [[apollo|Apollo]]'s oracle — a speech he has kept within himself since his [[exile-and-return|return]]. The stage direction notes he "speaks with increasing horror as he proceeds."
- **Form**: rhessis (extended narrative speech), with a final turn to self-collection and renewed resolve

## Analysis

This is the play's most sustained depiction of divine command as psychological horror. [[apollo|Apollo]]'s oracle is presented not as a blessing or a mandate but as a threat: the god has described, in visceral physical detail, what will happen to [[orestes|Orestes]] if he *fails* to kill his mother. The description moves through stages of escalating bodily destruction — "savage-fangèd worms / Climbing the flesh," lichens that "eat away / Even unto nothingness our natural clay," hair turned white, madness, [[exile-and-return|exile]]. The punishment is not merely death but "a carcase to the core / Through-rotted, and embalmed to suffer more."

The Furies are already present in embryo: "the night / Hath Furies, shapen of his father's blood" — the very beings who will appear visible to [[orestes|Orestes]] at the play's end. The speech thus prefigures its own aftermath; the punishment [[apollo|Apollo]] threatens for disobedience is substantially identical to what will pursue [[orestes|Orestes]] *after* obedience.

Murray identifies this as the Aeschylean moment of psychological complexity that [[euripides|Euripides]] would later develop: "the character of [[orestes|Orestes]], in particular, contains here in germ just the ideas that are so subtly developed in [[euripides|Euripides]]" (P01-006). The speech's structure — horror rising to a pitch, then a deliberate self-collection ("So spake he … God, and is one to believe / Such oracles as these?") — shows [[orestes|Orestes]] struggling to convert terror into resolve. The final turn to the political ("That never shall these [[argos|Argive]]s, famed afar, / High conquerors of [[troy|Troy]] … Cower to … two women") is a shift of register that feels like self-persuasion: the oracle's horror cannot be faced directly, so it must be displaced into contempt for [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]].

## In Performance

The stage direction — "He speaks with increasing horror as he proceeds" — is crucial. The actor's delivery must move from controlled report to mounting terror, then deliberately recover at "So spake he … God." The speech's physical imagery is intensely somatic — worms, lichens, white hair, the "bronze horror clanging" — and would reward a delivery that makes the body's destruction palpable. The final "For he bears, I know, / A woman's heart" is a misogynistic jibe aimed at [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]] — a moment of savage contempt that punctures the speech's cosmic register and [[exile-and-return|return]]s [[orestes|Orestes]] to the language of warrior honour.
