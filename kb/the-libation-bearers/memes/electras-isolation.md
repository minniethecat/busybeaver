# Electra's Isolation

In the midst of the great kommos at Agamemnon's tomb — the ritual lamentation shared by Orestes, Electra, and the Chorus — Electra delivers a brief but devastating account of her condition in the house of her father's murderers. The image of the princess reduced to a dog driven from the door is one of the most compressed expressions of injustice in Greek tragedy.

## Text

> Thou tellest the doom he died, but I saw him not;
> I was far off, dishonoured and nothing worth.
> Like a dog they drove me back, and the door was shut,
> And alone I poured my tears to him through the earth.
> I laughed not, yet rejoiced that none saw me weep.—
> Write this in thine heart, O Father; grave it deep. (PN: 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: Electra, daughter of Agamemnon and Clytemnestra
- **Addressee**: The dead Agamemnon, Orestes, and the Chorus at the tomb
- **Dramatic moment**: During the climactic kommos (ritual lament) at Agamemnon's tomb. Electra has just heard Orestes describe the mutilation of their father's body. She responds by recounting her own exclusion from the burial rites.
- **Form**: Kommos — a lyrical dialogue of lamentation between actors and Chorus, structured in strophe/antistrophe pairs

## Analysis

Electra's three couplets compress a world of suffering into six lines. The first couplet establishes the fundamental injustice: she was "far off" and "dishonoured," deprived of even the minimal dignity of witnessing her father's burial. The second couplet provides the indelible image — "Like a dog they drove me back, and the door was shut" — in which the princess, rightful heir of the House, is reduced to the status of an animal barred from entry. The third couplet reveals the psychological cost: even her grief must be hidden, wept in solitude "through the earth" rather than openly expressed.

The command "Write this in thine heart, O Father; grave it deep" is a pivotal dramatic moment. Electra is not merely lamenting; she is actively inscribing her suffering into her father's memory, ensuring that the dead king will rise with full knowledge of the wrongs done to his children (PN: 002-044). This is the mechanism by which grief becomes fuel for vengeance.

The image of the shut door resonates throughout the play: it is the same door through which Orestes will later enter in disguise, and through which the bodies will be displayed at the end. The threshold Electra was denied becomes the site of her vindication.

## In Performance

Electra delivers these lines kneeling at the tomb, her voice shifting from the narrative past tense ("they drove me back") to the urgent imperative ("Write this in thine heart"). The physical gesture of pressing her hands to the earth reinforces the image of tears poured "through the earth" to reach the dead below.
