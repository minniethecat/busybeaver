## Overview

Sacrifice and offering — particularly the ritual pouring of [[libation-ritual|libations]] (χοαί, *[[libation-ritual|choai]]*) to the dead — is the defining religious act of *The Libation Bearers*. The play's Greek title, Χοηφόροι (*Choēphoroi*), means "The Libation Bearers," naming the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of captive women who carry offerings to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb. The libation ritual opens the action, structures the [[kommos]], and provides the theological framework through which the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] understands the relationship between the living and the dead.

## The Libation Ritual

In Greek funerary practice, [[libation-ritual|libations]] (from σπένδω, *spendō*, "I pour") were liquid offerings — typically wine, water, honey, milk, or oil — poured onto the earth at a grave to nourish and honor the dead. The ritual established a bond between the living and the deceased: the dead received sustenance and honor; the living hoped for the dead's goodwill and, in some traditions, their active assistance.

The [[libation-ritual|libations]] in *The Libation Bearers* are brought at [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s command, sent to appease [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] after a terrifying dream. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] explains:

> Craving to fly that curse / With graceless gift hither she urgeth me — / O Earth, Mother and Nurse! — / She whom God hateth. (001-001)

The offering is "graceless" because it comes from the murderer. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s theological reflection — "When blood is spilt, how shall a gift set free?" (001-001) — poses the central question of the ritual: can offerings from polluted hands achieve what they intend, or does the [[blood-guilt|pollution]] infect the gift itself?

## Offerings in the Play

### The Opening Procession

The play begins with the procession of libation bearers approaching the tomb. [[orestes|Orestes]], observing from hiding, recognizes the ritual:

> Is it to my father's grave they go / With offerings, to appease the wrath below? (001-001)

The word "appease" (θέλγειν, *thelgein*, to charm or soothe) captures the ritual's purpose: the dead are angry, and offerings are meant to calm their wrath. But [[orestes|Orestes]] immediately reinterprets the ritual as an opportunity for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] rather than appeasement.

### Electra's Dilemma

At the tomb, [[electra|Electra]] confronts the paradox of the offering brought by her mother. She hesitates before pouring:

> In shame and silence shall I pour this urn / Of offering to the dust, and pouring turn, / As men cast out some foulness they abhor, / And fling the cup, and fly, and look no more? (001-002)

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] advises her to transform the ritual: rather than a peace offering to the dead, make it a prayer for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. The libation, meant to soothe, becomes a weapon — a request for aid in killing the sender of the gift.

### The Kommos as Offering

[[the-great-kommos|The Great Kommos]] (lines 306–478) functions as an extended ritual offering. Brother and sister, joined by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]], pour not wine but words onto the tomb — a λοιβή λόγων (*loibē logōn*), a libation of speech. They invoke their father, recall his death, and summon his spirit to aid their [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. The structure of [[choral-ode|strophe and antistrophe]], with its alternation of voices, mirrors the ritual pouring of liquid offerings: each voice adds its [[moira|portion]] to the accumulating power of the rite.

### Orestes's Exclusion from Offering

[[orestes|Orestes]]'s description of the punishment awaiting the disobedient includes the ultimate ritual exclusion:

> For such as he there is no mixing bowl, / No dear libation that binds soul to soul. (001-002)

The "mixing bowl" (κρατήρ, *kratēr*) and "libation" are the instruments of social bonding through shared ritual. To be excluded from them is to be excluded from human community itself. This is the terror that drives [[orestes|Orestes]]: not merely physical punishment but ritual and social death.

## Sacrifice in the Oresteia

The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] traces a transformation in the meaning of sacrifice. In *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, sacrifice is perverted — Iphigenia's death at her father's hands echoes through the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] as the primal sacrificial crime. In *The Libation Bearers*, sacrifice becomes ambiguous: the [[libation-ritual|libations]] at the tomb seek both to honor the dead and to authorize more killing. The murder of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] is described in sacrificial language, as if her death were an offering to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s spirit.

In *Eumenides*, sacrifice finds its proper form at last. The Furies, transformed into the Eumenides, receive sacrifices not of blood but of honor — "sacrifices prior to marriage and to birth" (002-131). The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s arc is from perverted sacrifice through ambiguous sacrifice to reconciled sacrifice, from blood spilled on altars to offerings that sustain rather than destroy.

## The Title's Significance

The Greek title Χοηφόροι ("The Libation Bearers") identifies the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] — captive Trojan women who carry the offerings — as the play's defining presence. These women, enslaved and silenced, become the ritual agents through whom the dead are honored, the gods are invoked, and the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] is authorized. The title declares that the play is not fundamentally about [[orestes|Orestes]] or [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] but about the ritual act — the pouring of offerings — that mediates between the living and the dead, between crime and punishment, between the old order of [[divine-vengeance|blood-vengeance]] and the new order of law.
