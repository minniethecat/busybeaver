# The Chorus's Lament for Agamemnon

The parodos (entrance song) of *The Libation Bearers*, sung by the Chorus of enslaved women as they accompany [[Electra]] to the tomb of [[Agamemnon]], bearing libation offerings sent by [[Clytemnestra]]. This opening choral ode establishes the play's emotional and thematic register: grief suppressed into ritual, terror erupting from dreams, the irreversibility of blood-guilt, and the peculiar position of enslaved mourners who must obey the very hands that killed their king.

## Text

> **Strophe 1**
> Driven, yea, driven I come: I bear
> Peace-offering to the dead,
> Mine hands as blades that tear, my tresses riven,
> And cheek ploughed red.
> But all my years, before this day as after,
> Have been fed full with weeping as with bread.
> And this dumb cry of linen, as in pain,
> Deep rent about my bosom, speaketh plain
> Of a life long since wounded, where no laughter
> Sounds nor shall sound again.
>
> **Antistrophe 1**
> Dread, very dread,
> And hair upstarting and the wrath that streams
> From the heart of sleep, have first interpreted
> What manner of dreams
> This house hath dreamed; a voice of terror, blasting
> The midnight, up from the inmost place it grew,
> Shaking the women's chambers; and the Seer,
> Being sworn of God, made answer, there is here
> Anger of dead men wronged, and hate outlasting
> Death, against them that slew.
>
> **Strophe 2**
> Craving to fly that curse
> With graceless gift hither she urgeth me
> —O Earth, Mother and Nurse!—
> She whom God hateth. But my spirit fears
> To speak the word it bears.
> When blood is spilt, how shall a gift set free?
> O hearthstone wet with tears!
> O pillars of a house broken in twain!
> Without sun, without love,
> Murk in the heart thereof and mist above,
> For a lord slain!
>
> **Antistrophe 3**
> The shrine of maidenhood
> Once broken ne'er may be unbroke again.
> And where man's life hath flowed,
> All the world's rivers in their multitude
> Rolling shall strive in vain
> To clean from a brother's hand that ancient blood.
>
> For me, God in far days
> Laid hand upon my city, and herded me
> From my old home to the House of Slavery,
> Where all is violence, and I needs must praise,
> Just or unjust,
> The pleasure of them that rule, and speechless hold
> The ache of a heart that rageth in the dust.
> Only behind the fold
> Of this still veil for a little I hide my face
> And weep for the blind doings of this race,
> And secret tears are in my heart, ice-cold.
> (PN 001-002)

## Context

- **Speaker**: The Chorus of enslaved women, libation-bearers
- **Addressee**: The world at large, the Earth, and by implication the dead [[Agamemnon]]
- **Dramatic moment**: The very first choral ode of the play. The Chorus enters with [[Electra]], bearing the libation offerings that [[Clytemnestra]] has sent in terrified response to her serpent dream. They process toward Agamemnon's tomb, their ritual mourning barely containing suppressed fury.
- **Form**: choral ode (parodos) in strophe/antistrophe pairs, lyric meters

## Analysis

This parodos is one of the densest choral passages in extant Greek tragedy, layering three distinct perspectives into a single movement. First, the Chorus speaks as *mourners* — their grief is not performative but visceral ("fed full with weeping as with bread"), rooted in years of living under the usurper [[Aigisthos]]. Second, they speak as *interpreters of dreams* — it is the Chorus, not Clytemnestra, who first announces the "voice of terror" from the midnight, the dream that sets the entire plot in motion. Third, they speak as *slaves* — the closing antistrophe is one of the few passages in Greek tragedy that directly represents the consciousness of the enslaved: forced to "praise, just or unjust, the pleasure of them that rule," hiding their true faces behind a veil.

The central theological claim of the ode — "when blood is spilt, how shall a gift set free?" — is the question the entire trilogy seeks to answer. The Chorus knows that libations cannot wash away murder; the blood cries up from the earth. This is the archaic religious logic that drives the revenge plot, but it is also the logic that the trilogy will ultimately transcend in *The Eumenides*, when the [[Erinyes]] are transformed from private avengers into civic guardians.

The Chorus's dual identity — loyal retainers who loved Agamemnon, yet slaves who must serve his killers — creates the play's distinctive moral texture. They are not neutral observers; they are partisan, hungry for the revenge to come, yet powerless to act except through speech and witness. Their "secret tears, ice-cold" anticipate the heat of the vengeance that Orestes will bring.

## In Performance

The parodos combines ritual procession with choral song and dance. The Chorus enters with torn garments, scratched cheeks, and disheveled hair — the traditional signs of mourning. Their movement should convey the tension between outward ritual obedience (they carry Clytemnestra's offerings) and inward rage. The strophe/antistrophe structure creates a physical pattern of advance and retreat, mirroring the Chorus's oscillation between speaking truth and hiding behind the veil.
