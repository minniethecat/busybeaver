# The Nurse's Grief

The entrance of Cilissa, Orestes' old nurse, is one of the most unexpected and humanizing moments in Greek tragedy. Sent by Clytemnestra to summon Aigisthos to hear the news of Orestes' death, she pauses to pour out her grief in a monologue that recalls the intimate, bodily reality of caring for an infant — a world utterly remote from the heroic cycle of murder and revenge that drives the play.

## Text

The Nurse enters weeping:

> The stranger works some mischief, it would seem! Yonder I see Orestes' Nurse, a-stream With tears. (PN: 001-004)

Cilissa's monologue:

> The mistress bids me call Aigisthos here Quickly, to see these two, and learn more clear, As man from man, the truth of what they tell. Oh, to us slaves she makes it pitiable And grievous, and keeps hid behind her eyes The leaping laughter. Aye, 'tis a rich prize For her, and for the House stark misery, This news the travellers tell so trippingly. (PN: 001-004)

Her memories of baby Orestes:

> But little Orestes, my heart's care, the child I took straight from his mother; and save me He had no other nurse! And, Oh, but he Could scream and order me to tramp the dark! Aye, times enough, and trouble enough, and stark Wasted at that! A small thing at the breast, That has no sense, you tend it like a beast, By guesswork. For he never speaks, not he, A babe in swaddling clothes, if thirst maybe Or hunger comes, or any natural need. The little belly takes its way. Indeed, 'Twas oft a prophet he wanted, not a nurse; And often enough my prophecies, of course, Came late, and then 'twas clothes to wash and dry, And fuller's work as much as nurse's. Aye, I followed both trades, from the day when first His father gave me Orestes to be nursed. (PN: 001-004)

Her final lament:

> And now he is dead; and strangers come and tell The news to me. And this poor miserable Old woman must go tell the plunderer Who shames this house! Oh, glad he will be to hear! (PN: 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: Cilissa (the Old Nurse of Orestes)
- **Addressee**: The Chorus Leader
- **Dramatic moment**: After Clytemnestra has welcomed the disguised Orestes and retreated to the women's quarters. She sends the Nurse to summon Aigisthos — but the Nurse, loyal to the memory of the child she raised, can barely contain her grief and resentment at being forced to deliver news that will bring joy to the usurper.
- **Form**: Rhesis (extended speech), with a distinctive colloquial, almost comic register that contrasts with the elevated diction of the main characters

## Analysis

### The Ordinary in the Midst of Tragedy

Cilissa's monologue is a radical tonal departure. Where the rest of the play speaks in the language of gods, curses, and heroic vengeance, the Nurse speaks of soiled diapers, hungry bellies, and the guesswork of infant care. "A small thing at the breast, / That has no sense, you tend it like a beast, / By guesswork" — this is the most un-heroic language in the *Oresteia*, and its very ordinariness makes it devastating (PN: 001-004). In a play about the cosmic consequences of murder, Aeschylus pauses to remind us that every avenger was once a baby who could not speak, whose needs were met by the rough love of a servant.

### Comedy as Dramatic Relief and Deepening

The Nurse's speech has a comic quality — "I followed both trades, from the day when first / His father gave me Orestes to be nursed" — that provides dramatic relief after the intense kommos at the tomb. But the comedy is not mere relief; it deepens the tragedy. Orestes is not only Agamemnon's avenger and Apollo's instrument; he was once a screaming infant whose every need was met by this old woman. The Nurse's grief makes the abstraction of "vengeance" concrete: when Orestes kills Clytemnestra, he is killing the mother of the child Cilissa raised (PN: 001-004).

### Class and Loyalty

Cilissa sees through Clytemnestra's performance of grief with the clarity of the powerless: "she makes it pitiable / And grievous, and keeps hid behind her eyes / The leaping laughter." The Nurse, a slave, possesses moral clarity that the queen, in her web of deception, has lost. Her loyalty is not to the ruling power but to the child she raised — a loyalty that transcends class and politics. The Chorus Leader, recognizing this loyalty, will use it to manipulate the message she carries to Aigisthos, stripping his bodyguard for the assassination (PN: 001-004).

### The "Plunderer" and the True King

Cilissa's description of Aigisthos as "the plunderer / Who shames this house" is the plainest condemnation of the usurper in the play. While the Chorus speaks of Aigisthos in elaborate metaphors of lions and serpents, the Nurse speaks the simple truth: he stole what was not his and shamed the house he stole. Her moral vocabulary is simpler than the Chorus's but no less powerful — perhaps more so, because it comes from a place of unadorned feeling rather than poetic convention.

## In Performance

The Nurse's scene requires a complete shift in theatrical register. After the sonorous, incantatory kommos, the audience is suddenly confronted with a character who speaks in the rhythms of everyday complaint. The actor must balance comedy and pathos: the audience should smile at the memories of dirty diapers, then feel the weight of the loss when the Nurse says "And now he is dead."

The scene also functions as a crucial plot mechanism: the Chorus persuades the Nurse to alter Clytemnestra's message, telling Aigisthos to come alone and unarmed. The Nurse, whose grief has made her pliable, becomes an unwitting accomplice in the assassination — a fact that adds a layer of tragic irony to her simple, loving monologue.
