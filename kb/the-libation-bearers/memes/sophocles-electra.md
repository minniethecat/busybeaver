[[sophocles|Sophocles]]' *[[electra|Electra]]*, whose date of composition is unknown, represents a deliberate sidestep from the religious problem that [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] placed at the centre of his *Choephoroe*. Gil Murray characterises the choice with precision: "[[sophocles|Sophocles]], apparently from a deliberate adoption of the '[[homer|Homeric]]' tone, suppresses the religious problem and concentrates on the elements of direct passion" (P01-002).

## Dramatic Conception

[[sophocles|Sophocles]] makes [[electra|Electra]] the undisputed protagonist — the play is named after her, not after the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of libation-bearers. Where [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] distributes attention between [[orestes|Orestes]], [[electra|Electra]], the [[the-chorus|Chorus]], and the ghost of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], [[sophocles|Sophocles]] concentrates the emotional weight in [[electra|Electra]]'s sustained suffering. Her grief is immediate, physical, and unphilosophical. The religious question — is [[orestes|Orestes]]' [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] a sin or a duty? — is simply not asked. Murray argues that [[sophocles|Sophocles]] "would not, in that region, play at make-believe" but also "would not elude the horror of this story by simply not mentioning it, like [[homer|Homer]], or by pretending that an evil act was a good one, like [[sophocles|Sophocles]]" (P01-002). The parenthetical judgment is pointed: [[sophocles|Sophocles]], by suppressing the theological problem, effectively pretends "that an evil act was a good one."

## Absence of the Kommos

The most telling structural difference is the absence of the invocation scene. "There is no invocation scene in [[sophocles|Sophocles]]; there is a brief one in [[euripides|Euripides]] ([[electra|Electra]] 671–685)" (002-044). [[the-great-kommos|The Great Kommos]] — [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s two-hundred-line antiphonal lament that summons the dead [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] — has no equivalent in [[sophocles|Sophocles]]. Without it, the play loses the ritual dimension that makes [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s treatment "a union of primitive ghostliness with high intellectual passion" (P01-004). [[sophocles|Sophocles]]'s play is more human, more emotionally direct, and — on the theological level — simpler.

## Character Treatment

Murray's comparison of the three [[electra|Electra]]s is illuminating:

- **[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s [[electra|Electra]]**: "much less minutely and without doubt less cruelly studied: almost all her words are beautiful, and she keeps a kind of tenderness even in her prayers of hate" (P01-007).
- **[[euripides|Euripides]]'s [[electra|Electra]]**: "bears the main weight of the tragedy on her own shoulders" (P01-007) — a figure consumed and hardened by her bitterness.
- **[[sophocles|Sophocles]]'s [[electra|Electra]]**: By implication, stands between these poles — heroic, suffering, but without [[euripides|Euripides]]'s psychological complexity or [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s religious depth.

## Clytemnestra's Treatment

[[sophocles|Sophocles]]'s [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] is different again. The Endnotes note: "In [[sophocles|Sophocles]] ([[electra|Electra]] 516) she bursts in upon [[orestes|Orestes]] and [[electra|Electra]] in a sudden agony of rage" (002-091). This contrasts with the "dream attributed to [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] by [[sophocles|Sophocles]] ([[electra|Electra]] 445), and to the witticism of the sophists we perhaps owe the criticism of it" (002-050). Where [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] gives [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] a somnambulistic dream-vision that drives her to send the [[libation-ritual|libations]], [[sophocles|Sophocles]] handles the dream material with a more rationalist touch.

## Relationship to Aeschylus

Murray positions [[sophocles|Sophocles]] as standing aside from the main current of development. "The development of Greek tragedy moves almost straight from [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] to [[euripides|Euripides]], with [[sophocles|Sophocles]] standing aside" (P01-006). [[sophocles|Sophocles]]'s *[[electra|Electra]]* represents not an evolution but a different species of tragedy — one that chooses not to engage with the religious problem [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] placed at the heart of the myth. "The date of [[sophocles|Sophocles]]' play is unknown" (P01-008), making it impossible to know whether it predates or postdates [[euripides|Euripides]]'s version, but its artistic stance is clearly defined: [[homer|Homeric]] in tone, focused on character rather than cosmology.
