## Overview

Anagnorisis (ἀναγνώρισις, "[[recognition-scene|recognition]]" or "discovery") is the dramatic moment when a character's true identity or the true nature of a situation is revealed. In Aristotle's *Poetics*, [[recognition-and-tokens|anagnorisis]] is one of the three core elements of complex tragic plot alongside peripeteia (reversal) and pathos (suffering). *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* contains one of Greek tragedy's most famous [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scenes — [[electra|Electra]]'s identification of [[orestes|Orestes]] through the traditional [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] of the lock, the footprint, and the weaving.

## The Recognition Scene

The [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* (lines 164–234) is built around three traditional "[[recognition-and-tokens|signs]]" (σύμβολα, *symbola*) by which [[electra|Electra]] identifies her brother:

1. **The lock of hair** — [[orestes|Orestes]] has left a lock of hair on [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb. [[electra|Electra]] discovers it and compares it to her own: "surely they are shaped like mine" (001-002). The similarity of family members' hair was a traditional [[recognition-scene|recognition]] motif in Greek folklore.

2. **The footprint** — "Ah see, the print of feet, a second sign! The same feet: surely they are shaped like mine" (001-002). [[electra|Electra]] matches the footprint in the earth to her own, identifying a familial resemblance in the "mark of the long muscle thrown athwart them on the sand."

3. **The weaving** — [[orestes|Orestes]] reveals a piece of weaving that [[electra|Electra]] herself made, the final and conclusive proof of his identity.

The scene is notable for [[electra|Electra]]'s psychological hesitation. She longs to believe the [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] but fears disappointment:

> Had it but a voice to speak / Like some kind messenger, I had not been / This phantom tossing in the wind between / Two fancies. (001-002)

This "phantom tossing" between hope and doubt gives the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene its dramatic power. [[electra|Electra]] does not rush to belief; she interrogates the evidence, aware that desire can distort perception.

## The Poetic Tradition

The [[recognition-scene|recognition]] by [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] was part of the traditional story well before [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]. The endnotes to *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* record that Aristophanes in the *Clouds* (534 ff.) alludes to [[electra|Electra]] "recognizing its brother's tress" (002-029). Sophocles in his *[[electra|Electra]]* omits the traditional [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] entirely, while [[euripides|Euripides]] uses them "in a completely original way to illustrate [[electra|Electra]]'s state of mind," having an old peasant try to show her the [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] while she, "in fear of disappointment refuses to look at them and rejects every suggestion of comfort" (002-029).

The translator notes that "it would be a mistake to apply realist canons to this ancient tale," observing that among barefooted peoples, family likenesses are primarily traced in feet and hair — both Arab and Australian "trackers" are cited to this effect, as is the *Odyssey* (IV 148 ff., XIX 358, 381) (002-029).

[[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s version stands between the archaic simplicity of the folktale and the psychological sophistication of his successors. The [[recognition-and-tokens|signs]] are taken seriously as evidence, but [[electra|Electra]]'s hesitation introduces a layer of emotional realism absent from the bare tradition.

## Dramatic Function

Within the structure of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene serves three functions:

| Function | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| **Plot pivot** | Transforms the [[kommos]] from a lament for the dead into a conspiracy of the living. Before [[recognition-scene|recognition]], brother and sister mourn separately; after, they plan revenge together |
| **Emotional release** | Provides the first moment of joy in a play dominated by grief and dread. [[electra|Electra]]'s "phantom tossing" resolves into certainty |
| **Thematic bridge** | The "[[recognition-and-tokens|signs]]" that prove [[orestes|Orestes]]'s identity also prove his claim to [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. Recognition of the person is [[recognition-scene|recognition]] of the duty |

The scene concludes with [[electra|Electra]]'s joyful exclamation — a rare moment of unalloyed happiness that makes the subsequent descent into [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] all the more devastating.

## Anagnorisis in the Oresteia

Beyond the literal [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene, [[recognition-and-tokens|anagnorisis]] operates thematically across the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]:

- **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]**: [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s "[[recognition-scene|recognition]]" of her own power after killing her husband — a dark [[recognition-and-tokens|anagnorisis]] of self-realization through violence
- **The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers**: The formal [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene, followed by [[orestes|Orestes]]'s gradual "[[recognition-scene|recognition]]" of the horror of what he must do
- **Eumenides**: Athena's "[[recognition-scene|recognition]]" of the Furies' legitimate claim, transforming them from avengers into guardians — an [[recognition-and-tokens|anagnorisis]] of institutional wisdom

The final [[recognition-scene|recognition]] — that the cycle of violence can be broken not by denying the claims of the dead but by incorporating them into civic order — is the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s ultimate [[recognition-and-tokens|anagnorisis]].
