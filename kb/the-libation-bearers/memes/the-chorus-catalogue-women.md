# The Chorus's catalogue of monstrous women

Before Orestes enters the palace to kill Aegisthus, the Chorus delivers one of the most striking choral passages in *The Libation Bearers*: a catalogue of mythological women whose crimes serve as precedents for Clytemnestra's. This *paradeigma* (paradeigmatic catalogue) is both a rhetorical device and a window into Greek anxieties about female power.

## 原文与来源

The Chorus invokes three mythic exempla (001-004):

> Althaea, who burned the brand that was her son
> Meleager's life … Scylla, who for Minos's gold
> Betrayed her father Nisus and his immortal hair …
> And the Lemnian women, who killed their husbands
> Every one, and made their island a byword
> Through all the world for horror.
> (001-004)

## 说话人与语境

The Chorus of Libation Bearers — captive Trojan women serving in the Argive palace — sing this ode as Orestes prepares to confront Aegisthus (001-004). They have already endorsed the vengeance as just, and this catalogue serves to place Clytemnestra within a tradition of female transgression that demands punishment (001-004).

The three myths span the Greek world: Althaea of Calydon (central Greece), Scylla of Megara (the Isthmus), and the Lemnian women (the northern Aegean). This geographical range universalizes the point: female violence against kin is not a local aberration but a recurring pattern across Hellenic myth (001-004).

## 含义解析

Each exemplum illustrates a different dimension of Clytemnestra's crime:

1. **Althaea** killed her son Meleager by burning the brand that was magically linked to his life — a mother's destruction of her own child, mirroring Clytemnestra's willingness to curse Orestes and her spiritual (if not literal) attack on her son (001-004).

2. **Scylla** betrayed her father Nisus for the love of the besieging King Minos, cutting the immortal purple lock of his hair — a daughter's betrayal of her father, paralleling Clytemnestra's murder of Agamemnon, her husband and the father of her children (001-004).

3. **The Lemnian women** killed all the men on their island — a collective act of gendered violence that made Lemnos a "byword through all the world for horror," foreshadowing how Clytemnestra's crime will be remembered (001-004).

The catalogue culminates in the assertion that Clytemnestra surpasses them all: "But she who slew her husband, her lord, her king — / Above all these in daring shall her name be stablished" (001-004). This rhetorical escalation serves the Chorus's purpose — to justify the matricide by establishing Clytemnestra as the ultimate transgressive woman whose death restores cosmic order (001-004).
