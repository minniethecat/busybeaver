# Atreus

The legendary king whose crime against his brother Thyestes started the cycle of blood-vengeance consuming the House of Atreus across three generations, from Atreus to Agamemnon to Orestes.

## The Sin of Atreus

Atreus, king of Mycenae, committed one of the most infamous crimes in Greek mythology: he murdered Thyestes' children and served them to their father at a feast. This act of revenge (for Thyestes' seduction of Atreus' wife Aerope) established the ancestral curse — the *Ara* — that would haunt the family for generations.

## In the Oresteia

The Chorus of *The Libation Bearers* invokes Atreus' sin as the origin of the house's suffering. "There came upon Atreus' Hall / Lion and lion friend" (001-004), they sing, connecting the present violence to the primordial crime. "First came the sin of Atreus against the children of Thyestes" (002-130), the notes explain, tracing the trilogy's chain of vengeance to its root.

## Dramatic Significance

Atreus himself never appears on stage in the Oresteia, but his shadow looms over every scene. The audience understands that Orestes' matricide is not an isolated act but the latest link in a chain that began generations earlier. The trilogy's resolution in the *Eumenides* — the transformation of private vengeance into civic justice — represents not just the redemption of Orestes but the breaking of Atreus' curse.
