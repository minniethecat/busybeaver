# Purification Rite

Purification (*katharmos*) is the ritual means by which blood-guilt is cleansed in Greek religion. In [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe*, the play's very title names the central [[catharsis|purification]] ritual — the pouring of [[libation-ritual|libations]] (*[[libation-ritual|choai]]*) at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb — yet the play demonstrates that mere ritual is insufficient to break the cycle of [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]].

## Definition

In Greek religious practice, *katharmos* encompassed a range of purificatory actions: washing with water (especially sea water or spring water), sacrifice of a purifying animal (often a piglet), [[exile-and-return|exile]] for a prescribed period, and — crucially for the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* — trial by law. The *Choephoroe* dramatizes the limits of ritual [[catharsis|purification]]: [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] sends [[libation-ritual|libations]] to the tomb hoping to appease the dead (001-002), but the offerings, instead of purifying her, become the instrument of her destruction when [[electra|Electra]] and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] repurpose them as an "appeal for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]" (002-023).

## Greek Religious Context

Purification was ubiquitous in Greek life — from the sprinkling of worshippers at temple entrances to the elaborate [[catharsis|cleansing]] of murderers at [[delphi|Delphi]]. The paradox [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] explores is that [[catharsis|purification]] from [[the-mother-murder|kin-murder]] cannot be achieved by the murderer's own ritual actions. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s [[libation-ritual|libations]] are rejected; her dream of the serpent at her breast (001-002) reveals that her [[blood-guilt|pollution]] cannot be washed away by offerings. True [[catharsis|purification]] requires either the death of the polluted person or divine intervention.

## Role in The Libation Bearers

The play's ritual framework is established from its opening: the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of [[the-chorus|Libation-Bearers]] approaches the tomb to pour offerings — "Peace-offering to the dead" (001-002). The [[libation-ritual|libations]] are the *[[libation-ritual|choai]]* that give the play its Greek name (*[[the-chorus|Choephoroi]]*). Yet the ritual is inverted: what [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] intends as appeasement becomes, through [[electra|Electra]]'s prayer, a summoning of the dead king to aid his avengers.

The grave itself is described as a site of potential [[catharsis|purification]], but one that requires blood: "the offerings as an appeal for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]" (002-023). [[orestes|Orestes]] frames his mission as purificatory — [[catharsis|cleansing]] his house of the usurpers — but the act of killing generates new [[blood-guilt|pollution]], as the final scene reveals: [[orestes|Orestes]] flees, pursued by the Furies, his [[catharsis|purification]] incomplete (P01-008).

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] traces the evolution of [[catharsis|purification]] from blood-ritual to civic institution:

- In the **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]**, [[catharsis|purification]] is absent — the killing is unavenged and unatoned.
- In the **Choephoroe**, ritual [[catharsis|purification]] ([[libation-ritual|libations]]) is attempted but proves insufficient; the killing generates as much [[blood-guilt|pollution]] as it removes.
- In the **Eumenides**, [[catharsis|purification]] is achieved through Apollo's [[catharsis|cleansing]] of [[orestes|Orestes]] at [[delphi|Delphi]] and Athena's establishment of the Areopagus — "the [[catharsis|purification]] of [[orestes|Orestes]] from the murder of his mother and the conversion of the spirits of Punishment from Furies to Beneficent Beings" (P01-001).
