# Inachos

The river god of Argos, whose waters sustained the Argive plain and whose mythological lineage connected the House of Atreus to the divine order.

## In the Opening Prayer

Orestes, returning from exile to avenge his father, opens *The Libation Bearers* with a prayer at Agamemnon's tomb. He invokes "Inachos river, by whose grace I live" (001-001), acknowledging the river god as the source of life and civic identity. The invocation is paired with a hair-cutting ritual — a traditional Greek offering to rivers as "life-giving" or "rearers of young men" (κουροτρόφοι).

The notes explain the significance: "So Achilles on reaching manhood cut off his long hair as a gift to the River Spercheios. Rivers in a land subject to drought were worshipped as 'life-giving' or 'rearers of young men'" (002-129).

## Mythological Role

Inachos was the father of Io, the Argive princess beloved by Zeus who was transformed into a cow and driven across the world by Hera's gadfly. Through Io's descendant Danaus, Inachos is the ultimate ancestor of the Argive royal line — including Agamemnon, Orestes, and the entire House of Atreus.

## Dramatic Function

Orestes' invocation of Inachos at the play's opening does more than establish setting: it roots his personal vengeance in the deep soil of Argive identity. By offering his hair to the river that nourished his ancestors, Orestes claims his place in a lineage stretching back to the gods — and asserts his right to the throne usurped by Aegisthus.
