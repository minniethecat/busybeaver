# Justice vs. Vengeance

The *Choephoroe* dramatizes the difficulty — perhaps the impossibility — of distinguishing justice from vengeance when both take the same form.

## Overview

Orestes kills his mother under divine command. The act is framed as *Dikē* (justice): "For the blow of death the blow of death atone" (001-002). But its immediate consequence — the Furies' pursuit, Orestes' madness — suggests it is indistinguishable from the blood-vengeance it purports to end. "On him that doeth shall be done: / Speaks a grey word of long ago" (001-002). The trilogy's great achievement is showing that the archaic law of retribution cannot simply be obeyed — it must be transcended.

## Manifestation in the Play

### Key Scenes
The great *kommos* at the tomb is simultaneously a lament for the dead and a war-cry for vengeance — the two modes are indistinguishable. The killing of Clytemnestra is simultaneously the fulfilment of Apollo's command and the most abhorrent crime in Greek thought. The final scene, with Orestes displaying the bloodstained robe and fleeing the Furies, refuses to resolve the ambiguity.

### Characters
Orestes embodies the paradox — he is divine agent and matricide. Clytemnestra argues that her own killing of Agamemnon was equally an act of justice (for Iphigenia's sacrifice), and the play offers no definitive refutation of her claim.

## Across the Trilogy
The *Agamemnon* presents vengeance as triumph; the *Choephoroe* presents it as tragedy; the *Eumenides* resolves it through law. The trilogy's arc is the transformation of private vengeance into civic justice.
