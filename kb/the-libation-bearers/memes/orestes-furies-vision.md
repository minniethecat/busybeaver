# They are here — like Gorgons, gowned in darkness

The matricide committed, Orestes stands over his mother's body displaying the fatal robe — and then he sees them. The Furies (Erinyes) manifest as Gorgon-like figures, visible only to him, marking the moment when divine justice transforms into personal madness. This visionary passage is the hinge between *The Libation Bearers* and *The Eumenides*, the moment when the avenger becomes the pursued and the entire theological framework of the trilogy pivots from human justice to divine adjudication (001-004).

## Text

> Ah! Ah! Ye bondmaids! They are here: like Gorgons, gowned
> In darkness, and their locks are thick with serpents curled!
> I cannot stay! … Apollo! They are here! … Help! Help!
> (001-004)

Orestes cries out these lines in the final moments of the play, after displaying the robe-net in which Agamemnon was killed. The triumphant rhetoric of the prosecution speech collapses into terror; the display of evidence gives way to the vision of pursuers that only he can see (001-004).

## Speaker and Dramatic Context

Orestes has just completed his revenge: Clytemnestra and Aegisthus are dead, and he has publicly displayed the evidence of his father's murder, calling upon the Sun to witness that he acted in justice (001-004). The Chorus has sung a hymn of justice, celebrating the purification of the house. But as Orestes speaks his final words of justification, his tone shifts abruptly from triumph to terror. The transition is so sudden — from "bear me witness, when they seek some day to judge me, that in justice I did slay this woman" to "Ah! Ah! Ye bondmaids! They are here!" — that it feels like a rupture in the dramatic fabric itself (001-004).

The Chorus does not see what Orestes sees. Their attempt to reassure him — "Thou art sane... the fantasy is fled" — fails because the Furies are not a fantasy to Orestes. This selective visibility is crucial to Aeschylus' conception: the Erinyes are not a shared reality but a **subjective torment**, the externalization of Orestes' guilt made visible only to the guilty (001-004). They are, in the most literal sense, his personal demons.

## The Gorgon Comparison

The Gorgon comparison is one of the richest mythological allusions in the *Oresteia*. The Gorgons — Medusa and her sisters Stheno and Euryale — were chthonic monsters whose gaze turned living beings to stone. Perseus, the archetypal monster-slayer, killed Medusa by using his polished shield as a mirror, avoiding direct eye contact (002-103). The myth carries several layers of significance for Orestes' situation:

**The impossibility of looking away**. Unlike Perseus, Orestes cannot use a reflective surface to deflect the Furies' gaze. They are not external monsters to be slain but emanations of his own act. No shield — not even Apollo's protection — can shield him from what he has done. The Furies are **his** Gorgons, born from **his** hand, and they will pursue him until some higher authority intervenes (001-004).

**Petrification as moral paralysis**. The Gorgon's gaze turns men to stone — literally petrifies them. Orestes' flight from the Furies is an attempt to avoid this petrification, to keep moving, to stay alive. But in a deeper sense, the petrification has already occurred: Orestes' moral agency is frozen at the moment of the matricide. From this point in the play until Athena's intervention in *Eumenides*, Orestes cannot act freely; he can only flee and supplicate. The Gorgon-vision marks the moment when his will is turned to stone (001-004).

**Mythological inversion**. Perseus used Medusa's severed head as a weapon, turning his enemies to stone. Orestes, by contrast, is himself the target of the Gorgon-gaze. The monster-slayer becomes the monster; the avenger becomes the prey. This inversion is Aeschylus' way of dramatizing the moral ambiguity of the matricide — the same act that Orestes presents as justice is, from another perspective, a Gorgon-worthy horror (001-004).

## The Imagery of Darkness and Serpents

The description — "gowned in darkness," "locks thick with serpents curled" — aligns the Erinyes with the chthonic powers of the underworld. They are not Olympian deities of light and reason but ancient, pre-Olympian spirits of blood-vengeance who dwell beneath the earth and rise to claim their due (001-004). The darkness of their gowns is the darkness of the grave, of the blood that cries out from the ground, of the primeval moral order that predates Zeus' justice.

The serpent imagery connects the Furies backward to Clytemnestra's dream, in which she gave birth to a serpent that drew blood with its mother's milk (001-002), and forward to the *Eumenides*, where the Furies will describe themselves as hunting their prey by the scent of blood (002-103). The serpent is the unifying image of the trilogy's chthonic dimension: it appears in the dream, in the Furies' hair, and ultimately in the Athenian cult of the Eumenides, where the "Kindly Ones" are associated with the earth's fertility as benevolent snakes (002-103).

The serpents also evoke the Gorgon's own iconography — Medusa is traditionally depicted with snakes for hair. By describing the Furies as both "like Gorgons" and "thick with serpents curled," Orestes collapses the simile: the Furies are not merely *like* Gorgons; they *are* Gorgon-like in their very substance. The boundary between metaphor and reality dissolves, which is precisely the experience of hallucinatory terror (001-004).

## The Invisible Chorus

One of the most striking features of this scene is the asymmetry of perception between Orestes and the Chorus. The Chorus of slave women, who have supported Orestes throughout the play, cannot see what he sees. Their reassurance — "Thou art sane... the fantasy is fled" — is well-meaning but fundamentally inadequate. They are still operating within the framework of the revenge drama, where justice has been done and the house is cleansed. Orestes has already moved into the framework of the *Eumenides*, where justice is contested and cleansing requires divine intervention (001-004).

This perceptual gap creates a powerful theatrical effect. The audience, like the Chorus, cannot see the Furies (at least not in the text; productions vary in whether they stage the Furies visibly). The question "Are they real?" is left deliberately unresolved. Aeschylus does not ask us to believe or disbelieve in the Furies' objective existence; he asks us to inhabit Orestes' experience of them — the experience of guilt so overwhelming that it takes visible form (001-004).

## Apollo and the Flight to Delphi

Orestes' cry "Apollo! They are here! … Help! Help!" invokes the god who commanded the matricide. The irony is brutal: Apollo ordered Orestes to kill Clytemnestra, threatening him with "dreadful wrath" if he refused (001-004), and now Orestes calls upon the same god to save him from the consequences of obedience. The *Eumenides* will test whether Apollo's authority is strong enough to protect his agent from the older gods whose prerogatives he has violated (001-004).

The flight to Delphi — "I cannot stay!" — initiates the physical journey that structures the third play. The *Eumenides* opens at Delphi, moves to Athens, and ends at the Areopagus. Orestes' panicked exit from Argos is the first step on a journey that will transform not only his own fate but the entire cosmic order, as the Furies are persuaded to become the Eumenides, the "Kindly Ones" who bless rather than curse (001-004).

## Theatrical Staging in Performance History

The staging of the Furies has been the central challenge of every production of the *Oresteia*. In the original production of 458 BCE, the Furies were almost certainly played by the same chorus members who had just been the sympathetic slave women — a doubling that would have made Orestes' terror all the more disorienting for the audience, as familiar faces suddenly became monstrous (001-004).

Modern productions have ranged from naturalistic (actresses in black robes and snake-wigs) to abstract (puppets, projections, sound design) to psychological (no visible Furies at all — Orestes reacts to empty space, making his madness uncomfortably real). Peter Hall's 1981 National Theatre production used masked figures that emerged from traps in the stage floor, while Peter Stein's 1994 Russian production used a chorus of elderly women whose sudden transformation into Furies was achieved through movement and lighting alone (001-004).

The question of whether to make the Furies visible on stage is fundamentally a question about the nature of guilt in the play. If they are visible, guilt is an external, divine force; if they are invisible, guilt is an internal, psychological state. Aeschylus' text supports both readings, and the ambiguity may be precisely the point (001-004).

## The Furies in Earlier Greek Literature

The Erinyes appear in Homer — notably in the *Iliad*, where they punish oath-breakers and those who violate the natural order (a son striking a father, for example), and in the *Odyssey*, where they are associated with the curses of parents. But Aeschylus is the first poet to give them a central dramatic role and to stage the confrontation between the old chthonic deities and the new Olympian order (002-103).

The innovation of the *Oresteia* is to make the Erinyes not merely punitive spirits but characters with a moral argument. In the *Eumenides*, they will defend their prerogatives in court, arguing that the ancient law of blood-vengeance is not mere savagery but a form of cosmic balance. The seeds of that argument are already present in this vision scene: the Furies are terrifying, but they are not wrong. Orestes has shed his mother's blood, and something in the universe demands a response. The vision is the first stirring of that demand taking shape (001-004).
