Troy, the city in Asia Minor besieged for ten years by the Greek forces under [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], is the distant but crucial origin point of the [[oresteia|Oresteia]]'s action. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s departure for and [[exile-and-return|return]] from Troy frame the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s entire arc of crime and punishment.

## Mythological Background

Troy's fall is the victory that makes [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s murder possible. The king who "High conquerors of Troy in joyous war" (001-002) [[exile-and-return|return]]s home only to be killed in his bath. The irony is bitter: the conqueror of Asia's greatest city cannot survive his own threshold.

## Significance in The Libation Bearers

Troy functions in the play primarily as a memory and a measuring-stick. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] sings of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s past glory, now reduced to a grave mound in a desolate place: "To them that conquered Troy" — and now "cower to two" (001-002), [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and [[aigisthos|Aigisthos]]. The fall of the great city sets the scale against which [[argos|Argos]]'s domestic catastrophe is measured.

The Endnotes draw the connection explicitly: "Justice has come both to Troy and to [[argos|Argos]]" (002-121). Troy fell through Paris's violation of *xenia* (guest-friendship); [[argos|Argos]]'s ruling house falls through [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s violation of marriage. Both cities are sites where cosmic [[dike|justice]] operates across different but parallel transgressions.

The Endnotes also observe that the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] members "certainly do not seem to be Trojans" (002-014) — they are likely captured from raids on the Greek coast, distinguishing the play's bondmaids from the Trojan women of [[euripides|Euripides]]' chorus in *Hecuba* or *Trojan Women*.

## Context in the Oresteia

The Trojan War provides the [[oresteia|Oresteia]]'s moral frame. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s sacrifice of Iphigenia to secure the fleet's departure for Troy (*[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*) makes his own murder a punishment within the logic of [[divine-vengeance|blood-vengeance]]. The war's conclusion — the sack of Troy — is also the conclusion of an era of heroic warfare. What follows in [[argos|Argos]] is the unresolved question of how blood-debt is to be settled in peacetime, a question that will only be answered by Athena's court in *Eumenides*.
