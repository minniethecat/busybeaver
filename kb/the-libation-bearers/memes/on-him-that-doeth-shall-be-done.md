The ancient maxim of [[justice-and-vengeance|retributive justice]] — "On him that doeth shall be done" (δράσαντι παθεῖν, *drasanti pathein*) — delivered by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of bondmaids at the climax of [[orestes|Orestes]]' revelation of [[apollo|Apollo]]'s command. It is the play's most compressed theological statement.

## Text

> Ye great Apportionments of God,
> The road of Righteousness make straight:
> "For tongue of hate be tongue of hate
> Made perfect": thus, as falls her rod,
> God's justice crieth: "For the blow
> Of death the blow of death atone."
> "On him that doeth shall be done":
> Speaks a grey word of long ago. (001-002)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of Bondmaids
- **Addressee**: [[orestes|Orestes]] (indirectly — the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] responds to his speech of revelation)
- **Dramatic moment**: [[orestes|Orestes]] has just revealed [[apollo|Apollo]]'s full command — that he must kill his mother or face unspeakable torment. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] answers not with horror but with theological affirmation, invoking "a grey word of long ago" to authorise the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]].
- **Form**: [[choral-ode|choral ode]] (strophe/antistrophe structure)

## Analysis

This passage is dense with legal and theological meaning. The "great Ap[[moira|portion]]ments of God" (*Moirai* — the Fates) are invoked as guarantors of cosmic order; the "road of Righteousness" (*Dikē*) must be "made straight." The maxim is structured as a chain of equivalences: tongue-for-tongue, blow-for-blow, deed-for-deed. The Endnotes observe that "the rule of [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] seems to the actors, and in this special case to [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], a natural and divine law" (002-038).

The phrase "a grey word of long ago" signals that this is ancestral wisdom, not innovation. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] is not arguing — it is citing precedent. The [[dike|justice]] they invoke is ancient, impersonal, and absolute. The passage does not distinguish between [[dike|justice]] and [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] because, in the world of the *Choephoroe*, they are the same thing. The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s entire dramatic arc is the long process by which they are separated.

Gilbert Murray's preface captures the paradox: "The mother-murder, even if done by a god's command, is a sin; a sin to be expiated by unfathomable suffering. Yet, since the god cannot have commanded evil, it is a duty also. It is a sin that must be committed" (P01-002). The [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s maxim papered over this paradox with the authority of tradition — but the play will not let it stand.

## In Performance

The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] delivers these lines immediately after [[orestes|Orestes]]' long, horrified speech about [[apollo|Apollo]]'s threats. The shift from [[orestes|Orestes]]' personal terror to the [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s impersonal theological pronouncement creates a jarring dramatic effect — the collective voice overwrites individual doubt. On stage, the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] would likely turn outward to the audience at this moment, the maxim delivered as public truth rather than private consolation. The rhythmic, almost incantatory quality ("For tongue of hate be tongue of hate / Made perfect") lends the words ritual force.
