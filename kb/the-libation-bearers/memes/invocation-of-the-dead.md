# Invocation of the Dead

The invocation of the dead — the ritual summoning of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s spirit at his tomb through [[libation-ritual|libations]], prayer, and lamentation — is the central ritual act of [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' *Choephoroe*, giving the play its Greek name and structuring its most powerful scene.

## Definition

The invocation of the dead (*nekyia*) in Greek religion involved pouring liquid offerings (*[[libation-ritual|choai]]*) into the earth to reach the dead below, accompanied by prayers and ritual lamentation. In the *Choephoroe*, the [[libation-ritual|libations]] are brought by the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of [[the-chorus|slave-women]] at [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s command, but [[electra|Electra]] transforms them from appeasement offerings into a summons for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]: "The offerings as an appeal for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]" (002-023). The dead [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], like Homer's ghosts, cannot speak "till they had drunk the blood of sacrifice" (P01-004) — the [[libation-ritual|libations]] awaken his dim senses and enable him to aid his children.

## Greek Religious Context

Ritual communication with the dead was a recognized but carefully regulated practice in Greek religion. The *choē* (libation) was distinct from the *spondē* ([[libation-ritual|drink-offering]] to the Olympians) — it was poured directly onto the ground or into a pit, directed downward to the chthonic powers. The offerings at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb include milk, honey, wine, and water (001-002), the traditional ingredients of chthonic ritual. [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] draws on this ritual vocabulary but transforms it into the dramatic engine of his play: "Where in all literature, except [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], could one find this union of primitive ghostliness with high intellectual passion?" (P01-004).

## Role in The Libation Bearers

The great invocation scene at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb is the play's emotional and structural centre. It unfolds in stages: [[electra|Electra]]'s hesitant prayer, the discovery of the lock of hair, the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] of [[orestes|Orestes]], and the extended *[[kommos]]* (lamentation) in which [[orestes|Orestes]], [[electra|Electra]], and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] alternate in calling the dead king to wakefulness and aid. "Behold, ye have made a long and yearning praise, / This sepulchre for unlamented days / Requiting to the full" (001-002).

The invocation achieves its purpose: "as the great litany grows in intensity of longing, the dead seem to draw nearer to the living, and conviction comes to the mourners, one after another, that he who was once King of Kings is in power among them" (P01-004). By the scene's end, [[orestes|Orestes]] has received the spiritual authorization he needs to proceed with the killing.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The invocation of the dead serves as the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s pivot from passive suffering to active [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]:

- In the **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]**, the dead are invoked only in memory — the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] recalls Iphigenia's sacrifice, but no ritual seeks to reach her.
- In the **Choephoroe**, the dead are actively summoned and respond — [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s spirit is awakened, and the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] plot is set in motion.
- In the **Eumenides**, the dead recede as the living institutions of [[dike|justice]] (the Areopagus, Athena's authority) take over. The Furies, themselves chthonic powers, are transformed into civic guardians.
