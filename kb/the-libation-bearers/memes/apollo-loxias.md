# Apollo Loxias

The god whose command to Orestes — "kill the killers in return" — sets *The Libation Bearers* in motion, and whose promise of purification is both Orestes' shield and his burden.

## Loxias the Seer

"Loxias" (Λοξίας) is Apollo's cult title at Delphi, the god in his aspect as oracle and prophet. The name derives from the oblique, riddling nature of his prophecies — "Loxias" meaning "the Oblique One." When Orestes tells the Chorus "Lord Apollo hath foretold, / Loxias the Seer" (001-002), he invokes Apollo in this specific prophetic capacity.

The command Apollo gave was unambiguous: avenge Agamemnon's death by killing the killers — Orestes' own mother and her consort. "Even as Apollo gave / His charge on the Mountain" (001-004), the Chorus confirms, rooting the vengeance in divine authority.

## The Promise of Purification

Apollo's command came with a promise: that he would purify Orestes of the matricide's pollution. This promise is crucial to the play's moral framework — without it, Orestes' act would be simply murder. With it, the act becomes a divinely mandated duty, though the pollution is no less real.

The notes observe that "there is a mention of Delphi and Apollo's command" at a key moment (002-035), and the god is invoked as "Apollo of the Cavern of Delphi" (002-100) — the chthonic, earth-rooted aspect of the Delphic cult.

## Dramatic Significance

Apollo's role in the Oresteia is both theological and structural. Theologically, he represents the Olympian order's claim to authority over the ancient chthonic powers (the Furies). Structurally, his command provides the plot's motor, while his promised purification points toward the trilogy's resolution in the *Eumenides*, where Apollo himself defends Orestes at the Areopagus.
