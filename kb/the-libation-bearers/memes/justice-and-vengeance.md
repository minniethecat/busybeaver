Justice and Vengeance form the central thematic opposition of the [[oresteia|Oresteia]] [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]. In *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, the two concepts are indistinguishable: doing [[dike|justice]] means exacting [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. The [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s arc is the long, painful process by which they are separated.

## The Problem

In the world of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, [[dike|justice]] (*[[dike|dikē]]*) and [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] are the same thing. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] invokes "Justice who doth hold us fast" (001-002); [[electra|Electra]] prays to "the Daughter of [[zeus|Zeus]], the pure, / Justice yclept below" (001-004). But the [[dike|justice]] they call for is blood for blood — the killing of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] in [[exile-and-return|return]] for her [[murder-of-agamemnon|killing of Agamemnon]]. As Gil Murray puts it in his preface: "The mother-murder, even if done by a god's command, is a sin; a sin to be expiated by unfathomable suffering. Yet, since the god cannot have commanded evil, it is a duty also. It is a sin that must be committed" (P01-002).

The Endnotes elaborate: "the rule of [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] seems to the actors, and in this special case to [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]], a natural and divine law" (002-038). The phrase "seems to" is crucial — what seems like divine law to the characters is the problem the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]] exists to solve.

## Vengeance as Justice in the Play

Vengeance structures the play at every level. [[orestes|Orestes]] opens by calling on [[hermes|Hermes]] as "Help in War" (001-002) — the god of travellers is invoked as an ally in [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]]. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]]'s opening ode describes the mechanism: "A blind pain draweth the slayer, draweth him, / On, on, till he is filled even to the brim / With sickness of the soul to atone for all" (001-002). Vengeance is presented as an almost physical force — inexorable, the law of the universe.

The murder of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] is described as a wrong that "crieth" from the earth: "God's [[dike|justice]] crieth: 'For the blow / Of death, the blow of death is due'" (001-002). The parallel with [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s own justification is striking: [[orestes|Orestes]], before killing her, says "that in [[dike|justice]] I did slay / This woman" (001-004). Both sides claim [[dike|justice]] — and both are right, within the logic of the [[divine-vengeance|blood-feud]]. That is the problem.

## Toward Civic Resolution

The play cannot resolve the [[dike|justice]]-[[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] conflation. It can only enact it to its terrible conclusion — [[orestes|Orestes]] standing over his mother's body, seeing the Furies gather. The Endnotes note [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]'s clarity: "the exact problem of the Trilogy" (002-130) is how to escape the cycle. The resolution comes in *Eumenides*, where Athena's court transforms [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] from an automatic, personal, and endless mechanism into a civic, deliberative, and finite one.
