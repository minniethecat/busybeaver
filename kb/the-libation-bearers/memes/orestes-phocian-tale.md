# Orestes' Phocian Tale

At the pivot of *The Libation Bearers*, Orestes, disguised as a merchant from Phocis, delivers a carefully constructed fiction: the story of his own death. This speech is the engine of the play's revenge plot — it is through this lie that the avenger gains entry to the palace, and it is this lie that lulls Clytemnestra and Aegisthus into fatal complacency.

## Text

> I come from Phôkis, of the Daulian clan,
> And, travelling hither, bearing mine own load
> Of merchandise, toward Argos, as the road
> Branched, there was one who met me, both of us
> Strangers to one another: Strophius,
> A Phocian prince, men called him. On we strode
> Together, till he asked me of my road
> And prayed me thus: "Stranger, since other care
> Takes thee to Argos, prithee find me there
> The kin of one Orestes.… Plainly said
> Is best remembered: tell them he is dead.
> Forget not. And howe'er their choice may run,
> To bear his ashes home, or leave their son
> In a strange grave, in death an exile still,
> Discover, and bring back to me their will.
> Tell them his ashes lie with me, inurned
> In a great jar of bronze, and richly mourned."
> (PN 001-004)

## Context

- **Speaker**: [[Orestes]], son of Agamemnon, disguised as a Phocian merchant
- **Addressee**: [[Clytemnestra]], who has come to the gate to receive the strangers
- **Dramatic moment**: Orestes and Pylades, disguised as travelers from Phocis, have knocked at the palace gate. Clytemnestra offers them hospitality. Orestes responds with this fabricated tale, invoking the name of Strophius — the very man to whom Clytemnestra sent the young Orestes for safekeeping.
- **Form**: rhesis (extended speech), with embedded direct quotation of "Strophius"

## Analysis

The brilliance of Orestes' fiction lies in its strategic use of truth. He claims to come from Phocis — where the real Orestes was raised by Strophius (PN 001-004). He names Strophius as his informant — the actual Phocian prince who sheltered the exiled child. He describes the death of "Orestes" in exactly the region where the real Orestes spent his childhood. Every detail that can be verified is true; only the central claim — that Orestes is dead — is false.

The speech is a test as much as a deception. By invoking Strophius by name, Orestes watches for Clytemnestra's reaction. Does she flinch at the name of the man who protected her exiled son? Does she show relief at the news of death? The audience, knowing Orestes' true identity, experiences the scene as unbearable dramatic irony: Clytemnestra welcomes her killer with promises of "warm bathing and the couch that soothes away toil" (PN 001-004).

The embedded message from "Strophius" contains a practical ambiguity: "howe'er their choice may run, to bear his ashes home, or leave their son in a strange grave" (PN 001-004). This detail is psychologically acute — it presents the Phocian prince as respectful of the family's wishes, and it forces Clytemnestra and Aegisthus to make a decision about the "dead" Orestes, further entangling them in the fiction.
