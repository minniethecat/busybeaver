## Overview

The kommos (κομμός, *kommos*) — a ritual lament in antiphonal form, alternating between actors and chorus — is one of the defining structural features of Aeschylean tragedy. In *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, the Great Kommos (lines corresponding to the tomb-scene after the [[recognition-scene|recognition]]) is the longest and most elaborate kommos in extant Greek tragedy: an extended sequence of strophes and antistrophes in which [[orestes|Orestes]], [[electra|Electra]], and the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] of slave women build a crescendo of grief, invocation, and resolve that transforms lamentation into a call for [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]].

## The Great Kommos: Structure and Function

### Dramatic Position

[[the-great-kommos|The Great Kommos]] occupies the structural center of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*, positioned between the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] scene ([[recognition-and-tokens|anagnorisis]]) and the plotting of the revenge (the [[guile-and-justice|deception]] plan). This placement is architecturally deliberate:

| Section | Function |
|---------|----------|
| [[anagnorisis|Recognition]] Scene | [[orestes|Orestes]] and [[electra|Electra]] identify each other |
| **[[the-great-kommos|The Great Kommos]]** | Grief is ritually expressed and transformed into resolve |
| Deception Planning | [[orestes|Orestes]] and [[electra|Electra]] devise the strategy for revenge |

The kommos thus serves as the emotional and theological pivot of the play — it is the point at which private sorrow becomes public ritual, and ritual generates action. Without the kommos, the revenge plot would be mere stratagem; through the kommos, it becomes a sacred obligation.

### Strophic Structure

The kommos unfolds in a tightly structured alternation of voices:

| Voice | Contribution |
|-------|-------------|
| [[orestes|Orestes]] | Strophes — invoking the dead father, questioning the gods, seeking purpose |
| [[electra|Electra]] | Antistrophes — recounting wrongs suffered, demanding [[dike|justice]], fueling resolution |
| [[the-chorus|Chorus]] / Leader | Responding strophes — providing theological interpretation, channeling communal grief |

The strophe/antistrophe pattern mirrors the physical movement of the chorus in the orchestra — turning one way (strophe), then the other (antistrophe) — so that the kommos is simultaneously a verbal, musical, and choreographic ritual.

### From Lamentation to Invocation

The kommos progresses through identifiable stages:

1. **Grief Expressed**: The opening strophes are pure lament — "O Father, Father of Doom, What word, what deed from me, Can waft afar to the silent room Where thy sleep holdeth thee?" [[orestes|Orestes]]'s first words establish the central problem: the dead are silent, the living must reach them.

2. **Theological Reflection**: The [[the-chorus|Chorus]]- Leader introduces the key concepts: "When they weep for one who dies His Avenger doth arise, Yea, for father and life-giver There is Justice, when the cries And the tears run as a river." Lamentation (tears) is the channel through which Justice ([[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]]) enters the world.

3. **Narrative of Wrongs**: [[electra|Electra]] catalogs the dishonors — [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] was buried without rites, his body mutilated ("His hands and feet, they were hacked away from him"), his children [[exile-and-return|exile]]d and enslaved. Each detail sharpens the demand for response.

4. **Transformation to Resolve**: The turning point comes when the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] declares: "Yet still it may be — God is strong — A changèd music shall be born To sound above this dirge forlorn, And the King's House with Triumph-song Lead home a Friend in love new-sworn." The "dirge forlorn" (the kommos itself) is to be replaced by a "Triumph-song" — the lament transformed into a victory paean.

5. **Sacred Vow**: [[orestes|Orestes]]'s cry — "All, all dishonour, so thy story telleth it! And for that dishonour shall the woman pay, As the gods have willed it, as my right hand willeth it! Then Death may take me, let me only slay!" — is the ritual climax. The kommos has done its work: grief has become purpose, lamentation has become a vow of blood.

## Ritual Dimensions

### Invocation of the Dead

The kommos is fundamentally necromantic — it seeks to awaken the dead and enlist their power. [[orestes|Orestes]]'s repeated invocations of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] ("O Father, Father of Doom") and the "Princedoms of the Dead" are ritual speech-acts designed to bridge the gap between the living and the underworld. The physical setting — at [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tomb — reinforces this: the kommos is chthonic ritual, performed at the threshold where the dead can hear.

[[electra|Electra]]'s role in this invocation is equally essential. As the female mourner, she performs the culturally prescribed function of the lamenting woman — a role with deep roots in Greek funerary practice. Her tears are not merely emotional expression but ritual action:

> Do ye set lamentation like a wreath Round them, and cry the triumph-song of death. (001-002)

The "wreath" of lamentation is a ritual offering, as tangible as the [[libation-ritual|libations]] poured on the tomb.

### The Dirge as Weapon

The kommos transforms grief into a weapon. The [[the-chorus|Chorus]] compares their lamentation to the ritual practices of Eastern mourners:

> With the dirge of Agbatana I beat my breast: Like the Keeners of Kissia, I make songs of pain. Lo, yearning of arms abundant, east and west: Tearing they smite, again and yet again, From above, from high; yea, God hath smitten red This bitter bleeding bosom, this bended head. (001-004)

The allusion to Agbatana (Ecbatana) and Kissia (Cissia) — Persian regions associated with elaborate funerary lamentation — elevates the kommos to a universal scale. This is not merely Greek ritual practice but a cross-cultural language of grief, recognized as potent by gods and mortals alike.

The physical actions described — beating the breast, tearing hair, smiting the body — are ritual self-violence that mirrors and channels the desire for violence against the murderers. The body becomes the site where grief is transmuted into the energy of revenge.

## The Kommos and Dramatic Form

### Aeschylean Innovation

The extended kommos is a distinctly Aeschylean form. While Sophocles and [[euripides|Euripides]] both use lyric exchanges between actors and chorus, neither approaches the scale or ritual intensity of the Aeschylean kommos. The translator notes that Sophocles "suppresses the religious problem and concentrates on the elements of direct passion" (P01-002) — this suppression includes the ritual kommos, which is the primary vehicle for the play's religious dimension.

### The Kommos as Trilogy Hinge

Within the [[oresteia|Oresteia]]'s triadic structure, the kommos of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is the central ritual act:

| Play | Ritual Center |
|------|--------------|
| *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* | Cassandra's prophetic scene + the carpet scene |
| ***The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers*** | **[[the-great-kommos|The Great Kommos]] at the tomb** |
| *Eumenides* | The trial at the Areopagus |

The kommos bridges the two modes of the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]: it completes the movement of lamentation that began with the Watchman's opening speech in *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* and initiates the movement of [[dike|justice]] that culminates in Athena's court. It is the point at which the dead are most present — and most powerful — in the living world.
