# The Trilogy Form

The *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* (*[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, *Choephoroe*, *Eumenides*) is "the only trilogy preserved to us from the Athenian stage" (P01-001), making it the unique witness to a dramatic form that once dominated Greek tragedy — the connected trilogy in which three plays trace a single story arc from crime through punishment to [[deliverance-and-salvation|deliverance]].

## Definition

The connected tragic trilogy was [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' favoured form. Each year at the City Dionysia, three tragedians each presented three tragedies followed by a satyr-play (a *tetralogy*). [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]] often linked his three tragedies into a continuous narrative — the *[[oresteia|Oresteia]]* is the most famous example, but fragments suggest his other trilogies (the *Danaid* trilogy, the *Prometheus* trilogy) followed the same pattern. Later tragedians, particularly Sophocles and [[euripides|Euripides]], largely abandoned the connected trilogy in favour of self-contained plays.

## Greek Context

The choice of the connected trilogy form has profound consequences for the *Choephoroe*. "The crucial difference is that the Choëphoroe is not self-contained, while the other plays are. They are concerned with a particular story; it is part of a trilogy dealing with the great problem which lies at the centre of Greek religion — Hubris, [[justice-and-vengeance|Dike]], Soteria or Crime, Punishment and Deliverance" (P01-002). Because the *Choephoroe* is the middle play, it can end in crisis without resolution — [[orestes|Orestes]] flees, pursued by the Furies, and the audience must wait for the *Eumenides* to see how (or whether) the dilemma will be resolved.

This structural feature distinguishes [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' treatment of the [[orestes|Orestes]] story fundamentally from Sophocles' and [[euripides|Euripides]]' *[[electra|Electra]]* plays, which, being self-contained, must resolve their action within a single play.

## Role in The Libation Bearers

As the middle play, the *Choephoroe* has a distinctive structural position. It must:

1. **Connect backward** to the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* — the prologue immediately recalls [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s crime and establishes [[orestes|Orestes]]' mission.
2. **Advance the action** — the [[recognition-scene|recognition]] and [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] are completed within this play.
3. **Set up the *Eumenides*** — the final scene, with the Furies visible to [[orestes|Orestes]] (if not to the [[the-chorus|Chorus]]), creates the momentum that carries into the third play.

The play's ending is deliberately unresolved: "The final words of the [[the-chorus|Chorus]] ask the question which is to be answered, or at least attempted, in the Eumenides" (P01-008). This forward-pointing structure is possible only because the *Choephoroe* is not self-contained.

## Relationship to the Oresteia Trilogy

The trilogy's structure can be mapped to [[aeschylus|Aeschylus]]' theological framework:

| Play | Stage | Dramatic Mode | Resolution |
|------|-------|---------------|------------|
| *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* | Hubris (Crime) | Tragedy | The king is murdered; the chorus despairs |
| *Choephoroe* | Dikē (Punishment) | Tragedy verging on resolution | The avenger completes his task but is himself pursued |
| *Eumenides* | Sōtēria (Deliverance) | Tragicomedy / divine drama | The cycle is broken; the Furies are transformed |

This arc — from crime through punishment to [[deliverance-and-salvation|deliverance]] — is "the great problem which lies at the centre of Greek religion" (P01-002), and the trilogy form is its necessary dramatic vehicle. A single play could not contain the full movement.
