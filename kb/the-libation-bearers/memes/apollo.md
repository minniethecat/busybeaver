## Identity

Apollo, son of [[zeus|Zeus]] and Leto, is the Olympian god of prophecy, music, healing, and [[catharsis|purification]]. His primary cult site at [[delphi|Delphi]] — "the Cavern of [[delphi|Delphi]], the God of Light" — was the most important oracular centre in the Greek world, and it is from [[delphi|Delphi]] that Apollo issues the command that drives the action of *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* (002-100).

In the play, Apollo is most frequently invoked by his cult title **Loxias** — "the Oblique One," a name that acknowledges the ambiguity of oracular utterance. [[orestes|Orestes]] calls upon "Loxias the Seer" as both warrant and shield: the god who commanded the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] and who must therefore protect its doer (001-002).

## The Delphic Command

The central fact of Apollo's role in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is the command delivered at [[delphi|Delphi]]: [[orestes|Orestes]] must avenge his father by killing his mother. [[orestes|Orestes]] invokes this command at every moment of crisis:

"As Lord Apollo hath foretold, Loxias the Seer" — the opening invocation frames the [[divine-vengeance|vengeance]] as divinely mandated (001-002).

"Loxias, Lord of Pytho, who decreed" — standing over [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s body, [[orestes|Orestes]] cites Apollo's authority as his defence (001-004).

The Endnotes clarify the theological weight of this claim: "there is a mention of [[delphi|Delphi]] and Apollo's command" as the framework within which the [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] must be understood — not as personal revenge but as obedience to divine ordinance (002-035).

## Apollo and the Moral Problem

Apollo's command creates the central moral tension of the play. The god ordains an act — [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]] — that is both a sacred duty (to avenge a father) and a polluting crime (to kill a mother). The Endnotes note that [[orestes|Orestes]] "feels the horror of the deed to which the god has commanded him" — he is not a cold executioner but a man torn between divine command and natural revulsion (002-035).

This tension is not resolved within *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* itself. It is only in the *Eumenides* — when Apollo himself appears as a character to defend [[orestes|Orestes]] before the Areopagus — that the god's responsibility is fully confronted. The Apollo of *Choephoroe* is an offstage presence whose command is absolute but whose moral logic remains opaque (P01-006).

## Loxias and Prophetic Ambiguity

The title "Loxias" — meaning "the Oblique One" or "the Ambiguous One" — is significant. Apollo's oracles at [[delphi|Delphi]] were famously ambiguous, capable of multiple interpretations. [[orestes|Orestes]]' appeal to "Loxias" thus carries an implicit question: has the god spoken clearly, or is [[orestes|Orestes]] acting on a partial understanding of the divine will? (001-002).

The Endnotes observe that [[orestes|Orestes]]' interpretation of Apollo's command shifts under pressure: "Oh, Loxias shall not mock my great design" — the verb "mock" suggests a fear that the oracle may have deceived him, a fear that the *Eumenides* will ultimately resolve (002-035).

## Relationships

- [[orestes]] — the mortal agent of Apollo's command
- [[clytemnestra]] — the victim of the divinely ordained [[killing-of-clytemnestra|matricide]]
- [[the-furies]] — the chthonic powers against whom Apollo must defend [[orestes|Orestes]] in the Eumenides

## In the Eumenides

Apollo's role in *The [[sacrifice-and-offering|Libation]] Bearers* is entirely offstage — a voice from [[delphi|Delphi]], invoked but never seen. In the *Eumenides*, he appears as a character, defending [[orestes|Orestes]] before Athena's court. This progression — from invoked authority to present advocate — is part of the [[the-trilogy-form|trilogy]]'s movement from archaic divine decree to civic legal process (P01-006).
