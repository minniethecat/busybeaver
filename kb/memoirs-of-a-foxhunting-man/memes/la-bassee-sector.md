# La Bassée Sector

The La Bassée sector was the narrator's introduction to trench warfare on the Western Front. Though his experiences there were, by his own account, "quite mild," the Givenchy trenches and the nightly working parties at Festubert formed the essential initiation into life at the front, documented in the opening chapters of Part 10.

## Context & Background

The narrator had only been with the battalion a week when they moved away from the La Bassée sector on a fine bright morning. The Givenchy trenches on the La Bassée Canal had taken their toll in casualties, and the battalion had been much below full strength before being filled up with drafts (039-008). The Division was being withdrawn to a back area for a long rest — the narrator and Dick had arrived at a fortunate time for their introduction to the front.

Despite the mildness of the narrator's own experiences, Barton and Durley and young Ormand were always talking about the Givenchy trenches and how their dugout had been "plastered with trench-mortars and whizzbangs." Now that they were out of it, they seemed to take an almost morbid delight in remembering their escapes (040-001).

## The Trenches at Givenchy

The Givenchy trenches represented the grim reality of static warfare. On the La Bassée Canal, the battalion had held a sector that was typical of the Western Front — wet, dangerous, and exhausting. The narrator's diary records the rhythm of those early days in spare, unembellished prose:

Working parties marched to Festubert, a ruined village shelled to bits. In darkness and rain, they started up half a mile of light-railway lines through marsh with sixty men. Then they carried hurdles up the communication trenches, about three quarters of a mile, which took two hours (039-012). Flares went up frequently, and a few shells passed high overhead, exploding far behind them.

On another night, under bright moonlight and iron frost, they dug from midnight till two. The men got soup in a ruined house at Festubert, "with the moon shining through matchwood skeleton rafters." Up behind the trenches, the frost-bound morasses and ditches and old earthworks lay in moonlight, with dusky figures filing across the open, hobbling to avoid slipping (039-013).

## The Working Parties

The diary captures the monotony and physical hardship of these nights:

> "Monday. Went with working-party at 3 p.m. Wet day. Awful mud. Tried to dig, till 7:30, and came home soaked. Back 9:45. Beastly night for the men, whose billets are wretched." (039-015)

The narrator's fellow officer Julian Durley, a shy clerk from Somerset House, took the men's discomforts very much to heart. His jokes when things were going badly reminded the narrator of "a facetious bus conductor on a wet winter day" — and the narrator saw in Durley "an inspiration toward selfless patience" and "an ideal platoon officer" (039-016).

## The Quartermaster

Among the enduring figures of the La Bassée period was Joe Dottrell, the Acting Quartermaster. He had been quartermaster-sergeant before the War and was now a captain, since the real Q.M. had found a "cushy job" at Army Headquarters before the battalion went into action at the first battle of Ypres — whence it had emerged with eighty-five men and one officer: Joe Dottrell (040-004).

"Whatever might happen Joe was always there, and he never failed to get the rations up; no bombardment could have prevented him doing that" (040-004). He was a small, spare man — a typical "old soldier" — who had won his D.C.M. in South Africa and had a row of ribbons to match his face, which was "weather-beaten and whiskyfied to purple tints which became blue when the wind was cold" (040-004).

## Moving South

When the battalion moved away from the sector, no one knew their destination, but the quartermaster had told Barton they might be going south. "New Army" battalions were beginning to arrive in France, and the British line was being extended (040-001). On the march they passed an infantry brigade of Kitchener's Army — the narrator watched the column of men sweating under their packs, with worried civilian officers on horses, and the heavy transport horses plodding through the sludge. "It was the War all right, and they were going in the direction of it" (040-002).

The battalion's migration — fifty miles by a straight line, perhaps sixty-five by road — was not an easy journey for the rank and file. A private soldier's censored letter summarized it: "Everywhere we go seems such a long way" (040-009). The narrator reflected: "In those last words one infantry private speaks for them all."

## Aftermath & Significance

The La Bassée sector was the narrator's threshold into the war. Though his experiences were mild compared with what lay ahead, the trenches at Givenchy, the ruined village of Festubert, and the nightly working parties established the physical and psychological landscape of the Western Front. The episode also introduces the fellowship of C Company — Barton, Durley, Ormand, Dick, and Joe Dottrell — whose characters would sustain the narrator through the months to come.

The move south, with its long night march and the exhausted arrival at a straggling village on the wooded uplands, marked the transition from the industrial Flanders plain to a different kind of war. Ahead lay Morlancourt, the Divisional Rest, and the first tours of the front-line trenches.
