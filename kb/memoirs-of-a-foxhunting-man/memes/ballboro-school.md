# Ballboro' School

Ballboro' School (referred to simply as "Ballboro'" throughout the memoir) is the preparatory school attended by the narrator, George Sherston, during his boyhood. The school appears in the early chapters of *Memoirs of a Foxhunting Man*, forming the backdrop to Sherston's childhood experiences of cricket, schoolboy life, and the anticipation of summer holidays.

## School Life

The school is depicted through brief but vivid recollections. Sherston describes the "cubicled dormitory at Ballboro'" (008-011), a detail that evokes the institutional character of Edwardian boarding-school life. The dormitory setting serves as a contrast to the freedom of home, where Sherston wakes to the sound of a cock crowing — "drowsily grateful" to be away from the school environment (008-011).

Academic life at Ballboro' included the study of classical texts. Sherston recalls struggling with Homer "in the fifth form at Ballboro'" (013-013), finding the Greek poet "impossible to enjoy" in the classroom setting. The arrival of a six-volume first edition of Pope's Homer during the holidays prompts the reflection that "he would seem ever so much easier now" — a testament to the contrast between the constrained school curriculum and the pleasure of independent reading.

## Cricket at Ballboro'

Cricket played a significant role in Sherston's school identity. His highest score at the school was 51 runs, achieved "only in a practice game at Ballboro'" (009-006). This innings becomes a point of reference and self-deprecation when he contemplates his prospects in the Butley Flower Show Match, calculating that his first-class cricketing namesakes' averages "worked out at 64" — well above his Ballboro' best.

The school's cricketing reputation is invoked by Dennis Milden, who hopes Sherston is "going to show them a little crisp Ballboro' batting" (010-009) during the Flower Show match. The epithet "crisp" suggests that Ballboro' was known for producing a certain style of play — neat, confident, and effective.

## Notable Alumni

Among Sherston's contemporaries at Ballboro' was **[[stephen-colwood]]**, a "quiet, slender boy with very large hands and feet" who came to Sherston's House "about two years after I went there" (015-022). Colwood, the son of a Sussex parson, later reappears as a steeplechase jockey riding Lieut.-Col. Hesmon's horse "Jerry" — a connection that bridges the schoolboy past with the hunting and racing world of the memoir's later parts.

## The Holidays

The emotional centre of Sherston's relationship with Ballboro' is the joy of leaving it. The opening of "The Flower Show Match" chapter captures this feeling precisely: "I was back from Ballboro' for the summer holidays" (008-001). The railway journey home, the familiar station at Baldock Wood, and the stationmaster's respectful salute (Aunt Evelyn "always sent him a turkey at Christmas") mark the transition from school discipline to the freedom of the Kentish countryside.
