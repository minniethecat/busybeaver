## Overview

The mobilization of August 1914 was the event that divided *Memoirs of a Foxhunting Man* into before and after. For George Sherston and the community of foxhunting men centered on [[the-ringwell-hunt|the Ringwell Hunt]], the declaration of war transformed a distant political crisis into an immediate personal summons. Within weeks, the hunting field was empty, the horses dispersed, and the men who had ridden to hounds were in uniform — first in [[the-yeomanry|the Yeomanry]] and territorial cavalry regiments, later in the [[trench-warfare|trenches of France]]. The memoir treats mobilization not as a heroic call to arms but as the quiet, inexorable end of a world.

## From Hunting Field to Recruiting Office

The transition was swift and, for the hunting community, apparently seamless. Men who had spent years learning to ride, manage horses, and navigate country found their skills in immediate demand. [[denis-milden|Denis Milden]], the Master of the Ringwell, "had disappeared into a cavalry regiment" (036-004). [[stephen-colwood|Stephen Colwood]], the narrator's schoolfellow and hunting companion, enlisted alongside him. The narrator himself, "regretting Dixon and resenting mobilization" (035-001), joined the Yeomanry — a territorial cavalry unit that was the natural destination for mounted men.

> The Yeomanry, as a Territorial regiment, was formed about three weeks after mobilization. (035-005)

The language is matter-of-fact, almost bureaucratic. The memoir denies mobilization any of the patriotic rhetoric that accompanied it in the popular press. There are no banners, no speeches, no declarations of noble purpose — only men reporting to depots, horses being sold, and the slow realization that nothing would be the same.

## The End of the Sporting World

| Before Mobilization | After Mobilization |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Ringwell Hunt meets | Cavalry training at Clitherland Camp |
| Point-to-Point races | Route marches and drill |
| Denis Milden, MFH | Denis Milden, cavalry officer |
| Harkaway and Cockbird | Army remounts |
| Butley and the Weald | Etaples and the Front |
| Dixon in the stable | Dixon in the army (and dead by memoir's end) |

The table of losses is cumulative and understated. The memoir does not lament mobilization; it records its consequences. [[tom-dixon|Dixon]], who had made a sportsman of the narrator, was killed. The horses — Harkaway, Cockbird, Sheila — were dispersed or sold. The [[ringwell-country|Ringwell country]] stood empty of riders. The narrator, who had once bicycled eagerly toward his first hunt meet, now found himself "polishing a cavalry saddle and wondering how it was all going to end" (035-001).

## Resentment and Resignation

The narrator's emotional response to mobilization is characteristically muted and complex. He is "regretting Dixon and resenting mobilization" (035-001) — the pairing is significant. Dixon represents everything the pre-war world meant: competence, loyalty, the quiet transmission of skill from one generation to the next. To resent mobilization is to resent the force that destroyed Dixon's world and, with it, the narrator's.

Yet there is no refusal, no protest. The narrator reports for duty, trains with his regiment, and eventually ships for France. The memoir's treatment of mobilization is an exercise in what it does not say — the emotions are present in the syntax of absence, the way the hunting chapters end and the military chapters begin without transition or explanation.

## Legacy

In the architecture of the memoir, mobilization is the watershed. Everything before it — [[foxhunting]], [[cricket]], the Flower Show Match, the Colonel's Cup, Dixon's coaching, Aunt Evelyn's household — belongs to one world. Everything after — the Yeomanry, the transport duties, the trenches, the deaths — belongs to another. The memoir's title itself becomes ironic: a "foxhunting man" is what the narrator was, not what the war allowed him to remain.
