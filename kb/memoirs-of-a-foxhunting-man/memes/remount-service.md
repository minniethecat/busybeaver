# Remount Service

The Remount Service was the branch of the British Army responsible for procuring, training, and supplying horses for military use during the First World War. In *Memoirs of a Foxhunting Man*, the Remount Service appears as the destination of an early commission offer that George Sherston — characteristically — declines.

## The Commission Offer

In early September 1914, shortly after his enlistment as a trooper in the Yeomanry, Sherston received a letter "from someone at the War Office whom I knew slightly" offering him "a commission, with the rank of captain, in the Remount Service" (035-002). For a man who had served only five weeks as a trooper, the offer of an immediate captaincy was a significant opportunity — one that recognized his equestrian expertise developed through years of foxhunting.

Sherston's response was immediate and instinctive. Reading Hardy's "Song of the Soldiers" in the same day's *Times* — "What of the faith and fire within us, men who march away ere the barncocks say night is growing gray?" — he concluded: "I did not need Hardy's 'Song of the Soldiers' to warn me that the Remounts was no place for me" (035-002).

## A "Cushy Job"

The Remount Service was perceived — fairly or not — as a safe rear-echelon posting for horsemen who might otherwise have been at the Front. This reputation is reflected in a later passage of the memoir, when a new transport officer arrives at Morlancourt: "A new transport officer had arrived — a Remount man from England. It was said that he had been combed out of a cushy job" (043-003).

The phrase "combed out" referred to the progressive tightening of manpower regulations that forced men from comfortable home postings into front-line service. The "Remount man" arriving in the transport section carried the stigma of having been extracted from a position of relative safety — though Sherston notes, with characteristic dry humour, that he "was glad I'd given up the transport" and glad "to be able to ride out on the black mare" (043-003). The Remount man had apparently "got the better of the new transport officer" in some contest over the black mare — a small victory for a displaced professional horseman.

## Sherston's Rejection

Sherston's refusal of the Remount commission reveals his complex attitude toward military service. He had already been "offered a commission in my own Yeomanry," but "how could I have accepted it when everybody was saying that the Germans might land at Dover any day?" (035-002). He felt "safe in the Army, and that was all I cared about" — a statement that in retrospect reads as deeply ironic, given that his path through the ranks would eventually lead him to the trenches of the Western Front.

Several officers in the Yeomanry "had known me out hunting with the Ringwell," and Sherston's "presence in the ranks was regarded as a bit of a joke" (035-003). His equestrian skill made him a natural candidate for the Remounts, yet his own sense of what was fitting — shaped equally by class loyalty to his fellow troopers and by a diffidence about his own abilities — led him to stay where he was.

## Significance

The Remount Service episode illuminates the crossroads that the War presented to men of Sherston's background. His hunting expertise was a genuine military asset in an army still dependent on horses, but the Remount posting would have kept him safely in England, far from the experiences that give the memoir's later sections their gravity. His refusal of the "cushy job" — motivated by a mixture of vague heroic aspiration, social solidarity with his troop, and a conviction that he was "absurd" as an officer — sets the course for everything that follows.
