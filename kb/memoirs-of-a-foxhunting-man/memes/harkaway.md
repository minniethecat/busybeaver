## Role in the Memoir

Harkaway III was a chestnut gelding hunter acquired by the narrator George Sherston during his most active period as a foxhunting man. Previously the property of Cosmo Gaffikin, Esq., Harkaway was discovered through [[tom-dixon|Dixon]]'s network of sporting contacts and became the horse that elevated Sherston from a novice rider to a serious hunting presence. The horse's arrival was, as the narrator recalled, "a red-letter day" for the household, marking a rite of passage into adult sporting life. Harkaway carried Sherston through the hunting seasons described in the middle chapters of the memoir, his three white socks becoming a familiar sight on the wet fields of the Weald.

## Background and Acquisition

Harkaway was a chestnut gelding described as "aged" — an older, experienced hunter who knew his business in the field (014-008). He was owned by Cosmo Gaffikin, a sporting acquaintance in Dixon's circle, and the negotiation for his purchase was handled entirely by Dixon. The narrator's former guardian opposed the acquisition:

> … prevented me from purchasing Harkaway (or any other quadruped) he was unaware of. (014-025)

Dixon, undeterred by the guardian's objections, completed the purchase and brought the horse to the narrator's home himself. His arrival at the front door with the words "Your horse, sir" was a theatrical touch that Dixon savoured — a demonstration that George Sherston was now a proper gentleman with a proper hunter (014-026).

## Character and Traits

Harkaway was not a young or flashy horse — he was "aged" and "not a difficult horse to ride" (014-031). What he lacked in youth he compensated for in reliability and the affection he inspired. Cosmo Gaffikin spoke of him with genuine fondness:

> … he pulled Harkaway's neat little ears affectionately … (014-016)

The narrator, too, was captivated. He described the horse as having "three white socks" (014-034) and recalled "hanging about the loose-box while Harkaway was being rubbed down" after each day's hunting (014-027). The horse became a centre of the narrator's daily life, his well-being a constant preoccupation.

The narrator's own riding on Harkaway was, by his own candid admission, not yet polished:

> … hung spurless on each side of Harkaway, stiff, ill-shaped, and palpably a novice. (014-029)

Yet Harkaway carried him steadily through the season, giving him the experience and confidence that would define his identity as a foxhunting man.
