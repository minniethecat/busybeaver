# Migration to the Midlands

The migration to the Midlands marks a decisive turning point in the narrator's hunting career: his choice to leave the familiar [[ringwell-country]] for the more prestigious hunting territory of the Midlands, following [[denis-milden]], who had recently become Master of the [[the-ringwell-hunt]].

## Background

The narrator had long admired Denis Milden, remembering him from earlier encounters. Milden was a figure of quiet authority and natural horsemanship — qualities that had made a lasting impression on the young Sherston. When Milden returned as Master of the Ringwell, it was more than a professional appointment; it was the rekindling of an old admiration.

Dixon, the narrator's groom, had his own recollections of Milden's earlier days:

> "Dixon gravely admitted that he remembered Milden as a boy at Limmore, adding that he'd been a young chap with a hound-like way with him even then" (007-004).

## The Ringwell Kennels

Staying at the Ringwell Kennels was a formative experience for the narrator. It was his first immersive encounter with the daily rhythms of a hunt establishment — the early mornings, the hound exercise, the smell of leather and damp coats. The Kennels represented a world he had longed to enter since childhood.

> "Staying at the Kennels was the most significant occasion of my early hunting days" (029-002).

The road by the Kennels offered a pastoral landscape that the narrator would later recall with wistful affection:

> "The road by the Kennels had wide green borders to it, and as I jogged back in the November dusk, soaked to the skin but quietly satisfied with my day, I felt I was getting the hang of things" (030-001, 029-005).

## The Decision to Move

Denis Milden, though Master at Ringwell, was widely recognized as destined for greater things. The Ringwell country — while beloved by the narrator — was understood to be a stepping stone rather than a final destination:

> "He was away up in the Midlands. And the Midlands, to put it plainly, represented the next stage of a hunting career" (030-018).

The sporting press had already taken note of the Midlands territory. The narrator had read about cubbing in the Midlands, where it was described at some length, further fueling his ambition to follow Milden into the new country (027-007).

## Exodus from the Kennels

The decision to leave the Ringwell country was not made lightly. The narrator had formed deep attachments — to the landscape, the hounds, the familiar faces. But Milden's departure created a gravitational pull that Sherston could not resist. The exodus from the Kennels marked the end of one chapter and the beginning of another:

> "My hope of another season in the Midlands, my exodus from the Kennels marked the close of a chapter which had begun with the first discovery of the Ringwell country" (034-004).

## Significance

The migration to the Midlands operates on multiple levels. Geographically, it represents a move from the intimate, local hunting world of Kent and Sussex to a nationally recognized hunting territory. Socially, it signals the narrator's ambition to advance beyond the amateur status of his youth. And narratively, it foreshadows the larger, more disruptive displacement that would come with the outbreak of the [[first-battalion|Great War]] — a migration from which there would be no simple return.
