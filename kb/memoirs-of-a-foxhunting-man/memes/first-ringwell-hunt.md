# First Ringwell Hunt

The First Ringwell Hunt marks the narrator's debut with the Ringwell Hounds, the hunt that replaced the Dumborough as the center of his sporting life. Staying at Hoadley Rectory with his friend Stephen Colwood, he experienced a new hunting country and the exhilaration of riding his new horse Cockbird.

## Context & Background

The Ringwell Hunt operated on the other side of the county from the narrator's familiar Dumborough territory (015-004). After parting ways with the Dumborough, the narrator accepted an invitation to stay at Hoadley Rectory for the Polesham Races (015-045). Stephen Colwood, a former schoolfellow, was the son of the Rector and an enthusiastic hunting companion who helped introduce the narrator to the new country.

The train journey into Sussex was accompanied by the *Southern Daily News*, which the narrator had bought specifically to read about the Ringwell Hounds, whose doings were regularly reported therein (019-001). As the train entered Ringwell territory, the landscape itself seemed to promise a different kind of hunting experience (015-005).

## Key Participants

- **george-sherston** — the narrator, making his first appearance with the Ringwell
- **[[stephen-colwood]]** — Sherston's schoolfellow, son of the Hoadley Rector, enthusiastic hunting companion
- **[[cockbird]]** — the narrator's new horse, a recent acquisition that performed admirably in the Ringwell country
- **The Ringwell Hunt** — the hunt club, notable for being more fortunate in its farmers than some neighboring packs, whose staunch support of the Hunt was a local point of pride (015-014)

## The Meet

On the morning of his first appearance at a meet of the Ringwell Hounds, the narrator struggled to tie his white stock neatly — he had never been shown how to do it, and his repeated failures did not surprise him, though he was naturally anxious not to disgrace the Rectory (021-001). The breakfast bell was supplemented by Stephen's incitements to hurry.

Arriving at the meet, the narrator began forming his mental picture of the Ringwell Hunt. Among those present was the Master, and the field included several figures who would become familiar in the seasons to come (021-008). Stephen Colwood showed a special interest in the narrator, and was never so companionable as when they were out together in the Ringwell country (025-017).

## Cockbird's Performance

Cockbird, the narrator's new horse, proved himself entirely suited to the Ringwell country. When asked how he liked the horse, the narrator found it difficult to answer before the conversation moved on (021-041). Cockbird's legs were examined and pronounced perfect (021-042). After the day's sport, Cockbird jogged smoothly along with his head turned toward home, carrying the narrator happily back to the Rectory (021-044).

The horse's performance was so promising that the narrator began considering entering Cockbird for the Ringwell Heavyweight Race (023-001). This marked the beginning of a deeper engagement with competitive riding.

## Aftermath & Significance

The first Ringwell Hunt established the narrator's connection to a new hunting country and marked his transition from the familiar Dumborough world. Despite the narrator's own characterization of the Ringwell as "anything but a cold-scenting country" (021-052), the experience proved deeply rewarding. The Hunt became a lasting presence in his life — even years later, during army training, he encountered a farmer's son from the Ringwell country in his section (035-020), and after the war he would receive the Hunt's balance sheet, which made him reflect on what had been lost (044-004).
