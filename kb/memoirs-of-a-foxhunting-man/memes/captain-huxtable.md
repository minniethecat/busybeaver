## Role in the Memoir

Captain Huxtable was a retired army officer living at Fircroft, a house with a mossy, evergreen-shaded lawn that served as the venue for the final of the Flower Show Match cricket competition. Rosy-faced and of an older generation, he embodied the comfortable, well-connected retired officer class that populated Sherston's pre-war Kent and Sussex. When the Great War came, Captain Huxtable was the man Sherston turned to for advice about obtaining a commission, and it was through Huxtable's letter and regimental connections that the narrator entered the Royal Westmoreland Fusiliers — making him a pivotal figure in the transition from Sherston the foxhunting man to Sherston the soldier.

## Cricket and Country Life

Captain Huxtable first appears in the memoir during the Flower Show Match episode, walking between cricket matches with a characteristic entourage:

> … walking between rosy faced old Captain Huxtable and his clucking, oddly dressed wife … (010-016)

His lawn at Fircroft was the setting for the exciting match-play final, a place the narrator recalled fondly for its "mossy and evergreen-shaded lawn" (027-003) where the decisive cricket game was played. Huxtable was a fixture of the local sporting scene, his presence lending a military respectability to the cricketing events that so absorbed the young Sherston.

## The Army Commission

When war broke out, it was to Captain Huxtable that Sherston went for guidance. The narrator rode over one afternoon:

> … to consult Captain Huxtable about a commission in an infantry regiment. (036-008)

Huxtable was, in the narrator's assessment, "an epitome of all that was leisurely and landed-gentry-like in the countryside" (036-009). His house, set among tall trees where "the rooks were holding their usual autumn parliament" (036-010), represented the settled pre-war world that was about to be shattered.

The captain took practical action on Sherston's behalf. As a result of their conversation:

> … Captain Huxtable wrote a letter about me to someone in the Royal Westmoreland Fusiliers. (037-001)

Huxtable's regimental career, though long past, suddenly became relevant again — "everything connected with Captain Huxtable's regimental career had suddenly become alive and urgent" (037-002). He wanted Sherston to "do the thing properly" (037-003), arranging for him to be taken on by the Special Reserve rather than the temporary commission route that was becoming common. The captain "had given me all I needed in the way of clothes and my credentials" (037-010), equipping Sherston for the transition from civilian to officer.

## Character and Traits

Captain Huxtable was defined by his rosy complexion and his comfortable, established place in county society. He was neither a heroic figure nor an intimidating one — rather, he was the steady, reliable type of retired officer who lent dignity and order to local life. His home at Fircroft, with its shaded lawn and tall rook-haunted trees, was an image of the Edwardian country-gentleman ideal. In the crisis of war, he proved himself an effective patron, using old regimental connections to smooth Sherston's path into the army.
