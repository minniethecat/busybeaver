# Captain Harry Hinnycraft

**Captain Harry Hinnycraft** was the Honorary Secretary of the Hunt for many years and a dominant figure in the Packlestone hunting country. He belonged to the old school of country gentlemen and was regarded as a "complete anachronism" even in his own time (032-004).

## Role in the Hunt

Captain Hinnycraft was a fixture of the local hunting establishment. As the narrator describes:

> Near him was a highly important personage, Captain Harry Hinnycraft, who for a vast number of years had been Honorary Secretary of the Hunt. "Dear old Captain Harry," as the young ladies called him (for on them he was wont to turn an appreciative eye), was by no means an easy old gentleman to please unless it suited him to be amiable. (032-004)

His opinion carried weight in hunt politics. He gave his "unqualified approval" to the new Master but harboured "an unconcealed prejudice" against the Field-Master, whom he denounced to all who would listen as "as wild as a hawk" or "mad as a hatter." The narrator notes that "compromise was a word of which Captain Hinnycraft had never mastered the meaning" (032-004).

His influence extended across the local hunt divisions. He presided over the Monday hunting country as its central personage:

> While Captain Hinnycraft lived and bulked big in the middle of the Monday country, all roads in the Wednesday district converged on Mrs. Oakfield at Thurrow Park. (032-007)

The Hunt's survival under his domineering personality was a testament to the diplomacy of others — "it may well be wondered how the Hunt had survived the despotism of this old-world grandee" (032-005).

## Character and Appearance

Captain Hinnycraft cut an imposing figure in the field:

> Massive and white-moustached on his magnificent weight-carriers, he had always ridden about the Packlestone country with the air of a monarch. (032-004)

He was known to the young ladies of the neighbourhood as "Dear old Captain Harry," for "on them he was wont to turn an appreciative eye" (032-004). His social sensibilities were distinctly old-fashioned; he held "conventional eighteenth-century ideas about what constituted masculine gallantry and sprightly conversation" (032-004). The narrator summarizes him memorably:

> Captain Harry defied all criticism because he was a complete anachronism. (032-004)

## Estate and Social Order

On his own estate, Captain Hinnycraft ruled with what the narrator calls "semi-benevolent tyranny," turning his back on "all symptoms of social innovation" (032-004). His management of the Packlestone country followed what the narrator terms "feudal system lines":

> His method of dealing with epistolary complaints from discontented farmers was to ignore them; in verbal intercourse he bullied them and sent them about their business with a good round oath. (032-004)

The narrator observes that Captain Hinnycraft firmly believed such people "were put there by Providence to touch their hats and do as they were told by their betters" (032-004).

## Later Life

Captain Harry continued in his accustomed role "beyond his eightieth year, until he fell into a fishpond on his estate and was buried by the parson whose existence he had spurned by his arrogance" (032-004).
