# The Colonel's Cup

The Colonel's Cup is the steeplechase that forms the narrative and emotional center of Part 6 of the memoir. Run as part of the [[dumborough-park|Dumborough Hunt]] Steeplechase on Easter Monday, the race sees the narrator riding *Jerry*, a horse owned by Lieut.-Col. C. M. F. Hesmon, in what becomes one of the defining sporting moments of his pre-war life.

## Prelude

The Dumborough Hunt Steeplechase was a fixture in the local sporting calendar. The narrator had attended in previous years and understood its prestige — it drew competitors and spectators from across the hunting community. On one earlier occasion he had seen Mr. Gaffikin ride in the event, a memory that lingered:

> "I had attended the Dumborough Hunt Steeplechase on Easter Monday and had seen Mr. Gaffikin ride in it" (015-001).

By the time the narrator himself was to ride, the stakes felt immeasurably higher. The race was no longer a spectacle to be watched — it was a trial to be survived.

## The Horse and the Colonel

Colonel Hesmon was a distinctive figure in the hunting world — his wizened face and bushy grey eyebrows made him instantly recognizable at any meet. He owned *Jerry*, the horse entered for the Colonel's Cup, and it was arranged that the narrator would take the ride:

> "This was Lieut.-Col. C. M. F. Hesmon's Jerry was to be ridden by Mr. G. Sherston" (015-022).

The announcement gave the narrator a jolt of pride and terror in equal measure. Dixon, his groom, had been hard at work preparing for the race, and the combined efforts of Colonel Hesmon and Dixon were solemnly focused on one objective — getting horse and rider to the post in winning condition:

> "Colonel Hesmon looked almost forlorn when they led the horse away, as though he were losing his last friend" (015-036).

Before the race, Colonel Hesmon's presence was both reassuring and unnerving. At the meet beforehand, his scarlet coat made him unmistakable:

> "Scarlet-coated Colonel Hesmon had proffered me a couple of sandwiches from his case, and I had accepted them gratefully" (021-007).

After the race, win or lose, Hesmon's manner remained generous:

> "Colonel Hesmon commiserated with us for having come such a long way for nothing — though it wasn't nothing to me" (021-039).

## The Race

The Colonel's Cup steeplechase was held subject to National Hunt rules (015-011). For the narrator, the experience of riding in a genuine steeplechase — under the eyes of the hunting community, on a horse owned by a man of Colonel Hesmon's standing — was transformative. It validated years of patient horsemanship and quiet ambition.

The race itself was a blur of sensation: the thunder of hooves on turf, the urgent calls of riders, the fence coming up too fast, and the singular focus that comes when nothing matters except the next stride.

## Significance

The Colonel's Cup steeplechase represents the culmination of the narrator's engagement with the equestrian world before the war. More than any foxhunt or point-to-point, the steeplechase demanded a different order of nerve — it was a public test, formally conducted, with consequences.

The episode also deepens the narrator's relationship with the hunting community: Colonel Hesmon, Stephen Colwood, Mr. Gaffikin, and even Lord [[lord-dumborough|Dumborough]] all play roles in this chapter. The race bridges the sporting life of the Edwardian countryside with the personal aspirations of the young narrator, who would soon exchange his racing silks for an officer's uniform.
