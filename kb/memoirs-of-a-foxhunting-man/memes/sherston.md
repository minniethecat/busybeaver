# Sherston

**Sherston** is the family name of the narrator and protagonist of *Memoirs of a Foxhunting Man*, appearing throughout the memoir both as the narrator himself — referred to as "Mr. Sherston" (014-015), "G. Sherston" (011-008), or simply "old Sherston" (015-032) — and as his aunt, "Miss Sherston" (005-038).

## The Narrator (G. Sherston)

The narrator, G. Sherston, is a young man from [[butley-village|Butley]] who pursues [[foxhunting]] with enthusiasm across the various hunting countries of the memoir.

His academic credentials are noted when Dixon remarks that "G. Sherston, M.A., will rank higher than plain G. Sherston" (011-008). He is known to his hunting companions simply as "Sherston" or "Mr. Sherston," as when the stud groom introduces him at a stable visit:

> The stud groom explained me as "Mr. Sherston, sir; come over from Butley to have a look at the horses." (014-015)

At the saddler's shop, he is greeted with professional courtesy:

> "Ah, yes, Mr. Sherston, of course; your coat and breeches will be ready by Thursday." (016-006)

His hunting career is documented in formal terms as well: a certificate attests that "Mr. G. Sherston's bay gelding Cockbird has been fairly and regularly hunted with the Ringwell Hounds" (026-006).

His relationships in the hunting community are warm. On arriving at [[the-ringwell-hunt|Ringwell]], he is greeted with casual familiarity — "Why, it's old Sherston!" (015-032). After a challenging ride, one companion exclaims "I really think Mr. Sherston saved my life!" (028-010). His friend [[tom-dixon|Tom Dixon]] describes his riding companion as "my friend Sherston" who "likes the feel of him" when assessing a horse (021-041).

## Miss Sherston

Miss Sherston, the narrator's aunt, is a figure of domestic authority at Butley. She is missed by the household staff when the narrator is away at school:

> "Miss Sherston won't half be pleased to see you back," the household staff remark on the narrator's return. (008-007)

She is referenced humorously on one occasion when Dixon, after a minor hunting mishap, remarks that it was a "good thing Miss Sherston couldn't see you" (005-038), suggesting her protective vigilance over her nephew's pursuits.
