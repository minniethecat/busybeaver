# Morlancourt Interlude

## Overview

The Morlancourt Interlude represents a period of Divisional Rest for Sherston and his battalion, tucked away in the village of Morlancourt on the Somme front. After the grim reality of the Givenchy trenches, this peaceful interlude offered a brief return to something approaching normal life — estaminets, evening conversations, and companionship with Dick Tiltwood and Mansfield. It stands in the narrative as one of the last truly peaceful moments before the intensification of the war.

## Context & Background

By the summer of 1916, Sherston's battalion had already experienced the harsh conditions of the La Bassée sector and the Givenchy trenches. "Divisional Rest" was the army's term for rotating units away from the front line for recovery, retraining, and reorganisation. For Sherston and his comrades, it represented the difference between "Divisional Rest and those ruddy Givenchy trenches" (041-001).

Morlancourt itself was "a village in the strip of unspoilt country" (041-029), "tucked away among the foliage" (042-001), offering a stark contrast to the devastated landscape of the front. The battalion arrived after marching several miles, entering a world that, while still within range of the war, felt temporarily removed from its immediate horrors.

## Key Participants

- **Dick Tiltwood** — Sherston's closest friend, a young officer who had left school only six months before (038-017). Their friendship deepened during the Morlancourt period.
- **Mansfield** — A fellow officer with a sardonic sense of humour. He made no secret of his opinions (037-009) and was known for his "lurid language" (041-003) and witty remarks, including the observation that "God seemed to have started on making the world and then gone away and left it" (041-022).
- **Ormand** and **Durley** — Other officers in the close circle, often found playing cards or snoring through candlelit evenings.
- **Colonel Kinjack** — The newly arrived commanding officer, who "stopped one night in Morlancourt" (043-002) and whose presence marked the transition from rest back to active operations.

## The Event

The days at Morlancourt were filled with simple pleasures. In the evenings, the officers would gather at the local estaminet — a small café that served as their social hub. "Estaminet! What a memory evokes" (044-019) — Sherston's exclamation captures the nostalgic significance of these moments, the warmth of companionship amid the cold reality of war.

> There we sat, Dick and I and Mansfield (at last released from peacetime soldiering), enjoying the simple luxury of being alive and together. (039-004)

The routine included training during the day and convivial evenings. Dottrell, one of the officers, was noted "having a glass" when Sherston returned late one evening (041-033). The atmosphere was captured in the small rituals: "Ormand and Mansfield playing 'nap,' and Durley sobbing against his nose" (040-003), a scene of almost domestic normalcy.

But the war was never far away. Colonel Kinjack's arrival signalled the end of this period of rest. "Kinjack stopped one night in Morlancourt, and on the following afternoon" (043-002) the transition began. The battalion returned to Morlancourt once more before being thrust back into action — "when the battalion was back at Morlancourt, and Kinjack was having a look round" (043-020).

## Aftermath & Significance

The Morlancourt interlude is significant precisely because of its transience. It represents the last sustained period of peace and companionship Sherston experienced with Dick Tiltwood and Mansfield before the war's demands closed in again. The memories of the estaminet, the card games, and the candlelit evenings would become touchstones of what was lost.

"Safe in Morlancourt, I slept like a log. Sleep was a thing to be thankful for" (044-021) — this simple statement captures the essence of the interlude: a brief, precious moment of safety and rest in a world increasingly defined by danger and loss. Shortly after, the battalion would return to the front lines, and the war would claim many of the faces that populated those Morlancourt evenings.
