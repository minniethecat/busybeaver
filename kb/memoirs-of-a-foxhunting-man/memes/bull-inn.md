## Location

The Bull Inn was a country inn situated in the hunting country within reach of point-to-point meetings. It provided stabling for horses and accommodation for hunting men traveling to race meets.

## The Narrator's Stay

The narrator stayed at the Bull Inn while traveling with his horse Harkaway to a point-to-point meeting. He arrived with a sense of self-importance befitting a young man with his own horse in tow:

> ...portantly into the yard at the Bull Inn with Harkaway's rug rolled on the saddle... [(017-005)](017-005).

The inn yard served as a place to prepare horses and observe the local characters who frequented such establishments during the hunting season:

> ...patch of grass in front of the Bull Inn. He was a dumpy little man with a knowing eye for horseflesh... [(017-010)](017-010).

## Character

The Bull Inn, like many country inns of the Edwardian hunting world, was a node in the social network of the sport — a place where grooms, riders, and local characters intersected between the hunting field and the racecourse.
