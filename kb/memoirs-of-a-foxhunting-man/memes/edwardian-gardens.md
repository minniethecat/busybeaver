## Overview

The memoir opens not with a person or an event, but with a garden: "a house with its large, untidy garden" (001-001). This is no incidental detail—the garden is both a physical setting and a metaphor for the sheltered, abundant world of Sherston's childhood. Gardens in Edwardian rural England were sites of labour, leisure, and social display, maintained by gardeners and enjoyed by the gentry in an elaborate seasonal rhythm.

## Aunt Evelyn's Garden

The garden at Aunt Evelyn's house was the primary landscape of Sherston's early years. It was a "large rambling garden, with its Irish yews and sloping lawns" (004-004)—a space of exploration and solitude for the young boy.

> The large rambling garden, with its Irish yews and sloping lawns, was my undisputed territory. (004-004)

Aunt Evelyn herself was often found there, "wearing her leather gauntlets" (004-008), actively engaged in gardening rather than merely directing her staff. The garden was also a place of emotional retreat—Sherston recalls going to "cry by myself in the kitchen garden" (004-016) after a childhood disappointment.

## The Gardener's Role

The gardener was a distinct figure in the Edwardian estate hierarchy. Aunt Evelyn employed Mabb, with whom she had "serious discussion about the transportation of a rhododendron" (009-002). Dixon, Sherston's beloved groom, was officially the "groom-gardener"—a combined role that reflected the modest scale of the establishment.

> She employed an unpretentious groom-gardener (who would really have suited a small suburban villa). (002-001)

## Garden Parties and Social Display

Garden parties were a staple of Edwardian country social life, occasions where the gentry displayed their horticultural achievements while conducting the essential business of class networking. Aunt Evelyn met the Rector at "a diocesan garden party" (018-024), and Sherston recalls the "Flower Show Tent with a bevy of head-gardeners" (009-012)—a competitive horticultural event that drew the county's gardening elite.

## Gardens and the Rural Landscape

Beyond the formal estate garden, the memoir's landscape is shaped by orchards, hop gardens, and parkland. The journey to the Flower Show Match passes "along between orchards and hop gardens" (008-001), and the train from London runs "down hill, by orchards and hop gardens and parks crowded with ancient trees" (011-006). The distinction between productive agricultural land and ornamental garden reflects the mixed economy of the Edwardian countryside.

| Garden Type | Function | Key Locations |
|-------------|----------|---------------|
| Estate garden | Ornamental, leisure | Aunt Evelyn's house, Rectory garden |
| Kitchen garden | Food production | Behind Aunt Evelyn's house |
| Hop gardens | Commercial agriculture | Along the railway line, Kentish Weald |
| Orchard | Mixed use | Between villages |

## The Rectory Garden

The Rectory garden, adjoining the churchyard, was another significant garden space in the memoir. It was there, "across the field which divided the Rectory garden from the churchyard" (020-001), that Sherston encountered the quiet winter beauty of the English country church.
