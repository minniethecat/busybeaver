## Role in the Memoir

Rob Roy was the first pony given to the narrator George Sherston, arriving when the boy was nine years old. Described as "a perfect picture of a miniature hunter" (003-001), the small black pony with a flowing mane and tail marked the beginning of Sherston's equestrian life. Under [[tom-dixon|Dixon]]'s guidance, Rob Roy carried the boy from his first tentative rides around the paddock to increasingly ambitious expeditions across the Kentish countryside, laying the foundation for everything that followed in Sherston's career as a foxhunting man. The pony's sale, when the narrator had grown too large to ride him, occasioned one of the memoir's first moments of genuine heartbreak.

## Arrival and Early Rides

The acquisition of Rob Roy was Dixon's doing. Having persuaded [[aunt-evelyn|Aunt Evelyn]] that "Master George" needed a pony, Dixon set about finding one suitable for a nervous nine-year-old:

> When I began my rides on Rob Roy, Dixon used to walk beside me, with a leading rein in his hand. (003-002)

The early lessons were patient and deliberate. Dixon built a brush-fence in the paddock for jumping practice and gradually extended their rides to five or six miles, allowing the boy's confidence to grow alongside his skill. The pony's steady temperament was precisely what the young Sherston needed — though Rob Roy was not without spirit, and the narrator would later recall moments of "mischief in Rob Roy" (003-007).

## Independence and Growth

Within eighteen months, the narrator was riding alone, his world expanding with each solo expedition:

> By that time I was going for rides of six or seven miles by myself. (003-004)

These solitary rides on Rob Roy were formative — the first taste of the freedom that hunting would later provide. The pony carried him through the wooded lanes and across the fields of the Weald, a small dark shape viewed with affectionate indulgence by the farmers and villagers who knew the boy by sight.

## Parting

Growing out of Rob Roy was inevitable but painful. The narrator's Aunt Evelyn, ever practical, "showed her inevitable sagacity" in recognizing that the boy needed a larger mount (004-016). But for the young Sherston, the prospect of losing Rob Roy was devastating. He recorded the moment with unguarded emotion:

> … as fond of anyone again as I was of Rob Roy … (004-017)

The pony was eventually replaced by [[sheila|Sheila]], a larger Welsh cob. But Rob Roy's place in the narrator's memory remained fixed — the first horse, the first taste of equestrian freedom, and the beginning of his identity as a horseman.

## Character and Traits

Rob Roy was small, black, and handsome — "a perfect picture of a miniature hunter" with "a flowing mane and tail" (003-001). His temperament balanced steadiness with occasional flashes of independence; he could, as the narrator recalled, show "mischief" on certain afternoons (003-007). The pony's patience with his novice rider was the defining quality that made those early years possible — a steady, forgiving nature that gave a nervous boy the courage to keep riding.
