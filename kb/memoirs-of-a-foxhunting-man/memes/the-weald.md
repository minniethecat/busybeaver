# The Weald

## Location & Setting

The Weald is a broad region of low-lying countryside stretching across parts of Kent and Sussex, characterized by hop-farms, pollard willows, and wooded slopes. From the garden at Butley, Sherston could look out across the Weald, seeing "the belt of river mist" [008-012] that marked the valley below. The landscape is dotted with villages, railway lines, and church towers, offering "lovely glimpses" [009-020] throughout the memoir.

## Significance in the Memoir

The Weald is more than mere scenery—it is the emotional and sensory foundation of Sherston's pre-war world. Nearly every outdoor scene in the early chapters is set against the Weald's changing seasons: summer cricket matches, autumn hunting, winter floods, and spring rides. The landscape provides continuity across the memoir's first four parts.

The Weald also represents a psychological boundary. After Sherston leaves for the Midlands and eventually the war, the Weald becomes a remembered landscape of home and innocence. References to the Weald thin out in the later chapters, marking the break between his Edwardian youth and his military service.

## Key Scenes

In summer, the Weald was beautiful:

> Certain lovely glimpses of the Weald, and the smell of mown hayfields [009-020]

In winter, the floods transformed it:

> The floods were out along the Weald, and the pollard willows by the roadside stood with their feet in water [014-032]

A train journey across the Weald offered a moment of reflection:

> The whistle of a train going along the Weald would remind me of that interval of intense delight [024-004]

Late in the pre-war sections, the Weald remained a touchstone:

> The Weald and the wooded slopes were blue with spring [027-006]
