## Overview

The Yeomanry was the territorial cavalry regiment in which George Sherston and many of his hunting companions enlisted at the outbreak of the First World War. A natural destination for foxhunting men — the skills of horsemanship translated directly to cavalry service — the Yeomanry represented both the continuity of the pre-war sporting world and its ultimate dissolution in the mechanised warfare of the Western Front. The regiment was a bridge: on one side lay the [[ringwell-country|Ringwell country]], the [[the-ringwell-hunt|Point-to-Point races]], and the rhythms of the hunting season; on the other lay [[trench-warfare|the trenches]], gas, and the end of the Edwardian age.

## From Hunting Field to Cavalry

| Aspect | Pre-War Hunting | Yeomanry Service |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Core skill | Riding to hounds | Mounted drill and maneuver |
| Community | Hunt subscribers and farmers | Fellow officers and troopers |
| Horses | Personal hunters (Harkaway, Cockbird) | Army remounts |
| Territory | Kent and Sussex coverts | Training camps, then France |
| Outcome | Sporting glory | Survival or death |

The transition from hunting to cavalry was, on the surface, seamless. Men who had ridden to hounds all their lives found the physical demands of mounted service familiar. [[denis-milden|Denis Milden]] "had disappeared into a cavalry regiment" (036-004); the narrator's knowledge of horse management, learned in Dixon's stable, transferred directly to the care of army remounts. The Yeomanry's officer mess retained something of the atmosphere of a hunt breakfast — the same faces, the same social codes, the same assumption that the war would be a larger version of what they already knew how to do.

## The Illusion and Its Breaking

The memoir is unsparing about the inadequacy of this assumption. Cavalry training — "polishing a cavalry saddle and wondering how it was all going to end" (035-001) — proved to be preparation for a kind of war that no longer existed. The narrator's eventual assignment as a Transport Officer was, he acknowledged, "an anticlimax, for it meant that I shouldn't go into the trenches" (041-022) — and yet the trenches were where the Yeomanry's story, and the story of the hunting world it represented, would reach its terminus.

> Denis had disappeared into a cavalry regiment and was still in England. I envied him his cavalry regiment. (036-004)

## The Regiment's Fate

The Yeomanry regiments of the Great War were among the last expressions of a mounted military tradition stretching back centuries. By 1916, the machine gun and the trench line had rendered the cavalry charge obsolete. The regiment's horses, like the hunters of the Ringwell country, were dispersed; its men, if they survived, returned to a world in which the social order that had produced both foxhunting and the yeomanry cavalry had been irrevocably altered.
