# Mrs. Oakfield

**Mrs. Oakfield** of [[thurrow-park|Thurrow Park]] was a central figure in the Packlestone hunting community. A brilliant horsewoman of remarkable style, she was the dominant personage of the Wednesday hunting country and the only person in the Hunt capable of managing the formidable [[captain-harry-hinnycraft|Captain Harry Hinnycraft]].

## Background and Position

Mrs. Oakfield was deeply rooted in the Hunt. Her father had served as Master for more than twenty years, and she herself had been "intimately associated with the Hunt" since childhood (032-006). The narrator credits her with sustaining the prosperity of the entire Packlestone country:

> An explanation of the continued prosperity of the Packlestone was largely to be found in Mrs. Oakfield of Thurrow Park, a lady who made friends wherever she went. (032-006)

Her influence defined the social geography of the hunting district:

> While Captain Hinnycraft lived and bulked big in the middle of the Monday country, all roads in the Wednesday district converged on Mrs. Oakfield at Thurrow Park. (032-007)

## Estate Management and Relations with Farmers

From Thurrow Park, Mrs. Oakfield ran a large and well-managed estate set an example of "up-to-date (though somewhat expensive) farm-management" (032-006). Her standing among the farming community was exceptional:

> Every farmer in the country (except a few stubborn Radicals) swore by Mrs. Oakfield as the feminine gender of a jolly good fellow. As a fine judge of cattle and sheep they respected her; and to this was added her reputation for boundless generosity. (032-006)

## Riding and Style

Mrs. Oakfield was renowned as a horsewoman. The narrator describes her as a vivid and admired presence in the hunting field:

> A fine figure of a woman she was, they all agreed, as she sailed over the fences in her tall hat and perfectly fitting black habit with a bunch of violets in her buttonhole. (032-006)

Her riding combined natural ability with apparent effortlessness:

> This brilliant horsewoman rode over the country in an apparently effortless manner: always in the first flight, she never appeared to be competing for her prominent position; quick and dashing, she was never in a hurry; allowing for the fact that she was very well mounted and knew the country by heart, she was undoubtedly a paragon of natural proficiency. (032-006)

The narrator adds that "John Leech would have drawn her with delight" (032-006), and confesses his own admiration:

> I admired Mrs. Oakfield enormously; her quickness to hounds was a revelation to me, and in addition she was gracious and charming in manner. Whether she bowed her acknowledgement to a lifted hat at the meet or cantered easily at an awkward bit of timber in an otherwise unjumpable hedge, she possessed the secret of style. (032-006)

## Managing Captain Harry

Mrs. Oakfield had a singular distinction within the Hunt:

> Needless to say, she was the only person in the Hunt who knew how to manage Captain Harry, who always spoke of her as "a splendid little woman." (032-006)

This diplomatic skill, the narrator suggests, was the true explanation for how Captain Hinnycraft's "intractable" behaviour failed to cause as much trouble as might have been expected (032-006).

## Social Life

Mrs. Oakfield was active in the social calendar of the Hunt. In the same week that [[sir-jocelyn-porteus-porteous|Sir Jocelyn Porteus-Porteous]] gave a ball for his eldest daughter's coming-out, "Mrs. Oakfield gave one in the same week — an intensely exciting week for the graceful nymphs, dashing sparks, and diamonded dowagers of the Hunt" (032-009).
