# Yeomanry Training Camp

The Yeomanry training camp represents the narrator's initiation into military life as a trooper in the Yeomanry, a Territorial Army cavalry unit. This period is covered in Part 9 ("In the Army") of the memoir.

## Joining the Yeomanry

The narrator served as a trooper in the Yeomanry during the period before the Great War. In a conversation, he was asked whether he was in the Yeomanry, to which he reluctantly confessed that he was (020-016). His service was healthier than anything he had known before — he was in better physical condition than ever (035-001).

## Camp Life

After mobilization, the Yeomanry as a Territorial unit had its own procedures to follow (035-005). The unit was stationed in a camp of huts (036-002). The narrator marched with the dust on the roads alongside his fellow troopers, though he noted there was no tragic element to this phase of service (035-013).

## Routine and Boredom

As time passed, the narrator began to find Yeomanry life increasingly monotonous. It was becoming a routine existence without the stimulation of his former sporting life (035-018). When Aunt Evelyn asked about his returning to the Yeomanry in the spring, she had already sensed his waning enthusiasm (036-007).

## The Yeomanry Team-Race

During the camp, interest was focused on the Yeomanry Team-Race, a sporting event that provided a welcome diversion from the tedium of military routine (026-041).

## Significance

The Yeomanry service marks a transitional phase between the narrator's hunting life and his eventual deployment to the Western Front. It represents the shift from the Edwardian sporting world to the realities of military service, though the training camp itself remained, at this stage, more a continuation of outdoor life than a premonition of war.
