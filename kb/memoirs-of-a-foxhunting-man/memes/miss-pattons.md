## Role in the Memoir

The Miss Pattons were two elderly sisters who appeared in the memoir as gentle fixtures of the local scene — regular attendants at cricket matches, flower shows, and other village occasions. They belonged to that class of neighbourly figures who, without doing anything remarkable, contributed to the texture of Sherston's remembered world. Their cameo appearances, always on the periphery of more dramatic events, evoke the settled and unhurried rhythms of Edwardian village life.

## At the Flower Show Match

The Miss Pattons first appear during the Flower Show Match, where they were being escorted by Captain Huxtable:

> He was escorting the Miss Pattons, two elderly sisters who always appeared together and were dressed so much alike that no one could tell which was which. (010-016)

At the flower show itself, Sherston noted their modest success:

> … though I observed that the Miss Pattons had got second prize for a tea-cosy … (010-023)

This small domestic triumph — a second-prize tea-cosy — perfectly captures the scale and character of the lives the Miss Pattons led: quiet, industrious, and content within the gentle competitions of the parish flower show.

## On Tricycles

Long after the flower show season, the Miss Pattons continued to be a familiar sight in the neighbourhood. The narrator later recalled:

> … two elderly ladies, the Miss Pattons, who passed me on their tricycles … (026-009)

The image of the two indistinguishable elderly sisters pedalling through the countryside on tricycles is one of the memoir's most charming vignettes — a reminder of a world where two old ladies on tricycles constituted a recognised and fondly regarded element of the local landscape.

## Character and Traits

The Miss Pattons were defined by their inseparability — "two elderly sisters who always appeared together" (010-016). Dressed so alike that they were indistinguishable, they moved through life as a single social unit. Their interests were modest (tea-cosies, flower shows, cricket matches) and their presence was quiet, but they were clearly regarded with affection by the narrator, who remembered them with the same fond precision he brought to the more dramatic figures of his youth.
