## Overview

Education in Edwardian England was stratified by class and geography. For the rural gentry depicted in *Memoirs of a Foxhunting Man*, the typical educational path began with home tutoring in early childhood, followed by boarding school. George Sherston's own experience reflects this pattern: years in the schoolroom under the tutelage of Mr. Star, followed by his time at Ballboro' (a fictionalized version of Marlborough College). The memoir captures the peculiar isolation and intensity of home education, as well as the rough social awakening of boarding school life.

## Home Tutoring: The Schoolroom

Sherston's early education took place entirely within the confines of his Aunt Evelyn's house. The schoolroom was a distinct physical and psychological space—a quiet, fire-lit room where the young George spent his days with his tutor, Mr. Star.

> For several years I never went to school. So I remained at home until I was nine years old, and my only companion was my tutor, Mr. Star. (001-003)

The schoolroom was more than a classroom; it was the center of Sherston's childhood world. It was there that he read Surtees by the fire, studied Beethoven on the piano, and received parcels from Dixon.

> I have a very clear picture of that quiet schoolroom: myself in a brown woollen jersey, sitting at the table with my books. (007-002)

## Mr. Star: The Resident Tutor

The silver-haired schoolmaster Mr. Star was Sherston's first teacher and a formative influence. A gentle, cultured man, he introduced the boy to music and literature while providing the structured education that Aunt Evelyn's prejudice against schools demanded.

> The silver-haired schoolmaster greeted me with his usual gentle gravity. (003-008)

## Boarding School: Ballboro'

When Sherston finally went to school, it was to Ballboro', a public school where he encountered the competitive world of boys' athletics and social hierarchy. Cricket became his "sole credential"—the one area where he could distinguish himself.

> It was a place in my House Eleven at school, and it was my sole credential. (010-001)

The transition from the protected schoolroom to the rough-and-tumble of boarding school life marked a decisive break in Sherston's childhood. Friendships formed there—particularly with Stephen Colwood, a "former schoolfellow"—would carry into his adult hunting life.

## Education and Social Class

The educational experience Sherston describes was inseparable from the Edwardian class system. Home tutoring with a resident master was a privilege of the rural gentry. The village children, by contrast, attended the local school—a distinction Sherston notes in passing but accepts as natural.
