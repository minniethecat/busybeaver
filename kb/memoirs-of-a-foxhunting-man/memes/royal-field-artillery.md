# Royal Field Artillery

The Royal Field Artillery (RFA) was the branch of the British Army responsible for mobile field artillery during the First World War. In *Memoirs of a Foxhunting Man*, the RFA is significant primarily as the service arm of **[[stephen-colwood|Stephen Colwood]]**, whose military career provides a parallel and a contrast to George Sherston's own path through the War.

## Stephen Colwood's Commission

Stephen Colwood's ambition to join the Royal Field Artillery is introduced during a dinner at the Rectory early in the memoir. After recounting the Potford gallop, Sherston learns of Stephen's military aspirations: "If only he could get into the Gunners he'd be happy. His elder brother was in the Rutland Militia and had been drilling every day since the War started" (019-009). But the path was not straightforward — "you couldn't possibly get into the Gunners through the Special Reserve" (020-014), a proposition that was "considered a certainty" at the time but which Stephen would eventually disprove.

## Aldershot and the Special Reserve

By the summer of Denis Milden's mastership, the military world has begun to claim its own. Sherston notes: "I saw next to nothing of Stephen, who was at Aldershot, learning how to be a Special Reserve officer in the Royal Field Artillery" (027-001). Aldershot was the British Army's principal training establishment, and Stephen's presence there marked his transition from hunting companion to professional soldier.

The Special Reserve commission was a compromise between the regular army and the territorial forces, allowing men like Stephen — with social standing and hunting credentials but without the Sandhurst pedigree — to become artillery officers. Stephen's successful navigation of this path was a quiet triumph over the pessimism expressed earlier.

## A Hunting Man in Uniform

Stephen briefly escapes Aldershot to visit the Rectory, where Sherston notes: "Artillery life had caused no apparent change in him" (027-002). The two friends "indulged in cheerful nostalgia for the chase," visiting Cockbird and Harkaway in the paddock, sighing "for a nice moist winter morning" (027-002).

Stephen's military ambition was now focused on getting "attached" to "some Gunners who were conveniently stationed in the Ringwell country" (027-004) — a scheme that would allow him to combine his artillery duties with proximity to the hunting field. This blending of military and sporting identities is characteristic of the memoir's pre-trench-warfare sections, where the War is still something that might be accommodated within the rhythms of foxhunting life.

## The Fate of a Gunner

Stephen Colwood's service in the RFA would eventually take him to the Western Front, where his letters — "long and full of amusing references to the sport that for him symbolized everything enjoyable which the War had interrupted and put an end to" (038-018) — became a lifeline of humour and continuity for Sherston and Dick Tiltwood in their hut at Clitherland Camp.

One such letter describes an "eight-inch" shell landing "twenty yards from our mess," yet Stephen's tone remains facetious: the "fourth footman soon cleared it and my sausage wasn't even cracked, so I had quite a good breakfast" (038-018). He admitted to "looking forward to 'the outbreak of peace,'" comparing his mood to being "booked for a week with the Pytchley and it froze the whole time" (038-018) — measuring the catastrophe of war in the only terms a hunting man truly understood.

Stephen Colwood was killed in action. The telegram — signed simply "Colwood" — arrived at Clitherland Camp while Sherston was orderly officer, and the news of his friend's death in the Royal Field Artillery becomes one of the memoir's most devastating moments.
