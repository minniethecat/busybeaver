# The Front

## Location & Setting

The Front refers to the Western Front in France and Flanders during the First World War (1914–1918). In the memoir's final chapters (Parts 9–10), Sherston is deployed to the line, specifically the Givenchy trenches on the La Bassée Canal [039-008]. The landscape is one of "frost-bound morasses" [039-013], communication trenches, dugouts, and the constant proximity of death.

## Significance in the Memoir

The Front represents the complete rupture from Sherston's Edwardian hunting life. Throughout Parts 9 and 10, "going to the Front" is the looming reality that the training and preparation at home are leading toward. The inevitability is captured in the dry observation: "as I'd got to go to the Front, the sooner I went the better" [036-007].

The Givenchy trenches become a specific, named location where Sherston experiences the war firsthand. The contrast between the Baptist Chapel sermons at home and the reality of the trenches—"the Baptist Chapel made the trenches seem very remote" [038-015]—underscores the psychological distance between the home front and the battle line.
