## Role in the Memoir

Lord Dumborough was the Master of Foxhounds at Dumborough Park and the dominant figure of the Dumborough Hunt, the country where George Sherston's hunting life began. To the young narrator, Lord Dumborough was an object of sheer terror — "the most terrifying man I had ever seen" (005-025) — whose stern gaze and commanding presence on the hunting field represented the formidable adult world of serious foxhunting. He presided over a hunt country that was, for Sherston's family, the nearest and most accessible, and his name recurs throughout the memoir as a symbol of hunting authority and aristocratic sporting life.

## The Dumborough Hunt

The Dumborough country lay on the edge of Sherston's neighbourhood, and from his earliest hunting days the narrator was drawn towards it. His groom [[tom-dixon|Dixon]] often spoke of the Dumborough Hounds with respect:

> … he would pull up his horse and speak a few words of rapture about the Dumborough Hounds; hunting with them was the one thing in life that he still hankered after. (005-001)

The Dumborough, as Sherston later wrote, was a hunt of the highest order (005-002). Dixon's employer had occasionally been given a day with the Dumborough Hounds (002-008), and Dixon himself regarded a day out with them as the pinnacle of sporting experience — "a jolly good day with the Dumborough" (003-005).

## Character and Presence

Lord Dumborough's impact on the young narrator was immediate and visceral. The first time Sherston saw him in the hunting field, he was struck silent with fear:

> Silently I decided that Lord Dumborough was the most terrifying man I had ever seen. (005-025)

His authority in the field was absolute. Sherston recalled him "keeping an intent eye on the proceedings" (005-030), and at a critical moment "galloping up the ride" with an air of command that left no doubt who was in charge (005-034). Even his household staff were part of his legend — Dixon told stories of an ex-butler who "had been with Lord Dumborough for years" (005-042), as if service to the great man conferred a reflected dignity.

The Dumborough establishment extended beyond the Master himself. Lady Dumborough appeared at meets with her own contingent, and "the conspicuous Dumborough Park contingent" was a fixture at local hunting events (006-008). The family's influence reached even to the younger generation — a "good-for-nothing nephew of Lord Dumborough who blundered about the country" represented the less admirable side of aristocratic sporting privilege (017-012).

## Relations and Connections

Lord Dumborough maintained a long-standing annual covert-shooting fixture (021-049) and his hunt employed figures such as Mr. Macdoggart, his hard-riding agent whom Dixon spoke of often (005-002). The Dumborough name carried weight far beyond the hunt field — Sherston's fear that Lord Dumborough "would be certain to hear about" his mistakes (007-023) showed how deeply the Master's judgment was feared across the whole neighbourhood.
