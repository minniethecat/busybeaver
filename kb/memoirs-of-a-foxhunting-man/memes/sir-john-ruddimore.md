## Role in the Memoir

Sir John Ruddimore of Rapworth Park was a local magnate of the reticent, imposing sort — a man whose authority in the hunting field was conveyed not through words but through weighty silence. He belonged to the class of "wiseacres" whom the sporting world treated with automatic deference, and his cameo in the memoir serves to illustrate the hierarchy of county hunting society in the years before the Great War.

## Character and Status

Sir John was not a man given to speech. When Stephen Colwood made a dramatic ejaculation, it was to Sir John Ruddimore that he addressed himself — "a stolid and rather exclusive personage" (028-010). The choice of target was significant: Sir John was the man whose nod mattered.

The narrator placed him among the elect of the hunting field:

> … the real wiseacres, such as Sir John Ruddimore and Fred Buzzaway, nodded confidentially … (028-015)

In the world of the hunt, Sir John was one of those whose quiet judgment carried more weight than the loudest opinion from lesser men. He was described as "that reticent local magnate" (028-016), a phrase that captures both his social position and his characteristic restraint.
