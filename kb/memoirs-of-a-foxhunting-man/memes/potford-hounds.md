# Potford Hounds

The Potford Hounds are a foxhunting pack whose country lies adjacent to the [[the-dumborough-hunt|Dumborough Hunt]]. The Potford appear primarily in Part 4 of *Memoirs of a Foxhunting Man*, which is devoted to George Sherston's "day with the Potford" — a decisive turning point in his early hunting career.

## Organisation and Character

The Potford were reputed to be "short of foxes" (017-004), but their new Master had been "showing good sport" — enough to tempt Sherston away from his disappointing experiences with the Dumborough. The Hunt was notable for its economical operation under Master **Guy Warder**, a middle-aged man who "hunted the hounds himself and did everything as cheaply as possible" (017-009).

Warder's methods were unconventional and divisive: he "bought the most awful old screws for next to nothing at Tattersalls, made his stablemen ride them all the way down from London to save the expense of a horsebox, and brought them out hunting next day" (017-009). The Hunt was "already divided into factions for and against him," and it was "doubtful whether he would be allowed to hunt the country another season" (017-009).

## The Master: Guy Warder

Warder was a controversial figure. Detractors said he was "a bad rider and always held on to the pommel of his saddle when jumping his fences," and it was "also rumoured that he sometimes got very drunk" (017-009). Complaints were made that he "was slow, and often drew the coverts on foot." Yet he was "popular with the farmers, and had been killing an abnormal number of foxes" (017-009) — a record that spoke for itself among those who valued results over style.

## The Potford Country

The Potford country contained "the only good bit of vale" in the region (017-024). The hunt described in Part 4 covers "about four miles" across this vale, ending at a park wall where the huntsman blew his horn "while the hounds scrabbled and bayed rather dubiously over a rabbit-hole" (017-024). Only "eight or ten riders" were up at the finish — a testament to the pace and the quality of the going.

The **Potford Vale** itself became a touchstone in Sherston's sporting memory. His "successful scramble across the Potford Vale obliterated all the dreariness and disappointment" of his days with the Dumborough (018-001). The experience "reinforced" his faith in foxhunting "in the nick of time."

## Sherston's Day with the Potford

The expedition required elaborate logistics: the meet was "nearly fourteen miles" from home, and Dixon rode Harkaway over the night before, staying at the Bull Inn (017-005). Sherston travelled by train the next morning, tying his white stock with difficulty and worrying whether he would "know any of the people out with the Potford" (017-008).

The day itself was Sherston's first genuine hunting success. Mounted on Harkaway — "a bold jumper" who "took complete control" of his rider — Sherston followed [[denis-milden|Denis Milden]]'s friend Mr. Gaffikin "over one fence after another" (017-024). He later acknowledged that "the credit of my being among them belonged to Harkaway" (017-024).

The aftermath was equally memorable. Sherston returned home feeling "a hero," armed with "a big story" to tell Dixon. He later wrote an account of the "Potford gallop" that impressed his friend [[stephen-colwood|Stephen Colwood]], who declared: "You've got a regular gift for writing, you funny old cock!" (019-003).

## Significance

The Potford day marks a structural hinge in the memoir's foxhunting narrative. After the dreary woodland hunts with the Dumborough, the Potford gallop restores Sherston's enthusiasm and sets the stage for his deeper immersion in the hunting world — including his eventual connection to the [[the-ringwell-hunt|Ringwell Hunt]].
