# Church at Hoadley

The Sunday morning service at Hoadley Church, followed by luncheon with Colonel Hesmon, forms one of the most vivid social vignettes in the memoir. Set within the Part 5 narrative "At the Rectory," the episode captures the rhythms of village church life, the characters of the hunting community, and the narrator's sense of being an outsider among habitual worshippers.

## Context & Background

The narrator was staying at Hoadley Rectory with his schoolfellow Stephen Colwood, son of the Rector. The visit had been arranged through the connection of the hunting world — the narrator had come for the Polesham Races (015-045) and was making his first appearances with the Ringwell Hounds. Sunday brought the obligation of church attendance, a ritual that had lapsed in the narrator's life since leaving Aunt Evelyn's household.

## The Morning Service

A tenor bell in Hoadley Church tower was making its ultimate appeal to those who were still on their way to morning service (020-001). The narrator and Stephen hurried hatless across the sloping cricket-field which divided the Rectory garden from the churchyard. The narrator sniffed the quiet wintry-smelling air and wondered how long Mr. Colwood's sermon would last — he had never been to this church before and felt a suggestion of embarrassment at the idea of seeing the Rector in a long white surplice (020-001).

Mrs. Colwood awaited them at the Rectory pew, her face "solemnly cheerful" as one who made the responses as though she meant every word. Stephen, too, had "the serene sobriety of an habitual public-worshipper" — there was no likelihood of his standing up at one of those awkward places when everyone kneels down when you don't expect them to (020-001).

During the service the narrator's attention wandered. He glanced furtively around at the "prudent Sunday-like faces of the congregation" and felt the outside world seem "queer and unreal." His very name suddenly seemed as though it scarcely belonged to him. But Stephen's presence was reassuring — "there was no doubt about his identity," the narrator thought, noting what a nice face he had, "gentle and humorous and alight with natural intelligence" (020-002).

## Colonel Hesmon's Lesson

Colonel Hesmon's appearance to read the First Lesson transformed the service into something between church parade and a point-to-point. His "wizened face and bushy grey eyebrows appeared above the shiny brass eagle" (020-003). This was not quite the same Colonel who had been in a frenzy of excitement over the point-to-point race eight months before. Now he was "every inch a churchwarden," going through the lesson from Isaiah with "dispassionate distinctness and extreme rapidity" — as he would have said, "a rattling good gallop" (020-004).

The narrator found the words incongruous when uttered by the Colonel — the passage from Isaiah spoke of ensigns lifted to the nations, arrows sharp and bows bent, horses' hoofs counted like flint and wheels like a whirlwind (020-004). The brisk little man then turned to a passage from Peter, arranged the gold-embroidered marker, and returned to his pew with erect and decorous demeanour.

## The Sermon

Mr. Colwood climbed the pulpit steps to the strains of "O God our help in ages past." The narrator's vocal contribution was inconspicuous, and a stealthy look at his watch caused Stephen to nudge him with his elbow. The sermon lasted "a laborious twelve minutes" (020-005). The Rector had a nervous mannerism — he continually gathered up his surplice with his left hand as if testing the quality of the linen. The offertory was for a missionary society, and he took as his text: "He that hath two coats, let him impart to him that hath none; and he that hath meat, let him do likewise."

Colonel Hesmon collected the offering on a wooden plate and later remarked at lunch that he "didn't mind saying that with the best will in the world he'd have preferred to give his half-sovereign to someone nearer home" — prompting Stephen to make his rather obvious joke: "Whatever the Guv'nor may say in his sermon about 'imparting,' if I ever get a new hunting-coat I'm going to ruddy well keep my old one for wet days!" (020-005).

## Sunday Stables and Luncheon

After the service, the sun was shining and the Colonel, with his "nattily rolled umbrella, perfectly brushed bowler hat, and nervously blinking eyes," shepherded Stephen and the narrator to his stables. The establishment was a monument to the Colonel's meticulous nature: six hunters in well-aired loose-boxes with the name of each horse suspended above the manger on a tablet; elegant green stable-buckets with the Colonel's numerous initials painted on them in white arranged at regular intervals (020-007).

The Colonel was one of those "fussy riders with indifferent hands who are always trying their horses with a new bit," and the harness-room was, as Stephen observed, "a regular museum of mouth-gear" (020-007). Twenty-seven pairs of hunting-boots lined the cleaning room — each pair signalling the advent of yet another opening meet over the Colonel's decades of methodical hunting (020-010).

At luncheon — roast beef and apple tart — the Colonel was the most attentive of hosts. Over port he gave most of his favourite anecdotes an airing, and the conversation turned to Stephen's ambition to get into the Gunners through the Special Reserve. The Colonel's face lit up: "I tell you what, my boy, I'll write at once to an old friend of mine at the War Office. Excellent officer — used to be in the Twenty-Third. Very useful man on a horse, too" (020-015). The narrator, asked whether he was in the Yeomanry, reluctantly confessed he was not but had been thinking about it — a thought that filled him with "unutterable alarm" (020-016).

## Aftermath & Significance

The Hoadley church episode illuminates the social fabric of the Edwardian hunting community at its most characteristic. The church was not merely a religious institution but a gathering point where hunting, military, and village life converged. Colonel Hesmon, with his twenty-seven pairs of boots, his Balaclava spirit, and his solitary evenings reading "Brooksby" in *The Field*, embodied a generation that had patterned itself on mid-Victorian sporting ideals but had "never seen any active service" (020-011).

The narrator's outsider perspective — his wandering attention during the service, his sense that his very name scarcely belonged to him — foreshadows the deeper alienation that would come with the war. And yet the day ends with warmth and belonging: the Colonel waving from his doorstep "with a certain access of grand seigneur dignity" (020-016), having promised to help Stephen secure a commission.
