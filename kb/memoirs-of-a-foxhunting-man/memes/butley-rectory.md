# Butley Rectory

**Butley Rectory** was the childhood home of the narrator, George Sherston, situated in the village of [[butley-village|Butley]] in Kent. It was here, under the guardianship of his [[aunt-evelyn|Aunt Evelyn]], that the young Sherston spent his formative years before his immersion in the world of [[foxhunting]] and, later, military service.

## Life at the Rectory

The rectory was the setting for the narrator's early education and introduction to country life. It was from the rectory that he was taken by [[tom-dixon|Tom Dixon]] to his first experiences of riding and hunting, and to which he returned between the various hunting seasons and cricket matches that filled his young adulthood.

The rectory's position within Butley placed it within the sound of Butley Church:

> Butley Church struck five in mellow tones. (008-013)

The church clock "was tolling twelve" (009-016) during the cricket matches that formed a central part of village life. The proximity of rectory to church, and of both to the cricket ground, reflects the integrated world of the country parish that shaped the narrator's early sensibilities.

## Relationship to Butley Village

The rectory belonged to the village world described in the memoir: a community of tradesmen, farmers, and parishioners among whom the young Sherston moved as the nephew of the local figure "Miss Sherston." [[folesford-hall|Folesford Hall]], the estate of [[colonel-hesmon|Colonel Hesmon]], lay nearby, and the ringing tones of the [[the-ringwell-hunt|Ringwell Hunt]] marked the seasonal rhythm that increasingly drew the narrator away from his rectory home.

## The Rectory in the Memoir's Structure

The rectory serves as the memoir's emotional point of origin. The narrative begins there with the earliest memories of childhood, and returns to it as a touchstone throughout the pre-war sections. Its quiet domesticity — the schoolroom, the garden, the presence of Aunt Evelyn — stands in implicit contrast to the hunting field and, ultimately, to the trenches of the Western Front, though the memoir never makes this contrast explicit.
