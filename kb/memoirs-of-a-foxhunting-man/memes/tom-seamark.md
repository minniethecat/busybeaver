# Tom Seamark

**Tom Seamark** was the umpire for the Rotherden cricket team, a memorable figure in the annual match against [[butley-village|Butley]] described in the narrator's reminiscences of the [[the-flower-show-match|Flower Show Match]]. A red-faced sporting publican by trade, he was a "tremendous judge of the game" whose emphatic mannerisms and jovial personality made him a fixture of local cricket (010-002).

## Appearance and Manner

The narrator paints a vivid picture of Seamark:

> Tom Seamark, the Rotherden umpire, is a red-faced sporting publican who bulks as large as a lighthouse. (010-002)

His physical presence was matched by a personality that commanded attention on the cricket field.

## Umpiring Style

Seamark's umpiring was characterized by unmistakable gestures:

> As an umpire he has certain emphatic mannerisms. When appealed to he expresses a negative decision with a severe and stentorian "Not Out": but when adjudicating that the batsman is out, he silently shoots his right arm toward the sky — an impressive and irrevocable gesture which effectively quells all adverse criticism. (010-002)

His approach stood in direct contrast to Bill Sutler, the Butley umpire — a cobbler with a wooden leg and a reputation for gross partisanship, who "invariably signifies 'not out' by a sour shake of the head" and "bawls 'Hout' as if he'd been stung by a wasp" (010-003).

## Voice and Presence on the Field

Before the match began, Seamark's voice carried across the ground:

> "Same boundaries as usual, Bill?" shouts Seamark, as loudly as if he were talking to a deaf customer in his taproom. (010-004)

It was Seamark who formally started the match, pronouncing the single word "Play!" as the fierce-browed fast bowler Peckham prepared his run-up (010-006).

## Character and Sociability

Beyond his umpiring duties, Seamark was a man of warmth and good humour. The narrator notes that "when not absorbed by his grave responsibilities he is one of the most jovial men you could meet with" (010-002). At the luncheon during the match, Seamark took it upon himself to welcome a young farmer returned from the Boer War:

> Conversation, however, is scanty, until Tom Seamark, who is always glad of a chance to favour the company with a sentiment, clears his throat impressively, elevates his tankard, fixes Jack Barchard with his gregarious regard, and remarks, "I should like to say, sir, how very pleased and proud we all are to see you safe 'ome again in our midst." (010-010)

The sentiment was warmly received, with glasses raised and gruff acknowledgements around the table.

## Encouragement to the Young Narrator

When the young George Sherston came out to bat, Seamark offered a gruff but kindly welcome: "'Well, young man, you've got to look lively this time,' he observed with intimidating jocosity." The narrator adds that "there seemed to be a twinkle of encouragement in Seamark's light blue eye as I established myself in his shadow" (010-036).
