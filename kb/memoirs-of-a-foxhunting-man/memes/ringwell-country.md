## Overview

Ringwell Country was the hunting territory on the far side of the county that became the center of George Sherston's sporting life during his most active foxhunting years. Home to [[the-ringwell-hunt|the Ringwell Hunt]] and the kennels where [[denis-milden|Denis Milden]] served as Master of Foxhounds, it represented everything the narrator's own unhunted neighbourhood lacked: established coverts, a committed network of supportive farmers, and the social world of serious hunting people. The narrator's migration to the Ringwell country — first as a visitor for Point-to-Point races, later as a subscriber and regular — marks his transition from Dixon's protégé to an independent foxhunting man.

The Ringwell country was not without its challenges. The narrator noted that it was "a bad country for thorns" (028-001), requiring careful management of horses' legs. The farmers' "staunch support" (015-014) was considered exceptional even by the standards of hunting counties, and their cooperation in maintaining coverts and tolerating the hunt's passage across their land was central to the Hunt's viability.

## The Ringwell Hunt

The [[the-ringwell-hunt|Ringwell Hunt]] was the defining institution of the country. Under [[denis-milden|Denis Milden]]'s mastership, it became the narrator's sporting home. The Hunt's activities structured the calendar: cubbing in early autumn, regular meets through the winter season, and the Ringwell Point-to-Point Races in spring. The narrator's subscription to the Hunt — a rite of passage — confirmed his status as a proper hunting man rather than a casual visitor.

> Ringwell cubbing days are among my happiest memories of the hunting field. (030-007)

## The Point-to-Point

The Ringwell Hunt Point-to-Point Races were a highlight of the sporting calendar and a crucial milestone in the narrator's development. His first visit, when Dixon encouraged him to go "as it was a real sporting event" (015-003), planted the ambition that would culminate in his entry of Cockbird in the Ringwell Heavyweight Race. The race meeting drew competitors and spectators from across the county, and success there carried genuine prestige.

> I had made up my mind to go to the Ringwell Hunt Point-to-Point Races. I still wanted to go, for Dixon had said that it was a real sporting event. (015-001/003)

## The Kennels

Ringwell Kennels served as the Master's residence and the operational center of the Hunt. Staying there as [[denis-milden|Denis Milden]]'s guest was a mark of the narrator's acceptance into the inner circle of the hunting world. The narrator reflected on "how different staying at Ringwell Kennels was from what I'd expected" (029-013), suggesting both the glamour and the reality of life at the center of a hunt establishment.

## Migration and Departure

The narrator's gradual migration to the Ringwell country was both geographical and social — a movement from the margins of the hunting world to its center, from Dixon's protégé to a peer of the Master. The [[the-ringwell-hunt|Ringwell Hunt]] subscription, the entry at the Point-to-Point, and the invitation to the Kennels all marked stages in this ascent. When the war came, the narrator's thoughts turned back to "England and the Ringwell Hunt" (039-007) as an emblem of the life he had left behind — a world of horses, hounds, and countryside that the trenches made unimaginably distant.
