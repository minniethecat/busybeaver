# Dick Tiltwood

**Dick Tiltwood** was a school friend of George Sherston at [[ballboro-school|Ballboro' School]] and later a fellow officer at Clitherland Camp. He was the son of Canon Tiltwood, and through his Sandhurst training and natural good nature, he became an indispensable companion to the narrator during the early months of the war.

## Background and Character

Dick was the product of a long line of country gentlemen. As the narrator describes:

> He was the son of a parson with a good family living. Generations of upright country gentlemen had made Dick Tiltwood what he was, and he had arrived at manhood in the nick of time to serve his country in what he naturally assumed to be a just and glorious war. (038-017)

He left Ballboro' School six months before Sherston and passed through [[sandhurst|Sandhurst]] before joining the Army (038-017). His father, Canon Tiltwood, is described at Victoria Station as "proud and burly, pacing the platform beside his slender son and wearing cheeriness like a light unclerical overcoat, which couldn't conceal the gravity of a heart heavy as lead" (039-002).

## A Representative of His Generation

The narrator casts Dick Tiltwood in symbolic terms, as an embodiment of the young men who went to the First World War without cynicism:

> Everyone told him so; and when he came to Clitherland Camp he was a shining epitome of his unembittered generation which gladly gave itself to the German shells and machine-guns — more gladly, perhaps, than the generation which knew how much (or how little, some would say) it had to lose. (038-017)

## Companionship at Clitherland

At Clitherland Camp, Dick Tiltwood became the narrator's mainstay. His presence transformed the experience of military training:

> Dick made all the difference to my life at Clitherland. Apart from his cheerful companionship, which was like perpetual fine weather, his Sandhurst training enabled him to help me in mine. (038-017)

Tiltwood assisted the narrator with the practical demands of soldiering, patiently hearing him through "repetitions of the mechanism of the rifle" and helping with company drill, in which Sherston was "slow in learning" (038-017).
