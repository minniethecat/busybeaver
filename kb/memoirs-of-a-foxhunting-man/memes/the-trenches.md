# The Trenches

## Location & Setting

The trenches refer to the defensive trench systems of the Western Front in France, specifically the Givenchy trenches on the La Bassée Canal [039-008] where Sherston was deployed. The trench network included front-line positions, communication trenches "about three quarters of a mile" long [039-012], and dugouts. The surrounding landscape was "frost-bound morasses" [039-013] in winter, with skeleton rafters marking destroyed buildings.

## Significance in the Memoir

The trenches are the physical embodiment of the war's grim reality, standing in stark opposition to the pastoral Weald of Sherston's youth. The memoir's shift to trench warfare in Parts 9–10 marks its tonal transformation from Edwardian sporting memoir to war narrative.

The trenches appear as both a dreaded destination and an inevitability. Men who had been to the trenches and returned were marked by the experience [038-006]. The anticipation of going "into the trenches" [041-022] pervades the training chapters. For Sherston, a late deployment as Transport Officer meant he "shouldn't go into the trenches" [041-022]—a reprieve that colors the memoir's closing perspective.
