## Overview

The Ringwell Hunt was the foxhunt that became the center of George Sherston's sporting life during his most active years as a foxhunting man. Under the mastership of [[denis-milden|Denis Milden]], the Hunt combined competitive racing at the annual Ringwell Point-to-Point with the daily rituals of the chase across well-wooded country. Its farmers were exceptionally supportive, its coverts reliably held foxes, and its Kennels — where the narrator stayed as the Master's guest — represented the inner circle of the hunting world. The narrator's subscription to the Ringwell marked his transition from Dixon's protégé to an independent hunting man.

Denis Milden's mastership gave the Hunt its distinctive character. Young, competent, and quietly charismatic, Milden maintained the Hunt on a financial model that required him to put "money out of his own pocket to hunt the Ringwell country" (031-002). His dedication earned the loyalty of the farming community and attracted riders from across the region, including the narrator, who first encountered the Hunt as a spectator at the Point-to-Point and gradually worked his way into its inner circle.

## The Point-to-Point

The Ringwell Hunt Point-to-Point Races were the sporting highlight of the season, drawing competitors and spectators from across the county. The narrator's first visit, encouraged by [[tom-dixon|Dixon]] who "had said that it was a real sporting event" (015-003), planted the ambition that would culminate in his entry of Cockbird in the Ringwell Heavyweight Race. The Heavyweight was a serious contest — success there confirmed a rider's standing in the hunting community.

## The Ringwell in the Memoir

The Ringwell Hunt appears in the memoir at every stage of the narrator's development. In the early chapters, it is the distant ideal that Dixon invokes — the hunt that subscribed to [[the-dumborough-hunt|Lord Dumborough's Hounds]], the standard against which Butley's unsporting neighbourhood was measured. In the middle chapters, it is the narrator's sporting home, the scene of his happiest hunting days. And in the final chapters, it is the memory that haunts the trenches:

> I had been straight back to England and the Ringwell Hunt. (039-007)

## Decline and Memory

The war effectively ended the Ringwell as the narrator had known it. [[denis-milden|Denis Milden]] "had disappeared into a cavalry regiment" (036-004), the horses were sold or requisitioned, and the hunting country stood empty of the riders who had crossed it each winter. In the memoir's closing pages, the Ringwell survives only in memory — a "cold-scented country" (021-052) whose real value lay not in the quality of its hunting but in the community it sustained.
