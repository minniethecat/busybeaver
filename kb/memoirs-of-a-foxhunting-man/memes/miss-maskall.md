## Role in the Memoir

Miss Clara Maskall was one of the more colourful characters of Sherston's neighbourhood — a remarkable old lady whose eccentricities and sharp tongue provided a memorable interlude during the Flower Show Match episode. Her appearance at the cricket match, and her dry commentary on local gardening pretensions, offered the narrator a glimpse of village character that stood apart from the hunting-and-cricket set. She was a figure whom people sought out for the sheer entertainment of her company.

## Eccentric Gardening

Miss Maskall was known for an unusual approach to hedge maintenance. The neighbourhood had more than once found her:

> … sitting on the top step of a ladder, clipping her hedge with a pair of garden shears. (010-026)

This precarious gardening method was only one of her peculiarities, but it captured her determined and unconventional spirit — a woman of advanced years who saw no reason to abandon the management of her own hedges, however unorthodox the method.

## The Flower Show Match

Miss Maskall's most vivid appearance comes at the Flower Show Match, where she engaged the young Sherston in conversation. With characteristic dry humour, she made sport of the local flower-show ambitions. When Sherston mentioned a neighbour's gardening pretensions, she delivered a devastating dismissal:

> … "not even a cucumber frame." (010-029)

The narrator noted that "Miss Maskall chuckled delightedly at this" (010-029), enjoying her own wit as much as her audience did. The encounter left an impression:

> Miss Maskall had made the game seem rather … (010-032)

Her presence transformed the cricket match from a mere sporting event into something richer — a social occasion animated by the peculiar and endearing personalities of the local neighbourhood.

## Character and Traits

Miss Maskall belonged to a type Sherston clearly relished — the elderly eccentric whose idiosyncrasies were a source of affection rather than ridicule. She was sharp-witted, unpretentious, and wholly herself. Her habit of perching on a ladder to clip hedges, her frank assessments of neighbours' gardening, and her delighted chuckle at her own dry remarks all paint a portrait of a woman who had long since ceased to care what anyone thought of her and had become, in the process, one of the neighbourhood's most cherished characters.
