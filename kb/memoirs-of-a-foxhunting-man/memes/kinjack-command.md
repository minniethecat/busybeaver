# Colonel Kinjack's Command

## Overview

Lieutenant-Colonel Kinjack arrived to take command of the battalion in the summer of 1916, bringing with him a reputation for strict discipline and military efficiency. His arrival was anticipated with a mixture of dread and dark humour by the officers, who predicted sweeping changes. Kinjack's tenure as commanding officer marked a sharp transition in the battalion's character — from the more relaxed atmosphere under the previous commander to a regime of exacting standards that reflected the intensifying nature of the war.

## Context & Background

The battalion had been serving on the Western Front under its previous commanding officer, who had received his brigade and moved on. The vacancy was filled by Major Kinjack, who was expected from the Second Battalion and promoted to Lieutenant-Colonel upon assuming command (042-010). The news of his impending arrival spread through the battalion with a palpable sense of apprehension.

"There'll be a few changes when Kinjack rolls up to take command of this battalion," the officers told each other (042-009). His reputation preceded him — a man known for exacting standards, particularly in matters of turnout and discipline.

## Key Participants

- **Lieutenant-Colonel Kinjack** — The new commanding officer, promoted from Major upon taking command (043-001). Known for his beautifully brushed hair and immaculate appearance (042-012). His presence inspired both fear and respect.
- **Sherston (the narrator)** — A junior officer in the battalion, observing Kinjack's impact with a foxhunting man's eye for character.
- **O'Brien** — An officer who accompanied Kinjack on reconnaissance patrols into no man's land.
- **The Transport Officer** — Subject of one of Kinjack's most memorable inspections.

## The Event

The arrival of Kinjack was heralded by a wave of anticipatory anxiety. The officers exchanged knowing comments:

> "Kinjack'll soon have the horse-clippers on him," they said of anyone whose turnout was less than immaculate, a reference to Kinjack's own "beautifully brushed hair" (042-012).

When Kinjack finally arrived, he did not disappoint. "Lieutenant-Colonel Kinjack (to give him his new rank) exceeded all expectations" (043-001). His first actions set the tone for his command. He stopped one night in Morlancourt, and on the following afternoon, the battalion felt his presence immediately.

> The Boches saw Kinjack coming all right. The Transport Officer wanted to be, that afternoon; Kinjack frightened the life out of me. (043-003)

His inspections were legendary. When the battalion was back at Morlancourt, "Kinjack was having a look round the Transport" (043-020), an event that officers recounted with nervous laughter. His standards extended to every aspect of battalion life — from the condition of equipment to the bearing of the men.

The impact was not merely punitive. Kinjack represented a new phase of the war — one in which efficiency and discipline were essential to survival. "Being chivvied by Kinjack, and then being 'crumped' all night" (043-015) became the rhythm of existence, the relentless pressure of command followed by the relentless pressure of enemy artillery.

When tactical action was needed, Kinjack led from the front. He sent O'Brien and Sherston on reconnaissance patrols, judging that "would be better than nothing, Kinjack said" (044-016) — a laconic assessment typical of his pragmatic leadership style.

## Aftermath & Significance

Colonel Kinjack's arrival marked the end of the battalion's more informal early-war character. Under his command, the unit became sharper, more disciplined, and better prepared for the intensifying conflict. His presence loomed over every aspect of battalion life — "messages came from Kinjack or the Adjutant" (044-009), a constant reminder of the chain of command.

For Sherston personally, Kinjack represented something familiar in an unfamiliar world — the figure of the exacting, formidable authority that echoed the hunting field's discipline. The mixture of fear, respect, and dark humour with which the officers regarded Kinjack captures the peculiar psychology of military life under a strong commander.

The Kinjack period, as it might be called, was the battalion's transition from the early-war amateur spirit to the hardened professionalism that the later phases of the war demanded. His command bridged the Morlancourt interlude and the brutal campaigns that followed.
