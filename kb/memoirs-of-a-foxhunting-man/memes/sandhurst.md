# Sandhurst

The Royal Military College at Sandhurst is the British Army's officer training establishment, referenced several times in the later sections of *Memoirs of a Foxhunting Man* as a marker of social and military distinction among the young officers of the First World War.

## Institution

Sandhurst represented the traditional route to a regular army commission. In the Edwardian and wartime period depicted in the memoir, attendance at Sandhurst conferred immediate professional credibility — a distinction not lost on George Sherston, who had come to soldiering through the Yeomanry and a temporary commission rather than the regular army path.

## Sandhurst Men in the Memoir

### Young Nunburne

The first Sandhurst reference comes during the Yeomanry's early mobilization in the autumn of 1914. "Young Nunburne (the M.P.'s son) was whisked away to Sandhurst, his father having decided that he would be more suitably situated as a subaltern in the Guards" (035-015). Nunburne's departure from the Yeomanry camp for Sandhurst marks a social demarcation: the M.P.'s son was being steered toward the more prestigious regular army commission, while Sherston and his comrades remained with the territorial force.

Sherston notes that while "his departure made a difference," it did "not convince me that I ought to become an officer myself" (035-015) — a characteristic moment of self-awareness about his own ambiguous military identity.

### Dick Tiltwood

The most significant Sandhurst figure in the memoir is **[[dick-tiltwood|Dick Tiltwood]]**, Sherston's closest companion during the Clitherland Camp period. Tiltwood had "left school six months before and had since passed through Sandhurst" (038-017). Sherston describes him as "the son of a parson with a good family living," whose "generations of upright country gentlemen had made Dick Tiltwood what he was" (038-017).

Tiltwood's Sandhurst training had practical benefit for Sherston: "Patiently he heard me while I went through my repetitions of the mechanism of the rifle. And in company drill, which I was slow in learning, he was equally helpful" (038-017). The formal military instruction Tiltwood received at Sandhurst stood in contrast to Sherston's more improvised path through the Yeomanry.

### Social Cachet

When Sherston, Tiltwood, and Mansfield arrive at the Front and join C Company of the First Battalion, the battalion's social dynamics are described in revealing terms: "Mansfield (who had been received with reservations of cordiality), Dick (*persona grata* on account of his having been at Sandhurst, and also because no one could possibly help liking him at sight), and I (no comment required)" (039-006).

This passage makes explicit the social hierarchy of the officers' mess: Sandhurst attendance marked a man as *persona grata* — instantly acceptable in regular army circles — while civilian volunteers like Sherston were tolerated but unclassified. Sherston's wry parenthesis "(no comment required)" is a characteristic self-deprecating acknowledgment of his own ambiguous status.

Later, Sherston notes that "all the battalion officers except the C.O., the Second-in-Command, and the quartermaster, and four or five subalterns from Sandhurst" were civilians (039-007). The "four or five subalterns from Sandhurst" represent the thin thread of professional military continuity in a battalion largely composed of wartime volunteers.

## Significance

Sandhurst functions in the memoir not as a location in its own right, but as a marker of the social and professional distinctions that structured the British Army of the First World War. The contrast between Sandhurst-trained regulars and civilian volunteers like Sherston mirrors the larger tension in the memoir between inherited tradition and individual experience — a tension that runs through the foxhunting sections as much as the military ones.
