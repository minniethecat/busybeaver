# Joining the First Battalion

The journey to join the first battalion marks the narrator's entry into the actual war — the transition from training camps and preparation to the reality of the Western Front. This episode, which opens Part 10 of the memoir, carries the narrator and his friend Dick Tiltwood across the Channel into France and toward the trenches.

## Context & Background

After months of training with the Yeomanry and service in England, the narrator and Dick Tiltwood were finally posted to active service. The first battalion had been in action — it had lost more than half its strength (038-014) — and was being filled up with drafts. The two friends had been fortunate to secure postings to the same battalion, thanks to the Adjutant at Clitherland who had sworn to do his best to get them both sent together (039-005).

## Crossing the Channel

For the first time in their lives, the narrator and Dick crossed the Channel. They crossed in bright moonlight on a calm sea, sitting together on a tarpaulin cover in the bow of the boat, which was happily named *Victoria* (039-001). Long after midnight they had left Folkestone; had changed course in an emergency avoidance of Boulogne, caused by the sinking of a hospital ship; had stared at Calais harbour, and seen sleepy French faces in the blear beginnings of November daylight.

There followed four sunless days of north wind among pine-trees at Etaples, waiting to be posted to their battalion. The hiatus of uncertainty was finally behind them, and in a soiled fawn-coloured first-class compartment, they clanked and rumbled along: "The real War, that big bullying bogey, had stood up and beckoned to us at last, and now the Base Camp was behind us with its overcrowded discomforts that were unmitigated by esprit de corps" (039-001).

## The Journey Inland

The narrator travelled with Dick, Mansfield (at last released from peacetime Army conventions), and Joe Barless — a gimlet-moustached ex-sergeant-major who submitted philosophically to his elevation into officerdom and spat on the floor at frequent regular intervals (039-004). Barless had been out before; had been hit at the first battle of Ypres; had left a wife and family behind him. When the group heard distant bangs at St. Pol — like the slamming of a heavy door — Barless knocked out his pipe and expectorated with a grim little jerk of his bullet head (039-004). The others looked to him for guidance.

The French countryside struck the narrator as a "hopeless hunting country" — and he reflected that "the opening meet would have been last week if there hadn't been this war" (039-005). Dick munched chocolate and read the *Strand Magazine*, with its cosy reminder of London traffic on the cover.

## Reporting at Bethune

They arrived at Bethune by half-past ten. Reporting at battalion headquarters — possibly a wine-merchant's warehouse — the narrator was received by Colonel Winchell, who was wearing dark brown field boots that insinuated by every supple contour that they came from Craxwell (039-006).

In a small-world coincidence, Colonel Winchell was a distant relative of Colonel Hesmon and had heard all about how the narrator won the Colonel's Cup. Colonel Hesmon's conversational repertoire being limited, the new Commanding Officer could tell the narrator the name of his horse and was already well known to the narrator by reputation (039-006). The narrator reflected that being known as a hunting man was "a distinct asset" when in close contact with officers of the Regular Army — "it gave one an almost unfair advantage in some ways" (039-007).

## C Company

The narrator, Dick, and Mansfield were all posted to C Company, which was short of officers. The battalion had been much below full strength and was now being filled up with drafts. They had arrived at a good time — the Division was about to be withdrawn to a back area for a long rest (039-008). Captain Barton, the company commander, was found by a smoking stove in a small tiled room. A big, burly, good-natured man who had been at Harrow and until the War had lived a comfortably married life on an adequate unearned income, Barton was "a man of snug and domesticated habits" whose mere presence in a front-line trench "made one feel that it ought, at any rate, to be cosy" (039-009).

The narrator's platoon accepted him apathetically. It was a diminished and exhausted little platoon, its mind occupied with anticipations of Divisional Rest (039-010). Among his fellow officers was Julian Durley, a shy, stolid-faced platoon commander who had been a clerk in Somerset House. He took the men's discomforts very much to heart, and his jokes when things were going badly reminded the narrator of "a facetious bus conductor on a wet winter day." The narrator noted that Durley had never hunted — "he could swim like a fish, but no social status was attached to that" (039-016).

## Working Parties

The narrator's first days were spent not in combat but on working parties — marching to ruined Festubert in darkness and rain, carrying hurdles up communication trenches for three quarters of a mile (039-012). His diary records the rhythm of those early days: digging from midnight till two, watching flares go up frequently overhead, and coming home soaked at quarter to ten (039-013).

## Aftermath & Significance

The arrival at the first battalion is a threshold moment. Everything the narrator had known — Aunt Evelyn's desperate smile at Victoria Station, Dick's father Canon Tiltwood pacing the platform, the hunting fields of the Ringwell — was now behind him. "To have finished with farewells; that in itself was a burden discarded" (039-003). The chapter marks the definitive end of the Edwardian world and the beginning of the narrator's life as a soldier on the Western Front.
