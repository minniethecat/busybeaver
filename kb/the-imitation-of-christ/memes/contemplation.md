# Contemplation

The elevation of the soul to God in loving, wordless attention — a gift of divine sweetness bestowed upon those who have separated themselves from [[creation|creatures]] and mortified their inward affections, yet a gift that can never be wholly continuous in this mortal life and that must be received with deep humility lest it become an occasion of pride — in the teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Contemplation, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not a technique of meditation or a method of mental prayer but a supernatural gift by which the soul is raised above [[creation|creatures]] and even above its own natural operations into a direct, loving attention to God. Thomas à Kempis distinguishes it sharply from acquired knowledge: the wisdom that "floweth down from the divine fulness above" is better than "that which is acquired laboriously by human study" (068-024 to 068-025). Contemplation is not the fruit of human effort but the gift of divine largesse — "the sweetness of the contemplation of Thee, which Thou bestowest upon those who serve Thee with their whole heart" (047-007). Yet for all its gratuity, contemplation has conditions: the soul must be "freed from all [[creation|creatures]]" (068-011), detached "in spirit from outward things" (011-017), and emptied of the self-will that clogs the channels of grace. Contemplation is at once the highest act of which the human soul is capable and the one in which the soul's own activity is most radically suspended in favour of God's.

## Biblical Foundation

The Kempian theology of contemplation is rooted in the biblical tradition of seeking the face of God. The Psalmist's cry — "One thing have I desired of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], that will I seek after; that I may dwell in the house of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] all the days of my life, to behold the beauty of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Psalm 27:4) — is the essential contemplative aspiration. Mary of Bethany, who "sat at [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]' feet, and heard His word" (Luke 10:39), is the biblical type of the contemplative soul, and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s word that she "hath chosen that good part, which shall not be taken away from her" (Luke 10:42) is the charter of the contemplative life.

The contemplative ascent from [[creation|creatures]] to God reflects the Pauline progression: "We all, with open face beholding as in a glass the glory of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], are changed into the same image from glory to glory" (2 Corinthians 3:18). Thomas's insistence that contemplation requires detachment from [[creation|creatures]] echoes Colossians 3:2: "Set your affection on things above, not on things on the earth." The incompleteness of contemplation in this life — the necessity of descending from the heights — reflects 1 Corinthians 13:12: "Now we see through a glass, darkly; but then face to face." Contemplation is the dim mirror; the beatific vision is the face-to-face.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Contemplation is a recurrent and elevated theme in The Imitation, appearing in connection with the soul's progress, the practice of solitude, the danger of pride, and the inevitable alternation between heights of spiritual sweetness and the lowlands of human weakness.

### The Conditions of Contemplation

Thomas is insistent that contemplation is not available to everyone at every time — not because God withholds it arbitrarily but because the soul's attachments block its reception. "For this reason are there found so few contemplative persons, because few know how to separate themselves entirely from perishing and [[creation|created things]]" (068-013 to 068-014). The condition is absolute: "except a man be freed from all [[creation|creatures]], he will not be able to reach freely after Divine things" (068-011). The word "freely" is key — the soul can certainly strive after God while still entangled with [[creation|creatures]], but it cannot move freely, without hindrance, as contemplation requires. Every attachment is a weight; every inordinate desire is a chain. The contemplative soul is the soul that has cut every chain and travels light.

This separation is not merely external — a physical withdrawal from [[world|the world]] — but interior: freedom "from our affections and desires" (011-019). The monk in his cell may be as entangled as the merchant in his counting-house if his heart clings to [[creation|created things]]. True contemplation requires that the soul be "fully watchful of ourselves, and not bound in spirit to outward things" (011-016 to 011-017). The metaphor of being "bound" — tied, tethered — captures the spiritual physics: the soul cannot rise because it is fastened to the ground. Contemplation is what becomes possible when the cords are cut.

Yet Thomas also notes the gap between desire and practice: "Many are found who desire contemplation, but they do not strive to practice those things which are required thereunto" (068-026 to 068-027). Desire without mortification is fantasy. The contemplative life is not a mood one can enter at will but the fruit of sustained ascetical preparation — the long, slow work of detaching the heart from everything that is not God.

### The Sweetness of Contemplation

When contemplation is granted, its sweetness surpasses every natural pleasure. Thomas describes the saints who "spent their whole time profitably; every hour seemed short for retirement with God; and through the great sweetness of contemplation, even the need of bodily refreshment was forgotten" (018-022 to 018-024). The sweetness is so great that it suspends the body's natural demands — hunger, thirst, fatigue are simply not felt. This is not a denial of the body but a transcendence of it: when the soul is absorbed in God, the body's claims are temporarily silenced.

This sweetness is described as a "visitation from above" (035-012) that differs entirely from "false liberty of spirit and great confidence in self" (035-013). True contemplation brings humility, not pride; [[contrition|compunction]], not self-satisfaction. Thomas draws a crucial line: "I desire no [[comfort|consolation]] which taketh away from me [[contrition|compunction]], I love no contemplation which leadeth to pride" (035-020 to 035-021). The test of genuine contemplation is its fruit. If it leaves the soul humbler, more wary, more ready to renounce itself, it is from God. If it leaves the soul puffed up, self-satisfied, contemptuous of others, it is a counterfeit — "not holy, nor is everything that is sweet good" (035-022).

### The Limits of Contemplation in This Life

With characteristic realism, Thomas acknowledges that contemplation cannot be continuous in this mortal state. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself teaches the soul: "Thou art not always able to continue in very fervent desire after virtues, nor to stand fast in the loftier region of contemplation; but thou must of necessity sometimes descend to lower things because of thine original corruption, and bear about the burden of this mortal life" (088-003 to 088-006). The "loftier region of contemplation" is a real country that the soul may visit, but it cannot dwell there permanently on earth. The weight of original sin — the "burden of the flesh" — pulls the soul back down. "Thou canst not give thyself to spiritual studies and divine contemplation unceasingly" (088-011).

This is not a counsel of despair but of realism. The soul that expects to remain always on the heights will be crushed by disappointment when it inevitably descends. The wise soul accepts the rhythm: ascent in contemplation when God grants it, descent to humble manual works and outward duties when the gift is withdrawn — and gratitude in both states. "At such a time it is expedient for thee to flee to humble and outward works" (088-012). Contemplation and action are not enemies but alternating expressions of the single life of love.

### Contemplation and Human Misery

Book I, Chapter 22 is titled "On the Contemplation of Human Misery" — a striking formulation that reorients contemplation away from exclusively sweet experiences and toward the sober consideration of man's fallen condition. "Thou art miserable wheresoever thou art, and whithersoever thou turnest" (022-002). This is contemplation not of God's sweetness but of one's own poverty — a necessary preparation for the higher contemplation, for the soul that has not faced its own misery is not yet ready to receive the gift of divine sweetness without pride. Contemplation of human misery is the purgative ground of all higher contemplative gifts.

This theme is echoed in Thomas's ad[[sin|vice]] to "let thy contemplation be placed upon the most High and unchangeable Good" (026-035) rather than upon passing earthly things. The object of contemplation determines its value: contemplation that fixes upon [[creation|creatures]] — their beauty, their order, their passing sweetness — is a detour at best and an idolatry at worst. Contemplation that fixes upon God is the one thing necessary.

## Related Concepts

- [[prayer]] — Contemplation is prayer raised to its highest simplicity; it is prayer beyond words, the soul's silent attention to God
- [[devotion]] — The fervent love that disposes the soul for contemplation; devotion is the fuel, contemplation the flame
- [[humility]] — The essential safeguard of contemplation; without humility, contemplation becomes a path to pride and ruin
- [[grace]] — Contemplation is a gift of grace, not an achievement; "the sweetness of the contemplation of Thee" is "bestowed"
- [[consolation]] — The felt sweetness that often accompanies contemplation; to be received with gratitude but not clung to
- [[self-denial]] — The negative condition of contemplation; the soul must be freed from [[creation|creatures]] before it can reach freely after God
- [[mortification]] — The practical preparation for contemplation; "few know how to separate themselves entirely from perishing things"
- [[pride]] — The great danger of contemplation; the soul that claims for itself what God gives falls from the height it has reached
- [[love]] — The goal of contemplation; the contemplative gazes upon God because she loves Him, and gazing, loves Him more
