# Simplicity

Thomas à Kempis presents simplicity not as naivety or intellectual poverty but as the highest achievement of the spiritual life: the unification of the whole person around a single end. "The more a man hath unity and simplicity in himself, the more things and the deeper things doth he understand" (003-021). This is a striking paradox — that simplicity, not complexity, is the condition of deep understanding. The simple heart is not the ignorant heart but the undivided heart, the heart that has ceased to be pulled in many directions and has gathered all its energies toward God.

## Definition

Simplicity, in The Imitation, is inward singleness of purpose. It is the opposite of duplicity — the state of the man who serves two masters, who tries to love both God and [[world|the world]], whose heart is "divided" and therefore "found wanting" (cf. Daniel 5:27). Thomas defines it by its effects: "simplicity of heart" (100-015) makes a man transparent before God, able to say with the psalmist, "I have walked in mine integrity." The simple man has no hidden agenda, no secret attachment, no reservation in his self-offering. What he appears to be before men, he is before God; what he professes with his lips, he holds in his heart. Simplicity is the virtue that makes hypocrisy impossible.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas treats simplicity under several related aspects — as the quality of pure intention, as the characteristic of grace as opposed to nature, and as the disposition that attracts divine companionship. Throughout, simplicity is presented not as a beginner's virtue but as the mature fruit of long purification.

### Simplicity as Pure Intention

The chapter "Of a Pure Mind and Simple Intention" (029-001) places simplicity at the centre of the moral life. "By two wings is a man lifted above earthly things, even by simplicity and purity" (029-002). Simplicity is one wing — the orientation of the heart toward God — and purity is the other — the cleansing of the heart from all that is not God. Together they enable the soul to rise above the distractions and attachments that weigh it down. A man with simple intention "seeketh not himself in anything, but doth all things for God" (029-004); the absence of self-seeking is the surest sign that simplicity has taken root.

Thomas contrasts the man of simple intention with the man of divided heart. The latter "is tossed about and carried away by many things" (029-005), never at rest because he is never fully committed to any one end. The simple man, by contrast, "hath peace, because he seeketh the glory of God and his own rest in God" (029-006). [[peace|Peace]] is the daughter of simplicity; anxiety is the child of a divided heart.

### Simplicity of Heart Before God

Thomas places in the mouth of the [[discipleship|disciple]] a prayer that reveals the interior form of simplicity: "O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], in the simplicity of my heart, in good and firm faith, and at Thy commandment, I offer and commend myself this day unto Thee" (100-015). Simplicity of heart is the condition of genuine self-offering. The man whose heart is complicated by self-interest cannot wholly give himself, because part of him is always held back, calculating the cost, measuring the return. Only the simple heart can make a total gift.

This simplicity before God is cultivated in prayer and expressed in action. Thomas exhorts the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "walk before Me in truth, and in the simplicity of thy heart" (041-002). The pairing of truth and simplicity is significant: simplicity without truth is mere credulity; truth without simplicity is mere cleverness. Together they form the disposition of the one who stands transparent before God, hiding nothing, reserving nothing.

### Grace Walking in Simplicity

One of Thomas's most profound observations on simplicity comes in his contrast between nature and grace. "[[nature|Nature]] is subtle, and draweth many to her net of deceit; but [[grace|Grace]] walketh in simplicity" (091-008). [[nature|Nature]] — fallen [[nature|human nature]] — is characterised by subtlety, indirection, and hidden motives. It knows how to disguise self-interest as virtue, how to appear generous while secretly grasping. [[grace|Grace]], by contrast, is straightforward: it says what it means, does what it professes, and seeks no hidden advantage.

This contrast explains why Thomas so consistently treats simplicity as a mark of spiritual maturity. The beginner is still tangled in the complexities of self-love; the saint has been simplified by grace. "Blessed is the simplicity which leaveth alone the difficult questions of disputings, and goeth forward in the plain and firm path of God's [[law|commandments]]" (114-011). Simplicity does not solve every intellectual problem; it bypasses them, content to walk the plain path of obedience rather than to map every byway of speculation.

### God's Companionship with the Simple

Thomas closes The Imitation with a [[blessedness|beatitude]] on simplicity that gathers all his teaching into a single promise: "God walketh with the simple, revealeth Himself to the humble, giveth understanding to the little ones, openeth the sense to pure minds, and hideth His grace from the curious and the proud" (114-034). This is the final word on the subject. Simplicity attracts divine companionship; complexity repels it. The God who is infinite simplicity — who is what He is without division, reserve, or shadow of turning — draws near to those who have become like Him. The simple soul is the soul in which God is at home.

## Biblical Foundation

Thomas's teaching on simplicity echoes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s words in the Sermon on the Mount: "The light of the body is the eye: if therefore thine eye be single, thy whole body shall be full of light" (Matthew 6:22). The "single eye" is the simple intention — the eye that looks at one thing only, undivided between God and mammon. St Paul's concern for the Corinthians — "I fear, lest by any means … your minds should be corrupted from the simplicity that is in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (2 Corinthians 11:3) — identifies simplicity as the distinctive mark of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian soul, the quality that most directly images [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself.

The apostle's exhortation that "he that giveth, let him do it with simplicity" (Romans 12:8) extends the virtue from the interior life to outward action. The early Church "did eat their meat with gladness and singleness of heart" (Acts 2:46), the same word that Thomas renders "simplicity." The servant is to obey "in singleness of your heart, as unto [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (Ephesians 6:5; Colossians 3:22). In each case, simplicity is the quality of action done wholly for God, without the complication of human respect or self-seeking. This is the simplicity that Thomas commends: not the absence of intelligence but the presence of undivided love.
