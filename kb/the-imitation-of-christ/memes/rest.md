# Rest

The settled peace of the soul that has ceased from its anxious striving and placed its whole trust in God — in The Imitation of Christ, rest is not the absence of labor but the fruit of [[humility]] and surrender, and the soul that seeks rest apart from union with Christ remains in perpetual agitation.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, rest denotes the interior condition of the soul that has found its center in God. It is not idleness, cessation of activity, or worldly comfort, but the deep stability of spirit that comes from placing one's trust entirely in divine providence. The restless soul is tossed by every circumstance because its anchor is in created things; the soul at rest is unmoved because its anchor is in God alone.

Thomas à Kempis contrasts two kinds of rest: the false rest of those who seek ease and avoidance of suffering, and the true rest of those who rest "wholly upon God" (012-010). The former is a flight from the cross; the latter is found precisely in bearing the cross with Christ.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Throughout The Imitation rest appears as both promise and imperative — the soul is summoned to rest in God, and only in God is rest to be found. The restless heart, ever seeking satisfaction in creatures, is the natural state of fallen humanity; rest is the accomplishment of [[grace]].

### Resting Wholly Upon God

Book I, Chapter XII presents rest as an act of radical trust: "Therefore ought a man to rest wholly upon God, so that he need not seek many consolations of men" (012-010). The multiplication of human consolations is a symptom of insufficient trust — the soul that rests fully on God no longer requires the constant reassurance of creatures. This rest is not passive but requires the active mortification of the desire to be comforted by others.

The same chapter warns that the soul must not "rest upon thyself" (012-012) — self-reliance is as disruptive of true rest as reliance on others. True rest requires the dispossession of every false security, whether it be one's own strength, the approval of others, or the consolations of the spiritual life itself.

### The Restlessness of the Unsurrendered Soul

Book I, Chapter XX diagnoses restlessness as a consequence of disordered desire: the person "who loveth not to rest at home" — that is, within the interior castle of the soul — "no man safely talketh abroad" (020-016). The soul that has not found its center in God flees outward, seeking in external things what can only be found within, and in this flight finds only exhaustion.

Book I, Chapter XI deepens the analysis: "Gladly would we have rest from all misery; but because through sin we have lost innocency, we have lost also the true blessedness" (022-049–050). The human longing for rest is genuine — it reflects the soul's memory of the paradise it has lost — but it cannot be satisfied by the removal of external trials. True rest requires the recovery of [[holiness|innocency]], which is the work of [[grace]] through the cross.

### The Rest Found in Christ's Passion

Book II, Chapter XII offers a striking directive: "rest thou in the passion of Christ" (026-038). The cross, which appears as the ultimate site of suffering, is paradoxically the place of deepest rest. The soul that enters into Christ's passion discovers that the one who shares His suffering also shares His victory. To rest in the passion is to cease from all efforts at self-justification and to accept that one's salvation has been accomplished by Another.

The alternative is made clear: "unless thou art closely united with Christ, thou shalt find no rest" (026-030). Rest is not a technique to be mastered but a union to be entered. The soul that is "closely united" with Christ rests in Him as a [[discipleship|disciple]] rests in the Master's word, as a child rests in the Father's house.

### Rest and the Contemplative Life

Book II, Chapter I presents rest as the fruit of the [[contemplation|contemplative]] disposition: the person "at rest in God" is the one who has achieved "steadfastness of spirit" (003-016). This rest is not the cessation of activity but the orientation of the whole person toward God, so that even labor becomes restful because it is done in God and for God. "Learn to despise external things and to give thyself to things within, and thou shalt see the kingdom of God come into thee" (026-004).

## Biblical Foundation

The scriptural foundation of rest is most perfectly expressed in Christ's invitation: "Come to Me, all you who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take My yoke upon you and learn from Me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls" (Matthew 11:28–29). Rest is found not in the absence of yoke but in taking Christ's yoke — the burden that paradoxically lightens all other burdens.

The author of Hebrews speaks of a "rest" that "remains for the people of God" (Hebrews 4:9), a Sabbath-rest entered by faith, not by works. The psalmist declares: "Truly my soul silently waits for God; from Him comes my salvation" (Psalm 62:1). Jeremiah calls the people back to "the old paths, where the good way is, and walk in it; then you will find rest for your souls" (Jeremiah 6:16). And the Apocalypse promises: "Blessed are the dead who die in the Lord... that they may rest from their labors" (Revelation 14:13).

## Practical Application

The practice of rest, as taught in The Imitation, is learned through the progressive surrender of every false security. The [[disciple]] first learns to rest "wholly upon God" rather than upon the consolations of others (012-010); then learns to rest in the midst of activity rather than fleeing outward to escape interior discomfort (020-016); and finally learns to rest in the passion of Christ, where all striving for self-justification ceases (026-038). This is not achieved in a day; it is the slow work of [[grace]] over a lifetime of [[discipleship]].
