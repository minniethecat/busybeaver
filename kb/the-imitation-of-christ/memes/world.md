# World

The **world** (*mundus*) is one of the central concepts in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]*. Thomas a Kempis presents it not merely as the physical creation but as the entire order of temporal values, human ambition, fleshly desire, and earthly attachments that draw the soul away from God. From the very first line of Book I, the work announces its theme as being about "the [[discipleship|imitation of Christ]], and of contempt of the world and all its [[vanity|vanities]]" (001-001).

## The World as Vanity

The Imitation's treatment of the world is grounded in the biblical declaration that "vanity of [[vanity|vanities]], all is vanity" (Ecclesiastes 1:2). Kempis echoes this directly: "[[vanity|Vanity]] of [[vanity|vanities]], all is vanity, save to love God, and Him only to serve" (001-021). This is not a rejection of creation itself but a reordering of priorities — all earthly pursuits are empty unless they are directed toward the love and ser[[sin|vice]] of God.

The text enumerates specific forms of worldly vanity: "It is vanity then to seek after, and to [[trust-in-god|trust]] in, the riches that shall perish... It is vanity, too, to covet honours... It is vanity to follow the desires of the flesh... It is vanity to desire a long life, and to have little care for a good life" [001-024-029]. Each of these represents a form of misplaced [[trust-in-god|trust]] — seeking permanence and satisfaction in what is inherently transient.

## Contempt of the World

The phrase "contempt of the world" (*[[contempt-of-the-world|contemptus mundi]]*) serves as the programmatic opening of the entire work (001-001) and recurs as a key ascetical theme. In Book I, Chapter 23, Kempis speaks of "a perfect contempt of the world" as something to be cultivated alongside fervent desire for spiritual progress (023-030). This contempt is not hatred of creation but a deliberate reevaluation — seeing worldly goods, honors, and pleasures from the perspective of eternity, where their true value is exposed as negligible.

The [[wisdom|highest wisdom]], Kempis declares, is "to cast the world behind us, and to reach forward to the heavenly kingdom" (001-022). This echoes the call of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] to deny oneself, take up the cross, and follow Him.

## Living in the World but Not of It

Kempis acknowledges the inescapable reality of life in the world: "So long as we live in the world, we cannot be without trouble and temptation" (013-002). The problem is not physical presence in the world but spiritual entanglement with it. The text counsels withdrawal not necessarily in body but in heart: "Leave vain things to vain men, and mind thou the things which concern thy salvation" (020-068).

The devout soul is called to "enter into thy chamber and shut out the tumults of the world, as it is written, Commune with your own heart in your own chamber and be still" (020-039). This interior retreat is the practical means of living the contempt of the world — cultivating silence and recollection so that "the devout soul goeth forward and learneth the hidden things of the Scriptures" (020-045).

### Worldly Consolation vs. Divine Consolation

A recurring contrast in the text is between the [[comfort|consolation]]s of the world and the [[comfort|consolation]]s of God. Kempis asks rhetorically: "O how good a conscience would that man keep, who never sought a joy that passeth away, who never became entangled with the world!" (020-033). Worldly joys are fleeting and leave the soul emptier than before: "all carnal joy begin pleasantly, but in the end it gnaweth away and destroyeth" (020-059).

In contrast, divine [[comfort|consolation]] comes through retirement, silence, and contemplation. "In silence and [[silence|quiet]] the devout soul goeth forward and learneth the hidden things of the Scriptures. There findeth she rivers of tears, wherein to wash and cleanse herself each night" [020-045-047].

### The Transience of Worldly Glory

Kempis emphasizes the brevity of all worldly [[discernment|distinction]]: "Oh how quickly passeth the [[glory|glory of the world]] away!" (003-053). The masters and teachers who once flourished are soon replaced by others who "perhaps never have one thought of them" (003-050). This reflection on mortality serves as a practical exhortation — since all earthly fame and honor are so fleeting, the wise person invests in what endures. The ultimate judgment will not ask "what we have read, but what we have done; not how well we have spoken, but how holily we have lived" [003-046-047].

## The Example of the Saints

Book I, Chapter 18 presents the holy fathers as exemplars of the proper stance toward the world. "They were strangers to the world, but unto God they were as kinsmen and familiar friends" (018-031). The saints appeared contemptible "in the world's eyes" but were precious in God's sight (018-033). Their complete detachment was the secret of their spiritual fruitfulness:

> They desired nothing from the world; they ate the bare necessaries of life; they were unwilling even to give to the body what was necessary... Though poor to the outer eye, within they were filled with grace and heavenly consolation. [018-025-029]

The chapter concludes by praising those "holy and perfect men, who fighting so bravely trod the world underfoot" (018-045) — an image of active, militant detachment, not passive withdrawal.

## Practicing Contempt of the World

The Kempian theology of the world is not a philosophy to be contemplated but a discipline to be practiced. The Imitation provides concrete counsel for translating contempt of the world into daily life, and this counsel shaped the spirituality of the *devotio moderna* and its heirs.

### Daily Examination of Attachments

The most practical tool for living the contempt of the world is the daily examination of conscience and attachments. Kempis exhorts the disciple to ask continually: "What doth it profit thee to be able to discourse deeply concerning the Trinity, if thou lack humility and displease the Trinity thereby?" (001-006). The question is diagnostic — it probes not what one knows but what one is attached to. The disciple is invited each evening to review the day's desires, ambitions, and anxieties, asking of each: Does this draw me toward God or away from Him? Is this attachment ordered to charity or to self?

This practice, developed systematically in the Ignatian *Examen*, finds its roots in the Kempian insistence that the highest knowledge is not speculative learning but self-knowledge before God. "A humble rustic that serveth God is better than a proud philosopher who pondereth the courses of the stars and neglecteth himself" [001-008, 001-009].

### The Discipline of Interior Retreat

Kempis repeatedly counsels what the later tradition would call "recollection" — the deliberate withdrawal of the heart from worldly noise. "Enter into thy chamber and shut out the tumults of the world" (020-039) is not an architectural command but a spiritual one. The "chamber" is the interior sanctuary where the soul meets God in silence, and the disciple is to practice entering it daily — in moments of prayer, in the quiet intervals of the day, in the stillness before sleep.

This discipline does not require physical removal from the world. The laity living amid family, trade, and civic obligation are called to the same interior poverty as the cloistered religious — not by abandoning their duties but by transfiguring them through purity of intention. "It is no small matter to dwell in the world without defilement and to live in the body without corruption" (034-017). The practice is to offer each task to God, to perform each duty as service rendered to Christ, and to refuse the soul's consent to the flattery, envy, and ambition that the world constantly proposes.

### The Joy of Detachment

The most counterintuitive teaching of The Imitation is that contempt of the world is not a counsel of grim renunciation but the path to joy. "The soul that loveth God despiseth all things that are beneath God" (026-026) — and this despising is experienced as liberation, not loss. The world's goods are burdens: they must be acquired, defended, and eventually surrendered. The one who has learned to hold them lightly is free.

Kempis describes the contemplative soul as one who "standeth above the present and looketh upon the eternal" — a perspective from which the anxieties of the world appear small and manageable. "All the gold that is under the sun cannot purchase one moment's peace for a troubled conscience" (022-006). The joy proper to detachment is the peace of an unburdened heart, the liberty of a soul that has transferred its treasure to heaven and therefore lives without fear of loss.
