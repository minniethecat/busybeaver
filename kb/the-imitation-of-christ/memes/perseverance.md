# Perseverance

The virtue by which the soul, upheld by [[grace|divine grace]], presses forward in the way of holiness through every obstacle — temptation, desolation, the weariness of long effort — and does not turn back, knowing that it is God who "gave them perseverance" and who alone "crowned their patience" (095-030 to 095-031), in the teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Perseverance, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not mere human stubbornness or natural tenacity but a supernatural gift by which the soul endures to the end. Thomas à Kempis distinguishes sharply between the self-willed determination that soon exhausts itself and the grace-given steadfastness that outlasts every [[temptation|trial]]. The soul that perseveres does so not because it is naturally strong but because it is supernaturally sustained. In [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own words as recorded in Book III: "I poured mighty [[comfort|consolation]]s upon them, I gave them perseverance, I crowned their patience" (095-030 to 095-031). The giving, the persevering, and the crowning are all God's work. The soul's part is to consent, to receive, and to continue — to "be [[patience|long-suffering]] and strong" (067-016) in the confidence that God who began the work will bring it to completion.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Perseverance appears in The Imitation not as an isolated topic treated in a single chapter but as a thread that runs through the whole fabric of the work — in the discourses on temptation, on patience, on [[comfort|consolation]] and its withdrawal, and above all in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own promises to those who faithfully endure.

### The Gift of Perseverance

The most concentrated teaching on perseverance comes in Book III, Chapter 43, where [[jesus-christ|Christ]] recounts how He dealt with His faithful ones: "I led them on through sundry temptations. I poured mighty [[comfort|consolation]]s upon them, I gave them perseverance, I crowned their patience" (095-029 to 095-031). The sequence is crucial: temptations come first, then [[comfort|consolation]]s to strengthen, then perseverance as the abiding gift, and finally the crown. Perseverance is the bridge between the fluctuating experiences of the spiritual life — the alternation of [[comfort|consolation]] and desolation — and the final reward. Without perseverance, [[comfort|consolation]]s are wasted and temptations are fatal; with it, both [[comfort|consolation]] and desolation serve the same end.

This passage also establishes that perseverance is divinely given, not humanly achieved. Thomas does not exhort the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "persevere harder" but to receive perseverance as a gift from the hand of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. This is entirely consistent with the broader Kempian theology: every good, including the good of enduring in goodness, comes from God. The soul that recognises its own poverty and dependence is precisely the soul that will persevere, for it leans not on its own strength but on the unshakeable [[faith|faithfulness]] of God.

### Perseverance in Temptation

The [[temptation|testing]] ground of perseverance is temptation. The Imitation teaches that "he who only resisteth outwardly and pulleth not up by the root, shall profit little; nay, rather temptations will return to him the more quickly, and will be the more terrible" (013-024). Superficial resistance — the attempt to overcome temptation by willpower alone — is not perseverance but its counterfeit. True perseverance works from the interior, striking at the root of disordered desire, and it works "little by little, through patience and [[patience|long-suffering]]... by the help of God" (013-026). The phrase "little by little" captures the essential spirit of Kempian perseverance: it is not a single heroic act but a patient, daily, almost imperceptible advance. The soul that expects to conquer all at once will soon be disillusioned; the soul that accepts the slow pace of grace will, in time, find that it has gone far.

Flight from temptation is sometimes necessary, but flight that avoids the interior work is useless. Perseverance means staying in the fight — not by never retreating but by never abandoning the field. "To him that overcometh is the hidden manna given, but great misery is reserved for the slothful" (072-011). The contrast between the overcomer and the slothful is the contrast between the soul that perseveres and the soul that, weary of the struggle, simply lets go.

### Perseverance and the Withdrawal of Consolation

The severest test of perseverance is not the presence of temptation but the absence of [[comfort|consolation]]. When God withdraws the sweetness of His presence — and He does withdraw it, as a pedagogy of humility and gratitude — the soul is tempted to conclude that all is lost and to abandon the way. Thomas addresses this with pastoral realism in Chapter 35: "He who seeketh to retain the favour of God, let him be thankful for the favour which is given, and patient in respect of that which is taken away. Let him pray that it may return; let him be wary and humble that he lose it not" (035-051 to 035-053). Perseverance in desolation means continuing to pray, continuing to obey, continuing to thank — doing everything one did when [[comfort|consolation]] was present, but now without the felt sweetness, and therefore with a purer love.

The divine command "Be [[patience|long-suffering]] and strong" (067-016) is given precisely for these seasons. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] speaks it in the context of the soul's complaint that deliverance is delayed. "Wait thou upon Me until the time of reaping; for I will perfect thee in its due season" (067-016 to 067-017). Perseverance is the virtue of the in-between time — the time after the promise and before the fulfilment, when all visible evidence seems to contradict the promise and only [[trust-in-god|trust]] keeps the soul moving forward.

### The Crown of Perseverance

The end of perseverance is glory. Thomas is emphatic that perseverance is not its own reward — it looks beyond itself to the crown that [[jesus-christ|Christ]] gives. "I gave them perseverance, I crowned their patience" (095-030 to 095-031). The perseverance and the patience are distinct moments in a single divine work: God gives the grace to endure, and then He rewards the [[patience|endurance]] as though it were the soul's own achievement. This is the mystery of grace and merit working together — all is gift, and yet the gift is truly crowned.

The faithful who persevered are described by [[jesus-christ|Christ]] with extraordinary tenderness: "I acknowledge the first and the last; I embrace all with inestimable love" (095-032). The embrace at the end is the point toward which all perseverance tends. The soul that holds fast through every [[temptation|trial]] is not earning love that was absent but moving toward the full reception of a love that was there from the beginning.

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of perseverance in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is grounded in the New Testament's teaching on patient endurance. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own words supply the ultimate warrant: "He that shall endure unto the end, the same shall be saved" (Matthew 24:13). The Epistle to the Hebrews frames the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life as a race to be run "with patience" (Hebrews 12:1), looking to [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] who endured the cross. James 1:12 pronounces the [[blessedness|beatitude]] upon the man who "endureth temptation: for when he is tried, he shall receive the crown of life." Thomas's conjunction of perseverance and crowning draws directly from this Jacobean promise.

The teaching that perseverance is a divine gift rather than a human achievement reflects the Pauline understanding: "He which hath begun a good work in you will perform it until the day of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]" (Philippians 1:6). Romans 5:3–4 traces the chain from [[suffering|tribulation]] to patience to experience to hope — the same chain Thomas describes in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s leading of the faithful through temptations to perseverance to the crown. The final word belongs to 2 Timothy 4:7: "I have fought a good fight, I have finished my course, I have kept the faith." This is perseverance's epitaph and its triumph.

## Related Concepts

- [[patience]] — The companion virtue of perseverance; where patience bears the present [[temptation|trial]], perseverance endures through time to the end
- [[grace]] — Perseverance is itself a gift of grace, not a human achievement; God "gave them perseverance"
- [[temptation]] — The arena in which perseverance is exercised and proved; without temptation there is no occasion for perseverance
- [[hope]] — Perseverance is hope sustained over time; it looks forward to the crown that is promised
- [[faith]] — [[faith|Faith]] provides the vision without which perseverance has no direction; it is faith that sees the invisible goal
- [[consolation]] — The sweetness that God gives to strengthen perseverance and the withdrawal that tests its purity
- [[humility]] — The humble soul perseveres because it knows its own weakness and leans wholly on God
- [[suffering]] — The material of perseverance; suffering endured in grace becomes the stuff of victory
- [[love]] — The motive that makes perseverance more than grim [[patience|endurance]]; the soul that loves God perseveres not out of duty but out of desire for union
