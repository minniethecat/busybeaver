# Worship

Worship is the central act of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]——the soul's turning toward God in adoration, whether in liturgical assembly, private prayer, or the silent offering of the heart. It is the creature's fitting response to the Creator and a participation, even now, in the worship of heaven.

## Definition

Worship (from Old English *weorthscipe*, "worth-ship") is the ascription of supreme worth to God through acts of adoration, praise, [[gratitude|thanksgiving]], and self-offering. The Imitation uses the language of worship primarily in the context of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] and in echoes of the heavenly liturgy. Worship is both an external act (kneeling, praying, receiving the sacrament) and an interior disposition (humility, love, reverence). The two must correspond: outward worship without inward devotion is empty; inward devotion naturally seeks outward expression.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation's most direct reference to worship appears in a eucharistic passage describing the heavenly court: the blessed "fell on their faces before the Lamb, and worshipped Him that liveth forever and ever" (095-081). This image——drawn from the Book of Revelation——sets the standard for earthly worship: it is prostrate, adoring, and directed toward [[jesus-christ|Christ]] the Lamb.

The entire fourth book of The Imitation is a sustained catechesis on worthy reception of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], which Thomas à Kempis treats as the supreme act of worship this side of heaven. The communicant is instructed to approach with "reverence and devout affection" (095-080), to prepare the soul through [[conscience|examination of conscience]], and to receive [[jesus-christ|Christ]] not merely in symbol but in truth. The repeated emphasis on humility——"what shall I do, O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], that I may worthily receive Thee?"——underscores that worship is not human achievement but divine condescension met by human surrender.

Beyond the eucharistic context, The Imitation presents the whole of the devout life as worship. The counsel to "seek a convenient time for thy leisure, and think often of the benefits of God" (020-001, adapted) frames contemplation as an act of adoration. The repeated call to prefer God above all [[creation|creatures]] and to "love Him and serve Him alone" (001-001) makes worship the organizing principle of existence: not one activity among many but the activity toward which all others are ordered.

## Biblical Foundation

Worship in The Imitation draws from the full biblical tradition. The Old Testament establishes the pattern of sacrificial worship (Leviticus), the Psalms provide the language of adoration ("O come, let us worship and bow down," Psalm 95:6), and the prophets insist on the correspondence of outward rite and inward [[justice|righteousness]] ([[isaiah|Isaiah]] 1:11–17, Hosea 6:6).

The New Testament transforms and fulfills this tradition. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s conversation with the Samaritan woman——"the true worshippers shall worship the Father in spirit and in truth" (John 4:23)——is the charter of interior worship. The Book of Revelation provides the heavenly model that The Imitation echoes directly: the four living [[creation|creatures]] and twenty-four elders fall down before the Lamb (Revelation 4:10–11, 5:8–9), singing "worthy is the Lamb that was slain." Paul's admonition to "present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy, acceptable unto God, which is your reasonable ser[[sin|vice]]" (Romans 12:1, where "ser[[sin|vice]]" translates *latreia*, worship) unites worship with the whole of life.

## Practical Application

The Imitation teaches that worship is not confined to the church or the hour of Mass. Every act of self-denial is an act of worship, offering the will to God. Every [[perseverance|patient endurance]] of [[temptation|trial]] is worship, [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing [[providence|God's providence]]. Every silent turning of the heart toward God in the midst of daily labor is worship. The eucharistic reception is the summit, but the ascent toward it——through [[conscience|examination of conscience]], acts of humility, and the daily discipline of prayer——is itself a life of worship. The heavenly worship described in Revelation and echoed in The Imitation is both the model and the goal: what the soul practices imperfectly on earth, it will fulfill perfectly in eternity.
