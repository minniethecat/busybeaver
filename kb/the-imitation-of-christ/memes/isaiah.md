# Isaiah

The eighth-century BC Hebrew prophet of Judah — called the "Evangelical Prophet" for the richness and clarity of his messianic visions — whose oracles appear throughout The Imitation of Christ as authoritative testimony to human mortality, divine majesty, and the coming consolation of God's people.

## Identity

Isaiah ben Amoz prophesied in Judah during the eighth century BC, spanning the reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah (Isaiah 1:1). His ministry unfolded against the backdrop of the Assyrian crisis — the fall of the northern kingdom of Israel (722 BC) and the near-destruction of Jerusalem under Sennacherib (701 BC). Tradition holds that he suffered martyrdom under King Manasseh, sawn in two (cf. Hebrews 11:37). His book, the longest of the prophetic corpus, contains some of the most sublime passages in all of Scripture: the vision of God in the Temple (Isaiah 6), the Immanuel prophecies (Isaiah 7–12), the oracles against the nations (Isaiah 13–23), the "Little Apocalypse" (Isaiah 24–27), and — most significantly for Christian devotion — the four Servant Songs (Isaiah 42, 49, 50, 52–53), which the Church has always understood as prophecies of Christ's passion and glorification.

In the Christian tradition, Isaiah has been called the "Fifth Evangelist" because his prophecies contain such detailed anticipations of the life, suffering, and victory of Jesus Christ. The Vulgate's rendering of Isaiah 7:14 — "Behold, a virgin shall conceive, and bear a son" — is read at Advent and Christmas as the announcement of the Incarnation. Isaiah 53, the great song of the Suffering Servant, provides the Church's most profound meditation on the atoning death of Christ. It is this Isaiah — the prophet of both divine transcendence and redemptive suffering — whom Thomas à Kempis invokes throughout The Imitation of Christ.

## Biblical Narrative

Isaiah's call narrative (Isaiah 6) is among the most dramatic in Scripture: in the year of King Uzziah's death, the prophet sees the Lord seated on a throne, high and lifted up, attended by seraphim who cry "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts." Overwhelmed by his own uncleanness, Isaiah's lips are touched with a burning coal from the altar, and he responds to the divine summons — "Whom shall I send?" — with the words "Here am I; send me."

His public ministry spanned decades of political turmoil. He confronted King Ahaz during the Syro-Ephraimite war, delivering the Immanuel sign (Isaiah 7). He counseled King Hezekiah during Sennacherib's siege of Jerusalem, prophesying the Assyrian withdrawal (Isaiah 36–37). He denounced the social injustice, empty ritualism, and idolatry of his contemporaries while holding forth the vision of a purified Jerusalem to which all nations would stream (Isaiah 2:1–4). His later chapters (Isaiah 40–66) shift in tone from judgment to consolation, proclaiming the return from exile, the coming of the Servant of the Lord, and the creation of new heavens and a new earth.

### Key Events

- **The Temple Vision (Isaiah 6)** — Isaiah's inaugural call, in which he beholds the holiness of God and receives his prophetic commission. This vision establishes the note of divine transcendence that pervades all his oracles.
- **The Immanuel Prophecy (Isaiah 7:14)** — The sign given to King Ahaz: "Behold, a virgin shall conceive, and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel." The Church reads this as the direct prophecy of Christ's virginal conception.
- **The Suffering Servant (Isaiah 52:13–53:12)** — The fourth and greatest of the Servant Songs: the portrait of one who "hath borne our griefs and carried our sorrows," "wounded for our transgressions," and "brought as a lamb to the slaughter." This is the Old Testament text most frequently applied to Christ's passion in the New Testament and in subsequent Christian devotion.
- **The Consolation of Israel (Isaiah 40)** — The opening of the book of consolation: "Comfort ye, comfort ye My people, saith your God." The voice crying in the wilderness prepares the way of the Lord — a passage applied by all four Evangelists to John the Baptist.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis cites Isaiah more frequently than any other Old Testament prophet. The references, concentrated in Book III (the longest book, containing the dialogue between Christ and the disciple), serve a consistent theological purpose: Isaiah is the prophetic voice that reminds the disciple of human mortality, divine sovereignty, and the consolation that awaits those who trust in God.

The citations fall into several thematic clusters. **Human frailty** — "All flesh is grass, and all the goodliness thereof is as the flower of the field" (Isaiah 40:6, cited at 032-029) — underpins Thomas's teaching on contempt of the world and the transience of created things. If all flesh is grass, then "they would care nothing for the kingdom of God" who fix their hearts on what is passing (022-030). **Divine sovereignty** — "Shall the clay say to him that fashioned it, What makest thou?" (Isaiah 29:16, cited at 051-035) — is quoted to reinforce the virtue of humble submission to God's providence. The creature has no standing to question the Creator. **Divine guidance** — "I will go before thee, and make the crooked places straight" (Isaiah 45:2, cited at 060-053) — assures the disciple that God does not merely command the journey but actively prepares the way.

The most striking deployment of Isaiah comes in the context of eschatological hope. "Thou shalt see, and flow together, and thine heart shall fear and be enlarged" (Isaiah 60:5, cited at 111-043) is invoked in Book IV, where the soul approaching the Eucharist is invited to contemplate the abundance of grace that flows from the Sacrament. Isaiah's vision of the nations streaming to Zion becomes a figure of the soul drawn to the altar, where Christ — the true Zion — dispenses the riches of His grace.

## Spiritual Significance

Isaiah's significance for the Kempian spiritual program lies in his unique combination of themes: divine transcendence and human frailty, judgment and consolation, suffering and glory. Thomas finds in Isaiah the perfect prophetic voice to reinforce his central teaching — that the soul must be emptied of pride, detached from creatures, and wholly submitted to God. Isaiah's vision of the "Holy, holy, holy" Lord grounds the virtue of humility: before such holiness, every human pretense collapses. Isaiah's insistence that "all flesh is grass" fuels the contempt of the world: if everything visible is passing, only the invisible, eternal God merits the soul's ultimate attachment. And Isaiah's vision of the Suffering Servant — though never quoted directly in the Servant Song passages — provides the implicit Old Testament background for Thomas's theology of the Cross. The Christ who says "I have received the cross at Thy hand; I have carried it, and will carry it even unto death" (093-036) is the Christ prefigured in Isaiah's Servant, who "opened not His mouth" and was "numbered with the transgressors."

The prophet who saw the Lord high and lifted up, and who confessed "Woe is me! for I am undone; because I am a man of unclean lips" (Isaiah 6:5), models the posture of the disciple before the divine majesty — a posture of reverent fear, honest self-knowledge, and grateful acceptance of purification. Isaiah's burning coal, touching unclean lips and making them clean, prefigures the Eucharist in which Christ — "the Word of God who is the light of the soul" (107-050) — touches and purifies the soul that receives Him.
