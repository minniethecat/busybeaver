# Jeremiah

Jeremiah, the prophet called from his mother's womb to speak God's word to a rebellious nation — known as the "weeping prophet" for his laments over Jerusalem's destruction. In The Imitation of Christ, his prophetic word is invoked to affirm the soul's absolute dependence on God.

## Identity

Jeremiah prophesied in Judah during its final decades before the Babylonian exile (c. 627–587 BC). Called by God in his youth — "Before I formed thee in the belly I knew thee" (Jeremiah 1:5) — he preached repentance to a people who refused to hear, witnessed the fall of Jerusalem, and was carried into Egypt against his will. His book combines oracles of judgment with the promise of a new covenant: "I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts" (Jeremiah 31:33). In Christian tradition, Jeremiah is a type of Christ in his suffering and rejection, and his prophecy of the new covenant is read as pointing to the Eucharist and the gift of the Holy Spirit.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Jeremiah is cited in Book I, Chapter 19 ("Of the Exercises of a Good Religious"), where Thomas à Kempis quotes Jeremiah 10:23 as a scriptural anchor for the doctrine of total dependence on God: "(1) Jeremiah x. 23" (019-066). The verse declares: "O Lord, I know that the way of man is not in himself: it is not in man that walketh to direct his steps." The citation appears at the close of a chapter that has urged the religious to exercise himself in all virtues and to "renew himself day by day in fervor" (019-004–006), reminding the reader that even this renewal is not his own achievement but the work of grace.

The reference is brief but theologically precise. Thomas does not invoke the weeping prophet's biography or his laments; he invokes the single sentence of Jeremiah that most perfectly expresses the Kempian anthropology: man cannot direct his own steps. The chapter's exhortation to virtue would be Pelagian arrogance without Jeremiah's corrective — all growth in holiness is gift.

## In the Christian Tradition

Jeremiah's life and writings have left a deep impress on Christian spirituality. The Church Fathers read his sufferings as a figura Christi: the prophet rejected by his own people, cast into a miry dungeon (Jeremiah 38:6), and lamenting over Jerusalem's destruction prefigures Christ weeping over Jerusalem (Luke 19:41) and descending into the darkness of death. St. Jerome, who wrote a lengthy commentary on Jeremiah, identified deeply with the prophet's combination of reluctant vocation and burning compulsion to speak — "His word was in mine heart as a burning fire shut up in my bones" (Jeremiah 20:9) — and saw in Jeremiah the pattern of every Christian who must speak God's truth at personal cost.

The prophecy of the new covenant (Jeremiah 31:31–34) has been among the most influential Old Testament texts in Christian theology. The author of Hebrews quotes it at length (Hebrews 8:8–12) as the charter of Christ's priesthood, and the promise that God will "put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts" (Jeremiah 31:33) became a cornerstone of Augustinian theology of grace — the interiority of the new law, written not on stone tablets but on the heart by the Holy Spirit. This theme resonates profoundly with The Imitation's insistence that the disciple must receive God's word inwardly, not merely outwardly: "Blessed are the ears which receive the echoes of the divine whisper, and which listen not to the whisperings of the world" (001-003).

In the medieval devotional tradition, Jeremiah was also a patron of compunction — the gift of tears for one's sins. The Lamentations of Jeremiah, read during Holy Week in the Tenebrae liturgy, gave voice to the Church's grief over Christ's passion and over her own infidelity. The weeping prophet became, for writers of the *Devotio Moderna*, an image of the penitent soul mourning its distance from God — a theme that pervades The Imitation's teaching on [[contrition]] and [[compunction]].

For the reader of The Imitation, Jeremiah's legacy is twofold. His declaration that "the way of man is not in himself" guards the spiritual life from self-reliance. And his tears over Jerusalem model the holy sorrow that accompanies true repentance — a sorrow that Thomas à Kempis consistently prefers to worldly consolation.

## Spiritual Significance

Jeremiah's role in The Imitation, though limited to a single scriptural citation, is essential. His declaration — "the way of man is not in himself" — guards the whole project of spiritual self-discipline from the illusion of self-sufficiency. The disciple who strives to amend his life must do so in the constant recognition that it is God who "worketh in you both to will and to do of his good pleasure" (Philippians 2:13). Jeremiah's voice, brief as it is, is the voice that prevents The Imitation from becoming a manual of works-righteousness.
