# Gratitude

The disposition of the soul that acknowledges every good thing as the gift of God and returns thanks to the Giver without ceasing — not only for [[comfort|consolation]]s and benefits but even for stripes and withdrawals, knowing that all that God permits works for the profit of the soul — in the teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Gratitude, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not a mere polite sentiment or occasional expression of thanks but a fundamental orientation of the soul toward God as the sole Fountain of every good. Thomas à Kempis devotes an entire chapter — Book II, Chapter 10, "Of Gratitude for the [[grace|Grace]] of God" — to this virtue, a signal of its centrality in the interior life. Gratitude is defined not primarily by what the soul feels but by what it does with what it receives: it "returneth them wholly to the Fountain whence they flow" (035-017). The grateful soul ascribes nothing to itself but all to God; the ungrateful soul, by contrast, claims for itself what belongs to God and thereby stops the flow of grace. "The gifts of grace are not able to flow unto us, because we are ungrateful to the Author of them" (035-016). Gratitude is thus not an optional ornament of the spiritual life but its very circulatory system — the channel through which grace continually passes from God to the soul and back again in the form of praise.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats gratitude as a matter of spiritual life and death. The entire economy of grace — its giving, receiving, withholding, and restoring — turns upon the soul's gratitude or want of it. Book II, Chapter 10 is the fullest treatment, but the theme runs through the whole work, culminating in Book IV where the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] itself is presented as the supreme act of thanksgiving.

### Gratitude as the Condition of Grace

The central teaching of Chapter 35 is that gratitude is not merely the proper response to grace but its very condition. Thomas writes with startling directness: "the gifts of grace are not able to flow unto us, because we are ungrateful to the Author of them, and return them not wholly to the Fountain whence they flow" (035-016). The image is hydraulic: grace flows from God the Fountain toward the soul, but when the soul blocks the return current of thanksgiving, the inflow itself is obstructed. "[[grace|Grace]] ever becometh the portion of him who is grateful and that is taken away from the proud, which is wont to be given to the humble" (035-018 to 035-019).

God Himself "doeth well in giving us the grace of comfort, but man doeth ill in not immediately giving God thanks thereof" (035-014 to 035-015). The speed of thanksgiving matters — "immediately" — because a delay in gratitude is a form of claiming the gift as one's own before reluctantly acknowledging the Giver. This is the root of the whole problem: ingratitude is pride in disguise, a practical atheism that receives as though one deserved.

### Giving God What Is God's, in All Things

Thomas articulates a precise rule of spiritual accounting: "Give unto God the thing which is God's, and ascribe to thyself that which is thine; that is, give thanks unto God for His grace, but for thyself alone confess thy [[sin|fault]], and that thy punishment is deserved for thy [[sin|fault]]" (035-028 to 035-031). This is an echo of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s words about rendering to Caesar what is Caesar's and to God what is God's — applied now to the interior life. To God belongs all the good one has received; to oneself belongs only one's [[sin|sins]] and the punishment they merit. Gratitude, in this scheme, is simply justice — rendering to each what is properly due. The grateful soul is the just soul; the ungrateful soul is a thief of God's glory.

This accounting produces humility as its natural fruit. "He who is made learned by the gift of grace and taught wisdom by the stroke of the withdrawal thereof, will not dare to claim any good thing for himself, but will rather confess that he is poor and needy" (035-025 to 035-028). Thomas insists that the measure of gratitude must be the majesty of the Giver, not the apparent magnitude of the gift: "Be thankful, therefore, for the least benefit and thou shalt be worthy to receive greater. Let the least be unto thee even as the greatest, and let that which is of little account be unto thee as a great gift" (035-043 to 035-045). If the majesty of the Giver be considered, "nothing that is given shall seem small and of no worth, for that is not a small thing which is given by the Most High God" (035-046 to 035-048).

Even punishment and stripes fall under this rule. Thomas makes the claim that "though He gave punishment and stripes, we ought to be thankful, because He ever doth for our profit whatever He suffereth to come upon us" (035-049 to 035-050). Gratitude in [[suffering|adversity]] is not stoic resignation but a lively faith that God's fatherly hand is present even — and especially — in what hurts. The grateful soul "prayeth that it may return; let him be wary and humble that he lose it not" (035-051 to 035-053). This is the highest pitch of the grateful life: to thank God not only for the gift but for its withdrawal, not only for [[comfort|consolation]] but for the cross.

### Christ as the Model

In a striking passage from Book III, [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself speaks of His own experience of ingratitude: "I received ingratitude for benefits, blasphemies for My miracles, rebukes for My doctrine" (055-009 to 055-010). The sinless One who gave every good thing received back only contempt from those He came to save. This is held up not to accuse [[world|the world]] but to console the [[discipleship|disciple]] who suffers the same treatment — if the Master was repaid with ingratitude, the servant should not expect better. And yet [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own response — "I gently bore contradictions and hard words" (055-008) — is the model: He did not cease to give because His gifts were scorned, nor did He retaliate.

### The Eucharist as Supreme Thanksgiving

The theme reaches its culmination in Book IV, where the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] is described in the language of gratitude: "reverence, praise, and honour, with such gratitude, worthiness, and love, with such faith, hope, and purity do I desire to receive Thee" (113-019). The very word [[eucharist|Eucharist]] means thanksgiving, and Thomas's treatment of the Sacrament is saturated with the vocabulary of grateful reception. The communicant approaches the altar not as one who deserves but as one who has received all and can return only thanks. The [[eucharist|Eucharist]] is thus gratitude made sacrament — the grateful soul's definitive act of re[[conversion|turning to God]] the gift of God Himself.

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of gratitude in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] rests upon a broad scriptural foundation. The command to "give thanks in all circumstances" (1 Thessalonians 5:18) is the most direct warrant for Thomas's insistence that even stripes and withdrawals merit thanksgiving. The Psalmist's refrain "O give thanks unto the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], for He is good" (Psalm 107:1) grounds gratitude in the character of the Giver rather than the pleasantness of the gift. The healing of the ten lepers (Luke 17:11–19), where only one returns to give thanks, illustrates the same dynamic Thomas describes: grace is given to all, but the grateful one alone receives the fuller blessing — "thy faith hath made thee whole."

Thomas's rule of spiritual accounting — give God what is God's, keep for yourself only your [[sin|fault]] — is a direct application of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s words: "Render therefore unto Caesar the things which are Caesar's, and unto God the things that are God's" (Matthew 22:21). James 1:17 provides the theological premise: "Every good gift and every perfect gift is from above, and cometh down from the Father of lights." If every good gift is from God, then to claim any good as one's own is a false ascription. The grateful soul is simply the truthful soul, acknowledging the real source of every benefit.
