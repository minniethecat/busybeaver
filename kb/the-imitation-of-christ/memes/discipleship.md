# Discipleship

The very title of Thomas à Kempis's work — *De Imitatione [[jesus-christ|Christ]]i* — announces that discipleship is its central concern. The book is not a treatise on theology but a manual for following Christ. From the opening sentence, "He that followeth me shall not walk in darkness" (001-003, quoting John 8:12), to the final pages, the entire spiritual program of The Imitation can be understood as an extended meditation on what it means to be a disciple. Discipleship is not one theme among many but the organizing framework within which every other concept — humility, obedience, self-denial, love — finds its proper place.

## Definition

Discipleship in The Imitation is defined by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own words as Thomas receives and applies them: "If thou wilt be My disciple, deny thyself" (093-019). This is not offered as one spiritual path among others but as the *only* path. The disciple is one who hears the summons "Follow Me" (037-003) and responds with the totality of life — renouncing self-will, taking up the cross, and conforming every affection and action to the pattern of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Thomas does not distinguish between "believing in" [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and "following" [[jesus-christ|Christ]]; the two are inseparable, and any profession of faith unaccompanied by the practice of self-denial is, in his implicit judgment, no discipleship at all. "The servant is not greater than his lord, nor the disciple above his master" (093-026, echoing Matthew 10:24) — this is the governing principle that makes discipleship a project of downward mobility, of progressive diminishment before God.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Discipleship is treated across all four books of The Imitation, each contributing a distinct dimension: Book I establishes the call and the cost, Book II describes the interior dispositions of the disciple, Book III unfolds the disciple's intimate dialogue with [[jesus-christ|Christ]], and Book IV culminates in the sacramental union that is discipleship's highest earthly form.

### The Call and the Cost

The summons to discipleship is presented as universal and uncompromising. Thomas opens Book I, Chapter 1 with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s promise — "He that followeth me shall not walk in darkness" — and immediately makes clear that following means imitating: "Let it be our chief study to meditate upon the life of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]" and "imitate His life and character" (001-004 to 001-005). In Book II, Chapter 37 ("Of the Pure and Entire [[resignation|Resignation]] of Self"), the cost is made explicit through [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s threefold command: "deny himself and take up his Cross and follow Me" (037-003). Thomas does not soften this demand or suggest it applies only to the specially called. "For both the disciples who followed Him, and all who desire to follow [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (037-136) — the circle is universal. Whoever desires to follow must also deny and carry.

### The Interior Disposition

Book III contains the most intimate treatment of discipleship. In Chapter 93 ("That We Ought to Deny Ourselves, and to Imitate [[jesus-christ|Christ]] by Means of the Cross"), [[jesus-christ|Christ]] speaks directly to the disciple: "If thou wilt be My disciple, deny thyself" (093-019). The chapter is structured as a dialogue in which the soul repeatedly resists and [[jesus-christ|Christ]] repeatedly returns to the same demand. Discipleship is presented not as a single decisive act but as an ongoing struggle against the self's persistent reassertion. The disciple's prayer — "Make me a pious and lowly disciple, as Thou hast been" (087-052) — acknowledges that discipleship is itself a grace, not an achievement of willpower.

### The Sacramental Culmination

In Book IV, discipleship reaches its earthly summit in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]]. "Thou therefore, if thou wilt be My disciple, offer thyself unto Me" (104-025) — the shift from "deny thyself" in Book III to "offer thyself" in Book IV marks a progression. Self-denial is the necessary preparation for self-offering; the cross is the way to the altar. Discipleship is not merely renunciation but participation — a real sharing in the life of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] through the sacrament of His body and blood.

### Key Passages

> "If thou wilt be My disciple, deny thyself. If thou wilt possess all things, give up all things." (093-019)

> "The servant is not greater than his lord, nor the disciple above his master." (093-026)

## Biblical Foundation

Discipleship in The Imitation is grounded in the Gospels' call narratives. The foundational text is [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s summons: "If any man will come after Me, let him deny himself, and take up his cross, and follow Me" (Matthew 16:24; cf. Luke 9:23), which Thomas quotes both at the beginning (037-003) and end (037-138 to 037-139) of his treatment of self-resignation. The opening verse of the entire work, "He that followeth me shall not walk in darkness" (John 8:12), establishes discipleship as the book's framing promise. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s self-identification as "the way, the truth, and the life" (John 14:6) is quoted directly: "Follow Me: I am the way, the truth, and the life" (093-007). The servant-master maxim (Matthew 10:24) provides the theological logic: the disciple cannot expect to be treated better than the Master who was crucified. Thomas also draws on the Pauline theme of imitation — "Be ye followers of me, even as I also am of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (1 Corinthians 11:1) — though he focuses more on the direct imitation of Christ than on apostolic mediation.

## Practical Application

Thomas's practical counsel on discipleship is brutally concrete. First, do not ask whether the cost can be reduced — it cannot. The cross is non-negotiable. Second, begin each day with the conscious resolve to "deny thyself" in small things, since "he who hath not learned to deny himself in small matters will not be able to do so in great ones" (a principle implicit throughout Book I). Third, understand discipleship as progressive: the disciple grows not by dramatic renunciations but by the daily, often tedious, practice of preferring God's will to one's own. Fourth, receive the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] as the supreme act of discipleship, in which the soul "offers itself" in union with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s self-offering. Discipleship is not a path that ends in this life — it is the path itself, and to walk it is already to participate in the life to which it leads.
