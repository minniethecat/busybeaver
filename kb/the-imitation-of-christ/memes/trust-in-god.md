# Trust in God

**Trust in God** (*fiducia in Deum*) is a fundamental disposition of the spiritual life in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]*. It is the soul's wholehearted reliance upon [[providence|divine providence]], goodness, and promises — the antidote to anxiety, self-reliance, and the restless search for created [[comfort|consolation]]s. Thomas à Kempis presents trust not as a passive sentiment but as an active surrender of the will, cultivated through prayer, humility, and the experience of divine [[faith|faithfulness]].

## Trust Versus Anxiety

The text directly contrasts [[faith|trust in God]] with the anxious state of soul that seeks security in [[creation|created things]]. Kempis diagnoses the root of anxiety as a failure of trust:

> Many are not fully trust in God, therefore they think that they must look to themselves and provide for their own affairs [074-017]

This passage identifies self-reliance as the practical consequence of deficient trust. Those who do not truly believe that God will care for them inevitably grasp at control, exhausting themselves in the management of circumstances they cannot ultimately command.

The image of the ship without a rudder captures the condition of the soul that lacks trust: the text speaks of "want of [[faith|trust in God]]; for even as a ship without a rudder is tossed about by the waves, so a man who is negligent and forsaketh his purpose, is tempted in many things" (013-032). Without trust as the guiding principle, the soul drifts into indecision, temptation, and spiritual instability.

## The Call to Wholehearted Trust

Kempis's teaching on trust is unconditional and total. There is no reserve, no hedging, no partial commitment permitted:

> Put thy whole trust in God and let Him be thy fear and thy love [026-026]

The text draws a direct connection between trust and spiritual rest: "He answereth for thee, and will do what is best for thee, according to thy circumstances." The soul that genuinely trusts does not need to manage outcomes — God assumes responsibility for the one who has entrusted everything to Him.

This trust extends even to the interior life. The soul must trust God not only with external circumstances but with its own spiritual progress: "Rest in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], and He will give thee the desires of thine heart." The paradox is that by giving up the anxious striving for holiness, the soul actually advances: "Commit thy way unto the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], and trust in Him, and He will bring it to pass."

## Trust and the Cross

The supreme test of trust is suffering. Kempis does not promise that [[faith|trust in God]] eliminates [[temptation|trial]]s — rather, trust is what enables the soul to endure them without losing peace. The text's treatment of [[suffering|adversity]] consistently returns to the theme of trusting [[wisdom|divine wisdom]] over human understanding.

The soul that trusts God in [[suffering|affliction]] is compared to gold refined in the furnace: what appears destructive is in fact purifying. The cross, embraced in trust, becomes the instrument of transformation rather than the occasion of despair.

## Biblical Foundation

The teaching on [[faith|trust in God]] draws deeply from the Psalms and the [[wisdom|Wisdom]] literature. The psalmist's repeated exhortations to "trust in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Psalm 37:3-5) and the proverb's command to "trust in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] with all thine heart, and lean not unto thine own understanding" (Proverbs 3:5) form the scriptural substructure of Kempis's treatment. The Gospel injunction against anxious care — "Behold the fowls of the air... your heavenly Father feedeth them" (Matthew 6:26) — confirms that trust is not optional but is the very disposition of the child of God.

## Trust and Divine Mercy

Beyond the confident reliance on providence, Kempis presents trust as the soul's response to the specific promise of divine mercy. The act of trusting does not presume upon one's own worthiness but rests entirely on God's goodness: "Trusting in Thy goodness and great mercy, O Lord, I draw near, the sick to the Healer, the hungry and thirsty to the Fountain of life" (098-003). Trust is not the fruit of spiritual achievement but the posture of acknowledged need.

This trust in mercy extends even to seasons of spiritual desolation. The voice of Christ counsels: "trust in My mercy. When thou thinkest thyself far removed from Me, I am often nearer to thee" (067-029). The experience of God's absence is not evidence of His actual distance — it is a trial permitted for the soul's purification. The one who trusts "even when God hideth His face" (042-063) discovers that trust itself is a grace given precisely for the darkness.

The deepest expression of trust in the text is the comprehensive abandonment of every alternate security. The soul confesses: "Whereupon then can I hope, or wherein may I trust, save only in the great mercy of God alone?" (034-055). This is not despair but clarity — the recognition that "for many friends shall not profit, nor strong helpers be able to succour… if Thou Thyself do not assist, help, strengthen, comfort, instruct, keep in safety" (096-024). Every human support is conditional; divine mercy alone is unconditional. The soul that has learned this has passed from anxious self-management to the "strongest solace" of hoping in God above all things (096-032).
