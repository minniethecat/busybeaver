# Compunction

The gift of inward [[contrition|sorrow for sin]] that pierces the heart and opens the way to devotion — a grace so central to the interior life that Thomas à Kempis devotes an entire chapter to it, teaching that without [[contrition|compunction]] there is no true progress, and that the spirit of [[contrition|compunction]] is itself a gift for which the soul must humbly pray — in the teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Compunction, from the Latin *compunctio* (a pricking or piercing), is in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] the grace-given sorrow of heart that arises from the remembrance of one's [[sin|sins]] and the consideration of one's banishment from God. It is not mere natural remorse or psychological guilt but a supernatural gift — "the spirit of [[contrition|compunction]]" (021-048) — that the soul must pray to receive. Thomas treats [[contrition|compunction]] not as an occasional sentiment but as a habitual disposition of the heart, the very ground in which devotion grows: "Give thyself to compunction of heart and thou shalt find devotion. Compunction openeth the way for many good things, which dissoluteness is wont quickly to lose" (021-004). The entire twenty-first chapter of Book I is given to this theme, signalling its foundational importance for the spiritual life.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats [[contrition|compunction]] as the indispensable condition for spiritual progress. Book I, Chapter 21, "Of Compunction of Heart," is one of the most concentrated treatments of a single interior disposition in the entire work, and the theme recurs throughout — in the teaching on contrition, on confession, on the [[death|remembrance of death]], and on the longing for heavenly joys.

### Compunction as the Door to Devotion

The central axiom of the chapter is expressed with lapidary clarity: "Give thyself to compunction of heart and thou shalt find devotion" (021-004). The order is crucial: [[contrition|compunction]] precedes devotion, not the other way around. Thomas here cuts against every natural instinct, which would seek [[comfort|consolation]] first and sorrow only when compelled. The way to devotion is downward — through the pierced heart, not around it. "Compunction openeth the way for many good things, which dissoluteness is wont quickly to lose" (021-004). Dissoluteness (*dissolutio*) is the opposite of [[contrition|compunction]] — the relaxed, unguarded spirit that laughs when it should weep, that seeks comforts instead of tears.

This teaching is reinforced negatively: "It is wonderful that any man can ever rejoice heartily in this life who considereth and weigheth his banishment, and the manifold dangers which beset his soul" (021-004). The man who truly considers his state — an exile from his true homeland, surrounded by spiritual dangers — finds mirth not natural but inexplicable. Compunction is simply realism about the soul's condition.

### The Spirit of Compunction as a Gift

Thomas is careful to teach that [[contrition|compunction]] is not something the soul can manufacture by its own effort. The closing section of the chapter makes this explicit: "Pray therefore humbly unto the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] that He will give thee the spirit of [[contrition|compunction]] and say in the language of the prophet, *Feed me, O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], with bread of tears, and give me plenteousness of tears to drink*" (021-048). The citation of Psalm 80:5 anchors the teaching in Scripture: tears are not a merely human response but a gift from God, a "bread" that nourishes the soul.

This is the paradox of [[contrition|compunction]] — it is both the soul's act (giving itself to sorrow) and God's gift (the spirit of [[contrition|compunction]] poured into the heart). "When a man hath perfect [[contrition|compunction]], then all [[world|the world]] is burdensome and bitter to him" (021-031). Perfect [[contrition|compunction]] reorders the affections entirely: [[world|the world]] that once delighted now tastes bitter, and the soul that once sought comfort now finds its nourishment in [[contrition|sorrow for sin]].

### The True Ground of Sorrow

Thomas anchors [[contrition|compunction]] in specific realities rather than vague feeling. "Grounds for just grief and inward [[contrition|compunction]] there are in our [[sin|sins]] and [[sin|vice]]s, wherein we lie so entangled that we are but seldom able to contemplate heavenly things" (021-037). The cause is not abstract but concrete: the soul's real [[sin|sins]], its real [[sin|vice]]s, its real entanglement. [[sin|Sin]] is not merely a past event but a present condition — the soul lies entangled now — and [[contrition|compunction]] is the sharp awareness of this entanglement.

This sorrow is fruitful, not destructive. "Through lightness of heart and neglect of our shortcomings we feel not the sorrows of our soul, but often vainly laugh when we have good cause to weep" (021-015). Lightness of heart — the refusal to take one's [[sin|sins]] seriously — is the great enemy. Thomas contrasts two kinds of [[blessedness|happiness]]: the false [[blessedness|happiness]] of those who laugh without cause, and the [[blessedness|true happiness]] of those who mourn: "Happy is he who putteth away from him whatsoever may stain or burden his conscience" (021-015). The happy man is not the one who ignores his [[sin|sins]] but the one who deals with them.

### Compunction and the Fear of God

The practice of [[contrition|compunction]] is inseparable from the [[fear-of-the-lord|fear of God]]. The chapter opens: "If thou wilt make any progress keep thyself in the [[fear-of-the-lord|fear of God]], and long not to be too free, but restrain all thy senses under discipline" (021-001). Compunction is not an isolated emotion but the fruit of a disciplined life lived in the [[reverence|fear of the Lord]]. The senses must be restrained; the desire for freedom curtailed.

This connection between [[contrition|compunction]] and discipline explains why Thomas warns against seeking worldly comforts: "But that we lack [[comfort|divine comfort]]s or feel them rarely is to our own blame, because we seek not compunction of heart, nor utterly cast away those comforts which are vain and worldly" (021-028). The soul that clings to worldly comforts cannot receive divine ones — not because God withholds them but because the heart has no room. Compunction is the emptying of the heart that makes space for [[comfort|consolation]].

### Compunction in the Wider Kempian Vision

The theme of [[contrition|compunction]] extends beyond Chapter 21. In Book II, Chapter 35, Thomas writes: "I desire no [[comfort|consolation]] which taketh away from me [[contrition|compunction]]" (035-020). This is a remarkable preference: [[contrition|compunction]] is ranked above [[comfort|consolation]]. The soul that has learned [[contrition|compunction]] would rather keep its sorrow than exchange it for a comfort that numbs the heart to its true condition.

In Book I, Chapter 20, Thomas teaches that the soul must "exercise itself in holy compunction" (020-037) — the verb is active, implying sustained practice, not a passing mood. Compunction is a habit to be cultivated, like prayer or fasting, through deliberate exercise.

### Practical Wisdom on Compunction

| Aspect | Teaching | Reference |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| Source | The spirit of [[contrition|compunction]] is a gift from God, obtained by prayer | 021-048 |
| Means | Self-examination, remembrance of [[sin|sins]], restraint of the senses | 021-001, 021-037 |
| Fruit | [[devotion|Devotion]], spiritual progress, liberation from worldly comforts | 021-004, 021-031 |
| Obstacle | Lightness of heart, neglect of shortcomings, love of vain comforts | 021-015, 021-028 |
| Measure | Perfect [[contrition|compunction]] makes the whole world burdensome and bitter | 021-031 |

> If thou wilt make any progress keep thyself in the fear of God, and long not to be too free, but restrain all thy senses under discipline and give not thyself up to senseless mirth. Give thyself to compunction of heart and thou shalt find devotion. (021-001 to 021-004)

> Through lightness of heart and neglect of our shortcomings we feel not the sorrows of our soul, but often vainly laugh when we have good cause to weep. There is no true liberty nor real joy, save in the fear of God with a good conscience. (021-015)

> When a man hath perfect compunction, then all the world is burdensome and bitter to him. A good man will find sufficient cause for mourning and weeping; for whether he considereth himself, or pondereth concerning his neighbour, he knoweth that no man liveth here without tribulation. (021-031)

> Grounds for just grief and inward compunction there are in our sins and vices, wherein we lie so entangled that we are but seldom able to contemplate heavenly things. (021-037)

> Pray therefore humbly unto the Lord that He will give thee the spirit of compunction and say in the language of the prophet, *Feed me, O Lord, with bread of tears, and give me plenteousness of tears to drink*. (021-048)

> I desire no consolation which taketh away from me compunction. (035-020)
