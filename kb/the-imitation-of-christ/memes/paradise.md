# Paradise

The state and place of eternal blessedness — the direct, face-to-face vision of God in which the blessed participate in the divine nature, are transformed into the likeness of Christ, and enjoy forever the "sweet paradise" of the presence of Jesus, described in The Imitation of Christ as the soul's true homeland toward which every step of the spiritual journey is directed.

## Location

Paradise, in Catholic theology, is the state of definitive and eternal happiness enjoyed by those who die in the state of grace and are purified of all attachment to sin. It is not a place in the physical sense — though Scripture and tradition use spatial imagery (the "new heaven and new earth," the "heavenly Jerusalem") — but a state of being: the beatific vision, in which the blessed see God "face to face" (1 Corinthians 13:12), knowing Him not through creatures or concepts but directly, as He is in Himself. This vision so transforms the soul that it becomes "partaker of the divine nature" (2 Peter 1:4), deified by grace without ceasing to be a creature.

The word "paradise" is of Persian origin (*pairidaeza*, an enclosed garden) and enters Scripture through the Septuagint's use of *paradeisos* for the Garden of Eden (Genesis 2:8). Christ uses the word from the Cross: "Verily I say unto thee, Today shalt thou be with Me in paradise" (Luke 23:43). St. Paul speaks of being "caught up into paradise" where he "heard unspeakable words, which it is not lawful for a man to utter" (2 Corinthians 12:4). The Book of Revelation promises: "To him that overcometh will I give to eat of the tree of life, which is in the midst of the paradise of God" (Revelation 2:7). The word thus spans the whole of salvation history — from the lost garden of Eden, through the foretaste granted to the saints, to the new creation in which "the tabernacle of God is with men" (Revelation 21:3).

## Biblical Significance

The biblical vision of paradise is not the static contemplation of a distant deity but the dynamic union of love: "We shall see Him as He is" (1 John 3:2). The beatific vision is not merely intellectual — it is transformative, conforming the blessed to the object of their vision: "we shall be like Him, for we shall see Him as He is" (1 John 3:2). The blessed "see His face" (Revelation 22:4), a privilege denied even to Moses in this life (Exodus 33:20). They "shall hunger no more, neither thirst any more... and God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes" (Revelation 7:16–17). The beatitude is total and eternal: no sorrow, no sin, no separation, no diminishment — only the eternal now of loving and being loved by God.

The joy of paradise is corporate as well as individual: it is the joy of the "communion of saints," the heavenly Church in which the blessed, the angels, and (in a different mode) the souls in purgatory are united in the one Body of Christ, praising the thrice-holy God. The liturgy of heaven, glimpsed in the Book of Revelation, is the eternal worship of the Lamb who was slain: "Worthy is the Lamb that was slain to receive power, and riches, and wisdom, and strength, and honour, and glory, and blessing" (Revelation 5:12). Earthly liturgy is a participation in this heavenly liturgy, and the Eucharist is its foretaste: "Blessed are they which are called unto the marriage supper of the Lamb" (Revelation 19:9).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis speaks of paradise with the language of intimate devotion and ardent longing. Paradise is not a distant abstraction but the object of the soul's deepest desire — and, already, a present foretaste granted to those who love Jesus and are united to Him in grace.

The most concentrated expression of this theology appears in Book II, Chapter VIII: "To be without Jesus is the nethermost hell, and to be with Jesus is sweet paradise" (033-013). This is one of the most quoted lines in all of The Imitation of Christ, and it captures the essence of Thomas's vision: paradise is not so much a place to which one goes as a Person with whom one is. The presence of Jesus *is* paradise; the absence of Jesus *is* hell. Everything else — the light, the peace, the joy, the company of angels and saints — is secondary to and derived from this central reality.

This Christocentric definition has immediate practical implications. The disciple who cultivates the presence of Jesus in this life — through prayer, meditation, the sacraments, and the practice of virtue — already possesses paradise in its seed. "When Jesus is present, all is well, and nothing seems difficult; but when Jesus is absent, everything is hard" (033-002). The alternation of consolation and desolation that marks the spiritual life is the alternation between the foretaste of paradise and the foretaste of its loss — and both are permitted by God for the soul's formation.

Paradise also serves as the horizon of hope that makes the sufferings of this life bearable. The disciple is exhorted to "have a good courage, and lift up thine eyes to God in heaven" (022-012), for "in heaven ought thy habitation to be" (026-037). The classic text from Hebrews — "Here have we no continuing city" (023-075) — is invoked to remind the disciple that earth is not home. Every Cross, every tribulation, every act of self-denial is a step toward paradise. The vision of the heavenly homeland gives meaning to the pilgrimage: "There shall appear the brightness of everlasting glory, at which all the Saints shall rejoice" (086-073).

The Eucharist is the privileged foretaste of paradise in this life. In receiving the Body and Blood of Christ, the disciple receives the One who is paradise, and in that reception the distance between the pilgrim on earth and the blessed in heaven is momentarily collapsed. The holy Angels "do feed upon Him in paradise" (107-066), but the disciple on earth feeds upon the same food — a mystery that fills Thomas with wonder and gratitude.

> "To be without Jesus is the nethermost hell, and to be with Jesus is sweet paradise." (033-013)

> "The holy Angels do feed upon Him in paradise, and the pilgrim upon the earth is refreshed by the same food." (107-066)

## Symbolic Meaning

Paradise, like all the deep symbols of the Christian tradition, operates on multiple levels simultaneously. It is the lost garden of Eden — the innocence forfeited by Adam's sin but restored, superabundantly, in Christ (Romans 5:15–17). It is the soul in the state of grace — the interior garden where God walks with the soul in the cool of the day. It is the Church — the earthly paradise where the tree of life (the Cross) and the river of living water (Baptism, the Spirit) nourish the faithful. And it is the eternal city — the New Jerusalem descending from heaven, where God dwells with His people and "there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain" (Revelation 21:4).

Thomas à Kempis, in accordance with the *devotio moderna*'s emphasis on interiority, concentrates most of his attention on the second of these levels: paradise as the present possession of the soul united to Christ. But he never loses sight of the eschatological horizon. The soul must "be ready always, that thou mayest be able to depart, and to be with Christ" (cf. 023-050). The whole spiritual life is a preparation for paradise — and, mysteriously, a participation in it even now.
