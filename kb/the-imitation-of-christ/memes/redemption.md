# Redemption

Redemption, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not a speculative doctrine to be debated but a saving reality to be received with gratitude and lived in daily [[faith|fidelity]]. Thomas à Kempis treats it not in a chapter of its own but as the underlying truth that gives the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] its meaning: the [[discipleship|disciple]], approaching the altar, "celebrates the work of thy redemption" (098-056). Every Mass is a re-presentation of redemption, and every worthy communion is a personal participation in its fruits. Thomas's treatment of redemption is therefore brief but deep — less a theological exposition than a sustained meditation on what it means to stand before God as one who has been bought with a price.

## Definition

Redemption, for Thomas, is the objective work of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] by which the soul is rescued from the guilt and power of sin. It is the answer to the most fundamental question of the spiritual life: on what ground can a sinner hope to stand before a holy God? The [[discipleship|disciple]] who prays "Thou art my salvation and redemption, my hope and my strength" (099-006) acknowledges that redemption is not something he has achieved but something he has received — a gift that precedes all effort and makes all effort possible. Thomas's language echoes the biblical vocabulary of ransom: the soul has been "redeemed with Thy precious blood" (105-027), purchased at an infinite cost. The blood of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is the price of redemption, and the [[discipleship|disciple]] who forgets this price will soon forget the gravity of sin and the magnitude of grace.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas treats redemption primarily within the context of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], where the work of redemption is not merely remembered but made present and applied to the individual soul. His treatment falls into three interconnected themes.

### The Work of Redemption Celebrated

Thomas places in the mouth of the [[discipleship|disciple]] approaching communion an act of recognition: "Thou dost celebrate the work of thy redemption, and art made partaker of all My merits" (098-056). The celebration is not a passive commem[[prayer|oratio]]n but an active participation. In receiving the body and blood of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], the [[discipleship|disciple]] enters into the very act by which he was redeemed — the self-offering of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] on the cross, made sacramentally present on the altar. Thomas understands the Mass not as a mere memorial but as a making-present: what [[jesus-christ|Christ]] accomplished once for all on [[crucifixion|Calvary]] is applied to the individual soul in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]].

This understanding gives the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] its gravity and its [[comfort|consolation]]. Its gravity: the [[discipleship|disciple]] who approaches unworthily profanes the blood by which he was redeemed. Its [[comfort|consolation]]: the [[discipleship|disciple]] who approaches worthily "receives the fruit of [[salvation|eternal salvation]]" (097-107), the actual benefit of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s redeeming work. "He Himself is our sanctification and redemption" (097-106): Thomas insists that redemption is not an impersonal transaction but a personal union with [[jesus-christ|Christ]], who is Himself the redemption He gives. To receive [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] is to receive redemption itself.

### The Soul Redeemed by Blood

The most vivid image of redemption in The Imitation appears in a prayer of the devout [[discipleship|disciple]]: "Behold, I commend unto Thee my soul, which Thou hast redeemed with Thy precious blood" (105-027). The adjective "precious" — valuable beyond all reckoning — captures the whole theology of redemption in a single word. The soul is precious because it was bought at a precious price; to despise one's own soul is to despise the blood that was shed for it. Thomas's emphasis on the blood of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] echoes the apostolic teaching that "ye were not redeemed with corruptible things, as silver and gold … but with the precious blood of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (1 Peter 1:18-19). Redemption is the most costly transaction in the history of the universe.

This blood-purchased redemption carries an implicit moral claim. The soul that has been bought at such a price no longer belongs to itself. "Ye are bought with a price; therefore glorify God in your body and in your spirit, which are God's" (1 Corinthians 6:20). Thomas does not state this explicitly but his whole doctrine of the [[discipleship|imitation of Christ]] depends on it: the [[discipleship|disciple]] must imitate [[jesus-christ|Christ]] precisely because he has been redeemed by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Redemption creates not only a new status but a new obligation — the obligation to live as one who belongs to God.

### Redemption as Ground of Hope

The [[discipleship|disciple]] who prays "Thou art my salvation and redemption, my hope and my strength" (099-006) locates the ultimate ground of confidence not in his own efforts but in the finished work of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Redemption is past, accomplished, objective; it does not depend on the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s fluctuating [[devotion|fervour]] or partial victories. The same prayer addresses [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as "my refuge and my deliverer" — the one in whom the soul finds safety from the accusations of conscience and the terrors of judgment.

This confidence in redemption undergirds Thomas's entire spiritual programme. The [[discipleship|disciple]] can labour earnestly for amendment precisely because his acceptance with God does not depend on the success of his labour. He strives not to be redeemed but because he has been redeemed; the indicative of redemption grounds the imperative of holiness. "He is the [[comfort|consolation]] of His servants" (097-107): the [[comfort|consolation]] of redemption is not a future hope but a present possession, the daily comfort of knowing that one's salvation has been secured by Another.

## Key Passages

> "Thou dost celebrate the work of thy redemption, and art made partaker of all My merits." (098-056 to 098-057)

> "Thou art my salvation and redemption, my hope and my strength, my refuge and my deliverer." (099-006 to 099-007)

> "Behold, I commend unto Thee my soul, which Thou hast redeemed with Thy precious blood." (105-027)

> "He Himself is our sanctification and redemption. He is the consolation of His servants." (097-106 to 097-107)

## Biblical Foundation

The doctrine of redemption in The Imitation rests on the New Testament teaching that [[jesus-christ|Christ]] "hath redeemed us from the curse of the law, being made a curse for us" (Galatians 3:13). St Paul's declaration that "in whom we have redemption through His blood, the forgiveness of [[sin|sins]], according to the riches of His grace" (Ephesians 1:7) provides the vocabulary that Thomas adopts: redemption is through blood, it brings forgiveness, and it manifests the richness of [[grace|divine grace]]. The apostle's parallel statement in Colossians 1:14 — "in whom we have redemption through His blood, even the forgiveness of [[sin|sins]]" — confirms that this is not an isolated Pauline theme but a central strand of apostolic teaching.

St Peter's insistence that redemption was accomplished "not with corruptible things, as silver and gold … but with the precious blood of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], as of a lamb without blemish and without spot" (1 Peter 1:18-19) provides the language of "precious blood" that Thomas adopts in the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s prayer (105-027). The image of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as the Passover lamb, whose blood delivers from death, links redemption to the Exodus and to the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], where the Lamb is received as food. The author of Hebrews, contrasting the old covenant sacrifices with the new, declares that [[jesus-christ|Christ]] "by His own blood … entered in once into the holy place, having obtained eternal redemption for us" (Hebrews 9:12). This eternal redemption — once accomplished, never to be repeated — is the objective ground of every hope that The Imitation holds out to the [[discipleship|disciple]].
