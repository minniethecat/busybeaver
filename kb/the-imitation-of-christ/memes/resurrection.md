# Resurrection

The bodily rising of Jesus Christ from the dead on the third day after His crucifixion — the central saving act of Christian faith, by which His atoning death is vindicated, sin and death are definitively conquered, and the path to eternal life is opened for all who are united to Him through faith and suffering.

## Biblical Account

The Resurrection of Jesus Christ is attested in all four Gospels (Matthew 28:1-10; Mark 16:1-8; Luke 24:1-12; John 20:1-18) and is the foundational proclamation of the apostolic preaching throughout the Acts of the Apostles. On the first day of the week, women disciples — among them Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James, and Salome — came to the tomb to anoint the body of Jesus and found the stone rolled away and the tomb empty. Angels declared to them that He had risen. Jesus subsequently appeared to Mary Magdalene (John 20:14-17), to the disciples on the road to Emmaus (Luke 24:13-35), to the gathered apostles (John 20:19-23), to Thomas (John 20:26-29), and to more than five hundred brethren at once (1 Corinthians 15:6). (PN 037-057, 037-141)

### Gospel Accounts

Each Gospel contributes a distinctive witness to the Resurrection. Matthew emphasizes the earthquake, the descent of the angel, and the great commission. Mark preserves the starkness of the empty tomb and the women's fear and astonishment. Luke presents the Emmaus road encounter and Christ's demonstration that He is flesh and bone. John gives the most intimate accounts — Mary Magdalene's encounter with the Risen Lord, Thomas's confession "My Lord and my God," and Christ's threefold restoration of Peter. Together the four accounts establish the historicity and transformative power of the event: the one who was crucified is alive, seen, touched, and heard.

## Theological Significance

The Resurrection is the divine "Yes" to the sacrifice of the Cross. It confirms that Christ's death was not a defeat but a victory — that sin has been expiated, death has been swallowed up, and the new creation has broken into history. The Resurrection is the ground of the believer's justification (Romans 4:25), the firstfruits of the general resurrection of the dead (1 Corinthians 15:20-22), and the guarantee that those united to Christ shall also rise (Romans 6:4-5). In St. Paul's words, "if Christ be not risen, your faith is vain" (1 Corinthians 15:17). The empty tomb and the appearances of the Risen Lord constitute the historical and theological hinge upon which the whole Christian confession turns.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats the Resurrection not as an isolated event but as the necessary completion of Christ's redemptive work — the pattern of suffering followed by glory that every disciple must embrace. The most explicit citation appears in Book II, Chapter XII, where Thomas directly invokes the words of the Risen Christ recorded in Luke 24:46: "It behooved, He said, Christ to suffer and to rise from the dead, and so enter into his glory" (037-057). This statement functions as a premise for the entire chapter: the way of the Cross is not the final word, for suffering leads to resurrection, and the Cross leads to the Kingdom. The Resurrection is the guarantee that suffering borne with Christ is not meaningless but is the road to eternal life.

> "It behooved, He said, Christ to suffer and to rise from the dead, and so enter into his glory." (037-057 to 037-058)

### Dying and Rising with Christ

The Resurrection, for Thomas, is not merely an event to be believed but a pattern to be lived. The disciple who is united to the Crucified shall also share in His Resurrection: "For if thou be dead with Him, thou shalt also live with Him, and if thou be a partaker of His sufferings thou shalt be also of His glory" (037-020 to 037-022). This mystical identification with the death and resurrection of Christ is the heart of the imitation. To die to self, to the world, and to pride is to enter already into the power of Christ's resurrection — a life oriented toward God rather than toward self.

> "For if thou be dead with Him, thou shalt also live with Him, and if thou be a partaker of His sufferings thou shalt be also of His glory." (037-020 to 037-022)

This dying-and-rising pattern is central to the Kempian spiritual life. "The more a man dieth to himself, the more he beginneth to live towards God" (037-123 to 037-124). The resurrection life is not postponed to the eschaton but is begun now through daily mortification — every act of self-denial, every suffering borne with patience, is a participation in Christ's own passage from death to life.

### The Resurrection and the Cross

Thomas holds the Cross and the Resurrection in inseparable unity. The Cross is the way, but the Resurrection is the goal: "Why fearest thou then to take up the cross which leadeth to a kingdom?" (037-011). He insists that "In the Cross is health, in the Cross is life... no hope of eternal life, save in the Cross" (037-012 to 037-016), yet the Cross is efficacious precisely because it leads to Resurrection — "Take up therefore, thy cross and follow Jesus and thou shalt go into eternal life" (037-017 to 037-018). The Cross without the Resurrection would be mere suffering; the Resurrection without the Cross would be cheap glory. Together they form the "royal way" (037-058) by which Christ Himself passed and by which every disciple must follow.

### Resurrection as Pattern for the Disciple

Throughout The Imitation, Thomas presents the life of the believer as a prolonged participation in the paschal mystery. The sufferings of this life, borne in union with Christ, are intrinsically ordered toward resurrection. "Through much tribulation [we] enter into the kingdom of God" (037-141), and this is not an unfortunate necessity but the very pattern Christ Himself established. The hour of tribulation is the hour in which the believer is most conformed to Christ and therefore nearest to His glory. The Resurrection is thus both the historical event that vindicates Christ and the eschatological hope that sustains the disciple in every affliction.

> "We must through much tribulation enter into the kingdom of God." (037-141)

### The Raising of Lazarus as Sign

In Book III, Chapter XXXIII, Thomas references Lazarus, "whom he had raised from the dead" (070-019), as a cautionary sign: even those drawn to Christ may seek Him not for Himself but for the wonders He performs. The raising of Lazarus is a foreshadowing of the general resurrection that Christ's own Resurrection inaugurates, yet it also serves as a warning that the sign must lead to the reality — to Christ Himself, not merely to His gifts.

### Eternal Life as the Fruit of Resurrection

Because Christ rose, the believer's desire for eternal life is not an illusion but a response to a reality. Book III, Chapter XLIX is devoted to "the desire after eternal life" (086-001), where Christ speaks to the soul: "Already thou longest to be in the glorious liberty of the children of God; already the eternal home delighteth thee" (086-029 to 086-030). The longing for resurrection and eternal life is itself a gift of grace, "poured into thee from above" (086-003 to 086-004), and the believer is to "enlarge thine heart, and take in this holy inspiration with all thy desire" (086-006 to 086-007). The Resurrection guarantees that this desire is not in vain.

In the Eucharist, the Risen Christ gives Himself as the food of immortality: "if I desire to obtain eternal life and glory. Come unto Me, sayest Thou, all that labour and are heavy laden, and I will refresh you" (097-016 to 097-017). The sacramental life of the Church is the believer's ongoing participation in the Resurrection of Christ — receiving the Risen Lord in the Blessed Sacrament as the pledge of future glory.

## Liturgical Connection

The Resurrection is commemorated annually at Easter (Pascha), the Queen of Feasts and the summit of the liturgical year. The Easter Vigil and Easter Sunday celebrate Christ's passage from death to life, and the fifty days of Eastertide extend this celebration through Pentecost. Every Sunday is a "little Easter" — a weekly commemoration of the Resurrection that structures Christian worship. In the liturgy, the paschal mystery — Christ's Passion, Death, Resurrection, and Ascension — is made present to the faithful, and through the sacraments, especially Baptism and the Eucharist, believers are incorporated into Christ's own dying and rising (Romans 6:3-5).
