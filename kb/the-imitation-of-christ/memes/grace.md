# Grace

The unmerited, supernatural gift of God that alone enables the soul to overcome its corrupted nature, progress in virtue, and attain union with God — the central animating principle of the spiritual life in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Grace, in the theology of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is far more than divine favour. It is "a supernatural light, and a certain special gift of God, and the proper mark of the elect, and the pledge of [[salvation|eternal salvation]]" (091-087). It transforms the human person from the inside, exalting him "from earthly things to love those that are heavenly" and making "the carnal man spiritual" (091-090). Thomas à Kempis identifies grace with love itself: "the proper gift of the elect is grace—that is, love—and they who bear the mark thereof are held worthy of everlasting life" (092-041). Without it, even "neither faith, nor hope, nor any other virtue is accepted with Thee without love and grace" (092-044).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Grace pervades every book of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as the unearned enabling power without which no spiritual progress is possible. Thomas à Kempis presents grace not as an abstract theological category but as the experiential reality that makes the entire spiritual life possible — its source, its sustenance, and its perfection.

### Grace as the Necessary Foundation

Thomas insists that without grace nothing of spiritual value can be accomplished: "Oh how entirely necessary is Thy grace to me, O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], for a good beginning, for progress, and for bringing to perfection" (092-034). The [[discipleship|disciple]] confesses that "without it I can do nothing, but I can do all things through Thy grace which strengtheneth me" (092-036). Human nature, corrupted by the fall, possesses only "one spark lying hid in the ashes" of natural reason — insufficient to fulfill the good it dimly perceives (092-018). Hence the urgent cry: "There is need of Thy grace, yea, and of a great measure thereof, that my nature may be conquered, which hath alway been prone to evil from my youth" (092-010). Grace is not a supplement to human effort; it is the very ground of the spiritual life: "We cannot place too little confidence in ourselves, because grace and understanding are often lacking to us" (030-002).

### The Nature-Grace Dialectic

The most sustained analysis of grace in the work appears in Book III, Chapter LIV — "Of the Diverse Motions of [[nature|Nature]] and of Grace" — which presents a systematic contrast between the two principles operating within the human soul. "[[nature|Nature]] is deceitful and draweth away, ensnareth, and deceiveth many, and always hath self for her end; but Grace walketh in simplicity and turneth away from every appearance of evil, maketh no false pretences, and doeth all entirely for the sake of God, in whom also she finally resteth" (091-007). This dialectic runs through some eighteen contrasts: nature seeks honour and fears shame, while grace ascribes "all honour and glory to God" (091-024). [[nature|Nature]] "loveth ease and bodily [[silence|quiet]]," while grace "cannot be unemployed, but gladly embraceth labour" (091-027). [[nature|Nature]] reaches after temporal things; grace "reacheth after things eternal" (091-035). The relationship between the two is inverse: "So far therefore as [[nature|Nature]] is utterly pressed down and overcome, so far is greater Grace bestowed and the inner man is daily created anew by fresh visitations, after the image of God" (091-091).

### Grace, Humility, and Gratitude

Grace and humility are inseparably linked in the Kempian vision. Grace is given to the humble and withdrawn from the proud: "For grace ever becometh the portion of him who is grateful and that is taken away from the proud, which is wont to be given to the humble" (035-017). This dynamic is so fundamental that Thomas devotes an entire chapter to it — "Of Hiding Our Grace Under the Guard of [[humility|Humility]]" (Book III, Chapter VII). [[jesus-christ|Christ]] counsels the [[discipleship|disciple]] that it is "better and safer for thee to hide the grace of devotion, and not to lift thyself up on high, nor to speak much thereof, nor to value it greatly; but rather to despise thyself, and to fear as though this grace were given to one unworthy thereof" (044-002). The [[discipleship|disciple]] is warned: "Think when thou art in a state of grace how miserable and poor thou art wont to be without grace" (044-007). Those who grow presumptuous because of spiritual [[comfort|consolation]]s "have destroyed themselves, because they would do more than they were able, not considering the measure of their own littleness" (044-021), and thereby "quickly lost grace" (044-025).

[[gratitude|Gratitude]] is the practical disposition that keeps the channel of grace open. Thomas teaches that "the gifts of grace are not able to flow unto us, because we are ungrateful to the Author of them, and return them not wholly to the Fountain whence they flow" (035-015). Conversely, "be thankful, therefore, for the least benefit and thou shalt be worthy to receive greater" (035-043).

### Grace and Detachment from the World

Grace cannot coexist with attachment to [[creation|created things]]. In Book III, Chapter LIII, [[jesus-christ|Christ]] declares: "precious is My grace, it suffereth not itself to be joined with outward things, nor with earthly [[comfort|consolation]]s" (090-003). Therefore the [[discipleship|disciple]] is instructed to "cast away all things which hinder grace, if thou longest to receive the inpouring thereof" (090-005). This demands solitude and interior recollection: "thou canst not be alone with Me, and at the same time be delighted with transitory things" (090-011). For contemplative ascent, "much grace is necessary, which may lift up the soul and raise it above itself" (068-015).

### Grace and the Sacramental Life

In Book IV, grace is closely associated with worthy reception of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]]. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is instructed to "seek earnestly the grace of devotion, to ask it fervently, to wait for it patiently and faithfully, to receive it gratefully, to preserve it humbly" (111-003). Yet grace is not at the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s command: "If grace were always given immediately, and were at hand at the wish, it would be hardly bearable to weak man" (111-012). The deferral of grace serves a pedagogical purpose: "It is sometimes a small thing which hindereth and hideth grace" (111-016), and by removing it the soul learns to relinquish every inordinate attachment. The one who has "lifted up his will unto God with singleness of heart" and "delivered himself from every inordinate love or dislike of any created thing" becomes "the most fit for receiving grace, and worthy of the gift of devotion" (111-026).

### Key Passages

> "This Grace is a supernatural light, and a certain special gift of God, and the proper mark of the elect, and the pledge of eternal salvation; it exalteth a man from earthly things to love those that are heavenly; and it maketh the carnal man spiritual" (091-087)

> "Oh how entirely necessary is Thy grace to me, O Lord, for a good beginning, for progress, and for bringing to perfection. For without it I can do nothing, but I can do all things through Thy grace which strengtheneth me" (092-034)

> "It is the mistress of truth, the teacher of discipline, the light of the heart, the solace of anxiety, the banisher of sorrow, the deliverer from fear, the nurse of devotion, the drawer forth of tears. What am I without it, save a dry tree, a useless branch, worthy to be cast away!" (092-057)

> "My Son, it is better and safer for thee to hide the grace of devotion, and not to lift thyself up on high, nor to speak much thereof, nor to value it greatly; but rather to despise thyself, and to fear as though this grace were given to one unworthy thereof" (044-002)

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] grounds its theology of grace in the Pauline and Johannine writings. The absolute necessity of grace echoes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s words: "Without Me ye can do nothing" (John 15:5). The [[discipleship|disciple]]'s confession of impotence before his corrupted nature directly mirrors Romans 7:18-25, explicitly cited in Book III, Chapter LV: "To will to do good is present with me, but how to perform it I find not" (092-027). The sufficiency of grace draws from 2 Corinthians 12:9 — "Thy grace is sufficient for me" (092-051) — while the power of grace to enable all things reflects Philippians 4:13 (092-036). The dynamic of grace given to the humble draws from the wisdom tradition: "God resisteth the proud, but giveth grace unto the humble" (James 4:6, quoting Proverbs 3:34), a verse that appears throughout the work (007-008). The identification of grace with love reflects the Johannine teaching that God is love (1 John 4:8).

- [[nature]] — The fallen human condition that grace alone can overcome and transform
- [[gratitude]] — The practical response that keeps the channel of grace open; ingratitude blocks its flow
- [[self-denial]] — The ascetical discipline by which the obstacle to grace (inordinate self-love) is removed
- [[consolation]] — The experiential sweetness of grace, given and withdrawn according to God's wisdom
- [[love]] — Identified with grace as "the proper gift of the elect," the transforming fire of divine union
- [[devotion]] — The [[devotion|fervour]] of spiritual affection that is a particular expression of grace
