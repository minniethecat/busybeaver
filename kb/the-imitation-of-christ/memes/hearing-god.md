# Hearing God

"My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me" (John 10:27). To hear God is the beginning of the Christian life — faith itself is a response to a word heard — and the condition of its progress. Scripture constantly presents God as one who speaks, and the human person as one summoned to listen: "Today if ye will hear his voice, harden not your hearts" (Hebrews 3:7–8, quoting Psalm 95:7–8). Yet hearing God is not a matter of the physical ear; it is a spiritual sense, the *auditus Dei*, cultivated in silence, prayer, and obedience. The young Samuel's reply — "Speak, Lord; for thy servant heareth" (1 Samuel 3:9–10) — is the posture of the listening disciple.

## Definition

Hearing God means perceiving and responding to divine communication — a communication that comes through multiple channels. The primary channel is Sacred Scripture, where God's word is objectively accessible. A secondary channel is the interior movements of grace — what the tradition calls "the motions of the soul" — by which the Holy Spirit prompts, warns, consoles, and directs. A third channel is the teaching of the Church and the counsel of spiritual directors, through which the individual's discernment is tested against communal wisdom. Hearing God rightly requires the virtue of discernment, because not every interior voice is divine — the enemy can counterfeit the voice of the Shepherd.

## In The Imitation of Christ

*The Imitation of Christ* is structured as a dialogue between the disciple and Christ — a literary form that presupposes the possibility of hearing God. The disciple speaks; Christ answers. The book itself is a training in listening.

### The Interior Teacher

Kempis emphasizes that Christ is the interior teacher (*magister interior*), who instructs the soul directly: "Let not Moses speak to me, nor any of the prophets, but rather do Thou speak, O Lord God" (003-001). This is not a rejection of external teaching but an insistence that external teaching is lifeless unless the interior Teacher makes it living. "Speak, Lord, for thy servant heareth" — the *Imitation* puts this prayer on the disciple's lips at the beginning of Book III, establishing the posture of receptivity that the whole book requires.

### Silence as the Condition of Hearing

"If thou wilt hear, be silent" — this principle, implicit throughout the *Imitation*, links silence and listening. The soul filled with noise — external noise of conversation and business, internal noise of ambition and anxiety — cannot hear the still small voice in which God prefers to speak. Kempis urges the disciple to "love to be silent and to be unknown" (002-005), not from misanthropy but from the desire to hear.

### Obedience as the Proof of Hearing

To hear without obeying is not to hear at all. The *Imitation* insists that the test of hearing is action: "He that heareth my words and doeth them, he it is that loveth me" (004-001). The obedient soul — obedient to Scripture, to lawful superiors, to the duties of one's state — is the soul that has truly heard.

## Biblical Foundation

Scripture is a book of divine speech and human response. The prophets are summoned with "the word of the Lord came unto me" — they speak because they have first heard. Christ presents Himself as the good shepherd whose sheep know His voice (John 10:3–4). Saint Paul teaches that "faith cometh by hearing, and hearing by the word of God" (Romans 10:17). The risen Christ stands at the door and knocks: "If any man hear my voice, and open the door, I will come in to him" (Revelation 3:20). The promise is that "thine ears shall hear a word behind thee, saying, This is the way, walk ye in it" (Isaiah 30:21) — hearing God is the ordinary condition of the faithful soul, not a rare mystical privilege.
