# Bethany

Bethany — the village on the eastern slope of the Mount of Olives, home to Martha, Mary, and Lazarus — is remembered in the Gospels as the place where Jesus raised the dead and received the anointing of love. In The Imitation of Christ, it becomes a figure of the soul's mixed motives: the place where men come seeking not Christ but the signs of His power.

## Location

Bethany lay about two miles (fifteen stadia) east of Jerusalem on the road to Jericho (John 11:18), on the south-eastern slope of the Mount of Olives. It was a modest village whose name in Hebrew, *Beth-aniah*, likely means "house of affliction" or "house of the poor" — a fitting name for a place associated with Lazarus's death and the compassion of Christ. In the time of Jesus, it was the home of Martha, Mary, and Lazarus, and served as the lodging place of Jesus during His final week in Jerusalem before the Passion.

## Biblical Significance

Bethany is the setting of three of the most intimate Gospel episodes. It was here that Mary sat at Jesus's feet listening to His teaching while Martha busied herself with serving (Luke 10:38–42). It was here that Jesus wept at the tomb of Lazarus and then called the dead man forth from the grave, demonstrating His power over death (John 11:1–44). And it was here — specifically in the house of Simon the leper — that Mary anointed Jesus with costly ointment of spikenard, an act He interpreted as preparation for His burial (John 12:1–8). In each scene, Bethany is not a backdrop but a character: the quiet place of domestic intimacy where the deepest revelations of Christ's person and mission are given.

### Key Events at This Place

- **The Raising of Lazarus** — Jesus, having delayed two days after hearing of Lazarus's illness, arrived at Bethany to find him four days in the tomb. He wept with the sisters, then called Lazarus forth alive (John 11:1–44).

- **Mary's Anointing of Jesus** — In the house of Simon the leper at Bethany, Mary took a pound of ointment of spikenard and anointed the feet of Jesus, wiping them with her hair, filling the house with fragrance (John 12:1–8).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Bethany is mentioned once in The Imitation of Christ, in Book III, Chapter 38, where Thomas à Kempis draws a spiritual lesson from the curious visit of the Jews to the village:

> "So the Jews of old came to Bethany, to the house of Martha and Mary, that they might see not Jesus, but Lazarus, whom He had raised from the dead." (070-018)

The reference is from John 12:9, where a crowd came to Bethany not only to see Jesus but also to see Lazarus — the walking proof of Christ's power. Thomas seizes upon this detail to diagnose a perennial spiritual disorder: the tendency to seek the gifts of God rather than the Giver. The Jews "came to Bethany" — went to the right place, to the house where Jesus was — but their intention was bent. They wanted to see the miracle, not the Miracle-Worker; the raised man, not the One who raises the dead.

Thomas applies this to the interior life: "Therefore must the eye of the intention be cleansed, that it may be single and right, and above all things which come in its way, may be directed unto Me" (Book III, Chapter 38). Bethany thus becomes a symbol of the soul that does all the right external things — goes to the right places, keeps the right company — but whose inward gaze is fixed on something short of God Himself.

## The Domestic Sanctuary

Bethany stands in the Gospels as the domestic counterpoint to Jerusalem. Jerusalem is the city of the Temple, the priesthood, and the public ministry of Christ — the place of confrontation with the authorities and, ultimately, of the Passion. Bethany, by contrast, is the village of the household: the home where Jesus is welcomed not as Rabbi or Wonder-Worker but as friend. It is at Bethany that Jesus weeps (John 11:35), and it is at Bethany that a woman's act of extravagant love fills the house with fragrance — an act of devotion so pure that Christ declares it will be told "wheresoever this gospel shall be preached in the whole world" (Matthew 26:13).

This domestic character of Bethany is significant for the spirituality of The Imitation. Thomas à Kempis, writing within the devotio moderna, valued the interior life of the home and the cell over the public spaces of ecclesiastical power. The disciple is urged to "love to be unknown and to be accounted as nothing" (Book I, Chapter 2) — a counsel more easily kept in the quiet of Bethany than in the tumult of Jerusalem. The house at Bethany, where Martha served and Mary listened and Lazarus sat at table with the Lord, is an image of the soul in its proper order: action and contemplation and restored life all gathered around Christ.

Yet the one mention of Bethany in The Imitation warns that even this holy household can be approached with a crooked intention. The same village that sheltered the most intimate friendships of Jesus also drew a crowd of curiosity-seekers. The place itself confers no immunity from self-deception.

## Symbolic Meaning

In the Christian spiritual tradition, Bethany has sometimes been read as a figure of the contemplative life: the quiet village away from the noise of Jerusalem, where Mary sits at the Lord's feet and Lazarus rests after his return from the grave. But Thomas à Kempis's use is more searching. Bethany becomes the test of intention. The same village that housed the holy household of Martha, Mary, and Lazarus also hosted the curiosity-seekers who came for the wrong reason. The place itself confers no virtue; what matters is why the soul has come there.

Bethany is also, in a deeper sense, the place of the heart that has known both death and resurrection. Lazarus emerged from the tomb at Bethany, but he still wore the grave-clothes that needed to be unwound. In the same way, the soul that has been raised from the death of sin still bears the residual bindings of old habits and must be unbound by the ongoing work of grace. Bethany is not only the scene of the miracle but the continuing place of convalescence — the village where the once-dead man learns to live again.
