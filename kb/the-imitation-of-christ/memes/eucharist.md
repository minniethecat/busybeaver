# Eucharist

The Most Blessed Sacrament of the Altar — the true Body and Blood of Jesus Christ, given under the appearances of bread and wine — instituted by Christ at the Last Supper on the night before His Passion, and forming in The Imitation of Christ the consummation of the entire spiritual life, the "bread of angels" by which the soul is nourished, united to Christ, and fortified for the journey toward eternal life.

## Biblical Account

The institution of the Eucharist is narrated in the Synoptic Gospels and St. Paul with solemn brevity. On the night before His Passion, "the same night in which He was betrayed" (1 Corinthians 11:23), Jesus gathered with His twelve apostles in the upper room to eat the Passover. "As they were eating, Jesus took bread, and blessed it, and brake it, and gave it to the disciples, and said, Take, eat; this is My body. And He took the cup, and gave thanks, and gave it to them, saying, Drink ye all of it; for this is My blood of the new testament, which is shed for many for the remission of sins" (Matthew 26:26–28).

This fourfold action — He took, blessed, broke, and gave — has been the pattern of every Eucharistic celebration from the apostolic age to the present. The command "This do in remembrance of Me" (Luke 22:19; 1 Corinthians 11:24) established the Eucharist as the perpetual act of the Church, the making-present of the one sacrifice of Calvary across every time and place. St. Paul's stern warning — "whosoever shall eat this bread, and drink this cup of the Lord, unworthily, shall be guilty of the body and blood of the Lord... let a man examine himself, and so let him eat of that bread, and drink of that cup" (1 Corinthians 11:27–28) — established the necessary preparation of soul that must precede worthy reception, a theme Thomas à Kempis develops across the whole of Book IV.

The Eucharist is prefigured throughout the Old Testament. Melchizedek, king of Salem, brought forth bread and wine (Genesis 14:18); the manna in the wilderness fed Israel for forty years (Exodus 16); the Passover lamb, whose blood marked the doors of the Israelites and whose flesh was eaten in haste (Exodus 12), was the type of "Christ our Passover sacrificed for us" (1 Corinthians 5:7). Jesus Himself, in the Bread of Life discourse, declared: "I am the living bread which came down from heaven: if any man eat of this bread, he shall live for ever: and the bread that I will give is My flesh, which I will give for the life of the world" (John 6:51).

## Theological Significance

The Eucharist is the "source and summit of the Christian life" (Lumen Gentium, 11). It is at once sacrifice and sacrament, memorial and banquet, the making-present of Christ's Passion and the pledge of future glory. As sacrifice, the Mass re-presents the one offering of Calvary: Christ, the eternal High Priest, offers Himself through the ministry of the priest, and the faithful unite their own self-offering to His. As sacrament, the Eucharist is true food and true drink: the Body and Blood of Christ are received not as dead flesh but as the living Lord, who "dwelleth in Me, and I in him" (John 6:56, echoed throughout Book IV of The Imitation).

The doctrine of transubstantiation — that the whole substance of bread is changed into the substance of Christ's Body, and the whole substance of wine into the substance of His Blood, while the accidents (appearances) of bread and wine remain — safeguards the Church's faith that what is received in Holy Communion is not a symbol but the reality: Christ Himself, whole and entire, Body, Blood, Soul, and Divinity. This is the faith presupposed throughout Book IV, where Thomas speaks of receiving not a sign of Christ but Christ Himself, "the Word of God who is the light of the soul" (107-050).

## In The Imitation of Christ

The whole of Book IV of The Imitation of Christ — "A Devout Exhortation to the Holy Communion" — is devoted to the Eucharist. Its eighteen chapters form the longest sustained treatment of any single theme in the work, and its placement at the conclusion signals the Eucharist's role as the summit toward which the entire spiritual life tends. Book I teaches the disciple to despise the world and acquire virtue; Book II deepens the interior life; Book III, the dialogue between Christ and the disciple, trains the soul in the dispositions of humility, patience, and trust; but Book IV brings the soul to the altar, where all that has been learned and practiced finds its fulfillment in sacramental union with Christ.

### Preparation for Communion

Thomas devotes extraordinary care to the proper disposition for receiving the Eucharist. The disciple must approach not casually but with "great reverence and a special devotion" (097-001). The model is Christ Himself, "the Priest entering into the Holy of Holies" (101-002), and the disciple must imitate this reverence by examining conscience, stirring up devotion, and approaching with the awareness that "he that toucheth pitch shall be defiled" (101-007) — only a soul cleansed by repentance should draw near to the all-holy Sacrament.

The examination of conscience before Communion is treated with great seriousness. The communicant must ask: "Am I in a state of grace? Have I true sorrow for my sins? Do I approach with firm purpose of amendment?" (cf. 101-010–020). Thomas acknowledges the tension between the sense of unworthiness and the command to receive: "Thou oughtest not to be away from this holy Sacrament because thou feelest thyself not to have the devotion which thou desirest" (102-005). Grace, not felt fervor, is the ground of worthy reception.

### The Eucharist as Nourishment

Thomas presents the Eucharist as the food that sustains the soul on its pilgrimage. "The holy Eucharist obtaineth the great grace of devotion and love of God; it refresheth the mind that is wearied, and strengtheneth the spiritual life of man" (111-040). As bodily food restores physical strength, so the "bread of angels" restores spiritual strength. The soul that approaches frequently and devoutly "shall find great help and refreshment" (097-003), while the soul that neglects the Sacrament grows cold and weak.

Christ speaks directly to the disciple about the gift He gives: "I have given Myself wholly unto thee; I have left nothing to Myself. I gave My whole self to thee upon the Cross; and I am daily prepared to give Myself wholly to thee in the Sacrament" (104-008). This total self-gift of Christ demands a corresponding total self-gift of the disciple: "As I of My own will offered Myself unto God the Father on the Cross for thy sins... so also oughtest thou every day to offer thyself willingly unto Me for a pure and holy oblation" (104-003).

### Union with Christ

The ultimate purpose of the Eucharist is union — Christ dwelling in the disciple and the disciple in Christ. "He that eateth My Flesh and drinketh My Blood dwelleth in Me and I in him" (094-036). This indwelling is not metaphorical but real: the same Christ who was born of the Virgin, who taught in Galilee, who suffered under Pontius Pilate, who rose from the dead, comes to dwell bodily within the communicant. Thomas describes this union in the most intimate terms: Christ is the Bridegroom, the soul is the bride, and the Eucharist is the marriage feast. "The Word of God is the light of the soul," and in receiving the Word made flesh, the soul is illuminated from within (107-050).

The fruit of this union is transformation. The soul that receives Christ worthily begins to take on the mind of Christ. "He calleth to mind the mystery of Christ's incarnation and His Passion, and is inflamed with the love of Him" (106-064). The Eucharist is the furnace of charity: it enkindles love, strengthens virtue, weakens concupiscence, and makes the soul more like the Christ it receives. Thomas quotes the patristic maxim: we become what we eat — receiving the Body of Christ, we are made members of that Body.

### Thanksgiving After Communion

Thomas devotes several chapters to the proper disposition after receiving. The moments following Communion are precious beyond measure: Christ is present within the soul in a unique sacramental mode, and the disciple must not squander this time in distraction. "Keep thyself in silence and solitude after thou hast received the Sacrament; for thou bearest the Lord of all, before whom the powers of heaven do tremble" (105-032). The post-Communion thanksgiving should include acts of adoration, thanksgiving, petition, and self-offering. "Behold I commit myself to Thee," the disciple prays, "do with me according to Thy good pleasure" (105-027). The graces of Communion are not automatic; they must be guarded, cultivated, and acted upon.

## Liturgical Connection

The Eucharist is celebrated daily in the Holy Sacrifice of the Mass, the unbloody re-presentation of the one sacrifice of Calvary. The Mass is structured in two principal parts: the Liturgy of the Word, in which the Scriptures are proclaimed and expounded, and the Liturgy of the Eucharist, in which the bread and wine are consecrated and become the Body and Blood of Christ. The words of institution — "This is My Body... This is the chalice of My Blood" — spoken by the priest *in persona Christi*, effect the transubstantiation. The faithful who are properly disposed receive Holy Communion, uniting themselves to the sacrifice and to one another as members of Christ's Mystical Body.

The feast of Corpus Christi (the Body and Blood of Christ), instituted by Pope Urban IV in 1264, is the Church's solemn celebration of the Eucharist outside the Paschal Triduum. Its traditional sequence, the *Lauda Sion*, composed by St. Thomas Aquinas, expounds the theology of the Eucharist in verse. The Maundy Thursday Mass of the Lord's Supper, with its distinctive rite of the washing of feet (the *Mandatum*), commemorates the institution of both the Eucharist and the priesthood. The Eucharist is reserved in the tabernacle for the communion of the sick and for the adoration of the faithful, who may visit the Blessed Sacrament at any time for private prayer — a practice that grows directly from the Kempian insistence that "to be without Jesus is the nethermost hell, and to be with Jesus is sweet paradise" (033-013).
