# Discernment

**Discernment** (*discretio*) in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* is the spiritual capacity to distinguish good from evil and truth from falsehood. Thomas à Kempis treats it as a natural power of reason — a "spark lying hid in the ashes" of fallen nature — that survives the corruption of sin but requires [[grace|divine grace]] to be restored to its full function. Discernment is not esoteric or extraordinary; it is the ordinary operation of sanctified reason in the spiritual life.

## The Remnant of Natural Discernment

Kempis presents discernment as a faculty that survives the Fall, though in a weakened and diminished state. The natural reason, though corrupted, retains a genuine capacity to recognize moral truth:

> "For the little power which remaineth is as it were one spark lying hid in the ashes. This is Natural reason itself, encompassed with thick clouds, having yet a discernment of good and evil, a distinction of the true and the false, though it be powerless to fulfil all that it approveth, and possess not yet the full light of truth, nor healthfulness of its affections" [092-019]

This passage offers a remarkably precise account of the state of natural discernment after the Fall. It can still *recognize* good and evil — the faculty of moral perception is not destroyed — but it cannot *fulfill* what it perceives. The light is real but dim; the affections are disordered; the will lacks the power to execute what the mind approves.

The image of the "spark lying hid in the ashes" is particularly evocative. A spark is real fire, capable of ignition — but buried in ashes, it gives neither light nor heat. [[grace|Grace]] does not replace this spark but blows upon it, clearing away the ashes and kindling it into flame.

## Discernment, Grace, and the Interior Conflict

The relationship between discernment and grace is central to Kempis's anthropology. The natural power of discernment, unaided, can perceive the good but cannot pursue it effectively:

> "Hence it cometh to pass that I recognize the way of perfectness, and see very clearly what things I ought to do; but pressed down by the weight of my own corruption, I rise not to the things which are more perfect" [092-026]

This is the tragic situation of the [[nature|natural man]]: clear sight without effective power. Discernment without grace becomes a torment rather than a help — the soul sees the good, knows the good, approves the good, and yet cannot attain it.

The remedy is not to abandon discernment but to supply it with grace. Kempis prays for "Thy most holy grace assist me, fervently poured into my heart" — grace that does not bypass the natural faculties but elevates and empowers them. Under grace, discernment becomes not merely the ability to see the good but the beginning of the capacity to pursue it.

## Discernment and the Law

Discernment operates in concert with the [[law|divine law]]. While the law provides the objective standard, discernment is the subjective faculty that applies it:

> "Hence it is, O my God, that I delight in Thy law after the inward man, knowing that Thy commandment is holy and just and good; reproving also all evil, and the sin that is to be avoided" [092-026]

The "inward man" delights in the law because discernment recognizes its goodness. The same faculty that distinguishes good from evil also recognizes the [[law|divine law]] as the supreme expression of the good. Discernment is thus not autonomous — it is ordered toward and completed by the revealed [[law|law of God]].

## Practical Discernment

While the most developed treatment of discernment in the text concerns its metaphysical status, the practical dimension is present throughout. The counsel to "cautiously and patiently try the matter, whether it be of God" (Chapter IV) is an exercise of discernment. The exhortation to take counsel with the wise rather than following one's own inventions presupposes that discernment is sharpened by community and tradition, not exercised in isolation.

The practice of discernment requires humility, for the proud are confident in their own judgment and do not submit it to [[temptation|testing]]. It requires patience, for discernment is slow — "cautiously and patiently." And it requires prayer, for only the light of grace can penetrate the "thick clouds" that surround natural reason.

## Biblical Foundation

The treatment of discernment in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* draws on the Pauline contrast between the "[[nature|natural man]]" who cannot receive the things of [[holy-spirit|the Spirit]] and the "spiritual man" who judges all things (1 Corinthians 2:14-15). The Epistle to the Hebrews speaks of those "who by reason of use have their senses exercised to discern both good and evil" (Hebrews 5:14), linking discernment to practice and maturity. John's exhortation to "try the spirits whether they are of God" (1 John 4:1) provides the explicit command for the exercise of discernment in the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life.
