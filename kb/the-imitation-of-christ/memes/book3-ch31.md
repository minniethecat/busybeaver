[068-001] Of the neglect of every creature, that the Creator may be found

[068-002] O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], I still need more grace, if I would arrive where neither man

[068-003] nor any other creature may hinder me. For so long as anything keepeth

[068-004] me back, I cannot freely fly unto Thee. He desired eagerly thus to fly,

[068-005] who cried, saying, Oh that I had wings like a dove, for then would I

[068-006] flee away and be at rest. What is more peaceful than the single eye?

[068-007] And what more free than he that desireth nothing upon earth? Therefore

[068-008] must a man rise above every creature, and perfectly forsake himself,

[068-009] and with abstracted mind to stand and behold that Thou, the Creator of

[068-010] all things, hast among Thy [[creation|creatures]] nothing like unto Thyself. And

[068-011] except a man be freed from all creatures, he will not be able to reach

[068-012] freely after Divine things. Therefore few are found who give themselves

[068-013] to contemplation, because few know how to separate themselves entirely

[068-014] from perishing and created things.

[068-015] 2. For this much grace is necessary, which may lift up the soul and

[068-016] raise it above itself. And except a man be lifted up in the spirit, and

[068-017] freed from all creatures, and altogether united to God, whatsoever he

[068-018] knoweth, whatsoever even he hath, it mattereth but little. He who

[068-019] esteemeth anything great save the one only incomprehensible, eternal,

[068-020] good, shall long time be little and lie low. For whatsoever is not God

[068-021] is nothing, and ought to be counted for nothing. Great is the

[068-022] difference between a godly man, illuminated with wisdom, and a scholar

[068-023] learned in knowledge and given to books. Far nobler is that doctrine

[068-024] which floweth down from the divine fulness above, than that which is

[068-025] acquired laboriously by human study.

[068-026] 3. Many are found who desire contemplation, but they do not strive to

[068-027] practice those things which are required thereunto. It is also a great

[068-028] impediment, that much is made of symbols and external signs, and too

[068-029] little of thorough mortification. I know not how it is, and by what

[068-030] spirit we are led, and what we who would be deemed spiritual are aiming

[068-031] at, that we give so great labour and so eager solicitude for transitory

[068-032] and worthless things, and scarcely ever gather our senses together to

[068-033] think at all of our inward condition.

[068-034] 4. Ah, me! Forthwith after a little recollection we rush out of doors,

[068-035] and do not subject our actions to a strict examination. Where our

[068-036] affections are set we take no heed, and we weep not that all things

[068-037] belonging to us are so defiled. For because all flesh had corrupted

[068-038] itself upon the earth, the great deluge came. Since therefore our

[068-039] inmost affections are very corrupt, it followeth of necessity that our

[068-040] actions also are corrupt, being the index of a deficient inward

[068-041] strength. Out of a pure heart proceedeth the fruit of good living.

[068-042] 5. We demand, how much a man hath done; but from how much virtue he

[068-043] acted, is not so narrowly considered. We ask if he be strong, rich,

[068-044] handsome, clever, whether he is a good writer, good singer, good

[068-045] workman; but how poor he may be in spirit, how patient and gentle, how

[068-046] devout and meditative, on these things many are silent. [[nature|Nature]] looketh

[068-047] upon the outward appearance of a man, grace turneth its thought to the

[068-048] heart. The former frequently judgeth amiss; the latter trusteth in God,

[068-049] that it may not be deceived.
