# Aaron

Aaron, the elder brother of Moses, was the first High Priest of Israel and the founder of the Aaronic priesthood. As Moses' spokesman before Pharaoh and the mediator of the sacrificial system, Aaron stands at the head of the Old Testament priestly tradition.

## Biblical Narrative

Aaron first appears in Exodus when God appoints him as Moses' spokesman: "Is not Aaron the Levite thy brother? I know that he can speak well" (Exodus 4:14). He accompanied Moses before Pharaoh, and his rod was among those instruments through which God worked the plagues of Egypt. At Mount Sinai, Aaron was consecrated as the first High Priest of Israel (Exodus 28:1–4; Leviticus 8:1–13), charged with offering sacrifices for the sins of the people and maintaining the sanctuary. His priesthood established the pattern of mediatorial worship that structured Israel's religious life until the destruction of the Temple.

### Key Events

- **Spokesman for Moses** — Aaron spoke on Moses' behalf before Pharaoh and the Israelites (Exodus 4:14–16).
- **The Golden Calf** — While Moses was on Sinai, Aaron yielded to the people's demand and fashioned the golden calf (Exodus 32:1–6), a failure that underscores the weakness even of consecrated leaders.
- **The Budding Rod** — God confirmed Aaron's priestly authority when his rod alone budded, blossomed, and bore almonds among the twelve tribal staffs (Numbers 17:1–11).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Aaron is not mentioned by name in The Imitation of Christ. His significance for the Kempian reader lies in his typological relationship to Christ, the true and eternal High Priest. The Aaronic priesthood, with its animal sacrifices, its consecrated vestments, and its annual Day of Atonement, was "a shadow of good things to come" (Hebrews 10:1).

When the disciple approaches the Eucharist, he approaches not an Aaronic sacrifice but the Body and Blood of Christ — the substance of which Aaron's ministry was only the type. The Imitation sets the tone for this reverence by recalling how the ancient priesthood embodied the dignity of sacred office:

> Great is the dignity of the priests, to whom is given that which is not granted unto angels [101-007].

> Consider therefore the dignity of the priest, and of the office of the priest [101-001].

The reverence with which Moses and Aaron approached the holy things of the tabernacle becomes, in Thomas à Kempis' hands, a measure of how much more reverently the Christian ought to approach the Sacrament:

> In the old law were consecrated only by one priest in the whole world, with what reverence thinkest thou that so great a mystery was then celebrated? [097-114]

The Imitation also reminds the disciple that even consecrated leaders face trials, for the world opposes the priestly life:

> He patiently suffered even God's chief priest, Sixtus, to be slain [034-015].

This pattern — the priest set apart yet suffering — mirrors Aaron's own story of dignity and fall, and points the disciple toward [[humility]] in every sacred calling.

## In the Christian Tradition

Aaron's priesthood has been a rich source of typological reflection throughout Christian history. The Epistle to the Hebrews provides the definitive New Testament interpretation: Aaron's priesthood was provisional and preparatory, yielding to the eternal priesthood of Christ "after the order of Melchizedek" (Hebrews 7:11). The Aaronic high priest entered the Holy of Holies once a year with the blood of goats and calves; Christ entered the heavenly sanctuary once for all with His own blood (Hebrews 9:11–12).

The Church Fathers developed this typology extensively. St. Ambrose of Milan, in his treatise *On the Duties of the Clergy*, presented Aaron as the pattern of priestly dignity and the golden calf episode as a warning that even the consecrated are vulnerable to the people's pressure. St. John Chrysostom, in his homilies on Hebrews, dwelt on the contrast between Aaron's silent submission when his sons Nadab and Abihu were struck down (Leviticus 10:3) and the disciple's calling to accept divine chastisement without murmuring — a theme that resonates deeply with The Imitation's teaching on [[patience]] and [[resignation]].

In medieval devotional tradition, Aaron's budding rod (Numbers 17:8) was read as a type of the Virgin Mary — the rod that blossomed without being planted, just as Mary bore Christ without human seed. This Marian reading, found in St. Bernard of Clairvaux and later writers, connects Aaron's priestly symbol to the mystery of the Incarnation, giving the Old Testament type a Christological and Marian fulfilment that the Kempian reader would have recognised.

For the reader of The Imitation, Aaron's legacy in Christian tradition reinforces two interrelated lessons. The dignity of priestly office is real — Thomas à Kempis devotes an entire book (Book IV) to reverence for the Sacrament — but it is conferred by God, not achieved by the minister's virtue. And the fallibility of even the consecrated leader points the disciple away from trust in human office toward trust in Christ alone, the one High Priest who "is able also to save them to the uttermost that come unto God by him" (Hebrews 7:25).

## Spiritual Significance

Aaron exemplifies both the dignity and the frailty of the priestly vocation. As the consecrated mediator, he was set apart for sacred service; yet his failure at the golden calf demonstrates that no merely human priest is sufficient. The Christian tradition reads Aaron's priesthood as pointing beyond itself to Christ, the High Priest "holy, harmless, undefiled, separate from sinners" (Hebrews 7:26), who alone offers the perfect sacrifice.

For the reader of The Imitation, Aaron's story offers two lessons. First, the greatness of sacred office demands corresponding greatness of [[humility]] and [[obedience]] — the priest who forgets he is a servant courts his own fall. Second, the Aaronic priesthood was always provisional; it prepared for and yielded to the eternal priesthood of Christ, in whom alone the disciple finds access to God.
