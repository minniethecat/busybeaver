# Death

The inevitable termination of mortal life, presented in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] under a twofold aspect: as a solemn object of daily meditation that impels the soul toward amendment and detachment, and as the interior principle of "dying to self" whereby the [[discipleship|disciple]] is freed from self-love and conformed to the Crucified — in both senses, death is not the end but the threshold of [[eternity|eternal life]].

## Definition

In The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], death is treated not as a remote biological terminus but as a present spiritual reality confronting every soul. Thomas a Kempis develops the concept along two inseparable axes. First, physical death is the universal and uncertain event that awaits every mortal: "Thus cometh death to all, and the life of men swiftly passeth away like a shadow" (023-063). The [[discipleship|disciple]] is commanded to keep the hour of death always before his eyes, living each day as if it were the last. Second, "dying to [[world|the world]]" is the ascetical and mystical principle by which the soul renounces self-will, worldly attachments, and inordinate affections: "Learn now to die to [[world|the world]], so shalt thou begin to live with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (023-052). This interior death is the condition of authentic spiritual life: "Behold everything dependeth upon the Cross, and everything lieth in dying; and there is none other way unto life and to true [[peace|inward peace]], except the way of the [[cross|Holy Cross]] and of [[mortification|daily mortification]]" (037-023).

The two themes converge in the person of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], who "went before thee bearing His Cross and died for thee upon the Cross, that thou also mayest bear thy cross and mayest love to be crucified upon it" (037-018). Death, whether physical or mystical, is thus never an end in itself but a passage — through the Cross to the resurrection, through dying to self to living unto God.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Death pervades The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as one of its most urgent and pastorally charged themes. Thomas a Kempis dedicates an entire chapter — Book I, Chapter XXIII, "Of Meditation upon Death" — to the subject, while the theme recurs throughout the work in connection with judgment, the Cross, and the call to interior renunciation.

### Meditation upon Death

Book I, Chapter XXIII opens with a bracing reminder of mortality: "Very quickly will there be an end of thee here; take heed therefore how it will be with thee in another world. To-day man is, and to-morrow he will be seen no more" (023-002). Thomas laments "the dulness and hardness of man's heart, which thinketh only of the present, and looketh not forward to the future" (023-006), and enjoins the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "in every deed and thought so to order thyself, as if thou wert to die this day" (023-007).

The practice of keeping death before the eyes is presented not as morbid preoccupation but as the foundation of spiritual seriousness. "Happy is the man who hath the hour of his death always before his eyes, and daily prepareth himself to die" (023-019). This daily preparation consists in the concrete practices of the devout life: contempt of [[world|the world]], fervent desire for virtue, love of discipline, repentance, obedience, self-denial, and [[obedience|submission]] to [[suffering|adversity]] — "these are the things which shall give great confidence of a happy death" (023-034).

Thomas underscores the uncertainty of the hour with vivid examples: "How many have been deceived, and suddenly have been snatched away from the body! How many times hast thou heard how one was slain by the sword, another was drowned, another falling from on high broke his neck, another died at the table, another whilst at play!" (023-061). The conclusion is inexorable: "Thus cometh death to all, and the life of men swiftly passeth away like a shadow" (023-063). Against this background, the only wise course is preparation: "Always be thou prepared, and so live that death may never find thee unprepared" (023-024).

Meditation upon death is not merely personal but profoundly realist about human forgetfulness: "Who will remember thee after thy death? And who will entreat for thee?" (023-065). The [[discipleship|disciple]] is urged to "make to thyself friends, by venerating the saints of God and walking in their steps, that when thou failest, thou mayest be received into everlasting habitations" (023-070). The chapter closes by framing the whole [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life as pilgrimage: "Keep thyself as a stranger and a pilgrim upon the earth, to whom the things of [[world|the world]] appertain not" (023-073), directing all prayer toward the end of passing "happily after death unto its [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (023-077).

### Death and Judgment

Death is inseparable from judgment in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], and the two are treated in immediate succession — Book I, Chapter XXIV (on judgment and the punishment of the wicked) follows directly upon Chapter XXIII (on meditation upon death). The conjunction is deliberate: death is the door to judgment, and the remembrance of judgment lends death its moral urgency.

Thomas asks pointedly, "If even unto this day thou hadst ever lived in honours and pleasures, what would the whole profit thee if now death came to thee in an instant?" (024-072). The answer is stark: "All therefore is vanity, save to love God and to serve Him only" (024-073). The one who has ordered his life toward God need not fear: "He who loveth God with all his heart feareth not death, nor punishment, nor judgment, nor hell, because perfect love giveth sure access to God" (024-074). Conversely, "he who still delighteth in sin, no marvel if he is afraid of death and judgment" (024-077).

This intimate coupling of death and judgment is reflected also in the practical counsel of Book I, Chapter XXI: "If thou thoughtest upon thy death more often than how long thy life should be, there is no doubt but thou wouldst be more zealous to amend" (021-039). The fear of death, though imperfect, is pedagogically useful: "Nevertheless it is a good thing, if love as yet cannot restrain thee from evil, that at least the fear of hell should hold thee back" (024-078).

### Dying to Self and the Remembrance of Last Things

The second great dimension of death in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is the interior death — the "dying to self" that constitutes the essence of [[discipleship|disciple]]ship. This theme reaches its fullest expression in Book II, Chapter XII, on the Royal Way of the [[cross|Holy Cross]]. Thomas declares with lapidary force: "Behold everything dependeth upon the Cross, and everything lieth in dying; and there is none other way unto life and to true [[peace|inward peace]], except the way of the [[cross|Holy Cross]] and of [[mortification|daily mortification]]" (037-023).

The [[discipleship|disciple]] is summoned to a continual mortification of self-will: "Know thou of a surety that thou oughtest to lead the life of a dying man. And the more a man dieth to himself, the more he beginneth to live towards God" (037-124). This dying is not a single dramatic renunciation but a daily discipline: "If thou settest thyself to that thou oughtest, namely, to suffer and to die, it shall soon go better with thee, and thou shalt find peace" (037-110).

The mystical dimension of this death is explicitly [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ological. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] "died for thee upon the Cross, that thou also mayest bear thy cross and mayest love to be crucified upon it. For if thou be dead with Him, thou shalt also live with Him" (037-018). Dying to self is thus a participation in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own death, the necessary prelude to sharing in His resurrection. The language of Book I, Chapter XXIII anticipates this: "Learn now to die to [[world|the world]], so shalt thou begin to live with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Learn now to contemn all earthly things, and then mayest thou freely go unto [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (023-052).

Thomas also warns of the converse: spiritual death that follows from attachment to sin. In Book III, the soul is cautioned that "because men rather follow their own pleasures than My will, and are eager to flatter the flesh even unto the end of this corruptible life, they incur death of the soul" (049-030). The choice is between two deaths — the voluntary death to self that leads to life, and the death of the soul that follows from clinging to sin.

The remembrance of death belongs to the broader tradition of the "four last things" — death, judgment, heaven, and hell — and Thomas integrates these elements throughout the work. In Book III, Chapter XLVII, the [[discipleship|disciple]] is reminded that the hope of [[eternity|eternal life]] transforms the present experience of death and suffering: "Thou shalt not say then, Who shall deliver me from the body of this death? because death will be utterly destroyed, and there shall be salvation which can never fail" (084-016). The cry of [[apostle-paul|St. Paul]] in Romans 7:24 becomes, through the lens of The Imitation, a cry answered by the promise of resurrection. The eschatological horizon of death is everywhere present: the spirit that passes "happily after death unto its [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (023-077) is the spirit that has learned, through [[mortification|daily mortification]] and the remembrance of death, to keep itself "as a stranger and a pilgrim upon the earth" (023-073).

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of death in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is deeply rooted in Scripture. The memento mori tradition draws from Ecclesiastes 7:2 — "It is better to go to the house of mourning than to the house of feasting, for this is the end of all mankind, and the living will lay it to heart" — which provides the biblical warrant for meditation upon death as a spiritual discipline. Hebrews 9:27 — "It is appointed unto men once to die, but after this the judgment" — anchors the inseparable pairing of death and judgment that structures Book I, Chapters XXIII and XXIV.

The warning of Matthew 24:44 — "Therefore be ye also ready: for in such an hour as ye think not the Son of Man cometh" — is explicitly cited in the chapter on meditation upon death (023-026), linking the hour of death with the eschatological coming of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Romans 6:23 — "The wages of sin is death; but the gift of God is [[eternity|eternal life]] through [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] our [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" — undergirds the dual theme of spiritual death through sin and [[eternity|eternal life]] through grace.

The Pauline language of dying and rising with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — "if we be dead with Him, we shall also live with Him" (037-020) — echoes Romans 6:8 and 2 Timothy 2:11-12, providing the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ological foundation for the interior death to self. The cry "Who shall deliver me from the body of this death?" (084-016) is a direct quotation of Romans 7:24, placed in the context of the promise that "death will be utterly destroyed" (084-018). The pilgrim identity — "here have we no continuing city" (023-075) — draws on Hebrews 13:14, framing the whole of mortal life as a journey toward the true homeland.
