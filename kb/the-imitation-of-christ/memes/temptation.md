# Temptation

The inevitable spiritual trial or testing by which God permits the soul to be proved, purified, and instructed — an inescapable condition of mortal life that, when endured with patience and humility, becomes a means of growth in virtue and deeper dependence upon [[grace|divine grace]].

## Definition

Temptation, in the theology of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not merely the solicitation to sin but the comprehensive condition of spiritual testing that characterizes human existence after the Fall. Thomas à Kempis grounds its inevitability in the very constitution of fallen [[nature|human nature]]: "we have the root of temptation within ourselves, in that we are born in concupiscence" (013-017). Because of this indwelling source, "there is no man wholly free from temptations so long as he liveth" (013-016), and "there is no position so sacred, no place so secret, that it is without temptations and adversities" (013-014). Temptation is the universal lot of humanity, extending even to the saints — "All Saints have passed through much [[suffering|tribulation]] and temptation, and have profited thereby" (013-012). It is therefore neither a sign of divine [[desolation|abandonment]] nor of spiritual failure, but of the ordinary condition of the soul in via.

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] anchors its teaching on temptation in the wisdom and apostolic literature. The universality of trial is drawn from [[job|Job]]: "The life of man upon the earth is a trial" ([[job|Job]] 7:1, cited at 013-004). The divine promise of deliverance echoes 1 Corinthians 10:13: "with the temptation make a way to escape that we may be able to bear it" (013-057). The purifying function of temptation recalls the testing of faith in James 1:12-14 and the refinement imagery of the wisdom tradition. The [[jesus-christ|Lord]]'s [[prayer|Prayer]] petition "Lead us not into temptation" (Matthew 6:13) undergirds the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s constant recourse to prayer. The promised reward for those who overcome draws from Revelation 2:7 and 2:17: "To him that overcometh I will give to eat of the tree of life" (034-069) and "to him that overcometh is the hidden manna given" (072-011). The eschatological hope that relativizes present suffering is founded on Romans 8:18: "the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed in us" (072-028).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Temptation is one of the most extensively treated themes in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], receiving its own dedicated chapter in Book I (Chapter XIII, "Of Resisting Temptation") and recurring throughout Books II, III, and IV as a leitmotif of the spiritual life. Thomas à Kempis presents temptation not as an enemy to be eliminated but as a divinely permitted instrument of spiritual formation.

### The Necessity of Temptation

Thomas opens his treatment with a stark declaration of the universality of temptation: "So long as we live in [[world|the world]], we cannot be without trouble and trial" (013-002). This is not a counsel of pessimism but a sober reckoning with the human condition. Drawing on the book of [[job|Job]], he notes that "The life of man upon the earth is a trial" (013-004). Therefore, "each of us ought to give heed concerning trials and temptations, and watch unto prayer, lest the devil find occasion to deceive" (013-005). The vigilance required is perpetual, for "the devil never sleepeth, but goeth about seeking whom he may devour" (013-006).

The necessity of temptation is grounded in anthropology. The root of temptation lies within: "One temptation or sorrow passeth, and another cometh; and always we shall have somewhat to suffer, for we have fallen from perfect [[blessedness|happiness]]" (013-018). This is why flight from temptation is ultimately futile — "Many who seek to fly from temptations fall yet more deeply into them" (013-020). The only remedy is [[patience|endurance]]: "By flight alone we cannot overcome, but by [[patience|endurance]] and true humility we are made stronger than all our enemies" (013-022).

Book III, Chapter XXXV reinforces this teaching from the divine perspective. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] warns the [[discipleship|disciple]]: "My Son, thou art never secure in this life, but thy spiritual armour will always be needful for thee as long as thou livest. Thou dwellest among foes, and art attacked on the right hand and on the left" (072-002). There is, in this life, no safe harbour from temptation — and there was never meant to be: "If thou seek rest in this life, how then wilt thou attain unto the rest which is eternal?" (072-014).

### Resisting Temptation

Thomas provides both strategic and tactical counsel for the resistance of temptation. The foundational requirement is [[faith|trust in God]] rather than in one's own strength: "The beginning of all temptations to evil is instability of temper and want of [[faith|trust in God]]; for even as a ship without a helm is tossed about by the waves, so is a man who is careless and infirm of purpose tempted, now on this side, now on that" (013-031). Without a steady anchorage in God, the soul is at the [[forgiveness|mercy]] of every gust of suggestion.

The most critical practical counsel concerns the beginnings of temptation. Thomas urges rapid and decisive resistance: "we must watch, especially in the beginnings of temptation; for then is the foe the more easily mastered, when he is not suffered to enter within the mind, but is met outside the door as soon as he hath knocked" (013-037). He traces the psychology of temptation through five stages — "first cometh to the mind the simple suggestion, then the strong imagination, afterwards pleasure, evil affection, assent" (013-043) — and observes that "the longer a man delayeth his resistance, the weaker he groweth, and the stronger groweth the enemy against him" (013-047).

Surface resistance is useless without interior transformation: "He who only resisteth outwardly and pulleth not up by the root, shall profit little; nay, rather temptations will return to him the more quickly, and will be the more terrible" (013-024). True conquest comes not through violence or self-will but "little by little, through patience and [[patience|long-suffering]]... by the help of God" (013-026). In the midst of struggle, the [[discipleship|disciple]] should "often seek counsel; and deal not hardly with one who is tempted, but comfort and strengthen him" (013-028).

In Book III, [[jesus-christ|Christ]] instructs the afflicted [[discipleship|disciple]]: "It is temptation which vexeth thee, and a vain fear which terrifieth thee" (067-018). The remedy is not to respond to every suggestion with anxiety but to wait upon God with steady confidence: "Wait for Me; yea, wait; I will come and heal thee" (067-017).

### Temptation as Purification

Perhaps the most distinctive teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] on temptation is its instrumental value for spiritual purification. Far from being merely an evil to be avoided, temptation serves a providential function in God's pedagogy of the soul.

"As fire testeth iron, so doth temptation the upright man" (013-035). The fire of temptation reveals the true quality of the soul in a way that comfort and [[comfort|consolation]] cannot: "Oftentimes we know not what strength we have; but temptation revealeth to us what we are" (013-036). The discovery of one's hidden weaknesses is mortifying but salutary: "In temptations and troubles a man is proved, what progress he hath made, and therein is his reward the greater, and his virtue doth the more appear" (013-061).

This purifying function also serves as a safeguard against spiritual pride. Book I, Chapter XX observes that "it is very profitable unto many that they should not be without inward temptation, but should be frequently assaulted, lest they be over confident, lest they be indeed lifted up into pride" (020-028). In Book II, Chapter IX, this logic is applied to the rhythm of [[comfort|consolation]] and desolation. After [[comfort|spiritual comfort]] is given, "temptation followeth, lest he be lifted up because of the benefit" (034-072). The withdrawal of [[comfort|consolation]] and the onset of temptation are not signs of God's displeasure but of His wise governance of the soul.

Thomas states the principle with uncompromising clarity: "he is not worthy of the great vision of God, who, for God's sake, hath not been exercised by some temptation" (034-066). The [[patience|endurance]] of temptation is thus a necessary preparation for divine intimacy: "temptation is wont to go before as a sign of the comfort which shall follow, and heavenly comfort is promised to those who are proved by temptation" (034-067).

The practical outworking of this teaching is the counsel never to despair. "Therefore we ought not to despair when we are tempted, but the more fervently should cry unto God, that He will vouchsafe to help us in all our [[suffering|tribulation]]" (013-054). God's [[faith|faithfulness]] guarantees that He "will, as [[apostle-paul|St. Paul]] saith, with the temptation make a way to escape that we may be able to bear it" (013-057).

### The End of Temptation

For all its pedagogical value, temptation is not the soul's permanent condition. It is the lot of the pilgrim, not of the blessed. The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] directs the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s gaze beyond present struggle to the promised victory — "to him that overcometh is the hidden manna given, but great misery is reserved for the slothful" (072-011). The saints endured "manifold griefs, and divers temptations, and heavy desolations" (072-026) not because they enjoyed suffering but because they "[[trust-in-god|trust]]ed in God more than in themselves, knowing that the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed in us" (072-028). The final word on temptation is eschatological hope: the trial is real, but it is also temporary, and it serves an eternal weight of glory.
