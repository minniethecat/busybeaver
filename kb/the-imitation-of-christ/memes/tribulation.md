# Tribulation

The trials, sorrows, and adversities that befall the faithful — in The Imitation of Christ, tribulation is the crucible in which authentic love for Jesus is tested and purified, separating those who follow Him for consolations from those who bless Him equally in anguish.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, tribulation encompasses the full range of human adversity: sorrow, affliction, temptation, persecution, and the inner anguish of heart that accompanies spiritual trial. It is not a sign of divine abandonment but is understood within the framework of the cross — the [[discipleship|disciple]] who would share in Christ's glory must first share in His suffering.

Thomas à Kempis presents tribulation not as an anomaly in the spiritual life but as its ordinary condition. The soul that has never known tribulation has not yet learned to love Jesus for His own sake, since "He hath many seekers of comfort, but few of tribulation" (036-003).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats tribulation throughout all four books as a necessary dimension of Christian discipleship. Its presence tests the sincerity of love; its endurance forges patience and humility; its acceptance marks the passage from mercenary devotion to the pure love of God.

### The Fewness of Those Who Love the Cross

Book II, Chapter XI opens with a sharp contrast that defines tribulation's role in the spiritual life: "Jesus hath many lovers of His heavenly kingdom, but few bearers of His Cross. He hath many seekers of comfort, but few of tribulation" (036-003). Many follow Jesus for the loaves, few for the cup of His passion (036-005–007). This distinction is not merely rhetorical — it exposes the fundamental orientation of the soul.

The authentic disciple is distinguished precisely by their response to tribulation: "They who love Jesus for Jesus' sake, and not for any consolation of their own, bless Him in all tribulation and anguish of heart as in the highest consolation" (036-014). Even if Jesus should never grant consolation, "nevertheless they would always praise Him and always give Him thanks" (036-016). This is the threshold beyond which love is no longer mercenary.

### Tribulation as Divine Pedagogy

Book I, Chapter XII presents tribulation under the aspect of divine pedagogy: "It is good for us that we sometimes have sorrows and adversities, for they often make a man lay to heart that he is only a stranger and sojourner, and may not put his trust in any worldly thing" (012-002–004). Tribulation serves to detach the soul from worldly securities and reorient it toward God alone.

When a God-fearing person is "afflicted or tried or oppressed with evil thoughts, then he seeth that God is the more necessary unto him" (012-012–013). The experience of tribulation thus becomes a school of humility, teaching the soul its radical dependence on grace. Without it, the [[discipleship|disciple]] would be tempted to "put his trust in any worldly thing" (012-004).

### Patience and Humility in Adversity

Book III, Chapter LVII addresses the interior experience of tribulation with pastoral realism. Christ speaks to the soul that finds itself brave "so long as no adversity meeteth thee" — giving good counsel to others, "but when tribulation suddenly knocketh at thine own door, thy counsel and strength fail" (094-008–011). This sudden encounter with one's own frailty is not failure but revelation: "Consider thy great frailty, which thou dost so often experience in trifling matters; nevertheless, for thy soul's health these things are done" (094-012–013).

The practical counsel is modest: "If tribulation hath touched thee, yet let it not cast thee down nor entangle thee long. At the least, bear patiently, if thou canst not joyfully" (094-015–016). The goal is not heroic stoicism but patient endurance under the promise that "soon the storm which hath been raised shall be stilled, and inward grief shall be sweetened by returning grace" (094-020–021).

### Tribulation Endured for Eternal Life

Book III, Chapter XLVII places tribulation within an eschatological horizon: "Let not the labours which thou hast undertaken for Me break thee down, nor let tribulations cast thee down in any wise, but let My promise strengthen and comfort thee in every event" (084-002–003). The promise of eternal reward relativizes all present suffering: "Not long shalt thou labour here, nor always be weighed down with sorrows. Wait yet a little while, and thou shalt see a speedy end of thine evils" (084-006–007).

The [[discipleship|disciple]] is summoned to active endurance — "Write, read, sing, weep, be silent, pray, endure adversities manfully; eternal life is worthy of all these conflicts, yea, and of greater" (084-011–012). Tribulation is not merely to be suffered passively but to be engaged as the arena of spiritual combat whose prize is "light eternal, infinite clearness, steadfast peace, and undisturbed rest" (084-015).

### Tribulation and Consolation

The Imitation of Christ holds tribulation and consolation in dynamic tension. The [[discipleship|disciple]] prays: "What should I do in my so great tribulations and anxieties, unless Thou didst comfort me with Thy holy words?" (094-032–033). Consolation follows tribulation by divine rhythm — the Lord promises to "give thee more than wonted consolation if thou put thy trust in Me" (094-022–023). Yet the mature disciple no longer requires consolation as a condition of fidelity, blessing God equally in tribulation as in comfort (036-014).

### Key Passages

> "He hath many seekers of comfort, but few of tribulation. He findeth many companions of His table, but few of His fasting. All desire to rejoice with Him, few are willing to undergo anything for His sake." (036-003–005)

> "They who love Jesus for Jesus' sake, and not for any consolation of their own, bless Him in all tribulation and anguish of heart as in the highest consolation." (036-013–014)

> "When tribulation suddenly knocketh at thine own door, thy counsel and strength fail. Consider thy great frailty, which thou dost so often experience in trifling matters; nevertheless, for thy soul's health these things are done." (094-010–012)

> "What should I do in my so great tribulations and anxieties, unless Thou didst comfort me with Thy holy words? If only I may attain unto the haven of salvation, what matter is it what things or how many I suffer?" (094-032–034)

> "Not long shalt thou labour here, nor always be weighed down with sorrows. Wait yet a little while, and thou shalt see a speedy end of thine evils." (084-006–007)

## Biblical Foundation

The understanding of tribulation in The Imitation of Christ is rooted in the New Testament's consistent testimony. Christ Himself foretold tribulation as the disciples' portion: "In the world you will have tribulation; but be of good cheer, I have overcome the world" (John 16:33). Paul develops tribulation as a chain of spiritual formation: "tribulation produces perseverance; and perseverance, character; and character, hope" (Romans 5:3–4). The apostolic pattern is unambiguous — "through many tribulations we must enter the kingdom of God" (Acts 14:22).

James reframes tribulation as occasion for joy: "count it all joy when you fall into various trials, knowing that the testing of your faith produces patience" (James 1:2–3). And the God of all comfort "comforts us in all our tribulation, that we may be able to comfort those who are in any trouble" (2 Corinthians 1:4) — tribulation thus becomes not only a means of personal purification but the ground of mutual consolation within the Body of Christ.
