# Last Supper

The meal on the night before Christ's Passion when He instituted the Sacrament of His Body and Blood, commanding the Church to celebrate it in remembrance of Him. Recorded in all three Synoptic Gospels with a theological deepening in John's Gospel, the Last Supper is the scriptural foundation of the Eucharist (the Blessed Sacrament), which Book IV of *The Imitation of Christ* treats as the supreme gift of divine love.

## Biblical Account

The Last Supper took place on the evening before Christ's crucifixion, in an upper room in Jerusalem, during the Passover feast. The Synoptic Gospels (Matthew 26:17–30, Mark 14:12–26, Luke 22:7–20) narrate the institution:

> "Take, eat; this is My body. ... Drink ye all of it; for this is My blood of the new testament, which is shed for many for the remission of sins" (Matthew 26:26–28).

Jesus took bread, blessed and broke it, and gave it to His disciples, declaring it His Body. He then took the cup, gave thanks, and gave it to them, declaring it His Blood of the New Covenant. He commanded them, "This do in remembrance of Me" — words that ground the Church's perpetual celebration of the Eucharist (096-052). The Apostle Paul later reiterated this institution account as authoritative tradition received from the Lord (1 Corinthians 11:23–26).

### Gospel Accounts

All four Gospels record the events of that night, though with differing emphases:

- **Matthew (26:17–30)** and **Mark (14:12–26)** present the institution formula and Jesus' prediction of betrayal and denial.
- **Luke (22:7–38)** adds the dispute among the disciples about greatness, prompting Jesus' teaching on servant leadership, and the promise of the kingdom.
- **John (13–17)** omits the institution formula but provides the most extensive account: the washing of the disciples' feet, the farewell discourse, the promise of the Holy Spirit, and the high-priestly prayer — framing the meal as the supreme revelation of Christ's love and unity with the Father.

## Theological Significance

The Last Supper is the foundation of the Sacrament of the Altar. Christ, the true Passover Lamb, transformed the ancient Passover meal into the Eucharistic sacrifice. As Thomas a Kempis notes, the Eucharist is "the true sacrifice of Thy Body, the completion of all the ancient sacrifices" (097-057), fulfilling what the Old Testament prefigured in the Ark of the Covenant [(097-054)–058].

The event reveals several central Christian doctrines: the Real Presence of Christ in the consecrated elements; the sacrificial nature of the Eucharist as a participation in Calvary; the New Covenant sealed in Christ's Blood; and the commandment of love and unity among believers. The Eucharist is both a memorial ("this do in remembrance of Me" (096-052)) and a living encounter with the risen Christ.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Book IV, "On the Blessed Sacrament," is an extended meditation on the meaning of Christ's institution at the Last Supper and the proper disposition for receiving Holy Communion. Thomas a Kempis dwells on the immense condescension of God in offering Himself as food:

> "Thou, O Lord my God, very God and very man, givest Thyself altogether to us in a little bread and wine, and art so our inexhaustible food" (098-046).

He marvels that Christ should have "consecrated and instituted" this mystery "both for Thine own honour, and for a perpetual memorial" [(098-051)–052]. The devout soul is exhorted to approach Communion with the same reverence as if Christ "for the first time were descending into the Virgin's womb and becoming man, or hanging on the cross, suffering and dying for the salvation of mankind" [(098-063)–065].

The Eucharist is described as "the living bread, which didst descend from heaven; and givest life to the world" [(098-034)–035], an inexhaustible fountain of grace in which "lost virtue is regained in the soul" (097-099). Thomas treats it as a foretaste of the heavenly banquet: "the whole world has prepared a great supper, in which Thou hast set forth" (107-062) — an echo of the Last Supper's eschatological dimension.

The same event provokes the disciple's humility before so great a mystery: "But who am I, O Lord, that I should presume to approach unto Thee?" (097-020) — reflecting the tension between unworthiness and the divine invitation that pervades Book IV.

## Liturgical Connection

The Last Supper is commemorated on Maundy Thursday (Holy Thursday) of Holy Week, when the Church recalls the institution of the Eucharist and the priesthood. In every celebration of the Mass, the words of institution ("This is My Body ... This is My Blood") are pronounced, making present the same sacrifice Christ offered at the Last Supper and on the Cross. The feast of Corpus Christi (the Body and Blood of Christ) also flows from this foundational event.
