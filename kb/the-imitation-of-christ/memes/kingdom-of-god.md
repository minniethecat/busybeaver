# Kingdom of God

The sovereign reign of God over the human soul and over [[creation|all creation]] — at once a present inward reality, "within you" (Luke 17:21), and a future consummation toward which the whole [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life is directed as a pilgrim journey, forming the ultimate horizon of every spiritual teaching in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

The Kingdom of God in the Kempian framework is not a territorial domain but a relationship of loving [[obedience|submission]]. It is the state in which God reigns unopposed in the soul, and the eternal inheritance promised to those who persevere in that [[obedience|submission]] to the end. Thomas à Kempis holds these two dimensions — the present inward kingdom and the future heavenly kingdom — in constant tension: the [[discipleship|disciple]] already possesses the Kingdom insofar as [[jesus-christ|Christ]] dwells within (026-002), yet must still "wait for the kingdom of God" (086-034) with [[perseverance|patient endurance]]. The Kingdom is thus both the path and the destination, the seed and the harvest, the foretaste and the feast.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Kingdom of God appears throughout The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as the organizing purpose of the spiritual life. Every act of self-denial, every renunciation of worldly comfort, every humble [[obedience|submission]] to [[providence|divine providence]] is oriented toward this single goal: that the soul may belong wholly to God's Kingdom both now and for eternity. Thomas treats the Kingdom not as a distant abstraction but as the immediate, practical horizon of daily [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian living.

### The Kingdom Within

Book II, Chapter I opens with one of the most important statements in the entire work: "The kingdom of God is within you, saith the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (026-002, quoting Luke 17:21). Thomas immediately draws out the practical consequence: "Turn thee with all thine heart unto the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], and forsake this miserable world, and thy soul shall find rest" (026-003). The Kingdom is not somewhere else — it is the soul's interior when that interior is turned wholly toward God. This inwardness is the hallmark of the *devotio moderna*: the spiritual life is not primarily about external observances but about the purification and reorientation of the heart. "Learn to despise outward things, and to give thyself to things inward, and thou shalt perceive the kingdom of God to come into thee" (026-004).

This inward Kingdom brings with it "[[justice|righteousness]], and peace, and joy in the [[holy-spirit|Holy Ghost]]" (026-005, echoing Romans 14:17). These are not rewards to be waited for; they are the present possession of the soul in which God reigns. Yet Thomas never allows the [[discipleship|disciple]] to settle into complacent possession — the Kingdom within must be guarded, cultivated, and continually renewed through the practices of prayer, self-examination, and the frequent reception of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]].

### The Kingdom as Future Inheritance

While the Kingdom is present within, it is also "the kingdom which Thou hast prepared for Thine elect from the beginning" (085-026). The [[discipleship|disciple]] lives in the tension between the already and the not-yet. Book III contains some of the most poignant expressions of longing for the fullness of the Kingdom. The soul cries out: "Oh, when shall I be with Thee in Thy Kingdom which Thou hast prepared for Thy beloved from all eternity?" (085-026). This is not escapism but eschatological hope — the recognition that the present experience of grace, real as it is, is only a foretaste of what is to come. "There shall appear the morn, the dawn of light, the brightness of everlasting glory" (086-073).

The Kingdom's future dimension provides the motivational structure for the entire ascetical program of The Imitation. "For a little while, saith the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], and I will come and take you to Myself, that where I am, there ye may be also" (086-036). The brevity of earthly life, the fleeting nature of [[suffering|tribulation]], the relative lightness of the Cross — all are measured against the weight of the Kingdom that awaits. "He that followeth Me shall not walk in darkness, but shall have the light of life" (086-037).

### The Kingdom and the Eucharist

In Book IV, the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] becomes the privileged locus where the present Kingdom and the future Kingdom meet. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] promises: "I will drink it new with you in My Father's kingdom" (093-034, echoing Matthew 26:29). Every celebration of the Mass is an anticipation of the heavenly banquet. The soul that receives [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in the Sacrament receives the King Himself, and in that reception the Kingdom is not merely hoped for but tasted. "The Kingdom of God is within you" finds its most concrete realization at the altar, where the King comes to dwell bodily within the [[discipleship|disciple]].

Thomas exhorts the communicant to approach with the awareness that this Sacrament is a pledge of the Kingdom to come: "He that eateth My Flesh and drinketh My Blood dwelleth in Me and I in him" (094-036). The [[eucharist|Eucharist]] is the bridge between the present, partial possession of the Kingdom and its future, full consummation. The faithful soul who receives worthily may say with confidence: "The Kingdom of God is not meat and drink, but [[justice|righteousness]] and peace and joy in the [[holy-spirit|Holy Ghost]]" — and yet in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], meat and drink become the very means by which [[justice|righteousness]], peace, and joy are communicated.

> "The kingdom of God is within you, saith the Lord. Turn thee with all thine heart unto the Lord, and forsake this miserable world, and thy soul shall find rest." (026-002–003)

> "Learn to despise outward things, and to give thyself to things inward, and thou shalt perceive the kingdom of God to come into thee. For the kingdom of God is righteousness, and peace, and joy in the Holy Ghost." (026-004–005)

> "Wait for Me, until the kingdom of God shall come. Thou must be tried still in another tabernacle, and exercised in many things." (086-034)

## Biblical Foundation

The Kingdom of God is the central theme of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]' public preaching: "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (Matthew 4:17). Thomas inherits the full biblical theology of the Kingdom and transposes it into the key of personal devotion. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s teaching that "the kingdom of God is within you" (Luke 17:21) provides the warrant for the interior emphasis. The parables of the hidden treasure and the pearl of great price (Matthew 13:44–46) establish the Kingdom as worth everything — "for this is the way to the kingdom of heaven" (067-037). The [[apostle-paul|Apostle Paul]]'s definition — "the kingdom of God is not meat and drink, but [[justice|righteousness]], and peace, and joy in the [[holy-spirit|Holy Ghost]]" (Romans 14:17) — is quoted directly and made the measure of authentic spiritual experience (026-005). The eschatological promise — "Come, ye blessed of My Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you from the foundation of [[world|the world]]" (Matthew 25:34) — is the ground of the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s hope. And the vision of the New [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] (Revelation 21:1–4), where God dwells with His people and wipes away every tear, is the distant light toward which every soul on the royal [[cross|way of the Cross]] is traveling.

## Practical Application

The Kingdom of God is not merely a theological concept to be understood but a reality to be entered. Thomas presents the spiritual life as a continual movement toward the Kingdom, a "royal way of the holy Cross" (Book II, Chapter XII) that leads through self-denial to eternal glory. This has immediate practical implications. First, every decision is to be weighed against the standard of the Kingdom: "What doth this profit me for the kingdom of God?" The [[discipleship|disciple]] who asks this question honestly will find many attachments loosen their grip. Second, the awareness that "here we have no continuing city" (023-075) frees the soul from excessive attachment to earthly comforts, honors, and securities. Third, the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] — the pledge and foretaste of the Kingdom — becomes the centre of the devotional life, the point at which the present and future Kingdoms intersect most intimately. Fourth, the [[perseverance|patient endurance]] of [[suffering|tribulation]] is made possible by "waiting for the kingdom of God" (086-034) — every cross, every disappointment, every season of spiritual [[desolation|dryness]] is reframed as a step nearer to the Kingdom.
