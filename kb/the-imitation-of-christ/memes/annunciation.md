# Annunciation

The Annunciation is the biblical event in which the Angel Gabriel appeared to the Virgin Mary in Nazareth and declared that she would conceive by the Holy Spirit and bear the Son of God (Luke 1:26–38). Mary's response — "Behold the handmaid of the Lord; be it unto me according to thy word" — is the moment of the Incarnation and the supreme model of humble, faithful receptivity to the divine will.

## Biblical Account

The event is narrated in Luke 1:26–38. The Angel Gabriel, sent by God to the Galilean town of Nazareth, greets the Virgin Mary with the words "Hail, thou that art highly favoured, the Lord is with thee: blessed art thou among women" (Luke 1:28). Troubled by the greeting, Mary hears the angelic message: she will conceive a son by the power of the Holy Spirit, and he "shall be called the Son of the Highest" (Luke 1:32). Mary's question — "How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?" (Luke 1:34) — receives the answer that "the Holy Ghost shall come upon thee, and the power of the Highest shall overshadow thee" (Luke 1:35). Her final response is the fiat: unconditional surrender to God's will.

## Biblical Foundation

The Annunciation rests on Luke 1:26–38 as its sole scriptural account, a passage that stands at the threshold of the New Testament and fulfills the Old Testament prophecy of Isaiah 7:14: "Behold, a virgin shall conceive, and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel." The event is the hinge of salvation history — the moment when the eternal counsel of God enters time and space through the free consent of a humble virgin in an obscure Galilean village. The angelic salutation "Hail, full of grace" (Luke 1:28) indicates that Mary was uniquely prepared by divine [[grace]] for this vocation, while her question in Luke 1:34 demonstrates not doubt but a discerning [[faith]] that seeks to understand the manner of God's working. The [[holy-spirit|Holy Spirit]]'s overshadowing (Luke 1:35) echoes the Spirit brooding over the waters at creation (Genesis 1:2), marking the Annunciation as the beginning of the new creation in Christ.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas a Kempis evokes the Annunciation in Book IV, where the disciple preparing for Holy Communion recalls how the Virgin Mary "humbly and devoutly answered the Angel who brought unto her the glad tidings of the mystery of the Incarnation" [113-021–024]. The passage draws a parallel between Mary's reception of the Word into her womb and the communicant's reception of Christ in the Eucharist — both are acts of humble faith by which the divine presence enters the human sphere.

The role of the Angel Gabriel in the Annunciation reflects the broader teaching of the Imitation on the angelic life. Thomas holds the angels before the disciple as exemplars of purity, reverence, and unwavering devotion before God:

> …walk pure in His presence as do the angels. We ought daily to renew our vows, and to kindle our hearts to zeal… [019-006]

> …above Angels and Archangels and all the army of heaven, above all things visible and invisible… [058-012]

> …worthy to enter into the angelic choirs, having shut out from himself, both outwardly and inwardly, all worldly things. [085-068]

Mary's response to Gabriel exemplifies the [[humility]] that the Imitation places at the centre of the spiritual life. Thomas repeatedly teaches that humility is the foundation of all virtues, the disposition that makes the soul capable of receiving God. Mary does not exalt herself at the angel's greeting; she is troubled, she questions, and finally she surrenders — not in passive resignation but in active, trusting self-offering. This pattern of humble receptivity — being troubled by divine approach, seeking understanding, and then consenting in faith — is the very pattern the Imitation prescribes for every disciple who would hear and answer God's call.

Her fiat is also the supreme act of [[obedience]]. In the Kempian framework, obedience is not mere external compliance but the interior alignment of the will with God's will. Mary's "Be it unto me according to thy word" is the perfect expression of this obedience, and the Imitation holds it before every soul as the model response to whatever God commands. The Annunciation thus becomes the archetype of every faithful response to divine calling: the Word seeks entrance into each soul, and each soul is invited to give its own fiat.

## Spiritual Significance

The Annunciation exemplifies the virtue of humble obedience — Mary does not argue, demand proof, or negotiate; she simply consents. In the Kempian framework, this becomes the model for the disciple's response to divine grace: not self-assertion but receptive surrender. The event also establishes the pattern for sacramental life, where the Word continues to take flesh under humble forms through the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], received with Mary's own disposition of faith.
