# Trust

The virtue by which the soul turns away from every creaturely support — human opinion, worldly goods, natural abilities — and casts itself wholly upon the [[faith|faithfulness]] of God, who alone "remaineth for ever and standeth by us firmly even to the end" (026-021), in the teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Trust, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not a vague optimism or natural confidence but a definite supernatural virtue: the deliberate renunciation of all human and creaturely props in favour of sole reliance upon God. Thomas à Kempis opens his treatment of the subject in Book I, Chapter 7 — "Of Fleeing from Vain [[hope|Hope]] and [[pride|Pride]]" — with a stark declaration: "Vain is the life of that man who putteth his [[trust-in-god|trust]] in men or in any creature" (007-002). The word "vain" here carries its full biblical weight: such a life is empty, futile, built upon what cannot bear the weight placed upon it. Trust wrongly placed is not merely unwise but spiritually deadly, for it anchors the soul to what must inevitably fail. True [[trust-in-god|trust]] is the soul's anchor cast not upon the shifting sea of [[creation|creatures]] but upon the unmoving rock of God.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Trust is a pervasive theme throughout The Imitation, appearing in connection with hope, humility, patience, and the whole structure of the interior life. Thomas treats misplaced [[trust-in-god|trust]] as the root of spiritual instability, and well-placed [[trust-in-god|trust]] — full [[resignation|abandonment to God]] — as the summit of the soul's peace.

### The Vanity of Trust in Creatures

Thomas opens his chapter on vain hope with a comprehensive rejection of every creaturely object of [[trust-in-god|trust]]. Trust is not to be placed "in men or in any creature" (007-002), in one's own riches, or in powerful friends, "but in God, who giveth all things" (007-010). The sweep is total: nothing created can serve as a reliable foundation for the soul's confidence. The reason is both metaphysical and experiential. Metaphysically, [[creation|creatures]] are contingent and mutable — they come and go, arise and pass away — and the soul that rests upon them must share their instability. Experientially, Thomas observes that those "who to-day are on thy side, may to-morrow be against thee, and often are they turned round like the wind" (026-025 to 026-026). Human [[faith|fidelity]] is a broken reed; to lean upon it is to guarantee a fall.

Thomas extends this critique even to self-[[trust-in-god|trust]]. "Rest not upon thyself, but build thy hope in God" (007-005). And elsewhere: "Trust not too much to thine own opinion" (009-015). The self is the most insidious object of false [[trust-in-god|trust]] because it is the nearest — one's own understanding, virtue, or strength — and therefore the hardest to renounce. Yet the self, no less than other [[creation|creatures]], is contingent, fallible, and prone to change. The man who [[trust-in-god|trust]]s in himself has placed his confidence in the most treacherous of all [[creation|creatures]].

### Want of Trust as the Root of Instability

In one of the most striking images of The Imitation, Thomas identifies lack of [[faith|trust in God]] as the source of all spiritual instability: "The beginning of all temptations to evil is instability of temper and want of [[faith|trust in God]]; for even as a ship without a helm is tossed about by the waves, so is a man who is careless and infirm of purpose" (013-032 to 013-033). The ship without a helm is a powerful figure: it has sails and can move, but it has no direction, no governance, and is at the [[forgiveness|mercy]] of every wind and wave. [[trust-in-god|Trust in God]] is the soul's helm — the fixed point that gives direction and stability amid the storms of temptation. When [[trust-in-god|trust]] fails, the soul is not merely weakened but rendered directionless, vulnerable to every gust of passion and every assault of the enemy.

This connection between [[trust-in-god|trust]] and stability illuminates the whole Kempian program: the interior life is not first about doing but about [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing. All the ascetical practices — fasting, vigils, prayer — are secondary to this foundational disposition: "Do what lieth in thy power, and God will help thy good intent" (007-006). The soul that [[trust-in-god|trust]]s God acts from a place of confidence, not anxiety; it does what it can and leaves the outcome to God. This is the opposite of the anxious, self-reliant striving that Thomas repeatedly warns against.

### Full Abandonment to God

The summit of [[trust-in-god|trust]], in Thomas's teaching, is total [[desolation|abandonment]]: the soul "committeth himself altogether and with full [[trust-in-god|trust]] unto God, who is all in all to him, to whom nothing perisheth nor dieth, but all things live" (025-082 to 025-083). This is [[trust-in-god|trust]] raised to its highest power — not [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing God for this or that particular outcome, but [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing God with one's whole being, past, present, and future, in life and in death. The one who reaches this degree of [[trust-in-god|trust]] "will be well contented with whatsoever shall happen unto him" and "will neither rejoice for much nor be sorrowful for little" (025-080 to 025-081). This is the peace that passes understanding — not the peace of having everything one wants, but the deeper peace of having ceased to want anything but God's will.

The model of such [[trust-in-god|trust]] is [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself, who "remaineth for ever and standeth by us firmly even to the end" (026-021). The immutability of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is the ground of [[trust-in-god|trust]]: because He does not change, the soul that [[trust-in-god|trust]]s in Him shares in His stability. "There is no great [[trust-in-god|trust]] to be placed in a frail and mortal man, even though he be useful and dear to us" (026-022). The contrast is absolute: every human object of [[trust-in-god|trust]] is "frail and mortal"; God alone is eternal and faithful. To [[trust-in-god|trust]] God fully is therefore not recklessness but the highest reasonableness — it is to build upon the only foundation that cannot be shaken.

### Trust and the Pilgrim Condition

Thomas frames [[trust-in-god|trust]] within the larger theme of man's pilgrim state on earth. The soul is "a stranger and sojourner, and may not put his [[trust-in-god|trust]] in any worldly thing" (012-004). The pilgrim does not build a permanent house in the country through which he passes; he travels light, [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing in the destination and the One who called him there. Trust in [[world|worldly things]] is not merely a mistake but a category error — it treats the temporary lodging as though it were the eternal home. "It is good that we sometimes endure contradictions, and are hardly and unfairly judged" (012-005), because these [[temptation|trial]]s remind the soul of its pilgrim condition and redirect [[trust-in-god|trust]] from [[world|the world]] back to God.

## Key Passages

> "Vain is the life of that man who putteth his trust in men or in any creature. Be not ashamed to be the servant of others for the love of Jesus Christ, and to be reckoned poor in this life. Rest not upon thyself, but build thy hope in God." (007-002 to 007-005)

> "Trust not in thine own knowledge, nor in the cleverness of any that lives, but rather trust in the favour of God, who resisteth the proud and giveth grace to the humble." (007-007 to 007-008)

> "The beginning of all temptations to evil is instability of temper and want of trust in God; for even as a ship without a helm is tossed about by the waves, so is a man who is careless and infirm of purpose." (013-032 to 013-033)

> "He committeth himself altogether and with full trust unto God, who is all in all to him, to whom nothing perisheth nor dieth, but all things live and obey His every word without delay." (025-082 to 025-083)

> "Put not thy trust in men, for men soon change and swiftly pass away, but Christ remaineth for ever and standeth by us firmly even to the end. There is no great trust to be placed in a frail and mortal man, even though he be useful and dear to us." (026-020 to 026-022)

> "They who to-day are on thy side, may to-morrow be against thee, and often are they turned round like the wind. Put thy whole trust in God and let Him be thy fear and thy love." (026-025 to 026-026)

## Biblical Foundation

The Kempian theology of [[trust-in-god|trust]] is deeply rooted in Scripture. The warning against [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing in men echoes the Psalmist: "It is better to [[trust-in-god|trust]] in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] than to put confidence in man. It is better to [[trust-in-god|trust]] in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] than to put confidence in princes" (Psalm 118:8–9). The image of the man who [[trust-in-god|trust]]s God as a well-rooted tree — implicit in Thomas's ship-without-a-helm metaphor — draws from Jeremiah 17:5–8: "Cursed be the man that [[trust-in-god|trust]]eth in man... Blessed is the man that [[trust-in-god|trust]]eth in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]." Proverbs 3:5–6 supplies the classic formulation: "Trust in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] with all thine heart, and lean not unto thine own understanding."

The immutability of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as the ground of [[trust-in-god|trust]] recalls Hebrews 13:8: "[[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] the same yesterday, and to-day, and for ever." The call to total [[desolation|abandonment]] — committing oneself "altogether and with full [[trust-in-god|trust]] unto God" — echoes Psalm 37:5: "Commit thy way unto the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]; [[trust-in-god|trust]] also in Him, and He shall bring it to pass." Even the pilgrim motif is biblical: the faithful are "strangers and pilgrims on the earth" (Hebrews 11:13), seeking a better country and putting no ultimate [[trust-in-god|trust]] in the cities of this world.
