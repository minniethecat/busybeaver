# Lukewarmness

**Lukewarmness** (*tepor*, also rendered "tepidity") is the spiritual condition of being neither hot nor cold — the great adversary of [[fervor|fervent devotion]] in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]*. Thomas à Kempis treats lukewarmness not as a minor [[sin|fault]] but as the most dangerous state of the spiritual life: it is the [[silence|quiet]] death of the soul, in which religious practice continues outwardly while the interior fire has gone out.

## The State of Lukewarmness

The text's diagnosis of lukewarmness is unsparing in its accuracy. Kempis traces it to negligence in small things:

> And so we remain lukewarm and unspiritual [011-015]

This sentence follows a passage about tolerating "a single [[sin|fault]]" and failing to be "zealous for daily growth." Lukewarmness does not arrive suddenly — it accumulates through small omissions, unexamined habits, and the gradual cooling of first fervor. The soul that was once eager in prayer now prays from custom; the soul that once hungered for virtue now contents itself with avoiding gross sin.

The text captures the self-deception of the lukewarm: "We look upon the lives of the holy fathers, and we admire their virtues, but we think it impossible for us to imitate them" (018-038). The lukewarm soul admires sanctity from a safe distance, convinced that such fervor is for others — for the exceptional few — while resigning itself to mediocrity as the normal condition.

## The Danger of Lukewarmness

Kempis's warnings about lukewarmness carry an eschatological urgency:

> Woe unto us, if we are lukewarm and idle! Woe unto us, if we desire to rest here in this present life and will not labor for the life to come [022-063]

The lukewarm are not necessarily conscious sinners — they may be outwardly observant, even regular in their religious duties — but they have made peace with [[world|the world]]. They "desire to rest here" — to enjoy the comforts of this life while maintaining the minimum of religion necessary to [[silence|quiet]] conscience. This is the "broad way" that leads to destruction, disguised as moderation.

The internal experience of lukewarmness is described with clinical precision: "When a man begins to grow lukewarm, then he fears a little labor" (029-021). The first symptom of cooling fervor is aversion to effort. What was once done with eagerness now feels burdensome; the thought of extra prayer, of fasting, of self-denial, provokes resistance rather than desire.

## Lukewarmness and the Cross

The text draws a direct connection between lukewarmness and the avoidance of the cross:

> Behold we are still lukewarm [055-030]

This exclamation comes in the context of comparing the saints' heroic [[patience|endurance]] with the reader's comfortable religion. The saints embraced crosses; the lukewarm arrange their lives to avoid them. The saints sought suffering for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]; the lukewarm seek to preserve themselves from every inconvenience. The cross — essential to [[discipleship|disciple]]ship — becomes impossible for the tepid soul.

## The Confession of Lukewarmness

One of the most striking passages is the soul's own confession of its condition:

> Without Thy grace, I am altogether lukewarm and reckless [077-009]

This is not self-justification but honest acknowledgment: the soul admits that left to itself, it sinks into tepidity. The remedy is not self-generated fervor but dependence on [[grace|divine grace]]. The humble admission of lukewarmness is paradoxically the beginning of its cure — for the proud soul that denies its tepidity will never seek the fire that alone can rekindle it.

The text also records the sorrow of the penitent: "We mourn and lament over our lukewarmness" (097-103). This godly sorrow — the recognition that one has settled for far less than what God deserves — is the first movement of genuine conversion from tepidity back to fervor.

## From Lukewarmness to Fervor

Kempis does not leave the lukewarm without hope. The remedy is deliberate and practical: recognize the danger, renew one's resolution, return to the practices that kindle devotion — prayer, Scripture, [[conscience|examination of conscience]], the [[eucharist|Eucharist]]. The text asks sharply: "If I now be so negligent and lukewarm, what should I become in time of [[suffering|tribulation]]?" (099-033). This honest self-interrogation is the beginning of amendment.

The call is clear: "Be zealous, therefore, for thy spiritual profit" (025-004). [[zeal|Zeal]] is the deliberate antonym of lukewarmness — the eager, energetic pursuit of God that refuses the comfortable middle ground.

## Biblical Foundation

The primary scriptural foundation is [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s words to the church of Laodicea: "I know thy works, that thou art neither cold nor hot: I would thou wert cold or hot. So then because thou art lukewarm, and neither cold nor hot, I will spue thee out of my mouth" (Revelation 3:15–16). The warning that "the love of many shall wax cold" (Matthew 24:12) provides the eschatological dimension — lukewarmness is not merely an individual failing but a sign of the times.
