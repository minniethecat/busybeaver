# Knowledge

Thomas à Kempis opens The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] with a radical revaluation of knowledge. The very first chapter after the programmatic opening is titled "Of the Knowledge of [[truth|Truth]]" (003-001), and the very first gesture of Book I is to relativise all human learning before the one thing necessary. "What doth it profit thee to dispute deeply about the Trinity, if thou be lacking in humility?" (001-009 to 001-010). This is not anti-intellectualism — it is a reordering of priorities: knowledge must serve love, learning must lead to humility, and all understanding must terminate in God.

## Definition

Knowledge, in the vocabulary of The Imitation, carries two distinct and opposing meanings. On one hand, it designates the natural human pursuit of understanding — the desire "to know many things" (002-010), the pride of the learned who "desire to appear wise" (002-011), the accumulation of information that brings no moral transformation. This is the knowledge that "puffeth up" in St Paul's phrase, the knowledge that makes its possessor "more severely judged" (002-018) because greater light brings greater accountability. On the other hand, knowledge in its true and saving sense means the knowledge of God and the knowledge of oneself — two forms of knowing that are inseparable in Thomas's thought: "He who knoweth himself becometh vile in his own eyes, and delighteth not in the praises of men" (002-005). The goal of all true knowledge is not speculation but transformation, not mastery but humility, not reputation but sanctity.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas does not condemn knowledge itself but diagnoses its characteristic distortions and redirects it toward its proper end. His treatment of knowledge forms one of the most sustained arguments in the book, appearing across multiple chapters of Book I.

### The Vanity of Learning Without Humility

The most famous passage on knowledge in The Imitation is also the most uncompromising. "Truly, at the [[judgment|day of judgment]], we shall not be asked what we have read but what we have done; not how well we have spoken but how holily we have lived" (003-003 to 003-004). This is the hermeneutic key to everything Thomas says about learning. Knowledge is not rejected — it is measured against a higher standard. The learned man who has mastered the Sentences of Peter Lombard but has not mastered his own [[sin|passions]] has gained nothing; the simple peasant who knows only the Creed but lives it with his whole heart has attained all.

Thomas identifies the specific temptation of the learned: "knowledge without the [[fear-of-the-lord|fear of God]]" (002-003). When the intellect operates independently of reverence, it becomes an instrument of pride. "Those who have knowledge desire to appear wise" (002-011) — the desire to be thought learned is itself a form of vanity, a subtle seeking of the praise that belongs to God alone. The remedy is not ignorance but the [[reverence|fear of the Lord]], which is "the beginning of wisdom" and the only knowledge that can make the learned man truly wise. "If thou knewest the whole Bible by heart, and the sayings of all the philosophers, what should it profit thee without the love of God, and grace?" (003-006 to 003-007). The question is rhetorical; the answer is implicit in every line.

### The Ordering of Knowledge Toward Love

Thomas does not merely negate learning; he reorders it. Knowledge must serve [[love|charity]]. "Many seek knowledge rather than good living" (003-040) — this is the fundamental disorder. The intellect is a good thing, but when it seeks its own satisfaction rather than the ser[[sin|vice]] of God and neighbour, it becomes an idol. Thomas contrasts two kinds of desire: the desire to know and the desire to love. "He who hath [[charity|true charity]] and the knowledge of God hath no desire after vain knowledge" (002-010). The knowledge that is rooted in love is content with what it has; the knowledge that is rooted in curiosity is never satisfied.

This ordering has practical consequences. "The greater and more complete thy knowledge, the more severely shalt thou be judged" (002-018). Knowledge increases responsibility; every truth understood and not lived becomes an accusation at the judgment. This is not a discouragement to study but a call to integrity: what the mind knows, the will must embrace, and what the will embraces, the life must express. A "lowly knowledge of thyself" (003-036) is worth more than all the subtleties of the schools, because it leads to amendment, whereas speculative learning often leaves the soul unchanged.

### Knowledge of Self and Knowledge of God

For Thomas, the two forms of saving knowledge — of self and of God — are inseparable. One cannot truly know God without knowing one's own nothingness, and one cannot truly know oneself without the light of God's truth. "He who knoweth himself" discovers not his greatness but his vileness: his sin, his weakness, his utter dependence on grace. This is the "lowly knowledge of thyself" (003-036) that Thomas prizes above all philosophy.

At the same time, the knowledge of self opens the soul to the knowledge of God. "If thy knowledge of the Scriptures and of all [[creation|creatures]] be taken from thee, what will remain?" (003-037). The implied answer is: only God. Knowledge of [[creation|creatures]] — of the natural world, of human affairs, of books and languages — is good in its place, but it is not ultimate. The knowledge that saves is the knowledge of "Thee alone, who art the [[truth|Truth]]" (003-039), the personal knowledge of God in [[jesus-christ|Christ]] that is [[eternity|eternal life]] (John 17:3). Everything else is scaffolding; this alone is the building.

## Key Passages

> "What doth it profit thee to dispute deeply about the Trinity, if thou be lacking in humility, and so be displeasing to the Trinity? Verily, sublime words make not a man holy and just; but a virtuous life maketh him dear to God." (001-009 to 001-011)

> "Truly, at the day of judgment, we shall not be asked what we have read but what we have done; not how well we have spoken but how holily we have lived." (003-003 to 003-004)

> "The greater and more complete thy knowledge, the more severely shalt thou be judged, unless thy life be also more holy." (002-018 to 002-019)

> "A lowly knowledge of thyself is a surer way to God than a deep search after learning." (003-036 to 003-037)

> "If thy life and knowledge had agreed together, then hadst thou both knowledge and profit." (003-053 to 003-054)

## Biblical Foundation

Thomas's teaching on knowledge echoes St Paul's warning that "knowledge puffeth up, but [[love|charity]] edifieth" (1 Corinthians 8:1). The apostle's further admission — "if any man think that he knoweth any thing, he knoweth nothing yet as he ought to know" (1 Corinthians 8:2) — underlies Thomas's insistence on the radical inadequacy of all merely human knowledge. The theme of the [[reverence|fear of the Lord]] as the beginning of knowledge (Proverbs 1:7) runs throughout Thomas's treatment: knowledge without reverence is a tree without roots, destined to wither.

The Preacher's melancholy observation that "in much wisdom is much grief, and he that increaseth knowledge increaseth sorrow" (Ecclesiastes 1:18) finds its echo in Thomas's acknowledgment that knowledge brings severity of judgment. Yet the answer is not to flee learning but to subordinate it to the knowledge that matters: "Thus saith the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], Let not the wise man glory in his wisdom … but let him that glorieth glory in this, that he understandeth and knoweth Me, that I am the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Jeremiah 9:23-24). St Paul, who counted all his former learning "but dung" that he might "win [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (Philippians 3:8), is the living exemplar of Thomas's principle that all knowledge must be revalued in the light of the surpassing worth of knowing [[jesus-christ|Christ]].
