[020-001] Of the love of solitude and silence

[020-002] Seek a suitable time for thy meditation, and think frequently of the

[020-003] mercies of God to thee. Leave curious questions. Study such matters as

[020-004] bring thee [[contrition|sorrow for sin]] rather than amusement. If thou withdraw

[020-005] thyself from trifling conversation and idle goings about, as well as

[020-006] from novelties and gossip, thou shalt find thy time sufficient and apt

[020-007] for good meditation. The greatest saints used to avoid as far as they

[020-008] could the company of men, and chose to live in secret with God.

[020-009] 2. One hath said, “As oft as I have gone among men, so oft have I

[020-010] returned less a man.” This is what we often experience when we have

[020-011] been long time in conversation. For it is easier to be altogether

[020-012] silent than it is not to exceed in word. It is easier to remain hidden

[020-013] at home than to keep sufficient guard upon thyself out of doors. He,

[020-014] therefore, that seeketh to reach that which is hidden and spiritual,

[020-015] must go with [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] “apart from the multitude.” No man safely goeth

[020-016] abroad who loveth not to rest at home. No man safely talketh but he who

[020-017] loveth to hold his peace. No man safely ruleth but he who loveth to be

[020-018] subject. No man safely commandeth but he who loveth to obey.

[020-019] 3. No man safely rejoiceth but he who hath the testimony of a good

[020-020] conscience within himself. The boldness of the Saints was always full

[020-021] of the [[fear-of-the-lord|fear of God]]. Nor were they the less earnest and humble in

[020-022] themselves, because they shone forth with great virtues and grace. But

[020-023] the boldness of wicked men springeth from pride and [[pride|presumption]], and at

[020-024] the last turneth to their own confusion. Never promise thyself security

[020-025] in this life, howsoever good a monk or devout a solitary thou seemest.

[020-026] 4. Often those who stand highest in the esteem of men, fall the more

[020-027] grievously because of their over great confidence. Wherefore it is very

[020-028] profitable unto many that they should not be without inward temptation,

[020-029] but should be frequently assaulted, lest they be over confident, lest

[020-030] they be indeed lifted up into pride, or else lean too freely upon the

[020-031] consolations of [[world|the world]]. O how good a conscience should that man

[020-032] keep, who never sought a joy that passeth away, who never became

[020-033] entangled with the world! O how great peace and [[silence|quiet]] should he

[020-034] possess, who would cast off all vain care, and think only of healthful

[020-035] and divine things, and build his whole hope upon God!

[020-036] 5. No man is worthy of [[consolation|heavenly consolation]] but he who hath diligently

[020-037] exercised himself in [[compunction|holy compunction]]. If thou wilt feel compunction

[020-038] within thy heart, enter into thy chamber and shut out the tumults of

[020-039] the world, as it is written, Commune with your own heart in your own

[020-040] chamber and be still.(1) In retirement thou shalt find what often thou

[020-041] wilt lose abroad. Retirement, if thou continue therein, groweth sweet,

[020-042] but if thou keep not in it, begetteth weariness. If in the beginning of

[020-043] thy conversation thou dwell in it and keep it well, it shall afterwards

[020-044] be to thee a dear friend, and a most pleasant solace.

[020-045] 6. In silence and quiet the devout soul goeth forward and learneth the

[020-046] hidden things of the Scriptures. Therein findeth she a fountain of

[020-047] tears, wherein to wash and cleanse herself each night, that she may

[020-048] grow the more dear to her Maker as she dwelleth the further from all

[020-049] worldly distraction. To him who withdraweth himself from his

[020-050] acquaintance and friends God with his holy angels will draw nigh. It is

[020-051] better to be unknown and take heed to oneself than to neglect oneself

[020-052] and work wonders. It is praiseworthy for a religious man to go seldom

[020-053] abroad, to fly from being seen, to have no desire to see men.

[020-054] 7. Why wouldest thou see what thou mayest not have? The world passeth

[020-055] away and the lust thereof. The desires of sensuality draw thee abroad,

[020-056] but when an hour is past, what dost thou bring home, but a weight upon

[020-057] thy conscience and distraction of heart? A merry going forth bringeth

[020-058] often a sorrowful return, and a merry evening maketh a sad morning? So

[020-059] doth all carnal joy begin pleasantly, but in the end it gnaweth away

[020-060] and destroyeth. What canst thou see abroad which thou seest not at

[020-061] home? Behold the heaven and the earth and the elements, for out of

[020-062] these are all things made.

[020-063] 8. What canst thou see anywhere which can continue long under the sun?

[020-064] Thou believest perchance that thou shalt be satisfied, but thou wilt

[020-065] never be able to attain unto this. If thou shouldest see all things

[020-066] before thee at once, what would it be but a vain vision? Lift up thine

[020-067] eyes to God on high, and pray that thy [[sin|sins]] and negligences may be

[020-068] forgiven. Leave vain things to vain men, and mind thou the things which

[020-069] God hath commanded thee. Shut thy door upon thee, and call unto thyself

[020-070] Jesus thy beloved. Remain with Him in thy chamber, for thou shalt not

[020-071] elsewhere find so great peace. If thou hadst not gone forth nor

[020-072] listened to vain talk, thou hadst better kept thyself in good peace.

[020-073] But because it sometimes delighteth thee to hear new things, thou must

[020-074] therefore suffer trouble of heart.

[020-075] (1) Psalm iv. 4.
