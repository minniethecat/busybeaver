# Self-Denial

The voluntary renunciation of one's own will, desires, and worldly attachments in order to follow [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], it is not an optional counsel for the few but the universal, indispensable condition of [[discipleship|disciple]]ship, the "royal way" without which "thou canst not possess perfect liberty" (069-002).

## Definition

Self-denial in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is the soul's fundamental turning from self-love to the love of God, expressed in the concrete renunciation of one's own will, judgment, and comfort. Thomas a Kempis places this renunciation at the very heart of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life, opening one of the most concentrated treatments of the subject with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s direct words: "My Son, thou canst not possess perfect liberty unless thou altogether deny thyself" (069-002). The practice is presented not as a single act but as a lifelong work: "O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], this is not the work of a day, nor children's play; verily in this short saying is enclosed all the perfection of the religious" (069-011).

The meaning and scope of self-denial are crystallized in the "short and complete saying" that [[jesus-christ|Christ]] gives the [[discipleship|disciple]]: "Renounce all things, and thou shalt find all things; give up thy lust, and thou shalt find rest" (069-008). Self-denial is thus not merely negative — the stripping away of attachments — but the positive means by which the soul finds everything in God. It is the gate of liberty, for "all they are enslaved who are possessors of riches, they who love themselves, the selfish, the curious, the restless; those who ever seek after soft things, and not after the things of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]" (069-004).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas a Kempis treats self-denial as the constitutive act of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[discipleship|disciple]]ship, woven through all four books and presented under several interconnected aspects: as the command of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], as the path of true progress, as the inseparable companion of the Cross, as the condition of receiving grace, and as the source of spiritual liberty.

### The Foundation: "Deny Thyself"

The command to self-denial is established at the very opening of the work. The title of Book I, Chapter I — "Of the [[discipleship|imitation of Christ]], and of contempt of [[world|the world]] and all its [[vanity|vanities]]" (001-001) — already contains the negative movement (contempt of [[world|the world]]) that self-denial requires. But it is in Book III that Thomas develops the doctrine most fully. Book III, Chapter XXXII is devoted entirely to "self-denial and the casting away all selfishness" (069-001), and Book III, Chapter LVI bears the title "That we ought to deny ourselves, and to imitate [[jesus-christ|Christ]] by means of the Cross" (093-001). Here [[jesus-christ|Christ]] speaks with uncompromising clarity: "I will that thou learn perfect self-denial, living in My will without contradiction or complaint" (093-006), and sets forth the chain of conditions that define the spiritual life: "If thou wilt be perfect, sell all that thou hast. If thou wilt be My [[discipleship|disciple]], deny thyself" (093-019). So far as the soul goes out of itself, so far it enters into [[jesus-christ|Christ]]: "My Son, so far as thou art able to go out of thyself so far shalt thou be able to enter into Me" (093-003).

### Self-Denial as True Progress

Thomas identifies self-denial as the very measure of spiritual progress. "The true progress of man lieth in self-denial, and a man who denieth himself is free and safe" (076-014). This is one of the most concise and theologically dense statements in the entire work: it identifies self-denial with both progress (the dynamic forward movement of the spiritual life) and freedom (the safe condition of the soul delivered from the tyranny of self-love). Progress does not consist in [[comfort|consolation]]s, spiritual experiences, or external works — it consists in the degree to which the self is renounced. The necessity of self-denial is rooted in the spiritual combat itself, for "the old enemy, opposer of all good things, ceaseth not from temptation; but day and night setteth his wicked snares, if haply he may be able to entrap the unwary" (076-016). Self-denial is the defense against this enemy, for the one who has renounced self has nothing in him for temptation to grasp.

### Self-Denial and the Cross

Self-denial and the Cross are inseparable in Thomas's teaching. The foundational text of the whole work — "If any man will come after Me, let him deny himself and take up his Cross and follow Me" (037-003) — joins the two in a single breath. Self-denial is the interior act; Cross-bearing is its outward expression. Book II, Chapter XII develops this union at length, declaring that "everything dependeth upon the Cross, and everything lieth in dying; and there is none other way unto life and to true [[peace|inward peace]], except the way of the [[cross|Holy Cross]] and of [[mortification|daily mortification]]" (037-023). Thomas insists that self-denial is not within natural capacity but requires supernatural assistance: "It is not in the nature of man to bear the cross, to love the cross, to keep under the body and to bring it into [[obedience|subjection]], to fly from honours, to bear reproaches meekly, to despise self and desire to be despised" (037-080). Only grace can produce the disposition by which a person "draweth to and loveth through [[devotion|fervour]] of spirit" what nature hates and flees from (037-079).

The spiritual logic is laid bare in Book III, Chapter LVI: "If thou wilt reign with Me, bear the cross with Me; for only the servants of the cross find the way of blessedness and of true light" (093-022). The [[discipleship|disciple]] responds with complete surrender: "I have received the cross at Thy hand; I have carried it, and will carry it even unto death, as Thou hast laid it upon me" (093-036). Self-denial is thus the door through which one enters into the fellowship of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s sufferings and the eventual participation in His glory.

### Grace, Mortification, and Spiritual Liberty

Self-denial is the condition for receiving the grace of devotion and the path to perfect liberty. Book IV, Chapter XV bears the significant title "That the grace of devotion is acquired by humility and self-denial" (111-001). Thomas teaches a direct proportion: "the more perfectly a man forsaketh things which cannot profit, and the more he dieth to himself, the more quickly doth grace come, the more plentifully doth it enter in, and the higher doth it lift up the free heart" (111-031). This is because "where the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] findeth empty vessels, there giveth He His blessing" (111-030). Self-denial creates the [[vanity|emptiness]] — the vessel — into which grace can be poured. The one who is full of self has no capacity for God.

Self-denial is not a single heroic act but the daily texture of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life. "Thou hast still many things to renounce, which if thou resign not utterly to Me, thou shalt not gain what thou seekest" (069-020). Thomas catalogues the concrete forms this mortification takes: the will must be subdued to obedience, personal judgment surrendered to the direction of another, natural preferences set aside. "Scarcely is there anything in which thou hast need to mortify thyself so much as in seeing things which are adverse to thy will; especially when things are commanded thee to be done which seem to thee inexpedient or of little use to thee" (086-053). Yet the fruit of this dying is life: "Know thou of a surety that thou oughtest to lead the life of a dying man. And the more a man dieth to himself, the more he beginneth to live towards God" (037-122).

The paradoxical fruit of self-denial is perfect liberty. Those "who perfectly despise [[world|the world]] and strive to live unto God in holy discipline" are not ignorant of "the divine sweetness promised to all who truly deny themselves" (057-044). The renunciation of self-will yields a freedom that self-assertion can never achieve: "Renounce all things, and thou shalt find all things; give up thy lust, and thou shalt find rest" (069-008). The one who has abandoned everything for [[jesus-christ|Christ]] possesses everything in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Book I summarizes the practical scope of this renunciation with remarkable concision: "a perfect contempt of [[world|the world]], a fervent desire to excel in virtue, the love of discipline, the painfulness of repentance, readiness to obey, denial of self, [[obedience|submission]] to any [[suffering|adversity]] for love of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (023-030) — these are the marks of one prepared for a happy death.

## Key Passages

> "My Son, thou canst not possess perfect liberty unless thou altogether deny thyself. All they are enslaved who are possessors of riches, they who love themselves, the selfish, the curious, the restless; those who ever seek after soft things, and not after the things of Jesus Christ." (069-003)

> "The true progress of man lieth in self-denial, and a man who denieth himself is free and safe." (076-014)

> "So far as thou art able to go out of thyself so far shalt thou be able to enter into Me. As to desire no outward thing worketh internal peace, so the forsaking of self inwardly joineth unto God. I will that thou learn perfect self-denial, living in My will without contradiction or complaint." (093-006)

> "It is not in the nature of man to bear the cross, to love the cross, to keep under the body and to bring it into subjection, to fly from honours, to bear reproaches meekly, to despise self and desire to be despised... If thou lookest to thyself, thou wilt of thyself be able to do none of this; but if thou trustest in the Lord, endurance shall be given thee from heaven." (037-080)

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of self-denial in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is rooted first in the words of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself. The foundational text is Matthew 16:24 — "If any man will come after Me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow Me" — which Thomas quotes at both the opening (037-003) and closing (037-138) of Book II, Chapter XII, and which is echoed in the Lukan parallel at Luke 9:23 (037-139). The Pauline theology of dying and rising with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] underlies the entire treatment: "If thou be dead with Him, thou shalt also live with Him" (037-020), echoing 2 Timothy 2:11-12 and Romans 6:8. Galatians 2:20 — "I am crucified with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]: nevertheless I live; yet not I, but [[jesus-christ|Christ]] liveth in me" — captures the very essence of what Thomas means by self-denial: the displacement of the self by the indwelling [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. The dominical sayings in Matthew 19:21 ("If thou wilt be perfect, go and sell that thou hast") and John 12:25 ("He that loveth his life shall lose it; and he that hateth his life in this world shall keep it unto life eternal") are woven directly into the text (093-019, 093-020). The eschatological dimension — self-denial now for glory hereafter — draws on Romans 8:18: "the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed in us" (037-101). The summary and conclusion, drawn from Acts 14:22, is that "We must through much [[suffering|tribulation]] enter into the kingdom of God" (037-141).

## Related Concepts

- [[cross]] — The external form and instrument of self-denial; the Cross is what self-denial embraces, and self-denial is the interior act by which the Cross is taken up
- [[humility]] — The disposition that makes self-denial possible, acknowledging one's own nothingness and refusing [[trust-in-god|trust]] in self (111-001)
- [[obedience]] — The concrete expression of self-denial in the surrender of one's own will to God and to those placed in authority (069-018)
- [[contempt-of-the-world]] — The negative aspect of self-denial: the rejection of worldly [[vanity|vanities]] that opens the way to the positive cleaving to God (001-001, 023-030)
- [[love]] — The motive of self-denial: [[jesus-christ|Christ]] bore the Cross "out of love" (037-091), and the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s renunciation of self is the proof of pure love unmixed with self-seeking (036-017)
- [[grace]] — The supernatural power that enables self-denial, for it "is not the virtue of man, but the grace of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (037-077); and self-denial is the condition for receiving more grace (111-031)
- [[suffering]] — The substance of self-denial experienced in the concrete; the universal human condition that, when embraced in self-renouncing faith, becomes the path to glory
