# Humility

The virtue of rightly acknowledging one's own lowliness and complete dependence upon God, foundational to all spiritual progress in the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian devotional tradition.

## Definition

Humility is the spiritual disposition by which a person recognizes their own nothingness before God and refuses to [[trust-in-god|trust]] in their own strength, learning, or merit. In The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], humility is not merely one virtue among many — it is the very ground of the spiritual life, without which no progress is possible (001-015). The humble person accepts obscurity, correction, and suffering without resentment, knowing that "through [[patience|endurance]] and true humility we are made stronger than all our enemies" (013-022).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats humility as the indispensable foundation of the entire spiritual edifice. Throughout the four books, humility appears as the necessary condition for receiving grace, the proper disposition for hearing God's word, and the safeguard against the ever-present danger of pride.

### Humility as Safeguard of Grace

Book III, Chapter VII — "Of Hiding Our [[grace|Grace]] Under the Guard of Humility" — contains the work's most concentrated teaching on this virtue. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] counsels the [[discipleship|disciple]] that it is "better and safer for thee to hide the grace of devotion, and not to lift thyself up on high" (044-002). The grace of spiritual [[comfort|consolation]] is not given for [[pride|self-exaltation]] but must be received with the awareness that one is unworthy of it. Those who become presumptuous because of devotion "have destroyed themselves, because they would do more than they were able, not considering the measure of their own littleness" (044-021).

### Humility in Hearing God's Word

The [[discipleship|disciple]] is instructed to receive God's words "in silence, and to be received with all humility and with deep love" (040-007). This is not the humility of mere outward deference but the inward posture of one who knows that [[wisdom|divine wisdom]] surpasses "all the knowledge of the philosophers and wise men of this world" (040-004).

### Humility Before Others

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] extends humility into the social sphere: the humble person does not resent being unknown or corrected. "It is no harm to thee if thou place thyself below all others; but it is great harm if thou place thyself above even one" (007-021). Even suffering unjust rebuke serves a purpose, "keeping us in greater humility, that others know and rebuke our [[sin|fault]]s" (027-010).

### Key Passages

> "My Son, it is better and safer for thee to hide the grace of devotion, and not to lift thyself up on high, nor to speak much thereof, nor to value it greatly; but rather to despise thyself, and to fear as though this grace were given to one unworthy thereof" (044-002)

> "Think when thou art in a state of grace how miserable and poor thou art wont to be without grace. Nor is there advance in spiritual life in this alone, that thou hast the grace of consolation, but that thou humbly and unselfishly and patiently takest the withdrawal thereof" (044-008)

> "Trust not in thy learning, nor in the cleverness of any that lives, but rather trust in the favour of God, who resisteth the proud and giveth grace to the humble" (007-006)

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] grounds its teaching on humility in several scriptural themes. The contrast between the proud and the humble draws directly from the [[wisdom|Wisdom]] literature and the New Testament: "God resisteth the proud, but giveth grace unto the humble" (James 4:6, quoting Proverbs 3:34). [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own example — "I am meek and lowly in heart" (Matthew 11:29) — provides the model. The Virgin Mary's *Magnificat* (Luke 1:46–55), though not directly quoted, echoes throughout the work's treatment of God exalting the lowly.
