# Zeal

The fervent and diligent application of the will to growth in holiness — an energetic virtue that Thomas à Kempis presents as the necessary antidote to the lukewarmness that besets the spiritual life. "Be zealous, therefore, for thy spiritual profit" (025-004): this exhortation, placed at the head of the chapter "Of the Zealous [[amendment|Amendment]] of Our Whole Life," signals zeal's importance as the motive force of all genuine progress in the interior life.

## Definition

Zeal, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not the noisy enthusiasm of the no[[sin|vice]] or the combative ardour of the polemicist. It is the [[silence|quiet]] but unrelenting determination to advance in holiness — a holy dissatisfaction with one's present state that refuses to settle for mediocrity. Thomas defines the zealous man negatively: he is the one who has not grown "lukewarm and unspiritual" (011-015), who has not allowed "anxious care for transitory things" (011-013) to cool his first [[devotion|fervour]]. Positively, zeal is "earnest zeal for that good in which we are most lacking" (025-039) — it directs its energy not indiscriminately but precisely toward the areas of greatest spiritual need. Zeal is the virtue that transforms good intentions into concrete amendment, that turns the vague desire for holiness into "daily growth in grace" (011-014).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas treats zeal less as a discrete topic than as a pervasive quality that ought to characterise the whole [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life. It appears as the energy behind every other virtue — the fire that makes prayer fervent, obedience prompt, and amendment real.

### Zeal and the Danger of Lukewarmness

Thomas diagnoses lukewarmness as the great spiritual sickness of those who have begun well but have lost their first [[devotion|fervour]]. "We seldom conquer even a single [[sin|fault]], nor are we zealous for daily growth in grace" (011-014). This is the voice of honest self-examination: the [[discipleship|disciple]] who looks at his own life and sees not progress but stagnation, not victory over sin but comfortable coexistence with it. The cause, Thomas suggests, is that "we are too anxious about transitory things" (011-013) — the cares of [[world|the world]] have choked the seed of the word, and the soul that was once on fire has grown cold.

The remedy is a deliberate rekindling of zeal. Thomas exhorts the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "be watchful and diligent in God's ser[[sin|vice]], and bethink thee often why thou hast renounced [[world|the world]]. Was it not that thou mightest live to God and become a spiritual man?" (025-002 to 025-004). The recollection of one's original purpose — the radical act of renunciation that marked the beginning of the religious life — is the fuel that rekindles dying zeal. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who remembers why he left [[world|the world]] will not be content to live as a worldly man within the cloister.

### Zeal Manifested in Trial

Thomas makes an important [[discernment|distinction]]: zeal is most reliably tested not in times of [[comfort|consolation]] but in times of [[suffering|adversity]]. "Nor is it a great thing if a man be devout and zealous so long as he suffereth no [[suffering|affliction]]; but if he behave himself patiently in the time of [[suffering|adversity]], then is there hope of great progress" (013-063 to 013-065). The zeal that flourishes only in fair weather is not true zeal but natural enthusiasm. The zeal that endures through temptation, [[desolation|dryness]], and suffering — that is the zeal which "hath its reward the greater" (013-062) and which proves the genuineness of the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s love.

This linking of zeal with patience is characteristic of Thomas's spiritual realism. Zeal without patience burns hot and quickly dies; patience without zeal grows passive and complacent. The two virtues temper one another: zeal gives energy to patience, and patience gives steadiness to zeal. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who combines them "hath great profit in a short time" (025-039), advancing rapidly in holiness precisely because his [[devotion|fervour]] is sustained rather than sporadic.

### The Focus of True Zeal

Thomas directs zeal not toward every conceivable good but toward "that good in which we are most lacking" (025-039). This is a crucial practical insight. The zealous soul does not dissipate its energy in a hundred scattered pursuits; it identifies its predominant [[sin|fault]], its weakest point, the virtue it most conspicuously lacks, and directs its earnest effort there. Equally, Thomas counsels the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "strive also very earnestly to guard against and subdue those [[sin|fault]]s which displease thee most frequently in others" (025-040 to 025-041). The [[sin|fault]]s we most readily condemn in our neighbour are often the very [[sin|fault]]s to which we ourselves are blind. True zeal turns the critical gaze inward and wages war on the [[sin|sins]] it discovers there.

The goal of this focused zeal is nothing less than "the [[amendment|zealous amendment]] of our whole life" (025-001). Thomas does not counsel piecemeal reform but a comprehensive reorientation of the entire person toward God. Zeal is the engine of this transformation: "Now shalt thou labour a little, and thou shalt find great rest, yea everlasting joy" (025-007 to 025-008). The labour of zeal is intense but brief compared to the rest it purchases; the zealous [[discipleship|disciple]] trades a little present effort for an eternity of peace.

### The Example of the Saints

Thomas holds up the saints as exemplars of zeal. "How strict fasts did they endure! What fervent zeal and desire after spiritual profit did they manifest!" (018-016 to 018-017). The saints were not naturally stronger than other men; they were simply more zealous. They prayed more fervently, fasted more strictly, struggled more resolutely against sin. Their example is meant not to discourage but to provoke: "If they could do so much, what canst not thou attempt?" (018-018). Zeal looks to the saints not for impossible standards but for proof that progress in holiness is possible for those who will earnestly labour.

## Key Passages

> "Be thou watchful and diligent in God's service, and bethink thee often why thou hast renounced the world. Was it not that thou mightest live to God and become a spiritual man? Be zealous, therefore, for thy spiritual profit, for thou shalt receive shortly the reward of thy labours." (025-002 to 025-005)

> "We seldom conquer even a single fault, nor are we zealous for daily growth in grace. And so we remain lukewarm and unspiritual." (011-014 to 011-015)

> "Nor is it a great thing if a man be devout and zealous so long as he suffereth no affliction; but if he behave himself patiently in the time of adversity, then is there hope of great progress." (013-063 to 013-065)

> "Two things specially avail unto improvement in holiness, namely firmness to withdraw ourselves from the sin to which by nature we are most inclined, and earnest zeal for that good in which we are most lacking." (025-037 to 025-039)

> "If thou knewest what peace to thyself thy holy life should bring to thyself, and what joy to others, methinketh thou wouldst be more zealous for spiritual profit." (011-043 to 011-045)

## Biblical Foundation

The call to zeal in The Imitation echoes the apostolic exhortation: "Not slothful in business; fervent in spirit; serving the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Romans 12:11). Paul's admonition that "it is good to be zealously affected always in a good thing" (Galatians 4:18) underlies Thomas's insistence that zeal must be directed toward the right objects — not toward the [[sin|fault]]s of others but toward one's own amendment. The warning against lukewarmness draws its ultimate force from the words of the risen [[jesus-christ|Christ]] to the church at Laodicea: "Because thou art lukewarm, and neither cold nor hot, I will spue thee out of my mouth" (Revelation 3:16). This is the most terrifying judgment on spiritual mediocrity in all of Scripture, and it haunts Thomas's exhortations to [[devotion|fervour]].

The Psalmist's cry — "the zeal of Thine house hath eaten me up" (Psalm 69:9) — quoted of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in John 2:17, provides the archetype of holy zeal: a consuming passion for the honour of God that overtakes every lesser concern. The saints' example of "fervent zeal and desire after spiritual profit" (018-016) likewise stands in the tradition of Hebrews 6:11-12: "We desire that every one of you do shew the same diligence to the full assurance of hope unto the end: that ye be not slothful, but followers of them who through faith and patience inherit the promises."

## Related Concepts

- [[fervor]] — The warmth and energy of devotion that zeal sustains and rekindles
- [[devotion]] — The inward disposition of the heart toward God, which zeal protects from cooling into routine
- [[perseverance]] — The companion virtue to zeal; zeal provides energy, perseverance provides duration
- [[diligence]] — The practical expression of zeal in daily duties; the zealous soul is diligent in small things
- [[amendment]] — The concrete goal of zeal; zeal for amendment transforms good desires into changed behaviour
- [[love]] — The root and motive of all true zeal; zeal without love degenerates into bitterness or fanaticism
- [[grace]] — The source of zeal's strength; the [[discipleship|disciple]] cannot sustain zeal by natural effort alone
- [[lukewarmness]] — The spiritual sickness that zeal cures; the zealous soul is the opposite of the lukewarm
- [[prayer]] — The fuel of zeal; the saints' "frequent and fervid prayers" sustained their fervent lives
- [[humility]] — The governor of zeal; without humility, zeal becomes pride in one's own spiritual achievements
