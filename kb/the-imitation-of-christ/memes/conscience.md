# Conscience

The interior tribunal and moral faculty by which the soul stands transparent before God, bearing witness either to its innocence or its guilt — a good conscience being the foundation of all spiritual joy, peace, and boldness, and an evil conscience the source of unremitting fear and interior anguish, in the theology of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Conscience, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is the interior witness by which a person knows his own spiritual condition before God. Thomas à Kempis declares that "the testimony of a good conscience is the glory of a good man" (031-002). It is not merely a psychological faculty but a theological reality — the point at which the soul stands naked before its Creator. A pure conscience "giveth great confidence towards God" (002-016), for it is the soul's own awareness that it has done what is right and is at peace with its [[jesus-christ|Lord]]. Conversely, the conscience also accuses: when a man yields to inordinate desire, "he is weighed down by the condemnation of his conscience" (006-012). The conscience is thus both comforter and judge, acquitting or condemning the soul according to its deeds and intentions.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Conscience pervades every book of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as the interior ground of the spiritual life. Thomas à Kempis treats conscience not as an abstract faculty but as the experiential register of one's real relationship with God — the place where peace or dis[[silence|quiet]] reveals the true state of the soul.

### The Good Conscience

The good conscience is, for Thomas, the wellspring of authentic joy — a joy independent of all outward circumstances. "Have a good conscience and thou shalt ever have joy" (031-003). This interior joy is so powerful that "a good conscience is able to bear exceeding much, and is exceeding joyful in the midst of adversities" (031-004). The good conscience confers a peace that [[world|the world]] cannot give and cannot take away: "He will easily be contented and filled with peace, whose conscience is pure" (031-023). This peace extends even to the hour of death, for "if thou hadst a good conscience thou wouldst not greatly fear death" (023-008). The good conscience is the sole foundation of genuine boldness: "No man safely rejoiceth but he who hath the testimony of a good conscience within himself" (020-019 to 020-020). It is the divine defence against all human malice, for [[jesus-christ|Christ]] teaches: "Have a good conscience and God will defend thee, for he whom God will help no man's perverseness shall be able to hurt" (027-004).

Thomas further teaches that the good conscience renders one indifferent to the praise or blame of men. "The glory of the good is in their conscience, and not in the report of men" (031-016). At the final judgment, "a pure and good conscience shall more rejoice than learned philosophy" (024-056), for the conscience bears witness to what one truly is before God, not to what one appears before men.

### The Accusing Conscience

The evil or impure conscience is the mirror image of the good: it produces ceaseless fear and interior torment. "An evil conscience is ever fearful and un[[silence|quiet]]" (031-005). Where the pure conscience is the seat of joy, "if there is anywhere [[suffering|tribulation]] and anguish, the evil conscience knoweth it best" (029-017). Thomas warns that [[sin|sins]] "defile the conscience, and destroy the [[grace|grace of God]]" (001-037), teaching that conscience is defiled not by theoretical knowledge of wrong but by the actual commission of sin — especially [[sin|sins]] of the flesh and of the tongue. Even the seemingly harmless activity of speech is rarely without cost: "Why do we talk and gossip so continually, seeing that we so rarely resume our silence without some hurt done to our conscience?" (010-007).

The accusing conscience is an ever-present reality. When the [[discipleship|disciple]] approaches the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], he confesses that "my own [[sin|sins]] terrify me, and my impure conscience driveth me away from receiving so great mysteries" (097-011). Even the just man must acknowledge that "my own conscience sufficeth me not" (083-046), for conscience itself, unaided by grace, can become an accuser: "my conscience, which lieth wait against me" (083-052).

### Purifying the Conscience

Because the conscience can be defiled, it must be purified — and this purification is a central spiritual practice. Thomas devotes an entire chapter of Book IV to "The examination of conscience, and purpose of amendment" (103-001). The [[discipleship|disciple]] is instructed: "Diligently examine thy conscience and with all thy might with true contrition and [[confession|humble confession]] cleanse and purify it, so that thou mayest feel no burden, nor know anything which bringeth thee remorse and impedeth thy free approach" (103-006 to 103-008). This examination of conscience is not morbid introspection but a deliberate act of standing before God in truth, confessing one's failures, and resolving upon amendment. It is the prerequisite for worthy reception of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]]: "Preserve my heart and my body undefiled, that with a joyful and pure conscience I may be able" to receive the sacred mysteries (098-049).

The path to a pure conscience involves both active cleansing and passive receptivity. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] instructs the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "pour out thy devout prayer to God, that thou mayest possess a contrite mind and a pure conscience" (090-009). The cleansing of conscience requires separation from [[world|the world]]: "enter into thy chamber and shut out the tumults of [[world|the world]]" (020-038 to 020-039). The happy man is he "who putteth away from him whatsoever may stain or burden his conscience" (021-016). The conscience is purified through [[compunction|compunction of heart]], confession, and the deliberate renunciation of everything that defiles it.

### Conscience and Peace

The relationship between conscience and peace is one of identity in the Kempian vision. A clean conscience is peace; a defiled conscience is unrest. "He will easily be contented and filled with peace, whose conscience is pure" (031-023). The regenerate soul cries out: "O how good a conscience should that man keep, who never sought a joy that passeth away" — and immediately follows with: "O how great peace and [[silence|quiet]] should he possess, who would cast off all vain care" (020-031 to 020-034). [[peace|Peace]] is not an additional gift added to a good conscience; it is its very substance. Conversely, the loss of peace is the direct consequence of a burdened conscience: "A merry going forth bringeth often a sorrowful return... what dost thou bring home, but a weight upon thy conscience and distraction of heart?" (020-057). True liberty and joy exist only "in the [[fear-of-the-lord|fear of God]] with a good conscience" (021-013). The one who cares "for neither praises nor reproaches hath great [[peace|tranquillity]] of heart" (031-021 to 031-022), for his conscience rests in God alone.

## Key Passages

> "The testimony of a good conscience is the glory of a good man. Have a good conscience and thou shalt ever have joy. A good conscience is able to bear exceeding much, and is exceeding joyful in the midst of adversities; an evil conscience is ever fearful and unquiet. Thou shalt rest sweetly if thy heart condemn thee not." (031-002 to 031-006)

> "He will easily be contented and filled with peace, whose conscience is pure. Thou art none the holier if thou art praised, nor the viler if thou art reproached. Thou art what thou art; and thou canst not be better than God pronounceth thee to be." (031-023 to 031-026)

> "Then a pure and good conscience shall more rejoice than learned philosophy. Then contempt of riches shall have more weight than all the treasure of the children of this world." (024-056)

> "Diligently examine thy conscience and with all thy might with true contrition and humble confession cleanse and purify it, so that thou mayest feel no burden, nor know anything which bringeth thee remorse and impedeth thy free approach." (103-006 to 103-008)

## Biblical Foundation

The biblical foundation for the Kempian theology of conscience draws primarily from the Pauline and [[wisdom|Wisdom]] traditions. The good conscience as the aim of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life echoes 1 Timothy 1:5: "the end of the commandment is [[love|charity]] out of a pure heart, and of a good conscience, and of faith unfeigned." Paul's practice of keeping a conscience void of offence (Acts 24:16) is the apostolic model for Thomas's teaching. The confidence before God that a pure conscience brings reflects 1 John 3:21: "Beloved, if our heart condemn us not, then have we confidence toward God."

The text of The Imitation itself explicitly grounds its teaching on conscience in several scriptural passages. The declaration that "there is no peace, saith my God, to the wicked" (031-008) quotes [[isaiah|Isaiah]] 57:21. The teaching that "man looketh on the outward appearance, but the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] looketh on the heart" (031-028) draws from 1 Samuel 16:7. Thomas cites 2 Corinthians 10:18 — "not he that commendeth himself is approved, but whom the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] commendeth" (031-035) — to affirm that the only true witness is God's, not man's. The frank acknowledgment that "although I know nothing against myself, yet I am not hereby justified" (083-053 to 083-054) is a direct citation of 1 Corinthians 4:4, expressing the insufficiency of even a clear conscience apart from [[mercy|divine mercy]].
