# Prudence

**Prudence** (*prudentia*) in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* is the cardinal virtue of right judgment in action, treated not as worldly cleverness but as the spiritual wisdom that governs speech, counsel, and decision through humility and obedience to God. Thomas à Kempis devotes an entire chapter — Chapter IV of Book I — to "prudence in action," establishing it early in the work as a foundational disposition of the spiritual life.

## Prudence and Speech

The most extended treatment of prudence in the text concerns the government of the tongue. Kempis begins with a warning about credulity:

> "We must not trust every word of others or feeling within ourselves, but cautiously and patiently try the matter, whether it be of God. Unhappily we are so weak that we find it easier to believe and speak evil of others, rather than good" [004-001]

Prudence here is not suspicion but discernment — the patient [[temptation|testing]] of what one hears before accepting or repeating it. The acknowledgment of human weakness ("we are so weak") grounds the need for caution: prudence is not a counsel of perfection for the elite but a necessary guard for fallen nature.

The prudent person exercises restraint in both receiving and transmitting speech:

> "This is great wisdom, not to be hasty in action, or stubborn in our own opinions. A part of this wisdom also is not to believe every word we hear, nor to tell others all that we hear, even though we believe it" [004-001]

This dual discipline — not believing everything, not repeating everything — is the practical expression of prudence in community life. It prevents both the corruption of one's own judgment and the corruption of others through careless speech.

## Prudence and Silence

The exercise of prudence often requires silence, particularly in moments of conflict or danger. Kempis notes that knowing when not to speak is itself a mark of wisdom:

> "It is small prudence to keep silence in an evil time and to turn inwardly to Me, and not to be disturbed by human judgment" [065-006]

This passage from Book III reveals that prudence is not merely external caution but an interior [[conversion|turning to God]]. The silence that prudence counsels is not the silence of fear but the silence of recollection — the soul turns inward to God rather than outward to self-defense. When human judgment threatens, the prudent response is not argument but interior refuge.

## The Sources of Prudence

Kempis identifies three sources from which prudence grows: humility, good counsel, and a good life. Each is essential, and no one alone suffices:

First, humility: "The more humble a man is in himself, and the more obedient towards God, the wiser will he be in all things, and the more shall his soul be at peace" (004-001). [[humility|Humility]] opens the mind to receive wisdom; pride closes it.

Second, counsel: "Take counsel with a man who is wise and of a [[conscience|good conscience]]; and seek to be instructed by one better than thyself, rather than to follow thine own inventions" (004-001). The prudent person knows the limits of their own judgment and seeks the wisdom of others.

Third, a good life: "A good life maketh a man wise toward God, and giveth him experience in many things" (004-001). Prudence is not purely intellectual — it is forged in the practice of virtue. The one who lives well acquires a wisdom that mere study cannot provide.

Together these three sources form a complete pedagogy: humility opens the door, counsel provides external guidance, and a good life supplies the internal experience by which prudence becomes second nature.

## Biblical Foundation

The treatment of prudence in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* echoes the wisdom literature of the Old Testament, particularly Proverbs, where prudence is personified as dwelling with wisdom (Proverbs 8:12) and the prudent man "looketh well to his going" (Proverbs 14:15). The Gospel counsel to be "wise as serpents and harmless as doves" (Matthew 10:16) provides the dominical warrant for spiritual prudence. James's description of the "wisdom from above" as "pure, then peaceable, gentle, and easy to be intreated" (James 3:17) corresponds closely to Kempis's portrait of the prudent soul.
