# Mercy

The gratuitous, unearned loving-kindness of God toward sinners — in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], divine mercy is not merely one divine attribute among many but the very atmosphere in which the spiritual life is lived. Every advance in holiness, every recovery from sin, every [[comfort|consolation]] received is traced back to [[forgiveness|mercy]]: "Thou hast had [[forgiveness|mercy]] beyond all hope upon Thy servant, and hast showed Thy grace and friendship beyond all deserving" (047-014). Mercy is the ground beneath the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s feet — more fundamental than virtue, more reliable than effort, more extensive than any human calculus of deserving.

## Definition

Mercy, in the Kempian understanding, is the divine disposition to relieve misery and to forgive sin, grounded solely in God's goodness and not in the merit of the recipient. Thomas à Kempis distinguishes divine mercy sharply from human [[forgiveness|mercy]]: the former is infinite, gratuitous, and prevenient — it goes before any human deserving and creates the conditions for repentance and love. Human [[forgiveness|mercy]], by contrast, is the finite and imperfect imitation of this divine pattern. The soul that has received [[forgiveness|mercy]] is called to show [[forgiveness|mercy]], but the primary emphasis in The Imitation falls on receiving [[forgiveness|mercy]] rather than dispensing it. "[[trust|Trust]] in My [[forgiveness|mercy]]," [[jesus-christ|Christ]] says to the [[discipleship|disciple]]; "When thou thinkest thyself far removed from Me, I am often the nearer" (067-029). Mercy bridges the gap between the soul's sinfulness and God's holiness, and this bridging is entirely God's initiative.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Mercy is a pervasive theme in The Imitation, appearing in every book and in every register — as ground of confidence, as motive for gratitude, as pattern for human conduct, and as the ultimate horizon of the spiritual life.

### Mercy as the Ground of Hope

The most fundamental function of [[forgiveness|mercy]] in The Imitation is to provide the ground of hope. When the soul has exhausted its own resources, when friends and books and [[comfort|consolation]]s have all failed, one thing remains: "the great [[forgiveness|mercy]] of God, and the hope of heavenly grace" (034-056). This is not a secondary or fallback position; it is the primary and ultimate ground. Thomas asks rhetorically: "Whereupon then can I hope, or wherein may I [[trust-in-god|trust]], save only in the great [[forgiveness|mercy]] of God?" (034-055). The answer is that there is no other ground — not virtue, not knowledge, not the companionship of the good, not even the sacraments considered apart from the [[forgiveness|mercy]] they convey. Mercy alone is reliable because [[forgiveness|mercy]] alone depends on God's character rather than human performance.

### Mercy Beyond Deserving

Thomas insists repeatedly on the disproportion between divine mercy and human deserving. "Thou hast had [[forgiveness|mercy]] beyond all hope upon Thy servant, and hast showed Thy grace and friendship beyond all deserving" (047-014). The phrase "beyond all hope" is striking — [[forgiveness|mercy]] exceeds not only what the sinner deserves but even what the sinner dares to hope for. God's mercy is excessive, prodigal, disproportionate. This theme reaches its climax in the prayer before [[communion|Communion]]: "Pardon me also, and of Thy [[forgiveness|mercy]] deal gently with me, whensoever I shall do anything unworthy in Thy sight" (085-048). The [[discipleship|disciple]] approaches the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] not in confidence of his own worthiness but in confidence of divine mercy, which "vouchsafest to the unworthy, to the sinner, and to the vile" (089-012) the gift of God's own self.

### Mercy Evokes Gratitude

The recognition of [[forgiveness|mercy]] received produces gratitude — a gratitude that is the wellspring of the entire spiritual life. "What reward shall I render Thee for this Thy grace? For it is not given unto all to renounce this world and its affairs, and to take up a religious life" (047-014). The [[discipleship|disciple]] who has received [[forgiveness|mercy]] knows that his very vocation is a gift: it was "not given unto all" but granted to him by the sheer liberality of God. This awareness of unearned privilege produces humility rather than pride: the religious life is an occasion for gratitude, not for spiritual self-congratulation. "What then hath Thy servant to say in Thy presence, save to humble himself greatly before Thee, being alway mindful of his own [[sin|iniquity]] and vileness?" (058-061). Mercy, rightly received, leads not to [[pride|presumption]] but to deeper humility.

### Divine Mercy and Human Misery

Thomas draws a deliberate contrast between the greatness of divine mercy and the depth of human misery. The soul confesses: "How can I bear this miserable life unless Thy [[forgiveness|mercy]] and grace strengthen me?" (040-056). Mercy is proportioned to misery — the greater the misery, the more conspicuous the [[forgiveness|mercy]] that relieves it. This is not a counsel of despair but an encouragement: the soul that knows its own wretchedness most deeply is best positioned to receive [[forgiveness|mercy]] most abundantly. "When thou reckonest that almost all is lost, then often is greater opportunity of gain at hand" (067-029). Mercy's native territory is precisely the place of lostness, failure, and despair.

## Key Passages

> "Whereupon then can I hope, or wherein may I trust, save only in the great mercy of God, and the hope of heavenly grace? For whether good men are with me, godly brethren or faithful friends, whether holy books or beautiful discourses, whether sweet hymns and songs, all these help but little, and have but little savour when I am deserted by God's favour and left to mine own poverty." (034-055 to 034-057)

> "Thou hast had mercy beyond all hope upon Thy servant, and hast showed Thy grace and friendship beyond all deserving. What reward shall I render Thee for this Thy grace? For it is not given unto all to renounce this world and its affairs, and to take up a religious life." (047-014)

> "Let not thy heart be troubled, neither let it be afraid. Believe in Me, and put thy trust in My mercy. When thou thinkest thyself far removed from Me, I am often the nearer. When thou reckonest that almost all is lost, then often is greater opportunity of gain at hand." (067-029)

> "Pardon me also, and of Thy mercy deal gently with me, whensoever I shall do anything unworthy in Thy sight, and turn not away the eyes of Thy pity from me, a sinner, for the merits and intercession of Thy blessed Mother and of all the saints." (085-048)

## Biblical Foundation

The Kempian theology of [[forgiveness|mercy]] is firmly grounded in the great biblical declarations of God's compassionate nature. The fundamental Old Testament text is Exodus 34:6 — "The [[jesus-christ|Lord]], the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abundant in goodness and truth" — which establishes [[forgiveness|mercy]] as a defining divine attribute. Psalm 103:8 elaborates: "The [[jesus-christ|Lord]] is merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and plenteous in [[forgiveness|mercy]]." Thomas's insistence that [[forgiveness|mercy]] exceeds all deserving echoes Micah 7:18: "Who is a God like unto thee, that [[forgiveness|pardon]]eth [[sin|iniquity]]... he retaineth not his anger for ever, because he delighteth in [[forgiveness|mercy]]."

The New Testament deepens this understanding by linking [[forgiveness|mercy]] to the person and work of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Ephesians 2:4–5 declares that "God, who is rich in [[forgiveness|mercy]], for his great love wherewith he loved us, even when we were dead in [[sin|sins]], hath quickened us together with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]." The [[forgiveness|mercy]] that saves is not an abstract divine disposition but the concrete action of God in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Thomas's teaching that the [[discipleship|disciple]] must "put [his] [[trust-in-god|trust]] in [[[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s] [[forgiveness|mercy]]" (067-029) reflects this [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ological concentration of [[forgiveness|mercy]]. The Lucan exhortation — "Be ye therefore merciful, as your Father also is merciful" (Luke 6:36) — provides the pattern for human [[forgiveness|mercy]] as imitation of the divine, though Thomas's emphasis falls more heavily on receiving [[forgiveness|mercy]] than on dispensing it.
