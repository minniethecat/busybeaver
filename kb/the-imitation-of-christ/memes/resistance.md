# Resistance

The active spiritual effort by which the soul opposes temptation, sin, and the disordered inclinations of fallen human nature — in The Imitation of Christ, resistance is not a single heroic act but a continuous disposition of the will, and the moment of decision is decisive: the longer one delays, the more one weakens.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, resistance is the will's deliberate opposition to temptation, evil inclination, and the assaults of the enemy. It belongs to the active dimension of the spiritual life — not passive endurance alone but the conscious, prompt refusal of what draws the soul away from God. The text emphasizes the urgency of this act: delayed resistance compounds spiritual damage, while swift resistance severs temptation at its root.

Thomas à Kempis frames resistance within the ascetical struggle that defines Christian [[discipleship]]: the [[disciple]] cannot avoid temptation, but can and must resist it at its first stirring. This is the practical outworking of [[self-denial]] and the necessary precondition for spiritual progress.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation of Christ treats resistance as a central practice of the interior life, intimately connected to temptation, grace, and the soul's progress toward God. Its teaching is concentrated and practical: resistance must be prompt, rooted in humility, and sustained by divine help.

### The Danger of Delayed Resistance

Book I, Chapter XIII addresses the urgency of resistance directly: "the longer a man delayeth his resistance, the weaker doth he become, and the enemy stronger against him" (013-046). Delay is not neutral — it shifts the balance of power in favor of the enemy. Every moment of hesitation feeds the temptation and diminishes the will's capacity to refuse.

This psychological realism reflects the broader Kempian understanding of spiritual combat: the [[temptation|tempter]] works by gradual encroachment, and the soul that does not resist the first suggestion will soon find itself overwhelmed by what it could have easily repelled at the outset. The antidote is not elaborate strategy but simple decisiveness — the will's immediate "no" at the first stirring.

### Resistance in the Context of Spiritual Combat

The spiritual life is portrayed in The Imitation as continuous warfare, in which resistance is the soul's primary weapon. Book I counsels that "some there are who keep themselves in peace, and the same have peace also with others" (048-022) — but this peace is not passivity; it is the fruit of habitual resistance that has subdued interior disorder. The soul that routinely resists temptation becomes a source of peace rather than conflict.

Book III deepens this teaching by placing resistance within the framework of divine assistance: "before a little resistance [thou dost] fail. Hence it cometh to pass that thou art so often afflicted" (092-030). The soul's frailty is exposed precisely at the point where resistance falters. The acknowledgment of this weakness is not discouragement but the path to [[humility]] — recognition that the strength to resist comes not from oneself but from [[grace]].

### Resistance as Daily Practice

Resistance in The Imitation of Christ is not reserved for extraordinary trials but is woven into the fabric of daily life. Every occasion of [[temptation]], every stirring of disordered desire, every impulse toward sin becomes an opportunity for the decisive act of refusal. This transforms resistance from a reactive measure into a positive spiritual exercise — each act of resistance strengthens the habit of virtue, while each failure to resist entrenches the contrary habit.

### Key Passages

> "The longer a man delayeth his resistance, the weaker doth he become daily, and the enemy stronger against him." (013-046)

> "Some there are who keep themselves in peace, and the same have peace also with others. And some there are who neither have peace themselves, nor suffer others to have peace; they are troublesome to others, but always more troublesome to themselves." (048-022–024) (048-022–024)

> "Before a little resistance thou dost fail. Hence it cometh to pass that thou art so often afflicted with sorrow of heart." (092-030–031) (092-030–031)

## Biblical Foundation

The scriptural basis for resistance is rooted in the apostolic exhortations to spiritual combat. James commands: "Resist the devil and he will flee from you" (James 4:7), linking resistance directly to victory. Peter frames resistance within vigilance: "Be sober, be vigilant; because your adversary the devil walks about like a roaring lion, seeking whom he may devour. Resist him, steadfast in the faith" (1 Peter 5:8–9). Paul places resistance within the full armor of God: "Put on the whole armor of God, that you may be able to stand against the wiles of the devil" (Ephesians 6:11). And Christ's exhortation in Gethsemane — "Watch and pray, lest you enter into temptation. The spirit indeed is willing, but the flesh is weak" (Matthew 26:41) — reveals the interior dynamic that resistance must address: the gulf between the willing spirit and the weak flesh, bridged only by grace-enabled vigilance.

## Practical Application

The practice of resistance, as taught in The Imitation, consists in the soul's prompt refusal of temptation at its first suggestion — without delay, without negotiation, without entertaining the thought. It requires the daily acknowledgment of one's frailty (092-030) and the simultaneous trust that God supplies what human weakness cannot sustain. The faithful [[disciple]] resists not in their own strength but in the strength of Him who "hath overcome the world" (John 16:33).
