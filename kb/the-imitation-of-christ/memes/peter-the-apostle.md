# Peter the Apostle

Simon Peter, the Galilean fisherman called by Christ to be a "fisher of men" (Matthew 4:19), chief of the Twelve Apostles, the rock upon whom the Church was founded (Matthew 16:18) — whose threefold denial and threefold restoration, pastoral authority, and martyr's death make him the paradigmatic disciple who learned through failure that spiritual strength comes not from human resolution but from divine grace alone.

## Identity

Peter — originally named Simon, son of Jonah (or John) — was a fisherman of Bethsaida in Galilee when Jesus called him, together with his brother Andrew, to leave his nets and become a disciple. Christ gave him the Aramaic name *Cephas* (Greek *Petros*), meaning "Rock," declaring: "Thou art Peter, and upon this rock I will build My church; and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it" (Matthew 16:18). He belonged to Christ's innermost circle, present at the Transfiguration and the agony in Gethsemane, yet he is equally remembered for his threefold denial of Jesus during the passion — an episode recorded in all four Gospels.

After the Resurrection, Christ restored Peter with a threefold question — "Simon, son of Jonas, lovest thou Me?" — and a threefold commission: "Feed My lambs... Feed My sheep" (John 21:15–17). At Pentecost, Peter delivered the first Christian sermon, and three thousand were baptized (Acts 2:41). His subsequent ministry — healing the lame man at the Temple gate, opening the Church to the Gentiles through the conversion of Cornelius, presiding at the Council of Jerusalem (Acts 15) — established him as the visible head of the apostolic college. Tradition holds that he was crucified upside down in Rome under Nero (c. AD 64), considering himself unworthy to die in the same manner as his Lord.

Peter's two epistles — 1 Peter, an exhortation to steadfastness under persecution, and 2 Peter, a warning against false teachers and a defense of the apostolic hope — are part of the New Testament canon. It is principally through these epistles, and through the Johannine account of his restoration, that Peter appears in The Imitation of Christ.

## Biblical Narrative

### Key Events

- **The Call by the Sea of Galilee (Matthew 4:18–20; Luke 5:1–11)** — Peter leaves his nets at Christ's word, beginning his journey from fisherman to apostle. The miraculous draught of fishes, which overwhelms Peter with the sense of his own sinfulness — "Depart from me; for I am a sinful man, O Lord" (Luke 5:8) — prefigures the pattern of his whole discipleship: divine call, human inadequacy, and the sufficiency of grace.

- **The Confession at Caesarea Philippi (Matthew 16:13–20)** — Peter, speaking for the Twelve, confesses Jesus as "the Christ, the Son of the living God." Christ responds with the promise of the keys of the kingdom and the declaration that Peter is the rock upon which the Church will be built. This is the high point of Peter's confession and the foundation of his unique role in the apostolic college.

- **The Denial and Restoration (Matthew 26:69–75; John 21:15–19)** — On the night of Christ's trial, Peter denies knowing Him three times before the cock crows, fulfilling Jesus' prediction. After the Resurrection, by the Sea of Tiberias, Christ restores Peter with a threefold question: "Simon, son of Jonas, lovest thou Me more than these?" The symmetry of three denials and three affirmations is the Gospel's most profound treatment of repentance and restoration.

- **Pentecost (Acts 2:1–41)** — Filled with the Holy Spirit, Peter preaches to the Jerusalem crowds, and three thousand souls are added to the Church. The man who cowered before a servant girl now stands boldly before the nations, declaring the lordship of the crucified and risen Christ.

- **The Vision of the Sheet (Acts 10:9–16)** — Peter's vision of unclean animals descending from heaven, with the command "Rise, Peter; kill, and eat," opens the door to the Gentile mission. His subsequent baptism of Cornelius the centurion marks the decisive step in the Church's recognition that salvation is for all peoples.

- **Martyrdom in Rome (c. AD 64)** — According to tradition recorded by Clement of Rome and Tertullian, Peter was crucified under Nero, requesting to be inverted on the cross out of humility before his Lord. His death seals his life's trajectory: a fallible man made strong by grace, who learned that love for Christ must be proved not in words alone but in the laying down of life.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis invokes Peter primarily through his canonical epistles rather than through extended narrative reflection on his life. Peter appears as a source of apostolic authority and as an implicit example of the spiritual themes most dear to The Imitation: humility learned through failure, the necessity of watchfulness in the spiritual combat, and the soul's status as a pilgrim and stranger on earth.

The most sustained engagement with Peter occurs in Book II, Chapter IX — "Of the Lack of All Comfort" — where Thomas depicts Peter beseeching Christ on behalf of the faithful: "Peter beseecheth, that Christ's faithful ones may be strengthened in every tribulation" (090-014). The context is the soul's experience of desolation and the need for the intercession of the saints. Peter, who knew from personal experience the fragility of human resolution — who boasted "Though all men shall be offended because of Thee, yet will I never be offended" (Matthew 26:33) only to fall that very night — is the apostle best qualified to intercede for struggling disciples. Having himself been sifted by Satan (Luke 22:31), he knows the necessity of Christ's prayer that "thy faith fail not."

Thomas draws on 1 Peter for two key themes. First, the call to **spiritual sobriety and watchfulness**: "Be sober, be vigilant; because your adversary the devil, as a roaring lion, walketh about, seeking whom he may devour" (1 Peter 5:8, referenced at 061-020). The disciple who would persevere must treat the spiritual life as a combat requiring constant vigilance — a theme Peter learned in Gethsemane, where Christ warned him that "the spirit indeed is willing, but the flesh is weak" (Matthew 26:41). Second, the call to **pilgrim detachment**: "Dearly beloved, I beseech you as strangers and pilgrims, abstain from fleshly lusts, which war against the soul" (1 Peter 2:11, referenced at 090-040). This text directly supports the Kempian teaching that the Christian has "no continuing city here" (023-075) and must live as an exile on earth, whose true citizenship is in heaven.

Though Thomas does not narrate Peter's denial and restoration, the apostle's example pervades the work's understanding of how grace operates in human weakness. Peter's fall was not the end of his apostleship but — through repentance and Christ's restoring love — its deeper beginning. The disciple who, in Book III, laments "the old man liveth in me: he is not yet all crucified, not yet quite dead" (071-033) is a spiritual child of Peter: one who knows that sanctification is gradual, that falls do not disqualify, and that the same Christ who prayed for Peter's faith prays now for every struggling soul.

## Spiritual Significance

Peter's spiritual significance in the Christian tradition, and implicitly in The Imitation of Christ, lies in the paradox that the rock upon whom the Church is built was also the man who sank in the waves for lack of faith (Matthew 14:30–31) and who denied his Lord with oaths and curses. This is not a contradiction but the very texture of sanctity: the grace of God operating in earthen vessels, "that the excellency of the power may be of God, and not of us" (2 Corinthians 4:7).

For the disciple of The Imitation, Peter models several indispensable lessons. First, that **spiritual strength is received, not achieved** — the Peter who preaches at Pentecost is not the Peter of his own resolution but the Peter filled with the Holy Spirit. Second, that **failure, when met with repentance, deepens rather than disqualifies** — Peter's threefold denial, answered by Christ's threefold question of love, becomes the foundation of a pastoral ministry marked by profound humility. Third, that **love for Christ must be proved in action** — the question "Lovest thou Me?" is answered not with words alone but with the lifelong shepherding of Christ's flock, culminating in the final witness of martyrdom.

Peter's epistles furnish the disciple with a rule of life: "Be sober, be vigilant" — the spiritual combat requires constant watchfulness. "As strangers and pilgrims, abstain from fleshly lusts" — the soul's true homeland is not here. These are not abstract counsels but the hard-won wisdom of an apostle who learned, through failure and restoration, that "the end of all things is at hand: be ye therefore sober, and watch unto prayer" (1 Peter 4:7).
