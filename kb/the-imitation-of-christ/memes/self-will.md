# Self-Will

The stubborn adherence to one's own desires and judgments in opposition to the will of God — in The Imitation of Christ, self-will is the great enemy of interior peace, the hidden source of endless conflict and restlessness, and the disposition that must be broken for the soul to find rest in God.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, self-will names not the ordinary operation of the human will — which is a God-given faculty — but the will's disordered attachment to its own preferences and judgments. Self-will is the will curving in upon itself rather than opening outward toward God. It is distinguished from [[self-love]] in that self-love names the disordered affection, while self-will names the disordered action — the stubborn execution of what self-love desires.

The Imitation identifies self-will as operating even in apparently pious contexts: "it springeth from natural inclination, self-will, hope of reward" (015-013). That is, what presents itself as devotion may in fact be self-will masquerading in religious dress. The soul that insists on its own way in spiritual matters — its own preferred practices, its own chosen consolations — is still governed by self-will, even if the external form appears devout.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation of Christ wages war against self-will on every page, though it often names it by implication rather than directly. The text's constant call to [[obedience]], to [[self-denial]], to [[resignation]] — all of these are campaigns against the citadel of self-will.

### The Root of Human Misery

The Imitation traces human misery to its source: the refusal to surrender the will. Every interior disturbance, every conflict with neighbor, every experience of frustration and disappointment — all can be traced to the collision between what the self wills and what God wills. "It springeth from natural inclination, self-will, hope of reward" (015-013) — in these words, The Imitation exposes the mixed motives that corrupt even the pursuit of virtue.

### The Cure of Self-Will

The remedy is not the suppression of the will but its redirection — not the annihilation of desire but its reordering toward God. The soul that learns to say with Christ, "not as I will, but as You will" (Matthew 26:39), discovers that the death of self-will is the birth of true freedom. The Imitation teaches that [[obedience]] — to God directly and to lawful superiors for God's sake — is the school in which self-will is progressively broken and the will of God progressively embraced.

## Biblical Foundation

The scriptural diagnosis of self-will is nowhere clearer than in Paul's anguished confession: "For I know that in me (that is, in my flesh) nothing good dwells; for to will is present with me, but how to perform what is good I do not find" (Romans 7:18). The war between the renewed mind and the stubborn will is the universal experience of fallen humanity. Christ Himself provides both the diagnosis and the cure in Gethsemane: "not as I will, but as You will" (Matthew 26:39). This prayer of surrender is the pattern for every disciple. And Paul reminds the Philippians that "it is God who works in you both to will and to do for His good pleasure" (Philippians 2:13) — the healing of self-will is ultimately a work of grace.

## Practical Application

The teaching of The Imitation on self-will calls the [[disciple]] to examine not only actions but motives. Self-will can survive the renunciation of gross sins and take up residence in the very practices that appear most devout — in the choice of spiritual exercises, in the preference for one form of service over another, in the secret satisfaction of being right. The practical path is the daily prayer of Gethsemane: "not my will, but Yours be done" — spoken not once but continually, until the soul's default movement is no longer self-assertion but self-surrender.
