# Apostles

The apostles occupy a unique position in The Imitation of Christ: they are at once historical figures, spiritual exemplars, and a living summons. Thomas à Kempis does not provide extended biographical portraits of individual apostles — the work is not a hagiography — but he invokes them repeatedly as the archetype of the total response to Christ's call. They are the ones who "left all and followed" (cf. Matthew 19:27), and their witness stands as a permanent judgment on the half-hearted discipleship that Thomas labors to cure.

## Identity

The apostles are the Twelve chosen by Christ — "whom He also named apostles" (Luke 6:13) — and sent forth to preach the Gospel to all nations. In the vocabulary of The Imitation, they are "Thine apostles witness whom Thou hast made princes in all lands" (059-037). The title "princes in all lands" captures Thomas's understanding of their role: they are not merely historical messengers but universal rulers in the spiritual order, their authority extending across all times and places through the apostolic succession of the Church's teaching. Thomas mentions individual apostles by name sparingly — Peter (061-020, 090-014, 090-040), Paul (013-056, 031-035, 037-111, 073-007), and John (001-038 and others) appear as distinct figures — but most often he invokes the apostles collectively, as a choir of witnesses whose unanimity is itself part of their testimony. The apostles also appear among the "Prophets and Apostles and other teachers" (107-060) whose instruction forms the foundation of the faith.

## Biblical Narrative

The biblical account of the apostles runs from their calling by Christ through their post-Pentecost mission. The key episodes include: the call of the first disciples by the Sea of Galilee (Matthew 4:18-22); the formal selection of the Twelve (Matthew 10:2-4, Mark 3:14-19, Luke 6:13-16); their being sent forth to preach (Matthew 10:5-15); their presence at the Last Supper and in Gethsemane; their scattering at the crucifixion and regathering after the resurrection; the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20); and the descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost (Acts 2), after which they "went forth into all the earth, and their words unto the ends of the world" (cf. Romans 10:18, Psalm 19:4).

### Key Events

- The calling of the Twelve — Christ chooses and names the apostles (Matthew 10:2-4)
- The sending forth — the apostles are commissioned to preach and given authority (Matthew 10:5-15)
- The Last Supper — the apostles receive the Eucharist and the mandatum (John 13)
- Pentecost — the Holy Spirit descends upon the apostles (Acts 2:1-4)
- The apostolic mission — the apostles "went forth into all the earth" (cf. Romans 10:15)

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas invokes the apostles in two principal contexts: as witnesses to Christ and as models of the fervent spiritual life. The first context is doxological. In Book III, Chapter 59 ("Of the Offering of All Our Works to God"), Thomas prays: "Thine apostles witness whom Thou hast made princes in all lands" (059-037). The apostles are presented not as figures of the distant past but as present intercessors whose witness continues before the throne of God. The second context is parenetic. In Book III, Chapter 107, the apostles are grouped with "Prophets and Apostles and other teachers" (107-060) as those whose instruction the faithful must heed — a chain of teaching that descends from the prophets through the apostles to the Church's present teachers. The implicit message is clear: to disregard apostolic teaching is to sever oneself from the very foundation of the faith.

The Imitation does not treat the apostles as remote saints to be admired from a distance. Rather, by invoking them in prayer and exhortation, Thomas draws the reader into the same relationship with Christ that the apostles themselves enjoyed. What they saw with their eyes, the devout soul may possess through faith; what they preached to the nations, the devout soul must live. The apostles are not merely past witnesses but present companions on the way of discipleship.

> "Thine apostles witness whom Thou hast made princes in all lands." (059-037)

> "Thanks be to Thee... for the teaching of Thy servants the Prophets and Apostles and other teachers." (107-060)

## Spiritual Significance

The apostles exemplify three virtues central to The Imitation. First, **total obedience**: they "left all and followed" Christ without reservation, and their willingness to abandon nets, tax booths, and families models the radical detachment that Thomas demands of every disciple. Second, **evangelical poverty**: the apostles went forth "without gold or silver" (cf. Matthew 10:9), trusting entirely in divine providence — the very poverty of spirit that Thomas extols throughout the work. Third, **universal mission**: as "princes in all lands" (059-037), the apostles demonstrate that the call to follow Christ is not confined to one nation or era but extends to every soul in every age. Their witness is the permanent refutation of any Christianity that would settle for less than total self-gift.
