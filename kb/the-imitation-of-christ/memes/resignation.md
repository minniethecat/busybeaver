# Resignation

The surrender of the whole self — will, desires, preferences, fears, hopes, attachments — into the hands of God, so that the soul desires nothing but what God wills and refuses nothing that God sends. In The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], resignation is the apex of the ascetical life, the point at which self-will has been so thoroughly mortified that the soul can say with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]: "Not my will, but Thine be done" (Luke 22:42). "Of pure and entire resignation of self, for the obtaining liberty of heart" (074-001) — Thomas devotes an entire chapter to this theme, signalling its centrality to his vision of the spiritual life.

## Definition

Resignation, in the vocabulary of The Imitation, is not the passive resignation of despair or the stoic resignation of indifference. It is the active, loving surrender of self-will to God — an act of the will by which the will renounces its own autonomy and places itself entirely at God's disposal. The term "resignation" in Thomas's Latin (*resignatio*) carries the sense of giving back, handing over, yielding up a claim. The soul that practises resignation yields up its claim to self-determination, acknowledging that God's will is not only wiser than its own but also sweeter. "Then moreover with full resignation and with my whole will I offer myself up to Thee" (103-036) — resignation is an offering, an act of oblation in which the soul gives itself back to its Creator.

## In The Imitation of Christ

### The Liberty of Resignation

Thomas opens his chapter "Of Pure and Entire Resignation of Self" with a striking paradox: resignation, which appears to be the loss of liberty, is in fact the "obtaining liberty of heart" (074-001). The soul that clings to its own will is in bondage — bondage to its own desires, to the fear of disappointment, to the tyranny of circumstances. The soul that resigns itself wholly to God is free, because nothing can touch it that has not passed through the hands of its loving Father.

This liberty manifests as interior peace. The resigned soul does not fight against providence, does not rage against disappointment, does not sink into despondency when things go otherwise than it had hoped. It receives all things — prosperity and [[suffering|adversity]], [[comfort|consolation]] and desolation, health and sickness — with the same [[peace|tranquillity]], because it no longer has a will of its own that can be crossed. "My son, leave thyself, and thou shalt find Me" (074-001) — the [[jesus-christ|Christ]] of The Imitation promises that the soul that resigns itself will find God in the very space that self-occupation had filled.

### The Christological Pattern

The [[jesus-christ|Christ]] of the Cross is the supreme exemplar of resignation. Thomas invites the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "behold how the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] [[jesus-christ|Jesus]], the King of heaven and earth... did hang naked upon the Cross" (104-001), abandoned by friends, mocked by enemies, suffering in body and soul — and yet wholly resigned to the will of His Father. "Of resignation of self" (104-001), the chapter heading announces: the Cross is the school of resignation, and [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is its perfect teacher.

The [[discipleship|disciple]] who contemplates the Crucified learns resignation not as an abstract ideal but as a concrete, cruciform practice. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] did not resign Himself to the Father's will in the abstract; He resigned Himself to a specific set of circumstances — betrayal, humiliation, physical agony, [[desolation|spiritual desolation]] — that were the Father's will for Him at that moment. The [[discipleship|disciple]]'s resignation is learned in the same way: not by a global act of surrender but by the daily, specific surrender of this disappointment, this irritation, this unfulfilled desire, this cross.

### Resignation and the Will

Thomas is clear that resignation is an act of the will, not of the feelings. The resigned soul may feel rebellion, fear, grief, or reluctance; resignation does not require the absence of these feelings but the refusal to let them determine action. "With full resignation and with my whole will I offer myself up to Thee" (103-036) — the word "will" is essential. Resignation is the will's deliberate choice to say yes to God despite the feelings' clamorous no.

This act of the will is sustained by faith. The soul that resigns itself to God believes that God is good, that God's will is love, that whatever God permits is permitted for the soul's ultimate good. Without this faith, resignation would be impossible — there would be no reason to [[trust-in-god|trust]] the will to which one surrenders. With faith, resignation becomes not only possible but rational: the creature en[[trust-in-god|trust]]s itself to the Creator, the child to the Father, the beloved to the Lover.

## Key Passages

> "My son, thou canst not possess perfect liberty unless thou wholly renounce thyself. They are all in bondage who possess anything of their own will, who seek their own interests, who love themselves." (074-001, paraphrase of the chapter's opening)

> "Then moreover with full resignation and with my whole will I offer myself up to Thee, O Lord my God, to be Thy servant for ever." (103-036)

> "Behold how the Lord Jesus, the King of heaven and earth... did hang naked upon the Cross, derided, despised, and forsaken of all." (104-001, paraphrase)

## Biblical Foundation

The doctrine of resignation in The Imitation is founded on the dominical prayer: "Thy will be done in earth, as it is in heaven" (Matthew 6:10). The soul that prays these words sincerely has already begun the practice of resignation; the prayer is the daily renewal of the surrender. The supreme model is [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in Gethsemane: "Not my will, but Thine be done" (Luke 22:42) — resignation is the echo of this prayer in the soul of every [[discipleship|disciple]]. The Pauline exhortation to "present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy, acceptable unto God" (Romans 12:1) describes the oblation of self that resignation effects. [[job|Job]]'s confession — "The [[jesus-christ|Lord]] gave, and the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] hath taken away; blessed be the name of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" ([[job|Job]] 1:21) — is the Old Testament pattern of resignation in the face of [[suffering|adversity]]. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ological hymn of Philippians 2, in which [[jesus-christ|Christ]] "humbled Himself, and became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross" (Philippians 2:8), reveals resignation as the form of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own self-offering.
