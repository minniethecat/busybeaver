# Conversion

Thomas à Kempis writes for souls that have already made the great renunciation — men and women who have left [[world|the world]] and entered the cloister. Yet even for these, conversion remains the central fact of the spiritual life, not as a single past event but as a present reality that must be renewed daily. "Help me, O God, from the first day of our conversion" (019-008): the prayer presupposes that conversion has a definite beginning, a "first day" that can be remembered and celebrated. But it also presupposes that the grace of that first day must be continually sought anew. Conversion, in The Imitation, is both punctual and progressive — a decisive break with the past and an ongoing turning toward God that is never complete in this life.

## Definition

Conversion, for Thomas, is the whole-souled turning of the person from sin to God. It is distinguished from mere remorse or regret by its completeness: the truly converted soul does not merely lament the past but leaves it behind. "The kingdom of God is within you, saith the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]. Turn thee with all thine heart unto the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], and forsake this miserable world, and thy soul shall find rest" (002-009). The three elements of conversion — turning the heart, forsaking [[world|the world]], finding rest — form a single movement. The heart cannot turn to God while still clinging to [[world|the world]], and it cannot find rest until the turning is complete. Conversion is total or it is not conversion at all.

Yet Thomas is also a realist about the human condition. He knows that the initial [[devotion|fervour]] of conversion cools, that the soul newly turned to God is "more zealous in the beginning of our conversion than after many years" (011-033). This cooling is not a negation of the original conversion but a test of its genuineness. The true convert is the one who, discovering that [[devotion|fervour]] has waned, does not despair but begins again: "Help me, O God, from the first day of our conversion" (019-008). To pray this prayer after many years in the religious life is to acknowledge that conversion is a daily necessity, not a past achievement.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas's teaching on conversion is woven through the early chapters of Book I, where he addresses the no[[sin|vice]] who has just entered the religious life, and reappears in the sacramental chapters of Book IV, where conversion becomes a preparation for worthy communion. Throughout, he treats conversion less as a doctrine to be explained than as a posture to be maintained.

### The Beginning of Conversion

Thomas speaks of "the first day of our conversion" (019-008) with a certain tenderness. The beginning is marked by special graces — an unusual clarity about one's [[sin|sins]], a readiness to renounce all for God, a [[devotion|fervour]] in prayer and mortification that astonishes the convert himself. "Some preserve a good [[devotion|fervour]] until their conversion, some at the end" (013-049): conversion can occur at different moments in a life, and the timing is in God's hands. What matters is not when conversion happens but whether it endures.

The danger of the beginning is the illusion that its [[devotion|fervour]] will last of itself. Thomas warns that the newly converted soul is like a child learning to walk — it needs constant support and will fall often. "We were more zealous in the beginning of our conversion than after many years" (011-033): the frank admission that [[devotion|fervour]] declines is meant not to discourage but to prepare. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who expects his first [[devotion|fervour]] to last forever will be scandalised when it fades; the [[discipleship|disciple]] who knows that [[devotion|fervour]] must be daily rekindled will not be surprised by its ebb and flow.

### The Daily Renewal of Conversion

The heart of Thomas's teaching on conversion is his insistence on daily renewal. "He who resolveth bravely often falleth; what shall he do who seldom or feebly resolveth?" (019-013). Conversion is not a single resolution made once and for all but a resolution that must be renewed every morning, because the forces that oppose it — [[world|the world]], the flesh, and the devil — never sleep. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who thinks his conversion is complete because he once made a good confession or once felt a strong movement of the heart toward God has misunderstood the nature of the spiritual life entirely.

This daily renewal takes concrete form in Thomas's exhortations to self-examination, confession, and the [[eucharist|Eucharist]]. "Except ye be converted and become as little children, ye shall not enter into the kingdom of heaven" (095-090): Thomas quotes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s words to emphasise that conversion is not a single crisis but a permanent disposition. The little child does not convert once and then forget about it; the little child lives in a state of continual dependence on the Father. To be converted is to become a child — to acknowledge one's helplessness and to turn to God anew each day.

### Conversion and the Mercy of God

Thomas anchors the possibility of conversion in the character of God. "He knoweth well how to convert the evil into good" (016-010): the God who can bring good out of evil can bring a saint out of a sinner. No one is beyond the reach of converting grace. The prophet Ezekiel's words — "Turn ye, turn ye from your evil ways; for why will ye die?" (Ezekiel 33:11) — echo behind Thomas's conviction that conversion is always possible, even after great falls, even after long years of lukewarmness. "If the wicked turn from his wickedness, and do that which is lawful and right, he shall live thereby" (103-049). The [[forgiveness|mercy]] of God sets no limit on the number of times a soul may turn back. The only irreversible state is the refusal to turn at all.

This confidence in [[mercy|divine mercy]] has a practical consequence: the [[discipleship|disciple]] who has fallen should rise again immediately. "A good, peaceable man converteth all things into good" (028-005), Thomas observes — and the conversion of all things into good begins with the conversion of one's own failures into occasions of humility. The fall that leads to a deeper knowledge of oneself and a firmer [[faith|trust in God]] has been, in a paradoxical sense, a blessing.

> "He knoweth well how to convert the evil into good, and in the good there abideth no evil." (016-010)

> "A good, peaceable man converteth all things into good." (028-005)

## Biblical Foundation

Thomas's teaching on conversion draws from the prophetic call to return to God that runs through the Old Testament. Joel's exhortation — "turn ye even to Me with all your heart, and with fasting, and with weeping, and with mourning: and rend your heart, and not your garments" (Joel 2:12-13) — provides the pattern of inward conversion that Thomas commends: not an external show but an interior turning of the whole heart. Ezekiel's plea — "turn yourselves from all your [[sin|transgression]]s; so [[sin|iniquity]] shall not be your ruin" (Ezekiel 18:30) — makes clear that conversion is both a human responsibility and a divine gift.

[[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own preaching begins with the call to conversion: "Repent ye, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (Matthew 4:17). The word Thomas renders "conversion" is the same as the New Testament "repentance" — metanoia, the change of mind that is also a change of life. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s insistence that "except ye be converted, and become as little children, ye shall not enter into the kingdom of heaven" (Matthew 18:3) provides Thomas with his controlling image: conversion is becoming childlike, recovering the simplicity and dependence that were lost in the fall. St Peter's preaching at Pentecost — "Repent ye therefore, and be converted, that your [[sin|sins]] may be blotted out" (Acts 3:19) — links conversion with the forgiveness of [[sin|sins]], the objective ground of the whole spiritual life.
