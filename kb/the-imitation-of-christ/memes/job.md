# Job

The Old Testament patriarch and righteous sufferer whose unwavering faith through the loss of all earthly goods, his children, and his health made him the supreme biblical exemplar of patience — cited by Thomas a Kempis at three pivotal junctures in The Imitation of Christ.

## Identity

Job is a patriarch of the land of Uz, presented in the biblical book that bears his name as "perfect and upright, and one that feared God, and eschewed evil" (Job 1:1). He is neither an Israelite nor a priest or prophet in the conventional sense, but a righteous layman whose life becomes the stage for the great drama of undeserved suffering and divine sovereignty. In Christian tradition, Job is venerated as the archetype of patient endurance — Saint James holds him up as the model of steadfastness: "Ye have heard of the patience of Job, and have seen the end of the Lord" (James 5:11). The book of Ezekiel names him alongside Noah and Daniel as one of three paragons of righteousness (Ezekiel 14:14).

## Biblical Narrative

The book of Job, likely composed between the 7th and 4th centuries BC, recounts how a man of exemplary piety is plunged into catastrophic suffering — not as punishment for sin, but as the subject of a heavenly wager. The narrative unfolds in three movements: the prologue set in heaven and on earth (Job 1–2), the long poetic dialogue between Job and his three friends (Job 3–37), and the divine response that issues in Job's restoration (Job 38–42).

### Key Events

- **The Heavenly Council and the First Trial** (Job 1:1–22): Satan appears before God among the "sons of God" and challenges Job's integrity, alleging that his piety is merely a transaction for divine protection. God permits Satan to strip Job of his possessions and his children in a single day. Job's response — "Naked came I out of my mother's womb, and naked shall I return thither: the Lord gave, and the Lord hath taken away; blessed be the name of the Lord" — becomes the classic utterance of faithful submission (106-012, which alludes to the scene of Satan among the sons of God).

- **The Second Trial and the Ash Heap** (Job 2:1–13): Satan returns to the heavenly court and obtains permission to afflict Job's body with "sore boils from the sole of his foot unto his crown." Job sits among the ashes, scraping himself with a potsherd. His wife urges him to "curse God, and die," but Job refuses: "What? shall we receive good at the hand of God, and shall we not receive evil?" His three friends — Eliphaz, Bildad, and Zophar — arrive to comfort him, but sit in silence for seven days, overwhelmed by the magnitude of his suffering.

- **The Protest and the Divine Answer** (Job 3–42:6): Job breaks the silence with a curse upon the day of his birth, triggering a long cycle of speeches in which his friends insist that suffering must be punishment for hidden sin, while Job maintains his innocence and demands an answer from God. God finally speaks "out of the whirlwind" with questions that reveal the unfathomable gulf between divine wisdom and human understanding. Job's final reply — "I have heard of thee by the hearing of the ear: but now mine eye seeth thee. Wherefore I abhor myself, and repent in dust and ashes" — marks his transformation from protest to humble adoration (013-003, which cites Job 7:1 to ground the universal truth of human trial).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas a Kempis invokes the figure of Job at three critical junctures, each time to illuminate a distinct aspect of the spiritual life: the universality of trial, the rhythm of consolation and desolation, and the reality of spiritual warfare. These references, though brief, carry considerable weight because they anchor the Imitation's teaching in the concrete example of a man whose experience of suffering exceeded nearly every other biblical figure.

Job appears first in Book I, Chapter XIII ("Of Resisting Temptation"), where his words supply the fundamental axiom of the entire Kempian treatment of suffering: "So long as we live in the world, we cannot be without trouble and trial. Wherefore it is written in Job, The life of man upon the earth is a trial" (013-003). This single citation — drawn from Job 7:1 — sets the frame for everything the Imitation teaches about the inevitability of adversity. No state of life, no degree of holiness, no retreat from the world can exempt the soul from trial, because trial is the very condition of earthly existence (013-002).

The second invocation comes in Book III, Chapter XXXIV ("Of the Lack of All Comfort"), where Thomas draws on Job's experience to illuminate the spiritual rhythm of consolation and withdrawal. Citing Job 7:18 — "Thou dost visit him in the morning, and suddenly Thou dost prove him" — the author presents Job as the witness to the truth that even the greatest saints experience the alternation of divine favor and divine testing (034-053). This passage appears in the context of the author's observation that "with the great saints and the ancient prophets there was often this manner of change" (034-038), and the section concludes with the remarkable assertion that "there is no better remedy, then, than patience and denial of self, and an abiding in the will of God" (034-061). Job's example thus serves to normalize the experience of spiritual desolation: what the disciple undergoes is not a sign of reprobation but the common lot of the righteous.

The third reference, in Book IV, Chapter VI ("That Holy Communion Is Not Lightly to Be Omitted"), draws on the prologue of Job to explain a different form of trial — the spiritual assault that afflicts the devout soul precisely when it draws near to God. "The very evil spirit himself (as is written in Job), cometh among the sons of God that he may trouble them by his accustomed evil dealing" (106-012). Thomas uses Job's heavenly scene to interpret the experience of his readers: "For when some set about to prepare themselves for Holy Communion, they suffer from the more evil suggestions of Satan" (106-011). Here Job becomes the prototype of the devout soul who suffers demonic assault not because of sin but because of proximity to God.

Throughout the Imitation, Job stands as the living proof of the patience Thomas commends — the patience that "considereth not by what man he is tried... but indifferently from every creature... he gratefully accepteth all from the hand of God" (056-022). And the Imitation's promise that "every burden of tribulation is turned into an assurance of divine comfort" (037-069) finds its narrative fulfillment in Job's own story, which ends not in despair but in the restoration of all he had lost.

## Spiritual Significance

Job's spiritual significance in the Christian tradition lies in his embodiment of a profound and difficult truth: that suffering is not necessarily punishment for sin, and that faith that endures without understanding is the highest form of trust in God. Where Job's friends insisted on a mechanical connection between sin and suffering — a view the Imitation itself resists — Job held fast to both his innocence and his faith in a God whose ways he could not comprehend. His cry, "Though he slay me, yet will I trust in him" (Job 13:15), distills the essential posture of the disciple in The Imitation of Christ: absolute confidence in divine goodness even when all evidence of that goodness has vanished from sight.

This posture makes Job the biblical archetype of the "royal way of the holy cross" that Thomas a Kempis celebrates. The Imitation teaches that "if thou willingly bear the Cross, it will bear thee, and will bring thee to the end which thou seekest" (037-047) — a truth Job embodied centuries before the Cross was raised. His patience was not passive resignation but an active, agonized holding-on to God against every reason to let go. He is therefore the patron not of those who suffer easily but of those who suffer honestly, who protest and question and yet refuse to curse God and die.

The Imitation draws on Job to teach three specific lessons: that trial is universal and inescapable (013-003), that consolation and desolation alternate even in the lives of the greatest saints (034-053), and that spiritual assault may be a sign of nearness to God rather than distance from Him (106-012). In all three respects, Job exemplifies the virtue of patience — not as a placid temperament but as the disciplined habit of the soul that "endureth all things" in confident hope of God's ultimate vindication.
