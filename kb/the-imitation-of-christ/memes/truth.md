# Truth

The eternal and unchanging reality of God Himself — not a proposition to be assented to but a Divine Person who teaches the soul inwardly, making all merely human learning, philosophy, and sensory knowledge appear as vanity when set beside His direct instruction.

## Definition

In the spiritual theology of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], Truth is not primarily an intellectual category — it is a name for God. Thomas à Kempis opens his treatment of this theme with one of the most striking declarations in all of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian devotional literature: "Happy is the man whom Truth by itself doth teach, not by figures and passing sounds, but as it is in itself" (003-002). Truth here is not an abstract principle but a living Teacher, identified with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself who declared "I am the way, the truth, and the life" (John 14:6). The Truth that teaches from within is the Eternal Word, the second Person of the Trinity, whose inward instruction renders all human opinion, eloquence, and reasoning ultimately superfluous. "To whom the Eternal Word speaketh, is delivered from a multitude of opinions," Thomas writes (003-018). The discipline of the spiritual life consists in silencing every other voice so that this one Voice — Truth itself — may be heard.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis devotes the whole of Book I, Chapter III — "Of the Teaching of Truth" — to this theme, and the concept of Truth as divine inward Teacher recurs throughout all four books. The chapter opens with an uncompromising thesis: the teaching that matters is not that which comes through human words or books but that which comes directly from God to the interior of the soul. This is the foundation of the *devotio moderna*'s characteristic emphasis on interiority and personal experience of God over against scholastic erudition.

### Truth as Inward Teacher

The central claim is that Truth — that is, God Himself — instructs the soul without intermediary. "Happy is the man whom Truth by itself doth teach, not by figures and passing sounds, but as it is in itself" (003-002). Human words, even the words of Scripture read outwardly, are "figures and passing sounds" — they point toward reality but are not themselves the reality. Only when the Eternal Word speaks inwardly does the soul receive [[knowledge|true knowledge]]. "Our own opinion and our own sense do often deceive us, and they see but little" (003-003). The solution is not to acquire more information but to submit the intellect to the inward teaching of divine Truth.

This emphasis on interior teaching does not denigrate Scripture — Thomas is himself steeped in the Bible — but it radically relativizes all external authorities. "What doth it profit thee to dispute deeply about the Trinity, if thou be lacking in humility, so that thou displeasest the Trinity?" (003-008). The test of [[knowledge|true knowledge]] is not theological precision but the transformation of life.

### Truth in Holy Scripture

Thomas distinguishes carefully between the outward letter of Scripture and the inward Truth to which it points. "It is Truth which we must look for in Holy Scripture, not cunning," he insists — neither eloquence nor intellectual ingenuity but the living God who speaks through the sacred page (005-002). Scripture is to be read "in the same spirit with which it was made" (005-006), which is to say, with the disposition of humility and receptivity rather than critical or curious inquiry. "Ask not, who said this, but mark what is said" (005-009) — the authority of the divine word resides not in its human author but in the Truth who speaks through it.

### Truth as the Antidote to Vanity

Book III returns to the theme of Truth in the context of human vanity and self-deception. "This is the Truth, and by it the vanity of boasting is shown, and the weakness of man is discovered" (046-020). The encounter with divine Truth exposes every pretense, every hidden motive, every self-congratulatory impulse. This exposure is painful but salutary: "Confounded be all [[glory|vain-glory]] which cometh from men" (046-019). The soul who has been taught by Truth no longer seeks human approval because it has seen how empty such approval is when measured against the [[judgment|divine judgment]].

### Christ as Truth Personified

Throughout the work, Truth is a divine title. Thomas addresses God as "O God my Truth and my [[mercy|Mercy]]" (077-043) and speaks of "Eternal Truth" in the vocative — it is a Person to whom one prays, not a concept one masters. "I will teach thee, saith the Truth, the things which are right and pleasing unto Me" (041-011). The soul's proper posture before this Teacher is silence, receptivity, and the willingness to be corrected: "Speak, [[jesus-christ|Lord]], for Thy servant heareth" (041-007).

> "Happy is the man whom Truth by itself doth teach, not by figures and passing sounds, but as it is in itself. Our own opinion and our own sense do often deceive us, and they see but little." (003-002–003)

> "It is Truth which we must look for in Holy Scripture, not cunning. All Scripture ought to be read in the same spirit with which it was made." (005-002, 005-006)

> "I will teach thee, saith the Truth, the things which are right and pleasing unto Me. If thou think rightly and in Truth, thou wilt count thyself but dust and acknowledge thyself to be very nothing." (041-011)

## Biblical Foundation

The Kempian doctrine of Truth as inward Teacher draws on a rich scriptural substrate. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s self-identification — "I am the way, the truth, and the life: no man cometh unto the Father, but by Me" (John 14:6) — is the theological cornerstone: Truth is not a what but a Who. The promise that "ye shall know the truth, and the truth shall make you free" (John 8:32) is understood by Thomas not as intellectual liberation but as freedom from sin, self-deception, and the tyranny of human opinion. The [[holy-spirit|Holy Spirit]] is "[[holy-spirit|the Spirit]] of truth" who "will guide you into all truth" (John 16:13), and this promise of inward guidance by [[holy-spirit|the Spirit]] grounds Thomas's confidence that the soul can be taught directly by God. The psalmist's declaration — "Thy word is true from the beginning" (Psalm 119:160) — and the Apostle's assurance that "[[holy-spirit|the Spirit]] is truth" (1 John 5:6) complete the biblical foundation: Truth is eternal, personal, and communicated to the soul by the indwelling Spirit. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s words before Pilate — "Every one that is of the truth heareth My voice" (John 18:37) — define the [[discipleship|disciple]]: one who hears and obeys the inward voice of Truth.

## Practical Application

The teaching on Truth carries an immediate practical consequence: the cultivation of interior silence. If Truth speaks from within, the soul must learn to be still enough to hear. Thomas counsels the [[discipleship|disciple]] to withdraw from "the manifold babble of men" (003-004) because "a perfect contempt of [[world|the world]] and a fervent remembrance of God is a great step forward to the kingdom of heaven" (003-015). This is not anti-intellectualism but a reordering of priorities: "What doth it profit a man to gain the whole world of knowledge and lose his own soul?" The [[discipleship|disciple]] is urged to approach Scripture, prayer, and the sacraments not as occasions for gathering information but as encounters with the living God who is Truth. Every reading, every sermon, every spiritual exercise is to be measured by a single question: has it brought the soul nearer to that inward Teacher who alone can satisfy the hunger for truth?
