# Amendment

**Amendment** (*emendatio vitae* — "amendment of life") is a foundational ascetical practice in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]*. It denotes the deliberate, continual, and zealous reformation of one's entire manner of living, turning away from sin and [[lukewarmness|spiritual sloth]] toward a life wholly ordered to God. Thomas à Kempis devotes an entire chapter to "the zealous amendment of our whole life" (Book I, Chapter 25) (025-001) and returns to the theme throughout all four books, particularly in the context of sacramental preparation.

## The Urgency of Now

One of the most distinctive features of Kempis's teaching on amendment is its insistence on immediacy. There is no room for postponement:

> Arise, begin this very moment, and say, "Now is the time to do: now is the time to fight, now is the proper time for amendment." [022-043]

This urgency is rooted in the uncertainty of life and the danger of spiritual complacency. The text warns that "we cannot promise to ourselves one day, yea, one hour, for amendment of life" (023-047). Every moment of delay is a moment of spiritual peril; the present instant is the only time we are sure of possessing.

The same note sounds in the [[death|meditation on death]]: "How many, taken away by sudden death, have perished before their time? And what has become of them? They had no time for amendment" (023-047). This *[[death|memento mori]]* logic drives the practical conclusion — do not wait, begin now.

## Zeal and the Whole Life

Kempis insists that amendment must be "zealous" and must encompass the "whole life" (025-001). Partial amendment — reforming one area while neglecting another — is insufficient. The chapter title itself signals this totality: "Of the zealous amendment of our whole life."

The text exhorts: "Be thou watchful and diligent in God's ser[[sin|vice]], and bethink thee often why thou hast renounced [[world|the world]]. Was it not that thou mightest live to God and become a spiritual man? Be zealous, therefore, for thy spiritual profit" [025-002-005]. The monastic vocation of renunciation is meaningless without corresponding amendment of life.

A crucial passage identifies the obstacle to amendment: "the dread of the difficulty and the labour of the conflict holdeth back many from progress and fervent amendment" (025-027). Fear of the cost — the effort required to truly change — keeps souls in mediocrity.

## The Story of the Anxious Man

Book I, Chapter 25 contains a memorable exemplum: a man "continually tossed about between hope and fear" casts himself in prayer before an altar, crying "Oh! if I but knew that I should still persevere" [025-013-017]. The divine answer comes as a voice: "And if thou didst know it, what wouldst thou do? Do now what thou wouldst do then, and thou shalt be very secure" [025-018-019].

This narrative encapsulates the entire theology of amendment: **stop speculating about the future and act in the present**. The man, comforted, "committed himself to the will of God" and "studied rather to inquire what was the good and acceptable will of God, for the beginning and perfecting of every good work" [025-020-024].

## Amendment as Continual Process

Amendment is not a one-time conversion but a lifelong discipline. Kempis acknowledges human frailty: "To-day thou confessest thy [[sin|sins]], and to-morrow thou committest again the very same things" (022-055). Yet this realism does not lead to despair — it leads to renewed resolution.

In Book IV, preparation for [[communion|Holy Communion]] includes "a firm resolution of continual amendment of life" (103-035). The [[eucharist|Eucharist]] becomes both the motivation for amendment and its supernatural source: receiving [[jesus-christ|Christ]] kindles the soul "anew to amendment of life" (108-034).

The self-aware confession of slowness to change appears in the prayer of Book III: "I have been ever prone to sin and slow to amendment" (089-018). This honest acknowledgment, far from being an excuse, becomes the foundation for humble dependence on [[grace|divine grace]].

## The Fruit of Amendment

In Book IV, Kempis frankly notes that for many who receive [[communion|Communion]] frequently, "very little fruit of amendment is borne away" (097-081). This sober observation underscores that external religious practice, even the highest sacramental act, is not automatic in its effects. Amendment requires the cooperation of the will.

Yet those "who order their whole life to amendment" find that even their [[sin|fault]]s work toward their good (097-093). Divine providence draws spiritual profit from human weakness when the fundamental orientation of the will is toward reform.
