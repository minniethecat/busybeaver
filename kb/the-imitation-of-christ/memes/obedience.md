# Obedience

The willing submission of one's own will to God and to lawful human authority, understood in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as the outward expression of inward humility, the safeguard of grace, and the path of peace and spiritual progress.

## Definition

In The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], obedience is the voluntary surrender of self-will — the renunciation of one's own desires, opinions, and inclinations in favour of the will of another, undertaken for the love of God. Thomas à Kempis presents obedience not primarily as a legal or institutional requirement but as a spiritual discipline essential to the interior life. It is "verily a great thing to live in obedience, to be under authority, and not to be at our own disposal" (009-002), for subjection is "far safer" than the exercise of authority itself. Obedience is so closely tied to [[grace|divine grace]] that "he who striveth to withdraw himself from obedience, withdraweth himself also from grace" (050-003). The disposition of obedience is therefore the outward expression of inward humility — it is the concrete form that humility takes when it encounters the concrete claims of God and neighbour (009-008, 050-021).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Throughout the four books, Thomas à Kempis treats obedience as a pillar of the spiritual life, inseparable from humility, self-denial, and love. Obedience is not mere compliance but a deliberate mortification of the will, which is the seat of pride.

### Obedience to God

The primary and ultimate object of obedience is God Himself. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is called to hear God's words "in silence, and to be received with all humility and with deep love" (040-007). This obedience to God is a matter of the heart: "The more humble a man is in himself, and the more obedient towards God, the wiser will he be in all things, and the more shall his soul be at peace" (004-015). Such obedience is not burdensome to the one who loves God; rather, love itself "is subject and obedient to all that are in authority, vile and lowly in its own sight" (042-061). Yet Thomas à Kempis recognizes the persistent drag of fallen nature: even the devout soul experiences "captivity to the obedience of sensuality in many things" (092-007) and finds itself obeying "sensuality rather than reason" (092-026), which makes grace indispensable for true obedience.

### Obedience to Superiors

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] gives sustained attention to obedience within hierarchical relationships — the monk to his abbot, the subject to the ruler, the inferior to the superior. This obedience is a school of humility: "If a man submit not freely and willingly to one set over him, it is a sign that his flesh is not yet perfectly subject to himself, but often resisteth and murmureth" (050-006). The unwillingness to submit reveals the root problem: "Because as yet thou inordinately lovest thyself, therefore thou shrinkest from yielding thyself to the will of others" (050-015). Conversely, the holy fathers of old "stood fast in true humility, they lived in simple obedience, they walked in love and patience; and thus they waxed strong in spirit, and obtained great favour before God" (018-035). Thomas à Kempis distinguishes between two kinds of obedience: "Many are in obedience from necessity rather than from love; these take it amiss, and repine for small cause. Nor will they gain freedom of spirit, unless with all their heart they submit themselves for the love of God" (009-004). Only the obedience born of love brings the reward of interior peace.

### The Example of Christ

The theological centre of the Kempian teaching on obedience is the example of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]. The voice of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in Book III draws the sharpest possible contrast between the pretensions of human pride and the humility of the Incarnate Word:

> "But what great thing is it that thou, who art dust and nothingness, yieldest thyself to man for God's sake, when I, the Almighty and the Most High, who created all things out of nothing, subjected Myself to man for thy sake? I became the most humble and despised of men, that by My humility thou mightest overcome thy pride." (050-016)

[[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own subjection — even to death — is the paradigm and the motive for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian obedience. The command "Learn to obey, O dust! Learn to humble thyself, O earth and clay, and to bow thyself beneath the feet of all" (050-020) is grounded entirely in what [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself has done.

### Obedience vs Self-Will

The central obstacle to obedience is self-will — the stubborn attachment to one's own opinion, desire, and judgment. Thomas à Kempis repeatedly contrasts obedience with the prideful insistence on having one's own way. The [[discipleship|disciple]] must "sometimes yield up our own opinion for the sake of peace" (009-013), for a refusal to listen to others "is a mark of pride or wilfulness" (009-022). The difficult cases are precisely those where the command seems unreasonable: "because thou darest not resist a higher power, being under authority, therefore it seemeth hard for thee to shape thy course according to the nod of another, and to forego thine own opinion" (086-057). Yet it is in such crucibles that obedience is proved genuine. Readiness to obey stands alongside repentance and self-denial as one of the things "which shall give great confidence of a happy death" (023-032).

## Key Passages

> "My Son, he who striveth to withdraw himself from obedience, withdraweth himself also from grace, and he who seeketh private advantages, loseth those which are common unto all." (050-003)

> "It is verily a great thing to live in obedience, to be under authority, and not to be at our own disposal. Far safer is it to live in subjection than in a place of authority." (009-002)

> "Learn to obey, O dust! Learn to humble thyself, O earth and clay, and to bow thyself beneath the feet of all. Learn to crush thy passions, and to yield thyself in all subjection." (050-020)

> "No man safely ruleth but he who loveth to be subject. No man safely commandeth but he who loveth to obey." (020-018)

> "There shall appear the fruit of obedience, the labour of repentance shall rejoice, and humble subjection shall be crowned gloriously." (086-074)

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] grounds its teaching on obedience in the entire biblical narrative of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s kenosis. The foundational text is Philippians 2:8, where [[jesus-christ|Christ]] "humbled himself, and became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross." This is the pattern for every [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian: as [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s obedience reversed Adam's disobedience (Romans 5:19), so the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s obedience reverses the disobedience of pride. Hebrews 5:8 declares that [[jesus-christ|Christ]] "learned obedience by the things which he suffered" — a truth reflected in the Kempian insistence that obedience is learned through the hard discipline of yielding one's will. The link between love and obedience echoes John 14:15: "If ye love me, keep my [[law|commandments]]." The obedience to earthly authority taught in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] draws on 1 Peter 2:13: "Submit yourselves to every ordinance of man for the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]'s sake." Throughout the work, the contrast between the obedience of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and the disobedience of fallen humanity runs as a silent presupposition beneath every admonition to submit.

## Related Concepts

- [[humility]] — The inward disposition of which obedience is the outward expression; "by My humility thou mightest overcome thy pride" (050-020)
- [[self-denial]] — The practical discipline that makes obedience possible by mortifying self-will
- [[patience]] — The companion virtue that enables the [[discipleship|disciple]] to endure the difficulties of obedience without resentment
- [[cross]] — Obedience to the point of suffering, [[discipleship|following Christ]] who was "obedient unto death"
- [[love]] — The motive that transforms obedience from necessity into freedom; "Love is subject and obedient" (042-061)
- [[pride]] — The chief obstacle to obedience, manifested in stubborn attachment to one's own opinion
- [[grace]] — The divine gift that obedience safeguards; withdrawal from obedience is withdrawal from grace itself (050-003)

## Practical Application

Obedience in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is not a theoretical ideal but a daily practice. It begins in the small renunciations — yielding one's opinion in conversation, accepting an inconvenient command without murmuring, foregoing one's own preference for the sake of another. Thomas à Kempis counsels the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "bow thyself humbly under the hands of all men" and to fulfill with good will whatever is required, "whether thy superior, thy inferior, or thy equal" (086-076). The reward of such obedience is twofold: peace in this life — "though thou run hither and thither, thou wilt not find peace, save in humble subjection" (009-008) — and glory in the next, where "humble subjection shall be crowned gloriously" (086-075).
