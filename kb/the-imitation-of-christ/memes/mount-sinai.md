# Mount Sinai

Mount Sinai (also called Horeb) is the mountain in the Sinai Peninsula where God descended in fire, smoke, and earthquake to deliver the Ten Commandments to Moses and establish the Mosaic Covenant with Israel (Exodus 19–20). It is the supreme place of Old Testament revelation — the meeting point between the holy God and His chosen people through the mediation of Moses.

## Biblical Foundation

The Sinai narrative unfolds in Exodus 19–34. When Israel arrived at Sinai after the [[exodus]] from Egypt, "there were thunders and lightnings, and a thick cloud upon the mount, and the voice of the trumpet exceeding loud; so that all the people that were in the camp trembled" (Exodus 19:16). Moses alone ascended into the divine presence, fasting forty days and forty nights, and received the tablets of the Law written by the finger of God (Exodus 24:12–18). The mountain was so holy that boundaries were set around it — whoever touched it would die (Exodus 19:12). There God established His covenant: "If ye will obey my voice indeed, and keep my covenant, then ye shall be a peculiar treasure unto me above all people" (Exodus 19:5).

### Key Events at This Place

- **The Theophany** — God descended upon the mountain in fire and smoke, and the whole mount "quaked greatly" (Exodus 19:18).
- **The Giving of the Law** — Moses received the Ten Commandments and the detailed statutes of the Mosaic covenant (Exodus 20–23).
- **The Golden Calf and Covenant Renewal** — After Israel's apostasy, Moses interceded and received the Law a second time (Exodus 32–34).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Though Thomas a Kempis does not name Sinai directly, he draws extensively on its central event — the giving of the Law and the people's fear before the divine voice. In Book III, he recalls how "the children of Israel spake in old time to Moses, Speak thou unto us and we will hear, but let not the Lord speak unto us lest we die" (039-005). This is the Sinai moment — the people recoiling from the unmediated presence of God. Thomas uses this to contrast the disciple who does not flee the divine voice but seeks it: "Let not Moses speak to me, nor any prophet, but rather speak Thou, O Lord" (039-009). The external law given at Sinai must become the interior word spoken in the soul.

### From External Law to Interior Delight

At Sinai, the Law was inscribed on tablets of stone. In the Imitation's vision, the disciple moves from the external commandment to a law written on the heart by [[grace]]:

> Open, O Lord, my heart in Thy law, and teach me to walk in the way of Thy commandments. [059-002]

> O my God, that I delight in Thy law after the inward man. [092-023]

The transition from Sinai's thunder to the soul's quiet delight in God's law is the work of the [[holy-spirit|Holy Spirit]], who transforms the commandment from an external burden into an interior sweetness.

### The Law of Love and the Law of Sin

The Imitation recognises the inner conflict that the Law alone cannot resolve — the tension between the law of God and the law of sin that wars within the soul:

> I find then a law, that when I would do good, evil is present with me. For I delight in the law of God after the inward man: but I see another law in my members, warring against the law of my mind. [092-006]

Yet the resolution is not the abolition of the Law but its fulfilment through love:

> …as the law of love commandeth which shineth above all things. [042-055]

The Sinai commandment and the love that fulfils it are not opposed; the disciple who has passed through Sinai learns that [[obedience]] is not servile fear but filial love. The external law given at Sinai, received inwardly through grace, becomes the path of freedom rather than the chain of compulsion.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Christian spiritual tradition, Sinai represents the encounter with the holy God that both terrifies and transforms. The mountain's fire and thunder signify the majesty of the Law and the impossibility of approaching God on merely human terms. For the Kempian disciple, Sinai is not only the place where the Law was given but the place where the soul learns that the Law alone — without the inward grace of Christ — cannot save: "the letter killeth, but the spirit giveth life" (2 Corinthians 3:6). The movement from Sinai to the Upper Room is the movement from outward commandment to inward communion.
