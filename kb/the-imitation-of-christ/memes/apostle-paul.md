# Apostle Paul

The Apostle to the Gentiles, convert on the Damascus Road, author of thirteen New Testament epistles, and the preeminent model of apostolic suffering, zeal, and total surrender to Christ in the Christian tradition — cited by Thomas à Kempis in The Imitation of Christ as the supreme example of one whose life was hidden in God and whose approval came from God alone.

## Identity

Paul of Tarsus, also called Saul, was a Pharisaic Jew and Roman citizen who, after a dramatic conversion on the road to Damascus (Acts 9:1–19), became the most influential missionary of the early Church and the author of the Pauline epistles, which constitute nearly half the New Testament. As the "Apostle to the Gentiles" (Romans 11:13), he carried the Gospel beyond the Jewish world into the Greco-Roman Mediterranean, establishing churches across Asia Minor, Macedonia, and Achaia. His theological vision — centered on justification by faith, union with Christ in His death and resurrection, the life of the Spirit, and the Church as the Body of Christ — has shaped Christian doctrine more than any other single figure save Christ Himself. In the calendar of the Western Church, his feast is celebrated together with St. Peter on June 29, and his martyrdom in Rome under Nero (c. AD 64–68) sealed his witness with blood.

## Biblical Narrative

Paul's life is recounted principally in the Acts of the Apostles (chapters 9, 13–28) and in the autobiographical passages of his own letters. His story divides naturally into three acts: his life as a zealous Pharisee and persecutor of the Church; his conversion and calling on the Damascus Road; and his three missionary journeys, culminating in his arrest in Jerusalem, his imprisonment in Caesarea, his voyage to Rome, and his eventual martyrdom.

### Key Events

- **Conversion on the Damascus Road** (Acts 9:1–19) — Saul, "breathing out threatenings and slaughter against the disciples of the Lord," is struck down by a light from heaven and hears the voice of Jesus: "Saul, Saul, why persecutest thou me?" Blinded and led into Damascus, he receives his sight through Ananias and is baptized, immediately beginning to preach Christ in the synagogues. This event is the archetype of radical conversion and divine election in the Christian tradition (011-033).

- **The Apostolic Commission** (Acts 9:15–16; Galatians 1:15–16) — The Lord declares to Ananias that Paul is "a chosen vessel unto Me, to bear My name before the Gentiles, and kings, and the children of Israel: For I will show him how great things he must suffer for My name's sake." This prophecy of suffering defines Paul's apostolate and is explicitly recalled by Thomas à Kempis, who places the words in the mouth of Christ (037-111).

- **Missionary Journeys and Founding of Churches** (Acts 13–20) — Across three journeys, Paul preaches from Cyprus to Corinth, from Ephesus to Philippi, establishing churches, training leaders, and writing letters that would become Christian Scripture. His labors included "hunger and thirst, in cold and nakedness, in labour and weariness, in watchings and fastings" (cf. 2 Corinthians 11:23–28), an apostolic pattern of endurance that The Imitation of Christ holds up for the disciple (018-009).

- **Imprisonment and Journey to Rome** (Acts 21–28) — Arrested in Jerusalem, Paul testifies before the Sanhedrin, Felix, Festus, and Agrippa, appealing to Caesar. Shipwrecked on Malta, he finally reaches Rome, where he "dwelt two whole years in his own hired house, and received all that came in unto him, preaching the kingdom of God" (Acts 28:30–31). Tradition holds that he was beheaded under Nero.

- **Martyrdom in Rome** (c. AD 64–68) — Though the New Testament does not record his death, early and unanimous tradition affirms that Paul was martyred in Rome during the Neronian persecution. His own words — "I am now ready to be offered, and the time of my departure is at hand. I have fought a good fight, I have finished my course, I have kept the faith" (2 Timothy 4:6–7) — were understood by the Church as his valediction.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Paul is one of the few named persons from the New Testament (apart from Christ, Peter, and the Virgin Mary) whom Thomas à Kempis explicitly invokes in The Imitation of Christ. He is cited in four chapters, across Books I, II, and III, each time as an authoritative exemplar of a distinct spiritual virtue: patience in tribulation, refusal of self-commendation, the necessity of suffering, and the right relationship to human judgment.

### Paul as the Model of Trust in God Amid Tribulation

In Book I, Chapter XIII, Thomas exhorts the disciple not to despair in temptation but to cry fervently to God, "that He will, as St. Paul saith, with the temptation make a way to escape that we may be able to bear it" (013-056). This direct citation of 1 Corinthians 10:13 presents Paul as the voice of confident trust, assuring the tempted soul that God's faithfulness will not fail. The chapter's teaching that "in temptations and troubles a man is proved, what progress he hath made" (see 013-011) is grounded in Paul's own apostolic endurance: the saints "have passed through much tribulation," and Paul's word is invoked as the divine promise that sustains them through it.

### Paul as the Model of Divine Approval

In Book II, Chapter VI — "Of the Joy of a Good Conscience" — Thomas teaches that the only authentic witness is God's. "Not he that commendeth himself is approved, as St. Paul saith, but whom the Lord commendeth" (031-035), quoting 2 Corinthians 10:18. Paul exemplifies the "spiritual man" who walks "inwardly with God, and not to be held by any outer affections" (see (031-035) context). The passage is placed immediately after the teaching that true joy, peace, and glory reside solely in the testimony of a good conscience (031-002 to 031-006), and Paul's apostolic word seals the point: no human witness or self-approval avails before the judgment seat of God.

### Paul as the Model of Necessary Suffering

The most theologically concentrated Pauline reference appears in Book III, Chapter XXXVII — "Of Pure and Entire Resignation of Self" — where Christ Himself speaks: "Though thou shouldest be caught up with Paul unto the third heaven, thou art not on that account secure from suffering evil. I will show him, saith Jesus, what great things he must suffer for My Name's sake" (037-111). Thomas here binds together two Pauline experiences — the ecstatic rapture of 2 Corinthians 12:2–4 and the prophetic commissioning of Acts 9:16 — to teach that no height of spiritual privilege exempts the soul from the cross. The chapter's governing theme of total self-resignation draws its most powerful proof from Paul: even the man who "heard unspeakable words, which it is not lawful for a man to utter" was not spared suffering, but was shown "what great things he must suffer." The conclusion is inexorable: "It remaineth, therefore, to thee to suffer, if thou wilt love Jesus and serve Him continually" (037-111 context).

### Paul as the Model of Christ-Centered Service

In Book III, Chapter XXXVI — "Against Vain Judgments of Men" — Thomas invokes Paul's pastoral strategy and his indifference to human opinion. "Although Paul studied to please all men in the Lord, and to become all things to all men, yet nevertheless with him it was a very small thing that he should be judged by man's judgment" (073-007). The passage cites 1 Corinthians 9:22 and 1 Corinthians 4:3, presenting Paul as the Apostle who labored abundantly "for the building up and the salvation of others" (see (073-007) context) while remaining radically free from the need for human approval. He "committed all to God, who knew all, and by patience and humility defended himself against evil" — a pattern the disciple is called to imitate.

### The Apostolic Pattern

Beyond the explicit Pauline citations, The Imitation of Christ holds up the Apostles collectively as the pattern of Christian discipleship. Book I, Chapter XVIII invokes "how many and grievous tribulations did the Apostles, Martyrs, Confessors, Virgins, endure; and all others who would walk in the footsteps of Christ" (018-009) — presenting the apostolic life not as the exception but as the norm for all who follow Christ. Book IV, Chapter XI gives thanks for "that table of holy doctrine which Thou hast furnished unto us by Thy servants the Prophets and Apostles and other teachers" (107-060), acknowledging the apostolic deposit of teaching as the enduring nourishment of the Church.

## Spiritual Significance

In the devotional tradition represented by The Imitation of Christ, Paul stands as the supreme exemplar of the soul entirely surrendered to God — a man whose conversion was so radical that he could say, "I live; yet not I, but Christ liveth in me" (Galatians 2:20). Four dimensions of his spiritual significance emerge from the Kempian reading.

**First, Paul is the model of conversion and divine election.** His transformation from persecutor to Apostle demonstrates that no soul is beyond the reach of grace and that the most fervent zeal, once redirected toward Christ, becomes the instrument of apostolic fruitfulness. The Imitation's teaching on the fervor of "the beginning of our conversion" (011-033) finds its archetype in Paul, whose conversion was instantaneous, total, and irreversible.

**Second, Paul is the model of suffering accepted as gift.** The Kempian Christ declares, "I will show him what great things he must suffer for My Name's sake" (037-111), and Paul's own litany of sufferings — beatings, shipwrecks, imprisonments, the daily "care of all the churches" — becomes the living demonstration that suffering is not an accident of discipleship but its very substance. As The Imitation teaches, "if thou be a partaker of His sufferings thou shalt be also of His consolation" (037-021), and Paul's life embodies this mystery: the man who suffered most also wrote most radiantly of joy, peace, and the "eternal weight of glory" (2 Corinthians 4:17).

**Third, Paul is the model of divine approval over human judgment.** Thomas à Kempis draws on Paul's insistence from 2 Corinthians that "not he that commendeth himself is approved... but whom the Lord commendeth" (031-035), and on his declaration that human judgment was "a very small thing" (073-007). In an age when honor and reputation were paramount social goods, Paul's radical indifference to the opinion of men — grounded in the knowledge that he was known and approved by God alone — supplies the spiritual logic for the Imitation's sustained polemic against vainglory.

**Fourth, Paul is the model of pastoral love.** "Although Paul studied to please all men in the Lord, and to become all things to all men" (073-007) — this is the apostolic charity that adapts itself to every circumstance in order to win souls, while never compromising the truth. The Kempian disciple is called to the same tension: wholly given to the salvation of others, yet wholly free from the need for their approval.

## Legacy in the Devotional Tradition

Beyond his foundational role in Christian theology, Paul has exercised a profound influence on the devotional and ascetical life of the Church — an influence of which The Imitation of Christ is a particularly concentrated witness. Three streams of this devotional legacy deserve attention.

### The Pauline Pattern of Conversion as Ongoing

In the monastic and devotional tradition, Paul's conversion was not read merely as a past event but as a paradigm of the soul's continual turning toward God. The *Rule of St. Benedict* invokes Paul's question — "Lord, what wilt thou have me to do?" (Acts 9:6) — as the disposition of the monk entering the cloister, and the same Pauline posture of obedient listening permeates the Kempian program of daily self-examination and amendment of life. As The Imitation teaches, the fervor of conversion must be renewed daily: "today one man is converted, and tomorrow we know not what he may be" (011-042). Paul's conversion is not an event to be admired from a distance but a pattern to be entered into — a lifelong dying to self and rising with Christ.

### Paul and the Theology of Spiritual Combat

Paul's language of warfare, athletic contest, and the "armor of God" (Ephesians 6:11-17) gave the devotional tradition its vocabulary for describing the interior struggle against sin and the devil. When The Imitation exhorts the disciple to "resist temptations in the beginning" and to "break the head of the serpent" (013-019), it draws upon the Pauline imagery of the Christian as soldier and athlete — images that would flourish in the spiritual theology of the Counter-Reformation, in the *Spiritual Exercises* of Ignatius of Loyola, and in the Carmelite tradition of John of the Cross. Paul's declaration that "I keep under my body and bring it into subjection" (1 Corinthians 9:27) became the charter text for Christian asceticism, and the Kempian insistence that "the whole life of a Christian ought to be one continual exercise of mortification" (019-019) stands squarely in this Pauline lineage.

### Paul as Pastoral Model for Spiritual Direction

Paul's letters reveal him not only as theologian and missionary but as spiritual father — weeping over his converts, nursing them as a mother, warning them with tears, and laboring "until Christ be formed in you" (Galatians 4:19). This pastoral dimension of Paul's character has made him the patron and model of spiritual directors across the Christian tradition. The author of The Imitation, writing in the context of the *devotio moderna* — a movement deeply concerned with the guidance of souls — draws upon Paul's pastoral example when commending the combination of gentleness and firmness, adaptability and truthfulness, that marks the genuine spiritual guide. "Although Paul studied to please all men in the Lord" (073-007) — this is not the servile pleasing of the flatterer but the pastoral flexibility of one who loves souls enough to meet them where they are.

### Paul in Divine Office and Liturgy

The Church's daily prayer is saturated with Paul. Thirteen of his epistles are read in the cycle of the Divine Office, and his hymns — the kenosis hymn of Philippians 2, the Christ-hymn of Colossians 1, the love-hymn of 1 Corinthians 13 — form the Church's language of praise. On the Feast of the Conversion of St. Paul (January 25) and on the Solemnity of Saints Peter and Paul (June 29), the Church gathers these Pauline themes into liturgical celebration: the radical grace that transforms persecutor into Apostle, the suffering that follows from election, the love that surpasses knowledge. The Christian who prays the Office daily encounters Paul not as a figure from history but as a living voice — an apostolic presence whose words continue to "teach and admonish in all wisdom" (Colossians 3:16).

### Prayer of the Church

*O God, who taught the whole world through the preaching of the blessed Apostle Paul: draw us, we pray, nearer to Thee through the example of him whose conversion we celebrate, and so make us witnesses of Thy truth in the world. Through our Lord Jesus Christ, Thy Son, who liveth and reigneth with Thee in the unity of the Holy Spirit, God, world without end. Amen.*

(Collect for the Feast of the Conversion of St. Paul, Roman Missal)
