# Repentance

The painful but saving labour by which the soul, moved by grace, turns from sin to God, amends its conduct, and prepares itself to meet death without terror — a work that must not be postponed, for "the labour of repentance shall rejoice, and humble [[obedience|subjection]] shall be crowned gloriously" (086-074 to 086-075), in the teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Repentance, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not a single act — a moment of sorrow or a formula of confession — but a comprehensive labour that encompasses the whole reorientation of the soul from sin to God. Thomas à Kempis names it among those things that "shall give great confidence of a happy death": "the painfulness of repentance, readiness to obey, [[self-denial|denial of self]], [[obedience|submission]] to any [[suffering|adversity]] for love of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (023-032 to 023-034). Repentance is placed at the head of this list because it is the gateway: without repentance, there is no readiness to obey, no authentic self-denial, no fruitful [[obedience|submission]] to [[suffering|adversity]]. It is the turning point upon which the whole spiritual life pivots.

The word "painfulness" is deliberate. Repentance hurts. It requires the soul to face what it has been, to own its guilt without evasion, and to undertake the difficult work of amendment. Thomas has no patience with a repentance that costs nothing and changes nothing. True repentance is measured not by the intensity of feeling it produces but by the [[amendment|amendment of life]] it effects.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Repentance appears throughout The Imitation not as an isolated doctrine but as the constant undertone of the spiritual life — the ever-renewed beginning without which all progress is illusory. It is connected intimately with the [[death|meditation on death]], the danger of delay, the labour of amendment, and the eschatological hope that makes the labour worthwhile.

### The Labour of Repentance

The phrase "the labour of repentance" (086-074) captures the Kempian understanding perfectly. Repentance is work — not the work of earning forgiveness, which is impossible, but the work of cooperating with grace in the hard task of change. This labour includes the [[conscience|examination of conscience]], the acknowledgment of specific [[sin|fault]]s, the effort to root out habitual [[sin|sins]], the practice of the contrary virtues, and the [[perseverance|patient endurance]] of the humiliations that accompany the recognition of one's own weakness.

Thomas connects this labour to obedience and self-denial: "the painfulness of repentance, readiness to obey, [[self-denial|denial of self]], [[obedience|submission]] to any [[suffering|adversity]] for love of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (023-031 to 023-033). The sequence is logical. Repentance begins with the recognition of sin. It proceeds to obedience — the positive embrace of God's will in place of the self-will that was sin's root. It deepens into self-denial — the ongoing refusal of the impulses that once led to sin. And it culminates in [[obedience|submission]] to [[suffering|adversity]] — the acceptance of suffering as a participation in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s cross and a medicine for the soul. Repentance is thus not merely a turning from but a turning to: from sin to obedience, from self to God, from rebellion to [[obedience|submission]].

### The Danger of Delay

One of Thomas's most urgent warnings concerns the postponement of repentance. "Alas, some there are, lukewarm and undisciplined, who willingly find excuses for delaying repentance, and desire to defer [[communion|Holy Communion]], lest they should be bound to keep stricter watch over themselves" (106-042 to 106-043). The psychology is acute: the soul that postpones repentance is not merely procrastinating but actively rationalising its own lukewarmness. The excuse — "I am not yet ready," "I will repent when I am older," "after this one thing is settled" — disguises the real motive: the desire not to give up sin. Delay is not neutrality; it is resistance dressed in the language of prudence.

This warning is reinforced by the [[death|meditation on death]]. "What doth it profit to live long, when we amend so little? Long life doth not always amend, but often the more increaseth guilt" (023-013 to 023-014). Time does not automatically produce repentance; it often hardens the soul in its habits. The assumption that one will repent later — that there will be time, that the desire will come, that circumstances will be more favourable — is a wager with one's own soul, and Thomas warns that the stakes are eternal.

### Amendment of Life

The proof of repentance is amendment. Thomas repeatedly returns to this theme: the years since conversion are meaningless if the life has not changed. "Many there are who reckon the years since they were converted, and yet how little fruit of amendment is there in them" (023-016). Counting years is a substitute for counting virtues; it is easier to remember when one began than to examine whether one has advanced. True repentance is not punctiliar — an event in the past — but progressive: a continual turning, a daily amendment, an ever-deepening conversion.

"Happy and wise is he who now striveth to be such in life as he would fain be found in death" (023-029 to 023-030). This is the criterion: live now as you wish to be found at the end. The soul that lives in continual repentance — turning from sin moment by moment, amending what was amiss, beginning again after every fall — is the soul that need not fear death, for death will find it already facing in the right direction.

### The Joy of Repentance

For all its painfulness, repentance tends toward joy. Thomas makes the eschatological promise that "the labour of repentance shall rejoice, and humble [[obedience|subjection]] shall be crowned gloriously" (086-074 to 086-075). The labour is real, but it is not permanent. What begins in sorrow ends in rejoicing; what begins in painful self-knowledge ends in glory. This is the gospel logic that undergirds the whole Kempian teaching: repentance is not an end in itself but a means to joy, and the sorrow it involves is "the sorrow that is according to God" which "worketh repentance to salvation not to be repented of" (2 Corinthians 7:10).

The daily practice of repentance is thus sustained not by grim determination but by hope. The soul that labours at amendment does so in the confidence that the labour is not wasted — that every renunciation of sin, every difficult confession, every act of self-denial, is seed sown for an eternal harvest. "The labour of repentance shall rejoice" is not a wish but a promise.

## Key Passages

> "The desire to excel in virtue, the love of discipline, the painfulness of repentance, readiness to obey, denial of self, submission to any adversity for love of Christ; these are the things which shall give great confidence of a happy death." (023-031 to 023-034)

> "What doth it profit to live long, when we amend so little? Long life doth not always amend, but often the more increaseth guilt. Oh that we might spend a single day in this world as it ought to be spent!" (023-013 to 023-015)

> "Happy and wise is he who now striveth to be such in life as he would fain be found in death!" (023-029 to 023-030)

> "Alas, some there are, lukewarm and undisciplined, who willingly find excuses for delaying repentance, and desire to defer Holy Communion, lest they should be bound to keep stricter watch over themselves." (106-042 to 106-043)

> "The fruit of obedience, the labour of repentance shall rejoice, and humble subjection shall be crowned gloriously." (086-074 to 086-075)

> "Always be thou prepared, and so live that death may never find thee unprepared." (023-024)

## Biblical Foundation

The call to repentance is the first word of the gospel: "Repent ye, and believe the gospel" (Mark 1:15). Thomas's entire treatment of repentance is an extended meditation on this dominical command. The urgency of amendment — the danger of delay — echoes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own warning: "Except ye repent, ye shall all likewise perish" (Luke 13:3). The promise that the labour of repentance shall rejoice reflects Acts 3:19: "Repent ye therefore, and be converted, that your [[sin|sins]] may be blotted out, when the times of refreshing shall come from the presence of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]."

The call to continual amendment finds its biblical warrant in 2 Peter 3:9: "The [[jesus-christ|Lord]] is not slack concerning His promise... but is longsuffering to us-ward, not willing that any should perish, but that all should come to repentance." The divine patience is not an excuse for delay but a gift of opportunity — time given for the very amendment that the procrastinator squanders. Thomas's teaching on the painfulness of repentance and its movement toward joy is a direct echo of 2 Corinthians 7:10: "Godly sorrow worketh repentance to salvation not to be repented of." The sorrow is real, but it is a sorrow that carries within itself the seed of joy.
