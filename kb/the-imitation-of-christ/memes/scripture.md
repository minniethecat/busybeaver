# Scripture

The inspired written Word of God — in The Imitation of Christ, Scripture is the primary means by which God speaks to the soul, demanding a posture of humility and a single-minded desire for spiritual profit rather than intellectual curiosity.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, Scripture is not one pious practice among many but the indispensable foundation of the spiritual life. Thomas à Kempis devotes an entire chapter — Book I, Chapter V — to "the reading of Holy Scriptures," setting forth principles that have guided Christian lectio divina for six centuries. Scripture is presented not as a text to be mastered but as a living voice to be heard; not as a subject for academic disputation but as the "pleasant pastures" (088-018) in which the soul finds rest and nourishment.

The Imitation insists that "all Scripture ought to be read in the spirit in which it was written" (005-003) — a principle that links the reader's interior disposition to the inspired character of the text. One cannot approach Scripture in a merely human spirit and expect to receive its divine fruit.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Book I, Chapter V provides the most concentrated treatment, but references to Scripture and its right use appear throughout the four books.

### The Right Spirit for Reading

The Imitation's foundational principle is that "all Scripture ought to be read in the spirit in which it was written" (005-003). This brief sentence contains an entire hermeneutic: the Spirit who inspired the sacred authors must also illuminate the reader. Without this illumination, the letter remains dead.

The text sharpens this further: "We must rather seek for what is profitable in Scripture, than for what is subtle" (005-004). The purpose of reading is not intellectual satisfaction but moral and spiritual transformation. The soul that reads to become learned rather than to become holy has missed the point.

### Scripture and the Interior Life

Book III deepens the teaching by contrasting external instruction with interior illumination: "They give us scriptures, but Thou makest known the sense" (039-015). Human teachers can deliver the words, but only God can open the understanding. This is the difference between information and formation, between knowing about God and knowing God.

The Imitation also warns against pride in scriptural knowledge: "visions or consolations, or that he is skilled in the Scriptures" (044-057) — these things are no proof of holiness. The demons know Scripture better than any human being, and it profits them nothing.

### Scripture and the Eucharist

Book IV places Scripture alongside the Eucharist as the two great means by which Christ comes to the faithful: "the Body and Blood of Christ and the Holy Scriptures are most necessary" (107-001). Together, Word and Sacrament sustain the soul on its pilgrimage.

### Key Passages

> "All Scripture ought to be read in the spirit in which it was written." (005-003)

> "We must rather seek for what is profitable in Scripture, than for what is subtle." (005-004)

> "They give us scriptures, but Thou makest known the sense." (039-015)

> "Before thee the pleasant pastures of the Scriptures, that with enlarged heart thou mayest begin to run the way of My commandments." (088-018)

## Biblical Foundation

The scriptural foundation is extensive. Paul declares that "all Scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness" (2 Timothy 3:16). The author of Hebrews testifies that "the word of God is living and powerful, and sharper than any two-edged sword" (Hebrews 4:12). The Psalmist confesses: "Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path" (Psalm 119:105). And Christ Himself exhorted the religious leaders: "You search the Scriptures, for in them you think you have eternal life; and these are they which testify of Me" (John 5:39).

## Practical Application

The teaching of The Imitation on Scripture calls the [[disciple]] to a renewed practice of sacred reading: not to cover ground but to be covered by it; not to accumulate information but to be formed by the Word. The practical test is whether reading Scripture leaves the soul more humble or more proud — for as The Imitation reminds us, the devil knows Scripture better than any of us, and it profits him nothing because he lacks [[humility]] and [[charity]].
