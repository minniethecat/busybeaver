# Consolation

The inward gift of [[comfort|spiritual comfort]], peace, and sweetness bestowed by God upon the soul — given not as a reward for merit but as a divine kindness to strengthen the faithful for further [[temptation|trial]]s, and ordinarily followed by temptation.

## Definition

Consolation, in the vocabulary of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], refers to the interior experience of [[comfort|divine comfort]] — a supernatural sweetness, peace, and sense of God's presence that lifts the soul above its natural condition. It is sharply distinguished from "human comforts," which Thomas à Kempis regards as fleeting, deceptive, and even dangerous to spiritual progress. [[heaven|Heaven]]ly [[comfort|consolation]] is a sovereign gift: "No man is worthy of heavenly consolation but he who hath diligently exercised himself in [[compunction|holy compunction]]" (020-036). The [[discipleship|disciple]] must receive it with [[gratitude|thanksgiving]] yet without [[pride|presumption]], for it belongs entirely to God's free [[forgiveness|mercy]], not to human desert.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats [[comfort|consolation]] as a central element of the spiritual life, but always with a characteristic sobriety. Consolation is real, good, and divinely given — yet never to be sought for its own sake, never to be presumed upon, and never to be mistaken for the goal of the spiritual life. The entire work is marked by a rhythm: [[comfort|consolation]] comes, [[comfort|consolation]] is withdrawn, and the faithful soul learns to [[trust-in-god|trust]] God equally in both.

### The Gift and Its Giver

Consolation is received with gratitude but held lightly. "When, therefore, [[comfort|spiritual comfort]] is given by God, receive it with giving of thanks, and know that it is the gift of God, not thy desert" (034-029). The [[discipleship|disciple]] is warned against the spiritual pride that can follow [[comfort|consolation]]: "Be not lifted up, rejoice not overmuch nor foolishly presume, but rather be more humble for the gift" (034-031). The purpose of [[comfort|consolation]] is practical — it strengthens the soul for what lies ahead: "Divine comfort is given that a man may be stronger to bear adversities" (034-071).

### The Withdrawal and Its Purpose

The withdrawal of [[comfort|consolation]] is not a sign of God's [[desolation|abandonment]] but a normal and even necessary phase of the spiritual life. "When comfort is taken from thee, do not straightway despair, but wait for the heavenly visitation with humility and patience, for God is able to give thee back greater favour and [[comfort|consolation]]" (034-036). This pattern — [[comfort|consolation]], withdrawal, faithful waiting, and return — is "not new nor strange to those who have made [[temptation|trial]] of the way of God, for with the great saints and the ancient prophets there was often this manner of change" (034-038).

### Consolation Through the Cross

Book II, Chapter XII presents one of the work's most paradoxical teachings: [[suffering|tribulation]] itself, when borne for [[jesus-christ|Christ]], becomes the truest [[comfort|consolation]]. "God will have thee learn to suffer [[suffering|tribulation]] without [[comfort|consolation]], and to submit thyself fully to it, and by [[suffering|tribulation]] be made more humble" (037-035). Yet the one who bears the cross willingly "is not without refreshment of [[comfort|consolation]], because he feeleth abundant fruit to be growing within him out of the bearing of his cross" (037-067). Thomas reaches the staggering conclusion that the [[discipleship|disciple]] should "leave [[comfort|consolation]]s to God, let Him do as seemeth best to Him concerning them... reckon them [[[suffering|tribulation]]s] the best [[comfort|consolation]]s" (037-100).

### Unworthiness and Pure Mercy

Book III, Chapter LII deepens the theology of [[comfort|consolation]] by placing it entirely within the framework of [[mercy|divine mercy]]. The [[discipleship|disciple]] confesses: "O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], I am not worthy of Thy [[comfort|consolation]], nor of any spiritual visitation" (089-003). Even if he could "pour forth tears like the sea," he would still not deserve [[comfort|consolation]] (089-006). And yet God "vouchsafest even beyond all his own deserving, to comfort Thy servant above the measure of mankind" (089-013). This [[comfort|consolation]] is unlike any merely human comfort: "Thy [[comfort|consolation]]s are not like unto the discoursings of men" (089-014).

### The Rhythm of Temptation and Comfort

Thomas à Kempis observes a recurring pattern in the spiritual life: temptation precedes [[comfort|consolation]], and [[comfort|consolation]] strengthens for further temptation. "[[temptation|Temptation]] is wont to go before as a sign of the comfort which shall follow, and heavenly comfort is promised to those who are proved by temptation" (034-067). This is not an accidental feature of the spiritual life but part of God's pedagogy: the soul is exercised by temptation so that it may receive [[comfort|consolation]] with greater humility and gratitude.

## Biblical Foundation

The concept of divine [[comfort|consolation]] has deep scriptural roots. The [[apostle-paul|Apostle Paul]] blesses God as "the Father of mercies and the God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our [[suffering|affliction]]" (2 Corinthians 1:3–4). The Psalmist confesses, "When the cares of my heart are many, your [[comfort|consolation]]s cheer my soul" (Psalm 94:19). [[isaiah|Isaiah]] promises that God will comfort His people "as one whom his mother comforts" ([[isaiah|Isaiah]] 66:13). Thomas à Kempis draws on Psalm 30 to illustrate the rhythm of [[comfort|consolation]] and withdrawal: "I said in my prosperity I shall never be moved... Thou didst turn Thy face from me, and I was troubled" (034-041–043). The Third Book of the Imitation is titled *On Inward Consolation*, indicating that the entire dialogue between [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and the [[discipleship|disciple]] is understood as a work of [[comfort|divine comfort]].
