# Miracle

Miracle — the supernatural work of divine power — appears in The Imitation of Christ not as an object of devotion in itself but as a point of contrast: the extraordinary and the spectacular are consistently subordinated to the ordinary and the interior. Thomas à Kempis twice invokes miracles, both times to relativize their importance against the virtues of the interior life.

## Biblical Narrative

Miracles pervade the biblical narrative: the plagues of Egypt, the parting of the Red Sea, the manna in the wilderness, the stopping of the sun at Joshua's command, the healing of Naaman, the deliverance of Daniel. In the New Testament, the miracles of Christ — the changing of water to wine at Cana (John 2:1–11), the feeding of the five thousand, the healing of the sick, the raising of Lazarus, and supremely the Resurrection — are signs that manifest His divine identity: "Believe me that I am in the Father, and the Father in me: or else believe me for the very works' sake" (John 14:11). The apostles continued this ministry of signs, and Paul acknowledged the gifts of miracles in the Church (1 Corinthians 12:10).

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation mentions miracles only twice, and both instances share a common rhetorical strategy: the miracle is invoked to be surpassed. In Book III, Chapter 55 ("Of the Corruption of Nature and the Efficacy of Divine Grace"), Christ speaks of His own suffering: "I endured... blasphemies for My miracles" (055-010). The miracles that should have evoked faith instead provoked blasphemy — a sobering reminder that the spectacular does not compel belief. In Book III, Chapter 58 ("Of Deeper Matters and God's Hidden Judgments"), the disciple is warned not to seek after extraordinary spiritual experiences: "neither the grace of prophecy nor the working of miracles, nor any speculation" (092-044) should be the object of the soul's desire, for all these may exist without charity, and without charity they profit nothing (cf. 1 Corinthians 13:2).

> "neither the grace of prophecy nor the working of miracles, nor any speculation" (092-044)

The pattern is consistent with The Imitation's thoroughgoing emphasis on the interior life. Miracles are real, they are gifts of God, and Christ Himself worked them — but they are not the measure of holiness, nor are they to be sought. The soul that hungers for miracles has not yet understood that the greatest miracle is the transformation of the will through grace.

## Spiritual Significance

The Imitation's treatment of miracles is an application of the principle articulated in the final beatitude: "Blessed are they that have not seen, and yet have believed" (John 20:29). Miracles are for those who need signs; faith is for those who trust the word of God without them. The devout soul is called not to work miracles but to practice humility — a far harder and far more meritorious task. In this, Thomas à Kempis echoes the consistent teaching of the spiritual masters: extraordinary phenomena are often a distraction from the one thing necessary.
