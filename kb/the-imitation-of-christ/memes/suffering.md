# Suffering

The experience of tribulation, adversity, and pain that in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian spirituality — and especially in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — is not a sign of divine [[desolation|abandonment]] but the very means by which the soul is purified and conformed to the pattern of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own Passion.

## Definition

Suffering, understood in the Kempian framework, is the universal human experience of tribulation — whether physical pain, [[desolation|spiritual desolation]], persecution by others, or inward [[temptation|trial]]s — that no mortal can escape (037-053). Rather than being a meaningless evil, suffering is presented as the "royal way" appointed by God for the soul's purification and growth in virtue. Thomas a Kempis teaches that suffering willingly embraced for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s sake is "nothing more acceptable to God, nothing more healthful for thyself in this world" (037-127).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Suffering pervades all four books of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], but it receives its most concentrated and systematic treatment in Book II, Chapter XII — "Of the Royal Way of the [[cross|Holy Cross]]" — which the text presents as the "conclusion of the whole matter" (037-140). Throughout the work, Thomas a Kempis develops suffering not as a marginal topic but as the central axis of the spiritual life: the means by which the [[discipleship|disciple]] is humbled, proved, and made like [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

### The Universality of Suffering

The text insists that suffering is inescapable in this life. "Thou canst not flee from it whithersoever thou hurriest, for whithersoever thou comest, thou bearest thyself with thee" (037-041). This theme echoes throughout the work: "always we shall have somewhat to suffer, for we have fallen from perfect [[blessedness|happiness]]" (013-019). No state of life, no degree of holiness, no place of retreat can exempt a person from tribulation — not even the [[apostle-paul|Apostle Paul]], caught up to the third heaven, was "on that account secure from suffering evil" (037-112).

### Suffering as Participation in Christ's Passion

The central theological move of the text is to reframe suffering as participation in the [[passion-of-christ|Passion of Christ]]. "No man understandeth the [[passion-of-christ|Passion of Christ]] in his heart so well as he who hath had somewhat of the like suffering himself" (037-039). The [[discipleship|disciple]] who suffers is not merely enduring hardship — he is being conformed to the Crucified, and by this conformity he is promised glory: "if thou be a partaker of His sufferings thou shalt be also of His glory" (037-021). [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself exhorts the [[discipleship|disciple]]: "Cease to complain; consider My suffering and that of My saints" (056-002), reminding the sufferer that his [[temptation|trial]]s are small compared to what [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and the saints endured.

### The Fruit of Suffering

Paradoxically, the text presents suffering as the source of spiritual fruit. "Whilst he willingly submitteth himself to it, every burden of tribulation is turned into an assurance of [[comfort|divine comfort]], and the more the flesh is wasted by affliction, the more is the spirit strengthened mightily by inward grace" (037-069). The purpose of suffering is not punishment but purification: "through them we are humbled, purified, instructed" (013-011). When suffering is willingly accepted, it becomes sweet: "When thou hast come to this, that tribulation is sweet and pleasant to thee for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s sake, then reckon that it is well with thee, because thou hast found paradise on earth" (037-104).

### Suffering Without Consolation

A distinctive emphasis of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is that the highest form of suffering is that which is borne without any felt [[comfort|consolation]]. "God will have thee learn to suffer tribulation without [[comfort|consolation]], and to submit thyself fully to it, and by tribulation be made more humble" (037-036). The true value of suffering lies not in how it feels but in the disposition with which it is borne: "So far as thou settest thyself to bear patiently, so far thou dost wisely and art deserving of the more merit" (056-012). This teaching steers a middle path between Stoic indifference and the expectation that suffering will always be relieved by [[comfort|divine comfort]].

## Key Passages

> "In the Cross is health, in the Cross is life, in the Cross is protection from enemies, in the Cross is heavenly sweetness, in the Cross strength of mind, in the Cross joy of the spirit, in the Cross the height of virtue, in the Cross perfection of holiness. There is no health of the soul, no hope of eternal life, save in the Cross." (037-013)

> "If thou willingly bear the Cross, it will bear thee, and will bring thee to the end which thou seekest, even where there shall be the end of suffering; though it shall not be here." (037-047)

> "Nothing more acceptable to God, nothing more healthful for thyself in this world, than to suffer willingly for Christ. And if it were thine to choose, thou oughtest rather to wish to suffer adversities for Christ, than to be refreshed with manifold consolations, for thou wouldest be more like Christ and more conformed to all saints." (037-127)

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] anchors its theology of suffering in the New Testament. Romans 8:18 — "the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed in us" — is directly quoted within the key chapter (037-101), providing the eschatological horizon that makes present suffering bearable. Acts 14:22 — "we must through much tribulation enter into the kingdom of God" — supplies the concluding verse of the entire treatment (037-141). The author also draws on 2 Corinthians 12:2 regarding Paul's rapture to the third heaven (037-111) and Acts 9:16 — "I will show him what great things he must suffer for My Name's sake" (037-113). Hebrews 12:4 — "Ye have not yet resisted unto blood" — is quoted to remind the [[discipleship|disciple]] that his suffering is light compared to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s (056-003). The call to take up one's cross, drawn from Matthew 16:24 and Luke 9:23, frames the entire discussion (037-002, 037-139).
