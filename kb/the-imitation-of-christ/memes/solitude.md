# Solitude

The sacred withdrawal from the noise of human company and worldly business in order to be alone with God. Solitude is not the rejection of charity toward neighbor but the school in which charity is purified — a necessary rhythm of the spiritual life by which the soul regains its center and learns to hear the voice that the crowd drowns out.

## Definition

Solitude (from Latin *solitudo*, "being alone") is the deliberate practice of withdrawing from external distractions to attend to God and to the state of one's own soul. It is not mere isolation — which can be self-indulgent or pathological — but a positive discipline ordered toward communion. Christ Himself modeled the rhythm: after teaching the multitudes and healing the sick, He "departed into a solitary place, and there prayed" (Mark 1:35). Solitude is the necessary complement to community: the soul cannot live wholly in the crowd and remain healthy any more than it can live wholly in isolation.

The practice takes different forms according to one's state in life: the religious enters a formal solitude of enclosure; the layperson practices interior solitude in the midst of daily duties, preserving a "cell of the heart" into which to retreat. In every form, solitude is the condition for the three foundational acts of the spiritual life: self-knowledge, prayer, and the hearing of God's word.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis, writing as a canon regular in a semi-monastic community, treats solitude and silence as indispensable conditions of spiritual progress. The opening chapter of Book One already counsels the reader concerning "the love of solitude and silence" as the beginning of the imitation of Christ.

### Solitude and Spiritual Progress

"In silence and quiet the devout soul goeth forward and learneth the hidden things of the Scriptures" (020-002). Solitude is the classroom of the interior life: what is learned in private prayer and silent reading could never be learned from human teachers alone. The soul that never withdraws from conversation will remain superficial, nourished only by the opinions of others rather than by the word of God.

### The Danger of Too Much Company

*The Imitation* is famously severe on the dangers of excessive socializing: "As often as I have been amongst men, I have returned home less a man," Kempis writes, paraphrasing a saying attributed to Seneca. The disciple who is "so unguarded in thine outward senses, so often entangled in many vanities" (103-002) needs the discipline of withdrawal to recover spiritual sobriety. "He who diligently attendeth to himself easily keepeth silence concerning others" (030-001).

### Solitude Before God

The highest purpose of solitude is not self-analysis but encounter with God. The disciple who sits alone with the Lord discovers that "all creation keep silence before Thee: speak Thou alone to me" (003-007). In the silence of solitude, the noise of created things falls away and the soul hears only the Creator — a foretaste of the eternal sabbath in which the soul rests in God alone.

### Not an Escape but a Discipline

Kempis is realistic that solitude is hard: the solitary soon discovers how disordered his interior life truly is. The thoughts that were drowned out by company become deafening in silence. But this very discovery is the beginning of healing — the self-knowledge without which there can be no conversion. Solitude is not an escape from the battle but its intensification, and precisely therefore its resolution.

## Biblical Foundation

The Old Testament prophets knew solitude as the condition of revelation: Elijah heard the still small voice not in the wind or earthquake but in the silence of Horeb (1 Kings 19:12). Jeremiah counsels, "He sitteth alone and keepeth silence, because he hath borne it upon him" (Lamentations 3:28). The Psalmist commands, "Be still, and know that I am God" (Psalm 46:10). In the New Testament, Christ repeatedly withdraws to pray alone — before choosing the Twelve (Luke 6:12), after feeding the five thousand (Matthew 14:23), and on the night of His passion in Gethsemane (Matthew 26:36). He instructs His disciples: "When thou prayest, enter into thy closet, and when thou hast shut thy door, pray to thy Father which is in secret" (Matthew 6:6).
