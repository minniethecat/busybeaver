# Holy Spirit

The **Holy Spirit** (*Spiritus Sanctus*, also rendered "Holy Ghost" in traditional English) is the third Person of the Blessed Trinity in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]*. Thomas à Kempis invokes the Spirit as the Paraclete — the Comforter, Advocate, and Teacher — whose indwelling presence is the animating principle of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian's interior life. The text's treatment is consistently doxological and practical: the Spirit is adored as God, and His grace is sought as the soul's indispensable aid.

## The Spirit as Comforter and Advocate

Kempis identifies the Holy Spirit by the Johannine title "Paraclete" — the one called alongside to help, comfort, and defend. A solemn doxology in Book III confesses:

> The only-begotten Son and the Holy Ghost, the Paraclete, for ever and ever [042-007]

This Trinitarian formula places the Spirit in the full equality of [[glory|divine glory]] with the Father and the Son. The Spirit is not a subordinate power but God Himself, worthy of eternal praise alongside the Father and the Word.

The text describes the blessings of the Spirit-filled life: "Inspired with the grace of the Holy Ghost," the soul is lifted above its natural capacities and made capable of what flesh and blood could never accomplish (097-068). The Spirit's inspiration is the source of every holy impulse, every good resolution, every act of genuine virtue.

## The Grace of the Holy Spirit

The practical theology of *The Imitation* centers on the necessity of grace — and grace is consistently associated with the person and work of the Holy Spirit. The text prays:

> Give me, O God, by the grace of Thy Holy Spirit, the help of Thy presence [064-029]

This prayer reveals the double movement of the Spirit's work: He brings grace (the divine assistance) and He brings presence (the intimacy of God with the soul). [[grace|Grace]] is not an impersonal force but the personal gift of the indwelling Spirit.

Kempis is realistic about the human condition without grace: "Because the grace of the Holy Ghost does not fill him, therefore he [[trust-in-god|trust]]s in himself" (079-017). Self-reliance is the symptom of spiritual [[vanity|emptiness]]; the remedy is the Spirit's inward strengthening. The soul that is "filled" with the Spirit no longer needs to grasp at created supports, for it possesses the Creator within.

## The Narrow Way and the Spirit

A striking passage links the Spirit to the ascetical path: "They who enter the narrow way... these are they who are led by the Holy Ghost" (047-043). The "narrow way" of self-denial, humility, and the cross is not undertaken by human fortitude alone — it requires the Spirit's guidance and empowerment. Those who attempt the narrow way in their own strength will fail; those who are "led by the Spirit" (Romans 8:14) will find strength they did not know they possessed.

The Spirit's leadership is interior and gentle: "although it appear to a man" in one way, the Spirit may lead in another (052-008). [[discernment|Discernment]] of the Spirit's movement requires humility and attentiveness — the Spirit does not shout over the noise of self-will.

## The Spirit's Joy

Kempis associates the Holy Spirit with joy — not worldly pleasure but the deep [[comfort|consolation]] of the soul that possesses God. The text recalls that the Virgin Mary herself rejoiced "for joy in the Holy Ghost; and afterwards discerning" the Spirit's work (113-028). Joy in the Spirit is compatible with outward suffering — it is the joy of union with God, not the absence of [[suffering|affliction]].

The Magnificat's "my spirit hath rejoiced in God my [[jesus-christ|Saviour]]" (Luke 1:47) echoes behind this treatment: the Spirit is the source of the soul's exultation, a joy that transcends circumstances because it rests in God Himself.

## Biblical Foundation

The Holy Spirit's role as Paraclete is drawn from [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s farewell discourse in John's Gospel (John 14:16–17, 26; 15:26; 16:7–15). The Spirit as the one who "teaches all things" and "brings to remembrance" anchors Kempis's confidence that the Spirit illumines the soul's understanding of divine truth. The Pauline teaching that "the Spirit also helpeth our infirmities" and "maketh intercession for us" (Romans 8:26) undergirds the emphasis on the Spirit as the soul's indispensable aid in prayer and the spiritual life.
