# Israel

Israel appears in The Imitation of Christ not as a geographical location to be described but as the theatre of sacred history — the people to whom God spoke through Moses, the nation whose kings built the temple, the community whose worship and sin alike are held up as instruction for the Christian soul. Thomas à Kempis does not linger over topography; his interest in Israel is entirely typological and moral. The history of Israel is the history of every soul: called, delivered, tested, and — when faithful — brought to the promised rest.

## Geography and Identity

Israel is at once a land, a people, and a theological category. As a land, it is the territory promised to Abraham and his descendants, the place where the temple stood and where the prophets spoke. As a people, Israel is "the children of Israel" (039-005) — the twelve tribes descended from Jacob, the nation whom God delivered from Egypt and to whom He gave the law. As a theological category, Israel represents the community of the covenant, the people set apart for God, whose entire history is a pedagogy preparing the world for Christ. The Imitation uses "Israel" exclusively in this third, typological sense: Israel is the mirror in which the Christian sees both the pattern of God's faithfulness and the pattern of human frailty.

## Biblical Narrative

The biblical account of Israel spans from the patriarchal call of Abraham (Genesis 12) through the exodus from Egypt, the giving of the law at Sinai, the conquest of Canaan under Joshua, the establishment of the monarchy, the building of Solomon's temple (1 Kings 8), the division of the kingdom, the exile, and the return — a vast narrative arc of covenant, disobedience, judgment, and restoration. For Thomas, the most significant moments are those in which Israel's worship or unfaithfulness directly instructs the Christian: Israel's plea to Moses — "Speak thou unto us and we will hear, but let not the Lord speak unto us lest we die" (cf. Exodus 20:19) — becomes a type of the soul's reluctance to hear God directly; Solomon's building of the temple becomes a figure of the interior temple the soul must prepare for God; and Israel's praise with "the whole heart" becomes the model of Christian devotion.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas's references to Israel fall into three categories: the giving of the law, the example of worship, and the warning of judgment.

### Israel and the Giving of the Law

The most extended reference to Israel appears in Book III, Chapter 39, where Thomas recalls how "the children of Israel spake in old time to Moses, Speak thou unto us and we will hear thee: but let not the Lord speak unto us, lest we die" (039-005, echoing Exodus 20:19). Thomas draws a sharp contrast: Israel feared the direct voice of God and asked for a mediator, but the Christian is called to desire that very immediacy — to hear God speak in the depths of the soul without intermediary. Israel's fear becomes a cautionary example: do not prefer human mediation to divine intimacy.

### Israel and the Example of Worship

Thomas invokes Israel's worship as a pattern for Christian devotion. Solomon, the greatest of Israel's kings, "was seven years building his magnificent temple to the praise of Thy name" (097-039), and "taught the people of Israel to praise God with the whole heart" (097-069). The temple and its worship are types of the interior temple that every Christian must build — a soul consecrated to the praise of God. The external splendour of Solomon's temple points toward the inward beauty of the sanctified soul.

### Israel as Warning

The third category is admonitory. Thomas notes that "Joshua and the children of Israel were deceived by the Gibeonites because they asked not counsel at the mouth of the Lord" (075-024, referencing Joshua 9). The lesson is drawn not from theological speculation but from the narrative of Israel's failure: even the chosen people, when they acted without consulting God, fell into error. The same can happen to any soul. Israel's history is thus a permanent warning against spiritual presumption.

> "The children of Israel spake in old time to Moses, Speak thou unto us and we will hear thee: but let not the Lord speak unto us, lest we die." (039-005)

> "Solomon, king of Israel, was seven years building his magnificent temple to the praise of Thy name." (097-039)

> "He taught the people of Israel to praise God with the whole heart." (097-069)

## Spiritual Significance

Israel's significance in The Imitation is fundamentally typological: the history of Israel is the history of the soul written in large letters. The exodus from Egypt prefigures the soul's deliverance from sin; the giving of the law at Sinai prefigures the moral life; the temple prefigures the interior sanctuary where God is worshipped "in spirit and in truth"; Israel's repeated falls into idolatry warn against the soul's persistent tendency to prefer creatures to the Creator. Thomas treats Israel not as a distant historical curiosity but as the immediate predecessor of the Christian — and the Christian, in turn, as the fulfillment of what Israel prefigured. The "God of Israel" (057-019) is the same God whom the Christian soul addresses in prayer; the covenant made with Abraham is fulfilled in Christ, and the Christian participates in that fulfillment through faith and the sacraments.
