# Moses

The great prophet and lawgiver of the Old Testament — in The Imitation of Christ he appears as the paradigmatic servant of God whose faithful mediation on Sinai, his recourse to the tabernacle for divine counsel, and his reverent construction of the Ark all become object lessons for the interior life of the disciple.

## Identity

Moses is the central human figure of the Pentateuch: prophet, lawgiver, and leader of Israel's exodus from Egypt. Born a Hebrew slave, raised in Pharaoh's court, called by God at the burning bush (Exodus 3:1–15), he led Israel through the Red Sea, received the Law on Mount Sinai (Exodus 20:1–21), and guided the people through forty years in the wilderness to the threshold of the Promised Land. Scripture declares that "there arose not a prophet since in Israel like unto Moses, whom the Lord knew face to face" (Deuteronomy 34:10). In Christian tradition, Moses is revered as the giver of the Law, a type of Christ the true Lawgiver, and among the greatest of the Old Testament saints.

## Biblical Narrative

Moses' life spans the books of Exodus through Deuteronomy. Born under Pharaoh's decree of death for Hebrew male infants, he was providentially saved, adopted by Pharaoh's daughter, and raised in the Egyptian court. After killing an Egyptian taskmaster, he fled to Midian where he encountered God in the burning bush — a theophany that commissioned him to deliver Israel. His confrontations with Pharaoh, the institution of the Passover, and the exodus through the Red Sea form the foundational narrative of Israel's identity as God's redeemed people. At Mount Sinai, Moses received the Ten Commandments and the whole Mosaic Law, establishing the covenant between God and Israel. His final act was to view the Promised Land from Mount Nebo, dying there without entering it.

### Key Events

- **The Burning Bush** — God calls Moses from the midst of a bush that burns without being consumed, revealing the divine name and commissioning him to deliver Israel from Egypt (Exodus 3:1–15). This theophany establishes Moses as the prophet who speaks with God face to face.

- **The Exodus and the Red Sea** — Moses leads Israel out of Egypt through the Passover and the miraculous parting of the Red Sea (Exodus 12–14). This deliverance becomes the Old Testament's defining act of salvation and the type of Christian baptism.

- **The Giving of the Law at Sinai** — Moses ascends Mount Sinai and receives the Ten Commandments and the Mosaic Law amid fire, smoke, and earthquake (Exodus 19–20). The people, terrified by the divine presence, plead with Moses: "Speak thou unto us and we will hear, but let not the Lord speak unto us lest we die" (039-005).

- **The Tabernacle** — Moses constructs the tent of meeting according to the pattern shown on the mountain, and "the Lord spake unto Moses face to face, as a man speaketh unto his friend" (Exodus 33:11). Into this tabernacle Moses entered to seek divine counsel in every perplexity.

- **The Ark of the Covenant** — Moses fashions an ark of acacia wood overlaid with pure gold to house the tablets of the Law, the supreme token of God's covenant presence among His people (Exodus 25:10–22).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas a Kempis invokes Moses in three distinct passages, each treating him not merely as a historical figure but as a model for the interior life. Moses represents the pivot from outward religious mediation to inward divine communion — a theme at the very heart of the Kempian project.

### Moses and the Inward Word (Book III, Chapter 2)

The most theologically significant Mosaic passage in The Imitation occurs in Book III, Chapter 2 ("What the Truth saith inwardly without noise of words"). Thomas recalls how the children of Israel, terrified by the divine presence at Sinai, "spake in old time to Moses, Speak thou unto us and we will hear, but let not the Lord speak unto us lest we die" (039-005). The disciple, however, inverts this petition: "Let not Moses speak to me, nor any prophet, but rather speak Thou, O Lord, who didst inspire and illuminate all the prophets" (039-009). The movement is from outward mediation to inward illumination. Moses and all the prophets "can indeed utter words, but they give not the spirit" (039-013); "they utter commandments, but Thou helpest to the fulfilling of them" (039-017). The disciple does not reject Moses but transcends him, desiring not the law written on stone tablets but the Lawgiver Himself speaking within the soul. The passage climaxes in the threefold renunciation: "Therefore let not Moses speak to me, but Thou, O Lord my God, Eternal Truth; lest I die and bring forth no fruit, being outwardly admonished, but not enkindled within; lest the word heard but not followed, known but not loved, believed but not obeyed, rise up against me in the judgment" (039-022).

> "Therefore let not Moses speak to me, but Thou, O Lord my God, Eternal Truth; lest I die and bring forth no fruit, being outwardly admonished, but not enkindled within" (039-022)

This is not a repudiation of the Old Testament but its interiorization. Moses gave the Law; Christ the Eternal Truth writes it on the heart. The disciple who hears only the outward word of Moses without the inward enkindling of grace is in peril — the word "heard but not followed, known but not loved, believed but not obeyed" (039-025) becomes an accusation rather than a salvation.

### Moses and the Recourse to Prayer (Book III, Chapter 38)

In Book III, Chapter 38 ("Of a good government in external things, and of having recourse to God in dangers"), Thomas presents Moses as the model of seeking divine counsel. Christ instructs the disciple to be "a free and true Hebrew, entering into the lot and the liberty of the children of God" (075-007), and to imitate Moses: "straightway in every cause enterest with Moses into the tabernacle to ask counsel of God; thou shalt hear a divine response and come forth instructed concerning many things that are and shall be" (075-017). The historical practice is then recounted: "For always Moses had recourse to the tabernacle for the solving of all doubts and questionings; and fled to the help of prayer to be delivered from the dangers and evil deeds of men" (075-020). The application is immediate and personal: "Thus also oughtest thou to fly to the secret chamber of thy heart, and earnestly implore the divine succour" (075-022). Moses' outward tabernacle becomes the type of the soul's inward sanctuary, and his recourse to God becomes the pattern for the disciple's life of prayer.

### Moses and Reverent Service (Book IV, Chapter 1)

In the opening chapter of Book IV ("With how great reverence Christ must be received"), Thomas places Moses in a gallery of Old Testament saints — Noah, Moses, Solomon, David — whose reverent devotion shames the disciple's lukewarm preparation for Communion. "Moses, Thy servant, Thy great and especial friend, made an ark of incorruptible wood, which also he covered with purest gold, that he might lay up in it the tables of the law" (097-034), and yet "I, a corruptible creature, shall I dare thus easily to receive Thee, the Maker of the Law and the Giver of life?" (097-037). The contrast is deliberately humiliating: Moses spent forty days on Sinai, built an ark of imperishable materials, covered it in gold — all to house stone tablets — while the disciple "scarce know how to spend half an hour in devotion" (097-044) to receive not the Law but the Lawgiver, not the Ark but the Body of Christ. The passage drives toward the staggering disproportion: "Yet there is a vast difference between the Ark of the Covenant with its relics, and Thy most pure Body with its ineffable virtues" (097-054). Moses' reverent labour becomes both an example to emulate and a measure of how far the disciple falls short.

## Spiritual Significance

In the Christian devotional tradition, Moses exemplifies several interrelated virtues:

**Mediation and Its Surpassing.** Moses is the mediator par excellence of the Old Covenant, the one through whom God gave the Law and spoke to Israel. The Imitation honours this role while insisting on its penultimate character: the soul that rests content with Moses — with outward teaching, law, and commandment — has not yet arrived at the inward communion with Christ the Eternal Truth. Moses points beyond himself.

**Recourse to God.** Moses' habitual turning to the tabernacle for divine counsel (075-020) models the essential spiritual discipline of bringing every perplexity, every danger, and every decision before God in prayer. The secret chamber of the heart is the Christian's tabernacle, and the disciple who imitates Moses in this recourse "shall hear a divine response and come forth instructed" (075-018).

**Reverent Service.** Moses' painstaking construction of the Ark — using incorruptible wood and purest gold (097-035) — embodies the reverent care with which the disciple ought to approach the holy things of God. If Moses laboured so for the vessel that contained the Law, how much more ought the Christian to prepare for the reception of Christ Himself?

**Humility and Obedience.** Though not the explicit theme of the Mosaic passages in The Imitation, Moses' character as Scripture's paradigmatic meek man (Numbers 12:3) and obedient servant undergirds his selection as the type of the faithful soul. He is "Thy servant, Thy great and especial friend" (097-034) — a title earned through a life of costly obedience.
