# Bethlehem

The small Judean town — "little among the thousands of Judah" (Micah 5:2) — chosen by God as the birthplace of Jesus Christ and the ancestral home of King David, whose very name (House of Bread) prefigures Christ as the Bread of Life.

## Location

Bethlehem is situated in the hill country of Judea, approximately 10 kilometers south of Jerusalem on the road to Hebron. Its elevation is about 775 meters above sea level, with terraced hillsides and rocky grazing lands that have supported shepherds from antiquity. The town is known in the Old Testament as Bethlehem-Ephrathah, and in modern geography lies within the West Bank.

## Biblical Significance

Bethlehem holds a unique position in the biblical narrative as the bridge between the Old and New Covenants — the city of David's birth and anointing, and the birthplace of David's greater Son. It embodies the biblical pattern of God choosing what is small and humble in the eyes of the world to accomplish His greatest purposes.

### Key Events at This Place

- **Rachel's Death and Burial** — The matriarch Rachel died near Bethlehem and was buried there (Genesis 35:19), marking it as a place of ancestral memory and sacred sorrow for Israel.
- **The Anointing of David** — The prophet Samuel came to Bethlehem and anointed the youngest son of Jesse, the shepherd boy David, as king over Israel (1 Samuel 16:1-13), establishing Bethlehem as "the City of David." Thomas a Kempis invokes David as the model of devout worship: "The most devout king David danced with all his might before the ark of the Lord" (097-064).
- **The Birth of Christ** — In fulfillment of Micah's prophecy (Micah 5:2), Jesus was born in Bethlehem, laid in a manger because there was no room at the inn (Luke 2:4-7), revealing that the King of Kings enters the world not in a palace but in a stable.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Although Thomas a Kempis does not mention Bethlehem by name, the spiritual significance of Bethlehem — as the place where God chose humility, obscurity, and poverty over worldly greatness — permeates the theology of The Imitation of Christ. The foundational Kempian principle that "he is truly great who deemeth himself small, and counteth all height of honour as nothing" (003-059) finds its supreme enacted example in Bethlehem, where the eternal Word embraced the estate of a helpless infant in a feeding-trough.

The mystery of the Incarnation, which took flesh in Bethlehem, is central to Kempian devotion: the disciple is exhorted to call to mind "the mystery of Christ's incarnation and His Passion" (106-064). Bethlehem's paradox — that the Almighty should be born a child in a stable — is the incarnate form of the Kempian insistence that "poor and lowly of heart abide in the multitude of peace" (006-004). The disciple who would follow Christ must trace His path not only to Calvary but to Bethlehem: "from the hour of My birth, until My death upon the Cross" (055-006).

Bethlehem also prefigures the rejection Christ would endure throughout His earthly life. "When He was in the world, was despised and rejected of men" (026-043) — a rejection that began at the hour of His birth, when there was no room for Him at the inn. The pattern of divine humility that Thomas urges the disciple to imitate is the spiritual life lived in the spirit of Bethlehem: to become small, to embrace obscurity, and to find greatness in lowliness.

The incarnation at Bethlehem is not only a past event but the continuing model of how Christ comes to the soul. Just as He entered the world through the humility of a stable, so He enters the heart through the humility of the disciple who has made room for Him by emptying himself of pride. Thomas's Christ declares: "Blessed is the simplicity which leaveth the difficult ways of questionings, and goeth on in the firm and straight path of the commandments of God" (009-013). The simplicity of Bethlehem — a child in a manger, shepherds as the first witnesses, a place too insignificant to be named among the cities of Judah — is the outward form of this inward rule.

> "He who knoweth and judgeth lowly of himself, to account nothing of himself, and to think always favorably and well of others, this is a token of the truest humility" (002-029) — the very disposition incarnated at Bethlehem, where God made Himself nothing for the sake of humanity.

> "From the hour of My birth, until My death upon the Cross, I never ceased to suffer and to endure." (055-006)

## Symbolic Meaning

Bethlehem's name in Hebrew, "House of Bread" (Beit Lehem), carries deep symbolic resonance in Christian tradition. Just as Bethlehem provided the earthly wheat for which it was named, so it became the birthplace of Him who declared Himself the Bread of Life (John 6:35). The manger — a feeding-trough — becomes a symbol of Christ offered as food for the world.

The smallness of Bethlehem is itself theologically significant. Micah 5:2 declares it "little among the thousands of Judah," yet from it comes the ruler of Israel. This pattern — God choosing what is weak and foolish in the world's eyes to shame the wise and strong — is the very logic of Bethlehem, and it is the same logic that Thomas a Kempis applies to the spiritual life: the disciple who becomes small in his own eyes becomes great before God.

Bethlehem is also the place where heaven and earth meet in the most unremarkable circumstances. No Temple, no priesthood, no altar — only a child, a mother, and the beasts of the stable. This is the Kempian spirituality in its most concentrated form: God is found not in the grand apparatus of public religion but in the hiddenness of the interior life, in the stable of the heart where no one else is watching.
