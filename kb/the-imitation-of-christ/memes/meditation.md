# Meditation

The devout and attentive consideration of divine things and spiritual truths — in The Imitation of Christ, meditation is an essential exercise of the interior life, a deliberate turning of the mind away from outward distractions toward the things of God, death, judgment, and the hidden mysteries of grace.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, meditation is the soul's intentional dwelling upon divine realities — not mere mental reflection but a form of interior prayer that engages the affections as well as the intellect. Thomas à Kempis presents meditation as a discipline to be cultivated through the choice of suitable times, the reading of [[scripture]], and the turning of the mind toward ultimate things.

Meditation is distinguished from [[contemplation]] by its more active character: it is the soul's effort to direct thought toward God, aided by sacred texts and holy images, whereas contemplation is the simpler, more passive resting in the divine presence that may flow from it.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation of Christ treats meditation as integral to the devout life, naming it among the exercises that sustain the soul in its progress toward God.

### Meditation as a Daily Practice

Book I counsels the reader to "seek a suitable time for thy meditation, and think frequently of the benefits of God" (020-002). Meditation is not an occasional exercise but a regular practice requiring discipline and the prudent ordering of one's time. The soul that neglects meditation loses its orientation toward God and becomes vulnerable to the distractions and temptations of the world.

The text links meditation with other spiritual exercises: "fastings, in prayer and holy meditations" (018-007). Meditation belongs to a triad of practices — fasting, prayer, and meditation — that together form the foundation of the interior life. It is by these exercises that the soul is strengthened against temptation and drawn into deeper communion with God.

### Meditation on Death

Book I dedicates a chapter to "meditation upon death" (023-001), urging the reader to dwell frequently upon the reality of mortality. This is not morbid preoccupation but a spiritual discipline: the remembrance of death cuts through the illusions of the [[world]] and clarifies the soul's true priorities. The one who meditates upon death lives differently — with greater sobriety, detachment from passing things, and readiness for judgment.

### Meditation on the Hidden Judgments of God

The Imitation also commends "meditation upon the hidden judgments of God" (051-001), a theme that deepens the soul's humility. The hidden judgments are those divine decrees whose reasons remain inscrutable to human reason — why one soul is drawn to grace while another resists, why the righteous suffer while the wicked prosper. Meditation upon these mysteries does not resolve them but teaches the soul to trust in God's wisdom beyond its own understanding.

### Key Passages

> "Seek a suitable time for thy meditation, and think frequently of the benefits of God." (020-002)

> "The greatest saints used to avoid the company of men as much as they could, and chose to live to God in secret." (020-007)

> "In all things remember thy end, and how thou canst not abide here long." (023-009)

## Biblical Foundation

The scriptural foundation for meditation is established in the Old Testament wisdom tradition. The Psalmist declares the blessedness of the one whose "delight is in the law of the Lord, and in His law he meditates day and night" (Psalm 1:2). Joshua is commanded: "This Book of the Law shall not depart from your mouth, but you shall meditate in it day and night" (Joshua 1:8). The Psalmist again: "I will meditate on Your precepts, and contemplate Your ways" (Psalm 119:15). Paul extends the tradition: "Whatever things are true, whatever things are noble... meditate on these things" (Philippians 4:8).

## Practical Application

The practice of meditation, as taught in The Imitation, requires the deliberate pursuit of silence and solitude — finding "a suitable time" (020-002) away from the distractions of daily life. It involves reading [[scripture]] slowly and attentively, allowing the words to penetrate the heart rather than merely the intellect. The remembrance of death (023-001) and the hidden judgments of God (051-001) serve as particular themes that foster [[humility]] and detachment from the [[world]].
