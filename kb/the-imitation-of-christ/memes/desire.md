# Desire

The orientation of the human will toward an object of love — in The Imitation of Christ, desire is never neutral: it either ascends toward God, becoming the engine of sanctity, or descends toward creatures, becoming the source of all restlessness and dissatisfaction.

## Definition

In the anthropology of The Imitation of Christ, desire is the fundamental vector of the human heart. The soul is constituted by what it loves and what it longs for; its desires reveal its true allegiance more reliably than its words or outward observances. Thomas à Kempis does not counsel the extinction of desire but its radical reorientation — from creatures to the Creator, from self to God, from the transitory to the eternal.

The central diagnostic question is not whether one desires but what one desires: "How senseless and vain if thou desirest aught beyond Jesus!" (033-009). Every disordered desire is a misdirection of the heart's native capacity for God.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis presents desire as the deep structure of the spiritual life, threading through every book. The purification of desire — its detachment from creatures and its concentration upon God — is presented as the core work of Christian discipleship.

### Desire Beyond Jesus is Vain

Book II, Chapter VIII states the principle with uncompromising clarity: "How senseless and vain if thou desirest aught beyond Jesus! Is not this greater loss than if thou shouldst lose the whole world?" (033-009–011). All desire that terminates in anything less than God is not merely suboptimal but actively destructive — it is "senseless and vain," a loss greater than losing the world itself.

The positive corollary follows: the soul that desires Jesus alone finds everything: "He who findeth Jesus findeth a good treasure, yea, good above all good; and he who loseth Jesus loseth exceeding much, yea, more than the whole world" (033-014–016).

### The Desire Not to Be Praised

In the same chapter, Thomas turns desire inward to expose a particular subtlety: "Never desire to be specially praised or loved, because this belongeth to God alone, who hath none like unto Himself" (033-034–035). The desire for human esteem is a usurpation of what belongs to God — a disordered longing that displaces the Creator from His rightful place. The [[discipleship|disciple]] must instead "be pure and free within thyself, and be not entangled by any created thing" (033-039).

### He That Desireth Nothing Upon Earth

Book III, Chapter XXXI heightens the trajectory: "What is more peaceful than the single eye? And what more free than he that desireth nothing upon earth?" (068-006–007). The freedom Thomas envisions is not the absence of all longing but the concentration of all longing upon God, such that earthly things lose their gravitational pull. "Therefore must a man rise above every creature, and perfectly forsake himself, and with abstracted mind to stand and behold that Thou, the Creator of all things, hast among Thy creatures nothing like unto Thyself" (068-008–010).

The chapter penetrates a further irony: "Many are found who desire contemplation, but they do not strive to practice those things which are required thereunto" (068-026–027). Even the desire for spiritual goods can be disordered when it is not matched by the ascetical practice that makes contemplation possible. Desire alone, without the concrete disciplines of [[mortification|self-denial]] and [[solitude|recollection]], remains sterile.

### Ordered and Disordered Desire

The Imitation of Christ distinguishes between desire that expands the soul and desire that contracts it. Desire for God, for [[humility|virtue]], for [[eternity|eternal life]] is the motor of spiritual progress — "All desire to rejoice with Him" (036-005) is acknowledged as a natural and fitting longing. But the desire for "comforts" (036-010), for "outer things" (033-022), for "transitory and worthless things" (068-031) is the mark of a soul still entangled in creatures.

The remedy is not the suppression of desire but its conversion: "Thou oughtest to bring a bare and clean heart to God, if thou desirest to be ready to see how gracious the Lord is" (033-040–041). The heart purified of disordered desire becomes capable of receiving God — and only God satisfies it.

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of Christ anchors its theology of desire in the Psalms and the New Testament. The Psalmist's cry — "Whom have I in heaven but You? And there is none upon earth that I desire besides You" (Psalm 73:25) — captures the singleness of desire Thomas commends. "As the deer pants for the water brooks, so pants my soul for You, O God" (Psalm 42:1) models desire as thirst directed toward its only adequate object.

The New Testament sharpens the antithesis: "Do not love the world or the things in the world. If anyone loves the world, the love of the Father is not in him" (1 John 2:15). Christ's own teaching — "where your treasure is, there your heart will be also" (Matthew 6:21) — establishes the law that desire configures the heart to its object. Paul's confession — "I count all things loss for the excellence of the knowledge of Christ Jesus my Lord" (Philippians 3:8) — incarnates the reordering of desire that The Imitation of Christ presents as the substance of the spiritual life.
