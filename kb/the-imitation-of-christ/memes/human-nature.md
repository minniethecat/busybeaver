# Human Nature

The created human constitution as it now exists in fallen man — in The Imitation of Christ, human nature is the primary interior adversary of the spiritual life, an inclination toward self, creatures, and comfort that must be resisted at every turn.

## Definition

In the anthropology of The Imitation of Christ, human nature is the human person considered under the wound of original sin: an inborn inclination toward self-preference, creaturely attachments, and resistance to divine grace. Thomas à Kempis does not deny the goodness of nature as created — but he writes with relentless honesty about nature as it actually is in fallen man: "It is not in the nature of man to bear the cross, to love the cross, to chastise the body and bring it into subjection" (037-080).

Nature, in this sense, is the gravitational pull of the old self — what Paul calls "the flesh" (Galatians 5:17). It is not eradicated by [[baptism]] but remains as an active principle of resistance that must be mortified daily through [[self-denial]].

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis develops a sustained contrast between nature and [[grace]] — the one pulling downward toward self and creatures, the other lifting upward toward God and eternal things.

### The Necessities of Nature

The text acknowledges that "nature must be sustained" (063-030) — the body has legitimate needs for food, rest, and care. But Thomas immediately adds that "to desire superfluous things is not lawful" (063-030). Nature's necessities are modest; nature's cravings are insatiable. The disciple must learn to distinguish need from want and satisfy the former while mortifying the latter.

The soul should give "a little to the necessities of nature" (022-020) without indulging every inclination. The body is a servant, not a master; to feed its every demand is to become its slave.

### Nature's Resistance to the Cross

The sharpest statement comes in Book III: "It is not in the nature of man to bear the cross, to love the cross, to chastise the body and bring it into subjection, to flee honours, to bear reproaches meekly, to despise self and desire to be despised" (037-080–082). This is an anthropology in a sentence: left to itself, human nature flees everything the [[cross]] demands. It seeks comfort, honor, and vindication — the precise opposite of the Christian program.

### Nature's Sorrow

"The necessities of nature" are borne "with sorrow" (041-041) — even when the will consents to [[mortification]], nature groans. This is not sin but the condition of the viator, the pilgrim still journeying toward the homeland. The disciple must expect nature to protest; the test is whether the will, sustained by grace, overrules the protest.

### The Sin to Which We Are Most Inclined

Thomas counsels the disciple to examine "the sin to which by nature we are most" inclined (025-038) — each person has a particular vulnerability rooted in temperament and constitution. The spiritual life requires not generic remedies but targeted warfare against one's dominant fault.

### Nature's Deception

"Oftentimes it is the nature of man to be deceived by fancy" (067-023) — nature not only inclines toward sin but also rationalizes it. The mind, darkened by self-love, manufactures justifications for what the will already desires. This is why the disciple must not trust his own judgment but submit it to [[obedience|spiritual direction]] and the teaching of [[Scripture]].

### Key Passages

> "a little to the other necessities of nature, is truly" (022-020)

> "the sin to which by nature we are most" (025-038)

> "It is not in the nature of man to bear the cross, to love the cross" (037-080)

> "the necessities of nature with sorrow" (041-041)

> "nature must be sustained, but to desire superfluous things is not lawful" (063-030)

> "Oftentimes it is the nature of man to be deceived by fancy" (067-023)

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of Christ's teaching on human nature is a sustained meditation on Paul's confession in Romans 7: "I know that in me (that is, in my flesh) nothing good dwells" (Romans 7:18). The warfare between flesh and Spirit — "the flesh lusts against the Spirit, and the Spirit against the flesh" (Galatians 5:17) — structures the Kempian account of the interior life. Jeremiah's diagnosis — "the heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked" (Jeremiah 17:9) — grounds the counsel never to trust one's own judgment uncritically. And Genesis records that "every intent of the thoughts of man's heart was only evil continually" (Genesis 6:5), establishing the universal scope of nature's corruption. The Imitation of Christ takes these biblical truths and translates them into daily ascetical practice: know your nature, distrust its promptings, and lean wholly upon grace.
