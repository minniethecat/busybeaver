# Samuel

Samuel is the great prophet and last judge of Israel — in The Imitation of Christ he is invoked not for his historical acts but for the prayer that defines his name: "Speak, Lord, for Thy servant heareth" (1 Samuel 3:9). Thomas à Kempis places this prayer on the lips of the disciple as the fundamental posture of the interior life.

## Identity

Samuel is the transitional figure between the period of the judges and the monarchy of Israel. Born to Hannah in answer to her prayer at Shiloh (1 Samuel 1), he was dedicated to the Lord's service from childhood under Eli the priest. Called by God in the night — "Speak, Lord, for Thy servant heareth" (1 Samuel 3:9) — he became prophet, judge, and anointer of kings. Samuel anointed both Saul and David, guiding Israel through its transformation from a tribal confederation into a unified kingdom. In Christian tradition, he is revered among the great prophets of the Old Testament.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis invokes Samuel at the most pivotal moment in the entire Imitation — the opening of Book III, Chapter 2, "What the Truth saith inwardly without noise of words." The chapter begins with the disciple's prayer: "Speak, Lord, for Thy servant heareth" (039-001), drawn directly from 1 Samuel 3:9. But Thomas immediately transforms Samuel's prayer into a principle of the interior life: "Not thus, O Lord, not thus do I pray, but rather with Samuel the prophet, I beseech Thee humbly and earnestly, Speak, Lord, for Thy servant heareth" (039-007). The disciple distances himself from the Israelites' fear at Sinai — "Speak thou unto us and we will hear, but let not the Lord speak unto us lest we die" (039-005) — and instead adopts Samuel's posture of open, receptive listening. This inversion is the theological hinge of the entire chapter.

> "Not thus, O Lord, not thus do I pray, but rather with Samuel the prophet, I beseech Thee humbly and earnestly, Speak, Lord, for Thy servant heareth." (039-007)

Samuel is also invoked implicitly in the scriptural footnotes that undergird the chapter. The reference notes cite 1 Samuel 3:9 (039-030) as the scriptural source, anchoring the chapter's central prayer in the narrative of Samuel's calling.

## In the Christian Tradition

Samuel's night-time calling at Shiloh has been one of the most fruitful texts for Christian spiritual theology. The young Samuel, "ministered unto the Lord before Eli" in an age when "the word of the Lord was precious" and "there was no open vision" (1 Samuel 3:1), heard God's voice three times before Eli recognised it as divine — a progression that spiritual writers have read as the soul's gradual awakening to God's inward speech.

St. Gregory the Great, in his *Moralia on Job*, used Samuel's calling as the paradigm of prophetic discernment: the soul must learn, like Samuel, to distinguish God's voice from its own thoughts, and this capacity grows only through humble service in God's house. The Venerable Bede, in his commentary on 1 Samuel, emphasised that Samuel was called while sleeping in the temple near the ark — a detail that medieval writers connected to the disciple's need to rest near the sacramental presence of Christ in order to hear His voice.

The Carthusian tradition, to which Thomas à Kempis' spiritual masters belonged, held Samuel in particular esteem. The *Devotio Moderna* movement emphasised inward listening — the practice of sitting in silence before God and attending to the interior word — and Samuel's "Speak, Lord" was its biblical charter. Ludolph of Saxony, whose *Vita Christi* influenced The Imitation, dwelt at length on the contrast between Eli (who represents the outward, institutional word that has grown dim) and Samuel (who represents the inward, living word that is ever fresh). Thomas inherits this reading and radicalises it: the disciple is not merely to hear God's word through the outward ministry but to become Samuel — to "hear what the Lord God will speak within me" (039-026).

In the liturgy, Samuel is commemorated on August 20 in the Roman Martyrology, and his canticle — "Speak, Lord, for Thy servant heareth" — has entered the Church's prayer as the quintessential expression of vocational receptivity. For the reader of The Imitation, Samuel is not a figure of the remote past but the permanent model of the soul's posture before God.

## Spiritual Significance

In The Imitation, Samuel represents the soul's fundamental disposition: humble, attentive receptivity to the divine word. His prayer — "Speak, Lord, for Thy servant heareth" — is not a request for a new revelation but the offering of an obedient ear. The disciple who prays "with Samuel the prophet" (039-007) renounces the demand for outward mediators and opens the inward ear to Christ the Eternal Truth speaking within. Samuel thus becomes the type of every soul that has passed beyond the outward word of prophets and law to the inward word of divine illumination.
