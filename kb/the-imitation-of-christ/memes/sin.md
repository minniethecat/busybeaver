# Sin

The transgression of the [[law|divine law]] and the wounding of [[love|charity]] by which the soul turns from its Creator to the creature — the universal condition of fallen humanity from which no person, however advanced in holiness, is wholly exempt, demanding daily [[conscience|examination of conscience]], sorrow of heart, and [[confession|humble confession]], in the ascetical theology of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Sin, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not merely the violation of an external commandment but the interior turning of the heart from God to something less than God. It is a wound in [[love|charity]], a defilement of conscience, and a weight upon the soul that hinders its ascent to union with God. Thomas treats sin as the universal human condition — "we are all frail" (014-004) — and insists that no one, not even the most advanced in virtue, is exempt from the daily battle against it. The frank acknowledgment of one's own sinfulness is not a counsel of despair but the necessary preamble to grace, for "the more thou knowest thine own infirmity, the more thou profitest" (014-004).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Sin pervades The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as the dark background against which the whole work of grace is set. Thomas does not treat sin abstractly or theologically but as the concrete daily experience of every soul that strives to love God and finds itself hindered by its own weakness.

### The Universality and Persistence of Sin

Thomas insists with unwavering realism that sin is not a problem confined to beginners in the spiritual life but a permanent condition of the viator — the pilgrim soul still on the way. The just man "falleth seven times" (Proverbs 24:16), and the [[discipleship|disciple]] who imagines himself beyond the reach of particular sins is the one in greatest danger. The spiritual life is not a straight line of uninterrupted progress but a rhythm of fall and rise, sin and repentance, wound and healing.

This realism extends to the recognition that sins persist even after conversion and sincere repentance. "To-day thou confessest thy sins, and to-morrow thou committest again the sins thou didst confess" (022-055 to 022-056). Thomas does not soften this hard truth — what matters is not the absence of sin but the refusal to make peace with it and the prompt return to God through confession and [[amendment|amendment of life]].

### The Roots of Sin

Thomas locates the roots of sin in the interior dispositions of the soul rather than in external circumstances. [[pride|Pride]] is the primordial root — "because of their pride and curiosity, they fall into great temptations and sins" (041-030). The passions, when not governed by reason and grace, draw the will downward: "labour to resist sins and passions" (025-091) is the daily work of every [[discipleship|disciple]]. Most fundamentally, sin springs from the inordinate love of self — the soul that is "led captive" by its own desires rather than by the will of God has already committed the interior sin from which all exterior acts flow.

Thomas also identifies "those sins which most easily beset us" (019-027) — the particular weaknesses and habitual patterns unique to each soul. These besetting sins are the most dangerous because they are the most familiar; the soul grows accustomed to them, excuses them, and ceases to wage serious war against them. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is instructed to identify these sins by name and to direct the full force of his spiritual effort against them.

### The Effects of Sin

Sin produces multiple effects in the soul, each of which Thomas catalogs with clinical precision. First, sin defiles the conscience: "sins defile the conscience, and destroy the [[grace|grace of God]]" (001-037). The defiled conscience is the direct antithesis of the peace and joy that are the birthright of the child of God. Second, sin weighs down the soul: the [[discipleship|disciple]] prays to be delivered from "the weight of sins" (060-044), for sin is not a light thing that can be borne without consequence but a crushing burden that impedes prayer, darkens the understanding, and drains the will of its energy for good. Third, sin exposes the soul to judgment: "what shall the eternal fire devour, save thy sins?" (024-023). Every unrepented sin is fuel for the fire that awaits.

Yet Thomas does not leave the soul in despair. The same text that names the universality of sin also proclaims the greater universality of [[forgiveness|mercy]]. The remedy is always at hand, for "Thou, O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], art my refuge" (024-023).

### The Remedies of Sin

The remedies Thomas prescribes for sin are the classic ascetical practices of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition, intensified by his characteristic emphasis on interiority.

The first remedy is **[[contrition|compunction]]** — genuine [[contrition|sorrow for sin]] that springs not from fear of punishment alone but from love of God offended. "Think upon thy sins with great displeasure and inward bitterness of heart" (041-012). This sorrow is not morbid but medicinal; it cleanses the soul of attachment to sin and opens it to the healing action of grace.

The second remedy is [[confession|humble confession]] — the acknowledgment of one's sins before God and, where appropriate, before a confessor. Thomas devotes sustained attention to the [[conscience|examination of conscience]] that must precede confession (Book IV, Chapter 7), urging the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "diligently examine thy conscience" and to "cleanse and purify it" with true contrition. [[confession|Confession]] is not a mere listing of faults but an act of self-exposure before God that is itself a mortification of pride — the root sin.

The third remedy is **vigilance** — the daily, even hourly guard over the heart that refuses to give sin a foothold. The [[discipleship|disciple]] must be "zealous to conquer even a single fault" (011-014) rather than tolerating small sins that prepare the way for greater ones.

The fourth remedy is **mortification** — the deliberate putting to death of disordered desires. "The mortification of thy sins" (080-007) is more profitable than all speculation about high matters, for the soul that is not willing to die to its own will cannot live to God.

## Key Passages

> "We are all frail, but thou shalt esteem none more frail than thyself. Thou shalt not easily fall into sin if thou shalt judge and examine thine own self before thou judgest others." (014-004)

> "Think upon thy sins with great displeasure and inward bitterness of heart; never think thyself to be anything because of thy good works, but rather acknowledge thyself to be a sinner, entangled and weighed down by many passions." (041-012)

> "To-day thou confessest thy sins, and to-morrow thou committest again the sins thou didst confess. Now dost thou purpose to beware of them, and within an hour thou so behavest thyself as if thou hadst never purposed at all." (022-055 to 022-056)

> "Labour to resist sins and passions, than to toil in bodily exercises." (025-091)

> "Keep thy soul from sin, rather than to cling to sins from which we must be separated against our will." (024-020)

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of sin in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is grounded in the full sweep of Scripture. The universality of sin echoes Romans 3:23 — "all have sinned and come short of the glory of God" — and 1 John 1:8: "If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves." The mechanism of sin — desire conceiving and bringing forth death — follows James 1:14-15, where "every man is tempted when he is drawn away of his own lust and enticed."

The penitential keynote of the work draws upon Psalm 51, the Miserere — "my sin is ever before me" (Psalm 51:3) — which Thomas knew as part of the daily Divine Office. The rhythm of daily confession and daily relapse that Thomas describes so unsparingly echoes the petition of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]'s [[prayer|Prayer]]: "forgive us our trespasses" (Matthew 6:12) — a prayer meant to be said daily precisely because sin is a daily reality. And the hope that runs beneath every acknowledgment of sin rests on the promise that "a just man falleth seven times, and riseth up again" (Proverbs 24:16) — not the sinlessness of the saint but the rising.
