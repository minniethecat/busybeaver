# Incarnation

The Incarnation is the foundational Christian mystery in which the eternal Son of God took human flesh in the womb of the Virgin Mary, uniting divine and human natures in the single Person of Jesus Christ (John 1:14; Luke 1:26–38).

## Biblical Account

The Incarnation is preeminently proclaimed in the Prologue of John's Gospel: "the Word was made flesh and dwelt among us" (John 1:14). The event itself is narrated in the Annunciation, where the Angel Gabriel declares to the Virgin Mary that she will conceive by the Holy Spirit and bear the Son of God (Luke 1:26–38). Thomas a Kempis directly evokes this scene, describing how Mary "humbly and devoutly answered the Angel who brought unto her the glad tidings of the mystery of the Incarnation" [(113-021)–024]. The Apostle Paul articulates the theological depth of the mystery: Christ, "being in the form of God... emptied himself, taking the form of a servant" (Philippians 2:6–7).

### Gospel Accounts

John's Gospel provides the most explicit theological affirmation of the Incarnation — the Logos who was "in the beginning" and "was God" took on human nature, so that the blessed in heaven "taste the Word of God made flesh, as He was in the beginning and remaineth for everlasting" (107-027). Luke offers the historical narrative of how this occurred through Mary's fiat. Matthew traces the royal and Abrahamic lineage into which Christ was born (Matthew 1:1–25). Though Mark does not include an infancy narrative, his Gospel opens with the declaration of Jesus as "the Son of God" (Mark 1:1), presupposing the incarnate reality.

## Theological Significance

The Incarnation is the hinge of salvation history. By taking human nature, the Son of God sanctified human existence from within, making possible both his atoning sacrifice on the Cross and the gift of the Eucharist. The mystery reveals two interconnected truths: first, the radical humility of God, who "humbled himself exceedingly" by walking among men (113-029); second, the dignity conferred on human nature through its assumption by the divine Word.

The event also establishes Christ as the perfect exemplar for Christian discipleship. "The whole life of Christ was a cross and martyrdom" (037-060) — from his first descent into the Virgin's womb to his death on Calvary — forming an unbroken arc of self-giving love that believers are called to imitate. Thomas a Kempis exhorts the reader to dwell continually on "the life of Jesus Christ" (001-007), whose incarnate years from Bethlehem to the Cross constitute the supreme pattern for the spiritual life.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas a Kempis treats the Incarnation not as an abstract doctrine but as a mystery to be "called to mind" for spiritual nourishment, paired inseparably with the Passion: the devout soul "calleth to mind the mystery of Christ's incarnation and His Passion, and is inflamed with the love of Him" (106-064). This pairing reflects the conviction that the entire earthly life of Christ — from the Annunciation to Calvary — is the sustained act of divine self-giving.

The most extended treatment of the Incarnation in the work appears in Book IV, where the disciple, yearning for union with Christ in the Eucharist, recalls how the Virgin Mary received the Word with humility and devotion at the Annunciation, and desires to imitate her faithful receptivity [(113-021)–025]. Thomas further exhorts the communicant to approach the Sacrament with the same reverence as if Christ "for the first time were descending into the Virgin's womb and becoming man, or hanging on the cross, suffering and dying for the salvation of mankind" [(098-063)–065] — drawing an explicit parallel between the Incarnation, the Eucharist, and the Passion.

The Imitation draws from the Incarnation a practical spirituality of humility and obedience. In a striking passage where Christ himself speaks, the Lord declares: "I became the most humble and despised of men, that by My humility thou mightest overcome thy pride" [(050-019)–020]. The incarnate descent becomes the ultimate model for the disciple's self-abasement and submission to God. The virtues exemplified in the Incarnation — humility, obedience, and love "born of God" (042-028) — are those the disciple is called to reproduce through grace, for God "resisteth the proud and giveth grace to the humble" (007-008).

> "Behold the handmaid of the Lord; be it unto me according to thy word." — Mary's fiat at the Incarnation, as quoted by Thomas a Kempis [(113-024)–025]

## Liturgical Connection

The Incarnation is celebrated liturgically in the Feast of the Annunciation (March 25) and the Nativity of the Lord (Christmas, December 25). The mystery is also commemorated in the Angelus prayer and in every recitation of the Nicene Creed ("by the Holy Spirit was incarnate of the Virgin Mary and became man"). In the Eucharist, the Word-made-flesh continues to be truly present under sacramental signs, extending the incarnate presence through time — Thomas reflects that Christ "hidest Thyself under the Sacrament" in condescension to human weakness [(107-016)–018].
