# Despair

**Despair** (*desperatio*) is treated in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* as a dangerous spiritual temptation — the loss of hope in [[mercy|God's mercy]] and the [[desolation|abandonment]] of [[trust-in-god|trust]] in divine assistance. Thomas à Kempis addresses it directly across multiple contexts: temptation, spiritual [[desolation|dryness]], and the alternation of [[comfort|consolation]] and desolation. In every case, the remedy is the same: humble, persistent prayer grounded in the promise that God provides a way through every [[temptation|trial]].

## Despair in Temptation

The most direct treatment of despair appears in Book I, Chapter 13, on resisting temptation. After describing the gradual process by which temptation gains mastery over the soul, Kempis issues a firm prohibition:

> Therefore we ought not to despair when we are tempted, but the more fervently should cry unto God, that He will vouchsafe to help us in all our tribulation; and that He will, as St. Paul saith, with the temptation make a way to escape that we may be able to bear it. [013-054-057]

The argument is theological: despair is unwarranted because God has promised a way of escape. The promise of 1 Corinthians 10:13 is invoked as the ground of hope. To despair is, in effect, to doubt God's [[faith|fidelity]] to His own word.

[[humility|Humility]] is the proper alternative: "Let us therefore humble ourselves under the mighty hand of God in all temptation and trouble, for He will save and exalt such as are of an humble spirit" [013-058-059]. [[humility|Humility]] [[trust-in-god|trust]]s; despair presumes to know the outcome and judges it hopeless.

## Despair in Spiritual Dryness

Book III, Chapter 34 addresses the experience of losing spiritual [[comfort|consolation]] — a common trigger for despair. The counsel is direct:

> When comfort is taken from thee, do not straightway despair, but wait for the heavenly visitation with humility and patience, for God is able to give thee back greater favour and consolation. [034-034-036]

The pattern of [[comfort|consolation]] and desolation is not a sign of divine [[desolation|abandonment]] but a normal feature of the spiritual life: "This is not strange to those who have made [[temptation|trial]] of the way of God, for with the great saints and the ancient prophets there was often this manner of change" [034-037-038]. The example of the Psalmist is invoked: he experienced both prosperity of soul and divine withdrawal — "Thou didst turn Thy face from me, and I was troubled" — yet "in no wise despaireth, but the more instantly entreateth God, and saith, Unto Thee, O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], will I cry, and will pray unto my God" [034-040-044].

The central lesson is clear: **the right response to desolation is not despair but intensified prayer**. The Psalmist who cries to God is heard: "The [[jesus-christ|Lord]] heard me and had [[forgiveness|mercy]] upon me, the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] was my helper. Thou hast turned my heaviness into joy" [034-047-048].

## Resting on the Will of God

In Book I, Chapter 33, counsel against despair is linked to surrender to [[providence|divine providence]]. When adversities come, the soul is not to be "cast down nor to despair, but to rest with calm mind on the will of God" (033-047). Despair is revealed as, at root, a failure of [[faith|trust in God]]'s ordering of events.

This connection between despair and dis[[trust-in-god|trust]] of providence is theologically important. The soul that believes God orders all things for good cannot ultimately despair, because it [[trust-in-god|trust]]s that even present darkness serves a divine purpose. The practical means is calm surrender rather than anxious self-analysis.

## The Example of the Saints

Kempis argues from the experience of the saints: if even they — who far surpassed ordinary souls in virtue — experienced alternations of light and darkness, "we who are poor and needy ought not to despair if we be sometimes cold" (034-050). The great saints were not exempt from desolation. Their perseverance, not their exemption from [[temptation|trial]], is what made them great.

This reasoning is pastoral and effective: by normalizing the experience of spiritual [[desolation|dryness]], Kempis removes the shame that can drive a soul toward despair. What the saint endured with [[trust-in-god|trust]], the ordinary believer can endure with the same [[trust-in-god|trust]].

## The Danger of Despairing Self-Perception

Book III also warns against a subtler form of despair: the excessively negative view of oneself that paralyzes spiritual effort. The soul "beareth himself too despairingly, and feeleth concerning Me otherwise than is to be believed" (044-043). This distorted self-perception — seeing oneself as beyond help — is itself a temptation to be resisted, not a realistic assessment to be accepted.

Divine [[forgiveness|mercy]] is always greater than human sin. To despair is to place a limit on the limitless [[forgiveness|mercy]] of God — a subtle form of pride disguised as humility.
