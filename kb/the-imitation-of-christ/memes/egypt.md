# Egypt

The great empire of the Nile delta from which the children of Israel were delivered by the hand of God through Moses, and which serves throughout Scripture and Christian tradition as the archetypal symbol of bondage — the state of spiritual slavery from which Christ redeems the soul.

## Location

Egypt is the ancient civilization of the lower Nile valley in northeastern Africa, bounded by the Mediterranean Sea to the north and the Red Sea to the east. In biblical geography, Egypt was the dominant imperial power to the southwest of Canaan, and the site of Israel's four-hundred-year sojourn and subsequent enslavement (Exodus 1:1-14). The exodus route traditionally departs from the region of Goshen in the eastern Nile delta toward the Red Sea and Sinai.

## Biblical Significance

Egypt is one of the most theologically freighted places in the biblical narrative. It is the house of bondage from which God delivered His covenant people by mighty acts of judgment and mercy, establishing the Exodus as the foundational event of Israel's identity and the defining paradigm of divine redemption throughout Scripture. The deliverance from Egypt is the gospel of the Old Testament: "I am the Lord thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage" (Exodus 20:2).

### Key Events at This Place

- **The Enslavement of Israel** — The children of Israel multiplied in Egypt after the time of Joseph, and a Pharaoh arose "which knew not Joseph," reducing them to bitter servitude (Exodus 1:8-14).
- **The Exodus** — Through Moses, God sent ten plagues upon Egypt, culminating in the Passover and Israel's departure through the Red Sea, while Pharaoh's army was overthrown in the waters (Exodus 7–14).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Egypt itself is not named directly in The Imitation of Christ; Thomas a Kempis writes not biblical history but interior devotion, and his references to the Old Testament always serve the purpose of spiritual instruction. Nevertheless, Egypt's significance is woven through the work by means of three connected threads: the figure of Moses, the citation of the Exodus, and the theme of deliverance from slavery.

Moses appears at several key moments. In Book III, Chapter II, Thomas recalls how "the children of Israel spake in old time to Moses, Speak thou unto us and we will hear, but let not the Lord speak unto us lest we die" (039-005 to 039-006), citing Exodus 20:19 directly (039-030). The disciple, however, reverses Israel's request: "Let not Moses speak to me, nor any prophet, but rather speak Thou, O Lord" (039-009). Where Israel at Sinai — fresh from Egypt — drew back in fear, the Kempian disciple presses forward, desiring the direct voice of God.

In Book IV, Chapter I, Moses is invoked as "Thy servant, Thy great and especial friend" who "made an ark of incorruptible wood, which also he covered with purest gold, that he might lay up in it the tables of the law" (097-034 to 097-036). The disciple contrasts Moses's reverent preparation with his own unworthiness before the Eucharist — a humility instructed by the very lawgiver who led Israel from Egypt.

Most significantly, in Book III, Chapter XXXVIII, Christ exhorts the disciple to be "not a slave or hireling, but rather a free and true Hebrew, entering into the lot and the liberty of the children of God" (075-007 to 075-008). The phrase "free and true Hebrew" is laden with Exodus significance: the Hebrew is precisely the one whom God delivered from Egypt. The interior liberty Christ offers is the antitype of the outward deliverance at the Red Sea.

The echo of Egypt's bondage sounds throughout the work's treatment of sin as slavery. In Book III, Chapter XLVIII, the disciple cries: "When shall I be delivered from the wretched slavery of my sins?" (085-019). The language of deliverance from slavery — the very language of the Exodus — is Thomas's chosen idiom for sanctification. Every soul delivered from sin recapitulates Israel's departure from Egypt, and Christ is the greater Moses who leads His people not through a sea of water but through the waters of baptism into true liberty.

> "Let not Moses speak to me, nor any prophet, but rather speak Thou, O Lord, who didst inspire and illuminate all the prophets." (039-009 to 039-010)

> "Be not a slave or hireling, but rather a free and true Hebrew, entering into the lot and the liberty of the children of God." (075-007 to 075-008)

## Symbolic Meaning

In Christian spiritual tradition, Egypt is the archetype of the world, the flesh, and bondage to sin. The Exodus from Egypt prefigures baptism and the Christian's passage from the dominion of darkness into the kingdom of light. The Church Fathers developed this typology extensively: Egypt is the "iron furnace" (Deuteronomy 4:20) from which only divine power can deliver; Pharaoh is the type of Satan who refuses to let the soul go; the Red Sea crossing is baptism (1 Corinthians 10:1-2); and the wilderness journey is the Christian life of testing and sanctification on the way to the promised land.

Thomas a Kempis inherits this entire typology and interiorizes it. His "wretched slavery of my sins" (085-019) is Egypt internalized — not a geographical place but a spiritual condition from which only Christ can deliver. The "free and true Hebrew" (075-007) is the Christian who has passed through the waters and now walks in the liberty of the children of God, no longer under the taskmaster of sin but under the gentle yoke of Christ.

The Kempian treatment of Egypt also illuminates a distinctive feature of his spirituality: the Exodus is not merely a past event to be commemorated but a present reality to be undergone. The soul that would be free must not only recall that God delivered Israel from Egypt but must itself pass from the Egypt of sin into the liberty of grace. Each act of confession, each renunciation of a besetting sin, each turning of the will from self to God is a personal Exodus — a departure from the house of bondage under the leadership of Christ, the greater Moses.
