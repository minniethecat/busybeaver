# Jerusalem

The holy city of biblical faith and revelation — site of Christ's crucifixion, burial, and resurrection, location of Solomon's Temple, and the earthly type of the heavenly city toward which every Christian pilgrim journeys, in the theology and devotional imagination of The Imitation of Christ.

## Location

Jerusalem is situated in the hill country of Judea, on a limestone plateau in the southern Levant, approximately 60 km east of the Mediterranean Sea and 35 km west of the northern tip of the Dead Sea. Built across two principal ridges — the Western Hill (Mount Zion) and the Eastern Hill (the Ophel and Mount Moriah) — and bordered by the Kidron Valley to the east and the Hinnom Valley to the south, it has been continuously inhabited for over five millennia. At the time of Christ, it was the capital of Roman Judea under the procuratorship of Pontius Pilate, centred upon the Second Temple rebuilt by Herod the Great.

Known in Scripture as "the city of the great King" (Psalm 48:2) and "the joy of the whole earth" (Psalm 48:2), Jerusalem occupies a unique place in salvation history as the city where God chose to place His name and where the climactic acts of redemption — the Passion, Death, Resurrection, and Ascension of Jesus Christ — were accomplished.

## Biblical Significance

Jerusalem is the geographical centre of the biblical narrative. It is the city David conquered and made his capital (2 Samuel 5:6–9), the site where Solomon erected the First Temple as the dwelling place of the divine Name (2 Chronicles 6:2, 6–7), and the destination of Israel's three annual pilgrimage festivals. It is the city toward which the psalmists sang (Psalm 122:1–4), the city over which the prophets wept (Jeremiah 9:1; Luke 19:41–44), and — most decisively for Christian faith — the city where Jesus Christ was condemned, crucified, buried, and raised from the dead on the third day.

After Pentecost, Jerusalem became the birthplace of the Church (Acts 2:1–4), the site of the first apostolic council (Acts 15), and the point from which the gospel radiated outward to Judea, Samaria, and the ends of the earth (Acts 1:8). The New Testament closes with a vision of the New Jerusalem descending from heaven as a bride adorned for her husband (Revelation 21:2), transforming the earthly city into the eternal type of the Church triumphant.

### Key Events at This Place

- **The Passion and Crucifixion of Christ** — Jesus was condemned before Pilate in Jerusalem, carried His Cross through its streets, and was crucified at Golgotha outside the city walls (Matthew 27:33–35). Thomas a Kempis exhorts the disciple to contemplate that "He went before thee bearing His Cross and died for thee upon the Cross, that thou also mayest bear thy cross and mayest love to be crucified upon it" (037-018).

- **The Building of Solomon's Temple** — Solomon, the wisest of the kings of Israel, "was seven years building his magnificent temple to the praise of Thy Name, and for eight days celebrated the feast of its dedication, offered a thousand peace offerings" (097-039). Thomas a Kempis uses this episode in Book IV to illustrate the reverence due to the Eucharistic mystery, contrasting Solomon's elaborate temple preparations with the disciple's careless approach to the altar.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Jerusalem appears in The Imitation of Christ in a twofold register: as a specific biblical place referenced in traditional scriptural language, and as a spiritual type of the interior and heavenly realities that are the work's constant preoccupation. Thomas a Kempis, writing for a monastic audience in the devotio moderna tradition, is less interested in the geography of the holy city than in what it signifies for the soul's journey toward God.

The sole explicit mention of the city by name occurs in Book III, Chapter 43, in the context of the final judgment. There Thomas declares that Christ "will search Jerusalem with candles, and the hidden things of darkness shall be made manifest" (080-017). The citation — drawn from Zephaniah 1:12 via liturgical tradition — invokes Jerusalem as the place of divine scrutiny, where no secret escapes the searching light of the divine Judge. In this eschatological context, Jerusalem becomes the symbol of the soul laid bare before its Maker.

More frequently, Thomas a Kempis invokes Jerusalem indirectly through its defining events. The Passion of Christ, which unfolded entirely in and around Jerusalem, is held up as the supreme model for the disciple's own life of self-denial. The disciple is instructed to "rest thou in the passion of Christ and dwell willingly in His sacred wounds" (026-038), and to exercise himself "seriously and devoutly in the most holy life and passion of our Lord" (025-058). Every reference to the Cross in The Imitation of Christ is, implicitly, a reference to the city where that Cross was planted — Golgotha, outside the walls of Jerusalem, where the salvation of the world was accomplished.

The Temple of Jerusalem — Solomon's first and, by implication, the Second Temple of Christ's own day — appears as a figure of the reverent preparation the soul must make before receiving Holy Communion. Thomas contrasts Solomon's seven years of labour and his lavish peace offerings with the disciple's often careless approach to the Eucharistic Christ, who is greater than the Temple: "Solomon, the wisest of the kings of Israel, was seven years building his magnificent temple to the praise of Thy Name" (097-039). If Solomon expended such care for a building of stone, how much more care ought the Christian to expend in preparing the temple of the soul?

> "And then will He search Jerusalem with candles, and the hidden things of darkness shall be made manifest, and the arguings of tongues shall be silent." (080-017)

## Symbolic Meaning

In Christian tradition, Jerusalem operates on multiple symbolic levels: it is simultaneously the earthly city of David and Christ's Passion, the liturgical assembly of the Church militant, and the heavenly city of the Church triumphant. The Imitation of Christ participates fully in this threefold understanding.

The most prominent symbolic use of Jerusalem in the work is through the New Testament theme of the "continuing city" — or rather, its absence in this present life. Twice Thomas a Kempis quotes or paraphrases Hebrews 13:14: "Here have we no continuing city" (023-075, 026-028). The earthly Jerusalem, like every earthly reality, is passing away. The true homeland of the Christian is the heavenly Jerusalem, "the city of the living God" (Hebrews 12:22), toward which the soul must direct its gaze. "Lift up thine eyes to God in heaven" (022-012), the disciple is counselled, for "in heaven ought thy habitation to be" (026-037).

This eschatological orientation transforms Jerusalem from a geographical location into a spiritual destination. The soul is a pilgrim and stranger on earth, having no abiding city here below. Jerusalem thus becomes, in the Kempian vocabulary, synonymous with the heavenly kingdom itself — the place of rest, peace, and vision of God that awaits those who have walked faithfully the royal way of the Cross. Every chapter that speaks of the kingdom of God, of the heavenly fatherland, of the soul's yearning for its true home, draws upon this deep symbolic identification of Jerusalem with the eternal city.

## The Devout Life: Pilgrimage of the Heart

The Imitation of Christ transforms Jerusalem from a geographical destination into a spiritual itinerary. For the Kempian disciple — likely a canon regular or a member of a lay devotional community who would never physically travel to the Holy Land — the true pilgrimage to Jerusalem was interior. This interiorization of pilgrimage is one of the most characteristic features of the *devotio moderna* spirituality, and it deserves careful attention.

### The Interior Pilgrimage

When Thomas à Kempis writes that "here have we no continuing city" (023-075, citing Hebrews 13:14), he is not merely rehearsing a doctrinal truth but inviting the disciple into a lived experience of pilgrimage. The soul is to understand itself as a traveler — not yet arrived, not yet at rest, always in motion toward the heavenly Jerusalem. This self-understanding transforms every day into a stage of the journey and every trial into a step along the royal way of the Cross.

The discipline of interior pilgrimage involves three movements. The first is **detachment from the earthly city**: the disciple must "leave vain things to vain men" (020-068) and refuse to set down roots in what is passing away. The second is **orientation toward the heavenly city**: "lift up thine eyes to God in heaven" (022-012), fixing the gaze of the heart upon the Jerusalem that is above, "which is the mother of us all" (Galatians 4:26). The third is **perseverance on the way**: "he who followeth Me walketh not in darkness" (026-011), and the daily labor of obedience, charity, and self-denial is the actual walking.

### Christ as the Way to Jerusalem

In the Kempian scheme, Christ Himself is the road to the heavenly Jerusalem. Every meditation on the Passion places the disciple at Golgotha — outside the walls of the earthly Jerusalem, at the foot of the Cross — and the paradox of Christian pilgrimage is that one reaches the heavenly city only by way of the Cross planted outside the earthly one. "He went before thee bearing His Cross and died for thee upon the Cross, that thou also mayest bear thy cross and mayest love to be crucified upon it" (037-018).

The Passion is thus the map of the pilgrimage. Each station of the Way of the Cross — condemned by Pilate, carrying the beam, falling under its weight, meeting His Mother, being stripped and nailed, dying in darkness, being laid in the tomb — is a stage on the soul's journey from the earthly Jerusalem (the city that condemned Him) to the heavenly Jerusalem (the city where He reigns). The disciple who meditates the Passion daily is making the pilgrimage daily.

### The Eucharist as Foretaste of the Heavenly Jerusalem

For the Kempian Christian, the supreme earthly encounter with the heavenly Jerusalem occurs in the Eucharist. When the disciple receives Holy Communion, the heavenly city descends to meet the pilgrim on the way. The liturgy of the Mass is the liturgy of the New Jerusalem — "with angels and archangels and all the company of heaven" — and the one who participates in it with devotion is granted a foretaste of the eternal worship toward which all pilgrimage tends.

Thomas à Kempis makes this connection explicit in Book IV: the soul preparing for Communion is preparing to receive within itself the One who is the Temple of the heavenly Jerusalem (Revelation 21:22). "How great the loving kindness of Thy goodness, that Thou Lord God, Creator and Quickener of all spirits, dost vouchsafe to come to a poor soul" (107-061). The Eucharist is the pilgrim's viaticum — food for the journey — and the pledge of arrival.

### The City as Symbol of the Soul

In a final, daring move, the tradition that The Imitation represents comes to identify Jerusalem not only as the destination but as a figure of the soul itself. The soul is to become a Jerusalem — a city of peace, a dwelling place of God, a temple where the divine Name abides. "If any man love Me, he will keep My words, and My Father will love him, and We will come unto him and make Our abode with him" (John 14:23). The goal of the pilgrimage is not merely to arrive at the city but to become the city — to be so transformed by grace that the soul itself becomes a dwelling place of the Most High, a Jerusalem of the heart.
