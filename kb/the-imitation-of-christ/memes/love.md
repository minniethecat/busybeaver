# Love (Charity)

The supreme theological virtue by which God is loved above all things for His own sake, and all [[creation|creatures]] are loved in Him and for Him — the central animating fire of the entire spiritual life in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Love — *caritas* in the Latin of Thomas à Kempis — is not one virtue among many but the very form and soul of all virtue. In The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], love is identified with grace itself and is described as "a great thing, a good above all others, which alone maketh every heavy burden light, and equaliseth every inequality" (042-017). The work declares that "love was born of God and cannot rest save in God above all [[creation|created things]]" (042-028), locating its origin and end entirely in the divine nature. This Kempian understanding of love is not mere sentiment or natural affection but a supernatural participation in the life of God: the proper gift of the elect is "grace — that is, love — and they who bear the mark thereof are held worthy of everlasting life" (092-041). The opening chapter establishes love of God as the sole enduring reality amid universal vanity: in a world where "all is vanity, save to love God, and Him only to serve" (001-021), love alone gives meaning and permanence to human existence.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats love as the supreme virtue, woven through every book of The Imitation. From the opening declaration of Book I to the [[eucharist|Eucharist]]ic meditations of Book IV, love is the thread that unites the entire work. Book III, Chapter V — "Of the Wonderful Effect of Divine Love" — contains the most concentrated and elevated treatment, a sustained lyrical meditation on the nature and power of divine love that ranks among the finest passages in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian devotional literature.

### The Nature of Divine Love

Book III, Chapter V presents an extended portrait of love's qualities. "Nothing is sweeter than love, nothing stronger, nothing loftier, nothing broader, nothing pleasanter, nothing fuller or better in heaven nor on earth" (042-026). Love imparts supernatural energy to the soul: "He who loveth flyeth, runneth, and is glad; he is free and not bound" (042-030). It knows no limits, for "love oftentimes knoweth no measure, but breaketh out above all measure; love feeleth no burden, reckoneth not labours, striveth after more than it is able to do" (042-035). It is tireless and indomitable: "Love is watchful, and whilst sleeping still keepeth watch; though fatigued it is not weary, though pressed it is not forced, though alarmed it is not terrified, but like the living flame and the burning torch, it breaketh forth on high and securely triumpheth" (042-041).

The chapter closes with a catalogue of love's moral attributes that draws unmistakably on 1 Corinthians 13: "Love is swift, sincere, pious, pleasant, gentle, strong, patient, faithful, prudent, [[patience|long-suffering]], manly, and never seeking her own; for wheresoever a man seeketh his own, there he falleth from love" (042-056). This self-emptying quality is essential to the Kempian understanding: love is subject, obedient, humble, and "devout and grateful towards God, faithful and always [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing in Him even when God hideth His face, for without sorrow we cannot live in love" (042-064).

### The Exclusive Love of Jesus

Book II, Chapter VII — "Of the Intimate Love of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]" — teaches that divine love demands exclusivity. "[[jesus-christ|Jesus]] will be loved alone above all things. The love of [[creation|created things]] is deceiving and unstable, but the love of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] is faithful and lasting" (032-004). To love [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] is to despise oneself, for "blessed is he who understandeth what it is to love [[jesus-christ|Jesus]], and to despise himself for [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]' sake" (032-002). This is no mere preference but a radical reordering of the affections: [[jesus-christ|the Beloved]] "will suffer no rival, but alone will possess thy heart, and as a king will sit upon His own throne" (032-014). The love of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] alone endures when all other attachments fail, for "He will not forsake thee when all depart from thee" (032-008).

### True Charity and Its Counterfeits

In Book I, Chapter XV — "Of Works of [[charity|Charity]]" — Thomas carefully distinguishes genuine *caritas* from its imitations. "He doth much who loveth much" (015-010), for God values the love behind the act more than its outward greatness. Without charity, nothing avails: "Without charity no work profiteth, but whatsoever is done in charity, however small and of no reputation it be, bringeth forth good fruit" (015-005). Yet Thomas warns that mere natural benevolence can masquerade as supernatural charity: "Oftentimes that seemeth to be charity which is rather carnality, because it springeth from [[nature|natural inclination]], self-will, hope of repayment, desire of gain" (015-012). The one who possesses [[charity|true charity]] "in no wise seeketh his own good, but desireth that God alone be altogether glorified" (015-015). So transformative is this virtue that even "he who hath but a spark of [[charity|true charity]], hath verily learned that all [[world|worldly things]] are full of vanity" (015-021).

### The Testing of Love

True love is proven in [[suffering|adversity]]. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] addresses the [[discipleship|disciple]] in Book III, Chapter VI: "My Son, thou art not yet strong and prudent in thy love" (043-002), for the immature lover falls away at the first opposition. The "strong lover standeth fast in temptations, and believeth not the evil persuasions of the enemy" (043-006). The "prudent lover considereth not the gift of the lover so much as the love of the giver" (043-009), resting not in [[comfort|consolation]]s but in God alone. Book II, Chapter XI deepens this theme: "Oh what power hath the pure love of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]], unmixed with any gain or love of self!" (036-017). Those who love only for [[comfort|consolation]]s "prove themselves lovers of self more than of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (036-019). The summit of love is to "retain nothing of self-love" (036-032), for divine love and self-love are inversely proportional.

### Love, Grace, and the Eucharist

Throughout The Imitation, love is inseparable from grace. Indeed, "the proper gift of the elect is grace — that is, love" (092-041), an identification that makes love not merely a human virtue but the very substance of the divine life in the soul. "Divine charity conquereth all things, and enlargeth all the powers of the soul" (046-023). In Book IV, love reaches its [[eucharist|Eucharist]]ic consummation: "the charity of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] never groweth less, and the greatness of His propitiation is never exhausted" (098-057). The [[discipleship|disciple]]'s prayer for enlargement in love — "Enlarge Thou me in love, that I may learn to taste with the innermost mouth of my heart how sweet it is to love, to be dissolved, and to swim in love" (042-048) — expresses the soul's deepest longing for complete transformation into the [[charity|divine charity]].

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] grounds its entire theology of love in Scripture, most notably in the Pauline hymn of 1 Corinthians 13, which Thomas à Kempis echoes and amplifies in Book III, Chapter V. The declaration that "God is love" (1 John 4:8, 16) underlies the work's identification of love with grace and the very being of God. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s new commandment — "love one another, as I have loved you" (John 15:12) — provides the pattern of self-giving love that the Imitation holds up for imitation. The greatest commandment to "love the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] thy God with all thy heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy mind" (Deuteronomy 6:5, Matthew 22:37) is the text's constant refrain, echoed in the opening chapter's "all is vanity, save to love God, and Him only to serve" (001-021). The Pauline insistence that nothing avails without charity (1 Corinthians 13:1-3) directly informs Book I, Chapter XV's teaching that "without charity no work profiteth" (015-005). The identification of charity with the bond of perfection (Colossians 3:14) and the teaching that faith works through love (Galatians 5:6) provide the theological framework for the Kempian synthesis of love and grace.
