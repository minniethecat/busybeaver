# Holiness

The lifelong pursuit of conformity to the will of God — not a sinlessness this side of heaven claims to possess, but a love so purified by grace that the soul's every movement tends Godward. Holiness is at once God's gift and the disciple's most strenuous labor: "Be ye holy, for I am holy" (Leviticus 19:2; 1 Peter 1:16) is a command human effort cannot fulfill, yet grace can — and does — when the will cooperates.

## Definition

Holiness (Hebrew *qadosh*, Greek *hagios*) means "set apart" — consecrated to God and separated from what is profane or common. In the Old Testament, holiness first attaches to God Himself, who is "Holy, holy, holy" (Isaiah 6:3), and from Him it extends to persons, places, and objects consecrated to His service. The New Testament deepens the concept: holiness is not merely ritual purity but moral and spiritual transformation — the progressive renewal of the whole person in the image of Christ. "Follow peace with all men, and holiness, without which no man shall see the Lord" (Hebrews 12:14). The call to holiness is universal, not reserved for a spiritual elite: every Christian is summoned to "perfecting holiness in the fear of God" (2 Corinthians 7:1).

The Imitation of Christ teaches that holiness is found not in extraordinary experiences but in the ordinary path of self-denial, obedience, and love. "There is therefore no holiness, if Thou O Lord, withdraw Thine hand" (051-001) — a frank acknowledgment that all holiness is participation in the holiness of God, not a human achievement.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis describes the pursuit of holiness as a journey inward and upward, one in which the disciple continually discovers his distance from the goal, yet presses forward.

### The Impossibility of Perfect Holiness in This Life

*The Imitation* is unsentimental about the limits of human sanctity: "No man is so perfect in holiness that he hath never sometimes temptations, and we cannot be altogether without them" (013-001). This realism is not discouragement but liberation — the disciple who expects to attain sinless perfection will either despair or delude himself. True holiness is compatible with ongoing struggle.

### Holiness Through the Cross

The distinctive mark of Kempis's teaching on holiness is its cruciform shape. Holiness is not found by escaping suffering but by embracing it in union with Christ: "In the Cross is the height of virtue and the perfection of holiness" (037-001). The cross purifies love of self-interest, mortifies concupiscence, and unites the soul to its crucified Lord. There is no other way: "If any man will come after me, let him deny himself, and take up his cross, and follow me" (Matthew 16:24).

### Growth in Holiness

Holiness admits of degrees and growth. The disciple "may progress in holiness" (018-001) through the diligent use of the means of grace: prayer, the sacraments (especially the Eucharist), meditation on Scripture, and the practice of virtue. Specially availing unto "improvement in holiness" (025-001) are firmness of purpose, custody of the heart, and perseverance through dryness. The goal is nothing less than "to become by holiness like unto the Son of God" (091-001).

## Biblical Foundation

The Old Testament establishes the call: "Ye shall be holy: for I the Lord your God am holy" (Leviticus 19:2). Saint Peter repeats it for the new covenant: "As he which hath called you is holy, so be ye holy in all manner of conversation" (1 Peter 1:15). Christ Himself sets the standard: "Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect" (Matthew 5:48). The Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3-12) outline the shape of holiness in the kingdom — poverty of spirit, purity of heart, mercy, peacemaking. The Pauline epistles develop the theology of progressive sanctification, while the Epistle to the Hebrews presents Christ as "the author and finisher of our faith" (Hebrews 12:2) in whom holiness has its source and its goal.
