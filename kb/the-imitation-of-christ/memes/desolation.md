# Desolation

The condition of felt abandonment in which the soul finds no sweetness in prayer, no light in meditation, and no comfort in any spiritual exercise — a [[temptation|trial]] which Thomas à Kempis treats not as a sign of God's displeasure but as His peculiar favour to His friends, the furnace in which love is purified of all self-seeking and the soul learns to seek the God of [[comfort|consolation]]s rather than the [[comfort|consolation]]s of God.

## Definition

Desolation, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is the experiential withdrawal of divine [[comfort|consolation]] — the condition in which "Thou withdraw Thyself as too often Thou art wont" (087-001) and the soul finds itself unable to pray with [[devotion|fervour]], to meditate with light, or to feel any sweetness in the things of God. It is not sin; it is not even necessarily a consequence of sin. It is a divinely ordained [[temptation|trial]], a pedagogy of purification. Thomas devotes Book III, Chapter 50 — "How a Desolate Man Ought to Commit Himself into the Hands of God" — to this state, and the theme recurs throughout the work, especially in the long dialogue sequences of Book III where [[jesus-christ|Christ]] explains the reasons for the withdrawal and return of [[comfort|consolation]].

## In The Imitation of Christ

### The Desolate Man's Prayer

Chapter 50 opens with a prayer that is at once a confession of desolation and an act of [[trust-in-god|trust]]. The desolate soul does not deny its condition but speaks it directly to God: "I am poor and in misery even from my youth up, and my soul is sorrowful unto tears, sometimes also it is dis[[silence|quiet]]ed within itself, because of the sufferings which are coming upon it" (087-001). The honesty is total: misery, sorrow, dis[[silence|quiet]] — nothing is hidden. Yet the prayer begins with blessing: "O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], Holy Father, be Thou blessed now and evermore; because as Thou wilt so it is done, and what Thou doest is good" (087-001). The desolate man blesses God not despite his condition but in the midst of it, precisely because God's will is operative even in the withdrawal.

This is the Kempian response to desolation in its purest form: not complaint, not despair, not even petition for relief, but surrender into the hands of the God who hides Himself. "Let Thy servant rejoice in Thee, not in himself, nor in any other; because Thou alone art the true joy" (087-001).

### The Return of Consolation and Its Withdrawal

The prayer acknowledges the rhythm of the spiritual life — the alternation between [[comfort|consolation]] and desolation that Thomas describes so precisely. "If Thou give peace, if Thou pour into me holy joy, the soul of Thy servant shall be full of melody, and devout in Thy praise. But if Thou withdraw Thyself as too often Thou art wont, he will not be able to run in the way of Thy [[law|commandments]], but rather he will smite his breast and will bow his knees; because it is not with him as yesterday and the day before, when Thy candle shined upon his head" (087-002).

The contrast is drawn with the psalmist's imagery: the candle shining upon the head ([[job|Job]] 29:3), walking under the shadow of God's wings (Psalm 17:8). Yesterday, all was light and shelter; today, darkness and exposure. The soul that remembers yesterday's [[comfort|consolation]] feels today's desolation all the more keenly. And yet Thomas does not interpret the withdrawal as punishment: it is simply "as too often Thou art wont" — God's customary way with souls.

### Desolation as Proof and Purification

The most surprising teaching of the chapter is that desolation is a sign of divine favour, not divine anger. "For this is Thy favour to Thy friend, that he should suffer and be troubled in [[world|the world]] for Thy love's sake, how often soever, and by whomsoever and whosoever Thou hast suffered it to be done" (087-004). Desolation is God's gift to His friends; the suffering it brings is a mark of love, not of rejection.

This is a complete reversal of natural reasoning. The soul naturally interprets felt abandonment as proof that God has abandoned it. Thomas insists the opposite: "Without Thy counsel and providence, and without cause, nothing cometh to pass on the earth" (087-004). The desolation is permitted by God's counsel for a specific purpose — and that purpose is always the soul's good.

### The Fruit of Desolation

Thomas is careful to specify what desolation produces in the soul. First, it teaches humility: "It is good for me, [[jesus-christ|Lord]], that I had been in trouble, that I may learn Thy statutes, and may cast away all pride of heart and [[pride|presumption]]" (087-004). [[consolation|Consolation]] can inflate; desolation humbles. The soul that in [[comfort|consolation]] thought itself advanced discovers in desolation how little it can do on its own.

Second, desolation redirects the soul's seeking from [[creation|creatures]] to the Creator: "It is profitable for me that confusion hath covered my face, that I may seek to Thee for [[comfort|consolation]] rather than unto men" (087-004). When men cannot help — when shame isolates the soul — it turns at last to the only true source of comfort.

Third, desolation prepares the soul for greater union: the desolate man is "for a little while... outwardly bow down, but always live inwardly with Thee; when for a little while he should be little regarded, humbled, and fail in the eyes of men; should be wasted with sufferings and weaknesses, to rise again with Thee in the dawn of the new light, and be glorified in the heavenly places" (087-003). The outward humiliation is temporary; the inward union is permanent. Desolation is the dark hour before dawn.
