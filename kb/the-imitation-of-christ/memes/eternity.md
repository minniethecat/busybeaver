# Eternity

The blessed day which the night cannot obscure and the Supreme [[truth|Truth]] ever enlightens — the ultimate horizon against which Thomas à Kempis measures every earthly joy, every suffering, and every decision — a reality so central to the Kempian vision that he devotes two consecutive chapters to it, teaching the soul to live now in the light of that day when God shall be all in all.

## Definition

Eternity, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not an abstract theological concept but the soul's true homeland, set in constant contrast with the "straitnesses of this life" — the few, evil days of pilgrimage filled with sorrows, [[sin|sins]], [[sin|passions]], fears, and cares. Thomas devotes Book III, Chapters 48 and 49 to this theme: the first a meditation on the nature of the eternal day as contrasted with present misery, the second a dialogue of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] with the soul on the desire for eternal life and the disciplines of waiting. Throughout the work, eternity functions as the ultimate horizon — it is the reason the soul can endure suffering (084-001), the measure by which all worldly goods are found wanting (001-031), and the end toward which every virtue tends. "God alone is eternal and incomprehensible, filling all things" (030-028); all else passes.

## In The Imitation of Christ

### The Day of Eternity and the Straitness of This Life

Book III, Chapter 48 ("Of the Day of Eternity and of the Straitnesses of This Life") is one of the most lyrical passages in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Thomas opens in rapture: "Oh most blessed mansion of the City which is above! Oh most clear day of eternity which the night obscureth not, but the Supreme [[truth|Truth]] ever enlighteneth! Day always joyful, always secure and never changing its state into those which are contrary" (085-001 to 085-003). The eternal day is characterized by three perfections: joy without interruption, security without threat, and immutability without change. Everything the present life is not, eternity is.

The present life, by contrast, is painted with devastating honesty. "The days of this life are few and evil, full of sorrows and straits, where man is defiled with many [[sin|sins]], ensnared with many [[sin|passions]], bound fast with many fears, wearied with many cares, distracted with many questionings, entangled with many [[vanity|vanities]], compassed about with many errors, worn away with many labours, weighed down with temptations, enervated by pleasures, tormented by poverty" (085-003). The list is exhaustive — twelve distinct miseries — and leaves no aspect of earthly existence untainted. This is not despair but realism: only by seeing the present life as it truly is can the soul rightly desire eternity.

### The Groaning of Exile

The chapter then breaks into a series of desperate "when" questions, among the most passionate in the entire work: "Oh when shall there be an end of these evils? When shall I be delivered from the wretched slavery of my [[sin|sins]]? When shall I be mindful, O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], of Thee alone? When shall I rejoice in Thee to the full? When shall I be in true liberty without any impediment, without any burden on mind or body? When shall there be solid peace, peace immovable and secure, peace within and without, peace firm on every side?" (085-003).

These are not rhetorical questions; they are the cry of the soul that has seen the vision of the eternal day and can no longer be content with anything less. The repetition of "when" (*quando* in the Latin) gives the passage the character of a litany of longing. The answer is not given because the question itself is the prayer — the soul stretched toward God in desire.

> Oh when shall there be an end of these evils? When shall I be delivered from the wretched slavery of my sins? When shall I be mindful, O Lord, of Thee alone? When shall I rejoice in Thee to the full? (085-003)

### The Interior War

Thomas describes the human condition as a civil war: "So, wretched man that I am, I fight with myself, and am made grievous even unto myself, while the spirit seeketh to be above and the flesh to be beneath" (085-004). The soul desires heaven but is weighed down by the body; the mind discourses on heavenly things but carnal thoughts rush in during prayer. This division is the very texture of the pilgrim state — not quite beast, not yet fully citizen of heaven.

The cry for deliverance is addressed to God alone: "My God, be not Thou far from me, nor depart in wrath from Thy servant. Cast forth Thy lightning and scatter them; send out Thine arrows, and let all delusions of my enemy be confounded" (085-005). The soul cannot deliver itself; only God's intervention can still the interior tumult.

### The Desire for Eternal Life

Chapter 49 shifts from meditation to dialogue: [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself speaks to the soul about its desire for eternal life. "My Son, when thou feelest the desire of eternal [[blessedness|happiness]] to be poured into thee from above, and longest to depart from the tabernacle of this body, that thou mayest contemplate My glory without shadow of turning, enlarge thine heart, and take in this holy inspiration with all thy desire" (086-001). The desire for eternity is itself a gift — "poured into thee from above" — not a human achievement but a divine visitation.

[[jesus-christ|Christ]] immediately warns against [[pride|presumption]]. The desire for heaven may be mixed with self-seeking: "often the fire burneth, but the flame ascendeth not without smoke. So also the desires of some men burn towards heavenly things, and yet they are not free from the temptation of carnal affection" (086-002). Even the holiest longings need purification. The soul must learn to desire heaven not as escape from suffering but as union with God for His own sake.

### The Season of Warfare

The most searching teaching in Chapter 49 is [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s delay of the soul's desire: "Already thou longest to be in the glorious liberty of the children of God; already the eternal home delighteth thee, and the heavenly country full of joy; but the hour is not yet come; there remaineth still another season, even a season of warfare, a season of labour and probation" (086-003). The desire is real and good, but fulfillment is deferred. "Thou desirest to be filled with the Chief Good, but thou canst not attain it immediately. I AM that Good; wait for Me, until the [[kingdom-of-god|Kingdom of God]] shall come" (086-003).

This is the Kempian doctrine of patience in its most exalted form: not patience with petty inconveniences but patience with the very delay of [[blessedness|beatitude]]. The soul must be "tried upon earth, and exercised in many things" (086-004), receiving [[comfort|consolation]]s from time to time but never the full satisfaction it craves, until it has been sufficiently purified.

### Eternity as Motivation for Endurance

Throughout the work, eternity is presented as the reason to endure present sufferings. "All that we suffer in this world is little, if we compare it with the glory that is to come" (084-001). The sufferings of time, seen in the light of eternity, shrink to nothing. Thomas makes the practical calculation explicit: "For a little while of labour, eternal rest is given; for a short season of abstinence, eternal satisfaction; for a passing shame, eternal glory" (084-001 to 084-012).

This is not escapism but a radical revaluation. Eternity does not make the present life meaningless; it gives it meaning by anchoring it in the only thing that endures. "Seek those things that are eternal" (038-017) is both counsel and command — the practical program of the entire spiritual life.
