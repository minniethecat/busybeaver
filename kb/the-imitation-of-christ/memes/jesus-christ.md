# Jesus Christ

The incarnate Son of God, Saviour of the world, and the supreme model for Christian discipleship — whose life of humility, suffering, and obedience provides the pattern that Thomas à Kempis calls every believer to imitate.

## Identity

Jesus Christ is the second Person of the Holy Trinity, the Word made flesh, who "was despised and rejected of men" (026-043) and through His passion and death redeemed humanity. In The Imitation of Christ, He is addressed under many titles: Lord, Saviour, Beloved, Bridegroom, and Master. He is both the divine Teacher whose words open the work — "These are the words of Christ; and they teach us how far we must imitate His life" (001-004) — and the intimate companion of the soul, "for Jesus will be loved alone above all things" (032-004).

In the Kempian framework, Christ is not a distant historical figure but the living Lord who speaks inwardly to the faithful disciple. The whole work is structured around this dual presence: Christ as the external pattern to be imitated (Book I–II) and Christ as the inward voice that instructs and consoles (Book III). "Christ will come to thee, and show thee His consolation, if thou prepare a worthy mansion for Him within thee" (026-007).

## Biblical Narrative

The New Testament presents Jesus as the long-awaited Messiah of Israel, conceived by the Holy Spirit and born of the Virgin Mary. His public ministry — teaching, healing, calling disciples — unfolds across the four Gospels. He proclaims the kingdom of God, delivers the Sermon on the Mount, and gathers a company of followers whom He calls to take up their cross and follow Him.

### Key Events

- **Incarnation** — The eternal Word becomes flesh, born in humility at Bethlehem. The mystery of the Incarnation is the ground of all Christian hope, for in Christ "the mystery of Christ's incarnation and His Passion" (106-064) reveals the depth of divine love.
- **Baptism and Temptation** — Jesus is baptized by John in the Jordan and then driven into the wilderness, where He overcomes the tempter. This establishes the pattern that divine consolation and trial alternate in the spiritual life.
- **Public Ministry** — Teaching, healing, and gathering disciples, Christ reveals the Father and calls all to repentance and faith. He teaches that "the kingdom of God is within you" (026-002) and that His followers must be poor in spirit, meek, and pure of heart.
- **The Passion** — Betrayed, condemned, scourged, and crucified, Christ "was willing to suffer and be despised" (026-045). The cross is the climax of His earthly life and the supreme revelation of divine love: "He who loveth Jesus, and is inwardly true and free from inordinate affections, is able to turn himself readily unto God" (026-056).
- **Resurrection** — Christ rises from the dead, conquering sin and death. As the text reminds us, it was necessary for "Christ to suffer and to rise from the dead" (037-057), and His resurrection seals the promise that those who suffer with Him will also reign with Him.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis makes Jesus Christ the axis around which the entire spiritual life revolves. From the opening sentence — "Of the imitation of Christ, and of contempt of the world and all its vanities" (001-001) — the work establishes that the Christian life consists in conforming oneself to Christ, both outwardly in conduct and inwardly in disposition.

### Christ as the Pattern for Imitation

Book I grounds the spiritual life in the concrete example of Christ's earthly life. The disciple is urged to "dwell upon the life of Jesus Christ" (001-007) because "they who follow their own sensuality, defile their conscience and lose the grace of God, because they have not the mind of Christ" (001-011). The goal is nothing less than to "endeavour that his whole life to that mind of Christ" (001-013). Christ's humility, obedience, and patient endurance of suffering become the measure against which all spiritual progress is judged.

### Christ as the Inward Teacher

In Book II, the focus shifts from external imitation to inward union. The kingdom of God is within, and "Christ will come to thee, and show thee His consolation, if thou prepare a worthy mansion for Him within thee" (026-007). The disciple is instructed to "give, therefore, place to Christ and refuse entrance to all others. When thou hast Christ, thou art rich, and hast sufficient" (026-017). This inward presence is not an abstract theological concept but a living reality: Christ speaks, consoles, and guides the soul from within.

### Christ as the Beloved

Book II, Chapter VII — "Of loving Jesus above all things" — presents Christ as the supreme object of the soul's affection. The love of Jesus surpasses all created loves: "The love of created things is deceiving and unstable, but the love of Jesus is faithful and lasting" (032-005). The disciple must "cleave thou to Jesus in life and death, and commit thyself unto His faithfulness" (032-012). This language of intimate devotion culminates in Book II, Chapter VIII, where Thomas writes: "To be without Jesus is the nethermost hell, and to be with Jesus is sweet paradise" (033-013). The resolution is absolute: "Let all be loved for Jesus' sake, but Jesus for His own" (033-030).

### Christ as the Crucified Lord

The cross stands at the center of the Kempian vision of Christ. Jesus has "many lovers of His heavenly kingdom, but few bearers of His cross" (036-002). The whole life of Christ is presented as the pattern of redemptive suffering: "The whole life of Christ was a cross and martyrdom, and dost thou seek for thyself rest and joy?" (037-060). The disciple who would follow Christ must learn that suffering is the path to glory: "sustain thyself with Christ and for Christ if thou wilt reign with Christ" (026-050). Far from being merely endured, the cross becomes something to be loved — "He who loveth Jesus, and is inwardly true and free from inordinate affections, is able to turn himself readily unto God" (026-056).

### Christ in the Sacrament

In Book IV, Christ is present in the Eucharist, the "bread of angels" by which the soul is nourished unto eternal life. The disciple approaches the altar with reverence and longing, aware that in the Sacrament "the Word of God is the light of the soul" (107-050). The devout reception of Christ in the Eucharist completes the arc of imitation: what was external pattern becomes inward presence, and what was inward presence becomes sacramental union.

> "Sustain thyself with Christ and for Christ if thou wilt reign with Christ." (026-050)

> "To be without Jesus is the nethermost hell, and to be with Jesus is sweet paradise." (033-013)

## Spiritual Significance

In the Christian devotional tradition as mediated by Thomas à Kempis, Jesus Christ is not merely the object of belief but the comprehensive pattern of the spiritual life. Every virtue the disciple seeks to acquire — humility, obedience, patience, charity — is first seen perfected in Christ. Every trial the disciple endures is understood as a participation in the cross of Christ. Every consolation the disciple receives is a foretaste of union with Christ.

Christ's example redefines greatness: His humility overturns pride, His obedience reverses Adam's disobedience, His suffering transforms the meaning of pain, and His resurrection guarantees the hope of eternal life. The Imitation of Christ presents a Christ who is at once transcendent Lord and intimate friend, the Judge before whom all must appear (037-009) and the Beloved who "standeth by us firmly even to the end" (026-021). This paradoxical union of majesty and intimacy is the heart of Kempian spirituality: "He who findeth Jesus findeth a good treasure, yea, good above all good" (033-014).
