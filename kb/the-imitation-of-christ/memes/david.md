# David

David, the shepherd-king of Israel, psalmist, and man after God's own heart (1 Samuel 13:14) — in The Imitation of Christ he appears as the supreme Old Testament example of fervent, whole-hearted devotion. Thomas à Kempis invokes him in Book IV to shame the disciple's meager preparation for Holy Communion.

## Identity

David is the central figure of the books of Samuel and the traditional author of the Psalter. Anointed by Samuel in his youth (1 Samuel 16:1–13), he rose from shepherd to giant-slayer to king. He united the tribes of Israel, established Jerusalem as his capital, and brought the Ark of the Covenant to the city with extravagant rejoicing (2 Samuel 6:14–16). Though flawed — his sins of adultery and murder are recorded without flattery (2 Samuel 11) — his profound repentance (Psalm 51) made him the model of the contrite heart in both Jewish and Christian tradition.

## In The Imitation of Christ

David appears in Book IV, Chapter 1 ("With How Great Reverence Christ Must Be Received"), where Thomas places him in a gallery of Old Testament saints — Noah, Moses, Solomon, David — whose devotion exposes the disciple's spiritual poverty. The passage describes how "the most devout king David danced with all his might before the Ark of God" (097-064), recalling God's past mercies. David did not merely dance; he "fashioned musical instruments of various sorts, put forth Psalms, and appointed them to be sung with joy, played also himself ofttimes on the harp, being inspired with the grace of the Holy Ghost" (097-066–068). He "taught the people of Israel to praise God with the whole heart, and with unity of voice to bless and praise Him every day" (097-069).

> "The most devout king David danced with all his might before the Ark of God, calling to mind the benefits granted to his forefathers in days past; he fashioned musical instruments of various sorts, put forth Psalms, and appointed them to be sung with joy." (097-064–067)

Thomas then draws the humiliating conclusion: "If so great devotion was then exercised... before the Ark of the Testimony, how great reverence and devotion ought now to be shown by me and all Christian people at the ministering of the Sacrament, at receiving the most precious Body and Blood of Christ" (097-070–075). David's ark contained stone tablets; the Christian receives Christ Himself. The disproportion is deliberate and devastating.

## Spiritual Significance

David embodies the fervent, embodied devotion that The Imitation relentlessly commends. His dancing is not decorous — "with all his might" (097-064) — and his musical leadership is neither passive nor restrained. He is the antithesis of the lukewarm soul who "scarce know how to spend half an hour in devotion" (097-044). By placing David alongside Noah, Moses, and Solomon among those whose "great affection of devotion" (097-063) puts the disciple to shame, Thomas makes David a permanent goad to spiritual fervor.
