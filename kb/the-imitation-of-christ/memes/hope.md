# Hope

The theological virtue of confident expectation in God's promises and mercies — in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], hope is rigorously theocentric: it is placed "in God alone" (007-005), never in [[creation|creatures]], and is purified through the alternation of [[comfort|consolation]] and desolation. "It is thy duty to have a good hope that thou wilt attain the victory, but thou must not fall into security lest thou become slothful or lifted up" (025-010). Hope occupies the territory between [[pride|presumption]] and despair, sustained by the memory of God's past mercies and the promise of His future [[faith|faithfulness]].

## Definition

Hope, as Thomas à Kempis presents it, is the soul's confident orientation toward God as its final good. Unlike the secular concept of hope as optimistic wishing, Christian hope is anchored in the character and promises of God. "Build thy hope in God" (007-005) — the imperative is clear and absolute. Hope placed in learning, powerful friends, riches, or one's own virtue is misplaced hope, inevitably disappointed. The theological structure of hope in The Imitation involves three elements: the recognition of one's own poverty and inability, the experience of [[mercy|God's mercy]] in the past, and the confident expectation that God will continue to be merciful in the future. "Whereupon then can I hope, or wherein may I [[trust-in-god|trust]], save only in the great [[forgiveness|mercy]] of God, and the hope of heavenly grace?" (034-055 to 034-056). Hope and [[forgiveness|mercy]] are inseparable — hope feeds on the memory of [[forgiveness|mercy]] received and the anticipation of [[forgiveness|mercy]] yet to come.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Hope appears throughout The Imitation as the virtue that sustains the [[discipleship|disciple]] through the long stretches of spiritual [[desolation|dryness]] and [[suffering|adversity]] that characterise the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life. Thomas treats hope not as a static possession but as a dynamic disposition that must be exercised, tested, and purified.

### Hope in God Alone

The foundational teaching on hope appears in Book I, where Thomas warns against "fleeing from vain hope and pride" (007-001). Vain hope is hope misplaced — hope in one's own learning, in powerful patrons, in riches or reputation. "Rest not upon thyself, but build thy hope in God. Do what lieth in thy power, and God will help thy good intent" (007-005). The positive content of this teaching is that hope in God is never vain; God "resisteth the proud and giveth grace to the humble" (007-005). The one who hopes in God does what lies within his power and leaves the outcome to [[providence|divine providence]], neither presuming upon success nor despairing at failure. This teaching is reinforced throughout The Imitation: the [[discipleship|disciple]] is called to "build his whole hope upon God" (020-035), recognising that no human [[comfort|consolation]] can substitute for the divine.

### Hope Tested by Adversity

Hope is not genuinely Christian hope until it has been tested. Thomas observes that many appear virtuous "so long as he suffereth no [[suffering|affliction]]; but if he behave himself patiently in the time of [[suffering|adversity]], then is there hope of great progress" (013-065). Adversity is the crucible in which authentic hope is forged. The soul that hopes in God only when things go well has not yet learned hope; the soul that continues to hope when [[comfort|consolation]] is withdrawn has begun to acquire the virtue. The experience of being "tossed about between hope and fear" (025-014) is characteristic of the spiritual life — the [[discipleship|disciple]] is never permitted the security of certain knowledge but must walk by faith, sustained by hope.

### The Prayer of Hope

In Book III, hope finds its fullest expression in prayer. The soul in desolation cries out: "Whereupon then can I hope, or wherein may I [[trust-in-god|trust]], save only in the great [[forgiveness|mercy]] of God, and the hope of heavenly grace?" (034-055 to 034-056). This is not a rhetorical question but the authentic cry of a soul that has exhausted all creaturely resources and turns to God as its sole refuge. Hope is here indistinguishable from prayer — the act of hoping is the act of turning toward God and confessing one's total dependence upon Him. The response to this prayer is not always immediate [[comfort|consolation]]; often the answer is simply the grace to continue hoping. "There is no better remedy, then, than patience and [[self-denial|denial of self]], and an abiding in the will of God" (034-057).

### Hope and the Active Life

Thomas does not permit hope to degenerate into passive waiting. "Hope in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] and be doing good, saith the Prophet; dwell in the land and thou shalt be fed with its riches" (025-014). The quotation from Psalm 37:3 links hope inseparably to active obedience. The one who hopes in God does not sit idle but "does good" — performs the duties of his state, practices [[love|charity]], resists temptation — while en[[trust-in-god|trust]]ing the outcome to God. This integration of hope and action distinguishes Christian hope from both the [[pride|presumption]] that expects reward without effort and the despair that ceases to strive.

## Key Passages

> "Rest not upon thyself, but build thy hope in God. Do what lieth in thy power, and God will help thy good intent. Trust not in thy learning, nor in the cleverness of any that lives, but rather trust in the favour of God, who resisteth the proud and giveth grace to the humble." (007-005 to 007-006)

> "If thou shalt remain faithful and zealous in labour, doubt not that God shall be faithful and bountiful in rewarding thee. It is thy duty to have a good hope that thou wilt attain the victory, but thou must not fall into security lest thou become slothful or lifted up." (025-009 to 025-010)

> "Whereupon then can I hope, or wherein may I trust, save only in the great mercy of God, and the hope of heavenly grace? For whether good men are with me, godly brethren or faithful friends, whether holy books or beautiful discourses, whether sweet hymns and songs, all these help but little, and have but little savour when I am deserted by God's favour and left to mine own poverty." (034-055 to 034-057)

> "Hope in the Lord and be doing good, saith the Prophet; dwell in the land and thou shalt be fed with its riches. One thing there is which holdeth back many from progress and fervent amendment, even the dread of difficulty, or the labour of the conflict." (025-014)

## Biblical Foundation

The Kempian doctrine of hope is deeply rooted in the Psalter and the prophetic literature, with significant Pauline resonances. The central imperative — "Hope in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] and be doing good" (025-014) — is a direct citation of Psalm 37:3: "[[trust|Trust]] in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], and do good; so shalt thou dwell in the land, and verily thou shalt be fed." This psalm provides the template for Thomas's integration of hope and active obedience. The confession that hope rests "only in the great [[forgiveness|mercy]] of God" (034-056) echoes Lamentations 3:22–24: "It is of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]'s mercies that we are not consumed... The [[jesus-christ|Lord]] is my portion, saith my soul; therefore will I hope in him."

The New Testament background includes Romans 8:24–25 — "We are saved by hope: but hope that is seen is not hope" — which establishes the eschatological character of Christian hope; it is oriented toward what is not yet visible. Hebrews 6:19 describes hope as "an anchor of the soul, both sure and stedfast," an image consonant with Thomas's teaching that hope stabilises the soul amid the storms of temptation and desolation. 1 Peter 1:3 speaks of "a lively hope by the resurrection of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] from the dead," grounding hope not in human effort but in the objective event of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s victory over death — the ultimate ground of all [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian confidence.
