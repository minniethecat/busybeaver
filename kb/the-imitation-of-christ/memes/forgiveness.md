# Forgiveness

**Forgiveness** (*remissio*, *indulgentia*) is a central theme in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]*, appearing both as the [[mercy|divine mercy]] that pardons the sinner and as the human duty to forgive others. Thomas à Kempis treats forgiveness not as an optional virtue but as the indispensable condition of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life: the one who would receive mercy must extend it, and the one who withholds forgiveness from others cuts himself off from the forgiveness of God.

## The Cry for Pardon

The text gives voice to the sinner's plea for divine forgiveness in language of profound humility. The soul cries out:

> I have sinned; have mercy upon me, forgive me. Let me alone, that I may bewail my sorrow a little while, before I go to the dark land covered with the shadow of death [089-029]

This prayer — drawn from the tradition of the penitential psalms and the Book of [[job|Job]] — captures the urgency of the request for pardon. Forgiveness is not a theological abstraction but the sinner's desperate need, sought before the door of mercy closes.

In another passage, Kempis turns the plea for forgiveness into a liturgical invocation: "Forgive, O God, forgive me my [[sin|sins]] for Thy holy Name's sake" (105-026). The appeal to God's Name — His revealed character of mercy — grounds the request not in human merit but in [[goodness|divine goodness]].

## Forgiving Others as the Condition of Being Forgiven

The most distinctive emphasis in the text's treatment of forgiveness is its insistence on reciprocity. The soul that seeks God's pardon must first extend pardon to others:

> Forgive freely all offenses committed against thee, and God shall freely forgive thee [106-029]

This is an almost verbatim echo of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]'s [[prayer|Prayer]] and its commentary in the Gospels: "If ye forgive men their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also forgive you" (Matthew 6:14). Kempis makes no concession to the difficulty of forgiving — the command is absolute.

The practical counsel in the text includes the exhortation to "hasten to confess" one's own [[sin|sins]] and simultaneously to "forgive freely all offenses committed" against oneself (106-027). [[confession|Confession]] and forgiveness are two movements of the same act: acknowledging one's own debt while canceling the debts of others.

The figure of the righteous man who "despitefully use him, and forgives them from his heart" (024-016) provides the model. Forgiving "from the heart" means forgiveness is not merely a verbal formula — it must be an interior act, a genuine release of resentment and the desire for retaliation.

## Forgiveness and the Vanity of Creatures

In a striking connection, Kempis links forgiveness with the renunciation of worldly attachments: "Forgiven. Leave vain things to vain men, and attend thou to those things which God commandeth thee" (020-068). The forgiven soul is freed from the entanglement of vanity — having received divine pardon, it no longer needs to grasp at created satisfactions or nurse grievances. Forgiveness is liberation not only from guilt but from the whole weight of worldly concern.

## Forgiveness in the Context of the Eucharist

The approach to [[communion|Holy Communion]] intensifies the duty of forgiveness. The penitent who comes to the altar harboring resentment against another receives the Sacrament unworthily. The text's treatment of preparation for [[communion|Communion]] includes a searching examination of one's relationships: are there any against whom one holds a grudge? Is there anyone from whom one has withheld pardon?

The peace of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] received in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] is incompatible with the refusal to be at peace with one's neighbor. The forgiven must be forgivers — this is the sacramental logic that runs through Book IV.

## Biblical Foundation

The teaching on forgiveness is grounded in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]'s [[prayer|Prayer]] itself: "Forgive us our trespasses, as we forgive those who trespass against us" (Matthew 6:12). The parable of the unforgiving servant (Matthew 18:21–35) provides the narrative warrant: the servant who, having been forgiven an enormous debt, refuses to forgive a small one, is delivered to the tormentors. Kempis's stark formulation — forgive, or you will not be forgiven — is nothing other than the plain sense of the Gospel.
