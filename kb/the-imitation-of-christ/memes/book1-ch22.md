[022-001] On the contemplation of human misery

[022-002] Thou art miserable wheresoever thou art, and whithersoever thou

[022-003] turnest, unless thou turn thee to God. Why art thou disquieted because

[022-004] it happeneth not to thee according to thy wishes and desires? Who is he

[022-005] that hath everything according to his will? Neither I, nor thou, nor

[022-006] any man upon the earth. There is no man in [[world|the world]] free from trouble

[022-007] or anguish, though he were King or Pope. Who is he who hath the

[022-008] happiest lot? Even he who is strong to suffer somewhat for God.

[022-009] 2. There are many foolish and unstable men who say, “See what a

[022-010] prosperous life that man hath, how rich and how great he is, how

[022-011] powerful, how exalted.” But lift up thine eyes to the good things of

[022-012] heaven, and thou shalt see that all these worldly things are nothing,

[022-013] they are utterly uncertain, yea, they are wearisome, because they are

[022-014] never possessed without care and fear. The [[blessedness|happiness]] of man lieth not

[022-015] in the abundance of temporal things but a moderate portion sufficeth

[022-016] him. Our life upon the earth is verily wretchedness. The more a man

[022-017] desireth to be spiritual, the more bitter doth the present life become

[022-018] to him; because he the better understandeth and seeth the defects of

[022-019] human corruption. For to eat, to drink, to watch, to sleep, to rest, to

[022-020] labour, and to be subject to the other necessities of nature, is truly

[022-021] a great wretchedness and [[suffering|affliction]] to a devout man, who would fain be

[022-022] released and free from all sin.

[022-023] 3. For the inner man is heavily burdened with the necessities of the

[022-024] body in this world. Wherefore the prophet devoutly prayeth to be freed

[022-025] from them, saying, Deliver me from my necessities, O [[jesus-christ|Lord]].(1) But woe

[022-026] to those who know not their own misery, and yet greater woe to those

[022-027] who love this miserable and corruptible life. For to such a degree do

[022-028] some cling to it (even though by labouring or begging they scarce

[022-029] procure what is necessary for subsistence) that if they might live here

[022-030] always, they would care nothing for the [[kingdom-of-god|Kingdom of God]].

[022-031] 4. Oh foolish and faithless of heart, who lie buried so deep in worldly

[022-032] things, that they relish nothing save the things of the flesh!

[022-033] Miserable ones! they will too sadly find out at the last, how vile and

[022-034] worthless was that which they loved. The saints of God and all loyal

[022-035] friends of Christ held as nothing the things which pleased the flesh,

[022-036] or those which flourished in this life, but their whole hope and

[022-037] affection aspired to the things which are above. Their whole desire was

[022-038] borne upwards to everlasting and invisible things, lest they should be

[022-039] drawn downwards by the love of things visible.

[022-040] 5. Lose not, brother, thy loyal desire of progress to things spiritual.

[022-041] There is yet time, the hour is not past. Why wilt thou put off thy

[022-042] resolution? Arise, begin this very moment, and say, “Now is the time to

[022-043] do: now is the time to fight, now is the proper time for amendment.”

[022-044] When thou art ill at ease and troubled, then is the time when thou art

[022-045] nearest unto blessing. Thou must go through fire and water that God may

[022-046] bring thee into a wealthy place. Unless thou put force upon thyself,

[022-047] thou wilt not conquer thy faults. So long as we carry about with us

[022-048] this frail body, we cannot be without sin, we cannot live without

[022-049] weariness and trouble. Gladly would we have rest from all misery; but

[022-050] because through sin we have lost innocence, we have lost also the true

[022-051] happiness. Therefore must we be patient, and wait for the [[forgiveness|mercy]] of God,

[022-052] until this tyranny be overpast, and this mortality be swallowed up of

[022-053] life.

[022-054] 6. O how great is the frailty of man, which is ever prone to evil!

[022-055] To-day thou confessest thy [[sin|sins]], and to-morrow thou committest again

[022-056] the sins thou didst confess. Now dost thou resolve to avoid a fault,

[022-057] and within an hour thou behavest thyself as if thou hadst never

[022-058] resolved at all. Good cause have we therefore to humble ourselves, and

[022-059] never to think highly of ourselves, seeing that we are so frail and

[022-060] unstable. And quickly may that be lost by our negligence, which by much

[022-061] labour was hardly attained through grace.

[022-062] 7. What shall become of us at the end, if at the beginning we are

[022-063] lukewarm and idle? Woe unto us, if we choose to rest, as though it were

[022-064] a time of peace and security, while as yet no sign appeareth in our

[022-065] life of true holiness. Rather had we need that we might begin yet

[022-066] afresh, like good novices, to be instructed unto good living, if haply

[022-067] there might be hope of some future amendment and greater spiritual

[022-068] increase.

[022-069] (1) Psalm xxv. 17.
