# Self-Examination

Self-examination is a foundational ascetical discipline in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Thomas à Kempis repeatedly counsels the devout soul to scrutinize its conscience, to "diligently examine" (103-006) the state of the heart, and to prepare for communion through honest self-assessment. The practice is not morbid introspection but a necessary means of spiritual growth: one cannot amend what one refuses to see.

## Definition

Self-examination is the intentional practice of reviewing one's interior and exterior life——thoughts, desires, words, actions, and omissions——against the standard of divine truth. It differs from mere self-awareness in being explicitly oriented toward repentance and amendment: the purpose is not to catalog [[sin|fault]]s but to root them out. The Imitation presents self-examination as a regular discipline tied especially to preparation for the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] and to the nightly review of the day's conduct.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation treats self-examination under several related themes: the [[conscience|examination of conscience]] before communion, the governance of the heart's desires, and the general counsel to "straitly examine" one's life (019-028).

The most direct counsel appears in Book IV's preparation for the [[eucharist|Eucharist]]. The communicant is instructed: "Diligently examine thy conscience, and with all thy might strive to purify and enlighten it by true contrition and [[confession|humble confession]]" (103-006). The examination is not optional but essential——the soul must know its state before approaching the altar. This echoes the Pauline warning that "whosoever shall eat this bread and drink this cup of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] unworthily shall be guilty of the body and blood of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" with the corollary: "let a man examine himself" (1 Corinthians 11:27–28).

Book III extends the practice beyond eucharistic preparation to the governance of interior life generally. The chapter on the desires of the heart opens by observing that "the desires of the heart are to be examined and governed" (048-001), implying that unexamined desires——whether for comfort, honor, or merely one's own way——become tyrants. The [[conscience|examination of conscience]] is thus a means of spiritual freedom: what is brought into the light of honest scrutiny can be surrendered to God.

### Key Passages

> "Diligently examine thy conscience, and with all thy might strive to purify and enlighten it by true contrition and humble confession" (103-006)

> "The desires of the heart are to be examined and governed" (048-001)

> "Our whole life should be straitly examined and ruled by us" (019-028)

## Biblical Foundation

The practice of self-examination has deep biblical roots. The Psalmist's prayer——"Search me, O God, and know my heart: try me, and know my thoughts" (Psalm 139:23)——models the invitation of divine scrutiny as the deepest form of self-examination. The wisdom tradition counsels that "the righteous man wisely considereth the house of the wicked" (Proverbs 21:12) and urges the examination of one's own path. Jeremiah's lament——"let us search and try our ways, and turn again to the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Lamentations 3:40)——directly links self-examination to repentance.

In the New Testament, Paul's eucharistic instruction is the most direct warrant: "let a man examine himself, and so let him eat of that bread and drink of that cup" (1 Corinthians 11:28). The same apostle's counsel to "examine yourselves, whether ye be in the faith; prove your own selves" (2 Corinthians 13:5) extends the practice to the whole of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life. The parable of the Pharisee and the publican (Luke 18:9–14) illustrates both false and true self-examination: the Pharisee examines himself and finds only virtue; the publican examines himself and finds only need of [[forgiveness|mercy]]——and it is the latter who goes home justified.

## Practical Application

The Imitation commends self-examination as a daily practice, not an occasional crisis response. The traditional "examen" involves two moments: the evening review of the day (what went well, what went wrong, what calls for repentance) and the morning anticipation (what temptations or opportunities likely lie ahead). Thomas à Kempis does not prescribe a detailed method but gives the essential principle: be honest, be thorough, and let the examination lead not to despair but to contrition and amendment. The test of genuine self-examination is not how much sin is discovered but how sincerely the discovered sin is brought to God for healing.
