# Fear of the Lord

The reverential awe and filial dread that is the "beginning of wisdom" (Proverbs 9:10) — in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], an indispensable disposition for spiritual progress that progresses from servile fear of punishment to chaste reverence grounded in love and humility.

## Definition

Fear of the Lord (*timor Domini*) in the Kempian vision is not the cringing dread of a slave before a punitive master, but the reverential awe of a child before a beloved Father whose grace, once received, must not be lost. It is both the beginning and the safeguard of the spiritual life. Thomas à Kempis draws upon the full scriptural and patristic tradition, distinguishing implicitly between a servile fear that dreads punishment and a filial, chaste fear that trembles not at hell but at the possibility of being separated from God. While the text never uses the scholastic terms "servile" and "filial," the [[discernment|distinction]] is unmistakable throughout: the work moves the [[discipleship|disciple]] from the fear that restrains sin (024-078) to the "chaste fear of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], which feareth to lose the grace offered" (044-040).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Fear of the Lord pervades every book of The Imitation. It appears in the very first chapter among the counsels of the spiritual life and culminates in Book IV as the reverent disposition with which the Blessed Sacrament must be approached. Thomas presents holy fear not as a passing emotion but as a permanent quality of the soul — the atmosphere in which all other virtues flourish.

### Filial Fear and Servile Fear

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] presents a spectrum of fear that moves from the elementary to the perfect. At the lower end stands servile fear — the dread of hell and judgment that restrains the sinner when love is yet too weak: "Nevertheless it is a good thing, if love as yet cannot restrain thee from evil, that at least the fear of hell should hold thee back" (024-078). This fear, though imperfect, is never despised in the text; it is a divinely given curb on sin for those still bound by carnal affections. Yet it is not the goal. The work warns that "he who putteth aside the fear of God cannot long continue in good, but shall quickly fall into the snares of the devil" (024-079), making clear that some fear — even imperfect fear — is better than none at all.

At the higher end is filial, chaste fear — the reverential awe of a soul that loves God and trembles at the thought of losing the grace it has received. Book III, Chapter VII speaks of "the chaste fear of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], which feareth to lose the grace offered" (044-040). This is the fear of the saints: "The boldness of the Saints was always full of the fear of God. Nor were they the less earnest and humble in themselves, because they shone forth with great virtues and grace" (020-021). The saint is bold because he [[trust-in-god|trust]]s God, yet his boldness is inseparable from holy fear — the two are not contradictory but complementary.

### Fear, Humility, and Love

Holy fear and humility are inseparable companions in the Kempian vision. Fear of the Lord continually reminds the soul of its utter dependence on God, and thereby produces and preserves humility. Book I opens the practical teaching with "knowledge without the fear of God?" — contrasting the proud philosopher with "a lowly peasant who serveth God" (002-003). Fear is not opposed to knowledge; it is what makes knowledge profitable and safe. Without fear, even learning becomes a snare, for "be not lifted up by any skill or knowledge that thou hast; but rather fear concerning the knowledge which is given to thee" (002-020).

[[humility|Humility]] is both the fruit of holy fear and its guardian. When the soul knows itself to be capable of falling, it clings to God with reverent dread: "O how humbly and abjectly must I reckon of myself. O how profoundly ought I to submit myself to Thy unfathomable judgments" (051-019). The [[discipleship|disciple]] who "feareth God" finds God necessary in every [[suffering|affliction]]: "When a man who feareth God is afflicted or tried or oppressed with evil thoughts, then he seeth that God is the more necessary unto him" (012-011).

Thomas à Kempis presents fear not as the enemy of love but as its tutor and safeguard. Fear initiates the spiritual life; love perfects it. Book I, Chapter XXIV presents this progression with clarity: "he who loveth God with all his heart feareth not death, nor punishment, nor judgment, nor hell, because perfect love giveth sure access to God" (024-074). This is an unmistakable echo of 1 John 4:18 — "perfect love casteth out fear, because fear hath torment." Yet even this verse was understood in medieval exegesis as referring to servile fear, not to filial fear. Thomas follows this tradition: the fear that love casts out is the servile terror of the slave; the chaste, filial fear of the child endures even in [[charity|perfect charity]], for it is itself an expression of love. In the [[nature|Nature]]-[[grace|Grace]] dialectic of Book III, Chapter LIV, the contrast reveals what each disposition fears: "[[nature|Nature]] feareth confusion and contempt, but [[grace|Grace]] rejoiceth to suffer shame for the name of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]" (091-025). [[nature|Nature]] fears what [[world|the world]] thinks; grace fears only what offends God. The transformation of fear — from worldly to holy — is itself a work of grace.

### Fear in the Spiritual Life

Fear of the Lord is not an optional accessory to the spiritual life in The Imitation; it is its very atmosphere. Book I, Chapter XXI — "Of [[compunction|Compunction]] of Heart" — opens with the programmatic declaration: "If thou wilt make any progress keep thyself in the fear of God" (021-002). Fear is presented as the condition of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian liberty, not its opposite: "There is no true liberty nor real joy, save in the fear of God with a [[conscience|good conscience]]" (021-012). The paradoxical truth is that only those who fear God are truly free, for fear of God liberates from every lesser fear.

This is most powerfully stated in the chapter on death: "Oh, dearly beloved, from what danger thou mightest free thyself, from what great fear, if only thou wouldst always live in fear, and in expectation of death! Strive now to live in such wise that in the hour of death thou mayest rather rejoice than fear" (023-050). Holy fear in life delivers from terror at death — the one who lives in reverent fear of God need not fear the judgment that awaits all.

The practical criterion of companionship captures this disposition: "Open not thine heart to every man, but deal with one who is wise and feareth God" (008-003). Fear of God is the mark of the spiritually wise, and the [[discipleship|disciple]] is counseled to keep company with such. Even earthly possessions are never secure: "they are never possessed without care and fear" (022-014), reminding the [[discipleship|disciple]] that only heavenly goods bring true peace. And in the sacramental life, the highest fear is required: "therefore with fear and reverence is this work to be approached" (101-017), for in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] the soul meets its God.

## Biblical Foundation

The [[reverence|fear of the Lord]] is one of the most pervasive themes in Scripture. The [[wisdom|Wisdom]] literature establishes it as the foundation of all wisdom: "The [[reverence|fear of the Lord]] is the beginning of wisdom" (Proverbs 9:10; Psalm 111:10). Proverbs declares that "the [[reverence|fear of the Lord]] is the beginning of knowledge: but fools despise wisdom and instruction" (Proverbs 1:7), while Sirach teaches that "the [[reverence|fear of the Lord]] is the beginning of wisdom: and it was created with the faithful in the womb" (Sirach 1:14). [[isaiah|Isaiah]] lists the [[reverence|fear of the Lord]] among the seven gifts of the [[holy-spirit|Holy Spirit]] resting upon the Messiah ([[isaiah|Isaiah]] 11:2-3).

The New Testament transfigures but does not abolish this fear. The early Church "walked in the [[reverence|fear of the Lord]], and in the comfort of the [[holy-spirit|Holy Ghost]]" (Acts 9:31), demonstrating that fear and [[comfort|consolation]] coexist. The Johannine teaching that "there is no fear in love; but perfect love casteth out fear: because fear hath torment" (1 John 4:18) was understood in medieval theology — and in The Imitation — as the casting out of servile fear alone, while chaste, filial fear endures eternally in the blessed. The Imitation weaves this full scriptural tradition into a coherent practical teaching: fear leads to wisdom, wisdom leads to humility, humility leads to love, and love brings the soul into the very presence of the God it once feared.
