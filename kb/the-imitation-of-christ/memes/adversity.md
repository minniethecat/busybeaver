# Adversity

The experience of hardship, suffering, and trial permitted by God for the proving and perfecting of the soul — in The Imitation of Christ, adversity is the crucible in which virtue is tested and refined, and through which the [[disciple]] grows in conformity to Christ.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, adversity encompasses the full range of human suffering — physical affliction, spiritual desolation, opposition from others, and the interior trials that accompany the pursuit of holiness. Thomas à Kempis treats adversity not as a sign of divine disfavor but as the necessary condition for spiritual growth: just as gold is proved in the furnace, so the soul is proved in the fire of tribulation.

Adversity stands in contrast to [[consolation]] — the experience of spiritual sweetness and divine closeness — and The Imitation teaches that the alternation between the two is the normal rhythm of the spiritual life. The soul that has known only consolation remains spiritually untested; it is through adversity that the substance of virtue is revealed.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation of Christ devotes an entire chapter to "the uses of adversity" (Book I, Chapter XII), and the theme of trial as divine medicine runs throughout the text.

### The Uses of Adversity

Book I, Chapter XII teaches that adversity serves multiple purposes in the spiritual life. It reveals what a person truly is: "in the time of adversity, then is there hope of great profit" (013-065), because adversity exposes the hidden roots of character that prosperity conceals. The person who seems devout in times of peace may prove frail in times of trial; conversely, one whose virtue seemed unremarkable may display heroic patience under affliction.

Adversity also purifies the soul: "for such occasions do not make a man frail, but show what he is" (016-029). This is a crucial distinction — adversity does not create weakness but reveals it, bringing to light what was already present in the soul. The humbling recognition of this weakness is itself a spiritual benefit.

### Adversity as Path to the Cross

Book II deepens the theme by linking adversity to conformity with Christ. The text asks: "how could patience attain her crown if no adversity had tried her?" (026-048). Without adversity, there is no occasion for the exercise of [[patience]], and without patience, there is no crown. The rhetorical question underscores the essential role of trial in the economy of salvation.

The Imitation also asks: "if thou art unwilling to suffer any adversity, how shalt thou be the friend of Christ?" (026-049). This is a sharp challenge: Christ Himself "endured the cross" (Hebrews 12:2), and those who would be His friends must walk the same path. The refusal of adversity is, in effect, the refusal of [[discipleship]].

### The Consolation of Affliction

Book III offers comfort to the soul in tribulation: "we are often wasted by affliction, and the more is the spirit strengthened within" (037-071). The paradox of the spiritual life is that outward wasting becomes inward strengthening. The text also speaks of "tribulation and adversity, through love of conformity to Him" (037-073), framing suffering not as meaningless pain but as participation in the mystery of the Cross.

## Biblical Foundation

The apostolic writings are rich in their treatment of adversity as divine pedagogy. Paul teaches that "tribulation produces perseverance; and perseverance, character; and character, hope" (Romans 5:3–4), a chain of spiritual formation that begins precisely with affliction. James exhorts his readers to "count it all joy when you fall into various trials, knowing that the testing of your faith produces patience" (James 1:2–4). Peter speaks of trials as the fire that proves the genuineness of faith, "more precious than gold that perishes" (1 Peter 1:6–7). And Paul's own experience becomes a paradigm: "My grace is sufficient for you, for My strength is made perfect in weakness" (2 Corinthians 12:9).

## Practical Application

The teaching of The Imitation on adversity calls the [[disciple]] to a radical reorientation: suffering is not meaningless misfortune but divine medicine, permitted by God's [[providence]] for the soul's healing and perfection. The practical response to adversity is neither stoic resignation nor bitter complaint, but the humble acknowledgment that one does not yet know the full extent of one's frailty (016-029) — and the simultaneous trust that "to those who love God, all things work together for good" (Romans 8:28).
