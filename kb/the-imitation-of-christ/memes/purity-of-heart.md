# Purity of Heart

Purity of heart is the goal of the Kempian spiritual program. Every ascetical practice, every act of self-denial, every [[conscience|examination of conscience]], and every worthy reception of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] converges on this single end: a heart so cleansed of sin and so unified in love that it becomes transparent to God. Without purity of heart, the Imitation teaches, the soul cannot see God, enter into intimate communion with Him, or receive [[jesus-christ|Christ]] worthily in the sacrament.

## Definition

Purity of heart is not merely freedom from sensual sin (though it includes this) but the positive condition of an undivided heart——a heart that loves God above all things and all things in God. It is both a gift of grace ("I am the Lover of purity, and Giver of sanctity," 108-004) and the fruit of sustained ascetical effort. Purity begins in the cleansing of conscience through contrition and confession; it matures in the progressive simplification of desire, as the soul learns to love nothing apart from God; and it is perfected in the beatific vision, where "the pure in heart shall see God" (Matthew 5:8).

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation addresses purity of heart in several interconnected passages. The most direct statement comes from [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself in the interior dialogue: "I am the Lover of purity, and Giver of sanctity. I seek a pure heart, and there is the place of My rest" (108-004). This declaration establishes purity as the condition for divine indwelling——God does not merely command purity; He seeks it as the place where He delights to dwell.

The language of purity pervades the eucharistic teaching of Book IV. The communicant is instructed to approach the sacrament "with such faith, hope, and purity" (113-020). The [[conscience|examination of conscience]] before communion aims at purity: "strive to purify and enlighten" the conscience through contrition and confession (103-006). A soul in a state of impurity is warned not to approach the altar lightly, for "no purity is secure, if Thou cease to preserve" it (051-013). This last remark is theologically significant: purity, however diligently pursued, is ultimately sustained by God, not by human effort alone.

The Imitation also connects purity to the simplicity that is a hallmark of Kempian spirituality. In one of the most striking passages, Thomas writes: "[[simplicity|Simplicity]] reacheth towards God, purity apprehendeth Him and tasteth Him" (029-004). [[simplicity|Simplicity]] and purity are paired virtues: simplicity strips away the unnecessary; purity makes the heart fit to receive God. Together they describe the soul's movement toward union.

### Key Passages

> "I am the Lover of purity, and Giver of sanctity. I seek a pure heart, and there is the place of My rest" (108-004)

> "Simplicity ought to be in the intention, purity in the affection. Simplicity reacheth towards God, purity apprehendeth Him and tasteth Him" (029-004)

> "If thou hadst angelic purity and the holiness of holy John the Baptist, thou wouldst not be worthy to receive or handle this Sacrament" (101-003)

## Biblical Foundation

Purity of heart has its most famous biblical expression in the Sixth Beatitude: "Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God" (Matthew 5:8). This single verse provides both the promise (vision of God) and the condition (purity of heart) that structures the Kempian treatment. The Old Testament preparation includes David's penitential cry——"Create in me a clean heart, O God" (Psalm 51:10)——and the wisdom tradition's observation that "who can say, I have made my heart clean, I am pure from my sin?" (Proverbs 20:9), which underscores that purity is finally a divine work.

The New Testament deepens the theme. James calls the faithful to "purify your hearts, ye double-minded" (James 4:8), linking purity to singleness of intention. Peter declares that God "purified their hearts by faith" (Acts 15:9). And the author of Hebrews insists that without holiness——which includes purity of heart——"no man shall see the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Hebrews 12:14). The Apocalypse completes the narrative: only those with clean hands and a pure heart shall ascend the hill of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] and stand in His holy place (Psalm 24:3–4, echoed in Revelation 21:27).

## Practical Application

The Imitation teaches that purity of heart is pursued through the ordinary means of grace: [[conscience|examination of conscience]], contrition, confession, worthy reception of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], and the daily discipline of self-denial. No single act secures it; it is the cumulative fruit of a life ordered toward God. The test of growing purity is simplicity of desire: wanting fewer things, and wanting what one wants more for God's sake than for one's own. The danger is discouragement——the sense that purity is an impossible ideal. Against this, The Imitation sets the promise that [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is both the Lover and the Giver of purity: what He commands, He also bestows on those who ask.
