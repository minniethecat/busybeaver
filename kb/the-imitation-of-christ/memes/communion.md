# Communion

The sacred reception of the Body and Blood of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — the summit of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life to which Thomas à Kempis devotes the entire fourth book of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — and, in its wider spiritual sense, the intimate union of the soul with its Creator in prayer, with the Church militant and triumphant, and with the faithful departed — taught not as a duty merely but as the soul's deepest longing and the medicine of its every infirmity.

## Definition

Communion, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], operates on two planes. The primary and central meaning is the sacramental reception of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s Body and Blood in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] — the subject of all eighteen chapters of Book IV, the longest book of the work. Here Thomas treats every aspect: preparation, worthy reception, the assaults of temptation before Communion, the fruits of frequent reception, and the grateful remembrance afterward. In a secondary but inseparable sense, communion also denotes the spiritual union of the soul with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in prayer and contemplation — "keep thyself in a secret place, and hold communion" (108-044) — and the bond of [[love|charity]] that unites the living with the departed in the one Body of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] (101-037). Both senses converge in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], which is at once the most intimate communion of the individual soul with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and the sacrament of the Church's unity.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Book IV — "A Devout Exhortation to the Holy Communion" — is the longest and most pastorally detailed section of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Thomas speaks throughout in the voice of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] addressing the soul, and the tone is at once urgent, tender, and searching. No other theme in the work receives such sustained and systematic treatment.

### The Divine Invitation

Thomas opens Book IV with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own voice issuing the invitation: "Come unto Me, all ye that labour and are heavy laden, and I will refresh you" (096-004). The Communion is not first a command but an invitation — the Physician calling the sick, the Bread of Life offered to the hungry. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] speaks as the Lover of souls: "Thou oughtest to come to the fountain of life and to drink, to the table of heaven and to be fed" (098-056).

The tone is intensely personal: "Behold, I come unto thee, saith the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], as if on this self-same day I were born for thee, and suffered for thee, and rose again for thee" (098-062). The communicant is not merely remembering past events; in the liturgy, [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s saving acts are made present to this particular soul, on this particular day. Every Communion is [[jesus-christ|Christ]]mas, Good Friday, and Easter — for you.

### Preparation and the Fear of Unworthiness

Thomas is meticulous about preparation, knowing well the soul's twin temptations: to stay away through a false humility of fear, and to come carelessly through a negligence of [[pride|presumption]]. He devotes chapters to examining conscience, to the need for purity of intention, and to the battle against the devil's suggestions before Communion: "The enemy, knowing what fruit and what mighty healing lieth in the Holy Communion, striveth by all means and occasions to draw back and hinder the faithful and the devout" (106-008).

Yet the counsel is balanced. To the overly fearful soul, [[jesus-christ|Christ]] says: "It is not right that a man should keep away from this holy Sacrament because he seeth himself unprepared, so long as he hath a good will and intention, and is heartily sorry for his [[sin|fault]]s" (097-102 to 097-104). The very sense of unworthiness, if it draws the soul humbly to the altar rather than driving it away, is itself the best preparation.

### The Fruits of Worthy Communion

The benefits Thomas enumerates are comprehensive. The Communion "draweth us back from evil, and strengtheneth us in good" (099-032). It is the soul's food for the journey: "afterwards being refreshed with heavenly food and drink, they depart with joy and exultation" (099-085 to 099-087). It is the remedy for every spiritual ill: against temptation, against lukewarmness, against the weakness of the flesh, against the distractions of [[world|the world]].

> The Holy Communion therefore draweth us back from evil, and strengtheneth us in good. For if I am now so often negligent and lukewarm when I communicate or celebrate, what would become of me if I received not this medicine? (099-032 to 099-034)

The word *medicine* is crucial. Thomas does not present Communion as a reward for the already-holy but as healing for the still-sick. The soul that feels its weakness most keenly has the strongest claim to approach.

### Spiritual Communion and the Secret Place

Beyond the sacramental act, Thomas teaches the practice of spiritual communion — the desire for [[jesus-christ|Christ]] even when the sacrament cannot be received: "It is thy duty therefore to draw near to spiritual communion with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (106-058). This is not a substitute for the sacrament but an extension of its spirit into every hour.

The broader sense of communion — intimate prayer — appears in the counsel to "keep thyself in a secret place, and hold communion" (108-044). The soul's entire life is to be a communion with God, of which the sacramental Communion is the summit and source.

### Communion with the Whole Church

Thomas teaches that the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] unites the communicant not only with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] but with the whole Body: "this holy and devout action... helpeth the living, hath communion with the departed, and is profitable for the salvation of all the faithful" (101-037). The Communion is cosmic in its reach — it touches the living, the dead, the angels, and the saints. The individual soul at the altar rail is not alone; the whole Church, militant and triumphant, communicates in that act.
