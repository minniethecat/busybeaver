# Blood of Christ

The blood of Jesus Christ, poured out in His passion and death, stands at the center of Christian soteriology as the price of human redemption and the source of cleansing from sin. "Without shedding of blood is no remission" (Hebrews 9:22) — the Old Testament principle is fulfilled once for all in the sacrifice of Calvary, where Christ "by his own blood entered in once into the holy place, having obtained eternal redemption for us" (Hebrews 9:12). In the devotional life, the blood of Christ is invoked as a protection against evil, a plea for mercy, and the ground of confidence before God.

## Definition

The blood of Christ refers both to the historical blood shed in His passion — the scourging, the crowning with thorns, the crucifixion, the piercing of His side — and to its theological significance as the sacrificial offering that atones for sin and reconciles humanity to God. The New Testament consistently presents Christ's death in sacrificial terms: He is "the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world" (John 1:29), and His blood is "the blood of the new testament, which is shed for many for the remission of sins" (Matthew 26:28). Saint Peter calls it "the precious blood of Christ, as of a lamb without blemish and without spot" (1 Peter 1:19), emphasizing both its value and its purity.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Though *The Imitation* is more ascetical than doctrinal in emphasis, the passion of Christ — and therefore His blood — is central to its spirituality. Kempis considers meditation on Christ's sufferings to be the primary school of virtue: "In the cross is salvation, in the cross is life, in the cross is protection from enemies" (047-001). The blood shed on the cross is the visible proof of divine love, and the disciple who contemplates it learns both to suffer patiently and to love in return.

### Devotion and the Precious Blood

Kempis urges the disciple to "feel with Christ" (002-002), entering into the reality of His sufferings not as a distant spectator but as one who participates mystically in the passion. Devotion to the blood of Christ belongs to this affective piety: the wounds are places of refuge, and the blood is cleansing. The disciple who meditates on the passion finds that "the whole cross is filled with every virtue" (047-002).

### The Eucharist

The blood of Christ is made sacramentally present in the Eucharist, which Kempis treats at length in Book IV. Receiving Christ's body and blood is the supreme act of union with Him in this life. "As thou callest this mystery to mind and receivest the body of Christ" (098-055), the disciple is drawn into the very life of God. Kempis counsels frequent and devout communion, prepared for by examination of conscience and true contrition.

## Biblical Foundation

The blood of Christ is a unifying thread through the New Testament. In the Gospels, Christ Himself institutes the Eucharist with the words "This is my blood of the new testament" (Matthew 26:28). In the Pauline epistles, believers are "justified by his blood" (Romans 5:9) and have "redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins" (Ephesians 1:7). The Epistle to the Hebrews develops the most sustained treatment, presenting Christ as both high priest and victim whose blood surpasses the blood of bulls and goats (Hebrews 9:13–14). In the Apocalypse, the saints overcome the accuser "by the blood of the Lamb" (Revelation 12:11).
