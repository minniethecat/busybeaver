# Piety

The reverent devotion to God and the sincere practice of religious duties — in The Imitation of Christ, true piety is a matter of the heart's inward disposition, not of outward display, and the text repeatedly warns against the subtle danger of "pretended piety" that seeks the esteem of men rather than the approval of God.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, piety encompasses both the interior affection of reverence toward God and the exterior practices that express and nourish that affection. It is not mere religious sentiment but the lived orientation of the whole person toward God — in thought, word, and deed.

The Imitation draws a sharp distinction between genuine and counterfeit piety. Genuine piety is humble, unobtrusive, and patient; it is tested and proven in adversity. Counterfeit piety "can easily be taken up" (019-022) — it is the piety that flourishes in times of [[consolation]] but withers under trial. The text's warning against being "deceived by pretended piety" (075-027) is a call to rigorous self-examination.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation of Christ does not devote a single chapter to piety as such, but the theme pervades the entire work. The text's constant insistence on the primacy of the interior life — of the heart's real disposition over its visible performances — constitutes a sustained theology of authentic piety.

### The Fragility of False Piety

The Imitation warns that a piety founded on external observances or on the natural sweetness of devotion "can easily be taken up" (019-022) and just as easily set aside. The piety that depends on favorable circumstances — health, tranquility, the absence of temptation — is not yet the piety that pleases God. True piety is forged in the crucible of [[adversity]], where the soul's real love for God is revealed.

### The Danger of Pretended Piety

Book III issues a searching warning: "deceived by pretended piety" (075-027). The danger is not only hypocrisy before others but self-deception — the soul that has convinced itself of its own devotion while its heart remains unrenewed. The Imitation's remedy is the constant return to [[humility]] and the acknowledgment that all genuine piety is a gift of grace, not an achievement of the will.

### Key Passages

> "Piety, or of some brotherly kindness, it can easily be taken up." (019-022)

> "Deceived by pretended piety." (075-027)

## Biblical Foundation

The apostolic teaching on piety echoes The Imitation's emphasis on inward reality over outward form. Paul declares that "godliness is profitable for all things" (1 Timothy 4:8), but James insists that true piety is demonstrated in action: "Pure and undefiled religion before God and the Father is this: to visit orphans and widows in their trouble, and to keep oneself unspotted from the world" (James 1:27). Christ Himself warns against the piety that "does their deeds to be seen by men" (Matthew 6:1), calling His disciples to pray, fast, and give alms in secret, "and your Father who sees in secret will reward you openly" (Matthew 6:6).

## Practical Application

The teaching of The Imitation on piety calls the [[disciple]] to examine not whether devotion is present, but whether it is real. The test is twofold: does piety endure when [[consolation]] is withdrawn? And does it issue in [[charity]] toward others, or does it terminate in self-satisfaction? The soul that answers honestly will find in The Imitation a guide that leads beyond religious performance into the substance of [[humility]] and the love of God for His own sake.
