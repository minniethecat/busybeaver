# Comfort

The solace, refreshment, and [[peace|inward peace]] that sustain the soul amid the [[temptation|trial]]s of the spiritual life — a gift that Thomas à Kempis treats with exquisite pastoral wisdom, steering a middle course between the contempt of comfort that would deny God's tenderness and the addiction to comfort that would make the [[discipleship|disciple]] dependent on feelings rather than faith. "It is often better and safer for a man not to have many comforts in this life" (021-025), yet the [[discipleship|disciple]] who "seeketh not much comfort at the hand of men" (012-011) finds in God a consolation that no creature can give.

## Definition

Comfort, in the vocabulary of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], spans a range of meanings: the mutual encouragement of brethren bearing one another's burdens, the inward sweetness that accompanies devotion, and the ultimate consolation that God Himself is to the [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing soul. Thomas is characteristically dialectical in his treatment: comfort is a real good, a gift of divine tenderness, not to be despised; yet it is also a potential snare, for the soul that has grown accustomed to sensible sweetness may falter when it is withdrawn. The mature [[discipleship|disciple]] learns to "rest wholly upon God, so that he needeth not seek much comfort at the hand of men" (012-010 to 012-011), receiving comfort when God gives it with gratitude, and enduring its absence with patience. The supreme comfort is God Himself: "I am thy salvation, I am thy peace and thy life" (038-015).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas weaves the theme of comfort through every book of The Imitation, treating it under several interrelated aspects — the comfort of human fellowship, the danger of seeking comfort in [[creation|creatures]], the sweetness of divine consolation, and the spiritual maturity that can live without it.

### The Limited Value of Human Comfort

Thomas opens his treatment of comfort with a sharp observation about human conversation: "We willingly speak and talk of those things which we love and desire... but alas! it is often to no purpose and in vain. For this outward consolation is no small hindrance to the inner comfort which cometh from God" (010-008 to 010-013). The [[discipleship|disciple]] naturally seeks "mutual comfort" in conversation, refreshing "our wearied spirits by variety of discourse" (010-009), but Thomas warns that this outward consolation competes with inward comfort. The soul that fills its [[vanity|emptiness]] with talk will never learn to be filled by God.

This is not a counsel of misanthropy but of spiritual prioritisation. Thomas affirms the duty of mutual comfort among brethren: "it behoveth us to bear with one another, to comfort one another, to help, instruct, admonish one another" (016-027 to 016-028). The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian community is a community of mutual consolation, each member strengthening the others. But this horizontal comfort must never become a substitute for the vertical comfort that comes from God alone. The [[discipleship|disciple]] must "rest wholly upon God" (012-010) — not upon the sympathy of friends, the reassurance of superiors, or the distraction of conversation.

### The Danger of Fleeing to the World

Thomas diagnoses a characteristic spiritual reflex: "When even a little trouble befalleth us, too quickly are we cast down, and fly to [[world|the world]] to give us comfort" (011-021 to 011-022). The discomfort of [[temptation|trial]] — a [[desolation|dryness]] in prayer, a misunderstanding with a brother, a bodily ailment — sends the soul scrambling for distraction. It reaches for conversation, entertainment, food, or busyness — anything to escape the pain of the present moment. But [[world|the world]]'s comfort is narcotic, not therapeutic; it dulls the pain without healing the wound. Thomas's alternative is to "quit ourselves like men, and strive to stand firm in the battle" (011-023), [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing that "we should see the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] helping us from [[heaven|Heaven]]" (011-024). The comfort that follows spiritual combat is infinitely sweeter than the comfort that evades it.

### The Sweetness and Danger of Divine Consolation

Thomas does not deny the reality or value of divine comfort. The experience of inward sweetness in prayer, the sense of God's presence, the lifting of the heart in devotion — these are genuine goods, gifts of the heavenly Bridegroom to His beloved. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is not to "utterly cast away those comforts which are" given by God (021-028). But Thomas insists with equal force that the absence of such comforts "is to our own blame, because we seek not [[compunction|compunction of heart]], nor utterly cast away those comforts which are vain and worldly" (021-027 to 021-029). The soul that complains of [[desolation|dryness]] has often filled itself with worldly comforts and left no room for divine consolation.

The mature [[discipleship|disciple]] learns to hold comfort with an open hand — receiving it gratefully when it comes, but not demanding it as a right or collapsing when it is withdrawn. "It is often better and safer for a man not to have many comforts in this life, especially those which concern the flesh" (021-025 to 021-026). The comforts of the flesh — physical ease, sensory pleasure, emotional gratification — are particularly dangerous because they attach the heart to what is passing. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who has learned to do without them has acquired a freedom that the comfort-addicted soul cannot imagine.

### The Ultimate Comfort

Beyond the dialectic of consolation and desolation lies the simple truth that God Himself is the soul's comfort. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] declares: "I am thy salvation, I am thy peace and thy life. Keep thee unto Me, and thou shalt find peace" (038-015 to 038-016). The comfort that is a Person cannot be lost as the comfort that is a feeling can be lost. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who cleaves to [[jesus-christ|Christ]] possesses comfort in its source, even when consolation is experientially absent. "The [[jesus-christ|Lord]] is my light and my salvation: whom then shall I fear?" (043-044) — this is the confidence of one who has found a comfort deeper than feeling.

The eschatological dimension is essential: "No [[silence|quiet]] shall be there, no comfort for the lost" (024-036). The loss of comfort is not merely a this-worldly deprivation but a foretaste of hell, where comfort is eternally absent. Conversely, the possession of divine comfort here and now is a foretaste of heaven, where God "shall wipe away all tears" (Revelation 21:4). The [[discipleship|disciple]] who learns to find comfort in God alone is already practising for eternity.

### Comfort in the Communion of Saints

Thomas also treats the horizontal dimension of comfort positively when it flows from [[love|charity]] rather than escapism. "It behoveth us to bear with one another, to comfort one another, to help, instruct, admonish one another" (016-027). Mutual comfort is a work of [[forgiveness|mercy]], an expression of the law of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] by which "we that are strong ought to bear the infirmities of the weak" (Romans 15:1). The difference between this holy mutual comfort and the "outward consolation" that hinders inward comfort lies in its source and end: holy comfort strengthens the recipient to cleave more firmly to God; worldly comfort distracts the recipient from God. The same words, the same presence, can be either, depending on the [[love|charity]] with which they are given and received.

## Key Passages

> "We willingly speak and talk of those things which we love and desire... but alas! it is often to no purpose and in vain. For this outward consolation is no small hindrance to the inner comfort which cometh from God." (010-008 to 010-013)

> "Therefore ought a man to rest wholly upon God, so that he needeth not seek much comfort at the hand of men. When a good man is afflicted or tried or oppressed with evil thoughts, then he seeth that God is most necessary to him." (012-010 to 012-012)

> "It is often better and safer for a man not to have many comforts in this life, especially those which concern the flesh. Nevertheless, that we lack divine comforts or feel them rarely is to our own blame, because we seek not compunction of heart, nor utterly cast away those comforts which are vain and worldly." (021-025 to 021-029)

> "These things saith thy Beloved, 'I am thy salvation, I am thy peace and thy life. Keep thee unto Me, and thou shalt find peace.'" (038-015 to 038-016)

> "When even a little trouble befalleth us, too quickly are we cast down, and fly to the world to give us comfort. If we would quit ourselves like men, and strive to stand firm in the battle, then should we see the Lord helping us from Heaven." (011-021 to 011-024)

## Biblical Foundation

The dialectic of comfort in The Imitation is deeply scriptural. Paul opens 2 Corinthians with a blessing of "the God of all comfort, who comforteth us in all our [[suffering|tribulation]], that we may be able to comfort them which are in any trouble, by the comfort wherewith we ourselves are comforted of God" (2 Corinthians 1:3-4). This Pauline pattern — God comforts us so that we may comfort others — underlies Thomas's teaching on mutual comfort as a work of [[love|charity]]. The warning against seeking comfort in [[creation|creatures]] echoes Jeremiah 2:13: "My people have committed two evils; they have forsaken Me the fountain of living waters, and hewed them out cisterns, broken cisterns, that can hold no water."

The promise of the [[holy-spirit|Paraclete]] — [[holy-spirit|the Comforter]] whom [[jesus-christ|Christ]] sends (John 14:16) — is the ultimate ground of the soul's confidence in divine consolation. The words of [[isaiah|Isaiah]] 40:1 — "Comfort ye, comfort ye my people, saith your God" — stand behind the entire tradition of spiritual consolation in which Thomas writes. The insistence that "it is better and safer not to have many comforts" (021-025) echoes the wisdom of Ecclesiastes 7:3-4: "Sorrow is better than laughter... The heart of the wise is in the house of mourning." The eschatological comfort that awaits the faithful is the fulfillment of the Beatitude: "Blessed are they that mourn: for they shall be comforted" (Matthew 5:4).
