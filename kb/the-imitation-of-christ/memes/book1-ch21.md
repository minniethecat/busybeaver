[021-001] Of [[compunction|compunction of heart]]

[021-002] If thou wilt make any progress keep thyself in the [[fear-of-the-lord|fear of God]], and

[021-003] long not to be too free, but restrain all thy senses under discipline

[021-004] and give not thyself up to senseless mirth. Give thyself to [[contrition|compunction]]

[021-005] of heart and thou shalt find devotion. Compunction openeth the way for

[021-006] many good things, which dissoluteness is wont quickly to lose. It is

[021-007] wonderful that any man can ever rejoice heartily in this life who

[021-008] considereth and weigheth his banishment, and the manifold dangers which

[021-009] beset his soul.

[021-010] 2. Through lightness of heart and neglect of our shortcomings we feel

[021-011] not the sorrows of our soul, but often vainly laugh when we have good

[021-012] cause to weep. There is no true liberty nor real joy, save in the fear

[021-013] of God with a [[conscience|good conscience]]. Happy is he who can cast away every

[021-014] cause of distraction and bring himself to the one purpose of holy

[021-015] compunction. Happy is he who putteth away from him whatsoever may stain

[021-016] or burden his conscience. Strive manfully; custom is overcome by

[021-017] custom. If thou knowest how to let men alone, they will gladly let thee

[021-018] alone to do thine own works.

[021-019] 3. Busy not thyself with the affairs of others, nor entangle thyself

[021-020] with the business of great men. Keep always thine eye upon thyself

[021-021] first of all, and give advice to thyself specially before all thy

[021-022] dearest friends. If thou hast not the favour of men, be not thereby

[021-023] cast down, but let thy concern be that thou holdest not thyself so well

[021-024] and circumspectly, as becometh a servant of God and a devout monk. It

[021-025] is often better and safer for a man not to have many comforts in this

[021-026] life, especially those which concern the flesh. But that we lack divine

[021-027] comforts or feel them rarely is to our own blame, because we seek not

[021-028] compunction of heart, nor utterly cast away those comforts which are

[021-029] vain and worldly.

[021-030] 4. Know thyself to be unworthy of divine [[comfort|consolation]], and worthy rather

[021-031] of much [[suffering|tribulation]]. When a man hath perfect compunction, then all the

[021-032] world is burdensome and bitter to him. A good man will find sufficient

[021-033] cause for mourning and weeping; for whether he considereth himself, or

[021-034] pondereth concerning his neighbour, he knoweth that no man liveth here

[021-035] without tribulation, and the more thoroughly he considereth himself,

[021-036] the more thoroughly he grieveth. Grounds for just grief and inward

[021-037] compunction there are in our [[sin|sins]] and vices, wherein we lie so

[021-038] entangled that we are but seldom able to contemplate heavenly things.

[021-039] 5. If thou thoughtest upon thy death more often than how long thy life

[021-040] should be, thou wouldest doubtless strive more earnestly to improve.

[021-041] And if thou didst seriously consider the future pains of hell, I

[021-042] believe thou wouldest willingly endure toil or pain and fear not

[021-043] discipline. But because these things reach not the heart, and we still

[021-044] love pleasant things, therefore we remain cold and miserably

[021-045] indifferent.

[021-046] 6. Oftentimes it is from poverty of spirit that the wretched body is so

[021-047] easily led to complain. Pray therefore humbly unto the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] that He

[021-048] will give thee the spirit of compunction and say in the language of the

[021-049] prophet, _Feed me, O Lord, with bread of tears, and give me

[021-050] plenteousness of tears to drink_.(1)

[021-051] (1) Psalm lxxx. 5.
