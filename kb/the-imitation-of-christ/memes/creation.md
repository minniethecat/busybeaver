# Creation

**Creation** (*creatio*) in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* is understood not as an object of scientific curiosity but as the theater of [[wisdom|divine wisdom]] and the school of the soul. All created things proceed from the one Eternal Word and speak of Him, yet they must ultimately fall silent before the Creator. Thomas à Kempis treats the created order as purposeful, good, and instructive — but also as a potential snare when the soul rests in the creature rather than in God.

## Creation as Divine Testimony

Kempis teaches that all things proceed from and point toward the one Word of God. The multiplicity of creation is unified at its source:

> "From this One Word are all things, and all things speak of Him; and this is the Beginning which also speaketh unto us. No man without Him understandeth or rightly judgeth" [003-020]

This passage opens Book I, Chapter III, establishing creation's epistemological function: the created order is a divine speech that only the one instructed by the Eternal Word can rightly interpret. Natural reason alone cannot penetrate to the meaning of things — the same Word through whom all things were made must also illuminate the mind.

Yet the testimony of creation is preparatory, not final. The soul that has heard the Creator's voice through creatures must ultimately pass beyond them:

> "Let all the doctors hold their peace; let all creation keep silence before Thee: speak Thou alone to me" [003-020]

This is not a rejection of creation but an ordering of loves. Creation speaks truly, but God's direct speech is of another order. The creature points; the Creator satisfies. The proper posture of all created things before God is reverent silence — a silence that acknowledges the infinite qualitative difference between the Maker and the made.

## The Purposefulness of Creation

Kempis insists that nothing in creation is arbitrary. Everything is ordered by [[wisdom|divine wisdom]] toward an end:

> "The Master Workman, who hath left nought in His creation without aim and end" [075-013]

This conviction anchors the soul's confidence in providence. If even the least detail of creation has its appointed purpose, then the soul's own circumstances — however perplexing — are not random. The same wisdom that orders the stars orders the saint's [[suffering|affliction]]s.

The practical implication is that temporal things are to be used, not cleaved to. They were "ordained of God to do good ser[[sin|vice]]" — instruments in the soul's journey, not destinations. The free soul "stand[s] above the present and look[s] upon the eternal," using creation without being enslaved by it.

## Creation and the Interior Life

The movement from creation to Creator is the central dynamic of the spiritual life in Kempis's teaching. The soul that lingers in the creature rather than pressing through to God remains immature. The man who has achieved interior unity "bringeth all things to one, who seeth all things in one" — creation is not denied but integrated, seen no longer as a scattered multitude but as a coherent whole in the light of the One who made it.

The danger is not creation itself but the undisciplined heart that attaches itself to created goods as if they were ultimate. The remedy is not contempt of creation as such but the recognition that nothing created can satisfy the soul made for the Creator. Creation is good; the Creator is better.

## Biblical Foundation

The treatment of creation in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* echoes the opening chapters of Genesis, where God declares creation "good" and orders it toward human flourishing. The conviction that creation speaks of its Maker draws from Psalm 19 ("the heavens declare the glory of God") and Romans 1:20 ("the invisible things of Him from the creation of [[world|the world]] are clearly seen"). The theme of creation's silence before God recalls [[job|Job]]'s repentance — "I lay my hand upon my mouth" ([[job|Job]] 40:4) — and Habakkuk's declaration that "the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] is in His holy temple: let all the earth keep silence before Him" (Habakkuk 2:20).

## Creation and the Devout Life

The Kempian doctrine of creation is not an abstract cosmology but a practical spirituality. For the soul seeking to imitate Christ, the created order is simultaneously gift, teacher, and potential snare — and the art of the devout life consists in navigating these three dimensions with wisdom.

### Creation as Gift

Every created thing exists because God willed it into being and pronounced it good (Genesis 1:31). The Imitation insists that this original goodness is not canceled by the Fall but ordered toward the sanctification of the soul. Temporal goods — food, shelter, companionship, beauty, rest — are "ordained of God to do good service" and are to be received with gratitude, not guilt. The problem is never the creature but the heart that cleaves to the creature rather than pressing through to the Creator.

This has practical consequences. The disciple is not called to despise the body, the natural world, or human affection, but to receive them all as gifts that point beyond themselves. A meal received with thanksgiving becomes a sacrament of divine provision; a friendship ordered in Christ becomes a foretaste of the communion of saints; the beauty of the natural world becomes a veil through which the divine beauty is glimpsed. "From this One Word are all things, and all things speak of Him" (003-020) — the task of the devout soul is to learn the language in which creation speaks.

### Creation as Teacher

Kempis teaches that the created order is a school in which the soul learns divine wisdom. The ordering of the seasons, the fidelity of the sun's rising, the patience of the earth in bearing fruit — all these natural rhythms teach the soul something about God's faithfulness and the virtues that correspond to it. The man who has achieved interior unity "bringeth all things to one, who seeth all things in one" — this is not a denial of creation's multiplicity but an integration of it, seeing every fragment as part of a coherent whole ordered by divine wisdom.

The practical discipline is to cultivate what the later tradition would call "sacramental vision" — the habit of seeing the invisible through the visible, the eternal through the temporal. The Imitation trains the disciple to ask of every encounter, every object, every circumstance: "What does this teach me about God? How does this draw me toward Him or away from Him?" This is not an intellectual exercise but a spiritual habit, formed through prayer, silence, and the repeated refusal to rest in the creature rather than in God.

### Creation and Detachment

The danger of creation is not that it is evil but that it is good enough to satisfy the soul at a superficial level. The disciple who rests in created comfort will never hunger for the Creator; the soul that is full of the world's goods will not thirst for God. Hence the necessity of detachment — not hatred of creation but a disciplined freedom from it.

Thomas à Kempis describes the free soul as one who "standeth above the present and looketh upon the eternal" — a posture that does not reject creation but relativizes it. The one who has tasted the Creator can enjoy the creature without clinging to it; the one who has set his treasure in heaven can use earthly goods without being enslaved by them. This is the "poverty of spirit" that Christ pronounced blessed (Matthew 5:3) — not the absence of possessions but the absence of possessiveness.

### Prayer for the Right Use of Creation

*Almighty and everlasting God, who hast made all things in wisdom and ordered them toward their proper end: grant that we, who are surrounded by the gifts of Thy creation, may never rest in the gift but press through to the Giver; and that we, who see Thee now through created things as in a glass darkly, may one day behold Thee face to face in the eternal light. Through Jesus Christ our Lord. Amen.*

## Creation and the Eucharistic Mystery

Kempis brings the doctrine of creation to its fullest expression in the context of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], where the Creator is received within the creature. The paradox is staggering: the soul exclaims, "How great the loving kindness of Thy [[grace|goodness]], that Thou Lord God, Creator and Quickener of all spirits, dost vouchsafe to come to a poor soul" (107-061). The One through whom all things were made descends to dwell within the very being He fashioned from nothing.

This descent transforms the creature's relation to all other created things. The soul that has received the Creator learns to withdraw its heart "from all created things" (109-010) not in contempt but in right ordering. The proper hierarchy is restored: the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] teaches that "no creature is able to give me rest, save Thou, O my God" (107-032). Creation is good, but only the Creator satisfies. The [[eucharist|sacrament]] is the school in which this truth is learned existentially rather than abstractly.

The practical consequence is a transformed relationship to the entire created order. The soul prays: "that no man may henceforth look upon me, nor any creature move me or regard me" (109-006) — not out of misanthropy but because the gaze of the Creator has become sufficient. The [[suffering|affliction]]s and distractions that once loomed so large recede in proportion as the soul learns to rest in the One who "hath left nought in His creation without aim and end" (075-013). The [[eucharist|Eucharist]] is the foretaste of that eternal rest where the blessed shall see all things in God and God in all things — creation redeemed, integrated, and brought to its intended [[peace|perfection]].
