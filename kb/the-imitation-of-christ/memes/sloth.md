# Sloth

Sloth, known in the tradition as *acedia* or *accidie*, is one of the seven capital vices — but it is often misunderstood. It is not mere laziness or physical idleness; a person may be frantically busy and yet deeply slothful, if the busyness is flight from God. The desert fathers called acedia "the noonday demon" (Psalm 91:6) because it strikes the monk at midday with a restless desire to be anywhere else, doing anything else, than the work of prayer. "Slothfulness casteth into a deep sleep" (Proverbs 19:15) — a sleep not of the body but of the soul, a torpor that numbs the will and dims the desire for God.

## Definition

Sloth is a capital vice because it is a source (*caput*) of other sins: the slothful soul, unable to bear the effort of the interior life, seeks compensation in gossip, curiosity, restlessness, and the indulgence of bodily appetites. Saint Thomas Aquinas defines acedia as *tristitia de bono spirituali* — sorrow at spiritual goods. The slothful person perceives spiritual things (prayer, self-examination, the demands of charity) as burdensome rather than joyful, and this sorrow drains the will of the energy to pursue them. The antidote is not willpower but love: zeal, diligence, the rekindling of desire through meditation on the goodness of God and the shortness of life.

## In The Imitation of Christ

While *The Imitation* does not use the technical term *acedia* frequently, the entire work is an antidote to sloth — a sustained exhortation to rouse the soul from spiritual drowsiness and "run with patience the race that is set before us" (Hebrews 12:1).

### The Danger of Lukewarmness

Kempis reserves some of his sharpest warnings for the lukewarm — the soul that has enough religion to be comfortable but not enough to be transformed. "I would thou wert cold or hot," says Christ in the Apocalypse; "so then because thou art lukewarm, and neither cold nor hot, I will spue thee out of my mouth" (Revelation 3:15–16). The *Imitation* echoes this: "It is a great matter to live in obedience, to be under a superior, and not to be at our own disposal" (032-001). The slothful soul seeks ease and autonomy; the fervent soul seeks God's will whatever the cost.

### Resistance to Interior Effort

Kempis describes the interior resistance that is the mark of sloth: "The flesh is unwilling, the spirit is weak" — but he adds: "Yet a little violence is good" (001-002). The slothful soul shrinks from the "violence" of prayer, self-denial, and concentration; the remedy is to "accustom thyself to suffer a little" (021-001), rebuilding the capacity for sustained spiritual effort through small daily acts of fidelity.

### The Remedy: Fervor and Perseverance

The *Imitation* constantly urges fervor — not the flash of emotional excitement but the steady heat of a will fixed on God. "Be fervent in spirit" (001-001) is a recurring command. Perseverance, the virtue that outlasts the noonday demon, is the proof that fervor is genuine. "He that persevereth unto the end shall be saved" (Matthew 24:13).

## Biblical Foundation

The book of Proverbs warns repeatedly against sloth: "The slothful man saith, There is a lion without, I shall be slain in the streets" (Proverbs 22:13) — the slothful invents excuses. The "wicked and slothful servant" of the parable of the talents (Matthew 25:26) is condemned not for doing evil but for doing nothing with what he was given. Saint Paul exhorts the Romans to be "not slothful in business; fervent in spirit; serving the Lord" (Romans 12:11). The early monastic literature — Evagrius Ponticus, John Cassian, Saint Benedict — developed a detailed psychology of acedia and its remedies: manual labor, stability of place, and above all the invocation of the name of Christ.
