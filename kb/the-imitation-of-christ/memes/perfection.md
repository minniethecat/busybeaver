# Perfection

The goal of the Christian life — complete conformity to the will of God and the example of Christ — which in The Imitation of Christ is pursued through [[humility]], [[love]], and the renunciation of self, yet is never fully attained on earth: "all perfection hath some imperfection joined to it" (003-035).

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, perfection is not the flawless execution of religious duties nor the absence of all fault, but the progressive transformation of the soul into the likeness of Christ through the practice of virtue and the surrender of self-will. Thomas à Kempis holds perfection before the reader as the ultimate aim — "Be perfect, even as your Father in heaven is perfect" (Matthew 5:48) — while simultaneously insisting upon the soul's permanent incompleteness in this life.

This tension is essential to the Kempian understanding: perfection is the direction of the spiritual life, not its present attainment. The soul that presumes to have arrived has fallen into the worst imperfection of all — spiritual [[pride]]. The beginning of true perfection is the recognition of one's radical imperfection.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation of Christ treats perfection as both the summit toward which the spiritual life tends and the path by which it proceeds, emphasizing that the path itself — [[humility]], obedience, and love — is the substance of perfection.

### The Limits of Perfection in This Life

Book I opens the discussion with a sober observation: "all perfection hath some imperfection joined to it" (003-035). This is not a counsel of despair but a guard against presumption. No matter how far the soul has advanced, there remains in it some element of imperfection — some hidden self-love, some unperceived attachment, some defect known only to God. The acknowledgment of this incompleteness is itself a mark of spiritual maturity.

### The Cross as the Perfection of Holiness

Book II locates perfection not in spiritual consolations or elevated experiences but in the mystery of the Cross: "in the Cross is the height of virtue, in the Cross perfection of holiness" (037-015). This is a radical redefinition: perfection is not found in the absence of suffering but in the willing embrace of it, not in the removal of difficulty but in loving conformity to Christ crucified.

The Cross gathers into itself every virtue — [[humility]], [[patience]], obedience, and above all [[love]] — and perfects them. There is no path to perfection that bypasses Calvary.

### Love as the Consummation of Perfection

Book III teaches that love is the summit of the spiritual life: "love willeth to be raised up, and not to be held down by any low thing" (042-022). The soul that has attained to the love of God for His own sake has attained the substance of perfection, for love is "the bond of perfection" (Colossians 3:14). All other virtues serve love; without love, they are empty forms.

The same chapter declares that "in this short saying is enclosed all the perfection of the religious" (069-012) — referring to the abandonment of self and the complete surrender of the will to God. Perfection is not complexity but simplicity: the single-hearted offering of the whole self.

### The Role of Grace

The Imitation is clear that perfection is not the product of human effort alone. The text speaks of progress "for bringing to perfection" (092-035) — divine grace working through the soul's cooperation. The soul does not perfect itself; it is brought to perfection by the hand of Another. The [[disciple]]'s role is to remove the obstacles of self-will and disordered attachment, making room for grace to do its work.

### Key Passages

> "All perfection hath some imperfection joined to it." (003-035)

> "In the Cross is the height of virtue, in the Cross perfection of holiness." (037-015)

> "Love willeth to be raised up, and not to be held down by any low thing." (042-022)

## Biblical Foundation

The call to perfection is rooted in Christ's own command: "Be perfect, even as your Father in heaven is perfect" (Matthew 5:48). Paul identifies love as the bond of perfection (Colossians 3:14) and urges believers to press on toward the goal (Philippians 3:12), acknowledging that he has not yet attained it. The letter to the Hebrews exhorts believers to "go on to perfection" (Hebrews 6:1), leaving behind the elementary teachings and pressing forward toward maturity.

## Practical Application

The pursuit of perfection, as taught in The Imitation, begins with the honest acknowledgment that "all perfection hath some imperfection joined to it" (003-035). This frees the soul from the paralysis of unrealistic expectations and the [[pride]] of imagined attainment. The daily practice is twofold: the steady renunciation of self-will — the "short saying" (069-012) that encompasses the whole of religious perfection — and the cultivation of [[love]], which alone gives value to every other virtue.
