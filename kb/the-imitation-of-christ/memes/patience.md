# Patience

The virtue by which the soul bears all adversities, injuries, and delays with tranquility, [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing in [[providence|God's providence]] and following the patient example of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Patience is the spiritual strength by which a person endures [[temptation|trial]]s, [[suffering|tribulation]]s, and the [[sin|fault]]s of others without murmuring, resentment, or despair. In The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], patience is not passive resignation but an active virtue — a disciplined habit of the soul that must be "carefully trained" (056-014) and exercised through real [[suffering|adversity]]. Thomas a Kempis presents patience as both a necessary condition for spiritual progress and a means of sharing in the sufferings of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas a Kempis treats patience as an indispensable virtue woven throughout all four books of The Imitation. It is the means by which the [[discipleship|disciple]] navigates the inevitable [[temptation|trial]]s of earthly life, the disposition that safeguards interior peace, and the path by which one follows the suffering [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

### The Necessity of Patience

The Imitation insists that patience is not optional for the spiritual life. "I see that patience is very necessary unto me; for many things in this life fall out contrary" (049-003). The [[discipleship|disciple]] acknowledges that "for howsoever I may have contrived for my peace, my life cannot go on without strife and trouble" (049-005). [[jesus-christ|Christ]] responds that He does not will a peace "without [[temptation|trial]]s" but one found through [[suffering|tribulation]] patiently borne (049-008). Patience is therefore the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian's essential tool for navigating a fallen world.

### The True Nature of Patience

In Book III, Chapter XIX, the author offers a searching examination of what genuine patience entails. The truly patient person "considereth not by what man he is tried, whether by one above him, or by an equal or inferior, whether by a good and holy man, or a perverse and unworthy; but indifferently from every creature, whatsoever or how often soever [[suffering|adversity]] happeneth to him, he gratefully accepteth all from the hand of God" (056-024). To select one's [[temptation|trial]]s — to be willing to suffer from some but not from others — is "foolish" and "considereth not the virtue of patience" (056-019).

### Patience and the Cross

Patience is inseparable from [[cross|bearing the cross]]. "Thou shalt everywhere possess patience if thou wilt have internal peace and gain the everlasting crown" (037-045). The cross of suffering is unavoidable — "if thou cast away one cross, without doubt thou shalt find another and perchance a heavier" (037-051). Yet patience, when practiced willingly, transforms the cross into a means of spiritual progress.

### Patience with Others

Patience extends beyond the endurance of personal hardship to bearing with the [[sin|fault]]s of others. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is taught: "Endeavour to be patient in bearing with other men's [[sin|fault]]s and infirmities whatsoever they be, for thou thyself also hast many things which have need to be borne with by others" (016-011). The [[sin|fault]]s of others are permitted by God "for thy [[temptation|trial]] and patience, without which our merits are but little worth" (016-004).

### Key Passages

> "O Lord God, I see that patience is very necessary unto me; for many things in this life fall out contrary. For howsoever I may have contrived for my peace, my life cannot go on without strife and trouble." (049-003)

> "He is not truly patient who will only suffer as far as seemeth right to himself and from whom he pleaseth. But the truly patient man considereth not by what man he is tried... but indifferently from every creature... he gratefully accepteth all from the hand of God." (056-022)

> "Thy way is our way, and by holy patience we walk to Thee who art our Crown." (055-027)

> "Then enduring patience shall have more might than all the power of the world." (024-053)

> "All men commend patience, although few be willing to practise it." (037-119)

## Biblical Foundation

The biblical roots of patience in The Imitation are anchored in the New Testament. The apostle James teaches that "the trying of your faith worketh patience" and that patience perfects the soul (James 1:3-4). [[apostle-paul|Saint Paul]] likewise presents [[suffering|tribulation]] as producing patience, which in turn produces "experience; and experience, hope" (Romans 5:3-4). [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own words — "In your patience possess ye your souls" (Luke 21:19) — provide the dominical warrant. The author also draws on Hebrews: [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own passion is the supreme model, and the [[discipleship|disciple]] is reminded, "Ye have not yet resisted unto blood, striving against sin" (Hebrews 12:4, cited at 056-003).

The Old Testament figure of [[job|Job]] — "the life of man upon the earth is a [[temptation|trial]]" (quoted at 013-003) — provides the archetype of [[perseverance|patient endurance]]. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s entire earthly life, from birth to crucifixion, is presented as the perfect model: "from the hour of My birth, until My death upon the Cross, I ceased not from bearing of sorrow" (055-006).
