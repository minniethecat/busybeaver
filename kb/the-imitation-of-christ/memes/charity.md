# Charity

The infused theological virtue and queen of all virtues — a supernatural love poured into the soul by grace that unites man to God and neighbour for God's own sake, without which no work, however great or costly, profits a man unto [[eternity|eternal life]], in the teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Charity, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is not mere natural kindness or philanthropy but the very life of God in the soul. It is the love by which God loves Himself in us and we love Him and our neighbour in return. Thomas à Kempis defines the man who has true charity as one who "in no wise seeketh his own good, but desireth that God may be glorified in all things" (015-015). Charity is the form and soul of every good work: without it, even the most splendid outward deeds are dead; with it, "whatsoever is done in [[love|charity]], however small and of no reputation, bringeth forth good fruit" (015-006). It is, in Thomas's teaching, the sole measure of human greatness — "he is truly great who hath great [[love|charity]]" (003-058).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Charity stands at the summit of Thomas à Kempis's theology of the interior life. It is the fountainhead from which all genuine virtue flows and the sole criterion by which every work, word, and thought is judged. Book I, Chapter 15 — dedicated entirely to "Of Works of Charity" — is one of the most concentrated treatments of any single virtue in the whole work, indicating its primacy in the Kempian spiritual vision.

### Charity as the Soul of Works

The central teaching of Chapter 15 is that the moral value of any deed is determined entirely by the [[love|charity]] with which it is done. Thomas writes that "the outward work, without [[love|charity]], profiteth nothing" (015-005). A man may give all his goods to the poor, endure great austerities, and perform every act of external religion — yet if [[love|charity]] be absent, all of it is "of no price in the sight of God" (015-005). Conversely, the smallest act performed in [[love|charity]] — a cup of cold water, a word of comfort, a silent prayer — "bringeth forth good fruit" (015-006), for God regards not the magnitude of the work but the measure of love in the heart of the doer.

This teaching echoes and extends 1 Corinthians 13, but Thomas applies it with particular sharpness to the religious life itself. The monk who performs his rule with exactness but without [[love|charity]] is a sounding brass; the layperson who suffers in patience united to the [[passion-of-christ|Passion of Christ]], though their suffering be unseen and unremarked, is greater before God. "God weigheth more the affection and purity of the heart from which a work proceedeth than the work itself" (015-006).

### True Charity vs. Carnal Affection

Thomas warns that not everything called [[love|charity]] is truly [[love|charity]]. "Oftentimes that seemeth to be [[love|charity]] which is rather carnality" (015-012). True [[love|charity]] is distinguished from its counterfeits by three signs. First, it seeks not its own — the truly charitable soul desires only that God be glorified, even at the cost of its own honour or comfort. Second, it is universal in scope — it does not pick and choose its objects based on natural affinity but extends even to those who are difficult, ungrateful, or hostile. Third, it endures — true charity "conquereth all [[suffering|adversity]]" (046-023) and does not fail when the labour is thankless or the cost is high. Natural affection, by contrast, is self-regarding, selective, and fickle; it loves because the object is pleasing, not because God is in the object.

Thomas further identifies [[love|charity]] with humility. "The true charity and humility" (028-018) are inseparable companions; the man who loves truly is also the man who esteems himself least. Charity and humility together purge the soul of self-will and make it a fit habitation for God, for he who is "filled with divine charity" (044-059) no longer seeks his own satisfaction but the glory of God alone.

### The Fruits of Charity

The possession of [[love|charity]] transforms the entire spiritual life. Charity conquers all things: "divine charity conquereth all [[suffering|adversity]]" (046-023), enabling the soul to bear every [[temptation|trial]] with patience and even joy. It constitutes the bond of unity among believers — [[love|charity]] makes them "all one through the bond of [[love|charity]]" (095-040), thinking the same things and willing the same end. It is the fire that never goes out: "the fire of inextinguishable [[love|charity]]" (095-047) burns in the hearts of those who love God, and nothing — not [[suffering|tribulation]], nor persecution, nor death itself — can extinguish it.

The soul that possesses even a spark of true charity is transformed in its perception of all things. Such a one "hath verily learned that all earthly things are full of vanity" (015-021), for [[love|charity]] so fills the heart with the sweetness of God that [[world|the world]]'s pleasures become insipid by comparison.

## Key Passages

> "He who hath true and perfect charity, in no wise seeketh his own good, but desireth that God may be glorified in all things. He envieth none, because he seeketh no private good, neither doth he desire to rejoice in himself, but desireth to be made worthy of blessedness in the enjoyment of God." (015-015)

> "The outward work, without charity, profiteth nothing; but whatsoever is done in charity, however small and of no reputation, bringeth forth good fruit. For verily God weigheth more the affection and purity of the heart from which a work proceedeth than the work itself." (015-005 to 015-006)

> "Oftentimes that seemeth to be charity which is rather carnality; for natural inclination, self-will, hope of reward, and desire of our own interest, are seldom absent from the heart." (015-012)

> "He is truly great who hath great charity. He is truly great who is little in his own eyes, and maketh no account of any height of honour." (003-058)

> "If heavenly grace and true charity shall enter into thee, there shall be no envy, nor narrowness of heart, nor self-love, but divine charity shall keep in order all the powers of the soul, and shall bring them into captivity to itself." (046-021 to 046-023)

## Biblical Foundation

The entire theology of [[love|charity]] in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is grounded in the New Testament's teaching on love. The primacy of [[love|charity]] echoes 1 Corinthians 13, where Paul declares that without love, even the gift of prophecy, the knowledge of all mysteries, and the surrender of one's body to the flames profit nothing. Thomas's insistence that [[love|charity]] must be the soul of every work draws directly from this Pauline foundation.

The identification of [[love|charity]] as the bond of perfection reflects Colossians 3:14: "Above all these things put on [[love|charity]], which is the bond of perfectness." The teaching that [[love|charity]] "conquereth all [[suffering|adversity]]" (046-023) echoes Romans 8:35-39, where Paul names every conceivable [[temptation|trial]] and declares that none "shall be able to separate us from the love of God." The fire of inextinguishable [[love|charity]] (095-047) recalls the Song of Songs 8:6-7 — "many waters cannot quench [[love|charity]]" — a text long read by the medieval tradition as an allegory of [[love|divine love]]. Thomas's teaching on true vs. counterfeit [[love|charity]] echoes 1 John 4:16: "God is [[love|charity]]; and he that dwelleth in [[love|charity]] dwelleth in God, and God in him."
