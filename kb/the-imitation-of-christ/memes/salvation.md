# Salvation

The deliverance of the soul from sin and eternal loss, and its rest[[prayer|oratio]]n to union with God — the one thing necessary, in the teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], to which every other concern must be subordinated. "Think of nought but of thy salvation; care only for the things of God" (023-069): this is the unifying imperative that runs through the whole work.

## Definition

Salvation, for Thomas à Kempis, is not merely a future state to be hoped for but a present work to be pursued with vigilance and urgency. It is both God's gift and the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s task — [[jesus-christ|Christ]] declares "I am thy salvation" (038-015), yet the [[discipleship|disciple]] is commanded to "work out" that salvation through daily [[faith|fidelity]]. Salvation comprehends the whole journey of the soul from its turning away from sin through its progressive transformation in grace to its final [[blessedness|beatitude]] in the vision of God. It is, in the Kempian vocabulary, the antonym of worldliness: those who "neglect themselves and their salvation" (041-029) are those absorbed in vain curiosity and pride, while those who "care only for the things of God" (023-069) are the truly wise.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The theme of salvation pervades The Imitation as the ultimate horizon against which every counsel, warning, and exhortation is set. Thomas treats it not as an abstract doctrine but as a practical, daily urgency that ought to shape every decision and every hour.

### The Urgency of the Present Moment

One of the most characteristic emphases of The Imitation is the insistence that the work of salvation cannot be postponed. "[[trust|Trust]] not thy friends and kinsfolk, nor put off the work of thy salvation to the future, for men will forget thee sooner than thou thinkest" (023-038 to 023-039). Thomas echoes the Apostle: "Now is the time most precious. Now is the day of salvation" (023-044). The devil's great deception is to persuade the soul that there will always be time later — tomorrow, next year, on one's deathbed — to turn seriously to God. Thomas demolishes this illusion relentlessly. The present moment is the only moment the [[discipleship|disciple]] possesses; to waste it in negligence is to gamble with eternity.

This urgency is reinforced by the reminder of death and judgment. "Thou knowest not when thou shalt die, nor what shall happen unto thee thereafter" (023-067). The wise [[discipleship|disciple]] lives each day as if it were the last, storing up "undying riches" (023-068) while time remains. The counsel to "think of nought but of thy salvation" (023-069) is not hyperbole but the logical conclusion of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian eschatology: if the soul's eternal destiny hangs on how it lives now, then nothing else deserves the name of seriousness.

### Christ as Salvation Itself

Thomas places the person of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] at the centre of salvation. In the great dialogue of Book III, [[jesus-christ|Christ]] speaks directly to the faithful soul: "I am thy salvation, I am thy peace and thy life. Keep thee unto Me, and thou shalt find peace" (038-015 to 038-016). Salvation is not an impersonal mechanism but a personal relationship — union with the [[jesus-christ|Saviour]] who "came down from heaven for thy salvation" and "took upon Me thy miseries not of necessity, but drawn by love" (055-003 to 055-004). The [[discipleship|disciple]]'s salvation is grounded not in his own efforts but in the merciful condescension of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], who bore the miseries of human life precisely in order to teach the [[discipleship|disciple]] "patience and mightest bear temporal miseries without murmuring" (055-005).

The soul's confidence in salvation is thus confidence in a Person, not in its own merits. "The [[jesus-christ|Lord]] is my light and my salvation: whom then shall I fear?" (043-044) — this cry of the Psalmist, placed on the lips of the [[discipleship|disciple]] beset by spiritual assault, anchors salvation in the unchanging character of God. The devil may plot, [[world|the world]] may threaten, the flesh may weaken, but the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] who is salvation cannot be overcome.

### The Danger of Neglect

Thomas identifies a particular spiritual peril: the neglect of one's own salvation through absorption in secondary things. He observes those who, "being led by curiosity and pride, desire to know my secret things and to understand the deep things of God, whilst they neglect themselves and their salvation" (041-028 to 041-029). These are souls who busy themselves with theological speculation, the [[sin|fault]]s of others, or the affairs of [[world|the world]], while their own spiritual state goes unexamined. The result is catastrophic: "These often fall into great temptations and [[sin|sins]] because of their pride and curiosity" (041-030). The remedy is simple and severe: turn the gaze inward, attend to one's own soul, and make salvation the first and constant business of life.

### Salvation Through the Cross

The path of salvation, in The Imitation, passes necessarily through the cross. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] "came down from heaven for thy salvation" by way of suffering, and the [[discipleship|disciple]] who would be saved must follow the same road. Temporal miseries, borne patiently in union with [[jesus-christ|Christ]], become instruments of salvation rather than obstacles to it. The man who "beareth temporal miseries without murmuring" (055-005) is the man who has learned the lesson of the Cross — that suffering embraced in love is redemptive. Salvation is not an escape from the cross but a transformation through it.

## Key Passages

> "Trust not thy friends and kinsfolk, nor put off the work of thy salvation to the future, for men will forget thee sooner than thou thinkest. It is better for thee now to provide in time, and to send some good before thee, than to trust to the help of others." (023-038 to 023-040)

> "Now is the time most precious. Now is the day of salvation. But alas! that thou spendest not well this time, wherein thou mightest lay up treasure which should avail thee for ever." (023-044 to 023-045)

> "Think of nought but of thy salvation; care only for the things of God. Make to thyself friends, by venerating the saints of God and walking in their steps, that when thou failest, thou mayest be received into everlasting habitations." (023-069 to 023-071)

> "These things saith thy Beloved, 'I am thy salvation, I am thy peace and thy life. Keep thee unto Me, and thou shalt find peace.' Put away from thee all transitory things, seek those things that are eternal." (038-015 to 038-017)

> "I came down from heaven for thy salvation; I took upon Me thy miseries not of necessity, but drawn by love that thou mightest learn patience and mightest bear temporal miseries without murmuring." (055-003 to 055-005)

## Biblical Foundation

The Kempian theology of salvation is saturated with Scripture. The cry "Now is the day of salvation" (023-044) is a direct quotation of 2 Corinthians 6:2: "Behold, now is the accepted time; behold, now is the day of salvation." The exhortation to "work out your own salvation with fear and trembling" (Philippians 2:12) underlies Thomas's insistence that salvation requires the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s active cooperation with grace. The confession "The [[jesus-christ|Lord]] is my light and my salvation: whom then shall I fear?" (043-044) is taken verbatim from Psalm 27:1, and its placement in the context of spiritual warfare shows Thomas reading the Psalter as the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s own prayer book.

[[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s self-identification as "I am thy salvation" (038-015) echoes the divine name revealed in Exodus and fulfilled in the person of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]], whose very name means "Yahweh saves" (Matthew 1:21). The warning that the [[discipleship|disciple]] knows not "when thou shalt die" (023-067) recalls the parable of the rich fool (Luke 12:20) and the dominical command to "watch therefore, for ye know neither the day nor the hour" (Matthew 25:13). Thomas's insistence that salvation cannot be delegated to others — "men will forget thee sooner than thou thinkest" (023-039) — stands in the tradition of the prophets who called Israel to personal repentance rather than reliance on the merits of ancestors.
