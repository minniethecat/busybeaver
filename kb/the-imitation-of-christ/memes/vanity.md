# Vanity

The emptiness and futility of all [[creation|created things]] when pursued as ends in themselves — the opening declaration and governing theme of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], where Thomas à Kempis establishes at the very outset that "all is vanity, save to love God and Him only to serve" (001-021), setting the entire spiritual programme of the work upon the conviction that nothing under the sun can satisfy the soul made for God.

## Definition

Vanity, in the vocabulary of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], means more than mere pride or self-admiration. It is the ontological emptiness of everything that is not God — the radical insufficiency of all created goods to confer lasting [[blessedness|happiness]], meaning, or rest upon the human soul. "Vanity of vanities, all is vanity, save to love God and Him only to serve" (001-021). This declaration, an echo of Ecclesiastes, is not a counsel of despair but the necessary starting-point of wisdom: only when a person has grasped the vanity of all things can he turn with his whole heart to the One who alone is not vanity. The concept runs through every book of the work, returning climactically in Book III: "all is vanity and vexation of spirit" (064-035). Vanity is the shared condition of the entire created order considered apart from its Creator.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The theme of vanity is not one topic among many in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] but the very ground-bass of the entire work. The first chapter — "Of the Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], and Contempt of All the Vanities of the [[world|World]]" — uses the word "vanity" ten times in rapid succession, building an overwhelming rhetorical case for the emptiness of all merely human pursuits.

### The Catalogue of Vanities

In the opening chapter, Thomas enumerates the specific forms that vanity takes in human life. "It is vanity to seek after perishing riches and to [[trust-in-god|trust]] in them" (001-024). "It is vanity to covet honours and to lift ourselves up on high" (001-025). "It is vanity to follow the desires of the flesh and to labour for that for which heavy punishment must afterwards be borne" (001-026). "It is vanity to wish for long life and to care little for a good life" (001-027). "It is vanity to take thought only for the present life and not to look forward to the things which shall be hereafter" (001-029). "It is vanity to love that which quickly passeth away and not to hasten thither where everlasting joy abideth" (001-030).

This catalogue covers the entire range of human desire — wealth, honour, pleasure, longevity, worldly prudence, and earthly attachment. Nothing in [[creation|the created order]] escapes the verdict of vanity, for everything that is not God is passing, and to set one's heart upon what passes is to guarantee one's own ultimate misery. "It is vanity to desire things to continue which shall not continue, and to cleave to things which quickly pass away" (001-027).

### Vanity and the Interior Life

Thomas extends the concept of vanity beyond external pursuits to the interior life itself. Even spiritual [[comfort|consolation]]s, when sought for their own sweetness rather than for God, partake of vanity. "The rejoicing of the wicked is short and false, and their joy is but a shadow" (040-032). The [[discipleship|disciple]] confesses that his own speech is tainted: "we are often led captive and defiled by vanity" (010-004), for idle words and the desire to be heard betray a heart still attached to its own reputation.

The definitive cure for vanity is the inward turn to truth. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] declares: "I am He who in an instant elevateth the humble mind, that it may learn to understand more reasons of the eternal truth than if a man had studied ten years in the schools" (046-020). The truth that [[jesus-christ|Christ]] teaches is precisely that "the vanity of boasting is put to silence" (046-020) — not by suppression but by the overwhelming reality of God, before whom all human pretension dissolves. The soul that has truly encountered God no longer needs to be argued out of vanity, for it has seen for itself that "all [[world|worldly things]] are full of vanity" (015-022).

### The Practical Remedy

The practical remedy for vanity is the deliberate practice of contempt of [[world|the world]] — not a gloomy rejection of creation but a clear-eyed realism about its status as means rather than end. The wise [[discipleship|disciple]] does not pretend that earthly things are worthless in themselves; he sees that they are worthless as ultimate ends. "All therefore is vanity, save to love God and to serve Him only" (024-073). This same formulation reappears at the end of Book III, forming an inclusio that marks the theme as foundational: the first word and the lasting conviction of the entire work is that only God is not vanity.

> "O Lord, what confidence shall I have in this life, or what is the greatest comfort of all things under the sun? Is it not Thou, my Lord God, whose mercies are without number? All is vanity and vexation of spirit." (064-035)

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of vanity in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is explicitly rooted in the Old Testament wisdom tradition, above all in Ecclesiastes. The opening words of the work — "Vanity of vanities, all is vanity" — are a direct quotation of Ecclesiastes 1:2, and Thomas returns to this text at key moments throughout the work. The Preacher's exhaustive survey of all human pursuits and his conclusion that all is "vanity and vexation of spirit" (Ecclesiastes 1:14) provides the scriptural warrant for Thomas's unsparing diagnosis of the human condition.

The New Testament supplies the remedy that Ecclesiastes could only gesture toward. Where the Preacher concludes that the whole duty of man is to "fear God and keep His [[law|commandments]]" (Ecclesiastes 12:13), Thomas fills this counsel with specifically [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian content: to love God and serve Him alone. The call to "lay not up for yourselves treasures upon earth" (Matthew 6:19) and the Johannine warning that "[[world|the world]] passeth away, and the lust thereof" (1 John 2:17) confirm and deepen the Ecclesiastes verdict. The fleeting character of human life — "a vapour that appeareth for a little time, and then vanisheth away" (James 4:14) — seals the argument: to stake one's [[blessedness|happiness]] upon what vanishes is to choose misery with one's eyes open.
