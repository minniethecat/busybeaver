# Penance

The sacrament instituted by Christ for the forgiveness of sins committed after Baptism, in which the penitent confesses sins with contrition to a priest, receives absolution, and performs the satisfaction imposed. More broadly, penance names the entire disposition of a soul turned from sin toward God — sorrow for the past, confession in the present, and the resolve to amend in the future.

## Definition

In its sacramental sense, penance is one of the seven sacraments of the Church, grounded in Christ's words to the Apostles after the Resurrection: "Receive ye the Holy Ghost: whosesoever sins ye remit, they are remitted unto them; and whosesoever sins ye retain, they are retained" (John 20:22-23). The sacrament requires three acts on the part of the penitent — contrition (sorrow for sin), confession (oral acknowledgment of sins to a priest), and satisfaction (performing the penance assigned) — and one act on the part of the minister: absolution, spoken in the name of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit.

Beyond its sacramental form, penance describes the whole penitential disposition of the Christian life — the continual turning (*metanoia*) from sin to God that marks authentic discipleship. In this broader sense, penance encompasses all the works by which the soul expresses sorrow for sin and seeks restoration: prayer, fasting, almsgiving, and the patient endurance of suffering.

## In The Imitation of Christ

*The Imitation of Christ* breathes the spirit of penance from its opening pages to its close. The entire work can be read as an extended exhortation to the penitential life — a call to turn from the vanity of the world, to sorrow for sin, to confess sincerely, and to amend one's life in earnest.

### Sorrow for Sin as the Beginning of Wisdom

Thomas à Kempis identifies compunction — heartfelt sorrow for sin — as the necessary beginning of the spiritual life. "If thou hadst true compunction of heart and didst shed tears of devotion, then wouldst thou rejoice in the Lord" (021-003). The penitent heart is preferable to the learned head: "I had rather feel compunction than know the definition thereof" (001-003). True sorrow is not a passing emotion but a settled disposition of humility before God. As Thomas writes, "Give thyself to compunction of heart and thou shalt find devotion. Compunction openeth the way for many good things" (021-004 to 021-005).

### The Practice of Confession

The work presupposes the sacrament of penance as the ordinary means of reconciliation. The disciple is exhorted to prepare for confession with careful self-examination: "Search thine own heart, and see whether thou hast that full and true devotion which thou oughtest to have" (092-006). The examination of conscience is not morbid introspection but a sober accounting before God — the recognition that hidden faults remain even after sincere effort, and that only divine mercy can cleanse them.

### Amendment of Life

For Thomas à Kempis, penance is incomplete without amendment. Sorrow that does not produce change is sterile. "If every year we should root out one vice, we should soon become perfect men" (011-023). The work repeatedly links penitence to the concrete effort of reforming one's life: "Be thou sorry for thy sins, and purpose to amend thy life" (021-005). The penitent is not one who merely grieves but one who turns — actively, deliberately, and perseveringly — toward the good.

### The Penitential Psalms

The language of the penitential psalms pervades the devotional passages of *The Imitation*, especially in Book III where the soul pours out its sorrow before Christ. "Have mercy upon me, O God, according to Thy great mercy" is the unspoken prayer beneath every confession of unworthiness. The disciple who "hath true compunction of heart" joins the psalmist in the cry of the broken and contrite heart which God will not despise.

## Biblical Foundation

The sacrament of penance rests on Christ's explicit grant of the power to bind and loose: "Whatsoever thou shalt bind on earth shall be bound in heaven: and whatsoever thou shalt loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven" (Matthew 16:19). After the Resurrection, this power is specifically directed to the forgiveness of sins: "Whosesoever sins ye remit, they are remitted" (John 20:23). Saint James exhorts: "Confess your faults one to another, and pray one for another, that ye may be healed" (James 5:16). The entire Old Testament prophetic tradition calls Israel to penitence, and John the Baptist's ministry begins with "Repent ye, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (Matthew 3:2) — the same word with which Christ begins His public preaching.
