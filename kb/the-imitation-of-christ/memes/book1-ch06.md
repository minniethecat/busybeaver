[006-001] Of inordinate affections

[006-002] Whensoever a man desireth aught above measure, immediately he becometh

[006-003] restless. The proud and the avaricious man are never at rest; while the

[006-004] poor and lowly of heart abide in the multitude of peace. The man who is

[006-005] not yet wholly dead to self, is soon tempted, and is overcome in small

[006-006] and trifling matters. It is hard for him who is weak in spirit, and

[006-007] still in part carnal and inclined to the pleasures of sense, to

[006-008] withdraw himself altogether from earthly desires. And therefore, when

[006-009] he withdraweth himself from these, he is often sad, and easily angered

[006-010] too if any oppose his will.

[006-011] 2. But if, on the other hand, he yield to his inclination, immediately

[006-012] he is weighed down by the condemnation of his conscience; for that he

[006-013] hath followed his own desire, and yet in no way attained the peace

[006-014] which he hoped for. For true [[peace|peace of heart]] is to be found in resisting

[006-015] passion, not in yielding to it. And therefore there is no peace in the

[006-016] heart of a man who is carnal, nor in him who is given up to the things

[006-017] that are without him, but only in him who is fervent towards God and

[006-018] living the life of [[holy-spirit|the Spirit]].
