# Pride

The capital sin and root of all [[sin|vice]]s — the inordinate love of one's own excellence by which the soul exalts itself above God and above neighbour, refusing the creaturely posture of dependence, obedience, and [[humility|lowliness]] that is the foundation of all true virtue, in the teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Pride, in the theology of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is the primordial sin — the sin of Lucifer and of Adam, and the one from which all other [[sin|sins]] derive their energy. It is the soul's refusal to accept its status as a creature, its grasping after a height that belongs to God alone, and its restless impatience with any authority, correction, or obscurity that would remind it of its limits. Thomas treats pride not as one [[sin|vice]] among many but as the hidden spring of all disordered action and the great obstacle to every spiritual good. A whole chapter is devoted to "fleeing from vain hope and pride" (007-001), and the theme pervades the counsels on obedience, humility, and self-knowledge.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Pride appears throughout The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in multiple forms — intellectual pride, spiritual pride, pride of will, and pride of life — each of which Thomas diagnoses and remedies with the concrete asceticism of humility and obedience.

### The Many Faces of Pride

Thomas identifies pride operating under various guises. Intellectual pride manifests as "curiosity and pride" (041-027) in the pursuit of knowledge, where men "desire to be called learned and to be accounted wise" (041-030) rather than to become holy. This form of pride is especially dangerous because it can clothe itself in the appearance of virtue — the scholar who busies himself with subtle questions about God while neglecting the purification of his own heart is practicing pride under the name of theology.

Spiritual pride appears in the presumption that "springeth from pride and presumption" (020-023), where a man [[trust-in-god|trust]]s in his own strength and spiritual attainments rather than in [[grace|divine grace]]. Thomas warns that "the peace of the wicked" is not true peace at all but a false confidence born of pride. Even contemplation, the highest exercise of the spiritual life, can become an occasion of pride if the soul forgets that "all that is high is not holy" (035-021) and that the gift is from God, not from itself.

Pride of will — what Thomas calls simply "wilfulness" — is the stubborn refusal to yield one's own judgment to another. "To stand too stiffly in one's own opinion," Thomas writes, "is a mark of pride or wilfulness" (009-022). The proud man cannot bear to be contradicted, corrected, or placed in a position of [[obedience|submission]]; he "desireth to be above others" and "seeketh to be preferred before all" (041-027). This form of pride is the direct antagonist of obedience, and Thomas returns to it repeatedly in his counsels to monks and religious.

### The Consequences of Pride

Thomas treats pride as the great destroyer of the spiritual life. "Because of it many are led into error" (043-050) — error not only in thought but in the whole direction of life. Pride "nourisheth contentions" and "vain glory, whence envyings and dissensions arise" (095-017). It makes a man incapable of receiving correction, for the proud heart "despiseth others and will not be subject to them" (050-024). Most seriously, pride makes the soul an unfit habitation for grace, for "God resisteth the proud" — a text Thomas knew from both the Old and New Testaments — and the proud man, by exalting himself, ensures his own eventual humiliation.

The ultimate consequence of pride is self-deception on the most comprehensive scale. The proud man "thinketh himself to be something when he is nothing" and thus deceives himself (020-030). He measures himself by his own standard, compares himself with those he considers inferior, and congratulates himself on a spiritual eminence that exists only in his imagination. The cure for this is nothing less than the radical realism of humility, which sees oneself as one truly is before God.

### The Remedy of Pride

The remedy Thomas prescribes for pride is consistent and uncompromising: obedience, humility, and the deliberate acceptance of [[humility|lowliness]]. "Learn to obey, O dust! Learn to humble thyself, O earth and clay, and to bow thyself down under the feet of all" (050-020). The language is deliberately humiliating — "dust," "earth and clay" — because Thomas understands that pride is not reasoned out of the soul but driven out by the concrete practice of [[obedience|subjection]]. The proud man must do what his pride most resists: submit, be silent, accept correction, take the lowest place, and "cast away all pride of heart" (087-037).

The foundation of this remedy is the example of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself, who "emptied Himself, taking the form of a servant" (Philippians 2:7). The [[discipleship|disciple]] is instructed to "overcome thy pride" (050-020) by looking upon the humility of the Cross, where the God who had every right to exalt Himself chose instead the lowest abasement. Pride cannot survive the sustained contemplation of a crucified God.

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of pride in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] draws from the full sweep of Scripture. The root text is Proverbs 16:18: "Pride goeth before destruction, and a haughty spirit before a fall" — a verse that supplies the logic for Thomas's repeated warnings that the proud man is preparing his own ruin. The New Testament deepens this with James 4:6: "God resisteth the proud, but giveth grace unto the humble" (also cited at 1 Peter 5:5), which Thomas takes as the definitive statement of the spiritual law governing pride and humility.

The Lucifer narrative ([[isaiah|Isaiah]] 14:12-15) stands behind Thomas's understanding of pride as the primordial sin — the creature who said "I will ascend above the heights of the clouds; I will be like the most High" and was cast down to the pit. The teaching that "whosoever exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted" (Luke 14:11) is the dominical promise that grounds Thomas's asceticism of humility. And the "pride of life" named in 1 John 2:16 as one of the three things that are "of [[world|the world]], and not of the Father" connects the interior [[sin|vice]] to the whole fabric of worldly attachment that the [[discipleship|disciple]] must renounce.

- [[vanity]] — Pride and vanity are intimately connected; the proud man seeks his worth in the vain things that pass away
- [[obedience]] — The concrete practice by which pride is mortified; to obey is to renounce the wilfulness that pride nourishes
- [[self-denial]] — The daily discipline of saying no to the self that pride would exalt; self-denial is pride's negation
- [[wisdom]] — True wisdom is humble; the pride that "desireth to be called learned" is the enemy of the wisdom that comes from above
- [[grace]] — Pride drives out grace and makes the soul an unfit habitation for God; only the humble receive grace
- [[temptation]] — Pride exposes the soul to temptation, for the proud man [[trust-in-god|trust]]s in his own strength and falls
- [[sin]] — Pride is the root sin from which all actual [[sin|sins]] derive; to conquer pride is to strike at the head of the serpent
