# Last Judgment

The eschatological event at the consummation of history when Jesus Christ shall return in glory to judge all humanity, living and dead, rendering to every soul according to its deeds — the righteous to eternal life and the wicked to eternal punishment. In *The Imitation of Christ*, the Last Judgment serves as the ultimate horizon against which all spiritual striving is measured, the "day of judgment" toward which every admonition to repentance, self-denial, and holy living is directed.

## Biblical Account

The Last Judgment is revealed across both Old and New Testaments as the definitive act of divine justice at history's close. The prophetic books anticipate a "day of the Lord" when God will judge the nations (Joel 3:12-14; Daniel 12:2). In the New Testament, Jesus Himself gives the most detailed account in Matthew 25:31-46, where the Son of Man comes in glory with all the angels, sits upon the throne of His glory, and separates the nations as a shepherd separates sheep from goats — the righteous to the right hand, inheriting the kingdom prepared for them, and the wicked to the left, departing into everlasting fire prepared for the devil and his angels.

The Apostle Paul teaches that "we must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ, that each one may receive what is due him for the things done while in the body, whether good or bad" (2 Corinthians 5:10), and that "each of us shall give account of himself to God" (Romans 14:12). The Revelation of John describes the Great White Throne judgment, where "the dead, small and great, stand before God, and books were opened ... and the dead were judged out of those things which were written in the books, according to their works" (Revelation 20:12). The author of Hebrews declares it is "appointed unto men once to die, but after this the judgment" (Hebrews 9:27), while the Acts of the Apostles affirms that God "hath appointed a day, in the which he will judge the world in righteousness by that man whom he hath ordained" (Acts 17:31).

The sapiential tradition likewise attests to the reversal of fortunes at the judgment: "Then shall the righteous man stand in great boldness before the face of such as have afflicted him and made no account of his labours" (Wisdom 5:1) — a passage that Thomas a Kempis expressly cites in his meditation on the Last Judgment [(024-040), 024-041].

## Theological Significance

The Last Judgment is the definitive manifestation of divine justice, truth, and mercy. It reveals that history is not morally neutral or cyclical but moves toward a divinely appointed end in which all hidden things shall be brought to light — every secret thought, every unrepented sin, and every forgotten act of mercy. The doctrine affirms that God is not indifferent to evil: the wrongs that seem to escape redress in this life shall be judged with perfect righteousness. At the same time, the judgment is inseparable from the mercy of Christ, for the standard by which the righteous are welcomed into the kingdom is not mere human merit but the works of love performed for "the least of these" — works themselves the fruit of grace (Matthew 25:40).

The Last Judgment also reveals the finality of human choice. It seals forever the orientation of each soul toward God or away from God, making definitive what was provisional in earthly life. The two outcomes — eternal life and eternal punishment — stand as the boundary markers of all Christian moral theology, giving ultimate weight to every decision made in time.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Last Judgment pervades *The Imitation of Christ* as the master-motif of spiritual urgency. Thomas a Kempis calls the disciple to live always with the judgment in view, for it strips away every illusion and exposes every self-deception. The most sustained treatment appears in Book I, Chapter 24, "Of the Judgment and Punishment of the Wicked," but references to the Day of Judgment recur throughout the work, especially in connection with death, the knowledge of truth, and the fear of God.

### The Certainty and Character of the Judgment

Thomas a Kempis opens his meditation on the judgment by urging the disciple to "remember the end, and how thou wilt stand before a strict judge, from whom nothing is hid, who is not bribed with gifts, nor accepteth excuses, but will judge righteous judgment" [(024-002), 024-003, 024-004]. The judge is omniscient, impartial, and incorruptible — no human advocacy, no excuse, and no bribe can sway His verdict. Each soul must answer alone: "Why dost thou not provide for thyself against the day of judgment, when no man shall be able to be excused or defended by means of another, but each one shall bear his burden himself alone?" [(024-007), 024-008, 024-009].

This insistence on individual accountability strips the disciple of every false comfort. The standard of judgment is not reputation, learning, or worldly status, but the actual deeds of a life. In Book I, Chapter 3, Thomas a Kempis warns: "Of a surety, at the Day of Judgment it will be demanded of us, not what we have read, but what we have done; not how well we have spoken, but how holily we have lived" [(003-045), 003-046, 003-047, 003-048]. The judgment is the great equalizer that exposes the vanity of all merely human achievement.

### The Present Life as Preparation

For Thomas a Kempis, the Last Judgment transforms the present life into a season of urgent preparation. "Now doth thy labour bring forth fruit, now is thy weeping acceptable, thy groaning heard, thy sorrow well pleasing to God, and cleansing to thy soul" [(024-010), 024-011, 024-012]. The "now" echoes throughout the chapter — the opportunity for repentance, for patient endurance, for the purifying of the soul — is bounded by the coming judgment. What is not done now cannot be done then. "Better is it now to purify the soul from sin, than to cling to sins from which we must be purged hereafter. Truly we deceive ourselves by the inordinate love which we bear towards the flesh" [(024-020), 024-021, 024-022].

The author explicitly counsels anticipatory penitence as the wise response: "Be thou anxious now and sorrowful for thy sins, that in the day of judgment thou mayest have boldness with the blessed" [(024-038), 024-039]. The boldness of the righteous at the judgment is not presumption but the confidence born of a life lived in sorrow for sin and pursuit of holiness.

### The Great Reversal

A defining feature of Thomas a Kempis's treatment of the Last Judgment is the theme of reversal — the complete overturning of worldly values and appearances. "Then shall it be seen that he was the wise man in this world who learned to be a fool and despised for Christ" [(024-045), 024-046]. Everything that the world esteems — wealth, status, pleasure, learning — shall be shown worthless, while everything the world despises — humility, patience, poverty of spirit, silent endurance — shall be vindicated:

> "Then the humble garment shall put on beauty, and the precious robe shall hide itself as vile. Then the little poor cottage shall be more commended than the gilded palace. Then enduring patience shall have more might than all the power of the world. Then simple obedience shall be more highly exalted than all worldly wisdom."
[(024-050), 024-051, 024-052, 024-053, 024-054, 024-055]

This reversal extends even to the interior life: "Then a pure and good conscience shall more rejoice than learned philosophy. Then contempt of riches shall have more weight than all the treasure of the children of this world" [(024-056), 024-057, 024-058]. The judgment does not merely punish and reward; it manifests the true order of value that the world has inverted.

### The Humble Judge and the Proud

One of the most striking theological moves in Chapter 24 is the identification of the humble disciple with the judging Christ. Thomas a Kempis writes: "Then shall he stand up to judge, he who now submitteth himself in humility to the judgments of men. Then shall the poor and humble man have great confidence, while the proud is taken with fear on every side" [(024-042), 024-043, 024-044]. The one who humbly accepted unjust judgment in this life shall share in Christ's own authority at the Last Judgment — a daring promise grounded in Matthew 19:28 and 1 Corinthians 6:2-3.

### Punishment of the Wicked

Thomas a Kempis does not soften the severity of the judgment for the unrepentant. No sin escapes: "No sin will there be which shall not be visited with its own proper punishment. The proud shall be filled with utter confusion, and the covetous shall be pinched with miserable poverty" [(024-032), 024-033, 024-034]. Each vice receives its fitting torment: "the slothful be pricked forward with burning goads, and the gluttons be tormented with intolerable hunger and thirst" [(024-027), 024-028, 024-029]. The proportional correspondence between sin and punishment — "For wherein a man hath sinned, therein shall he be the more heavily punished" [(024-026), 024-027] — reveals a divinely ordered justice in which the punishment fits the crime.

The temporal calculus is devastating: "An hour's pain there shall be more grievous than a hundred years here of the bitterest penitence" [(024-035), 024-036]. This is not speculative cruelty but pastoral urgency — the author drives his reader to repent now rather than suffer eternally.

### Love as the Antidote to Fear of Judgment

The chapter closes with a profound turn from fear to love. Thomas a Kempis acknowledges the pastoral utility of fearing judgment — "it is a good thing, if love as yet cannot restrain thee from evil, that at least the fear of hell should hold thee back" [(024-078), 024-079] — but the ultimate answer to the terror of judgment is perfect love for God: "For he who loveth God with all his heart feareth not death, nor punishment, nor judgment, nor hell, because perfect love giveth sure access to God" [(024-074), 024-075, 024-076]. The judgment remains, but the one united to God by charity passes through it not as a terror but as a vindication. "But he who putteth aside the fear of God cannot long continue in good, but shall quickly fall into the snares of the devil" [(024-079), 024-080, 024-081].

This final movement — from fear to love, from terror to confidence — is the essence of the Kempian teaching on the Last Judgment. The judgment is not abolished; it is transfigured by love into the moment when every hidden act of patience, humility, and charity is brought into the light and crowned.

## Liturgical Connection

The Last Judgment figures prominently in the traditional liturgical calendar. In the Western tradition, the final Sundays of the liturgical year — particularly the Sundays before Advent in the Extraordinary Form — are devoted to the contemplation of the Last Judgment, with the Gospel of Matthew 24 (the destruction of Jerusalem and the signs of the end) and Matthew 25 (the parable of the ten virgins and the separation of sheep and goats) read successively. The *Dies Irae*, the great sequence of the Requiem Mass attributed to Thomas of Celano, is a sustained meditation on the terror and hope of the Day of Wrath: "*Dies irae, dies illa, solvet saeclum in favilla, teste David cum Sibylla*" ("Day of wrath, that day shall dissolve the world in ashes, as David and the Sibyl testify").

In the Eastern tradition, the Sunday of the Last Judgment (Meatfare Sunday) is celebrated two Sundays before Great Lent, with the Gospel reading from Matthew 25:31-46, calling the faithful to works of mercy in preparation for the judgment. The kontakion of the day confesses: "When Thou, O God, shalt come to earth with glory, all things shall tremble, and a river of fire shall flow before Thy judgment seat."

## Practical Discipline of Eschatological Awareness

The Kempian teaching on the Last Judgment is not a speculation about the distant future but a practical discipline for the present moment. Thomas à Kempis expects the disciple to live *now* in such a way that the judgment, whenever it comes, will find the soul prepared. This eschatological orientation shapes the daily practice of the devout life in four concrete ways.

### The Discipline of the Present Moment

The most immediate practical consequence of the Last Judgment is the urgency it imparts to the present. "Now doth thy labour bring forth fruit, now is thy weeping acceptable" (024-010, 024-011) — the repeated "now" is the keynote of Kempian eschatology. The judgment is not a distant event for which one can prepare later; it is the horizon that gives every present moment its eternal weight. This conviction transforms the ordinary duties of the day — prayer, work, meals, conversation, rest — into materials for judgment. Each act is either an investment in the kingdom or a withdrawal from it, and there is no neutral ground.

The discipline of the present moment involves asking, at intervals throughout the day: *If I were to stand before Christ the Judge at this moment, what in my heart would condemn me? What would speak for me?* This is not morbid introspection but the vigilance that Christ Himself commanded: "Watch therefore, for ye know neither the day nor the hour" (Matthew 25:13). The Imitation trains the disciple in this vigilance, not to produce anxiety but to produce readiness.

### Anticipatory Penitence

The most distinctive practical counsel in Thomas à Kempis's treatment of the Last Judgment is the practice of anticipatory penitence. "Be thou anxious now and sorrowful for thy sins, that in the day of judgment thou mayest have boldness with the blessed" (024-038, 024-039). The logic is compelling: the judgment will expose every sin; therefore, expose them now, in the safe context of sacramental confession and private contrition, rather than in the terrifying context of the public judgment.

This counsel gave shape to the penitential practices of the *devotio moderna*: frequent confession (not merely annual, as the medieval minimum required, but monthly or even weekly), the daily examination of conscience, the cultivation of the gift of tears (*donum lacrimarum*) as a sign of true contrition, and the regular meditation on the Four Last Things — death, judgment, heaven, and hell. The goal was not to live in fear but to do the work of repentance so thoroughly that the judgment would arrive as vindication rather than terror.

### Works of Mercy as Foretaste of the Judgment

The Gospel account of the Last Judgment (Matthew 25:31-46) makes the works of mercy — feeding the hungry, giving drink to the thirsty, welcoming the stranger, clothing the naked, visiting the sick and imprisoned — the very content of the judgment. Thomas à Kempis does not develop this theme at length (his audience was primarily monastic rather than engaged in active corporal works of mercy), but the logic is implicit in his appeal to the judgment as the standard by which all deeds are measured.

The disciple who takes the Last Judgment seriously will examine the day not only for sins to repent but for opportunities of mercy that were neglected. "I was hungry, and ye gave Me no meat" (Matthew 25:42) — the sins of omission weigh as heavily at the judgment as the sins of commission, and the devout life includes the deliberate cultivation of charity in action. For the Kempian religious, this might mean kindness to an irritable brother, patience with a slow novice, or faithful intercession for the living and the dead. For the lay disciple, it extends to every sphere of daily responsibility.

### The Gift of Holy Fear

Thomas à Kempis closes his meditation on the Last Judgment not with terror but with the gift of holy fear — that "fear of the Lord" which Scripture calls the beginning of wisdom (Proverbs 9:10; Psalm 111:10). This fear is not servile dread but a reverent awe that preserves the soul from presumption and keeps it attentive to God. "He who putteth aside the fear of God cannot long continue in good, but shall quickly fall into the snares of the devil" (024-079, 024-080, 024-081).

The practical cultivation of holy fear involves meditating regularly on the judgment, not as a threat but as a truth — the truth that everything hidden will be revealed, that every act has eternal consequence, and that the judge is also the savior. The goal is not to live in terror of a stern tribunal but to live in the sober confidence that comes from having made preparation — having confessed one's sins, practiced mercy, and entrusted oneself to the love that "casteth out fear" (1 John 4:18). The soul that has done this work can, with Kempis, look toward the judgment and say with the ancient Church: *Maranatha — come, Lord Jesus* (Revelation 22:20).
