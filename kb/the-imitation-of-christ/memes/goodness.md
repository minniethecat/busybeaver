# Goodness

The perfection of God by which He is infinitely beneficent — the bountiful source from whom "every good gift and every perfect gift" descends, and the attribute that Thomas à Kempis most frequently invokes as the ground of the soul's [[trust-in-god|trust]], gratitude, and approach to the Holy Table. "How plentiful is Thy goodness which Thou hast laid up for them that fear Thee!" (047-004): this exclamation captures the characteristic Kempian tone of wonder at the sheer abundance of divine beneficence.

## Definition

Goodness, in the vocabulary of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], belongs first and properly to God alone. All created goodness is derivative, a participation in the infinite goodness of the Creator. "There is no creature so small and vile but that it showeth us the goodness of God" (029-010) — the entire created order is a mirror reflecting divine beneficence. Thomas's understanding of goodness is dynamic rather than static: God's goodness is not a passive quality but an active, outpouring generosity that "never ceaseth to do good even to ingrates and to those who are turned far from Thee" (045-025 to 045-026). It is goodness that gives without respect of persons, that "pourest forth Thy gifts so richly, so freely and largely, without respect of persons" (059-024 to 059-025). The [[discipleship|disciple]]'s proper response to such goodness is twofold: humble [[trust-in-god|trust]] and grateful praise.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The goodness of God is one of the great recurring themes of The Imitation, appearing across all four books and reaching its fullest expression in the prayers surrounding [[communion|Holy Communion]] in Book IV.

### Creation as Mirror of Goodness

Thomas develops a remarkable theology of creation in a single sentence: "If thine heart were right, then should every creature be a mirror of life and a book of holy doctrine. There is no creature so small and vile but that it showeth us the goodness of God" (029-008 to 029-010). This is the sacramental imagination at its purest: the visible world is not a distraction from God but a revelation of Him, provided the heart is purified to see it aright. The "small and vile" creature — a sparrow, a wildflower, a drop of rain — becomes a theophany, a visible sign of invisible goodness. Thomas's point is that the problem is not with creation but with the beholder: "If thou wert good and pure within, then wouldst thou look upon all things without hurt and understand them aright" (029-012 to 029-013). The purified heart sees goodness everywhere; the corrupted heart sees only occasions for sin.

### Goodness as Ground of Confidence

Thomas repeatedly appeals to divine goodness as the basis for the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s [[trust-in-god|trust]]. When the soul approaches [[communion|Holy Communion]], it does so "[[trust-in-god|trust]]ing in Thy goodness and great [[forgiveness|mercy]]" (098-003) — not in its own worthiness, which it knows to be nonexistent, but in the character of the God who invites it. The [[discipleship|disciple]] confesses: "I come to Thee sick to the Healer, hungering and thirsting to the Fountain of life, poverty-stricken to the King of heaven" (098-004 to 098-005). Every one of these titles presumes the goodness of God: a healer who would not heal, a fountain that would not quench thirst, a king who would not relieve poverty, would be a contradiction. The [[discipleship|disciple]]'s confidence rests on the certainty that God's goodness is as reliable as His being — that He cannot not be good, and therefore cannot turn away the soul that comes to Him in need.

This confidence extends beyond the Sacrament to every circumstance of life. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who has received fewer gifts than another "ought not to be cast down, nor to take it amiss, nor to envy him who is richer; but rather ought he to look unto Thee, and to greatly extol Thy goodness" (059-022 to 059-024). The equality of God's goodness — "without respect of persons" (059-025) — means that every soul receives exactly what it needs for its salvation. The comparison that breeds envy is a failure to see that God's goodness is tailored to each soul's particular vocation.

### The Goodness of God in the Eucharist

The prayers of Book IV represent the high point of Thomas's theology of divine goodness. Here goodness and the Sacrament converge: the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] is the supreme expression of God's beneficence, the gift in which the Giver gives not merely something but Himself. Thomas prays: "According to the greatness of Thy goodness and the multitude of Thy mercies look upon me, and hear the prayer of Thy poor servant" (096-038 to 096-039). The "greatness of Thy goodness" is the measure by which God is asked to act — not according to the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s merits, which are negligible, but according to the boundless scale of His own character.

Thomas explicitly links goodness to the mystery of predestination and grace: "I foreknew my beloved ones from everlasting, I chose them out of [[world|the world]]; they did not choose Me. I called them by grace, I drew them by [[forgiveness|mercy]]; by the blessings of My goodness I prevented them" (095-027 to 095-028). Goodness here is prevenient — it goes before, preparing the soul for grace before the soul knows to ask. The saints are what they are not because they were inherently better than others but because divine goodness "prevented them with the blessings of My goodness" (095-026 to 095-027). This is Thomas's most concentrated statement of the Augustinian doctrine of grace: all merit is rooted in prevenient goodness.

### The Response to Goodness: Gratitude and Praise

Thomas insists that the recognition of divine goodness must issue in gratitude. "Blessed be Thou, O my God, because though I be unworthy of all Thy benefits, Thy bountiful and infinite goodness never ceaseth to do good" (045-024 to 045-025). The unworthiness of the recipient only magnifies the goodness of the Giver: that God should continue to pour out benefits on those who "are turned far from Thee" (045-026) is the supreme proof that His goodness is essential to His nature, not dependent on the merit of its objects. The proper response is "to look unto Thee, and to greatly extol Thy goodness" (059-024) — that is, to turn the very gifts back toward the Giver in praise.

The [[discipleship|disciple]]'s gratitude finds expression in the language of wonder: "Oh how plentiful is Thy goodness which Thou hast laid up for them that fear Thee!" (047-004). Thomas borrows the exclamatory mode of the Psalms to convey the sheer disproportion between what the [[discipleship|disciple]] deserves and what he receives. The goodness of God is "plentiful" — abundant, overflowing, more than sufficient. It is "laid up" — stored, reserved, kept secure for those who fear Him. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who grasps this lives not in anxiety about his own resources but in confident expectation of divine provision.

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of goodness in The Imitation is deeply rooted in the Psalter and the wisdom literature. "Oh how plentiful is Thy goodness which Thou hast laid up for them that fear Thee" (047-004) is a direct echo of Psalm 31:19: "Oh how great is Thy goodness, which Thou hast laid up for them that fear Thee." The insistence that creation manifests divine goodness reflects Psalm 145:9: "The [[jesus-christ|Lord]] is good to all: and His tender mercies are over all His works." The affirmation that "every creature" is "a mirror of life and a book of holy doctrine" (029-009) echoes Romans 1:20 — "the invisible things of Him from the creation of [[world|the world]] are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made" — and the wisdom tradition's conviction that "out of the greatness and beauty of [[creation|created things]], by way of analogy, the author of their being is perceived" ([[wisdom|Wisdom]] 13:5, LXX).

The teaching that God's goodness is prevenient — "by the blessings of My goodness I prevented them" (095-027) — draws on the Pauline doctrine of election in Ephesians 1:4-6: "He hath chosen us in Him before the foundation of [[world|the world]]... to the praise of the glory of His grace." The note that God's goodness extends "even to ingrates" (045-026) echoes Luke 6:35: "He is kind unto the unthankful and to the evil." The confidence that the [[discipleship|disciple]] may approach God "[[trust-in-god|trust]]ing in Thy goodness" (098-003) rests on James 1:17: "Every good gift and every perfect gift is from above, and cometh down from the Father of lights, with whom is no variableness."
