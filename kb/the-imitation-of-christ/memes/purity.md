# Purity

The quality of the soul that is free from the defilement of sin and wholly ordered toward God — in The Imitation of Christ, purity is not merely the absence of vice but the positive orientation of the whole person toward divine things, intimately linked with [[simplicity]] of intention and the capacity to "apprehend and taste" God.

## Definition

In the spiritual vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, purity denotes the soul's cleanness before God — a state in which the will is undivided, the conscience is unburdened, and the affections are directed toward their true end. It comprehends both the negative aspect (freedom from sin and defilement) and the positive aspect (the capacity for intimate communion with God).

Thomas à Kempis links purity inseparably with [[simplicity]]: "Simplicity ought to be in the intention, purity in the affection. Simplicity reacheth towards God, purity apprehendeth Him and tasteth Him" (029-003–004). These two virtues form the interior architecture of the [[contemplation|contemplative]] life: simplicity gathers the scattered powers of the soul into a single intention, and purity enables the soul to receive the divine sweetness that is its reward.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation treats purity as the indispensable condition for approaching God, and as a gift that must be continually guarded because it is never secure apart from divine preservation.

### Purity and Simplicity

Book II, Chapter IV establishes the twin pillars of the interior life: "Simplicity ought to be in the intention, purity in the affection. Simplicity reacheth towards God, purity apprehendeth Him and tasteth Him" (029-003–004). The distinction is precise: simplicity is the arrow pointed at God; purity is the eye that sees the target. One without the other is incomplete — simplicity without purity becomes rigid and dry; purity without simplicity becomes sentimental and diffuse.

The passage continues: "No good work will be difficult to thee if thou be inwardly free from inordinate affection" (029-005). Purity is thus not only the condition for [[contemplation]] but the secret of ease in action — the soul that is not entangled in disordered affections moves freely toward whatever God asks.

### The Precarity of Purity

The Imitation of Christ is sober about the fragility of purity. Book III, Chapter XIV acknowledges that "no purity is secure, if Thou dost cease to preserve" (051-013). This is not a counsel of despair but of [[humility]]: the purest soul on earth is pure only by the continuous preservation of [[grace]]. The moment the soul presumes upon its own purity, it has already begun to lose it — for the root of impurity is pride, and the first mark of authentic purity is the acknowledgment that it is wholly a gift.

Book IV deepens this teaching in a eucharistic context. Christ declares: "I am the Lover of purity, and Giver of sanctity. I seek a pure heart, and there is the place of My rest" (108-004). The divine Lover is drawn to purity as to His proper dwelling; but the purity He loves is one He Himself creates. The soul that would host Christ must receive from Christ the purity that makes it a fit habitation.

### Angelic Purity and Human Frailty

Book IV, Chapter I sets an impossibly high standard only to relativize it: "If thou hadst angelic purity and the holiness of holy John the Baptist, thou wouldst not be worthy to receive or handle this Sacrament" (101-003). Even the purity of the angels is insufficient to approach the Eucharist worthily — the point is not to discourage the [[disciple]] but to expose the futility of approaching God on the basis of one's own worthiness. Purity is real, but it is never sufficient; the [[Eucharist|sacrament]] is received not because the soul is pure enough but because [[grace]] supplies what purity lacks.

### Purity and the Beatific Vision

The promise of purity is vision: "purity apprehendeth Him and tasteth Him" (029-004). This is the fulfillment of the dominical beatitude — "Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God" (Matthew 5:8). In The Imitation, purity is ordered toward vision: the pure soul sees God in this life by faith, in the [[Eucharist]] by sacramental presence, and in the next life by face-to-face vision. Purity is thus not an end in itself but the clearing of the eye through which God becomes visible.

## Biblical Foundation

Christ's beatitude — "Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God" (Matthew 5:8) — is the scriptural center around which the Kempian teaching on purity orbits. David's penitential cry — "Create in me a clean heart, O God" (Psalm 51:10) — acknowledges that purity is a divine work, not a human achievement. Paul identifies the end of the commandment as "love from a pure heart, from a good conscience, and from sincere faith" (1 Timothy 1:5). James exhorts: "Draw near to God and He will draw near to you. Cleanse your hands, you sinners; and purify your hearts, you double-minded" (James 4:8). John writes that "everyone who has this hope in Him purifies himself, just as He is pure" (1 John 3:3) — purity is both gift and task, received from Christ and pursued by the [[disciple]].

## Related Concepts

- [[holiness]] — the comprehensive sanctity of which purity is a dimension
- [[conscience]] — the interior tribunal that purity keeps clean
- [[humility]] — the guardian of purity, which prevents the soul from presuming on its own cleanness
- [[contemplation]] — the vision of God to which purity is ordered

## Practical Application

The cultivation of purity, as taught in The Imitation, is not primarily a matter of external discipline but of interior ordering. The [[disciple]] keeps the intention simple (directed solely toward God) and the affection pure (free from inordinate attachment to creatures). This requires daily self-examination, frequent recourse to the Sacrament of [[confession]], and the humble acknowledgment that "no purity is secure" apart from God's preserving [[grace]] (051-013). The reward is proportioned to the effort: the pure heart sees God — dimly now by faith, truly now in the [[Eucharist]], and perfectly at last in the beatific vision.
