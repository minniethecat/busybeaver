# John the Apostle

One of the Twelve Apostles, the beloved disciple who reclined on Christ's breast at the Last Supper, author of the Fourth Gospel, three Epistles, and Revelation — John stands among the most significant figures in the New Testament and serves as the most cited scriptural authority throughout The Imitation of Christ.

## Identity

John, son of Zebedee and brother of James the Greater, was a Galilean fisherman called by Christ alongside his brother while mending their nets by the Sea of Galilee. Together with Peter and James, he belonged to the innermost circle of the Twelve, present at the Transfiguration, the raising of Jairus's daughter, and the agony in Gethsemane. He is identified in the Fourth Gospel as "the disciple whom Jesus loved" — the beloved disciple — and Christian tradition holds that he is the author of the Gospel that bears his name, the three Johannine Epistles, and the Book of Revelation.

John's epithet in the tradition is "the Theologian" — not because he produced a systematic theology but because his Gospel soars to the highest contemplation of the Word made flesh. Where the Synoptic Gospels begin with earthly genealogy or prophetic ministry, John opens with eternity: "In the beginning was the Word." His writings are distinguished by their profound theology of divine love: "God is love, and he who abides in love abides in God, and God abides in him" (1 John 4:16).

In Christian iconography, John is represented by the eagle — the evangelist who gazes upon the highest mysteries of the divinity of Christ. He is the only apostle traditionally believed to have died a natural death, in extreme old age at Ephesus, having outlived all his companions and having distilled the Gospel message to a single refrain: "Little children, love one another."

## Biblical Narrative

John's story spans the Gospels, Acts, and his own writings. He was present at the key moments of Christ's ministry and, after Pentecost, became a pillar of the Jerusalem Church alongside Peter. His later years in Ephesus produced the writings that would shape Christian theology more deeply than perhaps any other corpus outside the teaching of Christ himself.

### Key Events

- **The Call by the Sea of Galilee** — John and his brother James were called from their fishing nets to become "fishers of men," leaving their father Zebedee to follow Christ without hesitation (Matthew 4:21-22; Mark 1:19-20). Christ named the brothers "Boanerges," Sons of Thunder (Mark 3:17), a mark of their impetuous zeal later transformed into the gentleness of love.

- **The Beloved Disciple at the Last Supper** — John reclined next to Christ at the Last Supper, leaning on His breast, and at Peter's prompting asked who would betray the Lord (John 13:23-25). This posture of intimate friendship — the disciple who rests upon the heart of Christ — became the defining image of John's relationship to the Lord and the model of contemplative intimacy with God that permeates The Imitation of Christ.

- **At the Foot of the Cross** — John alone among the Twelve stood at the foot of the Cross, where Christ entrusted His mother to him: "Woman, behold your son" and to John, "Behold your mother" (John 19:26-27). This moment established John as the model of faithful love that remains when all others flee, and from that hour he took Mary into his own home.

- **Witness of the Resurrection** — John outran Peter to the empty tomb on Easter morning, and stooping to look in, "he saw and believed" (John 20:8). The beloved disciple was the first to grasp the meaning of the empty tomb — faith born not from seeing the risen Lord but from the signs He left behind. Later, by the Sea of Tiberias, John was the first to recognize the risen Christ on the shore: "It is the Lord!" (John 21:7).

- **The Post-Pentecost Church** — After Pentecost, John stood with Peter before the Sanhedrin, and the rulers "perceived that they were uneducated, common men, they were astonished; and they recognized that they had been with Jesus" (Acts 4:13). John's boldness in witness and his partnership with Peter in the early Church established the apostolic foundation upon which the Church was built.

- **The Revelation on Patmos** — In his old age, exiled to the island of Patmos "on account of the word of God and the testimony of Jesus," John received the great apocalyptic vision of the Book of Revelation (Revelation 1:9). The book that closes the Christian canon opens with John's own witness as its seal: "He who saw it has borne witness, and his testimony is true" (Revelation 1:1-2).

## In The Imitation of Christ

No biblical author is cited more frequently in The Imitation of Christ than John. His Gospel, Epistles, and Revelation supply the work with its most characteristic theological vocabulary: abiding in Christ, the gift of peace, the commandment of love, the way of truth, and the promise of eternal life. Thomas a Kempis draws on John not merely as a proof-text but as the apostle whose writings articulate the interior, contemplative, love-centered spirituality that The Imitation of Christ seeks to cultivate.

### The Beloved Disciple as Model

Thomas a Kempis invokes the collective memory of the apostles in their humility and suffering, noting that Christ made them "princes in all lands" yet "they had their conversation in this world blameless, so humble and meek, without any malice or deceit" (059-037 to 059-039). But it is Christ's own words about His disciples that carry the weight of John's particular identity: "As My Father hath loved Me, so love I you; thus have I spoken unto My beloved disciples: whom I sent forth not unto worldly joys, but to great strivings; not unto honours, but unto contempt; not unto ease, but to labours; not unto rest, but to bring forth much fruit with patience" (067-055 to 067-058).

John, the disciple whom Jesus loved, is the paradigmatic recipient of this commissioning. His life — from the fishing boat to the foot of the Cross, from the empty tomb to the exile on Patmos — embodies the pattern: not worldly joy but great striving, not honour but contempt, not ease but labour, not rest but fruit-bearing patience. The beloved disciple did not receive comfort but mission, and his writings testify that the love of God and the suffering of the disciple are not opposed but united in the mystery of abiding.

### The Gospel of Love

The Imitation of Christ draws its theology of love directly from the Johannine writings. Christ's words in John xv.9 — "As the Father hath loved Me, so love I you" — are cited as the divine warrant for the disciple's confidence in tribulation (067-055). The commandment of John xv.4 — "Abide in Me, and I in you" — anchors the work's teaching on union with Christ through sacramental devotion: "He who eateth My flesh and drinketh My blood abideth in Me and I in him" (109-041).

The great Johannine declaration "I am the way, the truth, and the life; no one cometh unto the Father but by Me" (John xiv.6) is invoked as the ultimate answer to the soul's seeking (093-048). And the Johannine promise of the Spirit's teaching — "He will guide you into all truth" (John xvi.13) — undergirds the entire project of The Imitation as a conversation between Christ the Interior Teacher and the listening disciple.

### The Theology of Peace

John xiv.27 — "Peace I leave with you; My peace I give unto you" — is the most frequently cited Johannine verse in The Imitation of Christ (062-039, 067-060). This peace is not the world's peace but the interior rest of the soul united to God. For Thomas a Kempis, John's record of this dominical promise anchors the entire spirituality of detachment and interior recollection: the peace Christ gives is the peace the disciple must guard against the turbulence of outward circumstance. The Imitation's great theme of interior consolation — its coming, its withdrawal, its renewal — finds its scriptural ground in this verse.

### The Eucharist and the Bread of Life

John's discourse on the Bread of Life (John 6) provides the theological foundation for Book IV of The Imitation of Christ. Christ's words — "I am the living bread which came down from heaven; if any man eat of this bread, he shall live for ever; and the bread that I will give is My flesh, which I will give for the life of the world" (John vi.51) — are cited directly in the context of worthy reception of the Sacrament (096-055, 096-056, 098-066). The Johannine identification of Christ as the Bread of Life is the scriptural anchor for the work's most solemn teaching on the Eucharist.

### John's Epistle and Detachment from the World

The First Epistle of John supplies The Imitation with its sharpest warning against worldly attachment: "Love not the world, neither the things that are in the world. If any man love the world, the love of the Father is not in him" (1 John ii.15), cited at 071-041. This verse crystallizes the ascetical demand at the heart of The Imitation — the renunciation of creaturely consolations for the sake of divine love. John's theology of love as both gift and commandment (1 John iv.19: "We love because He first loved us") informs the work's insistence that true devotion is a response to prevenient grace.

### The Apocalyptic Vision

The Book of Revelation enters The Imitation primarily through its promises to the faithful: the hidden manna and the white stone with a new name (Revelation ii.17, cited at 001-038 and 072-035), the anointing of the eyes with eye-salve (Revelation iii.18, cited at 069-032), and the heavenly worship of the elders casting their crowns before the throne (Revelation iv.10, cited at 095-101). These apocalyptic images serve Thomas a Kempis as figures of the soul's hidden communion with God, the illumination of interior vision, and the self-emptying worship that awaits the faithful in the heavenly Jerusalem.

### Contrast with John the Baptist

The Imitation of Christ distinguishes between two Johns: John the Baptist, "the most excellent of Saints" and blessed forerunner, named explicitly (101-003, 113-026); and John the Apostle, whose writings supply the scriptural fabric of the work. This distinction is significant: the Baptist represents the ascetical preparation — the voice crying in the wilderness — while the Apostle represents the contemplative consummation, the disciple who rests upon Christ's heart and beholds the Word made flesh.

## Spiritual Significance

John the Apostle embodies the contemplative dimension of the Christian life. Where Peter represents the active, pastoral, and institutional dimension of the Church, John represents the interior, mystical, and theological dimension — the soul's direct and loving gaze upon the mystery of the Incarnate Word. The eagle, John's evangelist symbol, soars higher than any other creature in the bestiary of the four evangelists, and his Gospel soars higher than any other in its contemplation of Christ's divinity.

### The Apostle of Love

John is called the Apostle of Love not merely because he wrote more extensively on the theme than any other New Testament author but because his entire life and witness were transformed by the love of Christ. The son of thunder who wished to call down fire on the Samaritan village became the aged apostle whose single sermon, repeated to the point of tedium, was "Little children, love one another." This transformation — from natural zeal to supernatural charity — is the very pattern of spiritual growth that The Imitation of Christ seeks to effect in every disciple.

### Contemplation and Intimacy

John's posture at the Last Supper — leaning on Jesus' breast — is the archetype of contemplative intimacy. He does not merely listen to Christ's words; he rests upon the source from which those words flow. This image, dear to the medieval devotional tradition in which Thomas a Kempis stood, represents the highest state of the soul: not activity for God but receptivity to God, not speaking about Christ but resting in Christ. The Imitation of Christ draws the disciple toward this same intimacy through its dialogical form, in which the soul speaks directly with the Interior Master.

### The Witness Who Remains

Christ's cryptic word about John — "If it is my will that he remain until I come, what is that to you?" (John xxi.22) — is cited at (061-020) and gave rise to the tradition that John did not die but sleeps at Ephesus awaiting the Lord's return. Whether understood literally or spiritually, this saying points to John's enduring role: he is the witness who remains, whose testimony abides when all else has passed. His Gospel ends with the declaration "and we know that his testimony is true" (John xxi.24), and his Revelation opens with the same claim (Revelation i.2). For The Imitation of Christ, the truth of John's witness is verified not in argument but in the transformation it works in the soul that receives it.
