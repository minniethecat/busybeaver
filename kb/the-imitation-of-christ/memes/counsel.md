# Counsel

Wise spiritual guidance sought from others — in The Imitation of Christ, counsel is both a remedy for [[temptation]] and a safeguard against the self-deception to which every soul is prone, for "it is better to ask counsel than to give it" (009-020).

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, counsel is the practice of seeking wisdom and direction from those who possess experience, discretion, and spiritual maturity. Thomas à Kempis treats counsel not as a sign of weakness or indecision but as an exercise of [[humility]]: the soul that seeks counsel acknowledges the limits of its own judgment and submits itself to the wisdom of others, and through them to the wisdom of God.

Counsel is closely related to [[obedience]] and stands in opposition to [[pride]], which resists external guidance and insists upon the sufficiency of its own understanding. The text's emphasis on seeking counsel reflects the conviction that the spiritual life is not a solitary enterprise — it is lived within the community of those who have gone before and can guide those who follow.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation of Christ commends the practice of seeking counsel as a remedy for temptation, a means of spiritual progress, and a protection against the dangers of self-will.

### Counsel as Safeguard Against Self-Deception

Book I teaches that one should "take counsel with a man who is wise and of good conscience" (004-011). The specification is important: counsel is only as valuable as the counselor. One must seek not merely any opinion but the guidance of those whose wisdom has been tested and whose conscience is formed.

The text also counsels humility in the role of counselor: "it is better to ask counsel than to give it" (009-020). This is a sharp word against the presumption of those who are quick to direct others while neglecting their own need for guidance. The one who readily offers counsel to all may be acting from [[pride]] rather than love; the one who seeks counsel acts from [[humility]].

### Counsel in the Midst of Temptation

Book I, Chapter XIII advises that one under temptation should "often seek counsel" (013-028). Temptation distorts perception; the soul in the grip of a powerful temptation loses the clarity to judge its own situation. The counsel of a wise and trusted person provides an external perspective that can pierce through the confusion.

The same passage adds: "and deal not hardly with one that is tempted, but give him such words of comfort as thou wouldst wish to be spoken to thyself" (013-028). This is the flip side of seeking counsel — the obligation to offer it with gentleness and compassion, not harshness or superiority.

### Counsel and the Ordering of Life

Book I also connects counsel to the ordering of daily life: "those who rule themselves after the counsel of the wise may easily be kept in peace" (044-030). The soul that governs itself by wise counsel enjoys a stability and peace that the self-directed soul lacks. This is a practical promise: the way of counsel is the way of peace.

The text warns against acting "without taking counsel of Me" (048-014) — here counsel is elevated to a divine dimension. The soul that acts without consulting God in prayer is like one who makes decisions without seeking any advice at all.

### Key Passages

> "Take counsel with a man who is wise and of good conscience." (004-011)

> "It is better to ask counsel than to give it." (009-020)

> "In the midst of temptation often seek counsel; and deal not hardly with one that is tempted." (013-028)

> "They who rule themselves after the counsel of the wise may easily be kept in peace." (044-030)

## Biblical Foundation

The wisdom literature of the Old Testament places a high value on counsel. Proverbs teaches: "Where there is no counsel, the people fall; but in the multitude of counselors there is safety" (Proverbs 11:14). Another proverb exhorts: "Listen to counsel and receive instruction, that you may be wise in your latter days" (Proverbs 19:20). Sirach advises: "Let those who are at peace with you be many, but let your counselors be one in a thousand" (Sirach 6:6). And the way of the [[pride|proud]] is contrasted with the way of the wise: "The way of a fool is right in his own eyes, but he who heeds counsel is wise" (Proverbs 12:15).

## Related Concepts

- [[humility]] — the virtue that makes the soul willing to seek and receive counsel
- [[obedience]] — the submission of one's own judgment to legitimate authority
- [[wisdom]] — the quality that makes counsel worth seeking and following
- [[prudence]] — the discretion to distinguish good counsel from bad
- [[pride]] — the vice that refuses counsel and insists on self-sufficiency
- [[temptation]] — the condition in which counsel is most urgently needed

## Practical Application

The practice of seeking counsel requires the [[humility]] to admit that one does not see clearly enough to guide oneself alone — especially when one is under [[temptation]] (013-028). The choice of counselor matters: one should seek those who are "wise and of good conscience" (004-011), whose lives bear witness to the counsel they give. And the one who receives counsel should remember "it is better to ask counsel than to give it" (009-020), maintaining a teachable disposition rather than rushing to direct others.
