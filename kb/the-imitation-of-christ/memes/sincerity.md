# Sincerity

The virtue of inner singleness — the correspondence between what the soul is before God and what it shows before men — in The Imitation of Christ, sincerity is the ground of all authentic devotion, without which every spiritual exercise is hollow.

## Definition

Sincerity, in the Kempian vocabulary, is the quality of a soul that intends what it professes and loves what it practices. It is opposed to hypocrisy, duplicity, and every form of spiritual performance. Thomas à Kempis draws no sharp line between sincerity and [[simplicity]]: both describe the soul that has been unified in its intention toward God, no longer divided between serving God and seeking human esteem.

> Sincerity is not primarily about truth-telling to others but about truth-being before God — a heart that is "pure, sincere, and steadfast" (003-024), not "distracted by many things" (003-024).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Throughout the work, Thomas à Kempis returns to the theme of inner integrity — the alignment of heart, word, and deed — as the foundation of the spiritual life.

### Pure, Sincere, and Steadfast

Book I describes the truly devout soul as one whose heart is "pure, sincere, and steadfast" (003-024). This triplet captures the essential structure: purity of intention, sincerity of execution, and steadfastness in perseverance. The sincere soul is not blown about by changing circumstances or the opinions of others; it is anchored in God, and its exterior life faithfully reflects its interior orientation.

### A Strict Life and Sincere Penitence

At the judgment, "a strict life and sincere penitence shall bring deeper" comfort than any words of self-justification (024-062). Sincerity in [[repentance|penitence]] means an honest reckoning with one's sins — not the performative sorrow that seeks sympathy but the genuine [[contrition]] that seeks amendment. The Imitation of Christ insists that God reads the heart, and no outward display can substitute for inward truth.

### Sincere Love

The text describes true love as "swift, sincere, pious, pleasant, gentle, strong" (042-056). Sincerity is an attribute of authentic [[charity]]: love that is sincere loves God for His own sake and neighbor for God's sake, without hidden agendas or mixed motives. It is the opposite of the calculating love that asks, "What can this person do for me?"

### Sincere Devotion

"If thy love be pure, sincere" — then all outward works are acceptable to God (064-005). The converse is chilling: without sincerity, even the most impressive spiritual exercises are weightless. Thomas does not despise outward observance but insists it must be the vessel of inward reality. Sincerity is what makes [[devotion]] true.

### Sincere Charity

The book's final chapters exhort the disciple to exercise "steadfast faith, devout hope, and sincere charity" (097-087). Sincerity crowns charity — it is what distinguishes the love that is [[grace]]-infused from the love that is merely natural affection or self-interest dressed in spiritual language.

### Key Passages

> "which is pure, sincere, and steadfast, is not distracted" (003-024)

> "a strict life and sincere penitence shall bring deeper" (024-062)

> "Love is swift, sincere, pious, pleasant, gentle, strong" (042-056)

> "If thy love be pure, sincere" (064-005)

> "steadfast faith, devout hope, and sincere charity" (097-087)

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of Christ draws on the biblical ideal of the "single eye" (Matthew 6:22) — the undivided heart that serves one master. Paul's testimony — "our boasting is this: the testimony of our conscience that we conducted ourselves in the world in simplicity and godly sincerity" (2 Corinthians 1:12) — captures the Kempian ideal exactly. Joshua's exhortation to "serve Him in sincerity and in truth" (Joshua 24:14) establishes sincerity as an Old Testament requirement that Christ intensifies in the Sermon on the Mount: alms, prayer, and fasting must be done "in secret" (Matthew 6:1–6), before the Father who sees what is hidden. Paul calls the Corinthians to celebrate the feast "with the unleavened bread of sincerity and truth" (1 Corinthians 5:8). The Imitation of Christ applies this biblical teaching to the interior life: examine every motive, purify every intention, and serve God — not the audience.
