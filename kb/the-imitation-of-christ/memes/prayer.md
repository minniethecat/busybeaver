# Prayer

The soul's intimate conversation with God — the indispensable means of seeking grace, resisting temptation, and growing in union with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. In The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], prayer is not one devotional exercise among many but the very form of the spiritual life: the entire Book III is structured as a dialogue of prayer between [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and the Disciple.

## Definition

Prayer, in the theology of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is the lifting of the mind and heart to God — a personal, intimate conversation with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] that is at once petition, [[worship|adoration]], confession, and contemplation. It is the "exercise of prayer" (044-011) by which the soul turns from [[creation|created things]] to the Creator, and the "devout prayer" (090-008) by which grace is sought and received. Thomas a Kempis presents prayer not as an occasional practice but as the continual disposition of the soul toward God, a "[[contemplation|mental prayer]]" (018-021) that persists even amid labour and the duties of daily life. Prayer is the channel through which every spiritual gift flows and the remedy to which the soul must turn in every [[suffering|affliction]].

## In The Imitation of Christ

Prayer pervades every book of The Imitation, but it receives its fullest treatment in Book III, where the entire discourse is cast as an intimate colloquy between [[jesus-christ|Christ]] ([[jesus-christ|the Beloved]]) and the Disciple (the faithful soul). This structure itself embodies the central Kempian conviction: the spiritual life is prayer — a living conversation with God conducted not only with the lips but with the whole heart and life.

### Prayer as Conversation with God

Book III of The Imitation opens with the fundamental posture of prayer: attentive listening. "I will hearken what the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] God shall say within me. Blessed is the soul which heareth the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] speaking within it, and receiveth the word of [[comfort|consolation]] from His mouth" (038-002). Prayer is first receptivity — the soul silencing itself to hear the inward voice of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — before it is speech. The [[discipleship|disciple]] does not merely speak to God but listens for God. This dialogue pattern runs throughout the entire work: [[jesus-christ|Christ]] speaks, and the [[discipleship|disciple]] responds in praise, petition, or lament. This is not theological abstraction but lived experience: "The ardent affection of the soul is a great clamour in the ears of God" (042-046). Prayer is not measured by the number of words but by the intensity of the heart's longing for God.

### Humility in Prayer

The indispensable disposition of all prayer in The Imitation is humility. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who prays approaches God not as an equal but as a beggar before the King. "Who am I that I should dare to speak unto Thee? I am the very poorest of Thy servants, an abject worm, much poorer and more despicable than I know or dare to say" (040-048). This liturgical humility is the precondition for receiving grace, for "a free spirit" is that "which humble prayer more deserveth than doth much reading" (063-001). The very posture of Book III's opening prayer — "A PRAYER FOR THE SPIRIT OF DEVOTION" (040-047) — models a supplication so lowly that it acknowledges the soul has no claim upon God whatsoever: "I am nothing, I have nothing, and can do nothing. Thou only art good, just and holy; Thou canst do all things" (040-051). [[humility|Humility]] in prayer is not self-deprecation for its own sake but the honest acknowledgment of one's creaturely dependence, which opens the soul to receive what only God can give. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is warned that the devil seeks to "draw thee away from prayer and holy reading" (043-033), and the principal defence against this is humble self-knowledge.

### Perseverance in Prayer

Thomas a Kempis insists that prayer must not cease even when God seems distant. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] reproves the [[discipleship|disciple]] precisely for delaying to pray: "This it is which chiefly hindereth [[consolation|heavenly consolation]], that thou too slowly betakest thyself unto prayer. For before thou earnestly seekest unto Me, thou dost first seek after many means of comfort, and refresheth thyself in outward things" (067-005). The remedy for every [[suffering|affliction]] is not to turn to secondary comforts but to turn immediately to God in prayer. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is to seek God's help with confident perseverance, for [[jesus-christ|Christ]] promises: "I am nigh, saith the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], that I may restore all things not only as they were at the first, but also abundantly and one upon another" (067-012). Many a soul "fled to the help of prayer to be delivered from the snares" (075-021) and found deliverance.

Even the withdrawal of spiritual [[comfort|consolation]] must not interrupt prayer. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] counsels: "Cease not from the exercise of prayer, nor suffer thy other common duties to be in anywise neglected; rather do thy task more readily, as though thou hadst gained more strength and knowledge" (044-011). This is the test of authentic devotion — not praying only when it feels sweet, but continuing faithfully through seasons of [[desolation|dryness]]. The holy fathers exemplified this perseverance: "By day they laboured, and at night they gave themselves ofttimes unto prayer; yea, even when they were labouring they ceased not from [[contemplation|mental prayer]]" (018-020). The faithful are to "be sober and watch unto prayer" (061-018) and to "cease not to groan and knock" (108-029) at the door of [[mercy|divine mercy]]. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who perseveres in prayer "receiveth the fruit of his prayer, and testifieth how he hath been saved" (034-046).

### The Prayer of the Heart

For Thomas a Kempis, authentic prayer is not primarily a matter of words but of the heart's orientation. "Mental prayer" (018-021) — the interior, wordless communion of the soul with God — is the ideal toward which all vocal prayer tends. The entire rhythm of the spiritual life is an alternation between outward labour and inward prayer: "Be thou never without something to do; be reading, or writing, or praying, or meditating, or doing something that is useful to the community" (019-038). The goal is that prayer become so woven into the fabric of life that the [[discipleship|disciple]] can offer "a pure prayer" (085-067) with a "serene conscience" (085-067). What matters is not the quantity of words but the [[devotion|fervour]] of the heart: "what frequent and fervid prayers did they offer unto God!" (018-015) speaks of the holy fathers whose intensity of devotion, not merely their discipline of prayer, set them apart. "O what [[devotion|devoutness]] of prayer! what rivalry in the ser[[sin|vice]] of God!" (018-041) — the true measure of prayer is the ardour of love. The ideal is a prayer that flows from love: "the ardent affection of the soul is a great clamour in the ears of God" (042-046).

### Distractions in Prayer

The Imitation is remarkably realistic about the difficulty of prayer. Book III, Chapter XLVIII contains one of the most candid confessions in all of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian spiritual literature: "Pardon me also, and of Thy [[forgiveness|mercy]] deal gently with me, whensoever in prayer I think on anything besides Thee; for truly I confess that I am wont to be continually distracted. For often and often, where in the body I stand or sit, there I myself am not; but rather am I there, whither I am borne by my thoughts" (085-049). This admission that the mind wanders even during the most solemn prayer is both a comfort to the struggling soul and a call to vigilance. Thomas traces the source of distraction to disordered love: "Where my thought is, there am I; and there commonly is my thought where that which I love is" (085-053). The cure for distraction, therefore, is not a technique of concentration but a reordering of the affections — learning to love God above all [[creation|created things]] so that the mind naturally returns to Him. The one who "loveth heaven" will "gladly meditate on heavenly things" (085-058), and the one who would offer a pure prayer must "do violence to his [[nature|fleshly nature]] and crucifieth the lusts of the flesh by the [[devotion|fervour]] of his spirit" (085-066).

## Key Passages

Nevertheless remember, O Lord, that I am nothing, I have nothing, and can do nothing. Thou only art good, just and holy; Thou canst do all things" (040-048)

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] grounds its theology of prayer in both the teaching and the example of Scripture. The fundamental command to watchfulness in prayer echoes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s words in Gethsemane: "Watch and pray, that ye enter not into temptation" (Matthew 26:41), which Thomas directly incorporates: "watch unto prayer, lest the devil find occasion to deceive" (013-005). The structure of the Book III dialogue, with the [[discipleship|disciple]] listening for the inward voice of God, explicitly cites Psalm 85:8: "I will hearken what the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] God shall say within me." The teaching on perseverance in prayer draws from [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s parable of the persistent widow (Luke 18:1), while the counsel to pray without ceasing reflects the apostolic command: "Pray without ceasing" (1 Thessalonians 5:17). The humble posture of the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s supplication — acknowledging total dependence on God — echoes the prayer of the tax collector in Luke 18:13: "God be merciful to me a sinner." The [[trust-in-god|confidence in God]]'s readiness to answer prayer reflects [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s promise: "Ask, and it shall be given you; seek, and ye shall find; knock, and it shall be opened unto you" (Matthew 7:7), which underlies the exhortation to "cease not to groan and knock" (108-029). The ideal of lifting "holy hands" in pure prayer recalls 1 Timothy 2:8, and the entire Book III's dialogical form embodies the psalmist's cry: "My heart said unto Thee, Thy face, [[jesus-christ|Lord]], will I seek" (Psalm 27:8). The community dimension of prayer — "the prayers of holy men more fervently besought" (019-053) — reflects the apostolic practice of intercessory prayer commended throughout the New Testament.
