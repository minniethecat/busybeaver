# Renewal

The continual inward renovation of the soul by grace — in The Imitation of Christ, renewal is not a single decisive event but an ongoing disposition of the heart, a daily turning from the old life of self toward the new life of conformity to Christ.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, renewal names the process by which the soul is progressively transformed into the likeness of Christ. It is not the same as [[conversion]], which marks the initial turning — renewal is the daily recommitment that sustains and deepens that turning. Thomas à Kempis presents renewal as "the continual renewal of thy spirit" (098-059), a phrase that captures both the agency of grace and the soul's active cooperation.

Renewal stands in opposition to spiritual stagnation. The soul that ceases to renew itself — that ceases to examine, to repent, to re-offer itself to God — drifts imperceptibly toward spiritual mediocrity. The Imitation's insistence on continual renewal is a recognition that the spiritual life is a dynamic process, not a static state.

## In The Imitation of Christ

While The Imitation of Christ does not devote a separate chapter to renewal, the theme runs through the entire work as the practical engine of spiritual progress.

### The Imperative of Continual Renewal

The Imitation teaches: "by continual renewal of thy spirit, thou oughtest to dispose thyself" (098-059) for the reception of divine grace. The language of "continual" renewal underscores that the spiritual life is never a completed acquisition. What was offered to God yesterday must be offered again today — not because yesterday's offering was insincere, but because the soul is ever in motion, ever tending either toward God or away from Him.

### Renewal and Self-Denial

Renewal is intimately linked to [[self-denial]] in The Imitation. The old self does not die once and for all in this life; it revives and reasserts itself, requiring the daily mortification that makes space for the new creation. Each act of self-denial is an act of renewal — a fresh clearing of the ground in which grace can take root.

### Renewal and the Eucharist

Book IV places particular emphasis on renewal through sacramental communion. The frequent reception of the Eucharist is presented as the great means by which the soul is renewed in its union with Christ. The preparation for communion is itself an act of renewal — a fresh examination of conscience, a fresh act of contrition, a fresh offering of the whole self to God.

### Key Passages

> "By continual renewal of thy spirit, thou oughtest to dispose thyself." (098-059)

## Biblical Foundation

The apostolic writings are rich in the language of renewal. Paul exhorts the Roman Christians: "Do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind" (Romans 12:2). He confesses that "though our outward man is perishing, yet the inward man is being renewed day by day" (2 Corinthians 4:16). The Colossians are reminded that they "have put on the new man who is renewed in knowledge according to the image of Him who created him" (Colossians 3:10). And the Ephesians are called to "be renewed in the spirit of your mind" (Ephesians 4:23) — the same language of continual inward renovation that The Imitation employs.

## Practical Application

The teaching of The Imitation on renewal calls the [[disciple]] to resist the subtle assumption that spiritual growth is automatic. The soul that coasts on past graces will find itself adrift. The practical response is the daily renewal of the morning offering: the deliberate, conscious act by which the [[disciple]] places the coming day — its thoughts, words, deeds, and sufferings — into the hands of God, trusting that "His mercies are new every morning" (Lamentations 3:23).
