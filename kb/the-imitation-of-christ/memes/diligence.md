# Diligence

If zeal is the fire of the spiritual life, diligence is the fuel that keeps it burning. Thomas à Kempis returns to this virtue again and again, not as a grand theological theme but as the [[silence|quiet]], indispensable foundation of all progress in holiness. "Without care and diligence thou shalt never get virtue" (025-086): the statement is absolute, admitting no exceptions. Diligence is not one virtue among many but the condition of acquiring any virtue at all — the steady hand that plants, waters, and tends the garden while zeal supplies the sunshine.

## Definition

Diligence, in the vocabulary of The Imitation, is the consistent, careful application of the will to the duties of the spiritual life. It is distinguished from zeal by its quality of steadiness: zeal is intense but can be sporadic; diligence is less dramatic but more reliable. Thomas defines it by what it opposes — negligence, sloth, the "custom" that makes spiritual exercises mechanical (108-021). The diligent man does not simply go through the motions; he "prepareth his heart with all diligence" (108-035) before every sacred act, approaching each prayer, each communion, each duty as though it were his first and might be his last. Diligence is the virtue that keeps the soul from drifting, that resists the universal tendency of all things spiritual to cool, slacken, and decay.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas treats diligence less as a topic for analysis than as a habit to be inculcated. It appears throughout The Imitation as the characteristic note of the serious [[discipleship|disciple]] — the one who has understood that the spiritual life is not a single dramatic conversion but a daily labour that must be renewed each morning.

### The Necessity of Diligence

Thomas is unsparing in his insistence on the indispensable role of effort. "Without care and diligence thou shalt never get virtue" (025-086). This is the hard saying that separates the wisdom of The Imitation from the sentimental religiosity that expects holiness without cost. Virtue is not infused in a moment; it is acquired through sustained, deliberate effort — the "care" that watches against temptation and the "diligence" that practises the corresponding good act until it becomes second nature.

The necessity of diligence extends to every area of the spiritual life. "Much diligence is needful for him who would make progress" (019-012). The word "much" is carefully chosen: a little diligence will not suffice. The path of progress is not a gentle incline but a steep ascent, and the soul that slackens its effort begins immediately to slide backward. Thomas does not promise that diligence will make the journey easy — only that it will make the journey possible. "He who diligently attendeth to himself easily keepeth silence concerning others" (030-013): the diligent man is too occupied with his own amendment to have leisure for the [[sin|fault]]s of his neighbour.

### Diligence in Specific Duties

Thomas applies the principle of diligence to the particular practices of the spiritual life. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is to "be watchful and diligent in God's ser[[sin|vice]]" (025-002) — the phrase pairs two complementary virtues, watchfulness against the approach of temptation and diligence in the performance of duty. In prayer, the [[discipleship|disciple]] must apply himself with "diligent meditation" (059-005), not skimming the surface of holy words but weighing them carefully in the heart. In receiving the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], he is to "prepare thy heart with all diligence" before approaching and "keep thyself with all diligence therein after" receiving (108-035 to 108-038) — diligence before communion ensures worthy reception, and diligence after ensures that the grace received is not quickly lost.

Even in the ordinary round of daily duties, Thomas insists on diligence of a particular kind: "Do it diligently, not of custom, nor of necessity, but with the [[fear-of-the-lord|fear of God]] and love" (108-021). The diligence that is mere habit — the mechanical repetition of what was once done with [[devotion|fervour]] — is not true diligence but its counterfeit. True diligence engages the will afresh each time, renewing the intention and directing it toward God. It is not enough to do the right thing; it must be done in the right spirit, with the heart fully present to the act.

### The Fruit of Diligence

Thomas does not leave diligence without its promised reward. "He who is diligent shall attain more profit, although he have many [[sin|passions]], than he who is well disposed but less diligent" (025-034 to 025-035). This is a remarkable claim: diligence can compensate for temperamental disadvantages. The man of naturally strong [[sin|passions]] who diligently struggles against them will advance further than the placid man who makes no effort. [[nature|Nature]] matters less than application; the will, steadily applied, can overcome almost any natural disability.

"A zealous and diligent man is ready for all things" (025-090). Diligence creates a kind of spiritual readiness — the state of the soul that is always prepared, always watchful, never caught off guard by temptation or opportunity. This readiness is the fruit of a life in which nothing is done carelessly, in which every action is performed with the full attention and intention of the will. The diligent man is the man whose whole life has become a single act of ser[[sin|vice]] to God, uninterrupted by the slackness that makes most lives a patchwork of fervent moments and long stretches of negligence.

## Key Passages

> "Without care and diligence thou shalt never get virtue. If thou beginnest to grow lukewarm, thou wilt soon be in evil case." (025-086 to 025-087)

> "Much diligence is needful for him who would make progress; and if he who resolveth bravely often falleth, what shall he do who seldom or feebly resolveth?" (019-012 to 019-013)

> "A zealous and diligent man is ready for all things. It is greater labour to resist sins and passions than to toil in bodily labours." (025-090 to 025-091)

> "Do it diligently, not of custom, nor of necessity, but with the fear of God and love." (108-021)

> "He who is diligent shall attain more profit, although he have many passions, than he who is well disposed but less diligent." (025-034 to 025-035)

## Biblical Foundation

The wisdom literature of the Old Testament prepares the ground for Thomas's teaching: "The hand of the diligent shall bear rule, but the slothful shall be under tribute" (Proverbs 12:24). The soul of the sluggard desireth and hath nothing, but "the soul of the diligent shall be made fat" (Proverbs 13:4). These proverbs establish the principle that Thomas develops: spiritual results require spiritual effort, and the one who will not labour cannot expect to harvest.

St Paul's exhortation to be "not slothful in business; fervent in spirit; serving the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Romans 12:11) unites diligence with zeal in a single command. The apostle's own example — "I press toward the mark for the prize of the high calling of God in [[jesus-christ|Christ]] [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]" (Philippians 3:14) — embodies the diligence Thomas commends: not a single effort but a sustained pressing forward that continues until the goal is reached. St Peter's ladder of virtues places diligence at the foundation: "giving all diligence, add to your faith virtue" (2 Peter 1:5). Virtue is built upon diligence; without that foundation, every higher attainment is unstable. The author of Hebrews likewise desires "that every one of you do shew the same diligence to the full assurance of hope unto the end" (Hebrews 6:11), identifying diligence as the quality that carries the believer from initial faith to [[perseverance|final perseverance]].
