# Peace

The tranquility of soul that flows from the right ordering of the interior life — resisting passion, embracing humility, and resigning the self wholly to the will of God — rather than the mere absence of outward trouble or the satisfaction of worldly desire.

## Definition

In the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian devotional tradition represented by The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], peace is not merely the absence of conflict or the satisfaction of worldly desire. True peace of heart is a positive spiritual condition — the tranquility of a soul rightly ordered toward God. Thomas à Kempis distinguishes sharply between [[world|the world]]'s peace, which depends on favorable circumstances, and divine peace, which endures through [[suffering|adversity]]. "True peace of heart is to be found in resisting passion, not in yielding to it" (006-014). The poor and lowly of heart — not the proud or avaricious — "abide in the multitude of peace" (006-004). This peace is therefore inseparable from humility, self-denial, and the progressive mortification of disordered affections.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Peace is one of the most pervasive themes in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], appearing across all four books as both the goal of the spiritual life and the sign of genuine progress toward God. Thomas treats it not as a passive state but as the fruit of deliberate ascetical effort and interior discipline.

### Peace as the Fruit of Interior Discipline

Book I, Chapter XI — "Of Seeking Peace of Mind and of Spiritual Progress" — establishes the foundational principle that peace depends on the governance of one's interior attention. "We may enjoy abundance of peace if we refrain from busying ourselves with the sayings and doings of others, and things which concern not ourselves" (011-002). The single-hearted, who are not scattered among outward concerns, receive the promise: "Blessed are the single-hearted, for they shall have abundance of peace" (011-007). Conversely, the person occupied with "other men's matters" cannot "abide long time in peace" (011-004). Peace requires the progressive cleansing of inordinate affections; only "being cleansed from affections, we may possess our souls in peace" (011-030).

### The Peaceable Man

Book II, Chapter III — "Of the Good, Peaceable Man" — offers the most concentrated portrait of the peaceful soul. The chapter opens with the priority of interior peace as the precondition of all peacemaking: "First keep thyself in peace, and then shalt thou be able to be a peacemaker towards others" (028-002). The peaceable person is contrasted with the passionate: "A passionate man turneth even good into evil and easily believeth evil; a good, peaceable man converteth all things into good" (028-005). Thomas distinguishes three kinds of people: those who keep peace for themselves and others, those who have neither peace nor permit it in others, and those who "hold themselves in peace, and study to bring others unto peace" (028-028). The chapter culminates in the paradox that peace in this life "lieth in humble suffering rather than in not feeling adversities," and "he who best knoweth how to suffer shall possess the most peace" (028-030 to 028-031).

### Peace Through Resignation and Detachment

In Book III, the interior voice of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] deepens the teaching on peace. Chapter XXV — "Wherein Firm Peace of Heart and True Profit Consist" — invokes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own farewell words: "Peace I leave with you, My peace I give unto you, not as [[world|the world]] giveth give I unto you" (062-002). The peace [[jesus-christ|Christ]] gives is not [[world|the world]]'s peace of favorable circumstances: "My peace is with the humble and lowly in heart. Thy peace shall be in much patience" (062-005). True peace is not proven by the absence of grief or the presence of spiritual [[comfort|consolation]]; rather, "if thou come to an utter contempt of thyself, know that then thou shalt enjoy abundance of peace" (062-037). This theme of peace through radical self-resignation recurs in Book III, Chapter XXXVII: "Give thyself up, resign thyself, and thou shalt have great inward peace" (074-026).

The link between detachment from [[world|the world]] and peace is strongly emphasized in Book I, Chapter XX on solitude and silence: "O how great peace and [[silence|quiet]] should he possess, who would cast off all vain care, and think only of healthful and divine things, and build his whole hope upon God!" (020-033). The solitary who remains with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in his chamber shall not "elsewhere find so great peace" (020-071).

### Peace and the Good Conscience

Book II, Chapter VI connects peace to the state of one's conscience, drawing on the prophet [[isaiah|Isaiah]]: "There is no peace, saith my God, to the wicked" (031-008). The wicked "have never true joy, nor feel internal peace" (031-007), whereas the person of [[conscience|pure conscience]] "will easily be contented and filled with peace" (031-023). In this framework, peace is the experiential accompaniment of [[justice|righteousness]] — not a feeling one can manufacture independently of the moral condition of the soul. The kingdom of God itself is described as "peace and joy in the [[holy-spirit|Holy Ghost]]" (026-006), identifying peace as both the sign and the substance of God's indwelling presence.

### Key Passages

> "True peace of heart is to be found in resisting passion, not in yielding to it. And therefore there is no peace in the heart of a man who is carnal, nor in him who is given up to the things that are without him, but only in him who is fervent towards God and living the life of the Spirit." (006-014)

> "First keep thyself in peace, and then shalt thou be able to be a peacemaker towards others. A peaceable man doth more good than a well-learned." (028-002)

> "He who best knoweth how to suffer shall possess the most peace; that man is conqueror of himself and lord of the world, the friend of Christ, and the inheritor of heaven." (028-031)

> "My peace is with the humble and lowly in heart. Thy peace shall be in much patience. If thou heardest Me, and didst follow My voice, thou shouldest enjoy much peace." (062-005)

> "Give thyself up, resign thyself, and thou shalt have great inward peace. Give all for all; demand nothing, ask nothing in return; stand simply and with no hesitation in Me, and thou shalt possess Me." (074-026)

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] anchors its teaching on peace in several scriptural sources. The most significant is [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s farewell discourse in John 14:27 — "Peace I leave with you, My peace I give unto you; not as [[world|the world]] giveth, give I unto you" — which Thomas quotes directly at the opening of Book III, Chapter XXV (062-002). The contrast between the peace of the righteous and the dis[[silence|quiet]] of the wicked draws on [[isaiah|Isaiah]] 57:21 — "There is no peace, saith my God, to the wicked" — cited in the chapter on the joy of a [[conscience|good conscience]] (031-008). The Beatitude "Blessed are the peacemakers" (Matthew 5:9) underlies the teaching of Book II, Chapter III on the peaceable man. The Pauline affirmation that "the kingdom of God is not meat and drink, but [[justice|righteousness]] and peace and joy in the [[holy-spirit|Holy Ghost]]" (Romans 14:17) echoes throughout the work's insistence that peace is a spiritual, not carnal, reality (026-006). Additionally, Psalm 4:4 — "Commune with your own heart in your own chamber and be still" — is invoked to connect peace with silence and solitude (020-040).

## Related Concepts

- [[humility]] — The indispensable ground of peace: "My peace is with the humble and lowly in heart" (062-005)
- [[patience]] — The means by which peace is sustained amid [[temptation|trial]]s: "Thy peace shall be in much patience" (062-005)
- [[self-denial]] — The practical discipline through which the soul is freed from the affections that disturb peace
- [[grace]] — Peace as a divine gift, not a human achievement; the peace [[jesus-christ|Christ]] gives, "not as [[world|the world]] giveth" (062-002)
- [[obedience]] — Peace through humble [[obedience|subjection]] to God and to others for the sake of peace
- [[conscience]] — The connection between moral integrity and interior peace: the [[conscience|pure conscience]] is "filled with peace" (031-023)
