# Hell

The state and place of eternal separation from God, prepared for the devil and his angels (Matthew 25:41), into which those who die in a state of unrepented mortal sin are cast by the just judgment of God — invoked throughout The Imitation of Christ as the solemn warning that reframes every earthly attachment, every act of self-indulgence, and every delay in conversion within the light of eternity.

## Location

Hell, in Catholic theology, is not a mythological construct but a real state of definitive self-exclusion from communion with God. The Catechism of the Catholic Church teaches that "to die in mortal sin without repenting and accepting God's merciful love means remaining separated from Him for ever by our own free choice" (CCC 1033). The chief punishment of hell is the pain of loss — eternal separation from God, the only source of beatitude — accompanied by the pain of sense, traditionally understood as a real and unending suffering of body and soul consonant with the nature of the damned.

Scripture describes hell under several images: the "unquenchable fire" (Mark 9:43), the "outer darkness" where there is "weeping and gnashing of teeth" (Matthew 8:12), the "lake of fire" which is "the second death" (Revelation 20:14), and the place where "their worm dieth not" (Mark 9:48). The rich man in the parable of Lazarus (Luke 16:23–24) is depicted as being "in torments" in Hades, begging for a drop of water to cool his tongue. These images, while analogical, convey the Church's solemn teaching that hell is a real possibility for every human being who dies rejecting the grace of God.

## Biblical Significance

The doctrine of hell unfolds progressively through Scripture. The Old Testament speaks of Sheol, the underworld abode of the dead (Psalm 16:10; Isaiah 14:9), but the full revelation of eternal punishment awaits the New Testament. Christ Himself speaks of hell more frequently than any other figure in Scripture: "Fear Him which is able to destroy both soul and body in hell" (Matthew 10:28). The separation of the sheep from the goats at the Last Judgment culminates in the irrevocable sentence: "Depart from Me, ye cursed, into everlasting fire, prepared for the devil and his angels" (Matthew 25:41). The Apostle Paul warns that those who do not obey the gospel "shall be punished with everlasting destruction from the presence of the Lord, and from the glory of His power" (2 Thessalonians 1:9). The Book of Revelation depicts the final judgment in which "death and hell were cast into the lake of fire. This is the second death" (Revelation 20:14).

The Church has always understood these texts as teaching the reality and eternity of hell. The Athanasian Creed declares that those who have done evil "shall go into everlasting fire." The Council of Trent defined that those who die in mortal sin descend immediately into hell to suffer eternal punishments. The teaching is not a marginal speculation but an article of faith, integral to the gospel's call to conversion.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis invokes hell not as a topic for theological curiosity but as a practical existential warning. The meditation on hell serves a precise spiritual purpose: to break the soul's attachment to sin by making vivid the consequences of sin. In the Kempian framework, the remembrance of hell is a medicine against complacency, a spur to conversion, and a guardrail keeping the disciple on the narrow way of the Cross.

The most striking deployment of the theme occurs in Book II, Chapter VIII — "Of the Intimate Love of Jesus" — where Thomas writes: "To be without Jesus is the nethermost hell, and to be with Jesus is sweet paradise" (033-013). This remarkable formulation defines hell not primarily in terms of fire and torment but in terms of absence — the absence of Jesus. Hell is the state of the soul that has definitively rejected or lost the one thing necessary. Conversely, paradise — already, in foretaste — is simply the presence of Jesus. This Christocentric definition of heaven and hell is one of the most distinctive features of Thomas's theology.

The warning function of the doctrine appears throughout the work. The disciple is urged to consider "what the damned suffer in hell for one sin which they committed" as a goad to penance and amendment of life (cf. 023-040). The brevity and lightness of earthly tribulation are measured against the eternity of hell: "For a little while thou shalt suffer here, and then thou shalt rejoice for ever" (086-060). The fear of hell is not the highest motive for serving God — love is higher — but it is a legitimate and salutary motive, especially for those still struggling with grave sin. "If thou canst not yet be all on fire with love, at least be wise through fear" (cf. 022-035).

The remembrance of hell also serves to relativize earthly sufferings. Whatever the disciple endures in this life — sickness, persecution, spiritual dryness, the inward combat with the old man — it is light and momentary compared with the pains of the damned. "All that we suffer here is little, yea, nothing, in comparison with the torments which the damned endure" (023-045). This is not a morbid obsession but a sober realism that enables the disciple to bear present trials with patience and even gratitude: if this suffering preserves the soul from that suffering, it is mercy, not cruelty.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Christian tradition, the reality of hell carries a profound symbolic significance that is inseparable from its literal meaning. Hell is the logical terminus of a life closed in upon itself — the final and irrevocable "No" to God. As the Catechism teaches, "God predestines no one to go to hell; for this, a willful turning away from God (a mortal sin) is necessary, and persistence in it until the end" (CCC 1037). The door of hell is locked from the inside.

The great paradox of hell is that it is both the just punishment for sin and the expression of divine mercy — for a soul that has definitively rejected God cannot be happy in God's presence; hell is, in one sense, the divine respect for human freedom taken to its ultimate consequence. Thomas à Kempis is less interested in these speculative questions than in the practical conclusion: the soul must choose now, in this life, between the two eternal destinies. There is no third way. "He that followeth Me shall not walk in darkness, but shall have the light of life" (086-037) — the alternative to following Christ in the light is wandering without Him in the darkness, the darkness of the nethermost hell.
