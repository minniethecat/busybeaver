# The Cross

The divinely appointed instrument of self-denial, [[suffering|tribulation]], and spiritual transformation through which the soul is conformed to [[crucifixion|Christ crucified]] and led to [[eternity|eternal life]] — in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], it is not merely a historical event but a lived reality, the "royal way" that every [[discipleship|disciple]] must walk.

## Definition

The Cross in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is both the historical instrument of [[passion-of-christ|Christ's Passion]] and the universal principle of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[discipleship|disciple]]ship: the daily embrace of suffering, mortification, and self-denial in imitation of the Crucified [[jesus-christ|Lord]]. Thomas a Kempis presents the Cross not as an abstract symbol but as the concrete path of salvation, declaring that "In the Cross is health, in the Cross is life, in the Cross is protection from enemies, in the Cross is heavenly sweetness, in the Cross strength of mind, in the Cross joy of the spirit, in the Cross the height of virtue, in the Cross perfection of holiness" (037-012). There is, he insists, "no health of the soul, no hope of [[eternity|eternal life]], save in the Cross" (037-016).

The Cross is the divinely willed means by which the soul is weaned from self-love and transformed into the likeness of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. "Behold everything dependeth upon the Cross, and everything lieth in dying; and there is none other way unto life and to true [[peace|inward peace]], except the way of the Holy Cross and of [[mortification|daily mortification]]" (037-023). Thomas grounds this teaching in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own words: "If any man will come after Me, let him deny himself and take up his cross, and follow Me" (037-139).

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Cross is one of the most pervasive and theologically central themes in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], warranting two dedicated chapters in Book II (Chapters XI and XII) and recurring throughout Books III and IV as the fundamental pattern of the spiritual life. Thomas a Kempis presents the Cross under several interconnected aspects.

### The Royal Way of the Holy Cross

Book II, Chapter XII opens with the arresting title "Of the Royal Way of the Holy Cross" — one of the most famous phrases in the entire work. The Cross is "royal" because [[jesus-christ|Christ]] the King walked it first: "He went before thee bearing His Cross and died for thee upon the Cross, that thou also mayest bear thy cross and mayest love to be crucified upon it" (037-017). Thomas insists there is no alternative path: "Go where thou wilt, seek whatsoever thou wilt, and thou shalt find no higher way above nor safer way below, than the way of the Holy Cross" (037-025). To seek rest and joy apart from the Cross is delusion: "The whole life of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] was a cross and martyrdom, and dost thou seek for thyself rest and joy? Thou art wrong, thou art wrong, if thou seekest aught but to suffer [[suffering|tribulation]]s" (037-060). No saint has escaped this path: "Which of the saints in [[world|the world]] hath been without the cross and [[suffering|tribulation]]? For not even [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] our [[jesus-christ|Lord]] was one hour without the anguish of His Passion, so long as He lived" (037-054).

### Bearing the Cross Daily

The Cross is not an occasional crisis but the daily texture of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life. "The Cross therefore is always ready, and every where waiteth for thee. Thou canst not flee from it whithersoever thou hurriest" (037-040). One cannot escape it by changing circumstances, for the need for the Cross is rooted in [[nature|human nature]] itself: "thou bearest thyself with thee, and shalt ever find thyself" (037-042). Thomas teaches that suffering is unavoidable in this mortal life, which is "full of miseries, and set round with crosses" (037-063). The question is only how one bears it: "If thou willingly bear the Cross, it will bear thee, and will bring thee to the end which thou seekest... If thou bear it unwillingly, thou makest a burden for thyself and greatly increaseth thy load, and yet thou must bear it" (037-047). To cast away one cross is only to find "another and perchance a heavier" (037-051).

The daily character of the Cross is reinforced by Thomas's insistence that the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian must "lead the life of a dying man. And the more a man dieth to himself, the more he beginneth to live towards God" (037-124). This dying is not a single act but a continual mortification. Book II, Chapter X counsels the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "prepare thyself for patience more than for comforts, and for bearing the cross more than for joy" (035-003), framing the Cross as the ordinary, expected condition of the spiritual life rather than an interruption of it.

### The Cross as Purification

The Cross serves a purifying function in the soul, burning away self-love and producing interior transformation. Thomas observes a paradoxical law: "the higher a man hath advanced in the spirit, the heavier crosses he will often find, because the sorrow of his banishment increaseth with the strength of his love" (037-064). The Cross thus becomes a sign of spiritual progress, not of divine disfavour. Through willing acceptance of [[suffering|tribulation]], "every burden of [[suffering|tribulation]] is turned into an assurance of [[comfort|divine comfort]], and the more the flesh is wasted by [[suffering|affliction]], the more is the spirit strengthened mightily by inward grace" (037-068).

This purification enables a deeper participation in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own Passion: "No man understandeth the [[passion-of-christ|Passion of Christ]] in his heart so well as he who hath had somewhat of the like suffering himself" (037-038). [[suffering|Suffering]] ceases to be merely endured and becomes desirable: "of so greatly is he comforted by the desire for [[suffering|tribulation]] and [[suffering|adversity]], through love of conformity to the Cross of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], that he would not be without sorrow and [[suffering|tribulation]]" (037-073). Thomas insists this transformation is supernatural: "This is not the virtue of man, but the grace of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] which hath such power and energy in the weak flesh" (037-077). Indeed, "it is not in the nature of man to bear the cross, to love the cross" (037-080) — only grace can produce such a disposition.

### The Cross and Christ

The Cross is inseparable from the person of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. The [[discipleship|disciple]]'s cross is a participation in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own Cross: "Set thyself, therefore, like a good and faithful servant of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], to the manful bearing of the Cross of thy [[jesus-christ|Lord]], who out of love was crucified for thee" (037-091). [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is both the exemplar and the enabler of Cross-bearing: "He went before thee bearing His Cross" (037-018), establishing the pattern the [[discipleship|disciple]] must follow.

Book III, Chapter LVI, titled "That We Ought to Deny Ourselves, and to Imitate [[jesus-christ|Christ]] by Means of the Cross," makes the connection explicit: "If thou wilt reign with Me, bear the cross with Me; for only the servants of the cross find the way of blessedness and of true light" (093-022). The [[discipleship|disciple]] responds with complete surrender: "I have received the cross at Thy hand; I have carried it, and will carry it even unto death, as Thou hast laid it upon me" (093-036). The life of [[discipleship|disciple]]ship is thus defined: "Truly the life of a truly devoted servant is a cross, but it leadeth to paradise" (093-039). Thomas adds the corporate dimension: "Come, my brothers, let us together go forward. [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] shall be with us. For [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]' sake have we taken up this cross, for [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]' sake let us persevere in the cross" (093-042).

Even in Book IV, on the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], the Cross remains central. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s self-oblation on the Cross is the model for the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s self-offering: "As I of my own will offered myself unto God the Father on the Cross for thy [[sin|sins]] with outstretched hands and naked body, so that nothing remained in Me that did not become altogether a sacrifice for the Divine propitiation; so also oughtest thou every day to offer thyself willingly unto Me for a pure and holy oblation" (104-003). The Cross is thus not only the way to [[jesus-christ|Christ]] but the pattern of union with Him.

### The Fewness of Those Who Love the Cross

Book II, Chapter XI, titled "Of the Fewness of Those Who Love the Cross of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]," offers a sobering complement. "[[jesus-christ|Jesus]] hath many lovers of His heavenly kingdom, but few bearers of His Cross. He hath many seekers of comfort, but few of [[suffering|tribulation]]" (036-003). The natural desire is for [[comfort|consolation]], not crucifixion: "Many are astonished at His Miracles, few follow after the shame of His Cross" (036-008). This chapter distinguishes between mercenary love — which seeks [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] for what He gives — and pure love, which clings to Him even in darkness and desolation. The Cross thus becomes the test of authentic [[discipleship|disciple]]ship.

There is no health of the soul, no hope of eternal life, save in the Cross." (037-012)

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of the Cross in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is rooted directly in the words of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself. The foundational text is Matthew 16:24 — "If any man will come after Me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow Me" — which Thomas quotes at the opening (037-003) and closing (037-139) of Book II, Chapter XII, and which provides the title phrase. The Lukan parallel, Luke 9:23, is also cited (037-139, footnote). Thomas invokes Romans 8:18 — "the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed in us" (037-101) — to place Cross-bearing in the perspective of eternal hope. The summary of the whole matter is drawn from Acts 14:22: "We must through much [[suffering|tribulation]] enter into the kingdom of God" (037-141). The eschatological dimension of the Cross — that "this sign of the Cross shall be in heaven when the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] cometh to [[judgment|Judgment]]" (037-007) — draws from Matthew 24:30, the sign of the Son of Man appearing in heaven. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own pattern of suffering and glorification provides the template: "It behooved [[jesus-christ|Christ]] to suffer and to rise from the dead, and so enter into his glory" (037-057), citing Luke 24:46. The Pauline theology of glory through suffering — "if we be dead with Him, we shall also live with Him; if we suffer, we shall also reign with Him" (037-020) — echoes 2 Timothy 2:11-12 and Romans 6:8.

## Related Concepts

- [[suffering]] — The experiential substance of the Cross; the universal human condition that, when embraced in faith, becomes the instrument of salvation
- [[self-denial]] — The fundamental act required by the Cross: the renunciation of self-will without which [[discipleship|disciple]]ship is impossible
- [[humility]] — The disposition that makes Cross-bearing possible, accepting [[suffering|tribulation]] as merited and purifying
- [[patience]] — The virtue by which the Cross is borne without murmuring; "needful is it that thou everywhere possess patience if thou wilt have internal peace" (037-045)
- [[love]] — The motive and fruit of the Cross: [[jesus-christ|Christ]] bore it "out of love" (037-091), and the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s willingness to suffer is the proof of pure love
- [[death]] — The meaning of the Cross applied to the interior life: [[death|dying to self]] that one may live to God (037-124)
- [[consolation]] — The sweetness that sometimes accompanies Cross-bearing but must never be sought as its substitute; the Cross is to be preferred to [[comfort|consolation]]s (037-100)
