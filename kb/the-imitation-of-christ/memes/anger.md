# Anger

The disordered passion of irritation, resentment, and the desire for retribution — in The Imitation of Christ, anger is a manifestation of the unsubdued self, a betrayal of the peace that should characterize the [[disciple]], and one of the most reliable indicators that the soul has not yet died to its own will.

## Definition

In the spiritual vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, anger encompasses the spectrum of disordered irritation, from the quick flash of indignation to settled resentment. It is understood not merely as an emotional state but as a spiritual disorder — a sign that the [[self-denial|old self]] still holds sway, that the soul has not yet attained to the [[humility]] and [[patience]] which are the marks of authentic conversion.

Thomas à Kempis treats anger as a diagnostic of the interior life: the person who is easily provoked has not yet learned to see their own faults before the faults of others. The movement from anger to peace is thus not a matter of emotional management but of spiritual transformation — the progressive dying to self that is the substance of Christian [[discipleship]].

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation of Christ addresses anger both as a personal vice to be mortified and as a divine attribute to be feared. The two perspectives converge: the [[disciple]] who contemplates God's righteous anger at sin is compelled to examine the petty angers that arise from wounded pride.

### Anger as a Fault of the Unsubdued Self

Book I, Chapter VI identifies anger as one of the natural passions that, left unchecked, reveals the soul's distance from true [[humility]]. The proud man "is often sad, and easily angered" (006-009) because his peace depends on outward circumstances and the esteem of others. When these are threatened, anger erupts as the defense mechanism of the wounded ego. The humble soul, by contrast, is not easily provoked because it has already accepted its own [[human-nature|nothingness]] and expects neither praise nor deference from others.

Book I, Chapter XXIV sharpens this teaching: the wrathful man is "moved to pity rather than to anger" only when he has first learned to see his own sins as greater than the faults of others (024-018). Anger at another's sin is hypocritical when one has not wept over one's own. This is the Kempian inversion: true spiritual perception begins with self-knowledge, and self-knowledge extinguishes anger at its root.

### The Soul Moved to Anger by Wounded Pride

Book III, Chapter LIII offers a penetrating self-examination: the [[disciple]] confesses "how quickly moved to anger, how ready for contention" (103-029) — not because of righteous zeal but because of pride that cannot bear contradiction. This is the voice of the soul that knows its own weakness and sees anger for what it is: a reflex of the unsubdued will that resists even the gentlest correction.

The same passage notes that the soul is "easily angered" precisely at the moment when it should be "ready for obedience" (103-029). Anger thus becomes not merely a vice but a barrier to spiritual progress — it closes the soul to the very correction by which it might be healed. The one who is quick to anger is slow to learn.

### Divine Anger and Human Fear

The Imitation of Christ also speaks of anger in its divine register. The [[disciple]] prays that God might not "keep up Thine anger for ever" (053-027), acknowledging that divine wrath against sin is a reality that only mercy can avert. This awareness of God's righteous anger serves to chasten human anger: if the holy God has cause for wrath against sin, what cause has the sinful creature to be angry at slights and injuries? The contrast between divine anger (measured, just, against real evil) and human anger (disordered, disproportionate, triggered by wounded pride) is implicit but forceful.

### Key Passages

> "He is often sad, and easily angered, for a little cause." (006-009)

> "He is moved to pity rather than to anger; when he frequently denieth himself and striveth to subdue the flesh altogether by the Spirit." (024-018)

> "How quickly moved to anger, how ready for contention!" (103-029)

## Biblical Foundation

The scriptural teaching on anger is unambiguous. James exhorts: "Let every man be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath; for the wrath of man does not produce the righteousness of God" (James 1:19–20). Human anger is categorically disqualified as an instrument of righteousness. Ephesians acknowledges the reality of anger while setting strict limits: "Be angry, and do not sin: do not let the sun go down on your wrath" (Ephesians 4:26). Christ deepens the commandment to include interior anger: "Whoever is angry with his brother without a cause shall be in danger of the judgment" (Matthew 5:22). Proverbs contrasts the wise and the foolish: "He who is slow to wrath has great understanding, but he who is impulsive exalts folly" (Proverbs 14:29). Paul includes anger among the works to be put off: "put off all these: anger, wrath, malice" (Colossians 3:8).

## Practical Application

The practice of overcoming anger, as taught in The Imitation, begins with the recognition that one's anger is almost never righteous — it springs from wounded pride, not from zeal for God's honor. The [[disciple]] who truly knows the depth of their own sin will be "moved to pity rather than to anger" at the faults of others (024-018). Each flare of irritation is an occasion for self-examination: what in me is being threatened? What pride is being wounded? This inquiry, pursued honestly, leads not to self-hatred but to the [[humility]] that is the precondition of authentic [[peace]].
