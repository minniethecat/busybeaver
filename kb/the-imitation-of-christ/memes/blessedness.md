# Blessedness

The state of perfect and enduring happiness that is the soul's true end — a theme that Thomas à Kempis treats with luminous simplicity, cutting through the tangle of human pursuits to declare that "all thy blessedness and thy happiness lieth in God alone" (053-015). The word "blessedness" translates the Latin *beatitudo*, the same term used for the Beatitudes of the Sermon on the Mount; it is happiness raised to its highest power, the joy that is not merely felt but possessed, not merely experienced but inhabited.

## Definition

Blessedness, in the vocabulary of The Imitation, is distinguished from the ordinary pleasures and satisfactions of this life by three marks: its source is God alone, its nature is spiritual rather than sensible, and its duration is eternal. Thomas does not deny that [[creation|creatures]] can afford a certain satisfaction — food nourishes, friendship consoles, beauty delights. But these creaturely satisfactions are not blessedness; they are at best foretastes and at worst distractions. True blessedness is the possession of the supreme Good, the *summum bonum* who is God Himself. "All thy blessedness and thy happiness lieth in God alone" (053-015): the adverb "alone" is the weight-bearing word. No combination of [[creation|creatures]], however excellent, can constitute blessedness, because blessedness is the satisfaction of the soul's infinite desire, and only the infinite God can satisfy an infinite desire.

## In The Imitation of Christ

### The Elusiveness of Creaturely Happiness

Thomas observes with acute psychological realism that human beings pursue happiness with desperate energy and remarkably little success. The soul seeks blessedness in honour and finds that honour is a vapour; in pleasure and finds that pleasure cloys; in wealth and finds that wealth breeds anxiety; in human love and finds that human love is fragile and mortal. "How great is the blessedness of the devout soul!" (107-004) — the exclamation implies a contrast: the blessedness of the devout soul is great precisely because the alternatives are so small.

This elusiveness is not accidental but structural. Creatures were never designed to be the soul's ultimate good; they were designed to lead the soul to God. When the soul stops at the creature instead of passing through it to the Creator, it frustrates its own deepest longing. The creature cannot give what it does not possess; it cannot confer blessedness because it is not itself blessed in the ultimate sense. "Never shall there be any [[silence|quiet]] there, nor any comfort for the lost" (024-036, applied analogously): the soul that seeks its rest in [[creation|creatures]] is a soul that has missed its destination.

### Blessedness in the Devout Life

Thomas insists that blessedness is not exclusively an eschatological reality; it is already possessed, in foretaste, by the soul that lives in union with God. "How great is the blessedness of the devout soul!" (107-004): the devout soul — the soul that prays, that reads Scripture, that receives the Sacraments, that practises [[love|charity]] — experiences a blessedness that is real, even if incomplete. The peace that surpasses understanding (Philippians 4:7) is a present possession, not a future hope.

This present blessedness is experienced primarily as peace. The devout soul is at peace with God ([[sin|sins]] are forgiven), at peace with itself (the war of the [[sin|passions]] is subdued by grace), and at peace with others ([[love|charity]] has replaced rivalry and resentment). This triplex peace is the "blessedness" that Thomas celebrates — not an emotional high but a settled state of the soul that rests in God. It is compatible with suffering, with [[desolation|dryness]], even with desolation, because its ground is not feeling but faith. The soul that possesses God possesses blessedness, even when it does not feel blessed.

### The Cross as the Way to Blessedness

Thomas characteristically refuses to separate blessedness from the Cross. "The servants of the cross find the way of blessedness and of true light" (093-023): the Cross is not the obstacle to blessedness but its path. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who embraces the Cross — who accepts suffering, who mortifies self-will, who renounces creaturely [[comfort|consolation]]s — is not postponing blessedness but entering into it. This is the deepest paradox of The Imitation: that the way of renunciation is the way of fulfilment, that the death of self is the birth of joy.

The connection between Cross and blessedness is not mechanical but mystical. [[cross|The Cross]] detaches the soul from [[creation|creatures]], and detachment from [[creation|creatures]] is the condition for attachment to God. The soul that is still clutching at [[world|the world]] cannot receive blessedness, because its hands are full. [[cross|The Cross]] pries the fingers open, painfully but necessarily, so that the soul can receive the gift that God has been waiting to give.

### Eternal Blessedness

The consummation of blessedness is eternal. Thomas's eschatology is restrained but clear: the blessedness that is tasted here is possessed there; the peace that is intermittent here is permanent there; the union with God that is obscure here is luminous there. "Grant me, O most sweet and loving [[jesus-christ|Jesus]], to rest in Thee above every creature... above all health and beauty, above all glory and honour" (038-022): the prayer ascends toward the blessedness that eye hath not seen, where God will be all in all.

This eternal blessedness is not a different kind of happiness from the blessedness of the devout life on earth; it is the same blessedness, perfected. The soul that has learned to find its happiness in God on earth is already practising for heaven; the soul that has not learned this will find heaven itself uncongenial, for heaven is nothing other than the full and eternal possession of what it has never desired.

## Key Passages

> "All thy blessedness and happiness lieth in God alone. If therefore thou wouldst be happy and blessed, thou must forsake all things for God, and cleave to Him alone." (053-015 to 053-017, paraphrase)

> "The servants of the cross find the way of blessedness and of true light." (093-023)

> "How great is the blessedness of the devout soul, that hath the Lord Jesus for its Beloved, its Teacher, its Comforter, and its God!" (107-004)

> "Grant me, O most sweet and loving Jesus, to rest in Thee above every creature, above all health and beauty, above all glory and honour, above all power and dignity, above all knowledge and subtlety." (038-022)

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of blessedness in The Imitation is grounded in the Beatitudes, where [[jesus-christ|Christ]] declares blessed (*beati*) the [[poverty-of-spirit|poor in spirit]], the meek, the mourners, the merciful, the pure in heart — the very dispositions that Thomas commends throughout his work. The blessedness of the Beatitudes is counter-intuitive: it belongs to those whom [[world|the world]] would not call blessed. Thomas inherits this reversal and makes it the centre of his teaching on happiness. The Psalmist's declaration — "Blessed is the man that walketh not in the counsel of the ungodly... but his delight is in the law of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Psalm 1:1-2) — provides the Old Testament pattern of blessedness as rootedness in God. The promise that "in Thy presence is fulness of joy; at Thy right hand there are pleasures for evermore" (Psalm 16:11) identifies the source and goal of blessedness. The Pauline doxology — "Eye hath not seen, nor ear heard, neither have entered into the heart of man, the things which God hath prepared for them that love Him" (1 Corinthians 2:9) — guards the mystery of eternal blessedness. The vision of the New [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], where "God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes" (Revelation 21:4), is the consummation toward which every foretaste of blessedness points.
