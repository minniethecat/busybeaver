# Self-Love

The inordinate and disordered love of oneself that sets the self against God and neighbor — in The Imitation of Christ, self-love is the root from which every other vice springs, the great obstacle to interior peace, and the primary affection that must be mortified for the soul to advance in union with God.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, self-love is not the healthy care for one's own existence that God ordains — it is the disordered turning of the soul toward itself rather than toward God. Thomas à Kempis identifies self-love as the deepest source of human resistance to grace. Where [[humility]] empties the self to make room for God, self-love fills the self with self, leaving no space for the divine indwelling.

Self-love manifests in subtle and overt forms: in the unwillingness to accept correction, in the preference for one's own will over the will of God, in the secret satisfaction taken in praise, and in the reluctance to surrender comfortable attachments. It is, as The Imitation teaches, the great impediment to the love of God because it anchors the heart in the creature rather than the Creator.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation of Christ treats self-love not merely as one vice among many but as the root and principle of all disordered affection. The text's call to "go forth from himself utterly, and retain nothing of self-love" (036-032) reveals the radical nature of the Kempian spiritual program: not the moderation of self-love but its complete renunciation.

### Self-Love as the Root of Sin

The Imitation traces all inward unrest and outward conflict to the unbroken attachment to self. Self-love inclines the soul to grasp at what pleases it and to recoil from what pains it — and in this grasping and recoiling, the soul is perpetually agitated. The text warns of "self-love taking possession of thee" (046-023) as the condition in which the will, the affections, and the understanding are all bent inward, incapable of the generous outward movement that constitutes charity.

### The Path Beyond Self-Love

Book III holds out the remedy: the soul that "goes beyond self-love" goes "all straightforward to the love of Me" (095-044). The movement beyond self-love is not achieved by self-effort alone — it is the fruit of grace cooperating with the soul's willing renunciation. The text presents the crucifixion of self-love as the necessary condition for the birth of divine love in the soul: one love must die for the other to live.

### Key Passages

> "Go forth from himself utterly, and retain nothing of self-love." (036-032)

> "Self-love take possession of thee." (046-023)

> "Beyond self-love, they go all straightforward to the love of Me." (095-044)

## Biblical Foundation

The scriptural foundation for the rejection of self-love is Christ's own call to discipleship: "If anyone desires to come after Me, let him deny himself, and take up his cross, and follow Me" (Matthew 16:24). Paul warns that in the last days "men will be lovers of themselves" (2 Timothy 3:2), placing self-love at the head of a catalogue of vices that characterize the spirit of the age. The apostolic antidote is the kenotic pattern of Christ Himself, who "made Himself of no reputation" (Philippians 2:7) — the very opposite of self-love — and Paul's exhortation to "esteem others better than himself" (Philippians 2:3).

## Practical Application

The teaching of The Imitation on self-love calls the [[disciple]] to an unsparing examination of motive: how much of what passes for zeal, for devotion, even for love, is in reality self-love in disguise? The practical path is the daily mortification of self-preference — in small choices as much as in great renunciations — trusting that as self-love diminishes, the love of God expands to fill the space it vacates.
