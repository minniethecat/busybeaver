# Wisdom

The practical knowledge of how to live well before God — in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], true wisdom is radically redefined: it is not the fruit of study or intellectual brilliance but the habit of humility, the [[contempt-of-the-world|renunciation of the world]], and the intimate possession of [[Jesus Christ]]. "That is the highest wisdom, to cast [[world|the world]] behind us, and to reach forward to the heavenly kingdom" (001-022).

## Definition

Wisdom, in the Kempian vocabulary, is a supernatural prudence — the capacity to order one's life toward God by rightly judging the value of [[creation|created things]]. Thomas à Kempis draws a sharp contrast between worldly wisdom and heavenly wisdom throughout The Imitation. [[world|World]]ly wisdom consists in the accumulation of knowledge, reputation, and skill in managing earthly affairs; heavenly wisdom consists in humility, self-knowledge, and the deliberate choice to prefer eternal goods to temporal ones. "It is better to have a small portion of wisdom with humility, and a slender understanding, than great treasures of sciences with vain self-esteem" (044-036). The wise man is not the one who knows many things but the one who lives well: "What doth it profit thee to enter into deep discussion concerning the Holy Trinity, if thou lack humility?" (001-012). This redefinition of wisdom echoes the biblical tradition in which "the [[reverence|fear of the Lord]] is the beginning of wisdom" (Proverbs 1:7), but Thomas sharpens it into an intensely practical principle: wisdom is measured not by what one knows but by how one lives before God.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats wisdom as one of the governing themes of the spiritual life, weaving it through all four books of The Imitation. His treatment is distinctive in its relentless practicality — wisdom is never an abstraction but always a matter of concrete choices, habits, and dispositions.

### The Highest Wisdom: Detachment

The most radical claim Thomas makes about wisdom appears early in Book I: "That is the highest wisdom, to cast [[world|the world]] behind us, and to reach forward to the heavenly kingdom" (001-022). This is not a rejection of creation but a reordering of desire — the wise person sees that "all is vanity, save to love God, and Him only to serve" (001-022). True wisdom therefore involves a comprehensive detachment from riches, honours, and the desires of the flesh, all of which Thomas diagnoses as forms of vanity that distract the soul from its true end. The highest wisdom is, in effect, the practical living-out of the Ecclesiastes theme: vanity of [[vanity|vanities]], all is vanity except the love and ser[[sin|vice]] of God.

### Wisdom in Conduct

Thomas devotes considerable attention to the practical expression of wisdom in daily life. "This is great wisdom, not to be hasty in action, or stubborn in our own opinions" (004-008). The wise person is deliberate rather than impulsive, slow to speak and quick to listen: "A part of this wisdom also is not to believe every word we hear, nor to tell others all that we hear, even though we believe it" (004-009). Wisdom here takes the form of [[prudence|discretion]] — the ability to sift information, to withhold judgment, and to seek counsel from those better instructed. This is the wisdom not of the scholar but of the mature [[discipleship|disciple]] who has learned through experience the dangers of rash speech and hasty action.

### Wisdom and Humility

The inseparable connection between wisdom and [[humility]] is one of Thomas's most emphatic teachings. "It is great skill to know how to live with [[jesus-christ|Jesus]], and to know how to hold [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] is great wisdom" (033-019). But holding [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] requires humility — "Be thou humble and peaceable and [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] shall be with thee" (033-019). The proud man, by contrast, "quickly drive[s] away [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] and lose[s] His favour" by turning "away to the outer things" (033-019). Wisdom and pride are mutually exclusive: the wise man knows his own weakness and frailty, and this self-knowledge is the foundation of his humility. "It is better to have a small portion of wisdom with humility... than great treasures of sciences with vain self-esteem" (044-036).

### Heavenly Wisdom as Gift

In Book III, the discourse shifts from practical counsel to interior prayer, and wisdom is increasingly presented as a gift that must be asked for. "Give me, O [[jesus-christ|Lord]], heavenly wisdom, that I may learn to seek Thee above all things and to find Thee; to relish Thee above all things and to love Thee; and to understand all other things, even as they are, according to the order of Thy wisdom" (064-037). Wisdom here becomes synonymous with the capacity to love God rightly — to "relish" Him above all [[creation|creatures]] and to see all other things in their proper proportion. This wisdom is "heavenly" precisely because it cannot be produced by human effort; it descends as a gift from above, and the appropriate posture toward it is prayerful petition.

## Key Passages

> "That is the highest wisdom, to cast the world behind us, and to reach forward to the heavenly kingdom. It is vanity then to seek after, and to trust in, the riches that shall perish. It is vanity, too, to covet honours, and to lift up ourselves on high. It is vanity to follow the desires of the flesh and to labour for that for which we must afterwards suffer more grievous punishment." (001-022 to 001-025)

> "This is great wisdom, not to be hasty in action, or stubborn in our own opinions. A part of this wisdom also is not to believe every word we hear, nor to tell others all that we hear, even though we believe it. Take counsel with a man who is wise and of a good conscience; and seek to be instructed by one better than thyself, rather than to follow thine own inventions." (004-008 to 004-010)

> "It is great skill to know how to live with Jesus, and to know how to hold Jesus is great wisdom. Be thou humble and peaceable and Jesus shall be with thee. Be godly and quiet, and Jesus will remain with thee." (033-019 to 033-020)

> "Give me, O Lord, heavenly wisdom, that I may learn to seek Thee above all things and to find Thee; to relish Thee above all things and to love Thee; and to understand all other things, even as they are, according to the order of Thy wisdom." (064-037)

## Biblical Foundation

The Kempian doctrine of wisdom is deeply rooted in the Wisdom literature of the Old Testament and the apostolic contrast between worldly and divine wisdom in the New. The declaration that "all is vanity, save to love God" (001-022) is a direct echo of Ecclesiastes 1:2: "[[vanity|Vanity]] of [[vanity|vanities]], all is vanity." The priority of humility over intellectual attainment reflects Proverbs 1:7 — "The [[reverence|fear of the Lord]] is the beginning of knowledge" — and Proverbs 9:10: "The [[reverence|fear of the Lord]] is the beginning of wisdom." The practical character of wisdom — deliberation, slowness to speak, teachability — mirrors the description of heavenly wisdom in James 3:17: "The wisdom that is from above is first pure, then peaceable, gentle, and easy to be intreated, full of [[forgiveness|mercy]] and good fruits."

The sharp opposition between worldly and heavenly wisdom is most fully developed by the [[apostle-paul|Apostle Paul]] in 1 Corinthians 1:19–25, where God declares that He "will destroy the wisdom of the wise" and has made the wisdom of [[world|the world]] foolishness. Thomas's teaching that the highest wisdom is to "cast [[world|the world]] behind us" (001-022) and that "no wisdom profiteth" without God's guidance (051-012) stands squarely in this Pauline tradition. The prayer for heavenly wisdom in Book III (064-037) also echoes the sapiential prayer of Solomon in Wisdom 7:7: "Wherefore I prayed, and understanding was given me."

## Related Concepts

- [[humility]] — The indispensable companion of wisdom; without humility, wisdom degenerates into pride and vain self-esteem
- [[knowledge]] — [[world|World]]ly knowledge is distinguished from and subordinated to heavenly wisdom; knowledge without [[love|charity]] "profiteth nothing"
- [[prudence]] — The natural virtue perfected by heavenly wisdom; prudence without grace is mere calculation
- [[fear-of-the-lord]] — The biblical foundation of wisdom (Proverbs 1:7); the reverent awe that orders the soul toward God
- [[vanity]] — The foil to true wisdom; the wise man sees that all [[creation|created things]] are vanity apart from the love and ser[[sin|vice]] of God
- [[contemplation]] — The activity of the wise soul; wisdom inclines the mind toward the contemplation of God and heavenly things
- [[discernment]] — The practical fruit of wisdom; the capacity to distinguish true good from apparent good
- [[simplicity]] — Wisdom simplifies the soul's desires, reducing them to the one necessary thing: the love of God
- [[peace]] — The fruit of heavenly wisdom; the soul ordered by wisdom enjoys interior tranquility
- [[grace]] — The source of heavenly wisdom; it is "given" and "received" rather than acquired by natural effort
