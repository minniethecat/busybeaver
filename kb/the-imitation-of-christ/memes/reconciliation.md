# Reconciliation

Reconciliation names the central work of Christ: to bring back together what sin has separated. Saint Paul teaches that "God was in Christ, reconciling the world unto himself, not imputing their trespasses unto them" (2 Corinthians 5:19). The word itself — *reconciliatio* — means a bringing together again, a restoration of friendship after estrangement. In the Christian understanding, the estrangement is humanity's doing (sin), and the reconciliation is God's doing (grace). The reconciled soul is one that has received the "ministry of reconciliation" (2 Corinthians 5:18) and now lives in peace with God and neighbor.

## Definition

Reconciliation operates at two levels in Christian theology. Objectively, it is the once-for-all accomplishment of Christ on the cross: "when we were enemies, we were reconciled to God by the death of his Son" (Romans 5:10). Subjectively, it is the ongoing application of that reconciliation to each soul — the turning from sin, the receiving of pardon, the restoration of friendship. The two levels are linked by the sacraments, especially baptism and penance, which are the ordinary means by which the objective reconciliation touches the individual Christian. "And you, that were sometime alienated and enemies in your mind by wicked works, yet now hath he reconciled in the body of his flesh through death" (Colossians 1:21–22).

## In The Imitation of Christ

*The Imitation* is deeply concerned with the subjective side of reconciliation — the soul's turning from sin and the restoration of intimacy with God. Kempis writes as a spiritual director whose primary concern is not to explain the atonement doctrinally but to lead the disciple into the experience of being reconciled.

### The Call to Return

The *Imitation* repeatedly sounds the note of return: "Return unto the Lord, and forsake the world" (032-003). The prodigal son is the implicit model — the soul that has wandered into the "region of unlikeness" and must come to itself and return to the Father. What Kempis adds to the parable is the insistence that this return is not a single event but a daily discipline: every morning, every examination of conscience, every confession is a small reconciliation.

### Peace with Neighbor

Reconciliation with God is inseparable from reconciliation with neighbor. Kempis urges the disciple to "seek peace and pursue it" (018-004), to "bear with others and to be borne with" (021-003). The horizontal dimension of reconciliation — forgiveness of injuries, patience with others' faults, the refusal to nurse grievances — is the test of whether the vertical reconciliation is genuine. "He who is not at peace with his neighbor cannot be at peace with God" (cf. Matthew 5:23–24).

### The Sweetness of Reconciliation

Kempis repeatedly praises the peace that follows reconciliation: "The humble man hath great peace of heart" (013-004). The reconciled soul is not troubled by ambition, rivalry, or resentment, because it has nothing to defend and everything to receive. This peace is the experiential proof that reconciliation is real.

## Biblical Foundation

The theme of reconciliation runs from the Old Testament — God's repeated restoration of Israel after exile and apostasy — to its definitive fulfillment in the New Testament. Christ's parable of the prodigal son (Luke 15:11–32) is the Gospel's most vivid picture of reconciliation: the father runs to meet the returning son, and the feast begins. Saint Paul makes reconciliation the summary of his gospel: "All things are of God, who hath reconciled us to himself by Jesus Christ, and hath given to us the ministry of reconciliation" (2 Corinthians 5:18). The command to be reconciled before offering sacrifice (Matthew 5:24) shows that ritual worship is void without restored relationship.
