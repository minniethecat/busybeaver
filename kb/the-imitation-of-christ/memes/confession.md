# Confession

The humble acknowledgment of [[sin|sins]], [[sin|fault]]s, and human infirmity before God — a practice that Thomas à Kempis treats not merely as a sacramental obligation but as a vital discipline of the interior life, essential for cleansing the conscience, recovering lost devotion, and approaching the Holy Table with a heart made pure by "contrition and humble confession" (103-007).

## Definition

Confession, in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], is the act by which the soul honestly names its [[sin|sins]], weaknesses, and inward troubles before God, and — where the context implies — before a priest. Thomas does not separate sacramental confession from the broader spiritual attitude of humble self-accusation; the two are continuous expressions of the same interior disposition. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who confesses rightly does so not out of mere duty but from a genuine [[contrition|sorrow for sin]] and a desire to be cleansed. "I will acknowledge my sin unto Thee; I will confess to Thee, [[jesus-christ|Lord]], my infirmity" (057-002) — this verse, placed at the head of the chapter "Of Confession of Our Infirmity," sets the tone: confession is both a naming of particular [[sin|sins]] and a comprehensive acknowledgment of one's creaturely weakness. Its purpose is not to wallow in guilt but to remove every obstacle that stands between the soul and God, so that the penitent "mayest feel no burden, nor know anything which bringeth thee remorse and impedeth thy free approach" (103-008 to 103-009) to the Sacrament.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis weaves the theme of confession throughout The Imitation, giving it concentrated treatment in Book IV in the context of preparation for [[communion|Holy Communion]]. For him, confession is not an isolated rite but an integral part of the soul's daily conversion — the regular cleansing without which devotion grows cold and the devil gains a foothold.

### Confession of Infirmity as a Way of Life

Book III, Chapter 57 — "Of Confession of Our Infirmity and of the Miseries of This Life" — frames confession within the broader recognition of human weakness. Thomas has the [[discipleship|disciple]] confess not only particular [[sin|sins]] but the very condition of being a fallen creature: "It is often a small thing which casteth me down and troubleth me" (057-003). This confession of infirmity is not a counsel of despair but an act of humility that places the soul in its true relation to God. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who honestly confesses "I am weak, I am prone to fall, I am in need of Thy grace at every moment" is precisely the one whom God delights to help. Confession thus becomes the portal through which [[mercy|divine mercy]] enters: the acknowledgment of poverty is the condition for receiving riches.

### Confession as Preparation for Holy Communion

The most sustained treatment of confession in The Imitation appears in Book IV, where Thomas instructs the [[discipleship|disciple]] on worthy reception of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]]. He urges the communicant: "Diligently examine thy conscience and with all thy might with true contrition and humble confession cleanse and purify it" (103-006 to 103-007). Confession here works in tandem with contrition — [[contrition|sorrow for sin]] opens the wound, and confession cleanses it. The goal is practical and immediate: that the soul may approach the altar without the burden of unconfessed sin, "nor know anything which bringeth thee remorse and impedeth thy free approach" (103-009). Thomas is a spiritual realist; he knows that an un[[silence|quiet]] conscience makes genuine devotion impossible, and that confession is the God-given remedy for that dis[[silence|quiet]].

### The Danger of Deferred Confession

Thomas warns sharply against the temptation to postpone confession. "What profiteth it to put off for long time the confession of thy [[sin|sins]], or to defer [[communion|Holy Communion]]?" (106-030 to 106-031). The devil, Thomas observes, exploits delay: he stirs up "anxiety or some other trouble touching confession" (106-022 to 106-023) precisely in order to prevent the soul from obtaining devotion. The remedy is promptness — go quickly to confession, lay aside scruple, and receive the peace that follows. Thomas counsels the [[discipleship|disciple]] to seek the "counsel of wise men, and lay aside anxiety and scruple" (106-024), recognizing that the over-scrupulous delay of confession is itself a snare of the enemy. Humble confession is not meant to be a burden but a liberation.

### Confession and the Devil's Tactics

Thomas is keenly aware that the devil actively works to prevent confession. In Chapter 43 of Book III, he depicts the enemy sowing "many evil thoughts, that he may work in thee weariness and terror, and so draw thee away from prayer and holy reading" (043-032 to 043-033). The devil's strategy is to make the soul feel so unworthy, so burdened, and so distracted that it abandons the very practices — prayer, reading, and humble confession — that would restore it. The antidote is to "despise [the devil's] evil instigations, and to laugh him to scorn" (043-042), persisting in confession and devotion regardless of inward turmoil. Confession is thus not only a remedy for sin but an act of spiritual warfare: by it the devil is routed and the soul regains its peace.

## Key Passages

> "I will acknowledge my sin unto Thee; I will confess to Thee, Lord, my infirmity. It is often a small thing which casteth me down and troubleth me." (057-002 to 057-003)

> "Diligently examine thy conscience and with all thy might with true contrition and humble confession cleanse and purify it, so that thou mayest feel no burden, nor know anything which bringeth thee remorse and impedeth thy free approach." (103-006 to 103-009)

> "What profiteth it to put off for long time the confession of thy sins, or to defer Holy Communion? Cleanse thyself at once, spit out the venom quickly, hasten to take the remedy, and thou shalt feel thyself better than if thou didst long delay." (106-030 to 106-031)

> "Often also too much carefulness or some anxiety or other touching confession hindereth from obtaining devotion. Do thou according to the counsel of wise men, and lay aside anxiety and scruple, because it hindereth the grace of God and destroyeth devotion of mind." (106-022 to 106-024)

## Biblical Foundation

The practice of confession in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] rests on a deep biblical foundation. The opening of the confession chapter — "I will acknowledge my sin unto Thee" (057-002) — is a direct echo of Psalm 32:5: "I acknowledged my sin unto Thee, and mine [[sin|iniquity]] have I not hid." The call to confess flows from the conviction that God desires truth in the inward parts (Psalm 51:6) and that "he that covereth his [[sin|sins]] shall not prosper: but whoso confesseth and forsaketh them shall have [[forgiveness|mercy]]" (Proverbs 28:13).

The New Testament witness is equally central. James 5:16 exhorts believers to "confess your [[sin|fault]]s one to another, and pray one for another, that ye may be healed." John assures the faithful that "if we confess our [[sin|sins]], He is faithful and just to forgive us our [[sin|sins]], and to cleanse us from all un[[justice|righteousness]]" (1 John 1:9). Thomas's insistence on examining the conscience before [[communion|Communion]] echoes Paul's admonition in 1 Corinthians 11:28: "let a man examine himself, and so let him eat of that bread, and drink of that cup." The urgency of prompt confession — "what profiteth it to put off?" — recalls the prophetic call of [[isaiah|Isaiah]] 55:6: "Seek ye the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] while He may be found, call ye upon Him while He is near."
