# Detachment

The spiritual discipline of holding all created goods with an open hand — renouncing not necessarily the possession of things but the inordinate attachment to them, so that the soul may love God without division of heart. Detachment is the practical expression of the first commandment: to love the Lord with the whole heart means to love nothing else more than Him, and to love everything else only in Him and for His sake.

## Definition

Detachment (also called renunciation, holy indifference, or poverty of spirit) is the interior freedom by which the soul stands ready to keep or to lose any created good according to God's will. It is not stoic apathy — a refusal to feel — but a reordering of love: the affections are not extinguished but redirected, so that God alone is loved for His own sake and all else is loved in reference to Him. "Set your affection on things above, not on things on the earth" (Colossians 3:2).

Christ's words to the rich young ruler state the radical principle: "If thou wilt be perfect, go and sell that thou hast, and give to the poor, and thou shalt have treasure in heaven: and come and follow me" (Matthew 19:21). Not every disciple is called to literal poverty (though some are), but every disciple is called to the interior disposition of which literal poverty is the outward sign: the willingness to lose all for Christ. "Whosoever he be of you that forsaketh not all that he hath, he cannot be my disciple" (Luke 14:33).

## In The Imitation of Christ

The theme of detachment runs through every book of *The Imitation* as an essential condition of progress in the spiritual life.

### Renounce All, Find All

Kempis records a spiritual maxim of great compactness: "Renounce all things, and thou shalt find all things" (069-006). This paradox is the heart of detachment — the soul that clings to nothing possesses everything, because it possesses God, in whom all goods are contained. The disciple is not impoverished by renunciation but liberated. The saints "renounced all" (018-003) not because they despised creation but because they loved the Creator more.

### The Ongoing Nature of Renunciation

Detachment is not a single act but a repeated one: "Thou hast still many things to renounce" (069-008). The human heart is endlessly resourceful in attaching itself to new objects — even spiritual consolations can become idols. The disciple who has renounced wealth may cling to reputation; the one who has renounced reputation may cling to his own will. There is always something more to surrender.

### Detachment from the World

*The Imitation* gives classic expression to the *contemptus mundi* tradition: "It is not given unto all to renounce this world and its affairs, and to live a life of solitude" (047-001), yet every Christian must cultivate an interior detachment from the world's values. The disciple "renounceth creatures, fleeth from the world, hateth the desires of the flesh" (091-001) — not from misanthropy but from the recognition that "the world passeth away, and the lust thereof" (1 John 2:17).

### Detachment at the Hour of Death

Kempis repeatedly urges the disciple to live now as he will wish to have lived at the hour of death. The detachment that death will force upon us — the loss of wealth, honors, bodily pleasures — should be freely embraced now, while it can be offered as an act of love rather than suffered as a necessity.

## Biblical Foundation

The call to detachment is Christ's own: "If any man will come after me, let him deny himself, and take up his cross daily, and follow me" (Luke 9:23). Saint Paul counts all things "but loss for the excellency of the knowledge of Christ Jesus my Lord" (Philippians 3:8) and urges the Corinthians to "use this world, as not abusing it: for the fashion of this world passeth away" (1 Corinthians 7:31). The Epistle to the Hebrews commends the patriarchs who "confessed that they were strangers and pilgrims on the earth" (Hebrews 11:13). Detachment is the condition of pilgrimage: the traveler carries little, because home lies ahead.
