# Mary

The figure of Mary appears in The Imitation of Christ in two distinct but theologically related forms: Mary of Bethany, the contemplative sister who "chose the better part" (Luke 10:42), and the Blessed Virgin Mary, Mother of God, invoked in the text's most solemn prayer. Both figures embody the receptive disposition that is the heart of Thomas's spiritual program — the capacity to sit still before God, to receive His word without resistance, to let grace do its work in silence. In a work so relentlessly focused on self-denial, activity, and struggle, Mary represents the other pole: the soul that has already arrived at rest because it has wholly surrendered to God.

## Identity

Mary of Bethany is the sister of Martha and Lazarus, known from the Gospel of Luke (10:38-42) and John (11:1-44, 12:1-8). She is the one who "sat at Jesus' feet, and heard his word" while Martha was "cumbered about much serving" — and Christ declared that she "hath chosen that good part, which shall not be taken away from her." The Blessed Virgin Mary is the Mother of Jesus Christ, and in The Imitation she is invoked as "Thy most blessed Mother, the glorious Virgin Mary" (113-021). Thomas distinguishes the two figures — one a disciple, one the Mother of God — but unites them in the single paradigm of virginal receptivity. The text also speaks of "the Virgin's womb" (098-063) when referring to the Incarnation, and of "Confessors, Virgins" (018-010) among the company of saints, with Mary as the archetype of consecrated virginity.

## Biblical Narrative

The biblical account of Mary unfolds across the Gospels. The Annunciation (Luke 1:26-38) is the moment of Mary's fiat — "Behold the handmaid of the Lord; be it unto me according to thy word" — the act of obedience by which the Incarnation became possible. The Visitation (Luke 1:39-56) gives us the Magnificat, Mary's great hymn of praise. The Nativity (Luke 2:1-20) is followed by Mary "keeping all these things, and pondering them in her heart" (Luke 2:19). At Cana (John 2:1-11), Mary intercedes with her Son and instructs the servants, "Whatsoever he saith unto you, do it" — the last recorded words of Mary in the Gospels, and a perfect summary of the disciple's posture. At the foot of the Cross (John 19:25-27), Christ entrusts Mary to the beloved disciple and the disciple to Mary, establishing a maternal relationship with the whole Church. For Mary of Bethany, the key episode is Christ's visit to her home (Luke 10:38-42), where she sits at His feet while Martha serves — an image Thomas would certainly have recognized as the model of the contemplative life.

### Key Events

- The Annunciation — Mary's fiat, the Incarnation through obedience (Luke 1:38)
- The Visitation — the Magnificat, Mary's prophetic hymn (Luke 1:46-55)
- At the feet of Christ — Mary of Bethany chooses "the better part" (Luke 10:38-42)
- At the foot of the Cross — Mary receives the beloved disciple as her son (John 19:26-27)

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas's treatment of Mary is characteristically restrained — he invokes her not at length but at moments of highest spiritual intensity. The most significant reference comes in Book IV, the culminating section on the Holy Eucharist. In the prayer of preparation for Communion, Thomas writes: "Grant unto me... to bring to mind... that surpassing and burning love of Thine, which led Thee... to come down from the bosom of the Father into this vale of tears, namely, into the most sacred womb of the ever-blessed Virgin" — the reference to the Incarnation through "the Virgin's womb" (098-063) places Mary at the center of salvation history. Later, in the prayer after Communion, Thomas invokes "Thy most blessed Mother, the glorious Virgin Mary" (113-021) in a litany of the saints whose intercession the communicant implores. The tone is one of profound reverence rather than theological elaboration — Mary is present as the one through whom Christ entered the world, the first and most perfect tabernacle.

The Mary of Bethany reference is more oblique. When Thomas speaks of how "Jesus came to Bethany, to the house of Martha and Mary" (070-018), the context is the consoling presence of Christ among those who love Him — a figure of the devout soul receiving its Lord. The contemplative repose of Mary of Bethany at Christ's feet is the unspoken model for every passage in The Imitation that calls the soul to be still before God.

> "Grant unto me... to bring to mind... that surpassing and burning love of Thine, which led Thee... to come down from the bosom of the Father into this vale of tears, namely, into the most sacred womb of the ever-blessed Virgin." (098-063)

> "This day, as Thy most blessed Mother, the glorious Virgin Mary, brought Thee forth corporally into this world." (113-021)

## Spiritual Significance

Mary exemplifies the virtue that is hardest for the active temperament: **contemplative receptivity**. In a work that constantly exhorts the soul to *do* — to deny itself, to take up the cross, to struggle, to labor — Mary represents the prior truth that all doing must be preceded by receiving. She "kept all these things and pondered them in her heart" (Luke 2:19); she sat at Christ's feet while Martha served. This is not passivity but the highest activity of the soul: the active reception of divine grace. For Thomas, Mary is also the model of **humility raised to its perfection** — she who called herself "the handmaid of the Lord" (Luke 1:38) is exalted above all creatures precisely because she made herself nothing. Her maternal intercession, invoked at the moment of Communion (113-021), makes her not only an exemplar but an advocate — the Mother who presents her children's needs to her Son.
