# Galilee

The northern region of ancient Palestine — a land of hills, lake, and humble villages — where Jesus of Nazareth spent His childhood, called His first disciples by the Sea of Galilee, preached the Sermon on the Mount, and lived the greater part of His earthly ministry in obscurity and lowly obedience, making it the geographical cradle of the Christian life of discipleship that The Imitation of Christ summons every soul to embrace.

## Location

Galilee is the northernmost region of ancient Palestine, bounded by the Mediterranean Sea to the west, the Jordan Valley and Sea of Galilee to the east, the Lebanon mountains to the north, and the Plain of Esdraelon to the south. In the time of Christ, it was a fertile and populous district under Herodian rule, divided into Upper Galilee (mountainous) and Lower Galilee (hill country). Key settlements included Nazareth — the town of Christ's childhood — and the fishing villages around the Sea of Galilee: Capernaum, Bethsaida, and Magdala. The region was traversed by the Via Maris, the great trade route connecting Egypt with Damascus, giving it a cosmopolitan character that earned it the prophetic name "Galilee of the nations" (Isaiah 9:1).

## Biblical Significance

Galilee is central to the Gospel narrative as the primary theater of Jesus's earthly ministry. After His baptism by John in the Jordan, Jesus "returned in the power of the Spirit into Galilee" (Luke 4:14), and it was there — not in Jerusalem — that He called His first disciples, delivered His most famous teachings, and performed many of His miracles. The prophet Isaiah foretold that "the land of Zebulun and the land of Naphtali... by the way of the sea, beyond Jordan, in Galilee of the nations" would see a great light (Isaiah 9:1-2), and Matthew presents Jesus's Galilean ministry as the fulfillment of this prophecy (Matthew 4:14-16). It was also the place of the Resurrection appearance, when the angel told the women, "He goeth before you into Galilee; there shall ye see Him" (Matthew 28:7).

### Key Events at This Place

- **The Annunciation** — The angel Gabriel appeared to Mary in Nazareth of Galilee, announcing the Incarnation (Luke 1:26-38).
- **The Hidden Life** — Jesus spent approximately thirty years in Nazareth, living in obedience to Mary and Joseph, growing "in wisdom and stature, and in favour with God and man" (Luke 2:52).
- **The Calling of the First Disciples** — By the Sea of Galilee, Jesus called Simon Peter, Andrew, James, and John from their fishing boats, saying, "Follow Me, and I will make you fishers of men" (Matthew 4:18-22).
- **The Sermon on the Mount** — On a Galilean hillside, Christ delivered the Beatitudes and the core teaching of the Kingdom (Matthew 5-7).
- **The Post-Resurrection Commission** — The risen Lord met His disciples in Galilee and gave the Great Commission (Matthew 28:16-20).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Though Thomas à Kempis never names Galilee directly, the entire spiritual program of The Imitation is rooted in the pattern of life that Christ lived in Galilee — a life of hidden humility, lowly obedience, and the calling of ordinary souls to radical discipleship. The opening words of the text establish Christ's earthly life as the supreme model: "He that followeth me shall not walk in darkness, saith the Lord. These are the words of Christ; and they teach us how far we must imitate His life and character, if we seek true illumination" (001-003 to 001-005). The very phrase "the life of Jesus Christ" — with its focus on His earthly, historical existence — points to the incarnate life lived in the villages and hills of Galilee.

### The Hidden Life and the Pattern of Obedience

Galilee is, above all, the place of the hidden life — the thirty years Christ spent in obscurity before His public ministry. This hiddenness is the very pattern that The Imitation holds up for every disciple. Thomas devotes an entire chapter to "the obedience of one in lowly subjection after the example of Jesus Christ" (050-001). He teaches that "he who striveth to withdraw himself from obedience, withdraweth himself also from grace" and exhorts the disciple: "Learn therefore quickly to submit thyself to him who is over thee, if thou seekest to bring thine own flesh into subjection" (050-003, 050-008). The hidden, obedient life of Nazareth is the lived theology behind this counsel — Christ Himself, the Lord of all, submitting to Mary and Joseph in a Galilean carpenter's shop.

The mystery that grounds all of this is the Incarnation itself — the eternal Word taking flesh in a particular place and time. When the devout soul prepares for Communion, it "calleth to mind the mystery of Christ's incarnation and His Passion, and is inflamed with the love of Him" (106-064 to 106-065). Galilee is the geographic theater of that mystery: the place where God became man, where the eternal entered time, and where the divine humility was lived out in the ordinary rhythms of a rural Jewish village.

### The Calling of Ordinary Souls

Galilee was not a place of learned scribes or priestly elites — it was a region of fishermen, farmers, and laborers. It was precisely from this humble soil that Christ drew His apostles, and The Imitation repeatedly calls the soul to the same radical self-abandonment that these Galilean disciples exemplified. Thomas writes that "both the disciples who followed Him, and all who desire to follow Him, He plainly exhorteth to bear their cross, and saith, If any man will come after Me, let him deny himself and take up his cross" (037-136 to 037-138). The echo of the Galilean lakeshore is unmistakable — the call to leave nets, boats, and father, and to follow without reserve.

This call is made personal and universal in the interior dialogue of the disciple with Christ: "If thou wilt be My disciple, deny thyself. If thou wouldst possess the blessed life, despise the life which now is. If thou wilt be exalted in heaven, humble thyself in the world. If thou wilt reign with Me, bear the cross with Me" (093-019 to 093-022). Every Christian, in every age, is summoned to the same total surrender that Christ asked of the fishermen of Galilee.

### The Witness of the Galilean Apostles

Thomas à Kempis reflects with reverent wonder on the transformation of the apostles — those simple Galilean men who became the princes of the Church. "Thine apostles witness whom Thou hast made princes in all lands. Yet they had their conversation in this world blameless, so humble and meek, without any malice or deceit, that they even rejoiced to suffer rebukes for Thy Name's sake" (059-037 to 059-040). The movement from Galilean fishermen to apostolic witnesses is one of the great testimonies to the power of divine grace working through the lowly — a theme central to The Imitation's entire spirituality.

### The Prayer of the Lowly Disciple

The posture of the soul before God in The Imitation mirrors the posture of the Galilean disciple before Christ — a simple, trusting self-offering. The disciple prays: "Make me a pious and lowly disciple, as Thou wert wont to be kind, that I may walk according to every nod of Thine" (087-052 to 087-053). This is the essence of the Galilean spirituality: not theological sophistication or ecclesiastical rank, but humble, attentive obedience — the willingness to be taught, led, and transformed by the Master who walked the Galilean roads.

> "Make me a pious and lowly disciple, as Thou wert wont to be kind, that I may walk according to every nod of Thine." (087-052 to 087-053)

## Symbolic Meaning

In Christian tradition, Galilee carries a rich symbolic resonance that extends beyond geography. It is the "Galilee of the Gentiles" — the place where light first dawns on those who sit in darkness, signifying God's universal salvific will and His preference for the marginal and the overlooked. The contrast between Galilee and Jerusalem runs through the Gospels: Jerusalem is the center of religious power, the Temple, and the priestly establishment; Galilee is the periphery, the place of ordinary life, and the unexpected theater of divine visitation. In the spiritual life, "Galilee" represents the hidden, unglamorous, daily terrain where most discipleship is actually lived — not in moments of public triumph but in the quiet obedience of the carpenter's shop, the patient labor of the fishing boat, and the thousand unseen acts of fidelity that The Imitation champions as the true path to holiness.

The Imitation's insistence that "they were poor in earthly things, but rich above measure in grace and virtue" (018-027 to 018-028) — though spoken of the holy fathers — perfectly captures the Galilean paradox: that the richest divine life is found not in the centers of earthly power but in the humble places where souls say yes to God without reservation.
