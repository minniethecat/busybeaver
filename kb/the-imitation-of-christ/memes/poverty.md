# Poverty

The state of the soul that acknowledges its own radical emptiness and complete dependence on God — in The Imitation of Christ, poverty is valued chiefly in its spiritual sense as the interior recognition that "without Me ye can do nothing," and this poverty of spirit becomes the opening through which the riches of grace pour in.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, poverty refers primarily to the interior condition of the soul that knows it possesses nothing of its own — no virtue, no merit, no strength — and stands before God in utter need. The text does not disparage material poverty but consistently elevates poverty of spirit as the foundational disposition of the Christian life.

Thomas a Kempis develops poverty of spirit as the fruit of true humility: the soul that has genuinely descended into self-knowledge discovers not hidden resources but fundamental emptiness. This discovery, which would be despair if it were the last word, becomes the gateway to hope when it is met by divine grace. "There is no better remedy," writes the author, than to acknowledge one's "own poverty" (034-060), for this acknowledgment opens the soul to receive what only God can give.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation treats poverty both as a beatitude to be embraced and as a remedy for the soul's deepest disorder — the illusion of self-sufficiency.

### Poverty of Spirit and Interior Freedom

Book I, Chapter XXI links poverty of spirit to the very possibility of spiritual consolation: "Oftentimes it is from poverty of spirit that the wretched body is drawn to complaint" (021-046). The body complains in poverty because it has not yet learned the blessedness of having nothing; but the soul that embraces poverty of spirit discovers that its emptiness is precisely the space into which God pours His fulness.

The same passage presents poverty as a condition of authentic prayer: it is the "poverty-stricken" who cry out most earnestly, for they know they have nothing of their own to offer and must receive everything as gift (021-046). The rich soul — rich in its own virtues, merits, and consolations — prays as one who gives; the poor soul prays as one who begs.

### Poverty and the Remedy for Self-Sufficiency

Book III, Chapter VII identifies the soul's own poverty as the condition for receiving divine remedy: "There is no better remedy, than to deny ourselves and to be delivered from ourselves, and in all things to stand in fear, and to acknowledge our own poverty" (034-060–061). The threefold prescription — self-denial, holy fear, and acknowledgment of poverty — constitutes the soul's preparation for grace. The one who denies self ceases to rely on its own resources; the one who fears acknowledges the danger of trusting anything created; the one who confesses poverty opens empty hands to receive.

### Nature's Complaint Against Poverty

Book III, Chapter LIV contrasts the attitudes of nature and grace toward poverty: "Nature quickly complaineth of poverty and of trouble; Grace beareth poverty with joy" (091-067). Nature sees poverty as deprivation — the loss of what it deserves or needs. Grace sees poverty as freedom — the shedding of everything that hinders the soul's flight toward God. This contrast is not merely descriptive but diagnostic: the soul's reaction to poverty (material or spiritual) reveals whether it is governed by nature or by grace.

### Poverty and the Cross

Book II, Chapter XII treats material poverty within the imitation of Christ, who "had not where to lay His head" (026-046). The disciple who complains of poverty is invited to contemplate the poverty of the Master: "Christ stood in need of alms, and darest thou complain of any?" The poor Christ is the pattern and consolation of the poor disciple — not only in material want but in the deeper poverty of bearing the world's rejection and the Father's apparent absence.

### Poverty-Stricken Before the King

Book IV offers a culminating image: the soul approaches the altar "poverty-stricken to the King of heaven" (098-005). This is the proper posture of every communicant — not the confidence of one who has prepared worthily but the beggar's trust that the King gives freely to those who have nothing. The liturgical poverty of the soul mirrors its ontological poverty: before the Lord of heaven, every creature is poor, and the only acceptable offering is the acknowledgment of this poverty.

## Biblical Foundation

The first beatitude — "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven" (Matthew 5:3) — is the scriptural foundation of the Kempian teaching on poverty. Christ Himself "though He was rich, yet for your sakes He became poor, that you through His poverty might become rich" (2 Corinthians 8:9), establishing poverty as the path of redemption. The counsel to "sell what you have and give alms; provide yourselves money bags which do not grow old, a treasure in the heavens that does not fail" (Luke 12:33) relativizes all material possession. James declares: "Has God not chosen the poor of this world to be rich in faith?" (James 2:5). And the risen Christ warns the church at Laodicea: "You say, 'I am rich, have become wealthy, and have need of nothing' — and do not know that you are wretched, miserable, poor, blind, and naked" (Revelation 3:17) — spiritual poverty is the truth, whether the soul acknowledges it or not.

## Practical Application

The practice of poverty, as taught in The Imitation, is learned not by divesting oneself of material goods alone but by the daily acknowledgment of one's spiritual emptiness before God. The disciple who confesses "I have nothing of my own" is not indulging in false humility but stating the ontological truth of the creature before the Creator. This acknowledgment, made concrete in prayer and tested in tribulation, becomes the foundation of the spiritual life: the poor soul receives everything as gift, therefore it is grateful for everything, attached to nothing, and ready to let go of anything. "Poverty-stricken to the King of heaven" (098-005) is not a temporary posture but the permanent identity of the redeemed.
