# Nature

The fallen human condition that is the great antagonist of the spiritual life — that which "will suffer no rival" (032-014), that which is ever "wearied with many cares" (085-003), that which "must be conquered" (092-011) and "crucified" (085-065) — not the creation as God made it but human nature as sin has wounded it, the stubborn weight within every soul that pulls downward against every movement of grace, in the teaching of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Nature, in the vocabulary of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], almost always means fallen human nature — not the created goodness of man but the corruption of that goodness through sin. It is not identified with the body alone; it is the whole complex of inclinations, desires, and habits that resist God's will and prefer self to the Creator. "It is not in the nature of man to bear the cross, to love the cross, to chastise the body and bring it into [[obedience|subjection]], to flee from honours, to bear contempt willingly, to despise self and desire to be despised" (037-080). This catalogue makes clear that nature is defined by what it cannot do and will not choose — the entire program of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[discipleship|disciple]]ship runs against its grain.

## In The Imitation of Christ

### Nature as the Rival of Grace

Thomas teaches that nature is not merely weak but actively resistant to grace. [[grace|Grace]] and nature are set in opposition as two principles at war within the soul. "Thy Beloved is such, by nature, that He will suffer no rival, but alone desireth to possess thy heart, and as a King to sit upon His own throne" (032-014). [[jesus-christ|Christ]] will not share the heart with nature — one must yield. The language is absolute: nature is a rival king, and the soul is the contested throne.

This rivalry manifests practically in every spiritual exercise. Nature shrinks from the cross; grace embraces it. Nature seeks comfort; grace accepts [[desolation|dryness]]. Nature demands recognition; grace hides in silence. "The necessities of nature" (041-041) are acknowledged as real — Thomas is not a Manichaean — but they must be borne "with sorrow," not indulged as though they were innocent.

### The Necessity of Mortification

Because nature opposes grace, it must be mortified — put to death by deliberate acts of self-denial. Thomas writes that the devout man "doth violence to his fleshly nature and crucifieth the lusts of the flesh" (085-065). The verb is *crucifigere* — the same word used of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s death. [[mortification|Mortification]] is not mere self-improvement; it is a participation in the Cross. What was crucified in [[jesus-christ|Christ]] must be crucified in the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian.

The counsels are practical. The soul that would overcome nature must "withstand its beginnings" — refusing the first suggestion of a sinful inclination before it gains strength — must examine the particular [[sin|sins]] "to which by nature we are most inclined" (025-038), and must accept that the battle will not end in this life: "For this cause nature shall sometimes be filled with bitterness, and shall be constrained to bear things which are against thy nature" (086-049).

### The Interior War Fought Daily

Nature is not defeated in a single battle but in a lifelong campaign. "I fight with myself, and am made grievous even unto myself, while the spirit seeketh to be above and the flesh to be beneath" (085-004). The self is divided against itself; the spiritual life is a civil war. Thomas offers no promise that nature will cease to resist — only that grace is stronger, if the soul will consistently side with grace.

The daily conflict includes the necessities that nature imposes: hunger, fatigue, the need for sleep, the distractions of the senses. These are not [[sin|sins]], but they are the conditions of the pilgrim state against which the spirit must contend. "Nature must be sustained, but to desire superfluous things is not lawful" (063-030). The line between necessity and indulgence is narrow and must be watched constantly.

> It is not in the nature of man to bear the cross, to love the cross, to chastise the body and bring it into subjection, to flee from honours, to bear contempt willingly, to despise self and desire to be despised. (037-080)

### Nature and Consolation

Nature craves [[comfort|consolation]] and shuns desolation. But Thomas teaches that this very craving is disordered: "it is the nature of man to be deceived by fancies" (067-023). The soul that follows its natural inclination toward comfort will be deceived, because nature cannot distinguish between true [[comfort|consolation]] (which comes from God) and false (which comes from [[world|the world]] or the devil). [[discernment|Discernment]] requires the mortification of natural judgment.

The teaching reaches its sharpest point in Book III, Chapter 54: "Thou knowest how little I am able to do of myself... that I may conquer my wicked nature" (092-004). The admission of nature's wickedness is not self-hatred but honesty — the necessary first step toward relying wholly on grace.
