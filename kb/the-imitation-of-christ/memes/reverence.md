# Reverence

**Reverence** (*reverentia*) is a fundamental disposition of the devout soul in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]*. It denotes the profound respect, [[fear-of-the-lord|holy fear]], and humble devotion with which one approaches the divine presence. Thomas à Kempis treats reverence as essential to the spiritual life, and nowhere more so than in the context of sacramental worship — receiving the Body of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]].

## Reverence in the Presence of God

The theme of reverence appears early in The Imitation, in the counsel on the exercise of a [[conscience|good conscience]]: "We must reverence God with all our hearts wheresoever we are" (019-005). This universal reverence is not confined to sacred spaces or liturgical moments; the whole of life is lived *coram Deo* — before the face of God.

In Book III, the soul acknowledges receiving divine benefits "with great reverence and ardent love" (059-004). Reverence is the natural response of the creature who recognizes the disproportion between the gift and the giver — between the infinite generosity of God and human unworthiness.

## Eucharistic Reverence

The fullest treatment of reverence in The Imitation occurs in Book IV, which is devoted to the Blessed Sacrament. The opening chapter poses the question directly: "With how great reverence [[jesus-christ|Christ]] must be received" (097-001). Kempis answers by evoking Old Testament types:

> Moses, Thy servant, Thy great saint, made an ark of incorruptible wood, and covered it with the purest gold, wherein to place the tables of the Law. And I, a corruptible creature, shall I so lightly receive Thee, the Maker of the Law and the Giver of life? [097-034]

The contrast is deliberate and humbling: if the Ark of the Covenant — containing only stone tablets — was treated with such profound veneration, how much more reverence is owed to the living God made present in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]]?

The text recalls the Israelites' reverence before the Ark: "how great reverence and devotion they showed, when they carried on before the Ark of the Testimony" (097-072). These Old Testament precedents establish a minimum standard that the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian must far exceed.

## Fear and Reverence in Approach

Kempis insists that the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] is to be approached "with fear and reverence" (101-017). This [[fear-of-the-lord|holy fear]] is not servile terror but the awe proper to an encounter with divine majesty. The soul preparing for [[communion|Communion]] prays: "I draw nigh unto Thee with hope and reverence, believing that Thou art present here in the Sacrament" (100-016).

The proper disposition combines fear with affection: "with fear, reverence, and affection, receive the Body of thy [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (108-022). Fear without love would be despair; love without fear would be [[pride|presumption]]. Reverence holds both in balance.

The [[conscience|examination of conscience]] before [[communion|Communion]] includes "supplicating reverence, with full faith and pious desire" (103-004). The exterior posture of kneeling and the interior posture of humility together express the soul's creaturely acknowledgment before its Creator.

## Nature vs. Grace

Book III, Chapter 54 draws a sharp contrast between nature and grace regarding reverence. "[[nature|Nature]] willingly receiveth honour and reverence; but [[grace|Grace]] faithfully attributeth all honour and reverence to God" (091-023). Unfallen [[nature|human nature]] craves being reverenced; grace redirects all reverence to its proper object — God alone.

This dichotomy reveals reverence as a grace-given virtue. In our fallen state, we are inclined to seek honor for ourselves; only grace enables us to give God the reverence that is His due.

## The Example of the Saints

The prayer in Book IV invokes the saints as exemplars of reverence. The devout soul asks to approach the Sacrament "with such vehement desire and becoming reverence which any Saint ever had or could have" (113-009). The saints — who enjoy the beatific vision — set the standard for creaturely reverence before the divine.

The chapters on devout reception continually return to this theme: "with supplicating reverence" the soul should receive the Sacrament (114-031). The language is liturgical, humble, and filled with longing.

## Practical Manifestations

The Imitation also notes reverence in communal religious life. Looking back to the fervor of the early monks, Kempis remarks: "what reverence and devotion under the rule of the master showed they in all things!" (018-042). Reverence is not only interior but visible — expressed in posture, silence, attention, and obedience.

The practical counsel to receive [[communion|Communion]] "with reverence" is balanced by the pastoral note that imperfect reverence due to drowsiness or weakness is not blameworthy, provided the heart's intention is right (106-051). The interior disposition matters more than the exterior circumstance.
