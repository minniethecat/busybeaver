# Affliction

The experience of spiritual and bodily suffering that purifies the soul — in The Imitation of Christ, affliction is the divine medicine through which the spirit is strengthened even as the outward self is wasted: "the more the body is wasted by affliction, the more is the spirit strengthened within" (037-071).

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of Christ, affliction is the concrete experience of suffering — whether bodily pain, spiritual desolation, or mental anguish — that God permits for the soul's purification and growth. Thomas à Kempis treats affliction not as an anomaly to be eliminated but as the ordinary condition of the soul's journey toward God, a condition to be embraced with [[patience]] and even gratitude.

Affliction overlaps with [[adversity]] but carries a more personal and interior connotation: adversity names the external circumstance of trial, while affliction emphasizes the felt experience of suffering that penetrates the heart. In the spiritual life, affliction is the furnace in which dross is burned away and the gold of virtue emerges.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation of Christ returns repeatedly to the theme of affliction as the crucible of spiritual growth, insisting upon its necessity and its hidden fruitfulness.

### Affliction as Spiritual Medicine

Book I teaches that the soul untested by affliction remains spiritually immature: "as long as he suffereth no affliction, he is in danger" (013-064). Prosperity conceals character; affliction reveals it. The person who has not been tried by suffering does not yet know the true state of their own soul. Affliction is therefore a diagnostic instrument in the hands of God — it shows the [[disciple]] what they truly are and what still needs to be purified.

The same chapter counsels that the soul should respond to affliction with patience: "if he behave himself patiently in the time of adversity, then is there hope of great profit" (013-065). Affliction is not mere endurance but an opportunity — the occasion for the exercise and strengthening of virtue.

### The Interior Wasting and Strengthening

Book III articulates the paradoxical dynamic of affliction: "the more the body is wasted by affliction, the more is the spirit strengthened within" (037-071). This is the central paradox of the spiritual life: outward diminishment becomes inward increase. What the flesh loses through suffering, the spirit gains in fortitude, patience, and reliance upon God.

The text also speaks of the "great wretchedness and affliction" that a devout soul experiences when separated from the sweetness of divine presence (022-021). This is spiritual affliction — the pain of divine absence — which is often more difficult to bear than bodily suffering. The soul in desolation feels itself abandoned, yet The Imitation teaches that this too is a form of divine medicine: the withdrawal of consolation purifies love, teaching the soul to serve God for His own sake rather than for the sweetness He gives.

### Affliction and Conformity to Christ

The Imitation frames affliction within the mystery of the Cross: "tribulation and adversity, through love of conformity to Him" (037-073). The soul that suffers is not being punished but being conformed — shaped into the likeness of Christ, who "learned obedience by the things which He suffered" (Hebrews 5:8). Affliction is thus a participation in the passion of Christ, a share in the fellowship of His sufferings.

## Biblical Foundation

The apostolic writings interpret affliction within the horizon of eternal glory. Paul writes that "our light affliction, which is but for a moment, is working for us a far more exceeding and eternal weight of glory" (2 Corinthians 4:17). The afflictions of the present are not worth comparing with the glory to be revealed (Romans 8:18). The Psalmist assures that "many are the afflictions of the righteous, but the Lord delivers him out of them all" (Psalm 34:19). And Peter exhorts believers not to be surprised at the fiery trial, but to rejoice in so far as they share Christ's sufferings (1 Peter 4:12–13).

## Practical Application

The teaching of The Imitation on affliction calls the soul to a patient and even grateful acceptance of suffering as divine medicine. The practical response is not to seek affliction for its own sake, but to receive it when it comes as permitted by God for the soul's good — trusting that "the more the body is wasted by affliction, the more is the spirit strengthened within" (037-071). The soul in affliction should resist the temptation to bitterness and instead unite its suffering to the passion of Christ, in whom all affliction finds its meaning.
