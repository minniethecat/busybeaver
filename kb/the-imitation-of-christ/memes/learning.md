# Learning

The acquisition of knowledge through study and intellectual effort — in The Imitation of Christ, learning is not condemned but rigorously subordinated: it is profitable when it serves humility and love, and becomes a spiritual danger when it feeds [[curiosity|curiosity]], [[pride|pride]], or the desire for reputation.

## Definition

In The Imitation of Christ, learning refers primarily to the intellectual acquisition of knowledge — the reading of books, the study of theology, the mastery of disputation, the accumulation of scholarly reputation. Thomas à Kempis, himself a man of letters who spent his life copying manuscripts, does not despise learning as such. What he attacks is learning pursued as an end in itself, learning that puffs up rather than builds up, learning that distracts from the one thing necessary.

The antithesis is not between learning and ignorance but between learning that leads to God and learning that becomes a substitute for Him: "What doth it profit to argue about hidden and dark things, concerning which we shall not be even reproved in the judgment, because we knew them not? Oh, grievous folly, to neglect the things which are profitable and necessary, and to give our minds to things which are curious and hurtful!" (003-005–009).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis addresses the question of learning most directly in Book I, Chapters II through V, and returns to the theme throughout as a danger particularly acute for the learned religious.

### The Vanity of Curious Learning

Book I, Chapter III opens the critique with the question: "What have we to do with talk about genus and species?" (003-010). The scholastic preoccupation with logical categories — genus, species, distinctions — is swept aside by the claim that "he to whom the Eternal Word speaketh is free from multiplied questionings" (003-011). The one Word of God renders all human words about God provisional and, when pursued for their own sake, distracting.

The weariness induced by sheer intellectual consumption is noted with psychological acuity: "It wearieth me oftentimes to read and listen to many things; in Thee is all that I wish for and desire" (003-018–019). The multiplication of books and opinions creates not wisdom but fatigue. The remedy is silence before God: "Let all the doctors hold their peace; let all creation keep silence before Thee: speak Thou alone to me" (003-019–020).

### How to Read Holy Scripture

Book I, Chapter V provides the positive discipline: "It is Truth which we must look for in Holy Writ, not cunning of words" (005-002). The reader is not to approach Scripture as a literary or rhetorical object but as the living address of God. "All Scripture ought to be read in the spirit in which it was written" (005-003) — a principle that requires the reader's spirit to be conformed to the Spirit in which the text was composed, a conformity achieved not by scholarly technique but by humility and prayer.

The practical counsel is specific: "Let not the weight of the writer be a stumbling-block to thee, whether he be of little or much learning, but let the love of the pure Truth draw thee to read" (005-007–009). The authority of a text does not reside in the author's credentials but in the truth it conveys. "Ask not, who hath said this or that, but look to what he says" (005-009–010).

Most pointedly, the reader is warned: "If thou wouldst profit by thy reading, read humbly, simply, honestly, and not desiring to win a character for learning" (005-015–016). The desire "to win a character for learning" — to be known as learned — corrupts the act of reading at its root, transforming what should be a reception of truth into a project of self-construction.

### Knowledge Without Love

Book I, Chapter II sets the frame: "Every man naturally desireth to know; but what doth knowledge avail without the fear of God?" (002-004). The natural desire for knowledge is acknowledged but immediately qualified. "A lowly rustic who serveth God is better than a proud philosopher who pondereth the courses of the stars and neglecteth himself" (002-010–011). The point is not that astronomy is sinful but that knowledge of the cosmos without knowledge of self is spiritually useless.

True self-knowledge — "he who knoweth himself becometh vile in his own sight" (002-013) — is the only learning that conduces to humility. All other learning, when it becomes an occasion for pride, "rather hindereth than helpeth" the soul's progress toward God.

### The Better Portion

The Imitation of Christ consistently prefers the experiential knowledge born of love to the abstract knowledge born of study. "Far nobler is that doctrine which floweth down from the divine fulness above, than that which is acquired laboriously by human study" (068-023–025). The contrast is not between two kinds of intellectual content but between two modes of knowing: one received in humility from God, the other constructed by human effort. Learning of the first kind is a gift; learning of the second, when isolated from the first, is a burden.

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of Christ draws its caution toward learning from several scriptural sources. Paul's confession — "Knowledge puffs up, but love builds up" (1 Corinthians 8:1) — provides the fundamental distinction: learning without love is spiritually destructive. His further warning — "if anyone thinks he knows anything, he knows nothing yet as he ought to know" (1 Corinthians 8:2) — relativizes all human knowledge before the divine.

The Preacher's observation — "of making many books there is no end, and much study is wearisome to the flesh" (Ecclesiastes 12:12) — anticipates Thomas's sense of weariness with intellectual multiplication. Jeremiah's divine word — "Let not the wise man glory in his wisdom … but let him who glories glory in this, that he understands and knows Me" (Jeremiah 23:23–24) — reorders the hierarchy of values: the knowledge of God surpasses all other knowledge.

The beginning of wisdom is "the fear of the Lord" (Proverbs 1:7), and Paul's radical statement — "though I have the gift of prophecy, and understand all mysteries and all knowledge … but have not love, I am nothing" (1 Corinthians 13:2) — confirms that learning, even at its most exalted, is worthless without charity. This is the principle that governs The Imitation's entire treatment of the subject.
