# Temptation of Christ

The Temptation of Christ in the wilderness — the forty days during which Jesus fasted and was tempted by Satan — is the foundational biblical event underlying The Imitation of Christ's extensive teaching on spiritual combat. Thomas à Kempis does not narrate the event directly but assumes it as the theological premise for every chapter on resisting temptation: Christ has gone before, Christ has conquered, and the disciple fights in His strength.

## Biblical Narrative

After His baptism in the Jordan, Jesus was led by the Spirit into the wilderness, where He fasted forty days and forty nights. At the point of His physical extremity, Satan approached Him with three temptations. First: "If thou be the Son of God, command that these stones be made bread" (Matthew 4:3) — the temptation to use divine power for self-gratification. Jesus answered: "Man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word that proceedeth out of the mouth of God" (Matthew 4:4). Second: "Cast thyself down" from the pinnacle of the temple (Matthew 4:6) — the temptation to presumption, to test God's protection. Jesus answered: "Thou shalt not tempt the Lord thy God" (Matthew 4:7). Third: "All these things will I give thee, if thou wilt fall down and worship me" (Matthew 4:9) — the temptation to idolatrous power. Jesus answered: "Get thee hence, Satan: for it is written, Thou shalt worship the Lord thy God, and Him only shalt thou serve" (Matthew 4:10). Then the devil left Him, and angels came and ministered unto Him.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation does not recount the Temptation of Christ as a narrated event. Its presence is pervasive but implicit: the entire work is a manual for the soul engaged in the same spiritual combat Christ fought in the wilderness. Book I, Chapter 13 ("Of Resisting Temptation") opens with the declaration: "So long as we live in the world, we cannot be without tribulation and temptation" (013-001). The chapter teaches that "temptations turn greatly unto our profit, although they be grievous and troublesome" (013-009), and that "there is no man wholly free from temptations so long as he liveth, because we have the root of temptation within ourselves, in that we are born in concupiscence" (013-016–018). Behind every such exhortation stands the figure of Christ in the wilderness — the one who was tempted in all points yet without sin, and whose victory is the ground of the disciple's hope.

The event is also the unstated premise of the disciple's recourse to Scripture in temptation. Christ answered each temptation with "It is written," and The Imitation consistently directs the tempted soul to the words of God. The pattern established in the wilderness — temptation, Scripture, victory, angelic consolation — is the pattern the devout soul is called to reproduce in every spiritual trial.

## Spiritual Significance

The Temptation of Christ is the great reversal of the Fall. Where Adam in the garden succumbed to the serpent's temptation — the lust of the flesh, the lust of the eyes, and the pride of life — Christ in the wilderness conquered the same tempter with the same threefold assault. The Imitation draws on this typology without always naming it: every chapter on resisting temptation, on humility, on watchfulness, and on trust in God assumes that victory is possible because Christ has already won it. The disciple does not face temptation alone; he faces it united to Christ, whose forty days in the wilderness sanctified every subsequent Christian's struggle.
