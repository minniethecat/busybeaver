# Mortification

The deliberate, grace-empowered putting to death of the old man — his disordered affections, his attachment to [[creation|creatures]], his subtle self-seeking, his resistance to the will of God — so that the new man, created in [[justice|righteousness]] and true holiness, may rise and reign. In The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], mortification is not an optional counsel for the specially devout; it is "the King's highway of the [[cross|Holy Cross]]" (037-025), the path that every [[discipleship|disciple]] must walk who would follow [[jesus-christ|Christ]] to glory. "[[grace|Grace]] studieth self-mortification, resisteth sensuality, seeketh to be in [[obedience|subjection]]" (091-014).

## Definition

Mortification, from the Latin *mortificatio* (a putting to death), is the ascetical practice by which the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian, cooperating with [[grace|divine grace]], puts to death the sinful inclinations that remain after baptism — what Paul calls "the deeds of the body" (Romans 8:13) and "your members which are upon the earth" (Colossians 3:5). In the vocabulary of The Imitation, mortification extends beyond the obvious mortification of sensual desires to encompass the "entire mortification of the affections" (017-012), the "mortification of thy [[sin|sins]]" (080-007), and the daily practice of "self-mortification" by which the soul "resisteth sensuality" and "seeketh to be in [[obedience|subjection]]" (091-014). It is, in Thomas's understanding, the practical form that love of the Cross takes in the ordinary circumstances of daily life.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas treats mortification not as a discrete topic cordoned off in a single chapter but as a thread woven through the entire fabric of the spiritual life. It appears in connection with conversion, the Cross, grace, and the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], each context illuminating a different facet of this essential practice.

### The Radical Demand of Conversion

Thomas places mortification at the very threshold of the spiritual life. The soul that would follow [[jesus-christ|Christ]] must undergo not merely a superficial amendment of behaviour but a "change of character and entire mortification of the affections" (017-012). The word "entire" is crucial: mortification is not piecemeal reform but a comprehensive reordering of the soul's loves. The affections — the deep inclinations of the heart that draw the soul toward certain goods and away from others — must be mortified because they have been disordered by sin. The soul that loves [[creation|creatures]] more than the Creator, that prefers comfort to the Cross, that seeks its own will rather than God's, has affections that need to die.

This mortification of the affections is the negative side of conversion; its positive side is the acquisition of new affections, ordered toward God. The soul cannot simply add love of God to its existing loves; the old loves must be put to death so that the new love may have room to grow. Thomas's teaching here echoes the Pauline pattern of putting off the old man and putting on the new (Colossians 3:9-10).

### Daily Mortification and the Cross

The most concentrated treatment of mortification in The Imitation appears in the context of the Cross. Thomas writes: "Go where thou wilt, thou shalt find no higher way above, nor safer way below, than the way of the [[cross|Holy Cross]] and of daily mortification" (037-025). Mortification is here identified with the [[cross|way of the Cross]] — not as an alternative path but as the same path, considered from the side of human cooperation. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] bore the Cross once for all; the [[discipleship|disciple]] bears it daily through the practice of mortification.

Daily mortification means the continual, unglamorous, often unnoticed work of saying no to self: the word left unspoken, the comfort refused, the preference surrendered, the irritation borne in silence. These small deaths, accumulated over a lifetime, constitute the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s participation in the Cross. Thomas rejects the notion that mortification requires dramatic feats of asceticism; what it requires is [[faith|fidelity]] in the small, daily opportunities for self-denial that every life provides.

### Mortification and Grace

Thomas is careful to insist that mortification is the work of grace, not of unaided human effort. "[[grace|Grace]] studieth self-mortification, resisteth sensuality, seeketh to be in [[obedience|subjection]]" (091-014). It is grace that studies mortification — that is, grace that makes mortification its study, its business, its characteristic activity. The soul that attempts mortification by sheer willpower will either fail through weakness or succeed through pride, and the latter outcome is the more dangerous of the two. Mortification that is truly [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian is mortification that flows from grace and returns to grace, the [[holy-spirit|Holy Spirit]] putting to death the deeds of the body so that the life of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] may be manifested.

The connection between mortification and the sacraments is implicit but clear. In the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], Thomas notes that the faithful approach with "contrition" for their [[sin|sins]] (097-083); the sacrament both requires and effects the mortification of sin. "Mortification of thy [[sin|sins]]" (080-007) is the preparation of heart that makes the soul a fit recipient of [[grace|divine grace]]; grace in turn empowers a deeper mortification. The two move in a spiral: mortification opens the soul to grace, and grace enables a more thorough mortification.

### Mortification of the Flesh

Thomas includes bodily mortification within the broader practice: "mortification of the flesh" is listed among the marks of a true lover of God (071-019), paired with "contempt of [[world|worldly things]]." But the flesh, for Thomas, is not merely the body; it is the whole complex of disordered self-love that resists God. Mortification of the flesh includes, but is not limited to, bodily penance. Its primary target is the will — the stubborn, self-assertive will that refuses to bend to God. "Through mortification and the contempt of [[world|worldly things]]" the saints "are found to have advanced in spiritual progress" (071-019); the mortification that matters most is the interior mortification that breaks the will's attachment to its own way.

The teaching is balanced: Thomas does not counsel contempt for the body as such (the body is God's creation), nor does he minimise the value of bodily penance. What he insists on is that bodily mortification is a means, not an end; the end is the liberation of the soul for union with God. "Little is the whole of thorough mortification" (068-029) — a rueful admission that even the most zealous [[discipleship|disciple]] falls short of the complete [[death|dying to self]] that the spiritual life demands.

## Key Passages

> "Go where thou wilt, thou shalt find no higher way above, nor safer way below, than the way of the Holy Cross and of daily mortification." (037-025)

> "If thou wouldst profit by reading, read with humility, simplicity, and faith... If thou wouldst profit, thou must suffer tribulation patiently, and with mortification of heart." (017-012; paraphrase of the broader context)

> "Little is the whole of thorough mortification. I know not how it is, and by what deceit we are led astray." (068-029)

> "Through mortification and the contempt of worldly things, and mortification of the flesh, are found to have advanced in spiritual progress." (071-019)

> "Grace studieth self-mortification, resisteth sensuality, seeketh to be in subjection." (091-014)

## Biblical Foundation

The doctrine of mortification in The Imitation is rooted in the Pauline corpus. The Apostle's teaching — "If ye live after the flesh, ye shall die: but if ye through [[holy-spirit|the Spirit]] do mortify the deeds of the body, ye shall live" (Romans 8:13) — establishes both the necessity of mortification and its agency (through [[holy-spirit|the Spirit]]). The command to "mortify therefore your members which are upon the earth" (Colossians 3:5) provides the vocabulary that Thomas inherits. The declaration that "they that are [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s have crucified the flesh with the affections and lusts" (Galatians 5:24) makes mortification the mark of belonging to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. The dominical saying — "If any man will come after Me, let him deny himself, and take up his cross daily, and follow Me" (Luke 9:23) — is the warrant for Thomas's identification of daily mortification with the [[cross|way of the Cross]]. Paul's personal testimony — "I keep under my body, and bring it into [[obedience|subjection]]" (1 Corinthians 9:27) — models the practice for every [[discipleship|disciple]].

## Related Concepts

- [[cross]] — The [[cross|way of the Cross]] and the way of daily mortification are one and the same; mortification is the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s daily participation in the Cross
- [[self-denial]] — The broader category within which mortification falls; to deny oneself is to mortify the old man
- [[humility]] — The disposition that makes mortification possible; the proud soul cannot mortify itself because it clings to its own excellence
- [[penance]] — The sacramental and practical expression of mortification; penance is the outward form of the inward mortification of sin
- [[contrition]] — The [[contrition|sorrow for sin]] that mortification both expresses and deepens; true contrition mortifies sin at its root
- [[resignation]] — The fruit of mortification; the soul that has mortified its own will is capable of [[resignation|pure resignation]] to the will of God
- [[grace]] — The agent of mortification; "grace studieth self-mortification" (091-014)
- [[conversion]] — The beginning of mortification; conversion demands an "entire mortification of the affections" (017-012)
- [[amendment]] — The positive counterpart of mortification; as the old man is put to death, the new man is amended and reformed
- [[temptation]] — The occasion for mortification; temptation tests the soul's attachment to sin, and mortification severs it
