# Faith

The theological virtue by which the soul en[[trust-in-god|trust]]s itself wholly to God — in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], faith is understood less as the intellectual acceptance of revealed truths and more as a lived fidelity: the daily practice of cleaving to [[Jesus Christ]] through every vicissitude, remaining steadfast when [[comfort|consolation]] is withdrawn, and [[trust-in-god|trust]]ing absolutely in [[providence|divine providence]]. "If thou shalt remain faithful and zealous in labour, doubt not that God shall be faithful and bountiful in rewarding thee" (025-009).

## Definition

Faith, in the Kempian vocabulary, is primarily a matter of the will and the heart rather than the intellect. Thomas à Kempis does not deny the doctrinal content of faith — the soul must "believe in [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and put [its] [[trust-in-god|trust]] in [His] [[forgiveness|mercy]]" (067-029) — but his emphasis falls overwhelmingly on faithfulness as a mode of living. The faithful soul is the one who continues to serve God "without complaint" (017-005), who remains "faithful and zealous in labour" (025-008), and who clings to God when all human supports fail. This conception of faith as persevering fidelity aligns closely with the Hebrew *emunah* ([[perseverance|steadfastness]], reliability) and the Pauline understanding of faith as a life-orienting [[hope|trust in God's promises]]. For Thomas, the primary evidence of faith is not orthodox confession but [[perseverance|patient endurance]]: "Set thyself, therefore, like a good and faithful servant of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], to the manful bearing of the Cross of thy [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (037-090).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Faith pervades The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as the fundamental posture of the soul toward God. Thomas does not devote a discrete treatise to faith but weaves it into the fabric of the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s entire relationship with God, treating it under several interrelated aspects.

### Faith as Fidelity in the Religious Life

The earliest treatment of faith in The Imitation appears in the context of the religious vocation. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who has entered the cloister is exhorted to "remain faithful even unto death" (017-005), embracing the stability and obedience of the common life. Faithfulness here means not seeking after change or escape but accepting the place and the duties God has appointed. "Blessed is he who hath lived a good life in such a body, and brought it to a happy end" (017-005). The faithful religious holds himself "as an exile and a pilgrim upon the earth" (017-005), counting himself "as a fool for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" if [[world|the world]] so judges him.

### The Faithful God and the Faithful Soul

Thomas establishes a reciprocal relationship between divine and human faithfulness. God's fidelity is the ground of the [[discipleship|disciple]]'s confidence: "doubt not that God shall be faithful and bountiful in rewarding thee" (025-009). The [[discipleship|disciple]]'s fidelity, in turn, is the condition of receiving God's reward: "If thou shalt remain faithful and zealous in labour, doubt not that God shall be faithful" (025-008 to 025-009). This mutuality is not a Pelagian bargain but the natural operation of grace — the God who gives the will to be faithful also crowns that faithfulness with blessing. The soul's [[trust-in-god|trust]] is therefore never presumptuous; it rests on the character of God, who is "faithful above all friends" (033-032) and whose help endures "when all men fail" (032-013).

### Faith as Armour in Spiritual Combat

In Book III, faith takes on a martial character. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is exhorted: "thou shalt not even fear thine adversary the devil, if thou be armed with faith and signed with the Cross of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (037-088). Here Thomas echoes Ephesians 6:16 — "the shield of faith, wherewith ye shall be able to quench all the fiery darts of the wicked" — and 1 Peter 5:8–9: "Your adversary the devil... whom resist steadfast in the faith." Faith is depicted as protective armour, an active defence against temptation and demonic assault. It is not a passive state of belief but a weapon with which the [[discipleship|disciple]] actively resists the enemy: armed with faith, the devil's threats lose their power, and the [[discipleship|disciple]] can "manfully" bear whatever cross the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] permits.

### The Inward Voice to the Faithful Soul

The opening of Book III — "Of the inward voice of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] to the faithful soul" (038-001) — signals a crucial development. Faith is the condition for hearing [[jesus-christ|Christ]] speak inwardly. The "faithful soul" is one prepared to listen, to receive, and to obey. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s inward speech is not granted indiscriminately; it is addressed to those who have already demonstrated their fidelity by turning away from the noise of [[world|the world]] and inclining the ear of the heart toward God. The entire dialogue of Book III presupposes a soul made faithful through grace and habit — a soul that has learned, through long practice, to prefer the inward word of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] to all outward voices.

## Key Passages

> "If thou shalt remain faithful and zealous in labour, doubt not that God shall be faithful and bountiful in rewarding thee. It is thy duty to have a good hope that thou wilt attain the victory, but thou must not fall into security lest thou become slothful or lifted up." (025-008 to 025-010)

> "Go to, faithful soul, prepare thy heart for this bridegroom that he may vouchsafe to come to thee and dwell within thee, for so He saith, if any man loveth me he will keep my words: and my Father will love him, and we will come unto him and make our abode with him. Give, therefore, place to Christ and refuse entrance to all others." (026-013 to 026-014)

> "Thou shalt not even fear thine adversary the devil, if thou be armed with faith and signed with the Cross of Christ. Set thyself, therefore, like a good and faithful servant of Christ, to the manful bearing of the Cross of thy Lord, who out of love was crucified for thee." (037-088 to 037-090)

> "Oh foolish and faithless of heart, who lie buried so deep in worldly things, that they relish nothing save the things of the flesh! Miserable ones! they will too sadly find out at the last, how vile and worthless was that which they loved." (022-031)

## Biblical Foundation

The Kempian theology of faith draws primarily from the Pauline and Johannine traditions, with supporting echoes from the Psalms and [[wisdom|Wisdom]] literature. The exhortation to "prepare thy heart for this bridegroom" and the quotation of John 14:23 — "if any man loveth me he will keep my words: and my Father will love him, and we will come unto him and make our abode with him" (026-013 to 026-014) — anchors faith in the mutual indwelling of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and the believer, the characteristic Johannine theme. The martial imagery of faith as armour against the devil (037-088) explicitly recalls Ephesians 6:16 and 1 Peter 5:8–9.

The teaching that God "will be faithful and bountiful in rewarding" the faithful labourer (025-009) reflects the covenantal pattern of the Old Testament, in which God's faithfulness (*hesed*) calls forth Israel's faithful response. The exhortation to remain "faithful even unto death" (017-005) echoes Revelation 2:10: "Be thou faithful unto death, and I will give thee a crown of life." Thomas's insistence that faith must be lived and not merely professed stands in the tradition of James 2:17: "Faith, if it hath not works, is dead, being alone." The Kempian emphasis on fidelity-through-[[temptation|trial]] also draws on Hebrews 11, the great catalogue of the faithful who "obtained a good report through faith" (Hebrews 11:39) by enduring suffering and waiting for the promises.
