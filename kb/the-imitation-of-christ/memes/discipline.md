# Discipline

The ordered regimen of spiritual exercises, self-examination, and ascetical practices by which the soul is trained in virtue — in The Imitation of Christ, discipline is not an optional adornment of the religious life but its indispensable framework, without which "he who is living without discipline is exposed to grievous ruin" (025-068).

## Definition

In The Imitation of Christ, discipline denotes the practical structure of the spiritual life: the daily round of prayer, reading, self-examination, the custody of the senses, and the faithful discharge of one's religious duties. It is the outward form that protects and nurtures the inward fervour of devotion, the "good discipline" (025-048) that renders the religious life stable and fruitful.

Thomas à Kempis presents discipline as the necessary complement of zeal: zeal supplies the fire, discipline supplies the ordered channel through which that fire warms rather than consumes. A soul without discipline is a soul without defense — "exposed to grievous ruin" (025-068) — while a soul that seeks only the easiest discipline "shall always be in distress, because one thing or another will give him displeasure" (025-069–070).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats discipline throughout Book I as the practical armature of the interior life. It is closely linked to [[diligence|diligence]], [[amendment|amendment of life]], and the daily exercises of the religious state.

### The Discipline of Daily Exercises

Book I, Chapter XIX prescribes the regular practice of spiritual exercises as the framework of progress: "According to our resolution so is the rate of our progress, and much diligence is needful for him who would make good progress" (019-011–012). The chapter outlines the discipline of daily self-examination: "In the morning make thy resolves, and in the evening inquire into thy life, how thou hast sped to-day in word, deed, and thought" (019-032–033).

The discipline of regularity itself is protective: a "trivial omission of holy exercises can hardly be made without some loss to us" (019-015–016). Yet discipline is not rigid — a holy exercise "sometimes omitted for the sake of some act of piety, or of some brotherly kindness" can be taken up afterwards; neglected "through distaste or slothfulness" it becomes sin (019-022–024).

### The Example of Well-Disciplined Brethren

In Book I, Chapter XXV, Thomas sets the example of "zealous and godly brethren temperate and of good discipline" against the sorrowful sight of those "walking disorderly, not practising the duties to which they are called" (025-048–049). The community itself is shaped by the discipline — or lack thereof — of its members. Discipline is not merely personal but has a social dimension: "As thine eye observeth others, so again are the eyes of others upon thee" (025-046–047).

### The Danger of Living Without Discipline

The starkest warning comes at the close of Chapter XXV's seventh section: "He who is living without discipline is exposed to grievous ruin" (025-068). This is not hyperbole but a structural claim about the spiritual life — discipline is to the soul what walls are to a city. Without it, the soul is defenseless against the assaults of temptation, the erosions of sloth, and the gradual drift into [[lukewarmness|lukewarmness]].

The paradox of discipline follows immediately: "He who seeketh easier and lighter discipline shall always be in distress, because one thing or another will give him displeasure" (025-069–070). The easy way is the hard way; the disciplined way is ultimately the restful way. Those who evade the rigour of discipline find not freedom but perpetual irritation — every small difficulty becomes insupportable because the soul lacks the training to bear it.

### Discipline and Zeal

Discipline and zeal are paired throughout The Imitation of Christ as the active and structural dimensions of spiritual progress. Book I, Chapter XXV exhorts: "Be zealous, therefore, for thy spiritual profit" (025-004), and immediately grounds this zeal in the concrete discipline of labour: "Now shalt thou labour a little, and thou shalt find great rest, yea everlasting joy" (025-007–008). Zeal without discipline burns out; discipline without zeal grows cold. Both are necessary.

The counsel to "strive also very earnestly to guard against and subdue those faults which displease thee most frequently in others" (025-040–041) exemplifies the disciplined attention to one's own faults that is among the most practical fruits of a well-ordered spiritual life.

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of Christ draws its understanding of discipline from the Pauline images of athletic training and military service. Paul's athletic metaphor — "Every athlete exercises self-control in all things" (1 Corinthians 9:25) and "train yourself for godliness" (1 Timothy 4:7) — presents the spiritual life as one requiring structured effort and consistent practice. "For the moment all discipline seems painful rather than pleasant, but later it yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained by it" (Hebrews 12:11) directly anticipates Thomas's teaching that present labour yields "great rest, yea everlasting joy" (025-008).

The wisdom tradition reinforces the necessity of self-governance: "A man without self-control is like a city broken into and left without walls" (Proverbs 25:28) — a vivid parallel to Thomas's warning about the soul "living without discipline" being "exposed to grievous ruin" (025-068). Paul's assurance that "God gave us a spirit not of fear but of power and love and self-control" (2 Timothy 1:7) grounds discipline not in mere human effort but in the gift of divine grace.
