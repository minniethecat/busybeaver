# Tabernacle

The Tabernacle — the portable tent-sanctuary that accompanied Israel through the wilderness — appears in The Imitation of Christ not as an object of antiquarian interest but as a spiritual type: the place where the soul enters to seek counsel from God, apart from the noise of human opinion and the deceptions of the senses.

## Location

The Tabernacle was the portable sanctuary constructed by Moses at Mount Sinai according to the pattern shown by God (Exodus 25:8–9). It stood at the centre of the Israelite camp during the forty years of wilderness wandering, a tent of acacia wood overlaid with gold, divided into the Holy Place and the Holy of Holies, where the Ark of the Covenant rested beneath the wings of golden cherubim. It was the visible sign of God's presence among His people — the place where heaven touched earth and where Moses spoke with God "face to face, as a man speaketh unto his friend" (Exodus 33:11).

## Biblical Significance

The Tabernacle was the theological centre of Israel's wilderness life. Here the law was read, sacrifices were offered, and the high priest entered once a year on the Day of Atonement to sprinkle blood upon the mercy seat. More importantly, it was the place of oracular inquiry — Moses would enter the tent whenever a question of judgment or direction arose, and God would answer him from between the cherubim (Numbers 7:89). The Tabernacle was thus not merely a cultic structure; it was the divinely appointed means by which the leader of Israel received wisdom for governing the people.

### Key Events at This Place

- **The Giving of the Pattern** — On Mount Sinai, God showed Moses the heavenly pattern of the Tabernacle and commanded, "Let them make me a sanctuary, that I may dwell among them" (Exodus 25:8–9). The Tabernacle's construction was an act of obedience to a divine blueprint.

- **Moses Seeks Counsel** — Whenever doubts or disputes arose, Moses "had recourse to the tabernacle for the solving of all doubts and questionings, and fled to the help of prayer to be delivered from the dangers and wickednesses of men" (075-020). The Tabernacle was the place of divine consultation.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis invokes the Tabernacle exactly once, in Book III, Chapter 39, but the reference is freighted with meaning. Addressing the disciple who complains that he cannot discern God's will amidst the clamour of competing voices, Thomas offers the Tabernacle as the model of interior prayer:

> "Straightway in every cause enterest with Moses into the tabernacle to ask counsel of God; thou shalt hear a divine response and come forth instructed concerning many things that are and shall be." (075-017)

The Tabernacle here is the figure of the recollected soul. Just as Moses left the tumult of the camp — the "dangers and wickednesses of men" (075-020) — and entered the quiet of the tent to hear God's voice, so the disciple must withdraw from the noise of the world and enter the interior sanctuary of prayer. The counsel that Moses received was not his own reasoning but a "divine response" — a supernatural word that resolves doubts no human wisdom can settle.

Thomas's use of the tabernacle is characteristically interior: the physical tent in the wilderness becomes the prayer of the heart. The Moses who entered the tabernacle is the type of every Christian who, beset by perplexity, turns from human counsel to divine consultation. The promise attached to this figure is unqualified: "thou shalt hear a divine response."

> "For always Moses had recourse to the tabernacle for the solving of all doubts and questionings; and fled to the help of prayer to be delivered from the dangers and wickednesses of men." (075-020)

### The Tabernacle of the Body

A second use of the term appears in Book III, Chapter 49, where the language of tabernacle is applied to the mortal body itself. Christ speaks to the soul that longs for eternal life: "When thou feelest the desire of eternal happiness to be poured into thee from above, and longest to depart from the tabernacle of this body, that thou mayest contemplate My glory without shadow of turning, enlarge thine heart" (086-004).

Here the tabernacle is no longer the tent of meeting but the tent of the flesh — the temporary dwelling in which the soul sojourns during its earthly pilgrimage. The usage draws on 2 Corinthians 5:1, where Paul speaks of the earthly "tabernacle" (tent-dwelling) of the body being dissolved and replaced by an eternal house in heaven. Thomas's Christ does not rebuke the desire to depart; He instructs the disciple to "enlarge thine heart" — to receive the holy longing as a gift rather than suppress it.

The same word that in Book III, Chapter 39 signified the place of divine counsel now signifies the place of divine exile. The body is a tabernacle, a temporary tent pitched in a wilderness, and the soul that has tasted the desire of eternal happiness rightly longs to strike the tent and enter the permanent dwelling. Yet Thomas does not permit contempt of the body: the tabernacle, however temporary, was constructed according to a divine pattern, and the soul must honour it until the hour appointed for its departure.

> "When thou feelest the desire of eternal happiness to be poured into thee from above, and longest to depart from the tabernacle of this body, that thou mayest contemplate My glory without shadow of turning, enlarge thine heart." (086-004)

## Symbolic Meaning

In Christian typology, the Tabernacle prefigures both the Incarnation — God tabernacling among men in the flesh of Christ (John 1:14) — and the interior life of prayer. For Thomas à Kempis, writing within the devotio moderna tradition, the second sense is paramount: the Tabernacle is the soul at prayer, the place where God speaks and the disciple listens. The "counsel of God" sought in the tabernacle is not esoteric knowledge but the practical wisdom needed to navigate the dangers of the spiritual life without falling into self-deception or the deceptions of others.

Yet Thomas also deploys the tabernacle in its secondary sense, as the mortal body — the tent in which the soul makes its wilderness journey. These two uses are not contradictory but complementary: the body that is a tabernacle of exile becomes, through prayer, a tabernacle of encounter. The same physical frame that weighs the soul down and makes it long for departure is also the appointed place where the soul enters with Moses to hear the divine voice. The Christian life is thus lived within a double tabernacle: the body (the temporary tent) and the heart (the tent of meeting), and the one is never abandoned for the other.
