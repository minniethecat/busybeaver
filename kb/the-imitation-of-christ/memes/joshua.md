# Joshua

Joshua, the successor of Moses who led Israel across the Jordan into the Promised Land — in The Imitation of Christ he is invoked not for his conquests but for his single failure: the deception by the Gibeonites, which Thomas à Kempis traces to one cause — "they asked not counsel at the mouth of the Lord" (075-025).

## Identity

Joshua son of Nun was Moses' attendant and successor, commissioned by God to lead Israel into Canaan after Moses' death. He led the people across the Jordan, conquered Jericho, and oversaw the division of the land among the tribes. His farewell address — "choose you this day whom ye will serve... but as for me and my house, we will serve the Lord" (Joshua 24:15) — is among the most quoted declarations of fidelity in Scripture.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Joshua appears in Book III, Chapter 38 ("Of a Good Government in External Things"), where Thomas recounts how "Joshua and the children of Israel were deceived by the Gibeonites, that they asked not counsel at the mouth of the Lord" (075-024–025). The Gibeonites, fearing Israel's advance, disguised themselves as travelers from a far country and secured a treaty of peace by deception (Joshua 9:3–15). The biblical text records the fatal omission: "the men took of their victuals, and asked not counsel at the mouth of the Lord" (Joshua 9:14).

> "For this cause we read that Joshua and the children of Israel were deceived by the Gibeonites, that they asked not counsel at the mouth of the Lord, but being too ready to listen to fair speeches, were deceived by pretended piety." (075-024–027)

Thomas places this example immediately after his exhortation to "enter with Moses into the tabernacle to ask counsel of God" (075-017). Joshua is the negative counterpart to Moses: where Moses always "had recourse to the tabernacle for the solving of all doubts" (075-020), Joshua relied on his own judgment — and was deceived. The juxtaposition is deliberate: one asked counsel, the other did not; one was delivered, the other was ensnared.

## In the Christian Tradition

Joshua's significance in Christian tradition extends well beyond his single appearance in The Imitation. His very name — *Yeshua* in Hebrew, *Iesous* in Greek, "Jesus" in English — made him the preeminent Old Testament type of Christ. The Church Fathers, from Justin Martyr onward, read the succession from Moses (the Law) to Joshua (the one who leads into the Promised Land) as the essential pattern of salvation history: the Law cannot bring God's people into their inheritance; only Jesus-Joshua can accomplish the entry. St. Augustine, in his *Questions on the Heptateuch*, drew extensive parallels between Joshua's crossing of the Jordan and Christian baptism, and between the conquest of Canaan and the soul's warfare against sin.

The Gibeonite episode — the very failure that The Imitation highlights — received particularly rich treatment in medieval spiritual commentary. St. Gregory the Great, in his *Pastoral Rule*, used it to warn spiritual leaders against the danger of trusting appearances: "Fair speeches" and "pretended piety" (075-027) are the devil's favoured instruments against those who govern souls. The Carthusian writer Ludolph of Saxony applied the episode to the discernment of spirits, noting that even the most divinely commissioned leader can be deceived when he ceases to consult God in prayer. Thomas à Kempis inherits this tradition and makes Joshua's failure the cautionary centrepiece of a chapter on external governance — a reminder that no amount of experience or authority substitutes for the "secret chamber of the heart" (075-023) where the soul consults its Lord.

In monastic and devotional tradition, Joshua's farewell — "as for me and my house, we will serve the Lord" (Joshua 24:15) — became a formula of personal dedication, recited at professions of religious vows and at the blessing of Christian households. For the reader of The Imitation, Joshua is both warning and pattern: his single failure teaches the disciple never to act on appearances without prayer, while his lifelong fidelity declares that the soul's ultimate choice — "choose you this day whom ye will serve" — admits no compromise.

## Spiritual Significance

Joshua's role in The Imitation is as a cautionary tale. He demonstrates that even the divinely appointed leader, the conqueror of Jericho, can fall into deception through the seemingly innocent omission of prayer. The Gibeonites came "with pretended piety" (075-027) — a danger the disciple faces daily from "fair speeches" that appeal to the natural judgment. Joshua thus stands as a perpetual warning: never act on appearances without first seeking counsel from God in the "secret chamber of thy heart" (075-023).
