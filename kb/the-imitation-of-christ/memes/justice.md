# Justice

**Justice** (*iustitia*) in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* is understood primarily as the righteous ordering of all things by God. Thomas à Kempis treats divine justice not as a threatening tribunal but as the hidden wisdom that apportions [[temptation|trial]]s and [[comfort|consolation]]s to each soul according to its true condition and need. The justice of God is inseparable from His [[forgiveness|mercy]], His knowledge, and His [[providence|providential care]] for the elect.

## The Hidden Wisdom of Divine Ordering

The most significant treatment of justice in the text occurs in the context of temptation and spiritual [[temptation|trial]]. Kempis observes that souls experience very different degrees of [[suffering|affliction]], and this diversity is not arbitrary:

> "Some are sorely tried their whole life long. Some there are who are tempted but lightly, according to the wisdom and justice of the ordering of God, who knoweth the character and circumstances of men, and ordereth all things for the welfare of His elect" [013-051]

This passage reveals the character of divine justice as it is understood in the text. Justice is not a mechanical distribution of equal portions — it is the perfectly calibrated apportionment of what each soul needs. The God who "knoweth the character and circumstances of men" dispenses [[temptation|trial]]s not as punishments but as medicine, measured to the precise condition of the patient.

The conjunction "wisdom and justice" is significant. Justice without wisdom would be mere accounting; wisdom without justice would lack moral seriousness. Together they describe a [[providence|Providence]] that is both intelligent and righteous, never arbitrary, never cruel, always purposeful.

## Justice and the Hiddenness of God

Kempis acknowledges that divine justice is often opaque to human perception. What appears unjust from the limited vantage point of the sufferer is, seen from eternity, the work of perfect righteousness. The text's treatment of unjust accusations and undeserved suffering consistently returns to the theme of God's hidden but certain justice:

> "The testimony of men often deceiveth. My judgment is true; it will stand, and it shall not be overturned. It commonly lieth hid, and only to few in certain cases is it made known; yet it never erreth, nor can err, although it seem not right to the eyes of foolish men" [087-042]

This is the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]'s own speech in the text, reassuring the soul that divine justice operates beneath the surface of events. The hiddenness of God's justice is not a defect but a feature — it calls forth faith, patience, and humility. The soul that must wait for vindication learns to [[trust-in-god|trust]] not in human approval but in the Judge who "trieth the hearts and reins."

## The Practical Response to Divine Justice

The proper human response to divine justice is humility and patience, not complaint or despair. Kempis counsels the soul to recognize its own ignorance before the divine ordering:

> "Thou knowest what I know not; and therefore ought I under all rebuke to humble myself, and to bear it meekly" [087-042]

This humility before God's hidden justice is the foundation of spiritual peace. The soul that insists on understanding why it suffers as it does will find no rest, for God's reasons lie beyond mortal comprehension. The soul that [[trust-in-god|trust]]s the justice of God without demanding to see it demonstrated finds stability even in the storm.

The justice of God also grounds the exhortation against [[judgment|judging others]]. [[sin|Sin]]ce only God knows the "character and circumstances" of each person, human judgment is necessarily partial and often wrong. The acknowledgment of divine justice leads not to passivity but to a suspension of premature verdicts, leaving room for God's own disclosure in His time.

## Biblical Foundation

The concept of divine justice in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* draws from the Psalms, where the righteousness of God is celebrated as the foundation of His throne (Psalm 97:2) and the ground of the believer's confidence (Psalm 37:6). The Pauline conviction that "all things work together for good to them that love God" (Romans 8:28) underlies the teaching that divine justice orders all circumstances for the welfare of the elect. The Song of [[moses|Moses]] — "He is the Rock, His work is perfect: for all His ways are judgment" (Deuteronomy 32:4) — supplies the fundamental conviction that God's justice is inseparable from His perfection.
