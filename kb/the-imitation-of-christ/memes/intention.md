# Intention

The inward purpose and orientation of the will that gives moral and spiritual quality to outward acts — in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], intention is the "eye" of the soul: if it be single and directed toward God, the whole body of one's works is full of light; if it be divided or bent upon self, even spiritual exercises become occasions of delusion.

## Definition

In the moral psychology of The Imitation of Christ, intention is the interior act of the will by which a person directs their actions toward an end. It is distinguished from the outward deed itself: two persons may perform the same external act, yet the act's spiritual value depends entirely on the intention with which it is done. "According as the eye of intention be the more pure, even so doth a man make steadfast progress" — the [[intention|purity of intention]] is the measure of authentic spiritual growth.

Thomas à Kempis returns repeatedly to the theme of intention because he recognizes that religious observance itself can become a theatre of self-deception. Outward piety animated by impure intention — the desire for human praise, the pursuit of spiritual [[consolation|consolation]], the ambition to appear holy — is not merely worthless but actively harmful.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats intention as the hidden dimension of every spiritual act. It is especially prominent in the context of sacramental reception and the duty of perseverance through spiritual trials.

### The Pious Intention in Sacramental Communion

Book IV, Chapter X addresses the situation of the devout soul who is "hindered by sufficient cause" from receiving sacramental Communion. The response is striking: if the soul maintains "a good will and pious intention to communicate," it "shall not be lacking in the fruit of the Sacrament" (106-056–057). The intention to receive, when circumstances prevent actual reception, participates in the sacramental fruit — not by a mechanical substitution but because the will's orientation toward God is itself a real communion.

This teaching extends to the whole of the spiritual life: "Any devout man is able every day and every hour to draw near to spiritual communion with Christ to his soul's health and without hindrance" (106-058–059). The key is not to "seek after the praise and honour of God, than his own comfort" — intention oriented toward God's glory rather than one's own [[consolation|consolation]] is the mark of authentic devotion (106-061–062).

### Upright Intention in the Midst of Trial

Book III, Chapter VI addresses the soul troubled by "strange fancies" and unwanted thoughts. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s counsel is to return to the stability of intention: "Bravely observe thy purpose and thy upright intentions towards God" (043-021). The presence of disturbing thoughts is not a sign that one's intention has failed; it is precisely when such thoughts "displease thee and thou strivest against them" that they become "a merit and no loss" (043-025–026).

The enemy of the soul "altogether striveth to hinder thy pursuit after good, and to deter thee from every godly exercise" (043-027–028), including "the steadfast purpose to grow in virtue" (043-031). The defense is not the absence of temptation but the firmness of intention — the resolute orientation of the will toward God that persists despite interior turbulence.

### Will and Intention Accepted by God

Book IV, Chapter X reassures the soul that "the Lord will help his desire for the good will which he hath, which God specially approveth" (106-053–054). The good will — the upright intention — is not merely a human disposition but something that God "specially approveth" and responds to with help. Intention is thus both the ground of human action and the site of divine assistance.

This aligns with the broader teaching of The Imitation of Christ that God judges not by outward appearance but by the heart: "God is a discerner of our heart, Whom we must reverence with all our hearts" (019-004–005). The [[intention|purity of intention]] is therefore a matter of the first importance — not as a form of scrupulosity but as the continual orientation of the will toward God alone.

### Key Passages

> "Bravely observe thy purpose and thy upright intentions towards God. It is not an illusion when thou art sometimes suddenly carried away into rapture, and then suddenly art brought back to the wonted vanities of thy heart." (043-021–023)

> "When he is hindered by sufficient cause, yet will he ever have a good will and pious intention to communicate; and so he shall not be lacking in the fruit of the Sacrament." (106-056–057)

> "The Lord will help his desire for the good will which he hath, which God specially approveth." (106-053–054)

> "And rather to seek after the praise and honour of God, than his own comfort." (106-061–062)

> "God is a discerner of our heart, Whom we must reverence with all our hearts wheresoever we are, and walk pure in His presence as do the angels." (019-004–006)

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] grounds its teaching on intention in Christ's discourse on the "single eye": "The lamp of the body is the eye. If therefore your eye is good, your whole body will be full of light" (Matthew 6:22). The "eye" in this passage was traditionally interpreted as the intention — when it is single and directed toward God, all one's works are illuminated; when it is double and divided, the works are darkness.

Paul's exhortation — "whatever you do, do it heartily, as to the Lord and not to men" (Colossians 3:23) — and "whether you eat or drink, or whatever you do, do all to the glory of God" (1 Corinthians 10:31) — extends the domain of intention to every human act. Proverbs teaches that "all the ways of a man are pure in his own eyes, but the Lord weighs the spirits" (Proverbs 16:2), while Hebrews declares that "the word of God is living and powerful … a discerner of the thoughts and intents of the heart" (Hebrews 4:12) — confirming Thomas's conviction that intention is the ultimate object of divine scrutiny.
