# Praise

**Praise** (*laus*) in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* is understood primarily as an act directed toward God — the offering of honor, glory, and [[gratitude|thanksgiving]] to the Creator. Thomas à Kempis treats human praise, by contrast, as a snare of vanity from which the devout soul must detach itself. The entire spiritual life can be conceived as a movement from seeking the praise of men to offering praise to God alone.

## Divine Praise as the Soul's Highest Work

Kempis presents the praise of God as the occupation that most befits the human soul. In a striking passage, he imagines a life entirely devoted to this single act:

> Oh, if no other duty lay upon us but to praise the Lord our God with our whole heart and voice! Oh, if thou never needest to eat, or drink, or sleep, but wert always able to praise God, and to give thyself to spiritual exercises alone! [025-071-073]

This longing for uninterrupted praise reflects the monastic ideal of *laus perennis* — perpetual praise — and anticipates the life of heaven, where the blessed "praise Him and always give Him thanks" (036-016).

The note of desire is crucial: those who truly love God "desire that God shall be praised in Himself and in all His saints" (035-040). Praise is not a grudging duty but the spontaneous overflow of a heart that recognizes God's goodness.

## The Emptiness of Human Praise

In sharp contrast to divine praise, the praise of men is treated as a spiritual danger. The truly wise man "neither regardeth he the praises of men" (002-006). External approval is irrelevant to one's standing before God:

> Thou art none the holier if thou art praised, nor the viler if thou art reproached. What thou art, that thou art; and thou canst not be greater in the sight of God than what thou art declared to be by thy words. [031-024]

This radical indifference to reputation — "he who careth not for praises nor reproaches hath great [[peace|tranquillity]] of heart" (031-022) — is the fruit of genuine humility. The soul fixed on God no longer needs the mirror of human approval.

## The Danger of Desiring Praise

Book I, Chapter 33 counsels directly: "Never desire to be specially praised or loved, for this belongeth to God alone" (033-034). The desire for singular recognition is a form of theft from God, who alone deserves all honor.

This warning is connected to the broader theme of vainglory. In Book III, the text warns of those "who once were lifted to the clouds with the praises of men" but who have since fallen (051-009). Human praise is not only empty — it is dangerous. It creates an illusory sense of worth that can collapse catastrophically.

## Praise in Suffering

Kempis integrates praise into the theology of suffering. The devout soul is called to "bear all things which come upon thee unto the praise of God" (033-048). Even adversities become occasions for praising [[wisdom|divine wisdom]] and providence:

> Many praise Him and bless Him, so long as comforts be given unto them... nevertheless they would always praise Him and always give Him thanks. [036-009-016]

This unshakable praise — in prosperity and [[suffering|adversity]] alike — marks the mature spiritual life. The saints who praise God in suffering "counted as nothing the [[comfort|consolation]]s of this world, but desired rather to serve God with all their strength" [036-009-016].

## Liturgical and Interior Praise

The Imitation's treatment of praise spans both liturgical worship and interior devotion. In Book IV, Kempis declares [[jesus-christ|Christ]] "worthy of all ser[[sin|vice]], all honour, and praise without end" (047-034). This liturgical language anchors praise in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], the Church's supreme act of [[gratitude|thanksgiving]] (*eucharistia* meaning "[[gratitude|thanksgiving]]").

Yet praise is equally an interior act. The prayer of Book III expresses a personal longing: "Let me praise Thee, my God, exulting with love" (042-053). Praise unites the outer word of liturgy with the inner fire of devotion.

## Praise and the Communion of Saints

The praise of God is not a solitary act but one that unites the soul with the whole company of heaven. Kempis describes the devotion surrounding the Ark of the Covenant as a type of this heavenly worship, where the faithful were called "with unity of voice to bless and praise Him every day" (097-070). The earthly [[worship|liturgy]] participates in the eternal praise offered by angels and saints before the throne of God.

This communion in praise has implications for how the saints themselves are honored. The text cautions against contentious speculation about the relative merits of individual saints — "which is holier than another, or which is the greater in the Kingdom of Heaven" (095-015) — for such questions "beget useless strifes and contentions" and "nourish pride and vain glory" (095-017). The proper disposition is not to rank the saints but to praise God in them: "I am to be praised in all My Saints; I am to be blessed above all things, and to be honoured in every one whom I have so gloriously exalted" (095-033).

The mature spiritual life thus integrates all goods into praise. Those who follow Christ "through contempt of worldly things, and mortification of the flesh" find that "whatsoever good they find in creatures, they count it all unto the praise of the Creator" (071-022). The distinction between Creator and creature is absolute — "Unlike, yea, very unlike is the enjoyment of the Creator to enjoyment of the Creature" (071-023) — and it is precisely this recognition that turns the entire created order into a choir of praise. Every good thing, rightly received, becomes material for glorifying the One who made it.

The highest expression of this desire transcends even the consolations of heaven: "above all joy and exultation, above all fame and praise, above all sweetness and consolation... above Angels and Archangels and all the army of heaven, above all things visible and invisible, and above everything which Thou, O my God, art not" (058-007). The soul that has learned to praise God in His saints, through His creatures, and above all consolations, has begun to taste the eternal worship of the blessed.
