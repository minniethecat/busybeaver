[002-001] Of thinking humbly of oneself

[002-002] There is naturally in every man a desire to know, but what profiteth

[002-003] knowledge without the [[fear-of-the-lord|fear of God]]? Better of a surety is a lowly

[002-004] peasant who serveth God, than a proud philosopher who watcheth the

[002-005] stars and neglecteth the knowledge of himself. He who knoweth himself

[002-006] well is vile in his own sight; neither regardeth he the praises of men.

[002-007] If I knew all the things that are in [[world|the world]], and were not in

[002-008] [[love|charity]], what should it help me before God, who is to judge me

[002-009] according to my deeds?

[002-010] 2. Rest from inordinate desire of knowledge, for therein is found much

[002-011] distraction and deceit. Those who have knowledge desire to appear

[002-012] learned, and to be called wise. Many things there are to know which

[002-013] profiteth little or nothing to the soul. And foolish out of measure is

[002-014] he who attendeth upon other things rather than those which serve to his

[002-015] soul’s health. Many words satisfy not the soul, but a good life

[002-016] refresheth the mind, and a [[conscience|pure conscience]] giveth great confidence

[002-017] towards God.

[002-018] 3. The greater and more complete thy knowledge, the more severely shalt

[002-019] thou be judged, unless thou hast lived holily. Therefore be not lifted

[002-020] up by any skill or knowledge that thou hast; but rather fear concerning

[002-021] the knowledge which is given to thee. If it seemeth to thee that thou

[002-022] knowest many things, and understandest them well, know also that there

[002-023] are many more things which thou knowest not. Be not high-minded, but

[002-024] rather confess thine ignorance. Why desirest thou to lift thyself above

[002-025] another, when there are found many more learned and more skilled in the

[002-026] Scripture than thou? If thou wilt know and learn anything with profit,

[002-027] love to be thyself unknown and to be counted for nothing.

[002-028] 4. That is the highest and most profitable lesson, when a man truly

[002-029] knoweth and judgeth lowly of himself. To account nothing of one’s self,

[002-030] and to think always kindly and highly of others, this is great and

[002-031] perfect wisdom. Even shouldest thou see thy neighbor sin openly or

[002-032] grievously, yet thou oughtest not to reckon thyself better than he, for

[002-033] thou knowest not how long thou shalt keep thine integrity. All of us

[002-034] are weak and frail; hold thou no man more frail than thyself.
