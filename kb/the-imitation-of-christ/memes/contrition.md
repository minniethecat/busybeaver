# Contrition

The piercing of the heart by sorrow for sin — not the superficial regret that fears punishment, nor [[world|the world]]ly remorse that is merely wounded pride, but the deep, grace-given grief of a soul that loves God and grieves to have offended Him. In The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], contrition is one of the most frequently invoked spiritual dispositions, for it is the gateway to every grace. "I had rather feel contrition than be skilful in the definition thereof" (001-018): Thomas places this declaration near the very beginning of the work, signalling that contrition belongs to the heart, not the head.

## Definition

Contrition, from the Latin *contritio* (a grinding, a crushing), is the sorrow of the soul for sin, conceived not as a merely human emotion but as a movement of grace. The word "grinding" is theologically precise: contrition grinds the hard heart to powder, breaking up the clods of pride and self-justification so that the seed of grace may take root. In the vocabulary of The Imitation, contrition is often paired with compunction (*compunctio*, a pricking or stinging), and the two terms overlap considerably. Both refer to the interior pain of a soul that recognises its sinfulness and turns to God in sorrow and hope. "In true contrition and humiliation of heart" (089-038), the sinner finds the path back to God.

## In The Imitation of Christ

### The Primacy of Heart over Intellect

Thomas's most famous statement on contrition is also his most programmatic: "I had rather feel contrition than be skilful in the definition thereof" (001-018). The entire spiritual method of The Imitation is contained in these words. Contrition is not a concept to be analysed but a disposition to be experienced. The theologian who can parse the [[discernment|distinction]] between attrition and contrition with precision but has never wept for his [[sin|sins]] is further from God than the simple layman who knows nothing of definitions but whose heart is broken with sorrow.

This anti-intellectualism is not anti-theological; it is a protest against a theology that has become detached from its existential ground. Thomas does not deny that contrition can be defined; he denies that the definition matters more than the reality. The knowledge that puffs up (1 Corinthians 8:1) is the knowledge that rests content with accurate formulations while the heart remains untouched. The knowledge that saves is the knowledge that leads to the foot of the Cross in tears.

### Contrition and the Eucharist

Contrition is the essential preparation for worthy reception of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]]. In Book IV, Thomas insists that the communicant must approach the Sacrament "in true contrition and humiliation of heart" (089-038), for "the humble contrition of sinners is an acceptable sacrifice unto Thee, O [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (089-038). The [[eucharist|Eucharist]] is the food of the humble; the proud, the self-satisfied, the impenitent cannot digest it. Contrition makes the soul small enough to receive the immensity of [[love|divine love]].

The connection between contrition and the [[eucharist|Eucharist]] is deepened by the fact that it is precisely in the Sacrament that "many devout persons find great benefit of compunction and devotion" (097-083). The [[eucharist|Eucharist]] both requires contrition and produces it; the soul that approaches with sorrow for sin departs with a deeper sorrow, now mingled with joy because the [[jesus-christ|Saviour]] has been received. "Contrition and [[confession|humble confession]] cleanse the soul" (103-007) — contrition is the interior purification that the sacrament both presupposes and perfects.

### Sorrow That Heals

Thomas draws a careful [[discernment|distinction]] between the sorrow of contrition and the sorrow of [[world|the world]]. The latter, as Paul teaches, "worketh death" (2 Corinthians 7:10); it is the sorrow of wounded pride, of disappointed self-love, of resentment at being found out. The sorrow of contrition "worketh repentance to salvation not to be repented of" (2 Corinthians 7:10); it is the sorrow of love, of a soul that grieves because it has wounded the One it loves.

This healing sorrow has a characteristic movement: it turns the soul away from sin and toward God. The contrite soul does not wallow in guilt; it flees to [[forgiveness|mercy]]. "Contrition and [[confession|humble confession]] cleanse the soul" (103-007) — contrition is dynamic, not static; it propels the soul toward the confessional, toward amendment, toward the new life that grace makes possible. The weeping of contrition is the rain that softens the ground for the seed of virtue.

### The Spirit of Penitence

Thomas understands contrition not as an isolated act but as a habitual disposition — the "spirit of [[repentance|penitence]]" that pervades the entire [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life. "We ought continually to accuse ourselves" (089-033): the contrite soul lives in a state of ongoing self-examination and sorrow, not because it is scrupulous or morose, but because it knows that sin is not a past event but a present tendency. The contrite heart is the heart that has learned to "weep and be sorrowful" (103-018) for its [[sin|sins]], not once but continually, as part of its daily conversation with God.

Yet Thomas is not a rigorist. He knows that "we are so prone to ease and the indulgence of the flesh" (103-018) that the spirit of [[repentance|penitence]] is easily lost. The [[discipleship|disciple]] must deliberately cultivate contrition — by examining the conscience, by meditating on the Passion, by recalling the last things — lest the heart grow hard and the tears dry up. "They supply manifold [[comfort|consolation]]" (080-005), Thomas says of contrition: the tears of [[repentance|penitence]] are themselves a gift, a foretaste of the joy that awaits the forgiven sinner.

## Key Passages

> "I had rather feel contrition than be skilful in the definition thereof." (001-018)

> "In true contrition and humiliation of heart let us pray unto the Lord, for the humble contrition of sinners is an acceptable sacrifice unto Thee, O Lord." (089-038 to 089-040, paraphrase)

> "Contrition and humble confession cleanse the soul, and make it meet to receive so great a Mystery." (103-007)

> "We ought continually to examine ourselves, to accuse ourselves, and to humble ourselves for our sins." (089-033, paraphrase)

> "Oh, how great a benefit is contrition of heart! They who truly repent supply manifold consolation, and they who grieve for their sins shall be filled with joy." (080-005 to 080-006, paraphrase)

## Biblical Foundation

The theology of contrition in The Imitation rests on the penitential tradition of the Old Testament and its fulfilment in the New. The paradigmatic text is David's confession: "The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit: a broken and a contrite heart, O God, Thou wilt not despise" (Psalm 51:17). This verse establishes the principle that contrition is itself a sacrifice — the interior sacrifice that external rites express but cannot replace. The prophetic call — "Turn ye even to Me with all your heart, and with fasting, and with weeping, and with mourning: and rend your heart, and not your garments" (Joel 2:12-13) — insists on interior conversion over external display. The Isaianic promise that God dwells "with him also that is of a contrite and humble spirit" ([[isaiah|Isaiah]] 57:15) assures the penitent that contrition is not a descent into despair but an ascent toward divine intimacy. The dominical parable of the Publican, who "would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven, but smote upon his breast, saying, God be merciful to me a sinner" (Luke 18:13), provides the evangelical model of contrite prayer.

## Related Concepts

- [[compunction]] — The near-synonym of contrition; compunction is the "pricking" of the heart that leads to contrition's "crushing"
- [[confession]] — The sacramental expression of contrition; contrition is the interior sorrow, confession its outward voice
- [[sin]] — The object of contrition; contrition is sorrow for sin precisely as an offence against God
- [[humility]] — The disposition that contrition both presupposes and deepens; only the humble heart can be truly contrite
- [[penance]] — The broader practice within which contrition is situated; penance includes contrition, confession, and satisfaction
- [[mortification]] — The practical consequence of contrition; contrition mortifies sin at its root by grieving over it
- [[mercy]] — The divine attribute to which contrition appeals; the contrite heart throws itself on [[mercy|God's mercy]]
- [[forgiveness]] — The gift that contrition receives; God does not despise a contrite heart but forgives it
- [[amendment]] — The purpose of contrition; the contrite soul resolves to amend its life and sin no more
- [[grace]] — The source of contrition; true contrition is not self-generated but is a movement of [[grace|divine grace]] in the soul
