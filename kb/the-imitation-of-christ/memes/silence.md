# Silence

Silence is a foundational ascetical practice in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], commended not merely as the absence of speech but as a positive spiritual discipline that protects the soul, deepens humility, and prepares the heart for communion with God.

## Definition

Silence is the intentional restraint of speech——refraining both from unnecessary talking and from the inward compulsion to speak——undertaken for the sake of spiritual growth. It is closely allied with solitude and humility, forming a triad of practices that together create the conditions for contemplation. The Imitation presents silence as both a protective discipline (shielding the soul from the many [[sin|sins]] that begin with the tongue) and a receptive posture (opening the ear of the heart to divine instruction).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis devotes an entire chapter to the love of solitude and silence (020-001), treating silence not as mere muteness but as an active spiritual good. His treatment weaves together several distinct arguments for the practice.

### Silence as Receptivity to God

The primary motive for silence in The Imitation is theological: the soul must be quiet to hear God. The opening invocation of Book I sets this tone: "let [[creation|all creation]] keep silence before Thee: speak Thou alone to my soul" (003-020). This counsel reappears in a eucharistic context where the devout soul is taught that prayer "ought to be heard in silence, and to be received with all humility and devout affection" (040-007). Silence is not [[vanity|emptiness]] but receptivity——the quieting of human speech so that divine speech may be heard.

### Silence as Protection from Sin

The Imitation repeatedly warns that much speech is dangerous to the soul. "It is easier to keep silence altogether than not to exceed in words" (010-007). The author observes a universal human frailty: "seldom do we return to silence without some hurt done to our conscience" (010-007). The plain implication is that speech——even when well-intentioned——often leads to gossip, boasting, detraction, or idle words. Silence is the safer path. Chapter 20 characterizes the practice in terms of sorrow and spiritual benefit: "thou oughtest rather to rejoice in having kept silence than in having talked much" (024-060).

### Silence and the Devout Soul

Silence is presented as the natural climate of the devout life. "In silence and quiet the devout soul goeth forward, and learneth the mysteries of the Scriptures" (020-045). The one who speaks little is better positioned to speak truth when speech is necessary: "he that seeketh to please God and to be kept from [[world|the world]], to him easily keepeth silence concerning other men's matters" (030-013). This is not antisocial withdrawal but spiritual prioritization——the tongue is kept for what serves God rather than for what serves curiosity or vanity.

### Divine Silence as Model

The Imitation makes the striking observation that even God's truth operates through silence: "[[truth|Truth]] speaketh within me without sound of words. My truth is without speech, and my silence speaketh to Thee" (058-041). This interior speech——wordless yet authoritative——is the pattern for the soul's own silence. What is heard inwardly needs no noisy outward expression; grace itself is "preserved in silence in this frail life" (082-057).

### Key Passages

> "In silence and quiet the devout soul goeth forward, and learneth the mysteries of the Scriptures" (020-045)

> "Truth speaketh within me without sound of words. My truth is without speech, and my silence speaketh to Thee" (058-041)

> "Let all creation keep silence before Thee: speak Thou alone to my soul" (003-020)

## Biblical Foundation

The Kempian doctrine of silence draws from multiple scriptural sources. The counsel that "[[creation|all creation]] keep silence before Thee" echoes Habakkuk 2:20 ("let all the earth keep silence before Him"). The wisdom tradition provides the core practical teaching——Proverbs 10:19 ("in the multitude of words there wanteth not sin") and Proverbs 17:28 ("even a fool, when he holdeth his peace, is counted wise") are the implied warrants for the argument that speech inevitably leads to spiritual harm. The contemplative dimension draws from Psalm 46:10 ("be still, and know that I am God") and 1 Kings 19:12 (the "still small voice" heard by Elijah). The New Testament reinforces the theme in James 3:2–10 (the tongue as "a fire, a world of [[sin|iniquity]]") and James 1:19 ("let every man be swift to hear, slow to speak").

## Practical Application

The Imitation recommends silence as a daily ascetical practice, not a monastic ideal reserved for cloisters. The counsel is remarkably practical: avoid unnecessary conversation, resist the impulse to be heard, and when you must speak, do so briefly and to the point. Chapter 20 specifically counsels the reader to prefer private prayer to public conversation and to guard against the "disorderly" desire to talk (103-022). Silence is presented not as an end in itself but as a means: the less the soul is dissipated in speech, the more it is gathered in God.
