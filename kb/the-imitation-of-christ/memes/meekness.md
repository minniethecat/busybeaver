# Meekness

The virtue of gentle endurance under provocation — in The Imitation of Christ, meekness is not weakness but strength under divine control, the disposition of a soul that has ceased to defend itself and entrusted all to God.

## Definition

Meekness in the Christian tradition is the virtue by which a person bears injury, insult, and provocation without resentment, anger, or retaliation. It is distinguished from mere passivity or timidity: the meek person has the power to retaliate but chooses to entrust judgment to God. In The Imitation of Christ, meekness is inseparable from [[humility]] — the humble soul, having seen its own nothingness before God, no longer feels entitled to resent the wrongs it receives from others.

> "Learn of Me, for I am meek and lowly in heart" (Matthew 11:29) — Christ's own self-description — provides the paradigm. Meekness is Christ-likeness.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis presents meekness as an essential disposition of the disciple, woven through the text's teaching on bearing wrongs, enduring correction, and living peaceably with others.

### Bearing with the Meek

The Imitation commends association with the meek as naturally pleasing to human nature: "with the good and the meek, for this is naturally pleasing to all" (028-020). Meekness creates the conditions for peaceful community — where pride divides, meekness unites.

### Meekly Bearing Reproaches

Book III teaches that the disciple must "bear reproaches meekly" (037-082), linking meekness to the broader ascetical program of [[self-denial]]. To bear a reproach meekly is to receive it as from the hand of God rather than from the malice of the accuser. This is a concrete application of the principle that "every event, great or small, comes from God" (012-010).

### Without Malice or Deceit

The Christ-like soul is described as "meek, without any malice or deceit" (059-039). Meekness purifies the interior life: where malice nurses grievances and deceit manipulates others, meekness stands in simplicity before God and man. There is no room for hidden resentment in a soul that has truly surrendered its rights.

### Meekly Bearing What God Permits

The disciple "must bear meekly for God whatsoever ills are" permitted (101-032). This is the heart of the Kempian understanding: meekness is not merely interpersonal gentleness but the fundamental posture of the soul toward divine providence. The meek person receives suffering, injustice, and hardship as permitted by God for the soul's purification.

### Key Passages

> "with the good and the meek, for this is naturally pleasing to all" (028-020)

> "to bear reproaches meekly, to despise self and desire nothing" (037-082)

> "meek, without any malice or deceit" (059-039)

> "He must bear meekly for God whatsoever ills are" (101-032)

## Biblical Foundation

Meekness is one of the few virtues Christ explicitly claims for Himself: "Learn of Me, for I am meek and lowly in heart" (Matthew 11:29). The Beatitudes promise that "the meek shall inherit the earth" (Matthew 5:5), overturning the world's expectation that the aggressive and self-assertive prevail. Paul lists meekness among the fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22–23), and Peter commends "the incorruptible beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit" (1 Peter 3:4). The Imitation of Christ takes these scriptural foundations and translates them into concrete ascetical practice: bear reproaches, endure ills, renounce malice — and in so doing, become like Christ.
