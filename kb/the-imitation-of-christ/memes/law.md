# Law

**Law** (*lex*) in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* encompasses both the external precepts of Scripture and the interior law written by God upon the soul. Thomas à Kempis treats the divine law not as a burdensome code but as a path of rest, a school of love, and the remedy for the "law of sin" that wars within fallen nature. The soul instructed in God's law finds stability, direction, and peace.

## The Law as Divine Instruction

Kempis presents the divine law primarily as instruction (*eruditio*) rather than mere regulation. The soul that receives God's teaching through the law finds rest amid [[suffering|adversity]]:

> "Blessed is the man whom Thou teachest, O Lord, and instructest him in Thy law, that Thou mayest give him rest in time of adversity, and that he be not desolate in the earth" [040-010]

The law is not distant or abstract — it is communicated personally by God to the soul. The same [[jesus-christ|Lord]] who taught the prophets from the beginning continues to speak, though many are "deaf and hardened" against His voice. The tragedy is not that the law is obscure, but that the heart is slow to attend.

The believer's posture toward the law is one of receptive openness. Kempis prays:

> "Open, O Lord, my heart in Thy law, and teach me to walk in the way of Thy commandments. Grant me to understand Thy will and to be mindful of Thy benefits" [059-002]

This prayer reveals the interior character of law in Kempis's understanding — it is not merely external compliance but the illumination of the heart, intimately connected to gratitude for divine benefits.

## The Interior Conflict: Law of the Mind Versus Law of Sin

The most developed treatment of law in the text appears in the context of the soul's interior warfare. Following [[apostle-paul|Saint Paul]], Kempis describes a divided self:

> "I feel in my flesh the law of sin, contradicting the law of my mind, and bringing me into captivity to the obedience of sensuality in many things; nor can I resist its passions, unless Thy most holy grace assist me" [092-006]

The "law of the mind" (*lex mentis*) delights in God's commandments and recognizes their goodness, while the "law of sin" (*lex peccati*) in the flesh wars against it. This Pauline tension (Romans 7:22-25) is not resolved by human effort but by [[grace|divine grace]]:

> "Hence it is, O my God, that I delight in Thy law after the inward man, knowing that Thy commandment is holy and just and good; reproving also all evil, and the sin that is to be avoided: yet with the flesh I serve the law of sin, whilst I obey sensuality rather than reason" [092-026]

The presence of this conflict is not evidence of the law's failure but of nature's corruption. The law itself remains "holy and just and good" — the problem lies in the flesh that resists it. [[grace|Grace]] alone bridges the gap between delighting in the law inwardly and fulfilling it outwardly.

## The Law of Love and the Fulfillment of Types

Kempis situates the Mosaic law within the broader sweep of salvation history, recognizing it as preparatory and figurative. The Ark of the Covenant, the tables of the law, and the ancient sacrifices all pointed toward [[jesus-christ|Christ]]:

> "There is a vast difference between the Ark of the Covenant with its relics, and Thy most pure Body with its ineffable virtues, between those sacrifices of the law, which were figures of things to come, and the true sacrifice of Thy Body, the completion of all the ancient sacrifices" [097-056]

The old law, embodied in the Ark and the sacrifices, was real but incomplete — "figures of things to come." The new law is [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself, the "Maker of the Law and the Giver of life," whose Body completes what the ancient types prefigured. This understanding prevents the law from becoming an end in itself; it is ordered toward its fulfillment in the [[eucharist|Eucharist]].

The highest form of law in the text is the "law of love" (*lex amoris*), which surpasses mere precept:

> "The law of love commandeth which shineth forth from Thee" [042-055]

This law of love is not a different content but a different principle — it is the law internalized and animated by [[love|charity]], shining from God rather than imposed from without.

## Biblical Foundation

The teaching on law in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]* draws heavily from the Pauline epistles, particularly Romans 7-8, where the apostle describes the conflict between the law of the mind and the law of sin. The Psalms provide the devotional language of delight in God's law (Psalm 119), while Jeremiah's promise of the law written on the heart (Jeremiah 31:33) underlies the theme of interiorization. The treatment of the Mosaic law as shadow and type reflects the argument of the Epistle to the Hebrews.

## The Sweetness of the Law and Interior Illumination

For Kempis, the divine law is not only a rule to be obeyed but a sweetness to be tasted. The [[grace|grace]] of devotion transforms the law from external demand into interior delight. The disciple prays for this illumination: "Open, O Lord, my heart in Thy law, and teach me to walk in the way of Thy commandments. Grant me to understand Thy will and to be mindful of Thy benefits" (059-002). The law's commands are enfolded within the memory of divine generosity — obedience is the grateful response to remembered mercies.

The sweetness of the law is rooted in its origin. It is not an arbitrary imposition but the expression of the divine nature itself. When Kempis prays to be ruled by "the law of love commandeth which shineth forth from Thee" (042-055), he acknowledges that the law is not an external code but the radiance of God's own character. The law of love is the law of God's own being, communicated to the creature as both command and gift.

Yet the law retains its disciplinary function. Because the flesh "would wax insolent against the spirit" (063-032) without restraint, the prohibitions of the law are a mercy — a fence against self-destruction. The law not only forbids what destroys but commands what restores. Its prohibitions are the shadow cast by its affirmations: the "thou shalt not" serves the "thou shalt," protecting the [[love|love]] and [[peace|peace]] that the law was given to establish. The mature disciple does not chafe against the law's boundaries but recognizes in them the wisdom of a Father who knows the full measure of human weakness.
