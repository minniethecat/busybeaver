# Devotion

The heartfelt orientation of the soul toward God expressed through prayer, reading, meditation, and the worthy reception of the sacraments — in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], devotion is both a sovereign gift of [[grace|divine grace]] and a discipline the faithful must diligently cultivate, the fervour that animates the entire spiritual life.

## Definition

In the vocabulary of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], devotion is the interior fervour and affectionate inclination of the soul toward God. It is not merely a subjective feeling of warmth but the concrete orientation of the heart that finds expression in prayer, spiritual reading, the worthy reception of [[communion|Communion]], and the faithful discharge of religious duties.

Thomas à Kempis presents devotion under a twofold aspect: it is first a gift of grace — "the nurse of devotion" is grace itself (092-059) — and second a discipline to be cultivated through [[contrition|compunction]], humility, and perseverance. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is therefore instructed to "seek earnestly the grace of devotion, to ask it fervently, to wait for it patiently and faithfully, to receive it gratefully, to preserve it humbly" (111-003).

Devotion is the animating warmth of the spiritual life; without it, the soul grows cold and the spirit of prayer languishes.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats devotion not as an occasional pious sentiment but as the habitual fervour of the soul that permeates every aspect of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life. Its presence quickens prayer, reading, and sacramental reception; its withdrawal exposes the soul's native lukewarmness and tests the sincerity of its love for God.

### Devotion as a Gift of Grace

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] consistently traces devotion to its source in [[grace|divine grace]]. In the great litany of grace's attributes in Book III, Chapter LV, the [[discipleship|disciple]] names grace as "the nurse of devotion, the drawer forth of tears" (092-059). Devotion is not manufactured by human effort but is poured out from above: "give the waters of devotion to water the face of the earth, and cause it to bring forth good and perfect fruit" (060-042).

In the sacramental context, the faithful "gain from this most excellent Sacrament great grace of devotion and love of virtue" (097-094). Left to itself, the soul is "cold, hard of heart, indevout" — through God it becomes "fervent, zealous, and devout" (100-042). Devotion is therefore never a ground for pride but a mark of dependence: the [[discipleship|disciple]] must "hide the grace of devotion, and not to lift thyself up on high" (044-002).

### The Cultivation of Devotion

Yet devotion is not passive. The [[discipleship|disciple]] is commanded to "strive earnestly" for it (111-003) and to prepare the heart through concrete spiritual disciplines. [[compunction|Compunction]] opens the way: "Give thyself to [[compunction|compunction of heart]] and thou shalt find devotion" (021-005).

[[humility|Humility]] and self-denial are its preconditions: "Whosoever therefore shall have lifted up his will unto God with singleness of heart, and shall have delivered himself from every inordinate love or dislike of any created thing, he will be the most fit for receiving grace, and worthy of the gift of devotion" (111-026). The daily exercises of the religious life are to be ordered by devotion: "give thyself to thyself as thy devotion leadeth thee" (019-046).

[[perseverance|Perseverance]] in spiritual exercises sustains devotion; negligence lets it slip away. "For hereby is a man made entirely indisposed to good, if he immediately return from [[communion|Communion]] to give himself up to outward [[comfort|consolation]]s" (108-042).

### The Danger of Presumption

Thomas à Kempis warns sharply against the spiritual pride that can accompany devotion. In Book III, Chapter VII, "Of Hiding Our [[grace|Grace]] Under the Guard of [[humility|Humility]]," [[jesus-christ|Christ]] teaches that "some who were presumptuous because of the grace of devotion within them, have destroyed themselves, because they would do more than they were able, not considering the measure of their own littleness" (044-021).

Devotion that becomes an occasion for [[pride|self-exaltation]] is quickly lost: "they quickly lost grace" (044-025). The [[discipleship|disciple]] must remember that even those with "great virtue and zealous devotion, yet much is lacking unto him, yea, one thing which is the most necessary" (036-029) — the complete renunciation of self-love. True devotion is inseparable from the awareness of one's own unworthiness.

### Devotion and Interior Recollection

Devotion requires detachment from worldly concerns. The place of outward retirement "availeth little if the spirit of devotion is wanting" (064-023), and the [[discipleship|disciple]] who has rejected human [[comfort|consolation]]s "whether for the sake of gaining devotion, or because of the necessity by which I was compelled to seek Thee" (077-020) finds himself able to [[faith|trust in God]]'s grace and receive new [[comfort|consolation]].

The contrast between authentic interior devotion and its merely external forms is drawn pointedly: "There are some who carry their devotion only in books, some in pictures, some in outward signs and figures; some have Me in their mouths, but little in their hearts" (041-036). Those who are truly devout "being enlightened in their understanding and purged in their affections, continually long after eternal things" (041-039).

### The Sacramental Life of Devotion

Book IV, devoted to the Blessed Sacrament, presents devotion as the proper disposition for receiving [[communion|Communion]]. The [[discipleship|disciple]] laments: "I, unhappy and poorest of mankind, how shall I bring Thee into my house, who scarce know how to spend half an hour in devotion?" (097-045). The holy men of old "manifested so great affection of devotion towards Thy Divine Ser[[sin|vice]]" (097-063).

David's example shows that "if so great devotion was then exercised," how much more should the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian people show "reverence and devotion" at the reception of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s Body and Blood (097-071). The [[discipleship|disciple]] offers himself "with the deepest devotion and fervent love, with all affection and fervour of heart" (113-003), longing for the "ardent devotion" of the saints (113-007).

Many, however, "have lost devotion whilst they sought to search into deeper things" (114-013), preferring intellectual curiosity to the simplicity of faith. The [[discipleship|disciple]]'s constant prayer is that "with coming often to Thy mysteries the zeal of my devotion may increase" (100-068).

### Key Passages

> "Give thyself to compunction of heart and thou shalt find devotion. Compunction openeth the way for many good things, which dissoluteness is wont quickly to lose." (021-005)

> "My Son, it is better and safer for thee to hide the grace of devotion, and not to lift thyself up on high, nor to speak much thereof, nor to value it greatly; but rather to despise thyself, and to fear as though this grace were given to one unworthy thereof." (044-002)

> "It is the mistress of truth, the teacher of discipline, the light of the heart, the solace of anxiety, the banisher of sorrow, the deliverer from fear, the nurse of devotion, the drawer forth of tears." (092-057)

> "Whosoever therefore shall have lifted up his will unto God with singleness of heart, and shall have delivered himself from every inordinate love or dislike of any created thing, he will be the most fit for receiving grace, and worthy of the gift of devotion." (111-026)

## Biblical Foundation

The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] grounds its understanding of devotion in several scriptural themes. The Psalmist's prayer — "With my whole heart I seek you" (Psalm 119:10) and "Unite my heart to fear your name" (Psalm 86:11) — expresses the undivided orientation of the heart that Thomas presents as devotion. The early Church's [[perseverance|steadfastness]] in "the apostles' teaching and the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers" (Acts 2:42) models the communal and sacramental dimension of devotion.

Paul's exhortation to "not be slothful in zeal, be fervent in spirit, serving the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Romans 12:11) captures the energetic character of true devotion — not a passive sentiment but an active and persevering fervour. The [[discipleship|disciple]]s on the road to Emmaus whose hearts "burned within them" while [[jesus-christ|Christ]] opened the Scriptures (Luke 24:32) exemplify the devotion that is kindled by encounter with the Word of God and culminates in the breaking of bread.

The prophetic devotion of David, who "danced with all his might before the Ark of God" and "taught the people of Israel to praise God with the whole heart" (097-064 to 097-070), provides the work's primary Old Testament model of wholehearted devotional worship.

## Related Concepts

- [[grace]] — The source and sustainer of devotion; grace is "the nurse of devotion" (092-059)
- [[prayer]] — The primary expression of devotion and the remedy for its withdrawal; "be instant in prayer, cease not to groan and knock" (108-028)
- [[consolation]] — The experiential sweetness that accompanies devotion, given and withdrawn according to God's wisdom
- [[humility]] — The indispensable safeguard of devotion; without it devotion turns to [[pride|presumption]] and is quickly lost
- [[love]] — Identified with grace and devotion's ultimate aim: the fervent love of God for His own sake
- [[contemplation]] — The interior recollection and heavenly-mindedness that devotion fosters and requires
- [[compunction]] — The [[contrition|sorrow for sin]] that "openeth the way" for devotion (021-005)
- [[self-denial]] — The ascetical discipline by which obstacles to devotion (inordinate attachments) are removed
