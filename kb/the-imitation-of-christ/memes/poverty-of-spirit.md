# Poverty of Spirit

"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven" (Matthew 5:3). The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] takes this first Beatitude not as a metaphor but as a literal summons to interior nakedness. For Thomas à Kempis, poverty of spirit is the foundational disposition of the spiritual life — the condition of soul without which no other virtue can flourish, because every attachment to [[creation|created things]], to reputation, or to one's own will is an obstacle to union with God.

## Definition

Poverty of spirit, in the vocabulary of The Imitation, is the radical interior dispossession by which the soul renounces not merely material goods but all clinging to [[creation|created things]], including one's own opinions, reputation, and will. Thomas defines it through a remarkable question: "For who shall find a man truly poor in spirit and free of all [[creation|created things]]?" (036-024). The adjective "truly" signals that this poverty is not outward destitution — a man may give away all his goods, "yet that is nothing" (036-026) — but an inward condition of nakedness before God. The perfected soul in this state can say with the Psalmist, "I am poor and needy" (036-039), yet Thomas immediately adds the paradox: "Nevertheless, no man is richer than he, no man stronger, no man freer" (036-039 to 036-040). Poverty of spirit is thus the condition of having nothing that one may possess everything — having let go of the finite that one may receive the infinite.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas does not devote a single chapter exclusively to poverty of spirit, but the theme pervades the entire work. It appears in three registers: as a critique of worldly attachment, as a description of the interior disposition required for grace, and as an eschatological promise.

### The Critique of Possession

In Book I, Chapter 36 ("Against the Vain [[judgment|Judgment]]s of Men"), Thomas locates poverty of spirit as the remedy for dependence on human opinion. The passage dismisses [[world|the world]]'s standards — learning, beauty, wealth, reputation — and insists that true valuation comes from what a man is before God. "A man may give away all his goods, yet that is nothing" (036-026). Spiritual poverty means being stripped even of the satisfaction of having given everything away. It is not measured by what one has renounced but by what one still clings to, and Thomas anchors this in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s words: "When ye have done all things that are commanded you, say, We are unprofitable servants" (036-037).

### The Interior Disposition

The second register is personal and transformational. In Book III, Chapter 92, Thomas prays: "O most blessed grace that makest the poor in spirit rich in virtues, and renderest him who is rich in many things humble in spirit" (092-047). [[grace|Grace]] itself is the agent of this poverty — it is not a human achievement but a divine gift. The paradox is complete: poverty of spirit is richness in virtues; humility of spirit is the true wealth. The soul emptied of self is filled with God.

### The Eschatological Promise

The third register is eschatological. In Book I, Chapter 24 ("Of [[judgment|Judgment]] and the Punishment of [[sin|Sin]]ners"), Thomas contrasts the outward judgments of men with the final verdict: "Then shall the poor and humble man have great confidence" (024-043). The [[blessedness|beatitude]]'s promise — "theirs is the kingdom of heaven" — is understood as both present possession and future fulfillment. The poor in spirit already possess God, and at the judgment this possession will be made manifest.

### Key Passages

> "Rarely is any one found so spiritual as to be stripped of all selfish thoughts, for who shall find a man truly poor in spirit and free of all created things?" (036-024)

> "O most blessed grace that makest the poor in spirit rich in virtues, and renderest him who is rich in many things humble in spirit." (092-047)

> "Then may he be truly poor and naked in spirit, and be able to say with the Prophet, As for me, I am poor and needy." (036-038 to 036-039)

## Biblical Foundation

Poverty of spirit is rooted in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s first Beatitude: "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven" (Matthew 5:3). Thomas draws this directly into the devotional life through two further scriptural anchors. The first is the Psalmist's cry, "I am poor and needy" (Psalm 40:17), which Thomas cites in the "poor and naked in spirit" passage (036-039). The second is [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s teaching on the unprofitable servant: "When ye have done all things that are commanded you, say, We are unprofitable servants" (Luke 17:10), which Thomas invokes (036-037) to strip away even the self-congratulation of having performed good works. The Pauline warning — "though I bestow all my goods to feed the poor... and have not [[love|charity]], it profiteth me nothing" (1 Corinthians 13:3) — underlies Thomas's insistence that outward renunciation without inward detachment counts for nothing.

## Practical Application

The text's practical counsel is twofold. First, examine not what one has given up but what one still clings to — reputation, the good opinion of others, the secret satisfaction of having done well. Second, pray for the grace of poverty of spirit itself, since it "makest the poor in spirit rich in virtues" (092-047) — it is a gift received, not a state achieved. The measure of spiritual poverty is not the absence of possessions but the presence of this paradoxical richness: the soul that has nothing and possesses everything because it possesses God.
