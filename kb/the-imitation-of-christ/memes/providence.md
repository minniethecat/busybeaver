# Providence

Providence is the theological conviction that undergirds the Kempian call to resignation and [[trust-in-god|trust]]. If God orders all things, the soul may rest in peace regardless of outward circumstances. The Imitation invokes providence explicitly as the ground for accepting whatever befalls the believer.

## Definition

Divine providence is the continuous activity of God by which He governs creation, ordering all events——both great and small——toward His purposes. In the Kempian understanding, providence is not a distant deistic watchmaking but an intimate and personal guidance: every [[temptation|trial]], [[comfort|consolation]], loss, and blessing comes either by God's direct will or by His permissive will, and nothing lies outside His ordering. This conviction is the logical foundation of the virtue of resignation——if God orders all, resistance is both futile and impious.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Imitation treats providence most directly in Book III, where the soul is taught to accept suffering and [[suffering|adversity]] as divinely appointed. The explicit statement appears in a passage on bearing [[suffering|tribulation]]: nothing comes to pass "without cause"——all events fall under "counsel and providence" (087-035). This brief but theologically weighty assertion anchors the entire Kempian program of interior peace.

The doctrine of providence runs beneath the surface of many passages that do not name it directly. When Thomas à Kempis urges the reader to "cast thyself wholly upon the [[jesus-christ|Lord]] and commit thyself to His keeping" (084-003, via [[trust-in-god|trust]]-in-god), he is drawing on the logic of providence: [[trust-in-god|trust]] is rational only if God is [[trust-in-god|trust]]worthy, and God is [[trust-in-god|trust]]worthy because He governs all things wisely. The counsel to accept [[suffering|tribulation]] as "sent from God" and to bear it with patience presupposes providential ordering. Likewise, the recurring theme that "all is vanity except to love God and serve Him alone" implies that [[world|the world]]'s apparent chaos is ordered toward a single worthy end: union with God.

### Key Passages

> "Nothing cometh to pass on earth without cause, and without counsel and providence" (087-035)

## Biblical Foundation

The doctrine of providence is richly attested throughout Scripture. The Old Testament foundation includes Joseph's recognition that what his brothers intended for evil "God meant unto good" (Genesis 50:20), the Psalmist's confidence that "the steps of a good man are ordered by the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Psalm 37:23), and the wisdom tradition's insistence that "the lot is cast into the lap, but the whole disposing thereof is of the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Proverbs 16:33). The Book of [[job|Job]] provides the most sustained biblical meditation on providence and suffering, with [[job|Job]]'s final confession——"I know that Thou canst do everything" ([[job|Job]] 42:2)——echoing the Kempian surrender to [[wisdom|divine wisdom]].

The New Testament deepens the doctrine. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] teaches that not a sparrow falls "without your Father" and that "the very hairs of your head are all numbered" (Matthew 10:29–30). Paul's great declaration in Romans 8:28——"all things work together for good to them that love God"——is the New Testament warrant for the Kempian conviction that every circumstance serves the believer's ultimate good. The Pauline doxology in Romans 11:36 ("for of Him, and through Him, and to Him, are all things") frames providence as cosmic in scope.

## Practical Application

The Imitation applies the doctrine of providence to the concrete [[temptation|trial]]s of the spiritual life. When [[comfort|consolation]] is withdrawn and the soul feels abandoned, providence teaches that even this [[desolation|dryness]] is divinely appointed for growth in patience and humility. When others mistreat or slander the believer, providence reframes the injury as permitted by God for the soul's purification. The practical response to every [[suffering|adversity]] is not to ask "why me?" but to say with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in Gethsemane: "not as I will, but as Thou wilt" (Matthew 26:39). This interior surrender, grounded in the conviction of providence, is the essence of Kempian peace.
