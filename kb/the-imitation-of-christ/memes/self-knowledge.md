# Self-Knowledge

The humble and honest awareness of who one truly is before God — not the self-esteem of modern psychology but the spiritual realism that knows its own misery, its absolute dependence on grace, and its true place in the order of creation. Self-knowledge is the foundation of the spiritual life: without it, the disciple builds virtues on the sand of self-deception; with it, even the lowest place becomes a firm ground for growth.

## Definition

Self-knowledge, in the Christian ascetical tradition, is not introspection for its own sake but a grace-illumined realism about the human condition. It encompasses three truths: the truth about God (infinite majesty, the source of all good), the truth about oneself (created from nothing, inclined to sin, sustained entirely by grace), and the truth about the relation between the two (creaturely dependence, the debt of love).

The classic formulation comes from Saint Augustine: "Noverim me, noverim Te" — "Let me know myself, that I may know Thee." Self-knowledge and knowledge of God are inseparable; growth in one is growth in the other. The soul that truly knows itself cannot be proud, for it sees that whatever good it possesses is a gift. The soul that knows God cannot despair, for it sees that mercy is greater than misery.

The Imitation of Christ places self-knowledge at the threshold of the spiritual life: "Know thyself to be unworthy of divine consolation, and worthy rather of much tribulation" (021-001). This is not morbid self-hatred but honest realism — the disposition of the publican who "would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven, but smote upon his breast, saying, God be merciful to me a sinner" (Luke 18:13).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis presents self-knowledge as the most neglected and most necessary of virtues. The disciple who knows many things but does not know himself knows nothing of value.

### The Foundation of Humility

Self-knowledge is the root of humility. The soul that truly sees its own poverty cannot exalt itself above others. "He is truly great who hath great charity. He is truly great who is little in his own eyes" (007-008). Self-knowledge dissolves the illusions by which the ego inflates itself — the imaginary virtues, the comparison with those perceived as weaker, the tendency to attribute to oneself what belongs to grace.

### Knowledge of One's Misery

*The Imitation* insists that the disciple must "judge himself to be the more unworthy" (059-002) — not from false modesty but from the recognition that every sin committed is one's own, and every good done is God's. This knowledge is painful: to see oneself without the flattering lens of self-love is to see something ugly. But the pain is medicinal. The soul that knows its wound will seek the physician; the soul that imagines itself healthy will perish.

### The Practical Effects

Self-knowledge produces concrete fruits in the spiritual life. It makes the disciple patient with the faults of others, since he knows his own. It makes him grateful for corrections, since he knows how blind he is to his own defects. It makes him persevering in prayer, since he knows he cannot stand for a moment without grace. Most importantly, self-knowledge keeps the soul close to Christ, for "they that are whole need not a physician, but they that are sick" (Matthew 9:12).

## Biblical Foundation

The Psalmist asks the fundamental question: "What is man, that thou art mindful of him?" (Psalm 8:4). Jeremiah warns that "the heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked: who can know it?" (Jeremiah 17:9) — a sober acknowledgment that self-knowledge requires divine assistance. Saint Paul counsels "every man that is among you not to think of himself more highly than he ought to think; but to think soberly" (Romans 12:3). Saint James compares the one who hears the word but does not act to "a man beholding his natural face in a glass: for he beholdeth himself, and goeth his way, and straightway forgetteth what manner of man he was" (James 1:23-24) — self-knowledge must be acted upon, not merely acknowledged.
