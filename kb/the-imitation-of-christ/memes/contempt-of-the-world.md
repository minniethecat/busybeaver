# Contempt of the World

The deliberate spiritual discipline, central to the entire programme of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], by which the soul esteems all temporal honour, riches, pleasures, and attachments as nothing in comparison with the love of God — a contempt that is not hatred of creation but the clear-sighted recognition that nothing created can satisfy the heart made for the Creator, and the practical renunciation of whatever hinders the soul's union with [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Definition

Contempt of [[world|the world]] — *contemptus mundi* in the Latin tradition from which Thomas à Kempis writes — is not cynicism, misanthropy, or ingratitude for the goods of creation. It is the spiritual discipline of relativizing all [[creation|created things]] in light of their ultimate end. The world, in Thomas's vocabulary, means not the physical creation nor human society as such but the whole system of values, attachments, and desires that is organized around the self rather than around God. To despise [[world|the world]] is to refuse to grant ultimate value to what is only penultimate — to see riches, honour, pleasure, reputation, and even natural affection as means to be used on the way to God, not as ends to be rested in. It is "the [[wisdom|highest wisdom]], by despising the world, to tend towards [[paradise|the Kingdom of Heaven]]" (001-021).

## In The Imitation of Christ

The contempt of [[world|the world]] is not one theme among many in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] but the founding spiritual posture from which every subsequent counsel flows. The very first chapter is titled "Of the Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], and of Contempt of the World and All Its Vanities" (001-001), and the work returns to this theme at every major structural juncture. Book III contains an entire chapter titled "That It Is Sweet to Despise the [[world|World]] and to Serve God" (047-001), and Book IV extends the theme to the contempt of all temporal honour (078-001).

### The Foundation of Discipleship

Thomas places the contempt of [[world|the world]] at the very gate of the spiritual life. Before the [[discipleship|disciple]] can learn to pray, to obey, to love, or to suffer, he must first learn to despise what [[world|the world]] prizes and to prize what [[world|the world]] despises. This is not an advanced teaching reserved for the proficient but the elementary grammar of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[discipleship|disciple]]ship. "He that followeth Me walketh not in darkness" (001-001) — but [[discipleship|following Christ]] means walking away from [[world|the world]]'s values, [[world|the world]]'s ambitions, and [[world|the world]]'s definition of a successful life.

The contempt of [[world|the world]] is inseparable from the [[discipleship|imitation of Christ]], for [[jesus-christ|Christ]] Himself lived in contempt of [[world|the world]]'s standards: He was born in poverty, lived in obscurity, died in shame, and rose in power. To imitate [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is to adopt His scale of values, in which the last are first, the humble are exalted, and those who lose their lives for His sake find them. "I teach," [[jesus-christ|Christ]] declares in the interior colloquy of Book III, "the contempt of all [[world|worldly things]] and in the contempt of [[creation|created things]] the desire of eternal things" (049-034).

### The Sweetness of Contempt

One of the most distinctive features of Thomas's treatment is his insistence that the contempt of [[world|the world]], far from being a grim and joyless renunciation, is actually sweet. The chapter title itself declares: "That It Is Sweet to Despise the [[world|World]] and to Serve God" (047-001). This sweetness is not a paradox but a psychological and spiritual reality: the soul that has detached itself from created goods discovers a liberty, a lightness, and a peace that [[world|the world]] cannot give and cannot take away. The man who no longer needs [[world|the world]]'s approval has been set free from an exacting and merciless master.

"They who perfectly despise [[world|the world]] and strive to live unto God in holy discipline — these know the divine sweetness" (057-042). Contempt of [[world|the world]] is not an end in itself but the negative condition for a positive good — the emptying of the heart that makes room for God. "In the contempt of all [[world|worldly things]] and in the contempt of [[creation|created things]], the desire of eternal things" (049-034) — the negative and the positive are two sides of a single movement. One cannot cling to [[world|the world]] and embrace God at the same time; the arms are not long enough.

### Contempt of World and Contempt of Self

Thomas draws an intimate connection between contempt of [[world|the world]] and contempt of self. The same soul that learns to despise [[world|the world]]'s honours must also learn to despise its own claim to honour. "Conceive contempt for thyself, if thou wilt prevail against flesh and blood" (050-013). "When thou shalt come to an utter contempt of thyself, know that then thou shalt enjoy abundance of peace" (062-036). Self-contempt is not pathological self-hatred but the honest acknowledgment that one is a creature and a sinner — that one has no claim on God and no ground for boasting. The self that has been emptied of self-regard is the self that God can fill.

This double contempt — of [[world|the world]] and of the self — is the key to the interior peace that Thomas promises as the fruit of the spiritual life. The soul that cares nothing for [[world|the world]]'s praise and nothing for its own reputation "enjoyeth abundance of peace" (062-036) because it has nothing left to lose. "Behold, in the contempt of all [[world|worldly things]], there shall be great sweetness" (049-034).

### Contempt and the Cross

The contempt of [[world|the world]] finds its ultimate expression and its ultimate vindication in the Cross of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. [[cross|The Cross]] is [[world|the world]]'s contempt for God made visible — God despised, rejected, and crucified by [[world|the world]] He came to save. And the Cross is also God's contempt for [[world|the world]]'s values — the demonstration that power is made perfect in weakness, that glory is hidden in shame, and that life springs from death. To despise [[world|the world]] is to stand with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] at the foot of the Cross and to accept that [[world|the world]]'s verdict on Him is [[world|the world]]'s verdict on all who follow Him.

This is why Thomas can speak of "a perfect contempt of [[world|the world]]" (023-030) as one of the fruits of the holy death — the death of the old self, crucified with [[jesus-christ|Christ]], that rises to a new life in which [[world|the world]] has no more claim. "The world is crucified unto me, and I unto [[world|the world]]" (Galatians 6:14) is the Pauline text that sums up the Kempian programme. Contempt of [[world|the world]] is not a strategy for worldly success disguised as piety; it is the form of life that corresponds to the Cross.

## Biblical Foundation

The biblical foundation for the contempt of [[world|the world]] is the New Testament's radical relativization of all earthly goods in light of the Kingdom. "What shall it profit a man, if he shall gain the whole world, and lose his own soul?" (Matthew 16:26) — the dominical question that Thomas places at the head of his work. "Love not [[world|the world]], neither the things that are in [[world|the world]]. If any man love [[world|the world]], the love of the Father is not in him" (1 John 2:15) — the Johannine antithesis that admits of no compromise. "The world is crucified unto me, and I unto [[world|the world]]" (Galatians 6:14) — the Pauline declaration that Thomas takes as the charter of the ascetical life.

The Old Testament wisdom tradition prepares the way: "vanity of [[vanity|vanities]], all is vanity" (Ecclesiastes 1:2) is the opening keynote of The Imitation, and the Preacher's exhaustive demonstration that all created goods fail to satisfy the human heart provides the experiential warrant for Thomas's contempt of [[world|the world]]. The Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3-12) invert [[world|the world]]'s values systematically — blessed are the poor, the mourners, the meek, the persecuted — and "blessed are the [[poverty-of-spirit|poor in spirit]]" is the dominical foundation of the contemptus mundi tradition. Finally, "I count all things but loss for the excellency of the knowledge of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] my [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Philippians 3:8) provides the apostolic model: [[world|the world]] is not so much rejected as surpassed, its goods rendered worthless by the surpassing worth of knowing [[jesus-christ|Christ]].
