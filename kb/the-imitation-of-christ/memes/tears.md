# Tears

In the Christian spiritual tradition, tears are not a sign of weakness but a gift of grace — *donum lacrimarum*, the gift of tears. "They that sow in tears shall reap in joy" (Psalm 126:5). The monastic tradition in particular developed a rich theology of tears as a charism of prayer: tears of compunction for one's own sins, tears of compassion for the sufferings of Christ, tears of intercession for the world. Unlike ordinary weeping, which is involuntary, the gift of tears is understood as a supernatural softening of the heart — a grace that prepares the soul for the infusion of divine love and the gift of consolation.

## Definition

Tears in the spiritual sense — *compunctio cordis*, compunction of heart — are the outward sign of an inward piercing. The word "compunction" comes from the Latin *compungere*, to prick or puncture: the soul is pierced by the awareness of its sin, pierced by the sight of Christ's wounds, pierced by love. Tears are the natural overflow of this piercing. The tradition distinguishes several kinds: tears of repentance (weeping for one's own sins), tears of compassion (weeping with Christ in His passion), tears of desire (weeping from longing for heaven), and tears of consolation (weeping from the sweetness of God's presence). Saint Gregory the Great classifies tears as a gift that purifies the memory, sharpens the understanding, and kindles the will.

## In The Imitation of Christ

*The Imitation of Christ* belongs to the spiritual tradition of the *Devotio Moderna*, which valued inwardness and affective piety. Tears are the language of a heart that has been moved.

### Contrition and the Hardened Heart

Kempis is acutely aware of the problem of the hardened heart — the soul that can contemplate its sins, the passion of Christ, and the vanity of the world without being moved. He prays for tears as a remedy: "Give me the grace of tears" (003-001). The gift of tears is not something one can produce by effort; it must be asked for. The disciple who cannot weep should at least desire to weep, for the desire itself is a form of compunction.

### Tears of Devotion

The *Imitation* frequently speaks of devotion and consolation as realities felt in the heart. Tears are the extreme form of this affective piety: the soul that is overwhelmed by the goodness of God weeps not from sorrow but from sweetness. "Devotion is the fire of divine love" (001-005), and tears are one of its visible flames. Yet Kempis also warns against prizing the gift above the Giver: "Seek not consolation but the God of consolation" (001-004).

### Sorrow That Leads to Joy

The paradox of holy tears is that they water the ground of joy. "He who sorrows for his sins shall rejoice in the mercy of God" — the pattern is constant in the *Imitation*: compunction leads to purification, purification to love, and love to the peace that surpasses understanding.

## Biblical Foundation

The Psalter is filled with tears — David waters his couch with weeping (Psalm 6:6), and Jeremiah is called the weeping prophet (Jeremiah 9:1). In the Gospels, the sinful woman who washed Christ's feet with her tears (Luke 7:38) became the archetype of penitential weeping; Christ's own tears over Jerusalem (Luke 19:41) and at the tomb of Lazarus (John 11:35) sanctify human grief. Saint Paul speaks of serving the Lord "with many tears" (2 Corinthians 2:4; Acts 20:19), showing that tears accompany apostolic labor. The promise that "God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes" (Revelation 21:4) does not denigrate earthly tears but transfigures them — they are the seeds of an eternal consolation.
