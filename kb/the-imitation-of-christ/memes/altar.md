# Altar

The altar — the table of sacrifice and the place of encounter between God and man — runs through the whole of Scripture from Abel's offering to the heavenly altar of the Apocalypse. In The Imitation of Christ, the altar is overwhelmingly the altar of the Eucharist: the place where Christ gives Himself to the soul in Holy Communion, and the place where the soul must prostrate itself in humility before approaching so great a mystery.

## Location

In the biblical world, altars were erected wherever God revealed Himself: by Noah after the flood (Genesis 8:20), by Abraham at Moriah (Genesis 22:9), by Moses at Sinai (Exodus 24:4), and most permanently in the Tabernacle courtyard and later in Solomon's Temple, where the altar of burnt offering stood before the sanctuary (1 Kings 8:22–53). In the medieval church of Thomas à Kempis's world, the altar occupies the east end of the church, the focal point of the liturgy where the priest celebrates Mass and the faithful receive the consecrated Host.

## Biblical Significance

The altar is the place of sacrifice — the point of transaction between the holy God and sinful humanity. On the altar, offerings are consumed, covenants are sealed, and sins are atoned. The Old Testament altar of burnt offering was kept perpetually burning (Leviticus 6:12–13), a flame that signified the unceasing worship due to God. In the New Testament, the physical altar yields to the once-for-all sacrifice of Christ (Hebrews 10:12), yet the altar remains as the Eucharistic table where that one sacrifice is re-presented and its fruits applied to the faithful.

### Key Events at This Place

- **Solomon's Dedication of the Temple** — Solomon "was seven years building his magnificent temple to the praise of Thy Name" (097-039) and then stood before the altar of the Lord and spread out his hands in prayer, dedicating the house where Israel would worship. The altar was the climax of the temple's consecration.

- **The Altar of Holy Communion** — Thomas à Kempis reflects on the disciple's approach to "Thine altar and table of Holy Communion" (110-008), the place where the Eucharist is received and the soul is united to Christ in the most intimate manner possible this side of heaven.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The altar appears in The Imitation of Christ in three distinct but related registers: as the place of prayer, as the site of Eucharistic encounter, and as a measure of the soul's devotion.

The first register is illustrated in Book I, Chapter 25, where Thomas recounts how a certain devout man, "being on a certain day overwhelmed with grief, cast himself down in prayer before the altar in a church" (025-015). There he heard an interior voice from God assuring him that perseverance — not foreknowledge of perseverance — is what God asks. The altar here is the place where the burdened soul casts itself down and receives divine consolation.

The second register appears in Book IV, on the Blessed Sacrament. Thomas confesses his own unworthiness to approach: "It is to my shame that I approach Thine altar and table of Holy Communion" (110-008). The altar is set in parallel with the "table" — it is at once the place of sacrifice and the place of feeding. The soul that approaches must do so not with levity or curiosity but with "steadfast faith, devout hope, and sincere charity" (Book IV, Chapter 1).

The third register is typological. Thomas recalls the devotion of Solomon, who "offered a thousand peace offerings" at the altar (097-039), and contrasts it with the carelessness many bring to the Eucharistic altar where Christ Himself is the offering. The external altar of the Old Covenant prefigures the interior altar of the heart, and the reverence shown at the one ought to instruct the reverence due at the other.

> "Being on a certain day overwhelmed with grief, cast himself down in prayer before the altar in a church, and meditated within himself, saying, 'Oh! if I but knew that I should still persevere,' and presently heard within him a voice from God." (025-015)

> "It is to my shame that I approach Thine altar and table of Holy Communion." (110-008)

### The Interior Altar

The most searching use of the altar in The Imitation of Christ occurs not in reference to any physical structure but to the interior altar of the heart. In Book IV, Chapter 8, Thomas writes that the devout soul must "offer thyself to the honour of My name on the altar of thine heart as a perpetual whole burnt-offering, even by faithfully presenting thy body and soul unto Me" (103-037).

This interiorization of the altar is characteristic of the devotio moderna tradition. The external altar of stone, however venerable, is but a figure of the altar within, where the soul offers itself in union with the self-offering of Christ. The "perpetual whole burnt-offering" that Thomas speaks of is not the cessation of the material sacrifices of the Old Law but the total consecration of the self — body and soul — laid upon the altar of the will in an act of unreserved surrender. The same passage immediately links this interior oblation to "the Sacrament of My Body and Blood to thy soul's health," binding the interior and sacramental altars into a single mystery: what is offered inwardly must be sealed and nourished at the visible altar of the Eucharist.

> "Offer thyself to the honour of My name on the altar of thine heart as a perpetual whole burnt-offering, even by faithfully presenting thy body and soul unto Me." (103-037)

The threefold pattern of altar in The Imitation — prayer, Eucharist, interior offering — corresponds to the threefold movement of the spiritual life: the soul casts itself down in humility, rises to receive the divine gift, and then offers itself back to God in a sacrifice of praise.

## Symbolic Meaning

The altar is at once a place of death and a place of life. Every offering laid upon it dies to its former state — grain is ground, wine is poured out, the lamb is slain — but this death is for the sake of communion with God. In the Eucharist, Christ's sacrifice is made present upon the altar, and the faithful who receive Him are nourished unto eternal life. For the disciple of The Imitation, the altar is therefore the school of humility: no one approaches worthily who does not first cast himself down in self-knowledge, acknowledging that "there is so much careless trifling and so little true contrition" in human hearts before the divine gift.
