# Fervor

**Fervor** (*fervor*) is the spiritual heat and intensity that distinguishes living devotion from dead routine in *The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]*. It is the quality of soul that prays with fire, loves without reservation, and pursues virtue with untiring energy. Thomas à Kempis presents fervor as both a gift to be sought and a disposition to be guarded — easily lost through negligence, and essential for spiritual progress.

## The Fervent Soul

The text describes the authentic spiritual person as one "who is fervent towards God" (006-017). This fervor is not a matter of emotional excitement but of the will's wholehearted orientation: the fervent soul directs all its energies toward God, holding nothing back. Kempis contrasts this with the tepid soul that divides its attention between God and [[creation|creatures]], never fully committed to either.

The prayer of the devout soul is described as rising "fervently": "The prayers of holy men were more fervently besought" (019-053). Fervent prayer is not a torrent of words but an intensity of desire — the heart aflame, crying out not from the lips alone but from the depths.

> Thou raisest me fervently, raisest me so powerfully that my soul cannot rest in herself, but is lifted up above all created things [086-009]

This passage from the dialogue of Book III captures the experience of fervor as divine elevation: God Himself kindles the flame, and the soul, set on fire, rises beyond its natural capacity.

## Fervor and Zeal

Fervor is closely linked to zeal (*zelus*), the eager and untiring pursuit of spiritual good. The text praises "[[zeal|fervent zeal]] and desire after spiritual things" (018-016) in the lives of the saints. The saints did not merely perform religious duties — they burned with desire for God, and this fire consumed every lesser attachment.

Kempis exhorts the soul to "strive to serve Me with fervent will" (086-015). The will itself must be ignited — ser[[sin|vice]] rendered from cold duty is insufficient. The fervent will delights in what it does for God; it runs rather than walks the path of obedience.

The text also acknowledges the reality that fervor naturally fluctuates: one is not "always able to continue in very fervent desire" (088-003). This realism prevents discouragement — the ebb and flow of sensible fervor is part of the human condition, and the essential thing is that the fundamental orientation of the will remains toward God.

## The Danger of Lukewarmness

Fervor is defined most sharply by its opposite: lukewarmness (*[[lukewarmness|tepor]]*). The text warns repeatedly against the spiritual mediocrity that is neither cold nor hot: "We remain lukewarm and unspiritual" (011-015). The lukewarm soul has enough religion to [[silence|quiet]] conscience but not enough to transform life.

Kempis traces the loss of fervor to negligence in small things: "When a man begins to grow lukewarm, then he fears a little labor" (029-021). The descent from fervor to [[lukewarmness|tepidity]] is gradual — a single omitted prayer, a small indulgence, a tolerated distraction — until the soul finds itself "altogether lukewarm and reckless" (077-009).

The antidote is vigilance: "Be thou watchful and diligent in God's ser[[sin|vice]]" — the fervent soul guards its flame against every draft of worldliness that would extinguish it.

## Fervor and Amendment

Fervor is the engine of [[amendment|amendment of life]]. The text notes that "the dread of the difficulty... holds back many from progress and fervent amendment" (025-027). True amendment — the zealous reformation of one's whole life — requires fervor. Without the fire of love, reform remains a burden to be avoided; with it, reform becomes the soul's delight.

In the context of the [[eucharist|Eucharist]], fervor receives its highest expression. The devout reception of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s Body is described as rekindling spiritual fervor: the sacrament "inflames the devout soul and draws it to fervent love" [cf. Book IV]. Here fervor is both the preparation for and the fruit of communion with God.

## Biblical Foundation

The command to be "fervent in spirit, serving the [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (Romans 12:11) is the direct scriptural foundation. The sobering words to the church of Laodicea — "because thou art lukewarm, and neither cold nor hot, I will spue thee out of my mouth" (Revelation 3:15-16) — give Kempis's warnings their ultimate gravity. Fervor is not optional; it is the temperature at which the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life must be lived.
