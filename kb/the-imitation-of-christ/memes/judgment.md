# Judgment

The divine assessment of every human life — in The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], the thought of judgment serves as a powerful moral restraint, a relativisation of all human opinion, and a motive for continual self-examination rather than the judging of others. "In all that thou doest, remember the end, and how thou wilt stand before a strict judge, from whom nothing is hid, who is not bribed with gifts, nor accepteth excuses, but will judge righteous judgment" (024-004). Thomas à Kempis treats judgment under two distinct but related aspects: the divine judgment that awaits every soul, and the rash human judgment that the [[discipleship|disciple]] must avoid.

## Definition

Judgment, in the Kempian vocabulary, refers primarily to the eschatological assessment of every soul by God at the end of life and at the end of history. This judgment is characterised by four attributes: it is certain (it will happen), it is comprehensive (nothing is hidden from the Judge), it is righteous (unlike human judgments, it is not corrupted by partiality or ignorance), and it is individual (each soul bears its own burden). The secondary sense — rash judgment of others — is treated as a serious moral [[sin|fault]] precisely because it usurps a divine prerogative. "In judging others a man laboureth in vain; he often erreth, and easily falleth into sin; but in judging and examining himself he always laboureth to good purpose" (014-006). The proper response to the reality of divine judgment is therefore twofold: examine yourself, and refrain from judging your neighbour.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Judgment appears throughout The Imitation as one of the "four last things" (death, judgment, heaven, hell) that concentrate the mind on eternal realities. Thomas's treatment is pastorally focused: the doctrine of judgment is not deployed to terrify but to sober, to redirect attention from the transient opinions of men to the enduring verdict of God.

### The Day of Judgment

Thomas devotes an entire chapter of Book I to "the judgment and punishment of the wicked" (024-001), in which he paints a vivid picture of the last day. On that day, "the righteous man [shall] stand in great boldness before the face of such as have afflicted him and made no account of his labours" (024-039). The judgment will effect a comprehensive reversal: the poor and humble will have confidence, while the proud will be "taken with fear on every side" (024-043). Every hidden thing will be brought to light, and the standards by which [[world|the world]] judges — wealth, power, learning, reputation — will be exposed as worthless. "Then a pure and [[conscience|good conscience]] shall more rejoice than learned philosophy. Then contempt of riches shall have more weight than all the treasure of the children of this world" (024-056). The judgment is thus the ultimate vindication of the values The Imitation has been commending all along: humility, [[purity-of-heart|purity of conscience]], contempt of [[world|the world]].

### Judgment as Present Restraint

The thought of judgment is not merely a future terror but a present discipline. "In all that thou doest, remember the end" (024-004) — the *[[death|memento mori]]* tradition transformed into a principle of daily conduct. Thomas asks pointedly: "Why dost thou not provide for thyself against the day of judgment, when no man shall be able to be excused or defended by means of another, but each one shall bear his burden himself alone?" (024-008). The answer, implicitly, is that we are distracted by present pleasures and present fears. The fear of an angry man looms larger in our imagination than the [[fear-of-the-lord|fear of God]]; the approval of our peers matters more than the approval of the Judge. Thomas's strategy is to reorient the imagination: "Be thou anxious now and sorrowful for thy [[sin|sins]], that in the day of judgment thou mayest have boldness with the blessed" (024-039). Sorrow now spares sorrow then; self-judgment now renders divine judgment bearable.

### Against Rash Judgment

The complementary teaching — "On avoiding rash judgment" (014-001) — addresses the human tendency to usurp the divine role. "Look well unto thyself, and beware that thou judge not the doings of others" (014-006). Thomas gives both a theological reason and a practical reason for this prohibition. The theological reason is that judgment belongs to God alone; when a human judges another, he claims a knowledge and a prerogative that are not his. The practical reason is that human judgment is almost always erroneous: "According as a matter toucheth our fancy, so oftentimes do we judge of it; for easily do we fail of true judgment because of our own personal feeling" (014-006 to 014-009). Our judgments are contaminated by self-interest, by secret envy, by incomplete information. "If God were always the sole object of our desire, we should the less easily be troubled by the erring judgment of our fancy" (014-009). The cure for rash judgment is purity of intention — wanting only what God wants, and therefore being indifferent to the [[sin|fault]]s and merits of others except insofar as they concern God.

### Human Judgment Relativised

In Book III, [[jesus-christ|Christ]] instructs the [[discipleship|disciple]] to disregard human judgment entirely: "My Son, anchor thy soul firmly upon God, and fear not man's judgment, when conscience pronounceth thee pious and innocent" (083-051). Human judgment is fickle, contradictory, and impossible to satisfy: "Many men have many opinions, and therefore little [[trust-in-god|trust]] is to be placed in them. But moreover it is impossible to please all" (083-051). Even the [[apostle-paul|Apostle Paul]] "studied to please all in the [[jesus-christ|Lord]], and made himself all things to all men, yet nevertheless he counted it a very small thing that he should be judged by man's judgment" (083-052 to 083-053). The [[discipleship|disciple]] who is anchored in God is freed from the tyranny of human opinion — neither praise nor blame can touch the soul that rests in the divine judgment alone.

## Key Passages

> "In all that thou doest, remember the end, and how thou wilt stand before a strict judge, from whom nothing is hid, who is not bribed with gifts, nor accepteth excuses, but will judge righteous judgment. O most miserable and foolish sinner, who art sometimes in fear of the countenance of an angry man, what wilt thou answer to God, who knoweth all thy misdeeds?" (024-004)

> "Look well unto thyself, and beware that thou judge not the doings of others. In judging others a man laboureth in vain; he often erreth, and easily falleth into sin; but in judging and examining himself he always laboureth to good purpose." (014-006)

> "Then a pure and good conscience shall more rejoice than learned philosophy. Then contempt of riches shall have more weight than all the treasure of the children of this world. Then shall a godly prayer avail more than a dainty banquet." (024-056 to 024-057)

> "My Son, anchor thy soul firmly upon God, and fear not man's judgment, when conscience pronounceth thee pious and innocent. It is good and blessed thus to suffer; nor will it be grievous to the heart which is humble, and which trusteth in God more than in itself." (083-051)

## Biblical Foundation

The Kempian teaching on judgment is firmly rooted in the New Testament, particularly the Synoptic Gospels and the Pauline epistles. The prohibition against rash judgment is a direct echo of Matthew 7:1–2: "Judge not, that ye be not judged. For with what judgment ye judge, ye shall be judged." Thomas develops this dominical saying with psychological insight, tracing the roots of rash judgment to self-love and the distortion of perception by personal feeling. The teaching that "each one shall bear his burden himself alone" (024-008) reflects Romans 14:10–12: "We shall all stand before the judgment seat of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]... So then every one of us shall give account of himself to God." The insistence that God is the sole legitimate judge echoes James 4:12: "There is one lawgiver, who is able to save and to destroy: who art thou that judgest another?"

The eschatological vision of the last day, in which the hidden is revealed and the first become last, draws on the Olivet Discourse (Matthew 25:31–46) and Revelation 20:12: "The dead were judged out of those things which were written in the books, according to their works." Thomas's emphasis that the judgment is individual — no one can be "excused or defended by means of another" — echoes Hebrews 9:27: "It is appointed unto men once to die, but after this the judgment." The Pauline teaching that "we must all appear before the judgment seat of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (2 Corinthians 5:10) provides the comprehensive framework for the Kempian integration of present self-examination and future divine assessment.
