# Glory

The splendour, honour, and renown that the soul either seeks from [[creation|creatures]] or receives from God — a theme that Thomas à Kempis treats with penetrating realism, stripping away the illusions of worldly glory to reveal both its transience and the authentic glory that is found in humility, a [[conscience|good conscience]], and the Cross of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. "Oh how quickly passeth the glory of the world away!" (003-053), yet "the glory of a [[conscience|good conscience]] is the glory of a good man" (031-002).

## Definition

Glory, in the spiritual vocabulary of The Imitation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], carries two opposed senses that Thomas plays against each other with deliberate force. On the one hand, glory denotes the esteem, praise, and honour that men confer upon one another — vain-glory, the "wind" of human approval that puffs up the soul without filling it. This glory "passeth away" (003-053) with the swiftness of all temporal things. On the other hand, glory denotes the authentic splendour that belongs to God alone and is shared with those who humble themselves: "all His glory and beauty is from within" (026-009), and the [[discipleship|disciple]] who "glorieth in the Cross of our [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (031-014) participates in a glory that [[world|the world]] cannot give and cannot take away. The [[discernment|distinction]] between these two glories is the axis on which the whole treatment turns.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas treats glory under three distinct but interrelated aspects: the vanity of worldly glory, the hidden glory of interior virtue, and the paradoxical glory of the Cross. Together they form a comprehensive ascetical doctrine of glory that inverts [[world|the world]]'s valuation.

### The Vanity of Worldly Glory

Thomas opens his treatment of glory with an exclamation of almost elegiac force: "Oh how quickly passeth the glory of the world away!" (003-053). [[world|World]]ly glory — the praise of men, the honour of office, the renown of achievement — is a vapour that vanishes even as the soul grasps at it. The [[discipleship|disciple]] who seeks "the glory of the world" (031-016) builds his house on air; the glory he covets today will be another's tomorrow, and neither will survive the grave. Thomas's counsel is not the contempt of honour for its own sake but the redirection of the desire for glory toward its true object. The soul was made for glory; it is simply that the glory it was made for is divine, not human.

This critique of vain-glory is sharpened by its connection to pride. Vain-glory is pride made visible — pride seeking confirmation in the eyes of others. Thomas prays that God would "shield us from vain-glory" (012-007), recognising that the desire for human esteem is among the subtlest and most persistent of temptations. The very act of performing a good work can be corrupted by the inward glance toward how it will be received. The [[discipleship|disciple]] must learn to do good in secret, content with the "glory of a [[conscience|good conscience]]" (031-002) that needs no audience.

### The Glory of a Good Conscience

Against the [[vanity|emptiness]] of worldly glory, Thomas sets the substantial glory of interior [[justice|righteousness]]: "The glory of a [[conscience|good conscience]] is the glory of a good man" (031-002). This glory is intrinsic, not reputational; it inheres in the quality of the soul, not in the opinion of others. A [[conscience|good conscience]] is a conscience at peace with God, unstained by unrepented sin, unburdened by guilt. Its glory is the reflection of divine light in a soul that has been cleansed.

This interior glory is necessarily hidden from [[world|the world]]. The man of [[conscience|good conscience]] may be obscure, disregarded, even despised; his glory is known to God alone. But it is a glory that endures when all earthly honours have crumbled to dust. Thomas exhorts the [[discipleship|disciple]] to "have a [[conscience|good conscience]] and thou shalt have joy" (031-002) — the joy that accompanies glory is the inward testimony that one stands aright before God, whatever men may think. This is the glory that [[world|the world]] cannot give and [[world|the world]] cannot take, because its source is not in [[world|the world]].

### Glorying in the Cross

The most distinctive element of Thomas's treatment of glory is his revaluation of [[suffering|tribulation]] as an occasion for glory. Drawing on the [[apostle-paul|Apostle Paul]] — "God forbid that I should glory, save in the cross of our [[jesus-christ|Lord]] [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]" (Galatians 6:14) — Thomas insists that "to glory in [[suffering|tribulation]] is not grievous to him who loveth, for such glorying is glorying in the Cross of our [[jesus-christ|Lord]]" (031-013 to 031-014). The [[discipleship|disciple]] who suffers with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] shares in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s glory; the cross, which [[world|the world]] regards as the emblem of shame and defeat, becomes the instrument and symbol of the highest glory.

This is the complete inversion of worldly values. The world glories in strength, success, reputation, and pleasure; the [[discipleship|disciple]] glories in weakness, failure, humiliation, and pain — because these unite him to the Crucified. "The glory of the world" is "that which is temporal" (031-016 to 031-019); the glory of the Cross is eternal. The one who glories in the Cross "careth not for that which is temporal" (031-019), having found a glory so far surpassing earthly honour that the latter appears as the nothing it truly is.

The eschatological dimension crowns this treatment: the [[discipleship|disciple]] who has shared [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s humiliation will share His exaltation. "He raiseth him to glory" (027-016) — the humble soul that has accepted obscurity and suffering for love of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] will be raised to a glory that eye hath not seen. The "glory which shall be revealed" (019-061) far exceeds any glory that can be sought or attained in this present life.

## Key Passages

> "Oh how quickly passeth the glory of the world away!" (003-053)

> "The glory of a good conscience is the glory of a good man. Have a good conscience and thou shalt have joy." (031-002)

> "To glory in tribulation is not grievous to him who loveth, for such glorying is glorying in the Cross of our Lord." (031-013 to 031-014)

> "All His glory and beauty is from within, and there He taketh delight." (026-009)

> "He raiseth him to glory: to the humble He revealeth His secrets." (027-016)

## Biblical Foundation

The dialectic of true and false glory in The Imitation is profoundly Pauline. The Apostle's declaration — "God forbid that I should glory, save in the cross of our [[jesus-christ|Lord]] [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]], by whom [[world|the world]] is crucified unto me, and I unto [[world|the world]]" (Galatians 6:14) — is the direct source of Thomas's teaching on glorying in [[suffering|tribulation]]. Paul's contrast between the glory that comes from men and the glory that comes from God (John 5:44: "How can ye believe, which receive honour one of another, and seek not the honour that cometh from God only?") is the scriptural foundation for Thomas's critique of vain-glory. The prophetic warning — "Let not the wise man glory in his wisdom... but let him that glorieth glory in this, that he understandeth and knoweth Me" (Jeremiah 9:23-24) — reinforces the reorientation of glory from self to God. The eschatological dimension echoes Romans 8:18: "The sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed in us."
