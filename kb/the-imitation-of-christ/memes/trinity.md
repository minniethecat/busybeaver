# Trinity

The central mystery of the Christian faith: that the one true God exists eternally as three distinct Persons — the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit — each fully God, co-equal in majesty, consubstantial in being, and undivided in essence, yet truly distinct in their relations: the Father unbegotten, the Son eternally begotten of the Father, and the Holy Spirit eternally proceeding from the Father and the Son.

## Definition

The doctrine of the Trinity is the Church's dogmatic articulation of what Scripture reveals about the identity of God. It affirms three interconnected truths: there is only one God (Deuteronomy 6:4); the Father is God, the Son is God, and the Holy Spirit is God; and these three are truly distinct — not three names for one Person (modalism) nor three separate gods (tritheism). The classic formulation of the Council of Nicaea (AD 325) and Constantinople (AD 381) is enshrined in the Nicene Creed, which confesses the Son as "God from God, Light from Light, true God from true God, begotten, not made, consubstantial with the Father" and the Holy Spirit as "the Lord, the giver of life, who proceeds from the Father and the Son, who with the Father and the Son is adored and glorified."

The Trinity is a mystery in the strict theological sense — not a puzzle to be solved but a reality to be adored, exceeding the capacity of created reason while not contradicting it. It is the ground of all Christian life: Baptism is conferred "in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost" (Matthew 28:19); the Eucharist is offered to the Father, through the Son, in the Holy Spirit; and the goal of the Christian life is participation in the inner life of the Triune God.

## In The Imitation of Christ

While *The Imitation of Christ* does not contain a developed treatise on Trinitarian theology, the doctrine of the Trinity pervades its devotional language and shapes its entire understanding of the spiritual life.

### Christ's Relation to the Father

Throughout *The Imitation*, Jesus Christ speaks and acts in constant reference to the Father. Every grace descends from the Father through the Son, and every prayer ascends to the Father through the Son. The work's famous opening declaration — "He that followeth Me walketh not in darkness" — presupposes that Christ is the eternal Word, the true light that "enlighteneth every man that cometh into this world" (John 1:9). Christ's divinity is never in question; He speaks with the authority of God because He is God. The dialogue form of Book III, in which Christ addresses the soul directly, is itself an implicit confession of Christ's divine identity — the voice that speaks to the disciple's inmost heart is the voice of the Second Person of the Trinity.

### The Holy Spirit

The Holy Spirit, while less explicitly developed than the person of Christ, is present throughout the work as the interior teacher and consoler. The Spirit is the one who "searcheth all things, yea, the deep things of God" (1 Corinthians 2:10) and who "teacheth the humble in His way" — the inward Master who makes the words of Christ living and effective in the soul. The Spirit's role in the work is primarily illuminative and sanctifying: He is the one who gives "the grace of devotion" and who leads the soul into truth. Where Christ is the Word spoken, the Spirit is the breath that carries the Word into the heart.

### Trinitarian Devotion

The devotional language of *The Imitation* is pervasively Trinitarian, even where the Trinity is not named as such. The disciple's prayer ascends to the Father, through the merits of Christ, in the grace of the Holy Spirit. The doxological impulse that closes so many of the work's chapters — giving "praise and glory to God" — is implicitly Trinitarian, for Christian praise is always praise of the Father, through the Son, in the Holy Spirit. The book's final prayer, "Grant me, O Lord, to know what I ought to know, to love what I ought to love, to praise what is most pleasing to Thee," is a prayer to the Triune God who alone is the soul's true end.

## Biblical Foundation

While the term "Trinity" does not appear in Scripture, the doctrine is the Church's faithful synthesis of the entirety of biblical revelation. The Old Testament hints at plurality within the one God (the "Spirit of God" moving over the waters in Genesis 1:2; the plural "Let us make man" in Genesis 1:26; the "Angel of the Lord" who speaks as God). The New Testament reveals the full reality: the Father sends the Son (John 3:16-17); the Son reveals the Father (John 14:9) and promises the Spirit (John 14:16-17, 15:26); the Spirit glorifies the Son (John 16:14). The baptismal formula of Matthew 28:19 explicitly names the three Persons. The apostolic benediction of 2 Corinthians 13:14 invokes "the grace of the Lord Jesus Christ, and the love of God, and the communion of the Holy Ghost."
