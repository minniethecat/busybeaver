# Exodus

The foundational deliverance event of the Old Testament, in which God brought the people of Israel out of bondage in Egypt through mighty signs and wonders, parting the Red Sea and leading them toward the Promised Land under the leadership of Moses. The event stands as the paradigmatic act of divine salvation in the Old Testament and the great type of the redemption accomplished by Christ.

## Biblical Account

The exodus narrative spans the book of Exodus and is woven throughout the entire Old Testament as Israel's defining confession of faith. After four centuries of sojourn and enslavement in Egypt, God raised up Moses to confront Pharaoh and demand the release of His people. When Pharaoh refused, God sent ten plagues upon Egypt, culminating in the death of the firstborn — from which Israel was spared by the blood of the Passover lamb. Pharaoh relented, and Israel departed. But when Pharaoh's army pursued, God parted the Red Sea, allowing Israel to cross on dry ground while the pursuing Egyptians were drowned when the waters returned.

The event is memorialized in the Song of the Sea: "I will sing unto the Lord, for he hath triumphed gloriously: the horse and his rider hath he thrown into the sea" (Exodus 15:1). The psalmist repeatedly recalls the exodus as the supreme demonstration of God's saving power: "When Israel went out of Egypt, the house of Jacob from a people of strange language; Judah was his sanctuary, and Israel his dominion. The sea saw it, and fled: Jordan was driven back" (Psalm 114:1-3). The Deuteronomist declares: "The Lord brought us forth out of Egypt with a mighty hand, and with an outstretched arm, and with great terribleness, and with signs, and with wonders" (Deuteronomy 26:8).

The New Testament author of Hebrews names the exodus as an act of faith: "By faith they passed through the Red Sea as by dry land: which the Egyptians assaying to do were drowned" (Hebrews 11:29).

## Theological Significance

The exodus is the great type of deliverance in Christian theology. Just as Israel was delivered from physical bondage in Egypt by the blood of the Passover lamb and the passage through the Red Sea, so humanity is delivered from the bondage of sin by the blood of Christ, the true Passover Lamb, and the passage through the waters of Baptism. The exodus reveals that salvation is entirely God's initiative — Israel did not liberate itself; God acted. It further reveals the pattern of salvation: election, affliction, deliverance, covenant, and journey toward the Promised Land.

The typological reading is deeply embedded in Christian tradition: Egypt represents the world and its bondage to sin; Pharaoh represents the devil; Moses the lawgiver prefigures Christ the true Lawgiver; the Red Sea crossing is Baptism; the manna in the wilderness is the Eucharist. The exodus journey through the wilderness toward the Promised Land is the image of the Christian life — a pilgrimage marked by testing, provision, and hope.

## In The Imitation of Christ

In *The Imitation of Christ*, the exodus is not thematized at length as an independent subject, but it underlies the work's entire conception of the spiritual life as a journey of deliverance. The disciple, like Israel, is called out of the "Egypt" of worldly attachments and led through a wilderness of testing toward the heavenly homeland. Moses, the great figure of the exodus, is referenced as the exemplar of obedience to God's commands: "Moses did always whatsoever the Lord commanded him" (039-030).

The exodus pattern — departure from bondage, testing in the wilderness, and journey toward promise — maps onto the threefold way traced throughout *The Imitation*: purgation (departure from sin), illumination (instruction in the wilderness), and union (arrival at the heavenly country). Egypt represents the "world" that the disciple must learn to despise in order to follow Christ: "He that followeth Me walketh not in darkness, but shall have the light of life" (John 8:12).

## Liturgical Connection

The exodus is celebrated liturgically in the Easter Vigil, where the reading of Exodus 14 — Israel's passage through the Red Sea — is proclaimed as the great type of Christ's Passover from death to life. The *Exsultet* sings of the night when God "led our forefathers, the children of Israel, dry-shod through the Red Sea." This liturgical context reinforces the exodus as the Old Testament type most directly fulfilled in the Paschal Mystery.
