# Concupiscence

The inward inclination toward sin that remains in human nature after the Fall — a disordered desire of the lower appetites that draws the soul away from God and toward created goods. Concupiscence is not sin itself, but it is the fuel of temptation: the "law in the members" warring against the "law of the mind" (Romans 7:23). The disciple who understands concupiscence understands why vigilance must never cease.

## Definition

Concupiscence (Latin *concupiscentia*, from *concupiscere* — "to long for, to covet") is the tendency of fallen human nature to desire what is contrary to reason and to divine law. Christian theology distinguishes concupiscence from actual sin: it is the disordered condition from which sinful acts arise, not the acts themselves. Saint Paul describes the experience with painful clarity: "For I know that in me (that is, in my flesh) dwelleth no good thing: for to will is present with me; but how to perform that which is good I find not" (Romans 7:18). The flesh and the spirit are locked in perpetual combat (Galatians 5:17).

The First Epistle of John identifies three forms concupiscence takes in the world: "the lust of the flesh, and the lust of the eyes, and the pride of life" (1 John 2:16). These three concupiscences correspond to the threefold temptation of Eve in the garden (Genesis 3:6) and of Christ in the wilderness (Matthew 4:1-11). Concupiscence is the battlefield within — the interior disorder that makes every external temptation dangerous.

## In The Imitation of Christ

Thomas à Kempis treats concupiscence not as an abstract theological category but as the daily terrain of spiritual combat. The disciple is "born in concupiscence" (013-004) and therefore must never imagine himself free from the fire that smolders within.

### The Root of Temptation

*The Imitation* locates the origin of temptation not primarily in the devil or the world but in the unconquered desires of the heart. The disciple who is "so unguarded in thine outward senses, so often entangled in many vanities" (103-002) has not yet dealt with the root of concupiscence within. External renunciation is useless if the interior appetite remains unchallenged.

### The Remedy: Mortification

The only remedy for concupiscence, in Kempis's teaching, is continual mortification — the deliberate refusal to indulge the desires that lead away from God. The disciple must learn to "rest from inordinate desire of knowledge" (002-003) and from every attachment that competes with the love of God. Concupiscence is not eradicated in this life (this would be the heresy of perfectionism), but it can be progressively weakened through grace, self-denial, and perseverance in prayer.

## Biblical Foundation

The doctrine of concupiscence is rooted in the biblical contrast between flesh and spirit. Saint Paul's confession in Romans 7:18–23 is the classic text: "For the good that I would I do not: but the evil which I would not, that I do." Saint James traces the genealogy of sin: "Every man is tempted, when he is drawn away of his own lust, and enticed. Then when lust hath conceived, it bringeth forth sin" (James 1:14–15). The Council of Trent defined concupiscence as remaining in the baptized "for the sake of combat" — not as sin itself, but as the condition that makes the warfare of the Christian life necessary.

## The Warfare of Flesh and Spirit

Kempis locates the experience of concupiscence within the broader drama of the [[grace|spirit]]'s combat against the flesh — a warfare that defines the spiritual life in this present age. The text gives voice to the disciple's anguished self-knowledge: "Thus, O Lord, I am divided against myself, and am made unbearable to myself, while the spirit seeketh to be above and the flesh to be beneath" (085-037). This is not the language of someone who has resolved the conflict but of one who lives within it daily.

The saint's progress does not eliminate concupiscence but intensifies the combat. "He who doeth violence to his fleshly nature and crucifieth the lusts of the flesh by the fervour of his spirit" (085-065) discovers that the warfare itself — faithfully endured — becomes the means of purification. The [[grace|grace]] that assists this struggle works in direct opposition to fallen nature: "Grace studieth self-mortification, resisteth sensuality, seeketh to be subject" (091-014), while "Nature inclineth thee to created things, to thine own flesh, to vanities and distractions" (091-043).

The practical remedy is the daily discipline of self-denial. The text insists that "the true progress of man lieth in self-denial, and a man who denieth himself is free and safe" (076-014). Concupiscence is not reasoned away or ignored — it is mortified through the concrete refusal of the flesh's demands. The disciple must learn to "rest from inordinate desire of knowledge, for therein is found much distraction and deceit" (002-010). Every act of self-denial weakens concupiscence's grip; every indulgence strengthens it. This is the slow, patient work of a lifetime — not the eradication of desire but its gradual reordering toward God alone.
