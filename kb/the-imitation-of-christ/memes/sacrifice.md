# Sacrifice

The act of rendering something precious to God as an act of worship, atonement, thanksgiving, or supplication. In the fullness of Christian revelation, sacrifice finds its definitive expression in the self-offering of Jesus Christ on the Cross — the one perfect sacrifice that fulfills and supersedes all the offerings of the Old Covenant — made sacramentally present in the Eucharist, and extended into the lives of believers who offer themselves as "a living sacrifice" united to His.

## Definition

Sacrifice, in its biblical sense, is the ritual or spiritual offering of a gift to God, signifying dependence, gratitude, atonement for sin, and the desire for communion. The Old Testament prescribed a complex system of sacrifices — holocausts, sin offerings, peace offerings, grain offerings — detailed in the book of Leviticus and centered on the altar of the Temple in Jerusalem. These sacrifices, however, were provisional types pointing toward the one definitive sacrifice of Christ. The author of Hebrews declares that "it is not possible that the blood of bulls and of goats should take away sins" (Hebrews 10:4); rather, "we are sanctified through the offering of the body of Jesus Christ once for all" (Hebrews 10:10).

In the New Covenant, the external ritual is interiorized and perfected. The one sacrifice of Christ on Calvary is made present on the altar at every celebration of the Eucharist, and the faithful are called to unite their own sufferings, prayers, and works to that singular offering. Saint Paul exhorts: "I beseech you therefore, brethren, by the mercies of God, that ye present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy, acceptable unto God, which is your reasonable service" (Romans 12:1).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Sacrifice is a central theme in *The Imitation of Christ*, where it appears in three interconnected dimensions: the sacrifice of Christ on the Cross, the Eucharistic sacrifice, and the personal sacrifice of the disciple's self-offering.

### The Sacrifice of Christ

Thomas à Kempis presents the Cross as the supreme act of sacrificial love. "Jesus hath now many lovers of His heavenly kingdom, but few bearers of His Cross" (056-001). The Passion is the sacrifice by which salvation is accomplished, and the disciple is called not merely to admire it from a distance but to enter into it: to bear the cross daily, to accept suffering as a participation in Christ's own sacrificial offering. "If thou carry the cross willingly, it will carry thee, and bring thee to thy desired end" (056-020).

### The Eucharistic Sacrifice

The fourth book of *The Imitation*, devoted to the Blessed Sacrament, presents the Eucharist as the sacramental perpetuation of Christ's sacrifice. The Mass is the memorial in which "the health-giving Victim is offered" for the living and the dead. Thomas à Kempis exhorts the disciple to approach the altar with the dispositions of sacrifice — humility, contrition, and self-offering — for Christ declares: "Whatsoever thou givest besides thyself, I nothing care for, for I ask not thy gift, but thee" (104-010 to 104-011). "Offer thyself to Me, and give thyself altogether for God, so shall thy offering be accepted" (104-014 to 104-015). The sacrifice of the Mass is not merely something the priest performs; it is something in which the faithful participate by offering themselves together with Christ.

### The Disciple's Self-Offering

The most personal dimension of sacrifice in *The Imitation* is the call for the disciple to offer himself entirely to God. "In offering thyself with all thy heart to the Divine Will, in not seeking the things which are thine own, whether great or small, whether in time or in eternity" (062-025 to 062-026) — here is the essence of the Kempian teaching on sacrifice: the complete, unreserved gift of self to God.

The disciple's sacrifice is perfected in the prayer of oblation: "I desire to reserve nothing unto myself, but freely and entirely to offer myself and all that I have unto Thee for a sacrifice" (113-016 to 113-018). This self-gift unites the believer to the one sacrifice of Christ, for "unless they first entirely resign themselves and daily offer themselves up as a sacrifice; without this the union which bringeth forth fruit standeth not nor will stand" (074-022 to 074-024). The life of the disciple becomes an extension of the Cross — a living sacrifice, holy and acceptable to God.

## Biblical Foundation

The theme of sacrifice runs through the entire Bible. The Old Testament establishes the sacrificial system (Leviticus 1–7) and the prophets call Israel beyond ritual to interior sacrifice: "The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit: a broken and a contrite heart, O God, thou wilt not despise" (Psalm 51:17). The New Testament reveals Christ as the fulfillment: "Christ also hath loved us, and hath given himself for us an offering and a sacrifice to God for a sweetsmelling savour" (Ephesians 5:2). The faithful are constituted as "a holy priesthood, to offer up spiritual sacrifices, acceptable to God by Jesus Christ" (1 Peter 2:5).
