# Passion of Christ

The totality of Christ's suffering — from His agony in the Garden of Gethsemane, through His betrayal by Judas, His trial before the Sanhedrin and Pilate, His scourging at the pillar, His crowning with thorns, His carrying of the Cross along the Via Dolorosa, to His crucifixion and death on Golgotha — understood in The Imitation of Christ not merely as the central event of salvation history to be remembered, but as the living pattern to be interiorly embraced by every disciple who would follow the Crucified Lord.

## Biblical Account

The Passion narrative occupies a uniquely privileged place in all four Gospels, unfolding across nearly a third of each Evangelist's account. The sequence is fixed in the Church's memory: after the Last Supper and the institution of the Eucharist, Jesus withdraws with His disciples to the Garden of Gethsemane, where He enters into an agony of prayer so intense that "His sweat was as it were great drops of blood falling down to the ground" (Luke 22:44). Betrayed by Judas with a kiss, He is arrested by the Temple guard, abandoned by His disciples, and led bound to the house of the high priest.

Before the Sanhedrin, false witnesses are produced, and when the high priest demands, "Art Thou the Christ, the Son of the Blessed?" Jesus answers, "I am: and ye shall see the Son of man sitting on the right hand of power, and coming in the clouds of heaven" (Mark 14:61–62). Condemned for blasphemy, He is delivered to the Roman governor Pontius Pilate, who — though declaring "I find no fault in this man" (Luke 23:4) — capitulates to the crowd's demand for crucifixion. Jesus is scourged, robed in purple, crowned with thorns, and mocked as "King of the Jews." He carries His Cross to Golgotha, the Place of the Skull, where He is nailed to the wood and lifted up between two criminals.

From the Cross, Jesus speaks seven times: "Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do" (Luke 23:34); "Verily I say unto thee, Today shalt thou be with Me in paradise" (Luke 23:43); "Woman, behold thy son" (John 19:26); "My God, My God, why hast Thou forsaken Me?" (Matthew 27:46); "I thirst" (John 19:28); "It is finished" (John 19:30); and "Father, into Thy hands I commend My spirit" (Luke 23:46). At the moment of His death, the veil of the Temple is torn from top to bottom, the earth quakes, rocks split, and tombs open. The centurion standing guard declares: "Truly this was the Son of God" (Matthew 27:54). Joseph of Arimathea requests the body and lays it in a new tomb hewn from rock, and a great stone is rolled across the entrance.

The Passion is the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy and type. Isaiah foretold the Suffering Servant who "was oppressed, and He was afflicted, yet He opened not His mouth: He is brought as a lamb to the slaughter" (Isaiah 53:7). The psalmist prefigured the piercing of hands and feet (Psalm 22:16), the parting of garments (Psalm 22:18), and the gall and vinegar offered to the sufferer (Psalm 69:21). Jesus Himself understood His death as the accomplishment of the Scriptures, "that all things which are written may be fulfilled" (Luke 22:37).

## Theological Significance

The Passion is the center of Christian theology — the event in which divine justice and mercy meet, sin is expiated, and the way to eternal life is opened. On the Cross, Christ bears "our sins in His own body on the tree" (1 Peter 2:24), becoming "sin for us, who knew no sin; that we might be made the righteousness of God in Him" (2 Corinthians 5:21). The Passion is at once the supreme act of divine love — "Greater love hath no man than this, that a man lay down his life for his friends" (John 15:13) — and the definitive satisfaction for sin: "by one offering He hath perfected for ever them that are sanctified" (Hebrews 10:14).

The Passion is inseparable from the Incarnation and the Resurrection. As the Apostle writes, Christ "humbled Himself, and became obedient unto death, even the death of the Cross" (Philippians 2:8). This obedience reverses the disobedience of Adam: where the first Adam grasped at equality with God, the last Adam "emptied Himself, taking the form of a servant." The Crucifixion is the hinge of salvation history — the old covenant fulfilled, the new covenant inaugurated in Christ's blood, the barrier between God and humanity removed. The veil of the Temple torn from top to bottom signifies that through the flesh of Christ — "a new and living way" (Hebrews 10:20) — all may now approach the throne of grace.

## In The Imitation of Christ

The Passion of Christ pervades every book of The Imitation as its spiritual center. Thomas à Kempis does not attempt a narrative retelling of the Passion — the Gospel accounts are assumed — but instead draws out its existential implications for the disciple. The Passion is not something merely to be remembered, but something to be lived. It is the lens through which every aspect of the spiritual life is understood: suffering, self-denial, humility, obedience, love, and even the Eucharist.

### The Passion as the Pattern of Discipleship

The disciple is summoned to a continual mindfulness of Christ's Passion: "He must always remember the Passion of Christ" (101-030). This is not an optional devotion but the very substance of the spiritual life. "The religious man who exercises himself seriously and devoutly in the most holy life and passion of our Lord shall find there abundantly all things that are profitable and necessary for him" (025-058). The Passion is a compendium of all virtues: in it the disciple sees humility — Christ "despised and rejected of men" (026-043); obedience — "not My will, but Thine, be done" (Luke 22:42); patience — "as a sheep before her shearers is dumb, so He openeth not His mouth" (Isaiah 53:7); and love — "having loved His own which were in the world, He loved them unto the end" (John 13:1).

Thomas insists that the Passion must become the disciple's own: "He went before thee bearing His Cross and died for thee upon the Cross, that thou also mayest bear thy cross and mayest love to be crucified upon it" (037-018). The imitation of Christ is, at its core, the imitation of Christ crucified. The soul that seeks "rest and joy" apart from suffering has not understood the Gospel: "The whole life of Christ was a cross and martyrdom, and dost thou seek for thyself rest and joy?" (037-060). Christ "was willing to suffer and be despised" (026-045) — the disciple must will the same.

### The Wounds as Refuge

One of the most distinctive features of Thomas's treatment of the Passion is his invitation to the disciple to take refuge in Christ's sacred wounds. "Rest thou in the passion of Christ and dwell willingly in His sacred wounds. For if thou devoutly fly to the wounds of Jesus, and the precious marks of the nails and the spear, thou shalt find great comfort in tribulation" (026-038). This is not morbid fascination but the most intimate form of devotion: the wounds are the openings through which divine mercy pours forth, and the disciple enters them as a refuge from every tribulation. The physical reality of the Passion — the nails, the spear, the blood — is presented as the medicine of the soul.

### The Passion and the Eucharist

In Book IV, the Passion is intimately connected to the Sacrament of the Altar. Christ says: "As I of My own will offered Myself unto God the Father on the Cross for thy sins with outstretched hands and naked body, so that nothing remained in Me that did not become altogether a sacrifice for the divine propitiation; so also oughtest thou every day to offer thyself willingly unto Me for a pure and holy oblation" (104-003). The once-for-all sacrifice of Calvary is made present at every Mass; the disciple, in receiving the Eucharist, is united to that sacrifice and is himself offered with Christ. The priest celebrating is exhorted to "always remember the Passion of Christ" and to "diligently look upon Christ's footsteps" (101-030). The faithful communicant "calleth to mind the mystery of Christ's incarnation and His Passion, and is inflamed with the love of Him" (106-064).

### The Passion and Salvation

Thomas underscores that the Passion is the ground of all hope. Before Christ's Passion, even the just of the Old Covenant "were not able, before Thy Passion and the ransom of Thy holy Death, to enter the kingdom of heaven" (055-023). The gates of heaven, closed since the Fall, were opened by the Cross. The disciple responds with grateful self-offering: "Behold I commit myself to Thee... whom Thou hast redeemed with Thy precious blood" (105-027). Every grace received in this life, and the hope of glory in the next, flows from the Passion as from its sole and sufficient source.

## Liturgical Connection

The Passion is commemorated liturgically throughout Holy Week, culminating in the solemn observance of Good Friday. On Palm Sunday, the Church reads the Passion narrative according to one of the Synoptic Gospels; on Good Friday, the Passion according to St. John (John 18:1–19:42) is proclaimed. The Veneration of the Cross, the chanting of the Reproaches (Improperia), and the reception of Holy Communion from the reserved Sacrament are the distinctive rites of Good Friday. The entire Paschal Triduum — Maundy Thursday (the institution of the Eucharist and the priesthood), Good Friday (the Passion and death of the Lord), and the Easter Vigil (the Resurrection) — forms a single liturgical action centered on the Paschal Mystery.

Every celebration of the Eucharist makes present the one sacrifice of Calvary. As the Apostle writes, "as often as ye eat this bread, and drink this cup, ye do show the Lord's death till He come" (1 Corinthians 11:26). The Sign of the Cross — made at prayer, at blessing, in times of temptation and trial — is the continual remembrance of the Passion written upon the body of every Christian. In the traditional calendar, the Feast of the Exaltation of the Holy Cross (September 14) celebrates the Cross not as an instrument of shame but as the trophy of Christ's victory and the sign of the Church's hope.
