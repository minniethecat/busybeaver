# Heaven

Heaven is the eternal dwelling place of God and the ultimate destination of the faithful soul, portrayed throughout _The Imitation of Christ_ as both a future reward and a present spiritual orientation that reorders earthly desires toward their celestial end (001-023).

## Location

In the theological geography of Thomas a Kempis, Heaven is not a physical landmark but a transcendent spiritual realm beyond the created order. The author sets it in deliberate contrast with the visible, transitory world: "Behold the heaven and the earth and the elements" (020-061). Because Heaven lies beyond sensory apprehension, the earthly-minded soul "canst not behold high and heavenly things" (026-037), placing it in a sphere accessible only through detachment, humility, and divine grace.

## Biblical Significance

In Scripture, Heaven is the throne of God (Isaiah 66:1), the dwelling of the angels, and the promised inheritance of the redeemed (Matthew 5:12). Thomas a Kempis draws on this cosmic framework when he reminds the reader of the ontological chasm between Creator and creature: "remember that the heavens are not clean in thy sight" (051-005). The eschatological promise of Heaven as the New Jerusalem (Revelation 21) undergirds the entire devotional program — every renunciation of the world is made meaningful by the hope of the heavenly country.

### Key Events Associated with Heaven

- **The Sign of the Cross at the Last Judgment**: Thomas gives the Cross a cosmic, eschatological dimension: "the sign of the Cross shall be in heaven when the Lord cometh to judgment" (037-007). This image links the historical Passion to the future Parousia, making the Cross the bridge between earth and Heaven.
- **The Promise of Eternal Rest**: Heaven is the answer to the soul's deepest restlessness. The author asks, "What is the place of thy rest? In heaven ought thy habitation to be, and all earthly things are to be looked upon as it were in the passing of time" (026-032).

## In The Imitation of Christ

Across all four books, Heaven functions as the gravitational center toward which every spiritual discipline is oriented. The work opens with the call to press "forward to the heavenly kingdom" (001-023), and the adjective "heavenly" forms a sustained lexical pattern: "heavenly consolation" (020-036), "heavenly visitation" (034-035), "heavenly grace" (034-056), "heavenly comfort" (034-068), "heavenly glory" (035-036), "heavenly sweetness" (037-013), and "heavenly mysteries" (038-011). Every spiritual gift, in Thomas's vocabulary, originates in and returns to Heaven.

> "Jesus hath many lovers of His heavenly kingdom, but few bearers of His cross" (036-002). This sharp observation distills the central tension of the book: many desire the heavenly destination but few embrace the cruciform path that leads there.

The author deploys "paradise" as a synonym, echoing Christ's words to the penitent thief: "to be with Jesus is sweet paradise" (033-013). The Cross itself is presented as the sole gateway: "the cross leadeth to paradise" (093-039). The soul granted contemplative intimacy with God receives "a foretaste of the heavenly country" (043-016) even during earthly life, a proleptic experience of what awaits beyond death.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Christian tradition as distilled by _The Imitation_, Heaven carries a cluster of interrelated symbolic meanings:

- **True Homeland**: The soul's native country, of which earthly life is but an exile. The devout soul learns "to despise earthly things and to love heavenly" (041-043), not out of world-hatred but from a rightly ordered desire.
- **The Two Eternal Poles**: The author contrasts "the very depths of heaven and hell" (029-014) as the final, irreversible termini of human existence — each soul tending toward one or the other according to its inner disposition.
- **Inheritance of the Patient**: To suffer with Christ is to become "the inheritor of heaven" (028-033), linking the Beatitudes' promise to the daily endurance of trials.
- **Measure of True Value**: All created goods shrink before the divine plenitude: "nothing fuller or better in heaven or in earth" (042-027) than the love and peace that God alone bestows.
