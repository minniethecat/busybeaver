# Beggar

The **beggar** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the soul stripped of pretense — the self that knows it has nothing of its own and must receive everything as gift. Far from being a figure of degradation, the beggar in the *Gitanjali* is an image of spiritual honesty: the one who does not pretend to possess what can only be given.

## In Gitanjali

### The Beggar on the Roads

Yeats opens his Introduction with an image of Tagore's own self-presentation:

> ...beggar on the roads. When there was... (P01-101)

The poet himself is a "beggar on the roads" — wandering, dependent, asking. This is not a metaphor for poverty but for the spiritual posture: the poet does not claim to own the songs he sings. He received them, and he passes them on.

### The Beggar Maid

The image of the beggar appears twice in the poems, each time gendered female:

> I sit like a beggar maid, drawing my skirt over my face... (001-081, echoed at 002-203)

The "beggar maid" sits covered, veiled — partly in shame, partly in modesty. She draws her skirt over her face not to hide from the divine but to hide her unworthiness from herself. Yet she sits, and waits, and the divine comes to her not despite her poverty but because of it.

### The Beggar's Heart

> When my beggarly heart sits crouched, shut up... (002-181)

The "beggarly heart" is crouched, closed, defensive. It is beggarly not in poverty but in fear — too afraid to open, too aware of its own need to risk asking. The heart that knows itself as beggar may also be the heart that cannot bring itself to beg.

### The Beggar Girl at the Temple

> ...side this ragged beggar girl a-tremble with shame and... (003-005)

The beggar girl stands at the temple threshold, trembling. Her "shame" is both social (ragged, poor) and spiritual (unworthy to enter). But the poem does not send her away; it places her at the threshold, in the liminal space where the divine might notice her.

### The Palm Opened

> ...was it to open thy palm to a beggar to beg! I... (003-125)

The image reverses the expected relationship: the divine opens its palm TO a beggar. God begs from the beggar. This is the radical inversion at the heart of the *Gitanjali*'s theology: the divine is not only the giver but also the asker, and the human, in its poverty, has something God wants — its [[love]], its [[surrender]], its [[song]].

## Philosophical Background

In Indian religious culture, the beggar (*bhikshu*) is a sacred figure — the renunciant who has given up worldly possessions and lives by alms. Tagore recasts this figure in devotional terms: the soul is a beggar before God not because it has renounced the world but because it has recognized that everything — even existence itself — is received. The beggar's empty palm is the soul's true shape.
