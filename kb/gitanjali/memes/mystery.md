# Mystery

**Mystery** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is not the absence of knowledge but a positive presence — something that "grows," that expands, that makes the world infinitely hospitable to [[love]]. For Tagore, the divine is not hidden behind mystery; the divine is the mystery within which all things live, and to be opened into this mystery is to be made fully alive.

## In Gitanjali

### Mystery that Grows

Tagore's most distinctive insight into mystery is that it is dynamic, not static. It "grows" — it does not shrink as knowledge advances but expands as the soul deepens:

> …mystery that was growing through the… (P01-188)

Yeats saw in Tagore's poetry what he called "a world I have dreamed of all my life long" — a world suffused with a mystery that was not archaic or credulous but fully conscious, alive, and increasing. Tagore's mystery grows because the divine is not finite, and every encounter with the infinite enlarges rather than exhausts what remains unknown.

### Mystery and Love

Tagore joins mystery with love, seeing the divine not as a puzzle to be decoded by the intellect but as a lover whose ways remain hidden:

> …her heart in tender and silent mystery of love — the sweet… (004-116)

The "tender and silent mystery of love" suggests intimacy, not distance. Mystery here is not what separates lovers but what draws them closer — the inexhaustibility of the beloved that keeps love from becoming stale. Tagore's God is not an answer but a lover whose depths are never fully plumbed.

### Opened into the Vast Mystery

The poet's final disposition toward mystery is not resistance but surrender — being opened into it:

> …made me open out into this vast mystery like a… (006-109)

To be "opened out into this vast mystery" is Tagore's image of spiritual maturity. It is not about understanding the mystery (which would reduce it) but about becoming capacious enough to dwell within it. The soul does not solve the mystery; the mystery opens the soul.

## Philosophical Background

- **Upanishadic neti neti**: The famous formula "not this, not this" from the *Brihadaranyaka Upanishad* insists that the ultimate reality cannot be captured by any predicate. Tagore's mystery inherits this apophatic tradition but renders it positively — not as negation but as infinite expansion.
- **Bhakti's unknowable beloved**: In Bhakti traditions, the divine is known through love, not through philosophical analysis. The lover's knowledge is intimate but never exhaustive — exactly Tagore's "tender and silent mystery of love."
- **Yeats and the Celtic twilight**: Yeats's Introduction to the *Gitanjali* reads Tagore's mystery through his own preoccupation with the Celtic otherworld — but where Yeats's mystery is nostalgic for a lost past, Tagore's is alive and growing in the present.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Growing mystery | "mystery that was growing" | Mystery as dynamic, not residual |
| Intimate mystery | "tender and silent mystery of love" | Mystery as depth of beloved, not distance |
| Vastness | "vast mystery" | Mystery as spacious rather than narrow |
| Opening out | "made me open out into this vast mystery" | Surrender as enlargement |
