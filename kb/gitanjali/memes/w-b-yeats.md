# W. B. Yeats

**William Butler Yeats** (13 June 1865 – 28 January 1939) was an Irish poet, playwright, and senator, widely regarded as one of the greatest poets of the twentieth century. He won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923, a decade after [[rabindranath-tagore|Rabindranath Tagore]] received the same honor in 1913. The two Nobel laureates are linked by Yeats's celebrated Introduction to Tagore's *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]*, in which Yeats declared that Tagore's prose translations "have stirred my blood as nothing has for years" (P01-015–016). This Introduction played a crucial role in bringing Indian devotional poetry to the attention of Western literary culture.

## The Gitanjali Introduction

### Context of the Meeting

In the summer of 1912, Tagore traveled to England carrying a notebook of his own English prose translations of his Bengali poems. The manuscript reached Yeats through the painter William Rothenstein, to whom the 1913 Macmillan edition of *Gitanjali* is dedicated (P01-007–009).

Yeats was deeply moved by the poems. He later described carrying the manuscript everywhere:

> I have carried the manuscript of these translations about with me for days, reading it in railway trains, or on the top of omnibuses and in restaurants. (P01-085–087)

This image of the Irish poet reading Tagore's devotional songs on London omnibuses captures the improbable cultural encounter that the *Gitanjali* represents — a meeting of the Celtic Twilight and the Bengali Renaissance, mediated by the English language.

### Key Themes of the Introduction

Yeats's Introduction to *Gitanjali* is not merely a preface but a critical appreciation that shaped the reception of Tagore's work in the West. Several themes emerge:

**The shock of unfamiliar beauty.** Yeats opens with a dialogue in which he asks a Bengali doctor to tell him about Tagore's life and thought, confessing his own ignorance of Indian culture:

> I know no German, yet if a translation of a German poet had moved me, I would go to the British Museum and find books in English that would tell me something of his life, and of the history of his thought. (P01-012–014)

The doctor's response — "We have other poets, but none that are his equal; we call this the epoch of Rabindranath" (P01-027–028) — establishes Tagore's stature in his own culture, a stature Yeats then validates for the Anglophone reader.

**The unity of poetry and religion.** Yeats identified as the distinctive quality of Tagore's work a cultural condition in which the sacred and the aesthetic had not yet been separated:

> A tradition, where poetry and religion are the same thing, has passed through the centuries, gathering from learned and unlearned metaphor and emotion. (P01-093–095)

This observation — that Tagore spoke from a culture where the poetic and the spiritual were one — resonated with Yeats's own lifelong preoccupation with the occult, mysticism, and the symbolic imagination. The Yeats who had joined the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn and compiled *The Celtic Twilight* (1893) recognized in Tagore a kindred sensibility.

**The saint who has not refused to live.** Yeats's most famous formulation about Tagore is also his most theologically acute:

> He is the first among our saints who has not refused to live, but has spoken out of Life itself. (P01-041–042)

This phrase distinguishes Tagore from ascetic traditions that renounce the world. Tagore's sanctity, in Yeats's reading, is a sanctity that embraces the sensuous — [[light]], [[love]], music, the natural world — rather than rejecting it. This insight has shaped the reception of Tagore's spirituality ever since.

### Comparative Parallels

Yeats drew parallels between the *Gitanjali* and the writings of Christian mystics:

> What have we in common with St. Bernard covering his eyes that they may not dwell upon the beauty of the lakes of Switzerland, or with the violent rhetoric of the Book of Revelations? (P01-140–142)

He also invoked Thomas à Kempis and John of the Cross (P01-150), placing Tagore within a lineage of contemplative writing that transcends confessional boundaries. This blending of the personal and the universal — what might be described as "the language of the personal made universal" — is the quality Yeats found most moving in Tagore's hymns.

## Literary Career

Beyond his role in the Tagore story, Yeats was a founding figure of modern Irish literature. He co-founded the Abbey Theatre in Dublin (1904), served as a senator of the Irish Free State (1922–1928), and produced a body of poetry that includes "The Second Coming," "Sailing to Byzantium," "Easter 1916," and "Among School Children." His work evolved from the dreamy Pre-Raphaelite manner of his early collections through the austere modernism of his later years — a trajectory that parallels, in its own way, the deepening of Tagore's art that Yeats himself noted.

| Period | Key Works | Style |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Early (1889–1904) | *The Wanderings of Oisin*, *The Celtic Twilight* | Romantic, mythological, Pre-Raphaelite |
| Middle (1904–1919) | *Responsibilities*, *The Wild Swans at Coole* | Austere, personal, politically engaged |
| Late (1919–1939) | *The Tower*, *The Winding Stair* | Modernist, symbolic, visionary |

## The Tagore-Yeats Relationship

The relationship between Yeats and Tagore was intense but short-lived. After the initial enthusiasm of 1912–1913, Yeats grew cooler toward Tagore's later English translations, finding them less compelling than the *Gitanjali*. Tagore, for his part, was wounded by what he perceived as Yeats's withdrawal. Nevertheless, their brief collaboration produced one of the most consequential literary introductions of the twentieth century — a document that not only launched Tagore's international career but also articulated, with uncanny precision, the spiritual and aesthetic principles that make the *Gitanjali* an enduring work of world literature.
