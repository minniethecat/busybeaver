# Sea

The **sea** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the image of boundlessness — the water without a farther shore, the deep that receives the [[river]], the expanse across which the soul must travel to reach the divine. The sea is at once beautiful and terrible, inviting and annihilating, the element in which the finite self learns to surrender its edges.

## In Gitanjali

### Across the Sea

The sea marks distance — the space the divine call crosses to reach the hearer:

> ...across the sea. (001-018)

The divine voice comes "across the sea" — from the far side, from the other shore, from the place the speaker cannot reach on his own. The sea is the obstacle that only the divine call can traverse. The speaker hears but cannot cross; the voice crosses but cannot be seen.

### The Shoreless Sea

The sea at its most overwhelming appears as a "shoreless" expanse:

> ...becomes an endless toil in a shoreless sea of toil. (001-050)

"Shoreless" — without boundary, without landing, without rest. The sea without a shore is labor without end, a "sea of toil" from which there is no exit. This is the sea's negative aspect: the infinite experienced as exhaustion rather than freedom.

### The Seashore of Worlds

The sea's edge — the shore — is the meeting place of children and worlds:

> ...play on the seashore of worlds. They know not how... (P01-194)

The "seashore of worlds" is the threshold where the finite touches the infinite. Children play there — unknowing, absorbed, at home on the boundary. The sea is not feared by them; it is the backdrop of their play, as natural as the sand they sit on.

### The Inmost Shrine

The divine has its seat not in the temple but at the sea's inmost point:

> ...knowing that thou hast thy seat in the inmost shrine of my... (001-042, sea-implicated context)

The divine dwells not on the far shore but in the "inmost shrine" — the deepest interior, the sea within. The outer sea and the inner sea reflect each other. To cross the sea is to go inward; to reach the far shore is to arrive at the heart.

### The Ocean-Cradle

The sea is also the place of origin — the "ocean-cradle of birth":

> ...life that is rocked in the ocean-cradle of birth and of... (005-025)

The ocean is a "cradle" — rocking, holding, nurturing. Birth is an oceanic event: the soul emerges from the unbounded into the bounded, from the sea into the [[river]], from the infinite into the finite form. And death is the return — dipping "my emptied life into that ocean" (006-026).

## The Sea in Bengal's Imagination

For a Bengali poet, the sea is the Bay of Bengal — the great water to the south, the destination of the Ganges, the horizon of the riverine world. But Tagore's sea is also the universal sea of mystical poetry — the "shoreless ocean" of the Upanishads (*bhuma*, the infinite), the "ocean of light" (006-122) into which the blessed soul is absorbed.
