# Servant

The **servant** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the soul that has chosen to belong to another — not through coercion but through love. The servant's waiting, the servant's readiness, the servant's presence at the door: these are images of the spiritual life as service freely given. The servant is not a biographical figure but a devotional archetype, one that runs through both the Bhakti tradition and the *Gitanjali*'s spiritual vocabulary.

## In Gitanjali

### The Servant Awaiting

Alongside the bride, Yeats names the servant as a figure of devotional waiting:

> ...her royal lover, the servant or the bride awaiting the master's... (P01-122)

The "servant" and the "bride" are paired — two images of the soul oriented toward the divine. The servant awaits the master as the bride awaits the bridegroom. The waiting is the same; only the name of the relationship differs. Service and love converge in the posture of expectant attention.

Yeats's grouping is deliberate. He is describing the emotional register of the *Gitanjali* — its combination of intimacy and reverence, its refusal to choose between the language of love and the language of service. The servant and the bride are not alternatives but complements: the soul serves because it loves, and loves because it serves. The "royal lover" stands between them, sharing the servant's humility and the bride's desire.

### Death, the Servant

In a striking image, death itself appears as a servant:

> Death, thy servant, is at my door. He has crossed... (006-007)

Death is not an enemy or a terror but "thy servant" — the divine's own messenger, sent on an errand. If death is a servant, then death serves the same master the soul serves. The fear of death is transfigured into recognition: the servant at the door is not a threat but a summons, and the summoner is the one the soul already serves.

The phrase "thy servant" is crucial: death belongs to God, not to the world. Death's authority is delegated, not inherent. The servant of the divine cannot harm the servant of the divine; both answer to the same household. This mutual recognition — "I am a servant, and death is a servant" — dissolves the terror of mortality into the peace of shared belonging.

### Service as Freedom

The paradox of the servant figure is that service becomes freedom. To belong entirely to another is to be released from the burden of self-direction. The servant does not need to decide what to do; the servant's entire existence is shaped by the master's will, and that shaping is liberation, not bondage. In the *Gitanjali*'s terms, the servant who waits at the door is more free than the self-sufficient person who has no master — because the servant's life has a center outside itself.

## The Bhakti Context

In the Bhakti tradition, *dasya-bhava* (the attitude of servitude toward God) is one of the five primary devotional moods. The classic example is Hanuman, whose devotion to Rama took the form of perfect service — carrying messages, building bridges, locating the lost. Service in this tradition is not demeaning; it is the natural expression of love for one who is infinitely greater than oneself.

Tagore's servant is quieter than Hanuman — less heroic, more domestic. The servant waits at the door; the servant recognizes death as a fellow-servant. The grandeur of divine service is found in the ordinary posture of availability. Where Hanuman leaps across oceans, Tagore's servant simply waits — and waiting, in the *Gitanjali*'s universe, is as powerful an act as any deed.

The *dasya-bhava* appears elsewhere in the *Gitanjali*'s spiritual vocabulary. When the speaker asks to be made into a flute for the divine to play through, or when he offers his life as a vessel for the divine will, he is speaking in the servant's voice. Service and surrender are inseparable: to serve is to give up the claim to autonomous action, and that surrender is itself the deepest act of devotion.

## Connection to Gitanjali

The servant in the *Gitanjali* is not a separate character but a posture the speaker assumes. When the poet speaks of waiting at the door, of belonging to the divine, of entering into a relationship defined by service rather than equality, he speaks as the servant. This is not self-abasement but self-definition: to know who you serve is to know who you are.

The servant's relationship to the master parallels the poet's relationship to the divine source of inspiration. The poet does not create autonomously; the poet receives, waits, makes himself available. The act of writing becomes an act of service — the poem arrives when the master summons, and the poet's role is readiness.

## Legacy

Tagore's servant figure has resonated with later spiritual writers who sought to articulate a relationship with the divine that is neither transactional nor egalitarian but grounded in love expressed as service. The image has particular force in a modern context where service is often coded as degradation: Tagore reclaims it as the highest form of dignity, a choice that defines the chooser rather than diminishing them.
