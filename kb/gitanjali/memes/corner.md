# Corner

The **corner** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the architecture of intimacy — the "dark corner" where a lamp burns, the "lonely dark corner of a [[temple]]" where silent worship unfolds, the corner where the singer sits alone, the hiding place the soul crouches in before being found. The corner in the *Gitanjali* is not marginal but central: it is where the most important encounters happen, the small space that holds the great presence.

## In Gitanjali

### The Lamp in the Dark Corner

> ...the lamp that lit my dark corner is out. A summons has come... (P01-148)

"The lamp that lit my dark corner is out" — the corner is dark, dependent on a lamp for its light. The extinguishing of the lamp is a summons, a call to leave the corner. The dark corner is the place of the soul's interiority, the private space lit by whatever small flame one tends. When the lamp goes out, one must step into a larger light.

### The Lonely Dark Corner of a Temple

> ...worship in this lonely dark corner of a temple with doors all shut... (001-099)

The "lonely dark corner" of the temple — the most intimate space within the most sacred architecture. Worship does not happen in the bright center of the temple but in its "lonely dark corner," behind shut doors. The corner is the place of private devotion, the space where the soul meets the divine without audience or display.

### A Corner in This Hall

> ...in this hall of thine I have a corner... (001-150)

"I have a corner" — the poet claims a small space within the vast "hall" of the divine. The corner is the portion allotted to the individual soul, the modest place one can call one's own within the infinite mansion. To have a corner is to belong, to have a place, however small, in the divine household.

### Quiet and Meek in a Corner

> ...in a corner and they sat quiet and meek... (002-123)

"In a corner and they sat quiet and meek" — the corner is the place of humility, of withdrawal. Those who sit in the corner are not asserting themselves; they are waiting, watching, being small. The corner is the architecture of modesty, the spatial form of the soul's self-effacement before the divine.

### Crouched in a Corner

> ...it sits crouched, shut up in a corner, break open... (002-181)

"Sits crouched, shut up in a corner" — the corner as prison, as hiding place. The soul that crouches in the corner has shut itself away, closed itself in. "Break open" — the divine must force entry into the corner where the soul hides. The corner is both refuge and cage, the place of safety that has become a place of confinement.

### Singing Alone in a Corner

> ...I was singing all alone in a corner, and the melody caught your ear... (003-108)

"Singing all alone in a corner" — the singer is hidden, private, unassuming. And yet the melody "caught your ear" — the divine hears the song from the corner. The corner is the place where the most private expression becomes the most public, where what is done in hiding is heard by the one who matters most.

### Weeping in Corners

> ...for me waiting and weeping in corners, no more coyness... (004-002)

"Weeping in corners" — the corner is the place of hidden grief, of tears shed in private. The one who waits and weeps in corners does so unseen, unconsoled. But the poem's "no more coyness" signals an end to corner-weeping: the time for hidden sorrow is over; the divine presence calls the mourner out of the corner into open encounter.

### Search in All Corners

> ...and search for her in all the corners of my... (006-017)

"Search for her in all the corners" — the corners are the places of hiddenness, of concealment. The divine searches every corner, leaving no hiding place unvisited. The corner is where one goes to disappear, but the divine seeker knows every corner and will search them all.

## Corner and Interiority

In the *Gitanjali*, the corner is the spatial image of the soul's interior life — the private, hidden, intimate space where one prays, sings, weeps, and hides. Tagore's corners are the dark corners of temples, the corners of halls, the corners where lamps burn and go out. They are the architecture of inwardness, the places where the self is most itself. And they are always also the places where the divine seeks and finds — the corner cannot hide the soul from the one who knows all corners.
