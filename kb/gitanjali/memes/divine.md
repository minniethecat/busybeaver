# Divine

**The divine** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is not a distant metaphysical abstraction but an intimate presence — addressed as "my God," "my lord," "my poet," and "my friend." Tagore's divine is simultaneously the creator who drinks from the overflowing cup of life and the beloved who waits at the end of every path. The relationship is one of radical intimacy: the divine gives itself to the soul and then tastes its own sweetness in the soul's response.

## In Gitanjali

### The Overflowing Cup

Tagore's most striking image of the divine-human relationship comes in poem 65:

> What divine drink wouldst thou have, my God, from this overflowing cup of my life? (004-166)

The question is bold — the poet does not ask what he may receive from God but what God desires from him. "This overflowing cup of my life" positions the poet's existence as an offering already full, already abundant. The divine is not a source of lack to be petitioned but a recipient who drinks from the soul's plenty. The word "divine" here qualifies the drink itself — life offered back to its giver becomes sacred.

The poem continues: "My poet, is it thy delight to see thy creation through my eyes and to stand at the portals of my ears silently to listen to thine own eternal harmony?" (004-168). The divine is imagined as an artist who experiences his own creation vicariously through the human soul — God seeing through human eyes, hearing through human ears. This is radical intimacy: the creator and the creature become collaborators in the ongoing act of creation.

### The Wings of Vishnu

In poem 53, the divine appears in a different register — not as the intimate drinker but as the wielder of cosmic power:

> ...thy sword with its curve of lightning like the outspread wings of the divine bird of Vishnu, perfectly poised in the angry red light of the sunset. (004-008)

The "divine bird of Vishnu" evokes Garuda, the mythic eagle who carries the preserver-god across the cosmos. The sword's curve is compared to wings outspread, poised at the moment before striking. This is the divine as terrifying beauty — "wrought with uttermost beauty, terrible to behold" (004-013). Tagore holds together two aspects of the divine: the intimate friend who drinks from the soul's cup and the cosmic lord whose sword flashes like lightning.

### The Common Mind

Yeats, in his Introduction, discerns a distinctive feature of Tagore's religious world:

> ...one divines — runs through all, is not, as with us, broken into a dozen minds that know nothing of each other... (P01-098)

"One divines" — the verb is carefully chosen. The unity of Indian civilization is not proven but perceived, felt, intuited. It "runs through all" like an underground river connecting disparate surfaces. Yeats contrasts this with the fragmentation of Western consciousness, "broken into a dozen minds." The divine here is the unifying principle, the common mind that holds a civilization together — not a doctrinal proposition but a shared mode of perception.

### Divine Service

The Introduction also records a telling anecdote. Tagore was to "read divine service in one of our churches" in Calcutta (P01-044) — the Brahma Samaj using the English word "church" for its own gathering place. The phrase "divine service" captures the paradox of Tagore's spirituality: it is service (active, communal, embodied) and divine (transcendent, sacred, oriented toward God). The church was "crowded" and "the streets were all but impassable because of the people" (P01-046) — the divine draws crowds not through doctrine but through the presence of a voice that speaks from life itself.

## Philosophical Background

- **Upanishadic non-duality**: The Upanishads teach *tat tvam asi* — "that thou art" — the identity of the individual soul (*atman*) with the universal ground (*Brahman*). Tagore's divine is filtered through this tradition: God is not wholly other but the innermost self of all beings, accessible through love rather than logic.
- **Bhakti personalism**: The bhakti movement transformed the abstract Brahman of the philosophers into a personal deity who can be loved, addressed, and even argued with. Tagore inherits this personalist revolution — his divine is "thou," not "it."
- **Brahma Samaj universalism**: Tagore's family led the Brahma Samaj, a reform movement that rejected idol worship and caste hierarchy while emphasizing a universal, non-sectarian divinity. The "divine service" in the Calcutta church represents this universalist impulse — a Hinduism opened to the world.
- **The poet-theologian**: Tagore's distinctive contribution is to make poetry the primary mode of theological reflection. The divine is not proven but sung; not argued but offered. "My poet, is it thy delight to see thy creation through my eyes?" — God is himself the original poet, and human poetry is participation in divine creativity.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Inverted petition | "What divine drink wouldst thou have, my God, from this overflowing cup of my life?" | The human offers to God; the flow of gift is reversed |
| Mythic imagery | "the outspread wings of the divine bird of Vishnu" | Cosmic scale — the divine as power and beauty |
| Vicarious intimacy | "see thy creation through my eyes... listen to thine own eternal harmony" | God and human as collaborators in perception |
| Intuited unity | "one divines — runs through all" | The divine as unifying undercurrent rather than doctrinal proposition |
