# Doubt

## Overview

In Tagore's Gitanjali, *doubt* appears not as a purely negative state but as a liminal moment — a pause before surrender, a hesitation that reveals the depth of what is at stake. Tagore treats doubt with characteristic gentleness, neither condemning it as faithlessness nor celebrating it as intellectual rigour, but situating it within the arc of the soul's movement toward love. The concept is explored through two contrasting modes: the inward self-doubt of the seeker and the childlike absence of doubt in the state of innocent communion.

## In Gitanjali

Doubt surfaces in several significant moments within the Gitanjali poems, forming a spectrum from self-questioning to the loss of primal certainty:

### Self-Doubt and Acceptance

In poem 1, the speaker reflects on the criticism directed at him, and rather than defend himself, accepts the possibility that his accusers may be right:

> People blame me and call me heedless; I doubt not they are right in their blame. (001-171)

The line that follows — "I am only waiting for love to give myself up at last into his hands" (001-173) — clarifies the speaker's perspective. His unconcern with self-justification stems not from indifference but from a deeper orientation: all human judgment is provisional when measured against the anticipated union with the divine beloved. Doubt here is absorbed into a larger faith.

### The Question and the Assurance

A related passage in poem 1 traces the transformation of doubt from anguished searching into certitude:

> The question and the cry "Oh, where?" melt into tears of a thousand streams and deluge the world with the flood of the assurance "I am!" (001-124–125)

The movement from question to assurance — from "Oh, where?" to "I am!" — enacts the Bhakti logic of doubt's dissolution. Doubt is not refuted but *melted*; the verb suggests a softening, a yielding, rather than a confrontation. The image of the flood connects this personal transformation to cosmic scale — the individual's doubt resolved into a universal affirmation.

### The Doubtful Maiden

In poem 64, the speaker encounters a maiden carrying a lamp at dusk and asks to borrow her light for his dark house:

> She raised her dark eyes on my face and stood for a moment doubtful. "I have come," she said at last, "to dedicate my lamp to the sky." (004-155–157)

Her moment of doubt — a hesitation between human need and divine dedication — resolves into a quiet declaration of purpose. The lamp is not for earthly dwellings but for the sky itself. This passage enacts doubt as the brief interval before vocation is reaffirmed.

### The Absence of Doubt — Innocent Communion

In poem 87, Tagore provides the counter-image to the doubtful seeker: the child who plays with the divine without self-consciousness:

> When my play was with thee I never questioned who thou wert. I knew nor shyness nor fear, my life was boisterous. (006-129–130)

The speaker recalls a state of Edenic intimacy — "I never questioned who thou wert" — in which doubt had no entry because the relationship was immediate and sensory. He did not "care to know the meaning of songs" (006-133); his voice simply "took up the tunes, and my heart danced in their cadence" (006-134). This passage stands as the foil to the poem 64 maiden: the child's unselfconscious absorption versus the maiden's conscious pause. Together they map doubt's entry into the spiritual life as a function of awakening self-awareness.

### Doubt as Prelude to Surrender

A distinctive feature of Tagore's treatment is that doubt is never final. In poem 1 the speaker's "I doubt not" (001-171) is not the elimination of doubt but its repositioning — the doubt is about the *self*, not about the beloved. The question "Oh, where?" (001-124) becomes the assurance "I am!" (001-125). The maiden's "doubtful" (004-156) pause lasts only a moment. Doubt in Gitanjali is a passage, never a destination.

## Philosophical Background

Indian philosophical traditions offer varied perspectives on doubt (*samsaya*). In classical Vedanta, doubt is recognised as an obstacle to knowledge (*jnana*) that must be resolved through inquiry (*vichara*). The Nyaya school treats doubt as a distinct cognitive state arising from conflicting evidence, resolvable only through systematic reasoning. The Bhakti tradition, by contrast, often transfigures doubt into an intensifier of devotion — the lover's uncertainty heightens the sweetness of eventual union.

Tagore's treatment aligns more closely with the Bhakti sensibility: doubt is a temporary cloud, not a permanent darkness. Yet his approach is distinctively modern in its psychological subtlety. He does not condemn the doubter, nor does he valorise doubt as intellectual virtue. Instead, he traces its natural arc in the spiritual life — from the child's pre-reflective certainty, through the seeker's self-questioning, to the lover's absorption of doubt into a larger faith.

## Poetic Expression

Tagore renders doubt not through argument but through gesture — a maiden's momentary stillness, a speaker's quiet concession. The "doubtful" pause (004-156) is brief, measured in the space of a single sentence, and resolves without drama. The speaker's "I doubt not" (001-171) is less a logical conclusion than an emotional posture. The "question and the cry" (001-124) melt rather than answer.

The structural contrast between poem 87's "I never questioned" and poem 64's "stood for a moment doubtful" is itself a poetic argument: doubt enters the spiritual life not as intellectual failure but as the shadow cast by growing self-awareness. Tagore's imagery keeps the treatment sensory — a lamp at dusk, a dark-eyed maiden, a child running from glade to glade — so that doubt never becomes abstract. It remains a matter of the body's relation to the world.
