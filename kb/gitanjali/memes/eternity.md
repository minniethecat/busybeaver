# Eternity

In [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]*, **eternity** is not an infinite extension of time but a different order of reality that intersects with time at specific moments. The "signet of eternity" is pressed upon "fleeting moments," transforming them without destroying their transience. Eternity is a brink, a threshold — the point at which "nothing can vanish" and the soul confronts what is deathless.

## In Gitanjali

### The Signet of Eternity

Eternity marks the transient without making it permanent:

> Entering my heart unbidden even as one of the common crowd, unknown to me, my king, thou didst press the signet of eternity upon many a fleeting moment. (P01-159, 003-026)

"Thou didst press the signet of eternity upon many a fleeting moment" — this is Tagore's central image for the relationship between eternity and time. A signet is a seal that leaves an impression; it marks without replacing. The "fleeting moment" does not become eternal — it remains fleeting — but it now bears the imprint of eternity. Eternity is not the abolition of time but its transfiguration. The divine enters "unbidden," "as one of the common crowd" — eternity does not announce itself dramatically but infiltrates ordinary life incognito.

W. B. Yeats, in his introduction to Gitanjali, singled out this passage as the essence of Tagore's vision: "This is no longer the sanctity of the cell and of the scourge; being but a lifting up, as it were, into a greater intensity of the mood of the painter, painting the dust and the sunlight." Eternity is not ascetic withdrawal but an intensification of the ordinary — dust and sunlight rendered luminous.

### The Brink of Eternity

Eternity is a threshold, not a destination:

> I have come to the brink of eternity from which nothing can vanish — no hope, no happiness, no vision of a face seen through tears. (006-024)

"The brink of eternity" — the speaker has not entered eternity but arrived at its edge. "From which nothing can vanish" — in eternity, nothing is lost. Hopes, happiness, visions seen through tears — all that time erodes is preserved on this brink. The "brink" image is crucial: eternity is not elsewhere but at the edge of the present moment, accessible from where the speaker already stands. The passage continues: "Oh, dip my emptied life into that ocean, plunge it into the deepest fullness." The brink is not the end; it is the place from which one can plunge into the ocean of fullness.

### The Double Appearance

The signet of eternity image appears twice in the corpus — once in Yeats's introduction (P01-159) and once in the poems themselves (003-026). This doubling is significant: Yeats chose this particular passage to represent Tagore's achievement, and the poem itself confirms that the image is central, not incidental. Eternity leaving its mark on the transient — this is as close to a definition of Tagore's spiritual aesthetic as Gitanjali offers.

### Melodies Eternally New

Eternity renews without repeating:

> This little flute of a reed thou hast carried over hills and dales, and hast breathed through it melodies eternally new. (001-008)

"Melodies eternally new" — the eternal is not the static or the frozen but the ever-fresh. The "little flute of a reed" is the human soul, a simple instrument carried by the divine across landscapes ("over hills and dales"). The divine breathes through it, and what emerges is always new yet always from the same source. Eternity is not monotonous permanence but infinite novelty grounded in unchanging love. "At the immortal touch of thy hands my little heart loses its limits" — the eternal touch does not freeze the heart but expands it beyond all limits.

### The Eternal Harmony

Eternity is an ongoing music that the divine listens to through the human:

> My poet, is it thy delight to see thy creation through my eyes and to stand at the portals of my ears silently to listen to thine own eternal harmony? (004-169)

"Thine own eternal harmony" — the divine creates and then experiences its own creation through human senses. The "portals of my ears" are thresholds where the eternal harmony becomes audible. This is a remarkable vision: eternity is not elsewhere but is sounding now, and the human role is to be the ear through which the divine hears its own music. "Thy world is weaving words in my mind" — the eternal harmony takes form as language, as poetry, as Gitanjali itself.

## Philosophical Background

- **Vedantic nitya and lila**: In Vedanta, *nitya* (the eternal) is opposed not to time but to *anitya* (the impermanent). The eternal is what is real; the impermanent is what passes. But Tagore does not denigrate the impermanent. He sees the eternal as pressing its seal *upon* the fleeting — the two orders are in contact, not opposition. This reflects the bhakti vision of *lila* (divine play): the eternal plays in time, leaving its traces everywhere.
- **Kierkegaard's "moment"**: Søren Kierkegaard described the "moment" (*Øieblikket*) as the point where eternity touches time — the instant of decision, of faith, of encounter with the absolute. Tagore's "fleeting moment" bearing the signet of eternity is remarkably close: both see the intersection of time and eternity not in some distant heaven but in the concrete now.
- **Eliot's "still point"**: T. S. Eliot, influenced by similar Indian sources, wrote of "the still point of the turning world" — where time and timelessness intersect. Tagore's "brink of eternity" is this same still point: a threshold where motion and stillness meet, where nothing can vanish.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Signet metaphor | "press the signet of eternity upon many a fleeting moment" | Eternity marks time without abolishing it, like a seal on wax |
| Incognito entry | "entering my heart unbidden... unknown to me" | Eternity arrives quietly, unannounced, in ordinary guise |
| Brink/threshold imagery | "come to the brink of eternity" | Eternity is accessible from where one already stands |
| Ocean of fullness | "dip my emptied life into that ocean" | Eternity as plenitude into which the emptied self plunges |
| Negative definition | "from which nothing can vanish" | Eternity defined by what it preserves, not by what it is |
