# Sky

The **sky** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the immense above — the "ever-wakeful blue," the overcast dome of grief, the infinite space through which music travels "from sky to sky." More than any other element of the physical world, the sky in the *Gitanjali* images the unbounded, the transcendent, the open space that is at once a physical reality above [[bengal]]'s landscape and a figure for the infinite that surrounds and penetrates the human soul.

## In Gitanjali

### From Sky to Sky

> ...music runs from sky to sky. The holy stream of th... (001-029)

Music moves "from sky to sky" — the sky is plural, layered, a series of vastnesses through which sound travels without obstruction. The "holy stream" that follows suggests the river, the [[ganges]], the sacred water that flows between earth and heaven. Sky and river mirror each other: one above, one below, both infinite in their reach.

### The Gift of Sky

> ...thou gavest to me unasked — this sky and the light, this body and... (001-142)

The sky is a gift — "gavest to me unasked." It arrives without request, pure grace. Listed alongside "light" and "body," the sky is one of the primary gifts, the fundamental conditions of existence. The poet's gratitude extends not just to the extraordinary but to the elemental: the sky overhead, given anew each day.

### The Far-Away Gloom

> ...on the far-away gloom of the sky, and my heart wanders... (001-183)

The "far-away gloom" of the sky draws the heart into wandering. The sky is a space of projection: what the poet sees in its distant darkness is what the heart sends there. The sky-gazing heart is the wandering heart, the unanchored heart, the heart that seeks in the infinite what it cannot find in the near.

### The Ever-Wakeful Blue

> ...ever-wakeful blue sky... (002-016)

"Ever-wakeful" — the sky does not sleep. It is the constant watcher, the unsleeping presence above the sleeping world. The blue sky of day is consciousness itself spread across the dome of the world, an image of the divine awareness that never closes its eye.

### The Groaning Sky

> The sky groans like one in despair... (002-023)

The sky "groans like one in despair" — the cosmic becomes personal. The sky is not indifferent; it participates in human emotion, groaning with the grief that fills the poems. This is not mere pathetic fallacy but a deep sense of the world as responsive, as alive with feeling.

### The Overcast Sky

> The sky is overcast with clouds and th... (002-060)

The overcast sky — the blue hidden, the light dimmed. The sky's moods in the *Gitanjali* track the spiritual weather: overcast skies accompany the rains of sorrow, the season of waiting, the dark night of the soul before the dawn of union.

### Lightning Across the Sky

> ...flashes of lightning startle the sky from end to end... (002-190)

Lightning "startles" the sky — the flash that runs from end to end, the sudden illumination across the whole dome. In the *Gitanjali*'s spiritual vocabulary, lightning is the moment of revelation, the instant when the hidden becomes visible, when the divine presence flashes across the dark sky of unknowing.

### The Infinite Sky

> ...children meet. The infinite sky is... (004-085)

The sky is "infinite" — a direct statement of what the sky images throughout the *Gitanjali*. The infinite is not an abstraction; it is the physical sky above, experienced directly, taken into the poem as the visible form of boundlessness.

### Thou Art the Sky

> Thou art the sky and thou art the nest as well... (004-190)

The climactic identification: "Thou art the sky and thou art the nest." The divine is both the infinite above (the sky) and the intimate enclosure (the nest). The *Gitanjali*'s spiritual vision holds these opposites together: the vast and the close, the boundless and the sheltering. The sky is not other than the divine; it is the divine in its aspect of infinity.

## Sky and Infinity

In the *Gitanjali*, the sky is the most consistent image of the infinite — the visible unbounded that surrounds every finite thing. It is the physical sky of Bengal: the monsoon sky overcast with clouds, the April sky of "sunny" brightness, the night sky of distant gloom. But it is always also more than physical. Tagore's sky draws on the Upanishadic image of *akasha* — space, the ether, the all-pervading — while remaining grounded in the simple act of looking up. The poet gazes at the sky; the sky gazes back; and in that mutual looking, the boundary between finite and infinite dissolves.
