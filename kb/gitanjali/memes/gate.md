# Gate

The **gate** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the architecture of arrival — the "King's gateway" where welcome music is struck up, the "palace gate" to which the journey leads, the opening the soul fears may be shut. Larger and more public than the [[door]], the gate in the *Gitanjali* marks the passage from the ordinary world to the sacred precinct, from wandering to homecoming.

## In Gitanjali

### The Gates Are Open

> ...my friend, my best beloved, the gates are open in my house — do not... (002-019)

"The gates are open in my house" — the invitation is extravagant, unconditional. Not the small door but the great gates, thrown wide. The house with open gates is the soul in a state of welcome, the heart that holds nothing back. "Do not" — the sentence breaks off, but the plea is clear: do not pass by.

### Lest Thy Gate Be Shut

> ...day I hasten in fear lest thy gate to be shut; but I... (005-174)

"I hasten in fear lest thy gate be shut" — the urgency of the spiritual life compressed into a single image. The gate may close; the window of welcome may not stay open forever. The fear of the shut gate drives the hastening, the hurry of the soul toward its divine destination. The gate is open now; now is the time to enter.

### Open My Gates

> ...open my gates and bow to him my welcome. I... (006-010)

"Open my gates and bow" — the double gesture of welcome. Opening the gates is the physical act of making space; bowing is the spiritual act of acknowledging the guest's superiority. The gates opened wide and the body bent low: this is the full welcome, the complete hospitality of the soul to the divine visitor.

### The King's Gateway

> ...be struck up from the King's gateway... (006-105)

Music "struck up from the King's gateway" — the gate is the place of announcement, of fanfare. The King's arrival or departure is marked at the gate; the music tells the world that something royal is happening at the threshold. The "King's gateway" is the passage between the monarch's domain and the common world, the place where sovereignty becomes visible.

### The Palace Gate

> ...and, at last, to what palace gate have they brought... (006-179)

"At last, to what palace gate" — the culmination of a long journey. The palace gate is the final threshold, the entrance to the place of presence. The question — "to what palace gate" — is wondering, awed: after all this wandering, what magnificent entrance have I arrived at? The palace gate is the destination the traveler did not dare hope for.

## Gate and Pilgrimage

In the *Gitanjali*, the gate is the threshold of arrival — the moment when the journey climaxes at the entrance to the sacred. The gate is more formal than the door, more public, more architectural. It is the entrance to the palace, the temple, the King's domain. But Tagore's gates, like his doors, are also states of soul: the heart with "open gates," the fear of the "shut gate," the music "struck up" at the gateway of welcome. The gate is where the human and the divine meet, and the meeting is always a matter of timing — the gate may be open or shut, and the soul must hasten while the opening lasts.
