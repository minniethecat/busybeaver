# Pride

**Pride** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is not simple arrogance but a complex spiritual obstacle. It is the wall the self builds around itself, the attachment to one's own strength and adornment that keeps the divine at a distance. The poet does not simply condemn pride from outside — he wrestles with it from within, acknowledging its grip on his own heart and the inevitable moment when it must fall.

## In Gitanjali

### Pride as Self-Adornment

Pride is figured as an attachment to one's own appearance and accomplishment:

> …ff her adornments. She has no pride of dress and (001-065)

"She has no pride of dress" — the ideal soul is one who has shed adornment, who no longer takes pride in external presentation. Pride of dress is a metaphor for all forms of self-display, all ways of constructing an impressive surface behind which the true self hides. To have no pride of dress is to stand naked before the divine, without defense or decoration.

### Pride as a Barrier the Self Builds

Pride constructs walls and then takes satisfaction in them:

> I take pride in this great wall, and I pla… (002-085)

"I take pride in this great wall" — the wall is the self's own construction, a barrier built to protect and define. But the wall that keeps danger out also keeps the divine out. To take pride in the wall is to take pride in one's own separation, to celebrate the very thing that prevents union. The poet's honesty is striking: he admits to loving his own prison.

### Pride as a Secret Attachment

Pride is not always public; it can be hidden, cherished in private:

> … this poverty. Ah, I hug this pride in the secret of my heart. (002-208)

"I hug this pride in the secret of my heart" — the image is intimate and almost tender. Pride is not a flag waved in public but a secret treasure hugged close. "Ah" — the interjection conveys recognition, the moment of seeing one's own hidden attachment. Even poverty, even humility itself, can become an object of pride. The ego is resourceful; it finds ways to survive in renunciation as much as in possession.

### Pride and Shame Entwined

Pride exists in proximity to its opposite:

> …girl a-tremble with shame and pride, like a (003-005)

"A-tremble with shame and pride" — the two emotions are not separate but fused in a single trembling. [[Shame]] and pride are not opposites but twins: both are forms of self-consciousness, both fixate on how one appears. The girl trembles because she is seen, and being seen activates both pride and shame at once. This suggests that pride is not cured by shame — they belong to the same self-regarding structure.

### The Certainty of Pride's Fall

The poet knows pride's destruction is inevitable:

> I surely know my pride will go to the wall, my life … (006-142)

"I surely know my pride will go to the wall" — there is no doubt about the outcome. Pride will be brought down; the wall will be breached. "Surely" — this is not hope or fear but knowledge. The poet has seen enough of the spiritual life to know that pride cannot survive the encounter with the divine. What remains uncertain is not whether pride will fall but what will remain when it does.

## Philosophical Background

- **Ahamkara in Indian philosophy**: In Samkhya and Vedanta, *ahamkara* (I-making) is the principle that generates the sense of individual self. Pride is *ahamkara* at its most assertive — the self not merely existing but insisting on its own importance.
- **Augustinian pride**: For Augustine, pride (*superbia*) is the root of all sin — the self turning toward itself rather than toward God. Tagore shares this structure: pride is curvature inward, the soul bent back upon itself.
- **Nietzschean pride**: Nietzsche distinguishes pride from vanity — true pride is the affirmation of one's own values, not dependence on others' opinions. Tagore's critique targets a different kind of pride: not self-affirmation but self-enclosure, the refusal to be open to what is beyond the self.

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Adornment metaphor | "no pride of dress" | Pride as attachment to external appearance |
| Architectural imagery | "I take pride in this great wall" | Pride as self-built barrier |
| Intimate confession | "I hug this pride in the secret of my heart" | Pride as cherished inner secret |
| Emotional pairing | "a-tremble with shame and pride" | Pride and shame as intertwined twins |
| Prophetic certainty | "I surely know my pride will go to the wall" | The inevitability of pride's destruction |
