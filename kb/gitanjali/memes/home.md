# Home

**Home** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is not a fixed location but a gravitational center — the place the soul departs from and returns to, the empty house awaiting the beloved's arrival, the destination that was present at the origin. Home in the *Gitanjali* is both a memory and a promise.

## In Gitanjali

### Literature and Music at Home

Yeats describes the atmosphere of Tagore's upbringing:

> ...had all round him in his home literature and music. (P01-072)

Tagore's own home was saturated with "literature and music" — the air he breathed, the inheritance he received. This home was not just a building but a cultural ecosystem, a tradition made domestic. The *Gitanjali* itself is a product of this home: songs sung in the family courtyard before they were poems published to the world.

### Home-Coming in the Empty House

The image of the empty house awaiting return is one of the *Gitanjali*'s most poignant:

> ...home-coming in the empty house, are... (P01-123)

"Home-coming" into "the empty house" — the house is empty because the beloved is not yet there. The return is anticipated but not yet fulfilled. Home is the place where one waits for the other, the space prepared for the arrival that will fill it.

### Men Going Home

The ordinary act of return becomes a spiritual image:

> ...for him: "Men going home glance at me and smile and fill..." (P01-174)

> ...drowsy with sleep. Men going home glance at me and smile and fill... (002-202)

"Men going home" — the daily return, the evening walk, the workers heading back to their families. The speaker watches them, excluded from their ordinary belonging, and their "glance" and "smile" mark the distance between the one who has a home and the one who is still on the way. The spiritual journey is measured against this ordinary homecoming.

### Women Gone Home

> ...slant, and the women had gone home with... (004-019)

The women "had gone home" — the past tense marks a departure already completed. The speaker remains, watching others leave, waiting for his own home-going. Home is where others have already gone; the speaker is still outside, still on the threshold, still looking in.

## Home in the Bhakti Tradition

In Bhakti poetry, the soul is often a wanderer, an exile, and God is the true home (*nijalaya*, *apana ghara*). Tagore draws on this vocabulary but makes it his own: the home is not a transcendent heaven but an "empty house" in this world, awaiting the beloved whose arrival will make it suddenly, fully, home.
