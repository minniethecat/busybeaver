# Purity

**Purity** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* appears not as a moral achievement but as a quality of light and air — the transparency of morning that makes perception possible:

> ...morning, in a coolness of purity transparent. (005-154)

The phrase is striking in its sensory concreteness. Purity is not abstract virtue but "coolness" — a temperature, a texture, something felt on the skin. It is "transparent" — not a substance but a quality of light, a medium through which things are seen clearly. Morning is the hour of this purity: the world fresh, the air clear, the heat of the day not yet arrived.

## In Gitanjali

### Poem 5 — The Morning Atmosphere

Poem 5 of the *Gitanjali* is a morning poem in which the speaker asks the divine to "lead" him, to guide his steps through the day. The final image of the poem arrives after a meditation on the speaker's own emptiness — he is "a remnant of a cloud of autumn uselessly roaming in the sky" (005-145), his vapour not yet "melted" (005-146) into the divine light. He offers his "fleeting emptiness" (005-150) to be painted, gilded, and spread — and then, at day's end:

> And again when it shall be thy wish to end this play at night, I shall melt and vanish away in the dark, or it may be in a smile of the white morning, in a coolness of purity transparent. (005-152–154)

The "coolness of purity transparent" is the atmosphere in which this dissolution occurs — not into darkness but into a "white morning," a smile of the divine. Purity is the condition of receptivity at the moment of self-dissolution: when the self vanishes, it vanishes into transparency, not into void.

The white morning echoes the "stainless white radiance" earlier in the same poem (005-007), where it was described as the ground of being beneath all colour: "there is no day nor night, nor form nor colour, and never, never a word" (005-007–008). Purity, in this register, is the fundamental condition before differentiation — the white light before it splits into the spectrum.

### Unclean Hands — The Contrast

In poem 1, Tagore provides the negative of the purity image — desire as contamination:

> Thy desire at once puts out the light from the lamp it touches with its breath. It is unholy — take not thy gifts through its unclean hands. Accept only what is offered by sacred love. (001-085–087)

The "unclean hands" of desire stand as the antithesis of the "purity transparent" of morning. Desire touches the lamp of perception and extinguishes it; purity lets the light through. The opposition is not between good and evil but between obstructed and clear — between the self that grasps and the self that is transparent.

Tagore's purity is not the purity of separation — keeping oneself unstained by the world — but the purity of clarity: being so transparent that the divine light passes through without obstruction. It is a quality of the morning soul, the self before it has been clouded by the day's accumulations.

## Philosophical Background

In Indian thought, purity (*saucha*) is one of the *niyamas* (observances) of classical yoga — a prerequisite for spiritual practice. Patanjali's Yoga Sutra describes it as both bodily cleanliness and mental purification, leading to detachment from the body and cessation of contact with others. The Bhagavad Gita frames purity (*sattva*) as one of the three *gunas* — the quality of luminosity, harmony, and clarity — contrasting it with the passion of *rajas* and the darkness of *tamas*.

But Tagore's treatment is closer to the Upanishadic intuition that the self (*atman*) is already pure, already luminous; what is needed is not to *become* pure but to remove the obstructions that hide an original purity. The "coolness of purity transparent" is not something achieved; it is something revealed, in the right hour, under the right light. The morning images — "coolness," "transparent," "white," "smile" — are drawn from sensory experience, not doctrinal categories, and they reflect Tagore's consistent preference for the palpable over the abstract.

## Poetic Expression

Tagore's rendering of purity is built from light, temperature, and transparency rather than from moral vocabulary. The adjective chain "coolness of purity transparent" (005-154) reverses expected grammar — it is not "transparent purity" but "purity transparent," treating "transparent" as an ambient quality, almost post-positional, as if purity were a medium one inhabits. The "white" that recurs in poem 5 — "stainless white radiance" (005-007), "awful white light" (005-018), "white morning" (005-153) — accumulates into a visual motif that associates purity with the full spectrum before its division.

Against this, the "unclean hands" (001-086) of desire offer a tactile, visceral contrast — purity is not only a quality of light but a quality of touch. What is unclean grasps and extinguishes; what is pure receives and transmits.
