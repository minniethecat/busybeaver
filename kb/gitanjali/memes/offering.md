# Offering

**Offering** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* — the very title announces the centrality of this concept. A *gitanjali* is a song-offering, a poem given as a gift to the divine. Offering is the fundamental gesture of devotion: the soul emptying itself before the beloved, laying down what it has and what it is. Yet Tagore complicates this — the offering is never purely the soul's own act. Even the ability to offer is itself received.

## In Gitanjali

### The Urgency of Offering

The moment for offering is now, and it can be missed:

> …aware, and the time of offering go by. (001-061)

"The time of offering go by" — offering has its appointed time, a window that opens and closes. To be unaware is to let the moment pass. The tragedy is not refusal but distraction, not rebellion but inattention. The time of offering goes by while the soul is absorbed in lesser things. Urgency belongs to offering: the gift must be given now, while the door is open, while the beloved is near.

### The Danger of Unholy Taking

Offering is threatened not only by neglect but by active violation:

> … snatch with unholy greed the offerings from (002-125)

"Snatch with unholy greed the offerings" — there are forces that would steal what is meant for the divine. "Unholy" — the opposite of offering is not refusal but desecration, treating as one's own what belongs to the beloved. The soul must guard its offerings, must protect the sacred space in which giving happens from the greed that would appropriate everything for the self.

### Offering as Patient Preparation

Offering requires time, care, and patient arrangement:

> …here weary hours spreading my offerings for thee, (002-198)

"Wear[y] hours spreading my offerings for thee" — offering is not instantaneous. It takes "weary hours" of preparation, the careful arrangement of gifts. "Spreading" — the image is of laying out a feast, a table prepared with attention to every detail. The weariness is not resentment but devotion: the soul gladly exhausts itself in preparation for the beloved.

### The Empty Altar

The most striking image is of an altar emptied of offerings:

> …and thine altar is empty of all offerings to the last. (005-173)

"Thine altar is empty of all offerings to the last" — this could be desolation or consummation. The altar is empty because everything has been given; there is nothing left to offer. "To the last" — the offering is total, exhaustive. The empty altar is the sign of complete self-gift. When nothing remains to give, the offering is perfect.

### Offering as Reciprocal Gift

Even as the soul offers, it receives:

> bring it to thee as my offering thou rewardest me with thy gr… (005-183)

"Bring it to thee as my offering thou rewardest me" — the economy of offering is paradoxical. The soul brings a gift and receives a reward. This is not a transaction but a circle of [[grace]]: the offering that the soul makes is enabled by what the beloved has already given, and the beloved responds to the offering with further gift. Offering and receiving are not separate acts but two movements of a single dance.

## Philosophical Background

- **Bhakti puja**: In Hindu devotional practice, *puja* (worship) centers on offering — flowers, food, light, song — to the deity. The offering is both symbolic (representing the self) and real (the act of giving transforms the giver).
- **Maussian gift theory**: Marcel Mauss argued that gifts create reciprocal bonds — to give is to enter into relationship. Tagore's offering shares this structure: the offering binds the soul to the divine, not through obligation but through the ongoing circulation of gift.
- **Kierkegaard's sacrifice**: For Kierkegaard, the knight of faith makes the movement of infinite resignation — giving everything up — and then receives everything back by virtue of the absurd. Tagore's offering follows a similar doubling: to give all is to receive all.
