# Silence

**Silence** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* functions as a theological category rather than merely the absence of sound. It is the medium of divine presence, the language of the deepest communion, and the destination toward which all [[song]] and striving ultimately flow. Where Western spirituality often privileges the word, Tagore's poetic idiom grants silence equal — and sometimes greater — spiritual authority.

## In Gitanjali

### Silence as Divine Dwelling

Tagore repeatedly associates silence with the presence of the divine. Silence is not where God is absent but where God is most fully present:

> ...I will fill my heart with thy silence and endure... (001-186)

> ...beyond, come to me, my lord of silence, with thy peace and rest. (002-180)

The address "my lord of silence" personifies silence as a divine attribute — the divine is not merely silent but is the *lord* of silence, the one who commands and bestows it. To "fill my heart with thy silence" is to make one's inner life a dwelling-place for the sacred.

### Silence and the Sea

The most concentrated image-complex around silence in the *Gitanjali* involves water — the sea, in particular — as a figure for the silent infinite into which all individual expression flows:

> The morning sea of silence broke into ripples of bird songs... (003-081)

> ...current and flow to a sea of silence in one salutation to thee. (007-002)

The "sea of silence" in Section 7 is the ultimate destination. The river of life "flows to a sea of silence," and this flowing is itself an act of worship — "in one salutation to thee." Silence is not the negation of song but its consummation: all songs eventually dissolve into the silent sea from which they arose.

### Silence of Night

Silence is particularly associated with night, when the distractions of the day recede and the soul becomes receptive:

> Only in the deepest silence of night the stars smile and... (005-126)

> In the silence of gathering night I asked... (004-153)

The night-silence is the time of questioning and reception — "I asked" — in contrast to the daytime of action and utterance. The stars "smile" in silence, suggesting a benevolent cosmic order that does not need to speak to be known.

### Silence as Farewell

In Section 6, silence marks the final leave-taking — death itself is figured as a silent departure:

> ...and life will take its leave in silence, drawing the last curtain... (006-079)

> ...gaze upon me and summon me in silence. (006-147)

[[death|Death]] comes in silence — not with drama or declaration. The divine summons ("summon me in silence") is wordless, and the soul's response is similarly quiet. This is the ultimate expression of Tagore's theology of silence: the deepest communication between human and divine requires no words.

## Philosophical Background

Tagore's valuation of silence has roots in several traditions:

- **Upanishadic *mauna***: The practice of silence (*mauna*) is a recognized spiritual discipline in Indian tradition, associated with the control of speech and the cultivation of inwardness. The *Brihadaranyaka Upanishad* describes *Brahman* as that before which "words turn back, together with the mind."
- **Bhakti devotionalism**: While [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] is often associated with ecstatic song, it also includes the tradition of silent absorption (*bhāva*) — the wordless state in which the devotee is simply present to the divine.
- **Poetic tradition**: Tagore was influenced by the English Romantic poets' treatment of silence as a mode of the sublime, as well as by the Baul singers of [[bengal|Bengal]], whose songs often celebrate the *maner manush* (the person of the heart) who dwells in silence.

## Poetic Expression

| Poetic Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------------|---------|--------|
| Personification | "my lord of silence" | Silence as a divine attribute |
| Sea/ocean imagery | "sea of silence," "morning sea of silence" | Silence as vast, encompassing, ultimate |
| Night association | "deepest silence of night" | Silence as receptive darkness |
| Gift/imparting | "fill my heart with thy silence" | Silence as something received, not achieved |
| Flow/course | "flow to a sea of silence" | Silence as destination, journey's end |
