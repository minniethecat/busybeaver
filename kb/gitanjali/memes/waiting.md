# Waiting

**Waiting** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is not mere passivity or empty time. It is the soul's active orientation toward the beloved, a posture charged with [[love]] and sustained by [[hope]]. To wait is to hold oneself in readiness — the servant awaiting the master, the bride awaiting the groom, the singer awaiting the song. Waiting is the shape that devotion takes in time.

## In Gitanjali

### Waiting as Love's Posture

The poet frames waiting as inseparable from love itself:

> I am only waiting for love to give myself up at… (001-166)

"I am only waiting for love" — waiting is not a delay before love arrives; waiting *is* love's present form. To wait is to be already given over, already oriented, already belonging. The waiting self is not empty but full — full of the anticipation of the beloved, full of readiness. "To give myself up" — waiting culminates in surrender, but the surrender is prepared in every moment of the wait.

### Waiting as the Servant's Role

Waiting defines a hierarchical relationship of devotion:

> …the servant or the bride awaiting the master's… (P01-122)

"The servant or the bride awaiting the master" — two images of waiting are offered: the servant and the bride. Both wait, but differently. The servant waits in dutiful expectation, the bride in loving anticipation. Both postures share a common structure: the waiting one is defined by the one waited for. Identity itself is suspended in waiting — who the servant or bride *is* depends on the master or groom who will come.

### The Danger of Waiting in Vain

Waiting carries risk — the possibility that the awaited one may not come:

> The night is nearly spent waiting for him in vain. I fear lest … (003-066)

"The night is nearly spent waiting for him in vain" — the possibility of vain waiting haunts every moment of genuine anticipation. The night is "nearly spent" — time is running out, the window of waiting is closing. "I fear" — waiting involves vulnerability, the exposure to disappointment. To wait is to risk that one's waiting may have no answer, that the night will end and dawn will reveal an empty doorstep.

### Waiting Without Demand

The poet contrasts impatient waiting with humble, undemanding patience:

> …gone back in anger. I am only waiting for (001-174)

There is an alternative to waiting — going back in anger, departing in frustration. The poet rejects this. "I am only waiting" — the "only" is crucial. Waiting is not one option among many; it is the only option. To leave in anger would be to sever the relationship; to wait is to keep it alive, even in silence, even in absence.

### Waiting in Solitude

Waiting isolates the one who waits:

> remained in her loneliness waiting for thy recognition. (004-188)

"Remained in her loneliness waiting" — waiting and loneliness are intertwined. The one who waits stands apart from the flow of ordinary life, fixed on a single point while the world moves on. "For thy recognition" — what is waited for is not a gift or reward but acknowledgment, the divine glance that sees and knows. To be recognized is to have one's waiting validated, to have the long vigil mean something.

## Philosophical Background

- **Vaishnava bhakti**: In the Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition that influenced Tagore's Bengal, *viraha* (separation) is a higher state than union — the pain of waiting is itself the deepest form of communion. Waiting is not deprivation but participation in divine love.
- **Simone Weil's "waiting for God"**: Weil describes attention as a form of waiting — "We do not obtain the most precious gifts by going in search of them but by waiting for them." Tagore's waiting shares this quality of receptive attention.
- **Heideggerian waiting**: Heidegger distinguishes waiting (expectant, calculative) from awaiting (open, receptive). Tagore's waiting aligns with the latter — not waiting *for* something specific but waiting in openness to whatever may come.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Existential framing | "I am only waiting for love" | Waiting as the soul's defining posture |
| Role imagery | "the servant or the bride awaiting" | Waiting through social-relational metaphors |
| Temporal pressure | "The night is nearly spent waiting" | The urgency and risk of waiting |
| Exclusivity marker | "I am only waiting" | Waiting as the sole possible response |
| Loneliness intensifier | "remained in her loneliness waiting" | The isolating cost of faithful waiting |
