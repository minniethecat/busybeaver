# Friend

The **friend** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the divine addressed in the language of intimacy. More than "lord" or "master" or "king," the word "friend" carries the weight of companionship — the one who walks beside, who waits in the dark, who is known not through awe but through nearness. The entire devotional vocabulary of the *Gitanjali* tilts toward this word.

## In Gitanjali

### The Name of Intimacy

The simplest and most radical act of address in the *Gitanjali* is the substitution of "friend" for "God":

> ...I forget myself and call thee friend who... (001-023)

"Call thee friend" — the phrase is deliberate. The speaker "forgets himself" — loses the sense of distance, hierarchy, appropriate formality — and speaks to the divine as to an equal. This is not disrespect but the deepest respect: to know God so well that formality would be a kind of lie.

### The Only Friend

Poem 2 of the *Gitanjali* intensifies the address:

> ...only friend, my best beloved, the gates are open... (002-019)

"Only friend" — singular, exclusive. There is no other. "Best beloved" — the superlative belongs to the language of love poetry, not theology. The divine is not one friend among many; the divine is friendship itself, the standard by which all other companionship is measured.

### Friend in Darkness

The friend is present not only in light but in darkness:

> ...the darkness, my friend! (002-025)

The exclamation mark carries the force of recognition. Even in darkness, the friend is there. Darkness is not absence; it is another mode of the friend's presence, a presence known by touch rather than sight.

### Friend on the Journey

The friend is a companion on the soul's journey:

> ...on this stormy night on thy [[journey]] of [[love]], my friend? (002-022)

The question — "on thy journey of love, my friend?" — is posed on a "stormy night." The friend's journey is a journey of love, and the speaker acknowledges it even as the storm rages. The friend is not sheltered from the storm; the friend is in it, moving through it, and the speaker recognizes the friend's presence there.

### The Friend's Hand

> ...grasp thy hand as my friend's. (005-107)

The handclasp is the gesture of friendship made physical. To "grasp thy hand" is to claim intimacy, to close the distance between human and divine. A hand held is a friend held. The gesture is simple, human, universal.

### Made Known to Friends

> Thou hast made me known to friends whom I knew not. Thou hast g... (004-133)

The divine not only IS a friend but MAKES friends — weaving a web of human connection that the speaker could not have woven alone. "Friends whom I knew not" become known through the divine's mediation. Friendship is both the relationship with God and the relationship God enables among humans.

## The Bhakti Context

The address of God as "friend" (*sakha*) has deep roots in the Bhakti tradition. In the *Bhagavata Purana*, Krishna is the friend of the cowherds — specifically of Arjuna and Sudama. The *sakhya-bhava* (attitude of friendship toward God) is one of the five primary devotional moods. Tagore's innovation is to strip the address of mythology and make it purely personal: no Krishna, no cowherds, just "my friend" spoken into the silence.
