# Morning

**Morning** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is far more than a time of day — it is a spiritual threshold, the privileged moment of divine arrival and human readiness. Across the poems, morning marks the breaking of [[light]] after the vigil of [[night]], the tuning of instruments before the day's [[song]], and the sudden, unannounced coming of the beloved.

## In Gitanjali

### Morning as Divine Arrival

Tagore repeatedly figures morning as the moment when the divine enters — not gradually but suddenly, catching the soul in its ordinary posture:

> ...morning he suddenly come to my door when... (003-067)

The adverb "suddenly" is essential: divine arrival is not scheduled or predictable. It comes in the morning, when the soul is still between sleep and full wakefulness — a liminal state of maximum receptivity. The poet is not preparing for the divine; he is simply present when it arrives.

### The Golden Harp

Section 1 establishes one of the *Gitanjali*'s most concentrated morning images:

> When in the morning air the golden harp is tuned... (001-156)

The "golden harp" being tuned in the morning air is both a literal image of dawn and a metaphor for the poet's own readiness. Morning is when the instrument of [[song]] — the poet's voice — is prepared. The tuning is not the song itself but its precondition: morning is the time of preparation, the moment when the soul readies itself for the day's creative and devotional work.

### Morning Light

Morning is inseparable from [[light]] in Tagore's symbolic vocabulary:

> ...the festival of morning light... (003-071)

> The young light of morning... (003-163)

The "festival of morning light" transforms dawn into a sacred occasion — not a daily inevitability but a celebration. "Young light" personifies morning as something fresh, innocent, and full of potential. This is characteristic of Tagore's nature imagery: the natural world is never merely natural; it is always also a revelation.

### Morning and the Sea of Silence

The most compressed image of morning in the *Gitanjali* links it with [[silence]] and birdsong:

> The morning sea of silence broke into ripples of bird songs... (003-081)

Morning is the moment when silence breaks into song — when the undifferentiated stillness of [[night]] gives way to the specific, articulated voices of day. The metaphor of "ripples" suggests both continuity (the ripples are still the sea) and transformation (the sea is no longer silent).

### The Sure Coming

Section 1 closes with a promise that anchors Tagore's morning theology:

> The morning will surely come, the darkness... (001-189)

This is not a hope but an assertion of faith. Morning "will surely come" — the certainty is absolute. The darkness of [[night]], of [[death]], of spiritual aridity — all of these are temporary. Morning is the ontological default, the reality toward which all darkness tends.

## Philosophical Background

Tagore's treatment of morning reflects several traditions:

- **Upanishadic dawn**: The *Upanishads* frequently invoke dawn (*ushas*) as a symbol of spiritual awakening — the moment when the *atman* recognizes its identity with *Brahman*. Tagore's morning carries this resonance of enlightenment.
- **Bhakti *mangala-arati***: In [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] practice, the morning *arati* (worship with lamps) is the day's first offering. Tagore's "golden harp tuned" in the morning echoes this ritual structure.
- **Tagore's own practice**: Tagore was known to rise early and compose in the pre-dawn hours. His mornings were literally creative — the time when songs came.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Sudden arrival | "morning he suddenly come to my door" | Divine as unpredictable, surprising |
| Musical tuning | "golden harp is tuned" | Morning as preparation, readiness |
| Festive framing | "festival of morning light" | Morning as celebration, not routine |
| Certainty declaration | "The morning will surely come" | Faith as absolute, beyond hope |
| Sea/sound fusion | "morning sea of silence broke into ripples of bird songs" | Silence → song, unity → multiplicity |
