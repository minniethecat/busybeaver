# Gitanjali (Song Offerings)

**Gitanjali** (*Gītāñjali*, Bengali: গীতাঞ্জলি), subtitled *Song Offerings*, is a collection of prose poems by [[rabindranath-tagore|Rabindranath Tagore]], published in 1913 by Macmillan in London. As Tagore's title page states, it is "a collection of prose translations made by the author from the original Bengali" (P01-004). The collection won Tagore the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1913, making him the first non-European laureate. With an Introduction by [[w-b-yeats|W.B. Yeats]] that declared the poems to have "stirred my blood as nothing has for years" (P01-015), *Gitanjali* became one of the most consequential acts of literary translation in the twentieth century.

## Title and Structure

The title *Gītāñjali* is a Sanskrit compound: *gīta* (song) + *añjali* (offering, literally "cupped hands"). The English subtitle *Song Offerings* renders this etymology faithfully: the poems are songs offered, like flowers or water, with cupped hands to the divine.

The 1913 Macmillan edition contains 103 numbered poems (or "lyrics," as Yeats called them), preceded by Yeats's Introduction. The poems range from a few lines to a page and a half, each a self-contained meditation or prayer. They are not arranged in a narrative sequence, but they trace a discernible arc: from longing and separation through the disciplines of waiting and humility to moments of union, illumination, and the embrace of mortality.

> I am only waiting for [[love]] to give myself up at last into his hands. (001-166)

This line, from the first grouping of poems, establishes the collection's governing spiritual posture: active waiting, self-surrender as the highest form of agency.

## Translation and Composition

The *Gitanjali* is not a direct translation of Tagore's Bengali *Gītāñjali* (published 1910) but a composite drawn from multiple Bengali collections, including *Naivedya* (Offerings), *Kheyā* (The Ferry), and *Gītimālya* (Garland of Songs). Tagore made the English versions during a period of recovery from personal loss and illness, working from his own Bengali texts with the freedom of an author-translator.

Yeats, who did not read Bengali, relied on Indian informants who told him the originals were "full of subtlety of rhythm, of untranslatable delicacies of colour, of metrical invention" (P01-088–089). The English prose versions deliberately sacrifice these formal qualities for a simpler, more universal idiom — what Yeats called "the language of the personal made universal."

### The Manuscript Journey

The manuscript of the translations traveled with Yeats through London:

> I have carried the manuscript of these translations about with me for days, reading it in railway trains, or on the top of omnibuses and in restaurants. (P01-085–086)

Through the painter William Rothenstein — to whom the volume is dedicated — the manuscript reached Yeats, who wrote the Introduction and helped secure publication. The book appeared in a limited edition of 750 copies in November 1912, followed by the trade edition in March 1913.

## Themes

The *Gitanjali* is organized around a handful of interlocking spiritual themes:

| Theme | Expression | Key Passages |
|-------|-----------|--------------|
| [[light|Divine Light]] | "Light, my light, the world-filling light" | 004-056–063 |
| [[love|Sacred Love]] | "I am only waiting for love to give myself up" | 001-166–169 |
| Humility and Poverty | "I sit like a beggar maid" | 002-181, 003-005 |
| Waiting and Surrender | "This is my delight, thus to wait and watch" |(003-035)|
| [[bhakti-devotion|Devotion]] (*bhakti*) | "Kindle the lamp of love with thy life" |(002-068)|
| Mortality and Union | "I know I shall love death as well" |(P01-151)|
| The Divine as Intimate | "My lord," "my king," "my beloved" | throughout |

The collection's spiritual vocabulary is drawn primarily from the Upanishadic and [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] traditions, but Tagore's idiom is deliberately ecumenical. Yeats noted affinities with Christian mystics such as Thomas à Kempis and John of the Cross (P01-150), and subsequent readers have found resonances with Sufi poetry, Zen Buddhism, and Romantic nature mysticism.

## Critical Reception

Yeats's Introduction set the terms for the initial reception, emphasizing the union of poetry and religion in Tagore's culture:

> A tradition, where poetry and religion are the same thing, has passed through the centuries, gathering from learned and unlearned metaphor and emotion, and carried back again to the multitude the thought of the scholar and of the noble. (P01-093)

The Nobel committee cited the *Gitanjali* in awarding Tagore the prize "because of his profoundly sensitive, fresh and beautiful verse, by which, with consummate skill, he has made his poetic thought, expressed in his own English words, a part of the literature of the West."

The initial enthusiasm was immense — the book went through ten printings within a year — but Tagore's reputation in English declined after the 1920s, partly because his later English translations did not match the quality of the *Gitanjali*. In [[bengal|Bengal]], however, Tagore's stature never diminished, and the Bengali originals from which the *Gitanjali* was drawn remain central to the canon of Bengali literature.

## Legacy

*Gitanjali* established the template for modern Indian poetry in English and opened a channel through which Indian spiritual and literary traditions could reach global audiences. Its influence can be traced through the work of later poets — from Pablo Neruda, who read Tagore as a young man, to the Hindi Chhayavad poets and the English-language Indian poets of the post-Independence generation. The collection remains Tagore's most widely read and translated work, a testament to Yeats's judgment that these prose translations had achieved something rare: "the language of the personal made universal."
