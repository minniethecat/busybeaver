# Prayer

**Prayer** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is not the conventional petition for worldly goods or divine intervention. It is a paradoxical act: the speaker trembles at the thought of being answered, asks to be struck rather than comforted, and discovers that the divine's love waits regardless of whether prayer is offered at all. Tagore transforms prayer from a transaction into a relationship — one in which the very act of asking changes the asker.

## In Gitanjali

### The Fear of Being Answered

The speaker approaches prayer with trepidation, not confidence:

> My debts are large, my failures great, my shame secret and heavy; yet when I come to ask for my good, I quake in fear lest my prayer be granted. (002-079)

This is prayer turned inside out. Instead of certainty that one deserves an answer, there is the fear of actually receiving one. "I quake in fear lest my prayer be granted" — the speaker knows that answered prayer brings responsibility, transformation, the unsettling presence of the divine. To have one's prayer granted is to be changed, and change is terrifying. The speaker's "debts," "failures," and "shame" make him feel unworthy of an answer, yet he asks anyway — and trembles.

### The Prayer That Asks to Be Struck

Tagore's most distinctive prayer is not a request for blessing but for destruction:

> This is my prayer to thee, my lord — strike, strike at the root of penury in my heart. (002-149)

"Strike, strike at the root of penury" — the repetition conveys urgency, and "penury" names not material poverty but spiritual impoverishment. The prayer asks for surgery, not comfort. The "root" must be struck because surface remedies are insufficient; only a direct blow at the source of spiritual poverty can free the heart. This is the prayer of a soul that knows its own sickness and trusts the physician enough to ask for the knife.

The prayer continues with a series of requests for strength: "Give me the strength lightly to bear my joys and sorrows. Give me the strength to make my love fruitful in service. Give me the strength never to disown the poor or bend my knees before insolent might." Each petition transforms weakness into capacity — prayer as empowerment, not dependency.

### Love That Waits Without Being Called

The divine's love does not depend on being invoked:

> If I call not thee in my prayers, if I keep not thee in my heart, thy love for me still waits for my love. (002-116)

This is the most radical statement about prayer in Gitanjali: prayer is not necessary for divine love. "Thy love for me still waits for my love" — the divine loves regardless; prayer is the soul's awakening to a love that already exists. The conditional "if I call not thee" and "if I keep not thee" establish the speaker's potential failure, but the main clause sweeps those conditions aside. Prayer does not activate divine love; it acknowledges it.

### The Bliss of Touch

The final prayer is for continuity of awareness:

> Oh, grant me my prayer that I may never lose the bliss of the touch of the one in the play of many. (004-143)

"Never lose the bliss of the touch of the one" — the prayer is for constancy of perception, the ability to see unity in diversity. "The one in the play of many" is a classic Vedantic formulation: the single divine reality manifesting as the multiplicity of the world. Prayer here is not a request for something new but a plea never to lose what one already has — the awareness of divine presence in all things.

### The Prayer of Sleep

The speaker prays not to be disturbed, valuing the sleep of waiting over the noise of devotion:

> I pray. I wish not to be called from my sleep by the clamorous choir of birds, by the riot of wind at the festival of morning light. Let me sleep undisturbed even if my lord comes of a sudden to my door. (003-070)

This prayer reverses every expectation. Instead of asking to be awakened for the divine visit, the speaker asks to remain asleep. "Let me sleep undisturbed even if my lord comes of a sudden to my door" — the sleep is "precious" because it "only waits for his touch to vanish." Sleep here is not laziness but a state of receptivity, a waiting that is itself a form of prayer. The "clamorous choir of birds" and "riot of wind" represent the noisy pieties that would interrupt the deeper communion of silent waiting.

## Philosophical Background

- **Bhakti prayer as relationship**: In the bhakti tradition, prayer (*prarthana*) is not a Vedic sacrifice or a transactional offering but an expression of loving relationship. The devotee speaks to the divine as a person, with intimacy and emotional range — fear, longing, demand, gratitude. Tagore's prayers in Gitanjali embody this bhakti mode, addressing "my lord" with the directness of a lover.
- **The Upanishadic paradox of asking**: The Upanishads teach that the divine is already the inmost self (*atman*). If the divine is what one already is, prayer cannot be a request for something external. Tagore resolves this paradox: his prayers ask not for external goods but for internal transformation — to be struck, to be given strength, to never lose awareness. The prayer changes the pray-er, not the divine.
- **Kierkegaard's "purity of heart"**: Søren Kierkegaard wrote that "purity of heart is to will one thing." Tagore's prayers share this quality of single-mindedness. They are not lists of requests but concentrated acts of willing one thing: union with the divine, freedom from the self's poverty, the bliss of touch.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Paradoxical fear | "I quake in fear lest my prayer be granted" | Inverts conventional prayer; answered prayer is terrifying |
| Violent imperative | "strike, strike at the root of penury" | Prayer as demand for surgical transformation |
| Conditional negation | "If I call not thee... thy love still waits" | Divine love is independent of human invocation |
| Series of petitions | "Give me the strength... Give me the strength..." | Builds momentum through anaphora; prayer as empowerment |
| Touch metaphor | "bliss of the touch of the one in the play of many" | Prayer as sensory intimacy with the divine |
