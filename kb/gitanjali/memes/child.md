# Child

The **child** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is not merely a stage of life but a spiritual archetype: the soul in its original state of nearness to the divine, before the separations and self-consciousness of adulthood have intervened. Children in the *Gitanjali* are close to the mystery in a way adults must labor to recover.

## In Gitanjali

### Nearness to the Divine

Yeats observes that Tagore's world retains something of the child's immediacy:

> ...as near to him as they are near to children, and the changes of the... (P01-183)

The divine is "near" — and the child is the measure of that nearness. What children possess naturally — closeness, trust, presence — adults must struggle to regain. The child is not a phase to be outgrown but a quality to be recovered.

### Children on the Seashore

Poem 4 of the *Gitanjali* contains one of its most celebrated images — children meeting on the seashore of endless worlds:

> ...the seashore of endless worlds children meet. The infinite sky is... (004-085)

> ...seashore of endless worlds the children meet with shouts and dance... (004-087)

The children "meet" on a shore that borders "endless worlds." Their play — "shouts and dance" — is not trivial but cosmic. The seashore is the threshold between the finite and the infinite, and children inhabit it naturally, without effort. They play where adults would stand in awe.

### The Child Decked with Prince's Robes

> The child who is decked with prince's robes and has jewelled chains round his neck... (001-072)

The image of the adorned child suggests both royalty and burden. The child wears "prince's robes" but these are not his own — they are put on him. The adornment that signifies status also signifies constraint. The child beneath the robes is simpler, freer, closer to the essential.

### Child's Play and the Sacred

> ...not turn in contempt from my childish play among dust, and... (003-031)

The speaker asks the divine not to scorn his "childish play." Play — even dusty, humble play — is a form of offering. The child's play is serious in its own way; it is the child's mode of being in the world, and the speaker asks that it be received as such.

### The World of Children

> their ships, while children gather pebbles and scatter... (P01-196)

The image is of parallel worlds: the adult world of "ships" — commerce, purpose, destination — and the child's world of "pebbles" — gathering and scattering, without utility but with absorption. The child's activity is circular (gather, scatter, gather again), not linear; it is play, not production.

## Philosophical Background

In Indian thought, the child represents *bala* — the divine child, particularly Krishna as the infant-god whose play (*lila*) is the world's creative principle. Tagore draws on this tradition but humanizes it: his children are not gods in disguise but human beings in their natural, unseparated state. The Romantic tradition of the child as "father of the man" (Wordsworth) also informs Tagore's vision — the child is not a blank slate but a bearer of wisdom the adult has forgotten.
