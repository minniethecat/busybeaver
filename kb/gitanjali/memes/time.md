# Time

**Time** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is neither the abstract measure of clocks nor the enemy the soul must escape. It is the element in which the spiritual journey unfolds — the medium of waiting, the span of the journey, and the threshold across which the soul passes from longing into union. Time in the *Gitanjali* is felt, not counted; it is charged with urgency and saturated with presence.

## In Gitanjali

### The Appointed Hour

The most persistent temporal note in the *Gitanjali* is that of the "appointed hour" — the time set by the divine for meeting, for offering, for the soul's release:

> Now it is time to sit quite, face to face with thee, and to sing dedication of life in this silent and overflowing leisure. (001-054)

The hour is not chosen by the speaker; it arrives. The speaker's part is to recognize it — "Now it is time" — and to respond with stillness and song. The divine sets the clock; the human keeps the appointment.

### The Urgency of Offering

Time in the *Gitanjali* carries an edge of urgency. The moment of offering has a limited span, and to miss it is to lose something irrecoverable:

> ...and the time of offering go by. (001-061)

> ...and pluck it while there is time. (001-063)

These lines share a single concern: the window is open now, but it will not stay open. "While there is time" is not a casual phrase — it acknowledges that the condition of possibility is temporary. The flower must be plucked, the offering must be made, before the moment passes. This is not anxiety but alertness.

### The Long Journey

Time is the dimension in which the journey toward the divine takes place, and that journey is long:

> The time that my journey takes is long and the way of it long. (001-112)

The repetition of "long" — journey and way both — conveys that the soul's pilgrimage is not a sudden illumination but an extended passage. Time here is not a barrier but the very path. The length is not a complaint; it is the condition of depth.

### The Unripe Hour

Not every moment is ready. Time has its own internal ripeness:

> The time has not come true, the words have not been rightly set... (001-129)

"The time has not come true" — an unusual phrasing that treats time as something that can be true or false, ripe or unripe. The poet's words wait on the time that will make them true. Until then, both time and word are incomplete, waiting together for the moment of convergence.

### Times of Languor

The journey is not continuous exertion. There are times of pause, of lingering:

> There are times when I languidly linger and times when I awaken and hurry... (001-144)

The alternation is natural — languor and hurry, lingering and awakening. Time is not uniform; it has different textures. The soul moves through these textures, sometimes slow, sometimes swift, but always within time's encompassing field.

### The Final Departure

The culmination of the temporal journey is the moment of departure — the crossing:

> Now, I ask, has the time come at last when I may go in and behold thy face... (001-163)

The question form — "has the time come?" — returns to the motif of the appointed hour. Even at the end, the speaker does not presume to know. The time of arrival, like the time of offering, is set by the divine. The soul can only ask, and wait, and be ready.

### Shelter at Evening

Time's arc bends toward rest. The day's span — morning, noon, evening — maps the soul's journey:

> ...exhausted and the time come to take shelter in a silent obscurity. (002-161)

> The morning time is past, and the noon. In the weary, darkening day, your call rings across the sky. (002-201)

Morning and noon are gone; evening approaches. The soul is "exhausted" — not from failure but from the fullness of the journey. The time for shelter has come. This is the temporal shape of the spiritual life: labor, then rest; journey, then home.

### The Night Vigil

Time also holds the hours of darkness — the long watch through the night:

> ...sometimes sit there all through the night, carrying on the duties of this world, and yet my heart was with Him... (P01-055, Yeats's account of the Baul singers)

The "night" here is not a metaphor but literal — the hours when others sleep and the devotee keeps vigil. Time at its slowest and most patient: the "all through the night" that tests and proves devotion.

### Time and Literature

Yeats recognized that Tagore's poetry marked a temporal threshold in literary history:

> ...perhaps for the first time in literature, our voice as a people entered into the world's creative literature... (P01-132)

This is "time" in its historical sense — the moment when something new breaks into the world. The *Gitanjali* was such a moment: "for the first time in literature," Indian spiritual poetry spoke with a voice the world could hear.

### Time Remembered

Time is also the medium of memory — the backward glance that gathers the past:

> Another time, remembering how his life had been a strange blending... (P01-177)

"Another time" — the phrase marks a shift in recollection, one moment among many in the narrative of a life. Time as container of memory, the storehouse from which the poet draws.

## Philosophical Background

Tagore's treatment of time resonates with several traditions:

- **Kala in Hindu thought**: *Kala* (time) is both a cosmic force and a divine attribute. In the *Bhagavad Gita*, Krishna declares "I am time" (*kalo 'smi*). Tagore's time is gentler than the destroyer-time of the *Gita*, but it shares the sense of time as a power that envelops human life.
- **Bhakti temporality**: Devotional poetry across traditions treats time as the medium of longing — the *viraha* (separation) that stretches across hours and seasons. The *Gitanjali*'s "times of languor" and "appointed hours" belong to this devotional temporality.
- **Tagore's own synthesis**: Tagore does not seek to escape time into timelessness, as some Vedantic traditions counsel. Instead, he sacralizes time — the journey, the waiting, the arrival — as the very field where the divine-human relationship unfolds.

## Poetic Expression

Tagore's temporal vocabulary is simple — "now," "then," "while," "at last" — but the effect is cumulative. Time words cluster at moments of transition: the threshold between waiting and meeting, between journey and arrival, between life and what lies beyond. The simplicity of the language keeps time from becoming abstract; it remains felt, embodied, urgent.
