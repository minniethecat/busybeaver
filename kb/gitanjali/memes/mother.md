# Mother

The **mother** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the image of nurturing love at its most primal — the one who rocks, who feeds, whose body is the child's first universe. The mother in the poems is not a specific individual but the archetype of care itself, the human form of the divine's tenderness.

## In Gitanjali

### The Mother Rocking

The most tender image of motherhood appears in Poem 4:

> ...even like a mother while rocking her baby's cradle... (004-097)

The mother rocks the cradle — a rhythm as old as human care. The rocking is repetitive, soothing, wordless. It is love expressed as motion, as presence, as the body's own lullaby. The divine's care for the world is "like" this — not abstract but physical, not distant but as close as a mother's arms.

### The Mother and Father

The mother appears in counterpoint to the father:

> ...mother on the day of the father's wrath... (002-194)

The "father's wrath" and the mother's sheltering presence form a pair. The mother stands between the child and the father's anger — not opposing but mediating, protecting. In the *Gitanjali*'s spiritual vocabulary, this maps onto the interplay of divine justice and divine mercy, with the mother embodying the merciful aspect.

### The Young Mother

> ...hidden so long? Yes, when the mother was a young girl it... (004-115)

The mother was once "a young girl" — the image restores the mother's own history, her own life before motherhood. The thing "hidden so long" is traced back to the mother's youth, suggesting that the mother carries mysteries that predate the child's existence.

### The Mother's Form

> ...its arms in the form of my own mother. (006-113)

The divine reaches out "in the form of my own mother." The universal becomes particular: not "a" mother but "my own" mother. The divine wears the face of the one who first held us. This is the *Gitanjali*'s incarnational theology at its most intimate.

### The Right Breast

> ...when from the right breast the mother takes it... (006-116)

The specificity — "the right breast" — grounds the image in bodily reality. The mother's body is the child's first world, and the act of feeding is the first experience of receiving. The divine feeds the soul as the mother feeds the infant: from her own substance, without the child's effort, before the child can even ask.

## The Maternal Divine

In the Bhakti tradition, the divine mother (*Devi*, *Shakti*) is a major theological figure. But Tagore's mothers are not goddesses; they are human mothers whose care images the divine's. The movement is from the human to the divine — "like a mother" — rather than from the divine to the human. The mother is not a symbol of God; God is known through the mother's love.
