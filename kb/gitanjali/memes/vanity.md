# Vanity

**Vanity** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* appears first in a single, devastating line — the poet's self-regard collapsing before the divine presence:

> My poet's vanity dies in shame before thy sight. O master poet, I have sat down at thy feet. [001-068–069]

The line is compact but complete. "My poet's vanity" — the possessive marks it as personal, owned, specific. This is not abstract vanity but the particular pride of the maker: the poet who crafts words, who shapes beauty, who might be tempted to think the gift is his own. "Dies in shame" — the verb is absolute; vanity does not diminish, does not retreat, it dies. And the cause is "thy sight" — the divine gaze, before which all human pretension is exposed as hollow. The address "O master poet" that follows immediately repositions the speaker: he is not the poet but a pupil at the master's feet.

## In Gitanjali

### The Death of Vanity in Poem 1

The line about vanity (001-068) is the culmination of a longer passage in which the speaker's song divests itself of ornament:

> My song has put off her adornments. She has no pride of dress and decoration. Ornaments would mar our union; they would come between thee and me; their jingling would drown thy whispers. [001-065–067]

The song is personified as a woman removing her jewellery before meeting her beloved — an image drawn from the conventions of Indian bridal poetry, where adornment signifies beauty offered to the beloved, yet here the ornaments are *removed* rather than put on. The logic is intimate: decoration obscures; simplicity reveals. The "jingling" that would "drown thy whispers" renders the cost of vanity sensory — it is not morally wrong but acoustically obstructing. Vanity makes it impossible to hear.

> My poet's vanity dies in shame before thy sight. O master poet, I have sat down at thy feet. Only let me make my life simple and straight, like a flute of reed for thee to fill with music. [001-068–070]

The death of vanity is not an end but a beginning. What follows is the image of the flute — hollow, simple, passive — an instrument that produces music only when filled by another's breath. The movement from "my poet's vanity" to "a flute of reed" is the movement from authorship to instrumentality: the poet who claimed the song as his own becomes the reed through which the master's music passes.

### The Pride of the Wall — Poem 2

A contrasting species of vanity appears in poem 2, where the speaker confesses to building a wall of identity:

> He whom I enclose with my name is weeping in this dungeon. I am ever busy building this wall all around; and as this wall goes up into the sky day by day I lose sight of my true being in its dark shadow. I take pride in this great wall, and I plaster it with dust and sand lest a least hole should be left in this name; and for all the care I take I lose sight of my true being. [002-082–087]

Where the poem 1 vanity was the pride of the *maker*, this is the pride of the *builder* — the ego constructing a name, an identity, a reputation. The "great wall" is built to enclose and protect, but it becomes a "dungeon" that imprisons the one it supposedly safeguards. The verbs accumulate: "enclose," "building," "plaster," "lose sight." Vanity here is industry — the busy, productive work of self-construction — and its cost is blindness: "I lose sight of my true being."

### The Pride of Poverty — Poem 2

A subtler form of vanity appears in the closing section of poem 2. The speaker waits for the divine in the posture of a beggar maid:

> I sit like a beggar maid, drawing my skirt over my face, and when they ask me, what it is I want, I drop my eyes and answer them not. Oh, how, indeed, could I tell them that for thee I wait, and that thou hast promised to come. How could I utter for shame that I keep for my dowry this poverty. Ah, I hug this pride in the secret of my heart. [002-203–208]

Here vanity has shape-shifted into the pride of renunciation. The speaker is ashamed to admit she waits for the divine — not because the waiting is shameful but because the admission would expose the intimacy of her devotion. The phrase "I hug this pride in the secret of my heart" reveals the paradox: even the renunciation of worldly status can become a covert form of self-regard. The spiritual ego is the most resilient species of vanity because it disguises itself as humility.

### The Typology of Vanity Across the Poems

Taken together, the three moments trace a typology of vanity in Gitanjali: the maker's pride (poem 1), the builder's pride (poem 2), and the renouncer's pride (poem 2). Each dies differently. The maker's vanity "dies in shame" — a sudden, absolute death brought on by the divine gaze. The builder's vanity collapses in self-recognition — the wall that was meant to display becomes a dungeon that confines. The renouncer's pride is the subtlest: it is not killed but *confessed* — "I hug this pride" — the acknowledgment itself being the beginning of its dissolution.

## Philosophical Background

Vanity in Tagore's sense echoes the Sanskrit *ahamkara* (ego-making) — the faculty that constructs a separate self and invests it with importance. In Vedantic thought, *ahamkara* is the root of bondage; its dissolution is liberation. The Samkhya system identifies it as one of the evolutes of *prakriti* (primordial nature), the point at which undifferentiated consciousness becomes individuated and self-conscious.

Tagore translates this metaphysical insight into a poetic moment: the gaze of the divine does what philosophy cannot — it shames vanity into death, not by argument but by presence. What distinguishes his treatment is the psychological nuance: he recognises vanity not only in its obvious form (the poet's pride of craft) but in its spiritual disguise (the pride of poverty, the secret hugging of renunciation). In this he aligns with the Bhakti tradition's wariness of spiritual pride — the danger that even devotion can become an occasion for self-congratulation.

## Poetic Expression

Tagore's rendering of vanity is built around the body: the "jingling" of ornaments, the sitting "at thy feet," the flute "to fill with music," the wall "going up into the sky," the beggar maid "drawing my skirt over my face," the "hugging" of pride "in the secret of my heart." Vanity is never an abstraction — it is always enacted, always embodied. The death of vanity is correspondingly physical: sitting down, becoming hollow, being seen.

The progression across the passages is from outer to inner: the poet's vanity is public (the song, the performance), the builder's wall is semi-public (a name, a reputation), the beggar maid's pride is wholly interior ("in the secret of my heart"). Tagore's treatment grows more subtle as it moves inward, culminating in the recognition that the most tenacious vanity is the one no one else can see.
