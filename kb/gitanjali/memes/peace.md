# Peace

**Peace** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is a destination, not an absence. It is what the storm seeks, what the [[soul]] arrives at, and what the divine presence bestows. Tagore's peace is not the quiet of an empty room but the deep stillness of a full [[heart]] — the quiet that comes after the arrow has been dropped and the bow laid aside.

## In Gitanjali

### Peace as Storm's Destination

Tagore describes peace not as the opposite of storm but as its intended end:

> ...storm still seeks its end in peace when it strikes against peace... (002-172)

The storm "seeks its end in peace" — peace is not what the storm avoids but what it aims at. The violent energy of the storm is not meaningless; it is a movement toward stillness, a disturbance whose purpose is its own resolution. It "strikes against peace" — peace is not passive but resistant, something that can be struck against.

### Peace as Divine Gift

In the same poem, Tagore images peace as something received from the divine:

> ...my lord of silence, with thy peace and rest. (002-180)

The divine is addressed as "lord of silence" — silence is a name for God. And the gift is "thy peace and rest" — peace that belongs to the divine and is shared with the supplicant. This is peace-as-grace: not self-generated calm but stillness received from beyond the self.

### Peace in the Natural World

Peace is not only a spiritual state; it appears in nature as a quality of the world:

> ...carrying cool draughts of peace in her golden... (005-004)

Nature carries "cool draughts of peace" — peace is refreshment, drink, sustenance. The "golden" quality suggests the peace of early morning or evening light, the peace that belongs to the world when it is at rest.

### The End of Strife

The closing lines of Poem 6 invoke peace as the definitive end of conflict:

> ...dropped the bow and the arrow; peace was... (006-003)

The bow is dropped. The arrow is set aside. The warrior has ceased to fight. "Peace was" — the past tense is significant. Peace arrives as the conclusion of struggle, the state that follows when the instruments of conflict are voluntarily laid down. In the [[gitanjali-song-offerings]], this is the condition of the soul that has surrendered to [[love]].

## Philosophical Background

- **Shanti in the Upanishads**: The peace-invocations (*shanti mantras*) of the Upanishads — "Om shantih shantih shantih" — invoke peace at three levels: the physical, the mental, and the cosmic. Tagore inherits this layered understanding of peace.
- **Bhakti rest**: In the Bhakti tradition, the soul that has found God rests — its seeking is over. Tagore's peace is the peace of arrival, not the peace of avoidance.
- **Poetic quiet**: Tagore's own temperament valued quiet contemplation. The peace of the *Gitanjali* is also the peace of the meditative poet, the still point around which the poems turn.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Storm paradox | "storm still seeks its end in peace" | Peace as fulfillment of disturbance |
| Divine name | "lord of silence" | God named by the stillness that surrounds Him |
| Refreshment image | "cool draughts of peace" | Peace as nourishment, drink |
| End of strife | "dropped the bow and the arrow; peace was" | Peace as voluntary cessation |
