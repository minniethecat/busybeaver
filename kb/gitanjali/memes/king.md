# King

The **king** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the divine in its aspect of majesty — the sovereign who rules not by force but by love, who claims the soul not as subject but as beloved. The kingly address ("my king," "thy signet") coexists with the more intimate names ("my [[friend]]"), and the alternation between them is one of the *Gitanjali*'s deepest structural rhythms.

## In Gitanjali

### The Signet of the King

The most concentrated image of divine kingship appears in Yeats's account:

> ...me, my king, thou didst press the signet... (P01-159)

The signet — the king's seal — is pressed upon the speaker. A signet marks ownership, authenticity, belonging. To be sealed by the king is to be claimed, to bear the mark of the one who has authority over you. But Tagore's king does not press a chain or a yoke; he presses a signet, a mark of identity, a seal that says "this one is mine."

### Day by Day Made Worthy

The speaker's relationship to the king is not static but progressive:

> Day by day thou art making me worthy of the simple, great... (001-141)

> Day by day thou art making me worthy of thy full acceptance... (001-147)

"Day by day" — the process is slow, cumulative, patient. The king does not demand instant worthiness; he makes the speaker worthy over time. The repeated phrase suggests a rhythm, a daily renewal of the work of transformation. Worthiness is not achieved but received, day by day.

### The King Among the Lowly

The king is not always on the throne:

> ...pathmaker is breaking stones. He is with them in sun... (001-102)

The divine king is "with them" — among the laborers, the stone-breakers, the path-makers. This is the paradox of the *Gitanjali*'s theology: the highest is also the lowest, the king is also the servant. The king who presses the signet also breaks stones in the sun.

### The King's Hereditary Pleasure

> ...hands, I find pleasure in thinking it hereditary, a... (P01-187)

The kingship of the divine is "hereditary" — not earned, not contingent, but intrinsic to what God is. The speaker takes "pleasure" in this thought: the king's authority is not fragile, not dependent on performance, but woven into the fabric of reality. It is a kingship one can rest in.

### The King and the Saints

> ...also speaking of the saints, "They build their... (P01-191)

The king's realm includes the saints — those who have given everything for the kingdom. The saints "build" something; their labor contributes to the kingdom's fabric. But the king remains the sovereign; the builders work within a realm they did not create.

## The Bhakti Context

In Vaishnava devotionalism, God as "king" (*raja*, *pati*) is one of the primary relational modes. But Bhakti theology typically subordinates the kingly mode to the modes of friendship (*sakhya*) and parental love (*vatsalya*) — the higher forms of intimacy transcend hierarchy. Tagore's innovation is to make the kingly and the friendly coexist in the same poem, sometimes in the same line, so that the majesty of God and the intimacy of the friend are never fully separable.
