# Grace

**Grace** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is unearned divine gift. It restores what has been lost, bends the infinite toward the finite, and rewards the [[soul]] beyond what it could possibly deserve. Grace is the mode of the divine's movement toward the human — not because the human has earned it, but because the divine is what it is.

## In Gitanjali

### Grace Lost and Restored

The most urgent treatment of grace appears in Poem 37, where the poet feels its absence:

> When grace is lost from life, come with... (002-178)

"Grace is lost from life" — this is a crisis. Without grace, life is deprived of its essential sustenance, its hidden fuel. The poet does not say *if* grace is lost but *when* — implying that the loss of grace is a recurring experience, a rhythm of the spiritual life. What has been lost must be restored, and only the giver of grace can restore it.

### The Cloud of Grace

Tagore images grace as a cloud that descends:

> Let the cloud of grace bend low from above like the... (002-193)

Grace is a "cloud" — something that comes from above, that moves of its own accord, that cannot be grasped or controlled. It "bends low" — the infinite condescends to the finite, the high stoops to the low. This is the central movement of grace: the divine's voluntary descent toward what it has made.

### Our Share in Grace

In a striking passage, Tagore reports a conversation about the proper human response to grace:

> ...kept only our own share in his grace; and then they took their seats... (002-122)

The wise ones "kept only our own share in his grace" — they did not grasp for more than what was given, did not compare their portion to others'. To know one's "own share" and to be content with it is itself a form of wisdom, itself a response to grace. "And then they took their seats" — the posture is one of rest, acceptance, arrival.

### Grace as Reward

The closing poems frame grace as divine reward:

> ...thou rewardest me with thy grace. (005-183)

The "reward" is not earned wages but "thy grace" — the reward *is* grace itself. There is no external compensation; the presence and favor of the divine is the only reward worth having. This is the logic of [[bhakti-devotion]]: love itself is both the means and the end.

## Philosophical Background

- **Prasada in Hindu tradition**: *Prasada* is the material substance — food, flowers, water — that has been offered to the deity and is then distributed to devotees as a blessing. Tagore spiritualizes this concept: grace is the immaterial *prasada* that the divine distributes to all.
- **Bhakti kripa**: In Bhakti theology, *kripa* (grace) is the unearned, freely given love of God. The soul cannot achieve union through its own effort; it can only receive what is given. Tagore's grace poems are Bhakti poems.
- **Christian grace**: Tagore was familiar with Christianity, and the Christian concept of unmerited grace — the free gift of God — resonates through these poems, though filtered through Tagore's distinctively Indian sensibility.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Loss/loss cycle | "When grace is lost from life" | Grace as recurring need, not permanent possession |
| Cloud descent | "cloud of grace bend low from above" | Grace as voluntary divine condescension |
| Share wisdom | "kept only our own share in his grace" | Contentment as response to grace |
| Reward paradox | "rewardest me with thy grace" | Grace is both the gift and the reward |
