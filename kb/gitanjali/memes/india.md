# India

## Overview

India is the homeland of Rabindranath Tagore and the cultural, spiritual, and geographical setting
against which Gitanjali was written. W. B. Yeats, in his Introduction to the English Gitanjali,
repeatedly invokes Indian civilization as the source of Tagore's poetic sensibility, describing
him as the voice of an ancient spiritual tradition encountering the modern world.

## Geography and Context

The Indian subcontinent stretches from the Himalayas to the Indian Ocean. In the early 20th century,
when Tagore composed the poems that became Gitanjali, India was under British colonial rule.
Bengal — Tagore's home region — was a center of the Indian Renaissance, a cultural and intellectual
awakening that sought to reconcile Indian spiritual traditions with modernity.

## In the Introduction to Gitanjali

W. B. Yeats's Introduction (Section 1 / P01) situates Tagore firmly within Indian civilization,
drawing a contrast between the spiritual depth of Indian culture and the materialism of the West:

> while Mr. Tagore, like the Indian civilization itself, has been content to discover the soul
> and surrender himself to its spontaneity. (P01-169–171)

Yeats describes the organic reach of Tagore's work across the subcontinent, noting that
"his songs are sung from the west of India into Burma wherever Bengali is spoken" (P01-030).
The natural world of the poems — "the heavy rain of the Indian July" (P01-124–125) — anchors
Gitanjali's spiritual imagery in a distinctly Indian landscape.

The reverence Tagore commanded among his countrymen struck Yeats as remarkable. He records
that "other Indians came to see me and their reverence for this man sounded strange in our world"
(P01-048–049), observing a cultural attitude toward poets and spiritual figures that differed markedly
from the European norm.

## Connection to Tagore

India was not merely Tagore's birthplace — it was the wellspring of his creative and spiritual
life. Born in Calcutta in 1861, he belonged to a prominent family of the Bengal Renaissance.
His poetry draws on the bhakti devotional tradition, the Upanishads, and the lived experience
of the Indian landscape: its rivers, monsoon rains, village life, and religious festivals.

Tagore's complex relationship with Indian nationalism informed his work. He was knighted by the
British Crown in 1915 but renounced the honor in 1919 after the Jallianwala Bagh massacre,
asserting a moral independence rooted in Indian spiritual values rather than political ideology.

## Cultural Significance

India in Gitanjali represents more than geography — it is a symbol of a civilization that,
in Yeats's reading, had preserved a unity of spiritual and everyday life that the West had lost.
The "Indian traveller" Yeats imagines (P01-018) embodies this: someone who might understand
Tagore's work intuitively, without needing the mediation of literary criticism.

The India of the poems is not exoticized but presented as the natural habitat of a particular
kind of soul — one at home with paradox, reverence, and the intimate address to the divine
that characterizes Gitanjali's most famous verses.
