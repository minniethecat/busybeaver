# Poet

The **poet** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is at once a historical individual — Tagore himself — and an archetypal figure, the singer whose song is not his own but comes through him. Yeats's Introduction to the *Gitanjali* dwells at length on what kind of poet Tagore is, and the poems themselves return repeatedly to the figure of the singer, the maker, the one through whom [[song]] passes into the world.

## In Gitanjali

### The Poet as Vessel

The *Gitanjali*'s opening poem establishes the poet's relationship to his own gift: the divine has "made me endless," and whatever the poet makes, he makes as a vessel, not a source. This humility before the creative act is central to Tagore's self-understanding as a poet:

> My poet's [[vanity]] dies in shame before thy sight. (001-068)

The "poet's vanity" — the pride of craft, the pleasure in one's own making — dies when confronted with the true source of the song. The poet is an instrument, and the instrument does not take credit for the music.

### The Poet in History

Yeats, in his Introduction, places Tagore within a lineage of poets while insisting on his uniqueness:

> ...if a translation of a German poet had moved me, I... (P01-012)

> No poet seems to me as... (P01-028)

The comparison with European poets serves to establish Tagore's stature: this is not a provincial figure but a world-poet. "No poet seems to me as" — the sentence trails into the specific, but the frame is universal. Tagore is being measured against the greats and found equal.

### The Tradition of Poets

Yeats asks about Tagore's place among Bengali poets:

> He answered, "We have other poets, but none that are his..." (P01-027)

The acknowledgment of "other poets" and the assertion that "none are his" — none are his equals — places Tagore at the apex of a living tradition. The poet does not stand alone; he stands at the head of a lineage.

### Poetry and Religion

The Introduction notes a distinctive feature of the Indian tradition: the inseparability of poetry and religion:

> A tradition, where poetry and religion are the... (P01-093)

In this tradition, the poet is also a spiritual figure. The song is also a prayer. The *Gitanjali* — "song-offerings" — embodies this unity in its very title. The poet offers his songs as a devotee offers flowers.

### The Poet's Heart

Yeats describes the movement of Tagore's poetic attention — outward from the self toward the world:

> ...the heart of this poet flows outward to these without... (P01-117)

The poet's heart "flows outward" — not inward in self-absorption but toward others, toward the world, toward the divine. This outward flow is the ethical dimension of Tagore's poetry: the poet does not sing for himself but for those who hear.

## Tagore as Poet

Rabindranath Tagore (1861–1941) was already a celebrated poet in Bengal before the *Gitanjali* brought him to world attention. His self-translated English versions of the Bengali originals won him the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1913 — the first non-European to receive it. The *Gitanjali* represents a particular moment: the poet at the height of his powers, distilling a lifetime of spiritual and artistic practice into brief, luminous song-offerings.

## Poetic Expression

Tagore's understanding of the poet rejects the Romantic model of the artist as autonomous creator. The poet in the *Gitanjali* is:
- A **vessel**: receiving the song rather than inventing it
- A **servant**: offering the song back to its source
- A **witness**: singing what he has seen and heard
- A **friend**: addressing the divine with intimacy
