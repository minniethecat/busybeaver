# Master

**Master** is one of the most frequently recurring terms of divine address in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]*. Unlike more abstract theological names, "Master" (*swami*, *prabhu*) carries a specific relational charge: it implies a bond of service, discipleship, and loving obedience. Tagore's use of this term fuses the [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] tradition's language of divine lordship with the poet's own sense of creative vocation — the divine master is also the "master poet" (001-068) before whom the human artist stands in humility.

## In Gitanjali

### The Master as Singer

Tagore's most characteristic move is to identify the divine master not as a king or judge but as a singer — the source of the cosmic music in which the poet seeks to participate:

> ...know not how thou singest, my master! I ever listen in silent amazement. (001-026)

> ...my master! (001-034)

The poet's position is that of a listener — "I ever listen in silent amazement." He does not claim to understand the master's song, only to be moved by it. This epistemological humility is central to Tagore's theology: the divine is not an object of knowledge but a presence to be received.

### The Master Poet

In Section 1, Tagore introduces a crucial variation: the divine as "master poet," the supreme artist whose creativity the human poet can only dimly reflect:

> ...in shame before thy sight. O master poet, I have... (001-068)

The shame is not moral but creative — the human poet's inadequacy before the source of all [[song]]. By calling the divine a "master poet," Tagore collapses the distance between aesthetic and spiritual categories: the creative act is itself a mode of worship, and God is the ultimate artist.

### Master and Servant

The master-servant relationship structures many poems, but Tagore's version of it is tender rather than oppressive. The master does not command harshly but invites and waits:

> ...midnight, command me, my master, to stand before thee to sing... (001-155)

> ...loud words from me — such is my master's will. Henceforth... (006-046)

The midnight command is not a demand but a permission — "command me... to sing" is the servant's own desire, framed as an obedience. The master's will in Section 6 is to reject "loud words" in favour of something quieter — an aesthetic preference as much as a spiritual one.

### The Parable of the Prisoner

Section 2 contains the most extended narrative treatment of the master-servant relationship:

> "It was my master," said the prisoner. "I thought... (002-098)

This parable — in which a prisoner explains that it was his master who bound him — turns the logic of liberation on its head. The chain is not an enemy's cruelty but the master's mysterious will, carrying a meaning the prisoner only partially understands. This is one of the *Gitanjali*'s most audacious theological gestures: freedom and bondage are not what they appear to be.

### The Master's Hall

A recurring spatial motif is the master's hall — the place from which the master's servants come and go, a threshold between the divine and human realms:

> ...came out first from their master's hall, where had... (005-194)

> ...day they came out from their master's hall. (005-197)

> ...marched back again to their master's hall... (006-001)

The repetition of this image across Sections 5 and 6 creates a sense of ritual movement — servants going out and returning, their lives structured by the master's dwelling-place.

## Philosophical Background

Tagore's "Master" draws from several intertwined traditions:

- **Bhakti *swami-bhava***: In the [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] tradition, the attitude of servant to master (*dāsya-bhāva*) is one of the five primary devotional moods. Tagore's usage reflects this, though he infuses it with an artistic dimension unique to his own sensibility.
- **Sufi *pir-murid***: Tagore was acquainted with Sufi poetry, which similarly uses the master-disciple relationship — particularly through the Baul singers of [[bengal|Bengal]], whose tradition blends Hindu *bhakti* and Sufi elements.
- **Guru-shishya tradition**: The Indian understanding of the guru as a spiritual master who guides the disciple informs Tagore's choice of "master" as the primary divine title.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Vocative intimacy | "my master!" | Possessive pronoun: master belongs to devotee |
| Aesthetic identification | "O master poet" | Divine as supreme artist |
| Paradoxical command | "command me... to sing" | Obedience as liberation to create |
| Parable/narrative | The prisoner and his master (002-098) | Embeds theology in story |
| Spatial framing | "master's hall" | Threshold space between realms |
| Silencing | "such is my master's will. Henceforth..." | Master's will as ending of speech |
