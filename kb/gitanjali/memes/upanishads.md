# Upanishads

The **Upanishads** (*Upaniṣad*, Sanskrit: उपनिषद्) are a collection of ancient Sanskrit philosophical texts that form the concluding portion of the Vedas (*Vedānta*, "end of the Vedas"). They are the foundational scriptures of Hindu philosophy, articulating the concepts of *Brahman* (ultimate reality), *Ātman* (the self), and the path of spiritual realization through knowledge (*jñāna*) and devotion (*[[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]]*). While the word "Upanishad" does not appear explicitly in the *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]*, the Upanishadic worldview is the invisible architecture beneath [[rabindranath-tagore|Tagore]]'s poetry — the framework within which his images of [[light]], [[love]], and divine intimacy acquire their full spiritual resonance.

## Core Teachings

The Upanishads teach that the individual self (*Ātman*) is ultimately identical with the supreme reality (*Brahman*). This identity is expressed in the great sayings (*mahāvākyas*) such as *tat tvam asi* ("That thou art") from the Chāndogya Upanishad and *aham brahmāsmi* ("I am Brahman") from the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upanishad. Realization of this identity — not through intellectual reasoning but through direct experience (*anubhava*) — liberates the self from the cycle of rebirth (*saṃsāra*).

Two complementary paths to realization emerge in the Upanishads:

1. **The path of knowledge** (*jñāna-mārga*): disciplined inquiry into the nature of the self, often expressed through the method of *neti neti* ("not this, not this") — stripping away all that is not the true self.

2. **The path of devotion** (*bhakti-mārga*): approached through love and surrender, developed more fully in later texts like the Śvetāśvatara Upanishad, which introduces the concept of a personal deity (*Īśvara*) to whom the soul relates in devotion.

Tagore's *Gitanjali* draws on both paths, but its dominant register is the second: the intimate, personal address to "my lord," "my king," "my beloved" that transforms Upanishadic non-dualism into [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] poetry.

## The Upanishads in Gitanjali

### Light as Brahman

The most direct Upanishadic inheritance in the *Gitanjali* is the imagery of light. The Upanishads repeatedly describe *Brahman* as light (*jyoti*): the Bṛhadāraṇyaka declares "the Self is light" (*ātmā jyotir bhavati*), and the Chāndogya describes the divine as "the light that shines beyond heaven."

Tagore's most celebrated passage —

> Light, my light, the world-filling light, the eye-kissing light, heart-sweetening light! (004-056–057)

— is a poetic transposition of the Upanishadic *jyoti* into the intimate devotional idiom of [[bengal|Bengali]] Vaiṣṇavism. The triple epithet ("world-filling," "eye-kissing," "heart-sweetening") traces the movement from cosmic to personal that is the signature of Tagore's Upanishad-inflected spirituality.

### The Divine as Immanent

The Upanishads teach that *Brahman* is not a remote creator but the indwelling reality of all existence. Tagore renders this as:

> Thou gavest to me unasked — this sky and the light, this body and the life. (001-142)

The divine gives not through external agency but through the very fact of existence — the sky, the light, the body, the life. This is Upanishadic immanence in the language of gratitude rather than philosophy.

### Poetry and Religion Unified

[[w-b-yeats|Yeats]]'s Introduction identified the Upanishadic inheritance without naming it:

> A tradition, where poetry and religion are the same thing, has passed through the centuries, gathering from learned and unlearned metaphor and emotion. (P01-093)

The "tradition" Yeats describes is, at its deepest root, the Upanishadic understanding that the ultimate truth cannot be communicated through propositional discourse but must be approached through symbol, paradox, and poetic utterance. The *Gitanjali*'s fusion of poetry and religion is not a stylistic choice but a metaphysical commitment inherited from the Upanishads.

## Influence on Tagore

Tagore's father, Debendranath Tagore, was a scholar of the Upanishads and a leader of the Brahmo Samaj, which sought to reform Hinduism by returning to what it saw as the pure monotheism of the Upanishadic texts. Tagore grew up hearing Upanishadic verses recited in the family home and later produced his own Bengali translations of selected Upanishads.

This immersion gave Tagore a spiritual vocabulary that was simultaneously metaphysical and personal. The Upanishadic *Brahman* — impersonal, formless, beyond attributes (*nirguṇa*) — becomes in Tagore's poetry the intimate "lord" and "beloved" without losing its cosmic scope. This synthesis of impersonal metaphysics and personal devotion is perhaps Tagore's most original contribution to modern religious literature.
