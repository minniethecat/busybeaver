# Love

**Love** is the animating emotional force of [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]*, appearing in over forty passages. More than a theme among others, love in the *Gitanjali* is the fundamental mode of relation between the human and the divine — a relation modeled on the intimate bonds of human affection (lover and beloved, friend and friend, child and parent) but oriented toward transcendence. This framework draws on the [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] tradition of medieval [[bengal|Bengal]], in which personal devotion (*bhakti*) rather than ritual or knowledge alone becomes the path to the divine.

## The Bhakti Framework

Tagore's treatment of love is rooted in the [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] tradition, which reimagines the divine-human relationship through the lens of intimate human bonds. In this framework, God is not a distant judge but a beloved (*priyatama*), a friend (*sakhā*), or a lord (*prabhu*) to whom the devotee relates with passionate personal feeling.

> Kindle the lamp of love with thy life. (002-068)

This single line from Section 2 condenses the bhakti ethos: love is not merely felt but enacted — it is a discipline, a kindling that requires the whole self as fuel. The imperative "kindle" (*jvālayatu*) echoes the Vedic fire ritual, transposed from external sacrifice to internal offering.

> Accept only what is offered by sacred love. (001-087)

The qualifier "sacred" (*pavitra*) distinguishes bhakti-love from ordinary attachment. Tagore's love is not desire (*kāma*) but a purified offering — a giving that expects no return. This is the *prem* of the Bengali Vaiṣṇava tradition: love that transcends ego.

## Dimensions of Love

### Lover and Beloved

The most pervasive register of love in the *Gitanjali* is the erotic-mystical. Tagore adopts the voice of the longing lover, and the divine is addressed as the absent-yet-present beloved.

> I am only waiting for love to give myself up at last into his hands. (001-166)

The phrase "give myself up" (*samarpaṇa*) is a technical term in bhakti theology for self-surrender — the final stage of devotion in which the individual will is offered entirely to the divine. Tagore renders this theological concept as a love poem: the devotee waits, like a bride, for the moment of union.

> He calls thee to the love-tryst through the darkness of night. (002-059)

The "love-tryst" (*abhisāra*) evokes the classical Sanskrit and Bengali convention of the trysting lover who braves storms and darkness to meet the beloved — a figure Tagore inherits from Jayadeva's *Gītagovinda* and the Vaiṣṇava *padāvalī* tradition.

### Friend and Companion

Alongside the erotic register, Tagore also addresses the divine as friend and companion — a relationship of equality and daily intimacy that softens the distance implied by lordship.

> Only friend, my best beloved, the gates are open in my house — do not pass by like a dream. (002-019)

The domestic setting ("my house," "gates are open") domesticates the divine encounter. The divine is not sought in temple or pilgrimage but welcomed into the home — a gesture that reflects Tagore's distinctive blend of Upanishadic universality with Bengali domestic spirituality.

### Love of Life and Death

A striking feature of Tagore's love-mysticism is its embrace of death as an extension of love, not its negation.

> "And because I love this life, I know I shall love death as well." (P01-150–151)

W.B. Yeats, in his Introduction to the 1913 edition, singled out this passage as capturing the distinctive temper of Tagore's spirituality: a love so capacious that it encompasses mortality itself. For Tagore, love is not an escape from the finite but a way of inhabiting it so fully that its boundaries dissolve.

### Love as Knowledge

Tagore's love is also epistemological — a way of knowing that exceeds reason.

> In murmuring them, this love of God a magic gulf wherein their own bitterest thoughts (P01-115)

The phrase "magic gulf" suggests that love opens a dimension of experience inaccessible to discursive thought. This is consistent with the Upanishadic view that *Brahman* is known not through reasoning (*tarka*) but through direct realization (*anubhava*), of which love is the affective correlate.

## Comparative Context

| Tradition | Love Concept | Gitanjali Expression |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| Bengali Vaiṣṇavism | *prem* (selfless divine love) | "sacred love" (001-087) |
| Upanishads | *bhakti* as path to *mokṣa* | "give myself up" (001-166) |
| Sufism | *ishq* (passionate longing) | "love-tryst" (002-059) |
| Christian mysticism | *caritas* / bridal mysticism | Bride awaiting groom imagery |

Yeats noted the affinities between the *Gitanjali* and the writings of Thomas à Kempis and John of the Cross (P01-150), suggesting that Tagore's love-mysticism, while culturally rooted in Bengali Vaiṣṇavism, resonates with contemplative traditions across cultures.
