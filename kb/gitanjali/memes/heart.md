# Heart

The **Heart** (*hridaya*) is the central locus of spiritual experience in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]*. It is not merely the seat of emotion but the innermost chamber of the self — the place where the divine enters "unbidden" (P01-158), where [[song]] struggles to be born, and where human limitation is transformed into boundlessness through the touch of [[love]].

## In Gitanjali

### The Heart as Divine Dwelling

Tagore's most radical claim about the heart is that it belongs not to the self but to the divine. The divine enters the heart without permission — and this intrusion is grace:

> "Entering my heart unbidden even as one of the common... (P01-158)

> ...draw my heart on to him; and I know not why... (P01-180)

The heart is not a fortress to be defended but a dwelling to be inhabited. The divine "enters unbidden" — the word "unbidden" capturing both the passivity of the self and the sovereign freedom of the divine. The heart is drawn toward the divine "and I know not why" — the attraction is prior to understanding, deeper than reason.

### The Heart's Transformation

The touch of the divine transforms the heart from a limited container into something boundless:

> ...touch of thy hands my little heart loses its limits in... (001-009)

The heart is "little" — finite, limited, human — but the divine touch makes it lose its limits. This is Tagore's version of the *bhakti* paradox: the self, by surrendering, does not diminish but expands. The heart that is given away becomes larger than the heart that is kept.

### The Heart and Song

The heart is the birthplace of [[song]]. But song does not come easily — the heart struggles, longs, and sometimes breaks in the attempt:

> My heart longs to join in thy song, but vainly struggles for a voice... (001-031)

> ...let me to sing it seems that my heart would break with... (001-014)

The heart "longs to join in thy song" — the divine [[song|music]] is already playing, and the heart's deepest desire is to participate. But the heart "vainly struggles for a voice": the longing outruns the capacity. This gap between desire and ability is not a failure but the very condition of devotional art — the unsung song is the truest offering.

### The Heart Captive

Tagore also figures the heart as a captive — but a willing one:

> Ah, thou hast made my heart captive in the endless meshes... (001-033)

The heart is "captive in the endless meshes" of the divine — an image of entanglement that is paradoxically liberating. To be caught in the divine net is to be freed from all lesser captivities. This is the logic of [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]]: the only true freedom is to be bound to the divine.

### The Restless Heart

The heart that knows the divine can know no rest anywhere else:

> ...from the sight of thy face my heart knows no rest nor respite... (001-049)

The heart's restlessness is the mark of its devotion. Once it has glimpsed the divine face, nothing less will satisfy it. This is both a gift and a burden — the heart is permanently unsettled, permanently longing, permanently alive.

## Philosophical Background

Tagore's treatment of the heart draws from multiple sources:

- **Upanishadic *hridaya***: In the *Upanishads*, the heart (*hridaya*) is the seat of the *atman* — the innermost self that is identical with *Brahman*. Tagore's "little heart loses its limits" echoes this metaphysics of identity.
- **Bhakti *bhava***: In [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] theology, the heart is the locus of *bhava* (devotional emotion) — the place where love for the divine takes root and grows.
- **Yeats's reading**: W.B. Yeats, in his Introduction, noted that "the heart of this poet flows outward to... (P01-117)" — recognising heart as the centre of Tagore's poetic and spiritual energy.

| Captivity | "made my heart captive in endless meshes" | Bondage as freedom |
| Restlessness | "my heart knows no rest nor respite" | Divine love as permanent unsettlement |
