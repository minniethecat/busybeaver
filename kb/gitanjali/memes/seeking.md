# Seeking

In [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]*, **seeking** is not a problem to be solved by finding. It is the soul's native condition — a dynamic rhythm of approach and withdrawal between the human and the divine. Tagore presents seeking as both necessary and ultimately paradoxical: the seeker searches everywhere for what already pervades everything, and the real discovery is not of the object sought but of the seeker's own intimate connection to it.

## In Gitanjali

### The Breath of Summer Seeking Completion

Nature itself models the seeking impulse:

> ...it seemed to me that it was the eager breath of the summer seeking for its completion. (001-200)

"The eager breath of the summer seeking for its completion" — seeking is natural, seasonal, built into the fabric of existence. Summer's breath is "eager," full of vitality, moving toward what it lacks. The speaker recognizes this seeking in himself only after the fact: "I knew not then that it was so near, that it was mine, and that this perfect sweetness had blossomed in the depth of my own heart." The object of seeking was already within — the outward journey was always a journey home.

### The Hiding and Seeking of Thee and Me

Seeking is reciprocal, a divine-human dance:

> The great pageant of thee and me has overspread the sky. With the tune of thee and me all the air is vibrant, and all ages pass with the hiding and seeking of thee and me. (005-052)

"Hiding and seeking of thee and me" — this is not one-sided. The divine hides as much as the human seeks, and the human hides as much as the divine seeks. It is a cosmic game (lila), played out across "all ages." The "great pageant" and "tune" suggest art, performance, music — seeking as the fundamental rhythm of a beautiful universe, not as a symptom of absence.

### Vain Is This Seeking

The most startling declaration about seeking is that it is unnecessary:

> Only in the deepest silence of night the stars smile and whisper among themselves — "Vain is this seeking! unbroken perfection is over all!" (005-127)

"Vain is this seeking! unbroken perfection is over all!" — the stars themselves pronounce the verdict. Seeking is vain not because the goal is unreachable but because the goal is already achieved. "Unbroken perfection" already pervades everything. The stars "smile" — this is not a harsh judgment but a gentle, knowing amusement at human striving. Yet the poem does not counsel stopping; the seeking continues, and the paradox is held rather than resolved.

### Seeking at the Door of the Infinite

Seeking leads to a threshold beyond which the seeker cannot go alone:

> But infinite is thy mansion, my lord, and seeking her I have to come to thy door. (006-020)

"Infinite is thy mansion" — the object of seeking dwells in a space without limits. "Seeking her I have to come to thy door" — the search for what is lost leads not to recovery but to a threshold. The seeker cannot enter the infinite mansion alone; seeking culminates in standing at a door, waiting. This is the end of the seeker's own power: to arrive at the right door, and then to wait.

### The Storm Seeking Peace

Seeking is not only gentle longing but also violent striving:

> As the storm still seeks its end in peace when it strikes against peace with all its might, even thus my rebellion strikes against thy love and still its cry is — I want thee, only thee. (002-172)

"The storm still seeks its end in peace" — the storm is not the opposite of peace but peace's most desperate seeker. The storm "strikes against peace with all its might" — the violence is directed at the very thing it seeks, as if trying to break through to peace by force. "Even thus my rebellion strikes against thy love" — the speaker's rebellion is not rejection of the divine but a storm-like seeking, a love so fierce it looks like opposition. The cry "I want thee, only thee" reveals the truth: beneath rebellion is unstoppable longing.

## Philosophical Background

- **The Upanishadic seeker**: The Mundaka Upanishad speaks of two birds on one tree — one eating the fruit, the other watching. The eating bird (the individual self) seeks satisfaction in the world; the watching bird (the divine self) is already at peace. Seeking ends when the eating bird turns and sees the other. Tagore's seekers move through this same arc: from outward searching to inward recognition.
- **Plato's eros as lack**: In the Symposium, Plato defines eros as the child of Poverty and Resource — always seeking what it lacks. Tagore's seeking shares this structure of lack but transforms its meaning: the lack is not a permanent deficiency but a temporary condition that points toward fullness. The "eager breath of summer" lacks completion but is already in motion toward it.
- **Bhakti's viraha (longing in separation)**: In bhakti poetry, viraha — the pain of separation from the beloved divine — is not a problem but the highest spiritual state. The soul's seeking is itself a form of union, because the longing keeps the beloved present in the lover's awareness. Tagore inherits this tradition: seeking is intimacy, not absence.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Natural metaphor | "eager breath of the summer seeking" | Seeking as organic, seasonal, universal |
| Reciprocal pairing | "hiding and seeking of thee and me" | Seeking as mutual divine-human dance |
| Cosmic pronouncement | "Vain is this seeking! unbroken perfection is over all!" | The stars undercut the seeker's striving |
| Spatial threshold | "seeking her I have to come to thy door" | Seeking culminates in arrival at a liminal space |
| Paradoxical revelation | "perfect sweetness had blossomed in the depth of my own heart" | What was sought was already within |
