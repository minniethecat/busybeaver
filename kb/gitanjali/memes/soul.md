# Soul

The **soul** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is not the isolated ego but the innermost self — that which waits to be awakened, filled, and set free. The soul appears throughout the poems as the seat of the deepest encounter between the human and the divine. It is what [[love]] encloses, what [[joy]] awakens, and what the vastness of the infinite sky invites to flight.

## In Gitanjali

### The Awakened Soul

The soul in the *Gitanjali* is often described as awakening — stirred from its ordinary slumber by the touch of divine joy:

> The first thrill of joy to my awakened soul let it come from his... (003-078)

The awakening is not achieved by effort but received as gift. "The first thrill of joy" — the inaugural experience of the spiritual life — is what brings the soul to consciousness. Before joy strikes, the soul is dormant; afterward, it is alive and responsive.

### The Soul Enclosed by Love

Tagore describes the soul as enveloped, held, and contained by divine [[love]]:

> ...is thy love that encloses the soul (004-191)

This enclosure is protective rather than confining. The soul is "enclosed" as a seed is enclosed by soil — not imprisoned but nurtured, given the conditions in which it can grow. The love that encloses is not possessive; it is generative.

### The Soul and Its Infinite Horizon

Poem 5 uses the image of the sky — vast, unbounded — to evoke the soul's true horizon:

> ...leads the infinite sky for the soul to take her (005-006)

The sky is "infinite" — the soul is invited to "take her" flight within it. The soul's proper element is not the confined space of self-interest but the boundless expanse of the divine. This infinity is not a threat to the soul's identity but its fulfillment.

### The Soul Discovered

Yeats, in his Introduction, captures Tagore's spiritual project with precision:

> ...been content to discover the soul and surrender himself to its (P01-170)

Tagore is "content to discover the soul" — not to construct it, not to improve it, but to find what is already there. The posture is receptive rather than active. And the discovery leads to surrender: to "surrender himself" to the soul's own deepest inclination, which is toward the divine.

### The Flesh-Soul Unity

Tagore does not separate soul from body in the manner of extreme asceticism:

> ...flesh and the cry of the soul seems one, forsake it harshly... (P01-139)

The "cry of the soul" and the "flesh" are so intertwined they "seem one." To "forsake it harshly" would be a mistake. The embodied life is not an obstacle to the soul's journey but its vehicle.

## Philosophical Background

Tagore's concept of the soul draws from several traditions:

- **Upanishadic Atman**: The Upanishads teach that the individual self (*atman*) is ultimately one with the universal self (*Brahman*). Tagore inherits this vision, though he emphasizes the personal and relational dimensions more than the purely monistic.
- **Bhakti devotionalism**: In the Bhakti tradition, the soul relates to God through love and surrender, not through intellectual realization alone. Tagore's soul is a *bhakta*'s soul — defined by its longing and its capacity for relationship.
- **Tagore's own humanism**: Tagore resists the radical world-denial of some Indian traditions. The soul in the *Gitanjali* is embodied, relational, and finds the divine through [[beauty]], [[song]], and human [[love]] — not by escaping the world but by seeing it rightly.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Awakening | "thrill of joy to my awakened soul" | Soul as dormant until touched |
| Enclosure | "thy love that encloses the soul" | Divine love as protective container |
| Infinite horizon | "infinite sky for the soul to take her" | Soul's true element is boundlessness |
| Discovery | "content to discover the soul" | Receptivity over activity |
| Embodied soul | "flesh and the cry of the soul seems one" | No radical dualism |
