# Night

**Night** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is not a time of fear or absence but of vigilance, intimacy, and hidden encounter. Where [[morning]] is the time of divine arrival, night is the time of waiting and watching — the soul keeping "starry vigil" (001-187) for the beloved who comes under cover of darkness. Night is also the time of [[song]]: the poet wakes at midnight to sing, the harp sounds in the stillness, and the "music of the night" calls the soul toward its tryst.

## In Gitanjali

### Night as Vigil

The most concentrated treatment of night appears in Section 2, where the poet keeps watch through the darkness:

> ...keep still and wait like the night with starry vigil... (001-187)

> silent as night, eluding all watchers. (002-013)

Night is personified as a watcher — patient, silent, full of stars. The soul is to imitate night's vigilance: not straining but waiting, "still" and watchful. This is an active passivity, a discipline of receptivity that is the opposite of the daytime's doing and striving.

### The Night Journey

Night is also the time of the divine beloved's journey — a risky, storm-crossed passage that the poet observes with concern:

> ...art thou abroad on this stormy night on thy journey of love, my friend? (002-022)

> I have no sleep tonight. Ever and again I open my door... (002-024)

The poet's sleeplessness is not insomnia but anxious vigilance — he keeps opening the door, watching for the beloved who travels through the storm. The "stormy night" is a figure for the difficulty of the divine-human relationship: love's journey is never easy, and it often takes place in conditions of obscurity and risk.

### Night and the Tryst

Night is the appointed time for the meeting between lover and beloved — the tryst:

> ...tryst through the darkness of night. (002-059)

> ...path to where the music of the night calls... (002-063)

The tryst is a central motif in [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] poetry: the soul as lover, the divine as beloved, meeting in secret under cover of darkness. The "music of the night" that calls the soul is a different register from the daytime's songs — quieter, more intimate, audible only to those who are listening.

### The Veil of Night

Tagore repeatedly images night as a veil drawn by the divine:

> ...thou who drawest the veil of night upon the tired eyes of the day... (002-044)

> ...under the cover of thy kindly night. (002-038)

Night is "kindly" — a cover of kindness, not a threat. The veil it draws over the "tired eyes of the day" is a mercy: the day has worked enough; now is the time for rest and hidden communion. This is consistent with Tagore's larger pattern of inverting negative symbols: like [[death]], night is transformed from something to be feared into something to be welcomed.

### Midnight Song

Night is not only for silent vigil; it is also the time when the poet is commanded to sing:

> ...midnight, command me, my master, to stand before thee to sing... (001-155)

Midnight singing is an image of absolute devotion — the poet is willing to perform his art at the most inconvenient hour, at the [[master]]'s command. The song sung at midnight is purer than the daytime's songs: it is offered when no one else is listening, for the master alone.

## Philosophical Background

Night holds a specific place in Indian spiritual traditions:

- **Upanishadic darkness**: The *Isha Upanishad* speaks of those who worship darkness entering "blinding darkness" — but Tagore inverts this, treating night as a positive spiritual condition. His night is closer to the *Brihadaranyaka*'s description of the self in deep sleep: at peace, unified, beyond duality.
- **Bhakti *viraha***: In [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] poetry, night is the classic time of *viraha* — the pain of separation from the beloved. Tagore's night carries this resonance but inflects it with more hope: the beloved *will* come.
- **Tagore's own nocturnes**: Many of Tagore's songs are set at night. His personal practice of late-night composition infuses the poems with an authenticity that goes beyond literary convention.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Personification | "night with starry vigil" | Night as watcher, model for the soul |
| Veil/cover imagery | "drawest the veil of night" | Night as kindly concealment |
| Tryst motif | "tryst through the darkness of night" | Night as meeting-place of lovers |
| Storm contrast | "stormy night on thy journey of love" | Difficulty of the divine-human bond |
| Sleeplessness | "I have no sleep tonight" | Vigilance as spiritual discipline |
