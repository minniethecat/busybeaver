# Surrender

**Surrender** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the opposite of what the word suggests in ordinary usage. It is not weakness yielding to superior force but strength voluntarily offered to love. It is not the cessation of will but its redirection — "surrender my strength to thy will with love." Tagore transforms surrender from a military metaphor (defeat, capitulation) into a devotional one (offering, gift, trust). The soul that surrenders does not become less; it becomes more, because what it surrenders to is its own deepest source.

## In Gitanjali

### The Strength That Surrenders

The definitive statement on surrender comes in poem 36, the great prayer of petition:

> And give me the strength to surrender my strength to thy will with love. (002-156)

"Give me the strength to surrender my strength" — the paradox is precise. Surrender is not something the weak can do; it requires strength. One must possess strength in order to give it away. The prayer asks for a meta-strength: the capacity to yield what one has. "To thy will with love" — the object of surrender is not blind fate but a personal will, and the mode of surrender is not resignation but love. Surrender without love would be mere submission; surrender with love is gift.

This line is the climax of a series of petitions: "Give me the strength lightly to bear my joys and sorrows. Give me the strength to make my love fruitful in service. Give me the strength never to disown the poor or bend my knees before insolent might" (002-152). Each petition asks for strength for a different kind of relationship — to circumstances, to others, to power. The final petition — surrender — is the summary: all these strengths must ultimately be given back to their source.

### The Strike at the Root

The prayer begins with a request that reframes surrender as surgery:

> This is my prayer to thee, my lord — strike, strike at the root of penury in my heart. (002-149)

"Strike, strike" — the repetition conveys urgency, even violence. Before strength can be surrendered, the "root of penury" must be struck. Penury here is "penury in my heart" — spiritual poverty, the soul's tendency to hoard, to cling, to hold back. Surrender requires the destruction of what resists surrender. The "root" must be struck because surface remedies are insufficient; only a blow at the deep source of self-centeredness can free the heart for gift.

### The Surrender of Mind

In poem 47, surrender takes a different form — not the active offering of strength but the passive yielding of consciousness:

> I forgot for what I had travelled, and I surrendered my mind without struggle to the maze of shadows and songs. (003-100)

"I forgot for what I had travelled" — surrender begins with forgetting the original purpose, releasing the grip of intention. "I surrendered my mind without struggle" — this is surrender as letting go, not forcing. There is "no struggle" because struggle would contradict surrender. The mind yields "to the maze of shadows and songs" — to beauty, to mystery, to the non-linear paths of aesthetic experience. This is not surrender to a commanding will but to an enchanting presence.

The context is crucial: the speaker has been mocked by companions who "held their heads high and hurried on" (003-096) while he lay down by the water. "I gave myself up for lost in the depth of a glad humiliation" (003-098) — the surrender is a "glad humiliation," a willing abandonment of the ego's projects. And the result? "When I woke from my slumber and opened my eyes, I saw thee standing by me, flooding my sleep with thy smile" (003-101). Surrender leads to vision. The divine was there all along, but only visible when the striving stopped.

### The Soul's Spontaneity

Yeats, in his Introduction, identifies surrender as the core of Tagore's spiritual posture:

> ...like the Indian civilization itself, has been content to discover the soul and surrender himself to its spontaneity. (P01-170)

"Content to discover the soul and surrender himself to its spontaneity" — surrender is not to an external authority but to the soul's own deepest nature. "Spontaneity" suggests that surrender is not the end of freedom but its beginning; the soul surrendered to its own truth moves freely, naturally, without the friction of self-will. Yeats contrasts this with those "who have loved more after our fashion, and have more seeming weight in the world" (P01-171) — the world's idea of achievement is accumulation and effort; the soul's idea is surrender and flow.

### The Overflowing Cup

Though the word "surrender" does not appear in poem 65, the image of the overflowing cup is surrender's perfect metaphor: "What divine drink wouldst thou have, my God, from this overflowing cup of my life?" (004-166). The cup is not drained by force; it overflows by its own fullness. Surrender is not depletion but overflow — the soul so full that it must give, must offer, must pour itself out. The divine drinks — and the drinking is itself a form of gift, because God's thirst validates the soul's abundance.

## Philosophical Background

- **Prapatti in Srivaishnavism**: *Prapatti* (self-surrender) is a central concept in Srivaishnava theology — the total yielding of the self to God's grace as the sole means of liberation. Unlike the "monkey way" (active effort), prapatti is the "cat way" — the soul is carried by God as a kitten is carried by its mother, in complete trust.
- **Bhagavad Gita's karma yoga**: Krishna teaches Arjuna to act without attachment to the fruits of action — "abandon all dharmas and take refuge in me alone." This is surrender as the culmination of all spiritual paths: not the abandonment of action but the abandonment of the ego's claim on results.
- **The paradox of freedom through surrender**: In the Christian mystical tradition, Dante's *Paradiso* speaks of the soul's will becoming one with God's will — "in His will is our peace." Tagore's "strength to surrender my strength" echoes this paradox: the greatest freedom is found in the alignment of the finite will with the infinite.
- **Taoist wu-wei**: The concept of *wu-wei* (non-action, effortless action) in Taoism shares with Tagore's surrender the idea that the highest form of action is the one that does not force, that flows with the grain of reality rather than against it.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Paradoxical petition | "give me the strength to surrender my strength" | Surrender as active, requiring power, not passive collapse |
| Surgical imagery | "strike, strike at the root of penury in my heart" | Surrender requires prior destruction of resistance |
| Yielding without struggle | "I surrendered my mind without struggle" | Surrender as letting go, not forcing — effortless yielding |
| Overflow metaphor | "this overflowing cup of my life" | Surrender as abundance, not depletion |
| Glad humiliation | "I gave myself up for lost in the depth of a glad humiliation" | Surrender as joyful defeat of the ego |
