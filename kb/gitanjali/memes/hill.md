# Hill

The **hill** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the elevated place — the "lonely places of hills" where delight dwells, the rise and fall of the land the reed is carried across, the upland the travelers cross among meadows. The hills of the *Gitanjali* are not the dramatic mountains of the Himalayas but the gentler elevations of [[bengal]]'s landscape — the undulations, the rises, the high ground that offers a different view of the [[sky]].

## In Gitanjali

### The Lonely Places of Hills

Yeats evokes the landscape from which Tagore's poetry emerges:

> ...delight in the lonely places of hills, in... (P01-155)

"Lonely places of hills" — the hills are solitary, set apart from the crowded world of villages and towns. Delight is found in their loneliness, in the quiet of elevation. The hill is the place of retreat, of withdrawal from the plain of ordinary life, where the poet finds the solitude necessary for song.

### Over Hills and Dales

> ...a reed thou hast carried over hills and dales... (001-007)

"Over hills and dales" — the reed (the poet, the flute) is carried across the varied landscape. Hills and dales together represent the fullness of the terrain, the ups and downs of the journey. The hill is the ascent, the effortful part of the way, but the carrier does not falter — the reed is borne over every rise.

### Across Meadows and Hills

> ...They crossed many meadows and hills, and passed... (003-094)

"Crossed many meadows and hills" — the plural suggests a long journey over varied ground. Hills are what must be crossed, not avoided; they are part of the way, not obstacles to it. The landscape of the spiritual journey includes both the level meadow and the rising hill, and the travelers traverse both.

## The Gentle Hill

Tagore's hills are not the sublime peaks of Romantic poetry. They are the gentle elevations of the Bengal landscape — the lateritic uplands of Birbhum where [[shantiniketan]] sits, the rolling terrain between the Ganges delta and the Chota Nagpur plateau. These are hills one can walk over, hills that vary the horizon rather than dominate it. In the *Gitanjali*, the hill is the modest rise that offers a modest transcendence — enough elevation to see a little farther, enough solitude to hear the inner music.
