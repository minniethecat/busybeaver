# Death

**Death** occupies a singular place in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]*, where it is repeatedly addressed not as an adversary but as a familiar, even a beloved. Tagore's treatment of mortality draws deeply on the [[upanishads|Upanishadic]] conviction that death is a passage rather than a terminus, and his poems transform the conventional fear of dying into an attitude of expectant openness.

## In Gitanjali

### Death as Fulfilment

The most striking feature of death in the *Gitanjali* is its positive valence. Tagore repeatedly figures death as the consummation of life — the moment when the soul is released from its earthly constraints into union with the divine.

> On the day when death will knock at thy door what wilt thou offer to him? (006-059)

> O thou the last fulfilment of life, Death, my death, come and whisper to me! (006-065–067)

In Section 6, the speaker addresses death directly in the vocative — "my death" — claiming it as an intimate possession rather than an external threat. This is a radical departure from the elegiac tradition, and it situates Tagore's spiritual vision within the *bhakti* framework: death, like the divine beloved, is something to be awaited with love and preparation.

### Death and Divine Love

Tagore's willingness to embrace death is inseparable from his understanding of divine love. In the Introduction, Yeats quotes a passage that became central to the reception of the *Gitanjali* in the West:

> ...life, I know I shall love death as well. (P01-151)

The grammar here is significant: "I shall love death" is a promise, not a resignation. The verb "love" places death in the same category as the divine beloved — something to be received with the same openness as [[love]] itself.

### Death and Darkness

Tagore also associates death with darkness, but not in a purely negative sense. The storm and darkness of death are expressions of divine will, to be accepted rather than resisted:

> ...and thy angry storm, dark with death, if it is thy wish, and with... (002-189)

> ...all my strife. Thou hast left death for my... (003-173)

The shroud of dust and death is acknowledged as something the speaker "hates" (002-076), but this hatred is not the final word. The movement of the poems is consistently toward acceptance and even welcome.

### Death, Life, and the Twin Motif

Throughout the *Gitanjali*, death and life are paired as complementary forces — twins dancing together across creation:

> ...the twin brothers, life and death, dancing over the wide world... (004-071)

> Through birth and death, in this world or in others... (004-139)

The image of life and death as "twin brothers" dancing together is one of Tagore's most compressed theological statements. It denies the opposition between living and dying that structures much Western thought, replacing it with a vision of rhythmic alternation — an insight drawn from the cyclical cosmology of Indian philosophy.

## Philosophical Background

Tagore's approach to death is shaped by several layers of Indian thought:

- **Upanishadic non-dualism**: The *Katha Upanishad* in particular takes death (Yama) as its central interlocutor, teaching Nachiketa that the self (*ātman*) does not die. Tagore's "death, my death" echoes this intimate address to Yama.
- **Bhakti devotionalism**: In the [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] tradition, death is the moment of ultimate *milan* (union) — the soul's release into the divine, akin to the bride joining the bridegroom.
- **Tagore's personal experience**: Tagore experienced the deaths of his mother, his wife, two daughters, and a son. His writing on death is not abstract philosophizing but a hard-won spiritual stance forged through personal grief.

## Poetic Expression

Tagore renders death in a distinctive poetic register that blends intimacy, natural imagery, and paradox:

| Poetic Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------------|---------|--------|
| Vocative address | "my death, come and whisper to me" | Intimacy; death as person |
| Paradox | "the last fulfilment of life" | Reverses conventional expectation |
| Dance imagery | "twin brothers... dancing" | Death as rhythmic, not catastrophic |
| Question form | "what wilt thou offer to him?" | Draws reader into spiritual preparation |
| Light imagery | "I see by the light of death" (006-084) | Death as a mode of vision, not blindness |

> ...and I see by the light of death thy world with its careless... (006-084)

The phrase "by the light of death" is characteristic of Tagore's inversions: death is not darkness but a new kind of illumination — a way of seeing the world stripped of its "careless" distractions.
