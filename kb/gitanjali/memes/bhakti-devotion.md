# Bhakti (Devotion)

## Overview

Bhakti — loving devotion to the divine — is the central spiritual thread running through all 103 poems of Gitanjali. Rooted in the Indian Bhakti tradition, Tagore's devotional poetry expresses the soul's yearning, surrender, and intimate relationship with God. The poet's voice alternates between humble supplicant, eager lover, and blissful devotee, creating what Yeats described as "the heart turning to God" (P01-123).

## In Gitanjali

Devotion permeates every poem of Gitanjali, expressed through recurring images of offering, worship, song, and surrender. The poet-devotee brings songs as offerings to the divine beloved, as in poem 2: "When thou commandest me to sing it seems that my heart would break with pride" (001-014). The act of worship is not confined to temples — it unfolds in the quiet intimacy of the heart: "the hour strikes for thy silent worship at the dark temple of" (001-154). The devotee offers everything — songs, life, love, and ultimately the self — in complete surrender: "give me the strength to surrender my strength to thy will with love" (002-156).

The bhakti relationship in Gitanjali is profoundly personal. The divine is addressed as "thou," "my lord," "my master," "my king," "my friend" — sometimes even as a child. Poem 8 speaks of the mother-child bond as a metaphor for divine care (001-072), while poem 62 depicts the devotee's impatience for union: "I weary of waiting in vain" (004-060). This intimate, emotional register distinguishes Tagore's bhakti poetry from more abstract theological discourse.

## Philosophical Background

Tagore's devotional poetics draw from multiple streams of Indian spirituality. The Bhakti movement, which swept across medieval India through poet-saints like Kabir, Nanak, and Chaitanya, emphasized personal devotion over ritual orthodoxy. The Upanishadic concept of the Atman (individual soul) seeking union with Brahman (universal soul) provides the metaphysical framework. Tagore's own family — leaders of the Brahma Samaj reform movement — shaped his understanding of a universal, non-sectarian divinity accessible through love and song rather than priestly mediation (P01-044).

Yeats noted in his Introduction that Tagore's Bengali contemporaries described him as "the first among our saints who has not refused to live, but has spoken out of Life itself" (P01-041). This embodied spirituality — finding the divine in everyday experience, in nature, in human relationships — is the hallmark of Gitanjali's bhakti.

## Poetic Expression

Tagore renders devotion through a rich vocabulary of natural and domestic imagery. The soul is a "little flute of a reed" through which the divine breathes "melodies eternally new" (001-007). Devotion is a flower offered at the divine feet (001-057), a lamp lit in darkness, a vessel emptied and refilled (001-005). The poet-devotee is a singer whose "song has put off her adornments" — stripped of vanity, simple and straight "like a flute of reed for thee to fill with music" (001-065).

The emotional range of bhakti in Gitanjali spans from ecstatic joy ("Drunk with the joy of singing I forget myself and call thee friend who art my lord" (001-023)) to profound humility ("Pride can never approach to where thou walkest in the clothes of the humble among the poorest, and lowliest, and lost" (001-093)). This oscillation between intimacy and awe, between the beloved and the worshipper, creates the distinctive texture of Tagore's devotional voice.
