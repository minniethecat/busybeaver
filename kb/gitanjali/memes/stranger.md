# Stranger

The **stranger** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is the figure through whom the illusion of separateness is dissolved. The stranger appears at the door, at the threshold, at the edge of the familiar — and is revealed to be kin. What first appears as otherness turns out to be the deepest form of identity: the stranger was never a stranger at all.

## In Gitanjali

### The Door Closed Against Strangers

Yeats observes a cultural habit of guardedness:

> ...often had to close it lest some stranger would... (P01-087)

Yeats is describing his own room in London, where passing "Indian students" and visitors would drop in. The door is closed against "some stranger" — the unnamed, the unknown, the potentially threatening. This is the ordinary human posture: protect the interior, guard the threshold, keep the stranger out. But the *Gitanjali*'s spiritual vision moves in the opposite direction — toward opening, toward welcome, toward the recognition that the stranger may be the divine in disguise.

The irony is that Yeats himself was, in a sense, a stranger to the world of the *Gitanjali* — an Irish poet encountering Bengali spirituality in translation. His own guardedness mirrors the human instinct to protect what is familiar. The *Gitanjali* invites him (and its readers) to do the opposite.

### The Brother Who Was a Stranger

The central revelation about the stranger comes in Poem 4:

> ...a brother of the stranger. (004-135)

The stranger is "a brother" — not merely tolerated or accepted but recognized as kin. The movement from stranger to brother is the movement from fear to love, from separation to belonging. The word "brother" carries the full weight of family obligation: the stranger is not a guest but family, owed everything that family is owed.

The phrase reverses the expected logic. We normally think of a "stranger" as someone who might *become* a friend or a brother through acquaintance. But Tagore's formulation — "a brother of the stranger" — suggests that brotherhood is prior to recognition. The stranger was always a brother; the only thing that changed was the speaker's perception. The spiritual life is not about transforming others but about recognizing what they already are.

### No Stranger in This World

The final word on the stranger is the dissolution of the category itself:

> ...was no stranger in this world, that the inscr... (006-112)

"No stranger in this world" — the statement is absolute. If there is no stranger, then everyone is kin. The "inscrutable" — the mystery of existence — is not a stranger but something known, something familiar at a depth below conscious recognition. The world is not alien; it is home, populated by brothers and sisters who have not yet been recognized.

The three passages together trace a spiritual progression: from closing the door (P01-087), through recognizing the stranger as brother (004-135), to the dissolution of the category of stranger altogether (006-112). This is the *Gitanjali*'s spiritual arc in miniature — a movement from separation through recognition to unity.

## Philosophical Background

The stranger (*atithi* in Sanskrit) holds a sacred place in Indian culture — the unexpected guest is to be treated as a god, an idea encoded in the formula *atithi devo bhava* ("the guest is God"). The guest appears at the door anonymous and unexpected; the householder's duty is to welcome without asking for credentials. This cultural imperative is built on a theological insight: the divine often comes in disguise, and to refuse a stranger is to risk refusing God.

Tagore radicalizes this. The stranger is not merely to be served but to be recognized as already one's own. The boundary between self and other, familiar and strange, is a convention the spiritual life dissolves. Where the *atithi* tradition asks for hospitality, Tagore's *Gitanjali* asks for recognition — for seeing through the appearance of strangeness to the underlying brotherhood.

The Upanishadic background is also relevant. The insight "tat tvam asi" (that thou art) — that the deepest self of every being is identical with the ground of all being — provides the metaphysical foundation for Tagore's dissolution of the stranger. If all selves share a single Self, then there can be no radical otherness; "stranger" is a category error, a failure of perception. The *Gitanjali*'s treatment of the stranger is a poetic rendering of this Upanishadic truth.

## Connection to Gitanjali

The stranger is not merely a theme in the *Gitanjali*; it is embedded in the volume's history. The poems were written in Bengali by an Indian poet, translated into English with the help of an Irish poet, and first published in London for a Western audience. The *Gitanjali* itself crossed boundaries — linguistic, cultural, religious — and its success depended on strangers recognizing something of themselves in Tagore's verses.

Yeats, in his Introduction, enacts his own movement toward the stranger. He begins with suspicion — a "stranger" from an unfamiliar tradition — and ends with admiration and gratitude. His Introduction is, in a sense, the story of a door being opened. The *Gitanjali* transformed Yeats from one who closed his door against strangers into one who recognized a brother in Tagore.

## Legacy

The stranger figure in *Gitanjali* has resonated in subsequent discussions of cosmopolitanism, postcolonial literature, and interfaith dialogue. Tagore's insistence that there is "no stranger in this world" — that otherness is a failure of recognition rather than a fact — offers a spiritual grounding for the cosmopolitan ideal. It is not merely that we should tolerate strangers; it is that there are no strangers to tolerate.
