# Life

**Life** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is not merely biological existence but a spiritual condition — the gift of divine presence that "fills ever with fresh life" (001-006) and transforms the mundane into the sacred. As the twin companion of [[death]], life is understood as one phase in a rhythmic cosmic alternation, to be embraced fully in the present while also surrendered at the appointed time.

## In Gitanjali

### Life as Divine Gift

Tagore's characteristic move is to frame life not as an autonomous possession but as something continually bestowed. The opening of Section 1 sets this tone:

> ...and fillest it ever with fresh life. (001-006)

> Life of my life, I shall ever try to keep my... (001-036)

The phrase "Life of my life" — a characteristic Tagorean construction — places the divine at the very centre of one's own existence. Human life is not self-sustaining; it is a vessel that the divine continually replenishes. This understanding transforms the speaker's relationship to existence: life is received, not achieved.

### Life and Death as Twins

Tagore's most distinctive theological statement on life comes through its pairing with death. They are not opposites but companions:

> ...the twin brothers, life and death, dancing over the wide world... (004-071)

> Through birth and death, in this world or in others... (004-139)

> ...life, I know I shall love death as well. (P01-151)

The dance imagery is critical: life and death are in motion together, inseparable, neither one dominating. The promise to "love death as well" as life is not morbid — it is the logical extension of a worldview in which all of existence, including its ending, is a manifestation of divine [[love]].

### Life in the World

Tagore does not retreat from worldly life into pure abstraction. The poems affirm the goodness of embodied existence — the sensory world of [[light]], sound, and relationship — while also insisting that this life points beyond itself.

> ...and thy angry storm, dark with death, if it is thy wish... (002-189)

> ...final stroke of death; it shines like the pure flame... (004-011)

The "pure flame" of death's stroke illuminates life retrospectively. Life's value is not diminished by its ending but is concentrated and revealed by it — a perspective that resonates with the [[upanishads|Upanishadic]] teaching that the finite and the infinite are not opposed but layered.

## Philosophical Background

Tagore's understanding of life draws from multiple streams:

- **Upanishadic cosmology**: The cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (*samsāra*) is the backdrop against which Tagore's "life" unfolds. But Tagore's emphasis is not on escape from the cycle (as in classical *moksha*) but on full, loving engagement with life as it is given.
- **Bhakti devotionalism**: In the [[bhakti-devotion|bhakti]] tradition, life is the arena of *līlā* (divine play) — the field in which the soul enacts its relationship with the divine. Tagore's "twin brothers, life and death, dancing" echoes this *līlā* framework.
- **Tagore's humanism**: Tagore was not an ascetic. His essays and lectures consistently affirm the value of creative work, sensory experience, and human relationship. The poems reflect this: life is to be sung, not renounced.

## Poetic Expression

Tagore renders life through several recurring poetic strategies:

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Direct address | "Life of my life" | Collapses distance between self and divine |
| The dance metaphor | "twin brothers... dancing over the wide world" | Life as rhythmic, dynamic, not static |
| Gift/filling imagery | "fillest it ever with fresh life" | Life as something received, not owned |
| Music/song | "life breath of thy music runs from sky to sky" (001-028) | Life as participation in cosmic harmony |
| Paradox | "the last fulfilment of life, Death" (006-065) | Life's meaning revealed by its ending |

> ...life breath of thy music runs from sky to sky... (001-028)

The breath of life and the breath of music are one — a synesthetic collapse that is characteristic of Tagore's poetic method. Life itself is a kind of song, and the [[song]] that the poet sings participates in this larger cosmic music.
