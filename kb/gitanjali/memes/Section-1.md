[P01-001][[gitanjali-song-offerings|Gitanjali]]

[P01-002][[song|Song]] Offerings

[P01-003]by [[rabindranath-tagore|Rabindranath Tagore]]

[P01-004]A collection of prose translations made by the author from the original

[P01-005][[bengal|Bengali]]

[P01-006]With an introduction by

[P01-007]W. B. YEATS

[P01-008]TO

[P01-009]WILLIAM ROTHENSTEIN

[P01-010]INTRODUCTION

[P01-011]A few days ago I said to a distinguished Bengali doctor of medicine, “I

[P01-012]know no German, yet if a translation of a German poet had moved me, I

[P01-013]would go to the British Museum and find books in English that would

[P01-014]tell me something of his life, and of the history of his thought. But

[P01-015]though these prose translations from Rabindranath Tagore have stirred

[P01-016]my blood as nothing has for years, I shall not know anything of his

[P01-017]life, and of the movements of thought that have made them possible, if

[P01-018]some Indian traveller will not tell me.” It seemed to him natural that

[P01-019]I should be moved, for he said, “I read Rabindranath every day, to read

[P01-020]one line of his is to forget all the troubles of the world.” I said,

[P01-021]“An Englishman [[life|living]] in London in the reign of Richard the Second had

[P01-022]he been shown translations from Petrarch or from Dante, would have

[P01-023]found no books to answer his questions, but would have questioned some

[P01-024]Florentine banker or Lombard merchant as I question you. For all I

[P01-025]know, so abundant and simple is this poetry, the new renaissance has

[P01-026]been born in your country and I shall never know of it except by

[P01-027]hearsay.” He answered, “We have other poets, but none that are his

[P01-028]equal; we call this the epoch of Rabindranath. No poet seems to me as

[P01-029]famous in Europe as he is among us. He is as great in music as in

[P01-030]poetry, and his songs are sung from the west of [[india|India]] into Burma

[P01-031]wherever Bengali is spoken. He was already famous at nineteen when he

[P01-032]wrote his first novel; and plays when he was but little older, are

[P01-033]still played in [[calcutta|Calcutta]]. I so much admire the completeness of his

[P01-034]life; when he was very young he wrote much of natural objects, he would

[P01-035]sit all day in his garden; from his twenty-fifth year or so to his

[P01-036]thirty-fifth perhaps, when he had a great sorrow, he wrote the most

[P01-037]beautiful love poetry in our language,” and then he said with [[ocean|deep]]

[P01-038]emotion, “words can never express what I owed at seventeen to his love

[P01-039]poetry. After that his art grew deeper, it became religious and

[P01-040]philosophical; all the inspiration of mankind are in his hymns. He is

[P01-041]the first among our saints who has not refused to live, but has spoken

[P01-042]out of Life itself, and that is why we give him our love.” I may have

[P01-043]changed his well-chosen words in my memory but not his thought. “A

[P01-044]little while ago he was to read [[holy|divine]] service in one of our

[P01-045]churches—we of the Brahma Samaj use your word ‘church’ in English—it

[P01-046]was the largest in Calcutta and not only was it crowded, but the

[P01-047]streets were all but impassable because of the people.”

[P01-048]Other Indians came to see me and their reverence for this man sounded

[P01-049]strange in our world, where we hide great and little things under the

[P01-050]same veil of obvious comedy and half-serious depreciation. When we were

[P01-051]making the cathedrals had we a like reverence for our great men? “Every

[P01-052]morning at three—I know, for I have seen it”—one said to me, “he sits

[P01-053]immovable in contemplation, and for two hours does not awake from his

[P01-054]reverie upon the nature of [[divine|God]]. His father, the Maha Rishi, would

[P01-055]sometimes sit there all through the next day; once, upon a river, he

[P01-056]fell into contemplation because of the beauty of the landscape, and the

[P01-057]rowers waited for eight hours before they could continue their

[P01-058]journey.” He then told me of Mr. Tagore’s family and how for

[P01-059]generations great men have come out of its cradles. “Today,” he said,

[P01-060]“there are Gogonendranath and Abanindranath Tagore, who are artists;

[P01-061]and Dwijendranath, Rabindranath’s brother, who is a great philosopher.

[P01-062]The squirrels come from the boughs and climb on to his knees and the

[P01-063]birds alight upon his hands.” I notice in these men’s thought a sense

[P01-064]of visible beauty and meaning as though they held that doctrine of

[P01-065]Nietzsche that we must not believe in the moral or intellectual beauty

[P01-066]which does not sooner or later impress itself upon physical things. I

[P01-067]said, “In the East you know how to keep a family illustrious. The other

[P01-068]day the curator of a museum pointed out to me a little dark-skinned man

[P01-069]who was arranging their Chinese prints and said, “That is the

[P01-070]hereditary connoisseur of the Mikado, he is the fourteenth of his

[P01-071]family to hold the post.’” He answered, “When Rabindranath was a boy he

[P01-072]had all round him in his home literature and music.” I thought of the

[P01-073]abundance, of the simplicity of the poems, and said, “In your country

[P01-074]is there much propagandist writing, much criticism? We have to do so

[P01-075]much, especially in my own country, that our minds gradually cease to

[P01-076]be creative, and yet we cannot help it. If our life was not a continual

[P01-077]warfare, we would not have taste, we would not know what is good, we

[P01-078]would not find hearers and readers. Four-fifths of our energy is spent

[P01-079]in the quarrel with bad taste, whether in our own minds or in the minds

[P01-080]of others.” “I understand,” he replied, “we too have our propagandist

[P01-081]writing. In the villages they recite long mythological poems adapted

[P01-082]from the Sanskrit in the Middle Ages, and they often insert passages

[P01-083]telling the people that they must do their duties.”

[P01-084]II

[P01-085]I have carried the manuscript of these translations about with me for

[P01-086]days, reading it in railway trains, or on the top of omnibuses and in

[P01-087]restaurants, and I have often had to close it lest some stranger would

[P01-088]see how much it moved me. These lyrics— which are in the original, my

[P01-089]Indians tell me, full of subtlety of rhythm, of untranslatable

[P01-090]delicacies of colour, of metrical invention—display in their thought a

[P01-091]world I have dreamed of all my live long. The work of a supreme

[P01-092]culture, they yet appear as much the growth of the common [[earth|soil]] as the

[P01-093]grass and the rushes. A tradition, where poetry and religion are the

[P01-094]same thing, has passed through the centuries, gathering from learned

[P01-095]and unlearned metaphor and emotion, and carried back again to the

[P01-096]multitude the thought of the scholar and of the noble. If the

[P01-097]civilization of Bengal remains unbroken, if that common mind which—as

[P01-098]one divines—runs through all, is not, as with us, broken into a dozen

[P01-099]minds that know nothing of each other, something even of what is most

[P01-100]subtle in these verses will have come, in a few generations, to the

[P01-101]beggar on the roads. When there was but one mind in England, Chaucer

[P01-102]wrote his _Troilus and Cressida_, and thought he had written to be

[P01-103]read, or to be read out—for our time was coming on apace—he was sung by

[P01-104]minstrels for a while. Rabindranath Tagore, like Chaucer’s forerunners,

[P01-105]writes music for his words, and one understands at every [[time|moment]] that he

[P01-106]is so abundant, so spontaneous, so daring in his passion, so full of

[P01-107]surprise, because he is doing something which has never seemed strange,

[P01-108]unnatural, or in need of defence. These verses will not lie in little

[P01-109]well-printed books upon ladies’ tables, who turn the pages with

[P01-110]indolent hands that they may sigh over a life without meaning, which is

[P01-111]yet all they can know of life, or be carried by students at the

[P01-112]university to be laid aside when the work of life begins, but, as the

[P01-113]generations pass, travellers will hum them on the [[road|highway]] and men

[P01-114]rowing upon the rivers. Lovers, while they await one another, shall

[P01-115]find, in murmuring them, this love of God a magic gulf wherein their

[P01-116]own more bitter passion may bathe and renew its youth. At every moment

[P01-117]the heart of this poet flows outward to these without derogation or

[P01-118]condescension, for it has known that they will understand; and it has

[P01-119]filled itself with the circumstance of their lives. The traveller in

[P01-120]the read-brown clothes that he wears that dust may not show upon him,

[P01-121]the girl searching in her bed for the petals fallen from the wreath of

[P01-122]her royal lover, the servant or the bride [[waiting|awaiting]] the [[king|master]]’s

[P01-123]home-coming in the empty [[home|house]], are images of the heart turning to God.

[P01-124]Flowers and rivers, the blowing of conch shells, the heavy rain of the

[P01-125]Indian July, or the moods of that heart in union or in separation; and

[P01-126]a man sitting in a boat upon a river playing lute, like one of those

[P01-127]figures full of mysterious meaning in a Chinese picture, is God

[P01-128]Himself. A whole people, a whole civilization, immeasurably strange to

[P01-129]us, seems to have been taken up into this imagination; and yet we are

[P01-130]not moved because of its strangeness, but because we have met our own

[P01-131]image, as though we had walked in Rossetti’s willow wood, or heard,

[P01-132]perhaps for the first time in literature, our voice as in a dream.

[P01-133]Since the Renaissance the writing of European saints—however familiar

[P01-134]their metaphor and the general structure of their thought—has ceased to

[P01-135]hold our attention. We know that we must at last forsake the world, and

[P01-136]we are accustomed in moments of weariness or exaltation to consider a

[P01-137]voluntary forsaking; but how can we, who have read so much poetry, seen

[P01-138]so many paintings, listened to so much music, where the cry of the

[P01-139]flesh and the cry of the soul seems one, forsake it harshly and rudely?

[P01-140]What have we in common with St. Bernard covering his eyes that they may

[P01-141]not dwell upon the beauty of the lakes of Switzerland, or with the

[P01-142]violent rhetoric of the Book of Revelations? We would, if we might,

[P01-143]find, as in this book, words full of courtesy. “I have got my leave.

[P01-144]Bid me farewell, my brothers! I bow to you all and take my departure.

[P01-145]Here I give back the keys of my door—and I give up all claims to my

[P01-146]house. I only ask for last kind words from you. We were neighbours for

[P01-147]long, but I received more than I could give. Now the day has dawned and

[P01-148]the lamp that lit my dark corner is out. A summons has come and I am

[P01-149]ready for my journey.” And it is our own mood, when it is furthest from

[P01-150]A Kempis or John of the Cross, that cries, “And because I love this

[P01-151]life, I know I shall love death as well.” Yet it is not only in our

[P01-152]thoughts of the parting that this book fathoms all. We had not known

[P01-153]that we loved God, hardly it may be that we believed in Him; yet

[P01-154]looking backward upon our life we discover, in our exploration of the

[P01-155]pathways of [[forest|woods]], in our [[joy|delight]] in the lonely places of hills, in

[P01-156]that mysterious claim that we have made, unavailingly on the woman that

[P01-157]we have loved, the emotion that created this insidious sweetness.

[P01-158]“Entering my heart unbidden even as one of the common crowd, [[stranger|unknown]] to

[P01-159]me, my [[master|king]], thou didst press the signet of eternity upon many a

[P01-160]fleeting moment.” This is no longer the [[sacred|sanctity]] of the cell and of the

[P01-161]scourge; being but a lifting up, as it were, into a greater intensity

[P01-162]of the mood of the painter, painting the dust and the sunlight, and we

[P01-163]go for a like voice to St. Francis and to William Blake who have seemed

[P01-164]so alien in our violent history.

[P01-165]III

[P01-166]We write long books where no page perhaps has any quality to make

[P01-167]writing a pleasure, being confident in some general design, just as we

[P01-168]fight and make money and fill our heads with politics—all dull things

[P01-169]in the doing—while Mr. Tagore, like the Indian civilization itself, has

[P01-170]been content to discover the soul and surrender himself to its

[P01-171]spontaneity. He often seems to contrast life with that of those who

[P01-172]have loved more after our fashion, and have more seeming weight in the

[P01-173]world, and always humbly as though he were only sure his [[journey|way]] is best

[P01-174]for him: “Men going home glance at me and smile and fill me with shame.

[P01-175]I sit like a beggar maid, drawing my skirt over my face, and when they

[P01-176]ask me, what it is I want, I drop my eyes and answer them not.” At

[P01-177]another time, remembering how his life had once a different shape, he

[P01-178]will say, “Many an hour I have spent in the strife of the good and the

[P01-179]evil, but now it is the pleasure of my playmate of the empty days to

[P01-180]draw my heart on to him; and I know not why this sudden call to what

[P01-181]useless inconsequence.” An innocence, a simplicity that one does not

[P01-182]find elsewhere in literature makes the birds and the leaves seem as

[P01-183]near to him as they are near to [[child|children]], and the changes of the

[P01-184]seasons great events as before our thoughts had arisen between them and

[P01-185]us. At times I wonder if he has it from the literature of Bengal or

[P01-186]from religion, and at other times, remembering the birds alighting on

[P01-187]his brother’s hands, I find pleasure in thinking it hereditary, a

[P01-188][[wonder|mystery]] that was growing through the centuries like the courtesy of a

[P01-189]Tristan or a Pelanore. Indeed, when he is speaking of children, so much

[P01-190]a part of himself this quality seems, one is not certain that he is not

[P01-191]also speaking of the saints, “They build their houses with sand and

[P01-192]they play with empty shells. With withered leaves they weave their

[P01-193]boats and smilingly float them on the vast [[sea|deep]]. Children have their

[P01-194]play on the [[shore|seashore]] of worlds. They know not how to swim, they know

[P01-195]not how to cast nets. Pearl fishers dive for pearls, merchants sail in

[P01-196]their ships, while children gather pebbles and scatter them again. They

[P01-197]seek not for hidden treasures, they know not how to cast nets.”
