# Bondage

## Overview

In Tagore's Gitanjali, *bondage* appears as a layered metaphor operating across multiple dimensions: the external confinement imposed by material ornamentation and social convention, the inherent limitation of language in expressing spiritual truth, and — most profoundly — the self-imposed imprisonment of the ego. The concept is framed within the Bhakti tradition's rejection of outward religiosity in favour of inward freedom, yet Tagore's treatment extends beyond inherited doctrine into a distinctly modern, psychological register.

## In Gitanjali

Tagore explores the image of bondage in several distinct but interlocking contexts within the Gitanjali poems:

### Bondage of Finery

In poem 1, the speaker addresses a maternal figure, likening worldly adornment to chains that separate the soul from authentic human experience:

> Mother, it is no gain, thy bondage of finery, if it keep one shut off from the healthful dust of the earth, if it rob one of the right of entrance to the great fair of common human life. (001-077)

The passage follows the image of a child "decked with prince's robes" and "jewelled chains" who "loses all pleasure in his play" — his dress "hampers him at every step" (001-072–076). The "bondage of finery" thus represents the spiritual cost of attachment to external forms, echoing the Bhakti emphasis on inner devotion over ritual display.

### Bondage of Laws and Codes

In the closing section of poem 1, the speaker confronts a second form of bondage — the imposition of social and moral codes:

> They come with their laws and their codes to bind me fast; but I evade them ever, for I am only waiting for love to give myself up at last into his hands. (001-168–169)

The juxtaposition is striking: to be "bound" by laws and codes is cast as evasion of a deeper surrender — the giving of oneself "into his hands." Tagore thus inverts the conventional moral polarity: the escape *from* social constraint is not lawlessness but a higher fidelity to love. The repetition of "I am only waiting for love" (001-166, 001-168, 001-174) underscores this single-minded orientation.

### The Self-Forged Chain — Poem 2 Parable

The most psychologically acute treatment of bondage appears in poem 2, which unfolds as a dramatic parable. A divine questioner interrogates a prisoner:

> "Prisoner, tell me, who was it that bound you?"
>
> "It was my master," said the prisoner. "I thought I could outdo everybody in the world in wealth and power, and I amassed in my own treasure-house the money due to my king. When sleep overcame me I lay upon the bed that was for my lord, and on waking up I found I was a prisoner in my own treasure-house." (002-097–102)

The questioner presses further:

> "Prisoner, tell me, who was it that wrought this unbreakable chain?"
>
> "It was I," said the prisoner, "who forged this chain very carefully. I thought my invincible power would hold the world captive leaving me in a freedom undisturbed. Thus night and day I worked at the chain with huge fires and cruel hard strokes. When at last the work was done and the links were complete and unbreakable, I found that it held me in its grip." (002-103–108)

This passage marks a turning point in Tagore's treatment of bondage: the chains are neither imposed from without nor inherited through metaphysical fate — they are *self-forged*. The prisoner's admission "It was I" transforms bondage from a condition endured into an act of self-construction. The phrase "I thought my invincible power would hold the world captive" reveals the irony at the heart of the parable: the will to dominate others binds the self.

### Bondage of Words

In poem 42, the poet envisions a state of perfect communion beyond language:

> In that shoreless ocean, at thy silently listening smile my songs would swell in melodies, free as waves, free from all bondage of words. (003-016–017)

The image of songs "free as waves" suggests that the deepest spiritual reality lies beyond verbal articulation — a theme resonant with the Upanishadic notion that ultimate truth (*Brahman*) cannot be captured in speech. The boat with its "chains" (003-021), waiting to be loosed, reinforces the motif of liberation as the dissolution of all bonds.

### Release Through Love

The poem 2 parable ends with a resolution. The speaker reflects that whereas human love seeks to "hold me secure" (002-111), divine love operates differently:

> By all means they try to hold me secure who love me in this world. But it is otherwise with thy love which is greater than theirs, and thou keepest me free. (002-111–113)

The contrast is absolute: earthly affection binds; divine love releases. This final turn ties the bondage theme back to the Bhakti framework — liberation is not achieved through effort but received through grace.

## Philosophical Background

The concept of bondage (*bandha* or *bandhana* in Sanskrit) has deep roots in Indian philosophical traditions. In Vedanta, bondage is understood as *avidya* (ignorance) that binds the individual self (*jivatman*) to the cycle of rebirth. The Samkhya-Yoga tradition elaborates five *kleshas* (afflictions) — ignorance, egoism, attachment, aversion, and clinging to life — that together constitute the mechanism of bondage.

Tagore's treatment in Gitanjali softens this metaphysical framework into a poetic sensibility: bondage is less a cosmological condition than an existential and psychological barrier — finery, words, social codes, and ultimately the ego's own ambition — that must be shed for authentic spiritual communion. The poem 2 parable, in particular, enacts a psychological interiority absent from the doctrinal sources: the prisoner is his own jailer, the chain his own handiwork.

## Poetic Expression

Tagore renders bondage through concrete sensory imagery rather than abstract declaration. The "jewelled chains" (001-076) are tangible and weighty; the "bondage of words" (003-017) is contrasted with the fluid, unbounded movement of ocean waves; the self-forged chain is built "with huge fires and cruel hard strokes" (002-107) — labour so vivid it becomes the bodily inscription of spiritual error. This preference for the sensory over the doctrinal is characteristic of Tagore's poetic method throughout Gitanjali.

The movement across the three poems traces a progression: from externally imposed fetters (finery, in poem 1) through social compulsion (laws and codes, also poem 1) to the deepest form — the inward chain (poem 2) that only recognition can dissolve. The poem 3 coda gestures toward release: will the chains "be off, and the boat, like the last glimmer of sunset, vanish into the night?" (003-021–022).
