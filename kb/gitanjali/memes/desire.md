# Desire

**Desire** in [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]* is an ambivalent force. Unpurified, it blinds the mind with delusion and multiplies into a thousand distracting wants. But when touched by the divine, desire ripens into [[love]]. Tagore does not reject desire — he seeks its transformation. The same fire that burns can illumine; the same longing that scatters the soul can gather it toward God.

## In Gitanjali

### The Poverty of Many Desires

The poet confesses the scattering power of desire:

> My desires are many and my cry is pitif... (001-138)

"Many" desires — plural, dispersed, each one pulling the soul in a different direction. "My cry is pitiful" — the voice of desire is a cry, not a song. It is needy, not generous. The confession is honest: desire makes the soul small, pitiable, pulled apart by its own wanting.

### The Perils of Desire

Tagore prays for protection from desire's excess:

> ...ng me from perils of overmuch desire. (001-143)

"Perils of overmuch desire" — desire itself is not the enemy; *overmuch* desire is. The danger is not wanting but wanting too much, wanting indiscriminately, wanting without limit. The prayer is for moderation, for the wisdom to want well.

### Desire That Blinds

Unchecked desire becomes a pathology of perception:

> When desire blinds the mind with delusion... (002-183)

Desire "blinds the mind with delusion" — it makes the soul see what is not there, chase what cannot satisfy. This is the language of *maya* (illusion): desire weaves a false world and persuades the soul to live in it. The mind under desire's spell cannot see clearly; it can only want.

### The Ripening of Desire

But desire is not only destructive. In Tagore's most hopeful image, it transforms:

> desires ripen into fruits of love. (005-073)

"Ripen into fruits of love" — this is the alchemy. Desire is the raw, green fruit; love is the ripe one. The difference is not substance but time and tending. Desire is love before it has matured, before it has been touched by grace, before it has learned to give rather than grasp.

### The Deepening of Desire

The final image is of desire deepening into something vast:

> ...n that deepens into loves and desires, into (005-190)

Desire "deepens" — it does not disappear but becomes more itself, more profound, more capacious. Deep desire is indistinguishable from love. The movement is not from desire to something else but from shallow desire to deep desire, from wanting *things* to wanting *God*.

## Philosophical Background

- **Kama in Hindu thought**: *Kama* (desire) is one of the four *purusharthas* (aims of life), alongside *dharma* (duty), *artha* (prosperity), and *moksha* (liberation). Desire is not denied but ordered — it has its proper place and its proper object.
- **Bhakti sublimation**: In Bhakti theology, desire for earthly things (*kama*) can be redirected toward God (*prema*). The energy of wanting is not extinguished but redirected. Tagore's image of desire "ripening" into love is a Bhakti image.
- **Plato and eros**: Tagore's understanding of desire as a ladder — beginning with the body and rising toward the divine — resonates with the Platonic tradition, though Tagore's version is more organic (ripening) than ascetic (climbing).

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Plural confession | "My desires are many" | Desire as dispersion, scattering |
| Peril language | "perils of overmuch desire" | Desire as danger to be navigated |
| Blindness metaphor | "desire blinds the mind with delusion" | Desire as epistemological failure |
| Ripening image | "desires ripen into fruits of love" | Desire as love in potential |
| Deepening movement | "deepens into loves and desires" | Desire transformed, not eliminated |
