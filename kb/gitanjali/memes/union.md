# Union

In [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s *[[gitanjali-song-offerings]]*, **union** with the divine is not the dissolution of the self into an undifferentiated absolute. It is the meeting of lover and beloved, a relationship in which difference is preserved even as separation is overcome. Tagore's union is intimate rather than absorptive, requiring the stripping away of ornaments and pretensions, and finding its consummation in "the perfect union of two" — not the erasure of one.

## In Gitanjali

### Ornaments Would Mar Our Union

Union requires simplicity, the removal of what comes between:

> My song has put off her adornments. She has no pride of dress and decoration. Ornaments would mar our union; they would come between thee and me; their jingling would drown thy whispers. (001-066)

"Ornaments would mar our union" — the things that decorate, that make impressive, are precisely what obstruct. The "jingling" of ornaments "would drown thy whispers" — the divine speaks quietly, and adornment is noise. Union requires silence, simplicity, the courage to be unadorned. "My poet's vanity dies in shame before thy sight" — the poet's pride in craft, in beauty of expression, is itself an ornament that must be shed. "Only let me make my life simple and straight, like a flute of reed for thee to fill with music." The goal is not to be beautiful but to be hollow, empty, available — a flute through which the divine can breathe.

### The Perfect Union of Two

Union is not oneness but twoness perfected:

> And for this thy love loses itself in the love of thy lover, and there art thou seen in the perfect union of two. (004-054)

"The perfect union of two" — this is Tagore's most precise formulation. Not the union that reduces two to one, but the union that perfects the relationship of two who remain two. "Thy love loses itself in the love of thy lover" — the divine does not absorb the human; the divine love "loses itself" in the human's love, a mutual surrender. Union is not a merger but a mutuality so complete that the boundary between giving and receiving, loving and being loved, dissolves — yet the lovers remain distinct.

### Union and Separation as a Single Rhythm

Union is understood through its opposite, not as its negation but as its context:

> Flowers and rivers, the blowing of conch shells, the heavy rain of the Indian July, or the moods of that heart in union or in separation... (P01-125)

"Moods of that heart in union or in separation" — union and separation are moods of the same heart, not mutually exclusive states. The heart moves between them as a musician moves between major and minor keys. Union is not the permanent achievement that ends all separation; it is one pole of a rhythm, one phase of a dance. The "heavy rain of the Indian July" — nature's cycle of drought and deluge — is the cosmic analogue: union and separation alternate like seasons.

### The Meeting Not Yet

Union exists first as hope, as deferred consummation:

> I live in the hope of meeting with him; but this meeting is not yet. (001-136)

"The hope of meeting with him; but this meeting is not yet" — union is anticipated before it is achieved. The "not yet" is not a failure but a structure of spiritual life: one lives *toward* union, and the living-toward is itself meaningful. The line is stark in its simplicity — no ornament, no metaphor, just the bare statement of hope and deferral. This is union as horizon, always approached, never reached, yet the approaching transforms the approacher.

### Coming Nearer to Meet

The divine approaches across vast distances of time:

> I know not from what distant time thou art ever coming nearer to meet me. Thy sun and stars can never keep thee hidden from me for aye. (003-057)

"Thou art ever coming nearer to meet me" — union is not a human achievement but a divine approach. The meeting is initiated from the other side, from "what distant time" the speaker cannot even name. "Thy sun and stars can never keep thee hidden" — cosmic distances, the vastness of space and time, cannot obscure the one who approaches. The "sun and stars" that might seem to separate are themselves witnesses to the approach. Union is not something the soul grasps but something it receives, a meeting the divine has been moving toward across ages.

## Philosophical Background

- **Yoga as union**: The Sanskrit word *yoga* literally means "yoke" or "union" — the joining of the individual self (*jivatman*) with the universal self (*paramatman*). In classical Vedanta, this union is understood as the recognition that the two were never actually separate. Tagore's bhakti-inflected version preserves the duality: union is relational, a joining of lover and beloved who remain themselves.
- **The bhakti paradox**: Bhakti theology holds that the highest state is not dissolution (*sayujya*) but proximity (*samipya*) — being near the divine, in relationship. Complete absorption would end the love-relationship, because there would be no lover left to love. Tagore's "perfect union of two" embodies this paradox: the perfection of union includes the preservation of the two who unite.
- **Buber's I-Thou**: Martin Buber distinguished between I-It relationships (instrumental, objectifying) and I-Thou relationships (mutual, present, sacred). Tagore's union is an I-Thou relationship raised to its highest power — a meeting in which each addresses the other as a subject, a presence, never as an object to be absorbed or used.

## Poetic Expression

| Strategy | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Negative definition | "Ornaments would mar our union" | Union defined by what obstructs it, not what constitutes it |
| Acoustic imagery | "their jingling would drown thy whispers" | The divine speaks quietly; noise prevents union |
| Instrument metaphor | "simple and straight, like a flute of reed" | The self as hollow vessel for divine music |
| Paradoxical number | "perfect union of two" | Perfection preserves duality, does not erase it |
| Mood alternation | "moods of that heart in union or in separation" | Union and separation as phases, not opposites |
