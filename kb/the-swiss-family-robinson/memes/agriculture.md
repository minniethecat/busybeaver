# Agriculture

Agriculture — the deliberate cultivation of plants for food, fibre, and other uses — is the material foundation of the family's island civilization. While hunting and gathering sustain them initially, it is agriculture that transforms their existence from precarious survival to settled prosperity, embodying the colonial ideal of "improving" the land through European methods.

## Definition

In the novel, agriculture encompasses the full cycle of cultivation: seed collection, soil preparation, planting, tending, harvesting, processing, and storage. The family brings European agricultural knowledge to bear on tropical flora, growing both familiar crops (maize, potatoes, wheat) and novel ones (manioc, sago, sugarcane). Agriculture is presented as the natural and proper use of land — the transition from wilderness to garden.

## Manifestations in the Narrative

**The Kitchen Garden.** The family establishes a garden early in their settlement, planting European seeds salvaged from the wreck alongside tropical discoveries. The garden becomes a site of both practical production and moral instruction, with the father using agricultural metaphors for character development.

**Tropical Discoveries.** The novel's agricultural content doubles as natural history instruction. The discovery of sugarcane, manioc, sago palm, and other tropical cultigens provides occasions for lessons in botany, processing techniques, and comparative agriculture.

**Agriculture as Civilization.** The family's agricultural projects mark their progress from castaways to colonists. The father frames cultivation as a duty: making the land productive is what distinguishes civilized Europeans from the "savages" who merely inhabit wilderness. This ideology, while historically revealing, is central to the novel's worldview.

## Key Passages

> "A variety of useful things, which Providence seems to have reserved for our use." (011-013)

## Thematic Significance

Agriculture in the novel is never merely practical. It is presented as the fulfillment of the Genesis mandate to "subdue the earth" — a religious and moral act as much as an economic one. The family's farming success validates their providential interpretation of events: God has not merely rescued them but provided a land they can cultivate and improve.

## Historical Context

The novel's detailed agricultural content reflects the early 19th-century surge of interest in tropical agriculture, driven by European colonial expansion. Botanical gardens, agricultural societies, and publications on tropical crops proliferated in this period. Wyss incorporates this knowledge into the novel, making agriculture both practical instruction and entertainment for young readers.
