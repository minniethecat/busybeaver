# Prayer

Prayer — both petitionary and thanksgiving — is the central religious practice in *The Swiss Family Robinson*, structuring each day and framing every crisis as an occasion for divine appeal. From the opening storm scene to the family's settled island life, prayer is presented as the natural and necessary response of the faithful to every circumstance.

## Definition

In the novel, prayer encompasses both formal morning and evening family worship and spontaneous crisis prayer. The father leads the family in prayer; his wife and children participate. Prayer is consistently linked to improved emotional state: after praying, characters are described as "more tranquil" (001-003) and possessed of "an improved serenity" (004-069).

## Manifestations in the Narrative

**Crisis Prayer.** The novel opens with prayer amidst catastrophe. As the ship founders, the sailors, who had previously been swearing, "were now employed in prayers, or in making the absurdest vows" (001-002). The father leads his family in formal supplication: "We all fell on our knees, and supplicated the God of mercy to protect us" (001-003). Throughout the terrible night, the parents "passed this terrible night in prayer, in agonizing apprehensions" (001-016).

**Daily Ritual.** Once safely ashore, prayer becomes a daily institution. The father begins each morning with: "When we had finished our morning prayer, I said, We now, my best beloved" (002-002). The rhythm of prayer structures the family's day, and the youngest child reminds the family when they forget: "Ernest, we have not said our prayers this morning" (003-008).

**Prayer and Emotional Regulation.** Prayer consistently produces emotional transformation. After the evening prayer on their first night on land, the family retires "with an improved serenity" (004-069). Prayer is presented not merely as religious duty but as psychological resource — the mechanism by which fear is transmuted into calm.

**Thanksgiving Prayer.** As the family prospers, the balance shifts from petition to thanksgiving. The father reports "offering up our earnest prayers for the safety and happiness" (005-040) of all, and later promises: "today we will repeat the prayers, and sing one of those affecting hymns" (012-014).

## Key Passages

> "We all fell on our knees, and supplicated the God of mercy to protect us; and the emotion and the fervour of the innocent creatures are a convincing proof, that even in childhood, devotion may be felt and understood, and that its effects, tranquillity and consolation, may be no less certainly experienced." (001-003)

> "We passed this terrible night in prayer, in agonizing apprehensions, and in forming various resolutions as to what we should next attempt." (001-016)

> "When we had finished our morning prayer, I said, We now, my best beloved, must not give way to despondency." (002-002)

## Thematic Significance

Prayer serves as the novel's primary vehicle for demonstrating the father's pastoral role within the family. In the absence of a church or clergy, the father becomes the family's spiritual leader, conducting worship and modelling piety. This reflects the Protestant emphasis on the family as a "little church" (*ecclesiola*) and the father as its domestic priest.

The children's participation in prayer — including the youngest Francis reminding the family of its omission — serves to naturalize religious practice. Prayer is portrayed not as an adult imposition but as something even a six-year-old intuitively understands and values. This is consistent with the novel's pedagogical project: to present Christianity as natural, reasonable, and appealing to children.

## Historical Context

The daily family prayer practice depicted in the novel reflects the *Hausandacht* (household devotion) tradition of German-speaking Protestantism. In early 19th-century Switzerland, daily family prayer was widely practiced and considered a marker of serious Christian household. Wyss, as a pastor, would have led such prayers in his own family and encouraged them in his congregation. The novel's depiction thus serves both a descriptive and prescriptive function — showing what is and advocating what should be.
