The Crocodile features in The Swiss Family Robinson through a memorable episode of misidentification: Jack, searching for water among sun-scorched rocks, excitedly reports having found a crocodile — an impossibility in such a dry location. The animal turns out to be a large iguana, and the ensuing capture becomes one of the novel's most entertaining hunting episodes. The crocodile-that-isn't exemplifies the gap between the boys' imagination of exotic dangers and the island's more benign reality.

## Jack's "Crocodile"

While the family searches for water during an inland expedition, Jack — who has run ahead to search among the rocks — calls out that he has found a crocodile. The father greets this claim with hearty skepticism:

> A crocodile! cried I with a hearty laugh, you have a fine imagination, my boy! who ever saw a crocodile on such scorching rocks as these, and with not a drop of water near? [021-021]

Jack persists, insisting the creature is "about as long as the height of our mother" and "certainly a crocodile, though perhaps only a young one" [021-021]. The father approaches cautiously with the family and discovers the truth: "instead of a crocodile I saw before me an individual of a large sort of lizard, named by naturalists Leguana or Iguana, and the flesh of which is considered in the West Indies as the greatest delicacy" [021-021].

## The Iguana Capture

What follows is an elaborate and comical capture operation. The father prevents Fritz from shooting the creature — "your piece might have missed, or you might have wounded him only slightly; for this sort of animal is protected by a coat of scales" — and instead orchestrates a live capture [021-021]. The iguana proves to be a valuable prize: its flesh, the father notes, is "considered in the West Indies as the greatest delicacy," transforming Jack's embarrassing misidentification into a culinary triumph.

The episode highlights several recurring themes: Jack's impulsive enthusiasm (he rushes ahead and jumps to conclusions), Fritz's readiness to shoot (which the father must temper), and the father's superior natural history knowledge (which converts a moment of alarm into an opportunity).

## Role in the Narrative

The crocodile/iguana episode serves as comic relief while reinforcing the novel's educational agenda. Jack's error is not mocked but corrected — the father uses the misidentification as a teaching moment about reptilian taxonomy, habitat, and the practical value of accurate natural history knowledge. The crocodile, like the lion and the serpent, belongs to the realm of the boys' imagination; the iguana belongs to the real, providentially abundant island.

## Natural History Context

The "leguana" or iguana described in the text is consistent with the green iguana (*Iguana iguana*), native to tropical regions of the Americas and widely introduced to Indian Ocean islands. The father's description of its "coat of scales" providing natural armor, its mild temperament, and its value as food all align with historical accounts of iguana consumption in the West Indies. The crocodile, by contrast, would require permanent freshwater bodies — conspicuously absent from the rocky, dry location where Jack makes his discovery.

## Literary Significance

The crocodile-that-isn't mirrors the novel's treatment of the lion and the serpent: all three are creatures the boys expect to encounter (informed by European adventure literature) but which the island's ecology does not support. Wyss systematically deflates these Romantic expectations, replacing them with the more modest but more useful reality of the iguana. The episode is a small allegory of the novel's larger project: replacing the fearful imagination of the exotic with rational, empirical engagement with the actual natural world.
