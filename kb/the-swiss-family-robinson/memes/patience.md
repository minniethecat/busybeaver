# Patience

Patience — the capacity to wait without complaint for divine or natural processes to unfold — is a recurring moral theme in *The Swiss Family Robinson*. The father presents it as the necessary corollary to industry: one works diligently but waits trustfully, "calling in the assistance of patience and reason" (001-040).

## Definition

In the novel's moral vocabulary, patience is not passive resignation but active trust. When young Francis asks when they will be rescued, Ernest answers: "Do you not know that it is our duty not to prescribe to God, but to wait for his assistance with patience and humility?" (001-011). Patience is the virtue that reconciles human effort with divine sovereignty.

## Manifestations in the Narrative

**Patience in Crisis.** The father repeatedly counsels patience when his sons grow frustrated. To Fritz's complaint about "this barren desert side," he replies: "Patience, patience, replied I, there is a time for everything" (002-044). Patience is taught through the father's calm responses to impatience.

**Patience as Domestic Virtue.** Elizabeth articulates patience as a principle of orderly life: "by degrees, observed my wife; patience and regularity in our plans will accomplish more than violence and impetuosity" (005-003). Her patience balances the boys' restlessness and the father's ambition.

**Impatience as Folly.** The novel repeatedly shows impatience leading to error. The sons "had not patience to wait, but had broken the cocoa-nuts prematurely" (004-064), wasting them. These small narrative moments reinforce the moral lesson: impatience destroys what patience would preserve.

## Key Passages

> "Patience, patience, replied I, there is a time for everything." (002-044)

> "Let us endeavour to call in the assistance of patience and reason; with the aid of these we shall be able to do more than by the exertion of our utmost bodily strength." (001-040)

> "By degrees, observed my wife; patience and regularity in our plans will accomplish more than violence and impetuosity." (005-003)

## Thematic Significance

Patience is the temporal virtue — the virtue that governs our relationship to time and process. In a narrative of survival, where every day brings new challenges, patience is what prevents panic and enables planning. It is also the theological virtue par excellence: to be patient is to trust God's timing, to accept that human desires must yield to divine wisdom.

## Historical Context

Patience as a Christian virtue has deep roots in the New Testament (*hypomone*, steadfast endurance). In the Protestant tradition, patience was particularly associated with providence: since God governs all events for the good of the elect, the proper response to adversity is patient trust. Wyss's novel translates this theological concept into narrative form, showing children (and readers) what patience looks like in practice.
