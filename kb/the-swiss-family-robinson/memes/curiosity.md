# Curiosity

Curiosity — the desire to know, to explore, to understand — is the psychological engine of *The Swiss Family Robinson*. The father's educational method is built on "awakening the curiosity of my sons" (001-036), and the novel's encyclopedic content is presented as the natural fruit of curious minds engaging with a world full of wonders.

## Definition

In the novel, curiosity is the innate human drive toward knowledge, which the father sees as implanted by God for the purpose of learning. The preface states explicitly that the book aims "to excite their curiosity, and to give them a wish to learn" (P03-005). Curiosity is not dangerous but providential — the appetite that makes education possible.

## Manifestations in the Narrative

**Curiosity as Educational Engine.** The father's "system of education" is designed "to awaken the curiosity of my sons by interesting observations" (001-036). He does not lecture but presents intriguing phenomena, allowing curiosity to motivate self-directed learning.

**Curiosity in Action.** The narrative is punctuated by moments of curiosity-driven exploration. Fritz cannot resist examining mysterious objects: "he could not resist the curiosity he felt to examine them closely" (003-035). The father shares this drive: "I had a great curiosity to discover what could possibly be the meaning of this phenomenon" (008-010).

**Curiosity Rewarded.** Curious investigation consistently yields practical benefits. Tasting an unknown plant's sap "prompted by curiosity" (003-078) leads to a useful discovery. The novel thus teaches that curiosity is not idle but productive — the beginning of useful knowledge.

## Key Passages

> "One of the points of my system of education was to awaken the curiosity of my sons by interesting observations, to leave time for the activity of their imagination, and then to correct any error they might fall into." (001-036)

> "The author carried out in the composition of his book the general ideas... to blend with them such lessons of instruction as are necessary to excite their curiosity, and to give them a wish to learn." (P03-005)

> "I had a great curiosity to discover what could possibly be the meaning of this phenomenon." (008-010)

## Thematic Significance

Curiosity bridges the novel's educational and religious themes. Unlike some Christian traditions that viewed curiosity as a vice (*curiositas* as the restless desire for novelty), Wyss presents it as a God-given faculty that, properly directed, leads to knowledge of creation and therefore of the Creator. This is the Enlightenment synthesis: curiosity serves piety by revealing the wisdom and goodness of divine design.

## Historical Context

The positive valuation of curiosity reflects the Enlightenment's rehabilitation of the concept. Francis Bacon had declared knowledge to be power; the Royal Society took *Nullius in verba* (take nobody's word for it) as its motto. By Wyss's time, curiosity was no longer a vice but the engine of scientific progress. Children's literature of the period — from Anna Laetitia Barbauld to Maria Edgeworth — made curiosity a central theme, and Wyss follows this tradition.
