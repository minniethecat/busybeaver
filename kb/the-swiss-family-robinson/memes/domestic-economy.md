# Domestic Economy

Domestic economy — the systematic management of household resources, labour, and production — is the invisible infrastructure of the family's island life. While the father's expeditions and constructions receive narrative prominence, it is the domestic economy managed primarily by Elizabeth that ensures daily survival: food preparation, clothing production, storage, and the countless tasks of maintaining a household under primitive conditions.

## Definition

In the novel, domestic economy encompasses the full range of household production: cooking, preserving, spinning, weaving, sewing, cleaning, childcare, and resource allocation. The term reflects the 18th-century concept of *oeconomia* — the orderly management of a household — which was considered both a practical skill and a moral virtue.

## Manifestations in the Narrative

**Elizabeth's Domain.** The mother's work is constant but often mentioned only in passing. She "set about dressing some of them" (008-033) after the lobster catch; she manages the kitchen garden; she oversees food preservation for the winter. Her labour is essential but narratively subordinate to the father's more dramatic exploits.

**Resource Management.** The family's survival depends on careful stewardship of limited resources. The father's repeated maxim — "A little at a time is the maxim that suits us best" (008-032) — reflects domestic economic thinking applied to island life.

**Production and Manufacturing.** Much of the novel's technical content concerns domestic production: candle-making, butter-churning, cloth-weaving, leather-working. These are presented as adventures in miniature, transforming the domestic sphere into a site of ingenuity.

## Key Passages

> "A little at a time is the maxim that suits us best." (008-032)

## Thematic Significance

The novel's attention to domestic economy reflects its bourgeois values. Survival is not enough; the family must create a comfortable, orderly, well-managed household. This distinguishes them from the mere animal existence of castaways and aligns them with the respectable European middle class. The island becomes not just a refuge but a home.

## Historical Context

The late 18th and early 19th centuries saw a flourishing of literature on domestic economy, from Hannah Glasse's cookery books to Maria Edgeworth's educational treatises. Domestic economy was considered a branch of practical knowledge suitable for women's education. Wyss's inclusion of detailed domestic content reflects this cultural emphasis and makes the novel useful for female as well as male readers.
