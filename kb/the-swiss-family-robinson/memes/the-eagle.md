The Eagle is a young Malabar eagle captured by Fritz during the family's expedition to establish the sago manufactory. Fritz's determination to tame this "noble" bird becomes a minor subplot in the novel's later chapters, blending the family's practical natural history with their playful adoption of chivalric titles and roles.

## Description and Classification

The eagle is identified as a Malabar eagle, distinct from European species: "it was evidently not a European eagle, and I supposed it to be the Malabar eagle" (028-002). The father educates the younger boys about the difference between eagles and common large birds when Francis mistakenly identifies a bustard as an eagle: "as if all large birds must be eagles!" (007-019). The eagle's nobility of species is contrasted with mere size: "the eagle cannot but ennoble it, since he fixes his abode there" (013-025).

## Role in the Narrative

The eagle serves as a marker of Fritz's maturation — his ability to capture and train a bird of prey demonstrates skills beyond those of his younger brothers. The eagle also becomes the centrepiece of the family's playful adoption of medieval titles, with Fritz declaring himself "knight of the eagle" (028-023).

## Key Episodes

### Discovery and Capture

Fritz discovers the young eagle during the sago expedition and brings it back alive: "Fritz did not neglect his eagle; he daily shot some small birds for it" (030-010). The eaglet is initially fed voraciously and kept tethered to Fritz's wrist (028-005).

### The Taming

The taming process occasions a family debate. Fritz insists on keeping the bird: "I shall not give you my eagle neither; it is mine, and I will keep it" (028-020). The father intervenes with a lesson in falconry: "You know not how to tame your eagle" (028-021). Jack offers a trade — "you shall be knight of the eagle if you think proper" — proposing a system of chivalric titles that the children eagerly adopt (028-023).

### A Noble Companion

The eagle becomes a fixture of Fritz's daily life: "he daily shot some small birds for it, and it began to show symptoms of attachment" (030-010). The father uses the eagle's carnivorous diet as an occasion to reflect on the practical cost of keeping predators: "your monkeys, jackals, and eagles, that do nothing but eat" (031-017).

## Behavior and Character

- **Predatory**: Feeds on small birds shot by Fritz (030-010)
- **Tamable**: Shows attachment to Fritz through consistent feeding
- **Noble**: The family associates eagles with dignity and chivalry (028-022)
- **Demanding**: Requires daily hunting by its keeper

## Literary Significance

The eagle episode illustrates the novel's layered approach to education. On one level, it is a practical lesson in falconry and zoology; on another, it feeds the family's imaginative life through the adoption of chivalric titles. Fritz's eagle — "a more noble and heroical animal than your monkey" (028-022) — represents the aspirational, romantic dimension of the family's island existence, where survival coexists with play.
