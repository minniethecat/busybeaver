# Jenny Montrose

Jenny Montrose is a young English woman discovered on the island by Fritz in the novel's later chapters. Her rescue introduces the first non-family human presence in the narrative and opens the possibility of romantic attachment — Fritz becomes her devoted protector — and of eventual contact with the outside world.

## Role in the Narrative

Jenny's appearance late in the novel serves several functions: it provides a romantic subplot (her growing attachment to Fritz), it introduces an outsider's perspective on the family's island civilization, and it raises the question of whether the family will return to Europe. She is also the vehicle for the novel's treatment of gender: as the first young woman the boys have encountered in years, her presence changes the family dynamic.

## Biography

### Background

Jenny is the daughter of a British officer, Colonel Montrose. She was shipwrecked on a different part of the island and had survived alone for an extended period before Fritz discovered her.

### Life on the Island

Fritz finds Jenny and brings her to the family settlement, where she is welcomed by Elizabeth and integrated into the household. Her presence is a catalyst for reflection on the family's isolation and their future.

## Character Traits

- **Courage and resilience**: Having survived alone, she demonstrates considerable fortitude
- **Gentility**: She is presented as a proper young English lady, embodying the feminine virtues the novel values
- **Gratitude**: Her response to rescue is one of heartfelt thankfulness

## Literary Significance

Jenny is significant primarily because she represents the outside world breaking into the family's self-contained island existence. Her presence forces questions the narrative had previously avoided: Will the family ever leave? What would reintegration into European society look like? Her romantic attachment to Fritz also introduces an element rare in the novel — the possibility of marriage and a new generation.
