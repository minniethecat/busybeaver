Courage is the virtue that makes all other Robinson family achievements possible. From the opening scene of the shipwreck — where the father exhorts his terrified children to courage by observing "the water is beginning to sink" (001-005) — to the final chapters of settled prosperity, courage operates as both a practical necessity and a moral ideal. Wyss presents courage not as the absence of fear but as the mastery of it: the father's own courage, he confesses, is sustained by "labour and watching" (001-002), not by innate fearlessness.

## Physical Courage

The novel's most dramatic displays of courage are physical. [[fritz|Fritz]]'s confrontation with the shark — seizing his gun and striking the monster on the head (002-050) — is the paradigmatic instance. But physical courage takes many forms: [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]] consenting to remain alone with the younger children while the father and [[fritz|Fritz]] explore the interior (003-002); [[ernest|Ernest]] swallowing an unfamiliar oyster "in the character of the most courageous" (002-048); the entire family climbing to the summit of a hill "without many courageous efforts and a plentiful perspiration" (003-070).

The father explicitly praises courage when he sees it. Of [[fritz|Fritz]] he records: "I was well satisfied with the courage of Fritz, who, instead of being frightened, had displayed as much coolness as resolution" (003-013). This coupling of courage with coolness — courage as disciplined response rather than reckless impulse — reflects Wyss's Enlightenment values.

## Moral Courage

Beyond physical bravery, the novel valorizes moral courage: the fortitude to persevere in adversity, to maintain hope when rescue seems impossible, and to uphold civilized standards in a wilderness. The mother embodies this form of courage. When her husband prepares for an expedition, "courageously consented to my proposal, after I had entreated her not to lose courage" (003-002). The reciprocity of encouragement — each spouse bolstering the other's resolve — reveals courage as a communal rather than individual virtue.

## Courage and Providence

The novel explicitly links courage to religious faith. [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]]'s practical wisdom — "Give them something to take to give them courage: our courage will strengthen with our bodies" (001-008) — is both pragmatic and theological. Courage is sustained by God's providence, manifested in food, shelter, and the gradual revelation of the island's resources. The father's exhortation — "a little patience and a little courage, dear Fritz" (004-003) — pairs the two virtues as complementary responses to hardship.

## Educational Function

Courage serves Wyss's pedagogical agenda in two ways. First, the children's growth in courage — from the terrified youngest clinging to his mother to [[franz|Franz]] confidently discovering the [[potato|potato]] plants — traces a developmental arc that young readers are meant to emulate. Second, the father's measured praise of courage (and his rebukes of recklessness) models the distinction between true courage and its counterfeit, rashness.
