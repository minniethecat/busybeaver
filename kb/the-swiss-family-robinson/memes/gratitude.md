# Gratitude

Gratitude — the active expression of thankfulness for benefits received — is one of the most frequently invoked moral concepts in *The Swiss Family Robinson*. The father consistently frames it not as a mere sentiment but as a moral duty: the family's proper response to the abundance of providential gifts they discover on the island.

## Definition

In the novel's moral vocabulary, gratitude operates at two levels: vertical gratitude toward God (Divine Providence) for survival, resources, and protection; and horizontal gratitude toward family members for their contributions. The father teaches that "gratitude to Providence" must be demonstrated through "the exercise of a more than ordinary care and industry" (008-032), making gratitude an active virtue rather than passive feeling.

## Manifestations in the Narrative

**Gratitude as Industry.** The father's most distinctive teaching on gratitude links it directly to productive labour. After the extraordinary catch of lobsters, he reflects: "May we never cease to evince our gratitude to Providence, by the exercise of a more than ordinary care and industry!" (008-032). Gratitude is not expressed through words alone but through diligent stewardship of divine gifts.

**Gratitude in Prayer.** Morning and evening prayers consistently include thanksgiving. After their first night on land, the family's "morning prayer" (002-002) becomes a daily ritual that structures time around grateful acknowledgment. The father models this before his sons, teaching by example that "having finished our prayers" (005-013) is the proper beginning of every day.

**Gratitude for Specific Deliverances.** Individual rescues and discoveries evoke explicit thanksgiving. After naming Safety Bay, the father suggests the name itself should express "gratitude to God, who conducted us hither in safety" (013-014). The slaughter of the shark prompts: "We ought to be thankful to Providence, and a little to our Fritz also" (008-015), balancing divine and human objects of gratitude.

**Ingratitude as Moral Failure.** The negative example reinforces the positive teaching. The father recounts the biblical story of "the ingratitude of the colonists" (012-029) as a cautionary tale, using narrative to instill the value. Gratitude is framed as the mark of civilized, Christian character.

## Key Passages

> "Every hint is good for something, cried I, and I begin to think that what Jack has suggested is worth a trial; the counsels of even a child may be worth consideration, and should be accepted with gratitude." (001-029)

> "May we never cease to evince our gratitude to Providence, by the exercise of a more than ordinary care and industry!" (008-032)

> "My advice would be that, out of gratitude to God, who conducted us hither in safety, we ought to call it Providence Bay, or the Bay of Safety." (013-014)

## Thematic Significance

Gratitude functions in the novel as the hinge between religion and work ethic. It transforms labour from mere survival necessity into an act of worship. This synthesis of piety and industry reflects the Protestant work ethic characteristic of Swiss Reformed culture: material prosperity is a blessing, and productive work is the appropriate response of the grateful recipient.

The concept also serves a pedagogical function. By repeatedly naming and modelling gratitude, the father teaches his sons an emotional discipline — the habit of interpreting circumstances through the lens of thankfulness rather than complaint. When Ernest shows "a warmth of gratitude for his share in the benefit" (016-002), the father rewards this as evidence of successful character formation.

## Historical Context

The novel's emphasis on gratitude reflects the broader tradition of Protestant devotional literature in which thankfulness is a primary religious affect. In the Swiss Reformed context of Wyss's Bern, gratitude (*Dankbarkeit*) was understood as the fundamental human response to divine grace — a theme central to Reformed covenant theology. The father's insistence on *active* gratitude (gratitude demonstrated through industry) echoes the Reformed rejection of "dead faith" without works.
