# Duty

Duty — the sense of moral obligation that governs action — is the ethical backbone of *The Swiss Family Robinson*. The father's every decision is framed in the language of duty: duty to God, duty to his family, duty to the truth. This framework transforms the survival narrative from a mere adventure into a sustained moral education.

## Definition

In the novel, duty operates on multiple levels: religious duty (to God), paternal duty (to children and wife), and social duty (to other humans). The father articulates this framework explicitly: "it was yet my duty to make every effort to appear serene before my family" (001-007). Duty is not presented as burden but as identity — to be a father, a Christian, a civilized man is to be governed by duties.

## Manifestations in the Narrative

**Paternal Duty.** The father's primary duty is to his family. He conceals his own fear because "it was yet my duty to make every effort to appear serene" (001-007). When making difficult decisions, he consistently frames them in terms of what he owes his wife and children.

**Religious Duty.** Duty to God structures the family's spiritual life. Ernest articulates this when correcting Francis: "Do you not know that it is our duty not to prescribe to God, but to wait for his assistance with patience and humility?" (001-011). The Sabbath is observed because "it was a duty incumbent on me to take it" (011-004).

**Social Duty.** Even on a deserted island, the father maintains a sense of duty toward absent humanity. When the family discovers valuable items, he reminds them that "it is a duty incumbent on us to return them" (012-054) to their rightful owners. Civilization persists through the maintenance of its moral obligations.

## Key Passages

> "Sunk and desolate from the loss of every chance for human aid, it was yet my duty to make every effort to appear serene before my family." (001-007)

> "Do you not know that it is our duty not to prescribe to God, but to wait for his assistance with patience and humility?" (001-011)

> "Let us before acquit ourselves of this duty before we engage in any other occupation." (005-013)

## Thematic Significance

Duty functions as the novel's answer to the question: what makes a man civilized when civilization is absent? The answer is internalized moral obligation. The family does not descend into savagery because the father carries civilization within him in the form of duties. This is a distinctively Kantian answer — the moral law within — though Wyss would have framed it in Christian rather than philosophical terms.

## Historical Context

The novel's emphasis on duty reflects the ethics of German Idealism and Protestant moral theology. Kant's categorical imperative (1785) had made duty the center of moral philosophy, and this emphasis permeated educated European culture. For Wyss, a Swiss Reformed pastor, duty was also a theological concept: the human response to divine grace. The novel synthesizes these philosophical and religious traditions into a practical, family-centered ethic.
