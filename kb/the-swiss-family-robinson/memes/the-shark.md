# The Shark

The shark is one of the most memorable wild animals encountered by the Robinson family. Discovered at the entrance to Safety Bay, this enormous predator — "more than twenty feet, from head to tail" (008-015) — represents both mortal danger and providential provision. Its death at Fritz's hands is one of the novel's dramatic high points.

## Encounter

Fritz discovers the shark stranded or swimming near the shore. He shoots it with two balls, which the father later finds "lodged in his hideous head" (008-014). The kill is celebrated as both a deliverance from danger and a triumph of the young hunter's skill.

## The Father's Interpretation

The father immediately frames the shark's death in providential terms: "We ought to be thankful to Providence, and a little to our Fritz also, for having delivered us from such a monster!" (008-015). The shark's skin proves useful — "one might almost use it for a file" — and the father orders strips cut from it for future use.

## Significance

The shark episode exemplifies the novel's pattern of animal encounters: danger appears, human courage and ingenuity overcome it, and the dead animal is received as a providential gift providing useful materials. The shark is never merely an animal — it is simultaneously a threat, a lesson in gratitude, a test of courage, and a source of raw materials.
