# Robinson Crusoe

Robinson Crusoe — the protagonist of Daniel Defoe's 1719 novel — is the most significant literary reference in *The Swiss Family Robinson*. The family explicitly models their island existence on Crusoe's example, and the father repeatedly invokes him as both practical inspiration and moral benchmark. The novel is self-consciously a "Robinsonade" — a narrative in the tradition Crusoe established.

## Role in the Narrative

Crusoe functions as an intertextual presence rather than a character in the story. He is the invisible model against which the family measures their own experience. The father's encyclopedic knowledge, mechanical ingenuity, and providential worldview all echo Crusoe's, but with a crucial difference: where Crusoe was alone, the Swiss family Robinson is a community, and the novel's emphasis shifts accordingly from individual survival to family education.

## Key Moments (in references)

- The father's preface acknowledges the Robinsonade tradition explicitly
- The family's resource extraction from the wreck mirrors Crusoe's methodical salvaging
- The father's providential interpretations echo Crusoe's religious conversion narrative

## Literary Significance

The novel's relationship to Robinson Crusoe is both homage and adaptation. Wyss adopts Defoe's basic situation — shipwreck, island, survival through ingenuity — but transforms it into a family-centered educational narrative. Where Defoe's novel is a spiritual autobiography, Wyss's is a pedagogical program. The reference to Crusoe signals the novel's genre and invites readers to compare the two works' treatments of isolation, providence, and civilization.
