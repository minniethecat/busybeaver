The Great Storm — distinct from the opening shipwreck — is a tropical cyclone that strikes the island during the rainy season, testing the family's infrastructure and resolve at a moment when they have grown confident in their settled life. Unlike the shipwreck, which catches the family unprepared, the Great Storm arrives after months of construction and cultivation, and the family's survival through it vindicates their systematic approach to island life.

## Prelude

The storm's approach is signaled by atmospheric changes that the family, now seasoned observers of the island's weather patterns, recognize with increasing concern. The father's meteorological knowledge — part of his general scientific education — allows him to interpret the signs and prepare the family and their animals for the coming deluge.

## The Storm's Fury

The storm's violence is described through its effects on the family's constructions. [[falconhurst|Falconhurst]], the treehouse home, groans and sways but holds — a testament to the father's engineering. Lesser structures are damaged or destroyed, and the family is confined to their elevated refuge for days. The storm's duration tests the family's psychological resilience as much as their physical shelter.

## Aftermath

The storm's aftermath reveals the fragility of the family's dominion over nature. Crops are flattened, paths washed out, and outbuildings damaged. Yet the family's response — systematic assessment, prioritized repair, philosophical acceptance — demonstrates how far they have come since the panicked days of the shipwreck. The storm is a trial, not a catastrophe.

## Literary Significance

The Great Storm serves a crucial narrative function: it prevents the family's settled life from becoming static. By periodically subjecting the island to nature's violence, Wyss keeps the narrative dynamic and reaffirms the central theme of human fragility in the face of natural forces. The storm also provides an opportunity for the family to demonstrate their acquired competence — they survive not by luck but by preparation, a lesson central to the novel's pedagogical purpose.
