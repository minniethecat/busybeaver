Ernest Robinson, the second of the four Robinson sons, is the family's intellectual and naturalist. At twelve years old, he prefers observation and reflection to the physical exploits of his elder brother [[fritz|Fritz]]. His knowledge of [[natural-history|natural history]] — drawn from his father's books and lectures — frequently proves invaluable in identifying the island's flora and fauna, though his tendency toward indolence and his preoccupation with meals provide moments of gentle comedy throughout the narrative.

## Role in the Narrative

Ernest serves as the novel's embodiment of intellectual curiosity and the contemplative life. Where [[fritz|Fritz]] acts and [[jack|Jack]] improvises, Ernest observes, classifies, and reflects. His father explicitly describes him at twelve years old as possessing "a peculiar turn for natural history" (002-012). Ernest's function in the family economy is to convert raw observation into systematic knowledge — he identifies species, proposes practical applications for discoveries, and articulates the philosophical implications of the family's island existence. His occasional laziness ("even our slothful Ernest submitted to the hard fate of rising early," 003-003) humanizes what might otherwise be a purely exemplary character.

## Biography

### Background

Ernest was approximately eleven years old when the family embarked on their fateful voyage. As the second son, he occupied a middle position in the sibling hierarchy — old enough to participate in serious discussions but young enough to be excused from the most dangerous tasks. His father's profession as a pastor and educator meant that Ernest had access to books on natural history, which shaped his intellectual interests before the shipwreck.

### Life on the Island

From the earliest days of the shipwreck, Ernest's distinctive character emerges. While [[fritz|Fritz]] retrieves weapons and tools from the wreck, "Ernest produced his hat filled with nails" (001-022), a seemingly modest contribution that proves essential for construction. His practical knowledge is often tempered by a certain fastidiousness: he is "ever eager about his meals" (002-024) and acutely concerned with whether unfamiliar foods are palatable.

> Why, this is well, Ernest, said I, and is what I call an excellent thought, replied his father approvingly.

Ernest's most distinctive contribution is his taxonomic knowledge. When the family encounters unfamiliar animals and plants, it is Ernest who can name and classify them. His identification of the [[coconut-palm|coconut palm]] is a crucial moment: "Ernest expressed his joy that he should now eat coconuts" (002-015). His natural history expertise transforms raw survival into systematic understanding, aligning with the novel's pedagogical purpose.

Despite his intellectual gifts, Ernest is not without flaws. His father gently rebukes his indolence: "even our slothful Ernest submitted to the hard fate of rising early" (003-003). His reluctance to exert himself physically — "Ernest was the only one who had been able to resist the allurement of the sport" (002-048) — provides a recurring comic counterpoint to his brothers' boundless energy.

### Later Life

Ernest's fate after the novel's main action is less prominent than [[fritz|Fritz]]'s, but his intellectual temperament suggests he would become the family's archivist, naturalist, and perhaps its future teacher. His betrothal or marriage is not recorded in the novel, consistent with his younger age and his characterization as a scholar rather than a romantic hero.

## Character Traits

- **Intellectual**: Possesses a "peculiar turn for natural history" and takes pride in correct identification (002-012, 005-008)
- **Observant**: Notices details his brothers overlook, particularly in flora and fauna
- **Indolent**: Prefers reading and thinking to physical labor; requires prompting from his father (003-003)
- **Gastronomically inclined**: "Ever eager about his meals" (002-024); his first thought about any discovery is whether it is edible
- **Precise**: Insists on correct nomenclature, as when he corrects Fritz about classifying an animal — "It is neither a dog nor a wolf, interrupted Ernest in a consequential tone" (005-008)
- **Pious**: Reminds the family of their religious duties: "I know what it is, said Ernest, we have not said our prayers" (003-008)

## Key Moments

- **Chapter 1 (001-022)**: Contributes his hatful of nails — a practical, if unglamorous, contribution to the family's survival
- **Chapter 2 (002-015)**: Identifies the coconut palm, establishing his role as the family naturalist
- **Chapter 2 (002-045)**: His manner of presenting his discoveries — "with the look and manner of a professor" — embodies his scholarly identity
- **Chapter 3 (003-008)**: Reminds the family to pray before their first morning on the island, revealing his religious sensibility
- **Chapter 4 (004-068)**: Demonstrates his practical knowledge by identifying edible plants and useful materials
- **Chapter 5 (005-008)**: Corrects his brothers' zoological errors with characteristic precision

## Literary Significance

Ernest Robinson embodies the Enlightenment ideal of the scholar-naturalist — a figure who transforms raw experience into systematic knowledge through observation, classification, and reflection. In Wyss's pedagogical scheme, Ernest represents the value of book-learning tempered by practical application. His character arc demonstrates that intellectual gifts, while less immediately dramatic than physical courage, are equally essential to the family's survival and flourishing. Ernest's tendency toward indolence and his obsession with food serve a dual narrative purpose: they humanize the character, preventing him from becoming a mere didactic mouthpiece, and they create opportunities for his father's gentle correction — modeling the educational relationship at the heart of the novel.
