The discovery of the potato marks a turning point in the Robinson family's food security — a transition from reliance on wild-gathered foods to systematic cultivation. Unlike the [[coconut-palm|coconut]], which nature provides ready-made, the potato requires recognition, propagation, and patience. Its discovery by [[franz|Franz]], the youngest son, is one of the novel's most satisfying moments: the child who contributes least to the family's physical labor proves instrumental in securing their long-term sustenance.

## Discovery

The potato's discovery is a dramatic set-piece. [[franz|Franz]], the youngest boy, calls out from the undergrowth:

> Papa! cried Francis, quite transported, raising his voice; we have found some potato seed! (013-046)

The father's response — "What say you? potato seed? inquired I joyfully" (013-047) — conveys the magnitude of the find. This is not merely another curiosity but a staple crop, a food that can sustain the family indefinitely. The father immediately frames the discovery in historic terms: "this discovery of the potatoes is one of inestimable value" (013-053).

## Botanical Description

The potato's discovery occasions another of the father's characteristic natural history lessons. [[franz|Franz]], having made the find, is bewildered by the plant's morphology — "Why, do potato plants bear fruit on their branches?" (013-050) — and his brothers explain that "the vegetable called the potato, is only the root of the plant" (013-051). The distinction between the plant's aerial fruit (inedible, possibly toxic) and its subterranean tuber (the familiar food) provides a vivid lesson in plant anatomy.

The family finds "a large plantation of potato plants; a part of them were completely covered over with their large and singular white flowers" (013-052). This detail — the potato in bloom — suggests both the plant's established presence on the island and its ornamental beauty alongside its utility.

## Cultivation

The potatoes are immediately integrated into the family's agricultural system. The father records harvesting and storing them in sacks (014-062), planting new beds, and incorporating potatoes into daily meals: "some fish, to which we added some potatoes" (016-034). [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]], the mother, sends the boys on foraging expeditions when supplies run low: "they were gone to get some potatoes, which we wanted" (018-004).

The potato's reliability as a staple is underscored by its role in the family's storage and preservation strategies. One of the boys proposes "how cleverly we could fill it with potatoes, or the other things we wanted" (019-021), demonstrating the crop's integration into the family's material economy.

## Culinary Role

[[jack|Jack]]'s enthusiasm for the potato is characteristically direct: "I could eat potatoes every day of my life, without ever being tired of them" (013-058). His declaration captures both the potato's culinary versatility and the family's relief at having secured a reliable food source. The potato supplements rather than replaces other foods — it appears alongside fish, game, and gathered fruits — but its dependable yield transforms the family's diet from precarious to predictable.

## Literary Significance

The potato episode reflects the novel's engagement with contemporary European agricultural debates. The eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries saw the potato's gradual acceptance as a European staple, championed by figures like Antoine-Augustin Parmentier in France and Frederick the Great in Prussia. [[franz|Franz]]'s discovery of wild potatoes — and the family's immediate recognition of their value — naturalizes this agricultural revolution, suggesting that the potato belongs to the providential order of the island as much as the [[coconut-palm|coconut]] or the [[gourd|calabash gourd]].
