Knips, the young monkey adopted by the Robinson family during their first major expedition, is one of the most memorable and entertaining members of the island household. Discovered among a troop of monkeys in the forest, the orphaned infant is brought back to [[falconhurst|Falconhurst]] by [[fritz|Fritz]] and quickly becomes a beloved pet, amusing the family with his antics and forming a particular bond with [[turk|Turk the dog]].

## Description and Classification

Knips is a small, agile monkey, likely a guenon or similar Old World species, consistent with an island setting in the Indian Ocean region. [[ernest|Ernest]], with his characteristic precision, would no doubt have attempted a formal classification, but the novel is content to describe Knips in terms of his behavior rather than his taxonomy. His "monkey instinct" (004-028) — a blend of curiosity, mischief, and imitation — makes him an inexhaustible source of entertainment and occasional irritation.

## Role in the Narrative

Knips serves as the novel's comic relief and a symbol of the family's domestication of the wild island. His adoption marks a transition from pure survival to settled life: the family now has the leisure and security to keep a pet. His interactions with [[turk|Turk]] — riding on the dog's back, sharing meals, competing for attention — provide some of the novel's most charming domestic scenes (004-039). The father explicitly frames Knips's adoption as a moral act, telling [[fritz|Fritz]]: "you will soon regard your little monkey as a member of our family" (004-033).

## Discovery and Adoption

Knips enters the narrative in Chapter 4, when the family's expedition encounters a troop of monkeys in the forest. [[fritz|Fritz]] spots a young monkey separated from its mother:

> A monkey, a live monkey! Papa, mamma, a monkey! were their joyful exclamations (004-036).

The decision to adopt the orphaned monkey reflects the family's compassionate ethos. Despite the practical challenges of feeding a young primate, the father supports [[fritz|Fritz]]'s impulse to care for the creature. Knips quickly adapts to human company, suckling from the goats and accepting the family's protection.

## Behavior and Character

- **Imitation**: Knips mimics human behavior with uncanny accuracy, from eating at the table to attempting to handle tools
- **Mischief**: His "grimaces" (011-009) and pranks — stealing food, upsetting containers, startling the younger children — are a constant source of exasperated affection
- **Bond with Turk**: Knips rides on [[turk|Turk]]'s back (004-039, 009-005), nurses alongside the goats, and is defended by the dog against threats
- **Vulnerability**: As an orphaned infant, Knips requires constant care, reinforcing the family's nurturing instincts

## Significance to the Family

The father reflects on Knips's presence with characteristic philosophical detachment:

> The little monkey, thanks to the kind temper of his protectors, was in his turn as gentle and familiar as a domestic animal (004-064).

Knips becomes "the life of the tent" (004-069), his antics lightening the family's labor and reminding them that their island home can be a place of joy as well as survival. His presence also serves an educational function: the children learn responsibility through caring for a dependent creature, and [[ernest|Ernest]] finds in Knips a living subject for his natural history observations.

## Literary Significance

Knips embodies the Enlightenment fascination with the natural world and the impulse to classify, tame, and domesticate it. His adoption represents the family's assertion of order over the island's untamed wilderness — a small but significant act of colonization rendered in miniature. At the same time, Knips's mischievous independence serves as a gentle reminder that nature can never be fully subdued, and that the wild retains its charm precisely through its resistance to complete domestication.
