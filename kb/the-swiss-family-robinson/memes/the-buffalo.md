The Buffalo is one of the first large game animals encountered by the Robinson family on their island. The hunt for buffalo becomes an early test of the family's cooperative skills and provides them with a significant store of meat and hide, establishing a pattern of resourceful exploitation of the island's fauna that continues throughout the novel.

## Description and Classification

The buffalo is described as a powerful bovine, similar to the water buffalo of tropical regions. The father distinguishes them from domestic cattle and notes their ferocity when provoked. The animals are found in herds grazing on the island's open plains, and their size makes them both a prized target and a serious danger during hunts.

## Role in the Narrative

The buffalo serves as the novel's archetypal large-game quarry — a test of courage, skill, and cooperation. The hunts are narrated as military-style expeditions, with the father assigning roles and the boys demonstrating their developing capabilities. The buffalo's flesh and hide become essential resources for the family's expanding homestead.

## Key Episodes

### The First Hunt

The buffalo hunt is one of the family's earliest coordinated hunting expeditions. The father devises a strategy that assigns specific roles to each boy based on their capabilities, transforming the hunt into a lesson in cooperation. The successful kill provides the family with a substantial food surplus for the first time.

### Domestication Attempts

The family captures a young buffalo calf and attempts to domesticate it: "you would perhaps teach the young buffaloes to carry burdens" (029-006). The taming of buffalo becomes part of the father's broader project of subordinating the island's wild fauna to human use, though the results are mixed.

### Sustained Provision

The buffalo becomes a recurring presence in the family's food economy. Buffalo meat is dried and stored as provision for expeditions, and buffalo hide is used for various purposes including footwear, straps, and shelter materials.

## Behavior and Character

- **Gregarious**: Lives in herds on the island's grasslands
- **Dangerous when threatened**: Requires careful approach and coordinated tactics
- **Valuable**: Provides abundant meat, tough hide, and sinew
- **Partially tamable**: Young calves can be domesticated for burden-carrying

## Literary Significance

The buffalo hunt epitomizes the novel's colonial narrative of mastery over nature. The hunt is simultaneously a survival necessity, a pedagogical exercise, and an assertion of European technological and organizational superiority over the island's wild inhabitants. The father's careful planning and the boys' enthusiastic participation model the Enlightenment ideal of rational dominion over the natural world.
