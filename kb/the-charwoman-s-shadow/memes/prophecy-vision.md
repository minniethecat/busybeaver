## Definition

Prophecy and vision in the novel represent supernatural modes of perception that transcend ordinary knowledge. Visions flood the mind with memories and insight [014-008], while omens serve as signs of things to come [027-069]. The posture of prophecy — kneeling in silent receptivity before a revelation [026-014] — captures the novel's treatment of supernatural knowledge as something received rather than seized.

## Visions in the Novel

The most striking vision sequence occurs when Ramon Alonzo flees Aragona and hears the church bells ringing against him. The bells' sound floods the gloaming with memories, carrying to him "vision on vision of times and occupations from which he was now cut off and debarred by a shadow" [014-008]. These visions represent the life he has lost through his association with magic — a form of clairvoyant nostalgia rather than future-sight.

> They seemed to be flooding all the gloaming with memories, as they carried to Ramon Alonzo there in his loneliness vision on vision of times and occupations from which he was now cut off and debarred by a shadow. [014-008]

## The Prophet's Posture

In Chapter XXVI, as Ramon Alonzo kneels to return the shadow to the charwoman, he is described in explicitly prophetic terms: "He kneeled gazing there silent and motionless, as a prophet might kneel and listen before a revelation, whose words were dying away" [026-014]. This image of the prophet as a silent, receptive figure — kneeling and listening rather than speaking — aligns with the novel's broader theme of knowledge as something granted by supernatural forces.

## Omens

Omens appear as signs of fortune. When Peter kneels to kiss Anemone's hand upon her arrival at the Tower, the gesture is described as "an omen full of good fortune" that "heartened Anemone for a fleeting instant" [027-069]. This suggests a folk belief in signs and portents coexisting with the more formal magical knowledge of the Magician.
