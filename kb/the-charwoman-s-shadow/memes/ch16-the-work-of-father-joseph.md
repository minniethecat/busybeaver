#### XVI

[016-001] The Work of [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]]

[016-002] [[mirandola|Mirandola]] came back from the edge of [[the-wood|the forest]] wondering, over wild heath to the garden. It had been her wont to know what her brother did, and even what he thought. But now he had some thought that she did not know, and it was at this that she wondered. She considered all the events that she thought might touch her brother; love first of all; and awhile she thought this was his motive, and then she thought it was something else. But she had not spoken with him long enough to guess that he went away so soon and so fast through the forest, with a packet of meat in his satchel, because he had lost what all [[earthly-elements|material things]] have in attendance upon them whenever they face the [[light-and-darkness|light]], and that he durst not show while other [[shadow|shadows]] were shorter his miserable strip of five feet of gloom. She had indeed heard tales of men who had sold their shadows, and knew that her brother had daily dealings with [[magic]], but she had not guessed the [[magical-contracts|fee]] that the Master took. She had told him not to bring [[gold-transmutation|gold]]. For what purpose then was his haste? Wondering, she returned to the garden.

[016-003] Who could tell her? Only one. One only, amongst the few Mirandola knew, was able to work out such puzzles, and that was the good Father Joseph. And just as she thought of him she saw his plump shape coming smiling across the garden. It was by a path through the garden he was wont to come from his house whenever he came to see [[lord-of-the-tower|the Lord]] of the Tower; and he came now to help make ready for that event, now near at hand, of which all the neighbourhood talked, the visit of the serene and glorious hidalgo, [[duke-of-shadow-valley|the Duke]] of [[shadow-valley|Shadow Valley]].

[016-004] And before he entered the house to take part in the preparations upon which the Lord of the Tower had long been occupied, except for the brief interruption of [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]]’s visit, Mirandola greeted him and turned him aside to another part of the garden, hoping to find from him the clue of her brother’s sudden departure. That he would discern it she had no doubt, that he might tell her she hoped; for these two were good friends, almost one might say comrades in [[soul-spirit|spiritual things]]. Mirandola’s confessions were the most complete of any that dwelt at the Tower, perhaps the most complete the good father heard, and indeed they were a joy to him. Often from these confessions he gathered such [[wisdom-and-knowledge|knowledge]] as it was right that he should have of the little earthly events that befell in that neighbourhood, which might not otherwise have come his way. He came much to rely on them; and so it was that he and Mirandola had a certain comradeship in the wars that the just wage ever against [[sacred-and-profane|sin]].

[016-005] “My brother came today,” she said as they walked.

[016-006] “He did?” said Father Joseph.

[016-007] “But he only stayed a short space and then went away.”

[016-008] “Oh. That is sad,” said Father Joseph.

[016-009] “He spoke with all of us and ate a dinner, and then he left at once.”

[016-010] “I trust he ate well,” said the good man.

[016-011] “Very well,” answered Mirandola.

[016-012] “Very well?” repeated Father Joseph.

[016-013] “Yes. He ate a large dinner.”

[016-014] “More than usual with him?”

[016-015] “Yes.”

[016-016] “Ah,” said the good man, “then he had travelled fast.”

[016-017] “I suppose so,” said Mirandola.

[016-018] “For what purpose did he come?” asked Father Joseph.

[016-019] Mirandola looked at him and smiled gently. “He came to see us,” she said.

[016-020] But Father Joseph had seen from that smile and from her eyes, before she spoke, that he would not get an answer to that question.

[016-021] “Very right. Very proper,” he said.

[016-022] “But he would not stay,” she said.

[016-023] “Ah. He should have stayed awhile,” said Father Joseph.

[016-024] “He went away very fast through the forest,” she said.

[016-025] “By what road did he come?” he asked.

[016-026] “Through the forest,” she said.

[016-027] “Ah. Hiding,” said Father Joseph.

[016-028] Not only was Father Joseph ready at all times with help for those that sought it, but one good turn deserved another, and he joyously used his wits for Mirandola. He argued thus with himself: a man hides either from enemies or from all. A man sometimes hid from the law; but the law came seldom to these parts, and in summer never, for la Garda slept much in the heat. From enemies then or from all. Now in all the confessions he had heard from men that had enemies he had noticed that none went back from their journeys by the same way by which they had come, as Ramon Alonzo had done. Did he then hide from all, except from his family? That would argue some [[transmutation-principle|change]] in him that he wished to conceal, or even in his clothing, for he had known young men as sensitive about their mere clothes as about the very form God had made, or﻿—alas﻿—about even the safety of their souls. But what [[transformation|change]] then? It would not have escaped the eyes of Mirandola.

[016-029] “I trust he was well,” he said.

[016-030] “Yes,” she said.

[016-031] “He looked as he ever looks?” he asked.

[016-032] “Oh, yes.”

[016-033] “Quite the same as ever. Yes, of course. And he was dressed the same?”

[016-034] “Yes,” she said. “All but his cloak.”

[016-035] “Ah, his cloak was different,” said Father Joseph.

[016-036] “It was not there,” she said.

[016-037] “No,” he said, and thought awhile. And now his thoughts ran deeper and stranger, touching the ways of magic, of which he knew much, but as an enemy.

[016-038] “My child,” he said, and he took her hand and patted it, lest his words should alarm her, “had he a shadow?”

[016-039] She gave a little gasp. “Yes, his shadow was safe.”

[016-040] That was as near as Father Joseph came with his guesses. He thought much more but strayed further away from the [[illusion-and-reality|truth]], and then he decided that more facts were needed, small things observed, short phrases overheard, which he knew so well how to weave; and determined to bide his [[time-in-magic|time]].

[016-041] “That is all now,” he said to soothe her, lest she should [[fear-and-dread|fear]] another question probing such dreadful things. “We shall find why he left.”

[016-042] They turned back then to the house to take part in the preparations.

[016-043] There Father Joseph found all the old repose gone. Comfortable chairs that stood in quiet corners had been moved, chairs that his body loved when a little wearied perhaps by spiritual work; and the corners that had seemed so quiet now glared with a harsh light with all their old cobwebs gone, and stared with a strange emptiness because their chairs had been taken away to the banquet-hall. The quiet old boar-spears, that had seemed a very part of lost years, no longer rested soberly on the wall, but flashed and sparkled uneasily, for they had been newly polished and seemed to have become all at once a part of the work-a-day present, and to have lost with their rust all manner of moods and memories that they used to whisper faintly to Father Joseph whenever he saw them there. And, though the moods that the dimness and rust of the old things brought him were always edged with sadness, yet he gently lamented them now. But news had just come that the morrow was the day when [[gulvarez|Gulvarez]] would bring the Duke of Shadow Valley, with four chiefs of the Duke’s bowmen and his own two men-at-arms. So Father Joseph was soon moving chairs with the rest; and, though somewhat lethargic of body, yet his great weight moved the chairs as the torrents swollen with snow move the small boulders. And by the middle of the afternoon nothing seemed left of that mysterious harmony that is the essence of any home: had Penates been set up there as in Roman days they would not have recognized the rooms that they guarded. But before the [[celestial-influences|sun]] had set a sudden change came over the confusion, and there was a new orderliness; and a tidiness that the Lord of the Tower had quite despaired to see was all at once around him. And [[peter|Peter]], who had come in from the garden to help, attributed this to the aid of all the Saints, and in particular to the aid of that fisherman from whom he had his name; but, likely as not, it was but the result of mere steady work. Then Father Joseph sank into one of the chairs and rested.

[016-044] And then the Lord of the Tower and his lady began to discuss the reception of the Duke; where they should meet him, who should go with them, and the hundred little points that make an occasion. And here a nimble [[power-and-lordship|power]] came to their aid from where the large man in his chair rested heavily, for the mind of Father Joseph was bright and agile, and the making of plans never tired it as pushing chairs tired his body. He it was that suggested that the two maids from the dairy and the girl that minded the house should go with Mirandola and strew the road with flowers. And he planned, or they planned under his encouragement, that Peter and three men from the stables should take each a boar-spear and stand two each side of the [[doors-and-thresholds|door]] like men-at-arms. And it was Father Joseph’s thought that another man should ride down the road till he saw the Duke arriving, and then spur back and tell them so that all should be ready. And the chamber that the Duke should have was prepared, and a room appointed by the Lady of the Tower for each of his four bowmen, and last of all they thought of Gulvarez. Lo, it was found that there was not room for him. But they thought of a long dark loft there was over the stables, where the sacks of corn were kept, longer than any room and nearly as warm: this they set apart for Gulvarez and his two men-at-arms.
