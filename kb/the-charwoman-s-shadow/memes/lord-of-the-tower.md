# The Lord of the Tower

The Lord of the Tower and Rocky Forest is the patriarch whose declining fortune sets the novel's plot in motion. His decision to send his younger son [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] to study under a [[magic|Magic]]ian is presented not as a romantic embrace of the arcane but as a practical economic calculation — [[magic]] is a career, like the Church or the army.

## Role in the Story

(001-028) His interaction with his son establishes his character: "his father shook his head with tolerant smiles and answered nothing at once, not hastening to reprove the lad's ill-founded opinion, for the wisdom of age expects these light conclusions from youth." He is patient, slightly condescending, and fundamentally practical.

(001-032) His financial situation is dire: "discernible [[shadow|shadows]] were cast by a want of [[gold-transmutation|gold]]" in his household. The phrase is almost punning — the house literally wants for [[gold-transmutation|gold]], and [[shadow|shadows]] (the novel's central [[magic]]al currency) are cast by that want.

(006-029) He consults [[gulvarez|Gulvarez]] on matters of marriage and alliance, navigating the social world with the caution of a man whose position is precarious.

## Economic Motivation

The Lord of the Tower is the novel's clearest embodiment of economic realism. He does not believe in [[magic]] — he believes in careers. His son's apprenticeship is an investment, and he expects a return. The irony is that the return comes not in [[gold-transmutation|gold]] but in moral growth — a currency the Lord of the Tower would not recognise.
