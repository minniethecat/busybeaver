# Bargains and Oaths

The world of *The Charwoman's Shadow* is held together not by written law but by a dense network of bargains and oaths — spoken agreements, implicit understandings, and sworn pledges that bind lord and magician, suitor and father, knight and crone. These informal compacts operate in the shadow of the novel's central magical contract, illuminating how obligation functions when there is no parchment to enforce it.

## The Magician's Bargaining

The magician is the novel's most artful bargainer, and his dealings with Ramon Alonzo reveal the asymmetry that informal agreements can conceal. When Ramon Alonzo prematurely guesses the magician's fee — his shadow — the magician is irked because "the sudden disclosure of the point was not in accordance with his plans for conducting a bargain" (004-020). The magician prefers to wrap his terms in mystification, letting the price emerge only after the value of his services has been fully appreciated.

When Ramon Alonzo hesitates, the magician "made a sudden concession, and so closed the bargain" (007-006), offering a false shadow as a substitute — a concession that costs him nothing but secures everything. Ramon Alonzo's rueful question — "Who would not have made such a bargain?" (007-010) — captures the retrospective clarity of one who has been out-negotiated.

As the apprenticeship unfolds, Ramon Alonzo comes to understand the bargain's true shape: "he cared far more for the fee that he had in his box than for any learning he might impart as his part of the bargain" (009-046). The magician has already received his payment; instruction is merely the formality that justifies it. "The Master was keeping to his bargain, thought Ramon Alonzo, and it was a hard bargain, and in the matter of the false shadow a sly one" (012-005).

## Honour-Bound Promises

Beyond formal bargains lie the promises of honour and chivalry. When Ramon Alonzo pledges to recover the charwoman's shadow, he understands it as a knightly obligation that no written contract could strengthen: "he was pledged to the knightly quest of the charwoman's shadow, whatever embarrassments might befall his own, and from this the laws of chivalry did not allow him to swerve" (021-003). This pledge — made to "an ancient and withered crone" who "had no other knight" — represents the novel's moral counterweight to the magician's predatory bargains.

The Lord of the Tower's dealings with Gulvarez operate in the same unwritten space: "there had been a bargain not in clear words, and without seals or parchment, inscribed only upon those two men's understanding" (017-010). These gentleman's agreements, dependent entirely on shared honour codes, prove as binding — and as vulnerable to interpretation — as any magical contract.

## Oaths as Social Currency

Oaths in the novel function as social currency, their value determined by the swearer's standing. When the Duke "swore an oath to eat no food in that house nor even drink water there" (018-013), the oath is performative — a display of noble intransigence rather than a binding commitment. By contrast, the charwoman's shadowless state is itself the consequence of an oath or bargain she made long ago, the terms of which the novel only gradually reveals, demonstrating that oaths outlive their makers and ripple across generations.
