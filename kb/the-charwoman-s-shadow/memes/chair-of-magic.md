# Chair of Magic

The Chair of Magic at Saragossa is an academic professorship in [[magic]] that represents the highest recognised authority in the magical arts. It is held by the Count of the Mountain, a figure of legendary status whose name is spoken with both reverence and dread.

## Prestige and Authority

(024-002) [[the-magician|The Magician]]'s deepest ambition is revealed through [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]]'s growing understanding: "Every day the Master's intention was growing clearer and the young man guessed it now: his was to be a name as revered, as dreaded, as his who had held the Chair of Magic at Saragossa."

(024-003) The Count of the Mountain's condition is described in striking terms: "Rather would I flame beside the Count of the Mountain, who held the Chair of Magic at Saragossa, and burn in that bright splendour that torments but cannot subdue him, than share with the ignorant populace their cheap immunity." The Chair confers immortal fame but at the cost of eternal torment.

## The Cost of Immortality

The Magician's speech about the Count of the Mountain (024-003) is the novel's most explicit meditation on the price of magical greatness. His scorn for "cheap immunity" — the ordinary salvation available to "the ignorant populace" — reveals a mind that has revalued all things. Salvation itself becomes contemptible when measured against the "bright splendour" of magical fame.

This inversion of values is the Chair's deepest significance. It represents a rival eschatology: not the soul's admission to heaven but the name's admission to legend. The Magician's ambition is not merely to be powerful but to be *remembered* — to hold a position in magical history comparable to the Chair's most illustrious occupant. His horror at the thought of dying into the anonymity of "countless millions" (024-003) drives every transaction he makes, including the taking of the charwoman's shadow.

The flame metaphor — "flame beside the Count," "burn in that bright splendour" — aligns magical fame with hellfire. The Chair is a seat of torment as much as honour. The Count "walks through" some unnamed, presumably hellish landscape (023-018), and the Magician willingly seeks to join him there. The Chair of Magic is thus a Faustian bargain institutionalised: a position that guarantees its holder's name will endure, at the cost of everything else.

## Institutional Significance

(023-018) The Chair is spoken of as an established institution: "Of this honour the magician had spoken once, when Ramon Alonzo had wonderingly enquired of the present state of that illustrious professor who had held the Chair of Magic at Saragossa. 'He walks through...'" The position exists within a tradition that outlasts any single holder.

The Chair's location at Saragossa places it within the intellectual geography of medieval Spain — a historical centre of scholarly exchange between Christian, Islamic, and Jewish traditions. Dunsany's choice of Saragossa gestures towards the historical reputation of Spanish universities as sites where forbidden knowledge (alchemy, astronomy, the occult sciences) found institutional shelter.

(020-002) The Chair's wider context involves the transmission of magical learning: "His care for humanity was solely this, that amongst its children, whether in Spain or elsewhere, were those that were worthy to receive and cherish, and carry to those that would bring it to the far distant ages the learning that he had." The Chair is not merely an honour; it is a node in a chain of magical transmission stretching from the ancient past to "the far distant ages."

## The Count of the Mountain

The Count of the Mountain is the Chair's most famous holder and the Magician's model of achievement. Though he never appears directly in the narrative, his shadow falls across the novel's entire magical economy. He is the proof that magical eminence is possible; his torment is the cost that the Magician, in his deepest ambition, has already accepted.

The Count's title — "of the Mountain" — suggests remoteness, elevation, and a certain inhumanity. He dwells apart from ordinary society, occupying a position that is simultaneously geographical and spiritual. His mountain is the pinnacle of magical achievement, and like all pinnacles, it is a place of isolation and exposure.

## Theme

The Chair of Magic embodies the novel's ambivalence about [[magic]]: it is both the highest intellectual honour and a source of torment. [[the-magician|The Magician]]'s pursuit of comparable status drives much of his behaviour, including his willingness to take [[the-charwoman|the charwoman]]'s [[shadow]] as [[magical-contracts|fee]].
