# The Charwoman

The charwoman — known in her youth as Anemone and in her servitude as Dockweed — is the emotional and moral centre of *The Charwoman's [[shadow|Shadow]]*. Her [[shadow]] was taken by [[the-magician|the Magician]] generations ago as payment for some forgotten service, and her condition — [[shadow]]less, ageless, outcast — drives the novel's plot and gives it its title.

## Role in the Story

(003-004) She first appears as an ancient figure: "Age had withered her beyond all fair seeming," holding a [[the-lantern|lantern]], coming to warn the new apprentice about the [[magical-contracts|price of magic]]. She is both victim and oracle — her condition is the warning [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] initially fails to heed.

(003-017) Her simple explanation of her plight is devastating: "He took it away and put it in his box. What did I know of the need one has of a [[shadow]]: that they would not speak to me, would not let me live? They never told me they set such store by their [[shadow|shadows]]." The tragedy is not only the theft but her ignorance of its consequences — she did not know what she was giving up.

(005-032) Her [[shadow]] becomes [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]]'s secret mission: "a plan to find the [[the-box|magician's box]], and open it and get the charwoman's [[shadow]], and give it to her to dance once more at her heels in the evening." The image of the [[shadow]] dancing at her heels captures everything she has lost — companionship, normalcy, the simple pleasure of being seen.

> Age had withered her beyond all fair seeming, but a certain curious brightness remained in her eyes. She held the lantern high and looked at the young man with an expression that was neither hope nor despair but some long patience that had outlasted both. (003-004)

## Attributes & Abilities

(004-002) Her [[shadow]]less condition is both curse and insight: "the crone... turned about with her [[the-lantern|lantern]] and went [[shadow]]less out of the room." She carries her own light because she cannot cast a [[shadow]] — a literal and metaphorical description of her state.

(005-064) Despite centuries of servitude, she retains hope: "she shook her head and went on with the scrubbing, for she despaired of him ever finding her [[shadow]]." Her despair is not anger but exhaustion — she has seen too many apprentices come and go.

(009-007) She possesses deep cultural memory: she listens "with quiet rapture to every word that he told of the [[aragona|old Aragona]] that lived in" earlier times. She is the last living link to a world before [[the-magician|the Magician]]'s Art consumed everything.

## Key Relationships

### With the Magician

(003-017) The Magician is her thief and master, but their relationship is not one of active cruelty — it is bureaucratic horror. He took her [[shadow]] as a routine transaction and filed it in his box. She cleans his floors. Centuries pass. The banality of the evil is what makes it so chilling: the Magician does not hate the charwoman; he simply does not think of her as a person whose shadow matters.

### With Ramon Alonzo

(005-032) Ramon Alonzo is the first person in generations to care about her loss. His youthful plan to rescue her [[shadow]] is naive but genuine, and it transforms her from background sufferer to active participant in the plot. Their relationship — the young apprentice and the ancient charwoman — is the novel's moral centre: the young man who *could* become another Magician chooses instead to become something better.

(009-007) Her arc is one of gradual re-engagement with hope. From the despairing scrubbing of Chapter 5 to the "quiet rapture" of Chapter 9, she begins to believe that her [[shadow]] might actually be recovered. Her liberation — when it comes — is the novel's true climax, more significant than any alchemical achievement.

## Thematic Significance

The charwoman embodies the novel's central moral argument: that magic, however wondrous, is built on invisible suffering. The Magician's Art — his transmutations, his potions, his mastery of Nature — depends on a simple act of theft from a woman who "did not know what she was giving up." Dunsany's genius is to make the victim of this cosmic crime not a hero or a princess but a charwoman: the least powerful person imaginable, whose loss no one noticed for centuries.
