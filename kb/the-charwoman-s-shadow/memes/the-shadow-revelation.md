# The Shadow Revelation

The [[the-charwoman|charwoman]]'s confession to [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] — that her [[shadow]] was taken as payment and stored in a box — is the novel's first major turning point. Before this moment, the story is about a [[ramon-alonzo|young man]] learning [[alchemy]]. After it, the story is about the moral imperative to right an ancient wrong.

## The Revelation

(003-017) Her words are devastatingly simple: "He took it away and put it in his box. What did I know of the need one has of a [[shadow]]: that they would not speak to me, would not let me live? They never told me they set such store by their [[shadow|shadows]]." The triple repetition — "He took it. He took it. He took it away" — conveys the trauma of a loss so old it has become the rhythm of her speech.

(003-004) The timing is significant: the revelation comes at night, by [[the-lantern|lantern]]-light, from a woman "withered beyond all fair seeming." The Gothic atmosphere reinforces the horror of her condition — she is a living ghost, centuries old, trapped in servitude.

## Impact on Ramon Alonzo

(005-032) The revelation germinates into his secret plan: "a plan to find the [[the-box|magician's box]], and open it and get [[the-charwoman|the charwoman]]'s [[shadow]], and give it to her to dance once more at her heels in the evening." The image of the [[shadow]] dancing is remarkably tender — his motivation is not justice in the abstract but the restoration of a simple, human pleasure.

## Narrative Significance

This event is the hinge on which the entire plot turns. Without it, [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] would simply study [[alchemy]], pay his [[shadow]], and go home rich. The revelation gives him — and the reader — a reason to care about [[shadow|shadows]] beyond their function as [[magic]]al currency.
