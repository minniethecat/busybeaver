The Saints in *[[the-charwoman|The Charwoman]]'s [[shadow|Shadow]]* represent the orthodox Christian framework that stands in opposition to [[the-black-art|the Black Art]]. They are invoked by characters as protectors, intercessors, and symbols of the moral order threatened by [[magic]].

## Invocations

[002-005] When confronted with the mysterious stranger at the inn, a character exclaims: "May all such blessed Saints defend us as can" — the first of many such invocations, establishing the Saints as a reflexive defence against the unknown.

[008-011] Villagers' fear of [[magic]] is expressed through saintly invocation: "'The Saints defend us,' cried another, 'there's no learned man in the wood.'" The Saints are the common person's shield against the terrors of arcane knowledge.

[008-013] A parallel cry: "The Saints forbid!" — reinforcing the pattern of spontaneous religious invocation in moments of supernatural unease.

[012-013] After a successful moment in his quest, [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] reverts to the language of his upbringing: "'Now the Saints be praised,' said Ramon Alonzo, reverting to an old way of speech that he did not use in that house." His return to saintly language marks a momentary return to his pre-magical identity.

[001-007] The Lord of the Tower invokes "the Saints and the glories of the sunlight upon the mountains" when addressing [[ramon-alonzo|his son]], situating the Saints within the aristocratic, pre-magical world of honour and family duty.

## Function in the Novel

The Saints function as a narrative counterweight to [[magic]]. Every saintly invocation reminds the reader of the world [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] is leaving behind — a world of faith, community, and moral clarity. [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]] embodies this tension most acutely, as a man of the Church who must reckon with the reality of the [[the-black-art|Black Art]].
