# Rocky Forest

The forest that lies beside the Tower, forming a natural boundary between the Tower's lands and the open country beyond. It features in the full title of the Tower's lord ("Lord of the Tower and Rocky Forest") and serves throughout the novel as both a physical and symbolic boundary.

## Location & Character

The Tower is described as "the last house that stood in the open lands; on the one side the forest cut it off entirely from the world" (006-003). The forest is a wild region of mixed terrain — heather, rocks, and ancient woodland. A path runs "through a part of the garden; then to the wild, then turned from the heather and rocks and ran awhile through the forest" (029-009), suggesting a gradual transition from cultivated land to true wilderness.

## Role in the Narrative

### Physical Boundary

The forest serves as the Tower's separation from the mundane world. Father Joseph, when organizing the Tower's defense during the Duke's visit, uses the forest as a natural fortification — it limits the directions from which visitors (or threats) can approach.

### Title and Identity

The full formal name "Ramon Alonzo Matthew-Mark-Luke-John of the Tower and Rocky Forest" (001-001) embeds the forest into the family's identity. It is not merely a geographical feature but part of the ancestral domain, giving the family's nobility a tangible territorial basis.

### Journey Context

When characters travel to and from the Tower, the forest provides the transitional space. It is through the forest that the path from Aragona passes, and it is within the forest's shadow that the Tower's isolation feels most complete.

> To the Tower beside the forest rumour came seldom, for it was the last house that stood in the open lands; on the one side the forest cut it off entirely from the world. (006-003)

## Symbolic Function

The Rocky Forest embodies the boundary between civilization and wilderness, knowledge and mystery. The Tower, standing at its edge, is literally and figuratively a liminal space — between settled Aragona and untamed nature, between mundane life and magical practice. The forest's "rocky" character — its wildness and resistance to cultivation — mirrors the magician's own resistance to social convention.
