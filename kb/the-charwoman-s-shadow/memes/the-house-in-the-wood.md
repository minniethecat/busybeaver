# The House in the Wood

[[the-magician|The Magician]]'s house in the wood is the novel's central location — a place of study, servitude, and hidden [[shadow|shadows]]. Its isolation from ordinary society mirrors [[the-magician|the Magician]]'s separation from normal human connection, while its interior — rooms sacred to [[magic]], stairs of timber and stone, wainscots cluttered with lumber — provides the physical framework for every [[magic]]al act in the story.

## Description

(003-002) The house is accessed through "the old green door" — a threshold between the mundane world and the realm of [[magic]]. Inside, it is a place of [[the-lantern|lantern]]s, spiral stairs, and rooms whose purposes shift between the domestic and the arcane. [[the-magician|The Magician]] sups with his apprentice in one room and performs [[shadow]]-copying in another.

(013-003) The most important chamber is "the room that was sacred to [[magic]]" — a sanctum reached by "the spiral stair of timber and stone," entered only at specific times and under specific conditions.

(005-064) The house's clutter serves as concealment: [[the-box|the box]] of [[shadow|shadows]] sits among "the lumber that ran all round the wainscot," its ordinariness its best disguise.

## Inhabitants

The house accommodates a hierarchy of beings: [[the-magician|the Magician]] at the top, his apprentice, [[the-charwoman|the charwoman]] who scrubs its floors, [[peter|Peter the messenger]], and the invisible [[supernatural-beings|imps]] who bake bread at a fire in the wood. It is both home and workplace, sanctuary and prison.
