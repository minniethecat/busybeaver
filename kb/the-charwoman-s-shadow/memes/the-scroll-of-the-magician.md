# The Scroll of the Magician

The ancient document that serves as [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]]'s credentials when he first approaches [[the-magician|the Magician]] — a letter of friendship written eighty years earlier for his grandfather.

## Description and History

The scroll was written by [[the-magician|the Magician]] some eighty years before the novel's main events, as a gesture of gratitude to [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]]'s grandfather, who had taught [[the-magician|the Magician]] the art of boar-hunting. [[the-magician|The Magician]] "withdrew a cork from a horn that hung from his girdle and that was filled with ink, and taking a goose-quill and writing there in the wood upon a little scroll" (001-019).

The scroll's content is simple: "it was a letter of friendship or welcome to my father or to whomever he should send with that scroll to the [[the-house-in-the-wood|house in the wood]]" (001-019). Though mundane in content, the scroll carries enormous weight — it represents a debt of gratitude owed by [[the-magician|the Magician]] to the family, a debt that entitles the bearer to [[the-magician|the Magician]]'s instruction.

The scroll was preserved among "a pale heap of boars' tusks" in [[lord-of-the-tower|the Lord]] of the Tower's rummage-room, alongside "old fishing nets that had become solid with dust... worn-out boar-spears... rusted bandilleros... blunt knives and broken tent-pegs" (001-028). This humble storage among hunting trophies reflects the family's relationship with [[the-magician|the Magician]]: rooted in the practical, earthy matter of boar-hunting rather than in [[magic|mystical arts]].

## Role in the Story

When [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] arrives at [[the-magician|the Magician]]'s [[the-house-in-the-wood|house in the wood]], the scroll is his only credential. "Then quickly, though still without fear, [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] thrust out the scroll that he bore, with the [[the-magician|magician]]'s own writing upon it all in black ink; saying, word for word as his father had bade him say, 'I am the grandson of him that taught you the taking of boars nigh eighty years ago'" (002-011).

The scroll works: "The [[the-magician|magician]] received it, and as he read his smile [[transmutation-principle|change]]d its nature and appeared to [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] somewhat more wholesome, having something in common with smiles of unlearned men that they smile at what is pleasant in earthly affairs" (002-011). The written word — even a simple letter of friendship — carries binding force in [[the-magician|the Magician]]'s world. The debt of boar-hunting wisdom, recorded in ink eighty years before, is honored.

The scroll also represents a crucial theme: that the [[the-magician|Magician]], for all his supernatural power, is bound by earthly obligations. A letter of friendship, a debt of gratitude, a promise made to a long-dead boar-hunter — these mundane things constrain even the [[the-magician|master]] of [[the-art|the Art]].
