# Power and Lordship

Power in *The Charwoman's Shadow* operates through a feudal hierarchy of lords, dukes, and magicians, each wielding different kinds of authority—temporal, spiritual, and supernatural.

## Definition

Lordship in the novel denotes legitimate temporal authority over land and people. The Lord of the Tower and Rocky Forest holds "such power as is permitted on earth" within his small domain at the edge of the forest (001-535). This power is limited and local, yet it commands the swift obedience of Father Joseph, who runs breathlessly to do the Lord's bidding, hoping "to turn towards heavenly uses" his earthly influence. Beyond the local lord, higher powers exist: the approach of the "dread Magnifico and illustrious prince, the serene and potent Duke of Shadow Valley" (001-533) causes the Tower household to reconsider all its social calculations.

## Manifestations

Temporal power is exercised through patronage, marriage alliances, and the control of resources. The Lord's authority manifests in his ability to command Father Joseph's pen, to arrange his daughter's marriage to Gulvarez, and to send his son to study under the magician. Yet his power is constrained by economic necessity—the rocky soil unsuited to husbandry forces him to seek gold through magical rather than feudal means.

The magician represents a different form of lordship: power derived from knowledge rather than birth. He speaks of "the rank that I now hold amongst wizards" (001-157), positioning himself within a hierarchy of magical attainment that mirrors the feudal order. His power over imps, draughts, and the very substance of shadow places him outside ordinary social structures while remaining dependent on them for fees and pupils.

## Narrative Significance

The structures of power create the conditions for the novel's central plot: a lord's need for gold to maintain his family's position sends his son into the magician's service. Power relations determine who may ask what of whom—Father Joseph's deference, the Lord's awe of the Duke, and the magician's transactional relationship with all who seek his Art. The novel suggests that all forms of lordship, whether temporal or magical, ultimately rest on precarious foundations.
