# The Magician Departs

The closing event of the novel, in which [[the-magician|the Magician]] abandons his [[the-house-in-the-wood|house in the wood]] and leads a fantastic procession of [[magic]]al beings over the Pyrenees, signalling the final passing of the [[the-golden-age|Golden Age]].

## Summary

In the final chapter, after losing both [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] and [[the-charwoman|Anemone]], [[the-magician|the Magician]] accepts that his time in Spain is over. [030-030] He "went all alone to his room that was sacred to magic, and took from the dust and darkness of a high shelf a volume in which he had written all he had learned about boar-hunting." Finding no purpose remaining, "he felt it was vain to look for another now who should be worthy to receive from him the tremendous secrets of old, he rose from his book and said, 'The years grow late.'" [030-030]

He goes to his tower and drinks one last dose of [[elixir-vitae|elixir vitae]]: "He went then to his tower and quaffed one gulp of that fluid that was named *elixir vitae*, and, carrying the bottle to that passage that for so long Anemone scrubbed, he cast it heavily down upon the stone." [030-030]

Taking a flute of reed and cloaking himself, he walks into the wood and blows "one bar upon it of curious music" [030-031]. In response, "there came to his ears the scurry of little things, nimble, elvish, and sprightly, over dead leaves" [030-031], and "there followed him all manner of magical things: fays, imps, and fauns, and all such children of Pan." [030-031]

In the open lands he raises his pipe again with "two strange notes" [030-032], summoning "tiny enchanted folk from many an elf-mound and many a fairy ring" [030-032]. On the high hills the same two notes draw forth "all that had their sole dwelling in moonlight and river-mist, or in the deep romance that overflows from old tales" [030-032].

[[the-magician|The Magician]] crosses the Pyrenees with this wild retinue and "butterflies that had followed him out of Spain" [030-032]. As he passes, "all the cathedrals rang their bells against him" [030-032], but the bells cannot stop the exodus: "the things of romance and mystery went leaping after the Master" [030-032].

## Key Participants

- **[[the-magician|The Magician]]** — the departing figure who leads the [[magic]]al exodus after the collapse of his enterprise [030-030]
- The **[[magic]]al creatures** — "fays, imps, and fauns, and all such children of Pan" [030-031], plus "old shadows, some taken from earthly folk" [030-032] — who follow him out of Spain

## Causes & Context

[[the-magician|The Magician]]'s departure is precipitated by the collapse of his enterprise. [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] has reclaimed his [[shadow]], [[the-charwoman|Anemone]] has been freed, and [[the-shadows-release|the shadows have been released]]. Finding himself alone and without purpose, "he felt it was vain to look for another now who should be worthy to receive from him the tremendous secrets of old" [030-030].

His departure is framed as inevitable — the end of an era. The novel has consistently positioned the [[the-golden-age|Golden Age]] as a waning epoch, and [[the-magician|the Magician]] as one of its last representatives. His crossing of the Pyrenees is both a personal retreat and a cosmic event: the departure of [[magic]] itself from the world of men.

## Consequences

[[the-magician|The Magician]]'s departure marks the definitive end of [[the-golden-age|the Golden Age]]. With him go "whatever was magical, and all those things that dwelt in the olden time and are only known to us through legend and fable" [030-032]. The cathedrals' bells ring out in triumph, but the narrative suggests a deeper loss — the passing of wonder and mystery from the ordinary world.

For [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]] and [[the-charwoman|Anemone]], the departure means freedom. "He sent no spell to follow after Anemone and her lover, as for a while they had feared" [030-030]. Their wedding, conducted by [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]] [030-029], proceeds without [[magic]]al interference. The mortal world reasserts itself.

The novel's final paragraph records the Magician's ultimate journey: "on and on the magician strode, undaunted by rain or night or rivers or mountains, going onward guided by dawns, always due eastwards" [030-033]. At journey's end he sees "the luminous rock of the bastions and glittering rampart that rose up sheer from the frontier of the Country Towards Moon's Rising" [030-033]. The robed Masters "raised their hands and blessed him" [030-033]. He passes through the gates with his magical following, and with that, "the Golden Age was over" [030-034].

The Magician's track leaves "a track of fable and curious folklore that, except where it is lost near cities and highways, can be followed even yet" [030-032] — framing the entire narrative as a legend of what was lost when [[magic]] departed from Europe.
