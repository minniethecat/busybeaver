# White Magic

White magic in *[[the-charwoman-s-shadow|The Charwoman's Shadow]]* represents the holy power of Christianity, set in direct opposition to the [[magic|Black Art]] practised by [[the-magician|the Magician]]. It is introduced in Chapter 21 when [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]] rides into the wood and his mere presence disperses the supernatural creatures that inhabit it.

## Manifestation

(021-006) The approach of white magic is heralded by the flight of magical creatures. [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]], sitting in the wood, witnesses [[supernatural-beings|imps]] and [[supernatural-beings|gnomes]] fleeing in terror:

> "He saw their small round bodies bobbing by, then heard them brush through the bracken into the distance, and not for a moment did they pause in their flight."

The gnome Alaraba, when questioned, can only utter "White magic!" — the one thing "that magical folk dread most."

(021-009) The white magic is embodied in [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]] himself:

> "This was nothing that fled: this then was the white magic."

Unlike the fleeing imps and gnomes, [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]] approaches calmly on his mule — his presence is the cause of the disturbance, not subject to it. The white magic does not flee; it advances.

## Powers

(021-021) The sign of the Cross dispels magical illusions. When [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]] makes the sign before [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]]:

> "the false shadow fell off from his heels"

The false shadow — a magical construct — falls away under the power of the Cross. Holy water completes the banishment: the false shadow "got up and ran away," fleeing into the "great true shadows of trees."

(021-032) White magic is linked to salvation. [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]] explains that a magical shadow, if attached to a man at death, "leads him violently to Hell. Four angels could not drag him from it." White magic thus serves as the only force capable of breaking a magical bond that would otherwise damn the soul.

## Contrast with Black Magic

| Aspect | White Magic | Black Magic |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Source** | [[faith-and-magic|Heaven]], the Church | [[magic|The Black Art]], heathen learning |
| **Practitioner** | [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]] | [[the-magician|The Magician]] |
| **Effect on magical creatures** | Disperses them in terror | Commands them |
| **Effect on shadows** | Banishes false shadows | Captures and binds shadows |
| **Ultimate end** | Salvation | Damnation |

(021-024) The chapter's title — "White Magic Comes to the Wood" — frames [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]]'s entry into the narrative as an intrusion of divine power into a domain long ruled by the [[magic|Black Art]]. The wood, previously the magician's territory, is cleansed by the priest's passage.

## Significance

White magic in the novel is not a separate magical system but the antithesis of magic itself. It requires no study, no books, no bargains — only faith and ordination. Where [[the-magician|the Magician]]'s power is acquired through decades of study and the payment of terrible prices ([[shadow|shadows]]), [[father-joseph|Father Joseph]]'s power is inherent in his office. This asymmetry — effortless holy power against laborious dark magic — is central to the novel's moral framework and to [[ramon-alonzo|Ramon Alonzo]]'s eventual choice between the two paths.
