# The Folk of Aragona Strike for the Faith

A religiously-motivated confrontation in Chapter XIV, when the folk of Aragona — roused by fear of sorcery — rise up against the shadowless Ramon Alonzo as he attempts to pass through their lands. The episode blends elements of peasant superstition, religious zeal, and the practical perils of traveling without a shadow.

## Summary

Ramon Alonzo, having delayed too long in the wood with the charwoman, finds himself traveling when the shadows are already shortening. Unable to risk being seen by the folk of Aragona — who would recognize a shadowless man as one who has trafficked in sorcery — he hides under an oak until the afternoon heat passes (014-003). But when he ventures onto the road, he is spotted. The folk of Aragona, armed with a lantern and religious fervor, organize a hunt for the "witch" in their midst.

The confrontation becomes violent. One of the pursuers, aiming a blow "for the mother of St. Anne" (014-029), is met by Ramon Alonzo's sword — the young man's blade enters his ribs, and the pursuit scatters into the night.

## Key Participants

- **Ramon Alonzo** — The shadowless protagonist, forced to fight his way through hostile country. His combat skills, trained in the Tower, prove sufficient against untrained peasantry but the moral cost is evident.
- **The Folk of Aragona** — Not individually named, but acting as a collective — a mob driven by fear of the supernatural and the sanctioned violence of religious purification.

## Causes & Context

The folk of Aragona's hostility stems from deep-rooted beliefs about shadows and sorcery:

1. **Shadow as Moral Marker**: In the novel's world, a person without a shadow is assumed to have sold it to the Devil — a transaction marking them as a witch or sorcerer.

2. **Religious Fervor**: The strike is explicitly framed as one "for the Faith" (014-001), blending folk superstition with institutional religious authority. The cry "for the mother of St. Anne" (014-029) underscores this fusion.

3. **The Siesta Strategy**: Ramon Alonzo's attempt to travel during siesta — when others rest and shadows are shortest — is a clever adaptation, but ultimately fails when he must venture onto the open road.

## Consequences

The episode has several narrative consequences:

1. **Moral Stain**: Ramon Alonzo, previously an innocent apprentice, has now killed a man. Even in self-defense, this marks a transition from student to participant in the world's violence.

2. **Night Journey**: The confrontation delays his progress and forces him to flee through darkness, with voices "behind him" that eventually "dropped off and mingled with the far noises of night" (014-030).

3. **Country-Towards**: The strike sets the stage for the fear that spreads to "the country towards moon's rising" (Chapter XXVII), where similar superstitions arise.

> "Aye, it is gone," said they all. So they went to bed. (014-031–032)
