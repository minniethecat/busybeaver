# The Three Fair Fields

Three pastoral fields near the Tower in Aragona, situated under the evening shade of the forest. These lands are the price demanded by Señor Gulvarez for his daughter Mirandola's hand in marriage to the Duke, making them a critical territorial and narrative element in *The Charwoman's Shadow*.

## Location & Description

The three fair fields lie at the edge of the forest near the Tower, described as "the pastures that lay at evening under the shade of the forest" (017-025). They are visible from the Tower grounds and form part of the Aragona landscape that the Tower overlooks. Gonsalvo, observing the fields, reflects that "perhaps two" would suffice — but "no, Gulvarez" would demand all three (017-025).

## Significance in the Narrative

### Gulvarez's Demand

When the Duke seeks Mirandola's hand, Gulvarez demands the three fair fields as the price. This is not merely a land transaction — it is a test of the Duke's commitment and a display of Gulvarez's materialistic bargaining. The fields become a symbol of the transactional nature of power in the novel's world.

### The Duke's Illness

During the Duke's seven-day illness, the matter of the three fair fields remains unresolved. The Duke "had no wrath for Mirandola, and none else durst venture into his presence" (029-013). The fate of the fields hangs in the balance throughout his convalescence.

### The Resolution

On the seventh day, at the Duke's instruction, Gonsalvo escorts Gulvarez to view the fields to complete the transaction. Walking through the three fair fields, Gonsalvo attempts to speak of their beauty — "They are fair fields, are they not?" (029-033) — but Gulvarez's mind is occupied with darker thoughts regarding the casket of silver and oak. The fields, beautiful as they are, cannot distract Gulvarez from the material wealth he has secured.

> Gonsalvo believed that all things were as bright as his own mood, but when he perceived that this was not so with Gulvarez he spoke to him of the three fair fields... But Gulvarez was still thinking of that casket. (029-034)

## Thematic Role

The three fair fields represent the pastoral ideal that contrasts with the novel's darker magical elements. They are "fair" in multiple senses — beautiful, just, and legitimate. Unlike the shadow economy of the Tower (stolen shadows, alchemical secrets, love potions), the fields are transparent in their value: good pasture land, openly bargained for. The transaction over the three fair fields is one of the few deals in the novel conducted in honest daylight.
