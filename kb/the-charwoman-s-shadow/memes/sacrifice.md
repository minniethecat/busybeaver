## Definition

Though the word "sacrifice" never appears in the novel, the concept of cost — of giving up something precious in exchange for power or knowledge — runs through the narrative. The Magician's fees "are never material things, but are dreams, hopes, and illusions" [004-018], establishing that magic exacts immaterial but profound prices from those who practice it or seek its aid.

## The Cost of Magic

The Magician's explicit statement about his fees reveals the novel's underlying economy of sacrifice. He does not demand gold or goods but "dreams, hopes, and illusions, and whatever other great forces control the fortune of nations" [004-018]. These immaterial offerings — the stuff of human aspiration and meaning — are the true currency of magic.

## Thematic Sacrifices

Several characters make implicit sacrifices throughout the narrative. Ramon Alonzo forfeits his place in ordinary society when he becomes associated with magic, cut off from "times and occupations" by his altered shadow [014-008]. The charwoman loses decades of her life to the shadow's magic, her youth exchanged for an unnatural old age. These are not ritual sacrifices but narrative ones — losses incurred through entanglement with supernatural forces.

## The Offering of Sympathy

The word "offering" appears in the novel only in mundane contexts: characters "offering ministrations" to the sick [018-011], "offering to carry the plates" [018-043], or "offering her sympathy" [027-002]. Yet even these simple usages reflect the novel's broader pattern of giving and receiving — of what one person extends to another as a gift or comfort.
