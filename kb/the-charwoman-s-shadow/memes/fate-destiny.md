## Definition

Fate and destiny in the novel are cosmic forces that shape the course of human lives. Destiny is personified as an active feminine agent who "uses" moments "to perfect an event with which she will shape the years" [030-023]. Fortune governs nations and individual fates alike [004-018], and the ultimate "human destiny" overtakes even the Magician at the end of his earthly journey [030-033].

## Destiny Personified

The novel's most explicit treatment of destiny occurs in the final chapter, during the climactic confrontation between Ramon Alonzo and the assembled company. In the silence following his bitter words, the narrator invokes Destiny directly:

> During one of those brief moments that Destiny uses often to perfect an event with which she will shape the years, none of them spoke. [030-023]

This personification — Destiny as a craftsman using moments as tools — elevates the dramatic pause into a cosmic event, framing the characters' choices within a larger ordained pattern.

## Fortune and the Magician's Fee

When the Magician states his terms to Ramon Alonzo's father, he declares that his fees "are never material things, but are dreams, hopes, and illusions, and whatever other great forces control the fortune of nations" [004-018]. This equates fortune with other immaterial forces — dreams, hopes, illusions — that the Magician traffics in, suggesting that national and personal destinies are manipulable by those who command magic.

## The Magician's Final Destiny

The Magician's journey ends with "the human destiny" overtaking him as he approaches the gates of the Country Towards Moon's Rising. "Before the human destiny overtook him he saw one morning, clear where the dawn had been, the luminous rock of the bastions and glittering rampart" [030-033]. Even the master of magic cannot escape his mortal end — destiny is a universal force that claims all, magician and mortal alike.

## Doom

The word "doom" appears throughout the novel as the darker face of destiny — the inevitable end that awaits all things. The "dooms of the Last Judgment" [027-035] define the boundary of salvation, while the Magician's smile at his door may be "for welcome, or at a doom that hung over the strangers who troubled his door" [002-011].
