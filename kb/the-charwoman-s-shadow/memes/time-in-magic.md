## Definition

Time in the novel is not merely the medium in which events occur but a substance that magic can manipulate. The magician's most striking power — his survival across centuries — is achieved through the elixir vitae, a potion that arrests aging. Time becomes a resource to be managed, dosed, and resisted, setting the magician apart from ordinary mortals who are "unable to withstand the flight of the years" [002-021].

## The Elixir Vitae

The magician keeps "on a shelf in an upper room a bottle of that medicine philosophers use, which is named *elixir vitae*, wherein were sufficient doses to ensure his survival till the time when he knew that the world would begin to grow bad" [002-021]. He takes one dose every thirty years — a generational rhythm tied to the life-cycle of those around him: "By certain turns in the tide of life in those that he watched, a touch of grey over the ears, a broadening or a calming, he knew that the heyday of a generation was past" [002-021].

> He keeps on a shelf in an upper room a bottle of that medicine philosophers use, which is named elixir vitae, wherein were sufficient doses to ensure his survival till the time when he knew that the world would begin to grow bad. He took one dose in every generation. [002-021]

## Generations and Magic

The novel structures magical knowledge across generations. Ramon Alonzo's grandfather studied with the same magician eighty years earlier, receiving the scroll that initiates the young man's journey [001-020]. The magician speaks of "the researches of your grandfather" [002-032] with respect, and the scroll itself — "with the ink of the magician upon it written eighty years agone" [002-010] — bridges two generations of practitioners.

The magician's mind, "well stored with the wisdom of several ages and repeatedly refreshed with the nimble alertness of youth" [002-021], represents the ideal fusion of temporal depth and present vitality that magical practice enables.

## Age and Material Decay

In counterpoint to the magician's personal immortality, his house reveals the passage of time through material decay. When shown to his room, Ramon Alonzo sees "blankets and sheets mouldered all in one heap together and a cobweb covered them over" [003-003]. The room's condition prompts the magician's laconic observation: "Old age comes to all" [003-003] — a statement whose irony is sharpened by the speaker's own exemption from this rule.

## Time and the Shadow

The shadow itself is temporal: "it was then as many hours before noon as the making of the shadow had been after it, it was just as long as the shadows of other men" [012-005]. The shadow's length tracks solar time, and its loss removes Ramon Alonzo from the temporal rhythms shared by ordinary people. Recovering the shadow means re-entering time's natural flow.
