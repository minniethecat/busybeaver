# Blaze of the Winds

## Definition

The Blaze of the Winds is the central technological-religious concept in "It Takes a Thief" (002). It is what remains of a [[space-travel|space]] vessel's propulsion system — a rocket engine or launch mechanism — after generations of oral transmission have transformed technical knowledge into ritual mystery. To "kindle the Blaze of the Winds" (002-114) means to activate the drive, to make the ship fly again. In the story, this phrase carries the weight of both blasphemy and liberation.

## Dual Nature

The Blaze exemplifies Miller's recurring theme of degraded knowledge. In [[the-priest|the priest]]ly chants, it appears as a sacred force: "the key to the Blaze of the Winds is in the vaults. The god Roggins tells us in the words" (002-056). The vaults are temple vaults; the [[ritual|ritual formula]]s describe launch procedures; the god Roggins is a corruption of some forgotten technical acronym or authority. What was once an engineering manual has become scripture.

[[asir|Asir]]'s insistence that "the blaze can be rekindled, and the air renewed" (002-056) reveals the practical stakes beneath the ritual language. The Martian colony is dying — its air running out, its environment decaying — and the Blaze represents the only escape: the ability to leave [[mars|Mars]], to return to [[earth|Earth]] or go elsewhere. The priests who guard the ritual do not understand what they guard; the thief who steals it intuits, without fully comprehending, that it is a key to survival.

## The Act of Kindling

To "kindle" the Blaze is the story's climactic act. [[asir|Asir]]'s declaration — "I am going to kindle the Blaze of the Winds" (002-116) — is delivered "stubbornly," against the warnings of his companion who fears "the spirits of the ancients" (002-114) that supposedly dwell in the vaults. The verb "kindle" is revealing: the colonists think of the drive as a fire to be lit, a ritual act, not as the ignition of a fusion torch or chemical rocket. They have the words and the procedures but not the conceptual framework.

The resolution of the story rests on [[asir|Asir]]'s partial understanding. He knows enough ritual to access the vaults and enough courage to attempt what [[the-priest|the priest]]s forbid. Whether he succeeds or fails — and what he finds if he succeeds — Miller leaves to implication. The Blaze, like many of Miller's technological artifacts, matters less as hardware than as symbol: of knowledge lost, of knowledge potentially regained, and of the human drive to break free of a dying world.
