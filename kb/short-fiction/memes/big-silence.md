# Big Silence

## Background

The Big Silence is the forty-year diplomatic and communications blackout between the [[federation|Western Federation of Autonomous States]] and the Asian Proletarian League that serves as the geopolitical foundation of "Check and Checkmate" (003). It is not a war, though it followed one; it is a condition of total mutual isolation in which "even radio communication [is] impossible between the two halves of the globe" (003-037), enforced by the [[hell-wall|Hell Wall]] and by interference stations that jam all signals.

## Political Consequences

The Silence's duration — two generations — means that no one alive on either side has direct knowledge of the other. The East has become "a mysterious unknown" (003-108), known only through rumors, propaganda, and the fragmentary reports of intelligence services that do "the best that it could, but the picture was fuzzy and incomplete" (003-074).

This opacity is politically useful. "During the years of the Big Silence, a feeling of uneasy security had evolved. The [[federation|Federation]] had been in isolation too long" (003-108). The Silence allows each regime to project whatever narrative about the enemy serves its domestic purposes. The Presidency "had oscillated between suspicious unease and smug confidence, depending perhaps upon the personality of the particular president more than anything else" (003-108).

## The Breach

[[john-smith-xvi|John Smith XVI]]'s decision to contact [[ivan-ivanovitch-ix|Ivan Ivanovitch IX]] by televiewphone represents the first direct communication across the Silence in forty years — "the greatest [moment] since the [[peace-effort|last peace-effort]]" (003-004). The technical difficulty is substantial: it requires overcoming jamming stations and negotiating relay paths with a government that has been entirely unreachable. The political risk is greater: direct contact threatens the informational monopoly that the Silence sustains.

When the breach succeeds, what Smith discovers is not the monstrous enemy of propaganda but a fellow pragmatist — an adversary as domestically constrained and politically vulnerable as himself. The Silence, it turns out, had protected both regimes from the uncomfortable recognition that the Other is disappointingly familiar.

## Resolution

The story's conclusion does not end the Big Silence — the [[hell-wall|Hell Wall]] remains, the two halves of the globe remain divided — but it punctures its epistemological function. Smith and Ivan have seen each other's faces. The mystery that sustained forty years of mutual fear has been, if not dispelled, at least diminished. What follows from that diminishment Miller leaves to implication.
