# Sheriff Yates

## Character Profile

Sheriff Yates is the off-duty local sheriff in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]" — a gruff, pragmatic lawman whom [[norris|Inspector Norris]] calls upon to assist in enforcing Bio-Administration regulations. Found by the [[artificial-intelligence|robot]] operator in a Wylo City pool hall, Yates's first words upon answering [[norris|Norris]]'s call are characteristically irritable: "I'm getting so I hate that infernal gadget. I think it's got me psyched" (001-029).

His relationship with [[norris|Norris]] is professionally cooperative but tinged with the casual informality of small-community law enforcement. When [[norris|Norris]] delivers bad news about needing warrants, "Yates bellowed lusty laughter into the phone" (001-030) — responding to bureaucratic inconvenience with an amusement that borders on the unprofessional. But when [[norris|Norris]] explains it's a rush-order involving [[mutant-animals|neutroids]], Yates immediately sobers: "Oh. Well, I'll take care of it" (001-030).

## Role in Narrative

Yates represents the coercive apparatus that stands behind [[norris|Norris]]'s bureaucratic role. While [[norris|Norris]] negotiates, persuades, and tries to minimize harm, Yates wields warrants and the threat of force. [[norris|Norris]]'s instructions to him — "fix it so the charges will be dropped if they cooperate. Don't shake those warrants around unless they just won't listen to reason. But get those [[mutant-animals|neutroids]]" (001-031) — reveal the layered nature of enforcement: persuasion first, threat second, seizure always.

By the next day, "Yates had already brought in the three from yesterday" (001-050), efficiently executing the seizure that [[norris|Norris]]'s more delicate approach had deferred. His presence in the story, though brief, illuminates the gap between [[norris|Norris]]'s personal moral qualms and the institutional machinery he commands.

## Key Scenes

> "I'm getting so I hate that infernal gadget," Yates grumbled. "I think it's got me psyched. What do you want, Norris?" (001-029)

Yates's introduction — found by an automated system in a pool hall, grumbling about surveillance — establishes the texture of life in the [[federation|Federation]]: ubiquitous monitoring accepted as normal, with only mild irritation as protest.

> Yates bellowed lusty laughter into the phone. (001-030)

This brief moment of raucous humor, in a story otherwise heavy with moral weight, gives Yates a distinct personality. He finds bureaucratic absurdity genuinely funny. But [[norris|Norris]]'s rejoinder — explaining the urgency — cuts the laughter short, and Yates immediately shifts to professional mode.
