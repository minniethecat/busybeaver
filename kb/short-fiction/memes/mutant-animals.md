# Mutant Animals

## Definition

Mutant animals are genetically engineered creatures produced by the Anthropos Corporation using a device called the "evolvotron." They occupy a morally ambiguous position in Miller's future society — legally classified as property but possessing varying degrees of intelligence, from animal-level to near-human. The term encompasses the full spectrum: from simple pets like cat-Qs and dog-Fs to the highly intelligent "neutroids" ([[neutroid|chimp-K series]]).

The [[federation|Federation]] "insists that all mutants be neuter and infertile" (001-366), preventing them from establishing self-sustaining populations. This biological containment is the legal foundation of their commodity status: they are manufactured, not born, and therefore can be owned, traded, and destroyed.

## Production and Commerce

Anthropos went into "the mutant-animal business" after developing the evolvotron (001-016), creating a commercial industry around artificial life. The creatures are sold through authorized dealerships — O'Reilley is described as an "authorized dealer in [[neutroid|mutant animals]]" handling "all non-predatory mutants" (001-181). The market is stratified by intelligence and appeal: cat-Q-5s and dog-Fs occupy the lower tiers, while chimp-K neutroids command premium prices and deeper emotional investment.

The [[federation|Federation]]'s Bio-Administration regulates the trade, employing district inspectors like [[norris|Norris]] to manage the mutant population. Each district has a replacement quota — [[norris|Norris]]'s district handled "around three hundred animals a month" (001-034) — and inspectors are responsible for rounding up unlicensed animals, housing them in kennels, and destroying those that remain unclaimed.

The market is carefully controlled. [[norris|Norris]] notes there is "no instance of an intelligent mutant getting into the market" (001-247), suggesting a regulatory framework that keeps the most advanced specimens contained within research or administrative channels.

## Social and Emotional Dimensions

The story portrays a society that has grown emotionally attached to mutants. [[norris|Norris]] observes that people "loved the artificial mutants as children" (001-161), treating them as family members. This emotional investment creates the central conflict of "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]": a society that produces creatures capable of inspiring love but legally defines them as disposable.

The class system compounds the emotional stakes. Class-C citizens, denied the right to have children, channel their parental instincts toward mutant pets. "A [[class-c-citizen|class-C couple]], allowed no children of their own, could get quite attached to a cat-Q-5" (001-030). The mutants become substitute children — a role the [[federation|Federation]]'s system simultaneously encourages and denies legitimacy.

The emotional calculus is carefully tiered. Cat-Qs and dog-Fs are "emotionally safer than the quasi-human [[neutroid|chimp-K series]] called 'neutroids'" (001-030). When a pet neutroid dies, "a family was broken with grief" — a grief the system regards as category error, mourning for property as though it were kin.

## Ethical Tensions

Not everyone supports the industry. Some elements of society "took a dim view of the whole mutant business" (001-311), hinting at broader ethical debates about creating intelligent life for commercial purposes. [[anne|Anne]], [[norris|Norris]]'s wife, crystallizes this opposition: "I didn't know you killed them" (001-008). Her accusation reframes [[norris|Norris]]'s bureaucratic duties as moral complicity.

The most charged ethical case involves neutroids — creatures intelligent enough to be mistaken for human children. [[doctor-georges|Doctor Georges]]'s desperate call about a woman whose neutroid is dying (001-039) exposes the gap between legal classification and lived reality. The woman "believes it's her own" (001-053); the law says it is chattel. [[norris|Norris]] navigates between these truths, bending rules in ways that acknowledge the law's inadequacy without openly defying it.
