# Martha

## Character Profile

Martha is the wife of [[old-donegal|Old Donegal]] in "Death of a Spaceman." She has been deceived — with her husband's collusion — about the severity of his condition. "Martha can bear my death, he thought, but not my dying" (004-008). The deception is [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s last gift to her, and also his last act of control.

Martha is present throughout [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s final hours: sitting beside his bed, "pinching the bridge of her nose to hold back tears" (004-015), fetching whiskey, participating in the uneasy performance of normalcy that [[old-donegal|Donegal]] demands. Her love is quiet, practical, and enduring — the domestic counterweight to [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s rough irreverence.

## Role in Narrative

Martha anchors the story's emotional reality. While [[old-donegal|Donegal]] wisecracks and [[the-priest|the priest]] theologizes, Martha simply suffers. Her presence — "she had fled from the room in tears" (004-007) — is the story's most direct register of grief.

The deception at the story's heart — Martha's ignorance of [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s true condition — raises uncomfortable questions. Is [[old-donegal|Donegal]] protecting her, or protecting himself from the intimacy of shared grief? Martha's apparent acceptance of the fiction suggests either genuine ignorance or a willing complicity in [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s preferred narrative.

## Key Scenes

> Martha had fled from the room in tears. [004-007]

Earlier in the story, before the reader arrives, something has already broken. Martha's tears establish the emotional stakes before [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s bravado has a chance to minimize them.

> "I feel fine, Martha. I like parties. I'm glad they're giving it." [004-033]

[[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s forced cheerfulness in Martha's presence. He performs wellness for her, and she performs belief for him. Their mutual performance is the story's true subject.

> Martha arose and tiptoed to the window. [004-049]

Small, careful movements — Martha navigating the sickroom with the instinct of long practice. Her physical gentleness contrasts with [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s verbal roughness.
