# Slubil

## Character Profile

Slubil is the village executioner in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "It Takes a Thief" — a role defined by ritual, law, and the bleeding-blade. Physically, he is "a squat man with a thick face" and "empty blue eyes" who sits "spraddle-legged and calm while he chewed a blade of grass" (002-005), casually playing mumblety-peg with the very knife that will determine [[asir|Asir]]'s fate.

His impassivity is not cruelty but professional patience. He waits hours while [[asir|Asir]] hangs from the post, periodically asking the ritual question — "Ready for me yet, [[asir|Asir]]?" (002-006) — without malice. He is "bound by law to wait until his victim requested his fate" (002-017), a constraint that makes him an instrument of the [[council|Council]]'s justice rather than its author.

## Role in Narrative

Slubil is a supporting figure whose function is to embody the ritualized, impersonal nature of Martian justice. He is not a villain — the [[council|Council]] of Senior Kinsmen, who judged [[asir|Asir]] in secret, hold that role — but a functionary performing a prescribed social role. His blank stare and unhurried demeanor suggest a man for whom violence is not passion but profession.

When [[asir|Asir]] finally begs for the knife, Slubil's performance becomes theatrical: he moves "about him slowly, teasing him with the whistle of steel fanning the air" (002-031), occasionally laying the blade against [[asir|Asir]]'s skin and taking it away. This is not sadism but ritual — the condemned must demonstrate true desperation before the sentence is executed. And when the blade falls, it strikes the post above [[asir|Asir]]'s head: the [[council|Council]] has ordered banishment, not death. Slubil marks [[asir|Asir]]'s forehead "raw and rubbed with rust to make a scar" (002-033), branding him an outcast, then binds his wounds.

## Key Scenes

> The executioner chuckled and played mumblety-peg. (002-008)

Slubil's defining characteristic is his unflappable calm. He plays a knife game while a young man bleeds from nail-pierced forearms a few feet away. His chuckle is not cruel but professional — he has done this many times and will do it many more.

> "In view of your extreme youth, thief," the knifeman growled, "the council has ordered you perpetually banished. The sun is setting. Let dawn find you in the hills. If you return to the plains, you will be chained to a wild hüffen and dragged to death." (002-034)

Slubil delivers the [[council|Council]]'s sentence with formal gravity. The mercy — banishment rather than death or dismemberment — is the [[council|Council]]'s, but Slubil executes it with the same ritual precision he would bring to an amputation. He rolls [[asir|Asir]] over with his foot afterward, a gesture that is both contemptuous and, given the circumstances, almost gentle.

## Relationships

- **[[asir]]** — The young ritual-thief whom Slubil is tasked with punishing. Their relationship is formally adversarial — executioner and condemned — but Slubil's impersonal professionalism creates a strange intimacy between them across the hours of waiting.
