# Peace Effort

## The Cycle of Failure

The Peace Efforts are the recurring diplomatic attempts to bridge the divide between the [[federation|Western Federation]] and the Asian Proletarian League — each of which has "ended in stalemate and total isolation" (003-019). They form a historical pattern that precedes and contextualizes the [[big-silence|Big Silence]]: not a single event but a cycle, each failure reinforcing the barrier it attempted to breach.

The term itself is contested. When [[john-smith-xvi|John Smith XVI]] announces his televiewphone contact with [[ivan-ivanovitch-ix|Ivan IX]], an aide corrects him immediately: "'War' — and now 'peace-effort.' Our semantics has progressed nicely, John" (003-008). The exchange reveals that "peace-effort" is the acceptable term for what was once called "war," and that the regime's control extends to the vocabulary used to describe its actions.

## The Last Peace Effort

The most recent peace-effort before the story's events — "the last big peace-effort" — ended in stalemate and total isolation (003-019). Its physical legacy is visible in the landscape: a helicopter landing near "a long, windowless concrete building which lay in the shadow of an old control-tower's skeleton. The tower was twisted awry, and the concrete was pockmarked by shrapnel or bullets dating back to one of the peace-efforts" (003-189).

This physical detritus — twisted towers, bullet-pocked concrete — is the peace-effort's only monument. The efforts that were meant to end conflict have themselves become artifacts of conflict, indistinguishable in their material remains from the wars they were supposed to prevent.

## Smith's Variation

[[john-smith-xvi|John Smith XVI]]'s approach differs from previous peace-efforts in method and motive. Rather than a formal diplomatic initiative — which would require congressional authorization and public visibility — he arranges a direct personal contact with [[ivan-ivanovitch-ix|Ivan IX]] through technically clandestine channels. His motive is not ideological commitment to peace but political calculation: "It would also be a popular triumph he could toss to the rabble, bolstering his position with them, and thereby securing his hold on the Primary, Secondary, and [[stand-in|Tertiary Stand-in]]s" (003-082).

The irony is that Smith's cynical peace-effort may be the most successful precisely because it is cynical. Unburdened by ideology, he can deal with Ivan as one pragmatist to another. When his [[stand-in|Stand-in]]s gasp at the possibility — "You're *not* contemplating another peace-effort, John?" (003-088) — their horror is not at the goal but at the risk to their own position. A genuine peace would eliminate the threat that sustains the [[stand-in|Stand-in]] system.
