# Ivan Ivanovitch IX

## Character Profile

Ivan Ivanovitch IX is the Peoplesfriend and Vicar of the Asian Proletarian League in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "Check and Checkmate." A heavyset man who initially appears wearing "a red rubber or plastic helmet-mask" — the face of the first Soviet dictator, dead over a century ago (003-024) — he is [[john-smith-xvi|John Smith XVI]]'s counterpart across the [[hell-wall|Hell Wall]] that has divided East from West for forty years.

When Ivan removes his mask, he reveals "an impassive Oriental face with deepset black eyes and a glowering frown" (003-032). The unmasking is both a political statement — he has nothing to fear from his own people — and a tactical move, demonstrating a confidence that Smith cannot initially match. Smith is forced to dismiss his [[stand-in|Stand-in]]s and remove his own mask in response, an act that leaves him psychologically exposed.

## Role in Narrative

Ivan functions as Smith's foil and mirror. Where Smith is calculating, indirect, and conscious of appearances, Ivan is blunt to the point of aggression: "Stop talking Achesonian, President. What do you want?" (003-026). He dismisses diplomatic niceties as obsolete and insists on what he calls "poker-game protocol" — two opponents trying "to cheat each other honestly and jovially" (003-027).

Ivan's most unsettling claim is delivered with casual confidence: "I am in a position to destroy you anyway, a position I have occupied for several years" (003-027). Smith dismisses this as bluff, but Ivan's elaboration is specific: the Asian League's weapons program has become "unidirectional, and extremely inexpensive" (003-027). He does not elaborate, and the ambiguity — is it a superweapon or misdirection? — becomes the story's central tension.

## Key Scenes

> The Red leader ripped off the mask, revealing an impassive Oriental face with deepset black eyes and a glowering frown. The President sucked in his breath. (003-032)

Ivan's unmasking is the story's dramatic turning point. By exposing his face while Smith remains hidden behind the golden mask of tragedy, Ivan inverts the expected power dynamic. His challenge — "Afraid to take off your mask, President?" — forces Smith into a choice between security and dignity.

> "Let it suffice to say that we know more about you than you know about us. Our system has changed." (003-031)

Ivan positions the Asian League as a society that has transcended its own history of "dogmatism and senseless butchery" (003-031) through pragmatic reform — the third Ivan's bloody reformation having purged Marxist dogmatics in favor of "a harsh but practical utilitarian civilization" (003-020). His claim that the Asian system is now more stable than the Western one is perhaps the story's most subversive suggestion.
