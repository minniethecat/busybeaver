# Ritual

## Definition

In the Martian setting of "It Takes a Thief" (002), "ritual" refers to memorized formulaic utterances — chants, phrases, and invocations — that function simultaneously as currency, technical manual, and religious artifact. The Martian colonists, descended from a shipwrecked generation, have lost all understanding of the technology that brought them there and preserved only fragments of knowledge in orally transmitted ritual forms: "remembered only in memorized ritual chants, the possession of which was restricted to [[the-priest|the priest]]ly caste" (002-004).

These rituals are not merely ceremonial. They are the degraded remnants of scientific and technical knowledge — launch codes, engineering procedures, navigation data — that the original colonists failed to pass down in comprehensible form. What was once physics has become prayer; what was once procedure has become incantation.

## Social Function

Ritual formulas are explicitly treated as economic assets. [[asir|Asir]], the protagonist, "had squandered his last ritual formula to buy a wife" (002-003), an act that left him socially diminished. When Tokra gives [[asir|Asir]] "a ritual phrase" to buy a hüf-rug for a girl, the transaction reveals ritual's dual nature: it is at once a sacred trust and a commodity that can be spent, stolen, or squandered.

The priestly caste controls access to ritual knowledge, and the power this confers is substantial. [[asir|Asir]]'s theft of ritual formulas from temple vaults is the story's inciting crime — "he filched them with a stolen bit of ritual" (002-078). The irony is layered: he uses one stolen ritual to steal others, demonstrating that ritual is simultaneously the lock and the key.

## Cognitive Status

The colonists' relationship to ritual is characterized by rote repetition without comprehension. [[asir|Asir]] recites his stolen formulas "with the mechanical drone like memorised ritual" (002-089), a description that doubles as critique: ritual here *is* mechanical, memory without understanding. The phrases refer to technologies — "[[blaze-of-the-winds|the Blaze]] of the Winds" (002-082), descriptions of [[space-travel|space]] vessel vaults — whose referents the speakers can no longer identify.

This cognitive degradation serves Miller's thematic purpose. The Martians have built an entire social order — priesthood, temple, vault, and currency — around knowledge they don't possess. They are, in a sense, cargo-cult technologists: preserving the forms of science long after its substance has evaporated.
