# Conditionally Human

## Definition

"Conditionally human" names the central moral insight of Walter M. Miller Jr.'s story of the same title — and a theme that echoes across his short fiction. In the [[federation|Federation]] of Miller's future, humanity is not a binary state but a spectrum, assigned by the state through genetic classification. Class-A citizens carry "clean" heredity certificates and full reproductive rights. Class-C citizens, like [[norris|Norris]] and his wife [[anne|Anne]], are marked by "[[class-c-citizen|defective heredity]]" (001-007) — heart trouble in both their families — and are legally barred from having children.

The phrase captures a chilling inversion: being human is not something you are, but something the state permits you to be. And the permission can be partial, conditional, revocable.

## The Classification System

The [[federation|Federation]]'s class system determines who may reproduce, who may work, and what kind of companionship — animal or quasi-human — they are permitted. Class-C couples "were allowed two lesser units or one neutroid" (001-007), channeling their parental instincts toward genetically engineered substitutes. The system is self-reinforcing: [[class-c-citizen|class-C couple]]s cannot pass on their "defective" genes because they cannot have children, making the classification effectively permanent.

Smith's conversation with his [[stand-in|Stand-in]]s in "Check and Checkmate" reveals how the system evolved through semantic engineering: "The Qualifications Amendment that limited it to the psychologically fit. And then the Education Amendment... And the Genetic Amendment, and the Selection Amendment" (003-008). Each layer added a new condition, each condition excluded more people, until "human" became a credential rather than a birthright.

## The Mutant Economy

The conditionally human classification system is inseparable from the [[mutant-animals|mutant-animal]] economy. [[neutroid|Neutroid]]s and other engineered creatures exist because [[class-c-citizen|class-C]] citizens exist — and the reverse is also true: the genetic classification system creates a captive market for substitute children. [[norris|Norris]] observes that "society manufactured them because killing them was permissible" (001-052). The disposability of [[mutant-animals|mutants]] makes the system work, just as the system's need for disposable love-objects requires [[mutant-animals|mutants]] to be manufactured.

This creates a recursive moral horror: the [[federation|Federation]] defines some humans as conditionally human, denies them children, provides them with quasi-human substitutes, and then destroys the substitutes when they become inconvenient — all while insisting that the substitutes are not really human because they were manufactured.

## Beyond "Conditionally Human"

The theme extends beyond the title story. In "Check and Checkmate," the [[stand-in|Stand-in]]s and the Presidency itself function through depersonalization — the President has "no real name of his own" (003-001), his face hidden behind the golden mask of tragedy. The [[stand-in|Stand-in]]s are conditionally the President, their identities subsumed into a role. And the rabble, as Smith acknowledges, are conditionally free — their votes counted in the billions but their power channeled through institutions that render them "really impotent" only in the sense that Smith angrily corrects: impotent except through him.
