# Congress Investigation (Security Probe)

## Origins

The Congress Investigation is the domestic political purge ordered by [[john-smith-xvi|John Smith XVI]] in "Check and Checkmate" (003) after discovering that Asian intelligence had infiltrated the highest levels of his government. The catalyst is the televiewphone contact with [[ivan-ivanovitch-ix|Ivan IX]], during which the Asian leader reveals knowledge of Western affairs that could only have come from agents inside the [[federation|Federation]]. Smith's response is immediate and sweeping: "Assemble Congress. Security-probe. It's the only answer" (003-127).

Smith's decision is characteristically cynical. He knows that Congress "had not been assembled for fifteen years, since there had been nothing suspicious to investigate" (003-127). The investigation is as much a political tool as a security measure — a way to demonstrate strength, consolidate power, and purge potential rivals under cover of patriotism. "Start a big spy-scare, and keep it in the channels. I'll lead with a message to the rabble" (003-127).

## Scope and Method

The investigation is massive and indiscriminate. Smith delegates the witch-hunt to the "Rabble's Parliament" because "they were always good at purging themselves" (003-127). The speech that launches it — delivered by a [[stand-in|Stand-in]] rather than the President himself — quotes the Declaration of Independence, the Gettysburg Address, and the Old Testament ("If thy brother…would persuade thee secretly, saying, 'Let us go and serve strange gods,'…thou shalt presently put him to death") in a perverse fusion of democratic rhetoric and theocratic violence (003-129).

Congress, once assembled, proves ferociously responsive. The investigation sweeps in "many thousands," but the results are meager: only "sixty-nine actually confessed to espionage," and psychiatric examination revealed most confessions to be fabrications by "neurotic publicity-seekers who would have confessed to anything sufficiently dramatic" (003-130). After exhaustive verification, only seven cases could not be dismissed — and even these "could not substantiate one another's claims, nor furnish any evidence" of an organized network (003-130).

## The Disappearances

The most ominous outcome is not the confessions but the disappearances. "Seventeen men in key positions" — persons merely mentioned as possible candidates for the probe — "immediately vanished into thin air, leaving no trace" (003-130). Two cases are definitive: one fled by plane and crashed at sea; another, an ordnance officer, "spectacularly committed suicide by exploding an atomic artillery shell, vaporizing himself and certain key comrades including his superior officer" (003-130).

Smith recognizes these disappearances as evidence of real infiltration: "The disappearances and the suicides spelled careful discipline and planning. Their records had been impeccable" (003-130). If they had been innocent, they would have stayed. Their flight is the most damning evidence the investigation produces — but it produces it silently, through absence rather than testimony.

## Aftermath

The investigation fails its stated purpose (rooting out the spy network) while succeeding at its unstated one (consolidating Smith's political position). The real spies, if any remain, are undiscovered; the [[stand-in|Stand-in]] ranks have been thinned by mysterious deaths (Number Six "died mysteriously in the bathtub"); and [[ivan-ivanovitch-ix|Ivan IX]] reads the domestic turmoil correctly as a sign of Western insecurity. The probe that was meant to demonstrate strength has, to a perceptive adversary, revealed weakness.
