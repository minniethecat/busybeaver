# Qualifications Amendment

## The Legal Architecture of Exclusion

The Qualifications Amendment is not a single law but a series of amendments that, over time, transformed the [[federation|Federation]]'s definition of citizenship. Smith, in "Check and Checkmate," catalogs the progression: "The Qualifications Amendment that limited it to the psychologically fit. And then the Education Amendment... And the Genetic Amendment, and the Selection Amendment" (003-008).

Each amendment added a new condition. Each condition excluded more people from full citizenship. The process was incremental — no single amendment seemed unreasonable on its own — but the cumulative effect was total. By the time of the story, "human" has become a credential rather than a birthright.

## The Amendment-by-Amendment Process

**Psychological Fitness**: The first restriction — citizenship limited to those deemed psychologically fit. The criterion seems reasonable: unstable individuals should not govern. But "fitness" is defined by the state that administers the test.

**Education**: The second layer — citizenship restricted to the educated. Again, superficially reasonable; again, a tool for exclusion, since education correlates with class, and class with the existing power structure.

**Genetic**: The third layer — citizenship determined by heredity. With this amendment, exclusion becomes permanent and inheritable. You are disqualified not for what you have done but for who your parents were. [[class-c-citizen|Class-C]] is born, not made.

**Selection**: The final layer — active selection of who qualifies. The state no longer merely excludes the unfit; it actively chooses who belongs.

## Smith's Revelation

Smith describes the progression not as a critic but as a participant — a man who helped build the system. His tone is matter-of-fact, almost nostalgic, as he walks the [[stand-in|Stand-in]] through the legal architecture of his own disenfranchisement. The horror is in the normality: this is how laws are made, one amendment at a time.

## Miller's Critique

The Qualifications Amendment embodies Miller's critique of bureaucratic gradualism. Evil does not arrive as a single catastrophic law; it arrives as a series of reasonable-sounding amendments, each building on the last, until the structure is complete and no one remembers how it was built. The people who passed each amendment are not monsters; they are administrators solving problems. The monster is the process.
