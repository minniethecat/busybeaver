# Spaceman

## The Dying Breed

The spaceman in Miller's "Death of a Spaceman" is not a romantic hero but a working professional confronting obsolescence. [[old-donegal|Old Donegal]], the story's protagonist, is a veteran spacer dying of radiation-induced cancer — his body consumed by the profession that defined his life.

Miller strips the spaceman of glamour. Donegal is not an explorer but a worker, his body a record of the physical toll of space travel: radiation exposure, the accumulated damage of years in the void. His death is not heroic but clinical — the quiet extinction of a man whose profession is being phased out by automation and safer technology.

## The Body as Record

Donegal's failing body carries the history of the space program. Each ache, each cancer site, is a testament to a specific mission, a specific risk. His wife [[martha|Martha]] reads his body as she would a logbook: the scar from the Jovian run, the cough from the Venus orbit, the weakness that signals the end.

This is Miller's characteristic materialism — the refusal to separate the moral from the physical. Donegal's heroism, if it can be called that, is not in grand gestures but in the accumulated damage of a working life. His body tells the truth that the official histories omit.

## Obsolescence

The spaceman of Donegal's generation is being replaced. New ships have better shielding; new crews are younger, healthier; the risks Donegal accepted as normal are now considered unnecessary. The story's tragedy is not just that Donegal dies but that the way of life he represents — the rough, physical, irreplaceable experience of the old spacer — dies with him.
