# Mara

## Role in "It Takes a Thief"

Mara is the daughter of Senior Kinsman Welkir and the woman [[asir|Asir]] depends upon when he faces execution for ritual-theft. Her role in the story is defined by ambiguity: is she Asir's ally or betrayer? The answer remains uncertain until the knife falls — and even then, her motives resist simple interpretation.

## Character and Appearance

Mara is introduced as "a tall, slender girl with an arrogant strut and mocking eyes" (002-057). She is the daughter of a Senior Kinsman, which places her among the elite of the Mars colony village. Her status gives her access to power — she can speak with [[chief-commoner|Tokra]], the Chief Commoner — but also makes her a potential source of betrayal, since Asir is a thief and outcast.

Her physical description emphasizes her confidence and self-possession: "arrogant strut," "mocking eyes," "folded arms." She is not afraid to perform contempt publicly — she spits at the foot of Asir's post and smirks at his suffering (002-057) — but her private signals tell a different story.

## The Slow Wink

Mara's signature gesture in the story is the "slow wink" — a secret communication that Asir must learn to read. When she visits the execution post, she speaks contemptuous words aloud but gives Asir "a solemn wink" that contradicts them (002-057). Later, before walking away, she looks back and "repeated the slow wink" (002-075).

The wink is a message within a message: whatever Mara says publicly, her private communication promises something else. Asir's dilemma — to trust or not to trust — hangs on his interpretation of this gesture. "Suppose she hasn't betrayed me?" he thinks. The wink makes the question possible; it makes betrayal and loyalty indistinguishable until the moment of truth.

## Ambiguous Loyalty

Mara's speech — "I think that I should *like* to see him beg" (002-073) — could be either a sadistic taunt or a coded instruction. If she knows that begging will result in banishment rather than death, her words are not cruelty but strategy: the only way to save Asir is to make him ask for the knife.

The story never resolves this ambiguity. After Asir is banished, he goes to Welkir's house, and Mara greets him. The narrative moves forward without confirming her motives. This ambiguity is central to Miller's method: characters in his fiction are not transparent to us, and loyalty is always conditional — a matter of interpretation, not fact.

## Narrative Function

Mara serves as the hinge between Asir's two worlds: the village society that has condemned him and the knowledge that might save Mars. As the daughter of a Senior Kinsman, she has access to power and information that Asir, as an outcast, lacks. Her ambiguous role forces Asir — and the reader — to confront the uncertainty at the heart of trust: in a world where every ritual is a transaction, loyalty itself may be a gamble.
