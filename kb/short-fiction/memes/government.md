# Government

The federal **Government** serves as the institutional backdrop for political power struggles in several of Walter M. Miller Jr.'s short stories, most prominently in "Check and Checkmate" and "Way of a Rebel."

Miller portrays government as a vast, often impersonal machinery of armaments, espionage, and bureaucratic inertia—reflecting Cold War anxieties of his era.

## Structure and Power

Miller's government is heavily militarized. In "Check and Checkmate," the rationale for government spending is framed entirely in terms of armaments:

> "The answer should be evident, Peoplesfriend. I presume that your government spends a respectable sum for armaments. My government does likewise. The eventual aim should be economy." [003-037]

Government employment dominates civilian life, as President John Smith's [[stand-in|Stand-in]] observes:

> "John! A third of the population works for the government!" [003-153]

## Congress as Political Theater

Congress, dormant for fifteen years, is reactivated by President [[john-smith-xvi|John Smith XVI]] to conduct a security probe—ostensibly to root out spies, but practically as a tool for political purging:

> Congress had not been assembled for fifteen years, since there had been nothing suspicious to investigate, but once it was called to duty, heads would roll—some of them literally. [003-085]

The legislative body is portrayed as eager to perform, requiring no substantive evidence:

> The address was honestly an emotional one, not bothering with any attempt at logical analysis. None was needed. Congress was always eager to investigate subversion. [003-112]

## Government by Emergency Decree

In "Way of a Rebel," the constitutional order collapses into military rule under Secretary Garson:

> "In accordance with the provisions of the Twenty-Sixth Amendment, Congress today approved the Manlin Bill, declaring a state of total emergency for the nation. ...The executive, legislative, and judiciary are now in the hands of the Department of Defense." [005-002]

This transition from civilian to military governance is depicted not as a coup but as a constitutional process—one that Mitch Laskell, the story's protagonist, views with bitter detachment:

> But he had been conscious of no great resentment against the war or the Navy or the government. Historically speaking, wars had never done a great deal of harm—no more harm than industrial or traffic accidents. [005-052]

## Government as Impersonal Force

Throughout Miller's fiction, government functions less as a social contract and more as a force of nature—unaccountable, immense, and disconnected from individual lives. Mitch's reflection captures this resignation:

> Men would be just as calm after their cities had been reduced to rubble. The human capacity for calmness was almost unlimited, ex post facto, because the routine of daily living had to go on, despite the big business of governments whose leaders invoked the Deity in the cause of slaughter. [005-007]
