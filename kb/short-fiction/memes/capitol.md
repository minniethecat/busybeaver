# Capitol

The **Capitol** in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "Check and Checkmate" is the seat of the federal government—a mobile installation that shifts location to avoid enemy targeting. It serves as the stage for President [[john-smith-xvi|John Smith XVI]]'s security probe and the diplomatic maneuvering with the Asian representative [[ivan-ivanovitch-ix|Ivan Ivanovitch IX]].

## A Shifting Seat of Power

Unlike a fixed capital city, Miller's Capitol is designed to be mobile—a defensive measure in an age of constant military threat:

> "That is out of the question. I must insist on the capitol of my government as the only acceptable meeting-place. My government in contacting yours put itself in the position of extending an invitation, a position from which we could not depart without loss of dignity." [003-129]

The Asian representative's insistence on the Capitol as the meeting location reveals its symbolic importance: the Capitol is not merely a place of governance but a projection of sovereignty.

## Prisoner Transfers and Political Theater

The Capitol also serves as the destination for prisoners caught in Smith's security dragnet:

> ...they whisked him away to jail to await transfer to the capitol. During the night, the jailer... [003-155]

The shiftiness of the Capitol—both literal (mobile) and figurative (its operations are cloaked in secrecy)—mirrors the story's theme of power exercised without accountability.

## Command and Control

The Capitol is the nerve center from which Smith orchestrates his espionage operations:

> ...to be in constant contact with the capitol? [003-195]

In Miller's world, the Capitol embodies the paradox of government: simultaneously the locus of order and the engine of persecution.
