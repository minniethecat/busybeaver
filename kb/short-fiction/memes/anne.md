# Anne

## Character Profile

Anne is the wife of Bio-Administration inspector [[norris|Norris]] in "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]." A [[class-c-citizen|class-C]] woman barred from having children due to "[[class-c-citizen|defective heredity]]," she channels her maternal instincts toward the [[neutroid|mutant animals]] her husband is employed to regulate and destroy. Her moral clarity about the [[mutant-animals|mutants]] — expressed through cold contempt rather than hysterical protest — serves as the story's ethical compass.

Anne's opening position is unambiguous: "I didn't know you killed them" (001-008). The statement is quiet, venomous, and surgically precise. It reframes [[norris|Norris]]'s bureaucratic duties as moral complicity and sets the emotional temperature for the entire narrative.

## Role in Narrative

Anne functions as [[norris|Norris]]'s conscience, externalizing the doubts he suppresses through rationalization. Where [[norris|Norris]] tells himself he is "just a dogcatcher" (001-016), Anne sees a killer of "babies" (001-008). The gap between these framings is the story's central tension.

Her role is not merely oppositional. Anne helps [[norris|Norris]] with his work — she assists with the animals, asks informed questions about cases, and demonstrates genuine care for the creatures in their custody. "It wasn't caught at all?" she asks about a mutant (001-109), and when [[norris|Norris]] brings home an infant creature, "Anne snorted and caught the baby-thing" with maternal instinct (001-116).

## Key Scenes

> "I didn't know you killed them," she said venomously. [001-008]

The accusation that launches the story's moral inquiry. Anne's precision — she does not say "destroy" or "euthanize" but "kill" — strips away bureaucratic euphemism.

> "Don't tell him you're no murderer," Anne purred to it. [001-117]

Speaking to a rescued mutant infant, Anne draws the line that [[norris|Norris]] cannot: if the creature can understand comfort, the creature is a someone, not a something.

> Anne was standing in the bedroom doorway, her small figure framed in the light. [001-152]

A recurring visual image — Anne watching, judging, present. Her silence is often more powerful than speech.

## Relationships

- **[[norris]]** — Her husband, whose work she morally condemns while remaining emotionally entangled with him. Their marriage is the domestic stage on which the story's philosophical debate plays out.
