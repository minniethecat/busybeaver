# Peony

Peony is a neutroid child created by the Anthropos corporation in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s
"Conditionally Human." As an artificial life form, Peony embodies the story's central
tension between biological humanity and manufactured existence. Norris, the protagonist,
forms a protective bond with Peony that tests the legal and moral boundaries of the
Conditionally Human classification system.

Peony's presence drives the plot's emotional core, raising questions about what
constitutes personhood in a society that assigns graded citizenship. The character
appears throughout the narrative as both a catalyst for Norris's moral awakening
and a symbol of the system's victims.

## Story Role

Peony is one of many neutroids produced by Anthropos for off-world colonization.
Unlike adult neutroids conditioned for specific tasks, Peony retains childlike
qualities that evoke protective instincts in Norris. The character's fate becomes
entwined with Norris's decision to challenge the Classification Board.

## Significance

As Miller's most fully realized neutroid character, Peony represents the thematic
intersection of artificial life, innocence, and the arbitrary nature of legal
personhood. The name "Peony" itself — a flower — suggests both beauty and
fragility, qualities that the story attributes to all manufactured life.
