# Delmont

## The Delmont Negligency Case

Delmont is the laboratory technician whose actions set the plot of "Conditionally Human" in motion. Working at the Bermuda Factory — a production facility for [[neutroid|neutroids]] — Delmont was under pressure after "he worked a week and spoiled over a hundred ova without a single success" (001-235). Threatened with firing, he resorted to fraud.

## The Fraud

Delmont reported a success on an ovum he knew was defective. The ovum "had a couple of flaws — something wrong in the central nervous system's determinants, and in the glandular makeup. Not a standard neutroid ovum" (001-235). He passed it to the incubators anyway, calculating that the flaws would be caught after birth.

His reasoning reveals the logic of a desperate man gaming a bureaucratic system: he knew the ovum would develop sexuality — producing a female rather than the standard neuter neutroid — and expected the anomaly would be caught at final inspection. "They'd dispose of her without even bothering to examine for the other defects. And he could blame the sexuality on an equipment malfunction. He thought it was pretty smart" (001-243).

## The Cascade

Delmont's calculation was wrong. The female neutroid was not caught at inspection. She passed through, was sold, and eventually reproduced — producing offspring with the same defects in a chain that spread through the neutroid population. The result was the K-99 strain that [[norris|Norris]] is ordered to locate and destroy.

The cascade reveals the fragility of the system Delmont worked within. A single dishonest act, multiplied by the industrial scale of neutroid production, becomes a public health crisis. The system is designed to prevent errors through procedure, but procedure cannot compensate for the incentives that produce fraud — the production quotas, the threat of firing, the dehumanizing logic of the factory.

## Moral Significance

Delmont never appears directly in the story. He is a ghost, his actions described in retrospect by Norris and others. This narrative choice is significant: Delmont is not the villain but a symptom. The Bermuda Factory, [[organization-anthropos|Anthropos, Inc.]], the entire [[neutroid|neutroid]] industry — these are the structures that make Delmont's fraud inevitable. A system that treats living beings as products will produce desperate workers who cut corners.

Delmont's case is named multiple times — "the Delmont case," "the Delmont negligency" — giving it the weight of legal precedent. But the story makes clear that no legal resolution can address the underlying problem: an economy built on the manufacture and disposal of quasi-human beings.
