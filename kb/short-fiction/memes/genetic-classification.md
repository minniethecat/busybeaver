# Genetic Classification

## The System

The [[federation|Federation]]'s genetic classification system divides the population into ranked tiers — A, B, and C — based on hereditary fitness. The system is not advisory but legally binding: it determines who may reproduce, what kind of substitute companionship they are permitted, and ultimately what kind of human they are considered to be.

Class-A citizens carry "clean" heredity certificates. Class-C citizens, like [[norris|Norris]] and [[anne|Anne]], are marked by "[[class-c-citizen|defective heredity]]" (001-007) — in their case, heart trouble in both family histories — and are legally barred from having children. They are permitted instead "two lesser units or one neutroid" (001-007), channeling parental instinct toward manufactured substitutes.

## Origins and Justification

The classification system's origins are revealed obliquely in "Check and Checkmate." Smith describes how the system evolved through layers of legislation: "The Qualifications Amendment that limited it to the psychologically fit. And then the Education Amendment... And the Genetic Amendment, and the Selection Amendment" (003-008). Each amendment added a new condition; each condition excluded more people. The process was incremental and legalistic — no single law created the system, but the cumulative effect was total.

Miller's insight is that classification systems do not need to announce their true purpose. They need only seem reasonable at each step. By the time the full logic is apparent, the system has become self-reinforcing: Class-C citizens cannot reproduce because they are Class-C, and they remain Class-C because they cannot reproduce.

## Economic Function

The classification system is inseparable from the [[mutant-animals|mutant-animal]] economy. Class-C citizens form a captive market for manufactured substitutes — [[neutroid|neutroids]], cat-Q-5s, dog-Fs — produced by corporations like [[organization-anthropos|Anthropos, Inc.]] The system is circular: genetic classification creates demand, which creates an industry, which lobbies to maintain classification.

[[norris|Norris]], as a pound inspector, is both enforcer and beneficiary of this system. His job is to inspect and, when necessary, destroy the substitute creatures that the system creates. His moral crisis arises from recognizing his own role in a system whose full horror he has only begun to grasp.

## Classification and Personhood

The ultimate effect of genetic classification is to render "human" a conditional status. [[class-c-citizen|Class-C citizens]] are conditionally human — they have fewer rights than Class-A, cannot reproduce, and are assigned substitute rather than real children. [[neutroid|Neutroids]] are classified as property rather than persons. And the classification is permanent: "defective heredity" cannot be corrected, only managed.

This is the moral center of Miller's critique. Classification is not a neutral administrative act. It is an assertion of power over bodies, over reproduction, over the definition of the human. To classify is to control; to control is to define who counts.
