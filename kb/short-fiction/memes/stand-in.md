# Stand-in

## Definition

In the [[federation|Western Federation of Autonomous States]], Stand-ins form a three-tiered hierarchy of masked officials who serve as body-doubles, security personnel, and political advisors to the President. The system is described in "Check and Checkmate" (003): the nine Primary Stand-ins are the President's innermost circle — "also bodyguards, council-members, and advisors to the chief executive" (003-039). Below them sit Secondary Stand-ins (numbering two hundred, 003-066) and Tertiary Stand-ins, who extend the structure outward into the broader administration.

The Stand-ins wear golden masks identical to the President's own — "the mask of tragedy with its expression of pathos symbolizing the self-immolation of public service" (003-039). This uniformity is functional: it makes the President indistinguishable from his retinue, thwarting assassination attempts by concealing his identity. But it also produces a political symbiosis in which the President's security becomes inseparable from the ambitions of the men who protect him.

## Tiers and Functions

The three Stand-in ranks distribute power and risk unevenly across the [[federation|Federation]]'s executive apparatus.

**Primary Stand-ins** (nine members) constitute the President's personal staff. They attend the secret deliberations, control access to the President, and — by tradition — are excluded from the room when the President unmasks to speak privately with foreign leaders (003-130). The Primary Stand-ins' role as bodyguards is complicated by their role as policymakers: they debate strategy with the President, argue against his decisions ("You're *not* contemplating another peace-effort, John?" 003-084), and occasionally become subjects of investigation themselves.

**Secondary Stand-ins** (approximately two hundred) occupy a larger auditorium within the presidential complex. They wear the same official mask but operate at one remove from the innermost decisions. Their primary function appears ceremonial and administrative — they are the visible face of the Stand-in system to the wider apparatus.

**Tertiary Stand-ins** are the outermost ring. They quote the American Declaration of Independence "in the name of the [[federation|Federation]]'s rule" (003-076), a jarring juxtaposition that Miller uses to underscore the [[federation|Federation]]'s ideological decay: the words of revolution are now recited by functionaries of an absolutist state.

## Political Dynamics

The relationship between the President and his Stand-ins is one of mutual dependence laced with mutual suspicion. [[john-smith-xvi|John Smith XVI]] explicitly recognizes this tension: "Should I build my power on men like you? Or the Secondary Stand-ins? Baa! For all your securities, you are still stooges. Of the rabble" (003-061).

The President's power flows upward from the masses ("the rabble"), not downward through the Stand-in hierarchy — a counterintuitive dynamic that Smith articulates bluntly: "Do you obey me because I control military force? Or because I control rabble? The latter I think" (003-061). His hold on the Stand-ins themselves is secured "by bolstering his position with them, and thereby securing his hold on the Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Stand-ins, who were becoming a little too presumptuous of late" (003-082).

The system contains its own vulnerabilities. When Smith orders loyalty tests and "psych-phys rechecks" for his nine Primary Stand-ins, two die mysteriously the following day — "trapped in a burning building on the outskirts of the rabble city, and their remains had not been found" (003-115). The incident reveals that Stand-ins who face exposure can arrange their own disappearances, and that the President's control over his own security apparatus is less than absolute.
