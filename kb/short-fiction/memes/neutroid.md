# Neutroid

## Definition

Neutroids — officially the chimp-K series — are genetically engineered quasi-human pets manufactured by Anthropos, Inc. in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]." Physically, they are "doll-like" creatures with "blazing blond heads," "beaverlike tails decorated with fluffy curls of fur," and "an erect thatch of scalp-hair that grew up into a bright candleflame" (001-028). Otherwise, they appear "completely human, with baby-pink skin, quick little smiles, and cherubic faces" (001-028).

They are sexually neuter — deliberately engineered that way through suppressive doses of male hormone prenatally — and "never grew beyond a predetermined age-set" ranging from one to ten years human equivalent. Once a neutroid reaches its age-set, "it remained at the set's child-development level until death" (001-028). They are, in effect, perpetual children: designed to be loved, but not to grow up.

## Social Function

Neutroids serve a specific social purpose in the [[federation|Federation]]'s population-management regime. Class-C couples — those with "[[class-c-citizen|defective heredity]]" who are legally barred from having children — are "allowed two lesser units or one neutroid" (001-007). As [[norris|Norris]] explains: "A neutroid filled the cradle in his stead. A neutroid that never ate as much, or grew up to be unemployed. A neutroid could be killed if things got tough, but could still satisfy a woman's craving to mother something small" (001-052).

The system is brutally efficient: "Society manufactured them because killing them was permissible. Human babies could not be disposed of when the market became glutted" (001-052). The [[mutant-animals|neutroids]] offer "solace to childless women, kept them satisfied with a restricted birth rate" — a bioengineered solution to the problem of human longing within a system that treats reproduction as a privilege to be earned through genetic fitness.

## Manufacturing and Defects

Neutroids are created through a process involving "evolvotron" operators who bombard unfertilized chimpanzee ova with subatomic particles, "shooting subatomic billiards" to displace gene-structure links "by just the right amount" (001-032). The process is delicate and failure-prone — "a good operator can get one success out of seven tries" (001-032).

The [[delmont-negligency-case|Delmont case]] reveals what happens when production pressure meets biological complexity. A greenhorn operator faked a success on a flawed ovum with "something wrong in the central nervous system's determinants, and in the glandular makeup" (001-034). The result was a neutroid that would develop sexuality — a female, rather than the mandated neuter — and potentially other unanticipated traits.

## Moral Status

The story's central argument concerns whether [[mutant-animals|neutroids]] possess something that deserves moral consideration. The Church's position — articulated by [[the-priest|Father Paulson]] — is nuanced: "We say it's evil to create the creatures. We say *also* that it's evil to destroy them after they're made. Not murder, exactly, but — mockery of life, perhaps" (001-047).

[[anne|Anne Norris]]'s challenge to Paulson gets at the epistemological difficulty: "What makes *you* human?" "A soul, my child." "Can you put a voltmeter between your ears and measure it?" (001-052–001-053). If humanity cannot be empirically detected, and [[mutant-animals|neutroids]] can speak, feel, and form attachments, on what basis are they denied the protections afforded to humans? The [[federation|Federation]]'s answer — on the basis of their manufacture — is administratively convenient but morally unsatisfying.
