# Martian Society: Decay and Adaptation

## Thesis

The Martian colony in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "It Takes a Thief" (002) is one of science fiction's most poignant settings: a society that has forgotten everything its founders knew. The colonists are the descendants of a shipwrecked generation — people who arrived on [[mars|Mars]] with advanced technology and, over generations, lost it. What remains is a pre-industrial culture that preserves fragments of scientific knowledge as memorized [[ritual]] chants, controlled by a priestly caste that no longer understands what it guards.

This insight article examines how Miller constructs this society of forgetting — and what it says about knowledge, power, and cultural decay.

## The Shipwreck as Origin

The Martian colony's founding trauma is a shipwreck. The original colonists did not choose Mars; they crashed there. The story describes a society descended from people who "remembered only in memorized ritual chants, the possession of which was restricted to the priestly caste" (002-004). The ship that brought them — a spacecraft — became a temple. The engineering manuals became scripture. The launch codes became prayers.

This is not metaphor. Miller describes a literal transformation: technology becomes religion when the knowledge required to operate it is lost. The colonists' descendants do not know what a spaceship is — they know there are "vaults" containing sacred objects, and that certain ritual phrases must be spoken to enter them. But what the objects *are*, what the phrases *mean* — this is no longer accessible.

## Ritual as Currency

In this society of forgetting, [[ritual]] formulas function as currency. [[asir|Asir]], the protagonist, "had squandered his last ritual formula to buy a wife" (002-003). The economic function is explicit: rituals are things you can spend, steal, or hoard. They are the society's scarcest resource because they are its only remaining connection to the technological past.

The irony is layered. A ritual formula that once meant "initiate launch sequence" or "jettison cargo bay" now buys a hüf-rug. The original meaning has been completely stripped away; only the social function — the power to transact — remains.

## The Priest and the Thief

The story's central conflict is between [[asir|Asir]] — a thief of ritual formulas — and the priestly caste that monopolizes them. Asir stole his formulas "with a stolen bit of ritual" (002-078): one stolen ritual used to steal others. This recursion captures the society's predicament. The lock and the key are the same thing. Knowledge is both the prison and the escape.

[[asir|Asir]]'s crime is not theft in the ordinary sense. He is not stealing property; he is breaching a knowledge monopoly. The priests' power depends on exclusive access to ritual formulas. [[asir|Asir]] threatens that monopoly simply by possessing what he should not. The story's title — "It Takes a Thief" — suggests a recognition: sometimes the only way to break a corrupt system is to steal from it.

## The Spaceship Vaults

The most evocative image in the story is the spaceship vaults — the sealed chambers containing the original colonists' technology. [[asir|Asir]]'s stolen ritual formulas describe these vaults in terms no one understands: references to "[[blaze-of-the-winds|the Blaze of the Winds]]" (002-082), to metal chambers, to things that can be entered only by speaking the right words.

These vaults are museums of a forgotten civilization, but no one knows they are museums. They are temples, and the artifacts inside them are sacred relics. Miller's genius is to show us the spaceship through the colonists' eyes — not as a machine but as a mystery — while letting the reader understand what the characters cannot.

## The Sociology of Forgetting

Miller's Martian society illustrates a profound sociological insight: knowledge is not self-preserving. It requires institutions — schools, libraries, apprenticeships — to transmit it across generations. When those institutions fail, knowledge degrades. Not instantly: first it becomes tradition, then ritual, then superstition, then silence.

The Martians have reached the ritual stage. They still perform the forms of their ancestors' knowledge, but the content is gone. [[asir|Asir]] recites his stolen formulas "with the mechanical drone like memorised ritual" (002-089) — the very description captures the problem. Ritual is mechanical; it does not require understanding. And without understanding, it will eventually stop being performed at all.

## Parallels to the Federation

The Martian colony and the [[federation|Federation]] society of "Conditionally Human" and "Check and Checkmate" are mirror images. The Federation preserves knowledge but weaponizes it through classification systems. Mars has lost knowledge but preserved it in degraded form through ritual. Both are dystopian, but in opposite directions: the Federation is dystopian through excess of control; Mars is dystopian through loss of capability.

The [[space-travel]] technology that the Federation uses for war and commerce is the same technology that brought the Martians' ancestors to their new world. The same ships, the same physics. But the Federation remembers how to build and fly them, while Mars can only chant about them. The difference is not moral superiority — it is institutional continuity.

## Conclusion

Miller's Martian society is a warning about the fragility of knowledge. The colonists' ancestors were as technologically advanced as the [[federation|Federation]] — perhaps more so, since they achieved interplanetary travel. But they failed to build the institutions that would preserve that knowledge, and within a few generations, their descendants were trading launch codes for rugs. The story's deepest insight is not about Mars or the future: it is about any society that takes its knowledge for granted, assuming it will persist without effort. It will not. What is not actively transmitted becomes ritual. What is ritual becomes noise. What is noise is forgotten.
