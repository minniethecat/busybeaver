# Guilt in Miller's Fiction

## The Guilty Functionary

Miller's protagonists are not villains; they are functionaries who have come to recognize their complicity in systems they can neither escape nor reform. [[norris|Norris]] kills [[neutroid|neutroids]] for a living and feels "the night's guilt, like a sticky dew that refused to depart with morning" (001-335). The [[stand-in|Stand-in]] performs the role of President, knowing the role is a mask for a system that dispossesses the rabble. [[old-donegal|Donegal]] confronts the guilt of having outlived his usefulness — and his son.

## Guilt Without Resolution

Miller's treatment of guilt is distinctive in its refusal of easy resolution. Norris does not quit his job or sabotage the system; he goes back to work the next morning. His guilt is real but ineffectual — a private torment that changes nothing. This is a harder, more honest portrayal than the heroic renunciation: most people, Miller suggests, recognize their complicity and continue anyway, because the alternatives are unthinkable or nonexistent.

## The Theological Echo

Guilt in Miller's fiction carries a theological weight, even in stories without overt religious content. (Miller was a Catholic convert, and *A Canticle for Leibowitz* would make this dimension explicit.) The guilt his characters feel is not merely social but existential — a stain that cannot be washed off by improved policy. It is the guilt of existing within a fallen order, of being unable to opt out of structures that implicate everyone.
