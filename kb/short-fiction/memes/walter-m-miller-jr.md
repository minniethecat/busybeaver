# Walter M. Miller Jr.

## Overview

Walter Michael Miller Jr. (January 23, 1923 – January 9, 1996) was an American science fiction writer whose work, though not prolific, achieved enduring influence, particularly his only novel *A Canticle for Leibowitz* (1959), which won the 1961 Hugo Award. This collection gathers seven of his finest short stories, written between 1951 and 1955, showcasing his characteristic concerns: the moral weight of technological power, the bureaucratic erosion of humanity, and the tension between individual conscience and institutional authority.

## Biography

Miller served as a radioman and tail gunner in the U.S. Army Air Corps during World War II, flying over fifty combat missions. His most traumatic experience — participating in the bombing of Monte Cassino, a sixth-century Benedictine monastery — would later provide the emotional core of *A Canticle for Leibowitz*. After the war, he studied engineering at the University of Tennessee and the University of Texas, though he did not complete a degree.

Miller began publishing science fiction in 1951 with "Secret of the Death Dome" in *Amazing Stories*. Over the next five years, he produced approximately three dozen short stories, many for *Astounding Science Fiction* under editor John W. Campbell, and for *Galaxy Science Fiction* and *The Magazine of Fantasy & Science Fiction*. His short fiction appeared in three episodes within the same imagined future: the Western Federation of Autonomous States, a bureaucratic polity that serves as the backdrop for "Conditionally Human," "Check and Checkmate," and others in this collection.

## Themes and Style

Miller's fiction is distinguished by several recurring preoccupations:

- **Bureaucratic dehumanization**: His governments, corporations, and institutions do not merely administer — they classify, grade, and dispose of human beings through a logic of procedural efficiency that masks moral horror.
- **Genetic determinism and social control**: The classification of humans by genetic fitness ("Conditionally Human," "Check and Checkmate") anticipates later bioethical debates.
- **Religious sensibility in a technological world**: Even in stories without overt religious content, Miller's characters wrestle with guilt, sacrifice, and the search for meaning in systems that deny transcendence.
- **The competent loner**: His protagonists — Norris, Asir, Donegal — operate at the margins of their societies, drawing on practical knowledge that institutions lack.

## Publication History

Miller stopped publishing in the late 1950s and became increasingly reclusive. He wrote little after *A Canticle for Leibowitz*, though a sequel, *Saint Leibowitz and the Wild Horse Woman*, was completed by Terry Bisson and published posthumously in 1997. Miller died by suicide in 1996.

## Works in This Collection

The seven stories in this Standard Ebooks edition span Miller's most productive period (1951–1955):

1. **"Conditionally Human"** (1952) — A pound inspector discovers the horror beneath a system of genetic classification and manufactured substitute children.
2. **"It Takes a Thief"** (1952) — On a dying Mars colony, a ritual-thief's knowledge may hold the key to planetary survival.
3. **"Check and Checkmate"** (1953) — The Presidency is a mask, and a Stand-in must decide whether to become the man behind it.
4. **"Death of a Spaceman"** (1954) — An old spaceman confronts his mortality and the obsolescence of his way of life.
5. **"Way of a Rebel"** (1954) — A military officer faces the impossible choice between duty and conscience.
6. **"The Ties That Bind"** (1954) — Clan politics on Mars collide with ancient knowledge and the question of who rules.
7. **"The Hoofer"** (1955) — A space hobo's final journey home reveals the human cost of the space lanes.
