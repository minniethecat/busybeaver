# Asir

## Character Profile

Asir is a young ritual-thief — perhaps in his tenth [[mars|Mars]]year — in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "It Takes a Thief." Lithe and sleek, "with the quick knotty muscles and slender rawhide limbs of a wild thing, half-fed and hungry" (002-003), he embodies the desperate cunning of a post-technological [[mars|Mars]] colony where knowledge itself has become currency. His father was a renegade before him who "squandered his last [[ritual|ritual formula]] to buy a wife" and took her to the hills; Asir was born there but returned to the village "to work as a servant and steal the rituals of his masters" (002-012).

Unlike ordinary thieves who steal for wealth, Asir has stolen something far more valuable: the memorized holy phrases of the ancient gods — Fermi, Einstein, Elgermann, Hanser — which, when pieced together, reveal truths the [[council|Council]] of Senior Kinsmen cannot see. "A thief, if successful, frequently became endowed with wisdom, for he memorized more wealth than a score of honest men" (002-019).

## Role in Narrative

Asir functions as both protagonist and revelation-bearer. His predicament — hung from a post by nails through his forearms, awaiting the bleeding-blade at [[slubil|the executioner]] [[slubil|Slubil]]'s pleasure — is the story's central dramatic situation. But his real function is epistemological: through his stolen knowledge, he understands what the council does not — that [[mars|Mars]]'s air is leaking away, that "[[mars|Mars]], once dead, was dying again" (002-019), and that the colony's survival depends on restoring the technology its rituals only fragmentarily preserve.

The executioner [[slubil|Slubil]] is bound by law to wait until Asir requests his fate, and Asir is kept ignorant of what the [[council|Council]] has decreed — "his life, or an amputation" (002-017). This produces the story's central tension: whether to beg for a punishment whose severity is unknown.

## Key Scenes

> After three hours of dangling from the spikes that pierced his arms, Asir was weakening, and the blood throbbed hard in his temples, with each jolt of his heart a separate pulse of pain. (002-009)

The opening scene establishes Asir's physical ordeal and the ritualized cruelty of Martian justice. The executioner [[slubil|Slubil]] sits "spraddle-legged and calm while he chewed a blade of grass" (002-007), playing mumblety-peg with the bleeding-blade — a figure of impassive, bureaucratic violence.

> He knew now that Mars, once dead, was dying again, its air leaking away once more into space. And Man would die with it, unless something were done, and done quickly. (002-019)

This is Asir's moment of tragic knowledge. Through fragments of stolen ritual — quotations from the god Roggins's *Progress of the [[mars|Mars-Culture]]* — he has assembled an understanding that the colony's elders have lost. The irony is acute: a thief, the lowest of outcasts, is the only person who comprehends the existential threat.

> Asir closed his eyes and gritted his teeth. "Give it to me!" he yelped suddenly, and braced himself against the post. (002-028)

Asir's decision to beg for the knife, urged on by Mara's ambiguous wink, is an act of desperate courage. He does not know if the blade will kill, maim, or mark him — but the uncertainty of waiting has become worse than any outcome. [[slubil|Slubil]] strikes the post above his head, marking Asir's forehead with rust for a scar and banishing him as an outcast (002-033).

## Relationships

- **[[slubil]]** — The executioner, a squat man "with a thick face" and "empty blue eyes" (002-007), who wields the bleeding-blade with ritual precision and ultimately spares Asir's life, marking him for banishment instead.
