# Sanctuary

## Fragile Refuge

Across Miller's fiction, characters seek sanctuary from the systems that control them — places and relationships that offer temporary protection from the logic of [[genetic-classification|classification]], [[ritual-economy|ritual economy]], or [[bureaucracy|bureaucratic depersonalization]]. These sanctuaries are fragile, conditional, and never permanent.

## Forms of Sanctuary

In "Conditionally Human," [[norris|Norris]]'s home with [[anne|Anne]] offers a brief refuge from the pound — a space where the neutroids are not inventory but loved beings. In "It Takes a Thief," [[mara|Mara]]'s ambiguous loyalty provides [[asir|Asir]] a precarious sanctuary: her wink promises protection but offers no guarantees. In "Check and Checkmate," the [[stand-in|Stand-in]]'s private recognition of the system's injustice is a sanctuary of conscience — a refuge that exists only in the mind.

## The Limits of Refuge

Sanctuary in Miller's fiction is never permanent. The system always re-enters: Norris must return to the pound, Asir must face [[big-joe|Big Joe]], the Stand-in must act or continue the masquerade. The fragility of sanctuary is the point: in a total system, there is no outside, only temporary respites.
