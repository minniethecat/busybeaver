# Conditionally Human

**"[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]"** is a science fiction short story by Walter M. Miller Jr., first published in 1952. It is the longest and most thematically ambitious story in Miller's short fiction collection, exploring the moral boundaries of what constitutes humanity through the lens of artificial life forms.

## Synopsis

The story follows [[norris|Norris]], a supervisor at an "evolvotron" facility that manufactures genetically engineered pets and companion animals. When a new type of creature — the "neutroid" — is developed, a "human-chimp-K series" designed to resemble human children [PN=001-030], [[norris|Norris]] finds himself entangled in a web of ethical violations, corporate cover-ups, and the fundamental question of where the line between animal and human should be drawn. The replacement quota for neutroids was around three hundred animals per year, and finding and testing just forty neutroids would put an inspector in a tight squeeze [PN=001-034][PN=001-035]. The story weaves together themes of scientific hubris, bureaucratic corruption, and the human capacity for both cruelty and compassion.

## Major Characters

- [[norris|Norris]] — The protagonist, a facility supervisor who discovers the truth about the [[mutant-animals|neutroids]]
- [[anne|Anne]] — [[norris|Norris]]'s wife, who becomes deeply involved with a neutroid child
- [[doctor-georges|Doctor Georges]] — The scientist whose death sets the investigation in motion
- [[sheriff-yates|Sheriff Yates]] — Local law enforcement who assists [[norris|Norris]]

## Key Themes

- **The Definition of Humanity**: Miller probes the boundary between "human" and "animal" through the [[mutant-animals|neutroids]] — creatures engineered to be almost but not quite human, challenging the idea that humanity is a binary state.

- **Corporate and [[government|Government]] Complicity**: The evolvotron corporation and government regulators are portrayed as complicit in ethical violations, prioritizing profit and convenience over moral accountability.

- **The Banality of Evil**: Characters like [[norris|Norris]] drift into complicity through inaction and rationalization, echoing Arendt's concept of the banality of evil decades before it was fully articulated.

## Themes in Miller's Short Fiction

Miller's military and engineering background informs the story's preoccupation with systems — bureaucratic, technological, and biological — and how individuals navigate (or fail to navigate) within them. "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]" stands as the collection's most sustained meditation on the moral consequences of treating life as a product.
