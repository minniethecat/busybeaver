# Artificial Intelligence

## Definition

Artificial intelligence in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s short fiction encompasses a spectrum from simple automated systems to entities that approach — or challenge — human cognition. Miller wrote during the early years of computing, yet his treatment of machine intelligence is remarkably sophisticated, anticipating later debates about the relationship between logic, consciousness, and moral agency.

## Robot Systems

The most literal AI entities in the collection are the robots appearing in "It Takes a Thief" and "Check and Checkmate." These are not the autonomous androids of later science fiction but large, physically imposing machines governed by programmed rules. A "robot operator" with taped instructions handles routine communications (001-064); a "robot locator" tracks positions (001-250).

In "It Takes a Thief," the robots on an alien world speak "like low thunder" (002-228) and move with deliberate, unsettling precision. [[asir|Asir]], the protagonist, attempts to interact with them — "When did you begin to learn?" he asks a robot (002-252) — receiving answers that hint at a learning process beyond simple programming. The robots function as gatekeepers, enforcers, and ambiguous moral agents in a world where the line between tool and being is deliberately blurred.

## Logical Systems and Governance

"Check and Checkmate" extends the AI concept into the realm of governance. The story features a "leader" who is in Singapore (003-254), and the narrative implies a distributed system of decision-making that blends human judgment with machine logic. The President's weary reaction to a robot's pronouncement — "a great weariness swept over him" (003-255) — suggests the psychological cost of ceding authority to systems whose reasoning is opaque.

Miller's concern is not with AI as existential threat but with the subtler danger of outsourcing moral judgment to logical systems. "No logical defense was useful against sentimentality" (001-010), [[norris|Norris]] reflects — a line that captures Miller's recurring skepticism about the limits of pure reason.

## Consciousness and Classification

The [[neutroid|mutant animals]] in "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]" represent a different register of the AI question. They are biological rather than mechanical, but the central problem is the same: how do we determine when a created entity crosses the threshold from object to subject? The chimp-K "[[mutant-animals|neutroids]]" display intelligence that challenges the [[federation|Federation]]'s legal framework, which classifies them as property.

This biological AI — created through the "evolvotron" rather than programming — raises the same ethical questions that haunt discussions of machine consciousness. If a being can think, feel, and form attachments, by what right do we classify it as a tool?
