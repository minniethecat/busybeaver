# Ancient Gods (Ancient Fathers)

## Gods from the Sky

"*The ancient gods, our Fathers, rode down from the heavens in the Firebirds of the Sun.*" So begins the Martian origin myth in "It Takes a Thief" (002-002). The "gods" are the original Earth colonists — scientists, engineers, and settlers who terraformed Mars generations before the story's present. Over centuries, their technological achievements have been mythologized into divine acts.

The names of the gods reveal their true nature: **Fermi** (Enrico Fermi, nuclear physicist), **Einstein** (Albert Einstein), **Elgermann** and **Hanser** (presumably other scientists), and **Roggins** (author of *Progress of the Mars-Culture*). These are not deities but the founders of a scientific civilization whose descendants have lost the ability to distinguish technology from miracle.

## The Firebirds and the Blaze

The gods' central act was kindling the [[blaze-of-the-winds|Blaze of the Great Wind]] — the geothermal-atmospheric system that gave Mars breathable air. In myth: "Sun gave them of His fire and beneath the earth they kindled the Blaze of the Great Wind. Good air roared from the womb of Mars our Mother, the ice burned with a great thunder, and there was air for the breath of Man" (002-002).

The mythological language is beautiful and evocative, but it obscures the technical reality: the [[blaze-of-the-winds|Blaze]] is a machine, and machines require maintenance. The gods understood this; their descendants, who know only the myth, do not.

## Knowledge in Decay

The gods left written records — "the holy books" — but these no longer exist. Their content survives only in memorized ritual chants, "remembered only in memorized ritual chants, the possession of which meant wealth" (002-051). The chants contain fragments of technical knowledge: "It is realized that the colonists will be unable to maintain a technology without basic tools, and that a rebuilding will require several generations of intelligently directed effort" (002-049).

The irony is acute. The gods anticipated the problem — the decay of technical knowledge — and documented it. But the documentation itself became part of the decay: a warning about the loss of understanding, preserved in a form that gradually lost its own meaning.

## The Gods as Mythology

Miller's treatment of the ancient gods is neither mocking nor sentimental. The mythologization of science into religion is portrayed as an almost inevitable process when a technological civilization loses its material base. The colonists did not *choose* to become gods; they were remembered that way because their achievements exceeded their descendants' comprehension.

This is the same pattern Miller explores in the [[federation|Federation]] stories: systems created for human purposes that become opaque to the humans who live within them. The ancient gods built machines; their descendants pray to them without understanding what the machines do.
