# Navy

The **Navy** appears in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "Way of a Rebel" as the undersea branch of the American military, embroiled in an ongoing war against the Eurasian Soviet. It serves as both the professional home and the object of rejection for the story's protagonist, Mitch Laskell.

## Mitch Laskell's Service

Mitch is a submarine "fighter pilot" — the undersea equivalent of an aerial ace. His career in the Navy has been successful, marked by a killer instinct that his superiors value:

> He had always gotten along in the Navy. He was the undersea equivalent of a fighter pilot, and he had always liked his job. They had even said that "he had the killer instinct"—or whatever it was that made him grin maliciously when he spotted an enemy sub and streaked in for the kill. [005-025]

## The Desertion

Despite his record, Mitch abruptly resigns from the Navy in a moment of bitter impulse, triggered not by ideological conviction but by accumulated personal frustration:

> Mitch whitened and picked up the microphone. He keyed the transmitter's carrier and spoke in a quiet hiss. "Commsubron Killer from Sugar William Niner Zero. Message for Daddy. Sonnyboy just resigned from the Navy. Go to hell, all of you! Over and out!" [005-023]

His desertion is compounded by theft — stealing a Navy submarine:

> And now he was running like hell and stealing several million bucks worth of Uncle's Navy, all because Garson's pomposity and a radio operator's voice got under his skin. How could a man be so crazy? [005-051]

## Institutional Indifference

Miller portrays the Navy not as a villainous institution but as an impersonal one. Mitch's relationship with it is defined by routine and convenience rather than loyalty or hatred:

> But he had been conscious of no great resentment against the war or the Navy or the government. Historically speaking, wars had never done a great deal of harm—no more harm than industrial or traffic accidents. [005-052]

This moral detachment — the sense that institutions like the Navy are simply part of the world's machinery — underscores the story's theme of individual alienation from systems of power.
