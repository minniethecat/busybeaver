# Sarah Glubbes

## The Call That Starts It All

Sarah Glubbes is a Class-C woman whose panicked call to [[doctor-georges|Doctor Georges]] sets the events of "Conditionally Human" in motion. Her [[neutroid|neutroid]] baby is dying of eighteenth-order virus — a disease treatable in humans but, due to the economics of the classification system, not in manufactured substitutes.

Doctor Georges describes the call to [[norris|Norris]]: "One of my patients — a Mrs. Sarah Glubbes — called a while ago and said her baby was sick. I must be getting absentminded, because I forgot she was class-C until I got there. The baby turned out to be a neutroid" (001-093).

## Class-C and Motherhood

Mrs. Glubbes embodies the central tragedy of the [[class-c-citizen|Class-C]] condition. Barred from having biological children due to her genetic classification, she has formed a genuine maternal attachment to her [[neutroid|neutroid]] — a substitute manufactured to satisfy exactly this emotional need. But when the substitute falls ill, the system that created the need refuses to address it.

"She insists it's going to a hospital," Doctor Georges reports. "Worst part is that she's heard of the disease. Knows it can be cured with the proper treatment — in humans. Of course, no hospital would play along with her fantasy and take a neutroid, especially since she couldn't pay for its treatment" (001-105).

The cruelty is structural: Mrs. Glubbes has been given a substitute child that she has learned to love as real, and she has been given just enough knowledge to understand that its death is preventable — for humans.

## The Murder

When her neutroid dies, Mrs. Glubbes acts. Seven o'clock in the evening, she bursts into Doctor Georges' dining room "brandishing a pistol and screaming, 'You stole my baby! You gave me the wrong baby! Where's my baby?'" (001-293). She kills the doctor who, from her perspective, stole her child — the doctor who signed the death certificate, who wouldn't send the neutroid to a hospital, who participated in the system that gave her a disposable love-object and called it a child.

## A System's Victim

Mrs. Glubbes is not portrayed as a villain. Miller treats her with the same gravity he gives [[norris|Norris]] — as someone caught in a system that manufactures tragedy as efficiently as it manufactures [[neutroid|neutroids]]. Her violence is the eruption of a grief the system is designed to produce but not to acknowledge.

The story never resolves her fate. She is arrested, presumably, but Norris's meditation on the morning after — "Why should he have to kill the things?" (001-335) — extends the same question to her: why should she have to love a thing that can be killed, and then be punished for refusing to accept its death?
