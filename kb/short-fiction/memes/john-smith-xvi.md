# John Smith XVI

## Character Profile

John Smith XVI is the newly inaugurated President of the [[federation|Western Federation of Autonomous States]] in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "Check and Checkmate." Like all Presidents since the depersonalization amendments, he has no real name of his own and has been "trained for the executive post since birth" (003-001). He wears the golden mask of tragedy — "symbolizing the self-immolation of public service" (003-003) — which renders him publicly faceless and protects his identity from assassination.

Elected by 603,217,954 votes to 130, Smith is keenly self-aware about the nature of his power. He tells his [[stand-in|Stand-in]]s bluntly: "my power is of the rabble... Do you obey me because I control military force? Or because I control rabble? The latter I think" (003-010). A hard, blocky-faced man "slightly in need of a shave, with cool blue eyes and blond brows" and graying temples (003-033), he is fortyish and carries the accumulated political instincts of fifteen predecessors.

## Role in Narrative

Smith is the protagonist and driving force of "Check and Checkmate," a political chess game played across the [[hell-wall|Hell Wall]] that divides East from West after forty years of the [[big-silence|Big Silence]]. His goal — arranging a televiewphone conference with [[ivan-ivanovitch-ix|Ivan Ivanovitch IX]] — is both a genuine diplomatic initiative and a calculated political move to "bolster his position with the rabble, and thereby secure his hold on the Primary, Secondary, and [[stand-in|Tertiary Stand-in]]s" (003-020).

Smith's function is to embody the paradox of the modern executive: absolute power derived from popular will, anonymity that enables authority, and the tension between the ceremonial fiction of elections and the reality of a genetically selected, psychologically screened ruling class. He is neither hero nor villain but something more interesting — a pragmatist navigating a system whose absurdities he fully perceives.

## Key Scenes

> "Our present 'security-probe' was once called 'lynch.' 'Social-security' once meant a limited insurance plan, not connoting euthanasia and sterilization for the ellie-moes." (003-006)

In this scene with his [[stand-in|Stand-in]]s before the historic call, Smith demonstrates his sardonic historical consciousness. He understands that the [[federation|Federation]]'s institutions have evolved through semantic drift — old names preserved while meanings transformed — and that "as long as the names don't change, the rabble is happy" (003-008).

> "Afraid to take off your mask, President? The rabble? Or your self-appointed Stand-ins? Which frightens you, President — "

Ivan's taunt provokes Smith into an extraordinary act: dismissing his [[stand-in|Stand-in]]s and removing the golden mask, exposing his unprotected face to a foreign leader for the first time in the Presidency's history. "Greetings, human" (003-033), Ivan says. The unmasking is both an act of courage and a violation of every security protocol.

> "Sorry, John. I didn't mean — " The President stood up and paced slowly toward the window... "No, my power is of the rabble," he confessed, "and I am their friend." (003-010)

When a [[stand-in|Stand-in]] casually dismisses the populace as "really impotent," Smith explodes with anger. His retort reveals the core insight of his political philosophy: that the rabble's power, channeled through the Presidency, is real and must be respected. "No tyrant can survive by oppression" (003-010).
