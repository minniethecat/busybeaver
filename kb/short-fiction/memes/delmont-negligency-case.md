# Delmont Negligency Case

## Background

The Delmont Negligency Case is the administrative and legal event that drives the plot of "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]" (001). It originates at Anthropos, Inc.'s Bermuda neutroid manufacturing plant, where an [[evolvotron|evolvotron operator]] named Delmont falsified a production record, passing a defective ovum to incubation rather than admitting another failure in a week already marred by "over a hundred" spoiled ova (001-108).

## The Fraud

Delmont was a "greenhorn [[evolvotron|evolvotron operator]]" whose job involved bombarding chimpanzee ova with subatomic particles to reshape gene structures (001-107). The work was difficult: "a good operator can get one success out of seven tries" (001-107). After a week of complete failure and under threat of termination, Delmont "got hysterical" and "reported one success the next day. It was faked" (001-107).

The ovum he passed to incubation had "something wrong in the central nervous system's determinants, and in the glandular makeup. Not a standard neutroid ovum" (001-107). The specific defect: it would develop into a female — a neutroid capable of sexuality — violating the [[federation|Federation]]'s core requirement that all [[neutroid|mutant animals]] be neuter and infertile.

## Investigation and Scope

The case is officially designated the "Delmont Negligency Case" (001-033). When the defect is discovered, Anthropos and Bio-Administration issue a territory-wide seizure order: all [[neutroid|K-99]] [[mutant-animals|neutroids]] from the Bermuda plant, July production, are to be located, tested, and if found to be Delmont's deviants, seized and destroyed. [[norris|Norris]]'s district alone contains approximately forty such animals.

The scope of the investigation reveals the industrial scale of neutroid production. [[norris|Norris]] must find "all July's Bermuda [[neutroid|K-99]] serial numbers that had entered his territory, together with the retailers to whom the animals had been sold" (001-009). He has one week to test forty [[mutant-animals|neutroids]] across two hundred square miles, with only eleven empty cages in his kennel — the other forty-nine occupied by the previous inspector's unclaimed inventory awaiting destruction.

## Moral Consequences

The Delmont case forces [[norris|Norris]] into a moral collision he had long avoided. His job — destroying unclaimed [[mutant-animals|neutroids]] — had been tolerable as long as the creatures he destroyed were genuinely neuter, genuinely not-human. But Delmont's female neutroid, and the possibility of others like her, blurs the boundary between manufactured pet and person.

The case also introduces the mysterious "Peony" — a specific Delmont deviant that [[anne|Anne Norris]] has already adopted. When [[norris|Norris]] discovers that his own wife is sheltering one of the very animals he is ordered to seize, the administrative case becomes personal crisis. The story's resolution hinges on whether [[norris|Norris]] will enforce the [[federation|Federation]]'s classification system or reject it.
