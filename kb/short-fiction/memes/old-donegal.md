# Old Donegal

## Character Profile

Old Donegal is the dying protagonist of Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "Death of a Spaceman." A veteran fighter pilot who flew an R-43 during the war years (004-086), Donegal is now in the final stages of a terminal condition aboard a [[space-travel|space]] hospital. His body is failing — "chamber-pressure's dropping" (004-052) — and those around him have long known the end is near.

Donegal approaches death with the same rough irreverence he brought to life. He is prone to self-mockery ("How hammy can you get, Old Donegal? You maudlin ass" [004-020]) and gruff defiance of medical authority. His wife [[martha|Martha]] has been deliberately misled about the severity of his condition — he has conspired with the hospital staff to shield her from the truth (004-008).

## Role in Narrative

Donegal anchors a story about how a man confronts his own extinction in a technological age. Unlike [[norris|Norris]] — Miller's other conflicted protagonist — Donegal's moral struggle is internal. There is no bureaucracy to blame, no system to indict. There is only the body's betrayal and the question of whether a life spent among machines and war has prepared him for what comes next.

The story unfolds entirely within Donegal's hospital room, filtered through his deteriorating consciousness. His memories of the war, of his marriage to [[martha|Martha]], and of the life he built after combat form the narrative's emotional architecture. A young priest arrives to administer last rites, and their tense, darkly comic exchange becomes the story's spiritual center.

## Story Arc

Donegal's arc is compressed into his final hours. He begins in a state of managed denial — cooperating with the fiction that [[martha|Martha]] doesn't know, trading wisecracks with the nurses. As his condition worsens, the defenses crack.

His confrontation with the young priest forces him to articulate what he believes — or doesn't believe — about death, God, and meaning. "You're sorry for the wrong things" (004-206), [[the-priest|the priest]] tells him, challenging Donegal's self-pity. The exchange strips away his irreverence, revealing a man who has spent his life avoiding introspection and now has nowhere left to hide.

The resolution is not conversion but reconciliation — with [[martha|Martha]], with his own mortality, and with the limits of what a life of action can prepare you for.

## Key Scenes

> Old Donegal was dying. They had all known it for some time — the doctor, the nurses, the patients in the ward. Everyone but Martha. She thought he was getting better. [004-001, 004-008]

The opening establishes the central deception that structures the story. Donegal's protectiveness toward [[martha|Martha]] is both loving and patronizing — a dynamic the story will interrogate.

> "Well! Music!" grunted Old Donegal. "That's good. This is some send-off." [004-073]

When music begins playing in his room, Donegal's wry humor surfaces even as death approaches. His voice throughout the story oscillates between gallows humor and genuine fear.

> "You look like hell, Donegal," the padre offered jovially. [004-183]

The priest's refusal to treat Donegal with hushed reverence is the story's turning point. Their exchange — profane, combative, and unexpectedly tender — provides the emotional climax.

## Relationships

- **[[martha]]** — Donegal's wife, whom he has protected from the truth of his condition. Their marriage is the story's emotional anchor.
- **Caid** — A figure from Donegal's past, mentioned in his drifting memories. Represents the camaraderie of his military years.
- **The Priest** — A young clergyman who challenges Donegal's self-conception and guides him toward a more honest reckoning with death.
