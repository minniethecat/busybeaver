# Space Travel

## Definition

Space travel in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s short fiction serves as both a literal setting and a metaphorical framework for exploring human limitations. Across the stories in this collection, space appears as a theater of war, a site of personal ambition, and the ultimate horizon against which characters measure their lives. Unlike the optimistic space opera of much 1950s science fiction, Miller's treatment of space is characteristically ambivalent — it is a realm of achievement that nonetheless demands and extracts a human cost.

## Military Space Operations

The most fully realized depiction of space travel appears in "Death of a Spaceman," where [[old-donegal|Old Donegal]] is a veteran of "the close-space assaults against the Soviet satellites" (004-086), piloting an R-43 fighter during the war years. This is space as combat theater — claustrophobic, dangerous, and deeply formative for those who survive it.

"Check and Checkmate" extends the military dimension into orbital infrastructure, with references to a "missile-launching site in space" and a "satellite station" being readied for battle (003-087, 003-168). The Cold War logic of terrestrial conflict has been projected upward, turning orbital space into contested territory.

## The Spacer Identity

Miller develops a distinct "spacer" subculture through [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s memories and self-conception. Space travel produces a particular kind of person: tough, irreverent, and permanently marked by the experience. [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s "space-rig" drapes around him "in an old familiar way" (004-043); he judges others by whether they have "natural-born space-legs" (004-060).

This identity is aspirational as well as retrospective. [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s son wants to become a "space-engineer" (004-061), and [[martha|Martha]]'s opposition — "He doesn't want to go to space" (004-067) — reveals the generational tension between a mother's fear and a culture that valorizes the spacer life.

## Commercial and Civilian Dimensions

Beyond the military framework, space travel in Miller's world has a commercial dimension. [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s space career included "space-runs" (004-028) — civilian transport work that paid well enough to afford real whiskey. The line between military and civilian space work is porous; spacers move between roles as economics and war demand.

"Way of a Rebel" briefly touches on space as escape: a character fleeing [[earth|Earth]]'s political constraints considers the colonies as refuge. Space here is freedom, but a freedom purchased at the price of permanent exile.

## Cultural Significance

Space travel functions in Miller's fiction as a test of character. [[old-donegal|Donegal]]'s dying wish — "I want to be buried with my back to space" (004-004) — is a final act of defiance, a refusal to face the void he spent his life navigating. The gesture is simultaneously proud, terrified, and deeply human.

Miller's treatment resists both techno-utopianism and anti-technology moralism. Space is simply where humans have gone, bringing their wars, their ambitions, and their mortality with them.
