# Doctor Hiram Georges

## Character Profile

Doctor Hiram Georges is a family physician in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]" — "a man well known, well loved" in the Sherman II community, where he has practiced "for many years" (001-071). Though he appears only briefly before his death, his murder is the story's catalytic event, transforming [[norris|Norris]]'s abstract moral unease into concrete crisis.

Georges first contacts [[norris|Norris]] by radiophone with an urgent request involving a neutroid matter at his office, asking the inspector to come immediately. "This is Doctor Georges. We haven't met, but I imagine we will" (001-012). His tone is cooperative and collegial, hinting at the web of professional relationships [[norris|Norris]] navigates as a Bio-Administration inspector.

## Role in Narrative

Doctor Georges functions as a sacrificial figure whose death dramatizes the human cost of the [[mutant-animals|mutant-animal]] economy. At seven o'clock that evening, less than an hour after [[norris|Norris]] leaves his office, a woman — allegedly Mrs. Glubbes — "burst into Doctor Georges' dining room while the family was at dinner. She was brandishing a pistol and screaming, 'You stole my baby! You gave me the wrong baby! Where's my baby?'" (001-073).

The killing forces [[norris|Norris]] to confront the consequences of a system that treats intelligent creatures as disposable property. If a respected physician can be murdered over a neutroid, the boundary between the rational administration of [[mutant-animals|mutants]] and the irrational passions they inspire has collapsed. Georges's death — at his own dinner table, in front of his family — makes the abstract horror of [[norris|Norris]]'s work suddenly and terribly personal.

## Key Scenes

> "Inspector Norris? This is Doctor Georges. We haven't met, but I imagine we will. Are you extremely busy at the moment?" (001-012)

Georges's single direct appearance in the narrative establishes him as a competent professional reaching across institutional lines. His call sets in motion the chain of events — involving a woman who believes a neutroid is her child — that will lead directly to his death.

> "This is James Duncan, ladies and gentlemen, speaking to you from our mobile unit in front of the late Doctor Hiram Georges' residence... Doctor Georges' death has shocked the community deeply." (001-071)

The news broadcast of Georges's murder is filtered through the story's media apparatus, emphasizing how quickly private tragedy becomes public spectacle. The announcer's description of Georges as "well known, well loved" underscores the senselessness of the killing.
