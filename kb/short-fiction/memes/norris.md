# Norris

## Character Profile

Norris is a Bio-Administration district inspector — effectively a "dogcatcher" for genetically engineered [[neutroid|mutant animals]] — in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]." A [[class-c-citizen|class-C]] citizen with "[[class-c-citizen|defective heredity]]" (001-031), he is legally barred from having children and lives in a pastel suburban cottage with his wife [[anne|Anne]]. Norris holds a B.S. in biology and was assigned to Bio-Administration based on aptitude tests administered by the [[federation|Federation]]'s employment system (001-016).

Physically unremarkable and emotionally insecure, Norris is introduced on the first morning of his new assignment, already in retreat from his wife's cold fury. He "backed a few steps toward the door" and "stumbled down the hall and out of the house" (001-012), establishing his tendency toward avoidance in the face of domestic conflict.

## Role in Narrative

Norris serves as the protagonist and moral center of "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]." His function is to embody the ethical tension at the heart of Miller's world: a society that creates intelligent life for commercial purposes, then tasks ordinary bureaucrats with managing — and destroying — the surplus.

His job involves rounding up unlicensed [[neutroid|mutant animals]], housing them in kennels, and eventually destroying those that remain unclaimed. Norris tries to rationalize the work: "Destroying unclaimed units is the smallest part of it" (001-016). But his wife's accusation — "I didn't know you killed them" (001-008) — haunts him throughout the story.

He describes himself as "just a dogcatcher" (001-016), a self-diminishing comparison that reveals both his discomfort with the role and his need to minimize its moral weight. The [[federation|Federation]]'s classification of [[mutant-animals|mutants]] as property is legally clear, but Norris's encounters — with a desperate woman who believes a neutroid is her child, with a shady pet dealer, with the creatures themselves — gradually erode his certainty.

## Story Arc

Norris begins the story already morally compromised and aware of it. His honeymoon is over in both literal and figurative senses: his marriage to [[anne|Anne]] is strained by his profession, and his innocent belief in the system's righteousness is crumbling.

The arc progresses through a series of encounters that test his bureaucratic detachment. [[doctor-georges|Doctor Georges]] calls with an urgent request involving a woman who has bonded with a neutroid as though it were her human child (001-037–001-053). Norris agrees to bend the rules — "It bordered on being illegal" (001-054) — revealing a compassion that his official role does not permit.

As the story continues, Norris confronts the gap between the [[federation|Federation]]'s cold taxonomy and the lived reality of [[mutant-animals|mutants]] who demonstrate loyalty, fear, and something approaching love. His journey is not toward heroic defiance but toward a quieter, more painful recognition: the system he serves is built on convenient fictions about what counts as human.

## Key Scenes

> "They assigned me to it because I *liked* babies. And because I have a B.S. in biology and an aptitude for dealing with people. Can't you understand? Destroying unclaimed units is the smallest part of it." [001-016]

This early domestic scene establishes Norris's defensive self-justification. [[anne|Anne]]'s response — "I suppose you have an aptitude for killing babies?" — cuts through his rationalizations with surgical precision.

> Norris immediately regretted his consent. It bordered on being illegal. But he saw it as a quick way to get rid of an animal that might later have to be killed. [001-054]

After agreeing to help [[doctor-georges|Doctor Georges]], Norris recognizes that his compassion is tangled with expedience — he helps one mutant to avoid the burden of killing another.

> Norris thought glumly, there was also taking away. And if the two acts could be separated so that the recipients never saw the victims, both transactions could be carried on without guilt. [001-159]

This interior moment crystallizes Norris's moral insight. The system works by separating the joy of receiving a pet from the grief of its destruction — a partitioning of experience that Norris, uniquely positioned at the junction, can no longer maintain.

## Relationships

- **[[anne]]** — Norris's wife, a [[class-c-citizen|class-C]] woman whose moral clarity about the [[neutroid|mutant animals]] serves as Norris's conscience. Her cold contempt drives much of his internal conflict.
- **[[doctor-georges]]** — A physician who enlists Norris's help in a questionable scheme to replace a dying neutroid, testing the boundaries of Norris's rule-following.
- **[[sheriff-yates]]** — A local law enforcement officer who assists Norris in enforcing Bio-Administration regulations, representing the coercive apparatus behind Norris's bureaucratic role.
