# Mars

## Overview

Mars appears across multiple stories in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s collection as a world of layered meaning: an ancient dying civilization, a colonial frontier, and a red beacon in the night sky that signals both possibility and exile. Miller's treatment of Mars is more mythic than scientific, treating the planet as a stage for human dramas of discovery, exploitation, and loss.

## Mars in "It Takes a Thief"

The most fully realized depiction of Mars appears in "It Takes a Thief," where the planet is portrayed as a world with its own deep history and culture. The protagonist [[asir|Asir]] is "young, perhaps in his tenth Marsyear" (002-004), a native of a society with its own calendar and traditions. The landscape is alien but habitable: "the low hills of Mars, sun-washed and gray-green with lichen" (002-005).

This Mars has a spiritual dimension. The story opens with an invocation: "the cold air roared from the womb of Mars our Mother, the ice burned with the cold fire of eternal night" (002-001). The planet is personified as a maternal figure, a "Mother" whose womb is the source of life and whose death is mourned.

Central to the narrative is the "Mars-Culture" (002-022) — an indigenous civilization with its own gods, including "Roggins, Progress of the Mars-Culture." This civilization is ancient, measured in "Marsyears" (002-250) that stretch into the thousands. The story's central conflict involves the theft of cultural knowledge and the relationship between the native Martians and off-world interlopers.

The physical infrastructure of this Mars includes passages "into the bowels of Mars" (002-130) and native fauna like the "Marswolf" (002-152) and "Marsoxen" (002-244). It is a fully realized world, not merely a desert outpost.

## Mars as Alternative to Earth

In "[[the-ties-that-bind-work|The Ties That Bind]]," Mars appears as a practical alternative to [[earth|Earth]] for industrial development: "Why not on Mars? We can develop our own facilities" (006-022). But the response — "The job would take five years on Mars" (006-023) — reveals the planet's limitations as a colonial site. Mars is viable but inconvenient, close enough to reach but far enough to frustrate.

This pragmatic treatment contrasts with the mythic Mars of "It Takes a Thief," suggesting that different stories in the collection occupy different points in a shared timeline or different registers of the same universe.

## The Red Eye

In "The Hoofer," Mars appears in its simplest and most evocative form: "the red eye of Mars low in the southwest" (007-112). Here the planet is not a setting but a symbol — of distance, of watching, of the vastness against which human dramas unfold. This minimal treatment is arguably the most effective, reducing Mars to its emotional essence.
