# Father Paulson

## Character Profile

Father Paulson is an elderly Catholic clergyman in Walter M. Miller Jr.'s "[[conditionally-human|Conditionally Human]]," "erect in his black suit and radiating dignity" (001-036). He visits [[norris|Inspector Norris]]'s home on behalf of James O'Reilley, a pet-shop owner who has come to him for advice about a neutroid named Peony that appears to have human-like intelligence.

Unlike the zealots [[norris|Norris]] expects, Paulson approaches the mutant question with theological nuance. He delivers one of the story's key moral formulations: "We say it's evil to create the creatures. We say *also* that it's evil to destroy them after they're made. Not murder, exactly, but — mockery of life, perhaps. It's the entire institution that's evil" (001-047).

## Role in Narrative

Paulson serves as the story's moral interlocutor — an external voice that articulates the ethical complexity [[norris|Norris]] feels but cannot name. His mission is investigative: O'Reilley claims Peony can talk, which would make her exceptional among [[mutant-animals|neutroids]], and Paulson has come to verify the claim.

His presence catalyzes the [[norris|Norris]] household's moral debate. [[anne|Anne]], initially hostile ("I know your kind! You'd do anything to get rid of [[mutant-animals|neutroids]]!" [001-046]), is won over when Paulson refuses to condemn Peony. Asked what he would do in [[norris|Norris]]'s position, Paulson offers uncomfortable advice: "I'd withhold her from my superiors. I'd also quit my job and go away" (001-049). [[norris|Norris]] doesn't want to hear it — but [[anne|Anne]]'s expression "suddenly changed; she looked at the priest with a new interest" (001-049).

## Key Scenes

> "I came only to assure myself that O'Reilley's sane," Paulson told her.
> "I don't believe you," she snapped.
> He stared at her in wounded surprise; then he chuckled. "People used to trust the cloth. Ah, well." (001-047)

This exchange captures Paulson's character in miniature. He is motivated by genuine pastoral concern, not institutional agenda, and he responds to hostility with weary humor rather than indignation.

> "I fail to agree."
> "What!" Anne confronted him again. "What makes *you* human?"
> "A soul, my child."
> Anne put her hands on her hips and leaned forward to glare down at him like something unwholesome. "Can you put a voltmeter between your ears and measure it?" (001-052–001-053)

The theological confrontation between [[anne|Anne]]'s empiricism and Paulson's metaphysics is the story's intellectual heart. Paulson's gentle "A soul, my child" is both sincere belief and, under [[anne|Anne]]'s assault, an admission that faith cannot be proven — only held.

## Relationships

- **[[anne]]** — [[norris|Norris]]'s wife, whose fierce defense of Peony initially puts her at odds with Paulson, though they find unexpected common ground.
