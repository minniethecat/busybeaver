# Rye

Rye is a medieval town on the Sussex coast, situated near the [[romney-marshes|Romney Marshes]], that serves as [[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]]'s tactical base during the later chapters of the novel. Its proximity to the marshlands — where the criminal gang led by [[larry-hughes|Larry Hughes]] has established a hideout — makes it the natural staging point for the police operation that culminates in the gang's capture. Labar's choice of Rye reflects his preference for unobtrusive positioning: rather than announcing his presence, he blends into the town as a visiting golfer and naturalist.

## In the Novel

### Arrival Under Cover

Labar arrives in Rye carrying "a suitcase and a bag of golf clubs"（[[021-026]]）, adopting the persona of a leisure traveller to avoid drawing attention. He deliberately avoids the "old hostelries where visitors might have become curious and friendly"（[[021-027]]）, instead taking "humble lodgings in one of the small houses that look upon the cobbled square"（[[021-027]]）. The choice is tactical: anonymity is essential when the target may have lookouts watching for police activity.

When questioned by a local police officer about his presence, Labar maintains his cover: "'I'm staying at Rye. I've come out to have a walk over the marshes'"（[[021-037]]）. His declared purpose — naturalism — provides an innocuous explanation for his movements across the open marshland where a stranger would otherwise attract suspicion.

### Strategic Position

Rye's geographical position between the marshland hideout and the main road network makes it the logical command post. The "Folkestone—Rye road"（[[021-023]]） is identified as the route used by the gang's vehicles, and from Rye, Labar can monitor traffic without himself being observed. The town's proximity to the marsh also allows him to coordinate with the police forces assembling from [[lydd|Lydd]] and patrolling the coast as far as [[dungeness|Dungeness]]（[[024-024]]）.

### Aftermath

Following the successful roundup of the gang, Rye continues to function as a logistics hub. "Motor cars were sent for from various points by which the prisoners already gathered in might be escorted to Lydd, where they were to remain for the time guarded by a strong force of police"（[[028-022]]）. The town's role as a waypoint between London and the marshland crime scene reflects the novel's geography of pursuit, in which the metropolitan detective must extend his reach through a chain of provincial locations.
