# Criminal Record Office

The Criminal Record Office is a specialist department of [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]
that functions as the institutional memory of British policing. It maintains
dossiers on known criminals — photographs, physical descriptions, criminal
histories, and modus operandi — enabling detectives to identify suspects and
establish connections between crimes. In [[the-lazy-detective|The Lazy Detective]],
[[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]] calls upon the Record
Office to develop a portrait of [[billy-bungey|Billy Bungey]].

## Function

The Record Office serves as the central repository for criminal identification
data. When Labar needs to identify or locate a known criminal, the Record
Office provides the documentary and photographic evidence that can confirm
identity and establish criminal history:

> Then with such skill in portraiture as he possessed, added to the scientific formula for these matters, he drew a word picture of Billy Bungey, and sent the resulting composition along to the Criminal Record Office with the request that any amendments might be made and a photograph added if possible. [021-019]

## The Pre-Digital Era

The novel depicts a pre-computerised system of criminal records. Descriptions
are "word pictures" — textual portraits composed by detectives — supplemented
by physical photographs. The system relies on human memory, clerical
organisation, and the institutional knowledge of career officers who have
spent years studying the criminal population.

This analog system, while slower than modern databases, serves the narrative
purpose of demonstrating the craft of detection: the detective must know
which questions to ask and which records to consult.

## Relationship to Investigation

The Criminal Record Office is part of the broader investigative infrastructure
that supports Labar's work. Alongside forensic examination [012-002],
surveillance operations [009-006], and witness interviews, the records
system provides the background intelligence that allows detectives to
contextualise individual crimes within patterns of criminal behaviour.
