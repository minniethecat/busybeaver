# Stebbins

Stebbins is the inside man. Placed by [[larry-hughes|Larry Hughes]] in the
[[streetly-house|Streetly House]] household as an "odd-job man" shortly before
the robbery, he is the human vector through which the gang obtained the
information necessary for the crime — the layout of the house, the location of
the jewels, the habits of the household. In the novel's criminal hierarchy, he
occupies the vulnerable middle ground between the planners and the executioners:
useful enough to be employed, disposable enough to be sacrificed.

## The Placement

Stebbins enters the narrative through the indirect testimony of
[[mrs-gertstein|Mrs. Gertstein]], who recalls his hiring:

> She passed a hand with a weary gesture over her forehead. "No, I am sure that
> he had never been with Mr. Hughes. I believe he came from some big restaurant
> that was reducing its staff. I've got it. His name was Stebbins." [013-040]

The "big restaurant that was reducing its staff" is a cover story — plausible
enough to pass casual scrutiny, thin enough to unravel under investigation.
[[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]] recognises its
significance immediately: Stebbins was placed, and the placer was Hughes.

## The Taxonomy of Crooks

The novel provides a clinical classification of Stebbins's criminal type:

> Stebbins was a type of a shiftless cunning species of crook which is well
> known to the Criminal Investigation Department. He was a drifter, weak and
> unscrupulous, lacking the imagination or skill of many of his more lawless
> brethren. [018-027]

"Shiftless cunning" is a precise diagnosis: Stebbins is clever enough to be
useful to a criminal enterprise but lacks the discipline or ambition to rise
within it. He is, in the gang's economy, a consumable resource — to be used
and, when necessary, discarded.

## The Interrogation

Labar's confrontation with Stebbins is one of the novel's most compressed
dramatic scenes. Cornered in a "dismal three-storeyed house" [017-041],
Stebbins threatens violence — and Labar responds not with force but with
the measured description of its consequences:

> "Don't be a silly ass, Stebbins. It won't do you any good to kill me. Think
> what you'd feel like when they came to pinion you in the condemned cell."
> He crawled cautiously to a further step. "Think of the hangman adjusting the
> straps, and the parson reading the burial service." [017-059]

The speech is a masterpiece of psychological pressure. Labar does not threaten
Stebbins; he invites him to imagine the aftermath of his own action, step by
ritualised step. The "condemned cell," the "hangman adjusting the straps," the
"parson reading the burial service" — each detail is chosen for its sensory
specificity. Stebbins, who lacks imagination, is made to imagine.

## The Intelligence Value

Stebbins's value to the investigation is not his culpability but his knowledge.
As an insider at Streetly House and a member of the Hughes network, he occupies
a unique intersection of the legitimate and criminal worlds. Labar's interest in
him is not punitive but informational: what did Hughes ask you to observe? Whom
did you report to? The foot-soldier, properly interrogated, becomes a map to the
general.
