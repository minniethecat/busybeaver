# Dr. Ware

Dr. Ware is a local medical practitioner who becomes an incidental ally to [[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]] during his pursuit of the criminal gang in the Romney Marshes area. Described as a competent man, Ware provides local knowledge and acts as driver for Labar during a car chase through the byroads. Though only appearing briefly, his involvement reflects the network of ordinary citizens upon whom police detectives must rely during field operations outside their usual jurisdiction.

## Character

Ware introduces himself succinctly — "'Ware. I'm one of the local medicos'"（[[015-034]]）— a terse self-identification that suggests a pragmatic, no-nonsense professional. Labar quickly assesses him as "a competent man"（[[015-038]]） and judges that "with a competent man, such as Dr. Ware seemed to be, at his elbow it might be possible to regain and keep touch with the gang, until an opportune moment for dealing with them arrived"（[[015-038]]）. The doctor's competence is demonstrated during the car chase when he follows Labar's instructions without hesitation, closing the bonnet of his car and taking his seat at the wheel to give chase（[[015-047]]）.

Ware also possesses useful local geographical knowledge. When the pursuit turns off the main road, he identifies the new direction: "'That's the Worthing Road,' exclaimed Ware. 'They're keeping to the byroads'"（[[015-052]]）. Later, when the quarry suddenly vanishes, Ware reacts with quick recognition — "'Damn 'em. They've switched'"（[[015-056]]）— understanding the criminals' evasive tactic before Labar can voice it.

## Role in the Novel

### Field Assistance During the Gang Pursuit

Dr. Ware's primary function in the narrative is as Labar's local facilitator during the pursuit in Chapter XV. Labar, operating far from his London base at [[grape-street-police-station|Grape Street Police Station]], needs a local contact with transport and area knowledge. Ware provides both: his car and his familiarity with the Worthing Road and its twisting byroads enable Labar to maintain visual contact with the fleeing Rolls Royce carrying the gang members.

Labar's pre-existing relationship with Ware is hinted at through the pistol: "He remembered that he had failed to retrieve the pistol that he had lent to Dr. Ware"（[[017-054]]）. This detail, mentioned in Chapter XVII when Labar is searching in darkness, suggests that Ware was armed for potential confrontation — a significant level of trust extended by a detective to a civilian doctor. The loan of a firearm implies that Labar regarded Ware as reliable under pressure and anticipated the possibility of armed conflict during the field operation.

### Narrative Significance

Ware represents the capable local ally — a recurring type in detective fiction where the metropolitan investigator must depend on provincial resources. Unlike the institutional support Labar receives from [[chief-constable-winter|Chief Constable Winter]] or his own [[detective-inspector-labar|Criminal Investigation Department]] colleagues, Ware is a civilian volunteer whose assistance is motivated by professional duty rather than police obligation. His brief but effective presence reinforces the novel's depiction of police work as dependent on ad hoc networks of local cooperation.
