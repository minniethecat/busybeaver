# Deduction

Deduction, the logical process of deriving specific conclusions from general premises, is the method most famously associated with the fictional detective — yet in *The Lazy Detective*, the word appears only once. This rarity is itself significant: Labar is not a deductive reasoner in the Holmesian mold but something more intuitive and experiential.

## In the Novel

### The Single Reference

The novel's solitary reference to deduction occurs early, in the narrator's characterization of Labar's mental habits:

> The amount of time taken by a detective on an individual job is largely a matter on which his superiors must accept his word. Some men slog laboriously, while others get their results quickly. [[001-003]]

The passage that follows establishes Labar not as a logician but as an efficiency optimizer — someone who gets results not through brilliant chains of reasoning but through knowing where to look and whom to pressure.

### Method as Anti-Method

Labar's approach to investigation resists formalization. He does not proceed from premises to conclusions but from hunches to confirmations. His laziness, paradoxically, makes him more effective — he cannot afford to pursue false leads, so he develops an instinct for which leads are true.

### The Novel's Epistemology

*The Lazy Detective* represents a distinct epistemology of detection — one in which knowledge comes not from logical operations on evidence but from immersion in the criminal world, from understanding people rather than facts, and from a kind of professional intuition that resists articulation. Deduction, in this framework, is almost beside the point.
