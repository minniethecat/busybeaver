# Interrogation

In [[the-lazy-detective|The Lazy Detective]], interrogation is the primary
investigative tool of [[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]].
Operating within the constraints of British law — which explicitly forbids
the "Third Degree" — Labar develops a repertoire of psychological techniques:
the calibrated bluff, the strategic release, the feigned sympathy, and the
sudden shift from friend to interrogator. His methods are displayed most
vividly in his questioning of [[gold-dust-teddy|Gold Dust Teddy]], [[penelope-noelson|Penelope Noelson]],
and [[mrs-gertstein|Mrs. Gertstein]].

## Legal Context

The British legal framework of the early twentieth century placed firm limits
on police interrogation. Physical coercion was illegal, and confessions
obtained under duress were inadmissible. The narrative explicitly references
this constraint:

> He was determined to break Teddy down, and since the Third Degree is frowned upon by British law, there was only this way open to him. [008-028]

This prohibition shapes Labar's entire approach. Unable to compel testimony by
force, he must rely on psychological manipulation, information asymmetry, and
the suspect's own fear and uncertainty.

## The Bluff Technique

Labar's signature interrogation method is the bluff — a calculated assertion
designed to destabilise the suspect's confidence. Unlike a simple lie, Labar's
bluffs are grounded in enough real evidence to be plausible, amplified by the
suspect's guilty knowledge:

> "Bluffing, am I? Don't you believe it, son. I know all about Larry and the others. You think that Larry will help you out of this mess. He won't. He's on his way out of London, and he's leaving you and the others to hold the baby." [008-027]

The opening line of the novel establishes bluff as a central theme. The
Chief Constable's admonition — "And remember, Labar, you don't bluff me"
[001-001] — is both a warning and an ironic commentary: Labar will spend
the rest of the book bluffing everyone except his superior.

## The Strategic Release

A more subtle technique is the strategic release — letting a suspect go
free in order to use them as bait. Labar employs this with Gold Dust Teddy,
releasing him from custody while placing him under covert surveillance:

> "Don't you worry about Teddy. We'll get our hooks on him when we want. I'm using him as bait. What I want you to do is to watch when the big fish bites." [008-042]

This approach reflects Labar's strategic patience. Rather than charging
Teddy on what evidence he has — which might not secure a conviction —
he uses Teddy as a vector to reach the true target: [[larry-hughes|Larry Hughes]].

## Approach by Suspect Type

| Suspect | Technique | Outcome |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| [[gold-dust-teddy|Gold Dust Teddy]] | Bluff + strategic release | Teddy released as bait, later arrested |
| [[penelope-noelson|Penelope Noelson]] | Direct accusation + bluff | Fainted under pressure, later fled |
| [[mrs-gertstein|Mrs. Gertstein]] | Gentle questioning | Partial cooperation |
| Stebbins | Methodical extraction | Full confession of his role |

## Cross-Examination at Streetly House

Labar's initial investigation at [[streetly-house|Streetly House]] demonstrates
his systematic approach to witness examination. Rather than interrogating
suspects in isolation, he methodically questions every person in the household,
building a composite picture of movements and relationships:

> With the shrewd suavity of an Old Bailey lawyer he examined and cross-examined, an obese shorthand writer at his elbow, until he had a complete surface knowledge, at any rate, of the movements of everyone in the house for the last twenty-four hours. [003-002]

This method — exhaustive, patient, documentary — is the counterpoint to the
more theatrical bluff technique. Together they form the two poles of Labar's
interrogation practice: one designed to overwhelm by volume of information,
the other by psychological pressure.

## Limitations and Ethics

Labar's interrogation methods occupy an ethical grey area. He does not employ
physical force, and he operates within the letter of the law. But his
manipulation of suspects — lying about evidence, playing on their fears,
exploiting their personal relationships — raises questions that the novel
leaves unresolved. His success is measured by results: the solving of the
[[streetly-house-theft|Streetly House robbery]] and the dismantling of
Hughes's criminal network.
