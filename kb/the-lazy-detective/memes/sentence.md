# Sentence

The sentence — the term of imprisonment or other punishment imposed by a court upon conviction — is the final determination of the criminal justice system. In *The Lazy Detective*, sentences operate as a kind of currency: their length measures the gravity of crime, their certainty shapes criminal behavior, and their experience marks those who serve them.

## In the Novel

### The Three-Year Sentence

A specific sentence — three years — recurs as a biographical fact that defines a character:

> "You came out from a three years sentence." [[018-015]]

The sentence is not described in detail; its length alone conveys the information Labar needs. Three years is substantial enough to mark a serious offense, short enough that the person has returned to society and resumed criminal activity. It is a sentence that punishes without reforming.

### The Threat of Sentence

Throughout the investigation, the possibility of a sentence hangs over every suspect. The threat is rarely made explicit — Labar is too subtle for that — but it is the unspoken subtext of every interview. Cooperation means the possibility of a lighter sentence or none at all. Silence means risking the full weight of the law.

### Sentence as Biographical Marker

For characters who have served time, the sentence becomes part of their identity. It marks a before and after in their lives:

> Men against whom the police had found convincing evidence. There were others, cripples for life. [[008-031]]

The sentence does not end with release. Its effects — social, psychological, physical — persist indefinitely. The novel acknowledges this without sentimentalizing it: some emerge from prison as functional criminals, others as broken men.
