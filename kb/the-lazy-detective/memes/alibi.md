# Alibi

The alibi, a defendant's claim of having been elsewhere at the time of a crime, is one of the oldest and most contested forms of criminal defense. In *The Lazy Detective*, the alibi is not merely a factual claim but a strategic construct — something that can be manufactured, tested, and dismantled.

## In the Novel

### The Manufactured Alibi

When confronted by Labar, a suspect immediately reaches for the language of the alibi:

> "I can prove where I was all that night. I can bring witnesses." [[008-024]]

The speed and confidence of the response are themselves suspicious. Labar, with years of experience, does not dismiss the claim outright but probes its substance:

> "What kind of witnesses?" [[008-025]]

The experienced detective knows that an alibi is only as strong as its weakest witness — and that witnesses can be coached, bribed, or intimidated.

### The Alibi as Strategy

The novel recognizes that establishing an alibi is not just about truth but about strategy. A suspect who anticipates being questioned will prepare:

> He did not doubt that the other had taken some kind of steps to establish an alibi. [[008-025]]

The alibi becomes a kind of chess move — the suspect prepares a defense, and the detective must decide whether to attack it directly or look for weaknesses elsewhere.

### Truth and Fabrication

The novel's treatment of the alibi is sophisticated: not all alibis are false, and not all apparently solid alibis are true. The distinction between the genuinely exculpatory and the cleverly fabricated is the detective's art. Labar's method is not to disprove alibis but to verify them — and in verification, the truth tends to emerge.
