# Prison

Prison, the institution of state-sanctioned confinement, pervades the moral landscape of *The Lazy Detective*. It is the destination that every criminal act risks, the experience that marks certain characters, and the threat that Detective Inspector Labar deploys as a tool of interrogation and persuasion.

## In the Novel

### Prison as Threat

Labar's power derives ultimately from his ability to send people to prison. The threat is rarely explicit but always present — every interview, every request for cooperation, is conducted in the shadow of potential incarceration. The detective does not need to threaten; the institution does the work for him.

### Prison as Biography

For some characters, prison is not a threat but a past. A character's criminal record is revealed through fingerprint identification:

> "I sent your fingerprints to the Yard and had you looked up. You came out from a three years sentence." [[018-015]]

The prison sentence becomes a permanent mark — a fact that can be produced at any time, shaping how the character is seen by police, by employers, by society.

### Prison as System

The novel gestures toward the systemic reality of incarceration. Labar reflects on the men he has helped convict:

> There were men against whom the police had found convincing evidence to their hands. There were others, cripples for life, who dare not tell in what prison they had suffered. [[008-031]]

The passage acknowledges that prison does not simply punish — it transforms, and not always for the better. The "cripples for life" are the human cost of a system that the detective serves.

### The Prisoner's Knowledge

Prison also functions as a kind of education. Characters who have served time emerge with knowledge — of how the system works, of who holds power, of what can and cannot be proved. This knowledge makes them both more dangerous and more useful to the detective.
