# Pursuit

In [[the-lazy-detective|The Lazy Detective]], pursuit occupies a paradoxical position
in [[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]]'s methodology. He is a man
capable of decisive physical action — the pursuit across the fog-bound marshes of
[[mopes-bottom|Mope's Bottom]] is one of the novel's most dramatic set-pieces —
yet his investigative philosophy is fundamentally opposed to the impulse of the
chase. Labar's genius lies in knowing when not to pursue.

## The Impulse Restrained

The novel's first encounter with pursuit is an exercise in restraint. When
[[larry-hughes|Larry Hughes]] and [[penelope-noelson|Penelope Noelson]] flee,
Labar's instinct is to give chase:

> Although it would have pleased Harry Labar to tumble into the fastest motor
> car he could find and engage in swift and melodramatic chase of Larry Hughes
> and Penelope, he was deterred by many considerations. [008-001]

The "many considerations" that deter him are the essence of his method: a
melodramatic chase would alert the quarry, scatter the conspirators, and sacrifice
the strategic advantage of knowing where they are heading. Labar chooses
[[surveillance|surveillance]] over pursuit — the patient watch over the headlong chase.

## The Simplest Problem

Scotland Yard, the novel observes, regards pursuit as its most straightforward
function — and precisely because it is simple, it is the least interesting test
of detective ability:

> Although perhaps the most spectacular, in reality the most simple of the
> problems that arise at Scotland Yard is the pursuit of a known man for a
> known crime. A criminal may escape if there is nothing known against him,
> but once his identity and his guilt are established, he is inevitably run
> to earth. [021-001]

The passage articulates a hierarchy of police work in which pursuit is the
final, almost mechanical stage — necessary, dramatic, but intellectually
subordinate to the prior work of identification and proof. The real detective
work happens before the chase begins.

## The Fog Chase

The novel's most sustained pursuit sequence takes place in the fog around
Mope's Bottom, where Labar pursues not a suspect but a victim — [[sophie|Sophie]] —
through terrain that dissolves all certainties:

> Behind him he could hear the mutter of men's voices and someone speaking to
> the dog. In the strange way in which fog sometimes carries sound he heard the
> snap of the gate padlock and the whimper of the hound. [022-010]

The fog transforms pursuit into a sensory disorientation. Labar "strode stealthily
in what he imagined to be the direction of the house" [022-020], his spatial
certainty eroded by the very landscape he must traverse. The pursuit becomes a
test not of speed but of nerve — the capacity to keep moving when every landmark
has dissolved.

> He let her sink to the damp grass, and stood for a moment poised in fierce
> concentration. Dimly in the distance he could hear the muffled sounds made by
> the pursuit. [022-030]

## Pursuit as Counter-Pursuit

The novel complicates the hunter-hunted binary by staging moments where the
pursuer becomes the pursued. [[larry-hughes|Larry Hughes]], fleeing through
darkened streets, "halted abruptly and swinging in his tracks fired blindly
at the nearest man" — a shot intended "only as a demonstration to check
pursuit" [015-006]. The reversal is structural: every character in the novel
is simultaneously pursuing something and fleeing something else. Labar pursues
the criminals; Mrs. Gertstein pursues escape from [[blackmail]]; Hughes pursues
the jewels while fleeing the law.
