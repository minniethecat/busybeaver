# Appeal

In [[the-lazy-detective|The Lazy Detective]], the concept of appeal defines the
power relationships between characters. To appeal is to place oneself in a
position of dependence — to acknowledge that one cannot act alone and must
solicit the help of a higher power. The novel traces what happens when the
natural objects of appeal — a husband, the police, the law itself — are
unavailable, and characters must turn instead to the very people who have
ensnared them.

## The Unavailable Husband

[[mrs-gertstein|Mrs. Gertstein]]'s predicament is defined by the impossibility
of appeal. She owes a debt that she cannot pay, and the one person who could
discharge it — her millionaire husband [[solly-gertstein|Solly Gertstein]] —
is the one person she cannot ask:

> To the wife of a millionaire such a sum perhaps ought not to seem impossible.
> But there were reasons why Adèle Gertstein dare not appeal to her husband.
> There were limits to his devotion, and he might well ask questions that she
> could never answer. [011-004]

The word "dare" captures the dynamic: appeal is not a right but a risk. To appeal
to her husband would be to expose the secrets that the money was meant to bury.
The appeal that should be the most natural — wife to husband — is foreclosed by
the very circumstances that make it necessary.

## The Trap of the False Ally

When the legitimate channels of appeal are blocked, Mrs. Gertstein turns to the
one person who offers help — [[larry-hughes|Larry Hughes]]. The irony is revealed
by [[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]]:

> "It is the man to whom you appealed for aid. It is Larry Hughes himself who
> has been bleeding you. I want to know who he has been paying to get information
> from you and your husband." [012-047]

The appeal for aid has been answered by the predator himself. Hughes' genius is
to position himself as the solution to a problem he created — to be the only
available object of appeal in a landscape he has deliberately emptied of
alternatives. Mrs. Gertstein's tragedy is structural: she appealed to the one
person who could not help her because he was the one person who would not.

## The Personal Appeal

Beyond the legal and financial senses, the novel is saturated with interpersonal
appeals — the mute plea of a face, a gesture, a look:

> Adèle Gertstein felt sincerely sorry for herself. She turned an appealing
> face to Penelope. The other girl regarded her inquiringly. [020-024]

The "appealing face" is a form of speech without words — a request for sympathy
that bypasses the rational faculties and addresses itself directly to the
emotions. [[penelope-noelson|Penelope Noelson]]'s response — "inquiringly" rather
than sympathetically — suggests the limits of this mode of appeal. An appeal
that does not find its audience is merely a posture.

The novel's climax is itself an appeal — Mrs. Gertstein, "limp, hysterical, and
half-paralysed by her own emotions," flings herself before Labar in a final,
desperate plea for mercy [029-001]. The gesture is the culmination of every failed
appeal that preceded it: the unavailable husband, the false ally, the indifferent
friend. In the end, she appeals to the law itself, embodied in the detective who
has pursued her.
