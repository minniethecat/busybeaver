# Mrs. Gertstein

Mrs. Gertstein occupies the novel's moral center — neither innocent nor irredeemable, she is a woman whose forgery ([[forgery]]) of her husband's signature sets in motion consequences she could not have foreseen. Her crime is real, but the novel presents it as the act of a desperate person rather than a calculating criminal.

## Character

She lives in the shadow of her husband's wealth and authority. Her forgery is driven by financial pressure she cannot disclose — debts, or blackmail ([[blackmail]]) payments, or both. Labar's judgment is unexpectedly sympathetic:

> "I can understand a distracted woman in a moment of folly giving way to an impulse." [[012-054]]

## Role in the Novel

### The Forgery

The forged cheque on the Midland Bank is the thread that unravels the conspiracy. Mrs. Gertstein's forgery was "a silly thing to do because the forgery was bound to become known," yet she did it anyway — driven by pressures that Labar only gradually uncovers.

### Blackmail Victim

She has been paying for silence, making her both criminal and victim. Larry Hughes ([[larry-hughes]]) has exploited her vulnerability with precision, extracting payment while keeping her crime concealed from her husband.

### The Unraveling

When the forgery is discovered, Mrs. Gertstein must choose between continued silence and the truth. Her decision shapes the novel's resolution.
