# Fingerprint

The fingerprint, as a method of criminal identification, occupies a curiously marginal position in *The Lazy Detective* — precisely because its absence from the Streetly House crime scene reveals as much as its presence would. The novel's criminals understand the science well enough to avoid leaving traces, and its invocation marks the boundary between the professional and the amateur.

## In the Novel

### Absence at the Crime Scene

The investigation of Streetly House yields no fingerprint evidence whatsoever. Bill Malone reports with characteristic frankness:

> "Not so much as a blighting fingerprint." [[002-015]]

This complete absence is itself significant — it signals professional criminals who understand forensic methods and work with deliberate care.

### The Yard's Scientific Arsenal

The novel acknowledges the growing role of scientific detection at Scotland Yard, even as it notes its limitations in this case:

> No fingerprints, no trivialities from which a high-domed scientist in an easy chair might deduce the entire crime. [[004-033]]

The tone is sardonic — Labar is a detective of the old school, who relies on psychology and pressure rather than laboratory analysis.

### Fingerprints as Identity

Fingerprints serve as a marker of criminal identity and history. A character's criminal past is revealed through the fingerprint record:

> The hallmark of his failure was that his fingerprints were on record at Scotland Yard. [[006-007]]

In the novel's world, having fingerprints on file at the Yard is a permanent stigma — a record that can be consulted at any time, as Labar demonstrates:

> "Last night I sent your fingerprints to the Yard and had you looked up." [[018-015]]

The fingerprint becomes less a tool of detection than a symbol of the surveillance state — the Yard's bureaucratic memory that no criminal can escape.
