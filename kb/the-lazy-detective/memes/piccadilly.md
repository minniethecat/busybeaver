# Piccadilly

Piccadilly is a prominent London thoroughfare that appears at two significant points in the novel: first as the location of a Tube station used for covert communication within the criminal investigation, and later as the setting for a quiet celebratory drink between [[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]] and [[moreland|Moreland]] in the final chapter. The contrast between these two appearances — one furtive, one convivial — mirrors the arc of the narrative from conspiracy to resolution.

## In the Novel

### Covert Communication

During the investigation, Labar receives a cryptic message: "'Panjandrum. Urgent. All fixed for tonight. Keep Walloper straight, and inform. Have not seen him. Piccadilly Tube. Same place'"（[[006-006]]）. The Piccadilly Tube station serves as a dead-drop location — a public place where messages can be left or exchanged without the participants needing to meet directly. The use of a Tube station for clandestine communication is a hallmark of the novel's grounded, procedural approach to crime: the gang exploits London's public infrastructure rather than resorting to melodramatic secret meeting places.

### The Final Toast

In the novel's closing chapter, Labar and Moreland celebrate his promotion at "the little snuggery of a bar, known to a select few in one of the alleys off Piccadilly"（[[030-017]]）. Arm in arm, "the two departed for the threatened libation to Labar's promotion"（[[030-017]]）. The choice of venue — a quiet, discreet bar off the main thoroughfare rather than a crowded public house — befits two senior detectives who value privacy and restraint. The scene echoes Labar's earlier preference for the unobtrusive: just as he avoided attention in [[rye|Rye]], he seeks out a bar "known to a select few" to mark his professional triumph.

### London Geography

The reference to Chelsea at the novel's opening — Labar "setting his face towards Chelsea where his modest bachelor apartments were located"（[[004-041]]）— and Piccadilly at its close creates a geographical bracket around the narrative. From Chelsea in the west to Piccadilly in the centre, the London locations trace a modest, unglamorous circuit that reflects Labar's character: he is not a man of the grand West End clubs but of Tube stations, quiet bars, and unpretentious bachelor quarters.
