# Motive

Motive, the psychological reason behind a criminal act, is the framework through which Detective Inspector Labar interprets evidence in *The Lazy Detective*. In a case where physical clues ([[clue]]) are scarce, understanding motive becomes the primary investigative tool — determine why the crime was committed, and the how follows naturally.

## In the Novel

### The Professional Criminal's Motive

Labar understands that professional criminals operate on different motivational logic than amateurs. The jewel theft at Streetly House is not a crime of passion but a calculated operation:

> The men who could plan or carry out such an undertaking were few. [[002-016]]

The scale and precision of the theft point to a motive of pure profit — this is business, not revenge. The professional criminal's motive is transparent: money. It is the amateur whose actions require psychological interpretation.

### Financial Pressure as Motive

The forged cheque reveals a different kind of motive — not greed but desperation. Mrs. Gertstein's forgery is driven by financial pressure she cannot disclose to her husband. Labar recognizes the pattern:

> "She forged her husband's name to a cheque — a silly thing to do because the forgery was bound to become known. I can understand a distracted woman in a moment of folly giving way to an impulse." [[012-054]]

The detective's empathy is strategic — understanding the emotional logic of the crime helps him predict the criminal's next move.

### Blackmail as Compound Motive

Blackmail ([[blackmail]]) creates a chain of motives: the blackmailer is motivated by greed, the victim by fear, and the victim's desperate acts to raise money — forgery, theft, concealment — are all traceable back through this motivational chain. The novel suggests that the most complex crimes are driven not by a single motive but by a cascade.
