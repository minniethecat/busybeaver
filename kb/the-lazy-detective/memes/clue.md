# Clue

The clue is the basic currency of detection — a fragment of information that, properly interpreted, points toward the truth. In *The Lazy Detective*, Detective Inspector Labar demonstrates that the art of detection lies not in accumulating clues but in distinguishing the significant from the misleading.

## In the Novel

### The Scarcity of Clues

The Streetly House theft presents Labar with a paradox: a crime of enormous scale that leaves almost no physical trace. The absence of clues is itself the first clue — it tells Labar that he is dealing with professionals who understand forensic method:

> Here were no bloodstains, no fingerprints, no trivialities from which a high-domed scientist in an easy chair might deduce the entire crime. [[004-033]]

The professional criminal leaves nothing. The amateur leaves everything. The Streetly House thieves leave precisely the right amount — enough to suggest professionalism, too little to identify anyone.

### The Bribe as Clue

The hundred-pound note tossed from a passing car is not just an attempted bribe — it is a clue. Labar immediately grasps its significance:

> The most illiterate crook in London should have known that a hundred pound note was ridiculously easy to trace. [[001-026]]

The clue tells him the briber is not a professional criminal but someone desperate and amateurish — someone who does not understand how traceable money is. This deduction narrows the field dramatically.

### Following the Trail

Labar's method is to follow clues wherever they lead, without preconception:

> "May be. I'm not saying till I've looked into it." [[009-025]]

This reluctance to commit to a theory before the clues have spoken is the mark of the experienced detective. The clue leads; the detective follows.
