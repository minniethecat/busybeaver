# Solly Gertstein

Solly Gertstein is the ostensible victim in *The Lazy Detective* — a financier of considerable wealth whose collection of jewels and objets d'art, assembled over years and valued at a quarter-million pounds, is stolen from his residence at Streetly House ([[streetly-house]]). Yet as the investigation unfolds, it becomes clear that Gertstein is also a victim of crimes he did not know had been committed against him — and by people he trusted.

## Character

Gertstein is introduced as a figure of some physical comedy — "a rotund little man, with a gait that somehow suggested a milk can rolled by a railway porter" — but his pomposity masks genuine distress. His collection is not merely wealth to him but a life's work:

> "Insured! What is insurance to me? Do you think that I — Gertstein — want the money? That — poof — a fleabite. The insurance companies will pay, but will that help me to get back all my beautiful things?" [[002-005]]

## Role in the Novel

### The Victim

Gertstein's loss is material and psychological. The theft has violated his home and his sense of security. His outrage at the police — "For dis I pay my thousands a year to the rates for police salaries. What protection do I get for it? None" — is the cry of a man who has discovered that wealth cannot buy safety.

### The Unknowing Victim

As Labar's investigation proceeds, a deeper dimension of Gertstein's victimhood emerges. His wife has forged his signature on a cheque ([[forgery]]), driven by financial pressure she concealed from him. He has been blackmailed ([[blackmail]]) without his knowledge. The theft of his jewels is only the visible crime; the invisible crimes strike closer to home.
