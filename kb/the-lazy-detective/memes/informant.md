# Informant

In [[the-lazy-detective|The Lazy Detective]], the informant system is the hidden
nervous system of police work. [[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]]'s
investigative method depends on a network of anonymous sources whose information
shapes the direction of inquiry but whose identities must be protected — from
the criminals who would punish betrayal and from the courts that might demand
disclosure.

## The Anonymous Report

The informant enters the novel through the characteristic indirection of the
police report. A detective sergeant's memorandum about [[gold-dust-teddy|Gold Dust Teddy]]
contains the telltale parenthetical:

> Teddy, it appeared — one may observe the use of the informant in the detective
> sergeant's report — had been on the water-wagon for some time. But a week ago
> he had broken out. [006-008]

The narrative voice intrudes to draw attention to the informant's presence — "one
may observe the use of the informant" — as though teaching the reader how to read
a police report. The informant is not named, not described, not credited. Their
information is presented as objective fact while their existence is marked only
by the passive construction: "it appeared."

This is the informant's paradox: their information is essential, but their
identity is unspeakable. The police report must record what the informant said
without recording who the informant is.

## The Motive Problem

Every informant has a reason for informing, and the reason shapes the reliability
of the information. The novel's informants operate from a spectrum of motives:
money, fear, revenge, the desire to eliminate a rival. Labar's skill lies not
merely in receiving information but in weighing it — understanding that an
informant who is also a rival gang member may provide accurate intelligence about
a competitor while concealing their own activities.

The "inevitable string of reports" that awaits Labar at Grape Street [006-002]
represents the daily harvest of the informant system — a mixture of gold and
dross that must be sifted by experienced judgment. Not every report is true;
not every informant is reliable. The detective's art is discrimination.

## Surveillance and the Informant

The informant and [[surveillance|surveillance]] are complementary intelligence
sources. [[gold-dust-teddy|Gold Dust Teddy]], released rather than charged, becomes
an unwitting informant — his movements tracked by "Down and Heath, both ambitious
young officers, camping on his trail" [009-006]. He does not know he is informing;
his information is extracted by observation rather than interview. This blurring
of the line between informant and surveillance subject reflects the novel's
realism about police methods: the distinction between "source" and "target" is
often a matter of perspective.
