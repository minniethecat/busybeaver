# Bribery

Bribery, the offering of money or valuables to influence the conduct of a person in a position of trust, appears throughout *The Lazy Detective* as both a criminal tactic employed against law enforcement and a window into character. For Detective Inspector Labar, the bribe becomes a litmus test of professional integrity.

## In the Novel

### The Opening Bribe

The novel opens with Labar receiving a bribe in the most unconventional manner — a hundred-pound note in a blank envelope, tossed from a passing car by an unknown woman. Labar immediately recognizes it for what it is:

> It was a bribe of course. But the touch of melodrama was amateurish and clumsy. The most illiterate crook in London should have known that a hundred pound note was ridiculously easy to trace. [[001-026]]

Despite its amateurish execution, the bribe marks the beginning of Labar's entanglement with the case that will define his career.

### Penelope Noelson's Attempts

Penelope Noelson, a central figure in the Streetly House jewel theft, attempts to bribe Labar on multiple occasions. Labar confronts her directly about these attempts:

> "She only tried to bribe me yesterday. She only changed a forged cheque on the Midland Bank. She only tried to sandbag me last night." [[005-034]]

Later, when Labar places her in protective custody, she is warned that her usual methods will not work:

> "And I hope you will not waste your time trying to bribe or threaten her. We have known each other a long time, Sophie and I." [[010-008]]

This exchange establishes bribery as Penelope's default approach to obstacles, and Labar's immunity to it as a defining trait.

### Bribery as a Measure of Trust

Larry Hughes, the criminal mastermind, uses knowledge of bribery attempts as leverage. He tells Penelope what he claims to know:

> "I can't tell you what he knows, but what he asserted that he knew was that you had committed forgery, and that you tried first to bribe him, and then to knock him out." [[007-032]]

The bribery attempt, when revealed, becomes as damaging as the underlying crime — a tool for blackmail ([[blackmail]]) and control.
