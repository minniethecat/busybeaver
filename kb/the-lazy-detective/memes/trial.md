# Trial

The criminal trial is the destination toward which the entire investigation of *The Lazy Detective* moves — the forum where evidence ([[evidence]]) becomes proof, witnesses ([[witness]]) become testimony, and the detective's accumulated knowledge must withstand adversarial challenge. The novel culminates at the Old Bailey, the Central Criminal Court of England.

## In the Novel

### The Old Bailey

The novel's trial takes place at the Old Bailey, the historic seat of criminal justice in London. The setting carries symbolic weight — this is not a magistrate's hearing or a coroner's inquest but a full criminal trial before a jury, with Treasury Counsel conducting the prosecution:

> Labar took his stand in the witness box for examination at the hands of Treasury Counsel. [[030-073]]

The formality of the setting transforms Labar from investigator into witness — his entire case must be presented through the structured rituals of examination-in-chief and cross-examination.

### The Detective as Witness

Labar's appearance in the witness box is the novel's climactic moment. Everything he has discovered — the forged cheque, the blackmail, the jewel theft — must be presented as admissible evidence. The skills that served him in investigation — intuition, pressure, strategic ambiguity — are useless here. What matters now is what can be proved:

> His evidence began with the events of that morning when he had received the hundred-pound note. [[030-073]]

The trial imposes a narrative discipline on the detective's work. The story must be told in order, with each fact supported, each inference challenged.

### Justice and Its Limits

The trial represents the institutional endpoint of the detective's work, but the novel is aware of its limits. A trial can establish guilt; it cannot fully explain motive ([[motive]]). It can deliver a verdict; it cannot undo the damage that crime has caused. The trial closes the case but leaves the human questions open.
