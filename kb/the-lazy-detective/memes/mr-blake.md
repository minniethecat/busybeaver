# Mr. Blake

Mr. Blake is a name associated with a poste restante address in Bruges, Belgium, used as a communication drop point within the criminal network investigated by [[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]]. The name surfaces during [[stebbins|Stebbins]]'s interrogation, when he reveals that certain information was to be communicated to "a Mr. Blake at the poste restante at Bruges"（[[018-032]]）. The alias functions as a minor but telling detail of the novel's criminal infrastructure, illustrating how the gang maintained international communication channels beyond the reach of domestic police surveillance.

## Character

The identity behind the name "Mr. Blake" remains ambiguous. Labar recognises the inherent difficulty of tracing a name associated only with a poste restante address: "There was no likelihood that inquiries which would have to be undertaken about the 'Mr. Blake' of the Bruges poste restante would lead anywhere"（[[018-043]]）. This assessment — that a poste restante inquiry was a dead end — reflects the practical limitations faced by even experienced detectives when confronting criminal networks that operated across national borders. The name "Blake" may be an alias, a cut-out, or a real intermediary; the novel leaves this deliberately unresolved, treating Blake less as a character than as a node in the gang's communication topology.

## Role in the Novel

### Intelligence from Stebbins

The Blake reference emerges during the police interrogation of Stebbins. Labar had "hoped, but scarcely expected, more than he had got from Stebbins"（[[018-032]]）, and the Bruges poste restante detail represented one of the few concrete leads extracted. It confirms the international dimension of the criminal enterprise: the gang's network extended beyond London and the Romney Marshes into continental Europe, with Belgium serving as a communication relay.

### Symbol of Investigative Limits

Blake functions narratively as a symbol of the investigative horizon — the point beyond which police inquiry cannot practically proceed. Labar's frank acknowledgement that pursuing the Bruges lead would be fruitless demonstrates the calculated pragmatism that characterises his investigative method: resources are better spent on actionable leads closer to home. The Blake reference thus serves a dual purpose: it enriches the world-building by sketching the outlines of a wider criminal network, while simultaneously reinforcing the procedural realism of Labar's investigative constraints.
