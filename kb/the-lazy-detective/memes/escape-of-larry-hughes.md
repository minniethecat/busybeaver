# Escape of Larry Hughes

The escape of [[larry-hughes|Larry Hughes]] from [[mopes-bottom|Mope's Bottom]]
is a pivotal event in the closing chapters of [[the-lazy-detective|The Lazy Detective]].
When [[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]] frees himself and
turns the tables on his captors, Hughes — anticipating the collapse of his
position — flees through the secret tunnel with [[billy-bungey|Billy Bungey]],
triggering one final manhunt before justice is served.

## The Flight Through the Tunnel

The escape begins in the moments after Labar's ambush at the secret panel.
With the detective bound and under guard, Hughes and Bungey exit through
the tunnel to scout the marsh. Tom, another gang member, accompanies them
partway before being sent back:

> With an air of complete frankness he told of his excursion to the mouth of the tunnel, with his chief and Billy Bungey, and how he had been ordered to return while the two explored the immediate neighbourhood. He had, as Labar knew, gone back with one man but Larry had disappeared. [027-074]

Hughes's decision not to return is characteristic — he recognises the
situation has become untenable and acts decisively to preserve his freedom,
even at the cost of abandoning his confederates.

## The Manhunt

With the gang largely in custody but Hughes at large, the police face an
immediate operational question:

> The immediate question to be solved was whether Larry Hughes was still hiding on the marsh, or whether he had eluded the network of police and got safely away. [028-001]

A search of the marsh yields no immediate result. Labar, "satisfied, after
a couple of hours, that the likelihood of picking up Larry immediately was
remote," returns to Mope's Bottom to conduct a systematic search of the
tunnel and premises [028-004].

## Hughes's Account

When eventually recaptured, Hughes offers his own account of the escape.
His narrative reveals the role of local knowledge and happenstance:

> "Easy," said Larry, contemptuously. "By the time I got to the town every soul in it knew that there were happenings on the marsh. The police knew, and the tradesmen knew, that a detective down from London had started the affair. Rye isn't a big place and I know one or two of the tradesfolk." [030-007]

The escape's failure, paradoxically, is attributed not to police efficiency
but to the very visibility that made Hughes a successful criminal — in a
small town, news travels fast, and a stranger attracts attention.

## Aftermath

Hughes's capture completes the dismantling of his organisation. With
Bungey dead [029-013] and the remaining gang members in custody, the
escape and its resolution mark the definitive end of the criminal
conspiracy:

> The thing was nearly over. To scour out any of Larry's associates who had so far escaped would call for little effort. [029-021]
