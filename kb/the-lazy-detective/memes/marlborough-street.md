# Marlborough Street

Marlborough Street is a London thoroughfare whose police court appears in the novel as the venue for the formal legal processing of [[stebbins|Stebbins]] following his arrest. After apprehending Stebbins, [[detective-inspector-labar|Labar]] "betook himself to Marlborough Street where he had to give formal evidence of the arrest of Stebbins and asked for a remand"（[[018-046]]）. The court appearance is described as perfunctory — "the thing was over in a few minutes"（[[018-046]]）— reflecting the routinised nature of remand hearings within the novel's procedural realism.

## In the Novel

### The Remand Hearing

The sequence at Marlborough Street illustrates the bureaucratic machinery that follows an arrest. Stebbins, having been charged, "listened apathetically to the set official words in which he was accused"（[[018-041]]）. The phrase "set official words" conveys the formulaic character of the proceedings: the charge is read, the evidence of arrest is given, and the remand is requested — a sequence so standardised that it requires little more than the physical presence of the arresting officer and the accused.

### Procedural Geography

Marlborough Street forms part of the novel's procedural geography — the network of police stations, courts, and record offices through which the machinery of justice operates. Alongside [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]], the [[criminal-record-office|Criminal Record Office]], and the [[old-bailey|Old Bailey]], the Marlborough Street police court represents one node in a system that processes the arrested into the remanded and the remanded into the convicted. The street's brief appearance reinforces the novel's commitment to depicting the full institutional arc of a criminal investigation, from fieldwork to courtroom.
