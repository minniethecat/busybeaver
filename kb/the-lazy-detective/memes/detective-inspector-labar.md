# Detective Inspector Labar

Detective Inspector Labar is the central figure of *The Lazy Detective* — a divisional detective inspector at Scotland Yard ([[scotland-yard]]) whose defining characteristic, by his own admission and the judgment of his superiors, is laziness. Yet this laziness is revealed across the novel to be a kind of strategic efficiency: by refusing to waste effort on the routine, Labar reserves his formidable observational and deductive powers for what matters.

## Character

### The Lazy Detective

Labar's reputation is established in the novel's opening exchange. The Chief Constable does not mince words:

> "You're lazy — damned lazy. If things don't clear up in your division in the next month or so you can count on something happening." [[001-001]]

Labar's response is characteristic — smooth, unruffled, almost amused:

> "Thank you, sir," said the other, with the smooth suavity of a man who had received a compliment. [[001-002]]

He is under no illusions about himself:

> Divisional Detective Inspector Labar was under no illusions about himself. He was lazy. All Scotland Yard knew it. [[001-003]]

Yet the Chief Constable Winter, who knows him best, recognizes that even laziness has its uses:

> Yet that shrewd old veteran reckoned that even the quality of indolence had its uses. It could make a brilliant man concentrate fiercely on his work, in order to save time for his own purposes. [[001-003]]

### Method

Labar's investigative method is built on observation ([[observation]]) rather than systematic procedure. He notices what others miss, and his years of experience have given him an instinct for the significant detail. The novel presents him as a detective of the old school — reliant on psychology, pressure, and intuition rather than forensic science.

His signature move is the strategic interview — the conversation that seems casual but extracts precisely the information he needs. He is soft-spoken where others would bluster, patient where others would rush. His velvet manner conceals a razor mind.

## Role in the Novel

### The Streetly House Investigation

Labar is assigned to investigate the theft of a quarter-million pounds' worth of jewels and curios from Streetly House ([[streetly-house]]), the residence of financier Solly Gertstein ([[solly-gertstein]]). The case arrives at a critical moment — his superior has threatened his position, and failure could mean the end of his career.

### The Bribe

The investigation begins with a bribe — a hundred-pound note tossed from a passing car by an unknown woman. Labar immediately recognizes the amateurishness of the attempt, and the bribe becomes his first clue ([[clue]]):

> It was a bribe of course. But the touch of melodrama was amateurish and clumsy. [[001-026]]

### Unraveling the Web

Through patient investigation, Labar uncovers a web of forgery ([[forgery]]), blackmail ([[blackmail]]), and concealment. The jewel theft proves to be only the visible surface of deeper crimes, and Labar must navigate a landscape where victims and criminals are harder to distinguish than he first assumed.
