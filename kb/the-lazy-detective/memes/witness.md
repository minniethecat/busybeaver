# Witness

The witness is the linchpin of criminal justice in *The Lazy Detective* — in a world with scant physical evidence ([[evidence]]), who can testify, and what they will say, becomes the decisive question. Labar's investigation is shaped as much by the management of witnesses as by the discovery of facts.

## In the Novel

### The Alibi Witness

When confronted, suspects instinctively reach for the witness who can establish an alibi. The quickness of the response is itself revealing:

> "I can prove where I was all that night. I can bring witnesses." [[008-024]]

Labar's response is characteristically smooth and probing — he does not dismiss the claim but immediately examines its substance:

> "What kind of witnesses?" [[008-025]]

The experienced detective knows that not all witnesses are equal, and that the hastily assembled alibi is a staple of criminal defense.

### The Reluctant Witness

Labar's investigation is built on testimony from those who would prefer to remain silent. The challenge is not finding witnesses but persuading them:

> "I don't want to put you in the witness box unless you wish." [[023-036]]

This deference is strategic — a witness who feels coerced makes a poor impression. Labar must balance pressure with persuasion.

### The Crown's Witness

The novel culminates in a trial where Labar himself takes the stand:

> Labar took his stand in the witness box for examination at the hands of Treasury Counsel. [[030-073]]

The detective becomes the witness — his investigation, presented as testimony, must survive cross-examination. The moment transforms the entire preceding narrative into evidence.

### Witnesses and Intimidation

The novel acknowledges the reality of witness intimidation. The strategic question is whether to call a witness before the opposition can silence them:

> To express what I believe to be the important evidence of a vital witness. [[023-032]]

The adjective "vital" is carefully chosen — some witnesses are merely corroborative; others are indispensable.
