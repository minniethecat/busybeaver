# Disguise

Disguise, the deliberate concealment of identity through altered appearance, is one of the foundational tropes of detective fiction — yet in *The Lazy Detective*, it appears only glancingly. This near-absence is telling: the novel is set in a world where criminals conceal their actions, not their faces, and where the detective's challenge is to penetrate financial and social camouflage rather than physical disguise.

## In the Novel

### The Single Appearance

The novel's one reference to disguise is pointed. In a world of professional criminals who operate through intermediaries and shell identities, physical disguise is almost quaint — the tool of the amateur rather than the professional:

> The novel's criminal ecosystem is built on distance and indirection rather than transformation. The forgery, the blackmail, the bribe — these are methods of concealment that operate at the level of documents and relationships, not appearances.

### Social Disguise

If *The Lazy Detective* has a concept of disguise, it is social rather than physical. Characters assume false roles — the respectable wife who is a forger, the businessman who is a blackmailer, the lover who is a manipulator. These social disguises are more durable and more dangerous than any physical costume.

### The Detective's Own Disguise

Labar himself operates under a kind of disguise — the mask of laziness. His reputation for indolence conceals a sharp and relentless mind. The lazy detective is the novel's most successful disguise: it makes him consistently underestimated by both criminals and superiors.
