# Ypres Tower

Ypres Tower is a medieval fortification in [[rye|Rye]], East Sussex, that appears in the novel as a temporal landmark: [[detective-inspector-labar|Labar]] departs Rye past the tower at dawn, and returns past it almost exactly twenty-four hours later. The symmetry of these two passages frames the duration of the marshland manhunt — a single day and night of pursuit, capture, and return.

## In the Novel

### The Outward Journey

Labar sets out from Rye at dawn in the guise of a naturalist, "an unshaven man clad in a rough old suit of Harris tweeds, who might have been a tramp or a naturalist"（[[021-028]]）. His passage through the Ypres Tower marks his departure from the civility of the town into the desolation of the [[romney-marshes|Romney Marshes]].

### The Return

The chapter closes with Labar's return to Rye: "That picturesque town was beginning to stir as they passed through the Ypres Tower almost to the minute twenty-four hours after Labar had left it"（[[023-056]]）. The precision of the timing — "almost to the minute" — underscores the compressed intensity of the events that have transpired: a night crossing of the marsh, an escape, a pursuit, and a return, all contained within a single diurnal cycle. The tower, unchanged and impassive, stands as a silent witness to the human drama that has played out in the marshland beyond.

### A Landmark of Transition

The Ypres Tower functions in the novel as a threshold: on one side lies the ordered world of Rye with its cobbled streets and morning commerce; on the other, the formless, threatening expanse of the marsh. Labar's two passages through this threshold bracket the narrative's most physically demanding sequence, and the tower's medieval solidity contrasts with the shifting, treacherous ground of the marshland pursuit.
