# Robbery

In [[the-lazy-detective|The Lazy Detective]], robbery is the narrative engine. The theft
of jewels from [[streetly-house|Streetly House]] — the country residence of millionaire
[[solly-gertstein|Solly Gertstein]] — opens the novel and generates every subsequent
line of investigation. The crime was executed with professional precision: no forced
entry, no damaged locks, no trace of the perpetrators' method of ingress or egress
[002-014]. For [[detective-inspector-labar|Detective Inspector Labar]], the robbery
is not merely a case to be solved but a personal test — he is already "not in good
odour with the authorities" and the political pressure surrounding a millionaire's
loss sharpens the stakes [002-027].

## The Streetly House Robbery

The central crime of the novel is a jewel theft remarkable for its technical proficiency.
The absence of physical evidence at the scene marks it immediately as the work of skilled
professionals, not opportunists. Labar recognises the signature of organised criminal
enterprise:

> There are half a dozen men in London, another half a dozen in New York, three in
> Paris, a couple in Amsterdam, and a few more knocking about other capitals of the world,
> who run crime on the principle of big business; and these men do not do burglaries. [001-017]

The observation frames the robbery within a hierarchy of criminal sophistication —
the perpetrators are competent enough to leave no trace, yet the crime itself is
a "burglary," placing it below the highest tier of international criminal enterprise.

## The Ripple of Suspicion

The robbery sets off a chain reaction of accusation and counter-accusation.
[[mrs-gertstein|Mrs. Gertstein]] reacts with fury when Labar's questions imply
her involvement:

> She swept angrily to her feet. "You are insulting," she declared. "I can't
> misunderstand you. You suggest that I am mixed up in this robbery." [003-025]

Her indignation is performative — as the narrative unfolds, her connection to
[[larry-hughes|Larry Hughes]] and the web of [[blackmail]] surrounding her becomes
the true axis of the case. Labar's method is to let the accusations fly and observe
who flinches.

## Larry Hughes and the Daily Mail

[[larry-hughes|Larry Hughes]] receives the news of the robbery with studied
nonchalance. Propped on silk pillows with coffee and toast, he "lazily scanned
the Daily Mail with its account of the Streetly House robbery" [005-001]. When
Labar visits him, Hughes performs the role of amused bystander, laughing at the
suggestion that he might be involved:

> Larry Hughes flung back his head and laughed till exhaustion caused him to
> desist. "That's real funny. You don't look it I'll admit, but you must be one
> of those comic sleuths." [005-026]

The performance is too smooth. Labar's instinct that Hughes is central to the
robbery proves correct, though the path from suspicion to proof runs through
[[gold-dust-teddy|Gold Dust Teddy]], [[penelope-noelson|Penelope Noelson]], and
the fog-bound lanes of [[mopes-bottom|Mope's Bottom]].

## The Criminal Supply Chain

The robbery is not an isolated act but the visible node of a criminal supply chain.
[[gold-dust-teddy|Gold Dust Teddy]] — a suburban housebreaker — is identified as
one of the executors [006-007]. The jewels pass upward through layers of the
[[criminal-conspiracy|criminal conspiracy]]: from Teddy and his accomplices to
Larry Hughes to the shadowy receivers who convert stolen goods into untraceable
currency. Labar's strategy is to follow the chain upward rather than arrest at
the lowest rung:

> "Don't you worry about Teddy. We'll get our hooks on him when we want. I'm
> using him as bait." [008-042]
