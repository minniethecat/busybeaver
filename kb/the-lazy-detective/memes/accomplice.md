# Accomplice

The concept of accomplice liability runs through
[[the-lazy-detective|The Lazy Detective]] as a legal and moral thread.
[[larry-hughes|Larry Hughes]] structures his criminal operations to
maximise the distance between himself and the physical execution of
crimes, creating a hierarchy in which each participant bears a different
degree of culpability. The novel's depiction of this layered complicity
raises questions about responsibility, coercion, and the law's capacity
to distinguish between principal and accessory.

## The Hierarchy of Complicity

Hughes's organisation is structured in tiers, with each level insulated
from the next:

| Tier | Role | Example | Legal Exposure |
|------|------|---------|---------------|
| Principal | Mastermind | Larry Hughes | Highest |
| Enforcer | Direct operative | [[billy-bungey|Billy Bungey]] | High |
| Specialist | Skilled criminal | [[gold-dust-teddy|Gold Dust Teddy]] | High |
| Facilitator | Inside operative | Stebbins | Medium |
| Unwitting agent | Duped participant | [[penelope-noelson|Penelope Noelson]] | Low/ambiguous |

## Stebbins: The Reluctant Accomplice

Stebbins, the window-washer recruited by Bungey to work inside
[[streetly-house|Streetly House]], embodies the reluctant accomplice.
His economic precarity makes him vulnerable to recruitment, and his
limited knowledge of the wider conspiracy places him at the bottom
of the hierarchy. Yet legally, his actions — providing inside
information, facilitating access — make him an accomplice to the
robbery [018-028].

## Legal Doctrine

The novel's legal world reflects the common law doctrine of accomplice
liability: a person who aids, abets, counsels, or procures the commission
of an offence is liable to be tried and punished as a principal offender.
The doctrine is flexible enough to capture the full spectrum of
participation in the Hughes conspiracy, from Bungey's direct violence
to the more attenuated involvement of peripheral figures.

## Moral Complexity

The novel does not treat accomplice status as a simple binary. Characters
like Penelope Noelson occupy ambiguous territory — complicit in some
actions, coerced in others, and actively resistant in yet others. This
moral complexity is one of the novel's achievements, refusing to reduce
the question of guilt to a legal formula.
