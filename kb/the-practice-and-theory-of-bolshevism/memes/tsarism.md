# Tsarism

**Tsarism** — the autocratic system of government under the Romanov dynasty — was the political order that [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] overthrew and, in Bertrand Russell's analysis, partly reproduced. Russell examined the relationship between the old regime and the new not merely as a historical transition but as a revealing pattern: the methods the Bolsheviks used to suppress opposition and control information bore an uncomfortable resemblance to those of the Tsarist state they had replaced.

## The Tsarist Legacy

For centuries before the [[russian-revolution|Russian Revolution]], Russia was governed as an autocracy. The Tsar ruled by divine right, unconstrained by parliament, constitution, or popular consent. Political opposition was suppressed through a combination of censorship, secret police, exile, and execution. The Tsarist state was, Russell noted, "a more complete despotism" than any other in Europe.

The Bolsheviks defined themselves in opposition to this legacy. Their revolution promised to replace autocracy with popular sovereignty, secrecy with openness, and arbitrary rule with scientific administration. Russell, visiting Russia in 1920, was sensitive to the question of whether this promise had been fulfilled.

## Continuities Russell Observed

Russell identified several troubling continuities between Tsarist and Bolshevik governance:

**The secret police.** While Russell did not use the term "Cheka" extensively in his book, he noted the existence of an apparatus of political surveillance and repression that functioned outside the normal legal system — a direct inheritance from the Tsarist Okhrana.

**Censorship and propaganda.** The Tsarist regime had maintained strict control over the press and public expression. Russell observed that the Bolsheviks, despite their theoretical commitment to workers' control of the means of communication, had established a system of information control that was, in its effects, equally restrictive. The [[press-censorship|press]] was a state monopoly; dissent was suppressed (001-060).

**Centralisation of power.** Tsarism had concentrated all political authority in a single centre. Russell observed that the Bolsheviks, despite the formal federalism of the [[soviet-constitution|Soviet Constitution]], had reproduced this centralisation — and indeed intensified it through the discipline of the [[communist-party|Party]] (001-018).

## Russell's Interpretation

Russell did not argue that Bolshevism was identical to Tsarism — the differences were real and significant. The Bolsheviks had brought education to the masses, had promoted women's rights, and had broken the power of the landed aristocracy. But Russell insisted that the *form* of governance — the concentration of unchecked power, the suppression of dissent, the instrumentalisation of law — showed disturbing continuities with the autocratic past.

This analysis reflected Russell's broader thesis: that political means shape political ends, and that a [[revolution]] achieved through authoritarian methods would inevitably reproduce authoritarian structures, regardless of its theoretical commitments.
