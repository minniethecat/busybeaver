# Trade Unions

## Overview

Trade unions occupy a distinctive place in Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*. Unlike the other organizations he describes, unions appear in two sharply contrasting contexts: as instruments of state economic control in Soviet Russia, and as the legitimate vehicles of working-class advancement in Britain. This duality reflects Russell's broader argument that the Bolshevik method of achieving socialism through minority violence is inferior to the gradual, democratic approach represented by the British labour movement.

Russell's discussion of trade unions draws heavily on official Bolshevik documents, particularly the resolutions of the Ninth Party Congress (March-April 1920), which he quotes at length. These documents reveal a party attempting to harness union structures for purposes of industrial mobilization and labour discipline — purposes that fundamentally altered the traditional character of trade unionism.

## Role in the Bolshevik System

In Soviet Russia, trade unions had been stripped of their conventional function as independent representatives of workers' interests. The Ninth Congress of the Communist Party identified "one of the most urgent problems of Soviet Government and of the Trade Union organization" as the "firm, systematic and insistent" campaign against "labour desertion" (001-092) — the phenomenon of workers leaving their posts in search of better food or speculative opportunities. The language is revealing: unions were not to defend workers against the state but to assist the state in disciplining workers.

The Transport Trade Union exemplified the system's weaknesses. Party documents acknowledged "the weakness of the Transport Trade Union," attributing it partly to "the heterogeneity of the personnel of the railways" and partly to the fact that "the most class-conscious and best elements of the railway proletariat were at the various fronts of the civil war" (001-097). The Party's response was to advocate "exclusive and extraordinary measures (martial law, and so forth)" as "the only condition under which transport can be raised to its height" (001-098).

The Party also decreed that its organizations should "in every way assist the Trade Unions and the Labour Sections in registering all skilled workers with a view of employing them in the various branches of production with the same consistency and strictness as was done, and is being carried out at the present time, in relation to the commanding staff for army needs" (001-101). Russell presents this as evidence of "the mobilization of the industrial proletariat, compulsory labour service, militarization of production" (001-100) — a system in which unions served as instruments of conscription rather than defense of labour.

Yet even in this degraded role, Russell finds the Bolsheviks attempting to preserve some elements of union function. Party resolutions called for "rivalry between factories, regions, guilds, workshops, and individual workers" to become "the subject of careful organization and of close study on the side of the Trade Unions and the economic organs" (002-097) — a form of competitive emulation that, however distorted, gestured toward the traditional union role in workplace organization.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's discussion of trade unions is embedded in his larger argument about revolutionary method. The Soviet subordination of unions to state purposes illustrates what Russell sees as the inevitable consequence of minority revolution: the destruction of independent working-class institutions in favor of centralized state control. For Russell, the British trade union movement, for all its limitations, represented a preferable path — one that built working-class power through organization and negotiation rather than seizure of the state.

Russell's own political sympathies lay with the [[proletariat]]'s gradual advancement through democratic and trade union channels. His critique of the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] gains force from the contrast he draws between authentic working-class institutions like British unions and their Soviet counterparts, which had been transformed into administrative appendages of the Party-state. The Bolshevik claim to represent the proletariat was, in Russell's view, belied by the fate of the very institutions through which workers had historically organized themselves.

> Considering wide Trade Union assistance to the railway workers to be one of the principal tasks of the Party, and as the only condition under which transport can be raised to its height, the Congress at the same time recognizes the inflexible necessity of employing exclusive and extraordinary measures (martial law, and so forth). (001-098)

This passage captures the contradiction at the heart of Bolshevik labour policy: the Party called for trade union assistance while simultaneously resorting to martial law — a combination that, in Russell's analysis, exposed the hollowness of the regime's claim to represent working-class interests through working-class institutions.
