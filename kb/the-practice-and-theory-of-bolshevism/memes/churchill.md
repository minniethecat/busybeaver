# Winston Churchill

Winston Churchill (1874–1965) was the British Secretary of State for War and Air from 1919 to 1921, one of the most prominent advocates of Allied military intervention against the Bolshevik regime. Bertrand Russell invoked Churchill in his 1920 analysis of [[bolshevism|Bolshevik theory]] to argue that the [[bolshevik|Bolsheviks]]' own Marxian framework should have led them to expect exactly the kind of capitalist hostility Churchill represented.

## Anti-Bolshevik Interventionism

Churchill was, at the time of Russell's writing, the foremost British advocate of sustained military action against [[russia|Soviet Russia]]. Russell grouped Churchill with Georges Clemenceau and Alexandre Millerand as Entente leaders whose antagonism toward Bolshevism was entirely predictable — and, more pointedly, should have been predicted by Bolshevik theorists themselves. Russell wrote that "other men might have been excused for not foreseeing the attitude of Churchill, Clemenceau and Millerand; but Marxians could not be excused, since this attitude was in exact accord with their own formula" [002-087].

## Russell's Critique of Bolshevik Complaints

Russell's invocation of Churchill was part of a broader argument: that Bolshevik complaints [[About|about]] Western hostility were inconsistent with their own theoretical framework. According to Marxian [[materialism|economic determinism]], Russell argued, capitalist powers were bound to oppose a [[communism|communist]] state. He noted that the Bolsheviks "certainly did not expect" capitalist governments to be friendly, which made their grievances against Entente policy seem to Russell like a performative complaint [002-087]. Churchill's anti-Bolshevik stance was, in this reading, not an aberration but a confirmation of Marxian theory.

> Other men might have been excused for not foreseeing the attitude of Churchill, Clemenceau and Millerand; but Marxians could not be excused, since this attitude was in exact accord with their own formula. [002-087]
