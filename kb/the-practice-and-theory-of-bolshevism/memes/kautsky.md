# Karl Kautsky

**Karl Kautsky** (1854–1938) was the pre-eminent theoretician of German Social Democracy and orthodox [[marxism|Marxism]] in the generation before the [[world-war|World War]]. His critique of Bolshevik [[dictatorship|dictatorship]] — articulated most forcefully in his 1918 pamphlet *The Dictatorship of the Proletariat* — supplied Bertrand Russell with key arguments against Leninism from within the Marxist tradition.

## Kautsky vs. Lenin

Kautsky argued that the dictatorship of the proletariat, as conceived by Marx, referred to a class condition (the political dominance of the working class) rather than a specific institutional form (one-party rule). Lenin, Kautsky charged, had transformed Marx's concept into its opposite: a dictatorship exercised *over* the proletariat by a self-appointed vanguard. Russell found this argument persuasive and deployed it in his own critique of [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]].

## Russell's Use of Kautsky

Russell drew on Kautsky to establish a crucial point: that opposition to Bolshevism need not imply opposition to socialism. Kautsky was an orthodox Marxist arguing from within the tradition Lenin claimed to represent. His critique demonstrated that the Bolshevik path was a choice, not a necessity — one interpretation of Marxism among others, and, in Kautsky's view, a catastrophic misinterpretation.
