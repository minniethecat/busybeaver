# Allied Intervention in Russia

The **Allied Intervention in Russia** (1918–1920) was the military involvement of the Entente Powers — principally Britain, France, the United States, and Japan — in the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]] on the side of the anti-Bolshevik White forces.

Bertrand Russell devoted sustained critical attention to this intervention, arguing that it was both morally indefensible and politically counterproductive.

## Russell's Critique

Russell was unequivocal in his condemnation of the Allied intervention. He argued that the Allied Powers, having devastated their own countries in the [[world-war|World War]], had no moral standing to judge the Bolsheviks for the violence and disorder that accompanied revolution and civil war.

"People's reasons for disliking the Bolsheviks are very inadequate," Russell wrote. "It is said they have destroyed trade and industry — but so has every country involved in the World War" (001-073).

Russell identified several ways in which the intervention was counterproductive to its stated aims:

- **Strengthening Bolshevik legitimacy** — The intervention enabled the Bolsheviks to portray themselves as defenders of Russian sovereignty against foreign invasion, mobilising nationalist sentiment in support of the regime
- **Discrediting domestic opposition** — Anti-Bolshevik Russians who cooperated with the Allies were tainted as collaborators with foreign powers, undermining their political credibility
- **Justifying terror** — The external threat provided a rationale for the [[red-terror|Red Terror]] and the suppression of political opposition as matters of national security

## The Blockade Connection

Russell saw the [[allied-blockade|Allied blockade]] and the military intervention as two components of a single policy: the attempt to strangle Bolshevik Russia economically while supporting its military enemies. He argued that this policy inflicted immense suffering on the civilian population while failing to achieve any of its political objectives.
