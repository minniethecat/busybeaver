# Germany

**Germany** occupies a central but largely implicit role in Bertrand Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*. As the defeated power of the [[world-war|World War]], Germany was, in 1920, the country where Bolshevik-style revolution was simultaneously most anticipated by communists and most feared by the Western powers. Russell noted that "the rivalry between England and Germany had a great deal to do with causing the war" (002-011).

## The German Revolution

The German Revolution of 1918–1919, which overthrew the Kaiser and established the Weimar Republic, was deeply influenced by events in Russia. The German Communist Party (KPD), founded by [[karl-marx|Marx]]'s disciples Karl Liebknecht and Rosa Luxemburg, sought to replicate the Bolshevik model. The [[third-international|Third International]] directed much of its early energy toward Germany, believing that revolution in an advanced industrial country would transform the international balance of forces.

Russell, writing in 1920, was uncertain whether Germany would go the way of Russia. He noted the differences between the two countries — Germany's larger industrial working class, its stronger tradition of organised social democracy, its more developed civil society — and suggested that these differences might make a Bolshevik-style seizure of power both more difficult and less necessary.

## The Treaty of Versailles

The harsh terms of the Treaty of Versailles, which Russell condemned, created conditions in Germany that were conducive to both revolutionary and reactionary politics. Economic collapse, national humiliation, and political instability made the Weimar Republic vulnerable to extremism from both left and right. Russell's analysis anticipated — without predicting in detail — the polarisation that would ultimately destroy German democracy.

## Geopolitical Significance

For the Bolsheviks, Germany was the key to [[world-revolution|world revolution]]. If Germany went communist, the international isolation of Soviet Russia would be broken, and the industrial resources of Central Europe would be joined to the revolutionary project. If Germany remained capitalist — or worse, became a right-wing dictatorship — Bolshevik Russia would face a permanent threat on its western border.

Russell observed the asymmetry clearly, noting that the Bolsheviks were "doing everything to expose Germany to a Russian invasion of arms" while simultaneously fearing capitalist encirclement (001-128). His treatment of Germany reflected his broader internationalism: he saw the fate of Bolshevism not as a purely Russian question but as one intimately connected to the political evolution of Europe as a whole.
