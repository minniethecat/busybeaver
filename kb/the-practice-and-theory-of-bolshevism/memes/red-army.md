# Red Army

## Overview

The Red Army — formally the Workers' and Peasants' Red Army (RKKA) — was the military force established by the Bolshevik government in 1918 under the leadership of [[trotsky|Leon Trotsky]]. By the time of Russell's visit in 1920, it had become the principal instrument for prosecuting the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]], suppressing internal opposition, and projecting Soviet power into Asia. Russell encountered the Red Army in multiple contexts: as a fighting force, as performers of propaganda theater, and as enforcers of the regime's unpopular agricultural policies.

Russell's portrait of the Red Army reflects his ambivalence [[About|about]] its dual character. On one hand, it embodied genuine popular sentiment; on the other, it functioned as an instrument of state coercion that had departed from revolutionary ideals.

## Role in the Bolshevik System

The Red Army's primary military role was the defense of the [[revolution|Bolshevik regime]] against counter-revolutionary forces during the Russian Civil War. Russell witnessed a striking display of this at the [[moscow|Moscow]] Opera, where Trotsky called for "three cheers for our brave fellows at the front" and the audience responded with the same fervor "as a London audience would have responded in the autumn of 1914" (001-022). Russell recognized in this moment the emergence of nationalist sentiment around the Red Army — a development that he regarded with concern, observing that "experience of power is inevitably altering Communist theories" (001-022).

The Red Army also served as an instrument of domestic economic control. Russell reports that "when the food shortage was at its worst, the Government antagonized the peasants by forced requisitions, carried out with great harshness by the Red Army" (001-121). This use of military force against the peasantry was deeply unpopular and contributed to the alienation of the rural population from the Bolshevik regime. Russell notes that the peasants "still part unwillingly with their food, as is natural in view of the uselessness of paper and the enormously higher prices offered by private buyers" (001-121).

Beyond its coercive functions, the Red Army played a surprising cultural role. Russell describes "propaganda plays performed by the Red Army in its spare moments" (001-047), theater productions that served as vehicles for Bolshevik ideological instruction. He also witnessed a mass pageant depicting the formation of the Red Army "under its emblem the Red Star," with the army returning from the front in "triumphant review before the leaders of the Revolution" (001-054). These performances blended military celebration with religious imagery, casting the Red Army as the instrument of a providential historical process.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's evaluation of the Red Army reflects his broader concerns about the corrupting effects of militarized power on revolutionary ideals. He observes that the reconquest of Asiatic Russia had "revived what is essentially an imperialist way of feeling" (001-022) among Bolshevik leaders — a sentiment that contradicted the internationalist principles of [[communism]].

Russell also notes the strategic implications of the Red Army's strength, offering a blunt assessment of what truly constrained Western policy:

> Nothing produced a doubt in our governing minds as to the rightness of our policy except the strength of the Red Army and the fear of revolution in Asia. Is it surprising that professions of humanitarian feeling on the part of English people are somewhat coldly received in [[russia|Soviet Russia]]? (001-103)

In a passage that proved prescient, he writes that "it is only a question of a few years before India will be in touch with the Red Army" (001-128), warning that continued Western antagonism would accelerate Bolshevik expansion into Asia.

Russell's most telling observation concerns the Red Army's material privilege relative to other institutions. He reports that the Extraordinary Commission (Cheka) possessed regiments "who are better fed than the Red Army" (001-075) — a detail that underscores the regime's reliance on internal security forces even above its conventional military.

## Key Figures

- **Leon Trotsky** — Founder and commander of the Red Army, whom Russell met at the Moscow Opera and whose "military precision" he noted (001-022)
