# Petrograd

## Overview

Petrograd (formerly St. Petersburg) was the first city Russell encountered upon entering Soviet Russia in May 1920. Spending five days there before proceeding to Moscow, he found a city of immense architectural beauty now overlaid with the austere realities of revolutionary government. Petrograd was the cradle of the 1917 Revolution and remained symbolically important, though it lost capital status to Moscow in 1918. Russell's most vivid impression centred on the spectacular pageant of the "World Commune" -- a monumental piece of Bolshevik propaganda theatre performed at the opening of the Third International Congress.

## In Russell's Narrative

Russell arrived in Petrograd after being conveyed from the frontier "in a special train de luxe, covered with mottoes about the Social Revolution and the Proletariat of all lands." (001-011) He spent five days observing the city from both official and unofficial perspectives. (001-012)

The most detailed episode Russell records is the "World Commune" pageant, performed on the steps of the former Stock Exchange. He describes it as something not staged "since the days of the medieval mystery plays" -- a Communist mystery play "designed by the High Priests of the Communist faith to instruct the people." A densely packed audience of forty to fifty thousand watched as kings and courtiers danced before enslaved masses slowly climbed the steps carrying hammer, anvil, and stone. Russell found "the exquisite beauty of the slow movement of those dark figures aslant the broad flight of steps" unforgettable. (001-050)

Russell also met Maxim Gorky briefly in Petrograd, finding the writer "in bed, apparently very ill and obviously heartbroken." Gorky begged Russell "always to emphasize what Russia has suffered" and supported the government "not because he thinks it faultless, but because the possible alternatives are worse." (001-032)

## Role in Bolshevism

Petrograd was the birthplace of the Bolshevik Revolution -- the city where the October Revolution unfolded and where the Third International held its opening congress. The "World Commune" pageant embodied Bolshevik aspirations to universal revolution, presenting the Communist narrative of class struggle as public ritual. Yet Petrograd's political role had diminished since 1918; the transfer of government to Moscow left it a secondary centre. The food problem, Russell noted, was "mainly concentrated in Moscow and Petrograd," with workers forced to cultivate allotments after factory hours to supplement inadequate rations. (001-087) (001-118)

## Russell's Observations

Russell's observations blend aesthetic appreciation with social critique. The Stock Exchange pageant impressed him as a genuine artistic achievement, marrying Bolshevik propaganda with the Russian mastery of group movement inherited from ballet. On the practical side, Petrograd exemplified the infrastructure collapse afflicting Russian cities: frozen water-pipes forced inhabitants "for the most part, to go down to the river with pails and buckets." (001-088) Fuel and food supplies depended on broken transport networks that failed to bring timber from the countryside. (001-089) The contrast between the sublime pageant and the daily struggle for survival defined Russell's impression of Petrograd -- a city where revolutionary vision and material deprivation coexisted in uneasy tension.
