# Proletariat

## Definition

The proletariat, in Marxist theory, designates the class of industrial wage-labourers who own nothing but their labour-power and must sell it to the owners of capital in order to survive. As the productive class in capitalist society, the proletariat is at once exploited by the bourgeoisie and destined, by the logic of history, to become its grave-digger. In Bolshevik usage, the term carried strong normative force: to be "proletarian" was not merely a description of economic position but a mark of political virtue and revolutionary legitimacy.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell's encounter with the Bolshevik use of "proletariat" was marked by a notable discovery: the difference between how the term was understood by Western socialists and how it actually functioned in Soviet Russia. Western sympathizers thought of "the dictatorship of the proletariat as merely a new form of representative government, in which only working men and women have votes" (001-014). This, Russell found, was a fundamental misunderstanding.

> Friends of Russia here think of the dictatorship of the proletariat as merely a new form of representative government, in which only working men and women have votes, and the constituencies are partly occupational, not geographical. They think that "proletariat" means "proletariat." But it does not. It means the Communist Party. (001-014)

Russell reported that in practice, the dictatorship of the proletariat had become rule by a minority — the Bolshevik party — over the very proletariat in whose name it governed. The gap between theoretical justification and practical reality was one of Russell's central concerns.

Russell also observed the tension between the proletariat and the peasantry in Russia. When he asked whether it was possible "to establish Communism firmly and fully in a country containing such a large majority of peasants" (001-026), the answer acknowledged the difficulty. The dictatorship of the proletariat, in practice, became a dictatorship over the peasantry as well. Russell noted that Bolshevik leaders spoke of "propaganda among the poor peasants against the rich peasants" (001-027) and that Lenin acknowledged the peasants "are against us" (001-027) despite having more to eat than ever before.

The concept of the "World Proletariat" was a central element of Bolshevik propaganda. Russell witnessed a pageant in Petrograd in which the Red Army returned from defeating the White forces, and heralds "blew their golden trumpets and sang, foretelling victory for the proletariat" (001-053). The third act of this pageant, "Peaceful Construction," depicted "the victory of the World Proletariat" (001-054) as the culmination of history.

## Bolshevik Perspective

From the Bolshevik viewpoint, the proletariat was the sole legitimate agent of revolutionary change. The Communist International's rhetoric was saturated with appeals to proletarian solidarity: Russell's own journey into Soviet Russia was "in a special train de luxe, covered with mottoes about the Social Revolution and the Proletariat of all countries" (001-011), and the delegation was greeted with the "Internationale" at every stop.

The Bolsheviks regarded the mobilization of proletarian labour as essential to the survival of the revolution. Resolutions of the Ninth Party Congress called for "the mobilization of the industrial proletariat, compulsory labour service, militarization of production and the application of military detachments to economic needs" (001-100). The "class-conscious proletariat" — as distinct from the proletariat as a whole — was expected to show itself "particularly circumspect towards the survivals of national sentiment" (001-137) in colonized and oppressed nations.

Russell observed a practical paradox: the industrial proletariat was at once the mythic hero of the revolution and its principal conscript. The most "class-conscious and best elements of the railway proletariat" had been sent "to the various fronts of the civil war" (001-097), leaving the railways understaffed and contributing to the general breakdown of transport.

## Historical Context

The Russia of 1920 was overwhelmingly agrarian, with an industrial proletariat that constituted a small minority of the population. This demographic reality posed an acute problem for a regime that justified itself in the name of proletarian rule. Russell's conversations with Bolshevik leaders revealed their awareness of this contradiction, and their hope that industrialization and electrification — which Lenin estimated would "take ten years to complete" (001-026) — would eventually create the proletarian majority that Marxist theory presupposed.
