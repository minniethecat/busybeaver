# Kronstadt Rebellion

The **Kronstadt Rebellion** of March 1921 was a decisive turning point in the early history of Soviet Russia — a moment when the [[bolshevik|Bolshevik]] regime crushed an uprising by the very sailors who had been among its most ardent supporters in 1917. Russell regarded Kronstadt as the event that exposed, more clearly than any other, the gulf between Bolshevik theory and Bolshevik practice.

## Historical Context

The Kronstadt naval base, on an island in the Gulf of Finland, had been a bastion of revolutionary radicalism. Its sailors played a key role in both the February and October revolutions of 1917. By early 1921, however, the sailors' demands — free soviets, civil liberties, an end to forced grain requisitions — placed them in direct opposition to the Bolshevik government.

## Russell's Analysis

Though Russell published *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism* in 1920, before the Kronstadt uprising, the rebellion confirmed the central thesis of his book: that Bolshevik rule had degenerated into a dictatorship not of the proletariat but over the proletariat. The suppression of Kronstadt — carried out by [[trotsky|Trotsky]]'s Red Army with considerable loss of life — demonstrated that the Bolsheviks would not tolerate even socialist opposition to their monopoly of power.
