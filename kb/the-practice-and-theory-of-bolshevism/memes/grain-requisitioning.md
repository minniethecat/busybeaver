# Grain Requisitioning

**Grain requisitioning** (Russian: продразвёрстка, *prodrazvyorstka*) was the Bolshevik policy of forcibly seizing agricultural produce from the [[peasantry]] to supply the cities and the [[red-army|Red Army]]. Bertrand Russell, visiting Russia in 1920, observed the consequences of this policy firsthand: the breakdown of trust between town and country, the peasants' retreat into subsistence agriculture, and the emergence of a black market that the state could neither control nor suppress.

## The Policy

Grain requisitioning was the linchpin of [[war-communism|War Communism]]. The state set procurement targets for each region; armed detachments were sent into the countryside to seize grain; peasants who resisted or concealed their harvests were subject to punishment as "enemies of the people." In theory, the requisitions were paid for in increasingly worthless currency. In practice, they were confiscation.

The policy was driven by both ideology and necessity. Ideologically, the Bolsheviks regarded the peasantry's attachment to private property as a vestige of bourgeois consciousness that the revolution must overcome. Practically, the cities were starving and the Red Army needed food to fight the [[russian-civil-war|civil war]]. Without grain requisitioning, the Bolshevik regime could not survive.

## Russell's Observations

Russell documented the human consequences of grain requisitioning with characteristic attention to lived experience. He noted the contrast between the relative food availability in the countryside — where peasants could eat what they grew — and the desperate hunger of the cities, where workers depended on state rations that were consistently inadequate (001-074).

More profoundly, Russell observed the social and psychological effects of the policy. Grain requisitioning had transformed the relationship between the Bolshevik state and the peasantry into one of permanent warfare. The peasants, who had initially supported the revolution because it gave them land, now saw the Bolsheviks as a new form of oppressor — a regime that gave with one hand (land) and took with the other (grain).

## Consequences

Russell's analysis identified grain requisitioning as the policy that most clearly exposed the contradiction at the heart of Bolshevism: the conflict between the Party's claim to represent the masses and its willingness to coerce those masses when they resisted Party policy. A government that sent armed detachments to seize food from peasants could not claim to be the government of the peasants.

The policy was abandoned in 1921, shortly after Russell's book was published, in favour of a tax in kind — a tacit admission that requisitioning had failed. The damage, however, had been done. The breach between the Bolshevik state and the peasantry, opened by grain requisitioning, would never fully heal, and would culminate in the catastrophe of collectivisation under Stalin.
