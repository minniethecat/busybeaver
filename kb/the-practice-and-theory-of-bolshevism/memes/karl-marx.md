## Biographical Sketch

Karl Marx was born in Trier, Prussia, in 1818. After studying law and philosophy at the universities of Bonn and Berlin, he turned to journalism and radical politics. Expelled from Prussia and then France, he settled in London in 1849, where he spent the remainder of his life researching and writing. Together with Friedrich Engels, he authored *The Communist Manifesto* (1848) and developed the theoretical system known as Marxism. His magnum opus, *Das Kapital*, analyzed the capitalist mode of production and predicted its inevitable collapse. He died in London in 1883, decades before the [[russian-revolution|Russian Revolution]] that would claim his intellectual legacy.

## Role in Bolshevism

Marx is the foundational figure of [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]], though he died more than three decades before the [[bolshevik|Bolshevik Party]] existed. Russell treats Marx as the intellectual progenitor whose doctrines were elevated by the Bolsheviks to the status of "inspired scriptures" (P01-007). For [[lenin|Lenin]] and his followers, Marx's writings possessed quasi-religious authority: "When Lenin wishes to prove some proposition, he does so, if possible, by quoting texts from Marx and Engels" (P01-007).

Russell distinguishes Marx sharply from earlier revolutionary thinkers. In the Preface, he contrasts Marx with Rousseau, the intellectual father of the French Revolution: Rousseau was "sentimental and soft, appealing to emotion, obliterating sharp outlines," while Marx was "systematic like Hegel, full of hard intellectual content, appealing to historic necessity and the technical development of industry, suggesting a view of human beings as puppets in the grip of omnipotent material forces" (P01-001). This "hard intellectual content" is precisely what gave Bolshevism its distinctive character — a combination of revolutionary fervour with the inexorable logic of historical determinism.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell engages with Marx primarily as a thinker rather than a personality, and his assessment runs through the length of Part II, which is devoted to Bolshevik theory. He identifies two distinct Marxian doctrines: the materialist conception of history (or historical materialism) and philosophic materialism. Russell is careful to separate these:

> The materialistic conception of history, as it is called, is due to Marx, and underlies the whole Communist philosophy. ... The name does not convey at all accurately what is meant by the theory. It means that all the mass-phenomena of history are determined by economic motives. This view has no essential connection with materialism in the philosophic sense.
> （002-002）

Russell's critique is methodical. He argues that philosophic materialism "does not prove that economic causes are fundamental in politics" (002-003), noting that climate theories (Buckle) and Freudian psychology are equally compatible with materialism. More fundamentally, he objects to the dogmatic character Marxism acquires when a political theory is "based upon a philosophical doctrine": "it has the fixed certainty of Catholic theology, not the changing fluidity and sceptical practicality of modern science" (002-007).

### Russell's Four Objections to Marxian Doctrine

| Objection | Target | Russell's Argument |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| **Philosophical** | Materialism | Economic determinism doesn't follow from philosophic materialism; other causal theories are equally compatible (002-003) |
| **Psychological** | Rational self-interest | Marx inherited 18th-century rationalist psychology; overlooks vanity, rivalry, and love of power as motives (002-014) |
| **Dogmatic** | Certainty | Marxism has "the fixed certainty of Catholic theology" — immune to empirical challenge (002-007) |
| **Historical** | Inevitability | Marx taught Communism as "fatally predestined" — yet the revolution came in Russia, not in industrially advanced nations (001-019) |

The core of Russell's psychological critique is that Marx, inheriting "eighteenth-century rationalist psychology from the British orthodox economists," assumed "self-enrichment seemed the natural aim of a man's political actions" (002-014). Russell argues this psychology is too narrow — it overlooks vanity, rivalry, and love of power, which are at least as potent as acquisitiveness in driving political behaviour. "Marxians never sufficiently recognize that love of power is quite as strong a motive" as economic self-interest (002-034).

Nevertheless, Russell does not reject Marx wholesale. The materialist conception of history, taken as a partial truth rather than a total explanation, illuminates genuine causal connections. Russell's objection is to the totalizing ambition of Marxian theory — its claim to explain everything and its intolerance of doubt.

## Key Ideas / Contributions

Marx's contributions centre on three interrelated doctrines:

1. **Historical materialism** — The view that "all the mass-phenomena of history are determined by economic motives" (002-002); specifically, the mode of production determines the general character of social, political, and intellectual life
2. **Theory of class struggle** — History as the history of class conflicts, culminating in the struggle between bourgeoisie and [[proletariat]]
3. **Theory of surplus value** — Capitalists extract surplus labour from workers, creating exploitation resolvable only by abolishing private property

Russell focuses primarily on the first of these, and on the psychological assumptions underlying all three. He also notes the internationalist ambition of Marx's thought: "The revolution foretold by Marx, which is to abolish [[capitalism]] throughout the world, happened to begin in Russia, though Marxian theory would seem to demand that it should begin in America" (001-019). This observation captures the ironic gap between Marx's theoretical predictions and the historical reality of the Russian Revolution.

## Relationships

Marx's relationship with Engels was foundational: Russell notes that Lenin quoted "texts from Marx and Engels" as joint authorities (P01-007). His intellectual lineage runs through the entire Bolshevik leadership — **[[lenin|Vladimir Lenin]]** was Marx's most consequential disciple, adapting Marxian theory to Russian conditions. **[[trotsky|Leon Trotsky]]** developed the theory of permanent revolution as an extension of Marxian internationalism. The relationship between Marx's theory and Bolshevik practice is the central theme of Russell's book: [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] claimed Marx's authority, but Russell questions whether the practice was faithful to the theory, and whether the theory itself was adequate.
