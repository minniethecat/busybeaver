# Lev Kamenev

Lev Kamenev (1883–1936) was a prominent Bolshevik leader who served as President of the Moscow Soviet and was a close associate of Lenin. During his 1920 visit to Soviet Russia, Bertrand Russell spent significant time with Kamenev, gaining firsthand insight into the workings of Soviet municipal government.

## Role in Bolshevism

As President of the Moscow Soviet, Kamenev was responsible for the day-to-day administration of the capital city's Soviet apparatus. Russell placed him among the key Bolshevik leaders he encountered, noting that he "spent a night in the country with Kamenev" alongside meetings with Lenin and Trotsky [001-012]. Kamenev's role was administrative rather than ideological — he managed the practical operation of Soviet governance at the municipal level.

The Moscow Soviet under Kamenev's presidency represented the institutional backbone of Bolshevik rule in the capital. Russell observed that the Soviet system, unlike parliamentary democracies, concentrated power in a single body that combined legislative and executive functions. This institutional design reflected the Bolshevik theory that the "dictatorship of the proletariat" required undivided authority — a principle Russell critiqued at length [001-014]. Kamenev, as the administrative head of this system in Moscow, embodied the practical implementation of Bolshevik political theory.

## The Soviet Recall System

Kamenev provided Russell with detailed information about the Soviet electoral recall mechanism. As President of the Moscow Soviet, he explained that recall was "very frequently employed" — in Moscow, there were on average thirty recalls per month. He cited four principal reasons for recall: drinking, going to the front, change of politics on the part of the electors, and failure to make a report to the electors once a fortnight [001-070].

> Kamenev, the President of the Moscow Soviet, informed us that the recall is very frequently employed; he said that in Moscow there are, on an average, thirty recalls a month. I asked him what were the principal reasons for the recall, and he mentioned four: drinking, going to the front (and being, therefore, incapable of performing the duties), change of politics on the part of the electors, and failure to make a report to the electors once a fortnight, which all members of the Soviet are expected to do. [001-070]

Russell noted that while the recall system "affords opportunities for governmental pressure," he had no opportunity to determine whether it was actually used for that purpose. The transparency of the reasons Kamenev cited — particularly drinking and failure to report — suggested a system that was at least partially grounded in practical accountability. Russell's analysis of similar institutional mechanisms led him to observe that "the practice, so far as I could discover, does not differ greatly from the practice under parliamentary government" [001-016].

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's encounter with Kamenev formed part of a broader effort to understand the Bolshevik leadership beyond Lenin and Trotsky. He described his meetings: "I had an hour's talk with Lenin, virtually tête-à-tête; I met Trotsky, though only in company; I spent a night in the country with Kamenev; and I saw a great deal of other men who, though less known outside Russia, are of considerable importance in the Government" [001-012].

The extended time Russell spent with Kamenev — a full night in the country — distinguished this encounter from his brief, formal meetings with Lenin and Trotsky. This format allowed for more sustained observation of a Bolshevik administrator in a less guarded setting. Russell's willingness to include Kamenev alongside Lenin and Trotsky in his account indicates that he regarded these administrative figures as essential to understanding how Bolshevik rule actually operated, as distinct from how it was theorised. Russell was particularly interested in the contrast between the "fanatical faith by which the pure Marxians are upheld" [001-032] and the practical governance challenges that administrators like Kamenev faced daily.
