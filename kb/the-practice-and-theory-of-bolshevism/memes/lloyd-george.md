# David Lloyd George

**David Lloyd George** (1863–1945) was the British Prime Minister during the period of Bertrand Russell's visit to Russia and the publication of *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*. While Russell did not discuss Lloyd George extensively by name, the Prime Minister's cautious pragmatism on Russia formed the political context for Russell's intervention in the British debate over Bolshevism.

## Lloyd George's Russia Policy

Lloyd George occupied a middle position in the British debate over Russia. On one side, [[churchill|Winston Churchill]] demanded all-out military intervention to crush Bolshevism. On the other, elements of the Labour movement urged recognition of and cooperation with the Bolshevik government. Lloyd George steered a pragmatic course: maintaining the [[allied-blockade|blockade]] and providing limited support to White forces while also exploring the possibility of trade relations and resisting Churchill's demands for full-scale war.

Russell was closer to the Labour position than to either Lloyd George's centrism or Churchill's interventionism, but he recognised that Lloyd George's pragmatism — however unsatisfactory from a moral standpoint — was preferable to the Churchillian alternative. The Prime Minister's resistance to full-scale intervention had, Russell acknowledged, prevented an even greater catastrophe.

## Russell's Delegation

Russell's visit to Russia in 1920 was undertaken as part of an official Labour Party delegation, and the delegation's report was intended to inform British policy. Lloyd George's government had permitted the visit, reflecting the Prime Minister's willingness — however limited — to allow alternative sources of information about conditions in Russia to reach the British public. Russell's book, published upon his return, was part of this broader effort to shape British opinion and policy.

## Historical Significance

Lloyd George's Russia policy represented the dilemma faced by all Western statesmen in the early years of Bolshevik rule: how to respond to a regime that was simultaneously a military threat (through its support for [[world-revolution|world revolution]]), an economic opportunity (through the possibility of trade), and a moral challenge (through its claim to represent the interests of the working class). Lloyd George's pragmatism — neither recognition nor crusade — would characterise Western policy toward the Soviet Union for years to come.
