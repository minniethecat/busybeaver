# Internationalism

## Definition

Internationalism, in Russell's analysis of Bolshevism, refers to the Communist doctrine that the revolution must be worldwide in scope and that national loyalties must be subordinated to the interests of the international proletariat. It encompasses both the theoretical commitment — rooted in Marx's vision of a class struggle transcending national boundaries — and the organizational expression of that commitment in the Third International (Comintern), founded in 1919 to coordinate revolutionary activity across the globe.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell presents the Communist theory of international affairs in succinct and critical terms. "The Communist theory of international affairs is exceedingly simple. The revolution foretold by Marx, which is to abolish capitalism throughout the world, happened to begin in Russia, though Marxian theory would seem to demand that it should begin in America" (001-019). According to this theory, the duty of Communists in countries where revolution has not yet broken out is solely to hasten its advent. Agreements with capitalist states "can only be makeshifts, and can never amount on either side to a sincere peace" (001-019). Russell reports Lenin's view — startling to British Labour sensibilities — that he hopes to see a Labour Government in England "solely in order that the futility of Parliamentarism may be conclusively demonstrated to the British working man" (001-019).

Russell acknowledges, with a measure of respect, that "the true Communist is thoroughly international. Lenin, for example, so far as I could judge, is not more concerned with the interests of Russia than with those of other countries" (001-021). Lenin, Russell believes, would "sacrifice Russia rather than the revolution, if the alternative should ever arise" (001-021). This genuine internationalism is one of the features of Bolshevism that Russell finds most admirable.

But Russell's analysis is dominated by his concern that this internationalism is being undermined by the very successes of the Bolshevik state. "Nationalism is natural and instinctive; through pride in the revolution, it grows again even in the breasts of Communists" (001-021). Through the Polish war, the Bolsheviks acquired "the support of national feeling, and their position in the country has been immensely strengthened" (001-021). Russell reports his observation of Trotsky at the Moscow Opera, calling for "three cheers for our brave fellows at the front" to an audience that "responded as a London audience would have responded in the autumn of 1914" (001-022). He concludes that "Trotsky and the Red Army undoubtedly now have behind them a great body of nationalist sentiment" (001-022).

This nationalist resurgence leads Russell to a dark prediction. He warns that "the reconquest of Asiatic Russia has even revived what is essentially an imperialist way of feeling" (001-022), and that "if the Bolsheviks remain in power, it is much to be feared that their Communism will fade, and that they will increasingly resemble any other Asiatic Government" (001-022). The internationalism that inspired the revolution, Russell fears, is being consumed by the logic of state power.

> The true Communist is thoroughly international. Lenin, for example, so far as I could judge, is not more concerned with the interests of Russia than with those of other countries; Russia is, at the moment, the protagonist of the social revolution, and, as such, valuable to the world, but Lenin would sacrifice Russia rather than the revolution, if the alternative should ever arise. (001-021)

Russell's broader philosophical critique centres on what he regards as the Marxian neglect of nationalism as an independent force. He identifies nationalism as "the most obvious non-economic factor, and the one the neglect of which has led Socialists most astray" (002-011). A nation, once formed, has economic interests, but "it is not, as a rule, economic motives that decide what group of human beings shall form a nation" (002-011). Throughout the war, wage-earners "allowed themselves to be governed by nationalist feeling, and ignored the traditional Communist exhortation: 'Workers of the world, unite'" (002-011). Russell regards this not as a failure of propaganda but as evidence that nationalism taps instincts that Marxian psychology fails to recognize.

Russell also anticipates the practical vulnerability of any single nation attempting Communist revolution in a capitalist world. He observes that "unless and until an international Communist revolution becomes possible, we must expect that any other nation following Russia's example will have to pay an even higher price than Russia has had to pay" (002-052). The implication is that the internationalism of the Bolsheviks is not merely an ideal — it is a practical necessity for survival.

## Bolshevik Perspective

The Bolsheviks, through the Third International, pursued a coordinated global strategy. Russell attended the Second Congress of the Comintern in July 1920 and quotes Lenin's theses on national and colonial questions, which argue that "all the events of world politics are inevitably concentrated round one centre of gravity: the struggle of the international bourgeoisie against the Soviet Republic" (001-131). For the Bolsheviks, internationalism was not a moral preference but a strategic imperative: the survival of the Soviet state depended upon revolution spreading to the advanced capitalist countries.

Russell notes, however, that Bolshevik internationalism was already being adapted to the exigencies of Russian state interest. He reports that the Bolsheviks' Asiatic policy — supporting nationalist movements in Persia and India — was "adopted as a move against the British Empire, and as a method of inducing the British Government to make peace" (001-138). The methods employed were not Communist proselytism but support for nationalist movements, since "the Persians and Hindus are considered scarcely ripe for the doctrines of Marx" (001-138). Russell regards this as a pragmatic but troubling departure from the purity of Communist internationalism.

## Historical Context

Russell wrote in the immediate aftermath of the First World War, which had shattered the Second International and exposed the fragility of working-class internationalism in the face of nationalist mobilization. The founding of the Third International in March 1919 represented an attempt to rebuild international socialist coordination on a more militant basis. Russell's account captures a transitional moment: the Bolsheviks still genuinely believed in the imminence of world revolution, yet the consolidation of the Soviet state was already generating the pressures toward nationalism and great-power politics that would characterize the Stalin era.
