# World Revolution

## Definition

World revolution, in Bolshevik theory as examined by Bertrand Russell in 1920, is the doctrine that the establishment of communism in a single country cannot be permanently secured without revolutions in other advanced capitalist nations. Russell observed that the Bolshevik leadership regarded world revolution not merely as a desirable goal but as an existential necessity: without it, the Soviet state would remain vulnerable to the combined hostility of the capitalist powers. Russell records that the Bolsheviks "looked to the raising of the blockade as the only radical cure; but he was not very hopeful of this being achieved thoroughly or permanently except through revolutions in other countries" (001-026).

## Russell's Critique

Russell mounted a sustained argument against the doctrine of world revolution, which he regarded as the most dangerous element of Bolshevik theory. His objection was not to socialist internationalism as such but to the method of achieving it through simultaneous violent upheaval.

> For these reasons chiefly, I cannot support any movement which aims at world revolution. The damage to civilization done by revolution in one country may be repaired by the influence of another in which there has been no revolution; but in a universal cataclysm civilization might go under for a thousand years. (001-020)

Russell's argument rested on a careful weighing of costs and benefits. He acknowledged the "whole of its indictment of bourgeois capitalism" (001-020) and accepted that capitalism was a deeply flawed system. But he maintained that "the evils of war, especially of civil war, are certain and very great; the gains to be achieved by victory are problematical. In the course of a desperate struggle, the heritage of civilization is likely to be lost" (001-020).

A further element of his critique concerned the effect of war on the revolutionaries themselves. Russell argued that "in order to succeed in war, a concentration of power is necessary, and from concentration of power the very same evils flow as from the capitalist concentration of wealth" (001-020). This was an ironic tragedy: the very methods required to achieve world revolution would reproduce the concentration of power that the revolution was intended to abolish.

## Bolshevik Conviction

Russell found among the Bolshevik leadership an intense commitment to world revolution that bordered on the absolute. He noted that Lenin was prepared to "sacrifice Russia rather than the revolution" (001-021) if the alternative ever arose, and that the Bolsheviks regarded trade with capitalist countries as "a mere palliative of doubtful value" (001-026) compared to the definitive solution of world revolution.

The Bolsheviks' conviction was rooted in a particular reading of Marxian theory. Marx had foretold a revolution that would "abolish capitalism throughout the world" (001-019), and the Bolsheviks saw themselves as the vanguard of this global transformation. Russell observed that "he believes that nothing of real value can be achieved except through world revolution and the abolition of capitalism" (001-026), capturing the absolute character of Bolshevik revolutionary faith.

Russell noted, however, that the Bolsheviks were not blind to practical realities. The Soviet leader he interviewed "admitted that two years ago neither he nor his colleagues thought they could survive against the hostility of the world" (001-028), attributing their survival to "the jealousies and divergent interests of the different capitalist nations; also to the power of Bolshevik propaganda" (001-028).

## Western Responsibility

Russell concluded his analysis of world revolution with an unexpected twist: he argued that the capitalist powers, far from preventing world revolution, were actively creating the conditions for it. He wrote that "while I cannot advocate world revolution, I cannot escape from the conclusion that the Governments of the leading capitalist countries are doing everything to bring it about" (001-020). He warned that "abuse of our power against Germany, Russia, and India (to say nothing of any other countries) may well bring about our downfall, and produce those very evils which the enemies of Bolshevism most dread" (001-020).

This was characteristic of Russell's nuanced position: he opposed world revolution as a Bolshevik programme while recognizing that Western governments were, through their own imperialist actions, making that programme more plausible.
