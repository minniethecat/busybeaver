# Cheka

The **Cheka** (the All-Russian Extraordinary Commission for Combating Counter-Revolution and Sabotage) was the Bolshevik secret police — and, in Bertrand Russell's analysis, the institution that most starkly illustrated the distance between Bolshevik ideals and Bolshevik practice.

## Origins and Powers

Established in December 1917 under the leadership of Felix Dzerzhinsky, the Cheka was granted extraordinary powers to combat "counter-revolutionary" activity. By the time Russell visited Russia in 1920, it had become, in his description, "a body practically independent of the Government, possessing its own regiments, who are better fed than the Red Army." (001-018)

Russell was particularly struck by the Cheka's capacity to act outside legal constraints. "This body has the power of imprisoning any man or woman without trial on such charges as speculation or counterrevolutionary activity. It has shot thousands without proper trial." (001-018) The absence of judicial process was not, in Russell's view, a temporary wartime measure but a structural feature of Bolshevik rule.

## Continuity with Tsarism

Russell observed a disturbing continuity between the Cheka and the Tsarist police it had supposedly replaced. Many Cheka personnel, he noted, were "largely inherited from the Tsarist times." (001-018) The methods of surveillance, denunciation, and arbitrary arrest that had characterised the Okhrana were now deployed in the service of a revolution that had promised to abolish political oppression. "Ordinary mortals live in terror of it," Russell wrote, capturing the atmosphere of fear the secret police created.

## Russell's Judgement

The Cheka represented, for Russell, the logical endpoint of the [[dictatorship|dictatorship of the proletariat]] as practised by the Bolsheviks — a system in which the party's claim to represent the working class justified the suppression of any working-class voice that dissented. The instrument that was supposed to defend the revolution had become, in Russell's analysis, the instrument that betrayed it.
