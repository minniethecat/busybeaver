# Democracy

**Democracy** — government by the people, typically through elected representatives — served in Bertrand Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism* as the principal standard against which he measured the Bolshevik regime. Russell's critique of Bolshevism was, at its core, a defence of democratic values against what he saw as their systematic repudiation by a party that claimed to represent the people while refusing to be accountable to them.

## Bolshevik Critique of Democracy

Russell took seriously the Bolsheviks' own theoretical critique of democracy. The Bolsheviks, following [[lenin|Lenin]], argued that parliamentary democracy was a sham — a device by which the [[bourgeoisie]] maintained its rule behind a facade of popular consent. In a capitalist society, they contended, the press, the schools, and the churches all served to manufacture consent for the existing order, making genuine democratic choice impossible.

Russell did not dismiss this critique. He acknowledged that Western democracies were flawed, that wealth conferred disproportionate political influence, and that the formal equality of the ballot box coexisted with profound economic inequality. "The case against democracy," he wrote, "is that it gives too much power to the ignorant and the selfish" (001-073).

## The Democratic Alternative

Yet Russell insisted that the Bolshevik alternative — the one-party state — was worse than the democratic imperfection it claimed to replace. His argument rested on several pillars:

**Epistemological humility.** Democracy, Russell argued, is not justified by the wisdom of the majority but by the recognition that no one possesses infallible knowledge. The Bolsheviks' certainty — their conviction that they had discovered the "laws" of history — was, for Russell, precisely the quality that made them dangerous. Democracy institutionalises doubt; Bolshevism institutionalises dogma.

**Accountability.** Under democracy, leaders can be removed without violence. Under Bolshevism, opposition is equated with counter-revolution, and the only mechanism for changing leadership is insurrection or purge. Russell saw this as a recipe for perpetual tyranny.

**Liberty as an intrinsic good.** Russell rejected the Bolshevik argument that liberty was merely a "bourgeois prejudice" that could be sacrificed for the sake of economic equality. He maintained that freedom of thought, speech, and association were goods in themselves, not merely instruments of class rule.

## Russell's Middle Position

Russell's position was distinctive in refusing both the Bolshevik dismissal of democracy and the uncritical celebration of existing Western systems. He sought a path that would remedy capitalism's injustices while preserving democracy's freedoms — a democratic [[socialism]] that was, in his words, "experimental, democratic, and respectful of individual liberty."
