# Nationalization

**Nationalization** — the transfer of private property into state ownership — was one of the central economic policies of the Bolshevik regime, enacted through a series of decrees in the months following the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]]. Bertrand Russell, visiting Russia in 1920, observed the consequences of comprehensive nationalization and offered a characteristically nuanced assessment: acknowledging the socialist principle while documenting the economic disruption that followed its implementation.

## The Nationalization Decrees

Within months of seizing power, the Bolsheviks had:
- Nationalised all land, abolishing private property in agriculture
- Taken banks into state ownership, repudiating all foreign debts
- Nationalised major industrial enterprises, transport, and shipping
- Municipalised urban housing, transferring it from private landlords to local Soviets

These measures were driven by both ideological commitment and practical necessity. The Bolsheviks believed that private ownership of the means of production was the foundation of [[capitalism|capitalist]] exploitation, and that its abolition was the first step toward [[communism]]. At the same time, the flight of capitalists, managers, and technicians in the wake of the revolution made state assumption of ownership a practical imperative — the factories would have closed regardless of who owned them.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell acknowledged the logic of nationalization while documenting its destructive consequences. The seizure of land from the nobility and its redistribution to the [[peasantry]] was popular — indeed, it was one of the Bolsheviks' most effective political moves. But the nationalization of industry, combined with the collapse of transport and the [[allied-blockade|blockade]], had produced not workers' control but bureaucratic paralysis.

Russell noted the irony: a revolution made in the name of the industrial [[proletariat]] had presided over the destruction of industry. By 1920, Russian industrial output had fallen to a fraction of its pre-war level, and the working class itself had been dispersed — many workers returning to the countryside in search of food. Nationalization, whatever its theoretical merits, had not produced economic revival.

## The Question of Management

Russell raised a question that would recur throughout the history of state socialism: who manages nationalized enterprises, and to whom are they accountable? The Bolsheviks' answer — that management would be exercised by the workers' state through its appointed officials — meant in practice that factory committees were subordinated to central economic authorities, and workers had no more control over their workplaces than they had under private ownership.

This observation fed Russell's broader argument: the abolition of private property, without the establishment of democratic control over the state that now owned everything, simply concentrated economic power in the hands of a new ruling group — the Party bureaucracy.
