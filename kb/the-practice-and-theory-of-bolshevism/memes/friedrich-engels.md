# Friedrich Engels

**Friedrich Engels** (1820–1895) was, with [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]], the co-founder of [[scientific-socialism|scientific socialism]]. Russell regarded Engels as the thinker who, more than Marx himself, was responsible for transforming Marxism from a method of historical analysis into a comprehensive philosophical system — with profound consequences for the development of [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]].

## Engels and Philosophic Materialism

Russell was particularly attentive to Engels's role in developing [[philosophic-materialism|dialectical materialism]] — the philosophical doctrine that became official Bolshevik orthodoxy. In works such as *Anti-Dühring* and *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific*, Engels extended Marx's historical analysis into a universal philosophy claiming to explain nature, history, and thought through the laws of dialectics. Russell, himself a trained philosopher, regarded these claims as philosophically unsound — a confusion of scientific method with metaphysical speculation.

## Russell's Critique

Russell argued that Engels's systematising impulse had lasting and damaging effects. By presenting Marxism as a complete, closed system — a "science" of history and nature — Engels created the intellectual conditions for the dogmatism Russell observed in Bolshevik Russia. A theory that claims to explain everything, Russell noted, can never be falsified by anything; it ceases to be science and becomes religion.
