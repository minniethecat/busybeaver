# Anarchism

**Anarchism** — the political philosophy advocating the abolition of the state and all forms of coercive hierarchy — appears in Bertrand Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism* as a significant but ultimately rejected alternative to [[bolshevism|Bolshevik]] authoritarianism. Russell, who was sympathetic to certain anarchist ideas particularly the critique of state power, discussed anarchism as representing a revolutionary tradition that prioritised liberty over order.

## Anarchism and Marxism

Russell noted the historical relationship between anarchism and [[marxism|Marxism]], which had been locked in conflict since the split in the First International between Marx and Bakunin. The anarchists shared the Marxists' critique of [[capitalism]] and their commitment to revolution, but they rejected the Marxian programme of seizing state power and using it to transform society.

For anarchists, the state was inherently oppressive — not merely an instrument of class rule but a structure of domination that would reproduce itself regardless of which class controlled it. The Bolsheviks' experience, Russell suggested, had vindicated this anarchist insight: a party that seized state power in the name of the [[proletariat]] had simply replaced one form of authoritarian rule with another (001-016).

## Russell's Sympathies

Russell's own political philosophy contained elements that overlapped with anarchist thought. His scepticism toward state power, his defence of individual [[liberty]], and his advocacy of decentralised and cooperative forms of social organisation all resonated with anarchist themes.

However, Russell did not fully endorse anarchism. He believed that some form of state authority was necessary to coordinate complex modern societies and to prevent the powerful from exploiting the weak. His preferred model — [[socialism|democratic socialism]] combined with strong protections for individual liberty — represented a middle path between Bolshevik statism and anarchist anti-statism.

Russell's most significant engagement with anarchist ideas in the book concerns the Russian anarchist movement itself, which the Bolsheviks suppressed after the revolution. The fate of Russian anarchists — imprisoned, exiled, or silenced by a regime that claimed to be building the conditions for the state's eventual "withering away" — exemplified for Russell the tragedy of a revolution that devoured its own most idealistic supporters.
