# Mensheviks

The **Mensheviks** (Russian: меньшевики, "minority") were the moderate faction of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party, emerging from the same 1903 split that produced the [[bolshevik|Bolsheviks]]. Bertrand Russell mentioned the Mensheviks in his 1920 study as representatives of the democratic socialist tradition that Bolshevism had defeated, suppressed, and — in Russell's view — traduced.

## Origins

The split between Bolsheviks and Mensheviks at the 1903 party congress was ostensibly over organisational questions: Lenin demanded a party of professional revolutionaries with strict discipline, while the Mensheviks, led by Julius Martov, favoured a broader, more inclusive party modelled on Western European social democracy. Underlying this organisational dispute were deeper differences about the nature of revolution in a predominantly peasant society like Russia.

## Democratic Socialism vs. Vanguardism

The Mensheviks adhered to the orthodox Marxian view that a bourgeois-democratic revolution must precede the socialist revolution, and that in a country like Russia — where the [[proletariat]] was a small minority — the working class should ally with liberal bourgeois forces rather than attempting to seize power on its own. They regarded Lenin's vanguard party and his theory of the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat|dictatorship of the proletariat]] as departures from genuine [[marxism|Marxism]] that would lead not to working-class rule but to dictatorship over the working class.

Russell, while not a Menshevik, shared some of their criticisms of Bolshevik authoritarianism. His own preference for democratic [[socialism]] over vanguardist revolution placed him closer to the Menshevik tradition than to [[lenin|Lenin]]'s Bolshevism — though Russell's critique went deeper than the Mensheviks' in questioning the Marxian framework itself.

## Suppression

After the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]], the Bolsheviks moved rapidly to suppress the Mensheviks as a political force. Menshevik newspapers were shut down, their leaders arrested or exiled, and their organisation declared counter-revolutionary. Russell noted this suppression as evidence of the Bolsheviks' "general hostility to opposition" — a hostility directed not only against the old regime but against socialist rivals whose critique came from within the revolutionary tradition (001-009).

The fate of the Mensheviks exemplified for Russell the tragedy of the Russian Revolution: a movement that had begun with the democratic aspirations of the entire socialist movement had devolved into a one-party dictatorship that could not tolerate even loyal opposition from its own side.
