# Anatoly Lunacharsky

Anatoly Lunacharsky (1875–1933) was the Bolshevik People's Commissar for [[education|Education]] (Narkompros) from 1917 to 1929. Bertrand Russell encountered his influence during his 1920 visit to [[russia|Soviet Russia]], where Lunacharsky's educational policies and writings provided evidence for Russell's concerns [[About|about]] the direction of Bolshevik cultural policy.

## Russell's Observations on Education Policy

Russell cited Lunacharsky's articles in the Geneva-based journal *Le Phare* as evidence that Bolshevik educational ideals were already being subordinated to the demands of [[industrialism|industrial development]]. He wrote that "the [[bolshevik|Bolsheviks]] already realize this is proved by the articles of Lunacharsky which recently appeared in Le Phare (Geneva)" [001-062]. Russell was examining the tension between the Bolsheviks' ambitious educational vision — which included Montessori methods and holistic child development — and the practical imperative to rapidly industrialize Soviet Russia.

## The Spectre of Industry

In Russell's analysis, Lunacharsky's writings revealed a fundamental dilemma facing the Bolshevik project: the conflict between humanistic education and the grinding demands of industrial progress. Russell worried that "the fine flower of useless scientific enquiry" and the "full and humane development of the child" might come to be seen as "a mere luxury and ultimately neglected" [001-062]. He feared that Bolshevik educational policy, as reflected through Lunacharsky's ministry, would increasingly subordinate broad intellectual development to narrow technical training — a process he described as the "logic of necessity" overwhelming revolutionary ideals [001-062].

> That the Bolsheviks already realize this is proved by the articles of Lunacharsky which recently appeared in Le Phare (Geneva). It was the spectre of industry that haunted me throughout the consideration of education as in the consideration of art. [001-062]
