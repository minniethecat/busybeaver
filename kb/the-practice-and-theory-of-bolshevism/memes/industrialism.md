# Industrialism

## Definition

Industrialism, as analyzed by Bertrand Russell in his 1920 study of Bolshevism, refers to the system of machine-based mass production that defines modern economic life. In Russell's usage, the term operates on two levels: first, as a material force that — according to the materialistic conception of history — fundamentally shapes ideas, beliefs, and social relations; second, as a concrete programme of industrial development that the Bolsheviks pursued through electrification and technical education. Russell treats industrialism as a double-edged phenomenon, capable of liberating humanity from want while simultaneously threatening to subordinate human values to the demands of the machine.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell acknowledges the explanatory power of industrialism as a material force. He grants that the materialistic conception of history has "a very large measure of truth" (002-008) and offers industrialism itself as a primary example. It is industrialism, he argues, "rather than the arguments of Darwinians and Biblical critics, that has led to the decay of religious belief in the urban working class" (002-008). At the same time, industrialism has paradoxically "revived religious belief among the rich" (002-008), as the possessing classes turned to traditional religion as a defence against revolutionary movements. This dual effect — secularizing the workers while driving the wealthy back to orthodoxy — illustrates for Russell the complex and often contradictory ways in which material forces shape consciousness.

Russell extends this analysis to other domains. The emancipation of women, he observes, was advanced not by the philosophical arguments of Plato, Mary Wollstonecraft, or John Stuart Mill — who "influenced only a few impotent idealists" (002-008) — but by the war-driven employment of women in industry, which made the case for suffrage "irresistible" (002-008). Traditional sexual morality collapsed, Russell argues, "because its whole basis was the economic dependence of women upon their fathers and husbands" (002-008). In each case, industrial transformation accomplished what rational argument alone could not.

Russell's deeper concern, however, is with the cultural and human cost of industrialism. He warns that "a fundamental economic reconstruction, bringing with it very far-reaching changes in ways of thinking and feeling, in philosophy and art and private relations, seems absolutely necessary if industrialism is to become the servant of man instead of his master" (P01-006). This formulation — industrialism as either servant or master — is central to his analysis. He fears that in Russia, the urgent need for industrial development will subordinate art, education, and intellectual freedom to the demands of production.

Russell specifically criticizes the psychological reductionism that he associates with the industrial conception of humanity. He observes that materialist theory "requires the assumption that every politically conscious person is governed by one single desire — the desire to increase his own share of commodities" (002-013). But human beings, Russell insists, "desire power, they desire satisfactions for their pride and their sense of independence" (002-013) — motives that industrial civilization may channel but cannot create. He calls for a psychology that recognizes the full range of human passions, arguing that "in the conquests of modern industrialism, the secondary passions have been no less important than the primary" (002-024).

> Treated as a practical approximation, not as an exact metaphysical law, the materialistic conception of history has a very large measure of truth. Take, as an instance of its truth, the influence of industrialism upon ideas. It is industrialism, rather than the arguments of Darwinians and Biblical critics, that has led to the decay of religious belief in the urban working class. (002-008)

## Bolshevik Perspective

The Bolsheviks regarded industrial development as both a technical necessity and a Marxist imperative. Russell records his conversation with Lenin, in which the Bolshevik leader acknowledged the difficulty of establishing Communism in a peasant-majority country and looked to "electrification in industry, which, he says, is a technical necessity in Russia, but will take ten years to complete" (001-026). Lenin's conviction was that the material base must be transformed before the social superstructure could follow, and electrification was the vehicle of that transformation.

Russell also quotes a resolution from the Party Congress that makes "technical education, and administrative and scientific technical experience" (001-085) matters of equal importance to political propaganda. The Bolsheviks understood that industrial competence was integral to their revolutionary programme, but Russell worried that this very emphasis on industrial development would, over time, erode the humanistic ideals that had inspired the revolution.

## Historical Context

Russia in 1920 was a country at a stage of economic development that Russell compared to "America in the pioneer days" (001-064). The vast majority of the population were peasants, literacy rates were extremely low, and industrial infrastructure was minimal by Western standards. The Bolshevik electrification programme — encapsulated in Lenin's famous slogan "Communism is Soviet power plus the electrification of the whole country" — represented an ambitious attempt to compress decades of industrial development into a single decade. Russell recognized the necessity of this programme while warning that its human costs would be severe. He feared that Russia would become "as inartistic and dull and uniform as an American city" (001-065) during the transition period, and that economic evolution might "obscure Communist ideals" (001-065) before they could be realized.

## Related Concepts

- **[[materialism]]** — The philosophical doctrine that, in its historical form, treats industrial and economic forces as the fundamental determinants of social life (002-002)
- **[[communism]]** — The political goal whose realization, Russell argues, depends upon the transformation of industrial conditions (001-026)
- **[[capitalism]]** — The economic system whose collapse, according to Marxian theory, is driven by the internal contradictions of industrial development (P01-005)
- **[[art]]** — The domain that Russell sees as threatened by the early stages of industrial development, which "everywhere seems doomed to be the enemy of beauty and instinctive life" (001-041)
- **[[education]]** — The sphere that Russell argues must become "subordinate to the needs of industrial development" (001-062) in Russia's present circumstances
