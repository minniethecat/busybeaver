# Woodrow Wilson

Woodrow Wilson (1856–1924) was the 28th President of the United States, serving from 1913 to 1921. In *The Practice and Theory of [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]]*, Bertrand Russell cited Wilson as an example of how democratic politicians routinely win elections on one set of promises and govern on another — a practice he contrasted with the [[bolshevik|Bolsheviks]]' unusual honesty in openly declaring their intentions.

## Russell's Analysis of Democratic Politics

Russell used Wilson's 1916 re-election as a pointed illustration of the gap between electoral promises and governing actions in Western democracies. He observed that "President Wilson, in 1916, won his majority on neutrality, and used it to come into [[world-war|the war]]" [002-038]. Russell placed this in a broader pattern: "The Unionists in 1900 won a majority on the Boer War, and used it to endow brewers and Church schools. The Liberals in 1906 won a majority on Chinese labour, and used it to cement the secret alliance with France and to make an alliance with Tsarist [[russia|Russia]]" [002-038].

## The Bolshevik Contrast

Russell's mention of Wilson served a specific analytical purpose: he argued that while the Bolsheviks were often condemned for their methods, they were in one respect more honest than Western democratic leaders. The Bolsheviks "have the honesty to proclaim in advance their intention of using power for other ends than those for which it was given them" — unlike democratic politicians who, Russell suggested, practiced the same method while publicly repudiating it [002-038]. This was not an endorsement of Bolshevik tactics but rather a critique of the hypocrisy Russell perceived in democratic political systems.

> President Wilson, in 1916, won his majority on neutrality, and used it to come into the war. This method is part of the stock-in-trade of democracy. But its success depends upon repudiating it until the moment comes to practise it. [002-038]
