# Dictatorship of the Proletariat

## Definition

The dictatorship of the proletariat, in orthodox Marxist theory, designates a transitional political form between the overthrow of capitalism and the establishment of a classless communist society. In this period, the working class — the proletariat — was to hold political power and use the instruments of the state to suppress the former ruling class, abolish private property in the means of production, and reorganize society. The dictatorship was conceived not as a permanent arrangement but as a necessary phase that would "wither away" once class antagonisms had been eliminated. Russell, however, found that in Bolshevik practice the dictatorship of the proletariat meant something quite different from what Western socialists imagined.

## Russell's Analysis

The gap between theory and practice in the dictatorship of the proletariat is one of Russell's central critical themes. He discovered upon arriving in Soviet Russia that while Western sympathizers imagined the dictatorship as "merely a new form of representative government, in which only working men and women have votes" (001-014), the reality was rule by a small, disciplined party.

> They think that "proletariat" means "proletariat." But it does not. It means the Communist Party. (001-014)

Russell argued that this substitution of party for class was not a temporary aberration but a structural feature of Bolshevik rule. He identified what he called a "minority dictatorship" (002-034) — rule by a committed revolutionary elite over a population that had not consented to the transformation being imposed upon it. The Bolsheviks "assume that those to whom it is addressed are absolutely certain that Communism is desirable, so certain that they are willing, if necessary, to force it upon an unwilling population at the point of the bayonet" (002-032).

Russell traced how the dictatorship, ostensibly temporary, acquired a logic of self-perpetuation. He noted that the Communist Party argument held that "after a longer or shorter period, according to circumstances, the nation will be converted to Communism, the relics of capitalist institutions will have been obliterated, and it will be possible to restore freedom" (002-047). Yet Russell saw no mechanism that would lead those in power to voluntarily relinquish it. He suggested that the more likely outcome was a regime resembling Napoleonic imperialism rather than genuine communism — a regime in which the Bolsheviks, having "lost their ideals" (P01-004), would simply become a new ruling class.

> I should deny that this is likely to be cured by the class-war and the dictatorship of the Communist party. Only peace and a long period of gradual improvement can bring it about. (002-072)

Russell drew a striking parallel between the Bolshevik dictatorship and the government of Cromwell. Both, he observed, "starting from a combination of democracy and religious faith, were led by force of circumstances into a military despotism exercised by a minority, employing terrorist methods, and everywhere obnoxious to the majority" (001-016). This historical precedent reinforced his skepticism about the Bolshevik claim that their dictatorship would be self-liquidating.

A further argument Russell advanced against dictatorship concerns the psychology of power. He argued that "habits of power intensify the passion of rivalry; therefore a State in which power is concentrated will, other things being equal, be more bellicose than one in which power is diffused" (002-029). This was an argument "against all forms of dictatorship" (002-029), not merely the Bolshevik variant.

## Bolshevik Perspective

The Bolsheviks regarded the dictatorship of the proletariat as both inevitable and indispensible. The Second Congress of the Communist International declared that "the present world-situation in politics places on the order of the day the dictatorship of the proletariat" and that "all the events of world politics are inevitably concentrated round one centre of gravity: the struggle of the international bourgeoisie against the Soviet Republic" (001-131). From this perspective, the dictatorship was a defensive necessity forced upon the proletariat by the implacable hostility of the capitalist powers.

Russell reports that the Bolsheviks envisioned a specific sequence: first, the seizure of power through armed revolution; then, a period of dictatorship during which resistance would be crushed and institutions transformed; and finally, the withering away of the state itself. In this vision, "the State will no longer be required, since the State is essentially an engine of power designed to give the victory" (002-047) to a particular class — and in a classless society, no such engine would be needed.

## Historical Context

Russell wrote during the Russian Civil War, when the Bolshevik dictatorship was at its most embattled and most repressive. The White Armies, supported by the Entente powers, threatened the very existence of the Soviet regime, and emergency measures that might otherwise have been relaxed were maintained and intensified. Russell acknowledged this context but insisted that even making allowances for war conditions, the structural tendencies of the dictatorship were alarming.

The dictatorship of the proletariat, as it functioned in Russia, represented what Russell called "the difficulty of answering" the question of whether fundamental economic reform could be achieved by any other method (002-064). His own answer was a cautious "no" to the Bolshevik method and a guarded hope for gradual, democratic transformation.
