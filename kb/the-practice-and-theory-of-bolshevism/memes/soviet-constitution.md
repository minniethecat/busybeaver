## Definition

The Soviet Constitution, as observed by Bertrand Russell in 1920, was the formal legal framework establishing the All-Russian Soviet as the supreme governing body of the Russian Soviet Republic. It prescribed a pyramid of elected soviets — from village meetings to the All-Russian Congress — that were constitutionally empowered to direct the state through the Council of People's Commissaries.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell's examination of the Soviet Constitution focused on the wide gulf between its formal provisions and the actual exercise of power. The constitution designated the All-Russian Soviet as the supreme authority, to which the People's Commissaries were nominally responsible. In practice, however, Russell found that this body "meets seldom, and has become increasingly formal," its sole function being "to ratify, without discussion, previous decisions of the Communist Party on matters (especially concerning foreign policy) upon which the constitution requires its decision" (001-072).

Russell approached Russia expecting to find "an interesting experiment in a new form of representative government" but concluded that what he witnessed was "not in representative government" at all (001-067). The elaborate electoral machinery — the recall, occupational constituencies, and the tiered soviet structure — was presented by Bolsheviks as a more perfect mechanism for registering the popular will. Yet Russell observed that the reality diverged sharply from the theory.

In his theoretical analysis of Bolshevik constitutionalism, Russell argued that the Communist dismissal of parliamentary methods was premised on a deeper assumption: that "those to whom it is addressed are absolutely certain that Communism is desirable, so certain that they are willing, if necessary, to force it upon an unwilling population at the point of the bayonet" (002-032). This contempt for constitutional methods, Russell warned, fostered a broader "disrespect for law and constitutional government" (002-041) that risked undermining the civilizational achievements of legal order — a value medieval thinkers had rightly placed "extraordinarily high" in the struggle against arbitrary power (002-042).
