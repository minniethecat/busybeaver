# Allied Blockade

The **Allied Blockade** of Soviet Russia was the economic embargo maintained by the Entente Powers (principally Britain, France, and the United States) against Bolshevik-controlled territory after the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]] and throughout the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]].

Bertrand Russell, writing in 1920, devoted sustained attention to the blockade as both a practical factor in Russia's economic collapse and a moral indictment of Allied policy.

## Russell's Observations

Russell was unequivocal in his condemnation of the blockade. He described arriving in Soviet Russia and immediately encountering the human consequences of economic isolation: hunger, disease, and the breakdown of industrial production.

The blockade, he argued, was not a surgical instrument aimed at the Bolshevik leadership but a blunt weapon that fell most heavily on the civilian population — particularly women, children, and the urban poor (001-054).

Russell noted the irony of the Allied position. The Entente Powers denounced Bolshevik [[terror]] and authoritarianism while simultaneously maintaining a policy that deepened the suffering of the Russian people and strengthened the Bolsheviks' domestic position by allowing them to portray Russia as a fortress besieged by foreign enemies.

## Political Effects

Russell argued that the blockade was not merely cruel but counterproductive. By isolating Russia from the outside world, it consolidated the Bolsheviks' political control in several ways:

**Strengthening the siege mentality.** The blockade enabled the Bolsheviks to present themselves as defenders of Russia against foreign aggression, mobilising nationalist sentiment in support of the regime (001-026).

**Eliminating moderate alternatives.** By cutting off contact with the West, the blockade starved the Bolsheviks' domestic opponents of resources and legitimacy, leaving Russians with no visible alternative to Bolshevik rule.

**Providing a convenient explanation for economic failure.** The Bolsheviks could attribute the catastrophic decline in living standards to the blockade rather than to their own economic policies, deflecting responsibility for the [[industrialism|failure of Russian industry]] (001-073).

## Moral Assessment

Russell's critique of the blockade formed part of his broader argument against the hypocrisy of Allied anti-Bolshevism. The Entente Powers, he noted, had themselves "destroyed trade and industry" during the [[world-war|World War]], yet they condemned the Bolsheviks for presiding over economic collapse.

The blockade, Russell argued, revealed that the Allies' opposition to Bolshevism was not rooted in humanitarian concern but in the defence of capitalist interests.

Russell's position was not that the Bolsheviks were blameless — his book is a sustained critique of Bolshevik methods and ideology — but that the Allies' policy of blockade was both morally indefensible and politically foolish, strengthening the very regime it was intended to weaken.
