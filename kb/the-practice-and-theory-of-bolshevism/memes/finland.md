# Finland

**Finland** entered Russell's analysis of Bolshevism as an instance of the centrifugal forces that the [[russian-revolution|Russian Revolution]] released across the former Russian Empire — and as an example of how socialist revolution could be defeated when the conditions that favoured the [[bolshevik|Bolsheviks]] in Russia were absent.

## Geopolitical Context

Finland had been an autonomous Grand Duchy within the Russian Empire. It declared independence in December 1917, shortly after the Bolshevik seizure of power. Almost immediately, the country descended into a civil war between the socialist Red Guards — supported, with limited effectiveness, by Bolshevik Russia — and the conservative White Guards, backed by Germany. The Whites won decisively by May 1918, and Finland remained an independent, non-communist state.

## Russell's Perspective

Russell regarded the Finnish case as significant for what it demonstrated about the limits of Bolshevik revolutionary strategy outside Russia. The Finnish Reds failed despite geographical proximity to Petrograd and some Bolshevik support, because the domestic conditions — a largely peasant population with a functioning national identity, the absence of a complete state collapse — did not replicate those that had permitted the Bolsheviks to succeed. This was, for Russell, further evidence against the Bolshevik claim that their revolution represented a universally applicable model.
