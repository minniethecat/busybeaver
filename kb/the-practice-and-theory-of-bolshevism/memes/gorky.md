# Maxim Gorky

Maxim Gorky (1868–1936) was a Russian writer and political activist who occupied a distinctive position in early Soviet society — sympathetic to the Bolshevik government while remaining independent enough to criticise its excesses. During Bertrand Russell's 1920 visit to Soviet Russia, Gorky was one of the most memorable figures he encountered.

## Role in Bolshevism

Gorky's role in the Bolshevik regime was neither that of a party functionary nor a formal oppositionist. He supported the government, as Russell noted, "not because he thinks it faultless, but because the possible alternatives are worse" [001-032]. This nuanced position allowed him to serve as an intermediary between the Bolshevik state and Russia's embattled intellectual class. Russell observed that "all the intellectuals whom I met — a class who have suffered terribly — expressed their gratitude to him for what he has done on their behalf" [001-032].

Russell placed Gorky alongside Lenin and Trotsky as one of the defining personalities of the Soviet regime, titling a chapter section "Lenin, Trotsky and Gorky" [001-023]. This grouping was significant: where Lenin represented ideological certainty and Trotsky military-organisational energy, Gorky stood for the humanitarian and cultural dimension of Russian life that the Revolution threatened to extinguish. His position reflected the tension between revolutionary necessity and cultural preservation that ran through the early Soviet period.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's assessment of Gorky was deeply personal, marking a sharp departure from his more analytical treatment of other Bolshevik figures. He described Gorky as "the most lovable, and to me the most sympathetic, of all the Russians I saw" [001-032]. Russell's language here — "lovable" and "sympathetic" — is notably warmer than his characterisations of Lenin ("implacable") or Trotsky ("Napoleonic").

> He supports the Government — as I should do, if I were a Russian — not because he thinks it faultless, but because the possible alternatives are worse. One felt in him a love of the Russian people which makes their present martyrdom almost unbearable, and prevents the fanatical faith by which the pure Marxians are upheld. [001-032]

Russell drew an implicit contrast between Gorky's "love of the Russian people" and the ideological devotion — "the fanatical faith by which the pure Marxians are upheld" — that characterised leaders like Lenin and Trotsky. This distinction was central to Russell's broader argument about Bolshevism: that its theory demanded a kind of ideological certainty that was incompatible with the human sympathies that Gorky embodied.

Gorky's failing health at the time of Russell's visit became, in Russell's prose, a metaphor for the condition of Russian culture itself. Russell wrote that he feared "that he was dying, and that, perhaps, it was dying too" — the second "it" referring to the intellectual and artistic life of Russia under Bolshevik rule [001-032].
