# War Communism

**War Communism** was the economic policy pursued by the Bolshevik regime during the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]] (1918–1921), characterised by the forced requisitioning of grain from the [[peasantry]], the nationalisation of industry, the centralisation of economic decision-making, and the effective abolition of market exchange. Bertrand Russell, visiting Russia in 1920 at the height of this policy, provided a firsthand account of its economic and human consequences.

## Features of War Communism

Russell documented several defining features of the Bolshevik economic system as he encountered it:

**Grain requisitioning.** The state seized agricultural produce from peasants, paying in increasingly worthless currency or simply confiscating. Russell observed that this policy had created a catastrophic breakdown in the relationship between town and country, with peasants reducing production and hiding grain rather than surrendering it to the state (001-074).

**Nationalisation of industry.** All significant industrial enterprises were taken into state ownership. Russell noted that this had not led to workers' control but to bureaucratic management, with factory committees subordinated to central economic authorities.

**Abolition of money.** The Bolsheviks initially attempted to move toward a moneyless economy, distributing goods through rationing rather than market exchange. Russell observed the consequences in [[moscow|Moscow]]: the collapse of retail trade, the emergence of a black market, and the desperate ingenuity with which ordinary Russians sought to obtain the necessities of life.

**Labour mobilisation.** The state introduced compulsory labour service, mobilising workers like soldiers and assigning them to tasks determined by central planners rather than individual choice or market signals.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's assessment of War Communism was characteristically ambivalent. He acknowledged that some of its features were responses to emergency conditions — civil war, [[allied-blockade|blockade]], and economic collapse — rather than expressions of Bolshevik ideology. The Bolsheviks had inherited a ruined economy and faced existential military threats; any government in such circumstances would have resorted to extraordinary measures.

Yet Russell also argued that War Communism reflected deeper ideological commitments. The Bolsheviks' hostility to market exchange, their faith in central planning, and their determination to break the economic power of the peasantry were not merely tactical responses to emergency but expressions of their Marxian conviction that the market was a capitalist institution to be abolished.

## Human Consequences

Russell did not provide detailed economic statistics — his book was based on observation, not quantitative analysis — but he conveyed the human texture of economic collapse: the hunger in the cities, the queues for rations, the breakdown of transport, the precipitous decline in industrial output. He noted the irony that a regime ostensibly dedicated to the industrial [[proletariat]] had presided over the destruction of industry and the dispersal of the working class (001-073).

## Legacy

War Communism was abandoned in 1921 in favour of the New Economic Policy (NEP), which restored limited market mechanisms — too late for Russell's book to record, but a tacit admission of the policy's failure. Russell's analysis, written before this reversal, captures the Bolshevik regime at the moment of its greatest economic crisis, pursuing policies that were simultaneously imposed by circumstance and chosen by conviction.
