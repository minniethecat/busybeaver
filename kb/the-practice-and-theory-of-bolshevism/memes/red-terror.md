# Red Terror

The **Red Terror** was the campaign of mass arrests, executions, and political repression conducted by the Bolshevik regime during the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]] (1918–1921). Bertrand Russell, writing in 1920, addressed the Terror not as a sensational exposé but as a phenomenon that raised fundamental questions about the relationship between revolutionary means and revolutionary ends.

## Russell's Observations

Russell was characteristically nuanced in his treatment of Bolshevik terror. He refused to adopt the tone of moral outrage that characterised much Western commentary on the subject — not because he was indifferent to the suffering, but because he believed that moral condemnation without analysis was intellectually empty.

He acknowledged the extraordinary circumstances under which the Bolsheviks operated: civil war, foreign intervention, economic collapse, and the [[allied-blockade|Allied blockade]]. He recognised that all governments facing existential threats resort to measures they would otherwise condemn, and that the Bolsheviks' adversaries — the White armies, the intervening powers — were guilty of their own atrocities.

## The Theoretical Problem

Russell's distinctive contribution was to identify the *theoretical* dimension of Bolshevik terror. He argued that the Terror was not merely a pragmatic response to civil war conditions but an expression of the Bolsheviks' deeper theoretical commitments:

> It is not the atrocity of the Bolsheviks' deeds that makes them seem dreadful, so much as a general hostility to opposition. (001-009)

This "general hostility to opposition" was, Russell argued, rooted in the Bolsheviks' conception of their own historical role. If the Bolsheviks were the vanguard of the [[proletariat]], and if history's direction was scientifically determined by [[marxism|Marxian theory]], then opposition to the Bolsheviks was not merely mistaken — it was objectively counter-revolutionary. This logic transformed political disagreement into metaphysical treason and made terror not an aberration but a structural feature of Bolshevik governance.

## The Limits of Circumstantial Defence

Russell considered and ultimately rejected the argument that the Terror was merely a temporary expedient forced upon the Bolsheviks by civil war conditions. He noted that the Bolsheviks had employed terror against political opponents — including [[anarchism|anarchists]], Mensheviks, and Socialist Revolutionaries — who posed no military threat to the regime. The Terror, in Russell's analysis, was directed not only against armed enemies but against the very possibility of organised political opposition.

This was the crucial distinction: the Bolsheviks did not merely suppress their enemies; they defined opposition as enmity and dissent as treason, thereby making terror a permanent feature of their rule rather than a temporary response to emergency.

## Historical Significance

Russell's analysis of the Red Terror was prophetic. The patterns he identified — the equation of opposition with counter-revolution, the transformation of political disagreement into metaphysical heresy, the institutionalisation of repression — would characterise the Soviet system for decades to come, reaching their horrific culmination in the Stalinist purges of the 1930s.
