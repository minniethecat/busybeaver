# Press and Censorship

**Press and censorship** under [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] was, in Bertrand Russell's 1920 analysis, a system of comprehensive state control over all means of public communication. Russell found that the Bolsheviks had not merely restricted the press — they had abolished it as an independent institution, replacing newspapers, journals, and publishers with organs of Party propaganda whose function was not to inform but to mobilise.

## Russell's Observations

Russell, himself a prolific writer and public intellectual, was particularly sensitive to the suppression of press freedom in Soviet Russia. He observed that "the Press is entirely a Government institution" — every newspaper, every journal, every printing press was controlled by the state, and nothing could be published without official approval (001-060).

The justification offered by the Bolsheviks was familiar: in a class society, they argued, the capitalist press served capitalist interests under the guise of objectivity. The Bolsheviks claimed merely to be making explicit what was implicit in all media systems — that information serves power. Russell acknowledged the partial truth in this analysis while rejecting the conclusion that state monopoly was the remedy.

## Mechanisms of Control

Russell identified several mechanisms through which the Bolsheviks maintained their information monopoly:

**State ownership of printing facilities.** All printing presses were nationalised, making it physically impossible to produce unauthorised publications.

**Paper rationing.** Paper was allocated exclusively to state-approved publications. Even if an independent publisher could somehow access a press, they could not obtain the paper needed to print.

**Pre-publication censorship.** All content required approval before publication. Russell noted that this extended beyond explicitly political material to encompass literature, art criticism, and even scientific publications.

**Control of foreign information.** The [[allied-blockade|blockade]] served not only economic but informational purposes — limiting the flow of foreign newspapers, books, and periodicals into Russia and isolating the Russian population from alternative sources of information (001-060).

## Consequences

Russell argued that the suppression of press freedom had consequences beyond mere political control. By eliminating independent journalism and critical commentary, the Bolsheviks had created an information environment in which error could not be corrected and policy could not be evaluated. The government's own understanding of conditions in the country was degraded by the absence of honest reporting.

This point was central to Russell's broader epistemological critique of Bolshevism: a system that cannot tolerate criticism cannot learn from experience, and a system that cannot learn is doomed to repeat its mistakes (001-030).

## Comparison with Democratic Norms

Russell did not idealise the Western press. He acknowledged that capitalist newspapers served the interests of their owners, that sensationalism often displaced substance, and that the concentration of media ownership posed its own threat to democratic discourse. But he insisted that the existence of *some* independent space for criticism — however imperfect — was categorically different from its total abolition, and that this difference was fundamental to the distinction between democratic and authoritarian governance.
