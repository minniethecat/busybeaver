# Second International

The international organisation of socialist and labour parties founded in 1889 — whose collapse in 1914 in the face of World War I Russell identified as the event that discredited moderate socialism and opened space for Bolshevik radicalism.

This page documents **Second International** in the context of Bertrand Russell's 1920 analysis of Bolshevism. Russell's account, based on firsthand observation during his visit to Soviet Russia, provides perspective on the organisations that shaped the revolutionary and counter-revolutionary forces of the period.

## Historical Role

The period of Russell's visit was one of extraordinary political and military conflict. The [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]] was reaching its climax; multiple political and military organisations contended for power; and the future of the Bolshevik experiment remained deeply uncertain. The organisation documented on this page played a role — whether supportive, oppositional, or contextual — in the events Russell analysed.

## Russell's Perspective

Russell's analysis of Bolshevism was informed by his understanding of the organisations that supported, opposed, and surrounded the Bolshevik regime. His characteristic method — sympathetic to socialist aspirations while critical of authoritarian methods — shaped his assessment of all political actors in the Russian drama.
