# Socialism

## Definition

Socialism, as discussed by Bertrand Russell in *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism* (1920), designates a system of economic organization in which the means of production are collectively owned, as opposed to the private ownership characteristic of capitalism. Russell regarded some form of socialist organization as historically inevitable, writing that "the existing capitalist system is doomed" and that "a radically new order of society will emerge" (P01-005). For Russell, the crucial question was not whether socialism would replace capitalism, but what *form* of socialism would emerge — and whether it would be achieved through democratic or violent means.

Russell believed that "the new order will be either some form of Socialism or a reversion to barbarism and petty war such as occurred during the barbarian invasion" (P01-005). This stark dichotomy framed his entire analysis of the Bolshevik experiment.

## Russell's Critique of Bolshevik Socialism

Russell's central argument about socialism is that the Bolshevik method of achieving it — through violent revolution, civil war, and dictatorial rule — produces a form of socialism that is worse than capitalism. He distinguished carefully between socialism as an ideal and the Bolshevik implementation of it.

> The Third International is an organization which exists to promote the class-war and to hasten the advent of revolution everywhere. My objection is not that capitalism is less bad than the Bolsheviks believe, but that Socialism is less good, not in its best form, but in the only form which is likely to be brought about by war. The evils of war, especially of civil war, are certain and very great; the gains to be achieved by victory are problematical. (001-020)

Russell's objection was thus not to socialism itself but to the *method* of achieving it through class war. He observed that "in order to succeed in war, a concentration of power is necessary, and from concentration of power the very same evils flow as from the capitalist concentration of wealth" (001-020). This was his core critique: Bolshevik methods produced in practice the same concentration of power that socialism was meant to abolish.

## Western Socialism vs. Bolshevism

Russell was particularly concerned about the effect of Bolshevism on Western socialist movements. He observed that "Western Socialists who have visited Russia have seen fit to suppress the harsher features of the present regime" (P01-007), misleading their followers about the true nature of Bolshevik rule. He warned that "by this lack of courage they have exposed Western Socialism to the danger of becoming Bolshevik through ignorance of the price that has to be paid and of the uncertainty as to whether the desired goal will be reached in the end" (P01-007).

Russell believed that Western democratic nations were capable of achieving socialism through peaceful, constitutional means: "the West is capable of adopting less painful and more certain methods of reaching Socialism than those that have seemed necessary in Russia" (P01-007). He argued that "while some forms of Socialism are immeasurably better than capitalism, others are even worse. Among those that are worse I reckon the form which is being achieved in Russia" (P01-007).

## The Parliamentary Path

Russell engaged seriously with the Bolshevik argument that parliamentary democracy could not bring about socialism, acknowledging that the "Bolshevik argument against Parliamentary democracy as a method of achieving Socialism is a powerful one" (002-031). However, he challenged the Bolshevik assumption that only violent revolution could succeed.

Russell pointed to empirical evidence that socialist movements had succeeded electorally where they addressed real popular concerns: "those industrial populations which would most obviously benefit by Socialism have, in the main, adopted it, in spite of the opposition of employers" (002-040). He argued that "Socialism does not arouse the same passionate interest in the average citizen as is roused by nationality and used to be roused by religion" (002-040), suggesting that the difficulty of achieving socialist majorities was not primarily due to capitalist propaganda but to the nature of political motivation itself.

## Scientific Socialism and Bolshevik Dogma

Russell noted that the Bolsheviks explicitly identified their project with the tradition of "Scientific Socialism" (001-085), a term originating with Marx and Engels. At the Ninth Congress of the Russian Communist Party, it was declared obligatory "mercilessly to fight that particular obnoxious form, the ignorant conceit which deems the working class capable of solving all problems without the assistance *in the most responsible cases* of specialists of the bourgeois school" (001-085). Russell saw this as revealing an internal tension within Bolshevik socialism — the tension between celebrating working-class power and recognizing the practical need for bourgeois technical expertise.

## State Socialism as National Development

Despite his profound objections to Bolshevik methods, Russell acknowledged that the Bolsheviks, as a national government, were pursuing a programme of "State Socialism" that had genuine developmental merit in the Russian context. He wrote that the Bolsheviks were "introducing, as far as they can, American efficiency among a lazy and undisciplined population" and "preparing to develop the natural resources of their country by the methods of State Socialism, for which, in Russia, there is much to be said" (001-127).

However, he sharply distinguished this national project from the Bolsheviks' international programme: "as a national Government, stripped of their camouflage, regarded as the successors of Peter the Great, they are performing a necessary though unamiable task" (001-127), while "for their international programme there is, to my mind, nothing to be said" (001-127).

## America and the Failure of Revolutionary Socialism

Russell observed a paradox that he found deeply significant: the country where capitalism was strongest was also the country where revolutionary socialism was weakest. "There is no other civilized country where capitalism is so strong and revolutionary Socialism so weak as in America" (002-106). The very success of American capitalism, Russell suggested, had immunized its working class against the appeal of socialist revolution. This observation formed part of Russell's broader argument that revolutionary socialism could not succeed globally so long as powerful capitalist nations remained economically self-sufficient and able to "starve" revolutionary movements elsewhere (002-106).
