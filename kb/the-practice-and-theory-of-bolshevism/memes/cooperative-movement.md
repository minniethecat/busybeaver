# Cooperative Movement

The **Cooperative Movement** — the network of voluntary associations for production, distribution, and consumption owned and governed by their members — represented, for Bertrand Russell, a model of socialist organisation fundamentally different from Bolshevik state ownership. Cooperatives preserved individual initiative and democratic control while advancing collective welfare, embodying the decentralised, democratic [[socialism]] that Russell favoured.

## Cooperatives and Socialism

The cooperative tradition had deep roots in British socialism. The Rochdale Pioneers of 1844 had established the principles — democratic governance (one member, one vote), open membership, limited return on capital — that would define the movement. By Russell's time, consumer cooperatives had become major economic institutions in Britain, providing goods and services to millions of working-class families.

Russell saw in the cooperative movement an alternative path to socialism — one that built economic democracy from the ground up rather than imposing it from above through state decree. Cooperatives demonstrated that collective ownership could be efficient, democratic, and compatible with individual freedom in ways that [[bolshevism|Bolshevik]] state socialism did not.

## Cooperatives in Russia

Russell noted that the Bolsheviks had inherited a substantial cooperative movement in Russia — particularly in agriculture and retail distribution — but had largely subordinated it to state control. The Bolsheviks' suspicion of independent centres of economic power extended even to cooperative societies that were, in principle, compatible with socialist objectives.

This subordination of cooperatives was, for Russell, symptomatic of the Bolsheviks' deeper hostility to any form of organisation not under Party control. A movement that could not tolerate independent cooperatives could not, Russell argued, claim to represent the interests of workers and peasants — it represented only the interests of the Party.

## Russell's Alternative

Russell's advocacy of cooperatives was part of his broader political vision: a socialism that was democratic, experimental, and respectful of individual liberty. Cooperatives were not, in Russell's view, a complete solution to the problems of capitalism — they could not, by themselves, transform the structure of ownership in heavy industry or finance. But they demonstrated a principle: that collective ownership could be compatible with individual freedom, that economic democracy did not require political dictatorship.
