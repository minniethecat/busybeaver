## Overview

The Paris Commune was a radical socialist government that briefly ruled Paris from 18 March to 28 May 1871, following France's defeat in the Franco-Prussian War. Established by revolutionary workers and National Guardsmen, the Commune enacted a series of progressive measures -- separation of church and state, free secular education, the abolition of night work for bakers -- before being crushed by the French national army in the "Bloody Week" that left tens of thousands dead.

For Marx and his followers, the Commune became the archetype of the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] -- the political form through which the working class would exercise power during the transition to [[communism]]. Marx's pamphlet *The Civil War in France* (1871) immortalized the Commune as the model for future proletarian revolutions.

## Russell's Account

Russell discusses the Paris Commune not as a standalone historical event but through the lens of its legacy within Bolshevik thought. The Bolsheviks explicitly invoked the Commune as a historical precedent for their own seizure of power. Russell's critical analysis of Bolshevik methods applies implicitly to this lineage: the Commune, like the [[october-revolution]], was an attempt by a revolutionary minority to establish proletarian rule through armed insurrection.

Russell's treatment of historical parallels is evident when he compares the Bolsheviks to Cromwell and the French Directoire. (001-016) He notes that "the baser side of the present Russian Government is most nearly paralleled by the Directoire in France, but on its better side it is closely analogous to the rule of Cromwell." The Paris Commune haunts these comparisons as the most radical precursor to Bolshevik rule.

## Bolshevik Perspective

For the Bolsheviks, the Paris Commune was both inspiration and cautionary tale. It demonstrated that workers could seize and hold state power -- but also that revolutionary governments risk destruction if they fail to suppress counter-revolutionary forces decisively. Lenin studied the Commune extensively, concluding that its fatal error was excessive leniency toward its enemies.

The Bolshevik pageant of the World Commune, which Russell witnessed in Petrograd, dramatized this vision of history culminating in universal proletarian victory. Russell describes the final scene: "Cannon shots announce the breaking of the blockade against Soviet Russia, and the victory of the World Proletariat...The workers of the whole world, with the emblems of labour, gather for the celebration of the World Commune." (001-054)

Russell was deeply skeptical of this revolutionary eschatology. He recognized that "the Communists, who represent the class-conscious wage-earners, wait for some propitious moment" to seize power, and that the Bolshevik method "has been carried out in Russia, and is to be carried out, before very long, in every civilized country." (002-046)

## Significance

The Paris Commune's significance in Russell's analysis is as a template that the Bolsheviks adapted and radicalized. Where the Commune was local, brief, and relatively moderate in its terror, the Bolshevik experiment was national, prolonged, and systematically coercive. Russell's critique of Bolshevik methods -- his warning that "abandonment of law, when it becomes widespread, lets loose the wild beast, and gives a free rein to the primitive lusts and egoisms which civilization in some degree curbs" -- applies a fortiori to any attempt to replicate the Commune on a national scale. (002-042)

Russell argues that the Bolsheviks had "the honesty to proclaim in advance their intention of using power for other ends than those for which it was given them," and that this radical transparency distinguishes them from earlier revolutionaries while simultaneously rendering their method politically self-defeating. (002-038)
