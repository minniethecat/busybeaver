# Congress of the Peoples of the East

The **Congress of the Peoples of the East**, held in Baku in September 1920, was convened by the [[third-international|Communist International]] to extend Bolshevik influence into the colonial and semi-colonial world. Russell regarded the Congress as emblematic of the Bolsheviks' ambition to transform a Russian revolution into a world movement — and of the contradictions inherent in that ambition.

## Historical Context

The Congress assembled approximately 1,900 delegates from across Asia and the Middle East at a moment when the Bolshevik regime, having survived the [[russian-civil-war|civil war]], was turning its attention outward. The Baku meeting represented the first large-scale attempt to organise anti-colonial forces under Marxist leadership.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell noted the strategic intelligence of the Baku initiative — the Bolsheviks understood that European revolution was not imminent and that the colonial world offered an alternative path to weakening capitalist imperialism. Yet he also identified the inherent tension: a congress ostensibly convened to liberate oppressed peoples was controlled entirely by Russian Bolsheviks, with agendas determined in Moscow rather than in the colonies themselves.
