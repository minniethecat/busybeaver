# Communism

## Definition

Communism, as understood by Bertrand Russell in his 1920 analysis of Bolshevism, is a social and economic system characterized by the abolition of private property in the means of production, the establishment of a classless society, and the withering away of the state. It represents the ultimate goal toward which the [[russian-revolution|Russian Revolution]] was directed — a complete transformation of both the material conditions of life and the beliefs of mankind (P01-001). Russell distinguishes between Communism as an **ideal end-state** and the [[bolshevism|Bolshevik method]] of pursuing it, a distinction that structures his entire critique.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell regards Communism as "necessary to the world" (P01-003), seeing it as the only viable response to the destructive tendencies of industrial capitalism. For Russell, the fundamental issue is not whether Communism is desirable — he takes its necessity as given — but rather whether the Bolshevik method of achieving it can succeed without sacrificing the very ideals it purports to serve.

> By far the most important aspect of the Russian Revolution is as an attempt to realize Communism. I believe that Communism is necessary to the world, and I believe that the heroism of Russia has fired men's hopes in a way which was essential to the realization of Communism in the future. (P01-003)

Russell distinguishes sharply between the goal of Communism and the Bolshevik means of attaining it. He describes the Bolshevik method as "a pioneer method, rough and dangerous, too heroic to count the cost of the opposition it arouses" (P01-004). His core concern is that the brutality of the method will corrupt the outcome, producing not Communism but a new form of authoritarian rule.

### Three Possible Outcomes

Russell identifies three trajectories for the Bolshevik experiment:

| Outcome | Description | Russell's Assessment |
|---------|------------|---------------------|
| **Capitalist victory** | Bolshevism defeated by external capitalist forces | Plausible but self-defeating for the West |
| **Authoritarian degeneration** | Bolsheviks retain power but lose Communist ideals, becoming "a Bonapartist dictatorship" | Most likely in Russell's view |
| **Prolonged global conflict** | Indefinite struggle between Bolshevism and capitalism | Deeply undesirable for all parties |

> Source: (P01-004)

A key theme in Russell's analysis is the tension between the method and the goal. He observes that Communism requires a "group-mentality" (001-006) that the Bolsheviks foster through hope focused on the younger generation. Yet the harsh realities of Soviet life, Russell argues, have already begun to erode this hope among those subject to "the dictatorship of Moscow" (001-006).

Russell also notes an important asymmetry in how Communism is perceived globally. The dogmatic certainty of Marxian Communism "finds support in the supposed objectivity of the materialist conception of history" (002-007), lending Bolshevik arguments an appearance of scientific inevitability that Russell finds philosophically dubious but politically effective.

## Bolshevik Perspective

From the Bolshevik viewpoint, as Russell reports it, Communism is not merely an ideal to be approached gradually but a system that can and must be established through the dictatorship of the proletariat. The Bolsheviks regard the transitional dictatorship as an unavoidable instrument for suppressing counter-revolutionary forces and reorganizing society along Communist lines.

Russell notes that [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]] taught Communism as "fatally predestined to come about through the operation of economic forces" (001-014), and the Bolsheviks, as orthodox Marxists, see their revolution as the historical fulfillment of this inevitability. Russell is struck by what he calls "religious faith in the Marxian gospel, which takes the place of the Christian martyr's hopes of Paradise" (001-030).

The Bolsheviks' commitment to Communism extends beyond economics into culture. Russell observes with concern that art "directly inspired by Communism" (001-047) takes the form of propaganda plays and mass pageants performed by the [[red-army|Red Army]] — a narrowing of artistic expression that he finds troubling.

Russell's conversations with Bolshevik leaders, including [[lenin|Vladimir Lenin]], revealed their frank acknowledgment of the difficulties Communism faced in a peasant-majority country. When he asked whether it was possible "to establish Communism firmly and fully in a country containing such a large majority of peasants" (001-026), the response was pragmatic rather than ideological: electrification and industrialization would eventually produce goods to offer the peasantry, resolving the contradiction. Russell records this response with evident skepticism.

The Bolsheviks were "absolutely certain that Communism is desirable, so certain that they feel no need for argument" (002-032). For Russell the philosopher, this intellectual closure is one of Bolshevism's most troubling features.

## Historical Context

Russell wrote *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism* in 1920, three years after the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]] and during the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]]. Communism as a theoretical system had been elaborated by Marx and Engels in the mid-19th century, but the Bolsheviks were the first to attempt its implementation on a national scale. The Russian experiment was being conducted under conditions of extreme hardship — war, blockade, famine — that made it difficult to distinguish between what was intrinsic to Communism and what was a product of emergency circumstances.

Russell visited Soviet Russia at the invitation of the British Labour Party, and his account is shaped by direct observation. He met [[lenin|Lenin]], [[trotsky|Trotsky]], Gorky, and other leading figures, and his analysis draws on personal conversations as well as documentary evidence. The [[third-international|Third International (Comintern)]], founded in 1919, was simultaneously exporting the Bolshevik model of Communism globally — a development Russell viewed with considerable alarm.
