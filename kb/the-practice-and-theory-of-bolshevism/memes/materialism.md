# Materialism

## Definition

Materialism, in the context of Bolshevism and Marxist theory, operates on two distinct but related levels. First, there is philosophic materialism — the ontological doctrine that all phenomena, including mental events, are ultimately reducible to matter and its motions. Second, there is the materialistic conception of history (historical materialism), which holds that economic forces are the fundamental determinants of all large-scale historical and political developments. Russell devotes careful attention to distinguishing these two senses, arguing that they are logically independent and that their conflation has damaging consequences for political thought.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell opens his analysis of materialism by noting that it forms part of what he calls Bolshevism's "religion, with elaborate dogmas and inspired scriptures" (P01-007). He identifies "philosophic materialism" as one of the "elaborate and dogmatic beliefs" (P01-007) that full-fledged Communists are expected to accept — beliefs that, in Russell's view, cannot be known with the certainty that Communists attribute to them.

Russell's most important analytical move is to separate philosophic materialism from the materialistic conception of history. He argues that Marx's theory of economic determinism "has no essential connection with materialism in the philosophic sense" (002-002). Philosophic materialism, Russell explains, may be defined as "the theory that all apparently mental occurrences either are really physical" (002-002) — a metaphysical claim about the nature of reality. The Marxian claim, by contrast, is that "all the mass-phenomena of history are determined by economic motives" (002-002) — an empirical claim about historical causation.

> There is, therefore, no logical connection either way between philosophic materialism and what is called the "materialistic conception of history." (002-005)

Russell demonstrates this independence by observing that "philosophic materialism does not prove that economic causes are fundamental in politics" (002-003). The view of Buckle — that climate determines civilization — is "equally compatible with materialism" (002-003), as is the Freudian view that traces everything to sex. Conversely, "economic causes might be at the bottom of all political events even if philosophic materialism were false" (002-004), since economic causes "operate through men's desire for possessions" (002-004) — a psychological mechanism that does not require matter to be ontologically fundamental.

Beyond the logical point, Russell identifies a practical danger in the Marxian fusion of philosophy and politics. He argues that the claim that communist politics flows from materialist metaphysics gives political convictions an unwarranted appearance of philosophical necessity. This leads communists to become "absolute and sweeping, unable to admit that a general theory of history is likely, at best, to be only true on the whole and in the main" (002-007). Russell compares this to "the fixed certainty of Catholic theology" (002-007) — a dogmatic temper that, he believes, is incompatible with the scientific spirit.

Russell does not, however, dismiss the materialistic conception of history entirely. He grants that "treated as a practical approximation, not as an exact metaphysical law, the materialistic conception of history has a very large measure of truth" (002-008). He offers examples: industrialism, rather than Darwinian arguments, has been the primary cause of declining religious belief among urban workers; simultaneously, industrialism has "revived religious belief among the rich" (002-008) — a phenomenon Marxians capture with the concept of "bourgeois ideology" (002-009).

Where Russell believes the materialist theory goes wrong is in its neglect of non-economic factors. He identifies nationalism as "the most obvious non-economic factor, and the one the neglect of which has led Socialists most astray" (002-011). A nation, once formed, has economic interests, but "it is not, as a rule, economic motives that decide what group of human beings shall form a nation" (002-011). Russell also criticizes the Marxian assumption that a person's primary group identification is with their economic class, when in fact religion, nationality, and other affiliations may prove more decisive (002-012).

Russell's deepest objection to Marxian materialism is that it rests on a false psychology. The materialist theory, he argues, "requires the assumption that every politically conscious person is governed by one single desire — the desire to increase his own share of commodities" (002-013). But "men desire power, they desire satisfactions for their pride and their sense of independence" (002-013), motives that cannot be reduced to economic self-interest. Russell calls for "a treatment of political motives by the methods of psychoanalysis" (002-014), recognizing that in politics, "as in private life, men invent myths to rationalize their conduct" (002-014).

## Bolshevik Perspective

The Bolsheviks regarded the materialistic conception of history as a scientific truth underpinning their entire political programme. Russell observed that when Lenin "wishes to prove some proposition, he does so, if possible, by quoting texts from Marx and Engels" (P01-007), treating Marxian doctrine with the reverence due to revealed truth. The claim that Marxism was scientific — grounded in the material facts of history rather than in utopian aspiration — was central to Bolshevik self-understanding and to their rejection of rival socialist traditions.

For the Bolsheviks, the materialistic conception of history demonstrated that communism was "fatally predestined to come about through the operation of economic forces" (001-014). This conviction gave Bolshevik cadres the psychological strength to endure persecution, hardship, and civil war — they believed that history was on their side, whatever the immediate setbacks.

## Historical Context

Russell's philosophical critique of Marxian materialism draws on his own background as a philosopher trained in the analytic tradition, which valued logical clarity and the careful separation of distinct claims. His argument that the materialistic conception of history is logically independent of philosophic materialism reflects this training. Russell wrote at a time when the philosophical and scientific prestige of materialism was high, and his critique was intended not to defend idealism but to disentangle what he considered to be confused thinking that had real-world political consequences.
