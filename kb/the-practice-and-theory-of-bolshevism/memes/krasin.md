# Leonid Krasin

**Leonid Krasin** (1870–1926) was a Bolshevik diplomat, engineer, and trade negotiator who served as the Soviet government's principal representative in negotiations with Britain. Although Russell did not discuss Krasin at length in *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*, Krasin's role as the Bolsheviks' pragmatic face to the capitalist world exemplified the tension between revolutionary ideology and diplomatic necessity that Russell analysed throughout his book.

## The Bolshevik Diplomat

Krasin was unusual among leading Bolsheviks in combining revolutionary commitment with technical expertise and diplomatic skill. An engineer by training, he had been involved in underground Bolshevik activities before the revolution — including the procurement of funds through bank expropriations. After the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]], he emerged as the regime's most effective trade negotiator, securing agreements with Britain and other Western powers that began to break Russia's diplomatic isolation.

Russell, who had observed the Bolsheviks' combination of ideological intransigence and tactical flexibility, would have recognised in Krasin a figure who embodied this duality. Krasin negotiated trade agreements with capitalist governments while remaining a committed Bolshevik — a contradiction that the Bolsheviks managed by treating diplomacy as a tactical necessity rather than a revision of their ultimate objectives.

## The Trade Agreement

Krasin's most significant achievement was the Anglo-Soviet Trade Agreement of 1921, signed after Russell's book was published. The agreement did not constitute formal diplomatic recognition, but it established a framework for commercial relations and marked the beginning of the end of Russia's economic isolation. Russell, who had condemned the [[allied-blockade|blockade]] as both cruel and counterproductive, would have welcomed this step toward normal economic relations.

## Historical Significance

Krasin represented a strand within Bolshevism — pragmatic, technically competent, inclined toward engagement with the outside world — that would persist even as the regime became more ideologically rigid under Stalin. His career demonstrated that the Bolsheviks, for all their revolutionary rhetoric, were capable of traditional diplomacy when circumstances required it. Russell's analysis of the gap between Bolshevik theory and Bolshevik practice found in figures like Krasin a revealing case study.
