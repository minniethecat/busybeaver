# Hungary

**Hungary** occupied a prominent place in Russell's analysis as the site of the most significant attempt to replicate the [[bolshevik|Bolshevik]] revolution outside Russia — the Hungarian Soviet Republic of 1919, which lasted 133 days before collapsing under internal and external pressure.

## Geopolitical Context

The Hungarian Soviet Republic was proclaimed in March 1919, in the aftermath of the [[world-war|World War]] and the dissolution of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Led by [[bela-kun|Béla Kun]], a former journalist who had been radicalised as a prisoner of war in Russia, the regime attempted to implement Bolshevik-style policies — nationalisation of industry, land, and banks — in a country that was simultaneously facing territorial dismemberment by the Allied powers and military pressure from neighbouring states.

## Russell's Perspective

Russell saw the Hungarian Soviet Republic as a crucial test of the Bolshevik thesis that revolution could spread to advanced countries. The regime's failure — it was overthrown by Romanian military intervention after losing popular support — reinforced Russell's argument that Bolshevik methods could not simply be exported. The Hungarian communists, Russell argued, repeated the Bolshevik error of substituting party dictatorship for popular consent, alienating the peasantry and the urban middle class. The result was a counter-revolutionary backlash that set back the cause of socialism in Hungary for a generation.
