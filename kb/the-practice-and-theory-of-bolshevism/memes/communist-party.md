# Communist Party (Bolsheviks)

The **Communist Party (Bolsheviks)** — formally the Russian Communist Party (Bolsheviks), or RKP(b) — was the sole ruling party of [[russia|Soviet Russia]] at the time of Russell's 1920 visit. Russell describes it as the organization in whose hands "all real power" is concentrated, wielding an authority he explicitly compares to the guardians in [[plato-republic|Plato's Republic]] (001-017). Under [[lenin|Lenin]]'s leadership, the Party numbered about 600,000 members governing a population of 120 million, functioning simultaneously as a political vanguard, a doctrinal orthodoxy, and a quasi-religious order whose "dogmas are accepted not by reason but by faith" (001-142).

## Overview

Russell's analysis of the Communist Party is central to his critique of [[bolshevism]] as a system of governance. He describes it as the institution in whose hands "all real power" is concentrated, numbering about 600,000 members in a population of approximately 120 million (001-073). Despite its name, Russell emphasizes that the Party governed not as a representative body of the working class but as a self-selecting vanguard that claimed exclusive understanding of the [[proletariat]]'s true interests.

### Party Composition and Scale

The sheer demographic disproportion between Party and population was, for Russell, a revealing structural fact. The Party governed by consent neither of the majority nor of the working class whose name it invoked, but by the conviction of its own members that they alone possessed the theoretical insight necessary to guide Russia through the transition from capitalism to [[communism]]. Russell notes pointedly that when he spoke with ordinary Russians, "almost invariably they said they were of no party" (001-073).

### Comparison to Plato's Republic

The Party's structure, in Russell's account, mirrors Plato's Republic in a way that its leaders would have found unwelcome:

> The Communist Party corresponds to the guardians; the soldiers have about the same status in both; there is in Russia an attempt to deal with family life more or less as Plato suggested. (001-017)

This comparison underscores Russell's view of Bolshevism as a form of elite rule justified by doctrinal certainty rather than democratic consent. Just as Plato's guardians ruled by virtue of their access to the Form of the Good, the Communist Party ruled by virtue of its claimed insight into the laws of historical development.

## Internal Structure and Factions

Russell's account reveals a Party that was far from monolithic, despite its official ideology of unity. He distinguishes three distinct elements within the Party:

| Faction | Composition | Characteristics | PN |
|---------|------------|-----------------|-----|
| **Old revolutionaries** | Veterans of Tsarist persecution | "Honest men, with a profound belief that Communism will regenerate the world"; sentimental about Communism, unsentimental about individuals | 001-074 |
| ***Arrivistes*** | Late converts, careerists | Embraced Bolshevism for material success; "actuated by motives of greed" | 001-075 |
| **Inherited Tsarist police** | Cheka, former Okhrana personnel | A "body practically independent of the Government," better fed than the Red Army, with power to imprison or execute without trial | 001-075 |

### The Three Factions

This internal stratification was, Russell argues, not an accident but a structural consequence of one-party rule. When the Party holds a monopoly on power and career advancement, it inevitably attracts those motivated more by self-interest than by revolutionary conviction. The third element — the security apparatus — was particularly troubling: Russell notes that the Cheka "has the power of imprisoning and executing without trial all whom it thinks fit," operating with a degree of autonomy that made it "practically independent of the Government" (001-075).

### The Hollow Institutions

The formal institutions of the Soviet state were hollow shells. The All-Russian Soviet, constitutionally designated as the supreme body, "meets seldom, and has become increasingly formal. Its sole function at present, so far as I could discover, is to ratify, without discussion, previous decisions of the Communist Party" (001-072).

## Role in the Bolshevik System

The Party's absolute control extended to every domain of Russian life. Russell documents how the Ninth Congress of the Communist Party (March–April 1920) acknowledged the severity of the economic collapse, speaking of "the incredible catastrophes of public economy" and "the terrible collapse of the transport and the railway system" (001-079). The Congress's response was characteristic: it intensified state control, approving "the mobilization of the industrial proletariat, compulsory labour service, militarization of production and the application of military detachments to economic needs" (001-100).

### Economic Crisis and State Response

This pattern — crisis met with coercion — was, Russell argues, built into the Party's theory of governance. Rather than relaxing control when economic conditions worsened, the Party responded by intensifying it, treating economic dysfunction as a discipline problem requiring military solutions rather than as evidence that central planning was failing.

### The Peasantry Problem

The Party's relationship with the peasantry was especially troubled. Russell notes that some peasants openly declared themselves Tsarists. The Party's response was to rule by force and to invest its hopes in the rising generation, believing that only children raised under the new regime could develop the "group-mentality that Communism requires" (001-006).

> A great part of the despotism which characterizes the Bolsheviks belongs to the essence of their social philosophy, and would have to be reproduced, even if in a milder form, wherever that philosophy became dominant. (001-008)

## Bolshevik Perspective

From within the Party, its monopoly on power was not merely expedient but theoretically necessary. The Party understood itself as:

1. **The vanguard of the proletariat** — The most class-conscious section of the working class, entrusted with leading the revolution to its communist conclusion
2. **The guardian of doctrinal orthodoxy** — Only the Party could correctly interpret Marxism and apply it to concrete conditions
3. **The instrument of world revolution** — Through the [[third-international|Third International]], the Party extended its authority beyond Russia's borders, coordinating revolutionary movements globally
4. **The necessary instrument of terror** — Russell quotes the Party's own declaration: "For the transition from Capitalism to Communism, for the whole period of the dictatorship of the proletariat, a strong all-Russian Soviet Government is necessary... which will ruthlessly suppress the exploiters" (001-095)

### Theoretical Self-Understanding

Russell notes that the Party's self-conception as a vanguard was not merely pragmatic positioning but a core theoretical commitment. It followed from the Leninist adaptation of Marxism, which held that the working class left to itself could attain only "trade-union consciousness" and therefore required a disciplined party of professional revolutionaries to guide it to revolutionary consciousness.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's portrait of the Communist Party is nuanced but fundamentally critical. He acknowledges the sincerity and self-sacrifice of the old revolutionaries, writing that "they think themselves utterly free from sentiment, but, in fact, they are sentimental about Communism" (001-074). Yet this sincerity, in Russell's view, was precisely what made them dangerous — their conviction that private property was "the root of all evil" led them to "shrink from no measures, however harsh" in pursuit of its abolition.

### The Danger of Sincerity

Russell's most distinctive insight about the Party leadership concerns the moral psychology of revolutionary conviction. The old Bolsheviks were not cynics or opportunists — they were true believers whose very sincerity made them capable of measures that a more calculating leadership might have found politically imprudent. Their sentimental attachment to Communism as an abstraction coexisted with a striking unsentimentality about the fate of actual human beings.

### Broader Implications

Russell draws a broader lesson about the effect of concentrated power on revolutionary movements. He warns that if a Communist Party were to acquire power in England, it would likely be "far more tolerant than any government can hope to be in Russia" (001-008), but he insists that the Party's claim to a monopoly on political truth was the defining feature of Bolshevism and its most dangerous precedent. The Party had become, in Russell's analysis, not the servant of the revolution but its master — an institution whose preservation had become an end in itself, regardless of the human cost.
