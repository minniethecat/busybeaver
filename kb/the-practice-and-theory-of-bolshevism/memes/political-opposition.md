# Political Opposition under Bolshevism

The treatment of **political opposition** was, for Bertrand Russell, the decisive test of Bolshevik claims to represent democracy — and the point at which those claims collapsed under the weight of practice.

## The Theory

In Bolshevik theory, the [[dictatorship|dictatorship of the proletariat]] was the political form of the transitional period between [[capitalism]] and [[communism]]. In this period, the [[vanguard-party|Communist Party]] — as the "class-conscious" representative of the proletariat — exercised political power. Political opposition, on this account, was not a democratic right but a vestige of class society that the dictatorship was obliged to suppress.

## Russell's Observations

Russell was struck by the comprehensiveness with which opposition was eliminated. He noted that the Bolsheviks suppressed not only bourgeois parties — whose elimination a socialist might defend — but also rival socialist parties, anarchists, and even dissent within the Communist Party itself. The Mensheviks and Socialist Revolutionaries, who had led the [[russian-revolution|February Revolution]], were outlawed. (001-009)

The [[cheka|Cheka]], the Bolshevik secret police, operated with powers that Russell found indistinguishable from those of the Tsarist Okhrana. "Ordinary mortals live in terror of it," he wrote, noting that many of its personnel were inherited from the Tsarist regime. (001-018) The irony was bitter: a revolution against autocracy had reproduced autocracy's instruments of repression.

## The Constituent Assembly

Russell cited the fate of the [[constituent-assembly|Constituent Assembly]] as the definitive moment when Bolshevik democracy revealed itself as dictatorship. Elected in November 1917 — after the Bolshevik seizure of power — the Assembly had an anti-Bolshevik majority. Lenin dissolved it by force in January 1918. Russell compared Lenin's actions to Cromwell's dissolution of Parliament: both, he argued, "were driven to sacrifice democracy to religion enforced by military dictatorship." (001-016)

## Russell's Verdict

The suppression of opposition was not, Russell argued, a temporary defensive measure. It was structurally necessary to Bolshevik rule, because the Bolsheviks' claim to represent the proletariat could only be sustained by silencing the actual voices of workers and peasants who disagreed. When the [[kronstadt-rebellion|Kronstadt sailors]] — once the Bolsheviks' strongest supporters — demanded free soviets and were crushed by [[trotsky|Trotsky]]'s Red Army, the logic of one-party dictatorship reached its terrible conclusion.
