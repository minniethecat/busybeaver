# Poland

**Poland** — newly independent after the [[world-war|World War]] and immediately at war with Soviet Russia — formed the urgent military context for Bertrand Russell's visit to Russia in 1920. The Russo-Polish War was not merely a border conflict but a test of the Bolsheviks' capacity to project military power beyond Russia's borders and of their commitment to [[world-revolution|world revolution]].

## The Russo-Polish War

The war began in 1919–1920 as a clash over disputed border territories, but it quickly acquired larger significance. For the Bolsheviks, the Polish campaign was an opportunity to carry revolution westward into Europe — the [[red-army|Red Army]]'s advance toward Warsaw was accompanied by the establishment of a provisional Polish revolutionary government. For the Poles, it was an existential struggle for national independence against a traditional Russian enemy, now in Bolshevik form.

Russell was in Russia during the summer of 1920, as the Red Army advanced deep into Poland. He observed the mixture of revolutionary enthusiasm and nationalist calculation that characterised the Bolshevik approach to the war. The conflict, he noted, exposed the tension at the heart of Bolshevik foreign policy: the commitment to international revolution coexisted uneasily with the defence of Russian state interests.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell saw the Polish war as emblematic of the transformation of Bolshevik internationalism into an instrument of Russian national policy. The Red Army's invasion of Poland could be justified in revolutionary terms — carrying [[communism]] to the Polish workers — but it could also be understood as a traditional Russian attempt to dominate its western neighbour. Russell suspected that the latter interpretation was closer to the truth.

The war's outcome — the Red Army's defeat before Warsaw in August 1920 — was a turning point. It demonstrated the limits of Bolshevik military power and forced a reorientation of Soviet strategy from military expansion to diplomatic consolidation. Russell's book, published in 1920, captured this moment of uncertainty, before the full implications of the Polish defeat had become clear.
