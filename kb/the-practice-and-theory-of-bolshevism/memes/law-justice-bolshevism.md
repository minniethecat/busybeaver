# Law and Justice under Bolshevism

**Law and justice under Bolshevism** underwent a transformation that Bertrand Russell analysed as part of the broader breakdown of legal norms under revolutionary pressure. The Bolsheviks had abolished the Tsarist legal system — with its formal codes, professional judiciary, and procedural protections — and replaced it with revolutionary tribunals guided by "revolutionary consciousness" rather than statute law.

## The Revolutionary Conception of Law

The Bolsheviks approached law not as a neutral framework for resolving disputes but as an instrument of class rule. Tsarist law, in this analysis, had been an instrument of bourgeois and aristocratic domination dressed in the language of impartiality. Bolshevik law would be honest about its class character: it would serve the interests of the [[proletariat]] and suppress the [[bourgeoisie]].

Russell acknowledged the intellectual coherence of this position. If law is indeed an instrument of class rule — a central tenet of [[marxism|Marxian]] theory — then the Bolsheviks were merely making explicit what had always been implicit. But Russell argued that the abolition of formal legal protections had consequences that the theory did not anticipate.

## Revolutionary Tribunals

The institutions that replaced the Tsarist courts were revolutionary tribunals — bodies composed not of trained judges but of workers, soldiers, and Party members, guided by "revolutionary consciousness" rather than legal precedent. Their decisions were not subject to appeal on legal grounds, and the distinction between criminal law and political repression effectively disappeared.

Russell observed that a legal system in which the state is simultaneously prosecutor, judge, and executor of punishment provides no protection for the individual against the state. The revolutionary tribunals, whatever their theoretical justification, functioned in practice as instruments of political control rather than organs of justice.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's analysis of Bolshevik law reflected his broader concern with the relationship between means and ends. A legal system that subordinated justice to political objectives would, he argued, inevitably produce injustice — regardless of the nobility of the political objectives. The abolition of formal legal protections did not liberate the oppressed; it made everyone vulnerable to arbitrary state power.

This argument was of a piece with Russell's defence of [[liberty]] and [[democracy]]. Law, in Russell's view, was not merely an instrument of class rule — it was a necessary protection for the individual against the overwhelming power of the state. The Bolsheviks had abolished this protection in the name of the very people it was designed to defend.
