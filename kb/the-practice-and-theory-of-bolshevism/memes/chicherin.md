# Georgy Chicherin

Georgy Chicherin (1872–1936) was the Bolshevik People's Commissar for Foreign Affairs, responsible for Soviet diplomacy during the critical early years of the regime. Bertrand Russell cited him in his 1920 account as a key illustration of how Bolshevik political language diverged from ordinary usage — particularly in its elastic definition of the "proletariat."

## Russell's Critique of Bolshevik Class Theory

Russell used Chicherin as a central example in his analysis of the gap between Bolshevik terminology and social reality. He argued that when Bolsheviks spoke of the "dictatorship of the proletariat," the word "dictatorship" was meant literally but "proletariat" was used "in a Pickwickian sense" [001-014]. By this Russell meant that the term had been redefined to mean not the industrial working class as a whole, but rather "the 'class-conscious' part of the proletariat, i.e., the Communist Party" [001-014].

> When a Russian Communist speaks of dictatorship, he means the word literally, but when he speaks of the proletariat, he means the word in a Pickwickian sense. He means the "class-conscious" part of the proletariat, i.e., the Communist Party. He includes people by no means proletarian (such as Lenin and Chicherin) who have the right opinions, and he excludes such wage-earners as have not the right opinions, whom he classifies as lackeys of the bourgeoisie. [001-014]

The inclusion of Chicherin — an aristocrat by birth and a diplomat by profession — alongside Lenin as an example of someone "by no means proletarian" highlighted the ideological rather than sociological basis of Bolshevik class categorisation. Russell's critique was not that Chicherin was unfit for office, but that the Bolshevik theoretical framework obscured the actual social composition of its ruling class through linguistic sleight of hand.

## Role in Bolshevik Diplomacy

As People's Commissar for Foreign Affairs, Chicherin represented Soviet Russia to the outside world during a period of international isolation and hostility. His aristocratic background — he came from a family of Russian diplomats — made him an improbable Bolshevik diplomat, and it was precisely this contradiction that Russell found revealing. That a man of Chicherin's background could be classified as "proletarian" because he held "the right opinions" demonstrated the primacy of ideological conformity over class origin in the Bolshevik system.

Russell drew a broader parallel between the Communist Party's role in Soviet Russia and the guardians in Plato's Republic, observing that "the Communist Party corresponds to the guardians" in the Platonic scheme [001-017]. Within this framework, figures like Chicherin were the functional equivalent of philosopher-kings — selected not for their class background but for their possession of correct doctrine. Russell regarded this system as a revival of an ancient anti-democratic tradition, repackaged in the language of proletarian revolution.
