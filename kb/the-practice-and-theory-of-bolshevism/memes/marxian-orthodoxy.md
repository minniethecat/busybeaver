# Marxian Orthodoxy

**Marxian orthodoxy** — the treatment of [[karl-marx|Marx]]'s writings as an infallible body of doctrine rather than a set of testable hypotheses — was, in Bertrand Russell's analysis, the intellectual foundation of Bolshevik authoritarianism and the "essential tragedy" of the Russian Revolution. Russell argued that Bolshevism had transformed [[marxism]] from a scientific theory into a "religion, with elaborate dogmas and inspired scriptures" (P01-007).

## From Science to Religion

Russell's most distinctive contribution to the critique of Bolshevism was epistemological. He did not primarily object to the *content* of Marxian doctrine — he acknowledged that much of Marx's critique of [[capitalism]] was valid, and he shared the Marxian commitment to economic justice. His objection was to the *attitude* with which the Bolsheviks held their beliefs.

A scientific theory, Russell argued, is held provisionally — it is always open to revision in light of evidence. The Bolsheviks, by contrast, treated Marx's texts as revealed truth. "The texts of Marx and Engels are treated as infallible; dissent is heresy; doubt is apostasy" (001-030). This transformation from hypothesis to dogma was, Russell believed, the mechanism through which a liberating doctrine became a system of oppression.

## Consequences

Russell identified several destructive consequences of Marxian orthodoxy:

**Suppression of dissent.** If Marxian theory is infallible truth, then those who disagree are not merely mistaken — they are enemies of truth itself. This logic justified the suppression of [[mensheviks|Mensheviks]], [[anarchism|anarchists]], and other socialist critics whose disagreement was rooted in alternative interpretations of the same Marxian tradition.

**Immunity to evidence.** A doctrine that cannot be falsified cannot be corrected. Russell argued that the Bolsheviks' immunity to contrary evidence — the failure of [[war-communism|War Communism]], the resistance of the [[peasantry]], the collapse of industry — prevented the learning that might have moderated their policies.

**The personality cult.** Russell noted with discomfort the veneration of [[lenin|Lenin]] and [[trotsky|Trotsky]] as prophetic figures — "Moses and Aaron, deliverers of their people" (001-039). The transformation of political leaders into objects of religious devotion was, for Russell, a symptom of the deeper transformation of politics into faith.

## Russell's Alternative

Russell's alternative to Marxian orthodoxy was not anti-Marxism but intellectual humility. He advocated a [[socialism]] that was "experimental, democratic, and respectful of individual liberty" — a politics that treated its commitments as hypotheses to be tested rather than dogmas to be enforced. This epistemological modesty was, for Russell, the essence of the scientific spirit and the foundation of democratic governance.
