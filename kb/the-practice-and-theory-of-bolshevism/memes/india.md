# India

**India** figured in Bertrand Russell's analysis of [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] primarily as the largest and most significant target of Bolshevik anti-colonial strategy — and as a case study in the contradictions of [[imperialism]].

## Colonial Context

At the time Russell was writing in 1920, India was under British colonial rule. The [[world-war|World War]] had stimulated Indian nationalist movements, and the Bolshevik Revolution — with its explicit commitment to anti-imperialism — found a receptive audience among some Indian radicals.

## Russell's Perspective

Russell regarded the Bolshevik appeal to colonial peoples — dramatised at the 1920 [[baku-congress|Baku Congress]] — as politically astute but ideologically instrumental. The Bolsheviks sought to weaken the British Empire by encouraging Indian unrest, but Russell questioned whether their commitment to colonial liberation was genuine or merely tactical.
