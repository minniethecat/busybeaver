# October Revolution

The **October Revolution** was the decisive Bolshevik seizure of power on 7 November 1917 (25 October in the Julian calendar), which overthrew the Provisional Government of [[kerensky|Alexander Kerensky]] and established the Council of People's Commissars under [[lenin|Vladimir Lenin]]. Russell presents this event as the second and decisive act of the [[russian-revolution|Russian Revolution]]'s drama, the moment when [[bolshevism]] transitioned from revolutionary theory to governing reality. His analysis, written three years after the event, traces a "tragic fatality" that has unfolded in its wake — an inner failure proceeding "by inevitable stages" despite outward success (002-100).

## Overview

The Bolsheviks acted with speed and military precision on the night of 6–7 November 1917, capturing key points in Petrograd — the Winter Palace, telegraph offices, railway stations, and bridges — with relatively little bloodshed. Their success rested on the Provisional Government's catastrophic loss of legitimacy, particularly after its decision to continue Russia's participation in the [[world-war|Great War]], and on the disciplined organization of the Bolshevik [[communist-party|Party]] and its Military Revolutionary Committee under [[trotsky|Leon Trotsky]].

### The Night of Seizure

Russell captures the drama of this moment in his description of a revolutionary pageant staged in Petrograd:

> The Kerensky Government assumed control and drove them forth to war again, but soon they returned to the charge, destroyed the Provisional Government, and hoisted all the emblems of the Russian Soviet Republic. (001-053)

## Russell's Account

Russell treats the October Revolution as the inauguration of an unprecedented political experiment. He dates the entire trajectory of Soviet Russia from this moment:

> Since the revolution of October, 1917, the Soviet Government has been at war with almost all the world, and has had at the same time to face civil war at home. (002-052)

### The Tragic Trajectory

Russell's assessment is marked by a profound ambivalence. He acknowledges the revolution's heroic dimension while warning of a tragic trajectory:

> Over the whole development of Russia and of Bolshevism since the October revolution there broods a tragic fatality. In spite of outward success the inner failure has proceeded by inevitable stages. (002-100)

### The Psychological Root

He identifies the core problem as a psychological one rooted in "the Bolshevik outlook on life: in its dogmatism of hatred and its belief that human nature can be completely transformed by force" (002-100). The revolution, born of a liberatory impulse, had produced a regime that Russell found in many respects more oppressive than the autocracy it replaced.

## The Mechanics of Seizure

Russell's account, supplemented by his broader analysis, reveals the Bolshevik strategy of revolution:

| Element | Bolshevik Approach | Russell's Critique |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Timing** | Wait for the "propitious moment when the Government is unpopular" (002-046) | Makes revolution dependent on government failure, not positive consent |
| **Method** | Seize "the Central Telegraph and the railway stations" — the instruments of state power | Military coup, not popular uprising |
| **Justification** | The vanguard party acts on behalf of the proletariat | Party substitutes its will for the class it claims to represent |
| **Aftermath** | Suppress all opposition through dictatorship, "until the relics of capitalist institutions will have been obliterated" (002-047) | Open-ended repression with no mechanism for returning power |

### The Strategy of Coup

Russell is particularly struck by the Bolsheviks' frankness about their strategy. Unlike previous revolutionaries who claimed to represent the majority, the Bolsheviks openly avowed that they were a minority acting in what they believed to be the majority's true interests. This candour, Russell argues, makes the strategy self-defeating:

> This whole Bolshevik theory of revolution by a minority is one which might just conceivably have succeeded as a secret plot, but becomes impossible as soon as it is openly avowed and advocated. (002-036)

## Bolshevik Perspective

From the Bolshevik standpoint, the October Revolution was the practical fulfillment of [[karl-marx|Marxian]] theory — the long-awaited moment when the proletariat, led by its vanguard party, "expropriated the expropriators." Russell describes the Bolshevik view of revolution as a quasi-military operation:

1. The Party waits for a moment of government weakness and popular discontent
2. It seizes the instruments of state power — communications, transport, military command
3. Having secured the state, it uses the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] to suppress counter-revolution and gradually transform society
4. Once capitalist institutions have been "obliterated," it becomes "possible to restore freedom" (002-047)

### Lenin's Faith

Russell found [[lenin]] to possess "religious faith in the Marxian gospel, which takes the place of the Christian martyr's hopes of Paradise," and noted that Lenin "would sacrifice Russia rather than the revolution, if the alternative should ever arise" (001-021). This absolute commitment to the revolution as an end in itself, independent of its consequences for actual Russians, was for Russell the deepest flaw in the Bolshevik world-view.

## Significance

Russell considers the October Revolution a watershed not only for Russia but for global politics. He writes that "most other westerners who have written on Russia since the October Revolution" have shared his difficulty in forming reliable judgments, and that "Bolshevism is a matter of such importance that it is necessary, for almost every political question, to define one's attitude in regard to it" (P01-009).

### Global Dimensions

The revolution's global significance, in Russell's analysis, operates on multiple levels:

- **Ideological**: It demonstrated that a Marxist party could seize and hold state power, transforming communism from a theoretical critique into a governing reality
- **Strategic**: It provided a model of revolution through disciplined minority action that was studied and emulated worldwide through the [[third-international|Third International]]
- **Psychological**: It "created a hope" among the oppressed and disenfranchised that was, Russell believed, "essential to the realization of Communism in the future" (P01-003)
- **Cautionary**: It illustrated, for Russell, the danger of pursuing noble ends through brutal means — a lesson he considered urgently relevant to Western socialists

## Key Participants

| Participant | Role | Fate |
|------------|------|------|
| [[lenin|Vladimir Lenin]] | Architect and supreme commander of the insurrection | First head of Soviet government; died 1924 |
| [[trotsky|Leon Trotsky]] | Organizer of the Military Revolutionary Committee | Builder of the [[red-army|Red Army]]; later exiled and assassinated |
| [[kerensky|Alexander Kerensky]] | Prime Minister of the overthrown Provisional Government | Fled Petrograd; died in exile |
