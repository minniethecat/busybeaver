# Nikolai Yudenich

Nikolai Yudenich (1862–1933) was a Russian general of the White Army who commanded the Northwestern Army in an offensive against [[petrograd|Petrograd]] during the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]]. He appears in Bertrand Russell's account as one of the principal military opponents of the Bolshevik regime.

## Role in Bolshevik Propaganda

Russell mentioned Yudenich in his analysis of Bolshevik cultural policy and [[art|propaganda art]]. He observed that artists during the civil war were pressured to direct their creative output exclusively against the enemies of the regime: "To force such a man, however, to turn his talent only against Denikin, Yudenich and Kolchak, or the leaders of the Entente, is momentarily good for [[communism|Communism]], but it is discouraging to the artist, and may prove in the long run bad for art, and possibly for Communism also" [001-037].

> To force such a man, however, to turn his talent only against Denikin, Yudenich and Kolchak, or the leaders of the Entente, is momentarily good for Communism, but it is discouraging to the artist, and may prove in the long run bad for art, and possibly for Communism also. [001-037]

Russell's observation highlighted the tension between the Bolshevik regime's propaganda needs and the autonomy required for genuine artistic creation. Yudenich, along with Denikin and Kolchak, served as a stock enemy figure in Soviet propaganda of the period.
