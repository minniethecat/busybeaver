## Overview

World War I was the cataclysmic global conflict that reshaped the political order of Europe and directly precipitated the Russian Revolution. Russell, writing in 1920, refers to it repeatedly as "the late war" and "the Great War." He argues that the war destroyed the legitimacy of traditional institutions across Europe and created the revolutionary mood upon which Bolshevism fed. "The war destroyed the hold upon men's minds of everything merely traditional," he writes, noting that the war's devastation and the collapse of industry left populations open to radical alternatives. (001-005)

The war's effect on Russia was particularly acute. Russell observes that Russia entered the conflict as "one of the nations that were defeated in the war," which "produced a set of circumstances resembling those found in Germany and Austria." (001-008) The resulting food shortages, economic collapse, and military humiliation rendered the Tsarist regime unsustainable.

## Russell's Account

Russell sees the war as the great accelerator of all the forces that produced Bolshevism. The war had "left throughout Europe a mood of disillusionment and despair" which made the Bolshevik promise of rapid transformation attractive. He warns that the "loosening of restraints produced by the late war, and combined with a deliberate inculcation of disrespect for law and constitutional government" could produce "a state of affairs in which it would be habitual to murder men for a crust of bread." (002-042)

> "The Bolshevik outlook is the outcome of the cruelty of the Tsarist regime and the ferocity of the years of the Great War, operating upon a ruined and starving nation maddened into universal hatred." (002-101)

Russell's analysis links the war directly to the psychology of Bolshevism. He argues that the Bolshevik mentality was forged in the crucible of war and desperation: hatred and violence, once normalized by four years of industrialized slaughter, became the tools of revolutionary politics.

## Bolshevik Perspective

For the Bolsheviks, the war was both opportunity and vindication. They had opposed the war from its outbreak as an imperialist struggle between rival capitalist powers. Russell records the dramatic representation of this view in the pageant of the World Commune: the leaders of the Second International were portrayed as "decrepit professorial old men" who refused the Red Flag and "weakly received patriotic flags from their respective governments." (001-052)

The Bolsheviks drew a direct lesson from the war: the capitalist powers would never surrender peacefully; armed conflict was inevitable. Russell summarizes their position: "Capitalists, we are assured, will stick at nothing in defence of their privileges...as soon as the proletariat make a really vital attack upon the capitalists, they will be met by guns and bayonets." (002-045)

## Significance

The war's most consequential outcome, from Russell's perspective, was not the territorial settlement but the psychological and moral devastation it wrought. He argues that the war demonstrated the bankruptcy of the old order while simultaneously normalizing the very violence the Bolsheviks would employ. The [[revolution]] in Russia was not an accident but a direct consequence of the war's destructive logic. Russell offers a sobering warning: "Those who realize the destructiveness of the late war, the devastation and impoverishment, the lowering of the level of civilization throughout vast areas, the general increase of hatred and savagery...will hesitate to incur inconceivably greater horrors." (002-056)

## Related Figures

Russell does not discuss individual wartime leaders at length in the book, but the war's aftermath structures his entire analysis. The [[entente]] powers -- Britain, France, and their allies -- appear as the representatives of the old capitalist order, whose blockade and military intervention against Soviet Russia prolonged the suffering.
