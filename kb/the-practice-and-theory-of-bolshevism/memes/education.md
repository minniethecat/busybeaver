# Education

## Definition

Education, as analyzed by Russell in his 1920 study of Bolshevism, refers to the Bolshevik programme for the rearing and instruction of children and adults in Soviet Russia — encompassing crèches and progressive schools for the young, technical and political training for adults, and the systematic inculcation of Communist doctrine. Russell treats education as one of the most important and revealing domains of Bolshevik practice, a sphere in which the revolution's highest aspirations and deepest contradictions are laid bare.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell opens his discussion of education by noting that "in art and in education at least they have made great progress" (001-034) — a claim that had been widely circulated by sympathetic Western observers. He proceeds to examine this claim with characteristic scepticism. He acknowledges the genuine achievements of the Bolshevik educational programme. He visited crèches and children's homes "where Montessori methods prevail, where the pupils cultivate their little gardens, model in plasticine, draw and sing and act, and dance their Eurythmic dances barefoot on floors once sacred to the tread of the nobility" (001-058). He reports that the children were "bright-eyed and merry, and the rooms airy and clean" (001-058) and describes a performance by schoolchildren in Moscow that included "some quite wonderful Eurythmic dancing" (001-058).

But Russell insists that this favourable surface impression conceals deeper problems. The Bolshevik educational programme operates from the premise "that children should be delivered up almost entirely to the State" (001-059), with the link between mother and child deliberately weakened. Many of the schools that had existed under the old regime in provincial towns and villages had been closed — "in part, it would seem, from lack of personnel, and in part from fear of counterrevolutionary propaganda" (001-060) — so that "on the whole there would seem to be less diffusion of child education than before" (001-060).

Russell's most serious criticism concerns the ideological content of Bolshevik education. He describes how children are taught Communism "emotionally and fanatically, with an appeal to hate and militant ardour rather than to constructive reason" (001-061). This, Russell argues, "binds the free intellect and destroys initiative" (001-061). An industrial state requires not only obedient workers but also men and women capable of scientific research and independent thought. "It is idle to provide channels for scientific research later if it is to be choked at the source. That source is an enquiring and free intellect unhampered by iron dogma" (001-061).

The relationship between education and industrial development forms the climax of Russell's analysis. He predicts that "sooner or later education in Russia must become subordinate to the needs of industrial development" (001-062). The practical constraints are severe: "The number of people in Russia who can even read and write is extremely small, the need to get them employed industrially as rapidly as possible is very great, hence the system of education which develops out of this situation cannot be very ambitious or enlightened" (001-063). In adult education, the pupil "comes for a short period, learns Communism, reading and writing — there is hardly time to give him much more — and returns to leaven the army or his native village" (001-063).

> This teaching of Communism, however necessary it may appear for the building of the Communist state of the future, does seem to me to be an evil in that it is done emotionally and fanatically, with an appeal to hate and militant ardour rather than to constructive reason. It binds the free intellect and destroys initiative. (001-061)

## Bolshevik Perspective

For the Bolsheviks, education was inseparable from the creation of the new Communist society. Russell observed that the Bolsheviks had placed their hopes in the rising generation, explicitly acknowledging that "there is little hope for those who are already adult" and that "happiness can only be achieved by children who are educated as Communists from the start" (001-006). The Bolsheviks regarded the family as a potentially counterrevolutionary institution and sought to transfer the primary responsibility for child-rearing from parents to the state.

At the same time, the Bolsheviks understood that education must serve economic development. The Party Congress resolution that Russell quotes makes "technical education, and administrative and scientific technical experience" (001-085) a matter of equal priority with political propaganda. The Bolsheviks were not indifferent to intellectual excellence — they sought it — but they were convinced that excellence could and must be achieved within the framework of Communist orthodoxy.

## Historical Context

The educational situation Russell describes reflects the extraordinary ambitions and constraints of early Soviet Russia. The Bolsheviks inherited a country with mass illiteracy and a minimal educational infrastructure outside the major cities. They launched an ambitious literacy campaign and created new institutions — workers' faculties (rabfaks), children's colonies, experimental schools — that represented a genuine break with Tsarist educational traditions. Russell's analysis captures the moment when this programme was in its earliest stages, before the full consolidation of Soviet educational doctrine and long before the achievements of the Stalin-era literacy campaigns.

## Related Concepts

- **[[religion]]** — The dogmatic character of Communist teaching, which Russell compares to religious instruction in its emotional and authoritarian methods (001-061)
- **[[industrialism]]** — The economic imperative that Russell argues will inevitably subordinate education to the demands of production (001-062)
