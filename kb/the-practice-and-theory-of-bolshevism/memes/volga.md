# Volga

## Overview

The Volga River served as Russell's route into the Russian countryside during his 1920 visit. After sixteen days in Moscow and Petrograd, Russell and the British Labour Delegation travelled down the Volga from Nizhny Novgorod to Saratov, stopping at towns and villages to speak freely with the inhabitants. This journey, which Russell considered "extraordinarily instructive," provided his most direct contact with the Russian peasantry -- roughly eighty-five percent of the population -- and offered a window into economic conditions beyond the Bolshevik-controlled cities.

## In Russell's Narrative

The decision to travel the Volga came from the delegation's desire "to see something of the country, and to get in touch with the peasants." The Bolshevik government accommodated this with what Russell describes as "the greatest kindness." (001-013) The journey allowed Russell to learn "the unofficial point of view" among ordinary Russians.

The river itself became a setting for Russell's observations on the Soviet fuel and transport crisis. "All that I saw on the Volga made me believe that real efficiency and economy in the Government could have more than doubled the available fuel supply for next winter." (001-088) He recorded seeing "troops of thousands of horses on the Volga, which apparently belonged to Kalmyk tribes," suggesting that with better organisation, the transport of food and timber to the starving cities should have been feasible. (001-089)

Russell also noted the Volga basin's role in the broader economy: the recovery of Baku oil had produced "a revival along the Volga basin," connecting the Caspian Sea to the heart of Russia. (002-079) The river was central to regional trade, with goods from Persia and Turkestan being "loaded on to Volga steamers." (001-128)

## Role in Bolshevism

The Volga was not a political centre but a vital economic artery whose condition illustrated the practical failures of Bolshevik governance. The presence of thousands of horses suggested that the fuel crisis was not simply scarcity but mismanagement -- "if greater efficiency had existed in the Government, the food and fuel difficulties could not have been considerably alleviated." (001-089) The Volga journey also revealed the disconnection between the urban Bolshevik regime and the vast peasant majority. Peasants along the river "take no interest in war or government," Russell observed, in an economic system biased toward the cities. (001-120)

## Russell's Observations

Russell's Volga journey was, by his own account, the most instructive part of his visit. Free from the official programme in Moscow and Petrograd, he spoke directly with ordinary Russians. The experience reinforced his view that the Bolshevik government was an urban-predator state "encamped amid a peasant nation." (001-123) The thousands of horses, the revival potential with Baku oil, and the regional agricultural capacity all pointed to possibilities that the regime was failing to realise. His Volga experience stands as the empirical foundation for his critique that Bolshevism's hardships were not merely the result of war and blockade but also of administrative incompetence.
