# Third International (Comintern)

## Overview

The Third International, also known as the Comintern or Communist International, was the international organization founded by the Bolsheviks in 1919 to promote world revolution and unite communist parties under a single revolutionary program. Russell encountered the Third International at the height of its early influence, attending its Second Congress in Petrograd in July 1920. He describes it as "an organization which exists to promote the class-war and to hasten the advent of revolution everywhere" (001-020), placing it at the very center of the Bolshevik project for global transformation.

The Third International served as the doctrinal and organizational hub of the world communist movement. Unlike its predecessors — the First International (Marx's International Workingmen's Association) and the Second International (the pre-war socialist grouping) — the Third International was built on the principle of a highly centralized vanguard party model, demanding strict adherence to the Bolshevik line as a condition of membership.

## Role in the Bolshevik System

The Third International functioned as the Bolsheviks' primary instrument for projecting revolutionary power beyond Russia's borders. Russell observed firsthand the elaborate apparatus of propaganda and spectacle through which the International cultivated revolutionary sentiment. The opening of the Third International Congress in Petrograd was marked by a mass pageant of the "World Commune," which Russell describes as "a mystery play designed by the High Priests of the Communist faith to instruct the people" (001-050). This fusion of theatrical propaganda with political organization was characteristic of the International's methods.

The doctrinal demands of the International were formidable. Russell reports his conversation with a leading Bolshevik in which he inquired whether advocacy of violent revolution was "an indispensable condition of joining the Third International" (001-025). The answer, he notes with dissatisfaction, was evasive — the Bolshevik leadership demanded theoretical orthodoxy as well as practical commitment, including acceptance of the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] as a literal dictatorship, not a euphemism for representative government.

Russell also documents the International's programmatic documents, including Lenin's "First Sketch of the Theses on National and Colonial Questions," presented to the Second Congress (001-130). This thesis outlined a strategy of supporting nationalist movements in Asia — not to bring them [[communism]] immediately, but to weaken the Western imperial powers. As Russell notes, the Asiatic policy "plays a larger part in the schemes of the leading Bolsheviks than is realized by the Labour Party in this country" (001-138).

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's verdict on the Third International is sharply critical, grounded in his broader rejection of revolutionary violence as a method of social transformation. He writes:

> I find myself definitely and strongly opposed to it. The Third International is an organization which exists to promote the class-war and to hasten the advent of revolution everywhere. My objection is not that capitalism is less bad than the Bolsheviks believe, but that Socialism is less good, not in its best form, but in the only form which is likely to be brought about by war. (001-020)

His objection is consequentialist rather than moralistic: the certain evils of civil war — hatred, suspicion, cruelty, and the destruction of civilization — outweigh the problematical gains of revolutionary victory. He fears that a "universal class-war foreshadowed by the Third International" (002-042) would unleash destructive forces that no political outcome could justify.

Russell also expresses skepticism about the International's methods. "I do not believe that the methods of the Third International can lead to the desired goal" (P01-005), he states, attributing the Bolsheviks' failures to "an impatient philosophy, which aims at creating a new world without sufficient preparation in the opinions and feelings of ordinary men and women" (P01-005). For Russell, the International's insistence on minority-led violence was not merely a tactical error but a philosophical one — a failure to recognize that enduring social change requires democratic persuasion, not armed coercion.

## Key Figures

- **Vladimir Lenin** — Principal architect of the Third International, author of the Theses on National and Colonial Questions (001-130)
- **Leon Trotsky** — Leading Bolshevik figure whose military precision and revolutionary rhetoric exemplified the International's spirit
