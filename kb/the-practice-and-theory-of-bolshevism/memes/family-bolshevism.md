# Family under Bolshevism

The **family under Bolshevism** underwent a transformation that was, in Bertrand Russell's assessment, among the most radical experiments in social legislation ever attempted. The Bolsheviks sought to abolish the traditional patriarchal family as a legal institution and to replace it with new forms of social organisation that would liberate women, protect children, and dissolve the economic dependencies that had historically sustained family structures.

## Bolshevik Family Law

Russell noted that the Bolsheviks had enacted legislation that was extraordinarily progressive by the standards of the time — and indeed remains so by many measures today. The new Soviet family code:
- Established civil marriage as the only legally recognised form
- Made divorce available on demand to either party
- Abolished the legal concept of illegitimacy
- Established the principle of equal pay for equal work regardless of sex
- Created state obligations for child welfare and maternal support

These reforms were grounded in the Marxian analysis of the family as an economic institution. [[friedrich-engels|Engels]] had argued in *The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State* that the monogamous family was a product of private property relations and would be transformed under [[communism]]. The Bolsheviks were attempting to put this theory into practice.

## Material Obstacles

Russell observed that legal reform ran ahead of material possibility. The communal kitchens, laundries, and childcare facilities that were supposed to replace domestic labour remained largely aspirational under conditions of civil war and economic collapse. Russian women, legally liberated, remained burdened by the practical demands of survival in a society where food, fuel, and shelter were desperately scarce (001-071).

Russell also noted a deeper tension: the Bolsheviks' commitment to transforming the family coexisted uneasily with their need for social stability. The [[russian-civil-war|civil war]] and economic disintegration had already disrupted family life on a massive scale — through conscription, displacement, famine, and death. The state's capacity to provide alternative forms of care and support was extremely limited.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell recognised the Bolsheviks' family reforms as genuine achievements in the domain of legal equality. He was less sanguine about their practical effects. The abolition of traditional family structures, without the material conditions to support alternative forms of social organisation, had left many women and children more vulnerable than before.

This assessment exemplified Russell's broader method: measuring Bolshevik achievements not against Western standards but against their own theoretical promises, and finding the gap between the two to be the most revealing fact about the regime.
