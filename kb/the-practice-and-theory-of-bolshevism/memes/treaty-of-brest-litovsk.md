# Treaty of Brest-Litovsk

The **Treaty of Brest-Litovsk** (March 1918) was the peace agreement between Soviet Russia and the Central Powers (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Bulgaria, and the Ottoman Empire) that ended Russian participation in the [[world-war|World War]]. Bertrand Russell discussed the treaty as a pivotal moment that revealed the tension between Bolshevik ideological commitments and the brutal imperatives of state survival.

## The Decision for Peace

The Bolsheviks had come to power in the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]] on a platform of "peace, land, and bread." The war had devastated Russia — millions dead, the economy in ruins, the army disintegrating. Yet making peace with imperial Germany was ideologically agonising for a party that condemned the war as an imperialist conflict and called for its transformation into international civil war.

[[lenin|Lenin]] argued forcefully for accepting German terms, however punitive. Russia, he insisted, had no army capable of resisting a German offensive, and the survival of the Bolshevik regime — the "base" of [[world-revolution|world revolution]] — took precedence over territorial concessions. [[trotsky|Trotsky]], as Commissar for Foreign Affairs, initially pursued a policy of "neither war nor peace," which collapsed when Germany resumed its military advance.

## The Terms

The treaty was devastating. Russia ceded Ukraine, Finland, the Baltic provinces, and the Caucasus — territories containing a third of the empire's population, half its industry, and most of its coal and oil reserves. For a regime that had denounced the "imperialist" character of all Great Power diplomacy, the treaty represented a humiliating accommodation to geopolitical reality.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell treated the Brest-Litovsk episode as emblematic of the contradictions inherent in Bolshevik governance. A party that had denounced secret diplomacy and imperialist annexations was forced, within months of taking power, to accept a peace more punitive than anything imposed on Russia by the Tsarist regime.

Russell did not condemn the Bolsheviks for making peace — he acknowledged that they had no realistic alternative. But he saw in the episode a pattern that would recur throughout Bolshevik rule: the subordination of ideological commitment to the imperatives of state power, and the transformation of internationalist rhetoric into an instrument of Russian national interest.
