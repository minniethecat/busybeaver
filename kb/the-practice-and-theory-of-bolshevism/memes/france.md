# France

**France** appears in Bertrand Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism* primarily as one of the leading Entente Powers whose policy toward Soviet Russia Russell subjected to sustained criticism. French hostility to Bolshevism, Russell argued, was driven less by humanitarian concern than by the defence of financial interests and the fear of revolutionary contagion.

## French Hostility

Russell noted that France was among the most intransigent opponents of the Bolshevik regime. French investors had been the largest foreign holders of Tsarist bonds, and the Bolsheviks' repudiation of Tsarist debt had inflicted heavy losses on the French rentier class. This financial grievance, Russell suggested, was a more powerful motive for French anti-Bolshevism than any principled objection to Bolshevik methods.

France was also the European power most committed to the [[allied-intervention|Allied intervention]] in the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]]. French forces were deployed to Ukraine and Crimea, and France provided substantial material support to the White armies. Russell regarded this policy as both hypocritical and futile.

## Geopolitical Calculations

France's Russia policy was shaped by its broader strategic concerns. The destruction of Germany as a military rival had been France's primary war aim, and French policymakers viewed Bolshevik Russia — weakened, isolated, and renouncing its traditional claims in Eastern Europe — as an acceptable, even desirable, outcome. A strong Russia, whether Tsarist or Bolshevik, had historically been a counterweight to German power; a weak Russia removed that constraint.

Russell was sensitive to these geopolitical calculations, though he did not share them. He argued that the attempt to isolate and destroy Bolshevism by force was both morally wrong and practically counterproductive — strengthening the very regime it aimed to weaken.
