# Imperialism

**Imperialism** occupied a dual role in Bertrand Russell's analysis of [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]]: it was both the enemy the Bolsheviks set out to destroy and — Russell warned — a temptation to which they might themselves succumb.

## The Bolshevik Critique of Imperialism

The Bolsheviks, following [[lenin|Lenin]]'s *Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism*, regarded imperialism as the inevitable final phase of [[capitalism]] — a system in which finance capital, seeking ever-greater returns, drove states into competition for colonies, markets, and spheres of influence. The [[world-war|World War]] was, on this analysis, an imperialist war fought for the benefit of rival capitalist classes. Russell largely accepted this diagnosis of the war's economic roots.

## Russell's Warning

Russell saw that Bolshevik anti-imperialism was strategically potent. "All Asia lies open to Bolshevik ambitions," he noted, observing that Bolshevik influence was spreading through Persia, Turkey, and toward [[india|India]]. (001-128) The Bolsheviks were exploiting the legitimate grievances of colonised peoples against the [[entente|Entente]] powers.

Yet Russell warned of a paradox. He identified three possible outcomes of the Bolshevik experiment, one of which was "the victory of the Bolshevists accompanied by a complete loss of their ideals and a regime of Napoleonic imperialism." (P01-004) A revolutionary state that permanently mobilised against external enemies might, he feared, become imperial in its own right — not capitalist imperialism, but a new form of ideological empire.

## The Baku Strategy

The 1920 [[baku-congress|Baku Congress of the Peoples of the East]] represented the Bolshevik attempt to weaponise anti-imperialism. Russell acknowledged the strategic intelligence of this initiative but questioned its sincerity — was Bolshevik anti-imperialism a genuine commitment to colonial liberation, or an instrument for weakening rival powers?
