# Constituent Assembly

The **All-Russian Constituent Assembly** was the democratically elected representative body that convened in Petrograd in January 1918 — and was dissolved by the [[bolshevik|Bolshevik]] government after a single day. Russell cited this event as a historical watershed: the moment when the Bolsheviks definitively abandoned democratic legitimacy in favour of one-party [[dictatorship|dictatorship]].

## The Election

The elections to the Constituent Assembly were held in November 1917, after the Bolshevik seizure of power. The Socialist Revolutionary Party — representing primarily the peasantry — won a clear majority of seats. The Bolsheviks secured approximately 24% of the vote. The Assembly was thus an authentic expression of popular will — and it returned an anti-Bolshevik majority.

## The Dissolution

The Assembly met on 18 January 1918. After a single day of debate — during which the Bolshevik delegation walked out — it was dissolved by force on Lenin's orders. Russell compared this act to Oliver Cromwell's dissolution of the Rump Parliament: "Both, starting from a combination of democracy and religious faith, were driven to sacrifice democracy to religion enforced by military dictatorship." (001-016)

## Russell's Analysis

For Russell, the dissolution of the Constituent Assembly was not a tactical manoeuvre but a constitutional Rubicon. It demonstrated that the Bolsheviks would tolerate no democratic check on their power — even a check arising from the very "proletariat and peasantry" in whose name they claimed to rule. The event exposed the logic of [[vanguard-party|vanguard party]] doctrine: if the party alone possessed true revolutionary consciousness, then democratic outcomes that contradicted the party were illegitimate by definition.
