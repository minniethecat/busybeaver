# Capitalism

## Definition

Capitalism, as the Bolsheviks and Russell understood it, is an economic system founded on the private ownership of capital and the means of production, in which production and distribution are governed by market competition rather than by collective planning. For Marxists, capitalism is inherently exploitative, creating a class of propertyless wage-earners — the proletariat — whose labour generates surplus value appropriated by the capitalist class. Russell shared the Marxist view that the existing capitalist system was "doomed" (001-005) and that its injustice was "so glaring that only ignorance and tradition could lead wage-earners to tolerate it" (001-005).

## Russell's Analysis

Russell's position on capitalism occupies a distinctive middle ground between outright rejection and qualified acknowledgment. He fully accepts the Marxist indictment: he describes the capitalist system as one in which injustice is structurally embedded, and he concedes that its days are numbered. Yet his critique of capitalism is accompanied by a parallel critique of Bolshevism, which he fears may reproduce many of capitalism's worst features under a different name.

> I think there are two things that must be admitted: first, that many of the worst evils of capitalism might survive under Communism; secondly, that the cure for these evils cannot be sudden, since it requires changes in the average mentality. (002-065)

Russell identifies the Entente powers as the "main citadel of capitalism" with its "golden disc of power" (001-051). He observes that capitalist nations opposed Bolshevism not merely out of ideological antipathy but because they correctly perceived it as a threat to the economic order on which their wealth and power depended.

Despite his sympathy for the communist goal, Russell finds himself "definitely and strongly opposed" (001-020) to the Bolshevik method. His objection is pragmatic rather than ideological: he believes that the certain evils of civil war and violent revolution outweigh the speculative gains, and that a socialism achieved through war will be a deformed socialism. Russell's comparison is not between capitalism at its worst and communism at its best, but between capitalism as it is and communism as it is "likely to be brought about by war" (001-020).

Russell acknowledges that capitalist propaganda and "means of corruption" (002-032) make it difficult for communist movements to gain democratic majorities. The capitalists' control over the press and the public sphere is a genuine obstacle to peaceful transition. Yet Russell does not conclude from this that armed revolution is justified — rather, he argues that revolution would only succeed where a communist majority already exists, making constitutional methods equally viable (002-039).

## Bolshevik Perspective

For the Bolsheviks, capitalism was not merely a flawed system to be reformed but an irredeemable evil to be destroyed. The Communist theory held that the revolution foretold by Marx would "abolish capitalism throughout the world" (001-019). Agreements with capitalist states could only be "makeshifts" (001-019), temporary truces in an ongoing and inescapable conflict between classes.

The Bolsheviks regarded capitalism as a system that corrupted everything it touched — including the labour leaders in constitutional movements whom the Bolsheviks believed had been "bought, directly or indirectly, by the money of the capitalists" (002-034). Revolutionary communism, by contrast, would leave "the capitalists no money with which to attempt corruption" (002-034). Russell notes this argument but questions whether corruption-by-money is the only form of corruption possible in a revolutionary state.

Russell reports that some Bolshevik leaders expressed a kind of grim amusement at the contradictions of capitalism — notably when he discussed the peasantry's reluctance to exchange food for worthless paper currency, a situation that Lenin himself found "comic" (001-026). The Bolsheviks looked to industrialization and electrification as the long-term solutions to these contradictions.

## Historical Context

Russell wrote at a moment when capitalism appeared vulnerable worldwide. The Great War had "destroyed the hold upon men's minds of everything merely traditional" (001-005), and revolutionary movements had erupted across Europe. Russell speculated that "through the influence of America, the capitalist system will linger for another fifty years; but it will grow continually weaker, and can never recover the position of easy dominance which it held in the nineteenth century" (001-005). This prediction, while prescient in its recognition of American capitalism's resilience, underestimated the system's capacity for adaptation.
