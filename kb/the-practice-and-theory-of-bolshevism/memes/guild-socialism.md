# Guild Socialism

**Guild Socialism** was the distinctive British contribution to revolutionary socialist thought in the early twentieth century — and the alternative to [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] that Bertrand Russell himself found most congenial.

## The Guild Idea

Guild Socialists proposed that industry should be organised through national guilds — democratic bodies combining all workers in a given industry, from managers to labourers. These guilds would manage production, while the state — democratically reformed — would represent citizens as consumers and handle non-economic functions. The model drew on medieval guild traditions, syndicalist theory, and the pluralist critique of state sovereignty.

## Russell's Sympathy

Russell was attracted to Guild Socialism because it addressed what he regarded as Bolshevism's central defect: the concentration of both political and economic power in the hands of a single party-state. Guild Socialism, by dividing power between industrial guilds and the political state, offered a structural safeguard against [[dictatorship|dictatorship]] that Leninism lacked. Where the Bolsheviks trusted the [[vanguard-party|party]], Guild Socialists trusted democratic organisation at the point of production.

## Historical Significance

The Guild Socialist movement was small and short-lived, but Russell regarded its ideas as having lasting importance. It demonstrated that the choice facing socialists was not merely between capitalism and Bolshevism — that democratic, decentralised forms of socialist organisation were both conceivable and, in principle, practicable.
