# Class Struggle

## Definition

Class struggle (or class war), in the Marxian sense analysed by Bertrand Russell in *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*, is the doctrine that the fundamental dynamic of history is the conflict between economic classes — ultimately between the bourgeoisie (capitalist class) and the proletariat (working class). The Bolsheviks elevated class struggle from a descriptive theory into a prescriptive programme, using it to justify revolutionary violence, the suppression of opposing classes, and the dictatorship of the Communist Party.

Russell observed that the Bolsheviks understood class war not merely as an objective social reality but as a doctrine to be actively promoted and intensified. "It is customary among the apologists of Bolshevism in the West to excuse its harshness on the ground that it has been produced by the necessity of fighting the Entente and its mercenaries" (001-009), Russell wrote, but he pointed out that "the expectation of such opposition was always part of Bolshevik theory. A general hostility to the first Communist State was both foreseen and provoked by the doctrine of the class war" (001-009).

## Russell's Critique of the Class War Doctrine

Russell's critique of the class war doctrine was both moral and practical. He argued that the doctrine was self-fulfilling: it provoked the very hostility it claimed merely to anticipate. Those who adopted the Bolshevik standpoint, Russell contended, "must reckon with the embittered hostility of capitalist States; it is not worth while to adopt Bolshevik methods unless they can lead to good in spite of this hostility" (001-009).

Russell made a sharp logical point about Bolshevik responsibility: "To say that capitalists are wicked and we have no responsibility for their acts is unscientific; it is, in particular, contrary to the Marxian doctrine of economic determinism" (001-009). In other words, the Bolsheviks could not simultaneously claim that capitalism inevitably produced hostility — a deterministic claim — while disclaiming all responsibility for provoking that hostility.

## Class War and the Third International

The Third International (Comintern) was, in Russell's account, the institutional embodiment of the class war doctrine. He described it as "an organization which exists to promote the class-war and to hasten the advent of revolution everywhere" (001-020). Russell found this deeply troubling because it represented a deliberate intensification of social conflict on a global scale, rather than an attempt to resolve it.

Russell noted that the Bolsheviks saw class struggle as a necessarily violent process. He observed that "violence is in itself delightful to most really vigorous revolutionaries, and they feel no interest in the problem of avoiding it as far as possible. Hatred of enemies is easier and more intense than love of friends" (001-009). This psychological insight — that class war appeals to destructive impulses as much as constructive ones — was central to Russell's critique.

## The Danger to Civilization

Russell's most serious objection to the class war doctrine was that it risked destroying civilization itself. He warned that "the universal class-war foreshadowed by the Third International, following upon the loosening of restraints produced by the late war, and combined with a deliberate inculcation of disrespect for law and constitutional government, might, and I believe would, produce a state of affairs in which it would be habitual to murder men for a crust of bread" (002-040).

Russell drew on historical awareness to underscore the fragility of civilized order. "Every student of medieval thought must have been struck by the extraordinarily high value placed upon law in that period. The reason was that, in countries infested by robber barons, law was the first requisite of progress" (002-040). The Bolshevik class war doctrine threatened to undo centuries of civilizational effort by making violence, rather than law, the normal method of settling disputes.

## An Alternative: Democratic Methods

Against the class war doctrine, Russell proposed democratic government as the alternative. He acknowledged that "democratic government may have all the faults attributed to it, but it has the one great merit that people are, on the whole, willing to accept it as a substitute for civil war in political disputes" (002-040). He warned that "whoever sets to work to weaken this acceptance, whether in Ulster or in Moscow, is taking a fearful responsibility. Civilization is not so stable that it cannot be broken up; and a condition of lawless violence is not one out of which any good thing is likely to emerge" (002-040).

## Inequality of Power vs. Inequality of Wealth

Russell concluded with a broader philosophical objection to Bolshevik theory: it focused too narrowly on economic inequality while ignoring the more fundamental problem of inequality of power. He wrote that "Bolshevik theory seems to me to err by concentrating its attention upon one evil, namely inequality of wealth, which it believes to be at the bottom of all others. I do not believe any one evil can be thus isolated, but if I had to select one as the greatest of political evils, I should select inequality of power" (002-106).

He explicitly denied that the class war and dictatorship of the proletariat could solve the problem of concentrated power, arguing that "only peace and a long period of gradual improvement can bring it about" (002-106). This was Russell's alternative to the class struggle: not denial of social injustice, but a commitment to gradual, peaceful, democratic reform.
