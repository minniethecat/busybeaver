# Revolution

## Definition

Revolution, in the Marxian sense analyzed by Bertrand Russell, designates a complete overthrow of the existing social order — not merely a change of government, but a transformation of the economic foundations of society. The Russian Revolution of 1917 was the first attempt to realize this kind of revolution in practice, and Russell regarded it as "one of the great heroic events of the world's history" (P01-001). Unlike the French Revolution, which it naturally invites comparison with, the Russian Revolution aspired to change not only the structure of society but also "men's beliefs" (P01-001), making it, in Russell's view, of even greater historical importance.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell's analysis of revolution is nuanced and critical. While he acknowledges the heroic character of the Bolshevik revolutionaries and the necessity of a fundamental challenge to the capitalist order, he questions the Bolshevik insistence that violent revolution is the only path to socialism. He found the revolutionary theory of the Third International troubling in its absolutism.

> The Third International is an organization which exists to promote the class-war and to hasten the advent of revolution everywhere. My objection is not that capitalism is less bad than the Bolsheviks believe, but that Socialism is less good, not in its best form, but in the only form which is likely to be brought about by war. (001-020)

Russell observed firsthand the transformation of revolutionary energy into state power. He described a pageant in Petrograd performed for the Third International Congress, in which the story of the Russian Revolution was enacted on the steps of the former Stock Exchange — a building that Russell saw as "a monument to the power of capitalism over the earth" (001-050). The pageant depicted all stages: the Tsarist police, the February Revolution, the Provisional Government under Kerensky, and finally the triumph of the Bolsheviks and the formation of the Red Army (001-053).

A central question Russell posed to Bolshevik leaders was whether revolution could succeed by constitutional means in democratic countries. When he asked about the peculiarity of English conditions, the response he received was "unsatisfactory" (001-025) — the Bolshevik representative admitted there was "little chance of revolution in England now" while insisting that England would "go the same road as Russia" eventually (001-025).

Russell was particularly concerned about the Bolshevik assumption that world revolution was inevitable. He recognized that Lenin was a genuinely international revolutionary, willing to "sacrifice Russia rather than the revolution" (001-021) if the alternative ever arose. Yet Russell also noted that nationalism remained a powerful counter-force even within the Communist movement, observing that nationalism "is natural and instinct[ive]" (001-021).

## Bolshevik Perspective

From the Bolshevik viewpoint, revolution was not merely desirable but historically inevitable. Marx had foretold a revolution that would "abolish capitalism throughout the world" (001-019), and the Bolsheviks believed this revolution had begun in Russia — even though orthodox Marxian theory would have predicted it should begin in the more industrially advanced nations. For the Bolsheviks, every Communist's duty in countries where revolution had not yet occurred was simply "to hasten its advent" (001-019).

The Bolshevik conception of revolution was total: it involved not merely the seizure of political power but the complete reconstruction of economic relations, cultural institutions, and even human psychology. Russell records that the Bolsheviks believed no real good could come to any country "without a bloody revolution" (001-019) and dismissed gradualist approaches as illusory.

In the Bolshevik view, revolution in Russia was a defensive necessity as much as an offensive project. The Soviet Republic found itself "grouped round" by the "struggle of the international bourgeoisie" against it, and so world revolution became both a strategic imperative and an ideological commitment (001-131).

## Historical Context

Russell wrote in 1920, during the Russian Civil War, when the outcome of the Bolshevik revolution was still uncertain. The Entente powers had imposed a blockade and were supporting counter-revolutionary White armies. In this context, Russell noted that much of what Western observers attributed to Communist ideology might actually be attributable to the conditions of war and siege. He cautioned that "grim realities have done much to kill hope" (001-006) among ordinary Russians subject to the Bolshevik regime.

Russell saw parallels between the English Civil War and the Russian Revolution, comparing Lenin's dealings with the Constituent Assembly to Cromwell's with Parliament, and the sincere Communists to "the Puritan soldiers in their stern politico-moral purpose" (001-016). Yet he feared a darker outcome: that the revolution might degenerate into "Napoleonic imperialism" (P01-004).
