# Georges Clemenceau

Georges Clemenceau (1841–1929) was the Prime Minister of France from 1917 to 1920, a leading figure of the Entente powers during the final phase of [[world-war|World War I]] and the subsequent Allied intervention in [[russia|Russia]]. Bertrand Russell invoked Clemenceau in his analysis of why capitalist hostility to [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] was entirely foreseeable.

## Anti-Bolshevik Stance

Russell grouped Clemenceau with Winston Churchill and Alexandre Millerand as Entente leaders whose antagonism toward the Bolshevik regime was both predictable and, from a Marxian perspective, structurally inevitable. He wrote that "other men might have been excused for not foreseeing the attitude of Churchill, Clemenceau and Millerand; but Marxians could not be excused, since this attitude was in exact accord with their own formula" [002-087].

## Russell's Analytical Framework

Russell's point was not to condemn Clemenceau personally but to argue that Bolshevik complaints [[About|about]] capitalist hostility were disingenuous. The [[bolshevik|Bolsheviks]] "certainly did not expect" capitalist governments to be friendly, he noted, because their own deterministic economic theory predicted exactly such opposition [002-087]. Russell observed that however one might "blame Western Governments for their policy, we must realize that, according to the deterministic economic theory of the Bolsheviks, no other policy was to be expected from them" [002-087]. Clemenceau thus served in Russell's argument as an example of the structural forces that Marxian theory itself would have predicted.

> However we may blame Western Governments for their policy, we must realize that, according to the deterministic economic theory of the Bolsheviks, no other policy was to be expected from them. [002-087]
