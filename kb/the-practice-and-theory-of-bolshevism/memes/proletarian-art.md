## Definition

[[art|Proletarian art]], or Proletcult, was the Bolshevik programme of creating a new artistic culture that would express the values and experience of the [[proletariat|working class]], replacing what was seen as bourgeois and aristocratic aesthetic traditions. In Russell's account, it represented both a remarkable recognition of art's importance to revolutionary society and a field where Bolshevik theory encountered stubborn practical realities.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell observed that the [[bolshevik|Bolsheviks]] initially adopted an unusually liberal policy toward artists. In contrast to their control of other social activities, they "allowed the artist, whatever his political creed, complete freedom to continue his work" and "as regards clothing and rations they treated him especially well" (001-035). The theory was "that art and politics belonged to two separate realms," though special honour would be reserved "for those artists who would be inspired by the [[revolution|revolution]]" (001-036).

This policy, however, had unintended consequences. Russell noted that after three years of Bolshevik rule, "the artists have continued to perform the old classics in tragedy or comedy, and the old-style operette" — the revolutionary inspiration the Bolsheviks had hoped for did not materialize (001-037). The hoped-for proletarian art remained largely theoretical, and a "divorce between art and popular feeling" became apparent.

Nevertheless, Russell was not wholly pessimistic. He suggested that the paralysis might be temporary and drew a parallel with medieval church architecture: "Communist artists of the future may erect public buildings surpassing in beauty the medieval churches" (001-039). He also observed a genuine artistic impulse among the Russian peasantry — their carving, embroidery, and spontaneous decoration of railway carriages — which he thought might serve as the authentic foundation for a popular art (001-040).

Russell's broader reflection was that a state struggling with economic survival would inevitably subordinate art to practical needs, and that the professional artist class might diminish. Yet he considered this potentially salutary: "some of the finest work has been done by men and groups of men to whom artistic expression was only a pastime," and a "revival of this attitude to art is one of the good results which may be hoped for from a Communist revolution" (001-043).
