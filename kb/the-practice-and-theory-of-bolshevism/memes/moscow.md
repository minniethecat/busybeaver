# Moscow

**Moscow** was the capital of [[russia|Soviet Russia]] and the nerve-centre of [[bolshevism|Bolshevik]] political power at the time of Russell's visit in May–June 1920. Russell spent eleven of his thirty-six days in Russia there, observing the inner workings of the Soviet regime — from a private hour with [[lenin|Lenin]] in the Kremlin to the stage-managed proceedings of the Moscow Soviet, from the queues that dominated daily life to the guarded optimism of the regime's cultural projects. His account presents Moscow as a city of stark contrasts: the austere simplicity of its Bolshevik leaders set against the grim realities of urban survival under war communism, the declared sovereignty of its Soviet against the actual dictatorship of an unaccountable Presidium.

## Overview

Moscow was, in Russell's account, the political command centre from which the Bolsheviks projected their authority across the vast Russian territory and, through the [[third-international|Third International]], across the world. It was here that Russell had his hour-long conversation with Lenin, finding him "very friendly, and apparently simple, entirely without a trace of hauteur" (001-024). It was here that he attended sessions of the Moscow Soviet, noting how its formal sovereignty concealed the concentration of real power in the hands of a nine-member Presidium of orthodox Communists. And it was here that he observed the texture of daily life under a regime that promised abundance but delivered queues, permits, and the constant threat of arbitrary arrest.

### The Symbolic Capital

Russell's description of the journey that brought him to Moscow captures the city's symbolic weight in the Bolshevik imagination. Petrograd, the former capital and cradle of the [[russian-revolution|revolution]], lay to the north-west; Moscow, the old heart of Russia, had been reclaimed as the seat of the new order. The Bolsheviks, Russell observed, "represent the interests of the urban and industrial population, and [are], as it were, encamped amid a peasant nation" (001-123) — and Moscow was the camp's headquarters.

## In Russell's Narrative

Russell arrived in Moscow as part of the British Labour Delegation and was treated to a carefully managed programme of official events. He describes the atmosphere of his visit as "an amicable contest, degenerating at times into a game of hide and seek" between hosts and visitors (P01-004). While the Bolsheviks staged banquets and revolutionary pageants, Russell sought to explore independently, speaking with "casual people whom I met in the streets or on village greens."

### The Managed Visit

The Bolshevik authorities presented Russell with a curated version of their city, but Russell's sceptical temperament and his determination to look beneath the surface allowed him to construct a more complex picture. His account oscillates between the official programme — banquets, theatrical performances, formal interviews — and the unofficial realities he gleaned from chance encounters and critical observation.

### The Hollow Soviet

His examination of the Moscow Soviet revealed the hollow nature of representative institutions under Bolshevik rule:

> The Moscow Soviet is nominally sovereign in its locality. But the real power lies in the hands of the Presidium, a body of nine, which meets daily and is always composed of orthodox Communists. The whole Soviet meets seldom, and its sole function, so far as I could discover, is to ratify, without discussion, decisions already taken by the Presidium. (001-068, 001-069)

Elections to the Soviet were by show of hands, making opposition voters "marked men." Despite these obstacles, the [[mensheviks|Mensheviks]] managed to win forty of fifteen hundred seats — a testament, Russell notes, to the residual political consciousness of Moscow's large-factory workers, who organized word-of-mouth campaigns despite the risks (001-068).

Kamenev, the President of the Moscow Soviet, "informed us that the method of election was likely to be changed, as the existing method did not give sufficient representation to the Communist Party" (001-070). Russell records this detail without explicit comment, letting the irony speak for itself: an institution that already suppressed opposition through public voting and arbitrary arrest was being reformed to make it *even less* representative.

## Daily Life

Russell's account of daily life in Moscow is carefully balanced. He refuses both the sensationalism of the anti-Bolshevik press and the romanticism of Western sympathizers:

> Daily life in Moscow, so far as I could discover, has neither the horrors depicted by the Northcliffe Press nor the delights imagined by the more ardent of our younger Socialists. (001-105)

### Material Conditions

What he found instead was a regime of pervasive, low-grade hardship:

|--------|----------------------|-----|
| **Housing** | The population had been "concentrated in the centre" through the requisitioning of bourgeois apartments; many lived in cramped communal quarters | 001-105 |
| **Food** | The "food problem is mainly concentrated in Moscow and Petrograd"; the state struggled to induce peasants to supply the cities | 001-118 |
| **Transport** | "It often takes several days to get a seat" on trains; queues for travel permits were ubiquitous | 001-108 |
| **Shopping** | "Queues are a common feature in Moscow"; people waited hours or days for basic goods | 001-108 |

Russell compares life in Moscow to [[london|London]] and finds the contrast instructive: "Life in Moscow, as compared to life in London, is drab and monotonous and filled with endless petty anxieties" (001-113). Yet he also notes moments of unexpected colour: "In Moscow I constantly saw people in the streets who had been to the theatre" (001-114), and the city's cultural life retained a vitality that surprised him.

## Role in Bolshevism

Moscow was, in Russell's analysis, the physical embodiment of Bolshevik power — the place where theory met practice with often uncomfortable results. Its role can be understood along several axes:

1. **Political command centre**: The Kremlin housed Lenin, the Council of People's Commissars, and the Central Committee of the [[communist-party|Communist Party]]. From Moscow, "the dictatorship of Moscow" (001-006) was projected across the vast Russian territory
2. **Headquarters of world revolution**: The Third International (Comintern) was based in Moscow, from which it urged foreign socialists to launch insurrections modelled on the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]] (002-106)
3. **Model of Bolshevik governance**: The Moscow Soviet served as a template for the hollow representative institutions that characterized the Bolshevik system — bodies that met seldom, ratified without discussion, and concentrated real authority in unaccountable executive committees of orthodox Communists
4. **Showcase and fortress**: Moscow was simultaneously a carefully curated showcase for foreign delegations and a fortress-city where political opposition was suppressed by an Extraordinary Commission (Cheka) that operated with virtual impunity

### The Concentration of Power

Russell found the concentration of power particularly striking. A Presidium of nine men, meeting daily and always composed of orthodox Communists, wielded "all real power" in a city of over a million people. This was not, Russell emphasizes, an aberration — it was the logical expression of the Bolshevik theory of the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]], which vested authority not in the working class but in the Party that claimed to represent it.

## Russell's Observations

Russell's final verdict on Moscow is characteristic of his wider assessment of Bolshevism: nuanced, ambivalent, and unwilling to reduce complex realities to simple judgments. He found much to admire — the personal austerity of the Bolshevik leaders, the regime's commitment to education and culture, the genuine revolutionary hope that animated many of its supporters. But he found more to question.

### The Central Contradiction

The city embodied, for Russell, the central contradiction of the Bolshevik experiment: a regime that spoke of liberation while perfecting the techniques of control, that promised abundance while presiding over queues and rationing, that invoked the name of the proletariat while suppressing every expression of working-class opinion that diverged from Party orthodoxy. Moscow was not, in Russell's view, the radiant capital of a new world — but neither was it the "kingdom of Anti-Christ" depicted in the Western press. It was something more troubling: a laboratory of radical politics whose experiments were producing results that even its own architects had not anticipated.
