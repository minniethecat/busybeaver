Jean Jaurès was a French socialist leader, anti-militarist, and one of the most prominent figures of the Second International. His assassination on 31 July 1914, on the eve of the [[world-war|First World War]], made him an enduring symbol of socialist opposition to war.

## Jaurès in Bolshevik Pageantry

Russell witnessed a striking portrayal of Jaurès in the mass pageant of the "World Commune," performed at the opening of the [[internationalism|Third International]] Congress in [[petrograd|Petrograd]] in 1920. In the pageant's depiction of the outbreak of war in July 1914, "the leaders of the Second International, a group of decrepit professorial old men, who waddled in in solemn procession carrying tomes full of international learning" were shown sitting "in a row between the rulers and the people, deep in study, spectacles on nose" [001-052]. When the call to war came, these leaders "refused to accept the Red Flag, but weakly received patriotic flags from their respective governments" [001-052].

Amid this scene of the Second International's collapse, "Jaurès, elevated to be the symbol of protest, towered above the people, crying in a loud voice, but fell back immediately as the assassin's shot rang out" [001-052]. The Bolshevik pageant thus portrayed Jaurès as the sole figure of resistance among the Second International leaders — a tragic hero whose protest was cut short by murder.

The pageant's portrayal was ideologically charged: the [[bolshevik|Bolsheviks]] of the Third International saw the Second International's failure to prevent the war as proof of its bankruptcy, and Jaurès's martyrdom served as both a rebuke to the "social traitors" who supported their national governments and a legitimizing prelude to the Third International's revolutionary program.

## Historical Significance

Jaurès was a towering figure in pre-1914 European [[socialism|socialism]]. As the leader of the French Section of the Workers' International (SFIO), he advocated for democratic socialism, international solidarity, and the general strike as a means to prevent war. His assassination removed the last major obstacle to French socialist support for the war effort, as the SFIO — like most European socialist parties — joined the union sacrée and backed their government.
