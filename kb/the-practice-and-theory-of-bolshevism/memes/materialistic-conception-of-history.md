## Definition

The materialistic conception of history, also known as historical materialism, is the Marxian doctrine asserting that the economic structure of society — the mode of production and the class relations it generates — constitutes the foundation upon which political, legal, and ideological superstructures are built. Historical change, in this view, is driven primarily by the development of productive forces and the resulting class struggles.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell opened his discussion by noting that this doctrine "is due to Marx, and underlies the whole Communist philosophy," profoundly influencing Bolshevik "views as to politics and tactics" (002-002). He immediately observed, however, that "the name does not convey at all accurately what is meant by the theory" (002-002).

Russell's critique centred on a logical distinction he considered crucial. He argued that "philosophic materialism does not prove that economic causes are fundamental in politics" — one could be a philosophical materialist and yet attribute historical causality to climate (like Buckle) or to sex (like Freud) rather than to economics (002-003). Conversely, "economic causes might be at the bottom of all political events even if philosophic materialism were false" — since economic motives could dominate human behaviour regardless of one's metaphysical commitments (002-004). Russell concluded that "there is no logical connection either way between philosophic materialism and what is called the 'materialistic conception of history'" (002-005).

Despite this philosophical critique, Russell acknowledged the theory's practical value. "Treated as a practical approximation, not as an exact metaphysical law, the materialistic conception of history has a very large measure of truth" (002-008). He cited the influence of industrialism on religious belief as a compelling illustration: it was industrialism, "rather than the arguments of Darwinians and Biblical critics, that has led to the decay of religious belief in the urban working class" (002-008).

Russell also warned that "the attempt to base a political theory upon a philosophical doctrine is undesirable" because "the philosophical doctrine of materialism, if true at all, is true everywhere and always" — leading its adherents to become "absolute and sweeping, unable to admit that a general theory may be subject to limitations in its application to special cases" (002-007). This dogmatic tendency, Russell suggested, was among the most dangerous features of Bolshevik political reasoning.
