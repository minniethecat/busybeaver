# Religion

## Definition

In Russell's analysis of Bolshevism, religion operates on two distinct analytical levels. First, there is the Marxist and Bolshevik attitude toward traditional religion — the view, inherited from Marx, that religion is an ideological instrument of class rule that must be superseded. Second, and of far greater importance to Russell's argument, there is his claim that Bolshevism itself functions as a religion: a system of dogmatic beliefs, held with fanatical certainty, that structures the entire conduct of life and is inculcated by emotional and authoritarian rather than intellectual methods. Russell defines a religion as "a set of beliefs held as dogmas, dominating the conduct of life, going beyond or contrary to evidence, and inculcated by methods which are emotional or authoritarian, not intellectual" (001-142).

## Russell's Analysis

Russell devotes sustained attention to what he regards as the fundamentally religious character of Bolshevism. He opens his book by observing that Bolshevism "is not merely a political doctrine; it is also a religion, with elaborate dogmas and inspired scriptures" (P01-007). When Lenin wishes to prove a proposition, Russell notes, he quotes from Marx and Engels with the reverence of a theologian citing sacred texts. The full-fledged Communist is required to accept a constellation of metaphysical beliefs — philosophic materialism, the materialistic conception of history — that "may be true, but are not, to a scientific temper, capable of being known to be true with any certainty" (P01-007).

Russell's most systematic treatment of this theme comes in a passage where he explicitly classifies Bolshevism as a religious rather than a political phenomenon. He argues that the important mental attitudes toward the world divide into the religious and the scientific: "The scientific attitude is tentative and piecemeal, believing what it finds evidence for, and no more" (001-141). Bolshevism, by contrast, operates in the mode of religious certainty, and Russell warns that "those who accept Bolshevism become impervious to scientific evidence, and commit intellectual suicide" (001-142). He regards this as a tragic development, since "almost all the progress in the world from the earliest times is attributable to science and the scientific temper; almost all the major ills are attributable to religion" (001-141).

Russell further refines his classification by distinguishing among types of religion. He argues that Bolshevism is not to be compared with Christianity or Buddhism — which are "primarily personal religions, with mystical doctrines and a love of contemplation" (001-143) — but rather with Islam. "Mohammedanism and Bolshevism are practical, social, unspiritual, concerned to win the empire of this world" (001-143). This comparison allows Russell to suggest a historical trajectory: what Islam did for the Arabs — providing a unifying faith that enabled conquest — Bolshevism may do for Russia. The danger, as Russell sees it, is that just as Islam's spiritual content was in time subordinated to imperial ambition, so Bolshevism's Communist ideals may be eroded by the logic of state power.

> Bolshevism as a social phenomenon is to be reckoned as a religion, not as an ordinary political movement. The important and effective mental attitudes to the world may be broadly divided into the religious and the scientific. (001-141)

The tension between religious and scientific temperaments lies at the heart of Russell's critique. He observes that Lenin's strength comes from "religious faith in the Marxian gospel, which takes the place of the Christian martyr's hopes of Paradise" (001-030). Russell reports going to Russia a Communist but returning with his doubts intensified "a thousandfold" — not about Communism as an aim, but about "the wisdom of holding a creed so firmly that for its sake men are willing to inflict widespread misery" (001-030).

## Bolshevik Perspective

The Bolsheviks, as orthodox Marxists, regarded traditional religion as part of the ideological superstructure that served to keep the working class submissive. Marx's dictum that religion is "the opium of the people" was fundamental to Bolshevik self-understanding. The Soviet state was aggressively secular in its official orientation, suppressing the institutional power of the Russian Orthodox Church while tolerating — and in some cases encouraging — the transformation of religious art and architecture into cultural monuments.

Russell observes, however, that the Bolsheviks were unable to escape the psychological structure of religion even as they repudiated its content. He notes that the claim of scientific certainty was itself a dogmatic posture, incompatible with the genuinely scientific spirit of open inquiry. The Bolsheviks' certainty about the correctness of their doctrine produced what Russell calls a "habit of militant certainty about objectively doubtful matters" (P01-007) — a habit he traces to the medieval Church and regards as deeply regressive.

## Historical Context

Russell wrote at a moment when traditional religious belief in Europe had been profoundly shaken by the war and by the rise of industrial civilization. He observes that "the war has left throughout Europe a mood of disillusionment and despair which calls aloud for a new religion, as the only force capable of giving men the energy to live vigorously" (001-004). Bolshevism, in Russell's analysis, stepped into this spiritual vacuum, offering the comprehensive meaning and purpose that traditional Christianity had once provided. At the same time, Russell notes that industrialism itself — rather than Darwinian arguments — had been the primary force eroding religious belief among urban workers, while ironically "reviving religious belief among the rich" (002-008) as a bulwark against revolutionary movements.
