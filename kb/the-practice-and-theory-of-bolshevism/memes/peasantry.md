# Peasantry

The **peasantry** — the vast rural population of [[russia|Russia]] engaged in agricultural production — was, in Bertrand Russell's analysis, the social class whose relationship with Bolshevism would ultimately determine the fate of the revolution. Constituting "about eighty-five percent of the population," the peasants were too numerous to ignore and too independent to control (001-013).

## The Peasant Question

Russell recognised early in his study that the Bolsheviks faced a fundamental problem with the peasantry. [[communism|Communism]], as an economic doctrine, required the collective ownership of land and the abolition of private property. But the Russian peasant "wants his own land and no Communism" (001-074). The revolution had given peasants the land they had long coveted — and they had no intention of surrendering it to the state.

> Communism is anathema to the peasant, who wants his own land and no Communism. (001-074)

This tension between Bolshevik ideology and peasant aspiration was, Russell argued, the central economic contradiction of the Soviet regime. The government needed grain to feed the cities and the [[red-army|Red Army]], but it could only obtain it through coercion — the system of forced requisitioning that generated widespread rural resistance.

## Russell's Observations

Russell's journey to Russia included travel through the countryside, where he observed the conditions of peasant life firsthand. He noted the contrast between the relatively prosperous appearance of the villages and the desperate poverty of the cities — a paradox explained by the peasants' control of food production and their resistance to state requisitioning (001-071).

The government's response to peasant resistance was twofold: propaganda and coercion. Russell observed that the Bolsheviks directed propaganda at the peasantry, attempting to divide rich peasants (kulaks) from poor peasants and to mobilise the latter against the former (001-027). At the same time, armed detachments were sent into the countryside to seize grain, generating the cycle of resistance and repression that characterised the period of War Communism.

## Artistic Impulse

Russell found one aspect of peasant life genuinely admirable: the vitality of peasant art. "The Russian peasant's artistic impulse is no legend," he wrote, describing the carving, embroidery, and spontaneous decoration that enriched rural life (001-040). He saw in this popular creativity a resource that the Bolsheviks, for all their talk of cultural transformation, were in danger of destroying through the pressures of industrialisation and state control (001-041).

## Historical Significance

Russell's analysis of the peasant question proved prescient. The peasantry's resistance to grain requisitioning was a major factor in the Bolsheviks' decision to abandon War Communism in favour of the New Economic Policy (NEP) in 1921 — the year after Russell's book was published. The tension between Bolshevik modernisation and peasant traditionalism would remain a defining feature of Soviet history, culminating in the horrors of collectivisation under Stalin.
