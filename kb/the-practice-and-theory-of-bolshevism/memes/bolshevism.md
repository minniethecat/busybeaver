# Bolshevism

**Bolshevism**, as examined by [[russell-brandon|Bertrand Russell]] in his 1920 book *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*, is a political system and revolutionary creed that combines the characteristics of a dogmatic [[religion]] with a program of radical social transformation. Russell's analysis distinguishes between Bolshevism as a **revolutionary hope** for the non-Western world and Bolshevism as a **system of governance** in [[russia|Soviet Russia]], offering a nuanced critique that neither fully endorses nor wholly condemns the movement. The concept is central to understanding the political landscape of early 20th-century Europe and the intellectual debates surrounding the [[russian-revolution|Russian Revolution]].

## Definition

In Russell's framework, Bolshevism is not merely a political program but a **comprehensive world-view** that functions as a substitute religion. Writing in the Preface, he observes:

> Bolshevism combines the characteristics of a religious faith with the intolerance and dogmatism characteristic of a militant creed (P01-001)

Russell defines Bolshevism by its dual character: externally, it presents itself as the practical application of [[communism|Marxian Communism]] to Russian conditions; internally, it operates as an **aristocratic and intellectual regime** in which a small vanguard — "not proletarian" in origin — exercises absolute authority over the masses. He explicitly classifies Bolshevism as "a religion" whose "dogmas are accepted not by reason but by faith" (001-142), and compares its historical role to that of Mohammedanism in its early expansionist phase (001-143).

## Russell's Analysis

Russell's treatment of Bolshevism unfolds across two books: Part I examines its **actual operation in Russia**, while Part II subjects its **theoretical foundations** to systematic critique.

### Bolshevism as Revolutionary Hope

Russell acknowledges that Bolshevism has supplied what traditional religion once provided — "the energy to live vigorously" and a sense of collective purpose (001-004). He grants that without the Bolshevik victory, the Russian Revolution "would have been very improbable" and argues that Bolshevism "deserves the gratitude and admiration of all progressive people" for keeping revolutionary hopes alive in a period of European reaction (P01-003).

For the non-Western world especially, Russell sees Bolshevism's appeal as more immediate and less abstract:

> In the non-European World, the hope inspired by Bolshevism is more immediate, less shot through with intellectual doubt (001-007)

This insight reflects Russell's broader analysis of imperialism and colonial liberation, positioning Bolshevism as a rallying point for anti-imperialist movements from [[china|Asia]] to [[india|India]].

### The Critique of Bolshevik Governance

Russell's admiration for Bolshevism's revolutionary energy is tempered by his direct observations of Soviet Russia. He identifies several structural flaws:

| Defect | Manifestation | PN Source |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| **Democratic deficit** | Bolshevik rule is "internally aristocratic and intellectually insular" | 001-018 |
| **Religious dogmatism** | Party doctrine functions as sacred scripture, immune to empirical challenge | 001-003 |
| **Economic naivety** | Bolsheviks underestimate the complexity of industrial organization | 001-122 |
| **Repressive apparatus** | The regime relies on terror rather than consent | 001-009 |

Russell is particularly critical of Western apologists who "excuse its harshness" without having witnessed its effects firsthand. His own visit to Russia in 1920 informs his insistence that Bolshevism's practice diverges sharply from its theoretical promises (P01-009).

### The Two Fundamental Objections

In Part II, Russell crystallizes his critique into two definitive rejections:

> I am compelled to reject Bolshevism for two reasons: First, because the price to be paid for its achievement is too great in terms of human suffering; Second, because I believe that the ends it seeks can be attained by other methods (002-061)

This two-pronged argument — the **humanitarian objection** and the **pragmatic objection** — forms the philosophical core of Russell's case against Bolshevism. He does not reject its ultimate goals (social justice, economic equality) but disputes both the means employed and the necessity of those means.

## Bolshevik Perspective

From the Bolshevik standpoint, as reported by Russell, Bolshevism represents the **first successful implementation of Marxian theory** at a national scale. [[lenin|Vladimir Lenin]] and his followers understood Bolshevism as:

1. The **dictatorship of the proletariat** — a transitional phase between capitalism and full communism, in which the working class, led by its vanguard party, suppresses the former exploiting classes
2. The application of [[karl-marx|Marx's]] materialist conception of history to Russian conditions, with the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]] serving as the decisive rupture
3. A **world-historical mission** — Bolshevism was never intended as a purely Russian phenomenon but as the first spark of a global proletarian revolution, coordinated through the [[third-international|Third International (Comintern)]]

Russell notes the Bolsheviks' genuine conviction that "the end justifies the means" and their belief that the suffering of the transitional period would be vindicated by the eventual establishment of a classless society. He also observes their **intellectual certainty**, which he finds both admirable and dangerous:

> When Lenin wishes to prove some proposition, he quotes Marx, just as a Christian quotes the Bible (P01-007)

## Historical Context

Bolshevism emerged from the specific conditions of **late Tsarist Russia** — an overwhelmingly agrarian society with a weak bourgeoisie and a small but militant industrial working class. The February Revolution of 1917 abolished the monarchy but left unresolved the fundamental questions of land, peace, and economic justice. The Bolshevik seizure of power in October 1917, followed by the [[russian-civil-war|civil war]] of 1918–1921, created the conditions for Bolshevism's transformation from a revolutionary program into a state ideology.

Russell's analysis in 1920 captures a **pivotal moment**: the Bolsheviks had won the civil war but faced economic collapse, international isolation, and growing discontent (as evidenced by the [[kronstadt-rebellion|Kronstadt uprising]] of 1921, which occurred shortly after Russell's visit). His critique anticipates many of the criticisms that would later be leveled against Stalinism, while still engaging with Bolshevism as a living political experiment rather than a completed historical phenomenon.

The [[entente|Allied]] blockade and military intervention (1918–1920) provided the Bolsheviks with a powerful nationalist narrative — that external enemies, not internal contradictions, were responsible for Russia's suffering — and Russell acknowledges that Western policy "was always foolish and self-defeating" (001-129) in its attempt to crush Bolshevism by force.

## Related Concepts

- [[communism]] — The ultimate goal which Bolshevism claims to pursue; Russell distinguishes Bolshevism as a "pioneer method" from communism as an ideal
- [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] — The Marxian concept that Bolshevism claimed to instantiate, which Russell subjects to detailed critique
- [[socialism]] — The broader tradition from which Bolshevism emerged, and against which Russell measures Bolshevism's deviations
- [[imperialism]] — The global system that Bolshevism positioned itself against, and which Russell saw as a competing form of organized violence
- [[religion]] — Russell's recurring analogy for understanding Bolshevism's psychological and sociological function
