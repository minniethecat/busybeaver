# Britain

**Britain** occupies a unique position in Bertrand Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*, not only as one of the major powers whose Russia policy he analysed but as his own country — and the primary audience for his book. Russell's critique of British policy toward Soviet Russia was simultaneously a critique of his own countrymen's moral and political assumptions.

## Divided Policy

British policy toward Bolshevik Russia was, Russell observed, deeply divided. [[churchill|Winston Churchill]], as Secretary of State for War, was the most vociferous advocate of military intervention, famously describing Bolshevism as an "infant monster" to be strangled in its cradle. But Churchill's position was contested within the British government. [[lloyd-george|David Lloyd George]], the Prime Minister, was more cautious, recognising both the military impracticality of full-scale intervention and the political risk of appearing to make war on a regime that had promised peace and land to the Russian people.

Russell himself was directly involved in these debates. His visit to Russia in 1920 was undertaken as part of a Labour Party delegation, and his book was intended to inform British public opinion at a moment when the direction of British policy remained uncertain.

## Russell's Critique

Russell directed his sharpest criticism at the hypocrisy of British anti-Bolshevism. Britain had, he noted, "destroyed trade and industry" during the [[world-war|World War]] — yet British statesmen condemned the Bolsheviks for presiding over economic collapse (001-073). Britain had maintained the [[allied-blockade|blockade]] against Russia, inflicting suffering on civilians — yet British newspapers denounced Bolshevik [[red-terror|terror]].

Russell's point was not to exonerate the Bolsheviks but to insist on consistency: if the standard of judgement was human welfare, then all governments — including Britain's — stood condemned.

## The Labour Movement

Russell's book was also an intervention in British domestic politics. The British labour movement was divided over Bolshevism: some saw it as a model to emulate, others as a warning to avoid. Russell sought to provide a balanced account that would inform rather than inflame this debate, distinguishing between Bolshevism's critique of [[capitalism]] (which he largely shared) and its authoritarian methods (which he unequivocally rejected).
