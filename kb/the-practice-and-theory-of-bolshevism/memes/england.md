# England

## Overview

England serves a dual function in Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*: it is his home country and his principal comparative reference point for evaluating the applicability of Bolshevik methods to advanced industrial democracies. Russell measures Russian conditions against English circumstances throughout, arguing that England's political peculiarities make the Bolshevik model both unnecessary and dangerous. England appears as a constant counterpoint -- the stable democracy whose Labour movement Russell hoped would pursue socialism through constitutional means.

## In Russell's Narrative

Russell introduces England early when considering international hostility, drawing a parallel between the Entente's treatment of Soviet Russia and "England to Ireland and India." (P01-007) His most extended reflections concern the prospects for revolution. He asked Lenin directly "how far he recognized the peculiarity of English economic and political conditions." Lenin admitted "there is little chance of revolution in England now, and that the working man is not yet disgusted with Parliament." (001-025) This concession confirmed that even the supreme Bolshevik leader recognised England's political distinctiveness.

In discussing the conditions for a Communist revolution, Russell uses England as a thought-experiment. "If a Communist revolution were to occur in England tomorrow, immediately America would place an embargo on all trade with us. The cotton industry would collapse, leaving about five million of the most productive portion of the population idle." (002-105) This leads to his broader thesis that "in every country except America, a successful Communist revolution is impossible without the approval of the Anglo-Saxon nations." (002-106)

Russell also notes England's industrial advantage: "in this respect Russia was very badly off, whereas England and America would be much more favourably situated," owing to the wider diffusion of technical education among English wage-earners. (002-109)

## Role in Bolshevism

England occupied an ambiguous position in Bolshevik thinking. As the headquarters of global capitalism and the dominant naval power, it was both a strategic threat and a target for revolutionary agitation. Russell records his hope "that in England this may be avoided through the hold which democratic and constitutional habits have upon the great majority," referring to the risk of Bolshevism degenerating into oligarchy. (001-144) The Bolsheviks expected that a Labour Government in England would be useful to their cause, while regarding "peace with capitalist England" as impossible unless the communist programme were abandoned. (001-019) (001-140)

## Russell's Observations

Russell is fundamentally optimistic about democratic gradualism in England. He argues that English political culture -- parliamentary governance, civil liberties, an independent labour movement -- provides conditions for a peaceful transition to socialism. "I have hopes that in England this may be avoided through the hold which democratic and constitutional habits have upon the great majority." (001-144)

He warns that English socialists who uncritically admire Bolshevism are making a category error, applying methods suited to a peasant-majority country to an advanced industrial democracy. The Bolshevik model would be "unnecessarily retrograde" for England. (001-144) Russell's England is a mirror held up to Russia, showing the virtues of the constitutional path against the danger of mistaking Russian necessities for universal principles.
