## Overview

The Russian Civil War was the protracted armed conflict that followed the Bolshevik seizure of power in October 1917, pitting the Red Army against a shifting coalition of anti-Bolshevik forces -- the White armies under generals such as [[denikin|Anton Denikin]] and Admiral Kolchak, supported by military intervention from the [[entente]] powers. Russell, who visited Russia in 1920 during the war's final stages, observed its effects firsthand and viewed the conflict as an integral consequence of Bolshevik methods.

Russell is clear that the civil war and foreign intervention were not accidental: "Since the revolution of October, 1917, the Soviet Government has been at war with almost all the world, and has had at the same time to face civil war at home. This is not to be regarded as accidental, or as a misfortune which could not be foreseen. According to Marxian theory, what has happened was bound to happen." (002-052)

## Russell's Account

Russell's firsthand observations reveal the war's devastating material consequences. Traveling down the Volga, he witnessed the economic collapse that the war had accelerated. The Donets Basin, a crucial coal-mining region, had been "in the hands of Denikin, who so destroyed the mines before retreating that they are still not in working order." (001-088) The result was a "practically complete absence of coal" that left Russian cities freezing through the winter.

Transportation was crippled: "the most class-conscious and best elements of the railway proletariat were at the various fronts of the civil war." (001-097) The blockade imposed by the [[entente]] powers compounded the destruction, cutting Russia off from foreign machinery, spare parts, and technical expertise that its industries desperately needed.

Russell witnessed the militarization of Soviet society firsthand. He describes Trotsky at the Moscow Opera, calling for "three cheers for our brave fellows at the front," with an audience responding "as a London audience would have responded in the autumn of 1914." (001-022) Nationalism, suppressed in Communist theory, had resurfaced through the experience of war.

## Bolshevik Perspective

The Bolsheviks portrayed the civil war as a heroic struggle against world [[capitalism]]. As Russell records, apologists for Bolshevism in the West "excuse its harshness on the ground that it has been produced by the necessity of fighting the Entente and its mercenaries." (001-009) The pageant of the World Commune dramatized this narrative: "The Entente leaders, however, were seen preparing their troops for battle, and the pageant went on to show the formation of the Red Army under its emblem the Red Star." (001-053)

Russell grants that the civil war intensified the regime's worst features but insists that the conflict was inherent in Bolshevik strategy: "To say that capitalists are wicked and we have no responsibility for their acts is unscientific...The evils produced in Russia by the enmity of the Entente are therefore to be reckoned as essential in the Bolshevik method of transition to Communism." (001-009)

## Significance

Russell's analysis of the civil war cuts to the heart of his critique of Bolshevism. He argues that "the evils of war, especially of civil war, are certain and very great; the gains to be achieved by victory are problematical." (001-020) The war, he warns, ultimately broke the revolutionary spirit it was meant to defend: "By provoking the hostility of the outside world the Bolsheviks were forced to provoke the hostility of the peasants, and finally the hostility or utter apathy of the urban and industrial population. These various hostilities brought material disaster, and material disaster brought spiritual collapse." (002-100)

Despite recognizing that the Entente's "peevish and futile opposition" had caused immense suffering, Russell places ultimate responsibility on the Bolshevik philosophy itself. The civil war was not an unfortunate accident but the predictable outcome of a doctrine that assumed inevitable violent conflict with the capitalist world.
