# Fall of Tsarism

The **Fall of Tsarism** in February 1917 marked the end of the Romanov dynasty and three centuries of autocratic rule in Russia. Bertrand Russell, writing in 1920, treated the fall of the Tsar not as ancient history but as the immediate precondition for the Bolshevik experiment he was analysing.

## Background

[[tsarism|Tsarist autocracy]] had been weakening for decades before its final collapse. The 1905 Revolution had extracted concessions — a parliament (Duma), limited civil liberties — but the Tsar had steadily clawed back these reforms. By 1917, the strains of the [[world-war|World War]] had exposed the regime's incompetence: military defeats, economic dislocation, and the scandalous influence of Rasputin at court had eroded even the aristocracy's loyalty to the throne.

Russell understood the fall of Tsarism as part of a broader pattern: the collapse of traditional authority across Europe in the wake of the war. Russia's experience was extreme but not unique — the war had delegitimised old regimes everywhere, creating openings for revolutionary movements of both the left and the right.

## The Bolshevik Narrative

The Bolsheviks interpreted the fall of Tsarism through the lens of [[marxism|Marxian]] theory: it was the "bourgeois-democratic" revolution that Marxism had predicted as the necessary precursor to the [[proletariat|proletarian]] revolution. The Provisional Government that replaced the Tsar represented the political ascendancy of the [[bourgeoisie]], and its overthrow by the Bolsheviks in October was the logical next stage of historical development.

Russell was sceptical of this schematic interpretation. He noted that the February Revolution was a spontaneous popular uprising rather than the product of bourgeois class consciousness, and that the Provisional Government's failure was due less to its class character than to its inability to address the two overwhelming demands of the Russian people: peace and land.

## Consequences

The fall of Tsarism created a power vacuum that multiple forces competed to fill. The Provisional Government, the [[soviet|Soviets]], the Bolsheviks, the Mensheviks, the Socialist Revolutionaries, and the generals of the old army all contended for authority. Russell's analysis emphasised that the Bolshevik victory was not inevitable — it was the product of specific decisions, circumstances, and the ruthless effectiveness of [[lenin|Lenin]]'s leadership.
