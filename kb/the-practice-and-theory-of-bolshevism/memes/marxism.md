# Marxism

**Marxism** — or "Marxianism" as Russell often termed it — is the political and economic philosophy derived from the works of [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Friedrich Engels]]. It forms the ideological foundation of [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]], providing both the theoretical framework ([[materialistic-conception-of-history|historical materialism]], [[class-struggle|class struggle]]) and the revolutionary programme that the Bolsheviks claimed to implement.

In *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*, Russell subjected Marxian doctrine to sustained critical examination, focusing particularly on the tension between Marxian theory and Bolshevik practice. His critique was distinctive in being neither a dismissal nor an endorsement but an analysis of how a theoretical system that contained genuine insights could, when treated as infallible scripture, become an instrument of intellectual and political tyranny.

## Russell's Critique

Russell identified several key tensions between Marxian theory and Russian realities.

### The Peasant Problem

The Bolshevik Revolution occurred in a predominantly [[peasantry|peasant]] society, contrary to Marxian expectations that revolution would begin in the most advanced industrial countries:

> The Russian Revolution was supposed to begin in Russia, though Marxian theory would seem to demand that it should begin in an industrialized country. (001-019)

This was not merely an academic anomaly. The Bolsheviks' claim to represent the [[proletariat]] was undermined by the fact that the proletariat constituted a small minority of the Russian population, while the peasantry — whose interests Marxian theory subordinated to those of the industrial working class — formed the overwhelming majority.

### The Religious Character

Russell was struck by the quasi-religious nature of Marxian belief among Bolsheviks:

> Religious faith in the Marxian gospel, which takes the place of the Christian religion among the vast majority of Russian Communists... (001-030)

This observation was central to Russell's broader thesis: that Bolshevism had transformed Marxism from a scientific theory into a "religion, with elaborate dogmas and inspired scriptures" (P01-007). The texts of Marx and Engels were treated as infallible; dissent was heresy; doubt was apostasy. Russell, who valued the scientific spirit precisely because of its openness to falsification, found this transformation "the essential tragedy of Bolshevism."

### Against Psychological Explanation

Russell noted that Marxism systematically excluded psychological and individual factors from historical analysis:

> Indeed the whole tendency of Marxianism is against psychological interpretation. (001-025)

For Russell — who believed that human psychology, including the instincts of possessiveness, power, and creativity, was a fundamental force in history irreducible to economic relations — this was a serious theoretical deficiency. Marxism's economic determinism, he argued, blinded its adherents to the psychological consequences of their own policies.

## The Dogmatic Character

A recurring theme in Russell's analysis was the dogmatic character of Marxian belief under Bolshevism. He wrote:

> The dogmatic character of Marxian Communism finds support in the philosophic doctrine of materialism. (002-007)

This dogmatism, Russell argued, led Bolsheviks to treat Marx's writings as "scripture" — as he put it, "the Marxian scripture prove to be not infallible" (001-065). The result was a political movement whose theoretical rigidity prevented pragmatic adaptation to circumstances, and whose certainty about the direction of history justified the suppression of all who questioned it.

The epistemological critique was, for Russell, the deepest level of his disagreement with Bolshevism. He did not object primarily to the *content* of Marxian doctrine — he acknowledged that much of Marx's critique of [[capitalism]] was valid. His objection was to the *attitude* with which the Bolsheviks held their beliefs: the conviction of infallibility that foreclosed debate, suppressed dissent, and transformed a liberating doctrine into a system of intellectual oppression.

## Russell's Balanced Assessment

Despite his criticisms, Russell acknowledged that Marxian analysis had genuine explanatory power:

> Such facts as these justify Marxians in speaking, as they do, of the class war. (002-009)

He recognised that Marx had made lasting contributions to the understanding of history — the importance of economic factors, the reality of class conflict, the tendency of capitalism toward crisis. His critique was not a rejection of Marx but a defence of the scientific spirit that Marx himself had claimed to embody: the willingness to test hypotheses against evidence, to revise theories in light of experience, and to treat no doctrine as beyond question.

This was Russell's alternative to [[marxian-orthodoxy|Marxian orthodoxy]]: not anti-Marxism but intellectual humility. A [[socialism]] that was "experimental, democratic, and respectful of individual liberty" — a politics that treated its commitments as hypotheses to be tested rather than dogmas to be enforced.
