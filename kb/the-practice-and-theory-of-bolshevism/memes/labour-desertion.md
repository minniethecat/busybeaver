## Definition

Labour desertion was the term used in 1920 Soviet Russia to describe the practice of workers voluntarily abandoning their designated places of employment — typically to seek better food conditions in the countryside or to engage in speculation (unofficial trade). It represented a severe economic crisis for the Bolshevik state, which responded with industrial conscription, compulsory labour service, and punitive measures.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell documented labour desertion by quoting directly from the resolutions of the Ninth Congress of the Russian [[communism|Communist]] Party. The official report identified the problem bluntly: "a considerable part of the workers either in search of better food conditions or often for the purposes of speculation, voluntarily leave their places of employment or change from place to place, which inevitably harms production and deteriorates the general position of the working class" (001-092). The Party Congress deemed the establishment of "firm, systematic and insistent" control over labour movement one of "the most urgent problems of Soviet Government" (001-092).

The Bolshevik response was industrial conscription, which Russell described as "rigidly enforced. Every man and woman has to work, and slacking is severely punished, by prison or a penal settlement. Strikes are illegal, though they sometimes occur" (001-111). Russell pointed out the irony that a government proclaiming itself the friend of the proletariat had "been enabled to establish an iron discipline, beyond the wildest dreams of the most autocratic American magnate" (001-111).

The Ninth Congress further resolved to approve "the mobilization of the [[proletariat|industrial proletariat]], compulsory labour service, militarization of production and the application of military detachments to economic needs" (001-100). Russell situated this within the broader context of militarization: the [[bolshevik|Bolsheviks]] had created "a vast army recruited by conscription" from a population "utterly weary of war," and this militarism "has produced its inevitable result in the way of a harsh and dictatorial spirit" (002-083).

Russell's treatment of labour desertion was characteristic of his method: he allowed the Bolsheviks' own documents to reveal the gap between their professed ideals and the coercive practices that economic crisis compelled them to adopt. The phenomenon illustrated, in his view, how the material conditions of Russia — food shortages, disrupted industry, and the legacy of war — forced even a revolutionary government professing proletarian emancipation into forms of labour discipline indistinguishable from those of capitalist industry.
