## Definition

Representative government is the system by which citizens delegate political authority to elected officials who are accountable through periodic elections and constitutional constraints. In the context of Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*, it refers principally to the parliamentary democracies of Western Europe, which the Bolsheviks dismissed as irredeemable instruments of capitalist class rule.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell approached Soviet Russia expecting "an interesting experiment in a new form of representative government" but found instead a system in which the formal electoral machinery — from village meetings to the All-Russian Soviet — served merely to ratify decisions already taken by the Communist Party (001-067). The Bolsheviks had constructed an elaborate constitutional architecture, yet in Russell's judgment the reality of one-party rule rendered the representative form hollow.

Russell acknowledged, however, that "the Bolshevik argument against Parliamentary democracy as a method of achieving Socialism is a powerful one" (002-031). The Bolsheviks contended that capitalist control of propaganda and money made it nearly impossible for Communists to win parliamentary majorities, and that even if elected, a socialist government would face armed resistance from capital. Russell did not dismiss these arguments outright; he conceded that constitutional methods faced genuine obstacles.

Despite this, Russell ultimately rejected the Bolshevik alternative. His central criticism was that the contempt for parliamentary methods was premised on a certitude that Russell found fanatical — the assumption that Communists were "absolutely certain that Communism is desirable, so certain that they are willing, if necessary, to force it upon an unwilling population at the point of the bayonet" (002-032). Russell warned that this attitude fostered a broader "disrespect for law and constitutional government" (002-041) whose consequences, in his view, would outlast any immediate revolutionary gains.

Russell also advanced a practical argument for constitutional gradualism: proceeding by self-government "tends to prevent the Communist regime, when it comes, from having that truly terrible degree of centralization which now exists in Russia" (002-111). The alternative to Bolshevik methods, he suggested, was a more patient approach that preserved legal order while pursuing economic reconstruction.
