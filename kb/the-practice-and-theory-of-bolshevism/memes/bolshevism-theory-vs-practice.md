# Bolshevism: Theory versus Practice

> **核心论点**：Bertrand Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism* (1920) is organised around a single organising tension — the widening gulf between what Bolshevism *professes* in theory and what it *delivers* in practice. Russell argues that this gap is not accidental but structural, arising from the Bolsheviks' commitment to Marxian orthodoxy as a "religion" rather than a scientific hypothesis, and from their willingness to sacrifice every democratic principle for the preservation of Party power.

## The Theoretical Promise

[[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] presented itself to the world as the practical realisation of [[marxism|Marxian]] theory. Its theoretical architecture rested on several pillars that Russell found superficially attractive:

### Economic Justice

The Bolshevik programme promised the abolition of [[capitalism|capitalist]] exploitation, the transfer of the means of production to the working class, and the creation of a society in which "he who does not work, neither shall he eat" (001-012). Russell acknowledged that this critique of capitalism resonated with genuine grievances — the inequality, the cycles of war, the concentration of wealth — and that the Bolshevik diagnosis of capitalism's failings contained substantial truth.

### International Solidarity

In theory, Bolshevism was [[internationalism|internationalist]]. The [[third-international|Third International]] was founded to unite workers across national boundaries, and Bolshevik propaganda consistently invoked the ideal of [[world-revolution|world revolution]]. Russell notes that this internationalism was one of Bolshevism's most appealing features to Western socialists disillusioned by the nationalist betrayals of the Second International in 1914 (002-106).

### Scientific Pretension

Bolshevism claimed to be "scientific" — not a utopian fantasy but the necessary next stage of historical development as revealed by [[karl-marx|Marx]]'s analysis of historical [[materialism]]. Russell, himself trained in mathematics and logic, was not unsympathetic to the claim that politics could be grounded in systematic analysis rather than sentiment. The Bolsheviks' confidence that they had discovered the "laws" of history gave them a coherence that more ecumenical socialist movements lacked (P01-007).

## The Practical Reality

Russell's examination of Soviet Russia in 1920 revealed a reality that systematically betrayed every one of these theoretical commitments.

### The Dictatorship of the Few

The "[[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat|dictatorship of the proletariat]]" was, Russell found, in practice the dictatorship of the [[communist-party|Communist Party]] — and within the Party, the dictatorship of a small inner circle around [[lenin|Lenin]]. Russell observes that the Party included people "by no means proletarian" (001-014), and that real workers had no meaningful voice in decisions made in their name. The promised class rule had become rule *over* the class.

### The Betrayal of Internationalism

Russell was particularly struck by the gap between Bolshevik internationalist rhetoric and the reality of Russian national interest. The [[red-army|Red Army]]'s invasion of [[russia|Georgia]] (1921), the suppression of Ukrainian independence movements, and the use of the [[third-international|Comintern]] as an instrument of Russian foreign policy all suggested that Bolshevism in power had become — despite its theoretical commitments — a vehicle for Great Russian nationalism (002-106).

### From Science to Religion

The most profound transformation Russell documents is epistemological. Bolshevism began as a "scientific" doctrine but functioned in practice as a "religion, with elaborate dogmas and inspired scriptures" (P01-007). The texts of Marx and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]] were treated as infallible; dissent was heresy; doubt was apostasy. Russell, who valued the scientific spirit precisely because of its openness to falsification, found this transformation from hypothesis to dogma "the essential tragedy of Bolshevism" (001-030).

## The Structural Mechanism: Why Theory Becomes Religion

Russell does not attribute the gap between theory and practice merely to hypocrisy or corruption. He identifies structural mechanisms that make the divergence almost inevitable:

### The Logic of Minority Rule

A party that seizes power on behalf of a class it does not genuinely represent must govern through coercion rather than consent. As Russell observes, "a minority can only retain power by the methods of a minority" (001-016). The theoretical commitment to democracy becomes unsustainable when democracy would mean the Party's removal from power.

### The Epistemology of Certainty

Russell's most original insight concerns the psychology of revolutionary conviction. He found in Lenin a man of "unwavering faith" (001-030) — and he came to see this certainty not as a strength but as a danger. "Contact with those who have no doubts," Russell writes, "has intensified a thousandfold my own doubts, not as to communism in itself, but as to the wisdom of holding a creed so firmly that for its sake men are willing to inflict widespread misery" (001-030).

Russell's argument is that theoretical certainty is *structurally* incompatible with democratic governance. Democracy requires the possibility that one might be wrong; Bolshevism, as a "religion," could not admit this possibility without undermining its own authority.

### The Instrumentalisation of Theory

Under conditions of civil war and international isolation, theoretical commitments became instruments of political expediency. The [[soviet-constitution|Soviet Constitution]] of 1918, which Russell examined, gave the franchise to "the toilers" while disenfranchising the bourgeoisie — but who counted as a "toiler" was decided by the Party, which meant the Party could disenfranchise anyone who opposed it. Theory, in this context, functioned as post-hoc rationalisation for decisions already taken on other grounds.

## Russell's Alternative

Russell was not a defender of [[capitalism]] — the book is unequivocal in its condemnation of capitalist exploitation, imperialism, and war. His critique of Bolshevism was not a brief for the status quo but an argument for a different kind of socialism: democratic, gradualist, and epistemologically humble.

He advocated for:

| Principle | Bolshevik Approach | Russell's Alternative |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Method** | Violent seizure of power | Gradual democratic transformation |
| **Epistemology** | Certainty (Marxism as revealed truth) | Fallibilism (policies as testable hypotheses) |
| **Liberty** | Subordinate to revolutionary goals | An intrinsic good, not merely instrumental |
| **Internationalism** | Instrument of Russian state interest | Genuine solidarity across borders |
| **Economics** | State ownership through decree | Co-operative and syndicalist models |

Russell's alternative, sketched rather than fully developed in the book, was a socialism that retained Bolshevism's critique of capitalism while rejecting its authoritarian methods — a "[[socialism]]" that was democratic, experimental, and respectful of individual liberty.

## Conclusion

The gap between theory and practice in Bolshevism was, in Russell's analysis, not a temporary aberration but a logical consequence of treating a political programme as an infallible doctrine. Once Marxism became "religion" rather than science, the suppression of dissent — and therefore the suppression of democracy — became structurally necessary. Russell's book is an argument that the ends do not justify the means, because the means shape the ends: a [[revolution]] achieved through dictatorship will produce a dictatorship, not a democracy.

Russell's final judgment is characteristically nuanced. He does not dismiss Bolshevism entirely — he acknowledges its critique of capitalism, its courage, its historical significance. But he insists that the future of socialism must lie elsewhere: not in the "unwavering faith" of the Bolsheviks, but in the democratic and experimental spirit that Russell himself embodied.
