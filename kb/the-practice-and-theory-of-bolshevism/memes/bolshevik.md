# Bolshevik Party

The **Bolshevik Party** (Russian: большевики, "majority"), formally the Russian Communist Party (Bolsheviks) or RCP(B), was the revolutionary Marxist organization that under [[lenin|Vladimir Lenin]]'s leadership seized power in the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]] of 1917 and established the world's first socialist state.

In his 1920 study, Bertrand Russell examines the Bolsheviks not as distant historical actors but as a living political force he had personally observed during his visit to [[russia|Soviet Russia]], offering a portrait of an organization that combined extraordinary revolutionary energy with authoritarian methods.

## Organizational Structure

### Origins and Formation

The Bolshevik faction emerged from the 1903 split of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party, taking its name from the Russian word for "majority" after winning a key vote at the party congress. Under Lenin's direction, the Bolsheviks developed a distinctive model of the **vanguard party** — a disciplined, centralized organization of professional revolutionaries, distinct from the broader mass parties of Western European socialism.

Russell observes that the Bolshevik party in power retained this vanguard character:

> Bolshevism is internally aristocratic and intellectually insular (001-018)

He explicitly notes the non-proletarian composition of the Bolshevik leadership — figures like Lenin and Chicherin — and the party's transformation from an underground revolutionary organization into a state apparatus (001-014).

### Methods of Rule

Russell identifies several defining features of Bolshevik governance:

1. **Centralized authority** — All significant decisions flow from the party leadership, with local initiative systematically constrained
2. **Propaganda as statecraft** — "Every Bolshevik" functions as an agent of mass persuasion, with propaganda apparatuses rivaling military organization in their sophistication (001-028)
3. **Use of terror** — While Russell acknowledges the extraordinary circumstances of civil war and foreign intervention, he is troubled by the Bolsheviks' "general hostility to opposition" as a matter of theoretical principle (001-009)
4. **Educational transformation** — Russell grants the Bolsheviks genuine achievements in mass education and technical training, even as he criticizes their political indoctrination of the young (001-057)

## Russell's Assessment

### Achievements

Russell refuses to join the chorus of unconditional anti-Bolshevik condemnation. He identifies several areas where Bolshevik governance has produced genuine gains:

| Achievement | Evidence | PN |
|------------|----------|-----|
| **Revolutionary survival** | Bolsheviks maintained power against foreign intervention and civil war | (001-022) |
| **Nationalities policy** | Secured support of ethnic minorities through anti-imperialist program | (001-021) |
| **Industrial education** | Agricultural and technical training reaching the peasantry | (001-062) |
| **Cultural vitality** | Modernist energy in the arts, contrasting with Tsarist stagnation | (001-032) |

Russell also credits the Bolsheviks with having "created a hope" — a sense of historical purpose that had been absent in Russian life (001-002).

### Criticisms

Russell's criticisms are more searching because they are not rooted in reactionary hostility:

> People's reasons for disliking the Bolsheviks are very inadequate. It is said they have destroyed trade and industry — but so has every country involved in the World War (001-073)

His genuine objections center on democratic deficits, the Bolshevik equation of opposition with counter-revolution, and the psychological consequences of one-party rule. He worries that "if the Bolsheviks ultimately fall, it will be" because their methods are contrary to fundamental human instincts (001-016).

## Historical Role

Russell's analysis captures the Bolshevik Party at a critical juncture — victorious in civil war but facing economic collapse, international ostracism, and growing internal dissent. The [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]] (1918–1921) had both strengthened and distorted the party: it created a siege mentality that justified extreme measures while simultaneously demonstrating the Bolsheviks' organizational capacities.

### War and Transformation

The civil war period transformed the Party from a revolutionary organization into a military-administrative apparatus. The demands of fighting White armies, foreign interventions, and peasant uprisings accelerated the centralization of authority and the militarization of Party structures. Russell notes that this transformation, while perhaps unavoidable, had lasting consequences for the character of Bolshevik rule.

### International Ambitions

The party's relationship with international revolution was complex. Through the [[third-international|Third International (Comintern)]], founded in 1919, the Bolsheviks sought to export their model globally. Russell notes the tension between Bolshevik Russia's need for normal diplomatic relations with capitalist states and its commitment to overthrowing those states through propaganda and subversion (001-026).
