# Béla Kun

**Béla Kun** (1886–1938) was a Hungarian communist revolutionary who led the short-lived [[hungary|Hungarian Soviet Republic]] of 1919. His regime's 133-day existence and rapid collapse provided Bertrand Russell with a key case study in the limits of Bolshevik revolutionary strategy outside Russia.

## Revolutionary Career

Kun was radicalised as a prisoner of war in Russia during the [[world-war|World War]], where he came into contact with [[bolshevik|Bolshevik]] ideas. Returning to Hungary after the war, he founded the Hungarian Communist Party and, in March 1919, assumed power as the effective leader of a soviet-style government in Budapest.

## Russell's Perspective

Russell regarded Kun's regime as instructive for what it revealed about the non-universality of the Bolshevik model. The Hungarian Soviet Republic was overthrown by Romanian military intervention after losing popular support — particularly among the peasantry, whom the regime had alienated through forced collectivisation.
