# Treaty of Versailles

The **Treaty of Versailles** (1919) was the peace settlement that formally ended the [[world-war|World War]] between the Allied Powers and Germany. Bertrand Russell, though he did not devote extended analysis to the treaty in *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*, regarded the Versailles settlement as a crucial part of the international context within which Bolshevism flourished — a punitive peace that discredited liberal internationalism and strengthened the Bolsheviks' claim that only revolution could bring genuine peace.

## The Settlement

The Versailles Treaty imposed harsh terms on Germany: territorial losses, military restrictions, and a "war guilt" clause that assigned sole responsibility for the war to Germany, accompanied by reparations obligations of unprecedented scale. The treaty also redrew the map of Europe, creating new states from the ruins of the Austro-Hungarian, German, and Russian empires.

Russell viewed the settlement as a betrayal of the principles for which the war had supposedly been fought. The Allied leaders — [[georges-clemenceau|Clemenceau]], [[lloyd-george|Lloyd George]], and [[woodrow-wilson|Wilson]] — had promised a just peace based on national self-determination and the renunciation of punitive diplomacy. What they delivered, Russell argued, was a settlement that reproduced the imperialist logic of the pre-war era under a thin veneer of liberal rhetoric.

## Consequences for Bolshevism

Russell understood that the Versailles settlement was a powerful propaganda asset for the Bolsheviks. The treaty demonstrated, more effectively than any Bolshevik pamphlet, that the capitalist powers' talk of justice and democracy was hollow. The Bolsheviks could point to Versailles and say: this is what capitalist peace looks like — the victors dividing the spoils, the vanquished crushed under the weight of reparations, the principle of self-determination honoured only when it served the victors' interests.

The treaty also created the material conditions in which Bolshevism could spread. The economic dislocation, national humiliation, and political instability that Versailles imposed on Germany were precisely the conditions in which revolutionary movements thrive. Russell, who understood the relationship between material conditions and political consciousness, saw that Versailles had made [[world-revolution|world revolution]] more, not less, likely.
