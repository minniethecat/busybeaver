# Entente (Allied Powers)

## Overview

The Entente — also referred to as the Allied Powers — was the coalition of Western capitalist nations, principally Britain, France, and the United States, that had fought together in the First World War and subsequently opposed the Bolshevik regime through blockade, military intervention, and diplomatic isolation. Russell devotes sustained attention to the Entente's role in shaping the conditions of Soviet Russia, arguing that its policy was simultaneously a cause of Bolshevik harshness and a justification for it.

Russell's analysis of the Entente is unusually even-handed: he condemns the blockade and intervention as both morally blameworthy and strategically foolish, yet he refuses to excuse Bolshevik excesses by reference to capitalist hostility. The Entente, in his account, provides the external pressure that hardens internal repression — but the Bolsheviks chose methods that invited that pressure.

## Role in the Bolshevik System (as Antagonist)

The Entente functioned as the Bolshevik regime's principal external enemy, and its hostility served important internal political functions. Russell notes that "it is customary among the apologists of Bolshevism in the West to excuse its harshness on the ground that it has been produced by the necessity of fighting the Entente and its mercenaries" (001-009). He acknowledges that "undoubtedly it is true that this necessity has produced many of the worst elements in the present state of affairs" (001-009), and that "the Entente has incurred a heavy load of guilt by its peevish and futile opposition" (001-009).

However, Russell refuses to grant the Bolsheviks absolution on this basis. He argues that "the expectation of such opposition was always part of Bolshevik theory" (001-009) — the doctrine of the class war presupposed and even provoked the hostility of capitalist states. The Bolsheviks, having chosen a revolutionary strategy that guaranteed Western enmity, could not then cite that enmity as an extenuating circumstance for their own repressive measures. Russell goes further, suggesting that the relationship between Bolshevism and capitalist hostility was dialectical: "the exhaustion and misery caused by unsuccessful war were necessary to the success of the Bolsheviks" (001-009).

The Entente's blockade had severe economic consequences. Russell observes that Russia was "quite unusually dependent upon foreign assistance" — not only for machinery and locomotives but for "the organizing and technical brains in industry" (001-082). When the Entente became hostile, "the foreigners in Russian industry either left the country or assisted counterrevolution" (001-082), stripping the Soviet economy of essential expertise.

The Entente also served as a propaganda foil. Bolshevik propaganda art depicted "the Entente leaders making a sorry and grotesque appearance" (001-039), while mass pageants staged the formation of the Red Army directly in response to the sight of Entente leaders "preparing their troops for battle" (001-053).

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's verdict on the Entente is damning. He regards the blockade as a policy of collective punishment that produced only suffering while strengthening the Bolsheviks' domestic position. Every economic failure and tyrannical measure, he notes, "is used by the Entente as a justification of its policy" (001-103). He deploys a metaphor of deliberate cruelty:

> If a man is deprived of food and drink, he will grow weak, lose his reason, and finally die. This is not usually considered a good reason for inflicting death by starvation. But where nations are concerned, the weakness and struggles are regarded as morally culpable, and are held to justify further punishment. So at least it has been in the case of Russia. (001-103)

Russell nevertheless insists on holding the Bolsheviks partly accountable. "I recognize to the full the reasons for the bad state of affairs, in the past history of Russia and the recent policy of the Entente. But I have thought it better to record impressions frankly, trusting the readers to remember that the Bolsheviks have only a very limited share of responsibility for the evils from which Russia is suffering" (001-116). This balanced judgment — condemning the Entente's policy without exonerating the Bolsheviks — is characteristic of Russell's whole approach to the Soviet question.

He also recognized the strategic futility of continued Western hostility. "It is necessary for Russia to accept the risks and uncertainties involved in attempting to make peace with the Entente and to trade with America" (001-090), he writes, warning that continued antagonism would drive Russia further toward imperialism and Asian expansion at Western expense.
