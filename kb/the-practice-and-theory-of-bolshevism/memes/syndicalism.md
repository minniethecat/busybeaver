# Syndicalism

**Syndicalism** — the revolutionary trade union movement that advocated workers' control of industry through direct action, the general strike, and the organisation of society around industrial unions — appears in Bertrand Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism* as a significant but ultimately marginalised alternative to Bolshevik authoritarianism. Russell, who had written sympathetically about syndicalism in earlier works, regarded it as embodying democratic principles of workers' self-management that Bolshevism had betrayed.

## Core Principles

Syndicalism differed from both parliamentary socialism and Bolshevik communism in its analysis of how revolution should be made and what should follow it. Its key tenets included:

- **Direct action** — Workers should achieve their objectives through their own economic power (strikes, boycotts, sabotage) rather than through political parties or parliamentary legislation
- **The general strike** — The revolutionary moment would take the form of a general strike that paralysed the economy and demonstrated workers' capacity to run society without the capitalist class
- **Industrial federation** — Post-revolutionary society would be organised around industrial unions that managed their respective industries, coordinated through federal structures rather than a centralised state

## Syndicalism and Bolshevism

The Bolsheviks and syndicalists shared a revolutionary commitment and a critique of parliamentary [[socialism]] as insufficiently radical. But they diverged fundamentally on the role of the state and the party. For syndicalists, the revolution was to be made by workers themselves through their industrial organisations; for Bolsheviks, it was to be made by the vanguard party on behalf of workers.

Russell noted that the Bolsheviks, having seized power, moved quickly to suppress syndicalist tendencies within the Russian labour movement. Independent trade unions were subordinated to the Party; factory committees were stripped of authority; and the syndicalist vision of workers' self-management was replaced by bureaucratic state control. (002-082) The Bolsheviks' slogan — "all power to the Soviets" — had, in practice, become "all power to the Party."

## Russell's View

Russell was not an uncritical advocate of syndicalism. He recognised its limitations — its tendency toward sectionalism, its neglect of consumers' interests, its sometime hostility to intellectual and cultural pursuits. But he valued syndicalism's insistence that socialism must be democratic, that workers must control their own workplaces, and that the concentration of power in a centralised state was a danger, not a solution.

In the context of his critique of Bolshevism, syndicalism served as a reminder that there were revolutionary traditions that did not lead to dictatorship — that it was possible to be a radical critic of [[capitalism]] without embracing the authoritarian methods of [[lenin|Lenin]]'s Party.
