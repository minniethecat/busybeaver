# Bourgeoisie

The **bourgeoisie**, in Marxian theory, is the class that owns the means of production and employs wage labour — the capitalist class whose interests are fundamentally opposed to those of the [[proletariat]]. In his 1920 study, Bertrand Russell examined how the Bolsheviks applied this theoretical category in practice, systematically disenfranchising and suppressing those labelled "bourgeois" as a matter of state policy.

## Marxian Origins

The concept of the bourgeoisie was central to the thought of [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Friedrich Engels]], who argued in *The Communist Manifesto* that all of human history was the history of class struggle between oppressor and oppressed — and that the bourgeoisie, as the ruling class of the capitalist epoch, had created the conditions for its own overthrow by concentrating wealth and immiserating the proletariat.

This theoretical framework provided the Bolsheviks with their primary justification for revolution: the expropriation of the bourgeoisie and the establishment of the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat|dictatorship of the proletariat]].

## Under Bolshevik Rule

Russell observed the practical consequences of this theoretical classification during his visit to Soviet Russia. The Bolsheviks had institutionalised the disenfranchisement of the bourgeoisie through the [[soviet-constitution|Soviet Constitution of 1918]], which restricted the franchise to "the toilers" — those engaged in productive work. Those classified as bourgeois — former capitalists, merchants, and others who employed labour — were explicitly excluded from political participation (001-066).

The category proved elastic in practice. Russell noted that the Party included people "by no means proletarian" (001-014), while others were classified as bourgeois on political rather than economic grounds. The determination of who counted as a "toiler" and who as a "bourgeois" was made by the Party itself — a power that effectively allowed the Bolsheviks to disenfranchise anyone who opposed them.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell did not defend the bourgeoisie as a class. His critique of [[capitalism]] was unequivocal, and he acknowledged the genuine grievances that had made the Bolshevik attack on the bourgeoisie popular among workers and peasants.

His concern was rather with the arbitrariness of class classification under Bolshevik rule. When the Party alone could determine who belonged to which class, the theoretical framework of Marxism became an instrument of political repression rather than a tool of social analysis. Russell saw this as another instance of the gap between Bolshevik theory and Bolshevik practice (001-066).

## Historical Context

The suppression of the bourgeoisie during the Russian Civil War (1918–1921) was comprehensive. Bourgeois individuals were denied rations, excluded from housing allocation, barred from higher education, and subject to arbitrary arrest. Russell witnessed the human consequences of this policy — the former professionals, intellectuals, and merchants reduced to penury and powerlessness — and recorded them with characteristic ambivalence: acknowledging the revolutionary logic while questioning its humanity.
