# America

**America** (the United States) appears in Bertrand Russell's *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism* as the rising world power whose President, [[woodrow-wilson|Woodrow Wilson]], represented a liberal internationalist alternative to both Bolshevik revolution and old-style power politics.

Russell, characteristically, found this alternative inadequate — well-intentioned but naive, and ultimately complicit in the policies of blockade and intervention that he condemned.

## Wilsonian Internationalism

Russell acknowledged the appeal of Wilsonian ideals — national self-determination, open diplomacy, a league of nations to prevent war. These principles resonated with Russell's own internationalist convictions and his opposition to secret treaties and imperialist diplomacy.

Yet Russell was sceptical of their application. The Versailles settlement, negotiated under Wilson's influence, had produced not a just peace but a punitive one, and the League of Nations had become an instrument of the victorious powers rather than a genuinely international body.

The Bolsheviks, Russell noted, professed similar ideals — peace, self-determination, international cooperation — but proposed to achieve them through world revolution rather than liberal reform. The competition between Wilsonian and Leninist internationalism was, Russell suggested, the central ideological drama of the postwar world.

## American Policy Toward Russia

America's policy toward Bolshevik Russia was less aggressively interventionist than that of Britain or France, but Russell was not impressed by the distinction. The United States participated in the [[allied-intervention|Allied intervention]], contributed to the [[allied-blockade|blockade]], and refused to recognise the Bolshevik government.

American humanitarian relief — for which Russell acknowledged there was genuine American concern — was subordinated to political objectives.

Russell noted the irony that America, a country born in revolution against imperial rule, should find itself aligned with the forces seeking to suppress another revolution. The Bolsheviks, he observed, were in some respects the spiritual heirs of 1776 — a fact that American policymakers seemed unable to recognise.
