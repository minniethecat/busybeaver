# Soviet

The **Soviet** (Russian: совет, "council") was the fundamental political institution of revolutionary Russia — a council of workers', peasants', and soldiers' deputies that served as both a legislative and executive body. In his 1920 account, Bertrand Russell observed the Soviet system firsthand, describing the "All-Russian Soviet" as the formal apex of the state structure through which the people's commissars derived their authority (001-067).

## Origins and Structure

Soviets first emerged spontaneously during the [[russian-revolution|1905 Revolution]] as strike committees and workers' councils. They were revived in 1917 and became the institutional vehicle through which the Bolsheviks consolidated power after the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]].

Russell's visit to Soviet Russia in 1920 gave him direct exposure to the Soviet system in operation. He noted the contrast between the formal democratic structure — in which "the village meeting" elected delegates to higher bodies — and the practical reality of Party control. The Bolsheviks had made the Soviets the official organs of state power, but had simultaneously ensured that Party discipline determined their composition and decisions (001-018).

## Russell's Analysis

Russell observed that the Soviet system, as it functioned under Bolshevik rule, embodied a tension between democratic form and authoritarian practice. The franchise was restricted to "the toilers," with the [[bourgeoisie]] explicitly disenfranchised — a provision of the [[soviet-constitution|Soviet Constitution]] that Russell examined critically (001-066).

Russell acknowledged the practical appeal of the Soviet model. Unlike Western parliamentary systems, Soviets combined legislative and executive functions, theoretically enabling more direct popular control. Yet he was troubled by what he witnessed: a system in which the forms of popular sovereignty masked the reality of one-party rule.

> The All-Russian Soviet, to which the people's commissars are responsible, is elected by a system of indirect voting, with a franchise confined to those who are engaged in "productive" work. (001-067)

## Historical Context

At the time of Russell's visit, the Soviet system was still evolving. The civil war had concentrated power in executive bodies like the Council of People's Commissars (Sovnarkom), while the Soviets themselves met infrequently and exercised limited real authority. Russell's analysis captures this transitional moment, before the full consolidation of the one-party state under Stalin.
