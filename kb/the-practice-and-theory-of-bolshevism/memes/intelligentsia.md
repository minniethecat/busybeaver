# Intelligentsia

The **intelligentsia** — Russia's educated intellectual class of writers, scholars, scientists, and professionals — occupied an ambivalent position in Bolshevik society that Bertrand Russell observed during his 1920 visit. The Bolsheviks needed the intelligentsia's expertise to administer the state and manage the economy, but they distrusted the intelligentsia's independence of mind and its potential for political dissent.

## Historical Background

The Russian intelligentsia had a distinctive history and character. Unlike the Western European intellectual class, which had largely been absorbed into the professional and administrative structures of bourgeois society, the Russian intelligentsia had defined itself in opposition to the [[tsarism|Tsarist]] state. From the Decembrists of 1825 through the Narodniks and the early Marxists, the intelligentsia had been the primary source of revolutionary ideas and oppositional politics in Russia.

This history gave the intelligentsia a complex relationship with the Bolsheviks. Many Bolshevik leaders — [[lenin|Lenin]], [[trotsky|Trotsky]], [[lunacharsky|Lunacharsky]] — were themselves products of the intelligentsia, yet the Party they led was suspicious of intellectuals as a class, viewing them as unreliable allies whose commitment to the revolution was always qualified by their attachment to bourgeois values of individual freedom and critical inquiry.

## Russell's Observations

Russell, himself one of the great intellectuals of his age, was particularly sensitive to the position of the intelligentsia under Bolshevik rule. He observed that the Bolsheviks' attitude toward intellectuals was deeply contradictory: they valued their skills but suppressed their independence.

The regime needed doctors, engineers, scientists, and administrators to keep the state functioning. Many members of the intelligentsia were therefore employed by the Soviet state, often in positions of considerable responsibility. Yet their conditions of work were determined by political rather than professional considerations. Intellectuals who expressed dissent, or who were suspected of disloyalty, faced dismissal, arrest, or worse.

Russell noted the case of [[gorky|Maxim Gorky]], the most famous Russian writer of the era, who had initially supported the revolution but became increasingly critical of Bolshevik methods. Gorky's privileged position as a cultural icon gave him a degree of protection, but Russell saw in Gorky's ambivalent relationship with the regime a microcosm of the intelligentsia's dilemma: the choice between silent cooperation and principled opposition that would cost them everything (001-031).

## The Arts and Sciences

Russell devoted particular attention to the intelligentsia's role in the arts and sciences. He noted that the Bolsheviks, to their credit, had made genuine efforts to preserve cultural institutions and to support scientific research. Museums were protected, universities continued to function, and scholars were granted rations and facilities that ordinary citizens lacked.

Yet this support came with strings attached. The regime expected artists and scholars to contribute to the revolutionary project, and those whose work did not serve Bolshevik purposes found their positions increasingly precarious. Russell saw in this instrumentalisation of intellectual life a fundamental threat to the values of free inquiry that he held most dear.
