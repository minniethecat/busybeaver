# Liberty

**Liberty** — the freedom of individuals to think, speak, and act without state coercion — was, for Bertrand Russell, the central value that [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] had betrayed. In *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*, Russell's defence of liberty was not a defence of [[capitalism]] or the status quo, but an argument that freedom was an intrinsic human good that no political programme, however egalitarian in its aspirations, was entitled to sacrifice.

## The Bolshevik View of Liberty

The Bolsheviks, following Marxian theory, regarded liberty in capitalist societies as merely formal — the freedom of the worker to choose which employer to be exploited by, the freedom of the press that was in practice the freedom of those who owned the presses. [[lenin|Lenin]] and his followers argued that true freedom could only be achieved after the abolition of class society, and that in the meantime, the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat|dictatorship of the proletariat]] necessarily involved the suppression of "bourgeois freedoms."

Russell acknowledged the force of this argument. He did not romanticise the liberty of the capitalist West. But he argued that the Bolsheviks had made a catastrophic error in dismissing liberty as merely a class prejudice. Freedom, Russell insisted, was not an epiphenomenon of class relations but a fundamental human need.

## Russell's Defence

Russell's defence of liberty rested on three interconnected arguments:

**Liberty and truth.** Without freedom of thought and expression, Russell argued, error cannot be corrected. The Bolsheviks' certainty — their conviction that they possessed infallible knowledge of history's direction — made them incapable of learning from experience. Freedom, for Russell, was not merely a right but an epistemological necessity: only through free inquiry and open debate could political programmes be tested and improved (001-030).

**Liberty and human dignity.** Russell rejected the utilitarian calculus that would trade individual freedom for collective welfare. He maintained that the suppression of thought, the censorship of art, and the silencing of dissent were harms in themselves — not merely instrumentally but intrinsically. A society that denied its members the freedom to think and speak was, in Russell's view, degrading to the human spirit regardless of its economic achievements.

**Liberty and progress.** Russell argued that social progress depended on the freedom to criticise existing institutions and to propose alternatives. By equating opposition with counter-revolution, the Bolsheviks had foreclosed the very process of social learning through which genuine improvement could occur. A system that could not tolerate criticism was, Russell believed, doomed to stagnation.

## Historical Significance

Russell's defence of liberty in *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism* placed him in a distinctive intellectual position — critical of capitalism, sympathetic to socialism, yet unwavering in his commitment to individual freedom. This position would define his political writing for the remainder of his long career and established a tradition of "left libertarianism" that continues to influence political thought.
