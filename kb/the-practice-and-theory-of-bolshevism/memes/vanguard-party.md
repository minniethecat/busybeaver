# Vanguard Party

The **vanguard party** — Lenin's concept of a disciplined, centralised organisation of professional revolutionaries — was, in Bertrand Russell's analysis, the organisational secret of [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]]'s success and the structural basis of its authoritarian character.

## Lenin's Innovation

Lenin developed the vanguard party concept in his 1902 work *What Is to Be Done?*, arguing that the working class, left to itself, could develop only "trade-union consciousness" — the desire for better wages and conditions within the capitalist system. Revolutionary class consciousness had to be brought to the workers from outside, by a party of dedicated, theoretically trained revolutionaries.

This organisational model — a small, disciplined, hierarchically structured party — was the instrument with which the [[bolshevik|Bolsheviks]] seized power in October 1917. It proved extraordinarily effective as a mechanism for revolutionary action.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell immediately grasped the significance of the vanguard concept. He noted that when Lenin spoke of the "proletariat," he meant "the 'class-conscious' part of the proletariat, i.e., the Communist Party." (001-006) The party substituted itself for the class it claimed to represent — and this substitution was not a deviation from Leninism but its essential logic.

The vanguard party included, Russell observed, "people by no means proletarian (such as Lenin and Chicherin) who have the right opinions, and excludes such wage-earners as have not the right opinions, whom it classifies as lackeys of the bourgeoisie." (001-006) The claim to represent the proletariat became a license to ignore what actual proletarians thought.

## The Political Consequences

Russell argued that the vanguard party structure inevitably produced [[dictatorship|one-party rule]]. If only the party possessed true revolutionary consciousness, then opposition to the party — even from workers — was objectively counter-revolutionary and had to be suppressed. The suppression of the [[kronstadt-rebellion|Kronstadt sailors]] and the outlawing of rival socialist parties were not aberrations but logical consequences of vanguard party doctrine.
