# Bolshevik Propaganda

**Bolshevik propaganda** was, in Bertrand Russell's 1920 analysis, one of the defining features of Soviet governance — an instrument of mass persuasion so pervasive and systematic that it rivalled military organisation in its sophistication. Russell observed propaganda operating at every level of Bolshevik rule, from international agitation to the education of children.

## Russell's Observations

Russell was struck by the centrality of propaganda to the Bolshevik project. During his visit to Russia, he noted that "every Bolshevik" functioned as an agent of mass persuasion. The Party devoted enormous resources to agitation not merely as a supplement to governance but as one of its primary activities (001-028).

Russell reports a conversation in which a Bolshevik official explained that the Party had "two tasks — war and propaganda" — to which all other concerns, including industrial production and food supply, were subordinated (001-089). This candid admission revealed the Bolsheviks' own understanding of their regime: a project sustained as much by ideological conviction as by material achievement.

## Methods and Reach

Russell documented propaganda operating across multiple domains:

1. **International agitation** — Through the [[third-international|Third International]], the Bolsheviks directed propaganda at workers in capitalist countries, urging them to overthrow their governments. Russell noted the tension between Bolshevik Russia's need for diplomatic relations with Western powers and its commitment to revolutionary subversion (001-026).

2. **Domestic mobilisation** — Within Russia, propaganda targeted every segment of the population. Russell observed propaganda directed at peasants, designed to exacerbate divisions between rich and poor villagers in support of Bolshevik land policy (001-027). The government also conducted propaganda "among the latter" (the poorer peasants) "against the former" (the richer ones).

3. **Educational indoctrination** — Russell was particularly concerned about propaganda in schools, where children were systematically taught Communist doctrine. While he acknowledged the Bolsheviks' genuine achievements in expanding educational access, he worried about the political content of that education (001-057).

4. **Artistic propaganda** — Russell devoted considerable attention to propaganda in the arts — the "propaganda pictures" depicting Lenin and Trotsky, the propaganda plays and mass pageants that communicated Bolshevik ideology through aesthetic means (001-039, 001-047).

## Effectiveness and Limits

Russell acknowledged the effectiveness of Bolshevik propaganda, particularly in comparison with the relatively amateurish propaganda efforts of the White forces during the [[russian-civil-war|civil war]]. A German observer told Russell that "the Germans had laughed at Bolshevik propaganda until they saw its effects" (001-028).

Yet Russell also noted the limits of propaganda. The [[peasantry]], who formed the vast majority of the Russian population, proved resistant to ideological persuasion when it conflicted with their material interests — particularly regarding land ownership and grain requisitioning (001-074).

## Critical Assessment

Russell's analysis of Bolshevik propaganda formed part of his broader critique of Bolshevism as a "religion." Propaganda, in this framework, was not merely a tool of communication but the mechanism by which Marxist doctrine was transformed into unquestionable dogma. The Bolsheviks' commitment to propaganda reflected their certainty — and for Russell, this certainty was precisely what made their system dangerous (001-030).
