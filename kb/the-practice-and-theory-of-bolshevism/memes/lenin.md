# Vladimir Lenin

**Vladimir Lenin** was the founding leader of [[russia|Soviet Russia]], the chief theoretician of [[bolshevism]], and the principal architect of the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]]. In *The Practice and Theory of Bolshevism*, [[russell-brandon|Bertrand Russell]] devotes sustained attention to Lenin — reporting on an hour-long private conversation in [[moscow]], analysing his role within the [[communist-party|Communist Party]], and assessing the psychological and ideological forces that drove his revolutionary project. Russell's portrait is one of the most vivid first-hand accounts of Lenin by a major Western philosopher, capturing a man he found at once disarmingly simple and profoundly dangerous.

## Biographical Sketch

### Early Life and Exile

Vladimir Ilyich Ulyanov, known universally as Lenin, was born in 1870 in Simbirsk, Russia. He became a revolutionary Marxist in the 1890s, leading the Bolshevik faction of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party from 1903. After years of exile in Western Europe, he returned to Russia in April 1917 following the February Revolution.

### Seizure of Power and Civil War

In October 1917 Lenin led the Bolshevik seizure of power. As Chairman of the Council of People's Commissars, he guided Soviet Russia through the Civil War (1917–1922), establishing the world's first Communist state before his death in 1924. Russell notes that this achievement rested on the ruthless application of [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat|dictatorship]], a concept Lenin interpreted as the unrestricted rule of the Party (001-016).

## Role in Bolshevism

### Chief Theoretician

Lenin was not only the supreme political leader of Bolshevism but also its chief theoretician and strategist. Russell describes him as a man for whom the [[materialistic-conception-of-history|materialist conception of history]] was his "lifeblood" — an ideologue who viewed all political questions through the lens of Marxian orthodoxy (001-024). His role was that of ultimate authority: the [[communist-party|Bolshevik Party]] was built around his interpretation of [[marxism|Marxism]], and his decisions on matters ranging from the dissolution of the Constituent Assembly to the conduct of the Civil War were final.

### Dictatorship in Practice

Russell situates Lenin within a historical tradition of revolutionary Puritanism, comparing his dealings with the Constituent Assembly to "Cromwell's dealings with Parliament" — both men, starting from a combination of democracy and religious faith, were "driven to sacrifice democracy to religion enforced by military dictatorship" (001-016). Lenin's dictatorship was, in Russell's analysis, literal rather than figurative: the "dictatorship of the [[proletariat]]" meant rule by the Communist Party, which included people "by no means proletarian" who held the right opinions (001-014).

### The Vanguard Party

Russell identifies Lenin's signal contribution to revolutionary theory as the concept of the **vanguard party** — a disciplined minority of professional revolutionaries who would lead the working class to [[revolution]]. The Party, in this conception, did not represent the proletariat democratically; it *substituted* for it, claiming superior insight into the proletariat's true, objective interests. Russell saw this substitution as the theoretical foundation of Bolshevik authoritarianism (001-019).

## Russell's Assessment

### Personal Impression

Russell's portrait of Lenin is one of the most detailed and psychologically penetrating passages in the book. After meeting Lenin in Moscow for an hour's conversation conducted largely in English, Russell describes a man of striking contradictions:

> He is very friendly, and apparently simple, entirely without a trace of hauteur. If one met him without knowing who he was, one would not guess that he is possessed of great power or even that he is in any way eminent. I have never met a personage so destitute of self-importance. (001-024)

Yet this simplicity concealed a formidable character. Russell found Lenin "dictatorial, calm, incapable of fear, extraordinarily devoid of self-seeking, an embodied theory" (001-024). His laugh, initially "merely friendly and jolly," gradually came to feel "rather grim" (001-024). Russell perceived in him an "intellectual aristocrat" who "despises a great many people" (001-024).

### Psychological Portrait

Russell's psychological dissection of Lenin proceeds along several dimensions:

| Dimension | Observation | PN |
|-----------|------------|-----|
| **Personal manner** | Friendly, simple, devoid of self-importance | 001-024 |
| **Intellectual style** | Rigidly orthodox, quotes Marx and Engels as scripture | P01-007 |
| **Political character** | Dictatorial, calm, incapable of fear | 001-024 |
| **Revolutionary faith** | Absolute conviction, intolerant of doubt | 001-030 |
| **Attitude to liberty** | Indifferent — "as little love of liberty as the Christians under Diocletian" | 001-030 |

### Strategic Disagreements

On questions of revolutionary strategy, Russell found Lenin rigidly orthodox. When Russell suggested that social transformation in [[england|England]] could be achieved without bloodshed, Lenin "waved aside the suggestion as fantastic" (001-025). Russell reports Lenin's tactical view that British Communists should work to elect a Labour government, "but solely in order that the futility of Parliamentarism may be conclusively demonstrated to the British working man" (001-019).

### Final Verdict

Russell's assessment is ambivalent but critical. He acknowledges Lenin's strength — "honesty, courage, and unwavering faith — religious faith in the Marxian gospel" — but faults him for having "as little love of liberty as the Christians who suffered under Diocletian, and retaliated when they acquired power" (001-030). Russell concludes:

> Contact with those who have no doubts has intensified a thousandfold my own doubts, not as to [[communism]] in itself, but as to the wisdom of holding a creed so firmly that for its sake men are willing to inflict widespread misery. (001-030)

## Bolshevik Perspective

### Theoretical Guardian

From the Bolshevik standpoint, Lenin was not merely a leader but the living embodiment of the [[proletariat]]'s historical mission. Russell reports how Lenin and his followers understood his role:

1. **Theoretical guardian** — Lenin was the authoritative interpreter of [[karl-marx|Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]], whose writings defined orthodoxy for the entire Communist movement. Russell observes: "When Lenin wishes to prove some proposition, he does so, if possible, by quoting texts from Marx and Engels" (P01-007)
2. **Strategic commander** — His tactical judgments, from the timing of the October insurrection to the Brest-Litovsk peace, were treated as irreproachable
3. **Moral exemplar** — The austerity of his Kremlin office — a desk, maps, two bookcases, and a few hard chairs — demonstrated the personal simplicity that Bolshevik propaganda celebrated

### Bolshevism as Religion

Russell notes that under Lenin, Bolshevism became "not merely a political doctrine" but "a religion, with elaborate dogmas and inspired scriptures" (P01-007). Lenin himself functioned, in Russell's analogy, as the prophet of this new faith, with [[karl-marx|Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]] as its sacred authors. This religious character, Russell argues, gave Bolshevism its fanatical intensity and intolerance of dissent (001-030).

## Key Ideas / Contributions

### Adaptation of Marxism

Lenin's contribution to Bolshevism was the adaptation of Marxian theory into a practical programme of revolutionary seizure of power:

- **Vanguard party**: A disciplined minority of professional revolutionaries who lead the proletariat to revolution, rather than waiting for the spontaneous development of class consciousness
- **Theory of imperialism**: [[capitalism|Capitalism]] had entered its highest and final stage, creating the conditions for [[world-revolution|world revolution]] — a theory that justified Bolshevik action in a country Marx himself would have considered too backward for socialist revolution
- **Democratic centralism**: The Party would debate internally but present a united front externally, concentrating all authority in its central leadership
- **Dictatorship of the proletariat**: A transitional state form in which the working class, led by the Party, suppresses the former exploiting classes — a concept Russell found to mean, in practice, the dictatorship of the Party over the proletariat

### Revolutionary Strategy

Lenin insisted on the necessity of violent revolution and the impossibility of peaceful transition to [[socialism]]. Russell reports that Lenin dismissed parliamentary gradualism as a "fantasy" (001-025), maintaining that only the forcible overthrow of the existing state apparatus could establish working-class rule. This conviction, Russell argued, made Lenin constitutionally incapable of compromise with democratic institutions (001-016).

## Relationships

### With Trotsky

Lenin appears alongside [[trotsky|Leon Trotsky]] throughout Russell's account. Russell reports that "the Communists do not by any means regard Trotsky as Lenin's equal" (001-031), and the two are presented as the twin pillars of the Bolshevik regime — already figuring "in woodcuts as Moses and Aaron, deliverers of their people" (001-039).

### With Russian Intellectuals

Lenin's relationship with [[gorky|Maxim Gorky]] was more complex. Gorky supported the Bolshevik government, but Russell felt in him "a love of the Russian people which makes their present martyrdom almost unbearable, and prevents the fanatical faith by which the pure Marxians are upheld" (001-032) — implicitly contrasting Gorky's human sympathy with Lenin's doctrinal rigidity.

### International Role

On the international stage, Lenin directed the [[third-international|Third International (Comintern)]], headquartered in Moscow, through which he sought to coordinate revolutionary movements worldwide. Russell observes that foreign socialists "are urged by Moscow" to launch insurrections modelled on the October Revolution (002-106).
