## Biographical Sketch

Alexander Fyodorovich Kerensky was born in 1881 in Simbirsk, Russia — the same town as Lenin, whose father had been Kerensky's father's superior at the local school. Trained as a lawyer, he rose to prominence as a defence attorney in political trials and was elected to the Fourth Duma in 1912 as a Trudovik (Labour) deputy. After the February Revolution of 1917, he served as Minister of Justice, then Minister of War, and finally as Minister-Chairman (Prime Minister) of the Provisional Government from July 1917 until the Bolshevik coup in October of that year. He fled Petrograd during the October Revolution and spent the rest of his long life in exile — in France, and later the United States — where he wrote and lectured extensively on the Revolution until his death in 1970.

## Role in the Russian Revolution

Kerensky occupies a peculiar position in Russell's account: he is neither hero nor villain, but rather a benchmark — the foil against which Bolshevik performance is measured. Russell does not idealize Kerensky's government. He acknowledges that "a great deal of disorganization occurred before the first revolution and under Kerensky" (001-081). Russian industry was weakened by dependence on Poland, the war was conducted "by methods of reckless extravagance, especially as regards rolling-stock," and under Kerensky "there was a tendency to universal holiday, under the impression that freedom had removed the necessity for work" (001-081).

Yet Russell's judgement is clear: "when all this is admitted to the full, it remains true that the state of industry under the Bolsheviks is much worse than even under Kerensky" (001-081). This is the devastating comparison that frames Kerensky's role in the book. Kerensky represents the best that non-Bolshevik Russia could offer — a flawed, chaotic, but fundamentally democratic alternative — and the Bolshevik claim to have improved the condition of Russia is measured against that standard and found wanting.

In a theatrical pageant Russell describes seeing, the whole revolutionary sequence passes before the audience: "Cars dashed by full of armed men, red flags appeared everywhere, the people stormed the citadel and hauled down the effigy of the Tsar. The Kerensky Government assumed control and drove them forth to war again, but soon they returned to the charge, destroyed the Provisional Government, and hoisted all the emblems of the Russian Soviet Republic" (001-053). This vivid passage captures Kerensky's historical fate: an interlude between Tsarism and Bolshevism, overwhelmed by forces he could neither control nor satisfy.

## The Industrial Benchmark

Russell returns to the Kerensky comparison in Part II of his book, where he examines Bolshevik theory in light of practical outcomes. The loss of skilled Communist workers to the front, to administration, and to party work produced a paradoxical result: their factories "were rendered even more inefficient than they were under Kerensky" (002-090). Russell uses this observation to challenge a core Bolshevik claim — that proletarian management would improve industrial productivity.

The comparison bears close examination. Russell concedes that Kerensky's regime was marked by serious failures: Russian industry "was partly dependent upon Poland, which was in the occupation of the Germans, and partly upon foreign trade, which was practically non-existent" (001-081). The war effort under Kerensky was conducted "by methods of reckless extravagance, especially as regards rolling-stock" (001-081). Workers, newly freed from Tsarist discipline, showed "a tendency to universal holiday" (001-081). In short, Kerensky's record offers no ideal to emulate.

But this is precisely what makes the comparison so damaging to Bolshevik claims. If Kerensky's record was dismal, and the Bolshevik record was "much worse" (001-081), then the Bolshevik method — minority dictatorship, forced labour, the suppression of independent trade unions — had failed even by the materialist standards it claimed to uphold. Russell does not argue that Kerensky could have succeeded where Lenin failed. His point is more fundamental: the Bolshevik claim to represent working-class interests was contradicted by empirical outcomes. The Kerensky benchmark transforms Russell's critique from philosophical disagreement into evidence-based argument.

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's assessment of Kerensky is indirect but significant. He uses Kerensky as a measuring stick in two distinct domains — industrial performance and regime durability — and in both cases the comparison reflects poorly on the Bolsheviks.

On industry, the comparison is explicit. Bolshevik apologists, Russell notes, might blame wartime disruption or Kerensky's mismanagement for Russia's economic collapse. Russell partly concedes the point: Kerensky's government was indeed marked by disorganization, indiscipline, and "reckless extravagance" (001-081). But this concession only strengthens the subsequent judgement: the Bolsheviks, who claimed to represent efficiency, discipline, and working-class rigour, had made things "much worse" (001-081). Russell returns to this comparison in Part II, noting that the loss of the best Communist workers to the front "rendered their factories even more inefficient than they were under Kerensky" (002-090).

Politically, Kerensky represents the democratic road not taken. The Bolsheviks destroyed the Provisional Government because it was bourgeois and ineffectual. Russell does not dispute that Kerensky's government was ineffectual; his point is that the Bolshevik alternative — minority dictatorship — produced outcomes that were, by empirical measures, worse. The Kerensky comparison thus serves Russell's larger argument: that the Bolshevik method, whatever its theoretical attractions, failed even on its own terms.

## Key Ideas / Contributions

Kerensky's principal contribution to Russian history was his attempt to hold together a democratic Russia between the February and October revolutions of 1917. He pursued a dual policy — continuing Russia's participation in the First World War while attempting domestic reform — that satisfied neither the army nor the radical Left. His decision to launch the June Offensive of 1917 proved disastrous militarily and politically, fatally weakening his government. Russell does not discuss these details; his interest is in the comparative outcome: the Bolshevik regime that replaced Kerensky's made Russia's situation worse on nearly every measurable dimension.

## Relationships

Kerensky's relationship with [[lenin]] was that of antagonist — the head of the Provisional Government versus the revolutionary who overthrew him. Russell does not explore the personal dimension of this relationship, but notes the political sequence: Kerensky's government "assumed control and drove them forth to war again, but soon they returned to the charge, destroyed the Provisional Government" (001-053). Where Kerensky attempted to govern within a democratic framework, Lenin imposed a [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] that, in Russell's view, was dictatorship in the literal sense.

Kerensky also stands in implicit contrast to figures like [[denikin]] and the White generals: Kerensky represented a non-Bolshevik alternative that was democratic rather than reactionary, reformist rather than restorationist. His failure, and the Bolshevik success that followed it, raised the question that haunts Russell's book: was the democratic centre doomed in Russia, and if so, was Bolshevik dictatorship the only alternative to White counter-revolution?
