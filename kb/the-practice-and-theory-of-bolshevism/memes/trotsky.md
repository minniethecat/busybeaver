## Biographical Sketch

Lev Davidovich Bronstein, known as Leon Trotsky, was born in 1879 in Yanovka, Ukraine. A Marxist revolutionary from his youth, he played a leading role in the 1905 Revolution as chairman of the St. Petersburg Soviet. After years of exile and factional struggle, he joined the Bolshevik Party in 1917 and became Lenin's closest collaborator. As People's Commissar for Military and Naval Affairs, he built the Red Army from scratch and led it to victory in the Russian Civil War. Following Lenin's death in 1924, Trotsky was outmanoeuvred by Stalin, exiled in 1929, and assassinated in Mexico in 1940.

## Role in Bolshevism

Trotsky was the military architect of Bolshevik survival. Russell encountered him at the height of his power, when he had organized the Red Army into an effective fighting force and was directing operations against the White armies on multiple fronts. At the Opera in Moscow, Russell witnessed the theatrical dimension of Trotsky's leadership: "After speaking with us in the antechamber, he stepped to the front of the box and stood with folded arms while the house cheered itself hoarse. Then he spoke a few sentences, short and sharp, with military precision" (001-022).

Russell recognized that Trotsky had mobilized nationalist sentiment behind the Bolshevik cause, noting that the Polish war and "the reconquest of Asiatic Russia has even revived what is essentially an imperialist way of feeling" (001-022). Yet Russell also observed that "Trotsky and the Red Army undoubtedly now have behind them a great body of nationalist sentiment" — a pragmatic success that, Russell suggests, was already altering Communist theories through "experience of power" (001-022).

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's portrait of Trotsky is vivid and personal, establishing an immediate contrast with Lenin:

> Trotsky, whom the Communists do not by any means regard as Lenin's equal, made more impression upon me from the point of view of intelligence and personality, though not of character. I saw too little of him, however, to have more than a very superficial impression. He has bright eyes, military bearing, lightning intelligence and magnetic personality. He is very good-looking, with admirable wavy hair; one feels he would be irresistible to women.
> （001-031）

Russell adds a more troubling dimension: "I felt in him a vein of gay good humour, so long as he was not crossed in any way. I thought, perhaps wrongly, that his vanity was even greater than his love of power — the sort of vanity that one associates with an artist or actor. The comparison with Napoleon was forced upon one" (001-031).

The Napoleon comparison is significant. Russell, having earlier compared Lenin to Cromwell, now places Trotsky in the tradition of the charismatic military leader whose ambition may exceed his ideological commitments. He is careful to qualify: "I had no means of estimating the strength of his Communist conviction, which may be very sincere and profound" (001-031). But the implication is clear — Trotsky's personality, more than Lenin's, raised questions about whether the [[revolution]] was becoming a vehicle for individual ambition.

Trotsky and Lenin already appear together in Bolshevik propaganda as figures of almost mythic stature: "Lenin and Trotsky already figure in woodcuts as Moses and Aaron, deliverers of their people" (001-039). Russell's account captures Trotsky at the apex of his revolutionary career, before his later fall.

## Trotsky and Gorky: A Study in Contrast

In a chapter devoted jointly to "Lenin, Trotsky and Gorky" (001-023), Russell sets up a deliberate juxtaposition between Trotsky — the magnetic man of action — and Maxim [[gorky]], the voice of suffering Russia. The contrast illuminates a tension at the heart of Russell's book: between revolutionary will and human cost.

Russell's encounter with Gorky occurred in Petrograd, where the writer lay ill and heartbroken:

> An extraordinary contrast to both these men was Gorky, with whom I had a brief interview in Petrograd. He was in bed, apparently very ill and obviously heartbroken. He begged me, in anything I might say about Russia, always to emphasize what Russia has suffered.
> （001-032）

Where Trotsky embodied the disciplined, forward-looking energy of the Bolshevik state, Gorky represented what was being left behind — the suffering of the Russian people, the fragility of culture, the human toll of revolution. Russell found Gorky "the most lovable, and to me the most sympathetic, of all the Russians I saw" (001-032), a telling admission after his extended analysis of Lenin and Trotsky's political power.

Gorky's defence of the Bolshevik Government — "not because he thinks it faultless, but because the possible alternatives are worse" (001-032) — parallels Russell's own position in the book's final pages. Yet Russell detected in Gorky "a love of the Russian people which makes their present martyrdom almost unbearable, and prevents the fanatical faith by which the pure Marxians are upheld" (001-032). The implicit distinction is between Trotsky's ideological certainty and Gorky's anguished humanism — between the soldier and the artist, the commissar and the conscience.

This tension is not merely personal. It reflects a structural problem in Bolshevik rule that Russell diagnoses throughout the book: the suppression of the anarchic, the artistic, and the human in favour of organization, discipline, and doctrine. When a Bolshevik official tells Russell "We haven't time for a new art, any more than for a new religion" (001-032), the remark captures what Gorky was struggling to preserve, and what Trotsky, for all his intelligence and magnetism, was complicit in marginalizing.

## Key Ideas / Contributions

Trotsky's most significant contribution to Bolshevism was the theory of "permanent revolution" — the idea that the Russian Revolution should not stop at the bourgeois-democratic stage but proceed directly to proletarian rule, and that socialism in one country was impossible without world revolution. As Commissar for War, he demonstrated remarkable organizational genius, turning disorganized armed workers' detachments into a disciplined, conscript-based Red Army of five million. His military successes against [[denikin]], Kolchak, and other White generals were decisive in preserving the Bolshevik state.

## Relationships

Trotsky's relationship with [[lenin]] is the central interpersonal dynamic of the early Bolshevik regime. Russell notes that Communists "do not by any means regard Trotsky as Lenin's equal" (001-031), positioning him as the brilliant but secondary figure. The two are paired throughout Russell's narrative: together they appear in propaganda woodcuts, together they personify the dual character of Bolshevik rule — Lenin the ideological authority, Trotsky the man of action. His relationship with [[gorky]], by contrast, represents the tension between revolutionary discipline and human sympathy (001-032), a theme Russell develops through the book's examination of Bolshevik methods and their consequences.
