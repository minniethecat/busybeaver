Yakov Sverdlov was a Bolshevik party organizer and the chairman of the All-Russian Central Executive Committee, effectively serving as the head of state of the Russian Soviet Republic from 1917 until his death in 1919.

## Encounters with Russell

Bertrand Russell met Sverdlov during his 1920 visit to Soviet Russia. Russell was travelling down the Volga River from Nizhny Novgorod to Saratov, and continued on to Astrakhan when his companion Clifford Allen fell ill. On the boat he "made me acquainted with Sverdlov, Acting Minister of Transport, who was travelling on the boat to organize the movement of oil from Baku up the Volga, and who was one of the ablest as well as kindest people whom I met in Russia" [001-013].

Russell's personal impression of Sverdlov stands out in his account of the trip. While Russell was critical of many Bolshevik officials he encountered, he described Sverdlov in notably warm terms, singling him out as both able and kind — a rare personal compliment in a narrative otherwise marked by political scepticism.

## Role in the Bolshevik State

As a skilled organizer, Sverdlov played a crucial role in consolidating Bolshevik power after the October Revolution. He was responsible for party administration and cadre management, earning a reputation as the party's most effective organizational operative. His early death in 1919 at the age of 33, during the Spanish flu pandemic, was a significant loss to the Bolshevik leadership. Sverdlov had been instrumental in building the party apparatus that enabled the Bolsheviks to govern during the Civil War.
