# Dictatorship

The concept of the **dictatorship of the proletariat** was, in Bertrand Russell's analysis, the theoretical keystone of [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] — and the point at which Bolshevik theory diverged most decisively from the democratic traditions of Western socialism.

## Marx's Concept

In Marx's original formulation, the dictatorship of the proletariat was conceived as a transitional state form between the overthrow of [[capitalism]] and the establishment of a classless communist society. The term "dictatorship" was used in the Roman sense — a temporary concentration of power to meet an emergency — rather than in the modern sense of permanent authoritarian rule.

## The Bolshevik Interpretation

Russell observed that the Bolsheviks radically transformed the meaning of the concept. As he wrote: "When a Russian Communist speaks of dictatorship, he means the word literally, but when he speaks of the proletariat, he means the word in a Pickwickian sense. He means the 'class-conscious' part of the proletariat, i.e., the Communist Party." (001-006) The dictatorship of the proletariat became, in practice, the dictatorship of the [[communist-party|Communist Party]] over the proletariat.

## Russell's Critique

Russell argued that this semantic shift was not accidental but essential to Bolshevik practice. The theoretical claim to represent the proletariat allowed the party to suppress actual proletarian opposition — as it did at [[kronstadt-rebellion|Kronstadt]] — while maintaining the fiction of democratic legitimacy. Russell noted that the Bolsheviks, like Cromwell's Puritans, were "driven to sacrifice democracy to religion enforced by military dictatorship." (001-016)

## Kautsky's Challenge

Russell drew on [[kautsky|Karl Kautsky]]'s critique that dictatorship and democracy were fundamentally incompatible. The Bolsheviks' response — that all states are class dictatorships, and proletarian dictatorship was merely more honest — Russell found logically coherent but politically disastrous, since it justified any measure, however oppressive, as historically necessary.
