# Rationing under Bolshevism

**Rationing under Bolshevism** was the system of state-controlled distribution of food and consumer goods that Bertrand Russell observed as a central feature of daily life during his 1920 visit to [[moscow|Moscow]]. Rationing was simultaneously an instrument of economic policy, a mechanism of social control, and — for most Russians — the difference between survival and starvation.

## The Ration System

The Bolshevik state had assumed responsibility for the distribution of all essential goods. In theory, rations were allocated according to class and occupation: workers received more than the [[bourgeoisie]]; soldiers and Party members received more than ordinary citizens; children and nursing mothers received special allocations. In practice, Russell observed, the ration was often more a promise than a reality — the state simply did not have enough food to distribute.

Russell described the queues that were a ubiquitous feature of Moscow life — lines of women waiting hours for bread, milk, or fuel that might or might not arrive. The ration card, he noted, was simultaneously a lifeline (without it, one could not legally obtain food) and a symbol of the state's failure (with it, one still could not obtain enough food to live decently).

## Class and Rationing

Russell was particularly attentive to the class dimensions of the ration system. The Bolsheviks had designed ration categories to reward the [[proletariat]] and punish the [[bourgeoisie]] — but the categories were administered by the Party, which meant that political loyalty rather than class position often determined who ate and who starved.

Russell also noted the irony that the former bourgeoisie — professionals, intellectuals, skilled administrators — were often those whose expertise the state most needed, yet they were consigned to the lowest ration categories. The ration system, like so much of Bolshevik policy, was caught between ideological commitment and practical necessity.

## The Black Market

Where the state failed, the black market filled the gap. Russell observed the emergence of an illegal economy in which food, fuel, clothing, and other necessities were traded at prices far above the official rates. The Bolsheviks periodically cracked down on "speculators," but the black market persisted because it performed a function the state could not: it got goods to those who needed them.

Russell saw the black market as evidence of a deeper truth about the Bolshevik economy: the abolition of market exchange did not eliminate the need for distribution; it merely drove that distribution underground, where it operated without regulation, without accountability, and at enormous human cost.
