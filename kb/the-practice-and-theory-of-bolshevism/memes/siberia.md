# Siberia

**Siberia** — the immense territory stretching from the Urals to the Pacific — was a significant theatre of the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]] and the [[allied-intervention|Allied intervention]]. Bertrand Russell discussed Siberia primarily in the context of Allied military operations and the fate of the White armies that contested Bolshevik control of the region.

## The Siberian Theatre

Siberia posed unique challenges for both the Bolsheviks and their adversaries. Its vast distances, sparse population, and limited transport infrastructure made military operations logistically punishing. The Trans-Siberian Railway was the single artery connecting the region to European Russia, and control of the railway was, in effect, control of Siberia.

The White forces in Siberia under Admiral [[kolchak|Alexander Kolchak]] posed the most serious military threat to the Bolsheviks in the east. Supported by Allied, particularly British, material assistance, Kolchak's army advanced deep into European Russia in 1919 before being turned back by the [[red-army|Red Army]]. Russell noted the irony that foreign intervention — intended to destroy Bolshevism — had the effect of strengthening it, by enabling the Bolsheviks to portray themselves as defenders of Russian territory against foreign invasion.

## The Czechoslovak Legion

The Siberian theatre was also the site of one of the war's most extraordinary episodes: the odyssey of the Czechoslovak Legion, a force of Czech and Slovak former prisoners of war who fought their way across the entire length of Siberia to reach the Pacific and eventual evacuation. Their presence in Siberia — initially as an Allied force, later as a factor complicating all sides' military calculations — illustrated the chaotic multinational character of the Russian Civil War.

## Russell's Perspective

Russell's treatment of Siberia was brief but significant. He saw the Siberian campaign as evidence of the futility of the Allied intervention: a massive expenditure of resources and lives in a remote theatre, for objectives that were unclear, unachievable, or contradictory. The Siberian experience reinforced his broader argument that the attempt to destroy Bolshevism by force was both immoral and impractical.
