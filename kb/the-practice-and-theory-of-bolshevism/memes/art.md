# Art

## Definition

Art, in Russell's analysis of Bolshevism, encompasses the full range of creative and aesthetic production — theatre, painting, sculpture, crafts, music, poetry, and mass pageantry — as it existed in Soviet Russia in 1920 and as it was being reshaped by the revolutionary state. Russell treats art as a sensitive indicator of the deeper tensions within the Bolshevik experiment: the conflict between inherited cultural traditions and revolutionary ideology, between individual expression and collective purpose, and between the aesthetic values of the past and the industrial imperatives of the future.

## Russell's Analysis

Russell devotes one of the most detailed sections of his book to the condition of art under the Bolsheviks. He begins by acknowledging that the Soviet government "began by recognizing, as perhaps no other revolutionary government would, the importance and spontaneity of the artistic impulse" (001-035). Artists of all political creeds were granted freedom to continue their work, were well treated in terms of rations and clothing, and the Bolsheviks devoted remarkable care to the preservation of churches, monuments, and museums.

Yet Russell argues that this policy of tolerance has produced unintended consequences. "Three years' experience, however, have proved the falsity of this doctrine and led to a divorce between art and popular feeling which a sensitive observer cannot fail to remark" (001-037).

This divorce is most visible in the theatre, where companies continued to perform the old classics to audiences whose daily experience was utterly disconnected from the world depicted on stage. Russell observes that "as one sits in the theatre, one is so acutely conscious of the discrepancy between the daily life of the audience and that depicted in the play that the latter seems utterly dead and meaningless" (001-037).

Russell identifies two forces threatening the vitality of Russian art. The first is the pressure toward propaganda. More fiery Communists, he reports, "complain that bourgeois art is being preserved long after its time" and "would like to see only the revolutionary mood embodied in art" (001-037). While Russell notes that direct censorship has thus far been avoided, he warns that "from the religious nature of Communism in Russia, such controlling of the impulse to artistic creation is inevitable, and that propaganda art alone can flourish in such an atmosphere" (001-037).

The poet who satirizes Bolshevik leaders rather than White generals, or the artist whose temperament does not conform to revolutionary orthodoxy, finds no outlet for publication.

The second and more fundamental threat, in Russell's view, is industrial development. He argues that "industry in its early stages seems everywhere doomed to be the enemy of beauty and instinctive life" (001-041). The craftsman who works with his hands, following ancestral traditions, is "useless when brought face to face with a machine" (001-044).

A state struggling with economic difficulties "is bound to be slow to admit an artistic vocation, since this involves exemption from practical work" (001-042). Russell reports an actual instance of a man with the talent and temperament to become an opera singer who, "under the pressure of Communist enthusiasm and Government encouragement," turned his attention to economics instead (001-042).

Russell does not, however, regard the artistic situation as entirely bleak. He finds grounds for hope in the popular artistic impulse of the Russian peasant — the carving, embroidery, and spontaneous decoration of daily life — and in the possibility that the plastic arts "have shown that they can live by mass action, and flourish under an intolerant faith" (001-039).

He describes with admiration the propaganda pictures in which "Lenin and Trotsky already figure in woodcuts as Moses and Aaron, deliverers of their people" (001-039) and notes that "the Church tradition in painting is passing with incredible smoothness into the service of Communist doctrine" (001-039).

> Three years' experience, however, have proved the falsity of this doctrine and led to a divorce between art and popular feeling which a sensitive observer cannot fail to remark. It is glaringly apparent in the hitherto most vital of all Russian arts, the theatre. (001-037)

## Bolshevik Perspective

The Bolsheviks approached art with a distinctive blend of tolerance and instrumentalism. The initial policy — articulated most clearly by Lunacharsky, the Commissar of Enlightenment — was that art and politics belonged to separate realms, and that artists should be free to pursue their work without political interference. This was rooted in a genuine appreciation for the value of culture and a recognition that the revolution needed the legitimacy that cultural continuity could provide.

At the same time, the Bolsheviks expected that the revolution would inspire a new art, and they honoured those artists who embraced this calling. Russell witnessed two forms of Communist-inspired performance: the propaganda play *Zarevo* (The Dawn), depicting the struggles of a revolutionary working-class family (001-048), and the pageant of the "World Commune" performed at the opening of the Third International Congress in Petrograd — a "mystery play designed by the High Priests of the Communist faith to instruct the people" (001-050).

## Historical Context

Russell's observations on art were made at a moment of extraordinary cultural ferment in Russia. The pre-revolutionary artistic avant-garde — figures like Malevich, Tatlin, and Mayakovsky — had initially embraced the revolution as a liberation from bourgeois aesthetic constraints. The period of War Communism saw bold experiments in public art, agitprop trains, and mass spectacle. Russell's analysis captures the moment before the full consolidation of state control over artistic production that would characterize the Stalinist period, when the tension between freedom and propaganda was still unresolved.
