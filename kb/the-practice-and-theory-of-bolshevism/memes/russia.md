# Russia

**Russia**, as Russell encountered it in May–June 1920, was a nation in the grip of revolutionary transformation, civil war, and material hardship. A predominantly peasant country — "they form about eighty-five percent of the population" — it had become the world's first [[communism|Communist]] state, governed from [[moscow]] by the [[communist-party|Bolshevik Party]] (001-123). Russell spent five weeks there, travelling from Petrograd to the [[volga|Volga]] steppe, observing everything from Kremlin audiences to village markets, and working to disentangle what was specifically [[bolshevism|Bolshevik]] from what was the common aftermath of war, blockade, and imperial collapse.

## Overview

Russell presents Russia as a nation of stark paradoxes. It was simultaneously the most backward of the Great Powers — its peasant masses illiterate and its industry concentrated in a few urban islands — and the most ideologically advanced, attempting to leap from feudalism to communism in a single revolutionary bound. The revolution, Russell notes, "happened to begin in Russia, though Marxian theory would seem to demand that it should begin in America" (001-019). This accident of history shaped everything that followed.

### A Nation of Paradoxes

Russell's Russia is a country whose revolutionary government "represents the interests of the urban and industrial population, and is, as it were, encamped amid a peasant nation, with whom its relations are rather diplomatic and military than governmental in the ordinary sense" (001-123). The demographic reality created an insoluble political tension: "if Russia were governed democratically, according to the will of the majority, the inhabitants of Moscow and Petrograd would die of starvation" (001-123).

## In Russell's Narrative

Russell's account of Russia weaves together observation, analysis, and historical comparison. He presents the Russian Revolution as "one of the great heroic events of the world's history," exceeding the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] in its ambition to "change daily life and the structure of society" (P01-001). Yet his admiration is tempered by sharp criticism of Bolshevik methods.

### The Urban-Rural Divide

A central theme is the **urban-rural divide**. The Bolshevik government, based in Moscow and Petrograd, depended on peasants for food yet had little organic connection to the countryside:

> The Bolshevik Government represents the interests of the urban and industrial population, and is, as it were, encamped amid a peasant nation, with whom its relations are rather diplomatic and military than governmental in the ordinary sense. (001-123)

Russell carefully separates the effects of war and blockade from Bolshevik policy. He insists that many of Russia's visible hardships — hunger, disease, industrial collapse — were the common inheritance of defeated nations:

> To begin with, Russia is one of the nations that were defeated in the war; this has produced a set of circumstances resembling those found in Germany and Austria. (001-008)

Yet he refuses to let the Bolsheviks escape responsibility for those hardships that were of their own making. He acknowledges the Entente's guilt in imposing the blockade but insists that "the expectation of such opposition was always part of Bolshevik theory" (001-009).

### Historical Parallels

Russell draws historical parallels between Bolshevik Russia and Cromwell's Puritan England. The sincere Communists, he writes, are "not unlike the Puritan soldiers in their stern politico-moral purpose" (001-016). Both movements combined religious intensity with military discipline, and both produced regimes in which a zealous minority imposed its vision on an unwilling majority.

### Demographic Realities

The overwhelming peasant majority — 85 percent of the population — was the fundamental fact of Russian social life, and it imposed constraints on Bolshevik policy that no amount of ideological commitment could overcome. Russell grasped that the regime's urban base was a tiny island in a peasant sea, and that this asymmetry explained much of the regime's authoritarian turn.

### Economic Collapse

The economic situation was catastrophic. Russell reports that "the collapse of industry in Russia is extraordinarily complete" (002-079), with factories idle for want of fuel and raw materials, railways barely functioning, and skilled workers dispersed to the countryside in search of food. Russia's pre-war dependence on foreign assistance — machinery, locomotives, and "technical brains were mainly imported" — made it unusually vulnerable to collapse after the Entente turned hostile (001-082).

## Role in Bolshevism

Russia was both the **laboratory and proving-ground** of Bolshevism. It was here that Marxian theory first confronted the realities of governance, and Russell's book is, in large part, a report on the results of that confrontation. He identifies several ways in which Russia's specific conditions shaped — and distorted — Bolshevik practice:

1. **Backwardness as catalyst**: The very backwardness of Russia made Bolshevik methods possible. In a more developed country with a literate population and democratic traditions, Russell argues, "they are not the methods appropriate" (001-144)
2. **Peasant resistance**: The peasant majority's indifference or hostility to Communism forced the regime into an adversarial relationship with 85% of its population
3. **Isolation and siege mentality**: The Entente blockade and foreign intervention reinforced the Bolsheviks' conspiratorial world-view and justified the extension of terror
4. **Imperial inheritance**: The Bolsheviks inherited the Tsarist state's bureaucratic and coercive apparatus, which they repurposed rather than dismantled

### Russia as Laboratory

Russell's central argument about Russia is that its specific conditions both enabled and distorted the Bolshevik experiment. The country's backwardness — its illiterate peasantry, its collapsed industry, its lack of democratic traditions — made Bolshevik methods possible in a way they would not be in Western Europe. But this same backwardness meant that the experiment could not serve as a model for the developed world, whatever the Bolsheviks and their Western admirers might claim.

## Russell's Observations

Russell's assessment of Russia is nuanced to the point of paradox. He believes "the heroism of Russia has fired men's hopes in a way which was essential to the realization of Communism in the future" (P01-003). He acknowledges that Bolshevik methods "are probably more or less unavoidable" in a backward country where "industry is extraordinarily complete" in its collapse and the alternatives to Bolshevik rule might well be worse (001-144, 002-079).

### A Qualified Judgment

Yet he refuses to endorse the Bolshevik project. His position is one of **qualified sympathy for the Russian experiment and firm opposition to its export**. Russia, in his analysis, demonstrated both the necessity of radical social transformation and the perils of pursuing it through authoritarian means. The lesson he draws is not that communism is wrong, but that "the price to be paid for its achievement is too great in terms of human suffering" when pursued through Bolshevik methods (002-061).
