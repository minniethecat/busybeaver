# Russian Revolution

The **Russian Revolution** was the sequence of political and social upheavals in 1917 that overthrew the three-centuries-old Romanov autocracy and installed the world's first [[communism|Communist]] government. Russell opens his book by declaring it "one of the great heroic events of the world's history," comparing it to the French Revolution but arguing it is "something of even more importance" because it aims to change not only daily life and the structure of society but "also does more to change men's beliefs" (P01-001). Russell's treatment of the Revolution weaves together his direct observations of [[russia|post-revolutionary Russia]], his conversations with [[lenin|Lenin]] and other Bolshevik leaders, and his philosophical assessment of the [[bolshevism|Bolshevik]] experiment as both a revolutionary hope and a cautionary tale.

## Overview

The Revolution unfolded in distinct stages. In February 1917 (March in the Western calendar), mass strikes and mutinies in Petrograd forced Tsar Nicholas II to abdicate, ending three centuries of Romanov rule. A Provisional Government under [[kerensky|Alexander Kerensky]] attempted to establish liberal democracy while continuing Russia's disastrous participation in the [[world-war|Great War]]. Eight months later, in October 1917 (November in the Western calendar), the Bolsheviks under Lenin seized power in a swift, relatively bloodless coup, overthrowing the Provisional Government and establishing the Council of People's Commissars.

### Two Revolutions

Russell captures the drama of this sequence in his account of the "World Commune" pageant staged in Petrograd:

> Cars dashed by full of armed men, red flags appeared everywhere, the people stormed the citadel and hauled down the effigy of the Tsar. The Kerensky Government assumed control and drove them forth to war again, but soon they returned to the charge, destroyed the Provisional Government, and hoisted all the emblems of the Russian Soviet Republic. (001-053)

## Russell's Account

Russell visited Soviet Russia in May–June 1920, travelling with the British Labour Delegation. He entered through a "desolate region of marsh, pine wood, and barbed wire entanglements" — a frontier that physically embodied the rupture between revolutionary Russia and the capitalist West. His assessment is nuanced: he admires the revolution's heroism and accepts that communism is "necessary to the world," but condemns the Bolshevik methods as "a pioneer method, rough and dangerous, too heroic to count the cost of the opposition it arouses" (P01-004).

### Three Possible Futures

Russell identifies three possible outcomes from the revolution:

| Scenario | Description | PN |
|----------|------------|-----|
| **Defeat by capitalism** | "The ultimate defeat of Bolshevism by the forces of capitalism" | P01-004 |
| **Napoleonic corruption** | "The victory of the Bolshevists accompanied by a complete loss of their ideals and a regime of Napoleonic imperialism" | P01-004 |
| **Prolonged world-war** | "A prolonged world-war, in which civilization will go under" | P01-004 |

### A Hybrid Phenomenon

His core insight is that Bolshevism is a hybrid phenomenon, unprecedented in world history:

> Bolshevism combines the characteristics of the French Revolution with those of the rise of Islam; and the result is something radically new, which can only be understood by a patient and passionate effort of imagination. (P01-001)

## Stages of the Revolution

Russell's narrative, combined with his later analysis, allows us to reconstruct the Revolution's key phases:

1. **February 1917** — Mass strikes and military mutinies in Petrograd force the Tsar's abdication. A Provisional Government is formed, dominated initially by liberals and moderate socialists, committed to continuing the war and convening a Constituent Assembly
2. **Dual power (March–October 1917)** — The Provisional Government competes for authority with the Petrograd Soviet and a growing network of workers' and soldiers' councils. The Bolsheviks, initially marginal, gain influence by opposing the war and demanding "peace, land, and bread"
3. **October 1917** — The Bolsheviks, now commanding majorities in the key Soviets, seize power in Petrograd on 7 November (25 October O.S.). Russell describes this as the moment when the Bolsheviks "destroyed the Provisional Government, and hoisted all the emblems of the Russian Soviet Republic" (001-053)
4. **Civil War (1917–1922)** — The Bolsheviks consolidate power through the [[russian-civil-war|Civil War]], fighting White armies, foreign interventions, and peasant uprisings. The period of "War Communism" — characterized by grain requisitioning, nationalization, and terror — hardens the regime's authoritarian character

### From February to October

The eight months between the February and October revolutions were the period in which Russia's political options narrowed from liberal democracy to Bolshevik dictatorship. Russell's account suggests that the Provisional Government's fatal decision to continue the war destroyed its legitimacy, creating the opening the Bolsheviks needed. The logic of the dual-power period led inexorably toward the October seizure, as the Bolsheviks' simple demands — peace, land, bread — gained force against the Provisional Government's temporizing.

## Bolshevik Perspective

From the Bolshevik standpoint, the Revolution was the inevitable historical moment foretold by [[karl-marx|Marx]]. Russell explains: "A general hostility to the first Communist State was both foreseen and provoked by the doctrine of the class war" (001-009). The Bolsheviks saw themselves not as initiating violence but as responding to the inevitable counter-revolutionary resistance predicted by Marxist theory.

### Creating Hope

The Bolshevik leaders believed fervently in the world-historical mission of their movement. Russell writes:

> The chief thing that the Bolsheviks have done is to create a hope, or at any rate to make strong and widespread a hope which was formerly confined to a few. (001-002)

Yet Russell was troubled by the Bolsheviks' open embrace of minority revolution as a strategy. He argues that this openness makes the strategy self-defeating:

> This whole Bolshevik theory of revolution by a minority is one which might just conceivably have succeeded as a secret plot, but becomes impossible as soon as it is openly avowed and advocated. (002-036)

## Significance

Russell considers the Revolution's wider significance to lie in its attempt to realize communism under the most unpromising conditions. "By far the most important aspect of the Russian Revolution is as an attempt to realize Communism" (P01-003). He argues that "the heroism of Russia has fired men's hopes in a way which was essential to the realization of Communism in the future" (P01-003), while warning that the Revolution's methods risk undermining its own ideals.

### Lasting Impact

The [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]], established as a temporary measure, had by 1920 hardened into a permanent system of minority rule. Russell's verdict on the Revolution is thus profoundly ambivalent: he honours its heroism, acknowledges its historical necessity, but refuses to endorse the methods through which it consolidated power. The Revolution, in his analysis, demonstrated both the transformative potential of collective action and the dangers of sacrificing means to ends.
