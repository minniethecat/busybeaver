# German Revolution of 1918–1919

The **German Revolution of 1918–1919** — the revolutionary upheaval that overthrew the Hohenzollern monarchy and established the Weimar Republic — was, for the Bolsheviks, the great hope and the great disappointment of the postwar period. Bertrand Russell, writing in 1920, discussed the German Revolution as the crucial test case for Bolshevik expectations of [[world-revolution|world revolution]].

## Revolutionary Expectations

The Bolsheviks had always argued that the Russian Revolution was the first act of a world revolutionary drama. Russia, as the "weakest link" in the imperialist chain, had broken first — but the survival of the revolution depended on its spread to advanced industrial countries, above all [[germany|Germany]].

The German Revolution of November 1918 seemed, briefly, to fulfil these expectations. The Kaiser was overthrown; workers' and soldiers' councils were formed on the Russian model; and the German Communist Party (KPD) was founded by revolutionaries who had been inspired by the Bolshevik example. For a moment, it appeared that the world revolution Lenin had predicted was unfolding.

## The Disappointment

The German Revolution did not follow the Bolshevik script. The moderate Social Democrats (SPD), led by Friedrich Ebert, outmanoeuvred the revolutionary left. The workers' councils were subordinated to parliamentary institutions; the old imperial bureaucracy and military command were preserved; and the revolutionary left — including [[karl-marx|Marx]]'s own disciples, Karl Liebknecht and Rosa Luxemburg — was suppressed, in Luxemburg's case with the murder of its leaders.

Russell observed this outcome with a mixture of relief and concern. The failure of communist revolution in Germany meant that Bolshevik Russia would remain isolated — a development Russell welcomed insofar as it limited the spread of Bolshevik methods, but regretted insofar as it perpetuated the international tensions that had produced the [[world-war|World War]].

## Russell's Analysis

The German Revolution illustrated, for Russell, both the appeal and the limitations of the Bolshevik model. The revolution had occurred — the Kaiser had fallen, the old order had collapsed — but the outcome was not Bolshevik. Democratic socialism, not vanguard dictatorship, had prevailed. Russell saw in this outcome evidence that the Bolshevik claim to represent the only possible form of revolutionary transformation was false.
