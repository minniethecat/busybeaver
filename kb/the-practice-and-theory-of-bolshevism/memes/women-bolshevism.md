# Women under Bolshevism

**Women under Bolshevism** was a subject Bertrand Russell addressed in his 1920 study with characteristic attention to the gap between theoretical commitment and practical reality. The Bolsheviks had enacted some of the most progressive legislation on women's rights and family law in the world — and yet, Russell observed, the material conditions of Russian women under war, blockade, and economic collapse were often desperate.

## Bolshevik Reforms

Russell acknowledged the genuine achievements of Bolshevik policy regarding women. The new Soviet government had:
- Legalised divorce and made it available to women on equal terms with men
- Abolished the legal distinction between legitimate and illegitimate children
- Removed legal barriers to women's employment and education
- Established communal facilities (kitchens, laundries, crèches) intended to reduce women's domestic burden and enable their participation in public life

These reforms represented, Russell noted, a radical departure from both Tsarist law and the laws of most Western countries at the time. They reflected the Marxian principle that women's emancipation was inseparable from the abolition of class society and the transformation of the [[family-bolshevism|family]] as an institution (001-057).

## Material Reality

Russell balanced his recognition of legal progress with observations of material hardship. The women he encountered in [[moscow|Moscow]] and [[petrograd|Petrograd]] were not primarily concerned with legal equality — they were struggling to feed their children, to find fuel for heating, and to survive the combined pressures of civil war, blockade, and economic collapse.

The Bolsheviks' ambitious social programmes — communal kitchens, public childcare — remained largely aspirational under conditions of extreme scarcity. Russell noted that the promise of liberation from domestic drudgery had, for most women, remained a promise unfulfilled (001-071).

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's analysis of women's condition under Bolshevism exemplified his broader method: recognising the sincerity of Bolshevik intentions while insisting on the primacy of actual outcomes. The Bolsheviks had correctly identified women's oppression as a feature of class society and had designed legal reforms to address it. But legal reform, Russell argued, was insufficient when material conditions made equality impossible, and when the political system denied women — like men — any voice in the decisions that governed their lives.

Russell also noted the tension between Bolshevik rhetoric of women's emancipation and the patriarchal culture that persisted within the Party itself. The Bolshevik leadership was almost entirely male, and the Party's organisational culture reflected the gender hierarchies of the society it claimed to be transforming.
