# Viéra's First Dream

Viéra's first dream occurs in Part I, Chapter XII, after Lopukhóf has introduced her to materialist philosophy and the theory of egotism. The dream marks the moment when Viéra's intellectual awakening crystallizes into a conscious rejection of her circumstances.

## Narrative Context

The dream occurs during the period when Marya Alekséyevna is actively scheming to marry Viéra to Storeshnikof, and Lopukhóf is serving as Viéra's tutor. The conversation about the theory of egotism that Marya Alekséyevna overheard has convinced her that Lopukhóf is not a threat — a miscalculation that the dream's symbolism will explode.

## Summary

Viéra dreams she is "locked up in a damp, gloomy cellar" when suddenly a door opens and she finds herself in a field, running and frolicking freely. She thinks: "How is it that I did not die in the cellar? It is because I had never seen the fields before! Had I seen them, I must have died in the cellar" (028-003). She then dreams she is paralyzed — an affliction of the old, not the young — but an unknown voice tells her she will soon be well: "Let me only touch your hand; you see you are well already; now get up!" (028-004).

## Thematic Significance

The dream's imagery is transparent: the cellar is the Rozálsky household, the field is the world of freedom she has glimpsed through Lopukhóf's books and conversation, and the paralysis is the condition of passive acceptance that her mother's world imposes on women. The dream establishes the novel's central metaphor — liberation from confinement through knowledge — and inaugurates the pattern of allegorical dreams that structure Viéra's development.
