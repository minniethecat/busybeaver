# Viéra's Fourth Dream

Viéra Pavlovna's Fourth Dream is the novel's culminating vision. Occurring late in the narrative, it presents a fully realized utopia — a society transformed by the principles of rational egotism, cooperative labor, and women's emancipation.

## Narrative Context

The dream occurs after Viéra has achieved economic independence through the sewing cooperative, navigated the transition from Lopukhóf to Kirsánof, and established a life of work, love, and intellectual development. It represents the full flowering of her philosophical understanding — the personal journey completed, the dream now projects outward to imagine a transformed world.

## Summary

A familiar voice guides Viéra through a landscape of extraordinary abundance: fields of wheat with heads larger than any known variety, orchards of tropical fruit growing in the open air, groves carefully tended. At the center stands "an enormous, enormous edifice, such as can be seen only in the largest capitals — or, no, at the present time there is none such in the world" (088-079). The palace is built of cast-iron and glass, with interior walls and furniture of aluminum — then a precious metal, here the common building material of the future. Inside, the inhabitants share labor and leisure: children do much of the housework, meals are communal, and "whoever pleases has a better one, with whatever he may prefer" (088-087). The elder sister explains that "every year the people, you Russians, are pushing away the boundary of the desert to the south" — the utopia is not static but expanding (088-106).

## Thematic Significance

The Fourth Dream gives architectural and social form to the novel's philosophical program. The Crystal Palace — modeled on Joseph Paxton's building at Sydenham — represents the material abundance that rational social organization can produce. The society within is the realization of the principles Viéra has been practicing: cooperative labor, mutual respect, the integration of work and pleasure, the abolition of the distinction between private and public life. The dream answers the novel's title question: What is to be done? Build the Crystal Palace.
