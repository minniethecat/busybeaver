# Viéra's Third Dream

Viéra's Third Dream marks a crucial transition in her psychological development — from intellectual understanding to emotional self-awareness, from passive recipient of the "new people's" philosophy to an active, desiring consciousness.

## Narrative Context

The dream occurs in Part III, as Viéra's feelings for Kirsánof are beginning to surface and her relationship with Lopukhóf is undergoing its subtle transformation. The dream is the mechanism by which her unconscious awareness becomes conscious — the moment when she can no longer avoid recognizing what she feels.

## Summary

The dream begins not with sleep but with reading — Viéra lies on her bed, a book falling from her hands, and her thoughts drift. She thinks about the opera singer Angiolina Bosio, whose voice she adores: "I would go every night to the opera, if there were opera, no matter how bad it might be, provided only Bosio sang the chief role" (060-003). She thinks about Kirsánof's failure to get opera tickets, about her husband's sweetness, and about her own vague dissatisfaction. The dream weaves together aesthetic longing, emotional restlessness, and intellectual reflection — the threads that, once woven, will constitute her new understanding of herself.

## Thematic Significance

The Third Dream is the novel's most psychologically sophisticated passage. It captures the texture of consciousness — the way thoughts drift from Bosio to opera tickets to Kirsánof to Lopukhóf to the nature of love — and in doing so, it enacts the very self-awareness that the philosophy of egotism requires. Viéra is learning to observe her own mind, to recognize the stirrings of desire before they become demands, and to analyze them with the same rational clarity she applies to the cooperative's accounts.
