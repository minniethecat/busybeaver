# The Lopukhófs' Apartment

The Lopukhófs' apartment is the novel's most detailed architectural argument — a domestic space designed to embody the philosophy of the "new people."

## The Architecture of Autonomy

The apartment's defining feature is the "neutral room" (later "neutral rooms") that connect the separate bedrooms of husband and wife. This spatial arrangement is the architectural correlative of the fictitious marriage: private spaces for each partner, shared spaces for companionship, and neutral spaces for the transition between them. The design refuses the conventional marital architecture of the shared bedroom — the spatial assumption that marriage means fusion — in favor of a model where intimacy is chosen rather than assumed.

## A Social Space

Beyond the bedrooms and neutral rooms, the apartment functions as a social center for the "new people's" circle. Young instructors from the sewing cooperative, Lopukhóf's former classmates, the Mertsálofs — all gather here for evenings of conversation, singing, and intellectual exchange. The apartment is not a retreat from the world but a base from which to engage it.
