# Sewing Cooperative (Union)

The Sewing Cooperative, founded by [[Viéra Pavlovna]] on the principle of shared ownership and equal profit distribution, is the novel's central organizational experiment — an enterprise where seamstresses govern themselves, share profits equally, and pursue education alongside their labor. It evolves from a modest workshop of three seamstresses into a multi-faceted institution with its own bank, purchasing agency, residential apartments, and lyceum for continuing education.

> The cooperative is the organizational counterpart to the domestic philosophy of the "new people" — just as the [[The Lopukhófs Apartment|Lopukhófs' apartment]] spatializes equality through "neutral rooms," the sewing union institutionalizes it through profit-sharing and collective governance. (045-001, 045-018)

## Founding and Principles

Viéra Pavlovna established the sewing union with deliberately simple foundations. She did not impose rules upon her first three seamstresses; she simply paid them "a trifle more than the regular seamstresses were getting at the shops" (045-001). The founding principle was not charity but demonstration — to show that a workshop organized on cooperative principles could be both economically viable and personally liberating for its workers.

From the outset, the cooperative's legitimacy rested on Viéra Pavlovna's own labor: "For about a year Viéra Pavlovna spent the larger part of each day at the shop, and really worked as hard as anyone else, according to the schedule of hours" (045-017). She refused a special share of the profits, insisting that equality of ownership must be matched by equality of contribution.

## Organizational Evolution

### Profit-Sharing (First Three Years)

The cooperative's profit-sharing system evolved through several stages, each representing a deeper understanding of economic equality:

| Period | Profit-Sharing Method | Principle |
|---|---|---|
| Founding (Year 1) | Proportionally by wages earned | Reward for individual productivity |
| Early Year 2 | Equal share regardless of work days missed | Solidarity with the ill and absent |
| Mid Year 2 | Cutters with extra wages excluded from extra profit share | Distinction between wage compensation and ownership returns |
| Mid Year 3 | All members receive equal profit share regardless of role | The profit belongs to the cooperative as a system, not to individual skill |

> "The receiving of the profits was not the reward for the art of one or two of their number, but the result of the general character of the shop — the result of its arrangement, its aim." (045-018)

### Institutional Expansion

As the cooperative grew from three to over twenty members, it developed subsidiary institutions:

| Institution | Function | Established |
|---|---|---|
| Reserve Bank | Interest-free loans to members in need; accumulation of capital | Year 1 |
| Commission House | Wholesale purchasing of tea, coffee, sugar, footwear, and provisions for members | Year 1–2 |
| Residential Apartments | Communal living quarters with rooms for seamstresses, elderly relatives, and children | Year 1.5 |
| Common Table | Shared meals, provisions purchased through the agency, "exactly as is done in great establishments" (045-020) | Year 2 |
| Retail Agency | Sale of ready-made clothing through arrangements with shops in Gostinui Dvor and a stall in the Pushing Market | Year 2–3 |
| Lyceum | Regular courses of instruction in history, science, and languages, with professors including Alekséi Petróvitch Mertsálof, [[Dmitri Lopukhóf|Lopukhóf]], and students | Year 3 |

## Governance

The cooperative operated through democratic decision-making: "giving a decisive voice to the entire shop in the management of its business" (045-017). The first collective decision was that Viéra Pavlovna should not work without pay — a decision she accepted as correct while redirecting the offered share of profits back into the common fund to establish the bank.

Viéra Pavlovna's leadership style was deliberately self-effacing: "she took pains to bring others to the front, so that a good many of the ladies, who came to the shop to give orders, could not distinguish her from the other cutters" (045-034). Her "pet dream" was that the shop "could get along without her, so that, in time, other shops might be established of the same kind, entirely spontaneously — by the thought and planning of the seamstresses themselves" (045-034).

## Social and Educational Function

The cooperative functioned as more than a workplace. Reading aloud during working hours began from the very first days — Viéra Pavlovna would read for half an hour, then the seamstresses would rest from listening, then more reading (045-024). Within three months, two or three good readers among the seamstresses had been delegated to share the reading duty, with the half-hour counted as regular work.

> "My sewing union is becoming a lyceum for all possible knowledge." (045-025)

The lyceum's curriculum included Russian history, international history, and other subjects taught by Mertsálof, Lopukhóf, students, and Viéra Pavlovna herself. Two girls passed their examinations as private teachers in the third year (045-034).

The cooperative also organized amusements — evening parties, picnics out of town, boxes at the theatre, and during the third winter, ten places in the parquet circle of the Italian opera (045-032).

## Key Passages

> "Viéra Pavlovna's sewing union was established. The foundations were very simple at first — so simple, indeed, that it is not worth while to speak of them." (045-001) — The opening of the cooperative narrative, emphasizing its organic, undramatic beginnings.

> "The most difficult task of all was to develop the idea that the ordinary seamstresses were all entitled to an equal share in the profits, notwithstanding the fact that some of them were earning more wages than others." (045-018) — The cooperative's central organizational challenge: separating wage compensation from profit distribution.

> "The silent acquiescence of the most hesitating and of the least gifted is no less beneficial for the preservation and development of the scheme, no less profitable for all of them, and for the success of the whole enterprise, than the active zeal of the most lively and gifted." (045-018) — The philosophical justification for equal profit-sharing: all forms of participation sustain the cooperative.

> "She took pains to bring others to the front... Her pet dream was that the shop could get along without her, so that other shops might be established of the same kind, entirely spontaneously." (045-034) — Viéra Pavlovna's vision of a self-replicating cooperative movement.

## Narrative Role

The Sewing Cooperative is the practical embodiment of the novel's economic philosophy. Where the "rational egoism" articulated by [[Dmitri Lopukhóf|Lopukhóf]] and [[Aleksandr Kirsánof|Kirsánof]] provides the theoretical framework, the cooperative provides the material demonstration: a working institution that proves cooperative labor can be both profitable and emancipatory.

The cooperative also functions as a narrative device for measuring Viéra Pavlovna's development. Her management of the cooperative — from initial founding through institutional expansion to the dream of a self-sustaining movement — parallels and extends the personal emancipation she achieves through her [[Fictitious Marriage|fictitious marriage]] and her intellectual awakening.

## Thematic Connections

The cooperative links the novel's domestic, economic, and educational themes. The residential apartments extend the architectural philosophy of [[The Lopukhófs Apartment|the Lopukhófs' apartment]] from the private household to a collective living arrangement. The lyceum extends the "new people's" commitment to self-education from individual study to institutional instruction. The profit-sharing system extends the principle of rational self-interest from personal ethics to organizational economics.
