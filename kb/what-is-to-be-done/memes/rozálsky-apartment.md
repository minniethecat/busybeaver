# The Rozálsky Apartment

The Rozálsky apartment on Gorokhovaïa Street is the novel's primary image of the old order's domestic space — a "cellar" from which Viéra Pavlovna must escape.

## The Domestic Prison

The apartment is ruled by Marya Alekséyevna's commodifying logic: every relationship is a transaction, every person has a price, and Viéra is the family's most valuable asset. The spatial arrangement of the apartment — the parlor where Storeshnikof is entertained, the corners where Viéra reads forbidden books, the spaces where Marya Alekséyevna eavesdrops on Lopukhóf's conversations — maps the structure of surveillance and control that defines the old order's domesticity.

## The Cellar in the Dream

Viéra's First Dream transforms the apartment into allegory: "She dreamed that she was locked up in a damp, gloomy cellar" (028-003). The cellar is the psychological truth of the apartment — not a home but a prison, not a family but a market. Liberation comes when the door opens and Viéra finds herself in a field — the world beyond Gorokhovaïa Street.
