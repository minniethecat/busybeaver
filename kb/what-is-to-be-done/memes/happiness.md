# Happiness

Happiness is the goal toward which the entire philosophical apparatus of *What Is to Be Done?* is directed. Chernyshevsky's conception of happiness is not the passive enjoyment of pleasure but the active satisfaction of a fully developed human being exercising their capacities in work, love, and social life. The novel systematically demonstrates that happiness achieved through the [[Theory of Egotism|rational pursuit of self-interest]] is inseparable from the happiness of others, and that social arrangements — particularly economic independence through institutions like the [[Sewing Cooperative]] — are the foundation upon which genuine human flourishing can be built.

## The Right to Happiness

The novel's most direct address to the reader frames happiness as both a right and a duty:

> "Desire to be happy! that is all; only this desire is wanted. And for this sake with delight watch over your development; there is happiness in it" (072-013)

This is the novel's democratic promise — happiness is not reserved for exceptional natures but is accessible to all who pursue self-development. The narrator insists that the "new people" are not extraordinary: "It is not they that stand so high, but you that stand so low... Upon the height upon which they stand all people can stand, and must" (072-015).

Chernyshevsky further argues that happiness is fundamentally social:

> "Our own happiness is impossible without the happiness of others" (004-006)

This principle distinguishes the novel's egoism from crude selfishness: properly understood, self-interest requires attending to the conditions that allow others to flourish alongside oneself.

## Happiness and Work

[[Viéra Pavlovna]]'s sewing cooperative is presented as a source of happiness not because it generates profit but because it provides meaningful activity. The narrator describes her pleasure in the work:

> "Bright and gay was the ordinary course of the business, and it filled Viéra Pavlovna's heart with constant happiness" (045-034)

But her happiness was much more than work-satisfaction alone: her life had become rich with friendships, intellectual growth, and the sense of building something meaningful with others. The cooperative model transforms work from a necessity endured for survival into a source of satisfaction — one of the novel's central arguments for economic reorganization.

## Happiness and the Theory of Egotism

The novel's [[Theory of Egotism]] resolves the apparent tension between self-interest and altruism by arguing that the highest happiness comes from acting in accordance with one's conception of human dignity. [[Dmitri Lopukhóf|Lopukhóf]], explaining his decision to withdraw from Viéra's life, describes "what a great pleasure it is to feel yourself acting like a noble man... simply and purely a man!" (074-025). The "noble calculation" produces happiness that egoistic pleasure-seeking cannot match.

Lopukhóf articulates the core feeling that motivates the new people:

> "I feel joy and happiness; consequently, I want all people to feel joy and happiness" (021-006)

## The Fourth Dream's Vision

In the Fourth Dream, the elder sister explains that in the society of the future "here is a healthful and peaceful life; it preserves the freshness" — people grow old very late because life itself is a source of continuous satisfaction (088-080). The palace society's happiness is not the result of material abundance alone but of social arrangements that allow human capacities to develop harmoniously.

|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| [[Viéra Pavlovna]] | Work + love + friendship | [[Sewing Cooperative]] | Economic independence enables flourishing |
