# Vasilyevsky Island

Vasilyevsky Island is the St. Petersburg district where the "new people" establish their households — a geography of emancipation carved out of the imperial capital. It is the spatial center of the novel's domestic revolution, home to [[The Lopukhófs Apartment]] with its famous "neutral rooms," and later to [[The Kirsánofs Apartment]], which continues the same architectural philosophy. Across the water lie the wider streets of the old city — [[Gorokhovaïa Street]], [[Sadovïa Street]] — and the [[Semyonovsky Bridge]] that connects the island to the rest of [[St. Petersburg]].

## The "New People's" Territory

The Lopukhófs' apartment, with its separate bedrooms and connecting "neutral rooms," spatializes the philosophy of the "new people." The architecture of the apartment — private spaces for work and reflection, shared spaces for conversation and companionship — embodies the novel's argument that autonomy and intimacy are not opposed but mutually reinforcing. The Kirsánofs' later apartment continues the same spatial principles, as does the sewing workshop with its communal workrooms and individual stations.

## A Different Petersburg

Vasilyevsky Island represents the alternative Petersburg — the city that exists within the old capital but is not of it. The "new people's" gatherings, with their singing, dancing, serious conversation, and shared meals, create a social world that operates according to principles of mutual respect and rational enjoyment rather than hierarchy and transaction. The island is not a utopian elsewhere but a transformation of the existing city from within.

> "The apartment on Vasilyevsky Island had three rooms — hers, his, and the neutral room between them, where they met as equals." (015-022) — The spatial arrangement is a material argument: equality is not an abstraction but a practice embedded in architecture.

> "Across the Semyonovsky Bridge lay another Petersburg — the Petersburg of ranks and uniforms, of drawing rooms where women were displayed and men assessed." (012-005) — The bridge marks a threshold between two social worlds, and the "new people" cross it only on their own terms.

## Thematic Significance

The island is the novel's central spatial metaphor. It is physically separated from the mainland by water, yet connected by bridges — a geography that mirrors the "new people's" relationship to Russian society: distinct but not withdrawn, autonomous but engaged. The domestic architecture of the island — neutral rooms, separate bedrooms, workshops rather than parlors — makes visible the novel's argument that social transformation begins with the reorganization of everyday space.
