# Aleksandr Kirsánof

Aleksandr Matvéitch Kirsánof is the character who most fully embodies the practical application of the theory of egotism. As a medical scientist who has given up private practice for research, and as the man who wins Viéra Pavlovna's love after Lopukhóf's disappearance, he represents the novel's ideal of rational conduct in both professional and personal life.

## Professional Life

Kirsánof is a professor of medicine whose reputation rests on experimental research rather than clinical practice. He declares: "I do not cure; I only observe, and make experiments" (051-003). This self-description is belied by the hospital servants' view of him: "Keep up good heart; it takes a tough sickness to stand up against this surgeon; he is a master, and a real father" (051-003). The disjunction between his theoretical self-presentation and his practical effectiveness is characteristic of the novel's method — the "new people" redescribe their most humane actions in the language of egoistic calculation.

## The Withdrawal

When Kirsánof realizes he has fallen in love with Viéra Pavlovna, his response is a methodical withdrawal from the Lopukhófs' household — a maneuver executed "so slowly, so steadily, like the hands of a clock" that it is almost imperceptible (059-003). He analyzes his decision in rigorously self-interested terms: "Every man is an egotist; so am I; now the question comes up, 'Is it more profitable for me to go away, or to remain?'" (058-003). The months-long withdrawal is framed not as moral virtue but as "coldly calculating common sense" — the satisfaction of preserving his capacity for self-respect outweighs the pleasure of Viéra's company.

## The Theorist's Pleasure

Like Lopukhóf, Kirsánof derives ironic satisfaction from observing his own egotism at work. After his conversation with Lopukhóf about Viéra, he would have reflected: "How true this theory is. I wanted to preserve my own peace, to rest upon my laurels... and therefore fall on your knees before the grandeur of my soul!" (063-047). This recursive self-mockery — the recognition that even the act of recognizing one's egotism is itself egoistic — is the novel's most sophisticated gesture.

## Life with Viéra

After Lopukhóf's disappearance, Kirsánof and Viéra marry and establish a household that embodies the novel's ideals. Their relationship, as Viéra describes it after three years, disproves the conventional wisdom that "love grows weak, when there is nothing to hinder people from belonging wholly to each other" (087-001). Their love deepens through proximity and security because it is based not on obstacles and longing but on the ongoing satisfaction of shared life between equals.
