# Opera and Culture

Art, particularly music and opera, plays a significant role in the novel's vision of human development. The "new people" are distinguished not only by their rational philosophy and economic arrangements but by their cultivation of aesthetic sensibility — their capacity for the enjoyment of beauty as an essential component of a fully human life.

## Bosio and the Third Dream

Viéra Pavlovna's Third Dream begins not with slumber but with reading, and quickly turns to thoughts of the opera singer Angiolina Bosio. "I would go every night to the opera, if there were opera, no matter how bad it might be, provided only Bosio sang the chief role; if I had such a voice as Bosio, it seems to me I would sing all day long" (060-003). The dream weaves together Viéra's aesthetic longings with her growing emotional and intellectual independence — the desire to hear Bosio sing is inseparable from her desire to live a fuller, more autonomous life.

## Music in Daily Life

Viéra's domestic life includes regular musical practice. "Now Viéra Pavlovna sometimes works or reads, or rests from reading by playing on the piano, till very late, even till two o'clock" (046-006). The piano — a "grand" bought at a bargain for a hundred rubles — is both a luxury and a necessity: "this also was a great happiness, to own their own grand" (046-006). Music is not an ornament to life but an integral part of it, a source of happiness and self-expression.

## Culture as Self-Development

The novel's narrator exhorts the reader: "Observe, think, read the works of those who tell you about the pure enjoyments of life, about the fact that a man can be kind and happy! Read them; they are books which fill the heart with joy. Observe life; for it is interesting to observe. Think; for it is delightful to think" (072-015). Cultural engagement — reading, observation, reflection — is presented as the path to self-development and, through self-development, to happiness.
