# The Sapient Reader

The "sapient reader" (*pronitsatelny chitatel*) is a fictional construct whom Chernyshevsky's narrator repeatedly addresses in satirical colloquies throughout the novel. This figure embodies the conventional expectations — literary, moral, and social — that the novel is designed to subvert.

## Narrative Function

The sapient reader appears at key moments to voice the objections that a conventional reader of 1860s Russian literature might be expected to raise: Why isn't the hero more heroic? Why isn't the love triangle more tragic? Why don't the characters experience guilt? The narrator responds to these objections with a mixture of mockery and instruction, using the sapient reader as a foil to clarify the novel's philosophical positions.

## The "New People" Framed

The colloquy with the sapient reader that closes the Rakhmétof chapter is the novel's most explicit statement of its anthropological argument: "It is not they that stand so high, but you that stand so low" (072-015). The sapient reader misperceives the "new people" as idealized heroes because he is accustomed to a literature of exceptional individuals. The narrator insists that Viéra, Lopukhóf, and Kirsánof are ordinary — "simple, ordinary people, like ourselves" — and that it is the reader's degraded expectations that make them appear extraordinary.
