# Theory of Egotism

The philosophical doctrine at the heart of Chernyshevsky's novel — the idea that all human actions are governed by calculated self-interest (*vygoda*), and that this principle, far from being cynical, provides the foundation for the most rational and noble conduct.

## Definition

The theory holds that every human action arises from a single cause: **self-interest** (*vygoda*, translated as "profit" or "advantage"). When an action has vital importance, the stimulus is called self-interest (or "egotism"), and its operation in human beings is the **calculation of self-interests**. According to this theory, a person always acts under the influence of the strongest motive — there is no free will independent of necessity, only the balancing of competing interests (024-049).

Lopukhóf introduces the theory with a memorable analogy: "A match is cold, the matchbox on which you scratch the match is also cold; but there is fire in them which gets a man warm food, and warms him also." The theory appears cold and prosaic, but "it reveals true motives of life and poetry in the truth of life" (024-024).

## Origin in the Novel

The theory is first expounded in **Part I, Chapter VIII ("The Temptation of Hamlet")**, during a pivotal conversation between **[[Dmitri-Lopukhóf|Lopukhóf]]** and **[[Viéra-Pavlovna|Viéra Pavlovna]]**. Lopukhóf, a medical student and materialist philosopher, lays out the doctrine in response to Viérotchka's questions about whether all actions can really be explained by self-interest (024-016). Viéra initially resists — "this theory is cold!" (024-023) — but is gradually convinced, acknowledging that she had long felt the same truth even before reading Lopukhóf's books (024-025).

## Key Passages

> Love for science was only a result arising from a certain state of things; it was not its cause; the cause was just one thing — self-interest. (024-016)

> This theory is merciless; but if it is followed, people will not become the wretched objects of idle charity. The lancet must not bend; otherwise it will be necessary to pity the patient, who will suffer none the less because of your sympathy. (024-024)

> When an action has vital importance, the stimulus is called self-interest; its interaction in man is the calculation of self-interests, and therefore a man must always act in accordance with the motive of self-interest. (024-049)

> It is pleasant for a man, as a theorist, to notice what tricks his egotism plays with him in practical life. You are retreating from the battle because the battle is lost for you, but egotism turns your gestures so that you are playing the man who is doing noble actions. (063-046)

> If you had only heard how he explained his behavior towards me and Dmitri Sergéitch in the course of three years, you would learn, if his words were to be taken literally, that he did everything through egotistical calculation for his own pleasure. And I long ago learned this custom... Yes, if one were to hear us, all three of us would be taken to be such egotists as the world has never seen. (075-003)

## In Practice: Noble Calculation

The theory's most distinctive application is the concept of **noble calculation** — the recognition that apparently selfless actions (renouncing a professorship, giving up a romantic rival, even faking one's own death) are themselves expressions of self-interest, because the satisfaction of acting according to one's highest conception of human dignity is itself the greatest possible profit.

Kirsánof, applying the theory to his decision to withdraw from his growing attraction to Viéra Pavlovna, reasons: "Every man is an egotist; so am I; now the question comes up, 'Is it more profitable for me to go away, or to remain?' ... I must not call my decision either noble or even virtuous, these are too high words; I must simply call it coldly calculating common sense" (058-003).

Lopukhóf, after suppressing the most important truth in his conversation with Kirsánof, reflects with satisfaction: "How true the theory is, Egotism makes sport of men. Now here the most important thing he entirely suppressed... I held my peace because it was not to my advantage to say it" (063-046).
