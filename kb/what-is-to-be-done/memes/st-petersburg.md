# St. Petersburg

St. Petersburg is the primary setting of Chernyshevsky's novel — the city where Viéra Pavlovna is born, imprisoned, liberated, and transformed. It functions not merely as backdrop but as the social and architectural embodiment of the forces the "new people" must navigate.

## The City as Social Structure

The Petersburg of the novel is a city of sharp social stratification. The Rozálsky household on Gorokhovaïa Street represents the petty bourgeois world of officials and marriage markets. The Lopukhófs' apartment, with its "neutral rooms" and carefully designed domestic space, represents the "new people's" transformation of urban living. The sewing workshop, the hospital where Kirsánof works, and the apartments where the "new people's" circle gathers map a geography of emancipation — spaces carved out of the old city where new forms of life can be practiced.

## Two Cities

Viéra Pavlovna experiences two Petersburgs: the "cellar" of her parents' world and the "field" of freedom she discovers through Lopukhóf and Kirsánof. These are not different locations but different ways of inhabiting the same city — the difference between a Petersburg governed by hierarchy and transaction and a Petersburg organized around work, friendship, and mutual development.
