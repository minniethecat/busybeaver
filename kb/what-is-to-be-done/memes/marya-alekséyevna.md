# Marya Alekséyevna

Marya Alekséyevna Rozálskaya, Viéra Pavlovna's mother, is the novel's most fully realized representative of the "old order" — a woman whose domineering pragmatism and cynical manipulation of her daughter reveal the logic of a society that treats women as commodities.

## A Product of Her World

Marya Alekséyevna is introduced as the antagonist of Viéra's early life — the keeper of the "cellar" from which Viéra must escape. She schemes to marry Viéra to the wealthy Storeshnikof, viewing her daughter as an asset whose value must be maximized before it depreciates. Yet the novel is careful not to present her as simply evil. Her overheard reflections on Lopukhóf's conversation with Viéra reveal a mind capable of recognizing intelligence: "What a wise, intelligent, and I may say noble, young man he is!" (024-053). Her tragedy is that she recognizes merit but can only conceive of it in instrumental terms.

## The Distorted Mirror

Marya Alekséyevna's philosophy — that one must calculate advantage and act accordingly — is a degraded version of the theory of egotism. She operates on the same fundamental premise (self-interest governs action) but within a social system that offers her no path to satisfaction except through domination and exploitation. Her famous reflection — "If I had been a well-educated woman, would it have been with me as it is now?" (024-053) — is a flash of self-awareness that the novel treats with surprising sympathy. She knows that her ignorance is her prison.

## Narrative Function

Marya Alekséyevna serves as the foil against which Viéra's development is measured. The "cellar" she maintains is not merely a physical prison but an epistemological one — a world in which women's value is determined by their market price. Viéra's escape is not an escape from her mother's personality but from the social logic her mother embodies. The novel's argument is that Marya Alekséyevna, too, could have been different under different conditions — a point that deepens rather than excuses her cruelty.
