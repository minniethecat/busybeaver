# Science and Progress

Natural science, particularly medicine, serves in Chernyshevsky's novel as both a profession for the "new people" and a metaphor for their approach to life. The scientific method — observation, experiment, rational analysis — is the epistemological foundation of the novel's entire philosophical apparatus.

## Kirsánof's Scientific Vocation

Kirsánof embodies the scientific ethos in its purest form. As a professor of medicine who has given up private practice, he declares: "I do not cure; I only observe, and make experiments" (051-003). The hospital servants, who see him take hopeless cases into his own quarters and restore them to health, have a different view: "Keep up good heart; it takes a tough sickness to stand up against this surgeon; he is a master, and a real father" (051-003). The contrast between Kirsánof's self-description as a pure researcher and the servants' perception of him as a healer captures the novel's synthesis of scientific rigor and human concern.

## Science as Social Authority

The novel repeatedly invokes scientific authority to validate its philosophical claims. The narrator references Virchow and Claude Bernard — contemporary giants of medical science — as the standard against which Kirsánof's work is measured. When Kirsánof is appointed professor despite the opposition of the medical faculty's old guard, it is the recognition of these European scientific authorities that forces the appointment through (051-003). Science, in the novel's world, is the solvent of traditional authority and the foundation of legitimate expertise.

## The Scientific Worldview

The theory of egotism itself is presented as a scientific discovery about human nature — the application of naturalistic observation to moral psychology. Lopukhóf's book, which Viéra reads before meeting him, is presumably a work of popular science that applies materialist analysis to human motivation. The novel's ambition is to extend the scientific method from the natural world to the social world, making ethics a branch of natural science.
