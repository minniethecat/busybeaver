# Rakhmétof

Rakhmétof is the novel's "extraordinary man" — a figure of revolutionary asceticism whose self-transformation through discipline and renunciation places him in a category beyond the "new people." The narrator presents him not as a model to emulate but as a benchmark against which the "new people's" ordinariness can be measured.

## The Extraordinary Man

The narrator introduces Rakhmétof with a deliberate framing: "I have shown you a slight sketch of the profile of one of them; you see very different features" (072-015). Unlike Viéra, Lopukhóf, and Kirsánof — who are "simple, ordinary people, like ourselves" — Rakhmétof represents a higher type: the person who dedicates their entire existence to a single purpose. The narrator insists that only a handful of such people exist in each generation, "the flower of the best people, the motive powers of the motive powers, the salt of the salt of the earth."

## The Rigorist's Discipline

Rakhmétof's self-cultivation takes the form of systematic physical and moral discipline. He sleeps on a bed of nails to test his endurance, eats only what is necessary for strength (refusing luxury of any kind), and trains his body through manual labor — earning the nickname "Nikitushka Lomof" after a legendary Volga boatman. He reads systematically and voraciously, absorbing knowledge according to a strict plan. His famous rule: "I am not going to drink a drop of wine; I shall not touch a woman" (070-029) — not from puritanism but from the calculation that these pleasures would divert energy from his purpose.

## The Intervention

Rakhmétof appears in the novel at a single crucial moment: after Lopukhóf's disappearance, he visits Viéra to deliver Lopukhóf's letter and to explain — in characteristically harsh terms — why the suicide was necessary. Viéra describes him as "the formidable Rakhmétof — and what a kind, tenderhearted man he is!" (075-005). The combination of formidable rigor and hidden tenderness is the essence of his character. He speaks harsh truths because kindness, in his calculus, would be a form of self-indulgence.

## Function in the Novel

Rakhmétof serves a specific narrative and philosophical function. By showing a figure of extraordinary dedication, the narrator establishes the scale: the "new people" are not the ceiling of human development but a floor — "upon the height upon which they stand all people can stand, and must" (072-015). Rakhmétof occupies a higher elevation, visible but not immediately accessible. The narrator explicitly warns that he is not a model: "I describe him not because I want to teach you to follow his example, for that is impossible for you."
