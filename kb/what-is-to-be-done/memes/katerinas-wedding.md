# Katerina's Wedding

Katerina Vasílyevna's marriage to Charles Beaumont extends the novel's pattern of relationships beyond the central triangle of Viéra, Lopukhóf, and Kirsánof — demonstrating that the "new people's" principles can be replicated.

## Narrative Context

Katerina, daughter of the wealthy contractor Pólozof, has become Viéra Pavlovna's close friend and correspondent. Her letters to Viéra after Lopukhóf's disappearance reveal a mind already shaped by the "new people's" philosophy. When Charles Beaumont arrives in Petersburg — an American businessman of Russian origin — Katerina recognizes in him a kindred spirit.

## Summary

Katerina and Beaumont marry, establishing a household that mirrors the Kirsánofs' in its principles of mutual respect and autonomy. The revelation that Beaumont is Lopukhóf returned adds a layer of narrative satisfaction without introducing melodrama: Katerina has married not a stranger but the man whose philosophy she already admired through Viéra's accounts. The wedding is not the novel's climax but its confirmation — evidence that the "new people's" way of life is reproducible, not dependent on exceptional individuals.

## Thematic Significance

Katerina's wedding demonstrates the novel's faith in the expandability of its principles. The "new people" are not a closed circle of three extraordinary individuals but a model that can be replicated by anyone who embraces rational self-interest and mutual respect. The marriage also completes the novel's structural symmetry: two couples, two marriages, two demonstrations of the same thesis.
