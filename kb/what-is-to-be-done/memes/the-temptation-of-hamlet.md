# The Temptation of Hamlet

"The Temptation of Hamlet" (Part I, Chapter VIII) is the novel's philosophical foundation — the conversation between Lopukhóf and Viéra Pavlovna in which the theory of egotism receives its first and most complete articulation.

## Narrative Context

Lopukhóf is visiting the Rozálsky household as Viéra's tutor. Marya Alekséyevna, having concluded that his learned conversation is harmless, eavesdrops on their discussion — a dramatic irony that deepens the scene, as she misinterprets his philosophical argument as conventional advice to accept a rich suitor.

## Summary

Lopukhóf explains that all human actions arise from a single cause: self-interest (*vygoda*). When Viéra objects that "this theory is cold," he responds with the novel's most famous analogy: "A match is cold, the matchbox on which you scratch the match is also cold; but there is fire in them which gets a man warm food, and warms him also" (024-024). He extends the argument: "This theory is merciless; but if it is followed, people will not become the wretched objects of idle charity" (024-024). Viéra, initially resistant, reveals that she has already arrived at the same conclusions independently: "I myself long ago felt the same thing, especially after I read your book" (024-025). The conversation then moves from theory to practice as Viéra tests the doctrine against a concrete case: should she accept Storeshnikof's proposal? Lopukhóf's response — "Reason out what is for your best" (024-048) — is the theory's characteristic refusal of moral judgment in favor of rational calculation.

## Thematic Significance

This conversation is the intellectual event that makes all subsequent events possible. By giving Viéra the philosophical framework to analyze her situation as a problem of self-interest rather than a conflict of duties, Lopukhóf equips her with the intellectual tools she needs to escape. The chapter title — "The Temptation of Hamlet" — signals the novel's ambition: to replace Hamlet's paralyzing moral introspection with a philosophy of decisive, rational action.
