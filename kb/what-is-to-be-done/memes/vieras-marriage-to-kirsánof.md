# Viéra's Marriage to Kirsánof

Viéra Pavlovna's second marriage represents the novel's ideal of a union between fully developed, autonomous individuals — a relationship chosen freely and sustained by mutual satisfaction rather than obligation.

## Narrative Context

After Lopukhóf's fake suicide, the legal obstacle to Viéra and Kirsánof's union is removed. The marriage that follows is presented not as a reward for virtue or patience but as the natural culmination of a process in which all three parties acted according to rational self-interest. There is no guilt, no melodrama, and no moral accounting — only the recognition that relationships, like all human arrangements, should serve the happiness of those involved.

## Summary

Viéra and Kirsánof marry and establish a household that embodies the novel's principles. Their domestic life includes work (his research, her cooperative), intellectual companionship, and a circle of like-minded friends. After three years, Viéra reflects that their love has not weakened but deepened: "Where did people get the idea that love grows weak, when there is nothing to hinder people from belonging wholly to each other? These people did not know true love" (087-001).

## Thematic Significance

The marriage to Kirsánof demonstrates the novel's thesis that true love is based not on obstacles and longing but on the ongoing satisfaction of shared life between equals. It is the positive demonstration — the Lopukhóf marriage was the instrumental one — that the "new people's" principles can produce relationships that are both more rational and more deeply satisfying than the romantic conventions they replace.
