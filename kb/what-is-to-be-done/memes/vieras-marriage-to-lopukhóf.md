# Viéra's Marriage to Lopukhóf

Viéra Pavlovna's marriage to Dmitri Lopukhóf is the novel's founding event — the legal act that liberates her from the "cellar" and establishes the conditions for her development into a "new woman."

## Narrative Context

Marya Alekséyevna has been scheming to marry Viéra to Storeshnikof. Lopukhóf, serving as Viéra's tutor, has introduced her to materialist philosophy and the theory of egotism. Recognizing both her intelligence and her desperation, he proposes a solution: a legal marriage that will free her from parental authority while preserving her independence.

## Summary

The marriage is performed by Father Mertsálof, a priest who belongs to the "new people's" circle. The domestic arrangement that follows is carefully designed: separate bedrooms connected by a "neutral room" — a spatial metaphor for their relationship as equal partners rather than fused identities. The marriage is "fictitious" in the sense that it uses the legal form of the old order to achieve the substantive goals of the new — individual autonomy and freedom of development. The form is hollowed out and repurposed.

## Thematic Significance

The marriage to Lopukhóf is the novel's first demonstration that the "new people" can use the institutions of the old order against themselves. Rather than demanding the abolition of marriage, the novel shows how marriage can be transformed from within — stripped of its patriarchal content and repurposed as an instrument of liberation. The marriage also establishes the pattern of the "new people's" relationships: entered into for rational purposes, sustained by mutual respect, and dissolvable without catastrophe when they have served their purpose.
