# Establishment of the Sewing Cooperative

The founding of the sewing cooperative is the novel's central economic event — the translation of philosophical principle into practical institution.

## Narrative Context

After her marriage to Lopukhóf, Viéra Pavlovna seeks meaningful activity beyond the domestic sphere. The decision to establish a sewing workshop grows from her recognition that economic independence requires productive work — and that the existing system of dressmaking, with its exploitative wages and degrading conditions, is incompatible with human dignity.

## Summary

Viéra begins with deliberate simplicity: three carefully selected seamstresses, paid "a trifle more than the regular seamstresses were getting at the shops" (045-001). There are no formal rules at first — the foundation is trust. At the end of the first month, she presents a complete account of income and expenses, then divides the remaining profits equally among all workers. The moment of division is transformative: the seamstresses "could not believe their senses, so great was their surprise" (045-010). Viéra then explains her philosophy: the cooperative is not a business but a "hobby" — work pursued for its intrinsic satisfaction rather than for profit (045-011). Over three years, the cooperative grows from three to twenty workers, establishes a mutual bank, evolves its profit-sharing from proportional to equal division, and develops a system of democratic self-governance.

## Thematic Significance

The establishment of the cooperative is the novel's demonstration that economic reorganization is both possible and practical. The cooperative's success — it never loses a customer, expands steadily, and produces genuine satisfaction for its workers — constitutes an empirical argument against the claim that exploitation is necessary for efficiency. The event marks Viéra's transition from passive beneficiary of liberation to active creator of institutions.
