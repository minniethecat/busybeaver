# Lopukhóf's Fake Suicide

Lopukhóf's fake suicide is the novel's central plot mechanism — the event that resolves the love triangle of Viéra, Lopukhóf, and Kirsánof without melodrama, guilt, or tragedy.

## Narrative Context

After Viéra's Third Dream reveals to her — and to Lopukhóf — that her feelings have shifted to Kirsánof, Lopukhóf faces the problem of dissolving a marriage that all three parties recognize has reached its natural end. Divorce is effectively impossible in the Russia of the 1860s. The only mechanism for dissolution is death.

## Summary

Lopukhóf travels to Riazan on the pretense of business. From there, he stages his own suicide — leaving his clothes and personal effects on the bank of a river, creating the impression that he has drowned. In reality, he has departed for America, where he will eventually resurface under the name Charles Beaumont. Before leaving, he arranges for Rakhmétof to visit Viéra and deliver a letter explaining his decision — and, more importantly, to speak to her with characteristic harshness about why the suicide was necessary, thereby giving her the intellectual framework to accept it without guilt.

## Thematic Significance

The fake suicide is the practical application of the theory of egotism to the most intimate of human problems. Lopukhóf's decision is framed not as noble sacrifice but as "noble calculation" — the recognition that his own highest satisfaction lies in acting according to his conception of human dignity. The elaborate staging — the journey, the clothes by the river, the letter, Rakhmétof's visit — is itself an expression of care: by making the suicide appear real, Lopukhóf spares Viéra the social complications of an absent husband. The fake suicide thus demonstrates that the "new people's" principles can solve problems that the old order's institutions cannot.
