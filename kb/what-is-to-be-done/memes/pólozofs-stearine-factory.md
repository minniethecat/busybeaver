# Pólozof's Stearine Factory

Pólozof's Stearine Factory is the industrial enterprise through which the novel represents the world of capitalist production, speculation, and international commerce. It appears late in the narrative as the remnant of [[Pólozof]]'s once-vast fortune — the one asset that survives his catastrophic quarrel with government officials and the vehicle through which [[Charles Beaumont]] enters the novel's world as the agent of a London firm.

> "He had some shares in a stearine factory, and without hanging down his nose, accepted the position of manager of this factory, at a good salary." (092-003)

## The Factory as Narrative Device

The stearine factory serves three interconnected narrative functions. First, it marks the scale of Pólozof's financial ruin — from "three or four millions" to a few tens of thousands of rubles and a salaried position (092-003). Second, it provides the occasion for Beaumont's entry into the story, since his role as purchasing agent for Hodgson, Lotter and Company brings him into sustained contact with Pólozof's household and, through it, with [[Katerina Vasílyevna]]. Third, it represents the world of capitalist enterprise that the "new people" navigate but do not inhabit — a world of contracts, shares, dividends, and international trade that exists alongside the cooperative economy of [[Viéra Pavlovna]]'s [[Sewing Cooperative (Union)|sewing union]].

## Ownership and Management

| Period | Ownership Structure | Pólozof's Role |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-ruin (before the quarrel) | Pólozof holds shares; factory operates as a joint-stock enterprise | Shareholder; manager after his financial collapse |
| During the novel | Shareholders collectively own; Pólozof manages day-to-day operations | Manager at a salary; leads effort to sell |
| Post-sale | Hodgson, Lotter and Company (London) purchases | Pólozof retires on the proceeds; Beaumont oversees transition |

The factory's financial condition is described as "wretched" under the shareholders' management: "The factory could not exist under the wretched financial and administrative conduct of its shareholders; but in the hands of a solid firm it would be sure to bring great profits" (100-001). The firm's calculation — "After spending on it five or six hundred thousand rubles, the firm could count on having one hundred thousand rubles of profits" (100-001) — reveals the logic of capitalist acquisition: the factory is worth more to a well-capitalized owner than to its current shareholders.

## The Sale

The sale of the factory occupies the second half of Chapter 100 and provides the narrative mechanism for Beaumont's integration into the novel's social world. The negotiations are described as "excessively long and according to the nature of Russian business transactions; even the patient Greeks who spent ten years in the siege of Troy would have found them tedious" (100-001).

During these protracted negotiations, Pólozof extends traditional Russian hospitality to the foreign agent: "Pólozof all this time was with the agent, according to the old custom of being hospitable to people who are of use, and he invited him to dinner every day" (100-001). These daily dinners at the factory flat become the setting in which Beaumont meets and eventually marries Katerina Vasílyevna.

The sale completed, Pólozof converts his remaining capital into five-percent government bonds and retires — "to live the remainder of his days peacefully, remembering his past grandeur, the loss of which he bore bravely, preserving all his gayety and firmness" (092-003).

## Pólozof's Business Career

The factory is the endpoint of a commercial career that the novel traces in some detail. Pólozof began with ten thousand rubles in assignats after squandering his patrimonial estate as a cavalry officer; through retail grain trading, small contracts, and "every profitable enterprise which was within his means," he rebuilt his fortune over ten years (092-002). A strategic marriage to a merchant's daughter with a dowry of half a million rubles (all in assignats) accelerated his ascent, and within another decade he was "regarded as the possessor of three or four million rubles" (092-002).

His ruin came not from market forces but from pride: a quarrel with "an important personage" over a major contract — whether for linen, provisions, or boot-leather, the novel leaves unspecified — in which Pólozof refused to "eat humble pie" and was consequently destroyed: "His goods were rejected... his three or four millions vanished" (092-003).

> Pólozof's career arc — from inheritance through speculation and marriage to ruin and modest recovery — is a compressed history of Russian capitalism in the mid-nineteenth century, contrasting sharply with the cooperative economics of the sewing union.

## The Tambof Connection

The factory has a secondary narrative presence through the biography of Charles Beaumont himself. Beaumont's father, James Beaumont, after the failure of a utopian cotton-plantation scheme in the Crimea, "found a position as distiller in a factory in the government of Tambof" where Charles was born and lived until age twenty (101-001). This Tambof factory — a distillery rather than a stearine works — represents the provincial industrial world from which the cosmopolitan Beaumont emerged, a world of "only one American or Englishman in a circle of twenty or fifty or one hundred versts around" (101-001).

## Key Passages

> "He had some shares in a stearine factory, and without hanging down his nose, accepted the position of manager of this factory, at a good salary. Besides that, there remained, by some chance, a few tens of thousands of rubles." (092-003) — The factory as the remnant of a ruined fortune and the vehicle of Pólozof's dignified recovery.

> "After spending on it five or six hundred thousand rubles, the firm could count on having one hundred thousand rubles of profits." (100-001) — The capitalist logic of the factory's valuation and acquisition.

> "The negotiations were excessively long and according to the nature of Russian business transactions; even the patient Greeks who spent ten years in the siege of Troy would have found them tedious." (100-001) — Chernyshevsky's satirical commentary on Russian commercial culture.

> "He thought only about arranging as quickly as possible the sale of the factory... and to live the remainder of his days peacefully, remembering his past grandeur." (092-003) — Pólozof's pragmatic retirement strategy after his fall.

## Thematic Significance

The stearine factory represents the economic world that the "new people" must engage with but do not endorse. Where the sewing cooperative demonstrates that economic relations can be reorganized on principles of equality and mutual benefit, the factory represents the default condition of Russian industry — speculative, personally contingent, vulnerable to both market forces and government caprice. That the factory is ultimately sold to a London firm rather than reformed from within suggests Chernyshevsky's implicit judgment: some institutions cannot be transformed; they must be superseded by entirely different organizational forms.

The factory also illustrates the novel's treatment of commerce as a narrative resource. Pólozof's business dealings — his contracts, his quarrel, the sale of the factory — are not described as economic processes but as plot mechanisms that bring characters together and reveal their moral qualities. The factory's sale is important not for the transaction itself but for the dinners at which Beaumont meets Katerina Vasílyevna.
