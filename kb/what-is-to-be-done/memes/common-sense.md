# Common Sense

"Common sense" in Chernyshevsky's novel is not the conventional wisdom of the majority but precisely its opposite — the clear, self-aware, unsentimental reasoning that cuts through social convention to identify what actually serves human welfare.

## Kirsánof's "Coldly Calculating Common Sense"

The phrase receives its most memorable formulation when Kirsánof analyzes his decision to withdraw from the Lopukhófs' household: "I must not call my decision either noble or even virtuous, these are too high words; I must simply call it coldly calculating common sense" (058-003). The rhetorical move is characteristic of the novel's method — to redescribe what conventional morality would call noble self-sacrifice as simple prudence. Common sense, in this usage, is the refusal to be flattered by one's own virtue.

## Against Romantic Illusion

The novel's common sense stands in opposition to the romantic and sentimental conventions that governed nineteenth-century literature and social life. The narrator's colloquies with the "sapient reader" are exercises in common sense — deflating the reader's expectations of melodrama, heroism, and tragic passion, and insisting that the "new people's" conduct is not extraordinary but merely rational. "It is not they that stand so high, but you that stand so low" (072-015).

## Common Sense and Egotism

Common sense is the everyday language of the theory of egotism. Where the theory provides the philosophical framework — all actions are motivated by self-interest — common sense provides the practical maxim: don't deceive yourself about your motives, and don't make decisions that will poison your future satisfaction. It is the intellectual habit that enables the "new people" to navigate complex social situations without the distortions of vanity, guilt, or conventional expectation.
