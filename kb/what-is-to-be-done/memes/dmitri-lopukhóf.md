# Dmitri Lopukhóf

Dmitri Sergéitch Lopukhóf is the novel's primary philosophical spokesman in its early chapters — a medical student and materialist who articulates the [[Theory of Egotism]], engineers [[Viéra Pavlovna]]'s liberation, and ultimately stages his own disappearance to preserve her autonomy. His trajectory from impoverished student to successful American entrepreneur under the alias Charles Beaumont embodies the novel's argument that rational self-interest, properly understood, leads to both personal fulfillment and social progress.

## Role in the Novel

### The Philosopher-Tutor

Lopukhóf enters the story as Viéra's tutor, hired by her mother [[Marya Alekséyevna]]. His conversations with Viéra introduce the novel's central philosophical doctrine — the theory of egotism. In their pivotal discussion in [[The Temptation of Hamlet]], he explains that all human actions arise from self-interest, but that properly understood, this principle leads not to cynicism but to rational and noble conduct:

> "A match is cold, the matchbox on which you scratch the match is also cold; but there is fire in them which gets a man warm food, and warms him also" (024-024)

Lopukhóf belonged, together with Kirsánof, to that rare class of medical students who supported themselves entirely through teaching:

> Lopukhóf knew for a certainty that he would be appointed interne in one of the military hospitals of Petersburg and would soon receive a chair (018-004)

### The Fictitious Marriage

Recognizing Viéra's desperation to escape her family, Lopukhóf proposes a fictitious marriage — a legal form designed to liberate her while preserving her independence. The domestic arrangement includes separate bedrooms connected by a "neutral room," spatializing their relationship as one of equal partnership rather than fusion or domination. When Viéra asks whether her choice would deserve condemnation, he replies with the theory's characteristic refusal of moral judgment:

> "Who has the right to condemn the results of a fact when the fact itself is in existence?" (024-044)

### Self-Awareness and Irony

Lopukhóf is distinguished by his capacity for ironic self-observation. After suppressing inconvenient truths in a conversation with Kirsánof, he reflects with satisfaction:

> "How true the theory is! Egotism makes sport of men. Now here the most important thing he entirely suppressed... I held my peace because it was not to my advantage to say it" (063-046)

This recursive self-awareness — the willingness to recognize one's own nobility as a form of egoistic satisfaction — is characteristic of the novel's mature "new people," who refuse the sentimentality of self-congratulation even as they act virtuously.

### The Disappearance

When Viéra's feelings shift to [[Aleksandr Kirsánof|Kirsánof]], Lopukhóf executes an elaborate plan: he travels to Riazan, stages his own suicide at the [[Semyonovsky Bridge]], and eventually resurfaces in America under the name Charles Beaumont. The fake suicide is the mechanism that dissolves the legal marriage while preserving everyone's dignity. Viéra's analysis of his motives is characteristically acute:

> "I prize his nobility, oh, how deeply! but I know that he was noble, not for my sake, but his own" (075-004)

### The Return as Beaumont

Lopukhóf returns to Russia years later as Charles Beaumont, having made a fortune in America. He marries [[Katerina Vasílyevna]] — a union that completes the novel's pattern of rational, freely chosen partnerships — and becomes a business associate of [[Pólozof]].

## Philosophical Significance

Lopukhóf embodies Chernyshevsky's argument that the "new people" are not saints but rational egoists — their nobility is a product of clear thinking about self-interest, not moral heroism. His self-aware irony, his willingness to orchestrate and abandon a fictitious marriage, and his eventual reinvention as Beaumont all demonstrate the theory of egotism in practice: that properly understood self-interest produces conduct indistinguishable from the highest virtue.
