# Marya Alekséyevna's Downfall

The collapse of Marya Alekséyevna's marriage scheme is the novel's first decisive victory of the "new people" over the old order — the moment when Viéra's liberation becomes irreversible.

## Narrative Context

Marya Alekséyevna has invested all her calculating energy in marrying Viéra to Storeshnikof. Her scheme represents the old order's reduction of women to commodities and marriage to transaction. Lopukhóf's presence in the household — as Viéra's tutor and clandestine liberator — is the mechanism of the scheme's undoing.

## Summary

Marya Alekséyevna, having overheard Lopukhóf's philosophical conversation with Viéra, misinterprets his rational arguments as conventional advice to accept a wealthy suitor. Her misreading is a structural irony: she hears the words "calculation of self-interests" and assumes they mean what she would mean by them — mercenary self-advancement. She cannot conceive of a calculation that leads away from Storeshnikof's wealth rather than toward it. When Viéra, armed with Lopukhóf's philosophy, refuses Storeshnikof and marries Lopukhóf instead, Marya Alekséyevna's world collapses — not because she is defeated by superior force but because her entire framework of understanding has been rendered obsolete.

## Thematic Significance

Marya Alekséyevna's downfall is the novel's demonstration that the old order defeats itself. Her tragedy — and the novel grants it tragic dimensions — is that she is intelligent enough to recognize merit but imprisoned in a social system that allows her to express intelligence only as manipulation. Her reflection that "if I had been a well-educated woman, would it have been with me as it is now?" (024-053) is the novel's most sympathetic moment for a character it otherwise condemns. The downfall is not a punishment but a structural inevitability.
