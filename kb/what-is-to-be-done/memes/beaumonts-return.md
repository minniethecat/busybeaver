# Beaumont's Return

The return of Charles Beaumont — revealed to be Dmitri Lopukhóf, alive and transformed — is the novel's culminating plot event, the resolution that transforms potential tragedy into harmonious comedy.

## Narrative Context

Years have passed since Lopukhóf's fake suicide. Viéra and Kirsánof are happily married. The sewing cooperative thrives. Katerina Vasílyevna Pólozova has become Viéra's close friend and correspondent. Into this stable world arrives Charles Beaumont, an American businessman of Russian origin — who is, the reader gradually discovers, Lopukhóf returned.

## Summary

Beaumont courts and marries Katerina Vasílyevna, establishing himself in Petersburg society. The revelation of his true identity is handled with characteristic understatement — there is no dramatic confrontation, no tearful reunion, no moral reckoning. The "new people" have always understood that Lopukhóf's suicide was a fiction; his return as Beaumont is simply the fiction's natural conclusion. The two couples — Viéra and Kirsánof, Katerina and Beaumont/Lopukhóf — settle into a life of friendship and mutual respect.

## Thematic Significance

Beaumont's return completes the novel's argument that rational conduct produces harmonious outcomes. No one is punished, no one is guilty, no relationship is destroyed — because the "new people" never treated relationships as property claims in the first place. The return also demonstrates the novel's faith in transformation: Lopukhóf returns not as the man who disappeared but as someone who has grown through his American experience, ready for a new life with a new partner.
