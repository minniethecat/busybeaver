# Viéra's Second Dream

Viéra's Second Dream presents the novel's materialist theory of human nature through the allegory of soil — some composed of healthy elements needing only proper coordination, others rotten at the core and incapable of producing anything healthy.

## Narrative Context

The dream occurs in Part II, after Viéra's marriage to Lopukhóf. She is beginning to understand the social and economic dimensions of the philosophy she has embraced. The dream provides theoretical grounding for her practical work with the sewing cooperative.

## Summary

Viéra dreams of a field where Lopukhóf walks with Alekséi Petróvitch (Mertsálof). Lopukhóf explains an allegory: some soil — "actual dirt" — smells moist and disagreeable but is composed of healthy elements. "When they are gathered together, they make dirt; but let the atoms change in some degree their relative coordination, and something else will take its place, and all that takes its place will be healthy, because the fundamental elements are healthy" (044-003). Other soil is "putrid" — rotten at its core, incapable of producing anything healthy. The allegory applies to human beings: people are products of their social environment, and those formed in healthy conditions (the "new people") will naturally flourish, while those formed in putrid conditions (the old order) cannot produce genuine goodness.

## Thematic Significance

The Second Dream translates the theory of egotism from individual psychology to social analysis. It argues that human nature is not fixed but shaped by conditions — a materialist position with radical political implications. If people are products of their environment, then changing the environment changes the people.
