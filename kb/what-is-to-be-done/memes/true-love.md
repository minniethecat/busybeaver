# True Love

The concept of "true love" in Chernyshevsky's novel is a philosophical argument about the nature of human relationships under conditions of rational freedom. It is defined in opposition to "erotic selfishness" — the possessive, jealous, and ultimately degrading form of love that the novel associates with the old social order.

## The Definition

Viéra Pavlovna articulates the distinction most clearly after three years of living with Kirsánof: "Where did people get the idea that love grows weak, when there is nothing to hinder people from belonging wholly to each other? These people did not know true love. They felt only an erotic selfishness, or an erotic fancy. Real love begins only when people begin to live together" (087-001). The paradox is deliberate: true love does not weaken through proximity and security but deepens — because it is based not on the thrill of obstacles but on the ongoing satisfaction of shared life.

## Love and the Theory of Egotism

The novel's theory of love is an application of its broader theory of egotism. When Viéra Pavlovna's feelings shift from Lopukhóf to Kirsánof, none of the three characters treats this as a moral crisis — because in their framework, love cannot be owed or demanded. Viéra explicitly refuses the language of guilt and gratitude: "I do not even feel that I owe him any gratefulness... I was not false, not for his sake, but for myself; not because falsity would be injurious to him, but to myself" (075-004). The apparently "selfish" refusal to stay in a loveless marriage is reframed as the highest ethical conduct — because honesty about one's own interest is the only honest basis for relations with others.

## The "New People's" Relationships

The relationships among the "new people" are characterized by mutual respect for autonomy. Lopukhóf's decision to fake his own death rather than stand in the way of Viéra and Kirsánof is framed not as sacrifice but as "noble calculation" — the recognition that his own highest satisfaction lay in acting according to his conception of human dignity. Kirsánof's careful, months-long withdrawal from the Lopukhófs' household when he realizes his feelings for Viéra is likewise framed as "coldly calculating common sense" (058-003). Both men apply the theory of egotism to love and arrive at conduct that appears selfless but is, in their own analysis, the purest self-interest.

## Contrast with the Old Order

The novel's treatment of love is set against the background of the "old order" represented by Viéra's parents' household, where marriage is a financial transaction and women are commodities. Marya Alekséyevna's scheme to marry Viéra to the wealthy Storeshnikof is the foil against which the "new people's" concept of love is defined. The novel's argument is that true love is only possible between equals who are economically and emotionally independent.
