# Nástenka's Confession

Kirsánof's recounting of Nástenka Kryukova's story is a narrative within the narrative — a cautionary tale that sharpens the novel's argument about women's vulnerability under the old order by showing what [[Viéra Pavlovna]] escaped. The confession stands as a dark mirror to the novel's central romance: where Viéra finds [[Lopukhófs Fake Suicide|liberation through rational love]], [[Nástenka]] suffers the predation of [[Sólovtsof]]. The episode also illuminates the ethical framework of [[Theory of Egotism|rational egotism]] — Kirsánof's care for Nástenka exemplifies the "new man's" calculated devotion to others' well-being as his own highest good — and foreshadows [[The Temptation of Hamlet|the Hamlet test]] that Viéra herself will face, where the temptation to succumb to the old order's seductions must be consciously refused. Through the confession, [[True Love|true love]] is defined by what it is not: not the fleeting passion of a Sólovtsof, but the steady commitment of a Kirsánof.

## Narrative Context

During one of his visits to the Lopukhófs, Kirsánof tells Viéra the story of Nástenka Kryukova. The story serves multiple purposes: it illustrates Kirsánof's practice as a physician who takes on the most difficult cases, it provides Viéra with a concrete example of the old order's predation, and it establishes Sólovtsof as a negative type against which the "new men" are measured.

## Summary

Nástenka, a factory worker, was seduced by Sólovtsof — a man of superficial charm who promised marriage, took what he wanted, and abandoned her when she became inconvenient. Without resources, education, or social connections, Nástenka was left with nothing. Kirsánof took her case, treating not only her physical condition but the social and psychological damage of her betrayal. His care for Nástenka is characteristic: though he describes himself as a researcher who "does not cure," the hospital servants know him as "a master, and a real father" (051-003). Nástenka's story is the road not taken by Viéra — the fate that would await a woman without the intellectual and social resources that the "new people" provide.

> "Kirsánof said that he does not cure, but only researches; but the hospital servants knew better." (051-003) — The hospital attendants' judgment overrides Kirsánof's self-description, affirming that his scientific detachment is belied by his practical compassion.

> "Sólovtsof was a man of excellent manners, a master of the art of pleasing — and Nástenka had no one to warn her." (052-011) — The tragedy is structural: Nástenka's isolation, not her naivety, is what delivers her into Sólovtsof's hands.

## Thematic Significance

Nástenka's confession operates as the novel's negative demonstration. Where Viéra's story shows what is possible under rational social arrangements, Nástenka's shows what is inevitable under the old order. The juxtaposition makes visible the structural nature of women's oppression: it is not individual villainy but systemic vulnerability that destroys women like Nástenka.

> "Viéra Pavlovna listened and understood: this was the fate that had been waiting for her." (052-015) — The confession transforms Viéra's understanding of her own biography from personal good fortune to structural possibility: she was saved not by luck but by the intervention of the "new people" and their ideas.
