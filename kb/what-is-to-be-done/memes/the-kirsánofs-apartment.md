# The Kirsánofs' Apartment

The Kirsánofs' apartment is the domestic space of the novel's second marriage — a home that embodies the "new people's" ideals not through the careful architecture of neutrality (as in the Lopukhófs' apartment) but through the natural rhythms of a relationship based on mutual love.

## A Home of True Love

Where the Lopukhófs' apartment was designed around the principle of autonomy — separate rooms, neutral spaces, carefully calibrated distance — the Kirsánofs' apartment is organized around the satisfactions of shared life. Viéra works, reads, plays the piano, and sings; Kirsánof comes and goes from the hospital; friends gather for evenings of conversation and music. The apartment is not a spatial argument but a lived space — the domestic expression of a relationship that has moved beyond the strategic deployment of architecture to the unselfconscious enjoyment of companionship.

## Continuity and Change

The apartment continues the "new people's" social function — it remains a gathering place for the circle of young instructors, friends, and fellow travelers. But it sheds the architectural self-consciousness of the Lopukhófs' apartment. The neutral rooms are gone, replaced by the ordinary intimacy of a marriage based on love rather than legal fiction.
