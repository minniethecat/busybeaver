# Rational Calculation

Rational calculation is the practical decision-making method that translates the theory of egotism into action. It is the process by which the "new people" determine their conduct — not by reference to abstract moral rules but by weighing competing self-interests to identify the course of action that will produce the greatest genuine satisfaction.

## The Method

The formal definition emerges from Viéra's summary of Lopukhóf's teaching: "When an action has vital importance, the stimulus is called self-interest; its interaction in man is the calculation of self-interests, and therefore a man must always act in accordance with the motive of self-interest" (024-049). The crucial move is the expansion of "self-interest" to include the satisfaction of acting according to one's conception of human dignity — what the novel calls "noble calculation."

## Kirsánof's Case Study

Kirsánof's deliberation over whether to withdraw from the Lopukhófs' household is the novel's most extended example of rational calculation in practice. He frames the decision in rigorously self-interested terms: "Every man is an egotist; so am I; now the question comes up, 'Is it more profitable for me to go away, or to remain?'" (058-003). He compares the situation to a flask of wine that may be poisoned: the pleasure of staying must be weighed against the risk of poisoning his entire future life by acting against his human nature. The decision to withdraw is presented not as moral virtue but as "coldly calculating common sense" (058-003).

## Self-Awareness and Irony

The novel's treatment of rational calculation includes a layer of ironic self-awareness. Both Lopukhóf and Kirsánof are shown to derive pleasure from observing how their own egotism operates: "It is pleasant for a man, as a theorist, to notice what tricks his egotism plays with him in practical life" (063-046). The calculation is not naive — it recognizes that the very act of framing a decision as "noble" is itself a form of egoistic satisfaction. This recursive self-awareness distinguishes the novel's rational calculation from simple utility maximization.
