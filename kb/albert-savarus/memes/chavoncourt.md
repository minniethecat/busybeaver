# Monsieur de Chavoncourt

The **Chavoncourt family** represents the established parliamentary nobility of Besançon, serving as both social anchor and political foil to [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]] in the 1834 election. Monsieur de Chavoncourt is a Royalist of the "famous 221," the deputies whose address to Charles X precipitated the July Revolution; his wife maintains one of Besançon's principal winter salons, and their children form part of the town's young social circle.

## Overview

The Chavoncourts embody the cautious, property-minded provincial aristocracy of the Restoration. Their position in Besançon society is secure but not opulent: a fortune of roughly fifteen thousand francs a year, stretched thin by a son and three daughters, places them in the peculiar position of being "highly respected" yet perpetually constrained.

> Monsieur de Chavoncourt, highly respected in Besancon, was the representative of an old parliamentary family; his fortune, of about fifteen thousand francs a year, was not an offence to anybody, especially as he had a son and three daughters. — [001-639]

Monsieur de Chavoncourt's political stance — preaching the doctrine of "taking the oaths and resisting the present order of things, after the pattern of the Tories against the Whigs in England" — places him in an ambiguous middle ground: not fully trusted by the Legitimists, yet preferred by the Moderates over a Republican candidate.

## Role in the Novel

The Chavoncourt family operates at multiple levels of the narrative. Politically, Monsieur de Chavoncourt is the opponent Albert Savarus must defeat in the Besançon election. The election subplot turns on Rosalie de Watteville's manipulation: she persuades [[amedee-de-soulas|Amédée de Soulas]] to rally support for Savarus specifically "to hinder that poor Monsieur de Chavoncourt from representing the town of Besancon" (001-617).

Socially, Madame de Chavoncourt's Tuesday salon is the venue where Albert's anonymously published novella, _L'Ambitieux par amour_, is discussed and dissected. It is through the critical chatter of this salon that Rosalie first intuits the connection between the fictional Albert of the story and the real Albert Savarus:

> Also, from what she had heard said, Rosalie had by intuition conceived a notion of it which strangely enhanced the interest of this novel. — [001-757]

The Chavoncourt daughters — Victoire (the eldest), Sidonie (sixteen), and two younger girls — complete the family's social web. Victoire is the intended bride of Monsieur de Vauchelles, while the younger Sidonie becomes, briefly and tragically, a pawn in the Abbe de Grancey's scheme to marry Savarus into Besançon society.

## Relationships

- **Madame de Chavoncourt** — At forty, "one of the beauties of Besançon," she manages the family's social position with shrewd economy, living meagrely in the country during parliamentary sessions to recoup expenses.
- **Young Chavoncourt** — The son, aged twenty-two, forms an inseparable trio with [[vauchelles|Monsieur de Vauchelles]] and [[amedee-de-soulas|Amédée de Soulas]].
- **[[sidonie-chavoncourt|Sidonie de Chavoncourt]]** — The sixteen-year-old daughter, briefly proposed as a bride for Albert Savarus by the [[abbe-de-grancey|Abbé de Grancey]].
- **Victoire de Chavoncourt** — Eldest daughter, god-daughter of a wealthy aunt who promises her a settlement of seven thousand francs a year.
- **[[vauchelles|Monsieur de Vauchelles]]** — Intended husband of Victoire; part of the young male trio; later votes for Savarus in the election.
- **[[rosalie-de-watteville]]** — Intimate with the Chavoncourt girls; she uses her knowledge of the family's internal dynamics to manipulate electoral outcomes.

## Key Appearances

The Chavoncourt salon appears as a recurring social set-piece. In the winter season, Madame de Chavoncourt "received very creditably once a week, on Tuesdays, understanding her business as mistress of the house" (001-689). It is here that Albert Savarus's literary talent first becomes a subject of public discussion, setting in motion Rosalie's investigation into his identity.

The election contest forms the political climax: Savarus assembles a coalition that includes defectors from the Chavoncourt camp, notably the Boucher committee and the wavering Vauchelles. Monsieur de Chavoncourt's defeat is sealed when thirty voters from his own side are turned by Rosalie's covert machinations.

> Then, as twenty remain to you of the Boucher committee, you will have a substantial majority. — [001-247]

## Significance

The Chavoncourts serve as the novel's portrait of provincial legitimacy — stable, respected, but ultimately outmaneuvered by the disruptive forces of ambition, love, and clandestine manipulation that Albert Savarus (and, more decisively, Rosalie) bring to Besançon. Monsieur de Chavoncourt's political defeat is not simply an electoral loss; it represents the triumph of personal passion over institutional order, a theme Balzac develops throughout _La Comédie Humaine_.
