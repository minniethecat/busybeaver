# Rosalie de Watteville

**Rosalie de Watteville** is the daughter of the Baronne and Monsieur de Watteville, and the character whose actions drive the central conflict of Balzac's *Albert Savarus*. A young woman of Besançon's aristocracy, her discovery of [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus's]] secret past and her subsequent obsession with him set in motion the novel's tragic events. Rosalie is at once the novel's hidden antagonist and one of Balzac's most psychologically complex portraits of female desire in a repressive social order.

## Overview

Rosalie is introduced as a young woman living under the strict and pious supervision of her mother, the Baronne de Watteville. Sheltered within the confines of [[besancon|Besançon]] society, she develops an intense fascination with Albert Savarus, the mysterious lawyer who frequents her mother's salon:

| Phase | Development |
|-------|-------------|
| **Innocence** | Sheltered daughter of the Wattevilles, raised under her mother's rigid piety |
| **Fascination** | Drawn to the mysterious Savarus, who represents a world beyond Besançon |
| **Discovery** | Secretly reads Savarus's private papers, learning of his love for Francesca |
| **Obsession** | Infatuation transforms into a determination to possess Savarus |
| **Sabotage** | Intercepts correspondence, manipulates the [[election]], destroys Savarus's ambitions |
| **Aftermath** | Left to confront the moral consequences of her actions |

## Role in the Novel

Rosalie functions as the novel's hidden antagonist. While appearing to be a passive young woman of good family, she secretly orchestrates events to separate Savarus from [[francesca|Francesca]]. She intercepts correspondence, manipulates those around her, and ultimately contributes to Savarus's electoral defeat by revealing information that damages his candidacy.

Her arc traces a progression from innocent curiosity to obsessive love to destructive jealousy. The discovery of Savarus's private papers — the framed narrative within the novel — is the turning point: before this moment, Rosalie is a curious admirer; after it, she becomes a calculating adversary.

## Relationships

- **[[albert-savarus]]** — The object of Rosalie's obsessive love. She is initially drawn to his mystery and brilliance, but her love curdles into jealousy when she discovers his devotion to Francesca.

- **[[baronne-de-watteville]]** — Rosalie's mother, whose strict religious piety shapes the constrained environment in which Rosalie grows up. The Baronne's world is the prison from which Rosalie's obsession represents an attempted escape.

- **[[baron-de-watteville]]** — Rosalie's father, a gentle and peaceable man who provides a stark contrast to his infamous ancestor and who is largely absent from the emotional life of his daughter.

- **[[francesca]]** — Rosalie's rival for Savarus's love. Though Rosalie never meets Francesca directly, her jealousy of this absent woman drives much of the novel's plot.

## Significance

Rosalie de Watteville is one of Balzac's most psychologically complex young female characters. She illustrates the destructive potential of unexamined desire within a repressive social order. Her transformation from sheltered daughter to calculating manipulator serves as a critique of the provincial aristocracy — a world where surface propriety masks deep currents of passion and ambition.

In the economy of the novel, Rosalie is the agent of fate: the human instrument through which the novel's themes of [[hidden-identity|hidden identity]], [[love-and-desire|love and desire]], and social ambition achieve their tragic resolution. She is not a simple villain but a product of her environment — a young woman whose natural longing for passion and meaning has been so thoroughly suppressed that it emerges in distorted, destructive forms.
