# Secret Crimes and Moral Guilt

"Secret crimes and moral guilt" is the theme of a pivotal sermon preached
by the Abbé de Grancey in Balzac's _Albert Savarus_, establishing the
theological and moral framework through which the novel's central conflicts
are understood.

## The Sermon

The Abbé de Grancey, the Vicar-General of Besançon and the novel's most
shrewd political operator, preaches a sermon on secret crimes — sins
committed in private that escape human justice but cannot escape divine
judgment. This sermon has a profound effect on Rosalie de Watteville,
who recognizes herself in its description of hidden guilt[^1].

## Rosalie's Secret Crime

Rosalie's interception of Albert's letters to Francesca constitutes the
novel's central "secret crime." Unlike public crimes that are punished by
law, Rosalie's act takes place in the private sphere — a violation of
trust and correspondence that no court would prosecute. Yet the novel
insists that such hidden transgressions carry a moral weight that cannot
be evaded.

The Abbé's sermon introduces the possibility that divine justice operates
independently of social justice — a theme that resonates throughout
Balzac's _La Comédie Humaine_.

## Broader Implications

The concept extends beyond Rosalie to encompass other characters. Albert
himself carries a secret past. The Abbé de Grancey navigates a world
where political maneuvering and spiritual guidance blur together. The
question the novel poses is whether any character can truly escape the
moral consequences of their hidden actions.
