# Love Triangle and Rivalry

The love triangle in *Albert Savarus* — Albert, Francesca, and Rosalie — drives the novel's central plot and embodies Honoré de Balzac's characteristic preoccupation with the destructive power of romantic obsession in a society governed by convention and class.

## The Structure of the Triangle

The triangle is asymmetrical in both knowledge and power. Albert loves Francesca Colonna, the Italian Duchess d'Argaiolo, the woman from his past whom he has never ceased to adore. Rosalie de Watteville loves Albert, the mysterious provincial lawyer who fascinates her with his brilliance and his secrets. Francesca, for most of the novel, is unaware of Rosalie's existence as a rival.

This one-sided awareness is key to the triangle's destructive potential. Rosalie possesses knowledge that Albert believes is safely hidden, and this knowledge asymmetry — rather than any direct confrontation between rivals — becomes the engine of the novel's tragic resolution.

## The Three Points of the Triangle

### Albert Savarus

Albert occupies the apex of the triangle, loved by two women but aware of only one. His devotion to Francesca is absolute and idealized — she represents not merely a romantic partner but an entire lost world of youth, passion, and possibility. His blindness to Rosalie's feelings is not merely naivety but a form of psychological protection: to acknowledge Rosalie's interest would be to admit that his carefully constructed Besançon identity is under threat.

### Francesca Colonna

Francesca, the absent rival, exerts more influence through her absence than most characters do through their presence. She exists in Besançon only as a miniature portrait that Albert keeps hidden, as letters that Rosalie intercepts, as a fantasy of Italian beauty and sophistication that provincial society cannot match. Francesca's unawareness of the triangle makes her both its most powerful figure — she is the object of Albert's complete devotion — and its most vulnerable — she cannot defend against a rival she does not know exists.

### Rosalie de Watteville

Rosalie is the triangle's active agent, the one who transforms latent tensions into catastrophic action. Her position is doubly frustrating: she loves a man who does not love her, and she competes with a woman she has never met. Rosalie's rivalry with Francesca is peculiar because Francesca exists for her only as a portrait, as letters, as a fantasy of romantic perfection. This makes the rivalry both more intense and more abstract — Rosalie fights not a woman but an idea that she can never match.

## Rosalie's Rivalry with an Absent Woman

Rosalie's systematic campaign to discover the truth about Albert's past is driven not by love alone but by a competitive desire to understand — and thus defeat — her invisible rival. She employs every resource available to a young woman of her station: the confessional (manipulating the Abbé Giroud), surveillance (observing Albert at Mass), and social intelligence (piecing together fragments of gossip and observation).

Her interception of Albert's correspondence with Francesca represents the moment when passive rivalry becomes active destruction. By reading letters meant for another woman, Rosalie violates not merely Albert's privacy but the sanctity of the romantic bond itself. The letters become her instruments of power precisely because they are not meant for her eyes.

## The Amédée de Soulas Dimension

The triangle is complicated by a fourth figure: Amédée de Soulas, who courts Rosalie and views Albert as his rival. Soulas's presence introduces a secondary rivalry — between Albert and Soulas for Rosalie's hand — that parallels the primary triangle. Soulas's eventual success in marrying Rosalie represents the triumph of provincial convention over romantic passion, of calculated advantage over genuine feeling.

## The Destructive Resolution

The triangle resolves catastrophically. Rosalie intercepts Albert's letters to Francesca, reveals the truth to her rival at the Opera ball, and effectively destroys both the relationship she envied and any chance of her own happiness. Her declaration — "I do not choose to be the only sufferer" — captures the psychology of the romantic rival who, finding herself excluded from happiness, ensures that no one else can enjoy it either.

Albert's departure from Besançon, following the exposure of his secret, is the triangle's terminal event. The three points of the triangle scatter: Albert disappears, Francesca retreats into her Italian world, and Rosalie is left with the hollow satisfaction of destruction without gain.

## Literary Context

The love triangle in *Albert Savarus* belongs to a tradition of triangular desire in French literature, from the classical *précieux* novels of the seventeenth century to Stendhal's *The Red and the Black*. Balzac's innovation is to make the triangle not merely a plot device but a structural principle: the entire novel is organized around the asymmetries of knowledge, desire, and power that the triangle creates.

The triangle also serves Balzac's sociological project. Through it, he examines how romantic passion — which seems the most private and personal of emotions — is in fact shaped by the forces of class, provincialism, and social convention that govern Restoration France.
