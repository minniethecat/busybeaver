# Lucerne

Lucerne, the Swiss city on the northwestern shore of Lake Lucerne
(Vierwaldstättersee / Lake of the Four Cantons), serves as the
narrative launch point for Rodolphe's fateful Swiss journey in Balzac's
*Albert Savarus*. In July 1823, two young Parisian friends — Rodolphe
(the future Albert Savarus) and Leopold — set out from Lucerne in a
boat pulled by three oarsmen, intending to explore the lake's notable
sites en route to Fluelen. Their planned Swiss headquarters at Lucerne
is abandoned when a chance encounter with a mysterious mute girl and her
English charges redirects their journey — and Rodolphe's life.

## Overview

Lucerne functions in the novel primarily as a point of departure. "In
1823 two young men, having agreed as a plan for a holiday to make a tour
through Switzerland, set out from Lucerne one fine morning in the month
of July in a boat pulled by three oarsmen" [005-Section-5]. The city
is their intended base of operations — "We meant to make Lucerne our
headquarters for seeing Switzerland" [005-Section-5] — but Rodolphe's
fascination with the mysterious Francesca (then passing as an English
invalid) causes him to abandon this plan. He sends the boat back to
Lucerne "to fetch all our luggage" [005-Section-5] and settles instead
at the village of Gersau, on the opposite shore of the lake.

The city reappears at key narrative moments. When Rodolphe suffers an
accident and is injured at Gersau, Leopold "sent to Lucerne for the
best surgeon there" [007-Section-7] and later "begged him to go to
Lucerne to fetch their luggage and letters" [007-Section-7]. It is from
Lucerne that the "most fatal, most dreadful news" arrives — Rodolphe's
mother has died: "While the two friends were on their way from Bale to
Lucerne, the fatal letter, written by Leopold's father, had reached
Lucerne the day they left for Fluelen" [007-Section-7].

## Description in the Novel

Balzac's Lucerne is evoked through its relationship to the lake. The
journey from Lucerne to Fluelen passes through landscapes of
extraordinary beauty: "The views which shut in the waters on the way
from Lucerne to Fluelen offer every combination that the most exacting
fancy can demand of mountain and river scenery" [005-Section-5].

The Bergmann family's connection to Lucerne is established through
medical geography — they had settled by the lake because physicians had
"recommended him the air of Lucerne" for the father's health [006-Section-6].
The practical details of daily life are sketched through small incidents:
Miss Fanny, the Bergmanns' music-loving daughter, "had hired a piano
from Lucerne, for she seemed to be crazy about music" [006-Section-6].

## Significance

Lucerne embodies the narrative logic of the *fork in the road* — the
path not taken. Rodolphe and Leopold intend a grand Swiss tour; instead
they are drawn into the orbit of the Bergmann household and the
mysterious Francesca. The city represents the life Rodolphe abandons
(his law career, his Parisian identity, his friendship with Leopold)
in favor of romantic obsession.

The recurring motif of boats approaching from Lucerne — "A boat was
approaching from Lucerne; Gina, who had been watching it attentively,
gave a joyful start" [008-Section-8] — creates a rhythmic sense of
connection and anticipation between the city and the lakeside village.
The boat from Lucerne brings news, luggage, surgeons, and eventually
the fatal letter that severs Rodolphe from his mother.
