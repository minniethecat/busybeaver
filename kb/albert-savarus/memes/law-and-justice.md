# Law and Justice in Restoration France

Law and justice in *Albert Savarus* function as both a central plot mechanism and a lens through which Balzac examines Restoration-era French society. The legal profession serves as the primary vehicle for the protagonist's ambition, while the Rouxey lawsuit drives the novel's political and romantic intrigue.

## Overview

In post-Revolutionary France, the legal profession occupied a unique position in the social hierarchy. Unlike the army or the Church, which were traditional avenues of advancement for the nobility, the law offered a path to prominence for men of talent regardless of birth. The Restoration (1814–1830) saw the fusion of old aristocratic legal institutions with Napoleonic reforms, creating a complex judicial landscape that Balzac, himself a former law clerk, depicts with insider precision.

The legal world of Besancon — its Court of Appeal, its barristers, its procedural rituals — forms the backdrop against which Albert Savarus rises from an unknown newcomer to a figure of local prominence. The "wits of the legal profession" described by Balzac reflect the social currency that legal acumen commanded in provincial society.

## Relevance to the Novel

### The Rouxey Lawsuit

The lawsuit over the Rouxey estate is the engine that drives the novel's political plot. The Watteville family's claim to the land at Les Rouxey, contested by the Riceys family, becomes the means by which Albert Savarus demonstrates his professional brilliance. The lawsuit had been "going on for four years" and the Chapter had "lost it in the lower Court" before Savarus was retained (ch01). The Vicar-General, Abbe de Grancey, announces the victory in the Watteville salon with theatrical satisfaction:

> "Won! The verdict of the Court, from which there is no appeal, is in our favor on every point, and reverses the decision of the Lower Court." (ch01)

The social impact of the verdict is immediate and transformative. As the Vicar-General recounts, Savarus "completely beaten the celebrated lawyer whom our adversaries had sent from Paris," and the townsfolk "applauded him as they came out of court." This applause is more than mere admiration — it is the sound of an outsider breaching the walls of provincial society through professional excellence alone.

Balzac also uses the lawsuit to reveal character. When Monsieur de Watteville asks whether the case might be "carried to the Court of Appeal," the Vicar-General replies confidently that "our adversaries will think twice before they do that" (ch01). The exchange reveals both the finality of the victory and the power dynamics of provincial litigation: even adversaries backed by Parisian legal talent cannot match the local networks that Savarus has cultivated.

### Law as Social Ladder

The novel presents the law as one of the few institutions through which an outsider can penetrate the closed world of provincial aristocracy. Albert's legal victories are treated as performances — public demonstrations of competence that override the usual barriers of birth and connection. This reflects a broader tension in Restoration society between the old order of inherited privilege and the new order of merit, codified in the Napoleonic legal reforms.

The voice itself becomes a marker of professional authority. The Vicar-General notes that Savarus has "a true orator's voice, now clear and insinuating, now terrible and thunderous" — the instrument by which a barrister transforms legal argument into social capital (ch01). In a provincial capital where oral advocacy before the Court was a form of public theater, the quality of a lawyer's voice was as important as the quality of his reasoning.

### Outsider Status and Legal Strategy

Albert Savarus's arrival in Besancon is a calculated professional maneuver. He presents himself to the Court with precise formality — "showing his title in due form" and leaving his card for "all the Councillors of the Court, and the members of the bench, with the name, 'ALBERT SAVARON'" (ch01). Yet this adherence to professional protocol coexists with a deliberate social invisibility. He works only in the early morning hours and is never seen in the streets or drawing-rooms of Besancon.

The local legal establishment's reaction to this stranger is ambivalent. "He was received without hesitation by the lawyers of the town," but they were "surprised, if not vexed" by his peculiar habits (ch01). Balzac captures the provincial mindset with a telling detail: Savarus committed the social blunder of "leaving cards on the Judges instead of calling in person! What a blunder!" This seemingly minor breach of etiquette — treating judges as professional colleagues rather than social superiors — encapsulates the tension between Parisian efficiency and provincial formality that structures Savarus's entire experience in Besancon.

#### Strategic Case Selection

Savarus approaches legal practice not as a profession but as a political instrument. His first three cases establish a pattern of escalating prestige:

| Case | Type | Strategic Value |
|------|------|-----------------|
| 1. Criminal defense at the Assizes | Pro bono — a "half-witted peasant accused of forgery" | Demonstrates forensic skill before the Court and influential jury members |
| 2. Merchant's commercial suit | Paid, for "one of the most influential merchants of Besancon" | Earns the trust of the commercial class that elects deputies |
| 3. Cathedral Chapter litigation | Free — defended the Chapter "against the town" in a four-year-old case | Secures the patronage of the Abbe de Grancey and the Legitimist faction |

The first case is particularly revealing of Savarus's method. The President of the Court appointed him to defend the peasant "to make game of him," but Savarus transformed the trap into an opportunity. He "very dramatically secured the arrest of the real criminals, who had come forward as witnesses," and the Court and jury were "united in their admiration" (ch01). The merchant juror became his first important connection — and he "won his suit" shortly after.

Beyond these opening moves, Savarus systematically cultivated the clergy as a client base. "In the course of this winter he won seven lawsuits for various priests of Besancon" (ch14). This ecclesiastical legal practice was both genuinely remunerative and strategically calculated: by serving the Church, Savarus deepened his ties to the Legitimist faction whose political patronage he required for his election campaign.

### The Moral Economy of Legal Work

Balzac subjects Savarus's legal career to a subtle moral scrutiny. The barrister's "disinterestedness was lauded, and he took his clients' fees without comment." But the narrator immediately adds a devastating qualification: "this disinterestedness was, in truth, moral usury; he counted on a reward far greater to him than all the gold in the world" (ch14). The language of finance — "moral usury" — reframes legal service as a form of investment, in which professional labor is tendered not for its immediate fee but for a deferred political payoff.

This insight is sharpened by the Abbe de Grancey's remark upon observing Savarus's machinations: "You are really a remarkable man. You are a priest who has taken the wrong turning" (ch14). The Vicar-General, himself a master of ecclesiastical politics, recognizes a kindred spirit — someone who treats his profession as a vehicle for power rather than an end in itself. The comparison to the priesthood is telling: both law and the Church offered routes to influence in Restoration France, and Savarus applies to the former the patient, strategic sensibility more commonly associated with the latter.

### The Limits of Legal Meritocracy

For all its celebration of forensic brilliance, the novel also suggests the fragility of a system in which justice depends on individual advocacy. Savarus secures an acquittal for the peasant accused of forgery because "the man was innocent" — but what of the defendants without an Albert Savarus to defend them? The novel does not answer this question directly, but Balzac's depiction of the legal system as a theater of individual talent rather than institutional reliability implies a critique. Justice in Restoration France is only as good as the advocate who argues for it.

Savarus's own trajectory reinforces this point. His legal skills open doors in Besancon, but those doors remain open only as long as his political coalition holds. When Rosalie's interception of his correspondence destroys his romantic and political prospects simultaneously, his legal reputation counts for nothing. The law, for all its meritocratic promise, cannot protect the outsider from the social forces it was meant to transcend.
