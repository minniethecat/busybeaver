# Monsieur Girardet

**Monsieur Girardet** is a Besançon attorney who serves as Albert Savarus's legal proxy and trusted local agent during the election period. A member of the Boucher electoral committee, Girardet is among the small handful of individuals who remain loyal to Savarus after his sudden departure and one of only three people "seriously affected by his disappearance."

## Overview

Girardet occupies the role of the dependable provincial professional — competent, discreet, and genuinely attached to the brilliant outsider he serves. His law practice provides the local infrastructure Savarus needs: when the Watteville lawsuit demands attention during the election campaign, it is Girardet who conducts the case under Savarus's remote instruction.

> Until then Girardet must conduct the case under my instructions. That is the most I can do. — [001-501]

Savarus delegates to Girardet with evident trust, sending him to inspect localities and handle procedural matters that cannot be managed from a distance. This arrangement is born of necessity — Savarus refuses "in the face of a town I know so well, to take any step which might compromise the supreme interests that lie beyond my election" — and Girardet's willingness to serve as the lawyer's hands in Besançon proves essential.

## Role in the Novel

Girardet's most significant contributions occur at the novel's edges — before the election and after Savarus's disappearance.

During the campaign, Girardet is part of the Boucher committee, the coalition of professionals and businessmen that forms Savarus's electoral base. His father-in-law, "a very shrewd old vine-grower from the upper town," is described as one of Savarus's "staunch supporters."

It is in the aftermath of Savarus's flight that Girardet emerges as a figure of genuine loyalty. On the day of departure:

> He settled everything with Monsieur Girardet, who went to him and stayed till seven. — [001-599]

Savarus leaves his keys with Girardet, who takes him home, shares a simple meal ("at half-past seven Monsieur Girardet had not yet dined"), and witnesses Savarus's departure for the Geneva road. Girardet lends Savarus five thousand francs "on the occasion of his inexplicable departure" and undertakes to collect his assets.

When a clerk arrives from Paris with power of attorney from [[leopold-hannequin|Léopold Hannequin]], Girardet assists in selling Savarus's house "out of friendship." When he asks what has become of "the handsome and noble pleader, to whom he had been so much attached," the clerk can only reply that no one knows but his master.

## Relationships

- **[[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]]** — Girardet is Savarus's legal proxy and loyal supporter; he receives Savarus's keys and final instructions before the departure.
- **[[alfred-boucher|Alfred Boucher]]** — Fellow member of the Boucher electoral committee; the two represent the professional class backing Savarus.
- **[[baron-de-watteville|Baron de Watteville]]** — Girardet handles the Watteville lawsuit under Savarus's remote direction.
- **[[leopold-hannequin|Léopold Hannequin]]** — The Paris notary who sends a clerk to coordinate with Girardet on liquidating Savarus's Besançon assets.
- **Girardet's father-in-law** — "A very shrewd old vine-grower" who votes for Savarus.

## Key Appearances

Girardet first appears in the narrative during Savarus's conversation with the Abbé de Grancey, where the attorney is designated as the local proxy for the Watteville case. His most poignant scene is his final evening with Savarus: a quiet dinner at half-past seven, the handover of keys, the loan of five thousand francs, and a last glimpse of the lawyer "looking like death" as his carriage sets off on the Geneva road.

The epilogue confirms Girardet's place among the elect: only three people — "Girardet the attorney, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie" — are "seriously affected by his disappearance." Girardet alone knows that the white-haired stranger who visited Savarus was Prince Soderini, having seen his calling card.

## Significance

Girardet represents the quiet, dependable provincial whose attachment to Savarus is personal rather than political. He asks no questions, keeps no secrets he is not entrusted with, and acts out of friendship when all other incentives have vanished. In a novel driven by ambition and manipulation, Girardet's uncomplicated loyalty offers a rare instance of disinterested human connection.
