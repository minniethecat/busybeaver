# The Besançon Election

The **Besançon election** is the central political event of Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842). Albert Savarus's campaign for a seat in the Chamber of Deputies represents the culmination of his public ambitions in Besançon, and its failure — engineered by Rosalie de Watteville — marks the turning point of the novel. The election serves as the narrative mechanism through which Balzac explores the intersection of personal passion and political power in Restoration-era France.

## Political Context

Under the Bourbon Restoration, elections to the Chamber of Deputies were fought along increasingly polarized lines between Legitimists (supporters of the elder Bourbon line) and Liberals. Besançon, as a provincial stronghold, was dominated by Legitimist interests, and Savarus — though originally an outsider — secured the backing of this faction through his legal reputation and social maneuvering.

## Savarus's Ambition

For Albert Savarus, the election represents more than political advancement — it is the threshold of his "true life." His legal work in Besançon has been a preparation for this moment:

> "I mean to engage only in commercial cases till I leave the place as deputy."
> — Albert Savarus (ch17-section-17)

Savarus's confidence in his electoral prospects reflects both his genuine talent and his underestimation of provincial political intrigue. He believes his legal brilliance and social connections will suffice; he fails to account for the ways in which private passions can disrupt public calculations.

## Rosalie's Sabotage

Rosalie de Watteville's discovery of Savarus's hidden identity as "Rodolphe" — and her reading of the framed narrative recounting his love for Francesca — transforms her from an infatuated admirer into a calculating adversary. Recognizing that Savarus's election would mean his departure from Besançon (and from her sphere of influence), Rosalie systematically undermines his candidacy.

Her methods include intercepting letters between Savarus and Francesca, manipulating electoral sentiment through her family's social network, and exploiting the political divisions within Besançon's ruling class. The election thus becomes a proxy battle: Rosalie's personal obsession versus Savarus's public ambition.

## Significance

The election episode exemplifies Balzac's recurring theme of the collision between private desire and public life. Savarus, a man of genuine talent and ambition, is brought low not by unworthy opponents but by the intimate machinations of a woman whose feelings he has unwittingly aroused. The episode also illuminates the closed, interdependent nature of provincial society under the Restoration, where political outcomes could be determined by drawing-room intrigue rather than electoral merit.
