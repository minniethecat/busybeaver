# Rue Neuve

The **Rue Neuve** is a street in [[besancon|Besançon]] where [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]] (then known as Albert Savaron) takes his first lodgings upon arriving in the city — "three very scantily furnished rooms at the end of the Rue Neuve, just where it opens into the Rue de la Préfecture" (Section 1). The street occupies a strategic position in the novel's geography: it lies close to both the Watteville mansion and the house of [[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie's]] suitor Amedée de Soulas, making it a node in the network of observation and surveillance that drives the plot.

## Overview

The Rue Neuve is introduced in the novel's opening pages as part of the physical description of Besançon society. Its significance is not architectural but situational: it is the street where Albert Savarus lives, and therefore the street that Rosalie de Watteville watches, the street from which Amedée de Soulas spies on his rival, and the street through which the novel's central mystery — the identity and past of the enigmatic lawyer — is gradually unravelled by the town's collective curiosity.

## Albert's Lodgings

Albert's choice of the Rue Neuve reflects his deliberate strategy of self-concealment. He arrives in Besançon as a stranger and takes modest rooms that suggest a man "uncertain of the future":

> This opinion allowed of his lodging, like a man uncertain of the future, in three very scantily furnished rooms at the end of the Rue Neuve, just where it opens into the Rue de la Préfecture.
> — (Section 1)

The modesty of these lodgings is part of Albert's cover: he buries "his life in the deepest mystery," adopting habits that prevent his accepting any invitations, and consulting only between six and eight in the morning (Section 11). The Rue Neuve rooms, with their sparse furnishings and their garden backing onto the Watteville property, are the physical expression of his self-imposed exile.

## The Network of Surveillance

The Rue Neuve exists at the intersection of several lines of sight. Amedée de Soulas lives at the corner of the Rue Neuve and the Rue du Perron, "looking out on the house where this mysterious personage lodges," and uses his tiger Babylas to gather intelligence on Albert's daily routine:

> In the first place, madame, I live in the Rue Neuve, at the corner of the Rue du Perron; I look out on the house where this mysterious personage lodges; then, of course, there are communications between my tiger and Jerome.
> — (Section 3)

The Watteville mansion, on the Rue de la Préfecture, has "an immense garden stretching to the Rue du Perron" (Section 1), and the Vicar-General notes that "Monsieur Savaron lives in the Rue du Perron; the garden of his house joins on to yours" (Section 1). This contiguity — the garden of Albert's house adjoining the Watteville garden — is the geographical condition that makes possible Rosalie's nocturnal observations from her kiosk and, later, her interception of Mariette and Jerome.

## The Neighborhood

The Rue Neuve belongs to the dense urban fabric of Besançon's old quarter. Nearby landmarks include:

- **The Hôtel de la Préfecture** — the seat of departmental government, at the head of the Rue de la Préfecture
- **The Watteville mansion** — the "fine old mansion of the Rupts" on the Rue de la Préfecture
- **Mademoiselle Galard's house** — on the Rue du Perron, where Albert later takes more permanent rooms after his initial stay at the Hôtel National
- **The Hôtel National** — where Albert first stays upon arriving from Paris

When Albert first arrives in Besançon, he stops at the Hôtel National before finding "apartments in favor of the first floor in Mademoiselle Galard's house, Rue du Perron" (Section 3). His move from the hotel to the Rue du Perron lodgings, whose garden adjoins the Watteville property, is the geographical event that sets the plot in motion.

## Significance

The Rue Neuve and its adjoining streets form what might be called the novel's domestic geography — the network of rooms, gardens, windows, and walls through which characters observe one another and through which information flows. Rosalie watches Albert from her kiosk; Soulas watches Albert from his corner window; Jerome and Mariette pass messages across the garden wall. The Rue Neuve is the centre of this web, and Albert's modest rooms are its blind spot — the place whose occupant is watched by everyone but sees nothing of the forces gathering around him.
