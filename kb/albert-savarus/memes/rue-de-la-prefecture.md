# Rue de la Préfecture

The **Rue de la Préfecture** is the principal aristocratic street in [[besancon|Besançon]], and the address of the [[hotel-de-watteville|Watteville family mansion]]. Its name encodes the political-administrative authority that defines Restoration-era provincial hierarchy — the prefecture being the seat of government power in each French department.

## Overview

The Rue de la Préfecture functions in the novel as a spatial marker of social rank. To live on this street is to belong to Besançon's legitimist aristocracy; to merely pass through it, as Albert Savarus does, is to be reminded of one's outsider status:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Location** | Central Besançon, administrative quarter |
| **Notable Residents** | Watteville family (former Hôtel de Rupt) |
| **Adjacent Streets** | Rue Neuve (opens into it), Rue du Perron (garden boundary) |
| **Social Character** | Upper-class, legitimist, clerical |

## Description in the Novel

The Rue de la Préfecture is introduced at the very beginning of the novel, establishing its importance to the narrative's spatial and social geography. The Wattevilles' residence there represents their consolidation of property after marriage:

> They settled in the Rue de la Préfecture in the fine old mansion of the Rupts, with an immense garden stretching to the Rue du Perron.
> — (ch01-section-1)

This single sentence encodes a complex social history: the extinction of the Rupts family, the consolidation of their property through the Watteville marriage, and the physical expanse of aristocratic privilege embodied in the mansion's "immense garden."

The garden becomes a spatial nexus. The Abbé de Grancey, in one of his conversational revelations, points out the proximity of Albert's lodgings to the Watteville mansion:

> "Why, you can see his windows from hence," replied the Vicar-General. "Monsieur Savaron lives in the Rue du Perron; the garden of his house joins on to yours."
> — (ch01-section-1)

This topological detail — the shared garden wall — is the geographical premise on which the narrative's surveillance plot depends. The Abbé's comment, made in passing at the salon, is freighted with dramatic irony: it is precisely this adjacency that Rosalie will exploit.

Madame de Watteville's reaction to learning of Albert's presence in the neighborhood is telling:

> "But who is he?" asked Madame de Watteville, taking the Abbé's arm to go into the dining-room. "If he is a stranger, by what chance has he settled at Besançon? It is a strange fancy for a barrister."
> — (ch01-section-1)

Her instinctive suspicion — that a stranger settling in the neighborhood is an anomaly requiring explanation — reveals the closed, scrutinizing character of the Rue de la Préfecture's elite.

## The Street as Social Border

Albert Savarus, living in the modest Rue Neuve, is located precisely at the boundary of the aristocratic quarter:

> He lodged at the end of the Rue Neuve, just where it opens into the Rue de la Préfecture.
> — (ch01-section-1)

This intersection — the Rue Neuve meeting the Rue de la Préfecture — is a spatial metaphor for Albert's social position: adjacent to aristocratic power but not yet admitted to it. The narrator notes his lodgings reflect "a man uncertain of the future": three scantily furnished rooms.

The contrast is sharpened by comparison with Amedée de Soulas, who also lives in the Rue Neuve but in a very different style. Soulas, the Besançon dandy, keeps a tiger (a liveried footman) named Babylas, dresses him in "bright blue plush breeches, a red waistcoat, polished leather top-boots," and uses him as a source of neighborhood intelligence:

> "In the first place, madame, I live in the Rue Neuve, at the corner of the Rue du Perron; I look out on the house where this mysterious personage lodges; then, of course, there are communications between my tiger and Jerome."
> — (ch01-section-1)

Soulas's surveillance of Albert from the Rue Neuve mirrors Rosalie's from the Rue de la Préfecture — both observe the stranger, but from different social altitudes and with different intentions.

## The Street as Social Code

In Besançon, street names carry an almost feudal weight. The Rue de la Préfecture derives its prestige from its association with administrative authority (the prefecture) and its aristocratic residents. The Rue du Perron — the unremarkable side street — and the Rue Neuve ("New Street") occupy different social strata. This hierarchy of streets is not merely descriptive but prescriptive: where you live determines who you are, and living on the Rue de la Préfecture means being someone of consequence.

The garden that stretches from the Watteville mansion on the Rue de la Préfecture to the Rue du Perron creates a spatial link between aristocratic display and hidden surveillance — a link that Rosalie de Watteville will exploit to devastating effect.

## The Watteville Salon

The Rue de la Préfecture is also the location of the renowned Watteville salon, the gathering place of Besançon's legitimist elite:

> This was an allusion to the members of the First Court of Appeal of 1830; the Legitimists had almost all withdrawn.
> — (ch01-section-1)

The salon, hosted in the mansion on the Rue de la Préfecture, is the scene of the novel's key social encounters — the place where Albert is first discussed, where his reputation is launched, and where his fate is ultimately decided.

## Significance

The Rue de la Préfecture exemplifies Balzac's technique of making urban geography carry social meaning. It is not merely a street address but a statement of belonging — to live there is to be inscribed in the power structure of Restoration France. Albert Savarus's entire trajectory in Besançon can be measured by his movement toward the Rue de la Préfecture: first as a lodger at its boundary, then as a guest at the Watteville salon, and finally as a suitor whose elimination from society is marked by his permanent exclusion from the street's closed world.
