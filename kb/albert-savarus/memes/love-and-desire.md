# Love and Desire

**Love and desire** form the emotional core of Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842), manifesting in multiple registers: the passionate, all-consuming love between Rodolphe (Albert Savarus) and Francesca; the obsessive, destructive infatuation of Rosalie de Watteville; and the calculating social ambition that masquerades as romantic interest in the provincial marriage market. Balzac examines love not as a simple emotion but as a force capable of shaping — and shattering — lives, careers, and social orders.

## The Framed Romance: Rodolphe and Francesca

At the heart of the novel lies a story-within-the-story: the novella "L'Ambitieux par amour" ("The Ambitious Man Through Love"), which recounts the romance between Rodolphe (Savarus's true identity) and Francesca, an Italian woman of noble birth. This embedded narrative — which Rosalie discovers and reads — reveals the depth of Savarus's passion and the circumstances that separated him from his beloved:

The framed narrative is Balzac's device for exploring love in its most idealized form: a passion that transcends distance, social convention, and even the passage of time. Rodolphe's love for Francesca is the hidden spring of all his actions, the secret that explains his arrival in Besançon under an assumed name.

## Rosalie's Destructive Infatuation

In counterpoint to the idealized romance of Rodolphe and Francesca, Balzac presents Rosalie de Watteville's obsession with Albert Savarus. Rosalie's "love" is revealed to be a form of possessiveness — a desire to control and possess its object rather than to see it flourish:

| Dimension | Rodolphe–Francesca | Rosalie–Savarus |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Nature** | Mutual, passionate, thwarted by circumstance | One-sided, obsessive, self-serving |
| **Expression** | Sacrifice, concealment, devotion | Manipulation, sabotage, control |
| **Outcome** | Tragic separation | Destruction of the beloved's ambitions |
| **Moral Register** | Sympathetic, tragic | Calculating, destructive |

This juxtaposition is central to Balzac's moral vision: genuine love, even when thwarted, ennobles; possessive desire, even when successful, corrupts.

## Love and Social Ambition

The novel also explores love as a commodity in the provincial marriage market. Amédée de Soulas's courtship of Rosalie is motivated not by genuine feeling but by social calculation — a desire to secure the Watteville fortune and position. Balzac thus presents a spectrum of romantic motivations, from the pure passion of Rodolphe and Francesca, through the obsessive desire of Rosalie, to the cold calculation of Soulas.

## Significance

Love in *Albert Savarus* is never merely personal. It is always entangled with social position, political ambition, and economic calculation. Balzac's genius lies in his demonstration that the most intimate emotions are inextricable from the social world in which they arise — and that the concealment of love can be as destructive as its expression.
