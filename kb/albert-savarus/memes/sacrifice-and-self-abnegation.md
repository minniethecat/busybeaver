# Sacrifice and Self-Abnegation

Sacrifice and self-abnegation form a recurring thematic pattern in Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842), manifesting in the renunciations that characters make — or refuse to make — in pursuit of love, ambition, and social standing. The theme interrogates the moral value of self-denial in a world where sacrifice is rarely rewarded and often exploited.

## Albert's Self-Abnegation

Albert Savarus (Rodolphe) is the novel's primary figure of self-abnegation. He has sacrificed his name, his past, and his identity to rebuild himself in Besançon. The persona of "Albert Savaron" is itself an act of renunciation — the abandonment of Rodolphe's Italian past, his relationship with Francesca, and the social world that defined him.

This self-abnegation is both strategic and psychological. Strategically, it enables his political ambitions: a provincial lawyer with no visible past is more electable than a man whose history invites scrutiny. Psychologically, it represents an attempt to cauterize the wound of lost love by becoming someone else entirely. The irony is that this very self-denial makes him more intriguing to Rosalie, who senses the hidden depths beneath his austere surface.

## Francesca's Sacrifice

Francesca Colonna is, in a sense, the sacrifice that Albert has already made before the novel begins. His separation from her — enforced by the political upheavals that destroyed his earlier career — is the original trauma that the Besançon chapters attempt to overcome. Francesca herself, in accepting their separation and later reuniting with Albert only through the fragile medium of letters, embodies a form of passive sacrifice: the woman who waits, whose life is suspended by the decisions of others.

## Rosalie's Refusal to Sacrifice

Rosalie de Watteville represents the antithesis of self-abnegation. Where Albert renounces, Rosalie seizes. Where Francesca waits passively, Rosalie acts decisively. Her refusal to sacrifice her own desires — her insistence that if she cannot have Albert's love, no one else shall — is the engine of the novel's tragedy.

Rosalie's position is in some ways the most modern in the novel. She rejects the Victorian/romantic ideal of the self-sacrificing woman who nobly renounces her love for the happiness of the beloved. Instead, she embodies a more Nietzschean will to power: the conviction that her desire grants her the right to act, regardless of consequences.

## The Moral Economy of Sacrifice

Balzac's treatment of sacrifice is characteristically unsentimental. In the moral economy of *Albert Savarus*, self-abnegation brings no rewards. Albert's years of renunciation lead not to redemption but to destruction. Francesca's patient waiting is rewarded with betrayal. Rosalie's refusal to sacrifice leads not to happiness but to the hollow satisfaction of mutual ruin.

This pessimism reflects Balzac's broader vision of Restoration society as a system in which virtue — including the virtue of self-denial — offers no protection against the operations of power, desire, and social manipulation. Sacrifice, in Balzac's world, is not a path to transcendence but simply another form of loss.

## The Religious Framework

The theme of sacrifice in the novel is subtly reinforced by its religious context. Besançon is a city dominated by the Catholic Church; the Abbé de Grancey, the Abbé Giroud, and the Archbishop of Besançon are prominent figures. The language of sacrifice — of renunciation, of mortification of desire, of the submission of individual will to higher authority — permeates the novel's social world.

Yet Balzac consistently undercuts the redemptive potential of this religious framework. The Church in Besançon is a political institution rather than a spiritual one. The Abbé Giroud, Rosalie's confessor, becomes an unwitting instrument of her schemes rather than a guide to moral self-examination. The novel's characters perform the gestures of religious self-denial — attending Mass, frequenting confession — without experiencing the spiritual transformation these practices are meant to effect.
