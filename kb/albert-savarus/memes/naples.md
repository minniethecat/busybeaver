# Naples

Naples, the capital of the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies, appears in
Balzac's *Albert Savarus* as a seat of displaced aristocratic power
and a site of political upheaval. The city is linked to the Gandolphini
family — relations of the King of Naples — whose exile in Geneva
requires delicate diplomacy involving "the Pope's interest in the
Colonna family" and permission from "foreign powers and the King of
Naples" [009-Section-9]. Naples also serves as one of the coordinates
in Albert's desperate search for Francesca after her disappearance from
Belgirate.

## Overview

Naples enters the novel through the figure of Prince Gandolphini, a
"connection of King Murat's, and a victim of the last revolution"
[009-Section-9]. The revolution in Naples is one of the "three
revolutions in Naples, Piemont, and Spain" for which the bookseller
Lamporani "felt all the enthusiasm of a Philhellene for that worship
of liberty" [007-Section-7].

The city is also the destination that Francesca and Rodolphe plan as
their eventual reunion point. Before Rodolphe departs Geneva to pursue
his legal and political career in Besançon, Francesca tells him: "Go
this evening. We meet no more till we meet at Naples" [010-Section-10].
This promise — "we meet at Naples" — becomes a talisman of hope that
sustains Rodolphe through his years of labor in Besançon.

## Description in the Novel

Balzac's Naples is evoked through sensory notations rather than extended
description. Francesca, in a moment of frank self-revelation at Geneva,
lists the objects of her love: "I love luxury as I love the arts, as
I love a picture by Raphael, a fine horse, a beautiful day, or the
Bay of Naples" [009-Section-9]. This enumeration places Naples among
the supreme aesthetic pleasures — a landscape of perfect beauty.

The political dimension is sketched through the Gandolphini affair.
The family's exile is a consequence of the revolution that deposed
King Murat (Napoleon's brother-in-law, executed in 1815), and their
eventual reinstatement comes with the Restoration: "the Prince and
Princess had been reinstated in their place and rights on the King's
accession" [011-Section-11].

## Significance

Naples functions in the novel as a symbol of deferred happiness and
the costs of ambition. Francesca's "we meet at Naples" represents the
life Rodolphe is working toward — a life of love, ease, and aristocratic
splendor that his legal and political career in Besançon is meant to
secure. But the reunion at Naples never occurs. Instead, after
Rosalie's schemes destroy his political prospects, "Albert went in
search of her to Naples, and from Naples to Rome, where she was now
engaged to the Duc de Rhetore" [020-Section-20]. Naples becomes the
first waypoint in a chain of losses — the city where he discovers that
Francesca has married another.

The Bay of Naples, mentioned by Francesca as one of the objects of
her love, thus acquires a retrospective irony. It is a beauty she
loves in the abstract, but the actual city becomes the site of
Albert's definitive loss.
