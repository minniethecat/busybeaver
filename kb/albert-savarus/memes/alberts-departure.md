# Albert Savarus's Departure from Besançon

Albert Savarus's departure from Besançon is the climactic resolution of Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842), triggered by Rosalie de Watteville's interception and manipulation of his correspondence with Francesca Colonna. The event represents the catastrophic convergence of the novel's central conflicts — love versus ambition, provincial intrigue versus cosmopolitan aspiration, and the destructive power of secrets exposed.

## The Trigger

After Rosalie intercepts Albert's letters to Francesca and reveals the truth to the Duchess at the Opera ball, Albert's position in Besançon becomes untenable. His secret is exposed, his romantic hopes destroyed, and his carefully constructed identity as "Albert Savaron" — the promising provincial lawyer — collapses.

Rosalie's scheme is elegant in its cruelty: by revealing Albert's hidden identity and his ongoing attachment to Francesca, she simultaneously destroys his reputation in Besançon, sabotages his political prospects, and extinguishes any hope of his reunification with Francesca. The Opera ball revelation is the culmination of months of systematic surveillance — Rosalie has observed Albert at Mass, intercepted his letters to Francesca, and methodically gathered the intelligence needed to destroy him.

## The Departure

Albert leaves Besançon without explanation, an exit described in the novel as occurring "without any reason assigned for his evasion." The phrase captures both the abruptness of his departure and the social embarrassment it causes. His disappearance without accounting becomes a matter of local speculation, with his creditors and supporters left to wonder at the cause.

The Vicar-General had lent Albert five thousand francs before his "inexplicable departure," suggesting that even those closest to him in Besançon were kept in the dark about the real reasons for his flight. Albert's departure, in its silence and finality, mirrors his arrival in Besançon — both are acts of radical discontinuity, of cutting ties with a past that can no longer sustain him.

## The Political Dimensions

Albert's departure carries specific political consequences. As a Legitimist candidate for the Chamber of Deputies, his sudden disappearance destabilizes the carefully assembled electoral coalition. The Besançon election, which had pitted Albert against the Orleanist candidate, becomes a casualty of personal scandal. His political allies, particularly the clergy represented by the Vicar-General, are left compromised by their association with a man who fled without explanation.

This intersection of personal scandal and political ruin is a recurring theme in Balzac's [[la-comedie-humaine|Comédie Humaine]], where private passions routinely derail public careers. Albert's fate prefigures that of other Balzacian protagonists — Lucien de Rubempré in *Lost Illusions*, Raphaël de Valentin in *The Wild Ass's Skin* — whose ambitions are destroyed by the collision of desire and circumstance.

## Rosalie's Pyrrhic Victory

Rosalie's role in Albert's departure represents one of the novel's darkest ironies. Having succeeded in destroying the man she claims to love, she gains nothing but the bitter satisfaction of having been, in her own words, "the only sufferer." Her victory is Pyrrhic in the truest sense: she has eliminated her rival, but the object of her desire is now lost to her forever.

The psychological realism of Rosalie's motivation distinguishes Balzac's treatment from simpler revenge narratives. She acts not from hatred but from a twisted form of love — the conviction that if she cannot possess Albert, no one else shall. Her manipulation of the postal system, her cultivation of the Abbé Giroud as an unwitting informant, and her theatrical revelation at the Opera all demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of social dynamics that makes her one of Balzac's most compelling antagonists.

## The Aftermath in Besançon

In the wake of Albert's departure, the Watteville salon — the social center of legitimist Besançon — is forced to absorb the scandal. Madame de Watteville, who had championed Albert as a suitable match for Rosalie, finds her judgment questioned. Amédée de Soulas, Albert's rival for Rosalie's affections, emerges as the apparent beneficiary of Albert's disgrace, though his ultimate success is tainted by the manner of his rival's destruction.

The departure also affects the legal community of Besançon. Albert had established himself as a capable lawyer, and his unexplained flight leaves clients and colleagues without recourse. The professional vacuum he leaves behind underscores the fragility of reputation in provincial society, where a man's standing depends as much on appearances as on competence.

## Literary Significance

Albert's departure is a masterful example of Balzac's technique of the "dénouement" — the untying of the narrative knot. Unlike the elaborate set-pieces that typically conclude Balzac's novels, the departure is notable for its absence of drama. Albert simply vanishes, and the novel's final pages are occupied not with his fate but with the consequences his absence creates for those who remain.

This narrative strategy — ending not with the protagonist's resolution but with his dissolution — places *Albert Savarus* within the tradition of the Romantic anti-climax. Albert's silence, his refusal to provide the explanation that Besançon society craves, is itself a form of resistance, a final assertion of autonomy in a world that has stripped him of everything else.
