# Comte de Soulas

The **Comte de Soulas** refers to [[amedee-de-soulas|Amédée de Soulas]] in his capacity as a titled nobleman and eligible bachelor within Besançon society. The title is invoked throughout Balzac's *Albert Savarus* as a social marker — representing the comital rank that makes Amédée a desirable match, first for Rosalie de Watteville and later for her widowed mother.

## The Title's Role in the Novel

The Comte de Soulas appears in three distinct narrative moments:

1. **Rosalie's Refusal**: Early in the novel, it becomes publicly known in Besançon that "Mademoiselle de Watteville had positively refused the Comte de Soulas" — a rejection that underscores Rosalie's determination to pursue [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]] over the socially approved match.

2. **Post-Savarus Reconciliation**: After Albert is lost to Rosalie, she is asked whether she will "be reconciled to your mother and marry the Comte de Soulas" — to which Rosalie replies she would only do so if Albert ordered it, demonstrating her continued emotional bondage to a lost love.

3. **The Mother's Match**: In the novel's denouement, Rosalie encourages her mother, the widowed [[baronne-de-watteville|Baronne de Watteville]], to marry the Comte de Soulas herself, settling her own fortune on him and securing her mother's future. This arrangement makes the Baronne the new [[comtesse-de-soulas|Comtesse de Soulas]], completing a dark irony: the title Rosalie once spurned becomes her mother's consolation prize, and Rosalie's scheme ensures her own disinheritance.

## Narrative Significance

The Comte de Soulas title functions as a symbol of provincial aristocratic ambition and matrimonial calculation. Amédée de Soulas bears the title but lacks the fortune to sustain it properly — his father left him only four thousand francs a year. The title thus represents hollow nobility: impressive in name, impoverished in substance, and dependent on marriage to a wealthy heiress for its survival.
