# Gender Roles in Restoration France

The position of women in Restoration-era provincial France was defined by an intricate web of legal, religious, and social constraints that assigned them a sharply circumscribed sphere of action. Balzac's *Albert Savarus* offers a sustained portrait of this system at work in Besançon, where "women were standing in a circle round the drawing-room fire, and the men in groups by the windows" — the spatial arrangement itself encoding the gendered division of social space. The novel traces how feminine identity was constructed through three institutions: marriage (as economic and social destiny), religion (as moral regulation), and domesticity (as the only permissible arena of female agency).

## The Matriarch and the Daughter

Madame de Watteville represents one pole of Restoration womanhood: the matriarch who wields power from within the domestic sphere. Described as "a red-haired woman, and of a stern nature that became proverbial," she exercises an authority that is absolute within its domain yet entirely bounded by it. Her husband, "from the first days of his married life," perceived "that he would never be his wife's master," and retreated into "a mechanical occupation and good living." This inversion — the wife as domestic sovereign, the husband as abdicated figure — reveals the paradoxical nature of feminine authority in the Restoration: fierce within the household, invisible beyond it.

Her daughter Rosalie bears the full weight of this system. From infancy, she is subjected to "an exclusively religious education, and by her mother's severity of which the principles were rigidly enforced." The Baroness "taught her daughter every conceivable stitch in tapestry and little women's work — plain sewing, embroidery, netting" — a curriculum designed to fill the years between childhood and marriage with the acquisition of ornamental skills whose true purpose was the demonstration of compliance. At seventeen, Rosalie could leave the Hôtel de Rupt only to attend Mass at the Cathedral "every morning, taken there by her mother," returning immediately afterward to a domestic enclosure that was both protection and prison.

## Marriage as the Organizing Principle

For women of the Restoration aristocracy, marriage was not a choice but an inevitability — the sole legitimate destination of a female life. The novel registers this through the institution of the salon, where "the forty noble families composing the high society of Besançon" gathered to negotiate alliances that were simultaneously romantic, economic, and political. A young woman's value was measured in dowry and lineage; her preferences were, at most, data to be tactfully managed by her mother.

The social gulf that marriage was meant to bridge — or reinforce — is captured in the narrator's observation of enduring feuds "between a woman of rank and a citizen's wife, which endure till death, and widen the impassable gulf which parts the two classes of society." The marriage market was a mechanism for preserving hierarchy, not disrupting it.

## Paris as Counter-Model

The novel repeatedly invokes Parisian femininity as a subversive counter-image to Besançon's provincial constraints. The narrator speaks of "the splendid part played by a woman of fashion in Paris," and describes how "women in Paris bestow their special attention on simpletons who visit them." Paris represents a world where feminine influence extends beyond the household into culture and politics — a possibility that is both thrilling and threatening to the provincial order. It is toward this freer model that Rosalie's imaginative life secretly reaches, even as her mother's system works to foreclose any such future.
