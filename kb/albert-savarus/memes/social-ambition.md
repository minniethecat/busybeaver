# Social Ambition in _Albert Savarus_

Ambition is the public face of the novel's private passions. Albert Savarus arrives in Besançon with a carefully constructed persona designed to advance his legal and political career; the Watteville family and their circle are consumed with social position and strategic advancement. In Restoration France, ambition operates through institutions — the law, the Church, the salon, the electoral system — and Balzac maps this social machinery with precision. This page aggregates all entries that illuminate the novel's treatment of social ambition.

## Notes

Social ambition in _Albert Savarus_ is always double-edged: it drives the novel's public plot (the election, the legal career, the salon politics) but it is also the mask behind which private passions hide. Albert's ambition appears to be purely professional — but it is in fact driven by his desire to be worthy of Francesca. Rosalie's manipulation of Albert's political hopes is the novel's cruelest irony: she destroys him through the very mechanism he used to construct his public self. See also [[politics-and-power]] for the institutional dimension, [[legal-system]] for law as a vehicle of advancement, and [[love-and-marriage]] for how ambition and love intersect.
