# Jérôme

Jérôme is Albert Savarus's servant — his cook, valet, and household manager during the lawyer's residence in Besançon. A former servant of the late Monsieur Galard, Jérôme becomes an unwitting link between the Watteville and Savarus households through his romantic involvement with Mariette, Rosalie's lady's maid.

## Overview

Jérôme occupies a position structurally parallel to Mariette's: he is the servant through whose eyes we glimpse Albert's private life and whose loyalty makes him the keeper of his master's secrets. His romance with Mariette creates a servants' subplot that mirrors and counterpoints the main romantic intrigue.

## Role in the Novel

Jérôme first appears as the means by which Mariette gains access to information about Albert Savarus [004-Section-4]. His courtship of Mariette — crossing the garden wall between the two households — provides the literal and figurative connection that Rosalie exploits [011-Section-11]:

> "Mariette and Jerome!" said she to herself. "Mariette, such an ugly creature!"

Rosalie's discovery of their relationship gives her leverage over Mariette, whom she persuades to act as an intermediary in her scheme.

Jérôme's perspective becomes particularly valuable in the novel's later chapters, when he witnesses Albert's reaction to the news that destroys his political hopes [018-Section-18]. It is Jérôme who reports that Albert "turned as pale as a sheet" and who observes the lawyer's final departure from Besançon [019-Section-19]:

> "the lawyer set out, leaving Jerome with three months' wages, and telling him to find another place."

Through Jérôme's reports — filtered through Mariette to Rosalie — the reader learns of Albert's devastated state and the aftermath of the revelation that ruins him.

## The Servants' Subplot as Narrative Device

Jérôme and Mariette's romance constitutes one of Balzac's most economical uses of a secondary plot. On the surface, it is a simple love story between two servants. Structurally, however, it performs multiple narrative functions simultaneously. Their relationship provides the physical conduit — across the garden wall, through shared confidences — by which information flows between the Watteville and Savarus households. Their courtship, conducted in the interstices of their masters' lives, creates a parallel romantic narrative that throws the main plot's dysfunctions into relief.

Balzac uses this servants' subplot with characteristic irony. While Rosalie and Albert's romance is destroyed by the very strategies intended to secure it — intercepted letters, manufactured evidence, political manipulation — Jérôme and Mariette's relationship succeeds precisely because it operates below the threshold of strategic calculation. They have nothing to scheme for and therefore nothing to lose:

> "Jerome was an honest fellow who made an excellent cook, and who had taken a great liking to Mariette."

The word "honest" is freighted with significance in a novel where nearly every major character practices some form of deception. Jérôme's honesty is not a moral achievement but a structural condition: as a servant, he lacks the social capital that would make dishonesty profitable.

## Key Appearances

Jérôme is present throughout the novel's middle and later chapters as a background figure whose observations become crucial to the plot [014-Section-14], [016-Section-16], [018-Section-18], [019-Section-19]. In the novel's resolution, he marries Mariette and the couple succeeds Modinier as caretakers of the Rouxey estate [020-Section-20].

## Significance

Jérôme and Mariette's marriage provides the novel's counter-example to the political and strategic unions that dominate the main plot. As servants without fortunes to consolidate or lineages to extend, they are free to marry for affection — a freedom that Balzac presents not as romantic idealism but as a structural consequence of their class position [020-Section-20].

Jérôme's role as witness to Albert's downfall also makes him the novel's most important secondary narrator: much of what we know about Albert's private anguish comes through Jérôme's reports. In a novel structured around intercepted communications — letters read by unintended recipients, conversations overheard, secrets betrayed — Jérôme stands as an example of a different kind of intermediary: one who transmits information without altering it, whose loyalty to his master is never in question, and whose testimony, though filtered through Mariette's reports to Rosalie, retains its essential reliability.

The marriage of Jérôme and Mariette at Les Rouxey, and their succession to Modinier's position as caretakers, represents a quiet resolution to the servants' subplot. While the principal characters are destroyed or exiled by the novel's end, the servants survive, marry, and inherit the estate they once served — a conclusion that Balzac presents without sentimentality but with a clear-eyed recognition of the resilience that class position, paradoxically, can confer.
