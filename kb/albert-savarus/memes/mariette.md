# Mariette

Mariette is Rosalie de Watteville's lady's maid and the closest confidante of the novel's protagonist during the central intrigue. A thirty-six-year-old woman of notable ugliness but possessing a "neat figure," she becomes Rosalie's instrument for penetrating the domestic life of Albert Savarus.

## Overview

Mariette occupies a distinctive position in the Watteville household: she is simultaneously a domestic servant and Rosalie's partisan in matters of the heart. Her romantic involvement with Jérôme, Albert Savarus's servant, places her at the intersection of the two households whose contiguity — the Watteville mansion garden adjoining Albert's lodgings on the Rue du Perron — is the geographical premise of the novel's central surveillance plot.

## Role in the Novel

Mariette serves as Rosalie's primary source of intelligence about Albert Savarus [004-Section-4]. When Rosalie decides to learn everything she can about the mysterious lawyer, she turns first to Mariette, who has access to information through the servants' network. Mariette, though initially alarmed by Rosalie's schemes, becomes complicit in them once her own romantic interest in Jérôme is revealed.

The dynamic between mistress and servant is one of the novel's most psychologically acute passages. Rosalie, discovering that Mariette loves Jérôme, uses this knowledge to secure her loyalty [005-Section-5]:

> "Listen, Mariette; I am in love too! In secret and without any return...."

Mariette, "rejoiced at the unexpected turn of affairs," becomes Rosalie's willing ally. She carries letters, reports on Albert's movements, and facilitates Rosalie's clandestine observations. Her confession scene — when Rosalie questions her about Jérôme — reveals the complex blend of fear, loyalty, and self-interest that binds servant to mistress [011-Section-11].

## Key Appearances

Mariette appears throughout the novel's middle chapters as the intermediary of Rosalie's surveillance apparatus [013-Section-13], [014-Section-14]. She accompanies Rosalie to Mass, providing cover for Rosalie's observations of Albert at Saint-Pierre. She dresses Rosalie for evening parties and reports on household developments. When Rosalie falls into despair after her schemes succeed too well, Mariette is the witness to her tears [016-Section-16].

In the novel's later chapters, Mariette continues to serve as Rosalie's confidante, and after the resolution of the main plot, she marries Jérôme [018-Section-18], [020-Section-20]. Their marriage is presented as a counterpoint to the strategic alliances of the aristocracy — a union of genuine affection between people who have no fortunes to consolidate.

## Servants as Structural Counterpoint

The Mariette-Jérôme marriage is the sole example in the novel of a union motivated by affection rather than social calculation. This is not merely a sentimental subplot but a structural feature of Balzac's design. By placing the story's only love-marriage among the servant class, Balzac makes an implicit sociological argument: servants can marry for love precisely because they have no fortunes to consolidate and no political alliances to forge.

This counterpoint operates throughout the novel. The aristocratic characters — Rosalie, Amédée de Soulas, the Wattevilles — navigate marriage as a transaction, weighing fortunes, titles, and political advantage. Albert Savarus himself subordinates love to ambition for most of the narrative. Only Mariette and Jérôme, standing outside the regime of aristocratic alliance, are free to choose each other without calculation.

Balzac's juxtaposition is not sentimental. Mariette is described as "ugly" and Jérôme as a minor domestic servant; their happiness is modest and their social consequence negligible. Yet their marriage is the only one in the novel that is not shadowed by deceit, strategic maneuvering, or tragic consequence. In this sense, Balzac suggests that genuine human connection flourishes not at the summit of society but at its base — a rare note of democratic optimism in an otherwise relentlessly cynical vision of Restoration France.

## Significance

Mariette exemplifies Balzac's attention to the servant class as active participants in — rather than mere background to — the dramas of their employers. Her relationship with Jérôme serves as the novel's only example of a marriage motivated by affection rather than social calculation, providing a structural counterpoint to the political marriages that dominate the plot [020-Section-20]. Balzac's juxtaposition is not sentimental but sociological: servants can marry for love precisely because they stand outside the regime of aristocratic alliance.
