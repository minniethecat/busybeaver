# Duchesse de Rhétoré

The **Duchesse de Rhétoré** is the title held by [[francesca|Francesca Colonna]] after her marriage to [[duc-de-rhetore|Alphonse, Duc de Rhétoré]], eldest son of the Duc de Chaulieu. The title change — from Duchesse d'Argaiolo to Duchesse de Rhétoré — marks the narrative's definitive closure of Albert Savarus's romantic hopes, signaling Francesca's absorption into the Parisian aristocracy and her permanent removal from Albert's world.

## Overview

The title "Duchesse de Rhétoré" appears only in the novel's final sections, after the marriage that Rosalie de Watteville's machinations have engineered. It signifies not merely a change of name but a change of world: the Duchesse d'Argaiolo belonged to the Italian exile community of Lake Lucerne and Lago Maggiore; the Duchesse de Rhétoré belongs to the Parisian diplomatic aristocracy, connected through her new father-in-law to the highest circles of Restoration France.

## The Wedding

The wedding of the Duc de Rhétoré to the Duchesse d'Argaiolo is announced in the newspapers with "great splendor" and "numerous entertainments." The wedding notice, dated May 25th, formalizes what Albert has been pursuing across Europe to prevent:

> "The wedding of Monsieur le Duc de Rhétoré, eldest son of the Duc de Chaulieu, the former Ambassador, to Madame la Duchesse d'Argaiolo, née Princess Soderini, was solemnized with great splendor."
> — (Section 19)

The language of society journalism — neutral, celebratory, impersonal — renders Albert's private catastrophe as a public social event. The wedding is the institutional ratification of everything Rosalie's scheme has achieved.

## Albert's Response

Albert's letter to the Abbé de Grancey registers the marriage as a completed fact that forecloses all possibility of reunion:

> "The Duchesse d'Argaiolo, now Duchesse de Rhétoré, seems to me to have carried severity to an extreme."
> — (Section 19)

The shift from "Duchesse d'Argaiolo" to "now Duchesse de Rhétoré" in Albert's own prose captures the temporal rupture the marriage represents. The woman he loved exists now only in the past; the title "Duchesse de Rhétoré" names a present from which Albert is excluded.

## Rosalie's Confrontation

Rosalie de Watteville, having achieved the destruction of Albert's prospects, seeks out the new Duchesse de Rhétoré at the Opera ball in Paris. Her purpose is "to meet the Duchesse de Rhétoré, to see this wonderful woman, and to overwhelm her with perennial remorse" (Section 19). The confrontation is Rosalie's attempt to extract satisfaction from the ruin she has caused — to witness, firsthand, the suffering of the woman who was her rival.
