# Alfred Boucher

**Alfred Boucher** is a young lawyer of Besançon and the eldest son of the Boucher publishing family, connected by marriage to one of the great Parisian publishing houses. He becomes Albert Savarus's most fervent political supporter, his enthusiasm serving as both the engine and, ultimately, the Achilles' heel of Savarus's electoral campaign.

## Overview

The Boucher family occupies a distinctive niche in Besançon society: neither noble nor peasant, they belong to the rising professional bourgeoisie. Monsieur Boucher _père_ is a contractor positioned to profit from the town's public works; his eldest son Alfred represents the family's literary and professional ambitions through his involvement with the _Eastern Review_ and his legal practice.

> Boucher, connected by marriage with one of the great publishers of Paris, had shares in one of the most important newspapers, the management of which was a family affair. — [001-687]

Alfred's character is defined by a youthful susceptibility to enthusiasm — a trait that makes him a loyal disciple but an unreliable political operator. When Savarus arrives in Besançon with his literary reputation and Parisian allure, Alfred is immediately captivated.

## Role in the Novel

Alfred Boucher's primary narrative function is to serve as the conduit through which Savarus builds his electoral coalition. As the son of an influential contractor and the literary manager of the _Eastern Review_, Alfred controls channels of influence that Savarus, as an outsider, cannot access directly:

> Alfred Boucher, the eldest son, a lawyer who coveted the position of public prosecutor, was the responsible editor. His father, who was to be manager, kept the upper hand and made Alfred Boucher his devoted adherent. — [001-709]

The Boucher committee — a coalition of "the contractor who expected to get the work for the aqueduct for the Arcier waters," "Monsieur Boucher's son," "Girardet the lawyer," "the printer of the _Eastern Review_," and "the paper-maker's agent" — forms the backbone of Savarus's electoral machine.

However, Alfred's enthusiasm proves a double-edged sword. His early, infectious confidence inflates expectations and masks the fragility of the coalition:

> Alfred Boucher's enthusiasm, at first infectious, presently became burdensome. The voters in the Boucher committee diminished in number. — [001-614]

When Savarus mysteriously vanishes after the election, Alfred Boucher is among the few who receive a parting letter. His final act in the narrative is to forward Savarus's instructions to the faithful [[girardet|Monsieur Girardet]], settling the lawyer's remaining affairs.

## Relationships

- **[[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]]** — Alfred's political idol; he becomes "his devoted adherent" after Savarus assumes literary management of the _Eastern Review_.
- **Monsieur Boucher _père_** — His father, the contractor who keeps the upper hand in the family's business affairs.
- **[[girardet|Monsieur Girardet]]** — Fellow lawyer and Savarus supporter; part of the Boucher electoral committee.
- **[[chavoncourt|Monsieur de Chavoncourt]]** — The electoral opponent; Boucher's committee provides the defecting votes that tip the election.
- **[[amedee-de-soulas|Amédée de Soulas]]** — Social rival; both young men move in the same Besançon circles but with different alignments.

## Key Appearances

Alfred Boucher appears at critical electoral junctures. His most vivid moment is his entrance "full of enthusiasm, to announce the decision of the Boucher Committee" — a scene in which Savarus, already sensing the fragility of his position, must temper Alfred's premature celebration:

> Before listening to Monsieur Boucher, who was about to deluge him with a speech announcing the decision of the Boucher Committee, Albert begged for silence, and, as he shook hands with Monsieur Boucher, tried to warn him. — [001-919]

## Significance

Alfred Boucher represents the provincial young professional who, lacking the aristocratic connections of an Amédée de Soulas or the intellectual force of an Albert Savarus, attaches himself to a superior talent in hopes of riding its ascent. His trajectory — from infectious enthusiasm to diminished influence — mirrors the arc of Savarus's own Besançon adventure: a bright beginning that fades into silence.
