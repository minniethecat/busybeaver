# Madame de Chavoncourt

Madame de Chavoncourt is the wife of Monsieur de Chavoncourt, President of the Tribunal and a Royalist deputy representing Besançon. A celebrated beauty of the provincial capital, she maintains one of the most influential salons in the city and serves as a key figure in the social and political networks that structure the novel's world.

## Position in Besançon Society

Madame de Chavoncourt occupies a central position in the upper circle of Besançon society. At the time of the novel's main action, she is described as "a woman of forty" and "one of the beauties of Besançon" (s16). Her Tuesday receptions are a fixture of the provincial social calendar, and she "understands her business as mistress of the house" (s16) with considerable skill. Unlike the more austere Madame de Watteville, Madame de Chavoncourt is portrayed as worldly, inquisitive, and alert to the currents of social ambition that flow through her drawing-room.

Her salon serves multiple functions simultaneously: it is a marketplace for marriage negotiations, a clearing-house for political intelligence, and a stage on which the rivalries and alliances of Besançon's elite are enacted. When Albert Savarus first arrives in the city, it is at Madame de Chavoncourt's that the speculation about his origins and intentions reaches its most intense pitch. "So little is he a native of any place, that no one knows where he comes from," she remarks of the mysterious lawyer (s01), giving voice to the collective curiosity of provincial society.

## The Marriage Market

As the mother of three daughters — Victoire, Sidonie, and a younger girl — Madame de Chavoncourt is deeply invested in the marriage politics of Besançon. Her eldest daughter Victoire is the goddaughter and favorite niece of an elderly aunt who has promised her an estate worth seven thousand francs a year and a hundred thousand francs in cash upon her marriage (s16). This makes Victoire a highly desirable match, and Monsieur de Vauchelles actively pursues her hand.

The marriage calculus becomes entangled with electoral strategy when the Vicar-General, Abbé de Grancey, proposes that Albert Savarus marry the second daughter, Sidonie, as a condition for receiving the Chavoncourt family's electoral support. "Sidonie is sixteen, and your ambition is as good as a gold mine," the Abbé reports, adding that "some one has told Madame de Chavoncourt that she will do better by getting her daughter married than by sending her husband to waste his money in Paris" (s18). The Abbé notes with satisfaction: "That some one manages Madame de Chavoncourt, and Madame de Chavoncourt manages her husband" (s18).

## Political Role in the Election

Madame de Chavoncourt's political influence is exercised indirectly but effectively through her control of social access and her management of her husband's career. While the Chamber is sitting, she lives frugally in the countryside to economize for Monsieur de Chavoncourt's expenses in Paris (s16). Her reward for this sacrifice is the social prestige of being the wife of a deputy — and the power to broker the marriages that will secure her daughters' futures.

During Albert Savarus's electoral campaign, Madame de Chavoncourt is persuaded — through Abbé de Grancey's covert maneuvering — to deliver her husband's votes to Albert's candidacy, on the understanding that Albert will marry Sidonie. At the decisive evening reception at the Hotel de Rupt, she appears "with her second daughter Sidonie, exquisitely dressed" (s18), ready to seal the political-matrimonial bargain. When Albert fails to appear that evening, it is Madame de Chavoncourt who attempts to soothe the growing tension: "My dear Baroness, do not let such serious issues turn on such a trifle. The varnish on his boots is not dry — or a consultation, perhaps, detains Monsieur de Savarus" (s18).

## Character and Temperament

Madame de Chavoncourt is distinguished by a sharp tongue and a keen eye for social detail. Her exchange with Amédée de Soulas about the kiosk in the Watteville garden reveals her characteristic acid wit: "You know the very least things that are done in that house," she says sourly, "as she looked at one of her great girls waiting to be married for a year past" (s05). This remark simultaneously rebukes Soulas's excessive familiarity with the Watteville household and expresses her own frustration with the slow pace of her daughters' marriage prospects.

Yet she is also capable of strategic forbearance. Her willingness to deliver her husband's electoral support to Albert — a political outsider and rival — demonstrates a pragmatic recognition that marriage alliances can transcend political competition. When the plan collapses with Albert's sudden disappearance, her husband benefits: "Next day Monsieur de Chavoncourt was carried to the top by a majority of a hundred and forty votes" (s18).

## Relationship to Other Characters

Madame de Chavoncourt's salon serves as a nexus connecting the major families of Besançon. Her daughters are intimate friends of Rosalie de Watteville, and through this friendship Rosalie gains access to the intelligence network that circulates through the Chavoncourt drawing-room (s16). The Chavoncourt girls provide Rosalie with a mirror against which she measures her own situation — Victoire at sixteen and her younger sister at seventeen-and-a-half are both waiting to be married, their futures the subject of constant negotiation among the salon's habitués. The Chavoncourt household thus functions as a microcosm of provincial marriage politics: three daughters to place, limited resources, and social ambition that must be satisfied through strategic alliance rather than wealth alone.

## After the Novel's Conclusion

Following the election and Albert's permanent departure from Besançon, Monsieur de Chavoncourt secures the deputy's seat. In the novel's epilogue, set years later, we learn that Madame de Soulas (the former Rosalie) discusses family matters with "young Chavoncourt" (s21), suggesting that the Chavoncourt family remains a fixture of Besançon society into the next generation, their political and social position consolidated by the events of the novel.
