# The Press and Journalism in Restoration France

In Balzac's *Albert Savarus*, the periodical press functions as a crucial instrument of political ambition and social mobility. In a society where birth and wealth determine most outcomes, journalism offers a parallel path to influence — one that Albert Savarus exploits with calculated precision.

## The Provincial Press Landscape

The journalistic environment of Restoration Besançon was shaped by competition between established publications. A Parisian editor was imported to manage the "official newspaper" of the prefecture, tasked with defending it against "the little *Gazette*, dropped at Besancon by the great *Gazette*, and the *Patriot*, which frisked in the hands of the Republicans." (ch02-section-2) The tone of provincial journalism was deadly earnest: when the Parisian editor attempted the satirical style of the *Charivari*, he was sternly corrected by local power-brokers with the admonition that Besançon society "resent being amused, and are furious at having been made to laugh." (ch02-section-2)

## The Eastern Review

Albert Savarus's most significant institutional creation in Besançon was the *Eastern Review* (*Revue de l'Est*), a fortnightly periodical launched with the financial backing of forty shares of five hundred francs each, taken up by his first ten merchant clients. Savarus framed the enterprise in terms of regional development: "To compete with Strasbourg, was it not needful that Besancon should become a focus of enlightenment as well as of trade?" (ch05-section-5) The ostensible mission was to promote the economic interests of the region by positioning Besançon as the commercial crossroads between Mulhouse and Lyons, while simultaneously competing with Dijon and Strasbourg for literary and intellectual influence in eastern France.

Crucially, Savarus "did not commit the blunder of putting his name in" the publication (ch05-section-5). This strategic anonymity allowed the *Review* to serve as a platform for building political alliances without appearing self-promotional, while simultaneously establishing him as a figure of intellectual authority in the region.

## The Press as Political Instrument

The *Eastern Review* was never merely a literary undertaking. As the election approached, the printer of the *Review* appeared alongside other provincial notables — the contractor for the Arcier waters aqueduct, the President of the Chamber of Commerce, and twenty-seven "bigwigs" who each represented "on an average six votes" (ch16-section-16). The *Review* thus functioned as the organizing hub of Savarus's political coalition, converting cultural capital into electoral support. Balzac depicts journalism in the provinces not as an end in itself, but as a lever for political candidacy — a mechanism by which an outsider without noble birth could construct the network necessary for advancement in Restoration France.

## The Political Economy of the Provincial Periodical

The *Eastern Review* was both a literary enterprise and a machine for converting cultural capital into political votes. Balzac shows this transformation with documentary precision. Two months before the election, a meeting was held at the house of Monsieur Boucher senior, bringing together "the contractor who expected to get the work for the aqueduct for the Arcier waters," Monsieur Granet (the influential man whom Savarus had assisted), the lawyer Girardet, "the printer of the *Eastern Review*," and the President of the Chamber of Commerce — twenty-seven persons in all, each representing "on an average six votes" though "in estimating their values they said ten, for men always begin by exaggerating their own influence" (ch16-section-16).

The printer's presence at this political gathering reveals the dual nature of the *Review*: it was simultaneously a cultural organ and a patronage network. The shares in the *Review* functioned as political IOUs — each shareholder was a node in the electoral coalition Savarus was constructing. Among the twenty-seven "bigwigs," Balzac notes that "one was wholly devoted to the Prefet, one false brother who secretly looked for some favor from the Ministry" (ch16-section-16). In this ecosystem, the press was not a disinterested observer of politics; it was the mechanism through which political loyalties were bought, sold, and monitored. The *Eastern Review*'s printing press was, in effect, a vote-counting machine, and its subscription list was a map of Savarus's political future.
