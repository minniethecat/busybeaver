# Dent de Vilard

The **Dent de Vilard** is a mountain peak in the Jura range that forms part of the Rouxey estate, belonging to the [[baron-de-watteville|Baron de Watteville]]. Described by Rosalie as "the Jungfrau of that little Switzerland," it is the physical and legal focal point of a boundary dispute that Rosalie exploits to bring [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]] (known as Savaron de Savarus) into her father's affairs. The mountain's narrative significance far exceeds its modest physical character: it is the hinge on which the novel's central plot turns.

## Overview

The Dent de Vilard overlooks the ornamental lake on the Watteville estate at [[les-rouxey|les Rouxey]], standing at the upper end of the property. Its significance in the novel is not scenic but legal: a centuries-old grazing-rights dispute between the communes of les Rouxey and Riceys turns the mountain into the mechanism by which Rosalie orchestrates her first direct contact with Albert Savarus.

The Dent de Vilard is introduced when Rosalie takes a boat ride to the far end of the Rouxey lake. Her father and his steward Modinier guide her to the mountain as the subject of a pressing legal concern:

> Rosalie soon found herself at the upper end shut in by the Dent de Vilard, the Jungfrau of that little Switzerland.
> — (ch15-section-15)

This marks the first moment Rosalie learns of her father's legal vulnerability — and the first moment she sees an opportunity to advance her interest in Albert Savarus. Balzac frames the mountain within the novel's broader pattern of landscape-as-metaphor: the lake at les Rouxey, ringed by mountains, mirrors the enclosed provincial world in which Rosalie is trapped; the Dent de Vilard, at the lake's farthest reach, represents the boundary she must find a way to cross.

## The Boundary Dispute

The Baron de Watteville explains that difficulties have arisen since 1830 between the village authorities of Riceys and himself:

> Some difficulties have arisen since 1830 between the village authorities of Riceys and me, on account of this very Dent de Vilard, and I want to settle the matter without your mother's knowing anything about it, for she is stubborn; she is capable of flinging fire and flames broadcast, particularly if she should hear that the Mayor of Riceys, a republican, got up this action as a sop to his people.
> — (ch15-section-15)

Modinier elaborates that the people of Riceys have long enjoyed the right of grazing and cutting fodder on their side of the Dent de Vilard. The newly elected mayor, [[chantonnit|Monsieur Chantonnit]], now claims that the entire mountain belongs to Riceys and maintains that there was historically a right of way through the Watteville grounds. The Baron acknowledges privately that the Rouxey land is "an usurpation, with no title-deed but lapse of time" (ch15-section-15).

For a century the Dent de Vilard had served as an informal boundary between the two communes without dispute. It "stood as a sort of party wall between the communes of Riceys and les Rouxey, yielding little profit." The object in question, "being covered with snow for six months in the year, was of a nature to cool their ardor" (ch15-section-15). Only the political ferment of the July Revolution of 1830 gave Chantonnit the motivation to inflame the issue.

The Baron's proposed solution — to build a wall and plant thorn-bushes — reveals his passive temperament, the same passivity that has defined his domestic life under the Baronne's rule. It is precisely this weakness that Rosalie exploits.

## Rosalie's Intervention

Rosalie seizes on the dispute immediately. She advises her father to retain the celebrated lawyer Savaron, knowing this will place her in closer proximity to the man she is determined to possess:

> "My dear father, I agree with Modinier. If you wish to secure the joint possession of the Dent de Vilard, you must act with decision, and get a legal opinion which will protect you against this enterprising Chantonnit. Why should you be afraid? Get the famous lawyer Savaron — engage him at once, lest Chantonnit should place the interests of the village in his hands. The man who won the case for the Chapter against the town can certainly win that of Watteville versus Riceys!"
> — (ch15-section-15)

She frames her argument in terms of her future inheritance — les Rouxey will one day be hers — but her true motive is to bring Albert Savarus into the Watteville family's orbit.

In a private moment in the kiosk at les Rouxey, Rosalie later confesses the full scope of her manipulation to herself:

> "Ah! I would have dragged my father into a lawsuit — I would have done anything to get him here! I would have committed any mortal sin, and you will not enter the Wattevilles' drawing-room; I may not hear your fine voice! You make conditions when your help is required by the Wattevilles and the Rupts! — Well, God knows, I meant to be content with these small joys; with seeing you, hearing you speak, going with you to les Rouxey, that your presence might to me make the place sacred. That was all I asked. But now — now I mean to be your wife."
> — (ch15-section-15)

This soliloquy exposes the gulf between Rosalie's public performance of filial concern and her private, consuming obsession. The Dent de Vilard dispute was never about property — it was always about access to Albert.

## Resolution of the Dispute

The lawsuit is resolved offstage, its outcome reported tersely by the Baron:

> "The Court has settled our quarrel with the authorities of Riceys by fixing the boundary line at three hundred feet up the side of the Dent de Vilard. I am having a trench made to collect all the water and carry it into the lake. The village did not appeal, so the decision is final."
> — (ch19-section-19)

Madame de Watteville reveals the real cost of this legal victory: "This decision cost me thirty thousand francs handed over to Chantonnit. That peasant would take nothing else; he sold us peace." The bribe underscores the transactional nature of provincial justice — the boundary is not settled on legal merit but purchased. Chantonnit, the republican mayor who launched the action to burnish his political credentials, emerges as a quiet beneficiary of the old aristocracy's money.

The resolution's timing is significant. By this point in the narrative, Albert Savarus has already been destroyed by Rosalie's machinations: she has intercepted his letters to Francesca and arranged to marry him herself. The Dent de Vilard, having served its narrative purpose — bringing Albert into the Watteville circle — disappears from the story, its legal resolution treated as an afterthought.

## Significance

The Dent de Vilard functions as a narrative pivot: a geographical feature that becomes the hinge on which the plot turns. Without the boundary dispute, Rosalie has no natural pretext to engage Albert Savarus as her father's lawyer. The mountain is thus both a literal landmark and a plot device — Balzac's means of bringing the provincial heiress into professional contact with the man whose destiny she will ultimately destroy.

The mountain also serves a thematic function. It stands at the far end of the enclosed Rouxey valley — the limit of Rosalie's known world. Her journey to it, and her exploitation of the dispute it occasions, marks her first step beyond the confines of provincial life and into the ruthless agency that will define her character for the remainder of the novel.
