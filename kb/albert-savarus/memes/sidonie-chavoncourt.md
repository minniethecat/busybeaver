# Sidonie de Chavoncourt

**Sidonie de Chavoncourt** (aged sixteen) is the second daughter of the Chavoncourt family of Besançon. In the closing stages of the election, she becomes a bargaining chip: the Abbé de Grancey proposes her as a bride for [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]], trading the Chavoncourt family's electoral support for a marriage that would secure the fortunes of a daughter with no dowry.

## Overview

Sidonie appears only at the novel's periphery — a young girl whose future is discussed and traded by adults without her voice ever being directly heard. She exists in the narrative primarily as a structural element: the third iteration of the Abbé de Grancey's ultimatum to Savarus. Yet her very silence makes her one of the novel's more poignant figures, a sixteen-year-old whose life is bartered in a political transaction she cannot influence.

> Sidonie is sixteen, and your ambition is as good as a gold mine. — [001-332]

Her elder sister Victoire is betrothed to [[vauchelles|Monsieur de Vauchelles]] with the promise of an aunt's settlement. The two younger Chavoncourt daughters — Sidonie and her unnamed sister — lack even this security. With Monsieur de Chavoncourt's fortune of fifteen thousand francs stretched across five children, marriage is the only viable future, and Savarus's ambition represents the nearest available "gold mine."

## Role in the Novel

The marriage proposal is delivered with liturgical gravity by the Abbé de Grancey. Three times he intones the same sentence:

> "You will marry Mademoiselle Sidonie de Chavoncourt." — [001-627]

The repetition is not rhetorical flourish but practical negotiation. Each iteration carries a different weight: the first is a proposal, the second a condition, the third an ultimatum. By the third repetition, Albert understands that the priest will not be implicated in any further discussion — "a word more would have compromised the priest's dignity and honor."

The arrangement is transactional in every respect: Madame de Chavoncourt has been persuaded by an unnamed intermediary (almost certainly Rosalie) that marrying her daughter to the candidate is a better investment than sending her husband to waste money in Paris. Savarus, for his part, is to present himself at the Hôtel de Rupt "disinterested," treating the nomination as sufficient fortune.

The scheme collapses when Savarus vanishes on the night of the election. Rosalie's exultant observation — "If there is an end of the election, there is an end of the marriage with Sidonie" — captures the girl's position with brutal clarity: her marital fate is entirely contingent on a political outcome she did not choose and cannot control.

At the evening party where Savarus was expected to seal the engagement, Sidonie appears "exquisitely dressed" alongside her elder sister. When Savarus fails to arrive and a letter confirms his withdrawal, the Chavoncourt marriage plan evaporates without ceremony.

## Relationships

- **[[chavoncourt|Monsieur & Madame de Chavoncourt]]** — Her parents; Madame de Chavoncourt has been persuaded by Rosalie's intermediaries that Savarus is a better investment for Sidonie than her husband's Paris expenses.
- **[[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]]** — The man proposed as her husband; she never meets him, and the engagement dissolves with his disappearance.
- **[[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie de Watteville]]** — The unseen architect of both the proposal and its collapse; Rosalie uses Sidonie as a piece on her chessboard.
- **[[abbe-de-grancey|Abbé de Grancey]]** — The negotiator who presents the marriage as the price of electoral support.
- **[[vauchelles|Monsieur de Vauchelles]]** — Future brother-in-law, betrothed to Victoire.

## Significance

Sidonie de Chavoncourt is the novel's most transparent example of a character reduced to pure instrumentality. She is never given a line of dialogue, a described thought, or an independent action. She appears only as "exquisitely dressed" at a party, as "sixteen" in the Abbé's calculation, as "a gold mine" in Madame de Chavoncourt's maternal arithmetic. Her silence is Balzac's indictment: in the marriage economy of Restoration France, a sixteen-year-old girl is a unit of exchange, not a person.
