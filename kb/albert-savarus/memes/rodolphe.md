# Rodolphe

**Rodolphe** is the name Albert Savarus gives to his fictionalized self in the autobiographical novella "L'Ambitieux par amour" (The Ambitious by Love), which he publishes in the *Revue de l'Est* and which Rosalie de Watteville reads with consuming fascination. The novella-within-the-novel recounts the love story of a young Parisian lawyer for an Italian noblewoman, tracing his struggles through commercial disaster, political upheaval, and the long deferral of his hopes.

## Overview

Rodolphe is a young lawyer of talent and ambition who, traveling in Switzerland, encounters a mysterious Italian woman at the resort of Gersau on Lake Lucerne. She is traveling incognito as the companion to a gout-ridden old gentleman — merely "Gina" — and Rodolphe falls in love with her before learning her true identity: she is Francesca Colonna, married to Prince Gandolphini of Sicily, one of the most brilliant matches in the Papal States.

The novella traces Rodolphe's career through a series of setbacks, each of which postpones his hoped-for marriage to Francesca. His commercial ventures are ruined when the Villèle ministry falls; his political prospects are destroyed by the July Revolution of 1830. Between these disasters, he spends three idyllic months at the Villa Gandolphini in Naples — a brief respite that sustains him through years of struggle. The narrative is framed as a fiction, but its autobiographical core is transparent to readers who know Albert Savarus's own history.

## The Gersau Meeting

Rodolphe first encounters Francesca at Gersau, a small resort on Lake Lucerne where she is staying at the Bergmanns' lodging-house. She presents herself with deliberate obscurity — a young woman traveling as companion to an elderly gentleman — and Rodolphe is captivated before he knows who she is. The setting, with its Alpine lake and mountain views, provides the romantic backdrop for their initial intimacy:

> "The two lovers happened to be at a part of the terrace where the rock arises abruptly from the lake, and were leaning over the stone parapet that crowns the wall above a flight of steps leading down to a landing-stage."

Their meetings at Gersau establish the pattern of their relationship: restraint imposed by propriety, intimacy achieved through stolen moments and exchanged glances, and the constant awareness of a social distance that Rodolphe must work to bridge.

## The Geneva Revelation

The winter following their meeting, Rodolphe follows Francesca to Geneva, where she reveals her true rank. At the Villa Jeanrenaud, the Gandolphinis' Geneva residence, Rodolphe attends a grand party and sees Francesca for the first time as the Princess she truly is. Her transformation is complete: "Her sublime beauty was beyond all possible rivalry," and she moves through the aristocratic assembly as its natural center.

Francesca acknowledges the deception with characteristic wit: "I have for the last minute been Princess Rodolphini" — a jest that both confesses her love and restores the intimacy of Gersau. The portrait of Francesca painted by Schinner, which Rodolphe finds in his Paris rooms upon returning, becomes the talisman of their love: the artist "had passed through Geneva on his way to Italy" and Francesca "had bewitched him, and obtained from him — which was almost a miracle — an original portrait for Rodolphe."

## Ambition Sanctified by Love

Rodolphe's career is driven by a single purpose: to make himself worthy of Francesca. "Possessed by an ambition sanctified by his love," he risks his capital in a commercial enterprise, devoting "all his faculties as well as his capital" to the venture. But inexperience and the duplicity of his partners destroy him: "three years were lost in a vast enterprise, three years of struggling and courage."

The fall of the Villèle ministry compounds his ruin, and he turns to politics as a second avenue. The July Revolution of 1830 destroys his political prospects as thoroughly as the ministry's fall had destroyed his commercial ones. Through all these setbacks, his correspondence with Francesca — "the only indulgence they allowed themselves" — sustains him.

## Rodolphe and Albert Savarus

The relationship between Rodolphe and his creator is one of the novel's central interpretive questions. The novella is transparent autobiography: Albert, like Rodolphe, is a Parisian lawyer who has loved an Italian noblewoman and whose career has been frustrated by political reversals. By publishing the story, Albert makes his private history public — a decision that Rosalie de Watteville exploits with devastating effect.

Where Albert maintains a careful public persona in Besançon — the lawyer, the candidate, the salon habitué — Rodolphe is the passionate inner self he reveals only in fiction. The distance between the two personae measures the cost of Albert's ambition: to succeed in provincial society, he must conceal the romantic intensity that defines him.
