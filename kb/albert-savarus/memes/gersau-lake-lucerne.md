# Gersau and Lake Lucerne

**Gersau** is a small Swiss hamlet on the shores of **Lake Lucerne** (the Lake of the Four Cantons) where the embedded love story between Rodolphe (Albert Savarus) and Francesca (Princess Colonna) unfolds. Though geographically distant from [[besancon|Besançon]], it is the emotional and narrative origin of the novel's central tragedy.

## Overview

The Swiss episode occupies a substantial portion of the narrative as a story-within-a-story, told through Albert Savarus's autobiographical manuscript. The arrival at Gersau is narrated as a moment of aesthetic rapture:

## Arrival at Gersau

In 1823, two young friends — Rodolphe and Leopold — set out on a Swiss holiday, departing from Lucerne in a boat rowed by three oarsmen, bound for Fluelen:

> In 1823 two young men, having agreed as a plan for a holiday to make a tour through Switzerland, set out from Lucerne one fine morning in the month of July in a boat pulled by three oarsmen. They started for Fluelen, intending to stop at every notable spot on the lake of the Four Cantons.
> — (ch05-section-5)

The landscape between Lucerne and Fluelen is described in terms of its inexhaustible variety:

> The views which shut in the waters on the way from Lucerne to Fluelen offer every combination that the most exacting fancy can demand of mountains and rivers, lakes and rocks, brooks and pastures, trees and torrents. Here are austere solitudes and charming headlands, smiling and freshly green meadows, forests crowning perpendicular granite cliffs, deserted inlets expanding to view, and valleys whose beauty seems the lovelier in the dreamy distance.
> — (ch05-section-5)

The travelers reach Gersau during this boat journey. The moment is transformative — Rodolphe's eye is caught by a woman's silhouette framed in a window:

> As they passed the pretty hamlet of Gersau, one of the friends looked back at it with an expression of admiration.

The sight that arrests Rodolphe is a wooden house on a promontory, and within it, a woman — later known as Miss Fanny Lovelace:

> One of the young men met the glance thus indifferently given by the unknown fair.

Rodolphe's decision is instantaneous, impulsive, and fateful:

> "Let us stop here," said he to his friend. "We meant to make Lucerne our headquarters for seeing Switzerland; you will not take it amiss, Leopold, if I change my mind and stay here to take charge of our belongings."

He instructs the boatmen: "Be so good as to land, men, and put us out at this village; we will breakfast here."

> — (ch05-section-5)

Leopold's reply is laconic: "Here or at Lucerne, the difference is not so great that I need hinder you from following your whim."

The friends breakfast at the Swan Inn, then explore the hamlet. Rodolphe discovers a boarding-house overlooking the lake:

> While breakfast was prepared for them at the Swan Inn, the friends walked round the hamlet and came to the neighborhood of the pretty new house; here, while gazing about him and talking to the inhabitants, Rodolphe discovered the residence of some decent folk, who were willing to take him as a boarder, a very frequent custom in Switzerland. They offered him a bedroom looking over the lake and the mountains, and from whence he had a view of one of those immense sweeping reaches which, in this lake, are the admiration of every traveler.
> — (ch05-section-5)

## The Lovelace Household

Francesca, living under the alias Miss Fanny Lovelace, had settled at Gersau for her father's health:

> Miss Lovelace had come to settle by the lake for her father's health, the physicians having recommended him the air of Lucerne. These two English people had arrived with no other servant than a little girl of fourteen, a dumb child, much attached to Miss Fanny, on whom she waited very intelligently.

Poverty constrained the Lovelaces' exploration of the lake:

> The Lovelaces could not afford to hire boats to row on the lake, or horses and guides to explore the neighborhood. Poverty demanding such privation as this excites all the greater compassion among the Swiss, because it deprives them of a chance of profit.

The cook of the establishment fed the three English boarders for "a hundred francs a month inclusive." Miss Fanny was said to be nineteen, the old man's youngest child and "the object of his adulation." She had hired a piano from Lucerne: "She loves flowers and music, and she is unmarried! thought Rodolphe."

> — (ch05-section-5)

## The Summer Idyll

The summer months at Gersau form the emotional core of Albert's past life. The hamlet is repeatedly described as a kind of earthly paradise, a refuge from the world where love can flourish without social constraint. The natural setting mirrors the lovers' emotional state:

> In the evening he, of course, went out in a boat on the lake, round the promontory, and past the waterfall, returning by the light of the moon.
> — (ch05-section-5)

The geography of Gersau — its isolation, the enclosing mountains, and the lake's reflective surface — creates a world within a world, separate from the social hierarchies of Besançon and Paris that will later entrap Albert.

Rodolphe's courtship of Fanny proceeds through music. He secures an introduction through their shared love of the piano, declaring: "I have been told that Miss Lovelace is fond of music. I should be delighted if, during my residence by the lake to which I am condemned by my doctor's orders, she would allow me to join her."

## Rodolphe's Return after Francesca's Death

When Francesca dies, Rodolphe returns to Gersau as the sole place capable of containing his grief:

> Gersau, the only place in the world where his grief could grow calmer.

The community responds with sympathy:

> The young Frenchman's position, his despair, the circumstances which made such a loss worse for him than for any other man, were known, and secured him the pity and interest of every one in Gersau. Every morning...
> — (ch05-section-5)

But Gersau eventually becomes unbearable. Francesca had expressed a wish to leave the hamlet, saying she longed for luxury and the arts. After her death, Rodolphe feels the same urge:

> It then seemed to him intolerable to remain at Gersau.
> — (ch05-section-5)

The lovers cannot stay in their paradise after it has been emptied of love.

## Francesca's Final Return to Gersau

In the novel's climax, Francesca, finding herself dying, attempts a desperate return to Gersau. She commands the boatmen:

> "Get quickly back to Gersau," she said to the boatmen, "I will not let..."

The implication is that she wishes to die in the place where her love was born — a wish thwarted by death itself.

## Significance

Gersau and Lake Lucerne function as a narrative antithesis to Besançon. In Switzerland, love is possible; in provincial France, it is destroyed. The lake's beauty and the hamlet's simplicity serve as a permanent point of contrast for Albert, who measures all subsequent experience against the lost paradise of Gersau. The place name recurs throughout the novel as a marker of authentic emotion — a geographical symbol of what cannot be recovered. Balzac's description of the Swiss landscape is among the most sustained passages of natural description in the novel, evidence that the setting is not mere backdrop but an active agent in the love story.
