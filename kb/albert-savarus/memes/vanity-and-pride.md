# Vanity and Pride in Provincial Society

**Vanity** and **pride** are among the most reliable engines of human action in Balzac's fictional universe, and *Albert Savarus* is no exception. From the collective self-regard of the Besançon aristocracy — "pure vanity on its part" — to the individual pride that drives characters toward their fates, these related but distinct passions shape the novel's social and psychological landscape. Balzac treats them not as moral failings to be condemned but as structural features of a society organized around display, reputation, and the ceaseless negotiation of status.

## Collective Vanity: The Aristocracy's Self-Image

Besançon's aristocracy maintains its coherence through a shared investment in its own importance. The narrator observes of Amedée de Soulas:

> "If young Monsieur de Soulas did not fall in the esteem of Besançon society, it was out of pure vanity on its part; the aristocracy were happy to affect a modern air, and to be able to show any Parisians of rank who visited the Comte a young man who bore some likeness to them."

Vanity here is not an individual failing but a collective project. The aristocracy needs young men like Soulas because they function as mirrors — they reflect back an image of the class as modern, connected, and relevant. The "pure vanity" of the Besançon elite is the psychological glue that holds their exclusive world together, and any challenge to it (such as Albert Savarus's independent success) threatens the entire edifice.

## Pride as a Characterological Force

Balzac distinguishes carefully between vanity (the need for others' approval) and pride (the self-sufficient conviction of one's own worth). Madame de Watteville's "stern nature" is an expression of pride — she does not seek the approval of others because she is entirely certain of her own position. Rosalie, by contrast, inherits this pride but directs it toward objects her mother cannot comprehend: first toward religious fervor, then toward Albert. Her pride makes her capable of extraordinary action (the interception of letters, the manipulation of social networks) but also blinds her to the consequences of those actions.

## Vanity as Social Glue, Vanity as Trap

The novel suggests that vanity serves a genuine social function: it makes hierarchy bearable. When Amedée de Soulas "lays himself out to flatter Mademoiselle de Watteville's conceit," he is performing a ritual that reinforces the existing order. The aristocracy's vanity is satisfied by flattery, and flattery in turn reinforces the aristocrat's sense of entitlement. But this closed circuit of mutual admiration is also a trap — it prevents the perception of reality. Rosalie's tragedy unfolds precisely because the vanities of provincial life have equipped no one, least of all herself, to recognize the force of genuine passion when it arrives.

## The Balzacian Diagnosis

Balzac's treatment of vanity and pride places him in a tradition that runs from La Rochefoucauld to Stendhal: the tradition of French moral psychology that sees self-love (*amour-propre*) as the hidden spring of all human action. In *Albert Savarus*, vanity is not a sin to be expiated but a condition to be understood — and, perhaps, to be pitied. The novel's closing pages offer no moral condemnation of its characters' vanity; they offer only the quiet devastation of its consequences.
