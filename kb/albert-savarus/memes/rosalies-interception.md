# Rosalie's Interception of the Letters

**Rosalie's Interception of the Letters** is the central act of sabotage in Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus*. Discovering Albert's hidden correspondence with Francesca Colonna, Rosalie de Watteville intercepts and reads letters not intended for her eyes, setting in motion the chain of revelations and betrayals that destroys Albert's political career in Besançon, his relationship with Francesca, and ultimately his life. The event dramatizes the novel's central moral theme: the secret crime that leaves no trace in law but exacts its punishment through fate.

## Overview

The interception occurs after Rosalie has read Albert's autobiographical novella "L'Ambitieux par amour" in the *Revue de l'Est*. Consumed by jealousy of the woman Albert loves and desperate to prevent his departure from Besançon, Rosalie undertakes a systematic violation of privacy. She gains access to Albert's private correspondence — letters that pass between him and Francesca through an intermediary channel — and reads their contents. The intercepted letters reveal the full extent of Albert's attachment to Francesca and, crucially, the political implications of that attachment in the charged atmosphere of Restoration Besançon.

## Method and Discovery

Rosalie's method is never described in precise technical detail — Balzac prefers to emphasize the moral rather than the mechanical dimensions of the act — but the narrative makes clear that she exploits her access to the postal system and her knowledge of Albert's routines. As the daughter of one of Besançon's most prominent families and a regular at the Watteville salon, Rosalie moves through the city's social spaces with an invisibility born of privilege. No one suspects the intense young woman of systematic espionage.

The letters she reads reveal that Albert's political ambitions in Besançon are, in part, a means to an end: the establishment of a position sufficient to marry Francesca. His Legitimist candidacy, his cultivation of the clergy, his careful management of the Watteville connection — all are dedicated to the distant Italian Princess whose portrait hangs in his rooms. For Rosalie, who has imagined herself as the object of Albert's attention, the revelation is devastating.

## The Moral Crime

The Abbé de Grancey, when he learns of Rosalie's act, delivers the novel's most explicit moral judgment. His words transform a private misdeed into a theological and philosophical meditation on hidden guilt:

> "Purely moral crimes, which give no hold to human justice, are the most atrocious and detestable. God often punishes them on earth; herein lies the reason of the terrible catastrophes which to us seem inexplicable. Of all secret crimes buried in the mystery of private life, the most disgraceful is that of breaking the seal of a letter, or of reading it surreptitiously."

The Abbé's distinction between "moral crimes" and legally actionable offenses is crucial to Balzac's moral scheme. Rosalie has violated no statute; no court in Besançon could convict her. Yet the crime is, in the Abbé's judgment, "the most disgraceful" of all private sins — an act that stains the perpetrator's honor beyond recovery, regardless of its legal impunity.

## The Prince Soderini's Intervention

The interception's consequences are brought to their catastrophic conclusion by the arrival of Prince Soderini, Francesca's father. Coming to Besançon to retrieve his daughter's letters and portraits, the Prince represents the external world — Italy, the aristocracy, the network of honor and obligation that Albert inhabits beyond the confines of Besançon. His arrival is "the thunderbolt that fell on Albert's head," exposing the interception and destroying the careful edifice of Albert's provincial life:

> "I understand now that old Prince Soderini, the father of the Duchess d'Argaiolo, came here to take back his daughter's letters and portraits. This was the thunderbolt that fell on Albert's head, and he went off, no doubt, to try to justify himself."

The Prince's mission — to reclaim the tokens of a love that has been exposed to the gaze of a stranger — is an act of honor that completes Rosalie's act of dishonor.

## Consequences

1. **Albert's Departure**: Albert leaves Besançon immediately, abandoning his legal practice, his political candidacy, and his carefully cultivated social position. "In fourteen months he has given us no news of himself."

2. **The Destroyed Love**: The interception fatally compromises Albert's relationship with Francesca. The letters, read by a third party, can never be private again; the love they express has been contaminated by Rosalie's gaze.

3. **Rosalie's Remorse**: In the novel's denouement, Rosalie — now living in retreat at Les Rouxey — is haunted by the consequences of her act. Her encouragement of her mother's marriage to Soulas and her renunciation of her inheritance are acts of self-punishment, gestures toward a redemption that may never arrive.

4. **The Moral Architecture**: The interception establishes the novel's providential scheme. Balzac, through the Abbé de Grancey, insists that such crimes are punished — not by human justice but by the deeper logic of fate, which arranges "terrible catastrophes" as the instrument of divine retribution.
