# Parental Authority and Family Power in Restoration France

In the provincial world of Balzac's *Albert Savarus*, the family operates as a miniature autocracy. Parental authority — wielded primarily by Madame de Watteville over her daughter Rosalie — constitutes the central mechanism of social reproduction, ensuring that marriage choices, religious observance, and class boundaries are maintained across generations.

## Madame de Watteville's Domestic Regime

The Baroness de Watteville exercised a form of domestic power that Balzac explicitly characterizes as "despotism." Her control over Rosalie was total, extending to every dimension of the girl's existence: "Rosalie knew absolutely nothing. Is it knowledge to have learned geography from Guthrie, sacred history, ancient history, the history of France, and the four rules all passed through the sieve of an old Jesuit?" (ch01-section-1) Dancing and music were permitted only insofar as they served the purposes of social display within the tightly circumscribed world of the Besançon aristocracy.

Physical movement was equally controlled. Rosalie "quitted" the Hôtel de Rupt only rarely, for her mother "rarely quitted" it herself "so devoted was she to her dear Archbishop" (ch01-section-1). When they did go out, it was to the Cathedral each morning — mother and daughter together, the sacred and the disciplinary converging in a single daily ritual. In the evenings, Rosalie was permitted to accompany her mother to "other houses to spend the evening, without being expected to take part in the conversation" (ch01-section-1).

## Surveillance and Confession

The Baroness's control operated through what might be called a system of total surveillance. She "kept all the keener watch over her daughter, because she considered her honor as a mother to be at stake" (ch03-section-3). The domestic sphere was saturated with observation: the Baroness "studied" her daughter constantly, "tried to detect symptoms of jealousy in that youthful soul" (ch03-section-3), and when persuasion failed, "armed herself with the respect due from children to their parents to reduce Rosalie to passive obedience" (ch03-section-3).

The Church functioned as an extension of parental authority. The Abbé de Grancey, Vicar-General of the diocese and "chief Father Confessor," was positioned as mediator between mother and daughter. Yet Balzac notes pointedly that "neither mother nor daughter, confessed to him" — the confessional, theoretically a site of private conscience, had become another contested ground in the domestic power struggle (ch03-section-3).

## Rosalie's Counter-Strategy

Parental authority in the novel is not merely imposed — it is actively resisted, and it is in this resistance that Balzac's psychological penetration is most acute. Rosalie "knew her mother well enough to understand the danger of such thoughts" and developed a strategy of deliberate opacity: "to her mother's incitement she replied merely by such phrases as are wrongly called Jesuitical — wrongly, because the Jesuits were strong and Rosalie was weak" (ch03-section-3).

Rosalie's rebellion, when it comes, is all the more devastating for having been concealed. She persuades her father to engage in wood-turning projects — an "impulse of extreme affection for her father" that was in fact a calculated maneuver to secure unsupervised time. She systematically lies about the duration of her walks, "telling her mother eight, so as to have about a quarter of an hour near Albert" (ch03-section-3). The domestic despotism that was meant to produce obedience has instead produced a formidable dissembler.

## The Father's Role

Baron de Watteville occupies a peculiar position in this domestic economy: nominally the head of the household, he is in practice as subject to his wife's authority as his daughter is. He "spent his existence in a handsome workshop with lathes and tools" — a retreat into hobby that constitutes an abdication of paternal authority (ch01-section-1). His weakness makes Madame de Watteville's despotism possible, and Rosalie's manipulation of him demonstrates that the family power structure, though rigid, contains exploitable fissures.

## The Archbishop's Intervention and Its Limits

The novel provides one rare moment in which an external authority challenges the Baroness's domestic regime — and the outcome reveals the absolute limits of outside intervention. The Archbishop of Besançon himself, the highest religious authority in the diocese, rebuked the Baroness "for being too hard on the child" (ch03-section-3). Her reply is startling in its theological confidence and its diagnostic precision: "Leave me to manage her, monseigneur. I know her! She has more than one Beelzebub in her skin!" (ch03-section-3).

This exchange, condensed into two lines of dialogue, encapsulates the dynamics of parental authority in Restoration France. The Archbishop — representing the institutional Church, the monarchy's spiritual arm, and the patriarchal order — is reduced to an ineffectual admonisher. The Baroness invokes a private knowledge ("I know her") that trumps ecclesiastical authority. Her diagnosis of demonic inheritance — "more than one Beelzebub in her skin" — reframes maternal surveillance as a spiritual duty, preemptively justifying any severity as necessary exorcism. The phrase "leave me to manage her" delineates the domestic sphere as a zone of maternal jurisdiction from which even the Archbishop is politely but firmly excluded. Balzac shows that the family in Restoration France was not merely a microcosm of the absolutist state; it could, in the person of the matriarch, out-absolutize it.
