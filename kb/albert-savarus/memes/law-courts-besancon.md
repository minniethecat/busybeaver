# Law Courts of Besançon

The **Law Courts of Besançon** (formally the First Court of Appeal) are the judicial institution where [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]], practicing under the name "Savaron," builds his professional reputation and social standing in Besançon. The courts function as the primary arena of his ambition — and the stage for his eventual political downfall.

## Overview

The legal profession is central to Albert's identity. He arrives in Besançon as an unknown barrister and rapidly ascends through a combination of forensic skill and strategic self-presentation. The courts provide the institutional framework for this ascent:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Institution** | First Court of Appeal, Besançon |
| **Function** | Provincial appellate jurisdiction |
| **Albert's Role** | Pleader (barrister), rising star |

| **Social Function** | Gateway to Besançon's elite society |

## Political Context: The Court of 1830

The Court of Appeal carries a distinct political character that shapes its operations. After the July Revolution, the Legitimist judges had mostly withdrawn, altering the court's ideological composition:

> This was an allusion to the members of the First Court of Appeal of 1830; the Legitimists had almost all withdrawn.
> — (ch01-section-1)

This political realignment affects the Watteville family's legal strategy. When the Abbé de Grancey secures a favorable verdict, the news is received with astonishment:

> "The verdict is in our favor on every point, and reverses the decision of the Lower Court."
> "Everybody thought you were done for."
> — (ch01-section-1)

The Abbé credits their salvation to a new pleader: "I told our advocate to be off to Paris, and at the crucial moment I was able to secure a new pleader, to whom we owe our victory, a wonderful man—"

"At Besançon?" asks Monsieur de Watteville, guilelessly.

"At Besançon," replies the Abbé.

## The President's Trick and Albert's Breakthrough

The President of the Court tests the newcomer with a cruel joke that becomes Albert's opportunity for vindication:

> The President of the Court played this pleader a trick by appointing him to defend at the Assizes a half-witted peasant accused of forgery.
> — (ch04-section-4)

Albert transforms the setup into a triumph. He not only secures the peasant's acquittal but exposes the real culprits:

> But Monsieur Savaron procured the poor man's acquittal by proving his innocence and showing that he had been a tool in the hands of the real culprits. Not only did his line of defence succeed, but it led to the arrest of two of the witnesses, who were proved guilty and condemned. His speech struck the Court and the jury.
> — (ch04-section-4)

The immediate consequence: "One of these, a merchant, placed a difficult case next day in the hands of Monsieur Savaron, and he won it." The Abbé recounts this story with evident satisfaction to the assembled company at the Watteville salon, concluding: "As soon as I saw him and heard him, I felt faith in him, and I was not wrong."

## The Commercial Tribunal

Albert's reputation is consolidated through appearances at the Commercial Tribunal, the branch of the court system dealing with mercantile disputes:

> Nevertheless, he pleaded three times at the Commercial Tribunal in three knotty cases which had to be carried to the superior Court. He thus gained as clients four of the chief merchants of the place, who discerned in him so much good sense and sound legal purview that they placed their claims in his hands.
> — (ch04-section-4)

The merchants become Albert's power base. On the same day the Watteville family inaugurates the Belvedere, Albert launches his own monument: the *Eastern Review*, funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each from his merchant clients.

## The Law Courts as Social Theater

The Besançon courts are more than a professional venue — they are a form of public theater where reputations are made and broken. Albert understands this intuitively, using his courtroom performances to project an image of genius and ambition:

> He will be the greatest pleader in Besançon. By and by I will start a newspaper.
> — (ch09-section-9)

The narrative explicitly contrasts provincial legal practice with Parisian norms. In Besançon, a legal victory is measured not by fees but by social standing. Albert notes that "Eloquence is not at the Bar. The pleader rarely puts forth the real energy of his soul" (ch17-section-17), recognizing that courtroom rhetoric serves social rather than purely legal ends.

The legal infrastructure of Besançon also becomes the mechanism through which the Rouxey lawsuit — the plot's central legal conflict — is prosecuted:

> "It might be carried to the Court of Appeal, but our adversaries will think twice about that," replied the Abbé.
> — (ch09-section-9)

## The Garcenault Connection

When the Watteville lawsuit reaches a critical juncture, the usual advocate (Monsieur Berryer) is unavailable. Monsieur de Garcenault, a legal insider, recommends Albert as a replacement:

> Monsieur Berryer finding it impossible to come to Besançon, Monsieur de Garcenault advised him to employ this Monsieur Albert Savaron, foretelling our success.
> — (ch04-section-4)

This recommendation — from a member of the legal establishment — legitimizes Albert in the eyes of the Besançon aristocracy and opens the door to the Watteville salon.

## Significance

The Law Courts represent the institutional pathway through which Albert attempts to ascend from anonymous stranger to political figure. They are the theater where his talents are first displayed and the mechanism by which his reputation spreads. The courts also embody the tension between provincial and Parisian ambition — success in Besançon's courts opens doors to the Chamber of Deputies, but ultimately proves insufficient to secure Albert's larger dreams. The President's trick, the Commercial Tribunal cases, and the Watteville lawsuit each mark a rung on Albert's ladder of ascent — and each rung is constructed from legal victories that double as social conquests.
