# Neuchâtel

**Neuchâtel** is a Swiss city on the shore of Lake Neuchâtel, briefly referenced in Balzac's *Albert Savarus* as the place of origin of Monsieur Chantonnit, the mayor of Riceys. The reference typifies Balzac's technique of using personal origins to map the geographical and political networks that connect provincial France to the wider European world.

## Overview

In the novel, Neuchâtel serves a genealogical rather than a dramatic function — it is the birthplace that explains a character's name and, by implication, his political posture on the Franco-Swiss frontier:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Location** | Canton of Neuchâtel, Switzerland, on Lake Neuchâtel |
| **Novel significance** | Origin point of Monsieur Chantonnit, mayor of Riceys |
| **Geopolitical context** | Situated on the "peaceful frontier" of Switzerland and France |

## Historical Context

In the early nineteenth century, the Principality of Neuchâtel occupied a peculiar constitutional position: it was simultaneously a Swiss canton and a Prussian principality, having been ceded to the King of Prussia by the Congress of Vienna in 1815 while also joining the Swiss Confederation. This dual status — Swiss and Prussian, republican and monarchical — made Neuchâtel a site of political ambiguity that resonated with the complexities of Restoration-era Europe. It would not fully resolve its constitutional identity until the peaceful revolution of 1848, several years after the publication of *Albert Savarus*.

Balzac, always attuned to the political valences of geography, would have recognized Neuchâtel as more than a neutral Swiss town. It was a place where different political traditions intersected — much as Besançon itself sat at the intersection of French centralization and local Franche-Comté identity.

## Description in the Novel

Neuchâtel appears in the novel not through direct description but through the etymology of a character's name:

> "Chantonnit, as his name shows, was a native of Neuchâtel."
> — (ch15-section-15)

This single line does significant narrative work: it marks Chantonnit as an outsider in French provincial politics, a man whose ambitions — to "give a dramatic turn to his career on the peaceful frontier of Switzerland" — are shaped by his origins in a different political and linguistic environment.

The choice of the word "peaceful" to describe the Swiss frontier is pointed. It contrasts with the turbulent internal politics of Restoration France and suggests that Chantonnit's Swiss origins may equip him with a perspective that his French-born colleagues lack — or, alternatively, that his foreignness limits his understanding of the stakes involved in French political combat.

## Chantonnit and the Frontier

Chantonnit's Neuchâtel origins make him a liminal figure in the novel's political landscape. As mayor of Riceys — a French town near the Swiss border — he occupies a position that is geographically and culturally intermediate. His name, as the narrator notes, is itself a declaration of origin: "Chantonnit" is recognizably Swiss, marking its bearer as someone who comes from elsewhere and whose roots lie in a different political tradition.

This liminality serves a narrative purpose. Chantonnit is not a central figure in the novel's political drama, but his presence at its periphery — the Swiss-born mayor of a French border town — reminds the reader that the seemingly self-contained world of Besançon is in fact permeable, connected by personal histories to places and political traditions beyond the French frontier.

## Significance

The reference to Neuchâtel participates in the novel's broader pattern of situating Besançon within a European geography. The proximity of the Swiss frontier — Neuchâtel lies just across the border from Franche-Comté — means that Swiss figures like Chantonnit can cross into French political life, bringing with them different allegiances and ambitions. This geographical permeability mirrors the social permeability that is one of Balzac's recurring themes.

The novel's geographical references trace a pattern of expanding concentric circles: from the narrow confines of the Rue du Perron and the Watteville salon, outward to Besançon, to Franche-Comté, to the Franco-Swiss frontier at Neuchâtel, and ultimately to the great European capitals — [[paris|Paris]], [[london|London]], [[florence|Florence]] — that frame the embedded novella. Neuchâtel occupies the intermediate ring in this geography, the point at which the provincial world begins to open onto the European stage.
