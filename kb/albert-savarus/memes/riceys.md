# Riceys

**Riceys** is a small agricultural commune in Franche-Comté, situated on the far side of the [[dent-de-vilard|Dent de Vilard]] from the Watteville estate of [[les-rouxey|les Rouxey]]. For a century it maintained an informal grazing arrangement with the Watteville family, until the July Revolution of 1830 emboldened its republican mayor to claim the entire mountain for his district, setting in motion the legal dispute that Rosalie de Watteville exploits to engage the services of [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]].

## Overview

Riceys appears in the novel not as a setting but as a political entity — an opposing party in a lawsuit. It represents the post-1830 democratic spirit pressing against aristocratic privilege, and serves Balzac's need for a plot mechanism that draws the lawyer Savaron de Savarus into the Watteville family's orbit.

## The Boundary Dispute

The dispute between Riceys and the Watteville family is explained by Modinier, the Baron's steward, during a boat excursion on the Rouxey lake:

> "Mademoiselle, the people of Riceys," said Modinier, "have long enjoyed the right of grazing and cutting fodder on their side of the Dent de Vilard. Now Monsieur Chantonnit, the Maire since 1830, declares that the whole Dent belongs to his district, and maintains that a hundred years ago, or more, there was a way through our grounds."
> — (ch15-section-15)

For a century, the Dent de Vilard had served as an informal boundary without conflict. It "stood as a sort of party wall between the communes of Riceys and les Rouxey, yielding little profit" (ch15-section-15). The mountain, covered in snow for six months of the year, was of little economic value — too poor a prize to justify open conflict.

## The Political Context

The July Revolution of 1830 changed everything. The political agitation swept through the provinces, and Chantonnit, as a newly empowered republican official, seized upon the ancient ambiguity to advance his career:

> It required all the hot blast by which the revolution of 1830 inflamed the advocates of the people, to stir up this matter, by which Monsieur Chantonnit, the Maire of Riceys, hoped to give a dramatic turn to his career on the peaceful frontier of Switzerland, and to immortalize his term of office.
> — (ch15-section-15)

Chantonnit, as his name shows, was a native of Neuchâtel — a Swiss transplant who had crossed the border to become a French municipal official, bringing with him the republican energies of the Swiss cantons. This biographical detail is significant: the man threatening an aristocratic French family's property rights is not even French by birth, sharpening Balzac's satire of post-revolutionary political opportunism.

## Rosalie's Response

When Rosalie learns the Mayor of Riceys is a republican, she weaponises the information. She advises her father, the [[baron-de-watteville|Baron de Watteville]], to retain the celebrated lawyer Savaron before Chantonnit can engage him for the village's side:

> "Why should you be afraid? Get the famous lawyer Savaron — engage him at once, lest Chantonnit should place the interests of the village in his hands."
> — (ch15-section-15)

This advice, while ostensibly protecting Watteville interests, is in truth Rosalie's first move in a calculated campaign to bring Albert Savarus within her reach.

## Resolution

The lawsuit is resolved offstage. By the time the Baron reports the outcome, the affair has moved from the courts to a cash settlement:

> "The Court has settled our quarrel with the authorities of Riceys by fixing the boundary line at three hundred feet up the side of the Dent de Vilard. I am having a trench made to collect all the water and carry it into the lake. The village did not appeal, so the decision is final."
> — (ch19-section-19)

Madame de Watteville supplies the missing explanation for why Riceys chose not to appeal: "This decision cost me thirty thousand francs handed over to Chantonnit. That peasant would take nothing else; he sold us peace" (ch19-section-19). The bribe reveals the transactional logic beneath provincial justice. Chantonnit, who launched the suit to "immortalize his term of office," settles for cash — the revolutionary rhetoric of 1830 dissolves into a personal payoff. Riceys as a community gains nothing from the resolution; only its mayor profits.

The trench the Baron digs to redirect water into the Rouxey lake is a small act of physical appropriation — having won the boundary at law, he immediately reshapes the mountain's hydrology to his estate's advantage.

## Significance

Riceys is more than a village name — it is the political counterweight that makes the [[rouxey-lawsuit|lawsuit]] possible. Without Chantonnit's republican ambition, the century-old informal arrangement would have continued undisturbed, and Rosalie would have had no pretext for engaging Albert Savarus. Riceys thus represents the collision between old aristocratic France (the Wattevilles of les Rouxey) and the new democratic order (the republican municipality) — a collision that Balzac uses to set his personal tragedy in motion.

The village's quiet exit from the narrative — bought off for thirty thousand francs — mirrors the fate of republican principle in Balzac's fictional world: always, in the end, subordinate to money.
