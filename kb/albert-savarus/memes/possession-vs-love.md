# Possession versus Love

The distinction between love and the desire to possess is one of the most painful themes in Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842). The novel interrogates the boundary between genuine affection and the will to control, most starkly through the figure of [[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie de Watteville]], whose "love" for [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]] manifests entirely as an urge to dominate and destroy.

## Rosalie's Possessive Desire

Rosalie's feeling for Albert is described in the language of love, but its expression is indistinguishable from the will to possess. She does not seek Albert's happiness; she seeks Albert himself, and when she cannot have him, she ensures that no one else can either. Her actions — intercepting his letters, sabotaging his career, destroying his relationship with [[francesca|Francesca Colonna]] — are the acts of a proprietor, not a lover.

The narrator makes this distinction explicit through Rosalie's own words. After destroying Albert, she acknowledges herself as "the only sufferer" — a statement that captures the self-regarding nature of possessive desire. Her suffering is real, but it is suffering over the loss of something she wanted to own, not grief for the pain she has caused another person.

## Albert's Romantic Love

In contrast to Rosalie's possessive desire, Albert's love for Francesca represents a more idealized, romantic conception of love. His devotion to her memory, his literary sublimation of their relationship in the story-within-the-story, and his ultimate retreat from the world after losing her all suggest a love that is oriented toward the beloved rather than the self.

Yet Balzac complicates this contrast. Albert's love for Francesca, however genuine, is also marked by a certain passivity. He does not fight for her; he writes about her. His literary production — the autobiographical story that Rosalie reads — becomes a substitute for action, a way of possessing Francesca in art that he cannot achieve in life.

## Love as Destruction

The novel's darkest insight is that love, in its possessive form, is indistinguishable from the will to destroy. Rosalie "loves" Albert, and therefore she ruins him. This paradox structures the novel's entire plot: Rosalie's surveillance, interception, and revelation are all acts of love as she understands it.

> She was the only sufferer.
> — (ch08-section-2)

Rosalie's self-characterization reveals the solipsism at the heart of possessive desire. The suffering of others — Albert's destroyed career, Francesca's betrayed trust, the collateral damage to the Watteville salon — does not register. Only her own pain is real to her.

## The Social Dimension

The possessiveness that Rosalie exhibits is shaped by the social conditions of provincial [[besancon|Besançon]]. In a world where women's agency is severely limited, the desire to possess a man becomes one of the few forms of power available. Rosalie cannot pursue a career, travel, or shape her own destiny directly; she can only control others. Her possessive love for Albert is, in this sense, a deformed expression of a legitimate hunger for agency.

## Literary Context

The tension between love and possession runs throughout Balzac's [[la-comedie-humaine|Comédie Humaine]]. Characters across the cycle confuse ownership with affection, treating romantic partners as objects to be acquired rather than persons to be loved. *Albert Savarus* gives this theme one of its most concentrated treatments, embedding the love-possession distinction in the novel's central conflict.
