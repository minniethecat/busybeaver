# Monsieur de Vauchelles

**Monsieur de Vauchelles** is a young Besançon nobleman who forms one third of an inseparable trio with young [[chavoncourt|Chavoncourt]] and [[amedee-de-soulas|Amédée de Soulas]]. His matrimonial ambitions center on Victoire de Chavoncourt, and his political allegiance shifts to Albert Savarus during the election — a pivot that proves decisive.

## Overview

Like Amédée de Soulas, Vauchelles is a young man of good family but limited means. His fortune, or lack of it, shapes his social calculus: he is "no richer than Amedee" and his path to financial security runs through marriage to Victoire, the eldest Chavoncourt daughter and god-daughter of a wealthy aunt.

> Monsieur de Vauchelles had his matrimonial plans, as Amedee had his; he wished to marry Victoire, the eldest of the Chavoncourts, on whom an old aunt was to settle an estate worth seven thousand francs a year, and a hundred thousand francs in hard cash, when the contract was to be signed. — [001-711]

This dependence on the Chavoncourt family's fortunes makes Vauchelles a natural informant — Rosalie correctly calculates that any secret shared with Amédée will reach Vauchelles, and through Vauchelles, Monsieur de Chavoncourt. The "three young men had no secrets from each other," and they were "so well known to be inseparable that they were invited to the country together."

## Role in the Novel

Vauchelles's role is primarily structural: he completes the triangle of young Besançon manhood and provides the narrative with a mechanism for information flow between the various social circles. His engagement to Victoire de Chavoncourt ties the Vauchelles name to the Chavoncourt political fortunes.

During the election, Vauchelles and Amédée are credited with winning over the young men of the aristocracy:

> Amédée de Soulas and Vauchelles, who will both vote for you, have won over the young men. — [001-311]

This is significant, because Vauchelles's engagement to Victoire should, by all normal calculations, make him a Chavoncourt loyalist. His defection to the Savarus camp signals the depth of Rosalie's behind-the-scenes manipulation — she has managed to turn even the future son-in-law of the opposing candidate.

The electoral arithmetic acknowledges this: Vauchelles's vote, along with the Boucher committee's twenty, gives Savarus a decisive margin.

## Relationships

- **Victoire de Chavoncourt** — His intended bride; eldest daughter of the Chavoncourt family, with a promised settlement of seven thousand francs a year from her aunt.
- **Young [[chavoncourt|Chavoncourt]]** — His closest friend; the two make excursions to Granvelle and go shooting together.
- **[[amedee-de-soulas|Amédée de Soulas]]** — The third member of the inseparable trio; both are suitors with financial calculations.
- **[[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]]** — His vote and influence help secure Savarus's electoral victory.
- **[[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie de Watteville]]** — She exploits the young men's lack of secrecy to manipulate election information flows.

## Key Appearances

Vauchelles appears primarily in the novel's social and electoral sequences. He is present at the climactic dinner party that seals the election: the gathering includes "the two Chavoncourt girls, and Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbe de Grancey, Rosalie, Amedee de Soulas, and a retired magistrate."

## Significance

Vauchelles is a minor but structurally significant character. His engagement to Victoire de Chavoncourt illustrates the marriage economy of provincial nobility: a young man without fortune marries a young woman whose aunt provides the settlement. His vote for Savarus demonstrates how thoroughly Rosalie's schemes have penetrated every layer of Besançon society, turning even family loyalties into instruments of her design.
