# Tinti

Tinti is a celebrated singer who appears in Balzac's _Albert Savarus_
at a crucial social gathering at Princess Gandolphini's villa near
Geneva.

## Role in the Novel

Tinti performs at the Princess's villa during the musical evening where
Rodolphe (Albert Savarus) is formally introduced to Francesca Colonna
and her husband, Prince Colonna. She takes part in the famous quartette
_Mi manca la voce_, singing alongside Tinti and the Princess herself[^1].

The musical performance provides the elegant backdrop against which the
novel's central romantic relationship is formally re-established —
Rodolphe and Francesca, who had known each other in humbler circumstances
at Gersau and Bergmann's lodgings, now meet in the full splendor of
aristocratic society.

## Narrative Function

As a minor character, Tinti serves primarily to establish the
sophistication and cultural refinement of the Gandolphini circle.
Her presence signals that this is a world of art, music, and
cultivated taste — the world that the exiled Rodolphe aspires to
re-enter through political and professional achievement.
