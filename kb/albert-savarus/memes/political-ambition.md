# Political Ambition in Restoration France

**Political ambition** in Balzac's *Albert Savarus* is the force that drives the protagonist from Paris to Besançon and, ultimately, toward his destruction. Albert Savarus pursues a seat in the Chamber of Deputies not for its own sake but as the necessary precondition for winning Francesca Soderini — making political ambition the novel's central engine of plot and tragedy alike.

## Overview

The Bourbon Restoration created a distinctive political landscape: a constitutional monarchy with a restricted franchise, where power was contested between Ultra-Royalists (Legitimists) and constitutional liberals. The Chamber of Deputies was the arena where these forces clashed, and a parliamentary seat represented the summit of ambition for a man of talent without inherited wealth. For Savarus, politics is not a career choice but a necessity — the only ladder high enough to reach the woman he loves.

In the novel's embedded novella, "Ambition for Love's Sake," Savarus narrates his political strategy with the precision of a military campaign. He calculates every move — where to settle, whom to befriend, which cases to take — in terms of electoral arithmetic.

## Love as the Engine of Ambition

What distinguishes Savarus's ambition from ordinary political climbing is its origin in love. He is "possessed by an ambition sanctified by his love." Every political calculation is ultimately a romantic one — the Chamber seat is valuable because it will permit him to "marry a Princess Soderini." This fusion of public ambition and private desire is Balzac's characteristic insight: in the Restoration world, the political and the intimate are never separate spheres.

The tragedy of the novel lies in the fact that political ambition, which was meant to enable love, becomes the instrument of its destruction. Rosalie's interception of Savarus's letters — and her forgery of a reply — breaks the delicate machinery by which ambition and affection were meant to converge. The very political career Savarus sought to build for love's sake collapses because the province's surveillance apparatus has penetrated the secrecy on which that career depended.

Savarus himself frames his political campaign as his "last card in the game of ambition," comparing himself to a desperate gambler:

> "Oh! my dear Leopold, no gambler with the last remains of his fortune in his pocket, bent on staking it at the Cercle des Etrangers for the last time one night, when he must come away rich or ruined, ever felt such a perpetual ringing in his ears, such a nervous moisture on his palms, such a fevered tumult in his brain."

The gambling metaphor is significant: political ambition is figured not as rational calculation but as a wager in which everything — love, identity, life itself — is at stake.

## Savarus's Political Calculus

Albert Savarus arrives in Besançon with methodical intent. He registers "as a resident with all political qualifications" immediately upon arrival, and every subsequent action — his legal work for the Chapter, his cultivation of the Abbé de Grancey, his careful self-presentation in society — serves his electoral strategy. He explains his reasoning with remarkable candor:

> "The people of the Comte will not meet the outsider? The outsider will meet them! They refuse to admit him to their drawing-rooms, he will never go there! He never shows himself anywhere, not even in the streets! But there is one class that elects the deputies — the commercial class."

This passage reveals two key features of Savarus's political thinking: first, his willingness to accept social isolation as the price of political strategy; and second, his precise targeting of the commercial class as the electoral constituency that matters. He has studied the political sociology of Besançon before arriving and has a plan tailored to its specific conditions.

His theatrical invisibility — working only in the early mornings, dining alone, never appearing in society — is itself a form of political theater. By refusing the social obligations of provincial life, he creates an aura of mystery that enhances his professional reputation.

The tone of his letters reveals ambition as a form of suffering. He confesses to "crushed ambitions, but never giving them up," to wandering "gloomy and hurt, through the lonely places of Paris." Political ambition in the novel is not glamorous; it is a grinding, uncertain labor that consumes years and exacts a psychological toll.

## The Electoral Campaign: A Step-by-Step Strategy

Savarus's campaign for the Chamber of Deputies follows a deliberate sequence, which he outlines in his letter to Leopold Hannequin. The strategy can be mapped as a series of calculated moves:

| Phase | Action | Political Purpose |
|-------|--------|-------------------|
| 1. Installation | Register as resident with political qualifications | Legal eligibility for candidacy |
| 2. Professional Reputation | Win high-profile cases (criminal defense, merchant suit, Chapter litigation) | Build name recognition and client network |
| 3. Commercial Specialization | Accept only commercial cases, stating "commercial questions are my specialty" | Align with the commercial electorate that elects deputies |
| 4. Clerical Alliance | Defend the Cathedral Chapter's case without fee, in exchange for votes | Secure the Legitimist bloc through the Abbé de Grancey |
| 5. Property Qualification | Purchase a house in Besançon with borrowed capital | Satisfy the property qualification for candidacy |
| 6. The Review | Plan to launch a periodical defending "the interests of the country" | Shape public opinion and establish political voice |
| 7. The Unveiling | Allow "some circumstance" to bring his name forward | Emerge as candidate without appearing to seek office |

Each step is chosen to convert a liability — his status as an outsider — into an advantage. His invisibility in society prevents rivals from gathering damaging information; his specialization in commercial law makes him indispensable to the voters who matter.

## The Vicar-General Alliance

The most politically nuanced episode in Savarus's campaign is his negotiation with the Abbé de Grancey, the Vicar-General of Besançon. When Savarus agrees to defend the Cathedral Chapter's case without a fee, he makes the transaction explicit:

> "'Monsieur,' said I, 'I will win your suit, but I want no fee; I want more... I came here only to leave the place as deputy. I mean to engage only in commercial cases, because commercial men return the members; they will distrust me if I defend 'the priests' — for to them you are simply priests.'"

This negotiation demonstrates Savarus's political sophistication. He understands that the Legitimist clergy and the commercial bourgeoisie are natural antagonists, and that openly aligning with one would cost him the other. His solution — free legal service to the Church in exchange for votes delivered secretly — is an attempt to have both constituencies while appearing beholden to neither.

The Vicar-General's reply — slipping him five hundred francs and murmuring "The votes are a bargain all the same" — confirms that in Restoration politics, even the Church understood elections as a marketplace.

## The Election and Its Aftermath

Despite his meticulous planning, Savarus's electoral success ultimately proves hollow. He wins the seat — but loses Francesca. The intercepted correspondence, forged by Rosalie, convinces Francesca that Savarus has married another, and she marries the elderly Duc d'Argaiolo instead. When Savarus learns what has happened, the political victory that was meant to enable love becomes, instead, the monument to its loss.

Balzac's treatment of political ambition here is characteristically unsentimental: the machinery of ambition is so elaborate, so dependent on secrecy and delay, that it is fatally vulnerable to the very provincial surveillance it was designed to circumvent. The province, with its "rival ambitions" and its intimate knowledge of everyone's affairs, proves to be a surveillance system more thorough than any political police.
