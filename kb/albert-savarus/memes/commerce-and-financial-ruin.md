# Commerce and Financial Ruin

Commerce and the threat of financial ruin form an undercurrent in Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842). The novel depicts a society in which fortune — its acquisition, preservation, and loss — determines social standing, marital prospects, and political viability. From the Wattevilles' carefully husbanded estate to Albert's disastrous speculation, economic forces shape the characters' fates as decisively as romantic passion.

## The Watteville Fortune

The Watteville family's wealth exemplifies the conservative provincial approach to fortune. Madame de Watteville's fortune was "a fine one," while her husband's "consisted only of the estate of Rouxey, worth about ten thousand francs a year" and "was not increased by inheritance" (s01). The Rouxey estate, with its contested boundaries, becomes the legal dispute that draws Albert Savarus into the Wattevilles' orbit.

Despite an income of fifty thousand francs a year, the Wattevilles "did not spend more than four-and-twenty thousand francs" (s01). This frugality — saving over half their income — represents the prudence of an old aristocratic family adapting to the post-Revolutionary economic order. Rosalie's "enormous prospective fortune" gives her "considerable importance" (s01) in Besançon society, making her one of the most eligible young women in the province.

## Commerce as Social Mobility

Besançon's commercial class provides Albert Savarus with his professional foundation. After his successful defense of a peasant at the Assizes, "one of these, a merchant, placed a difficult case next day in the hands of Monsieur Savaron, and he won it" (s03). He gains as clients "four of the chief merchants of the place, who discerned in him so much good sense and sound legal purview that they placed their claims in his hands" (s03).

Albert leverages these commercial connections to launch the *Eastern Review*, "with the help of forty shares of five hundred francs each, taken up by his first ten clients" (s03). The merchants believe in his vision of Besançon as a regional commercial hub — "the town where the traffic should meet between Mulhouse and Lyons" (s03). Albert's political ideas, "put forward by Albert, were repeated by the ten merchants, who believed them to be their own" (s03). Commerce thus serves as the vehicle for Albert's political ambitions.

## Albert's Financial Catastrophe

The novel's most dramatic economic event occurs in the embedded novella of Rodolphe's past. After rebuilding his career in Paris, Rodolphe "longed to make his fortune, and risked his all in stock-exchange speculations" (s08). The outcome is devastating: "The Villèle ministry fell just when Rodolphe was ruined" (s08). The fall of the Villèle ministry in 1827, combined with his speculative losses, destroys in a single stroke both his political prospects and his financial security.

"The valiant combatant" then "began again to construct his fortune" (s08), rebuilding through his legal talents. This sequence — ambition, speculation, ruin, and reconstruction — mirrors the cyclical nature of Restoration-era finance, where political instability and speculative markets could make and break fortunes with equal speed.

## The Economics of Provincial Life

Balzac meticulously documents the economics of daily life in Besançon. Amedée de Soulas's two horses, "treated with sordid economy, cost, one with another, eight hundred francs a year" (s02). His "bills for articles received from Paris, such as perfumery, cravats, jewelry, patent blacking, and clothes" (s02) represent the hidden costs of maintaining a fashionable appearance in the provinces. The dandy's "six francs spent in polish" for boots and "yellow gloves at fifty sous a pair" (s02) reveal the precise arithmetic of social display.

The commercial world's enthusiasm for the *Eastern Review* — "enchanted at the success" (s03) — and the merchant's son Alfred Boucher who "fancied he was making capital out of the great man" (s03) show how economic and intellectual capital intertwine in provincial society.

## Ruin and Redemption

Financial ruin in *Albert Savarus* is never purely economic; it is always also moral and emotional. Rodolphe's ruin on the stock exchange precipitates his retreat from Paris to Besançon and his adoption of a new identity. The threat of ruin haunts Albert's political campaign — his enemies could expose not only his romantic history but also his financial past. The novel suggests that in Restoration France, a man's credit and his character are inseparable, and the loss of one inevitably taints the other.
