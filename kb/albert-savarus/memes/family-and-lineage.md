# Family and Lineage in _Albert Savarus_

The family is the fundamental social unit of Balzac's fictional world, and _Albert Savarus_ is no exception. The Watteville household — with its domineering baroness, its passive baron, and its hidden daughter Rosalie — is a microcosm of Restoration society. Family relationships determine marriage prospects, social standing, and the transmission of wealth and status. This page aggregates all entries connected to the novel's portrayal of family, lineage, and domestic life.

## Notes

Family in _Albert Savarus_ is both shelter and prison. The Watteville household protects Rosalie from the world but also isolates her, shaping her into the calculating manipulator she becomes. Parental authority — particularly the baroness's control over her daughter — is presented as a force that warps rather than guides. Balzac shows how the family, supposedly a haven of natural affection, is in practice a site of power, surveillance, and strategic calculation. See also [[love-and-marriage]] for the romantic dimension of marriage, and [[social-ambition]] for how family position enables or constrains social climbing.
