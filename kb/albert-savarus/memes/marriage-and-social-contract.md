# Marriage and Social Contract

**Marriage** in *Albert Savarus* functions not as the culmination of romantic love but as a social and political contract — a transaction through which families consolidate wealth, secure alliances, and manage inheritance. Balzac depicts marriage as an institution governed by strategic calculation rather than affection, reflecting the material realities of provincial aristocracy under the Restoration.

The novel opens with a marriage that exemplifies this logic: that of Monsieur and Madame de Watteville, "arranged in 1802, solemnized in 1815 after the second Restoration" [002-Section-2]. The union, engineered to combine the Rupt family inheritance with the Watteville name, is presented as a purely instrumental arrangement. Madame de Watteville, "devout as a girl, became even more so after her marriage" — piety serving as refuge from an emotionally hollow union with a husband described as "a dry, lean man devoid of intelligence" [002-Section-2].

## The Watteville–Rupt Alliance: A Template Marriage

The marriage of the Baron and Baronne de Watteville is the novel's archetypal Restoration union. The Baron married "the heiress of the celebrated house of Rupt," who "brought twenty thousand francs a year in the funds" [002-Section-2]. The thirteen-year gap between the arrangement (1802) and the solemnization (1815) reveals the institutional patience required by strategic matrimony: marriage was not a personal decision to be consummated at will but a political instrument to be deployed at the moment of maximum advantage — in this case, after the Bourbon Restoration secured the social order for which the alliance was designed.

The resulting household is a study in the emotional costs of marriage-as-contract. The Baron, "a little man of no mind," contributes his name; the Baronne contributes her fortune and her piety [002-Section-2]. Neither contributes affection. The Baron's sole assertion of domestic authority — his collection of natural history specimens and his habit of smoking — is described as a form of retreat: he "filled his study with smoke, and his collection with insects" [002-Section-2]. The marriage produces Rosalie, but it produces no visible warmth.

## Matrimonial Calculations: The Dowry System

The novel's matrimonial economy revolves around the dowry — a public calculation in which every figure is known and debated:

| Asset | Estimated Value | Significance |
|-------|----------------|-------------|
| Watteville estate (Les Rouxey) | ~10,000 francs/year | The ancestral land, the Baron's contribution |
| Rupt inheritance | ~10,000 francs/year | The Baronne's contribution, from "the celebrated house of Rupt" [002-Section-2] |
| Total dowry | ~20,000 francs/year | What makes Rosalie "the greatest heiress in Besançon" [002-Section-2] |
| de Soulas estate | ~15,000 francs/year | What Amédée must prove unencumbered to be considered a viable suitor [002-Section-2] |

These figures are public knowledge — discussed in salons, calculated over whist tables, factored into every social transaction. The dowry is not a private family matter but a form of social discourse in which the entire provincial community participates. When Rosalie's father reveals that a subscription to the *Revue de l'Est* is sent to "a Milanese lady at her country house at Belgirate," the information carries matrimonial weight: it suggests that Albert Savarus, the rising lawyer, may already have attachments that would preclude a Watteville alliance [011-Section-11].

## Marriage as Political Instrument

The novel's most explicit marriage-as-politics is the proposed union between Albert Savarus and Sidonie de Chavoncourt. This match is negotiated not between the principals but between political intermediaries — the Vicar-General and Albert's allies — as an instrument to advance Albert's parliamentary candidacy. Sidonie is described less as a person than as a strategic asset: her family connections, her social standing, her capacity to deliver votes [012-Section-12]. Albert treats the negotiation with detachment, viewing marriage as a necessary step in a political career rather than a personal commitment.

The marriage that concludes the novel — "the Duc de Rhétoré, eldest son of the Duc de Chaulieu, to Mademoiselle de Watteville" — is the apotheosis of marriage as political instrument [020-Section-20]. It unites two great families, consolidates influence, and is celebrated by Albert's own political party as a triumph of strategy over sentiment. That it is also the mechanism through which Rosalie secures her escape from Besançon and her entry into Parisian society only reinforces the theme: marriage is the institution through which personal ambition is channeled into social form.

## The Servants' Alternative: Love at the Margins

The marriage of Mariette and Jérôme, the servants who marry after the novel's resolution, provides the counter-case [020-Section-20]. Their union is presented as genuinely affectionate — a marriage of inclination rather than calculation. Tellingly, it exists at the social margins, beyond the calculations that govern aristocratic alliance. The servants can marry for love precisely because they have no fortunes to consolidate, no lineages to extend. Balzac's juxtaposition of the grand political marriages with this modest domestic union is not sentimental but structural: it demonstrates that the regime of marriage-as-contract is a function of class position, not human nature.
