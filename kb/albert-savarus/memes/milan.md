# Milan

Milan, the capital of Lombardy in northern Italy, appears in Balzac's
*Albert Savarus* primarily as a city of origin and exile. It is the
native city of Francesca Soderini (later Duchessa d'Argaiolo), the seat
of the noble Soderini family, and the home of the revolutionary
bookseller Lamporani, Francesca's husband and "one of the leaders of the
revolution" against Austrian rule [007-Section-7]. Milan functions as an
offstage presence — a city that defines identity, politics, and social
standing without ever becoming the scene of direct narrative action.

## Overview

Milan enters the novel through the figure of Lamporani, "the famous
bookseller Lamporani of Milan, one of the leaders of the revolution,
and the conspirator of all others whom Austria would most like to have
in the Spielberg" [007-Section-7]. Lamporani's revolutionary activities
are the reason for his exile in Switzerland, where he travels under
the guise of a modest bookseller while in reality orchestrating
political conspiracies. His Milanese identity is inseparable from his
revolutionary character.

Francesca herself is Milanese by birth (née Princess Soderini), and
Rodolphe's inquiries at Geneva are framed around this geographical
identity: he asks his landlord whether "some Italian refugees from Milan
had not lately come to reside at Geneva" [009-Section-9]. The landlord's
reply — "they are not Milanese" [009-Section-9], referring to recent
arrivals — underscores the specificity of Milanese identity in the
novel's social taxonomy.

## Description in the Novel

Milan is evoked through its social customs and class markers. When
Rodolphe questions whether his own humble birth is a barrier to
Francesca's love, she responds with a defense of Milan's commercial
nobility: "At Milan the noblest names are written over shop-doors:
Sforza, Canova, Visconti, Trivulzio" [008-Section-8]. This detail
characterizes Milan as a city where commerce and aristocracy intermingle,
unlike the more rigidly stratified society of Restoration France.

Francesca's letters from Belgirate reveal a darker view of the city.
She writes to Rodolphe: "No, dearest, do not go to Milan; stay at
Belgirate. Milan terrifies me. I do not like that odious Milanese
fashion of chatting at the Scala every evening with a dozen persons,
among whom sweet things are spoken to one but too often addressed to
all" [013-Section-13]. The Scala opera house emerges as the site of
Milan's social life, a space of flirtation and gossip that Francesca
finds threatening to the exclusivity of her love.

## Significance

Milan embodies the tension between political commitment and romantic
retreat. Lamporani's Milan is a city of revolution, conspiracy, and
sacrifice — the world Francesca inhabits as the wife of a political
exile. But her letters from Belgirate express a longing to keep Rodolphe
away from Milan's social whirl, preserving their love in the pastoral
seclusion of the lakeside villa. The city thus represents the competing
claims of public life and private passion that run throughout the novel.

The Milanese context also establishes Francesca's social credentials.
She is not merely a bookseller's wife — she is a Soderini, a princess
of Milan, whose family name is woven into the fabric of the city's
history. This duality (revolutionary husband, aristocratic wife) is
central to her character and to the novel's exploration of identity
and disguise.
