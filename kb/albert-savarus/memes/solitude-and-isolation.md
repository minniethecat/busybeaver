# Solitude and Isolation

Solitude and isolation form a pervasive thematic complex in Balzac's *Albert Savarus*, operating at multiple levels: geographical, social, emotional, and existential. The novel's characters are defined as much by what separates them from others as by what connects them, and the narrative trajectory moves steadily toward ever-deepening isolation.

## Overview

Besançon is presented from the novel's opening as a city cut off from the great currents of French life. Surrounded by mountains, distant from Paris, and dominated by its own insular aristocracy, the provincial capital is a world unto itself — a condition Balzac treats as both constriction and crucible. The narrator observes that in such a setting, "fired by a word, a look, or gesture, are started those feuds between house and house, between a woman of rank and a citizen's wife, which endure till death, and widen the impassable gulf which parts the two classes of society" (s01).

This geographical isolation intensifies every social interaction. Because there is no escape to Paris, no wider stage on which to play out one's ambitions, every slight, every alliance, every romantic possibility acquires an exaggerated significance. The closed world of Besançon is the condition that makes Rosalie's obsessive love possible: she has seen too little of the world to measure her passion against any standard but its own intensity.

## Relevance to the Novel

### Solitude as a Provincial Condition

Balzac presents solitude and isolation as inseparable from the provincial condition. The distance from Paris, the closed social circles, the obsessive attention to local minutiae — all contribute to a world in which individuals are simultaneously pressed too closely together and kept fundamentally apart. The novel's genius lies in its recognition that these two conditions are not opposed but complementary: it is precisely the impossibility of genuine privacy in Besançon that makes true connection impossible, and it is the failure of connection that drives each character into a solitude that is all the more painful for being visible to everyone.

### The Watteville Marriage: Isolation Within Marriage

The marriage of the Baron and Baronne de Watteville exemplifies isolation within intimacy. The Baron, a passive and ineffectual figure, has been effectively exiled from his own household by his wife's stronger will. His hobby of wood-turning — a solitary craft performed in a workshop separate from the main house — is a figure for his position in the family: present but irrelevant, engaged in a meaningless activity that occupies his hands while his domestic authority is turned to shavings.

The Baroness, for her part, is isolated by her piety and her rigid adherence to the social codes of the Legitimist aristocracy. She participates in the rituals of Besançon society without being genuinely connected to it, her true energies directed toward the consolidation of her family's declining fortunes and the control of her daughter's future.

### Albert Savarus: The Solitude of the Stranger

Albert Savarus arrives in Besançon as a stranger — "no one knows where he comes from," as Madame de Chavoncourt notes (s01) — and he remains one throughout his time in the city. His professional brilliance, his mysterious past, and his evident superiority to his provincial surroundings all mark him as an outsider. He cultivates this solitude deliberately, living on the Rue du Perron in a house whose garden adjoins the Watteville property, working alone through long hours, and keeping his own counsel.

Monsieur de Soulas's surveillance of Albert's daily routine reveals the extent of his isolation: "He gets up at five, writes till eight, has his breakfast, and then goes on working. He walks in his garden, going round fifty, or perhaps sixty times; then he goes in, dines, and goes to bed between six and seven" (s03). This monkish discipline — rising before dawn, working alone, walking in circles in a walled garden — prefigures the Carthusian cell that will become his final home.

Albert's solitude is both chosen and imposed. He has chosen to hide his past, but the necessity of that concealment isolates him from the society on which his professional and political ambitions depend. His heart belongs to Francesca, a woman thousands of miles away whom he may never see again, and this devotion makes every social interaction in Besançon a performance rather than a genuine connection.

### Rosalie de Watteville: The Solitude of the Watcher

Rosalie's isolation is of a different kind. Unlike Albert, who chose his solitude, Rosalie is born into hers. As the only child of elderly parents in a household governed by her mother's rigid piety, she has no companions her own age with whom she can share her inner life. Her world before Albert's arrival is circumscribed by the Hotel de Rupt, the convent school, and the limited social circle of her mother's Tuesday receptions.

The arrival of Albert transforms Rosalie's solitude from a passive condition into an active practice of watching. Balzac depicts her at the window, observing Albert in his garden, her entire consciousness absorbed in the figure of a man who does not know she exists. When, after Albert's departure and her father's death, Rosalie retires to Les Rouxey, her isolation becomes complete: "Mademoiselle de Watteville, who lived alone on her estate of les Rouxey, riding, hunting, refusing two or three offers a year, going to Besançon for four or five months in the winter" (s21). She has achieved precisely the solitude Albert sought — but as a prison rather than a refuge.

### The Carthusian Solution

The novel's conclusion — Albert's entry into the Grande Chartreuse — represents the institutionalization of solitude. The Carthusian order, founded by Saint Bruno in 1084, is the most austere of Catholic monastic communities: monks live in individual cells, eat alone, and observe a rule of silence broken only by communal liturgy and weekly walks. Albert's choice of this order is not a rejection of solitude but its embrace as a permanent condition.

His final letter describes the Carthusian life in terms that fuse religious vocation with the exhaustion of worldly desire: "I could not serve in the ranks of the Church Militant, and I lay the remains of an almost extinct life at the foot of the altar. This is the last time I shall ever write. You alone, who loved me, and whom I loved so well, could make me break the law of oblivion I imposed on myself" (s21). Solitude here is not merely physical separation but a deliberate self-erasure — an "oblivion" that extinguishes the very memory of the passions that drove him.
