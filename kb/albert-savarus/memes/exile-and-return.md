# Exile and Return

The narrative of Balzac's *Albert Savarus* is structured around the figure of exile and the impossibility of a clean return. The protagonist arrives in Besançon as a stranger — "a Swiss gentleman" whose "coat-of-arms" marks him as an outsider in a city where identity is determined by lineage and long residence. His self-imposed removal from Paris, his legal practice in a provincial city where "no stranger, no intruder, ever finds his way," and his eventual flight from Besançon after Rosalie's machinations all trace a pattern of displacement that gives the novel its distinctive emotional tonality: a condition of permanent exile punctuated by failed attempts at return.

## The Stranger in Besançon

Albert Savarus's arrival is immediately marked as anomalous. "If he is a stranger, by what chance has he settled at Besançon? It is a strange fancy for a barrister" — the Baroness's question captures the suspicion with which provincial society greets any outsider who cannot be placed within its existing networks. The narrator repeatedly emphasizes the difficulty of Albert's position: "how little chance a stranger has of succeeding here. The good folks of Besançon will make use of him, but they will not allow him to make use of them." The stranger is a resource to be exploited, never a member to be absorbed.

## The Swiss Identity

Albert's Swiss origin — the Wattevilles "are Swiss" — functions as a permanent mark of foreignness. The narrator notes that "Rub a Swiss ever so little, and you find the usurer," deploying an ethnic stereotype that Albert's professional success does nothing to dispel. His foreignness is doubled: he is both the outsider from Paris (a city of dangerous modernity) and the outsider from Switzerland (a nation of republican independence). Neither identity is compatible with the legitimist-aristocratic world of the Besançon salons.

## The Embedded Story: Rodolphe's Wanderings

The middle section of the novel — Rodolphe's first-person account of his love for Francesca — is itself a narrative of geographical and emotional wandering. The young man traverses the Swiss landscape, moving through "the lake" at Geneva, "the garden" at the Villa Bergmann, and the mountains of the Jura, always in pursuit of a woman who remains elusive. The physical journey mirrors the psychological one: exile is not simply the condition of being away from home, but of being away from oneself.

## The Catastrophic Return

The novel's climax turns on Rosalie's interception of Albert's correspondence — an act that triggers his departure from Besançon. His eventual return is not a homecoming but a catastrophe: the woman he loved has become a nun, his political ambitions have been thwarted, and the city that once promised a new beginning now offers only the evidence of his defeat. Balzac suggests that return is always a form of reckoning — a confrontation with the version of oneself that was left behind, and that can never be fully recovered.
