# Monsieur Chantonnit

**Monsieur Chantonnit** is the mayor of Riceys, a commune on the Swiss frontier, whose aggressive land claim against the Watteville family's estate at Les Rouxey sets in motion the legal case that brings [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]] into the Wattevilles' orbit. A native of Neuchâtel (as his name suggests), Chantonnit represents the post-1830 provincial official who uses legal disputes to manufacture a political reputation.

## Overview

Chantonnit appears in the narrative only through the discussion of others, yet his shadow looms large: his claim over the Dent de Vilard — a disputed mountain ridge between the communes of Riceys and Les Rouxey — is the proximate cause of the Watteville family hiring Savarus. In this sense, Chantonnit is the catalyst who, however unwittingly, sets the entire plot in motion.

> Now Monsieur Chantonnit, the Maire since 1830, declares that the whole Dent belongs to his district, and maintains that a hundred years ago, or more, there was a way through our grounds. — [001-247]

The dispute is, by all accounts, marginal: "For a century the Dent de Vilard had been used by both parties without coming to extremities." The land in question is "covered with snow for six months in the year" and "yielding little profit." Chantonnit's motivation is not agricultural but political — he hopes "to give a dramatic turn to his career on the peaceful frontier of Switzerland, and to immortalize his term of office."

## Role in the Novel

The Dent de Vilard dispute serves as Rosalie's pretext for engineering contact with Albert Savarus. When her father frets about the boundary, it is Rosalie who supplies the solution: engage the famous lawyer Savaron, whose recent victory for the Chapter against the town of Besançon has made his reputation.

> "Why should you be afraid? Get the famous lawyer Savaron — engage him at once, lest Chantonnit should place the interests of the village in his hands." — [001-316]

The irony is layered. Rosalie's apparent concern is a cover for her romantic interest in Savarus; she has already read his novella and deduced his identity. Yet the legal threat from Chantonnit is real enough that Monsieur de Watteville takes the advice seriously, providing Rosalie with exactly the introduction she desires.

The dispute is eventually resolved — not through legal argument but through a bribe of thirty thousand francs, as Madame de Watteville later reveals:

> "It has never occurred to you," said Madame de Watteville, "that this decision cost me thirty thousand francs handed over to Chantonnit. That peasant would take nothing else; he sold us peace." — [001-700]

The bribe reveals Chantonnit's true character: his civic posturing about "the interests of the village" conceals a venal pragmatism. Thirty thousand francs is the price of his silence, and he takes it.

## Relationships

- **[[baron-de-watteville|Baron de Watteville]]** — The landowner whose estate is threatened by Chantonnit's claims.
- **[[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie de Watteville]]** — She exploits the Chantonnit dispute as a pretext to engage Savarus.
- **[[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]]** — The lawyer Rosalie recommends to counter Chantonnit's "enterprising" ambitions.
- **Modinier** — The Wattevilles' steward, who advocates a firm response to Chantonnit's encroachments.

## Key Appearances

Chantonnit never appears directly in the novel's scenes; he is a figure discussed rather than shown. His presence is felt in the Watteville family's discussions at Les Rouxey, in Rosalie's strategic recommendation, and in Madame de Watteville's bitter admission of the thirty-thousand-franc bribe.

## Significance

Chantonnit is Balzac's thumbnail sketch of the post-Revolution provincial functionary: a mayor who inflates a trivial boundary question into a career-making crusade, then settles for cash. His name — unmistakably Swiss, "a native of Neuchâtel" — marks him as an outsider in French political life, a man who understands power as transaction. His role in the plot is that of the unwitting trigger: without his land claim, Rosalie would have needed another excuse to bring Savarus to Les Rouxey. Chantonnit, the most minor of mayors, is thus the most consequential of catalysts.
