# Mother-Daughter Conflict

The relationship between Madame de Watteville and her daughter Rosalie constitutes one of the most psychologically charged dynamics in Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842). It is a relationship defined by surveillance, repression, and the slow accumulation of resentment — a domestic power struggle that ultimately explodes into Rosalie's secret campaign to destroy Albert Savarus's love for Francesca Colonna. The mother-daughter conflict functions as both character study and social commentary, illustrating how provincial aristocratic child-rearing practices produce the very rebellion they are designed to prevent.

## Madame de Watteville's Dominion

Madame de Watteville exercises near-total control over Rosalie's existence from her earliest years. As the only offspring of the Watteville union — "Rosalie was the only offspring of the union between the Wattevilles and the Rupts" — she is the sole heir to "an enormous prospective fortune" (s01), and this wealth makes her both precious and policed. The Baroness "rarely quitted the Hotel de Rupt," and Rosalie is raised "exclusively within the precincts of the Hotel de Rupt" (s01).

This physical confinement is matched by a rigid educational regime. The Baroness "taught her daughter every conceivable stitch in tapestry and women's work — plain sewing, embroidery, netting" (s01). Rosalie's days are regimented: "she went to the Cathedral every morning, taken there by her mother, came back to lunch, played in the garden in summer, spent the rest of the day sewing, was present at her mother's toilette, dined with her father and mother, and in the evening her mother allowed her silk" (s01). Even her clothing is controlled — "the cut of her frocks, made at Besançon, almost made her ugly, while her mother tried to borrow grace, fashion, and elegance by ordering her own from Paris" (s01).

## The Hidden Volcano

Beneath the surface of compliance, Rosalie harbours a spirit of extraordinary intensity. Balzac describes "an iron will hidden under an angel's brow" (s01). The education designed to produce a docile heiress instead conceals "the imaginative faculty of the Maréchal de Saxe, whose natural granddaughter she was" — a lineage that links her to a famous military commander known for boldness and audacity.

Madame de Watteville "alone, perhaps, could know of the storm that raged" (s01) beneath her daughter's placid surface. The Baroness "kept all the keener watch over her daughter, because she considered her honor as a mother to be at stake" (s01). She "tried to detect symptoms of jealousy in that youthful soul," but "labored in vain" (s01) — Rosalie's real feelings remain inaccessible to her mother's surveillance.

## The Mechanism of Rebellion

The mother-daughter conflict drives the novel's plot through a paradox: the more Madame de Watteville controls Rosalie, the more resourceful Rosalie becomes at evasion. When Rosalie falls in love with Albert Savarus — a man her mother would never approve — she does not confront or defy her mother openly. Instead, she constructs an elaborate clandestine operation: intercepting letters, manipulating the Rouxey lawsuit to bring Albert into her orbit, and eventually engineering Francesca's apparent betrayal.

The Archbishop once "blamed her for being too severe with Rosalie," to which the Baroness replied with a self-justifying defense (s01). This exchange reveals that even the senior clergy of Besançon perceive the Baroness's parenting as excessive, yet Madame de Watteville persists, convinced that her severity is justified by the danger of laxity.

## A Generational Pattern

The mother-daughter conflict in *Albert Savarus* participates in a broader Balzacian theme: the ways in which the older generation's attempts to control the young produce the very outcomes they fear. Madame de Watteville's rigorous supervision does not produce a virtuous daughter but a skilled deceiver. Rosalie learns to present the exact surface her mother demands while pursuing an entirely independent agenda. The conflict is not resolved but merely transferred — when Rosalie ultimately marries the Comte de Soulas, she has already demonstrated that she can outmanoeuvre any system of control designed to contain her.
