# Restoration France

The **Bourbon Restoration** (1814–1830) is the historical period during which Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* is set. Following the defeat of Napoleon, the Restoration saw the return of the Bourbon monarchy to the French throne under Louis XVIII and later Charles X, establishing a constitutional monarchy marked by intense political conflict between royalist Legitimists and liberal opponents of the regime.

## Overview

The Restoration period began with the first abdication of Napoleon in 1814 and was briefly interrupted by the Hundred Days in 1815, after which the Second Restoration firmly reestablished Bourbon rule. The regime attempted to reconcile the revolutionary and Napoleonic inheritance with traditional monarchical principles, but deep divisions persisted between those who sought a return to the ancien régime and those who had benefited from the Revolution.

This was a period of limited constitutional government under the Charter of 1814, which guaranteed certain civil liberties while reserving substantial power for the monarch. The electoral system was based on a narrow property qualification, restricting the franchise to the wealthiest citizens — a crucial context for Albert Savarus's electoral campaign in the novel.

The Restoration ended with the July Revolution of 1830, which brought Louis-Philippe and the more liberal July Monarchy to power.

## Relevance to the Novel

The political dynamics of the Restoration are central to *Albert Savarus*. The novel depicts a Besançon sharply divided between Legitimists — supporters of the Bourbon monarchy, represented by figures like the Baronne de Watteville and the Archbishop — and Liberals, who favored constitutional reforms. Savarus's electoral campaign, backed by the Legitimist faction, takes place within this polarized environment.

Balzac uses the political landscape of the Restoration to explore broader themes: the ways in which personal ambition navigates political structures, the role of the Church in provincial society, and the tension between aristocratic tradition and the emerging professional classes represented by lawyers like Savarus. The very institution of the salon — central to the novel's social world — was a Restoration phenomenon, a space where the boundaries between private sociability and public politics were blurred.
