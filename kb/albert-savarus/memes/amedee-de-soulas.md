# Amédée de Soulas

**Amédée de Soulas** is a supporting character in Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842). A young dandy of Besançon's provincial aristocracy, Soulas positions himself as the arbiter of Parisian fashion in the city and serves as a rival suitor for [[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie de Watteville's]] hand. Through his character, Balzac satirizes the provincial obsession with metropolitan taste and the hollow pretensions of the Restoration-era aristocracy.

## Character Portrait

Amédée de Soulas is introduced as the self-appointed "lion" of Besançon — a young man who has spent time in Paris and returned to the provinces with an affected air of worldliness. He models himself on the fashionable dandies of Parisian society but succeeds only in being a provincial imitation:

## Role in the Novel

Soulas functions primarily as a foil to [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]]. Where Savarus represents genuine talent and hidden depths, Soulas represents surface polish and hollow pretension. His courtship of Rosalie de Watteville is motivated not by genuine feeling but by social calculation — a desire to secure the Watteville fortune and consolidate his position in Besançon society.

His affected Parisian mannerisms — his dress, his speech, his condescending attitude toward provincial ways — are rendered by Balzac with precise comic detail. Yet Soulas is not merely a figure of fun; he represents a real social type of the Restoration period: the provincial aristocrat who, having glimpsed Parisian society, can never again be content with provincial life, yet lacks the talent or ambition to succeed in the capital.

## Viewed by Others

Amédée de Soulas is appraised by the novel's characters with a mixture of amusement, disdain, and tactical interest:

- **The narrator's perspective**: Balzac introduces Soulas with the ironic observation that he "spelt Souleyas at the time of the Spanish occupation" — a detail that undercuts the young man's pretensions to ancient lineage by revealing a name opportunistically adapted to fashion. The narrator presents him as "perhaps the only man in Besançon" to belong to the category of "dandy" and "lion," a distinction that isolates him as much as it elevates him. Balzac's perspective on Soulas is fundamentally comic but not cruel: the dandy is a symptom of a provincial society that measures itself perpetually against a Paris it can never quite reach.

- **[[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie de Watteville]]'s perspective**: Rosalie views Soulas with the indifference of a young woman who has glimpsed something more compelling. His patient cultivation of her affection — motivated by her twenty-thousand-franc dowry — is transparent to her intelligence. Soulas "lays himself out to flatter Mademoiselle de Watteville's conceit," but Rosalie's heart is already claimed by the deeper mystery of Albert Savarus. She tolerates Soulas as part of the social furniture of her world, never as a serious possibility.

- **[[baronne-de-watteville|Baronne de Watteville]]'s perspective**: For the Baronne, Soulas represents the acceptable suitor — a young man of good family whose aristocratic credentials are beyond reproach. His very mediocrity is reassuring: he threatens nothing, transforms nothing, and would perpetuate the provincial order she has spent a lifetime mastering. His suit for Rosalie has her tacit approval, even if Balzac suggests she sees through his pretensions as clearly as anyone.

- **The Besançon aristocracy's collective perspective**: The narrator notes that "if young Monsieur de Soulas did not fall in the esteem of Besançon society, it was out of pure vanity on its part; the aristocracy were happy to affect a modern air, and to be able to show any Parisians of rank who visited the Comté a young man who bore some likeness to them." The aristocracy does not admire Soulas — it uses him. He is a living display piece, a provincial approximation of Parisian fashion that the Besançon elite can exhibit to visiting outsiders as evidence of their own cosmopolitanism.

- **[[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]]'s perspective**: Savarus barely registers Soulas as a rival. The lawyer's interests lie elsewhere — in Francesca, in political ambition, in the life he hopes to reclaim — and the posturing of a provincial dandy barely disturbs his attention. Savarus's indifference is, in its way, Soulas's deepest wound: to be a rival is at least to be taken seriously; to be invisible to one's supposed competitor is to be rendered null.

## Significance

Soulas embodies the theme of provincial imitation that runs throughout the novel. His character illustrates the cultural dependency of provincial France on Paris — a dependency that mirrors the political centralization critiqued throughout *La Comédie Humaine*. His failure to win Rosalie's hand, despite his social advantages, underscores Balzac's point that genuine merit (however concealed, as in Savarus's case) ultimately outshines mere pretension.
