# Duc de Rhétoré

The **Duc de Rhétoré** (Alphonse), eldest son of the Duc de Chaulieu, a former French ambassador, is the man who ultimately marries [[francesca|Francesca]] after Albert Savarus's hopes are destroyed. His wedding announcement in the newspapers is the final confirmation that Albert has lost everything.

## Overview

The Duc de Rhétoré appears only in the novel's epilogue, yet his presence has been gathering force beyond the narrative's visible frame. He is the embodiment of the aristocratic marriage market that Albert Savarus — a natural son with talent but no name — can never fully enter. Where Albert offers passion and literary brilliance, the Duc de Rhétoré offers a title, a diplomatic lineage, and the settled place in society that Francesca, as the widowed Duchesse d'Argaiolo, is expected to accept.

> "The wedding of Monsieur le Duc de Rhétoré, eldest son of the Duc de Chaulieu, the former Ambassador, to Madame la Duchesse d'Argaiolo, née Princess Soderini, was solemnized with great splendor." — [001-863]

## Role in the Novel

Rhétoré's marriage to Francesca is the culmination of Rosalie de Watteville's scheme. By intercepting Albert's correspondence and fabricating evidence of his engagement to [[sidonie-chavoncourt|Sidonie de Chavoncourt]], Rosalie creates the conditions in which Francesca, believing herself abandoned, accepts a conventional aristocratic marriage.

The geography of Albert's desperate pursuit traces the path of his undoing: from Belgirate to London to Naples to Rome — in each city he arrives too late, Francesca having already departed for the next. By the time he reaches Florence, she is "engaged to the Duc de Rhétoré." The wedding is announced with "great splendor" and "numerous entertainments," the language of society journalism rendering Albert's private catastrophe as a social event.

## Francesca's Marriage

Francesca's trajectory from the Bergmanns' lodgings in Gersau to the Duchesse de Rhétoré is the arc of a life that has chosen survival over passion. As the widowed Duchesse d'Argaiolo (née Princess Soderini), she brings "one of the finest fortunes in Italy" to the marriage — her late husband "left her everything."

> "The Duchess' fortune is one of the finest in Italy, for the late Duke left her everything." — [001-867]

When Rosalie later confronts Francesca at the Opera ball, handing over Albert's letters to prove his fidelity, it is too late. The marriage to Rhétoré is fact, and Francesca's reaction — "the victim of some odious plot" — can only register retrospectively the injustice that has been done.

## Significance

The Duc de Rhétoré is less a character than a structural necessity: the man who marries the woman Albert cannot have. He is never described, never given a scene of his own, never endowed with personality. His function is purely positional — he occupies the place in society that Albert's illegitimate birth and Rosalie's machinations have denied him. In this sense, Rhétoré is the novel's final, silent verdict on the limits of individual talent in a world governed by birthright.
