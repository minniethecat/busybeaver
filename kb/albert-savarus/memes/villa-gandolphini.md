# Villa Gandolphini

The **Villa Gandolphini** is the Neapolitan estate of Prince and Princess Gandolphini, where Rodolphe — the fictional alter ego of Albert Savarus — spent three months recovering from the wreck of his commercial fortunes. The villa appears in the autobiographical novella "L'Ambitieux par amour" as a place of refuge and renewal, a luminous interlude between the disasters of Rodolphe's public life.

## Overview

The Villa Gandolphini belongs to the Gandolphini family, one of the wealthiest landowning houses of Sicily, connected by generations of intermarriage to the Colonna family of Rome. After the restoration of the Prince and Princess to their Neapolitan "place and rights on the King's accession," the villa becomes their principal residence and the setting for Rodolphe's happiest moments with Francesca.

The villa functions in the novella as a threshold between two worlds: the world of French politics and commerce, where Rodolphe struggles and fails, and the world of Italian aristocratic life, where love flourishes but remains inaccessible to him as a permanent condition. His three months there are a gift — a respite granted by love before the next round of struggle.

## Rodolphe's Respite

The visit to Villa Gandolphini occurs at a critical juncture in Rodolphe's career. His commercial venture has collapsed and the Villèle ministry has fallen, leaving him "ruined," "wounded and sick at heart." Before entering the political arena, he travels to Naples:

> "He went, all wounded and sick at heart, to have his bruises healed and his courage revived at Naples, where the Prince and Princess had been reinstated in their place and rights on the King's accession. This, in the midst of his warfare, was a respite full of delights; he spent three months at the Villa Gandolphini, rocked in hope."

The phrase "rocked in hope" captures the villa's function: it is a cradle for the deferred future, a place where the lovers can be together under conditions that resemble — but are not yet — the permanent union they desire. The three months are idyllic precisely because they are temporary, a parenthesis in the long narrative of Rodolphe's ambition.

## Naples as Romantic Landscape

The Villa Gandolphini participates in a larger Romantic imaginary of Italy that pervades nineteenth-century French literature. For Balzac's French readers, Naples represented the south — warmth, sensuality, freedom from the constraints of Parisian and provincial society — and the villa is the architectural embodiment of that freedom. It stands in opposition to the enclosed spaces of Besançon: the Hotel de Watteville with its somber garden, the Rue de la Préfecture with its provincial proprieties.

Francesca's command at Gersau — "We meet no more till we meet at Naples" — makes the city and the villa the promised destination of love, the place where separation ends and union begins. The three months at the villa fulfill that promise, even as they prove to be a respite rather than a resolution.

## The Villa and the Portrait

The villa is intimately associated with the portrait of Francesca that Schinner paints. The artist passes through Geneva en route to Italy, and the portrait he creates becomes Rodolphe's talisman during his years of political struggle in Paris. The villa — the place where Francesca lives — and the portrait — the image of her that Rodolphe carries — form a complementary pair: one the reality of love's domestic setting, the other its portable, idealized form.

## Symbolic Significance

In the symbolic geography of *Albert Savarus*, the Villa Gandolphini occupies a position diametrically opposed to Besançon. Where Besançon is enclosure, surveillance, and provincial constraint, the villa is openness, freedom, and the natural expressiveness of love. Where Besançon's spaces — the Watteville salon, the law courts, the Rue du Perron — are stages for performance and concealment, the villa is a space of authenticity. That it is located in Italy, the land Balzac associated with passion and art, completes the opposition.
