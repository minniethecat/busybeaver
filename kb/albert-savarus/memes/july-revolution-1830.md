# The July Revolution of 1830

The July Revolution of 1830 (also known as _Les Trois Glorieuses_ — the
Three Glorious Days) was the second French Revolution of the 19th century,
overthrowing King Charles X and bringing Louis-Philippe to power. In Balzac's
_Albert Savarus_, this historical event serves as a decisive turning point
in the protagonist's political fortunes.

## Historical Background

The revolution erupted on July 27–29, 1830, in response to Charles X's
increasingly authoritarian rule, including the July Ordinances that
suspended press freedom and restricted voting rights. The uprising
replaced the Bourbon Restoration with the July Monarchy under
Louis-Philippe, the "Citizen King."

## Role in the Novel

The July Revolution is a recurring catastrophe in Albert's political
narrative. It strikes at two critical moments:

First, "flung to the depths once more by the revolution of July just
when my name was becoming known"[^1] — Albert, having risen to the
position of Master of Appeals and on the verge of becoming "a necessary
wheel in the political machine," makes the fatal decision to remain
faithful to the fallen Legitimist cause. This loyalty costs him his
political career.

Second, even after rebuilding his hopes under a new patron, "the storm
of July 1830 broke, and again his bark was swamped."[^2]

## Narrative Significance

The July Revolution functions as a symbol of the uncontrollable forces
that repeatedly destroy Albert's ambitions. His political misfortune
is not merely personal failure but is shaped by the great historical
currents of his time. The theme of the individual swept away by history
runs throughout Balzac's _La Comédie Humaine_, and the July Revolution
is its most potent expression in this novel.
