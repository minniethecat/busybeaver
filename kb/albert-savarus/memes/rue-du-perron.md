# Rue du Perron

The **Rue du Perron** is a street in [[besancon|Besançon]] whose narrative significance far exceeds its modest physical dimensions. It is here that [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]], living under the alias "Monsieur Savaron," takes up residence upon his arrival in the city — and it is the geographic adjacency between his garden and that of the [[hotel-de-rupt|Hôtel de Rupt]] that makes [[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie de Watteville's]] surveillance possible.

## Overview

The Rue du Perron is introduced early in the novel as a key spatial link between the Watteville mansion and Savarus's modest lodgings:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Location** | A side street in Besançon, bordering the garden of the Hôtel de Rupt |
| **Notable Resident** | Albert Savarus ("Monsieur Savaron"), lawyer, under an assumed identity |
| **Narrative Function** | Enables Rosalie's observation of Savarus; spatial proximity drives the plot |
| **Neighboring Property** | The Watteville mansion on the Rue de la Préfecture, with its garden extending to the Rue du Perron |

## Description in the Novel

The Rue du Perron is first mentioned in the context of the Watteville mansion's geography, when the Baronne de Watteville explains the layout of her property to a guest. The garden of the Hôtel de Rupt stretches "to the Rue du Perron" (ch01-section-1), and this spatial detail becomes crucial when Savarus takes up residence there:

> "Monsieur Savaron lives in the Rue du Perron; the garden of his house joins mine."
> — (ch01-section-1)

The stranger's choice of lodging reinforces his ambiguous social position. He lives on the Rue du Perron — a respectable but unremarkable street — rather than on the Rue de la Préfecture, the aristocratic quarter. Savarus himself notes his uncertain status:

> "His lodging, like a man uncertain of the future, in the Rue du Perron"
> — (ch01-section-1)

## The Geography of Surveillance

The Rue du Perron's narrative importance lies in the line of sight it provides. Because the gardens of the Hôtel de Rupt and the Rue du Perron residence are contiguous, Rosalie can observe Savarus's comings and goings. This spatial arrangement transforms the Rue du Perron from a simple street into the geography of intrigue:

> "I look out on the house where this mysterious personage lodges — the garden of his house joins the Hôtel de Rupt."
> — (ch03-section-3)

It is from her window overlooking the Rue du Perron that Rosalie first notices the "stranger" (Savarus), and it is through this garden boundary that she begins her campaign of observation and, ultimately, manipulation. The street itself — unremarkable, provincial, closed — mirrors the circumscribed world within which Rosalie's intelligence and will must operate.

## Significance

The Rue du Perron exemplifies Balzac's technique of making urban geography serve narrative purpose. The street is not merely a location; it is the spatial precondition for the novel's central plot. Without the garden adjacency between the Hôtel de Rupt and the Rue du Perron, Rosalie's surveillance — and therefore her discovery of Savarus's secret correspondence, her interception of his letters, and the catastrophic chain of events that follow — would be impossible.
