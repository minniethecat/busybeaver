# Legitimism

**Legitimism** is the political doctrine that gives shape to the social and political world of Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842). As the dominant political force in Restoration-era Besançon, the Legitimist faction — supporters of the elder Bourbon line deposed in 1830 — provides the framework within which Albert Savarus pursues his legal career and electoral ambitions. Balzac's portrayal of Legitimism in the novel is characteristically ambivalent: he depicts its social rituals with ethnographic precision while exposing the contradictions and vulnerabilities of a political order built on inherited privilege.

## Historical Context

Under the Bourbon Restoration (1814–1830), French politics was divided between Legitimists, who supported the absolute monarchy of the senior Bourbon line, and their opponents — Liberals, Bonapartists, and Republicans. The July Revolution of 1830, which brought the Orléanist Louis-Philippe to power, transformed Legitimism from a governing ideology into an oppositional stance. This is the political moment in which *Albert Savarus* unfolds: the Legitimists of Besançon, though still socially dominant, are fighting a rearguard action against the centralizing, modernizing forces emanating from Paris.

## Legitimism in Besançon

Besançon, as Balzac presents it, is a Legitimist stronghold — a provincial city where "the habit of watching others" substitutes for political participation and where social hierarchy is treated as natural law. The Watteville salon, presided over by the Baronne, is the epicenter of this world:

| Institution | Role in Legitimist Besançon |
|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Watteville Salon** | Center of Legitimist social life; where political alliances are forged |
| **Archbishop's Circle** | Religious sanction for Legitimist orthodoxy |
| **Besançon Bar** | Professional arena where Legitimist patronage determines advancement |
| **The Election** | Mechanism by which Legitimist elites select representatives for the Chamber |

## Savarus and Legitimism

Albert Savarus's relationship to Legitimism is strategic rather than ideological. He secures the backing of the Legitimist faction for his electoral campaign not because he is a true believer but because Legitimism is the dominant political force in Besançon — the only vehicle through which an ambitious outsider can achieve political power. His calculation is characteristic of the Balzacian hero: ideology is instrumental, a tool to be deployed in the service of ambition.

The fragility of this calculation is exposed when Rosalie de Watteville manipulates Legitimist sentiment against Savarus. His status as an outsider — a man without deep roots in Besançon's aristocratic society — makes him vulnerable to the very faction whose support he has cultivated.

## The Baron de Watteville's Rejection

The Baron de Watteville's horrified response to the suggestion that he participate in an election — "Go to the election! take the oath?" — epitomizes the Legitimist aristocracy's disdain for constitutional politics. For the old aristocracy, political participation through elections represents a degradation of natural authority; true legitimacy derives from birth and tradition, not from the vulgar mechanism of the ballot box. This attitude, Balzac suggests, is both the nobility's defining characteristic and the source of its political irrelevance.
