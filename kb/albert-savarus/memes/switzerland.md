# Switzerland

**Switzerland** serves as the framing setting for the embedded novella within Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842). The Swiss landscape — particularly the region around Lake Lucerne and the village of Gersau — provides the backdrop against which the central love story of Rodolphe (the future Albert Savarus) and Francesca Colonna unfolds. Switzerland functions in the novel as a space of refuge, exile, and romantic possibility, contrasted with the provincial enclosure of Besançon.

## Overview

Switzerland appears in the novel in two principal registers. First, the Watteville family itself is of Swiss origin: "The Swiss gentleman's coat-of-arms (the Wattevilles are Swiss) was then borne as an escutcheon of pretence on the old shield of the Rupts" (Section 1). This genealogical detail establishes a thread connecting the Besançon aristocracy to the Swiss cantons.

Second and more significantly, Switzerland is the setting of Rodolphe and Francesca's story within the story. After fleeing Italy, the two lovers settle at Gersau on Lake Lucerne, where they live in seclusion under assumed identities. The Swiss lakes and mountains become the symbolic landscape of their stolen happiness — a paradise from which Rodolphe is ultimately exiled.

## The Swiss Journey

The journey that brings Rodolphe to Gersau is one of aesthetic pilgrimage. The novel describes how Rodolphe and his friend Leopold Hannequin made "a tour through Switzerland, set out from Lucerne one fine morning in a boat" (Section 6). The voyage along Lake Lucerne is presented as a sublime encounter with nature:

> "The views which shut in the waters on the way from Lucerne to Fluelen are, to my thinking, superior to the most admired spots in the world."
> — (Section 6)

The friends, captivated by the landscape's beauty, decide to alter their travel plans. Upon passing the hamlet of Gersau, one friend declares: "Let us stop here. We meant to make Lucerne our headquarters for seeing Switzerland; you will not take it amiss, Leopold, if I change my mind and stay here" (Section 6).

## Gersau as Refuge

Gersau becomes the site of Rodolphe's deepest happiness and, later, his most profound grief. After Francesca leaves him, Rodolphe remains at Gersau, unwilling to abandon the place where he had briefly possessed everything:

> "Rodolphe stayed on at Gersau, the only place in the world where his grief could grow calmer."
> — (Section 8)

The Bergmann family — retired gardeners from the Borromean Islands on Lago Maggiore — serve as Rodolphe's caretakers during his convalescence. Their Swiss practicality and compassion provide the material conditions for Rodolphe's slow recovery from heartbreak. The Swiss landscape itself becomes a therapeutic agent; Rodolphe "secured him the pity and interest of every one in Gersau," and his morning vigils by the lake become a ritual of mourning.

## Switzerland and Besançon

The novel establishes an implicit geographical opposition between Switzerland and Besançon. Switzerland is open, horizontal, bathed in lake-light — a landscape of possibility. Besançon, by contrast, is enclosed by its mountain horseshoe and defined by provincial social enclosure. The Watteville family's Swiss ancestry serves as a genealogical echo of this geographical opposition: the Baron's "Swiss gentleman's" coat-of-arms marks him as connected to a world beyond the Franche-Comté, even as he has been absorbed into Besançon's provincial aristocracy.

Albert Savarus, in his political and professional life in Besançon, carries Switzerland within him as memory and as motivation. His ambition to succeed in France is inseparable from his desire to return to the Italian woman he loved in Switzerland. Every reference to the Swiss lakes in the later sections of the novel carries the weight of this unrealized return.
