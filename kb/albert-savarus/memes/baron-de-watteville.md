# Baron de Watteville

The **Baron de Watteville** is a supporting character in Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842). The gentle, peaceable husband of the formidable [[baronne-de-watteville|Baronne de Watteville]] and father of [[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie]], the Baron represents the domestication of the old French aristocracy under the Bourbon Restoration. His mildness stands in pointed contrast to his infamous ancestor — the "most successful and illustrious of murderers and renegades" — and to his wife's commanding social presence.

## Character Portrait

The Baron is defined by his gentleness, his passivity, and his complete subordination to his wife's authority in domestic and social matters:

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Lineage** | Descendant of the infamous Watteville of the Grand Siècle |
| **Character** | Gentle, peaceable, politically disengaged |
| **Family Role** | Subordinate to the Baronne; largely absent from Rosalie's emotional life |
| **Narrative Function** | Embodies the decline of aristocratic vigor into domesticated passivity |

## Political Significance

The Baron's most memorable moment in the novel is his horrified response to the suggestion that he participate in an election: "Go to the election! take the oath?" This exclamation encapsulates the Legitimist aristocracy's deep-seated disdain for constitutional politics. For the old nobility, political participation through elections represents a degradation of natural, inherited authority. The Baron's refusal epitomizes the political irrelevance into which the Restoration aristocracy was drifting.

## The Ancestral Shadow

> "Monsieur de Watteville, a descendant of the famous Watteville, the most successful and illustrious of murderers and renegades — his extraordinary adventures are too much a part of history to be related here — this nineteenth century Monsieur de Watteville was as gentle and peaceable as his ancestor of the Grand Siecle had been passionate and turbulent."
> — (ch01-section-1)

The contrast between the Baron and his ancestor is one of Balzac's most economical character sketches. In a single sentence, he evokes the transformation of the French aristocracy from a warrior class capable of "passionate and turbulent" action into a domesticated gentry whose defining characteristic is gentleness — and whose political influence has diminished accordingly.

## Viewed by Others

The Baron de Watteville is seen by those around him with varying degrees of affection, pity, and strategic calculation:

- **[[baronne-de-watteville|Baronne de Watteville]]'s perspective**: The Baronne views her husband as an obstacle to be managed rather than a partner to be consulted. She contrives to send him to Les Rouxey to manage the estate and the lawsuit — a strategic exile that removes him from Besançon society and allows her to control the household without interference. Her treatment of the Baron is a masterclass in domestic dominance: never openly cruel, always within the forms of wifely solicitude, yet unmistakably a sentence of irrelevance.

- **[[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie de Watteville]]'s perspective**: Rosalie's relationship to her father is marked by absence. He figures so little in her emotional life that his death, when it comes, provokes a sorrow that "had no visible outlet" — a grief not primarily for her father but for the consequences of her own manipulations. The Baron's gentle nature, which might in another household have been a source of warmth, becomes in the Watteville mansion merely another silence — a presence so unobtrusive that his daughter can scarcely grieve him as a person rather than as an event.

- **The narrator's perspective**: Balzac presents the Baron through the lens of historical irony. The juxtaposition of the "passionate and turbulent" ancestor with the "gentle and peaceable" descendant is more than a character sketch — it is a compressed history of the French aristocracy. The Baron is what two centuries of political centralization have made of a once-warrior class: a man so domesticated that the mere thought of political participation strikes him as an affront to his dignity.

- **Besançon society's perspective**: The Baron is a man who exists in the social record but not in the social action. He is known — as the husband of the Baronne, as the descendant of the infamous Watteville — but he is not consulted, feared, or courted. His gentleness, which Balzac renders with genuine affection, is also the quality that makes him socially invisible: a man whom everyone respects and no one needs.
