# Marriage as Social Strategy

In the provincial world of Balzac's *Albert Savarus*, **marriage** is not the culmination of romantic love but a strategic transaction — the primary mechanism by which families consolidate wealth, secure status, and negotiate alliances. The novel anatomizes this system through Amedée de Soulas's years-long campaign to win Rosalie de Watteville's hand, a project driven entirely by economic calculation.

## Overview

The Bourbon Restoration preserved the ancien régime's conception of marriage as a family affair rather than an individual choice. In the closed world of Besançon's aristocracy, "the marriages in these families are arranged in the cradle, so rigidly are the greatest things settled as well as the smallest" (ch01-section-1). A stranger could no more enter one of these houses than alter the matrimonial destiny already inscribed in the social order.

## Relevance to the Novel

### Amedée de Soulas: The Calculating Suitor

Amedée de Soulas is Balzac's case study in matrimonial strategy. His entire public performance — the fashionable dress, the cultivated appearance of wealth, the careful association with Parisian trends — serves a single purpose:

> "All this hidden labor, all this dust thrown in people's eyes, this display of folly and latent prudence, had an object... Amedee wanted to achieve a good marriage by proving some day that his farms were not mortgaged, and that he had some savings." (ch01-section-1)

For five years, Amedée "had worked like a mole to get into the highest favor of the severe Baroness, while laying himself out to flatter Mademoiselle de Watteville's conceit" (ch01-section-1). His target is not Rosalie herself but the financial calculus embodied in her dowry — "about twenty thousand francs a year."

### The Dowry Economy

Rosalie's value in the marriage market is precisely quantified by Besançon society. The public arithmetic of the dowry — combining Watteville land and Rupt inheritance — is a form of social discourse in which every figure is known, debated, and calculated:

| Asset | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Watteville estate (Les Rouxey) | ~10,000 francs/year |
| Rupt inheritance (from mother) | ~10,000 francs/year |
| Baron de Watteville's collection | Significant but illiquid |
| Total estimated dowry | ~20,000 francs/year |

The dowry figure of "twenty thousand francs a year" is the number around which the novel's matrimonial calculations revolve. It is what makes Rosalie "the greatest heiress in Besançon" and what drives Amedée de Soulas's five-year campaign (ch01-section-1).

The Baroness de Watteville, who "was in the secret of the devices by which Amedee succeeded in keeping up his rank," functions as the gatekeeper of this transaction — a role she embraces with the same rigor she applies to religious observance. Her own marriage to the Baron, "arranged in 1802" and "solemnized in 1815" after the Restoration, is itself a testament to the strategic patience that matrimonial calculation demanded: thirteen years elapsed between the arrangement and the ceremony (ch01-section-1).

### The Watteville Marriage: A Case Study

The Baron and Baroness de Watteville's own union provides a template for the matrimonial system the novel depicts. The Baron married "the heiress of the celebrated house of Rupt," who "brought twenty thousand francs a year in the funds" (ch01-section-1). The Baron, described as "a little man of no mind," was a negligible figure in the household — his sole contribution to the marriage was his name and his land. The Baroness, meanwhile, "devout as a girl, became even more so after her marriage," suggesting that the intensification of religious observance was a compensation for the absence of conjugal satisfaction (ch01-section-1).

This pattern — the capable wife, the diminished husband, the strategic merger of assets — recurs throughout Balzac's *Comédie Humaine* and represents his most sustained critique of the matrimonial system.

### The Alternative: Love Marriage

The novel's central romantic plot — Albert Savarus's devotion to Francesca Soderini — stands as the counterpoint to the provincial marriage market. Savarus's love is absolute, disinterested, and indifferent to financial calculation. The marriage he seeks is the reward for political success, not the means to it. This contrast between the calculating Amedée and the romantic Savarus structures the novel's moral economy: true love belongs to the world of the embedded novella ("Ambition for Love's Sake"), while strategic marriage is the province of Besançon's drawing-rooms.

The tragedy is that the two systems collide. Rosalie, raised within the strategic-marriage system, applies its logic to her pursuit of Savarus — intercepting letters, forging replies, engineering outcomes behind the scenes. She approaches love as a matrimonial strategist, and the result is the destruction of the very love she sought to secure.

### Suitors on the Market

The novel presents a spectrum of suitors, each embodying a different form of matrimonial strategy:

| Suitor | Strategy | Assets | Target |
|--------|----------|--------|--------|
| Amedée de Soulas | Five-year campaign of social performance | ~15,000 francs/year, unencumbered farms | Rosalie de Watteville |
| Monsieur de Vauchelles | "Matrimonial plans" for his son | Family name and connections | Sidonie de Chavoncourt |
| Albert Savarus | Political success → eligibility | Professional reputation, future parliamentary seat | Francesca Soderini (secret) |

Each suitor calculates differently, but all calculate. Even Savarus, the romantic hero, understands that love requires a political and financial foundation — the difference is that he pursues that foundation for love's sake rather than as an end in itself.

### The Family Economy and Matrimonial Accounting

Beneath the social rituals of the marriage market lies a rigorous system of household accounting. Balzac provides precise figures for the Watteville family's financial operations, revealing how matrimonial strategy is sustained by decades of disciplined saving. The Wattevilles, "who for twelve years had been drawing an income of fifty thousand francs a year, did not spend more than four-and-twenty thousand francs a year, while receiving all the upper circle of Besancon every Monday and Friday" (ch01-section-2). This annual surplus of twenty-six thousand francs, "saved and invested with the judgment that distinguishes those old families," was the hidden engine of Rosalie's marriageability — it transformed her dowry from a respectable sum into a competitive fortune (ch01-section-2).

The timing of these investments was as strategic as the savings themselves. It was "very generally supposed that Madame de Watteville, thinking she had land enough, had placed her savings in the three per cents, in 1830" (ch01-section-2) — the year of the July Revolution, when government bonds were depressed and could be acquired at favorable rates. This detail reveals the Baroness not merely as a religious devotee but as a shrewd financial operator whose piety coexisted with a keen eye for market timing. The dowry that Amedée de Soulas pursued was not an accident of inheritance but the product of systematic accumulation — a "war chest" built over twelve years of conspicuous frugality disguised beneath the rituals of provincial hospitality.
