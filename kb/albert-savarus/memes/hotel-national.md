# Hôtel National

The **Hôtel National** is the commercial hotel in [[besancon|Besançon]] where [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]] first lodges after arriving from Paris. It represents his initial, transient identity in the city — that of a stranger, a traveler whose business and intentions are unknown — before he establishes himself as a resident lawyer on the [[rue-du-perron|Rue du Perron]].

## Overview

The Hôtel National's narrative function is to mark Savarus's entry into Besançon as an outsider. Unlike the aristocratic residences of the Rue de la Préfecture, the Hôtel National is a place of passage — neutral, commercial, belonging to no family and aligned with no faction:

|--------|--------|

## Savarus's Arrival

The Hôtel National appears at the very beginning of Albert's Besançon story. The Abbé de Grancey narrates the arrival to the Watteville salon:

> "One fine morning the mail coach dropped at the Hotel National a gentleman from Paris, who, after seeking apartments, made up his mind in favor of the first floor in Mademoiselle Galard's house, Rue du Perron."
> — (ch03-section-3)

The arrival is methodical. The stranger immediately proceeds to establish his legal and civic identity in the city:

> Then the stranger went straight to the Mairie, and had himself registered as a resident with all political qualifications. Finally, he had his name entered on the list of the barristers to the Court, showing his title in due form, and he left his card on all his new colleagues, the Ministerial officials, the Councillors of the Court, and the members of the bench, with the name, "ALBERT SAVARON."
> — (ch03-section-3)

The sequence is significant: the Hôtel National is merely a way-station. Albert uses it as a base from which to execute his bureaucratic self-insertion — registering at the Mairie, enrolling at the Court, distributing calling cards. The hotel is a launch pad, not a home.

The phrase "modest means" is revealing. Savarus — the brilliant lawyer, the former minister, the man of grand ambitions — presents himself in Besançon as a man of limited resources. The Hôtel National, a place for travelers rather than residents, suits this persona of deliberate self-diminishment.

## Prince Colonna's Arrival

The Hôtel National reappears in a second, more dramatic arrival scene late in the novel. The arrival of Francesca's father, Prince Colonna, is reported to Rosalie through her servant Mariette:

> "Mademoiselle, an old gentleman from Paris arrived yesterday morning at the Hotel National; he came in his own carriage with four horses, and a courier in front, and a servant. Indeed, Jerome, who saw the carriage returning, declares he could only be a prince or a _milord_."
> — (ch17-section-17)

The Prince's entourage — a carriage with four horses, a courier, a liveried servant — contrasts sharply with Albert's unassuming arrival by mail coach. The Hôtel National thus serves as a stage for two very different modes of arrival: the anonymous professional and the ostentatious aristocrat.

The concierge does not record the Prince's name — presumably on his orders:

> "As the old gentleman did not mean to stay, he was not asked for it. The servant, by his orders no doubt, pretended not to speak French."
> — (ch17-section-17)

The Prince's departure is equally abrupt: he leaves "The Geneva Road!" — heading toward Switzerland, toward Gersau, toward the lost world of Francesca.

## From Hotel to Residence

Savarus's trajectory from the Hôtel National to the Rue du Perron maps his integration into Besançon society. The hotel represents his liminal phase — no longer in Paris, not yet established in Besançon. His move to the Rue du Perron, where his garden adjoins that of the [[hotel-de-rupt|Hôtel de Rupt]], represents a step toward permanence and, unwittingly, toward the surveillance that will destroy him.

This spatial transition from transient hotel to permanent lodging mirrors the larger arc of the novel: Savarus comes to Besançon to escape his past, but the city's insularity — symbolized by the garden adjacency between his new home and the Watteville mansion — ensures that his past will find him. Prince Colonna's own night at the Hôtel National is the mechanism of that finding: the Prince comes to retrieve Albert for the political mission that will cost him Rosalie, and the hotel serves both arrivals — the man who came to hide and the man who came to expose him.

## Significance

The Hôtel National functions as a narrative hinge: it is the threshold space between Savarus's Parisian past and his Besançon present, and later the threshold between his Besançon life and his return to exile. Its name — "National" — is ironic: the hotel that hosts strangers is, in a sense, the most cosmopolitan space in the city, and yet it is precisely through this anonymous portal that the forces of destiny (in the form of Prince Colonna) enter to disrupt provincial life.
