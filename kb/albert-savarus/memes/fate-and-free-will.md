# Fate and Free Will

The tension between **fate and free will** runs through *Albert Savarus* as both a narrative structure and a philosophical question. Characters consistently invoke fortune and destiny to explain outcomes, yet the novel's plot reveals those outcomes to be the product of deliberate human action — particularly Rosalie's systematic campaign of intervention. The gap between what characters attribute to fate and what the reader knows to be engineered constitutes one of the novel's central ironies.

The language of fortune permeates the novel. Mademoiselle de Watteville possesses an "enormous prospective fortune," making her the object of strategic pursuit. Savarus declares he has his "fortune to make," framing his career as a project of self-determination [002-Section-2]. Yet the novel relentlessly demonstrates that fortune is made and unmade by human hands — Rosalie's interception of letters, her manipulation of the Vicar-General, her strategic deployment of information. What appears to Albert as "a stroke of fate" is, the reader understands, the calculated work of a rival.

## The Vocabulary of Fate in the Novel

Balzac carefully distributes the language of destiny among his characters. Each invokes fate in a manner consistent with their social position and psychological makeup:

| Character | How They Invoke Fate | What It Conceals |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------|
| Albert Savarus | "My fortune to make" | His belief in meritocratic self-determination |
| Rosalie de Watteville | Providence as sanction for her schemes | Her calculated manipulation behind the language of destiny |
| Madame de Watteville | Religious submission to God's will | Her retreat from an emotionally hollow marriage |
| Baron de Watteville | Passive acceptance of whatever transpires | His utter absence of agency |
| Francesca d'Argaiolo | Romantic destiny as the justification of love | Her ultimate capitulation to family obligation over romantic choice |

Albert's relationship to fate is the most complex. He genuinely believes in the power of will and effort — his legal career, his founding of the *Revue de l'Est*, his political campaign are all projects of self-determination [011-Section-11]. Yet he also speaks of his love for Francesca in the language of destiny: she is "the woman to whom my life is pledged," as though his devotion were a force beyond choice [013-Section-13]. This doubleness — will in the professional sphere, fate in the romantic — is precisely what Rosalie exploits.

## Rosalie and the Paradox of Agency

Rosalie herself embodies the paradox of free will in a determined world. Her actions are the most freely chosen in the novel — she decides to pursue Albert, to investigate his past, to destroy his prospects with Francesca and Sidonie — yet her freedom is exercised entirely within the cage of her position as a provincial noblewoman [003-Section-3]. She can sabotage a marriage but cannot propose one; she can destroy a reputation but cannot build a career. Her will is powerful but its objects are limited by a social destiny she cannot escape.

The chain of Rosalie's interventions reveals the systematic nature of her agency:

1. She observes Albert's arrival and assesses him as a matrimonial target [003-Section-3]
2. She investigates his journal, the *Revue de l'Est*, and deduces its true purpose [011-Section-11]
3. She intercepts the letter to Francesca at Belgirate [013-Section-13]
4. She forges a false reply purporting to announce Francesca's marriage [018-Section-18]
5. She engineers the revelation of Albert's secret past to Besançon society [018-Section-18]

Each step is an act of will; together they constitute a counter-narrative to the language of fate that Albert employs to explain his misfortune.

## Balzac's Materialist Conception of Fate

Balzac's treatment of this theme reflects his broader materialist philosophy: in the world of *La Comédie Humaine*, fate is not a supernatural force but the accumulated weight of social structures, economic realities, and human calculation. The "misfortune" that separates Rodolphe (Albert's younger brother) from his beloved is not destiny but the consequence of social inequality and family opposition — forces that are immovable not because they are fate but because they are society [007-Section-7].

This materialist conception is dramatized through the novel's structure. The embedded novella, "Ambition for Love's Sake," presents the love story of Rodolphe and Francesca as a romantic destiny — "a passion such as one may feel at twenty" [007-Section-7]. But the framing narrative of Besançon reveals that this apparent destiny is vulnerable to the most mundane instruments of social manipulation: a stolen address, a forged letter, a whispered revelation in a salon. The structure itself thus argues that fate is what we call forces whose mechanisms we do not see.

## The Tragic Irony of Fatalism

The novel's ultimate irony is that the character most committed to a fatalistic worldview — Albert, who trusts in destiny to reunite him with Francesca — is destroyed by the character most committed to the exercise of free will — Rosalie, who refuses to accept that events are beyond her control. Albert's fatalism is not simply an error but a strategic vulnerability: by believing in fate, he fails to guard against the human forces arrayed against him. Rosalie's triumph is not that fate favored her but that she understood from the beginning that in Besançon, nothing happens by accident.
