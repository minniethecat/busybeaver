# The Watteville Marriage Negotiation

The **Watteville Marriage Negotiation** refers to the orchestrated plan, advanced principally by the Baronne de Watteville and Abbé de Grancey, to marry [[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie de Watteville]] to [[amedee-de-soulas|Amédée de Soulas]] — a match designed to consolidate the Watteville family's social position and secure control of the Rouxey estate in Balzac's *Albert Savarus*. The negotiation unfolds against the backdrop of Rosalie's secret and self-destructive passion for [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]], transforming what might have been a conventional aristocratic marriage arrangement into a complex drama of deception, resistance, and ultimate reversal.

## Context

By the time the marriage negotiation becomes explicit, Amédée de Soulas has long been positioned as Rosalie's most eligible suitor. The Comte de Soulas's son bears a comital title but possesses only four thousand francs a year — a hollow nobility that requires a wealthy marriage for its material survival. The Watteville family, by contrast, possesses substantial landed wealth, notably the Rouxey estate, but lacks the highest social distinction. A marriage between Rosalie and Soulas would thus serve the logic of aristocratic exchange: title for fortune.

The Baronne de Watteville, Rosalie's mother, is the negotiation's most forceful advocate. Her husband, the Baron, is described as a passive figure absorbed in his lathe-turning hobby, and the effective direction of the family's social strategy falls to his wife and the Vicar-General, Abbé de Grancey. The Baronne "had positively refused" to countenance any match that did not advance the family's standing, and Soulas represented the optimal candidate within Besançon's limited aristocratic pool.

## The Negotiation

The negotiation becomes explicit when the Vicar-General announces the terms: "We will marry Rosalie to Monsieur de Soulas; if you give her les Rouxey, you will keep her at Besançon" (Section 17). The Rouxey estate — worth approximately ten thousand francs a year — is the key bargaining chip. By settling it on Rosalie, the Wattevilles can keep their daughter close and secure the family's local influence, while Soulas gains the fortune his title requires.

The lawsuit over the Rouxey boundary, which Albert Savarus is retained to argue, becomes entangled with the marriage negotiation. The Vicar-General's political calculation is explicit: by winning the Rouxey lawsuit, Savarus would gain eighty Legitimist votes, which, combined with thirty votes commanded by the Church, would give Albert a majority in the electoral college. In this scheme, the lawsuit, the marriage, and the election form a single integrated strategy — law, love, and politics converging in a provincial calculus of power.

## Rosalie's Resistance

Rosalie's response to the proposed match is a flat refusal, publicly known throughout Besançon: "Mademoiselle de Watteville had positively refused the Comte de Soulas" (Section 19). Her resistance is not rooted in principle but in obsession: she will marry no one but Albert Savarus. She declares to her mother that "one thing I will have, and must have; my own choice of a husband" (Section 13), a declaration of autonomy that, in the Restoration aristocracy, amounts to rebellion.

When Albert is lost to her — his secret exposed, his political career destroyed, his departure from Besançon final — the marriage negotiation is resurrected in an altered form. Rosalie is asked: "Will you not be reconciled to your mother and marry the Comte de Soulas?" (Section 19). Her reply — that she would do so only if Albert ordered it — reveals that even in defeat, her emotional life remains organized around the man she could not possess.

## The Reversal

In the novel's denouement, the marriage negotiation undergoes a darkly ironic reversal. After the Baron's death, Rosalie encourages her mother, the widowed Baronne, to marry the Comte de Soulas herself: "Rosalie encouraged her mother to marry the Comte de Soulas and settle all her own fortune on him" (Section 19). The title Rosalie had spurned becomes her mother's consolation prize, and Rosalie's renunciation of her inheritance completes the reversal: the marriage that was meant to consolidate the Watteville fortune ultimately disperses it.

- [[amedee-de-soulas|Amédée de Soulas]] — The comital suitor whose title requires a wealthy marriage
- [[baronne-de-watteville|Baronne de Watteville]] — The negotiation's principal advocate
- [[abbe-de-grancey|Abbé de Grancey]] — The Vicar-General who integrates the marriage into his political strategy
- [[baron-de-watteville|Baron de Watteville]] — The passive father who defers to his wife's social management

## Consequences

1. Rosalie's refusal of Soulas publicly signals her determination to pursue Albert, intensifying the romantic triangle that drives the plot.
2. The integration of the marriage negotiation with the Rouxey lawsuit and Albert's election bid creates the novel's central web of interlocking ambitions.
3. The eventual marriage of the Baronne to Soulas, engineered by Rosalie, provides the novel's bitter denouement: Rosalie, having destroyed Albert, destroys her own inheritance as a form of self-punishment.
