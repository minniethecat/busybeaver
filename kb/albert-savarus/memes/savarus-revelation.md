# The Revelation of Albert Savarus's Identity

The revelation of Albert Savarus's true identity constitutes the pivotal event of Balzac's *Albert Savarus*. It is not a single dramatic moment but a chain of discoveries — some accidental, some engineered — through which the protagonist's carefully constructed anonymity in Besançon is destroyed, leading to the collapse of his political ambitions and his permanent exile.

## The Layered Identity

Albert Savarus arrives in Besançon as a man with a hidden past, known publicly as a brilliant lawyer but privately carrying the identity of Rodolphe, the passionate lover and political exile whose story forms the embedded novella "L'Ambitieux par amour." The biographical sketch in the *Revue de l'Est* is, in fact, his own story, published pseudonymously — a confession disguised as fiction.

The embedded novella reveals that Rodolphe was the natural son of a nobleman, raised in modest circumstances, who fell deeply in love with Francesca Soderini, an Italian exile living in Geneva. After Francesca's family fortune was restored and she was married first to the old Duc d'Argaiolo and then, after his death, to the Duc de Rhétoré, Rodolphe's political career in France collapsed. He fled to Besançon, adopted the name Albert Savarus, and began a new life — hanging between hope that Francesca might someday be free and the daily demands of his legal practice.

## Rosalie's Discovery

The revelation process begins not with public exposure but with private curiosity. Rosalie de Watteville, having fallen in love with Albert from the moment she saw him, becomes obsessed with uncovering the mystery of his past. Her investigation proceeds through several stages.

First, she reads the "L'Ambitieux par amour" story in the *Revue de l'Est* and recognizes — with the intuition of love — that it is autobiographical. The story describes the kiosk, the garden, the evening gatherings, and a love affair that mirrors the emotional intensity she herself feels. "After reading the first pages," Balzac writes, "this opinion took so strong a hold on her, that after reading the fragment to the end she was certain that it was no mistake" (s05).

## The Intercepted Correspondence

The most decisive act in the revelation chain is Rosalie's interception of Albert's correspondence with Francesca. Learning that Albert receives letters addressed in a woman's handwriting, Rosalie contrives to have the letters stolen and read. She discovers the continuation of the story from the *Revue de l'Est* — the letters between Albert and Francesca that reveal the full depth of their relationship and Albert's enduring devotion to the Italian duchess.

The key letter from Francesca, published in a *Gazette* notice from Florence, announces her marriage to the Duc de Rhétoré. "The wedding of Monsieur le Duc de Rhétoré, eldest son of the Duc de Chaulieu, the former Ambassador, to Madame la Duchesse d'Argaiolo, *née* Princess Soderini, was solemnized with great splendor" (s12). The notice adds the devastating detail: "The Duchess' fortune is one of the finest in Italy, for the late Duke left her everything." Albert's hopes, suspended on the possibility that the old duke might die and leave Francesca free, are shattered — she is now married to another, and more securely than before.

## The Weaponization of Knowledge

Rosalie does not merely discover Albert's secret; she uses it. Writing in Albert's name, she sends letters to the Duchesse de Rhétoré falsely representing Albert as faithless — "she had answered the news of the Duc d'Argaiolo's death by announcing the marriage ere long of Albert and Mademoiselle de Watteville" (s13). These forged letters destroy the possibility of reconciliation between Albert and Francesca, ensuring that Albert's future — if he is to have one in Besançon — must be with Rosalie.

The culmination of the revelation occurs when Albert himself discovers what Rosalie has done. At the Hotel de Rupt, on the evening when his election as deputy was to be celebrated and his engagement to Sidonie de Chavoncourt was to be brokered, Albert receives a letter that reveals the full extent of Rosalie's machinations. He leaves Besançon the same night, abandoning the election, his legal practice, and his political future.

## The Aftermath

The revelation's consequences radiate outward to affect every major character in the novel. Albert departs for Switzerland, enters the Carthusian monastery of the Grande Chartreuse, and eventually becomes a monk, renouncing the world. Rosalie, having achieved her aim of separating Albert from Francesca, finds that her victory is hollow — the man she loved is lost to her forever. Monsieur de Chavoncourt wins the election by default. The Baron de Watteville, already weakened by the shock of discovering his daughter's schemes, dies shortly after. Madame de Watteville and Rosalie retreat to the estate of Les Rouxey, living "in savage silence" (s13).

## Structural Significance

The revelation of Albert's identity is both the novel's climax and its central structural device. Balzac embeds one story within another — the tale of Rodolphe and Francesca within the tale of Albert and Rosalie — so that the act of reading and discovery within the fictional world mirrors the reader's own experience. Rosalie becomes a reader of the text that Albert has written, and her interpretation of that text — her conviction that she can replace Francesca in the narrative — drives the novel's tragic conclusion.
