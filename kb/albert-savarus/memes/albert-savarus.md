# Albert Savarus

**Albert Savarus** is the titular protagonist of Honoré de Balzac's novel *Albert Savarus* (1842), part of *La Comédie Humaine*. He arrives in [[besancon|Besançon]] as a brilliant and mysterious lawyer, quickly establishing himself in the city's social and professional circles while concealing a hidden past and a secret love for [[francesca|Francesca]]. His trajectory from promising arriviste to politically defeated and personally devastated figure exemplifies Balzac's exploration of ambition, identity, and the often-tragic collision between private passion and public life.

Albert Savarus embodies the tension between public ambition and private passion that characterizes much of Balzac's work. His decision to pursue a legal and political career in Besançon while harboring a secret love for Francesca places him at the center of the novel's exploration of identity, ambition, and the conflict between social convention and genuine feeling.

## Role in the Novel

Albert Savarus arrives in Besançon during the Bourbon Restoration period, a time of political maneuvering and social stratification. His legal prowess quickly attracts attention, and he becomes a candidate for the [[election]], backed by the Legitimist faction. His professional success, however, is shadowed by his hidden past.

The central tension of his character lies in the duality between his public persona — the accomplished, respectable lawyer Albert Savarus — and his private self as Rodolphe, a man deeply in love with Francesca. This duality is tested when [[rosalie-de-watteville|Rosalie de Watteville]], the daughter of the Baronne, becomes infatuated with him and begins to interfere with his plans, including his political ambitions and his relationship with Francesca.

His story is told in part through a framed narrative: a novella-within-the-novel titled "L'Ambitieux par amour" that recounts his past romance, which Rosalie discovers and reads, setting in motion the novel's climactic events.

- **ch17-section-17**: Savarus participates in an [[election]], experiencing the bitter disappointment of political defeat.
- **ch20-section-20**: The resolution of Savarus's story, as his hidden identity and romantic past are fully revealed.

## Relationships

- **[[francesca]]** — The great love of Savarus's life. Their relationship forms the emotional core of the framed narrative within the novel. Francesca is a woman of Italian origins whose connection with Rodolphe (Savarus) is passionate but complicated by circumstance and the interference of others.

- **[[rosalie-de-watteville]]** — The daughter of the Baronne de Watteville. Rosalie becomes obsessed with Savarus after reading his private papers, and her manipulations have devastating consequences for his political career and his relationship with Francesca.

- **[[baronne-de-watteville]]** — The most important lady of Besançon, in whose salon Savarus is a regular guest. She provides him with access to the city's social elite.

- **[[baron-de-watteville]]** — The gentle, peaceable husband of the Baronne, a descendant of the infamous renegade Watteville.

## Significance

Albert Savarus is a quintessential Balzacian hero: a man of talent and ambition whose personal passions complicate his public ascent. His story exemplifies the themes of *La Comédie Humaine* — the interplay between individual desire and social forces, the masks people wear in society, and the often-tragic consequences of concealing one's true self. The character's trajectory from promising arriviste to politically defeated and personally devastated figure reflects Balzac's broader pessimism about the possibility of authentic happiness within the rigid structures of Restoration-era French society.
