# La Comédie Humaine

**La Comédie Humaine** (The Human Comedy) is the title given by Honoré de Balzac to his monumental collection of interlinked novels, novellas, and short stories depicting French society in the first half of the 19th century. Published between 1829 and 1848, the cycle comprises over 90 completed works and features more than 2,000 characters, many of whom reappear across multiple stories. *Albert Savarus* (1842) belongs to the *Scènes de la vie privée* (Scenes from Private Life) section of this vast literary enterprise.

## Overview

Balzac conceived *La Comédie Humaine* as a comprehensive portrait of French society during the Bourbon Restoration and the July Monarchy. The cycle is organized into three major divisions: *Études de Mœurs* (Studies of Manners), *Études Philosophiques* (Philosophical Studies), and *Études Analytiques* (Analytical Studies). The *Études de Mœurs* is further subdivided into six "scenes" — private life, provincial life, Parisian life, political life, military life, and country life — each examining a different facet of French society.

The project was explicitly modeled on the natural sciences, with Balzac seeing himself as a "secretary" recording the "human comedy" of his time with the precision of a naturalist cataloging species. His innovation of recurring characters — figures who appear in multiple novels at different stages of their lives — created a fictional universe of unprecedented scope and realism.

## Relevance to the Novel

*Albert Savarus* is classified within the *Scènes de la vie privée*, a section focused on the intimate dramas of domestic and personal life. The novel embodies many of the defining characteristics of *La Comédie Humaine*: the detailed portrayal of provincial society (Besançon), the exploration of ambition and social climbing, the tension between passion and social convention, and the use of framed narratives.

The novel's setting in Besançon, a provincial city, connects it thematically to other works in the cycle that examine the dynamics of life outside Paris. Its focus on legal and political institutions — Savarus is a lawyer who stands for election — ties it to Balzac's broader interest in the machinery of French society.
