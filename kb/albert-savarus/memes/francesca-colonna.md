# Francesca Colonna

**Francesca Colonna**, later Princess Gandolphini, is the Italian noblewoman at the center of Albert Savarus's autobiographical novella "L'Ambitieux par amour." A Roman aristocrat of exceptional cultivation — she speaks five living languages and reads Greek, Latin, and Hebrew — Francesca embodies the ideal of refined Italian womanhood that captivates Rodolphe (Albert's fictional alter ego) and, through him, the novel's readers. Her presence haunts the entire work, even though she never appears in the frame narrative.

## Overview

Francesca is introduced as a mystery: when Rodolphe first encounters her at the Bergmanns' lodging-house in Gersau, she is traveling incognito as "Gina," the young companion to an elderly, gout-ridden gentleman. She is beautiful, reserved, and surrounded by an atmosphere of deliberate obscurity. Only later, in Geneva, does she reveal her true identity: she is the fourth child of Prince and Princess Colonna, married to Prince Gandolphini, one of the richest landowners in Sicily.

The revelation of her rank transforms but does not diminish the intimacy established at Gersau. Francesca manages the transition with characteristic grace: at the Villa Jeanrenaud party, she seats Rodolphe in her own chair and quips, "I have for the last minute been Princess Rodolphini" — a jest that confesses her love while preserving the decorum her position demands.

## Education and Character

Balzac endows Francesca with an education befitting a Renaissance princess. Under the direction of a Cardinal of the Colonna family, she has "read all through the library of the Colonnas" between the ages of nine and sixteen — a course of study encompassing "science, art, and letters." To the astonishment of Rodolphe, she knows "Greek, Latin, and Hebrew" in addition to "five living languages." Her learning is balanced by a profound understanding of social discretion: "the charming creature perfectly understood that, for a woman, the first condition of being learned is to keep it deeply hidden."

This education produces not a pedant but a woman of independent judgment. She and her husband join "the ranks of the revolution" — the Italian liberal movement — and her intellectual independence is matched by an emotional reserve that Rodolphe finds both maddening and fascinating. When he accuses her of having no feeling, she replies with Roman gravity: "I am a Roman!"

## The Portrait

The portrait of Francesca painted by the artist Schinner is one of the novella's central symbols. Schinner, who "had positively refused to paint the portraits of several women," is persuaded by Francesca to create "an original portrait for Rodolphe, and a duplicate for Emilio." The portrait represents the love that Rodolphe carries with him through his years of struggle — a material token of a distant and deferred union. It also establishes the theme of art as the medium through which love is preserved and transmitted, a theme that resonates through Balzac's larger *Comédie Humaine*.

## The Roman Reserve

Francesca's emotional style — dignified, restrained, capable of profound feeling concealed beneath perfect composure — is presented as characteristically Italian and, more specifically, characteristically Roman. When Rodolphe presses her at Gersau, she turns away to hide her tears, then composes herself with a firmness that borders on severity: "Go this evening. We meet no more till we meet at Naples."

This reserve is not coldness but discipline. Francesca is protecting not only her own reputation but Rodolphe's future — and her own capacity to sustain a love that must survive years of separation. Her command that they separate until Naples is both a test and a pledge: she is asking Rodolphe to match her own strength of purpose.

## Francesca and the Frame Narrative

Francesca never appears directly in the frame narrative of *Albert Savarus* — the world of Besançon, the Wattevilles, and Albert's political campaign. She exists only in the embedded novella that Albert publishes and that Rosalie reads. Yet her absence from the frame is itself a narrative strategy: she is the absent beloved, the distant ideal, the love that Albert has lost or is losing. Rosalie's jealousy of Francesca — a woman she has never met and knows only through Albert's fictionalized account — drives the novel's central act of sabotage.
