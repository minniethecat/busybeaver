# Cardinal Granvelle

Cardinal Antoine Perrenot de Granvelle (1517–1586) was a historical Burgundian statesman, diplomat, and cardinal of the Catholic Church who served as a chief minister to the Habsburg rulers Charles V and Philip II of Spain. In Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842), Granvelle is invoked as an ancestral figure whose legacy connects the Soulas family to the aristocratic history of the Franche-Comté region.

## Historical Background

Born in Besançon (then a Free Imperial City of the Holy Roman Empire) and later known as the Cardinal de Granvelle, Antoine Perrenot was one of the most influential European statesmen of the 16th century. He served as the Archbishop of Mechelen, was created cardinal in 1561, and acted as the principal advisor to Margaret of Parma during her regency in the Spanish Netherlands. His political career spanned the tumultuous period of the Dutch Revolt and the early stages of the Eighty Years' War.

Granvelle was a notable patron of the arts and scholarship, embodying the Renaissance ideal of the learned churchman-statesman. His art collection, housed at his palace in Besançon, was among the finest in Europe, and his library reflected the breadth of humanist learning.

## Role in the Novel

Granvelle appears in *Albert Savarus* through genealogical reference. The Soulas family, of Spanish origin, remained in the Franche-Comté "in consequence of their connection with Cardinal Granvelle" (s01). During the Spanish occupation of the region, "Spain sent men to manage her business in the Comté, but very few Spaniards settled there" (s01). The Soulas family was among those who did stay, and their connection to the cardinal provided the aristocratic pedigree that sustained their social standing across generations.

Young Amedée de Soulas, the dandy who becomes Rosalie de Watteville's eventual husband, inherits this Granvelle connection as part of his social capital. The reference serves to ground the Soulas family in the deep history of the Franche-Comté — a region whose aristocratic identities were shaped by centuries of Burgundian, Spanish, and French influence. The Granvelle connection "explained" how a Spanish family could establish itself permanently in Besançon, acquiring the local legitimacy that Amedée later leverages in his courtship of Rosalie.

Amedée and his school-friend "made excursions together to Granvelle" (s14), visiting the cardinal's ancestral seat. These excursions are part of the young aristocrats' social routine — hunting, visiting, and consolidating the regional connections that define their class identity.

## Significance

The Granvelle reference exemplifies Balzac's technique of anchoring his fictional characters in verifiable historical reality. By linking the Soulas family to a documented historical figure of European importance, Balzac confers historical depth and plausibility on his provincial characters. The cardinal represents a lost era of Franche-Comté greatness — a time when a son of Besançon could rise to become one of the most powerful men in Christendom — and the Soulas family's connection to this legacy underscores both their genuine antiquity and their present irrelevance in the new France of the July Monarchy.

> Spain sent men to manage her business in the Comté, but very few Spaniards settled there. The Soulas remained in consequence of their connection with Cardinal Granvelle. (Section 1)

> Amedée and his school-friend were his intimate allies. They made excursions together to Granvelle, and sometimes went out shooting. (Section 14)
