# Death and Mourning

Death and mourning permeate Balzac's *Albert Savarus* as both literal events and metaphorical conditions. The novel's emotional architecture is built on losses — some past, some present, some self-inflicted — that shape characters' motivations and determine the trajectory of the plot.

## The Baron de Watteville's Death

The most consequential death in the novel's present action is that of the Baron de Watteville. Madame de Watteville had contrived to send her husband to Les Rouxey to manage the estate and the lawsuit against the commune of Riceys — a strategic exile that removed him from Besançon society and allowed her to control the household without interference. The death results from an accident suffered while the Baron was inspecting the disputed property:

> "In spite of the intelligent treatment of the Rouxey doctor, the fright had produced serious effusion on the brain, and the shock to the digestion was helping to kill the poor man. This death, which would never have happened, said Madame de Watteville, if her husband had stayed at Besançon..." (s13)

Rosalie immediately recognizes her own culpability: "she accused herself of her father's death, and she feared another disaster, much greater in her eyes, and very certainly her own work" (s13). The "greater disaster" she fears is the destruction of Albert's love for Francesca — a catastrophe she has already set in motion through her intercepted letters. The Baron's death thus serves as a moral accelerant, deepening Rosalie's guilt while failing to deter her from the course she has chosen.

The aftermath of the Baron's death transforms the Watteville household. Rosalie and her mother "settled in the Hotel de Rupt, where they lived in savage silence" (s13). The Baron, passive and marginal in life, becomes in death a silent accuser whose absence haunts the domestic space.

## The Death of Albert's Mother

Albert Savarus's mother died when he was young, leaving him without family protection or the financial settlement that a legitimate son would have received. The narrator notes that "Rodolphe was the natural son of a man of rank, who was carried off by a premature death before he could make any arrangements for securing the means of existence to a woman he fondly loved" (s12). This dual loss — of both father and mother — defines Albert's social position as an outsider who must make his own way in the world.

The absence of maternal presence in Albert's life may also illuminate his susceptibility to Francesca, who combines in her person the roles of beloved, muse, and quasi-maternal figure of comfort. When Rodolphe first meets Francesca in Geneva, she is living in poverty with her aged father, caring for him while earning a meager living — a tableau of filial devotion that resonates with Albert's own history of loss.

## Symbolic Death and Social Extinction

Beyond physical deaths, the novel is saturated with the language and imagery of symbolic death. Albert's political career in Paris is described as a kind of death from which he must be reborn in Besançon. His entry into the Grande Chartreuse at the novel's conclusion represents a civil death — a withdrawal from the world that is functionally equivalent to burial. "I lay the remains of an almost extinct life at the foot of the altar," he writes in his final letter (s21).

The novel repeatedly invokes the classical image of the athlete who dies at the moment of triumph: "To reach the goal and die, like the runner of antiquity! To see fortune and death stand on the threshold hand in hand! To win the beloved woman just when love is extinct!" (s13). This rhetorical figure — achievement and death as simultaneous arrivals — captures the novel's pessimistic vision of ambition, in which success and loss are inseparable.

## Mourning as Social Performance

In the provincial society that Balzac depicts, mourning is both a private emotion and a public performance. The Baron de Watteville's death requires Rosalie and her mother to observe the conventions of bereavement — withdrawal from society, somber dress, restricted social activity. Yet beneath this surface of conventional grief, the real passions of the novel continue to operate. Rosalie's "real sorrow, which had no visible outlet" (s13) is not primarily for her father but for the consequences of her own actions.

This disjunction between public mourning and private feeling is characteristic of Balzac's social criticism. Provincial society demands the outward forms of grief while remaining indifferent to the interior states they purport to express. Madame de Watteville, who arranged her husband's convenient exile to Les Rouxey, observes the proprieties of widowhood while her daughter is consumed by a guilt that finds no legitimate expression in the social rituals available to her.

## The Carthusian Death-in-Life

Albert's final destination — the Grande Chartreuse — represents the ultimate fusion of death and mourning in the novel. The Carthusian order is famous for its vow of silence and its eremitic life, in which each monk lives in a solitary cell, speaking only during prescribed hours. Albert's choice of this order is not accidental: it institutionalizes the solitude and silence that have been forced upon him by the collapse of his worldly ambitions. His final letter to Léopold describes this state: "I could not serve in the ranks of the Church Militant, and I lay the remains of an almost extinct life at the foot of the altar" (s21). The monastery is both tomb and refuge — a place where death has already occurred and mourning has become a permanent condition.

## Death and the Balzacian Novel

In the broader context of Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*, *Albert Savarus* employs death in a manner characteristic of the novelist's mature technique. Physical deaths — like that of the Baron de Watteville — are relatively rare and serve specific narrative functions: they remove obstacles, redistribute fortunes, and concentrate moral agency in the survivors. But symbolic deaths — the "civil death" of the monastery, the "social death" of exile, the "emotional death" of disappointed love — proliferate throughout the narrative. Balzac's innovation is to treat these metaphorical deaths with the same seriousness as their physical counterparts, recognizing that in the world of ambition and social competition he depicts, to be removed from the game is, in a meaningful sense, to cease to exist.
