# Geneva

Geneva, the Swiss city on the western shore of Lake Geneva (Lac Léman),
serves as a crucial setting in the embedded love-story of Balzac's
*Albert Savarus*. It is here that Rodolphe — the young Parisian lawyer later
known in Besançon as Albert Savarus — spends the winter of 1823–24 in
pursuit of the beautiful Italian exile Francesca, Duchessa d'Argaiolo.
Geneva is depicted as both a refuge for political exiles and a
microcosm of European aristocratic society in the post-Napoleonic era,
a city anxious to please the Holy Alliance yet sheltering conspirators,
revolutionaries, and dispossessed nobility.

## Overview

Rodolphe arrives in Geneva in late October, drawn by the waters of the
lake where the "beautiful Italian awaited him" [009-Section-9]. He
takes rooms at Eaux-Vives, outside the town proper, to avoid the
discomforts of urban lodgings. Geneva serves as the stage for the most
intense period of his romance with Francesca: "Rodolphe spent the whole
winter at Geneva. This winter passed like a day" [010-Section-10].

The city is portrayed as a place of contradictions. It is a "gossiping
town" [009-Section-9] where the lovers must conceal their great and
"unchangeable affection from the eyes of the world" [009-Section-9], yet
it is also a cosmopolitan haven where Italian exiles, English tourists,
French refugees, and Genevan society intermingle. Francesca's husband,
the bookseller Lamporani, formally introduces Rodolphe to his wife at
Geneva, establishing the public fiction under which the lovers can meet
openly.

## Description in the Novel

Balzac's Geneva is rendered through its topography. From a terrace
overlooking the city, one sees "Geneva, the Jura, the hills covered with
villas, and below them a wide expanse of the lake" [009-Section-9].
The city's physical beauty — the alpine backdrop, the shimmering lake,
the hillside villas — provides a sensuous frame for Rodolphe's romantic
rapture.

The social landscape is equally vivid. Rodolphe encounters "some charming
women of Geneva, and other Italians, among them the dazzling and
illustrious Princess Varese, and the famous singer Tinti" [009-Section-9],
yet Francesca's "sublime beauty was beyond all possible rivalry." The
city's political character emerges through the efforts of the Prince
and Princess Gandolphini — relations of the King of Naples — to obtain
permission to reside there, a process requiring "the Pope's interest in
the Colonna family" and the assent of "foreign powers and the King of
Naples" [009-Section-9]. Geneva, Balzac notes, "is anxious to do nothing
to displease the Holy Alliance to which it owes its independence"
[009-Section-9].

## Significance

Geneva functions as a space of suspended reality — a winter interlude
cut off from the demands of career, ambition, and social obligation.
Francesca's promise to Rodolphe distills this: "Well, in three weeks
we shall be at Geneva, and living at our ease" [008-Section-8]. The
city stands in contrast to Besançon, the novel's primary setting: where
Besançon is provincial, calculating, and French, Geneva is cosmopolitan,
passionate, and European.

The Lake of Geneva itself becomes a symbolic link between the novel's
Swiss and Italian geography. Rodolphe's letters to Francesca — composed
from Besançon during his political campaign — recall "my arrival at
your villa on the Lake of Geneva" [013-Section-13], collapsing temporal
distance through the memory of place. The final rupture comes when
Albert, having lost both the election and Rosalie's fabricated letters,
orders the postilion to take "The Geneva Road!" [018-Section-18],
attempting to retrace his steps to the city of his happiness.
