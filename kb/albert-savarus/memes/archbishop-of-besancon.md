# Archbishop of Besancon

The **Archbishop of Besancon** is the most authoritative ecclesiastical figure in Restoration-era Besancon, a prelate whose presence at the Baronne de Watteville's salon certifies that household's position at the summit of provincial society. Though he never appears as a speaking character — he is always described through the perception and conversation of others — his symbolic weight pervades the novel's social world, linking religious orthodoxy, aristocratic prestige, and political influence into a single figure of institutional authority.

The Archbishop is never named personally in the novel; he is referred to only by his title. This anonymity is significant — Balzac treats him not as an individual personality but as the embodiment of the institutional Church, an office whose authority is inseparable from the hierarchical structure it represents.

## Role in the Novel

### Social Presence and Certification

The novel's opening paragraph establishes the Archbishop as the measure of social distinction. His presence at the Watteville salon is the first fact Balzac offers about Besancon society:

> "One of the few drawing-rooms where, under the Restoration, the Archbishop of Besancon was sometimes to be seen, was that of the Baronne de Watteville, to whom he was particularly attached on account of her religious sentiments." (ch01-section-1)

The phrase "one of the few" indicates the Archbishop's selectivity — he does not distribute his favor indiscriminately. His attachment to the Baronne "on account of her religious sentiments" marks her piety as exceptional within a provincial aristocracy whose devotion was often more conventional than sincere.

The Baronne's devotion to him is absolute: she "rarely quitted" the Hotel de Rupt, "so devoted was she to her dear Archbishop" (ch01-section-2). This devotion effectively structures her entire social existence — she stays at home to be near him, to receive him, to maintain the salon that is worthy of his presence.

### Relationship with the Baronne de Watteville

The Archbishop's relationship with the Baronne is the novel's most significant clerical-lay alliance. When the Archbishop "blamed her for being too hard on the child" (ch01-section-3), the Baronne's response — "Leave me to manage her, monseigneur. I know her! She has more than one Beelzebub in her skin!" — reveals the terms of their relationship. He has the authority to criticize her maternal regime, but she retains the practical power to reject his advice. The exchange demonstrates that even the Archbishop's moral authority has limits when it encounters the iron will of a provincial matriarch.

### The Handsome Prelate

The only physical description of the Archbishop comes filtered through Rosalie's consciousness, and it is revealing. When the Abbe de Grancey describes Albert Savarus's hands as "those of a prelate" (ch01-section-4), Rosalie's private response links the observation directly to the Archbishop:

> "I cannot remember our handsome Archbishop's hands, though he confirmed me." (ch01-section-4)

This fleeting memory constructs the Archbishop as physically attractive — "handsome" — and associates him with the sacrament of confirmation, a rite of spiritual coming-of-age. The juxtaposition of aesthetic appreciation ("handsome") with religious authority ("he confirmed me") suggests the way ecclesiastical power in Balzac's world is inseparable from personal magnetism.

### The Clerical Network

The Archbishop's influence radiates through the ecclesiastical hierarchy. Through his intimacy with the Watteville household, "the three or four clever or remarkable Abbes of the diocese who were not averse to good feeding were very much at home at her house" (ch01-section-1). The Archbishop thus functions as the hub of a clerical network — his favor licenses the attendance of other clergy, who in turn bring political intelligence and social legitimacy to the Watteville table.

The Abbe de Grancey, the Vicar-General and "the friend of the late Archbishop" (ch01-section-3), inherits and extends this network after the Archbishop's death. The reference to "the late Archbishop" establishes that this figure died during the novel's temporal span, but his institutional legacy — embodied in Grancey — continues to shape Besancon's political alignments.

### Institutional Pride and Political Weight

The Archbishop's "pride" is invoked by the Abbe de Grancey as a decisive factor in the Chapter's legal triumph:

> "The Archbishop's pride, his conscience, everything that makes up the life of the priesthood, was at stake. That Savaron does not know what he did for the Chapter! He saved it!" (ch01-section-15)

Here the Archbishop's pride is not a personal vanity but an institutional attribute — it represents the dignity and moral authority of the Church itself. Albert Savarus's successful defense of the Chapter's property rights is described as having "saved" not merely a legal position but something essential to the priesthood's identity, of which the Archbishop's pride is the symbolic expression.

### Death and Succession

The Archbishop dies during the novel's timeframe, and his death is not merely a biographical fact but a structural pivot in Besancon's power landscape. The passage from the living Archbishop to "the late Archbishop" marks the transition from a unified ecclesiastical authority to a more diffuse arrangement in which the Abbe de Grancey operates as the Archbishop's institutional heir without possessing the same symbolic weight.

This succession has practical consequences for the novel's political plot. The Archbishop, as a prince of the Church, commanded a kind of authority that could not be delegated — a presence that conferred legitimacy by its mere existence. The Vicar-General, however capable, remains a functionary operating in the Archbishop's shadow. The Watteville salon, which derived its prestige from the Archbishop's attendance, must now rely on Grancey's political talents rather than the Archbishop's silent charisma.

The Archbishop's death thus mirrors the broader trajectory of ecclesiastical power in the Restoration: the old order of bishops and princes, of authority vested in persons rather than procedures, is giving way to a more bureaucratic, politically instrumental form of clerical influence — embodied, in the novel's later chapters, by Grancey's role in managing the election campaign.

### The Silent Center

The Archbishop's defining narrative feature — his silence — is also his most powerful symbolic attribute. In a novel populated by orators, schemers, and letter-writers, the Archbishop speaks not a single word of dialogue. His authority operates through presence rather than speech, through the fact of his existence rather than the content of his utterances. This is authority in its purest, most pre-modern form: power that needs no justification because it is self-evident.

Balzac contrasts this silent, ontological authority with the performative authority of the legal profession — the oratory, the courtroom theater, the "voice now clear and insinuating, now terrible and thunderous" that characterizes Albert Savarus's rise. The Archbishop represents what the novel's ambitious lawyer-protagonist cannot attain through professional success alone: the kind of authority that is inherited rather than earned, embodied rather than demonstrated.

## Significance

The Archbishop of Besancon is a minor figure in terms of page-time but a major one in terms of symbolic function. He represents the alliance of throne and altar that defined the Restoration — a political order in which religious authority underwrote social hierarchy, and social hierarchy reinforced religious authority. His presence at the Watteville salon, his handsomeness, his pride, his ultimate absence through death, and above all his silence together trace the arc of ecclesiastical power in Balzac's world: pervasive, impressive, and ultimately dependent on the very social structures it is meant to transcend.
