# The Fall of the Villèle Ministry (1827)

The fall of the Villèle Ministry in 1827 was a pivotal political event in
Restoration France that directly shaped the trajectory of Rodolphe (Albert Savarus)
in Balzac's novel _Albert Savarus_.

## Historical Context

Jean-Baptiste de Villèle served as Prime Minister of France from 1821 to 1827
under Kings Louis XVIII and Charles X. His ultra-royalist ministry pursued
conservative policies that increasingly alienated the liberal opposition and
ultimately lost the 1827 elections, leading to his resignation.

## Role in the Novel

In Albert's autobiographical narrative, the fall of the Villèle ministry
comes at a critical juncture. Albert had spent three years in a vast commercial
enterprise to rebuild his fortune, but the venture failed. He then turned to
politics as an alternative path to restore his position and make himself worthy
of Francesca.

The timing proved catastrophic: "The Villele ministry fell just when Rodolphe
was ruined."[^1] The political instability destroyed whatever hopes Albert had
of using political connections to recover his fortunes. Wounded and sick at
heart, he fled to Naples to recover.

## Narrative Function

The Villèle ministry's fall serves as a double blow in the novel's plot:
it eliminates Albert's commercial hopes (through the simultaneous failure
of his business) and his political prospects (through the ministry change).
This convergence of disasters pushes Albert into a period of exile and
reinvention, ultimately leading him to Besançon under the assumed name
of Albert Savaron.
