# Madame Bergmann

**Madame and Monsieur Bergmann** are Swiss gardeners, retired from the service of Count Borromeo on the Borromean Islands of Lago Maggiore. From their house on Lake Lucerne, they serve as unwitting guardians of the love affair between Albert Savarus (then travelling as Rodolphe) and [[francesca|Francesca]], whose family lodges with them under the assumed name of Lovelace.

## Overview

The Bergmanns embody the careful, slightly suspicious respectability of retired Swiss domestics. Having spent their working lives in the service of an Italian nobleman, they have retired to Gersau on Lake Lucerne with an income of "about a thousand crowns a year" and a property that includes "beautiful gardens" and a hothouse.

> The Bergmanns had made beautiful gardens round their house, and had built a hothouse. The flowers, the fruit, and the botanical rarities of this spot were what had induced the young lady to settle on it as she passed through Gersau. — [001-079]

It is precisely these gardens that provide Rodolphe (Savarus) with his pretext for introduction. Drawn by the botanical reputation of the property, he presents himself to the Bergmanns as a convalescent tourist seeking permission to visit the gardens — and through them, to approach the mysterious Italian-English family living on their upper floor.

## Role in the Novel

The Bergmanns function as narrative gatekeepers: their distrust, their insistence on seeing Rodolphe's passport before admitting him, and their firm refusal to introduce him to the Lovelaces all serve to heighten the difficulty of access and, by extension, the value of the eventual meeting. They are, without knowing it, the first obstacle in a courtship that will define Albert's life.

> The retired gardeners asked, strangely enough, to see Rodolphe's passport; it was sent to them at once. The paper was not returned to him till next morning. — [001-101]

The Bergmanns' house itself becomes a romantic geography: the wooden balcony "covered by the roof, which projected deeply like the roof of a chalet on all four sides of the building, in the Swiss fashion" is the vantage from which Francesca sings Italian airs in the evening. Rodolphe, listening from a boat on the lake or from beneath the acacia hedge in the Bergmanns' garden, builds his passion from stolen sounds.

Monsieur Bergmann's curt dismissal — "They receive no one, and will not see anybody" — only sharpens Rodolphe's determination. The Bergmanns, with their "evident distrust," unwittingly fuel the intensity of a love that must be won against resistance.

## Relationships

- **[[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus / Rodolphe]]** — The persistent visitor who uses the Bergmanns' gardens as an entry point to meet Francesca.
- **[[francesca|Francesca / Fanny Lovelace]]** — The Bergmanns' tenant; they refer to her as "Miss Fanny" and describe her as "crazy about music."
- **Old Lovelace** — Francesca's elderly father, "a man of ninety, and much broken," who rarely leaves the house.
- **Count Borromeo** — Their former employer on the Borromean Islands (Isola Bella and Isola Madre).

## Key Appearances

The Bergmanns appear in the Gersau idyll sequence — the novel's extended flashback to Albert's youth. Their garden is the stage for romantic espionage: Rodolphe "at the cost of wetting his feet" positions himself beneath the acacia hedge to hear Italian voices above. The house becomes a shrine in Albert's memory; years later in Besançon, he will speak of "the Bergmanns' delightful house" with a nostalgia that puzzles his provincial listeners.

## Significance

The Bergmanns are minor figures whose narrative function belies their importance. Without their gardens — "the flowers, the fruit, and the botanical rarities" — there would be no pretext for Rodolphe's introduction. Without their Swiss reserve — the demanded passport, the refused invitation — there would be no obstacle to sharpen desire. They are the anonymous stagehands of a great romance, the gardeners who unwittingly cultivate love as skillfully as they cultivate their hothouse flowers.
