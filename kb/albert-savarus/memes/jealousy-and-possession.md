# Jealousy and Possession

**Jealousy and Possession** is a central psychological theme in Honoré de Balzac's *Albert Savarus*, explored primarily through the character of Rosalie de Watteville, whose consuming jealousy of Francesca Colonna drives the novel's tragic plot. Balzac presents jealousy not as a mere romantic complication but as a pathology of possession — a form of love that seeks to own its object rather than to serve it, and that destroys what it cannot control.

## Jealousy as Possession

Rosalie's jealousy is distinctive in Balzac's oeuvre because its object is a woman she has never met and knows only through Albert's fictionalized account. She is jealous not of a rival she can see or challenge but of an image — the portrait of Francesca painted by Schinner and the idealized figure in the novella "L'Ambitieux par amour." This structural feature of her jealousy elevates it from personal grievance to psychological type: she is jealous of love itself, of the capacity for devotion that Albert directs toward another.

When Rosalie reads the novella, her reaction is described in physiological terms that suggest possession by an external force:

> "As she finished this narrative, Mademoiselle de Watteville's cheeks were on fire; there was a fever in her blood. She was crying — but with rage."

The detail that she cries "with rage" rather than with sorrow is diagnostic. Her response to the story of Albert's love is not empathy for his suffering but fury that she is not its object. Love, for Rosalie, is a zero-sum contest in which another woman's happiness is her deprivation.

## Possession versus Devotion

The novel structures its treatment of love around a fundamental opposition. On one side stands Rosalie's possessive love: a desire to capture, control, and monopolize Albert. On the other stands Albert's devoted love for Francesca: a love expressed through years of patient struggle, self-denial, and deferred gratification. The contrast is not merely between two characters but between two conceptions of what love is.

Albert's love is characterized by sacrifice. He risks his fortune, endures commercial ruin, enters the dangerous world of Restoration politics, and accepts years of separation — all in service of a future with Francesca. His love is projective: it looks forward to a union that justifies present suffering. Rosalie's love, by contrast, is absorptive: it seeks to draw Albert into her world, to possess him within the confines of Besançon, to make him hers regardless of his own desires.

## The Destructive Logic of Jealousy

Rosalie's jealousy follows a logic that Balzac maps with clinical precision. The sequence runs:

1. **Discovery**: She reads Albert's novella and learns of Francesca's existence.
2. **Comparison**: She measures herself against the absent rival and finds herself wanting — not in beauty or station, but in the one dimension that matters: Albert does not love her.
3. **Possessiveness**: Unable to accept that Albert's love belongs elsewhere, she determines to sever the bond that excludes her.
4. **Sabotage**: She intercepts the letters, exposing Albert's private life to public view.
5. **Destruction**: The interception destroys Albert's career, his love, and his future — and in doing so, destroys Rosalie's own chance at happiness.

The final irony of this sequence is that Rosalie's possessiveness ensures she will never possess Albert. The act designed to clear the field of her rival makes Albert's presence in Besançon — and thus any possibility of a future with Rosalie — impossible. Jealousy, in Balzac's scheme, is self-defeating by its nature.

## The Provincial Dimension

Balzac situates Rosalie's jealousy within the specific social conditions of provincial life. In the confined world of Besançon, where the pool of eligible men is small and where a woman's future depends entirely on marriage, the emotional stakes of romantic competition are existential. Rosalie cannot simply find another Albert; the provincial marriage market offers her only Amédée de Soulas, whose hollow title and modest fortune represent everything she does not want.

The intensity of her jealousy is thus partly a function of scarcity. Besançon's limited social horizon concentrates her desire onto a single object, and the impossibility of replacing that object turns desire into obsession. The same provincial confinement that frustrates Albert's ambition intensifies Rosalie's passion — a dark symmetry that Balzac exploits to full tragic effect.

## In the Context of Balzac's Work

The theme of jealousy and possession connects *Albert Savarus* to larger currents in the *Comédie Humaine*. Balzac's fiction is populated by possessive lovers — from Cousin Bette's vindictive manipulations to the destructive passion of Henriette de Mortsauf in *Le Lys dans la vallée*. What distinguishes Rosalie's case is her youth and her profound ignorance of the world: she acts not from experienced malice but from a passion she does not understand and cannot control. Her tragedy is not that she is evil but that she is seventeen, sheltered, and in love with a man who belongs to someone else.
