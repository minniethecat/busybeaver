# Lago Maggiore

**Lago Maggiore** (Lake Maggiore) is a large lake on the south side of the Alps, straddling Lombardy and Piedmont in northern Italy and extending into Switzerland. In _Albert Savarus_, it is the location of the Duchesse d'Argaiolo's villa at Belgirate — the place where Albert Savarus (then known as Rodolphe) spent the happiest period of his life with [[francesca|Francesca Colonna]], and which thereafter haunts his memory as the irrecoverable site of his lost love.

## Overview

Lago Maggiore appears in the novel primarily in two registers: as a real geographical setting where Rodolphe and Francesca lived together after her escape from Italy, and as a poetic emblem of the paradise Albert has been exiled from. The lake is mentioned in connection with the Bergmann household at Gersau — retired gardeners of Count Borromeo of Isola Bella and Isola Madre — and later as the destination of Francesca's villa at Belgirate, where Albert's letters and thoughts continually return.

## Description in the Novel

The lake is introduced indirectly through the Bergmanns, the retired gardeners who once served Count Borromeo and who settle at Gersau on Lake Lucerne after leaving the Borromean Islands:

> The retired head-gardeners of His Excellency Count Borromeo of Isola Bella and Isola Madre in the Lago Maggiore.
> — (Section 6)

The most significant mention appears in Section 11, when Rosalie de Watteville's father reveals the destination of the one foreign subscription to Albert's _Review_:

> One copy, is in fact, sent to Italy, but it is not paid for — to a Milanese lady at her country house at Belgirate, on Lago Maggiore.
> — (Section 11)

Monsieur de Watteville further explains the significance of the lady and her residence:

> She was by birth a Princess Soderini, a Florentine, a very great lady, and quite as rich as her husband, who has one of the largest fortunes in Lombardy. Their villa on the Lago Maggiore is one of the sights of Italy.
> — (Section 11)

In Albert's confidential letter to his friend Leopold Hannequin (Section 13), the lake becomes a site of painful contrast between his present drudgery in Besançon and the paradise he has lost:

> The time I might have spent on Lago Maggiore, seeing the waters she sees, basking in her gaze, hearing her voice.
> — (Section 13)

At the climax of Rosalie's investigation, she intercepts a letter addressed to Francesca at Belgirate:

> To Madame la Duchesse d'Argaiolo (née Princesse Soderini), At Belgirate, Lago Maggiore, Italy.
> — (Section 13)

## The Belgirate Villa

The Villa d'Argaiolo at Belgirate is described as "one of the sights of Italy" — a magnificent estate belonging to one of the wealthiest families in Lombardy. It is here that Francesca lives with her husband the Duke after her return from exile in Switzerland. Albert's letter to Leopold identifies Belgirate as his ultimate destination and reward: if he succeeds in his political ambitions in France, he may return to Francesca and the life they briefly shared on the lake.

## Symbolic Significance

Lago Maggiore functions in the novel as the geographical antithesis of Besançon. Besançon is provincial, enclosed, political — the arena of Albert's public ambition. Lago Maggiore is private, open, romantic — the site of his true emotional life. Every reference to the lake in the later sections of the novel carries a tone of exile and longing: Albert is away from where he belongs, and the lake represents the place to which all his efforts are directed.

The lake also connects the novel's Swiss and Italian settings: the Bergmanns' service at the Borromean Islands establishes a thread from Lake Lucerne (Gersau), through the Alps, to Lago Maggiore, tracing the geography of Rodolphe and Francesca's flight and separation.
