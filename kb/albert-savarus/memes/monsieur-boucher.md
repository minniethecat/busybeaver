# Monsieur Boucher

**Monsieur Boucher** (senior) is a Besançon contractor and political figure in Balzac's *Albert Savarus* (1842). Connected by marriage with one of the great publishers of Paris, he is the father of [[alfred-boucher|Alfred Boucher]] and the head of the Boucher Committee, the political organization that supports [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus's]] candidacy for the Chamber of Deputies.

## Role in the Election

Monsieur Boucher leads the committee of voters that forms the backbone of Albert's electoral campaign. The committee includes the contractor expecting the Arcier waters aqueduct work, Monsieur Boucher's son-in-law, and other Besançon notables whose interests align with Albert's candidacy:

> Monsieur Boucher senior, composed of the contractor who expected to get the work for the aqueduct for the Arcier waters; of Monsieur Boucher's son-in-law; and of other worthy citizens.
> — (ch04-section-5)

Boucher's support is grounded in material interest — the aqueduct contract — rather than ideological alignment. This transactional basis for political support is characteristic of Balzac's treatment of provincial politics, where self-interest consistently trumps principle.

## The Boucher Committee

The Boucher Committee functions as Albert's political machine in Besançon. It canvasses for votes, organizes supporters, and provides the institutional infrastructure necessary for a political campaign. However, the committee's loyalty is fragile. As the election campaign wears on and Albert's position becomes more precarious, the committee's enthusiasm wanes:

> Alfred Boucher's enthusiasm, at first infectious, presently became tiresome. The voters in the Boucher committee diminished in number.
> — (ch06-section-2)

The erosion of the committee's support mirrors the broader trajectory of Albert's political fortunes — a gradual deflation that culminates in the catastrophic revelation at the Opera ball.

## Family and Connections

Monsieur Boucher is connected by marriage to the Paris publishing world. His eldest son, [[alfred-boucher|Alfred]], is a lawyer of twenty-two who becomes involved in the literary management of the [[review-of-the-east|Revue de l'Est]], the journal that Albert uses as a vehicle for his political ideas. This connection between the legal, political, and literary spheres — all mediated through the Boucher family — exemplifies the dense network of provincial relationships that Balzac meticulously documents.

## Political Significance

Monsieur Boucher represents the material infrastructure of provincial politics — the network of contractors, businessmen, and office-seekers whose support is essential to any political campaign but whose loyalty is contingent on the prospect of personal advantage. His eventual withdrawal from Albert's cause is not a betrayal in personal terms but a rational calculation: when Albert's electoral prospects dim, the material benefits that Boucher sought are no longer attainable.
