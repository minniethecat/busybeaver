# Les Rouxey

**Les Rouxey** is the hereditary country estate of the [[baron-de-watteville|Watteville family]], located near the village of Riceys outside [[besancon|Besançon]]. The estate — with its château, artificial lake, cascades, and neglected forests — is one of the novel's richest landscape set-pieces and functions as both a romantic backdrop and a legal battleground. Rosalie de Watteville's deep attachment to Les Rouxey drives key plot decisions, and the lawsuit over its boundaries becomes a pretext for her to draw [[albert-savarus|Albert Savarus]] deeper into the family's affairs.

## Overview

Les Rouxey was created by the formidable Abbé Watteville — the "ferocious old man" who dammed a mountain ravine to form a lake and who established the estate through a combination of landscape engineering and legal bullying. The property descends to the Baron de Watteville, though its management is neglected under his indifferent stewardship:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Location** | Valley between the Great and Little Rouxey peaks, with the Dent de Vilard at the head |
| **Revenue** | Ten thousand francs a year (undervalued; worth far more under proper management) |
| **Landmarks** | Artificial lake, dam, two cascades, chalets, neglected timber forests |
| **Manager** | Modinier, an old Watteville family servant |
| **Legal Status** | Subject to a long-running boundary dispute with the commune of Riceys |

## Description in the Novel

Balzac devotes an extended descriptive passage to Les Rouxey in the novel, painting it as a landscape of "sublime and unexpected beauty" shaped by the ambition and ruthlessness of its creator:

> "Between two precipitous hills — little peaks with bare summits known as the great and the little Rouxey — in the heart of a ravine where the torrents from the heights, with the Dent de Vilard at their head, come tumbling to join the lovely upper waters of the Doubs, Watteville had a huge dam constructed, leaving two cuttings for the overflow."
> — (ch14-section-14)

> "It was all wild and deserted, left to the care of nature, abandoned to chance growths, but full of sublime and unexpected beauty. You may now imagine les Rouxey."
> — (ch14-section-14)

The idyllic setting serves as the stage for Rosalie's deepening infatuation with Albert Savarus. When she convinces her father to bring Savarus to the estate to consult on the Rouxey lawsuit, she transforms the legal dispute into an opportunity for proximity:

> "Nothing would please me better than to spend my life in a place like this. I have sometimes heard you say that the land at les Rouxey might be brought into litigation. I will go there!"
> — (ch11-section-11)

## The Rouxey Lawsuit

The legal dispute over Les Rouxey's boundaries — Watteville *versus* Riceys — is a pivotal plot mechanism. The Abbé Watteville had unilaterally appropriated communal land and water rights, and after his death, the villagers of Riceys began contesting the boundaries. Rosalie weaponizes this dormant legal conflict to create a pretext for Albert Savarus's involvement:

> "By winning the Rouxey lawsuit you will gain eighty Legitimist votes; add them to the thirty you can rely on, and you will be returned."
> — (ch17-section-17)

The lawsuit thus connects the novel's landscape, legal, and political threads — Savarus's professional expertise, the Watteville family's economic interests, and the electoral mathematics of [[besancon|Besançon]] converge at Les Rouxey.

## Significance

Les Rouxey serves multiple narrative functions beyond its role as a setting:

- **Inheritance and class**: As the Watteville family's ancestral property, the estate anchors their social identity even as their fortune declines. The Baroness's eventual decision to settle Les Rouxey on Rosalie as a dowry (ch19-section-19) underscores its function as dynastic capital.
- **Rosalie's independence**: Rosalie's love for Les Rouxey is genuine and personal — "Rosalie is fond of les Rouxey" (ch19-section-19) — and the promise of the estate as her own property represents one of her few moments of authentic self-assertion.
- **The rural counterpoint**: Against the closed, surveilled world of the Besançon drawing-rooms, Les Rouxey offers the illusion of freedom — a "wild and deserted" landscape that mirrors Rosalie's unexpressed desires.
