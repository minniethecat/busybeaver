# Italy

Italy functions in Balzac's *Albert Savarus* as more than a geographical setting: it is the symbolic landscape of exile, lost love, and aristocratic destiny. Though never directly visited in the novel's present action, Italy pervades the narrative as the homeland of Francesca Soderini, the site of Albert's political refuge, and the location where the novel's central love story meets its tragic end.

## The Geography of Exile

Albert Savarus — or, more precisely, Rodolphe, the name under which the embedded novella "L'Ambitieux par amour" identifies him — first encounters Italy as a place of exile. Following political disgrace in France, Rodolphe travels to Switzerland, where he meets the Italian refugee family of the old bookseller Lamporani and his daughter Francesca, who have fled Milan after the failed revolutionary uprisings. This Italian diaspora in Geneva establishes the motif of political displacement that runs through the novel.

The Italian refugees are described as modest, industrious, and dignified in their reduced circumstances. Francesca herself earns money by copying music and making straw hats. "For more than eleven months have I been working," she tells Rodolphe, "and I was beginning to tire of it" (s09). Her Italian identity is insistently marked throughout the embedded narrative: she is repeatedly called "the Italian," and her cultural difference — a combination of pride, frankness, and passionate intensity — is contrasted with French manners.

## Francesca and Italian Nobility

Francesca Soderini is "the daughter of the Colonnas" (s09), a lineage that connects her to one of the most ancient families of the Roman aristocracy. When Rodolphe's friend Léopold presses him about the advisability of the match, Rodolphe defends his choice: "She is a Soderini, one of the greatest Italian families" (s10). The Soderini and Colonna names carry weight even in exile, and Francesca's noble birth becomes pivotal when her family's fortune is eventually restored.

The narrative traces Albert's pursuit of Francesca across the Italian peninsula after her departure from Geneva. "From London Albert went in search of her to Naples, and from Naples to Rome, where she was now engaged to the Duc de Rhétoré. When Albert succeeded in seeing Madame d'Argaiolo, at Florence, it was at the ceremony of her marriage" (s12). This desperate journey — London, Naples, Rome, Florence — maps the geography of Albert's emotional devastation, each Italian city marking a stage in his progressive loss.

## Italian Cities in the Novel

Several Italian cities are referenced as crucial nodes in the narrative:

**Milan** is the home Francesca and her father have fled; it represents the political upheavals of the Italian peninsula and the cause for which the Soderini family sacrificed their fortune.

**Naples** is where Albert first tracks Francesca after her departure from Geneva. It is also where the old Duc d'Argaiolo, Francesca's first husband, resides — the marriage that separates Francesca from Albert.

**Rome** marks the next stage of Albert's pursuit, where he learns that Francesca has become engaged to the Duc de Rhétoré.

**Florence** is the site of the catastrophic wedding, announced in a *Gazette* notice: "The wedding of Monsieur le Duc de Rhétoré, eldest son of the Duc de Chaulieu, the former Ambassador, to Madame la Duchesse d'Argaiolo, *née* Princess Soderini, was solemnized with great splendor. Numerous entertainments given in honor of the marriage are making Florence gay. The Duchess' fortune is one of the finest in Italy" (s12). Albert attends the ceremony only to witness his beloved married to another man.

## Italy as Symbol

Throughout Balzac's *Comédie Humaine*, Italy serves as a symbolic counterpoint to France — a land of passion, art, ancient lineage, and political intrigue. In *Albert Savarus*, this symbolism is concentrated in the figure of Francesca, who embodies what Balzac presents as the Italian character: "Francesca was quite of the Italian type, and such as imagination supposes or pictures, or, if you will, dreams, that Italian women are" (s09). Her declaration — "She is only a Frenchwoman; I am an Italian, my dear sir!" (s09) — marks the cultural boundary that, in Balzac's schema, separates Italian passion and frankness from French social calculation.

The Italian setting also links the novel to the broader Romantic fascination with Italy that characterized French literature of the 1830s and 1840s, from Stendhal to Lamartine. For Balzac's readership, "Italy" signified a realm of intensity and authenticity that provincial French life — with its salons, its lawsuits, and its electoral machinations — could not provide.
