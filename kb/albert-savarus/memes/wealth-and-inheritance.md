# Wealth and Inheritance

Wealth and inheritance in *Albert Savarus* function as the economic substructure beneath the novel's social and romantic dramas. Every major character's choices — from Rosalie's marriage prospects to Albert's professional ambition — are shaped by the calculus of fortune, dowry, and the transmission of property across generations.

## Overview

In Restoration-era France, wealth was inseparable from social identity. The Revolution had disrupted the old system of aristocratic landholding, but the Restoration brought a renewed emphasis on property as the foundation of status. Balzac chronicles this world with the precision of a notary: incomes are stated in francs, dowries calculated to the last *livre de rente*, and inheritances tracked across family lines.

The Watteville fortune exemplifies the layered nature of provincial wealth. Madame de Watteville brought twenty thousand francs a year from the Rupt family, while the Baron's estate at Les Rouxey contributed an additional ten thousand — modest by Parisian standards but sufficient to make the Wattevilles prominent in Besançon society.

## Relevance to the Novel

### Rosalie's Dowry

Rosalie de Watteville's prospective fortune is the gravitational center around which much of the novel's courtship plotting orbits. "The best informed opined" that her dowry would amount to "about twenty thousand francs a year" — a sum that makes her the most eligible heiress in Besançon. This fortune transforms Rosalie from a mere provincial girl into a prize, attracting suitors like Amedée de Soulas who calculate their marital strategies in purely financial terms.

> Rosalie's dowry would therefore, as the best informed opined, amount to about twenty thousand francs a year. So for the last five years Amedee had worked like a horse. (ch01)

### The Rouxey Estate

The Rouxey estate, valued at ten thousand francs a year, is more than a piece of land — it is the symbolic and literal foundation of the Watteville family's standing. The lawsuit over its boundaries becomes the vehicle for Albert Savarus's rise, as his legal victory secures not just the estate but the family's trust. Albert even offers to give the Baron "fifteen thousand francs" for the life interest, demonstrating how wealth calculations permeate every relationship in the novel.

### Money as Motivation

Albert Savarus's professional ambition cannot be separated from his financial aspirations. His success at the bar is measured in fees and retainers; his political campaign requires financial backing; his dream of reuniting with Francesca depends on achieving a position of sufficient wealth and status. Balzac shows that in Restoration society, love without money is as impossible as ambition without capital.
