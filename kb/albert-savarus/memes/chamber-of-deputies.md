# The Chamber of Deputies

The **Chamber of Deputies** (*Chambre des Députés*) was the lower house of the French Parliament during the Bourbon Restoration (1814–1830) and the July Monarchy (1830–1848). In Balzac's *Albert Savarus*, a seat in the Chamber represents the ultimate prize of political ambition — the point at which provincial influence translates into national power. Savarus's campaign for a Besançon seat in the Chamber is the novel's central political plotline, and its outcome — determined not by the electorate but by Rosalie de Watteville's machinations — exemplifies Balzac's understanding of how Restoration politics actually functioned.

## Overview

Under the Charter of 1814, the Chamber of Deputies was an elected body with limited suffrage — only wealthy male taxpayers could vote, producing an electorate of approximately 100,000 citizens nationwide. Elections were held by *arrondissement* (district), and provincial seats were typically controlled by local notables — landowners, lawyers, and aristocrats who could deliver blocs of votes through patronage, influence, and personal connection.

## The Besançon Election

The [[election|Besançon election]] in the novel illustrates the mechanics of Restoration politics at the provincial level. Savarus, a brilliant lawyer with Legitimist sympathies, needs approximately 110 votes to secure a seat. The mathematics of the election reveal the nature of Restoration political power: it is personal, not programmatic; transactional, not ideological. Votes are delivered through lawsuits won, favors granted, and alliances sealed in drawing-rooms, not through public argument or party platforms.

## The Province as Electoral Battleground

The Chamber of Deputies provides the novel's structural link between provincial and national politics. Besançon's local disputes — the Rouxey lawsuit, the Watteville salon's social hierarchy, the Archbishop's political influence — all acquire their significance through their connection to the Chamber. A seat in Paris is the reward for mastering the province's machinery of influence; conversely, the province's power to deny that seat is its ultimate weapon.

Rosalie's destruction of Savarus's candidacy is thus not merely a romantic revenge but a political act: by denying him the Chamber, she denies him the one prize that might have justified all his compromises and concealments.
