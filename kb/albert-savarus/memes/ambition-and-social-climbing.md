# Ambition and Social Climbing

**Ambition** in Balzac's *Albert Savarus* is the engine of the plot and the solvent of character. Every major figure in the novel is driven by the desire for advancement — professional, political, or social — and the collision of their ambitions produces the novel's tragic architecture. The theme is quintessentially Balzacian: in the world of *La Comédie Humaine*, ambition is both the mark of vitality and the path to ruin.

## Overview

Balzac's fiction is fundamentally about the relationship between individual desire and social structure. In *Albert Savarus*, the protagonist's ambition operates on multiple levels simultaneously — he seeks professional success as a lawyer, political power as a candidate for the Chamber of Deputies, and personal fulfillment through his secret love for Francesca. These ambitions are not separate but intertwined: his legal reputation secures political support, his political prospects make him an attractive match, and his romantic hopes fuel his professional drive.

## Savarus's Ambition

Albert Savarus arrives in Besançon as a man with everything to prove and everything to hide. His ambition is the product of exile — having lost his position as a minister in a previous regime, he must rebuild his career from scratch under an assumed identity. His trajectory in Besançon — from unknown stranger to the city's most celebrated lawyer to parliamentary candidate — is a case study in ambition's capacity to overcome provincial resistance:

| Stage | Achievement | Vulnerability |
|-------|------------|---------------|
| **Arrival** | Unknown stranger at the Hôtel National | No social position; subject to investigation |
| **Professional Rise** | Besançon's most sought-after lawyer | Dependent on Legitimist patronage |
| **Political Ascent** | Candidate for the Chamber of Deputies | Vulnerable to scandal and exposure |
| **Collapse** | Abandonment of all ambitions | Ambition's architecture cannot survive its foundation of concealment |

## Rosalie's Ambition

Rosalie de Watteville's ambition is less visible but equally powerful. Confined within the domestic sphere of the Hôtel de Rupt, she cannot pursue professional or political advancement directly. Her ambition is therefore channeled into the only arena available to her: romantic conquest. Her campaign to secure Albert Savarus — through surveillance, manipulation, and the destruction of his alternative prospects — is ambition by other means, no less calculating than any male character's pursuit of a parliamentary seat.

## The Price of Ambition

The novel's treatment of ambition is characteristically Balzacian in its ambivalence. Ambition is not condemned; it is admired as evidence of vitality and intelligence. But ambition pursued through concealment — through the suppression of identity and the manipulation of others — carries within it the seeds of its own destruction. Savarus's tragedy is not that he is ambitious, but that his ambition requires him to hide who he is.
