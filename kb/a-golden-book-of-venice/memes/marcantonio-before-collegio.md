## Overview

Shortly after his enrollment in the [[golden-book|Golden Book]], [[marcantonio-[[giustinian-giustiniani|giustinian]]i|Marcantonio Giustiniani]] received a formal summons from the [[council-of-ten|Council of Ten]] to appear before the Collegio in the Ducal Palace. The summons, presented with ceremony at the Palazzo Giustiniani during the birthnight fête, called him to receive the Senate's commendation for the [[murano|Murano]] crystal exhibit. Seizing the moment, Marcantonio resolved to use this maiden appearance to press a far more personal matter: his petition for the Republic's favor toward his betrothal to [[marina-[[girolamo-magagnati|magagnati]]|Marina Magagnati]], the glassmaker's daughter.

## Background

The summons from the Council of Ten was a rare honor for a young nobleman. As the novel notes, "it had rarely happened that a young noble had been summoned before the Collegio to receive such a testimonial" [016-021]. The occasion combined several strands: the Republic's interest in the [[murano-glass|Murano glass]] industry as a symbol of Venetian commercial dominance, the Giustiniani family's prestige, and the young Marcantonio's emerging role in public life.

But Marcantonio had a secret purpose. He had already sworn his love to Marina Magagnati, and he saw the summons as "an opportunity, that might never come again, of presenting his petition to ears not all unfavorable" [016-015]. For a young noble to address the Collegio on such a personal matter, intertwining private desire with public duty, was audacious — and very Venetian.

## In the Narrative

The summons was delivered during the [[banquet-at-palazzo-giustiniani|banquet at the Palazzo Giustiniani]] "with ceremony on the night of the fête" [016-015]. Marcantonio was grateful for the presence of his friend [[paolo-veronese|Paolo Veronese]], who accompanied him to the [[collegio|Sala di Collegio]]. The painter's companionship provided the young noble with the moral support he needed for what he knew would be a defining moment.

Arriving at the Ducal Palace, Marcantonio's father took his seat among the senators while Marcantonio and Veronese remained standing on the floor of the hall. The narrator captures the young man's excitement: "there was a thrill of triumph in the thought that his maiden speech before this august body should be his plea for Marina's admission to the favor of the [[signoria|Signoria]]" [016-015].

The moment embodied a central tension of the novel: Marcantonio's dual allegiance — to the Republic that had just claimed his oath and to the woman of the people who had claimed his heart. With "the daring of youth, he was resolved to claim the possible" [016-015], risking his family's expectations and his political future on a single, courageous appeal.
