## Overview

The Column of San Teodoro stands in the Piazzetta di San Marco, paired with the Column of San Marco at the waterfront entrance to [[piazza-san-marco|Piazza San Marco]]. San Teodoro was the first patron saint of Venice before his primacy was ceded to [[san-marco|San Marco]], and his statue surmounts the western column of the pair. The two columns frame the formal ceremonial entry to the city from the Bacino di San Marco and served as the site of public executions, lending them a dual significance as symbols of both welcome and dread justice.

## Location

The column stands on the Molo, at the southern edge of the Piazzetta San Marco, facing the [[bacino-di-san-marco|Bacino di San Marco]]. It forms a pair with the Column of [[san-marco|San Marco]] to its east, the two granite monoliths marking the traditional landing place for dignitaries arriving by water. The space between the columns—the very spot where executions were carried out—was long regarded with superstitious dread by Venetians.

## In the Narrative

The columns of San Marco and San Teodoro appear at several pivotal moments in the novel, serving as silent witnesses to the fates of the characters. They are first seen through the eyes of the young Marcantonio Giustiniani on the day he takes his oath as a member of the Gran Consiglio, when his father pauses to draw his attention to them.

The columns also frame the novel's darker themes. The narrative records that a patrician wrongly condemned for conspiracy was executed between them, and that his posthumous exoneration required the Republic to stage a solemn funeral pageant in reparation. In a moment of high drama, [[piero-salin|Piero Salin]] invokes the columns when he declares he would rather be mistaken for a traitor and hung between them than betray his promise to Marina.

## History

The two granite columns were brought to Venice from the East—likely Constantinople or Syria—in the twelfth century. San Teodoro, depicted as a warrior saint slaying a dragon (actually a crocodile, representing his Byzantine origins), was Venice's original patron. After the translation of the body of [[san-marco|St. Mark]] to Venice in 828, San Marco gradually supplanted San Teodoro in primacy, though the older saint retained a place of honor beside the city's new patron.

The space between the columns became the official site of capital punishment in Venice, a function it served throughout the Republic's history. This grim association is woven throughout the novel as a reminder of the severe justice of the Serenissima.

## Legacy

The Column of San Teodoro remains in its original position on the Molo today. Together with the Column of San Marco, it is one of the most photographed and recognizable landmarks of Venice. The superstitious avoidance of walking between the columns persists among some Venetians, a folk memory of the executions once carried out there.
