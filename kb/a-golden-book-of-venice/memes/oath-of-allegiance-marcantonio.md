## Overview

In November 1565, [[marcantonio-giustiniani|Marcantonio Giustiniani]], the only son of the distinguished Senator [[giustinian-giustiniani|Giustinian Giustiniani]], was formally enrolled in the "Libro d'Oro" — the [[golden-book|Golden Book]] of [[venetian-nobility|Venetian nobility]] — and swore his oath of allegiance to the [[la-serenissima|Most Serene Republic]]. The ceremony took place in the Ducal Palace before the Imperial Senate, marking the young nobleman's official entry into the ruling class of Venice.

## Background

Marcantonio Giustiniani came of age as the sole male heir of the ancient and powerful Giustiniani family. His father, Giustinian Giustiniani, served as one of the Chiefs of the [[council-of-ten|Council of Ten]]. The enrollment of a noble heir in the Golden Book was a rite of passage for patrician families: the young man's name was found among the birth records, registered by the three Avvogadori, confirmed before the Senate, and sealed by an oath sworn upon the sacred book itself.

The ceremony carried immense weight in Venetian political culture. As Senator Giustiniani reminded his son before entering the Consiglio, "Thou wilt not forget that thou comest of a house which has held honors in Church and State" [013-009]. For Marcantonio, however, the oath was complicated by a private conflict: he had already sworn his heart to [[marina-[[girolamo-magagnati|magagnati]]|Marina Magagnati]], a daughter of the people, and his dual vows — to Venice and to love — would soon test one another.

## In the Narrative

The novel presents the oath ceremony as both a public rite and a deeply personal threshold. The registration was witnessed by the three Avvogadori, "those officers of the law whose rulings in their department were inexorable," followed by "the act of confirmation before the Imperial Senate, whither, in grave procession, they immediately fared, preceded by the sacred 'Libro d'Oro,' upon which the oath of allegiance was sworn with bended knee" [013-011].

The ceremony was soon over, and Marcantonio "stood enrolled among the ruling body of the great Republic" [013-011]. But the meaning of the oath quickly became a point of inner tension. The narrator notes that "the remembrance of his vow of allegiance, just rendered before the Senate, returned to him rather as the public investiture of his rights as a man than as a claim of self-surrender" [014-008].

This tension surfaces dramatically in Marcantonio's confrontation with his mother, [[laura-giustiniani|the Lady Laura]], who demands that he renounce his love for Marina Magagnati. Marcantonio refuses, citing his honor as a Giustinian: "The vow of a Giustinian is never broken; that hast thou taught me, my mother, from the legends of our house. This sword, upon which I have sworn it, I lay at thy feet" [015-009]. The oath to Venice and the oath of the heart become intertwined, as Marcantonio declares: "As I have sworn a noble's oath of allegiance to Venice, so faithfully have I kept my vow to her whom I love" [017-011].
