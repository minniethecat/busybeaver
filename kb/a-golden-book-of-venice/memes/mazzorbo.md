## Overview

**Mazzorbo** is a small island in the northern Venetian Lagoon, connected to [[burano|Burano]] by a wooden footbridge. Unlike the industrial [[murano|Murano]] or the fishing villages of the outer lagoon, Mazzorbo was known for its orchards, vegetable gardens, and vineyards — an agricultural oasis whose produce graced the markets and tables of [[venice|Venice]].

## Location

Mazzorbo lies in the northern lagoon, separated from the main body of Venice by several miles of shallow water. It is adjacent to [[burano|Burano]], linked by a bridge that makes the two islands practically contiguous. The island's fertile soil — a rarity in the lagoon — supported intensive cultivation.

## The Garden Island

Mazzorbo's identity in the novel is olfactory and sensory: it is the source of fruit-blossom fragrance that drifts across the water to Venice. Its barges, laden with produce, are a familiar sight on the canals:

The island appears in the Giustiniani wedding narrative, its fruit-bloom scent marking the season and the continuity of lagoon life:

## In the Narrative

Mazzorbo appears in modest but telling contexts. It is a figure of speech for abundance and fertility, contrasted with Venice's sterility of stone:

The island's people are characterized by a simple, generous spirit. In the novel's final chapter, a Mazzorbo woman embodies the pride of lagoon motherhood:

> …" cried a smiling mother from Mazzorbo, proudly indicating her boy a… (049-001)

This brief appearance in the novel's closing pages links Mazzorbo to the theme of renewal — ordinary life continuing beyond the grand dramas of state and church.

## Key Passages

Mazzorbo is evoked through sensory detail rather than narrative action. Its gardens perfume the air; its produce fills the barges; its people embody the lagoon's resilient domesticity. These brief references collectively paint a picture of a place defined by growth, not glory — a quiet island that feeds the city that forgets to notice it.

## Agriculture and Economy

The lagoon islands supported specialized agriculture that Venice itself, built on water, could not. Mazzorbo's gardens produced fruit, vegetables, and flowers for the Venetian market. The island's economy was modest but essential — it was one of many small places that made the Republic's urban magnificence possible.
