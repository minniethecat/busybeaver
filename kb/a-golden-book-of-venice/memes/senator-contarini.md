## Overview

**Senator Contarini** is a Venetian noble of the ancient Contarini family who appears in the novel's Senate debate scenes. An eloquent and forceful orator, he represents the conservative faction of the [[venetian-nobility|Venetian patriciate]] — those who viewed any deviation from tradition with suspicion and who guarded the privileges of their class against encroachment from below.

## Role in the Narrative

Contarini appears in the Senate chamber during critical debates. In (015-001), he is mentioned in passing — Lady Agnesina Contarini, presumably a relation, described as "a face for a [[titian|Titian]]." His major scene comes in (029-001), where he resumes a speech interrupted earlier. The debate turns on matters of state policy, and Contarini's intervention (029-004, 029-005) is decisive — "the Contarini saw his advantage and broke in." His speech is received as "the faith and will of this august body" (029-007), marking him as a figure of considerable parliamentary weight.

## Historical Context

The Contarini were one of the oldest and most distinguished families of the [[venetian-nobility|Venetian nobility]], producing eight doges over the centuries. A Contarini senator in the late 16th century would have spoken with the accumulated authority of generations. The Senate (*[[venetian-senate|Consiglio dei Pregadi]]*) was the Republic's primary deliberative body, and its debates — often fierce — shaped Venetian policy on war, diplomacy, and domestic order.

## Key Passages

Lady Agnesina Contarini is introduced in (015-001) as a noted beauty. Senator Contarini takes the floor in (029-001): "when the Senator Contarini resumed the speech which had been interrupted." His parliamentary skill is demonstrated in (029-004): "the Contarini saw his advantage and broke in." The effect is recorded in (029-005): "The speech of the Contarini and the manner of its reception." His authority is confirmed in (029-007): "the speech of the noble Senator Contarini as the faith and will of this august body."

## Significance

Senator Contarini embodies the institutional gravity of the [[venetian-nobility|Venetian patriciate]] — the accumulated weight of tradition, eloquence, and class interest that [[marcantonio-giustiniani|Marcantonio]] must overcome to win the Senate's approval for his marriage. He is not a villain but a system personified: intelligent, articulate, and utterly committed to the preservation of the order that made him who he is.
