## Overview

**Piero Salin** is the *gastaldo grande* — chief of the [[traghetto|Nicolotti]] gondolier faction — who rises from bandit-gondolier to [[venetian-senate|Condottiere]] and General of the Border Forces.

His trajectory from commoner to military commander, orchestrated by the [[council-of-ten|Council of Ten]], shows that the [[la-serenissima|Venetian Republic]] could elevate talent when [[war-preparations-1606|exigencies of state]] demanded it.

Within the novel, Salin embodies the dignity and ambition of the Venetian commoner class — a reminder that leadership and honor existed at every level of society.

## Physical Presence

Piero Salin cuts an imposing figure, described with physical detail usually reserved for nobles:

> Piero Salin was a splendid specimen of the people — tall, broad-shouldered, gifted by nature and trained by wind and wave to the very perfection of his craft; positive, nonchalant, and masterful; affable when not thwarted; of fewer words than most Venetians; an adept at all the intricacies of gondolier intrigue, and fitted by intimate knowledge to circumvent the *tosi*. (036-013)

The description echoes heroic conventions — "splendid specimen," "gifted by nature" — applied to a common gondolier, signaling the novel's interest in dignifying characters from every stratum.

## Rise to Power

### Election as Gastaldo Grande

Salin's ascent begins with his unanimous election as chief of the [[nicolotti|Nicolotti]]:

> The Nicolotti knew it well when, a few months before, they had unanimously elected him to rule over them — as their chief officers had realized it when they had nominated him, without a dissenting voice, to this position of gastaldo grande — a position of great honor fully recognized by the government. (036-005)

So remarkable was his authority that members of the rival [[castellani|Castellani faction]] crossed factional lines to join him, undergoing a ceremonial "baptism" into the [[nicolotti|Nicolotti]] party.

### The Ceremonial Investiture

The gastaldo's investiture was a state-recognized ceremony before ducal officials:

> Here also had the gastaldo received, from those same patrician hands, the unfurled banner of the Nicolotti, with the sacramental words: "We consign to you the standard of San Nicolò, in the name of the Most Serene Prince and as proof that you are the chief gastaldo and head of the people of San Nicolò and San Raffaele." (036-011)

A common gondolier receiving a banner from patrician hands in the name of the [[doge|Doge]] illustrates how the Republic's institutional culture penetrated every social level.

### Marina's Influence

Salin hesitated to accept the position — and it was [[marina-magagnati|Marina Magagnati]] who persuaded him:

> Yet when the rival factions of the people had thus conspired to make him their chief it was Marina who had alone induced him to accept the honor. To all his objections her answer had been ready: "Nay, Piero, it is meet for thee; they need one strong and brave." (036-007)

This bond between the glass-maker's daughter and the gondolier chief enriches both characters with a cross-class dimension.

## Secret Service to the Republic

The Republic recognized Salin's talents for purposes beyond gondolier governance:

> Piero Salin had been secretly summoned by the Ten and given an indefinite leave of absence from Venice, together with a large discretionary power in the direction of his wanderings, with certain other passes and perquisites which bespoke a curious confidence in one who had been known for a successful and much dreaded bandit gondolier. (036-001)

The phrase "curious confidence" captures the paradox: a former bandit entrusted with secret missions, a commoner handed discretionary power by the state's most secretive body.

## Military Command

As war with the Papacy loomed, Salin's service earned an extraordinary promotion:

> Since the Senate hath need of his brave service and hath named Piero Salin, for exigencies of the Republic, Condottiere, with honors and men of artillery to do him service. (048-020)

The title *Condottiere* — traditionally for noble mercenary captains — bestowed upon a gondolier signals both the Republic's desperation and its recognition of merit.

He was further named General of the Border Forces and dispatched to [[murano|Murano]]:

> And so it chanced, that because of the stress of the time, Piero Salin floated off in triumph to Murano, named General of the Border Forces, with secret orders from the Ten. (048-021)

## Historical Context

The Venetian gondoliers were divided into two historic factions: the **[[nicolotti|Nicolotti]]** (San Nicolò dei Mendicoli) and the **[[castellani|Castellani]]** (Castello). Each elected its own *gastaldo grande* as leader and enforcer of guild rules.

Their rivalry occasionally erupted into pitched battles on Venice's bridges — a tradition the Republic tolerated as a safety valve, and even incorporated into civic spectacle.

The title *Condottiere* (from *condotta*, a contract) referred to mercenary captains during the Renaissance. Its application to a gondolier remained extraordinary.

## Significance

Piero Salin's trajectory — from bandit-gondolier to gastaldo grande to Condottiere — dramatizes a central insight of the novel: Venetian society, for all its rigid hierarchies, contained mechanisms for recognizing talent from every class.

The Republic needed strong men, and found them on the canals as readily as in the palazzi. Salin's story reveals a social fabric in which a gondolier could receive a banner from patrician hands and ultimately command artillery in the Republic's service.
