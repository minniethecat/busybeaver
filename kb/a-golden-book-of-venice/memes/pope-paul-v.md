## Overview

Pope Paul V, born Camillo Borghese, was the pontiff whose confrontation with the [[republic-of-venice|Republic of Venice]] over ecclesiastical jurisdiction became the central political drama of A [[golden-book|Golden Book of Venice]]. Elected unexpectedly in 1605, he brought to the papacy the instincts of a lawyer rather than a theologian — conceiving his duty as the "mere temporal aggrandizement" of the [[curia|Holy See]], while "misnaming the struggle a _spiritual_ one." His decision to fulminate the Interdict against Venice set in motion the crisis that [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo Sarpi]] and the [[venetian-senate|Venetian Senate]] would defy with "an ingenuity, a foresight, and a celerity which the watching courts of Europe not only viewed with amazement."

## The Interdict Crisis

Paul V's quarrel with Venice centered on two laws the Republic had enacted limiting ecclesiastical property rights and the jurisdiction of Church courts over clergy accused of civil crimes. To the Pope, these laws represented an assault on the liberty of the Church. To Venice, they were legitimate exercises of sovereign civil authority — rights "acknowledged by many previous popes."

On Christmas Day 1605, as [[marino-grimani|Doge Marino Grimani]] lay dying, Paul's nuncio delivered two Papal briefs "sealed with the sacred seal of the Curia" demanding the laws' revocation. The Republic's response, drafted by the [[savii|Savii]] and delivered by Ambassador [[agostino-nani|Agostino Nani]], assured the Pope of Venice's filial devotion while quietly insisting that "the Republic had transgressed no right in making laws for her own independent civil government."

Paul V declared the letter "frivolous and vain" and threatened that "the gates of hell should not prevail" to deter him from imposing the Interdict. He was, the narrator notes, "a lawyer Camillo Borghese" rather than a spiritual leader — a man who "conceiving that his duty in the exalted position of head of the Church... lay in its mere temporal aggrandizement."

## Defeat and Resolution

The Interdict, when it came, failed spectacularly. Venice declared it "null and void" and forbade its publication. The Republic's clergy, with few exceptions, continued to administer the sacraments. Foreign courts — England, Spain, France — offered mediation. After nearly a year of stalemate, the Interdict was lifted through French diplomacy in 1607, with Venice conceding nothing of substance. Paul V had overplayed his hand against a state whose legal defense — crafted by [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo Sarpi]] — was unassailable in its mastery of both civil and canon law.

## In the Novel

The novel portrays Paul V not as a villain but as a sincere antagonist — a man who "conscientiously believed himself in the right." His tragedy is that of institutional pride: he cannot distinguish between the spiritual authority Christ gave the Church and the temporal power the Renaissance papacy had accumulated. [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo]]'s arguments expose this confusion with devastating clarity: "Christ himself hath said, 'My kingdom is not of this world,' and the power of the Sovereign Pontiff over Christians is not limitless, but is restricted to spiritual matters."
