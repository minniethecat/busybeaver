## Overview

Following [[marcantonio-[[giustinian-giustiniani|giustinian]]i|Marcantonio Giustiniani]]'s enrollment in the [[golden-book|Golden Book]], a report was read before the [[council-of-ten|Council of Ten]] concerning a [[murano|Murano]] crystal masterpiece commissioned by the Giustiniani family. The matter was deemed of sufficient importance to summon the young noble before the Collegio in the Ducal Palace, where he received a formal testimonial. The event illustrated the close relationship between Venetian statecraft and the Republic's celebrated glassmaking industry.

## Background

The glass furnaces of Murano were among Venice's most jealously guarded industrial treasures. The Magagnati family, led by [[girolamo-magagnati|Girolamo Magagnati]], were master glassmakers whose innovations — including the opalescent "girasole" glass with iridescent borders — represented the pinnacle of Venetian craftsmanship [009-006]. When the Giustiniani commissioned a gift-piece of exceptional crystal work, it drew the attention of the state itself. As the narrator notes, "it had rarely happened that a young noble had been summoned before the Collegio to receive such a testimonial" [016-021].

The connection was personal as well as political: Girolamo Magagnati was the father of [[marina-magagnati|Marina Magagnati]], whom Marcantonio loved. The crystal piece thus served as an unspoken bridge between the patrician Giustiniani and the artisan Magagnati, between the world of the Golden Book and the world of Murano's furnaces.

## In the Narrative

The presentation unfolds with ceremonial gravity. Marcantonio, accompanied by the painter [[paolo-veronese|Paolo Veronese]], proceeded to the [[collegio|Sala di Collegio]] in the Ducal Palace. For the young noble, this was an opportunity that extended beyond the crystal commission: he resolved to use his appearance before the august body to present his [[marcantonio-senate-petition|petition for Marina]]'s recognition.

The Senate treated the affair of the crystal with remarkable seriousness. The narrator observes: "Thus the affair of this gift-piece of crystal was deemed of quite sufficient importance to occupy the attention of the senators, who prepared themselves to listen with every symptom of interest to this report of the exhibit of Murano, which had been read on the previous day before the Ten" [016-020].

The honor of the summons was reflected in the "increased dignity of the elder Giustinian, and in a tinge of urbanity new to him" [016-018]. Many of those present in the Collegio had witnessed the presentation of the summons in the banquet hall of the Palazzo Giustiniani on the night of the fête, lending the occasion a personal, almost theatrical dimension [016-021].
