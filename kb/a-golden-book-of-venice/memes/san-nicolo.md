## Overview

San Nicolò, formally San Nicolò dei Mendicoli, is one of the most ancient churches in Venice, located in the westernmost reaches of the Dorsoduro sestiere. It served as the patron church of the Nicolotti—one of the two great factions of Venetian gondoliers—and its bell tolled to summon the people for the election of their gastaldo grande. In the novel, San Nicolò is central to the world of the common people and especially to [[piero-salin|Piero Salin]], who is elected gastaldo grande of the Nicolotti in a solemn ceremony held there.

## Location

San Nicolò stands in a small campo at the far western end of Dorsoduro, near the Canale della Giudecca. The church gives its name to the surrounding parish and to the traghetto of San Nicolò, one of the ferry crossings by which gondoliers plied their trade. Its relative remoteness from the centers of patrician power made it a natural gathering place for the working people of Venice.

## In the Narrative

The island and church of San Nicolò appear first in connection with the Giustiniani family history: the young Nicolò Giustiniani, last of his line before the papal dispensation that allowed him to marry, took his priestly vows among the quiet groves of the island of San Nicolò. The narrative recounts how he later returned to his lonely cell there after fathering the renewed Giustiniani line.

The church takes on greater prominence in the Nicolotti episodes, where it serves as the setting for [[piero-salin|Piero Salin]]'s election as gastaldo grande. The bell of San Nicolò tolls forth the call to the election; the ducal secretary presides in state; Piero delivers his awkward maiden speech on "government" and "reform" from its pulpit. After the election, the Te Deum is chanted by the full-throated gondoliers within its walls, and the procession spills out into the Piazza San Nicolò amid cannon, bells, and the shouts of the populace.

Throughout the novel, characters invoke "the blessed San Nicolò" in oaths and prayers, underscoring the saint's role as protector of the gondolier class and the people of Venice.

## History

The church of San Nicolò dei Mendicoli was reputedly founded in the seventh century, making it one of Venice's earliest churches. Rebuilt in the twelfth century, it retains a Byzantine-Romanesque character unusual among Venetian churches. The name "dei Mendicoli" (of the beggars) reflects the historically modest character of its parishioners—fishermen, gondoliers, and artisans. The Nicolotti faction, one of the two great gondolier clans, took its name from this church, and their historic rivalry with the Castellani (based at San Pietro in [[castello|Castello]]) was centered on the two parishes.

## Legacy

San Nicolò dei Mendicoli survives as an active parish church and is noted for its well-preserved medieval interior. The church was extensively restored in the 1970s with support from the Venice in Peril fund. Though the Nicolotti-Castellani factional wars are long extinct, the church remains a reminder of Venice's unique social structure, where gondoliers wielded genuine political influence and their parish loyalties shaped civic life.
