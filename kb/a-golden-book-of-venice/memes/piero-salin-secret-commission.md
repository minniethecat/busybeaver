## Overview

During the height of the [[papal-interdict-proclamation|Interdict crisis]] in 1606, [[piero|Piero]] Salin — the gastaldo grande of the [[nicolotti|Nicolotti faction]] and a master glass-worker of [[murano|Murano]] — was secretly summoned before the [[council-of-ten|Council of Ten]]. He was given an indefinite leave of absence from his employment and commissioned to serve the Republic in a clandestine role that would draw upon his unique combination of skills: leadership of the people, knowledge of the waterways, and the loyalty of Venice's working class.

## Background

Piero Salin had risen from humble origins as a gondolier to become the elected gastaldo grande of the Nicolotti, one of the two great popular factions of Venice. His marriage to [[toinetta|Toinetta]], his leadership in the legendary bridge-battles against the rival [[castellani|Castellani]], and his proven courage had won him the devotion of his people. The Council of Ten, ever watchful for capable agents among the populace, had noted his qualities.

The Interdict crisis of 1606 placed Venice under extraordinary pressure from the Papacy. [[pope-paul-v|Pope Paul V]] had forbidden religious services throughout Venetian territory. The Republic needed agents who could operate outside the normal channels of diplomacy — men who could gather intelligence, organize resistance, and command the loyalty of the people without attracting the attention of Rome's informants.

## In the Narrative

The summons is conveyed with characteristic Venetian secrecy. "Piero Salin had been secretly summoned by the Ten and given an indefinite leave of absence from his duties at the glass-works of Murano" [036-001]. The nature of his commission is not disclosed in full — the Council of Ten did not reveal its purposes — but the narrative suggests that Piero's role involved surveillance, intelligence, and the mobilization of popular support.

Piero's position as gastaldo grande made him uniquely valuable. The novel notes that "in spite of opposition from certain quarters" he had been chosen for the role, and his authority among the Nicolotti was unquestioned [034-006]. The Ten recognized that Piero could command resources no patrician could access: the loyalty of the gondoliers, the fishermen, and the working people of the sestieri.

The secret commission transforms Piero's role in the novel. From a skilled artisan and faction leader, he becomes an agent of the state. Later chapters reveal the full scope of his service: "for brave service and hath named Piero Salin, for exigencies of the Republic" [048-020]. His commission culminates in actions that help secure Venice's triumph over the Interdict, proving that the Republic's strength lay not only in its patrician councillors but in the devotion of its people.
