## Overview

During the [[papal-interdict-proclamation|Interdict of 1606]], the Jesuits of Venice faced an impossible choice: disobey the Pope by continuing to minister in the interdicted Republic, or obey the Pope and be expelled from Venetian territory. Bound by their oath of special obedience to the [[curia|Holy See]], the Jesuits chose to cease public ministry. The Republic responded by expelling them, and their great church of I Gesuiti stood deserted — a powerful symbol of the rupture between Venice and Rome.

## Background

The [[jesuits|Society of Jesus]] occupied a unique position in the Catholic Church. Unlike diocesan clergy, Jesuits took a special fourth vow of obedience to the Pope, making them directly answerable to Rome. This placed them at the center of the Interdict crisis: while most Venetian clergy — secular priests and other religious orders — obeyed the Republic and continued offering Mass, the Jesuits could not, in conscience, defy a direct papal prohibition. The novel frequently references the Jesuit presence in Venice, including the "system of the Jesuit schools" that educated Venetian youth [022-016].

## In the Narrative

The novel depicts the Jesuit departure with a mixture of political triumph and human sympathy. The deserted church of I Gesuiti becomes a haunting image: "in this parish, more than in all the others, the people felt the crushing power of the censures of the Church" [031-002]. The narrative voice notes that "they told such tales of I Gesuiti, one thought the devils were there" [031-003] — reflecting popular suspicion of the order.

The expulsion is portrayed as strategically significant. The Jesuits had the means to remove valuable assets before departing: "it had been possible for the Jesuits to remove those coffers of treasure from the city before the interdict should be officially proclaimed" [037-002]. This detail hints at the worldly wealth and foresight of the order, even as it underscores their separation from the Venetian cause.

Some Venetians mock their departure: a character's triumph is described as coming "too soon, and missed its mark," because "the Jesuit Fathers went too soon" [031-003] — suggesting that had they stayed longer, the political dynamics might have shifted. The narrative also notes the "retreating ranks of the Jesuits, who, bound by their greater allegiance to the Holy See, had no alternative but to yield" [034-017].

The novel records that when the interdict was finally resolved, the reconciliation agreement included a "special point of including the Jesuits" [040-011] — permitting their return. However, the order had "in various ways rendered themselves obnoxious" [040-012] to the Venetians, ensuring that their full restoration to favor would be a slow and contested process. The Jesuit expulsion thus serves as both a dramatic set piece in the interdict narrative and a commentary on the competing loyalties — to faith, to country, and to conscience — that the crisis demanded of everyone in Venice.
