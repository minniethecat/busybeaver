# San Rocco

San Rocco is a Venetian church and confraternity (*Scuola Grande di San Rocco*) dedicated to Saint Roch (San Rocco), the patron saint of plague victims. It stands in the Campo San Rocco, adjacent to the great church of Santa Maria Gloriosa dei [[frari|Frari]], in the San Polo sestiere.

## The Campo San Rocco

The Campo San Rocco was a small square remote from the centers of traffic and the homes of the nobility, serving primarily as a landing-place for gondolas bringing visitors and worshippers to the shrine of the Frari. Despite its modest size, it was a site of constant activity, with gondolas arriving and departing in a ceaseless rhythm of Venetian life.

> Usually this small square, remote from the centres of traffic as from the homes of the nobility, seemed scarcely more than a landing-place for the gondolas which were constantly bringing visitors and worshippers thither, as to a shrine.[001-009]

## The Scuola Grande

The Scuola Grande di San Rocco is one of the most important confraternities in Venice, famous for its cycle of paintings by Tintoretto. The confraternity's wealth and influence made it a rival to the neighboring Frari in both splendor and learning. The Servite Brotherhood, another Venetian religious order, had its own points of rivalry with the Frari, and philosophical debates between the orders drew large crowds to the Campo San Rocco.

## In the Narrative

On a sunny November morning in 1565, the Campo San Rocco was the scene of unusual activity as crowds gathered for a philosophical debate between the Frari and the Servi. The church of Santa Maria Gloriosa dei Frari, "if only brick without, was all glorious within, in raiment of needlework and wrought gold," while the airy grace of the campanile rose against the brilliant Venetian sky.[001-008]
