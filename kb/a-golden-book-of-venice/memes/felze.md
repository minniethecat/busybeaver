## Overview

The felze (plural: felzi or felzes) was the removable covered cabin of a [[gondola|Venetian gondola]]. In the late 16th century, it was typically constructed of richly brocaded and embroidered fabrics stretched over a wooden frame, providing passengers with privacy from prying eyes and protection from sun and weather.

## In the Narrative

In _A [[golden-book|Golden Book of Venice]]_, the felze appears in several key scenes. During [[marina-[[girolamo-magagnati|magagnati]]|Marina Magagnati]]'s bridal procession, she rides behind a felze of azure velvet. In a later dramatic scene, [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo Sarpi]] uses the privacy of the felze to hold a secret conversation while his gondola approaches [[brondolo|Brondolo]]. The felze serves as a symbol of Venetian refinement and as a practical device for confidential discourse.

## Historical Context

The felze was a distinctive feature of Venetian gondolas from at least the 16th century. It was often made of fine fabrics—silk, damask, or velvet—and could be decorated with the owner's colors or emblems. The felze was removed during the 19th century as part of sumptuary reforms, and modern gondolas no longer feature it.
