## Overview

Marino Grimani (1532–1605) served as [[doge|Doge of Venice]] from 1595 until his death. He presided during the mounting tensions with the Papacy that would shortly erupt into the Interdict crisis under his successor, [[leonardo-donato|Leonardo Donato]]. The novel portrays him as "wise, beloved, and regretted," a placid ruler who died at Venice's moment of greatest need.

## Role in the Narrative

Doge Marino Grimani appears at a pivotal juncture in the novel, as the conflict between Venice and [[pope-paul-v|Pope Paul V]] intensifies. The Senate has just voted unanimously to send letters to the "imperious Paul," declaring "that Venice recognizeth for the temporal government of her states no superior, save God alone" [025-003].

Into this charged atmosphere, the Papal Nuncio delivers two briefs sealed with the sacred seal of the Curia, each addressed "A Marino Grimani, Duce; e alla Republica Veneta" [025-009]. The confrontation is set, but Grimani himself does not live to see its resolution. The narrative records his passing with elegiac brevity:

> "the placid old Grimani—wise, beloved, and regretted—laid down his sceptre of state in the moment of the greatest need of Venice, and passed on to a Court of Inquiry whose findings are inalterably just" [025-011].

His death triggers the elaborate Venetian electoral machinery. A commission of inquisitors reviews every official act of the late [[doge|Doge]]; five "correctors" revise the coronation oath; and the cumbersome process of selecting ducal electors begins [025-017]. The empty crown lying "idly waiting on the golden cushion" becomes a symbol of Venice's vulnerability in the face of papal pressure.

## Historical Context

Marino Grimani belonged to one of Venice's most distinguished patrician families, the House of Grimani, which had already produced two doges and several cardinals. His dogeship (1595–1605) coincided with a period of relative peace, but the final year of his rule saw the rapid deterioration of relations with the [[curia|Holy See]]. [[pope-paul-v|Pope Paul V]], elected in 1605, immediately challenged Venice's traditional rights over ecclesiastical appointments and the trial of clerics in secular courts. Grimani's death on December 25, 1605, left the Republic without its head of state just as the crisis reached a breaking point. His successor, [[leonardo-donato|Leonardo Donato]], would lead Venice through the dramatic events of the [[papal-interdict-proclamation|Interdict of 1606]]–1607.

## Key Passages

> Meanwhile in Rome, to the Ambassador Agostino Nani, Paul had already declared his intention, which was of a piece with the rumors which filled the air in Venice. "Only of that which lieth between; the end is triumph for Venice," Giustinian declared. [025-003]

> "Behold," said he, after due grace of apology, when the senators had withdrawn to the Sala di Collegio and taken their accustomed places, "here are two briefs which, by the imperative instructions of our Sovereign Lord the Pope, I must at once deliver to your Serene Highnesses." They were sealed with the sacred seal of the Curia, and each bore the inscription: "A Marino Grimani, Duce; e alla Republica Veneta." [025-009]

> Meanwhile there was no arresting of that other message, which came swiftly, and the placid old Grimani—wise, beloved, and regretted—laid down his sceptre of state in the moment of the greatest need of Venice, and passed on to a Court of Inquiry whose findings are inalterably just. [025-011]

## Relations

- **[[pope-paul-v|Pope Paul V]]** — The "imperious Paul" whose demands escalated during Grimani's final days.
- **[[agostino-nani|Agostino Nani]]** — Venetian Ambassador to Rome, who communicated the Pope's intentions during Grimani's dogeship.

## Significance

Marino Grimani's brief appearance in the novel serves a structural purpose: his peaceful death contrasts with the political storm about to break over Venice, heightening the sense of crisis. The author uses his passing to showcase the elaborate constitutional machinery of the [[la-serenissima|Venetian Republic]] — the inquisitors, correctors, and electoral processes that ensured continuity even at moments of grave danger. His epithet "wise, beloved, and regretted" establishes him as a model of the ideal Venetian ruler at the moment when that ideal was about to be tested as never before.
