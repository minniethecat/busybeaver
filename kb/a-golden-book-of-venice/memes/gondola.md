## Overview

The **gondola** is the quintessential symbol of Venice — a sleek, asymmetrical, flat-bottomed boat propelled by a single oar, perfectly adapted to the shallow waters and narrow canals of the lagoon city. In the 16th century, when *A [[golden-book|Golden Book of Venice]]* is set, gondolas were the primary means of transport for nobles and commoners alike, ranging from simple working craft to elaborately decorated vessels with brocaded canopies (*felzi*) that served as floating salons for the patriciate.

## Design and Craft

The gondola's distinctive asymmetrical design — curved to the right to compensate for the single oar on the right side — made it extraordinarily maneuverable in Venice's tight canals. In the Renaissance, gondolas were often brightly painted and gilded, their *felzi* (cabins) draped in silk and velvet. The sumptuary laws eventually mandated the uniform black that we associate with gondolas today, but in the 16th century they were still a canvas for conspicuous display.

## In the Novel

Gondolas pervade the novel as both practical transport and social signifier. The difference between a noble's gondola and a commoner's is immediately legible to every Venetian. The [[giustinian-giustiniani|Giustinian]]i gondolas are described in lavish detail:

> "princely gondolas they are, with felzes of brocaded and embroidered stuffs, the framework inlaid with ivory and mother of pearl, with metal fittings curiously wrought, and all that bravery of pomp so dear to the Venetian heart"
> — [009-009]

Gondolas serve as the setting for intimate conversations and dramatic arrivals. The novel's opening scene is alive with the sounds of gondoliers:

> "the gondoliers landed their passengers to a lively refrain of 'Stali!' their curses and appeals to the Madonna blending not discordantly with the general babel of sound which gives such a sense of companionship in Venice—human voices calling in ceaseless interchange from shore to shore"
> — [001-012]

The gondoliers themselves are characters — sharp-witted, observant, and full of gossip about their noble masters:

> "the gondoliers of the house of Giustiniani are unfolding, with quick, ringing, jubilant voices, vast confidential tales of the fêtes that are in preparation for the marriage of the young noble of the Council"
> — [009-009]

In moments of crisis and ceremony alike, gondolas are present:

> "while from the embassies, the homes of the senators and Signoria, the Patriarch and bishops of Venice, gondolas by twos and threes loomed through an atmosphere still and cold as a breath of dread"
> — sec25

## Significance

In Turnbull's novel, the gondola is more than transportation — it is a marker of social station, a setting for human drama, and a constant reminder of Venice's unique amphibious nature. The gondoliers' songs and calls form an acoustic backdrop to every scene, and the contrast between noble gondolas draped in silk and the bare craft of the common people visualizes the social hierarchy that the novel's plot seeks to challenge.
