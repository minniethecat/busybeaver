## Overview

**San Pietro in Castello** is a significant location in Renaissance Venice. The cathedral church of Venice until 1807, located on the eastern island of Castello, site of the annual 'Brides of Venice' ceremony.

## Role in the Narrative

Mentioned in the novel as part of the rich Venetian setting, this location contributes to the novel's vivid sense of place — the physical environment in which the story's personal and political dramas unfold. The novel draws on these real locations to ground its fiction in authentic Venetian geography.

## Historical Background

This location was an established part of Renaissance Venice by the late 16th century, when the novel is set. 9th century, rebuilt 16th century. It formed part of the urban fabric of the [[la-serenissima|Most Serene Republic]] — a city whose physical beauty and architectural splendor were legendary throughout Europe.

## Key Passages

Referenced in the novel's opening chapters as part of the festival and procession scenes that establish the setting. The novel's narrative is deeply embedded in the actual geography of Venice, and locations like this one appear as the backdrop for key scenes (001-009, 003-007).

## Relations

- [[San Marco]] — the civic and religious heart of Venice
- [[La Serenissima]] — the Republic whose physical fabric it formed
- [[Grand Canal]] — the main waterway of the city
- [[Rialto]] — the commercial center nearby

## Significance

This location represents a piece of the physical Venice that the novel lovingly describes. Its presence in the narrative reminds readers that the novel's human dramas unfold within a real city — a place of stone, water, and light whose beauty was the envy of the world.
