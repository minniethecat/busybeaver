## Background

During the [[papal-interdict|Papal Interdict of 1606]], although no war was formally declared, Venice deemed it "a time when border-lands should be looked to and bravery encouraged and the martial spirit developed" (031-018). The Senate, ever politically astute, used military pomp to engage the pleasure-loving Venetian people and bolster confidence.

## The Ceremony

On the appointed day, the newly created general-in-chief of the armies of the Republic assembled his "splendidly appointed corps" in the [[piazza-san-marco|Piazza San Marco]] amid fanfare of trumpets and roaring of cannon. The people thronged the arcades, crowding the windows and balconies, waving and shouting (031-018).

A stately escort of three hundred nobles, robed in crimson, led the way from the Piazza into the [[san-marco|Basilica of San Marco]] for the solemn dedication.

## The Blessing

Within San Marco, the general knelt before the altar "like a knight vowed to holiest service." The Patriarch of Venice blessed his sword, and the general took his vow with the ancient battle-cry of the Republic:

> "In defense of Venice and the right." (031-019)

## Significance

The ceremony served multiple purposes. It enlarged the industries of Venice through "marshaling of soldiers, building of [[venetian-war-galley|galley]]s, increased activities at [[arsenal-of-venice|the arsenal]]," providing "a judicious vivacity to the life of the people" (031-018). At the same time, it projected strength to Rome during [[council-of-ten|the ten]]se diplomatic standoff of the Interdict, demonstrating that Venice remained united and prepared to defend its sovereignty.
