## Overview

Brondolo is a small settlement at the southernmost edge of the Venetian Lagoon, where the waters of the Brenta River and the Bacchiglione meet the Adriatic. It lies just south of [[chioggia|Chioggia]] and marks the boundary where the lagoon yields to the open sea. In the narrative, Brondolo is the site of a clandestine rendezvous—a brig with orange and yellow sails waits there to spirit certain characters away from the reach of Venetian authorities.

## Location

Brondolo sits at the confluence of the Brenta River and the Adriatic Sea, at the southern entry to the Venetian Lagoon. It forms part of the comune of Chioggia and lies along the water route that connects Venice to the Po Valley and the ports of the Adriatic. The lock at Brondolo controls the passage between the Brenta's freshwater course and the salt waters of the lagoon.

## In the Narrative

Brondolo appears late in the novel, during the flight of [[marina-zeni|Marina Zeni]] and the Lady Beata from Venice. Having traveled through Padua and down the Brenta by gondola, the party approaches Brondolo at dawn after a sleepless night. [[piero-salin|Piero Salin]], serving as their protector and boatman, has arranged for a brig to await them there—a vessel flying orange and yellow sails, distinguished by the figure of a hunchback on deck, bound for Rome.

The passage through Brondolo represents a threshold: behind lies Venice and the authority of the Serenissima; ahead, the open sea and freedom from pursuit. Piero's urgent instructions to his lieutenant Antonio underscore the danger—should the party be captured, Antonio is to return to Venice and alert the bancali of the Nicolotti. The fishing-skiffs of Chioggia, described as "gaudy butterflies" with orange and crimson sails, provide both cover and local color as the gondola slips through.

## Legacy

Brondolo today is a quiet frazione of Chioggia, known primarily as a point of passage between the Lagoon and the Adriatic. Its role in the narrative reflects the historical reality of Venice's water routes: the Brenta served as a major artery connecting the Republic to the mainland, and Brondolo's position at the lagoon's southern gate made it a natural point of embarkation for journeys southward along the Adriatic coast toward Rome and the Papal States.
