## Overview

The Bocca di Leone (Lion's Mouth) was a system of secret denunciation boxes installed in Venice, typically carved in the shape of a lion's head with an open mouth. Citizens could deposit anonymous written accusations into these boxes, which were read by the [[council-of-ten|Council of Ten]] and the dreaded State Inquisitors. The system was a cornerstone of Venetian state security—and a source of terror—in the late Renaissance.

## In the Narrative

The Bocca di Leone appears in _A [[golden-book|Golden Book of Venice]]_ as a symbol of the Republic's dark underbelly. The novel describes how spies and informers used the Lion's Mouth to denounce enemies or curry favor, how innocent men could be destroyed by an anonymous accusation, and how the mere existence of these boxes created an atmosphere of fear. [[fra-francesco|Fra Francesco]] is denounced through the Bocca di Leone, and the narrator notes that even noble families' secrets could find their way into those merciless jaws.

## Historical Context

The Bocca di Leone system was unique to the [[la-serenissima|Venetian Republic]]. Boxes were placed in strategic locations including the [[ducal-palace|[[doge|Doge]]'s Palace]], and each was labeled with the jurisdiction it served (e.g., the Council of Ten, the State Inquisitors). Accusations had to be signed and include witnesses, but in practice anonymity was tolerated. The system outlasted the Republic itself, only being abolished when Venice fell to Napoleon in 1797.
