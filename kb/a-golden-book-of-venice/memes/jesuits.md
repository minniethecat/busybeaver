## Overview

The **Society of Jesus** (the **Jesuits**) was a Catholic religious order that became a central point of contention during the [[papal-interdict-proclamation|Papal Interdict]] of 1606–1607. The [[la-serenissima|Venetian Republic]], asserting its sovereignty against papal authority, expelled the Jesuits from its territories — an act that crystallized the conflict between Venice and Rome and reverberates through the later chapters of the narrative.

Unlike other religious orders that accommodated themselves to Venetian authority, the Jesuits' special vow of obedience to the Pope placed them in an untenable position when the Republic and the Papacy came into conflict.

## Structure

The Society of Jesus was a global religious order, but its Venetian province operated colleges, churches, and residences within the Republic's territory. The order's vow of direct obedience to the Pope — beyond the authority of local bishops or secular rulers — was the specific point of contention that led to their expulsion:

> [040-012] save only the Jesuits, who had in various ways rendered themselves obnoxious to the Republic.

## Role in the Narrative

The Jesuits appear in the narrative primarily as a political problem — a test case for Venetian sovereignty. The Republic's decision to expel them during the Interdict crisis is presented not as anti-Catholic sentiment but as an assertion of the state's right to determine which orders could operate within its borders:

> [040-011] special point of including the Jesuits.

Their departure is described in terms that underscore the wealth and influence they had accumulated:

> [037-002] had made it possible for the Jesuits to remove those coffers of treasure.

The military metaphor applied to their exit emphasizes the combative nature of the conflict:

> [034-017] the retreating ranks of the Jesuits, who, bound by their greater obedience, might not stay.

## Key Passages

The expulsion of the Jesuits was a defining moment in the Interdict crisis, demonstrating the Republic's willingness to enforce its authority even against the most loyal papal order. The narrative treats their removal as both a political necessity and a symbolic victory for Venetian independence from Rome.
