## Overview

The Election of the [[piero-salin|Gastaldo Grande]] was the ceremonial installation of [[piero|Piero]] Salin as the supreme leader (gastaldo grande) of the [[nicolotti|Nicolotti faction]] in Venice. The Nicolotti were one of the two great working-class factions of Venice — rivals to the [[castellani|Castellani]] — whose traditional bridge fights (guerre di pugni) were a celebrated feature of Venetian popular culture. Piero Salin's elevation to this position, depicted in Chapter 36 of the novel, represents a moment of personal triumph for a man whose loyalty and physical prowess had long served his community.

## Background

Venice's working-class population was organized into two great factions: the Nicolotti (identified by black caps and sashes) and the Castellani (identified by red). These factions, based in different parishes, engaged in ritualized fistfights on Venice's bridges — a tradition that channeled social tensions into controlled competition.

The gastaldo grande was the elected leader of each faction, a position of considerable local prestige. The gastaldo served as intermediary between the working people and the patrician government, and was expected to lead his faction in the bridge battles that were a key expression of neighborhood identity.

Piero Salin, a gondolier by trade, had earned his reputation through years of loyal service and victory in these faction battles. His election as gastaldo grande marks the apex of his public career.

## In the Narrative

Chapter 36 of the novel is devoted almost entirely to the ceremony of Piero Salin's election. The chapter opens with a vivid description of the Nicolotti gathering in their ceremonial space, the nomination by acclamation, and the formal investiture. The ritual is depicted with ethnographic precision — the chants, the insignia, the procession — revealing Mrs. Turnbull's interest in Venetian popular culture beyond the palaces of the [[venetian-nobility|nobili]]ty.

The election serves as a subplot culminating Piero's character arc. His authority as gastaldo is both a reward for past service and a burden: the office demands impartial justice even against his own friends, and his elevation places him in a precarious position between the people's demands and the government's expectations.

## Key Events

1. **Nomination by acclamation**: The Nicolotti gather and voice Piero's name for the office
2. **Investiture ceremony**: Piero receives the insignia of the gastaldo grande — the baton and chain of office
3. **Procession**: The Nicolotti procession through the streets of Venice, with Piero at their head
4. **Oath of office**: Piero swears to uphold faction law and represent his people before the Republic's authorities
5. **Celebratory feast**: A banquet for the Nicolotti in honor of their new gastaldo

## Participants

- **Piero Salin**, gondolier and newly elected gastaldo grande, the novel's principal working-class hero
- **The Nicolotti faction**, Venice's black-capped working-class faction, who acclaim Piero as their leader
- **The Castellani**, the rival red-capped faction, whose defeat by the Nicolotti under Piero is a source of factional pride
- **The gastaldo's officers**, subordinate leaders who administer faction affairs under Piero's authority

## Historical Significance

The office of gastaldo grande was a distinctive feature of Venetian popular organization. Unlike most European cities where working-class associations were suppressed, Venice recognized and regulated its factions as a form of social management. The gastaldo mediated disputes, maintained order, and represented faction interests to the patrician government.

Piero Salin's election is significant within the novel's broader theme: it demonstrates that Venice's hierarchical society contained channels for popular participation and recognition, however constrained. Just as the nobility had its councils and ceremonies, the working people had their own parallel institutions of governance and honor.

## Aftermath

Piero's tenure as gastaldo grande is marked by continued tension between his personal loyalties and the demands of impartial justice. The novel uses his office to explore the Republic's mechanisms for managing its working-class population — a system that was at once paternalistic, pragmatic, and in its own way respectful of popular traditions that predated the Serrata itself.
