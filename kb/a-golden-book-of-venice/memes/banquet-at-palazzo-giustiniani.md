## Overview

In late 1565, the Palazzo [[giustinian-giustiniani|Giustinian]]i on the [[grand-canal|Grand Canal]] hosted a magnificent banquet in honor of [[marcantonio-giustiniani|Marcantonio Giustiniani]]'s birthnight. The feast brought together the elite of Venetian society, with [[gondola|gondolas]] filling the canal from "every casa in the '[[golden-book|Libro d'Oro]]'" [009-009]. Amidst the splendor of [[murano-glass|[[murano|Murano]] glass]], velvet-bound volumes, and patrician ceremony, the banquet marked a pivotal moment in the novel: Marcantonio's formal entry into society, his mother's hopes for an aristocratic marriage alliance, and the undercurrent of his secret love for [[marina-[[girolamo-magagnati|magagnati]]|Marina Magagnati]].

## Background

The Giustiniani family was among the most ancient and distinguished of the [[venetian-nobility|Venetian nobility]]. The Palazzo Giustiniani, situated on the Grand Canal, had once hosted Henry III of France during his visit to Venice [009-003]. The birthnight banquet was a traditional occasion for the display of family wealth and influence. For [[laura-giustiniani|Lady Laura Giustiniani]], it was also an opportunity to present her son to eligible noble maidens: "there is no room on the Canal Grande for the gondolas that come to the palazzo — from every casa in the 'Libro d'Oro' — to win the favor of the donna nobile of the Giustiniani, for some bella donzella who shall be chosen for their young master" [009-009].

## In the Narrative

The preparations for the banquet are described in lavish detail. Lady Laura commissioned a set of goblets in the new opalescent "girasole" glass for the occasion — "a new opalescent glass, with iridescent borderings, such as had never yet been seen at any Venetian fête" [009-006]. Marcantonio, already esteemed as a connoisseur of Venetian craftsmanship, personally oversaw the ordering of these exquisite pieces.

The gondoliers of the household, in their gold-and-scarlet liveries, boasted of their masters' grandeur among the canal traffic. One gondolier proudly described the lantern of wrought brass by Alessandro Leopardi that would light the conveyance of "the noble lady of the Giustiniani" to the banquet [009-008]. The rivalries among the retainers of patrician houses played out on the water, each gondolier defending the honor of his household with oaths sworn by the Madonna of [[castello|Castello]].

The banquet itself involved the gathering of noble maidens who "had sat for hours in some high balcony of her palace, preparing for Venetian belle-ship" — their long hair bleached by the sun to "that rich, red, burnished gold which the Venetians prized" [009-005]. Yet beneath the ceremonial splendor lay [[council-of-ten|the ten]]sions of Marcantonio's secret betrothal to Marina Magagnati, the glass-maker's daughter from Murano. The banquet was understood by the household as "only the precursor" to a grander marriage feast (sposalizia) [009-009] — a marriage that Marcantonio intended to be with Marina, not with any of the noble candidates his mother favored.
