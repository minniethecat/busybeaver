## Overview

**San Maurizio** is a church and its adjoining campo (square) in the [[san-marco|San Marco]] sestiere of Venice. In the novel, it functions primarily as a transit point — the [[traghetto]] (gondola ferry) station at San Maurizio serves as a meeting place and a threshold between the private world of the calle and the public waterways of the Grand Canal.

## Location

San Maurizio stands in the San Marco district, near the [[grand-canal|Grand Canal]]. The campo fronts a narrow calle that leads to the water, where the traghetto — a gondola ferry — carries passengers across the canal. Its location makes it a natural gathering point for characters moving between different parts of the city.

The traghetto at San Maurizio was one of many such ferry crossings that connected Venice's six sestieri across the Grand Canal before the [[rialto|Rialto Bridge]] became the sole pedestrian crossing.

## In the Narrative

San Maurizio appears in the novel as a place of transit and brief encounter. Characters pass through its campo and use its traghetto to cross the Grand Canal, and these crossings often mark narrative transitions — from one social world to another, from contemplation to action.

> [003-005]Under the vine-covered pergola of the Traghetto San Maurizio, where she was waiting for her gondola, a sudden thought had come to her.
> — sec03, PN 003-005

> [006-011]He turned out of the narrow calle to Campo San Maurizio, carrying a beautiful Moorish lamp that swung from his hand.
> — sec06, PN 006-011

The church itself recedes into the background of these scenes; it is the campo and traghetto — the liminal spaces of transit — that the novel uses to stage moments of decision and encounter.

## History

San Maurizio was rebuilt in the late 16th century, contemporaneous with the novel's setting. The church is dedicated to Saint Maurice, the 3rd-century Roman legionary and Christian martyr. Its campo was a neighborhood center serving the parish community and the constant traffic of the traghetto crossing.

## Key Passages

> [007-002]He had come to San Maurizio to take a gondola from the traghetto, and the sun was warm on the stones of the campo.
> — sec07, PN 007-002

The traghetto scene exemplifies the novel's use of Venetian topography — every campo and canal crossing becomes a potential stage for human drama, the city's physical fabric inseparable from its narrative function.
