## Overview

In January 1606, following the death of [[marino-grimani|Marino Grimani]], the Cavalière [[leonardo-donato|Leonardo Donato]] was elected [[doge|Doge of Venice]]. A seasoned diplomat known for his gravity, wisdom, and resistance to papal overreach, Donà assumed the ducal throne at the most critical moment in Venice's long struggle with Rome. His election marked a decisive shift toward defiance, and he would become the face of Venice's resistance during the [[papal-interdict-proclamation|Interdict of 1606]]–1607.

## Background

Leonardo Donato had already distinguished himself as a senator and diplomat before his elevation to the dogeship. The novel introduces him as a man of measured words and firm principles. During a dinner conversation among [[venetian-nobility|Venetian nobles]] about [[council-of-ten|the ten]]sions with Rome, Donato speaks with characteristic reserve: "It is the eternal question" [022-005]. When a younger noble boasts of his readiness for confrontation, Donato answers gravely that he knows the man and "there is one fault" — a hint of the prudence and judgment that would define his leadership [022-008].

The political context was fraught. [[pope-paul-v|Paul V]] had ascended the papal throne in 1605 with ambitions to reassert full papal authority over Venice. The Senate had already voted unanimously to resist. The dying Grimani represented the old order; Donà represented the new.

## In the Narrative

The novel describes Donà's emergence with admiration. He is depicted as "full of dignity, of wisdom, of determination to uphold the ancient dignities committed to his charge" [025-020]. Upon his election, he scatters his coronation gift of coins in [[piazza-san-marco|the Piazza]], a traditional gesture of ducal generosity [025-020].

A commission of five "correctors" is appointed to review the coronation oath — a Venetian constitutional tradition designed to prevent any Doge from accumulating excessive power [025-017]. The formidable oath, "whose slightest infraction might bring Venice about his ears" [028-025], binds the new Doge to the laws and traditions of the Republic.

As Doge, Donato appears throughout the later chapters as the serene yet resolute leader of the Republic. When the Interdict is proclaimed, it is "Leonardo Donato, by the Grace of God Doge of Venice" who issues the solemn protest declaring the interdict invalid [030-021]. His eyes "quickly scan" the faces around him, reading men as skillfully as he reads state papers [028-023]. The novel presents him as the ideal Venetian ruler: firm in defense of the Republic's rights, yet never rash or intemperate. His judgment and diplomacy earn him respect even from his adversaries, and he guides Venice through the interdict crisis with steady resolve.
