## Overview

The Serrata del [[maggior-consiglio|Maggior Consiglio]] (Closing of the Great Council) was a constitutional reform enacted in Venice in 1297 that restricted membership in the Great Council — the Republic's sovereign legislative body — to families whose ancestors had sat in the council during the previous four years. This reform effectively created Venice's hereditary patriciate and established the oligarchic structure that would govern the Republic until its fall in 1797.

## Background

In the 13th century, Venice's ruling class faced pressure from newly wealthy merchant families seeking political access. The Serrata was a response to this pressure: by "closing" the council, the established noble families ensured that political power remained within a defined hereditary caste. The names of eligible families were recorded in the Libro d'Oro — the Golden Book — which became the definitive registry of [[venetian-nobility|Venetian nobility]].

The reform was gradual, taking full effect over several decades. By the 14th century, membership in the Great Council had become a hereditary right, and social mobility into the patriciate was effectively eliminated except by rare, extraordinary grants.

## In the Narrative

The Serrata haunts the political consciousness of the novel's characters, particularly in Chapter 17, where [[marcantonio-giustiniani|Marcantonio Giustiniani]]'s marriage case comes before the Great Council. An older senator, "of the strictest oligarchical proclivities," whispers "Remember the Serrata Consiglio" — a warning to uphold the ancient exclusivity of the nobility against the perceived threat of admitting a non-patrician bride into a noble house.

The tension between the Republic's oligarchic traditions and the individual rights of its citizens is a recurring theme. The Giustiniani family embodies this tension: proud of their ancient lineage yet forced to navigate the human costs of those same exclusive traditions.

## Key Events

1. **1297**: The initial Serrata decree, restricting council membership to families with recent council service
2. **1315**: Establishment of the Libro d'Oro (Golden Book) to register eligible noble families
3. **1319**: Further restrictions codified, making the patriciate effectively hereditary
4. **1370–1400**: Gradual hardening of the nobility's exclusivity, with rare exceptions for extraordinary service

- **[[giustinian-giustiniani|Giustinian Giustiniani]]**, Chief of the [[council-of-ten|Council of Ten]] and guardian of ancient nobility's prerogatives
- **Marcantonio Giustiniani**, whose marriage case tests the limits of patrician exclusivity
- **The Maggior Consiglio**, the Great Council itself, whose membership rules are at stake

## Historical Significance

The Serrata is one of the defining constitutional events in Venetian history. By creating a closed hereditary patriciate, it ensured political stability for over 500 years — Venice never suffered the factional civil wars that plagued other Italian city-states. However, it also created a rigid class system that became a source of social tension, particularly in the novel's era (late 16th–early 17th centuries), when declining noble families sought to preserve their status through strategic marriages.

The reform's name — "Serrata" (closing) — itself became shorthand for the oligarchic character of the [[la-serenissima|Venetian Republic]], invoked by both defenders and critics of the system.

## Aftermath

The Libro d'Oro remained the definitive registry of Venetian nobility until the Republic's fall in 1797. A parallel "Silver Book" was later created for cittadini originari — citizens of "original" (non-noble but old Venetian) families who held bureaucratic positions. The term "Golden Book" inspired the name of Mrs. Turnbull's novel — *A [[golden-book|Golden Book of Venice]]* — referencing both the Libro d'Oro and the novel's exploration of who belongs in that registry.
