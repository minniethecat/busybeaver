## Overview

The Ponte della Pugna (Bridge of Fists) is a bridge in Venice near the Servite convent of [[servi|Santa Maria dei Servi]]. In the novel, it is the site of the dramatic assassination attempt on [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo Sarpi]], the Servite theologian who served as Venice's chief advisor during the Papal [[papal-interdict-proclamation|Interdict crisis]].

## Location

The bridge lies in the Dorsoduro sestiere, near the Servite convent (Santa Maria dei Servi). It connects the fondamenta near the convent to the adjacent walkway, situated between the convent walls and the canal. The bridge's relative seclusion made it a natural ambush point in the narrow Venetian calli.

## In the Narrative

The Ponte della Pugna appears in Chapter 48 of the novel, in one of the most dramatic scenes: the attempted murder of Fra Paolo Sarpi. Sarpi is waylaid by five assailants as he descends the steps of the bridge alone. Stabbed in multiple places and left for dead with a stiletto embedded in his forehead, he is carried into the Servite convent as the city erupts in fury.

The scene is pivotal — it demonstrates the depth of allegiance Sarpi commanded among Venetians, who immediately storm the papal nuncio's palazzo in outrage and compel the Republic to post rewards for the assassins across the city.

## History

The Ponte della Pugna's name — literally "Bridge of Fistfight" — suggests it may have been a site for the traditional faction battles (guerre di pugni) between Venice's rival working-class factions, the [[nicolotti|Nicolotti]] and [[castellani|Castellani]]. These ritualized bridge fights, where combatants attempted to throw opponents off the bridge and into the canal, were a celebrated Venetian tradition until their prohibition in 1705.

However, the bridge's most notable historical association is the attempted assassination of Fra Paolo Sarpi on October 5, 1607. The attack, widely believed to have been orchestrated by agents of [[pope-paul-v|Pope Paul V]], occurred here and nearly killed the theologian-advisor. Sarpi survived his wounds, reportedly quipping "Agnosco stilum Curiae Romanae" ("I recognize the style of the [[curia|Roman Curia]]") — a reference to the distinctive stiletto still lodged in his skull.

## Associated Figures

- **Fra Paolo Sarpi**, Servite scholar and Venice's theological advisor, nearly assassinated here
- **The five unnamed assailants**, widely understood to be papal agents seeking to eliminate Sarpi for his defense of Venice during the Interdict
- **The gastaldo grande**, mentioned by the Senate in immediate response to the attack, tasked with securing the bridges and traghetti

## Architecture and Art

The bridge is a modest stone footbridge typical of minor Venetian canal crossings, with arched steps and iron railings. Its architectural significance lies not in grandeur but in its character as a transitional urban space — a liminal point between the Servite convent and the public fondamenta, where the enclosed world of the cloister meets the open Venice of canals and commerce.

It was precisely this transitional quality that made the bridge a vulnerable point. Mrs. Turnbull emphasizes the darkness of the convent wall and the isolation of the steps, conditions that permitted the attackers to strike without witnesses until the victim was discovered.

## Legacy

The bridge today is a minor landmark primarily remembered for the Sarpi assassination. A commemorative plaque near the site marks Fra Paolo Sarpi's survival and his service to the [[la-serenissima|Venetian Republic]]. The bridge was later restored and remains accessible in the quiet back streets of Dorsoduro, far from the more heavily touristed areas near [[san-marco|San Marco]].
