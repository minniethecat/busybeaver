## Overview

San Michele is a small island in the Venetian Lagoon, situated between Venice and [[murano|Murano]]. In the novel's late-sixteenth-century setting, it was home to a Camaldolese monastery and served as a burial place. Today it is best known as Venice's principal cemetery—the Isola di San Michele—where the dead of Venice have been laid to rest since the Napoleonic era.

## Location

San Michele lies in the northern lagoon, roughly midway between the northern shore of Venice's Cannaregio sestiere and the island of [[murano|Murano]]. Its position along the main water route to Murano means that all traffic between the two islands passes within sight of its walls. The island's cypress trees and enclosing walls give it a solemn, contemplative aspect that contrasts with the busy glass-making furnaces of Murano visible in the distance.

## In the Narrative

San Michele appears twice, each time in a context of death and solemnity. In an early chapter, as a party travels toward [[murano|Murano]], they pass the island just as a mournful funeral procession of torch-lit boats glides toward it, the dirge of cellos echoing across the water. The contrast between the beauty of the sky and sea and the sorrow of the scene underscores the Venetian consciousness of mortality within splendor.

Later, after a state funeral, a bystander reflects bitterly on the fate of a man unjustly executed by the Republic—observing that the [[signoria|Signoria]] would pay dearly for "the ten good years of his life and a quiet grave in San Michele." Here the island symbolizes the peace of an honorable death that the Republic's justice had denied him.

## Legacy

In 1807, under Napoleonic rule, San Michele was designated as Venice's municipal cemetery, and the canal separating it from the neighboring islet of San Cristoforo was filled in to create a single larger burial island. The church of San Michele in Isola, designed by Mauro Codussi in the 1460s, was one of the first Renaissance churches in Venice and still stands. The island remains an active cemetery and is the resting place of notable figures including Igor Stravinsky, Ezra Pound, and Sergei Diaghilev.
