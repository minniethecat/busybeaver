## Overview

Don Ambrogio Morelli was a Venetian priest and the first tutor to the young Pierino, who would later become the renowned [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo Sarpi]]. He served as Abbé at the school of San Marcuolo and was the brother of [[donna-isabella|Donna Isabella]], [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo]]'s mother.

## Role in the Narrative

Don Ambrogio first appears escorting his sister into the Church of the Servi for the great disputation where the thirteen-year-old Pierino is to make his public debut as a theological disputant. Throughout the contest, [[donna-isabella|Donna Isabella]] reads the fortunes of the debate in her brother's face with "painful intensity" [002-010].

When Fra Teodoro of [[frari|the Frari]] presents a seemingly unanswerable argument, Don Ambrogio reassures his anxious sister: "Nay, have no fear, Fra Teodoro is not incontrovertible, and the Servi have better methods" [002-011]. Asked to name the respondent, he answers with visible hesitation: "It is a brother who hath but entered their order yesterday, by name Pierino—nay, [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo]]. He is reputed learned" [002-012]. He repeats the words "for his own encouragement, with a heart less brave than he could have wished" [002-012].

As the boy triumphs, Don Ambrogio's face glows with pride and he counsels his sister: "Nay, have no fear; the boy is a wonder" [002-019]. It is acknowledged that young Pierino's education had long since "passed beyond the limits of Don Ambrogio's modest attainments," at which point the more learned [[fra-gianmaria|Fra Gianmaria]] became his mentor [002-020]. Nevertheless, Don Ambrogio remains a steady, reassuring presence for his sister, nodding his approval as she composes herself to accept the path her son has chosen [002-021].

Some years later, when [[fra-gianmaria|Fra Gianmaria]] marvels at the young friar's inexplicable learning, another friar suggests, "It is some lesson which Don Ambrogio hath taught him," to which [[fra-gianmaria|Fra Gianmaria]] replies, "Not so—for Don Ambrogio hath limited understanding" [003-004]. This exchange, while diminishing Don Ambrogio's scholarly standing, underscores his foundational role as the boy's first teacher.

## Historical Context

Don Ambrogio Morelli appears to be a fictional character, though he represents the class of parish priests who provided elementary education to Venetian boys in the sixteenth century. The school of San Marcuolo (the parish church of San Marcuola on the [[grand-canal|Grand Canal]]) is a real Venetian church. The character's limitations as a scholar serve to emphasize the prodigious nature of [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo]]'s intellect, which quickly outstripped ordinary tutelage.

## Relations

- **[[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo Sarpi]]** — Don Ambrogio was the boy Pierino's first tutor, teaching him the foundations before the limits of his own knowledge were surpassed.
- **[[fra-giulio|Fra Giulio]]** — The two men share a protective affection for the young [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo]]; [[fra-giulio|Fra Giulio]] comes to sit beside Don Ambrogio and [[donna-isabella|Donna Isabella]] at the disputation.
- **[[fra-gianmaria|Fra Gianmaria]]** — The more learned friar who became Pierino's teacher after he outgrew Don Ambrogio's instruction.

## Significance

Don Ambrogio Morelli plays a modest but meaningful role as the novel's first witness to [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo Sarpi]]'s extraordinary gifts. His combination of pride in his pupil and anxiety about the ordeal the boy faces humanizes the early chapters' depiction of intellectual precocity. Through his relationship with his sister [[donna-isabella|Donna Isabella]], he also provides a window into the family dynamics surrounding the young prodigy's entry into religious life.
