## Overview

The **Papal Interdict Proclamation of 1606** was the most serious confrontation between the [[la-serenissima|Republic of Venice]] and the [[curia|Papacy]] in the early modern period.

[[pope-paul-v|Pope Paul V]], angered by Venetian laws restricting Church property rights and the trial of clergymen in civil courts, placed the entire Republic under interdict — effectively excommunicating the state.

Venice, under the theological guidance of [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo Sarpi]], defiantly refused to submit, asserting the sovereignty of temporal power against ecclesiastical authority. The crisis became the defining public moment of [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Sarpi]]'s career and a landmark in the history of church-state relations.

## Role in the Narrative

The interdict crisis forms the political backdrop of the novel's later sections. While the romantic plot involving [[marcantonio-giustiniani|Marcantonio Giustiniani]] and [[marina-magagnati|Marina Magagnati]] unfolds, the interdict serves as a test of Venetian character:

> The terrible Interdict was already printed and would soon be proclaimed. (030-009)

The moment of proclamation is one of the novel's most dramatic turning points, forcing every character — noble, clergy, and commoner — to choose between loyalty to Rome and loyalty to Venice.

The crisis also precipitated military preparations, including the gift of a [[venetian-war-galley|war galley]] noted in (026-014), and elevated [[piero-salin|Piero Salin]] to military command.

## Historical Background

The crisis began over two Venetian laws: one restricting the establishment of new churches, another asserting civil jurisdiction over clergy accused of crimes. [[pope-paul-v|Pope Paul V]] demanded their repeal; Venice refused.

When the interdict was proclaimed, Venice ordered its clergy to continue services — an act of unprecedented defiance. [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Sarpi]]'s theological defense of secular sovereignty became a landmark in political thought.

## Key Passages

The interdict's imminence is announced in (030-009): "the terrible Interdict was already printed." The [[venetian-war-galley|war galley]] gift is noted in (026-014). [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Sarpi]]'s central role is emphasized throughout: "The victory had been won by [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo Sarpi]]" (003-001).

## Significance

The Papal Interdict Proclamation is the novel's supreme test of Venetian identity — a moment when the Republic chooses between submission to Rome and assertion of its own sovereignty. It crystallizes the central tension between faith and state, church and republic, that runs through the entire narrative.
