## Overview

The Porta della Carta is the grand ceremonial entrance to the Ducal Palace in Venice. Built in 1438 by Giovanni and Bartolomeo Bon, it connects the palace courtyard directly to the [[giant-stairway|Giant Stairway (Scala dei Giganti)]] where the newly elected Doge was crowned. Its name may derive from the clerks (cartari) who worked nearby, or from the practice of posting public decrees on its doors.

## Location

Situated on the western side of the Ducal Palace, the Porta della Carta opens from the [[piazzetta|Piazzetta San Marco]] into the inner courtyard. It sits between the Basilica di [[san-marco|San Marco]] and the waterfront of the [[bacino-di-san-marco|Bacino di San Marco]], near the Portico di Zen.

## In the Narrative

The Porta della Carta appears in Chapter 13 of the novel, in a scene where [[marcantonio-giustiniani|Marcantonio Giustiniani]] and his father, a member of the [[council-of-ten|Council of Ten]], enter the Ducal Palace. [[giustinian-giustiniani|The elder Giustiniani]], with characteristic gravity, pauses before the gate to instruct his son on the symbolic significance of the palace's sculptural program.

## History

The Porta della Carta was commissioned by Doge Francesco Foscari in 1438 and designed by the sculptors Giovanni and Bartolomeo Bon. Carved in Istrian stone, its elaborate Gothic decoration includes a portrait of Foscari kneeling before the Lion of St. Mark, flanked by allegorical figures of Justice, Courage, Prudence, Hope, and Charity.

The gate served as the ceremonial entrance through which newly elected doges ascended the Giant Stairway to receive the corno ducale, symbolizing their assumption of office. Public decrees, proclamations, and official notices were affixed to its doors — a practice referenced indirectly in the novel when the Signori della Notte post notices on San Marco and the [[rialto|Rialto]].

## Architecture and Art

The gate's sculptural program represents the ideology of the [[la-serenissima|Venetian Republic]]: the enthroned figure of Justice sits between the Lions of St. Mark, with Courage, Prudence, Hope, and Charity in attendance. The Doge Foscari kneels in reverence before the winged Lion — a visual statement of the subordination of the individual ruler to the Republic's sovereign symbol.

Each figure is carved in high relief in Istrian stone, with delicate Gothic tracery framing the arched portal. The gate connects to the portico of the Ducal Palace and opens directly onto the base of the Giant Stairway.

## Legacy

The Porta della Carta survives today as part of the Ducal Palace complex, a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the most visited museums in Venice. Though the flow of petitioners, senators, and merchants that Mrs. Turnbull describes is long gone, the gate remains a powerful visual statement of Venetian Republican ideology.
