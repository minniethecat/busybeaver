# Santorio

Santorio was a Venetian physician, referred to as "the learned Professor Santorio" [032-004] and "the great Santorio" [032-015], who attended [[marina-magagnati|Marina Magagnati]] during her severe mental collapse in the aftermath of the [[papal-interdict|Papal Interdict]].

## Treatment of Marina Magagnati

When [[marina-magagnati|Marina]] fell into a state of catatonic depression — lying "chiefly with closed eyes in the cavernous darkness of the great bed" [032-014] — Santorio was summoned. He advised rest as the initial treatment: "not yet; the shadow hath not left her eyes. Let her forget" [032-014].

When this approach failed to produce improvement, Santorio grew concerned. "She will not forget," he said in despair. "We must try to rouse her. Let her child be brought" [032-015]. The strategy briefly elicited a response — [[marina-magagnati|Marina]] half-opened her arms — but she fell back with a cry of grief.

## Diagnosis

Santorio recognized that [[marina-magagnati|Marina]]'s illness was bound up with religious terror. When she became fixated on the question of whether a Pope's acts were infallible and if a succeeding Pope could undo acts of mercy, Santorio confirmed "Yes, yes, it is true" [032-017], then advised [[marcantonio-giustiniani|Marcantonio]] privately: "We cannot keep her mind from it; it is essential to calm it with the right view — no argument, it might induce the most dangerous excitement. Send for [[fra-francesco|Fra Francesco]]" [032-019].

When [[marina-magagnati|Marina]] later attempted to engage [[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo]] in theological discussion about the Interdict, Santorio observed "She is tortured out of reverence" [035-013], recognizing that her suffering sprang not from rebellion against the Church but from an excess of pious submission.

## Role in the Narrative

Santorio appears as a figure of medical authority in the novel, representing the best that Venetian medicine could offer. Yet his limitations are also clear: he cannot cure a sickness of the soul. His recommendation to summon [[fra-francesco|Fra Francesco]] — a simpler, more pastoral confessor — acknowledges that [[marina-magagnati|Marina]]'s affliction required spiritual rather than purely medical intervention.
