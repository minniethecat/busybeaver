## Background

During the [[papal-interdict|Papal Interdict of 1606]], the Jesuit fathers had been expelled from Venice. Their great church, I Gesuiti, stood deserted — yet its doors were kept "ceaselessly open for a refuge" (031-002). The parish of I Gesuiti had been the centre of the disaffection, and the people were anxious that the consolations of religion might be taken from them.

## Fra Paolo's Ministry

[[fra-paolo-sarpi|Fra Paolo Sarpi]], now Consultore to the Republic, came daily to the Gesuiti to speak to the people. The crowd flocked to hear him — "drawn partly by curiosity to hear a man of whom it is whispered that he has just been individually put under the greater excommunication by the [[venetian-inquisition|Holy Inquisition]], because of his attitude in this quarrel" (031-002).

The gatherings drew people from all stations of Venetian life. A "sprinkling of red-robed senators" stood among the crowd, which was "picturesque, shabby, malodorous, composed chiefly of young women with bright-eyed babies" — yet also contained "a face pathetic in its questioning, to whom the answer meant life or death" (031-004).

## The Sermons

Fra Paolo did not speak from the pulpit. He stood beside a table in the nave, opening a great vellum-bound volume with massive golden clasps from the library of the Servi (031-007). He beckoned the people closer: "Come nearer, so that all may hear; put the old people and the little ones nearest" (031-008).

His words were "strong and beautiful, like an old poem, and in their own Venetian — not in the Latin which had been made for the great ones" (031-010). He read from an ancient book, written "long ago — before the Bishop of Altinum fled with his people to Torcello and built the old Duomo; before Venice began to be" (031-011).

> "It is all very simple. Love God and pray to him, and be faithful in your duty. And he will keep you happy and safe from harm." (031-016)

## Significance

The daily ministry demonstrated Fra Paolo's pastoral commitment to ordinary Venetians. While the Senate valued his intellectual gifts, it was at the Gesuiti that the "most sacred side of his reserved nature" found expression, revealing "the marvelous poise of will and judgment which kept him unspoiled in spite of intellectual gifts that would have ruined him without his absolute dependence on the One Supreme" (031-006).
