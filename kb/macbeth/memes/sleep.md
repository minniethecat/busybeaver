# Sleep

Sleep is not merely a recurring image in *Macbeth* but one of the play's central moral arguments. Shakespeare presents sleep as a gift of the natural order — "innocent," restorative, the "death of each day's life" — and then shows it systematically destroyed by the violation of that order. From Lady Macbeth's pre-murder invocation of "swinish sleep" to Macbeth's hallucinated voice crying "Sleep no more," from the Porter's comic meditation on drink-induced sleep to Lady Macbeth's tragic sleepwalking, the motif traces an arc from natural rest through violated slumber to pathological sleeplessness. By the play's end, both Macbeth and Lady Macbeth are denied sleep: he in perpetual wakeful paranoia, she in a waking nightmare that mimics sleep but offers no rest.

## Sleep Before the Murder: Dramatic Prefiguration

Shakespeare plants the sleep motif early, creating dramatic irony through characters who speak of sleep as a certainty they will not possess.

### Lady Macbeth's Plan (Act I, Scene VII)

When Lady Macbeth outlines the plan to murder Duncan, she frames sleep as the vulnerability she will exploit:

> When in swinish sleep / Their drenched natures lie as in a death... (007-011)

The phrase "swinish sleep" degrades the grooms to animal level, but "as in a death" is the line's true weight — it prefigures the play's central equation of sleep with death, an equation that will return with terrible irony when Macbeth later calls sleep "death's counterfeit" (010-036).

She continues:

> When we have mark'd with blood those sleepy two / Of his own chamber... (007-012)

"Sleepy" here is casual, almost dismissive. Lady Macbeth treats sleep as a mere inconvenience to be managed. The play will prove her catastrophically wrong.

### Banquo's Restless Night (Act II, Scene I)

On the night of the murder, Banquo cannot sleep:

> A heavy summons lies like lead upon me, / And yet I would not sleep. (008-012)

The natural order is already disturbed before the murder occurs. Banquo's "merciful powers, / Restrain in me the cursed thoughts that nature / Gives way to in repose" (008-013) links sleep to the temptation to evil — a connection that will define Macbeth's experience after the murder.

## The Murder of Sleep (Act II, Scene II)

The play's definitive statement on sleep comes immediately after Duncan's murder, in one of Shakespeare's most concentrated passages of motif-writing.

### The Hallucinated Voice

Macbeth, returning from Duncan's chamber with bloody hands, reports:

> Methought I heard a voice cry, "Sleep no more! / Macbeth does murder sleep," — the innocent sleep; / Sleep that knits up the ravell'd sleave of care, / The death of each day's life, sore labour's bath, / Balm of hurt minds, great nature's second course, / Chief nourisher in life's feast. (009-022)

This passage is structured as a blazon of sleep — a catalogue of its attributes — but framed as an elegy. Each phrase names what has been destroyed:

| Attribute | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| "knits up the ravell'd sleave of care" | Sleep mends the frayed fabric of daily worry |
| "death of each day's life" | Sleep is the daily death that makes renewal possible |
| "sore labour's bath" | Sleep cleanses physical exhaustion |
| "balm of hurt minds" | Sleep heals psychological wounds |
| "great nature's second course" | Sleep is nature's main restorative, after food |
| "chief nourisher in life's feast" | Sleep is the most essential sustenance |

The rhetorical structure is devastating: the more reasons Shakespeare gives that sleep is essential to human life, the more absolute Macbeth's loss becomes. He has murdered not just Duncan but the principle of rest itself.

### The Universal Curse

Macbeth reports that the voice did not speak only to him but to "all the house":

> "Glamis hath murder'd sleep, and therefore Cawdor / Shall sleep no more. Macbeth shall sleep no more!" (009-024)

The progression through Macbeth's three titles — Glamis, Cawdor, Macbeth — universalises the curse. His entire identity, past and present, is condemned to sleeplessness. The repetition of "no more" echoes forward to the "Tomorrow" speech, where "no more" becomes the refrain of absolute negation.

### Sleep Disturbed

Even the other characters in the castle feel the disturbance. Macbeth recalls:

> There's one did laugh in's sleep, and one cried, "Murder!" (009-016)

The sleeping grooms laugh and cry out — sleep in the castle has become troubled, prophetic. The natural boundary between sleep and waking, like the boundary between life and death, has been violated.

### Lady Macbeth's Dismissal

Lady Macbeth, still confident, dismisses the metaphysical weight of Macbeth's vision:

> The sleeping and the dead / Are but as pictures. (009-027)

This is the philosophy of the unrepentant: sleep and death are mere images, not realities. She reinforces this with physical pragmatism: "smear / The sleepy grooms with blood" (009-025). For her, sleep is still a tool to be manipulated. But her own sleepwalking in Act V will prove that the sleeping and the dead are not "but as pictures" — they are forces that cannot be managed away.

## Sleep Denied: The Waking Nightmare (Acts III–IV)

After the murder, Macbeth enters a state of perpetual wakefulness that is also a kind of living nightmare.

### "Ere we will eat our meal in fear, and sleep" (Act III, Scene II)

Macbeth tells Lady Macbeth:

> Ere we will eat our meal in fear, and sleep / In the affliction of these terrible dreams / That shake us nightly. (015-007)

He can sleep, but sleep brings no rest — only "terrible dreams." This is the intermediate stage: sleep is not yet fully denied, but it has ceased to be restorative. The "balm of hurt minds" has turned to poison.

### "You lack the season of all natures, sleep" (Act III, Scene IV)

After Banquo's ghost appears at the feast, Lady Macbeth says:

> You lack the season of all natures, sleep. (017-030)

"Season" carries the double sense of "time" and "preservative" (as in seasoning). Sleep is the preservative of human nature; without it, Macbeth's nature spoils. She then says, "Come, we'll to sleep" (017-031), but the invitation rings hollow — sleep has become something they chase but cannot catch.

## The Sleepwalking Scene (Act V, Scene I)

The sleep motif reaches its tragic culmination in Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking — the play's most sustained and devastating exploration of the relationship between sleep and guilt.

### The Condition

The Doctor and Gentlewoman observe a condition that defies medical category:

> I have seen her rise from her bed... yet all this while in a most fast sleep. (021-003)

The Doctor's diagnosis captures the paradox:

> A great perturbation in nature, to receive at once the benefit of sleep, and do the effects of watching. (021-004)

Lady Macbeth is asleep — she receives the "benefit" of the closed eye — but she performs the "effects of watching": walking, writing, speaking. Sleep and waking have collapsed into each other. This is what it means to "murder sleep": the category itself breaks down.

### The Hand-Washing

What she does in this state is the play's definitive image of guilt:

> It is an accustomed action with her, to seem thus washing her hands. I have known her continue in this a quarter of an hour. (021-013)

The hand-washing directly reverses her earlier confidence — "A little water clears us of this deed" (009-030) — by showing that no quantity of water or time can clear what she has done. Sleep, which should bring oblivion, instead forces her to re-enact the crime.

Her cry, "Out, damned spot! out, I say!" (021-016), and "What, will these hands ne'er be clean?" (021-018), and finally "All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand" (021-021) — these are the retort to her Act II dismissal. The spot is not on her hand but in her mind, and sleep, far from washing it away, keeps it perpetually before her.

### The Final "To Bed"

Lady Macbeth's exit line is a triple repetition:

> To bed, to bed, to bed. (021-029)

The word "bed" — the site of sleep, of Duncan's murder, of her own eventual death — becomes an incantation. It echoes the triple "tomorrow" that Macbeth will speak in Scene V, a matching incantation of meaninglessness. And her final words in the scene, "What's done cannot be undone" (021-029), invert her Act II assurance "what's done is done" — the confident maxim has become a confession of irreversibility.

## The Doctor's Verdict

The scene closes with the Doctor's refusal:

> This disease is beyond my practice. (021-026)

The medical frame makes the point explicitly: the sleep disorder is not physiological but moral. "More needs she the divine than the physician" (021-034). This is the play's final judgment on the sleep motif — what has been broken is not the body's capacity for sleep but the soul's.
