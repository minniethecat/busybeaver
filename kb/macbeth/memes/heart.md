## Overview

The heart in *Macbeth* serves as the anatomical metaphor for inward truth — the seat of courage, conscience, love, and terror. From Macbeth's first soliloquy, where his "seated heart knock at my ribs" (001-003) signals the physical shock of ambition, to Lady Macbeth's instruction that "false face must hide what the false heart doth know" (001-007), the heart is the organ of the self that cannot lie to itself.

> Why hath it given me earnest of success,
> Commencing in a truth? I am Thane of Cawdor:
> If good, why do I yield to that suggestion

## The Heart as Courage

Macbeth's self-diagnosis uses the heart as the index of fear. When the thought of murder "doth unfix my hair, / And make my seated heart knock at my ribs, / Against the use of nature" (001-003), the heart's knocking is the body's rebellion against the mind's dark intent. The "use of nature" — the heart's regular rhythm — is disrupted by the unnatural thought of regicide.

Lady Macbeth's demand that spirits "Stop up th' access and passage to remorse" (001-005) is an attempt to silence the heart's moral function. She seeks to transform herself into a creature without a conscience-heart, capable of murder without the "knocking" that torments her husband.

## The White Heart and the Stained Heart

After the murder, Macbeth's lament transforms the heart into a register of permanent guilt: "Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood / Clean from my hand? No; this my hand will rather / The multitudinous seas incarnadine, / Making the green one red" (002-002). Lady Macbeth's dismissive response — "My hands are of your colour; but I shame / To wear a heart so white" (002-002) — opposes the white heart of cowardice to the red (unstated) heart of bloody resolve. Yet this bravado crumbles; by Act V, her sleepwalking confession reveals the heart-white she scorned is precisely what she cannot achieve — the conscience will not wash clean.

Macduff's grief for his slaughtered family restores the heart to its proper register of feeling. Malcolm urges him to "give sorrow words" and treat grief "like a man," but Macduff insists: "I shall do so; / But I must also feel it as a man" (017-003) — the heart's capacity for love is not weakness but the measure of humanity.

## The Heart of Scotland

Malcolm's testing of Macduff concludes with the recognition of genuine feeling: Macduff's outburst of grief proves his "heart to love" for Scotland (017-003). The political resolution of the play turns on this moment — a heart capable of love is the prerequisite for legitimate rule, in stark contrast to Macbeth, whose heart has been progressively hardened against all human feeling.
