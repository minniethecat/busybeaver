# Macduff

Macduff, the Thane of Fife, is the play's moral counterweight to Macbeth. Where Macbeth yields to temptation, Macduff refuses; where Macbeth kills kings, Macduff kills a tyrant. His arc is structured around loss and righteous vengeance: he loses his family, his home, and nearly his faith in Scotland, but channels that grief into the act of justice that restores legitimate rule.

## The Discovery of Duncan's Body

Macduff is the first to discover the murdered king. Arriving at Inverness to wake Duncan, he enters the chamber and emerges shattered:

> MACDUFF. O horror, horror, horror! Tongue nor heart
> Cannot conceive nor name thee!
> ...
> Approach the chamber, and destroy your sight
> With a new Gorgon. Do not bid me speak;
> See, and then speak yourselves. ((010-026))

His repeated "horror" and the reference to the Gorgon — whose gaze turned men to stone — convey a world-shattering shock that is qualitatively different from Macbeth's and Lady Macbeth's feigned reactions. Macduff's response to Duncan's death is authentic grief, and his refusal to describe what he saw ("Do not bid me speak") suggests an integrity that contrasts with the Macbeths' glib performances.

## Suspicion and Refusal

Macduff never voices open suspicion of Macbeth, but his actions speak. He refuses to attend Macbeth's coronation at Scone — "No, cousin, I'll to Fife" ((011-013)) — and instead returns home. When Ross asks "Will you to Scone?" Macduff's answer is oblique: "No, cousin, I'll to Fife" ((011-013)). In a play where attendance at ceremonies signifies political loyalty, Macduff's absence is the first act of quiet resistance.

Ross's observation in Act II — "Thriftless ambition, that will ravin up / Thine own life's means!" ((011-017)) — though spoken about Duncan, applies equally to the pattern Macduff perceives in Macbeth. Macduff's decision to go to England and join Malcolm is the culmination of this private dissent:

> LENNOX. Sent he to Macduff?
> LORD. He did, and with an absolute "Sir, not I,"
> The cloudy messenger turns me his back,
> And hums, as who should say, "You'll rue the time
> That clogs me with this answer." ((017-004))

Macduff's "Sir, not I" to Macbeth's messenger is the play's definitive refusal — a subject rejecting a tyrant's summons.

## The Slaughter of His Family

While Macduff is in England seeking Malcolm's aid, Macbeth orders the massacre of his wife and children:

> MACBETH. The castle of Macduff I will surprise;
> Seize upon Fife; give to the edge o' the sword
> His wife, his babes, and all unfortunate souls
> That trace him in his line. ((018-069))

The murder of Lady Macduff and her son is the play's most unambiguously evil act — the killing of innocents who pose no threat. When Ross brings the news to Macduff in England, his response moves from disbelief to self-blame to a resolve to act:

> MACDUFF. All my pretty ones?
> Did you say all? — O hell-kite! — All?
> What, all my pretty chickens and their dam,
> At one fell swoop?
> MALCOLM. Dispute it like a man.
> MACDUFF. I shall do so;
> But I must also feel it as a man.
> I cannot but remember such things were,
> That were most precious to me. ((020-076))

Malcolm urges stoicism — "Dispute it like a man" — but Macduff insists on the validity of grief: "I must also feel it as a man." This moment redefines masculinity in the play: not as emotional numbness (Macbeth's version) but as the capacity to feel loss fully and still act.

## "Macduff Was from His Mother's Womb Untimely Ripp'd"

In the final confrontation, Macduff reveals the truth that fulfills the witches' equivocal prophecy:

> MACDUFF. Despair thy charm;
> And let the Angel whom thou still hast serv'd
> Tell thee, Macduff was from his mother's womb
> Untimely ripp'd. ((028-018))

Macbeth had been told he could not be harmed by "one of woman born" — but Macduff was delivered by Caesarean section, not natural birth. The technicality undoes Macbeth's supernatural protection and the tyrant's confidence collapses. Macduff kills Macbeth and presents his head to Malcolm, hailing the new king: "Hail, King! for so thou art" ((028-039)).

## Moral Significance

Macduff occupies a distinctive moral position in the play. He is neither fooled by Macbeth (like Duncan) nor complicit (like Banquo, who keeps silent about the witches' prophecy), nor corrupted. His moral clarity is earned through suffering: he loses everything before he acts, but the loss does not break him. His insistence on feeling "as a man" offers the play's most humane definition of masculinity — one that integrates emotion and action rather than opposing them.
