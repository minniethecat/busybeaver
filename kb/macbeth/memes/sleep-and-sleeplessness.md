# Sleep and Sleeplessness

Sleep is the play's most intimate register of moral damage. When Macbeth murders Duncan, he does not merely kill a king; he kills his own capacity for restorative sleep. The play's sleep imagery traces a double arc: Macbeth's progressive insomnia (the inability to sleep) and Lady Macbeth's somnambulism (the inability to *stop* sleepwalking). Together, these complementary disorders map the full territory of traumatic guilt — the mind that cannot rest, and the mind that can only confess in the oblivion of sleep.

## Sleep as Innocence

Before the murder, sleep is consistently associated with innocence, peace, and natural order. Duncan sleeps peacefully at Inverness — the trusting sleep of the guest who does not know his host intends to kill him. Macbeth's soliloquy before the murder invokes "the innocent sleep, / Sleep that knits up the ravelled sleave of care, / The death of each day's life, sore labour's bath, / Balm of hurt minds, great nature's second course, / Chief nourisher in life's feast." This catalogue of sleep's virtues — healer, restorer, nourisher — is the most beautiful passage Macbeth ever speaks, and it is spoken in the act of destroying what it praises. By murdering the sleeping Duncan, Macbeth murders sleep itself.

The Porter's scene immediately after the murder reinforces sleep's symbolic weight. "Shake off this downy sleep, death's counterfeit" (010-036) — the Porter describes the knocking that wakes him from a drunken stupor, but "death's counterfeit" names a truth the Porter does not intend: Duncan's sleep has become death, and the Porter's comic waking is the play's first image of a world in which sleep and death have become indistinguishable.

## Macbeth's Insomnia

After the murder, Macbeth hears a voice: "Methought I heard a voice cry 'Sleep no more! / Macbeth does murder sleep' — the innocent sleep, / Sleep that knits up the ravelled sleave of care." This hallucinated voice is the play's most explicit statement of the sleep-murder equation. Macbeth has killed Duncan in his sleep; in return, sleep will be killed in Macbeth.

The prophecy is exact. For the remainder of the play, Macbeth never rests. His insomnia manifests as paranoia — he cannot sleep because he cannot stop planning the next murder, because he cannot trust anyone, because his mind has become a machine for producing violence. "Ere we will eat our meal in fear, and sleep / In the affliction of these terrible dreams / That shake us nightly" — his sleeplessness is filled with nightmares, the mind's failed attempt to process what conscience refuses to acknowledge.
> Methought I heard a voice cry, "Sleep no more!
> Macbeth does murder sleep,"—the innocent sleep;
> Sleep that knits up the ravell'd sleave of care,
> The death of each day's life, sore labour's bath,
> Balm of hurt minds, great nature's second course,
> Chief nourisher in life's feast.

## Lady Macbeth's Sleepwalking

Lady Macbeth's trajectory is the inverse of her husband's. Where Macbeth cannot sleep, Lady Macbeth cannot wake. Her sleepwalking scene is the most clinically precise portrait of traumatic guilt in Renaissance drama. She rises from bed, writes a letter, washes her hands — all while unconscious, all while confessing. The Doctor observes: "A great perturbation in nature, to receive at once the benefit of sleep and do the effects of watching." Sleep has become not rest but rehearsal — the mind compulsively reenacting the crime it cannot consciously acknowledge.

The blood Lady Macbeth sees on her hands in this scene is the blood she claimed "a little water" would wash away. It has returned — not on her physical hands but in her unconscious mind, where water cannot reach it. "All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand." The spot that was erasable in Act II has become permanent in Act V — not because the blood is real, but because guilt is indelible.

## The Sleep-Death Continuum

Throughout the play, sleep and death are aligned as neighbouring states. The Porter calls sleep "death's counterfeit." Macbeth describes Duncan after the murder as looking like "sleep" rather than death. The word "knell" — the bell that summons Duncan to heaven or to hell (008-051) — is a death bell that rings while Duncan sleeps. Lady Macbeth's final offstage death is described as a release from "the fever of life" into "sleep" — suggesting that for her, death is the only sleep that remains after sleepwalking has made rest impossible.

## Synthesis

Sleep in *Macbeth* is the canary in the moral coal mine: its disruption signals damage that the conscious mind cannot register. Macbeth's insomnia and Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking are not separate pathologies but complementary manifestations of the same guilt — one that keeps the conscious mind eternally vigilant and terrified, the other that drives the unconscious mind to compulsive confession. Together they demonstrate the play's most sophisticated psychological insight: guilt is not a thought but a condition of the body, and the body will speak what the mind refuses to acknowledge, even — especially — in the oblivion of sleep.
