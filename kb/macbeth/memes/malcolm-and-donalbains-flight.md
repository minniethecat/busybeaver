# Malcolm and Donalbain's Flight

Malcolm and Donalbain's Flight is a turning point in *Macbeth*, occurring at the end of Act II Scene iii. In the immediate aftermath of Duncan's murder, his two sons flee Scotland — Malcolm to England and Donalbain to Ireland — unwittingly clearing Macbeth's path to the throne and drawing suspicion onto themselves.

## Dramatic Context

The flight occurs directly after the chaotic discovery of Duncan's murder. Macbeth has just killed the grooms; Macduff has questioned the killing. Malcolm and Donalbain, alone on stage after the others exit (010-074), speak privately and conclude that no one in Scotland is safe — not even the king's own sons. Their dialogue is brief, pragmatic, and steeped in the language of concealed danger.

## The Princes' Dialogue

> "DONALBAIN. ...Where we are,
> There's daggers in men's smiles: the near in blood,
> The nearer bloody.
> MALCOLM. This murderous shaft that's shot
> Hath not yet lighted; and our safest way
> Is to avoid the aim. Therefore to horse;
> And let us not be dainty of leave-taking,
> But shift away. There's warrant in that theft
> Which steals itself, when there's no mercy left." [010-076–010-077]

Donalbain's phrase "daggers in men's smiles" is one of the play's most compressed statements of the appearance-versus-reality theme. The smile — the outward sign of friendship — conceals the dagger of murderous intent. Malcolm's reply extends the metaphor: the "murderous shaft" (arrow) has been shot but "hath not yet lighted" — it is still in flight, and the princes must move before it lands. His justification for their clandestine departure — "There's warrant in that theft / Which steals itself" — is a paradox that captures the moral inversion of Macbeth's Scotland, where innocent flight looks like guilt.

Earlier, as the discovery scene ends, Malcolm had already foreshadowed this reasoning:

> "MALCOLM. What will you do? Let's not consort with them:
> To show an unfelt sorrow is an office
> Which the false man does easy. I'll to England." (010-075)

The distinction between felt and "unfelt sorrow" is crucial: Malcolm recognises that in a world where anyone can perform grief, genuine feeling offers no protection. His decision to leave is not cowardice but strategic retreat — the first step in a long campaign that will eventually restore legitimate rule to Scotland.

## Sequence

1. All characters exit except Malcolm and Donalbain (010-074)
2. Malcolm tells Donalbain they are not safe: "There's daggers in men's smiles" — those closest in blood to Duncan are nearest to being murdered
3. Donalbain agrees: "our fate / Hid in an auger-hole may rush and seize us"
4. They resolve to flee in opposite directions — Malcolm to England, Donalbain to Ireland
5. They acknowledge that their flight will make them look guilty but conclude that safety is more important than reputation

## Immediate Consequences

The princes' flight is immediately interpreted as evidence of their guilt. Macbeth is named Duncan's successor and crowned at Scone. The legitimate line of succession is fractured, and Scotland falls under a tyrant's rule.

## Long-term Significance

Malcolm's flight to England sets up the entire second half of the play. In Act IV Scene iii, Macduff will find Malcolm at the English court testing his virtue before joining forces to overthrow Macbeth. Donalbain, by contrast, disappears from the play entirely — a narrative asymmetry that suggests Shakespeare needed only one returning prince. The flight is also thematically rich: it illustrates how evil deeds corrupt the social fabric, making even innocent acts appear guilty. The princes' departure is both a necessity and a tragedy — the rightful heirs must abandon their homeland to save it.

## Thematic Connections
