# Knocking (Gate Motif)

The knocking at the gate in *Macbeth* is one of the most discussed sound effects in English drama. Beginning as Lady Macbeth's internal heart-knock of fear, it crescendos into the Porter's comic-turned-cosmic gatekeeping and ultimately becomes the persistent auditory marker of guilt that will not be silenced. The motif transforms a mundane sound into the play's most potent symbol of the boundary between the private self and public judgement.

## Definition & Context

Shakespeare structures the knocking motif as a three-phase progression: the internal ("my seated heart knock at my ribs," 003-046), the external (the Porter's "Here's a knocking indeed!", 010-002), and the psychological return (Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking: "There's knocking at the gate," 021-029). In Jacobean theological terms, the Porter scene plays on the tradition of the Harrowing of Hell — the Porter imagines himself as gatekeeper of hell, admitting a series of damned souls, while the real "devil" (Macbeth) is already inside the castle. The knocking thus serves as both comic relief (the drunken Porter) and theological commentary (who is truly damned?).

## Textual Evidence

### Anticipation (Act I)

> … make my seated heart knock at my ribs, / Against the use of nature. (003-046)

Lady Macbeth's invocation of the spirits to "unsex" her produces this first knock — an internal, physiological response to her own unnatural ambition. The heart knocking "against the use of nature" establishes the motif as a sign of disorder within the body politic and the body natural. Hers is a wilful disturbance; the knocking that follows Duncan's murder is its consequence.

### The Porter Scene (Act II)

> Here's a knocking indeed! If a man were porter of hell-gate, he should have old turning the key. (010-002)

The Porter's soliloquy transforms the castle gate into the gate of hell, with each knock admitting a new archetype of the damned — a farmer who hanged himself, an equivocator, and an English tailor. The comedy is bitter: the Porter imagines himself greeting sinners at hell's entrance, but the genuine evil has already been committed inside, by his master. The repeated knocking that the audience hears between his speeches is the structural heartbeat of the scene.

> Wake Duncan with thy knocking! I would thou couldst! (009-031)

Macbeth's response to the knocking — heard immediately after the murder — is the play's most concentrated expression of regret. The wish that knocking could undo death captures the irrevocability of his act. The knocking is now the sound of consequence arriving: it is Macduff and Lennox at the gate, come to discover the body.

### Return (Act V)

> To bed, to bed. There's knocking at the gate. (021-029)

Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking speech reprises the knocking motif, collapsing time and mental state. In her fractured consciousness, the knocking that followed Duncan's murder is happening again, perpetually. The gate that was once the Porter's comic domain has become the permanent threshold of her guilt. She cannot answer it; she can only go to bed.
