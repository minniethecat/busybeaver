# Scotland

Scotland is a location in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*.

## Overview

Scotland is the primary setting of the entire play. Under King Duncan's rule it is a kingdom of order and loyalty; under Macbeth's tyranny it becomes a "sickly" land of fear and oppression.

## Significance in the Play

Scotland's decline mirrors Macbeth's moral descent. The Dramatis Personae lists Scotland's noblemen — Macduff, Lennox, Ross, Menteith, Angus, and Caithness — who form the fabric of the kingdom's nobility (P01-006)(P01-007)(P01-008)(P01-009)(P01-010)(P01-011). Under Macbeth's reign, the country groans under tyranny. Macduff laments, "O Scotland, Scotland!" (020-019), and Malcolm observes that "Scotland hath foisons to fill up your will" — the land still has abundance to sustain itself once order is restored (020-017). Macduff, exiled in England, asks Ross desperately, "Stands Scotland where it did?" (020-035), reflecting the uncertainty and suffering of the nation under Macbeth's rule. The play ends with Malcolm's coronation, restoring legitimate rule to the kingdom.
