The **Divine Right of Kings** is the belief that monarchs rule by God's direct appointment, making their person sacred and their authority unchallengeable by human subjects. In *Macbeth*, this doctrine provides the moral architecture of the entire tragedy: Duncan is not merely a king but "the Lord's anointed temple"; his murder is not merely treason but a cosmic sacrilege that invites divine retribution.

## Definition & Context

King James I, Shakespeare's patron and the play's likely first royal audience, was the foremost English proponent of divine right theory. His 1598 treatise *The True Law of Free Monarchies* argued that kings are accountable only to God. Shakespeare embeds this doctrine throughout *Macbeth*: Duncan's body is described in sacred language, his blood is "golden," and his killer is damned. The play thus functions as both political warning and theological argument.

## Textual Evidence

### The Sacred King (Act I)

> …this castle hath a pleasant seat; the air Nimbly and sweetly recommends itself Unto our gentle senses. (006-004)

Duncan's arrival at Inverness is framed through natural sweetness and divine favour — the "heaven's breath" that "smells wooingly." The king's presence sanctifies the space around him, making the coming murder all the more blasphemous.

> …his virtues Will plead like angels, trumpet-tongu'd, against The deep damnation of his taking-off. (007-002)

Macbeth's pre-murder soliloquy acknowledges the theological dimension of the crime: Duncan's virtues will "plead like angels" at the final judgment. The language is explicitly eschatological — regicide is not a political act but a damnable sin.

### Heaven's Instruments (Act IV, Scene iii)

> Such sanctity hath heaven given his hand, They presently amend. (020-025)

The English king Edward the Confessor is offered as a living counter-example to Macbeth: a true king whose touch heals the sick because "heaven hath given his hand" sacred power. The juxtaposition reinforces that Macbeth's "bloody-scepter'd" rule is the inversion of divine kingship.

> How he solicits heaven, Himself best knows. (020-029)

Malcolm's description of the English king's private piety — "how he solicits heaven" — presents legitimate kingship as a relationship with God that the usurper can never replicate.

### Restoration (Act V)

> …that ever Scotland In such an honour nam'd. What's more to do, Which would be planted newly with the time. (028-028)

Malcolm's coronation speech closes the play by restoring the divine order: Scotland is "planted newly" under its rightful, heaven-sanctioned king. The organic metaphor — planting — signals the return of natural, God-ordained growth after the sterile, "untitled" tyranny of Macbeth.
