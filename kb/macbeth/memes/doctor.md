The Scottish Doctor is a physician who appears in Act V, first attending [[lady-macbeth|Lady Macbeth]] during her sleepwalking episodes, then reluctantly serving at [[macbeth|Macbeth]]'s court during the siege of Dunsinane. Though a minor character in stage time, he delivers some of the play's most penetrating psychological observations, diagnosing Lady Macbeth's condition as beyond the reach of medicine and giving voice to the play's extended metaphor of Scotland as a diseased body.

## Dramatic Role

The Doctor serves as a rational, empirical observer in a world collapsing into madness and violence. In the sleepwalking scene (Act V, Scene i), he and the Gentlewoman watch Lady Macbeth relive the murders through compulsive hand-washing. His clinical eye immediately recognises the depth of the disturbance:

> A great perturbation in nature, to receive at once the benefit of sleep, and do the effects of watching.
>
> (021-004)

His clinical observation cuts to the heart of her condition:

> This disease is beyond my practice: yet I have known those which have walked in their sleep who have died holily in their beds. ([(021-026)])

> More needs she the divine than the physician.
> God, God forgive us all! ([(021-033)])

The Doctor's diagnosis that Lady Macbeth's sickness is spiritual rather than physical provides an objective confirmation of the guilt that has destroyed her mind. Crucially, the Doctor's refusal to prescribe a physical remedy is an act of professional integrity: he will not pretend his art can reach where it cannot, unlike the equivocating prophecies that have led Macbeth to destruction.

## The Medical Metaphor and the Body Politic

The Doctor's presence activates the play's sustained analogy between the individual body and the body politic. Macbeth's opening demand to the Doctor in Act V, Scene iii — "If thou couldst, doctor, cast / The water of my land, find her disease, / And purge it to a sound and pristine health" ([(023-031)]) — explicitly conflates Scotland's political sickness with a medical condition requiring diagnosis and cure. But the Doctor cannot "cast the water" of a nation; his tools are for individuals, not polities.

Macbeth's request reveals his continued reliance on the logic of bloodletting: just as he believed killing Duncan would cure his ambition, he now believes a physician can purge Scotland. Both are category errors. The Doctor's measured response — "Therein the patient / Must minister to himself" ([(023-030)]) — is the play's most concise statement of an ethical principle: no external authority can heal what the self will not confront. Macbeth, who has refused to "minister to himself" since the murder of Duncan, cannot hear this truth.

## Key Scenes

### The Sleepwalking Scene

> DOCTOR. I have two nights watched with you, but can perceive no truth in your report. When was it she last walked? ([(021-002)])

The Doctor initially doubts the Gentlewoman's reports of Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking. His trained scepticism — he requires "perception," not report — aligns him with the empirical tradition. But witnessing the phenomenon firsthand, he is horrified. He recognizes that Lady Macbeth's compulsive hand-washing and fragmented speech reveal crimes she cannot confess while awake.

The Doctor's role in this scene also introduces a witnessing dynamic that the play has avoided until Act V. In the earlier murders, there are no impartial witnesses: the Macbeths kill in private; the grooms are framed; Banquo is ambushed. The Doctor and Gentlewoman are the first characters to observe the consequences of murder without being implicated in them. Their presence transforms private guilt into public knowledge — a necessary condition for the restoration of political order that Malcolm's victory will bring.

### At Dunsinane

> DOCTOR. Were I from Dunsinane away and clear, Profit again should hardly draw me here. ([(023-035)])

In Act V, Scene iii, the Doctor attends Macbeth. His aside reveals his desperation to flee; no profit could draw him back. This moment crystallizes the play's medical metaphor at its darkest: not only can the Doctor not heal Scotland, but the court's corruption is so complete that even the healer is contaminated by proximity. Fleeing Dunsinane becomes a moral imperative — survival, not cowardice.

The Doctor's exit from the play is an escape from tragedy itself. Unlike Lady Macbeth, who cannot escape her own mind; unlike Macbeth, who is trapped in his crumbling fortress; unlike Banquo, who returns as a ghost — the Doctor simply leaves. His departure is the play's most understated act of self-preservation, and perhaps its most damning verdict on Macbeth's rule: even the healers abandon you.

## Relationships

- **[[lady-macbeth|Lady Macbeth]]** — His patient; the Doctor witnesses her mental collapse and recognizes its irreversibility
- **[[macbeth|Macbeth]]** — The Doctor serves Macbeth at Dunsinane but is repulsed by the tyrant's court

## Themes

- [[guilt|Guilt and Conscience]] — The Doctor's diagnosis externalizes Lady Macbeth's internal torment, confirming that guilt operates beyond conscious control
- [[disease|Health and Disease]] — The Doctor embodies the play's extended metaphor of Scotland as a sick body that no physician can cure
- witnessing — Like the Porter and the Old Man, the Doctor is an ordinary person who glimpses the horror at the center of Macbeth's reign
- [[sleep|Sleep]] — The "death of each day's life" violated by Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking, which the Doctor observes but cannot treat
