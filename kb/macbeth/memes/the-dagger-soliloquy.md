# The Dagger Soliloquy

The Dagger Soliloquy is one of the most celebrated passages in *Macbeth*, occurring in Act II Scene i. Alone on stage, Macbeth hallucinates a floating dagger that leads him toward Duncan's chamber, marking the moment when his murderous intent crystallises into action.

## Dramatic Context

The soliloquy occurs late at night in Macbeth's castle at Inverness, moments before Duncan's murder. Banquo and Fleance have just departed (008-017), and a servant has exited (008-019). Macbeth is alone for the first time since resolving to kill Duncan. This is the threshold between deliberation and deed — the pivotal psychological moment of Act II. The soliloquy is structured as a progressive surrender to the hallucination: Macbeth moves from scepticism ("I have thee not, and yet I see thee still") to acceptance and finally to action ("I go, and it is done").

## The Soliloquy

> "Is this a dagger which I see before me,
> The handle toward my hand? Come, let me clutch thee:—
> I have thee not, and yet I see thee still.
> Art thou not, fatal vision, sensible
> To feeling as to sight? or art thou but
> A dagger of the mind, a false creation,
> Proceeding from the heat-oppressed brain?" [008-020–008-026]

The opening lines establish the central paradox: the dagger is visible but intangible, a "fatal vision" that Macbeth cannot determine is supernatural or psychological. The phrase "dagger of the mind" has become a touchstone of Shakespearean criticism, encapsulating the play's refusal to resolve whether its supernatural elements are real or projected.

> "Now o'er the one half-world
> Nature seems dead, and wicked dreams abuse
> The curtain'd sleep. Witchcraft celebrates
> Pale Hecate's off'rings; and wither'd murder,
> Alarum'd by his sentinel, the wolf,
> Whose howl's his watch, thus with his stealthy pace,
> With Tarquin's ravishing strides, towards his design
> Moves like a ghost." [008-036–008-043]

Macbeth aligns himself with the dark supernatural machinery of the cosmos — Hecate, witchcraft, the wolf as murder's sentinel, and Tarquin (the Roman tyrant who raped Lucrece). In doing so, he places his own crime within a mythological and literary tradition of nocturnal violence, almost as if to dignify it. The personification of "wither'd murder" moving "like a ghost" is a self-portrait: Macbeth is becoming the very force he invokes.

> "I go, and it is done. The bell invites me.
> Hear it not, Duncan, for it is a knell
> That summons thee to heaven or to hell." [008-050–008-052]

The bell that ends the soliloquy serves a double function: it is both a practical stage cue and a symbolic death knell. Macbeth's final couplet transforms the mundane sound into a metaphysical summons, closing the gap between the real and the hallucinatory that the soliloquy has explored.

## Sequence

1. Macbeth sees the dagger before him, handle toward his hand (008-020–008-022)
2. He questions whether it is a "fatal vision" or a "dagger of the mind, a false creation / Proceeding from the heat-oppressed brain" (008-023–008-026)
3. He draws his own dagger and sees the hallucination still, now with "gouts of blood" on its blade (008-027–008-034)
4. Macbeth reflects on how "Nature seems dead" as witchcraft and murder dominate the sleeping world (008-035–008-042)
5. He prays the earth not to hear his steps, then resolves: "Whiles I threat, he lives. Words to the heat of deeds too cold breath gives" (008-043–008-048)
6. A bell rings; Macbeth interprets it as Duncan's "knell / That summons thee to heaven or to hell" and departs (008-049–008-052)

## Immediate Consequences

Macbeth exits toward Duncan's chamber. The hallucination resolves the inner conflict that dominated Act I Scene vii ("If it were done when 'tis done..."). He is no longer deliberating; he is acting. The soliloquy is the psychological gateway to regicide.

## Long-term Significance

The soliloquy establishes the pattern of Macbeth's psychological deterioration: his guilt manifests as supernatural visions (the dagger, later Banquo's ghost). The "dagger of the mind" phrase encapsulates the play's central question — are the supernatural forces real, or are they projections of a guilty conscience? The invocation of Hecate, Tarquin, and the wolf also aligns Macbeth with the play's dark supernatural machinery, foreshadowing his later embrace of the Witches. The soliloquy also marks a formal turning point: it is the last time Macbeth speaks in fully coherent, poetic blank verse before the fragmented language of guilt takes over.
