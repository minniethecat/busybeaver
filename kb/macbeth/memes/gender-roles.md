**Gender roles**, in *Macbeth*, are not fixed but fiercely contested. Characters invoke, invert, and weaponise the codes of manhood and womanhood: Lady Macbeth calls on spirits to "unsex" her; Macbeth equates manhood with murder; Macduff insists that feeling grief is compatible with being a man. Shakespeare uses the instability of gender to expose the violence latent in rigid masculine codes and the power that patriarchal assumptions attempt to suppress.

## Definition & Context

Early modern gender ideology defined men by action, courage, and public authority; women by passivity, nurture, and domestic confinement. *Macbeth* tests every term in this binary. Lady Macbeth's ambition exceeds her husband's, yet she can only act through him. Macbeth's violence is repeatedly framed as proof of manhood, yet the most humane response to murder — Macduff's grief — is also claimed as masculine. The play suggests that the rigid equation of masculinity with violence is itself a source of tragedy.

## Textual Evidence

### The Unsexing (Act I, Scene v)

> …is too full o' th' milk of human kindness To catch the nearest way. (005-002)

Lady Macbeth's diagnosis of her husband uses maternal imagery — "milk of human kindness" — as a weakness. She positions compassion as feminine and therefore incompatible with ambition, setting up the play's central gender conflict.

> Come to my woman's breasts, And take my milk for gall, you murth'ring ministers. (005-017)

Lady Macbeth's invocation to the spirits is the play's most explicit gender transgression: she asks to have her "woman's breasts" turned to poison ("gall"), exchanging female nurture for male violence. The speech collapses the distance between femininity and murder.

### Manhood on Trial (Act I, Scene vii)

> MACBETH. I dare do all that may become a man; Who dares do more is none. (007-006)

Macbeth's initial refusal — "Who dares do more is none" — defines manhood by moral limit. His wife will spend the rest of the play dismantling this definition, replacing it with one in which manhood means the capacity for violence without restraint.

### Masculine Grief (Act IV, Scene iii)

> MACDUFF. O, I could play the woman with mine eyes, And braggart with my tongue! But, gentle heavens, Cut short all intermission. (020-066)

> MALCOLM. Dispute it like a man. / MACDUFF. I shall do so; But I must also feel it as a man. (020-066)

When Macduff learns of his family's slaughter, Malcolm urges him to "dispute it like a man" — to turn grief into vengeful action. Macduff's reply — "I must also feel it as a man" — is the play's most radical revision of gender: feeling is not womanly weakness but human necessity. Grief and manhood are not opposites.

### Born of Woman (Act V)

> …none of woman born Shall harm Macbeth. (018-036)

> …was from his mother's womb Untimely ripp'd. (028-011)

The play's climactic gender irony: Macbeth's invulnerability rests on a narrow definition of "woman born" — natural childbirth — which Macduff's Caesarean birth escapes. Gender, like the witches' prophecies, is trickier than it first appears.

## Critical Perspectives

Critics have long noted the play's preoccupation with gender inversion. Janet Adelman argues that *Macbeth* stages a "fantasy of absolute male self-generation" that fails catastrophically — the Macbeths can imagine power without women, but the play ends by restoring the feminine (through the witches, Lady Macbeth's guilt, and Macduff's birth story) as an inescapable reality.
