The **Order of Nature**, in *Macbeth*, is the divinely ordained hierarchy binding the cosmos, the state, and the human body. When Macbeth murders Duncan — God's anointed deputy — he fractures this chain at every level. Shakespeare registers the rupture through a series of unnatural portents: falcons killed by owls, horses turning cannibal, and darkness consuming the day. These are not decorative; they are the play's argument that political crime has metaphysical consequences.

## Definition & Context

Shakespeare's audience inherited the doctrine of the Great Chain of Being: a ranked order descending from God through angels, monarchs, nobles, commoners, animals, to inanimate matter. The king occupied the crucial link between divine and human realms. To kill a king was therefore not merely treason but a cosmic violation — an act that "unmakes" the natural order. The *Macbeth* witches exploit this rupture: by tempting Macbeth to "o'erleap" his station, they invite chaos into every level of creation.

## Textual Evidence

### The Heavens Rebel (Act II, Scene iv)

> OLD MAN. 'Tis unnatural, Even like the deed that's done. On Tuesday last, A falcon, tow'ring in her pride of place, Was by a mousing owl hawk'd at and kill'd. (011-004)

The old man's testimony — that a falcon (the king of birds) was killed by a mousing owl (a lowly night-hunter) — is a miniature of Macbeth's crime. The inversion of the natural hierarchy in the animal kingdom mirrors the murder of Duncan. "Even like the deed that's done" makes the analogy explicit.

> Thou seest the heavens, as troubled with man's act, Threatens his bloody stage. (011-003)

Ross observes that the sky itself is darkened by human sin — an eclipse that transforms day into night. The heavens are not indifferent; they are "troubled" and "threatened" by the murder.

### Disorder Multiplies (Act III)

> Stand not upon the order of your going, But go at once. (015-050)

Lady Macbeth's dismissal of the banquet guests — telling them not to observe "the order of your going" (the proper hierarchy of departure) — is bitterly ironic. The Macbeths have already destroyed all order; this social discourtesy is merely the surface expression of a much deeper chaos.

### Nature's Diseased State (Act IV–V)

> Unnatural deeds Do breed unnatural troubles: infected minds To their deaf pillows will discharge their secrets. (021-033)

The Doctor's diagnosis in the sleepwalking scene articulates the play's causal logic: unnatural acts produce unnatural sickness. Lady Macbeth's "infected mind" is nature's revenge on the body that invited evil in.
