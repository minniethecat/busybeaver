## Overview

The bear in *Macbeth* is concentrated in a single, devastating metaphor: Macbeth's self-description in the final act as a baited animal trapped in the arena. "They have tied me to a stake; I cannot fly, / But, bear-like, I must fight the course" (026-007). The bear-baiting image condenses the entire trajectory of the tragedy — the once-noble warrior reduced to a chained beast, fighting not for victory but because fighting is the only thing left.

## Bear-Baiting in Early Modern England

Bear-baiting was a popular blood sport in Shakespeare's London, in which a bear was chained to a stake and set upon by dogs. The bear could not escape; it could only fight until it was torn apart. The audience watched the bear's death as entertainment. Macbeth's invocation of this image forces the audience to recognise their own complicity in the spectacle of his destruction — they are watching a man be torn apart, and they have paid for the privilege.

Shakespeare had used the bear-baiting metaphor before — most memorably in *King Lear* ("I am bound / Upon a wheel of fire") — but in *Macbeth* the image is stripped to its brutal essence. There is no tragic grandeur in the baited bear, only the mechanical necessity of fighting when flight is impossible.

## The Bear and the "Russian Bear"

Earlier in the play, Macbeth's imagination had already conjured the bear as an image of terror he can face. Before the banquet, he declares he would dare "the rugged Russian bear, / The arm'd rhinoceros, or th' Hyrcan tiger" (012-004) rather than face Banquo's ghost. The irony is that the supernatural — the ghost — is what breaks him, while the natural predators (bear, tiger, rhinoceros) are foes he could fight with courage. By Act V, the distinction collapses: Macbeth has become the bear, and the supernatural prophecies that once armed him have become the chains that bind him to his fate.

## From Hunter to Hunted

Macbeth's first appearance in the play is as a victorious warrior, "Valour's minion" (001-002) who "unseam'd" his enemy "from the nave to th' chaps" (001-002). He was the hunter who carved his prey. The bear image marks the final inversion: the hunter has become the hunted, the carver the carved. Macduff's entrance with Macbeth's head on a pole completes the transformation — the bear has been baited to death, and the victors display the trophy.
> They have tied me to a stake. I cannot fly,
> But, bear-like I must fight the course.—What's he
> That was not born of woman? Such a one
> Am I to fear, or none.
>
