## Overview

Hospitality is one of the most sacred social bonds in Shakespeare's world, and its violation is at the heart of *Macbeth*'s moral horror. Duncan arrives at Inverness as an honoured guest; Macbeth, as host, owes him protection. The murder of a guest by his host is not merely homicide but a desecration of a bond that holds civilised society together — making it, in the play's moral calculus, a crime of particular monstrosity.

## The Sacred Bond of Host and Guest

The arrival of Duncan at Macbeth's castle is framed in the language of gracious welcome. Lady Macbeth receives the king with elaborate courtesy: "Your servants ever / Have theirs, themselves, and what is theirs, in compt, / To make their audit at your highness' pleasure" (001-006). Duncan responds with the language of love and trust: "Conduct me to mine host: we love him highly, / And shall continue our graces towards him" (001-006).

> Give me your hand;
> Conduct me to mine host: we love him highly,
> And shall continue our graces towards him.
>
> (006-009)

The word "host" appears repeatedly in the moments before the murder — Banquo speaks of Duncan being "in unusual pleasure" after the feast, and Macbeth himself, in the dagger soliloquy, sets the stage for the violation: "Nature seems dead, and wicked dreams abuse / The curtain'd sleep" (002-001).

## The Double Irony of Hospitality

The dramatic irony is doubled: not only does the host murder the guest, but the guest's trust is absolute. Duncan's final words of praise — "He was a gentleman on whom I built / An absolute trust" (001-004) (speaking of the former Thane of Cawdor) — are immediately followed by his entrance into Macbeth's castle. The audience knows that Duncan has walked from one traitor-host into the hands of another.

Lady Macbeth's instruction — "Look like the innocent flower, / But be the serpent under't" (001-005) — is the anti-creed of the false host. The outward show of welcome conceals murderous intent, perverting the rituals of hospitality into a theatre of betrayal.

> He's here in double trust:
> First, as I am his kinsman and his subject,
> Strong both against the deed; then, as his host,
> Who should against his murderer shut the door,
> Not bear the knife myself.
>
> (007-002)

## The Banquet as Tableau of Broken Hospitality

The banquet scene in Act III provides the visual and dramatic culmination of the hospitality theme. Macbeth's feast, intended to cement his kingship through communal celebration, collapses into chaos when Banquo's ghost appears. The host who once murdered his guest now sees an uninvited guest at his own table: "Thou canst not say I did it: never shake / Thy gory locks at me" (012-004). The violated bond of hospitality returns to haunt the violator.

Lady Macbeth attempts to salvage the scene with the language of offended hospitality: "You have displaced the mirth, broke the good meeting, / With most admired disorder" (012-004). But the irony is complete — the banquet that was meant to unite the thanes instead exposes the fracture at the heart of Macbeth's rule.
