# Free Will

The question of free will stands at the philosophical heart of *Macbeth*. When the Weird Sisters hail Macbeth as "king hereafter" (003-020), do they plant an inescapable destiny or simply name a possibility that Macbeth chooses to pursue? Shakespeare refuses a simple answer, instead tracing how prophecy, ambition, and choice intertwine in a way that preserves both the supernatural power of the witches' words and Macbeth's full moral responsibility for his crimes.

## Definition & Context

The relationship between divine foreknowledge and human free will was a live theological question in Shakespeare's England. Calvinist doctrine emphasized predestination—the idea that God had predetermined who would be saved and who damned—while other traditions stressed human free agency in choosing salvation or damnation. *Macbeth* transposes this theological debate into a political and psychological register: the witches' prophecies function like a secular predestination, but the play insists that Macbeth chooses his path. He is "rapt" (003-021) by what he hears, but he is not compelled.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> MACBETH. [Aside.] If chance will have me king, why, chance may crown me Without my stir. (003-048)

Macbeth's first response to the prophecy is the play's clearest statement of the free-will question. He hopes that "chance" (fortune, fate, accident) will make him king without his "stir"—without action, without choice, without crime. This line reveals that Macbeth knows, even before Lady Macbeth's persuasion, that becoming king will require his agency. The wish to be crowned "without my stir" is a wish to escape moral responsibility, and it is a wish the play will not grant him.

> LADY MACBETH. Which fate and metaphysical aid doth seem To have thee crown'd withal. (005-002)

Lady Macbeth's letter to her husband frames the prophecy as "fate and metaphysical aid"—supernatural forces working toward Macbeth's coronation. But the crucial word is "seem": the prophecy seems to point toward the crown, but it does not specify the means. Lady Macbeth fills in the gap, immediately concluding that murder is required. The prophecy does not compel the crime; human interpretation and ambition do.

> LADY MACBETH. But screw your courage to the sticking-place, And we'll not fail. (007-011)

Lady Macbeth's exhortation—"screw your courage to the sticking-place"—is the language of will overpowering hesitation. Macbeth wavers, and she applies her own will to fix his. This is choice, not fate: two human beings deciding, against their own moral awareness, to commit murder. The witches are nowhere in this scene; the decision is entirely human.

### Deepening (Act III)

> MACBETH. Rather than so, come, fate, into the list, And champion me to the utterance! (012-050)

By Act III, Macbeth has embraced a paradoxical relationship with fate. Rather than hoping fate will make him king "without my stir," he now challenges fate to single combat ("into the list"). This is the language of a man who believes he can fight destiny, but the very gesture of defiance acknowledges that he is bound by forces he cannot control. Macbeth's assertion of will has become indistinguishable from his submission to fate—he has chosen to embrace the prophecy while also choosing to fight its implications (that Banquo's heirs will rule).

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> MACBETH. I will be satisfied: deny me this, And an eternal curse fall on you! (018-006)

When Macbeth seeks out the witches in Act IV, he demands knowledge "from the masters" (018-006). His demand—"I will be satisfied"—is an assertion of will so absolute that it borders on blasphemy. Macbeth wants to compel the supernatural to serve his purposes, to give him certainty in a world of his own making. The apparitions' prophecies ("none of woman born / Shall harm Macbeth," "Macbeth shall never vanquish'd be until / Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane Hill / Shall come against him") give him exactly what he asks for: the illusion of invulnerability that his will demands.

> MACBETH. I'll fight, till from my bones my flesh be hack'd. Give me my armour. (025-008)

In the play's final scene, Macbeth's assertion of will reaches its extreme: "I'll fight, till from my bones my flesh be hack'd." Even with all prophecies collapsed—Birnam Wood has come to Dunsinane, Macduff was "from his mother's womb untimely ripp'd" (025-024)—Macbeth refuses to surrender. He chooses to die fighting rather than submit. This final assertion of will is both heroic and futile: it achieves nothing but proves that Macbeth's choices were always his own.

## Character Associations

| Character | Relationship to Free Will | Key Scene |
|-----------|--------------------------|-----------|
| Macbeth | The central case study. He hears a prophecy and chooses to fulfill it through murder, then spends the rest of the play trying to control the consequences of that choice. | 003-048 |
| Banquo | The foil. He hears prophecies too ("Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none," 003-027) but does not act on them. His "bosom franchised and allegiance clear" (008-014) shows that prophecy need not corrupt. | 008-014 |
| The Witches | The provocateurs. They speak truths but do not compel action. Their prophecies are accurate but ambiguous—they name outcomes, not means. | 003-020 |
| Lady Macbeth | The accelerator. Where Macbeth wavers, she exercises her will decisively: "screw your courage to the sticking-place" (007-011). Her later madness suggests that free will exercised toward evil exacts a psychological cost. | 007-011 |

## Related Concepts

- [[prophecy]] — The supernatural catalyst that raises the free-will question; the relationship between prophecy and choice is the play's central philosophical puzzle
- [[fate]] — The force that seems to govern outcomes in the play; whether fate is real or merely the retrospective name we give to chosen actions is the question Macbeth never resolves
- [[ambition]] — The human passion that exploits the ambiguity of prophecy, transforming "you shall be king" from prediction into permission
