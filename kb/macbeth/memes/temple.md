The **temple** appears twice in _Macbeth_ as an image of sacred space, each occurrence marking a stage in the play's progressive desecration of the holy.

## Appearances

### The Temple-Haunting Martlet

Arriving at Inverness, Banquo observes the castle with an architect's eye and notes: "This guest of summer, / The temple-haunting martlet, does approve, / By his loved mansionry, that the heaven's breath / Smells wooingly here" (006-004). The martlet (a swallow) that nests on churches has chosen Macbeth's castle — an observation laden with dramatic irony. The "temple-haunting" bird mistakes a place of impending murder for a sanctuary. Banquo's architectural metaphor casts Inverness as a sacred building, heightening the horror of the violation to come.

### The Lord's Anointed Temple

When Macduff discovers Duncan's body, he frames the murder in explicitly sacral language: "Most sacrilegious murder hath broke ope / The Lord's anointed temple, and stole thence / The life o' th' building" (010-028). Duncan's body is the temple — anointed (as kings were at coronation), sacred, and now violated. The metaphor draws on 1 Corinthians: "Know ye not that your body is the temple of the Holy Ghost?" Macduff casts the murder not as a political assassination but as an act of desecration.

## Symbolic Arc

The temple imagery traces a fall: from the false sanctuary of Inverness (where a bird mistakes a murder-house for a church) to the true violation of Duncan's anointed body. In both instances, the temple marks the boundary between the sacred and the profane — a boundary that Macbeth's crime obliterates.

## Architectural and Sacred Imagery

The temple is not the only architectural metaphor carrying sacred weight in _Macbeth_. Banquo, arriving at Inverness, also invokes "heaven's breath" and "loved mansionry" (006-004), layering ecclesiastical architecture with natural imagery. The Porter's scene (Act II, Scene III) imagines the castle gate as the entrance to Hell, inverting the sacred association: the same building compared to a temple becomes, within a single scene, a gateway to damnation.

Macduff's temple imagery is sharply distinct from Banquo's. Where Banquo's temple is a building mistaken for sacred, Macduff's temple is the king's body — not architecture but flesh. The two images bracket the murder: before it, the temple is a misrecognised building; after it, the temple is a violated corpse. This shift from stone to flesh, from architecture to body, maps the play's descent from political order into corporeal horror.

The temple motif participates in the play's wider pattern of sacred imagery: [[heaven]], [[hell]], [[grace]], [[knell]], and [[divine-right]] are all boundary markers between the holy and the violated. Duncan's murder does not merely kill a man — it tears the fabric of a sanctified cosmos.
