# Fleance's Escape

Fleance's Escape is one of the most consequential events in *Macbeth*, occurring in Act III Scene iii. When Macbeth's hired murderers ambush Banquo and Fleance, they succeed in killing Banquo but fail to kill his son, who flees (014-018). This escape means the Witches' prophecy — that Banquo's descendants will be kings — remains alive, and Macbeth's attempt to thwart fate fails at the critical moment.

## Dramatic Context

The ambush takes place in a park near the palace just before the banquet scene (Act III Scene iv). Macbeth has commissioned three murderers to kill both Banquo and Fleance, driven by his fear that the Witches' prophecy foretells a line of kings from Banquo's issue, not his own. The murderers waylay Banquo and Fleance on their way to the feast (014-010). The scene is brief — barely twenty lines — but its dramatic weight is immense: the success of the ambush against Banquo and the failure against Fleance together determine the trajectory of the play's second half.

## The Ambush

The scene is brutally economical. A third murderer joins the original two, and they confront Banquo and Fleance as they approach the palace on foot:

> "[*Assaults Banquo.*]
> BANQUO. O, treachery! Fly, good Fleance, fly, fly, fly!
> Thou mayst revenge—O slave!
> [*Dies. Fleance escapes.*]" [014-016–014-018]

Banquo's dying words are not about himself but about his son (014-017). The triple "fly, fly, fly" is an urgent parental imperative — Banquo's last act is to save his child, not to fight his killers. The incomplete sentence "Thou mayst revenge—" is cut off by death, leaving the promise of revenge hanging over the remainder of the play. The stage direction "[*Dies. Fleance escapes.*]" juxtaposes death and survival in a single line (014-018).

## The Murderer's Report

At the banquet, Macbeth receives the news:

> "MURDERER. Most royal sir, Fleance is 'scap'd.
> MACBETH. Then comes my fit again: I had else been perfect;
> Whole as the marble, founded as the rock,
> As broad and general as the casing air:
> But now I am cabin'd, cribb'd, confin'd, bound in
> To saucy doubts and fears." [015-011–015-012]

Macbeth's reaction is a study in thwarted ambition. He imagines what he would have been — "perfect," "whole as the marble," "founded as the rock" — had Fleance died. The alliterative chain "cabin'd, cribb'd, confin'd" is a verbal prison, each word tightening the sense of entrapment. He then recovers with a chilling metaphor: "There the grown serpent lies; the worm that's fled / Hath nature that in time will venom breed, / No teeth for th' present" (015-014) — Banquo is the dead serpent; Fleance is the toothless worm who will, in time, grow venomous.

## Sequence

1. Three murderers enter the park and take their positions (014-001–014-006)
2. Banquo and Fleance enter with a torch (implied from stage direction context)
3. The murderers assault Banquo; he calls out "O, treachery!" and urges Fleance to fly (014-016–014-017)
4. Banquo dies; Fleance escapes (014-018)
5. The murderers report back to Macbeth at the banquet, confessing that Fleance escaped (015-010–015-014)

## Immediate Consequences

The escape transforms the murder from a success into a partial failure. At the banquet, when the First Murderer reports the news, Macbeth's elation at Banquo's death collapses into renewed fear (015-011). The ghost of Banquo that appears to Macbeth at the feast is a psychological manifestation of this unfinished business — the prophecy still lives, and Banquo's spectral presence haunts the usurper.

## Long-term Significance

Fleance's escape preserves the dramatic tension of the prophecy throughout the remainder of the play. As long as Fleance lives, Macbeth's dynasty is not secure — despite his crown, he has "filed his mind" and "given his eternal jewel to the common enemy of man" for a barren victory. The escape also provides the historical connection to the Stuart line: James I of England (Shakespeare's patron) was believed to be descended from Banquo's line through Fleance, making this moment of escape a political compliment to the reigning monarch embedded within the tragedy.

- [[darkness]] — Fleance escapes because the torch is extinguished; the darkness that Macbeth has embraced becomes the means by which his plans unravel
- [[child]] — Banquo's dying act is to save his child, contrasting with Macbeth's childlessness and his murder of children
