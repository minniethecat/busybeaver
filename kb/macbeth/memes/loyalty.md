# Loyalty

Loyalty is the foundational political virtue of the feudal world depicted in *Macbeth* — the bond between king and subject, host and guest, that holds the social order together. The play's tragedy unfolds as Macbeth progressively violates every loyalty he owes: to his king, his kinsman, his guest, his friend, and ultimately his country. By the final act, loyalty has recalibrated around Malcolm as the legitimate centre of allegiance, and Macbeth's reign collapses under the weight of universal disloyalty.

## Loyalty as Feudal Bond

The play's language of loyalty is established in the opening acts through Duncan's relationship with his thanes. When Macbeth articulates the feudal ideal, he speaks the play's purest statement of loyal service:

> The service and the loyalty I owe,
> In doing it, pays itself.
>
> (004-005)

The subject's service is its own reward — a duty performed for its own sake rather than for material gain. Duncan's response — that he has "begun to plant thee, and will labour / To make thee full of growing" (004-007) — confirms the reciprocal nature of feudal loyalty: the king nurtures and rewards, the subject serves and protects. This bond is what Macbeth's ambition will shatter.

## Disloyalty as Treason

The play introduces the concept of violated loyalty through the original Thane of Cawdor, whose treachery in the opening battle against Norway is reported by Ross: "Assisted by that most disloyal traitor, / The Thane of Cawdor" (002-017). Cawdor functions as a structural prefiguration of Macbeth: the title passes from one disloyal thane to another. Duncan's reflection on the executed Cawdor — "There's no art / To find the mind's construction in the face: / He was a gentleman on whom I built / An absolute trust" (004-003) — applies with bitter irony to his own murderer, who is even then crossing the threshold of Inverness as Duncan's trusted host.

## Macbeth's Cascade of Betrayed Loyalties

Macbeth's crimes constitute a systematic destruction of every loyalty that defines his social identity. As Duncan's **kinsman and subject**, he owes the king a double duty of protection: "He's here in double trust: / First, as I am his kinsman and his subject, / Strong both against the deed; then, as his host, / Who should against his murderer shut the door, / Not bear the knife myself" (007-003). Each word — kinsman, subject, host — names a bond that Macbeth must violate to commit the murder.

As Banquo's **friend and comrade**, Macbeth shares the battlefield and the prophecy, yet he arranges Banquo's assassination to secure his own line. As **king**, he owes Scotland justice and protection, yet he rules as a tyrant whose "sole name blisters our tongues" (020-004). By the final act, he has no loyalties left to violate, and no one left loyal to him: his soldiers "move only in command, / Nothing in love" (022-019).

## The Tyrant's Weaponized Charge of Disloyalty

Paradoxically, as Macbeth becomes the supreme traitor, he becomes obsessed with the disloyalty of others. Malcolm's feigned confession — that he would "forge / Quarrels unjust against the good and loyal, / Destroying them for wealth" (020-016) — describes the tyrant's method of fabricating charges of disloyalty to eliminate opposition. This is precisely how Macbeth's Scotland operates: accusation of treason becomes a tool of terror, and the concept of loyalty is perverted into a weapon against the genuinely loyal.

## The Restoration of Rightful Loyalty

The play's resolution involves the re-centring of loyalty around Malcolm as the legitimate heir. Macduff's journey from Macbeth's court to the English camp represents a transfer of allegiance from the tyrant to the true king. His climactic declaration to Malcolm — "What I am truly, / Is thine and my poor country's to command" (020-047) — restores the language of feudal service to its proper object. When the rebels converge on Dunsinane and Macbeth falls, loyalty has been cleansed of its corruption and returned to its legitimate centre: the line of Duncan.
