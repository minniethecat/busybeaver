# Omen

In *Macbeth*, omens are the play's cosmic alarm system — disruptions in the natural world that mirror the moral disruption of regicide. When Macbeth kills Duncan, the universe itself registers the crime: falcons are killed by owls, horses eat each other, and darkness swallows the day.

## Definition & Context

In early modern thought, the natural order and the political order were linked by a great chain of being. A crime against the king — God's deputy — was expected to produce disturbances in nature. Shakespeare draws on this cosmology to transform Duncan's murder from a political act into a cosmic rupture. The omens that follow are not decorative; they are evidence that the world has been fundamentally broken.

## Textual Evidence

### The Night of the Murder (Act II)

> 'Tis unnatural, / Even like the deed that's done. On Tuesday last, / A falcon, towering in her pride of place, / Was by a mousing owl hawk'd at and kill'd. (011-004)

The Old Man reports the first omen: a falcon, the bird of kings, has been killed by an owl, a bird of darkness. The hierarchy of nature has been inverted — exactly as the political hierarchy has been upended by Macbeth's blade.

> And Duncan's horses (a thing most strange and certain), / Beauteous and swift, the minions of their race, / Turn'd wild in nature, broke their stalls, flung out, / Contending 'gainst obedience, as they would / Make war with mankind. (011-005)

Ross adds a second omen: Duncan's own horses, creatures of noble service, have turned cannibalistic. The word "minions" echoes "Valour's minion" (the description of Macbeth in Act I), making the omen personal. The horses' rebellion against "obedience" mirrors Macbeth's rebellion against allegiance.

> 'Tis said they eat each other. / They did so, to the amazement of mine eyes / That look'd upon't. (011-005)

The Old Man's final line makes the omen viscerally concrete: horses eating horses. Nature has become a mirror of the new political reality — a kingdom devouring itself.

### Proliferating Signs (Act IV–V)

The witches' apparitions in Act IV function as manufactured omens, deliberately ambiguous signs that Macbeth misreads. And by Act V, the omens have become internalised: Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking is a private omen of guilt, and Macbeth's own "Tomorrow" speech records a world drained of all significance — the endpoint of a cosmos that has been systematically broken.
