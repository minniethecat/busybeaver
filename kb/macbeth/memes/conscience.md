# Conscience

Conscience in *Macbeth* operates not as a reliable moral guide but as a ghost—a faculty that speaks too late, is suppressed until it erupts as hallucination, and ultimately proves powerless to prevent the catastrophe it foresees. Shakespeare traces the arc of conscience from active moral reasoning before Duncan's murder, through its transformation into nightmares and visions, to its final silencing. The tragedy lies partly in conscience's failure: both Macbeth and Lady Macbeth know what they are doing is wrong, and that knowledge torments them, but it cannot stop them.

## Definition & Context

In early modern thought, conscience was understood as the voice of God within the soul—a faculty of moral reasoning that applied divine law to particular actions. It was both intellectual (reasoning about right and wrong) and emotional (producing guilt or peace). Shakespeare's treatment of conscience in *Macbeth* draws on this tradition but darkens it considerably: conscience here is a faculty that can be heard, ignored, and eventually numbed—but never fully extinguished. Its return as hallucination (the dagger, Banquo's ghost, Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking) suggests that suppressed conscience does not disappear; it resurfaces in forms beyond rational control.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> MACBETH. He's here in double trust: First, as I am his kinsman and his subject, Strong both against the deed; then, as his host, Who should against his murderer shut the door, Not bear the knife myself. (007-002)

Before the murder, conscience speaks in Macbeth's careful moral reasoning. He catalogues the reasons against killing Duncan with precision: Duncan is his kinsman, his king, his guest. This is conscience as active, deliberative faculty—weighing duties and prohibitions. But the very clarity of Macbeth's moral logic makes his subsequent decision all the more damning: he knows exactly what he is violating.

> MACBETH. Is this a dagger which I see before me, The handle toward my hand? (009-014)

The dagger vision marks the transformation of conscience from rational deliberation to hallucinatory torment. Macbeth's "heat-oppressed brain" (009-008) projects his moral conflict onto the world. The dagger "marshall'st me the way that I was going" (009-014)—conscience no longer argues; it shows him what he is about to do and lets him walk toward it. This is conscience as witness rather than guide.

> MACBETH. Methought I heard a voice cry, "Sleep no more! Macbeth does murder sleep"—the innocent sleep. (009-022)

After the murder, conscience speaks as an auditory hallucination. The voice that cries "Sleep no more" is Macbeth's own moral sense, now externalized into a quasi-supernatural judgment. The punishment for killing conscience is the loss of "innocent sleep"—peace, rest, the daily renewal of the self. Macbeth will never sleep innocently again.

### Deepening (Act III)

> MACBETH. Then comes my fit again: I had else been perfect; Whole as the marble, founded as the rock. (012-061)

Banquo's ghost at the feast is conscience made visible to Macbeth alone. His "fit"—the terror that seizes him—is the return of the suppressed. Significantly, Lady Macbeth does not see the ghost: conscience has become a private Hell, isolating Macbeth from the social world of the banquet.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> DOCTOR. Unnatural deeds Do breed unnatural troubles: infected minds To their deaf pillows will discharge their secrets. (023-032)

The Doctor's diagnosis of Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking is the play's most clinical description of conscience's return. "Unnatural deeds" (regicide) breed "unnatural troubles" (somnambulistic confession). The "infected mind" discharges its secrets "to deaf pillows"—conscience speaks, but to no one who can help. It is a faculty that can torment but cannot redeem.

> LADY MACBETH. Here's the smell of the blood still: all the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand. Oh, oh, oh! (023-032)

Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking confession is conscience in its most pitiable form. She who once declared "a little water clears us of this deed" (009-030) now cannot wash the imagined blood from her hands. Her conscience has returned not as moral reasoning but as sensory hallucination—the smell of blood that no perfume can mask. The "Oh, oh, oh!" is the sound of a conscience that can feel but cannot articulate, can suffer but cannot repent.

## Character Associations

| Character | Relationship to Conscience | Key Scene |
|-----------|---------------------------|-----------|
| Macbeth | Conscience speaks clearly before the murder (007-002) but is suppressed. It returns as hallucination—the dagger, the voice, Banquo's ghost—but never regains its power to stop him. | 009-014 |
| Lady Macbeth | She calls on spirits to "stop up the access and passage to remorse" (005-012), deliberately suppressing conscience. It returns in her sleepwalking, when conscious control is impossible. | 023-032 |
| Banquo | The foil: he keeps his "bosom franchised and allegiance clear" (008-014), refusing to let ambition override conscience. His ghost haunts Macbeth precisely because Banquo represents the moral path not taken. | 008-014 |
