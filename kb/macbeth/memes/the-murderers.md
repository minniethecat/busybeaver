**The Murderers** are a group of hired killers employed by Macbeth to eliminate his political enemies. They appear in three scenes across Acts III and IV, serving as instruments of Macbeth's escalating violence. Though unnamed and socially marginal, their dialogue provides a window into the play's exploration of desperation, loyalty, and the moral economy of murder.

## The First Encounter (Act III, Scene I)

Macbeth summons two murderers to the palace. His conversation with them reveals that he has been priming them over multiple meetings, convincing them that Banquo — not he — is responsible for their misfortunes:

> MACBETH. Know / That it was he, in the times past, which held you / So under fortune, which you thought had been / Our innocent self? (012-057)

The murderers' responses are terse but telling. The First Murderer's reply — "You made it known to us" (012-058) — suggests men who have accepted Macbeth's narrative. When Macbeth questions whether they have the resolve to kill, the Second Murderer declares:

> I am one, my liege, / Whom the vile blows and buffets of the world / Have so incens'd that I am reckless what / I do to spite the world. (012-062)

The First Murderer echoes: "And I another / So weary with disasters, tugg'd with fortune, / That I would set my life on any chance, / To mend it or be rid on't" (012-063). These speeches reveal men pushed to the edge of existence — their willingness to kill is born of despair, not ambition. Both agree to murder Banquo and Fleance, and both declare themselves willing to do anything Macbeth commands (012-065).

Macbeth introduces a third murderer in Act III, Scene III (014-001), who joins the original two for the ambush at the palace gate. The trio kill Banquo but Fleance escapes — a failure that will haunt Macbeth.

## The Attack on Macduff's Family (Act IV, Scene II)

The murderers return in Act IV, Scene II, where they attack Macduff's castle at Fife. Their target is Lady Macduff and her young son. The scene is one of the play's most brutal: after tense wordplay between the boy and his mother, the murderers enter with the cry "What, you egg! / Young fry of treachery!" (019-045) and stab the child. Lady Macduff is pursued offstage "crying 'Murder!'" (019-047).

## Dramatic Function

The murderers serve multiple functions in the play's architecture. First, they mark Macbeth's transition from hands-on killer to distant orchestrator — he no longer does the killing himself but delegates it to society's desperate. Second, their self-descriptions ("reckless what I do to spite the world") mirror Macbeth's own nihilism in miniature, foreshadowing his "Tomorrow" soliloquy. Third, the murder of Macduff's son — a child killed onstage — represents the moral nadir of Macbeth's reign, exceeding even Duncan's murder in its senselessness.
