# Seyton

Seyton is Macbeth's armor-bearer and sole remaining attendant in the final act of Shakespeare's *Macbeth*. Though a minor character in terms of lines, his name -- homophonous with "Satan" -- and his function as Macbeth's last loyal servant make him a potent figure in the play's final movement.

## Dramatic Role

Seyton appears only in Act V, Scenes iii and v, during Macbeth's final stand at Dunsinane. His role is starkly functional: he helps arm Macbeth for battle and delivers reports from the field. Yet his presence resonates beyond his practical function.

Macbeth's first words to Seyton establish the tone of the final act: "Seyton! -- I am sick at heart, when I behold -- Seyton, I say!" (023-010)(023-011). The repeated, urgent call of Seyton's name, its Satanic echo audible to the audience, creates an atmosphere of diabolical association around the doomed king.

When Seyton enters, Macbeth immediately demands his armor: "Give me my armour" (023-021)(023-031). The arming scene, a ritual of preparation for battle, here becomes a gesture of desperate defiance. Seyton's presence as the last attendant who will arm Macbeth underscores how thoroughly the king has been abandoned -- all his thanes have fled, and only a household servant remains.

## Key Speeches

### "It is the cry of women, my good lord"

In Act V, Scene v, Seyton re-enters to report on a distant cry. When Macbeth asks what noise it was, Seyton replies: "It is the cry of women, my good lord" (025-005). Macbeth's indifference to the news -- he has "almost forgot the taste of fears" -- is juxtaposed with the audience's knowledge that the cry is for Lady Macbeth's death, revealed moments later.

Seyton returns moments later with the news that changes everything:

> SEYTON.
> The Queen, my lord, is dead.
>
> (025-008)

It is Seyton who delivers the play's most economical and devastating line. In five words -- "The Queen, my lord, is dead" -- he announces the death that triggers Macbeth's "Tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow" speech, the philosophical heart of the tragedy. That this news comes from a household servant, not a thane or messenger, underscores how completely the great affairs of Scotland have contracted to the walls of Dunsinane.
