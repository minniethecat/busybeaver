# Thane

A **thane** in *Macbeth* is a Scottish feudal noble holding lands and title from the king. The rank runs through the play like a spine: Macbeth's own story is told through the titles he acquires and loses—thane of Glamis, thane of Cawdor, and the kingship he seizes in between.

## Definition & Context

In eleventh-century Scotland, a thane was a hereditary landholder owing military service to the crown, roughly equivalent to an Anglo-Saxon thegn or a later baron. Shakespeare inherited the term from Holinshed's *Chronicles* and uses it as the play's default designation for the Scottish nobility. The word "thane" appears across all five acts and in the mouths of nearly every major character, making it one of the most pervasive lexical markers of the play's feudal world. The thanes collectively form the political community whose assent—or resistance—determines the legitimacy of the crown.

## Textual Evidence

### Macbeth's Elevation (Act I)

> Whence cam'st thou, worthy thane? (002-016)

Duncan's first address to Macbeth on the battlefield uses "thane" as a term of honour. The king's word elevates the soldier.

> Hail to thee, Thane of Glamis! / Hail to thee, Thane of Cawdor! / All hail, Macbeth, that shalt be king hereafter! (003-040–003-042)

The witches' greeting maps the arc of the play in three titles. At the moment of the prophecy, Macbeth holds only Glamis; Cawdor is still alive and the kingship belongs to Duncan. The sequence of thaneships—Glamis to Cawdor—becomes the stepping-stone logic that makes regicide thinkable.

### The Political Community (Act II)

> Sons, kinsmen, thanes, / And you whose places are the farthest, know (004-008–004-009)

Duncan, dying, addresses the thanes as his political family. The thanes are the kingdom's constitution: their loyalty is the foundation of legitimate rule.

> Our thane is coming. (005-005)

A servant announces Macbeth's return to Inverness. The possessive "our" signals a community that Macbeth still belongs to—before the murder severs that bond.

> Your face, my thane, is as a book where men / May read strange matters. (005-034–005-035)

Lady Macbeth's wifely counsel carries the feudal title as a term of intimate address, collapsing the distance between domestic partnership and political conspiracy.

### The Collapse of Loyalty (Act II–III)

> Why, worthy thane, / You do unbend your noble strength (009-025–009-026)

Banquo's last use of "thane" to Macbeth, on the night of the murder, still carries the old friendship.

> Is the King stirring, worthy thane? (010-014)

Macduff's innocent question at the gate marks the last moment before the murder is discovered. "Worthy thane" will soon become impossible to say honestly.

### The Rebellion (Act V)

> Then fly, false thanes, / And mingle with the English epicures (023-002–023-003)

Macbeth, now king and tyrant, dismisses the thanes who have abandoned him as "false"—a word that echoes the original Thane of Cawdor's treachery.

> The noble thanes do bravely in the war (027-017)

Siward's battlefield report restores "noble" to the thanes. The word has been reclaimed by the forces of legitimacy.

> My thanes and kinsmen, / Henceforth be earls (028-028–028-029)

Malcolm's first act as king is to rename the thanes as earls—a deliberate break with the Scottish feudal vocabulary and a gesture toward English political forms. The thane, like the tyrant, belongs to a chapter that is being closed.

## Critical Perspectives

The word "thane" in *Macbeth* does more than denote a feudal rank. It tracks legitimacy: before the murder, "thane" is an honour; after the murder, it becomes a lie that Macbeth's court must keep telling. Malcolm's closing gesture—elevating thanes to earls—is not just a promotion but a symbolic re-founding of the political order in a language that Macbeth never corrupted.
