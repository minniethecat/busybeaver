# Hell (in Macbeth)

**Hell** appears throughout *Macbeth* not as a literal setting but as a theological reference point — the destiny that awaits those who violate [[nature|natural order]], the domain of [[hecate|Hecate]] and her witches, and the comic territory of the Porter who imagines himself the gatekeeper of the underworld. In a play structured around the consequences of damnable acts, hell is the shadow that falls across every scene.

## The Porter Scene: Hell as Dark Comedy

The play's most sustained engagement with hell comes through the Porter in Act II, Scene III. Responding to the knocking at the gate — which we know signals the discovery of [[duncan|Duncan]]'s body — the Porter imagines himself as the porter of hell-gate:

>If a man were porter of hell gate, he should have old turning the key. (010-002)

He runs through a series of imagined sinners — a farmer who hanged himself, an equivocator who "could swear in both the scales against either scale" — before admitting [[macduff|Macduff]] and [[lennox|Lennox]]. The scene's dark comedy operates through dramatic irony: the audience knows that Macduff is about to discover a murder that makes the castle a kind of hell on earth. The Porter's fantasy is closer to reality than he knows.

## Hell in the Witches' Imagery

The witches' supernatural power is consistently described in hellish terms. The cauldron scene in Act IV is a descent into the infernal:

>Like a hell-broth boil and bubble. (018-007)

And Macbeth's final summons — "I conjure you, by that which you profess" — is answered by apparitions that speak with the authority of the pit. [[hecate|Hecate]], the goddess who rules the witches, meets them at "the pit of Acheron" — a classical reference to one of the rivers of the underworld.

## Macbeth's Damnation

Macbeth himself is acutely aware that his actions have damned him. After the murder of Duncan, he cannot say "Amen" to the prayer he overhears:

>I had most need of blessing, and "Amen"
>Stuck in my throat. (009-005)

By the play's end, he has accepted his fate. "I have lived long enough," he tells Seyton, recognizing that he has traded his "eternal jewel" — his soul — to "the common enemy of man" (016-015). The hell he inhabits is not a future destination but a present reality: the isolation of a tyrant who has cut every bond of loyalty and love.

## Lady Macbeth's Descent

[[lady-macbeth|Lady Macbeth]], who called on "thick night" and "the dunnest smoke of hell" (005-021) to conceal her deed, is ultimately consumed by the darkness she invoked. Her sleepwalking cry — "Hell is murky!" (021-016) — is one of the play's most compressed theological statements. The woman who thought she could wield hell as a tool discovers that hell wields her.
