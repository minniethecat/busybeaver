The angel in *Macbeth* represents the divine order that stands in judgment over human acts of evil. Angelic imagery enters the play at its most pivotal moral moment — Macbeth's soliloquy before the murder of Duncan — and recurs through the tragedy as a measure of how far Scotland and its [[tyrant]] have fallen from [[grace]].

## Heaven's Cherubin: The Witness Against Murder

The play's most concentrated angelic imagery appears in Macbeth's Act I, Scene vii soliloquy, as he contemplates the murder he is about to commit. He imagines Duncan's virtues pleading for justice before the heavens:

> MACBETH. Besides, this Duncan
> Hath borne his faculties so meek, hath been
> So clear in his great office, that his virtues
> Will plead like angels, trumpet-tongued, against
> The deep damnation of his taking-off.
> (007-002)

The simile "like angels, trumpet-tongued" draws on the Book of Revelation, where angels sound trumpets to announce divine judgment. Macbeth imagines Duncan's virtues — his meekness, his clarity, his fitness for office — as angelic advocates that will cry out for vengeance. The phrase "trumpet-tongued" is doubly powerful: it suggests both the piercing clarity of angelic speech and the apocalyptic urgency of the Last Judgment. Even before committing the deed, Macbeth already hears the [[heaven|heavenly]] court pronouncing his damnation.

## The Cherubin: Sightless Couriers of Justice

Shakespeare sharpens the angelic image with a specific theological figure — the "cherubin" — in the same soliloquy:

> MACBETH. And pity, like a naked new-born babe,
> Striding the blast, or heaven's cherubin, hors'd
> Upon the sightless couriers of the air,
> Shall blow the horrid deed in every eye,
> That tears shall drown the wind.
> (007-002)

The "cherubin" (cherubim) are not the chubby infants of Renaissance painting but the fierce guardians of sacred thresholds in the Hebrew Bible — the beings placed at the gate of Eden with a flaming sword. Macbeth conflates two images here: the vulnerability of a naked infant and the terrifying power of angelic heralds. The "sightless couriers of the air" — the invisible winds — become the cherubin's steeds, carrying the news of Duncan's murder across creation. The line "shall blow the horrid deed in every eye" completes the trumpet metaphor: the cherubin do not merely announce judgment; they force every eye to witness it. Shakespeare's syntax makes the cherubin and pity inseparable — divine compassion and divine vengeance ride the same wind.

## Holy Angels and the Flight to England

As Scotland descends into tyranny, angelic imagery shifts from warning to rescue. In Act III, Scene vi, a Lord expresses the hope that Malcolm and Macduff will receive divine assistance in their mission to save Scotland:

> LORD. Some holy angel
> Fly to the court of England, and unfold
> His message ere he come, that a swift blessing
> May soon return to this our suffering country
> Under a hand accurs'd!
> (017-006)

The "holy angel" here is not a figure of judgment but of deliverance — a messenger sent ahead to prepare England for the arrival of Scotland's rightful heir. The language of blessing and suffering sets up a stark opposition: the "hand accurs'd" of Macbeth versus the angelic hand of providence. The prayer-like quality of the Lord's speech ("Fly to the court of England") suggests that divine intervention is the only remaining hope for a nation crushed under tyranny.

## The Angel of Destruction

The angel image undergoes a final, terrible inversion in Act V. As Macbeth faces the army advancing on Dunsinane, he taunts Macduff with the prophecy that no man of woman born can harm him:

> MACBETH. Despair thy charm;
> And let the angel whom thou still hast serv'd
> Tell thee, from his soft and downy couch,
> That none of woman born shall harm Macbeth.
> (028-008)

Macbeth's "angel" here is not divine but demonic — the dark spirit he has served since he first entertained the thought of [[regicide]]. The "soft and downy couch" mocks the comfort of true angels, attributing to Macbeth's [[devil|diabolical]] patron the ease of a pampered courtier. This ironic usage marks the nadir of Macbeth's spiritual journey: he has exchanged the trumpet-tongued angels of judgment for a fallen spirit of false comfort.

## Symbolic Architecture

The trajectory of angelic imagery traces the play's moral arc. In Act I, angels are witnesses to innocence — the cherubin poised to carry Duncan's virtues across the world like a trumpet blast. In Act III, they are potential rescuers, invoked in prayer for Scotland's deliverance. By Act V, the "angel" has become a mockery — the devil Macbeth has served, lounging insouciantly while his servant marches toward destruction. The word that once signified divine justice now signifies demonic delusion.

The angel in *Macbeth* is never actually present — never descends onto the stage as a character, as it might in a miracle play or a romance. It exists only in language: in similes, in prayers, in taunts. This linguistic fragility mirrors the play's larger theological anxiety: in a world where a king can be murdered in his sleep, where are the angels? The answer, perhaps, is that they have fled — and their absence is the true measure of Macbeth's [[evil]].
