# Fleance

Fleance is the son of Banquo in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. He appears briefly in the play, yet his survival is pivotal: the witches prophesied that Banquo's descendants would be kings, and Fleance's escape from the murderers ensures the potential fulfillment of that prophecy.

## Overview

Fleance first appears alongside his father Banquo in Act II, Scene I at Inverness (008-001). It is late at night, and Fleance remarks that the moon has set and he has not heard the clock (008-003)(008-005). Banquo, troubled by dark thoughts, sends Fleance away before Macbeth enters and delivers the famous "dagger" soliloquy.

Fleance's most significant moment occurs offstage: in Act III, Scene III, Macbeth sends murderers to ambush Banquo and Fleance. Banquo is killed, but Fleance escapes. This escape is crucial — the Weird Sisters had prophesied that Banquo's children would inherit the throne, and Fleance's survival means Macbeth's attempt to thwart fate has failed. Macbeth's fear of Banquo's line is explicit: "They hail'd him father to a line of kings" (012-039).

## Dramatic Significance

Fleance himself has minimal dialogue and character development, but his narrative function is profound. He is the living thread connecting the present tragedy to a future beyond Macbeth's reach — a future where Banquo's "issue" shall reign (003-035). His escape creates dramatic irony: the audience knows that Macbeth, despite his ruthless efforts, cannot fully secure his ill-gotten crown. Historically, the play also flatters King James I, who traced his lineage back to Banquo, making Fleance's survival a political as well as dramatic necessity.
