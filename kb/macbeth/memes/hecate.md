# Hecate

**Hecate** is the goddess of witchcraft in Shakespeare's *Macbeth*, ruling over [[the-witches|the three Witches]] and commanding the supernatural forces that shape the play's central prophecies. She appears in two key scenes — berating the Witches for acting without her authority, and later orchestrating the final set of apparitions that seal [[macbeth|Macbeth]]'s fate.

## Role in the Play

Hecate's presence elevates the Witches from mere fortune-tellers to agents of a structured supernatural order. Her anger at being excluded from the Witches' dealings with Macbeth reveals a hierarchy within the play's dark powers:

> Have I not reason, beldams as you are,
> Saucy and overbold? How did you dare
> To trade and traffic with Macbeth
> In riddles and affairs of death... (016-002)

This speech establishes that the Witches' prophecies were not random mischief but a usurpation of Hecate's authority — a violation of the proper supernatural order that mirrors the political disorder Macbeth creates in Scotland.

## Key Appearances

### Act III, Scene V — Confrontation with the Witches

Hecate's first entrance is one of rebuke. She finds the three Witches have been meddling with Macbeth without her knowledge, offering him prophecies that set him on his bloody path. Her anger is not moral — she does not object to destroying Macbeth — but territorial: she was not consulted. She commands the Witches to meet her at "the pit of Acheron" where Macbeth will come to learn his destiny:

> I am for th' air; this night I'll spend
> Unto a dismal and a fatal end. (016-002)

### Act IV, Scene I — The Cauldron Scene

Hecate's second appearance is brief but pivotal. After the Witches have prepared their cauldron with "eye of newt and toe of frog," (018-011) Hecate enters to commend their work before departing (018-014), leaving them to summon the three apparitions that give Macbeth his final, deceptive prophecies.

## Symbolic Significance

Hecate represents the play's concern with **order and its violation**. Even evil, in Shakespeare's cosmology, has its proper hierarchy. When the Witches bypass Hecate, they mirror Macbeth's own usurpation of [[duncan|Duncan]] — both acts violate the natural chain of command. Her restoration of control in Act IV parallels the eventual restoration of legitimate rule under [[malcolm|Malcolm]].

As the goddess associated with "pale Hecate's off'rings" (008-039) and "black Hecate's summons," (013-014) she embodies the dark, chthonic forces that the Witches channel but cannot command. Her presence reminds the audience that the supernatural in *Macbeth* is not chaos — it is a rival order with its own rules and ruler.
