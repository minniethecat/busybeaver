# Grace

In *Macbeth*, grace is the spiritual condition that the protagonist progressively forfeits — the divine favour and capacity for prayer that separates the righteous from the damned, and whose loss signals Macbeth's irreversible moral collapse.

## Definition & Context

In Jacobean Christian theology, grace (*gratia*) was the unmerited favour of God that enabled salvation. Shakespeare deploys the concept with precision: grace is what Macbeth possessed as Duncan's loyal thane and what he loses the moment he murders his king. The play charts not merely a political fall but a *theological* one — from a state of grace to a state of reprobation. The Porter's comic hell-gate scene (II.iii) literalises this transition, casting Macbeth's castle as a place where grace has been expelled.

## Textual Evidence

### Initial State of Grace (Acts I–II)

> You greet with present grace and great prediction / Of noble having and of royal hope (003-021)

Banquo's early lines establish that grace is a gift bestowed — here by the witches' prophecy. The word slides between its secular sense ("gracious favour") and its theological weight.

> And shall continue our graces towards him. (006-009)

Duncan's language of "graces" reflects the king's role as God's deputy; to extend grace is a royal and divine prerogative. Macbeth will later invert this by extending murder instead.

### The Loss (Acts II–III)

> But wherefore could not I pronounce "Amen"? / I had most need of blessing, and "Amen" / Stuck in my throat. (009-018)

This is the play's definitive moment of lost grace. Macbeth's inability to speak the simplest prayer signals his severance from the divine order. He recognises his need ("most need of blessing") but cannot access grace. The physical image — "stuck in my throat" — makes theological alienation viscerally concrete.

> All is but toys: renown and grace is dead; / The wine of life is drawn, and the mere lees / Is left this vault to brag of. (010-047)

Macbeth's own diagnosis: grace is "dead." He has killed it along with Duncan. The metaphor shifts to drained wine — what remains is dregs, a hollow shell of existence. This is existential despair rooted in theological loss.

### Grace Restored Elsewhere (Acts IV–V)

> He hath a heavenly gift of prophecy, / And sundry blessings hang about his throne, / That speak him full of grace. (017-003)

The description of Edward the Confessor — the English king — offers a pointed contrast. While Scotland suffers under Macbeth the tyrant, England's king is "full of grace." The political and the theological are inseparable: a legitimate king channels grace; a usurper blocks it.
