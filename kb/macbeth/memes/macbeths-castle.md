# Macbeth's Castle

Macbeth's two castles — Inverness in the early acts and Dunsinane in the later acts — serve as the play's central architectural metaphor for the condition of his rule. At Inverness, Duncan arrives as an honoured guest and is murdered; at Dunsinane, Macbeth waits as a besieged tyrant while the prophecies close around him.

## Inverness: The Castle of Hospitality and Murder

Duncan's arrival at Inverness is greeted by Lady Macbeth's performance of welcome: "All our service / In every point twice done, and then done double" (006-006-007). The language of hospitality conceals the murder already planned. Banquo's observation that "the temple-haunting martlet" has "made his pendant bed and procreant cradle" at the castle (006-004-005) ironically praises the site's peacefulness even as it becomes the scene of regicide.

## Dunsinane: The Castle of Siege

By Act V, Dunsinane has become Macbeth's fortress and prison. His confidence in its walls — "Our castle's strength / Will laugh a siege to scorn" (025-004-005) — directly echoes the Apparitions' promises, and is shattered when the prophesied Birnam Wood comes to Dunsinane. The castle that was to protect him becomes the site of his death.
