# Knell

The knell in *Macbeth* is the sound of irrevocable action—a bell that rings not to announce death but to enact it, transforming the murderer's signal into a cosmic summons.

## Definition & Context

In early modern England, the "passing bell" or "death knell" was tolled to mark a death and summon prayers for the departing soul. Shakespeare exploits this ritual association throughout *Macbeth*, but he intensifies it: the knell is not rung after death but before it, becoming an agent of the murder rather than its announcement. The bell's sound marks the threshold between intention and action, between the living king and the murdered corpse, between heaven and hell. Each toll of the bell in the play registers a step in Macbeth's descent.

## Textual Evidence

### The First Knell (Act II)

> Hear it not, Duncan, for it is a knell / That summons thee to heaven or to hell. (008-051–008-052)

Macbeth's couplet closes the dagger soliloquy (Act II Scene i) by transforming a practical stage signal—the bell that tells him Duncan's grooms are drugged—into a theological pronouncement. The couplet does three things at once: it acknowledges the bell as a murder signal, it eulogises Duncan ("to heaven"), and it opens the possibility of damnation ("or to hell"). The ambiguity is the point: Macbeth does not know, and perhaps no longer cares, which destination the bell announces.

### The Dead Man's Knell (Act IV)

> The dead man's knell / Is there scarce ask'd for who (020-036)

In Act IV Scene iii, Malcolm describes the state of Scotland under Macbeth's tyranny. The "dead man's knell" is "scarce ask'd for who"—so many die that individual deaths no longer warrant individual bells. The knell has been drained of its ritual meaning; in a kingdom ruled by a serial murderer, death is too common for ceremony.

### The Final Knell (Act V)

> And so his knell is knoll'd. (028-022)

Seyton's report of Macbeth's death to Malcolm in Act V Scene viii closes the bell motif. The wordplay—"knell is knoll'd"—is the sound of an ending. The man who first heard the knell as a summons to murder has become the subject of the final bell. The circle closes.

## Critical Perspectives

The knell has been a touchstone for theatrical and literary analysis since De Quincey's "On the Knocking at the Gate in *Macbeth*." De Quincey argued that the knocking after Duncan's murder restores the audience to the human world; the knell, by contrast, performs the opposite function—it withdraws the human and replaces it with the cosmic. More recent sound-studies approaches have emphasised the play's acoustic landscape: *Macbeth* is a play of knocks, bells, cries, and silences, and the knell is the sound that most directly links human action to supernatural consequence.
