## Definition

Pity in *Macbeth* appears most vividly as a personified force in Macbeth's Act I, Scene vii soliloquy, where the protagonist imagines the murder of Duncan unleashing a cosmic reaction of exposure. Unlike the abstract moral concepts in the play — ambition, guilt, fear — pity is given a body and a trajectory: a newborn infant riding the winds of heaven, blowing the truth of the murder into every eye. The image is the play's most powerful articulation of the moral universe Macbeth is about to violate, and its deliberate suppression marks his irreversible commitment to evil.

## The personification: "pity, like a naked new-born babe"

In the soliloquy that immediately precedes Lady Macbeth's entrance, Macbeth confronts the full moral weight of his intended act. He enumerates the reasons against murder: Duncan is his kinsman, his king, his guest. Then he reaches the climactic image:

> And pity, like a naked new-born babe, / Striding the blast, or heaven's cherubin, hors'd / Upon the sightless couriers of the air, / Shall blow the horrid deed in every eye, / That tears shall drown the wind. (007-035–007-039)

The image is deliberately paradoxical. A newborn babe is the epitome of vulnerability — naked, helpless, dependent — yet here it "strides the blast" with the power of heaven's cherubim. Pity is simultaneously the weakest and the strongest force in the moral universe: weak because it can be ignored by a determined will, strong because its exposure is cosmic in scale. The "tears" that "shall drown the wind" are the universal grief that Duncan's murder will provoke — a flood of pity so overwhelming that it becomes a natural catastrophe.

## The strategic deployment of the image

Macbeth's vision of pity is remarkable for what it reveals about his moral imagination. At this moment, before the murder, he can still see the act from the outside — can still imagine how the world will react. The personification of pity as an infant riding the wind is not a rational argument but a visceral image, and it nearly persuades him. "I have no spur / To prick the sides of my intent, but only / Vaulting ambition" (007-040–007-042) — the soliloquy ends with Macbeth's recognition that ambition alone drives him, against every moral intuition he possesses.

Lady Macbeth's entrance immediately after this image — and her systematic demolition of his hesitation — constitutes the play's crucial moral turning point. She replaces pity with scorn, the babe with the "babe that milks me" (007-069) which she claims she would dash its brains out rather than break a vow. The personified pity of Macbeth's imagination is crushed by the anti-pity of Lady Macbeth's rhetoric.

## The suppression of pity

After the murder, pity disappears from the play's vocabulary. Macbeth invokes it once more — sarcastically — when he claims at the banquet that Banquo's absence is a matter of "unkindness" rather than "pity for mischance" (015-033), knowing full well Banquo is dead by his order. The word has become a weapon of hypocrisy. The play's trajectory from the cosmic pity of Act I to the nihilistic void of Act V — "signifying nothing" — is the story of a world from which pity has been systematically expelled, leaving only the "dusty death" toward which all yesterdays have lighted fools.

## Pity Hollowed: The Banquet Scene

At the banquet in Act III, Macbeth — who knows that Banquo lies murdered by his order — deploys the word "pity" as a weapon of hypocritical concern:

> Here had we now our country's honour roof'd, Were the grac'd person of our Banquo present; Who may I rather challenge for unkindness Than pity for mischance! (015-019)

The irony is savage. Macbeth invokes pity for Banquo's absence while knowing that Banquo's "mischance" was murder at his command. The word that once embodied the cosmic horror of Duncan's murder — the naked babe striding the blast — has been hollowed out into a political performance. This is the play's darkest demonstration of how thoroughly the moral imagination can be corrupted by power.
