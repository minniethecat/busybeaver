# Banquet

In *Macbeth*, the banquet—a ritual of hospitality, community, and legitimate rule—becomes the stage on which Macbeth's guilt is publicly exposed and his authority fatally undermined.

## Definition & Context

The banquet in Shakespeare's Scotland is not merely a meal; it is a ceremony of political order. The king who hosts a feast demonstrates his capacity to gather the realm's nobility around a shared table, symbolising unity and legitimate authority. When Macbeth's coronation banquet in Act III Scene iv is shattered by the apparition of Banquo's ghost, the disruption is more than personal embarrassment—it is the collapse of the social and political order that Macbeth's usurpation has already fractured. The Jacobean audience would have recognised the banquet as a microcosm of the body politic: a disordered feast signals a disordered kingdom.

## Textual Evidence

### The Ideal Banquet (Act I–II)

Duncan's court is defined by feasting and hospitality. In Act I Scene iv, Duncan publicly honours Macbeth and Banquo after the battle, framing royal gratitude as a form of nourishment: "It is a banquet to me," he tells Macbeth, treating loyalty itself as a feast. Lady Macbeth's role as hostess in Act I Scene vii—"We are your guest tonight," Duncan tells her (006-002)—establishes the sacred bond of host and guest that Macbeth will violate.

### The Disrupted Feast (Act III Scene iv)

> A banquet prepared. Enter Macbeth, Lady Macbeth, Ross, Lennox, Lords and Attendants. (015-001)

The stage direction opens Act III Scene iv with apparent order: the banquet is "prepared," the nobility is assembled. Macbeth begins as the gracious host—"Ourself will mingle with society"—but the appearance of Banquo's ghost shatters the performance. The ghost occupies "our stool" (Macbeth's own seat, symbolically usurping the usurper), and Macbeth's terrified reaction exposes his guilt to the assembled lords.

> [Ghost disappears.] (015-033)

The first disappearance of the ghost provides only temporary relief. Lady Macbeth attempts damage control, chiding her husband's fear as unmanly. But when the ghost reappears and vanishes again (015-044), the banquet is irrecoverable. Macbeth's declaration—"I am a man again. Pray you, sit still" (015-045–015-046)—rings hollow; the lords depart in disorder (015-053), and the feast ends in dissolution.

### Aftermath (Act III Scene vi)

Lennox's sardonic commentary in Act III Scene vi reflects on the broken social contract: "Free from our feasts and banquets bloody knives" (016-006). The line is bitter irony — Lennox wishes for feasts without murder, banquets without blood, but in Macbeth's Scotland, every ceremony of order has become a stage for violence. The banquet has become a figure for the perversion of hospitality—the host who kills his guests.

## Dramatic Structure: The Banquet as Pivot

The banquet scene falls at the exact structural centre of the play and functions as Macbeth's public turning point:

| Before the Banquet (Acts I–III.i–iii) | After the Banquet (Acts III.v–V) |
|---------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Macbeth's crimes are private | Macbeth's guilt is publicly visible |
| Lady Macbeth is the stronger partner | Lady Macbeth begins her descent into madness |
| Macbeth consults the supernatural (witches) | Macbeth consults the supernatural compulsively (apparitions) |
| The court is deceived or silent | The court sees and judges (Lennox's sarcasm) |

Once the banquet is disrupted, Macbeth can never reassemble the social order. The lords "stand not upon the order of their going, but go at once" — a line Ross uses to describe the thanes fleeing the feast, which re-stages the breakdown of ceremony as the breakdown of lordship itself.

## Critical Perspectives

The banquet scene has been extensively analysed as a turning point in Macbeth's trajectory. Before this scene, Macbeth's crimes are private; after it, his guilt is visible to the entire court. The ghost's occupation of Macbeth's seat literalises the play's concern with legitimate succession—Banquo's issue, not Macbeth's, will inherit the throne. Performance criticism has noted the scene's theatrical power: the ghost visible to Macbeth and the audience but not to the other characters stages the gap between private guilt and public performance that defines the play's second half.
