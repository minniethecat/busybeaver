# Toil

In *Macbeth*, "toil" appears most memorably in the witches' cauldron refrain — "Double, double toil and trouble" — where it pairs productive labour with supernatural malice. The word transforms the witches' brewing from mere spell-casting into a dark parody of honest work.

## Definition & Context

"Toil" in early modern English meant both "labour" and "struggle" — the effort required to produce something. In the cauldron scene (IV.i), the witches are not merely reciting incantations; they are *working*. Their ingredients — "eye of newt," "toe of frog," "finger of birth-strangled babe" — are grotesque, but the activity is framed as labour: something is being made. The doubling ("double, double") intensifies the sense of relentless production. This is the supernatural as industry: evil that works, and works hard.

## Textual Evidence

### The Cauldron Refrain (Act IV)

> ALL. Double, double toil and trouble; / Fire, burn; and cauldron, bubble. (018-006)

The refrain appears three times in the cauldron scene (018-006, 018-008, 018-010), structuring the witches' brewing as a work-song. The incantatory rhythm mimics manual labour — a supernatural assembly line producing doom for Macbeth. The pairing of "toil" with "trouble" is precise: toil is the means, trouble is the product.

> For a charm of powerful trouble, / Like a hell-broth boil and bubble. (018-007)

Between refrains, the witches declare their purpose: they are making "powerful trouble." The verb "boil" reinforces the labour metaphor — this is cooking, but the kitchen is hell and the dish is Macbeth's destruction.
