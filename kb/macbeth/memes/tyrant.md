# Tyrant

Tyranny is a central political concept in *Macbeth*, marking Macbeth's transformation from heroic general to despised despot. The word "tyrant" and its variants appear ten times in the play, concentrated in the later acts as Scotland turns against its king. The term encapsulates the play's exploration of illegitimate rule, the abuse of power, and the collapse of political order under a usurper.

## The Emergence of the Label (Act III)

The word "tyrant" first enters the play's political discourse in Act III Scene vi, where Lennox, in a heavily ironic conversation with another Lord, refers to Macbeth's "tyrant's feast" (017-002) — the banquet from which Macduff has conspicuously absented himself. Lennox's sarcastic tone reveals that the Scottish nobles have already seen through Macbeth's public performance of grief. The Lord adds that Malcolm, "the son of Duncan, / From whom this tyrant holds the due of birth" (017-003), lives in England, highlighting the fundamental illegitimacy of Macbeth's rule: he occupies a throne that belongs by right to Duncan's heir.

## Malcolm's Political Catalogue (Act IV)

The term reaches its fullest political articulation in Act IV Scene iii, where Malcolm and Macduff debate the nature of kingship and tyranny in the English court. Malcolm, testing Macduff's loyalty, describes Macbeth in terms that acknowledge the terror preventing open speech in Scotland:

> This tyrant, whose sole name blisters our tongues,
> Was once thought honest.
>
> (020-004) When Malcolm feigns his own unworthiness, Macduff laments Scotland's fate under "an untitled tyrant bloody-scepter'd" (020-021) — the compound epithet "bloody-scepter'd" fusing the instruments of royal authority with the violence that now defines Macbeth's reign.

Macduff further reports that "The tyrant has not batter'd at their peace" (020-044), explaining that Macbeth has not yet attacked the English-allied forces. His observation "I saw the tyrant's power afoot" (020-047) confirms that Macbeth's military strength is on the march, setting the stage for the final confrontation.

## The Tyrant Besieged (Act V)

As the rebel forces close in, the word "tyrant" becomes the standard designation for Macbeth among the Scottish thanes. Menteith's blunt question — "What does the tyrant?" (022-006) — treats the label as settled fact, requiring no justification. Caithness describes how "the confident tyrant / Keeps still in Dunsinane" (024-008), his confidence now reading as delusion. The transformation is complete: the "brave Macbeth" of Act I has become a figure universally reviled, his name literally too painful to speak (020-004).

## The Political Vocabulary of Legitimacy

The progression of "tyrant" through the play maps precisely onto Macbeth's moral trajectory. In the early acts, he is "brave Macbeth" (002-004), "valiant cousin" (002-009), "noble Macbeth" (004-003). After Duncan's murder and Banquo's death, the honorifics vanish, replaced by the cold political category of "tyrant." This linguistic shift is not merely descriptive — it is a political act performed by Malcolm's faction, stripping Macbeth of the language of legitimate rule and recasting him as an outlaw king whose overthrow is not regicide but justice.
