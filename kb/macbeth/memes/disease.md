# Disease and Healing

The metaphor of disease runs through *Macbeth* as the play's primary image for political corruption.

Scotland under [[macbeth]] is a sick body, and the king himself is the disease.

## Scotland as a Sick Body

The disease imagery intensifies in Act IV as the effects of Macbeth's tyranny spread.

[[malcolm]] tells [[macduff]] that Scotland "sinks beneath the yoke; It weeps, it bleeds, and each new day a gash is added to her wounds" (020-015).

The body politic bleeds from its ruler's crimes.

When Macduff appeals to Malcolm to return and save Scotland, he frames the country as a dying patient: "Bleed, bleed, poor country! Great tyranny, lay thou thy basis sure, for goodness dares not check thee" (020-025).

The metaphor is not decorative — it encodes the Elizabethan belief that the king's moral state directly affects the health of the nation.
> If thou couldst, doctor, cast
> The water of my land, find her disease,
> And purge it to a sound and pristine health,
> I would applaud thee to the very echo,
> That should applaud again.
>
> (023-031)


## The English King's Healing Power

The contrast is drawn sharply through Edward the Confessor, the English king who possesses the power to heal scrofula — the "King's Evil" — by touch alone.

A doctor reports: "At his touch, such sanctity hath heaven given his hand, they presently amend" (020-040).

Malcolm adds: "To the succeeding royalty he leaves the healing benediction" (020-043).

Where Macbeth spreads sickness, the legitimate king heals.

This contrast is the play's clearest statement of its political theology: legitimate monarchy is medicinal; tyranny is pathological.

## The Doctor's Limits

The Scottish Doctor who attends [[lady-macbeth]] embodies the limits of earthly medicine.

Watching her sleepwalk, he observes: "This disease is beyond my practice" (025-010).

Her illness is not physical but spiritual — the guilt of Duncan's murder has infected her soul.

The doctor's confession — "More needs she the divine than the physician" (025-012) — reinforces the play's insistence that Macbeth's crime has caused wounds that medicine cannot reach.
