# Bird

Birds appear throughout *Macbeth* as a coherent symbolic system: they are omens, moral barometers, and figures for the vulnerable and the predatory. From the martlet that chooses Macbeth's castle to the owl that shrieks on the night of Duncan's murder, avian imagery traces the play's descent from natural order into chaos.

## Definition & Context

Shakespeare uses birds in *Macbeth* with a precision that rewards close attention. Different species carry different symbolic weight: the martlet (a house swallow) signifies innocent trust betrayed; the owl is a bird of ill omen whose cry accompanies the murder; the raven is Lady Macbeth's chosen messenger of death; the wren — "the most diminutive of birds" — embodies fierce parental protectiveness against overwhelming predators. This aviary structures the moral world of the play: when the natural order is intact, birds behave as they should; when it is disrupted, the "obscure bird" clamours through the night and a falcon is killed by a mousing owl.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> This guest of summer, / The temple-haunting martlet, does approve, / By his loved mansionry, that the heaven's breath / Smells wooingly here. (006-004)

Banquo observes the martlet nesting at Macbeth's castle, reading it as a sign of the place's welcoming innocence. The dramatic irony is acute: the audience knows Duncan enters a death-trap. The martlet's trust in the "loved mansionry" prefigures Duncan's misplaced trust in his hosts.

> The raven himself is hoarse / That croaks the fatal entrance of Duncan / Under my battlements. (005-010)

Lady Macbeth's first soliloquy invokes the raven — a carrion bird associated with death and prophecy. The raven's "hoarse" voice suggests it has been croaking so long in anticipation of Duncan's death that it has lost its voice. Lady Macbeth aligns herself with the bird of ill omen.

> The obscure bird / Clamour'd the live-long night. (010-024)

Lennox reports that on the night of Duncan's murder, the night was unruly: an owl — "the obscure bird" — shrieked throughout. The owl, traditionally a bird of darkness and death, registers the cosmic disturbance that murder has caused.

### Deepening (Act IV)

> The most diminutive of birds, will fight, / Her young ones in her nest, against the owl. (019-006)

Lady Macduff, facing her own murder, uses the wren as an emblem of maternal courage: even the smallest bird will fight to protect its young. The simile cuts both ways — it ennobles her protective instinct but also underscores her vulnerability.

> Poor bird! thou'dst never fear the net nor lime, / The pit-fall nor the gin. (019-015)

Lady Macduff addresses her son as a "poor bird" — innocent of the traps set for it. The catalogue of trapping devices (net, lime, pitfall, gin) maps the world of political predation onto the imagery of fowling, making the child's innocence and the hunters' cruelty equally vivid.
