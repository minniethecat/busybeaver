**Macduff's son** is the young child of [[macduff|Macduff]] and [[lady-macduff|Lady Macduff]], murdered in Act IV, Scene II by assassins sent by [[macbeth|Macbeth]]. Though he appears only in a single scene, his witty, precocious dialogue and brutal death make him one of the play's most affecting minor characters. His murder represents the point at which Macbeth's tyranny abandons all restraint, targeting children who pose no political threat.

## Wit and Precocity

Before the murderers arrive, the boy engages in a sharp, playful exchange with his mother about his absent father. When Lady Macduff tells him his father is dead and asks how he will live, he answers with disarming wit:

> (019-012) As birds do, mother.

Pressed further — "What, with worms and flies?" — he replies, "With what I get, I mean; and so do they" (019-014). This natural simplicity and resilience highlights, by contrast, the unnatural violence about to befall him.

## The Traitor Exchange

The dialogue turns to a precocious meditation on the nature of [[treason|treason]]. When the boy asks whether his father was a traitor and Lady Macduff replies that a traitor is "one that swears and lies," the child delivers one of the play's sharpest pieces of dramatic irony:

> (019-032) Then the liars and swearers are fools: for there are liars and swearers enow to beat the honest men and hang up them.

The logic, delivered by a child, inadvertently exposes the moral inversion of Macbeth's Scotland — where honest men like his father are branded traitors while actual villains thrive. Lady Macduff's affectionate response — "Now, God help thee, poor monkey!" (019-033) — underscores the innocence about to be destroyed.

## The Murder

The murder itself is abrupt and brutal. When the First Murderer enters and calls Macduff a traitor, the boy defends his father: "Thou liest, thou shag-hair'd villain!" (019-042). The Murderer's response — "What, you egg! / Young fry of treachery!" (019-043) — and the stage direction "Stabs him" reduce the child to a political object, a "fry" to be crushed. His dying words are a plea for his mother: "He has kill'd me, mother: / Run away, I pray you!" (019-045).

## Dramatic Significance

Macduff's son is one of several child victims in the play (along with [[young-siward|Young Siward]] and the children of [[macduff|Macduff]]'s household implied by "all my pretty chickens and their dam"). His murder is the catalyst that transforms Macduff's political opposition into a personal vendetta. When [[ross|Ross]] breaks the news to Macduff in England, [[malcolm|Malcolm]] urges Macduff to "dispute it like a man" — to which Macduff famously replies that he must first "feel it as a man" (020-096). The boy's death thus fuels the righteous violence that ultimately destroys [[macbeth|Macbeth]].

## Language and Thematic Echoes

The language surrounding the boy's death reverberates through the play's wider symbolic patterns. The Murderer's epithet — "you egg" — reduces the child to an unhatched thing, an image that inverts the natural order of generation and growth. It echoes [[lady-macbeth|Lady Macbeth]]'s earlier claim that she would have "dash'd the brains out" of a nursing infant (007-017), linking the two scenes as illustrations of violated innocence. The image of the egg also suggests something fragile, sealed, and prematurely broken — a fitting symbol for a kingdom where, as the [[old-man|Old Man]] observes, even horses have turned to "eat each other" (011-006).

The boy's final cry — "Run away, I pray you!" — is among the play's most unadorned expressions of love and selflessness. Unlike the elaborate rhetoric of the adult characters, the child's language is direct, urgent, and entirely without self-regard. In a tragedy defined by the corrosive effects of [[ambition|ambition]] on human bonds, this brief moment of filial devotion stands as a reproach to the world the adults have made.
