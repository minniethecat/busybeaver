# Cry (Lamentation)

In *Macbeth*, the cry is the audible signature of a world in rupture — a sound that registers political violence, personal grief, and cosmic disorder. From the battlefield to the bedchamber, the play's cries map its descent from martial honour into moral chaos.

## Definition & Context

Shakespeare builds *Macbeth* as an acoustic experience: thunder, knocking, the owl's shriek, and above all the human cry. In a Jacobean context, the public lament (*planctus*) was a sanctioned expression of grief, but *Macbeth* strips it of ritual containment. Cries erupt unbidden — wounds "cry for help" (I.ii), voices "cry 'Hold, hold!'" (I.v), and finally "a cry of women within" (V.v) announces the death of the queen. The motif traces a trajectory from the heroic to the hollow.

## Textual Evidence

### The Battlefield Cry (Act I)

> But I am faint, my gashes cry for help. (002-008)

The wounded Sergeant's first words establish the cry as the body's involuntary testimony. His gashes "cry" — personified wounds that speak the truth of battle. This is the cry of honour, the loyal soldier's physical sacrifice.

> To cry, "Hold, hold!" (005-024)

Lady Macbeth, reading her husband's letter, imagines crying out to halt his moral hesitation. Her "cry" is rhetorical — she will later discover that real cries resist domestication.

### The Murder and Its Aftermath (Act II)

> I heard the owl scream and the crickets cry. (009-006)

Lady Macbeth's report of nature's cries during Duncan's murder. The animal world registers the crime before the human world does. The owl — a bird of ill omen — "screams"; the crickets "cry." Nature itself protests the violation.

> Methought I heard a voice cry, "Sleep no more! / Macbeth does murder sleep" (009-022)

Macbeth's hallucinated cry is the play's most famous. The voice that cries out is both external (his guilty conscience projected) and prophetic (he will indeed sleep no more). The cry here is supernatural — a judgement pronounced from beyond.

### Accumulation of Grief (Act IV)

> New widows howl, new orphans cry; new sorrows / Strike heaven on the face (020-003)

Macduff's lament for Scotland multiplies the cries: widows "howl," orphans "cry." The language of lamentation has become the nation's constant sound. Each "new" intensifies the sense of unending accumulation.

### The Final Cry (Act V)

> [A cry of women within.] (025-003)

The stage direction itself is a cry. When Seyton informs Macbeth that "the Queen, my lord, is dead" (025-003), the "cry of women" serves as the ritual lament that Macbeth himself cannot perform. His response — "She should have died hereafter" — turns away from the cry, and in doing so, marks his complete emotional deadening.
