# The Witches

The Witches — also called the Weird Sisters — are the supernatural agents in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. They appear in four scenes, delivering prophecies that set Macbeth on his path to kingship and ultimately to his destruction. Dark, ambiguous, and elemental, they embody the play's central tension between fate and free will.

## Overview

The Witches first appear in the play's opening scene amidst thunder and lightning on a desolate heath (001-001). They plan to meet Macbeth after the battle and utter one of the play's defining paradoxes: "Fair is foul, and foul is fair: / Hover through the fog and filthy air" (001-011). This inversion of moral categories sets the tone for the entire tragedy.

In Act I, Scene III, the Witches intercept Macbeth and Banquo on the heath. They greet Macbeth with three titles — Thane of Glamis, Thane of Cawdor, and "King hereafter" (003-019) — and tell Banquo that he shall be "Lesser than Macbeth, and greater" and "Not so happy, yet much happier" (003-025). They also prophesy that Banquo "shalt get kings, though thou be none" (003-027). These riddling pronouncements ignite Macbeth's ambition. Banquo later reflects on their nature: "The earth hath bubbles, as the water has, / And these are of them" (003-031).

The Witches reappear in Act IV, Scene I, brewing a potion and conjuring the Apparitions that deliver further prophecies to Macbeth. Their incantation — "Double, double, toil and trouble; / Fire, burn; and cauldron, bubble" (018-006)(018-008) — is among the most famous lines in English literature. Hecate, the goddess of witchcraft, presides over their arts (P01-023)(018-012).

## Dramatic Significance

The Witches function as more than plot devices. They externalize Macbeth's latent ambition, speaking what he already half-desires. Their prophecies are technically true but deliberately misleading — the essence of equivocation. They tell Macbeth he cannot be harmed by any man "of woman born" (018-030) and that he is safe until "Birnam wood to high Dunsinane hill / Shall come against him" (018-045). Both prophecies come true, but not as Macbeth expects, illustrating the play's tragic irony.
