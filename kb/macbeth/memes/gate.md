# Gate (Threshold Motif)

The gate in *Macbeth* serves as the architectural and symbolic boundary separating the world of private crime from the realm of public judgement. Shakespeare concentrates the gate motif in the Porter scene, where the castle gate becomes a comic-theological threshold, but its resonances extend across the play — from the palace gate that witnesses Banquo's final walk to the sleepwalking Lady Macbeth's hallucinated knocking.

## Definition & Context

In the symbolic geography of *Macbeth*, the gate is the liminal space where the inside (murder, secrecy, the Macbeths' private guilt) meets the outside (discovery, justice, Scotland's natural order). The Porter's conceit — "If a man were porter of hell-gate" (010-002) — explicitly maps Jacobean theological topography onto the castle architecture: hell begins at the threshold. This mapping draws on the medieval mystery play tradition of the Harrowing of Hell, where Christ descends to liberate souls, but Shakespeare inverts it: the Porter is a comic Christ-figure admitting the damned, while the real devil-king is already inside.

## Textual Evidence

### The Porter's Hell-Gate (Act II)

> If a man were porter of hell gate, he should have old turning the key. (010-002)

The Porter's opening gambit is the play's most explicit gate-imagery. He imagines himself as Saint Peter at the gates of hell rather than heaven — a carnivalesque inversion that sets the theological tone. Each knock admits a new type of sinner (farmer, equivocator, tailor), building a catalogue of Jacobean social types. The irony, as De Quincey noted in his famous essay "On the Knocking at the Gate in *Macbeth*," is that the true horror — Duncan's murder — has already occurred inside; the gatekeeper's comedy is oblivious to the tragedy behind him.

### The Palace Gate (Acts II–III)

> They are, my lord, without the palace gate. (012-024)

The palace gate marks the boundary of safety for Banquo, who will shortly be murdered after leaving it. The gate becomes a transition point between life and death — those who pass through it may not return.

> … from hence to the palace gate / Make it their walk. (014-010)

As the Macbeths' tyranny deepens, the palace gate becomes a surveilled boundary. The innocent walk of courtiers to and from the gate is now shadowed by murderers, and the gate that once symbolised welcome has become a checkpoint of the police state.

### Return of the Gate (Act V)

> There's knocking at the gate. Come, come, come, come, give me your hand. (021-029)

Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking speech ends with the gate-knock haunting her broken consciousness. The gate she cannot answer has become the permanent threshold of her guilt — always knocking, never entered. The gate here is no longer architectural but psychological, an internal boundary she cannot cross back to sanity.
