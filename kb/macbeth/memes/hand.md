# Hand (Symbol)

The **hand** is one of the most pervasive and powerful symbols in Shakespeare's *Macbeth*. It appears across the entire play — from the loyal handshake of a subject to the hallucinated bloody hands of a murderer, from Lady Macbeth's compulsive hand-washing to the "unlineal hand" of a usurper. The hand is the body part of action, and in a play about the consequences of action, it becomes the physical site where guilt, agency, and moral corruption converge.

## The Hand of Loyalty and Betrayal

Before the murder of [[duncan|Duncan]], hands in the play signal political bonds. The "bleeding Captain" reports how [[macdonwald|Macdonwald]] was one who "ne'er shook hands, nor bade farewell" (002-004) — a failure of the handshake, the foundational gesture of feudal loyalty. Duncan himself offers his hand to Lady Macbeth in welcome:

> Give me your hand; Conduct me to mine host. (006-009)

This gesture of trust, offered to the woman who will orchestrate his murder, transforms the innocent hand into an instrument of dramatic irony. The hand that welcomes is the hand that will be bloodied.

## The Dagger and the Hand

Macbeth's "Is this a dagger which I see before me" soliloquy frames the hand as the bridge between vision and action. The hallucinated dagger points "the handle toward my hand" (008-021), inviting the fatal grip. The hand is where thought becomes deed — and where deed becomes irreversible guilt.

## Bloody Hands: The Physical Mark of Guilt

After Duncan's murder, the motif intensifies dramatically. Macbeth looks at his hands with horror:

> What hands are here? Ha, they pluck out mine eyes. (009-029)

The blood on his hands is no longer a material stain but a metaphysical one. [[lady-macbeth|Lady Macbeth]] dismisses his terror — "My hands are of your color, but I shame to wear a heart so white" (009-030) — but her confidence is the play's great deception. It is she who will later be destroyed by what her hands cannot wash away.

## The Sleepwalking: When Hands Accuse

Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking scene is the play's most concentrated meditation on the hand as the physical record of moral guilt. The Doctor and Gentlewoman watch as she compulsively rubs her hands:

> Look how she rubs her hands. (021-012)
>
> It is an accustomed action with her, to seem thus washing her hands. I have known her continue in this a quarter of an hour. (021-013)

Her famous cry — "What, will these hands ne'er be clean?" (021-018) — and "All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand" (021-021) — marks the play's definitive statement: some acts cannot be undone, and the body remembers what the mind denies.
