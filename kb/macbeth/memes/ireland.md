# Ireland

Ireland appears briefly but significantly in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth* as the destination of Donalbain's flight. After Duncan's murder, the King's younger son flees there while Malcolm goes to England.

## Overview

In Act II, Scene III, following the discovery of Duncan's body, Malcolm and Donalbain recognize their mortal danger. Malcolm resolves: "I'll to England" (010-075). Donalbain chooses Ireland: "To Ireland, I. Our separated fortune / Shall keep us both the safer" (010-076). His reasoning is pragmatic — by separating, they ensure that at least one heir survives: "Where we are, / There's daggers in men's smiles: the near in blood, / The nearer bloody" (010-076).

Unlike Malcolm, who returns from England with an army to reclaim the throne, Donalbain never reappears in the play. Ireland thus becomes a narrative exit — the younger son vanishes into exile, his absence underscoring the fragmentation of Duncan's royal line.
