**Armour** functions as a concentrated motif in Act V of _Macbeth_, where the title character's repeated demands to be armed expose his psychological state in the final hours of his reign.

## Macbeth's Arming in Act V, Scene III

In a single scene at Dunsinane, Macbeth calls for his armour three times in rapid succession, each demand revealing a different layer of his deteriorating mental state:

First, with brute defiance: "I'll fight till from my bones my flesh be hack'd. / Give me my armour" (023-025). Moments later, snapping at Seyton's report of the approaching English army: "Hang those that talk of fear. Give me mine armour" (023-027). Finally, upon learning that his wife's illness is spiritual rather than physical: "Throw physic to the dogs, I'll none of it. / Come, put mine armour on; give me my staff" (023-031).

The triple iteration of the arming demand is structurally significant. Each request interrupts a different exchange — with a servant, with Seyton, and with the Doctor — creating a rhythmic pattern of deflection. Macbeth cannot face the news he receives (the English advance, Lady Macbeth's madness), so he retreats into the physical act of arming as a substitute for psychological preparedness.

## Dramatic and Symbolic Function

The arming sequence inverts the epic arming convention. Where a hero's arming traditionally marks the ascent to glory (compare Achilles in the _Iliad_), Macbeth's arming marks a descent into isolated brutality. His armour becomes not a symbol of honour but of a man who has substituted physical violence for moral reasoning. The sequence also prefigures his final confrontation with Macduff in Act V, Scene VIII, where armour gives way to the "warlike shield" (028-011) — Macduff's armour, not Macbeth's.

## The Tragic Arming: Inversion of a Convention

Shakespeare draws on a long literary tradition of the arming scene — from the epic arming of Homeric heroes to the chivalric donning of armour in medieval romance — and systematically inverts it. In the epic tradition, the arming of the hero immediately precedes his greatest feat: Achilles arms for his return to battle, Beowulf arms for the fight with Grendel's mother. The audience expects the armed hero to triumph.

Macbeth's arming achieves the opposite. The audience knows — and Macbeth increasingly suspects — that the Witches' equivocation has trapped him: "none of woman born / Shall harm Macbeth" (018-017) is not the guarantee of invincibility it appeared to be. The arming is therefore hollow: a physical ritual that cannot compensate for the collapse of his supernatural protection. Each demand for armour is also a denial of the truth pressing in on him.

The contrast with Macduff is instructive. Macduff's arming is purposeful and direct — he seeks Macbeth "With bloodier accusation" (028-010) — and it is Macduff who enters bearing Macbeth's head. The play's moral architecture is inscribed in its armour: the usurper's arming is frantic and defensive; the avenger's arming is measured and offensive.
