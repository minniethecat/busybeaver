# The Opening Battle

The Opening Battle is the first reported event in *Macbeth*, recounted in Act I Scene ii by a bleeding Captain and Ross to King Duncan. It establishes Macbeth's heroic reputation in absentia, priming the audience for the gap between his battlefield valour and his later treachery.

## Dramatic Context

The battle occurs before the play's main action and is reported rather than staged. It falls at the very start of the exposition (Act I, Scene ii), immediately after the Witches' brief opening scene. Shakespeare delays Macbeth's first appearance on stage — the audience hears of him before seeing him, learning that he is "brave Macbeth" and "Valour's minion" before witnessing the man himself. This structural choice makes Macbeth's subsequent moral collapse all the more shocking: the audience's first impression is not of a murderer but of a hero.

> "What bloody man is that? He can report,
> As seemeth by his plight, of the revolt
> The newest state." (002-002)

Duncan's first line in the play — asking about the "bloody man" who will report the battle — immediately establishes the motif of blood and violence that pervades the tragedy. The bleeding Captain is himself a visual emblem of the play's obsession with wounded bodies.

## Key Testimony: The Captain's Report

The Captain's description of Macbeth's battlefield performance is the most graphic passage in the first act and sets the tone for the violence to come:

> "For brave Macbeth (well he deserves that name),
> Disdaining Fortune, with his brandish'd steel,
> Which smok'd with bloody execution,
> Like Valour's minion, carv'd out his passage,
> Till he fac'd the slave;
> Which ne'er shook hands, nor bade farewell to him,
> Till he unseam'd him from the nave to the chops,
> And fix'd his head upon our battlements." (002-004)

The verb "unseam'd" — to rip open from navel to jaw — is one of Shakespeare's most visceral coinages. Macbeth's violence here is presented not as criminal but as heroic: it is the violence that defends the state. The same capacity for extraordinary brutality will later be turned inward against that very state.

> "As sparrows eagles, or the hare the lion.
> If I say sooth, I must report they were
> As cannons overcharg'd with double cracks;
> So they Doubly redoubled strokes upon the foe." (002-008)

The similes are deliberately hyperbolic — Macbeth and Banquo are to their enemies as eagles to sparrows, lions to hares. The "double cracks" image foreshadows the play's obsession with doubling and duplicity. Duncan's response ("So well thy words become thee as thy wounds: / They smack of honour both") reinforces the equation of violence and honour that Macbeth's later crimes will dismantle.

## Sequence

1. The bleeding Captain enters and reports to Duncan the course of the battle (002-001)
2. The Captain describes how Macbeth defeated Macdonwald, "unseam'd him from the nave to the chaps" (002-002–002-006)
3. The Captain recounts how the Norwegian king, seeing his advantage, launched a fresh assault (002-007–002-009)
4. The Captain exits, attended, due to his wounds (002-010)
5. Ross and Angus enter and confirm the victory — Scotland has defeated Norway, and the Thane of Cawdor has been exposed as a traitor (002-011–002-020)

## Immediate Consequences

Duncan orders the execution of the disloyal Thane of Cawdor and bestows that title upon Macbeth as a reward for his battlefield valour. This investiture plants the first seed of the Witches' prophecy coming true.

## Long-term Significance

The battle is Macbeth's origin point of honour. His extraordinary violence — described in graphic, heroic terms — foreshadows his later capacity for brutality, now redirected from enemies of the state to the state itself. The title of Cawdor, gifted here, will prove a poisoned honour, as it is the first fulfilment of the Witches' prophecy and the first step toward Macbeth's ambition. Structurally, the battle report also establishes the play's pattern of offstage violence reported onstage — a pattern that culminates in the reports of Lady Macbeth's death and Macbeth's own beheading.

## Thematic Connections

- [[valour]] — Macbeth's battlefield courage is the play's first depiction of him; the word "valour" is central to the Captain's report
- [[traitor]] — The battle introduces the motif of treason through the original Thane of Cawdor's betrayal, establishing a parallel between the old Cawdor and the new
- [[fair]] — The battle's outcome appears "fair" (victory), but "foul" consequences follow; the Witches' opening paradox already colours this triumph
- [[blood]] — The "bloody man" and the "reeking wounds" inaugurate the play's central symbolic register
- [[double]] — The "double cracks" of the cannons prefigure the play's motif of doubling, duplicity, and repetition
