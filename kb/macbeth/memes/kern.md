# Kern

A **kern** (Irish *ceithern*) is a light-armed Irish foot-soldier of the medieval and early modern period. In *Macbeth*, kerns appear three times, always as hired soldiers from the margins of the Scottish political world—mercenaries whose presence marks the intersection of military necessity and social disdain.

## Definition & Context

In early modern English usage, "kern" denoted an Irish light infantryman, typically armed with a sword and light shield, in contrast to the heavily armoured "gallowglass." The term carried pejorative connotations in English and Scottish sources: kerns were seen as undisciplined, mobile, and socially inferior. Shakespeare uses these connotations deliberately in *Macbeth*, placing kerns at the edges of the battlefield and the bottom of the military hierarchy.

## Textual Evidence

### The Opening Battle (Act I)

> From the Western Isles / Of kerns and gallowglasses is supplied (002-004)

The Sergeant's report of the battle against Macdonwald describes the rebel's army as reinforced by Irish mercenaries from the Hebrides. The phrase "kerns and gallowglasses" pairs the two categories of Irish soldiery, establishing a military world in which Scottish lords fight with and against imported Irish fighters.

> Compell'd these skipping kerns to trust their heels (002-006)

After the Scottish victory, the Irish kerns flee—"skipping" suggests both their light equipment and the contempt in which the Sergeant holds them. Their flight is the visual proof of Macdonwald's defeat.

### The Final Stand (Act V)

> I cannot strike at wretched kerns, whose arms / Are hired to bear their staves (027-014)

Macbeth, facing Macduff in single combat, refuses to fight "wretched kerns"—mercenaries beneath his rank. The line registers the feudal contempt for hired soldiers at the very moment that Macbeth's own legitimacy has collapsed to hiring mercenaries himself. The irony is pointed: a tyrant who rules through hired fear now claims the honour code that forbids fighting hired men.

## Thematic Significance

The kern operates at the intersection of three thematic concerns in *Macbeth*:

| Theme | Relation to Kern |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Military honour** | Kerns occupy the lowest rung of the warrior hierarchy; fighting them is beneath a thane |
| **Hired loyalty** | Kerns are mercenaries, their loyalty purchased—a degraded form of the feudal bond |
| **Margins and centre** | Kerns come from the "Western Isles," geographic and political periphery |

Macbeth's refusal to fight kerns in Act V is rich with dramatic irony. Throughout the play, he has degraded every feudal bond he touched—killing his king, his friend, his guest. By the final act, he rules through hired murderers rather than loyal thanes. His claim that kerns are beneath him is technically correct by the old honour code, but that code no longer applies to a man who has systematically destroyed its foundations.
