## Definition

The spur in *Macbeth* functions as the play's central metaphor for motivational force — the external or internal pressure that drives a character to action. Shakespeare deploys the image across three distinct registers: as a figure for Duncan's eager haste to reach Inverness, as Macbeth's self-diagnosis of his own inadequate motivation, and as an image of the anonymous traveller spurred by the approaching night. Together, these three uses trace the play's movement from virtuous urgency through moral paralysis to predatory haste.

## Duncan's spur: virtuous urgency

The spur first appears in Act I, Scene vi, as Duncan arrives at Macbeth's castle. Praising Macbeth's speed in returning home ahead of the royal party, Duncan tells Lady Macbeth:

> But he rides well; / And his great love, sharp as his spur, hath holp him / To his home before us.
>
> (006-007)

Duncan's spur is a figure for love — the affection Macbeth bears for his king is what drives him home swiftly to prepare a welcome. The irony is devastating. The "great love, sharp as his spur" will, within hours, be revealed as the ambition that drives Macbeth to murder the very king he has rushed home to honour. Duncan's innocent metaphor weaponizes itself retroactively: the spur he praises is the same spur that will kill him.

## Macbeth's missing spur: the self-diagnosis

The spur's most psychologically acute moment comes in Macbeth's Act I, Scene vii soliloquy. Having enumerated every moral argument against murdering Duncan — kinship, kingship, hospitality, Duncan's virtues, the cosmic reaction of pity — Macbeth concludes with a clinical self-assessment:

> I have no spur / To prick the sides of my intent, but only / Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself / And falls on th' other.
>
> (007-002)

The image is of a rider applying spurs to a horse. A "spur to prick the sides of intent" would be a legitimate reason to act — a moral imperative, a pressing necessity, a just cause. Macbeth confesses he has none of these. His only spur is "vaulting ambition" — and the word "vaulting" is chosen with precision. A horse that "o'erleaps itself" is one that jumps too high and falls; ambition that vaults beyond its proper bounds will collapse under its own momentum. Macbeth diagnoses his own tragedy before it happens: he knows his only motivation is inadequate, knows the act is wrong, and will do it anyway.

## The lated traveller: predatory haste

The spur returns in Act III, Scene iii, as the murderers wait for Banquo. One of them observes the fading light:

> The west yet glimmers with some streaks of day. / Now spurs the lated traveller apace, / To gain the timely inn.
>
> (014-005)

The "lated traveller" spurring his horse toward shelter is an image of ordinary human urgency — the universal desire to reach safety before darkness falls. But in the context of the scene, it becomes a metaphor for Banquo himself, who is about to be ambushed on the road. The traveller's spur drives him toward what he believes is safety; Banquo, too, is riding toward a "timely inn" that will prove to be his death. The anonymous image captures the pathos of the play's victims, who hurry toward destruction unaware.
