The **serpent** appears twice in _Macbeth_ as an image of hidden danger and hereditary threat, drawing on both biblical and natural associations.

Lady Macbeth deploys the image in Act I, Scene V as she coaches her husband in deception:

> Look like the innocent flower,
> But be the serpent under't.
>
> (005-034) The serpent here is the concealed truth beneath a pleasing surface — a deliberate echo of the serpent in Eden, where evil likewise entered the world through concealment and persuasion.

Macbeth returns to the image after Banquo's murder in Act III, Scene IV, when he learns that Fleance has escaped: "There the grown serpent lies; the worm that's fled / Hath nature that in time will venom breed" (015-014). The "grown serpent" is the dead Banquo; the "worm" that escaped is Fleance, who will grow into a new serpent in time. The image reveals Macbeth's paranoia: every serpent killed breeds another.

## The Flower-and-Serpent Pairing

Lady Macbeth's injunction pairs the serpent with the flower — "look like the innocent flower, / But be the serpent under't" (005-034) — establishing a visual antithesis that runs through the entire play. The flower represents the public face of hospitality and loyalty: Duncan's praise of Macbeth's castle as a place where "the heaven's breath / Smells wooingly here" (006-004), the feasting and ceremony of the royal visit. The serpent is the intention underneath.

This pairing participates in the play's deepest thematic structure: the gap between appearance and reality. What is "fair" is "foul" (001-009); what looks like honour is murderous ambition. The flower-and-serpent image condenses this into a single emblem. Macbeth himself will later articulate the same logic from the other side — "False face must hide what the false heart doth know" (007-013) — but it is Lady Macbeth who provides the definitive image.

Macbeth also uses flower imagery at a structurally opposite moment. In his "Tomorrow and tomorrow" soliloquy, life is "a walking shadow" — the flower has withered, the performance is hollow. The serpent, which began as a metaphor for concealment, has consumed the garden.
