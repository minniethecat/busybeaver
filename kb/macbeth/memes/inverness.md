# Inverness

Inverness is Macbeth's castle in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. It is the setting for several of the play's most pivotal scenes, including Lady Macbeth's first soliloquy and the murder of King Duncan.

## Overview

Inverness is first mentioned when Duncan announces he will stay there: "From hence to Inverness, / And bind us further to you" (004-019). The castle is described by Duncan and Banquo as having a pleasant situation: "This castle hath a pleasant seat; the air / Nimbly and sweetly recommends itself / Unto our gentle senses" (007-002). This gentle appearance is bitterly ironic — within hours, Duncan will be murdered within its walls.

Lady Macbeth is at Inverness when she receives Macbeth's letter about the Witches' prophecies. In Act I, Scene V — set in "Inverness. A Room in Macbeth's Castle" — she delivers her famous "unsex me here" soliloquy, calling on spirits to fill her with cruelty (005-011). When Macbeth arrives, she tells him to "look like the innocent flower, / But be the serpent under't" (005-023), setting their plan in motion.

Act II opens at Inverness with Banquo and Fleance on the castle grounds (008-001), followed by Macbeth's "dagger" soliloquy and Duncan's murder (Scene II). The following morning, Macduff discovers the King's body at Inverness (010-027). The castle thus transforms from the site of hospitality to the scene of the play's foundational crime.
