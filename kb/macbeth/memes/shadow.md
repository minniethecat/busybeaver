## Definition

The shadow in *Macbeth* functions on three distinct but interrelated levels: as a supernatural apparition conjured by the witches, as a metaphor for the insubstantiality of human life, and as a military stratagem of concealment. Across the play's five acts, "shadow" moves from the ghostly realm of the witches' prophecies through Macbeth's terrified recognition of Banquo's ghost to the existential nihilism of the "tomorrow" soliloquy, tracing the protagonist's descent from ambitious thane to hollow tyrant for whom reality itself has lost its substance.

## The witches' shadow-show

The witches introduce the shadow motif in Act IV, Scene i, when they conjure the apparitions that will seal Macbeth's fate. After the show of kings — Banquo's royal descendants stretching "to th' crack of doom" — the witches command:

> Come like shadows, so depart! (018-035)

The eight kings are shadows in the literal sense: spectral figures that appear and vanish without substance. But they are also shadows in the prophetic sense — prefigurations of a future that Macbeth cannot prevent. The witches' shadow-show establishes the play's central epistemological crisis: what appears real may be mere illusion, and what seems illusory may prove devastatingly real. The "shadows" that "so depart" leave behind a prophecy that will be fulfilled.

## Banquo's ghost: "Hence, horrible shadow"

At the banquet in Act III, Scene iv, Macbeth encounters the ghost of the murdered Banquo sitting in his place at the table. When the ghost appears a second time, Macbeth challenges it directly:

> Hence, horrible shadow! / Unreal mock'ry, hence! (015-052–015-053)

Macbeth's choice of the word "shadow" is telling. He cannot call the ghost a "ghost" — that would concede its supernatural reality. Instead he calls it a "shadow," an unreality, a trick of the mind. But the ghost does not obey; it remains until its own time to depart. The "horrible shadow" is more real than Macbeth's grasp on sanity, and his attempt to dismiss it as insubstantial only reveals how completely the boundary between the real and the unreal has collapsed for him.

## Malcolm's military shadow

In Act V, Scene iv, Malcolm orders his soldiers to cut down branches from Birnam Wood and carry them as camouflage:

> Let every soldier hew him down a bough, / And bear't before him. Thereby shall we shadow / The numbers of our host. (024-003–024-005)

This is shadow as tactical concealment — the one instance in the play where shadow operates as deliberate, controlled deception rather than supernatural or existential vertigo. Malcolm's "shadow" is the practical counterpoint to the witches' shadows: where the witches' apparitions reveal truths through illusion, Malcolm's camouflage conceals truth for strategic advantage. The irony is that this military "shadow" fulfills the witches' prophecy — Birnam Wood does come to Dunsinane, not as supernatural portent but as human stratagem.

## "Life's but a walking shadow"

The shadow reaches its philosophical summit in Act V, Scene v. Learning of Lady Macbeth's death, Macbeth delivers the play's definitive statement on the nature of existence:

> Out, out, brief candle! / Life's but a walking shadow; a poor player, / That struts and frets his hour upon the stage, / And then is heard no more. (025-009)

After the candle is extinguished, what remains is not darkness but shadow — a thing with the shape of life but none of its substance. The "walking shadow" is the final form of Macbeth's moral trajectory: from a man who could distinguish illusion from reality, through a murderer who sees "horrible shadows" at his own feast, to a tyrant for whom all of life has become a shadow-play signifying nothing. The progression from the witches' conjured shadows to Macbeth's existential shadow is the arc of the entire tragedy.
