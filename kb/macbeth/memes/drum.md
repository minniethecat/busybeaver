## Overview

The drum is the most persistent sound in *Macbeth*'s acoustic landscape. From the opening alarums to the final "drum and colours" entrance of Malcolm's victorious army (027-008), the drum marks the movement of military force across the stage. In a play where speech increasingly fails — "Amen" stuck in the throat, "signifying nothing" — the drum continues to speak, a language older and more reliable than words.

## The Drum as Aural Stage Direction

Shakespeare's stage directions deploy the drum with precision. "Drum within" (001-002) announces the bleeding Captain before he speaks. "A drum, a drum! / Macbeth doth come" (001-003) — the Witches hear the drum before they see the man. The drum precedes speech, preparing the audience for martial presence. It is the sound of the world outside the castle walls, the noise of the political realm that Macbeth's tyranny cannot silence.

Each entrance of an army is accompanied by "drum and colours" — the sound and the visual of banners together. Malcolm's forces enter three times to the drum (Act V Scenes ii, iv, vi), each entrance marking a stage in the tightening siege. Macbeth's entrance "with drum and colours" in Act V Scene v is the last time the drum serves him; thereafter, it belongs to his enemies.

## The Drum vs. The Knocking

The drum's steady military beat contrasts with the play's other percussive signature: the knocking at the gate. Where the drum signals organised force and legitimate authority, the knocking is disorderly, uncanny, the sound of justice demanding entry to a house of murder. The Porter scene transforms the knocking into a cosmic joke — the devil at hell's door — but the joke turns on the difference between the drum's martial order and the knock's anarchic insistence.

Macbeth greets the knocking with desperate bravado: "Wake Duncan with thy knocking! I would thou couldst!" (005-003). The knock is what the drum cannot be — the summons that the murderer cannot answer.

## The Final Flourish

The play's last stage direction — "Retreat. Flourish. Enter, with drum and colours, Malcolm..." (027-008) — assembles the full sonic vocabulary of resolution. The retreat signals the end of battle, the flourish signals triumph, and the drum signals the restoration of legitimate military authority. The sound that first accompanied Macbeth's victorious return from battle now accompanies his vanquisher. The drum, unlike the tongue, cannot equivocate: it beats for whoever holds the field.
