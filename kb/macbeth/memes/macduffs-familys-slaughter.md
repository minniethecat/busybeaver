# Macduff's Family's Slaughter

The Slaughter of Macduff's Family is one of the most brutal events in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. In Act IV, Scene II, Macbeth's hired murderers descend upon Macduff's castle in Fife and kill Lady Macduff and her children. Macduff, who has fled to England to join Malcolm, is not present — a fact that deepens the atrocity and fuels his drive for revenge.

## Overview

Macduff has departed for England, leaving his wife and children unprotected. Lady Macduff, unaware of the political necessity of her husband's flight, feels abandoned and betrayed. She tells Ross, "He loves us not: / He wants the natural touch" (019-006), comparing herself to the wren that "will fight, / Her young ones in her nest, against the owl" (019-006).

A messenger arrives too late with a warning: "I doubt some danger does approach you nearly: / If you will take a homely man's advice, / Be not found here; hence, with your little ones" (019-036). Lady Macduff hesitates, asking, "Whither should I fly? / I have done no harm" (019-038). Her reflection is bitterly ironic: "I am in this earthly world, where to do harm / Is often laudable; to do good sometime / Accounted dangerous folly" (019-038).

The murderers arrive and demand to know where Macduff is. Lady Macduff defies them, and her young son calls the murderer a "shag-ear'd villain" and "liar" (019-042). The murderer stabs the boy, who cries, "He has kill'd me, mother: / Run away, I pray you!" (019-046). Lady Macduff flees, crying "Murder!" as the killers pursue her (019-047).

## Dramatic Significance

This scene is the play's emotional nadir. Unlike the political killings of Duncan and Banquo, the murder of Macduff's innocent family serves no strategic purpose — it is pure, gratuitous cruelty. It marks Macbeth's complete moral degradation from hesitant murderer to slaughterer of women and children.

When Macduff learns the news in England, his grief is profound and wordless. Malcolm urges him to "Dispute it like a man," but Macduff replies, "I shall do so; / But I must also feel it as a man" (020-053). His grief transforms into a singular purpose: personal vengeance against Macbeth.
