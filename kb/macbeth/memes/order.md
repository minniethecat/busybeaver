# Order (Political and Natural)

The concept of order in *Macbeth* reflects the Elizabethan worldview of the Great Chain of Being — a hierarchical cosmic structure in which every element has its appointed place, and the king sits at the apex of the human realm.

When [[macbeth]] murders King [[duncan]], he does not simply commit a crime; he ruptures the fabric of creation itself.

## Duncan's Reign as Ordered World

The play opens with order under threat — a rebellion led by [[macdonwald]] and an invasion by Norway — but [[duncan]]'s authority is asserted and restored.

The Captain's report emphasizes the king's rightful power: "Justice, with valor arm'd" (002-010) prevails against "merciless Macdonwald."

Duncan's court at Forres is a space of ceremony, reward, and legitimate succession.

His naming of [[malcolm]] as Prince of Cumberland formalizes the orderly transfer of power.

## The Murder as Cosmic Rupture

The regicide in Act II is not merely political violence — it is a crime against nature.

Immediately after Duncan's death, the natural world convulses.

Ross and the Old Man report that "by the clock 'tis day, and yet dark night strangles the travelling lamp" (009-010), that a falcon was killed by a mousing owl, and that Duncan's horses "Turn'd wild in nature, broke their stalls" and "ate each other" (009-020).

These portents are not decorative — they encode the Elizabethan belief that political disorder manifests as natural disorder.

The king's body is the body politic, and wounding one wounds all creation.

## Restoration Under Malcolm

The play's resolution is the restoration of order through [[malcolm]]'s accession.

Malcolm's final speech re-establishes legitimate rule: "What's more to do, which would be planted newly with the time" (028-028).

He transforms thanes into earls — "My thanes and kinsmen, Henceforth be earls, the first that ever Scotland in such an honour nam'd" (028-030) — importing English feudal titles to signal the civilizing of a traumatized Scotland.

## The Banquet's Collapsed Order

The banquet scene embodies order's fragility under Macbeth's tyranny. When Banquo's ghost disrupts the feast, Lady Macbeth desperately attempts to enforce the forms of orderly departure:

> Stand not upon the order of your going,
> But go at once.
>
> (015-050)

Her command is rich with irony: she invokes "order" only to demand its violation. The guests' disorderly exit mirrors the broader collapse of political and cosmic order under Macbeth's illegitimate rule.

## The Chain of Being in Macbeth

The concept of order in *Macbeth* operates on multiple levels:

| Level | Order Preserved | Order Broken |
|-------|----------------|--------------|
| Cosmic | Sun rises, seasons turn | Day is dark, animals turn predatory |
| Political | Duncan rules legitimately | Macbeth usurps, tyranny follows |
| Social | Thanes serve the crown | "False thanes" flee, loyalty fractures |
| Personal/Moral | Conscience guides action | Macbeth's "single state of man" is shaken |
| Domestic | Husband and wife in harmony | The Macbeths' marriage becomes a partnership in crime |
