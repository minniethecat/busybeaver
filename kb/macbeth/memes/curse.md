The **curse** in _Macbeth_ is a weapon of language — a spoken invocation of harm that crosses the boundary between word and deed. From the witches' folkloric maledictions to Macbeth's progressive self-damnation and the community's final judgment, cursing traces the arc of the play's moral universe: those who embrace evil become what they speak.

## The Witches' Curses

The witches' curse on the sailor's wife — "I'll drain him dry as hay" — establishes the pattern: spoken malice has real-world consequences. The First Witch boasts of her power to control winds and wreck ships, framing the curse as a supernatural technology of revenge (018-049).

When Hecate threatens the witches with "an eternal curse" if they disobey her, the curse becomes a tool of hierarchy within the supernatural order itself (018-049). Even among witches, the curse enforces obedience.

## Macbeth's Self-Cursing

Macbeth's trajectory is marked by his relationship to cursing. Banquo's prayer — "Restrain in me the cursed thoughts that nature gives way to in repose" (008-006) — names the internal struggle against cursed impulses before Macbeth has fully succumbed.

By the play's end, Macbeth has become the object of universal cursing. Macduff cries "Accursed be that tongue that tells me so" upon hearing of his family's murder (028-009). The curse is now directed at Macbeth's agents and, by extension, at Macbeth himself. When Macduff finally displays "th' usurper's cursed head" (028-025), the curse is fulfilled — the tyrant has been reduced from king to accursed object.

## The Rabble's Curse

Macbeth's final soliloquy reveals his awareness of his cursed state: "I have lived long enough... to be baited with the rabble's curse" (028-011). The "rabble's curse" is the voice of the community — the collective judgment that Macbeth has violated every bond of honor, hospitality, and kingship. This public curse is the external echo of Macbeth's internal damnation: having cursed himself through his deeds, he now hears his condemnation returned by the people he has terrorized.
> I will not yield,
> To kiss the ground before young Malcolm's feet,
> And to be baited with the rabble's curse.
> Though Birnam wood be come to Dunsinane,
> And thou oppos'd, being of no woman born,
> Yet I will try the last.
>
> (028-011)


## The Accursed Hour

The witches' final scene is permeated with the language of cursing: "this pernicious hour / Stand aye accursed in the calendar" (018-059). The hour of Macbeth's second visit to the witches is marked as permanently cursed — a moment outside normal time, belonging to the supernatural rather than the human calendar.
