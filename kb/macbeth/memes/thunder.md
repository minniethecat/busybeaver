**Thunder** in _Macbeth_ is more than weather — it is the signature of the supernatural, the atmospheric manifestation of the witches' power, and the play's most consistent external marker of disorder. Whenever the witches appear, thunder rolls; whenever Scotland's moral order cracks, the skies answer.

## The Witches' Element

The play opens with thunder: "Thunder and Lightning. Enter three Witches" (001-001). This stage direction establishes a pattern that holds throughout the entire play — the witches are accompanied by thunder at every entrance (003-001) (016-001) (018-001). Thunder is their herald, their atmosphere, their natural element as much as fire belongs to dragons.

The witches' first line — "When shall we three meet again? In thunder, lightning, or in rain?" (001-002) — makes explicit that thunder is not incidental to their nature but constitutive of it. They meet _in_ thunder as others meet in a room or a garden. The distinction between the witches and the thunder they command is deliberately blurred.
> When shall we three meet again?
> In thunder, lightning, or in rain?
>
> (001-002)


## Thunder as Moral Sign

In the Renaissance worldview that Shakespeare inherited, thunder was understood as a sign of divine displeasure — "direful thunders break" as punishment for transgression (002-006). But in _Macbeth_, the moral physics is inverted. The thunder does not come from a righteous heaven punishing evil; it comes from the witches, who use it to announce their presence and amplify their power.

The absence of divine thunder is itself significant. When Macbeth murders Duncan, the natural world convulses — horses eat each other, falcons are killed by owls, darkness covers the day — but no thunder from heaven strikes the murderer. The thunder in _Macbeth_ belongs entirely to the witches' domain. Heaven, it seems, remains silent.

## Thunder and Sleep

Macbeth's invocation of thunder in his final soliloquy reveals the psychological cost of his alliance with the supernatural: "that which might appall the devil... sleep in spite of thunder" (018-038). The "thunder" here is both literal (the storm of supernatural forces) and metaphorical (the noise of a guilty conscience). Macbeth wishes for the ability to sleep through thunder — to be so hardened that even the loudest moral alarm cannot wake him. But as the sleep-walking scene demonstrates, such insulation is impossible.

## Dramatic Rhythm

Thunder functions as a dramatic punctuation mark throughout _Macbeth_. Each thunderclap signals a pivot in the action: the first prophecy (Act I), the cauldron scene (Act IV), the final apparitions. The audience learns to hear thunder as a warning — something terrible and transformative is about to happen. By Act V, the thunder stops, and so does the supernatural machinery. The final battle is fought in purely human terms, under a silent sky.
