**Tyranny**, in *Macbeth*, denotes the illegitimate and violent exercise of power by a ruler who seizes the throne through murder and rules through terror. Shakespeare contrasts Macbeth's tyrannical reign with the sacred, natural order of Duncan's kingship, drawing on Jacobean political theories of divine right and the distinction between a true king and a usurping tyrant.

## Definition & Context

In the political vocabulary of Shakespeare's England, a "tyrant" was defined by the means of acquiring power (usurpation) and the manner of exercising it (fear rather than consent). Macbeth satisfies both criteria: he murders the rightful king Duncan, and his reign depends on spies, assassinations, and terror. The play repeatedly juxtaposes the tyrant's sterile, fear-driven rule against the fertile, love-based governance Malcolm promises to restore. This distinction is central to the play's political argument: a tyrant, unlike a true king, governs for himself rather than the realm.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Recognition (Act III, Scene vi)

> …he fail'd His presence at the tyrant's feast, I hear, Macduff lives in disgrace. (017-002)

> …son of Duncan, From whom this tyrant holds the due of birth, Lives in the English court. (017-003)

In this scene between Lennox and another Lord, the word "tyrant" appears for the first time as a label for Macbeth. The shift in language is significant: previously called "king," Macbeth is now "the tyrant." The scene establishes that his rule is already understood as illegitimate by the Scottish nobility, setting the stage for the rebellion that follows.

### The Tyrant's Grip (Act IV, Scene iii)

> This tyrant, whose sole name blisters our tongues, Was once thought honest. (020-004)

Malcolm's description of Macbeth to Macduff captures the paradox of tyranny: the man once judged honourable has become a name so poisonous it blisters the tongue. The metaphor of physical injury from speech itself conveys how completely Macbeth's reputation has corroded.

> With an untitled tyrant bloody-scepter'd, When shalt thou see thy wholesome days again? (020-021)

Macduff's lament to Malcolm draws the sharpest contrast: Macbeth is an "untitled" tyrant — his sceptre is "bloody" rather than sanctified. The word "untitled" directly refutes any legitimacy Macbeth might claim, while "bloody-scepter'd" fuses the symbol of kingship with the instrument of murder.

### The Tyrant's Fall (Act V)

> MACDUFF. The tyrant has not batter'd at their peace. (020-044)

> MENTEITH. What does the tyrant? (022-006)

By Act V, "the tyrant" has effectively replaced Macbeth's name in the speech of the rebel forces. This rhetorical depersonalisation reduces him from a complex tragic figure to a political problem to be solved by force.

> SIWARD. Thou liest, abhorred tyrant. With my sword I'll prove the lie thou speak'st. (027-009)

Young Siward's dying defiance — calling Macbeth an "abhorred tyrant" — demonstrates that even the next generation of Scottish nobility recognises illegitimate rule and is willing to die opposing it.
