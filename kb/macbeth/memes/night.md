# Night

Night in *Macbeth* is more than a temporal backdrop; it is an active agent of concealment summoned by the play's central figures to cloak murder in darkness.

## Definition & Context

Throughout *Macbeth*, night serves as the primary dramatic setting for the play's most consequential actions—Duncan's murder, Banquo's assassination, and the Weird Sisters' prophecies all occur under cover of darkness. In Jacobean cosmology, night was associated with the diabolical and the inversion of natural order. Shakespeare exploits this association systematically: characters do not merely wait for nightfall but actively invoke it as an accomplice, and the play's disruption of the day–night cycle externalizes the moral and political chaos unleashed by regicide.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Act I)

> Come, thick night, / And pall thee in the dunnest smoke of hell (005-020–005-021)

Lady Macbeth's invocation in Act I Scene v is the motif's foundational moment. Having learned of the witches' prophecy, she calls upon "thick night" to wrap itself in "the dunnest smoke of hell" so that her "keen knife" may strike unseen. The invocation is notable for its agency: Lady Macbeth does not passively wait for darkness but commands it as a collaborator in murder.

> Nor heaven peep through the blanket of the dark / To cry, "Hold, hold!" (005-023–005-024)

The passage culminates in the image of heaven blinded by darkness. Lady Macbeth demands that even divine witness be prevented—night must be absolute, admitting no moral scrutiny. This establishes the motif's theological dimension: sin committed in darkness is sin that refuses accountability.

### The Murder and Its Aftermath (Act II)

The night of Duncan's murder is marked by portents. Macbeth's dagger soliloquy occurs in the dead of night, when "Nature seems dead, and wicked dreams abuse / The curtain'd sleep" (008-037–008-038). The bell that rings at 008-050—"I go, and it is done"—is the signal that transforms night from setting into irrevocable action. Banquo's opening unease in the same scene, entering with a torch against the darkness, underscores night as a state of moral peril.

> Ring the alarum bell.—Murder and treason! (010-034)

Macduff's cry upon discovering Duncan's body in Act II Scene iii marks the moment night's concealment is shattered. The "alarum bell" that follows (010-041) transforms the castle from a place of silent darkness into one of exposed horror.

### Deepening Motif (Act III)

> Till seven at night; to make society / The sweeter welcome (012-018–012-019)

Macbeth's instruction to his wife before Banquo's murder is thick with dramatic irony. He specifies "seven at night" as the appointed hour—night has become his operational framework, the time when society's rules are suspended and murder can be arranged under the guise of hospitality.

> If it find heaven, must find it out tonight. (012-074)

Macbeth's closing line in Act III Scene I completes the night-as-accomplice pattern. Banquo's soul must be dispatched "tonight"—the urgency is both practical (before the feast) and symbolic (darkness as the medium of evil deeds).

## Critical Perspectives

Critics have long noted *Macbeth*'s unusually dense night imagery. A. C. Bradley described it as Shakespeare's darkest tragedy in literal terms—more than half the play's action occurs at night or in pre-dawn darkness. The motif has been read politically (night as the eclipse of legitimate rule under a usurper), psychologically (night as the unconscious mind), and theologically (night as the absence of divine order). The systematic invocation of night by both Macbeth and Lady Macbeth distinguishes this play's use of darkness from Shakespeare's other tragedies.
