# The Porter's Scene

Immediately after Macbeth murders Duncan and Lady Macbeth frames the grooms, a drunken Porter stumbles to answer the knocking at the castle gate. In a monologue rich with dark comedy, he imagines himself the porter of hell-gate, unwittingly speaking truth about the castle he guards. The scene provides the play's only sustained comic relief while deepening its central themes of equivocation, damnation, and the thin line between the ordinary and the horrific.

## Dramatic Context

The Porter's Scene opens Act II Scene iii, placed with extraordinary structural precision between the murder of Duncan (Act II Scene ii) and the discovery of the body. Shakespeare uses the Porter as a "breathing space" — a moment of grotesque comedy that paradoxically intensifies the horror by giving the audience time to process what has just happened. The knocking at the gate, which began in the previous scene with Macbeth's terrified "Whence is that knocking?", now becomes the structural device that brings the outside world — Macduff and Lennox — into the castle to discover the murder.

## The Monologue

> Here's a knocking indeed! If a man were porter of hell gate, he should have old turning the key. ((010-002))

The Porter imagines three callers at hell-gate:

1. **A farmer** who "hanged himself on the expectation of plenty" — a reference to grain hoarding and the economic anxieties of Shakespeare's audience
2. **An equivocator** who "could swear in both the scales against either scale, who committed treason enough for God's sake, yet could not equivocate to heaven" — a direct allusion to the Jesuit Henry Garnet, executed in 1606 for his role in the Gunpowder Plot, and to the theme of equivocation that runs through the play
3. **An English tailor** who comes "for stealing out of a French hose" — a bawdy joke about fashion and theft

> But this place is too cold for hell. I'll devil-porter it no further. ((010-002))

The Porter's decision to stop playing "devil-porter" is darkly ironic — he has been describing Macbeth's castle with accidental precision.

## Sequence

1. Prolonged knocking wakes the drunken Porter ((010-001))
2. The Porter delivers his "hell-gate" monologue, imagining three damned souls ((010-002))
3. He opens the gate to Macduff and Lennox ((010-002))
4. The Porter engages Macduff in bawdy banter about drink's effects on "nose-painting, sleep, and urine" and "lechery" ((010-002))
5. Macbeth enters, and Macduff goes to wake the King ((010-011))

## Dramatic Function

### Comic Relief

The Porter's drunken rambling provides the audience with the only extended comedy in a relentlessly dark play. The physical humor of the hungover gatekeeper, the bawdy jokes about drink and sex, and the absurdist "Knock, knock, knock" refrain create a release of tension that Shakespeare knew his audience needed after witnessing a murder.

### Thematic Resonance

The comedy is not an escape from the play's themes but their re-statement in a different register. The Porter's hell-gate is no mere joke — Macbeth's castle *has* become hell, and the Porter guards its entrance. The equivocator reference ties the comedy directly to the play's central concern with language that means two things at once. The Porter, unknowingly, is the play's most honest commentator: he calls the castle "too cold for hell" without knowing a murdered king lies within.
