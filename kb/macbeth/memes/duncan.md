# Duncan

Duncan is King of Scotland at the opening of *Macbeth*, described in the *Dramatis Personae* simply as "DUNCAN, King of Scotland" ((P01-001)). His murder by Macbeth — his subject, kinsman, and host — is the central crime of the play, one that resonates on political, moral, and cosmic registers. Duncan embodies the principle of legitimate, sacred kingship; his death inaugurates the disorder that consumes Scotland until Malcolm's restoration.

## The Gracious King

Shakespeare characterizes Duncan not as a warrior-king in his prime but as a figure of benevolent authority — gracious, trusting, and perhaps too gentle for the violent world he rules. His first entrance establishes the tone: "Enter King Duncan, Malcolm, Donalbain, Lennox, with Attendants, meeting a bleeding Captain" ((002-001)). Even as he receives news of battle, Duncan is framed as the concerned ruler, attending to his wounded soldier. His response to the Captain's report is generous: he declares that "noble Macbeth" has won the title of the traitorous Cawdor, establishing both his authority to bestow honour and his inability to see the treachery that lurks beneath surface loyalty.

Duncan's subsequent entrance — "Enter Duncan, Malcolm, Donalbain, Lennox and Attendants" ((004-001)) — shows the peaceful court before it is shattered. His relationship with his sons Malcolm and Donalbain, and his easy command of his nobles, paint a picture of stable rule. This stability is precisely what Macbeth's ambition destroys.

## The Fatal Visit to Inverness

The dramatic irony of Duncan's arrival at Macbeth's castle is among the most potent in Shakespeare. Duncan approaches Inverness with admiration for its setting, oblivious to the fact that his hostess, Lady Macbeth, has already begun her invocation to "murdering ministers." The line "That croaks the fatal entrance of Duncan / Under my battlements" ((005-009)) — spoken by Lady Macbeth before Duncan's arrival — marks his entrance as a death sentence. The audience watches Duncan walk into the trap, his trust in Macbeth's hospitality rendered tragic by knowledge.

Macbeth's own soliloquy acknowledges the enormity of the crime by listing Duncan's virtues: he is Duncan's "kinsman and his subject," and as his host "should against his murderer shut the door, / Not bear the knife myself." The reference to "Hear it not, Duncan, for it is a knell" ((008-051)) — the bell that summons Duncan "to heaven or to hell" — is Macbeth's final acknowledgment of the innocence he is about to violate. Duncan is never depicted as anything less than a good king; his murder is unmotivated by grievance and driven purely by ambition.

## The Cosmic Violation

Duncan's murder is framed not merely as a political assassination but as a violation of the natural order. The play's imagery after his death — darkness at noon, horses eating each other, a falcon killed by a mousing owl — registers the murder as a rupture in the fabric of creation. In the play's theological framework, the king is God's anointed deputy on earth; to kill a king is to strike at divine order itself. Macbeth's later acknowledgment — "For them the gracious Duncan have I murdered" ((012-045)) — uses "gracious" not as a courtly formula but as a precise theological term. Duncan's grace, his divinely sanctioned kingship, is what makes the crime unforgivable and what ensures that Macbeth's reign can never achieve legitimacy.

> Here lay Duncan,
> His silver skin lac'd with his golden blood;
> And his gash'd stabs look'd like a breach in nature
> For ruin's wasteful entrance: there, the murderers,
> Steep'd in the colours of their trade, their daggers
> Unmannerly breech'd with gore.
>
> (010-055)


## Dramatic Function

Duncan appears in only two scenes before his murder, but his presence shapes the entire play. He is the moral baseline — the king whose virtues make tyranny legible by contrast. Malcolm's self-description of the "king-becoming graces" in Act IV is essentially a catalogue of Duncan's qualities. The play's political argument — that legitimate rule rests on virtue, not force; on divine sanction, not usurpation — is embodied in Duncan's brief but luminous presence. His blood, which "will ne'er be clean" from Macbeth's hands, stains the play from the moment of his death to the final restoration.
