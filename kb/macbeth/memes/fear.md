**Fear** is the emotional engine of _Macbeth_. The play traces a complete arc of fear: from the initial startle at the Witches' prophecy, through the paranoia of a tyrannical reign maintained by murder, to the terminal terror of a sleepwalking queen and a king facing his nemesis. Shakespeare maps fear not as a single state but as a progressive disease with distinct clinical stages across the five acts.

## Textual Evidence

### The First Startle: Banquo's Question

Fear enters the play in Act I, Scene III, the moment the Witches' prophecies begin to take hold. Banquo, observing Macbeth's reaction to the salutation "that shalt be king hereafter" (003-019), asks the diagnostic question: "Good sir, why do you start and seem to fear / Things that do sound so fair?" (003-021). The verb "start" captures a physical flinch — fear before it has become thought. Banquo's question marks the boundary between the fair appearance and the foul reality that the Witches embody.

Banquo himself articulates a theory of fear moments later: "Present fears / Are less than horrible imaginings" (003-046). This is the play's most compressed statement about the psychology of fear: what terrifies is not the present danger but the future the mind constructs.

> Present fears
> Are less than horrible imaginings.
>
> (003-046)

### Lady Macbeth and the Weaponisation of Fear

Lady Macbeth understands fear as a tool. Reading her husband's letter, she diagnoses his character as being "too full o' the milk of human kindness" (005-002) — a deficiency, in her view, of the ruthlessness that fearlessness enables. When Macbeth wavers before the murder, she asks: "Art thou afeard / To be the same in thine own act and valour / As thou art in desire?" (007-007). The word "afeard" reduces Macbeth's moral hesitation to mere cowardice. Her triumph is short-lived. By Act V, Scene I, the woman who prayed to be unsexed walks in her sleep, trying to wash invisible blood from her hands. The Doctor observes: "Unnatural deeds / Do breed unnatural troubles" (021-020).

### Macbeth's Fear: Three Stages

Macbeth's relationship to fear evolves through three stages. **Before the murder**, fear is indistinguishable from moral imagination — his "If it were done when 'tis done" soliloquy (007-001) weighs consequences rationally. **After the murder**, fear becomes immediate and sensory: he cannot say "Amen" (009-018), hears a voice cry "Macbeth does murder sleep" (009-020). **As king**, fear metastasises into paranoia: "Our fears in Banquo / Stick deep" (012-028). Each murder deepens the terror — "I am in blood / Stepp'd in so far that, should I wade no more, / Returning were as tedious as go o'er" (015-028).

By Act V, Macbeth's fear curdles into nihilism. The "Tomorrow and tomorrow" soliloquy (025-017) is fear's terminal stage: the recognition that nothing was worth the terror paid to secure it. Facing Macduff, Macbeth confronts the final fear — "I'll not fight with thee" (028-003) — before being driven back to the last defiance: "Lay on, Macduff" (028-012).

### The Transfer of Fear

The play's architecture is shaped by the transfer of fear between its two central figures. In Act I, Lady Macbeth is fearless and Macbeth afraid. By Act III, Macbeth's fear drives him to independent murder while Lady Macbeth begins to crack — "Nought's had, all's spent, / Where our desire is got without content" (013-003). In Act V, the transfer is complete: Lady Macbeth has been consumed by the fear she thought she had expelled, and Macbeth fights on in a state beyond fear altogether.

## Dramatic Function

Fear serves as the play's principal dramatic mechanism, driving action where ambition alone would stall. Macbeth's fear of exposure produces Banquo's murder; his fear of Macduff produces the slaughter of Macduff's family; his fear of the future produces the second visit to the Witches. Each escalation of violence is a response to fear, making fear not merely a theme but the engine of the plot itself.

The play's structure mirrors the psychology of fear. The compression of time between Duncan's murder and its discovery creates breathless suspense; the banquet scene's oscillation between public ceremony and private terror dramatises fear's power to dissolve social order; the sleepwalking scene renders fear as a physical condition that cannot be hidden. Shakespeare makes fear visible — in Macbeth's startle at the Witches, in Lady Macbeth's compulsive hand-washing, in the ghost that only the guilty can see — transforming an internal state into theatrical spectacle.
