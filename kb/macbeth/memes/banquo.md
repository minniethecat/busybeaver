# Banquo

Banquo is a Scottish thane, general in King Duncan's army, and Macbeth's comrade-in-arms. As recorded in the *Dramatis Personae*, he is "General in the King's Army" ((P01-005)), a status he shares with Macbeth. He is present when the Weird Sisters deliver their prophecies — to Macbeth, that he shall be king; to Banquo, that his descendants shall be kings, though he himself will not. Unlike Macbeth, Banquo resists the temptation to act on the prophecy through violence, making him a crucial moral counterpoint in the play's exploration of ambition, fate, and free will.

## Military Standing and Early Portrayal

Banquo enters the play alongside Macbeth in the reports of battle, and his first stage entrance — "Enter Duncan, Malcolm, Donalbain, Banquo, Lennox, Macduff, Ross, Angus and Attendants" ((006-002)) — places him among the highest-ranking nobles in Duncan's court. His military standing is equal to Macbeth's at the play's opening: both are generals who have defended Scotland against rebellion and invasion. The Captain's report names them jointly as "our captains," linking their fates from the start. Banquo's battlefield competence establishes him as Macbeth's peer, not his subordinate — a detail that sharpens the tragedy of their eventual divergence. Both men hear the same prophecies; both possess the martial skill to seize power; only one chooses murder.

## Encounter with the Weird Sisters

Banquo's response to the witches on the heath reveals his character in sharp contrast to Macbeth's. While Macbeth "starts" and fears the prophecies, Banquo remains composed, questioning the apparitions with detached curiosity. His immediate instinct is rational skepticism — are these beings real, or hallucination? This epistemological caution marks Banquo as the play's voice of reason in the face of the supernatural. When the witches vanish, he famously compares them to "bubbles" of the earth — insubstantial and perhaps illusory. Yet Banquo does not dismiss the prophecy entirely. He later admits that thoughts of the Weird Sisters have troubled his sleep, and he acknowledges their disturbing power. He is not immune to temptation; he simply refuses to act on it.

> And oftentimes to win us to our harm,
> The instruments of darkness tell us truths;
> Win us with honest trifles, to betray's
> In deepest consequence.—
>
> (003-045)


## The Temptation of Prophecy

The critical moral distinction between Banquo and Macbeth crystallizes in Act II, Scene I, when Banquo and Fleance enter "with a torch before him" ((008-001)). Banquo tells Macbeth that he has dreamed of the Weird Sisters. When Macbeth probes — hinting at a potential alliance — Banquo's reply is unambiguous: he will cooperate only insofar as he can keep his "bosom franchised and allegiance clear." He will not sacrifice honour for advancement. This is the road not taken — the path of loyalty that Macbeth abandons within the same scene, dagger in hand. The scene ends with Banquo and Fleance exiting ((008-017)), the torchlight disappearing into darkness — a visual prefiguration of Banquo's murder and Fleance's narrow escape.

## The Murder

Banquo's virtue is precisely what dooms him. Macbeth, now king, recognizes Banquo's "royalty of nature" and fears it: "Our fears in Banquo / Stick deep" ((012-028)). More threatening still is the prophecy that Banquo's children will inherit the throne — "For Banquo's issue have I filed my mind" ((012-044)), Macbeth confesses, resolving that "Banquo, thy soul's flight, / If it find heaven, must find it out tonight" ((012-073)). Macbeth hires murderers who assault Banquo ((014-016)) while he is traveling with Fleance. Fleance escapes — preserving the prophecy's promise — but Banquo is slain.

Banquo's murder is the pivot point of Macbeth's moral descent. Killing Duncan was regicide wrapped in ambition; killing Banquo is the murder of a friend, driven by fear and the desperate attempt to secure a barren crown. The act yields no strategic advantage — "the seed of Banquo kings" ((012-049)) survives through Fleance.

## The Ghost at the Banquet

The appearance of Banquo's ghost at the coronation banquet is the play's most theatrical manifestation of Macbeth's guilt. Banquo returns not as a vengeful spirit but as a silent accuser, visible only to Macbeth. His ghost sits in Macbeth's chair — the very seat of power Macbeth killed to claim — and his presence reduces the king to public hysteria. The ghost of Banquo is a structural irony: the man who refused to act on prophecy is dead, but his spectral presence makes the living king a prisoner of his own conscience. Banquo's silence in death mirrors his moral silence in life — he never accused Macbeth openly, yet his mere existence (and posterity) constitutes an unbearable judgment.

## The Line of Kings

The witches' prophecy that Banquo's descendants shall be kings is fulfilled not within the play but in its aftermath. The apparitions' show of eight kings in Act IV — "A show of eight kings appear, and pass over in order, the last with a glass in his hand; Banquo following" ((018-055)) — culminates in a figure widely interpreted as King James I of England (James VI of Scotland), who traced his lineage to Banquo. For Shakespeare's original audience, Banquo was not merely a character but an ancestor of the reigning monarch — a political fact that makes his moral portrayal in the play a matter of statecraft as much as drama.

## Character Significance

Banquo's dramatic function is to show what Macbeth might have been. Both men receive supernatural prophecies that promise greatness; both are tempted; only one murders. Banquo proves that the witches' words do not compel action — they reveal character. His restraint, his insistence on honour, and his refusal to trade integrity for advancement establish the moral standard against which Macbeth's choices are measured. That he must die for his virtue is the play's darkest comment on the cost of integrity in a world where power has abandoned conscience.
