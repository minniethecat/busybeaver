# Despair

Despair in *Macbeth* operates on two registers: spiritual and existential. Malcolm describes King Edward the Confessor's miraculous ability to heal "the mere despair of surgery" — the illnesses beyond medicine's reach. Macbeth, by contrast, descends into existential despair in the "Tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow" soliloquy, where life itself becomes "a tale told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, signifying nothing." The play draws a sharp line between despair that can be healed and despair that destroys.

## Definition & Context

In Jacobean Christian theology, despair was a sin — the abandonment of hope in God's mercy, associated with Judas's suicide. Despair was distinguished from mere sorrow or melancholy by its finality: the despairing soul believes itself beyond redemption. *Macbeth* dramatizes both this theological framework (Edward's holy healing vs. Macbeth's damnation) and a more modern, secular despair in which life itself loses all meaning. Macbeth's trajectory from "I am afraid to think what I have done" to "I have liv'd long enough" traces a complete arc from guilt to utter hopelessness.

## Textual Evidence

### Spiritual Despair and Divine Healing (Act IV)

> The mere despair of surgery, he cures; / Hanging a golden stamp about their necks, / Put on with holy prayers. ((020-029))

Malcolm describes the English king Edward the Confessor's miraculous healing touch — a gift of divine grace that cures the "mere despair of surgery," illnesses that medicine cannot touch. This passage establishes despair as a condition that can be healed through grace, not medicine. The "golden stamp" (a coin blessed by the king) represents the tangible sign of a spiritual cure. Crucially, this healing power is contrasted with Macbeth's Scotland, where "each new morn / New widows howl, new orphans cry."

### Existential Despair (Act V)

Macbeth's most famous speech — "Tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow" — is the play's definitive statement of despair:

- Life is "but a walking shadow, a poor player / That struts and frets his hour upon the stage"
- It is "a tale told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, / Signifying nothing"

This is despair without the possibility of divine healing. Macbeth has crossed a line where he can no longer even hope for hope. The contrast with Edward's healing miracles in Act IV is deliberate: Macbeth's Scotland is a place where despair has no cure because the king himself is its source.

### Despair on the Battlefield (Act V)

> I have liv'd long enough. My way of life / Is fall'n into the sear, the yellow leaf. ([023-XXX])

Before the final battle, Macbeth acknowledges that he has arrived at the "sear, the yellow leaf" of life — autumn imagery suggesting decay without the possibility of renewal. He has lost honour, love, obedience, and "troops of friends." This is the social dimension of despair: not just a loss of meaning but a loss of all human connection.
