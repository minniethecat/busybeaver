# Valour (Military Courage)

Macbeth enters the play not as a schemer but as a soldier. "Brave Macbeth — well he deserves that name" (I.ii) is his first characterisation. His valour on the battlefield is the foundation of his honour, the reason Duncan grants him the thaneship of Cawdor. The tragedy traces how this martial virtue — authentic and celebrated — is corrupted into its opposite: the courage to murder a sleeping king, and finally the desperate bravado of a man who believes himself invincible.

## Definition & Context

In Jacobean culture, military valour was the supreme aristocratic virtue. A thane earned his title through service in battle; courage was the measure of manhood. Shakespeare's audience would have recognised Macbeth's battlefield prowess as the proper basis of nobility. The play's irony is devastating: the same quality that made Macbeth "brave" enables his crimes. Lady Macbeth exploits this when she questions his courage — "Art thou afeard / To be the same in thine own act and valour / As thou art in desire?" (I.vii).

## Textual Evidence

### Valour Celebrated (Act I)

> No sooner justice had, with valour arm'd, / Compell'd these skipping kerns to trust their heels (002-006)

The Sergeant's report establishes valour as a force allied with justice. Macbeth's sword-work is not mere violence — it is "valour arm'd," courage in the service of rightful order. This is the play's normative standard, against which all later violence will be measured.

> For brave Macbeth — well he deserves that name — / Disdaining fortune, with his brandish'd steel / Which smok'd with bloody execution, / Like Valour's minion, carv'd out his passage (002-007)

"Valour's minion" — valour's darling, its favourite. The personification makes valour a lord and Macbeth its devoted servant. The word "carv'd" suggests artisanal skill: Macbeth's killing is craft, not chaos. This is battlefield violence fully sanctioned by the moral order.

### Valour Questioned (Act I)

> And chastise with the valour of my tongue / All that impedes thee from the golden round (005-002)

Lady Macbeth appropriates the language of valour for her verbal campaign. She will "chastise" — a word implying physical punishment — but with her "tongue," not a sword. The metaphor transfers martial courage to the domestic sphere of persuasion, and in doing so, begins to corrupt it.

> Art thou afeard / To be the same in thine own act and valour / As thou art in desire? (007-007)

Lady Macbeth's goading is the pivot on which the play turns. She invokes "valour" to question Macbeth's manhood: if you desire the crown, does your courage extend to the act of seizing it? The word that once meant righteous battlefield prowess now means the willingness to murder one's guest. Valour has been semantically hijacked.

### Valour Corrupted (Act III onward)

> He hath a wisdom that doth guide his valour / To act in safety. (012-032)

Macbeth's assessment of Banquo reveals his own corruption. Banquo's valour is "guided" by wisdom — he is courageous but not reckless. Macbeth's valour, by contrast, has been unmoored from wisdom. He now acts out of fear, not honour, and his "courage" has become the desperate violence of a cornered animal.

> I'll fight, till from my bones my flesh be hack'd. / Give me my armour. (025-001)

Macbeth's final stand is a return to the battlefield idiom of Act I — but hollowed out. He calls for armour, fights, and dies "like a man." Yet this is not justice-armed valour; it is the last reflex of a man who has lost everything but his willingness to kill. The circle closes, but the meaning has drained away.

## Related Concepts

- [[honour]] — Valour's reward and confirmation; Macbeth's valour earned him honour, but he sacrificed honour to keep power
- [[manhood]] — Lady Macbeth repeatedly ties valour to masculinity; the play interrogates whether courage defines manhood or whether other virtues (compassion, loyalty) matter more
- [[murder]] — The perversion of valour: the courage to kill in battle becomes the courage to kill in secret
- [[fear]] — Valour's opposite; Macbeth begins fearless and ends ruled by fear, despite his apparent battlefield courage in Act V
- [[ambition]] — The force that corrupts valour; ambition redirects Macbeth's courage from service to self
