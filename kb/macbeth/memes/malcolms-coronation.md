# Malcolm's Coronation

Malcolm's coronation as King of Scotland represents the restoration of legitimate rule after Macbeth's tyrannical usurpation. Occurring offstage at the close of Act V, the event is invoked through Malcolm's final speech rather than directly staged, its deferred presence emphasising the play's concern with the conditions of legitimate sovereignty rather than ceremonial display.

## The Restoration of Order

Malcolm's ascent to the throne reverses the chain of usurpation set in motion by Duncan's murder. Where Macbeth's investiture at Scone was shadowed by Macduff's refusal to attend (011-017-018), Malcolm's coronation is framed as a collective act of national restoration: "We shall not spend a large expense of time / Before we reckon with your several loves, / And make us even with you" (028-029-031). The language of accounting and reciprocity replaces the language of tyranny and fear.
