# Treason

Treason runs through *Macbeth* like a dark thread, connecting the play's opening rebellion to its final confrontation. Shakespeare presents treason not as a single act but as a chain reaction: one betrayal begets another, political disloyalty breeds personal treachery, and the kingdom itself becomes a landscape of suspicion where no bond holds firm. The concept serves as the moral counterweight to loyalty, defining the boundaries of legitimate political action.

## Definition & Context

In Jacobean law and political thought, treason was the most serious crime against the state, punishable by death. The Treason Act of 1351 defined high treason as compassing the death of the king, levying war against him, or adhering to his enemies. *Macbeth* opens with a treasonous rebellion and centers on the ultimate act of treason—the murder of the king by his own subject. The play systematically explores how treason corrupts not only the traitor but the entire political community, making loyalty impossible and trust a liability.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> ROSS. That now Sweno, the Norways' king, craves composition; Nor would we deign him burial of his men Till he disbursed, at Saint Colme's Inch, ten thousand dollars to our general use. (002-019)

The play's first mention of treason is indirect: the Norwegian invasion is itself a form of treason against Scotland, and its defeat establishes Duncan's legitimacy as a ruler who can protect his kingdom. The conquered enemy must pay tribute, reinforcing that treason against the rightful king carries material as well as moral costs.

> DUNCAN. No more that Thane of Cawdor shall deceive Our bosom interest. Go pronounce his present death, And with his former title greet Macbeth. (003-043–003-045)

The original Thane of Cawdor's treason is the play's first explicit betrayal. Duncan's language—"deceive our bosom interest"—emphasizes that treason is not merely a political crime but a personal violation of trust. The traitor's title passes to Macbeth, in a dark irony: the new Thane of Cawdor will commit an even greater treason against the same king.

> DUNCAN. He was a gentleman on whom I built An absolute trust. (004-003)

Duncan's reflection on the executed Cawdor—"a gentleman on whom I built an absolute trust"—deepens the theme. Treason works from within: the traitor is not an external enemy but someone trusted, someone close. This is the pattern Macbeth will repeat, as Duncan's kinsman, subject, and host, enjoying "double trust" (007-002) that he will betray.

### Deepening (Act III)

> MACBETH. Treasons capital, confess'd and prov'd, Have overthrown him. (012-045)

Macbeth's description of the former Cawdor's fate is chillingly ironic. He speaks of "treasons capital, confess'd and prov'd" while himself guilty of the capital treason of regicide. His inability to see the parallel reveals how thoroughly treason has normalized itself in his mind—betrayal has become so natural to him that he can discuss it as an abstract legal category without self-recognition.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> MACDUFF. I am not treacherous. (020-022)

In Act IV, when Malcolm tests Macduff's loyalty by claiming to be a worse tyrant than Macbeth, Macduff's anguished response—"I am not treacherous"—is the play's most direct avowal of loyalty. In a world where treason has become the norm (Macbeth's Scotland), the simple assertion "I am not treacherous" becomes a radical political statement. Macduff proves his loyalty by refusing to accept a corrupt king, even when that king is the supposed legitimate heir—true loyalty to Scotland means opposing tyranny, not submitting to it.

> ANGUS. Now minutely revolts upbraid his faith-breach. (022-008)

In Act V, Angus describes how Macbeth's "faith-breach"—his original treason against Duncan—now returns as the daily treason of his own soldiers. Men desert him "minutely," every minute bringing fresh betrayal. The traitor who broke faith with his king finds that no one keeps faith with him. Treason is self-perpetuating: it destroys the bonds that make loyalty possible.

## Character Associations

| Character | Relationship to Treason | Key Scene |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------|
| The Thane of Cawdor (original) | The play's first traitor, executed for conspiring against Duncan. His title and his crime both pass to Macbeth. | 003-043 |
| Macbeth | The arch-traitor: he betrays Duncan (his king, kinsman, and guest), Banquo (his friend), and Scotland (his country). | 007-002 |
| Macduff | The anti-traitor. He refuses to attend Macbeth's coronation, flees to England to support Malcolm, and ultimately kills Macbeth to restore legitimate rule. | 020-022 |
| Malcolm | The legitimate heir who must navigate a world where treason is rampant. He tests Macduff's loyalty before trusting him—the necessary caution of a king in a treason-haunted kingdom. | 020-022 |
