# Sovereign

The concept of sovereign power in *Macbeth* — the legitimate, divinely sanctioned authority of a true king, contrasted with the usurped, unstable rule of a tyrant who seized the crown through murder.

## Definition & Context

In *Macbeth*, "sovereign" and "sovereignty" carry distinct political and theological weight. Sovereignty is not merely power — it is legitimate authority, ideally sanctioned by God and recognized by the body politic. When Macbeth murders Duncan and seizes the crown, he gains power but not sovereignty in this deeper sense. The distinction was urgent for Shakespeare's audience: James I had recently survived the Gunpowder Plot (1605), and the question of what makes a ruler truly "sovereign" — as opposed to merely powerful — was at the centre of Jacobean political theology. Shakespeare draws on the medieval doctrine of the king's two bodies: the body natural (mortal, fallible) and the body politic (the undying sovereign office). Macbeth destroys one but cannot inhabit the other.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> Give solely sovereign sway and masterdom. ((005-034))

Lady Macbeth's invocation of "sovereign sway" as her ambition for her husband reveals her understanding of sovereignty as absolute power — "solely" sovereign, without restraint or counsel. Her formulation anticipates the tyranny to come: sovereignty conceived as pure domination rather than stewardship.

### Deepening (Act III)

> Then 'tis most like / The sovereignty will fall upon Macbeth. ((011-013))

Macbeth — now king but insecure — fixes on the witches' prophecy that Banquo's heirs will rule. "Sovereignty" here is imagined as an inheritance, a legitimate succession that Macbeth, childless, cannot provide. His sovereignty is a dead end; Banquo's line will extend into the future.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> And top of sovereignty? ((018-043))

Macbeth's question to the apparitions — "shall Banquo's issue ever / Reign in this kingdom?" — reduces sovereignty to a dynastic contest. He seeks to control the future as he controlled the present, but the show of kings answers: Banquo's line stretches to the crack of doom. True sovereignty belongs to the legitimate succession, not the usurper.

> To dew the sovereign flower, and drown the weeds. ((022-011))

Lennox, speaking of the English king Edward the Confessor's miraculous healing power, uses "sovereign flower" for the true king and "weeds" for the tyrant. The metaphor is organic: sovereignty grows naturally, tyranny is an invasive species that must be drowned by the return of legitimate rule.

## Critical Perspectives

The play's treatment of sovereignty reflects the post-Gunpowder Plot anxiety about legitimate rule. Macbeth's crime is not merely murder but the destruction of the body politic — the king's two bodies are violently separated. The "sovereign flower" that Malcolm represents at the play's end restores not just a person but a principle: that sovereignty is sanctioned by right, not seized by force. James I's own writings on divine right kingship inform this framework — the sovereign is God's deputy, and rebellion against the sovereign is rebellion against God.
