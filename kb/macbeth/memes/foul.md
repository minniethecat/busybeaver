# Foul

The complement to "fair" in *Macbeth*'s central paradox — denoting evil, moral corruption, and cosmic disorder that becomes indistinguishable from fairness in a world where regicide has overturned natural order.

## Definition & Context

"Foul" in *Macbeth* carries moral, natural, and supernatural weight. It describes murder ("most foully"), the weather that accompanies evil deeds (fog and filthy air), and the spiritual state of characters who have embraced darkness. The witches introduce the term in their incantatory couplet — "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" — collapsing the distinction between good and evil in a single line. For Shakespeare's Jacobean audience, "foul" also evoked the stench of sin, the physical manifestation of moral corruption in a world where divine order had been shattered by regicide.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> Fair is foul, and foul is fair: Hover through the fog and filthy air. ((001-011))

The witches' opening couplet establishes "foul" as the equal and opposite of "fair," spoken in unison as a choral incantation. The pairing with "fog and filthy air" ties moral foulness to physical atmospherics — the play's world is literally and metaphorically polluted.

> So foul and fair a day I have not seen. ((003-015))

Macbeth's first line is an unconscious quotation of the witches, marking his language as already contaminated by their inverted logic. That a victorious battle — fair — is also "foul" (bloody, violent) establishes the play's moral ambiguity before any crime has been committed.

### Deepening (Act III)

> Thou play'dst most foully for't; yet it was said / It stood not within the prospect of belief. ((012-002))

Banquo, suspecting Macbeth of Duncan's murder, uses "foully" as the definitive moral judgment on the crime. His private suspicion — that Macbeth played "foully" — is the clearest moral statement in the play's middle acts, contrasting with the public court's willed blindness.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> Though all things foul would wear the brows of grace, / Yet grace must still look so. ((020-006))

Malcolm's reflection on the inversion of foul and fair reaches its philosophical peak here — in a world where "all things foul" don the appearance of grace, what distinguishes true grace from its counterfeit? This is the play's deepest moral question.

## Critical Perspectives

The foul/fair coupling has been read politically — as a critique of Jacobean court rhetoric where treason and loyalty wore the same verbal costume — and theologically, as a meditation on how evil presents itself. In performance, the witches' delivery of "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" often sets the tonal key for the entire production: chanted in unison, whispered, or sung, it announces a world where moral categories have collapsed.
