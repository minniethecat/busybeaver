## Overview

The language of devils and fiends runs through *Macbeth* like a dark thread, intensifying as Macbeth descends from thane to tyrant to "hell-hound" (026-008). What begins as metaphor — Banquo's startled "What, can the devil speak true?" (001-003) — becomes, by the play's end, literal identity: Macbeth is a devil, his queen is fiend-like, and their castle has become an outpost of hell.

## "Can the Devil Speak True?"

Banquo's question after the Witches' first prophecy materialises — "What, can the devil speak true?" (001-003) — establishes the diabolical frame through which the play will view supernatural soliciting. The Witches may speak truth, but they are "instruments of darkness" (001-003) whose truths are traps. The devil, in theological tradition, is the father of lies — but in *Macbeth*, the devil's most dangerous weapon is partial truth.

This ambiguity haunts Macbeth throughout. He cannot distinguish between diabolical deceit and genuine prophecy until it is too late. The Apparitions in Act IV — conjured by the Witches in a ceremony that invokes "demons" and "spirits" — deliver truths that damn by being literally true but contextually false.

## The Porter's Hell-Comedy

The Porter scene provides the play's most concentrated dose of diabolical imagery, and it comes at the darkest possible moment — immediately after Duncan's murder. The knocking at the gate becomes, in the Porter's drunken fancy, the arrival of souls at hell's door: "Who's there, i' th' name of Belzebub?" (005-003). The Porter catalogues the damned — the farmer who hanged himself, the equivocator, the English tailor — in a grim jest that the audience alone can decode: the hell-gate is not metaphorical; a king lies murdered within.

The Porter's self-description — "I'll devil-porter it no further" (005-003) — and his mention of "th' everlasting bonfire" place the castle's inhabitants in a hell of their own making. The scene is comic in tone but tragic in implication: Macbeth's castle has become the devil's house.

## Fiend and Hell-Hound: Macbeth's Final Names

As the play accelerates toward its conclusion, the diabolical epithets for Macbeth multiply. Malcolm calls him "Devilish Macbeth" (017-004). Macduff, encountering him in the final battle, cries: "Turn, hell-hound, turn!" (026-008). The term is not an insult but a classification — Macbeth has become what the Porter joked about, a creature of hell.

Macbeth's own self-knowledge confirms the judgment. He tells Macduff: "Of all men else I have avoided thee: / But get thee back; my soul is too much charg'd / With blood of thine already" (026-008) — an acknowledgment that he is, in his own reckoning, damned beyond recovery.

The play's final judgment, spoken by Malcolm, pairs Macbeth and Lady Macbeth as a diabolical dyad: "this dead butcher, and his fiend-like queen" (028-008). "Fiend-like" is the last word on Lady Macbeth, and it seals the diabolical reading of the tragedy.
