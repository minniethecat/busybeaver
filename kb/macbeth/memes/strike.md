The motif of striking — the physical act of landing a blow — runs through *Macbeth* as a metaphor for moral commitment. To strike is to cross the threshold from thought to action, from ambition to murder. Conversely, hesitation before striking, or striking beside the mark, signals the persistence of conscience in a play that relentlessly interrogates the cost of decisive violence.

## Key Appearances

### The Signal to Strike

> LADY MACBETH. ...when my drink is ready, She strike upon the bell. Get thee to bed. ([(008-018)])

Lady Macbeth plans the murder with a domestic cue: when Duncan's drink is prepared, she will "strike upon the bell." The mundane verb "strike" — used for ringing a bell — is made sinister by its context. The bell that summons Duncan to heaven or hell also signals Macbeth to strike. The word bridges the domestic and the murderous in a single unsettling image.

### Striking Out the Light

> THIRD MURDERER. Who did strike out the light? ([(014-019)])

During Banquo's murder, the Third Murderer asks who "struck out the light" — extinguishing the torch that illuminated the scene. The question is both practical (they killed Fleance's father but lost Fleance himself in darkness) and symbolic: striking out the light enacts the moral darkness Macbeth has chosen. Banquo's murder happens in the dark because Macbeth's reign has already extinguished the light.

### The Blows That Miss

> MACBETH. I cannot strike at wretched kerns, whose arms are hired to bear their staves. ([(027-014)])

Confronting Young Siward in the final battle, Macbeth dismisses the hired soldiers as unworthy of his blade — yet his confidence is hollow. Moments later, Macduff reveals that Macbeth's power is spent. The inability to "strike at wretched kerns" captures Macbeth's tragic state: he can kill kings and children, but cannot strike meaningfully against those who threaten him.

> SIWARD. We have met with foes That strike beside us. ([(027-018)])

In the battle's aftermath, Siward observes that the enemy's blows have been glancing, "beside" their targets. The observation is a judgment: the tyrant's power, which seemed absolute, has become ineffectual. Striking beside the mark is the physical manifestation of a cause that has lost its force.

## Thematic Analysis

Striking in *Macbeth* is never just physical violence. Each act of striking is freighted with moral significance: the bell that signals the strike, the light struck out in the murder, the blows that glance off or miss. Lady Macbeth fears her husband is "too full o' the milk of human kindness" to strike decisively; she herself, having urged the blow, is ultimately destroyed not by a counter-strike but by the internal recoil of guilt. The play suggests that the true cost of striking is not in the blow delivered but in what the striker becomes.
