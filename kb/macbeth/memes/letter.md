# Letter

The letter Lady Macbeth reads aloud in Act I Scene v is one of the most consequential props in Shakespearean tragedy — a written message that transforms private prophecy into murderous intent and reveals the intimate dynamics of the Macbeths' marriage.

## Definition & Context

When Lady Macbeth enters reading her husband's letter, the audience gains privileged access to both the content of the Weird Sisters' prophecy and the relationship between the Macbeths. The letter serves multiple dramatic functions: it conveys exposition economically (the audience learns what the witches told Macbeth), it establishes that the Macbeths communicate as intellectual equals, and it reveals Lady Macbeth's instantaneous turn from reader to plotter. In a play obsessed with speech and silence, the letter is a rare written document — an object that carries meaning across distance and sets the tragedy in motion before Macbeth even arrives on stage. The letter also allows Shakespeare to dramatise Lady Macbeth's inner response: she reads, interprets, and immediately begins her transformation.

## Textual Evidence

### The Letter Scene (Act I Scene v)

> *Enter Lady Macbeth, reading a letter.* (005-001)

The stage direction itself is significant: Lady Macbeth enters already absorbed in reading. Before she speaks a word, the audience sees her engaged with her husband's written voice — a marriage conducted through shared intelligence.

> They met me in the day of success; and I have learned by the perfectest report, they have more in them than mortal knowledge. (005-001, letter content)

Macbeth's letter to his wife reports the witches' prophecy with evident excitement. His phrase "more in them than mortal knowledge" reveals that he already half-credits their supernatural claims. The letter's tone — intimate, confiding — shows a marriage of genuine partnership.

> That I may pour my spirits in thine ear, / And chastise with the valour of my tongue / All that impedes thee from the golden round. (005-002)

Lady Macbeth's immediate response to the letter is to plan her persuasive assault. The letter has done its work: it has planted the idea of kingship, and now Lady Macbeth will "pour her spirits" into her husband to overcome his scruple. The progression from written word to spoken persuasion to physical act traces the play's movement from thought to deed.

> Thy letters have transported me beyond / This ignorant present, and I feel now / The future in the instant. (005-028)

Lady Macbeth's response to a messenger bringing news of Duncan's arrival returns to the theme of letters. The written word "transports" her — the metaphor is both spatial (carried beyond the present moment) and psychological (ecstatic, beyond reason). The letter has dissolved the boundary between present and future.
