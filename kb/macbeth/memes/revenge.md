Revenge in *Macbeth* operates as both a personal motive and a structural principle of the tragedy. Unlike the classic revenge tragedies of the Elizabethan stage — *Hamlet*, *The Spanish Tragedy* — *Macbeth* does not centre on a revenger from the outset. Instead, revenge arrives late in the play, as the response to accumulated wrongs rather than the initial premise. It is the force that finally topples the [[tyrant]], transforming private grief into public [[justice]].

## The Cry for Revenge

The word "revenge" first enters the play at the moment of Banquo's murder. As Banquo falls, he calls out to his son:

> BANQUO. O, treachery! Fly, good Fleance, fly, fly, fly!
> Thou mayst revenge — O slave!
> (014-017)

Banquo's dying words charge Fleance with the duty of vengeance. The broken syntax — "Thou mayst revenge — O slave!" — captures the urgency of the moment: Banquo is dying, Fleance must flee, and revenge is deferred to an uncertain future. The word "slave" has a double edge: it is addressed to the murderers, but it also echoes the language of servitude and [[tyranny]] that pervades the play. A slave cannot avenge himself; only a free man can exact revenge.

This moment establishes a crucial pattern: in *Macbeth*, revenge is promised but not immediately delivered. Fleance escapes into the night, and his revenge — the fulfilment of the witches' prophecy that Banquo's children shall be kings — lies far in the future, beyond the play's time frame.

## Macduff's "Great Revenge"

The true engine of revenge in the play is Macduff. After Macbeth's murderers slaughter his wife and children, Macduff's grief is transformed into a focused desire for retribution. Malcolm, testing Macduff's loyalty, urges him to channel his sorrow into action:

> MALCOLM. Be this the whetstone of your sword. Let grief
> Convert to anger. Blunt not the heart, enrage it.
> (020-060)

Malcolm then makes the case for revenge as a kind of medicine:

> MALCOLM. Be comforted.
> Let's make us med'cines of our great revenge,
> To cure this deadly grief.
> (020-061)

The metaphor of revenge as "med'cine" is striking. Grief is a deadly illness; revenge is the cure. This medical framing transforms revenge from an act of blind passion into a therapeutic and even moral necessity — a way of restoring health to a wounded individual and a wounded nation. The word "great" elevates Macduff's personal vendetta to the scale of national deliverance.

Macduff's revenge differs fundamentally from the reflexive vengeance of a Hamlet or a Titus. He does not hesitate, plot, or descend into madness. Instead, he attaches his personal cause to Malcolm's military campaign, merging private grief with political legitimacy. When he finally confronts Macbeth on the battlefield at Dunsinane, his cry — "I have no words; My voice is in my sword" （028-018） — encapsulates the action-oriented nature of his revenge: it needs no soliloquy, only steel.

## Revenge and Gender

The play draws a sharp contrast between Macduff's mode of revenge and the feminine grief that precedes it. When Macduff first learns of his family's slaughter, Malcolm urges him to "dispute it like a man" （020-057）. Macduff's response is layered:

> MACDUFF. I shall do so;
> But I must also feel it as a man.
> I cannot but remember such things were
> That were most precious to me.
> (020-058)

Macduff's insistence on feeling "as a man" — rather than denying emotion — complicates the simple equation of masculinity with violent action. Revenge, in Macduff's formulation, is not the suppression of grief but its transformation. Malcolm's later command to "let grief convert to anger" （020-060） completes this alchemy: the "womanly" tears of lamentation become the "manly" steel of vengeance. The play grants Macduff the space to grieve before he kills, a courtesy it denies to Macbeth, whose emotional range narrows to numbness as the tragedy proceeds.

## Revenge and the Restoration of Order

The play's treatment of revenge is politically conservative. Revenge is legitimate only when it serves the restoration of rightful [[kingship]]. Macbeth's murders are a perversion of order; Macduff's revenge is its reassertion. The distinction lies in the moral framework: Macbeth kills out of [[ambition]], Macduff kills out of duty to the dead and to the living.

This is why Macduff's revenge does not taint him. In the classical revenge-tragedy tradition, the revenger is often morally compromised — Hamlet dies, Hieronimo descends into madness. Macduff, by contrast, survives and is honoured. Shakespeare seems to suggest that revenge, when it is indistinguishable from [[justice]], requires no tragic expiation. Macduff is not a revenger in the traditional sense; he is an instrument of providence, the "angel" whom Macbeth has scorned and who returns to deliver the final blow.
