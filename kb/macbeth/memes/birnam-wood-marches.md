# Birnam Wood Marches

The march of Birnam Wood to Dunsinane is one of the most dramatic reversals in *Macbeth*, transforming the Witches' seemingly impossible prophecy into a tactical stratagem that seals Macbeth's fate. The episode spans Act V Scenes ii through v, as Malcolm's army advances from Birnam Wood toward Macbeth's stronghold at Dunsinane, each soldier bearing a branch cut from the forest as camouflage.

## The Prophecy (Act IV)

The event's origins lie in Act IV Scene i, when the third apparition summoned by the Witches delivers Macbeth his final prophecy: "Macbeth shall never vanquish'd be, until / Great Birnam wood to high Dunsinane hill / Shall come against him" (018-045). Macbeth, interpreting this literally as an impossibility, asks incredulously: "Who can impress the forest, bid the tree / Unfix his earth-bound root?" (018-048). The prophecy itself is delivered in verse that Macbeth fatally misreads:

> Macbeth shall never vanquish'd be, until
> Great Birnam wood to high Dunsinane hill
> Shall come against him.
>
> (018-045) This false confidence, rooted in a failure to imagine figurative fulfilment, becomes the engine of his final act of self-deception. As the prophecies of the armed head and bloody child are realized, Macbeth clings ever more desperately to this third guarantee. When a messenger reports Birnam Wood approaching, Macbeth responds with disbelief: "Fear not, till Birnam wood / Do come to Dunsinane" (025-015).

## The March (Act V)

The strategy is introduced at the rebel council in Act V Scene ii. Angus reports that the Scottish thanes "Near Birnam wood / Shall we well meet them" (022-003), establishing Birnam as the rebel rallying point. By Scene iv, Siward gives the tactical order: "What wood is this before us? ... Let every soldier hew him down a bough / And bear't before him" (024-004, 024-006). Menteith confirms: "The wood of Birnam" (024-005). The camouflage disguises the army's true size, making it impossible for Macbeth's scouts to report an accurate count of the advancing forces.

Even as the prophecy collapses around him, Macbeth maintains his defiant literalism: "Till Birnam wood remove to Dunsinane / I cannot taint with fear" (023-002), and later, "Till Birnam forest come to Dunsinane" (023-033). His repetition of the impossible condition becomes increasingly desperate as the reality of the approaching wood-veiled army closes in.

## The Revelation (Act V Sc v)

The climax of the Birnam Wood motif arrives when a Messenger reports seeing "the wood begin to move" (025-012): "As I did stand my watch upon the hill, / I look'd toward Birnam, and anon, methought, / The wood began to move" (025-012). The literal truth is that men bearing branches have created the optical illusion of a marching forest. The prophecy is fulfilled not through magic but through military ingenuity.

## The Final Confrontation (Act V Sc viii)

In the play's climactic duel, Macbeth acknowledges the prophecy's fulfilment: "Though Birnam wood be come to Dunsinane, / And thou oppos'd, being of no woman born" (028-011). The twin impossibilities — a walking forest and a man not born of woman — converge in Macbeth's final moments, stripping him of his last supernatural guarantees.
