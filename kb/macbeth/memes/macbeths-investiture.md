# Macbeth's Investiture

Macbeth's investiture as King of Scotland at Scone represents the formal consolidation of his usurpation. Occurring offstage between Acts II and III, the ceremony is reported rather than shown, its absence from the stage emphasizing the illegitimacy of the transfer of power. The investiture transforms Macbeth from Duncan's murderer into Scotland's crowned king, but the play systematically undermines the ceremony's political validity.

## The Road to Scone

The investiture is first mentioned in Act II Scene iv, immediately following the discovery of Duncan's murder and Malcolm and Donalbain's flight. Macduff reports to Ross that "He is already nam'd; and gone to Scone / To be invested" (011-014-015), referring to Macbeth. Ross's response — "Will you to Scone?" / "No, cousin, I'll to Fife" (011-017-018) — is a quiet but decisive act of political refusal. Macduff's choice not to attend the coronation at Scone is the first public sign of resistance to Macbeth's rule, foreshadowing his eventual role as Macbeth's nemesis.

## The Political Significance of Scone

Scone was the traditional site of Scottish coronations, lending Macbeth's investiture the form of legitimacy. Duncan had earlier designated Malcolm as his heir, naming him "The Prince of Cumberland" — "which honour must / Not unaccompanied invest him only, / But signs of nobleness, like stars, shall shine / On all deservers" (004-008-011). The verb "invest" links Malcolm's legitimate designation to Macbeth's usurped coronation: both occur at Scone, but only one carries the moral authority of Duncan's blessing.

## The Hollow Crown

By the time Macbeth appears as king in Act III, the investiture has already occurred. But the play emphasizes the emptiness of the ceremony: Macbeth rules as "an untitled tyrant bloody-scepter'd" (020-021), his crown lacking the moral legitimacy that Malcolm's investiture would have carried. Macbeth himself acknowledges this hollow quality: "Upon my head they plac'd a fruitless crown, / And put a barren sceptre in my gripe" (013-005-006), recognizing that the investiture ceremony cannot confer what only legitimate succession provides — a dynasty.

## The Witches' Prophecies: Seeds of Investiture

The investiture is seeded early in the play, when the Weird Sisters greet Macbeth with successive titles. Each hail marks a stage in the trajectory from thane to king:

> FIRST WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Glamis! (003-018)

> SECOND WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Cawdor! (003-019)

The witches' salutation is the engine of the entire plot. Glamis is Macbeth's inherited title; Cawdor is a title he does not yet know is his. The sequence — Glamis, Cawdor, King — establishes the ladder of advancement that the prophecies dangle before Macbeth's ambition. When Ross arrives moments later to confirm the second title, the investiture of Cawdor becomes the proof that the witches speak truth:

> ROSS. And, for an earnest of a greater honour, He bade me, from him, call thee Thane of Cawdor: In which addition, hail, most worthy thane, For it is thine. (003-040)

This lesser investiture — the conferral of the traitor Cawdor's title upon the loyal Macbeth — is the play's model for all that follows. Duncan's gift of Cawdor's title demonstrates the king's power to bestow honours; Macbeth's acceptance of the title, while already imagining the crown, demonstrates how investiture can feed rather than satisfy ambition. The ceremony at Scone is the dark fulfillment of a trajectory begun on the heath.
