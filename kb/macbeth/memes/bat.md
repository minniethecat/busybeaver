The bat appears in *Macbeth* as a creature of the night, associated with the play's pervasive imagery of darkness, evil, and the supernatural. Though mentioned directly only twice in the play, the bat participates in Shakespeare's broader pattern of nocturnal animal symbolism that marks the moral descent of the tragedy.

## The Bat's Cloistered Flight

Macbeth invokes the bat in Act III, Scene ii, as he anticipates the murder of Banquo. Speaking to Lady Macbeth, he urges her to be cheerful before the evening's dark work:

> Then be thou jocund. Ere the bat hath flown
> His cloister'd flight, ere to black Hecate's summons
> The shard-borne beetle with his drowsy hums
> Hath rung night's yawning peal, there shall be done
> A deed of dreadful note.
> (013-014)

The "cloister'd flight" of the bat signals the onset of night-time malevolence. The word "cloister'd" — meaning enclosed or secluded — carries ironic religious overtones, contrasting monastic seclusion with the unholy business Macbeth is about to transact. The bat's flight marks the threshold between daylight order and nocturnal chaos, between the visible world of moral accountability and the hidden world of murder.

This passage places the bat in a trio of creatures — bat, beetle, and the goddess Hecate — that together sound "night's yawning peal," a death knell for Banquo. The bat is the first herald of the darkness that will swallow Duncan's former general.

## Black Hecate and the Bat's Patroness

The bat's "cloister'd flight" is not random; it answers "black Hecate's summons" （013-014）. Hecate, the classical goddess of witchcraft and the crossroads, presides over *Macbeth*'s nocturnal world. In Act III, Scene v — a scene whose authorship scholars dispute but whose thematic placement is telling — Hecate rebukes the Weird Sisters for meddling with Macbeth without her leave:

> HECATE. And, which is worse, all you have done
> Hath been but for a wayward son,
> Spiteful and wrathful, who, as others do,
> Loves for his own ends, not for you.
> (016-003)

Hecate's anger establishes a hierarchy within the play's demonic realm: the [[the-weird-sisters|witches]] are subordinate to a darker power, and the bat — Hecate's creature — flies at her summons. This is the same goddess whose "pale Hecate's off'rings" （008-039） Macbeth has already associated with the withered murder of sleep. The bat is thus doubly damned: it is the minister of a goddess who herself presides over the play's architecture of [[evil]].

The bat's association with Hecate also reinforces its liminal status. Hecate is the goddess of thresholds and boundaries — the spaces between life and death, order and chaos, the known and the unknown. The bat, a creature of twilight that inhabits the margins of the animal kingdom, is her fitting emblem.

## Wool of Bat: The Witches' Brew

The bat's second appearance comes in the most famous scene of witchcraft in English literature. In Act IV, Scene i, the [[the-weird-sisters|Weird Sisters]] gather around their cauldron and enumerate its grotesque ingredients:

> Eye of newt, and toe of frog,
> Wool of bat, and tongue of dog,
> Adder's fork, and blind-worm's sting,
> Lizard's leg, and howlet's wing.
> (018-007)

"Wool of bat" joins the catalogue of repulsive animal parts that give the witches' brew its power. The phrase is deliberately unsettling: wool belongs to sheep, creatures of pastoral innocence, but here it is attached to the bat, a creature associated with darkness and the demonic. The witches invert the natural order by assembling body parts from across the animal kingdom, and the bat — neither properly bird nor properly mammal — epitomises this transgression of categories.

## Symbolic Significance

The bat in Renaissance symbolism was a creature of ill omen, frequently associated with the devil and with witchcraft. Its nocturnal habits, its leathery wings (suggestive of demons in medieval iconography), and its habit of hanging upside-down in dark places all contributed to its reputation as an unclean beast. In the [[unnatural]] world of *Macbeth* — where "fair is foul and foul is fair" （001-010）, where [[horse|horses]] eat each other and [[owl|owls]] shriek through the night — the bat is at home. It belongs to the inverted cosmos that the Macbeths have called into being through [[regicide]].

The bat's two appearances frame the play's descent: the first marks Macbeth's decision to kill Banquo, extending his reign of terror beyond the initial [[murder]] of Duncan; the second appears in the cauldron scene, where Macbeth seeks knowledge of his fate from the very forces of darkness he has served. The bat thus traces the arc from private conspiracy to open commerce with the [[supernatural]].
