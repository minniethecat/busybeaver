# Darkness and Evil

In *Macbeth*, darkness is not merely the absence of light—it is the moral medium in which evil operates, the shroud sought by murderers, and the state to which the human soul descends through unchecked ambition.

## Definition & Context

The association of darkness with evil runs so deep in *Macbeth* that the two become nearly synonymous. From the Weird Sisters who deliver the inciting prophecy to Lady Macbeth's demand that "thick night" wrap itself in "the dunnest smoke of hell," the play constructs a moral cosmology in which light represents divine witness and darkness enables diabolical action. This framework draws on Jacobean demonology and the Christian tradition of light as grace, darkness as sin. But Shakespeare complicates the binary: characters who operate in darkness are not simply villains but human beings making choices whose consequences darken the world around them.

## Textual Evidence

### Invoking Darkness (Act I)

> Come, you spirits / That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here (005-010–005-011)

Lady Macbeth's first soliloquy in Act I Scene v is an invocation of demonic forces. Before she calls upon night, she summons "spirits" to strip her of feminine compassion—to "make thick my blood" (005-013) and "stop up th' access and passage to remorse" (005-014). The sequence is structured as a progressive descent: first the spirits, then the smoke of hell, then the murder. Darkness is the moral condition that makes each step possible.

> Come, thick night, / And pall thee in the dunnest smoke of hell (005-020–005-021)

Lady Macbeth's invocation explicitly links darkness to the demonic. She does not simply wish for nightfall; she summons the smoke of hell to blind her knife and heaven alike. The passage is theologically audacious—she asks hell to prevent heaven from witnessing her crime, choosing damnation knowingly.

> Nor heaven peep through the blanket of the dark / To cry, "Hold, hold!" (005-023–005-024)

The image of heaven blinded completes Lady Macbeth's descent. She demands that even divine witness be prevented, establishing darkness not as mere concealment but as active rebellion against moral accountability.

### Hell as Real Presence (Act II)

The Porter's scene—beginning with "Knocking within" at 010-001—transforms the idea of hell from metaphor to dark comedy. The Porter imagines himself as the gatekeeper of hell, admitting the damned. "Who's there, i' th' other devil's name?" he asks, as Macduff and Lennox arrive at the castle gates. The comic register does not diminish the theological point: Macbeth's castle, after Duncan's murder, is literally hell on earth.

### The Darkening of the Soul (Acts III–V)

By Act III, Macbeth has internalised the darkness he once invoked. His murder of Banquo is arranged without Lady Macbeth's knowledge—he no longer needs her to call upon spirits. By Act IV, he seeks out the Weird Sisters voluntarily, and the apparitions they conjure—the armed head (018-026), the bloody child (018-033), the crowned child (018-039)—are products of "Black Spirits" (018-013). The man who began as a noble thane has become, in the eyes of his enemies, a devil. The darkness that Lady Macbeth invoked in Act I has consumed them both.

## Critical Perspectives

The play's association of darkness with evil has occasioned significant critical debate. Traditional Christian readings see the darkness imagery as straightforward moral symbolism: sin operates in darkness, grace in light. More recent postcolonial and race-conscious criticism has complicated this reading, noting the play's repeated association of blackness with evil and its potential reinforcement of early modern racial hierarchies. The Porter's reference to an "equivocator" who "committed treason enough for God's sake" also ties the darkness-evil nexus to the Gunpowder Plot and contemporary anxieties about Catholic conspiracy, rooting the theme firmly in Jacobean political reality.
