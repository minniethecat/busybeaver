# The Apparitions

The Witches' conjuring of three apparitions in Act IV Scene i — an Armed Head, a Bloody Child, and a Crowned Child with a tree — delivers the most pivotal prophecies of the play, convincing Macbeth of his invulnerability while concealing the fatal conditions that will destroy him.

## Dramatic Context

The apparitions appear during Macbeth's second visit to the Witches on the heath, after Hecate's intervention and the Witches' preparation of their "charm" in the cauldron. This scene falls at the midpoint of Act IV, after Macbeth has already murdered Duncan, Banquo, and Macduff's family. Having secured his throne through blood, Macbeth now seeks supernatural reassurance about his future. The apparitions offer him exactly the kind of prophecy he wants to hear — but their ambiguity will prove fatal.

## The Three Apparitions

### First Apparition: The Armed Head

> Thunder. An Apparition of an armed Head rises. (018-026)

The Armed Head warns: "Macbeth! Macbeth! Macbeth! Beware Macduff. / Beware the Thane of Fife." Macbeth responds that he already suspected Macduff, and the warning reinforces his determination to "make assurance double sure" by murdering Macduff's family.

### Second Apparition: The Bloody Child

> Thunder. An Apparition of a bloody Child rises.

The Bloody Child declares: "Be bloody, bold, and resolute; laugh to scorn / The power of man, for none of woman born / Shall harm Macbeth." Macbeth interprets this as a guarantee of invincibility — every man is born of woman (018-036). He decides to spare Macduff, believing no mortal can threaten him, but resolves to kill Macduff anyway "that I may tell pale-hearted fear it lies."

### Third Apparition: The Crowned Child with a Tree

> Thunder. An Apparition of a Child crowned, with a tree in his hand, rises. (018-039)

> What is this, / That rises like the issue of a king, / And wears upon his baby brow the round / And top of sovereignty? (018-041)

The Crowned Child prophesies: "Macbeth shall never vanquish'd be, until / Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane Hill / Shall come against him." Macbeth rejoices — a forest cannot march (018-045). He does not realize that Malcolm's army will use branches from Birnam Wood as camouflage, fulfilling the prophecy literally.

## The Show of Kings

After the three apparitions, the Witches show Macbeth a procession of eight kings, the last holding a mirror, with Banquo's ghost smiling at the end (018-055–018-056). This vision reveals that Banquo's descendants — not Macbeth's — will rule Scotland, extending to James I of England (Shakespeare's patron). The show of kings shatters Macbeth's confidence and triggers his decision to murder Macduff's family immediately.

## Immediate Consequences

- Macbeth resolves to "seize upon Fife" and slaughter Macduff's family
- He shifts from political murder to indiscriminate tyranny: "The very firstlings of my heart shall be / The firstlings of my hand" (018-069)
- Lennox brings news that Macduff has fled to England, confirming Macbeth's suspicions

## Long-term Significance

The apparitions are the structural pivot of the play's second half. Each prophecy comes true, but not as Macbeth expects: Macduff was "from his mother's womb / Untimely ripp'd" (Caesarean birth), and Birnam Wood does advance on Dunsinane. The scene exemplifies the play's central theme of equivocation — words that seem to promise safety actually conceal destruction. It also marks Macbeth's final descent from reluctant murderer to reckless tyrant.
