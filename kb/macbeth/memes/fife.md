# Fife

Fife is a historic county on the east coast of Scotland, between the Firth of Tay and the Firth of Forth. In *Macbeth*, it is the thanage held by Macduff, whose loyalty to Duncan and Scotland makes Fife a symbolic locus of resistance against Macbeth's tyranny (002-017).

## Geographic Context

Fife is a peninsula in eastern Scotland, bordered by water on three sides. In the play, it is the domain of Macduff, the Thane of Fife, and the site of his castle, where Lady Macduff and her son are murdered on Macbeth's orders.

Ross brings news "From Fife" to King Duncan in Act I, reporting the Norwegian assault (002-017). The distance between Fife and the royal seats of power underscores Macduff's position as a semi-autonomous regional lord. When Macduff declares "I'll to Fife" (011-018) rather than attend Macbeth's coronation, the region becomes a geographical expression of political dissent.

## Symbolic Significance

Fife functions as the symbolic counterweight to Macbeth's tyrannical center. While Inverness and Dunsinane become sites of violated hospitality and false security, Fife represents authentic loyalty and the cost of resistance.

Macbeth's order to "Seize upon Fife" and murder Macduff's family (018-069) marks his descent from political assassination to indiscriminate violence against women and children. Macduff's refusal to attend the coronation at Scone -- choosing instead to go "to Fife" (011-018) -- signals his moral rejection of the usurper.

> No, cousin, I'll to Fife.
>
> (011-018)


Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking utterance "The Thane of Fife had a wife. Where is she now?" (021-018) ties the guilt for Fife's destruction directly to her conscience, linking the violated region to the play's central theme of guilt's inescapability.
