# Spirit

In *Macbeth*, "spirit" operates across a spectrum from mortal courage to supernatural agency — a word that characters invoke when they reach beyond ordinary human capacity, whether for martial valour, murderous resolve, or forbidden knowledge.

## Definition & Context

Shakespeare plays on the double sense of "spirit" throughout *Macbeth*: it can mean human mettle and courage (Macbeth's "lavish spirit" in battle), intoxicating drink ("spirits" that Lady Macbeth fortifies herself with), or supernatural beings ("spirits that tend on mortal thoughts"). This semantic range allows the play to trace a single word from heroic virtue down into demonic invocation. Lady Macbeth's famous plea — "Come, you spirits / That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here" — is the pivot on which the word turns from the natural to the unnatural. The Weird Sisters, described as oracular spirits by Banquo, complete the arc: by Act IV, Macbeth actively seeks out spirit-knowledge, no longer a passive recipient.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> Curbing his lavish spirit: and, to conclude, / The victory fell on us. (002-017)

The Captain's report uses "lavish spirit" to describe Macbeth's battle fury — spirit as martial excellence, wholly positive and sanctioned. This is the word's heroic register, which the play will systematically invert.

> Come, you spirits / That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here, / And fill me from the crown to the toe top-full / Of direst cruelty! (005-010)

Lady Macbeth's invocation is the play's most explicit conjuring. She calls on "spirits" to undo her feminine nature and replace it with remorseless cruelty. The passage links spirit-agency directly to gender transgression and the abandonment of natural order.

> That I may pour my spirits in thine ear, / And chastise with the valour of my tongue (005-002)

Lady Macbeth uses "spirits" here in its double sense — her own forceful character that she will infuse into Macbeth through persuasion. The metaphor of pouring liquid into an ear suggests both intoxication and possession.

### Deepening (Acts III–IV)

> Your spirits shine through you. Within this hour / I will advise you. (012-069)

Macbeth acknowledges the Weird Sisters' supernatural nature. "Your spirits shine through you" suggests translucency — these beings are vessels for something beyond the mortal.

> Thou are too like the spirit of Banquo. Down! / Thy crown does sear mine eye-balls. (018-056)

At the witches' cave, the apparition of Banquo's line of kings provokes Macbeth's terror. "The spirit of Banquo" here means his ghostly progeny — Banquo's spirit persisting through his descendants to threaten Macbeth's stolen crown.

### Culmination (Act V)

> The spirits that know / All mortal consequences have pronounced me thus (023-002)

Macbeth's final delusion is that spirit-prophecies protect him. The "spirits that know all mortal consequences" are the very forces that have led him to destruction, their riddling prophecies now revealed as traps.
