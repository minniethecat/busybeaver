# Growth (Planting & Harvest Motif)

The planting and growth metaphor in *Macbeth* traces a complete agricultural cycle — from Duncan's promise to cultivate loyal thanes, through the poisoned harvest of Macbeth's tyranny, to Malcolm's pledge to "plant" new virtues in the restored kingdom. The motif provides the play's most sustained organic metaphor for legitimate versus illegitimate rule, drawing on a deep tradition of horticultural political imagery in Renaissance thought.

## Definition & Context

Shakespeare inherits the planting metaphor from a rich tradition: the king as gardener was a commonplace of medieval and Renaissance political theory, from John of Salisbury's *Policraticus* to Richard II's famous garden scene. In *Macbeth*, the metaphor is concentrated and inverted. Duncan is the true gardener who plants and nurtures; Macbeth is the blight that chokes the crop. The motif extends from the opening act — where the witches claim to know "which grain will grow, and which will not" (003-021) — to the final speech, where Malcolm promises "what's more to do / Which would be planted newly with the time" (028-028). The arc from planting to harvest to replanting mirrors the play's larger structure of order disrupted and restored.

## Textual Evidence

### Duncan's Planting (Act I)

> I have begun to plant thee, and will labour / To make thee full of growing. (004-006)

Duncan's promise to Macbeth is the play's foundational agricultural metaphor. The king as gardener cultivates loyalty and virtue in his subjects; the "labour" he speaks of is the work of good governance. The image is feudal and organic — the king tends his realm as a husbandman tends his field. Banquo's reply — "There if I grow, / The harvest is your own" (004-007) — completes the metaphor: loyal growth returns its yield to the sovereign. The tragedy is that the very man Duncan plants will become the poison in the soil.

### The Witches' Grain

> … say which grain will grow, and which will not. (003-021)

Banquo's challenge to the witches introduces the motif even before Duncan's planting speech. The sisters are imagined as knowing which seeds will sprout — a claim to knowledge of fate and futurity that frames all subsequent uses of the metaphor. The question of which "grain" (Macbeth? Banquo? their descendants?) will grow into kingship drives the entire plot.

### The Growth of Evil (Acts III–IV)

> There the grown serpent lies; the worm that's fled / Hath nature that in time will venom breed. (015-014)

Macbeth's soliloquy after Banquo's murder perverts the growth metaphor. Banquo is the "grown serpent" cut down, but Fleance, the "worm," will grow into a new threat. The language of natural maturation is now applied to vengeance and danger — what grows is not virtue but venom. The organic metaphor of kingship has been poisoned by the very act of regicide.

### Malcolm's Renewal (Act V)

> … what's more to do, / Which would be planted newly with the time. (028-028)

Malcolm's final speech restores the planting metaphor to its proper, legitimate sense. As the new king, he will "plant" — cultivate, establish, renew — what the time requires. The cycle is complete: the garden that Duncan planted, that Macbeth blighted, will be replanted under legitimate rule. The metaphor closes the play's political arc with an image of organic restoration.
