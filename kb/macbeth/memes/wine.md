# Wine (Intoxication Motif)

Wine in *Macbeth* is never merely refreshment. It is Lady Macbeth's weapon for disarming Duncan's guards, Macbeth's desperate prop at the banquet, and the basis of the play's bleakest metaphor: "The wine of life is drawn" (010-047). The motif traces a downward arc from strategic intoxication to existential emptiness, mapping the moral trajectory of the Macbeths' reign.

## Definition & Context

Shakespeare uses wine along a spectrum: as a practical tool of deception (drugging the grooms), as social lubricant turned grotesque (the banquet scene), and as metaphysical symbol (life as wine, now spent). The motif participates in a broader Renaissance discourse about intoxication and reason — Lady Macbeth's plan to "convince" the chamberlains with wine (007-011) exploits the early modern understanding that alcohol dissolves the rational faculty, making men beast-like and vulnerable. The Porter's drunkenness in Act II provides the comic counterpoint: his intoxicated ramblings about drink's effects on "nose-painting, sleep, and urine" (010-002) form a Rabelaisian catalogue that both relieves and deepens the horror of the preceding murder.

## Textual Evidence

### Strategic Intoxication (Act I)

> … his two chamberlains / Will I with wine and wassail so convince / That memory, the warder of the brain, / Shall be a fume. (007-011)

Lady Macbeth's plan to drug Duncan's guards is the play's first deployment of wine as weapon. The precision of her language — "memory, the warder of the brain" turned to "fume" — reveals her understanding of intoxication as the dissolution of reason and vigilance. The guards will be blamed for the murder they were too drunk to prevent, making wine the instrument of a double crime: assassination and false accusation.

### The Wine of Life (Act II)

> The wine of life is drawn, and the mere lees / Is left this vault to brag of. (010-047)

Macbeth's metaphor after Duncan's murder is the play's most concentrated image of existential depletion. Life is a cask of wine, now drained; only the dregs ("lees") remain. The image links the murder to the waste of something vital — wine, which should sustain and celebrate, has been spilled and lost. The "vault" carries the double sense of wine-cellar and tomb, collapsing the convivial and the funereal.

### The Banquet (Act III)

> Give me some wine, fill full. — I drink to the general joy o' th' whole table. (015-039)

At the banquet, Macbeth's call for wine is a desperate attempt to reassert social order in the presence of Banquo's ghost. The filled cup is a prop of kingship — a gesture of hospitality and command — but it is immediately undermined by the ghost's reappearance. Wine here cannot perform its social function; the rituals of fellowship have been poisoned by murder, and the cup that should unite the thanes only highlights Macbeth's isolation.
