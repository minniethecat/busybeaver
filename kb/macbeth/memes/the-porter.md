# The Porter

The Porter is a minor but memorable character in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. His brief appearance in Act II, Scene III provides comic relief — yet his drunken humor is laced with dark irony, as he imagines himself the porter of hell's gate on the very night King Duncan has been murdered within the castle.

## Overview

The Porter enters after the murder of Duncan, while knocking at the castle gate resounds through the night (010-001). In his drunken stupor, he delivers a rambling monologue, imagining himself as the porter at the gates of hell: "Here's a knocking indeed! If a man were porter of hell gate, he should have old turning the key" (010-002). He comically envisions admitting a farmer, an equivocator, and an English tailor — each a sinner on the "primrose way to th' everlasting bonfire" (010-002).

When Macduff and Lennox arrive, the Porter engages in bawdy banter about the effects of drink. He declares that alcohol provokes three things — "nose-painting, sleep, and urine" (010-008) — and philosophizes on drink's paradoxical relationship with lechery: "it provokes the desire, but it takes away the performance" (010-008). When Macduff suggests drink gave him the lie the previous night, the Porter cheerfully admits it did, "i' the very throat on me" (010-010).

## Dramatic Function

The Porter scene serves as a calculated tonal shift. After the taut horror of Duncan's murder in Scene II, the Porter's bawdy comedy allows the audience to exhale — before the shock of the body's discovery. His metaphor of the castle as hell is bitterly apt: unbeknownst to him, a regicide has just occurred within. The "equivocator" in his speech (010-002) is widely read as a topical reference to the Jesuit Henry Garnet, tried for his role in the Gunpowder Plot — making this the play's most direct allusion to contemporary Jacobean politics.
