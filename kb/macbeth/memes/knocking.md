# Knocking

A recurring auditory motif in *Macbeth*, the sound of knocking punctuates the aftermath of Duncan's murder and returns in the final act, marking the boundary between private guilt and public reckoning.

## Definition & Context

Knocking in *Macbeth* functions as an intrusion of the external world into the sealed chamber of murder. Following Duncan's death, Macbeth and Lady Macbeth are isolated in their guilt, but the repeated knocking at the south entry (Act II Scenes ii–iii) insists that the private act cannot remain hidden. Shakespeare uses the sound to build tension between the Macbeths' desperate attempts to compose themselves and the inexorable approach of discovery. In the Porter scene that follows, the knocking is comically reframed as hell-gate admissions, creating a darkly ironic bridge between the Macbeths' psychological horror and the public world about to learn of the crime.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Act II)

> Whence is that knocking? How is't with me, when every noise appals me? (009-029)

Macbeth's first reaction to the knocking reveals his shattered nerves. Having just murdered Duncan and returned with bloody hands, even an ordinary sound terrifies him. The rhetorical question "How is't with me" marks the beginning of his psychological unraveling.

> Wake Duncan with thy knocking! I would thou couldst! (009-031)

Macbeth's desperate apostrophe to the knocking — wishing it could reverse the murder — is one of the play's most concentrated expressions of irrevocable guilt. The wish is immediately impossible, underlining the permanence of his act.

### The Porter Scene (Act II Scene iii)

> Here's a knocking indeed! If a man were porter of hell-gate, he should have old turning the key. (010-002)

The Porter's comic soliloquy transforms the knocking into a metaphysical gate — the entrance to a hell that Macbeth's castle has become. The exaggerated repetition ("knock, knock, knock!") provides the play's most famous comic relief while simultaneously deepening the thematic weight: whoever enters now will discover the horror within.

> Our knocking has awak'd him; here he comes. (010-011)

Lennox's innocent remark to Macbeth — that their knocking woke the Porter — carries dramatic irony: the audience knows what far graver awakening the knocking portends.

### Late Return (Act V)

> To bed, to bed. There's knocking at the gate. Come, come, come, come, give me your hand. (021-029)

Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking scene closes with her imagining knocking at the gate, echoing the Act II motif. The return of this auditory hallucination in her final appearance ties her madness directly back to the night of Duncan's murder.
