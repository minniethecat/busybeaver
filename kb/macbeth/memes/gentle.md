# Gentle

The word "gentle" in *Macbeth* — a term denoting nobility, softness, and civilised restraint that becomes increasingly ironic as the play progresses, until "gentle weal" and "gentle lady" sound like memories of a world that no longer exists.

## Definition & Context

"Gentle" in *Macbeth* operates on multiple registers: it can mean noble by birth ("gentleman"), kind in manner ("gentle lady"), or mild in nature ("gentle senses"). The word's semantic range is the point — "gentle" describes the entire fabric of civilised society, from rank to manners to feeling. Macbeth's crime is the destruction of gentleness at every level: he murders a gentle king, becomes a tyrant who cannot be gentle, and presides over a kingdom where the "gentle weal" (commonwealth) is replaced by terror. For Shakespeare's audience, "gentle" was not merely a pleasant adjective but a political value — the quality that distinguished legitimate rule from tyranny.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> O valiant cousin! worthy gentleman! ((002-005))

Duncan's first lines in the play pair "valiant" with "worthy gentleman" — martial courage with civilised gentleness. The ideal nobleman unites both. This is the standard from which Macbeth will fall.

> This castle hath a pleasant seat; the air / Nimbly and sweetly recommends itself / Unto our gentle senses. ((006-003))

Duncan's arrival at Inverness is the play's last moment of unironic gentleness. The "gentle senses" he attributes to the castle's air are about to be violated by murder. The dramatic irony — the audience knows what the gentle king does not — makes these lines almost unbearable.

### Deepening (Act III)

> O gentle lady, / 'Tis not for you to hear what I can speak. ((010-044))

Macduff's address to Lady Macbeth as "gentle lady" is the play's most richly ironic use of the word. He protects her from the news of Duncan's murder, unaware that she orchestrated it. "Gentle" here is a gallant fiction — the lady is anything but gentle.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> Ere humane statute purged the gentle weal. ((015-037))

Macbeth's reflection on the pre-civilised past — before "humane statute" purged the "gentle weal" — is the word's philosophical high point. "Gentle weal" means the civilised commonwealth. Macbeth, who has destroyed that commonwealth, here becomes its unlikely elegist.

## Critical Perspectives

The concept of "gentle" in *Macbeth* reflects early modern debates about the nature of nobility — was it inherent (by blood) or acquired (by conduct)? Shakespeare seems to answer: both are destroyed by regicide. The "gentle weal" that Macbeth elegises is precisely what his crime has made impossible. The word's trajectory across the play — from Duncan's sincere usage through Lady Macbeth's ironic misapplication to Macbeth's philosophical nostalgia — charts the collapse of the social order that "gentle" represents.
