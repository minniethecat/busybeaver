# Wrath

The dual nature of wrath in *Macbeth* — human rage as a symptom of tyrannical rule and divine anger as the cosmic response to regicide, both driving the play toward its violent conclusion.

## Definition & Context

Wrath operates on two registers in *Macbeth*. On the human level, it describes Macbeth's increasingly ungovernable temper as king — the "spiteful and wrathful" tyrant who lashes out at any perceived threat. On the divine level, wrath is God's response to the violation of natural and moral order: the "wrath" that hangs over a kingdom where the true king has been murdered and replaced by a usurper. In Jacobean theology, these two wraths were linked — a wrathful ruler was both a punishment from God and a provocation of further divine wrath, a spiral of violence that could only be broken by the restoration of legitimate sovereignty.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

Wrath is absent from the play's opening acts, where Macbeth's violence is controlled, purposeful, and — in battle — sanctioned. The absence of wrath in the early Macbeth underscores his later degeneration.

### Deepening (Act III)

> Spiteful and wrathful; who, as others do, / Loves for his own ends. ((016-003))

The description of Macbeth as "wrathful" comes as part of the broader portrait of his tyranny. Wrath here is not righteous anger but petty, spiteful rage — the emotion of a ruler who governs by fear rather than justice. The pairing with "spiteful" underscores the smallness of Macbeth's wrath compared to the grandeur of divine anger.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> Let me endure your wrath, if't be not so. ((025-014))

The Messenger at Dunsinane, reporting the approach of Birnam Wood, braces for Macbeth's wrath — now the predictable response of a cornered tyrant. The messenger's plea acknowledges Macbeth's reign of terror: wrath has become the defining feature of his rule, the emotion that subjects most fear from their king.

## Critical Perspectives

Macbeth's wrath follows the classical pattern of the tyrant's degeneration: as his power becomes less secure, his temper becomes more violent. This is the pathology of tyranny that Shakespeare would have known from Seneca and from contemporary political writing. The distinction between Macbeth's human wrath and the divine wrath that ultimately judges him is the play's final moral calibration — human rage is petty and futile; divine justice is inexorable.
