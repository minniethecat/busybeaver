## Overview

The eye in *Macbeth* is the site of a sustained meditation on seeing, not-seeing, and the moral implications of vision. The play asks what it means to witness horror, to refuse to look, and to be looked at by what one has done. From Duncan's trust in what his eye beholds to Macbeth's plea that the stars "hide your fires; / Let not light see my black and deep desires" (001-004), the eye is both witness and judge.

## The Eye That Fears to See

Macbeth's first soliloquy frames the eye as the organ of moral cowardice. He wishes for the murder to be done without his own gaze upon it: "The eye wink at the hand, yet let that be, / Which the eye fears, when it is done, to see" (001-004). The eye's wink is the self's refusal to witness its own action — a willed blindness that anticipates Lady Macbeth's later sleepwalking, where her eyes are "open" but "their sense is shut" (020-001).

After the murder, Macbeth cannot look at his own hands: "This is a sorry sight" (002-002), the understatement registering the gap between what the eye sees and what the mind can process. Lady Macbeth's rebuke — "The sleeping and the dead / Are but as pictures. 'Tis the eye of childhood / That fears a painted devil" (005-002) — reduces the murdered Duncan to a mere image, a "picture" that only a child's eye would fear. But the play proves Lady Macbeth wrong: the eye of adulthood fears the painted devil too, and the picture will not stay still.

> Let not light see my black and deep desires.
> The eye wink at the hand, yet let that be,
> Which the eye fears, when it is done, to see.

## The Apparitions and Ocular Proof

The Apparitions in Act IV dramatise the eye's capacity to be deceived while seeing clearly. Macbeth sees each vision — the armed head, the bloody child, the crowned child with a tree — and draws conclusions that are literally true but fatally wrong. He demands "ocular proof" but cannot read what his eyes show him. The Witches, like the devil of theological tradition, tell truths that damn by being seen but not understood.

The play's most terrible visual register belongs to Macduff's response to his family's murder. Malcolm urges him to "give sorrow words," but Macduff's first response is visual: "He has no children. All my pretty ones? / Did you say all? O hell-kite! All? / What, all my pretty chickens, and their dam, / At one fell swoop?" (017-003). The eye that sees massacre can only repeat the word "all" — language fails before what vision registers.

## The Eye of Scotland

Malcolm's final speech looks forward to a Scotland where "the eye" of the king sees his subjects clearly — the opposite of Macbeth's reign of "Masking the business from the common eye" (012-006). The restoration of legitimate rule is also a restoration of transparent vision, where what the eye sees and what is true are again aligned.
