# Duncan's Murder

Duncan's Murder is the central crime of William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. It occurs in Act II, Scene II at Inverness, Macbeth's castle, and sets in motion the chain of violence, guilt, and tyranny that consumes the remainder of the play.

## Overview

King Duncan arrives at Macbeth's castle as an honoured guest. Lady Macbeth, having resolved to seize the crown, has drugged the King's chamberlains and laid their daggers ready for Macbeth. After much hesitation and the famous "dagger" soliloquy, Macbeth enters Duncan's chamber and kills him. He returns to Lady Macbeth with the bloody daggers still in his hands, declaring, "I have done the deed" (009-005).

Macbeth is immediately overcome with horror and guilt. He reports that he heard the chamberlains cry "Murder!" in their sleep (009-016), and that when they said "God bless us," he could not respond "Amen" (009-018). More disturbingly, he describes hearing a voice: "Sleep no more! / Macbeth does murder sleep" (009-022). The voice, he claims, continued crying "'Glamis hath murder'd sleep, and therefore Cawdor / Shall sleep no more. Macbeth shall sleep no more!'" (009-024).

Lady Macbeth, by contrast, is ruthlessly practical. When Macbeth refuses to return the daggers to the scene, she takes them herself, declaring, "Infirm of purpose! / Give me the daggers" (009-027). She smears the sleeping grooms with Duncan's blood to frame them: "If he do bleed, / I'll gild the faces of the grooms withal, / For it must seem their guilt" (009-027).

Macbeth's guilt is visually monumental. Looking at his bloodied hands, he asks, "Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood / Clean from my hand? No, this my hand will rather / The multitudinous seas incarnadine, / Making the green one red" (009-029). Lady Macbeth dismisses this with chilling pragmatism: "A little water clears us of this deed" (009-030).

## Aftermath

The murder is discovered in the following scene by Macduff, who cries, "O horror, horror, horror! / Tongue nor heart cannot conceive nor name thee!" (010-027). Malcolm and Donalbain, Duncan's sons, flee — to England and Ireland respectively — fearing for their own lives. Their flight casts suspicion upon them, clearing Macbeth's path to the throne.
