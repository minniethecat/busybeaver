# Nature (Unnatural)

The word "nature" and its derivatives appear throughout *Macbeth* as the play's central moral vocabulary. Shakespeare builds a cosmology in which **nature** is not merely landscape or temperament but a divinely ordained order — and every crime in the play is simultaneously a violation of that order. When [[macbeth|Macbeth]] murders [[duncan|Duncan]], he does not merely kill a man; he commits an act "'Gainst nature" (011-013) that ripples outward into storms, monstrous animal behavior, and the collapse of political legitimacy.

## The Language of Nature

### Nature as Temperament

Early in the play, "nature" refers to character. [[lady-macbeth|Lady Macbeth]] fears that her husband's nature is "too full o' the milk of human kindness" (005-002) to seize the crown. She calls on spirits to "stop up the access and passage to remorse" so that "no compunctious visitings of nature" (005-015) will shake her resolve. Here nature means conscience — the innate moral sense that must be suppressed before murder is possible.

### Nature as Cosmic Order

After Duncan's murder, the word shifts meaning. Ross and an Old Man discuss the unnatural events that have accompanied the crime:

>A falcon, towering in her pride of place,
>Was by a mousing owl hawk'd at and kill'd. (011-006)
>
>And Duncan's horses ... Turn'd wild in nature, broke their stalls, flung out. (011-005)

Ross's summary — "'Gainst nature still" (011-013) — makes explicit what the imagery implies: the murder has torn the fabric of creation. The animal world reflects the political world; both are in rebellion against their proper order.

### Nature as Mortality

The play also uses "nature" in the sense of natural lifespan. Macbeth is told he shall "live the lease of nature" (018-047) — a deceptive promise that seems to grant him invulnerability. The [[macduff|Macduff]] revelation — that he "was from his mother's womb untimely ripp'd" — exploits the word's ambiguity: what is "natural" birth, and what happens when nature's rules are circumvented?

## The Unnatural as Political Metaphor

Macbeth's tyranny is consistently described as violence against nature. Macduff calls his rule a "breach in nature" (010-055). [[malcolm|Malcolm]], testing Macduff's loyalty, claims to possess "boundless intemperance in nature" (020-015) — only to recant, revealing that his true nature is virtuous (020-022). The political argument of the play is that illegitimate rule is *structurally* unnatural: a body politic with the wrong head cannot function.

## Restoration

The play's conclusion is a restoration of nature in every sense. Malcolm's coronation re-establishes legitimate succession. The "perturbation in nature" (021-004) that the Doctor observed in Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking is resolved by death. And the Birnam Wood prophecy — trees marching — is revealed not as nature overturned but as nature conscripted into the service of justice.
