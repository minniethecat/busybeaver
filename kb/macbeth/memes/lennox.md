# Lennox

Lennox is a Scottish thane in William Shakespeare's *Macbeth*, present at the royal court from the opening scenes through the final battle. His dramatic arc moves from silent court attendant to ironic political commentator to active rebel, making him a barometer of Scotland's political health.

## Dramatic Role

Lennox appears throughout the play as a court thane whose function shifts as Macbeth's tyranny deepens. In the early acts, he is a background presence at Duncan's court (002-001, 004-001) and among the nobles who discover Duncan's murder (006-002, 010-004). His entry with Macduff at the gate in Act II, Scene iii positions him as a witness to the aftermath of regicide (010-004).

Lennox's most distinctive contribution comes in Act III, Scene vi, where in dialogue with another Lord he delivers a heavily ironic assessment of Macbeth's crimes (017-001). Speaking in veiled sarcasm, he catalogs the suspicious deaths — Duncan, Banquo — while pretending to accept the official narrative, establishing himself as one of the first court figures to voice covert dissent.

By Act V, Lennox has joined the rebel forces marching against Macbeth, appearing alongside Menteith, Caithness, and Angus (022-001, 024-001). His trajectory from court insider to rebel soldier mirrors Scotland's transformation under Macbeth's rule.

## Key Speeches

### "Things have been strangely borne"

In Act III, Scene vi, Lennox speaks to another Lord in bitterly ironic tones about the "strange" deaths surrounding Macbeth. His speech catalogs the convenient deaths of Duncan and Banquo while ostensibly accepting the official explanations, creating a portrait of a court where truth can only be spoken in code (017-001).

> My former speeches have but hit your thoughts,
> Which can interpret farther: only, I say,
> Things have been strangely borne. The gracious Duncan
> Was pitied of Macbeth:—marry, he was dead:—
> And the right valiant Banquo walk'd too late;
> Whom, you may say, if't please you, Fleance kill'd.
>
> (017-002)
