# Pray

The motif of prayer in *Macbeth* — the natural human impulse to call upon divine aid that is systematically blocked, refused, or hollowed out by the play's cascade of sin.

## Definition & Context

Prayer in *Macbeth* functions as a barometer of spiritual health. In the play's early scenes, characters pray naturally — Duncan blesses, Banquo invokes "merciful powers." After the murder, prayer becomes impossible: Macbeth cannot say "Amen," Lady Macbeth fears the dark because "hell is murky," and the kingdom's prayers are redirected toward the absent saint-king Edward in England. For a Jacobean audience, the inability to pray was the definitive sign of damnation — a soul cut off from God. The play traces this spiritual death through the failure of prayer, from Macbeth's blocked "Amen" to Lady Macbeth's desperate, unheeded confession.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> But they did say their prayers, and address'd them / Again to sleep. ((009-016))

Macbeth reports, with envy, that Duncan's grooms prayed before sleep. Prayer is paired with sleep as twin blessings of the innocent — both are "murdered" by Macbeth's act. The grooms' prayers frame the murder as not just political but spiritual violence.

> I had most need of blessing, and "Amen" / Stuck in my throat. ((007-007))

Macbeth's most famous spiritual confession. After killing Duncan, he hears a voice cry "God bless us" and "Amen," but cannot himself utter the word. The blocked prayer is the play's most economical image of damnation — the soul severed from its Creator.

### Deepening (Act III)

> Are you so gospell'd, / To pray for this good man and for his issue? ((012-059))

Macbeth's bitter question to the murderers he has hired to kill Banquo reveals his own estrangement from prayer. He cannot pray for any "good man" — the very concept is now alien to him. Prayer has become a weapon he cannot wield.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> Thither Macduff / Is gone to pray the holy king, upon his aid. ((017-003))

The one successful prayer in the play is redirected to the English court, where Edward the Confessor — a true king — can intercede with God. Prayer has fled Scotland with the murder of Duncan; it now resides with the legitimate line.

## Critical Perspectives

The blocked prayer in *Macbeth* draws on the rich early modern literature of despair — the state in which the sinner believes himself beyond God's mercy. Macbeth's "Amen" that "stuck in my throat" echoes Calvinist anxieties about reprobation: the terrifying possibility that one might be predestined to damnation. In performance, actors often deliver this line with a physical constriction — a hand at the throat — making the spiritual blockage tangible.
