# Power

Power in *Macbeth* is examined through multiple lenses: legitimate kingship versus usurped tyranny, the supernatural powers that tempt and deceive, and the ultimate impotence of mortal power against nature and Providence. The play charts Macbeth's trajectory from loyal thane to "bloody-scepter'd" tyrant, using power as its central diagnostic of moral corruption.

## The Apparitions' Prophecy: Mortal Power Defied

In the cavern scene, the Second Apparition — a bloody child — delivers what appears to be a promise of invincibility:

> Be bloody, bold, and resolute. Laugh to scorn The power of man, for none of woman born Shall harm Macbeth. (018-036)

The prophecy is a calculated deception. By equating "the power of man" with birth from woman, the Apparition defines mortal power in a way that seems to exempt Macbeth — but in truth sets the trap that Macduff, "from his mother's womb / Untimely ripp'd" (028-013-014), will spring. The scene dramatises the play's deepest insight about power: those who trust in supernatural guarantees are most vulnerable to their equivocation.

## The Tyrant's Power: Ross's Report

Ross brings to Malcolm and Macduff in England the news of Scotland under Macbeth's rule. His description of "the tyrant's power afoot" links tyranny with urgent, military force:

> When I came hither to transport the tidings, Which I have heavily borne, there ran a rumour Of many worthy fellows that were out; Which was to my belief witness'd the rather, For that I saw the tyrant's power afoot. (020-047)

Ross's language distinguishes between rightful authority and tyrannical force. "The tyrant's power" is not legitimate rule but armed coercion — power that must be constantly mobilised because it cannot rest on consent. The scene's location in England, before the "King's Palace," emphasises the contrast with the "pious" Edward the Confessor, whose royal touch heals the sick.

## The Tyrant's Power Corroded: Lady Macbeth's Madness

In the sleepwalking scene, Lady Macbeth's fragmented speech reveals the psychological cost of power seized through murder. Her invocation "who knows it, when none can call our power to account?" exposes the isolation of tyranny:

> What need we fear who knows it, when none can call our power to account? Yet who would have thought the old man to have had so much blood in him? (021-016)

The rhetorical question collapses under its own weight. If no one can call their power to account, why does she fear? The answer is in the next sentence: the blood that cannot be washed away. Power without accountability becomes its own punishment — a theme that runs from Duncan's murder to Macbeth's final encounter with Macduff.

## Power and Legitimacy

The play carefully distinguishes between power that derives from rightful succession and power seized through violence. Duncan's "royalty of nature" (012-020) commands loyalty; Macbeth's rule commands only fear. Malcolm, tested by Macduff, must demonstrate that he understands power as service rather than self-aggrandisement — a lesson Macbeth never learns.
