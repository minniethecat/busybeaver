# Sorrow (Grief)

*Macbeth* is a play saturated with occasion for sorrow yet starved of its authentic expression. Duncan's murder, Lady Macduff's slaughter, the collapse of Scotland — each calls for grief, but the play's political logic demands that sorrow be hidden, performed, or converted into revenge. Only Macduff's raw lament in Act IV offers a momentary release, and even that is policed by Malcolm's call to arms.

## Definition & Context

In early modern England, grief was both a private emotion and a public performance. Aristocratic mourning followed strict protocols; excessive grief was suspect (associated with femininity and disorder), while insufficient grief signalled coldness or guilt. Shakespeare exploits this tension throughout *Macbeth*: characters repeatedly accuse each other of performing sorrow rather than feeling it, and the play's most grief-stricken figure — Macduff — is told his tears are unmanly.

## Textual Evidence

### Performed Sorrow (Acts I–II)

> There's daggers in men's smiles (008-005)

Donalbain's aphorism captures the play's foundational distrust of visible emotion. After Duncan's murder, every expression of sorrow is suspected of being counterfeit.

> To show an unfelt sorrow is an office / Which the false man does easy. (010-075)

Malcolm's assessment of the thanes' grief exposes the political economy of sorrow: it is an "office," a duty one performs. The "false man" — and Macbeth is the arch-example — finds this performance effortless.

### Authentic Grief (Act IV)

> He has no children. All my pretty ones? / Did you say all? O hell-kite! All? / What, all my pretty chickens and their dam / At one fell swoop? (020-036)

Macduff's response to the news of his family's murder is the play's most unguarded expression of grief. The repetition of "all" — four times in three lines — is grief as syntactic breakdown. The domestic imagery ("pretty chickens," "their dam") clashes violently with the "hell-kite" metaphor, showing a mind unable to reconcile love and loss.

> Give sorrow words. The grief that does not speak / Whispers the o'er-fraught heart and bids it break. (020-056)

Malcolm's counsel to Macduff is both wise and problematic. "Give sorrow words" suggests that verbalising grief is therapeutic, but Malcolm immediately pivots to militarisation: "Let grief / Convert to anger; blunt not the heart, enrage it" (020-057). The play asks whether sorrow can ever be simply felt, or must always be instrumentalised.

### Sorrow Denied (Act V)

> She should have died hereafter; / There would have been a time for such a word. (025-009)

Macbeth's response to his wife's death offers no space for sorrow. The famous "Tomorrow" speech that follows is grief suppressed so completely that it becomes nihilistic philosophy. Macbeth has moved beyond the capacity for the feeling Malcolm urged on Macduff.
