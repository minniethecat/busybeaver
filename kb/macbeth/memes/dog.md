The dog appears in *Macbeth* in three distinct registers: as a metaphor for the social hierarchy of worth, as an ingredient in the witches' cauldron, and as the final epithet hurled at Macbeth before his death. Across these appearances, the dog traces a descent from a figure of low but accepted rank to the very emblem of the damned.

## The Diversified Dogs

In Act III, Scene i, Macbeth attempts to persuade two murderers to kill Banquo. To test their mettle, he deploys an extended metaphor drawn from the world of hunting and hounds:

> MACBETH. Ay, in the catalogue ye go for men,
> As hounds and greyhounds, mongrels, spaniels, curs,
> Shoughs, water-rugs, and demi-wolves are clept
> All by the name of dogs: the valu'd file
> Distinguishes the swift, the slow, the subtle,
> The housekeeper, the hunter, every one
> According to the gift which bounteous nature
> Hath in him clos'd.
> (012-061)

Macbeth's taxonomy of dogs is a rhetorical device designed to shame the murderers into action. The "valu'd file" — the hierarchical catalogue — sorts dogs by their natural gifts. The implication is that the murderers, like the lowest breeds of dog, occupy a debased rank in "the catalogue" of men, and that killing Banquo will prove them to be of higher worth. This is characteristic of Macbeth's later rhetorical style: manipulative, degradingly intimate, enlisting his interlocutors in a shared project of proving themselves through violence.

The passage also reflects the Renaissance worldview of the Great Chain of Being, in which every creature has its ordained place. Macbeth's perversion of this hierarchy — using it to justify [[murder]] — is itself a sign of the cosmic disorder his reign has produced.

## The Cur and the Noble Hound

Macbeth's dog catalogue contains a tension that the play never fully resolves: the difference between the "cur" — the mongrel of no worth — and the noble hound. In the Renaissance hunting manuals Shakespeare would have known, the distinction between breeds was a matter of moral seriousness. A greyhound was a gentleman's companion; a "demi-wolf" was barely domesticated. Macbeth's rhetorical strategy depends on this hierarchy being socially legible, yet his own transformation from "brave Macbeth" （002-016） to "hell-hound" （028-003） demonstrates how easily the categories collapse.

The word "cur" carries particular weight. In the same scene where Macbeth deploys his dog taxonomy, the Second Murderer declares himself "a man so weary with disasters, tugged with fortune, that I would set my life on any chance" （012-049） — he is, in effect, presenting himself as the "cur" of the catalogue, ready to prove otherwise through violence. Macbeth preys on this self-loathing. The dog imagery is not merely descriptive; it is manipulative, a tool of psychological degradation that the [[tyrant]] wields against those he wishes to enlist in his crimes.

## Tongue of Dog

In the cauldron scene of Act IV, Scene i, the dog returns as a literal body part in the witches' brew:

> SECOND WITCH. Eye of newt, and toe of frog,
> Wool of bat, and tongue of dog,
> Adder's fork, and blind-worm's sting,
> Lizard's leg, and howlet's wing.
> (018-007)

"Tongue of dog" is paired with "wool of [[bat]]" in the witches' grotesque recipe. The dog's tongue — the organ of barking, licking, and tasting — becomes a magical ingredient, stripped of its living context and repurposed for sorcery. In the symbolic logic of the cauldron, the witches dismember the natural world to assemble an unnatural power. The dog, a creature of loyalty and domesticity in normal life, is reduced to a detached organ in the service of [[evil]].

The conjunction of dog and bat in this passage is significant: both are creatures of the threshold, associated with the domestic and the wild respectively. The witches collapse these distinctions, creating a brew that blurs the boundaries between the natural and the demonic, much as Macbeth's reign has blurred the boundaries between [[kingship]] and [[tyranny]].

## Hell-Hound: The Final Epithet

The dog image culminates in the final confrontation at Dunsinane. As Macduff prepares to fight Macbeth, he cries:

> MACDUFF. Turn, hell-hound, turn!
> (028-003)

"Hell-hound" is the last name anyone calls Macbeth in the play. It is the ultimate degradation: from "brave Macbeth" （002-016） to "hell-hound." The epithet combines the dog — the lowliest of creatures — with [[hell]], the domain of absolute evil. Macbeth is no longer even a man in Macduff's eyes; he is a beast of the underworld, a creature that belongs to the infernal rather than the human.

Macbeth's response is telling. He does not deny the charge:

> MACBETH. Of all men else I have avoided thee.
> But get thee back. My soul is too much charg'd
> With blood of thine already.
> (028-021)

Macbeth acknowledges Macduff's family's blood on his soul, implicitly accepting the "hell-hound" designation. In this final exchange, the dog metaphor that began as a rhetorical device for recruiting murderers has become the unanswerable judgment on the [[tyrant]].
