# Angus

**Angus** is a Scottish thane who serves as a consistent witness to the unfolding tragedy of *Macbeth*. He appears alongside [[ross|Ross]] in the early acts delivering news to [[macbeth|Macbeth]], and by the final act has joined [[malcolm|Malcolm]]'s forces marching against the tyrant at [[dunsinane|Dunsinane]]. Unlike many minor characters, Angus speaks across multiple acts, making him one of the play's most persistent observers of Macbeth's transformation.

## Early Appearances

Angus first enters with Ross in Act I, Scene III, bearing the news that Macbeth has been named Thane of [[cawdor|Cawdor]] (002-011). He returns with Ross moments later (003-037) to deliver the formal investiture. His early role is as a messenger of royal favor — an instrument of the legitimate order that Macbeth will soon overthrow:

> Who's here? Enter Ross and Angus. (003-037)

In this early phase, Angus is a figure of the stable kingdom: deferential, ceremonial, a bearer of honors from a rightful king.

## Witness to the Tyrant's Court

Angus reappears in Act I, Scene IV at [[duncan|Duncan]]'s court (004-004) and again among the thanes gathered after Duncan's murder (006-002). His continued presence at court — silent in these moments but physically on stage — positions him as a barometer of noble opinion. When Macbeth's tyranny grows, Angus is one of the thanes who quietly withdraws support.

## Joining Malcolm's Rebellion

By Act V, Angus has thrown his lot with Malcolm. He marches with [[menteith|Menteith]], [[caithness|Caithness]], [[lennox|Lennox]], and Ross under English banners to liberate Scotland:

> Enter, with drum and colours, Menteith, Caithness, Angus, Lennox and Soldiers. (022-001)

His final appearance comes at the decisive moment, standing with the assembled Scottish and English forces before Dunsinane, ready to overthrow the tyrant (024-001). Angus's arc — from loyal messenger of King Duncan to rebel soldier under Malcolm — mirrors the moral trajectory of Scotland itself.

## Dramatic Function

Angus serves a critical structural role that individual analysis risks overlooking. He is part of a **collective of Scottish thanes** — alongside Ross, Lennox, Menteith, and Caithness — whose gradual defection from Macbeth maps the collapse of political legitimacy. Each defection is small; together they are decisive. Angus's particular contribution is his early presence: he is there when Macbeth is honored, and there when Macbeth is destroyed, giving the audience a fixed point against which to measure the catastrophe.
