# Scone

Scone is a town in Perthshire, Scotland, historically the site where Scottish monarchs were crowned upon the Stone of Scone. In *Macbeth*, it appears as the location where Macbeth is invested as king, and where Malcolm is ultimately crowned, bookending the play's movement from legitimate rule through tyranny back to legitimacy.

## Geographic Context

Scone Abbey, near Perth in central Scotland, was the traditional coronation site of Scottish kings. In the play, Macbeth's hasty departure "to Scone to be invested" (011-014) occurs immediately after Duncan's murder, contrasting with the proper ceremonial investiture of Malcolm at the play's conclusion, where he invites the thanes "to see us crowned at Scone" (028-028).

## Symbolic Significance

Scone represents the legitimizing power of ceremony in the transfer of kingship. Macbeth's rushed journey to Scone -- reported rather than staged -- suggests a coronation born of haste

> He is already nam'd; and gone to Scone
> To be invested.
>
> (011-014)
 and usurpation rather than proper succession. Ross's question "Will you to Scone?" and Macduff's pointed refusal (011-017) establish Scone as a test of political allegiance: attendance at the coronation signifies acceptance of Macbeth's rule.

Malcolm's invocation of Scone in the play's final lines (028-028) reclaims the site for legitimate kingship. The circular return to Scone -- first mentioned as Macbeth's hasty destination, last mentioned as Malcolm's ceremonial invitation -- frames the entire arc of the tragedy as a deviation from and restoration of rightful order.
