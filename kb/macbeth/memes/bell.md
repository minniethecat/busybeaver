# Bell

The bell in *Macbeth* — the sound that summons Duncan to his death, the alarum that signals the kingdom's collapse, and the knell that tolls for every character who falls.

## Definition & Context

Bells ring at every critical juncture in *Macbeth*. Lady Macbeth strikes a bell to signal that Duncan's grooms are drugged — "the bell invites me," Macbeth says, and goes to commit the murder (008-050). The Porter imagines himself the gatekeeper of hell, answering the "knocking" that follows. Macduff discovers the body and cries "Ring the alarum bell! — Murder and treason!" (010-034). And at the end, Macbeth, besieged in Dunsinane, orders: "Ring the alarum bell! — Blow, wind! come, wrack!" (025-015). In Jacobean culture, bells marked the passage of souls from this world to the next — the "passing bell" was rung at the moment of death. Shakespeare makes the bell the play's central acoustic image: the sound of death, alarm, and final reckoning.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> She strike upon the bell. Get thee to bed. (008-018)

Lady Macbeth's instruction is chilling in its domesticity. The bell she will strike is not an alarm but a signal — the all-clear for murder. The verb "strike" is itself violent: she will strike the bell as her husband strikes the king.

> I go, and it is done. The bell invites me. / Hear it not, Duncan; for it is a knell / That summons thee to heaven or to hell. (008-050)

Macbeth's response to the bell is among his most famous lines. The bell "invites" — a social verb, almost polite — but the invitation is to murder. His address to the dead Duncan ("Hear it not") imagines the bell as a passing bell, ringing the king's soul out of this world.

### Deepening (Act III)

> Ring the alarum bell. — Murder and treason! (010-034)

Macduff's cry upon discovering Duncan's body transforms the bell from a private signal to a public alarm. The "alarum bell" is the sound of the kingdom in crisis — the body politic ringing its own death.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> Ring the alarum bell! — Blow, wind! come, wrack! / At least we'll die with harness on our back. (025-015)

Macbeth's final command, as Birnam Wood approaches Dunsinane, echoes Macduff's cry from Act II. But where Macduff rang the bell for justice, Macbeth rings it for defiance. The bell has come full circle — from the signal for murder to the signal for the murderer's last stand.

## Bell and Knocking: The Acoustic Architecture of Act II

Act II of *Macbeth* is structured around a pair of sounds that function as moral boundaries: the bell and the knocking. Their sequence maps the arc of the murder:

| Sound | Agent | Timing | Meaning |
|-------|-------|--------|---------|
| The Bell | Lady Macbeth | Before the murder | Signal: the way is clear. "The bell invites me" |
| Silence | — | During the murder | The act itself is wordless — Duncan never speaks on stage again |
| The Knocking | Macduff (eventually) | After the murder | Judgment: the outside world demands entry to a kingdom turned hell |

Between the bell and the knocking sits the Porter scene — the play's sole moment of comic relief — in which the Porter imagines himself as the gatekeeper of hell, admitting sinners of every profession. The comic interlude is not relief but commentary: the bell opened the gates of hell, and now the knocking demands they be answered.

### The Fatal Bellman

Lady Macbeth, hearing the owl's shriek during the murder, declares: "It was the owl that shriek'd, the fatal bellman, / Which gives the stern'st good-night" (008-024). The "fatal bellman" was the officer who rang the passing bell for condemned prisoners on the night before execution. By calling the owl the fatal bellman, Lady Macbeth conflates the natural world (owl) with the judicial (bellman) and the supernatural (fatality). The bell she herself struck becomes, in this re-framing, the bell of legitimate execution rather than illegitimate murder — a self-deception that the play's subsequent action dismantles.

## Critical Perspectives

The bell's transformation across *Macbeth* — from private signal to public alarm to defiant last stand — has been read as the play's acoustic map of political decay. In a well-ordered kingdom, bells mark prayer times, celebrations, and the passing of souls — they are the sound of community. In Macbeth's Scotland, the bell is the sound of murder and alarm, a community shattered. The Porter scene, with its incessant knocking, is the comic counterpoint: the bell summons death, the knocking demands admission to a hell that Scotland has become.
