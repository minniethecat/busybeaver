# Minion

**Minion** in early modern English meant a favourite, darling, or chosen one—a term of affection and special regard. In *Macbeth*, the word appears at two crucial junctures and traces a reversal: from martial heroism to unnatural disorder, from the battlefield's champion to the cosmos's broken creatures.

## Definition & Context

The word "minion" derives from Middle French *mignon* (" darling, favourite"). In Shakespeare's English, it carried no pejorative weight; it simply marked someone or something as specially favoured. The semantic range ran from the divine ("minion of God") to the romantic ("minion of the king's pleasure") to the martial ("minion of valour").

## Textual Evidence

### Valour's Minion (Act I)

> Like Valour's minion, carv'd out his passage (002-004)

In the Sergeant's battle report, Macbeth is "Valour's minion"—the chosen champion of courage itself, carving a path through the rebel army. The personification of Valour as a master who favours Macbeth as his minion is a compressed figure of heroic idealisation. At this moment, before the witches speak, before ambition stirs, Macbeth is simply the best soldier in Scotland.

### Minions of Their Race (Act II)

> Beauteous and swift, the minions of their race, / Turn'd wild in nature (011-005)

After Duncan's murder, Ross reports that the king's horses—"the minions of their race," the finest and most favoured of their breed—have broken their stalls and turned wild. The word "minions" here registers the height from which they fall: these were no ordinary horses but the prized champions of their kind. Their madness mirrors the cosmic disruption set in motion by regicide.

## Thematic Significance

The two uses of "minion" mark the play's arc from order to chaos:

| Instance | Context | Worldview |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| "Valour's minion" (002-004) | Battlefield report | Ordered cosmos: valour chooses its champion; hierarchy is legible |
| "minions of their race" (011-005) | After the murder | Disordered cosmos: even nature's favourites rebel against nature |

The same word, applied first to a man and then to animals, traces the play's central movement: the collapse of distinction between the human and the bestial, the natural and the unnatural.
