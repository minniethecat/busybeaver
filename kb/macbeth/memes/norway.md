# Norway

Norway is the foreign kingdom whose invasion of Scotland sets the military stage for William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. The Norwegian assault, combined with Macdonwald's rebellion, creates the crisis from which Macbeth emerges as a hero — and begins his fateful rise.

## Overview

Norway appears only in Act I, Scene II, through the reports of the battle. Ross brings news from Fife: "Where the Norweyan banners flout the sky / And fan our people cold. / Norway himself, with terrible numbers, / Assisted by that most disloyal traitor, / The Thane of Cawdor, began a dismal conflict" (002-017). The Norwegian invasion is a formidable threat, but Macbeth and Banquo repel it.

The Norwegian king, Sweno, sues for peace after the defeat: "Sweno, the Norways' king, craves composition; / Nor would we deign him burial of his men / Till he disbursed at Saint Colme's Inch / Ten thousand dollars to our general use" (002-019). The Norwegian threat — along with the treacherous Thane of Cawdor who aided it — is decisively crushed. Duncan's response is to execute Cawdor and bestow his title upon Macbeth (002-020).

## Dramatic Significance

Norway functions as an external antagonist whose defeat establishes Macbeth's military prowess and loyalty to Duncan. The contrast between Macbeth the loyal general and Macbeth the regicide is sharpened by the Norwegian episode — he who defended Scotland from foreign invasion will himself become its tyrant.
