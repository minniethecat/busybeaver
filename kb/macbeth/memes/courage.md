# Courage

Courage in *Macbeth* is a virtue that transforms into a weapon. The play opens by establishing Macbeth as "Valour's minion" on the battlefield, yet by its end, courage has been redefined — no longer the soldier's bravery in facing an enemy, but the moral fortitude required to face oneself. Lady Macbeth's manipulation of courage as a gendered concept drives the tragedy: she frames the murder of Duncan as a test of manly resolve, not a violation of loyalty.

## Definition & Context

In Shakespeare's Jacobean context, courage was both a martial virtue essential to the warrior-aristocrat and a Christian virtue of endurance. *Macbeth* systematically dismantles the distinction between these two forms: battlefield courage becomes indistinguishable from savagery, and moral courage is wielded as a rhetorical club. The word "courage" and its cognates appear at key moments where characters must decide between right action and self-interest. The play ultimately suggests that true courage lies not in the ability to kill, but in the ability to resist evil — a test Macbeth fails.

## Textual Evidence

### Establishing Courage: The Battlefield (Acts I–II)

> Who, like a good and hardy soldier, fought / 'Gainst my captivity. ((002-003))

The bleeding Captain's report establishes Macbeth as the embodiment of martial courage. The word "hardy" links courage to the masculine ideal of the warrior. Yet even here, the violence is unsettling — Macbeth "unseam'd" Macdonwald "from the nave to the chaps."

> But screw your courage to the sticking-place, / And we'll not fail. ((007-011))

Lady Macbeth's most famous manipulation transforms courage from a virtue into a demand. The metaphor "screw your courage to the sticking-place" — drawn from the tightening of a crossbow string — recasts courage as mechanical tension: wind yourself tight enough and you can kill. This line is the pivot where courage shifts from battlefield honour to chamber murder.

### Courage Tested and Transformed (Acts IV–V)

> Devotion, patience, courage, fortitude, / I have no relish of them. ((020-018))

Malcolm, testing Macduff's loyalty, falsely lists the virtues he claims to lack. The pairing of "courage" with "fortitude" — the Christian virtue of steadfast endurance — is significant. Malcolm's self-description as a coward who would "pour the sweet milk of concord into hell" is a test; Macduff's response reveals that true courage belongs to those who can bear moral horror, not inflict it.

> Why then, God's soldier be he! ((028-022))

When Old Siward learns his son died facing the enemy — "with his wounds before" — he reframes Young Siward's death as "God's soldier." The battlefield death that once defined Macbeth's courage now defines Young Siward's. The difference is moral: Young Siward fights against tyranny; Macbeth fought for personal glory that became tyranny.
