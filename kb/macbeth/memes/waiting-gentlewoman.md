The **Waiting-Gentlewoman** is an attendant to [[lady-macbeth|Lady Macbeth]] at [[dunsinane|Dunsinane]] Castle. Though unnamed, she plays a crucial role in Act V, Scene I as the first witness to Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking — the physical manifestation of [[guilt|guilt]] that reveals the psychological destruction wrought by [[duncan|Duncan]]'s murder.

## Role in the Sleepwalking Scene

The Gentlewoman appears only in the sleepwalking scene (Act V, Scene I), where she summons the [[scottish-doctor|Scottish Doctor]] to observe Lady Macbeth's nocturnal behaviour. She has watched for two nights and provides the Doctor with a detailed account of Lady Macbeth's actions:

> (021-003) Since his Majesty went into the field, I have seen her rise from her bed, throw her nightgown upon her, unlock her closet, take forth paper, fold it, write upon't, read it, afterwards seal it, and again return to bed; yet all this while in a most fast sleep.

Her testimony is the play's only source of information about Lady Macbeth's private torment, bridging the gap between the audience and the haunted queen's unseen deterioration.

## Moral Discretion

The Gentlewoman demonstrates notable moral discernment. Despite witnessing Lady Macbeth's confession-like sleepwalking, she refuses to repeat what she has heard to the Doctor, understanding both the gravity of the secrets revealed and her own vulnerability as a servant without corroborating witnesses:

> (021-005) That, sir, which I will not report after her.

When pressed by the Doctor, she maintains her refusal: "Neither to you nor anyone; having no witness to confirm my speech" (021-007). This discretion marks her as a character of integrity within a court defined by complicity and fear. In a castle where [[macbeth|Macbeth]]'s spies watch every thane, the Gentlewoman's silence is both a survival strategy and a moral choice — she refuses to traffic in the secrets of the damned.

## Dramatic Function

The Gentlewoman serves as the audience's surrogate — she introduces the Doctor (and the audience) to the spectacle of Lady Macbeth's psychological disintegration. Her observations establish the clinical framing of the scene: Lady Macbeth's eyes are open but "their sense are shut" (021-011). She also notes the compulsive hand-washing gesture that has become habitual — "It is an accustomed action with her, to seem thus washing her hands. I have known her continue in this a quarter of an hour" (021-013) — directly anticipating Lady Macbeth's famous "Out, damned spot" speech.

## The Witness as Moral Compass

The Gentlewoman's role illuminates a recurring pattern in the play: minor, unnamed figures who serve as moral witnesses to the corruption of the great. Like the [[old-man|Old Man]] who catalogues the cosmic disorder following [[duncan|Duncan]]'s murder, and the [[scottish-doctor|Scottish Doctor]] who diagnoses a sickness beyond physic, the Gentlewoman occupies the perspective of ordinary humanity observing the self-destruction of the powerful. Her refusal to speak what she has heard — "having no witness to confirm my speech" (021-007) — is both a legal instinct (the need for corroboration) and a moral one: the crimes she has overheard are too grave to be repeated without proof.

Her situation also reflects the gendered dynamics of service in the Macbeth household. As a woman attending on a queen, she has intimate access to [[lady-macbeth|Lady Macbeth]]'s private chambers and private suffering — access no male character, not even [[macbeth|Macbeth]] himself, possesses. The sleepwalking scene is structured around the paradox that the Gentlewoman knows more about Lady Macbeth's guilt than anyone, yet is the least empowered to act on that knowledge. Her only recourse is to summon the Doctor — a male authority figure — and even then, she carefully controls what she reveals.
