# Battle of Dunsinane

The Battle of Dunsinane is the climactic military engagement of *Macbeth*, spanning the final seven scenes of Act V. It pits Malcolm's rebel Scottish-English coalition against Macbeth's depleted garrison at Dunsinane Castle, culminating in Macduff's single combat with Macbeth and the restoration of Duncan's line to the Scottish throne.

## Prelude: The Gathering Storm (Act V Sc ii)

The rebel forces assemble in the countryside near Dunsinane, their cause galvanized by Macbeth's mounting atrocities. Menteith reports that "the confident tyrant / Keeps still in Dunsinane, and will endure / Our setting down before't" (024-008), framing the tactical situation: Macbeth has chosen to make his stand within the fortress rather than meet the rebels in open field. Caithness observes that "Great Dunsinane he strongly fortifies" (022-007), indicating that Macbeth has prepared for a siege. The rebel leaders note that Macbeth's own soldiers "move only in command, / Nothing in love" (022-019) — their loyalty is conscripted, not earned, weakening his defensive position from within.

## Macbeth's Defiant Stand (Act V Sc iii)

Inside Dunsinane, Macbeth alternates between fatalistic despair and desperate confidence. He clings to the Witches' prophecies, particularly the protection promised by the Second and Third Apparitions — that no man of woman born can harm him and that he cannot be defeated until Birnam Wood moves. When a servant reports ten thousand English soldiers approaching, Macbeth demands Seyton bring his armour: "I'll fight, till from my bones my flesh be hack'd. / Give me my armour" (023-012). The Doctor's aside — "Were I from Dunsinane away and clear, / Profit again should hardly draw me here" (023-035) — reveals the hollowed morale within Macbeth's own walls.

## The Wood Moves (Act V Sc iv–v)

At Birnam Wood, Siward issues the order for each soldier to "hew him down a bough / And bear't before him" (024-006), creating the optical illusion of a marching forest that fulfills the Third Apparition's prophecy. Inside Dunsinane, the Messenger's report that "the wood began to move" (025-012) shatters Macbeth's confidence, driving him to a final existential reckoning: the "Tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow" soliloquy (025-016), provoked by word of Lady Macbeth's death amid the siege.

## The Battle Joined (Act V Sc vi–vii)

Malcolm orders the army to "cast / Your leavy screens down, and show like those you are" (026-001) as they reach the plain before Dunsinane, abandoning their camouflage for direct assault. In the ensuing combat, Macbeth kills Young Siward, taking grim satisfaction that the boy "wast born of woman" (027-009), confirming (he believes) his supernatural invulnerability. Siward receives word of his son's death with stoic military honour: "He parted well, and paid his score. / And so, God be with him!" (027-017). Macduff, however, moves through the battlefield with a singular purpose: "If thou be'st slain, and with no stroke of mine, / My wife and children's ghosts will haunt me still" (027-021).

## The Climax: Macbeth vs. Macduff (Act V Sc viii)

The battle's decisive moment comes when Macduff confronts Macbeth. Macbeth, still confident in the prophecy, warns: "I bear a charmed life, which must not yield / To one of woman born" (028-004). Macduff's devastating reply strips away Macbeth's last supernatural protection:

> Macduff was from his mother's womb
> Untimely ripp'd.
>
> (028-008)

Macbeth's last supernatural protection gone Refusing to "yield, to kiss the ground before young Malcolm's feet" (028-016), Macbeth fights and falls. Macduff returns bearing "the usurper's cursed head" (028-018), and Malcolm is hailed as King of Scotland.
