# Trouble

In *Macbeth*, "trouble" operates on two registers: the supernatural trouble brewed by the witches in their cauldron, and the psychological trouble that infects guilty minds. The word threads through the play from Duncan's welcome to the Doctor's diagnosis of Lady Macbeth, tracing the arc from courtesy to catastrophe.

## Definition & Context

Early modern "trouble" carried the double sense it still holds: external disturbance and internal distress. Shakespeare exploits both. When Duncan tells Macbeth that "what follows us sometime is our trouble, / Which still we thank as love" he uses the word as polite courtly formula. By Act V, the Doctor confronts a Lady Macbeth whose "thick-coming fancies" are "troubled" beyond any physician's cure. The word's journey from social pleasantry to clinical symptom measures the play's descent.

## Textual Evidence

### Courtly Trouble (Acts I–II)

> That which hath made them drunk hath made me bold. / What hath quench'd them hath given me fire. (008-002)

Early uses of "trouble" are woven into the fabric of courtly exchange — Duncan speaks of "trouble" as the burden of hospitality. But the word already carries an undertow: the "joyful trouble" Macduff speaks of upon discovering Duncan's murder is a phrase that collapses under its own irony.

### The Witches' Formula (Act IV)

> ALL. Double, double toil and trouble; / Fire, burn; and cauldron, bubble. (018-006)

The witches make "trouble" their product. Their refrain (repeated three times: 018-006, 018-008, 018-010) frames trouble as something *manufactured* — a "charm of powerful trouble" brewed like "a hell-broth." The witches have industrialised malevolence.

### Psychological Trouble (Act V)

> Unnatural deeds / Do breed unnatural troubles: infected minds / To their deaf pillows will discharge their secrets. (021-033)

The Doctor names the mechanism: "unnatural deeds" (regicide) breed "unnatural troubles" (psychological torment). The body speaks what the mind cannot confess — Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking is trouble made visible.

> This disease is beyond my practice. (023-028)

The Doctor's verdict on Lady Macbeth's "troubled" mind is clinical defeat. Trouble has moved from metaphorical to medical, and medicine cannot help. "More needs she the divine than the physician" (023-029) — trouble has become a spiritual condition.
