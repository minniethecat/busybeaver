# Guilt

Guilt is the psychological engine of *Macbeth*. Unlike revenge tragedy, where the protagonist is wronged and seeks justice, or ambition tragedy, where the protagonist is punished by external forces, *Macbeth* locates the punishment for crime inside the criminal's own mind. From Macbeth's pre-murder vision of the dagger to Lady Macbeth's post-murder sleepwalking, Shakespeare presents guilt not as an abstract moral category but as a physical force — something that produces hallucinations, breaks sleep, and finally destroys the self.

## Anticipatory Guilt: The Conscience Before the Crime

One of the play's most distinctive features is that guilt operates *before* the murder as powerfully as after it. Macbeth's soliloquies in Act I are not the calculations of an amoral schemer but the wrestlings of a conscience in crisis.

### "If it were done when 'tis done" (Act I, Scene VII)

Macbeth's first soliloquy is a meditation on consequences that functions as an anatomy of anticipatory guilt:

> If it were done when 'tis done, then 'twere well / It were done quickly. (007-001)

The sentence cannot complete itself — the conditional "if" loops without reaching a main clause, performing in syntax what guilt does in the mind: it prevents resolution. He catalogues the reasons against the murder — Duncan is his kinsman, his king, his guest — and each reason is a thread of the "golden opinions" (007-008) he will lose.

Most tellingly, he recognises that the murder will not end with the act:

> We'd jump the life to come. (007-002)

"We'd jump" means "we'd risk" — Macbeth knows that killing Duncan risks not only earthly judgment but eternal damnation. This is guilt operating as theological fear: the "life to come" will hold him to account.

### "His virtues / Will plead like angels" (Act I, Scene VII)

Macbeth imagines Duncan's virtues as celestial prosecutors:

> His virtues / Will plead like angels, trumpet-tongued, against / The deep damnation of his taking-off. (007-006)

The image reverses the expected moral physics: the victim will not be silent but will testify. Duncan's blood, like Abel's in Genesis, will cry out from the ground. Macbeth's guilt has a witness before the crime is even committed.

## Guilt Embodied: Blood and Hands (Act II, Scene II)

After the murder, guilt ceases to be an internal state and becomes a physical reality. The body registers what the mind cannot process.

### "This is a sorry sight"

Returning from Duncan's chamber, Macbeth looks at his hands:

> This is a sorry sight. (009-013)

The line is almost absurdly inadequate — a vast understatement that measures the distance between what Macbeth has done and what he can say about it. Lady Macbeth's response — "A foolish thought, to say a sorry sight" (009-015) — tries to force guilt back into the category of mere thought. But guilt has already escaped that category.

### The Stuck "Amen"

When the grooms cried "God bless us," Macbeth could not answer:

> But wherefore could not I pronounce "Amen"? / I had most need of blessing, and "Amen" / Stuck in my throat. (009-020)

The inability to say "Amen" is the play's most economical image of guilt. It is not a thought or a feeling but a physical blockage — a word that will not come. The throat, the organ of speech and breath, becomes the site where guilt lodges. This prefigures Lady Macbeth's "damned spot": guilt is something that sticks.

### Lady Macbeth's Premature Confidence

Lady Macbeth's response to the murder is practical to the point of blindness:

> A little water clears us of this deed. (009-030)

And later, as she smears the grooms with blood:

> For it must seem their guilt. (009-027)

The line is devastatingly ironic. "It must *seem* their guilt" — but guilt is precisely what cannot be managed by appearances. The word "guilt" enters the play at this moment, and Lady Macbeth uses it as if it were a stage property to be arranged. The rest of the play is her education in how wrong she is.

## The Spreading Stain (Acts III–V)

After Act II, the word "guilt" largely disappears from the play's vocabulary — but guilt itself does not. It transforms from a named thing into a pervasive condition.

### Banquo's Ghost

At the feast, Macbeth sees Banquo's ghost — a hallucination that is guilt made visible:

> Thou canst not say I did it. Never shake / Thy gory locks at me. (016-026)

The ghost sits in Macbeth's seat, occupying the place of honour. Guilt usurps the position Macbeth usurped. No one else sees the ghost — it is a private manifestation, a guilt that isolates rather than connects.

### The Descent into Numbness

By Act V, Macbeth has ceased to feel guilt as acute pain. Instead, he has arrived at a kind of moral anaesthesia:

> I have almost forgot the taste of fears. (025-003)

This is not redemption but exhaustion. The capacity for guilt, like the capacity for sleep, has been burned out. When Lady Macbeth dies, he can only say, "She should have died hereafter" (025-008) — no grief, no guilt, only the flat acknowledgement that timing is the only thing that matters.

## Lady Macbeth's Guilt: The Body Remembers

The play's fullest exploration of guilt is Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking scene (Act V, Scene I). Where Macbeth's guilt numbs him, hers possesses her.

### The Hand-Washing

The Doctor and Gentlewoman watch as Lady Macbeth compulsively rubs her hands in her sleep:

> It is an accustomed action with her, to seem thus washing her hands. I have known her continue in this a quarter of an hour. (021-013)

This is guilt as repetition compulsion. The action is not voluntary — it is "accustomed," automatic. The body remembers the crime that the waking mind has suppressed. For "a quarter of an hour" — a specificity that makes the ritual almost clinical — she tries to wash away what cannot be washed.

### "Out, damned spot!"

Her speech in the sleepwalking scene is a collage of the play's murder scenes, guilt compressing time so that past and present collapse:

> Out, damned spot! out, I say! One; two. Why, then 'tis time to do't. (021-016)

She counts — "One; two" — as if the spot were a signal, a drumbeat, the knocking at the gate. Then she flashes to the moment of resolution: "'tis time to do't." The murder is not in the past; for her sleeping mind, it is always about to happen.

> Yet who would have thought the old man to have had so much blood in him? (021-016)

This is the line that undoes her Act II confidence. "A little water clears us of this deed" is answered by the sheer excess of blood — "so much blood." Guilt is not proportional; it overwhelms.

### "All the perfumes of Arabia"

Her final image is olfactory:

> Here's the smell of the blood still: all the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand. (021-021)

The hand is "little" — the diminutive that she used confidently in Act II ("this little hand") — but "little" no longer means manageable. It means that even the smallest part of the body can carry a stain that all the perfumes of the world cannot cover.

She completes the reversal of her own logic:

| Act II Confidence | Act V Confession |
|---|---|
| "A little water clears us of this deed" (009-030) | "What, will these hands ne'er be clean?" (021-018) |
| "My hands are of your color, but I shame / To wear a heart so white" (009-030) | "All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand" (021-021) |
| "These deeds must not be thought / After these ways; so, it will make us mad" (009-021) | She has gone mad. |

### The Doctor's Diagnosis

The Doctor's response marks the limit of what medicine can address:

> This disease is beyond my practice. (021-026)
>
> More needs she the divine than the physician. (021-034)

Guilt, the play insists, is not psychological in the modern sense — it is moral and theological. It requires a "divine," not a doctor. This is the play's final word on the subject: guilt is real, it is inescapable, and it destroys those who thought they could manage it.

## Macbeth's Final Position

Macbeth's last encounter with guilt is his refusal to face Macduff:

> My soul is too much charg'd / With blood of thine already. (028-015)

He will not add Macduff's blood to the "charge" — the word is legal and financial, as if guilt were a debt that cannot be increased. He fights and dies not because he seeks redemption but because he has exhausted even the capacity for guilt.
