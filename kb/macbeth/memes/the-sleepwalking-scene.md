# The Sleepwalking Scene

**The Sleepwalking Scene** (Act V, Scene I) is one of the most psychologically intense moments in Shakespeare. [[lady-macbeth|Lady Macbeth]], once the steely architect of Duncan's murder, is observed by a Doctor and Gentlewoman as she walks in her sleep, compulsively washing imaginary blood from her hands and confessing to crimes her waking self has suppressed. The scene's power lies not in action but in revelation: the mind that commanded "a little water clears us of this deed" (009-025) has been destroyed by what water cannot wash away.

## Dramatic Structure

### The Opening Exchange

The scene opens with the Doctor and Gentlewoman discussing Lady Macbeth's condition. The Gentlewoman reports that she has "seen her rise from her bed, throw her nightgown upon her, unlock her closet, take forth paper, fold it, write upon it, read it, afterwards seal it, and again return to bed; yet all this while in a most fast sleep" — but refuses to repeat what Lady Macbeth has said, knowing the gravity of her words.

### The Entrance

Lady Macbeth enters carrying a taper — she "has light by her continually; 'tis her command" — a gesture of profound psychological significance. The woman who once called on "thick night" (005-020) to conceal her deeds now cannot bear darkness.

### The Confession

In her trance, Lady Macbeth revisits the murders she helped orchestrate. She addresses the imagined bloodstain:

>Out, damned spot! out, I say! — One: two: why, then 'tis time to do't. — Hell is murky! (021-016)

She moves between Duncan's murder ("Yet who would have thought the old man to have had so much blood in him?") and the murder of [[macduff|Macduff]]'s family ("The thane of Fife had a wife: where is she now?"), her fragmented speech mapping the trail of blood she cannot escape.

### The Hand-Washing Gesture

The scene's defining visual motif is Lady Macbeth's compulsive hand-washing:

>Look how she rubs her hands. (021-012)
>
>It is an accustomed action with her, to seem thus washing her hands. I have known her continue in this a quarter of an hour. (021-013)

Her cry — "What, will these hands ne'er be clean?" (021-018) — and the devastating "All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand" (021-021) — is the tragic inversion of her earlier confidence that "a little water clears us of this deed." (009-025)

## Psychological Significance

The sleepwalking scene exposes the central hypocrisy of Lady Macbeth's character. She had assured Macbeth that "the sleeping and the dead are but as pictures" (009-011) — yet it is sleep itself that undoes her. The repression she enforced while awake collapses in sleep, proving that the mind cannot be commanded as easily as a husband. The Doctor's diagnosis is clinical and chilling:

>This disease is beyond my practice... More needs she the divine than the physician. [021-023, 021-032]

The scene diagnoses guilt not as a moral abstraction but as a **physical pathology** — something that manifests in the body, in gesture, in the compulsive repetition of traumatic memory.

## Thematic Resonance

The sleepwalking scene brings the play's [[hand]] motif to its tragic climax. Hands that once seized a crown now cannot be cleaned. The [[darkness]] that was Lady Macbeth's ally has become her torment. And the [[nature|natural order]] she violated — "unsex me here" (005-014) — has reasserted itself in the most intimate way: the woman who would not be woman is destroyed by her own body's testimony.
