# Death

Death permeates *Macbeth* from its opening battle report to its final duel. Unlike a revenge tragedy where death is the climax, Shakespeare makes death the play's constant companion: it is threatened, contemplated, enacted, and suffered at every turn. The play examines death not merely as an event but as an idea — what it means to cause death, to fear death, and to become so familiar with death that one loses the capacity to fear it.

## Definition & Context

In the Jacobean imagination, death carried both theological weight (the soul's judgment) and political significance (the king's body as the body politic). The murder of a king — regicide — was the gravest crime imaginable, a violation of divine order as much as human law. Macbeth's killing of Duncan is therefore not one death among many; it is a metaphysical rupture that disorders the natural world. From that first murder, death multiplies through the play: Banquo, Lady Macduff, her children, young Siward, and finally Macbeth himself. Each death diminishes the killer as much as the killed.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> DUNCAN. Go pronounce his present death, And with his former title greet Macbeth.

The play's first death sentence — Duncan ordering the execution of the traitorous Thane of Cawdor — establishes a brutal symmetry: death as justice becomes death as usurpation when Macbeth, the new Thane of Cawdor, commits the same treason of which his predecessor was guilty.

The battlefield is soaked in blood from the opening: "Enter King Duncan, Malcolm, Donalbain, Lennox, with Attendants, meeting a bleeding Captain" (002-001). Death enters the play not as an abstraction but as a bleeding body — the Captain who reports Macbeth's valor is himself near death, establishing the play's visual economy of blood and wounds.

> MACBETH. If it were done when 'tis done, then 'twere well It were done quickly.

Macbeth's first soliloquy on murder frames death as a hoped-for finality. He wishes that the act could be "the be-all and the end-all" — that death could be a clean closure rather than the beginning of endless consequences. This wish is the play's central delusion: death in *Macbeth* is never an ending, only a transformation.

Lady Macbeth invokes death's instruments before the murder: "That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here" (005-011). She calls on spirits to strip her of the feminine qualities that might prevent her from participating in death-dealing. The word "mortal" here carries its double sense — both "deadly" and "human" — linking death inextricably to the renunciation of ordinary humanity.

### Deepening (Act III)

> MACBETH. I am in blood Stepp'd in so far that, should I wade no more, Returning were as tedious as go o'er.

Macbeth's metaphor of wading through blood describes his relationship to death: each murder makes the next easier, not harder. Death has become a habit rather than a horror. His language suggests not guilt but weariness — he has killed so much that stopping would require as much effort as continuing. "For them the gracious Duncan have I murdered" (012-045) — the word "gracious" applied to the murdered king underscores the moral weight of regicide.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> MACBETH. She should have died hereafter; There would have been a time for such a word.

Upon hearing of Lady Macbeth's death, Macbeth delivers the play's most famous meditation on mortality. "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow" reduces life to "a tale told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, signifying nothing." Having caused so many deaths, Macbeth can no longer find meaning in any of them — not even his wife's. The bell that summoned Duncan to heaven or to hell (008-051) finds its echo in this ultimate nihilism.

## The Sleep-Death Parallel

> Death's counterfeit, the sleep that follows murder, recurs throughout the play.

The Porter describes the knocking at the gate as waking from "death's counterfeit" — "Shake off this downy sleep, death's counterfeit" (010-036). The alignment of sleep with death is structural: both Duncan and Lady Macbeth are discovered in states that blur the boundary. Duncan's sleeping body is mistaken for a living one until the murder is discovered; Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking body is mistaken for a dead one until she speaks. In the play's symbolic order, sleep and death are variations of the same state — the body present, the animating consciousness withdrawn.

## Character Associations

| Character | Relationship to Death | Key Scene |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------|
| Macbeth | Moves from fearing death's consequences to being unable to fear death itself; his moral numbness is the play's most complete portrait of spiritual death. | Act V |
| Lady Macbeth | Dies offstage, apparently by suicide; her death is the final evidence that the "illness" she once summoned has consumed her. | Act V |
| Duncan | The first murder; his death is the original sin from which all subsequent violence flows. | Act II, Scene II |
| Banquo | Murdered on Macbeth's order; his ghost returns to haunt the feast, proving that death in *Macbeth* is not the end of presence. | Act III, Scene III–IV |
