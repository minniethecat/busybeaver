The **Old Man** appears in a single scene — Act II, Scene IV — but his brief presence carries significant symbolic weight. Speaking with [[ross|Ross]] outside Macbeth's castle in the aftermath of [[duncan|Duncan]]'s murder, he gives voice to the play's central theme of cosmic disorder: unnatural human deeds produce unnatural disturbances in the natural world.

## Witness to Disorder

The Old Man's opening words establish his authority as a witness of long experience against which the present horrors can be measured:

> (011-002) Threescore and ten I can remember well, / Within the volume of which time I have seen / Hours dreadful and things strange, but this sore night / Hath trifled former knowings.

His seventy years of memory provide a historical baseline — in all his long life, nothing has equalled the unnatural darkness and disorder of the night of Duncan's murder. [[ross|Ross]] reinforces this, observing that though the clock shows day, "dark night strangles the travelling lamp" (011-003).

## The Catalogue of Portents

The Old Man contributes to the play's most concentrated passage of unnatural imagery by reporting specific violations of the natural order:

> (011-004) 'Tis unnatural, / Even like the deed that's done. On Tuesday last, / A falcon, towering in her pride of place, / Was by a mousing owl hawk'd at and kill'd.

The falcon — a royal bird, symbol of majesty — killed by a mousing owl (a lowly night-hunter) mirrors Duncan's murder: the noble king slain by his subordinate. When Ross reports that Duncan's horses "Turn'd wild in nature, broke their stalls" and "Contending 'gainst obedience" (011-005), the Old Man provides the grim climax:

> (011-006) 'Tis said they eat each other.

This image of cannibalistic horses — creatures of service turning against their own kind — completes the portrait of a world where all natural hierarchies, from the heavens to the stables, have been inverted by [[regicide|regicide]].

## Dramatic Function

The Old Man serves as a choric figure, common in Shakespearean tragedy, who articulates the moral significance of events that the main characters are too implicated to name. His namelessness — he is simply "Old Man" — universalises his perspective: he speaks not for a faction but for the natural and moral order itself. His scene with Ross bridges the intimate horror of the murder (Act II, Scene ii–iii) and the political consequences that unfold as [[macduff|Macduff]] and Ross discuss Macbeth's succession.

## The Great Chain of Being

The Old Man's catalogue of portents draws on the Elizabethan doctrine of the Great Chain of Being — the hierarchical order that links God, angels, humanity, animals, and inanimate matter in a single unbroken chain. [[regicide|Regicide]] — the killing of a [[duncan|divinely anointed king]] — is not merely a political crime but a cosmic rupture that propagates through every link of the chain. The falcon (high) falling to the owl (low), the horses (domesticated servants) eating each other (becoming predators), and the daylight itself failing (the heavens disrupted) together trace a single shock from the murder of the king outward through the entire created order.

This cosmological framework gives the Old Man's testimony a weight beyond mere superstition. His seventy years provide empirical corroboration — "I have seen / Hours dreadful and things strange" — and his civic identity as a commoner (he is the play's only named figure who is neither noble nor servant) grounds the cosmic disorder in ordinary human experience. When a man who has lived seventy years says the world has never been like this, the play signals that Macbeth's crime has broken something fundamental in the fabric of reality.
