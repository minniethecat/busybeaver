# Equivocation

Equivocation — the practice of speaking truth in a way that deceives — is one of *Macbeth*'s most pervasive themes. It operates at every level of the play: as comic wordplay in the Porter scene, as the structural mechanism of the Witches' prophecies, and as the moral logic of Macbeth's self-destruction.

## The Porter's Comic Equivocation

The term enters the play explicitly through the Porter in Act II, Scene III. Knocking within after Duncan's murder (010-001), the Porter — listed among the *Dramatis Personae* as "(P01-020) A Porter" — stumbles to open the gate (010-003). As he does, he imagines himself as the gatekeeper of hell, admitting a procession of damned souls, including an equivocator who "could swear in both the scales against either scale, who committed treason enough for God's sake, yet could not equivocate to heaven."

This is a topical reference to the Jesuit priest Henry Garnet, executed in 1606 for his role in the Gunpowder Plot

> Faith, here's an equivocator, that could swear in both the scales against
> either scale, who committed treason enough for God's sake, yet could not
> equivocate to heaven: O, come in, equivocator.
>
> (010-002)
 — Garnet defended the doctrine of equivocation (mental reservation) as a way for Catholics to answer under oath without technically lying. Shakespeare weaves this contemporary scandal into the fabric of the play, using the Porter's drunken comedy to establish equivocation as a key that unlocks the tragedy's deeper structure.

### The Gunpowder Plot Context

The reference to Garnet is not incidental. The Gunpowder Plot (November 1605) was the terrorist event of Shakespeare's age — a Catholic conspiracy to blow up Parliament and King James I. Garnet, the superior of the English Jesuits, was tried and executed in March 1606 for knowing of the plot through confession and failing to disclose it. At his trial, the doctrine of equivocation — the casuistic principle that one could truthfully deny knowledge under oath by reserving a mental qualification — became a national scandal. *Macbeth*, likely first performed in summer 1606 before King James, embeds this contemporary trauma in its architecture. The Porter's "equivocator" is a precise political reference, and the play's interest in language that is technically true but practically false is a sustained meditation on the crisis of truth-telling that the Plot produced.

## The Witches' Structural Equivocation

The [[the-weird-sisters|Weird Sisters]] are the play's master equivocators. Their prophecies are technically true — [[macbeth]] does become king — but the truths they tell always hide the fatal catch. The play opens with the witches themselves (001-001), immediately establishing the supernatural machinery that will drive the equivocal logic.

The [[apparitions]] in Act IV are the purest expression of this pattern: the armed head warns of Macduff, the bloody child declares that "none of woman born shall harm Macbeth" — which means Macduff, delivered by Caesarean section — and the crowned child with a tree promises safety until Birnam Wood comes to Dunsinane, which means soldiers carrying branches as camouflage.

Each prophecy is literally accurate but fatally misleading. The apparitions themselves are staged as spectacular equivocations: an armed Head rises (018-026), a bloody Child rises (018-033), a Child crowned with a tree in his hand rises (018-039). Each apparition embodies a truth that, understood wrongly, will destroy the man who trusts it.

## Macbeth's Self-Equivocation

By the play's end, Macbeth has internalized the logic of equivocation. He tells [[macduff]] that he bears "a charmed life, which must not yield to one of woman born," still believing the prophecy protects him. When Macduff reveals his Caesarean birth, Macbeth's response captures the tragedy of equivocation perfectly: "And be these juggling fiends no more believed, that palter with us in a double sense, that keep the word of promise to our ear, and break it to our hope."

### "Palter with us in a double sense"

Macbeth's final diagnosis of the witches' method — "palter with us in a double sense" — is one of the play's most important lines for understanding its theory of language. "Palter" means to equivocate, to shift, to deal evasively. But the phrase "double sense" names the structural principle: equivocation is not lying (which would be single and false) but doubleness — a statement that carries two meanings, one true and one fatally incomplete.

This doubleness has pervaded the play from its opening scene. The witches' chant — "Fair is foul and foul is fair" — is the template: a statement whose truth depends entirely on which meaning of "fair" and "foul" one takes. The witches do not lie; they speak a language in which truth and falsehood occupy the same words. Macbeth's tragedy is that he learns this too late.

## The Hell-Gate Imagery

The Porter's drunken fantasy of being "the gatekeeper of hell" connects equivocation to damnation. Lady Macbeth calls on "the dunnest smoke of hell" (005-021) to conceal her intentions, and Macbeth hears the bell that "summons thee to heaven or to hell" (008-052). The play links deceptive language to spiritual peril: to equivocate is to occupy hell's threshold, neither fully damned nor saved, a linguistic limbo that the Porter's scene makes both comic and terrifying. The Porter's gate (010-003) opens onto a world where language no longer reliably connects to truth — the world Macbeth's crime has created.

## Character Significance

Equivocation is the play's master trope because it unites the political, the supernatural, and the psychological. Politically, it invokes the Gunpowder Plot and the crisis of oath-taking in Jacobean England. Supernaturally, it defines the witches' method and the deceptive mechanism of prophecy. Psychologically, it maps onto Macbeth's self-deception — his progressive inability to hear the double sense in his own promises to himself. That the Porter, the play's lowest character, is the one who names "equivocation" explicitly is Shakespeare's dark joke: wisdom about language comes from the gatekeeper of hell.
