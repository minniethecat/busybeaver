# England

England appears in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth* as both a place of refuge and a source of military deliverance. Malcolm flees there after his father's murder, and returns with an English army to overthrow the tyrant.

## Overview

After Duncan's murder, Malcolm resolves to flee to England while his brother Donalbain goes to Ireland: "I'll to England" (010-075). In England, Malcolm finds sanctuary at the court of King Edward the Confessor, described as "the most pious Edward" (017-041). A scene set at the English court (Act IV, Scene III) shows Malcolm testing Macduff's loyalty before revealing his intention to march on Scotland.

England provides more than refuge — it supplies the military force that defeats Macbeth. Malcolm secures "Siward with ten thousand men" (020-022), and the combined English and Scottish force marches through Birnam Wood to Dunsinane. King Edward's support is portrayed as righteous: "pious Edward" lends "warlike Siward" to aid the Scottish rebels against Macbeth's tyranny (020-048).

## Dramatic Significance

England represents legitimate authority and divine order in contrast to Macbeth's usurped throne. The English king is described as having the healing touch — a gift of "the most pious Edward" (017-041) — in stark contrast to Macbeth, whose touch brings only death. England's military intervention restores the natural order, returning the crown to Duncan's rightful heir.
