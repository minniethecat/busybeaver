# Ross

Ross is a Scottish thane and frequent messenger in *Macbeth*, appearing in more scenes than any character except Macbeth himself. His role as an intermediary — shuttling between camps and delivering news — makes him a crucial structural device and a figure whose shifting loyalties mirror Scotland's political trajectory.

## Dramatic Role

Ross functions as the play's principal messenger and witness. He first appears alongside Angus, reporting to Duncan on the battlefield victories — announcing that "The worthy Thane of Ross" brings news from Fife (002-011, 002-013). In Act I, Scene iii, he and Angus deliver to Macbeth the news of his elevation to Thane of Cawdor, unwittingly helping to activate the witches' prophecy (003-037, 003-044).

Ross is present for nearly every pivotal moment: he witnesses Macbeth's investiture (004-004), discovers Duncan's murder (006-002), and discusses the unnatural portents with the Old Man in Act II, Scene iv (011-001). His conversation about Duncan's horses eating each other establishes the cosmic disorder theme.

A turning point comes in Act IV, Scene ii, when Ross visits Lady Macduff in Fife and delivers the devastating news that Macduff has fled to England (019-001). Later, in England, he reports to Macduff the slaughter of his family (020-029). By Act V, Ross has joined Malcolm's forces, marching with the rebel army (024-001) and appearing in the final victory scene (028-013).

## Key Speeches

### The Norway report

In Act I, Scene ii, Ross delivers a vivid account of the battle against Norway, describing how "Norway himself, with terrible numbers, assisted by that most disloyal traitor, the Thane of Cawdor, began a dismal conflict" (002-011). His report establishes both the military context and the treason theme that will dominate the play.

### "Alas, poor country"

In Act IV, Scene iii, Ross reports to Macduff on the state of Scotland under Macbeth: "Alas, poor country, almost afraid to know itself. It cannot be called our mother, but our grave" (020-029). This speech crystallizes the play's vision of tyranny as a sickness in the body politic.

> Alas, poor country,
> Almost afraid to know itself! It cannot
> Be call'd our mother, but our grave, where nothing,
> But who knows nothing, is once seen to smile;
> Where sighs, and groans, and shrieks, that rent the air,
> Are made, not mark'd; where violent sorrow seems
> A modern ecstasy.
>
> (020-036)


## Relationships

- **[[macbeth]]** — Ross is an early admirer who delivers the news of Macbeth's elevation, later becomes a witness to his tyranny, and finally joins the rebellion
- **[[macduff]]** — Ross brings Macduff the news of his family's murder, a turning point that converts grief into vengeance (020-029)
- **[[lady-macduff]]** — Ross's visit to Lady Macduff in Fife (019-001) is their only scene together, marked by his uneasy awareness of the danger she faces
- **[[old-man]]** — Their dialogue in Act II, Scene iv catalogs the unnatural events following Duncan's murder (011-001)

## Themes

- [[order-of-nature]] — Ross's catalog of unnatural portents with the Old Man establishes the cosmic response to regicide
- [[loyalty]] — His gradual shift from Duncan's court to Macbeth's court to the rebel forces traces the dissolution of political legitimacy
- messenger — Ross embodies the messenger figure who must deliver unbearable truths
