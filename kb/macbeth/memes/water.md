# Water

In *Macbeth*, water is the element of attempted cleansing—a symbol of the purification that guilt demands but that murder makes impossible.

## Definition & Context

Water in *Macbeth* is never neutral. It appears at moments of crisis as characters attempt to wash away evidence of their crimes, both physical (blood) and spiritual (guilt). The play traces an arc from confident assertion that water can cleanse—"A little water clears us of this deed" (009-030)—to the devastating recognition that it cannot: Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking hand-washing and Macbeth's vision of a blood-stained ocean. In the Jacobean imagination, water carried both practical and sacramental associations: it was the medium of baptism (spiritual cleansing) and of daily ablution (physical cleansing). The play systematically collapses the distinction between the two.

## Textual Evidence

### The First Cleansing (Act II)

> Go get some water, / And wash this filthy witness from your hand. (009-025)

Lady Macbeth's command to her husband immediately after Duncan's murder is brisk and practical. Blood is "filthy witness"—evidence, not sin. Her confidence that water can simply remove the stain establishes her character's defining trait: the belief that practical action can master moral consequence.

> A little water clears us of this deed: / How easy is it then! (009-030)

The line is the play's most devastating irony. Lady Macbeth's "how easy is it then" will be answered across the remaining three acts: it is not easy, water does not clear the deed, and the stain spreads from hands to conscience to the entire kingdom.

### The Permanent Stain (Act V)

By Act V, the water motif has inverted. Lady Macbeth, now sleepwalking, compulsively washes hands that bear no visible blood: "Out, damned spot! out, I say!" The "little water" she once trusted has become an ocean that cannot cleanse. The Doctor observes that "unnatural deeds / Do breed unnatural troubles"—the stain has moved from the hand to the mind, where water cannot reach.

> cast / The water of my land, find her disease (023-031)

Macbeth's command to the Doctor in Act V Scene iii appropriates the water motif for political metaphor. He demands the physician diagnose Scotland as one would examine urine ("cast the water")—reducing the kingdom's moral sickness to a medical symptom. The image collapses the personal and the political: Scotland is diseased because its king is damned, and no amount of diagnostic "water" can find a cure for regicide.

## Critical Perspectives

The water motif has been read psychoanalytically as a figure for the impossibility of repression. Lady Macbeth's attempt to "wash away" Duncan's murder is an attempt to erase memory, and the return of the compulsion in Act V demonstrates that what is repressed returns in more destructive form. Feminist criticism has noted that the water/hand-washing motif is gendered: Lady Macbeth's private, domestic act of attempted cleansing contrasts with Macbeth's public, political response to guilt (further murders). Water is the feminine element of a guilt that cannot be resolved through masculine violence.
