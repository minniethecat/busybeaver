# Supernatural

The supernatural in *Macbeth* is not merely decorative atmosphere; it is the play's structural engine. The witches' prophecies set the plot in motion, Banquo's ghost shatters Macbeth's sanity at the banquet, and the apparitions of Act IV entrap him in false confidence. Shakespeare deploys the supernatural to blur the boundary between external fate and internal desire, between cosmic order and psychological derangement.

## The Witches: Agents of Fate or Mirrors of Ambition?

The play opens with the witches — "THIRD WITCH. There to meet with Macbeth" ((001-007)) — establishing them as the framing consciousness of the entire tragedy. Their first exchange with Macbeth delivers the prophecies that will drive the action:

> FIRST WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Glamis!
> SECOND WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Cawdor!
> THIRD WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! that shalt be king hereafter! ([003-018–020])

Crucially, the witches never tell Macbeth to murder Duncan. They tell him what he will become, and he — with Lady Macbeth's encouragement — supplies the means. This ambiguity is the play's central supernatural question: do the prophecies cause Macbeth's actions, or do they merely give form to ambitions he already harbors?

Banquo's response reinforces the interpretive difficulty. The witches tell Banquo he will be "Lesser than Macbeth, and greater" and "Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none" ([003-025–027]). Unlike Macbeth, Banquo does not act on this knowledge, yet it unsettles him deeply. His prayer — "Merciful powers, / Restrain in me the cursèd thoughts that nature / Gives way to in repose!" ((008-004)) — reveals that the prophecies stir even the virtuous.

## "Is This a Dagger?": Hallucination as Supernatural

Macbeth's vision of the floating dagger in Act II, Scene I occupies the boundary between supernatural apparition and psychological projection:

> MACBETH. Is this a dagger which I see before me,
> The handle toward my hand? Come, let me clutch thee:
> I have thee not, and yet I see thee still.
> Art thou not, fatal vision, sensible
> To feeling as to sight? Or art thou but
> A dagger of the mind, a false creation,
> Proceeding from the heat-oppressed brain? ((008-014))

Macbeth himself offers both interpretations: "fatal vision" (supernatural omen) or "dagger of the mind" (psychological projection). The ambiguity is never resolved: "Mine eyes are made the fools o' the other senses, / Or else worth all the rest" ((008-015)). The dagger hovers between worlds, as does the entire supernatural apparatus of the play.

## Banquo's Ghost and the Disordered Banquet

The appearance of Banquo's ghost at the coronation banquet in Act III, Scene IV is the play's most theatrical supernatural moment. Only Macbeth can see the ghost, and his terror shatters the public ceremony:

> MACBETH. Thou canst not say I did it: never shake
> Thy gory locks at me. ((015-029))

Ross's puzzled question — "What sights, my lord?" ((015-030)) — confirms that the ghost is visible to Macbeth alone. Lady Macbeth attempts damage control: "My lord is often thus, / And hath been from his youth" ((015-032)), but the banquet's collapse into chaos enacts the disintegration of Macbeth's rule. That the ghost is invisible to everyone except Macbeth frames the haunting as both real (for Macbeth) and psychological (a projection of guilt) — the same ambiguity that structures the dagger vision.

## The Apparitions of Act IV

In Act IV, Scene I, Macbeth returns to the witches demanding further knowledge. They conjure three apparitions — "An armed Head," "A bloody Child," and "A Child crowned, with a tree in his hand" — delivering equivocal prophecies:

> FIRST APPARITION. Macbeth! Macbeth! Macbeth! Beware Macduff;
> Beware the Thane of Fife. ((018-029))
>
> SECOND APPARITION. Be bloody, bold, and resolute; laugh to scorn
> The power of man, for none of woman born
> Shall harm Macbeth. ((018-036))
>
> THIRD APPARITION. Macbeth shall never vanquish'd be until
> Great Birnam wood to high Dunsinane hill
> Shall come against him. ((018-044))

Each prophecy is technically true but semantically deceptive: Macduff was "from his mother's womb / Untimely ripp'd" (Caesarean birth, not "woman born"), and Malcolm's army uses Birnam Wood branches as camouflage. The apparitions embody the supernatural's mode of operation: they tell the truth in a way designed to mislead. The scene also features Hecate, the goddess of witchcraft, whose presence elevates the supernatural from folklore to a cosmic principle.

## Cosmic Disorder: Nature's Rebellion

The supernatural in *Macbeth* extends beyond personified agents to a pervasive sense that nature itself has been disrupted. The Old Man in Act II reports unnatural phenomena:

> OLD MAN. 'Tis unnatural,
> Even like the deed that's done. On Tuesday last,
> A falcon, towering in her pride of place,
> Was by a mousing owl hawk'd at and kill'd. ((011-007))

Ross adds that Duncan's horses "Turn'd wild in nature, broke their stalls, flung out, / Contending 'gainst obedience" and "they ate each other" ((011-008)). These prodigies reflect a world where the moral order — the Great Chain of Being — has been violated by regicide. The supernatural is not merely a set of encounters with witches and ghosts; it is the texture of a universe in which Macbeth's crime has unsettled the very structure of reality.
