# Ambition

Ambition is the engine of tragedy in *Macbeth*—the quality that elevates the protagonist from loyal thane to king, then plunges him into tyranny and destruction. Shakespeare presents ambition not as simple aspiration but as a force that, unmoored from moral restraint, devours the soul that harbors it.

## Definition & Context

In Jacobean thought, ambition occupied a precarious position: noble aspiration was a virtue in service of the crown, but personal ambition—the desire for power for power's sake—was a vice, bordering on the sin of *superbia* (pride). Lady Macbeth identifies her husband's fatal distinction early: he is "not without ambition, but without / The illness should attend it" (005-002). The tragedy unfolds precisely because ambition arrives *without* the necessary moral "illness"—the ruthlessness to stop at regicide.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> LADY MACBETH. Thou wouldst be great; Art not without ambition, but without The illness should attend it. (005-002)

Lady Macbeth's diagnosis is clinical: her husband has the desire but not the "illness"—the metaphorical sickness of moral indifference that would allow him to kill without hesitation. She positions herself as the solution to this deficiency.

> MACBETH. I have no spur To prick the sides of my intent, but only Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself And falls on th' other. (007-002)

Alone on stage, Macbeth articulates the physiology of ambition: it is a "spur" to action, but his "vaulting ambition" is a horse that jumps too far and lands on the wrong side—a metaphor of self-destruction. This is ambition's paradox: the very force that compels action ensures failure.

### Political Fallout (Act II)

> ROSS. 'Gainst nature still: Thriftless ambition, that will ravin up Thine own life's means! (010-003)

In the aftermath of Duncan's murder, Ross offers a political verdict on ambition: it is "thriftless"—wasteful, self-consuming—and "will ravin up / Thine own life's means." The image is of a beast devouring its own sustenance. Ross speaks of the princes' flight and the suspicion cast upon them, but his words resonate beyond the immediate context: ambition, once unleashed, consumes the very resources (honour, alliances, sanity) that sustain life.

### Deepening (Act III)

> BANQUO. Thou hast it now—King, Cawdor, Glamis, all As the Weird Women promis'd; and I fear Thou play'dst most foully for't. (012-001)

By Act III, ambition's consequences are visible to others. Banquo, who heard the same prophecies, recognizes that Macbeth's ambition "play'd most foully"—the means of achieving the crown have tainted its possession.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> MACBETH. For mine own good All causes shall give way. I am in blood Stepp'd in so far that, should I wade no more, Returning were as tedious as go o'er. (015-014)

Macbeth's ambition has metastasized into fatalism. The language of "wading in blood" and "stepp'd in so far" marks ambition's endpoint: not triumph but exhaustion, a prisoner of his own ascent.

## Structural Analysis

Ambition in *Macbeth* traces a distinct structural arc across the play's five acts:

| Act | Ambition's State | Key Marker |
|-----|-----------------|------------|
| I | Latent to Ignited | The witches' prophecy (003-Scene3), Lady Macbeth's catalysis ("golden round," 005-002) |
| II | Enacted | Duncan's murder; Ross's diagnosis of "thriftless ambition" (010-003) |
| III | Consolidated through violence | Banquo's murder; the feast scene breakdown |
| IV | Paranoiac | Apparitions double-edged reassurance; Macduff's family slaughtered |
| V | Exhausted | "Tomorrow" speech; ambition's final form is nihilism, not triumph |

This arc mirrors the classical tragic trajectory: a flaw (ambition) that initially propels the hero upward becomes the very mechanism of his descent. The structural pivot is Act II Scene IV, where Ross's public condemnation signals that ambition's private rationalizations have become publicly legible as "against nature."

## Ambition as Self-Consuming Force

A recurring pattern across the play is ambition's self-destructive quality, captured in three distinct metaphors:

- **The overleaping horse** (007-002): Macbeth's "vaulting ambition" jumps too far and lands on the wrong side—ambition as miscalculation.
- **The ravening beast** (010-003): Ross's "thriftless ambition" devours its own life's means—ambition as self-cannibalism.
- **The wading river** (015-014): Macbeth "in blood / Stepp'd in so far" cannot turn back—ambition as irreversible momentum.

Each metaphor escalates the sense of entrapment. The horse might be reined; the beast might be fed; but the river cannot be unwaded. By Act V, ambition has become indistinguishable from fate.
