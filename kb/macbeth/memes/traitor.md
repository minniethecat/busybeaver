# Traitor

In *Macbeth*, "traitor" is the word that defines, condemns, and ultimately consumes. The play opens with the execution of one traitor (the first Thane of Cawdor) and closes with the execution of another (Macbeth). Between them, the word circulates obsessively — in battlefield reports, in a child's innocent question, in the mutual accusations of a collapsing regime.

## Definition & Context

Treason in Jacobean Scotland carried the death penalty, and treason against a king was treason against God. Shakespeare structures *Macbeth* around a traitor's arc: the hero of Act I becomes the traitor of Act V. The word's journey through the play maps this inversion. The first use describes the rebel Macdonwald; the last describes Macbeth. What begins as a clear political category becomes, by the end, the mirror in which every character sees themselves.

## Textual Evidence

### The First Traitor (Act I)

> Assisted by that most disloyal traitor, / The Thane of Cawdor, began a dismal conflict (002-017)

The Captain's report establishes the traitor template: the Thane of Cawdor, a trusted nobleman, betrayed his king. Duncan's response — "No more that Thane of Cawdor shall deceive / Our bosom interest" — and the transfer of the title to Macbeth is the play's founding irony. The new Thane of Cawdor will prove an even greater traitor than the old one.

### The Anatomy of Treason (Act IV)

> SON. Was my father a traitor, mother? (019-022)

This is the play's most devastating question. Lady Macduff's young son, told his father has fled and is therefore a traitor, seeks a definition. "What is a traitor?" he asks (019-024). Lady Macduff's answer — "one that swears and lies" — is tragically wrong (Macduff is loyal), but the boy's follow-up is piercing: "And be all traitors that do so?" (019-026). "Every one that does so is a traitor, and must be hanged" (019-027). The child's innocent logic indicts the entire adult world: if traitors are those who swear and lie, then the whole court — starting with Macbeth — qualifies.

> FIRST MURDERER. He's a traitor. (019-041)

The murderer's justification for killing Macduff's son is the single word "traitor" — a word that has been emptied of meaning by overuse, now serving only as a warrant for violence.

### The Final Traitor (Act V)

> MACDUFF. Then yield thee, coward, / And live to be the show and gaze o' the time: / We'll have thee, as our rarer monsters are, / Painted upon a pole, and underwrit, / "Here may you see the tyrant." (028-024)

By the play's end, "traitor" has evolved into "tyrant." Macbeth, who began by executing traitors, ends as the spectacle of one — a painted monster for public display. The category has completed its circle.
