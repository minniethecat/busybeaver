The appearance of **Banquo's ghost** at the royal banquet (Act III, Scene IV) is the play's pivotal scene of psychological disintegration. It marks the moment when Macbeth's private guilt becomes publicly visible — even if only he can see its cause — and the point after which his rule can never achieve the legitimacy he craves.

## Overview

### Context: The Banquet Setting

The scene opens with Macbeth at the height of his ambition: he is king, the banquet is prepared, and he plays the gracious host — "You know your own degrees, sit down. At first and last, the hearty welcome" (015-001). The feast is the public ritual through which a monarch displays order, hierarchy, and largesse.

Unknown to the guests, Macbeth has arranged Banquo's murder. The First Murderer appears at the door to report success — Banquo's throat is cut, "safe in a ditch he bides, / With twenty trenched gashes on his head" (015-013) — but Fleance has escaped. This news, delivered in an aside, plants the first crack in Macbeth's composure: "Then comes my fit again: I had else been perfect" (015-016).

### The Apparition

Macbeth returns to the banquet table. When Ross invites him to sit, Macbeth protests that the table is full — and then sees what no one else can:

> MACBETH. The table's full.
> LENNOX. Here is a place reserv'd, sir.
> MACBETH. Where?
> LENNOX. Here, my good lord. What is't that moves your highness?
> MACBETH. Which of you have done this?
> LORDS. What, my good lord?
> MACBETH. Thou canst not say I did it: never shake / Thy gory locks at me. (015-021–027)

The ghost occupies Macbeth's own seat. It says nothing. It only shakes its "gory locks" — the wounds of the murdered Banquo made visible. The silence of the ghost is crucial: it is a projection of guilt that has no words because Macbeth has suppressed all language for what he has done.

### Lady Macbeth's Crisis Management

Lady Macbeth, who does not see the ghost, moves immediately into damage control:

> LADY MACBETH. Sit, worthy friends: my lord is often thus,
> And hath been from his youth: pray you, keep seat;
> The fit is momentary; upon a thought
> He will again be well. (015-028)

She then turns on Macbeth privately: "Are you a man?" (015-031) — the same question she used to goad him into murdering Duncan. Her explanation — that this is a childhood "fit" — is the first of many attempts to normalise what has become abnormal.

### The Second Appearance and Collapse

When the ghost vanishes, Macbeth toasts "our dear friend Banquo, whom we miss" (015-038). The ghost immediately reappears. Macbeth's response escalates:

> MACBETH. Avaunt! and quit my sight! let the earth hide thee!
> Thy bones are marrowless, thy blood is cold;
> Thou hast no speculation in those eyes
> Which thou dost glare with! (015-040)

The catalogue — "marrowless bones", "cold blood", eyes without "speculation" (sight) — is an inventory of a corpse. But the ghost refuses to be a corpse. It glares. The banquet shatters; the guests depart "without leave-taking" (015-052), a breach of ceremony that signals the collapse of courtly order.

### Dramatic Significance

The ghost of Banquo is unique among the play's supernatural phenomena. Unlike the Witches — visible to Banquo and Macbeth alike — or the floating dagger — explicitly acknowledged by Macbeth as "a false creation / Proceeding from the heat-oppressed brain" (008-030) — Banquo's ghost occupies an ambiguous ontological space. Macbeth sees it; the guests do not. Shakespeare refuses to resolve whether it is hallucination or genuine visitation, and this refusal is the scene's power.

The scene marks the structural pivot of the play. Before the banquet, Macbeth's crimes are concealed (Duncan's murder blamed on the grooms, Banquo's murder arranged through hired killers). After the banquet, the facade cracks permanently. The lords disperse; Macbeth resolves to visit the Witches again; the spiral toward Dunsinane has begun.
