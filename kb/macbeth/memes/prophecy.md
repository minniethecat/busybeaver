# Prophecy

Prophecy is the engine of *Macbeth*'s plot and its central philosophical problem. The Weird Sisters' predictions do not merely foretell events; they provoke the very actions that bring those events about, raising questions about free will, fate, and the ethics of foreknowledge that the play never fully resolves. Lady Macbeth's phrase — "Greater than both, by the all-hail hereafter" (005-027) — captures prophecy's double function: it is both a promise of future greatness and the mechanism by which that greatness destroys its recipient.

## Definition and Context

In the Jacobean imagination, prophecy was a contested category. Biblical prophecy was divine revelation; folk prophecy (associated with witches and cunning-women) was dangerous and potentially demonic. The Weird Sisters occupy this latter category — their prophecies are not from God but from the "instruments of darkness." Their ambiguity is the point: a prophecy that is technically true but fatally misleading is the play's model for how language itself can become an instrument of damnation.

## The Mechanism of Prophecy

The play stages prophecy as a three-part structure: utterance, interpretation, action. The witches speak; Macbeth interprets; and his interpretation — always self-serving, always blind to the "double sense" — drives the action. Banquo, who also receives a prophecy, demonstrates the alternative: he hears, he notes, but he does not act. "And bade them speak to him; then, prophet-like, / They hailed him father to a line of kings" (012-038–039) — Banquo's restraint is the proof that prophecy does not compel; it reveals character.

## The Apparitions

The second set of prophecies in Act IV is the play's most elaborate staging of the prophecy problem. Three apparitions rise, each delivering a truth that is also a trap:

> An Apparition of an armed Head rises. (018-026)

The armed head warns of Macduff — a truth Macbeth fatally over-interprets by ordering the murder of Macduff's innocent family.

> An Apparition of a bloody Child rises. (018-033)

"None of woman born shall harm Macbeth" — the bloody child embodies the Caesarean birth that makes Macduff the exception to the rule Macbeth takes as absolute.

> An Apparition of a Child crowned, with a tree in his hand, rises. (018-039)

Birnam Wood's march seems impossible — until Malcolm's soldiers cut branches for camouflage. The crowned child with a tree is the perfect image of prophecy: sovereignty and vulnerability held in the same hand.

## Self-Fulfillment and Free Will

The play's deepest question is whether the prophecies cause events or merely predict them. Macbeth "starts" at the first prophecy because it names a desire he already harbors. The witches do not plant ambition; they activate it. The armed head's warning "beware Macduff" becomes a death sentence for Macduff's family — not because the prophecy demands it, but because Macbeth's tyranny interprets all warnings as justifications for violence. In this sense, prophecy in *Macbeth* is not fate but temptation: it offers knowledge that can only be acted on sinfully, and every character chooses whether to accept that offer.

## The Fate Dimension

The play's single direct address to fate by name is Macbeth's challenge: "Rather than so, come, fate, into the list, / And champion me to the utterance!" (012-050). Here Macbeth treats fate as an opponent he can duel — a desperate assertion of agency at the moment when his entanglement in the prophecies' logic has become inescapable. The irony is that by challenging fate, he has already fulfilled its design.
