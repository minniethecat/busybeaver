# Light (and Darkness)

Light in *Macbeth* is the sensory correlative of moral order: when it fails, murder becomes possible; when it is extinguished, meaning itself dissolves into the "dusty death" (025-009) of Macbeth's final soliloquy. The motif moves through three distinct phases: the extinguishing of natural light (stars, the sun), the substitution of artificial light (torches, candles), and finally the metaphysical extinguishing of life-as-light in "Out, out, brief candle" (025-009). At every stage, light is what humans carry into darkness — and what darkness eventually consumes.

## Definition & Context

In the symbolic vocabulary of early modern England, light was synonymous with divine presence, truth, and moral clarity. The Book of Genesis begins with the creation of light; the Gospel of John identifies Christ as "the light of the world." When *Macbeth* opens with thunder and lightning, the natural order is already disturbed — light is not illumination but a weapon of the storm. The play's subsequent extinguishing of light — stars that must "hide your fires" (004-011), a sun that refuses to rise after Duncan's murder — tracks the progressive darkening of Scotland under Macbeth's rule. The Porter's scene, set at the gate of hell-in-the-castle, takes place in the deep darkness before dawn, lit only by torches.

## Textual Evidence

### The Stars Hidden (Act I)

> Stars, hide your fires; / Let not light see my black and deep desires. (004-011)

Macbeth's apostrophe to the stars is his first explicit acknowledgment of the darkness of his intentions. He asks the stars — the most distant, impersonal sources of light — to conceal their fires so that light itself cannot witness his "black and deep desires." The word "black" here carries its full early modern weight: morally evil, visually dark, and associated with the devil. Macbeth positions himself against light from the outset, and the rest of the play traces the consequences of this alignment.

### The Failed Dawn (Act II)

> … There's husbandry in heaven; / Their candles are all out. (008-006)

Banquo, unable to sleep on the night of Duncan's murder, speaks these words to Fleance. "Husbandry" means thrift: heaven is saving its candles, the stars, which is why the night is so unnaturally dark. The image is domestic and cosmic simultaneously — the stars are candles, and the economy of heaven has gone into austerity. The darkness that enables Duncan's murder is not simply night but a special darkness, a withdrawal of heaven's light. Fleance's response — "The moon is down; I have not heard the clock" (008-007) — compounds the sense of a world unmoored from its usual markers.

### The Extinguished Torch (Act III)

> SECOND MURDERER. A light, a light!
> THIRD MURDERER. 'Tis he.
> FIRST MURDERER. Stand to't.
> ...
> THIRD MURDERER. Who did strike out the light? (014-007, 014-019)

The murder of Banquo is staged around a torch. Fleance escapes because the light is "struck out" — an accident that allows Banquo's son to flee into the darkness. The extinguished light is both a practical detail (without light, the murderers cannot find Fleance) and a symbolic one: the darkness Macbeth summoned to hide his "black desires" now works against him, allowing the prophesied heir to escape. The torch, a small human light, is inadequate to the task, just as Macbeth's murderous strategy is inadequate to the prophecies it tries to thwart.

### The Perpetual Candle (Act V)

> DOCTOR. How came she by that light?
> GENTLEWOMAN. Why, it stood by her: she has light by her continually; 'tis her command. (021-008–021-009)

Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking is illuminated by a candle she refuses to be without. The irony is dense: the woman who called on darkness to hide Duncan's murder now cannot endure darkness at all. The "light by her continually" is a materialisation of conscience — the inward light she tried to extinguish has returned as an outward flame she cannot control. The Doctor's question is practical but the answer is metaphysical: she came by that light because she lost all other light, and what remains is a candle that illuminates only her guilt.

### The Brief Candle (Act V)

> Out, out, brief candle! / Life's but a walking shadow, a poor player / That struts and frets his hour upon the stage, / And then is heard no more. It is a tale / Told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, / Signifying nothing. (025-009)

Macbeth's final soliloquy extinguishes the light motif along with life itself. The "brief candle" is the culmination of every torch, star, and candle in the play: life is a small light in a vast darkness, easily snuffed out. The progression is from cosmic light (stars, Act I) through domestic light (torches, candles) to metaphysical light extinguished — and with it, meaning. The "sound and fury" that replaces light signifies "nothing." This is the endpoint of Macbeth's alignment with darkness: having hidden his desires from light, he finds that nothing is left when the light goes out.
