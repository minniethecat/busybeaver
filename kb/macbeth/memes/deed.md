# Deed

The concept of the **deed**—the completed act, irreversible and consequential—is the pivot on which *Macbeth* turns. Before the murder of Duncan, Macbeth and Lady Macbeth debate the nature of action; after it, they are consumed by what they have done. Shakespeare makes the deed not merely an event but a metaphysical rupture: once performed, it cannot be unwritten, only endured.

## Definition & Context

In the moral universe of *Macbeth*, a "deed" is more than an action—it is an act that permanently alters the relationship between the doer and the world. The play distinguishes sharply between the *thought* of a deed (fantasy, speculation, temptation) and its *execution* (irrevocable, consequential). When Lady Macbeth urges her husband to act, she speaks of "this night's great business" (005-034); when Macbeth recoils from what he has done, he understands that "to know my deed, 'twere best not know myself" (009-031). The deed creates a new self, one that cannot be returned to innocence.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> MACBETH. I have done the deed.—Didst thou not hear a noise? (009-005)

This is the central line of the murder scene: Macbeth's terse, almost childlike announcement to Lady Macbeth. The brevity of "I have done the deed" contrasts with the elaborate soliloquies that preceded it—the dagger vision, the debate with conscience. The deed itself, the actual act of murder, happens offstage. Shakespeare's choice to render it as a reported fact rather than a spectacle emphasizes the psychological rupture: the deed is already in the past, already irreversible.

> LADY MACBETH. These deeds must not be thought After these ways; so, it will make us mad. (009-021)

Lady Macbeth's practical counsel—do not dwell on what you have done—is the play's great irony. She who advises against thinking of the deed will later be destroyed by the relentless return of its memory in her sleepwalking.

> MACBETH. To know my deed, 'twere best not know myself. (009-031)

Macbeth articulates the central philosophical problem of the play: the deed transforms the self so completely that self-knowledge becomes unbearable. To acknowledge what he has done is to acknowledge what he has become.

### Deepening (Act III)

> MACBETH. Words to the heat of deeds too cold breath gives. (008-048)

Before the murder, Macbeth reflects on the gap between language and action. Words are "cold breath"—insubstantial—compared to the "heat of deeds." This metaphor of temperature (heat/cold) runs through the play's treatment of action: deeds burn, words merely fog the air. The line also marks Macbeth's growing preference for action over deliberation, a shift that will define his descent into tyranny.

> MACBETH. The very firstlings of my heart shall be The firstlings of my hand. (018-068)

By Act IV, Macbeth has inverted his earlier hesitation. In Act I, he debated every action; now he resolves that thought and deed shall be simultaneous—"the firstlings of my heart" shall instantly become "the firstlings of my hand." This is the terminus of the deed's evolution: from contemplated to automatic, from moral choice to reflex.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> LADY MACBETH. The Thane of Fife had a wife: where is she now? What, will these hands ne'er be clean? (021-018)

Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking is the ultimate return of the repressed deed. The "deed" she dismissed with "a little water clears us of this deed" (009-030) has not been cleared at all—it has waited in her unconscious, surfacing as compulsive hand-washing. The deed, her husband said, "must not be thought"; it has instead become an obsession that thinking cannot dissolve.
