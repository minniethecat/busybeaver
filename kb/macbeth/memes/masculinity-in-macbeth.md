# Masculinity in Macbeth

*Macbeth* conducts one of Shakespeare's most sustained interrogations of masculinity. The play does not assume what it means to be a man; it tests competing definitions against each other — courage as violence, courage as restraint, manhood as emotional suppression, manhood as integrated feeling — and traces the destructive consequences of each. Across five major characters, masculinity emerges not as a stable identity but as a contested performance, one whose demands drive the tragedy's central actions.

## Lady Macbeth's Challenge

The play's most radical gender intervention comes from Lady Macbeth, who explicitly links masculinity to violence and calls on spirits to strip her of the femininity that impedes murder:

> That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here (005-011)

> make thick my blood (005-013)

"Unsex me here" is a demand to be released from the constraints of womanhood — from the "milk of human kindness" she associates with Macbeth's hesitation. Her invocation equates masculinity with cruelty: to be a man is to be capable of murder without compunction. The irony is that Lady Macbeth herself becomes the play's most vivid demonstration that the masculinity she worships is unsustainable — her sleepwalking breakdown reveals the psychological cost of the violence she demanded.

## Macbeth's Performative Manhood

Macbeth's relationship to masculinity is reactive and anxious. Lady Macbeth's most effective manipulation is to question his manhood: "When you durst do it, then you were a man." Macbeth responds by performing — he murders Duncan to prove he is not a "coward," then escalates his violence each time his masculinity is threatened. His famous "I dare do all that may become a man; / Who dares do more is none" attempts to define manhood as bounded by honour, but Lady Macbeth immediately redefines it as unbounded audacity. Macbeth's tragedy is that he accepts her definition and spends the rest of the play proving he is "man enough" — at the cost of his humanity.

## Macduff's Integrated Manhood

Macduff offers the play's most radical alternative. When Malcolm urges him to "dispute it like a man" after learning of his family's slaughter, Macduff's response is extraordinary:

> I shall do so; / But I must also feel it as a man.

This single line redefines masculinity as the capacity to feel grief rather than suppress it. Macduff does not reject manhood — he "shall do so," he will act — but he insists that action without feeling is not manly but inhuman. His integrated response (grief *and* vengeance, feeling *and* action) is the play's normative counter to Macbeth's hollow performative violence.

## Malcolm's Virtuous Kingship

Malcolm's self-presentation to Macduff in Act IV, Scene III further complicates the play's gender logic. His catalogue of vices — lust, avarice, the absence of "king-becoming graces" — is a deliberate test, a performance of unworthiness designed to probe Macduff's loyalty. When he ultimately reveals his true character, Malcolm defines kingship (and by extension manhood) through a list of virtues: "justice, verity, temperance, stableness, / Bounty, perseverance, mercy, lowliness, / Devotion, patience, courage, fortitude." This is masculinity as moral completeness, not martial prowess — a vision that restores the order Macbeth's hyper-masculine violence has shattered.

## Banquo's Restraint

Banquo represents yet another model: masculinity as self-governance. He receives the same prophecies as Macbeth but refuses to act on them at the cost of his "bosom franchised and allegiance clear." His restraint is not passivity but a different kind of strength — the strength to resist temptation. In the play's taxonomy of masculinity, Banquo proves that "being a man" does not require seizing every advantage; it can mean keeping faith.

## Synthesis: The Contested Definition

The play's genius is that it never settles on one definition of masculinity. Instead, it stages a contest: Lady Macbeth's instrumental cruelty vs. Macbeth's anxious performance vs. Macduff's integrated feeling vs. Malcolm's virtuous completeness vs. Banquo's principled restraint. Each model has its costs and its casualties. The play's final judgment is structural rather than explicit: the hyper-masculine violence of the Macbeths destroys them both, while Macduff's feeling manhood and Malcolm's virtuous one survive to restore order. The implication is clear: masculinity defined as violence is self-consuming; only masculinity integrated with feeling, virtue, and restraint can sustain life and community.
