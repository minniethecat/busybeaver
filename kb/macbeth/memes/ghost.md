# Ghost

In *Macbeth*, ghosts and apparitions serve as the supernatural machinery through which guilt is externalised, the future is foretold, and the murdered dead refuse to stay buried.

## Definition & Context

Ghosts in *Macbeth* operate at the intersection of Jacobean demonology and dramatic convention. The period's theological debates questioned whether apparitions were truly the souls of the dead or demonic illusions; Shakespeare exploits this ambiguity to powerful effect. Banquo's ghost—visible to Macbeth alone among the banquet guests—may be a genuine supernatural visitation or a projection of Macbeth's guilt-ridden mind. The apparitions conjured by the Weird Sisters are explicitly demonic, summoned through "Black Spirits" (018-013) to deliver prophecies that mislead through ambiguous truth. Together, these spectral figures embody the play's central concern: the past cannot be safely buried, and the future is a trap baited with half-truths.

## Textual Evidence

### The Ghost as Guilt (Act II–III)

> Moves like a ghost.—Thou sure and firm-set earth (008-043)

Macbeth's own words during the dagger soliloquy in Act II Scene i prefigure Banquo's ghost. He describes the hallucinatory dagger moving "like a ghost," establishing the link between spectral imagery and murderous intent. The "sure and firm-set earth" he invokes will not keep his victims buried.

Banquo's ghost appears twice during the coronation banquet in Act III Scene iv. After the Murderers report Banquo's death, the ghost takes Macbeth's seat at the table—a visual assertion that Banquo's line, not Macbeth's, will occupy the throne. Macbeth's terror is proportionate to his guilt: he protests that the murdered man cannot accuse him, simultaneously denying and confessing the crime.

> [Ghost disappears.] (015-033)

The stage direction's brevity belies its dramatic weight. The ghost vanishes when Lady Macbeth recalls her husband to composure, only to reappear when Macbeth toasts Banquo's absence, forcing the ghost's presence once more. The double appearance and disappearance enacts the rhythm of guilt suppressed and guilt returned that structures the play's second half.

### Apparitions as Prophecy (Act IV)

> An Apparition of an armed Head rises. (018-026)
> An Apparition of a bloody Child rises. (018-033)
> An Apparition of a Child crowned, with a tree in his hand, rises. (018-039)

The three apparitions conjured in Act IV Scene i form a deliberate parallel to the three witches of Act I. Each delivers a prophecy that gives Macbeth false confidence: the armed head warns of Macduff, the bloody child declares no man of woman born can harm Macbeth, and the crowned child promises safety until Birnam Wood comes to Dunsinane. These apparitions are not the returning dead but demonically manufactured oracles, and their prophecies prove true only through equivocation.

### The Haunting of the Living (Act IV–V)

The spectral does not end with the banquet. The Weird Sisters conjure to "Black Spirits" (018-013), and the line of Banquo's royal descendants appears as a ghostly pageant of future kings. Even the righteous carry ghostly burdens: Macduff, upon learning of his family's slaughter, invokes the language of haunting to express his guilt for having left them unprotected. The ghost, as a dramatic figure, persists beyond any single apparition—it names the condition of a Scotland ruled by a murderer who cannot escape what he has done.

## Critical Perspectives

The ghost in *Macbeth* has been read through multiple critical lenses. Psychoanalytic approaches treat Banquo's ghost as the return of the repressed—Macbeth's guilt projected onto the external world. Theological readings emphasise the Jacobean debate about demonic illusion, noting that the Weird Sisters' apparitions speak "truth" that leads to damnation. Performance history shows the ghost staged variously as an empty stool, a bloody actor, or a lighting effect, each choice shaping the play's central question: is the ghost real, or is Macbeth mad?
