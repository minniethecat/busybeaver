# The Battlements

The battlements of Macbeth's castle at Inverness serve as a symbolic threshold in the play, appearing at two critical moments: first in the Captain's report of Macbeth's martial heroism, and later as the site of Lady Macbeth's dark invocation of spirits.

## The Captain's Report: Martial Heroism

In the opening act, a wounded soldier reports to King Duncan the outcome of the battle against the rebel Macdonwald. His vivid account culminates in an image of the battlements as the place where Macbeth displays his victory:

> For brave Macbeth (well he deserves that name), Disdaining Fortune, with his brandish'd steel, Which smok'd with bloody execution, Like Valour's minion, carv'd out his passage, Till he fac'd the slave; Which ne'er shook hands, nor bade farewell to him, Till he unseam'd him from the nave to the chops, And fix'd his head upon our battlements. (002-004)

The battlements here are a symbol of martial triumph: the display of the enemy's severed head announces order restored through violence. Macbeth is celebrated as "Valour's minion," and the battlements serve as the public stage for his loyalty to Duncan. The same battlements will soon shelter a far darker purpose.

## Lady Macbeth's Invocation: The Supernatural Threshold

When Lady Macbeth receives word of Duncan's impending visit, she steps onto the battlements and calls upon spirits to "unsex" her — transforming the space from a site of military display into a threshold for the supernatural:

> Under my battlements. Come, you spirits That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here. (005-010)

The possessive pronoun is telling: "my battlements." Lady Macbeth claims the architecture of masculine power as her own. The battlements — designed to repel enemies — become instead a gateway through which demonic forces are invited in. Where the Captain showed the battlements decorated with a rebel's head, Lady Macbeth imagines them sheltering the murder of a king.

## Architectural and Symbolic Significance

The battlements would have been represented by the upper gallery of the Globe theatre. Their dual function — defensive parapet and elevated platform — maps onto the play's central tension between public honour and private treachery.

Macbeth is first praised upon the battlements and later besieged upon them. The walls that once displayed his loyalty to Duncan cannot protect him from the consequences of his crimes.

## Symbolic Arc: Triumph, Invocation, Siege

The battlements trace the play's moral arc in miniature. At the opening, they are the site of public declaration — Macbeth's victory displayed for all to see. By Act I Scene v, they have become the site of private invocation — Lady Macbeth speaking words meant for spirits alone. By Act V, they are the walls of the besieged Dunsinane.

The Globe's architecture would have made this visible: the upper gallery serving as the battlements, with the main stage below representing both castle interior and the plain before it. Lady Macbeth's invocation, performed from the gallery, placed her literally above the common world — a spatial enactment of her claim to supernatural agency. When Macbeth later stands upon the same battlements facing Birnam Wood, the elevation that once signalled power becomes the height from which he must watch his doom approach.
