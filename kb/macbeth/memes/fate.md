# Fate

Fate is the philosophical ground bass of *Macbeth* — the question that sounds beneath every action. Do the Weird Sisters' prophecies reveal a fixed future, or do they create the future by shaping Macbeth's choices? The play withholds a definitive answer, but it traces the consequences of treating prophecy as fate with devastating precision.

## Fate vs. Free Will

The play's central philosophical tension appears in its opening scenes. The witches' first prophecy — that Macbeth will be king — is followed almost immediately by Duncan's announcement that Malcolm will be his heir. This creates a structural fork: either the prophecy is false (unlikely, given that the Thane of Cawdor prediction was immediately verified), or Macbeth must act to fulfill it. The prophecy does not tell Macbeth to murder Duncan; it tells him he will be king. The choice of *how* to become king is entirely his.

> Art thou not, fatal vision, sensible / To feeling as to sight? (008-023)

Macbeth's address to the imaginary dagger uses "fatal" in a precise double sense: the dagger is both destined (fated) and deadly (fatal). This fusion of fate and violence is the play's signature move — every appeal to fate is also an admission of murderous intent.

## Banquo's Alternative

Banquo receives a prophecy of equal magnitude — "thou shalt get kings, though thou be none" — but does not act on it. His restraint demonstrates that the witches' words do not compel action. "And bade them speak to him; then, prophet-like, / They hailed him father to a line of kings" (012-038–039) — Banquo records the prophecy but does not pursue it. He is the control case that proves the thesis: fate does not determine character; character determines what one does with fate.

## The Challenge to Fate

Macbeth's most direct confrontation with fate comes in Act III:

> Rather than so, come, fate, into the list, / And champion me to the utterance! (012-050)

He challenges fate to single combat — a knight's challenge, an assertion of agency in the very act of acknowledging fate's power. The irony is devastating: by "championing" himself against fate, Macbeth is already acting out the role the prophecies assigned him. The more he fights fate, the more he fulfills it.

## "The great doom's image"

The Porter's phrase — "The great doom's image" (010-038) — links the knocking at the gate to the Last Judgment. In the play's theological register, fate shades into providence and doom: the question is not merely whether events are predetermined, but whether that predetermination carries moral meaning. The Porter's comic eschatology — answering the gate of hell — suggests that the play's events have cosmic significance, that Duncan's murder is not merely a crime but a foreshadowing of final judgment.
