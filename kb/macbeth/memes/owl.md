# Owl

The owl in *Macbeth* — the bird of night and death whose shriek punctuates the murder of Duncan, whose unnatural predation mirrors the overturning of cosmic order, and whose howl marks the passage of time in a world gone dark.

## Definition & Context

The owl is *Macbeth*'s most insistent animal omen. It appears at the play's two most consequential moments: Lady Macbeth hears "the owl that shriek'd, the fatal bellman" as her husband murders Duncan ((009-002)), and the Old Man reports that "a mousing owl hawked at and killed" a falcon — an inversion of the natural hierarchy that mirrors the regicide ((011-004)). In Jacobean folklore, the owl was a bird of ill omen, its call a death knell. Shakespeare's innovation is to make the owl not merely a portent but an active participant — its shriek is the "fatal bellman" that rings Duncan to his grave.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> It was the owl that shriek'd, the fatal bellman, / Which gives the stern'st good-night. ((009-002))

Lady Macbeth's line is the owl's most famous appearance. Standing alone while Macbeth kills Duncan, she hears the owl's shriek and interprets it as the "fatal bellman" — the town crier who announced executions. The owl's cry is, in her reading, the death knell of Duncan, and she finds it almost exhilarating.

> I heard the owl scream and the crickets cry. ((009-006))

Lady Macbeth's second reference to the owl underscores the unnatural quiet of the murder night. The natural world is vocalising — screaming, crying — while the human world holds its breath.

### Deepening (Act III)

> A falcon, towering in her pride of place, / Was by a mousing owl hawked at and killed. ((011-004))

The Old Man's report of a falcon killed by an owl is the play's most potent image of cosmic inversion. The falcon (royal bird, predator of the heights) is killed by the owl (low-flying, mouse-hunting bird of the dark). This is the natural world's commentary on the political murder — a king (falcon) brought down by a subject (owl).

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> Whose howl's his watch, thus with his stealthy pace. ((008-041))

The owl's howl marks the watch — the dark hours when murder walks. The owl is the timekeeper of evil, its cry measuring out the night of Duncan's death.

## Critical Perspectives

The owl in *Macbeth* functions as what anthropologists call a "liminal" creature — a being that crosses boundaries (night/day, natural/supernatural, predator of small things). Its killing of the falcon is the play's symbol of all boundaries crossed. Critics have noted the owl's connection to Hecate, the goddess of witchcraft, whose rites took place at night and involved owl imagery — fitting for a play where the dark powers are literally invoked.
