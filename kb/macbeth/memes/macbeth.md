# Macbeth

Macbeth is the tragic protagonist of Shakespeare's *Macbeth*. A valiant Scottish general at the play's opening, he is seduced by the witches' prophecies and his wife's ambition into murdering King Duncan and usurping the throne. His reign unravels through paranoia, further violence, and psychological disintegration, ending in his death at the hands of [[macduff|Macduff]]. Macbeth embodies the play's central tensions between fate and free will, ambition and conscience, masculinity and moral sensibility.

## The Witches' Prophecy

The play introduces Macbeth through the witches' cryptic words before he even appears on stage:

> FIRST WITCH. When shall we three meet again? In thunder, lightning, or in rain?
> SECOND WITCH. When the hurlyburly's done, When the battle's lost and won.
> THIRD WITCH. That will be ere the set of sun.
> FIRST WITCH. Where the place?
> SECOND WITCH. Upon the heath.
> THIRD WITCH. There to meet with Macbeth. ((001-007))

The opening scene establishes Macbeth as the witches' object of interest before the audience knows anything about him — a structural choice that frames his entire story as a meeting with the supernatural. When Macbeth and Banquo encounter the witches in Act I, Scene III, the prophecies immediately unsettle him:

> FIRST WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Glamis!
> SECOND WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Cawdor!
> THIRD WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! that shalt be king hereafter! ([003-018–020])

Banquo observes that Macbeth "start[s] and seem[s] to fear / Things that do sound so fair" ((003-037)), suggesting that the prophecies resonate with ambitions Macbeth already harbors — a key interpretive crux: do the witches cause Macbeth's ambition, or merely activate it?

## "Brave Macbeth": The Warrior Before the Fall

Before his moral collapse, Macbeth is described in heroic terms. The Captain's report in Act I, Scene II paints him as the savior of Scotland:

> For brave Macbeth (well he deserves that name),
> Disdaining Fortune, with his brandish'd steel,
> Which smok'd with bloody execution,
> Like Valour's minion carv'd out his passage
> Till he fac'd the slave. ((002-004))

Duncan's response — "What he hath lost, noble Macbeth hath won" ((002-022)) — establishes Macbeth as both warrior and successor to the treacherous Thane of Cawdor. The irony is acute: Duncan transfers the title of a traitor to the man who will become an even greater traitor.

## Ambition and Conscience

Macbeth's central struggle is between "vaulting ambition" ((007-012)) and a conscience that will not stay silent. His soliloquy in Act I, Scene VII lays out every reason against the murder:

> He's here in double trust:
> First, as I am his kinsman and his subject,
> Strong both against the deed; then, as his host,
> Who should against his murderer shut the door,
> Not bear the knife myself. ((007-008))

He acknowledges Duncan's virtues — "This Duncan / Hath borne his faculties so meek, hath been / So clear in his great office" ((007-011)) — and identifies the only motive as ambition itself: "I have no spur / To prick the sides of my intent, but only / Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself / And falls on th' other" ((007-012)). Lady Macbeth's arrival interrupts this moral clarity, and her manipulation — questioning his manhood — tips him toward the murder.

## "Macbeth Shall Sleep No More"

Immediately after Duncan's murder, Macbeth experiences auditory hallucination — a voice crying "Sleep no more!":

> Methought I heard a voice cry, "Sleep no more!
> Macbeth does murder sleep," — the innocent sleep,
> Sleep that knits up the ravell'd sleave of care,
> The death of each day's life, sore labour's bath,
> Balm of hurt minds, great nature's second course,
> Chief nourisher in life's feast. ((009-022))

The voice declares: "Glamis hath murder'd sleep, and therefore Cawdor / Shall sleep no more. Macbeth shall sleep no more!" ((009-024)). This moment marks the psychological turn: having murdered Duncan, Macbeth has also murdered his own peace. The sleep motif recurs throughout — Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking scene in Act V mirrors this earlier moment, showing both characters destroyed by the same crime.

## The Tyrant's Descent

From Banquo's murder to the slaughter of Macduff's family, Macbeth's violence escalates as his conscience attenuates. By Act IV, he has fully embraced the logic of tyranny:

> MACBETH. Time, thou anticipat'st my dread exploits.
> The flighty purpose never is o'ertook
> Unless the deed go with it. From this moment
> The very firstlings of my heart shall be
> The firstlings of my hand. ((017-047))

He resolves to act on impulse rather than deliberation — a complete inversion of the hesitant man from Act I. This transformation from conscience-stricken murderer to reflexive killer is the play's most systematic portrait of moral corrosion.

## The Final Confrontation

Macbeth's death at Macduff's hands is framed as justice, but Shakespeare gives him a moment of defiant self-knowledge before the end. His "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow" soliloquy ((023-014)) is the play's bleakest philosophical statement — life as "a tale / Told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, / Signifying nothing." Even in defeat, Macbeth refuses to surrender: "Lay on, Macduff; / And damn'd be him that first cries, 'Hold, enough!'" ((028-037)).
