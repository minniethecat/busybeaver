## Overview

The tongue in *Macbeth* is the contested organ of speech and power. Lady Macbeth wields language as a weapon, "chastising" with the "valour of my tongue" (001-005) — her rhetorical force exceeds her husband's martial courage. By contrast, Macbeth's tongue is progressively silenced, from the "Amen" that sticks in his throat (002-002) to the hollow rhetoric of the "Tomorrow" speech. The tongue charts the transfer and collapse of verbal power across the tragedy.

## Lady Macbeth's Tongue

Lady Macbeth's first soliloquy establishes the tongue as the instrument of her power. She will "pour my spirits in thine ear" (001-005) — speech as infusion, as possession. Her command to Macbeth — "Your hand, your tongue: look like the innocent flower, / But be the serpent under't" (001-005) — yokes the two organs of action: the hand that murders and the tongue that dissembles. She divides the labour of evil between them.

Her rebuke of Macbeth's hesitation — "From this time / Such I account thy love" (001-007) — demonstrates the tongue's capacity to wound without a blade. She rhetorically transforms Macbeth's reluctance into a failure of love, of manhood, of identity. The tongue that "chastises with valour" is more effective than any sword in the play's opening acts.
> Your face, my thane, is as a book where men
> May read strange matters. To beguile the time,
> Look like the time; bear welcome in your eye,
> Your hand, your tongue: look like the innocent flower,
> But be the serpent under't.

> (005-034)


## Macbeth's Silenced Tongue

Macbeth's tragedy registers in the progressive failure of his tongue. After the murder, he reports: "I had most need of blessing, and 'Amen' / Stuck in my throat" (002-002). The word of blessing cannot be spoken — the tongue has been contaminated by the hand's deed. The Porter's comic riff on equivocation — a tongue that "could swear in both the scales against either scale" (005-003) — provides the theological frame: Macbeth's tongue, like the equivocator's, can no longer speak truth because it has been divided against itself.

The "Tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow" soliloquy (021-005) is the tongue's final surrender. Language empties into syllable and dust — "a tale / Told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, / Signifying nothing." The tongue that once could speak of honour, love, and ambition can now only describe its own futility.

## The Tongue of Lamentation

Macduff restores the tongue to its proper function: lament. When urged to "give sorrow words," his inarticulate repetition — "All my pretty ones? / Did you say all?" (017-003) — is the tongue struggling to catch up with grief. Malcolm's instruction — "Let grief / Convert to anger; blunt not the heart, enrage it" (017-003) — proposes that the tongue should convert feeling into action, as Lady Macbeth once converted ambition into murder. But Macduff's tongue is honest where hers was manipulative; his speech heals rather than wounds.
