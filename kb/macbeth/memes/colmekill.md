# Colmekill

**Colmekill** (modern Iona) is the sacred island off the west coast of Scotland where Scottish kings were traditionally buried. In *Macbeth*, it appears as a single reference — but that reference carries the weight of an entire political theology. When [[macduff|Macduff]] reports that [[duncan|Duncan]]'s body has been "carried to Colmekill, the sacred storehouse of his predecessors and guardian of their bones," the burial becomes an act of symbolic restoration amid the chaos of regicide (011-016).

## Historical Significance

### The Island of Iona

Colmekill — from the Gaelic "Cill Choluim Chille" (the church of St. Columba) — is the island of Iona, where St. Columba established a monastery in 563 CE. For centuries, it was the traditional burial place of Scottish, Irish, and Norwegian kings. Shakespeare's reference to it as "the sacred storehouse" of Duncan's predecessors is historically accurate (011-016): the island's Reilig Odhrain cemetery contains the graves of numerous early Scottish monarchs.

### The Real Macbeth

Historically, the historical King Macbeth (Mac Bethad mac Findlaích, r. 1040–1057) was one of the last Scottish kings to be buried on Iona. Shakespeare, writing centuries later, uses the burial tradition to establish legitimacy — Duncan belongs to Iona because he belongs to the legitimate line of kings.

## Dramatic Function

### A Single Line, A World of Meaning

Colmekill is mentioned exactly once, in a brief exchange between Macduff and [[ross|Ross]]:

>ROSS: Where is Duncan's body?
>MACDUFF: Carried to Colmekill,
>The sacred storehouse of his predecessors,
>And guardian of their bones.
>
>(011-016)

In three lines, Shakespeare does more than convey a plot point. The word "sacred" reminds the audience that Duncan was not merely a man but a consecrated king. The word "guardian" suggests that even in death, the legitimate line watches over Scotland. And the proper burial contrasts sharply with the fate of the usurper — Macbeth's head, borne on a pike, receives no such honor.

### Contrast with Macbeth's Death

The play's final stage direction is Macduff entering "with Macbeth's head." The contrast with Duncan's burial at Colmekill is deliberate and devastating. The rightful king rests with his ancestors on sacred ground; the usurper's body is dismembered and displayed. Burial practice becomes political argument.

## Modern Iona

The island of Iona remains a site of pilgrimage and historical significance. The Iona Abbey, restored in the 20th century, stands near the ancient burial ground. The connection between Shakespeare's single line and the real geography of Scotland reminds us that *Macbeth* is not fantasy — it is dramatized history, anchored in real places and real traditions.
