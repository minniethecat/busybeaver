# Manhood

In *Macbeth*, "manhood" is not a fixed biological category but a contested moral and social ideal — one that characters invoke to justify violence, question courage, and measure worth.

The word "man" and its variants appear more than forty times in the play, placing gender at the center of the tragedy's moral architecture.

## Lady Macbeth's Inversion

[[lady-macbeth]] is the play's primary theorist of manhood.

In her first soliloquy, she fears that [[macbeth]]'s nature is "too full o' the milk of human kindness" (005-015), associating femininity with compassion and manhood with ruthlessness.

She calls on spirits to "unsex me here" (005-020), asking to be stripped of womanhood so that she can commit the murder Duncan's resemblance to her father prevents her from performing.

When Macbeth hesitates before the murder, Lady Macbeth deploys her most devastating weapon — the accusation of failed manhood: "When you durst do it, then you were a man; And to be more than what you were, you would be so much more the man" (007-043).

The equation is explicit: manhood = the will to kill.
> What beast was't, then,
> That made you break this enterprise to me?
> When you durst do it, then you were a man;
> And, to be more than what you were, you would
> Be so much more the man. Nor time nor place
> Did then adhere, and yet you would make both:
> They have made themselves, and that their fitness now
> Does unmake you.
>
> (007-009)


## Macbeth's Evolving Definition

Macbeth initially resists this definition.

His pre-murder soliloquy weighs moral consequences, and his famous "Is this a dagger which I see before me" speech (006-030) is filled with doubt.

But after the murder, he adopts Lady Macbeth's equation.

By Act III, he praises the murderers he hires by framing their grievance in terms of manhood: "Now, if you have a station in the file, not i' the worst rank of manhood, say't" (012-040).

His definition narrows to pure violence — and by Act V, when told that "The Queen, my lord, is dead" (026-010), he has no emotional response beyond a nihilistic soliloquy that drains all meaning from life.

## Macduff's Alternative

[[macduff]] offers the play's most complex definition of manhood.

When [[malcolm]] urges him to "Dispute it like a man" after learning of his family's slaughter, Macduff answers: "I shall do so; But I must also feel it as a man" (020-030).

For Macduff, manhood includes grief — feeling deeply is not weakness but a necessary component of moral action.

His tears do not prevent him from killing Macbeth; they fuel his righteous violence.

This is the play's resolution of the manhood question: the capacity to feel makes the capacity to act justly possible.

## Manhood as a Weapon

Throughout the play, manhood is a rhetorical weapon:

|---------|--------|-----------|---------|

| [[malcolm]] | Macduff | "Dispute it like a man" (020-030) | After Macduff's family killed |
