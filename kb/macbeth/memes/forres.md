# Forres

Forres is the seat of King Duncan's court in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. It serves as the setting for several early scenes — the Captain's report of the battle, Duncan's reception of Macbeth and Banquo, and the proclamation of Malcolm as Prince of Cumberland.

## Overview

Forres appears in Act I as the centre of royal authority. In Act I, Scene II, the court at "a Camp near Forres" (002-001) hears the bleeding Captain's report of Macbeth's heroism against Macdonwald and the Norwegian invaders. The King is at Forres when he receives Ross's account of the victory over Norway and the Thane of Cawdor's treason (002-017).

In Act I, Scene IV, Duncan holds court "at Forres. A Room in the Palace" (004-001), where he receives Macbeth and Banquo and announces Malcolm as his successor — the decision that prompts Macbeth to begin contemplating regicide. The palace at Forres thus represents legitimate kingship, the order that Macbeth will shatter.

## Dramatic Significance

Forres is contrasted with Inverness, Macbeth's castle, throughout the early acts. Forres is the seat of lawful rule and hospitality; Inverness becomes the scene of murder and betrayal. The movement of the action from Forres to Inverness mirrors Macbeth's moral trajectory from loyal subject to regicide.
