# Bubble

In *Macbeth*, the bubble is an image of insubstantial threat. Banquo first names it when the witches vanish: "the earth hath bubbles, as the water has." By Act IV, the witches' cauldron bubbles and churns, producing apparitions that are at once terribly real and fundamentally hollow — exactly like Macbeth's kingship itself.

## Definition & Context

A bubble is surface without substance — something visible but empty, momentarily present and then gone. Shakespeare uses the image at two key structural moments: first when the witches disappear after their initial prophecy (I.iii), and then in the cauldron scene (IV.i) where "bubble" becomes the refrain marking the witches' production of false futures. The arc between these two moments is the arc of Macbeth's career: from a prophecy-bubble that vanishes into air, to a cauldron-bubble that manufactures the illusions a tyrant clings to.

## Textual Evidence

### Earth's Bubbles (Act I)

> BANQUO. The earth hath bubbles, as the water has, / And these are of them. Whither are they vanish'd? (003-031)

Banquo's response to the witches' disappearance is the play's first association of bubble with the supernatural. The image is naturalistic — bubbles rise from earth as they do from water — but the implication is unsettling: the supernatural is not otherworldly but subterranean, rising from the same ground mortals walk on. The witches are "bubbles" of the earth: insubstantial, momentary, yet capable of delivering world-altering prophecies.

> MACBETH. Into the air; and what seem'd corporal melted / As breath into the wind. (003-032)

Macbeth's corroboration extends the image: what looked solid ("corporal") was actually as thin as breath. The bubble metaphor is complete — surface, emptiness, disappearance.

### The Cauldron Refrain (Act IV)

> ALL. Double, double toil and trouble; / Fire, burn; and cauldron, bubble. (018-006)

The refrain's final word — "bubble" — is the visible sign of the invisible labour. The cauldron's bubbles are the surface evidence of the witches' toil, just as the apparitions that rise from the brew are the surface evidence of prophecies whose substance Macbeth will fatally misread. The refrain is repeated three times (018-006, 018-008, 018-010): bubble is the product of toil and trouble.

> For a charm of powerful trouble, / Like a hell-broth boil and bubble. (018-007)

The simile "like a hell-broth" makes the bubble explicitly demonic. What rises from this cauldron is not natural but infernal — bubbles from hell.
