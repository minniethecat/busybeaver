**Alarum** (also spelled "alarum") is a Shakespearean stage direction indicating a trumpet or drum call to arms — a military signal of alarm, battle readiness, or urgent summons. In _Macbeth_, the alarum bell sounds at two structurally significant moments, both connected to ruptures in the natural and political order.

## Appearances

After Duncan's murder is discovered, Macduff cries: "Ring the alarum bell. — Murder and treason!" (010-034). The alarum here is not a military signal but a civic alarm: the castle must be roused because its king has been killed within its walls. The stage direction that follows — "_Bell rings_" — makes the alarum audible to the audience, transforming sound into a physical marker of the play's central crime.

The alarum returns in Act V, Scene V, as Macbeth faces the final assault on Dunsinane. Seyton reports that the Queen is dead, and Macbeth responds with the "Tomorrow and tomorrow" soliloquy. A messenger then enters with news of Birnam Wood's movement, and Macbeth's bravado cracks: "Ring the alarum bell! — Blow, wind! come, wrack! / At least we'll die with harness on our back" (025-015). The alarum now signals not communal alarm but individual desperation — Macbeth's last stand.

A third usage appears in the verse itself: "alarum'd by his sentinel, the wolf" (011-003), where the noun becomes a verb meaning "called to action," blending the military signal with the predatory natural world.

## Dramatic Function

The alarum bell operates as a structural hinge. Its first sounding (Act II) announces the discovery of the crime; its second (Act V) accompanies the criminal's final defiance. Between these two rings, the entire tragedy unfolds.

## Alarum as Theatrical Punctuation

The alarum bell belongs to a wider soundscape of signals in _Macbeth_ that mark moments of rupture. The [[knell]] — "the bell invites me. / Hear it not, Duncan; for it is a knell / That summons thee to heaven, or to hell" (007-017) — tolls for death, while the alarum rings for alarm. The [[knocking]] at the gate (010-001) punctuates the discovery of the murder with insistent, almost comic repetition. The [[thunder]] that accompanies the Witches marks their entrances as supernatural irruptions.

What distinguishes the alarum from these other sounds is its military origin. The knell is sacred, the knock domestic, the thunder cosmic — but the alarum is a signal of war, and its two occurrences map the play's transformation from civic tragedy to military confrontation. Duncan's murder is announced with an alarum because it is an act of war upon the body politic; Macbeth's final stand is heralded by the same sound because the war has now become literal. The alarum bell thus traces the arc from covert violence to open battle.
