# Macdonwald

Macdonwald is an offstage character in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. He is a Scottish rebel who leads an insurrection against King Duncan and is defeated and killed by Macbeth in the battle that takes place before the play opens. Macbeth's victory over Macdonwald earns him the admiration of the King and sets the stage for his eventual rise — and fall.

## Overview

Macdonwald is described in the Captain's report to King Duncan in Act I, Scene II. The Sergeant, fresh from the battlefield, recounts how Macbeth confronted the rebel: "brave Macbeth — well he deserves that name — / Disdaining Fortune, with his brandish'd steel, / Which smok'd with bloody execution, / Like Valour's minion, carv'd out his passage / Till he fac'd the slave" (002-004). Macbeth then "unseam'd him from the nave to the chaps, / And fix'd his head upon our battlements" (002-004).

The Captain characterizes Macdonwald as "merciless" and notes that he was "Worthy to be a rebel" (002-004), having gathered forces from the Western Isles — the Hebrides — kerns and gallowglasses supplied by Irish allies. The rebellion was serious enough that Duncan's kingdom seemed imperilled until Macbeth's decisive action.

## Dramatic Significance

Though Macdonwald never appears on stage, his defeat serves a crucial dramatic function. It establishes Macbeth's prowess as a warrior, his loyalty (at the outset) to Duncan, and the violent world in which the play unfolds. The bloody imagery of Macbeth "unseaming" the rebel foreshadows the violence Macbeth will later inflict on Duncan himself — violence that transforms him from loyal thane to regicide.
