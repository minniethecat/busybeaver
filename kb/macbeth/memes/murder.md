# Murder

Murder is the foundational act of transgression in *Macbeth*—a political and moral crime that sets in motion the play's entire tragic trajectory. Shakespeare presents murder not simply as physical killing but as a violation of natural, political, and divine order, with consequences that reverberate through the individual psyche, the body politic, and the cosmos itself.

## Definition & Context

In the world of *Macbeth*, murder carries layers of meaning beyond simple homicide. The killing of a king (regicide) was understood in Jacobean thought as a triple crime: against the person, against the political order, and against God, since the monarch was held to rule by divine right. Shakespeare amplifies this by making Duncan a guest in Macbeth's castle, adding the violation of hospitality—a sacred bond—to the murder's sinfulness. The play traces how a single act of murder begets more murders, as Macbeth seeks to secure his ill-gotten crown.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> My thought, whose murder yet is but fantastical, Shakes so my single state of man. (003-046)

In Act I, Scene III, even before Macbeth has fully committed to the deed, the mere thought of murder destabilizes his psyche. The "fantastical" murder—existing only in imagination—already "shakes" his rational function. This foreshadows how the actual murder will unmake him.

> MACBETH. Methought I heard a voice cry, "Sleep no more! Macbeth does murder sleep"—the innocent sleep; Sleep that knits up the ravell'd sleave of care. (009-022)

Immediately after killing Duncan, Macbeth hears a prophetic voice declaring that he has "murder'd sleep." This is the first supernatural consequence of the murder: Macbeth will never again know peace or rest. The metaphor of sleep as a knitted garment ("ravell'd sleave") emphasizes that murder has torn the fabric of natural order beyond repair.

> MACDUFF. Confusion now hath made his masterpiece! Most sacrilegious murder hath broke ope The Lord's anointed temple, and stole thence The life o' th' building. (010-028)

Macduff's discovery of Duncan's body frames the murder as sacrilege. The king's body is "The Lord's anointed temple"—murder is not merely homicide but the desecration of something sacred, "breaking open" what should have been inviolable.

### Deepening (Act III)

> MACBETH. For them the gracious Duncan have I murder'd; Put rancours in the vessel of my peace Only for them. (012-045)

By Act III, murder has become a tool of political consolidation. Macbeth's language reveals his state: he "filed" (defiled) his mind and put "rancours" in his peace—all for nothing if Banquo's heirs take the throne. The commodification of murder is complete: killing has become transactional, desacralized.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> ANGUS. Now does he feel His secret murders sticking on his hands; Now minutely revolts upbraid his faith-breach. (022-008)

In the final act, Angus observes that Macbeth's "secret murders" have become visible stains—they "stick on his hands" in a secular echo of Lady Macbeth's "damned spot." Murder has stripped Macbeth of legitimacy: his soldiers obey from fear, not loyalty. The political consequence of murder is isolation and the collapse of authority.
