**Menteith** is a Scottish thane who appears in the final act of _Macbeth_. He is among the noblemen who defect from [[macbeth|Macbeth]]'s tyranny to join the English-backed rebellion led by [[malcolm|Malcolm]], [[macduff|Macduff]], and [[siward|Siward]].

## Appearances

Menteith enters "with drum and colours" alongside [[caithness|Caithness]], [[angus|Angus]], and [[lennox|Lennox]] in Act V, Scene II (022-001). His first speech announces the approaching English force:

> The English power is near, led on by Malcolm,
> His uncle Siward, and the good Macduff.
> Revenges burn in them; for their dear causes
> Would to the bleeding and the grim alarm
> Excite the mortified man. (022-002)

The phrase "Revenges burn in them" captures the personal dimension of the rebellion — Macduff seeks vengeance for his slaughtered family, Malcolm for his murdered father [[duncan|Duncan]].

After Caithness describes Macbeth's fortification of [[dunsinane|Dunsinane]], Menteith asks the blunt question "What does the tyrant?" (022-006). When Angus elaborates on Macbeth's crumbling authority, Menteith responds with a reflection on the king's psychological state:

> Who, then, shall blame
> His pester'd senses to recoil and start,
> When all that is within him does condemn
> Itself for being there? (022-009)

This line offers a fleeting moment of sympathy — who can fault Macbeth's senses for rebelling when his own conscience condemns him? Yet the sympathy is immediately undercut by Caithness's call to march.

In Act V, Scene IV, Menteith marches with Siward and the rebel army as Malcolm orders each soldier to cut a bough from [[birnam|Birnam Wood]] to conceal their numbers (024-001).

## Character

Menteith has three speeches, alternating with Caithness in a pattern that gives the scene its choric quality. His questions ("What does the tyrant?") serve as prompts for the others' more elaborate assessments, while his own reflections touch on Macbeth's interior torment. Alongside Caithness, he represents the collective voice of the Scottish nobility in revolt.

## The Psychology of Tyranny

Menteith's single reflective speech — "Who then shall blame / His pester'd senses to recoil and start, / When all that is within him does condemn / Itself for being there?" (022-009) — is remarkable for its psychological acuity. In a play where most characters judge Macbeth from the outside (as "tyrant," "butcher," "dwarf in a giant's robe"), Menteith briefly imagines the experience from within: a mind at war with itself, senses recoiling from a self that conscience condemns.

The verb "pester'd" is distinctive — it suggests not grand torment but petty, insistent agitation, a mind nagged rather than assaulted. Paired with "recoil and start" (the language of flinching), the line describes a man who cannot bear his own presence. This interior portrait anticipates [[lady-macbeth|Lady Macbeth]]'s sleepwalking in the following scene, where guilt manifests as compulsive, repetitive action rather than heroic suffering.

By framing his observation as a question — "Who, then, shall blame..." — Menteith holds open the possibility of judgment even as he extends understanding. The rhetorical structure performs its own argument: we are invited to sympathise with Macbeth's torment, then reminded that the torment is self-inflicted.
