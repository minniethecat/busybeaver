# Sight (Vision and Blindness)

Sight in *Macbeth* is both a faculty and a moral test. From the "sorry sight" (009-013) of Duncan's murdered body through the "horrible sight" (018-056) of the apparitions' kings to Lady Macbeth's haunted sleepwalking with a light always beside her, the play returns repeatedly to what characters can see, what they refuse to see, and what they cannot un-see. The motif is conceptually linked to the play's central concern with appearance and reality: to see truly in Macbeth's Scotland is dangerous, and the play's most perceptive characters — the Porter, the Doctor — are marginal figures whose clarity carries no power.

## Definition & Context

In Shakespeare's theatre, sight was the privileged sense — plays were "spectacles," audiences went to "see" a play. *Macbeth* exploits this by repeatedly staging acts of looking and failing to look. The "sightless substances" (005-019) that Lady Macbeth invokes are spirits that work in darkness; the "sightless couriers of the air" (007-002) are the winds that will carry news of Duncan's murder to heaven. In both cases, "sightless" means invisible as well as unseeing — a double sense that captures the play's epistemological anxiety. What cannot be seen may nonetheless be real; what is seen may be deceptive. The Porter's comic gatekeeper routine literalises this anxiety at the threshold: who is admitted, who is seen, what passes through the gate.

## Textual Evidence

### The Murder Discovered (Act II)

> MACBETH. This is a sorry sight. (009-013)

Macbeth's understated line after killing Duncan is one of the play's most famous litotes. "Sorry" here carries its older sense of sorrowful and wretched, but also the hint of inadequacy: the sight is sorry because it is the consequence of a sorry (pitiful) act. Lady Macbeth dismisses the line — "A foolish thought, to say a sorry sight" (009-015) — but the sight of Duncan's body cannot be dismissed so easily. It motivates the grooms' execution, Malcolm and Donalbain's flight, and the general suspicion that will unravel Macbeth's rule.

> … destroy your sight / With a new Gorgon. (010-031)

Macduff, returning from the murder scene, warns Lady Macbeth not to look. The Gorgon — Medusa, whose gaze turned viewers to stone — is the classical figure for sight as destruction. Macduff's allusion suggests that seeing Duncan's body would petrify Lady Macbeth: not just horrify, but immobilise, turn her to stone. The irony is that Lady Macbeth has already seen far worse in her imagination; it is the sight itself, not the idea, that Macduff believes would undo her.

### Banquo's Ghost (Act III)

> MACBETH. Avaunt! and quit my sight! let the earth hide thee! (015-041)

Macbeth's command to Banquo's ghost to leave his sight is the play's most explicit staging of the gap between seeing and being seen. The ghost is visible only to Macbeth; Lady Macbeth and the thanes see nothing. "What sights, my lord?" Ross asks (015-049), and Lady Macbeth insists that Macbeth is seeing "a thing of custom…the very painting of your fear" (015-044). The scene dramatises the play's epistemological crisis: is private vision madness or superior perception? Macbeth, who has seen the witches and their prophecies, now sees a ghost his guests cannot.

### The Apparitions (Act IV)

> HECATE. … show the best of our delights. / I'll charm the air to give a sound, / While you perform your antic round. (018-057)

> MACBETH. Horrible sight! — Now I see 'tis true; / For the blood-bolter'd Banquo smiles upon me, / And points at them for his. (018-056)

The show of kings — Banquo's descendants stretching "to th' crack of doom" — is the play's most elaborate visual spectacle. The word "sight" here carries its full weight: what Macbeth sees is true (the Stuart line will inherit the throne) and it is horrible (because it confirms his own sterility). The "horrible sight" is the truth Macbeth has been trying not to see since the witches first hailed Banquo as father of kings.

### The Sleepwalking (Act V)

> DOCTOR. How came she by that light? (021-008)
> GENTLEWOMAN. Why, it stood by her: she has light by her continually; 'tis her command. (021-009)

Lady Macbeth, who in Act I invoked "sightless substances" and demanded darkness to conceal Duncan's murder, now cannot bear to be without light. The "light by her continually" is the play's final comment on sight and conscience: what she chose not to see while awake returns in sleep, and the light she now requires is both a literal candle and a metaphor for the moral vision she suppressed. The Doctor's question — "How came she by that light?" — is never answered, because the light has been there all along, burning in the conscience she tried to extinguish.
