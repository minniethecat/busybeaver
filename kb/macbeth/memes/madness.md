# Madness

Madness in *Macbeth* is the psychological endpoint of guilt. The play charts madness not as a clinical condition but as the inevitable consequence of violating moral order — a disintegration of mind that mirrors the disintegration of Scotland under Macbeth's tyranny. Two moments define the motif: Lady Macbeth's early, casual invocation of madness, and Lady Macduff's bitter diagnosis of her husband's flight.

## Lady Macbeth's Warning: "It Will Make Us Mad"

Immediately after Duncan's murder, as Macbeth stares at his bloody hands and hears a voice cry "Sleep no more," Lady Macbeth attempts to steady him with a warning that proves prophetic:

> These deeds must not be thought After these ways; so, it will make us mad. (009-021)

The line is delivered as practical advice — stop dwelling on what you have done — but it contains the germ of her own destruction. Lady Macbeth has not yet internalised the horror of the murder; she can still dismiss blood as something "a little water clears us of" (009-040). But the play's final act will return her to these words, in the sleepwalking scene where her mind, refusing to "stop thinking," replays the murder in endless, fragmentary loops.

## Lady Macduff's Diagnosis: "His Flight Was Madness"

Lady Macduff, abandoned with her children after Macduff flees to England, offers the play's most direct definition of the relationship between madness and fear:

> He had none: His flight was madness: when our actions do not, Our fears do make us traitors. (019-004)

The paradox is precise: Macduff's flight is "madness" because it is rationally inexplicable, yet it is also rational — he has gone to seek help against Macbeth. Lady Macduff cannot know this, and her charge exposes the gap between private knowledge and public action that defines the play's political world. Her next lines are brutally cut short by the arrival of the murderers, making her speech one of the play's most tragic dramatic ironies.

## The Arc of Madness

Madness follows a gendered arc in *Macbeth*. Macbeth's madness is externalised as violence — he sees Banquo's ghost, consults the witches, and slaughters Macduff's family. Lady Macbeth's madness is internalised — she walks in her sleep, washes invisible blood from her hands, and speaks in fragments that her attendants cannot decipher. Both trajectories end in death: his by the sword, hers by her own hand. The play offers no cure for what guilt has broken.
