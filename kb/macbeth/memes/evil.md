## Overview

Evil in *Macbeth* is not an abstraction but a force with agency, personification, and transformative power. The play charts evil's operation on multiple registers: the supernatural soliciting of the Weird Sisters, the psychological corruption of Macbeth and Lady Macbeth, the political violence of tyranny, and the cosmic disturbance of the natural order. Evil is both the poison and the infected body — the deed and the doer's deformation.

## Evil as Supernatural Soliciting

The play opens with the Witches, whose very presence signals that evil is afoot before a word of human dialogue is spoken: "Fair is foul, and foul is fair: / Hover through the fog and filthy air" (001-001). Their prophecy to Macbeth is technically true — he will become Thane of Cawdor, he will be king — but the truth is a trap. Banquo warns: "The instruments of darkness tell us truths, / Win us with honest trifles, to betray's / In deepest consequence" (001-003).

Macbeth's own description of the Witches' influence — "This supernatural soliciting / Cannot be ill, cannot be good" (001-003) — captures the ambiguity of evil's approach. It does not force; it invites. Macbeth's tragedy is that he accepts the invitation.

## Evil Incarnate: The "Fiend of Scotland"

By Act IV, Macbeth has become something more — and less — than human. Macduff names him "this fiend of Scotland" (017-003), and the epithet is earned. Malcolm's catalogue of Macbeth's crimes describes a tyrant whose evil has become ontological: "I grant him bloody, / Luxurious, avaricious, false, deceitful, / Sudden, malicious, smacking of every sin / That has a name" (017-003).

The Porter's grim comedy in Act II provides a theological frame: the knocking at the gate is compared to the porter of hell, admitting souls to "th' everlasting bonfire" (005-003). Macbeth's castle has become hell's antechamber, and the Porter's jest — "Who's there, i' th' name of Belzebub?" — unknowingly speaks the truth.

## Evil's Final Judgment

The play's closing lines name evil plainly. Malcolm speaks of "this dead butcher, and his fiend-like queen" (028-008), reducing Macbeth's reign to its moral essence. Macduff, entering with Macbeth's head, calls him a "hell-hound" (026-008) — the term is not hyperbole but diagnosis. Macbeth has become what he served.

At the very end, Siward's brief elegy for his son — "He's worth no more; / They say he parted well, and paid his score" (027-008) — offers the counter-image: a death that pays its moral debts, in contrast to Macbeth's accumulating deficit of evil.
