## Overview

Birds in *Macbeth* function as an atmospheric and symbolic register of the play's moral darkness. The raven, crow, rook, and owl appear at moments of crisis as omen-bearers, their cries and shadows marking the boundary between natural order and its violation. The crow-omen motif reinforces the play's central concern: when murder unseats nature, the very creatures of the air bear witness.

## The Raven as Murder's Herald

Lady Macbeth's first soliloquy invokes the raven as the voice of murderous intent. Reading Macbeth's letter, she declares: "The raven himself is hoarse / That croaks the fatal entrance of Duncan / Under my battlements" (001-005). The raven's croak is a death-knell, and Lady Macbeth welcomes it. She aligns herself not with the natural world that would resist murder, but with the dark omens that presage it.

> The raven himself is hoarse
> That croaks the fatal entrance of Duncan
> Under my battlements.
>
> (005-008)(005-009)(005-010)

The image is intensified by her subsequent invocation of darkness: "Come, thick night, / And pall thee in the dunnest smoke of hell" (001-005). The raven's hoarseness suggests that even this bird of ill-omen has exhausted its voice crying warning — yet Lady Macbeth proceeds.

## Crows and Rooks at the Threshold of Night

As Macbeth moves toward Banquo's murder in Act III, his language turns to the falling darkness and its winged inhabitants. "Light thickens; and the crow / Makes wing to th' rooky wood" (012-002) is one of the play's most compressed and evocative images. The crow's flight to join the rooks signals the end of day and the beginning of the time when "good things of day begin to droop and drowse, / Whiles night's black agents to their preys do rouse" (012-002).

> Light thickens; and the crow
> Makes wing to th' rooky wood.
> Good things of day begin to droop and drowse,
> Whiles night's black agents to their preys do rouse.
>
> (013-016)

The crow here is not a symbol but a participant — a creature whose natural behaviour aligns with the moral darkness settling over Scotland. The "rooky wood" (a wood full of rooks) becomes a gathering-place for darkness's agents.

## Birds of Prey and Cosmic Disorder

The old man's report to Ross in Act II extends the bird omen into evidence of cosmic disturbance. An owl — "a falcon, towering in her pride of place, / Was by a mousing owl hawk'd at, and kill'd" (007-003) — enacts in the animal kingdom the same inversion of hierarchy that Duncan's murder represents in the human. The falcon, king of birds, killed by a mousing owl that hunts mice, not hawks: nature's order mirrors Scotland's.

This chain of bird omens — raven, crow, owl — traces the play's descent from anticipation of murder (raven), through commission (owl kills falcon), to the thickening darkness of tyranny (crow flies to rooky wood).
