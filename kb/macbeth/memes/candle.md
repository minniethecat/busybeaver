## Definition

The candle in *Macbeth* operates as a material symbol of mortal life — fragile, temporary, and easily extinguished. Shakespeare deploys the candle at three structurally significant moments, each marking a stage in Macbeth's moral descent: the extinguishing of heavenly light on the night of Duncan's murder, Lady Macbeth's compulsive need for artificial light during her sleepwalking, and Macbeth's nihilistic recognition that life itself is merely a "brief candle" destined for darkness.

## Banquo's torches and the darkened heaven

The first candle reference occurs in Act II, Scene i, when Banquo hands Fleance his sword before retiring, remarking on the night sky:

> There's husbandry in heaven; / Their candles are all out. (008-043–008-044)

Banquo's observation is deceptively casual: "husbandry" suggests frugality, but the extinguished stars signal a cosmos that has withdrawn its light from the world — a world about to witness regicide. The "candles" of heaven are the stars themselves, and their absence creates the moral darkness in which Macbeth's crime becomes possible. Banquo himself carries a torch, making him the only source of light in a scene that ends with Macbeth's vision of the dagger — an object seen only in darkness.

## Lady Macbeth's perpetual light

By Act V, Scene i, the candle has become Lady Macbeth's psychological necessity. The Gentlewoman reports to the Doctor:

> She has light by her continually; 'tis her command. (021-022)

The woman who once invoked "thick night" and demanded that "stars, hide your fires" (004-014–004-015) to conceal her murderous intent now cannot endure darkness. The candle she clutches during her sleepwalking is the material form of a conscience she cannot extinguish — the very light she summoned darkness against has returned as guilt. Her earlier confidence that "a little water clears us of this deed" (009-036) has inverted: no amount of light can clear the stain she now sees on her hands.

## "Out, out, brief candle"

The candle metaphor reaches its philosophical climax in Act V, Scene v, when Macbeth responds to news of Lady Macbeth's death with the play's most famous nihilistic utterance:

> Out, out, brief candle! / Life's but a walking shadow; a poor player, / That struts and frets his hour upon the stage, / And then is heard no more: it is a tale / Told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, / Signifying nothing. (025-009)

The candle here is no longer a star or a bedside light but life itself — brief, easily snuffed, and leaving only darkness behind. The progression from Banquo's cosmic candles to Lady Macbeth's neurotic night-light to Macbeth's existential candle traces the play's darkening vision: the extinguishing of heavenly order, the collapse of individual conscience into compulsive ritual, and finally the recognition that all human life is, in the end, a brief illumination followed by eternal darkness.

## Dramatic function

The candle forms one half of the play's central binary opposition with darkness. Where darkness conceals, enables, and corrupts, the candle reveals, exposes, and ultimately mourns. Its progressive extinguishing across the three references — heavenly stars, bedside lamp, mortal life — mirrors the play's structural movement from cosmic disorder through psychological collapse to metaphysical despair. The candle is what Macbeth tries to extinguish and what Lady Macbeth cannot live without; in both cases, it proves beyond their control.
