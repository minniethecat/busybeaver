# Prophecies of the Witches

The Prophecies of the Witches are the supernatural engine of William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. Delivered on a heath in Act I and elaborated in a cavern in Act IV, these equivocal predictions ignite Macbeth's ambition, shape his choices, and seal his doom.

## Overview

On a heath after the battle, the three Witches intercept Macbeth and Banquo (003-013). They hail Macbeth with three titles: "All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Glamis!" (003-018) — his current title; "All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Cawdor!" (003-019) — a title he does not yet hold; and the devastating promise: "All hail, Macbeth! that shalt be king hereafter!" (003-020).

To Banquo, they offer darker pronouncements: "Lesser than Macbeth, and greater" (003-025), "Not so happy, yet much happier" (003-026), and the crucial prediction: "Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none" (003-027). Banquo's descendants, not Macbeth's, will inherit the throne. When Ross and Angus confirm Macbeth's new title as Thane of Cawdor, the partial fulfillment convinces him: "Glamis, and Thane of Cawdor: / The greatest is behind" (003-044).

Macbeth returns to the Witches in Act IV, Scene I, where they conjure three Apparitions. The First — an Armed Head — warns "Beware Macduff" (018-030). The Second — a Bloody Child — declares that "none of woman born / Shall harm Macbeth" (018-036). The Third — a Crowned Child with a tree — promises Macbeth shall never be vanquished until "Great Birnam wood to high Dunsinane hill / Shall come against him" (018-045). All three come true, but not as Macbeth expects: Macduff was "from his mother's womb / Untimely ripp'd" (028-008), and Birnam Wood moves in the hands of Malcolm's soldiers.

The prophecies embody the play's central question: do they foretell the future, or create it? They are technically true but deliberately misleading — "juggling fiends... That palter with us in a double sense" (028-009).

## The First Prophecies

The Witches greet Macbeth and Banquo on the heath after the battle (003-013). Their three-fold hailing of Macbeth — Thane of Glamis, Thane of Cawdor, and King hereafter — plants the seed of ambition. Banquo's prophecy — that he shall father kings — makes him a threat. The immediate fulfillment of the Cawdor prophecy (003-042) convinces Macbeth of their truth. He confides to Banquo: "Do you not hope your children shall be kings?" (003-046). The prophecies work not by magic but by psychology: they speak the desire already latent in Macbeth's heart.

## The Second Prophecies

Desperate and isolated by Act IV, Macbeth seeks the Witches in their cavern (018-001). He demands answers and receives them through the Apparitions. The prophecies seem to promise safety — no man of woman born can harm him, he is safe until Birnam Wood moves — but each is an equivocation. Macbeth leaves the cavern emboldened, ordering the massacre of Macduff's family (019-039 through 019-047). His false confidence, born of misunderstood prophecy, accelerates his descent.
