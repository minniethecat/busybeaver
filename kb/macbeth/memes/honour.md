# Honour

Honour is the moral currency of the world Macbeth inhabits—and the first thing he sacrifices in his ascent to power. At the play's opening, honour defines the social order: men earn it through martial valour, kings bestow it as reward, and the loss of honour means death. By the play's end, honour has been so systematically violated that its restoration requires armed invasion and the death of the tyrant. Shakespeare traces honour's collapse from a lived code to a hollow word, and finally to a standard that can only be reclaimed through blood.

## Definition & Context

In the feudal Scotland of the play, honour was not an abstract virtue but a measurable quantity: it was conferred by the king (titles, lands, ceremonies), maintained through acts of loyalty, and lost through treason. The opening scenes establish honour as the play's governing value: the bleeding Captain's wounds "smack of honour" (002-009), and Duncan rewards Macbeth with the traitor Cawdor's title. But the play immediately destabilizes this economy—what honour meant by the old Thane of Cawdor, who died "as one that had been studied in his death" (004-003), Macbeth will betray the instant he becomes the new Thane.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> DUNCAN. They smack of honour both.—Go, get him surgeons. (002-009)

Duncan's praise of the bleeding Captain—whose wounds "smack of honour"—establishes the feudal equation: physical suffering in service of the king is the visible sign of honour. The word "smack" (taste of) suggests that honour is a sensory, embodied quality, not just an abstract one. This concreteness makes Macbeth's later violation all the more stark.

> MACBETH. He hath honour'd me of late; and I have bought Golden opinions from all sorts of people. (007-006)

Macbeth's hesitation before the murder is framed in the language of honour: Duncan has recently "honour'd" him, and he has "bought golden opinions"—honour as earned reputation. To kill the king would be to throw away this accumulated social capital. He does it anyway.

### Deepening (Act III)

> BANQUO. New honours come upon him, Like our strange garments, cleave not to their mould But with the aid of use. (003-049)

Banquo's metaphor of honour as ill-fitting garments is the play's first critical commentary on the honour system. Macbeth's new title of Thane of Cawdor sits on him like borrowed clothes—an image that foreshadows the illegitimacy of his eventual kingship.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> MACDUFF. I shall do so; But I must also feel it as a man. (019-017)

When Malcolm tests Macduff's honour, Macduff's reply defines true honour not as stoic suppression of feeling but as the capacity to *feel*—and still act rightly. This redefinition of honour as humane responsiveness rather than martial performance is the standard against which Macbeth's hollow tyranny is judged.
