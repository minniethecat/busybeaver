# The Grooms' Murder

The Grooms' Murder is a crucial secondary killing in *Macbeth*, occurring in Act II Scene iii. After Duncan's body is discovered, Macbeth kills the two drugged chamber grooms — ostensibly in a fit of righteous fury, but actually to eliminate potential witnesses and control the narrative of Duncan's death.

## Dramatic Context

The murder occurs during the chaotic discovery scene (Act II Scene iii) in Macbeth's castle. Macduff has just found Duncan's body and sounded the alarm. Lady Macbeth faints (or feigns fainting). In the confusion, Macbeth slips away and kills the grooms, then returns to justify the act. This is the first moment when Macbeth must perform innocence before a public audience, and it inaugurates his career as a dissembling tyrant.

## The Discovery and Justification

Lennox describes the scene that greets the household upon Duncan's body being found:

> "Those of his chamber, as it seem'd, had done't:
> Their hands and faces were all badg'd with blood;
> So were their daggers, which, unwip'd, we found
> Upon their pillows. They star'd, and were distracted;
> No man's life was to be trusted with them." (010-052)

Lady Macbeth has already staged the scene — the grooms have been drugged and their daggers smeared with Duncan's blood (see [[duncans-murder]]). Lennox's report unwittingly describes her handiwork, confirming that the cover-up was effective. The detail that the grooms "star'd, and were distracted" suggests the disorientation of drugged men waking to a nightmare.

Macbeth's justification for killing them is one of the play's most chilling performances of false virtue:

> "Who can be wise, amaz'd, temperate, and furious,
> Loyal and neutral, in a moment? No man:
> Th' expedition of my violent love
> Outrun the pauser, reason. Here lay Duncan,
> His silver skin lac'd with his golden blood;
> And his gash'd stabs look'd like a breach in nature
> For ruin's wasteful entrance." (010-055)

The phrase "violent love" is an oxymoron that encapsulates Macbeth's rhetorical strategy: to recast a cold-blooded silencing of witnesses as an outburst of passionate loyalty. His description of Duncan's body — "silver skin lac'd with golden blood" — is oddly aestheticised, a poetic excess that Lady Macbeth (who faints or feigns fainting at this moment) may recognise as dangerously overwrought.

## Sequence

1. Macduff discovers Duncan's body and raises the alarm (010-034–010-041)
2. Lady Macbeth enters, then is carried out after appearing distressed (010-042–010-062)
3. Macbeth, during the chaos, slips away and kills the two grooms
4. Macbeth returns and justifies the killing, claiming his "violent love" for Duncan overwhelmed his reason
5. Macduff questions the killing, asking "Wherefore did you so?" — his first expression of suspicion toward Macbeth (010-054)

## Immediate Consequences

Macduff's pointed question ("Wherefore did you so?") introduces the first crack in the facade of unity. Malcolm and Donalbain, fearing for their own safety, decide to flee Scotland — an act that allows Macbeth to pin suspicion on them.

## Long-term Significance

This event reveals Macbeth's strategic cunning in the immediate aftermath of his crime. Rather than panicking, he acts decisively to close off lines of inquiry. The killing of the grooms is also the moment when Macduff begins to suspect Macbeth, planting the seed of their eventual confrontation. Lady Macbeth's fainting — whether real or performed — shows her own role in the cover-up. Notably, Macbeth's justification speech is the longest he delivers in the discovery scene, and its rhetorical excess itself arouses suspicion: he protests too much.
