The **sword** in _Macbeth_ is the instrument of legitimate violence — the weapon of the soldier, the tool of honor, and the final arbiter of justice. Unlike the dagger (which represents hallucination and murderous intent), the sword belongs to the daylight world of combat between equals. Its trajectory through the play traces the collapse of honorable warfare into tyranny, and its restoration through Macduff's avenging blade.

## The Honorable Sword

Banquo sets the standard for the honorable sword in the play's opening moments of darkness. When Macbeth approaches with murderous intent, Banquo instinctively reaches for his weapon: "Hold, take my sword" (008-006). Banquo's sword is a tool of vigilance — held in readiness against the unknown, but never drawn in treachery.

The sword of honor reappears in Act IV when Malcolm, testing Macduff's loyalty, urges him to "Hold fast the mortal sword, and, like good men, / Bestride our down-fall'n birthdom" (020-003). The "mortal sword" is the weapon of patriotic defense — held not for personal gain but for the restoration of a wounded nation.
> Hold, take my sword.—There's husbandry in heaven;
> Their candles are all out. Take thee that too.
> A heavy summons lies like lead upon me,
> And yet I would not sleep. Merciful powers,
> Restrain in me the cursed thoughts that nature
> Gives way to in repose!
>
> (008-006)


## The Sword of Kings

Malcolm reveals the deeper symbolism of the sword when he speaks of "the sword of our slain kings" (020-017). The sword is not merely a weapon but an emblem of legitimate sovereignty — passed from monarch to monarch, carrying the authority of rightful rule. Macbeth, having seized the crown through murder, holds the physical sword of kingship but cannot claim its symbolic legitimacy.

## Macbeth's Desperate Blade

By Act V, Macbeth's relationship to the sword has become desperate. He boasts that "swords I smile at, weapons laugh to scorn" — believing himself invincible through the witches' prophecy (027-011). But his bravado conceals a deeper fear. When all hope is lost, he contemplates falling on his own sword: "Why should I play the Roman fool, and die / On mine own sword?" (028-002). Even in defeat, Macbeth cannot perform the honorable Roman suicide — he lacks the moral standing for even that last gesture of dignity.

## Macduff's Avenging Sword

Macduff reclaims the sword's honor. "My voice is in my sword," he declares, making the blade not merely a weapon but a statement — the physical articulation of justice (028-005). When he tells Macbeth "with my sword / I'll prove the lie thou speak'st" (027-009), the sword becomes an instrument of truth: it will demonstrate, through combat, what words alone cannot establish.

Malcolm's command — "Be this the whetstone of your sword. Let grief / Convert to anger" (020-065) — completes the arc. The sword, sharpened by grief and wielded in righteous anger, restores the order that Macbeth's dagger destroyed.

## Sword vs. Dagger

The distinction between sword and dagger is thematically crucial in _Macbeth_. The [[dagger]] represents the hidden, treacherous, hallucinatory violence of assassination. The sword represents open, honorable combat between declared enemies. Macbeth's tragedy is that he exchanges the sword for the dagger — and finds, at the end, that only Macduff's sword can write the final word.
