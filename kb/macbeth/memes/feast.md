# Feast

In *Macbeth*, the feast is more than a meal — it is the ritual centre of rightful kingship, the visible expression of social bonds, and the stage on which Macbeth's unfitness to rule is most spectacularly demonstrated.

## Definition & Context

The feast in Shakespeare's Scotland carries the full weight of medieval hospitality and kinship obligation. A king who cannot host a feast — or whose feast is disrupted by the ghost of a man he has murdered — is a king who has lost the sacred bond with his subjects. Macbeth's coronation banquet in Act III Scene iv is the turning point: it should be the moment that cements his kingship, but Banquo's ghost shatters the ceremony and exposes Macbeth's guilt to his thanes. The play traces a descent from Duncan as "chief nourisher in life's feast" to Macbeth's "suppertime" of blood. Feasting imagery also carries Eucharistic undertones — the host and the feast are sacred — making Macbeth's violation not just political but religious.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> Chief nourisher in life's feast. (009-022)

Duncan is described as the "chief nourisher" — the king as host of the nation's feast. The metaphor joins kingship, hospitality, and nourishment into a single ideal that Macbeth will systematically destroy.

> Fail not our feast. (012-011)

Macbeth's terse command to Banquo — "Fail not our feast" — is heavy with dramatic irony. Banquo will indeed fail to attend, but not through choice: Macbeth has arranged his murder even as he extends the invitation. The feast becomes a trap, the host a murderer.

### The Disrupted Banquet (Act III Scene iv)

> The feast is sold / That is not often vouched, while 'tis a-making, / 'Tis given with welcome. (015-016)

Lady Macbeth chides Macbeth for neglecting his hosting duties while he paces, distracted by the news of Banquo's death. Her words about the proper conduct of feasting — the host should "give welcome" — apply with savage irony to the man who has just had his friend killed.

> You do not give the cheer. (015-016)

Lady Macbeth's reproach cuts to the heart of the banquet scene: Macbeth, the host, cannot perform the ritual of hospitality because his guilt has already isolated him from the community the feast is meant to affirm.

### The Tyrant's Feast (Acts IV–V)

> His presence at the tyrant's feast, I hear, / Macduff lives in disgrace. (017-002)

Macduff's absence from Macbeth's feast is a political statement — refusal to attend the tyrant's table is refusal to legitimise his rule. The feast has become an instrument of surveillance and coercion rather than community.

> Free from our feasts and banquets bloody knives. (017-003)

The thanes' wish to be "free from our feasts and banquets bloody knives" collapses the distinction between the feast and the slaughterhouse. Under Macbeth, every banquet is a potential murder scene.
