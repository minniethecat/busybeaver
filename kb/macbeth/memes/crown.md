# Crown

The crown is the object around which *Macbeth*'s entire action revolves. It is what the witches' prophecy promises, what ambition reaches for, what murder secures, and what guilt cannot enjoy. Shakespeare makes the crown more than a symbol of kingship: it is a physical object charged with metaphysical meaning—the visible sign of a divine order that Macbeth's usurpation violates. From "the golden round" (005-002) to the "fruitless crown" (012-040) to the "crown that sears mine eyeballs" (018-056), the crown traces Macbeth's journey from desire to possession to torment.

## Definition & Context

In Jacobean political theology, the crown was the earthly manifestation of divine authority. The doctrine of the divine right of kings held that the monarch was God's anointed representative; to seize the crown was to rebel against God himself. Shakespeare encodes this understanding into the play's imagery: when Macbeth takes the crown by murder, nature itself revolts—"a falcon, tow'ring in her pride of place, was by a mousing owl hawk'd at and kill'd" (011-005). The crown is not merely a prize but a sacred trust, and its violation disorders the cosmos.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> BANQUO. That, trusted home, Might yet enkindle you unto the crown, Besides the Thane of Cawdor. (003-045)

Banquo's warning is precise: the witches' words "might yet enkindle you unto the crown"—the verb "enkindle" suggests a fire lit, a desire ignited. The crown is not yet possessed but already burns in Macbeth's imagination.

> LADY MACBETH. Thy letters have transported me beyond This ignorant present, and I feel now The future in the instant. (005-002)

Lady Macbeth's response to her husband's letter is the language of *already* possessing what is not yet hers. She speaks of the crown as though it were inevitable, collapsing future into present through sheer force of will.

### Deepening (Act III)

> MACBETH. Upon my head they plac'd a fruitless crown, And put a barren sceptre in my gripe. (012-040)

After Banquo's murder, Macbeth reflects on the prophecy that Banquo's descendants, not his own, will inherit the throne. The crown is "fruitless"—it secures power in the present but guarantees nothing for the future. This is the first moment Macbeth understands that possession of the crown is not the same as enjoyment of kingship.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> MACBETH. Thy crown does sear mine eyeballs. (018-056)

When Banquo's ghost appears at the witches' cavern, wearing a crown, Macbeth's language turns from desire to pain: the crown "sears" his eyes. The object he killed to obtain now causes him physical torment. It is the play's definitive image of the crown as curse rather than prize.
