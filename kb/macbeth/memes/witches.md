The **Witches** — also called the **Weird Sisters** — are the supernatural agents who set the tragedy of _Macbeth_ in motion. Their prophecies drive Macbeth's ambition and his eventual destruction, making them the play's central mechanism of fate, equivocation, and moral chaos.

## Nature and Appearance

The witches first appear on a heath amid thunder and lightning, immediately establishing their connection to disorder in the natural world:

> "When shall we three meet again? In thunder, lightning, or in rain?" (001-002)

Shakespeare deliberately leaves their physical nature ambiguous. Banquo describes them as "withered" and "wild in their attire," creatures that "look not like th' inhabitants o' th' earth" — beings that seem to exist between human and spirit, male and female, natural and supernatural. This ambiguity is their power: they cannot be categorized, and therefore cannot be safely dismissed.

Their association with the number three — three witches, three prophecies to Macbeth, three apparitions — reinforces their folkloric roots in the triple goddess or the three Fates of classical mythology. Hecate, the goddess of witchcraft, appears as their mistress in Act III Scene v, confirming their place within a larger supernatural hierarchy (016-001).

## The Prophecies

The witches deliver three sets of prophecies that structure the entire play:

1. **To Macbeth**: Thane of Glamis (already), Thane of Cawdor (soon), and "King hereafter"
2. **To Banquo**: "Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none"
3. **The Apparitions**: Beware Macduff; none of woman born shall harm Macbeth; Macbeth shall not be vanquished until Birnam Wood comes to Dunsinane

Each prophecy is technically true but fatally misleading — the essence of equivocation. The witches tell Macbeth what he wants to hear while concealing the destruction that awaits him. Their words are "imperfect speakers" whose truths are traps.

## Dramatic Function

The witches serve as the play's supernatural framing device. They appear at the beginning, at key pivot points, and at the climax. Every entrance is marked by thunder (001-001) (003-001) (016-001) (018-001), making the weather itself a character — an external sign of the moral disorder their presence brings.

Their incantations — "Double, double toil and trouble; Fire burn, and cauldron bubble" — are among the most recognizable lines in English drama. The cauldron scene (Act IV Scene i) with its "eye of newt and toe of frog" [018-001–018-058] catalogues the grotesque ingredients of their dark art, creating a visceral image of unnatural power.

The witches dance and vanish at the end of their final scene, leaving Macbeth to face the consequences of their prophecies alone (018-058).

## Relationship to the Weird Sisters

The witches are also called the **Weird Sisters** — a name that carries the older meaning of "wyrd" (fate or destiny). Already covered as an organization in this wiki, [[the-weird-sisters]] represent the witches' collective identity. The present page focuses on the witches as a concept: their supernatural nature, their dramatic function, and their thematic role in the play's exploration of fate, free will, and moral responsibility.
