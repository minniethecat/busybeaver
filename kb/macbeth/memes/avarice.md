# Avarice

**Avarice**, or insatiable greed, occupies a brief but pivotal moment in *Macbeth*: Malcolm's self-accusation in Act IV Scene iii that his own "staunchless avarice" would make him a worse king than Macbeth. The confession is a test, a lie designed to probe Macduff's loyalty—but it also sketches a moral landscape in which greed rivals tyranny as a destroyer of kingdoms.

## Definition & Context

In Christian moral theology, avarice (*avaritia*) is one of the seven deadly sins—the disordered desire for wealth that closes the heart to charity and justice. In the political theology of Shakespeare's England, a king's avarice was especially dangerous: a greedy king would confiscate nobles' lands ("cut off the nobles for their lands"), turning the kingdom into a predatory enterprise rather than a commonwealth. Malcolm's self-slander in Act IV Scene iii draws on this rich tradition.

## Textual Evidence

### Malcolm's Confession (Act IV)

> A staunchless avarice, that, were I king, / I should cut off the nobles for their lands (020-016)

In his dialogue with Macduff, Malcolm claims that if he were king, his greed would know no limit—"staunchless" meaning unstoppable, unquenchable. He imagines himself as a king who murders nobles not for political reasons but for property, making the crown an instrument of theft. The word "staunchless" is the key: it is the same quality that drives Macbeth's murderous ambition, a hunger that no satisfaction can close.

> This avarice / Sticks deeper; grows with more pernicious root (020-017)

Malcolm elaborates: his avarice is not a surface vice but one that "sticks deeper"—rooted in his character, organic, growing. The agricultural metaphor ("grows," "root") echoes the play's broader imagery of planting and harvest. Where Duncan "planted" Macbeth and expected growth, Malcolm claims to have roots of corruption that would yield a poisoned crop. Macduff's response—that Scotland has "wealth enough" to fill such greed—is the pivot on which the loyalty test turns. When Malcolm then reveals his confession was a lie, the contrast between feigned avarice and genuine tyranny illuminates both.

### The King-Becoming Graces

> But I have none: the king-becoming graces, / As justice, verity, temp'rance, stableness, / Bounty, perseverance, mercy, lowliness (020-018)

Immediately after confessing avarice, Malcolm denies possessing any of the "king-becoming graces" — a catalogue of royal virtues that functions as the play's most explicit statement of political philosophy. The list is carefully ordered: justice (the root of legitimate rule), verity (truthfulness), temperance (self-restraint, avarice's direct opposite), stableness (constancy), bounty (generosity), perseverance, mercy, lowliness (humility). By claiming to lack these graces while possessing avarice instead, Malcolm constructs a photographic negative of ideal kingship — and then reveals the entire portrait is a test.

| King-Becoming Grace | Malcolm's Claimed Opposite |
|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Justice | Confiscating nobles' lands for personal gain |
| Verity | Strategic lying to test Macduff's loyalty |
| Temperance | "Staunchless" greed that no satisfaction closes |
| Bounty | "More-having" as "sauce" — appetite disguised as enjoyment |
| Mercy | "The sword of our slain kings" — violence as policy |

## Thematic Significance

Malcolm's discussion of avarice serves three dramatic functions:

| Function | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| **Loyalty test** | Malcolm probes whether Macduff would accept a corrupt king—the answer, eventually, is no |
| **Moral contrast** | Feigned avarice makes Macbeth's genuine tyranny visible by comparison |
| **Political theory** | The dialogue stages a debate about what makes a legitimate king and what destroys one |

The scene is one of the play's most explicit moments of political philosophy. When Macduff finally exclaims "Fit to govern! / No, not to live!" — rejecting the feigned corrupt Malcolm — the test is passed. Macduff would rather a Scotland without a king than a Scotland with a corrupt one, proving his loyalty is to the office, not the person.
