# Doubling and Duplicity

In *Macbeth*, nothing is single. The play is structured around patterns of doubling—paired characters, echoed language, and the fundamental duplicity of "fair is foul, and foul is fair."

## Definition & Context

The motif of doubling operates at every level of *Macbeth*. The play's incantatory language doubles words and phrases ("double, double, toil and trouble"); its plot doubles murders (Duncan, then Banquo, then Macduff's family); its characters double each other (Macbeth and Banquo as mirror-figures, the two Macduffs—father and son). This structural doubleness is not decorative but thematic: it enacts the play's central concern with the gap between appearance and reality, truth and equivocation. What appears single—a loyal thane, a prophecy, a word—proves always to have a double meaning.

## Textual Evidence

### Doubled Language (Acts I–IV)

> As cannons overcharg'd with double cracks (002-008)

The Captain's report of Macbeth's battlefield prowess in Act I Scene ii introduces doubling as a figure of excessive force. Macbeth fights with "double cracks"—he is already, in the language applied to him, a figure of doubleness before he becomes a figure of duplicity.

> He's here in double trust: / First, as I am his kinsman and his subject, / Strong both against the deed; then, as his host (007-002)

Macbeth's soliloquy in Act I Scene vii is structured around the "double trust" Duncan places in him. The enumeration—kinsman, subject, host—makes the betrayal triply damning. Macbeth's moral clarity in this moment is precisely an awareness of doubleness: he knows exactly how many bonds he must break.

> Double, double, toil and trouble; / Fire, burn; and cauldron, bubble. (018-006, 018-008, 018-010)

The witches' refrain is the play's most explicit linguistic doubling. The word "double" is itself doubled, and the refrain is itself repeated three times—a structural echo of the three witches, three prophecies, and three apparitions. The doubling here is incantatory: language multiplied becomes spell, and spell becomes reality.

> I'll make assurance double sure, / And take a bond of fate (018-038)

Macbeth's response to the apparitions in Act IV Scene i is to demand more certainty—he will "make assurance double sure" by killing Macduff's family. The phrase is bitterly ironic: the "double sure" assurance is murder, and the "bond of fate" is a contract signed in blood. Doubling here is not protection but self-destruction.

### Doubled Deception (Act V)

> That palter with us in a double sense; / That keep the word of promise to our ear, / And break it to our hope. (028-009)

Macbeth's final recognition, spoken before his death in Act V Scene viii, is that the "juggling fiends" (the Weird Sisters) dealt in "double sense." The prophecies were true in one sense and false in another—equivocal. What Macbeth thought was a promise was a trap, and the doubling he trusted ("charmed life," "none of woman born") was duplicity from the start.

## Critical Perspectives

The doubling motif has been central to deconstructive readings of the play. The witches' "double, double" encodes what poststructuralist critics identify as the play's linguistic instability—words that mean one thing and its opposite, prophecies that are true and false. Psychoanalytic criticism reads doubling as the structure of the uncanny (Freud's *unheimlich*): Banquo's ghost is Macbeth's double returned to haunt him. Historically, the doubling may also reflect Jacobean anxieties about equivocation, the Jesuit doctrine (condemned at the Gunpowder Plot trials) that one could speak truth in one sense while meaning another.
