# Kingship

Kingship in *Macbeth* is the central political concept around which the play's moral universe is organized. Shakespeare presents two contrasting models of rule: the sacred, divinely-ordained kingship of Duncan and the illegitimate, blood-bought tyranny of Macbeth. Through this contrast, the play explores what makes a ruler legitimate and what happens to a kingdom when kingship is corrupted.

## Definition & Context

In Jacobean England, the doctrine of the divine right of kings held that monarchs derived their authority directly from God. To kill a king was therefore not merely murder but sacrilege—a crime against both the political and cosmic order. *Macbeth*, written shortly after the Gunpowder Plot (1605), is deeply engaged with questions of legitimate succession and the nature of true kingship. Shakespeare presents Duncan as a model of sacred kingship, while Macbeth's reign demonstrates the corrosive effects of illegitimate rule on both the ruler and the kingdom.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> That now Sweno, the Norways' king, craves composition; Nor would we deign him burial of his men Till he disbursed, at Saint Colme's Inch, ten thousand dollars to our general use. (002-019)

The play opens with a vision of kingship as victory in battle—Duncan's forces have defeated the Norwegian king. This establishes Duncan as a legitimate military leader whose authority is earned through conquest and courage. The mention of "Saint Colme's Inch" ties kingship to sacred geography, reinforcing the divine dimension of royal authority.

> MACBETH. [Aside.] If chance will have me king, why, chance may crown me Without my stir. (003-048)

Macbeth's first response to the witches' prophecy reveals his ambivalent relationship to kingship. He hopes kingship will come without his "stir"—without action, without violence. This line captures the tension between fate and free will that defines the play's treatment of royal ambition.

> MACDUFF. Confusion now hath made his masterpiece! Most sacrilegious murder hath broke ope The Lord's anointed temple, and stole thence The life o' th' building. (010-028)

Macduff's lament over Duncan's body frames kingship explicitly in sacred terms. The king is "The Lord's anointed"—killing him is "sacrilegious murder" that "broke ope" a temple. This language establishes that Duncan's murder is more than homicide; it is the destruction of a sacred bond between God, king, and kingdom.

### Deepening (Act III)

> MACBETH. For them the gracious Duncan have I murder'd; Put rancours in the vessel of my peace Only for them; and mine eternal jewel Given to the common enemy of man, To make them kings—the seed of Banquo kings! (012-045–012-048)

Here Macbeth confronts the ultimate failure of his usurpation: he has murdered Duncan and damned his own soul not to found a dynasty, but to benefit Banquo's heirs. The witches' prophecy that Banquo's children "shall be kings" (003-034) haunts him. Macbeth's kingship is sterile—he has "a fruitless crown" (012-040)—while true royal succession belongs to Banquo's line.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> MALCOLM. But I have spoke with one that saw him die; Who did report that very frankly he confess'd his treasons, Implor'd your Highness' pardon, and set forth A deep repentance. Nothing in his life Became him like the leaving it. (004-003)

Malcolm's report of the former Thane of Cawdor's death provides a model of penitent kingship. The traitor dies well, confessing and repenting—a contrast to Macbeth's descent into hardened tyranny. This passage, spoken by the future king, establishes the qualities of legitimate rule: justice, mercy, and the capacity for moral judgment.

> MALCOLM. What I am truly, Is thine and my poor country's to command. (020-021)

In the play's final act, Malcolm demonstrates what distinguishes legitimate kingship from tyranny: he does not seek power for himself but offers himself as servant to the country. The "healing benediction" (020-029) that the English king bestows on subjects suffering from "the Evil" (scrofula) provides the play's emblem of sacred kingship restored.

## Character Associations

| Character | Relationship to Kingship | Key Scene |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------|
| Duncan | The model of sacred, legitimate kingship. His murder is the primal violation that disorders the entire kingdom. | 002-019 |
| Macbeth | The usurper who seizes kingship through murder. His reign demonstrates that illegitimate rule corrupts the ruler and destroys the kingdom. | 012-040 |
| Malcolm | The legitimate heir who restores true kingship. He tests Macduff's loyalty before accepting the throne, showing the prudence of a good ruler. | 020-021 |
| Banquo | The progenitor of a line of future kings, according to the witches' prophecy. His virtue contrasts with Macbeth's crime. | 003-027 |
