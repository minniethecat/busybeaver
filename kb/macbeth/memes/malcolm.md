# Malcolm

Malcolm is Duncan's eldest son and the legitimate heir to the Scottish throne. After his father's murder, he flees to England — a strategic retreat that Macbeth misreads as guilt. In Act IV, Malcolm tests Macduff's loyalty through an extraordinary scene of feigned villainy, and in Act V he returns at the head of an English army to reclaim his birthright. Unlike Macbeth, Malcolm understands kingship as service rather than possession, and his coronation speech at the play's end establishes the values that will govern a restored Scotland.

## Flight and Strategic Patience

After Duncan's murder, Malcolm and his brother Donalbain flee Scotland, recognizing that "there's daggers in men's smiles" ((010-075)):

> MALCOLM. What will you do? Let's not consort with them.
> To show an unfelt sorrow is an office
> Which the false man does easy. I'll to England.
> DONALBAIN. To Ireland, I. Our separated fortune
> Shall keep us both the safer. ((010-076))

The decision is politically astute: by separating, they prevent Macbeth from killing both heirs at once. But it also allows Macbeth to accuse them of the murder — "We hear our bloody cousins are bestow'd / In England and in Ireland, not confessing / Their cruel parricide" ((012-012)). Malcolm's absence from Scotland during Macbeth's reign is a political liability that he must overcome in Act IV.

## The Testing of Macduff

The scene between Malcolm and Macduff in England (Act IV, Scene III) is the play's longest sustained dialogue and its most explicit discussion of kingship. Malcolm, uncertain whether Macduff is Macbeth's agent, tests him by pretending to be a worse tyrant than Macbeth:

> MALCOLM. ...there's no bottom, none,
> In my voluptuousness. Your wives, your daughters,
> Your matrons, and your maids, could not fill up
> The cistern of my lust; and my desire
> All continent impediments would o'erbear
> That did oppose my will. Better Macbeth
> Than such an one to reign. ((020-013))

He catalogs vices he does not possess — lust, greed, lack of kingly virtues — until Macduff, despairing, cries out: "O Scotland, Scotland!" and "Fit to govern? / No, not to live!" ((020-038)). Only then does Malcolm reveal the test:

> MALCOLM. Macduff, this noble passion,
> Child of integrity, hath from my soul
> Wip'd the black scruples, reconcil'd my thoughts
> To thy good truth and honour. ((020-043))

The testing scene establishes Malcolm's political intelligence: he understands that the difference between a king and a tyrant lies not in power but in virtue, and he verifies Macduff's character before trusting him.

## The "King-Becoming Graces"

In the same scene, Malcolm articulates the play's most complete theory of legitimate kingship. He lists the "king-becoming graces" — "justice, verity, temperance, stableness, / Bounty, perseverance, mercy, lowliness, / Devotion, patience, courage, fortitude" ((020-035)) — and claims to possess none of them. The irony is that he does possess them; his very self-abasement proves his fitness by showing he understands what a king should be. The catalogue serves as the play's moral checklist, each virtue a direct negation of Macbeth's reign.

## The Return and Restoration

In Act V, Malcolm returns at the head of Siward's English army. His military strategy — using branches from Birnam Wood as camouflage ((026-001)) — fulfills the witches' prophecy that Macbeth will not fall "until / Great Birnam wood to high Dunsinane hill / Shall come against him" ((017-030)). Malcolm's tactical cleverness is both a military victory and a symbolic one: nature itself, appropriated by the rightful king, marches against the usurper.

## Coronation Speech

Malcolm's final speech establishes the principles of his restored rule:

> MALCOLM. ...We shall not spend a large expense of time
> Before we reckon with your several loves,
> And make us even with you. My Thanes and kinsmen,
> Henceforth be Earls; the first that ever Scotland
> In such an honour nam'd. ((028-039))

He creates Scotland's first earls, elevating his supporters — a gesture of integration that contrasts with Macbeth's isolation. The speech also promises to call home "our exil'd friends abroad / That fled the snares of watchful tyranny" and to bring "to the account" Macbeth's "fiend-like queen" and "cruel ministers." The emphasis is on restoration, reckoning, and healing — the language of legitimate kingship rather than tyranny.
