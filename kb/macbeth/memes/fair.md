# Fair

The paradoxical concept of "fair" in *Macbeth* — denoting surface goodness, beauty, or justice that conceals or is inverted into foulness, introduced in the witches' opening incantation and threading through every act of the play.

## Definition & Context

In *Macbeth*, "fair" is never simply what it seems. From the first scene, Shakespeare establishes a world where moral categories are inverted: what appears fair is foul, and what seems foul may be fair. This paradox operates at multiple levels — political (fair loyalty masking treason), personal (fair appearance hiding murderous intent), and cosmic (fair weather disrupted by unnatural darkness). In the Jacobean context, the inversion of "fair" into "foul" mirrors the divine order overturned by regicide, a sin against God as much as against Duncan.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> Fair is foul, and foul is fair: Hover through the fog and filthy air. ((001-011))

The witches' opening chant establishes the play's central paradox. Spoken in unison at the close of Act I Scene i, this couplet collapses moral distinction — in a world where fair is foul, traditional moral reasoning becomes impossible. Macbeth's first line echoes it exactly.

> So foul and fair a day I have not seen. ((003-015))

Macbeth's very first words in the play unconsciously echo the witches' paradox, marking him as already entangled in their world before he has met them. The "foul" day (bloody battle) is also "fair" (victory). This verbal echo is the first sign that Macbeth's moral compass is already aligned with the witches' inverted order.

> Away, and mock the time with fairest show: False face must hide what the false heart doth know. ((007-014))

Macbeth, having resolved to murder Duncan, instructs Lady Macbeth to maintain a "fairest show" — the language of fair appearance as concealment for foul intent. The word "false" appears twice in a single line, underscoring the deliberate deception.

### Deepening (Act III)

> Things that do sound so fair?—I' th' name of truth, Are ye fantastical, or that indeed which outwardly ye show? ((003-021))

Banquo's question to the witches articulates the fair/foul problem explicitly: can things that "sound so fair" be trusted? His scepticism contrasts with Macbeth's ready embrace of the prophecy. Banquo's use of "fair" as potentially deceptive foreshadows the betrayal to come.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> Bless you, fair dame! I am not to you known. ((019-036))

The Messenger's polite address to Lady Macduff as "fair dame" is laced with dramatic irony — the messenger comes to warn of impending murder, and the "fair" greeting masks the foul reality of the assassins already en route. Lady Macduff, innocent and "fair," will be slaughtered in the next scene.

## Critical Perspectives

The fair/foul paradox has been read as Shakespeare's meditation on equivocation, a topic of intense debate in 1606 following the Gunpowder Plot and the trial of Jesuit Henry Garnet. The witches' "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" is a linguistic template for the moral equivocation that enables Macbeth's rise and fall. Critics have also noted the play's debt to the morality play tradition, where "Fair" and "Foul" would have appeared as allegorical figures — Shakespeare transforms this medieval convention into psychological realism.
