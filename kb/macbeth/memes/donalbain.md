# Donalbain

Donalbain is the younger son of King Duncan in William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. Together with his elder brother Malcolm, he is thrust into danger after their father is murdered at Inverness.

## Overview

Donalbain first appears alongside Malcolm in the aftermath of Duncan's murder in Act II, Scene III. Upon learning that the King has been slain, Donalbain's instinct is immediate caution. When Malcolm asks what should be spoken, Donalbain recognizes the danger they face: "What should be spoken here, where our fate, / Hid in an auger hole, may rush, and seize us?" (010-059). He urges immediate flight: "Let's away. Our tears are not yet brew'd" (010-059).

Donalbain's most decisive moment comes when he resolves to flee to Ireland, while Malcolm goes to England: "To Ireland, I. Our separated fortune / Shall keep us both the safer" (010-076). He perceives that in a court where the King has been murdered, the heirs are not safe: "Where we are, / There's daggers in men's smiles: the near in blood, / The nearer bloody" (010-076). This insight proves prescient — by fleeing, both brothers survive, though their flight initially casts suspicion upon them for the murder.

## Dramatic Significance

Donalbain speaks only four times in the play, yet his brief appearance carries significant weight. His flight to Ireland, combined with Malcolm's flight to England, fragments the royal family and leaves Scotland vulnerable to Macbeth's tyranny. Donalbain does not return in the play — unlike Malcolm, who returns with an English army to reclaim the throne. His absence underscores the scattering of Duncan's line and the completeness of Macbeth's usurpation.
