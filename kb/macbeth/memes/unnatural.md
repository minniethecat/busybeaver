# Unnatural

The concept of unnatural disorder in *Macbeth* — the inversion of the natural order triggered by Duncan's murder, expressed through cosmic prodigies, animal omens, and the psychological unravelling of those who commit unnatural deeds.

## Definition & Context

"Unnatural" in *Macbeth* is the signature of regicide inscribed on the cosmos itself. In the Great Chain of Being — the hierarchical order that structured Jacobean cosmology — the king stood at the apex of the human realm, directly below God. To kill a king was not merely a crime but a metaphysical rupture: the unnatural deed "breeds unnatural troubles," spreading disorder from the human realm outward into nature. Shakespeare dramatises this chain reaction through a series of unnatural phenomena: horses eating each other, a falcon killed by a mousing owl, daylight failing at noon, and the psychological disintegration of the murderers themselves.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> 'Tis unnatural, / Even like the deed that's done. ((011-004))

The Old Man's response to the cosmic portents following Duncan's murder — darkness at noon, a falcon killed by an owl, Duncan's horses turning wild and eating each other — is the play's most direct statement of the unnatural-as-cosmic-consequence. The "deed" (regicide) is the cause; the portents are the symptoms. The Old Man, a figure of folk wisdom, speaks for the natural order itself.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> Unnatural deeds / Do breed unnatural troubles: infected minds / To their deaf pillows will discharge their secrets. ((021-033))

The Doctor's observation generalises the pattern: unnatural deeds (murder, treason) produce unnatural effects (sleeplessness, sleepwalking, guilty confession). Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking — her "infected mind" discharging its secrets — is the psychological equivalent of the cosmic portents in Act II. The chain of causation is clear: the deed infects the mind, the mind infects the body, and the body betrays the secret.

## Critical Perspectives

The unnatural portents in *Macbeth* draw on a rich tradition of prodigy literature — accounts of strange natural events interpreted as divine commentary on political crimes. Shakespeare's audience would have recognized the pattern from chronicle histories and sermons: when the king is violated, nature itself protests. The play's innovation is to make the unnatural equally psychological — Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking is as much a prodigy as Duncan's horses, a sign that the natural order will reassert itself through the very bodies of those who violated it.
