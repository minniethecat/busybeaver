**Caithness** is a Scottish thane who appears in the final act of _Macbeth_. Alongside [[menteith|Menteith]], [[angus|Angus]], and [[lennox|Lennox]], he joins the English-backed rebel army marching on [[dunsinane|Dunsinane]]. His dialogue in Act V, Scene II provides one of the play's sharpest assessments of [[macbeth|Macbeth]]'s unravelling rule.

## Appearances

Caithness enters "with drum and colours" alongside Menteith, Angus, and Lennox in Act V, Scene II (022-001). His first question — "Who knows if Donalbain be with his brother?" (022-004) — establishes the rebels' uncertainty about the full composition of their own forces.

His most significant speech describes Macbeth's defensive posture at Dunsinane:

> Great Dunsinane he strongly fortifies.
> Some say he's mad; others, that lesser hate him,
> Do call it valiant fury: but, for certain,
> He cannot buckle his distemper'd cause
> Within the belt of rule. (022-007)

The image of a cause too swollen to fit within "the belt of rule" is a striking metaphor for Macbeth's loss of control. Caithness reports the divided opinion at court — some think the king insane, others charitably call it battle fury — but his own judgment is unequivocal: the regime no longer coheres.

In his final speech, Caithness rallies the thanes toward battle, calling [[malcolm|Malcolm]] "the med'cine of the sickly weal" and urging that they "pour we, in our country's purge, / Each drop of us" (022-010). The medical metaphor echoes the play's larger pattern: Scotland is a diseased body, and the rightful king is its cure — a theme [[macduff|Macduff]] later amplifies when he calls Malcolm "the sovereign'st thing on earth / That e'er a surgeon's hand did put in act."

In Act V, Scene IV, Caithness marches with [[siward|Siward]] and the combined rebel force as Malcolm orders each soldier to cut a bough from [[birnam|Birnam Wood]] (024-001) — the very action that fulfills the [[the-weird-sisters|Weird Sisters]]' prophecy and seals Macbeth's doom.

## Character

Though he speaks only three times, Caithness's lines are thematically dense. He is the character who most directly articulates the "disease and cure" metaphor that structures the play's final act. His pairing with Menteith — the two constantly appear and speak in alternation — suggests a choric function, the collective voice of the alienated Scottish nobility.

## Thematic Significance

Caithness's diagnosis of Macbeth — "He cannot buckle his distemper'd cause / Within the belt of rule" (022-007) — participates in the play's sustained imagery of ill-fitting garments as symbols of illegitimate rule. Earlier, [[angus|Angus]] had described Macbeth's title as hanging "loose about him, like a giant's robe / Upon a dwarfish thief" (022-008), and Macbeth himself feared that the crown might prove "a fruitless crown" and "a barren sceptre" (013-006). Caithness's "belt of rule" completes this pattern: the instruments of sovereignty no longer fit the man who seized them unlawfully.

His use of the word "distemper'd" is equally resonant, collapsing three meanings into one — the medical (a distempered body), the political (a distempered state), and the mental (a distempered mind). The word appears nowhere else in the play, making Caithness's single usage a concentrated statement of the tragedy's central concern: the inseparability of individual conscience and political order.
