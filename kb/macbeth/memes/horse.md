The horse appears throughout *Macbeth* as both a practical element of the medieval Scottish world and a powerful symbol of the natural order that Macbeth's [[regicide]] disrupts. Horses carry messengers, warriors, and murderers; their behaviour mirrors the cosmic disorder unleashed by the killing of a rightful king.

## Duncan's Cannibal Horses

The most striking equine image in the play comes from Ross's report of unnatural portents following Duncan's murder. In Act II, Scene iv, the Old Man and Ross discuss the strange events that have accompanied the regicide:

> ROSS. And Duncan's horses (a thing most strange and certain)
> Beauteous and swift, the minions of their race,
> Turn'd wild in nature, broke their stalls, flung out,
> Contending 'gainst obedience, as they would make
> War with mankind.
>
> OLD MAN. 'Tis said they eat each other.
>
> ROSS. They did so, to the amazement of mine eyes
> That look'd upon't.
> (011-005)

The image of Duncan's "beauteous" horses turning cannibal is a masterstroke of [[unnatural]] inversion. Horses — creatures renowned for their nobility and obedience — devour one another, enacting in the animal kingdom the same breakdown of order that [[murder]] has brought to the human realm. The phrase "minions of their race" underscores their former excellence; their fall mirrors Macbeth's own transformation from honoured thane to [[tyrant]]. The [[old-man]] who receives this news embodies the conventional wisdom that such prodigies signify a world out of joint.

## "Broke Their Stalls": The Symbolism of the Broken Stable

Ross's report that the horses "broke their stalls" （011-005） draws on a rich tradition of equine symbolism. In the medieval and Renaissance imagination, the well-ordered stable was a microcosm of the well-ordered kingdom: each horse in its stall, each subject in their station. When Duncan's horses "flung out, contending 'gainst obedience," the image echoes the [[unnatural]] revolt of Macbeth against his own king. The stable door that should have held is broken; the creatures that should have submitted rise up in rebellion.

This symbolism operates by analogy rather than allegory. The horses do not "represent" Macbeth; rather, their rebellion participates in the same cosmic disorder that Macbeth's crime has inaugurated. The cannibalism of Duncan's horses is not a separate portent but the same rupture of nature, manifesting simultaneously in the human and animal realms. The Old Man who reports "the heavens" being "troubled with man's act" （011-004） in the preceding lines draws no distinction between the meteorological and the zoological — in the play's world, both register the same wound.

## Swift Horses and Dark Purposes

Horses serve as instruments of murderous intent throughout the play. Before Banquo's assassination, Macbeth urges the murderers to speed:

> MACBETH. I wish your horses swift and sure of foot;
> And so I do commend you to their backs.
> Farewell.
> (012-015)

The blessing "swift and sure of foot" carries grim irony, for these are the horses that will carry the killers to ambush Banquo. Moments later, the Third Murderer hears their approach: "Hark! I hear horses" （014-006）, and the First Murderer confirms: "His horses go about" （014-009）. The sound of hooves becomes the sound of approaching [[death]].

Earlier, Banquo himself had used equine imagery to describe his own unease. Preparing to ride out on the day of his murder, he remarks to Fleance:

> BANQUO. Go not my horse the better,
> I must become a borrower of the night
> For a dark hour or twain.
> (012-010)

The horse here is a marker of Banquo's noble status, but "go not my horse the better" also carries a premonitory shiver — a sense that something is not right with this ride.

## The Galloping of Fate

In the play's final act, Lennox hears "the galloping of horse" （018-065） as the English army approaches Dunsinane. Horses that once served Macbeth's murderous purposes now carry the forces of justice. This reversal completes the play's equine symbolism: the same creatures that witnessed the [[unnatural]] cannibalism of Duncan's stable now participate in the restoration of legitimate [[kingship]].

Macbeth's final stand at Dunsinane is characterised by his determination to fight "bear-like" （027-006） — a beast brought to bay — while his enemies arrive on horseback, mobile and purposeful. The contrast between the cornered beast and the mounted warrior encapsulates the tragic trajectory of the [[tyrant]] who has lost everything, including the noble horses that once served his cause.
