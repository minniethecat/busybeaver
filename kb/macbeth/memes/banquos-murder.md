# Banquo's Murder

Banquo's Murder is a pivotal event in Act III of William Shakespeare's tragedy *Macbeth*. Fearing the witches' prophecy that Banquo's descendants will inherit the throne, Macbeth hires three murderers to ambush Banquo and his son Fleance. The attack succeeds only in part — Banquo is killed, but Fleance escapes, ensuring that the prophecy may yet be fulfilled.

## Overview

Macbeth, now King of Scotland, is tormented by the witches' declaration that Banquo "shalt get kings, though thou be none" (003-027). In Act III, Scene I, he convinces two murderers — and a third sent to ensure the job is done — that Banquo is their enemy. The murderers lie in wait at a park near the palace gate in Forres.

As Banquo and Fleance approach on horseback, the murderers strike. The Third Murderer identifies their target: "'Tis he" (014-012). Banquo's last words are to his son: "O, treachery! Fly, good Fleance, fly, fly, fly! / Thou mayst revenge — O slave!" (014-017). Banquo dies, but Fleance escapes into the darkness. The murderers acknowledge their failure: "There's but one down: the son is fled" (014-021). The Second Murderer laments, "We have lost / Best half of our affair" (014-022).

## Dramatic Significance

The murder of Banquo marks a turning point in Macbeth's descent. Unlike Duncan's murder — which required Lady Macbeth's orchestration — Banquo's murder is entirely Macbeth's initiative. He has grown into a tyrant who kills not only rivals but friends. The escape of Fleance means that despite all his efforts, Macbeth cannot control fate. The prophecy remains alive, embodied in the fleeing boy.

At the banquet that follows (Act III, Scene IV), Banquo's ghost appears to Macbeth — visible only to him — precipitating his public breakdown and signaling the beginning of his psychological unraveling.
