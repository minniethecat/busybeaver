# Regicide

Regicide—the killing of a king—is the central act of transgression in *Macbeth*, the crime from which all subsequent horrors flow. Shakespeare presents it not merely as murder but as a violation of the most fundamental bonds: guest against host, subject against sovereign, mortal against the divine. The play traces how this single act transforms Macbeth from honored thane to despised tyrant, and how the kingdom itself falls into chaos until legitimate rule is restored.

## Definition & Context

In the political theology of Jacobean England, regicide was understood as the most heinous of crimes. The king was held to rule by divine right—his authority came directly from God, and to strike at the king was to strike at God's appointed order. James I himself had survived the Gunpowder Plot of 1605, an attempted regicide that deeply shaped the political imagination of the period. Shakespeare wrote *Macbeth* in this climate, and the play can be read as an extended meditation on the consequences of killing a rightful king. Duncan is presented as a virtuous, divinely-sanctioned ruler, making Macbeth's crime not merely politically expedient but cosmically catastrophic.

## Textual Evidence

### Early Development (Acts I–II)

> MACBETH. He's here in double trust: First, as I am his kinsman and his subject, Strong both against the deed; then, as his host, Who should against his murderer shut the door, Not bear the knife myself. (007-002)

Before the murder, Macbeth himself catalogues the bonds that regicide would violate. Duncan is his kinsman (blood relation), his king (political subjection), and his guest (sacred bond of hospitality). Each of these relationships independently prohibits the killing; together they make regicide a "double trust" crime that wounds the fabric of civilization itself. Macbeth's clear-eyed recognition of what he is about to do makes the act all the more damning.

> MACDUFF. Most sacrilegious murder hath broke ope The Lord's anointed temple, and stole thence The life o' th' building. (010-028)

Macduff's discovery speech transforms the murder into sacrilege. Duncan's body is not merely a corpse but "The Lord's anointed temple"—a sacred vessel. Regicide "broke ope" what should be inviolable: the divine presence within the king. This language would resonate powerfully with a Jacobean audience familiar with the doctrine of the king's two bodies (the mortal body and the body politic) and the sacred character of monarchy.

### Deepening (Act III)

> MACBETH. For them the gracious Duncan have I murder'd. (012-045)

By Act III, Macbeth's language reveals the hardening of his conscience. He can now speak of the regicide in transactional terms—he "murder'd" Duncan "for them" (Banquo's heirs). The sacred horror of the act has been replaced by a political calculus: was it worth it? The answer, for Macbeth, is no: he has a "fruitless crown" (012-040) and "a barren sceptre" (012-041). Regicide has given him power but not dynasty, rule but not legitimacy.

> LADY MACBETH. Nought's had, all's spent, Where our desire is got without content. (012-058)

Lady Macbeth's admission that regicide has brought no satisfaction completes the arc of the crime's futility. She who urged her husband to the deed now finds that "all's spent"—the moral and psychological cost has consumed everything, leaving only emptiness.

### Culmination (Acts IV–V)

> ANGUS. Now does he feel His secret murders sticking on his hands; Now minutely revolts upbraid his faith-breach. (022-008)

In Act V, as the English forces approach, Angus observes that the consequences of regicide have finally caught up with Macbeth. The "secret murders" are no longer secret—they "stick on his hands" in a public, political manifestation of guilt. Every minute brings new "revolts" (desertions) as soldiers abandon the regicide. The political consequence of killing a king is the impossibility of commanding loyalty from others.
