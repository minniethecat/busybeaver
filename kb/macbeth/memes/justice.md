# Justice

Justice in *Macbeth* operates on two interconnected levels: as a cosmic principle of retribution that ensures evil deeds return upon their perpetrators, and as a political virtue — one of the "king-becoming graces" (020-018) that distinguishes legitimate rule from tyranny. The play traces the collapse of justice under Macbeth's reign and its eventual restoration through Malcolm's ascension.

## Macbeth's Recognition of Retributive Justice

The play's most explicit articulation of justice as cosmic retribution comes from Macbeth himself, in his soliloquy before Duncan's murder. Weighing the moral consequences of assassination, he acknowledges a principle of immanent justice: "But in these cases / We still have judgement here; that we but teach / Bloody instructions, which being taught, return / To plague th' inventor. This even-handed justice / Commends th' ingredience of our poison'd chalice / To our own lips" (007-002). The metaphor is precise: the poisoner drinks his own poison. Macbeth recognizes, even before the act, that murder sets in motion a chain of consequences that will inevitably circle back. His invocation of "even-handed justice" personifies the impartial, balancing force that the assassination will violate:

> This even-handed justice
> Commends th' ingredience of our poison'd chalice
> To our own lips.
>
> (007-002)

## Justice in Battle: The Opening Scene

The word "justice" enters the play in the Captain's report of the opening battle against Macdonwald's rebellion. Describing how Macbeth and Banquo rallied after the initial Norwegian assault, the Captain declares: "No sooner justice had, with valour arm'd, / Compell'd these skipping kerns to trust their heels" (002-006). Here, justice is personified as a warrior armed with valour — a fusion of moral right and military force that characterizes Duncan's legitimate rule. The rebellion is not merely a military threat but an offense against justice itself, which must be armed and deployed against it.

## Justice as Kingly Virtue (Act IV)

In the long testing scene between Malcolm and Macduff (Act IV Sc iii), justice appears among the catalogue of "king-becoming graces" that Malcolm falsely claims to lack: "As justice, verity, temp'rance, stableness, / Bounty, perseverance, mercy, lowliness, / Devotion, patience, courage, fortitude" (020-018). Malcolm pairs justice with verity (truthfulness) and temperance, placing it at the head of a list that constitutes the ideal sovereign's moral equipment. The rhetorical strategy is significant: by claiming to lack justice, Malcolm presents himself as the anti-Macbeth — not a tyrant who perverts justice but a potential king who must be tested for his fitness to wield it.

The testing scene also reveals how tyranny corrupts justice through perversion of law. Malcolm describes how a tyrant would "forge / Quarrels unjust against the good and loyal, / Destroying them for wealth" (020-016), transforming the legal system into an instrument of plunder. This is precisely what Macbeth's Scotland has become: a state where justice has been hollowed out and replaced by the arbitrary violence of a bloody-sceptered tyrant.

## The Restoration of Justice

The play's final act is, structurally, the restoration of justice. The combined forces of Malcolm, Macduff, and the English army function as an instrument of cosmic retribution, fulfilling Macbeth's own earlier insight that bloody deeds return to plague their inventor. Macduff, whose "wife and children's ghosts" (027-021) demand justice, becomes the sword of that retribution. When he carries Macbeth's severed head onto the stage and Malcolm is hailed as King, the play signals that the cosmic order — of which justice is the political expression — has been restored.
