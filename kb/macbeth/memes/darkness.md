# Darkness

Darkness is the play's dominant visual register. *Macbeth* is Shakespeare's darkest tragedy in the most literal sense — much of its action takes place at night, its murders are committed in darkness, and its language obsessively returns to images of blackness, shadow, and the extinguishing of light. Darkness in the play is more than atmosphere; it is the symbolic medium through which evil operates, conscience is suppressed, and the natural order is inverted.

## Lady Macbeth's Invocation

The play's most concentrated appeal to darkness comes from Lady Macbeth, who summons night as an accomplice to murder. Upon learning of Duncan's imminent arrival, she calls on spirits to "make thick my blood" (005-013) — to darken her very physical substance so that compassion cannot flow through it. The progression is relentless:

> Come, thick night, / And pall thee in the dunnest smoke of hell (005-020–021)

> Nor heaven peep through the blanket of the dark (005-023)

Lady Macbeth does not merely wish for darkness; she wishes for a darkness so complete that heaven itself cannot see through it. The word "pall" — a funeral cloth — aligns darkness with death from the outset. The "blanket of the dark" is maternal imagery inverted: darkness as womb of murder rather than life.

## The Unnatural Dark

After Duncan's murder, darkness becomes cosmic. Ross and the Old Man report that "by the clock 'tis day, / And yet dark night strangles the travelling lamp" — the sun itself has been extinguished. This unnatural darkness is the play's most direct statement that Duncan's murder is not merely a political crime but a rupture in the fabric of creation. The dark that Lady Macbeth called down has escaped her control and spread across the world.

## Macbeth's Relationship to Darkness

Macbeth's trajectory follows an inversion of the light-dark binary. At the play's opening he is associated with "brave" daylight battle; by Act III, he has become a creature of darkness who "will tomorrow (and betimes I will) to the Weird Sisters." His most famous speech — "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow" — ends with the extinction of the candle: "Out, out, brief candle." The man who once fought in daylight now names life itself as a "walking shadow" that "struts and frets his hour upon the stage, / And then is heard no more."

## The Witches' Darkness

The Weird Sisters enter in "thunder and lightning" — a darkness punctuated by violent light. Their ritual space in Act IV is a "dark cave" where they brew their charms. The music that accompanies the apparitions — "Black Spirits" (018-013) — aligns the supernatural with the chromatic symbolism of evil. The witches are creatures of the "pit of Acheron," a classical underworld; their darkness is not temporary (like night) but eternal.
