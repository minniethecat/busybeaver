# Macbeth's Coronation

Macbeth's Coronation occurs offstage between Act II, Scene III and Act II, Scene IV of William Shakespeare's tragedy. Following Duncan's murder and the flight of Malcolm and Donalbain, Macbeth seizes the throne and is crowned at Scone, the traditional coronation site of Scottish kings.

## Overview

Act II, Scene IV opens with Ross and an Old Man discussing the unnatural events that have accompanied Duncan's murder — darkness at midday, a falcon killed by an owl, Duncan's horses eating each other (011-002 through 011-006). These omens frame the coronation as a violation of natural order.

Macduff arrives and confirms that Macbeth has been named king and has already gone to Scone: "He is already nam'd; and gone to Scone / To be invested" (011-014). When Ross asks if he will attend the coronation, Macduff pointedly refuses: "No, cousin, I'll to Fife" (011-018). This refusal is the first public sign of resistance to Macbeth's rule, foreshadowing Macduff's eventual role as Macbeth's nemesis.

The coronation at Scone stands as a dark mirror to the rightful coronation at the play's end, when Malcolm — the legitimate heir — is crowned at the same site: "Whom we invite to see us crown'd at Scone" (028-028).

## Dramatic Significance

Macbeth's coronation is notable for occurring entirely offstage. Shakespeare denies the audience any ceremonial satisfaction. The event is reported in the midst of supernatural portents, making it clear that Macbeth's kingship is a usurpation of both political and cosmic order. Macduff's refusal to attend Scone — "Lest our old robes sit easier than our new!" (011-020) — introduces the theme of resistance that will build through the remaining acts.
