# The Weird Sisters

The Weird Sisters are a coven of three prophetic witches who appear at key moments in *Macbeth* to deliver prophecies that set the tragedy in motion. They are introduced as a collective — "Enter three Witches" (001-001) — and their first words establish their ritualistic, incantatory mode, speaking in trochaic tetrameter distinct from the blank verse of the mortal characters. They function as a single prophetic entity, marking them as beings from outside the natural order.

## The First Prophecy

The Sisters' first encounter with Macbeth and Banquo on the heath is the play's catalytic event. They hail Macbeth with three titles:

> FIRST WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Glamis! (001-002)

> SECOND WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Cawdor! (001-003)

> THIRD WITCH. All hail, Macbeth! that shalt be king hereafter! (001-004)

These three hails — Glamis, Cawdor, king — trace the arc of Macbeth's ambition from present reality to impossible future. When Banquo asks about his own fate, they respond with the cryptic formula: "Lesser than Macbeth, and greater. Not so happy, yet much happier. Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none." The witches define themselves through riddles (001-005 through 001-008) that demand interpretation and reward misinterpretation.

## Nature and Agency

The Sisters' nature is fundamentally ambiguous: are they agents of fate who cause events, or do they merely report what will happen? The play withholds a definitive answer. They operate under the authority of [[hecate]], the goddess of witchcraft, who rebukes them for dealing with Macbeth without her leave. Hecate's intervention reframes their earlier prophecies as unsanctioned meddling rather than fated necessity — the witches, it turns out, are subordinate beings whose interference requires correction from a higher supernatural authority.

Their speech pattern — trochaic tetrameter — marks them as beings outside the natural order. While mortal characters speak in blank verse (iambic pentameter) or prose, the witches chant in a rhythm that is literally inverted: trochees (STRESSED-unstressed) reverse the iambic pattern, just as the witches reverse the moral order. "Fair is foul and foul is fair" is their motto and their method.

## The Second Set of Prophecies (Act IV)

The Sisters' most elaborate performance comes in Act IV, Scene I. Summoned by Macbeth's demand for answers, they conjure three apparitions, each delivering a prophecy more deceptive than the last:

> An Apparition of an armed Head rises. (018-026)

The armed head warns Macbeth to "beware Macduff" — a straightforward prophecy whose truth Macbeth immediately perverts by ordering the slaughter of Macduff's family.

> An Apparition of a bloody Child rises. (018-033)

The bloody child declares that "none of woman born shall harm Macbeth" — a statement Macbeth takes as a guarantee of invincibility, but which means precisely that Macduff, delivered by Caesarean section, is his destined killer.

> An Apparition of a Child crowned, with a tree in his hand, rises. (018-039)

The crowned child promises safety until "Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane hill shall come against him" — which Macbeth interprets as impossible (forests don't move) but which is fulfilled when Malcolm's soldiers camouflage themselves with Birnam's branches.

A show of eight kings follows (018-055), culminating in Banquo's ghost, visually confirming the first prophecy: Banquo's issue shall be kings.

## Political and Theological Significance

The Weird Sisters embody the play's central tension between fate and free will. Their prophecies are technically true — Macbeth does become king, Birnam Wood does come to Dunsinane — but the Sisters deliberately present them in a way that invites fatal misinterpretation. Macbeth himself vacillates between treating them as oracles and cursing them as "juggling fiends." The coven's power lies not in controlling events but in controlling perception: they are "the instruments of darkness" who "win us to our harm" by telling truths that lead to damnation.

In the play's political theology, the witches represent the dark inverse of divine prophecy. Where God's prophets reveal truth to save souls, the Weird Sisters reveal truth to destroy them. Their gender is significant: as women exercising supernatural agency outside male authority (until Hecate's arrival), they represent a disorder in the natural hierarchy that mirrors the political disorder of Macbeth's usurpation.
