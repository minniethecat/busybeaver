# James Ison

James Ison is the head clerk in the law firm of S. and G. Rattar. His daily proximity to the machinery of the legal practice — "his [[letter]]s were always laid out on his desk" (003-002) — places him in a position to observe the subtle changes in "[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s" behaviour that mark the imposture.

## Character

Ison is the competent, long-serving employee — a figure whose institutional memory and daily routine make him sensitive to deviations from normal practice. The instruction "bring the [[letter]] book" (003-003) is routine, but the clerk's awareness of what belongs in the "[[letter]] book and ledger" (003-009) makes him a potential threat to the impostor's concealment.

## Role in the Plot

Ison's role is that of the institutional witness — the person whose professional observation of the daily workings of the office provides a baseline against which the impostor's deviations can be measured. His function in the novel illustrates how imposture, however carefully maintained, cannot survive the cumulative observation of those who knew the genuine article.

## The Clerk as Institutional Memory

Ison embodies a type essential to the functioning of any professional office: the long-serving subordinate whose practical knowledge exceeds that of his nominal superiors. He knows where every [[letter]] is filed, which precedents apply to which cases, and — most critically — the habitual patterns of behaviour that constitute the office's normal functioning. When [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] deviates from those patterns, Ison notices. He may not immediately interpret the deviations as evidence of imposture, but his awareness creates the documentary and testimonial record on which later detection depends.

## Professional Loyalty and Its Limits

Ison's position raises a recurring question in the novel's treatment of professional relationships: to whom does a clerk owe loyalty? To his employer (the impostor George), to the firm (which includes the murdered Simon's interests), or to the truth? Ison's conduct suggests a pragmatic resolution: he performs his duties faithfully while his observations quietly accumulate. He does not actively investigate — that is not his role — but neither does he suppress what he has seen. His professionalism becomes, paradoxically, a form of passive resistance to the imposture: by continuing to act as the competent clerk he has always been, he preserves the institutional standard against which the impostor will be judged.
