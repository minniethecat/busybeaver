# Lawyer

## Definition

A **lawyer** in early 20th-century Scotland was typically a solicitor — a legal professional who advised clients, drafted documents, managed estates, and represented parties in the lower courts. Unlike the English barrister, the Scottish solicitor often combined multiple professional functions: legal adviser, estate factor, and in some cases public prosecutor.

In *Simon*, the [[lawyer|legal profession]] is embodied by [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]], who practises as a lawyer in the unnamed northern town while simultaneously serving as procurator fiscal (public prosecutor) and factor (estate manager) to the Cromarty family. This concentration of roles — lawful and otherwise — is both historically accurate and narratively potent.

## The Lawyer's Professional Standing

The lawyer's standing is established at the novel's opening. [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] was

> lawyer to several of the larger landowners and factor on their estates, and lawyer and adviser also to many other people in various stations in life. (002-003)

This description reveals the breadth of a rural Scottish lawyer's practice: from the grandest estates to the humblest clients. "The lawyer's manner was peremptory and terse" (003-009) in business dealings, while his professional composure is described in physical terms:

> The lawyer's mouth was as hard set and his eyes as steady as ever. (003-010)

These descriptions establish [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]] as a figure of controlled professional authority — a control that takes on new significance when the reader learns that the man behind it is an impostor.

## The Dual Role: Lawyer and Fiscal

[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s professional identity spans two distinct but overlapping roles:

| Role | Function | Authority |
|------|----------|-----------|
| Lawyer (Solicitor) | Advise clients, draft documents, manage estates | Private — retained by clients |
| [[procurator-fiscal|Procurator Fiscal]] | Investigate deaths, gather evidence, prosecute crime | Public — appointed by Crown |

This dual appointment was historically common in rural Scotland, where the local solicitor often also served as fiscal. The novel exploits this concentration of power: when [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]]-fiscal becomes the criminal, the entire machinery of justice is compromised. As Carrington observes to [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]] during their interview:

> "I daresay you, Mr. Rattar, as a lawyer, and especially as a Procurator Fiscal, would be among the first to acknowledge that." (022-027)

## The Lawyer's Office as Setting

The office of the firm of S. and G. Rattar serves as a key setting in the novel. It is here, "as he shook hands with [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]] and then took a chair" (003-021), that clients come with their business and their secrets. The office is the stage on which the impostor performs his professional role — a performance rendered plausible precisely because [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]]'s professional identity is so well established in the community.

Several critical scenes unfold in this office:

- **[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s visit** — to discuss his inheritance prospects (ch. XIV)
- **Carrington's interview** — the private enquiry agent tests [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]]'s reactions (ch. XXII)
- **The suggestion** — Ned proposes an alternative theory to [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]] and observes his discomfort (ch. XVII)

Throughout these encounters, [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]]'s nod — "Again [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]] nodded, and again he made no comment" (003-027) — serves as both his signature gesture and his mask.

## The Lawyer's Professional Technique

[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s professional method is characterized by reserve, indirection, and strategic silence. "The lawyer merely nodded" (011-025) is his default response to any statement he does not wish to engage. When pressed, he retreats to professional caution: "It seems so," [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]] admitted cautiously (005-050). When the subject turns to the murder investigation, "The lawyer shook his head emphatically" (011-050) — a gesture that in retrospect signals concealment rather than professional [[suspicion|doubt]].

The impostor's performance is occasionally imperfect. During his conversation with [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]], "[[simon-rattar|the lawyer]] was displaying a fluency not altogether natural" (017-057), and a suggestion from Ned "seemed to disconcert [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]]" (017-063). These cracks in the professional facade are visible to the attentive reader, if not to the characters themselves.

## Real-World Background

The Scottish legal profession in the early 20th century differed markedly from its English counterpart. Solicitors in rural Scotland were often the most educated and influential figures in their communities — combining legal practice with estate management (factoring), banking, and local governance. The office of procurator fiscal, which [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] also holds, was typically held by a local solicitor as a part-time appointment.

This concentration of legal, financial, and prosecutorial power in a single individual was not unusual; it was the structure of rural Scottish life. Clouston's portrayal is thus grounded in institutional reality, which makes the novel's central crime — the usurpation of this concentrated power by an impostor — all the more plausible and disturbing.
