# The Hearth Brush

The hearth brush at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] became a significant piece of physical evidence in the investigation of [[the-cromarty-murder|the murder of Sir Reginald Cromarty]]. Its unusual position and the mud fragments associated with it were noted by [[james-bisset|James Bisset]], who regarded the brush as a crucial clue — though [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] worked systematically to dismiss its significance.

## The Discovery

The hearth brush was one of three pieces of evidence noted by Superintendent Sutherland in his initial report to Simon Rattar:

> "The second is that one or two small fragments of dried mud were found under the edge of the curtain, and the third is that the hearth brush was placed in an unusual position — according to Bisset." — Simon Rattar, summarizing Sutherland's report (014-021)

The brush had apparently been used to sweep up fragments of mud — a detail that connected the indoor crime scene to the outdoors and suggested the movements of the killer through the house.

## Bisset's Theory

James Bisset developed a coherent theory linking the brush, the mud, and the broken table leg:

> "That the man, whoever he was, had brought mud into the room and then swept it up with the hearth brush; these fragments being pieces that he had swept accidentally under the curtain and so overlooked." — Bisset's theory as relayed by Simon Rattar (014-023)

[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] was impressed by this reasoning, exclaiming "Good for Bisset! He has got there this time, I do believe" (014-024). Bisset's analytical approach — treating the hearth brush as part of a chain of physical evidence — demonstrated his growing competence as an investigator.

## Simon Rattar's Dismissal

Simon Rattar, as Procurator Fiscal, worked to undermine the significance of the hearth brush evidence:

> "Sir Reginald was in the library in his walking boots that afternoon. Naturally he would leave mud, and quite likely he swept it up himself then, though the only evidence of sweeping is Bisset's statement about the brush. And what proof is that of anything? Does your hearth brush always stay in the same position?" (014-026)

Simon's skepticism seemed reasonable on the surface, but his eagerness to dismiss physical evidence that might implicate the killer was, in hindsight, an attempt to protect himself. Ned Cromarty noticed "a defiant note in the Procurator Fiscal's voice" (014-031).

## The Evidence in Context

The hearth brush formed part of a constellation of physical evidence: the broken table leg (014-012), the mud fragments under the curtain, and the misplaced brush itself. Bisset's instinct that these small domestic details carried weight proved correct. [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] later incorporated the brush evidence into his own reconstruction of the crime.

Bisset personally collected mud fragments as material evidence:

> "Mud I picked up myself — and the hearth brush!" (024-014)

## At the Resolution

By the novel's conclusion, the hearth brush remained one of the physical facts that weighed against the accused:

> "Adding that fact to the unfastened window, the mud on the floor, and the hearth brush, the odds seemed heavy on entrance by the library window." (040-009)

The brush served as a tangible reminder that even small domestic objects could carry decisive weight in a criminal investigation.
