# Servant

## Definition

In Edwardian Scotland, domestic **servants** formed an essential but largely invisible stratum of society. A household of any standing employed at minimum a cook and a housemaid; larger establishments retained butlers, footmen, gardeners, and chauffeurs. Servants' proximity to their employers' private lives made them repositories of domestic secrets — a narrative function that Clouston exploits throughout *Simon*.

The servant class in the novel bridges the divide between the gentry's public façade and the hidden realities of the household. Their observations, gossip, and occasional interventions shape the flow of information on which the mystery depends.

## Context in the Novel

The novel's servant characters are not passive background figures but active participants in the narrative. [[james-bisset|James Bisset]], [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s butler, styles himself "a philosopher" (chapter 9) and provides both comic relief and shrewd observation. His awareness of the household's irregularities makes him a potential threat to the impostor.

[[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]], the housemaid, is the first to discover the mysterious stranger in [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]]'s garden (002-040). Her report — "it seemed so impudent like of the man coming into [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]]'s garden of all places!" — triggers the official response that begins the investigation, not knowing she has spoken to the impostor himself.

[[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s will includes "a few smaller legacies to servants" (014-068), acknowledging their place in the household economy. The "observant [[james-bisset|Bisset]]" (015-004) functions as an informal detective within the Rattar household, his domestic position granting him access to information that official investigators lack. ## Real-World Background

Domestic service was the largest single occupation for women in Edwardian Britain. In Scotland, the structure of rural service often included lifelong attachments between families and their servants, creating complex bonds of loyalty and obligation. Clouston's portrayal of [[james-bisset|Bisset]] as a "philosopher" butler reflects a literary tradition (stretching from Jeeves to Hudson) of servants who are intellectually superior to their employers — though [[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s philosophical pretensions are treated with affectionate irony rather than the reverential awe accorded to Wodehouse's Jeeves.
