# George Rattar's Confession

[[george-rattar|George Rattar]]'s full confession is the novel's climactic resolution. After Carrington's investigation has assembled the evidence and George's position has become untenable, the impostor admits to the murder of his twin brother and the subsequent impersonation. The confession transforms the novel's dramatic irony into explicit revelation.

## Content

The confession encompasses the entire scope of George's crime: the murder of Simon, the assumption of his identity, the exploitation of [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]]'s office to direct suspicion away from himself, and the sustained performance of respectability that held for nearly a month. The ex-convict "twin brother" (040-032) is revealed as the sole perpetrator, and the novel's layered deceptions collapse into a single, acknowledged truth.

## Significance

The confession serves both a narrative and a moral function. Narratively, it provides the explanation that the investigation has been building toward. Morally, it restores the order that George's crime disrupted — the truth is spoken, the impostor is identified, and the community can begin to reckon with the revelation that its most respected citizen was, for a month, a murderer.

## The Yarn

The confession takes the form of what George calls "the whole damned yarn" (038-101), a narrative delivered to an audience that includes Carrington, [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]], and the police. His opening admission sets the tone: "I needn't trouble you with my adventures before I came down here to visit brother Simon" (039-002). The confession is structured as a story — George is, in a perverse sense, performing for his captors.

## The Impersonation's Origin

George describes how the idea of impersonation came to him in a moment of desperation. Confronted with his brother's refusal to pay him off — "I'll buy a ticket back to London for you and leave it with the stationmaster, and that's every penny you'll ever get out of me!" (039-015) — George realized his blackmail scheme had failed. As he describes it, "all at once something—the devil if you like—put the idea into my head. I'd *be* Simon!" (039-017).

The murder weapon was opportunistic: "I spied that match box and the thing was settled. I got him to look the other way for a moment—and then he was settled" (039-021). George's confession reveals a crime driven not by elaborate planning but by the convergence of desperation, opportunity, and a resemblance between twins that made impersonation possible.

## George's Self-Assessment

Throughout his confession, George displays a strange pride in his performance. "I have kept it. No one can deny that!" (039-019) he declares of his composure during the month-long impersonation. His resentment focuses on [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s "damned glass eye" (039-023), which he claims "has been getting on my nerves long enough" — a telling detail that reveals how the impostor, who had successfully deceived the entire community, was ultimately unsettled by a single physical peculiarity.
