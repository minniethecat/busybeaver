George Rattar's return to [[simon-rattars-villa|Simon Rattar's villa]] is the inciting event of the entire novel. On a wet night, a solitary passenger arrives at [[the-railway-station|the railway station]] and makes his way to the house of his twin brother — the man whose life he will shortly take and whose identity he will assume.

## The Arrival

The return is recounted retrospectively through the observations of those who witnessed fragments of it. A solitary man was seen entering the villa's grounds; [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]] and the cook glimpsed a figure in the drive; footsteps were found in the gravel beneath the library window. The visitor came not to the front door but crept through the garden — already the manner of a man who did not wish to be seen.

## The Meeting of Brothers

What passed between [[george-rattar|George]] and [[simon-rattar|Simon]] in the library that night is never directly narrated. The novel reveals it only through its consequences: Simon was dead by morning, buried in the garden, and George sat at the breakfast table in his place. From the fragments of evidence assembled by [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] and [[james-bisset|Bisset]], it appears that George killed Simon almost immediately — perhaps the quarrel was brief, or perhaps the murder was premeditated from the moment of arrival.

## Significance

George's return is more than the beginning of the crime; it is the pivot upon which the entire narrative turns. Before this night, Simon Rattar was the respected Procurator Fiscal, the silent but upright pillar of the community. After it, an impostor occupied his place, and every subsequent event — [[the-cromarty-murder|the murder of Sir Reginald]], [[carringtons-investigation|Carrington's investigation]], [[the-impersonation|the impersonation]], and ultimately [[george-rattars-confession|George's confession]] — flowed from this single, fateful homecoming.

## The Title's Echo

The novel's first chapter is titled "The Solitary Passenger" — a phrase that captures both the literal description of George Rattar and the thematic isolation of a man returning to commit fratricide. The solitary passenger is alone not merely in his railway compartment but in his purpose, cut off from the bonds of family, law, and humanity.
