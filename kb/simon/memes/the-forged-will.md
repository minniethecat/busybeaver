# The Forged Will

The will of [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]] was the legal document that governed the disposition of his estate after his murder. Read and interpreted by [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] in his dual capacity as factor and Procurator Fiscal, the will became the focal point around which the investigation's theory of financial motive crystallised — and the document whose provisions would eventually help expose the conspiracy at the heart of the crime.

## The Reading of the Will

Simon Rattar reviewed the will's contents with [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] shortly after the murder, characterising the exercise as an assessment of who "might have affected this case." His method was systematic: he enumerated each legatee, assessed their financial gain, and considered whether they had the physical opportunity to commit the murder.

> "James Bisset gets a legacy of three hundred pounds. There are a few smaller legacies to servants, but the only two that might have affected this case do not actually do so." [014-068]

The two servant legatees — [[john-robertson|John Robertson]] the chauffeur (eliminated by alibi) and [[donald-mackay|Donald Mackay]] the gardener (eliminated by infirmity) — were quickly dismissed. But Rattar's method, as Ned observed, was implicitly accusatory:

> "I see," observed Ned, "you are giving everybody mentioned in the will credit for perhaps having committed the murder, supposing it was physically possible?" [014-069]

Rattar's reply was characteristically dry: "I am answering the question — who that could conceivably have committed it, had a motive for doing so?" (014-070).

Ned himself was among the legatees — Sir Reginald had cancelled a debt of £1,200 that Ned owed him. When Rattar pointed this out, Ned protested that he "hadn't the foggiest notion Sir Reginald meant to cancel that debt" (014-075). The question of who knew what, and when, would become central to the investigation.

## The £1,200

The sum of £1,200 recurs throughout the novel with the persistence of a leitmotif. It first appears as the debt Ned Cromarty owes Sir Reginald — a loan that allowed Ned to set up his household at Stanesland after returning from his "wild and woolly west" years:

> "If I hadn't got to pay Sir Reginald back the £1,200 it would be all right, so far I can figure out." [003-017]

When Sir Reginald is murdered, the debt is cancelled by the terms of his will. [[lilian-cromarty|Lilian Cromarty]], Ned's sister, immediately recognises the implication: "You've come into £1,200" (018-010), she tells her brother, and promptly argues for buying a motor car with the freed funds.

But the same figure of £1,200 also appears in a darker context. When [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] questions Simon Rattar about the will's beneficiaries, Rattar mentions that Ned Cromarty benefited to the tune of £1,200:

> "£1,200!" Carrington repeated the words with an odd intonation and stared very hard at the lawyer. [018-???]

Carrington's reaction — his sharp interest and the "different quality" of his attention — signals to the reader that the sum has a significance beyond the cancelling of a debt. The £1,200 connects the Cromarty inheritance to a larger pattern of financial irregularity in which Simon Rattar himself was implicated.

## Carrington's Investigation

The will was not, in the end, a forged document in the literal sense — Sir Reginald had genuinely written it. But the novel's title "The Forged Will" points to a different kind of forgery: the manipulation and concealment that surrounded its interpretation. Simon Rattar, who as factor had managed the Cromarty estate's affairs for years, used his knowledge of the will's provisions not to illuminate the investigation but to direct suspicion away from himself and toward the legatees.

Carrington's method was to follow the money. By tracing the £1,200 from Sir Reginald's will to Ned Cromarty's debt, and from Ned's debt to the financial pressures bearing on Simon Rattar — who "owed money everywhere" and was "head over ears in debt" (018-046) — Carrington uncovered a web of obligation in which the murder was not an act of passion but a calculated financial transaction.

The irony is that Rattar, by so carefully enumerating the will's beneficiaries and their motives, inadvertently exposed the logical structure that would eventually condemn him. As Ned had observed during the reading, the exercise of listing everyone who "gained by Sir Reginald's death" was itself an admission that the murder had a financial logic — and the person best positioned to orchestrate that logic was the man holding the will.

## Narrative Significance

The will functions in the novel as both a plot mechanism and a thematic device. As plot, it provides the investigation with its organising principle: identify who gained, then determine who could have acted. As theme, it embodies the novel's preoccupation with the gap between legal form and human reality. The will is a perfectly valid legal document; what makes it "forged" is not the signature but the uses to which it is put — the interpretations, concealments, and manipulations that transform a dead man's intentions into a living man's instrument of fraud.
