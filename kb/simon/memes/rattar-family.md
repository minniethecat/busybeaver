# The Rattar Family

The Rattar family — Simon and George Rattar, and their deceased father — stands at the dark center of the novel's plot. Their relationship, defined by rivalry, financial pressure, and ultimately fratricide, drives the central mystery of the [[the-cromarty-murder|Cromarty murder]].

Simon Rattar, the elder brother, had built a position of formidable respectability in the county. As the novel describes him:

> No man in that northern county was better known or more widely respected than Mr. Simon Rattar. In person, he was a thickset man of middle height and elderly middle age, with cold steady eyes and grizzled hair. His clean shaved face was chiefly remarkable for the hardness of his tight-shut mouth, and the obstinacy of the chin beneath it. (002-004)

He was simultaneously lawyer to several landowners, factor on their estates, and procurator fiscal for the county — "the setter in motion of all criminal processes, and generalissimo, so to speak, of the police" (002-004). This combination of legal, financial, and prosecutorial power made him one of the most formidable men in the district — and placed him in a position from which he could manipulate the very investigation he was supposed to lead.

## Overview

The Rattar family dynamic is the engine of the novel's plot. Two brothers, bound by blood but divided by temperament and circumstance, become entangled in a web of debt, [[identity-theft|identity theft]], and murder. Simon, the cold and calculating lawyer, has spent his life building an impregnable public reputation. George, his more volatile brother, returns to the district after years away and becomes simultaneously a financial burden and — through the resemblance between the brothers — an opportunity for impersonation.

The family's story unfolds against the backdrop of the Scottish legal system that Simon himself embodies. That the Procurator Fiscal — the very officer charged with prosecuting crime — should become the perpetrator is the novel's central irony. The Rattar family thus becomes a study in how institutional power can mask private criminality.

## Family Members

### Simon Rattar

[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] is the Procurator Fiscal — the local prosecutor for the Crown. A bachelor of rigid habits, he is described as "a machine for habit" (002-003) whose daily routine was as predictable as clockwork. He was "an elder of the Kirk, a nonsmoker, an abstemious drinker" (002-004) — a man whose "respectability was extreme and his observance of the conventions scrupulous to a fault" (002-004). Yet beneath this impeccable surface lay financial desperation and a capacity for violence that would culminate in murder.

His financial troubles and his brother's demands pushed him toward criminal acts, culminating in the murder of Sir Reginald Cromarty at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] and the impersonation of his own brother. The morning after the murder, even his housekeeper Mary noticed something amiss: "The master's no himsel'! Never have I seen Mr. Rattar look the way he looked at breakfast, nor yet the way he looked in the library!" (002-021).

### George Rattar

[[george-rattar|George Rattar]] is Simon's brother, a man of more volatile temperament who had been away from the district for some years. His unexpected return — and his physical resemblance to Simon — created the opportunity for the central impersonation plot. George is alternately a victim of Simon's schemes and a willing accomplice, his own moral compass compromised by financial dependency.

### George Rattar Snr

[[george-rattar-snr|George Rattar Sr.]] was the father of Simon and George. Little is known of him directly, but his legacy — and the inheritance he left behind — forms the financial substrate of the brothers' conflict.

## Family Dynamics

The relationship between Simon and George Rattar is characterized by mutual resentment and financial entanglement. George had been "visiting Simon's office to borrow money" (023-015), creating a financial dependency that Simon deeply resented. The imbalance of power between the successful elder brother and the dependent younger one poisoned whatever fraternal affection might once have existed.

> "There was certainly no love lost between them!" — On the relationship between Simon and George Rattar (011-029)

When George died — or appeared to die — his death created both an opportunity and a trap. Simon could now impersonate his brother and escape his financial predicament, but the impersonation bound him to a double life that grew increasingly unsustainable. The boundary between the two brothers blurred until the question of who was truly responsible for the crimes committed became difficult to answer.

## The Rattar Legacy

The Rattar family's story is ultimately about the corrosive effects of money, ambition, and family secrets. The brothers' rivalry destroys them both: Simon is exposed as a murderer and fraud, while George's identity is stolen and his reputation compromised. The family name, once associated with the dignity of the Procurator Fiscal's office, becomes synonymous with betrayal and crime.

The family's role in the novel also raises questions about the nature of identity itself. When Simon impersonates George, the question of where one brother ends and the other begins becomes not just a legal puzzle but a philosophical one. The Rattar family thus becomes a vehicle for the novel's exploration of [[identity-theft|identity]], [[reputation|reputation]], and the masks people wear in a society that prizes outward respectability above all.
