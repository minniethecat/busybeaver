[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s gold signet ring was a small object with outsized significance in the unraveling of [[george-rattar|George Rattar]]'s imposture. When George murdered Simon and assumed his identity, he discovered that the ring could not be removed from his dead brother's finger. This seemingly minor detail became one of the most important clues in [[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]]'s investigation and ultimately the decisive piece of physical evidence that broke the case.

## The Ring That Would Not Come Off

After killing Simon on the night of his return, George needed to complete his imposture by taking possession of everything that identified Simon — including his personal jewellery. But the signet ring presented an unexpected obstacle: it was stuck fast on the dead man's finger. As George later admitted to Carrington (039-026):

> Not being able to get his ring off his finger rather worried me.

The ring, immovable from the corpse, became a persistent vulnerability in an otherwise carefully constructed deception.

## The Advertisement

Unable to remove the ring, George devised a characteristically clever solution: he placed an advertisement in the newspaper claiming the ring had been lost. Carrington later referenced this in his conversation with George's associate (010-027):

> If you will go to his office you may find his signet ring and obtain the advertised...

The advertisement was a preemptive cover — if anyone noticed Simon Rattar was no longer wearing his accustomed ring, there would be a public explanation ready. It was also, however, one of the threads that Carrington pulled to unravel the entire scheme.

## The Decisive Evidence

The ring became the final, irrefutable proof of George's crime. When the body was exhumed from the flower bed at Keldale House, the ring was still on Simon's finger — identifying the corpse beyond any doubt. Carrington held it up before George with devastating effect (038-092–038-093):

> "We have just dug up your brother's body from that flower bed. Do you recognise his ring?"
>
> He held up a gold signet ring, and the lawyer fell back in his chair.

The ring thus served a dual function: it had been the one piece of Simon's identity George could not steal, and it became the one piece of evidence from which there was no escape.

## The Ring as Narrative Device

The signet ring is an exemplary piece of detective-fiction craftsmanship — a clue that satisfies the formal requirements of the genre while transcending mere mechanical function. In the classic tradition, a clue must be: (a) physically present in the narrative from early on; (b) available to the reader's notice, though its significance may be obscured; and (c) decisive in the final resolution, such that its meaning is retrospectively obvious. The ring satisfies all three criteria. Its immovability is established as a physical fact — not a contrivance — through the forensic detail of post-mortem swelling. George's advertisement is a piece of public misdirection that the reader, in retrospect, can identify as a defensive manoeuvre. And Carrington's final deployment of the ring as the coup de grâce — holding it up before George in the climactic confrontation — achieves the genre's ideal fusion of physical evidence and dramatic revelation. The ring is not merely a clue; it is an emblem of identity that cannot be stolen, a small gold assertion that the truth will out.

## Symbolic Significance

Beyond its role as physical evidence, the signet ring carried symbolic weight. A signet ring traditionally represents identity, authority, and lineage — all things George had usurped. That the ring alone resisted his appropriation, remaining faithful to its true owner even in death, made it a fitting instrument of poetic justice.
