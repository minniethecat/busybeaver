## Overview

The arrest of [[malcolm-cromarty|Sir Malcolm]] Cromarty marked a pivotal moment in the murder investigation — the brief detention of the murdered baronet's own son and heir on suspicion of parricide. The arrest was based on circumstantial evidence linking Malcolm to the crime scene, but was set in motion largely by the machinations of the impostor procurator fiscal, who stood to benefit from redirecting suspicion.

## Background

[[malcolm-cromarty|Sir Malcolm]] Cromarty was the son and heir of [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]. At the time of his father's murder, Malcolm was secretly engaged to [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] — an attachment his father had opposed on grounds of social eligibility (023-014). This secret engagement, combined with the discovery that Malcolm stood to inherit under his father's will, created a ready-made motive for murder in the eyes of the authorities. The prosecution could point to the old legal maxim that he who benefits most from the crime must be the prime suspect — a logic the impostor fiscal exploited to its fullest.

## The Arrest

News of the arrest reached the community as a shock. [[f-t-carrington|Mr. Carrington]], the private enquiry agent from London, heard of it while walking near [[keldale-house|Keldale House]]:

> "I hear they have arrested Sir Malcolm Cromarty." (021-014)

[[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]], upon hearing the news, was incredulous. When [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] found her and relayed what he knew, her reaction combined disbelief with practical concern:

> "If Sir Malcolm really has been arrested, it can only have been for something quite silly." (021-023)

She pressed Ned to explain exactly what charge had been brought (021-032), instinctively rejecting the possibility that Malcolm could have committed murder. Her conviction in his innocence was absolute — a loyalty that would later cost her her position at [[keldale-house|Keldale]] when the cloud of suspicion drove her to flee.

The arrest was carried out under the authority of the procurator fiscal — who was, in reality, the impostor [[george-rattar|George Rattar]]. By directing suspicion toward Malcolm, the impostor sought to deflect attention from his own crimes (022-002). The impostor fiscal himself would later articulate the cynical calculus behind the arrest, admitting that without proof there should be no arrest — yet allowing the suspicion to fester as a useful diversion (023-086).

## Carrington's Intervention

[[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]], working independently of the official investigation, quickly recognised that the case against Malcolm was thin. In his conversation with the procurator fiscal, Carrington probed the weaknesses in the official theory:

> "Your own attitude, so far has been, I take it — no proof, therefore no arrest; but a nasty family scandal left festering, so you decided to call me in." (022-062)

This was Carrington at his most penetrating — exposing the fiscal's strategy of maintaining a cloud of suspicion without the evidence to justify formal proceedings. Carrington's questioning exposed the lack of evidence against Malcolm and redirected the investigation toward the true perpetrator. The impostor fiscal had assumed that the very existence of a motive — the inheritance — would be sufficient to sustain suspicion indefinitely, but Carrington's logic demanded proof, not presumption.

## Aftermath

[[malcolm-cromarty|Sir Malcolm]] was eventually released, but not before the arrest had done its damage. The news travelled quickly: by the time [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] returned to [[stanesland|Stanesland]], the servants were already discussing it in hushed tones. A stable lad reported that he had heard Malcolm had been arrested for the murder (028-034), and even [[george-rattar|George Rattar]], in a moment of black humour, would later joke to Carrington about being arrested himself (030-024).

The arrest deepened the cloud of suspicion over the Cromarty family and heightened the stakes for [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]], who now understood that anyone connected to the murdered baronet could become a target of the investigation. The episode also demonstrated the impostor fiscal's willingness to sacrifice an innocent man to protect his own position — and Carrington's quiet determination to ensure that justice, not convenience, would prevail.
