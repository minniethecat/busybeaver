# The Sympathetic Stranger

The Sympathetic Stranger is a mysterious figure who appears at several critical junctures in the investigation of the [[the-cromarty-murder|Cromarty murder]]. He presents himself as a genial outsider — a potential tenant looking for shooting and fishing — but his repeated appearances and unusual knowledge suggest a deeper connection to the case. The stranger is ultimately revealed as a disguise adopted by [[f-t-carrington|F.T. Carrington]] in the course of his private investigation.

## Appearances

### The Muffled Passenger

The stranger first appears indirectly through the account of the muffled passenger — a man who traveled by train with his face wrapped up, avoiding recognition. [[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]] was alerted to this suspicious figure by his cousin [[robert-macalister|Robert MacAlister]]:

> "I didna see him myself," said the superintendent musingly. "It seems funny-like a man dressed like yon and with his face wrapped up too — and a man forbye that's a stranger to us both, coming along the platform and getting into that carriage, and me not noticing him. I'm not used not to notice people, Robbie." (019-024)

At the next station they searched the compartment, but "there was not a sign of the muffled stranger, under the seats or anywhere else" (019-041). The mysterious disappearance deepened the suspicion surrounding this unknown figure.

### Visit to Simon Rattar

The stranger visited [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] at his office, presenting himself as looking for a property to rent:

> The agreeable stranger began by explaining very frankly that he thought of renting a place for next season where he could secure good fishing and a little shooting, and wondered if any of the properties Mr. Rattar was agent for would suit him. Simon grunted and waited for this overture to develop. (022-003)

Simon was characteristically skeptical, but the visitor's affable manner and plausible cover story gained him an introduction to [[keldale-house|Keldale House]].

### Visit to Keldale House

The stranger then appeared at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] with a note from Simon Rattar, introducing himself to [[james-bisset|James Bisset]]:

> At eleven o'clock next morning a motor car drove up to Keldale House and an exceedingly affable and pleasing stranger delivered a note from Mr. Simon Rattar to Mr. James Bisset. Even without an introduction, Mr. Carrington would have been welcome. (024-002)

Bisset, who by this point had become the de facto master of Keldale House, welcomed the stranger warmly. [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]], who was already at Keldale House working undercover on the investigation, quickly recognized that this "stranger" was no ordinary visitor — because he was the stranger himself, maintaining a dual identity.

## Character

The stranger persona that Carrington adopts is carefully calibrated to disarm suspicion. He is invariably described as "agreeable" (022-003), "affable" (024-002), and "pleasing" (024-002) — a man whose charm opens doors that would otherwise remain closed. His cover story — a prospective tenant in search of fishing and shooting — is perfectly suited to the rural gentry society of the district, giving him a natural pretext to move among the key figures in the case.

Yet beneath this genial exterior, the stranger demonstrates a sharp investigative instinct. His questions are probing, his observations acute. The persona allows Carrington to gather information that would be inaccessible to an official investigator — [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] would never have admitted a police detective into his confidence, but a wealthy sporting tenant was a different matter entirely.

The duality of the character — friendly outsider on the surface, determined investigator beneath — is the key to his effectiveness. As Bisset himself reflected:

> "Why need the man have been a stranger at all?" — James Bisset (024-046)

## In the Eyes of Others

### Superintendent Sutherland's View

[[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]] regarded the muffled stranger with professional suspicion. The man's evasive behavior on the train — wrapped face, silent movements, disappearance from a locked compartment — matched every instinct that Sutherland's years of police experience had trained. Sutherland's account of the incident (019-024) reveals his chagrin at having failed to notice the man himself: "I'm not used not to notice people."

### Simon Rattar's View

[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] received the stranger with the wariness he extended to all visitors. His characteristic "grunt" in response to the stranger's overture (022-003) suggests the fiscal's habitual skepticism. Yet even Simon was persuaded to provide the introduction to Keldale House — evidence of the stranger's skillful manipulation.

### James Bisset's View

[[james-bisset|James Bisset]], isolated in his dominion over Keldale House, was perhaps the most receptive to the stranger. Having "begun to find that despotism has its lonely side, and to miss 'the gentry'" (024-002), Bisset welcomed the company of an agreeable gentleman. His trust made him an invaluable source of information — and his later reflection on the stranger's true identity (024-046) shows that Bisset was more perceptive than he appeared.

### F.T. Carrington's View

For [[f-t-carrington|F.T. Carrington]], the stranger was not another person but a role he himself performed. His ability to inhabit multiple identities — now the sporting visitor from London, now the sympathetic stranger — was the instrument of his successful investigation.

## Role in the Plot

The stranger serves multiple narrative functions: as a source of suspense in the early investigation, as a vehicle for Carrington's undercover work, and as a thematic echo of the novel's central concern with [[identity-theft|concealed identity]]. The revelation that Carrington and the stranger are the same person recasts all the stranger's earlier appearances, rewarding the attentive reader with a classic detective-story twist.
