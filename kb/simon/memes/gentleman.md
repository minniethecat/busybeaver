# Gentleman

## Definition

The Edwardian **gentleman** was defined by a combination of birth, education, occupation, and conduct. While landed gentry like [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]] inherited the status, professional men like [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] could earn it through the practice of law, and self-made men like [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] could claim it through manner and accomplishment. The gentleman's code prescribed honesty, restraint, protection of women, and loyalty to one's word.

*Simon* presents a spectrum of gentlemanly types, from the authentic ([[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]], [[ned-cromarty|Ned]]) to the counterfeit ([[george-rattar|George]]), and tests each against the pressures of crime, suspicion, and revelation.

## Context in the Novel

[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] is introduced as a gentleman by professional standing — a lawyer and fiscal whose reputation was formidable:

> "No man in that northern county was better known or more widely respected than Mr. Simon Rattar." (002-003)

The fact that his gentlemanly status can be usurped by his criminal twin indicates both the strength and the vulnerability of professional gentility: the office confers the status regardless of the office-holder's character.

[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] presents a different model entirely — the gentleman by nature rather than by nurture. His [[america|America]]n frontier experience has stripped away the conventional polish but left intact an instinctive sense of justice and loyalty. When he speaks:

> "…his voice had a marked tinge of the twang of the wild west that sounded a little oddly on the lips of a country gentleman in these northern parts." (004-002)

His "curious movements" as laird (004-046) mark him as unconventional, but his essential gentlemanliness is never in [[suspicion|doubt]].

[[f-t-carrington|Carrington]], the London enquiry agent, represents the professional gentleman par excellence — a man whose status derives from competence and discretion rather than land or title.

## Real-World Background

The definition of a gentleman underwent significant evolution between the Victorian and Edwardian eras. The Reform Acts, the expansion of the professions, and the decline of agricultural land as the primary source of wealth all contributed to a shift from birth-based to merit-based definitions. By 1919, when *Simon* was published, the gentleman was as likely to be a lawyer or enquiry agent as a baronet — a transition that Clouston's novel registers in its diverse cast of male characters, each of whom embodies a different relationship to the gentlemanly ideal.
