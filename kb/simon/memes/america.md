# America

## Definition

In *Simon*, **America** functions less as a geographical location than as a symbolic space — the site of [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s transformation from a younger son of the Scottish gentry into a self-reliant frontiersman. The American West represents freedom from inherited social constraints, directness of speech and action, and a form of justice that operates outside legal institutions.

The contrast between America and Scotland structures Ned's character and generates much of the novel's humour and tension. He is at once laird and cowboy, inheritor and self-made man, and the dual identity is the source of both his strength and his social awkwardness.

## Context in the Novel

Ned's American past is introduced as a startling transformation. The young man who left Scotland has returned as a figure who has been from "cow puncher into a high grade laird" (004-013). This compressed biography encapsulates the American experience as a crucible of character formation.

His stories of the West — the bar fight, the vigilante hanging, the glass eye — are told with a casualness that shocks his Scottish listeners. The American justice he describes ("we hanged him for murder, as a matter of fact," 004-028) operates without courts or procurators fiscal, a form of direct action that both impresses and disturbs. To the local community, Ned is simply a man "from the Colonies or America or one of thae wild places" (016-023), his transatlantic origins marking him as fundamentally exotic. His life "in the wilder parts of America" (023-063) has given him what one observer calls a character shaped by frontier experience rather than drawing-room convention.

America also supplies Carrington with a frame of reference for understanding Ned. The "sympathetic stranger" (chapter 32) who aids Ned is recognisable as a figure shaped by transatlantic mobility — a man comfortable operating outside the structures of family, class, and locality that define Scottish society.

## The Glass Eye Episode

Among Ned's frontier anecdotes, the [[glass-eye]] story stands out as the most fully elaborated tale. Ned tells of an American acquaintance who lost an eye in a bar fight and replaced it with a glass prosthesis — an object that became, in Ned's telling, a symbol of frontier resourcefulness. The episode encapsulates the American values of self-reliance and practical problem-solving that Ned brings back to Scotland: when life takes something from you, you replace it with whatever works. The anecdote's effect on Ned's Scottish audience — a mixture of fascination and mild horror — illustrates the cultural gulf between the two worlds.

## America as Moral Contrast

Throughout the novel, America functions as an implicit critique of Scottish social rigidity. Where Scottish society operates through inherited titles, family connections, and the slow machinery of legal institutions, America (in Ned's recollections) operates through direct action, personal merit, and rough justice. The contrast is not presented as simple — Ned's American methods are both admired and viewed with suspicion — but it provides a consistent counterpoint to the novel's Scottish setting.

The American frontier's most significant contribution to the plot is its production of [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] himself: a man who, having survived by his wits in a lawless environment, is prepared to act decisively when the Scottish legal system fails to deliver justice. His American experience makes him the novel's most effective agent of resolution.

## Real-World Background

By the early 20th century, the American West had already become a powerful imaginative construct in British literature. The cowboy, the frontier, and the vigilante represented a Romantic alternative to European social convention — a space where character was tested by action rather than inherited status. Clouston's use of this material reflects a broader Edwardian fascination with transatlantic mobility, while Ned's return to Scotland reverses the typical emigration narrative: instead of the Scot who goes to America and never returns, Ned is the American who comes back to Scotland, bringing the frontier with him.
