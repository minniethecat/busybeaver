# Miss Peterkin

Miss Peterkin is the manageress of the [[kings-arms-hotel|Kings Arms Hotel]] — a figure who combines commercial acumen with the local knowledge essential to [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]]'s investigation. Her willingness to talk, and her command of the town's gossip, make her the enquiry agent's most productive informant.

## Character

Miss Peterkin is a shrewd observer of class and character. She initially judges [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] "a trifle too superior" and not quite of her "class" (020-007), but his sympathetic manner disarms her reservations. "The most engaging feature of [[f-t-carrington|Mr. Carrington]]'s character was the interest he took in everything that was told him" (020-010) — a professional technique that Miss Peterkin interprets as personal charm.

She is described as "the capable manageress" (020-007), and her professional competence is evident in her command of the hotel and its guests. Her response to Carrington's question about a competing hotel is telling: "Absolutely none, in Miss Peterkin's opinion" (020-009). The pride she takes in her establishment — "we are" followed by an impressive claim (020-013) — reflects a woman who has built her identity around the hotel's reputation.

## The Evening at the Hotel

Miss Peterkin's most significant scene occurs during an evening gathering at the hotel, when Carrington draws her and the other guests into conversation. [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] asks her "what about these gentlemen here" (020-016), opening a line of inquiry that yields detailed local intelligence. With Miss Peterkin as "a willing and hospitable audience" (020-017), Carrington orchestrates an evening in which the town's gossip flows freely.

The evening culminates with Miss Peterkin "insisting on getting out the bottle" (020-020), a gesture of hospitality that also lubricates the flow of information. Her assessment of Carrington by the evening's end is striking: she "thought he was quite one of them" (020-023) — a remarkable conversion from her initial suspicion that he was too superior for her class. Carrington has, in the space of a single evening, transformed a gatekeeper into an ally.

## Role in the Plot

Miss Peterkin supplies [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] with the local knowledge that his outsider status denies him. Through her, he learns the geography of [[keldale-house|Keldale]], the history of the Cromarty family, and the texture of local opinion — the raw material from which his investigation builds its conclusions. She tells him "which road led to Keldale" (020-025) and, more importantly, who mattered in the community and why.

Her function as an informant illustrates the dependence of professional detection on local, often female, knowledge networks. The official investigation, conducted by [[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]], operates through formal channels — witness statements, medical evidence, procedural steps. Carrington's parallel investigation operates through conversation, hospitality, and the careful cultivation of informants. Miss Peterkin is the embodiment of this alternative method — and her effectiveness is a quiet rebuke to the official preference for masculine, institutional sources of knowledge.

## The Hotel as Information Hub

The [[kings-arms-hotel|Kings Arms Hotel]], under Miss Peterkin's management, functions as more than a commercial establishment. It is the town's information exchange — the place where news is received, assessed, and redistributed. Miss Peterkin's position at its centre gives her a panoramic view of local affairs that no official investigator could match. Carrington's genius lies in recognising this fact and treating her not as a servant but as a collaborator.
