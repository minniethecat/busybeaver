# Honour

## Definition

**Honour** in the Edwardian gentleman's code encompassed personal integrity, keeping one's word, protecting the weak, and maintaining family reputation. It was simultaneously a moral ideal and a social performance — something one *had* and something one *displayed*. The concept carried particular weight in rural Scotland, where the gentry's honour was understood to underwrite the social order.

*Simon* tests the concept of honour against the realities of crime and deception. The novel asks: what is honour worth when a man can be replaced by his criminal twin without anyone noticing? If reputation can be stolen, can honour be counterfeited too?

## Context in the Novel

[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] embodies a rough, transatlantic form of honour. His response to a request captures his willingness to act:

> "Rather—if you will!" (003-035)

His honour is personal and active, rooted in his cowboy past rather than in inherited social forms.

The impostor [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] represents honour's absence. Having murdered his brother and assumed his identity, George must simulate the honour that Simon's reputation implied. The impostor's performance of probity — the nods, the silences, the significant grunts — is a study in the counterfeitability of social virtue.

[[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]], by contrast, represents inherited honour in its most benevolent form. At [[keldale-house|Keldale House]], the family maintained the social rituals that honour demanded even under the shadow of tragedy:

> "Sir Malcolm, too, endeavoured to do the honours with some degree of cheerfulness; but short though the meal was, both were silent before the end and vaguely depressed afterwards." (015-008)

When suspicion falls on an innocent man, the cry of protest is framed in the language of honour:

> "I—the head of an ancient and honourable house—am actually suspected of having murdered my cousin, Sir Reginald Cromarty!" (030-022)

His concern for his family and dependents reflects a genuine commitment to the obligations that his title imposes.

## Real-World Background

The Edwardian gentleman's code of honour was under considerable strain by the early 20th century. The Boer War, the rise of commercial wealth, and the challenge of women's suffrage all exposed the limitations of a moral framework built on class privilege and masculine prerogative. Clouston, writing in 1919 after the cataclysm of the Great War, presents honour as a genuine value — [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s benevolence, Ned's directness — while demonstrating its vulnerability to exploitation. The novel's treatment of honour is thus both nostalgic and clear-eyed.
