# Donald Mackay

Donald Mackay was the gardener on the [[cromarty-estate|Cromarty estate]], one of the domestic staff who served at Keldale House under [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]. He was named as a legatee in Sir Reginald's will and was noted during the murder investigation for two facts that together removed him from suspicion: he had a financial motive, but no physical capacity to act on it.

## Role in the Estate

Mackay served as gardener on the Cromarty estate, one of several servants who maintained the grounds and household of Keldale House. His long service to the family was recognised in Sir Reginald's will, which made provision for household staff alongside larger bequests to figures such as [[james-bisset|James Bisset]] and [[john-robertson|John Robertson]].

## The Will and the Investigation

When [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] reviewed the contents of Sir Reginald's will with [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]], he systematically considered each legatee as a potential suspect — assessing both motive and opportunity. Mackay was one of only two legatees whose bequests might conceivably have "affected this case":

> "The other is Donald Mackay, the gardener, but he is an old man and was in bed with rheumatism that night." (014-068)

The other was John Robertson, the chauffeur, who had an alibi until two in the morning. Mackay's exemption was different in kind: his age and infirmity made it physically impossible for him to have committed the murder, regardless of motive. His inclusion in the list of potential suspects was thus a formality — Rattar's methodical approach demanded that every person with a financial interest be accounted for, but Mackay was dismissed as quickly as he was named.

## In the Eyes of Others

### Simon Rattar's View

For [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]], Mackay was a name on a list — one item in the systematic review of legatees conducted after the murder. Rattar's methodical elimination of Mackay ("he is an old man and was in bed with rheumatism that night," 014-068) exemplifies the fiscal's forensic approach: every possibility was considered, no matter how remote, and dismissed only on evidence. That this thoroughness was itself a performance — Rattar knew perfectly well who the real murderer was — adds a layer of irony to Mackay's brief moment in the fiscal's attention.

### The Author's View

Mackay's treatment in the narrative reveals the author's method. Rather than simply state that the servants were above suspicion, Clouston walks the reader through the logic of elimination, using Mackay as a limiting case. The gardener is so thoroughly incapable of the crime that his inclusion in the suspect list makes the investigation feel exhaustive rather than cursory — a technique that builds credibility for the eventual solution.

## Narrative Function

Mackay appears in the novel solely as a name on a document — he never speaks a line or appears in a scene. His narrative function is structural: he represents the outermost ring of the investigation's logic. By considering and dismissing even the bedridden gardener, Rattar (and through him the author) demonstrates the exhaustive scope of the inquiry, while also showing how the genuinely plausible suspects — men with both motive and physical capability — were progressively narrowed.

The contrast with John Robertson is instructive. Robertson had an alibi (he was away from home until 2 a.m.), but his physical capacity was never in question. Mackay had no alibi — he was at home in bed — but his physical incapacity was absolute. Together, the two "servant legatees" illustrate the two distinct ways a suspect could be eliminated: by proof of absence, or by proof of impossibility.
