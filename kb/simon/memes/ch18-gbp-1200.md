#### XVIII

[018-001] £1,200

[018-002] [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] had returned home and was going upstairs, when he heard a voice cry:

[018-003] “Ned!”

[018-004] The ancient stone stair, spiralling up round the timeworn pillar that seemed to have no beginning or end, gave at intervals on to doors which looked like apertures in a cliff. Through one of these he turned and at the end of a brief passage came to his sister’s sitting room. In that medieval setting of ponderous stone, it looked almost fantastic in its daintiness. It was a small room of many cushions and many colours, its floor covered with the softest rugs and its walls with innumerable photographs, largely of country houses where Miss Cromarty had visited.

[018-005] Evidently she was a lady accustomed to a comfortable life in her roving days, and her sitting room seemed to indicate very distinctly that she proposed to live up to this high standard permanently.

[018-006] “Oh Neddy dear, I want to talk to you about something,” she began in her brisk way and with her brightest smile.

[018-007] Her brother, though of a simple nature, was by this time aware that when he was termed “Neddy dear” the conversation was apt to turn on Miss Cromarty’s requirements.

[018-008] “Well,” said he, “how much is the cheque to be this time?”

[018-009] “How clever you’re getting!” she laughed. “But it isn’t a cheque I want this time. It’s only a motor car.”

[018-010] He looked at her doubtfully for a moment.

[018-011] “Pulling my leg; or a real car?”

[018-012] “Real car of course—nice one too!”

[018-013] “But, my dear girl, we’ve just put down our car. You agreed it was necessary.”

[018-014] “I agreed then; but it isn’t necessary now.”

[018-015] “Have you come into a fortune? I haven’t!”

[018-016] “You’ve come into £1,200.”

[018-017] Again he looked at her, and this time his expression changed.

[018-018] “That’s only a debt wiped out.”

[018-019] “Well, and your great argument for economy was that you had to pay back that debt. Now you haven’t. See, Neddy dear?”

[018-020] Her brother began to shake his head, and her smile became a little less bright.

[018-021] “I don’t want to get my affairs into a tangle again just yet.”

[018-022] “But they weren’t in a bad tangle. Cancelling that debt makes us absolutely all right again. It’s absurd for people like us not to have a car! Look at the distances from our neighbours! One can’t go anywhere. I’ll undertake to keep down the household expenses if you get the car.”

[018-023] Her brother frowned out of the window.

[018-024] “No,” he said, “it’s too soon to get a car again.”

[018-025] “But you told me you had got part of that £1,200 in hand and hoped to make up the rest very soon. What are you going to do with the money now?”

[018-026] He glanced at her over his shoulder for an instant and then his mouth assumed a grim and obstinate look she knew too well.

[018-027] “I may need the money,” he said briefly. “And I’m not much in the mood at this moment for buying things.”

[018-028] Behind his back Lilian made a little grimace. Then in a tone of sisterly expostulation she said:

[018-029] “You are worrying too much over this affair, Ned. You’ve done all you can—”

[018-030] He interrupted her brusquely:

[018-031] “And it’s dashed little! What have I actually done? Nothing! One needs a better man than me.”

[018-032] “Well, there’s your friend [[silent-simon|Silent Simon]], and all the police—”

[018-033] “A fat lot of good they are!” said Ned.

[018-034] His sister looked a little surprised at his unusual shortness of temper. To her he was very rarely like this.

[018-035] “You need a good day’s shooting to take your mind off it for a little,” she suggested.

[018-036] He turned upon her hotly.

[018-037] “Do you know the story that’s going about, Lilian?”

[018-038] “[[malcolm-cromarty|Sir Malcolm]] and the Farmond girl? Oh, rather,” she nodded.

[018-039] “Is that how it strikes you?”

[018-040] [[lilian-cromarty|Lilian Cromarty]] jumped. There was something very formidable in her brother’s voice.

[018-041] “My dear Ned, don’t frighten me! Eat me if you like, but eat me quietly. I didn’t say I believed the story.”

[018-042] “I hope not,” he said in the same grim tone, “but do you mean to say it doesn’t strike you as the damnedest slander ever spread?”

[018-043] “Between myself I hadn’t called it the ‘damnedest’ anything. But how do I know whether it’s a slander?”

[018-044] “You actually think it might conceivably be true?”

[018-045] She shrugged her well-gowned shoulders.

[018-046] “I never could stand Malcolm Cromarty—a conceited little jackanapes. He hasn’t a penny and he was head over ears in debt.”

[018-047] It was his turn to start.

[018-048] “Was he?”

[018-049] “Oh, rather! Didn’t you know? Owed money everywhere.”

[018-050] “But such a crime as that!”

[018-051] “A man with ties and hair like his is capable of anything. You know quite well yourself he is a rotter.”

[018-052] “Anyhow you can’t believe [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] had anything to do with it?”

[018-053] Again she shrugged her shoulders.

[018-054] “My dear Ned, I’m not a detective. A pretty face is no proof a woman is a saint. I told you before that there was generally something in the blood in those cases.”

[018-055] As he stared at her, it seemed as though her words had indeed rushed back to his memory, and that they hit him hard.

[018-056] “People don’t say that, do they?” he asked in a low voice.

[018-057] “Really, Ned, I don’t know everything people say: but they are not likely to overlook much in such a case.”

[018-058] He stood for a moment in silence.

[018-059] “She—I mean they’ve both got to be cleared!” he said, and strode out of the room.
