# Glass Eye

## Definition

[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s **glass eye** is a prosthetic replacement for an eye lost in a violent encounter during his years as a cowboy in the [[america|America]]n West. The artificial eye is at once a physical defect, a conversation piece, a marker of his unconventional past, and — within the novel's symbolic structure — an emblem of partial vision and hidden depths.

Throughout *Simon*, the glass eye recurs as a motif, generating humour, discomfort, and eventually menace. It sets Ned apart from the conventional Scottish gentry and links his present identity to his [[america|America]]n past.

## Context in the Novel

The glass eye is first mentioned in a domestic moment of self-deprecating humour. Ned tells his sister: "I can't see you out of this glass eye unless I turn round, so whether you approve or not I shan't know till I look at you" (007-025). The quip establishes the eye as both a physical limitation and a source of Ned's distinctive charm.

The eye's backstory fascinates Ned's friends and acquaintances. "There's that glass eye, too. That's very interesting" (009-020), observes one character, prompting the question: "His glass eye! Oh—you mean it has a story?" (009-022). The expectation that a prosthetic eye must signify a dramatic past proves correct.

In lighter moments, the eye is a source of comedy. Ned jokes that "if I ever proposed my glass eye would drop out!" (037-040), using it to deflect emotional intensity. But in the novel's darker turns, the eye provokes visceral reactions: "Your damned glass eye has been getting on my nerves" (039-023). This line, spoken in hatred, reveals how the eye is perceived by an antagonist — not as a curiosity but as a deformity.

In the climactic confrontation, a criminal demands that "this hangman with the glass eye tell me all about themselves" (039-031), and Ned's defiant reply — "That's for you and your glass eye to find out!" (039-066) — transforms the eye from a physical attribute into an instrument of justice.

## Real-World Background

Glass eyes in the early 20th century were typically made of cryolite glass by specialist ocularists. A well-made prosthetic was nearly indistinguishable from a natural eye at a distance, though close observation (and the lack of synchronized movement) would reveal it. In literature of the period, physical distinctiveness — a glass eye, a wooden leg, a scar — often served as a form of "character typing," but Clouston subverts this convention: Ned's eye is not a mark of villainy but of experience, and its narrative function evolves from comic relief to symbolic weight over the course of the novel.
