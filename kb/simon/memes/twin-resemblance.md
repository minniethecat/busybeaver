# Twin Resemblance

The **twin resemblance** between [[simon-rattar|Simon]] and [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] is the central plot mechanism of the novel. The two brothers are identical twins whose physical appearance is so alike that even those who have known Simon for years cannot tell them apart — provided George adopts Simon's mannerisms and conceals his identifying moustache. This resemblance is not merely a convenience of plotting: it is the novel's deepest inquiry into the relationship between appearance and identity, performance and essence.

## The Twins' Appearance

Simon and George Rattar were twins, with the moustache being the principal external distinguishing feature:

> George was the one with a moustache; one knew them apart by that. (007-099)

Both men are described as thickset, of middle height, with cold steady eyes and grizzled hair (002-005). Their identical appearance is so complete that Simon himself acknowledged the resemblance was indistinguishable when clothing and grooming matched. The twins' physical identity was not a secret — "I was told the brother had gone abroad and was not likely to return" (040-018) — but the community had forgotten about George, making the substitution possible.

## George's Exploitation of the Resemblance

[[george-rattar|George Rattar]], upon his return from penal servitude, exploits the twin resemblance to carry out an elaborate [[identity-theft|identity theft]]:

1. **Physical transformation**: He removes his beard and moustaches, knowing the effect:

   > I was wearing a beard and moustaches then, but I knew if I took them off I'd look so like Simon that no one meeting one of us would know which it was, supposing we were dressed exactly alike and I did Simon's grunting tricks and all that. (039-008)

2. **Behavioural mimicry**: George studies and replicates Simon's mannerisms — his "grunting tricks," his "tight-shut mouth," his habit of solitude, and his general air of respectability.

3. **Forensic caution**: George takes care to avoid leaving fingerprints by wearing [[kid-gloves]], knowing that his prints as an ex-convict would differ from Simon's.

## The Murder Enabled by Resemblance

The identical appearance means that once George has killed Simon and buried the body, no one suspects a substitution. He simply walks into Simon's life — his house, his office, his professional role as [[procurator-fiscal|Procurator Fiscal]] — and continues as if nothing has happened. The resemblance is so perfect that:

- Simon's clerk Ison notices only "recent peculiarities of conduct" (040-034) rather than any physical difference.
- [[james-bisset|James Bisset]], who had served Simon for years, observes deviations in habit but never questions that the man is Simon (006-002).
- Professional colleagues continue to deal with "Simon Rattar" without suspicion.
- The only clues to the substitution are behavioural: George's [[smoking-as-identity|smoking]] (Simon was a non-smoker), the [[kid-gloves|kid gloves]], and subtle changes in manner.

The novel's irony is that physical identity — the one thing everyone trusts — is precisely what enables the deception. In a community where people know each other by face and form, the identical face is an impenetrable mask.

## Real-World Context

The use of identical twins in crime fiction has a long tradition, dating to the sensation novels of the 1860s and continuing through the detective fiction of Clouston's era. The twin device exploits a deep anxiety about the reliability of visual recognition: if two people can share the same face, then identity itself becomes contingent, a matter of performance rather than essence. Clouston's novel, published in 1919, participates in this tradition while also subverting it — the twin resemblance is not a revelation at the climax (as in many sensation novels) but the premise from which the entire plot unfolds.

## Thematic Significance

The twin resemblance motif explores themes of identity, appearance versus reality, and the fragility of social recognition:

> I found for certain that Simon Rattar had never smoked in his life, and that George used to be a heavy smoker. (040-034)

In a community where everyone knows everyone, the fact that a man can be murdered and replaced by his identical twin — with no one noticing — is a dark commentary on how much of identity is performance rather than essence. The novel also plays on the Victorian and Edwardian literary tradition of doubles and doppelgangers, using the twin device within a detective fiction framework.

The resemblance ultimately fails not because anyone sees through the face, but because George cannot sustain the performance. The body is identical, but the soul is not — and it is the soul's small betrayals (a cigarette, a cut from shaving, a moment of temper) that undo the crime.
