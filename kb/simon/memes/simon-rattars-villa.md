# Simon Rattar's Villa

[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s villa is the domestic setting where the novel's central crime — the murder of the real Simon and his replacement by George — takes place. Acquired through "a very shrewd bargain a number of years ago" (002-006), the house, with its garden, dining-room, library, and smoking room, is both a respectable professional residence and the scene of a hidden murder. It is "commodious" (032-034), substantial enough to employ a butler and a housemaid, and its solidity projects the fiscal's position in the community — which makes the crime concealed within its walls all the more transgressive.

## Description

The villa stands on "a very secluded road, with a few villas and gardens upon the one side" and open country on the other (035-006). Its garden is "spacious" with "a belt of trees leading up to the house from the gate" (002-002), creating the sense of a private domain set apart from the town. The dining-room is "severely upholstered" (002-002), reflecting Simon's formal, undemonstrative character. A room on the ground floor — "evidently been intended for a smoking room or study when the villa was built, but was clearly never used for either purpose by the present proprietor" (033-011) — becomes an incidental clue: Simon did not smoke, and the impostor's use of this room marks one of the behavioural departures that eventually betray him.

When [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] moved to this villa, he brought with him his old servants, including [[james-bisset|James Bisset]], who had been in his service for years (006-002). This continuity of domestic staff is what makes the imposture both possible and perilous: Bisset and [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]] know their master's habits intimately, and their gradual recognition of deviation provides the domestic thread of the novel's detection plot.

## The Villa at Night

The villa's night-time aspect is central to the novel's atmosphere of concealment:

> The villa of Mr. Simon Rattar was now hidden in darkness. (035-008)

The narrator observes at a charged moment that "otherwise the villa was a lump of darkness in the dark" (035-009). A watcher stationed "in the quiet road where the villa stood" observes from "the shadow of the garden wall" (038-016) — the villa becomes, at night, not a home but an object of surveillance, its darkened windows suggesting secrets held within.

## The Domestic Stage

The villa is the stage on which the impersonation is performed. Every domestic interaction takes place within its walls: [[mary-maclean|Mary]] reporting the stranger in the garden, [[james-bisset|Bisset]] observing his employer's altered habits and revealing, over a game of billiards, his "expert knowledge" and the "other side of his character" (009-002, 009-005), visitors being received in the dining-room where [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]] follows her son to deliver a private word (010-051). The house becomes a theatre of imposture, its respectability a mask for murder.

The approach to the villa itself becomes a moment of reckoning. When [[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]] approaches it on the morning of the climax, "this morning he approached it with a sense of the irony of things" (036-007). The villa's solid, respectable exterior — "dim against the night sky" — cannot finally protect the secret it contains (035-008).

## Significance

The villa embodies the central irony of the novel: the most respectable house in the town harbours the deepest crime. Its "commodious" spaces, its garden and dining-room and smoking room, are the infrastructure of an impersonation. The question that haunts the villa — "of leaving his respectable villa at the dead of night, tramping..." (040-016) — is the question the novel ultimately answers: what would drive a man to abandon such a house, and what had he done within its walls?
