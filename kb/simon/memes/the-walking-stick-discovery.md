## Overview

The walking stick discovery was [[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]]'s most ingenious piece of detective work — a covert garden excavation carried out in broad daylight using nothing more than a specially selected walking stick. The discovery confirmed his suspicion that the impostor procurator fiscal had buried a body in his own flower beds, and provided the physical evidence that would ultimately lead to the arrest.

## Preparation

Carrington's method was characteristically understated. Dissatisfied with his light bamboo cane, he visited a shop and purchased a replacement chosen for very specific qualities:

> "The points on which he insisted were length, stiffness, and a long and if possible somewhat pointed ferule." (036-006)

The new walking stick was, in effect, a probe — long enough to penetrate deep into garden soil, stiff enough to transmit tactile information, and pointed enough to pierce the earth with minimal disturbance.

Carrington's choice of instrument was itself an act of concealment. A spade or garden fork would have been unmistakably suspicious, but a gentleman strolling with a walking stick was invisible — a figure so ordinary that even the sharpest observer would see nothing amiss. The walking stick was, as the shopkeeper's description suggests, "a very chaste and appropriate walking stick for such a gentleman as Mr. Carrington" (036-005).

## The Garden Investigation

Having enlisted [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]]'s unwitting cooperation — instructing her to keep cook occupied in the kitchen for half an hour — Carrington entered the garden alone:

> "When she first saw him, he seemed to be smoothing the earth in one of the flower beds with his foot. Then he moved on a few paces, stopped, and drove his walking stick hard into the bed. She saw him lean on it to get it further in and apparently twist it about a little." (036-027)

The impostor fiscal's sudden return by motor car nearly caught Carrington in the act. He leapt behind a bush as [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] entered the house, retrieved an overcoat, and departed again for [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] (036-027). The narrow escape demonstrated both Carrington's quick reflexes and the extreme risk he was running — had the impostor seen him, the entire investigation would have been compromised.

## The Discovery

Undeterred by the narrow escape, Carrington resumed his probing:

> "When she remembered to look again for her sympathetic friend, he was quietly driving his walking stick once more into a flower bed." (036-029)

The resistance and texture transmitted through the walking stick confirmed what Carrington had suspected — a body lay buried beneath the flower bed. This was the body of the real [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]], murdered and concealed by his twin brother.

The confirmation was more than physical. The walking stick transmitted not just the presence of something solid beneath the soil, but its dimensions — long enough to be a human body, positioned horizontally as a grave would require. Carrington's later confrontation with the impostor would reference this find directly: "your brother's body from that flower bed" (038-092).

## Aftermath

The walking stick discovery was the pivotal piece of physical evidence that transformed Carrington's investigation from theory to fact. It enabled the subsequent exhumation and identification of [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s body, and set in motion the final confrontation that would expose the impostor.

The exhumation itself, carried out by [[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]] and a party of constables, confirmed every detail Carrington had deduced through his walking stick probe. As he later told the superintendent, he had "prodded them all with a stout walking stick and found the spot" (040-038). The matter-of-factness of the statement belied the extraordinary daring of the method.

The walking stick itself became a symbol of Carrington's method — the application of civilian ingenuity to a problem that official procedure could not solve. Where the police required warrants and witnesses, Carrington required only a well-chosen walking stick, a plausible pretext, and the nerve to act.
