The burial of [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s body was the critical act of concealment that followed his murder by his twin brother [[george-rattar|George Rattar]]. Having killed Simon on the night of his return, George buried the body in a flower bed in the garden of [[simon-rattars-villa|Simon Rattar's Villa]] — the very villa where Simon had lived and which George now occupied in his stolen identity. The burial was both a practical necessity and a symbolic act: by planting his brother's body in the garden of the house Simon had made his own, George was literally burying the evidence beneath the surface of his new life.

## The Night of the Murder

After killing Simon, George faced the immediate problem of disposing of the body before the servants returned. As he later confessed (039-026):

> Before the maids came back I had to get Simon stowed away for the night — no time to bury him then of course, and I had to get into his clothes, shave, and learn the lie of the house and all that.

The transformation was physical as well as psychological. George had to strip his dead brother's body, dress himself in Simon's clothes, shave his own face clean, and then navigate a house whose geography he knew only from his twin's letters. Every creak of a floorboard, every unfamiliar door, was a potential exposure. The imposture that would last for weeks began in those first desperate hours with the dead body still warm in an adjacent room.

## The Burial

The following night — the night when [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] watched [[simon-rattars-villa|Simon Rattar's Villa]] from the garden — George buried Simon's body in one of the flower beds. This was, by his own account, the most difficult part of the entire imposture (039-031):

> It was burying the body that night.

The burial had to be carried out in darkness, in silence, within a garden that was visible from the house windows. George could not use a light; the sound of a spade striking soil would carry on the still night air; and at any moment a servant might glance out of a window at the wrong instant. The physical labour of digging a grave deep enough to conceal a body — all while maintaining the silence and invisibility that the imposture required — was an act of extraordinary nerve.

That same night, Carrington watched this very garden, observing the "thickset form of Simon Rattar" — actually George — crossing the lawn in the dark (035-011), and later following alone through the garden (035-017). Carrington did not yet know what was buried there, but his instincts told him the garden held a secret worth watching.

## Discovery

The burial site was discovered through the methodical investigation of [[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]]. After reconstructing the timeline from [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]]'s testimony, Carrington identified the critical observation: she had seen a man in the garden smoothing earth in the flower beds and probing with a walking stick (036-027). The image of a man poking at flower beds with a walking stick in the dead of night was suspicious enough on its face, but Carrington understood its deeper significance. The walking stick was not being used for walking — it was being used to test the depth of the soil, to probe for the right spot.

The absence of any wheeled vehicle sound eliminated the possibility that the body had been transported elsewhere: "she heard no sound of a wheeled vehicle" (040-038). If the body had been taken away by cart or motor, someone would have heard it. The silence of the night after the murder told Carrington that the body was still on the premises — and the flower beds, smoothed and disturbed, were the only possible hiding place.

Carrington tested the flower beds by driving his walking stick into them, finding the spot where the earth had been disturbed (036-027, 036-029). The resistance of the soil, its texture and give, told him what he needed to know. This evidence, combined with the signet ring that could not be removed from Simon's finger — "he never could get it off his finger before!" (033-046) — proved decisive.

## The Exhumation

The body was dug up in the presence of the authorities. The moment of revelation was devastating. Carrington held up the gold signet ring — still on the corpse's finger — before George Rattar with the quiet words (038-092):

> "We have just dug up your brother's body from that flower bed. Do you recognise his ring?"

George "fell back in his chair" (038-093). The ring — the same ring that had been mentioned in Simon's will, the same ring that Mary MacLean had seen on the dead man's hand — was the incontrovertible proof of identity. A man could change his face, his clothes, his voice, his manner; he could not change the ring that death had fixed upon his brother's finger. The imposture that had held for weeks collapsed in a single moment.

## Significance

The burial was both a practical act of concealment and the symbolic centre of the novel's mystery. That the body lay hidden in the garden throughout the entire investigation — while the murderer, posing as the victim, directed inquiries — created the central dramatic irony of the story. Every time the impostor fiscal questioned a witness about the murder, every time he suggested a theory of motive, every time he directed suspicion toward an innocent party, he was standing on ground that concealed the answer. The flower bed burial also served as the physical proof that finally broke George Rattar's impersonation, since the signet ring, immovable from the dead man's finger, established identity beyond dispute.
