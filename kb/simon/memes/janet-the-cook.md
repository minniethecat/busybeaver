# Janet the Cook

Janet is the cook in [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s household and the roommate of [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]], the housemaid. Her practical, no-nonsense personality and cheerful scorn provide a grounding presence amid the growing unease in the Rattar villa — a character whose earthy skepticism acts as a narrative foil to the Gothic atmosphere that increasingly pervades the household.

## Character

Janet is defined by her sharp tongue and instinctive skepticism toward anything that smacks of superstition or fancy. When Mary MacLean returns from an errand visibly shaken by a strange encounter — a man peering through the library window — Janet dismisses her fears with characteristic bluntness:

> "Hoots! It's just your Hieland fancies!" said cook, but she said it a little uncertainly. [002-053]

The qualifying clause — "she said it a little uncertainly" — is crucial. Despite her dismissive exterior, Janet is not entirely immune to the unease that affects the household. When Mary insists that their master Simon Rattar is genuinely afraid — "The master's feared of yon man, Janet!" (002-048) — even Janet is "a little impressed by her solemnity" (002-049). The cook's skepticism, the narrative suggests, is a pose that can be penetrated by sufficiently disturbing evidence.

This ambiguity in Janet's character recurs throughout the novel. She is described as "a practical person and apt to be a trifle unsympathetic" (002-051) — the word "trifle" suggesting that her unsympathetic manner is a matter of degree rather than absoluteness. She is not cruel, merely impatient with what she considers Highland superstition.

## Role in the Household

As the cook, Janet occupies a position of practical authority in the domestic sphere of [[simon-rattars-villa|Simon Rattar's villa]]. She and Mary share a bedroom in the servants' quarters, partitioned from the front of the house by a door at the end of the hall. Their nighttime conversations provide the reader with a servants'-eye view of the Rattar household's dynamics — a perspective unavailable through any other character.

Janet's kitchen serves as a space where the tensions of the larger plot are filtered through the lens of domestic routine. It is a domain of pots and pans, gossip and practicalities, standing in contrast to the locked rooms upstairs and the secrets they conceal.

## The Night of the Search

One of the novel's most atmospherically charged scenes involves Mary lying awake at night, listening to mysterious sounds from the top storey, while Janet sleeps soundly beside her:

> "Cook was asleep within ten minutes. Mary could hear her heavy breathing above the incessant droning and whistling of the wind, and she envied her with all her Highland heart." [006-006]

The contrast is deliberate and pointed: Janet, the practical Lowland woman, sleeps without difficulty; Mary, with her Highland sensitivity to the uncanny, lies awake trembling. When Mary later ventures to mention the sounds, Janet's response is characteristically dismissive:

> "There's something in your top storey!" snapped cook; and poor Mary said no more. [006-012]

Yet Janet's scorn, however wounding to Mary, serves a narrative purpose. It isolates Mary in her fear and forces her to confront the mystery alone — heightening the suspense for the reader.

## During the Investigation

Janet's kitchen becomes an unwitting stage for [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]]'s investigative stratagem. When Carrington needs to enter the garden of the Rattar villa without Simon's knowledge, he enlists Mary's help in a scheme that depends entirely on Janet's love of conversation:

> "I want you to go now and have a nice quiet talk with cook for half an hour — half an hour by the kitchen clock, Mary." [036-021]

Mary is explicitly instructed: "cook is to be kept talking in the kitchen" (036-023). She faithfully goes "to the kitchen for her talk with cook" (036-025), and the plan succeeds — though not without comic tension:

> "It was quite a pleasant gossip at first, but half an hour is a long time to keep talking, when one has been asked not to stop sooner, and it so happened, moreover, that cook was somewhat busy that morning and began at length to indicate distinctly that unless her friend had some matter of importance to communicate she would regard further verbiage with disfavour." [036-026]

Mary's tactical decision that "twenty minutes was practically as good as half an hour" (036-026) is one of the novel's small comic moments — and a testament to Janet's formidable personality, which can wear down even a determined conversationalist.

## Narrative Significance

Janet functions as a foil on multiple levels. Against Mary's Highland mysticism, she represents Lowland practicality. Against the Gothic secrecy of the Rattar household, she represents the ordinary, the domestic, the explicable. Her kitchen is a sanctuary of normality in a house increasingly defined by locked doors, night-time footsteps, and unspoken fear.

And yet the novel is careful not to make Janet merely a figure of comic relief or flat skepticism. Her "little uncertainly" when dismissing Mary's fears, her being "a little impressed" by Mary's solemnity — these moments suggest that the boundary between practical sense and denial is thinner than Janet would like to admit. In a novel where appearances are systematically deceptive, even the cook's common sense may be a form of wishful thinking.
