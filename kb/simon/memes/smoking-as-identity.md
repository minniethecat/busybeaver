The distinction between smokers and non-smokers runs through the novel as a quiet but persistent marker of identity — one of several small details by which [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] could be distinguished from his brother [[george-rattar|George]], and by which the imposture could ultimately be detected.

## Simon Rattar: The Non-Smoking Principle

Simon Rattar was, by settled principle, not a smoker. His character was defined by abstemious control:

> "An elder of the Kirk, a nonsmoker, an abstemious drinker (to the extent of one glass of sherry after dinner), and a worker of the kind that never hurries and never stops — these were the remaining fixed points of Simon Rattar's character." (002-005)

His housemaid [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]] confirmed this trait: "the master's not a smoking gentleman either — quite a principle against it" (033-057).

This was not merely a personal preference but part of Simon's broader public persona — the controlled, respectable, upright public man. The [[glass-match-box|glass match-box]] in his library was kept "for the benefit of visitors merely" (002-036), an ornamental object in a room whose owner had no use for it. Simon himself stated: "I do not smoke" (022-015).

## George and Ned: The Smokers

In contrast, [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] was a habitual smoker. [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]], too, was frequently depicted with cigar or pipe: "He had forgotten to light his cigar, and it was probably as a substitute for smoking that he found himself whistling" (004-064). When [[james-bisset|Bisset]] visited Stanesland, he was received in "the laird of Stanesland's smoking room" (016-002), and Ned was observed to have "been smoking a pipe" (016-056).

[[carringtons-investigation|Carrington]] himself was a distinctive smoker: "Even in the way in which he smoked his cigarette in its amber holder there was a suggestion of the epicure in the little things of life" (020-005). His cigarette smoke was often described rising thoughtfully toward the ceiling as he pondered the case.

## A Forensic Distinction

The smoking habit became a material clue in the investigation. [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]] noticed on the morning after George's arrival that "there was no nice redheaded bundle of matches in the glass match box" — a small anomaly that hinted at disturbance. The missing matches, the smell of pipe smoke, the ash — these were forensic traces that distinguished the imposter from the man he replaced.

More profoundly, the habit represented a philosophical divide. Carrington articulated the principle:

> "There must be some motive for everything one does — even for smoking this cigarette. If I disliked cigarettes, knew smoking was bad for me, and stood in danger of being fined if I was caught doing it, why should I smoke?" (034-072)

The answer — identity — was precisely what made smoking such a valuable clue. A man who "does not smoke" by principle cannot suddenly become a smoker without raising questions about who he really is.
