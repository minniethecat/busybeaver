# Procurator Fiscal

## Definition

In Scottish law, the **Procurator Fiscal** is a public official who serves as the public prosecutor, investigates sudden or suspicious deaths, and directs criminal investigations. The role combines functions that in other legal systems would be divided among a district attorney, coroner, and director of public prosecutions — a concentration of authority that J. Storer Clouston exploits to devastating narrative effect.

In *Simon*, Clouston embeds this distinctly Scottish institution at the heart of the narrative. [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]], the novel's title character, holds the office for an unnamed northern county, and his status as fiscal gives him authority over the criminal investigation that drives the plot (002-003).

## Context in the Novel

[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s position as procurator fiscal is fundamental to the novel's structure. As fiscal, he is officially "the setter in motion of all criminal processes, and generalissimo, so to speak, of the police" (002-003). This authority places him at the centre of the investigation into [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]'s murder — and, simultaneously, at the centre of the crime itself.

The novel's central dramatic irony springs from [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]]'s dual role. When the housemaid [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]] reports finding a suspicious stranger in the fiscal's garden, the official response is immediate: "As procurator fiscal, it is my business to inquire into such affairs. I'll see to it" (002-040). Every word of this assurance is technically true — and deeply deceptive, for the man speaking is not [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] at all, but his twin brother [[george-rattar|George Rattar]], who has murdered the real fiscal and assumed his identity.

The fiscal's daily routine is established in the novel's second chapter, where he breakfasts "at 8:30, punctually" and begins his day with mechanical regularity — until the morning when a mysterious figure in his garden disrupts the pattern (002-002). This disruption of routine is the novel's first signal that the institutional order the fiscal represents has been compromised.

## The Fiscal as Structural Device

Clouston's choice to make his central character a procurator fiscal was not incidental — it was a masterstroke of plotting that makes the novel's central deception both plausible and thematically resonant.

**Concentration of authority.** The Scottish fiscal's unique combination of prosecutorial, investigative, and coronial powers means that a single impostor in the fiscal's chair controls every lever of the official response to Sir Reginald's murder. George Rattar does not need to subvert multiple institutions; he needs only to occupy one office. The plausibility of the imposture — often the weakest point in doppelgänger plots — is shored up by the institutional reality that the fiscal really does have this much power.

**The impersonation of justice.** When George Rattar speaks as fiscal, he speaks with the voice of the law. His every pronouncement — "it is my business to inquire into such affairs" (002-040) — carries institutional weight, and the community's deference to that weight is what makes the deception sustainable. The novel thus becomes a study in how legal authority, when separated from moral legitimacy, becomes an instrument of crime rather than its remedy. The fiscal's office does not detect the impostor; it protects him.

**The fiscal's silence.** The real Simon Rattar's defining characteristic was his silence — "silent Simon" (002-050) was known for saying little and revealing less. This quality, which in the genuine fiscal was professional discretion, becomes in the impostor a convenient cover. George Rattar's silences are read as the fiscal's characteristic reserve, and the community does not press for answers because it has learned not to expect them. The fiscal's professional persona becomes the mask behind which the murderer hides.

## Narrative Function

The office of procurator fiscal serves four distinct narrative functions in the novel:

1. **Plot engine**: The fiscal's investigative authority makes him the natural centre of the murder inquiry. Every piece of evidence passes through his hands, and his ability to direct — and misdirect — the investigation drives the plot forward.

2. **Irony generator**: The reader's knowledge that the fiscal is an impostor transforms every official action into a double performance. When he assures Mary MacLean that he will investigate, when he directs the police, when he questions witnesses — every scene operates on two levels simultaneously.

3. **Institutional critique**: The novel implicitly asks whether any office should concentrate as much power as the Scottish fiscal's. The answer, dramatised through George Rattar's imposture, is that concentration of authority is an invitation to abuse — a critique that remains relevant to any legal system.

4. **Thematic vehicle**: The fiscal represents the law as institution, and the imposture dramatises the gap between legal authority and moral legitimacy. The novel's resolution — in which Carrington, an outsider operating outside official channels, exposes the truth — argues that justice sometimes requires working around, rather than through, institutional structures.

## Related Characters

- [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] — the original procurator fiscal, a man of "hard, cautious, never-caught-asleep" abilities (002-004), whose murder and replacement by his twin brother are the novel's central crime.
- [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] — Simon's twin brother, who impersonates him and assumes the fiscal's office and authority to cover his crimes.
- [[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]] — the local police chief who operates under the fiscal's direction during the murder investigation — and whose deference to institutional authority makes him an unwitting accomplice to the imposture.
- [[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]] — the London-based private enquiry agent whose independent investigation gradually uncovers the impostor's identity (020-005), representing an alternative model of justice that operates outside institutional channels.

## Real-World Background

The office of Procurator Fiscal originated in 16th-century Scotland and remains a cornerstone of the Scottish legal system today. Unlike England and Wales, where the Crown Prosecution Service prosecutes crimes and coroners investigate deaths separately, the Scottish fiscal combines both responsibilities. The fiscal also directs the police in criminal investigations — a structural feature that Clouston exploits to great effect, making it plausible that a single official might both commit and control the investigation of a crime.

Clouston, a Scottish author born in Cumberland and educated at Oxford, wrote *Simon* (1919) with intimate knowledge of Scottish legal institutions. The novel's accurate portrayal of the fiscal's wide authority was immediately recognisable to contemporary Scottish readers, lending verisimilitude to what might otherwise seem an improbable plot device. The novel thus functions simultaneously as a thriller and as a document of Scottish legal culture at the turn of the twentieth century.
