# Cicely Farmond

Cicely Farmond is the young gentlewoman whose romantic situation generates much of the novel's emotional tension. The narrative introduces her in a single composed sentence:

> Miss Cicely Farmond's air as she entered Simon Rattar's room seemed compounded of a little shyness, considerable trepidation, and yet more determination. (005-002)

This tripartite characterisation — shyness, trepidation, determination — is the key Clouston will play in every major scene that follows. Secretly engaged to [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]] — "a clandestine engagement" (014-083) — she is also the object of [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s direct and unaffected admiration. Her position at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] places her at the intersection of the novel's romantic and criminal plots.

## Character

Cicely's manner combines gentility with an underlying firmness. When she enters [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s room, her "air seemed compounded of a certain nervousness and a certain determination" (005-002). This combination of vulnerability and resolve defines her throughout the novel — she is a young woman navigating the constraints of Edwardian courtship while maintaining her own preferences and judgments.

## Role in the Plot

Cicely's secret engagement to [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]] becomes entangled with the question of the Cromarty inheritance. [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s opposition to the match — "a secret engagement, like, that [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] wouldn't have allowed" (016-042) — makes his death, however tragic, the removal of an obstacle to the couple's happiness. This morally complex situation is handled with the emotional restraint characteristic of the novel's period.

Her position is materially precarious: she is a guest with no independent means, dependent on [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]]'s goodwill. After Sir Reginald's death, Lady Cromarty "sent for Miss Farmond and tellt her then she was wanting her to stop on" (016-061) — an act of loyalty that subtly underscores Cicely's vulnerability: she stays not by right but by invitation, and in a household convulsed by murder, her security hangs on the thin thread of a dowager's affection.

## Key Relationships

- [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]] — her secret fiancé, the heir to the baronetcy
- [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] — the laird of [[stanesland|Stanesland]], who courts her with [[america|America]]n directness
- [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]] — her hostess at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]]

## In the Eyes of Others

How other characters in the novel perceive and speak of Cicely Farmond reveals as much about them as it does about her.

- **[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s frank admiration**: Ned makes no secret of his attraction from their very first drive together. His directness — "The fact is, Miss Farmond, you are a bit of a treat" (007-017) — is the antithesis of Edwardian courtship convention, and his self-deprecating humour when he jokes that "the only kind of lady likely to take an interest in me was a female collector of antique curiosities" (007-025) gives his admiration an unaffected warmth.

- **[[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s approving but calculating eye**: The late baronet was personally fond of Cicely — he was "unco' taken up with Miss Farmond" (016-042) — yet this warmth did not override dynastic considerations: "he'd have looked higher for his heir" (016-042). Sir Reginald's view encapsulates the tension between personal affection and social expectation that shapes Cicely's position throughout the novel.

- **[[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]]'s protective attachment**: After Sir Reginald's death, Lady Cromarty "sent for Miss Farmond and tellt her then she was wanting her to stop on" (016-061). The dowager's desire to keep Cicely at Keldale House speaks to a bond that runs deeper than mere hospitality — a recognition of Cicely's steadiness in a household unmoored by murder.

- **[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s guarded professionalism**: When Cicely consults him about the "identity" question, Rattar addresses her with careful formality — "May I ask, Miss Farmond, why, in that case, you think I shouldn't have told you before" (005-020). His guarded, legalistic manner with her contrasts sharply with the easy familiarity others extend, marking him as an outsider to the Keldale circle even before his imposture is known.

- **[[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]]'s gallant partiality**: Carrington, though a professional investigator, allows himself a personal note: "My relative Miss Cicely Farmond — a charming girl, I may mention; there was every excuse for me" (030-036). Even the detective, who ought to be impartial, cannot entirely resist the pull of Cicely's appeal.

## Author's Craft

J. Storer Clouston deploys several narrative techniques in his handling of Cicely Farmond that go beyond simple characterisation.

- **Contrast in suitors**: The opposition between Ned Cromarty's blunt [[america|America]]n manner and Malcolm Cromarty's restrained heir's propriety is drawn through their treatment of Cicely. Ned gives her his overcoat without ceremony:

> Under the grey autumnal sky Miss Cicely Farmond drove out of the town wrapped in Ned Cromarty's overcoat. (007-002)

Malcolm, by contrast, walks up the avenue beside her "pushing his bicycle" (008-002), their physical distance mirroring the social caution of their secret engagement. The reader understands both men better through the contrast their courtship styles provide.

- **Atmospheric framing**: Cicely's most significant appearances are set against weather that mirrors narrative tension. She first drives out "under the grey autumnal sky" (007-002) in Ned's trap — a scene that establishes both her vulnerability and the novel's tonal register. Clouston consistently places her against subdued, transitional landscapes, a technique that reinforces her suspended position between youth and adult commitment, between the man she is engaged to and the man who openly admires her.

- **Compression through omission**: Clouston never gives the reader a direct scene of Cicely alone. Every significant moment — her consultation with Rattar, her drives with Ned, her walks with Malcolm — involves an interlocutor. This technique keeps Cicely always *observed*, never self-revealing, which sustains the reader's sympathy by keeping her inner life an object of inference rather than statement — a restraint wholly in keeping with the novel's Edwardian sensibility.

- **The opening sentence as microcosm**: The novel's first description of Cicely — "a little shyness, considerable trepidation, and yet more determination" (005-002) — is a miniature of the narrative method itself: three qualities balanced in ascending order, the last overturning the first two. Shyness and trepidation are the expected notes for an Edwardian heroine; "yet more determination" is the surprise, and it is this third quality that drives every significant choice Cicely makes thereafter.

## Narrative Impact

Cicely Farmond occupies a structural position in the novel that is more complex than it first appears. She is, on the surface, the conventional romantic interest — the young woman whose heart is contested by two men. But Clouston uses her as a pivot: she is the person around whom the question of "what kind of man?" is asked and answered. Her suitors — Malcolm with his inheritance, Ned with his directness — represent two visions of masculinity, two ways of being a man in late-Edwardian Scotland, and her choice between them is also the novel's own judgment.

More subtly, Cicely is the novel's moral weathervane. When the household is nervous, her "trepidation" registers it; when the investigation demands courage, her "determination" supplies it. She is not merely acted upon — she is the instrument through which the novel measures the moral temperature of every scene she enters. That Clouston never allows the reader inside her thoughts is a deliberate technical choice: Cicely remains, like the novel's central mystery, something to be inferred from external evidence — a puzzle whose solution the reader must construct from fragments, exactly as Carrington constructs the case.
