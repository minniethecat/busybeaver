# The Murder of Simon Rattar

The murder of [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] by his twin brother George is the novel's originating crime — the act that occurs before the narrative proper begins and that makes possible all subsequent events. Simon, the procurator fiscal and widely respected lawyer, is killed by his criminal twin, who then assumes his identity, his villa, and his office — directing the investigation into his own crime.

## The Rattar Twins

Simon and George Rattar were twin brothers of sharply diverging character. Simon rose to become the town's procurator fiscal — "more widely respected than Mr. Simon Rattar" was a phrase that described his standing (002-003). George, by contrast, had been Simon's business partner before leaving the firm under circumstances that the community preferred not to discuss. When [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] innocently asks "Who was George Rattar?" at a dinner gathering, the question provokes visible discomfort — and her host's sharp "George Rattar? What do you know about him?" (010-003, 010-005). She learns that George "was once Simon Rattar's partner" and is warned never to mention him before the fiscal (010-016, 010-018). George is later confirmed to have died abroad "a short time" before the narrative's later events (032-012, 032-013) — a detail whose timing acquires retrospective significance.

## The Crime

The murder itself is never depicted on the page. It occurs in the space between chapters, at [[simon-rattars-villa|Simon Rattar's villa]], the "commodious" residence he had acquired when he came to the town (006-002). The act is one of substitution: George eliminates Simon and steps into his life, counting on their physical resemblance to escape detection. The impersonation is the murder's true purpose — George does not merely kill Simon, he *becomes* him.

## Discovery Through Inconsistency

The murder is not discovered through physical evidence but through the accumulating pattern of behavioural inconsistencies that the impostor cannot fully control. Simon had been proverbially cautious, a man of fixed habits and few words (004-004). The impostor, by contrast, cannot entirely suppress his own nature: he smokes — a habit the real Simon never indulged — and in unguarded moments becomes voluble, even humorous. "Even Simon Rattar's features relaxed for an instant," an observer notes, and "Simon Rattar might have been thought the kindest of men" (004-010, 004-029) — observations that read, in retrospect, as clues.

The housemaid [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]] is the first to sense something wrong. She had entered Simon's service with a "strong sense of duty" and an observant eye (002-007). She notices that her master is "not quite like the usual Simon Rattar" and begins to feel "a sympathy for him which was entirely new" (002-028) — the impostor, less guarded than the real Simon, inadvertently inspires the warmth that the original never did. Mary's later declaration of a premonitory "feeling of disquiet" on the morning of the investigation's climax adds to the retrospective patterning of the crime (006-004). The sighting of a stranger in the fiscal's garden (002-037) is, in the final accounting, connected to the moment of substitution.

## Significance

Simon's murder is significant not only as a crime but as the act that enables the novel's central dramatic irony. For the duration of the narrative, the impostor directs the investigation, receives reports from [[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]], and exercises the legal authority of the office — all from the position of the man he has killed. The murder weapon is not a knife or a gun but resemblance itself; the crime's concealment depends on the very physical identity that should have made detection inevitable. The novel thus transforms a sensational murder plot into a study of identity, authority, and the fragility of the social order that both depend upon.
