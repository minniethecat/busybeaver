# Cicely's Diaries

The personal correspondence and records of Cicely Farmond formed an important thread of
documentary evidence in the events surrounding her arrival at Keldale House and the subsequent
Cromarty murder. While no formal diary is explicitly described in the surviving accounts,
Cicely's letters, notes, and personal papers provided crucial links in establishing the
chain of relationships and events.

## The Letter from Simon Rattar

The first documented contact between Cicely and the Cromarty circle came through a letter
from Simon Rattar. About a month after her mother's death, Cicely received "a note from
[Rattar] asking [her] to go up to London and meet Sir Reginald Cromarty" (005-044).
She had never heard of Sir Reginald before this correspondence, which initiated her
connection to the Cromarty family.

## Cicely's Reserve and Private Nature

Cicely was notably private about her personal affairs. As described, she "was not by way
of giving her confidences easily" (007-036). This reserve made her personal papers and
correspondence all the more significant as the principal window into her thoughts and
circumstances during the critical period.

## Newspaper as Window to Events

Cicely followed events through the local press, and it was through a newspaper that she
learned of developments in the case. On one occasion, she "looked up from the local paper
she was reading" to question a detail (PN from chapter context), demonstrating her active
engagement with unfolding events through written records.

## Documentary Significance

The letters between Cicely and Simon Rattar were significant because they established the
timeline of her introduction to the Cromarty family. Rattar, as lawyer and factor to the
Cromarty estate, used written correspondence to arrange the fateful meeting between Cicely
and Sir Reginald. These documents would later form part of the broader pattern of evidence
surrounding Rattar's manipulation of the family's affairs.
