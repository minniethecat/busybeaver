## Summary

The discovery of [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s body buried in the garden flower bed of his own villa was the decisive physical evidence that brought the impostor [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] to justice. The discovery unfolded in two phases: Carrington's daytime reconnaissance of the garden, during which he probed the flower bed with his walking stick; and the nighttime excavation by Carrington, [[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]], and a constable while [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] occupied the impostor inside the house.

## Context

[[carringtons-investigation]] had established that the man occupying [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s office and home was an impostor, but circumstantial evidence alone was insufficient to make an arrest on a charge of capital murder. Carrington needed physical proof — the real [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s body. His questioning of [[mary-maclean|Mary]], the housekeeper, had revealed that "the master was out in the garden again last night" (036-011), a peculiar behaviour that suggested nocturnal visits to a specific location.

The impostor's earlier loss of his (Simon's) signet ring — advertised with a reward of one pound in the newspaper (see [[the-letter-chapter-x]]) — was another piece of the puzzle. The ring had not been lost on the road; it had come off Simon's finger during the burial.

## Sequence

### Phase One: Daytime Reconnaissance

Carrington visited [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s villa during the day while "Simon" was out. He told the housekeeper [[mary-maclean|Mary]] that he wanted "to have a look round the garden" (036-016). To protect [[mary-maclean|Mary]] from accountability, he instructed her: "If you don't look out of the window, you won't know that I'm in the garden, and then nobody can blame you whatever happens" (036-021).

[[mary-maclean|Mary]] watched briefly despite his instructions and observed Carrington's methodical examination:

> When she first saw him, he seemed to be smoothing the earth in one of the flower beds with his foot. Then he moved on a few paces, stopped, and drove his walking stick hard into the bed. (036-027)

Carrington's walking stick, which he had been testing and adjusting earlier (036-005), was in fact a probe — a tool for detecting disturbed earth and the presence of a body beneath the surface. Later, when "Simon" returned unexpectedly, Carrington watched from outside as the housekeeper saw him "quietly driving his walking stick once more into a flower bed" (036-029). The impostor, unaware he was being watched, had driven away in his car, and Carrington resumed his search.

### Phase Two: Nighttime Excavation

That night, Carrington, Cromarty, [[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]], and a constable approached the villa under cover of darkness. [[superintendent-sutherland|Sutherland]] and the constable each carried "something long-shaped wrapped up loosely in sacking" (038-016) — spades.

At the villa gates, the party divided. Cromarty entered the house to engage "Simon" in conversation while Carrington and the police began their work in the garden. The excavation was detected by the housekeeper [[mary-maclean|Mary]], who burst into the study to report:

> "Please, sir, there are men in the garden!" she cried. "It sounded almost as if they was digging." (038-062, 038-065)

The impostor, now trapped between Cromarty inside and the excavators outside, dismissed [[mary-maclean|Mary]]. Moments later, footsteps sounded on the gravel (038-078).

### The Revelation

Carrington entered with the police and produced the decisive evidence:

> "We have just dug up your brother's body from that flower bed," said Carrington quietly. "Do you recognise his ring?" He held up a gold signet ring, and the lawyer fell back in his chair. (038-092, 038-093)

The signet ring — the same one "Simon" had advertised as lost for a one-pound reward — had been on the real [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s finger when George buried him. It was the physical link between the buried body and the living impostor.

## Key Participants

- **[[f-t-carrington]]** — The private enquiry agent whose methodical investigation led him from [[mary-maclean|Mary]]'s gossip about nocturnal garden visits to the physical discovery of the body. His walking-stick probe and nighttime excavation demonstrated the transition from speculation to proof.
- **[[george-rattar]]** — The impostor, whose carefully maintained deception collapsed when the body of the brother he had murdered was unearthed from his own garden.
- **[[mary-maclean|Mary]]** — The housekeeper at [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s villa, whose observation that "the master was out in the garden again last night" (036-011) provided the crucial lead, and whose alarm at the sounds of digging signalled the moment of exposure.
- **[[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]]** — The local police superintendent who participated in the excavation and formal arrest.
- **[[ned-cromarty]]** — The laird of [[stanesland|Stanesland]] whose conversation with "Simon" inside the villa bought time for the excavation.

## Outcome / Significance

The discovery in the garden was the pivot on which the entire murder investigation turned. Until the body was found, Carrington had only circumstantial evidence of the imposture. The excavation provided:

1. **Physical proof of murder**: The real [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s body, buried in a shallow grave in the flower bed of his own property.
2. **The signet ring**: An item of incontrovertible identification linking the body to the missing procurator fiscal and to the man who had advertised its loss.
3. **Immediate grounds for arrest**: The evidence enabled Carrington to direct [[superintendent-sutherland|Sutherland]] to arrest "[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]" and reveal him as [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] (see [[the-confrontation-with-the-prisoner]]).

The discovery also illuminated the impostor's behaviour: his nocturnal visits to the garden were not the eccentricity of a reclusive lawyer but the compulsive revisiting of a murderer's secret grave.
