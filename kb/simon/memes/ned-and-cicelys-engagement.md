# Ned and Cicely's Engagement

The engagement of [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] and [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] provides the novel's romantic resolution. In contrast to Cicely's secret engagement to [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]] — conducted in fear of [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s opposition — her relationship with Ned is characterised by openness, directness, and mutual affection uncomplicated by inheritance calculations.

## Courtship

Ned's courtship of Cicely is conducted with the directness of his [[america|America]]n experience. Unlike Malcolm's cautious secrecy, Ned pursues his romantic interest openly, and his unconventional background — the cowboy turned laird, the glass eye, the frontier past — distinguishes him from the conventional suitors of Cicely's social world. His sister Lilian's quiet support provides domestic sanction for the match.

## Significance

The engagement represents the restoration of social order through romantic union. Where the novel's central crime represents disorder — murder, impersonation, the corruption of institutions — the marriage plot offers the countervailing promise of continuity, legitimacy, and renewal. The cowboy-laird and the gentlewoman, each unconventional in their own way, form a couple whose union symbolises the reconciliation of the novel's competing values.

## First Meeting

Ned and Cicely's first meeting occurs at [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s law office, where Cicely has come on legal business and Ned is consulting [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]] about his inheritance. "Miss [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] moved towards the door" (004-055) as Ned lingered — a crossing of paths that neither could have predicted would lead to romance. The setting is notable: their relationship begins not in a drawing room but in the professional spaces of the law, already embedded in the property and inheritance questions that structure the novel.

## American Courtship

Ned's courtship style reflects his [[america|America]]n years. Driving Cicely home from the station, "wrapped in [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s overcoat" (007-002), he demonstrates a direct concern for her comfort that Scottish convention would consider forward. "[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] had a singular gift of making his meaning clear without the formality of explicit statement" (007-050) — a gift that serves him well in cutting through the indirection and secrecy that characterized Cicely's previous engagement. Where [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]] pleaded for sympathy, Ned simply acts.

## The Resolution

Their engagement is not dramatized as a single moment but emerges gradually across the novel's closing chapters. After the murder is solved and the impostor exposed, the relationship that began in [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]]'s office and deepened through shared danger finds its natural conclusion. The engagement is the novel's affirmation that love conducted with honesty and directness can survive the deceptions that have corrupted nearly every other social bond in the story.
