# Whisky

## Definition

**Scotch whisky** is a distilled spirit produced in Scotland, governed by strict legal definitions and deeply embedded in Scottish cultural identity. In the early 20th century, whisky was both a quotidian social beverage — "whisky and soda" being the standard gentleman's order — and a potent symbol of Scottishness itself.

In *Simon*, whisky appears at moments of confession, relaxation, and violence, threading through the narrative as a marker of masculine social ritual and, in one crucial flashback, as a weapon.

## Context in the Novel

The most dramatic invocation of whisky occurs in [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s account of his cowboy past: "he had his eye put out with a broken whisky bottle and hanged the man who did it" (004-026). The whisky bottle here is not a drink but an instrument of violence, linking the Scottish spirit to the [[america|America]]n frontier justice that cost Ned his eye.

In more convivial settings, whisky serves as social currency. At the [[kings-arms-hotel|Kings Arms Hotel]], Carrington orders "another whisky and soda to cure the creeps" (020-022), using the drink to establish rapport with [[miss-peterkin|Miss Peterkin]] while gathering information about the Cromarty case. The ritual of offering and accepting whisky marks inclusion in the novel's masculine social world.

The suggestion that the glass eye's story might be embellished — "he always says himself it was done wi' a whisky bottle, but possibly that's merely his [[america|America]]n way of putting it" (009-023) — hints at the myth-making function of the whisky bottle as a narrative prop in Ned's self-presentation.

## Real-World Background

By the early 20th century, Scotch whisky had achieved global recognition, with blended whiskies dominating both domestic and export markets. In Scottish rural society, whisky was integrated into daily life — offered to guests, prescribed for ailments, and present at every significant social occasion. The "whisky and soda" of the Edwardian gentleman was typically a blended Scotch served with carbonated water, a ritual that signalled leisured masculine sociability. Clouston's use of whisky as both social lubricant and narrative symbol draws on this deep cultural embedding while exploiting its dramatic potential as an object that can shift registers from hospitality to violence.
