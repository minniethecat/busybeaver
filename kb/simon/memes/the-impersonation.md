# The Impersonation

The impersonation of [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] by his twin brother George is the novel's foundational crime — the act that precedes and enables all subsequent events. Before the narrative proper begins, George has murdered Simon and taken his place as procurator fiscal, lawyer, and factor. The entire novel unfolds under the shadow of this substitution.

## Execution

George's assumption of his brother's identity is facilitated by their physical identity as twins. "They were twins. George was the one with a mustache" (010-020) — a detail that George eliminates to perfect the resemblance. The impersonation is sustained for approximately one month, during which George conducts the official business of [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]]'s office, directs the murder investigation, and maintains the social façade of the respectable lawyer.

## Detection

The impersonation is undone by cumulative inconsistencies. Simon was a non-smoker; George is a heavy smoker. Simon was genuinely taciturn; George's occasional volubility produces "rather a long speech for silent Simon" (006-019). Behaviour that is "not at all characteristic of silent Simon" (017-057) accumulates until Carrington's investigation forces the truth into the open.

## The Twin Motif

The impersonation exploits one of detective fiction's most potent devices: the identical twin as the perfect substitute. Clouston's use of the twin motif is unusually sophisticated, however, because the impersonation is not merely a mechanical substitution of one body for another. George must sustain not only Simon's physical appearance but his professional competence, his social relationships, and — most precariously — his characteristic silence. The impossibility of perfectly replicating another person's entire mode of being is what ultimately dooms the imposture.

## The Irony of Position

A grim irony structures the impersonation: George, posing as Simon the procurator fiscal, is officially tasked with investigating the very murder he has committed. He must direct enquiries, examine evidence, and question witnesses — all while ensuring that the investigation never leads to himself. This narrative situation generates sustained dramatic irony, as the reader watches the murderer conduct the murder investigation with apparent professional diligence while subtly steering it away from the truth. The double position — criminal and investigator — makes the impersonation one of the most narratively rich identity deceptions in early detective fiction.

## Psychological Toll

The impersonation is not psychologically sustainable. As the narrative progresses, George's performance shows signs of strain: the uncharacteristic volubility, the lapses in judgment, the growing desperation. The imposture may be physically perfect, but the psychological cost of inhabiting another person's life — particularly when that person is one's murdered twin — proves unbearable. The confession, when it comes, is as much a psychological release as a legal admission.
