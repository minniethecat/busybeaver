# Kings Arms Hotel (establishment)

The **Kings Arms Hotel** is the principal commercial hotel in the small northern town where much of the novel's action unfolds. Managed by the capable **Miss Peterkin**, it functions as a social hub, a base of operations for visitors, and a key setting for the investigation into the murder of Simon Rattar.

## Role in the narrative

The Kings Arms serves as Mr. Carrington's headquarters during his investigation. He deliberately selects it because he has "gathered beforehand that the local gossip could best be collected" there (022-029). The hotel provides Carrington with a strategic vantage point: he can observe the town's comings and goings, collect intelligence from the bar's "choice spirits," and maintain his cover as a sporting visitor without arousing suspicion.

Miss Peterkin, the manageress, initially finds Carrington "a trifle too superior" and "la-de-da," but is quickly won over by his charm (020-007). She becomes an unwitting source of local knowledge, telling Carrington which road leads to Keldale House and how the house may be recognised.

## Key events at the Kings Arms

- **Carrington's arrival** (020-005): He selects the hotel after a careful survey of the town's establishments.
- **Evening gossip sessions**: Carrington collects enough hearsay in a single evening to "hang at least two people" (022-029).
- **Sandy Donaldson incident**: The town drunkard is ejected from the hotel after spreading wild theories about Mr. Rattar and the Cromarty case (021-039).
- **Sir Malcolm's brief visit**: Sir Malcolm Cromarty passes through the Kings Arms before heading to Keldale House (030-048).
- **Carrington's night expedition**: He departs from the hotel on the dark night when he confronts the figure in the garden at Keldale (035-018).

## Significance

The Kings Arms represents the commercial and social life of the town — in contrast to the landed estate world of Keldale House. It is a place where class boundaries are temporarily relaxed and information flows freely, making it indispensable to Carrington's detective work. Miss Peterkin's competent management of the establishment also provides one of the novel's few portrayals of a working woman in a position of authority.
