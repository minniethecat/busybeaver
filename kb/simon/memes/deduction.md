# Deduction

**Deduction** in *Simon* refers primarily to the amateur detective methodology practiced by [[james-bisset|James Bisset]], [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]'s butler. [[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s signature phrase — "Is that not a fair deduction, sir?" — recurs throughout the novel and constitutes one of its most distinctive comic motifs.

## Bisset's methodology

[[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s approach to deduction is formally articulated in Chapter XIII, "The Deductive Process" (013-001). He presents his method with a lecturer's air:

> "…the principle in a' these cases of crime and detection is noticing such sma' points of detail as escape the eye of the ordinar' observer, taking full and accurate measurements, making a plan with the principal sites carefully markit, and drawing, as it were, logical conclusions." (013-006)

[[james-bisset|Bisset]] prepares a sketch plan of the library with crosses marking the positions of the table and the body, takes measurements, and builds chains of inference from physical evidence — a parody of the systematic detective method.

## The self-defeating deduction

The most memorable instance of [[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s deductive process occurs when he builds an elaborate chain of reasoning about the murderer's identity: a man of roughly five feet nine inches, "pretty hefty but not a giant," who was certainly in the house on the night of the murder, knew the house intimately, and would never have been suspected by [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] of meaning mischief. When [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] points out that this description perfectly matches [[james-bisset|Bisset]] himself, the philosopher is devastated:

> "I've proved it was mysel'!" (013-052)

This comic deflation is immediately tempered by Ned's reassurance: "No, [[james-bisset|Bisset]], I do not believe it was you. But I'd advise you not to go spreading your deductions abroad!" (013-056)

## Comic motif

[[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s catchphrase — "Is that not a fair deduction, sir?" — appears in nearly every scene he features in after the murder (013-038, 013-045, 016-023, 024-030). The repetition underscores both his enthusiasm and the gap between his theoretical principles and practical results. [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] tolerates these performances with a mixture of amusement and genuine respect for [[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s energy, while the professional detective [[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]] and the [[procurator-fiscal|Procurator Fiscal]] operate on entirely different investigative principles.

## Role in the novel's detective structure

[[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s deductions serve a dual narrative function. On a plot level, his close observation of physical details — the broken table leg, the position of the hearth brush, fragments of dried mud — provides genuinely useful data points that the official investigation overlooks. On a thematic level, his comic self-undermining parodies the deductive reasoning celebrated in detective fiction, placing *Simon* in a tradition of novels that complicate the figure of the omniscient detective. Chapter XIII ends with [[james-bisset|Bisset]] undeterred: "the deductive process had already been resumed" (013-064).
