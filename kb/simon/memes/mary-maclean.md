# Mary MacLean

Mary MacLean is the housemaid in [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s household and the character who unknowingly initiates the investigation into his disappearance. Her discovery of a stranger in the fiscal's garden — and her report of it to the impostor himself — sets the novel's machinery of inquiry in motion. Described as "a girl with a strong sense of the dramatic" (002-007), Mary combines domestic diligence with an emotional perceptiveness that makes her, like [[james-bisset|Bisset]], an unwitting detector of the truth beneath the imposture.

## Character

Mary is a representative of the servant class whose proximity to the household's secrets makes her an unwitting participant in the drama. Her report to [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]] is deferential but insistent: she addresses him with the nervousness appropriate to "silent Simon," yet she persists in telling her story because she knows what she saw was wrong. Her characterisation as "the imaginative Mary MacLean" (003-011) suggests that her dramatic sensibility — however gently mocked by the narrative voice — is also what enables her to perceive the strangeness that others might dismiss.

Her fear of [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]] is one of the novel's most vivid domestic details: "He must have taken something to make silent Simon feared!" (002-050). This remark, offered as the housemaid's private theory, captures in miniature the relationship between the servant class and the legal establishment — a relationship of deference, observation, and uneasy intimacy. She knows her employer well enough to fear him, and to notice when his behaviour changes.

Mary's emotional sensitivity is also her weakness. "The feeling of disquiet which possessed Mary MacLean this morning" (006-004) is a premonition that proves accurate, but it also makes her vulnerable to the psychological pressure the impostor exerts. Her "strong sense of the dramatic" (002-007) is both an asset — it makes her the first to detect trouble — and a liability — it makes her testimony seem less reliable to those who prefer facts to feelings.

## Role in the Plot

Mary's sighting of the stranger is the novel's inciting incident at the household level. The fiscal's response — "As procurator fiscal, it is my business to inquire into such affairs" (002-040) — takes on a dark double meaning when the reader later understands that the man speaking is the impostor, and the "stranger" Mary saw was likely connected to the real Simon's disappearance.

Her later testimony confirms the impostor's true character. "Mary MacLean declared afterwards that she had never seen such a look come into his eye" (005-074) — a retrospective judgment that, like much of the novel's servant-class testimony, is offered only after the fact, when the danger of speaking has passed. When, in the novel's climactic scene, "Mary MacLean stood in the doorway, her face white with terror" (038-060), she has moved from unwitting witness to conscious participant in the drama — the housemaid who saw too much.

## Key Relationships

- [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] — her employer, both the real fiscal and the impostor whose behaviour she is among the first to find frightening
- [[james-bisset|James Bisset]] — the butler whose philosophical observations complement her emotional perceptiveness, forming a two-person domestic intelligence network
- [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] — the impostor whose increasingly erratic behaviour she witnesses with growing terror

## In the Eyes of Others

Mary MacLean occupies the lowest rung of the novel's social ladder, yet the perspectives of those above her reveal her importance to the narrative's architecture of discovery.

- **[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s guarded respect**: The genuine fiscal's assessment — that "in his present housemaid, Mary MacLean, he had a girl with a strong Highland strain of fidelity to a master" (002-007) — is offered with the dry tolerance of a man who values competence above all else. The narrative later characterises this as "a strong sense of the dramatic", containing both appreciation and condescension: she is useful, but her usefulness comes packaged with an inconvenient emotional intensity.

- **[[james-bisset|James Bisset]]'s collegial reliance**: Bisset and Mary MacLean form the novel's most effective domestic partnership — he the analyst, she the sensor. It is Mary who first discovers the open library door, Mary whose disquiet alerts the household that something is wrong, and Mary whose testimony later confirms the impostor's violent tendencies. Bisset's philosophical deductions depend on the raw data that Mary's emotional antennae collect, and the narrative quietly acknowledges that without her, his deductions would have no foundation.

- **The impostor's uneasy dismissal**: [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] treats Mary with the exaggerated normality of a man who knows he is being watched. His response to her report of the stranger is too smooth, too professionally reassuring — "As procurator fiscal, it is my business to inquire into such affairs" (002-040) — and his performance fools everyone except, perhaps, Mary herself. The housemaid's growing disquiet is the novel's earliest warning that the man in the fiscal's chair is not the man he claims to be.

## Author's Craft

Clouston's deployment of Mary MacLean exemplifies his skill at using minor characters to advance major themes.

- **The servant as dramatic chorus**: Mary MacLean serves a function analogous to the chorus in classical drama — she registers, in emotional terms, the significance of events that the principal characters are too compromised to acknowledge. Her terror at the impostor's look (005-074), her premonitory disquiet (006-004), and her final frozen witness (038-060) all mark turning points in the narrative that the other characters — entangled in their own motives — might otherwise glide past. Clouston uses her emotional transparency to cue the reader's own response.

- **Domestic testimony as evidence**: The novel consistently privileges domestic observation over official inquiry, and Mary MacLean is the purest example of this preference. Her testimony — about a stranger in the garden, about a look in the fiscal's eye — would be inadmissible in any court, yet it proves more reliable than the official investigation. Clouston's implicit argument is that truth resides not in institutions but in the accumulated observations of those who live closest to the facts.

- **The gendered economy of knowledge**: Mary MacLean and [[james-bisset|James Bisset]] represent two modes of domestic knowledge — hers emotional and intuitive, his philosophical and deductive. The novel values both, but it is notable that Bisset's observations are treated as clever while Mary's are treated as dramatic. Clouston may be registering, with gentle irony, the gendered hierarchy of knowledge in Edwardian society: a butler who deduces is a philosopher; a housemaid who intuits is merely imaginative.
