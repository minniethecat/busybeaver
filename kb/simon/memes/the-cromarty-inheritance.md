# The Cromarty Inheritance

The question of who inherits the Cromarty baronetcy and estate is one of the novel's primary engines of plot. The deaths of two successive baronets — first Charles, then [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] — set in motion a chain of legal and financial consequences that generate motives for murder, grounds for suspicion, and the conditions under which the novel's central impersonation can flourish.

## The Line of Succession

The Cromarty succession is disrupted twice in quick succession:

| [[baronetcy|Baronet]] | Status | Impact on Inheritance |
|---------|--------|----------------------|
| [[charles-cromarty|Charles Cromarty]] | Died months before the novel begins | Made [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] heir presumptive |
| [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]] | [[murder|Murder]]ed in the novel's central crime | Triggers the actual inheritance |

[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] explains his position to [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]:

> "Charles Cromarty died six months ago and I became the heir to the baronetcy." (003-038)

The statement is matter-of-fact, but its implications are far-reaching. As heir to the baronetcy, Ned stands to gain from [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s death — a fact that makes him the most obvious suspect in the community's eyes.

## The Legal Architecture

The inheritance is structured by legal instruments that [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]], as the family's lawyer and factor, is intimately familiar with. He explains the arrangement to Ned:

> "Lady Cromarty is life rented in the mansion house of Keldale. Your cousin Malcolm Cromarty will inherit after her lifetime — and afterwards falls heir to the whole estate." (014-054, 014-056)

The life-rent gives [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]] occupancy of [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] for her lifetime, after which the estate passes to Malcolm. This legal structure:

1. **Creates a waiting period**: Malcolm cannot inherit immediately, even after [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s death
2. **Preserves [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]]'s position**: She is not displaced by her husband's death
3. **Complicates Ned's expectations**: He is heir to the baronetcy but not necessarily to the estate

## The Will

[[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s intentions regarding his will are a source of speculation and concern. The lawyer notes:

> "Sir Reginald would have said nothing to his heir about his intention to make his will." (014-077)

The silence surrounding the will — what it contains, who benefits, whether it has been altered — generates uncertainty that the novel exploits for suspense. [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s financial expectations, and therefore his motives, depend on a document whose contents are unknown to him.

## Suspicion and Motive

The inheritance question generates suspicion in two directions:

1. **[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]**: As heir to the baronetcy, he has the most obvious financial motive for [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s death
2. **The impostor fiscal**: As the family's lawyer, [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] (posing as Simon) has unique knowledge of the inheritance arrangements and the opportunity to manipulate them

The novel's irony is that the person with the most obvious motive (Ned) is innocent, while the person with the most intimate knowledge of the inheritance ([[simon-rattar|the lawyer]]-fiscal) is the murderer — though not for inheritance alone.

## Resolution

The inheritance question is resolved not through legal proceedings but through the exposure of the larger crime. Once [[george-rattar|George Rattar]]'s imposture is revealed, the legal machinery that he controlled is freed to operate honestly. The baronetcy and estate pass to their rightful heirs, and the question that generated so much suspicion is answered.
