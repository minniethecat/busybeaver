# Identity Theft

Identity theft is the foundational crime of the novel, committed by [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] against his twin brother [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]. Having murdered Simon on the night of his return, George assumed not merely his brother's name but his profession as [[procurator-fiscal|Procurator Fiscal]], his position in society, his house, his servants, and his authority over a criminal investigation — including the very murder he himself would go on to commit.

## The Mechanism of Deception

George's imposture exploited the [[twin-resemblance|identical resemblance]] between the brothers. As twins, they shared the same face, the same build, the same voice — everything but the habits of a lifetime. The deception was sustained through a combination of natural resemblance, careful performance, and the silent gravity that Simon had cultivated:

> "Silent Simon" had so habituated people to expecting little conversation that George's reticence passed unremarked.

The murder itself occurred on the night of George's return. Simon was killed in his own villa, and the body was concealed — possibly in the garden, where Mary MacLean later reported footprints in the gravel outside the library window and the master looking "so strangely in the morning" (033-042). From that moment, the identity theft was complete: George sat down to breakfast in Simon's "severely upholstered" dining-room (002-002), and no one knew the difference.

## The Telltale Differences

The impersonation was not perfect. Several subtle differences betrayed George:

- **Smoking**: George was a heavy smoker; Simon was a [[smoking-as-identity|strict nonsmoker]]. The presence of tobacco smoke was a crucial clue.
- **Temperament**: Where Simon was cold and controlled, George was volatile — [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]] noticed the master was "no himsel'" (002-045), observing an uncharacteristic outburst when toast stuck in the rack.
- **The Red Cut**: George cut himself shaving — something Simon, with his rock-steady hand, never did.
- **The Signet Ring**: George could not remove [[rattars-signet-ring|Simon's signet ring]] from the corpse, leading to a telling advertisement.

[[james-bisset|James Bisset]] was the most perceptive observer of these discrepancies. The butler had been in Simon's service for years (006-002), and he gradually catalogued the behavioural departures that marked the impostor. Carrington later summarised the domestic evidence: "a mysterious man who entered the garden one night and left his footprints in the gravel, and whose visit had a strange and mysterious effect on Rattar" (040-028) — the household staff's accumulated observations, combined with Bisset's quiet vigilance, provided the domestic thread of exposure.

## The Theft Extended

George's identity theft encompassed every aspect of Simon's life:

1. **Professional identity**: He conducted legal business, managed estates, and directed criminal investigations as the Procurator Fiscal.
2. **Domestic identity**: He inhabited Simon's villa, commanded his servants, and lived in his rooms.
3. **Institutional identity**: He controlled the investigation into [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]'s murder — a crime he himself committed.

The most audacious dimension of the theft was George's control over the [[the-cromarty-murder|Cromarty murder]] investigation. As Procurator Fiscal, he was the chief legal officer responsible for bringing the murderer to justice — while himself being the murderer. This irony — the law's representative being the law's violator — is the novel's darkest comment on the fragility of institutional trust.

## The Unmasking

The imposture was ultimately exposed through the cumulative weight of small discrepancies, catalysed by [[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]]'s systematic investigation and [[james-bisset|James Bisset]]'s forensic observations. Carrington's method was to question the seemingly unquestionable: "I knew he had lied about engaging me," he explains (040-014), and from that lie the entire edifice of the imposture crumbled. The final unmasking came not through dramatic revelation but through the steady accumulation of evidence that could only point to one conclusion: the man known as Simon Rattar was not Simon Rattar at all.
