# The County Sheriff

In the Scottish legal system of the period, the county sheriff — known colloquially as the
"Shirra'" (002-040 context) — was formally the procurator fiscal: the official responsible
for setting in motion all criminal processes within the county. In the northern county
depicted in the novel, this office was held by Simon Rattar.

## The Office of Procurator Fiscal

The procurator fiscal was, in official terms, "the setter in motion of all criminal
processes, and generalissimo, so to speak, of the police" (002-003). The position combined
elements of what would today be separated into prosecutor, coroner, and director of
criminal investigations.

Simon Rattar described his duty succinctly: "As procurator fiscal, it is my business to
inquire into such affairs" (002-040). This broad mandate gave the office holder significant
authority over criminal investigations within the county.

## Dual Role: Prosecutor and Family Adviser

Rattar's position as procurator fiscal was complicated by his simultaneous role as "lawyer
and factor to the Cromarty family" (017-040). This dual capacity — public prosecutor and
private family solicitor — created an inherent conflict of interest that became central to
the handling of the Cromarty murder investigation.

Rattar himself acknowledged this tension when he stated: "I am the Procurator Fiscal in
charge of this case. But I am also lawyer and factor to the Cromarty family, and my father
was before me" (017-040). He argued that pursuing the investigation too aggressively would
"make a family scandal public," positioning himself as protector of the family's reputation
rather than impartial prosecutor.

## The Shirra' in Local Culture

The colloquial term "Shirra'" appears in the speech of local characters, reflecting the
office's deep roots in Scottish legal culture. One character threatened to bring a
grievance "afore the Shirra'" (reference from chapter context), indicating the sheriff's
role as the ultimate local arbiter of legal disputes.

## Investigative Authority

As procurator fiscal, Rattar had authority to direct police investigations. The
Superintendent of Police deferred to the Fiscal's judgment, and the office was described
as commanding respect throughout the county. An outside investigator, F. T. Carrington,
recognised the Fiscal's unique position, noting that Rattar "could pretty safely be
regarded as above suspicion yourself" and "knows more about the case than anybody else,"
while also being "a gentleman of unusual discretion" (022 reference).
