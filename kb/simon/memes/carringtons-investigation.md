# Carrington's Investigation

[[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]]'s investigation into the [[the-cromarty-murder|Cromarty murder]] runs parallel to — and increasingly in tension with — the official inquiry conducted by the impostor fiscal. As a private enquiry agent from London, Carrington operates without institutional authority but with a freedom that the official investigation, compromised by [[george-rattar|George Rattar]]'s control of [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]]'s office, fatally lacks.

## The Enquiry Agent

[[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] arrives in the unnamed Scottish town as a stranger — an "exceedingly affable gentleman" (024-002) whose pleasant manner conceals a sharp intelligence. He takes rooms at the [[kings-arms-hotel|Kings Arms Hotel]] and begins his work with no official standing, no local connections, and no visible agenda beyond that of a sporting visitor interested in the local shootings.

His identity as a "sporting visitor" — "What about [[keldale-house|Keldale House]]?" the sporting visitor enquired (022-004) — is a cover that allows him to ask questions without arousing suspicion. The pose of the gentleman sportsman gives him access to circles that a declared detective could not penetrate.

## Method

Carrington's method is the patient accumulation of information through social channels. He proceeds by:

1. **Observation**: Walking the ground, mapping the physical spaces of the crime
2. **Conversation**: Cultivating informants through charm and genuine interest
3. **Professional testing**: Probing the reactions of key figures under controlled conditions
4. **Independent verification**: Checking official findings against his own observations

His walk toward [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] — the chapter titled "[[f-t-carrington|Mr. Carrington]]'s Walk" (ch. XXI) — is both reconnaissance and symbolic approach to the heart of the mystery. He studies the topography, the approaches, the sightlines — the physical facts that the official investigation has either ignored or misinterpreted.

## Key Informants

Carrington builds his investigation on a network of carefully cultivated informants:

| Informant | Position | What They Provide |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| [[miss-peterkin|Miss Peterkin]] | Resident at Kings Arms | Local gossip, character assessments, social intelligence |
| [[james-bisset|James Bisset]] | [[servant|Butler]] at [[keldale-house|Keldale]] | Household observations, the servant's-eye view |
| [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] | [[laird|Laird of Stanesland]] | Access, local knowledge, physical force when needed |
| [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]] | [[servant|Housemaid]] | Domestic evidence of the imposture |

At the Kings Arms, Carrington cultivates [[miss-peterkin|Miss Peterkin]] as a key informant. "[[f-t-carrington|Mr. Carrington]] continued to produce favourable impressions" (020-006) — the phrase is carefully chosen. Carrington produces impressions as deliberately as a actor produces a performance, and the favourable impression he creates opens doors that official suspicion would keep closed.

## The Interview with the Lawyer

Carrington's interview with [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] at his office (ch. XXII) is the investigation's pivotal scene. The private enquiry agent and the impostor fiscal face each other across a professional desk, each testing the other:

> "Are you a detective?" asked the lawyer. (022-008)

The question is both defensive and revealing. Carrington deflects it with characteristic indirection, presenting himself instead as an agent of "a certain firm of London lawyers" (022-024). The interview becomes a duel of professional masks — [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]]'s nod against the enquiry agent's monocle.

Carrington leaves the interview with a conviction that he does not yet voice: the procurator fiscal is not merely incompetent or obstructive — he is actively concealing the truth. "The lawyer's nod seemed to imply emphatic agreement" (022-042) with propositions that Carrington knows to be false.

## The Stanesland Interview

Carrington's visit to [[stanesland|Stanesland]] (ch. XXVI) is the investigation's turning point. His interview with [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] "began on much the same lines as his interview at [[keldale-house|Keldale]]" (026-002) but diverged rapidly. Where [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]] had been guarded and evasive, Ned is direct and combative. The contrast tells Carrington what he needs to know: the laird of [[stanesland|Stanesland]] is not the murderer.

> "There are few like Mr. Cromarty of Stanesland." (040-046)

Carrington's verdict on Ned is also a verdict on his own method. The private enquiry agent judges character as carefully as he judges evidence, and in Ned he finds a man whose character is consistent with innocence.

## The Investigation's Arc

Carrington's investigation moves through distinct phases:

| Phase | Location | Activity | Outcome |
|-------|----------|----------|---------|
| Arrival | [[kings-arms-hotel|Kings Arms Hotel]] | Establish cover, cultivate informants | Access secured |
| Reconnaissance | [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] environs | Physical survey of the scene | Topography understood |
| Official interview | [[lawyer|Lawyer]]'s office | Test [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]]'s reactions | Suspicion confirmed |
| Site visit | [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] | Interview [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]], examine the scene | [[evidence|Evidence]] gathered |
| [[stanesland|Stanesland]] | Ned's estate | Test the prime suspect | Innocence confirmed |
| Synthesis | Various | Connect murder to impersonation | Truth assembled |

## Outcome

The investigation succeeds where the official inquiry fails for three reasons:

1. **Institutional independence**: Carrington answers to no local authority and is not constrained by [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]]'s control of the investigative machinery
2. **Methodological freedom**: He combines physical observation, social intelligence, and psychological testing in ways that official procedure cannot
3. **Character judgment**: His willingness to trust characters like [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] — whom official suspicion has already condemned — opens investigative paths that the official inquiry has closed

The investigation culminates not in a courtroom but in a confession. [[george-rattars-confession|George Rattar's Confession]] is the direct evidence that renders the circumstantial case unnecessary — and it is Carrington's patient work that makes that confession inevitable.
