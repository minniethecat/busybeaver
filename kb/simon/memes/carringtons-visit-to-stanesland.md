## Overview

Carrington's visit to [[stanesland|Stanesland]] was a masterclass in investigative interviewing disguised as a casual social call. Under the pretext of enquiring about shooting lets, the private enquiry agent from London conducted a thorough assessment of [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s character, motives, and knowledge — while simultaneously extracting from [[lilian-cromarty|Lilian Cromarty]] the crucial detail of the £1,200 legacy that confirmed Ned's financial motive.

## The Interview with Ned

Carrington began with the same cover story he had used with [[james-bisset|James Bisset]] — an interest in sporting lets. His easy, unhurried manner gave nothing away, and he allowed Ned to do most of the talking. The conversation soon turned to the Cromarty murder, and Carrington tested Ned's reactions to the suspicions against [[malcolm-cromarty|Sir Malcolm]] and [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]]:

> "I'll stake my life on her innocence!" said Ned, and it was hard to know whether his manner as he said this should be termed fierce or solemn. (026-002)

Carrington, observing Ned through his monocle, reached his own conclusion:

> "My own impression — for what it is worth — is that it would be a mere waste of time to investigate the suspicion against her." (026-002)

This testimony from a man who had met [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] on the road confirmed what Carrington had already sensed — that the case against Malcolm and Cicely was a fabrication. The spontaneity of Ned's defence was itself a form of evidence: no man who loved Cicely could have feigned that vehemence, and no man who had murdered his uncle could have spoken so openly about the investigation.

## The Motive Question

When Ned asked whether Carrington had any theories about the murderer, the visitor's reply was revealing:

> "Simply this, that though it *might* be conceivable to think of somebody or other, the difficulty that stares me in the face is — motive!" (026-002)

Carrington was already circling around the impostor fiscal, testing whether anyone else in the Cromarty circle could identify the motive that he had begun to suspect. His phrasing was deliberately vague — "somebody or other" — because he did not yet know whether Ned Cromarty was an ally or an obstacle. But the mention of motive was targeted: Carrington had already realised that the financial incentive behind the impersonation was the key to the entire mystery, and he was sounding out whether the Cromarty family had any inkling of it.

## Lilian Cromarty's Revelation

The visit's most productive moment came not from the formal interview but from tea with [[lilian-cromarty|Lilian Cromarty]]. Frustrated by her brother's refusal to buy a motor car despite having "come into a fortune," she let slip the amount:

> "In my experience, you can't get a decent car much under four hundred." "Well, that's just the figure it would bring it to." (026-002)

Carrington, affecting to choke on a cake crumb, absorbed this information. The calculation was simple: £400 for one motor car multiplied by three equalled £1,200 — precisely the sum of the legacy. But the importance of the revelation went beyond arithmetic. Lilian had confirmed not only the amount but also that the legacy *had actually been paid* — that Simon Rattar, as Ned's legal adviser, had disbursed the funds. This was the crucial link between the will's provisions and the impostor's motive. The money that should have gone to Ned had instead, in some form, enriched the impostor.

The casualness of the disclosure made it all the more valuable. Lilian was not testifying before a court; she was complaining about her brother's stinginess over tea. Her words carried the weight of unguarded truth — exactly the kind of evidence Carrington specialised in extracting.

## Aftermath

As Carrington departed in his car, he murmured with satisfaction:

> "That's settled anyhow!" (026-080)

The visit had eliminated Ned from suspicion while confirming the financial facts that pointed toward the impostor fiscal. On his drive home, Carrington passed [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] on her bicycle — a sight that would gain significance when she fled [[keldale-house|Keldale]] by train the following evening. The encounter was brief: a young woman on a bicycle, her face set with determination, pedalling toward the station. Carrington noted it and filed it away, as he filed everything. Within twenty-four hours, that brief glimpse would become a critical piece of the timeline.
