# Alfred Cromarty

**Alfred Cromarty** is the deceased brother of [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]] and is strongly implied to be the biological father of [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]]. Though he never appears in the narrative directly, his past actions have significant consequences for the younger generation of the Cromarty family.

## Family role

Mr. Alfred Cromarty was the younger brother of Sir Reginald. According to the butler Bisset, who was "practically one of the family," Alfred "was never married and seemed to have had rather a gay reputation" (008-0XX). This discreetly-phrased character assessment — drawn from a conversation between Cicely Farmond and Simon Rattar — hints at a life outside conventional respectability, in contrast with his brother Sir Reginald's position as a landed baronet.

## Character

Though Alfred Cromarty is deceased before the novel's events begin, his character is sketched through the recollections of those who knew him. The phrase "gay reputation" (008-0XX) — deployed by Bisset with characteristic discretion — suggests a man who lived outside the rigid social conventions that governed his brother's world. Where Sir Reginald was a public figure, the county's most popular baronet, Alfred appears to have been a more private and less respectable presence.

The absence of any mention of a marriage is significant in the social context of the novel. For a gentleman of Alfred's class to have fathered a child outside wedlock was a matter of acute social consequence — the kind of fact that could not be openly discussed but that everyone in the family circle quietly understood. [[cicely-farmond|Cicely's]] ambiguous position at Keldale House is the living consequence of Alfred's choices.

## Connection to Cicely Farmond

Cicely Farmond is raised at Keldale House as a ward of Sir Reginald, but her origins are ambiguous. The general understanding within the family circle is that she is "Alfred Cromarty's" daughter — "though of course her mother may have fibbed, for she doesn't look much like the Cromartys" (013-0XX). This uncertainty about her parentage places Cicely in a socially precarious position: she is treated as family but lacks the formal status that legitimate birth would confer.

## The parentage question

The ambiguity surrounding Cicely's parentage is a recurring theme. Cicely herself becomes aware of the rumours and directly asks Simon Rattar whether her mother "wasn't married" and whether she is "really Sir Reginald's niece though he never can acknowledge it" (008-0XX). Rattar, characteristically, gives nothing away.

Lilian Cromarty later expresses the social consequences bluntly: "If you were a mere nobody, it mightn't make so much difference, but your wife must have some sort of a family behind her. One needn't be a snob to think that one mother and a guess at the father is hardly enough!" (013-0XX). She adds that "there's always some devilment in the blood in these cases."

## In the Eyes of Others

### Bisset's View

[[james-bisset|James Bisset]], the Cromarty family's factor's clerk and later the de facto master of Keldale House, was "practically one of the family" (008-0XX) and knew the Cromarty brothers intimately. His characterisation of Alfred — "never married" with "a gay reputation" — is notable for its discretion. Bisset, despite his loquacity, knew where the boundaries of family privacy lay, and his careful phrasing is itself testimony to the delicacy of the subject.

### Sir Reginald's View

[[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] never speaks of his brother directly in the surviving narrative, but his actions speak loudly. By taking Cicely into his household and raising her as a ward, he assumed a responsibility that properly belonged to Alfred. This act of quiet familial duty — absorbing the consequences of his brother's conduct without public acknowledgment — is entirely consistent with Sir Reginald's character as a man whose "extraordinary kindness of heart" extended to all who needed it.

### Cicely Farmond's View

For [[cicely-farmond|Cicely]], Alfred is at once a father and an absence — a defining figure whom she never knew. Her direct questions to Simon Rattar about her parentage reveal both her awareness of the rumours and her desire for certainty. That she must ask a stranger — and a lawyer — rather than a parent speaks to the emotional vacuum that Alfred's choices created.

## Significance

Alfred Cromarty, though absent, functions as a reminder of the less respectable currents beneath the Cromarty family's surface. His "gay reputation" and the mystery of Cicely's parentage introduce themes of illegitimacy, class anxiety, and the tension between reputation and reality that run throughout the novel. In a story concerned with hidden identities — most dramatically in the case of George Rattar impersonating Simon — the uncertainty about Cicely's origins provides a parallel but more sympathetic case of identity under question.
