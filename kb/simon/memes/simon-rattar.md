# Simon Rattar

Simon Rattar is the title character of J. Storer Clouston's *Simon* (1919). A lawyer and procurator fiscal in an unnamed northern Scottish county, Rattar is a man of formidable reputation whose murder by his twin brother George and subsequent impersonation constitute the novel's central crime. He is both the novel's first victim and its structuring absence — a character known almost entirely through the memories of others and through the impostor's performance of his identity.

## Character and Reputation

Rattar's defining characteristic is his silence. Known throughout the county as "[[silent-simon|Silent Simon]]," his professional identity is built on the power of restraint:

> As for his abilities, they were undeniably considerable, of the hard, cautious, never-caught-asleep order; and his taciturn manner and way of drinking in everything said to him while he looked at you out of his steady eyes, and then merely nodded and gave a significant little grunt at the end, added immensely to his reputation for profound wisdom. People were able to quote few definite opinions uttered by "Silent Simon," but any that could be quoted were shrewdness itself. (002-004)

The silence is a professional asset — a mask of wisdom that conceals whatever lies beneath. It is also, in retrospect, the quality that makes his impersonation possible: a man who speaks so little leaves little trace of his authentic self.

Professionally, Rattar's standing is unmatched. He was

> lawyer to several of the larger landowners and factor on their estates, and lawyer and adviser also to many other people in various stations in life — and officially, procurator fiscal for the county, the setter in motion of all criminal processes, and generalissimo, so to speak, of the police. (002-003)

This triple role — private lawyer, estate factor, public prosecutor — concentrates remarkable power in one man. "No man in the county was better known or more widely respected than Mr. Simon Rattar" (002-003). It is precisely this concentration of respect and authority that makes his murder and replacement so devastating.

## Physical Presence

Rattar's physicality is described in terms of steadiness and control. His handshake is unnerving in its composure:

> Silent Simon's hand was as steady as a rock and she never remembered his having cut himself shaving before; certainly not as badly as this. (002-010)

Mary the housemaid notices the red cut on his chin — something so out of character that she "thought perhaps it was the cut on his chin that gave her this curious feeling" (002-010). Indeed, it was unimaginable that anything should "make [[silent-simon|Silent Simon]] cut himself with his razor" (003-010). His face, when the impostor wears it, "remained a mask" (005-057), and his gaze can chill: "[[silent-simon|Silent Simon]]'s sudden glance chilled her" (005-069).

## Professional Life

Simon Rattar's professional life is organized around three overlapping functions:

1. **Private [[lawyer|Lawyer]]** — Advising landowners, drafting documents, managing legal affairs for clients at all social levels
2. **Estate Factor** — Managing the Cromarty estates, collecting rents, overseeing tenants and land
3. **[[procurator-fiscal|Procurator Fiscal]]** — Directing criminal investigations, gathering evidence, deciding prosecutions

His office at the firm of S. and G. Rattar is the physical center of this professional network. Staffed by the head clerk [[james-ison|James Ison]] and others described as "not likely to be found on the staff of [[silent-simon|Silent Simon]]" were they unreliable (003-009), the office reflects its principal's values: competence, discretion, and an intimidating reserve.

As a prosecutor, Simon Rattar was known for his severity. He once "gave evidence against" a criminal "without mercy" (010-018). This reputation for professional ruthlessness makes the irony of his fate all the sharper: the merciless prosecutor becomes the victim of the most intimate of crimes, and his professional identity is turned against the justice system he once served.

## The Imposture

The novel's central conceit is that the Simon Rattar the reader meets — from Chapter II onward — is not Simon at all, but his twin brother [[george-rattar|George]]. The real Simon is already dead, murdered before the action begins. Every professional gesture, every judicial decision, every nod and grunt attributed to "Simon Rattar" is the performance of an impostor.

The imposture is made possible by two factors:

- **Physical identity**: The brothers are identical twins, indistinguishable in appearance
- **Professional silence**: Simon's habitual reticence means that small deviations in manner go unnoticed — or are attributed to the stress of the murder investigation

[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]], visiting the office, senses something amiss:

> It seemed to Ned, moreover, that in this effort the lawyer was displaying a fluency not altogether natural. (017-057)

[[james-bisset|Bisset]] the butler — a [[philosopher|philosophical]] observer — detects subtler inconsistencies. And [[mary-maclean|Mary MacLean]], the housemaid, discovers physical evidence of something wrong. But the professional mask, so carefully constructed by the real Simon over a lifetime, proves an almost perfect shield for his murderer.

## The Man and the Mask

The tragedy of Simon Rattar is that his professional identity was always, in a sense, a mask. The silence, the steady hands, the weighty nods — these were performances of authority that the real Simon perfected over decades of practice. [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] merely inherited a role that was already, in part, a performance.

Yet the novel insists on the difference between the man and the impostor. The real Simon, for all his severity, was competent, principled, and morally certain. The false Simon is none of these things — and the community's inability to tell the difference is Clouston's quiet indictment of a social order that judges men by their professional masks rather than their private selves.
