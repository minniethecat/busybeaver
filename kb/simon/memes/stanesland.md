# Stanesland

Stanesland is the coastal estate of the Cromarty family, home to [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] — "Mr. Cromarty of Stanesland" (003-058) — and his sister [[lilian-cromarty|Lilian]]. Unlike the formal grandeur of [[keldale-house|Keldale House]], Stanesland is a place of informality and authenticity: an old castle by the sea, kept by a laird whose character is as unconventional as his residence.

## Physical Description

The old castle of Stanesland is described in modest terms:

> The old castle of Stanesland was but a small house as castles go, yet as a residence it was sufficiently large to contain the laird and his sister with a room or two to spare for a guest, and to give them a sense of space and privacy. (007-065)

This is not the sweeping estate of [[keldale-house|Keldale]] but a "fortalice" — a defensible stronghold, adapted to domestic use. Its smallness is a virtue: Stanesland is intimate where [[keldale-house|Keldale]] is formal, personal where [[keldale-house|Keldale]] is institutional.

The house's most significant interior space is the smoking room — "an apartment with vast windows" (026-002) — where Ned receives visitors and conducts the informal business of the estate. Unlike the library at [[keldale-house|Keldale]] (a room of murder), the smoking room at Stanesland is a room of conversation, negotiation, and gradual revelation.

The estate's coastal location — on the edge of the land, facing the sea — reinforces its symbolic position at the margin of the conventional social world. Where [[keldale-house|Keldale]] is approached by a formal avenue, Stanesland is approached by a road that runs to the coast.

## "Mr. Cromarty of Stanesland"

The repeated use of the formula "Mr. Cromarty of Stanesland" throughout the novel signals Ned's identity as a landed gentleman. He is introduced in precisely these terms: "Mr. Cromarty of Stanesland to see you, sir" (003-058). The designation carries weight — it identifies him as a man of property, a laird, a member of the landed class whose status is tied to a specific place.

Yet Ned's relationship to Stanesland is different from [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s relationship to [[keldale-house|Keldale]]. Ned is a "different type" (004-002) — physically imposing, informally authoritative, impatient with social convention. His estate reflects his character:

| Quality | [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] | Stanesland |
|---------|--------------|------------|
| Architecture | Formal country house | Old castle, modest scale |
| Setting | Inland, parkland | Coastal, edge of land |
| Atmosphere | Social, institutional | Intimate, personal |
| Owner | [[baronetcy|Baronet]] (murdered) | [[laird|Laird]] (survivor) |
| Narrative role | Crime scene | Refuge and resolution |

## Domestic Life

Stanesland is kept by Ned's sister Lilian, whose quiet competence maintains the household. Their shared domestic life — "the laird and his sister sat down to lunch" (007-066) — provides a counterpoint to the intrigue and violence at [[keldale-house|Keldale]].

The household is small, informal, and resilient. When [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] needs refuge from the pressures of [[keldale-house|Keldale]], it is to Stanesland that she is brought:

> "I advised Miss Farmond to come to Stanesland and she being amenable, I just brought her." (037-029)

Stanesland is a place of safety — not because it is fortified (the old castle is no longer defensible) but because it is governed by a different set of values than those that prevail at [[keldale-house|Keldale]].

## A Place of Investigation

Stanesland is not exempt from the investigation. Carrington visits the estate as part of his inquiry:

> His interview with the laird of Stanesland began on much the same lines as his interview at Keldale. (026-002)

The chapter titled simply "At Stanesland" (ch. XXVI) is a key scene in the investigation. Carrington tests Ned, and Ned tests Carrington. The smoking room becomes an informal courtroom, where the private enquiry agent and the laird take each other's measure.

Carrington's observation after his visit is telling: "There are few like Mr. Cromarty of Stanesland" (040-046). The place and the man are inseparable — Stanesland shapes Ned as much as Ned shapes Stanesland.

## The Laird's Authority

[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s authority at Stanesland is of a different kind than [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s at [[keldale-house|Keldale]]. It is personal rather than institutional, earned rather than inherited:

> The laird of Stanesland had such an offhand authority about him. (007-002)

This "offhand authority" — casual, confident, unpretentious — is the quality that makes Stanesland a place of genuine refuge. When Ned tells Cicely to "Come straight to Stanesland!" (028-067), the command carries the weight of someone who means what he says and can protect what is his.

## Symbolic Significance

Stanesland represents:

- **Authenticity vs. formality**: The coastal fortalice against the inland manor
- **Refuge vs. danger**: Safety at Stanesland, murder at [[keldale-house|Keldale]]
- **Personal vs. institutional authority**: Ned's offhand confidence against [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s professional mask
- **The margin vs. the centre**: A place at the edge of the land that offers an alternative to the corrupted centre

The novel's resolution involves a movement toward Stanesland — toward the margin, the authentic, the personal. [[keldale-house|Keldale]] is the scene of the crime; Stanesland is the scene of the resolution.
