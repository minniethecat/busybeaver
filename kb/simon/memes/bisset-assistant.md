# Bisset's Assistant

The investigative partnership between James Bisset and F. T. Carrington was formed at Keldale House
in the aftermath of the Cromarty murder. Posing as a sporting gentleman interested in renting the
Keldale estate, Carrington ingratiated himself with Bisset and became, in the butler's own words,
a "helpful assistant" in the investigation (024-117).

## The First Meeting

Carrington arrived at Keldale House on the morning following the murder, delivering a note from
Simon Rattar to James Bisset. The introduction was immediately successful: Carrington's affable
manner and apparent interest in the estate's sporting possibilities impressed Bisset, and the
conversation soon turned to the murder investigation (024-002).

Bisset, who had by this time assumed almost despotic control of Keldale House, found in Carrington
a sympathetic audience for his detective theories. Carrington not only shared Bisset's enthusiasm
for detection but professed a deep interest in the butler's views on the mystery (024-003).

## The Library Examination

Bisset led Carrington to the library — the scene of the crime — and explained his theories.
The butler had developed several hypotheses, all sharing the assumption that the murderer must have
entered through the window and was certainly not a member of the household (024-009).

Carrington listened attentively but gently challenged Bisset's conclusions. He pointed out that
it would have been easy for someone within the house to enter the library, and that if Sir Reginald
knew the visitor, it would account for the absence of any struggle (024-013).

## The Body Reenactment

The partnership's most productive moment came when Carrington lay down on the floor in the exact
spot where Sir Reginald's body had been found, asking Bisset to note any differences between his
own appearance and that of the corpse (024-022).

Bisset observed that the dead man's coat had been pulled up around the shoulders, with the hanging
tag broken and exposed — a detail he had previously overlooked. This led to the crucial deduction
that the body had been dragged along the floor by the shoulders (024-027), suggesting the murder
had occurred near the window rather than where the body was discovered.

## Nature of the Partnership

Throughout their collaboration, Carrington played the role of a curious and helpful visitor,
while Bisset provided local knowledge and access to the house. Carrington's approach was to
ask pointed questions that guided Bisset toward new observations, rather than to impose his
own theories.

Bisset, for his part, was delighted with his assistant. As he reflected, he had seldom met
"a pleasanter gentleman or a more helpful assistant" (024-117). Their partnership was
characterised by mutual respect and a shared enthusiasm for the detective enterprise.
