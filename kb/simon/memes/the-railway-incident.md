On the same evening that [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] followed [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] onto a southbound train, [[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]] experienced a disturbing encounter of his own on the railway — an incident he ultimately decided not to report.

## The Encounter

Superintendent Sutherland was boarding a train to investigate a vague rumour of a suspicious person when he glanced into a carriage compartment. He saw it was empty and was about to enter, but was detained on the platform by his cousin [[robert-macalister|Mr. MacAlister]], who engaged him in conversation about the murder investigation (019-003). When the two men returned to the compartment less than a minute later, it was no longer empty.

In one of the far corners sat "a man wrapped in a dark coloured ulster. A black felt hat was drawn down over his eyes, and his muffled face was resting on his hand" (019-015). Sutherland was immediately unsettled. The man had appeared without being observed — an impossibility on a station platform where the superintendent prided himself on noticing everything. As he later put it:

> "I'm not used not to notice people, Robbie." (019-024)

MacAlister, characteristically, dismissed his cousin's concern as professional paranoia. But Sutherland's instincts, however unreflective, were sound. The muffled stranger was almost certainly [[george-rattar|George Rattar]], the impostor procurator fiscal, escaping the area under a guise of concealment.

## The Disappearance

MacAlister pulled his cousin into the adjacent compartment, eager to hear official confidences about the murder (019-016). But Sutherland could not shake his unease about the muffled stranger. He kept glancing toward the connecting door, and at the next station he sprang out to investigate — and discovered the compartment "empty" (019-029). The man had vanished.

Sutherland was furious at the missed opportunity, and his frustration found its natural target in his well-meaning but distracting cousin:

> "I ought to have stopped and looked under the seats!" (019-035)

MacAlister, with the reflexive defence of the unwitting accomplice, protested that there was nothing under the seats (019-036). But at the following station, a thorough search found "not a sign of the muffled stranger, under the seats or anywhere else" (019-041). The compartment was as empty as it had been the first time Sutherland looked in — except now it held the unsettling residue of a presence that could not be explained.

The practical difficulty of the disappearance was genuine. A man cannot vanish from a moving train. He cannot pass through a locked connecting door without being seen. He cannot drop from a window without the guard noticing. And yet the muffled stranger had done exactly that — because he had never been on the train at all in the ordinary sense. He had slipped out at the station while Sutherland and MacAlister argued, timing his exit with a precision born of desperation.

## The Concealment

Sutherland decided not to report the incident. The decision, which looked like cowardice, was in fact a form of unconscious wisdom:

> "I'm just wondering if it's worth while reporting the thing, Robbie. The fiscal might have a kin' of unpleasant way of looking at it." (019-042)

Sutherland's instinct was exactly right, though not for the reason he imagined. The fiscal — [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] — was himself the muffled stranger. Had Sutherland filed a report describing the encounter, he would have been handing the criminal a detailed account of how close the law had come to intercepting him. George Rattar would have read the report in his office, nodded gravely, and filed it where it would never be seen again.

The irony of the situation ran deeper still. Sutherland's reluctance to report was driven by his fear of the fiscal's sarcastic disapproval — the very quality that George Rattar had cultivated in the character of Simon Rattar as a shield against scrutiny. The impostor had made himself so unpleasant to deal with that his own chief of police preferred to suppress a potentially crucial sighting rather than face his mockery. Sutherland "thought over it very hard and finally decided that he was demonstrating his vigilance quite sufficiently without mentioning the trifling mystery of the empty compartment" (019-044).

## Significance

The railway incident demonstrated both Sutherland's genuine if unreflective vigilance and the limitations of a police investigation directed by the murderer. It also illustrated George Rattar's extraordinary coolness under pressure — to sit motionless with his face hidden while the chief of police stood at the open door of his compartment, and then to slip away into the darkness of a country station. The incident was the closest the official investigation came to intercepting the impostor before [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]]'s systematic inquiry closed the net.
