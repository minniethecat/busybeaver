George Rattar Sr. was the father of the twin brothers [[simon-rattar|Simon]] and [[george-rattar|George Rattar]]. Though deceased before the events of the novel, his legacy — particularly the family legal business and the pattern of favour and distinction he established between his sons — shaped the circumstances that led to the central tragedy.

## Family and Business

The elder George Rattar was the founder or proprietor of the legal firm later overseen by Simon as [[procurator-fiscal|Procurator Fiscal]]. The firm, and the Rattar name attached to it, were part of the inheritance that passed to Simon and became central to George Jr.'s imposture. Simon served as both Procurator Fiscal and "lawyer and factor to the Cromarty family," a dual role he had inherited: "my father was before me" (019-019).

The firm was described as a place where "nothing was ever changed — not even the clerks" (039-031), suggesting a conservative, traditional operation handed down through the family line. This institutional inertia — the unchanging office, the inherited client relationships, the fixed routines — was both the foundation of Simon's professional identity and the vulnerability that George Jr. exploited when he returned to step into his brother's role.

The elder Rattar's name lived on in the firm's title: [[firm-of-s-and-g-rattar|S. and G. Rattar]]. The "S" stood for Simon; the "G" for George — a partnership of names that, like the twins themselves, yoked two very different men into a single enterprise. The firm's continuity across generations made it possible for George Jr., after years of absence, to return and "know all the ropes from old days" (039-031), slipping into Simon's professional skin with an ease that astonished those who did not know the truth of the substitution.

## Father of Twins

George Sr. fathered twin sons — Simon and George — who, despite their identical appearance, could hardly have been more different in character. The novel's plot hinges on this biological accident and the divergent paths the two brothers took. As twins they shared the same face and build, a physical resemblance that made George's eventual imposture possible:

> "I daresay I have inherited some of the same tricks myself. I know I found them come quite easy — the stare and the silence and the grunts and the rest of them." (039-031)

The father's decision to name one son after himself (George) while the other (Simon) received a different name may reflect favouritism or simply convention, but it established a pattern of distinction that the brothers' subsequent lives would amplify into a gulf of character and circumstance. George Jr.'s resentment of Simon's professional success — and his sense of being the wronged twin, denied his rightful share — runs through the novel as a dark undercurrent, and the father's legacy sits at the source of that bitterness.

## The Kings Arms Gossip

The elder Rattar's history was sufficiently well known in the district that it formed part of the local lore retailed to visitors. At the Kings Arms, [[miss-peterkin|Miss Peterkin]] and her friends regaled [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] with the full history of the Rattar family, including "his father before him" (039-031). The story of the twin brothers — one respectable, one disreputable — was part of the town's collective memory, a narrative that Carrington absorbed as he pieced together the relationships underlying the Cromarty murder case.

## Legacy

The elder George Rattar's most significant legacy was the firm of [[firm-of-s-and-g-rattar|S. and G. Rattar]], the legal practice that employed both brothers in their younger days and later became the vehicle for George Jr.'s imposture. But his deeper legacy was the dynamic he set in motion between his sons — a dynamic of comparison, competition, and ultimately catastrophic deception. The identical faces that he gave them became, in George Jr.'s hands, an instrument of fraud; the professional standing he bequeathed to Simon became the prize that George coveted; and the family name he passed down became the mask behind which one brother hid from the law and the world.
