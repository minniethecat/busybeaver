# The Secret Engagement

The engagement between [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] and [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] begins as a secret — a clandestine attachment conducted in the shadow of [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]'s known opposition. The secrecy of the engagement is both a romantic necessity and a narrative device, generating the tension and suspicion that surround the young couple throughout the murder investigation.

## Why Secrecy

The engagement must be kept secret because [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]] opposes the match. As the head of the Cromarty family and Ned's kinsman, [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] has both the authority and the inclination to forbid a marriage he considers unsuitable. The baronet's opposition is not explained in detail — it is taken as a given, one of those arrangements of social power that the novel's characters understand without needing to articulate.

The secrecy is described in terms that emphasize its clandestine nature:

> "A secret engagement, like, that Sir Reginald would not have countenanced." (016-042)

The phrase "would not have countenanced" captures the social mechanism: [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s disapproval is not a legal barrier but a social one, and social barriers, in the world of the novel, are often more powerful than legal ones.

The lawyer confirms the secrecy when questioned:

> "No," said Simon. "It was a secret engagement; and marriage would have been..." (023-053)

The lawyer's trailing off is suggestive — the secret engagement is a fact that carries implications, and those implications touch on the murder investigation itself.

## Narrative Function

The secret engagement serves several narrative purposes:

| Function | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| Motive generator | Gives Ned a reason to wish [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] dead — a motive the community finds plausible |
| Suspicion red herring | Makes the engaged couple appear guilty when they are innocent |
| Romantic subplot | Provides emotional stakes beyond the murder investigation |
| Social commentary | Contrasts personal desire with institutional authority |

The secrecy of the engagement is the reason Ned is suspected. If the engagement were public, [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s opposition would be a known fact — and his removal by murder would be an obvious benefit to the engaged couple. The secrecy masks the engagement and therefore masks the motive, creating the dramatic irony on which the novel's suspense depends.

## Resolution

The secret engagement is resolved — made public, made legitimate — only after the murder is solved and the true criminal exposed. The public acknowledgment of the engagement is part of the novel's movement toward resolution: secrets are brought to light, the innocent are cleared, and the social order is restored.

The transition from secret to public engagement mirrors the novel's larger movement from concealment to revelation. Just as Carrington's investigation brings the hidden crime to light, the young couple's relationship moves from the shadows into the open.

## Relation to Other Events

The secret engagement is closely linked to [[the-cromarty-murder|the Cromarty murder]]. [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s death removes the principal obstacle to the marriage — a fact that makes the engaged couple suspects and that generates much of the novel's suspense. The engagement is also linked to [[ned-cromartys-return|Ned Cromarty's return]] from [[america|America]]: Ned returns, meets Cicely, and the secret engagement follows.
