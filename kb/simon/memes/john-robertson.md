John Robertson was the chauffeur at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]], employed by [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]. He was named as a beneficiary in Sir Reginald's will and was briefly considered as a possible suspect in the murder investigation, but was cleared by a verified alibi — one of only two legatees whose circumstances "might have affected this case."

## Employment at Keldale

As chauffeur, Robertson was part of the household staff at Keldale House, a position that placed him in the orbit of [[james-bisset|James Bisset]], the butler whose "passion for expounding the principles of their professions sometimes exceeding his tact" (016-002). Bisset, whose expertise in motoring was "more theoretical" than practical, was known to lecture the chauffeur on the principles of his own trade — a dynamic that Robertson endured as part of the household's peculiar hierarchy.

The chauffeur's duties included maintaining Sir Reginald's motor car and driving members of the household about the district. After the murder, the car continued in service at Keldale — [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] himself was driven in it on at least one occasion, telling the chauffeur "I shall want you again at two o'clock sharp" (027-010) when being conveyed to the Procurator Fiscal's office in town.

## Legacy and Alibi

When [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] reviewed Sir Reginald's will with [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]], he identified Robertson as one of only two legatees whose circumstances warranted scrutiny:

> "James Bisset gets a legacy of three hundred pounds. There are a few smaller legacies to servants, but the only two that might have affected this case do not actually do so. One is John Robertson, Sir Reginald's chauffeur, but on the night of the crime he was away from home and an alibi can be established till two in the morning. The other is Donald Mackay, the gardener, but he is an old man and was in bed with rheumatism that night." (014-068)

Ned Cromarty observed the underlying logic with some sharpness: "I see, you are giving everybody mentioned in the will credit for perhaps having committed the murder, supposing it was physically possible?" (014-069). Simon's methodical review of each legatee's circumstances was characteristic of his professional approach — exhaustive, dispassionate, and ultimately narrowing.

Later in the investigation, Simon returned to the point, confirming that "the chauffeur and gardener were excluded by circumstances from suspicion" (023-058). This systematic elimination of the household staff as suspects was an important early step in [[carringtons-investigation|Carrington's investigation]], clearing the ground for the more significant question of who within the family circle might have had both motive and opportunity.

## Significance

Robertson's role in the narrative, though small, illustrates a structural feature of the investigation: the careful sorting of possible suspects into those who could and could not have committed the crime. His alibi — being away from home on the night of the murder, verifiable until two in the morning — placed him firmly in the excluded category. Together with [[donald-mackay|Donald Mackay]], the elderly gardener bedridden with rheumatism, Robertson represented the outer edge of the suspect pool — people whose connection to the will gave them theoretical motive, but whose circumstances gave them no practical opportunity.

His brief appearance in the narrative also serves to sketch the texture of life at Keldale House — a substantial establishment with a full complement of servants, each with their own duties, relationships, and place in the hierarchy that [[james-bisset|Bisset]] so assiduously maintained.
