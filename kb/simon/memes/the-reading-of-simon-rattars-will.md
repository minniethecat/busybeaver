## Overview

The reading of [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]'s will — disclosed by [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] (in reality the impostor [[george-rattar|George Rattar]]) in a confidential conversation with [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] — was a calculated piece of investigation management. By selectively revealing the beneficiaries and their legacies, the procurator fiscal constructed a theory of motive that directed suspicion toward several members of the Cromarty circle.

## The Disclosure

In a private meeting with [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]], the procurator fiscal laid out the terms of the will with the precision of a man who had drafted it himself — which, as the Cromarty family's legal adviser, [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] had:

> "Sir Malcolm gets £1,000 a year to support him during the life time of Lady Cromarty, and afterwards falls heir to the whole estate. He therefore gains a baronetcy and £1,000 a year immediately." (014-056)

The fiscal systematically enumerated the beneficiaries. [[james-bisset|James Bisset]] received a legacy of three hundred pounds — a modest but significant sum for a family solicitor. [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]], secretly engaged to [[malcolm-cromarty|Sir Malcolm]], stood to benefit indirectly through her future husband's inheritance (014-061). Servants received smaller legacies, which the fiscal dismissed as irrelevant to the murder inquiry. The pattern was unmistakable: every person close to the murdered baronet became, in the fiscal's telling, a potential killer.

## The Fiscal's Strategy

The impostor's method was transparent. By asking "Who benefits by [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]'s death?" and then answering his own question with the will's provisions, he sought to channel the investigation toward the beneficiaries (014-069). [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] immediately recognised the implication and challenged it directly:

> "I see, you are giving everybody mentioned in the will credit for perhaps having committed the murder, supposing it was physically possible?" (014-069)

Ned's challenge was shrewd and went to the heart of the logical flaw in the fiscal's reasoning: benefit alone is not proof of guilt. Every will creates beneficiaries; that does not make every beneficiary a murderer. But the fiscal was not interested in logical rigour. He was interested in creating plausible suspects — the more the better — to keep the investigation circling away from his own crimes.

The impostor fiscal's personal relationship to the will made this strategy particularly insidious. As the Cromarty family's trusted legal adviser, [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] had drafted the will personally and possessed intimate knowledge of every provision. As [[george-rattar|George Rattar]], the impostor, he had every incentive to ensure that suspicion fell on the beneficiaries rather than on the man who understood the document's implications better than anyone. The will became a weapon in his hands, a document designed to protect the innocent but wielded to ensnare them.

## Hidden Motives

What the impostor did not disclose was his own relationship to the will's contents. The fiscal knew, better than anyone, exactly how much each beneficiary stood to gain — and precisely how to frame that knowledge as evidence of motive. When [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] spoke of the will's provisions with the authority of its drafter, he was simultaneously the man who had advised Sir Reginald on its terms and the impostor who was exploiting those terms to misdirect a murder investigation.

## Aftermath

The will's disclosure deepened the atmosphere of suspicion at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]]. [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]], upon learning of the rumours connecting her engagement to Malcolm with the murder, was devastated (016-062). The financial logic of the will — that she would benefit from Sir Reginald's death through her future husband's inheritance — was morally repugnant to her. That the fiscal had planted this logic in the public mind made her position at Keldale increasingly untenable, and contributed directly to her decision to flee by train on the evening of her departure.

The will-reading episode demonstrated how legal instruments could be manipulated by a cunning impostor to serve a hidden agenda. The document that should have brought clarity to the investigation instead became its principal instrument of confusion — a testament not to the will's ambiguity but to the fiscal's skill at exploiting the suspicion that any inheritance inevitably attracts.
