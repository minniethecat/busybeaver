# Laird

## Definition

In Scottish society, a **laird** is a landowner holding a lairdship — an estate with the associated rights, privileges, and social standing. The title is distinct from a peerage; a laird is not a lord in the English sense, but the position carries considerable local prestige. The laird's residence is often referred to as a "fortalice" or manor house, and the role traditionally includes certain feudal responsibilities toward tenants and the community.

In *Simon*, the lairdship of [[stanesland|Stanesland]] defines [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s social position and personal identity. He describes himself as having been transformed from "cow puncher into a high grade laird" (004-013), capturing both the elevation and the incongruity of his situation.

## Context in the Novel

[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] returns from the [[america|America]]n West to inherit the lairdship of [[stanesland|Stanesland]], a coastal estate that represents both his family heritage and his new social obligations. The transition from cowboy to laird is a source of humour and character development: "the laird of [[stanesland|Stanesland]]'s curious movements" (004-046) are noted by observers, and his unconventional manners set him apart from the traditional Scottish gentry.

The laird's smoking room at [[stanesland|Stanesland]] serves as a key setting for confidential conversations and plot developments. Visitors are "shown into the laird's smoking room—an apartment with vast windows" (026-002), establishing it as a masculine space of counsel and decision.

Ned's sister Lilian serves as his hostess, and their shared domestic life at [[stanesland|Stanesland]] — "the laird and his sister sat down to lunch" (007-066) — provides a counterpoint to the intrigue and violence at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]].

## Real-World Background

The Scottish lairdship system differs from the English peerage. A laird is not a member of the House of Lords by virtue of the title alone; the position is tied to land ownership rather than a patent of nobility. In the early 20th century, when *Simon* was written (1919), the lairdly class still exercised significant local influence in rural Scotland, though their feudal powers had substantially diminished. Clouston accurately portrays this transitional moment: figures like [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] inherit the social expectations of the lairdship while bringing distinctly modern, even transatlantic, sensibilities to the role.
