# Secret

## Definition

A **secret** is information intentionally withheld from some or all parties. In narrative fiction, secrets are engines of plot: their keeping creates tension, their discovery precipitates crisis, and their revelation provides resolution. The mystery genre is built on the architecture of secrets — [[murder|the crime]] that must be concealed, the clue that must be found, the identity that must be protected.

*Simon* deploys secrets at every level of its narrative structure. The central secret — [[george-rattar|George Rattar]]'s murder of his twin and assumption of his identity — generates the novel's primary dramatic irony. Subsidiary secrets — [[cicely-and-malcolms-engagement|the clandestine engagement]], the hidden evidence, the suppressed past — create a web of concealment that the detective figure (Carrington) must gradually unravel.

## Context in the Novel

The most socially fraught secret is the engagement between [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] and [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]]. When asked directly — "anything said about [[malcolm-cromarty|Sir Malcolm]]'s engagement to [[cicely-farmond|Miss Farmond]]?" (014-081) — the answer reveals a hidden commitment: "A clandestine engagement then?" (014-083). The secret is maintained because of [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s anticipated opposition: "a secret engagement, like, that [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] wouldn't hear of" (016-042).

The novel thus layers secrets: the murder secret (known only to George), the engagement secret (known to Cicely and Malcolm), the investigative secret (Carrington's true purpose), and the communal secret (what the servants suspect but dare not say). Each secret's keeper and each secret's discoverer occupies a different position in the novel's architecture of knowledge.

## Real-World Background

Edwardian society maintained a complex relationship with secrecy. The public sphere demanded transparency in business and legal affairs, while the private sphere tolerated — and sometimes required — concealment in matters of courtship, family discord, and personal history. The "clandestine engagement" was a recognized social category, distinct from a formal engagement announced with parental consent, and carried with it an atmosphere of romantic risk that novelists of the period exploited extensively.
