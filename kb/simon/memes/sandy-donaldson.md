Sandy Donaldson was a well-known figure at the [[kings-arms-hotel|Kings Arms Hotel]] — the town drunkard whose unguarded gossip made him both a nuisance and, inadvertently, a source of information for [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]]'s investigation. He was one of the "choice spirits" who gathered in the manageress's room at the Kings Arms, and his loose talk circulated through both the hotel and the wider town.

## Appearance and Manner

Carrington described him to [[miss-peterkin|Miss Peterkin]] as having "red hair, shaky knees, bit of a stammer" and answering to the name of Sandy (023-076). Miss Peterkin immediately identified him:

> "Old Sandy Donaldson!" she exclaimed. "That drunken old thing! He was simply talking nonsense as usual!" (023-078)

Sandy's physical appearance and his stammer made him instantly recognisable about the town. His reputation preceded him — when Carrington reported something he had heard from the old man, it was instantly dismissed by those who knew him as the unreliable ramblings of a habitual drunkard. Miss Peterkin's contemptuous dismissal — "talking nonsense as usual" — reflected the town's settled view of Sandy as a man whose word carried no weight.

## The Kings Arms Gossip Ecosystem

The [[kings-arms-hotel|Kings Arms]] served as the town's informal intelligence hub, and Sandy Donaldson was a fixture of its social ecosystem. Carrington had chosen the hotel deliberately because "the local gossip could best be collected" there (020-006), and in the manageress's room each evening, the "choice spirits" — including Sandy — gathered to talk. The conversations ranged freely over every subject of local interest, and the [[the-cromarty-murder|Cromarty Murder]] dominated them all.

Carrington methodically cultivated these evenings, aware that even the most compromised sources could yield usable intelligence:

> "I arrived last night, put up at the Kings Arms—where I gathered beforehand that the local gossip could best be collected, and in the course of the evening I collected enough to hang at least two people; and in the course of a few more evenings I shall probably have enough to hang half a dozen—if one can believe, say, a twentieth of what one hears." (020-006)

Sandy was part of this stream of information — unreliable in detail but indicative in direction. His drunken talk, whether truthful or invented, circulated through the hotel, sometimes reaching the ears of those with a professional interest in local rumours.

## Role in the Investigation

Despite his unsavoury reputation, Sandy Donaldson served a real function in the ecology of the town's gossip. His unguarded comments provided Carrington with raw material that, when checked against other sources, could point toward profitable lines of enquiry. Carrington, ever alert to the value of even the most compromised sources, took careful note of what Sandy said, while knowing to verify it independently.

The encounter that brought Sandy to Carrington's attention occurred during one of the evening gatherings at the Kings Arms, where [[miss-peterkin|Miss Peterkin]] and her friends regaled the visitor with every rumour and speculation circulating in the district. Sandy's contribution — whatever it was — was sufficiently noteworthy that Carrington mentioned it to Miss Peterkin the following day, prompting her explosive identification and dismissal.
