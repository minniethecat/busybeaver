## Overview

[[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]]'s flight from [[keldale-house|Keldale]] was a quiet, desperate act — a young woman leaving behind the household that had become a prison of suspicion. Spotted by [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] at a wayside railway station, her departure became the catalyst for his pursuit and, ultimately, the open declaration of his feelings for her.

## The Departure

On a dark, rainy evening after his visit from [[f-t-carrington|Mr. Carrington]], [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] was returning from a day's shooting when his dogcart was stopped at a level crossing by a passing train. In the light of the station platform, he saw a figure emerge:

> "It was a girl in black, and at the sight of her, Ned bent forward suddenly in his driving seat and stared intently into the night." (027-002)

The keeper identified her as [[cicely-farmond|Miss Farmond]] from [[keldale-house|Keldale House]]. She unstrapped a small suitcase from her bicycle, left the bicycle at the station, and boarded the train — clearly intending to leave the district.

## Ned's Pursuit

Without hesitation, Ned abandoned his dogcart and caught the same train:

> "As to the question of what business he had to be following Miss Farmond like this, he troubled his head about it not at all. If she needed him, here he was. If she didn't, he would clear out." (027-002)

He followed at a careful distance, watching each station to see where she would alight. When the train reached its terminus and Cicely discovered the branch line was closed for the winter, she found herself stranded in the dark — and Ned revealed himself.

## The Flight as Narrative Threshold

Cicely's flight marks a structural pivot in the novel's treatment of its heroine. Up to this point, Cicely has been largely passive — suspected, scrutinised, defended by others but rarely acting on her own behalf. The flight, though outwardly an act of retreat, is in fact her first autonomous decision: she chooses to remove herself from a situation that has become intolerable, even at the cost of social disgrace. That she is intercepted by Ned before she can complete this escape is narratively significant — the novel does not permit her to vanish into anonymity, because her story is only beginning. The episode thus functions as a threshold: the old Cicely, defined by her problematic engagement to Sir Malcolm and her silent endurance of scandal, departs on the train; the new Cicely, whose relationship with Ned will be conducted on terms of mutual agency, emerges from the darkened station platform. Clouston's choice to stage this transformation at a railway station — a liminal space of arrivals and departures — reinforces the sense of passage from one state of being to another.

## Significance

The flight marked a turning point in the relationship between Ned and Cicely. Her desperation revealed the toll that suspicion and scandal had taken on her; his pursuit demonstrated a devotion that went beyond casual interest. The episode set the stage for their eventual engagement and the resolution of the romantic subplot.

The flight also illustrated the broader atmosphere of fear surrounding [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] — where even its gentlest resident felt compelled to flee into the winter night.
