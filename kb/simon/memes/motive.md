# Motive

The search for motive drives the investigation in Clouston's *Simon*. Without a clear reason for the [[murder|killing]], the murder of [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]] remains an impenetrable puzzle — and the novel repeatedly returns to the question: who stood to gain, and why would they kill?

## The Central Question

[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]], the [[procurator-fiscal|Procurator Fiscal]], frames the investigation around motive with characteristic legal precision:

> "What's the motive? That's the point." (014-038)

He presses the legal logic: "who that could conceivably have committed it, had a motive for doing so?" (014-070). The investigation circles through possible motives — inheritance, revenge, concealment — before arriving at the truth.

The novel is explicit about the inheritance motive. [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]], as heir, stands to gain from [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s death. The will and the Cromarty inheritance create what the Fiscal calls:

> "…two people with a very strong motive for committing the crime." (017-062)

The Fiscal's interrogation of [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] is built entirely around the question of motive. He asks Ned directly what motives he expected the Fiscal to be looking for — and when Ned answers with the obvious ones (inheritance, money), the Fiscal probes deeper. The exchange reveals the Fiscal's deliberately misleading stance: "those are no motives at all" (014-076) — a statement that conceals the Fiscal's own guilty knowledge while appearing to exonerate the suspect.

## Motive and Character

Motive in the novel is not purely financial. [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]]'s motives are contrasted with mercenary ones. As [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] insists:

> "I assure you, Margaret, that Cicely is not a girl with mercenary motives. She is quite charming—" (008-050)

The distinction between love and greed, between honour and advantage, runs through every assessment of character and guilt.

The inheritance plots — the old will, the new will, the contested succession — provide the factual scaffolding for motive. But the novel's deeper interest is in how the *appearance* of motive can be as destructive as actual guilt. The phrase "had a motive" (014-070) becomes a kind of accusation in itself.

[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]] himself is angered by the Fiscal's relentless focus on motive. He complains of:

> "…this infernal jargon about the motive and the people who stood to gain…" (016-062)

His frustration captures the novel's ambivalence about motive as a legal tool — it is indispensable, but easily weaponised.

## The Investigation's Logic

The [[procurator-fiscal|Procurator Fiscal]]'s method is to eliminate those without motive: "those are no motives at all. For who knew what th—" (014-076). By process of elimination, the field narrows. But the novel complicates this tidy logic: motive can be concealed, invented, or misattributed. [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]]'s counter-investigation works by questioning the [[procurator-fiscal|Fiscal]]'s assumptions about who had reason to kill — and who did not.

In the end, motive proves to be the key that unlocks the case. The [[george-rattars-confession|confession]] reveals the true reason for the murder, which had been hidden beneath layers of misdirection and false suspicion.

## Motive and the Law

The novel's treatment of motive reflects the actual structure of Scottish criminal investigation in the early twentieth century. The [[procurator-fiscals-office|Procurator Fiscal's Office]] was responsible for directing police inquiries and deciding whether sufficient evidence existed to bring a case to trial. Motive was not a legal requirement for conviction, but it was essential to constructing a narrative that a jury could understand. The Fiscal's insistence on establishing motive — even when it meant pursuing a theory he knew to be false — dramatises the gap between legal procedure and truth.
