## Summary

The engagement between [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] and [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]] was a clandestine romantic attachment formed before and maintained during the early events of the novel. Their relationship was opposed by [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]] on the grounds that Cicely's birth carried a "blemish" rendering her ineligible to become the wife of the heir to the Cromarty baronetcy. The engagement later became a pivotal element in the murder investigation when the impostor procurator fiscal, [[george-rattar|George Rattar]], exploited knowledge of it to cast suspicion on Malcolm and Cicely.

## Context

Following the death of the previous baronet, [[charles-cromarty|Charles Cromarty]] (see [[charles-cromartys-death]]), [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]] became the heir to [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]], living at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] (see [[keldale-house]]). [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]], a young woman of charm and intelligence but ambiguous parentage, lived as a companion to [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]]. Their mutual affection developed against the backdrop of [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s disapproval.

[[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] articulated his objections directly to [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]], citing Malcolm's future position as requiring a wife "without the blemish on her birth" (008-053). He asserted emphatically that "neither Cicely nor Malcolm is in a position to contemplate matrimony for an instant" (008-048).

## Key Participants

- **[[cicely-farmond]]** — A young woman living at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] as companion to [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]]; described as sensible and attractive, but with a social disadvantage through no fault of her own.
- **[[malcolm-cromarty]]** — The heir to [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]; a would-be author whose affections for Cicely were openly acknowledged within the family but officially discouraged.
- **[[sir-reginald-cromarty]]** — The baronet who opposed the match on grounds of social eligibility and inheritance.
- **[[lady-cromarty]]** — Cicely's protector and advocate within the household; defended Cicely's character against [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s objections.
- **[[george-rattar]]** — The impostor procurator fiscal who weaponised knowledge of the engagement to construct a false case against Malcolm and Cicely in the [[the-cromarty-murder]] investigation.

## Sequence

The relationship between [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] and [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]] unfolded across the early chapters of the novel. In Chapter VIII, they were observed walking together at [[stanesland|Stanesland]], with Malcolm openly pressing Cicely for her affection: "I wish I knew how to win your sympathy, Cicely" and "Your sympathy, Cicely, would make all the difference to my life!" (008-002, 008-016, 008-022). Cicely maintained a gentle but firm distance, responding with measured caution.

During the same visit, [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] discussed the matter privately with [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]], making his position clear. [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]] defended Cicely vigorously, insisting that "Cicely is not a girl with mercenary motives" (008-050). [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] conceded Cicely's good qualities but maintained that Malcolm's future position demanded a wife of unblemished birth (008-053).

> I have told you before, and I repeat it now emphatically, that neither Cicely nor Malcolm is in a position to contemplate matrimony for an instant. (008-048)

Despite [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s opposition, the attachment persisted. Word of the engagement leaked into county gossip and first surfaced in the murder investigation when witnesses mentioned it to the authorities. In Chapter XIV, the engagement was explicitly named in questioning (014-061, 014-081), and the term "clandestine engagement" was used (014-083), capturing the tension between the couple's genuine affection and the family's official denial.

In Chapter XXIX, the engagement became the subject of interrogation when it was revealed that [[george-rattar|George Rattar]] had known of it: "Who told you that [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] was engaged to [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]]?" (029-005). Rattar admitted he "mentioned the engagement as a mere matter of course to somebody" and that this disclosure "started this slander about [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]] and [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] conspiring to murder" (029-019).

## Outcome / Significance

The engagement between Cicely and Malcolm had three significant consequences for the novel's trajectory:

1. **Suspicion and slander**: [[george-rattar|George Rattar]]'s knowledge and strategic deployment of the engagement information became the seed of the false theory that Malcolm and Cicely conspired to murder [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]. This misdirection was central to the impostor fiscal's effort to deflect investigation from himself.

2. **Revelation of character**: The engagement episode illuminated the social dynamics of [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] — [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s class-consciousness, [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]]'s protective loyalty to Cicely, and Malcolm's immaturity contrasted with Cicely's self-possession.

3. **Narrative foil to Ned's courtship**: The failure of this clandestine engagement stood in pointed contrast to the subsequent [[ned-and-cicelys-engagement]], in which [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s directness and disregard for convention carried the day where Malcolm's weaker character could not.
