# Letter

## Definition

In the mystery genre, a **letter** is a privileged narrative device: it can convey information across distances, serve as physical evidence, reveal character through voice, and — when discovered by the wrong person — function as a dramatic turning point. Unlike spoken dialogue, a letter is permanent, portable, and potentially incriminating.

In *Simon*, letters punctuate the narrative at critical moments. The novel even devotes an entire chapter — Chapter X, "The Letter" — to the consequences of written communication, marking its structural importance to the plot.

## Context in the Novel

The letter as a professional and personal tool is established early. Simon Rattar's daily routine includes his correspondence: "His letters were always laid out on his desk" (003-002). The instruction to "bring the letter book" (003-003) establishes the systematic record-keeping of the legal office, where "the letter book and ledger" (003-009) form the documentary backbone of the practice: "His letters were always laid out on his desk" (003-002), and the instruction to "bring the letter book" (003-003) introduces the systematic record-keeping of the lawyer's office. The "letter book and ledger" (003-009) are the documentary backbone of the legal practice — and potentially, a repository of evidence.

Chapter X centres on a letter that alters the course of the investigation. The chapter's very title signals the letter's status as a plot mechanism capable of redirecting suspicion and revealing motive. In a novel about identity theft, the written word carries a special evidentiary weight: unlike a person, a letter cannot be impersonated.

## Real-World Background

In Edwardian Britain, the postal service was the primary medium of long-distance communication. Letters were delivered multiple times daily in urban areas, and the morning post was a ritual of the professional classes. The letter book — a bound volume in which copies of outgoing correspondence were kept — was a standard tool of legal and commercial offices, providing an archival record of all official communications. Clouston's depiction of Rattar's letter book is accurate to period practice, and its dual function — as both administrative tool and potential evidence repository — is central to its narrative role.
