# Robert MacAlister

Robert MacAlister, known familiarly as Robbie, is the cousin of [[superintendent-sutherland|Superintendent Sutherland]] and a local resident whose insatiable curiosity — described by the narrator as his "dominating characteristic" — propels him into the investigation of the [[the-cromarty-murder|Cromarty murder]] as both witness and inadvertent impediment.

## Character

MacAlister's defining trait is established in his first appearance:

> An intelligent, inveterate and persevering curiosity was Mr. MacAlister's dominating characteristic. [019-004]

He is eager to hear official confidences from his policeman cousin and fancies himself something of an amateur detective, declaring "I may tell you I've notions of ma own; no bad notions either" (019-010). His speech is rendered in Scots dialect — "I'd like fine to hae a crack wi' you aboot it, George" (019-010) — marking him as a local man, rooted in the community, in contrast to the more professionally English idiom of figures like [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]].

His relationship with Sutherland is affectionate but asymmetrical: MacAlister pursues his cousin with questions and theories, while Sutherland treats him with the indulgent patience of a professional dealing with an enthusiastic amateur. When MacAlister pleads for information, Sutherland deflects with official caution: "we maybe have our notions, but there's no evidence yet, Robbie; that's the fair truth. As the fiscal says, there's no evidence" (019-008).

## The Railway Platform

MacAlister's most significant contribution to the plot occurs at the railway station, where a chance meeting with his cousin sets in motion one of the novel's most suspenseful sequences. Sutherland was travelling a few stations down the line "to investigate a rumour of a suspicious person" — setting out "merely as a matter of form, and to demonstrate his vigilance and almost abnormal sense of duty" (019-001). As he was about to board, he spotted MacAlister on the platform.

MacAlister hailed him with "even more than usual friendliness" and drew him "insidiously towards the far side of the platform" (019-005) — the adverb "insidiously" capturing the comic undertone of his relentless curiosity. He immediately began pressing for information about the murder investigation:

> "And what's doing aboot the murder noo, George?" [019-006]

When Sutherland mentioned he was catching the train, MacAlister — with the opportunism of the truly curious — declared "I'm for off by her mysel'!" (019-011) and invited himself along for the journey.

## The Muffled Stranger

Returning to the carriage, Sutherland opened a compartment door and found it no longer empty:

> In one of the further corners sat a man wrapped in a dark coloured ulster. A black felt hat was drawn down over his eyes, and his muffled face was resting on his hand. [019-014]

But before Sutherland could act on his professional instincts, MacAlister intervened:

> "Here, man, the carriage next door is empty!" cried he, and the superintendent closed the door and followed him. [019-015]

This moment — MacAlister's casual redirection of his cousin away from the suspicious figure — would prove fateful. Once aboard, MacAlister settled in for the conversation he craved, "prepared himself to receive official confidences" (019-016). But Sutherland was distracted, and eventually asked the questions that revealed the gravity of what they had seen:

> "Robbie, did ye see yon man next door sitting with his face in his hands?" [019-018]

MacAlister had seen the man but could identify nothing about him — neither his identity nor whether he had been on the platform. His observation, while accurate, was useless for identification. When Sutherland mused that "it seems funny-like a man dressed like yon and with his face wrapped up too" (019-024), MacAlister's "own grew suddenly animated" with the realisation:

> "You're not thinking he's to dae wi' the murder, are you!" he cried. [019-025]

## The Chase

When the train slowed at the next station, MacAlister's enthusiasm transformed into action:

> "We're slowing doon the noo! God, George, I'll come and hae a look wi' you!" [019-028]

The two men rushed to the adjacent compartment — but found it empty. "God! It's empty!" murmured Sutherland (019-029), and MacAlister, equally shaken, murmured "God save us!" (019-031).

It was at this point that MacAlister committed his second, more damaging error. In a moment of panicked inspiration, he cried:

> "This canna be the richt carriage! Come on, Geordie, let's hae a look in the ithers!" [019-031]

This diversion — searching all the other compartments while the real one went unexamined — gave the mysterious passenger time to escape. The superintendent later reproached him bitterly:

> "It was you hurrying me off to look in thae other carriages, Robbie!" [019-034]

The final search, conducted properly at the next station, confirmed the worst: "there was not a sign of the muffled stranger, under the seats or anywhere else" (019-041). MacAlister's well-meaning interference had cost Sutherland his best chance at intercepting the suspect.

## Narrative Function

MacAlister serves a triple narrative purpose. First, he provides comic relief — the overly curious local whose amateur detective ambitions are comically at odds with his actual competence. Second, he functions as a witness: his independent observation of the muffled stranger corroborates Sutherland's account and establishes the figure as objectively real, not a figment of the superintendent's imagination. Third, and most importantly, he serves as a plot mechanism: his interference explains why the stranger escaped, preserving the mystery that drives the novel's second half.

The irony of MacAlister's role is structural: a man whose "dominating characteristic" is curiosity — the drive to know — becomes the instrument by which knowledge is thwarted. His eagerness to be part of the investigation makes him an obstacle to it.
