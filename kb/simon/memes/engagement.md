# Engagement

## Definition

In Edwardian society, an **engagement** was a formal contract to marry — a binding commitment with legal, financial, and social consequences. A broken engagement could damage a woman's reputation irreparably, while a "secret engagement" carried the additional risk of family opposition and social scandal. Engagements among the landed classes often involved complex negotiations over settlements, inheritance expectations, and family alliances.

*Simon* uses the engagement convention to structure its romantic subplot and to deepen the stakes of the inheritance question. The question of who will marry whom is intertwined with the question of who will inherit what.

## Context in the Novel

The engagement plot centres on [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] and [[malcolm-cromarty|Malcolm Cromarty]]. When the question is asked — "anything said about [[malcolm-cromarty|Sir Malcolm]]'s engagement to [[cicely-farmond|Miss Farmond]]?" (014-081) — the response reveals a complication: "A clandestine engagement then?" (014-083). The secret nature of their commitment introduces dramatic tension, as Malcolm's uncertain inheritance prospects make an open engagement imprudent.

The question arises: "anything said about [[malcolm-cromarty|Sir Malcolm]]'s engagement to [[cicely-farmond|Miss Farmond]]?" (014-081), and the response indicates secrecy: "A clandestine engagement then?" (014-083). [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s attitude toward the match is suggested to be unfavourable — "a secret engagement, like, that [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]] wouldn't hear of" (016-042) — linking the romantic plot to the novel's central crime. [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald]]'s death, while tragic, removes the primary obstacle to the couple's happiness, a morally complex implication that the novel handles with the emotional reticence characteristic of its period.

[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s courtship of [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] provides a counterpoint: the straightforward [[america|America]]n cowboy-turned-laird pursues his romantic interest with a directness that contrasts with Malcolm's cautious secrecy.

## Real-World Background

The legal concept of breach of promise of marriage remained actionable in British courts until 1970. An engagement was not merely a social arrangement but carried legal force: a woman who had relied on a promise of marriage to her detriment could sue for damages. This legal backdrop lent weight to the secrecy and anxiety surrounding engagements in Edwardian fiction. Clouston's portrayal of [[cicely-and-malcolms-engagement|the clandestine engagement]] between Cicely and Malcolm reflects the genuine social pressures of the period, where parental approval (or its withholding) could determine the course of young lives.
