# Kid Gloves

The **kid gloves** are a key piece of forensic evidence in the investigation into the murder of Simon Rattar. Simon Rattar kept "an old pair of kid gloves in his desk which he was in the habit of wearing when he was alone in the office" (040-034). This seemingly minor personal habit becomes one of the most telling clues that the man posing as Simon Rattar is actually his twin brother George.

## Discovery

The information about the kid gloves comes from Ison, Simon Rattar's clerk, who is described as "the not too observant Ison." Carrington interviewed Ison extensively and extracted this detail among several "recent peculiarities of conduct" that Ison had noticed about his employer (040-034).

## Significance as evidence

Carrington immediately recognises the bearing of the kid gloves on the case. On his hypothesis, George Rattar — an ex-convict — wore the gloves "to avoid leaving finger marks" (040-036). This was a "very judicious precaution" because:

1. Fingerprints would have revealed that the person in Simon's office was not Simon but George, whose prints were on file from his criminal conviction.
2. It was "extremely difficult to catch him out by that means, for one could scarcely approach a respectable solicitor and ask him for an impression of his fingers" (040-036).
3. Nothing could be "definitely proved against him until we had found Simon's body" (040-036).

## Connection to identity theft

The kid gloves are part of a pattern of forensic caution exhibited by George Rattar during his impersonation of Simon. Together with the [[glass-match-box]] (which George kept in Simon's otherwise tobacco-free office), the gloves reveal the small but telling discrepancies between Simon's habits and George's behaviour. Simon was a non-smoker and would have had no reason to wear gloves while working alone; George, as an ex-convict accustomed to concealing his identity, had learned to take such precautions.

## Broader forensic context

The novel predates modern fingerprinting but engages with contemporary forensic awareness. Bisset, the butler, earlier explains that "Experienced criminals, that's been convicted before, frequently wears gloves for to prevent their finger prints being spotted" (011-088). The kid gloves thus connect local detection knowledge with Carrington's more sophisticated investigative reasoning.
