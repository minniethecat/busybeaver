# Bisset's Resignation

[[james-bisset|James Bisset]]'s resignation from [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s service is a significant turning point in the novel's domestic subplot. The philosophical butler, whose observational habits have made him increasingly suspicious of his employer's altered behaviour, finally reaches the point where continued service becomes impossible.

## Cause

The impostor's behaviour, tolerable at first, has deteriorated to the point where the observant [[james-bisset|Bisset]] can no longer overlook it. The "observant [[james-bisset|Bisset]]" (015-004) has noticed too many departures from the genuine Simon's established patterns. His resignation is a quiet but decisive act — the servant who knows too much choosing to leave rather than to confront.

## Significance

[[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s departure signals the collapse of the impostor's domestic control. If even the butler — trained to discretion and loyal service — cannot tolerate the impostor's presence, the impersonation has reached its limit. The resignation is the domestic equivalent of the legal case Carrington is building: evidence that the imposture cannot be sustained indefinitely.

## Bisset's Discovery

Before his resignation, [[james-bisset|Bisset]] had been observing the impostor's behavior with growing suspicion. As a butler "practically one of the family" (005-054), [[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s intimate knowledge of the household gave him a unique perspective. His early warnings about the master's altered habits were initially dismissed — "I applied to [[james-bisset|Bisset]]" (005-051) became a refrain when others needed household intelligence — but his observations accumulated.

[[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s philosophical turn of mind, revealed in his after-dinner conversations, made him a particularly dangerous witness. "Expert knowledge was [[james-bisset|James Bisset]]'s foible" (009-005), and his methodical nature meant he catalogued every deviation from the genuine Simon's established patterns. By the time of his resignation, [[james-bisset|Bisset]] had gathered enough evidence to be certain — and enough discretion to leave quietly rather than accuse openly.

## Aftermath

[[james-bisset|Bisset]]'s departure did not end his role in events. He resurfaced at [[stanesland|Stanesland]], bringing [[cicely-farmond|Cicely Farmond]] to safety after her own ordeal at [[keldale-house|Keldale]]. "I advised her vera strongly, sir, to come over with me to [[stanesland|Stanesland]]" (037-009), he explained to [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]], demonstrating that his loyalty had transferred from the impostor to those wronged by him. His detailed account of events at [[keldale-house|Keldale]] — "the haill story" (037-016) — provided crucial testimony that helped Carrington piece together the case against [[george-rattar|George Rattar]].
