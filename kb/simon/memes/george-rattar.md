# George Rattar

George Rattar is the novel's hidden antagonist — the identical twin brother of [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] who murders him, assumes his identity, and impersonates the procurator fiscal throughout the investigation into [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]]'s murder. As a convicted criminal who has served time, George's assumption of his respectable brother's identity represents the novel's most profound exploration of the gap between reputation and reality.

The novel introduces him, though the reader does not yet know it, on the very first page of the narrative proper:

> The procurator fiscal breakfasted at 8:30, punctually, and at 8:30 as usual he entered his severely upholstered dining-room and shut the door behind him. (002-002)

In retrospect, the detail of the "shut door" carries a double weight: the impostor is already at work, already sealing himself off from scrutiny, already performing the ritual of normalcy that is his only protection.

## Character

George differs from Simon in crucial respects that the imposture gradually reveals. Simon was, as the investigation ultimately establishes, "an anti-tobacco fanatic" (040-032); George smokes — and it is the discovery that the supposed Simon has "smoked two pipes of tobacco" (040-032) that delivers the decisive revelation to the investigator. Simon was genuinely taciturn; George's occasional volubility provides the observant with hints of the substitution. When the housemaid Mary hears a long speech from her master, the narrative notes:

> This was rather a long speech for silent Simon, and Mary thought it considerate of him to explain any nocturnal sounds beforehand; unusually considerate, in fact, for he seldom went out of his way to explain things. (006-019)

## Role in the Plot

George is the novel's central criminal, though his identity as such is concealed until the climax. Throughout the main action, he appears as "[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]" — [[simon-rattar|the fiscal]], [[simon-rattar|the lawyer]], the respectable gentleman — giving orders to the police, directing the investigation, and maintaining the facade of probity that his crime has made hollow.

The audacity of the conceit — a murderer directing the investigation into his own victim's murder — makes George one of the most ambitious antagonists in Edwardian crime fiction. The physical resemblance is perfect: "They were twins. George was the one with a moustache" (010-020). But his performance of Simon's character is imperfect, and it is through these imperfections that the truth eventually emerges.

## Key Relationships

- [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]] — his identical twin, murdered and replaced by George
- [[james-bisset|James Bisset]] — the butler whose philosophical observations detect the impostor's irregularities
- [[f-t-carrington|F. T. Carrington]] — the enquiry agent who methodically uncovers his crime

## Notable Quotes

The impostor's presence is registered not through his own words — he is a performer, and his words are Simon's — but through the reactions of those who sense something amiss:

> It struck the visitor, moreover, that in this effort the lawyer was displaying a fluency not at all characteristic of silent Simon. (017-057)

> Silent Simon's sudden glance chilled her smile. (005-069)

> "Smoked two pipes of tobacco! A man who was an anti-tobacco fanatic! The truth hit me straight in the eye — 'That man is not Simon Rattar!'" (040-032)

> "Your damned glass eye has been getting on my nerves long enough!" he exclaimed. "My God, that eye and your habit of hanging people — I've had enough of them!" (039-023)

## In the Eyes of Others

George Rattar is the man who is always seen through a mask — the perception of others is the very medium of his crime. Every gaze that falls upon him sees Simon; every judgment rendered is of a man who no longer exists.

- **[[james-bisset|James Bisset]]'s philosophical suspicion**: The butler is the first to sense something amiss. His method is empirical and cautious — "they were always gossiping about Mr. Malcolm and Miss Cicely, but there was never what I should call a data to found a deduction on; not for a sensible person" (016-068) — a credo that distinguishes him from the servants whose gossip he disdains. Bisset does not leap to conclusions, but he registers discrepancies: a speech too long, a manner not quite right, a silence where there should have been speech.

- **[[f-t-carrington|Carrington]]'s professional penetration**: The enquiry agent, a stranger to the county, brings no preconceptions about "Simon Rattar" to his investigation. When he presents his card and Rattar "gazed at it cautiously and in silence for the better part of a minute" (022-002), Carrington registers the caution of a man with something to hide. Carrington sees through the performance because he has no investment in believing it.

- **[[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]'s unseeing familiarity**: Ned, who has known Simon Rattar all his life, is paradoxically the slowest to suspect. The fiscal's familiarity — "My dear Ned, no hurry whatever" (004-006) — disarms rather than alerts. Ned's investigative instincts, so sharp at the crime scene, are blunted by the impostor's perfect command of Simon's social manner.

- **The community's unquestioning acceptance**: To the county at large, the impostor is simply the procurator fiscal doing his duty. His authority is institutional, not personal; the office shields the man. The community's failure to detect the imposture is not stupidity but a function of social trust — the same trust that makes the legal system work is what George Rattar exploits.

- **[[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s silent accusation**: The murdered brother, buried and replaced, is the one perspective that can never be voiced — yet it haunts every scene George occupies. When George sits at Simon's desk, receives Simon's clients, and directs Simon's investigation, the reader's awareness of the absent original gives every professional gesture a ghoulish second meaning.

## Author's Craft

Clouston's construction of George Rattar exploits the narrative possibilities of the doppelganger with unusual control, embedding the imposture in the texture of the prose itself.

- **The twin as narrative engine**: The identical twin is one of the oldest devices in fiction, but Clouston uses it with forensic precision. George and Simon are not merely similar — they are physically indistinguishable — yet their psychological differences (the smoking, the occasional volubility) are the very clues that undo the imposture. The plot is a machine for converting character into evidence.

- **Dramatic irony as structural principle**: From the moment the imposture is known (or suspected), every scene in which "Simon Rattar" appears is double-voiced. The reader reads George's words while the other characters hear Simon's. Clouston sustains this irony across dozens of scenes without allowing it to become monotonous — varying the distance between what the reader knows and what the characters perceive so that some scenes are almost painful in their irony while others are nearly neutral.

- **The criminal as method actor**: George Rattar is not merely a murderer but a performer, and Clouston gives him the actor's problem: how to sustain a role under scrutiny. His occasional lapses — a speech too long, a manner too familiar — are not failures of nerve but failures of craft, the small errors that accumulate until the performance collapses. This conception of the criminal as an artist manque gives George a complexity rare in the villains of Edwardian fiction.

- **The crime as domestic tragedy**: Unlike the Gothic villain who operates from a distant lair, George lives where Simon lived, eats where Simon ate, handles Simon's clients and receives Simon's mail. The imposture is not a remote conspiracy but a daily, domestic performance — and it is the intimacy of the crime, the fact that George must get every detail of ordinary life right every single day, that makes it both chilling and, in its doomed inevitability, almost pitiable.

## Narrative Impact

George Rattar is the novel's structural secret — the concealed character whose concealment is the plot. Every scene in which the fiscal appears is, in retrospect, a scene with George; every line of dialogue is a performance; every professional action is a criminal covering his tracks. The imposture is not a twist grafted onto the story but the story itself — and the reader who re-reads the novel with knowledge of the imposture experiences an entirely different book, one in which the villain is no longer hidden but everywhere visible, his every word a lie the reader can now decode.

He is not merely the villain; he is the system, the law, the investigation — the entire apparatus of justice, corrupted from within by the very crime it purports to solve.
