# Murder

Murder is the gravitational centre of J. Storer Clouston's *Simon* — the act that sets the plot in motion and the crime that every character circles, investigates, or conceals. The killing of [[sir-reginald-cromarty|Sir Reginald Cromarty]] at [[keldale-house|Keldale House]] is the novel's central event, and the investigation of that crime drives the narrative from its opening chapters to its final revelations.

## The Crime

The novel opens with the shadow of murder already present. [[f-t-carrington|Carrington]], the detective figure, remarks on the scarcity of serious crime in the region: "We hanged him for murder, as a matter of fact" (004-028) — a reference to an earlier execution that has shaped the Cromarty family's recent history. But the central event is the killing of Sir Reginald Cromarty at Keldale House. The discovery is announced starkly to [[ned-cromarty|Ned Cromarty]]:

> "There's a story that Sir Reginald was murdered last night." (011-004)

The physical facts are examined forensically at the scene. [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]], as Procurator Fiscal, and the police superintendent describe what they found:

> "We found Sir Reginald lying there. His skull had been fairly cracked, just over the right eye, sir. The blow would have been enough to kill him I'd think myself, but there were marks in his neck too, seeming to show that the murderer had strangled him afterwards to make sure." (011-015)

The violence of the crime — a blow to the head followed by strangulation — suggested a killer who was both determined and thorough, leaving nothing to chance.

## The Locked-Room Puzzle

The immediate mystery confronting the investigators was one of access. Sir Reginald had been killed in his own library, a room found locked from the inside with the key missing:

> "The question is — how did the murderer get into the room? The door was found locked and the key had been taken away, so whether he had locked it from the inside or the outside we can't tell." (011-059)

The window became the focus of intense scrutiny. [[james-bisset|James Bisset]], the butler, advanced his own theory with characteristic confidence, arguing that the door was the only possible entry point:

> "It's no in reason, Mr. Cromarty, to suppose he deliberately opened the windie to let his ain murderer in. And it's a' just stuff and nonsense to suggest Sir Reginald was sitting on a winter's night — or next door to winter onyhow, with his windie wide open." (014-007)

But Simon Rattar, with lawyerly precision, refused to foreclose any possibility: "If the murderer came in through the window while Sir Reginald was in the room, either the window was standing open or Sir Reginald opened it for him!" (012-030). The locked-room puzzle — was the killer already inside the house, or did they enter from outside? — became the hinge on which the entire investigation turned.

## Motive and Suspicion

The question of motive was equally perplexing. As [[lady-cromarty|Lady Cromarty]] exclaimed:

> "A man without an enemy in the world — no robbery attempted — sitting in his own library — in just about the most peaceful and out of the way county in Scotland — and yet murdered!" (012-036)

With robbery ruled out, the investigation turned inward — toward the household, the family, and the will. [[simon-rattar|Simon Rattar]]'s review of Sir Reginald's testament identified potential beneficiaries, but the narrowing of the suspect pool pointed toward those closest to the victim:

> "On the other hand, there were within the house two people with a very strong motive for committing the crime." (019-010)

The locked room, the missing key, the absence of forced entry — all pointed toward an inside job, a crime committed by someone who belonged at Keldale, someone whose presence in the house on the night of the murder required no explanation.

## Legal and Moral Dimensions

Murder in the novel is both a legal fact and a moral test. The law's response is embodied in the Procurator Fiscal — Simon Rattar, whose official duty was to prosecute, and whose personal knowledge complicated that duty:

> "I am the Procurator Fiscal in charge of this case. But I am also lawyer and factor to the Cromarty family, and my father was before me. If there was evidence enough — clear and proper evidence — to convict any person of this crime, it would be my duty as Procurator Fiscal to convict them. But there is no definite evidence, as you know yourself. All we can do, if we push this matter too far, is to make a family scandal public." (019-019)

This speech — delivered to Ned Cromarty at a critical juncture — encapsulates the novel's central moral tension. The law demands prosecution; family loyalty demands discretion. The murder investigation becomes a test not only of detective skill but of ethical character — of how far each person is willing to go to protect those they love, and at what cost to justice.

## The Two Murders

The novel is structured around two killings: the present-day murder of Sir Reginald and the historical murder of [[charles-cromarty|Charles Cromarty]] (for which a man had been hanged). The past crime haunts the present — it has thinned the Cromarty family line, elevated Ned to an unexpected inheritance, and left a residue of grief and suspicion that colours every relationship at Keldale. The echo between the two murders raises the question that the novel finally answers: are these isolated acts of violence, or do they spring from a common source — a pattern of concealment, imposture, and desperate self-preservation that runs through the Rattar family history?

## In the Investigation

[[f-t-carrington|Carrington]] approached the murder from outside the family circle, methodically collecting information at the [[kings-arms-hotel|Kings Arms]] and through carefully staged encounters. His method — appearing as a sporting visitor, asking seemingly casual questions — allowed him to gather what he called "facts — as distinguished from the Kings Arms' gossip" (020-018). The contrast between official investigation (Simon Rattar's conflicted prosecution) and private detection (Carrington's independent enquiry) gives the novel its distinctive double perspective on the crime.
