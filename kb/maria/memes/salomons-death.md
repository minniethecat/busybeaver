# Salomón's Death

Salomón's death in Jamaica occurs before the novel's main action but is the originating event of its entire plot. Salomón, a Colombian of Jewish descent, had married in Jamaica in May 1820 — this union produced María, the novel's heroine, and tied the family's fate to the Caribbean island (007-001). His death leaves María orphaned; Efraín's father, honoring his friendship with Salomón, brings the child María to live with his family in the Cauca Valley. Without this death, there would be no love story — and no tragedy (007-002) (007-007).

## Role in the Novel

Salomón's death exemplifies the novel's deep structure: love is always shadowed by prior loss. María's orphanhood is the precondition of her presence in Efraín's life, and the death that made their love possible also prefigures the death that will end it. Salomón's life in Jamaica — where he hunted stags and built a life in exile — remains a distant, romanticized memory, glimpsed only through brief recollections in the narrative (015-028). His final will, directing that something be sent to Kingston, extends his presence beyond death and ties the novel's conclusion back to its Caribbean origins (057-004).

## Key Passages
