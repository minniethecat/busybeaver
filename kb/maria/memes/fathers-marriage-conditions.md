# The Father's Marriage Conditions

Efraín's father sets the terms for his son's future: complete his medical studies in Europe, establish himself professionally, and only then return to marry. These conditions, delivered with paternal authority, mark the beginning of the separation that will prove fatal. (015-040)

## Role in the Novel

The father's conditions introduce the central conflict of the novel: love versus duty. The father's decision is rooted in Dr. Mayn's medical finding that María suffers from epilepsy — a condition that emotional strain could make fatal, making marriage imprudent until Efraín is established and independent (015-032). The father asks Efraín to hear his "judgment about this marriage, in case the disease continues" — framing the conditions as medical prudence rather than cruelty (015-039). Efraín, overwhelmed by these terms, is made to promise he will accept the deferral — a promise extracted under the weight of filial obligation (015-044). The father's authority, exercised in the belief that he is securing his son's future, instead destroys it.

## Key Passages

> "Four days ago I received a letter from Señor M——, asking María's hand for you. I have consented — on condition that you complete your studies first." (015-040)
