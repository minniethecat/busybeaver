# Hacienda Life in María

The hacienda in *María* is not merely a setting but a complete social world. It is an economic enterprise (producing sugar, raising cattle), a patriarchal domain (governed by Efraín's father), a multiracial community (family members, free workers, and slaves), and an emotional landscape (the site of childhood, love, and loss). Understanding the hacienda's structure is essential to understanding the novel — the love story unfolds within, and is constrained by, the material realities of 19th-century Colombian landed society (005-002) (005-003).

## Definition

Hacienda life in *María* is characterized by:

- **Patriarchal order**: Efraín's father is the absolute authority — his decisions about marriage, education, and property determine everyone's fate.
- **Racial hierarchy**: The hacienda is stratified by race and status — the white landowning family at the top, free workers in between, and enslaved Africans at the bottom.
- **Economic self-sufficiency**: The hacienda produces its own food, maintains its own buildings, and operates as a largely self-contained economic unit.
- **Rhythms of nature**: Daily life follows agricultural rhythms — planting, harvest, the movement of cattle — rather than clock time.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The estate tour**: Early in the novel, Efraín tours the hacienda with his father, a ritual that establishes the domain's extent and the father's authority.
- **Slave life**: The novel depicts enslaved workers with a combination of paternalistic affection and unexamined acceptance of the institution (005-002) (005-003).
- **Work rhythms**: Agricultural labor — planting, harvesting, cattle drives — provides the background rhythm against which the love story plays out.
- **Domestic hierarchy**: Within the house, the mother presides over a hierarchy of daughters, servants, and slaves — a parallel domestic order.
- **Hospitality**: The hacienda is a site of constant hospitality — visitors, travelers, and neighbors are received with elaborate courtesy.

## Key Passages

> The slaves were well clad, and as happy as slaves can be... (005-002)

> ...my father, accustomed to being a master, treated his slaves with kindness. (005-003)

## Literary Context

The hacienda novel is a distinct subgenre of Latin American literature, and *María* is one of its founding texts. Isaacs's portrayal of hacienda life is both documentary (recording the actual practices of the Cauca Valley) and idealizing (presenting slavery in a softened, paternalistic light). Later Latin American writers — from José Eustasio Rivera to Gabriel García Márquez — would return to the hacienda as a literary space, often with a more critical perspective on its social structures.
