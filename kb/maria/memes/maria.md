# María

María is the title character and emotional center of Jorge Isaacs's *María*. Orphaned as a child — her mother died in Jamaica, her father [[Salomon|Salomón]] placed her with Efraín's family — she grows up alongside [[efrain|Efraín]] in the [[the-hacienda|hacienda]] of the [[cauca-valley|Cauca Valley]]. Their childhood companionship matures into a love that defines both their lives, but María carries a fatal inheritance: her mother's epilepsy. Her beauty, piety, and quiet devotion make her the novel's ideal of feminine virtue; her illness and death make her its tragic heart (003-003) (015-039).

## Role in the Novel

María is less an actor than a presence — she is described, admired, and mourned more than she acts. Her narrative function is to be loved and to be lost, yet within the constraints of her role she exercises quiet agency: she encourages Efraín's [[garden-confession|confession of love]] through the symbolic gesture of gathering discarded lilies, and her letters reveal an interior life that her public reserve conceals (012-002). Her epilepsy is the biological obstacle that drives the plot — the reason Efraín's father makes marriage conditional, and ultimately the cause of her [[marias-death|death]] during Efraín's absence in Europe (015-039) (015-044).

> María quietly waited her turn, and after imprinting a kiss on my cheek, she said in a very low voice, "Come back soon." (001-003)

## Key Scenes

- **The garden morning**: Efraín's first sight of María as a young woman, among the rosebushes on an August morning — the novel's iconic image of beauty and innocence (002-004).
- **The lily sacrifice**: María picks up the lilies Efraín discarded, "sacrificing pride to love" — a gesture that speaks her feelings more eloquently than words and marks the turning point in their relationship (012-002).
- **The epileptic seizure**: María's first witnessed seizure — a scene of clinical horror and emotional devastation that brings the threat of death into the open, transforming the novel's romantic tone (024-078).
- **The farewell**: As Efraín departs for Europe, María's whispered "Come back soon" carries the weight of premonition — she will not live to see his return (001-004).

> "Then I will pick the most beautiful flowers every day," she said, her voice still whispering; "and I will put them at the foot of the Virgin." (011-013)

## Character Analysis

María embodies the Romantic idealization of womanhood — beautiful, pious, devoted, and doomed. Her characterization draws on the 19th-century cult of the "angel in the house," but Isaacs gives her specificity through the medical reality of her epilepsy and the cultural reality of her orphanhood. She is both type (the perfect beloved) and individual (a young woman struggling with a fatal illness in a world without effective treatment).

Her silence — she speaks relatively little in the novel — makes her a canvas for Efraín's projections. Yet her letters and the few direct words we hear from her suggest depths he never fully accesses. The novel's irony is that María, who is its title and its reason for being, remains in important ways unknowable — a presence defined by absence, a life known most fully through its end.

> "What lovely flowers!" I exclaimed, seeing her among the rosebushes. (003-005)
