# Work and Labor in María

Labor — enslaved, free, and managerial — forms the material foundation of life in the Cauca Valley depicted in Jorge Isaacs's *María*. While the novel is remembered primarily as a love story, its world is sustained by agricultural and domestic work that the narrative registers with documentary precision.

## The Sugar Mill and Agricultural Economy

Efraín's father has invested heavily in productive infrastructure during his son's absence: "a fine and expensive sugar-mill, many acres of cane to supply it, large pastures with droves of cattle and horses in them, good stables, and an excellent house for the overseer" (005-002). The sugar mill represents the transition from traditional hacienda agriculture to more industrialized production.

José, one of the mountain dwellers, complains about the declining profitability of small-scale farming: "honey down to a shilling; brown sugar, not worth talking about; the little sugar that comes out white, a dollar; cheese, given away; the pigs eating up the whole harvest of maize" (057-005). His lament reveals the economic pressures affecting even free farmers in the region.


> One evening, just at sunset, we were coming back with Higinio, the overseer, from the fields to the mill. They were talking about the work to be done; I was occupied with less serious things: I was thinking about the days of my childhood. The peculiar odor of trees just cut down, and of ripe cypress-cones; the clamor of the parrots in the neighboring reeds and among the guava-trees; the distant note of some shepherd's horn echoing among the mountains; the piping of the slaves as they slowly came from their work with their tools on their shoulders; the red glow in the sky seen beyond the fields of waving cane—all reminded me of the afternoons in which I, with María and my sisters, used to gather guavas from our favorite trees.
>
> (005-004)

## Enslaved Labor

[[slavery-and-servitude|Enslaved people]] perform the bulk of the hacienda's physical labor. The narrator describes them returning from the fields at sunset: "the piping of the slaves as they slowly came from their work with their tools on their shoulders" (005-004). This image — workers returning with tools, singing as they walk — aestheticizes labor while acknowledging its rhythms.

The sugar mill itself depends on enslaved labor. One character dismissively describes field hands as "putting cane in the mill. Such stupids, they are! All he can do now is to mind horses" (008-003), revealing the contempt with which manual laborers could be regarded.

## The Overseer System

[[higinio|Higinio]], the overseer, represents the managerial layer between landowners and laborers. He accompanies Efraín and his father on inspections of the fields, discussing "the work to be done" (005-004) while the narrator's mind wanders to childhood memories. The overseer's "excellent house" (005-002) marks his elevated status above the enslaved workers he supervises.

[[lorenzo|Lorenzo]] occupies a distinctive position as "a faithful companion of my father" who "enjoyed in our home the rights of an overseer and the privileges of a friend" (057-003). His status as a free man who is neither owner nor enslaved makes him a valuable intermediary.

## Domestic Labor

Women's work, though less visible, is essential to the hacienda's functioning. [[salome|Salome]] is praised as "neat in her clothes, and a housewife such as you'll scarcely find" (055-003), and her mother [[candelaria|Candelaria]] manages a household that includes a garden, a still, and a cow-yard. Efraín notes that Candelaria's wife "works night and day" (057-005) to supplement the family's income.

Enslaved women perform domestic labor within the main house. [[feliciana|Feliciana]] served as Efraín's nurse in childhood, and other enslaved women attend to the family's daily needs. The clearing of the table after supper, the evening prayers, and the care of children all depend on this largely unremarked labor.

## Cattle Ranching and the Roundup

The cattle roundup at Don Ignacio's property provides the novel's most vivid depiction of ranch labor. [[don-ignacio|Don Ignacio]] personally oversees the branding, cutting "a little notch with his knife in the *guásimo* branch which served him for a whip" (008-012) after each bull is marked. The vaqueros' shouts of "Another, another!" (008-012) and the bellowing of the hobbled bulls create a scene of controlled violence that is also productive labor.

## Horse Culture

Horses permeate the novel's world as instruments of work, travel, and leisure. Efraín, [[carlos|Carlos]], [[braulio|Braulio]], and other male characters are constantly mounting, dismounting, and caring for horses. [[braulio|Braulio]] is described as an expert horseman, and even [[tiburcio|Tiburcio]]'s courtship of Salome is observed through the lens of his weekly rides to visit her.

The care and breeding of horses represents skilled labor that commands respect. When a mare loses her foal, the loss is measured in economic terms: "she will have to pay me for them all together, crushing cane in the treadmill" (057-004).

## The Ideology of Work

The novel reflects the ideology of its landowning protagonists, for whom work is something others do. Efraín's father is depicted as a diligent manager, but his labor consists of supervision and decision-making rather than physical toil. The enslaved and free laborers who perform the actual work remain, for the most part, background figures whose labor is noted but not centered.
