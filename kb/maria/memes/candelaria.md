# Candelaria

Candelaria is the wife of [[jose|José]] and the mother of [[salome|Salome]], living with her family in the mountain region near the [[amaime|Amaime]] River. Though she appears only in dialogue reported by other characters, she emerges as a figure of domestic competence and maternal concern.

## Domestic Life

Candelaria manages a household in the mountain region that includes a garden, a still, and a cow-yard. When Efraín arranges for water to be brought closer to José's house, José imagines his wife's delight: "Candelaria will be delighted; water handy for her garden, for the still, for the cow-yard" (057-007). This brief inventory reveals the scope of her domestic responsibilities.

The water situation at their home underscores the hardships of mountain life. José complains that "the only clean water we can get in the house is by sending the stupid to the little Amaime to bring back gourdfuls on the mare" (057-007), and the water from the Honda stream is undrinkable — "it would be better to drink lye, it's so full of copperas" (057-007).

## Motherhood and Salome's Courtship

Candelaria appears most prominently in the story of [[tiburcio|Tiburcio]]'s courtship of Salome. José recounts how he noticed Tiburcio's interest in his daughter and told Candelaria, who "came out with the reply that my eyes must have been in a cloud, and that my story was stale" (055-001). Her skeptical response — dismissing her husband's observations as outdated — suggests a mother who is both protective and pragmatic.

Later, when Tiburcio stops visiting, "Candelaria could not find out the reason from the girl" (055-001). Her inability to extract information from Salome reveals the limits of maternal authority and the daughter's growing independence.

## Hospitality

Candelaria is the provider of hospitality when Efraín visits. She sends "a sheet, beautifully perfumed" (058-007) for Efraín's bath and calls after the group as they leave for the river: "You must not be long, for the dinner will be ready in a minute" (058-009). These details establish her as a competent, warm-hearted hostess whose domestic labor makes social life possible.

## Superstition and Folk Belief

José attributes to Candelaria a folk belief about the evil eye: "No one can get it out of Candelaria's head that it was that bat who cast the evil-eye upon our monkey, the one that knew so much and used to amuse you so" (055-005). This detail places Candelaria within the world of rural folk belief, contrasting with the more educated worldview of the landowning families.

## Social Position

Candelaria and her family occupy an intermediate social position — neither enslaved nor members of the landowning class. When Efraín's mother offers to have Salome stay with them to learn fine needlework, José recognizes this as an opportunity: "That is just what Candelaria wanted" (055-007). The offer represents upward mobility through patronage, and Candelaria's eagerness for it reveals her aspirations for her daughter.

Candelaria is a figure of domestic wisdom:

> One day I said so clearly to Candelaria, and she came out with the reply that my eyes must have been in a cloud, and that my story was stale.
>
> (042-052)
