# Feliciana's Death and Funeral

The death and funeral of [[feliciana|Feliciana]], Efraín's childhood nurse and [[juan-angel|Juan Ángel]]'s mother, is one of the most emotionally powerful episodes in Jorge Isaacs's *María*. It depicts the mourning rituals of the enslaved community with a dignity and detail that is rare in 19th-century Latin American fiction.

## The Deathbed

Efraín discovers Feliciana in desperate condition: "I went into Feliciana's room. Juan Ángel was already there, astonished that his mother did not respond to his greeting. I could not fail to be affected by finding my old nurse in so desperate a condition" (049-004). The presence of the child who does not yet understand death intensifies the pathos of the scene.

Efraín exercises his authority as the master's son to improve Feliciana's final hours: "I gave orders that more of the slaves should be in attendance upon her, and had her carried into a more comfortable room, though she humbly opposed the change" (049-004). Her humble resistance to being moved — a lifetime of deference asserting itself even in extremis — speaks to the depth of her internalized subordination.

## The Death

Feliciana dies "an hour after twilight" (049-005). The enslaved women dress her body and place her in a coffin, "covered from her throat down with white linen" (049-005). The coffin is placed "upon a table draped with crape, and with tapers burning at the four corners" (049-005), the ritual objects marking the transition from life to death.

Juan Ángel stands at the head of the coffin, "shedding tears upon his mother's forehead" (049-005). The image of the orphaned child weeping over his mother's body is one of the novel's most direct depictions of grief.

## The Evening Prayer

Efraín orders the overseer to bring all the enslaved workers for evening prayer. "They came in silently. The men and boys filled up the whole eastern corridor. The women kneeled in a circle about the coffin" (057-001). The spatial arrangement — men standing in the corridor, women kneeling around the body — reflects gendered divisions within the enslaved community.

The prayer itself, conducted across the threshold between the death chamber and the corridor, unites the indoor and outdoor spaces, the living and the dead, in a single ritual act.

## The Funeral Hymn

After the rosary, "a female slave intoned the first stanza of one of those chants filled with grieving melancholy and irrepressible laments so familiar to the hearts of slaves" (057-003). The entire company repeats the stanza in chorus, the "deep voices of the men harmonizing with the clear and sweet tones of the women and boys" (057-003).

Isaacs transcribes several verses of the hymn, preserving a fragment of enslaved musical culture that would otherwise have been lost. The verses — simple, direct, filled with longing for release from suffering — speak in the authentic voice of the enslaved, momentarily displacing the narrator's educated discourse.

## The Burial

At four in the morning, Efraín sets out with "the eight slaves bearing the corpse" (057-004). He has arranged for Juan Ángel to remain behind, "as I wished to keep him from the hard trial of taking leave of his mother" (057-004).

At the graveside, a moment of profound emotion occurs: "The coffin was lowered to the bottom of the grave. A slave threw in the first spadeful of earth. Juan Ángel rushed at him and seized the spade — an action that filled us all with distress" (057-005). The boy, who has defied Efraín's protective order and followed the funeral procession, asserts his right to participate in his mother's burial. His seizure of the spade is an act of filial devotion that transcends the constraints of his enslaved status.

## Significance

Feliciana's death and funeral serve multiple functions in the novel. They provide the most detailed depiction of enslaved religious and communal life. They establish Juan Ángel as a character whose suffering commands attention. And they foreshadow the novel's central death — that of [[maria|María]] — establishing a pattern of mourning that will be repeated on a larger scale.

The episode also reveals the emotional bonds that crossed the color line in the hacienda world. Efraín's grief for his old nurse is genuine, even if it coexists with the hierarchical assumptions of his class. The funeral brings together the entire enslaved community in an act of collective mourning that momentarily transcends the divisions of status and condition.
