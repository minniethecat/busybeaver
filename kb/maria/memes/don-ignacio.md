# Don Ignacio

Don Ignacio is a wealthy landowner in the Cauca Valley, the father of [[emigdio|Emigdio]], and a neighbor of [[efrain|Efraín]]'s family. He appears in several episodes that illuminate the social world of the region's landowning class.

## Appearance and Character

Don Ignacio is introduced through the striking description of his hat, which his son Emigdio wears to Bogotá: "a hat made of chocolate-colored skin, a thing that had been the pride of Don Ignacio, his father, in the long-past days of his youth" (006-003). The hat, worn at "an angle of ninety degrees," becomes a comic emblem of provincial eccentricity.

When Efraín and [[carlos|Carlos]] visit Don Ignacio at his pastures during a cattle roundup, the narrator provides a vivid portrait: "He was riding a lamentable bay nag, caparisoned with a saddle whose dilapidation bespoke its merits" (008-010). Despite his wealth, Don Ignacio's appearance is notably unfashionable — "jaguar-skin leggings, much worn; silver spurs with jingling rowels; a jacket of coarse sacking, and a very cottony wool cloak; crowning all, an enormous *jipijapa* hat" (008-010).

Isaacs describes Don Ignacio's face with comic precision: "under its shade, the tiny nose and little blue eyes of Don Ignacio had the same appearance as the projecting beak, and the bits of glass for pupils, in the head of a stuffed *paletón*" (008-010). This unflattering portrait establishes him as a figure of provincial color rather than romantic dignity.

## The Cattle Roundup

The cattle roundup scene reveals Don Ignacio's role as an active landowner who personally oversees his operations. He "would cut a little notch with his knife in the *guásimo* branch which served him for a whip" (008-012) as each bull was branded. His practical expertise contrasts with the bookish education of young men like Efraín, whom he gently mocks: "In Bogotá they are afraid of the sun or a fierce bull; that's the reason boys in college there simply waste themselves" (008-011).

## The Lawsuit with Don Jerónimo

Don Ignacio is involved in a long-running boundary dispute with [[don-jeronimo|Don Jerónimo]], another prominent landowner. This lawsuit is mentioned during a dinner at Efraín's home, where Don Jerónimo "unfolded his napkin without breaking off the wearisome history of his lawsuit with Don Ignacio over their boundary line" (019-001). The lawsuit represents the petty conflicts and social tensions that characterize relations among the landowning class.

## Fatherhood

As Emigdio's father, Don Ignacio exercises the patriarchal authority typical of his class. Emigdio confesses to Efraín that he has hidden his love for Zoila from his father, "who would be sure to try to break it all off, as the girl was not a lady" (008-025). This reveals Don Ignacio's concern with social hierarchy and his expectation that his son marry within their class.

## Social Significance

Don Ignacio represents a type: the provincial landowner whose wealth and status coexist with rustic manners and eccentric appearance. Through him, Isaacs sketches the social landscape of the Cauca Valley, where families like Efraín's navigate relationships with neighbors who are simultaneously peers, rivals, and characters of local color.

Don Ignacio is introduced through a memorable physical description:

> My fellow-countryman had come loaded down with a hat made of chocolate-colored skin, a thing that had been the pride of Don Ignacio, his father, in the long-past days of his youth. His lank figure, his limp and straggling whiskers, mingling with the most inconsolably abandoned hair that ever was seen; his flaming complexion putting the very bricks in the street to shame.
>
> (018-003)
