# Juan Ángel's Death

The death of Juan Ángel, a young enslaved boy on [[the-hacienda|the hacienda]], is one of the novel's most emotionally charged scenes. Earlier in the novel, Juan Ángel appears as a living presence — quieting Efraín's black horse during an errand, a small moment that establishes him as part of the hacienda's daily fabric before his death transforms him into an object of communal grief (014-008). The entire community — family, workers, slaves — gathers for prayers around his body. The scene is notable for its depiction of death as a leveler: in the presence of mortality, the hacienda's rigid social hierarchy momentarily dissolves into shared grief (038-006) (038-009).

## Role in the Novel

Juan Ángel's death scene serves multiple functions: it humanizes the enslaved population by giving one of them a named, mourned death; it demonstrates the integrative (if paternalistic) social order of the hacienda; and it prefigures María's own death, establishing the rituals of mourning that will later be repeated for her. Efraín finds Juan Ángel already in Feliciana's room, astonished and uncomprehending — the boy's innocence makes his death all the more poignant (038-002). The night before the death, Efraín stays with him, trying to get him to sleep, a small act of care that cannot prevent the inevitable (038-009). After the prayers and mass for the dead conclude, the community's ritual response to death is complete — a pattern that will be repeated for María herself (038-011).

## Key Passages
