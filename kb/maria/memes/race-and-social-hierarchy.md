# Race and Social Hierarchy in María

The world of Jorge Isaacs's *María* is organized by a complex system of racial and social hierarchy that reflects Colombia's colonial legacy and the realities of mid-19th-century slaveholding society. Race, class, and legal status intersect to determine every character's place in the social order.

## The Landowning Class

At the apex of the social pyramid stand the white landowning families — [[efrain|Efraín]]'s family, [[don-jeronimo|Don Jerónimo]], [[don-ignacio|Don Ignacio]], and their peers. Their status rests on land ownership, [[slavery-and-servitude|enslaved labor]], and the cultural capital of education and European connections. Efraín's father sends his son to study in [[bogota|Bogotá]], and the family's wealth is displayed through improvements like "a fine and expensive sugar-mill" (005-002).

The landowners' social world is defined by visits, dinners, and courtship rituals among families of equal standing. When [[emigdio|Emigdio]] falls in love with Zoila, he hides it from his father, knowing that Don Ignacio "would be sure to try to break it all off, as the girl was not a lady" (008-025). The phrase "not a lady" encodes both class and racial distinctions.

## The Enslaved Population

[[slavery-and-servitude|Enslaved people]] form the economic foundation of hacienda society. They are described through the paternalistic lens of their masters: "The slaves were well clad, and as happy as it is possible for slaves to be, and were docile and even affectionate towards their master" (005-002). Individual enslaved characters — [[pedro|Pedro]], [[bruno|Bruno]], [[remigia|Remigia]], [[feliciana|Feliciana]], [[julian|Julian]] — are granted distinct personalities, yet they remain defined by their servile status.

## Internal Stratification Among the Enslaved

The enslaved community itself contains internal hierarchies. [[julian|Julian]] serves as "the slave captain of the gang" (005-008), wielding authority over other enslaved people while remaining enslaved himself. His "long knife with silver-plated sheath which was the sign of his office" (005-008) marks his intermediate position. Similarly, domestic slaves who work in the main house occupy a different status from field hands who labor in the cane fields and pastures.

## Free People of Color

The novel's social landscape includes free people of color who occupy ambiguous positions. The musicians at [[bruno|Bruno]] and [[remigia|Remigia]]'s wedding are described as "a mixture of slaves and freedmen" (005-008), indicating the presence of a free black population alongside the enslaved. [[lorenzo|Lorenzo]], though not enslaved, is a "faithful companion of my father" who "enjoyed in our home the rights of an overseer and the privileges of a friend" (057-003).


> It was easy to see that my father, without ceasing to be a master, treated his slaves with kindness. He was anxious for their domestic happiness, and fondled the little ones.
>
> (005-003)

## The Patronage System

Social relations in the novel are mediated through patronage and personal loyalty. Efraín's father arranges marriages, provides medical care, and adjudicates disputes among those who depend on him. This patriarchal benevolence reinforces the existing hierarchy even as it softens its harsher edges. The enslaved are expected to be grateful for their master's kindness, and Efraín's father "was anxious for their domestic happiness, and fondled the little ones" (005-003).

## Gender and Hierarchy

Gender intersects with race and class to create distinct social positions. White women like [[maria|María]] and [[emma|Emma]] occupy a protected domestic sphere, while enslaved women like [[feliciana|Feliciana]] labor and suffer with little protection. [[salome|Salome]]'s position is particularly complex — neither enslaved nor fully accepted into the landowning class, her marriage prospects become a matter of negotiation.

## The Colonial Context

The social hierarchy of *María* reflects Colombia's position as a post-colonial society in the 19th century. Slavery would be abolished in Colombia in 1851, just a few years before the novel's publication in 1867, making the world Isaacs depicts one already passing into history even as he wrote. The novel's nostalgic tone — its mourning for a lost world — is thus inseparable from the social structures it describes.
