# Fathers and Authority in María

Paternal authority is one of the most powerful forces shaping the world of Jorge Isaacs's *María*. Fathers determine their children's education, careers, and marriage prospects, embodying a patriarchal order that the novel both respects and subtly questions.

## Efraín's Father: The Benevolent Patriarch

Efraín's father is the novel's central figure of paternal authority. A successful landowner who has built "a fine and expensive sugar-mill" and expanded the family's holdings (005-002), he embodies the virtues of industry, prudence, and benevolent mastery. His authority over his son is absolute: he sends Efraín to study in [[bogota|Bogotá]], summons him home, and ultimately determines the course of his romantic life.

The father's treatment of his [[slavery-and-servitude|enslaved workers]] models his approach to authority generally: "without ceasing to be a master, treated his slaves with kindness. He was anxious for their domestic happiness, and fondled the little ones" (005-003). This paternalism extends to his own family, where his decisions are final but presented as expressions of care.


> My father was pleased with my attentiveness during our visit to the farms; but when I told him that in the future I wished to share his labors, and stay by his side, he informed me, almost sorrowfully, that it was necessary for him to sacrifice his ease for my sake, and that he should keep the promise made to me before of sending me to Europe to study medicine; I should have to begin the journey, he said, at the end of four months at the latest. When he told me this his face wore an expression of unaffected gravity, which was always to be observed in him when he had taken an irrevocable resolution.
>
> (005-022)

## The Marriage Condition

The central conflict of the novel is set in motion by Efraín's father. He summons Efraín from Bogotá and, after allowing the romance with [[maria|María]] to develop, imposes a condition: Efraín must complete his medical studies in Europe before marrying. This decision is framed as both prudent and painful: the father weeps as he explains his reasoning, and Efraín acknowledges the justice of his father's concerns even as he suffers from them.

The father's authority is not tyrannical but tragic — he acts from genuine love and concern, yet his decision sets in motion the events that lead to María's death. The novel thus presents paternal authority as simultaneously necessary and destructive.

## Don Ignacio: Authority and Class

[[don-ignacio|Don Ignacio]] represents a less refined version of paternal authority. His son [[emigdio|Emigdio]] hides his love for Zoila from him, knowing that his father "would be sure to try to break it all off, as the girl was not a lady" (008-025). Don Ignacio's concern with class endogamy — marrying within one's social rank — reveals the economic and social calculations that underlie paternal decisions about marriage.

## Don Jerónimo: Authority and Conflict

[[don-jeronimo|Don Jerónimo]], [[maria|María]]'s father, represents paternal authority complicated by absence and death. Having lost his fortune and his wife, he entrusts María to Efraín's family while he pursues business elsewhere. His lawsuit with Don Ignacio "over their boundary line" (019-001) symbolizes the conflicts that occupy fathers and distract them from their children's emotional needs.

Don Jerónimo's ultimate plan to take María away from the Cauca Valley represents the collision between paternal authority and romantic love. His authority over his daughter is legally and socially unquestionable, yet the narrative implicitly condemns the separation it imposes.

## Surrogate Fathers

The novel features several characters who serve as surrogate or alternative father figures. [[lorenzo|Lorenzo]], "a faithful companion of my father" (057-003), provides a model of masculine loyalty and competence outside the biological family. The priest who marries [[transito|Tránsito]] and [[braulio|Braulio]] represents spiritual fatherhood, a form of authority based on moral guidance rather than economic power.

## The Limits of Authority

Despite the apparent absoluteness of paternal power, the novel reveals its limits. Efraín's father cannot prevent his son's grief, nor can he reverse the consequences of his own decisions. Don Ignacio's authority is undermined by his comic appearance and his son's quiet defiance. The novel suggests that paternal authority, however well-intentioned, cannot fully control the emotional lives of the young or the operations of fate.

The tragedy of *María* is in part a tragedy of paternal authority — of fathers who act for the best and produce the worst, of decisions made in love that lead to loss. In this sense, the novel offers a gentle but profound critique of the patriarchal order it otherwise depicts with respect.
