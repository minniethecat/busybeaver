The Amaime is a river in the Cauca Valley, near the family hacienda. It is described as a place for recreation — Efraín goes there for plunges and swims — as well as a practical obstacle to be forded during journeys (014-012). The "little Amaime" is a smaller branch where servants fetch water in gourds (042-076). (014-012)

## Role in the Novel

The Amaime River serves as a recurring geographical reference that grounds the novel in the real landscape of the Cauca Valley. It appears in scenes of leisure, work, and travel, connecting the hacienda to the wider world. The river swells with rains and becomes dangerous, while in calmer seasons it offers refreshing baths (018-024). At certain times of year, no better bathing place could be found in the whole Amaime (018-041). (017-012)

## Key Scenes

- The Amaime sweeps down, swollen by rains, presenting a dramatic natural spectacle (014-012).
- Efraín enjoys the magnificent scenery while resting on the rocks in the canyon of the Amaime (017-012).
- Efraín goes for a plunge in the Amaime during a hot day (018-024).
- No better bathing place could be found in the whole Amaime than at that time of year (018-041).
- Efraín fords the little Amaime while traveling, hearing someone call to him from the other side (042-031).
- Efraín fords the Amaime again on the plain, waiting for Juan Ángel to catch up (057-006).

The Amaime River is vividly rendered in the novel:

> The Amaime was sweeping down, swollen by the rains of the night, and I could hear its roar long before I reached its bank. By the moon's light, which was struggling through the trees on the shore, I could see how high the waters were; but I could not wait.
>
> (014-012)
