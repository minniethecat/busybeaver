# Dreams and Premonitions in María

Dreams and premonitions pervade Jorge Isaacs's *María*, functioning as both narrative devices and expressions of the novel's Romantic sensibility. From Efraín's childhood dreams to María's prophetic nightmares, the boundary between sleep and waking, presentiment and reality, is consistently blurred.

## The Opening Meditation on Dreams

The novel's second chapter opens with a philosophical reflection on the power of early emotional impressions: "we meet her of whom we dreamed at eighteen, and her glance burns us" (002-002). This framing establishes dreams not as mere nighttime fantasies but as formative experiences that shape desire and memory. The "dimpled smile of my childish dreams of love" (003-013) that Efraín associates with María links romantic love to the oneiric from the novel's earliest pages.


> I dreamed that María came in to renew the flowers on my table, and that when she went out she brushed against the curtains of my bed with her skirt of gauzy muslin dotted with blue flowers.
>
> (004-001)

## Pedro's Fairy Tale and Efraín's Dream

One of the most revealing dream sequences occurs when Efraín, recently returned from Bogotá, falls asleep after hearing a story from the enslaved [[pedro|Pedro]]: "I dreamed that María came in to renew the flowers on my table, and that when she went out she brushed against the curtains of my bed with her skirt of gauzy muslin dotted with blue flowers" (004-001). The dream blends childhood memory (Pedro's storytelling) with adolescent desire (María's intimate proximity) and sensory detail (the muslin, the blue flowers).

## Dream as Recurring Experience

Dreams are not isolated occurrences but a continuous presence in Efraín's inner life: "How many times in my dreams an echo of that voice has sighed beside me!" (004-002). This passage reveals that the dreaming mind preserves what waking consciousness cannot sustain — María's voice, her presence — making dreams a refuge for the lover separated from his beloved.

## María's Nightmares and Premonitions

María's own dreams carry darker significance. She writes to Efraín of her nightmares: "I dreamed of wandering through groves of palm trees, and a voice — your voice — called me, and I could not find you" (022-031). Her dreams foreshadow the separation and death that await her, aligning with the Romantic tradition of the sensitive heroine whose psychic vulnerability makes her attuned to fate.

The narrator himself notes the prophetic quality of María's nocturnal visions. When Efraín observes that María seems troubled, she responds evasively, and he reflects: "That night I dreamed — but what makes you look like that?" (033-044), suggesting that her premonitions communicate themselves even to those around her.

## Dreams and the Romantic Imagination

Efraín's dreaming is linked explicitly to the Romantic imagination: "I see no more, or admires only in my dreams: the world, nature, as Adam saw it on the first day of creation" (030-003). Dreams provide access to a purer, more intense mode of perception — one that the rational, daylight mind cannot sustain. This aligns with the Romantic valorization of dreams as portals to truth rather than mere fancy.

## Narrative Function

The novel's dream sequences serve multiple narrative purposes: they foreshadow tragic events (María's death), reveal character psychology (Efraín's desire and guilt), and create a lyrical, elegiac atmosphere. By interweaving dream and waking experience, Isaacs blurs the boundary between inner and outer reality, a hallmark of Romantic fiction.

The premonitory power of dreams in the novel also reflects the Catholic cultural context, where dreams could be understood as divine messages or spiritual warnings. María's foreboding dreams carry the weight of religious mystery as well as psychological insight.
