# Music and Song in María

Music in Jorge Isaacs's *María* is both a social practice and an emotional language. The novel is filled with singing: slaves chant in the fields, family members sing after dinner, and love is expressed through song when spoken words fail. Music bridges social divides — the "fine voices of the negroes" mingle with the family's evening songs — and serves as the purest vehicle for joy, grief, and devotion in the hacienda world (005-020) (051-021).

## Definition

Music in *María* functions in several registers:

- **Work songs**: Slaves sing as they work, their chants blending with the sounds of the landscape — a form of expression that humanizes labor and connects it to the rhythms of nature.
- **Devotional music**: Religious chants and hymns accompany prayers and rituals, giving voice to collective faith.
- **Love songs**: Characters sing to express romantic feeling, with song serving as a socially acceptable vehicle for emotions that cannot be directly stated (051-021).
- **Community bonding**: Shared singing — whether at family gatherings or during communal work — reinforces social cohesion across class and racial lines (005-020).

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Slave chants**: The voices of enslaved workers singing as they return from the fields are described as beautiful and moving — Isaacs does not diminish their artistry (005-020).
- **Evening songs**: After dinner, family members sing together, a ritual of domestic harmony and cultural refinement.
- **María's singing**: María's voice is part of her idealized characterization — her singing is presented as angelic, a reflection of her pure soul.
- **Songs of the mountains**: The hunting expedition includes songs sung in the wilderness, connecting music to the untamed landscape.
- **The funeral song**: The "deep and plaintive" music at a death scene harmonizes with the grieving natural world (051-021).

## Key Passages

> ...the fine voices of the negroes struck into the chants with uncommon sweetness. (005-020)

> ...its deep and plaintive melody harmonized dolorously with the nature about us... (051-021)

## Literary Context

Music in *María* reflects the multicultural soundscape of 19th-century Colombia — African-derived slave chants, Spanish Catholic hymns, and European parlor music coexisting within the same household. Isaacs's attention to the aesthetic quality of slave music is notable for its time; he does not exoticize or diminish it but presents it as genuinely beautiful. This treatment aligns with the Romantic interest in folk culture while anticipating the *costumbrista* documentation of regional traditions.
