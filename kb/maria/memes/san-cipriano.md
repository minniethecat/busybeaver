San Cipriano is a settlement on the right bank of the Dagua River, reached by Efraín's traveling party in the afternoon during their journey toward Buenaventura (051-050). The boatmen navigate the river to arrive there, and the mail-carrier passes through, connecting the remote river communities to the wider world (052-016). (051-050)

## Role in the Novel

San Cipriano is a waypoint in the river-travel sequence that occupies a significant portion of the novel's second half. Like Juntas, it represents one of the small settlements that punctuate the arduous journey from the highlands to the Pacific coast (051-054). (051-054)

## Key Scenes

- The traveling party is due at San Cipriano in the afternoon, and the boatmen work to meet this schedule (051-050).
- Efraín's party arrives at San Cipriano, finding it on the right bank of the Dagua River (051-054).
- The mail-carrier passes through San Cipriano, and Efraín sends word ahead about his arrival time (052-016).

> We arrived at San Cipriano. On the right bank, and in the angle formed by the river that gives its name to the place, and the Dagua, was the house, raised on piles in the midst of a leafy banana orchard. We had not yet leaped out on the shore when Gregorio shouted: "'ña' Rufina! Here I come!"
>
> (051-054)
