# Bruno and Remigia's Wedding

The wedding of [[bruno|Bruno]] and [[remigia|Remigia]] is one of the most vividly rendered scenes in Jorge Isaacs's *María*, offering a rare extended depiction of [[slavery-and-servitude|enslaved]] communal life and celebration. Arranged by Efraín's father, the wedding provides a window into the culture, music, and social relations of the hacienda's enslaved population.

## The Arrangement

Efraín's father takes a personal interest in the marriage of his enslaved workers. Meeting a group of slaves returning from the fields, he addresses Bruno directly: "Well, Bruno, your wedding is all arranged for the day after tomorrow?" (005-007). The question, posed before the assembled group, makes the wedding a matter of public acknowledgment and patriarchal blessing.

Bruno is described as "a young negro of remarkably pleasant appearance" (005-007), singled out by the narrator's gaze in a way that individualizes him within the mass of enslaved workers. His pleasant appearance — noteworthy enough to merit mention — suggests the master's favorable regard.

## The Wedding Night Celebration

The wedding celebration takes place on a Saturday night. Efraín and his father ride to the event, hearing "the music from afar" (005-008) as they approach. The scene that greets them is described with ethnographic attention to detail.

[[julian|Julian]], "the slave captain of the gang" (005-008), greets them in his ceremonial attire: "his Sunday's best, and there hung from his belt the long knife with silver-plated sheath which was the sign of his office" (005-008). His role as greeter marks the occasion's formality within the enslaved community's own social order.

## The Ballroom

The celebration space is improvised from the materials of everyday life: "One large room of the old farmhouse had been cleared of its furniture for a ballroom. They had built a platform around it; in a wooden chandelier hanging from one of the beams a half-dozen candles were swinging" (005-008). The transformation of a work space into a festive one underscores the separation between labor and celebration.

## Music and Dance

The music at the wedding represents a fusion of African-derived traditions with the materials available to enslaved musicians: "only two reed flutes, an improvised drum, two rattles, and a timbrel" (005-008). Despite the simplicity of the instruments, the narrator is deeply moved by the performance.

Isaacs's description of the music is remarkable for its mixture of appreciation and condescension: "yet the fine voices of the negroes struck into the chants with such skill, there was in their songs such an affecting combination of melancholy and light and joyful chords, the verses they sang were so simple and tender, that the most cultivated ear would have listened with the highest pleasure to that half-savage music" (005-008). The phrase "half-savage music" betrays the racial ideology of the era, even as the narrator insists on the music's power to move a cultivated listener.

## The Bridal Couple

Remigia's wedding attire is described in detail: "a blue flounced skirt, a girdle red-flowered, a white chemise embroidered with black, and necklace and earrings of ruby-colored glass" (005-009). She dances "with all the ease and grace to be expected of her lithe figure" (005-009). Bruno wears "his woollen cloak folded about his shoulders, breeches of fine cotton cloth, a starched white shirt, and a new knife in his belt" (005-009), dancing "with admirable dexterity" (005-009).

The detailed description of their clothing — the colors, fabrics, and accessories — grants the enslaved couple a dignity and visibility rare in the novel's depictions of enslaved life.

## Significance

The wedding scene is significant for several reasons. It provides the novel's most extended depiction of enslaved culture and community. It reveals the paternalistic ideology of the landowning class, for whom arranging slave marriages was both an act of kindness and an investment in a stable workforce. And it captures, through its description of music and dance, the aesthetic and emotional life that flourished within the constraints of slavery.

The wedding celebration is rendered with rich detail:

> Remigia and Bruno were then dancing. She, with a blue flounced skirt, a girdle red-flowered, a white chemise embroidered with black, and necklace and earrings of ruby-colored glass, was dancing with all the ease and grace to be expected of her lithe figure. Bruno, with his woollen cloak folded about his shoulders, breeches of fine cotton cloth, a starched white shirt, and a new knife in his belt, was footing it with admirable dexterity.
>
> (005-020)
