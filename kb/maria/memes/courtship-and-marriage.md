# Courtship and Marriage in María

In Jorge Isaacs's *María*, marriage is never simply a private matter between two individuals. It is a social contract negotiated between families, contingent on economic stability, health, and paternal approval. The novel portrays multiple models of courtship — from the idealized romance of Efraín and María to the pragmatic arrangements of secondary characters — revealing the tension between individual desire and social expectation in 19th-century Colombian hacienda society (015-032) (015-039).

## Definition

Courtship and marriage in the world of *María* are governed by several principles:

- **Paternal authority**: Fathers control the timing and terms of marriage. Efraín's father imposes a five-year waiting period and makes the engagement conditional on María's health (015-032).
- **Medical consideration**: Marriageability is tied to physical health — María's epilepsy becomes the central obstacle to the union (015-039).
- **Economic foundation**: Marriage requires financial stability; Efraín must complete his medical education in Europe before he can marry.
- **Social display**: Courtship occurs within the supervised spaces of the hacienda — gardens, parlors, family gatherings — not in private.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Efraín and María's engagement**: The central courtship — formally negotiated between Efraín and his father, with María's health as the condition that ultimately prevents the marriage (016-001).
- **Carlos and his bride**: A secondary courtship plot that provides contrast — Carlos's courtship is more straightforward, highlighting by comparison the exceptional obstacles facing Efraín and María (018-029).
- **Tránsito's wedding**: The wedding of a secondary character shows courtship culminating successfully, a counterpoint to the main plot's tragedy (030-015).
- **Efraín's father's marriage story**: The father's own love story — falling in love on a ship and pursuing his bride — establishes the family pattern of passionate courtship.
- **The returned ring**: María's ring, meant to be returned on their wedding day, becomes a symbol of the marriage that never was (041-031).

## Key Passages

> ...they would allow us to think of your marriage; but they do not; and María is threatened... (015-032)

> I want you to know my judgment about this marriage, in case the disease continues... (015-039)

## Literary Context

Marriage in *María* reflects the patriarchal structure of 19th-century Colombian landed society, where unions among the elite were economic and social alliances as much as romantic bonds. Isaacs's portrayal is notable for its sympathy toward the lovers' desires while acknowledging the practical wisdom of paternal restraint. The failed marriage plot — where love is genuine but circumstances prevent union — is a hallmark of Romantic tragedy, akin to the frustrated unions in Goethe's *Werther* or the doomed loves of Italian opera.
