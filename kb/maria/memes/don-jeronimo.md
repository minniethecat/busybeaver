Don Jerónimo is a neighboring landowner in the Cauca Valley and the father of Carlos, one of Efraín's closest friends. He is a man of strong opinions, described as someone who "always talks as if he were trying to pick a quarrel" (021-023), yet beneath his gruff exterior he is a loyal friend and an enthusiastic hunter (018-032).

## Role in the Novel

As a supporting character, Don Jerónimo represents the landed gentry of the Cauca Valley — men who manage their estates, hunt, and participate in the social world of the region. His friendship with Efraín's father and his role as Carlos's father make him part of the network of relationships that surround the central love story (018-032). He is "a terrible man" according to his son — but one whose bark is worse than his bite.

## Relationships

- **[[carlos|Carlos]]** — Don Jerónimo's son and Efraín's close friend. Their father-son relationship appears in several scenes, including during the tiger hunt.
- **[[efrain|Efraín]]** — Don Jerónimo interacts with Efraín during visits and the tiger hunt, treating him with the rough familiarity of a family friend.
- **[[efrains-father|Efraín's Father]]** — A friend and fellow landowner, the two men discuss business and hunting together (021-039).

## Key Scenes

- Don Jerónimo examines a quartz sample brought by Efraín's father, reacting with theatrical disbelief and spilling his coffee (021-040). He leans on his hips and steps back in astonishment (021-042).
- At dinner, Don Jerónimo astonishes the company by making it through the meal without provoking an argument (021-046).
- He questions Efraín about the tiger hunt, almost calling him a liar about their success (021-054)(021-068).
- During the tiger hunt, Don Jerónimo sallies out with his rifle, taking an active role in the dangerous pursuit.
