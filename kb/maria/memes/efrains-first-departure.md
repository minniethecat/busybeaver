# Efraín's First Departure from Home

The novel's very first scene is a departure: young Efraín, still a boy, leaves the hacienda for school in Bogotá. The night before, his sister cuts a lock of his hair — 'that precaution of love against death, even in the presence of abounding life.' This gesture establishes the novel's governing theme: love and death are inseparable, and every parting carries the weight of possible permanent loss (001-001)(001-002).

## Role in the Novel

This departure differs from the later ones in its innocence — the boy does not yet know what he is leaving, or what he will return to find (001-003). The lock of hair, preserved against death, will acquire retrospective weight as the novel unfolds and death claims what love has cherished.

## Key Passages

> I was still a mere boy when sent away from home to study... (001-001)\n\n> That lock of hair taken from a boy's head; that precaution of love against death... (001-002)
