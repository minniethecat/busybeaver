# Slavery and Servitude in María

Slavery forms the economic and social foundation of the hacienda world depicted in Jorge Isaacs's *María*. The novel presents the enslaved population through the lens of the landowning family, revealing the complex, often contradictory attitudes toward slavery in mid-19th-century Colombia.

## The Enslaved Population on the Hacienda

Efraín's father has made substantial improvements to the estate during his son's absence in Bogotá, including "a fine and expensive sugar-mill, many acres of cane to supply it, large pastures with droves of cattle and horses in them, good stables, and an excellent house for the overseer" (003-003). This economic prosperity rests on enslaved labor.

The narrator describes the enslaved people in terms that reflect the patriarchal ideology of the planter class: "The slaves were well clad, and as happy as it is possible for slaves to be, and were docile and even affectionate towards their master" (005-002). The qualifier "as happy as it is possible for slaves to be" acknowledges, however obliquely, the inherent misery of their condition.


> It was easy to see that my father, without ceasing to be a master, treated his slaves with kindness. He was anxious for their domestic happiness, and fondled the little ones.
>
> (005-003)

## Pedro: The Faithful Slave

Pedro, the elderly enslaved man who told Efraín fairy stories in childhood, represents the archetype of the loyal servant. He "had shed tears when he placed me on my horse the day I set out for Bogotá, saying, 'Dear little master, I shall never see you again.' His heart told him that he would die before my return" (005-002). Efraín's father confirms Pedro's death upon the son's return.

Pedro also appears in Efraín's dreams: "I slept as I used to when a child, after being lulled to slumber by a fairy story told me by the slave Pedro. I dreamed that María came in to renew the flowers on my table" (004-001). The conflation of Pedro's storytelling with romantic dreams of [[maria|María]] reveals how deeply the enslaved presence is woven into the fabric of Efraín's emotional life.

## Bruno and Remigia: Marriage Under Slavery

The wedding of [[bruno|Bruno]] and [[remigia|Remigia]] provides the novel's most detailed depiction of enslaved life. Efraín's father personally arranges the marriage: "Well, Bruno, your wedding is all arranged for the day after tomorrow?" (005-007). The wedding celebration features music performed by "a mixture of slaves and freedmen" playing "only two reed flutes, an improvised drum, two rattles, and a timbrel" (005-008).

The narrator's description of the music reveals both appreciation and condescension: "yet the fine voices of the negroes struck into the chants with such skill, there was in their songs such an affecting combination of melancholy and light and joyful chords, the verses they sang were so simple and tender, that the most cultivated ear would have listened with the highest pleasure to that half-savage music" (005-008).

## Feliciana: Maternal Devotion

[[feliciana|Feliciana]], Juan Ángel's mother and Efraín's old nurse, represents the depth of enslaved women's suffering. When Efraín finds her dying, he "gave orders that more of the slaves should be in attendance upon her, and had her carried into a more comfortable room" (049-004). Her funeral rites draw the entire enslaved community together, and "a female slave intoned the first stanza of one of those chants filled with grieving melancholy and irrepressible laments so familiar to the hearts of slaves" (057-003).

The funeral scene is one of the novel's most powerful depictions of enslaved solidarity and shared grief. The chorus of men's "deep voices" harmonizing with "the clear and sweet tones of the women and boys" (057-003) creates a moment of communal expression that transcends the constraints of their condition.

## Julian and the Slave Hierarchy

[[julian|Julian]], "the slave captain of the gang" (005-008), occupies an intermediate position in the hacienda's hierarchy. He wears "the long knife with silver-plated sheath which was the sign of his office" (005-008), marking him as both enslaved and entrusted with authority over other enslaved people. This internal stratification reflects the complex social order that slavery produced.

## The Master's Benevolence

Efraín's father embodies the ideology of the benevolent master: "without ceasing to be a master, treated his slaves with kindness. He was anxious for their domestic happiness, and fondled the little ones" (005-003). The novel presents this paternalism uncritically, yet the structural violence of slavery — the denial of freedom, the separation of families, the ownership of human beings — remains the unexamined foundation of the world it depicts.
