# Dagua River

The Dagua River flows through the landscape of [[efrain|Efraín]]'s childhood and appears at significant moments in the narrative. Like the [[zabaletas-river|Zabaletas]], it is part of the fluvial network that defines the Cauca Valley's geography and that Isaacs renders with cartographic precision. The river acquires an almost personal presence in the novel: "To the Dagua nothing is impossible" (052-002), and characters speak of it as if it had a temper — "Dagua has a very bad temper" (052-014).

## Role in the Novel

Rivers in the novel mark boundaries and transitions — the Dagua appears at moments of departure, its murmur a natural accompaniment to human emotion. As Efraín is carried away by its swift current, "my heart had said goodbye to so many things" (050-002). The river's waters are watched flowing "blue, green, and gold" (051-072), and the Falls of Arrayan feed into it below the village of Juntas (052-003)(052-008). Its presence reinforces the novel's deep integration of landscape and feeling.

## Key Passages

The Dagua River marks a moment of departure and melancholy:

> We were about two hundred yards from the beach, but I thought I could distinguish the collector's white bulk, motionless, in the spot where he had taken leave of me. The yellowish gleam of the moon, sometimes hidden, always funereal, lighted us until after we had entered the mouth of the Dagua.
>
> (051-010)

A house on the banks of the Dagua serves as a way-station:

> ...and the Dagua, was the house, raised on pillars of iron-wood, of the inspector of the road repairs. (051-054)
