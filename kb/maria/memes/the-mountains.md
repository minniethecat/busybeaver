# The Mountains

The mountains surrounding the [[cauca-valley|Cauca Valley]] represent nature in its wild, sublime aspect — a counterpoint to the cultivated beauty of the hacienda [[garden-and-flowers|garden]] (002-001). It is in the mountains that the [[hunting-expedition|tiger hunt]] takes place, and it is in the mountains that characters encounter a nature that is indifferent rather than nurturing (030-003). The mists cloaking the mountains during the night (002-001) and the naked summits against the starry sky (003-001) establish the range as a constant visual and emotional presence.

## Role in the Novel

The mountains embody the Romantic sublime — nature as overwhelming, dangerous, and awe-inspiring (009-001). They are the space of masculine adventure (the hunt) as opposed to the feminized space of the garden, and they remind the characters that beyond the hacienda's ordered world lies an untamed vastness. Efraín's ascent into the mountains with his father (008-002) and his solitary climb past "trees thrown down by the last mountain-slides" (009-005) trace a movement from the domestic to the wild that mirrors his emotional journey.

## Key Passages

> ...the world, nature, as Adam might have seen it on the first morning of creation... (030-003)

> The mountain was fresh and tremulous under the first light of day (009-001)
