# José

José is a hunter and worker on Efraín's family hacienda, representing the capable rural masculinity that complements the novel's more refined male characters. He first appears alongside his nephew Braulio, coming to find Efraín to prepare for the tiger hunt — from the outset, José is defined by action and practical purpose (020-002). He leads the tiger hunt — the novel's most extended action sequence — and his skill with horses and weapons establishes a standard of competence against which Efraín's more bookish qualities are measured. José's role, while secondary, is essential: he grounds the novel in the practical realities of hacienda life (030-015).

## Role in the Novel

José's primary function is to lead the hunting expedition into the mountains. In this role, he embodies practical knowledge — tracking, horsemanship, courage under pressure — that the educated Efraín lacks. The eagerness with which José and Braulio anticipate the hunt reveals their deep connection to the land and its rhythms (020-034). His courtship and marriage to Tránsito provides a secondary love plot, contrasting with the central romance: José and Tránsito's union is straightforward, uncomplicated by illness or parental objection, suggesting that love need not always be tragic.

## Relationships

- **[[efrain|Efraín]]** — The young master whom José guides on the hunt; their relationship is one of mutual respect across class lines.
- **[[transito|Tránsito]]** — His beloved and later wife; their successful courtship contrasts with Efraín and María's tragedy.
- **[[efrains-father|Efraín's Father]]** — His employer, who trusts José's competence.

## Key Scenes

- **The tiger hunt**: José leads the expedition, demonstrating the skills — tracking, courage, weaponry — that define his character (030-003).
- **The wedding announcement**: The community learns of José and Tránsito's impending marriage, a moment of uncomplicated joy (030-015).

## Character Analysis

José represents a masculine ideal different from Efraín's Romantic sensibility: practical, competent, physically courageous, and emotionally straightforward. His successful courtship of Tránsito — uncomplicated by illness, education, or parental interference — suggests that happiness is possible for those who live simply. Isaacs treats José with respect, avoiding the condescension that a lesser novelist might have brought to a working-class character.

> We tried to find out when the marriage was to be. José, to relieve his daughter's embarrassment, replied: "We want it to be a week from today." (030-015)
