# Return to the Cemetery

The novel ends at María's grave. [[efrain|Efraín]], returned from Europe, makes his way to the cemetery where she is buried, passing by the spot where [[juan-angels-death|Juan Ángel]] was laid to rest (059-001). [[braulio|Braulio]] takes Efraín's horse at the cemetery gate (059-006), and the mourner is left alone with the grave. His monologue — part prayer, part lament, part accusation — is the novel's emotional and rhetorical climax. The grave gives no answer: 'that cold and deaf grave... gave a reply all too eloquent.' The novel that began with departure ends with arrival at the one place from which there is no further journey (059-007).

## Role in the Novel

The cemetery scene completes the novel's elegiac structure. The entire narrative, we now understand, has been leading to this moment — Efraín's act of remembrance is the novel itself. The grave is the point at which memory, love, and death converge, and Efraín's silence before it ('my lips had not murmured a prayer over her') suggests that grief has overwhelmed even the consolations of faith. José's burial (038-011) prefigures this scene, establishing the cemetery as the novel's terminal space.

## Key Passages

> To that terrible monologue of the soul in the presence of death — of the soul which questions, which curses, which begs, which cries out — that cold and deaf grave, which my arms were embracing and my tears bathing, gave a reply all too eloquent. (059-007)

> I determined to leave the city the next morning, intending to pass by the cemetery where was María's grave. (059-001)
