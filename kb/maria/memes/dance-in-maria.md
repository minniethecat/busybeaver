Dance appears in several forms throughout the novel, from the couplets and songs of the river boatmen to the social dances and celebrations of the hacienda. Cortico and Laurean sing call-and-response couplets while navigating the Dagua River, their voices carrying over the water in a rhythmic exchange that evokes the musical traditions of the Afro-Colombian Pacific lowlands (051-020). Dance and music are woven into the fabric of daily life, from work songs to religious ceremonies to moments of celebration. (005-015)

## Role in the Novel

Dance and music represent the cultural richness of the Cauca Valley and Pacific regions. While the novel's main narrative focuses on the romantic tragedy of Efraín and María, the musical traditions of the workers and river communities provide a counterpoint — a world of embodied joy and communal expression that exists alongside the refined sensibilities of the hacienda family. (005-020)

## Key Scenes

- Cortico and Laurean trade couplets in call-and-response while navigating the Dagua River (051-020).
- The river workers sing as they travel, their music filling the tropical landscape.
- The hacienda workers celebrate with music and dance during moments of communal gathering.

> That dance over, the musicians struck up their best tune, for Julian told them it was to be for the master. Remigia, urged on by her husband and by the captain, at last agreed to dance a few moments with my father; but she dared not lift her eyes while she was doing it, and her dancing was rather constrained.
>
> (005-021)
