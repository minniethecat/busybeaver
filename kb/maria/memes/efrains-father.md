# Efraín's Father

Efraín's father is the unnamed patriarch whose decisions determine the fate of every character in *María*. A former soldier who fought in the wars of independence, he has transformed himself into a landed gentleman — managing the hacienda with a combination of military discipline and paternalistic care. His authority is absolute and benevolent, but his insistence on a five-year waiting period before Efraín and María can marry — and his requirement that Efraín complete medical studies in Europe — sets in motion the tragedy that the novel recounts (005-003) (015-032).

## Role in the Novel

The father serves as the novel's embodiment of practical reason and social authority. He is not a villain — he genuinely loves both Efraín and María — but his decisions are governed by prudence rather than passion. His own romantic history (he fell in love with a ship captain's daughter and pursued her determinedly) suggests he understands love, but his experience has taught him that love must be tempered by caution. The five-year delay he imposes is meant to protect both young people; instead, it ensures that María will die before the marriage can take place (007-001) (015-038).

## Relationships

- **[[efrain|Efraín]]** — His son, whose education and marriage he controls with patriarchal authority. He loves Efraín but subordinates emotion to practical judgment.
- **[[maria|María]]** — His ward, whose health he monitors with paternal concern. His judgment that her epilepsy makes immediate marriage dangerous is medically sound but tragically timed.
- **[[efrains-mother|Efraín's Mother]]** — His wife, who shares his authority in the domestic sphere.
- **[[salomon|Salomón]]** — His deceased friend, whose daughter (María) he has raised as his own.

## Key Scenes

- **The marriage negotiation**: The father explains to Efraín why marriage must wait five years — a scene of rational paternal authority that is also, in retrospect, the moment tragedy becomes inevitable (015-032).
- **The estate tour**: He guides Efraín through the hacienda improvements made during his absence — a ritual assertion of patriarchal domain (005-003).
- **His own love story**: He recounts falling in love with Efraín's mother on a ship — revealing that he was once as passionate as his son (007-001).

## Character Analysis

The father is the novel's most complex secondary character. He is neither tyrant nor sentimentalist — his decisions are reasonable, even wise, but they are made without full knowledge of the future. Isaacs treats him with respect, and his own romantic past humanizes him. In the hacienda's social order, he represents the best case of patriarchal authority: benevolent, competent, and genuinely invested in others' welfare. The tragedy is not that he is cruel but that even benevolent authority, when it controls others' fates, can produce devastating outcomes.

> It was easy to see that my father, without ceasing to be a master, treated his slaves with kindness. (005-003)
