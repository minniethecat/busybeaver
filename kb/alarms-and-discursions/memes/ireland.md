## Overview

Ireland is an island in the North Atlantic, separated from Great Britain by the Irish Sea. In Chesterton's essays, Ireland appears in two fleeting references, neither of which constitutes a sustained treatment. Yet each reference is revealing in its own way: one captures the complacent imperialism of the Edwardian press, the other frames Ireland as a climatic foil in Chesterton's rhapsody on English weather.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Ireland first appears in "The Man and His Newspaper" (essay 6). Chesterton, walking in the country, has just bought a copy of the *Daily Wire* and reads it aloud with theatrical gravity. The newspaper's rhetoric is a cascade of imperial complacency:

> (006-003) It opened with the striking phrase that the Radicals were setting class against class. It went on to remark that nothing had contributed more to make our Empire happy and enviable … the prosperity of all classes in our great cities, our populous and growing villages, the success of our rule in Ireland, etc., etc., than the sound Anglo-Saxon readiness of all classes in the State "to work heartily hand-in-hand."

The phrase "the success of our rule in Ireland" is, in Chesterton's mouth, bitterly ironic. He is quoting the newspaper not to endorse but to expose — the essay is a sustained satire of the way newspapers manufacture a false consensus. Ireland here is the silent subject of imperial boasting, its actual condition elided by the smooth phrase "success of our rule."

The second reference comes in "The Glory of Grey" (essay 18), where Chesterton mounts his famous defense of English weather. To establish England's climatic uniqueness, he contrasts it with everywhere else:

> (018-001) In France you have much sun and some rain; in Italy you have hot winds and cold winds; in Scotland and Ireland you have rain, either thick or thin; in America you have hells of heat and cold, and in the Tropics you have sunstrokes varied by thunderbolts. But all these you have on a broad and brutal scale, and you settle down into contentment or despair. Only in our own romantic country do you have the strictly romantic thing called Weather.

Ireland's rain — "either thick or thin" — is dispatched in a clause. It is not a description of Ireland but a dismissal: Irish weather, like all non-English weather, is merely meteorological, lacking the "strictly romantic" quality of English Weather with a capital W. The condescension is characteristic and, to modern ears, uncomfortable; but it serves Chesterton's larger argument that English landscape painting (Constable, Turner) made Weather itself the subject of art.

## Historical Significance

When Chesterton wrote in 1910, Ireland was still part of the United Kingdom, though the Home Rule movement was vigorously contesting that status. The "success of our rule in Ireland" that Chesterton's newspaper trumpeted was a highly contested claim: the land wars of the 1880s, the fall of Parnell, and the ongoing Home Rule debates made Ireland one of the most divisive political questions of the era. Chesterton's ironic quotation of the phrase suggests he was well aware of this.

## Chesterton's Connection

Chesterton was part Irish by ancestry on his mother's side, though he rarely emphasized this. His scattered references to Ireland in the essays are political rather than personal: Ireland appears as a test case for imperial rhetoric and as a meteorological afterthought. He was a supporter of Irish Home Rule, aligned in this with his friend [[Hilaire Belloc]] and the broader Distributist circle.
