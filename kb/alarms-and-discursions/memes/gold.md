# Gold

**Gold** appears in Chesterton's descriptive palette as a colour of splendour and legend. He writes of the gargoyle that "fell on the head and he lost his memory" — and with it "gold" (001-009), invokes "the gold of Glastonbury" as a legendary treasure of English myth (015-005), and notes gold among "the hues and colours of every varied dawn and eve" (018-002).

## In Chesterton's Essays

Gold in Chesterton carries a double register: it is both the metal of commerce and the colour of vision. In "The Surrender of a Cockney," he inverts the proverb: "the streets of London are paved with gold; and I mean to see it before I die" (002-004). The jest turns the mercenary cliché into a declaration of wonder at the ordinary, where gold is not wealth but radiance.

> I believe that the streets of London are paved with gold; and I mean to see it before I die.
>
> (002-004)

In "The Gold of Glastonbury," the legendary treasure becomes a figure for the spiritual claim of the local over the universal (015-011). A "faint brush of gold" on the horizon marks the difference between a place that is merely inhabited and one that is hallowed by story. Against modern ugliness — "Modern things are ugly" (004-002) — Chesterton sets the gold of craftsmanship and myth.
