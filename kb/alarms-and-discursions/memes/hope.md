# Hope

**Hope** appears in Chesterton's essays as a theological virtue and a disposition of the soul. He describes "the clear hope" in a sky of "white marble clouds and sapphire" (001-007), portrays a Cockney who "had no hope" when confronted with the alien countryside (002-002), and links hope to the "straightness of the soul" against which ugliness is measured (003-004).

## In Chesterton's Essays

Hope is for Chesterton not optimism about outcomes but an orientation of the will. In "A Drama of Dolls," he writes that where dolls are present "there is a little health and hope; wherever they are absent, pride and a incurable paralysis" (005-003). The doll, a fragment of imaginative play, becomes the unlikely vessel of hope precisely because it belongs to the realm of the gratuitous.

In "The Nightmare," Chesterton ties hope to moral geometry: the "straightness of the soul" is the standard against which all crookedness is measured (003-004). Despair is not a failure of circumstances but a curvature of the will. Hope is the refusal to accept that the world as given is the world as final — a conviction Chesterton finds confirmed in every child's plaything and every Gothic spire.
