## Overview

The devil and the demonic appear in *Alarms and Discursions* in three distinct contexts: as a figure in the medieval imagination, as a presence in folk belief, and as a metaphor for the spiritual stakes of ordinary life. Chesterton's treatment is characteristically sympathetic to the pre-modern understanding of evil as personal and active.

## In Chesterton's Essays

In "The Nightmare" (Essay 3), Chesterton writes that "there is (literally) the devil to pay — to pay in dancing girls" (003-004), using the idiom to suggest that spiritual debts are real and exacting. In "A Drama of Dolls" (Essay 5), he describes the medieval worldview as "a dance of death, full of devils and deadly sins" (005-002), but immediately clarifies that this was "not the life of the Middle Ages, but the death of the Middle Ages." The devils of medieval art, he suggests, are evidence not of morbid obsession but of a healthy recognition of spiritual danger. A third reference appears in the same essay: a folk belief that "the devils are more afraid of her than she is of them" (005-006).

## Significance

For Chesterton, belief in the devil is not a mark of primitivism but of spiritual realism. The medieval imagination, far from being morbid, was healthily aware that evil is personal, active, and dangerous — an awareness that modern rationalism has lost to its detriment.

## From the Essays


> It is when one of them goes wandering in deserts and finds a throne for himself that evil faiths begin, and there is (literally) the devil to pay--to pay in dancing girls or human sacrifice. As long as those misshapen elemental powers are around the throne, remember that the thing that they worship is the likeness of the appearance of a man.
>
> (003-004)
