# Glastonbury

## Overview

Glastonbury is a small town in Somerset, England, known for its ruined medieval abbey, the legendary burial place of King Arthur, and the Glastonbury Thorn — a hawthorn said to have sprouted from the staff of Joseph of Arimathea. In Chesterton's essay "The Gold of Glastonbury," the town becomes a vehicle for one of his most luminous meditations on myth, history, and the living quality of sacred places.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton opens "The Gold of Glastonbury" with a piercingly beautiful image: "One silver morning I walked into a small grey town of stone, like twenty other grey western towns, which happened to be called Glastonbury; and saw the magic thorn of near two thousand years growing in the open air as casually as any bush in my garden" (015-001). The juxtaposition — "magic thorn" and "any bush" — is pure Chesterton: the miraculous is ordinary, the ordinary is miraculous.

He immediately states his thesis: "In Glastonbury, as in all noble and humane things, the myth is more important than the history" (015-001). This is not a dismissal of truth but an elevation of meaning. Whether Joseph of Arimathea actually came to Glastonbury is a question that "it is now quite impossible to find out; and it does not, in a religious sense, very much matter" (015-009). What matters is that "he may have gone to Glastonbury" — that "all songs, arts, and dedications branching and blossoming like the thorn, are rooted in some such sacred doubt" (015-009).

The essay closes with a vision of two Glastonburys: the dead Glastonbury of the ruined abbey, appreciated by tourists with guidebooks, and "the living Glastonbury, gay with gold and coloured like the toy-book of a child" (015-011). Chesterton stands for an instant in both — but it is the living Glastonbury he chooses.

In "The Glory of Grey," Chesterton returns to the image of Glastonbury's stone, contrasting "the warm grey stone" of the western towns with "the painted flames of Croydon" (018-004). The grey is not dreary but genial — "as if it were better to warm one's hands at the ashes of Glastonbury than at the painted flames of Croydon."

## Historical Significance

Glastonbury has been a site of religious pilgrimage since at least the 7th century. The medieval abbey, dissolved by Henry VIII in 1539, was among the wealthiest in England. The town's association with Arthurian legend — as the Isle of Avalon — and with Joseph of Arimathea makes it unique in English mythology. The Glastonbury Thorn, which flowers twice yearly (at Christmas and Easter), is a living link to these traditions.

## Chesterton's Connection

Chesterton's treatment of Glastonbury reveals his deepest convictions about the relationship between place and belief. He is not interested in the archaeological question — did Joseph come here? — but in the imaginative one: what does it mean that a people has *believed* he came here? The ruined abbey is not a monument to dead faith but evidence of a faith so alive that it built in stone and planted thorns that still bloom.
