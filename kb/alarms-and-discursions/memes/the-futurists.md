# The Futurists

## Overview

The Futurists were an Italian avant-garde movement founded in 1909 by the poet Filippo Tommaso Marinetti. Their *Futurist Manifesto*, published on the front page of the Paris newspaper *Le Figaro* in February 1909, declared war on the past — museums, libraries, academies — and celebrated speed, machinery, violence, youth, and the modern city. For Chesterton, encountering this movement in real time, the Futurists represented everything wrong with the modern worship of novelty.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton's essay "The Futurists" (essay 16) is one of the longest and most sustained satires in *Alarms and Discursions*. He opens with cheerful ignorance: "If you ask me what Futurism is, I cannot tell you; even the Futurists themselves seem a little doubtful; perhaps they are waiting for the future to find out" (016-001). The joke is double-edged — the Futurists may not know what they are, but Chesterton is about to tell them.

He quotes the Manifesto directly, including its eleventh article: "Literature having up to now glorified thoughtful immobility, ecstasy, and slumber, we wish to exalt the aggressive movement, the feverish insomnia, running, the perilous leap, the cuff and the blow" (016-003). Chesterton's response is devastating in its mildness: "While I am quite willing to exalt the cuff within reason, it scarcely seems such an entirely new subject for literature."

The critique sharpens when Chesterton observes the social reality of Futurism: "The distinction, however, seems to be that the warriors of the past went in for tournaments, which were at least dangerous for themselves, while the Futurists go in for motor-cars, which are mainly alarming for other people" (016-003). The Futurist in his motor performs the "aggressive movement," but "it is the pedestrians who go in for the 'running' and the 'perilous leap.'"

Chesterton's most incisive observation is economic: "It is quite clear, however, that you cannot be a Futurist at all unless you are frightfully rich" (016-003). This cuts to the heart of the matter: a movement that claims to celebrate the future of all mankind is in practice an indulgence of the wealthy.

The essay ends with a final thrust at the emptiness of the Futurist posture: "But a weak-minded man who does not know what he wants in the future calls himself a Futurist" (016-009).

## History and Context

The Futurist movement burst onto the European cultural scene in 1909 and remained influential through the 1910s, expanding into painting (Boccioni, Balla, Severini), music (Russolo's *The Art of Noises*), and architecture (Sant'Elia). Its embrace of violence and war contributed to the climate that produced the First World War, and after the war many Futurists aligned with Mussolini's Fascism.

Chesterton was writing in 1910, when Futurism was at its maximum notoriety. Marinetti had visited London and lectured to applause and outrage in equal measure. Chesterton's essay is thus not an abstract critique but an engagement with a living controversy.

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton's critique of Futurism operates on multiple levels. The most obvious is satirical — he finds the manifestos ridiculous and says so. But beneath the humour, his argument is serious and consistent with his broader philosophy.

First, Futurism mistakes novelty for value. Wanting "the future" without knowing what you want in it is not a programme but an evasion. Second, Futurism's cult of speed and violence is ethically hollow — it endangers pedestrians while claiming to be heroic. Third, and most importantly, Futurism is a philosophy of ingratitude: it hates the past without understanding it, and in doing so, impoverishes the future it claims to serve.

This critique is the mirror image of Chesterton's praise for tradition and democracy. The Futurist is the anti-Cockney — the man who burns his inheritance because he doesn't know what it's worth.
