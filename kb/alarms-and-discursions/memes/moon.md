The moon appears throughout *Alarms and Discursions* as one of Chesterton's favored images for wonder, imagination, and the poetry of everyday perception. It serves variously as a symbol of folklore, dream, and the cosmic perspective that Chesterton believed modernity had lost.

## Folklore and Wonder

The most sustained treatment of the moon occurs in "The Appetite of Earth" (essay 07), where Chesterton plays with the old proverb that the moon is made of green cheese. He notes that a "rural appetite" once declared this (007-003) and reflects that the moon is perhaps not old enough — that the phrase about the moon and green cheese remains a good example of how folk wisdom preserves imaginative truths that scientific literalism cannot reach (007-004, 007-005). For Chesterton, the green-cheese moon is not an error to be corrected but a symbol of the human capacity to see the cosmos as nourishment for the soul.

## Cosmic Imagery and Perspective

In "The Nightmare" (essay 03), Chesterton evokes the dreamlike wish to fly over the moon "like that wild amorous cow" (003-005), linking lunar imagery to the liberated imagination of dreams. In "The Wings of Stone" (essay 22), he suggests it would be grander to fire a cannonball at the moon than merely to calculate its trajectory (022-003) — the poetic gesture matters more than the scientific measurement. Moonlight, too, serves as a transformative medium: visiting the Coliseum by moonlight struck Chesterton as a quintessentially romantic act (012-006).
