## Overview

The Daily News was a British daily newspaper founded in 1846 by [[Charles Dickens]], who also served as its first editor. By Chesterton's time it had become a leading Liberal paper, known for its literary coverage and its opposition to the Boer War. Chesterton contributed reviews to the Daily News, and two passing references in *Alarms and Discursions* testify to its role in his working life: it was the paper that paid him to read poems and the paper whose existence, he notes with characteristic perversity, depends on the very "doubtful things" that secularists dismiss.

## In Chesterton's Essays

The Daily News appears incidentally rather than as a subject, but each reference illuminates Chesterton's professional world. In "The Nightmare" (essay 3), he mentions it in the course of describing a decadent poem he had been reading:

> (003-001) I read this poem because I had to review it for the Daily News; still it was genuine poetry of its kind.

The sentence is a miniature Chesterton essay: the professional obligation ("had to review it") does not prevent the aesthetic judgment ("genuine poetry of its kind"). The Daily News here is simply the backdrop — the daily reality of a working literary journalist in Edwardian London.

The second reference comes in "Ethandune" (essay 30), where Chesterton is meditating on the historical importance of King Alfred — a figure whose very existence depends on debatable medieval sources:

> (030-003) And yet but for this doubtful thing you would have probably no Daily News on Saturday and certainly no church on Sunday.

The argument is characteristically Chestertonian: the "doubtful thing" (Alfred's victory at Ethandune) preserved Christian England, which in turn produced the civilization that includes both Saturday newspapers and Sunday church services. The Daily News stands here for the ordinary texture of modern English life — a thing so taken for granted that we forget the long chain of improbable events that made it possible.

## Historical Significance

The Daily News was founded by Dickens as a Liberal alternative to the *Morning Chronicle*. Under editors like John Forster and later H.W. Massingham, it became known for its literary quality and its progressive politics. It opposed the Crimean War, supported Italian unification, and was one of the few British papers to oppose the Boer War (1899–1902). Chesterton's association with the paper placed him in a tradition of Liberal journalism that valued literary excellence alongside political commitment. The paper merged with the *Morning Post* in 1930 and eventually ceased publication.

## Chesterton's Connection

Chesterton wrote regularly for the Daily News from approximately 1901 to 1913, contributing literary reviews and occasional essays. His relationship with the paper was professional rather than ideological — he was a Liberal in this period but his views were already diverging from party orthodoxy. The Daily News references in *Alarms and Discursions* are unguarded glimpses of his daily routine: the reviews were work, the poems were sometimes bad, but the life of a working journalist was, for Chesterton, a good life.
