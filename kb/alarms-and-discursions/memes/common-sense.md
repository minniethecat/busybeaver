# Common Sense

## Overview

Common sense in Chesterton's *Alarms and Discursions* is a philosophical virtue, not an intellectual failing. It names the shared rationality of ordinary people — the kind of thinking that sees through sophistry, distrusts abstraction, and anchors judgement in lived experience. For Chesterton, common sense is democratic, English in a distinctive and limited sense, and ultimately a moral rather than merely cognitive capacity.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Common sense appears most prominently in two political essays. In "The Man and His Newspaper," Chesterton contrasts the English people's sturdy judgement with the machinations of the press. He ventures a claim about English character: "the English people, with their sturdy common sense, will prefer to be in the hands of a gentleman" (006-003). The remark cuts both ways — it is partly a genuine tribute to democratic instinct and partly an ironic jab at English deference. His later remark sharpens the irony: "'The English people 'ave some common sense, and they'd rather be in the 'ands of their employers'" (006-007), where the dropped aitches signal that the appeal to common sense can be a tool of manipulation.

In "The Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds," Chesterton develops the theme more constructively. He admires the English constitutional arrangement whereby "with a shrewd, illogical old English common sense they have carefully built the House of Lords, and then carefully unbuilt it" (024-002) — a piecemeal, unsystematic approach that offends rationalist sensibilities but works in practice. This is Chesterton's Burkean side: common sense as the accumulated wisdom of institutions, resistant to blueprints. He extends the logic: "If the same common sense were applied to commercial law, I think something might be done with the commercial law" (024-005) — common sense is not only a conservative virtue but a reforming one, the basis for correcting injustices that theory would perpetuate.

In "Five Hundred and Fifty-five," common sense appears in a more surprising context: the psychology of numbers. Chesterton observes that a specific number, even if arbitrary, can supply "an invisible reinforcement of common courage and common sense" (029-002). The mere fact that five hundred and fifty-five people voted for something emboldens individual action. This is common sense as social phenomenon — not what one person thinks in isolation but what a community thinks together, the emergent wisdom of collective deliberation.

Chesterton returns to the theme in "The Strangeness of Luxury," arguing that "the broad bird's-eye view of common sense there abides a huge disproportion between the modern ideal of living in a large hotel and the modern ideal of living in a small house" (026-003). Common sense here functions as a corrective to fashion: it notices the obvious fact that a small house is more human than a large hotel, a fact that sophistication has trained people to overlook.

## Historical Context

Common sense philosophy has deep roots in the Scottish Enlightenment (Thomas Reid and the "Scottish School of Common Sense"), which argued against Humean scepticism that certain truths are self-evident to any sane person. Chesterton, though English and Catholic, inherits this tradition and democratises it further. For Reid, common sense was a philosophical doctrine; for Chesterton, it is the property of the charwoman on the train as much as the professor in his study. The Edwardian context gave the theme urgency: Fabian socialism, eugenics, and scientific management all threatened, in Chesterton's view, to replace the wisdom of ordinary people with the expertise of elites.

## Chesterton's Position

Common sense, for Chesterton, is not the lowest common denominator of thought but the highest common achievement — the shared rationality that makes democratic life possible. It is "common" not in the sense of vulgar but in the sense of *shared*, the property of a community rather than an individual. When Chesterton praises English common sense, he is praising something he believes the English have preserved better than most: the habit of trusting experience over ideology. But his praise is always qualified by irony — he knows that appeals to common sense can be lazy, self-congratulatory, and a cover for vested interests. True common sense is active, sceptical of its own conclusions, and perpetually open to the surprising evidence of things.


## From the Essays

The only difference is that things are now judged by the abnormal needs, when they might be judged merely by the normal needs.
>
> (026-003)
