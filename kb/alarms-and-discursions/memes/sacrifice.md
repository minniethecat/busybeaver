Sacrifice is a recurring theme in Chesterton's moral and theological thinking. He presents it not as mere loss but as the essential act that defines moral agency — the capacity of a being to give itself for something beyond itself.

## In Chesterton's Moral Theology

Chesterton articulates the centrality of sacrifice most directly in "The Field of Blood" (essay 25), where he argues that sacrifice is what distinguishes persons from mere instruments. A being that can sin and that can sacrifice itself possesses moral dignity (025-003). The corn may be destroyed as a sacrifice to some god, but whenever a person is destroyed, it must be for something greater than utility (025-005). This theological anthropology undergirds Chesterton's critique of both utilitarianism and militarism.

## Sacrifice and Principle

In "The Modern Scrooge" (essay 36), Chesterton addresses the concept of sacrificing principle — the modern tendency to treat moral conviction as a negotiable commodity (036-001). For Chesterton, the willingness to sacrifice comfort, reputation, or even life for principle is not fanaticism but the logical extension of having principles at all. This connects to his broader social criticism: a society that mocks self-sacrifice has lost the vocabulary of moral seriousness.

## Key Passages

> A people is a soul; and if you want to know what a soul is, I can only answer that it is something that can sin and that can sacrifice itself.
>
> (025-003)

> But whenever there is sacrifice we know there is a single will. Men may be disputatious and doubtful, may divide by very narrow majorities in their debate about how to gain wealth. But men have to be uncommonly unanimous in order to refuse wealth. This self-denial is the test and definition of self-government.
>
> (025-005)
