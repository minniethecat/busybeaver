## Biography

Charlotte Corday (1768–1793) was a figure of the French Revolution who assassinated the radical journalist and politician Jean-Paul Marat in his bathtub on 13 July 1793. A sympathiser of the moderate Girondin faction, she believed that eliminating Marat would halt the escalating Terror. She was executed by guillotine four days after the assassination, aged twenty-four. Her act and her composure at trial made her one of the Revolution's most studied and mythologised figures.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Charlotte Corday appears in essay 13, "A Criminal Head," as Chesterton's Exhibit A in his demolition of criminal anthropology. The context is a broader argument about the pretensions of scientific criminology: the discipline that claims to identify "criminal types" by measuring skulls is, Chesterton argues, not scientific at all — it merely projects fashionable prejudices onto physical measurements.

Chesterton recounts how "some famous and foolish professor measured the skull of Charlotte Corday to ascertain the criminal type" (013-003). The irony is double. First, the professor "had not historical knowledge enough to know that if there is any 'criminal type,' certainly Charlotte Corday had not got it" (013-003). Corday's act was political assassination, not common criminality — to classify her alongside ordinary murderers is to reveal a catastrophic ignorance of historical context.

Second, Chesterton adds with dry amusement, "The skull, I believe, afterwards turned out not to be Charlotte Corday's at all; but that is another story" (013-003). The detail is devastating: the entire scientific exercise was not only conceptually wrong but factually baseless. The professor had been measuring the wrong skull.

The Corday episode serves Chesterton's larger argument about the relationship between physical facts and moral judgement. A man may know everything about skulls and nothing about people. "The trouble with most sociologists, criminologists, etc., is that while their knowledge of their own details is exhaustive and subtle, their knowledge of man and society, to which these are to be applied, is quite exceptionally superficial and silly" (013-003).

## Key Works / Contributions

Corday's single historical act — the assassination of Marat — has generated an enduring cultural afterlife. She was painted by Jacques-Louis David (the famous *Death of Marat*), dramatised by numerous playwrights, and became a symbol of individual conscience against mass terror. Her calm dignity at trial and execution contributed to her mythologisation as a republican martyr.

In Chesterton's essay, she appears alongside [[Maximilien Robespierre]] as a test case for the limits of scientific criminology. Both figures are revolutionaries whose actions cannot be understood through skull measurement but only through historical and moral imagination.

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton's use of Corday is illustrative rather than evaluative. He does not pass judgement on her act — whether Marat's assassination was justified — because that is not his point. His point is methodological: you cannot understand Charlotte Corday by measuring her (alleged) skull. You must understand her ideas, her context, her motives — the human reality that skulls do not record.

Chesterton's Corday is therefore a demonstration of the necessity of the humanities. The criminologist who thinks he can bypass history, biography, and moral philosophy is not being scientific; he is being lazy. The Corday case exposes that laziness with perfect comic irony: the scientist who measured the wrong skull while making claims about the right one.
