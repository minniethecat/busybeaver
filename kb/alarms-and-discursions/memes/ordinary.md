## Overview

"The ordinary" is one of Chesterton's master concepts, though it rarely appears as an explicit theme under that name. In *Alarms and Discursions*, the word appears in contexts that reveal his characteristic inversion: the ordinary is not uninteresting; it is so interesting that we have grown numb to it.

## In Chesterton's Essays

In "The Surrender of a Cockney" (Essay 2), Chesterton argues that "trees and fields ought to be the ordinary things; terraces and temples ought to be the extraordinary" (002-005). The modern inversion — in which we take nature for granted and marvel at architecture — is, for Chesterton, a symptom of urban alienation. In "The Appetite of Earth" (Essay 7), a cheese appears as "an ordinary sensible Cheddar cheese" (007-004), only to become, on closer inspection, a locus of philosophical reflection on the relationship between humanity and nature. In "Simmons and the Social Tie" (Essay 8), Chesterton marks the distance between the ordinary and the ambitious: "It is extraordinary what a difference the substitution..." (008-001).

## Significance

Chesterton's defense of the ordinary is central to his philosophical project. The ordinary is not the absence of meaning but its saturation point — the place where meaning becomes so dense that we mistake it for banality. His essays are, in large measure, exercises in recovering the extraordinariness of ordinary things.

## Notable Passages

> Trees and fields ought to be the ordinary things; terraces and temples ought to be extraordinary. I am on the side of the prosaic and explicit, as against the exalted and inexplicable. I am on the side of the man who says "I am going to have my dinner," and not of the man who says "I am going to pay my devotions to the spirit of the sun."
>
> (002-005)

> It is extraordinary what a difference the substitute of a word for another word makes. If you call a thing a "social art," the educated classes will admire it; if you call it "society," they will despise it.
>
> (008-001)

> I myself remember running to school (an extraordinary thing to do) with mere internal ecstasy in repeating lines of Walter Scott about the tapering flags of Seville and the city's scarlet cluster. I thought I was enthusiastically enjoying literature when I was enjoying nothing of the kind.
>
> (008-003)
