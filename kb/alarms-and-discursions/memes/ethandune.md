## The Unknown Battlefield

Chesterton opens his essay "Ethandune" with a characteristic provocation: "Perhaps you do not know where Ethandune is. Nor do I; nor does anybody."

> Perhaps you do not know where Ethandune is. Nor do I; nor does anybody. That is where the somewhat sombre fun begins.
>
> (030-001)

He cannot tell you whether it is the name of a forest, a town, or a hill — "I can only say that in any case it is of the kind that floats and is unfixed." (030-001)(030-002)

## A Drifting Name

Ethandune exists as a kind of English mirage. Chesterton describes it in a cascade of metaphors: "If it is a forest, it is one of those forests that march with a million legs, like the walking trees that were the doom of Macbeth. If it is a town, it is one of those towns that vanish, like a city of tents. If it is a hill, it is a flying hill, like the mountain to which faith lends wings." Over a vast dim region of England this dark name "floats like an eagle doubtful where to swoop and strike." (030-003)

## The Battle That Saved Civilization

Yet for all its obscurity, Ethandune is of world-historical importance. Chesterton insists: "without this word that you cannot fit with a meaning and hardly with a memory, you would be sitting in a very different chair at this moment and looking at a very different tablecloth." Without Ethandune "you would not have any civilization, far less any Christian civilization." The battle, fought near Chippenham where the Danes surrendered their swords and were baptized, was Alfred's last epic victory — the one that "really broke the barbarian" — yet "has remained without a modern place or name." (030-003)

## Alfred the Great's Persistence

Chesterton places Ethandune in the context of Alfred's long struggle against the Danish invasions. "Alfred was defeated by the barbarians again and again, he defeated the barbarians again and again; but his victories were almost as vain as his defeats. Fortunately he did not believe in the Time Spirit or the Trend of Things or any such modern rubbish, and therefore kept pegging away." The earlier battles — Wilton, Basing, Ashdown — have names still in use; only the decisive one has vanished. (030-003)

## Chesterton's Visit to the Site

Chesterton describes passing the place "best reputed as Ethandune" under a wild sunset and moonrise: "a high, grim upland, partly bare and partly shaggy." The atmosphere created "that sense of monstrous incident which is the dramatic side of landscape. The bare grey slopes seemed to rush downhill like routed hosts; the dark clouds drove across like riven banners; and the moon was like a golden dragon, like the Golden Dragon of Wessex." He saw a black shapeless pile — a barrow "older than Alfred, older than the Romans, older perhaps than the Britons" — and reflected that the great king, sword in hand as the Danes poured down to Chippenham, may have looked at that same oppressive shape and understood it as little as we do. (030-003)
