# Toryism

## Overview

Toryism in Chesterton's *Alarms and Discursions* is an unexpected and distinctive presence. It is not the Toryism of the country house or the House of Lords — Chesterton describes those same families as "dulled with wealth or decayed with Toryism" (024-001) — but a democratic Toryism rooted in the defence of local tradition, popular custom, and the inherited wisdom of the ordinary Englishman.

This is the "Tory democracy" that runs from Cobbett through Disraeli to Chesterton himself: a politics that trusts the people precisely because it trusts the past that the people have made.

## In Chesterton's Essays

The single direct mention of Toryism in the essays appears in "The Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds." Standing in a Chiltern churchyard among the tombs of Puritan lords, Chesterton looks down on the country seats "of those same families now dulled with wealth or decayed with Toryism" (024-001). The phrase is double-edged: Toryism as decayed, as a political tradition that has lost its vitality and become mere wealth-preservation. Yet the essay itself is an exercise in Tory imagination — the fantasy of taking the ancient office of Steward seriously, of actually hunting the modern brigands who have replaced the medieval outlaws.

Chesterton's democratic Toryism is built on the conviction that ordinary people are the carriers of tradition. In "The Philosophy of Sight-seeing," he writes that "the peasants who preserve all traditions of the sites of battles or the building of churches" (012-007) are the true conservatives, not the aristocrats who abandon the land for London seasons. Tradition, in this view, is not imposed from above by elites but sustained from below by communities.

The democratic foundation of this politics appears throughout the essays. In "The Red Town," Chesterton writes: "When a man says that democracy is false because most people are stupid, we should answer: at least democracy gives every man the right to be stupid in his own way" (010-001). This is Tory logic in democratic form — the defence of variety, locality, and individuality against the uniform rationality of the expert.

In "The Field of Blood," Chesterton traces the connection between democracy and the particular more explicitly: "A people is a soul; and if you want to know what a democracy will do, you must know what it has done. A democracy does not choose a future; it recognizes a past" (025-003). This is the core of Chesterton's Tory democracy — not the sovereignty of the present electorate but the sovereignty of the dead, the tradition that the living inherit and transmit. He rejects "the argument which seeks to identify democracy with a drab utilitarianism" (025-004), insisting that democratic choice is not consumer preference but the recognition of what a people already is.

The defense of tradition against both plutocracy and bureaucracy appears in "The Furrows," where Chesterton writes of "the whole huge achievement of democracy. Here was mere equality" (011-005), finding democratic equality not in the ballot box but in the furrows of a ploughed field — the ancient, repeated, local work that makes all men equal before the soil.

## Historical Context

Chesterton's political development moved from late-Victorian liberalism through Fabian socialism to a position he called distributism, which drew on both radical liberalism and Tory radicalism. The "Tory democracy" tradition — associated with Richard Oastler, the Factory Acts, and the defence of working people against industrial capitalism — provided one stream of this synthesis.

The other stream was Catholic social teaching, particularly the encyclical *Rerum Novarum* (1891), which defended private property, local association, and the rights of the family against both socialism and laissez-faire capitalism.

## Chesterton's Position

Chesterton's Toryism is, in the end, a paradox: a conservatism that is more democratic than democracy, a traditionalism that is more radical than revolution. The Tory, for Chesterton, is the person who defends the local pub against the temperance reformer, the ancient festival against the efficiency expert, the crooked street against the urban planner.

This is not the conservatism of the rich (who, as Chesterton notes in "The Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds," are often the most radical destroyers of tradition) but the conservatism of the poor, who have nothing to conserve except their customs, their communities, and their faith.

## From the Essays

> When a man says that democracy is false because most people are stupid, there are several courses which the philosopher may pursue. The most obvious is to hit him smartly and with precision on the exact tip of the nose. But if you have scruples (moral or physical) about this course, you may proceed to employ Reason, which in this case has all the savage solidity of a blow with the fist. It is stupid to say that "most people" are stupid. It is like saying "most people are tall," when it is obvious that "tall" can only mean taller than most people. It is absurd to denounce the majority of mankind as below the average of mankind.
>
> (010-001)

> It is the peasants who preserve all traditions of the sites of battles or the building of churches. It is they who remember, so far as any one remembers, the glimpses of fairies or the graver wonders of saints. In the classes above them the supernatural has been slain by the supercilious. That is a true and tremendous text in Scripture which says that "where there is no vision the people perish." But it is equally true in practice that where there is no people the visions perish.
>
> (012-007)

> A people is a soul; and if you want to know what a soul is, I can only answer that it is something that can sin and that can sacrifice itself. A people can commit theft; a people can confess theft; a people can repent of theft. That is the idea of the republic.
>
> (025-003)
