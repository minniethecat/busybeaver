## Biography

Herbert George Wells (1866–1946) was an English writer, best known for his science fiction novels including *The War of the Worlds* (1898), *The Time Machine* (1895), and *The Invisible Man* (1897). A student of T. H. Huxley, Wells was a committed socialist and an advocate of scientific rationalism. His later works turned increasingly toward social commentary and utopian speculation. He was a prominent member of the Fabian Society and a contemporary—and frequent intellectual opponent—of G. K. Chesterton.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton engages Wells across multiple essays in *Alarms and Discursions*, often with the affectionate combativeness that characterized their public debates. The two men disagreed on nearly everything—religion, biology, politics, human nature—yet maintained a genuine mutual respect.

In the essay "How I Found the Superman," Chesterton opens with a direct and playful challenge: "Readers of Mr. Bernard Shaw and other modern writers may be interested to know that the Superman has been found. I found him; he lives in South Croydon" (020-001). He then contrasts his discovery with "Mr. Wells's notion of generating him out of gases in a private laboratory," a pointed satire of Wells's faith in biological and technological transformation. For Chesterton, the Superman is not a future product of science but an ordinary human being, discoverable in any suburb.

Chesterton's most sustained engagement with Wells appears in his discussion of *Ann Veronica* (1909), Wells's controversial novel about a young woman's rebellion against Edwardian conventions. Chesterton praises the novel's sincerity but questions its philosophy with characteristic inversion: "I deny that biology can destroy the sense of truth, which alone can even desire biology. My mind cannot find anything which denies my mind" (035-002). This is the core of the Chesterton-Wells debate: whether mind is reducible to matter, or whether matter itself is unintelligible without mind.

## Key Works / Contributions

Wells's major works include the scientific romances *The Time Machine*, *The Island of Doctor Moreau*, *The Invisible Man*, and *The War of the Worlds*, as well as the comic novels *Kipps* and *The History of Mr. Polly*, and the feminist novel *Ann Veronica*. His *Outline of History* (1920) was a massive popular success. As a public intellectual, Wells argued for world government, scientific progress, and the reconstruction of society along rational lines.

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton and Wells debated publicly for decades—most famously in a 1924 exchange at the Queen's Hall—and their disagreements were foundational to both men's thought. Chesterton's critique of Wells crystallizes in *Alarms and Discursions* around two related claims.

First, Wells's biological materialism is self-refuting. If all thought is a product of biological processes, then the thought that all thought is a biological product is itself merely a biological product—and we have no reason to trust it. Chesterton's counter-move is not to deny biology but to insist that truth-seeking is prior to and independent of any biological account of it.

Second, Wells's faith in progress underestimates human nature. The ordinary man in South Croydon is already more interesting, more complex, and more mysterious than any laboratory-generated Superman. Chesterton's comic discovery of the Superman in the suburbs is, beneath the humor, a serious theological point: the extraordinary is already present in the ordinary.
