# Fabian Society

## Overview

The Fabian Society, founded in London in 1884, was the leading intellectual force in British socialism during Chesterton's era. Named after the Roman general Fabius Maximus, who defeated Hannibal through patience and attrition rather than direct battle, the society advocated gradual, evolutionary socialism rather than revolutionary Marxism. Its early members included George Bernard Shaw, H.G. Wells, Sidney and Beatrice Webb, and Annie Besant — the intellectual elite of Edwardian progressivism.

## In Chesterton's Essays

The Fabian Society does not appear by name in *Alarms and Discursions*, but its presence is felt throughout the essays. When Chesterton critiques "the Socialist" or "the progressive reformer," the Fabian is often his implicit target. The essay "How I Found the Superman" is framed as a direct address to Shaw and Wells, both prominent Fabians: "Readers of Mr. Bernard Shaw and other modern writers may be interested to know that the Superman has been found" (020-001).

Chesterton's satire in this essay targets the Fabian faith in planned social progress. "I assure Mr. Wells that the Superman at Croydon was born in the ordinary way, though he himself, of course, is anything but ordinary" (020-001). Against the Fabian vision of engineering a better humanity through policy and education, Chesterton presents the Superman as an ordinary child living in the suburbs — the democratic miracle in place of the technocratic one.

The essay's description of the Superman's upbringing — his isolation in a quiet room, his inability to shake hands "not hands, you know" (020-004) — is a parable about the Fabian tendency to manage and control human development, with the implicit warning that such management produces not superhumanity but alienation.

Elsewhere, Chesterton's distributism — his preference for widely distributed property over state collectivism — positions him directly against the Fabian programme. The society's belief in state-administered progress collides with Chesterton's belief in popular democracy, tradition, and the small proprietor.

## History and Context

The Fabian Society was founded on 4 January 1884 as a split from the Fellowship of the New Life. Its early membership was small but extraordinarily influential: within a generation, Fabian ideas had reshaped British political discourse. The society was instrumental in founding the London School of Economics (1895) and the Labour Party (1900). Its method — research, publication, permeation of existing institutions — proved far more effective than revolutionary socialism.

Chesterton's relationship with the Fabians was complex. He admired Shaw personally and engaged in public debates with him that attracted enormous audiences. He respected the intelligence of the Fabian critique of capitalism. But he rejected their solution: state socialism was, for Chesterton, merely capitalism concentrated — the employer grown so large it became the government.

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton's opposition to Fabianism can be summarised in three points. First, the Fabians trusted experts more than ordinary people — a violation of Chesterton's democratic principle. Second, the Fabian programme concentrated power rather than dispersing it — the state would own everything, and the state would be run by people like the Fabians. Third, Fabian progressivism was historically shallow: it treated the accumulated wisdom of tradition as mere prejudice to be swept away.

These objections did not prevent Chesterton from treating Fabians with genuine affection. His debates with Shaw were famous for their good humour, and even in satire he grants them intelligence and good intentions. But the philosophical gulf was real and unbridgeable: Chesterton was a Distributist, committed to small property and local democracy; the Fabians were collectivists, committed to rational administration and central planning.
