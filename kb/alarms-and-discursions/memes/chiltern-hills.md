## Overview

The Chiltern Hills are a range of chalk hills stretching approximately 45 miles across Oxfordshire, Buckinghamshire, Hertfordshire, and Bedfordshire, northwest of London. An Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty, the Chilterns are known for their beech woodlands, rolling farmland, and historic villages. In Chesterton's *Alarms and Discursions*, the Chilterns appear both as a physical landscape and as the source of one of England's most peculiar constitutional fictions.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton encounters the Chilterns in Essay 24, "The Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds." He climbs "one of those high, abrupt, windy churchyards from which the dead seem to look down upon all the living" on a "stray spur of the Chiltern Hills" (024-001). The image is characteristically Chestertonian: the churchyard as a "mountain of ghosts as Olympus was a mountain of gods," the dead Puritan lords still surveying the land they once ruled.

The landscape serves as more than scenery. For Chesterton, the Chiltern churchyard embodies the layered quality of English history—Puritan bones beneath an Anglican church, on a hill that has looked down on Saxons, Normans, and moderns alike. The "high, abrupt, windy" quality of the place mirrors the abrupt, windy quality of the English constitution itself.

The essay then turns to the famous constitutional quirk that gives it its title: the office of Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds, a legal fiction by which Members of Parliament resign their seats (since MPs are technically prohibited from resigning directly). Chesterton describes it as one of those "crazy constitutional anomalies" of which the English are so proud (024-002), and notes with delight that the original duties of the Steward were "to put down the outlaws and brigands" in the Chilterns (024-003)—a medieval reality behind a modern absurdity.

## Historical Significance

The Chiltern Hundreds were three administrative districts—Stoke, Desborough, and Burnham—established in the medieval period. The Stewardship of the Chiltern Hundreds, along with the Manor of Northstead, remains one of the nominal offices of profit under the Crown used to effect resignation from the House of Commons. The legal fiction dates from the seventeenth century and is still in active use.

Beyond its constitutional role, the Chilterns region has a rich history. The hills were once covered by the ancient Chiltern Forest, a haunt of outlaws and brigands in the medieval period—exactly the problem the original Steward was appointed to address. The area's chalk streams, beech woods, and brick-and-flint villages have made it a model of the managed English countryside.

## Chesterton's Connection

Chesterton's interest in the Chilterns is characteristic of his method: he takes a local, even obscure, feature of English life and uses it to illuminate larger truths. The Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds is a joke, a legal fiction, a piece of "twisted tomfoolery"—and yet it works. It has facilitated the peaceful transfer of parliamentary seats for centuries. The absurdity, for Chesterton, is a feature, not a bug: the English genius for making unreasonable things work is one of his recurring themes.

The Chiltern Hills themselves—windy, ancient, haunted by Puritan ghosts—provide the physical counterpoint to the constitutional abstraction. Chesterton never lets an idea float free of its geography. The Steward's office is not merely a legal curiosity; it is rooted in a specific landscape of hills, churchyards, and the memory of brigands.


## From the Essays


> The other day on a stray spur of the Chiltern Hills I climbed up upon one of those high, abrupt, windy churchyards from which the dead seem to look down upon all the living. It was a mountain of ghosts as Olympus was a mountain of gods. In that church lay the bones of great Puritan lords, of a time when most of the power of England was Puritan, even of the Established Church.
>
> (024-001)
