## Overview

Wiltshire is a historic county in south-west England, known for its chalk downlands, prehistoric monuments, and the white horse hill figures that inspired Chesterton's essay "The White Horses" (Essay 34). Although Chesterton does not name the county directly in the essay, the landscape he describes — the chalk downs of Salisbury Plain, the White Horse of Westbury — is unmistakably that of Wiltshire.

## In Chesterton's Work

The white horses carved into the Wiltshire downs serve Chesterton as symbols of art older than history. In "The White Horses," he contemplates the antiquity of these hill figures and the tradition that maintained them across millennia. The essay's argument about tradition, landscape, and the continuity of human culture is rooted in the specific geography of Wiltshire.

## Geography

Wiltshire's chalk geology created the conditions for both the prehistoric monuments (Stonehenge, Avebury) and the white horse figures that populate Chesterton's essay. The county's open, rolling downland — described by Chesterton as "coarse and desolate heights" — has been inhabited since the Neolithic period.
