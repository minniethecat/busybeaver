## Overview

Canterbury appears in Chesterton's "The Philosophy of Sight-seeing" (Essay 12) as a single but telling reference. For Chesterton, the medieval builders intended their cathedrals not for self-conscious tourists but for ordinary people going about their daily business.

## In Chesterton's Essays

In a passage on the proper appreciation of historic architecture, Chesterton writes: "Buy a bicycle in Maidstone to visit an aunt in Dover, and you will see Canterbury Cathedral as it was built to be seen" (012-011).

The point is that cathedrals were designed to catch the eye of the casual passer-by, not the cultivated connoisseur. The true way to revive the magic of great minsters, Chesterton argues, is not to be more careful of them but more careless — to encounter them naturally, as part of ordinary life.

## Significance

Canterbury Cathedral serves as Chesterton's exemplar of authentic medieval architecture appreciation, contrasted with the modern touristic approach that he criticizes throughout the essay.
