## Biography

John Bunyan (1628–1688) was an English Puritan writer and Nonconformist preacher, best known as the author of *The Pilgrim's Progress* (1678), one of the most influential works of English religious literature. Born in Elstow, Bedfordshire, the son of a tinker, Bunyan served in the Parliamentary army during the English Civil War and underwent a profound religious conversion. His refusal to conform to the Church of England led to twelve years of imprisonment (1660–1672), during which he wrote much of his most enduring work.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton references Bunyan twice in *Alarms and Discursions*, both times using him as a touchstone of intense moral seriousness.

In Essay 13 ("A Criminal Head"), Chesterton defends Robespierre against charges of ethical deficiency by comparing him to Bunyan:

> He might as well say that John Bunyan was deficient in ethical instincts. You may say that Robespierre was morbid and unbalanced, and you may say the same of Bunyan. But if these two men were morbid and unbalanced they were morbid and unbalanced by feeling too much about morality, not by feeling too little. (013-004)

In Essay 15 ("The Gold of Glastonbury"), Chesterton deploys Bunyan in an absurdist analogy while defending the plausibility of the Joseph of Arimathea legend:

> The popular notion is that the thing is quite comic and inconceivable; as if one said that Wat Tyler went to Chicago, or that John Bunyan discovered the North Pole. (015-002)

Both references reveal Chesterton's instinctive reach for Bunyan as an emblem of the Puritan moral imagination — a figure whose passionate intensity transcends doctrinal difference.

## Key Works / Contributions

- **The Pilgrim's Progress** (1678) — Christian allegory, one of the most published books in the English language
- **Grace Abounding to the Chief of Sinners** (1666) — spiritual autobiography
- **The Holy War** (1682) — allegorical novel of the siege of Mansoul

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton treats Bunyan not as a theological opponent (despite their Catholic–Protestant divide) but as a moral exemplar. By pairing Bunyan with Robespierre — both "morbid and unbalanced by feeling too much about morality" — Chesterton distinguishes genuine moral passion from mere ethical deficiency. For Chesterton, Bunyan represents a kind of spiritual intensity that the modern world has lost, even if the particular doctrinal shape of that intensity differs from his own Catholicism. The North Pole joke in Essay 15 is playful rather than dismissive, using Bunyan's iconic status to make a point about historical plausibility rather than to mock the man.
