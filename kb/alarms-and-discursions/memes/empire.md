## Overview

Empire appears in *Alarms and Discursions* primarily in two registers: as a theme in Chesterton's critique of imperialism and as a metaphor for universal political order. The concept intersects with his treatment of Rome, the Roman Empire, and the moral claims of British colonialism.

## In Chesterton's Essays

In "Simmons and the Social Tie" (Essay 8), Chesterton describes the ordinary man's relationship to imperial ambition: "to grasp the sceptre of empire and the firebrand of speculation" (008-001) is presented as one of the grandiose impulses that compete with the quieter demands of social obligation. In "The Wrath of the Roses" (Essay 14), empire appears in a different key: roses become "their instinctive emblems of empire and rivalry" (014-010), suggesting the naturalness of territorial ambition even in the garden. In "The Gold of Glastonbury" (Essay 15), Chesterton contrasts the "open province of a polyglot empire" (015-005) with the particularity of local tradition.

## Significance

Chesterton's treatment of empire reflects his distributist sympathies and his suspicion of large-scale political organization. The polyglot empire of Rome serves as both a positive and negative model: it spread law and citizenship, but it also dissolved local particularity. The tension between universal order and local freedom runs through all his discussions of empire.

## From the Essays


> As long as empires go about urging their ideas on others, I always have a notion that the ideas are no good. If they were really so splendid, they would make the country preaching them a wonder of the world. That is the true ideal; a great nation ought not to be a hammer, but a magnet.
>
> (033-008)
