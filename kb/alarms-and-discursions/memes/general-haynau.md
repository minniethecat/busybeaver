# General Haynau

## Biography

Julius Jacob von Haynau (1786–1853) was an Austrian general who earned the epithet "the women-flogger" for his brutal suppression of the Hungarian Revolution of 1848–1849. As commander of the Austrian forces in Hungary, Haynau ordered mass executions, floggings of women, and harsh reprisals against civilians — actions that made him notorious across Europe even in an era accustomed to military severity.

On 4 September 1850, while visiting London, Haynau toured the Barclay, Perkins and Co. brewery. Upon emerging, he was recognized and physically attacked by the brewery's draymen, who beat him with sticks. The British government, bowing to immense popular approval of the assault, declined to prosecute the draymen. Haynau returned to Austria and settled on his estate at Szekeres. After his death, the estate passed to his daughter; upon her death, it was willed to the local commune. The commune, however, declined the inheritance and ordered the land left fallow as the "Bloody Meadow" (025-001).

## In Chesterton's Essays

Haynau is the central historical figure of essay-25, "The Field of Blood." Chesterton opens the essay by quoting a newspaper account of Haynau's 1850 beating and the Hungarian commune's subsequent refusal of his estate.

Chesterton uses Haynau on two distinct levels. First, as a touchstone for the decline of English popular militancy: "It recalls the days when Englishmen were potential lighters; that is, potential rebels" (025-002). Modern Englishmen denounce tyrants as heartily as their forebears denounced Haynau — but they no longer thrash them in the streets.

> "It is not the tyrants that are lacking, but the draymen." (025-002)

Second, and more importantly, Chesterton uses the Hungarian commune's refusal of Haynau's estate as a model of democratic action. The commune did not merely dislike Haynau; it refused his gift. It chose to let his land lie barren — a sacrifice of wealth for the sake of moral witness:

> "A people can commit theft; a people can confess theft; a people can repent of theft. That is the idea of the republic." (025-003)

For Chesterton, the commune's refusal is the purest expression of democratic will: "When there is sacrifice we know there is a single will" (025-005). The commune acted not as an aggregation of private interests but as a moral personality — a people capable of saying "no" to material benefit for the sake of a moral principle.

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton's treatment of Haynau is layered. He does not defend the general — the essay's sympathies are entirely with the draymen and the Hungarian commune. But Haynau is not the essay's real target. The real target is modern complacency — the assumption that democracy means bureaucracy rather than moral action, that progress means prosperity rather than justice.

The essay's closing paragraphs move from Haynau to a broader vision of what Chesterton calls "the strong gesture of a romantic refusal" (025-005). The Hungarian commune's decision to name a field "Bloody Meadow" and let it go to waste is, for Chesterton, an instance of democracy in its most essential form: not the administration of shared interests but the expression of shared moral judgment.

The Haynau story thus serves a triple function in the essay: as historical anecdote, as political critique of modern England, and as philosophical exemplar of Chesterton's theory of democracy as collective moral personality.
