## Biography

Maximilien Robespierre (1758–1794) was a central leader of the French Revolution and the Reign of Terror. A lawyer from Arras, he rose to prominence in the National Assembly and the Committee of Public Safety, advocating for universal suffrage, the abolition of slavery, and the execution of King Louis XVI. His identification of virtue with terror — "virtue without which terror is fatal, terror without which virtue is powerless" — made him the most controversial figure of the Revolution. He was overthrown and executed in the Thermidorian Reaction of July 1794.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Robespierre appears most prominently in essay 13, "A Criminal Head," where Chesterton mounts a fierce defence of the revolutionary leader against the pretensions of scientific criminology. Describing a portrait he saw in a gallery, Chesterton notes the "lean and lifted" face, the "eyes stared forward with a frightful sincerity," the "lip firm with a heroic firmness" (013-004). The face, he writes, was that of "a man in the manner of Shakespeare's Brutus, a man of piercingly pure intentions" (013-004).

What outraged Chesterton was the caption beneath the portrait: "Deficiency of ethical instincts." This dismissive phrase, he argues, betrays not knowledge but ignorance — the criminologist who wrote it understood skulls but understood nothing of human morality. Chesterton's response is devastating:

> The man who could say that Robespierre was deficient in ethical instincts is a man utterly to be disregarded in all calculations of ethics. He might as well say that John Bunyan was deficient in ethical instincts. (013-004)

Chesterton concedes that Robespierre may have been "morbid and unbalanced," but insists the morbidity was an excess of ethical feeling, not a deficiency. "If he was mad he was mad on ethics" (013-004). This is the central paradox: the accusation of moral deficiency is precisely backwards; Robespierre's problem was too much morality, not too little.

The essay broadens into a defence of the French Revolution itself. Chesterton describes Robespierre and his companions as "a company of keen and pugnacious men, intellectually impatient of unreason and wrong, resolved that Europe should not be choked up in every channel by oligarchies and state secrets that already stank" (013-005). He calls their work "the greatest that was ever given to men to do except that which Christianity did in dragging Europe out of the abyss of barbarism after the Dark Ages" (013-005).

In essay 33, "The Sentimentalist," Chesterton returns to Robespierre briefly, contrasting the revolutionary's authentic moral passion with the shallow sentimentalism of a modern Republican who "believed in the equality of citizens" (033-002) but hedges his principles at every turn.

## Key Works / Contributions

Robespierre's political legacy includes: leadership of the Jacobin Club, the establishment of the Revolutionary Tribunal, the Cult of the Supreme Being (his attempt to replace Christianity with deistic civic religion), and the articulation of revolutionary virtue as the fusion of moral purity with political terror. His fall in Thermidor marked the end of the radical phase of the French Revolution.

Chesterton's references to Robespierre appear alongside other French Revolutionary figures including Danton, Charlotte Corday, and Napoleon — all of whom feature elsewhere in *Alarms and Discursions*.

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton's treatment of Robespierre is fundamentally a defence of moral seriousness against the reductionism of scientific criminology. The "criminal head" of the essay's title is ironic: what the criminologists see as a criminal skull Chesterton sees as the face of a man consumed by conscience. This is not to say Chesterton endorses the Terror — he describes Robespierre as "morbid and unbalanced" — but he insists that any assessment must begin with the recognition that Robespierre was driven by genuine ethical passion.

The deeper argument is about method. The criminologist who measures skulls believes he is doing science, but Chesterton shows he is merely projecting his own ignorance onto a subject he has not troubled to understand. True knowledge of history requires entering into the minds of historical actors, not measuring their bones. In this, the Robespierre passage is one of Chesterton's most concentrated arguments for the humanities against scientism.
