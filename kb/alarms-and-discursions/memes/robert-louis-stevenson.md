## Biography

Robert Louis Stevenson (1850–1894) was a Scottish novelist, essayist, poet, and travel writer. Born in Edinburgh to a family of lighthouse engineers, Stevenson rejected the family profession for literature. Despite chronic ill health, he produced a remarkable body of work and travelled widely, eventually settling in Samoa where he died at age forty-four. His most celebrated works include *Treasure Island* (1883), *Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde* (1886), and *Kidnapped* (1886).

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton references Stevenson twice in *Alarms and Discursions*, each time with evident warmth and admiration.

In Essay 3 ("The Nightmare"), Chesterton opens with a quotation from Stevenson's letters:

> In one of Stevenson's letters there is a characteristically humorous remark about the appalling impression produced on him in childhood by the beasts with many eyes in the Book of Revelations: "If that was heaven, what in the name of Davy Jones was hell like?" Now in sober truth there is a magnificent idea in these monsters of the Apocalypse. (003-002)

Chesterton uses this remark to launch a meditation on how "beings really more beautiful or more universal than we are might appear to us frightful and even confused."

In Essay 38 ("The Chorus"), Chesterton discusses the literary pendulum between gritty realism and adventure romance. He notes that the reading public tired of relentlessly bleak fiction and "welcomed the return of adventurous novels about alien places and times, the trenchant and swordlike stories of Stevenson" (038-006). The phrase "trenchant and swordlike" captures Chesterton's appreciation for Stevenson's narrative energy and moral clarity.

## Key Works / Contributions

- **Treasure Island** (1883) — the definitive pirate adventure novel
- **Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde** (1886) — psychological horror classic
- **Kidnapped** (1886) — historical adventure set in the Scottish Highlands
- **A Child's Garden of Verses** (1885) — beloved children's poetry
- Extensive travel writing, including accounts of the South Seas

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton's treatment of Stevenson is notably positive compared to his treatment of many other contemporary literary figures. The phrase "trenchant and swordlike stories" is not ironic — it reflects Chesterton's genuine belief that adventure fiction serves a profound human need. In Essay 38, Chesterton argues that literature needs both the realist's "glimpses of the gloom" and the romantic's "jolly refrain" — and Stevenson, for Chesterton, represents the latter at its best. The opening of Essay 3, borrowing Stevenson's childhood terror at Revelation, also shows Chesterton finding in Stevenson a kindred sensibility: someone who takes theological imagination seriously while retaining a childlike capacity for wonder, humour, and metaphysical awe.
