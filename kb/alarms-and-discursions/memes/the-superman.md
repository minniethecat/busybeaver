## Overview

"The Superman" — the Nietzschean ideal of a being beyond ordinary human limitations — was one of the most discussed concepts of the Edwardian era. In essay-20 of *Alarms and Discursions*, "How I Found the Superman," Chesterton subjects the entire notion to devastating satire. Rather than arguing against the Superman in philosophical terms, he claims to have actually found him — living in the London suburb of South Croydon, the absurdly ordinary offspring of a fashionable lady philanthropist and a crackpot eugenicist doctor. The essay is at once a parody of the Superman cult and a defense of ordinary humanity against the pretensions of those who would improve upon it.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton opens "How I Found the Superman" with the mock-triumphant announcement: "Readers of Mr. Bernard Shaw and other modern writers may be interested to know that the Superman has been found. I found him; he lives in South Croydon" (020-001). The comedy works on multiple levels: South Croydon, a perfectly unremarkable London suburb, is the least Nietzschean location imaginable

> Readers of Mr. Bernard Shaw and other modern writers may be interested to know that the Superman has been found. I found him; he lives in South Croydon.
>
> (020-001)

The comedy works on multiple levels, and the claim of "finding" the Superman deflates the grandiose rhetoric of those who merely talk about him.

The parents of the Superman are Chesterton's vehicles for satirizing two strains of progressive thought. Lady Hypatia Smythe-Browne represents fashionable philanthropy, whose "constant cry of 'Save the children!'" (020-002) refers not to poverty or hunger but to "the cruel neglect of children's eyesight involved in allowing them to play with crudely painted toys" (020-002). She is the social reformer whose concerns are calibrated entirely to her own class's sensibilities. Dr. Hagg represents scientific eugenics: his "daring experiments in Neo-Individualist Eugenics" (020-003) combine pretensions to geology, socialism, and religion into a single incoherent modern creed. Their marriage is an alliance of "the two highest types of our civilization, the fashionable lady and the all but vulgar medical man" (020-003), and its product is the Superman — "that being whom all the labourers in Battersea are so eagerly expecting night and day" (020-003).

The climax of the essay is the revelation of what the Superman actually is. When Chesterton's narrator demands to see him, the parents prevaricate: he "creates his own standard" of beauty (020-004); his hair is not "what we call hair" (020-004); he cannot shake hands because "the structure, of course" (020-004) is different. When the narrator finally bursts into the room, he finds only darkness — and a "small sad yelp" (020-004). The Superman, it turns out, is something so deformed and fragile that a draft of air kills him. The cosmic funeral that follows is Chesterton's final mockery: "the whole universe weeping over the frustration of its most magnificent birth" (020-004). But the narrator hears "a hoot of laughter in the high wail of the wind" (020-004) — and so, Chesterton implies, should we.

## Historical Context

The Superman concept entered English intellectual life primarily through George Bernard Shaw's *Man and Superman* (1903), which popularized (and domesticated) Friedrich Nietzsche's *Übermensch* from *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* (1883–1885). Shaw's version blended Nietzsche with Lamarckian evolution and Fabian socialism. H.G. Wells, whom Chesterton also names in the essay, explored the idea through scientific romance and eugenic speculation. The early twentieth century saw a flourishing of eugenics movements across Europe and America, culminating (after Chesterton's time) in the horrors that would fully vindicate his satire. Chesterton's essay, published in 1910, stands as an early and prescient warning against the dream of engineering a superior humanity.

## Chesterton's Position

Chesterton's critique of the Superman is fundamentally a defense of the ordinary. The essay's comic strategy — locating the Superman in South Croydon, making his parents absurdly pretentious, and revealing him as a pitiable creature — works by inversion: the more grandiose the claim for human transcendence, the more it collapses into bathos when subjected to the test of reality. Chesterton is not arguing against excellence or aspiration; he is arguing against the specific modern fantasy that humanity can be transcended by its own effort, that some technical or biological breakthrough can produce a being superior to the ordinary human soul. His target is not Nietzsche so much as the progressive middlebrow culture that had absorbed Nietzsche's rhetoric without his tragic sense, turning the Übermensch into a suburban self-improvement project.

The essay also contains a theological undercurrent. For Chesterton, the attempt to create a Superman is a parody of the Incarnation — man trying to become God by his own devices, rather than God becoming man. The result, inevitably, is something less than human, not more.
