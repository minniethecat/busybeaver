## Overview

Custom occupies a distinguished place in G.K. Chesterton's moral and political philosophy throughout *Alarms and Discursions*. Against the progressive who sees custom as mere inertia, Chesterton argues that custom is democracy extended through time — the accumulated wisdom of ordinary people, preserved and passed down.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton's most sustained treatment of custom appears in "Cheese" (Essay 9), where he reflects on the nature of social conventions through the unlikely lens of food. He observes that in matters of eating and drinking, the truth is found "always in the type and custom" (009-001). Custom, far from being arbitrary, represents the tested and proven — what has survived because it works.

He develops this theme with characteristic paradox: "Bad customs are universal and rigid, like" iron laws, while good customs are living and flexible (009-004). The distinction is crucial: Chesterton does not defend all customs, only those that have grown organically from human life. The bad custom — like the fashionable diet or the modern social convention — is imposed from above; the good custom grows from below.

The essay reaches its comic climax when Chesterton, dining at a restaurant, asks for cheese and is told that "he was only obeying a custom of Modern Society" by being denied it (009-006). The inversion is perfect: what presents itself as enlightened freedom from custom is itself the most tyrannical custom of all.

In "The Futurists" (Essay 16), Chesterton extends the argument to aesthetics. He criticizes the Futurist movement for seeking to "glorify the love of danger, the custom of energy, the strength of daring" (016-002) — noting, with characteristic wit, that the Futurists' attempt to abolish tradition merely creates a new and less tolerable custom in its place.

In "Ethandune" (Essay 30), Chesterton makes his deepest claim for custom. Speaking of national traditions, he insists: "I do say that they are customs, and that you would not possess" them unless they had been earned (030-003). Custom is not inherited privilege but accumulated merit — the hard-won wisdom of generations.


> Bad customs are universal and rigid, like modern militarism. Good customs are universal and varied, like native chivalry and self-defence. Both the good and bad civilization cover us as with a canopy, and protect us from all that is outside. But a good civilization spreads over us freely like a tree, varying and yielding because it is alive.
>
> (009-004)

## Chesterton's Position

Chesterton's defence of custom is a defence of democracy. The democratic argument against custom — that it binds the living to the dead — Chesterton inverts: custom is the vote of the dead, and to ignore it is the worst kind of disenfranchisement. The man who respects custom listens to his ancestors; the man who despises custom listens only to himself. For Chesterton, the latter is the true tyranny.
