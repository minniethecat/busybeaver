## Overview

"Nature-worship" is the term Chesterton uses in *Alarms and Discursions* for a specific modern spiritual error: the elevation of the natural world from something ordinary and given into something sacred and ultimate. In essay-02, "The Surrender of a Cockney," he declares bluntly that he "sincerely maintains that Nature-worship is more morally dangerous than the most vulgar man-worship of the cities" (002-002), grounding his critique in a theological conviction that worship, properly understood, belongs to God alone — and that redirecting it toward trees, fields, or the sea is not harmless sentiment but a genuine moral hazard.

## In Chesterton's Essays

The primary source for Chesterton's treatment of nature-worship is "The Surrender of a Cockney" (essay-02). The essay opens as a comic confession: Chesterton, the proud Cockney who has always maintained that "the Lord is not in the wind or thunder of the waste, but if anywhere in the still small voice of Fleet Street" (002-001), has surrendered to the countryside and bought a country house. But the confession is a vehicle for a serious argument.

Chesterton identifies two dangers in nature-worship. The first is moral: it "can easily be perverted into the worship of an impersonal mystery, carelessness, or cruelty" (002-002). Nature, considered as an object of worship, has no moral character — it is equally the source of beauty and brutality. To worship it is to worship something that cannot distinguish between a sunset and a massacre. His examples are pointed: "Thoreau would have been a jollier fellow if he had devoted himself to a greengrocer instead of to greens. Swinburne would have been a better moralist if he had worshipped a fishmonger instead of worshipping the sea" (002-002). The greengrocer and the fishmonger, being human, have moral claims on us that greens and the sea do not.

The second danger is structural. "Modern nature-worship is all upside down," he writes. "Trees and fields ought to be the ordinary things; terraces and temples ought to be extraordinary" (002-005). The nature-worshipper makes a category error: he treats the background as if it were the subject. Nature is the setting of human life, not its object of devotion. To reverse this is to invert the proper order of things. Chesterton sides with "the man who lives in the country and wants to go to London" against "the man who lives in London and wants to go to the country" (002-005) — the former treats the city as a marvel, which it is, while the latter treats the country as an escape, which is a sentimental evasion.

The theme recurs in less explicit form elsewhere. In "Introductory: On Gargoyles" (essay-01), Chesterton notes that pre-Christian peoples "worshipped the sun, not idolatrously, but as a creature" (001-003), while the medieval Christians "summoned godlike things to worship their god" (001-010). The distinction is crucial: the Christian can appreciate nature *as creation* without worshipping it *as creator*. Nature-worship, whether pagan or modern, confuses the two.

## Historical Context

The critique of nature-worship must be understood against the background of the Romantic movement and its legacy. Wordsworth's "Nature never did betray the heart that loved her" had become a cultural commonplace by the Edwardian era. The "Simple Life" movement, influenced by thinkers from Thoreau to Edward Carpenter, urged urban intellectuals to return to the land. Chesterton's objection is not to living in the country — he was, after all, writing from a country house — but to the spiritual claim that nature is morally superior to civilization. His position is nearer to Dr. Johnson's: "When a man is tired of London, he is tired of life." The city, as a human artifact, reflects the image of its makers; nature, for all its beauty, does not.

## Chesterton's Position

Chesterton's theology underlies his critique of nature-worship. In a Christian cosmos, nature is good but not divine — it is a created thing, and worshipping the creation instead of the Creator is the definition of idolatry. But Chesterton characteristically gives this theological point a practical and comic twist: the real danger of nature-worship is not that it is blasphemous but that it makes people insufferable. The nature-worshipper who flees the city for the countryside is, in Chesterton's view, making the same mistake as the sentimentalist: wanting the pleasures of an idea without its consequences. Nature is real, beautiful, and essential — but it is not a religion, and treating it as one corrupts both nature and religion.

But when we wish to pay emphatic honour to a man, to praise the firmness of his nature, the squareness of his conduct, the strong humility with which he is interlocked with his equals in silent mutual support, then we invoke the nobler Cockney metaphor, and call him a brick.
>
> (002-002)

> Modern nature-worship is all upside down. Trees and fields ought to be the ordinary things; terraces and temples ought to be extraordinary. I am on the side of the man who lives in the country and wants to go to London. I abominate and abjure the man who lives in London and wants to go to the country; I do it with all the more heartiness because I am that sort of man myself.
>
> (002-005)
