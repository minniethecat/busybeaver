# Authority

## Overview

Authority in *Alarms and Discursions* is a paradoxical theme. Chesterton, the self-described democrat and distributist, is yet acutely aware that abolition of authority does not produce freedom but merely relocates coercion — from the visible throne to the invisible market, from the priest to the press, from the king to the committee. His treatment is characteristically dialectical: he defends authority against anarchists, yet attacks the false authority of landlords, newspaper editors, and modern experts. True authority, for Chesterton, is the precondition of genuine liberty, not its negation.

## In Chesterton's Essays

The most explicit treatment of authority appears in "The Anarchist" (Essay 19), a dialogue between Chesterton and a doctrinaire anarchist on a park bench overlooking Park Lane. The anarchist, armed with the writings of Pidge and Kruxy, declares that "just as religious authority and political authority have gone, so must emotional authority and psychological authority" (019-004). Chesterton's devastating reply cuts through the theoretical apparatus: "if the time ever comes when we all storm those houses, will you tell me one thing? Tell me how we shall do it without authority? Tell me how you will have an army of revolt without discipline?" (019-004). The point is not that Chesterton loves authority for its own sake — he has just been seething at the injustice of those same Park Lane mansions — but that effective action against injustice requires organization, and organization requires authority. The anarchist who abolishes all authority abolishes the very instrument of revolt.

The question of legitimate versus illegitimate authority surfaces again in "Simmons and the Social Tie" (Essay 8). Simmons is a schoolboy who develops "a wild worship of the ordinary" and a radical egalitarianism so extreme that he refuses even to answer questions correctly, leading to "bad feeling between him and the school authority, which ended in a rupture unexpectedly violent" (008-005). Simmons flees to the army — "the one place where every one is dressed alike." Chesterton's point is layered: school authority, when merely conventional, deserves rebellion; but Simmons' flight to military uniformity suggests that the human need for authority cannot be escaped, only redirected. The question is not whether authority shall exist but what kind.

A third, more mythic treatment of authority comes in the parable Chesterton relates in "The Long Bow" (Essay 35). An old king, confronted with four wandering storytellers, declares: "I will believe your mad tales about the exact machinery of arrows; for that is science. I will believe your mad tales about traces of life in the moon; for that is science. I will believe your mad tales about jellyfish turning into gentlemen, and everything turning into anything; for that is science. But I will not believe you when you tell me what I know to be untrue. I will not believe you when you say that you did not all set forth under my authority and out of my house" (035-014). The king grants science its full scope but draws an absolute line at the denial of observable moral truth. Authority here is not arbitrary power but the king's responsibility to adjudicate truth — an echo of Chesterton's belief that democracy itself requires a final authority, namely the people's collective conscience.

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period witnessed a crisis of authority on multiple fronts. The constitutional crisis of 1909–1911 pitted Commons against Lords, challenging the authority of hereditary aristocracy. The rise of syndicalism and anarchism challenged political authority tout court. Biblical higher criticism and Darwinian science challenged religious authority. In this context, Chesterton's defence of authority was not reactionary nostalgia but a distinctive intervention: authority must be democratised, not abolished. The people must become the authority, not escape from authority altogether.

## Chesterton's Position

Chesterton's position resists easy political classification. He is not an authoritarian: his critique of landlords, newspaper proprietors, and imperial officials is relentless. But neither is he a libertarian: he sees the abolition of authority as an impossibility that produces only hidden and unaccountable coercion. His solution is the democratisation of authority — put the people on the throne, not abolish the throne. The king in "The Long Bow" who insists on the truth of what he has witnessed is Chesterton's model: authority is the obligation to say what is true, and the democratic state is the people exercising that obligation collectively. "A people is a soul," he writes in "The Field of Blood"; authority without a soul is tyranny, but a soul without authority is incapable of action.

> It has been shown that just as religious authority and political authority have passed from one to another, so there must be a moral authority passing from one to another.
>
> (019-004)
