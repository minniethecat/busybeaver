# Simmons

## Biography

Simmons is a figure known only through G.K. Chesterton's essay "Simmons and the Social Tie" (1910). He was a schoolboy contemporary of Chesterton's — tall, healthy, with dark straight hair and a worried, hesitating face (008-001). Little is known of his life beyond what the essay reveals: he fled both school and home, eventually enlisting as a private in the Lancers regiment (008-005). Chesterton speculates he "was killed in Africa" (008-006), but acknowledges knowing nothing more.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Simmons appears in essay-08, "Simmons and the Social Tie," where Chesterton uses him to illustrate a philosophical principle: that general theories about types and tendencies must be tested against "an actual personality" (008-001). Simmons serves as Chesterton's exemplar of the schoolboy mentality — the inverse of Mrs. Buttons, who tests theories about women.

Chesterton describes Simmons as "exceptionally average" and "the incarnation and the exaggeration of a certain spirit which is the common spirit of boys" (008-001). The essay details Simmons's psychological peculiarity: an extreme aversion to any form of distinction or special knowledge among his peers. When a classmate demonstrated unusual learning — knowing German, musical terminology, or having read George Eliot — Simmons would writhe in discomfort, hide behind his desk lid, and whisper protests with "the hoarse emphasis of pain" (008-004).

> "He felt no personal anger, still less any petty jealousy, what he felt was an honourable and generous shame. He hated it as a lady hates coarseness in a pantomime; it made him want to hide himself." (008-004)

This aversion extended even to correct answers in class: "If asked the date of the battle of Hastings, he considered it due to social tact and general good feeling to answer 1067" (008-005). Eventually the tension with school authority led to a rupture, and Simmons fled.

Chesterton's final sighting of Simmons came at a public recreation ground, where he wore "the dashing uniform of a private in the Lancers" (008-005). The choice of the army was, Chesterton implies, the logical terminus of Simmons's philosophy: "He had gone to the one place where every one is dressed alike — a regiment" (008-006).

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton treats Simmons with wry sympathy rather than ridicule. The essay's closing paragraph — written during a period of jingoistic celebration — reveals deeper feeling:

> "But when England was full of flags and false triumphs, when everybody was talking manly trash about the whelps of the lion and the brave boys in red, I often heard a voice echoing in the under-caverns of my memory, 'Shut up... O, shut up... O, I say, shut it.'" (008-006)

This turns Simmons from a comic anecdote into an ironic memorial. The boy who could not bear to be distinguished from his peers becomes the soldier whose likely death in imperial service goes unremarked amid patriotic noise. Chesterton's recollection of Simmons's protests serves as a quiet rebuke to martial rhetoric.

For Chesterton, Simmons also embodies a broader insight about boyhood psychology: that the ritual of "not being romantic" is itself deeply romantic (008-002). The stoicism and uniformity boys enforce among themselves is, paradoxically, "the direct product of sentimentalism" (008-002).
