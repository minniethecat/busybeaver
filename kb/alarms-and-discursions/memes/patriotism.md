## Overview

Patriotism enters Chesterton's *Alarms and Discursions* in his critique of the Futurist manifesto (Essay 16). It appears not as a concept Chesterton himself expounds at length, but as one of several traditional values that the Futurists claimed to champion while Chesterton regarded their whole programme with ironic detachment.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Quoting the Futurist manifesto, Chesterton notes that the Futurists "will 'glorify war — the only true hygiene of the world — militarism, patriotism, the destructive gesture of Anarchism, the beautiful ideas which kill, and the scorn of woman'" (016-006). The passage is part of Chesterton's bemused response to F. T. Marinetti's 1909 declaration. Chesterton's own attitude toward patriotism — as a virtue requiring careful definition and grounding in real love of place — is only implicit here, but his skepticism toward the Futurists' overheated rhetoric is unmistakable.

## Significance

The Futurists' appropriation of patriotism illustrates a recurring Chestertonian theme: the way modern movements often adopt traditional virtues while evacuating them of content. For Chesterton, genuine patriotism is rooted in the particular — in love of a specific place with its specific customs — not in the abstract glorification of national power.
