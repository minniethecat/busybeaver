# Twilight

**Twilight** appears in Chesterton's essays as a liminal, transitional state between day and night, reality and imagination. He evokes 'a stormy twilight' with 'some such smell of death' (003-001), notes how we have 'underestimated the swiftness of twilight and the suddenness of night' (004-002), and describes the 'twilight condition' of legendary tales that hover between fact and fable (015-006).

## In Chesterton's Essays

Twilight for Chesterton is a philosophical category as much as a meteorological one. In "The Nightmare," "a stormy twilight" carries "some such smell of death" (003-001), setting the stage for his meditation on terror and the modern imagination. Twilight is the hour when the rational mind loosens its grip.

In "The Telegraph Poles," he observes that we have "underestimated the swiftness of twilight and the suddenness of night" (004-002). The warning is epistemological: transitions that seem gradual in retrospect are, in experience, abrupt. One moment it is day; the next, it is not.

The richest treatment comes in "The Gold of Glastonbury," where Chesterton describes the "twilight condition" of legendary tales (015-006). Such stories exist neither in full daylight of fact nor in the darkness of pure fiction. They occupy the intermediate zone where the imagination does its most important work.

In "The Three Kinds of Men," Chesterton plays with twilight as cliché and as truth. Everyone says that "twilight is sad and sentimental" (023-002), but the banality of the observation does not make it false. For Chesterton, the task is to recover the genuine mystery that cliché has encrusted.
