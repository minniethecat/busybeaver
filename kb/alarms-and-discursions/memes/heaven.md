# Heaven

**Heaven** appears in Chesterton's essays as a theological and imaginative concept. He leaves "the angels and the arches" to others in describing Gothic art (001-011), describes a ruin so strangely lit that "it looked like the abode of angels" (007-004), and evokes the "walls of heaven" as distant and high (017-003).

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton invokes heaven not as abstract doctrine but as an imaginative presence in the material world. In "On Gargoyles," he urges: "Let us point to heaven with tusks and horns and fins" (001-007). The gargoyle, grotesque and heavenward-pointing, embodies his conviction that transcendence runs through the earthbound and the strange.

In "The Nightmare," heaven becomes a vertiginous question: "If that was heaven, what in the name of Davy Jones is hell?" (003-002). The apocalyptic sky unsettles the very categories of salvation and damnation. Throughout the essays, heaven is less a destination than a direction — the upward glance that restores proportion to human things.
