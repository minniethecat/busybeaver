Chesterton consistently defends the fable as a mode of truth-telling that rationalism cannot replicate. Throughout his essays, the fable — along with the romance and the fairy tale — is presented as a vehicle of moral and metaphysical insight.

## The Fable as Moral Truth

In "A Drama of Dolls" (essay 05), Chesterton refers to "this grim but not unwholesome fable of Dr. Faustus" (005-002) — the Faust legend as a fable that conveys truths about pride and damnation more effectively than any treatise.

> This grim but not unwholesome fable of Dr. Faustus, with its rebuke to the mere arrogance of learning, is sound and stringent enough; but it is not a fair sample of the mediaeval soul at its happiest and sanest.
>
> (005-002) A fable, for Chesterton, is not a lie but a condensation of truth into narrative form. In "The Furrows" (essay 11), he writes of "giving the moral before the fable" (011-004) — a characteristically Chestertonian inversion that suggests the moral is not an afterthought but the very substance of the story.

## Fable Against Rationalism

In "The New House" (essay 21), Chesterton attacks those who "take refuge in the revolting fable that everything is mind" (021-001) — here using "fable" ironically for the rationalist's fiction of a world without material reality. In "Ethandune" (essay 30), he describes a Celt as "a fable on the right side" (030-003) — a legend that serves truth rather than falsehood. And in "The Long Bow" (essay 35), he exclaims: "let us have a romance or a fable or a fairy tale" (035-003) — the fable as liberation, as the imagination's rightful dominion.

> Let us change the subject; let us have a romance or a fable or a fairy tale.
>
> (035-003)
