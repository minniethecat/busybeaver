# Folk Culture

## Overview

Though Chesterton rarely uses the term "folk culture" directly, the concept pervades *Alarms and Discursions*. He is everywhere the champion of the popular, the customary, the orally transmitted, the locally rooted — everything that the Edwardian intellectual class dismissed as mere "superstition" or "provincialism." For Chesterton, folk culture is not a primitive stage to be outgrown but the permanent foundation of civilisation: "It is the peasants who preserve all traditions of the sites of battles or the building of churches" (012-007). The folk are the carriers of memory, and their culture — songs, festivals, local cheeses, puppet shows — embodies truths that philosophy can only gesture toward.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton's most vivid celebration of folk culture appears in his treatment of food. The essay "Cheese" (Essay 9) is at once a comic tour de force and a serious argument about the relationship between locality and culture. Cheese, for Chesterton, is the exemplary folk product: "It is ancient — sometimes in the individual case, always in the type and custom" (009-001). Unlike soap, which is "sent automatically all over the world" in identical form, cheese varies from valley to valley: "If the Grand Lama has soap it is Smith's Soap…I fancy the Grand Lama does not eat cheese (he is not worthy), but if he does it is probably a local cheese, having some real relation to his life and outlook" (009-006). The contrast is between the dead uniformity of industrial civilisation and the living variety of folk culture — the same argument Chesterton makes elsewhere about architecture, art, and politics.

The concept of custom as a repository of wisdom runs throughout. In "The Appetite of Earth" (Essay 7), Chesterton contrasts the "rich and gigantic vision" of folk culture with the thinness of commercial modernity. The "custom of Modern Society" (009-006) that the waiter invokes to justify serving cheese with biscuits rather than bread is, for Chesterton, not a custom at all — it is a fashion, arbitrary and rootless. True custom, by contrast, is "directly derived" from nature and the accumulated wisdom of generations.

"The Surrender of a Cockney" (Essay 2) offers a portrait of the folk spirit in the figure of the Cockney who ends his days "in a village, in the character of the Village Idiot" (002-002). The Cockney, for Chesterton, is the urban bearer of folk culture — the man who carries locality with him, whose love of place is as fierce and irrational as any peasant's. The essay "The Philosophy of Sight-seeing" (Essay 12) extends this theme: the tourists whom intellectuals despise are participating in a kind of folk pilgrimage, and their instinct to visit the scenes of great events is "the peasants who preserve all traditions" in modern dress.

"A Drama of Dolls" (Essay 5) describes a puppet-play of Faust performed in a Yorkshire village, "exactly as our fathers saw it five hundred years ago" (005-001). The dolls are "at once comic and convincing; but if you cannot at once laugh at a thing and believe in it, you have no business in the Middle Ages." Folk culture, for Chesterton, is characterised by this dual capacity: to laugh and to believe simultaneously, to hold the comic and the sacred in a single gesture. Modern culture, by contrast, forces a choice — either irony or credulity, never both.

In "The Furrows" (Essay 11), Chesterton connects folk memory to the physical landscape: the cornfields remind him of "the peasants who preserve all traditions of the sites of battles" (012-007). The land itself is a kind of text, inscribed with the memory of the folk who have worked it. This is not the romantic pastoralism of the Georgian poets but a democratic claim: the common people, not the landowners, are the true custodians of the countryside and its meanings.

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period saw the emergence of systematic folklore studies (Frazer's *The Golden Bough*, the founding of the Folklore Society) and the first wave of the English folk-song revival led by Cecil Sharp. But these were typically antiquarian or salvage operations — collecting the remnants of a supposedly dying culture. Chesterton's approach is radically different: he treats folk culture not as a survival to be archived but as a living force to be defended and extended. His distributism, with its emphasis on small property and local autonomy, is essentially a political programme for the restoration of folk culture against the centralising forces of industrial capitalism and the bureaucratic state.

## Chesterton's Position

For Chesterton, folk culture is not an aesthetic preference but a democratic necessity. The songs, customs, cheeses, and puppet-shows of the people are the infrastructure of liberty: they encode a world in which human scale, local knowledge, and inherited wisdom matter more than efficiency, uniformity, and expertise. When Chesterton defends the Cockney's love of London, the peasant's memory of battlefields, or the cheese-maker's refusal of industrial standardisation, he is defending the same thing: the right of ordinary people to be the authors of their own culture rather than its passive consumers. Folk culture is democracy in its cultural form.
