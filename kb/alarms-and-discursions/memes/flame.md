# Flame

**Flame** appears in Chesterton's essays as an image of arrested intensity and destruction. He describes turrets that "stand up like immobile flames" in the Red Town (010-009), contrasts the "painted flames of Croydon" with Glastonbury's quieter beauty (018-004), and muses that "York Minster covered with flames might happen to be quite as beautiful" as in normal state (030-003).

## In Chesterton's Essays

The image of flame in Chesterton's prose is characteristically paradoxical: it suggests both violent destruction and a kind of frozen ecstasy. In "The Red Town," turrets "stand up like immobile flames" (010-009), as if the architecture had been caught in the act of burning and preserved at the point of its greatest intensity.

In "The Glory of Grey," Chesterton contrasts the quiet beauty of Glastonbury with "the painted flames of Croydon" (018-004). The phrase is layered: Croydon's suburban red brick is literally flame-coloured, but "painted flames" suggests artifice, a fire that consumes nothing. The real Glastonbury, in its grey restraint, burns brighter.

The destructive register surfaces in "Ethandune," where Chesterton speculates that "York Minster covered with flames might happen to be quite as beautiful as York Minster covered with snow" (030-003). The thought is deliberately shocking, but the point is aesthetic: beauty is not identical with preservation.

In "The High Plains," a wine glass "held-up abruptly against a flame" reveals that "red wine is red" (037-005). Flame here serves as the test of authenticity, the light by which things show their true colour. For Chesterton, flame is what reveals, even as it consumes.

## Notable Passages

> ...as if it were better to warm one's hands at the ashes of Glastonbury than at the painted flames of Croydon.

> (018-004)
