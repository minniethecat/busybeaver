## Biography

Benjamin Disraeli, 1st Earl of Beaconsfield (1804–1881), was a British statesman, Conservative Prime Minister, and novelist. Born to a Jewish family of Italian descent, he was baptized into the Church of England at age twelve. Disraeli served twice as Prime Minister (1868, 1874–1880) and was a central architect of modern British Conservatism, combining a romantic vision of tradition with social reform. He was also a prolific novelist whose works explored political and social themes.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton references Disraeli twice in *Alarms and Discursions*, each time with a distinct critical dimension.

In Essay 26 ("The Strangeness of Luxury"), Chesterton groups Disraeli's novels with those of Ouida as examples of literary luxury that carry a hallucinatory quality:

> All romances that deal in extreme luxury, from the "Arabian Nights" to the novels of Ouida and Disraeli, have, it may be noted, a singular air of dream and occasionally of nightmare. (026-007)

In Essay 37 ("The High Plains"), the reference is more pointedly political. Describing the historical sweep of Eastern imperialism — "all that evil insolence... that rolled into Europe in the youth of Nero" — Chesterton traces its modern manifestation:

> ...after having been battered about and abandoned by one Christian nation after another, turned up in England with Disraeli and was christened (or rather paganed) Imperialism. (037-001)

The parenthetical "or rather paganed" is a sharp judgment on Disraeli's imperial policies, suggesting that Empire under Disraeli revived a pre-Christian spirit of domination.

## Key Works / Contributions

- **Sybil, or The Two Nations** (1845) — social novel of the "Condition of England" question
- **Coningsby** (1844) — political novel of the Young England movement
- Reform Act of 1867 — significant expansion of the electoral franchise
- Purchase of Suez Canal shares (1875) — strategic imperial investment
- Created the title "Empress of India" for Queen Victoria (1876)

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton's treatment of Disraeli is ambivalent but leans critical. As a distributist and anti-imperialist, Chesterton views Disraeli's association with Empire as a corruption — the "paganing" of Imperialism under a nominally Christian nation. The literary reference in Essay 26 is less hostile: Disraeli's novels are simply catalogued among works of exotic luxury. But taken together, the two references suggest Chesterton saw Disraeli as a figure who aestheticized power — turning politics into romance and empire into exotic fantasy, both moves Chesterton distrusted. The coupling of "Ouida and Disraeli" is particularly telling: the trashy sensationalist and the Prime Minister are literary equals in Chesterton's taxonomy of luxury.
