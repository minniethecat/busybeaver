## Overview

Napoleon appears in Chesterton's "A Drama of Dolls" (Essay 5) as an illustration of a characteristic Chestertonian insight: that we tend to remember historical figures and periods only by their decline, not by their vitality.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton writes: "We always think of him as a fat old despot, ruling Europe with a ruthless military machine. But that, as Lord Rosebery would say, was only 'The Last Phase'; or at least the last but one. During the strongest and most startling part of his career, the time that made him immortal, Napoleon was a sort of boy, and not a bad sort of boy either, bullet-headed and ambitious, but honestly in love with a woman, and honestly enthusiastic for a cause, the cause of French justice and equality" (005-002).

## Significance

Chesterton uses Napoleon to make a broader argument about historical memory: "It is very unfortunate that we so often know a thing that is past only by its tail end. We remember yesterday only by its sunsets." This applies equally to Napoleon, the Middle Ages, and — by implication — to any historical phenomenon reduced to its final decadence.
