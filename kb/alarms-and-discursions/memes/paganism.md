## Chesterton's Uses of "Pagan"

G.K. Chesterton uses the word "pagan" and its cognates across at least five essays in *Alarms and Discursions*, in three distinct but related senses: as healthy natural enjoyment, as the pre-Christian cultural inheritance, and as a spirit of pride opposed to Christian humility.

## Pagan as Healthy Enjoyment

In "The Red Town" (Essay 10), Chesterton describes the impulse toward "pure outbreaks of pagan enjoyment to which all healthy men have often been tempted" (010-007). This is the pagan as the natural human desire for levity, colour, and festivity — a mood he describes as wanting "to have levity on a large scale." The rowdy impulse to paint the whole city scarlet, rather than merely one door green, captures this essentially pagan gigantism of pleasure.

> The rowdy young man is not content to paint his tutor's door green: he would like to paint the whole city scarlet.
>
> (010-007)

He contrasts this innocent pagan recklessness with the more sinister modern form of imperial pride, noting that "the Jingo who wants to admire himself is worse than the blackguard who only wants to enjoy himself" (010-007). The pagan joy-seeker at least acts from honest impulse; the imperialist clothes the same impulse in self-flattery.

Chesterton returns to this theme shortly afterward, speaking of "ordinary pagan recklessness and pleasure seeking" (010-008), linking it to the image of filling the world with one's private folly and the surprising philosophical depth of the choice of the colour red.

## Pagan Cultural Inheritance

In "The Wrath of the Roses" (Essay 14), Chesterton makes a striking theological claim about the rose: it is "Christian in the one absolute and primordial sense of Christian — that it comes down from the age of pagans" (014-007). The rose appears in Greek, Latin, Provençal, Gothic, Renaissance, and Puritan poetry — a continuous thread from pagan antiquity through Christian civilization.

> The rose can be seen, and even smelt, in Greek, Latin, Provencal, Gothic, Renascence, and Puritan poems.
>
> (014-007)

This is the pagan as cultural ancestor: not something to be rejected but something to be absorbed and transformed. The continuity from pagan to Christian is, for Chesterton, a mark of the genuine universality of Christian culture.

## The Pagan Period in Art

In "The Wheel" (Essay 28), Chesterton discusses a church window depicting an angel on a bicycle — a work of "florid Renascence outline" that "belongs to the highly pagan period which introduced all sorts of objects into ornament" (028-001). The Renaissance, in his view, reintroduced pagan motifs and sensibilities into Christian art, sometimes to the point of absurdity. He treats this with characteristic humour rather than condemnation, noting that "personally I can believe in the bicycle more than in the angel" (028-001).

## Pagan as Imperial Pride

Chesterton's most pointed use of "pagan" comes in "The High Plains" (Essay 37), where he traces the Eastern imperial tradition — "all that evil insolence that rolled into Europe in the youth of Nero" — through history until it "turned up in England with Disraeli and was christened (or rather paganed) [[imperialism|Imperialism]]" (037-001). The coinage "paganed" is deliberately provocative: imperialism is not a Christian project but a reversion to pagan despotism, to the world of "white elephants and painted horses" and "dreadful mounted bowmen of the moving empires of the East."

Here "pagan" is not the healthy enjoyment of Essay 10 nor the cultural inheritance of Essay 14, but the antithesis of Christian political order — the spirit of conquest, self-worship, and domination.

## The Pagan Slave

A minor but evocative use appears in "The New House" (Essay 21), where Chesterton declares he is delighted to see his part of England built over, "so long as I am not myself built over, like a pagan slave buried in the foundations of a temple" (021-004). The image draws on the ancient practice of foundation sacrifice and serves as a metaphor for the individual crushed by modern urban development.

## Synthesis

Across these essays, Chesterton's paganism is not a single concept but a field of related meanings. The healthy pagan impulse toward enjoyment is good in itself but can be corrupted into pride. The pagan cultural inheritance — the rose, classical art, the Renaissance — is genuinely valuable and has been absorbed into Christendom. But the pagan political spirit — imperial conquest, self-worship, the despotism of the East — stands opposed to everything Chesterton values in Christian civilization.
