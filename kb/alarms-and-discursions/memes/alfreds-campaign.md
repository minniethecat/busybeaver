## Overview

In January 878, the Viking leader Guthrum launched a surprise winter attack on the West Saxon kingdom of Wessex, seizing the royal estate at Chippenham. King Alfred was driven into the marshes of the Somerset Levels, where he regrouped at the island fortress of Athelney. From this refuge, Alfred rallied the fyrd (militia) of Somerset, Wiltshire, and Hampshire, and in May 878 decisively defeated Guthrum's army at the Battle of Ethandune. The victory preserved Anglo-Saxon England as a Christian kingdom and led to the Treaty of Wedmore, under which Guthrum accepted baptism.

For Chesterton, this campaign was not merely a historical event but a founding myth of English civilization—a David-and-Goliath story in which the small, the obscure, and the faithful triumphed over overwhelming force.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Alfred haunts the landscape of *Alarms and Discursions*. Chesterton never narrates the campaign directly, but his presence flickers through the closing essays like firelight on a shield.

In "A Romance of the Marshes" (Essay 39), Chesterton visits Athelney itself, walking "the flat fenland round the Island of Athelney, which is now an island in the fields and no longer in the waters" (039-003). The stone still stands marking "that embattled islet in the Parrett where King Alfred held his last fort against the foreign invaders, in that war that nearly washed us as far from civilization" as the Danes threatened (039-003). The approach to the island—the "low long road like an interminable white string," the toll gate charging threepence—seems to Chesterton saturated with the memory of Alfred's desperate winter (039-004).

The landscape itself conspires in the memory of the siege. "Everything in that strange countryside seemed to be lying low, as if to avoid the incessant and rattling rain of the Danish arrows" (039-005). The "rock of Athelney" stands "as isolate as it was to Alfred"—a small, stubborn fact in a flat world (039-005).

In Essay 30 ("Ethandune"), Chesterton meditates on the battlefield itself and on the figure of Alfred the Great. He notes that "Alfred was defeated continually" before his final victory—a point of immense importance for Chesterton's philosophy of persistence (030-003). The White Horse of Uffington, carved into the Berkshire Downs, serves as the essay's central image: a vast, primitive figure that Chesterton links to Alfred's victory and, through it, to the deep continuity of English civilization.

In Essay 34 ("The White Horses"), Chesterton returns to the White Horse and the "hiding-places of Alfred the Great," treating the landscape of Wessex as a palimpsest of memory—every hill and covert bearing the impress of the ninth-century struggle (034-002).

## Historical Account

The campaign of 878 was the crisis of Alfred's reign. Guthrum's winter attack caught the West Saxons unprepared. Alfred, with a small retinue, withdrew to the Somerset Levels—a vast wetland offering natural defense. The story of Alfred burning the cakes while in disguise as a peasant, though likely legendary, captures the extremity of his situation.

From Athelney, Alfred sent word to the shire fyrds to assemble at Egbert's Stone in early May. The combined forces marched to Ethandune (probably Edington in Wiltshire), where they met Guthrum's army in a pitched battle. Alfred's victory was decisive. Guthrum withdrew to Chippenham, and after a two-week siege, surrendered and agreed to baptism.

The Treaty of Wedmore established the boundary between Alfred's Wessex and the Danish-controlled Danelaw, and Guthrum's conversion marked the beginning of the Christianization of the Danish settlers. The campaign secured the survival of an independent Anglo-Saxon kingdom and made possible the eventual unification of England under Alfred's descendants.

## Chesterton's Interpretation

For Chesterton, Alfred's campaign is the archetypal story of English civilization: the great thing done by a small thing in an obscure place. Athelney is not a grand battlefield but a nearly invisible island in the marshes. Alfred is not a conquering emperor but a fugitive king who "was defeated continually" before he finally won.

This pattern—the triumph of the small, the persistence of the defeated, the decisive importance of the obscure—runs through all of Chesterton's thought. It appears in his defense of the grotesque gargoyle over the classical statue, of the ordinary man over the Superman, of the local pub over the grand restaurant. Alfred's campaign is the historical instantiation of the Chestertonian principle: the stone the builders rejected becomes the cornerstone.

Chesterton's interest in Alfred would later culminate in *The Ballad of the White Horse* (1911), his epic poem on Alfred's victory. The essays of *Alarms and Discursions*, written just before the poem, can be read as a prose reconnaissance of the same imaginative territory.


## From the Essays


> Over a vast dim region of England this dark name of Ethandune floats like an eagle doubtful where to swoop and strike, and, indeed, there were birds of prey enough over Ethandune, wherever it was. But now Ethandune itself has grown as dark and drifting as the black drifts of the birds. And yet without this word that you cannot fit with a meaning and hardly with a memory, you would be sitting in a very different chair at this moment and looking at a very different tablecloth.
>
> (030-003)
