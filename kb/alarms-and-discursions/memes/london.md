# London

## Overview

London is the gravitational centre of Chesterton's imaginative geography in *Alarms and Discursions*. It appears not as a mere setting but as a living presence — the home of the Cockney, the destination of the country-dweller's longing, and the inexhaustible subject of Chesterton's affection. More than any other place in these essays, London embodies the democratic vitality that Chesterton celebrates.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton's most passionate declaration comes early, in "The Surrender of a Cockney." Leaving London for the countryside, he bursts out: "how beautiful London is!" (002-003). He has written verses celebrating the city — "'O Wild West End, thou breath of London's being'" — and confesses that even the great English poets are secondary to the city itself: "Chaucer and Spenser and Milton and Dryden lived in London; Shakespeare and Dr. Johnson came to London because they had had quite enough of the country" (002-004).

The Cockney's love for London is not fashion but instinct. "You hunger and thirst after the streets; you think London the finest place on the planet" (002-004). Chesterton insists that this is not a sophisticated preference but a natural one: "The real rustic does think London the finest place on the planet." He quotes a country labourer: "I believe that the streets of London are paved with gold; and I mean to see it before I die" (002-004).

Chesterton is clear about his own position: "I am on the side of the man who lives in the country and wants to go to London. I abominate and abjure the man who lives in London and wants to go to the country" (002-005). This is the philosophy of the Cockney: London is the object of desire, the place you go *to*, not the place you leave.

London appears again in "The Red Town" through the Nelson Column and its "gallons of red paint" (010-009), and in "The Philosophy of Sight-seeing," where Chesterton notes that you can pass through London a thousand times and never see it — "Go through London only as the shortest way between Croydon and Hampstead, and the Nelson Column will (for the first time in your life) remind you of Nelson" (012-011).

## Historical Significance

Edwardian London was the capital of the world's largest empire, a city of over six million undergoing rapid transformation — electric lighting, motor omnibuses, the first underground lines. The contrast between the ancient city Chesterton loved and the modernising metropolis created the productive tension that animates many of these essays.

## Chesterton's Connection

Chesterton was a Londoner by adoption and by conviction. Born in Kensington and educated at St Paul's School, he lived most of his life in or near London. His famous persona — the bulky figure in cape and crumpled hat, hailing a hansom cab — is inseparable from the London streetscape. The London of *Alarms and Discursions* is not the tourist's London of monuments and museums but the living city of cabs, pubs, telegraph poles, and ordinary streets — a city that belongs to its inhabitants.
