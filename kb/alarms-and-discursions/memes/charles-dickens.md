# Charles Dickens

## Biography

Charles Dickens (1812–1870) was the preeminent novelist of the Victorian era, author of *Oliver Twist*, *David Copperfield*, *Bleak House*, *A Christmas Carol*, and many other works that combined social criticism with vivid characterisation. His popularity across all classes of English society made him, for Chesterton, the definitive example of democratic literature.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton references Dickens repeatedly as a benchmark for what literature can and should be. In "The Three Kinds of Men," he classifies Dickens alongside Rabelais as one of the "Poets who wrote in prose" (023-008) — a category Chesterton uses to distinguish writers of vital imagination from mere prose stylists.

The essay "The Flat Freak" considers Dickens's style through the lens of popular reception. Chesterton notes that an "elemental person" — someone innocent of literary fashion — will read Dickens and call it "too silly" (031-004). Rather than defend Dickens against this charge, Chesterton embraces it: the silliness is part of the vitality. When Dickens described a character like Mr. Chuckster, he "was making a fool out of himself" in the best sense — committing fully to the comic vision (031-006).

"The Modern Scrooge" is Chesterton's most extended engagement with Dickens, using *A Christmas Carol* as a test case for what literature can do. He satirises the progressive reformer Mr. Vernon-Smith who, after surveying his "select and even severe library," concludes that Dickens is "very suitable to be read to charwomen" (036-001). The condescension is the point: Dickens's accessibility is treated as a failing by the intellectual class, when in fact it is his greatest virtue.

Chesterton pushes the irony further by having Vernon-Smith explain that "Dickens was not a writer of the first rank, since he lacked the high seriousness of Matthew Arnold" (036-002). Throughout, Chesterton defends Dickens precisely for reaching the charwomen — for being literature that ordinary people actually read and love.

## Key Works / Contributions

Dickens's major novels include *The Pickwick Papers* (1836–37), *Oliver Twist* (1837–39), *David Copperfield* (1849–50), *Bleak House* (1852–53), *A Tale of Two Cities* (1859), and *Great Expectations* (1860–61). His Christmas books, especially *A Christmas Carol* (1843), established enduring popular traditions.

Beyond his fiction, Dickens pioneered a model of the novelist as public figure — campaigning for social reform, editing periodicals, and performing public readings. Chesterton himself would later write a critical biography, *Charles Dickens* (1906), which shaped Dickens's twentieth-century reputation.

## Chesterton's View

Chesterton's admiration for Dickens is unreserved and foundational. He sees Dickens as proof that democracy and literature are natural allies. The qualities that literary snobs dismiss — exaggeration, sentiment, comic caricature — are for Chesterton the very marks of imagination working at full power.

This view is not mere nostalgia. Chesterton argues that Dickens's "low" qualities — his appeal to charwomen, his willingness to be silly — are philosophically superior to the "high seriousness" of his critics. The Dickensian grotesque, like the Gothic gargoyle, includes more of reality than classical restraint ever could.
