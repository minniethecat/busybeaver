# Humanity

**Humanity** in Chesterton's essays is portrayed as restless, ordinary, and wondrous. He notes that "only in our own romantic country" does humanity refuse to "settle down into contentment or despair" (018-001), celebrates "ordinary human life" as "dark and barbaric... only not as beautiful" as a forest (021-005), and explores the limits of the human in his essay on the Superman (020-004).

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton's view of humanity is democratic and anti-utopian. In "Dukes," he celebrates "the old rustic life of humanity" against the bloated pretensions of aristocracy (017-007). The virtues he prizes are not those of the exceptional individual but of the ordinary person living an ordinary life.

He is sceptical of scale. In "The New House," he observes that "it is not humanity that disgusts us in the huge cities; it is inhumanity" (021-003). The problem of modernity is not too many people but too little regard for what makes people human: neighbourhood, proportion, and personal relationship.

In "The Strangeness of Luxury," Chesterton warns that for "humanity as a whole, there is little doubt that luxury makes men materialistic" (026-005). The danger of wealth is not that it corrupts individuals but that it degrades the human capacity for wonder and gratitude.

The theme reaches its fullest expression in "The Chorus," where Chesterton imagines "the deafening chorus of everlasting humanity" (038-006) rising against tragedy. Humanity for Chesterton is not a collection of solitary souls but a choir, whose individual voices gain their meaning only in harmony.
