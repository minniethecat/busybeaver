## Overview

Futurism — the Italian avant-garde movement launched by Filippo Tommaso Marinetti in 1909 — receives a dedicated essay in *Alarms and Discursions*: "The Futurists" (essay-16). For Chesterton, Futurism is not merely one more artistic school but the purest distillation of everything he finds wrong with modernity: its contempt for the past, its glorification of violence and speed, its rejection of the ordinary human affections in favor of the machine. The essay is at once a close reading of the Futurist manifesto and a broader polemic against the idea that destruction is a form of creation.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton opens "The Futurists" (essay-16) by describing a warm October evening and his encounter with "the Declaration of Futurism" (016-001). The manifesto is printed "in enormous letters" (016-002), and Chesterton proceeds to unpack it with a mixture of genuine curiosity and satirical incredulity. He quotes the Futurist principles directly: courage, audacity, revolt; the rejection of all past art; the celebration of "the slap and the blow of the fist" (016-002).

His central critique is that Futurism, for all its bluster about the future, is philosophically empty. "Suffice it to say that Futurism has a gratifying dislike" of many things (016-005), he notes wryly, but it offers nothing positive in their place. The movement defines itself entirely by what it opposes — tradition, museums, the past, tenderness, domesticity — and this purely negative posture means it can never build anything. "I think the only other point of Futurism" (016-006), he concludes, is contained in a sentence that reduces to mere sensation-seeking.

Chesterton also observes that Futurism is not nearly as novel as it claims. Its contempt for the past is itself a well-worn tradition — the tradition of iconoclasm. And its celebration of war and violence, far from being a daring break with convention, echoes the oldest and darkest impulses of human nature dressed in modern rhetoric.

## Historical Context

Marinetti's "Futurist Manifesto" was published in *Le Figaro* on February 20, 1909. It proclaimed the beauty of speed ("a racing car is more beautiful than the *Victory of Samothrace*"), the glorification of war ("the world's only hygiene"), and the desire to "destroy museums, libraries, academies of every kind." By 1910, when Chesterton's essay appeared, Futurism had become a European sensation. The movement would later align itself with Italian Fascism — a trajectory that arguably vindicates Chesterton's early suspicion that a philosophy built on destruction and violence had nowhere else to go.

## Chesterton's Position

Chesterton does not dismiss Futurism as mere foolishness. He takes it seriously enough to engage with its actual text, recognizing it as a symptom of a genuine cultural pathology. His objection is not to the idea of the future — he is himself a writer deeply concerned with the future of civilization — but to the Futurist premise that the future requires the annihilation of the past. For Chesterton, tradition is not a weight that drags down creativity; it is the soil in which creativity grows. "The past is not a dead thing," he writes elsewhere; "it is not even past." A movement that defines itself by smashing museums has, by definition, nothing to put in their place.

> A brave man ought to ask for what he wants, not for what he expects to get. A brave man who wants Atheism in the future calls himself an Atheist; a brave man who wants Socialism, a Socialist; a brave man who wants Catholicism, a Catholic. But a weak-minded man who does not know what he wants in the future calls himself a Futurist.
>
> (016-009)
