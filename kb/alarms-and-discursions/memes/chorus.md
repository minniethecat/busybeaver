## The Decline of Popular Song

Chesterton opens his essay "The Chorus" with a lament: the decline of singing in chorus marks "the gradual disappearance in our time of the habit of true popular sympathy." In the old democracy, a man was simply told to sing and he did; in the new aristocracy of the drawing-room, a lady is asked whether she sings — as if singing were a specialized art rather than a communal act. Chesterton prefers the atmosphere of the old banquets, where middle-aged gentlemen "explained that they would never forget old days or friends with a rumpty-iddity-iddity." (038-001)

## The Greek Chorus and the Gods

The chorus serves a purpose beyond asserting the popular element in the arts. "The chorus of a song, even of a comic song, has the same purpose as the chorus in a Greek tragedy. It reconciles men to the gods. It connects this one particular tale with the cosmos and the philosophy of common things." The chorus provides what Chesterton calls "windows opened in the house of tragedy; momentary glimpses of larger and quieter scenes, of more ancient and enduring landscapes." (038-002)(038-003)

## The Berkshire Tragedy and the Jolly Refrain

Chesterton illustrates his point with the old ballad "The Berkshire Tragedy," about a murder committed by a jealous sister for which a miller is hanged. The chorus runs: "And I'll be true to my love / If my love'll be true to me." This "very reasonable arrangement" is introduced not as flippancy but as a "throw back to the normal, a reminder that even 'The Berkshire Tragedy' does not fill the whole of Berkshire." The chorus gazes "out beyond the particular complication to more open plains of peace... past the drowning maiden and the miller's gibbet, and sees the lanes full of lovers." (038-003)

## Against Modern Intensity

This use of the chorus to humanize and dilute a dark story is "strongly opposed to the modern view of art. Modern art has to be what is called 'intense.' It is not easy to define being intense; but, roughly speaking, it means saying only one thing at a time, and saying it wrong." Modern tragic writers write short, purely painful stories; when they attempt long books of unrelieved gloom, "the reading public began to rebel and to demand the recall of romance." (038-004)(038-006)

## The London Tragedy Has No Chorus

Chesterton draws his sharpest contrast: "The Berkshire tragedy had a chorus; but the London tragedy has no chorus." He acknowledges that "glimpses of the gloom of our civilization ought to be recorded" — the bewilderments of the solitary and sceptical soul should be preserved "for the pity (yes, and the admiration) of a happier time." But he wishes "that at the end of each chapter of stiff agony or insane terror the choir of humanity could come in with a crash of music and tell both the reader and the author that this is not the whole of human experience." (038-006)

## The Eternal Chorus of Mankind

Chesterton closes with the story of Blessed Brother Giles, a follower of St. Francis of Assisi. When theology students asked him whether he believed in free will and how to reconcile it with necessity, Giles "reflected a little while and then seized a fiddle and began capering and dancing about the garden, playing a wild tune and generally expressing a violent and invigorating indifference." The tune is not recorded, but Chesterton declares it "the eternal chorus of mankind, that modifies all the arts and mocks all the individualisms, like the laughter and thunder of some distant sea." (038-007)
