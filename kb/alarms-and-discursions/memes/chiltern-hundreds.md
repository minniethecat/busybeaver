# Chiltern Hundreds

## Overview

The Stewardship of the Chiltern Hundreds is one of the most peculiar institutions of the British constitution — an ancient office, now entirely nominal, that serves as the legal mechanism by which a Member of Parliament resigns. Since MPs cannot technically resign, they apply for the stewardship, which disqualifies them from sitting. In Chesterton's essay, this constitutional curiosity becomes the starting point for a meditation on taking one's official duties seriously — even the absurd ones.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton's essay "The Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds" begins with a subversive thought: "that I should like to go into Parliament, quarrel with my party, accept the Stewardship of the Chiltern Hundreds, and then refuse to give it up" (024-001). The humour lies in treating the stewardship — a deliberate sinecure — as a real job with real responsibilities.

He explains the constitutional anomaly for his readers: "We are so proud in England of our crazy constitutional anomalies that I fancy that very few readers indeed will need to be told about the Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds" (024-002). The ritual is both absurd and beloved — exactly the combination Chesterton finds most revealing.

The fantasy unfolds: one goes to the Prime Minister, concealing one's fatigue, and declares, "'It has been the ambition of my life to be Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds.' The Prime Minister then replies, 'I can imagine no man more fitted both morally and mentally for that high office'" (024-002). The comedy comes from treating the fiction as fact.

But Chesterton's deeper point is serious. The original duty of the Steward was "to put down the outlaws and brigands in that part of the world" (024-003). The office has become a joke because nobody performs it. What if someone actually did? "After all we only need a Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds who really understands cats and thieves" (024-004).

The essay closes on a note of comic resignation: "it will not be done: for the governing class either does not care, or cares very much, for the criminals, and as for me, I had a delusive opportunity of being Constable of Beaconsfield (with grossly inadequate powers), but I fear I shall never really be Steward of the Chiltern Hundreds" (024-005).

## Historical Account

The Chiltern Hundreds are ancient administrative divisions of Buckinghamshire — Stoke, Desborough, and Burnham. The stewardship originated in the medieval period when the Chiltern Hills were infested with robbers and the Crown appointed a steward to suppress them. By the 17th century, the office had become a sinecure. In 1751, John Willes MP first used the device of accepting the stewardship to resign his seat, establishing a precedent that continues today.

## Chesterton's Interpretation

Chesterton's treatment of the Chiltern Hundreds is a perfect example of his method: take a trivial constitutional oddity, treat it with complete seriousness, and discover in it a profound truth about human nature. The essay is comic on its surface but carries a real argument: institutions decay not because they are inherently absurd but because people stop performing them with genuine commitment.

The Chiltern Hundreds become, in Chesterton's hands, a parable about the gap between official function and actual practice — a theme that runs throughout his political thought. The cure for absurd institutions is not abolition but authenticity: do the job, even if the job seems ridiculous.
