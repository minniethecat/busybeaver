## Overview

The Declaration of Futurism (Futurist Manifesto) was published by the Italian poet Filippo Tommaso Marinetti on 20 February 1909 in the Paris newspaper *Le Figaro*. It proclaimed a new artistic movement that rejected the past, celebrated speed, technology, violence, and youth, and called for the destruction of museums and libraries. The manifesto was translated and circulated internationally, reaching G.K. Chesterton in England, who devoted the sixteenth essay of *Alarms and Discursions* ("The Futurists") to a sustained and hilarious demolition of its claims.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton receives the Declaration by post on a warm October evening while watching pigs being turned out of his garden — a detail that immediately establishes the comic disproportion between the manifesto's bombast and the settled English reality it invades:

> (016-001) It was a warm golden evening, fit for October, and I was watching (with regret) a lot of little black pigs being turned out of my garden, when the postman handed to me, with a perfunctory haste which doubtless masked his emotion, the Declaration of Futurism.

The essay proceeds to quote the manifesto clause by clause and subject each to Chestertonian scrutiny. The Futurists proclaim: "Literature having up to now glorified thoughtful immobility, ecstasy, and slumber, we wish to exalt the aggressive movement, the feverish insomnia, running, the perilous leap, the cuff and the blow." Chesterton's response is characteristically deflationary: the Futurist in his motor-car performs the aggressive movement, but it is the pedestrians who do the running and the perilous leap.

> (016-002) It is headed "Declaration of Futurism" in enormous letters; it is divided off with little numbers; and it starts straight away like this: "1. Literature having up to now glorified thoughtful immobility, ecstasy, and slumber, we wish to exalt the aggressive movement, the feverish insomnia, running, the perilous leap, the cuff and the blow."

Chesterton identifies two core errors in the Futurist programme. First, it is economically exclusive: "you cannot be a Futurist at all unless you are frightfully rich" — the motor-car, the aeroplane, the "beauty of speed" are the luxuries of a wealthy elite. Second, it is philosophically incoherent: to reject all tradition is to have no standard by which to judge anything. The Futurists, Chesterton suggests, do not even know what they want the future to be:

> (016-009) But a weak-minded man who does not know what he wants in the future calls himself a Futurist.

The essay is Chesterton at his most effective as a satirist: he takes an opponent's own words, reads them literally, and lets the absurdity speak for itself. The pigs in the garden are not incidental — they represent the enduring, comic, and healthy reality that the Futurists, in their worship of the machine, have forgotten.

## Historical Significance

The Futurist Manifesto was one of the founding documents of twentieth-century modernism. It influenced movements from Vorticism in England to Constructivism in Russia, and its celebration of speed, violence, and the machine had a complex relationship with the rise of Italian Fascism (Marinetti later became associated with Mussolini's regime). Chesterton's 1910 response was among the earliest and most penetrating English-language critiques of Futurism. Where many critics dismissed the manifesto as mere nonsense, Chesterton took it seriously enough to expose its internal contradictions — particularly the tension between its rhetoric of liberation and its economic dependence on the wealthy elite.

## Chesterton's Connection

Chesterton had no personal connection to Marinetti or the Futurist circle. His interest was journalistic and philosophical: the Futurist Manifesto was news, and it was also an extreme statement of a tendency Chesterton had been fighting his entire career — the worship of novelty for its own sake, the rejection of tradition without understanding what tradition is. The essay "The Futurists" is part of a larger Chestertonian project: the defence of the ordinary, the comic, and the democratic against the grandiosities of artistic and political elites.
