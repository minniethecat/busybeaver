# King Arthur

## Biography

King Arthur is the legendary British leader who, according to medieval histories and romances, led the defence of Britain against Saxon invaders in the late 5th and early 6th centuries. The historical existence of Arthur is debated, but his legend — developed through Geoffrey of Monmouth, Chrétien de Troyes, and Sir Thomas Malory — made him one of the central figures of Western medieval literature. The Arthurian cycle encompasses the Round Table, the quest for the Holy Grail, and the tragic love of Lancelot and Guinevere.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Arthur appears in Chesterton's essay [[Ethandune]] (Essay 30), in a passage that defines the stakes of the Dark Age struggle between civilization and barbarism. Chesterton writes that the varied chiefs of that era "were remembered or forgotten according to how they had resisted this almost cosmic raid. Nobody thought of the modern nonsense about races; everybody thought of the human race and its highest achievements. Arthur was a Celt, and may have been a fabulous Celt; but he was a fable on the right side" (030-003).

Chesterton places Arthur alongside Charlemagne and Alfred the Great as a figure who "fought for the tradition against the barbarians, the nihilists." The passage is notable for its explicit rejection of racial thinking — "the modern nonsense about races" — in favour of a civilizational criterion: what matters is not Arthur's ethnic identity but that he stood for order against chaos.

## Chesterton's View

For Chesterton, Arthur exemplifies a recurring theme throughout *Alarms and Discursions*: the defence of human civilization against the forces that would destroy it. Arthur's legendary status does not diminish his importance; on the contrary, Chesterton suggests that being "a fable on the right side" is itself a form of historical reality — a truth conveyed through myth rather than documentation.

This reading aligns with Chesterton's broader philosophy in essays such as "The Gold of Glastonbury" and "The Wings of Stone," where medieval legend and Gothic architecture are celebrated as expressions of a healthy, popular imagination. Arthur belongs to what Chesterton elsewhere calls "the democracy of the dead" — tradition as an extension of the franchise to those who came before.

## Key Works / Contributions

- Central figure of the Matter of Britain, the body of medieval literature concerning the legendary history of Great Britain
- Subject of foundational works by Geoffrey of Monmouth (*Historia Regum Britanniae*), Chrétien de Troyes, and Sir Thomas Malory (*Le Morte d'Arthur*)
- Symbol of chivalric ideals and Christian kingship in the Western literary tradition
