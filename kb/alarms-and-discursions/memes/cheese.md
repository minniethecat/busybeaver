## Overview

Chesterton's essay "Cheese" is one of the most beloved pieces in *Alarms and Discursions* — a comic tour de force that uses the humble foodstuff as a lens through which to examine civilization, locality, poetry, and the deadening effects of industrial standardization. What begins as mock-scholarly complaint about "The Neglect of Cheese in European Literature" (009-001) becomes a serious argument that cheese embodies the principle of healthy variety against unhealthy uniformity, and that the modern world's treatment of cheese — cutting it into "contemptibly small pieces" and serving it with biscuits — is a symptom of a larger civilizational malaise.

## In Chesterton's Essays

The essay "Cheese" (essay-09) is the primary source, but the theme also appears in "The Appetite of Earth" (essay-07), where Chesterton develops his broader philosophy of eating as a metaphor for apprehending solid reality.

In "The Appetite of Earth," Chesterton argues that the solidity of things can only be fully grasped through the metaphor of eating: "To express the cubic content of a turnip, you must be all round it at once. The only way to get all round a turnip at once is to eat the turnip" (007-003). He extends this to the famous phrase about the moon being made of green cheese, which he accepts "above reason, but not contrary to it" — though he demurs on the greenness, having observed the moon in many cheese-like colors but never green (007-003). He catalogs these lunar cheeses: "I have seen it look exactly like a cream cheese... I have seen it look very like a Dutch cheese, rising a dull red copper disk amid masts and dark waters at Honfleur. I have seen it look like an ordinary sensible Cheddar cheese in an ordinary sensible Prussian blue sky" (007-004).

In "Cheese" proper, Chesterton opens with the mock-grandiose announcement of his "forthcoming work in five volumes, 'The Neglect of Cheese in European Literature'" (009-001). He surveys the poetic record and finds it nearly empty — Virgil mentions cheese but "with too much Roman restraint. He does not let himself go on cheese" — except for the anonymous nursery rhyme that envisions a world where "all the trees were bread and cheese," which Chesterton calls "a rich and gigantic vision of the higher gluttony" (009-001).

The essay pivots from comedy to social criticism through a personal anecdote. Traveling across England, Chesterton ate cheese in four different counties and found each one distinct: "In each inn the cheese was good; and in each inn it was different. There was a noble Wensleydale cheese in Yorkshire, a Cheshire cheese in Cheshire, and so on" (009-004). From this he derives a principle of civilization:

> "Both the good and bad civilization cover us as with a canopy, and protect us from all that is outside. But a good civilization spreads over us freely like a tree, varying and yielding because it is alive. A bad civilization stands up and sticks out above us like an umbrella — artificial, mathematical in shape; not merely universal, but uniform." (009-004)

He contrasts cheese with soap: "If the Red Indians have soap it is Smith's Soap. If the Grand Lama has soap it is Brown's soap. There is nothing subtly and strangely Buddhist, nothing tenderly Tibetan, about his soap" (009-005). But cheese, produced locally from local milk, carries the signature of its place: "You are not approaching Nature in one of her myriad tints of mood, as in the holy act of eating cheese" (009-005).

The essay closes with comic outrage. Arriving at a large northern restaurant, Chesterton is served cheese "cut up into contemptibly small pieces" and — the final insult — biscuits instead of bread. He addresses the waiter in "warm and moving terms," asking "who he was that he should put asunder those whom Humanity had joined" (009-006). The joke is perfect because the principle is real: the divorce of cheese from bread is a miniature of the divorce of man from local soil.

> "I asked him if, when he said his prayers, he was so supercilious as to pray for his daily biscuits." (009-006)

## Historical Context

Chesterton's cheese essay belongs to a tradition of English comic essays that use food as a vehicle for social commentary — a tradition running from Charles Lamb's "A Dissertation upon Roast Pig" through to the Edwardian period. But his argument has a specifically anti-imperial and anti-industrial edge. The "whisky and soda at every outpost of the Empire" (009-005) is, for Chesterton, a false universalism that obliterates local character, while cheese represents a true universalism that expresses itself through local variation. This is the Distributist sensibility that would become more explicit in Chesterton's later political writing: the conviction that small-scale, local production is healthier — morally, aesthetically, and spiritually — than centralized industrial manufacture.

## Chesterton's Position

Chesterton's defense of cheese is, beneath the comedy, a defense of what might be called sacramental locality — the idea that material things, when rooted in a particular place and tradition, become bearers of meaning that mass-produced substitutes cannot replicate. Cheese is holy because it varies from valley to valley; soap is profane because it is everywhere the same. The essay's comic method — the mock-epic treatment of a humble subject — is itself an argument: the ordinary things of life deserve the same serious attention we give to grand subjects, because they are the actual texture of human existence. His final outburst against biscuits is funny precisely because it is proportionate: small indignities, accumulated, become a real spiritual deprivation.
