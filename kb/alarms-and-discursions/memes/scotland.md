## Overview

Scotland appears in Chesterton's "The Glory of Grey" (Essay 18) as part of a humorous catalogue of international climates, all of which are found wanting compared to English weather.

## In Chesterton's Essays

In his rhapsodic defence of English weather, Chesterton compares the climates of various nations: "In Scotland and Ireland you have rain, either thick or thin" (018-001).

The observation is part of a larger taxonomy: France has "much sun and some rain," Italy has "hot winds and cold winds," America has "hells of heat and cold," and the Tropics have "sunstrokes varied by thunderbolts." Only England, Chesterton insists, has the "strictly romantic thing called Weather; beautiful and changing as a woman."

## Significance

Scotland serves in Chesterton's argument as a foil for English exceptionalism in matters meteorological — a characteristically playful instance of his rhetorical method, in which a national stereotype is deployed in the service of a larger aesthetic point.

## Notable Passages

> In Scotland and Ireland you have rain, either thick or thin. ... But all these you have on a broad and brutal scale, and you settle down into contentment or despair. Only in our own romantic country do you have the strictly romantic thing called Weather; beautiful and changing as a woman.

> (018-001)
