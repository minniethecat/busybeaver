## Overview

In *Alarms and Discursions*, G.K. Chesterton's treatment of dukes and the aristocracy is characteristically paradoxical. Far from mounting a straightforward political critique of the peerage, he finds in the Duke a spiritual and romantic figure — the last representative of an older, more adventurous England.

## In Chesterton's Essays

The Duke appears most prominently in the essay "Dukes" (Essay 17), where Chesterton recounts a motoring excursion with the Duke of Aylesbury, "perhaps the largest landowner in that part of the country" (017-002). When told that the Duke's land begins at a certain point, the essay unfolds into a surprising defence of aristocracy.

Chesterton finds himself defending the idea of aristocracy against a progressive friend. The Duke of Aylesbury, who had "a clouded recollection" of certain political matters (017-005), is portrayed with genuine affection. "The elder Duke laughed heartily" at the conversation (017-006), and when Chesterton asks him whether aristocracy is not opposed to the spirit of Merry England, the Duke responds with "a noise in his throat, and a kind of knot in his sturdy forehead" (017-007).

Earlier in the collection, in "The Man and His Newspaper" (Essay 6), Chesterton reveals his ambivalence toward the peerage through a cockney voice: "They may laugh at Dukes; I'd like to see them 'alf as good as a dook" (006-007). The speaker defends not aristocratic privilege but the human being who happens to hold a title.

## Chesterton's Position

Chesterton's defence of aristocracy is aesthetic and spiritual rather than political. He declares: "I mean the idea of aristocracy. I regard this as the last stronghold of aristocracy" (017-002). For him, the aristocrat represents a human type that democracy ought to cherish rather than destroy.

Yet he insists that "the spirit of aristocracy is essentially opposed to the spirit of Merchandise" (017-007), aligning the Duke with the peasant and the craftsman against the capitalist. This is the core of Chestertonian distributism: the alliance of all who value the human against all who value the mechanical.
