The dragon recurs in Chesterton's essays as a symbol of the mythopoeic imagination — the capacity to see the world as still populated by marvels. It is the monster slain by the primeval hero, the flaming steed of legend, the golden wonder in the sky.

## Dragon and the Primeval Hero

The book opens with Chesterton contemplating a fallen gargoyle that looked "like the head of some huge dragon slain by a primeval hero" (001-001). This image sets the tone for the entire collection: the dragon is what the hero confronts, and dragonslaying is the archetypal human action — courage facing the monstrous. The dragon is not merely a threat but an opportunity for heroism.

> It lay there, scoured by ancient rains or striped by recent fungus, but still looking like the head of some huge dragon slain by a primeval hero.
>
> (001-001)

## The Dragon as Living Symbol

In "The Strangeness of Luxury" (essay 26), Chesterton writes that if it were "necessary to travel on a flaming dragon, I think he ought to give the" (026-006) — the dragon as a mode of transport as natural as any other, if only the world were not disenchanted. In "The Triumph of the Donkey" (essay 27), he wishes to "rather use it like a flying dragon" (027-001) — the dragon as vehicle of liberated motion, the imagination's alternative to mechanical transport. In "Ethandune" (essay 30), the moon is "like a golden dragon, like the Golden Dragon of Wessex" (030-003) — the dragon as heraldic emblem, woven into English landscape and history.

> The bare grey slopes seemed to rush downhill like routed hosts; the dark clouds drove across like riven banners; and the moon was like a golden dragon, like the Golden Dragon of Wessex.
>
> (030-003)
