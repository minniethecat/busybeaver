# Courage

## Overview

Courage in *Alarms and Discursions* is never reducible to martial valour. Chesterton treats it as a moral and even metaphysical virtue — the capacity to act in the face of uncertainty, to sleep when the world insists on feverish wakefulness, and to maintain fidelity to small things when grand schemes beckon. The charwoman commuting by train, the tourist visiting a tomb, the man who hires rather than buys: each embodies a form of courage the modern world fails to recognise.

## In Chesterton's Essays

Chesterton's most distinctive treatment of courage appears in his inversion of the Futurist manifesto. Marinetti had declared that modern poetry would celebrate "courage, audacity, and revolt" and exalt "the feverish insomnia" (016-003). Chesterton's reply is devastating in its simplicity: "It is only the madman who has not the courage to sleep" (016-010). The Futurists mistake agitation for bravery; true courage is the capacity for rest, for trust in the ordinary rhythms that the speed-worshipper cannot abide.

Ordinary courage is a recurring theme. In "Simmons and the Social Tie," Chesterton describes a charwoman's daily life through the lens of "the courage with which she supports the misfortunes of the train" (008-001) — a commuter's fortitude elevated to moral significance. This is not condescension but recognition that most courage is unspectacular: getting on the train again tomorrow, enduring the small indignities of modern life without collapse into bitterness.

The moral courage to live modestly appears in "The Strangeness of Luxury." Chesterton's "ideal hero would own his horse, but would have the moral courage to hire his motor" (026-006). The formulation is carefully balanced: owning a horse is traditional and modest; hiring a car requires the courage to participate in modernity without being owned by it. Fairy tales, Chesterton adds, "are the only sound guidebooks to life" (026-006) — they teach the courage of adventure without the pride of possession.

Chesterton extends the concept to intellectual courage. In "Five Hundred and Fifty-five," he notes how numbers, however arbitrary, can supply "an invisible reinforcement of common courage and common sense" (029-002) — the mere fact of a majority vote emboldens people to act. This is courage-by-institution, the democratic faith that collective decision-making supplies a form of moral fortification.

The tourists Chesterton defends in "The Philosophy of Sight-seeing" embody a quieter courage. "I reverence those great men who had the courage to die," he writes; "I reverence also these little men who have the courage to live" (012-002). The tourist visiting Nelson's death-scene is not a profane intruder but a participant in the transmission of moral memory, and the act of caring about the past is itself a form of courage — the courage to be interested in something beyond oneself.

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period valorised military courage through imperial adventure literature (Kipling, Henty, Newbolt) while simultaneously celebrating technological daring — the motor-car, the aeroplane, the speed-record. Chesterton stood apart from both. He was deeply suspicious of imperial adventure (which he saw as bullying dressed in uniform) and of technological bravado (which he saw as a substitute for moral courage). His recovery of domestic and democratic courage — the charwoman, the tourist, the sleeper — was a deliberate counter-tradition.

## Chesterton's Position

For Chesterton, courage is not the absence of fear but the presence of fidelity — to a person, a place, a principle. The Futurist's "courage" is really impatience; the imperialist's "courage" is really domination. True courage, in Chesterton's hands, becomes one of the democratic virtues: available to everyone, exercised daily, and almost entirely invisible to the theorists who proclaim its absence. The charwoman commuting to Battersea performs more genuine acts of courage each morning than the self-proclaimed superman performs in a lifetime.

> It is a platitude, and none the less true for that, that we need to have an ideal in our minds with which to test all realities. But it is equally true, and less noted, that we need a reality with which to test ideals.
>
> (008-001)
