## Chesterton's Use of Babylon

G.K. Chesterton references Babylon in two distinct modes across *Alarms and Discursions*: as the historical ancient city, subject of decadent poetry and symbol of pagan grandeur; and as a satirical metaphor for the follies of modern urban life.

## Babylon as Ancient City

In "The Nightmare" (Essay 3), Chesterton describes reading a decadent poem "about the old gods of Babylon and Egypt, about their blazing and obscene temples, their cruel and colossal faces" while sitting in his garden at twilight (003-001). The poem, with its "fragrant and suffocating smoke," conjures visions of "painted palaces huge, headless idols and monstrous solitudes of red or golden sand."

His thesis is striking: these ancient gods and cities must be treated as **toys**, not idols. The imagination may wander freely among them, but they must never be taken seriously as objects of worship:

> Man, a gigantic child, must play with Babylon and Nineveh, with Isis and with Ashtaroth. By all means let him dream of the Bondage of Egypt, so long as he is free from it.
>
> (003-001)

For Chesterton, the ability to treat pagan antiquity as a playground for the imagination — rather than as a source of terror or worship — is a mark of Christian sanity. "His central sanctities, his true possessions, should be Christian and simple." The old gods are dolls, not idols.

## Babylon as Modern Satire

Chesterton's second use of Babylon is comic. In "Cheese" (Essay 9), after a pilgrimage through four countryside public-houses enjoying honest bread and cheese, he arrives at a fashionable restaurant in a northern city. The waiter serves cheese "cut up into contemptibly small pieces" and — horrifyingly — biscuits instead of bread:

> I was sharply reminded that I had entered Babylon, and left England behind.
>
> (009-006)

Here Babylon stands for everything pretentious and anti-traditional in modern society: the replacement of honest English bread with fashionable biscuits, the violation of "the sanctity of the ancient wedding between cheese and bread." Chesterton's mock-heroic indignation — "I asked him who he was that he should put asunder those whom Humanity had joined" — turns the restaurant into a miniature Babylon, a place of exile from the true England of bread-and-cheese simplicity.

## The Two Babylons

The two uses are connected by a common Chestertonian theme: the contrast between the simple and the pretentious, the Christian and the pagan, the honest and the fashionable. The literal Babylon of antiquity is a magnificent toy for the imagination; the metaphorical Babylon of the modern restaurant is a target for satire. In both cases, Chesterton's allegiance is to the "old plain things of poetry and piety" — bread, the cross, the English countryside — against the glamour of empires ancient and modern.
