## Overview

In Chesterton's imaginative vocabulary, "miracle" does not typically denote a supernatural interruption of natural law so much as a sudden perception of the wonder inherent in familiar things. The word appears twice in *Alarms and Discursions*, both times in "The Surrender of a Cockney" (Essay 2), where it describes the shock of seeing the ordinary from an unfamiliar angle.

## In Chesterton's Essays

In "The Surrender of a Cockney," Chesterton imagines a London omnibus transplanted to a rural landscape: "if by some miracle a Bayswater omnibus could come down a white country road" (002-004), it would reveal both the strangeness of the city and the strangeness of the country. Later, he envisions a rural observer seeing London from a distance "as the miracle of man that it is" (002-006). In both cases, miracle is not the opposite of the ordinary but a mode of seeing the ordinary truly.

## Significance

Chesterton's use of "miracle" reflects his broader philosophical commitment to wonder as a cognitive virtue. The miraculous is not what violates nature but what reveals nature's inherent strangeness — a theme that runs throughout his work, from *Orthodoxy* to the Father Brown stories.
