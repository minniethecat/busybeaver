# Journalism

**Journalism** was Chesterton's own profession and a recurring subject of his social criticism. In "The Man and His Newspaper" (essay-06), he satirizes the Edwardian press, imagining a "cheap imperial organ" called the Daily Wire that exemplifies everything wrong with modern journalism (006-002). Chesterton himself was one of the great journalists of his age.

## Chesterton as Journalist

Though corpus references to journalism as an abstract concept are few, the whole of "The Man and His Newspaper" (essay-06) is a sustained satire of Fleet Street. Chesterton knew the trade from the inside. He wrote for the [[Daily News]], contributed regularly to the *Illustrated London News*, and produced an enormous body of newspaper prose throughout his career.

The essay's critique is directed not at journalism as such but at what Chesterton saw as its corruption: the "cheap imperial organ" (006-002) that substitutes sensation for truth and propaganda for reporting. Against this he set an ideal of journalism as honest witness, the craft of seeing things as they are and telling the reader what you saw.

## Notable Passages

> The fragments of futile journalism or fleeting impression which are here collected are very like the wrecks and riven blocks that were piled in a heap round my imaginary priest of the sun. They are very like that grey and gaping head of stone that I found overgrown with the grass. Yet I will venture to make even of these trivial fragments the high boast that I am a medievalist and not a modern.
>
> (001-011)
