## Overview

A [[estaminet|bar]]rage was a sustained artillery [[artillery|bombardment]] designed to suppress enemy defenses, cut [[barbed-wire|wire entanglements]], and protect advancing infantry. In the [[trench|trench warfare]] of World War I, the [[estaminet|bar]]rage was the essential tactical tool that made infantry attacks possible—without it, men crossing no-man's-land faced intact machine-gun positions and uncut [[estaminet|bar]]bed [[barbed-wire|wire]]. Holmes's memoir records several types of [[estaminet|bar]]rage: the preparatory [[estaminet|bar]]rage that preceded an attack, the creeping barrage that advanced ahead of infantry, and the smashing [[estaminet|bar]]rage that accompanied major offensives. His descriptions capture both the devastating power of well-executed [[estaminet|bar]]rages and the despair of finding that a [[estaminet|bar]]rage had failed to do its work.

## In the Trenches

For the infantryman, the [[estaminet|bar]]rage was a double-edged reality. When it worked, it flattened defenses and saved lives. Holmes describes reaching the German lines after a successful [[estaminet|bar]]rage: "the German front line [[trench|trenches]] had been flattened by our barrage fire" (003-073). The psychological boost of advancing behind a wall of [[shell|shells]] was considerable—men felt shielded, protected by the curtain of fire moving ahead of them.

But [[estaminet|bar]]rages could fail. Holmes records a [[trench-raid|raid]] where there had "been no [[estaminet|bar]]rage" (007-092), leaving the attackers exposed and the operation in chaos. A failed or absent [[estaminet|bar]]rage meant men walked into intact defenses—a near-certain death sentence. The coordination required between artillery and infantry was immense; timing had to be exact, and communication was primitive. When the [[estaminet|bar]]rage lifted too early or fell too short, the consequences were immediate and lethal.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes's most detailed description of a [[estaminet|bar]]rage comes during a major attack:

> The barrage fire kept up right up to zero [003-052]

This simple statement captures the precision required—the [[estaminet|bar]]rage continued until the exact moment of the infantry advance, creating a protective wall that lifted at precisely the right second.

He also describes the creeping barrage, a more sophisticated tactic developed as the war progressed:

> A creeping barrage preceded us. Row after row of shells falling ahead [010-091]

The creeping barrage moved forward in stages—typically 100 yards every few minutes—while the infantry followed close behind. The "row after row" image evokes the methodical, industrial nature of the [[artillery|bombardment]]. The tactic required perfect timing; if the infantry moved too fast, they risked walking into their own [[shell|shells]]; too slow, and the [[germans|Germans]] would have time to reman their positions.

For a [[trench-raid|raid]] that went wrong, he notes the stark consequence: "There's no orders for a charge. We've got to go" (007-092)—men compelled to advance even when the supporting [[estaminet|bar]]rage had been canceled.

The destructive power is most visible in the aftermath: "the German front line [[trench|trenches]] had been flattened by our barrage fire, but we had to go over and occupy them" (003-073). The [[estaminet|bar]]rage had done its job, but the infantry still had to cross the shell-cratered ground and occupy the ruins.

## Military Context

The [[estaminet|bar]]rage evolved significantly over the course of World War I. Early in the war, [[estaminet|bar]]rages were relatively short and targeted—a brief [[artillery|bombardment]] before a straightforward infantry advance. By 1916–1917, the period of Holmes's service, [[estaminet|bar]]rages had become massive, multi-day affairs. The [[battle-of-the-somme|Battle of the Somme]] (1916) saw a week-long preliminary [[estaminet|bar]]rage involving over 1.5 million [[shell|shells]].

The creeping barrage was a critical innovation, first used by the [[france|French]] at the [[battle|Battle]] of Verdun and later adopted by the British. Instead of lifting all at once (which gave the defenders time to reman their positions), the [[estaminet|bar]]rage moved forward in increments, keeping the defenders pinned down until the attacking infantry was almost upon them. Holmes's references to [[estaminet|bar]]rages "preceding" attacks and keeping up "right up to [[zero]]" reflect this evolving tactical doctrine.

Barrage fire also served a psychological purpose—the sustained [[artillery|bombardment]] was intended to break the enemy's morale as much as his physical defenses. The sound alone—described by Holmes as a continuous roar—was a weapon in itself.
