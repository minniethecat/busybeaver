## Overview

A ration party was a group of soldiers assigned to transport food, water, and supplies from rear-area dumps and field [[equipment|kit]]chens to the men holding the front-line [[trench|trenches]]. The original book's glossary defines it succinctly: "A party of men which goes to the rear and brings up [[trench-rations|rations]] for the front line." In Holmes's narrative, ration parties were a regular feature of [[daily-life-in-the-trenches|trench routine]] — essential, unglamorous, and often deadly. The men who drew this duty faced enemy [[shell|shelling]] and [[sniper]] fire both going and returning, making it one of the more hazardous non-[[battle|combat]] assignments in [[trench|trench warfare]].

## In the Trenches

Holmes describes the ration party as a nightly custom: "It was the custom for a ration party to go out each night and get up the grub. This party had to go over the duck walk and was under fire both going and coming." (007-253) The duck walk — a raised wooden pathway through the mud — was exposed to German observation and fire, making every ration run a gamble.

The duty was rotated among the men, but the strain of repeated exposure could provoke protest. Holmes recounts how "one of the corporals who had been out on [[trench-rations|rations]] two nights in succession began to 'grouse'" (007-253), a complaint that resulted in Holmes being sent in his place. This serendipitous reassignment likely saved Holmes's life.

The ration party's function extended beyond mere food delivery. On one occasion, Holmes's party "came across a Brigade Dump" — a supply station for companies to draw [[trench-rations|rations]], ammunition, and other necessities. Finding it unguarded, they helped themselves: "We found two cases of [[rum-ration|rum]], four gallons in a jar" (012-166). The discovery provided a stock of [[rum-ration|rum]] that would sustain Holmes and his comrades through a desperate isolated stand in a [[dug-out]] days later.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes's most dramatic experience with a ration party came during the [[mine-explosion|mining]] of the Pimple — a German underground explosion that devastated a section of the British line. He had been sent on the ration party in place of the g[[rum-ration|rum]]bling corporal, making the three-mile trek along the duck walk: "I did the three miles along the duck walk with the ration party, and there wasn't a shell came our way. Queer! Nor on the way back. Queerer!" (007-266)

Returning from this eerily quiet journey, Holmes halted the party and sensed the tension in the air: "There was an electric tension in the very air. You could tell by the feel that something big was going to happen." (007-275) Moments later, German mines detonated, tearing away the whole side of the Pimple. The aftermath was brutal: "The corporal whose place I had taken on the ration party was [[casualty|killed]] by falling stones. Inasmuch as he was where I would have been, I considered that I had had a narrow escape from 'going west!'" (007-298)

This episode illustrates the arbitrary nature of survival in the [[trench|trenches]] — a duty reassignment, born of a corporal's g[[rum-ration|rum]]bling, made the difference between life and death.

## Military Context

Ration parties were essential to the logistics of [[trench|trench warfare]]. Front-line positions were not self-sufficient; all food, water, ammunition, and other supplies had to be brought forward from support areas. The [[british-army|British Army]]'s supply chain ran from base depots through brigade and battalion dumps, with the final leg — from the dump to the individual soldier — carried out by ration parties under cover of darkness.

The danger of the duty reflected the broader truth of [[trench|trench warfare]]: there was no truly safe place. Communication [[trench|trenches]] and [[duck-walk|duck walks]], though rearward of the fire trench, were routinely shelled and swept by [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]]. Ration parties moved at night to reduce visibility, but German [[very-light|flare]]s ("[[very-light|Very lights]]") could expose them at any moment, inviting fire. The duty was considered fatiguing and risky enough that men were rotated through it, but the insatiable demands of supply meant someone was always on the duck walk.
