# Billet

## Overview

When soldiers were rotated out of the front-line [[trench|trenches]] for rest, they were quartered in billets — lodgings in [[france|French]] villages behind the lines. [[billets|Billets]] could be anything from a [[estaminet|bar]]n loft to a requisitioned house, and their quality varied wildly. What they all offered was distance from the constant threat of death, and that made even the worst billet a relative paradise.

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] describes billets with a mixture of gratitude and complaint — grateful for the break from the [[trench|trenches]], but honest about the squalor of some accommodations.

## In the Memoir

Holmes's first experience of billets came even before reaching the front:

> Next night we were billeted at Barlin
> — (002-011)

After his first [[trench-raid|raid]], the contrast between trench and billet was stark:

> out of the lines that night and in billets got hot food
> — (003-151)

Hot food — a rarity in the [[trench|trenches]] — was one of the chief comforts of billet life. Soldiers could cook, wash, and sleep without the constant backdrop of shellfire.

Not all billets were pleasant. Holmes is blunt about the conditions at one location:

> The worst feature of these billets was the rats. They were the
> — (004-044)

Rats were a universal plague — they infested [[trench|trenches]], [[dug-out]]s, and billets alike. But by comparison with the front, even rat-infested billets had their compensations.

Some billets were genuinely comfortable:

> we had real billets
> — (004-095)

Holmes also notes the social dimension of billet life — the proximity to [[france|French]] civilians, especially young women:

> There was a pretty mademoiselle
> — (004-059)

These encounters provided a tenuous connection to normal life and civilization, something the [[trench|trenches]] completely denied.

## The Billet Routine

In billets, soldiers resumed something approaching a normal schedule: morning drill and inspection, equipment maintenance in the afternoon, free time in the evening. Holmes describes the contrast:

The routine of billet life was essential to maintaining morale. Soldiers could write letters [[blighty|home]], play cards, and — for a few days at least — pretend they were not at war.
