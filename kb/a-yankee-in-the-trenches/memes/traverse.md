## Overview

A traverse was an angled bend or offset built into a trench line, designed to limit the effects of shell bursts, enfilade fire, and grenade blasts. By breaking the trench into short, zigzag sections, traverses prevented a single shell or burst of [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]] from traveling the entire length of the trench. In Holmes's account, traverses appear as the defining geometry of trench life—the corners around which men fought, fled, and died. They provided cover in [[battle|combat]], created distinct social spaces, and shaped how men moved, communicated, and survived in the forward [[trench|trenches]].

## In the Trenches

Holmes's memoir is filled with movement along and around traverses. The trench was not a single straight line but a series of connected bays, each separated by a traverse—a wall of earth or [[sandbag|sandbags]] extending from the front parapet partway across the trench, forcing anyone moving along it to weave through a series of sharp turns.

In [[battle|combat]], the traverse was both shield and trap. A man could duck around a traverse to escape [[shrapnel|shell fragments]] or grenade blasts—but he could also run into an enemy coming the other way around the same corner. Holmes describes racing "along the traverses" (003-101) during an attack, and encountering a German at close [[billets|quarters]] around "the corner of the next traverse" (003-116). These corners were the most dangerous points in the trench—unexpected meetings at point-blank range, with no time for tactics, only reflex.

Traverses also functioned as landmarks and gathering points. Men would take cover "into the next traverse" (009-182) when under fire, or receive orders from an NCO who "ran around the corner of a traverse" (009-192). The traverse created micro-geographies within the trench—"a crooked bit of traverse" (012-109) might be a place of relative safety or a known danger spot.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes provides detailed [[battle|combat]] descriptions centered on traverses. During a [[raid|trench raid]], the [[battle|action]] unfolds through a series of traverse encounters:

> We started to race along the traverses. We found a machine gun [003-101]

The rapid movement through the zigzag trench, rounding corner after corner, creates a sense of disorienting, compartmentalized [[battle|combat]]—each traverse a new room in a lethal corridor.

A close-[[billets|quarters]] encounter follows:

> Turning the corner of the next traverse, I saw Jerry drop his rifle [003-116]

The traverse corner was the ultimate meeting point—two men, from opposing armies, suddenly face to face with no warning and no cover. Holmes's matter-of-fact description captures the brutal intimacy of trench [[battle|fight]]ing.

Even in quieter moments, the traverse was central to trench life. Holmes seeks shelter "into the next traverse, where I sank" (009-182) exhausted; the traverse offered not just physical protection but a psychological refuge—a corner to hide behind when the strain became overwhelming.

In sector descriptions, he notes [[trench|trenches]] "connected by the ruined traverses" (012-104) and mentions [[machine-gun|machine guns]] placed on "all-around traverse" (016-146)—a mounting that allowed full rotation, the traverse serving dual purposes of defense and weapon emplacement.

## Military Context

Traverses were a standard feature of trench design on the [[france|Western Front]], specified in all armies' engineering manuals. The British trench manual prescribed traverses at intervals of 15 to 30 feet, with each traverse extending at least two-thirds of the way across the trench width. This created a characteristic zigzag pattern when viewed from above—a necessary compromise between the defensive value of straight firing lines and the life-saving protection of broken lines.

Traverses also served a [[gas-warfare|gas]]-protection function. Poison [[gas-warfare|gas]], being heavier than air, tended to pool in low areas; traverses helped contain [[gas-warfare|gas]] clouds within individual bays, giving men time to put on masks before the [[gas-warfare|gas]] seeped around the corner. Holmes's references to "the traverses" being "connected up by tubes" (003-014) in the context of [[gas-warfare|gas]] cylinders suggests the complex infrastructure woven through these defensive structures.

The German approach to traverses differed slightly—German [[trench|trenches]] often used more substantial traverses with deeper [[dug-out|dugout]]s built into them, reflecting their generally more defensive posture. British and [[france|French]] traverses were more utilitarian, designed for the shorter occupancy expected before the next offensive.
