## Overview

The fire step was the raised ledge or platform built into the forward wall of a World War I trench, allowing soldiers to stand high enough to fire over the parapet and observe the ground ahead. It was the soldier's station—where he stood watch, cleaned his rifle, awaited orders, and faced the enemy. In Holmes's account, the fire step appears as a site of both routine activity and intense experience: a place where men sat drinking tea, stood guard in the dark, endured [[artillery|bombardment]], and prepared for the moment of going "[[over-the-top|over the top]]."

## In the Trenches

Holmes introduces the fire step in a moment of relative calm: "We sat on the fire step drinking, and I told the corporal" (007-084). This scene captures the dual nature of trench life—the fire step was simultaneously a [[battle|combat]] position and a place of domestic routine. Soldiers would sit on it to eat, drink tea, write letters, and talk, their [[rifle|rifles]] always within reach. The fire step was the soldier's seat at the front-line table.

The physical toll of trench duty is evident in Holmes's account of a man "weak and faint on the fire step. I sat there the rest of the" (009-183) night, suggesting exhaustion, illness, or the lingering effects of shell shock. The fire step became a place of collapse as well as vigilance—a ledge where men's bodies gave out after days of sleeplessness and strain.

In quieter moments, the fire step was a place of maintenance and preparation: "the fire step cleaning my rifle and [[wellsie|Wellsie]]" (013-183), Holmes records, describing the endless cycle of weapon care that occupied soldiers between [[battle|action]]s. Rust, mud, and the corrosive effects of trench conditions meant that [[rifle|rifles]] required constant attention.

The fire step also served as the assembly point before attacks. Holmes describes men "standing shoulder to shoulder on the fire step" (014-065), packed together in the moments before an assault. This was the final position before [[over-the-top|going over the top]]—the place where men gathered their courage, checked their equipment, and waited for the whistle.

## Narrative Examples

The fire step vignettes in Holmes's narrative are notable for their ordinariness. Unlike the dramatic moments of [[artillery|bombardment]] and assault, the fire step scenes depict the texture of everyday trench life: drinking, talking, cleaning, waiting. These passages are among the most valuable in the memoir, preserving the quotidian reality that most war literature overlooks in favor of [[battle|action]].

The scene of the fainting soldier on the fire step is particularly evocative. Holmes's matter-of-fact recording—"weak and faint on the fire step"—contains no drama, no heroism, simply the acknowledgment that a man has reached his physical limit. That he "sat there the rest of the" night suggests both the impossibility of relief and the stoicism with which such limits were accepted.

## Military Context

The fire step was a standard feature of British trench design, typically constructed of wood planks or earth, standing 2 to 3 feet above the trench floor. The trench itself was dug to a depth of 6 to 8 feet, meaning that a man standing on the fire step could see and fire over the parapet while his body remained largely protected. At night or during quiet periods, sentries stood on the fire step observing [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]]; during [[artillery|bombardment]]s, men crouched below it, using the trench wall as cover.

The [[british-army|British Army]]'s trench manual specified that the fire step should be wide enough for a man to stand and move comfortably, with [[duck-walk|duckboards]] on the trench floor below to keep feet out of the water. In practice, fire steps varied enormously in quality—from well-constructed timber platforms in established sectors to c[[rum-ration|rum]]bling earthen ledges in hastily dug or recently shelled positions.
