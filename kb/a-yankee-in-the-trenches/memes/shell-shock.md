# Shell Shock

## Overview

[[shell|Shell]] shock was the term used in World War I to describe the psychological trauma suffered by soldiers exposed to prolonged artillery [[artillery|bombardment]] and the relentless stress of [[trench|trench warfare]]. The condition was poorly understood by the medical establishment of the time, and soldiers suffering from it were often viewed with suspicion — suspected of malingering or cowardice.

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir touches on shell shock with the same matter-of-fact brevity he applies to physical wounds, suggesting it was a common and accepted reality of trench life. Soldiers understood that it could remove a man from the line as effectively as a bullet.

## In the Memoir

Holmes mentions shell shock in the context of soldiers seeking any means of escape from the front:

> or shell shock, or anything that would take
> — (009-095)

This brief aside reveals the soldier's perspective: shell shock was listed alongside wounds and illnesses as a legitimate path out of the [[trench|trenches]] — a testament to how debilitating the condition was understood to be by the men who witnessed it.

Later, Holmes himself was diagnosed with the condition:

> Back in hospital I was found to be suffering from shell shock.
> — (012-305)

His own case appears to have manifested after intense [[battle|combat]]. The symptoms Holmes describes — shortness of breath, racing heart — align with what would now be recognized as acute stress re[[battle|action]] or panic disorder:

> I could only breathe in little gasps. I was still breathing in gasps, and I could feel my heart going in
> — (012-253), (012-270)

## Military Context

At the time of World War I, shell shock occupied a gray zone between medicine and [[trench-discipline|military discipline]]. Some doctors attributed it to physical brain trauma from concussive shell blasts (hence the name); others saw it as a failure of moral fiber. Treatment ranged from rest and recuperation behind the lines to harsh "disciplinary" measures intended to return men to duty.

By the war's end, the scale of shell shock cases forced a gradual medical reassessment. The condition anticipated modern diagnoses of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and [[battle|combat]] stress re[[battle|action]].
