# Stokes Mortar

The **Stokes mortar** — officially the 3-inch Stokes [[trench|Trench]] Mortar — was a simple but revolutionary British weapon that gave infantry units their own portable artillery. Its high-angle fire could drop high-explosive [[grenade|bomb]]s directly into enemy [[trench|trenches]], making it one of the most effective close-support weapons of the war.

## In the Narrative

Holmes mentions Stokes mortars in the context of [[raid|trench raid]]ing equipment, alongside [[grenade|Mills bomb]]s:

> "…of 'Mills' bombs and 'Stokeses.'" [003-082]

The [[grenade|bomb]]s were heavy — eleven pounds of high explosive:

> "…eleven-pound high-explosive 'Stokes' under it. Three…" [003-102]

[[raid|Raid]]ers placed Stokes [[grenade|bomb]]s as demolition charges:

> "…put several of our remaining Stokeses in that and…" [003-114]

Machine [[artillery|guns]] and Stokes mortars were used in combination:

> "…machine guns and were planting Stokeses under…" [003-130]

The glossary entry provides the technical specification:

> "Stokes — A bomb weighing about eleven…" [016-274]

## Design and Innovation

Designed by Sir Wilfred Stokes in 1915, the Stokes mortar was brilliantly simple:

- **Smoothbore tube** on a baseplate — no rifling needed
- **Simple bipod** for elevation adjustment
- **Fired by gravity**: The [[grenade|bomb]] was simply dropped into the tube, striking a fixed firing pin at the bottom
- **Rate of fire**: Up to 25 rounds per minute
- **Range**: Approximately 800 yards (730 meters)
- **Ammunition**: High-explosive, smoke, and [[gas-warfare|gas]] [[grenade|bomb]]s

The Stokes mortar gave [[platoon|infantry platoon]]s their own "pocket artillery" — it could be carried by a crew of two or three men, set up in minutes, and fired from within the trench. No longer did infantry always have to wait for distant artillery batteries to respond. The Stokes could drop [[grenade|bomb]]s with precision into enemy [[trench|trenches]], [[dug-out|dugout]]s, and machine-gun positions.

The design was so successful that it remained the basis for infantry mortars through the 20th century and into the 21st.
