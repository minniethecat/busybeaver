# Marching

## Overview

Before a soldier fired a shot, he marched. Before he saw a trench, he marched. Between [[battle|battles]], he marched. The [[british-army|British Army]] of World War I moved on its feet, and the route march was the common denominator of every soldier's experience — an ordeal of endurance that tested bodies and spirits in equal measure.

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] describes marching throughout his memoir, from his first training in [[england|England]] to the long slogs between sectors in [[france|France]]. The physical memory of marching — sore feet, aching shoulders, the hypnotic rhythm of boots on road — pervades his account.

## The Soldiers' Marching Song

Holmes records a sardonic marching song that his battalion sang to the incongruous tune of "Holy, Holy, Holy":

> Marching, marching, marching,
> Always ruddy well marching.
> Marching all the morning,
> And marching all the night.
> Marching, marching, marching,
> Always ruddy well marching,
> Roll on till my time is up
> And I shall march no more. [008-001–008]

The battalion sang it in full voice as they marched through Amiens:

> We sung it to the tune of "Holy, Holy, Holy", the whole blooming battalion. As we swung down the Boulevard Alsace-Lorraine in Amiens and passed the great cathedral up there to the left, on its little rise of ground… [008-009–011]

The juxtaposition — a hymn of worship repurposed as a soldier's lament about endless marching — captures the irreverent spirit of the men who endured it.

## Marching in Heavy Order

Holmes's descriptions of marching are vivid in their detail of physical misery. The soldiers carried full packs weighing sixty pounds or more: rifle, ammunition, [[trench-rations|rations]], water, entrenching tool, [[gas-mask|gas helmet]], spare clothing. In heavy marching order, progress was agonizingly slow:

> Troops in heavy marching order move very slowly, even with the music — and the hours drag. The ten minutes' rest though goes like a flash. [006-048–050]

The route march followed a ritual rhythm: an hour of trudging, ten minutes of collapse:

> At the end of each hour we came to a halt for the regulation ten minutes' rest. [006-047–048]

## Songs of the March

Music was not merely tolerated on the march — it was actively encouraged by the officers as a morale tool:

> It is always a good deal easier to march when the men sing, as it helps to keep time and puts pep into a column and makes the packs seem lighter. The officers see to it that the mouth organs get tuned up the minute a hike begins. [006-043–046]

Holmes noted a curious cultural detail: the British Tommies preferred American music:

> We began to sing the usual songs of the march and I noticed that the American ragtime was more popular among the boys than their own music. "Dixie" frequently figured in these songs. [006-040–042]

## A Night March to the Front

One of the most atmospheric passages in the memoir describes a night march toward the lines:

> We left Petite-Saens about nine o'clock Friday night and commenced our march for what we were told would be a short hike. It was pretty warm and muggy. There was a thin, low-lying mist over everything, but clear enough above, and there was a kind of poor moonlight. There was a good deal of delay in getting away… [006-001–005]

The sound of the [[artillery|guns]] served as a constant auditory compass:

> The sound of the guns became more distant as we swung away to the south and louder again as the road twisted back toward the front. [006-038–039]

## In the Memoir

Marching is the connective tissue of Holmes's war. It threads through every chapter: the march from training camp to troopship, the march from rest [[billet|billets]] to the front line, the march from one shattered sector to the next. Holmes's descriptions of marching are never merely geographical — they are always physical and psychological. The march is where soldiers sing, where they complain, where they bond, and where they confront the reality that every step forward brings them closer to the [[artillery|guns]].

The route march served multiple purposes: troop movement, physical conditioning, and psychological hardening. Holmes records that even after months at the front, the long march remained one of the soldier's most reliable sources of complaint — which, in the inverted world of the [[trench|trenches]], made it something of a comfort. Complaining about marching was normal; being shelled was not.
