[006-001] We left Petite-Saens about nine o'clock Friday night and commenced

[006-002] our [[marching|march]] for what we were told would be a short hike. It was

[006-003] pretty warm and muggy. There was a thin, low-lying mist over

[006-004] everything, but clear enough above, and there was a kind of poor

[006-005] moonlight. There was a good deal of delay in getting away, and we

[006-006] had begun to sweat before we started, as we were equipped as usual

[006-007] with about eighty pounds' weight on the back and shoulders. That

[006-008] eighty pounds is theoretical weight.

[006-009] As a matter of practice the pack nearly always runs ten and even

[006-010] twenty pounds over the official equipment, as [[british-army|Tommy]] is a great

[006-011] little accumulator of junk. I had acquired the [[souvenir]] craze early

[006-012] in the game, and was toting excess baggage in the form of a [[boche|Boche]]

[006-013] [[tin-hat|helmet]], a mess of shell noses, and a smashed German automatic. All

[006-014] this ran to weight.

[006-015] I carried a lot of this kind of stuff all the time I was in the

[006-016] service, and was constantly thinning out my collection or adding to

[006-017] it.

[006-018] When you consider that a soldier has to carry everything he owns on

[006-019] his person, you'd say that he would want to fly light; but he

[006-020] doesn't. And that reminds me, before I forget it, I want to say

[006-021] something about sending boxes over there.

[006-022] It is the policy of the British, and, I suppose, will be of the

[006-023] Americans, to move the troops about a good deal. This is done so

[006-024] that no one unit will become too much at [[blighty|home]] in any one line of

[006-025] [[trench|trenches]] and so get careless. This moving about involves a good

[006-026] deal of hiking.

[006-027] Now if some chap happens to get a twenty-pound box of good things

[006-028] just before he is shifted, he's going to be in an embarrassing

[006-029] position. He'll have to give it away or leave it. So--send the

[006-030] boxes two or three pounds at a time, and often.

[006-031] But to get back to Petite-Saens. We commenced our hike as it is was

[006-032] getting dark. As we swung out along the once good but now badly

[006-033] furrowed [[france|French]] road, we could see the [[very-light|Very lights]] beginning to go

[006-034] up far off to the left, showing where the lines were. We could

[006-035] distinguish between our own star lights and the German by the

[006-036] intensity of the flare, theirs being much superior to ours, so much

[006-037] so that they send them up from the second-line trenches.

[006-038] The sound of the [[artillery|guns]] became more distant as we swung away to the

[006-039] south and louder again as the road twisted back toward the front.

[006-040] We began to sing the usual songs of the march and I noticed that

[006-041] the American ragtime was more popular among the boys than their own

[006-042] music. "[[dixie|Dixie]]" frequently figured in these songs.

[006-043] It is always a good deal easier to march when the men sing, as it

[006-044] helps to keep time and puts pep into a column and makes the packs

[006-045] seem lighter. The officers see to it that the mouth organs get

[006-046] tuned up the minute a hike begins.

[006-047] At the end of each hour we came to a halt for the regulation ten

[006-048] minutes' rest. Troops in heavy marching order move very slowly,

[006-049] even with the music--and the hours drag. The ten minutes' rest

[006-050] though goes like a flash. The men keep an eye on the watches and

[006-051] "wangle" for the last second.

[006-052] We passed through two ruined villages with the battered walls

[006-053] sticking up like broken teeth and the gray moonlight shining

[006-054] through empty holes that had been windows. The people were gone

[006-055] from these places, but a dog howled over yonder. Several times we

[006-056] passed batteries of French artillery, and jokes and laughter came

[006-057] out of the half darkness.

[006-058] Topping a little rise, the moon came out bright, and away ahead the

[006-059] silver ribbon of the [[souchez|Souchez]] gleamed for an instant; the bare poles

[006-060] that once had been Bouvigny Wood were behind us, and to the right,

[006-061] to the left, a pulverized ruin where houses had stood. [[lieutenant-blofeld|Blofeld]] told

[006-062] me this was what was left of the village of Abalaine, which had

[006-063] been demolished some time before when the French held the sector.

[006-064] At this point guides came out and met us to conduct us to the

[006-065] trenches. The order went down the line to fall in, single file,

[006-066] keeping touch, no smoking and no talking, and I supposed we were

[006-067] about to enter a communication trench. But no. We swung on to a

[006-068] "duck walk." This is a slatted wooden walk built to prevent as much

[006-069] as possible sinking into the mud. The ground was very soft here.

[006-070] I never did know why there was no communication trench unless it

[006-071] was because the ground was so full of moisture. But whatever the

[006-072] reason, there was none, and we were right out in the open on the

[006-073] duck walk. The order for no talk seemed silly as we clattered along

[006-074] the boards, making a noise like a four-horse team on a covered

[006-075] bridge.

[006-076] I immediately wondered whether we were near enough for the Boches

[006-077] to hear. I wasn't in doubt long, for they began to send over the

[006-078] "Berthas" in flocks. The "Bertha" is an uncommonly ugly breed of

[006-079] nine-inch shell loaded with H.E. It comes sailing over with a

[006-080] querulous "squeeeeeee", and explodes with an ear-splitting crash

[006-081] and a burst of murky, dull-red flame.

[006-082] If it hits you fair, you disappear. At a little distance you are

[006-083] ripped to fragments, and a little farther off you get a case of

[006-084] shell-shock. Just at the edge of the destructive area the wind of

[006-085] the explosion whistles by your ears, and then sucks back more

[006-086] slowly.

[006-087] The Boches had the range of that duck walk, and we began to run.

[006-088] Every now and then they would drop one near the walk, and from four

[006-089] to ten [[casualty|casualties]] would go down. There was no stopping for the

[006-090] wounded. They lay where they fell. We kept on the run, sometimes on

[006-091] the duck walk, sometimes in the mud, for three miles. I had reached

[006-092] the limit of my endurance when we came to a halt and rested for a

[006-093] little while at the foot of a slight incline. This was the

[006-094] "Pimple", so called on account of its rounded crest.

[006-095] The Pimple forms a part of the well-known [[vimy-ridge|Vimy Ridge]]--is a

[006-096] semi-detached extension of it--and lies between it and the Souchez

[006-097] sector. After a rest here we got into the trenches skirting the

[006-098] Pimple and soon came out on the Quarries. This was a bowl-like

[006-099] depression formed by an old quarry. The place gave a natural

[006-100] protection and all around the edge were dug-outs which had been

[006-101] built by the French, running back into the hill, some of them more

[006-102] than a hundred feet.

[006-103] In the darkness we could see braziers glowing softly red at the

[006-104] mouth of each burrow. There was a cheerful, mouth-watering smell of

[006-105] cookery on the air, a garlicky smell, with now and then a whiff of

[006-106] spicy wood smoke.

[006-107] We were hungry and thirsty, as well as tired, and shed our packs at

[006-108] the dug-outs assigned us and went at the grub and the char offered

[006-109] us by the men we were relieving, the Northumberland Fusiliers.

[006-110] The dug-outs here in the Quarries were the worst I saw in France.

[006-111] They were reasonably dry and roomy, but they had no ventilation

[006-112] except the tunnel entrance, and going back so far the air inside

[006-113] became simply stifling in a very short time.

[006-114] I took one inhale of the interior atmosphere and decided right

[006-115] there that I would bivouac in the open. It was just getting down to

[006-116] "kip" when a sentry came up and said I would have to get inside. It

[006-117] seemed that [[germans|Fritz]] had the range of the Quarries to an inch and was

[006-118] in the habit of sending over "minnies" at intervals just to let us

[006-119] know he wasn't asleep.

[006-120] I had got settled down comfortably and was dozing off when there

[006-121] came a call for C company. I got the men from my platoon out as

[006-122] quickly as possible, and in half an hour we were in the trenches.

[006-123] [[platoon|Number 10 platoon]] was assigned to the center sector, Number 11 to

[006-124] the left sector, and Number 12 to the right sector. Number 9

[006-125] remained behind in supports in the Quarries.

[006-126] Now when I speak of these various sectors, I mean that at this

[006-127] point there was no continuous line of front trenches, only isolated

[006-128] stretches of trench separated by intervals of from two hundred to

[006-129] three hundred yards of open ground. There were no dug-outs. It was

[006-130] impossible to leave these trenches except under cover of

[006-131] darkness--or to get to them or to get up [[trench-rations|rations]]. They were awful

[006-132] holes. Any [[trench-raid|raid]] by the Germans in large numbers at this time would

[006-133] have wiped us out, as there was no means of retreating or getting

[006-134] up reinforcements.

[006-135] The Tommies called the trenches [[grouse-spots|Grouse Spots]]. It was a good name.

[006-136] We got into them in the dense darkness of just before dawn. The

[006-137] division we relieved gave us hardly any instruction, but beat it on

[006-138] the hot foot, glad to get away and anxious to go before sun-up. As

[006-139] we settled down in our cosey danger spots I heard Rolfie, the

[006-140] frog-voiced baritone, humming one of his favorite coster songs:

[006-141] Oh, why did I leave my little back room in old Bloomsbury?

[006-142] Where I could live for a pound a week in luxury.

[006-143] I wanted to live higher

[006-144] So I married Marier,

[006-145] Out of the frying pan into the bloomin' fire.

[006-146] And he meant every word of it.

[006-147] In our new positions in the Grouse Spots the orders were to [[patrol]]

[006-148] the open ground between at least four times a night. That first

[006-149] night there was one more patrol necessary before daylight. Tired as

[006-150] I was, I volunteered for it. I had had one patrol before, opposite

[006-151] [[grenay|Bully-Grenay]], and thought I liked the game.

[006-152] I went over with one man, a fellow named [[bellinger|Bellinger]]. We got out and

[006-153] started to crawl. All we knew was that the left sector was two

[006-154] hundred yards away. Machine-gun bullets were squealing and

[006-155] snapping overhead pretty continuously, and we had to hug the dirt.

[006-156] It is surprising to see how flat a man can keep and still get along

[006-157] at a good rate of speed. We kept straight away to the left and

[006-158] presently got into [[barbed-wire|wire]]. And then we heard German voices. Ow! I

[006-159] went cold all over.

[006-160] Then some "Very" lights went up and I saw the Boche parapet not

[006-161] twenty feet away. Worst of all there was a little lane through

[006-162] their wire at that point, and there would be, no doubt, a [[sap|sap head]]

[006-163] or a listening post near. I tried to lie still and burrow into the

[006-164] dirt at the same time. Nothing happened. Presently the lights died,

[006-165] and Bellinger gave me a poke in the ribs. We started to crawfish.

[006-166] Why we weren't seen I don't know, but we had gone all of one

[006-167] hundred feet before they spotted us. Fortunately we were on the

[006-168] edge of a shallow shell hole when the sentry caught our movements

[006-169] and Fritz cut loose with the "typewriters." We rolled in. A perfect

[006-170] torrent of bullets ripped up the dirt and cascaded us with gravel

[006-171] and mud. The noise of the bullets "crackling" a yard above us was

[006-172] deafening.

[006-173] The fusillade stopped after a bit. I was all for getting out and

[006-174] away immediately. Bellinger wanted to wait a while. We argued for

[006-175] as much as five minutes, I should think, and then the lights having

[006-176] gone out, I took matters in my own hands and we went away from

[006-177] there. Another piece of luck!

[006-178] We weren't more than a minute on our way when a pair of bombs went

[006-179] off about over the shell hole. Evidently some bold Heinie had

[006-180] chucked them over to make sure of the job in case the machines

[006-181] hadn't. It was a close pinch--two close pinches. I was in places

[006-182] afterwards where there was more [[battle|action]] and more danger, but,

[006-183] looking back, I don't think I was ever sicker or scareder. I would

[006-184] have been easy meat if they had rushed us.

[006-185] We made our way back slowly, and eventually caught the gleam of

[006-186] steel helmets. They were British. We had stumbled upon our left

[006-187] sector. We found out then that the line curved and that instead of

[006-188] the left sector being directly to the left of ours--the center--it

[006-189] was to the left and to the rear. Also there was a telephone wire

[006-190] running from one to the other. We reported and made our way back to

[006-191] the center in about five minutes by feeling along the wire. That

[006-192] was our method afterwards, and the patrol was cushy for us.
