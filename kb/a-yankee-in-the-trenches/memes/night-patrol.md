## Overview

Night [[patrol]] was one of the most dangerous and psychologically demanding duties an infantryman could draw on the [[france|Western Front]]. Small groups of men would crawl out into [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]] under cover of darkness to scout enemy positions, listen for German [[mine-explosion|mining]] activity, and report back to direct [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]]. Unlike a [[raid]], a [[patrol]]'s primary objective was observation and intelligence gathering — but the distinction was academic when any encounter between the lines could turn lethal in an instant.

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] volunteered for [[patrol]] duty and, remarkably, came to prefer it. His memoir provides vivid accounts of specific patrol operations at [[vimy|Vimy Ridge]] and in the [[grouse-spots|Grouse Spots trenches]].

## The Author's First Patrol

Holmes's introduction to [[patrol|patrol work]] came during his early trench service:

> I also had one night on patrol, which later became my favorite
> [004-089]

What made [[patrol]] appealing was the relative freedom it offered. On [[patrol]], a soldier operated independently, away from the rigid [[trench-discipline|discipline]] of the trench line. The darkness provided a kind of liberty — terrifying, but preferable to the claustrophobia of the trench and the constant scrutiny of NCOs.

## Patrol at the Grouse Spots

After moving to new positions in [[trench|trenches]] nicknamed the "[[grouse-spots|Grouse Spots]]," [[patrol]] orders came again:

> In our new positions in the Grouse Spots the orders were to patrol
> [006-147]

> That night there was one more patrol necessary before daylight. Tired as I was, I volunteered for it. I had had one patrol before, opposite Bully-Grenay
> [006-149]

The second [[patrol]] proved uneventful — "cushy," in trench slang — partly because the [[patrol]] had learned to use the telephone [[barbed-wire|wire]] as a guide back to their own lines:

> That was our method afterwards, and the patrol was cushy for us.
> [006-192]

## The Nature of Patrol Work

Holmes devoted an entire chapter ("Fascination of [[patrol|Patrol]] Work") to describing why this duty captivated him. The [[patrol]] man moved lightly armed — a [[revolver|pistol]], two or three [[grenade|Mills bomb]]s, and a [[persuader|persuader stick]] or [[knuckle-knife|trench knife]] — but was trained to avoid [[battle|combat]]. His mission was observation:

> The patrol goes out armed and equipped lightly. He has to move softly and at times very quickly. It is his duty to get as close to the enemy lines as possible and find out if they are repairing their wire or if any of their parties are out, and to get back word to the machine gunners, who immediately cut loose on the indicated spot.
> [007-029]

Listening for the faint "scrape, scrape" of German [[mine-explosion|mining]] parties was another critical task, as detecting a mine before it detonated could save an entire trench section.

## Patrol Technique and Discipline

The [[patrol]] developed near-feline night vision. When a Very light went up, he froze — standing or lying, as long as he was not silhouetted, he remained invisible. Approaching corpses required patient observation: a "dead" body might be a live German [[patrol]] lying "doggo."

> The patrol stares constantly through the dark and gets so he can see almost as well as a cat. He must avoid being seen. When a Very light goes up, he lies still. If he happens to be standing, he stands still. Unless the light is behind him so that he is silhouetted, he is invisible to the enemy.
> [007-045]
