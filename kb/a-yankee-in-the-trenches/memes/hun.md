# Hun

The **[[boche|Hun]]** was the most propagandistic of the [[tommy|British soldier]]'s nicknames for the German enemy during the First World War. Unlike the more familiar "[[germans|Fritz]]," calling [[germans|Germans]] "[[germans|Huns]]" invoked images of [[estaminet|bar]][[estaminet|bar]]ism and atrocity — the enemy as inhuman savage rather than fellow soldier.

## Usage in the Narrative

Holmes records the term's use from his earliest motivation for enlisting:

> "…get a single chance to kill a Hun." [001-048]

The narrator's eagerness is tempered with dark humor:

> "…kill a Hun until it was suggested that I…" [001-050]

As the war ground on, the term reflected genuine hatred born of experience:

> "…beastliness of the Hun closer than ever before. We…" [004-146]

The enemy became an inescapable presence:

> "Hun. You can't get rid of him when…" [004-165]

The [[germans|Huns]] held the high ground and used it:

> "…position was downhill from the Huns, and they kept sending over…" [007-203]

Up close, across [[no-mans-land|no man's land]], the enemy was human after all:

> "…could hear the murmur of the Huns as they chatted in…" [012-090]

In the final tank assault, the [[germans|Huns]] suffered devastating [[casualty|losses]]:

> "Suddenly one of the Huns burst into flames and came tumbling…" [012-219]

## Etymology and Propaganda

The term traces to July 27, 1900, when Kaiser Wilhelm II addressed German troops departing to suppress the Boxer Rebellion. He urged them to be like Attila's [[germans|Huns]] — "let the name of Germany be known in China for a thousand years." British propaganda seized on this speech after 1914, portraying [[germans|Germans]] as modern [[estaminet|bar]][[estaminet|bar]]ians. Posters depicted "The [[boche|Hun]]" as a bestial figure, and atrocity stories reinforced the image.
