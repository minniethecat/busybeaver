## Overview

The [[tommy|British soldier]]'s **uniform** in the First World War was the khaki service dress, designed for practicality in [[trench|trench warfare]]. It consisted of a woolen tunic, trousers, puttees (wound cloth leg wraps), and ankle boots. The uniform was a source of identity and [[trench-discipline|discipline]] but also a practical challenge in the mud and wet of the [[trench|trenches]].

## In the Trenches

Uniforms in the [[trench|trenches]] were subject to constant wear and harsh conditions. Mud, rain, and the general filth of trench life meant that keeping a uniform presentable was a constant struggle. Despite the conditions, [[trench-discipline|military discipline]] demanded that uniforms be maintained. Holmes records the routine: "Uniforms are brushed up, and equipment put in order" before inspection (004-161). This ritual of tidiness was one of the few constants in the chaotic trench environment.

The uniform also carried deep symbolic meaning. Holmes describes his eagerness to "get into uniform" when he first enlisted, feeling the transformation from civilian to soldier (001-013). The khaki uniform represented belonging — a visible mark of service and sacrifice.

## Practical Challenges

The wool uniform was warm but became heavy when wet — a frequent problem in the rain-soaked [[trench|trenches]] of [[france|France]] and Flanders. The puttees, wrapped tightly around the lower legs, provided support but could become waterlogged. Soldiers often modified their uniforms in the field, sewing on extra pockets or reinforcing worn areas. The uniform distinguished regiment and rank, but in the muddy equality of the [[trench|trenches]], these distinctions often blurred.

## Narrative Examples

The desire to wear the uniform was a driving motivation for Holmes and many other volunteers. He recounts the moment of enlistment with pride, noting how every effort was directed toward getting into uniform with a unit (001-013). The uniform transformed a factory worker from [[boston|Boston]] into a soldier of the King.

## Military Context

The [[british-army|British Army]]'s adoption of khaki in the late 19th century was a significant shift from the traditional scarlet coats. Khaki — from the Hindi word for "dust" — provided camouflage in the field. By WWI, the standard Service Dress was universal across the [[british-army|British Expeditionary Force]]. The uniform included the distinctive peaked cap (later replaced by the steel [[tin-hat]] in [[battle|combat]]), a wool serge tunic with four pockets, trousers, and puttees. Regimental badges, shoulder titles, and rank insignia provided identification and unit pride.
