## Overview

**Leave** was the authorized absence from military duty that every soldier cherished. In the context of WWI, leave took several forms: embarkation leave before shipping to [[france|France]], short furloughs during training, and the rare but treasured home leave during active service. For men facing the constant threat of death in the [[trench|trenches]], leave represented a temporary return to humanity.

## In the Trenches

The prospect of leave was a powerful motivator for soldiers in the line. Holmes describes embarkation leave — the brief period granted before departing for [[france|France]] — as a time of intense emotion. "Em[[estaminet|bar]]kation Leave, that is, leave before going to [[france|France]]," he writes, marking it as a distinct and significant event (001-191). This was the last chance to see family, to sleep in a real bed, to eat civilian food before the unknown horrors of the [[france|Western Front]].

Once in [[france|France]], leave became scarcer. Soldiers might wait months for a few days of home leave. The system was a lottery of survival — those who lived long enough might eventually get their turn. For many, the first leave was also the last.

## Embarkation Leave

The embarkation leave was particularly poignant. Holmes recounts how his training ended abruptly with "a furlough of five days" — a final gift of time before the crossing to [[france|France]] (001-190). These days were filled with family visits, last meals, and the unspoken fear of never returning. Soldiers were expected to report back promptly; failure to return from leave was treated as desertion.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes's brief mention of embarkation leave carries the weight of the experience. The five days of furlough before departure (001-190) and the formal embarkation leave (001-191) are presented without embellishment, yet they mark the boundary between the world of training and the world of [[battle|combat]]. After this leave, nothing would be the same.

## Military Context

The [[british-army|British Army]]'s leave policy in WWI evolved over the course of the war. Early in the conflict, leave was granted sparingly; as the war dragged on, the military recognized its importance for morale. By 1917, soldiers on the [[france|Western Front]] could expect roughly 10 days of home leave per year, though actual frequency depended on unit requirements and transport availability. The journey [[blighty|home]] could itself consume several days of the precious leave period. Despite these challenges, leave was a vital pressure valve, allowing men to reconnect with civilian life and return to the front with renewed — if temporary — spirits.
