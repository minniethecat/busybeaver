## Overview

**Fatigue duty** was the routine manual labour assigned to soldiers when not in the front line. It encompassed a wide range of tasks — road mending, trench repair, carrying supplies, burying dead, and general camp maintenance. Fatigue was both a necessary part of military life and a burden that soldiers dreaded, particularly after exhausting frontline stints.

## In the Trenches

In the rhythm of [[trench|trench warfare]], soldiers rotated between frontline duty, support positions, and rear-area rest. But "rest" often meant [[trench-fatigue|fatigue duty]] rather than actual rest. Holmes records that the phrase "fall in without equipment" was the telltale sign: "we knew that meant fatigue" (013-017). Soldiers preferred frontline danger to the back-breaking labour of fatigue, which could mean long hours of digging, carrying, and road work under shellfire.

Fatigue duty took many forms. Road mending was one of the most common assignments, as constant [[shell|shelling]] and heavy traffic destroyed the vital supply arteries behind the lines (005-135). Soldiers would be roused from their [[billet|billets]] and [[marching|march]]ed to work sites, sometimes within range of enemy artillery.

## As Informal Punishment

Fatigue duty served as an informal disciplinary tool. Soldiers who displeased NCOs or officers might find themselves on extra fatigue assignments. The line between routine duty and punishment was often blurred. For men who had just come out of the [[trench|trenches]], the physical demands of fatigue were a cruel addition to already depleted reserves of energy and morale.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes describes the sinking feeling when ordered to fall in without equipment — the universal signal for [[trench-fatigue|fatigue duty]] (013-017). The men preferred any alternative. Even a [[raid|trench raid]], dangerous as it was, offered the possibility of excitement and recognition. Fatigue offered only sweat and exhaustion.

Road mending was particularly loathsome work, often done within range of German [[artillery|guns]] (005-135). The soldiers would labor for hours, filling shell holes and laying new surface, knowing that the next [[artillery|bombardment]] would undo their work.

## Military Context

In the [[british-army|British Army]] of WWI, fatigue duties were an essential part of the military economy. Thousands of man-hours were required to maintain [[trench|trenches]], roads, and camps. While the [[army-service-corps|Army Service Corps]] handled much of the supply transport, infantrymen provided the bulk of manual labour. This system ensured that the [[battle|fight]]ing troops were never idle — a principle of [[trench-discipline|military discipline]] that dated back centuries. Fatigue was not recognized as [[battle|combat]] service, but it was just as essential to the war effort.
