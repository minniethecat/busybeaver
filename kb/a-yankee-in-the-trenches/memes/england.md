# England

**England** — known to every [[tommy|British soldier]] as "**[[blighty|Blighty]]**" — was the [[blighty|home]] country that loomed large in the minds of men at the front. It represented safety, healing, training, and ultimately the place soldiers dreamed of returning to. For [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]], an American volunteer who made his way to England to enlist, the country was both a transit point and a sanctuary.

## In the Narrative

Holmes's relationship with England begins before he even arrives, shaped by his New England upbringing:

> "…I was mildly sympathetic with England, and mighty sorry for France…" [001-011]

He learned the soldier's slang for [[blighty|home]] early in his training:

> "which is trench slang for England." [001-033] — referring to the term "Blighty"

The author's journey to the front began with passage across the Atlantic:

> "…across to England on a horse transport." [001-054]

He describes the advertisement that drew him and [[hun]]dreds of others:

> "…advertising for horse tenders for England. About three hundred…" [001-057]

Arriving as a stranger, Holmes found comfort in the friendships he formed:

> "Stranger without friends in England I can imagine that this…" [001-194]

Later, after being [[casualty|wounded]], England became the destination for recovery. Holmes describes meeting a comrade months later:

> "Months later in England I met up with Epstein and asked…" [009-113]

And the recurring hope of being sent back:

> "…duty if he couldn't send me to England." [012-341]

## Role in the War

For British and Commonwealth soldiers of the First World War, England served several crucial functions:

- **Training and em[[estaminet|bar]]kation**: Recruits were trained at camps like [[dover|Dover]] before shipping to [[france|France]]
- **Medical care**: Wounded soldiers were evacuated to hospitals in [[london|London]] and elsewhere — a "[[blighty-wound|Blighty wound]]" was one severe enough to warrant a trip [[blighty|home]]
- **[[leave|Leave]] and respite**: The dream of leave in England sustained morale at the front
- **Home front support**: Families, war industries, and volunteer organizations operated from England

## "Blighty" — The Soldier's England

The term "[[blighty|Blighty]]" — derived from the Hindi word *bilāyatī* meaning "foreign" or "British" — was widely used by [[tommy|British soldier]]s in the [[trench|trenches]]. A "[[blighty-wound|Blighty wound]]" was a non-fatal injury serious enough to warrant evacuation to England, and thus paradoxically a stroke of luck. The term captured the bittersweet relationship soldiers had with the [[blighty|home]] they longed to see but could only reach through serious injury.
