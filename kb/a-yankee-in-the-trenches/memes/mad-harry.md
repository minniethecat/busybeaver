# Mad Harry

## Overview

Mad Harry was one of the most memorable figures in [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir — a British lieutenant whose nickname was earned honestly. Holmes describes him with a mixture of admiration and bemusement: a man whose courage was indistinguishable from madness, whose eccentricities fascinated his men, and whose death was as strange and singular as his life.

## Role in the Narrative

Holmes introduces Mad Harry during [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]] offensive:

> up came Mad Harry, one of our "leftenants"
> — (008-106)

The nickname requires explanation, and Holmes provides it:

> This Mad Harry,--I do not give his surname for obvious reasons--was the most eccentric fellow I ever met.
> — (008-113)

The diagnosis, in the soldier's vernacular, was clear:

> He was absolutely without fear, and he was, in short, balmy. Hence the nickname Mad Harry.
> — (008-119)

## Key Appearances

Mad Harry's defining trait was an almost supernatural composure under fire:

> Mad Harry was a fiend for walking. And he would walk about in No Man's Land as if he were on parade.
> — (008-120)

Holmes describes him strolling through the most dangerous ground on the [[france|Western Front]] with the casual air of a man taking an afternoon constitutional. This was not bravado — it was, Holmes implies, a genuine disconnection from the normal human fear response.

His death matched his life in its strangeness:

> It may do no harm to tell how Mad Harry died. He came very near
> — (008-127)

Holmes records that Mad Harry was walking — as always — when he was shot. But the manner of his death was extraordinary: he continued speaking, in the same monotone he always used, even as he was dying. A comrade had to leap at him and snatch his [[revolver|pistol]] away to prevent him from continuing to fire at the enemy as he collapsed.
