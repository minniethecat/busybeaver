## Overview

A Very light was an illumination flare fired from a specialized signal pistol, named after its American inventor Edward Wilson Very. In the [[trench|trenches]] of World War I, Very lights were a defining feature of night ope[[trench-rations|rations]] — their pale greenish-white glow periodically transformed [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] from impenetrable darkness into a harshly lit killing ground. Holmes's memoir is punctuated by the sudden appearance of Very lights, which expose [[patrol]]s, signal attacks, and create moments of electric tension. The glossary of Holmes's book does not define the term directly, but the narrative itself provides ample illustration of its central role in the sensory experience of [[trench|trench warfare]].

## In the Trenches

Very lights served both sides as a means of seeing into the darkness between the lines. Sentries fired them at the slightest suspicion of movement — "[[boche|the Boche]] would send up the 'Very' lights on the slightest provocation" — illuminating the ground in front of their [[trench|trenches]] and exposing any [[patrol]]s, wiring parties, or [[raiding-party|raiders]] caught in the open. Soldiers in [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] learned to freeze at the first hiss of a Very [[revolver|pistol]], lying motionless until the flare burned out and darkness returned.

The flares were not only a defensive tool but also an offensive one. Before an attack, Very lights could be fired in salvos to blind enemy sentries and illuminate objectives. After an attack, they were used to assess positions and direct artillery fire. The distinctive greenish-white light became, for soldiers on both sides, a visual shorthand for the tension and unpredictability of the night front.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes describes the panic of being caught by Very lights during a [[patrol|night patrol]] that had gone too far. Crawling through the dark with his comrade [[bellinger|Bellinger]], he suddenly found himself illuminated at point-blank range:

> Then some "Very" lights went up and I saw the Boche parapet not twenty feet away. Worst of all there was a little lane through their wire at that point, and there would be, no doubt, a sap head or a listening post near. I tried to lie still and burrow into the dirt at the same time. (006-163)

The flare exposed Holmes and [[bellinger|Bellinger]] within twenty feet of the German line — close enough to see the lane through the enemy [[barbed-wire|wire]]. His instinct to "lie still and burrow into the dirt at the same time" captures the paradox of the moment: absolute stillness was survival, but every fiber of his body wanted to dig into the earth and di[[mine-explosion|sap]]pear.

The most dramatic Very light sequence in the memoir comes just before the [[mine-explosion|mining]] of the Pimple. Holmes had halted the ration party at the end of the duck walk when:

> Suddenly the "Very" lights went up from the German side, literally in hundreds, illuminating the top of the ridge and the sky behind with a thin greenish white flare. Then came a deep rumble that shook the ground, and a dull boom. (007-275)

The [[estaminet|bar]]rage of [[hun]]dreds of flares was the prelude to the detonation of two German mines that tore away the side of the Pimple. Here, Very lights served as a spectacular and terrifying signal — the illumination that preceded destruction.

## Military Context

The Very [[revolver|pistol]] was invented in 1877 by Edward Very, an American naval officer, as a means of ship-to-ship signaling. By World War I, it had been adopted by all major armies as a standard-issue illumination and signaling device. The flares came in various colors — white for illumination, red or green for signaling — and were fired from a short-[[estaminet|bar]]reled [[revolver|pistol]] with a bore of approximately one inch (25mm).

On the [[france|Western Front]], Very lights were manufactured in staggering quantities. A single night's routine illumination could consume [[hun]]dreds of flares per mile of front. The flares were fired high into the air, where a parachute or simple ballistic trajectory would slow their descent, providing 30–60 seconds of illumination. Soldiers in exposed positions — [[patrol]]s, wiring parties, [[ration-party|ration detail]]s — lived in dread of the sudden "pop" of a Very [[revolver|pistol]], followed by the harsh white light that turned darkness into a shooting gallery.

The Very light was also a tool of communication between infantry and artillery. Pre-arranged flare signals could indicate "lift [[estaminet|bar]]rage," "enemy counter-attack," or "friendly troops have reached objective." The flares were visible for miles, making them one of the few reliable means of battlefield communication in an era before portable radios.
