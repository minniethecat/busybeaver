# Divisional Follies

## Overview

The Divisional Follies was a concert party — a vaudeville-style entertainment troupe — organized within a British division on the [[france|Western Front]]. Made up of professional actors and singers who had enlisted or been conscripted into the army, the Follies were officially detailed to entertain the troops during periods of rest behind the lines. In an army where recreation was scarce and the psychological toll of [[trench|trench warfare]] immense, the Follies represented an institutional acknowledgment that morale was a military asset worth investing in. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, the Divisional Follies appear as a welcome flash of civilian color in the gray monotony of war.

## The Show at Petite-Saens

Holmes first encountered the Follies at Petite-Saens, a village behind the lines where his battalion was [[billet]]ed for rest. He describes the experience with evident appreciation:

> It was at Petite-Saens that I first saw the Divisional Folies. This was a vaudeville show by ten men who had been actors in civil life, and who were detailed to amuse the soldiers. They charged a small admission fee and the profit went to the Red Cross.
> — (005-105)

The arrangement was elegantly practical: soldiers got entertainment, the performers got to practice their civilian trade, and the [[red-cross|Red Cross]] got the proceeds. Holmes, who elsewhere in the memoir advocates passionately for more soldier recreation and welfare services, clearly approved.

## The Music

The Follies' repertoire drew from the popular music hall songs of the era. In the book's final chapter — a guide of advice for new soldiers heading to [[france|France]] — Holmes recalls one of the Follies' singers:

> One of the singers in the Divisional Follies recently revived the once popular music-hall song, "If You Can't Be Good Be Careful." It should appeal to the soldier as much as "Smile, smile, smile", and is equally good advice.
> — (016-137)

The song choice was pointed. Holmes's advice to new soldiers — be careful with the "attractive girls and women" they would meet in [[france|France]], remember "those at [[blighty|home]]" — was echoed in the Follies' musical programming. Entertainment and moral guidance, in the world of the [[france|Western Front]], were not separate functions.

## Significance

The Divisional Follies belonged to a broader tradition of "concert parties" that flourished in all the armies of the Great War. Every division, it seemed, had its complement of amateur and semi-professional talent. These troupes — with names like "The Bow Bells," "The Whizz-Bangs," and "The Roosters" — performed in [[estaminet|bar]]ns, [[ymca|Y.M.C.A. hut]]s, and open fields, providing the only live entertainment most soldiers would see for months at a time. The Follies, as Holmes describes them, were a small but significant part of the army's informal welfare apparatus: soldiers looking after soldiers' spirits.
