## Overview

Artillery was the dominant weapon of World War I and the defining feature of [[trench|trench warfare]] on the [[france|Western Front]]. From the perspective of an American infantryman serving with the [[british-army|British Army]], artillery was a constant presence—its roar a permanent backdrop to [[daily-life-in-the-trenches|life in the trenches]], its [[shell|shells]] a daily threat, and its [[estaminet|bar]]rages the necessary prelude to any infantry advance. Holmes describes artillery not as a distant strategic inst[[rum-ration|rum]]ent but as an immediate, sensory reality: the continuous roar of German guns (002-013), the protective curtain of [[barrage|barrage fire]] (003-052), and the terrifying experience of being on the receiving end of a bombardment (003-040).

## In the Trenches

Holmes's account emphasizes the intimate relationship between the infantryman and artillery. [[marching|Marching]] toward the front lines for the first time, he notes how "the roar of artillery was continuous and sounded like a distant t[[hun]]derstorm" (002-013). The sound was so pervasive that soldiers learned to distinguish between different types of artillery fire—the sharp crack of field guns, the deeper boom of howitzers, and the distinctive whine of incoming [[shell|shells]].

Infantry formations adapted to the artillery threat. While advancing, men were "extended into artillery formation" (002-034), spreading out to minimize [[casualty|casualties]] from a single shell burst. The [[trench|trenches]] themselves were shaped by artillery—constantly repaired after bombardments, with [[dug-out|dugout]]s built deeper and deeper as shell power increased. Even in rest areas behind the lines, artillery remained a presence; Holmes records passing "batteries of [[france|French]] artillery, and jokes and laughter came from the men lounging about the guns" (006-056).

## Narrative Examples

Holmes provides vivid accounts of artillery in [[battle|action]]. Describing his first experience under bombardment, he recalls the initial shock:

> It was the first bombardment I had ever been under [003-040]

The preparatory [[estaminet|bar]]rage before an attack is described with precise timing:

> At ten minutes of eleven, our artillery opened up [003-039]

> The barrage fire kept up right up to zero [003-052]

The aftermath of successful bombardments was starkly visible: "the German front line [[trench|trenches]] had been flattened by our [[barrage|barrage fire]]" (003-073). Yet artillery was not always effective—Holmes notes moments when "there was no artillery [[battle|action]] to amount to anything" (014-058), reflecting the stalemate that characterized much of the war.

He also records the psychological toll of constant [[shell|shelling]]: "[[boche|the Boche]] naturally" concentrated fire on certain positions (013-199), and men lived with the knowledge that a single well-placed shell could end their lives without warning. The "cream of the artillery fire" (013-199) fell on the most exposed positions, making certain sections of trench particularly dreaded.

## Military Context

In World War I, artillery underwent a massive expansion in scale and sophistication. By 1916–1917, the period of Holmes's service, artillery had evolved from the relatively small batteries of 1914 into the dominant arm of all armies on the [[france|Western Front]]. The [[british-army|British Army]]'s artillery grew from a few [[hun]]dred guns to thousands, with specialized roles for field artillery, heavy howitzers, and long-distance guns.

Holmes distinguishes between different artillery types: "field artillery" (008-289) for direct support, "heavies" (010-025) for long-range bombardment, and "long-distance artillery, which are fully capable of searching out positions far behind the lines" (005-208). He also mentions the organizational structure, referring to "artillery companies" (011-100) that provided support services, suggesting that artillery units were integrated into the broader military community, sharing in entertainments and camp life.

Artillery was not only a weapon of destruction but also a tool of communication and signaling. [[barrage|Barrage]]s served as the clockwork of attacks—timed to the minute, lifting and shifting according to precise schedules. The infantry relied absolutely on this coordination; when artillery failed to deliver, attacks stalled or failed. Holmes's narrative captures this dependency implicitly, as every successful advance in his account is preceded by bombardment, and every setback is accompanied by artillery's absence or ineffectiveness.
