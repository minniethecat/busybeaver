# Canchy

**Canchy** was a [[france|French]] village behind the front lines where [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] and his brigade were sent for what was euphemistically called "rest." As Holmes wryly records, the ten days they spent there brought anything but respite.

## In the Narrative

Canchy appears in a list of [[france|French]] villages the battalion passed through — names that blurred together in the endless movement of military life:

> "…Maisincourt, Barly, Oneaux, Canchy…" [008-034]

The brigade was brought to Canchy on what should have been a day of rest:

> "…brought up at Canchy of a Sunday afternoon. Here the whole brigade…" [008-305]

But rest was not what they found. German artillery had other plans:

> "Canchy ten days, and they gave it to us good and plenty. We would…" [008-309]

The ironic tone — "they gave it to us good and plenty" — captures the soldier's dark humor about the constant danger of shellfire, even in supposed rest areas. Behind the lines did not mean out of range.

## The "Rest" Area Illusion

Canchy exemplifies a common experience of the First World War: the "rest" [[billet]] that was anything but. While officially behind the lines, villages like Canchy were well within artillery range and constantly subject to harassing fire. Soldiers would be rotated back for rest and refit, only to find themselves under [[artillery|bombardment]], digging new positions, or running supply details. The ten days at Canchy that Holmes describes were typical — shelled, harassed, and anything but restful.
