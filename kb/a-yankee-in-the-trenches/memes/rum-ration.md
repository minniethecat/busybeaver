## Overview

The rum ration was a daily issue of rum to [[tommy|British soldier]]s serving in the [[trench|trenches]] during World War I. More than just alcohol, it was a ritual of trench life — a moment of warmth and comfort in the cold, wet, and terrifying conditions of the front line. Soldiers looked forward to it with an intensity that spoke to its psychological importance.

## In the Memoir

Holmes describes the rum ration as a cherished moment amid the hardships of the [[trench|trenches]]. After a long, dangerous [[patrol]], the [[sergeant-major|Sergeant Major]] would issue the ration:

> I went among the boys with a spoon and the rum in a mess tin and served out two tots instead of the customary one.
> (010-118)

The rum was consumed eagerly. Holmes notes the ritual's significance in the hours before [[over-the-top|going over the top]]:

> I got an extra large supply, as the Sergeant Major was good humored. It was the last rum he ever served.
> (010-118)

## The Rum Issue Ritual

The issuing of rum was a formalized routine. Men would queue with their [[dixie|mess tin]]s or tin cups, and the [[sergeant-major|Sergeant Major]] or a designated NCO would measure out the "tot" — a small but potent serving. On special occasions, or before an attack, extra [[trench-rations|rations]] might be issued to steady nerves.

Holmes also records the [[tommy|Tommy]]'s pragmatic attitude toward rum:

> The average Tommy will steal rum whenever he can without getting caught.
> (012-176)

## Morale and Psychology

The rum ration served a dual purpose: it provided a brief physical warmth in the cold [[trench|trenches]] and a psychological buffer against the constant stress of shellfire, [[sniper|snipers]], and the prospect of death. Officers understood its value as a morale tool, and the rum issue was one of the few routines that men genuinely anticipated with pleasure.
