# Battle

## Overview

Battle in World War I spanned an enormous range of scale — from the million-man offensives that consumed whole landscapes to the desperate hand-to-hand fight for a single traverse of trench. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, battle is experienced not as grand strategy but as personal ordeal: the terror of [[over-the-top|going over the top]], the chaos of close combat, and the long aftermath of counting who was still alive. Holmes participated in the [[battle-of-high-wood|Battle of High Wood]] (part of the larger [[battle-of-the-somme|Somme offensive]] of 1916), and his first-person account provides one of the most vivid enlisted man's perspectives on combat in the war.

## The Somme and High Wood

Holmes's major battle experience came during [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]] offensive — referred to in the army vernacular as the "[[big-push|Big Push]]":

> Big push — The battles of the Somme
> — (016-159)

The offensive, launched on July 1, 1916, was the largest battle the [[british-army|British Army]] had ever fought. Holmes's battalion was committed to the fight for [[high-wood|High Wood]] — a heavily fortified German position that became the scene of intense and costly fighting.

Holmes acknowledges the significance of the engagement in measured terms:

> battle — one of the most important in the history of the world
> — (010-020)

The claim is not hyperbole. The [[battle-of-the-somme|Somme offensive]] marked a turning point in the war, absorbing German reserves and wearing down the enemy's ability to sustain the conflict — at a cost of over a million [[casualty|casualties]] on both sides.

## Going Over the Top

The defining moment of battle for the infantryman was going "[[over-the-top|over the top]]" — climbing out of the trench and advancing across [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] into enemy fire. Holmes's battalion went over from the second line:

> Our battalion went over from the second trench
> — (010-081)

The assault was met with devastating fire. Holmes records the [[casualty|casualties]] with stark economy:

> as much as a whole platoon went down
> — (010-102)

A platoon — roughly fifty men — destroyed in a single sentence. The speed with which death came is captured in a single, terrible image:

> I saw a man double up, drop his rifle, and somersault, hanging on to what had been his stomach
> — (010-105)

In the chaos of the advance, units became hopelessly intermingled:

> I couldn't find my battalion. I threw in with a battalion of the Royal Fusiliers
> — (010-148)

This was the reality of battle — not the orderly advance of staff maps but a desperate scramble through shell holes and [[barbed-wire|wire]], men attaching themselves to whatever unit they could find, fighting to survive.

## The Aftermath

Holmes's account of the battle's conclusion is laconic, almost anticlimactic:

> The net result of the battle of High Wood was that our troops gained their objective
> — (010-159)

But the cost was staggering. When the fighting subsided, Holmes returned to find his unit:

> When I found my battalion, the battle of High Wood had pretty well died down
> — (012-001)

The [[casualty|casualties]] were enormous. Holmes records the prisoners taken:

> At the battle of High Wood the prisoners we took numbered over a thousand
> — (011-109)

But the British [[casualty|losses]] were equally severe. Holmes himself was among the [[casualty|wounded]], his participation ended by a shell fragment:

> of it my participation in the battle of High Wood ended
> — (010-123)

## A Soldier's Eye View

What distinguishes Holmes's account from official histories is its ground-level perspective. He describes watching another unit advance against a defended position:

> We had watched this battle from the rear from the slight elevation
> — (013-139)

The detachment of watching others fight — the strange combination of relief (it is not you going over) and guilt (it could be you) — is a recurring theme in combat memoirs.

Holmes reflects on the peculiar psychology of battle, where men performed extraordinary acts not out of patriotism or hatred but out of loyalty to the man next to them. He records the tenacity of the British officer class under fire:

> In battle, no British officer can relax his vigilance for a moment
> — (008-167)

The officer's burden was different from the private's — not only to fight but to lead, to set an example, to be seen to be calm when every instinct screamed panic.
