## Overview

"Going [[over-the-top|over the top]]" was the soldier's term for the most dangerous moment in [[trench|trench warfare]] — climbing out of the relative safety of the trench and advancing across [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]] toward enemy lines. It was the moment of maximum vulnerability, when men were exposed to [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]], artillery, and [[estaminet|bar]]bed [[barbed-wire|wire]]. In Holmes' glossary, a "wave" is defined simply as "A line of men [[over-the-top|going over the top]]" (016-090).

## In the Memoir

Holmes describes multiple instances of [[over-the-top|going over the top]], most notably during the night [[grenade|bomb]]ing [[trench-raid|raid]] led by [[lieutenant-may|Lieutenant May]]. The party climbed out of the trench and advanced across open ground in complete silence:

> Lieutenant May took us out over the top on schedule time, and we started for the position to be taken. We walked erect but in the strictest silence for about a thousand yards.
> (003-018)

The distances involved during the [[big-push|Big Push]] period were vast compared to earlier [[trench|trench warfare]], with demolished trench systems offering no cover:

> At that time the distances were great on the Somme, as the Big Push was in full swing, and the advance had been fast. Trench systems had been demolished, and in many places there were only shell holes and isolated trenches.
> (003-018)

## Trench Lore

The memoir records a piece of trench humor that circulated widely about [[over-the-top|going over the top]] — a story of a soldier who bent down to catch a cootie (louse) just as he was climbing out, and in that moment a shell passed through the space where his head had been:

> A soldier was going over the top when one of his cootie friends bit him on the calf. The soldier reached down and captured the biter. Just as he stooped, a shell whizzed over where his head had been.
> (012-070)

This story, which Holmes notes "might have happened" and was "told from the North Sea to the Swiss border," illustrates how soldiers used dark humor to cope with the terror of [[over-the-top|going over the top]].

## Psychological Dimension

Going [[over-the-top|over the top]] required overcoming what Holmes describes as "temporary involuntary fear" — the natural terror of advancing into enemy fire. The memoir captures both the dread and the determination required, reflecting the stoic culture of the [[tommy|British Tommy]].
