# Raid

## Overview

The trench raid was one of the most distinctive and harrowing forms of [[battle|combat]] to emerge from the static warfare of the [[france|Western Front]]. Unlike the massed assaults of set-piece [[battle|battles]], a [[trench-raid|raid]] was a small-scale operation — typically a dozen to thirty men, hand-picked volunteers, crossing [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] under cover of darkness to infiltrate the enemy's [[trench|trenches]]. The objectives were specific: capture prisoners for interrogation, gather intelligence on enemy dispositions, destroy positions, and — crucially — maintain the offensive spirit of the unit. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, the [[trench-raid|raid]] he participated in is the emotional and narrative centerpiece of the book — a few minutes of intense, intimate violence that he recounts in extraordinary detail.

## Preparation and Selection

A raiding party was assembled from volunteers — men who, for reasons of courage, ambition, or simple fatalism, agreed to the most dangerous work in the [[trench|trenches]]. Holmes's party was led by [[lieutenant-blofeld|Lieutenant Blofeld]]:

> Mr. Blofeld, who had organized the raid, beat our instructions into us thoroughly
> — (003-005)

The men rehearsed every movement — the approach across [[no-mans-land|no man's land]], the entry into the enemy trench, the assignments of each man (bayonet men first, [[grenade|bomb]]ers behind, support troops in reserve). Nothing was left to chance.

Holmes was assigned as first [[bayonet|bayonet man]], the point of the spear:

> This was the first raid that "Batt" had ever tried
> — (003-003)

The battalion's first [[trench-raid|raid]] — an operation for which there was no institutional experience to draw upon, only the grim lessons of other units passed down through [[rum-ration|rum]]or and warning.

## The Raid Itself

Holmes's account of crossing [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] is a masterpiece of tension. Every sound — the sucking of boots in mud, the rattle of equipment — seemed loud enough to alert the entire German army. The party crawled through the darkness toward the enemy parapet, each man alone with his fear.

The object was clear:

> The object of a raid is to get into the enemy's trenches by stealth
> — (003-007)

Once inside the German trench, the world contracted to a few yards of mud walls and the immediate threat of the next corner. Holmes describes the moments of contact with stark immediacy — the German sentry appearing before him, the bayonet finding its mark, the shouts and explosions as the [[grenade|bomb]]ers went to work.

The entire operation lasted just over three minutes inside the enemy trench:

> We were in their trenches for three minutes and then we scrambled back
> — (003-154)

Three minutes — an eternity of terror compressed into the time it takes to boil water. The [[raiding-party|raiders]] [[casualty|killed]] several [[germans|Germans]], captured others, and fled back across [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] as the enemy counter-attack materialized.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The post-[[trench-raid|raid]] accounting was both official and personal. Men were missing — some [[casualty|killed]], some captured, some simply vanished into the dark:

> One party of twelve had all been killed or captured
> — (003-154)

But the [[trench-raid|raid]] was judged a success:

> Altogether it was a successful raid. The best part of it was
> — (003-162)

The "best part" went unstated in the memoir — perhaps the intelligence gathered, perhaps simply that Holmes himself had survived. For those who went, the [[trench-raid|raid]] left a permanent mark:

> I am afraid. It is only one of many such
> — (004-106)

The sentence is ambiguous — af[[trench-raid|raid]] of what? Another [[trench-raid|raid]]? The memory of this one? Holmes does not elaborate, and the ambiguity is telling. Some experiences resist neat summary.

## Retaliation and the Cycle

The [[germans|Germans]] did not let the [[trench-raid|raid]] go unanswered:

> after the licking we had given Fritz on the raid, he behaved himself for quite a while
> — (004-001)–(004-002)

But this period of quiet was deceptive. The enemy was preparing his own [[trench-raid|raid]]:

> he was so keen that another raid that was attempted ten days later
> — (004-009)

The [[trench-raid|raid]]-and-counter-[[trench-raid|raid]] cycle was a defining rhythm of [[trench|trench warfare]] — each side probing, testing, exacting revenge. The calculus was brutal: a few prisoners for a few dead, the intelligence gained weighed against the lives spent.

In the immediate aftermath, Holmes notes a curious fact about the [[raiding-party|raiders]]:

> Directly after the raid, we who were in the party had a special privilege
> — (004-017)

The privilege — likely extra leave, [[rum-ration|rum]], or relief from duty — was a tacit acknowledgment from the command that the [[raiding-party|raiders]] had done something extraordinary and terrible.
