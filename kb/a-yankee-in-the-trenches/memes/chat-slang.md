# Chat

A **[[cootie|chat]]** was the [[tommy|British Tommy]]'s word for a [[cootie|body louse]] — the tiny, relentless parasite that made [[daily-life-in-the-trenches|life in the trenches]] a misery of constant itching and discomfort. Along with mud, rats, and shellfire, [[chat|chats]] were among the most universal and hated features of trench existence.

## Usage in the Narrative

Holmes explains the alternate term for the familiar "cootie":

> "'cootie.' The cootie, or the 'chat' as he is called by the…" [002-193]

The endless struggle to rid oneself of the pests:

> "…a-gittin' rid o' the bloomin' chats on…" [005-229]

## Life with the Chat

Body [[cootie|lice]] were a near-universal affliction in the [[trench|trenches]]. They lived in the seams of uniforms, multiplied rapidly, and caused intense itching and skin infections. Soldiers spent hours "[[cootie|chat]]ting" — picking [[cootie|lice]] from their clothing — during quiet moments. The ritual was so common that groups of men sitting around delousing themselves became a standard scene of trench life.

Lice also carried trench fever, a debilitating disease that caused recurring high fevers and could put a soldier out of [[battle|action]] for weeks. The [[cootie|chat]] was thus not merely a nuisance but a genuine medical threat.

The word itself, with its East Indian etymology, was one of many terms brought back to the [[trench|trenches]] by soldiers who had served in Britain's imperial garrisons — like "[[blighty|Blighty]]" and "char."
