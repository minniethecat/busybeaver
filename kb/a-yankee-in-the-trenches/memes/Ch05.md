[005-001] Food is a burning issue in the lives of all of us. It is the main

[005-002] consideration with the soldier. His life is simplified to two

[005-003] principal motives, _i.e._, keeping alive himself and killing the

[005-004] other fellow. The question uppermost in his mind every time and all

[005-005] of the time, is, "When do we eat?"

[005-006] In the [[trench|trenches]] the backbone of [[british-army|Tommy]]'s diet is [[trench-rations|bully beef]],

[005-007] "Maconochie's Ration", cheese, bread or biscuit, jam, and tea. He

[005-008] may get some of this hot or he may eat it from the tin, all

[005-009] depending upon how badly [[germans|Fritz]] is behaving.

[005-010] In [[billet|billets]] the diet is more varied. Here he gets some fresh meat,

[005-011] lots of bacon, and the [[bully-beef|bully]] and the Maconochie's come along in the

[005-012] form of stew. Also there is fresh bread and some dried fruit and a

[005-013] certain amount of sweet stuff.

[005-014] It was this matter of grub that made my life a burden in the

[005-015] [[billet|billets]] at Petite-Saens. I had been rather proud of being lance

[005-016] corporal. It was, to me, the first step along the road to being

[005-017] field marshal. I found, however, that a corporal is high enough to

[005-018] take responsibility and to get bawled out for anything that goes

[005-019] wrong. He's not high enough to command any consideration from those

[005-020] higher up, and he is so close to the men that they take out their

[005-021] grievances on him as a matter of course. He is neither fish, flesh,

[005-022] nor fowl, and his life is a burden.

[005-023] I had the job of issuing the rations of our platoon, and it nearly

[005-024] drove me mad. Every morning I would detail a couple of men from our

[005-025] platoon to be standing mess orderlies for the day. They would fetch

[005-026] the char and bacon from the field kitchen in the morning and clean

[005-027] up the "[[dixie|dixies]]" after breakfast. The "dixie", by the way, is an

[005-028] iron box or pot, oblong in shape, capacity about four or five

[005-029] gallons. It fits into the field kitchen and is used for roasts,

[005-030] stews, char, or anything else. The cover serves to cook bacon in.

[005-031] Field kitchens are drawn by horses and follow the battalion

[005-032] everywhere that it is safe to go, and to some places where it

[005-033] isn't. Two men are detailed from each company to cook, and there is

[005-034] usually another man who gets the sergeants' mess, besides the

[005-035] officers' cook, who does not as a rule use the field kitchen, but

[005-036] prepares the food in the house taken as the officers' mess.

[005-037] As far as possible, the company cooks are men who were cooks in

[005-038] civil life, but not always. We drew a plumber and a navvy (road

[005-039] builder)--and the grub tasted of both trades. The way our company

[005-040] worked the kitchen problem was to have stew for two [[platoon|platoons]] one

[005-041] day and roast dinner for the others, and then reverse the order

[005-042] next day, so that we didn't have stew all the time. There were not

[005-043] enough "dixies" for us all to have stew the same day.

[005-044] Every afternoon I would take my mess orderlies and go to the

[005-045] quartermaster's stores and get our allowance and carry it back to

[005-046] the billets in waterproof sheets. Then the stuff that was to be

[005-047] cooked in the kitchen went there, and the bread and that sort of

[005-048] material was issued direct to the men. That was where my trouble

[005-049] started.

[005-050] The powers that were had an uncanny knack of issuing an odd number

[005-051] of articles to go among an even number of men, and vice versa.

[005-052] There would be eleven loaves of bread to go to a platoon of fifty

[005-053] men divided into four sections. Some of the sections would have ten

[005-054] men and some twelve or thirteen.

[005-055] The [[tommy|British Tommy]] is a scrapper when it comes to his rations. He

[005-056] reminds me of an English sparrow. He's always right in there

[005-057] wangling for his own. He will bully and browbeat if he can, and he

[005-058] will coax and cajole if he can't. It would be "Hi sye, corporal.

[005-059] They's ten men in Number 2 section and fourteen in ourn. An' blimme

[005-060] if you hain't guv 'em four loaves, same as ourn. Is it right, I

[005-061] arsks yer? Is it?" Or,

[005-062] "Lookee! Do yer call that a loaf o' bread? Looks like the [[army-service-corps|A.S.C.]]

[005-063] (Army Service Corps) been using it fer a piller. Gimme another,

[005-064] will yer, corporal?"

[005-065] When it comes to splitting seven onions nine ways, I defy any one

[005-066] to keep peace in the family, and every doggoned Tommy would hold

[005-067] out for his onion whether he liked 'em or not. Same way with a

[005-068] bottle of pickles to go among eleven men or a handful of raisins or

[005-069] apricots. Or jam or butter or anything, except bully beef or

[005-070] Maconochie. I never heard any one "argue the toss" on either of

[005-071] those commodities.

[005-072] Bully is high-grade corned beef in cans and is O.K. if you like it,

[005-073] but it does get tiresome.

[005-074] Maconochie ration is put up a pound to the can and bears a label

[005-075] which assures the consumer that it is a scientifically prepared,

[005-076] well-balanced ration. Maybe so. It is my personal opinion that the

[005-077] inventor brought to his task an imperfect knowledge of cookery and

[005-078] a perverted imagination. Open a can of Maconochie and you find a

[005-079] gooey gob of grease, like rancid lard. Investigate and you find

[005-080] chunks of carrot and other unidentifiable material, and now and

[005-081] then a bit of mysterious meat. The first man who ate an oyster had

[005-082] courage, but the last man who ate Maconochie's unheated had more.

[005-083] Tommy regards it as a very inferior grade of garbage. The label

[005-084] notwithstanding, he's right.

[005-085] Many people have asked me what to send our soldiers in the line of

[005-086] food. I'd say stick to sweets. Cookies of any durable kind--I mean

[005-087] that will stand chance moisture--the sweeter the better, and if

[005-088] possible those containing raisins or dried fruit. Figs, dates,

[005-089] etc., are good. And, of course, chocolate. Personally, I never did

[005-090] have enough chocolate. Candy is acceptable, if it is of the sort to

[005-091] stand more or less rough usage which it may get before it reaches

[005-092] the soldier. Chewing gum is always received gladly. The army issue

[005-093] of sweets is limited pretty much to jam, which gets to taste all

[005-094] alike.

[005-095] It is pathetic to see some of the messes Tommy gets together to

[005-096] fill his craving for dessert. The favorite is a slum composed of

[005-097] biscuit, water, condensed milk, raisins, and chocolate. If some of

[005-098] you folks at [[blighty|home]] would get one look at that concoction, let alone

[005-099] tasting it, you would dash out and spend your last dollar for a

[005-100] package to send to some lad "over there."

[005-102] After the excitement of dodging [[shell|shells]] and bullets in the front

[005-103] trenches, life in billets seems dull. Tommy has too much time to

[005-104] get into mischief. It was at Petite-Saens that I first saw the

[005-105] [[divisional-follies|Divisional Folies]]. This was a vaudeville show by ten men who had

[005-106] been actors in civil life, and who were detailed to amuse the

[005-107] soldiers. They charged a small admission fee and the profit went to

[005-108] the [[red-cross|Red Cross]].

[005-109] There ought to be more recreation for the soldiers of all armies.

[005-110] The [[ymca|Y.M.C.A.]] is to take care of that with our boys.

[005-111] By the way, we had a Y.M.C.A. hut at Petite-Saens, and I cannot say

[005-112] enough for this great work. No one who has not been there can know

[005-113] what a blessing it is to be able to go into a clean, warm, dry

[005-114] place and sit down to reading or games and to hear good music.

[005-115] Personally I am a little bit sorry that the secretaries are to be

[005-116] in [[uniform|khaki]]. They weren't when I left. And it sure did seem good to

[005-117] see a man in civilian's clothes. You get after a while so you hate

[005-118] the sight of a uniform.

[005-119] Another thing about the Y.M.C.A. I could wish that they would have

[005-120] more women in the huts. Not frilly, frivolous society girls, but

[005-121] women from thirty-five to fifty. A soldier likes kisses as well as

[005-122] the next. And he takes them when he finds them. And he finds too

[005-123] many. But what he really wants, though, is the chance to sit down

[005-124] and tell his troubles to some nice, sympathetic woman who is old

[005-125] enough to be level-headed.

[005-126] Nearly every soldier reverts more or less to a boyish point of

[005-127] view. He hankers for somebody to mother him. I should be glad to

[005-128] see many women of that type in the Y.M.C.A. work. It is one of the

[005-129] great needs of our army that the boys should be amused and kept

[005-130] clean mentally and morally. I don't believe there is any

[005-131] organization better qualified to do this than the Y.M.C.A.

[005-132] Most of our chaps spent their time "on their own" either in the

[005-133] Y.M.C.A. hut or in the _[[estaminet|estaminets]]_ while we were in Petite-Saens.

[005-134] Our stop there was hardly typical of the rest in billets. Usually

[005-135] "rest" means that you are set to mending roads or some such [[fatigue]]

[005-136] duty. At Petite-Saens, however, we had it "cushy."

[005-137] The routine was about like this: Up at 6:30, we fell in for

[005-138] three-quarters of an hour physical drill or bayonet practice.

[005-139] Breakfast. Inspection of ammo and [[gas-warfare|gas]] masks. One hour drill. After

[005-140] that, "on our own", with nothing to do but smoke, read, and gamble.

[005-141] Tommy is a great smoker. He gets a [[fag|fag issue]] from the government,

[005-142] if he is lucky, of two packets or twenty a week. This lasts him

[005-143] with care about two days. After that he goes smokeless unless he

[005-144] has friends at home to send him a supply. I had friends in [[london|London]]

[005-145] who sent me about five hundred fags a week, and I was consequently

[005-146] popular while they lasted. This took off some of the curse of being

[005-147] a lance corporal.

[005-148] Tommy has his favorite in "fags" like anybody else. He likes above

[005-149] all Wild Woodbines. This cigarette is composed of glue, cheap

[005-150] paper, and a poor quality of hay. Next in his affection comes

[005-151] Goldflakes--pretty near as bad.

[005-152] People over here who have boys at the front mustn't forget the

[005-153] cigarette supply. Send them along early and often. There'll never

[005-154] be too many. Smoking is one of the soldier's few comforts. Two

[005-155] bits' worth of makin's a week will help one lad make life

[005-156] endurable. It's cheap at the price. Come through for the smoke

[005-157] fund whenever you get the chance.

[005-158] Café life among us at Petite-Saens was mostly drinking and

[005-159] gambling. That is not half as bad as it sounds. The drinking was

[005-160] mostly confined to the slushy [[france|French]] beer and vin blanc and citron.

[005-161] Whiskey and absinthe were barred.

[005-162] The gambling was on a small scale, necessarily, the British soldier

[005-163] not being at any time a bloated plutocrat. At the same time the

[005-164] games were continuous. "House" was the most popular. This is a game

[005-165] similar to the "lotto" we used to play as children. The backers

[005-166] distribute cards having fifteen numbers, forming what they call a

[005-167] school. Then numbered cardboard squares are drawn from a bag, the

[005-168] numbers being called out. When a number comes out which appears on

[005-169] your card, you cover it with a bit of match. If you get all your

[005-170] numbers covered, you call out "house", winning the pot. If there

[005-171] are ten people in at a franc a head, the banker holds out two

[005-172] francs, and the winner gets eight.

[005-173] It is really quite exciting, as you may get all but one number

[005-174] covered and be rooting for a certain number to come. Usually when

[005-175] you get as close as that and sweat over a number for ten minutes,

[005-176] somebody else gets his first. Corporal [[wellsie|Wells]] described the game as

[005-177] one where the winner "'ollers 'ouse and the rest 'ollers 'ell!"

[005-178] Some of the nicknames for the different numbers remind one of the

[005-179] slang of the crap shooter. For instance, "Kelly's eye" means one.

[005-180] "Clickety [[click]]" is sixty-six. "Top of the house" is ninety. Other

[005-181] games are "crown and anchor", which is a dice game, and "pontoon",

[005-182] which is a card game similar to "twenty-one" or "seven and a half."

[005-183] Most of these are mildly discouraged by the authorities, "house"

[005-184] being the exception. But in any _estaminet_ in a billet town you'll

[005-185] find one or all of them in progress all the time. The winner

[005-186] usually spends his winnings for beer, so the money all goes the

[005-187] same way, game or no game.

[005-188] When there are no games on, there is usually a sing-song going. We

[005-189] had a merry young nuisance in our platoon named Rolfe, who had a

[005-190] voice like a frog and who used to insist upon singing on all

[005-191] occasions. Rolfie would climb on the table in the _estaminet_ and

[005-192] sing numerous unprintable verses of his own, entitled "Oh, What a

[005-193] Merry Plyce is Hengland." The only redeeming feature of this song

[005-194] was the chorus, which everybody would roar out and which went like

[005-195] this:

[005-196] Cheer, ye beggars, cheer!

[005-197] Britannia rules the wave!

[005-198] 'Ard times, short times

[005-199] Never'll come agyne.

[005-200] Shoutin' out at th' top o' yer lungs:

[005-201] Damn the German army!

[005-202] Oh, wot a lovely plyce is Hengland!

[005-203] Our ten days _en repos_ at Petite-Saens came to an end all too

[005-204] soon.

[005-205] On the last day we lined up for our official "bawth."

[005-206] Petite-Saens was a coal-[[lens|mining town]]. The mines were still operated,

[005-207] but only at night--this to avoid shelling from [[boche|the Boche]]

[005-208] long-distance artillery, which are fully capable of sending shells

[005-209] and hitting the mark at eighteen miles. The water system of the

[005-210] town depended upon the pumping apparatus of the mines. Every

[005-211] morning early, before the pressure was off, all hands would turn

[005-212] out for a general "sluicing" under the hydrants. We were as clean

[005-213] as could be and fairly free of "[[cootie|cooties]]" at the end of a week, but

[005-214] official red tape demanded that we go through an authorized

[005-215] scouring.

[005-216] On the last day we lined up for this at dawn before an old

[005-217] warehouse which had been fitted with crude showers. We were turned

[005-218] in twenty in a batch and were given four minutes to soap ourselves

[005-219] all over and rinse off. I was in the last lot and had just lathered

[005-220] up good and plenty when the water went dead. If you want to reach

[005-221] the acme of stickiness, try this stunt. I felt like the inside of a

[005-222] mucilage bottle for a week.

[005-223] After the official purification we were given clean underwear. And

[005-224] then there was a howl. The fresh underthings had been boiled and

[005-225] sterilized, but the immortal cootie had come through unscathed and

[005-226] in all its vigor. Corporal Wells raised a pathetic wail:

[005-227] "Blimme eyes, mytie! I got more'n two 'undred now an' this supposed

[005-228] to be a bloomin' clean shirt! Why, the blinkin' thing's as lousy

[005-229] as a cookoo now, an me just a-gittin' rid o' the bloomin' [[chat|chats]] on

[005-230] me old un. Strike me pink if it hain't a bleedin' crime! Some one

[005-231] ought to write to John Bull abaht it!"

[005-232] _John Bull_ is the English paper of that name published by Horatio

[005-233] Bottomley, which makes a specialty of publishing complaints from

[005-234] soldiers and generally criticising the conduct of army affairs.

[005-235] Well, we got through the bath and the next day were on our way.

[005-236] This time it was up the line to another sector. My one taste of

[005-237] trench [[battle|action]] had made me keen for more excitement, and in spite of

[005-238] the comfortable time at Petite-Saens, I was glad to go. I was yet

[005-239] to know the real horrors and hardships of modern warfare. There

[005-240] were many days in those to come when I looked back upon

[005-241] Petite-Saens as a sort of heaven.
