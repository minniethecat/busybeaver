# Patrol

## Overview

Patrol work was among the most nerve-wracking duties a soldier could draw. Small groups of men would venture into [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] under cover of darkness to scout enemy positions, cut [[barbed-wire|wire]], or simply ensure that the [[germans|Germans]] had not crept closer. Unlike a [[trench-raid|raid]], a patrol's primary objective was observation and intelligence gathering, not [[battle|combat]] — but the distinction was academic when any encounter in [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] could turn lethal in an instant.

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] volunteered for patrol duty and, remarkably, came to prefer it. His account provides one of the memoir's most vivid descriptions of the psychological landscape between the lines.

## In the Memoir

Holmes's introduction to patrol work was a single night's duty:

> I also had one night on patrol, which later became my favorite
> — (004-089)

What made patrol appealing to Holmes was the relative freedom it offered. On patrol, a soldier operated independently, away from the rigid [[trench-discipline|discipline]] of the trench. The darkness provided a kind of liberty — terrifying, but preferable to the claustrophobia of the line.

He later expanded on patrol work in detail:

> I want to say a word right here about patrol work in general
> — (007-001)

The chapter that follows is one of the memoir's most reflective passages. Holmes describes the strange etiquette of [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] — both sides patrolled, and encounters were not always hostile. Sometimes patrols from opposing armies would pass within yards of each other in the darkness, each pretending not to notice.

Holmes recounts a grim discovery from one patrol:

> Some idiotic joker on patrol hung a helmet on the foot
> — (007-019)

The macabre humor of the [[trench|trenches]] — a dead man's [[tin-hat|helmet]] used as a marker — was the kind of thing patrols encountered.

## Techniques

Patrols moved silently, often crawling on their bellies through mud and [[barbed-wire|wire]]. [[equipment|Equipment]] was stripped to the essentials: rifle, bayonet, and perhaps a couple of [[grenade|Mills bomb]]s for emergencies. Speech was forbidden; commands were passed by hand signals. The patrol leader carried the responsibility of navigation in complete darkness, often using landmarks like [[crater|shell craters]] or the contours of the terrain.

Holmes notes that after a few successful patrols, the work became "cushy" — a trench term for easy or comfortable:

> the patrol was cushy for us
> — (006-192)
