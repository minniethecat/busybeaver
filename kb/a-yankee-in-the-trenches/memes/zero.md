## Overview

"Zero" was the universal military timing convention on the [[france|Western Front]] during World War I. In "A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es," [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] explains: "The system on the [[france|Western Front]], and, in fact, all fronts, is to indicate the time fixed for any event as zero. Anything before or after is spoken of as plus or minus zero" (003-020, 003-021, 003-022). This simple but powerful convention synchronized complex military ope[[trench-rations|rations]] — from [[raid|trench raid]]s to major offensives — across multiple units without revealing actual clock times over insecure communication channels.

## In the Trenches

For infantrymen like Holmes, zero hour was not an abstract planning concept but a visceral reality. The moments before zero — counted down as "zero minus ten," "zero minus five" — were often the most terrifying. Holmes describes the feeling: like "a condemned man in his last hour" (010-063). The tension built minute by minute until the appointed moment arrived.

When zero came, it brought release: artillery opened up, men went [[over-the-top|over the top]], and the waiting ended. Holmes records the sequence precisely: "At ten minutes of eleven, our artillery opened up. The [[barrage|barrage fire]] kept up right up to zero, as per schedule" (003-038, 003-052). At zero exactly, the [[estaminet|bar]]rage lifted or shifted, and the infantry advanced.

The timing convention also governed smaller ope[[trench-rations|rations]]. Before a [[raid|trench raid]], Holmes notes: "The time set for our stunt was eleven P.M. Eleven o'clock was 'zero'" (003-020). Even for a minor [[trench-raid|raid]], the same [[trench-discipline|discipline]]d timing applied — a testament to the standardization of military procedure on the [[france|Western Front]].

## Narrative Examples

Holmes provides a firsthand account of the zero-hour experience during a [[trench-raid|raid]]. After blacking their faces and receiving a hearty meal — "rather like giving a condemned man a hearty meal, but grub is always acceptable to a soldier" — the party prepared (003-020). The countdown was precise:

> The barrage fire kept up right up to zero, as per schedule. At zero [003-052, 003-038]

Another passage captures the psychological weight of the countdown:

> At zero minus ten the word went [010-063]

The clinical language — "zero minus ten" — contrasts with the emotional reality: men waiting in darkness, knowing that at the appointed moment they would rise and advance into [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]].

## Military Context

The zero-hour convention addressed a fundamental military problem: how to coordinate widely dispersed units to act simultaneously without revealing precise timing to the enemy. In an era before portable radios, attacks had to be pre-scheduled down to the minute. Using "zero" as a reference point meant that written orders could say "zero plus two hours" without stating the actual time — if the enemy intercepted the order, they still wouldn't know when the operation began.

The system was used across all theaters of the war. [[artillery|Artillery]] [[estaminet|bar]]rages, infantry advances, relief rotations, and supply movements were all scheduled relative to zero. The convention was so deeply ingrained that soldiers used "zero" as everyday vocabulary, and the phrase "zero hour" entered the English language permanently as a metaphor for any decisive moment.

For the infantry, zero hour represented the culmination of hours or days of preparation and dread. It was the pivot point between waiting and [[battle|action]], between relative safety and maximum danger. Holmes's matter-of-fact descriptions reflect how deeply this timing convention structured the soldier's experience of [[battle|combat]].
