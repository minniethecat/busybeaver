## Overview

Lieutenant Blofeld was the officer in command of [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s platoon and one of the memoir's most vividly drawn characters. Competent, democratic in manner, and personally brave, Blofeld represents the ideal of the British front-line officer — a man who earned rather than demanded respect, and who led from the front when the bullets began to fly.

## First Meeting

Holmes introduces Blofeld with evident approval. On his first night in the [[trench|trenches]], a young officer pushed into the cramped [[dug-out|dugout]]:

> His name was Blofeld. Blofeld was most democratic. He shook hands with the new men and said he hoped we'd be live wires, and then he told us what he wanted us to do.
> (002-171)

Unlike some officers who maintained a rigid class distance from their men, Blofeld treated the enlisted soldiers with genuine respect. Holmes, as an American volunteer in a British regiment, was particularly sensitive to such treatment. When Blofeld called for volunteers for a dangerous [[patrol]], Holmes stepped forward:

> Blofeld accepted me, and one of the other new men offered. He was taken too.
> (002-182)

Holmes later learned that Blofeld had given him "a clean bill of moral character" — an assessment that mattered deeply to the newcomer seeking acceptance among the Tommies.

## The Trench Raid

Blofeld's defining moment came when he organized and led the battalion's first [[raid|trench raid]]. The staff was anxious for its success, and Blofeld personally drilled the fifty-man [[raid|raiding party]]:

> Blofeld, who had organized the raid, beat our instructions into us until we knew them by heart.
> (003-005)

During the [[trench-raid|raid]] itself, Blofeld led from the front. When the [[gas-weapon|gas attack]] commenced, he dashed through the trench to check on his men:

> Blofeld came dashing up just then, very much excited because he found we had not put on our masks, through some slip-up in the orders.
> (003-046)

He personally threw [[grenade|Mills bomb]]s into German positions during the close-[[billets|quarters]] [[battle|fight]]ing in the trench system:

> Blofeld chucked in two or three Mills bombs.
> (003-110)

## Leadership Style

What distinguished Blofeld from other officers in the memoir was his democratic approach. [[captain-green|Captain Green]] issued orders from above; Blofeld shared the danger alongside his men. His willingness to shake hands with new recruits, to explain rather than simply command, and to be first into the [[battle|fight]] made him the kind of officer soldiers would follow voluntarily rather than merely obey.

After the [[trench-raid|raid]], when [[sergeant-page|Sergeant Page]] briefed the men on their performance, Blofeld's approval was noted — another sign of his concern for his men's morale and recognition.
