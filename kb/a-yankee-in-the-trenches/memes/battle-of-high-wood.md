## Overview

The Battle of High Wood was one of the most significant and costly [[battle|engagement]]s in [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]] offensive of 1916. [[high-wood|High Wood]] (Bois des Fourcaux) was a heavily fortified German position — a dense woodland that dominated the surrounding terrain. Holmes's battalion was committed to the [[battle|fight]] for this critical objective, and the battle would prove to be the climax of his war.

The [[battle|engagement]] was notable for the debut of [[tank|tanks]] in close support of infantry, making it one of the first ope[[trench-rations|rations]] in history to combine armored vehicles with ground assault troops.

## In the Memoir

Holmes describes the battle as one of the defining moments of the war:

> "Lads, I want to tell you that there is to be a most important battle — one of the most important in the whole war."
> (010-020)

The terrain itself bore the scars of prolonged [[battle|fight]]ing:

> High Wood rose covered with the splintered remnants of what had once been a forest.
> (010-077)

## The Assault

Holmes's battalion went into [[battle|action]] at [[high-wood|High Wood]] with [[tank|tanks]] in support. He describes the advance of the [[tank|tanks]] toward the wood:

> The tanks passed beyond us and half-way up the slope toward High Wood.
> (010-020)

The [[battle|fight]]ing was intense and disorienting. Holmes was separated from his unit during the assault, a common experience in the chaos of battle. The objective was achieved at heavy cost:

> The net result of the battle of High Wood was that our troops gained their objective.
> (010-159)

Holmes's participation in the battle ended when he was [[casualty|wounded]] by shrapnel:

> At the end of it my participation in the battle of High Wood ended.
> (010-123)

## Tank Debut

Holmes witnessed the first [[tank|tanks]] in [[battle|action]] at [[high-wood|High Wood]]:

> I obtained my first view of tanks at the battle of High Wood.
> (011-012)

The [[tank|tanks]] made a strong impression on the men, representing a technological leap in warfare. Holmes sheltered behind one during the assault, and he later wrote of the psychological impact of these new machines on both the men who fought alongside them and the enemy who faced them.

## Prisoners

The British took large numbers of prisoners at [[high-wood|High Wood]]:

> At the battle of High Wood the prisoners we took numbered over a thousand.
> (011-109)

The prisoners were described as being in poor condition — exhausted, [[hun]]gry, and demoralized by the intensity of the Allied assault.

## Aftermath

When Holmes found his battalion again after being separated in the [[battle|fight]]ing, the battle was winding down:

> When I found my battalion, the battle of High Wood had pretty well died down.
> (012-001)

Shortly afterward, [[lieutenant-may|Lieutenant May]] led Holmes and a [[grenade|bomb]]ing party on a night [[trench-raid|raid]] to capture a German [[mine-explosion|sap]] beyond [[high-wood|High Wood]], an operation that [[captain-green|Captain Green]] personally briefed them for:

> "Just beyond High Wood and to the left there is a sap or small trench leading to the sunken road that lies between the towns of Albert and Bapaume. That position commands a military point that we find necessary to hold before we can make another attack."
> (012-034)
