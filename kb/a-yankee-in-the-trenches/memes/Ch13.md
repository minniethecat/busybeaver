[013-001] When I rejoined the battalion they were just going into [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]]

[013-002] again after a two weeks' rest. They didn't like it a bit.

[013-003] "Gawd lumme," says [[wellsie|Wellsie]], "'ave we got to [[battle|fight]] th' 'ole blinkin'

[013-004] war. Is it right? I awsks yer. Is it?"

[013-005] It was all wrong. We had been told after [[high-wood|High Wood]] that we would

[013-006] not have to go into action again in that part of the line but that

[013-007] we would have a month of rest and after that would be sent up to

[013-008] the Ypres sector. "Wipers" hadn't been any garden of roses early in

[013-009] the war, but it was paradise now compared with the [[somme|Somme]].

[013-010] It was a sad lot of men when we swung out on the road again back to

[013-011] the Somme, and there was less singing than usual. That first night

[013-012] we remained at [[mametz|Mametz Wood]]. We figured that we would get to kip

[013-013] while the [[kip|kipping]] was good. There were some old [[boche|Boche]] dug-outs in

[013-014] fair condition, and we were in a fair way to get comfortable. No

[013-015] luck!

[013-016] We were hardly down to a good sleep when C company was called to

[013-017] fall in without equipment, and we knew that meant [[fatigue]] of some

[013-018] sort. I have often admired the unknown who invented that word

[013-019] "fatigue" as applied in a military term. He used it as a disguise

[013-020] for just plain hard work. It means anything whatever in the way of

[013-021] duty that does not have to do directly with the manning of the

[013-022] [[trench|trenches]].

[013-023] This time we clicked a [[trench-fatigue|burial fatigue]]. It was my first. I never

[013-024] want another. I took a party of ten men and we set out, armed with

[013-025] picks and shovels, and, of course, [[rifle|rifles]] and bandoliers (cloth

[013-026] pockets containing fifty rounds of ammo).

[013-027] We hiked three miles up to High Wood and in the early morning began

[013-028] the job of getting some of the dead under ground. We were almost

[013-029] exactly in the same place from which we had gone over after the

[013-030] [[tank|tanks]]. I kept expecting all the time to run across the bodies of

[013-031] some of our own men. It was a most unpleasant feeling.

[013-032] Some cleaning up had already been done, so the place was not so bad

[013-033] as it had been, but it was bad enough. The advance had gone forward

[013-034] so far that we were practically out of shell range, and we were

[013-035] safe working.

[013-036] The burial method was to dig a pit four feet deep and big enough to

[013-037] hold six men. Then we packed them in. The worst part of it was that

[013-038] most of the bodies were pretty far gone and in the falling away

[013-039] stage. It was hard to move them. I had to put on my [[gas-warfare|gas mask]] to

[013-040] endure the stench and so did some of the other men. Some who had

[013-041] done this work before rather seemed to like it.

[013-042] I would search a body for identification marks and jot down the

[013-043] data found on a piece of paper. When the man was buried under, I

[013-044] would stick a rifle up over him and tuck the record into the trap

[013-045] in the butt of the gun where the oil bottle is carried.

[013-046] When the pioneers came up, they would remove the rifle and

[013-047] substitute a little wooden cross with the name painted on it. The

[013-048] indifference with which the men soon came to regard this burial

[013-049] fatigue was amazing. I remember one incident of that first morning,

[013-050] a thing that didn't seem at all shocking at the time, but which,

[013-051] looking back upon it, illustrates the matter-of-factness of the

[013-052] soldier's viewpoint on death.

[013-053] "Hi sye, Darby," sang out one fellow. "Hi got a blighter 'ere wif

[013-054] only one leg. Wot'll Hi do wif 'im?"

[013-055] "Put him under with only one, you blinking idiot," said I.

[013-056] Presently he called out again, this time with a little note of

[013-057] satisfaction and triumph in his voice.

[013-058] "Darby, Hi sye. I got a leg for that bleeder. Fits 'im perfect."

[013-059] Well, I went over and took a look and to my horror found that the

[013-060] fool had stuck a German leg on the body, high boot and all. I

[013-061] wouldn't stand for that and had it out again. I wasn't going to

[013-062] send a poor fellow on his last pilgrimage with any [[germans|Boche]] leg, and

[013-063] said so. Later I heard this undertaking genius of a [[british-army|Tommy]] grousing

[013-064] and muttering to himself.

[013-065] "Cawn't please Darby," says he, "no matter wot. Fawncy the

[013-066] blighter'd feel better wif two legs, if one was Boche. It's a fair

[013-067] crime sendin' 'im hover the river wif only one."

[013-068] I was sure thankful when that burial fatigue was over, and early in

[013-069] the forenoon we started back to rest.

[013-070] Rest, did I say? Not that trip. We were hardly back to Mametz and

[013-071] down to breakfast when along came an order to fall in for a

[013-072] [[ration-party|carrying party]]. All that day we carried boxes of Millses up to the

[013-073] dump that was by High Wood, three long miles over hard going. Being

[013-074] a corporal had its compensations at this game, as I had no carrying

[013-075] to do; but inasmuch as the bombs were moved two boxes to a man, I

[013-076] got my share of the hard work helping men out of holes and lending

[013-077] a hand when they were mired.

[013-078] Millses are packed with the bombs and detonators separate in the

[013-079] box, and the men are very careful in the handling of them. So the

[013-080] moving of material of this kind is wearing.

[013-081] Another line of man-killers that we had to move were "toffy

[013-082] apples." This quaint toy is a huge [[grenade|bomb]], perfectly round and

[013-083] weighing sixty pounds, with a long rod or pipe which inserts into

[013-084] the mortar. Toffy apples are about the awkwardest thing imaginable

[013-085] to carry.

[013-086] This carrying stunt went on for eight long days and nights. We

[013-087] worked on an average sixteen hours a day. It rained nearly all the

[013-088] time, and we never got dried out. The food was awful, as the

[013-089] advance had been so fast that it was almost impossible to get up

[013-090] the supplies, and the men in the front trenches had the first pick

[013-091] of the grub. It was also up to us to get the water up to the front.

[013-092] The method on this was to use the five-gallon gasoline cans.

[013-093] Sometimes they were washed out, oftener they weren't. Always the

[013-094] water tasted of [[gas-weapon|gas]]. We got the same thing, and several times I

[013-095] became sick drinking the stuff.

[013-096] When that eight days of carrying was over, we were so fed up that

[013-097] we didn't care whether we clicked or not. Maybe it was good mental

[013-098] preparation for what was to come, for on top of it all it turned

[013-099] out that we were to go [[over-the-top|over the top]] in another big attack.

[013-100] When we got that news, I got Dinky out and scolded him. Maybe I'd

[013-101] better tell you all about Dinky before I go any farther. Soldiers

[013-102] are rather prone to superstitions. Relieved of all responsibility

[013-103] and with most of their thinking done for them, they revert

[013-104] surprisingly quick to a state of more or less savage mentality.

[013-105] Perhaps it would be better to call the state childlike. At any rate

[013-106] they accumulate a lot of fool superstitions and hang to them. The

[013-107] height of folly and the superlative invitation to bad luck is

[013-108] lighting three [[fag|fags]] on one match. When that happens one of the

[013-109] three is sure to [[click]] it soon.

[013-110] As one out of any group of three anywhere stands a fair chance of

[013-111] "getting his", fag or no fag, the thing is reasonably sure to work

[013-112] out according to the popular belief. Most every man has his unlucky

[013-113] day in the trenches. One of mine was Monday. The others were

[013-114] Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, Friday, Saturday, and Sunday.

[013-115] Practically every soldier carries some kind of mascot or charm. A

[013-116] good many are crucifixes and religious tokens. Some are coins.

[013-117] Corporal Wells had a sea shell with three little black spots on

[013-118] it. He considered three his lucky number. Thirteen was mine. My

[013-119] mascot was the aforesaid and much revered Dinky. Dinky was and is a

[013-120] small black cat made of velvet. He's entirely flat except his head,

[013-121] which is becomingly round with yellow glass eyes. I carried Dinky

[013-122] inside my tunic always and felt safer with him there. He hangs at

[013-123] the head of my bed now and I feel better with him there. I realize

[013-124] perfectly that all this sounds like tommyrot, and that superstition

[013-125] may be a relic of barbarism and ignorance. Never mind! Wellsie

[013-126] sized the situation up one day when we were talking about this very

[013-127] thing.

[013-128] "Maybe my shell ayen't doin' me no good," says Wells. "Maybe Dinky

[013-129] ayen't doin' you no good. But 'e ayen't doin' ye no 'arm. So 'ang

[013-130] on to 'im."

[013-131] I figure that if there's anything in war that "ayen't doin' ye no

[013-132] 'arm", it is pretty good policy to "'ang on to it."

[013-133] It was Sunday the eighth day of October that the order came to move

[013-134] into what was called the "O.G.I.", that is, the old German first

[013-135] line. You will understand that this was the line the Boches had

[013-136] occupied a few days before and out of which they had been driven in

[013-137] the [[big-push|Big Push]]. In front of this trench was Eaucort Abbaye, which had

[013-138] been razed with the aid of the tanks.

[013-139] We had watched this battle from the rear from the slight elevation

[013-140] of High Wood, and it had been a wonderful sight to see other men go

[013-141] out over the top without having ourselves to think about. They had

[013-142] poured out, wave after wave, a large part of them Scotch with their

[013-143] kilted rumps swinging in perfect time, a smashing barrage going on

[013-144] ahead, and the tanks lumbering along with a kind of clumsy majesty.

[013-145] When they hit the objective, the tanks crawled in and made short

[013-146] work of it.

[013-147] The infantry had hard work of it after the positions were taken, as

[013-148] there were numerous underground caverns and passages which had to

[013-149] be mopped out. This was done by dropping smoke bombs in the

[013-150] entrances and smoking the Boches out like bees.

[013-151] When we came up, we inherited these underground shelters, and they

[013-152] were mighty comfortable after the kipping in the muck. There were

[013-153] a lot of souvenirs to be picked up, and almost everybody annexed

[013-154] helmets and other truck that had been left behind by the Germans.

[013-155] Sometimes it was dangerous to go after souvenirs too greedily. The

[013-156] inventive Hun had a habit of fixing up a body with a bomb under it

[013-157] and a tempting wrist watch on the hand. If you started to take the

[013-158] watch, the bomb went off, and after that you didn't care what time

[013-159] it was.

[013-160] I accumulated a number of very fine razors, and one of the

[013-161] saw-tooth [[bayonet|bayonets]] the Boche pioneers use. This is a perfectly

[013-162] hellish weapon that slips in easily and mangles terribly when it is

[013-163] withdrawn. I had thought that I would have a nice collection of

[013-164] souvenirs to take to [[blighty|Blighty]] if I ever got leave. I got the leave

[013-165] all right, and shortly, but the collection stayed behind.

[013-166] The [[dug-out]] that Number 10 drew was built of concrete and was big

[013-167] enough to accommodate the entire platoon. We were well within the

[013-168] Boche range and early in the day had several [[casualty|casualties]], one of

[013-169] them a chap named Stransfield, a young Yorkshireman who was a very

[013-170] good friend of mine. Stransie was sitting on the top step cleaning

[013-171] his rifle and was blown to pieces by a falling shell. After that we

[013-172] kept to cover all day and slept all the time. We needed it after

[013-173] the exhausting work of the past eight days.

[013-174] It was along about dark when I was awakened by a runner from

[013-175] headquarters, which was in a [[dug-out]] a little way up the line, with

[013-176] word that the platoon commanders were wanted. I happened to be in

[013-177] command of the platoon, as Mr. [[lieutenant-blofeld|Blofeld]] was acting second in command

[013-178] of the company, [[sergeant-page|Sergeant Page]] was away in [[havre|Havre]] as instructor for a

[013-179] month, and I was next senior.

[013-180] I thought that probably this was merely another detail for some

[013-181] fatigue, so I asked Wells if he would go. He did and in about half

[013-182] an hour came back with a face as long as my arm. I was sitting on

[013-183] the fire step cleaning my rifle and Wellsie sank dejectedly down

[013-184] beside me.

[013-185] "Darby," he sighed hopelessly, "wot th' blinkin' 'ell do you think

[013-186] is up now?"

[013-187] I hadn't the faintest idea and said so. I had, however, as the

[013-188] educated [[bones|Bones]] used to say "a premonition of impending disaster."

[013-189] As a premonitor I was a success. Disaster was right.

[013-190] Wellsie sighed again and spilled the news.

[013-191] "We're goin' over th' bleedin' top at nine. We don't 'ave to carry

[013-192] no tools. We're in the first bloomin' wave."

[013-193] Going without tools was supposed to be a sort of consolation for

[013-194] being in the first wave. The other three waves carry either picks

[013-195] or shovels. They consolidate the trenches after they have been

[013-196] taken by the first wave. That is, they turn the trench around,

[013-197] facing the other way, to be ready for a counter attack. It is a

[013-198] miserable job. The tools are heavy and awkward, and the last waves

[013-199] get the cream of the artillery fire, as the Boche naturally does

[013-200] not want to take the chance of [[shell|shelling]] the first wave for fear of

[013-201] getting his own men. However, the first wave gets the machine-gun

[013-202] fire and gets it good. At that the first wave is the preference. I

[013-203] have heard hundreds of men say so. Probably the reason is that a

[013-204] bullet, unless it is explosive, makes a relatively clean wound,

[013-205] while a shell fragment may mangle fearfully.

[013-206] Wells and I were talking over the infernal injustice of the

[013-207] situation when another runner arrived from the [[sergeant-major|Sergeant Major]]'s,

[013-208] ordering us up for the [[rum-ration|rum issue]]. I went up for the rum and left

[013-209] Wells to break the news about going over.

[013-210] I got an extra large supply, as the Sergeant Major was good

[013-211] humored. It was the last rum he ever served. I got enough for the

[013-212] full platoon and then some, which was a lot, as the platoon was

[013-213] well down in numbers owing to casualties. I went among the boys

[013-214] with a spoon and the rum in a [[dixie|mess tin]] and served out two tots

[013-215] instead of the customary one. After that all hands felt a little

[013-216] better, but not much. They were all fagged out after the week's

[013-217] hard work. I don't think I ever saw a more discouraged lot getting

[013-218] ready to go over. For myself I didn't seem to care much, I was in

[013-219] such rotten condition physically. I rather hoped it would be my

[013-220] last time.
