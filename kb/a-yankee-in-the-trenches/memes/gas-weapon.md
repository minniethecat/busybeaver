# Poison Gas

**Poison [[gas-warfare|gas]]** was one of the defining horrors of the First World War — a weapon that could blind, choke, burn, and kill without warning. First deployed by the [[germans|Germans]] in 1915, [[gas-warfare|gas]] became a permanent feature of [[trench|trench warfare]], requiring every soldier to carry a [[gas-mask|gas helmet]] and live with the constant anxiety of a [[gas-warfare|gas]] alarm.

## In the Narrative

Holmes's memoir opens with him recovering from a gas attack — the incident that frames his entire war story:

> "Once, on the Somme in the fall of 1916, when I had been over a month and had been gassed…" [001-001]

Training included education about the various types of [[gas-warfare|gas]]:

> "…the various forms of gas and protection from it, barbed…" [001-227]

Gas [[tin-hat|helmet]]s were essential equipment, and replacement was constant:

> "…second gas helmet. A good many of the men had been…" [002-023]

Holmes describes the apparatus for releasing [[gas-warfare|gas]] across [[no-mans-land|no man's land]]:

> "gas containers. They were arranged…" [003-013]

The cylinders were placed in the trench system for offensive use:

> "sandbags. This was the poison gas ready for release over the top…" [003-015]

The author's attitude toward [[gas-warfare|gas]] was grim, hoping it would destroy the enemy:

> "gas. May it finish the blighters…" [003-045]

Wind direction was critical — [[gas-warfare|gas]] could blow back on its users:

> "…wind blowing, which carried the gas…" [003-049]

Gas masks were subject to daily inspection along with other equipment:

> "…breakfast. Inspection of ammo and gas masks. One hour drill. After…" [005-139]

The full burden of protective [[equipment|gear]] is catalogued:

> "…rounds of ammo, a tin hat, two gas helmets, and a lot of…" [008-072]

## Types of Gas

The war saw an escalating chemical arms race:

- **Chlorine** (1915): First used at Ypres; greenish-yellow cloud that attacked the [[gas-helmet|respirator]]y system
- **Phosgene** (1915): More lethal than chlorine; colorless and odorless; delayed effects
- **Mustard [[gas-warfare|gas]]** (1917): Caused severe blistering of skin, eyes, and lungs; persistent in the environment for days
- **Tear [[gas-warfare|gas]]**: Lachrymatory agents used to force soldiers to remove protective masks before a lethal attack

## Psychological Impact

More than its actual lethality ([[gas-warfare|gas]] caused about 4% of WWI deaths), [[gas-warfare|gas]] exerted a disproportionate psychological terror. It was invisible, silent, and struck without warning. Soldiers lived in constant low-grade anxiety about [[gas-warfare|gas]], and the sound of a [[gas-warfare|gas]] alarm produced instant, visceral fear. The [[gas-helmet|gas mask]] itself was a constant reminder — hot, claustrophobic, and never far from reach.
