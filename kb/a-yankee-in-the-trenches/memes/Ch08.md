[008-001] [[marching|Marching]], marching, marching,

[008-002] Always ruddy well marching.

[008-003] Marching all the morning,

[008-004] And marching all the night.

[008-005] Marching, marching, marching,

[008-006] Always ruddy well marching,

[008-007] Roll on till my time is up

[008-008] And I shall march no more.

[008-009] We sung it to the tune of "Holy, Holy, Holy", the whole blooming

[008-010] battalion. As we swung down the Boulevard Alsace-Lorraine in Amiens

[008-011] and passed the great cathedral up there to the left, on its little

[008-012] rise of ground, the chant lifted and lilted and throbbed up from

[008-013] near a thousand throats, much as the unisoned devotions of the

[008-014] olden monks must have done in other days.

[008-015] Ours was a holy cause, but despite the association of the tune the

[008-016] song was far from being a holy song. It was, rather, a chanted

[008-017] remonstrance against all hiking and against this one in

[008-018] particular.

[008-019] After our service at [[vimy-ridge|Vimy Ridge]] some one in authority somewhere

[008-020] decided that the 22nd [[battalion|Battalion]] and two others were not quite good

[008-021] enough for really smart work. We were, indeed, hard. But not hard

[008-022] enough. So some superior intellect squatting somewhere in the

[008-023] safety of the rear, with a finger on the pulse of the army, decreed

[008-024] that we were to get not only hard but tough; and to that end we

[008-025] were to hike. Hike we did.

[008-026] For more than three weeks we went from place to place with no

[008-027] apparent destination, wandering aimlessly up and down the

[008-028] country-side of Northern [[france|France]], imposing ourselves upon the people

[008-029] of little villages, shamming battle over their cultivated fields,

[008-030] and sleeping in their hen coops.

[008-031] I kept a diary on that hike. It was a thing forbidden, but I

[008-032] managed it. One manages many things out there. I have just read

[008-033] over that diary. There isn't much to it but a succession of town

[008-034] names,--Villiers du Bois, Maisincourt, Barly, Oneaux, [[canchy|Canchy]],

[008-035] Amiens, Bourdon, Villiers Bocage, Agenvilliers, Behencourt, and

[008-036] others that I failed to set down and have forgotten. We swept

[008-037] across that country, sweating under our packs, hardening our

[008-038] muscles, stopping here for a day, there for five days for

[008-039] extended-order drills and bayonet and musketry practice, and

[008-040] somewhere else for a sham battle. We were getting ready to go into

[008-041] [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]].

[008-042] The weather, by some perversity of fate, was fair during all of

[008-043] that hiking time. Whenever I was in the [[trench|trenches]] it always rained,

[008-044] whether the season warranted it or not. Except on days when we were

[008-045] scheduled to go [[over-the-top|over the top]]. Then, probably because rain will

[008-046] sometimes hold up a planned-for attack, it was always fair.

[008-047] On the hike, with good roads under foot, the soldier does not mind

[008-048] a little wet and welcomes a lot of clouds. No such luck for us. It

[008-049] was clear all the time. Not only clear but blazing hot August

[008-050] weather.

[008-051] On our first march out of the Cabaret Rouge communication trench we

[008-052] covered a matter of ten miles to a place called Villiers du Bois.

[008-053] Before that I had never fully realized just what it meant to go it

[008-054] in full heavy equipment.

[008-055] Often on the march I compared my lot with that of the medieval

[008-056] soldier who had done his fighting over these same fields of

[008-057] Northern France.

[008-058] The knight of the Middle Ages was all dressed up like a hardware

[008-059] store with, I should judge, about a hundred pounds of armor. But he

[008-060] rode a horse and had a squire or some such striker trailing along

[008-061] in the rear with the things to make him comfortable, when the

[008-062] fighting was over.

[008-063] The modern soldier gets very little help in his war making. He is,

[008-064] in fact, more likely to be helping somebody else than asking for

[008-065] assistance for himself. The soldier has two basic functions: first,

[008-066] to keep himself whole and healthy; second, to kill the other

[008-067] fellow. To the end that he may do these two perfectly simple

[008-068] things, he has to carry about eighty pounds of weight all the time.

[008-069] He has a blanket, a waterproof sheet, a greatcoat, extra boots,

[008-070] extra underwear, a haversack with iron [[trench-rations|rations]], entrenching tools,

[008-071] a bayonet, a water bottle, a [[mess-tin|mess kit]], a rifle, two hundred fifty

[008-072] rounds of ammo, a [[helmet|tin hat]], two [[gas-warfare|gas]] helmets, and a lot of

[008-073] miscellaneous small junk. All this is draped, hung, and otherwise

[008-074] disposed over his figure by means of a web harness having more

[008-075] hooks than a hatrack. He parallels the old-time knight only in the

[008-076] matter of the [[tin-hat|steel helmet]] and the rifle, which, with the bayonet,

[008-077] corresponds to the lance, sword, and battle-ax, three in one.

[008-078] The modern soldier carries all his worldly goods with him all the

[008-079] time. He hates to hike. But he has to.

[008-080] I remember very vividly that first day. The temperature was around

[008-081] 90°, and some fool officers had arranged that we start at one,--the

[008-082] very worst time of the day. The roads so near the front were

[008-083] pulverized, and the dust rose in dense clouds. The long straight

[008-084] lines of poplars beside the road were gray with it, and the heat

[008-085] waves shimmered up from the fields.

[008-086] Before we had gone five miles the men began to wilt. Right away I

[008-087] had some more of the joys of being a corporal brought [[blighty|home]] to me.

[008-088] I was already touched with trench fever and was away under par.

[008-089] That didn't make any difference.

[008-090] On the march, when the men begin to weaken, an officer is sure to

[008-091] trot up and say:

[008-092] "[[r-derby-holmes|Corporal Holmes]], just carry this man's rifle," or "Corporal

[008-093] Collins, take that man's pack. He's jolly well done."

[008-094] Seemingly the corporal never is supposed to be jolly well done. If

[008-095] one complained, his officer would look at him with astounded

[008-096] reproach and say:

[008-097] "Why, Corporal. We cawn't have this, you know! You are a

[008-098] Non-commissioned Officer, and you must set an example. You must,

[008-099] rahly."

[008-100] When we finally hit the town where our [[billet|billets]] were, we found our

[008-101] company quartered in an old barn. It was dirty, and there was a

[008-102] pigpen at one end,--very smelly in the August heat. We flopped in

[008-103] the ancient filth. The [[cootie|cooties]] were very active, as we were

[008-104] drenched with sweat and hadn't had a bath since heavens knew when.

[008-105] We had had about ten minutes' rest and were thinking about getting

[008-106] out of the harness when up came [[mad-harry|Mad Harry]], one of our "leftenants",

[008-107] and ordered us out for foot inspection.

[008-108] I don't want to say anything unfair about this man. He is dead now.

[008-109] I saw him die. He was brave. He knew his job all right, but he was

[008-110] a fine example of what an officer ought not to be. The only reason

[008-111] I speak of him is because I want to say something about officers in

[008-112] general.

[008-113] This Mad Harry,--I do not give his surname for obvious

[008-114] reasons,--was the son of one of the richest-new-rich-merchant

[008-115] families in [[england|England]]. He was very highly educated, had, I take it,

[008-116] spent the most of his life with the classics. He was long and thin

[008-117] and sallow and fish-eyed. He spoke in a low colorless monotone,

[008-118] absolutely without any inflection whatever. The men thought he was

[008-119] balmy. Hence the nickname Mad Harry.

[008-120] Mad Harry was a fiend for walking. And at the end of a twenty-mile

[008-121] hike in heavy marching order he would casually stroll alongside

[008-122] some sweating soldier and drone out,

[008-123] "I say, Private Stetson. Don't you just love to hike?"

[008-124] Then and there he made a lifelong personal enemy of Private

[008-125] Stetson. In the same or similar ways he made personal enemies of

[008-126] every private soldier he came in contact with.

[008-127] It may do no harm to tell how Mad Harry died. He came very near

[008-128] being shot by one of his own men.

[008-129] It was on the [[somme|Somme]]. We were in the middle of a bit of a show, and

[008-130] we were all hands down in shell holes with a heavy [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]]

[008-131] crackling overhead. I was in one hole, and in the next, which

[008-132] merged with mine, were two chaps who were cousins.

[008-133] Mad Harry came along, walking perfectly upright, regardless of

[008-134] danger, with his left arm shattered. He dropped into the next shell

[008-135] hole and with his expressionless drawl unshaken, said, "Private X.

[008-136] Dress my arm."

[008-137] Private X got out his own [[first-aid|emergency bandage]] and fixed the arm. When

[008-138] it was done Mad Harry, still speaking in his monotonous drone,

[008-139] said:

[008-140] "Now, Private X, get up out of this hole. Don't be hiding."

[008-141] Private X obeyed orders without a question. He climbed out and fell

[008-142] with a bullet through his head. His cousin, who was a very dear

[008-143] friend of the boy, evidently went more or less crazy at this. I saw

[008-144] him leap at Mad Harry and snatch his [[revolver|pistol]] from the holster. He

[008-145] was, I think, about to shoot his officer when a shell burst

[008-146] overhead and [[casualty|killed]] them both.

[008-147] Well, on this first day of the hike Mad Harry ordered us out for

[008-148] foot inspection, as I have said. I found that I simply couldn't get

[008-149] them out. They were in no condition for foot inspection,--hadn't

[008-150] washed for days. Harry came round and gave me a royal dressing down

[008-151] and ordered the whole bunch out for parade and helmet inspection.

[008-152] We were kept standing for an hour. You couldn't blame the men for

[008-153] hating an officer of that kind.

[008-154] It is only fair to say that Mad Harry was not a usual type of

[008-155] British officer. He simply carried to excess the idea of [[trench-discipline|discipline]]

[008-156] and unquestioning obedience. The principle of discipline is the

[008-157] guts and backbone of any army. I am inclined to think that it is

[008-158] more than half the making of any soldier. There has been a good

[008-159] deal of talk in the press about a democratic army. As a matter of

[008-160] fact fraternization between men and officers is impossible except

[008-161] in nations of exceptional temperament and imagination, like the

[008-162] French. The French are unique in everything. It follows that their

[008-163] army can do things that no other army can. It is common to see a

[008-164] French officer sitting in a cafe drinking with a private.

[008-165] In the British army that could not be. The new British army is more

[008-166] democratic, no doubt, than the old. But except in the heat of

[008-167] battle, no British officer can relax his dignity very much. With

[008-168] the exception of Mr. [[lieutenant-blofeld|Blofeld]], who was one of those rare characters

[008-169] who can be personally close and sympathetic and at the same time

[008-170] command respect and implicit obedience, I never knew a successful

[008-171] officer who did not seem to be almost of another world.

[008-172] Our Colonel was a fine man, but he was as dignified as a Supreme

[008-173] Court Judge. Incidentally he was as just. I have watched Colonel

[008-174] Flowers many times when he was holding orders. This is a kind of

[008-175] court when all men who have committed crimes and have been passed

[008-176] on by the captains appear before the Colonel.

[008-177] Colonel Flowers would sit smiling behind his hand, and would try

[008-178] his hardest to find "mitigating circumstances"; but when none could

[008-179] be [[dug-out|dug out]] he passed sentence with the last limit of severity, and

[008-180] the man that was up for orders didn't come again if he knew what

[008-181] was good for himself.

[008-182] I think that on the hike we all got to know our officers better

[008-183] than we had known them in the trenches. Their real characters came

[008-184] out. You knew how far you could go with them, and what was more

[008-185] important, how far you couldn't go.

[008-186] It was at Dieval that my rank as lance corporal was confirmed. It

[008-187] is customary, when a rookie has been made a non-com in training, to

[008-188] reduce him immediately when he gets to France. I had joined in the

[008-189] trenches and had volunteered for a [[trench-raid|raiding party]] and there had been

[008-190] no opportunity to reduce me. I had not, however, had a corporal's

[008-191] pay. My confirmation came at Dieval, and I was put on pay. I would

[008-192] have willingly sacrificed the pay and the so-called honor to have

[008-193] been a private.

[008-194] Our routine throughout the hike was always about the same, that is

[008-195] in the intervals when we were in any one place for a day or more.

[008-196] It was, up at six, breakfast of tea, bread, and bacon. Drill till

[008-197] noon; dinner; drill till five. After that nothing to do till

[008-198] to-morrow, unless we got night 'ops, which was about two nights out

[008-199] of three.

[008-200] There were few [[ymca|Y.M.C.A.]] huts so far behind the lines, and the short

[008-201] time up to nine was usually spent in the _[[estaminet|estaminets]]_. The games of

[008-202] house were in full blast all the time.

[008-203] On the hike we were paid weekly. Privates got five francs,

[008-204] corporals ten, and sergeants fifteen to twenty a week. That's a lot

[008-205] of money. Anything left over was held back to be paid when we got

[008-206] to Blighty. Parcels and mail came along with perfect regularity on

[008-207] that hike. It was and is a marvel to me how they do it. A battalion

[008-208] chasing around all over the place gets its stuff from Blighty day

[008-209] after day, right on the tick and without any question. I only hope

[008-210] that whatever the system is, our army will take advantage of it. A

[008-211] shortage of letters and luxury parcels is a real hardship.

[008-212] We finally brought up at a place called Oneux (pronounced Oh, no)

[008-213] and were there five days. I fell into luck here. It was customary,

[008-214] when we were marching on some unsuspecting village, to send the

[008-215] quartermaster sergeants ahead on bicycles to locate [[billet|billets]]. We had

[008-216] an old granny named Cypress, better known as Lizzie. The other

[008-217] sergeants were accustomed to flim-flam Lizzie to a finish on the

[008-218] selection of billets, with the result that C company usually slept

[008-219] in pigpens of stables.

[008-220] The day we approached Oneux, Lizzie was sick, and I was delegated

[008-221] to his job. I went into the town with the three other quartermaster

[008-222] sergeants, got them into an _estaminet_, bought about a dollar's

[008-223] worth of drinks, sneaked out the back door, and preempted the

[008-224] schoolhouse for C company. I also took the house next door, which

[008-225] was big and clean, for the officers. We were royally comfortable

[008-226] there, and the other companies used the stables that usually fell

[008-227] to our lot.

[008-228] As a reward, I suspect, I was picked for Orderly Corporal, a cushy

[008-229] job. We all of us had it fairly easy at Oneux. It was hot weather,

[008-230] and nights we used to sit out in the schoolhouse yard and talk

[008-231] about the war.

[008-232] Some of the opinions voiced out there with more frankness than any

[008-233] one would dare to use at home would, I am sure, shock some of the

[008-234] patriots. The fact is that any one who has fought in France wants

[008-235] peace, and the sooner the better.

[008-236] We had one old-timer, out since Mons, who habitually, night after

[008-237] night, day after day, would pipe up with the same old plaint.

[008-238] Something like this:

[008-239] "Hi arsks yer. Wot are we fightin' for? Wot'd th' Belgiums hever do

[008-240] fer us? Wot? Wot'd th' Rooshians hever do fer us? Wot's th' good of

[008-241] th' Frenchies? Wot's th' good of hanybody but th' Henglish? Gawd

[008-242] lumme! I'm fed up."

[008-243] And yet this man had gone out at the beginning and would [[battle|fight]]

[008-244] like the very devil, and I verily believe will be homesick for the

[008-245] trenches if he is alive when it is all over.

[008-246] [[bones|Bones]], who was educated and a thoughtful reader, had it figured out

[008-247] that the war was all due to the tyranny of the ruling classes, with

[008-248] the Kaiser the chief offender.

[008-249] A lot of the men wanted peace at any reasonable price. Anything, so

[008-250] they would get back to 'Arriet or Sadie or Maria.

[008-251] I should say offhand that there was not one man in a hundred who

[008-252] was fighting consciously for any great recognized principle. And

[008-253] yet, with all their grousing and criticism, and all their

[008-254] overwhelming desire to have it over with, every one of them was

[008-255] loyal and brave and a hard fighter.

[008-256] A good deal has been written about the brilliancy of the Canadians

[008-257] and the other Colonials. Too much credit cannot be given these men.

[008-258] In an attack there are no troops with more dash than the Canadians,

[008-259] but when it comes to taking punishment and hanging on a hopeless

[008-260] situation, there are no troops in the wide world who can equal,

[008-261] much less surpass, the English. Personally I think that comparisons

[008-262] should be avoided. All the Allies are doing their full duty with

[008-263] all that is in them.

[008-264] During most of the war talk, it was my habit to keep discreetly

[008-265] quiet. We were not in the war yet, and any remarks from me usually

[008-266] drew some hot shot about Mr. Wilson's "blankety-blinked bloomin'

[008-267] notes."

[008-268] There was another American, a chap named [[sanford|Sanford]] from Virginia,

[008-269] in B company, and he and I used to furnish a large amount of

[008-270] entertainment in these war talks. Sanford was a F.F.V. and didn't

[008-271] care who knew it. Also he thought General Lee was the greatest

[008-272] military genius ever known. One night he and I got started and had

[008-273] it hot and heavy as to the merits of the Civil War. This for some

[008-274] reason tickled the Tommies half to death, and after that they would

[008-275] egg us on to a discussion.

[008-276] One of them would slyly say, "Darby, 'oo th' blinkin' 'ell was this

[008-277] blighter, General Grant?"

[008-278] Or, "Hi sye, Sandy, Hi 'eard Darby syin' 'ow this General Lee was a

[008-279] bleedin' swab."

[008-280] Then Sanford and I would pass the wink and go at it tooth and

[008-281] nail. It was ridiculous, arguing the toss on a long-gone-by

[008-282] small-time scrap like the Civil War with the greatest show in

[008-283] history going on all around us. Anyway the Tommies loved it and

[008-284] would fairly howl with delight when we got to going good.

[008-285] It is strange, but with so many Americans in the British service, I

[008-286] ran up against very few. I remember one night when we were making a

[008-287] night march from one village to another, we stopped for the

[008-288] customary ten-minutes-in-the-hour rest. Over yonder in a field

[008-289] there was a camp of some kind,--probably [[artillery|field artillery]]. There was

[008-290] dim light of a fire and the low murmur of voices. And then a fellow

[008-291] began to sing in a nice tenor:

[008-292] Bury me not on the lone prairie

[008-293] Where the wild coyotes howl o'er me.

[008-294] Bury me down in the little churchyard

[008-295] In a grave just six by three.

[008-296] The last time I had heard that song was in New Orleans, and it was

[008-297] sung by a wild Texan. So I yelled, "Hello there, Texas."

[008-298] He answered, "Hello, Yank. Where from?"

[008-299] I answered, "[[boston|Boston]]."

[008-300] "Give my regards to Tremont Street and go to hell," says he. A gale

[008-301] of laughter came out of the night. Just then we had the order to

[008-302] fall in, and away we went. I'd like to know sometime who that chap

[008-303] was.

[008-304] After knocking about all over the north of France seemingly, we

[008-305] brought up at Canchy of a Sunday afternoon. Here the whole brigade,

[008-306] four battalions, had church parade, and after that the band played

[008-307] ragtime and the officers had a gabfest and compared medals, on top

[008-308] of which we were soaked with two hours' steady drill. We were at

[008-309] Canchy ten days, and they gave it to us good and plenty. We would

[008-310] drill all day and after dark it would be night 'ops. Finally so

[008-311] many men were going to the doctor worn out that he ordered a whole

[008-312] day and a half of rest.

[008-313] Mr. Blofeld on Saturday night suggested that, as we were going into

[008-314] the Somme within a few weeks, the non-coms ought to have a little

[008-315] blow-out. It would be the last time we would all ever be together.

[008-316] He furnished us with all the drinkables we could get away with,

[008-317] including some very choice Johnny Walker. There was a lot of

[008-318] canned stuff, mostly sardines. Mr. Blofeld loaned us the officers'

[008-319] phonograph.

[008-320] It was a large, wet night. Everybody made a speech or sang a song,

[008-321] and we didn't go home until morning. It was a farewell party, and

[008-322] we went the limit. If there is one thing that the Britisher does

[008-323] better than another, it is getting ready to die. He does it with a

[008-324] smile,--and he dies with a laugh.

[008-325] Poor chaps! Nearly all of them are pushing up the daisies somewhere

[008-326] in France. Those who are not are, with one or two exceptions, out

[008-327] of the army with broken bodies.
