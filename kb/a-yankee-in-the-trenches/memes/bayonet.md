# Bayonet

## Overview

The bayonet — a blade fixed to the muzzle of a rifle — occupied a paradoxical place in World War I. In an age of [[machine-gun|machine guns]], [[barrage|artillery barrage]]s, and [[gas-warfare|poison gas]], the bayonet was a weapon from an earlier century, yet it remained central to infantry training, doctrine, and the psychological preparation of soldiers for close [[battle|combat]]. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, the bayonet appears as a tool of lethal intimacy in the confined spaces of [[raid|trench raid]]s, a symbol of the "cold steel" that every infantryman was trained to use, and a constant physical weight added to the already overburdened soldier.

## Training and Doctrine

The [[british-army|British Army]] placed heavy emphasis on bayonet training. Holmes's own preparation included:

> an hour of physical drill or bayonet practice
> — (001-162)

He was classified as a:

> bayonet man, and was well grounded in bayonet fighting
> — (001-177)

This designation — "bayonet man" — was a specific role in a [[raid|raiding party]]. The bayonet men went first, clearing the way into the enemy trench with cold steel before the [[grenade|bomb]]ers and support troops followed. Holmes's training culminated in formal certification:

> I qualified and was sent out as bayonet instructor
> — (015-106)

The bayonet was not merely a weapon but a mark of status and specialization within the platoon.

## The Raid: Bayonet in Action

The bayonet's true moment came in the [[raid|trench raid]] — a stealth infiltration of enemy lines where speed, silence, and close-[[billets|quarters]] violence were paramount. Holmes describes his position in a [[raid|raiding party]]:

> ahead of me as first bayonet man
> — (003-064)

The detail is matter-of-fact, but the implication is stark: Holmes was the first man into the German trench, expected to kill with the blade. The proximity of [[battle|combat]] was terrifyingly intimate:

> I was almost on the point of his bayonet
> — (003-086)

In the chaos of the [[trench-raid|raid]], the bayonet was used not only against men but against obstacles:

> I had broken my bayonet prying the dug-out door off
> — (003-120)

The bayonet, designed for killing, became an improvised tool in the desperate moments of [[battle|combat]]. Holmes's understated account of the killing itself is all the more chilling for its brevity:

> I jumped down and in on him, and the bayonet went
> — (003-098)

The sentence trails off. The bayonet "went" — the reader is left to fill in what happened next.

## Equipment and Burden

The bayonet was a constant physical presence. In the litany of equipment carried by the infantryman, it appears alongside the rifle as a single combined burden:

> a bayonet, a water bottle, a mess kit, a rifle, two hundred fifty rounds of ammunition
> — (008-071)

Holmes specifically notes the combined weight:

> the weight of the steel helmet and the rifle, which, with the bayonet, is no mean burden
> — (008-076)

Before [[over-the-top|going over the top]], he describes a moment of grim ritual:

> I gave their bayonets a last fond rub, and I looked at my own
> — (010-067)

The "fond rub" — the almost affectionate attention to the blade before battle — captures the soldier's relationship with the weapon that might save his life or take another's.

## Variations and the Enemy

Holmes notes the distinctive German saw-tooth bayonet with a mixture of fear and disgust:

> saw-tooth bayonets the Boche pioneers use
> — (013-161)

The serrated blade was designed to cause maximum tissue damage and was deeply feared by Allied soldiers. It represented, in the [[tommy|British Tommy]]'s imagination, a particularly German form of cruelty — though both sides used the bayonet to equally lethal effect.

A German short rifle with bayonet, captured as a [[souvenir]], catches Holmes's eye in a [[dug-out]]:

> I had seen a new short German rifle in the dug-out with a bayonet
> — (014-005)

The bayonet, even when not in use, served as a trophy — a symbol of captured enemy equipment and, by extension, mastery over the foe.

## In the Attack

When the moment came to go [[over-the-top|over the top]], Holmes describes a telling ambivalence:

> I don't intend to rely too much on the bayonet
> — (010-069)

This was not cowardice but realism. In the open ground of an assault, the bayonet was a weapon of last resort — what mattered was reaching the enemy trench alive, through the storm of [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]] and shrapnel. But in the [[trench|trenches]] themselves, the bayonet remained king. As the German prisoners were brought in:

> packed in, with the bayonets gleaming
> — (014-066)

The gleaming bayonets of the guards, visible even in the dark, embodied the power of the victors over the captured.
