[001-001] Once, on [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]] in the fall of 1916, when I had been over the

[001-002] top and was being carried back somewhat disfigured but still in the

[001-003] ring, a cockney stretcher bearer shot this question at me:

[001-004] "Hi sye, Yank. Wot th' bloody 'ell are you in this bloomin' row

[001-005] for? Ayen't there no trouble t' 'ome?"

[001-006] And for the life of me I couldn't answer. After more than a year in

[001-007] the British service I could not, on the spur of the moment, say

[001-008] exactly why I was there.

[001-009] To be perfectly frank with myself and with the reader I had no very

[001-010] lofty motives when I took the King's shilling. When the great war

[001-011] broke out, I was mildly sympathetic with [[blighty|England]], and mighty sorry

[001-012] in an indefinite way for [[france|France]] and Belgium; but my sympathies were

[001-013] not strong enough in any direction to get me into uniform with a

[001-014] chance of being [[casualty|killed]]. Nor, at first, was I able to work up any

[001-015] compelling hate for Germany. The abstract idea of democracy did not

[001-016] figure in my calculations at all.

[001-017] However, as the war went on, it became apparent to me, as I suppose

[001-018] it must have to everybody, that the world was going through one of

[001-019] its epochal upheavals; and I figured that with so much history in

[001-020] the making, any unattached young man would be missing it if he did

[001-021] not take a part in the big game.

[001-022] I had the fondness for adventure usual in young men. I liked to see

[001-023] the wheels go round. And so it happened that, when the war was

[001-024] about a year and a half old, I decided to get in before it was too

[001-025] late.

[001-026] On second thought I won't say that it was purely love for adventure

[001-027] that took me across. There may have been in the back of my head a

[001-028] sneaking extra fondness for France, perhaps instinctive, for I was

[001-029] born in Paris, although my parents were American and I was brought

[001-030] to [[boston|Boston]] as a baby and have lived here since.

[001-031] Whatever my motives for joining the British army, they didn't have

[001-032] time to crystallize until I had been wounded and sent to [[england|Blighty]],

[001-033] which is trench slang for England. While recuperating in one of the

[001-034] pleasant places of the English country-side, I had time to acquire

[001-035] a perspective and to discover that I had been fighting for

[001-036] democracy and the future safety of the world. I think that my

[001-037] experience in this respect is like that of most of the young

[001-038] Americans who have volunteered for service under a foreign flag.

[001-039] I decided to get into the big war game early in 1916. My first

[001-040] thought was to go into the ambulance service, as I knew several men

[001-041] in that work. One of them described the driver's life about as

[001-042] follows. He said:

[001-043] "The _blessés_ curse you because you jolt them. The doctors curse

[001-044] you because you don't get the _blessés_ in fast enough. The

[001-045] Transport Service curse you because you get in the way. You eat

[001-046] standing up and don't sleep at all. You're as likely as anybody to

[001-047] get killed, and all the glory you get is the War Cross, if you're

[001-048] lucky, and you don't get a single chance to kill a [[boche|Hun]]."

[001-049] That settled the ambulance for me. I hadn't wanted particularly to

[001-050] kill a Hun until it was suggested that I mightn't. Then I wanted to

[001-051] slaughter a whole division.

[001-052] So I decided on something where there would be fighting. And having

[001-053] decided, I thought I would "go the whole hog" and work my way

[001-054] across to England on a horse transport.

[001-055] One day in the first part of February I went, at what seemed an

[001-056] early hour, to an office on Commercial Street, Boston, where they

[001-057] were advertising for horse tenders for England. [[About]] three hundred

[001-058] men were earlier than I. It seemed as though every beach-comber and

[001-059] patriot in New England was trying to get across. I didn't get the

[001-060] job, but filed my application and was lucky enough to be signed on

[001-061] for a sailing on February 22 on the steam-ship _Cambrian_, bound

[001-062] for [[london|London]].

[001-063] [Illustration: REDUCED FACSIMILE OF DISCHARGE CERTIFICATE OF

[001-064] CHARACTER.]

[001-065] We spent the morning of Washington's Birthday loading the horses.

[001-066] These government animals were selected stock and full of ginger.

[001-067] They seemed to know that they were going to France and resented it

[001-068] keenly. Those in my care seemed to regard my attentions as a

[001-069] personal affront.

[001-070] We had a strenuous forenoon getting the horses aboard, and sailed

[001-071] at noon. After we had herded in the livestock, some of the officers

[001-072] herded up the herders. I drew a pink slip with two numbers on it,

[001-073] one showing the compartment where I was supposed to sleep, the

[001-074] other indicating my bunk.

[001-075] That compartment certainly was a glory-hole. Most of the men had

[001-076] been drunk the night before, and the place had the rich, balmy

[001-077] fragrance of a water-front saloon. Incidentally there was a good

[001-078] deal of unauthorized and undomesticated livestock. I made a limited

[001-079] acquaintance with that pretty, playful little creature, the

[001-080] "cootie," who was to become so familiar in the [[trench|trenches]] later on.

[001-081] He wasn't called a cootie aboard ship, but he was the same bird.

[001-082] Perhaps the less said about that trip across the better. It lasted

[001-083] twenty-one days. We fed the animals three times a day and cleaned

[001-084] the stalls once on the trip. I got chewed up some and stepped on a

[001-085] few times. Altogether the experience was good intensive training

[001-086] for the trench life to come; especially the bunks. Those sleeping

[001-087] [[billets|quarters]] sure were close and crawly.

[001-088] We landed in London on Saturday night about nine-thirty. The

[001-089] immigration inspectors gave us a quick examination and we were

[001-090] turned back to the shipping people, who paid us off,--two pounds,

[001-091] equal to about ten dollars real change.

[001-092] After that we rode on the train half an hour and then marched

[001-093] through the streets, darkened to fool the Zeps. Around one o'clock

[001-094] we brought up at Thrawl Street, at the lodgings where we were

[001-095] supposed to stop until we were started for home.

[001-096] The place where we were quartered was a typical London doss house.

[001-097] There were forty beds in the room with mine, all of them occupied.

[001-098] All hands were snoring, and the fellow in the next cot was going

[001-099] it with the cut-out wide open, breaking all records. Most of the

[001-100] beds sagged like a hammock. Mine humped up in the middle like a

[001-101] pile of bricks.

[001-102] I was up early and was directed to the place across the way where

[001-103] we were to eat. It was labeled "Mother Wolf's. The Universal

[001-104] Provider." She provided just one meal of weak tea, moldy bread, and

[001-105] rancid bacon for me. After that I went to a hotel. I may remark in

[001-106] passing that horse tenders, going or coming or in between whiles,

[001-107] do not live on the fat of the land.

[001-108] I spent the day--it was Sunday--seeing the sights of Whitechapel,

[001-109] Middlesex Street or Petticoat Lane, and some of the slums. Next

[001-110] morning it was pretty clear to me that two pounds don't go far in

[001-111] the big town. I promptly boarded the first bus for Trafalgar

[001-112] Square. The recruiting office was just down the road in Whitehall

[001-113] at the old Scotland Yard office.

[001-114] I had an idea when I entered that recruiting office that the

[001-115] sergeant would receive me with open arms. He didn't. Instead he

[001-116] looked me over with unqualified scorn and spat out, "Yank, ayen't

[001-117] ye?"

[001-118] And I in my innocence briefly answered, "Yep."

[001-119] "We ayen't tykin' no nootrals," he said, with a sneer. And then:

[001-120] "Better go back to Hamerika and 'elp Wilson write 'is blinkin'

[001-121] notes."

[001-122] Well, I was mad enough to poke that sergeant in the eye. But I

[001-123] didn't. I retired gracefully and with dignity.

[001-124] At the door another sergeant hailed me, whispering behind his hand,

[001-125] "Hi sye, mytie. Come around in the mornin'. Hi'll get ye in." And

[001-126] so it happened.

[001-127] Next day my man was waiting and marched me boldly up to the same

[001-128] chap who had refused me the day before.

[001-129] "'Ere's a recroot for ye, Jim," says my friend.

[001-130] Jim never batted an eye. He began to "awsk" questions and to fill

[001-131] out a blank. When he got to the birthplace, my guide cut in and

[001-132] said, "Canada."

[001-133] The only place I knew in Canada was Campobello Island, a place

[001-134] where we camped one summer, and I gave that. I don't think that

[001-135] anything but rabbits was ever born on Campobello, but it went. For

[001-136] that matter anything went. I discovered afterward that the sergeant

[001-137] who had captured me on the street got five bob (shillings) for me.

[001-138] The physical examination upstairs was elaborate. They told me to

[001-139] strip, weighed me, and said I was fit. After that I was taken in to

[001-140] an officer--a real officer this time--who made me put my hand on a

[001-141] Bible and say yes to an oath he rattled off. Then he told me I was

[001-142] a member of the [[royal-fusiliers|Royal Fusiliers]], gave me two shillings, sixpence

[001-143] and ordered me to report at the Horse Guards Parade next day. I was

[001-144] in the British army,--just like that!

[001-145] I spent the balance of the day seeing the sights of London, and

[001-146] incidentally spending my coin. When I went around to the Horse

[001-147] Guards next morning, two hundred others, new rookies like myself,

[001-148] were waiting. An officer gave me another two shillings, sixpence. I

[001-149] began to think that if the money kept coming along at that rate the

[001-150] British army might turn out a good investment. It didn't.

[001-151] That morning I was sent out to Hounslow Barracks, and three days

[001-152] later was transferred to [[dover|Dover]] with twenty others. I was at Dover a

[001-153] little more than two months and completed my training there.

[001-154] Our barracks at Dover was on the heights of the cliffs, and on

[001-155] clear days we could look across the Channel and see the dim

[001-156] outlines of France. It was a fascination for all of us to look away

[001-157] over there and to wonder what fortunes were to come to us on the

[001-158] battle fields of Europe. It was perhaps as well that none of us had

[001-159] imagination enough to visualize the things that were ahead.

[001-160] I found the rookies at Dover a jolly, companionable lot, and I

[001-161] never found the routine irksome. We were up at five-thirty, had

[001-162] cocoa and biscuits, and then an hour of physical drill or bayonet

[001-163] practice. At eight came breakfast of tea, bacon, and bread, and

[001-164] then we drilled until twelve. Dinner. Out again on the parade

[001-165] ground until three thirty. After that we were free.

[001-166] Nights we would go into Dover and sit around the "pubs" drinking

[001-167] ale, or "ayle" as the cockney says it.

[001-168] After a few weeks, when we were hardened somewhat, they began to

[001-169] inflict us with the torture known as "night ops." That means going

[001-170] out at ten o'clock under full pack, hiking several miles, and then

[001-171] "manning" the trenches around the town and returning to barracks at

[001-172] three A.M.

[001-173] This wouldn't have been so bad if we had been excused parades the

[001-174] following day. But no. We had the same old drills except the early

[001-175] one, but were allowed to "kip" until seven.

[001-176] In the two months I completed the musketry course, was a good

[001-177] [[bayonet|bayonet man]], and was well grounded in bombing practice. Besides

[001-178] that I was as hard as nails and had learned thoroughly the system

[001-179] of British [[trench-discipline|discipline]].

[001-180] I had supposed that it took at least six months to make a

[001-181] soldier,--in fact had been told that one could not be turned out

[001-182] who would be ten per cent efficient in less than that time. That

[001-183] old theory is all wrong. Modern warfare changes so fast that the

[001-184] only thing that can be taught a man is the basic principles of

[001-185] discipline, bombing, trench warfare, and musketry. Give him those

[001-186] things, a well-conditioned body, and a baptism of fire, and he will

[001-187] be right there with the veterans, doing his bit.

[001-188] Two months was all our crowd got at any rate, and they were as good

[001-189] as the best, if I do say it.

[001-190] My training ended abruptly with a [[leave|furlough]] of five days for

[001-191] Embarkation Leave, that is, leave before going to France. This is a

[001-192] sort of good-by vacation. Most fellows realize fully that it may be

[001-193] their last look at Blighty, and they take it rather solemnly. To a

[001-194] stranger without friends in England I can imagine that this

[001-195] Embarkation Leave would be either a mighty lonesome, dismal affair,

[001-196] or a stretch of desperate, homesick dissipation. A chap does want

[001-197] to say good-by to some one before he goes away, perhaps to die. He

[001-198] wants to be loved and to have some one sorry that he is going.

[001-199] I was invited by one of my chums to spend the leave with him at his

[001-200] home in Southall, Middlesex. His father, mother and sister welcomed

[001-201] me in a way that made me know it was my home from the minute I

[001-202] entered the door. They took me into their hearts with a simple

[001-203] hospitality and whole-souled kindness that I can never forget. I

[001-204] was a stranger in a strange land and they made me one of their own.

[001-205] I shall never be able to repay all the loving thoughts and deeds of

[001-206] that family and shall remember them while I live. My chum's mother

[001-207] I call Mother too. It is to her that I have dedicated this book.

[001-208] After my delightful few days of leave, things moved fast. I was

[001-209] back in Dover just two days when I, with two hundred other men, was

[001-210] sent to Winchester. Here we were notified that we were transferred

[001-211] to the [[queens-royal-west-surrey|Queen's Royal West Surrey Regiment]].

[001-212] This news brought a wild howl from the men. They wanted to stop

[001-213] with the Fusiliers. It is part of the British system that every man

[001-214] is taught the traditions and history of his regiment and to _know_

[001-215] that his is absolutely the best in the whole army. In a

[001-216] surprisingly short time they get so they swear by their own

[001-217] regiment and by their officers, and they protest bitterly at a

[001-218] transfer.

[001-219] Personally I didn't care a rap. I had early made up my mind that I

[001-220] was a very small pebble on the beach and that it was up to me to

[001-221] obey orders and keep my mouth shut.

[001-222] On June 17, some eighteen hundred of us were moved down to

[001-223] Southampton and put aboard the transport for [[havre|Havre]]. The next day we

[001-224] were in France, at Harfleur, the central training camp outside

[001-225] Havre.

[001-226] We were supposed to undergo an intensive training at Harfleur in

[001-227] the various forms of [[gas-warfare|gas]] and protection from it, barbed [[barbed-wire|wire]] and

[001-228] methods of construction of entanglements, musketry, bombing, and

[001-229] bayonet fighting.

[001-230] Harfleur was a miserable place. They refused to let us go in town

[001-231] after drill. Also I managed to let myself in for something that

[001-232] would have kept me in camp if town leave had been allowed.

[001-233] The first day there was a call for a volunteer for musketry

[001-234] instructor. I had qualified and jumped at it. When I reported, an

[001-235] old Scotch sergeant told me to go to the quartermaster for

[001-236] equipment. I said I already had full equipment. Whereupon the

[001-237] sergeant laughed a rumbling Scotch laugh and told me I had to go

[001-238] into kilts, as I was assigned to a Highland contingent.

[001-239] I protested with violence and enthusiasm, but it didn't do any

[001-240] good. They gave me a dinky little pleated petticoat, and when I

[001-241] demanded breeks to wear underneath, I got the merry ha ha. Breeks

[001-242] on a Scotchman? Never!

[001-243] Well, I got into the fool things, and I felt as though I was naked

[001-244] from ankle to wishbone. I couldn't get used to the outfit. I am

[001-245] naturally a modest man. Besides, my architecture was never intended

[001-246] for bare-leg effects. I have no dimples in my knees.

[001-247] So I began an immediate campaign for transfer back to the Surreys.

[001-248] I got it at the end of ten days, and with it came a hurry call from

[001-249] somewhere at the front for more troops.
