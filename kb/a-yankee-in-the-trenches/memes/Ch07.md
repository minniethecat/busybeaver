[007-001] I want to say a word right here about [[patrol|patrol work]] in general,

[007-002] because for some reason it fascinated me and was my favorite game.

[007-003] If you should be fortunate--or unfortunate enough, as the case

[007-004] might be--to be squatting in a front-line trench this fine morning

[007-005] and looking through a periscope, you wouldn't see much. Just over

[007-006] the top, not more than twenty feet away, would be your barbed-[[barbed-wire|wire]]

[007-007] entanglements, a thick network of wire stretched on iron posts

[007-008] nearly waist high, and perhaps twelve or fifteen feet across. Then

[007-009] there would be an intervening stretch of from fifty to one hundred

[007-010] fifty yards of [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]], a tortured, torn expanse of muddy

[007-011] soil, pitted with [[crater|shell craters]], and, over beyond, the German wire

[007-012] and his parapet.

[007-013] There would be nothing alive visible. There would probably be a

[007-014] few corpses lying about or hanging in the wire. Everything would be

[007-015] still except for the flutter of some rag of a dead man's uniform.

[007-016] Perhaps not that. Daylight movements in No Man's Land are somehow

[007-017] disconcerting. Once I was in a trench where a leg--a booted German

[007-018] leg, stuck up stark and stiff out of the mud not twenty yards in

[007-019] front. Some idiotic joker on patrol hung a [[tin-hat|helmet]] on the foot, and

[007-020] all the next day that helmet dangled and swung in the breeze. It

[007-021] irritated the periscope watchers, and the next night it was taken

[007-022] down.

[007-023] Ordinarily, however, there is little movement between the wires,

[007-024] nor behind them. And yet you know that over yonder there are

[007-025] thousands of men lurking in the [[trench|trenches]] and shelters.

[007-026] After dark these men, or some of them, crawl out like hunted

[007-027] animals and prowl in the black mystery of No Man's Land. They are

[007-028] the patrol.

[007-029] The patrol goes out armed and equipped lightly. He has to move

[007-030] softly and at times very quickly. It is his duty to get as close

[007-031] to the enemy lines as possible and find out if they are repairing

[007-032] their wire or if any of their parties are out, and to get back word

[007-033] to the machine gunners, who immediately cut loose on the indicated

[007-034] spot.

[007-035] Sometimes he lies with his head to the ground over some suspected

[007-036] area, straining his ears for the faint "scrape, scrape" that means

[007-037] a German [[mine-explosion|mining]] party is down there, getting ready to plant a ton

[007-038] or so of high explosive, or, it may be, is preparing to touch it

[007-039] off at that very moment.

[007-040] Always the patrol is supposed to avoid encounter with enemy

[007-041] patrols. He carries two or three [[grenade|Mills]] bombs and a [[revolver|pistol]], but not

[007-042] for use except in extreme emergency. Also a [[persuader|persuader stick]] or a

[007-043] [[knuckle-knife|trench knife]], which he may use if he is near enough to do it

[007-044] silently.

[007-045] The patrol stares constantly through the dark and gets so he can

[007-046] see almost as well as a cat. He must avoid being seen. When a Very

[007-047] light goes up, he lies still. If he happens to be standing, he

[007-048] stands still. Unless the light is behind him so that he is

[007-049] silhouetted, he is invisible to the enemy.

[007-050] Approaching a corpse, the patrol lies quiet and watches it for

[007-051] several minutes, unless it is one he has seen before and is

[007-052] acquainted with. Because sometimes the man isn't dead, but a

[007-053] perfectly live [[boche|Boche]] patrol lying "doggo." You can't be too

[007-054] careful.

[007-055] If you happen to be pussyfooting forward erect and encounter a

[007-056] German patrol, it is policy to scuttle back unless you are near

[007-057] enough to get in one good lick with the persuader. He will retreat

[007-058] slowly himself, and you mustn't follow him. Because: The British

[007-059] patrol usually goes out singly or at the most in pairs or threes.

[007-060] The [[germans|Germans]], on the other hand, hunt in parties. One man leads. Two

[007-061] others follow to the rear, one to each side. And then two more, and

[007-062] two more, so that they form a V, like a flock of geese. Now if you

[007-063] follow up the lead man when he retreats, you are baited into a trap

[007-064] and find yourself surrounded, smothered by superior numbers, and

[007-065] taken prisoner. Then back to the Boche trench, where exceedingly

[007-066] unpleasant things are apt to happen.

[007-067] It is, in fact, most unwholesome for a British patrol to be

[007-068] captured. I recall a case in point which I witnessed and which is

[007-069] far enough in the past so that it can be told. It occurred, not at

[007-070] [[vimy-ridge|Vimy Ridge]], but further down the line, nearer [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]].

[007-071] I was out one night with another man, prowling in the dark, when I

[007-072] encountered a Canadian sergeant who was alone. There was a Canadian

[007-073] battalion holding the next trench to us, and another farther down.

[007-074] He was from the farther one. We lay in the mud and compared notes.

[007-075] Once, when a light floated down near us, I saw his face, and he was

[007-076] a man I knew, though not by name.

[007-077] After a while we separated, and he went back, as he was

[007-078] considerably off his patrol. An hour or so later the mist began to

[007-079] get gray, and it was evident that dawn was near. I was a couple of

[007-080] hundred yards down from our battalion, and my man and I made for

[007-081] the trenches opposite where we were. As we climbed into a [[sap|sap head]],

[007-082] I was greeted by a Canadian corporal. He invited me to a tin of

[007-083] "char", and I sent my man up the line to our own position.

[007-084] We sat on the fire step drinking, and I told the corporal about

[007-085] meeting the sergeant out in front. While we were at the "char" it

[007-086] kept getting lighter, and presently a pair of Lewises started to

[007-087] rattle a hundred yards or so away down the line. Then came a sudden

[007-088] commotion and a kind of low, growling shout. That is the best way I

[007-089] can describe it. We stood up, and below we saw men going over the

[007-090] top.

[007-091] "What the dickens can this be?" stuttered the corporal. "There's

[007-092] been no barrage. There's no orders for a charge. What is it? What

[007-093] is it?"

[007-094] Well, there they were, going over, as many as two hundred of

[007-095] them--growling. The corporal and I climbed out of the trench at the

[007-096] rear, over the parados, and ran across lots down to a point

[007-097] opposite where the Canadians had gone over, and watched.

[007-098] They swept across No Man's Land and into the Boche trench. There

[007-099] was the deuce of a ruckus over there for maybe two minutes, and

[007-100] then back they came--carrying something. Strangely enough there had

[007-101] been no [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]] turned on them as they crossed, nor was

[007-102] there as they returned. They had cleaned that German trench! And

[007-103] they brought back the body of a man--nailed to a rude crucifix. The

[007-104] thing was more like a T than a cross. It was made of planks,

[007-105] perhaps two by five, and the man was spiked on by his hands and

[007-106] feet. Across the abdomen he was riddled with bullets and again with

[007-107] another row a little higher up near his chest. The man was the

[007-108] sergeant I had talked to earlier in the night. What had happened

[007-109] was this. He had, no doubt, been taken by a German patrol. Probably

[007-110] he had refused to answer questions. Perhaps he had insulted an

[007-111] officer. They had crucified him and held him up above the parapet.

[007-112] With the first light his own comrades had naturally opened on the

[007-113] thing with the Lewises, not knowing what it was. When it got

[007-114] lighter, and they recognized the hellish thing that had been done

[007-115] to one of their men, they went over. Nothing in this world could

[007-116] have stopped them.

[007-117] The M.O. who viewed the body said that without question the man had

[007-118] been crucified alive. Also it was said that the same thing had

[007-119] happened before.

[007-120] I told [[captain-green|Captain Green]] of the occurrence when I got back to our own

[007-121] trenches, and he ordered me to keep silent, which I did. It was

[007-122] feared that if the affair got about the men would be "[[windy]]" on

[007-123] patrol. However, the thing did get about and was pretty well talked

[007-124] over. Too many saw it.

[007-125] The Canadians were reprimanded for going over without orders. But

[007-126] they were not punished. For their officers went with them--led

[007-127] them.

[007-128] Occasionally the temptation is too great. Once I was out on patrol

[007-129] alone, having sent my man back with a message, when I encountered a

[007-130] Heinie. I was lying down at the time. A flock of lights went up and

[007-131] showed this fellow standing about ten feet from me. He had frozen

[007-132] and stayed that way till the flares died, but I was close enough to

[007-133] see that he was a German. Also--marvel of marvels--he was alone.

[007-134] When the darkness settled again, I got to my feet and jumped at

[007-135] him. He jumped at me--another marvel. Going into the clinch I

[007-136] missed him with the persuader and lost my grip on it, leaving the

[007-137] weapon dangling by the leather loop on my wrist. He had struck at

[007-138] me with his automatic, which I think he must have dropped, though

[007-139] I'm not sure of that. Anyway we fell into each other's arms and

[007-140] went at it barehanded. He was bigger than I. I got under the ribs

[007-141] and tried to squeeze the breath out of him, but he was too rugged.

[007-142] At the same time I felt that he didn't relish the clinch. I slipped

[007-143] my elbow up and got under his chin, forcing his head back. His

[007-144] breath smelled of beer and onions. I was choking him when he

[007-145] brought his knee up and got me in the stomach and again on the

[007-146] instep when he brought his heel down.

[007-147] It broke my hold, and I staggered back groping for the persuader.

[007-148] He jumped back as far as I did. I felt somehow that he was glad. So

[007-149] was I. We stood for a minute, and I heard him gutter out something

[007-150] that sounded like "Verdamder swinehunt." Then we both backed away.

[007-151] It seemed to me to be the nicest way out of the situation. No doubt

[007-152] he felt the same.

[007-153] I seem to have wandered far from the Quarries and the [[grouse-spots|Grouse Spots]].

[007-154] Let's go back.

[007-155] We were two days in the Grouse Spots and were then relieved, going

[007-156] back to the Quarries and taking the place of Number 9 in support.

[007-157] While lying there, I drew a patrol that was interesting because it

[007-158] was different.

[007-159] The [[souchez|Souchez River]] flowed down from Abalaine and Souchez villages

[007-160] and through our lines to those of the Germans, and on to [[lens|Lens]].

[007-161] Spies, either in the army itself or in the villages, had been

[007-162] placing messages in bottles and floating them down the river to the

[007-163] Germans.

[007-164] Somebody found this out, and a net of chicken wire had been placed

[007-165] across the river in No Man's Land. Some one had to go down there

[007-166] and fish for bottles twice nightly. I took this patrol alone. The

[007-167] lines were rather far apart along the river, owing to the swampy

[007-168] nature of the ground, which made livable trenches impossible.

[007-169] I slipped out and down the slight incline, and presently found

[007-170] myself in a little valley. The grass was rank and high, sometimes

[007-171] nearly up to my chin, and the ground was slimy and treacherous. I

[007-172] slipped into several shell holes and was almost over my head in the

[007-173] stagnant, smelly water.

[007-174] I made the river all right, but there was no bridge or net in

[007-175] sight. The river was not over ten feet wide and there was supposed

[007-176] to be a footbridge of two planks where the net was.

[007-177] I got back into the grass and made my way downstream. Sliding

[007-178] gently through the grass, I kept catching my feet in something hard

[007-179] that felt like roots; but there were no trees in the neighborhood.

[007-180] I reached down and groped in the grass and brought up a human rib.

[007-181] The place was full of them, and skulls. Stooping, I could see them,

[007-182] grinning up out of the dusk, hundreds of them. I learned afterwards

[007-183] that this was called the Valley of Death. Early in the war several

[007-184] thousand Zouaves had perished there, and no attempt had been made

[007-185] to bury them.

[007-186] After getting out of the skeletons, I scouted along downstream and

[007-187] presently heard the low voices of Germans. Evidently they had found

[007-188] the net and planned to get the messages first. Creeping to the edge

[007-189] of the grass, I peeped out. I was opposite the bottle trap. I could

[007-190] dimly make out the forms of two men standing on the nearer end of

[007-191] the plank bridge. They were, I should judge, about ten yards away,

[007-192] and they hadn't heard me. I got out a Mills, pulled the pin, and

[007-193] pitched it. The bomb exploded, perhaps five feet this side of the

[007-194] men. One dropped, and the other ran.

[007-195] After a short wait I ran over to the German. I searched him for

[007-196] papers, found none, and rolled him into the river.

[007-197] After a few days in the Quarries we were moved to what was known as

[007-198] the Warren, so called because the works resembled a rabbit warren.

[007-199] This was on the lower side and to the left end of [[vimy|Vimy Ridge]], and

[007-200] was extra dangerous. It did seem as though each place was worse

[007-201] than the last. The Warren was a regular network of trenches,

[007-202] burrows, and [[funk-hole|funk holes]], and we needed them all.

[007-203] The position was downhill from the Huns, and they kept sending over

[007-204] and down a continuous stream of "pip-squeaks", "whiz-bangs", and

[007-205] "minnies." The "[[pip-squeak]]" is a shell that starts with a silly

[007-206] "pip", goes on with a sillier "squeeeeee", and goes off with a

[007-207] man's-size bang.

[007-208] The "[[whiz-bang]]" starts with a rough whirr like a flushing cock

[007-209] partridge, and goes off on contact with a tremendous bang. It is

[007-210] not as dangerous as it sounds, but bad enough.

[007-211] The "[[minnies|minnie]]" is about the size of a two-gallon kerosene can, and

[007-212] comes somersaulting over in a high arc and is concentrated death

[007-213] and destruction when it lands. It has one virtue--you can see it

[007-214] coming and dodge, and at night it most considerately leaves a trail

[007-215] of sparks.

[007-216] The Boche served us full portions of all three of these man-killers

[007-217] in the Warren and kept us ducking in and out pretty much all the

[007-218] time, night and day.

[007-219] I was lucky enough after the first day to be put on sappers' duty.

[007-220] The Sappers, or Engineers, are the men whose duty it is to run

[007-221] mines under No Man's Land and plant huge quantities of explosives.

[007-222] There was a great amount of mining going on all the time at Vimy

[007-223] Ridge from both sides.

[007-224] Sometimes Fritz would run a [[sap-head|sap]] out reasonably near the surface,

[007-225] and we would counter with one lower down. Then he'd go us one

[007-226] better and go still deeper. Some of the mines went down and under

[007-227] hundreds of feet. The result of all this was that on our side at

[007-228] least, the Sappers were under-manned and a good many infantry were

[007-229] drafted into that service.

[007-230] I had charge of a gang and had to fill [[sandbag|sandbags]] with the earth

[007-231] removed from the end of the sap and get it out and pile the bags on

[007-232] the parapets. We were well out toward the German lines and deep

[007-233] under the hill when we heard them digging below us. An engineer

[007-234] officer came in and listened for an hour and decided that they were

[007-235] getting in explosives and that it was up to us to beat them to it.

[007-236] Digging stopped at once and we began rushing in H.E. in fifty-pound

[007-237] boxes. I was ordered back into supports with my section.

[007-238] Right here I began to have luck. Just see how this worked out.

[007-239] First a rushing party was organized whose duty it was to rush the

[007-240] crater made by the mine explosion and occupy it before the Germans

[007-241] got there. Sixty men were selected, a few from each company, and

[007-242] placed where they were supposedly safe, but where they could get up

[007-243] fast. This is the most dangerous duty an infantryman has to do,

[007-244] because both sides after a mine explosion shower in fifty-seven

[007-245] varieties of sudden death, including a perfect rain of machine-gun

[007-246] bullets. The chances of coming out of a rushing party with a whole

[007-247] hide are about one in five.

[007-248] Well, for a wonder, I didn't get drawn for this one, and I breathed

[007-249] one long, deep sigh of relief, put my hand inside my tunic and

[007-250] patted Dinky on the back. Dinky is my mascot. I'll tell you about

[007-251] him later.

[007-252] On top of that another bit of luck came along, though it didn't

[007-253] seem like it at the moment. It was the custom for a ration party to

[007-254] go out each night and get up the grub. This party had to go over

[007-255] the duck walk and was under fire both going and coming. One of the

[007-256] corporals who had been out on [[trench-rations|rations]] two nights in succession

[007-257] began to "grouse."

[007-258] Of course [[sergeant-page|Sergeant Page]] spotted me and detailed me to the

[007-259] "wangler's" duty. I "groused" too, like a good fellow, but had to

[007-260] go.

[007-261] "Garn," says [[wellsie|Wellsie]]. "Wot's the diff if yer gets it 'ere or there.

[007-262] If ye clicks, I'll draw yer [[fag|fags]] from [[blighty|Blighty]] and say a prayer for

[007-263] yer soul. On yer way."

[007-264] Cheerful beggar, Wellsie. He was doing me a favor and didn't know

[007-265] it.

[007-266] I did the three miles along the duck walk with the ration party,

[007-267] and there wasn't a shell came our way. Queer! Nor on the way back.

[007-268] Queerer! When we were nearly back and were about five hundred yards

[007-269] from the base of the Pimple, a dead silence fell on the German side

[007-270] of the line. There wasn't a gun nor a mortar nor even a rifle in

[007-271] [[battle|action]] for a mile in either direction. There was, too, a kind of

[007-272] sympathetic let-up on our side. There weren't any lights going up.

[007-273] There was an electric tension in the very air. You could tell by

[007-274] the feel that something big was going to happen.

[007-275] I halted the ration party at the end of the duck walk and waited.

[007-276] But not for long. Suddenly the "Very" lights went up from the

[007-277] German side, literally in hundreds, illuminating the top of the

[007-278] ridge and the sky behind with a thin greenish white [[very-light|flare]]. Then

[007-279] came a deep rumble that shook the ground, and a dull boom. A spurt

[007-280] of blood-red flame squirted up from the near side of the hill, and

[007-281] a rolling column of gray smoke.

[007-282] Then another rumble, and another, and then the whole side of

[007-283] the ridge seemed to open up and move slowly skyward with a

[007-284] world-wrecking, soul-paralyzing crash. A murky red glare lit up the

[007-285] smoke screen, and against it a mass of tossed-up debris, and for an

[007-286] instant I caught the black silhouette of a whole human body

[007-287] spread-eagled and spinning like a pin-wheel.

[007-288] Most of our party, even at the distance, were knocked down by the

[007-289] gigantic impact of the explosion. A shower of earth and rock

[007-290] chunks, some as big as a barrel, fell around us.

[007-291] Then we heard a far-away cheering, and in the light of the flares

[007-292] we saw a newly made hill and our men swarming up it to the crater.

[007-293] Two mines had exploded, and the whole side of the Pimple had been

[007-294] torn away. Half of our rushing party were [[casualty|killed]] and we had sixty

[007-295] casualties from shock and wounds among men who were supposed to be

[007-296] at a safe distance from the mining operation. But we took and held

[007-297] the new crater positions.

[007-298] The corporal whose place I had taken on the ration party was killed

[007-299] by falling stones. Inasmuch as he was where I would have been, I

[007-300] considered that I had had a narrow escape from "going west!" More

[007-301] luck!
