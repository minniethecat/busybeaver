## Overview

Bones (also called "Bonesie") was a [[tommy|British soldier]] in [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s platoon, distinguished by his education and thoughtful nature. Unlike the more colorful characters in the memoir, Bones represents the introspective soldier — a reader and philosopher who tried to make sense of the war through intellect rather than bravado.

## Shared Ordeal in the Shell Hole

Bones appears most prominently in a harrowing episode where he, Holmes, and other men were trapped together in a shell hole under [[artillery|bombardment]]. The cramped, desperate conditions brought out Bones' character:

> Around nine we all waked up, and Bones--he was the fellow in the middle--began to complain of thirst. Then we all took another nip and wished it was water.
> (012-075)

When Holmes turned to him for reassurance as the [[shell|shelling]] intensified, Bones' response captured the dark humor of the [[trench|trenches]]:

> I turned to Bonesie and said, "What about that safety stuff, old top?"
> "Cheer, cheer, Darby," said he. "We may pull through yet."
> (013-082)

## A Philosopher in Khaki

Bones was notably well-educated — a reader who had asked Holmes whether he had visited Emerson's [[blighty|home]] and was "astounded that I hadn't" (012-076). In the shell hole, he recited [[kip|Kip]]ling to keep spirits up:

> Bones, who was pretty well educated--he once asked me if I had visited Emerson's home and was astounded that I hadn't--quoted Kipling... Then Bones cursed the rum and took another nip. So did the rest of us.
> (012-076)

Bones had developed his own analysis of the war's causes, which Holmes records:

> Bones, who was educated and a thoughtful reader, had it figured out that the war was all due to the tyranny of the ruling class.
> (004-029)

## Air War Observer

During a lull, Bones watched an aerial [[battle|engagement]] and "began to philosophize on the cold-bloodedness of air [[battle|fight]]ing and really worked himself up into an almost optimistic frame of mind" (013-081). His ability to intellectualize even the most brutal aspects of war marked him as unusual among the Tommies.
