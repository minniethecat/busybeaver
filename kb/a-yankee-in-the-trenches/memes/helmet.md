# Steel Helmet

The **steel helmet** — known to [[tommy|British soldier]]s as the "**tin hat**" or "battle bowler" — was the single most important piece of protective equipment issued during the First World War. Introduced in 1916 in response to the appalling rate of head wounds from artillery shrapnel, it saved countless lives and became the iconic silhouette of the modern soldier.

## In the Narrative

Holmes refers to the [[tin-hat|helmet]] multiple times, often pairing it with the [[gas-helmet|gas mask]] as the two items a soldier could not do without:

> "…rounds of ammo, a tin hat, two gas helmets, and a lot of…" [008-072]

Even in the chaos of battle, the [[tin-hat|helmet]] was non-negotiable:

> "…matter of the steel helmet and the rifle, which, with the…" [008-076]

The enemy also wore steel helmets, as Holmes observed:

> "steel helmets. They were British. We had seen…" [006-186]

A macabre detail — a [[tin-hat|helmet]] [[hun]]g as a decoy on a [[patrol]] footbridge:

> "…idiotic joker on patrol hung a helmet on the foot, and…" [007-019]

The dangling [[tin-hat|helmet]] became an odd landmark:

> "all the next day that helmet dangled and swung in the breeze…" [007-020]

Helmet inspection was part of the daily routine:

> "…whole bunch out for parade and helmet inspection." [008-151]

Holmes even saved a [[souvenir]] [[tin-hat|helmet]]:

> "helmet, a mess of shell noses, and a…" [006-013]

## The Brodie Helmet

The British Mark I [[tin-hat|helmet]] — commonly called the [[tin-hat|Brodie helmet]] after its designer, John Brodie — was introduced in early 1916. Made of manganese steel, it featured:

- **A shallow bowl shape** designed to deflect shrapnel from above (the primary source of head wounds in [[trench|trench warfare]])
- **A wide brim** that provided some protection for the shoulders and neck
- **A leather chinstrap and liner** for wearing comfort
- **Weight**: approximately 1.3 pounds (0.6 kg)

Before the steel helmet, [[tommy|British soldier]]s went into battle wearing only soft cloth caps. The introduction of the [[tin-hat|Brodie helmet]] reduced head wound fatalities by an estimated 75%. It was so effective that it remained in service through the Second World War with only minor modifications.
