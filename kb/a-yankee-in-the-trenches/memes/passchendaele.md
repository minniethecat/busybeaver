# Passchendaele

## Overview

The Third Battle of Ypres, better known as Passchendaele, was fought from July to November 1917 in Belgium. It became synonymous with mud — the heaviest rains in decades turned the battlefield into a quagmire where men and horses drowned in [[crater|shell craters]]. The offensive achieved minimal gains at enormous cost. [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] was there and records the experience in one of his memoir's grimmest chapters.

## In the Memoir

Holmes's account of Passchendaele is dominated by the mud. It was everywhere — knee-deep, waist-deep in places — and it shaped every aspect of the battle. Soldiers struggled to move; [[casualty|wounded]] men sank and di[[mine-explosion|sap]]peared; [[artillery|guns]] and equipment were swallowed whole. The mud, Holmes suggests, was as much an enemy as the [[germans|Germans]].

The chapter captures the grinding exhaustion of a battle that seemed to have no end and no purpose. Holmes's prose becomes terse and functional, mirroring the soldiers' own psychological withdrawal from the horror around them.
