# Rifle

## Overview

The rifle was the primary individual weapon of the World War I infantryman and, in the [[trench|trenches]], much more than a firearm — it was a constant physical burden, a symbol of military identity, and an object of daily ritual. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, the rifle appears not only as a weapon of [[battle|combat]] but as an ever-present article of the soldier's life, carried, cleaned, and cursed in equal measure. The [[british-army|British Army]] issued the Short Magazine Lee-Enfield (SMLE) .303, a bolt-[[battle|action]] rifle renowned for its smooth [[battle|action]] and ten-round magazine; [[germans|German soldier]]s carried the Mauser Gewehr 98. In the close [[billets|quarters]] and mud of [[trench|trench warfare]], the rifle's long [[estaminet|bar]]rel and wood stock made it as much a liability as an asset.

## In the Trenches

Holmes's early impressions of trench life include the rifle as a central, if mundane, feature of the sentry's existence:

> I settled down in the mud of the fire step, nursing a dirty and muddy rifle on "sentry go."
> — (002-062)

The rifle was rarely far from the soldier's hands. On the [[marching|march]], it was slung over the shoulder along with a full pack. On the fire step, it was held ready for the enemy's head to appear over the parapet. In the [[dug-out]], it was propped within reach, cleaned and inspected obsessively — for a jammed rifle in a [[raid|trench raid]] meant almost certain death.

The standard infantryman's load included not only the rifle but a substantial weight of ammunition and equipment:

> a water bottle, a mess kit, a rifle, two hundred fifty rounds of ammunition, a shovel or pick, a steel helmet, two gas helmets, a pair of extra boots, a pair of puttees, a housewife [sewing kit], a pack, a greatcoat, and a blanket
> — (008-071)–(008-076)

Holmes notes the particular burden of the rifle with its fixed bayonet:

> the weight of the steel helmet and the rifle, which, with the bayonet, is no mean burden
> — (008-076)

## The Sniper's Rifle

The rifle took on a specialized and deadly form in the hands of the [[sniper]]. Holmes contrasts the British [[sniper]], who used a plain telescopic sight and relied on field craft and concealment, with the German [[sniper]]'s more elaborate equipment:

> The German sniper is usually armed with a periscope-telescope rifle. This is a specially built super-accurate rifle mounted on a periscope frame.
> — (002-084)–(002-085)

The British [[sniper]], by comparison, used a simpler approach:

> a plain telescope rifle and had to hide himself behind an armored plate
> — (002-098)

Holmes notes the asymmetry with grim respect: "it's about ten to one he gets his man" — reflecting the [[sniper]]'s lethal efficiency on both sides of [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] (002-079).

## In Combat

The rifle's role in the defining ope[[trench-rations|rations]] of [[trench|trench warfare]] — [[trench-raid|raid]]s, [[patrol]]s, going "[[over-the-top|over the top]]" — was central but complicated. In the chaos of a [[raid|trench raid]], the rifle could be as much hindrance as help. During one [[trench-raid|raid]], Holmes describes how:

> Jerry had lost his rifle, and mine had no
> — (003-140)

The sentence breaks off in the narrative, leaving the reader to imagine the desperate hand-to-hand [[battle|combat]] that followed, fought with [[grenade|bomb]]s (grenades) and [[bayonet|bayonets]].

In an attack across open ground, the rifleman was terrifyingly exposed:

> out in the open, exposed to rifle and machine-gun fire
> — (009-135)

Holmes witnesses a comrade struck while advancing:

> double up, drop his rifle, and somersault, hanging on to what had been his stomach
> — (010-105)

The image of the dropped rifle — the soldier's primary tool, abandoned in the instant of mortal injury — is one of the memoir's most haunting details.

## Maintenance and Ritual

The rifle was the object of constant, often seemingly futile, cleaning rituals. Holmes describes one such session amidst prepa[[trench-rations|rations]]:

> A general cleaning of rifles started, although it was dark
> — (014-001)

In the mud of the [[trench|trenches]], where every surface was fouled, keeping the rifle's [[battle|action]] clean and operational was a matter of life and death. Soldiers developed improvised methods — Holmes mentions one man who:

> would stick a rifle up over him and tuck the recoil pad of the butt under his shoulder
> — (013-044)

firing blindly at the enemy lines in a macabre routine. Officers would later:

> remove the rifle and drag the body down
> — (013-046)

The rifle outlasts the rifleman; it is recovered, passed to another soldier, and the cycle continues.
