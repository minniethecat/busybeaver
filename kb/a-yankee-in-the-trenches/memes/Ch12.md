[012-001] When I found my battalion, the battle of [[high-wood|High Wood]] had pretty well

[012-002] quieted down. We had taken the position we went after, and the

[012-003] fighting was going on to the north and beyond the Wood. The Big

[012-004] Push progressed very rapidly as the summer drew to a close. Our men

[012-005] were holding one of the captured positions in the neighborhood of

[012-006] the Wood.

[012-007] It must have been two days after we went [[over-the-top|over the top]] with the

[012-008] [[tank|tanks]] that [[captain-green|Captain Green]] had me up and told me that I was promoted.

[012-009] At least that was what he called it. I differed with him, but

[012-010] didn't say so.

[012-011] The Captain said that as I had had a course in bombing, he thought

[012-012] he would put me in the [[battalion|Battalion]] Bombers.

[012-013] I protested that the honor was too great and that I really didn't

[012-014] think I was good enough.

[012-015] After that the Captain said that he didn't _think_ I was going in

[012-016] the bombers. He _knew_ it. I was elected!

[012-017] I didn't take any joy whatever in the appointment, but orders are

[012-018] orders and they have to be obeyed. The bombers are called the

[012-019] "Suicide Club" and are well named. The mortality in this branch of

[012-020] the service is as great if not greater than in any other.

[012-021] In spite of my feelings in the matter, I accepted the decision

[012-022] cheerfully--like a man being sentenced to be electrocuted--and

[012-023] managed to convey the impression to Captain Green that I was

[012-024] greatly elated and that I looked forward to future performances

[012-025] with large relish. After that I went back to my shelter and made a

[012-026] new will.

[012-027] That very night I was called upon to take charge of a bombing party

[012-028] of twelve men. A lieutenant, [[lieutenant-may|Mr. May]], one of the bravest men I ever

[012-029] knew, was to be of the party and in direct command. I was to have

[012-030] the selection of the men.

[012-031] Captain Green had me up along with [[mr-may|Lieutenant May]] early in the

[012-032] evening, and as nearly as I can remember these were his

[012-033] instructions:

[012-034] "Just beyond High Wood and to the left there is a [[mine-explosion|sap]] or small

[012-035] trench leading to the sunken road that lies between the towns of

[012-036] Albert and Bapaume. That position commands a military point that we

[012-037] find necessary to hold before we can make another attack. The

[012-038] [[germans|Germans]] are in the trench. They have two [[machine-gun|machine guns]] and will

[012-039] raise the devil with us unless we get them out. It will cost a good

[012-040] many lives if we attempt to take the position by attack, but we are

[012-041] under the impression that a bombing party in the night on a

[012-042] surprise attack will be able to take it with little loss of life.

[012-043] Take your twelve men out there at ten o'clock and _take that

[012-044] trench_! You will take only bombs with you. You and Mr. May will

[012-045] have revolvers. After taking the trench, consolidate it, and before

[012-046] morning there will be relief sent out to you. The best of luck!'"

[012-047] The whole thing sounded as simple as ABC. All we had to do was go

[012-048] over there and take the place. The captain didn't say how many

[012-049] Germans there would be nor what they would be doing while we were

[012-050] taking their comfortable little position. Indeed he seemed to quite

[012-051] carelessly leave [[boche|the Boche]] out of the reckoning. I didn't. I knew

[012-052] that some of us, and quite probably most of us, would never come

[012-053] back.

[012-054] I selected my men carefully, taking only the coolest and steadiest

[012-055] and the best bombers. Most of them were men who had been at [[dover|Dover]]

[012-056] with me. I felt like an executioner when I notified them of their

[012-057] selection.

[012-058] At nine-thirty we were ready, stripped to the lightest of necessary

[012-059] equipment. Each of the men was armed with a bucket of bombs. Some

[012-060] carried an extra supply in satchels, so we knew there would be no

[012-061] shortage of Millses.

[012-062] Lieutenant May took us out over the top on schedule time, and we

[012-063] started for the position to be taken. We walked erect but in the

[012-064] strictest silence for about a thousand yards. At that time the

[012-065] distances were great on [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]], as the [[big-push|Big Push]] was in full

[012-066] swing, and the advance had been fast. [[trench|Trench]] systems had been

[012-067] demolished, and in many places there were only shell holes and

[012-068] isolated pieces of trench defended by machine [[artillery|guns]]. The whole

[012-069] movement had progressed so far that the lines were far apart and

[012-070] broken, so much so that in many cases the fighting had come back to

[012-071] the open work of early in the war.

[012-072] Poking along out there, I had the feeling that we were an awfully

[012-073] long way from the comparative safety of our main body--too far away

[012-074] for comfort. We were. Any doubts on the matter disappeared before

[012-075] morning.

[012-076] At the end of the thousand yards Lieutenant May gave the signal to

[012-077] lie down. We lay still half an hour or so and then crawled forward.

[012-078] Fortunately there was no barbed [[barbed-wire|wire]], as all entanglements had been

[012-079] destroyed by the terrific [[barrage|bombardment]] that had been going on for

[012-080] weeks. The Germans made no attempt to repair it nor did we.

[012-081] We crawled along for about ten minutes, and the Lieutenant passed

[012-082] the word in whispers to get ready, as we were nearly on them. Each

[012-083] of us got out a [[grenade|bomb]], pulled the pin with our teeth, and waited for

[012-084] the signal. It was fairly still. Away off to the rear, guns were

[012-085] going, but they seemed a long way off. Forward, and away off to

[012-086] the right beyond the Wood, there was a lot of rifle and machine-gun

[012-087] fire, and we could see the sharp little lavender stabs of flame

[012-088] like electric flashes. It was light enough so that we could see

[012-089] dimly.

[012-090] Just ahead we could hear the murmur of the Huns as they chatted in

[012-091] the trench. They hadn't seen us. Evidently they didn't suspect and

[012-092] were more or less careless.

[012-093] The Lieutenant waited until the sound of voices was a little louder

[012-094] than before, the Boches evidently being engaged in a fireside

[012-095] argument of some kind, and then he jumped to his feet shouting,

[012-096] "Now then, my lads. All together!"

[012-097] We came up all standing and let 'em go. It was about fifteen yards

[012-098] to Fritz, and that is easy to a good bomber, as my men all were. A

[012-099] yell of surprise and fright went up from the trench, and they

[012-100] started to run. We spread out so as to get room, gave them another

[012-101] round of Millses, and rushed.

[012-102] The trench wasn't really a trench at all. It was the remains of a

[012-103] perfectly good one, but had been bashed all to pieces, and was now

[012-104] only five or six [[crater|shell craters]] connected by the ruined [[traverse|traverses]].

[012-105] At no point was it more than waist high and in some places only

[012-106] knee high. We swarmed into what was left of the trench and after

[012-107] the Heinies. There must have been forty of them, and it didn't take

[012-108] them long to find out that we were only a dozen. Then they came

[012-109] back at us. We got into a crooked bit of traverse that was in

[012-110] relatively good shape and threw up a barricade of [[sandbag|sandbags]]. There

[012-111] was any amount of them lying about.

[012-112] The Germans gave us a bomb or two and considerable rifle fire, and

[012-113] we beat it around the corner of the bay. Then we had it back and

[012-114] forth, a regular seesaw game. We would chase them back from the

[012-115] barricade, and then they would rush us and back we would go. After

[012-116] we had lost three men and Lieutenant May had got a slight wound, we

[012-117] got desperate and got out of the trench and rushed them for further

[012-118] orders. We fairly showered them as we followed them up, regardless

[012-119] of danger to ourselves. All this scrap through they hadn't done

[012-120] anything with the machine guns. One was in our end of the trench,

[012-121] and we found that the other was out of commission. They must have

[012-122] been short of small-arm ammunition and bombs, because on that last

[012-123] strafing they cleared out and stayed.

[012-124] After the row was over we counted noses and found four dead and

[012-125] three slightly [[casualty|wounded]], including Lieutenant May. I detailed two

[012-126] men to take the wounded and the Lieutenant back. That left four of

[012-127] us to consolidate the position. The Lieutenant promised to return

[012-128] with relief, but as it turned out he was worse than he thought, and

[012-129] he didn't get back.

[012-130] I turned to and inspected the position. It was pretty hopeless.

[012-131] There really wasn't much to consolidate. The whole works was

[012-132] knocked about and was only fit for a temporary defence. There were

[012-133] about a dozen German dead, and we searched them but found nothing

[012-134] of value. So we strengthened our cross-trench barricade and waited

[012-135] for the relief. It never came.

[012-136] When it began to get light, the place looked even more

[012-137] discouraging. There was little or no cover. We knew that unless we

[012-138] got some sort of concealment, the airplanes would spot us, and

[012-139] that we would get a shell or two. So we got out the entrenching

[012-140] tools and dug into the side of the best part of the shallow

[012-141] traverse. We finally got a slight overhang scraped out. We didn't

[012-142] dare go very far under for fear that it would cave. We got some

[012-143] sandbags up on the sides and three of us crawled into the shelter.

[012-144] The other man made a similar place for himself a little distance

[012-145] off.

[012-146] The day dawned clear and bright and gave promise of being hot.

[012-147] Along about seven we began to get hungry. A [[british-army|Tommy]] is always hungry,

[012-148] whether he is in danger or not. When we took account of stock and

[012-149] found that none of us had brought along "iron [[trench-rations|rations]]", we

[012-150] discovered that we were all nearly starved. Killing is hungry work.

[012-151] We had only ourselves to blame. We had been told repeatedly never

[012-152] to go anywhere without "[[iron-ration|iron rations]]", but [[tommy|Tommy]] is a good deal of

[012-153] a child and unless you show him the immediate reason for a thing he

[012-154] is likely to disregard instructions. I rather blamed myself in this

[012-155] case for not seeing that the men had their emergency food. In

[012-156] fact, it was my duty to see that they had. But I had overlooked it.

[012-157] And I hadn't brought any myself.

[012-158] The "iron ration" consists of a pound of "[[bully-beef|bully]] beef", a small tin

[012-159] containing tea and sugar enough for two doses, some Oxo cubes, and

[012-160] a few biscuits made of reinforced concrete. They are issued for

[012-161] just such an emergency as we were in as we lay in our isolated

[012-162] [[dug-out]]. The soldier is apt to get into that sort of situation

[012-163] almost any time, and it is folly ever to be without the ration.

[012-164] Well, we didn't have ours, and we knew we wouldn't get any before

[012-165] night, if we did then. One thing we had too much of. That was [[rum-ration|rum]].

[012-166] The night before a bunch of us had been out on a ration party, and

[012-167] we had come across a Brigade Dump. This is a station where rations

[012-168] are left for the various companies to come and draw their own, also

[012-169] ammo and other necessities. There was no one about, and we had gone

[012-170] through the outfit. We found two cases of rum, four gallons in a

[012-171] case, and we promptly filled our bottles, more than a pint each.

[012-172] Tommy is always very keen on his rum. The brand used in the army is

[012-173] high proof and burns like fire going down, but it is warming. The

[012-174] regular ration as served after a cold sentry go is called a "tot."

[012-175] It is enough to keep the cold out and make a man wish he had

[012-176] another. The average Tommy will steal rum whenever he can without

[012-177] the danger of getting caught.

[012-178] It happened that all four of us were in the looting party and had

[012-179] our bottles full. Also it happened that we were all normally quite

[012-180] temperate and hadn't touched our supply.

[012-181] So we all took a nip and tightened up our belts. Then we took

[012-182] another and another. We lay on our backs with our heads out of the

[012-183] burrow, packed in like sardines and looking up at the sky. Half a

[012-184] dozen airplanes came out and flew over. We had had a hard night and

[012-185] we all dozed off, at least I did, and I guess the others did also.

[012-186] Around nine we all waked up, and [[bones|Bones]]--he was the fellow in the

[012-187] middle--began to complain of thirst. Then we all took another nip

[012-188] and wished it was water. We discussed the matter of crawling down

[012-189] to a muddy pool at the end of the traverse and having some out of

[012-190] that, but passed it up as there was a dead man lying in it. Bones,

[012-191] who was pretty well educated--he once asked me if I had visited

[012-192] Emerson's [[blighty|home]] and was astounded that I hadn't--quoted from Kipling

[012-193] something to the effect that,

[012-194] When you come to slaughter

[012-195] You'll do your work on water,

[012-196] An' you'll lick the bloomin' boots of 'im that's got it.

[012-197] Then Bones cursed the rum and took another nip. So did the rest of

[012-198] us.

[012-199] There was a considerable bombardment going on all the forenoon, but

[012-200] few [[shell|shells]] came anywhere near us. Some [[shrapnel|shrapnel burst]] over us a

[012-201] little way off to the right, and some of the fragments fell in the

[012-202] trench, but on the whole the morning was uncomfortable but not

[012-203] dangerous.

[012-204] Around half-past ten we saw an airplane [[battle|fight]] that was almost worth

[012-205] the forenoon's discomfort. A lot of them had been circling around

[012-206] ever since daybreak. When the fight started, two of our planes were

[012-207] nearly over us. Suddenly we saw three Boche planes volplaning down

[012-208] from away up above. They grew bigger and bigger and opened with

[012-209] their guns when they were nearly on top of our fellows. No hits.

[012-210] Then all five started circling for top position. One of the Boches

[012-211] started to fall and came down spinning, but righted himself not

[012-212] more than a thousand feet up. Our anti air-craft guns opened on

[012-213] him, and we could see the shells bursting with little cottony puffs

[012-214] all around. Some of the shrapnel struck near us. They missed him,

[012-215] and up he went again. Presently all five came circling lower and

[012-216] lower, jockeying for position and spitting away with their guns. As

[012-217] they all got to the lower levels, the anti air-craft guns stopped

[012-218] firing, fearing to get our men.

[012-219] Suddenly one of the Huns burst into flames and came toppling down

[012-220] behind his lines, his [[gas-warfare|gas]] tank ablaze. Almost immediately one of

[012-221] ours dropped, also burning and behind the Boche lines.

[012-222] After that it was two to one, and the fight lasted more than ten

[012-223] minutes. Then down went a Hun, not afire but tumbling end over end

[012-224] behind our lines. I learned afterwards that this fellow was unhurt

[012-225] and was taken prisoner. That left it an even thing. We could see

[012-226] half a dozen planes rushing to attack the lone Boche. He saw them

[012-227] too. For he turned tail and skedaddled for home.

[012-228] Bonesie began to philosophize on the cold-bloodedness of air

[012-229] fighting and really worked himself up into an almost optimistic

[012-230] frame of mind. He was right in the midst of a flowery oration on

[012-231] our comparative safety, "nestling on the bosom of Mother Earth",

[012-232] when, without any warning whatever, there came a perfect avalanche

[012-233] of shell all around us.

[012-234] I knew perfectly well that we were caught. The shells, as near as

[012-235] we could see, were coming from our side. Doubtless our people

[012-236] thought that the trench was still manned by Germans, and they were

[012-237] shelling for the big noon attack. Such an attack was made, as I

[012-238] learned afterwards, but I never saw it.

[012-239] At eleven o'clock I looked at my watch. Somehow I didn't fear

[012-240] death, although I felt it was near. Maybe the rum was working. I

[012-241] turned to Bonesie and said, "What about that safety stuff, old

[012-242] top?"

[012-243] "Cheer, cheer, Darby," said he. "We may pull through yet."

[012-244] "Don't think so," I insisted. "It's us for pushing up the daisies.

[012-245] Good luck if we don't meet again!"

[012-246] I put my hand in and patted Dinky on the back, and sent up another

[012-247] little prayer for luck. Then there was a terrific shock, and

[012-248] everything went black.

[012-249] When I came out of it, I had the sensation of struggling up out of

[012-250] water. I thought for an instant that I was drowning. And in effect

[012-251] that was almost what was happening to me. I was buried, all but one

[012-252] side of my face. A tremendous weight pressed down on me, and I

[012-253] could only breathe in little gasps.

[012-254] I tried to move my legs and arms and couldn't. Then I wiggled my

[012-255] fingers and toes to see if any bones were broken. They wiggled all

[012-256] right. My right nostril and eye were full of dirt; also my mouth. I

[012-257] spit out the dirt and moved my head until my nose and eye were

[012-258] clear. I ached all over.

[012-259] It was along toward sundown. Up aloft a single airplane was winging

[012-260] toward our lines. I remember that I wondered vaguely if he was the

[012-261] same fellow who had been fighting just before the world fell in on

[012-262] me.

[012-263] I tried to sing out to the rest of the men, but the best I could do

[012-264] was a kind of loud gurgle. There was no answer. My head was

[012-265] humming, and the blood seemed to be bursting my ears. I was

[012-266] terribly sorry for myself and tried to pull my strength together

[012-267] for a big try at throwing the weight off my chest, but I was

[012-268] absolutely helpless. Then again I slid out of consciousness.

[012-269] It was dark when I struggled up through the imaginary water again.

[012-270] I was still breathing in gasps, and I could feel my heart going in

[012-271] great thumps that hurt and seemed to shake the ground. My tongue

[012-272] was curled up and dry, and fever was simply burning me up. My mind

[012-273] was clear, and I wished that I hadn't drunk that rum. Finding I

[012-274] could raise my head a little, I cocked it up, squinting over my

[012-275] cheek bones--I was on my back--and could catch the far-off flicker

[012-276] of the silver-green [[very-light|flare]] lights. There was a rattle of musketry

[012-277] off in the direction where the Boche lines ought to be. From behind

[012-278] came the constant boom of big guns. I lay back and watched the

[012-279] stars, which were bright and uncommonly low. Then a shell burst

[012-280] near by,--not near enough to hurt,--but buried as I was the whole

[012-281] earth seemed to shake. My heart stopped beating, and I went out

[012-282] again.

[012-283] When I came to the next time, it was still dark, and somebody was

[012-284] lifting me on to a stretcher. My first impression was of getting a

[012-285] long breath. I gulped it down, and with every grateful inhalation I

[012-286] felt my ribs painfully snapping back into place. Oh, Lady! Didn't I

[012-287] just eat that air up.

[012-288] And then, having gotten filled up with the long-denied oxygen, I

[012-289] asked, "Where's the others?"

[012-290] "Ayen't no hothers," was the brief reply.

[012-291] And there weren't. Later I reconstructed the occurrences of the

[012-292] night from what I was told by the rescuing party.

[012-293] A big shell had slammed down on us, drilling Bonesie, the man in

[012-294] the middle, from end to end. He was demolished. The shell was a

[012-295] "dud", that is, it didn't explode. If it had, there wouldn't have

[012-296] been anything whatever left of any of us. As it was our overhang

[012-297] caved in, letting sandbags and earth down on the remaining man and

[012-298] myself. The other man was buried clean under. He had life in him

[012-299] still when he was [[dug-out|dug out]] but "went west" in about ten minutes.

[012-300] The fourth man was found dead from shrapnel. I found, too, that the

[012-301] two unwounded men who had gone back with Lieutenant May had both

[012-302] been killed on the way in. So out of the twelve men who started on

[012-303] the "suicide club" stunt I was the only one left. Dinky was still

[012-304] inside my tunic, and I laid the luck all to him.

[012-305] Back in hospital I was found to be suffering from shell shock. Also

[012-306] my heart was pushed out of place. There were no bones broken,

[012-307] though I was sore all over, and several ribs were pulled around so

[012-308] that it was like a knife thrust at every breath. Besides that, my

[012-309] nerves were shattered. I jumped a foot at the slightest noise and

[012-310] twitched a good deal.

[012-311] At the end of a week I asked the M.O. if I would get [[england|Blighty]] and he

[012-312] said he didn't think so, not directly. He rather thought that they

[012-313] would keep me in hospital for a month or two and see how I came

[012-314] out. The officer was a Canadian and had a sense of humor and was

[012-315] most affable. I told him if this jamming wasn't going to get me

[012-316] Blighty, I wanted to go back to duty and get a real one. He laughed

[012-317] and tagged me for a beach resort at Ault-Onival on the northern

[012-318] coast of [[france|France]].

[012-319] I was there a week and had a bully time. The place had been a

[012-320] fashionable watering place before the war, and when I was there the

[012-321] transient population was largely wealthy Belgians. They entertained

[012-322] a good deal and did all they could for the pleasure of the four

[012-323] thousand boys who were at the camp. The [[ymca|Y.M.C.A.]] had a huge tent

[012-324] and spread themselves in taking care of the soldiers. There were

[012-325] entertainments almost every night, moving pictures, and music. The

[012-326] food was awfully good and the beds comfortable, and that pretty

[012-327] nearly spells heaven to a man down from the front.

[012-328] Best of all, the bathing was fine, and it was possible to keep the

[012-329] [[cootie|cooties]] under control,--more or less. I went in bathing two and

[012-330] three times daily as the sloping shore made it just as good at low

[012-331] tide as at high.

[012-332] I think that glorious week at the beach made the hardships of the

[012-333] front just left behind almost worth while. My chum, Corporal [[wellsie|Wells]],

[012-334] who had a quaint Cockney philosophy, used to say that he liked to

[012-335] have the stomach ache because it felt so good when it stopped. On

[012-336] the same theory I became nearly convinced that a month in the

[012-337] trenches was good fun because it felt so good to get out.

[012-338] At the end of the week I was better but still shaky. I started

[012-339] pestering the M.O. to tag me for Blighty. He wouldn't, so I sprung

[012-340] the same proposition on him that I had on the doctor at the

[012-341] base,--to send me back to duty if he couldn't send me to England.

[012-342] The brute took me at my word and sent me back to the battalion.

[012-343] I rejoined on the [[somme|Somme]] again just as they were going back for the

[012-344] second time in that most awful part of the line. Many of the old

[012-345] faces were gone. Some had got the wooden cross, and some had gone

[012-346] to Blighty.

[012-347] I sure was glad when old Wellsie hopped out and grabbed me.

[012-348] "Gawd lumme, Darby," he said. "Hi sye, an' me thinkin' as 'ow you

[012-349] was back in Blighty. An' 'ere ye are yer blinkin' old self. Or is

[012-350] it yer bloomin' ghost. I awsks ye. Strike me pink, Yank. I'm glad."

[012-351] And he was. At that I did feel more or less ghostly. I seemed to

[012-352] have lost some of my confidence. I expected to "go west" on the

[012-353] next time in. And that's a bad way to feel out there.
