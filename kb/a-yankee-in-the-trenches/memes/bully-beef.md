## Overview

Bully beef was the quintessential food of the [[tommy|British soldier]] in World War I—a tinned corned beef ration that was issued in staggering quantities to troops on the [[france|Western Front]]. The name derives from the [[france|French]] *bouilli* (boiled beef), anglicized by [[tommy|British soldier]]s into "bully." In Holmes's account, [[trench-rations|bully beef]] appears as a constant, often monotonous, presence in the soldier's diet: a reliable source of protein that sustained men through the physical demands of [[trench|trench warfare]], but also a symbol of the culinary privations of military life.

## In the Trenches

Holmes introduces [[trench-rations|bully beef]] early in his narrative, setting the tone for life at the front: "nothing but [[trench-rations|bully beef]] and biscuits. We made 'char'" (002-008). This terse description captures the spartan reality—meals consisted of cold tinned beef, hard biscuits, and tea brewed over makeshift fires. The pairing of [[trench-rations|bully beef]] and biscuits was so standardized that it became a metonym for [[trench-rations|army rations]] themselves.

The centrality of [[trench-rations|bully beef]] to the soldier's existence is underscored by Holmes's observation that "the backbone of [[tommy|Tommy]]'s diet is [[trench-rations|bully beef]]" (005-006). This was not merely a food item but a dietary staple, consumed day after day, in the [[trench|trenches]] and in rest areas alike. The monotony of the diet was keenly felt: "without butter or anything, except [[trench-rations|bully beef]]" (005-069), Holmes notes, describing the absence of variety or condiments that might have made meals more palatable.

The "iron ration" carried by soldiers included a pound of [[trench-rations|bully beef]], reserved for emergencies when normal supply lines broke down. Holmes describes this emergency reserve: "The 'iron ration' consists of a pound of '[[trench-rations|bully beef]]', a small tin" (012-158) of other provisions. Soldiers were forbidden from consuming their [[iron-ration|iron rations]] without orders, and Holmes records the [[hun]]ger that resulted when men went without proper resupply.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes provides a dryly humorous glimpse of soldiers' relationship with their [[trench-rations|rations]]: a brief, almost resigned comment—"on [[trench-rations|bully beef]]" (002-165)—that stands alone as a complete observation on the state of army catering. The matter-of-fact tone reflects the soldier's acceptance of, and exhaustion with, the relentless monotony of tinned meat.

The iron ration episode is particularly revealing. Holmes notes that "none of us had brought along '[[iron-ration|iron rations]]'" (012-149) on a particular movement, a breach of regulations that left the men [[hun]]gry. But, as he adds with characteristic understatement, "[[tommy|Tommy]] is a good deal of" (012-152) a scrounger, suggesting that soldiers found ways to supplement their diet despite official shortages.

## Military Context

Bully beef was produced in vast quantities by the [[british-army|British Army]]'s supply system, with meat sourced from Britain, Argentina, Australia, and the United States. The tins were rectangular, easily packable, and could be opened with a bayonet in the field. Each tin typically held about one pound of compressed corned beef, providing approximately 1,000 calories—a substantial portion of a soldier's daily energy requirement.

The logistical challenge of feeding millions of men in the field made [[trench-rations|bully beef]] an ideal solution: it required no cooking, was shelf-stable indefinitely, and could be transported in bulk without refrigeration. However, the nutritional limitations of a diet dominated by tinned meat and hard biscuits contributed to health problems including constipation and vitamin deficiencies. Soldiers supplemented their [[trench-rations|rations]] through parcels from [[blighty|home]], purchases from military [[water-bottle|canteen]]s, and foraging in abandoned villages and farms.
