## Overview

Casualty — the military term for a soldier rendered unfit for duty through death, wounds, illness, or capture — was the constant companion of the trench soldier. On the [[france|Western Front]], casualty rates were staggering: [[battle|battles]] could cost tens of thousands of lives for gains measured in yards. Every soldier knew the odds, and every man coped with that knowledge in his own way.

## In the Memoir

Holmes describes the grim arithmetic of [[trench|trench warfare]] with a soldier's detachment. After a [[raid|trench raid]]:

> Besides that we lost seven men killed. The German loss was estimated at about one hundred casualties.
> (004-052)

The disparity in casualties often reflected the advantage of the defender, but the numbers themselves became abstract — until it was someone you knew.

## Types of Casualties

A casualty could mean many things:

- **Killed in [[battle|action]]** — The final and most terrible category
- **Wounded** — From minor shrapnel cuts to catastrophic injuries
- **Missing** — Men who simply di[[mine-explosion|sap]]peared, swallowed by the mud or vaporized by shellfire
- **[[shell|Shell]] shock** — Psychological casualties, not yet fully understood
- **Sick** — [[trench|Trench]] fever, dysentery, and other diseases that could incapacitate as effectively as a bullet

Holmes notes the peculiar way soldiers processed casualties:

> There was no stopping for the wounded. They lay where they fell.
> (010-075)

In the heat of an attack, the living could not pause for the fallen — the objective came first.

## Self-Inflicted Wounds

One of the darker aspects of [[trench|trench warfare]] was the self-inflicted wound — a desperate act by men who could no longer face the front:

> The suspicion of a self-inflicted wound, which is one of the worst things a soldier can be accused of.
> (011-078)

A self-inflicted wound, if detected, was a court-martial offense. Yet the pressure that drove men to such measures was understood, if never openly discussed.

## The Casualty's Hope

For many, a wound was the only way out of the [[trench|trenches]] short of death or the war's end. A "cushy" wound — serious enough for [[blighty|Blighty]] but not life-changing — was the soldier's secret wish:

> Puts in his time hoping for a wound that will be "cushy" enough not to kill or permanently disable him but sufficient to get him sent to Blighty.
> (011-078)
