# Dover

**Dover** is a historic port city on the English Channel coast, famous for its white cliffs and its role as [[england|England]]'s gateway to the Continent. During the First World War, Dover served as a major em[[estaminet|bar]]kation point for troops heading to [[france|France]] and a key training location for new recruits — including [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]].

## In the Narrative

After arriving in [[london|London]] and his initial processing, Holmes was transferred to Dover for training:

> "…later was transferred to Dover with twenty others. I was at…" [001-152]

The [[estaminet|bar]]racks at Dover occupied a dramatic position atop the famous cliffs:

> "Our barracks at Dover was on the heights of the cliffs…" [001-154]

Holmes found his fellow trainees to be good company — a diverse group of volunteers drawn by the call of war:

> "I found the rookies at Dover a jolly, companionable lot, a…" [001-160]

The training at Dover was the author's first real taste of military life. Before this, the journey from [[boston|Boston]] and brief stay in [[london|London]] had the flavor of an adventure. At Dover, the serious business of becoming a soldier began in earnest.

## Dover's Wartime Role

During WWI, Dover was far more than a training camp:

- **Primary em[[estaminet|bar]]kation port**: Tens of thousands of troops departed from Dover for the [[france|Western Front]] via cross-Channel steamers
- **Naval base**: The Dover [[patrol|Patrol]], a Royal Navy command, operated from the port to protect Channel shipping and block German U-boats
- **Coastal defense**: The port was fortified against potential German naval [[trench-raid|raid]]s
- **[[hospital|Hospital]] ships**: Wounded soldiers returning from [[france|France]] often landed at Dover before transfer to hospitals inland

## The Cliffs as Symbol

The white cliffs of Dover have long served as a powerful symbol of [[england|England]] for travelers and soldiers alike. For troops departing for [[france|France]], the cliffs were the last sight of [[blighty|home]]. For the [[casualty|wounded]] returning, they were the first sign of safety and [[england|England]]. Holmes's brief mention of the "heights of the cliffs" captures this visual symbol that meant so much to a generation of soldiers.
