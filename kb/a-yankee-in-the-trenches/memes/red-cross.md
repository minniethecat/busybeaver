# Red Cross

## Overview

The Red Cross was the preeminent humanitarian organization of World War I, providing medical treatment, ambulance transport, and relief services to [[casualty|wounded]] soldiers on all sides of the conflict. Founded in 1863, the International Committee of the Red Cross operated with a mandate of neutrality, treating [[casualty|casualties]] regardless of nationality. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, the Red Cross appears at three pivotal moments in his journey from the front to recovery — as a recipient of charitable funds, as the operator of the hospital train that carried him from the battlefield, and as the provider of the motor ambulance that brought him to a [[hospital|London hospital]].

## In the Memoir

### Behind the Lines

Holmes first mentions the Red Cross in the context of troop entertainment behind the lines at Petite-Saens. A vaudeville show put on by ten soldiers — former actors detailed to amuse the men — charged a small admission fee, with the profits going to the Red Cross (005-108). This detail, mentioned almost in passing, illustrates how deeply the organization was woven into the fabric of army life: even soldiers' amusements were harnessed to support its work.

### The Hospital Train

The Red Cross appears most dramatically in the memoir after Holmes is [[casualty|wounded]]. Following emergency treatment — a shot of anti-lockjaw se[[rum-ration|rum]] injected into his chest — he finds himself aboard a hospital train:

> I was tucked away in a white enameled Red Cross train with a pretty nurse taking my temperature. I loved that nurse. She looked sort of cool and holy.
> — (014-138)

The image is vivid: the sterile white carriage, the calm nurse, the [[casualty|wounded]] soldier suspended between the carnage of the front and the safety of the rear. The Red Cross train was, for countless [[casualty|wounded]] men, the first concrete evidence that they had survived and were being cared for.

### Return to England

Holmes's final encounter with the Red Cross comes after his arrival back in [[england|England]]. Following his Channel crossing, he was:

> taken in a big Red Cross motor loaned by Sir Charles Dickerson to the Fulham Hospital in Hammersmith
> — (015-050)

The motor ambulance delivered him to a hospital conveniently close to his own [[blighty|home]] — a detail Holmes notes with obvious relief. From battlefield to [[hospital|base hospital]] to [[london|London]], the Red Cross had shepherded him through every stage of his medical evacuation.
