# Tank

## Overview

The tank was one of the most dramatic technological innovations of World War I — a tracked, armored vehicle designed to cross [[no-mans-land|no man's land]], crush [[estaminet|bar]]bed [[barbed-wire|wire]], and provide mobile firepower in support of advancing infantry. It made its [[battle|combat]] debut during the [[battle-of-the-somme|Battle of the Somme]] in September 1916, and [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] was among the soldiers who witnessed its first use in battle. His account captures both the wonder and the limitations of this new weapon:

> I was to know afterwards as the "tanks." I shall never forget the first time I saw one
> — (009-197)

The tank was an absolute novelty — something no soldier on either side had ever seen before. Its psychological impact, on friend and enemy alike, was enormous.

## First Appearance

Holmes describes the tank's unveiling with palpable excitement:

> The tanks were an absolutely new thing — something that could and would win battles
> — (009-206)

The confidence was premature — the early tanks were slow, mechanically unreliable, and vulnerable to artillery — but the hope they inspired was real. After two years of static warfare, of attacks that gained yards at the cost of thousands of lives, the tank promised a way to break the deadlock.

Holmes's battalion went into [[battle|action]] at [[high-wood|High Wood]] with tanks in support:

> The tanks passed beyond us and half-way up the slope toward High Wood
> — (010-001)

The sight of these massive steel machines crawling forward through the shell holes must have been extraordinary — something between a prehistoric monster and a piece of industrial machinery, come to life on the battlefield.

## In Action

Holmes used a tank for cover during the advance, sheltering behind its armored bulk:

> There was a tank just ahead of me. I got behind it and followed along
> — (010-095)

The tactic was effective: the tank's steel plates could stop rifle and [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]]. But the tank could not stop shrapnel:

> The tank machine guns were spitting fire over the top of me, and I was pretty well protected from rifle bullets, but not the shrapnel
> — (010-089)–(010-097)

The [[shrapnel|shrapnel burst]] overhead, its steel balls pattering in the dirt all around. The tank provided cover from direct fire but not from the artillery that was the real killer on the [[france|Western Front]].

Holmes saw the tank formations at the battle of [[high-wood|High Wood]]:

> I obtained my first view of tanks at the battle of High Wood
> — (011-012)

And later, he records the prisoners taken:

> At the battle of High Wood the prisoners we took numbered over a thousand
> — (011-109)

The tanks had contributed to the psychological collapse of the German defenders, who had no answer to these steel beasts that bullets could not stop.

## The Limits of Innovation

For all their terrifying novelty, the early tanks were fragile machines. They broke down frequently, bogged in soft ground, and were vulnerable to direct artillery hits. Holmes does not dwell on the tanks' failures — his account is that of a soldier grateful for any advantage — but the historical record is clear: the tank of 1916 was a promising prototype, not a war-winning weapon.

Nevertheless, Holmes's testimony captures the moment when warfare changed. The tank he sheltered behind at [[high-wood|High Wood]] was the ancestor of every armored vehicle that would dominate the battlefields of the twentieth century. Its psychological impact — on the men who fought alongside it and the men who fled before it — was perhaps more significant than its immediate tactical contribution.
