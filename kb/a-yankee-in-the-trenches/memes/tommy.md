# Tommy

## Overview

"Tommy" — short for Tommy Atkins — was the universal nickname for the British soldier of World War I. More than a label, the name embodied an entire culture: the stoic, g[[rum-ration|rum]]bling, darkly humorous infantryman who endured the mud, rats, [[shell|shells]], and death of the [[france|Western Front]] with a resilience that astonished his allies and frustrated his enemies. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, Tommy is not a single individual but a collective character — the everyman of the [[trench|trenches]], equally capable of savage complaint and extraordinary courage. Holmes, an American who volunteered in the [[british-army|British Army]], writes about Tommy with the affectionate insight of an outsider who became an insider.

## Character and Temperament

Holmes introduces Tommy through one of his defining traits: the capacity to complain about absolutely anything, in any circumstances:

> The British Tommy growls or grouses over anything and everything
> — (002-025)

This grousing was not a sign of low morale — quite the opposite. It was a coping mechanism, a way of asserting one's humanity in conditions designed to crush it. Holmes captures the flavor of Tommy's speech in the authentic slang of the [[trench|trenches]]:

> "Garn," cuts in another Tommy. "Yer blinkin' 'igh wif yer wants, yer are!"
> — (002-157)

The Cockney-inflected dialogue, rendered phonetically, gives the modern reader a direct window into the sounds of the trench. Tommy's speech was a rich dialect of its own, blending working-class English, military jargon, and a growing vocabulary of trench slang.

## In Battle

For all his g[[rum-ration|rum]]bling, Tommy was a formidable [[battle|fight]]er. Holmes is unequivocal:

> The British Tommy is a scrapper when it comes to a fight
> — (005-055)

This scrappiness was tested constantly — in [[trench-raid|raid]]s, in holding the line under [[artillery|bombardment]], and in [[over-the-top|going over the top]]. Holmes describes an episode where the men, despite orders, spontaneously joined a rescue effort:

> every doggoned Tommy would hold the ladder
> — (005-066)

The phrase "every doggoned Tommy" captures the collective identity — Tommy was not just a type of soldier but a brotherhood. When one was in trouble, the others came, orders or no orders.

## Daily Life

Holmes's memoir is rich with the details of Tommy's daily existence — what he ate, what he smoked, what he wore, and how he coped with the long stretches of boredom between the moments of terror.

**Food**: The foundation of Tommy's diet in the [[trench|trenches]] was notorious:

> In the trenches the backbone of Tommy's diet is bully beef
> — (005-006)

But Tommy was resourceful. Holmes describes the improvised meals that emerged from scarcity:

> it is pathetic to see some of the messes Tommy gets together to eat
> — (005-095)

The word "pathetic" here carries its older meaning — evoking pathos, a mixture of pity and admiration for Tommy's determination to find comfort in appalling conditions.

**Smoking**: Tobacco was a universal comfort:

> Tommy is a great smoker. He gets a generous issue of tobacco
> — (005-141)

**[[boche|Hun]]ger**: Holmes observes a truth that any infantryman would recognize:

> A Tommy is always hungry
> — (012-147)

Even under fire, even with "[[iron-ration|iron rations]]" in his pack, Tommy found ways to supplement his diet — including the universal soldier's vice:

> The average Tommy will steal rum whenever he can get it
> — (012-176)

## The Blighty Ambition

One of the most revealing aspects of Tommy's psychology was his relationship with the concept of "[[blighty|Blighty]]" — [[england|England]], [[blighty|home]], and specifically a wound serious enough to be sent back there:

> It is the constant, and always ambition of the Tommy to get
> — (009-075)

Holmes describes the "[[blighty-wound|Blighty wound]]" — not a wound that [[casualty|killed]] or maimed, but one that was bad enough to be evacuated to [[england|England]]:

> the Tommy in swinging a "blighty" case
> — (009-074)

And when, after months of [[battle|combat]], a soldier finally got his ticket [[blighty|home]], the relief was overwhelming:

> Blighty! Blighty!! Blighty!!!
> — (014-162)

The triple exclamation, with its mounting intensity, is one of the memoir's most emotionally charged moments — Holmes's own [[blighty-wound|Blighty wound]], the end of his war.

## Tommy's Vocabulary

Holmes documents the rich linguistic world Tommy inhabited, much of which he g[[casualty|losses]] for the reader in a dedicated appendix. Some representative entries:

> **Fed up** — Tommy's way of saying "too much is enough"
> — (016-204)

> **Strafe** — One of the few words Tommy has borrowed from Fritz
> — (016-276)

This linguistic exchange — Tommy borrowing from [[germans|Fritz]], [[germans|Fritz]] borrowing from Tommy — is a reminder that the two sides of [[no-mans-land|no man's land]], for all their mutual killing, shared a common experience and, occasionally, a common vocabulary.
