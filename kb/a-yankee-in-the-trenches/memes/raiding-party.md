## Overview

A [[raid|raiding party]] was a small, specially selected force of soldiers tasked with conducting a surprise attack on enemy [[trench|trenches]]. Unlike major offensives involving thousands of men, a [[raid|trench raid]] was a limited operation — typically involving fifty to a few [[hun]]dred men — aimed at capturing prisoners, gathering intelligence, destroying enemy positions, or simply maintaining an aggressive posture. Holmes participated in and describes the first [[raid|trench raid]] his battalion ever attempted, offering a rare front-line account of the preparation, execution, and aftermath of these dangerous missions.

## In the Trenches

Holmes describes the preparation for a [[raid|trench raid]] in detail. The morning of the operation, "the members of the [[raid|raiding party]] were taken back a mile or so to the rear and were given instruction and rehearsal" (003-001). The rehearsal was necessary because "this was the first [[trench-raid|raid]] that 'Batt' had ever tried, and the staff was anxious to have it a success" (003-001). Fifty men were selected for the party, organized by [[lieutenant-blofeld|Lieutenant Blofeld]], who "beat our instructions into us until we knew exactly what we were to do" (003-001).

The [[raid|raiding party]] was composed of volunteers, and participation carried both risks and rewards. Holmes notes that he "had volunteered for a [[raid|raiding party]]" (008-189), and this act of volunteering had practical consequences for his career. It was customary for newly promoted non-commissioned officers to be reduced in rank upon arrival in [[france|France]], but because Holmes had joined in the [[trench|trenches]] and immediately volunteered for a [[trench-raid|raid]], "there had been no opportunity to reduce me" (008-189). His lance corporal's rank was thus confirmed — though he would later reflect that he "would have willingly sacrificed the pay and the so-called honor to have been a private" (008-189).

## Narrative Examples

The [[trench-raid|raid]] Holmes describes was a major undertaking involving careful coordination. The raiders, their faces blackened for night concealment, were equipped with specialized weapons including clubs, grenades, and [[revolver|pistol]]s rather than the cumbersome rifle and bayonet. They advanced across [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] under cover of darkness, reached the German [[barbed-wire|wire]], and after the [[barrage|artillery barrage]] lifted, stormed into the enemy trench.

Holmes's account emphasizes the chaotic, close-[[billets|quarters]] nature of [[raid|trench raid]]ing — the confusion of grenade explosions in confined spaces, the brutal hand-to-hand [[battle|fight]]ing with improvised weapons, and the desperate scramble to withdraw before German reinforcements could organize a counter-attack. The [[trench-raid|raid]] succeeded in its objectives but at a cost in [[casualty|casualties]] that Holmes records with his characteristic matter-of-factness.

## Military Context

[[trench|Trench]] [[raid|raiding]] was a defining feature of World War I [[trench|trench warfare]], particularly on the [[france|Western Front]] from 1915 onward. British, [[france|French]], and German armies all employed [[trench-raid|raid]]s as a means of maintaining [[battle|combat]] readiness during the long periods of static warfare between major offensives. [[raid|Raid]]s served multiple purposes: gathering intelligence through prisoners and documents, destroying enemy machine-gun posts and mortar positions, under[[mine-explosion|mining]] enemy morale, and giving inexperienced troops their first taste of [[battle|combat]] in controlled circumstances.

The typical [[raid|raiding party]] carried specialized equipment: [[barbed-wire|wire]] cutters, bangalore torpedoes for blowing gaps in [[estaminet|bar]]bed [[barbed-wire|wire]], [[grenade|Mills bomb]]s for close [[battle|fight]]ing, and clubs or "knobkerries" for silent killing. Faces were blackened with burnt cork, and identification discs were removed to prevent the enemy from gathering intelligence if a [[trench-raid|raid]]er was [[casualty|killed]] or captured. The [[trench-raid|raid]] Holmes describes reflects these standard practices, adapted to the specific conditions of his sector of the front.
