# Click

**To click** — or more commonly, to **"click it"** — was the [[tommy|British Tommy]]'s matter-of-fact euphemism for being [[casualty|killed]]. In a war where death was an everyday occurrence, the understated Cockney term captured the fatalistic humor of the [[trench|trenches]]: you didn't die gloriously, you simply "clicked it."

## Usage in the Narrative

Holmes introduces the term in the context of [[sniper]] danger:

> "…pulls the trigger, — and you 'click it' if you happen to…" [002-091]

The randomness of death is underscored:

> "…evidently was high, for they only 'clicked' two…" [003-077]

In a vivid passage, Holmes describes a comrade's close call:

> "…had been clicked on the first fire. Jerry got…" [003-146]

The term was so common it generated derivatives:

> "'Clickety click' is sixty-six. 'Top of the house'…" [005-180]

A comrade uses it casually in banter:

> "If ye clicks, I'll draw yer fags from Blighty…" [007-262]

Holmes himself used the term freely about [[sniper|sniping]]:

> "…and I would click a dozen or more." [010-119]

Seeing a [[casualty|wounded]] enemy, he wondered:

> "…wondered whether the poor chap 'clicked' it." [011-084]

Even mundane [[fatigue]] duties were described with the term:

> "This time we clicked a burial fatigue. It was my…" [013-023]

## The Language of Death

The understatement of "click it" speaks volumes about the psychology of [[trench|trench warfare]]. Soldiers developed an entire vocabulary of euphemism for death — "going west," "getting it," "clicking it." These phrases allowed men to discuss the omnipresent possibility of death without confronting its full horror. The mechanical metaphor — a click, like a camera shutter or a rifle's dry-fire — reduced the finality of death to something almost casual.
