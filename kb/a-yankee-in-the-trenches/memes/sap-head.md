## Overview

A "[[mine-explosion|sap]]" was a trench [[dug-out|dug out]]ward from the main line toward the enemy positions — typically narrower and shallower than a standard [[trench|communication trench]]. The "[[sap|sap head]]" was its forward-most point, often just yards from the German line. From here, men could listen for enemy [[mine-explosion|mining]] activity, observe enemy movements, or launch surprise attacks.

## In the Memoir

Holmes describes [[sap|sap head]]s as key tactical positions. [[trench|Trench]] [[raiding-party|raiders]] would use them as staging points:

> If it hits you fair, you disappear. At a little distance you are safe.
> (007-279)

He also describes their use in the [[trench-raid|raid]] briefing for [[lieutenant-may|Lieutenant May]]'s [[grenade|bomb]]ing party:

> "Just beyond High Wood and to the left there is a sap or small trench leading to the sunken road that lies between the towns of Albert and Bapaume. That position commands a military point that we find necessary to hold before we can make another attack."
> (012-034)

## Construction and Use

[[sap|Sap]]s were dug by specialist troops — [[mine-explosion|sap]]pers — who worked at the head of the trench, digging forward while protected by a shield of [[sandbag|sandbags]]. Holmes himself served on [[mine-explosion|sap]]per duty:

> I was lucky enough after the first day to be put on sappers' duty.
> (007-279)

Sometimes the [[germans|Germans]] would run their own [[mine-explosion|sap]]s out close to the surface, creating [[listening-post|listening posts]] that could detect British [[mine-explosion|mining]]:

> Sometimes Fritz would run a sap out reasonably near the surface.
> (007-279)

A [[sap|sap head]] was a dangerous place — exposed to enemy fire and the constant threat of being cut off. But it was essential for intelligence-gathering and as a launch point for [[trench-raid|raid]]s.

On [[raid|raiding]] missions, men would climb into the [[sap|sap head]] before going over:

> As we climbed into a sap head, the orders were whispered back.
> (007-279)
