[011-001] For weeks after our first introduction to the [[tank|tanks]] they were the

[011-002] chief topic of conversation in our battalion. And, notwithstanding

[011-003] the fact that we had seen the monsters go into [[battle|action]], had seen

[011-004] what they did and the effect they had on [[boche|the Boche]], the details of

[011-005] their building and of their mechanism remained a mystery for a long

[011-006] time.

[011-007] For weeks about all we knew about them was what we gathered from

[011-008] their appearance as they reeled along, camouflaged with browns and

[011-009] yellows like great toads, and that they were named with quaint

[011-010] names like "Creme de Menthe" and "Diplodocus."

[011-011] Eventually I met with a member of the crews who had manned the

[011-012] tanks at the battle of [[high-wood|High Wood]], and I obtained from him a

[011-013] description of some of his sensations. It was a thing we had all

[011-014] wondered about,--how the men inside felt as they went over.

[011-015] My tanker was a young fellow not over twenty-five, a machine

[011-016] gunner, and in a little _estaminet_, over a glass of citron and

[011-017] soda, he told me of his first battle.

[011-018] "Before we went in," he said, "I was a little bit uncertain as to

[011-019] how we were coming out. We had tried the old boats out and had

[011-020] given them every reasonable test. We knew how much they would stand

[011-021] in the way of [[shell|shells]] on top and in the way of bombs or mines

[011-022] underneath. Still there was all the difference between rehearsal

[011-023] and the actual going on the stage.

[011-024] "When we crawled in through the trapdoor for the first time over,

[011-025] the shut-up feeling got me. I'd felt it before but not that way. I

[011-026] got to imagining what would happen if we got stalled somewhere in

[011-027] the [[germans|Boche]] lines, and they built a fire around us. That was natural,

[011-028] because it's hot inside a tank at the best. You mustn't smoke

[011-029] either. I hadn't minded that in rehearsal, but in action I was

[011-030] crazy for a fag.

[011-031] "We went across, you remember, at eleven, and the sun was shining

[011-032] bright. We were parboiled before we started, and when we got going

[011-033] good it was like a Turkish bath. I was stripped to the waist and

[011-034] was dripping. Besides that, when we begun to give 'em hell, the

[011-035] place filled with [[gas-warfare|gas]], and it was stifling. The old boat pitched a

[011-036] good deal going into shell holes, and it was all a man could do to

[011-037] keep his station. I put my nose up to my loop-hole to get air, but

[011-038] only once. The machine-gun bullets were simply rattling on our

[011-039] hide. Tock, tock, tock they kept drumming. The first shell that hit

[011-040] us must have been head on and a direct hit. There was a terrific

[011-041] crash, and the old girl shook all over,--seemed to pause a little

[011-042] even. But no harm was done. After that we breathed easier. We

[011-043] hadn't been quite sure that the Boche shells wouldn't do us in.

[011-044] "By the time we got to the Boche [[trench|trenches]], we knew he hadn't

[011-045] anything that could hurt us. We just sat and raked him and laughed

[011-046] and wished it was over, so we could get the air."

[011-047] I had already seen the effect of the tanks on the Germans. The

[011-048] batch of prisoners who had been turned over to me seemed dazed. One

[011-049] who spoke English said in a quavering voice:

[011-050] "Gott in Himmel, Kamarad, how could one endure? These things are

[011-051] not human. They are not fair."

[011-052] That "fair" thing made a hit with me after going against tear [[gas-weapon|gas]]

[011-053] and hearing about liquid fire and such things.

[011-054] The great number of the prisoners we took at High Wood were very

[011-055] scared looking at first and very surly. They apparently expected to

[011-056] be badly treated and perhaps tortured. They were tractable enough

[011-057] for the most part. But they needed watching, and they got it from

[011-058] me, as I had heard much of the treachery of the Boche prisoners.

[011-059] On the way to the rear with my bunch, I ran into a little episode

[011-060] which showed the foolishness of trusting a German,--particularly an

[011-061] officer.

[011-062] I was herding my lot along when we came up with about twelve in

[011-063] charge of a young fellow from a [[leicester-regiment|Leicester]] regiment. He was a

[011-064] private, and as most of his non-commissioned officers had been put

[011-065] out of action, he was acting corporal. We were walking together

[011-066] behind the prisoners, swapping notes on the fight, when one of his

[011-067] stopped, and no amount of coaxing would induce him to go any

[011-068] farther. He was an officer, of what rank I don't know, but judging

[011-069] from his age probably a lieutenant.

[011-070] Finally Crane--that was the Leicester chap--went up to the officer,

[011-071] threatened him with his bayonet, and let him know that he was due

[011-072] for the cold steel if he didn't get up and hike.

[011-073] Whereupon Mr. Fritz pulled an automatic from under his coat--he

[011-074] evidently had not been carefully searched--and aimed it at Crane.

[011-075] Crane dove at him and grabbed his wrist, but was too late. The gun

[011-076] went off and tore away Crane's right cheek. He didn't go down,

[011-077] however, and before I could get in without danger to Crane, he

[011-078] polished off the officer on the spot.

[011-079] The prisoners looked almost pleased. I suppose they knew the

[011-080] officer too well. I bandaged Crane and offered to take his

[011-081] prisoners in, but he insisted upon carrying on. He got very weak

[011-082] from loss of blood after a bit, and I had two of the Boches carry

[011-083] him to the nearest [[first-aid|dressing station]], where they took care of him. I

[011-084] have often wondered whether the poor chap "clicked" it.

[011-085] Eventually I got my batch of prisoners back to headquarters and

[011-086] turned them over. I want to say a word right here as to the

[011-087] treatment of the German prisoners by the British. In spite of the

[011-088] verified stories of the brutality shown to the Allied prisoners by

[011-089] the Hun, the English and [[france|French]] have too much humanity to

[011-090] retaliate. Time and again I have seen British soldiers who were

[011-091] bringing in Germans stop and spend their own scanty pocket money

[011-092] for their captives' comfort. I have done it myself.

[011-093] Almost inevitably the Boche prisoners were expecting harsh

[011-094] treatment. I found several who said that they had been told by

[011-095] their officers that they would be skinned alive if they surrendered

[011-096] to the English. They believed it, and you could hardly blame the

[011-097] poor devils for being scared.

[011-098] Whenever we were taking prisoners back, we always, unless we were

[011-099] in too much of a hurry, took them to the nearest [[water-bottle|canteen]] run by the

[011-100] [[ymca|Y.M.C.A.]] or by one of the artillery companies, and here we would

[011-101] buy English or American [[fag|fags]]. And believe me, they liked them. Any

[011-102] one who has smoked the tobacco issued to the German army could

[011-103] almost understand a soldier surrendering just to get away from it.

[011-104] Usually, too, we bought bread and sweets, if we could stand the

[011-105] price. The Heinies would bolt the food down as though they were

[011-106] half starved. And it was perfectly clear from the way they went

[011-107] after the luxuries that they got little more than the hard

[011-108] necessities of army fare.

[011-109] At the battle of High Wood the prisoners we took ran largely to

[011-110] very young fellows and to men of fifty or over. Some of the

[011-111] youngsters said they were only seventeen and they looked not over

[011-112] fifteen. Many of them had never shaved.

[011-113] I think the sight of those war-worn boys, haggard and hard,

[011-114] already touched with cruelty and blood lust, brought [[blighty|home]] to me

[011-115] closer than ever before what a hellish thing war is, and how keenly

[011-116] Germany must be suffering, along with the rest of us.
