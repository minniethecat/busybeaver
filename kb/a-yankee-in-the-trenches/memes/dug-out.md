# Dug-out

## Overview

The dug-out was a shelter excavated into the wall or floor of a trench, providing soldiers with some protection from [[shell|shelling]], weather, and — in theory — a place to sleep. Dug-outs ranged from crude scrapes [[estaminet|bar]]ely large enough for one man to elaborate underground rooms with bunks, tables, and even electric light. For the front-line soldier, a good dug-out was a treasure.

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir offers detailed, firsthand descriptions of dug-outs at every scale — from the claustrophobic British front-line "apartments" to the deep, multi-entrance German burrows encountered during [[raid|trench raid]]s.

## First Impressions: The Front-Line Dug-out

Holmes's first experience of a front-line dug-out shattered whatever romantic notions he had brought from training:

> My previous idea of a dug-out had been a fairly roomy sort of cave, somewhat damp, but comparatively comfortable. Well, this hole was about four and a half feet high — you had to get in doubled up on your hands and knees — about five by six feet on the sides, and housing ten. [002-108–111]

The dug-out was introduced to him as his "apartments" — a grim joke among the veteran soldiers:

> Wells started right in to make me feel at home and took me along with two others of the new men down to our "apartments", a dug-out built for about four, and housing ten. [002-105–107]

## German Dug-outs in the Raid

During the [[raid|trench raid]] described in Chapter III, Holmes's party encountered German dug-outs of very different construction. The [[germans|Germans]], like woodchucks, built multiple exits:

> Then we came to a dug-out door — in fact, several, as Fritz, like a woodchuck, always has more than one entrance to his burrow. We broke these in in jig time and looked down a thirty-foot hole on a dug-out full of graybacks. [003-105–108]

Holmes used his bayonet to pry open a dug-out door — and broke it in the process:

> I had broken my bayonet prying the dug-out door off and had my gun up-ended — clubbed. [003-120–121]

The [[trench-raid|raid]]'s results included extensive destruction of German underground positions:

> The German loss was estimated at about one hundred casualties, six machine guns and several dug-outs destroyed, and one mine shaft put out of business. We also brought back documents of value found by one party in an officer's dug-out. [003-156–159]

## Dug-outs in the Quarries

Later, near the [[vimy|Vimy Ridge]] sector, Holmes encountered a network of dug-outs built into an old quarry — impressive in scale but dreadful in comfort:

> This was a bowl-like depression formed by an old quarry. The place gave a natural protection and all around the edge were dug-outs which had been built by the French, running back into the hill, some of them more than a hundred feet. In the darkness we could see braziers glowing softly red at the mouth of each burrow. There was a cheerful, mouth-watering smell of cookery on the air, a garlicky smell, with now and then a whiff of spicy wood smoke. [006-099–106]

The atmospheric charm at the entrance, however, masked a suffocating reality within:

> The dug-outs here in the Quarries were the worst I saw in France. They were reasonably dry and roomy, but they had no ventilation except the tunnel entrance, and going back so far the air inside became simply stifling in a very short time. [006-110–113]

## Construction and Hierarchy

Dug-outs were typically reinforced with corrugated iron, timber, and [[sandbag|sandbags]]. The floor might be covered with [[duck-walk|duckboards]] to keep feet above the perpetual mud. A well-made dug-out had a drainage sump, ventilation shafts, and multiple layers of overhead cover to absorb shell blasts.

There was a clear hierarchy: the deeper and better-reinforced the dug-out, the higher its status. Officers had private dug-outs. Sergeant-majors lurked in theirs, emerging to brief newcomers — as Holmes himself experienced on his first day in the line:

> Arrived in the front trench, the sergeant-major appeared, crawling out of his dug-out — the usual place for a sergeant-major — and greeted us with, "Keep your nappers down, you rooks. Don't look over the top. It ayen't 'ealthy." [002-070–074]

## In the Memoir

Dug-outs appear throughout Holmes's memoir as the fixed points in a landscape of chaos. They are where soldiers slept, where officers planned, where the [[casualty|wounded]] were sheltered, and where men gathered around braziers to share food, smoke, and stories. The dug-out was, for all its discomfort, the closest thing to [[blighty|home]] a front-line soldier had.

Yet Holmes never lets the reader forget how precarious these shelters were. A direct hit from even a medium-caliber shell could collapse a dug-out, burying its occupants alive. The destruction of "several dug-outs" in the [[trench-raid|raid]] report (003-157) is a matter-of-fact reminder that what passed for safety in the [[trench|trenches]] was always conditional.
