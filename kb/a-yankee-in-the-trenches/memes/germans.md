# Germans

## Overview

The German soldier — "Fritz," "Jerry," "[[boche|the Boche]]," "the [[boche|Hun]]" — was the adversary who defined the [[tommy|British Tommy]]'s war. Separated by as little as 60 yards of [[no-mans-land|no man's land]], the two enemies lived parallel lives in mirrored trench systems, endured the same mud and [[shell|shells]], and [[casualty|killed]] each other with industrialized efficiency. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, Germans appear not as a monolithic enemy but as a varied cast: the s[[casualty|killed]] [[sniper]], the terrified prisoner, the [[trench-discipline|discipline]]d [[trench-raid|raid]]er, the corpse in [[no-mans-land|no man's land]]. Holmes's portrait, while filtered through the lens of a soldier [[battle|fight]]ing for his life, contains moments of surprising nuance and even respect.

## On the Other Side of No Man's Land

Holmes's first encounters with the Germans established them as a constant, almost intimate presence. The [[trench|trenches]] were so close that the enemy was more often heard than seen:

> I had frequently heard the low voices of Germans. Evidently they had found
> — (007-187)

This proximity created a strange kind of familiarity. The Germans were not an abstr[[battle|action]] — they were men with voices, heard murmuring just across the [[sandbag|sandbags]]. Holmes notes that both sides developed routines, rhythms of violence and quiet that became almost predictable.

The asymmetry between the two sides was often marked. Holmes observes a telling difference in [[battle|fight]]ing style:

> The Germans, on the other hand, hunt in pairs as snipers
> — (007-060)

The German tactical approach was methodical, systematic — "[[hun]]ting" rather than merely shooting. This professionalism commanded a degree of respect, even as it made the German [[sniper]] a more dangerous adversary.

## In Combat

The [[trench-raid|raid]] brought the two sides into the most intimate and violent contact. Holmes's account of entering a German trench during a [[trench-raid|raid]] captures the chaos of close-[[billets|quarters]] [[battle|combat]]:

> three or four Germans appeared, running down communication trench
> — (003-103)

And then, the sudden escalation:

> coming over the parapet we saw a big party of Germans
> — (003-136)

The German counter-attack was swift and overwhelming. Holmes's party, their [[grenade|bomb]]s exhausted, was forced to flee back across [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] — a harrowing retreat under fire that left several men dead or missing.

In a later [[battle|action]], Holmes describes the Germans' tenacious defense of a captured position:

> Germans are in the trench. They have
> — (012-038)

The broken sentence reflects the urgency of the moment — there is no time for complete thoughts when the enemy is in your trench. The [[battle|fight]]ing that followed was hand-to-hand:

> The Germans gave us a bomb or two and considerable rifle fire
> — (012-112)

## Prisoners and the Wounded

Holmes's descriptions of German prisoners are among the memoir's most human moments. Captured Germans are no longer the fearsome "[[boche|Hun]]" of propaganda but exhausted, frightened men:

> bringing in Germans stop and spend their own scant
> — (011-091)

The image of captors and captives, sharing a brief moment of rest during the chaotic withdrawal, underscores the common humanity that the circumstances of war could not entirely erase.

Walking through the aftermath of battle, Holmes records the grim landscape:

> were all over the place, both Germans and our own men. And in all
> — (009-151)

The dead of both sides lay together — a final, terrible equality. Holmes does not distinguish between "our" dead and "theirs" in this passage; the horror is universal.

## Equipment and Innovation

Holmes notes several areas where German military technology or practice was superior:

> The Germans, by the way, have had the "edge" on us in the matter of
> — (002-081)

He acknowledges German innovations in trench construction, [[sniper|sniping]] equipment, and defensive works. The German trench systems were often described as deeper, better reinforced, and more elaborately constructed than the British equivalents.

The distinctive German [[bayonet|saw-tooth bayonet]] was a particular object of fascination and dread:

> saw-tooth bayonets the Boche pioneers use
> — (013-161)

And Holmes notes with grim respect the German development of flamethrowers and [[gas-warfare|gas]] — weapons that the Allies would eventually adopt and deploy in turn.

## After the Withdrawal

One of the most striking passages describes the aftermath of a German withdrawal:

> The Germans had gone, leaving everything behind them
> — (007-163)

The abandoned German [[dug-out]]s, with their deeper construction and relative comfort, were a revelation to the occupying British troops. Holmes's description of exploring these captured positions reads almost like an archaeological account — exa[[mine-explosion|mining]] the material culture of an enemy who had, until hours before, been trying to kill him.
