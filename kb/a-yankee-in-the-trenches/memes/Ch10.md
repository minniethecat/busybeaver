[010-001] The [[tank|tanks]] passed beyond us and half-way up to the first line and

[010-002] stopped. Trapdoors in the decks opened, and the crews poured out

[010-003] and began to pile [[sandbag|sandbags]] in front of the machines so that when

[010-004] day broke fully and the mists lifted, the enemy could not see what

[010-005] had been brought up in the night.

[010-006] Day dawned, and a frisky little breeze from the west scattered the

[010-007] fog and swept the sky clean. There wasn't a cloud by eight o'clock.

[010-008] The sun shone bright, and we cursed it, for if it had been rainy

[010-009] the attack would not have been made.

[010-010] We made the usual last preparations that morning, such as writing

[010-011] letters and delivering farewell messages; and the latest rooks made

[010-012] their wills in the little blanks provided for the purpose in the

[010-013] back of the pay books. We judged from the number of dead and the

[010-014] evident punishment other divisions had taken there that the

[010-015] chances of coming back would be slim. Around nine o'clock Captain

[010-016] [[captain-green|Green]] gave us a little talk that confirmed our suspicions that the

[010-017] day was to be a hard one.

[010-018] He said, as nearly as I can remember:

[010-019] "Lads, I want to tell you that there is to be a most important

[010-020] battle--one of the most important in the whole war. [[high-wood|High Wood]] out

[010-021] there commands a view of the whole of this part of [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]] and is

[010-022] most valuable. There are estimated to be about ten thousand [[germans|Germans]]

[010-023] in that wood and in the surrounding supports. The positions are

[010-024] mostly of concrete with hundreds of [[machine-gun|machine guns]] and field

[010-025] artillery. Our heavies have for some reason made no impression on

[010-026] them, and regiment after regiment has attempted to take the woods

[010-027] and failed with heavy [[casualty|losses]]. Now it is up to the 47th Division to

[010-028] do the seemingly impossible. Zero is at eleven. We go over then.

[010-029] The best of luck and God bless you."

[010-030] We were all feeling pretty sour on the world when the sky pilot

[010-031] came along and cheered us up.

[010-032] He was a good little man, that chaplain, brave as they make 'em.

[010-033] He always went [[over-the-top|over the top]] with us and was in the thick of the

[010-034] fighting, and he had the military cross for bravery. He passed down

[010-035] the line, giving us a slap on the back or a hand grip and started

[010-036] us singing. No gospel hymns either, but any old rollicking,

[010-037] good-natured song that he happened to think of that would loosen

[010-038] things up and relieve the tension.

[010-039] Somehow he made you feel that you wouldn't mind going to hell if he

[010-040] was along, and you knew that he'd be willing to come if he could do

[010-041] any good. A good little man! Peace to his ashes.

[010-042] At ten o'clock things busted loose, and the most intense

[010-043] [[artillery|bombardment]] ever known in warfare up to that time began. Thousands

[010-044] of guns, both [[france|French]] and English, in fact every available gun

[010-045] within a radius of fifteen miles, poured it in. In the Bedlamitish

[010-046] din and roar it was impossible to hear the next man unless he put

[010-047] his mouth up close to your ear and yelled.

[010-048] My ear drums ached, and I thought I should go insane if the racket

[010-049] didn't stop. I was frightfully nervous and scared, but tried not

[010-050] to show it. An officer or a non-com must conceal his nervousness,

[010-051] though he be dying with fright.

[010-052] The faces of the men were hard-set and pale. Some of them looked

[010-053] positively green. They smoked fag after fag, lighting the new ones

[010-054] on the butts.

[010-055] All through the [[barrage|bombardment]] Fritz was comparatively quiet. He was

[010-056] saving all his for the time when we should come over. Probably,

[010-057] too, he was holed up to a large extent in his concrete dug-outs. I

[010-058] looked over the top once or twice and wondered if I, too, would be

[010-059] lying there unburied with the rats and maggots gnawing me into an

[010-060] unrecognizable mass. There were moments in that hour from ten to

[010-061] eleven when I was distinctly sorry for myself.

[010-062] The time, strangely enough, went fast--as it probably does with a

[010-063] condemned man in his last hour. At [[zero]] minus ten the word went

[010-064] down the line "Ten to go" and we got to the better positions of the

[010-065] trench and secured our footing on the side of the parapet to make

[010-066] our climb over when the signal came. Some of the men gave their

[010-067] [[bayonet|bayonets]] a last fond rub, and I looked to my bolt [[battle|action]] to see

[010-068] that it worked well. I had ten rounds in the magazine, and I didn't

[010-069] intend to rely too much on the bayonet. At a few seconds of eleven

[010-070] I looked at my wrist watch and was afflicted again with that empty

[010-071] feeling in the solar plexus. Then the whistles shrilled; I blew

[010-072] mine, and over we went.

[010-073] To a disinterested spectator who was far enough up in the air to be

[010-074] out of range it must have been a wonderful spectacle to see those

[010-075] thousands of men go over, wave after wave.

[010-076] The terrain was level out to the point where the little hill of

[010-077] High Wood rose covered with the splintered poles of what had once

[010-078] been a forest. This position and the supports to the left and rear

[010-079] of it began to fairly belch machine-gun and shell fire. If Fritz

[010-080] had been quiet before, he gave us all he had now.

[010-081] Our battalion went over from the second trench, and we got the

[010-082] cream of it.

[010-083] The tanks were just ahead of us and lumbered along in an imposing

[010-084] row. They lurched down into deep [[crater|craters]] and out again, tipped and

[010-085] reeled and listed, and sometimes seemed as though they must upset;

[010-086] but they came up each time and went on and on. And how slow they

[010-087] did seem to move! Lord, I thought we should never cover that five

[010-088] or six hundred yards.

[010-089] The tank machine guns were spitting fire over the heads of our

[010-090] first wave, and their Hotchkiss guns were rattling. A beautiful

[010-091] creeping barrage preceded us. Row after row of [[shell|shells]] burst at just

[010-092] the right distance ahead, spewing gobs of smoke and flashes of

[010-093] flame, made thin by the bright sunlight. Half a dozen airplanes

[010-094] circled like dragonflies up there in the blue.

[010-095] There was a tank just ahead of me. I got behind it. And marched

[010-096] there. Slow! God, how slow! Anyhow, it kept off the machine-gun

[010-097] bullets, but not, the shrapnel. It was breaking over us in clouds.

[010-098] I felt the stunning patter of the fragments on my [[helmet|tin hat]], cringed

[010-099] under it, and wondered vaguely why it didn't do me in.

[010-100] Men in the front wave were going down like tenpins. Off there

[010-101] diagonally to the right and forward I glimpsed a blinding burst,

[010-102] and as much as a whole platoon went down.

[010-103] Around me men were dropping all the time--men I knew. I saw Dolbsie

[010-104] clawing at his throat as he reeled forward, falling. I saw Vickers

[010-105] double up, drop his rifle, and somersault, hanging on to his

[010-106] abdomen.

[010-107] A hundred yards away, to the right, an officer walked backwards

[010-108] with an [[pistol|automatic pistol]] balanced on his finger, smiling, pulling

[010-109] his men along like a drum major. A shell or something hit him. He

[010-110] disappeared in a welter of blood and half a dozen of the front file

[010-111] fell with him.

[010-112] I thought we must be nearly there and sneaked a look around the

[010-113] edge of the tank. A traversing machine gun raked the mud, throwing

[010-114] up handfuls, and I heard the gruff "row, row" of flattened bullets

[010-115] as they ricocheted off the steel armor. I ducked back, and on we

[010-116] went.

[010-117] Slow! Slow! I found myself planning what I would do when I got to

[010-118] the front [[trench|trenches]]--if we ever did. There would be a grand rumpus,

[010-119] and I would [[click]] a dozen or more.

[010-120] And then we arrived.

[010-121] I don't suppose that trip across [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]] behind the tanks

[010-122] took over five minutes, but it seemed like an hour.

[010-123] At the end of it my participation in the battle of High Wood ended.

[010-124] No, I wasn't wounded. But when we reached [[boche|the Boche]] front trenches

[010-125] a strange thing happened. There was no fight worth mentioning. The

[010-126] tanks stopped over the trenches and blazed away right and left with

[010-127] their all-around traverse.

[010-128] A few Boches ran out and threw silly little bombs at the monsters.

[010-129] The tanks, noses in air, moved slowly on. And then the Graybacks

[010-130] swarmed up out of shelters and dug-outs, literally in hundreds, and

[010-131] held up their hands, whining "Mercy, kamarad."

[010-132] We took prisoners by [[platoon|platoons]]. [[lieutenant-blofeld|Blofeld]] grabbed me and turned over a

[010-133] gang of thirty to me. We searched them rapidly, cut their

[010-134] suspenders and belts, and I started to the rear with them. They

[010-135] seemed glad to go. So was I.

[010-136] As we hurried back over the five hundred yards that had been No

[010-137] Man's Land and was now British ground, I looked back and saw the

[010-138] irresistible tanks smashing their way through the tree stumps of

[010-139] High Wood, still spitting death and destruction in three

[010-140] directions.

[010-141] Going back we were under almost as heavy fire as we had been coming

[010-142] up. When we were about half-way across, [[shrapnel|shrapnel burst]] directly

[010-143] over our party and seven of the prisoners were killed and half a

[010-144] dozen wounded. I myself was unscratched. I stuck my hand inside my

[010-145] tunic and patted Dinky on the back, sent up a prayer for some more

[010-146] luck like that, and carried on.

[010-147] After getting my prisoners back to the rear, I came up again but

[010-148] couldn't find my battalion. I threw in with a battalion of

[010-149] Australians and was with them for twenty-four hours.

[010-150] When I found my chaps again, the battle of High Wood was pretty

[010-151] well over. Our company for some reason had suffered very few

[010-152] casualties, less than twenty-nine. Company B, however, had been

[010-153] practically wiped out, losing all but thirteen men out of two

[010-154] hundred. The other two companies had less than one hundred

[010-155] casualties. We had lost about a third of our strength. It is a

[010-156] living wonder to me that any of us came through.

[010-157] I don't believe any of us would have if it hadn't been for the

[010-158] tanks.

[010-159] The net result of the battle of High Wood was that our troops

[010-160] carried on for nearly two miles beyond the position to be taken.

[010-161] They had to fall back but held the wood and the heights. Three of

[010-162] the tanks were stalled in the farther edge of the woods--out of

[010-163] fuel--and remained there for three days unharmed under the fire of

[010-164] the German guns.

[010-165] Eventually some one ventured out and got some juice into them, and

[010-166] they returned to our lines. The tanks had proved themselves, not

[010-167] only as effective fighting machines, but as destroyers of German

[010-168] morale.
