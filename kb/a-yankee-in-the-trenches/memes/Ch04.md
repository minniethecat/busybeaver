[004-001] After the strafing we had given [[germans|Fritz]] on the [[trench-raid|raid]], he behaved

[004-002] himself reasonably well for quite a while. It was the first raid

[004-003] that had been made on that sector for a long time, and we had no

[004-004] doubt caught the Germans off their guard.

[004-005] Anyhow for quite a spell afterwards they were very "[[windy]]" and

[004-006] would send up the "Very" lights on the slightest provocation and

[004-007] start the "typewriters" a-rattling. Fritz was right on the job with

[004-008] his eye peeled all the time.

[004-009] In fact he was so keen that another raid that was attempted ten

[004-010] days later failed completely because of a rapidly concentrated and

[004-011] heavy [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]], and in another, a day or two later, our men

[004-012] never got beyond our own [[barbed-wire|wire]] and had thirty-eight [[casualty|casualties]] out

[004-013] of fifty men engaged.

[004-014] But so far as anything but defensive work was concerned, Fritz was

[004-015] very meek. He sent over very few "minnies" or rifle grenades, and

[004-016] there was hardly any [[shell|shelling]] of the sector.

[004-017] Directly after the raid, we who were in the party had a couple of

[004-018] days "on our own" at the little village of [[grenay|Bully-Grenay]], less than

[004-019] three miles behind the lines. This is directly opposite [[lens|Lens]], the

[004-020] better known town which figures so often in the dispatches.

[004-021] Bully-Grenay had been a place of perhaps one thousand people. It

[004-022] had been fought over and through and around early in the war, and

[004-023] was pretty well battered up. There were a few houses left unhit and

[004-024] the town hall and several shops. The rest of the place was ruins,

[004-025] but about two hundred of the inhabitants still stuck to their old

[004-026] homes. For some reason the Germans did not shell Bully-Grenay, that

[004-027] is, not often. Once in a while they would lob one in just to let

[004-028] the people know they were not forgotten.

[004-029] There was a suspicion that there were spies in the town and that

[004-030] that accounted for the Germans laying off, but whatever was the

[004-031] cause the place was safer than most villages so near the lines.

[004-032] Those two days in repose at Bully-Grenay were a good deal of a

[004-033] farce. We were entirely "on our own", it is true, no parade, no

[004-034] duty of any kind--but the quarters--oof! We were billeted in the

[004-035] cellars of the battered-down houses. They weren't shell-proof. That

[004-036] didn't matter much, as there wasn't any shelling, but there might

[004-037] have been. The cellars were dangerous enough without, what with

[004-038] tottering walls and overhanging chunks of masonry.

[004-039] Moreover they were a long way from waterproof. Imagine trying to

[004-040] find a place to sleep in an old ruin half full of rainwater. The

[004-041] dry places were piled up with brick and mortar, but we managed to

[004-042] clean up some half-sheltered spots for "kip" and we lived through

[004-043] it.

[004-044] The worst feature of these [[billet|billets]] was the rats. They were the

[004-045] biggest I ever saw, great, filthy, evil-smelling, grayish-red

[004-046] fellows, as big as a good-sized cat. They would hop out of the

[004-047] walls and scuttle across your face with their wet, cold feet, and

[004-048] it was enough to drive you insane. One chap in our party had a

[004-049] natural horror of rats, and he nearly went crazy. We had to "kip"

[004-050] with our greatcoats pulled up over our heads, and then the beggars

[004-051] would go down and nibble at our boots.

[004-052] The first day somebody found a fox terrier, evidently lost and

[004-053] probably the pet of some officer. We weren't allowed to carry

[004-054] mascots, although we had a kitten that we smuggled along for a long

[004-055] time. This terrier was a well-bred little fellow, and we grabbed

[004-056] him. We spent a good part of both mornings digging out rats for him

[004-057] and staged some of the grandest fights ever.

[004-058] Most of the day we spent at a little _estaminet_ across the way

[004-059] from our so-called [[billet|billets]]. There was a pretty mademoiselle there

[004-060] who served the rotten [[france|French]] beer and _vin blanc_, and the Tommies

[004-061] tried their French on her. They might as well have talked Choctaw.

[004-062] I speak the language a little and tried to monopolize the lady, and

[004-063] did, which didn't increase my popularity any.

[004-064] "I say, Yank," some one would call, "don't be a blinkin' 'og. Give

[004-065] somebody else a chawnce."

[004-066] Whereupon I would pursue my conquest all the more ardently. I was

[004-067] making a large hit, as I thought, when in came an officer. After

[004-068] that I was ignored, to the huge delight of the Tommies, who joshed

[004-069] me unmercifully. They discovered that my middle name was Derby, and

[004-070] they christened me "[[r-derby-holmes|Darby the Yank]]." Darby I remained as long as I

[004-071] was with them.

[004-072] Some of the questions the men asked about the States were certainly

[004-073] funny. One chap asked what language we spoke over here. I thought

[004-074] he was spoofing, but he actually meant it. He thought we spoke

[004-075] something like Italian, he said. I couldn't resist the temptation,

[004-076] and filled him up with a line of ghost stories about wild Indians

[004-077] just outside [[boston|Boston]]. I told him I left because of a raid in which

[004-078] the redskins scalped people on Boston Common. After that he used to

[004-079] pester the life out of me for Wild West yarns with the scenes laid

[004-080] in New [[blighty|England]].

[004-081] One chap was amazed and, I think, a little incredulous because I

[004-082] didn't know a man named Fisk in Des Moines.

[004-083] We went back to the [[trench|trenches]] again and were there five days. I was

[004-084] out one night on barbed wire work, which is dangerous at any time,

[004-085] and was especially so with Fritz in his condition of jumpy nerves.

[004-086] You have to do most of the work lying on your back in the mud, and

[004-087] if you jingle the wire, Fritz [[traverse|traverses]] [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]] with his

[004-088] rapid-firers with a fair chance of bagging something.

[004-089] I also had one night on [[patrol]], which later became my favorite

[004-090] game. I will tell more about it in another chapter.

[004-091] At the end of the five days the whole battalion was pulled out for

[004-092] rest. We marched a few miles to the rear and came to the village of

[004-093] Petite-Saens. This town had been fought through, but for some

[004-094] reason had suffered little. Few of the houses had been damaged, and

[004-095] we had real billets.

[004-096] My section, ten men besides myself, drew a big attic in a clean

[004-097] house. There was loads of room and the roof was tight and there

[004-098] were no rats. It was oriental luxury after Bully-Grenay and the

[004-099] trenches, and for a wonder nobody had a word of "grousing" over

[004-100] "[[kip|kipping]]" on the bare floor.

[004-101] The house was occupied by a very old peasant woman and a very

[004-102] little girl, three years old, and as pretty as a picture. The old

[004-103] woman looked ill and sad and very lonesome. One night as we sat in

[004-104] her kitchen drinking black coffee and cognac, I persuaded her to

[004-105] tell her story. It was, on the whole, rather a cruel thing to ask,

[004-106] I am afraid. It is only one of many such that I heard over there.

[004-107] France has, indeed, suffered. I set down here, as nearly as I can

[004-108] translate, what the old woman said:

[004-109] "Monsieur, I am very, very old now, almost eighty, but I am a

[004-110] patriot and I love my France. I do not complain that I have lost

[004-111] everything in this war. I do not care now, for I am old and it is

[004-112] for my country; but there is much sadness for me to remember, and

[004-113] it is with great bitterness that I think of the pig Allemand--beast

[004-114] that he is.

[004-115] "Two years ago I lived in this house, happy with my daughter and

[004-116] her husband and the little baby, and my husband, who worked in the

[004-117] mines. He was too old to [[battle|fight]], but when the great war came he

[004-118] tried to enlist, but they would not listen to him, and he returned

[004-119] to work, that the country should not be without coal.

[004-120] "The beau-fils (son-in-law), he enlisted and said good-by and went

[004-121] to the service.

[004-122] "By and by [[boche|the Boche]] come and in a great battle not far from this

[004-123] very house the beau-fils is wounded very badly and is brought to

[004-124] the house by comrades to die.

[004-125] "The Boche come into the village, but the beau-fils is too weak

[004-126] to go. The Boche come into the house, seize my daughter, and

[004-127] there--they--oh, monsieur--the things one may not say--and we so

[004-128] helpless.

[004-129] "Her father tries to protect her, but he is knocked down. I try,

[004-130] but they hold my feet over the fire until the very flesh cooks. See

[004-131] for yourselves the burns on my feet still.

[004-132] "My husband dies from the blow he gets, for he is very old, over

[004-133] ninety. Just then mon beau-fils sees a [[pistol|revolver]] that hangs by the

[004-134] side of the German officer, and putting all his strength together

[004-135] he leaps forward and grabs the revolver. And there he shoots the

[004-136] officer--and my poor little daughter--and then he says good-by and

[004-137] through the head sends a bullet.

[004-138] "The Germans did not touch me but once after that, and then they

[004-139] knocked me to the floor when they came after the pig officer. By

[004-140] and by come you English, and all is well for dear France once more;

[004-141] but I am very desolate now. I am alone but for the petite-fille

[004-142] (granddaughter), but I love the English, for they save my home and

[004-143] my dear country."

[004-144] I heard a good many stories of this kind off and on, but this

[004-145] particular one, I think, brought home, to me at least, the general

[004-146] beastliness of the Hun closer than ever before. We all loved our

[004-147] little kiddie very much, and when we saw the evidence of the

[004-148] terrible cruelties the poor old woman had suffered we saw red. Most

[004-149] of us cried a little. I think that that one story made each of us

[004-150] that heard it a mean, vicious fighter for the rest of our service.

[004-151] I know it did me.

[004-152] One of the first things a [[tommy|British soldier]] learns is to keep

[004-153] himself clean. He can't do it, and he's as filthy as a pig all the

[004-154] time he is in the trenches, but he tries. He is always shaving,

[004-155] even under fire, and show him running water and he goes to it like

[004-156] a duck.

[004-157] More than once I have shaved in a [[periscope|periscope mirror]] pegged into the

[004-158] side of a trench, with the bullets snapping overhead, and rubbed my

[004-159] face with wet tea leaves afterward to freshen up.

[004-160] Back in billets the very first thing that comes off is the big

[004-161] clean-up. Uniforms are brushed up, and equipment put in order. Then

[004-162] comes the bath, the most thorough possible under the conditions.

[004-163] After that comes the "[[cootie|cootie carnival]]", better known as the "shirt

[004-164] hunt." The cootie is the soldier's worst enemy. He's worse than the

[004-165] Hun. You can't get rid of him wherever you are, in the trenches or

[004-166] in billets, and he sticks closer than a brother. The cootie is a

[004-167] good deal of an acrobat. His policy of attack is to hang on to the

[004-168] shirt and to nibble at the occupant. Pull off the shirt and he

[004-169] comes with it. Hence the shirt hunt. [[british-army|Tommy]] gets out in the open

[004-170] somewhere so as not to shed his little companions indoors--there's

[004-171] always enough there anyhow--and he peels. Then he systematically

[004-172] runs down each seam--the cootie's favorite hiding place--catches

[004-173] the game, and ends his career by cracking him between the thumb

[004-174] nails.

[004-175] For some obscure psychological reason, Tommy seems to like company

[004-176] on one of these hunts. Perhaps it is because misery loves company,

[004-177] or it may be that he likes to compare notes on the catch. Anyhow,

[004-178] it is a common thing to see from a dozen to twenty soldiers with

[004-179] their shirts off, hunting cooties.

[004-180] "Hi sye, 'Arry," you'll hear some one sing out. "Look 'ere. Strike

[004-181] me bloomin' well pink but this one 'ere's got a black stripe along

[004-182] 'is back."

[004-183] Or, "If this don't look like the one I showed ye 'fore we went into

[004-184] the blinkin' line. 'Ow'd 'e git loose?"

[004-185] And then, as likely as not, a little farther away, behind the

[004-186] officers' quarters, you'll hear one say:

[004-187] "I say, old chap, it's deucedly peculiar I should have so many of

[004-188] the beastly things after putting on the Harrisons mothaw sent in

[004-189] the lawst parcel."

[004-190] The cootie isn't at all fastidious. He will bite the British

[004-191] aristocrat as soon as anybody else. He finds his way into all

[004-192] branches of the service, and I have even seen a dignified colonel

[004-193] wiggle his shoulders anxiously.

[004-194] Some of the cootie stories have become classical, like this one

[004-195] which was told from the North Sea to the Swiss border. It might

[004-196] have happened at that.

[004-197] A soldier was [[over-the-top|going over the top]] when one of his cootie friends bit

[004-198] him on the calf. The soldier reached down and captured the biter.

[004-199] Just as he stooped, a shell whizzed over where his head would have

[004-200] been if he had not gone after the cootie. Holding the captive

[004-201] between thumb and finger, he said:

[004-202] "Old feller, I cawn't give yer the Victoria Cross--but I can put

[004-203] yer back."

[004-204] And he did.

[004-205] The worst thing about the cootie is that there is no remedy for

[004-206] him. The shirt hunt is the only effective way for the soldier to

[004-207] get rid of his bosom friends. The various dopes and patent

[004-208] preparations guaranteed as "good for cooties" are just that. They

[004-209] give 'em an appetite.
