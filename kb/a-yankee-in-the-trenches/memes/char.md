# Char

## Overview

"Char" was the [[tommy|British soldier]]'s word for tea — and tea was, by universal agreement, the most important substance in the [[trench|trenches]] after ammunition. Derived from the Hindustani *cha* (brought into British military slang through long service in India), "char" captured the particular reverence that [[tommy|Tommy]] held for the dark, strong, sweetened brew that sustained him through cold nights, long watches, and the aftermath of battle. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, char appears as a constant presence — the first thing made upon settling into a position, the comfort offered to the [[casualty|wounded]], and the ritual that marked the beginning and end of every day.

## The Ritual

Holmes describes the first thing soldiers did upon stopping — even before establishing defensive positions:

> We slung together a dixie of char
> — (002-020)

A "dixie" was a large iron cooking pot, and "slinging together" a dixie of char meant boiling water over whatever fuel was available — a charcoal brazier, a scrap of wood, a candle stub — and adding tea leaves to produce the brew that was the foundation of [[tommy|Tommy]]'s morale.

The scene Holmes paints is one of the memoir's few moments of genuine warmth:

> There was a charcoal brazier going in the middle of the dug-out, and mess tins of char boiling away. Everybody was smoking, and the air was thick with tobacco smoke
> — (002-115)–(002-116)

Char and tobacco — the twin pillars of trench comfort. The [[dug-out]], for a brief moment, became almost [[blighty|home]]like.

## Char and Food

Char was the essential accompaniment to every meal, however meager. Holmes records the routine:

> we made "char", which is tea, and had our bully beef and biscuits
> — (002-008)

Bully beef (tinned [[bully-beef|corned beef]]) and hard biscuits, washed down with char — this was the [[tommy|Tommy]]'s standard meal, repeated day after day, week after week, until the very thought of it became nauseating. But the char at least was hot, and hot liquid in a cold, wet trench was a comfort that no quantity of food could equal.

When [[trench-rations|rations]] arrived from the rear, char was the first priority:

> the char and bacon from the field kitchen came up
> — (005-026)

The arrival of the char dixie from the company cookers was a daily highlight — a sign that somewhere behind the lines, normal life continued, and that the army remembered its men in the front line.

## Etymology and Culture

The word "char" is one of many Hindustani-derived terms in British military slang, a legacy of the Army's long presence in India. Like "[[blighty|Blighty]]" (from *vilayati*), "char" had crossed from the colonial army into the mass citizen army of the Great War. It was part of a rich linguistic tapestry that included words like "dixie" (cooking pot), "[[uniform|khaki]]" (uniform color), and "pukka" (genuine).

A "char wallah" was the man responsible for making and serving tea — a role of immense importance in any unit. The char wallah might be an officially designated cook or simply the man in the platoon who was best at scrounging tea leaves and keeping the dixie clean. In either case, he held a position of informal but real authority.

## Comfort and Survival

Char was more than a beverage — it was a psychological necessity. After a [[artillery|bombardment]], after a [[trench-raid|raid]], after the death of a friend, someone would put on a dixie of char. The act of making it, the ritual of drinking it, the warmth of it in the stomach — these were small anchors of normality in a world gone mad.

Holmes does not dwell on char at length — it was too ordinary, too much a part of daily life to merit extended description. But its constant, quiet presence throughout the memoir is itself a testament to its importance. Where there was char, there was still life, still community, still something worth [[battle|fight]]ing for.
