# Trench Raid

## Overview

The [[raid|trench raid]] was a specialized [[battle|combat]] operation in World War I: a small party of soldiers would stealthily cross [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] under cover of darkness, penetrate the enemy's [[trench|trenches]], and attempt to capture prisoners, gather intelligence, destroy positions, or simply inflict [[casualty|casualties]] before retreating. [[raid|Raid]]s were high-risk, high-adrenaline missions that combined elements of stealth, close-[[billets|quarters]] [[battle|combat]], and rapid extr[[battle|action]].

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] participated in at least one major raid and described it in visceral detail. His account captures both the tactical mechanics and the raw terror of these ope[[trench-rations|rations]].

## In the Memoir

Holmes was part of a [[raid|raiding party]] organized by an officer named [[lieutenant-blofeld|Blofeld]]. The tactical objective was clear:

> The object of a raid is to get into the enemy's trenches by stealth
> — (003-007)

Preparation involved special training behind the lines:

> the members of the raiding party were taken back a mile or so to the rear and were given instruction and rehearsal. This was the first raid that "Batt" had ever tried, and the staff was anxious to have it a success.
> — (003-001)–(003-004)

There were fifty men in the party, and the group practiced their movements until they could execute them blindfolded. The raid itself was brief — Holmes records that they spent just over three minutes inside the German [[trench|trenches]] — but the intensity of those moments was staggering.

## Raid Tactics

[[raid|Raid]]s typically involved crossing the narrow strip of [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] (often only 120 yards wide at Holmes's position) under cover of darkness or a diversionary [[artillery|bombardment]]. Soldiers carried specialized close-[[battle|combat]] equipment — Holmes mentions [[persuader|persuader stick]]s ([[persuader|trench club]]s) and [[grenade|Mills bomb]]s ([[mills-bomb|hand grenade]]s) as standard [[raid|raiding]] tools.

The [[raiding-party|raiders]] had to distinguish friend from foe in the darkness of the enemy trench, a difficulty Holmes notes:

> distinguish your own men when you get to the Boche trench
> — (003-029)

Speed was everything. The longer a [[raid|raiding party]] stayed in the enemy [[trench|trenches]], the greater the risk of counterattack or being cut off from their own lines.

## Aftermath

The psychological relief of returning safely was immense:

> Did that muddy old trench look good when we tumbled in? Oh, Boy!
> — (003-149)

[[raid|Raid]]s served purposes beyond their immediate tactical objectives: they maintained an offensive spirit among the troops, gathered vital intelligence, and denied the enemy the comfort of an uncontested front.
