## Overview

The **[[grenade|Mills bomb]]** (officially the No. 5 grenade) was the standard British hand grenade of World War I, introduced in 1915 and manufactured by the millions throughout the conflict. Its distinctive serrated cast-iron body—designed to fragment into lethal shrapnel upon detonation—made it one of the most recognizable weapons of the war. The [[grenade|Mills bomb]] became the infantryman's primary close-[[battle|combat]] weapon for [[trench-raid|trench raids]], [[bombing-party|bombing parties]], and clearing [[dug-out|dugout]]s, fundamentally shaping the tactics of [[trench|trench warfare]].

## In the Trenches

In the confined spaces of the [[france|Western Front]], where rifle fire was often impractical and [[bayonet]] charges suicidal, the hand grenade became the weapon of choice for close-[[billets|quarters]] [[battle|fight]]ing. A [[grenade|bomb]]ing party—a squad of men armed primarily with [[grenade|Mills bomb]]s—would advance along an enemy trench, lobbing grenades ahead of them into each [[traverse]] and [[dug-out]] before rounding the corner. The [[grenade|Mills bomb]]'s timed fuse (approximately four seconds after releasing the safety lever) required steady nerves and precise judgment: throw too soon and the enemy might throw it back; hold too long and it would detonate in the thrower's hand.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes references the [[grenade|Mills bomb]] in the context of the arms carried on a [[raid|trench raid]]—alongside [[rifle|rifles]], [[bayonet|bayonets]], and specialized close-[[battle|combat]] weapons. The grenade was standard issue for any soldier participating in a [[trench-raid|raid]] or [[grenade|bomb]]ing [[battle|action]], and proficiency in its use was part of the infantryman's basic training. In the [[gas-warfare|gas]]-saturated environment of the [[trench|trenches]], the [[grenade|Mills bomb]] was one of the few weapons whose effectiveness was entirely unaffected by weather, [[gas-warfare|gas]], or mud—it remained lethal in all conditions.

## Military Context

William [[grenade|Mills]], a British engineer, designed the grenade in 1915 to address the shortcomings of earlier British grenades, which were unreliable and dangerous to the user. The [[grenade|Mills bomb]]'s key innovation was the "automatic" ignition system: pulling the pin released a spring-loaded striker that ignited the fuse only when the safety lever was released (i.e., when thrown), making it far safer to handle than its predecessors. The serrated exterior was intended to produce uniform fragmentation, though in practice the cast iron often shattered unpredictably. Over 75 million [[grenade|Mills bomb]]s were produced during the war, and variants remained in British service through the Korean War. The term "[[grenade|Mills bomb]]" became so ubiquitous in the [[tommy]]'s vocabulary that it was often used generically for any hand grenade, British or otherwise.
