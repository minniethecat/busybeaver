## Overview

Stretcher bearers were the first link in the medical evacuation chain of World War I. Unarmed or lightly armed, they ventured into [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] and across fire-swept ground to recover the [[casualty|wounded]], often under the same [[shell|shelling]] and [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]] that had struck down the men they came to save. Holmes opens his memoir with a stretcher bearer's question — "Hi sye, Yank. Wot th' bloody 'ell are you in this bloomin' row for?" (001-003) — establishing the stretcher bearer as both a witness to the carnage and a representative of the cockney soldier's dark humor. Later, Holmes himself becomes a casualty dependent on stretcher bearers, and his account captures both their bravery and their vulnerability.

## In the Trenches

Holmes's narrative opens with a stretcher bearer: [[casualty|wounded]] on [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]], "being carried back somewhat disfigured but still in the ring," he is addressed by a cockney stretcher bearer who demands to know why an American is [[battle|fight]]ing in a British war (001-003). The stretcher bearer's question frames the entire memoir — it is the question Holmes spends the book trying to answer.

The stretcher bearer's role was physically demanding and psychologically grueling. Carrying a [[casualty|wounded]] man on a canvas stretcher across shell-torn ground required strength and endurance; doing so under fire required a particular kind of courage. Stretcher bearers worked in pairs, and their burden — a full-grown man plus equipment — could exceed two [[hun]]dred pounds. The stretcher itself was a simple canvas affair suspended between two poles, offering no protection to either bearer or patient.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes provides a harrowing account of being [[casualty|wounded]] and carried by stretcher bearers during his final [[battle|action]]. Hit by [[shrapnel|shell fragments]], he lost consciousness repeatedly as he was carried: "The next thing I remember I was on a stretcher. The beastly thing swayed and pitched, and I got seasick." (014-118) The journey was interrupted by another shell burst: "Then came another crash directly over head, and out I went again. When I came to, my head was as clear as a bell. A shell had burst over us and had [[casualty|killed]] one stretcher bearer. The other had di[[mine-explosion|sap]]peared." (014-118)

This passage is remarkable for its understatement. A stretcher bearer is [[casualty|killed]] while carrying Holmes, the other vanishes — and Holmes, regaining consciousness, simply gets to his feet with his [[casualty|wounded]] comrade Smith and staggers onward. The stretcher bearer who died carrying him is mentioned only in passing, a silent casualty of a role that demanded men risk their lives for the [[casualty|wounded]] without weapons, without glory, and often without recognition.

## Military Context

In the [[british-army|British Army]] of World War I, stretcher bearers were typically drawn from the Royal Army Medical Corps (RAMC) or were regimental stretcher bearers assigned from within [[battalion|infantry battalion]]s. Regimental stretcher bearers received basic first-aid training and were responsible for evacuating [[casualty|casualties]] from the front line to the Regimental Aid Post (RAP). From there, RAMC stretcher bearers carried the [[casualty|wounded]] further back through the evacuation chain: Advanced [[dressing-station|Dressing Station]], [[casualty|Casualty]] Clearing Station, and eventually [[hospital|base hospital]]s.

The mortality rate among stretcher bearers was notoriously high. They were targeted by [[sniper|snipers]] and shellfire, as the enemy recognized that killing stretcher bearers disrupted the enemy's ability to recover [[casualty|wounded]] soldiers — men who might otherwise return to the [[battle|fight]]. The Geneva Convention protected medical personnel, but in the chaos of [[trench|trench warfare]] such protections were frequently ignored. Stretcher bearers operated primarily at night to reduce exposure, but emergencies demanded daylight evacuations regardless of risk.
