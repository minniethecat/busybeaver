# Blighty Wound

## Overview

A "Blighty wound" was, in the grim calculus of trench life, the best outcome a soldier could reasonably hope for: an injury serious enough to require evacuation to [[england|England]] ([[blighty|Blighty]]) but not so serious as to be permanently disabling or fatal. The term captures the desperate pragmatism of men who had come to see a non-fatal wound as a form of salvation.

The concept appears repeatedly in [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir, always with the same ambivalent tone — part longing, part dark humor. Soldiers openly discussed and even envied those who received Blighty wounds.

## In the Memoir

Holmes introduces the term early, during training in [[england|England]]:

> their last look at Blighty, and they take it rather solemnly
> — (001-193)

The soldiers departing for [[france|France]] knew they might not see [[england|England]] again — except as [[casualty|casualties]]. The term "[[blighty|Blighty]]" itself was trench slang for [[england|England]] or [[blighty|home]], and a wound that sent you there was the closest thing to a prize the [[trench|trenches]] offered.

Holmes describes the envy that accompanied a successful Blighty wound:

> one chap who had been trying to get a "cushy" one, as a blighty wound is called, offered his pal a fancy price for the one that had got him a trip to Blighty.
> — (002-169)

After a [[trench-raid|raid]], Holmes notes matter-of-factly:

> Jerry got Blighty on his wound
> — (003-146)

The casual tone underscores how normalized this calculus had become — a wound was evaluated not by its severity but by its destination.

## Trench Culture

The Blighty wound occupied a unique place in trench folklore. The song "Take Me Back to Dear Old [[blighty|Blighty]]" was a popular [[marching]] tune, and Holmes quotes its lyrics in full:

> Tyke me back to dear old Blighty,
> Put me on the train for London town
> — (002-135)

The Blighty wound was the physical manifestation of the song's plea — a real, if painful, path [[blighty|home]]. Soldiers distinguished between a "cushy" [[blighty|Blighty]] (a mild wound that still qualified for evacuation) and more serious injuries. The ideal was painful enough to get you out, but not so bad you'd regret it for life.
