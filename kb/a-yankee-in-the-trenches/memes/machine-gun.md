# Machine Gun

## Overview

The machine gun was the defining weapon of positional warfare on the [[france|Western Front]]. Capable of firing [[hun]]dreds of rounds per minute, a single well-placed machine gun could halt an infantry assault, sweep a stretch of [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] clear of life, and render the most carefully planned attack futile. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir, the machine gun appears as an inst[[rum-ration|rum]]ent of near-supernatural lethality — the weapon that turned every exposure to open ground into a dance with death.

## The German Guns

Holmes's most vivid encounter with machine guns came during a [[raid|trench raid]], when the German defenders brought their weapons to bear:

> They must have turned twenty machine guns on us, or at least that's what it sounded like
> — (003-076)

The hyperbole — "twenty machine guns" — captures the overwhelming sensory experience of automatic fire. In the darkness of [[no-mans-land|no man's land]], the distinctive rattle of the German MG 08 (a Maxim derivative) was a sound of pure terror.

The response was immediate:

> When the machine guns started, we charged. Jerry and I were ahead as bayonet men
> — (003-080)

The instinct was to close the distance — to get inside the arc of fire before the gunners could adjust their aim. It was a desperate tactic, but in the close [[billets|quarters]] of a [[trench-raid|raid]], sometimes it worked.

## In the Attack

Going [[over-the-top|over the top]] meant advancing directly into the teeth of the machine guns. Holmes's description of the assault on [[high-wood|High Wood]] makes clear the devastating effect:

> Our battalion went over from the second trench. The tanks were just ahead of us
> — (010-081)–(010-083)

Even with [[tank|tanks]] providing cover, the machine guns took their toll. Holmes notes that the [[tank|tanks]] had their own machine guns, firing back at the German positions:

> The tank machine guns were spitting fire over the top of me
> — (010-089)

The exchange of machine-gun fire between the [[tank|tanks]] and the German positions created a lethal crossfire through which the infantry had to advance.

Holmes describes the concrete fortifications that housed the German [[artillery|guns]]:

> a maze of concrete with hundreds of machine guns and field pieces
> — (010-024)

"[[boche|Hun]]dreds" is exaggeration born of experience — to the infantryman under fire, the number of enemy machine guns always seemed infinite.

## Counter-Measures

The [[raid|raiding party]] Holmes joined carried specialized equipment to deal with machine guns:

> We had found two more machine guns and were planting Stokeses under them
> — (003-130)

"[[stokes-mortar|Stokeses]]" — [[stokes-mortar|Stokes]] [[stokes-mortar|trench mortar]]s — were one of the few infantry weapons capable of knocking out a machine-gun position. The [[grenade|bomb]]ers would work their way close and drop a mortar [[grenade|bomb]] into the gun emplacement, silencing it before it could do more damage.

Holmes also notes the work of the battalion's own machine gunners, who provided covering fire:

> the machine gunners, who immediately cut loose with everything they had
> — (007-033)

The machine gun was a weapon used by both sides with equal proficiency. The difference was often not the weapon but the position — a well-sited machine gun with a clear field of fire was worth a company of riflemen.

## Legacy

The machine gun, more than any other weapon, shaped the tactical reality of World War I. It forced armies underground into [[trench|trenches]], made cavalry charges obsolete, and ensured that the infantry assault — the basis of military doctrine for centuries — would never again be the same. Holmes's memoir is a testament to the machine gun's fearsome effectiveness, recorded from the perspective of a man who had to advance into its fire and survive.
