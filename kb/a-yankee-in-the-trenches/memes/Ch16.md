[016-001] I cannot end this book without saying something to those who have

[016-002] boys over there and, what is more to the point, to those boys who

[016-003] may go over there.

[016-004] First as to the things that should be sent in parcels; and a great

[016-005] deal of consideration should be given to this. You must be very

[016-006] careful not to send things that will load your Sammy down, as every

[016-007] ounce counts in the pack when he is hiking, and he is likely to be

[016-008] hiking any time or all the time.

[016-009] In the line of eatables the soldier wants something sweet. Good

[016-010] hard cookies are all right. I wish more people in this country knew

[016-011] how to make the English plum pudding in bags, the kind that will

[016-012] keep forever and be good when it is boiled. Mainly, though,

[016-013] chocolate is the thing. The milk kind is well enough, but it is apt

[016-014] to cause overmuch thirst. Personally I would rather have the plain

[016-015] chocolate,--the water variety.

[016-016] Chewing gum is always in demand and is not bulky in the package.

[016-017] Send a lot of it. Lime and lemon tablets in the summertime are

[016-018] great for checking thirst on the [[marching|march]]. A few of them won't do any

[016-019] harm in any parcel, summer or winter.

[016-020] Now about smoking materials. Unless the man to whom the parcel is

[016-021] to be sent is definitely known to be prejudiced against cigarettes,

[016-022] don't send him pipe tobacco or a pipe. There are smokers who hate

[016-023] cigarettes just as there are some people who think that the little

[016-024] paper roll is an invention of the devil. If any one has a boy over

[016-025] there, he--or she--had better overcome any possible personal

[016-026] feeling against the use of cigarettes and send them in preference

[016-027] to anything else.

[016-028] From my own experience I know that cigarettes are the most

[016-029] important thing that can be sent to a soldier. When I went out

[016-030] there, I was a pipe smoker. After I had been in the [[trench|trenches]] a week

[016-031] I quit the pipe and threw it away. It is seldom enough that one has

[016-032] the opportunity to enjoy a full pipe. It is very hard to get

[016-033] lighted when the matches are wet in bad weather, which is nearly

[016-034] always. Besides which, say what you will, a pipe does not soothe

[016-035] the nerves as a fag does.

[016-036] Now when sending the cigarettes out, don't try to think of the

[016-037] special brand that Harold or Percival used when he was [[blighty|home]]. Likely

[016-038] enough his name has changed, and instead of being Percy or Harold

[016-039] he is now Pigeye or Sour-belly; and his taste in the weed has

[016-040] changed too. He won't be so keen on his own particular brand of

[016-041] Turkish. Just send him the common or garden Virginia sort at five

[016-042] cents the package. That is the kind that gives most comfort to the

[016-043] outworn [[british-army|Tommy]] or Sammy.

[016-044] Don't think that you can send too many. I have had five hundred

[016-045] sent to me in a week many times and have none left at the end.

[016-046] There are always men who do not get any parcels, and they have to

[016-047] be looked out for. Out there all things are common property, and

[016-048] the soldier shares his last with his less fortunate comrade.

[016-049] Subscribe when you get the chance to any and all smoke funds.

[016-050] Don't listen to the pestilential fuddy-duds who do not approve of

[016-051] tobacco, particularly the fussy-old-maids. Personally, when I hear

[016-052] any of these conscientious objectors to My Lady Nicotine air their

[016-053] opinions, I wish that they could be placed in the trenches for a

[016-054] while. They would soon change their minds about [[rum-ration|rum]] issues and

[016-055] tobacco, and I'll wager they would be first in the line when the

[016-056] issues came around.

[016-057] One thing that many people forget to put in the soldier's parcel,

[016-058] or don't see the point of, is talcum powder. Razors get dull very

[016-059] quickly, and the face gets sore. The powder is almost a necessity

[016-060] when one is shaving in luke-warm tea and laundry soap, with a

[016-061] safety razor blade that wasn't sharp in the first place. In the

[016-062] summer on the march men sweat and accumulate all the dirt there is

[016-063] in the world. There are forty hitherto unsuspected places on the

[016-064] body that chafe under the weight of equipment. Talc helps. In the

[016-065] matter of sore feet, it is a life saver.

[016-066] Soap,--don't forget that. Always some good, pure, plain white

[016-067] soap, like Ivory or Castile; and a small bath towel now and then.

[016-068] There is so little chance to wash towels that they soon get

[016-069] unusable.

[016-070] In the way of wearing apparel, socks are always good. But, girlie,

[016-071] make 'em right. That last pair sent me nearly cost me a court

[016-072] martial by my getting my feet into trench-foot condition. If you

[016-073] can't leave out the seams, wear them yourself for a while, and see

[016-074] how you like it.

[016-075] Sleeveless sweaters are good and easy to make, I am told. They

[016-076] don't last long at the best, so should not be elaborate. Any

[016-077] garment worn close to the body gets cooty in a few weeks and has to

[016-078] be ditched. However, keep right on with the knitting, with the

[016-079] exception of the socks. If you're not an expert on those, better

[016-080] buy them. You may in that way retain the affection of your

[016-081] sweetheart over there.

[016-082] Knitted helmets are a great comfort. I had one that was fine not

[016-083] only to wear under the [[helmet|tin hat]] but to sleep in. I am not keen on

[016-084] wristlets or gloves. Better buy the gloves you send in the shops.

[016-085] So that's the knitted stuff,--helmets, sweaters, and mufflers and,

[016-086] for the expert, socks.

[016-087] Be very moderate in the matter of reading matter. I mean by that,

[016-088] don't send a lot at a time or any very bulky stuff at all.

[016-089] If it is possible to get a louse pomade called Harrison's in this

[016-090] country, send it, as it is a cooty killer. So far as I know, it is

[016-091] the only thing sold that will do the cooty in. There's a fortune

[016-092] waiting for the one who compounds a louse eradicator that will kill

[016-093] the cooty and not irritate or nearly kill the one who uses it. I

[016-094] shall expect a royalty from the successful chemist who produces the

[016-095] much needed compound.

[016-096] For the wealthier people, I would suggest that good things to send

[016-097] are silk shirts and drawers. It is possible to get the [[cootie|cooties]] out

[016-098] of these garments much easier than out of the thick woollies. There

[016-099] are many other things that may be sent, but I have mentioned the

[016-100] most important. The main thing to remember is not to run to bulk.

[016-101] And don't forget that it takes a long time for stuff to get

[016-102] across.

[016-103] Don't overlook the letters,--this especially if you are a mother,

[016-104] wife, or sweetheart. It is an easy thing to forget. You mustn't.

[016-105] Out there life is chiefly squalor, filth, and stench. The boy gets

[016-106] disgusted and lonesome and homesick, even though he may write to

[016-107] the contrary. Write to him at least three times a week. Always

[016-108] write cheerfully, even although something may have happened that

[016-109] has plunged you into the depths of despair. If it is necessary to

[016-110] cover up something that would cause a soldier worry, cover it up.

[016-111] Even lie to him. It will be justified. Keep in mind the now famous,

[016-112] war song, "Pack up your troubles in your old [[equipment|kit]] bag and smile,

[016-113] smile, smile." Keep your own packed up and don't send any over

[016-114] there for some soldier to worry over.

[016-115] Just a few words to the men themselves who may go. Don't take

[016-116] elaborate shaving tackle, just brush, razor, soap, and a small

[016-117] mirror. Most of the time you won't need the mirror. You'll use the

[016-118] [[periscope|periscope mirror]] in the trenches. Don't load up on books and

[016-119] unnecessary clothing. Impress it upon your relatives that your

[016-120] stuff, tobacco and sweets, is to come along in small parcels and

[016-121] often and regularly. Let all your friends and relatives know your

[016-122] address and ask them to write often. Don't hesitate to tell them

[016-123] all that a parcel now and again will be acceptable. Have more than

[016-124] one source of supply if possible.

[016-125] When you get out there, hunt up the [[ymca|Y.M.C.A.]] huts. You will find

[016-126] good cheer, warmth, music, and above all a place to do your

[016-127] writing. Write home often. Your people are concerned about you all

[016-128] the time. Write at least once a week to the one nearest and dearest

[016-129] to you. I used to average ten letters a week to friends in [[england|Blighty]]

[016-130] and back here, and that was a lot more than I was allowed. I found

[016-131] a way. Most of you won't be able to go over your allowance. But do

[016-132] go the limit.

[016-133] Over there you will find a lot of attractive girls and women. Most

[016-134] any girl is attractive when you are just out of the misery of the

[016-135] trenches. Be careful of them. Remember the country has been full of

[016-136] soldiers for three years. Don't make love too easily. One of the

[016-137] singers in the [[divisional-follies|Divisional Follies]] recently revived the once popular

[016-138] music-hall song, "If You Can't Be Good Be Careful." It should

[016-139] appeal to the soldier as much as "Smile, smile, smile", and is

[016-140] equally good advice. For the sake of those at home and for the sake

[016-141] of your own peace of mind come back from overseas clean.

[016-142] After all it is possible to no more than give hints to the boys who

[016-143] are going. All of you will have to learn by experience. My parting

[016-144] word to you all is just, "The best of luck."

[016-145] GLOSSARY OF ARMY SLANG

[016-146] All around traverse - A machine gun placed on a swivel to turn

[016-147] in any direction.

[016-148] Ammo - Ammunition. Usually for [[rifle|rifles]], though occasionally used

[016-149] to indicate that for artillery.

[016-150] Argue the toss - Argue the point.

[016-151] Back of the line - Anywhere to the rear and out of the danger

[016-152] zone.

[016-153] Barbed [[barbed-wire|wire]] - Ordinary barbed wire used for entanglements. A

[016-154] thicker and heavier military wire is sometimes used.

[016-155] [[barrage|Barrage]] - Shells dropped simultaneously and in a row so as to

[016-156] form a curtain of fire. Literal translation "a barrier."

[016-157] Bashed - Smashed.

[016-158] Big boys - Big [[artillery|guns]] or the [[shell|shells]] they send over.

[016-159] Big push - The [[battle|battles]] of [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]].

[016-160] [[billets|Billets]] - The quarters of the soldier when back of the line.

[016-161] Any place from a pigpen to a palace.

[016-162] Bleeder or Blighter - Cockney slang for fellow. Roughly

[016-163] corresponding to American "guy."

[016-164] Blighty - England. East Indian derivation. The paradise looked

[016-165] forward to by all good soldiers,--and all bad ones too.

[016-166] [[blighty-wound|Blighty one]] - A wound that will take the soldier to Blighty.

[016-167] Bloody - The universal Cockney adjective. It is vaguely

[016-168] supposed to be highly obscene, though just why nobody seems to

[016-169] know.

[016-170] Blooming - A meaningless and greatly used adjective. Applied to

[016-171] anything and everything.

[016-172] Bomb - A [[mills-bomb|hand grenade]].

[016-173] Bully beef - Corned beef, high grade and good of the kind, if

[016-174] you like the kind. It sets hard on the chest.

[016-175] Carry on - To go ahead with the matter in hand.

[016-176] [[char|Char]] - Tea. East Indian derivation.

[016-177] [[chat|Chat]] - Officers' term for cootie; supposed to be more delicate.

[016-178] Click - Variously used. To die. To be [[casualty|killed]]. To kill. To draw

[016-179] some disagreeable job, as: I clicked a [[trench-fatigue|burial fatigue]].

[016-180] Communication trench - A trench leading up to the front trench.

[016-181] Consolidate - To turn around and prepare for occupation a

[016-182] captured trench.

[016-183] Cootie - The common,--the too common,--body louse. Everybody

[016-184] has 'em.

[016-185] Crater - A round pit made by an underground explosion or by a

[016-186] shell.

[016-187] Cushy - Easy. Soft.

[016-188] [[dixie|Dixie]] - An oblong iron pot or box fitting into a field kitchen.

[016-189] Used for cooking anything and everything. Nobody seems to know why

[016-190] it is so called.

[016-191] Doggo - Still. Quiet. East Indian derivation.

[016-192] Doing in - Killing.

[016-193] Doss - Sleep.

[016-194] Duck walk - A slatted wooden walk in soft ground.

[016-195] Dud - An unexploded shell. A dangerous thing to fool with.

[016-196] [[dug-out|Dug-out]] - A hole more or less deep in the side of a trench

[016-197] where soldiers are supposed to rest.

[016-198] Dump - A place where supplies are left for distribution.

[016-199] Entrenching tool - A sort of small shovel for quick digging.

[016-200] Carried as part of equipment.

[016-201] [[estaminet|Estaminet]] - A [[france|French]] saloon or cafe.

[016-202] [[fag|Fag]] - A cigarette.

[016-203] Fatigue - Any kind of work except manning the trenches.

[016-204] Fed up - [[tommy|Tommy]]'s way of saying "too much is enough."

[016-205] Firing step - A narrow ledge running along the parapet on which

[016-206] a soldier stands to look [[over-the-top|over the top]].

[016-207] Flare - A star light sent up from a [[revolver|pistol]] to light up out in

[016-208] front.

[016-209] [[germans|Fritz]] - An affectionate term for our friend the enemy.

[016-210] Funk hole - A [[dug-out]].

[016-211] Gas - Any poisonous [[gas-warfare|gas]] sent across when the wind is right.

[016-212] Used by both sides. Invented by the Germans.

[016-213] Goggles - A piece of equipment similar to that used by

[016-214] motorists, supposed to keep off tear [[gas-weapon|gas]]. The rims are backed with

[016-215] strips of sponge which Tommy tears off and throws the goggle frame

[016-216] away.

[016-217] Go west - To die.

[016-218] Grouse - Complain. Growl. Kick.

[016-219] [[boche|Hun]] - A German.

[016-220] Identification disc - A fiber tablet bearing the soldier's

[016-221] name, regiment, and rank. Worn around the neck on a string.

[016-222] Iron [[trench-rations|rations]] - [[About]] two pounds of nonperishable rations to be

[016-223] used in an emergency.

[016-224] Knuckle knife - A short dagger with a studded hilt. Invented by

[016-225] the Germans.

[016-226] Lance Corporal - The lowest grade of non-commissioned officer.

[016-227] [[machine-gun|Lewis gun]] - A very light machine gun invented by one Lewis, an

[016-228] officer in the American army.

[016-229] Light railway - A very narrow-gauge railway on which are pushed

[016-230] little hand cars.

[016-231] Listening post - One or more men go out in front, at night, of

[016-232] course, and listen for movements by the enemy.

[016-233] Maconochie - A scientifically compounded and well-balanced

[016-234] ration, so the authorities say. It looks, smells, and tastes like

[016-235] rancid lard.

[016-236] M.O. - Medical Officer. A foxy cove who can't be fooled with

[016-237] faked symptoms.

[016-238] Mess tin - A combination teapot, fry pan, and plate.

[016-239] Military cross - An officer's decoration for bravery.

[016-240] Military medal - A decoration for bravery given to enlisted

[016-241] men.

[016-242] [[grenade|Mills]] - The most commonly used hand grenade.

[016-243] [[minnies|Minnies]] - German [[stokes-mortar|trench mortar]] projectiles.

[016-244] Napper - The head.

[016-245] Night 'ops - A much hated practice manoeuvre done at night.

[016-246] [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]] - The area between the trenches.

[016-247] On your own - At liberty. Your time is your own.

[016-248] Out or over there - Somewhere in France.

[016-249] Parados - The back wall of a trench.

[016-250] [[parapet|Parapet]] - The front wall of a trench.

[016-251] [[patrol|Patrol]] - One or more men who go out in front and prowl in the

[016-252] dark, seeking information of the enemy.

[016-253] Periscope - A boxlike arrangement with two mirrors for looking

[016-254] over the top without exposing the napper.

[016-255] [[persuader|Persuader]] - A short club with a nail-studded head.

[016-256] Pip squeak - A German shell which makes that kind of noise when

[016-257] it comes over.

[016-258] Push up the daisies - To be killed and buried.

[016-259] Ration party - A party of men which goes to the rear and brings

[016-260] up rations for the front line.

[016-261] Rest - Relief from trench service. Mostly one works constantly

[016-262] when "resting."

[016-263] Ruddy - Same as bloody, but not quite so bad.

[016-264] [[sandbag|Sandbag]] - A bag which is filled with mud and used for building

[016-265] the parapet.

[016-266] Sentry go - Time on guard in the front trench, or at rest at

[016-267] headquarters.

[016-268] Shell hole - A pit made by the explosion of a shell.

[016-269] Souvenir - Any kind of junk picked up for keepsakes. Also used

[016-270] as a begging word by the French children.

[016-271] Stand to - Order for all men to stand ready in the trench in

[016-272] event of a surprise attack, usually at sundown and sunrise.

[016-273] Stand down - Countermanding "stand to."

[016-274] Stokes - A bomb weighing about eleven pounds usually thrown

[016-275] from a mortar, but sometimes used by hand.

[016-276] Strafing - One of the few words Tommy has borrowed from Fritz.

[016-277] To punish.

[016-278] Suicide club - The battalion bombers.

[016-279] Tin hat - Steel [[tin-hat|helmet]].

[016-280] Wave - A line of men going over the top.

[016-281] Whacked - Exhausted. Played out.

[016-282] Whiz-bang - A German shell that makes that sort of noise.

[016-283] Wind up or [[windy]] - Nervous. Jumpy. Temporary involuntary fear.

[016-284] Wooden cross - The small wooden cross placed over a soldier's

[016-285] grave.
