## Overview

Mine explosions were a distinctive and terrifying form of underground warfare on the [[france|Western Front]]. Both sides dug tunnels—called "saps"—beneath [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]] to plant massive quantities of high explosives under enemy [[trench|trenches]], creating [[crater|craters]] that infantry "rushing parties" would then race to occupy.

> The Sappers, or Engineers, are the men whose duty it is to run mines under No Man's Land and plant huge quantities of explosives. There was a great amount of mining going on all the time at Vimy Ridge from both sides. [007-222]

## How Mining Worked

The mining war was a three-dimensional contest: each side would dig deeper to get under the other's tunnels. [[sap|Sap]]pers excavated narrow galleries [[hun]]dreds of feet underground, packing the ends with high explosive (H.E.) in fifty-pound boxes. The work was continuous and high-stakes—engineer officers would listen for hours to detect enemy digging below.

> Sometimes Fritz would run a sap out reasonably near the surface, and we would counter with one lower down. Then he'd go us one better and go still deeper. Some of the mines went down and under hundreds of feet. [007-222]

Infantrymen were frequently drafted into sapper duty to fill [[sandbag|sandbags]] and remove excavated earth, as the specialist Engineers were undermanned.

## The Rushing Party

Once a mine was detonated, the most dangerous infantry duty began: the **rushing party**. Sixty men, selected from each company, would be positioned nearby. When the explosion occurred, they were expected to sprint across [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]] and occupy the newly formed crater before the enemy could reach it.

> This is the most dangerous duty an infantryman has to do, because both sides after a mine explosion shower in fifty-seven varieties of sudden death, including a perfect rain of machine-gun bullets. The chances of coming out of a rushing party with a whole hide are about one in five. [007-240]

## The Vimy Ridge Explosion

The author witnessed a major mine detonation at [[vimy|Vimy Ridge]] while returning with a ration party. An eerie silence fell across the line—no [[artillery|guns]], no [[rifle|rifles]], an "electric tension in the very air." Then the German [[very-light|Very lights]] went up in [[hun]]dreds:

> Suddenly the "Very" lights went up from the German side, literally in hundreds, illuminating the top of the ridge and the sky behind with a thin greenish white flare. Then came a deep rumble that shook the ground, and a dull boom. A spurt of blood-red flame squirted up from the near side of the hill, and a rolling column of gray smoke. [007-240]

> Then another rumble, and another, and then the whole side of the ridge seemed to open up and move slowly skyward with a world-wrecking, soul-paralyzing crash. A murky red glare lit up the smoke screen, and against it a mass of tossed-up debris, and for an instant I caught the black silhouette of a whole human body spread-eagled and spinning like a pin-wheel. [007-240]

## Tactical Significance

Mine warfare was central to the [[battle|fight]]ing at [[vimy-ridge]]. The contested high ground was riddled with tunnels from both sides, making the ridge as dangerous below ground as above. Successful mine detonations could destroy entire trench sections, [[dug-out]]s, and mine shafts in an instant.
