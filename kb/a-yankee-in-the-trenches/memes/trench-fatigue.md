# Trench Fatigue

## Overview

Fatigue duty was the endless, grinding manual labor that filled the hours between [[battle|combat]] on the [[france|Western Front]]. Soldiers carried ammunition, [[trench-rations|rations]], and engineering materials forward; dug new [[trench|trenches]] and repaired damaged ones; filled [[sandbag|sandbags]] and laid [[duck-walk|duckboards]]. Unlike [[battle|combat]], which came in terrifying bursts, [[fatigue]] was constant — a dull, physical exhaustion that wore men down as surely as bullets.

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir captures this dimension of trench life with vivid detail, recounting burial fatigues, eight-day carrying parties, and the bitter irony that "rest" behind the lines meant more hard labor.

## The Military Use of "Fatigue"

Holmes himself paused to remark on the euphemistic genius of the military term:

> I have often admired the unknown who invented that word "fatigue" as applied in a military term. He used it as a disguise for just plain hard work. It means anything whatever in the way of duty that does not have to do directly with the manning of the trenches. [013-018–022]

When soldiers were ordered to "fall in without equipment," they knew immediately what it meant:

> We were hardly down to a good sleep when C company was called to fall in without equipment, and we knew that meant fatigue of some sort. [013-016–018]

## Burial Fatigue

The most harrowing fatigue duty Holmes experienced was burial fatigue — recovering and interring bodies from the battlefield. His first burial detail came after the [[battle|fight]]ing at [[high-wood|High Wood]]:

> This time we clicked a burial fatigue. It was my first. I never want another. I took a party of ten men and we set out, armed with picks and shovels, and, of course, rifles and bandoliers (cloth pockets containing fifty rounds of ammo). We hiked three miles up to High Wood and in the early morning began… [013-023–027]

The psychological toll of handling corpses quickly gave way to a hardened indifference:

> The indifference with which the men soon came to regard this burial fatigue was amazing. I remember one incident of that first morning, a thing that didn't seem at all shocking at the time, but which, looking back upon it, illustrates the matter-of-factness of the soldier's viewpoint on death. [013-048–052]

Holmes recorded a fellow soldier's grim humor while handling remains:

> "Hi sye, Darby," sang out one fellow. "Hi got a blighter 'ere wif… It's a fair crime sendin' 'im hover the river wif only one." [013-053, 013-066–067]

When it was over, relief was the dominant emotion:

> I was sure thankful when that burial fatigue was over, and early in the forenoon we started back to rest. [013-068–069]

## Carrying Parties

No sooner had the burial fatigue ended than a new labor assignment began — the [[ration-party|carrying party]], ferrying ammunition and supplies to the front:

> Rest, did I say? Not that trip. We were hardly back to Mametz and down to breakfast when along came an order to fall in for a carrying party. All that day we carried boxes of Millses up to the dump that was by High Wood, three long miles over hard going. [013-070–073]

The work was relentless, and rank offered only minor relief:

> This carrying stunt went on for eight long days and nights. We worked on an average sixteen hours a day. It rained nearly all the time, and we never got dried out. The food was awful, as the advance had been so fast that it was almost impossible to get up the supplies. [013-086–090]

The cumulative effect was devastating:

> When that eight days of carrying was over, we were so fed up that we didn't care whether we clicked or not. Maybe it was good mental preparation for what was to come, for on top of it all it turned out that we were to go over the top in another big attack. [013-096–099]

## The Irony of "Rest"

"Rest" behind the lines was a misnomer — it typically meant more fatigue duty under a different name:

> Usually "rest" means that you are set to mending roads or some such fatigue duty. [005-134–136]

This constant cycle of labor — [[battle|fight]], work, "rest" (more work) — was the true rhythm of trench life, and it ground soldiers down in ways that [[battle|combat]] alone could not.

## In the Memoir

Fatigue duty pervades Holmes's memoir. It appears as burial details, as ammunition-carrying slogs through the mud, as the punishment of extra work for minor infr[[battle|action]]s. It is the unglamorous substrate of war — the labor that made battle possible, and the labor that cleaned up after it.

Holmes's account of [[fatigue]] is ultimately an account of endurance: men who could [[marching|march]] three miles to dig graves, then carry [[grenade|bomb]]s another three miles, then face an attack — all while underfed, underslept, and soaked to the bone. The [[fatigue]] was not a break from the war; it was the war by other means.
