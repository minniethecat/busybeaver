# Over the Top

## Overview

To "go over the top" was to leave the relative safety of the trench and advance across [[no-mans-land|no man's land]] toward the enemy line. It was the moment of maximum exposure and maximum terror — the point at which a soldier exchanged the protection of earthworks for the bullet-swept open ground between the lines. For the men of the [[france|Western Front]], those three words encapsulated everything they feared and everything they were asked to do.

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] describes going over the top multiple times in his memoir, each account capturing a different emotional register: fear, adrenaline, grief, and sometimes a strange exhilaration.

## In the Memoir

Holmes's first experience going over the top came during a [[raid|trench raid]]. After the preparatory [[estaminet|bar]]rage lifted and a tense silence fell, the command came:

> And in that silence over the top we went.
> — (003-057)

The sentence's brevity mirrors the moment itself — a sudden transition from waiting to [[battle|action]]. Later, during larger offensives, the experience became more routinized but never less terrifying. Holmes notes that even veteran soldiers dreaded it:

> Don't look over the top. It
> — (002-073)

This warning to rookies underscores the danger: exposing oneself above the parapet was often fatal, and even the act of looking could draw [[sniper]] fire.

Holmes describes one particularly grim moment when a soldier was hit just as he prepared to go over:

> A soldier was going over the top when
> — (004-197)

The matter-of-fact tone with which Holmes records these moments reflects the grim normalization of the act. Men went over the top because orders demanded it, and they did so knowing that some of them would not return.

## The Ritual

Going over the top was not chaotic — it followed a precise sequence. The preparatory [[artillery|bombardment]] would lift at a scheduled moment. Whistles would sound along the line. Officers would lead the way up the scaling ladders. Each man carried his rifle, ammunition, grenades, and often extra equipment — [[gas-mask|gas helmet]], entrenching tool, [[trench-rations|rations]] for 48 hours. The weight alone made the advance exhausting before it began.

Holmes records that [[lieutenant-blofeld|Lieutenant Blofeld]] always went over the top with his men:

> He always went over the top with us and was in the thick
> — (010-033)

This leadership from the front was both a source of morale and a reason officer [[casualty|casualties]] were disproportionately high.
