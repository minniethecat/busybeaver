## Overview

A "dixie" was an oblong iron pot or box used in the [[british-army|British Army]] field [[equipment|kit]]chens of World War I. Holmes provides the most detailed description in his narrative: "The 'dixie', by the way, is an iron box or pot, oblong in shape, capacity about four or five gallons. It fits into the field [[equipment|kit]]chen and is used for roasts, stews, char, or anything else. The cover serves to cook bacon in." (005-027) The term appears in the original book's glossary, where it is defined as "an oblong iron pot or box fitting into a field [[equipment|kit]]chen. Used for cooking anything and everything. Nobody seems to know why it is so called."

## In the Trenches

The dixie was central to the daily routine of trench life. Each morning, mess orderlies would fetch tea (called "char") and bacon from the field [[equipment|kit]]chen, and afterward clean the dixies (005-027). Holmes served as the platoon's ration issuer, a duty that involved overseeing the mess orderlies who handled the dixies daily.

The [[equipment|kit]]chen system rotated between stew and roast dinners, but a practical limitation arose from an insufficient number of dixies: "There were not enough 'dixies' for us all to have stew the same day" (005-043). This forced the company to alternate — stew for two [[platoon|platoons]] one day, roast for the others, then reversing the order the following day. This small logistical detail reveals how even basic cooking equipment could shape the rhythm of a soldier's diet.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes mentions dixies in the context of the morning rush to the front lines. On his first tour in the [[trench|trenches]], a message came at daybreak demanding immediate reinforcement: "We slung together a [[char|dixie of char]] and some bacon and bread for breakfast, and [[marching|march]]ed around to the '[[billets|quarters]]'" (002-020). The dixie here serves as both a practical cooking vessel and a marker of the hurried, improvised character of [[daily-life-in-the-trenches|trench routine]] — meals were prepared quickly, often under the pressure of sudden orders.

The field [[equipment|kit]]chens that housed the dixies were horse-drawn and followed the battalion. Holmes notes that the cooks were men drawn from various civilian trades — they "drew a plumber and a navvy (road builder) — and the grub tasted of both trades" (005-043). The dixie, as the universal cooking vessel, bore the results of whoever happened to be assigned to [[equipment|kit]]chen duty.

## Military Context

The [[british-army|British Army]]'s field [[equipment|kit]]chen system in World War I was designed for mobility, supporting [[battalion|infantry battalion]]s as they rotated through trench sectors and rest areas. The dixie was standardized to fit these mobile [[equipment|kit]]chens, functioning as a multi-purpose vessel for boiling, stewing, roasting, and even frying (using the cover for bacon). Its four-to-five-gallon capacity made it suitable for feeding a section or platoon.

Unlike the individual mess tin carried by each soldier, the dixie was a shared communal resource — its contents fed groups of men, and its cleaning was a daily [[trench-fatigue|fatigue duty]] assigned to mess orderlies. The term "dixie" itself, as the glossary notes, has an unknown etymology, but it became firmly embedded in [[british-army|British Army]] slang of the period. (Not to be confused with the American song "Dixie," which Holmes also mentions hearing the men sing on the [[marching|march]] — a different cultural artifact entirely.)
