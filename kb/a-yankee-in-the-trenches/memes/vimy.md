# Vimy Ridge

**Vimy Ridge** is a prominent escarpment in the Pas-de-Calais region of northern [[france|France]] that became one of the most fought-over pieces of high ground on the [[france|Western Front]]. Rising approximately 60 meters above the surrounding plain, it offered commanding views of the Douai Plain and was a strategic objective for both sides throughout the war.

## In the Narrative

Holmes encountered Vimy Ridge during his service on the [[france|Western Front]], describing it as a well-known terrain feature:

> "…forms a part of the well-known Vimy Ridge — is a…" [006-095]

The ridge was a reference point for trench positions. Soldiers used landmarks like Vimy Ridge to orient themselves in the featureless landscape of the front:

> "Vimy Ridge, but further down the line…" [007-070]

Holmes describes positions relative to the ridge's geography:

> "…side and to the left end of Vimy Ridge, and…" [007-199]

The author's unit operated in proximity to the ridge, and it served as a constant reminder of the strategic importance of high ground in [[trench|trench warfare]]. While Holmes's memoir focuses on individual and small-unit experiences rather than grand strategy, the recurring mention of Vimy Ridge underscores how terrain dominated the soldiers' physical and mental landscape.

## Military Significance

The **[[battle|Battle]] of Vimy Ridge** (April 9–12, 1917) was a defining moment for the Canadian Corps, which captured the ridge from German forces in a meticulously planned assault. It was one of the few clear Allied victories on the [[france|Western Front]] up to that point.

Key military aspects of Vimy Ridge:

- **Strategic observation**: The ridge provided views over the Douai Plain and German rear positions
- **Fortified position**: German forces had held the ridge since 1914, building extensive tunnels, [[dug-out|dugout]]s, and trench systems
- **Canadian achievement**: The successful capture by Canadian forces became a symbol of national identity and military competence

## The Ridge as Terrain

In the flat or gently rolling landscape of Artois, even modest elevation was invaluable. Vimy Ridge dominated the surrounding countryside, making it a prize that both sides spent years contesting. For the ordinary soldier like Holmes, the ridge was simply a fact of the landscape — a landmark that meant you were in a particular sector, with all the dangers and associations that entailed.
