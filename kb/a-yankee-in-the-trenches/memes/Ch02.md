[002-001] The excitement of getting away from camp and the knowledge that we

[002-002] were soon to get into the thick of the big game pleased most of us.

[002-003] We were glad to go. At least we thought so.

[002-004] Two hundred of us were loaded into side-door Pullmans, forty to the

[002-005] car. It was a kind of sardine or [[boston|Boston]] Elevated effect, and by the

[002-006] time we reached [[rouen|Rouen]], twenty-four hours later, we had kinks in our

[002-007] legs and corns on our elbows. Also we were hungry, having had

[002-008] nothing but [[trench-rations|bully beef]] and biscuits. We made "char", which is

[002-009] trench slang for tea, in the station, and after two hours moved up

[002-010] the line again, this time in real coaches.

[002-011] Next night we were billeted at Barlin--don't get that mixed up with

[002-012] Berlin, it's not the same--in an abandoned convent within range of

[002-013] the German [[artillery|guns]]. The roar of artillery was continuous and sounded

[002-014] pretty close.

[002-015] Now and again a shell would burst near by with a kind of hollow

[002-016] "spung", but for some reason we didn't seem to mind. I had expected

[002-017] to get the shivers at the first sound of the guns and was surprised

[002-018] when I woke up in the morning after a solid night's sleep.

[002-019] A message came down from the front [[trench|trenches]] at daybreak that we

[002-020] were wanted and wanted quick. We slung together a [[char|dixie of char]] and

[002-021] some bacon and bread for breakfast, and marched around to the

[002-022] "[[billets|quarters]]", where they issued "[[tin-hat|tin hats]]", extra "ammo", and a

[002-023] second [[gas-warfare|gas helmet]]. A good many of the men had been out before, and

[002-024] they did the customary "grousing" over the added load.

[002-025] The British [[british-army|Tommy]] growls or grouses over anything and everything.

[002-026] He's never happy unless he's unhappy. He resents especially having

[002-027] anything officially added to his pack, and you can't blame him, for

[002-028] in full equipment he certainly is all dressed up like a pack horse.

[002-029] After the issue we were split up into four lots for the four

[002-030] companies of the battalion, and after some "wangling" I got into

[002-031] Company C, where I stopped all the time I was in [[france|France]]. I was

[002-032] glad, because most of my chums were in that unit.

[002-033] We got into our packs and started up the line immediately. As we

[002-034] neared the lines we were extended into artillery formation, that

[002-035] is, spread out so that a shell bursting in the road would inflict

[002-036] fewer [[casualty|casualties]].

[002-037] At [[grenay|Bully-Grenay]], the point where we entered the communication

[002-038] trenches, guides met us and looked us over, commenting most frankly

[002-039] and freely on our appearance. They didn't seem to think we would

[002-040] amount to much, and said so. They agreed that the "bloomin' Yank"

[002-041] must be a "bloody fool" to come out there. There were times later

[002-042] when I agreed with them.

[002-043] It began to rain as we entered the communication trench, and I had

[002-044] my first taste of mud. That is literal, for with mud knee-deep in a

[002-045] trench just wide enough for two men to pass you get smeared from

[002-046] head to foot.

[002-047] Incidentally, as we approached nearer the front, I got my first

[002-048] smell of the dead. It is something you never get away from in the

[002-049] trenches. So many dead have been buried so hastily and so lightly

[002-050] that they are constantly being uncovered by shell bursts. The acrid

[002-051] stench pervades everything, and is so thick you can fairly taste

[002-052] it. It makes nearly everybody deathly sick at first, but one

[002-053] becomes used to it as to anything else.

[002-054] This communication trench was over two miles long, and it seemed

[002-055] like twenty. We finally landed in a support trench called

[002-056] "Mechanics" (every trench has a name, like a street), and from

[002-057] there into the first-line trench.

[002-058] I have to admit a feeling of disappointment in that first trench. I

[002-059] don't know what I expected to see, but what I did see was just a

[002-060] long, crooked ditch with a low step running along one side, and

[002-061] with [[sandbag|sandbags]] on top. Here and there was a muddy, bedraggled [[tommy|Tommy]]

[002-062] half asleep, nursing a dirty and muddy rifle on "sentry go."

[002-063] Everything was very quiet at the moment--no [[rifle|rifles]] popping, as I

[002-064] had expected, no bullets flying, and, as it happened, absolutely no

[002-065] [[shell|shelling]] in the whole sector.

[002-066] I forgot to say that we had come up by daylight. Ordinarily troops

[002-067] are moved at night, but the communication trench from Bully-Grenay

[002-068] was very deep and was protected at points by little hills, and it

[002-069] was possible to move men in the daytime.

[002-070] Arrived in the front trench, the [[sergeant-major]] appeared, crawling

[002-071] out of his [[dug-out]]--the usual place for a sergeant-major--and

[002-072] greeted us with,

[002-073] "Keep your nappers down, you rooks. Don't look [[over-the-top|over the top]]. It

[002-074] ayen't 'ealthy."

[002-075] It is the regular warning to new men. For some reason the first

[002-076] emotion of the rookie is an overpowering curiosity. He wants to

[002-077] take a peep into [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]]. It feels safe enough when things

[002-078] are quiet. But there's always a Fritzie over yonder with a

[002-079] telescope-sighted rifle, and it's about ten to one he'll get you if

[002-080] you stick the old "napper" up in daylight.

[002-081] The [[germans|Germans]], by the way, have had the "edge" on the Allies in the

[002-082] matter of sniping, as in almost all lines of artillery and musketry

[002-083] practice. The [[boche|Boche]] [[sniper]] is nearly always armed with a

[002-084] periscope-telescope rifle. This is a specially built super-accurate

[002-085] rifle mounted on a periscope frame. It is thrust up over the

[002-086] parapet and the image of the opposing parapet is cast on a little

[002-087] ground-glass screen on which are two crossed lines. At one hundred

[002-088] fifty yards or less the image is brought up to touching distance

[002-089] seemingly. Fritz simply trains his piece on some low place or

[002-090] anywhere that a head may be expected. When one appears on the

[002-091] screen, he pulls the trigger,--and you "[[click]] it" if you happen to

[002-092] be on the other or receiving end. The shooter never shows himself.

[002-093] I remember the first time I looked through a periscope I had no

[002-094] sooner thrust the thing up than a bullet crashed into the upper

[002-095] mirror, splintering it. Many times I have stuck up a cap on a stick

[002-096] and had it pierced.

[002-097] The British sniper, on the other hand--at least in my time--had a

[002-098] plain telescope rifle and had to hide himself behind old masonry,

[002-099] tree trunks, or anything convenient, and camouflaged himself in

[002-100] all sorts of ways. At that he was constantly in danger.

[002-101] I was assigned to [[platoon|Platoon]] 10 and found they were a good live bunch.

[002-102] Corporal [[wellsie|Wells]] was the best of the lot, and we became fast friends.

[002-103] He helped me learn a lot of my new duties and the trench "lingo",

[002-104] which is like a new language, especially to a Yank.

[002-105] Wells started right in to make me feel at [[blighty|home]] and took me along

[002-106] with two others of the new men down to our "apartments", a [[dug-out]]

[002-107] built for about four, and housing ten.

[002-108] My previous idea of a dug-out had been a fairly roomy sort of cave,

[002-109] somewhat damp, but comparatively comfortable. Well, this hole was

[002-110] about four and a half feet high--you had to get in doubled up on

[002-111] your hands and knees--about five by six feet on the sides, and

[002-112] there was no floor, just muck. There was some sodden, dirty straw

[002-113] and a lot of old moldy sandbags. Seven men and their equipment were

[002-114] packed in here, and we made ten.

[002-115] There was a charcoal brazier going in the middle with two or three

[002-116] mess tins of char boiling away. Everybody was smoking, and the

[002-117] place stunk to high heaven, or it would have if there hadn't been a

[002-118] bit of burlap over the door.

[002-119] I crowded up into a corner with my back against the mud wall and my

[002-120] knees under my chin. The men didn't seem overglad to see us, and

[002-121] groused a good deal about the extra crowding. They regarded me with

[002-122] extra disfavor because I was a lance corporal, and they disapproved

[002-123] of any young whipper-snapper just out from [[england|Blighty]] with no trench

[002-124] experience pitchforked in with even a slight superior rank. I had

[002-125] thought up to then that a lance corporal was pretty near as

[002-126] important as a brigadier.

[002-127] "We'll soon tyke that stripe off ye, me bold lad," said one big

[002-128] cockney.

[002-129] They were a decent lot after all. Since we were just out from

[002-130] Blighty, they showered us with questions as to how things looked

[002-131] "t' 'ome." And then somebody asked what was the latest song. Right

[002-132] here was where I made my hit and got in right. I sing a bit, and I

[002-133] piped up with the newest thing from the music halls, "Tyke Me Back

[002-134] to Blighty." Here it is:

[002-135] Tyke me back to dear old Blighty,

[002-136] Put me on the tryne for [[london|London town]],

[002-137] Just tyke me over there

[002-138] And drop me anywhere,

[002-139] Manchester, Leeds, or Birmingham,

[002-140] I don't care.

[002-141] I want to go see me best gal;

[002-142] Cuddlin' up soon we'll be,

[002-143] Hytey iddle de eyety.

[002-144] Tyke me back to Blighty,

[002-145] That's the plyce for me.

[002-146] It doesn't look like much and I'm afraid my rendition of cockney

[002-147] dialect into print isn't quite up to Kipling's. But the song had a

[002-148] pretty little lilting melody, and it went big. They made me sing it

[002-149] about a dozen times and were all joining in at the end.

[002-150] Then they got sentimental--and gloomy.

[002-151] "Gawd lumme!" says the big fellow who had threatened my beloved

[002-152] stripes. "Wot a life. Squattin' 'ere in the bloody mud like a

[002-153] blinkin' frog. Fightin' fer wot? Wot, I arsks yer? Gawd lumme! I'd

[002-154] give me bloomin' napper to stroll down the Strand agyne wif me

[002-155] swagger stick an' drop in a private [[estaminet|bar]] an' 'ave me go of 'Aig an'

[002-156] 'Aig."

[002-157] "Garn," cuts in another Tommy. "Yer blinkin' 'igh wif yer wants,

[002-158] ayen't ye? An' yer 'Aig an' 'Aig. Drop me down in Great Lime Street

[002-159] (Liverpool) an' it's me fer the Golden Sheaf, and a pint of bitter,

[002-160] an' me a 'oldin' 'Arriet's 'and over th' bar. I'm a courtin' 'er

[002-161] when," etc., etc.

[002-162] And then a fresh-faced lad chirps up: "T' 'ell wif yer Lonnon an'

[002-163] yer whuskey. Gimme a jug o' cider on the sunny side of a 'ay rick

[002-164] in old Surrey. Gimme a happle tart to go wif it. Gawd, I'm fed up

[002-165] on [[bully-beef|bully]] beef."

[002-166] And so it went. All about pubs and bar-maids and the things they'd

[002-167] eat and drink, and all of it Blighty.

[002-168] They were in the midst of a discussion of what part of the body was

[002-169] most desirable to part with for a permanent [[blighty-wound|Blighty wound]] when a

[002-170] young officer pushed aside the burlap and wedged in. He was a

[002-171] lieutenant and was in command of our platoon. His name was [[lieutenant-blofeld|Blofeld]].

[002-172] Blofeld was most democratic. He shook hands with the new men and

[002-173] said he hoped we'd be live wires, and then he told us what he

[002-174] wanted. There was to be a [[trench-raid|raid]] the next night and he was looking

[002-175] for volunteers.

[002-176] Nobody spoke for a long minute, and then I offered.

[002-177] I think I spoke more to break the embarrassing silence than

[002-178] anything else. I think, too, that I was led a little by a kind of

[002-179] youthful curiosity, and it may be that I wanted to appear brave in

[002-180] the eyes of these men who so evidently held me more or less in

[002-181] contempt as a newcomer.

[002-182] Blofeld accepted me, and one of the other new men offered. He was

[002-183] taken too.

[002-184] It turned out that all the older men were married and that they

[002-185] were not expected to volunteer. At least there was no disgrace

[002-186] attaching to a refusal.

[002-187] After Blofeld left, [[sergeant-page|Sergeant Page]] told us we'd better get down to

[002-188] "kip" while we could. "[[kip|Kip]]" in this case meant closing our eyes and

[002-189] dozing. I sat humped up in my original position through the night.

[002-190] There wasn't room to stretch out.

[002-191] Along toward morning I began to itch, and found I had made the

[002-192] acquaintance of that gay and festive little soldier's enemy, the

[002-193] "cootie." The cootie, or the "[[cootie|chat]]" as he is called by the

[002-194] officers, is the common body louse. Common is right. I never got

[002-195] rid of mine until I left the service. Sometimes when I get to

[002-196] thinking about it, I believe I haven't yet.
