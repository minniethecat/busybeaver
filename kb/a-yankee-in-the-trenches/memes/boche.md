# Boche

## Overview

"Boche" (also spelled "Bosche") was the [[tommy|British Tommy]]'s most common term for the [[germans|German soldier]] during World War I. Borrowed from [[france|French]] slang — where it had been in use since the late 19th century — the word carried a weight of contempt, fear, and grudging respect. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, "Boche" appears throughout the narrative as the default label for the enemy, more common than "German" and far more common than the other slang alternatives "[[germans|Fritz]]," "[[germans|Jerry]]," or "Hun."

## In the Memoir

Holmes uses "Boche" consistently and without explanation — the word was so universal among [[tommy|British soldier]]s that no gloss was needed. The Boche appears as [[sniper]], [[trench-raid|raid]]er, defender, and prisoner. Holmes describes German [[sniper|sniping]] practices:

> The Boche sniper is nearly always armed with a periscope-telescope rifle
> — (002-083)

The Boche sniper was a figure of particular dread — methodical, patient, and lethally effective. Holmes's description of the periscope-telescope rifle, a German innovation, carries a note of professional respect even as it describes a weapon used to kill his comrades.

Before a [[trench-raid|raid]], the men were briefed on what to expect:

> recognize your own men when you get to the Boche trench
> — (003-029)

The Boche trench — the mirror image of the British trench — was a place of mystery and terror. To cross into it was to enter the enemy's domain, where every corner might conceal a bayonet or a [[grenade|bomb]].

## In Combat

Holmes's account of a [[raid|trench raid]] provides the most intense close-up of the Boche in [[battle|combat]]. Emerging onto the German parapet, he comes face to face with the enemy:

> coming over the parapet, there was a whaling big Boche
> — (003-084)

The encounter is terrifyingly intimate — two men, enemies, close enough to see the expression on each other's faces. Holmes's bayonet finds its mark. The Boche falls. The [[trench-raid|raid]] continues.

Later, describing the aftermath of a successful [[battle|action]], Holmes records:

> By and by the Boche come and in a great battle not far from this place
> — (004-122)

The Boche retaliated. The cycle of [[trench-raid|raid]] and counter-[[trench-raid|raid]], attack and counter-attack, was the rhythm of [[trench|trench warfare]] — and the Boche was as tenacious in defense as [[tommy|Tommy]] was in assault.

## A Complex Portrait

Despite the derogatory nature of the term, Holmes's portrait of the Boche is not one-dimensional. The [[germans|German soldier]] is acknowledged as a s[[casualty|killed]] and dangerous adversary. The Boche sniper with his telescopic [[periscope|periscope rifle]], the Boche pioneers with their [[bayonet|saw-tooth bayonet]]s, the Boche engineers who built deeper and more elaborate [[dug-out]]s — these are not caricatures but formidable opponents.

Yet the term "Boche" remained fundamentally a term of othering. It reduced the enemy to a label, stripping away the individuality that [[tommy|Tommy]] claimed for himself. The [[germans|German soldier]] was always "the Boche" — never "Hans" or "Karl" or the name of an individual. This linguistic dehumanization was a necessary psychological mechanism in a war where killing was a daily reality.
