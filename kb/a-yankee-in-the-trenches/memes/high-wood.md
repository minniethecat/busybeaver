# High Wood

## Overview

High Wood ([[battle-of-high-wood|Bois des Fourcaux]] in [[france|French]]) was a heavily fortified German position on [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]] battlefield, a dense woodland that dominated the surrounding terrain and became the objective of repeated British assaults during the summer and autumn of 1916. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir, High Wood is the place where his war reached its violent climax — the scene of his battalion's attack, his wounding, and the end of his participation in the conflict. The wood, reduced by shellfire to a nightmare landscape of shattered trunks and [[crater|craters]], became a byword for the intensity and futility of [[trench|trench warfare]].

## The Battlefield

Holmes describes High Wood as it appeared before the assault:

> High Wood rose covered with the splintered remnants of what had once been a forest
> — (010-077)

The wood had been fought over for weeks. Trees that had stood for centuries were reduced to jagged stumps in hours. The ground was churned by constant [[shell|shelling]] into a morass of mud, bodies, and shattered equipment. Holmes's battalion approached the wood as part of a major offensive:

> battle — one of the most important in the whole war. High Wood
> — (010-020)

## The Assault

Going [[over-the-top|over the top]] toward High Wood was Holmes's defining experience of battle. The attack was delivered from the second line:

> Our battalion went over from the second trench
> — (010-081)

The advance across the open ground between the [[trench|trenches]] and the wood was met with devastating fire. Machine [[artillery|guns]] concealed in the wood's shattered edge scythed through the advancing infantry. Holmes records the devastating [[casualty|losses]]:

> as much as a whole platoon went down
> — (010-102)

The [[tank|tanks]] — new weapons, deployed for the first time in this sector — advanced ahead of the infantry:

> The tanks passed beyond us and half-way up the slope
> — (010-001)

Holmes used a tank as cover, sheltering behind its massive steel bulk as it crawled forward through the German fire:

> There was a tank just ahead of me. I got behind it and followed along
> — (010-095)

## The Wounding

Holmes's war ended at High Wood. A shell fragment struck him:

> of it my participation in the battle of High Wood ended
> — (010-123)

The laconic sentence belies the violence of the moment. Holmes was [[casualty|wounded]], evacuated, and would eventually be sent back to [[england|England]] — his personal [[blighty-wound|Blighty wound]], earned in the shattered woodland on [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]].

## Aftermath

When the [[battle|fight]]ing subsided, the wood was in British hands — but at enormous cost:

> The net result of the battle of High Wood was that our troops gained their objective
> — (010-159)

Holmes returned to find his battalion transformed:

> When I found my battalion, the battle of High Wood had pretty well died down
> — (012-001)

The faces he had trained with, [[marching|march]]ed with, joked with — many were gone. The wood had consumed them.

In later weeks, passing the wood again, Holmes describes watching another unit [[battle|fight]] over the same ground:

> We had watched this battle from the rear from the slight elevation
> — (013-139)

High Wood remained contested for months, changing hands repeatedly, accumulating its harvest of dead with each assault.
