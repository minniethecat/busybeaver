# No Man's Land

## Overview

No man's land was the contested ground between the opposing trench lines — a strip of earth that belonged to neither side and to death alone. Its width varied from as little as fifty yards to several [[hun]]dred, and it was the most dangerous real estate on earth. Every offensive, every [[trench-raid|raid]], every [[patrol]] had to cross it; many never returned.

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]] crossed no man's land multiple times — on [[trench-raid|raid]]s, on [[patrol]], and in major offensives. His descriptions capture its physical character: churned earth, rusted [[barbed-wire|wire]], water-filled [[crater|craters]], and the ever-present dead.

## The View from the Periscope

Holmes begins his chapter on [[patrol|patrol work]] with a description of what a soldier would see looking out over no man's land by day:

> Just over the top, not more than twenty feet away, would be your barbed-wire entanglements, a thick network of wire stretched on iron posts nearly waist high, and perhaps twelve or fifteen feet across. Then there would be an intervening stretch of from fifty to one hundred fifty yards of No Man's Land, a tortured, torn expanse of muddy soil, pitted with shell craters, and, over beyond, the German wire and his parapet. [007-006–012]

Daylight gave the scene a macabre stillness:

> There would be nothing alive visible. There would probably be a few corpses lying about or hanging in the wire. Everything would be still except for the flutter of some rag of a dead man's uniform. [007-013–015]

Holmes recalled one grim detail that haunted the periscope watchers:

> Once I was in a trench where a leg — a booted German leg, stuck up stark and stiff out of the mud not twenty yards in front. Some idiotic joker on patrol hung a helmet on the foot, and all the next day that helmet dangled and swung in the breeze. It irritated the periscope watchers, and the next night it was taken down. [007-017–022]

## Night in No Man's Land

After dark, the dead zone came alive with furtive movement:

> After dark these men, or some of them, crawl out like hunted animals and prowl in the black mystery of No Man's Land. They are the patrol. [007-026–028]

[[patrol|Patrol]]s moved lightly, armed for speed rather than pitched battle:

> The patrol goes out armed and equipped lightly. He has to move softly and at times very quickly. It is his duty to get as close to the enemy lines as possible and find out if they are repairing their wire or if any of their parties are out, and to get back word to the machine gunners, who immediately cut loose on the indicated spot. [007-029–034]

## The Valley of Death

Holmes describes a solo [[patrol]] along the [[souchez|Souchez River]] through a section of no man's land called the Valley of Death, where thousands of [[france|French]] Zouaves had fallen early in the war and never been buried:

> I slipped out and down the slight incline, and presently found myself in a little valley. The grass was rank and high, sometimes nearly up to my chin, and the ground was slimy and treacherous. I slipped into several shell holes and was almost over my head in the stagnant, smelly water. [007-169–173]

The ground itself was carpeted with remains:

> Sliding gently through the grass, I kept catching my feet in something hard that felt like roots; but there were no trees in the neighborhood. I reached down and groped in the grass and brought up a human rib. The place was full of them, and skulls. Stooping, I could see them, grinning up out of the dusk, hundreds of them. [007-178–182]

## Crossing Behind Tanks

Later in the memoir, Holmes describes the experience of crossing no man's land behind [[tank|tanks]] during the [[battle-of-high-wood|Battle of High Wood]] — a mechanized crossing that felt entirely different from the stealthy infiltration of [[trench-raid|raid]]s:

> I don't suppose that trip across No Man's Land behind the tanks took over five minutes, but it seemed like an hour. [010-121–122]

The [[tank|tanks]] drew fire that would otherwise have cut down unprotected infantry:

> I ducked back, and on we went… There would be a grand rumpus, and I would click a dozen or more. And then we arrived. [010-115–120]

## The Canadians' Raid

Holmes witnessed an impromptu Canadian charge across no man's land in retaliation for the crucifixion of one of their sergeants:

> They swept across No Man's Land and into the Boche trench. There was the deuce of a ruckus over there for maybe two minutes, and then back they came — carrying something. Strangely enough there had been no machine-gun fire turned on them as they crossed, nor was there as they returned. They had cleaned that German trench! [007-098–103]

## Psychological Terrain

No man's land occupied a unique place in the soldier's imagination. It was the boundary between life and death, between the known world of the trench and the unknown world of the enemy. Holmes describes the profound relief of crossing it in reverse — of reaching one's own trench after a [[trench-raid|raid]]:

> Did that muddy old trench look good when we tumbled in? Oh, Boy! [003-149]

No man's land was not empty — it was densely populated with hazards. Barbed [[barbed-wire|wire entanglements]] stretched its length. Unexploded [[shell|shells]] lay half-buried in the mud. The bodies of soldiers from both sides, unrecovered and unburied, marked failed attacks. [[sap|Sap]]pers tunneled underneath it, planting mines [[hun]]dreds of feet deep (007-220–227). For men [[over-the-top|going over the top]], every step through no man's land was a step into the unknown.

The trench, for all its squalor, was [[blighty|home]]. No man's land was the opposite: a place where no one belonged and everyone was a target.
