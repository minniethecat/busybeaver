# Bombing Party

## Overview

The [[grenade|bomb]]ing party was a specialized assault unit in World War I [[trench|trench warfare]] — a small team of men, typically twelve, armed primarily with [[mills-bomb|hand grenade]]s (then called "[[grenade|bomb]]s") and tasked with clearing enemy [[trench|trenches]], [[mine-explosion|sap]]s, and strongpoints in close-[[billets|quarters]] night ope[[trench-rations|rations]]. The work was among the most dangerous on the [[france|Western Front]]. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, the [[grenade|bomb]]ing party provides the setting for one of the book's most gripping episodes: a night assault on a German-held [[mine-explosion|sap]] near [[high-wood|High Wood]], led by [[lieutenant-may|Lieutenant May]] — an officer Holmes describes as "one of the bravest men I ever knew."

## In the Narrative

### The Call for Volunteers

Holmes's involvement with a [[grenade|bomb]]ing party came unexpectedly. After surviving a series of near-misses and making out a new will — a darkly humorous ritual among front-line soldiers — he was summoned:

> That very night I was called upon to take charge of a bombing party of twelve men. A lieutenant, Mr. May, one of the bravest men I ever knew, was to be of the party and in direct command. I was to have the selection of the men.
> — (012-027)

The assignment, delivered by [[captain-green|Captain Green]], was to capture a German-held [[mine-explosion|sap]] — a narrow trench connecting to the sunken road between Albert and Bapaume — that commanded a strategic point necessary for the next British attack. The [[germans|Germans]] had two [[machine-gun|machine guns]] in the position, and a frontal assault would be costly. The solution was a stealth attack by a small [[grenade|bomb]]ing party under cover of darkness (012-041).

### The Mission Brief

[[captain-green|Captain Green]]'s instructions, as recalled by Holmes, captured the deceptive simplicity of such ope[[trench-rations|rations]]:

> "Just beyond High Wood and to the left there is a sap or small trench leading to the sunken road that lies between the towns of Albert and Bapaume. That position commands a military point that we find necessary to hold before we can make another attack. The Germans are in the trench. They have two machine guns and will raise the devil with us unless we get them out. ... Take your twelve men out there at ten o'clock and _take that trench_!"
> — (012-041)

The men were to carry only [[grenade|bomb]]s; Holmes and [[lieutenant-may|Lieutenant May]] would have [[pistol|revolver]]s. After taking the trench, they were to consolidate it — and await relief before morning.

### The Reality

Holmes notes the gulf between the briefing-room map and the reality on the ground. "The whole thing sounded as simple as ABC," he writes, adding with characteristic dry humor that the captain "didn't say how many [[germans|Germans]] there would be nor what they would be doing while we were taking their comfortable little position" (012-041). The [[grenade|bomb]]ing party, for all the matter-of-fact language of orders, was a mission into the unknown — twelve men, darkness, grenades, and a German machine-gun position.

## The Bombers' Identity

Within the battalion, the men who specialized in grenade work were known as "[[grenade|bomb]]ers" and had their own darkly humorous self-designation. Holmes records that the battalion [[grenade|bomb]]ers called themselves the "Suicide Club" (016-278) — a name that acknowledged, with the gallows humor of the [[trench|trenches]], the extreme risk of their trade.
