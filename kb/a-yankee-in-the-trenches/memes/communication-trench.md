## Overview

The [[trench|communication trench]] was the essential connective tissue of the World War I trench system, linking the front-line firing [[trench|trenches]] with support lines, reserve positions, and the rear areas. Unlike the front-line trench, which was designed for [[battle|fight]]ing, communication trenches were built for movement—allowing troops, supplies, [[trench-rations|rations]], and [[casualty|wounded]] to travel through the trench network with relative protection from enemy fire. In Holmes's account, these [[trench|trenches]] form the literal pathway from safety to danger, and his first experience of them on the way to the front is a visceral introduction to life in the line.

## In the Trenches

Holmes's first encounter with a [[trench|communication trench]] came during his initial [[marching|march]] to the front lines near [[grenay|Bully-Grenay]]. The experience was immediate and sensory:

> It began to rain as we entered the communication trench, and I had my first taste of the mud and water that make the trenches so uncomfortable (002-043)

The scale of these [[trench|trenches]] was considerable: "This [[trench|communication trench]] was over two miles long" (002-054), winding through the landscape as a protected artery. Their length and narrowness made movement slow and arduous, particularly when carrying full equipment or when meeting other parties coming the opposite direction.

Communication [[trench|trenches]] also served as the route for night-time logistics. Holmes notes that supplies and reinforcements "are moved at night, but the [[trench|communication trench]] from [[grenay|Bully-Grenay]]" (002-067) was a critical supply line. This nocturnal traffic pattern meant that communication trenches were often crowded in darkness, with men stumbling through mud and water, trying to navigate by feel and memory.

The vulnerability of communication trenches became apparent during enemy attacks. Holmes describes how "the [[germans|Germans]] appeared, running down communication trenches" (003-103) during a British assault, showing that these passages could become avenues for counter-attack. In another instance, [[germans|German soldier]]s were "coming across lots of communication trenches" (003-138), using the same routes the British relied upon.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes references communication trenches at pivotal moments. [[marching|Marching]] toward the front at [[grenay|Bully-Grenay]], the [[trench|communication trench]] marked the threshold between the relatively safe rear areas and the dangerous forward positions. He describes passing through the "Ca[[estaminet|bar]]et Rouge [[trench|communication trench]]" (008-051), a named landmark in the trench geography that soldiers used for navigation and as a reference point for trench maps.

The absence of communication trenches in certain sectors was a significant tactical liability. Holmes observes a sector where "there was no [[trench|communication trench]] unless it" (006-070) existed elsewhere, noting that "from Mill Street there were no communication trenches" (009-134). Without these protected passageways, troops were forced to move over open ground, exposed to enemy observation and fire. Such gaps in the trench network meant that even routine movements became dangerous, and the isolation of forward positions increased.

## Military Context

Communication [[trench|trenches]] were a fundamental element of the trench systems that stretched across the [[france|Western Front]] from 1914 to 1918. Typically dug in a zigzag or serpentine pattern to prevent enemy enfilade fire from sweeping the entire length, they connected three parallel lines: the front-line trench, the [[trench|support trench]], and the reserve trench. The [[british-army|British Army]]'s trench doctrine called for communication trenches to be deep enough to allow passage without crouching, lined with revetments to prevent collapse, and wide enough for [[stretcher-bearer|stretcher-bearers]] to carry [[casualty|wounded]] men.

The naming conventions for communication trenches reflected British military culture and humor: names like "Regent Street," "Oxford Circus," and "Ca[[estaminet|bar]]et Rouge" appeared on trench maps, transforming the subterranean landscape into an ironic mirror of [[london|London]]. These names served practical purposes—aiding navigation and map-reading—while also providing psychological comfort through familiarity.
