[014-001] A general cleaning of [[rifle|rifles]] started, although it was dark. Mine

[014-002] was already in good shape, and I leaned it against the side of the

[014-003] trench and went below for the rest of my equipment. While I was

[014-004] gone, a shell fragment undid all my work by smashing the breech.

[014-005] I had seen a new short German rifle in the [[dug-out]] with a bayonet

[014-006] and ammo, and decided to use that. I hid all my souvenirs, planning

[014-007] to get them when I came out if I ever came out. I hadn't much nerve

[014-008] left after the bashing I had taken a fortnight before and didn't

[014-009] hold much hope.

[014-010] Our instructions were of the briefest. It was the old story that

[014-011] there would probably be little resistance, if any. There would be a

[014-012] few [[machine-gun|machine guns]] to stop us, but nothing more. The situation we had

[014-013] to handle was this: A certain small sector had held on the attacks

[014-014] of the few previous days, and the line had bent back around it.

[014-015] All we had to do was to straighten the line. We had heard this old

[014-016] ghost story too often to believe a word of it.

[014-017] Our place had been designated where we were to get into extended

[014-018] formation, and our general direction was clear. We filed out of the

[014-019] trench at eight-thirty, and as we passed the other [[platoon|platoons]],--we

[014-020] had been to the rear,--they tossed us the familiar farewell hail,

[014-021] "The best o' luck, mytie."

[014-022] We soon found ourselves in the old sunken road that ran in front of

[014-023] Eaucort Abbaye. At this point we were not under observation, as a

[014-024] rise in the ground would have protected us even though it had been

[014-025] daylight. The moon was shining brilliantly, and we knew that it

[014-026] would not be anything in the nature of a surprise attack. We got

[014-027] into extended formation and waited for the order to advance. I

[014-028] thought I should go crazy during that short wait. Shells had begun

[014-029] to burst over and around us, and I was sure the next would be mine.

[014-030] Presently one burst a little behind me, and down went [[captain-green|Captain Green]]

[014-031] and the [[sergeant-major|Sergeant Major]] with whom he had been talking. Captain

[014-032] Green died a few days later at [[rouen|Rouen]], and the Sergeant Major lost

[014-033] an arm. This was a hard blow right at the start, and it spelled

[014-034] disaster. Everything started to go wrong. Mr. [[lieutenant-blofeld|Blofeld]] was in

[014-035] command, and another officer thought that he was in charge. We got

[014-036] conflicting orders, and there was one grand mix-up. Eventually we

[014-037] advanced and went straight up over the ridge. We walked slap-bang

[014-038] into perfectly directed fire. Torrents of machine-gun bullets

[014-039] crackled about us, and we went forward with our heads down, like

[014-040] men facing into a storm. It was a living marvel that any one could

[014-041] come through it.

[014-042] A lot of them didn't. Mr. Blofeld, who was near me, leaped in the

[014-043] air, letting go a hideous yell. I ran to him, disregarding the

[014-044] instruction not to stop to help any one. He was struck in the

[014-045] abdomen with an explosive bullet and was done for. I felt terribly

[014-046] about Mr. Blofeld, as he had been a good friend to me. He was the

[014-047] finest type of officer of the new English army, the rare sort who

[014-048] can be democratic and yet command respect. He had talked with me

[014-049] often, and I knew of his family and [[blighty|home]] life. He was more like an

[014-050] elder brother to me than a superior officer. I left Mr. Blofeld and

[014-051] went on.

[014-052] The hail of bullets grew even worse. They whistled and cracked and

[014-053] squealed, and I began to wonder why on earth I didn't get mine. Men

[014-054] were falling on all sides and the shrieks of those hit were the

[014-055] worst I had heard. The darkness made it worse, and although I had

[014-056] been [[over-the-top|over the top]] before by daylight this was the last limit of

[014-057] hellishness. And nothing but plain, unmixed machine-gun fire. As

[014-058] yet there was no artillery [[battle|action]] to amount to anything.

[014-059] Once again I put my hand inside my tunic and stroked Dinky and said

[014-060] to him, "For God's sake, Dink, see me through this time." I meant

[014-061] it too. I was actually praying,--to my mascot. I realize that this

[014-062] was plain, unadulterated, heathenish fetish worship, but it shows

[014-063] what a man reverts to in the barbaric stress of war.

[014-064] By this time we were within about thirty yards of [[boche|the Boche]] parapet

[014-065] and could see them standing shoulder to shoulder on the fire step,

[014-066] swarms of them, packed in, with the [[bayonet|bayonets]] gleaming. Machine

[014-067] [[artillery|guns]] were emplaced and vomiting death at incredibly short intervals

[014-068] along the parapet. Flares were going up continuously, and it was

[014-069] almost as light as day.

[014-070] We were terribly outnumbered, and the [[casualty|casualties]] had already been

[014-071] so great that I saw we were in for the worst thing we had ever

[014-072] known. Moreover, the next waves hadn't appeared behind us.

[014-073] I was in command, as all the officers and non-coms so far as I

[014-074] could make out had snuffed. I signalled to halt and take cover, my

[014-075] idea being to wait for the other waves to catch up. The men needed

[014-076] no second invitation to lie low. They rolled into the shell holes

[014-077] and burrowed where there was no cover.

[014-078] I drew a pretty decent hole myself, and a man came pitching in on

[014-079] top of me, screaming horribly. It was Corporal Hoskins, a close

[014-080] friend of mine. He had it in the stomach and clicked in a minute or

[014-081] two.

[014-082] During the few minutes that I lay in that hole, I suffered the

[014-083] worst mental anguish I ever knew. Seeing so many of my closest

[014-084] chums go west so horribly had nearly broken me, shaky as I was when

[014-085] the attack started. I was dripping with sweat and frightfully

[014-086] nauseated. A sudden overpowering impulse seized me to get out in

[014-087] the open and have it over with. I was ready to die.

[014-088] Sooner than I ought, for the second wave had not yet shown up, I

[014-089] shrilled the whistle and lifted them out. It was a hopeless charge,

[014-090] but I was done. I would have gone at them alone. Anything to close

[014-091] the act. To blazes with everything!

[014-092] As I scrambled out of the shell hole, there was a blinding,

[014-093] ear-splitting explosion slightly to my left, and I went down. I did

[014-094] not lose consciousness entirely. A red-hot iron was through my

[014-095] right arm, and some one had hit me on the left shoulder with a

[014-096] sledge hammer. I felt crushed,--shattered.

[014-097] My impressions of the rest of that night are, for the most part,

[014-098] vague and indistinct; but in spots they stand out clear and vivid.

[014-099] The first thing I knew definitely was when Smith bent over me,

[014-100] cutting the sleeve out of my tunic.

[014-101] "It's a [[blighty-wound|Blighty one]]," says Smithy. That was some consolation. I was

[014-102] back in the shell hole, or in another, and there were five or six

[014-103] other fellows piled in there too. All of them were dead except

[014-104] Smith and a man named Collins, who had his arm clean off, and

[014-105] myself. Smith dressed my wound and Collins', and said:

[014-106] "We'd better get out of here before [[germans|Fritz]] rushes us. The attack was

[014-107] a ruddy failure, and they'll come over and [[grenade|bomb]] us out of here."

[014-108] Smith and I got out of the hole and started to crawl. It appeared

[014-109] that he had a bullet through the thigh, though he hadn't said

[014-110] anything about it before. We crawled a little way, and then the

[014-111] bullets were flying so thick that I got an insane desire to run and

[014-112] get away from them. I got to my feet and legged it. So did Smith,

[014-113] though how he did it with a wounded thigh I don't know.

[014-114] The next thing I remember I was on a stretcher. The beastly thing

[014-115] swayed and pitched, and I got seasick. Then came another crash

[014-116] directly over head, and out I went again. When I came to, my head

[014-117] was as clear as a bell. A shell had burst over us and had killed

[014-118] one stretcher bearer. The other had disappeared. Smith was there.

[014-119] He and I got to our feet and put our arms around each other and

[014-120] staggered on. The next I knew I was in the Cough Drop dressing

[014-121] station, so called from the peculiar formation of the place. We had

[014-122] tea and [[rum-ration|rum]] here and a couple of [[fag|fags]] from a sergeant major of the

[014-123] R.A.M.C.

[014-124] After that there was a ride on a flat car on a light railway and

[014-125] another in an ambulance with an American driver. Snatches of

[014-126] conversation about Broadway and a girl in Newark floated back, and

[014-127] I tried to work up ambition enough to sing out and ask where the

[014-128] chap came from. So far I hadn't had much pain. When we landed in a

[014-129] regular [[first-aid|dressing station]], the M.O. gave me another going over and

[014-130] said,

[014-131] "[[england|Blighty]] for you, son." I had a piece of shrapnel or something

[014-132] through the right upper arm, clearing the bone and making a hole

[014-133] about as big as a half dollar. My left shoulder was full of

[014-134] shrapnel fragments, and began to pain like fury. More tea. More

[014-135] rum. More fags. Another faint. When I woke up the next time,

[014-136] somebody was sticking a hypodermic needle into my chest with a shot

[014-137] of anti-lockjaw serum, and shortly after I was tucked away in a

[014-138] white enameled [[red-cross|Red Cross train]] with a pretty nurse taking my

[014-139] temperature. I loved that nurse. She looked sort of cool and holy.

[014-140] I finally brought up in General [[hospital|Hospital]] Number 12 in Rouen. I was

[014-141] there four days and had a real bath,--a genuine boiling out. Also

[014-142] had some shrapnel picked out of my anatomy. I got in fairly good

[014-143] shape, though still in a good deal of dull pain. It was a glad day

[014-144] when they put a batch of us on a train for [[havre|Havre]], tagged for

[014-145] Blighty. We went direct from the train to the hospital ship,

[014-146] _Carisbrook Castle_. The [[billets|quarters]] were good,--real bunks, clean

[014-147] sheets, good food, careful nurses. It was some different from the

[014-148] crowded transport that had taken me over to [[france|France]].

[014-149] There were a lot of German prisoners aboard, wounded, and we

[014-150] swapped stories with them. It was really a lot of fun comparing

[014-151] notes, and they were pretty good chaps on the whole. They were as

[014-152] glad as we were to see land. Their troubles were over for the

[014-153] duration of the war.

[014-154] Never shall I forget that wonderful morning when I looked out and

[014-155] saw again the coast of England, hazy under the mists of dawn. It

[014-156] looked like the promised land. And it was. It meant freedom again

[014-157] from battle, murder, and sudden death, from [[trench|trenches]] and stenches,

[014-158] rats, [[cootie|cooties]], and all the rest that goes to make up the worst of

[014-159] man-made inventions, war.

[014-160] It was Friday the thirteenth. And don't let anybody dare say that

[014-161] date is unlucky. For it brought me back to the best thing that can

[014-162] gladden the eyes of a broken [[british-army|Tommy]]. Blighty! Blighty!! Blighty!!!
