# Windy

To be **windy** or to have the **wind up** was to be nervous, jumpy, or frightened — a condition every soldier in the [[trench|trenches]] understood from personal experience. It was not cowardice but a normal human re[[battle|action]] to sustained shellfire, and the slang captured it with characteristic understatement.

## Usage in the Narrative

After the British [[trench-raid|raid]]ed the German [[trench|trenches]], the [[germans|Germans]] were on edge:

> "…afterwards they were very 'windy' and…" [004-005]

The condition was contagious — men watched each other for signs:

> "…got about the men would be 'windy' on…" [007-122]

The glossary entry formalizes the term and its causes:

> "Wind up or windy — Nervous. Jumpy. Temporary indisposition caused by shell fire or the anticipation of same." [016-283]

## The Psychology of Fear

"Windy" was a temporary state, distinguished in the soldiers' vocabulary from outright cowardice or shell shock. A man who was "windy" might flinch at sudden noises, show tremors, or be reluctant to expose himself — but he was expected to recover and function. The term was an acknowledgment that fear was normal and universal, not a moral failing. Only when a man became permanently incapacitated by fear did the diagnosis shift to "shell shock" — a more serious and stigmatized condition.
