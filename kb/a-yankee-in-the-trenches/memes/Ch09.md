[009-001] Late in the summer I accumulated a nice little case of trench

[009-002] fever.

[009-003] This disease is due to remaining for long periods in the wet and

[009-004] mud, to racked nerves, and, I am inclined to think, to sleeping

[009-005] in the foul air of the dug-outs. The chief symptom is high

[009-006] temperature, and the patient aches a good deal. I was sent back to

[009-007] a place in the neighborhood of Arras and was there a week

[009-008] recuperating.

[009-009] While I was there a woman spy whom I had known in Abalaine was

[009-010] brought to the village and shot. The frequency with which the duck

[009-011] walk at Abalaine had been shelled, especially when [[ration-party|ration parties]]

[009-012] or troops were going over it, had attracted a good deal of

[009-013] attention.

[009-014] There was a single house not far from the end of that duck walk

[009-015] west of Abalaine, occupied by a woman and two or three children.

[009-016] She had lived there for years and was, so far as anybody knew, a

[009-017] Frenchwoman in breeding and sympathies. She was in the habit of

[009-018] selling coffee to the soldiers, and, of course, gossiped with them

[009-019] and thus gained a good deal of information about troop movements.

[009-020] She was not suspected for a long time. Then a gunner of a battery

[009-021] which was stationed near by noticed that certain children's

[009-022] garments, a red shirt and a blue one and several white garments,

[009-023] were on the clothesline in certain arrangement on the days when

[009-024] troops were to be moved along the duck walk the following night.

[009-025] This soldier notified his officers, and evidence was accumulated

[009-026] that the woman was signalling to [[boche|the Boche]] airplanes.

[009-027] She was arrested, taken to the rear, and shot. I don't like to

[009-028] think that this woman was really [[france|French]]. She was, no doubt, one of

[009-029] the myriad of spies who were planted in France by the [[germans|Germans]] long

[009-030] before the war.

[009-031] After getting over the fever, I rejoined my battalion in the early

[009-032] part of September in [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]] district at a place called Mill

[009-033] Street. This was in reality a series of dug-outs along a road some

[009-034] little distance behind our second lines, but in the range of the

[009-035] German [[artillery|guns]], which persistently tried for our artillery just beside

[009-036] us.

[009-037] Within an hour of my arrival I was treated to a taste of one of the

[009-038] forms of German kultur which was new at the time. At least it was

[009-039] new to me--[[gas-warfare|tear gas]]. This delectable vapor came over in [[shell|shells]],

[009-040] comparatively harmless in themselves, but which loosed a [[gas-weapon|gas]],

[009-041] smelling at first a little like [[grenade|pineapple]]. When you got a good

[009-042] inhale you choked, and the eyes began to run. There was no

[009-043] controlling the tears, and the victim would fairly drip for a

[009-044] long time, leaving him wholly incapacitated.

[009-045] Goggles provided for this gas were nearly useless, and we all

[009-046] resorted to the regular [[gas-mask|gas helmet]]. In this way we were able to

[009-047] stand the stuff.

[009-048] The [[gas-helmet|gas mask]], by the way, was the bane of my existence in the

[009-049] [[trench|trenches]]--one of the banes. I found that almost invariably after I

[009-050] had had mine on for a few minutes I got faint. Very often I would

[009-051] keel over entirely. A good many of the men were affected the same

[009-052] way, either from the lack of air inside the mask or by the

[009-053] influence of the chemicals with which the protector is impregnated.

[009-054] One of the closest calls I had in all my war experience was at

[009-055] Mills Street. And Fritz was not to blame.

[009-056] Several of the men, including myself, were squatted around a

[009-057] brazier cooking char and getting warm, for the nights were cold,

[009-058] when there was a terrific explosion. Investigation proved that an

[009-059] unexploded bomb had been buried under the brazier, and that it had

[009-060] gone off as the heat penetrated the ground. It is a wonder there

[009-061] weren't more of these accidents, as [[british-army|Tommy]] was forever throwing away

[009-062] his Millses.

[009-063] The Mills bomb fires by pulling out a pin which releases a lever

[009-064] which explodes the bomb after four seconds. Lots of men never

[009-065] really trust a bomb. If you have one in your pocket, you feel that

[009-066] the pin may somehow get out, and if it does you know that you'll go

[009-067] to glory in small bits. I always had that feeling myself and used

[009-068] to throw away my Millses and scoop a hatful of dirt over them with

[009-069] my foot.

[009-070] This particular bomb [[casualty|killed]] one man, wounded several, and shocked

[009-071] all of us. Two of the men managed to "swing" a "blighty" case out

[009-072] of it. I could have done the same if I had been wise enough.

[009-073] I think I ought to say a word right here about the psychology of

[009-074] the [[tommy|Tommy]] in swinging a "blighty" case.

[009-075] It is the one first, last, and always ambition of the Tommy to get

[009-076] back to [[blighty|Blighty]]. Usually he isn't "out there" because he wants to

[009-077] be but because he has to be. He is a patriot all right. His love of

[009-078] [[england|Blighty]] shows that. He will [[battle|fight]] like a bag of wildcats when he

[009-079] gets where the fighting is, but he isn't going around looking for

[009-080] trouble. He knows that his officers will find that for him

[009-081] a-plenty.

[009-082] When he gets letters from home and knows that the wife or the

[009-083] "nippers" or the old mother is sick, he wants to go home. And so he

[009-084] puts in his time hoping for a wound that will be "cushy" enough not

[009-085] to discommode him much and that will be bad enough to swing

[009-086] Blighty on. Sometimes when he wants very much to get back he

[009-087] stretches his conscience to the limit--and it is pretty elastic

[009-088] anyhow--and he fakes all sorts of illness. The M.O. is usually a

[009-089] bit too clever for Tommy, however, and out and out fakes seldom get

[009-090] by. Sometimes they do, and in the most unexpected cases.

[009-091] I had a man named Isadore Epstein in my section who was

[009-092] instrumental in getting Blighty for himself and one other. Issy was

[009-093] a tailor by trade. He was no fighting man and didn't pretend to be,

[009-094] and he didn't care who knew it. He was wild to get a "blighty one"

[009-095] or shell shock, or anything that would take him home.

[009-096] One morning as we were preparing to go [[over-the-top|over the top]], and the men

[009-097] were a little jumpy and nervous, I heard a shot behind me, and a

[009-098] bullet chugged into the [[sandbag|sandbags]] beside my head. I whirled around,

[009-099] my first thought being that some one of our own men was trying to

[009-100] do me in. This is a thing that sometimes happens to unpopular

[009-101] officers and less frequently to the men. But not in this case.

[009-102] It was Issy Epstein. He had been monkeying with his rifle and had

[009-103] shot himself in the hand. Of course, Issy was at once under

[009-104] suspicion of a self-inflicted wound, which is one of the worst

[009-105] crimes in the calendar. But the suspicion was removed instantly.

[009-106] Issy was hopping around, raising a terrific row.

[009-107] "Oi, oi," he wailed. "I'm ruint. I'm ruint. My thimble finger is

[009-108] gone. My thimble finger! I'm ruint. Oi, oi, oi, oi."

[009-109] The poor fellow was so sincerely desolated over the loss of his

[009-110] necessary finger that I couldn't accuse him of shooting himself

[009-111] intentionally. I detailed a man named Bealer to take Issy back to a

[009-112] [[first-aid|dressing station]]. Well, Bealer never came back.

[009-113] Months later in England I met up with Epstein and asked about

[009-114] Bealer. It seems that after Issy had been fixed up, the surgeon

[009-115] turned to Bealer and said:

[009-116] "What's the matter with you?"

[009-117] Bealer happened to be dreaming of something else and didn't answer.

[009-118] "I say," barked the doctor, "speak up. What's wrong?"

[009-119] Bealer was startled and jumped and begun to stutter.

[009-120] "Oh, I see," said the surgeon. "Shell shock."

[009-121] Bealer was bright enough and quick enough after that to play it up

[009-122] and was tagged for Blighty. He had it thrust upon him. And you can

[009-123] bet he grabbed it and thanked his lucky stars.

[009-124] We had been on Mill Street a day and a night when an order came for

[009-125] our company to move up to the second line and to be ready to go

[009-126] over the top the next day. At first there was the usual grousing,

[009-127] as there seemed to be no reason why our company should be picked

[009-128] from the whole battalion. We soon learned that all hands were going

[009-129] over, and after that we felt better.

[009-130] We got our equipment on and started up to the second line. It was

[009-131] right here that I got my first dose of real honest-to-goodness

[009-132] modern war. The big push had been on all summer, and the whole of

[009-133] the [[somme|Somme district]] was battered and smashed.

[009-134] Going up from Mill Street there were no [[communication-trench|communication trenches]]. We

[009-135] were right out in the open, exposed to rifle and [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]]

[009-136] and to shrapnel, and the Boches were fairly raining it in on the

[009-137] territory they had been pushed back from and of which they had the

[009-138] range to an inch. We went up under that steady fire for a full

[009-139] hour. The casualties were heavy, and the galling part of it was

[009-140] that we couldn't hurry, it was so dark. Every time a shell burst

[009-141] overhead and the shrapnel pattered in the dirt all about, I kissed

[009-142] myself good-by and thought of the baked beans at home. Men kept

[009-143] falling, and I wished I hadn't enlisted.

[009-144] When we finally got up to the trench, believe me, we didn't need

[009-145] any orders to get in. We relieved the Black Watch, and they

[009-146] encouraged us by telling us they had lost over half their men in

[009-147] that trench, and that Fritz kept a constant fire on it. They didn't

[009-148] need to tell us. The big boys were coming over all the time.

[009-149] The dead here were enough to give you the horrors. I had never seen

[009-150] so many before and never saw so many afterwards in one place. They

[009-151] were all over the place, both Germans and our own men. And in all

[009-152] states of mutilation and decomposition.

[009-153] There were arms and legs sticking out of the trench sides. You

[009-154] could tell their nationality by the uniforms. The Scotch

[009-155] predominated. And their dead lay in the trenches and outside and

[009-156] hanging over the edges. I think it was here that I first got the

[009-157] real meaning of that old quotation about the curse of a dead man's

[009-158] eye. With so many lying about, there were always eyes staring at

[009-159] you.

[009-160] Sometimes a particularly wide-staring corpse would seem to follow

[009-161] you with his gaze, like one of these posters with the pointing

[009-162] finger that they use to advertise Liberty Bonds. We would cover

[009-163] them up or turn them over. Here and there one would have a scornful

[009-164] death smile on his lips, as though he were laughing at the folly of

[009-165] the whole thing.

[009-166] The stench here was appalling. That frightful, sickening smell that

[009-167] strikes one in the face like something tangible. Ugh! I immediately

[009-168] grew dizzy and faint and had a mad desire to run. I think if I

[009-169] hadn't been a non-com with a certain small amount of responsibility

[009-170] to live up to, I should have gone crazy.

[009-171] I managed to pull myself together and placed my men as comfortably

[009-172] as possible. The Germans were five hundred yards away, and there

[009-173] was but little danger of an attack, so comparatively few had to

[009-174] "stand to." The rest took to the shelters.

[009-175] I found a little two-man shelter that everybody else had avoided

[009-176] and crawled in. I crowded up against a man in there and spoke to

[009-177] him. He didn't answer and then suddenly I became aware of a stench

[009-178] more powerful than ordinary. I put out my hand and thrust it into a

[009-179] slimy, cold mess. I had found a dead German with a gaping,

[009-180] putrefying wound in his abdomen. I crawled out of that shelter,

[009-181] gagging and retching. This time I simply couldn't smother my

[009-182] impulse to run, and run I did, into the next traverse, where I sank

[009-183] weak and faint on the fire step. I sat there the rest of the night,

[009-184] regardless of shells, my mind milling wildly on the problem of war

[009-185] and the reason thereof and cursing myself for a fool.

[009-187] It was very early in the morning when [[wellsie|Wells]] shook me up with, "Hi

[009-188] sye, Darby, wot the blinkin' blazes is that noise?"

[009-189] We listened, and away from the rear came a tremendous whirring,

[009-190] burring, rumbling buzz, like a swarm of giant bees. I thought of

[009-191] everything from a Zeppelin to a donkey engine but couldn't make it

[009-192] out. [[lieutenant-blofeld|Blofeld]] ran around the corner of a traverse and told us to get

[009-193] the men out. He didn't know what was coming and wasn't taking any

[009-194] chances.

[009-195] It was getting a little light though heavily misty. We waited, and

[009-196] then out of the gray blanket of fog waddled the great steel

[009-197] monsters that we were to know afterwards as the "[[tank|tanks]]." I shall

[009-198] never forget it.

[009-199] In the half darkness they looked twice as big as they really were.

[009-200] They lurched forward, slow, clumsy but irresistible, nosing down

[009-201] into shell holes and out, crushing the unburied dead, sliding over

[009-202] mere trenches as though they did not exist.

[009-203] There were five in all. One passed directly over us. We scuttled

[009-204] out of the way, and the men let go a cheer. For we knew that here

[009-205] was something that could and would win battles.

[009-206] The tanks were an absolutely new thing to us. Their secret had been

[009-207] guarded so carefully even in our own army that our battalion had

[009-208] heard nothing of them.

[009-209] But we didn't need to be told that they would be effective. One

[009-210] look was enough to convince us. Later it convinced Fritzie.
