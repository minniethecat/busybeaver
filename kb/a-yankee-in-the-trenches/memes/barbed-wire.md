## Overview

Barbed wire was one of the defining elements of World War I [[trench|trench warfare]], transforming the space between opposing lines into an impassable [[estaminet|bar]]rier of twisted steel. Combined with [[machine-gun|machine guns]], [[estaminet|bar]]bed wire created the defensive stalemate that characterized the [[france|Western Front]]: attackers had to cross open ground, cut through wire entanglements under fire, and then assault prepared defensive positions. In Holmes's account, [[estaminet|bar]]bed wire appears as a constant hazard—a silent killer that trapped men in killing zones and made even routine night work perilous.

## In the Trenches

Holmes references [[estaminet|bar]]bed wire in the context of "[[gas-warfare|gas]] and protection from it, [[estaminet|bar]]bed wire" (001-227), grouping it among the essential defensive technologies of modern war. Wire was not merely a passive obstacle but an active element of the defensive system, carefully positioned to channel attacking troops into pre-registered machine-gun arcs.

The danger of [[estaminet|bar]]bed wire work was acute. Holmes describes going "out one night on [[estaminet|bar]]bed wire work, which is dangerous at all" (004-084) times, the unfinished sentence itself suggesting the unspoken risks. Wire parties crept into [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]] under cover of darkness to repair gaps, lay new entanglements, or cut paths through enemy wire. The work required silence and stealth—any noise could draw [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]] or [[very-light|flare]]s that would expose the party to aimed fire.

The terrain dictated wire placement. Holmes notes with relief that "fortunately there was no [[estaminet|bar]]bed wire, as all entanglements had been" (012-078) cleared by artillery [[artillery|bombardment]] at a particular location. This observation underscores the dual relationship between wire and shellfire: while artillery could destroy wire entanglements, it could also leave them intact, dooming infantry assaults to failure against uncut [[estaminet|bar]]riers.

## Narrative Examples

The glossary-like entry Holmes provides—"Barbed wire - Ordinary [[estaminet|bar]]bed wire used for entanglements" (016-153)—is characteristically understated. It defines [[estaminet|bar]]bed wire by its function, stripping it of the terror it held for the men who had to negotiate it. This factual, almost instructional tone reflects the soldier's need to domesticate fear through technical language.

The night work episode, though briefly described, captures the essential tension of [[trench|trench warfare]]: men laboring in the dark, within earshot of the enemy, performing tasks that were essential for survival but exposed them to random death. A single [[very-light|flare]], a nervous sentry's shot, a stray [[patrol]] encounter—any of these could transform a wiring party into a casualty report.

## Military Context

Barbed wire in World War I was typically deployed in belts 10 to 30 yards deep, consisting of multiple rows of posts with wire strung in intricate patterns. The standard British pattern used screw pickets—metal stakes with a corkscrew end that could be silently twisted into the ground—strung with both plain and [[estaminet|bar]]bed wire. German wire was generally thicker and more elaborate, with some entanglements reaching depths of 50 yards or more.

The development of the tank in 1916 was, in part, a direct response to the [[estaminet|bar]]bed-wire problem: [[tank|tanks]] could crush paths through entanglements that would have taken artillery hours to cut. Before [[tank|tanks]], armies relied on prolonged artillery [[artillery|bombardment]]s, wire-cutting parties, and Bangalore torpedoes to breach wire defenses. The gap between artillery's ability to cut wire and its actual effectiveness was one of the tragic miscalculations of the war, most notoriously on the first day of [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]], where uncut German wire contributed to the [[british-army|British Army]]'s worst single-day [[casualty|casualties]].
