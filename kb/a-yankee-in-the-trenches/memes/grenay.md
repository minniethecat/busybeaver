## Overview

Bully-Grenay was a small [[france|French]] village of perhaps a thousand inhabitants, located less than three miles behind the British front lines opposite [[lens|Lens]]. By the time Holmes arrived, it had already been fought through in the early years of the war and was largely in ruins. Yet, remarkably, the [[germans|Germans]] rarely shelled it, making it a precious haven for troops rotating out of the [[trench|trenches]].

## In the Memoir

After the [[raid|trench raid]], Holmes's battalion was given two days' rest at Bully-Grenay:

> At Bully-Grenay, the point where we entered the communication trenches, guides met us and looked us over.
> (004-052)

The village, though battered, offered relative safety. The deep [[trench|communication trench]] leading to it provided cover:

> The communication trench from Bully-Grenay was very deep and was protected at points by little hills.
> (004-052)

## A Ruined Village

Holmes describes the village with a soldier's eye for detail:

> Bully-Grenay had been a place of perhaps one thousand people. It had been fought over and through and around early in the war.
> (004-053)

The houses were largely destroyed, but cellars and partial buildings still offered shelter. The [[germans|Germans]], for reasons unknown, generally spared the village from artillery:

> For some reason the Germans did not shell Bully-Grenay, that is, not often. Once in a while they would lob one in just to let us know they were there.
> (004-054)

## Rest and Farce

Those two days at Bully-Grenay were a strange interlude — officially rest, but with a comedic quality:

> Those two days in repose at Bully-Grenay were a good deal of a farce. We were entirely "on our own", it is true.
> (004-056)

After the filth and vermin of the front-line [[trench|trenches]], the village felt like luxury:

> There were no rats. It was oriental luxury after Bully-Grenay and the trenches, and for a wonder nobody had a word of "grousing" over anything.
> (005-011)
