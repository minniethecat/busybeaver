# Cootie

## Overview

The "cootie" — the body louse (*Pediculus humanus corporis*) — was, after the German Army, the [[tommy|Tommy]]'s most persistent and intimate enemy. Every man in the [[trench|trenches]] had cooties; those who claimed otherwise were liars or had not yet been in the line long enough. The cootie was the subject of constant complaint, dark humor, and elaborate ritualized [[hun]]ting. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir, the cootie makes its first appearance even before the author reaches the [[trench|trenches]]:

> I met the "cootie," who was to become so familiar in the trenches later on
> — (001-080)

## Ubiquity and Torment

The cootie was the soldier's worst enemy, worse even than [[boche|the Boche]], because [[boche|the Boche]] could at least be avoided or [[casualty|killed]], while the cootie was inescapable:

> The cootie is the soldier's worst enemy. He sticks closer than a brother
> — (004-165)–(004-166)

Holmes's description is both grim and grimly humorous. The cootie "stuck closer than a brother" — a dark play on the biblical phrase. Wherever the soldier went, the cootie went with him, nestled in the seams of his uniform, feeding, breeding, and driving him to distr[[battle|action]].

The cootie's habitat was the soldier's clothing — the thick wool of the service tunic, the lining of the greatcoat, the folds of the puttees. In the cold and damp of the [[trench|trenches]], where bathing was a rare luxury and changes of clothing were infrequent, the cootie population exploded.

## The Cootie Carnival

The ritual of delousing was known formally as a "shirt [[hun]]t" and colloquially as the "cootie carnival":

> After that comes the "cootie carnival", better known as the "shirt hunt"
> — (004-163)–(004-164)

The process was simple but laborious: the soldier removed his shirt, turned it inside out, and ran a candle flame or [[fag|cigarette]] lighter along the seams, burning the cooties and their eggs where they clustered. The popping sound of burning lice was as familiar a sound in the [[trench|trenches]] as the crack of rifle fire.

Holmes notes the almost festive quality of the cootie carnival — men gathered together, shirts off, [[hun]]ched over candles, united in their common war against the parasite. In a world where so much was beyond their control, killing cooties offered a small, satisfying victory.

The body louse was more than a nuisance — it was a vector of trench fever, a debilitating illness that sent thousands of men to hospital. But for most soldiers, the cootie's primary offense was psychological: the constant, maddening sensation of something crawling on the skin, the knowledge that one's own body had become a habitat for vermin.

## Names and Taxonomy

Holmes records that the cootie had multiple names:

> The cootie, or the "chat" as he is sometimes called
> — (002-193)

"[[chat|Chat]]" was an alternative term, possibly derived from the [[france|French]] *chat* (cat) as an ironic nickname for the parasitic insect. The linguistic creativity that produced "cootie," "chat," and the "cootie carnival" is characteristic of trench slang — a language that found dark humor in even the most miserable conditions.

The cootie even appeared in naval contexts before Holmes reached [[france|France]]:

> He wasn't called a cootie aboard ship, but he was the same fellow
> — (001-081)

The parasite was universal — whether on ship or in trench, it found the soldier and made itself at [[blighty|home]].
