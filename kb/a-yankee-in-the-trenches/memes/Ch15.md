[015-001] [[blighty|Blighty]] meant life,--life and happiness and physical comfort. What

[015-002] we had left behind over there was death and mutilation and bodily

[015-003] and mental suffering. Up from the depths of hell we came and

[015-004] reached out our hands with pathetic eagerness to the good things

[015-005] that [[england|Blighty]] had for us.

[015-006] I never saw a finer sight than the faces of those boys, glowing

[015-007] with love, as they strained their eyes for the first sight of the

[015-008] homeland. Those in the bunks below, unable to move, begged those on

[015-009] deck to come down at the first land raise and tell them how it all

[015-010] looked.

[015-011] A lump swelled in my throat, and I prayed that I might never go

[015-012] back to the [[trench|trenches]]. And I prayed, too, that the brave boys still

[015-013] over there might soon be out of it.

[015-014] We steamed into the harbor of Southampton early in the afternoon.

[015-015] Within an hour all of those that could walk had gone ashore. As we

[015-016] got into the waiting trains the civilian populace cheered. I, like

[015-017] everybody else I suppose, had dreamed often of coming back sometime

[015-018] as a hero and being greeted as a hero. But the cheering, though it

[015-019] came straight from the hearts of a grateful people, seemed, after

[015-020] all, rather hollow. I wanted to get somewhere and rest.

[015-021] It seemed good to look out of the windows and see the signs printed

[015-022] in English. That made it all seem less like a dream.

[015-023] I was taken first to the Clearing [[hospital|Hospital]] at Eastleigh. As we got

[015-024] off the train there the people cheered again, and among the

[015-025] civilians were many [[casualty|wounded]] men who had just recently come back.

[015-026] They knew how we felt.

[015-027] [Illustration: CORPORAL HOLMES WITH STAFF NURSE AND ANOTHER

[015-028] PATIENT, AT FULHAM MILITARY HOSPITAL, LONDON, S.W.]

[015-029] The first thing at the hospital was a real honest-to-God bath. _In

[015-030] a tub. With hot water!_ Heavens, how I wallowed. The orderly helped

[015-031] me and had to drag me out. I'd have stayed in that tub all night if

[015-032] he would have let me.

[015-033] Out of the tub I had clean things straight through, with a neat

[015-034] blue uniform, and for once was free of the [[cootie|cooties]]. The old

[015-035] uniform, blood-stained and ragged, went to the baking and

[015-036] disinfecting plant.

[015-037] That night all of us newly arrived men who could went to the

[015-038] [[ymca|Y.M.C.A.]] to a concert given in our honor. The chaplain came around

[015-039] and cheered us up and gave us good [[fag|fags]].

[015-040] Next morning I went around to the M.O. He looked my arm over and

[015-041] calmly said that it would have to come off as gangrene had set in.

[015-042] For a moment I wished that piece of shrapnel had gone through my

[015-043] head. I pictured myself going around with only one arm, and the

[015-044] prospect didn't look good.

[015-045] However, the doctor dressed the arm with the greatest care and told

[015-046] me I could go to a [[london|London]] hospital as I had asked, for I wanted to

[015-047] be near my people at Southall. These were the friends I had made

[015-048] before leaving Blighty and who had sent me weekly parcels and

[015-049] letters.

[015-050] I arrived in London on Tuesday and was taken in a big [[red-cross|Red Cross]]

[015-051] motor loaned by Sir Charles Dickerson to the Fulham Hospital in

[015-052] Hammersmith. I was overjoyed, as the hospital was very near

[015-053] Southall, and Mr. and Mrs. Puttee were both there to meet me.

[015-054] The Sister in charge of my ward, Miss Malin, is one of the finest

[015-055] women I have met. I owe it to her care and skill that I still have

[015-056] my good right arm. She has since married and the lucky man has one

[015-057] of the best of wives. Miss Malin advised me right at the beginning

[015-058] not to submit to an amputation.

[015-059] My next few weeks were pretty awful. I was in constant pain, and

[015-060] after the old arm began to come around under Miss Malin's treatment

[015-061] one of the doctors discovered that my left hand was queer. It had

[015-062] been somewhat swollen, but not really bad. The doctor insisted upon

[015-063] an X-ray and found a bit of shrapnel imbedded. He was all for an

[015-064] operation. Operations seemed to be the long suit of most of those

[015-065] doctors. I imagine they couldn't resist the temptation to get some

[015-066] practice with so much cheap material all about. I consented this

[015-067] time, and went down for the pictures on Lord Mayor's Day. Going to

[015-068] the pictures is [[british-army|Tommy]]'s expression for undergoing an anesthetic.

[015-069] I was under ether two hours and a half, and when I came out of it

[015-070] the left hand was all to the bad and has been ever since. There

[015-071] followed weeks of agonizing massage treatments. Between treatments

[015-072] though, I had it cushy.

[015-073] My friends were very good to me, and several Americans entertained

[015-074] me a good deal. I had a permanent walking-out pass good from nine

[015-075] in the morning until nine at night. I saw almost every show in the

[015-076] city, and heard a special performance of the Messiah at Westminster

[015-077] Abbey. Also I enjoyed a good deal of restaurant life.

[015-078] London is good to the wounded men. There is entertainment for all

[015-079] of them. A good many of these slightly wounded complain because

[015-080] they cannot get anything to drink, but undoubtedly it is the best

[015-081] thing for them. It is against the law to serve men in the blue

[015-082] uniform of the wounded. Men in [[uniform|khaki]] can buy all the liquor they

[015-083] want, the public houses being open from noon to two-thirty and from

[015-084] six P.M. to nine-thirty. Treating is not allowed. Altogether it

[015-085] works out very well and there is little drunkenness among the

[015-086] soldiers.

[015-087] I eventually brought up in a Convalescent Hospital in Brentford,

[015-088] Middlesex, and was there for three weeks. At the end of that time I

[015-089] was placed in category C 3.

[015-090] The system of marking the men in England is by categories, A, B,

[015-091] and C. A 1, 2, and 3 are for active service. A 4 is for the

[015-092] under-aged. B categories are for base service, and C is for home

[015-093] service. C 3 was for clerical duty, and as I was not likely to

[015-094] become efficient again as a soldier, it looked like some kind of

[015-095] bookkeeping for me for the duration of the war.

[015-096] Unless one is all shot to pieces, literally with something gone, it

[015-097] is hard to get a discharge from the British army. Back in the early

[015-098] days of 1915, a leg off was about the only thing that would produce

[015-099] a discharge.

[015-100] When I was put at clerical duty, I immediately began to furnish

[015-101] trouble for the British army, not intentionally, of course, but

[015-102] quite effectively. The first thing I did was to drop a typewriter

[015-103] and smash it. My hands had spells when they absolutely refused to

[015-104] work. Usually it was when I had something breakable in them. After

[015-105] I had done about two hundred dollars' damage indoors they tried me

[015-106] out as bayonet instructor. I immediately dropped a rifle on a

[015-107] concrete walk and smashed it. They wanted me to pay for it, but the

[015-108] M.O. called attention to the fact that I shouldn't have been put at

[015-109] the work under my category.

[015-110] [Illustration: CORPORAL HOLMES WITH COMPANY OFFICE FORCE, AT

[015-111] WINCHESTER, ENGLAND, A WEEK PRIOR TO DISCHARGE.]

[015-112] They then put me back at bookkeeping at Command Headquarters,

[015-113] Salisbury, but I couldn't figure English money and had a bad habit

[015-114] of fainting and falling off the high stool. To cap the climax, I

[015-115] finally fell one day and knocked down the stovepipe, and nearly set

[015-116] the office afire. The M.O. then ordered me back to the depot at

[015-117] Winchester and recommended me for discharge. I guess he thought it

[015-118] would be the cheapest in the long run.

[015-119] The adjutant at Winchester didn't seem any too pleased to see me.

[015-120] He said I looked as healthy as a wolf, which I did, and that they

[015-121] would never let me out of the army. He seemed to think that my

[015-122] quite normal appearance would be looked upon as a personal insult

[015-123] by the medical board. I said that I was sorry I didn't have a leg

[015-124] or two gone, but it couldn't be helped.

[015-125] While waiting for the Board, I was sent to the German Prison Camp

[015-126] at Winnal Downs as corporal of the permanent guard. I began to fear

[015-127] that at last they had found something that I could do without

[015-128] damaging anything, and my visions of the U.S.A. went a-glimmering.

[015-129] I was with the Fritzies for over a week, and they certainly have it

[015-130] soft and cushy.

[015-131] They have as good food as the Tommies. They are paid ninepence a

[015-132] day, and the work they do is a joke. They are well housed and kept

[015-133] clean and have their own canteens, where they can buy almost

[015-134] anything in the way of delicacies. They are decently treated by the

[015-135] English soldiers, who even buy them fags out of their own money.

[015-136] The nearest thing I ever saw to humiliation of a German was a few

[015-137] good-natured jokes at their expense by some of the wits in the

[015-138] guard. The English know how to play fair with an enemy when they

[015-139] have him down.

[015-140] I had about given up hope of ever getting out of the army when I

[015-141] was summoned to appear before the Travelling Medical Board. You can

[015-142] wager I lost no time in appearing.

[015-143] The board looked me over with a discouraging and cynical suspicion.

[015-144] I certainly did look as rugged as a navvy. When they gave me a

[015-145] going over, they found that my heart was out of place and that my

[015-146] left hand might never limber up again. They voted for a discharge

[015-147] in jig time. I had all I could do to keep from howling with joy.

[015-148] It was some weeks before the final formalities were closed up. The

[015-149] pension board passed on my case, and I was given the magnificent

[015-150] sum of sixteen shillings and sixpence a week, or $3.75. I spent the

[015-151] next few weeks in visiting my friends and, eventually, at the 22nd

[015-152] Headquarters at Bermondsey, London, S.C., received the papers that

[015-153] once more made me a free man.

[015-154] The papers read in part, "He is discharged in consequence of

[015-155] paragraph 392, King's Rules and Regulations. No longer fit for

[015-156] service." In another part of the book you will find a reproduction

[015-157] of the character discharge also given. The discharged man also

[015-158] receives a little silver badge bearing the inscription, "For King

[015-159] and Empire, Services Rendered." I think that I value this badge

[015-160] more than any other possession.

[015-161] Once free, I lost no time in getting my passport into shape and

[015-162] engaged a passage on the _St. Paul_, to sail on the second of June.

[015-163] Since my discharge is dated the twenty-eighth of May, you can see

[015-164] that I didn't waste any time. My friends at Southall thought I was

[015-165] doing things in a good deal of a hurry. The fact is, I was fed up

[015-166] on war. I had had a plenty. And I was going to make my get-away

[015-167] before the British War Office changed its mind and got me back in

[015-168] uniform. Mrs. Puttee and her eldest son saw me off at Euston

[015-169] Station. Leaving them was the one wrench, as they had become very

[015-170] dear to me. But I had to go. If Blighty had looked good, the

[015-171] thought of the U.S.A. was better.

[015-172] My passage was uneventful. No submarines, no bad weather, nothing

[015-173] disagreeable. On the eighth day I looked out through a welter of

[015-174] fog and rain to the place where the Statue of Liberty should have

[015-175] been waving a greeting across New York harbor. The lady wasn't

[015-176] visible, but I knew she was there. And even in a downpour equal to

[015-177] anything furnished by the choicest of Flanders rainstorms, little

[015-178] old New York looked better than anything I could imagine, except

[015-179] sober and staid old [[boston|Boston]].

[015-180] That I am at home, safe and free of the horrors of war, is to me a

[015-181] strange thing. I think it comes into the experience of most of the

[015-182] men who have been over there and who have been invalided out of the

[015-183] service. Looking back on the awfulness of the trenches and the

[015-184] agonies of mind and body, the sacrifice seems to fade into

[015-185] insignificance beside the satisfaction of having done a bit in the

[015-186] great and just cause.

[015-187] Now that our own men are going over, I find myself with a very deep

[015-188] regret that I cannot go too. I can only wish them the best of luck

[015-189] and rest in confidence that every man will do his uttermost.
