## Overview

The **trench periscope** was an essential observation tool in World War I [[trench|trench warfare]], allowing soldiers to survey [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]] and enemy positions without exposing their heads above the [[parapet]]. Periscopes ranged from simple mirror-on-a-stick devices to sophisticated periscope-telescope [[rifle|rifles]] used by [[sniper|snipers]]. The periscope mirror also served everyday purposes, with soldiers using it for shaving and personal grooming while under fire.

## In the Trenches

Periscopes were deployed along the entire front-line trench system. Fixed periscope positions were manned by designated "periscope watchers" who maintained continuous observation of the German lines. Any movement spotted—a dangling [[tin-hat|helmet]], a stirring corpse—drew immediate attention and irritation from the watchers. The periscope's dual function as both tactical inst[[rum-ration|rum]]ent and daily utility item made it ubiquitous in trench life: a soldier might scan for enemy movements one moment and use the same mirror to shave the next.

> More than once I have shaved in a periscope mirror pegged into the side of a trench, with the bullets snapping overhead, and rubbed my face with wet tea leaves afterward to freshen up. [004-157]

## Narrative Examples

Holmes describes his first encounter with a trench periscope as a harrowing introduction to the risks of observation:

> I remember the first time I looked through a periscope I had no sooner thrust the thing up than a bullet crashed into the upper mirror, splintering it. Many times I have stuck up a cap on a stick and had it pierced. [002-093]

The view through a periscope revealed the desolate landscape of [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]]—[[estaminet|bar]]bed-[[barbed-wire|wire entanglements]], [[crater|shell craters]], and the ever-present German parapet beyond. As Holmes notes, "looking through a periscope, you wouldn't see much" in terms of visible activity, for daylight hours rendered the contested ground eerily still. [007-005]

The periscope watchers were sensitive to any anomalies in their field of view. Holmes recounts how a German corpse's booted leg stuck up from the mud near his trench, and when a "joker on [[patrol]] [[hun]]g a [[tin-hat|helmet]] on the foot," the dangling [[tin-hat|helmet]] so irritated the watchers that it was removed the following night. [007-021]

In a practical note to prospective soldiers, Holmes advises against bringing elaborate shaving mirrors: "You'll use the periscope mirror in the [[trench|trenches]]." [016-118]

## Military Context

The periscope-telescope rifle represented a significant technological development in trench [[sniper|sniping]]. The German army had a particular advantage in this area:

> The Boche sniper is nearly always armed with a periscope-telescope rifle. This is a specially built super-accurate rifle mounted on a periscope frame. It is thrust up over the parapet and the image of the opposing parapet is cast on a little ground-glass screen on which are two crossed lines. At one hundred fifty yards or less the image is brought up to touching distance seemingly. Fritz simply trains his piece on some low place or anywhere that a head may be expected. When one appears on the screen, he pulls the trigger,—and you "click it" if you happen to be on the other or receiving end. The shooter never shows himself. [002-084] [002-085]

The British [[sniper]], by contrast, used a plain telescope rifle and had to conceal himself behind masonry, tree trunks, or improvised camouflage—a more dangerous proposition. The periscope rifle thus gave German marksmen a significant tactical edge in the static warfare of the [[france|Western Front]].
