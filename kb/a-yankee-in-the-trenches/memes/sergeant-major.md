# Sergeant Major

## Overview

The Sergeant Major appears at several points in Holmes's memoir — an unnamed but distinct figure whose [[dug-out]] marked the front line, whose [[rum-ration|rum issue]] sustained the men, and whose death in the final offensive was one of the first and hardest blows. Though never given a name, he is one of the memoir's more fully realized secondary characters.

## Role in the Narrative

Holmes first encounters him on arrival at the [[trench|front trench]]. The Sergeant Major emerges from his [[dug-out]] — a detail Holmes notes as characteristic:

> Arrived in the front trench, the sergeant-major appeared, crawling out of his dug-out — the usual place for a sergeant-major — and greeted us with, "Keep your nappers down, you rooks."
> — (002-070)

The greeting is practical and paternal: keep your heads down, you rookies. It is the voice of experience.

In the hours before the final attack, the Sergeant Major issues the [[rum-ration|rum]] ration — a ritual of trench life and the last such ration he would ever serve:

> I got an extra large supply, as the Sergeant Major was good humored. It was the last rum he ever served.
> — (013-210)

When the attack begins, the Sergeant Major is talking with [[captain-green|Captain Green]] when a shell bursts behind them:

> down went Captain Green and the Sergeant Major with whom he had been talking. Captain Green died a few days later at Rouen, and the Sergeant Major lost an arm.
> — (014-033)

The loss of the Sergeant Major, along with [[captain-green|Captain Green]], is described as "a hard blow right at the start" — it "spelled disaster." The Sergeant Major, the steady presence from the [[dug-out]], was gone in the opening minutes.
