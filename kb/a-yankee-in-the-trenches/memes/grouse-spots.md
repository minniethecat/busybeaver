## Overview

The Grouse Spots were a section of British front-line [[trench|trenches]] in the [[vimy|Vimy Ridge]] sector. The name was coined by the Tommies themselves — soldiers' slang for a position where there was plenty to complain about. It was here that Holmes conducted some of his most daring solo [[patrol|patrol work]], venturing into [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]] under cover of darkness to scout German positions.

## In the Memoir

The name was soldier humor, plain and simple:

> The Tommies called the trenches Grouse Spots. It was a good name.
> (006-135)

"Grouse" was British military slang for "complain," and these [[trench|trenches]] evidently gave the men ample reason to do so.

## Patrol Operations

After moving to the Grouse Spots, [[patrol]] orders came down. Holmes, who had developed a fascination with [[patrol|patrol work]], volunteered:

> In our new positions in the Grouse Spots the orders were to patrol.
> (006-147)

Holmes conducted solo [[patrol]]s from these positions — dangerous missions that required crawling through shell holes in [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]], sometimes within yards of German sentries, to gather intelligence on enemy positions and movements.

## Relief

The battalion spent two days in the Grouse Spots before being relieved:

> We were two days in the Grouse Spots and were then relieved, going back to rest billets.
> (007-155)

Holmes reflects on how quickly such places could come to feel almost familiar, even as they remained deadly:

> I seem to have wandered far from the Quarries and the Grouse Spots.
> (007-153)

## Significance

The Grouse Spots represent the routine yet perilous nature of [[trench|trench warfare]] — holding a section of line, [[patrol]]ling [[no-mans-land|No Man's Land]], and waiting for relief. For Holmes, these [[trench|trenches]] were where he honed the [[patrol]] skills that would later make him a valued scout and [[grenade|bomb]]ing party member.
