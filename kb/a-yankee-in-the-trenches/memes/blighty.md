# Blighty

## Overview

"Blighty" was the [[tommy|British soldier]]'s word for [[england|England]] — and, by extension, for home, safety, and everything that lay beyond the mud and horror of the [[trench|trenches]]. Derived from the Hindustani *vilayati* ("foreign," "European"), the word entered British military slang through the Indian Army and spread throughout the ranks during World War I. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir, Blighty is the constant, aching reference point — the place every soldier dreamed of reaching, whether on leave, through a wound, or at war's end.

> Take me back to dear old Blighty
> — (002-135)

The line, quoted from a popular trench song, captures the universal longing of the men in the line. Blighty was not just a geographic location but an emotional state — the opposite of the [[trench|trenches]].

## As a Destination

For the soldier in [[france|France]], Blighty was impossibly distant yet constantly present in conversation and imagination. Holmes records the ritual of departure:

> they take their last look at Blighty, and they take it rather solemnly
> — (001-193)

The "last look" — the final glimpse of [[england|England]] from the troopship before crossing the Channel — was a moment freighted with the awareness that some men would never see it again.

Fresh arrivals from [[england|England]] — "just out from Blighty" — were objects of curiosity and gentle mockery from the veterans:

> a whipper-snapper just out from Blighty with no trench experience
> — (002-123)

The new arrivals, clean and eager, had not yet learned the ways of the trench. The veterans, caked in mud and hardened by experience, regarded them with a mixture of pity and condescension.

## The Blighty Wound

The most coveted thing in the [[trench|trenches]] — more than food, more than tobacco, more than [[rum-ration|rum]] — was the "[[blighty-wound|Blighty wound]]": an injury serious enough to warrant evacuation to [[england|England]] but not serious enough to kill or permanently disable. Holmes describes the calculus:

> It is the constant, and always ambition of the Tommy to get a "Blighty"
> — (009-075)

This was not cowardice but a rational response to an irrational situation. A [[blighty-wound|Blighty wound]] meant clean sheets, proper food, [[first-aid|medical care]], and — most importantly — survival. Men spoke openly of hoping for one, a fact that shocked civilians but made perfect sense to anyone who had spent time in the line.

Holmes documents a specific case:

> the Tommy in swinging a "blighty" case
> — (009-074)

"swinging a blighty case" meant managing to get oneself classified for evacuation to [[england|England]] — whether through genuine injury or, as sometimes happened, through the soldier's own efforts to ensure the wound was deemed serious enough.

## The Song

Blighty was immortalized in the soldiers' own music — the unofficial songs that traveled through the [[trench|trenches]] faster than any official communication. Holmes records a fragment:

> Take me back to dear old Blighty,
> Put me on the train for London town
> — (002-135)

The song, sung in the [[dug-out]]s and on the [[marching|march]], was a collective expression of the [[tommy|Tommy]]'s deepest wish. The irony — that men sang about Blighty while living in conditions as far from "dear old" [[england|England]] as it was possible to imagine — was not lost on anyone.

## The Real Thing

When a soldier finally reached Blighty — whether through leave or wound — the experience was overwhelming. Holmes describes the moment of arrival:

> Blighty! Blighty!! Blighty!!!
> — (014-162)

The triple exclamation, each more intense than the last, captures the overwhelming relief of the soldier who has survived and is going home. For Holmes, this moment came through his own [[blighty-wound|Blighty wound]] — a shell fragment that ended his war and sent him back across the Channel.
