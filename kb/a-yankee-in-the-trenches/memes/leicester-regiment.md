# Leicester Regiment

## Overview

The Leicester Regiment (formally the Leicestershire Regiment) was a line infantry regiment of the [[british-army|British Army]] that served on the [[france|Western Front]] during World War I. Like many county regiments, it expanded massively during the war, raising numerous service [[battalion|battalions]]. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, the regiment appears not as an abstract unit but through the person of a single soldier: Private Crane, a young Leicester man who, after his NCOs were put out of [[battle|action]], was acting as corporal while escorting German prisoners rearward from [[battle-of-the-somme|the Somme]] battlefield.

## In the Memoir

Holmes encountered the Leicester soldier during the confused aftermath of an attack. He was herding his own group of German prisoners toward the rear when he came upon another party of about twelve prisoners:

> in charge of a young fellow from a Leicester regiment. He was a private, and as most of his non-commissioned officers had been put out of action, he was acting corporal.
> — (011-063)

The two men fell in together behind the prisoners, swapping notes on the [[battle|fight]]. But one of the German prisoners — an officer, judging by his age probably a lieutenant — abruptly sat down and refused to go any farther (011-063). What followed was a tense confrontation:

> Finally Crane — that was the Leicester chap — went up to the officer, threatened him with his bayonet, and let him know that he was due for the cold steel if he didn't get up and hike.
> — (011-070)

The German officer responded by pulling a concealed [[pistol|automatic pistol]] — he had evidently not been searched thoroughly — and firing at Crane. The shot tore away Crane's right cheek, but he did not go down. Before Holmes could intervene without endangering his comrade, Crane "polished off the officer on the spot" (011-070). The episode, grim as it was, served Holmes as an illustration of the "foolishness of trusting a German — particularly an officer" (011-063).

## Character and Significance

Private Crane embodies the junior leadership that emerged organically in the [[trench|trenches]]. With his NCOs out of [[battle|action]], he stepped into the corporal's role — commanding prisoners, making decisions, and ultimately facing down an armed enemy officer with nothing but a bayonet and nerve. His story, though brief in the memoir, captures the reality that regiments were defined less by their official designations than by the individual courage of the men who filled their ranks.
