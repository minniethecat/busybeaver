[003-001] In the morning the members of the [[trench-raid|raiding party]] were taken back a

[003-002] mile or so to the rear and were given instruction and rehearsal.

[003-003] This was the first raid that "Batt" had ever tried, and the staff

[003-004] was anxious to have it a success. There were fifty in the party,

[003-005] and [[lieutenant-blofeld|Blofeld]], who had organized the raid, beat our instructions into

[003-006] us until we knew them by heart.

[003-007] The object of a raid is to get into the enemy's [[trench|trenches]] by stealth

[003-008] if possible, kill as many as possible, take prisoners if

[003-009] practicable, do a lot of damage, and get away with a whole hide.

[003-010] We got back to the front trenches just before dark. I noticed a lot

[003-011] of metal cylinders arranged along the parapet. They were about as

[003-012] big as a stovepipe and four feet long, painted brown. They were the

[003-013] [[gas-warfare|gas]] containers. They were arranged about four or five to a

[003-014] traverse, and were connected up by tubes and were covered with

[003-015] [[sandbag|sandbags]]. This was the [[gas-weapon|poison gas]] ready for release [[over-the-top|over the top]]

[003-016] through tubes.

[003-017] [Illustration: A HEAVY HOWITZER, UNDER CAMOUFLAGE. Copyright, by

[003-018] Underwood & Underwood, N.Y.]

[003-019] The time set for our stunt was eleven P.M. Eleven o'clock was

[003-020] "[[zero]]." The system on the [[france|Western Front]], and, in fact, all fronts,

[003-021] is to indicate the time fixed for any event as zero. Anything

[003-022] before or after is spoken of as plus or minus zero.

[003-023] Around five o'clock we were taken back to Mechanics trench and

[003-024] fed--a regular meal with plenty of everything, and all good. It

[003-025] looked rather like giving a condemned man a hearty meal, but grub

[003-026] is always acceptable to a soldier.

[003-027] After that we blacked our faces. This is always done to prevent the

[003-028] whiteness of the skin from showing under the [[very-light|flare]] lights. Also to

[003-029] distinguish your own men when you get to [[boche|the Boche]] trench.

[003-030] Then we wrote letters and gave up our identification discs and were

[003-031] served with persuader sticks or knuckle knives, and with "[[grenade|Mills]]"

[003-032] bombs.

[003-033] The persuader is a short, heavy bludgeon with a nail-studded head.

[003-034] You thump [[germans|Fritz]] on the head with it. Very handy at close quarters.

[003-035] The knuckle knife is a short dagger with a heavy brass hilt that

[003-036] covers the hand. Also very good for close work, as you can either

[003-037] strike or stab with it.

[003-038] We moved up to the front trenches at about half-past ten. At zero

[003-039] minus ten, that is, ten minutes of eleven, our artillery opened up.

[003-040] It was the first [[artillery|bombardment]] I had ever been under, and it seemed

[003-041] as though all the guns in the world were banging away. Afterwards I

[003-042] found that it was comparatively light, but it didn't seem so then.

[003-043] The guns were hardly started when there was a sound like escaping

[003-044] steam. Jerry leaned over and shouted in my ear: "There goes the

[003-045] gas. May it finish the blighters."

[003-046] Blofeld came dashing up just then, very much excited because he

[003-047] found we had not put on our masks, through some slip-up in the

[003-048] orders. We got into them quick. But as it turned out there was no

[003-049] need. There was a fifteen-mile wind blowing, which carried the gas

[003-050] away from us very rapidly. In fact it blew it across the Boche

[003-051] trenches so fast that it didn't bother them either.

[003-052] The [[barrage|barrage fire]] kept up right up to zero, as per schedule. At

[003-053] thirty seconds of eleven I looked at my watch and the din was at

[003-054] its height. At exactly eleven it stopped short. Fritz was still

[003-055] sending some over, but comparatively there was silence. After the

[003-056] ear-splitting racket it was almost still enough to hurt.

[003-057] And in that silence over the top we went.

[003-058] Lanes had been cut through our [[barbed-wire|wire]], and we got through them

[003-059] quickly. The trenches were about one hundred twenty yards apart and

[003-060] we still had nearly one hundred to go. We dropped and started to

[003-061] crawl. I skinned both my knees on something, probably old wire, and

[003-062] both hands. I could feel the blood running into my puttees, and my

[003-063] rifle bothered me as I was afraid of jabbing Jerry, who was just

[003-064] ahead of me as first [[bayonet|bayonet man]].

[003-065] They say a drowning man or a man in great danger reviews his past.

[003-066] I didn't. I spent those few minutes wondering when the machine-gun

[003-067] fire would come.

[003-068] I had the same "gone" feeling in the pit of the stomach that you

[003-069] have when you drop fast in an elevator. The skin on my face felt

[003-070] tight, and I remember that I wanted to pucker my nose and pull my

[003-071] upper lip down over my teeth.

[003-072] We got clean up to their wire before they spotted us. Their

[003-073] entanglements had been flattened by our barrage fire, but we had to

[003-074] get up to pick our way through, and they saw us.

[003-075] Instantly the "Very" lights began to go up in scores, and hell

[003-076] broke loose. They must have turned twenty [[machine-gun|machine guns]] on us, or at

[003-077] us, but their aim evidently was high, for they only "clicked" two

[003-078] out of our immediate party. We had started with ten men, the other

[003-079] fifty being divided into three more parties farther down the line.

[003-080] When the machine guns started, we charged. Jerry and I were ahead

[003-081] as bayonet men, with the rest of the party following with buckets

[003-082] of "Mills" bombs and "[[stokes-mortar|Stokeses]]."

[003-083] It was pretty light, there were so many flares going up from both

[003-084] sides. When I jumped on the parapet, there was a whaling big Boche

[003-085] looking up at me with his rifle resting on the sandbags. I was

[003-086] almost on the point of his bayonet.

[003-087] For an instant I stood with a kind of paralyzed sensation, and

[003-088] there flashed through my mind the instructions of the manual for

[003-089] such a situation, only I didn't apply those instructions to this

[003-090] emergency.

[003-091] Instead I thought--if such a flash could be called thinking--how I,

[003-092] as an instructor, would have told a rookie to act, working on a

[003-093] dummy. I had a sort of detached feeling as though this was a silly

[003-094] dream.

[003-095] Probably this hesitation didn't last more than a second.

[003-096] Then, out of the corner of my eye, I saw Jerry lunge, and I lunged

[003-097] too. Why that Boche did not fire I don't know. Perhaps he did and

[003-098] missed. Anyhow I went down and in on him, and the bayonet went

[003-099] through his throat.

[003-100] Jerry had done his man in and all hands piled into the trench.

[003-101] Then we started to race along the [[traverse|traverses]]. We found a machine

[003-102] gun and put an eleven-pound high-explosive "Stokes" under it. Three

[003-103] or four Germans appeared, running down [[communication-trench|communication trenches]], and

[003-104] the bombers sent a few Millses after them. Then we came to a

[003-105] [[dug-out]] door--in fact, several, as Fritz, like a woodchuck, always

[003-106] has more than one entrance to his burrow. We broke these in in jig

[003-107] time and looked down a thirty-foot hole on a [[dug-out]] full of

[003-108] graybacks. There must have been a lot of them. I could plainly see

[003-109] four or five faces looking up with surprised expressions.

[003-110] Blofeld chucked in two or three Millses and away we went.

[003-111] A little farther along we came to the entrance of a mine shaft, a

[003-112] kind of incline running toward our lines. Blofeld went in it a

[003-113] little way and flashed his light. He thought it was about forty

[003-114] yards long. We put several of our remaining Stokeses in that and

[003-115] wrecked it.

[003-116] Turning the corner of the next traverse, I saw Jerry drop his rifle

[003-117] and unlimber his persuader on a huge German who had just rounded

[003-118] the corner of the "bay." He made a good job of it, getting him in

[003-119] the face, and must have simply caved him in, but not before he had

[003-120] thrown a bomb. I had broken my bayonet prying the dug-out door off

[003-121] and had my gun up-ended--clubbed.

[003-122] [Illustration: OVER THE TOP ON A RAID. Photograph from Underwood &

[003-123] Underwood, N.Y.]

[003-124] When I saw that bomb coming, I bunted at it like Ty Cobb trying to

[003-125] sacrifice. It was the only thing to do. I choked my bat and poked

[003-126] at the bomb instinctively, and by sheer good luck fouled the thing

[003-127] over the parapet. It exploded on the other side.

[003-128] "Blimme eyes," says Jerry, "that's cool work. You saved us the

[003-129] wooden cross that time."

[003-130] We had found two more machine guns and were planting Stokeses under

[003-131] them when we heard the Lewises giving the recall signal. A good

[003-132] gunner gets so he can play a tune on a Lewis, and the device is

[003-133] frequently used for signals. This time he thumped out the old

[003-134] one--"All policemen have big feet." Rat-a-tat-tat--tat, tat.

[003-135] It didn't come any too soon.

[003-136] As we scrambled over the parapet we saw a big party of Germans

[003-137] coming up from the second trenches. They were out of the

[003-138] communication trenches and were coming across lots. There must have

[003-139] been fifty of them, outnumbering us five or six to one.

[003-140] We were out of bombs, Jerry had lost his rifle, and mine had no

[003-141] "ammo." Blofeld fired the last shot from his [[pistol|revolver]] and, believe

[003-142] me, we hooked it for [[blighty|home]].

[003-143] We had been in their trenches just three and a half minutes.

[003-144] Just as we were going through their wire a bomb exploded near and

[003-145] got Jerry in the head. We dragged him in and also the two men that

[003-146] had been clicked on the first fire. Jerry got [[england|Blighty]] on his wound,

[003-147] but was back in two months. The second time he wasn't so lucky. He

[003-148] lies now somewhere in France with a wooden cross over his head.

[003-149] Did that muddy old trench look good when we tumbled in? Oh, Boy!

[003-150] The staff was tickled to pieces and complimented us all. We were

[003-151] sent out of the lines that night and in [[billet|billets]] got hot food,

[003-152] high-grade "[[fag|fags]]", a real bath, a good stiff [[rum-ration|rum]] ration, and

[003-153] letters from home.

[003-154] Next morning we heard the results of the raid. One party of twelve

[003-155] never returned. Besides that we lost seven men [[casualty|killed]]. The German

[003-156] loss was estimated at about one hundred casualties, six machine

[003-157] guns and several dug-outs destroyed, and one mine shaft put out of

[003-158] business. We also brought back documents of value found by one

[003-159] party in an officer's dug-out.

[003-160] Blofeld got the military cross for the night's work, and several of

[003-161] the enlisted men got the D.C.M.

[003-162] Altogether it was a successful raid. The best part of it was

[003-163] getting back.
