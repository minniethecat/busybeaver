# Fritz

**[[germans|Fritz]]** (plural: [[germans|Fritz]]es or [[germans|Fritz]]ies) was the most widely used [[tommy|British soldier]]'s nickname for the German enemy during the First World War. Unlike the more hostile "[[boche|Hun]]" or "[[boche|Boche]]," "[[germans|Fritz]]" often carried a degree of familiar, almost personal quality — the [[germans|German soldier]] as a fellow sufferer in the [[trench|trenches]] rather than a faceless enemy.

## Usage in the Narrative

Holmes uses "[[germans|Fritz]]" throughout the memoir, personifying the German enemy as a constant, almost neighborly presence across [[no-mans-land|no man's land]]:

> "But there's always a Fritzie over yonder with a…" [002-078]

[[germans|Fritz]] was methodical and predictable in his habits:

> "Fritz simply trains his piece on some part of the…" [002-089]

The term captures the intimate, personal nature of [[trench|trench warfare]] — the enemy was close enough to feel like a neighbor:

> "You thump Fritz on the head with it. Very handy…" [003-034]

Holmes describes [[germans|Fritz]]'s defensive alertness:

> "…Fritz, like a woodchuck, always…" [003-105]

After the British [[trench-raid|raid]]ed the German [[trench|trenches]], [[germans|Fritz]] retaliated:

> "…after the strafing we had given Fritz on the raid, he behaved…" [004-001]

The German response was immediate and professional:

> "…the 'typewriters' a-rattling. Fritz was right on the job with…" [004-007]

Holmes acknowledges German competence:

> "…defensive work was concerned, Fritz was…" [004-014]

## The Familiar Enemy

"[[germans|Fritz]]" represents a distinctive feature of [[trench|trench warfare]]: proximity bred a strange familiarity. The German line was often only yards away. Soldiers could hear [[germans|Fritz]] talking, coughing, cooking. They learned his routines, respected his [[sniper|snipers]], and recognized his professionalism. This was not the dehumanized enemy of propaganda posters — it was the soldier on the other side of the [[sandbag|sandbags]].
