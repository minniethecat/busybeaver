## Overview

**"Minnies"** (singular: "Minnie") was the British [[tommy]]'s slang term for [[shell|shells]] fired by the German _Minenwerfer_ ("mine thrower"), a short-range [[stokes-mortar|trench mortar]]. These high-trajectory projectiles were lobbed from the German lines and dropped almost vertically into the British [[trench|trenches]], making them especially dangerous—unlike conventional [[artillery]] [[shell|shells]] that followed a flatter trajectory and gave some warning, minnies seemed to appear from directly overhead with little time to take cover. Along with the smaller "[[pip-squeak]]," the minnie was one of the most dreaded sounds of [[trench|trench warfare]].

## In the Trenches

The minnie's effectiveness came from its high-angle trajectory, which allowed it to drop behind [[parapet|parapets]] that protected against rifle and [[machine-gun|machine-gun fire]]. A minnie attack was often sudden and devastating—the shell's relatively slow flight meant it could be seen approaching if one happened to be looking up, but its near-vertical descent gave men in the trench almost no lateral cover. Holmes records that a particularly quiet sector was characterized by the German artillery being "meek," sending over "very few 'minnies' or rifle grenades." [004-015] The implication is clear: a sector without minnies was an unusually peaceful one.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes mentions minnies in the context of regular harassing fire. The [[germans|Germans]] were "in the habit of sending over 'minnies' at intervals just to let us know they were awake"—a form of psychological warfare that denied British troops any true respite even in supposedly quiet periods. [006-118]

In a detailed passage cataloguing the sounds of the front, Holmes distinguishes the minnie from other projectiles by its distinctive flight characteristics and places it alongside the "[[pip-squeak]]," described as "a shell that sounds like its name"—a small, whining projectile. The taxonomy of German [[shell|shells]] was a matter of survival for the trench soldier, who learned to identify each type by sound and take appropriate cover. [007-205]

## Military Context

The German _Minenwerfer_ came in several calibers—light (7.58 cm), medium (17 cm), and heavy (25 cm)—each delivering increasingly devastating payloads at short to medium range. The weapon was developed after the initial mobile phase of the war gave way to static trench lines, where conventional artillery's flat trajectory was less effective against deeply dug positions. The British developed their own [[stokes-mortar|trench mortar]]s in response, notably the [[stokes-mortar|Stokes]] mortar, but "minnie" remained the soldiers' term specifically for the German weapon and its projectiles. The psychological impact of the minnie was disproportionate to its actual casualty count—the sense of vulnerability it created, dropping death from directly above into what was supposed to be the safety of the trench, made it one of the more demoralizing weapons of the war.
