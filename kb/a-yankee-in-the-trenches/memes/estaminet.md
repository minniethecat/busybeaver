## Overview

The **_estaminet_** was a small [[france|French]] bar or cafe that served as the primary social institution for British and Allied soldiers during rest periods behind the lines in World War I. These establishments, typically run by [[france|French]] civilians in [[billet]] towns, offered beer, _vin blanc_ (white wine), and a space for soldiers to escape the monotony and horror of trench life. For the [[tommy]], the estaminet was more than a drinking establishment—it was where card games were played, songs were sung, and the brief respite of "rest" was truly felt.

## In the Trenches

Soldiers did not visit estaminets while stationed in the front-line [[trench|trenches]]. The estaminet belonged to the rhythm of [[billet]] life—the rotation between front-line duty, support positions, and rest [[billet|billets]]. When a unit was pulled back to a [[france|French]] village for "rest" (which often meant mending roads or other [[trench-fatigue|fatigue duties]]), the estaminet became the center of off-duty hours. Holmes notes that at Petite-Saens the rest was unusually "cushy," and most soldiers spent their time "either in the [[ymca|Y.M.C.A. hut]] or in the _estaminets_." [005-133]

The estaminet's atmosphere varied by location, but certain patterns were universal:

> But in any _estaminet_ in a billet town you'll find one or all of them in progress all the time. The winner usually spends his winnings for beer, so the money all goes the same way, game or no game. [005-184]

## Narrative Examples

Holmes's account is rich with estaminet scenes. During one rest period, he and his comrades spent most of a day at an estaminet across from their [[billet|billets]], where "a pretty mademoiselle" served "rotten [[france|French]] beer and _vin blanc_" while the Tommies attempted their broken [[france|French]] on her—"They might as well have talked Choctaw." [004-058]

The estaminet was also the stage for impromptu entertainment. Rolfe, a "merry young nuisance" in Holmes's platoon with "a voice like a frog," would climb onto the estaminet table and launch into song, much to the mixed amusement and exasperation of his comrades. [005-184]

Evening hours up to nine o'clock were "usually spent in the _estaminets_," where the games of house, [[nap]], and vingt-et-un were played with an intensity that belied the casual setting. [008-201]

In a quieter moment, Holmes describes a gunner he met in "a little _estaminet_, over a glass of citron and" quiet conversation—a reminder that these establishments also offered soldiers the simple comfort of human connection away from the [[artillery|guns]]. [011-075]

## Military Context

The estaminet was tolerated and sometimes mildly regulated by military authorities. While gambling games like "house" (a card game similar to twenty-one) and "crown and anchor" were "mildly discouraged by the authorities," enforcement was lax and the estaminet's role in maintaining morale was implicitly recognized. The [[billet]] system that made estaminet culture possible was itself a crucial component of the [[france|Western Front]]'s rotational deployment model—units cycled through front-line, support, and rest positions, and the estaminet was the social anchor of the rest phase.
