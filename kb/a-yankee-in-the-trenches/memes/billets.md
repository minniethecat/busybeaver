## Overview

In the [[british-army|British Army]] of WWI, "[[billet|billets]]" referred to the temporary quarters assigned to soldiers when they were rotated out of the front-line [[trench|trenches]] for rest. As Holmes' glossary defines it, [[billet|billets]] could be "any place from a pigpen to a palace" (016-090). The quality of [[billet|billets]] varied enormously depending on location and the state of the surrounding villages.

## In the Memoir

Chapter IV of the memoir is titled "A FEW DAYS' REST IN BILLETS" (004-025) and provides the most detailed account. After the intense experience of a [[raid|trench raid]], Holmes and his comrades were rotated back:

> We were sent out of the lines that night and in billets got hot food, high-grade "fags", a real bath, a good stiff rum ration, and letters from home.
> (003-024)

Billets were not always comfortable. In one village, Holmes described the grim conditions:

> The worst feature of these billets was the rats. They were the biggest I ever saw, great, filthy, evil-smelling, grayish-red fellows, as big as a good-sized cat.
> (004-026)

## Good Billets

Later in the memoir, Holmes' unit found excellent quarters in Petite-Saens, a town that had survived the [[battle|fight]]ing largely intact:

> We had real billets. My section, ten men besides myself, drew a big attic in a clean house. There was loads of room and the roof was tight and there were no rats.
> (008-046)

## Social Life in Billets

Rest periods in [[billet|billets]] allowed soldiers to visit local *[[estaminet|estaminets]]* (small cafes), interact with [[france|French]] civilians, and recover their physical and mental health. Holmes describes men spending days at a nearby estaminet "across the way from our so-called [[billet|billets]]," where a "pretty mademoiselle" served beer and wine (004-027).
