## Overview

The water bottle was a standard piece of [[british-army|British Army]] infantry equipment in World War I, carried by every soldier as part of his field [[equipment|kit]]. Holmes lists it among the essential items a soldier bore: "a bayonet, a water bottle, a [[mess-tin|mess kit]], a rifle, two [[hun]]dred fifty rounds of ammo, a [[helmet|tin hat]], two [[gas-helmet|gas helmets]], and a lot of miscellaneous small junk" (008-071). Though a mundane item compared to [[rifle|rifles]] and [[bayonet|bayonets]], the water bottle was as vital as any weapon — dehydration in the [[trench|trenches]], whether from heat, exertion, or [[gas-warfare|gas]]-induced vomiting, could incapacitate a soldier as surely as a wound.

## In the Trenches

The water bottle was part of the web equipment system — the 1908 Pattern webbing — that distributed the soldier's load across his shoulders and waist. Holmes describes this harness as having "more hooks than a hatrack" (008-071), with each item of [[equipment|kit]] clipped, strapped, or [[hun]]g in its designated position. The water bottle, typically made of enamelled metal or aluminum with a cork or screw stopper and a felt cover, sat at the soldier's hip where it could be reached without removing the webbing.

Water [[trench-discipline|discipline]] was a practical concern in the [[trench|trenches]]. Clean water had to be brought forward by [[ration-party|ration parties]] along with food, and the water bottle was the individual soldier's personal reserve between resupply runs. In the heat of summer offensives or the dry atmosphere of [[gas-weapon|gas attack]]s, the water bottle could mean the difference between functioning and collapse. Holmes does not dwell on the water bottle as an object — it was too ordinary to merit extended description — but its inclusion in his [[equipment|kit]] inventory signals its taken-for-granted necessity.

## Narrative Examples

The water bottle appears in Holmes's account only once, in the comprehensive list of equipment a soldier carried into [[battle|action]]. Its very ordinariness is telling: unlike the rifle, the bayonet, or the [[gas-mask|gas helmet]] — items associated with killing, being [[casualty|killed]], or surviving poison — the water bottle was simply there, an unremarkable constant in an otherwise remarkable environment.

Yet this single mention in a list of equipment carries context. Holmes is describing the sheer weight of the infantryman's burden — "about eighty pounds of weight all the time" (008-071) — and the water bottle, small and light as it was individually, contributed to the cumulative load that made [[marching]], running, and [[battle|fight]]ing exhausting physical endeavors. Every ounce mattered when a man had to carry his entire existence on his back through mud and under fire.

## Military Context

The [[british-army|British Army]]'s 1908 Pattern water bottle was designed for durability and practicality. The enamelled steel body resisted rust, while the felt cover, when wetted, provided evaporative cooling — a small but welcome comfort on summer [[marching|march]]es. The capacity was approximately one imperial pint (568 ml), sufficient for a day's moderate exertion but inadequate for the intense thirst produced by [[battle|fight]]ing, [[marching]], or [[gas-warfare|gas]] exposure.

Water supply in the [[trench|trenches]] was a logistical challenge. [[battalion|Battalion]] water carts brought water forward to designated points, and [[ration-party|ration parties]] distributed it to the men in the line. In active sectors where [[shell|shelling]] disrupted supply routes, water could become critically scarce. The individual water bottle was the last link in this chain — the soldier's personal reserve, to be used sparingly between resupply.

The water bottle also served as an improvised cooking vessel. Soldiers used their [[dixie|mess tin]]s and water bottles to brew tea (char), the universal comfort of the [[british-army|British Army]]. A water bottle filled with char was a small piece of normality in the abnormal world of the [[trench|trenches]].
