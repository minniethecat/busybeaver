## Overview

A funk hole was a small [[dug-out]] or burrow excavated into the side of a trench, offering soldiers minimal but vital protection from shellfire, weather, and the unrelenting exposure of trench life. Holmes's book glossary defines it simply as "a [[dug-out]]," but in his narrative the funk hole is presented as part of a desperate architecture of survival — one element in a "regular network of [[trench|trenches]], burrows, and funk holes" that men depended on for their lives (007-202). The name itself, "funk hole," carries the dual sense of a place for shelter and a place to hide from fear ("funk" being British slang for fear or panic), suggesting that these crude refuges served a psychological function as much as a physical one.

## In the Trenches

Holmes encounters the Warren, a particularly dangerous section of the front near [[vimy|Vimy Ridge]], described as "a regular network of [[trench|trenches]], burrows, and funk holes, and we needed them all" (007-202). The position was "downhill from the [[germans|Huns]]," who kept up a continuous stream of shellfire — [[pip-squeak]]s, [[whiz-bang]]s, and [[minnies|minnie]]s. In this environment, funk holes were not luxuries but necessities — the only shelter available against the relentless [[artillery|bombardment]].

The funk hole was distinct from the more elaborate [[dug-out]], which could be a substantial underground chamber with timber supports, bunks, and even electric lighting in rear areas. Funk holes were improvised — scratched out of trench walls with entrenching tools, just large enough for one or two men to crouch or lie in. They offered no comfort, only the minimal geometry of survival: a roof of earth between the soldier and the shrapnel, depth enough to reduce the chance of a direct hit, and darkness enough to allow a few hours of exhausted sleep.

## Narrative Examples

Holmes does not dwell on individual funk holes as he does on the dramatic episodes of [[trench-raid|raid]]s, [[artillery|bombardment]]s, and wounds. But the funk hole is mentioned at a moment of heightened danger — the Warren — where his survival depended on being able to di[[mine-explosion|sap]]pear into the earth at a moment's notice. The phrase "we needed them all" (007-202) conveys the urgency of the situation: in a position under constant fire, every burrow and scrape in the earth was occupied, and there were never enough.

The funk hole represents the baseline of trench architecture — below the fire step and the parapet, below the [[trench|communication trench]] and the duck walk, at the very bottom of the hierarchy of military construction. It was the soldier's most personal space in the [[trench|trenches]]: a hole just big enough for his body, where he could briefly be neither a [[battle|combat]]ant nor a target, but simply a man trying to endure.

## Military Context

Funk holes occupied the lowest tier of the trench shelter hierarchy. Above them were:

- **Fire bays**: the forward-facing trench sections where men stood to fire
- **[[traverse|Traverse]]s**: angled bends in the trench that contained blast and limited enfilade fire
- **[[dug-out|Dug-out]]s**: larger underground chambers, often timber-lined, for officers, command posts, medical stations, or group shelter
- **Deep [[dug-out]]s**: elaborate multi-room structures up to thirty feet deep, proof against all but the heaviest [[shell|shells]]

Funk holes were dug by the soldiers themselves, typically scraped into the forward wall of the trench (the parados, or rear wall, to avoid collapse from [[shell|shelling]] on the parapet side). They were shallow, cramped, and prone to collapse under heavy [[artillery|bombardment]] — but they were immediately available, required no materials beyond what was at hand, and could be created in minutes with an entrenching tool. In static sectors where the same [[trench|trenches]] were occupied for weeks or months, funk holes evolved into more elaborate personal shelters, lined with salvaged timber, corrugated iron, or [[sandbag|sandbags]].

The name "funk hole" entered British English as a lasting legacy of the war — still used colloquially to mean a place of refuge or a hiding place from responsibility, preserving the trench soldier's sardonic association of shelter with fear.
