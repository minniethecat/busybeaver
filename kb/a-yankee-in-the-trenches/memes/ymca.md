# Y.M.C.A.

## Overview

The Young Men's Christian Association (Y.M.C.A.) was one of the most important morale and welfare organizations serving the Allied armies on the [[france|Western Front]]. Operating a network of huts, tents, and [[water-bottle|canteen]]s that stretched from the front-line areas to the base camps, the Y.M.C.A. provided soldiers with warmth, food, reading material, writing supplies, music, and — perhaps most importantly — a place that felt like civilization. In [[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es*, the Y.M.C.A. is mentioned repeatedly and with genuine warmth, a rare note of unqualified approval in a narrative otherwise marked by the grim irony of trench life.

## The Huts

The Y.M.C.A. hut was a simple structure — often little more than a wooden building with benches, tables, and a stove — but to men coming out of the line, it was a sanctuary. Holmes describes the hut at Petite-Saens with evident gratitude:

> By the way, we had a Y.M.C.A. hut at Petite-Saens, and I cannot say enough for this great work. No one who has not been there can know what a blessing it is to be able to go into a clean, warm, dry place and sit down to reading or games and to hear good music.
> — (005-119)

He adds a personal note: "Personally I am a little bit sorry that the secretaries are to be in [[uniform|khaki]]. They weren't when I left. And it sure did seem good to see a man in civilian's clothes. You get after a while so you hate the sight of a uniform" (005-119). The Y.M.C.A. secretary in civilian dress was, for Holmes, a reminder that a world beyond the war still existed.

## A Wish for Women Workers

Holmes devotes a remarkable passage to what he saw as the Y.M.C.A.'s greatest unmet need — more women workers of a particular kind:

> Another thing about the Y.M.C.A. I could wish that they would have more women in the huts. Not frilly, frivolous society girls, but women from thirty-five to fifty. ... what he really wants, though, is the chance to sit down and tell his troubles to some nice, sympathetic woman who is old enough to be level-headed.
> — (005-128)

He elaborates that "nearly every soldier reverts more or less to a boyish point of view. He hankers for somebody to mother him" (005-128). The passage is unusually candid — a soldier acknowledging, without irony or bravado, the emotional needs that the war created and that organizations like the Y.M.C.A. tried to meet. Holmes concludes that he does not "believe there is any organization better qualified to do this than the Y.M.C.A." (005-131).

## Across the Theater

The Y.M.C.A. appears at every stage of Holmes's journey. Far behind the lines, at the rest camp, "the Y.M.C.A. had a huge tent and spread themselves in taking care of the soldiers. There were entertainments almost every night, moving pictures, and music" (015-050). Even in the forward areas, where "there were few Y.M.C.A. huts so far behind the lines," soldiers gravitated to the nearest [[water-bottle|canteen]] — often Y.M.C.A.-run — for English or American [[fag|cigarette]]s (005-119). Later, recuperating in [[england|England]], Holmes attended a Y.M.C.A. concert given in the [[casualty|wounded]] men's honor, where "the chaplain came around and cheered us up and gave us good [[fag|fags]]" (014-138).

## A Parting Recommendation

Holmes's memoir concludes with practical advice for new soldiers heading to the front. His recommendation is characteristically direct:

> When you get out there, hunt up the Y.M.C.A. huts. You will find good cheer, warmth, music, and above all a place to do your writing. Write home often. Your people are concerned about you all the time.
> — (014-138)

The advice encapsulates everything the Y.M.C.A. meant to the front-line soldier: a place of warmth, a connection to [[blighty|home]], and a reminder of humanity.
