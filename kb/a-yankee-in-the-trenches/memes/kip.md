# Kip

## Overview

"Kip" was the [[tommy|Tommy]]'s word for sleep — not the sleep of a comfortable bed at [[blighty|home]], but the snatched, precarious, often-interrupted sleep of the [[trench|trenches]]. The word entered British military slang from dialectal English, where it originally meant a brothel or a lodging-house. In the [[trench|trenches]], it acquired a simpler and more precious meaning: any opportunity to close one's eyes and escape, however briefly, from the war.

## In the Memoir

Holmes uses "kip" throughout *A Yankee in the [[trench|Trench]]es* to describe the soldier's eternal quest for rest. The word appears as both verb and noun, always in quotation marks — a marker of its slang status even in 1917:

> we were allowed to "kip" until seven
> — (001-175)

This seemingly small privilege — sleeping until seven o'clock — was a luxury worth noting. In a world where stand-to at dawn was the daily ritual, an extra hour of kip was a gift.

> "kip" while we could. "Kip" in this case meant closing our eyes and
> — (002-188)

Holmes pauses to define the term for his American readers, confirming that "kip" was not yet universally understood outside the [[british-army|British Army]]. The phrase "while we could" captures the uncertainty of trench life — kip was always conditional, always at risk of interruption by [[shell|shelling]], sentry duty, or a German [[trench-raid|raid]].

## The Conditions of Kip

The physical circumstances of kip were often appalling. Holmes describes:

> some half-sheltered spots for "kip" and we lived through
> — (004-042)

A "half-sheltered spot" was the best most soldiers could hope for. More often, kip was taken on [[estaminet|bar]]e floors, in mud, under shellfire:

> "kipping" on the bare floor
> — (004-100)

> mighty comfortable after the kipping in the muck
> — (013-152)

The contrast between "mighty comfortable" and "the muck" is telling. Comfort was relative — after sleeping in mud, any dry surface felt like luxury.

## Kip and Sentry Duty

Sleep was never truly safe. Even when a soldier managed to find a place to kip, the demands of [[daily-life-in-the-trenches|trench routine]] could interrupt at any moment:

> "kip" when a sentry came up and said I would have to get inside
> — (006-116)

The sentry's interruption — moving men inside for safety — was a routine hazard of kip. Sleep and survival were constantly at odds.
