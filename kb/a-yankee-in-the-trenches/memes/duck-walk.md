## Overview

A duck walk was a slatted wooden pathway constructed across the muddy ground of the [[france|Western Front]]. Holmes provides the authoritative definition: "a slatted wooden walk built to prevent as much as possible sinking into the mud" (006-068). The original book's glossary echoes this: "A slatted wooden walk in soft ground." In Holmes's narrative, the duck walk is more than a piece of engineering — it is a stage for some of the memoir's most harrowing episodes, a three-mile stretch of exposed planking that men traversed under German shellfire, knowing that every step produced noise and every moment exposed them to death.

## In the Trenches

Holmes's introduction to the duck walk came on his first approach to the front lines. Expecting to enter a [[trench|communication trench]], he was surprised when the column "swung on to a 'duck walk'" instead (006-068). The reason for the exposed walkway was practical: "the ground was so full of moisture" that [[trench|trenches]] could not be dug. The duck walk was the only viable path through the waterlogged terrain.

The exposed nature of the walk made it terrifying. Holmes describes the noise: "we clattered along the boards, making a noise like a four-horse team on a covered bridge" (006-073). The sound was so loud that he "immediately wondered whether we were near enough for [[boche|the Boche]]s to hear." He was not in doubt for long.

The [[germans|Germans]] had the duck walk ranged, and the experience of traversing it under fire was brutal:

> The Boches had the range of that duck walk, and we began to run. Every now and then they would drop one near the walk, and from four to ten casualties would go down. There was no stopping for the wounded. They lay where they fell. We kept on the run, sometimes on the duck walk, sometimes in the mud, for three miles. (006-087)

The absence of cover meant the [[casualty|wounded]] could not be helped — the column had to keep moving, stepping over or past fallen comrades. This was Holmes's initiation into the reality of the front: men died, and the living kept going.

## Narrative Examples

The duck walk features repeatedly in Holmes's account. The ration party that saved his life traveled "three miles along the duck walk" (007-266) to fetch supplies. Each ration run was "under fire both going and coming" (007-253), and the duck walk was the exposed artery that made this duty so dangerous.

A darker episode reveals the duck walk as a site of espionage and betrayal. Holmes recounts the case of a woman spy in Abalaine who used children's clothing on a clothesline to signal German aircraft about troop movements: "certain children's garments, a red shirt and a blue one and several white garments, were on the clothesline in certain arrangement on the days when troops were to be moved along the duck walk the following night" (009-010). The woman was arrested and shot. The duck walk, in this account, becomes an intelligence asset — its traffic patterns were military information worth killing for and dying for.

## Military Context

Duck walks (also called duckboards) were a ubiquitous feature of the [[france|Western Front]], particularly in sectors like Flanders where the water table was high and the ground turned to liquid mud under [[shell|shelling]]. Built from wooden slats nailed to longitudinal runners, they provided a firm walking surface over otherwise impassable terrain. Without them, men would sink to their knees or waists in mud — a fate that could be fatal if [[shell|shelling]] prevented rescue.

Duck walks were not, however, protective. Unlike [[communication-trench|communication trenches]], which provided cover from observation and fire, duck walks were fully exposed. They were used where the ground could not [[trench|support trench]] construction, accepting the trade-off of visibility for mobility. Soldiers learned to move along them at night whenever possible, but emergencies and attacks demanded daylight transit regardless of risk. The three-mile duck walk Holmes describes was long enough to make it a significant tactical feature — both sides knew its location, its range was calculated, and its traffic was watched.
