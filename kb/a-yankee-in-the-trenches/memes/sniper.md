# Sniper

## Overview

The sniper was one of the most feared and respected figures of [[trench|trench warfare]]. Unlike the impersonal shell or the random machine-gun burst, the sniper's bullet was personal — aimed, deliberate, and often fatal before the victim heard the shot. Snipers enforced a permanent [[trench-discipline|discipline]] on the [[trench|trenches]]: keep your head down, or lose it.

[[r-derby-holmes|R. Derby Holmes]]'s memoir provides one of the most detailed technical comparisons of German and British sniping equipment to be found in a soldier's personal narrative. His account captures both the technological asymmetry between the two sides and the grim reality of life under the sniper's gaze.

## The German Sniper: Periscope-Telescope Rifle

Holmes was deeply impressed — and chilled — by the sophistication of German sniping technology:

> The Germans, by the way, have had the "edge" on the Allies in the matter of sniping, as in almost all lines of artillery and musketry practice. The Boche sniper is nearly always armed with a periscope-telescope rifle. This is a specially built super-accurate rifle mounted on a periscope frame. It is thrust up over the parapet and the image of the opposing parapet is cast on a little ground-glass screen on which are two crossed lines. [002-081–087]

The [[periscope|periscope rifle]] allowed the German sniper to aim and fire without ever exposing himself:

> At one hundred fifty yards or less the image is brought up to touching distance seemingly. Fritz simply trains his piece on some low place or anywhere that a head may be expected. When one appears on the screen, he pulls the trigger — and you "click it" if you happen to be on the other or receiving end. The shooter never shows himself. [002-087–092]

## The British Sniper: Exposed and Vulnerable

British snipers, by contrast, operated at a distinct technological disadvantage:

> The British sniper, on the other hand — at least in my time — had a plain telescope rifle and had to hide himself behind old masonry, tree trunks, or anything convenient, and camouflaged himself in all sorts of ways. At that he was constantly in danger. [002-097–100]

This asymmetry was a source of bitter frustration among the British ranks. While German snipers could shoot in safety from behind their parapets, British marksmen had to improvise concealment and risk exposure with every shot.

## Periscope as Target

Holmes learned the sniper's lethality firsthand when he first attempted to use a trench periscope:

> I remember the first time I looked through a periscope I had no sooner thrust the thing up than a bullet crashed into the upper mirror, splintering it. Many times I have stuck up a cap on a stick and had it pierced. [002-093–096]

The lesson was unmistakable: any object appearing above the parapet — periscope, cap, or head — would be shot at within seconds:

> But there's always a Fritzie over yonder with a telescope-sighted rifle, and it's about ten to one he'll get you if you stick the old "napper" up in daylight. [002-078–080]

## The Sergeant-Major's Warning

The danger was drilled into every new arrival. Holmes records the standard greeting for rookies entering the front line for the first time:

> "Keep your nappers down, you rooks. Don't look over the top. It ayen't 'ealthy." [002-073–074]

Holmes dryly notes the psychology behind the warning:

> It is the regular warning to new men. For some reason the first emotion of the rookie is an overpowering curiosity. He wants to take a peep into No Man's Land. It feels safe enough when things are quiet. [002-075–078]

## Psychological Impact

The sniper's psychological effect exceeded their tactical impact. A single sniper could paralyze a section of trench, making men af[[trench-raid|raid]] to raise their heads even to eat or relieve themselves. The knowledge that someone was watching — waiting for a single mistake — created a tension that wore on soldiers more than the random violence of [[shell|shelling]].

Holmes's account makes clear that sniping was not merely a military tactic but a condition of existence. Soldiers lived their entire front-line lives below the level of the parapet, their world bounded by the knowledge that exposure meant death. Daylight movements in view of the enemy were simply impossible; even periscopes had to be used with caution.

The counter-sniper duel was a grim subculture of [[trench|trench warfare]], but Holmes's memoir suggests that in the sectors he served, it was the [[germans|Germans]] who held the technological advantage — and with it, the initiative.
