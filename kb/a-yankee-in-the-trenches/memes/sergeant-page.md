# Sergeant Page

## Overview

Sergeant Page was a British non-commissioned officer serving in Holmes's company during the First World War. As the company sergeant, he was a fixture of [[trench-life|trench routine]] — assigning [[trench-fatigue|fatigues]], detailing men to [[trench-rations|rations]], and maintaining the daily rhythm of military life. His presence bookends Holmes's time at the front: from the early days in the [[trench|trenches]] to the final months when Page was detached to [[havre|Havre]].

## Role in the Narrative

Sergeant Page appears at three distinct points in Holmes's memoir, each revealing a different facet of [[trench-discipline|trench discipline]]. His role is not that of a [[battle|combat]] leader but of an administrator of soldierly routine:

After an officer leaves the men in a forward position, Page gives the practical order to rest while possible (002-187). This moment establishes him as the voice of trench pragmatism — the NCO who translates officer instructions into soldier-level reality.

Later, when corporals who had been on [[trench-rations|rations]] two nights in succession begin to complain, Page singles out Holmes for the next ration party:

> Of course Sergeant Page spotted me and detailed me to the "wangler's" duty.
> — (007-258)

This assignment, though resented at the time, was the duty during which Holmes was reassigned — indirectly saving his life. Page's matter-of-fact enforcement of the rotation, however unpopular, was simply how the trench world worked.

By the final months of Holmes's service, Page is absent:

> Sergeant Page was away in Havre as instructor for a month, and I was next senior.
> — (013-178)

Page's absence elevates Holmes to acting [[platoon|platoon command]]er, placing him in the chain of command for the first time. Sergeant Page, in his absence, had changed Holmes's war more than he ever did in his presence.
