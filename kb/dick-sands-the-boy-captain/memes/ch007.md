[007-001]Cousin Benedict gave a look of utter contempt, and [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] and Mrs

[007-002][[mrs-weldon|Weldon]] could not resist laughing.

[007-003]Such is only one instance of the way in which Cousin Benedict

[007-004]invariably brought it about that all conversation with him ultimately

[007-005]turned upon his favourite pursuit, and all along, throughout the

[007-006]monotonous hours of smooth sailing, while the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" was making her

[007-007]little headway to the east, he showed his own devotion to his pet

[007-008]science, by seeking to enlist new disciples. First of all, he tried his

[007-009]powers of persuasion upon [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]], but soon finding that the young

[007-010]apprentice had no taste for entomological mysteries, he gave him up and

[007-011]turned his attention to the negroes. Nor was he much more successful

[007-012]with them; one after another, Tom, Bat, Actæon, and Austin had all

[007-013]withdrawn themselves from his instructions, and the class at last was

[007-014]reduced to the single person of Hercules; but in him the enthusiastic

[007-015]naturalist thought he had discovered a latent talent which could

[007-016]distinguish between a parasite and a thysanura.

[007-017]Hercules accordingly submitted to pass a considerable portion of his

[007-018]leisure in the observation of every variety of coleoptera; he was

[007-019]encouraged to study the extensive collection of stag-beetles,

[007-020]tiger-beetles and lady-birds; and although at times the enthusiast

[007-021]trembled to see some of his most delicate and fragile specimens in the

[007-022]huge grasp of his pupil, he soon learned that the man's gentle docility

[007-023]was a sufficient guarantee against his clumsiness.

[007-024]While the science of entomology was thus occupying its two votaries,

[007-025]Mrs. Weldon was giving her own best attention to the education of

[007-026]Master Jack. Reading and writing she undertook to teach herself, while

[007-027]she entrusted the instruction in arithmetic to the care of Dick Sands.

[007-028]Under the conviction that a child of five years will make a much more

[007-029]rapid progress if something like amusement be combined with his

[007-030]lessons, Mrs. Weldon would not teach her boy to spell by the use of an

[007-031]ordinary school primer, but used a set of cubes, on the sides of which

[007-032]the various letters were painted in red. After first making a word and

[007-033]showing it to Jack, she set him to put it together without her help,

[007-034]and it was astonishing how quickly the child advanced, and how many

[007-035]hours he would spend in this way, both in the cabin and on deck. There

[007-036]were more than fifty cubes, which, besides the alphabet, included all

[007-037]the digits; so that they were of service for Dick Sands' lessons as

[007-038]well as for her own. She was more than satisfied with her device.

[007-039]On the morning of the 9th an incident occurred which could not fail to

[007-040]be observed as somewhat remarkable. Jack was half lying, half sitting

[007-041]on the deck, amusing himself with his letters, and had just finished

[007-042]putting together a word with which he intended to puzzle old Tom, who,

[007-043]with his hand sheltering his eyes, was pretending not to see the

[007-044]difficulty which was being labouriously prepared to bewilder him; all

[007-045]at once, [[dingo|Dingo]], who had been gambolling round the child, made a sudden

[007-046]pause, lifted his right paw, and wagged his tail convulsively. Then

[007-047]darting down upon a capital S, he seized it in his mouth, and carried

[007-048]it some paces away.

[007-049]"Oh, Dingo, Dingo! you mustn't eat my letters!" shouted the child.

[007-050]But the dog had already dropped the block of wood, and coming back

[007-051]again, picked up another, which he laid quietly by the side of the

[007-052]first. This time it was a capital V. Jack uttered an exclamation of

[007-053]astonishment which brought to his side not only his mother, but the

[007-054]captain and Dick, who were both on deck. In answer to their inquiry as

[007-055]to what had occurred, Jack cried out in the greatest excitement that

[007-056]Dingo knew how to read. At any rate he was sure that he knew his

[007-057]letters.

[007-058]Dick Sands smiled and stooped to take back the letters. Dingo snarled

[007-059]and showed his teeth, but the apprentice was not frightened; he carried

[007-060]his point, and replaced the two blocks among the rest. Dingo in an

[007-061]instant pounced upon them again, and having drawn them to his side,

[007-062]laid a paw upon each of them, as if to signify his intention of

[007-063]retaining them in his possession. Of the other letters of the alphabet

[007-064]he took no notice at all.

[007-065]"It is very strange," said Mrs. Weldon; "he has picked out S V again."

[007-066]"S V!" repeated the captain thoughtfully; "are not those the letters

[007-067]that form the initials on his collar?"

[007-068]And turning to the old negro, he continued,--

[007-069]"Tom didn't you say that this dog did not always belong to the captain

[007-070]of the 'Waldeck'?"

[007-071]"To the best of my belief," replied Tom, "the captain had only had him

[007-072]about two years. I often heard him tell how he found him at the mouth

[007-073]of the Congo."

[007-074]"Do you suppose that he never knew where the animal came from, or to

[007-075]whom he had previously belonged?" asked Captain Hull.

[007-076]"Never," answered Tom, shaking his head; "a lost dog is worse to

[007-077]identify than a lost child; you see, he can't make himself understood

[007-078]any way."

[007-079]The captain made no answer, but stood musing; Mrs. Weldon interrupted

[007-080]him.

[007-081]"These letters, captain, seem to be recalling something to your

[007-082]recollection.

[007-083]"I can hardly go so far as to say that, Mrs. Weldon," he replied; "but

[007-084]I cannot help associating them with the fate of a brave explorer."

[007-085]"Whom do you mean? said the lady.

[007-086]"In 1871, just two years ago," the captain continued, "a French

[007-087]traveller, under the auspices of the Geographical Society of Paris, set

[007-088]out for the purpose of crossing [[africa|Africa]] from west to east. His

[007-089]starting-point was the mouth of the Congo, and his exit was designed to

[007-090]be as near as possible to Cape Deldago, at the mouth of the River

[007-091]Rovouma, of which he was to ascertain the true course. The name of this

[007-092]man was Samuel Vernon, and I confess it strikes me as somewhat a

[007-093]strange coincidence that the letters engraved on [[dingos-collar|Dingo's collar]] should

[007-094]be Vernon's initials."

[007-095][Illustration: Jack cried out in the greatest excitement that Dingo

[007-096]knew how to read]

[007-097]"Is nothing known about this traveller?" asked Mrs. Weldon.

[007-098]"Nothing was ever heard of him after his first departure. It appears

[007-099]quite certain that he failed to reach the east coast, and it can only

[007-100]be conjectured either that he died upon his way, or that he was made

[007-101]prisoner by the natives; and if so, and this dog ever belonged to him,

[007-102]the animal might have made his way back to the sea-coast, where, just

[007-103]about the time that would be likely, the captain of the 'Waldeck'

[007-104]picked him up."

[007-105]"But you have no reason to suppose, Captain Hull, that Vernon ever

[007-106]owned a dog of this description?"

[007-107]"I own I never heard of it," said the captain; "but still the

[007-108]impression fixes itself on my mind that the dog must have been his; how

[007-109]he came to know one letter from another, it is not for me to pretend to

[007-110]say. Look at him now, madam! he seems not only to be reading the

[007-111]letters for himself, but to be inviting us to come and read them with

[007-112]him."

[007-113]Whilst Mrs. Weldon was watching the dog with much amusement, Dick

[007-114]Sands, who had listened to the previous conversation, took the

[007-115]opportunity of asking the captain whether the traveller Vernon had

[007-116]started on his expedition quite alone.

[007-117]"That is really more than I can tell you, my boy," answered Captain

[007-118]Hull; "but I should almost take it for granted that he would have a

[007-119]considerable retinue of natives."

[007-120]The captain spoke without being aware that [[negoro|Negoro]] had meanwhile quietly

[007-121]stolen on deck. At first his presence was quite unnoticed, and no one

[007-122]observed the peculiar glance with which he looked at the two letters

[007-123]over which Dingo still persisted in keeping guard. The dog, however, no

[007-124]sooner caught sight of the cook than he began to bristle with rage,

[007-125]whereupon Negoro, with a threatening gesture which seemed half

[007-126]involuntary, withdrew immediately to his accustomed quarters.

[007-127]The incident did not escape the captain's observation.

[007-128]"No doubt," he said, "there is some mystery here;" and he was pondering

[007-129]the matter over in his mind when Dick Sands spoke.

[007-130]"Don't you think it very singular, sir, that this dog should have such

[007-131]a knowledge of the alphabet?"

[007-132]Jack here put in his word.

[007-133]"My mamma has told me about a dog whose name was Munito, who could read

[007-134]as well as a schoolmaster, and could play dominoes."

[007-135]Mrs. Weldon smiled.

[007-136]"I am afraid, my child, that that dog was not quite so learned as you

[007-137]imagine. I don't suppose he knew one letter from another; but his

[007-138]master, who was a clever American, having found out that the animal had

[007-139]a very keen sense of hearing, taught him some curious tricks."

[007-140]"What sort of tricks?" asked Dick, who was almost as much interested as

[007-141]little Jack.

[007-142]"When he had to perform in public," continued Mrs. Weldon, "a lot of

[007-143]letters like yours, Jack, were spread out upon a table, and Munito

[007-144]would put together any word that the company should propose, either

[007-145]aloud or in a whisper, to his master. The creature would walk about

[007-146]until he stopped at the very letter which was wanted. The secret of it

[007-147]all was that the dog's owner gave him a signal when he was to stop by

[007-148]rattling a little tooth-pick in his pocket, making a slight noise that

[007-149]only the dog's ears were acute enough to perceive."

[007-150]Dick was highly amused, and said,--

[007-151]"But that was a dog who could do nothing wonderful without his master."

[007-152]"Just so," answered Mrs. Weldon; "and it surprises me very much to see

[007-153]Dingo picking out these letters without a master to direct him."

[007-154][Illustration: Negoro, with a threatening gesture that seemed half

[007-155]involuntary, withdrew immediately to his accustomed quarters.]

[007-156]"The more one thinks of it, the more strange it is," said Captain Hull;

[007-157]"but, after all, Dingo's sagacity is not greater than that of the dog

[007-158]which rang the convent bell in order to get at the dish that was

[007-159]reserved for passing beggars; nor than that of the dog who had to turn

[007-160]a spit every other day, and never could be induced to work when it was

[007-161]not his proper day. Dingo evidently has no acquaintance with any other

[007-162]letters except the two S V; and some circumstance which we can never

[007-163]guess has made him familiar with them."

[007-164]"What a pity he cannot talk!" exclaimed the apprentice; "we should know

[007-165]why it is that he always shows his teeth at Negoro."

[007-166]"And tremendous teeth they are!" observed the captain, as Dingo at that

[007-167]moment opened his mouth, and made a display of his formidable fangs.

[007-168]CHAPTER VI.

[007-169]A WHALE IN SIGHT.

[007-170]It was only what might be expected that the dog's singular exhibition

[007-171]of sagacity should repeatedly form a subject of conversation between

[007-172]Mrs. Weldon, the captain, and Dick. The young apprentice in particular

[007-173]began to entertain a lurking feeling of distrust towards Negoro,

[007-174]although it must be owned that the man's conduct in general afforded no

[007-175]tangible grounds for suspicion.

[007-176]Nor as it only among the stern passengers that Dingo's remarkable feat

[007-177]was discussed; amongst the crew in the bow the dog not only soon gained

[007-178]the reputation of being able to read, but was almost credited with

[007-179]being able to write too, as well as any sailor among them; indeed the

[007-180]chief wonder was that he did not speak.

[007-181]"Perhaps he can," suggested Bolton, the helmsman, "and likely enough

[007-182]some fine day we shall have him coming to ask about our bearings, and

[007-183]to inquire which way the wind lies."

[007-184]"Ah! why not?" assented another sailor; "parrots talk, and magpies

[007-185]talk; why shouldn't a dog? For my part, I should guess it must be

[007-186]easier to speak with a mouth than with a beak."
