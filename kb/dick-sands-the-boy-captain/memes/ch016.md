[016-001]Old Tom was in a frantic state of alarm lest the compass should be

[016-002]broken. He uttered a cry of consternation so loud that it roused [[dick-sands|Dick]]

[016-003]from the light slumber into which he had fallen in the cabin, and he

[016-004]rushed to the deck. By the time he had reached the stern, [[negoro|Negoro]] had

[016-005]not only regained his feet, but had managed successfully to conceal the

[016-006]bit of iron which he had again extracted from beneath the binnacle

[016-007]where he had himself laid it. Now that the wind had shifted to the

[016-008]south-west, it suited his machinations that the magnetic needle should

[016-009]indicate its true direction.

[016-010][Illustration: Quick as lightning, Dick Sands drew a revolver from his

[016-011]pocket.]

[016-012]"How now?" asked Dick eagerly; "what is the meaning of all this noise?"

[016-013]Tom explained how the cook had fallen against the binnacle, and how he

[016-014]had been terrified lest the compass should be injured. Dick's heart

[016-015]sank at the thought of losing his sole remaining compass, and his

[016-016]anxiety betrayed itself in his countenance as he knelt down to examine

[016-017]its condition; but he breathed freely as he ascertained that the

[016-018]instrument had sustained no damage; by the dim light he saw the needle

[016-019]resting on its two concentric circles, and felt his fears at once

[016-020]relieved; of course, he was quite unconscious of the fact that the

[016-021]removal of the bit of iron had made the magnet change its pointing. The

[016-022]incident, however, excited his misgiving; although he felt that Negoro

[016-023]could not be held responsible for an accidental fall, the very presence

[016-024]of the man in such a place at such a time perplexed him.

[016-025]"And what brings you here, this hour of the night?" he asked.

[016-026]"That's not your business," retorted Negoro insolently.

[016-027]"It is my business," replied Dick resolutely; "and I mean to have an

[016-028]answer; what brought you here?"

[016-029]Negoro answered sullenly that he knew of no rule to prevent his going

[016-030]where he liked and when he liked.

[016-031]"No rule!" cried Dick; "then I make the rule now. From this time

[016-032]forward, I make the rule that you shall never come astern. Do you

[016-033]understand?"

[016-034]Roused from his accustomed doggedness, the man seemed to make a

[016-035]threatening movement. Quick as lightning, Dick Sands drew a revolver

[016-036]from his pocket.

[016-037]"Negoro, one act, one word of insubordination, and I blow out your

[016-038]brains!"

[016-039]Negoro had no time to reply; before he could speak he was bowed down

[016-040]towards the deck by an irresistible weight. Hercules had grasped him by

[016-041]the shoulder.

[016-042]"Shall I put him overboard, captain? he will make a meal for the

[016-043]fishes; they are not very particular what they eat," said the negro,

[016-044]with a grin of contempt.

[016-045]"Not yet," quietly answered Dick.

[016-046]The giant removed his hand, and Negoro stood upright again, and began

[016-047]to retreat to his own quarters, muttering, however, as he passed

[016-048]Hercules,--

[016-049]"You cursed nigger! You shall pay for this!"

[016-050]The discovery was now made that the wind apparently had taken a sudden

[016-051]shift of no less than forty-five degrees; but what occasioned Dick the

[016-052]greatest perplexity was that there was nothing in the condition of the

[016-053]sea to correspond with the alteration in the current of the air;

[016-054]instead of being directly astern, wind and waves were now beating on

[016-055]the larboard. Progress in this way must necessarily be full of danger,

[016-056]and Dick was obliged to bring his ship up at least four points before

[016-057]he got her straight before the [[storm-at-sea|tempest]].

[016-058]The young captain felt that he must be more than ever on the alert; he

[016-059]could not shake off the suspicion that Negoro had been concerned in the

[016-060]loss of the first compass, and had some further designs upon the

[016-061]second. Still he was utterly at a loss to imagine what possible motive

[016-062]the man could have for so criminal an act of malevolence, as there was

[016-063]no plausible reason to be assigned why he should not be as anxious as

[016-064]all the rest to reach the coast of America. The suspicion continued,

[016-065]however, to haunt him, and when he mentioned it to [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] he found

[016-066]that a similar feeling of distrust had agitated her, although she, like

[016-067]himself, was altogether unable to allege a likely motive why the cook

[016-068]should contemplate so strange an act of mischief. It was determined

[016-069]that a strict surveillance should be kept upon all the fellow's

[016-070]movements.

[016-071]Negoro, however, manifested no inclination to disobey the captain's

[016-072]peremptory order; he kept strictly to his own part of the ship; but as

[016-073][[dingo|Dingo]] was now regularly quartered on the stern, there was a tolerably

[016-074]sure guarantee that the cook would not be found wandering much in that

[016-075]direction.

[016-076]A week passed, and still the tempest showed no signs of abating; the

[016-077]barometer continued to fall, and not once did a period of calmer

[016-078]weather afford an opportunity of carrying sail. The "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" still

[016-079]made her way northeast. Her speed could not be less than two hundred

[016-080]miles in twenty-four hours. But no land appeared. Vast as was the range

[016-081]of the American continent, extending for 120 degrees between the

[016-082]Atlantic and the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]], it was nowhere to be discerned. Was he

[016-083]dreaming? was he mad? Dick would perpetually ask himself: had he been

[016-084]sailing in a wrong direction? had he failed to steer aright?

[016-085]But no: he was convinced there was no error in his steering. Although

[016-086]he could not actually see it for the mist, he knew that day after day

[016-087]the sun rose before him, and that it set behind him. Yet he was

[016-088]constrained in bewilderment to ask, what had become of those shores of

[016-089]America upon which, when they came in sight, there was only too great a

[016-090]fear the ship should be dashed? what had become of them? where were

[016-091]they? whither had this incessant hurricane driven them? why did not the

[016-092]expected coast appear?

[016-093]To all these bewildering inquiries Dick could find no answer except to

[016-094]imagine that his compass had misled him. Yet he was powerless to put

[016-095]his own misgivings to the test; he deplored more than ever the

[016-096]destruction of the duplicate instrument which would have checked his

[016-097]registers. He studied his chart; but all in vain; the position in which

[016-098]he found himself as the result of Negoro's treachery, seemed to baffle

[016-099]him the more, the more he tried to solve the mystery.

[016-100]The days were passing on in this chronic state of anxiety, when one

[016-101]morning about eight o'clock, Hercules, who was on watch at the fore,

[016-102]suddenly shouted,--

[016-103]"Land!"

[016-104]Dick Sands had little reliance upon the negro's inexperienced eye, but

[016-105]hurried forward to the bow.

[016-106]"Where's the land?" he cried; his voice being scarcely audible above

[016-107]the howling of the tempest.

[016-108]"There! look there!" said Hercules, nodding his head and pointing over

[016-109]the larboard side, to the north-east.

[016-110]Dick could see nothing.

[016-111]Mrs. Weldon had heard the shout. Unable to restrain her interest, she

[016-112]had left her cabin and was at Dick's side. He uttered an expression of

[016-113]surprise at seeing her, but could not hear anything she said, as her

[016-114]voice was unable to rise above the roaring of the elements; she stood,

[016-115]her whole being as it were concentrated in the power of vision, and

[016-116]scanned the horizon in the direction indicated by Hercules. But all to

[016-117]no purpose.

[016-118]Suddenly, however, after a while, Dick raised his hand.

[016-119]"Yes!" he said; "yes; sure enough, yonder is land."

[016-120]He clung with excitement to the netting; and Mrs Weldon, supported by

[016-121]Hercules, strained her eyes yet more vehemently to get a glimpse of a

[016-122]shore which she had begun to despair of ever reaching.

[016-123]Beyond a doubt an elevated peak was there. It must be about ten miles

[016-124]to leeward. A break in the clouds soon left it more distinct. Some

[016-125]promontory it must be upon the American coast. Without sails, of

[016-126]course, the "Pilgrim" had no chance of bearing down direct upon it; but

[016-127]at least there was every reason to believe that she would soon reach

[016-128]some other portion of the shore; perhaps before noon, certainly in a

[016-129]few hours, they must be close to land.

[016-130]The pitching of the ship made it impossible for Mrs. Weldon to keep

[016-131]safe footing on the deck; accordingly, at a sign from Dick, Hercules

[016-132]led her back again to her cabin.

[016-133]Dick did not remain long at the bow, but went thoughtfully back to the

[016-134]wheel.

[016-135]He had, indeed, a tremendous [[duty|responsibility]] before him. Here was the

[016-136]land, the land for which they had longed so eagerly; and now that their

[016-137]anticipations were on the point of being realized, what was there, with

[016-138]a hurricane driving them on towards it, to prevent that land being

[016-139]their destruction? What measures could he take to prevent the schooner

[016-140]being dashed to pieces against it?

[016-141][Illustration: "There! look there!"]

[016-142]At the very moment when the promontory was just abreast of them, Negoro

[016-143]appeared on deck; he nodded to the peak familiarly, as he might have

[016-144]saluted a familiar friend, and retired as stealthily as he had come.

[016-145]Two hours later, and the promontory was lying to the larboard wake.

[016-146]Dick Sands had never relaxed his watchfulness, but he had failed to

[016-147]discover any further indications of a coast-line. His perplexity could

[016-148]only increase; the horizon was clear; the Andes ought to be distinct;

[016-149]they would be conspicuous twenty miles or more away. Dick took up his

[016-150]telescope again and again; he scrutinized the eastern horizon with

[016-151]minutest care; but there was nothing to be seen; and as the afternoon

[016-152]waned away the last glimpse had been taken of the promontory that had

[016-153]awakened their expectation; it had vanished utterly from their gaze; no

[016-154]indication of shore could be seen from the "Pilgrim's" deck.

[016-155]Dick Sands uttered a sigh of mingled amazement and relief. He went into

[016-156]Mrs. Weldon's cabin, where she was standing with her party.

[016-157]"It was only an island!" he said; "only an island!"

[016-158]"How? why? what island? what do you mean?" cried Mrs. Weldon

[016-159]incredulously; "what island can it be?"

[016-160]"The chart perhaps will tell us," replied Dick; and hurrying off to his

[016-161]own cabin, he immediately returned with the chart in his hands.

[016-162]After studying it attentively for a few minutes, he said,--

[016-163]"There, Mrs. Weldon; the land we have just passed, I should suppose

[016-164]must be that little speck in the midst of the Pacific. It must be

[016-165]Easter Island. At least, there seems to be no other land which possibly

[016-166]it could be."

[016-167]"And do you say," inquired Mrs. Weldon, "that we have left it quite

[016-168]behind us?"

[016-169]"Yes, entirely; almost to windward."

[016-170]Mrs. Weldon commenced a searching scrutiny of the map that was

[016-171]outspread before her.

[016-172]"How far is this," she said, after bending a considerable time over the

[016-173]chart; "how far is this from the coast of America?"

[016-174]"Thirty-five degrees," answered Dick; "somewhere about 2500 miles."

[016-175]"What ever do you mean?" rejoined the lady astonished; "if the

[016-176]'Pilgrim' is still 2500 miles from shore, she has positively made no

[016-177]progress at all. Impossible!"

[016-178]In thoughtful perplexity, Dick passed his hand across his brow. He did

[016-179]not know what to say. After an interval of silence, he said,--

[016-180]"I have no account to give for the strange delay. It is inexplicable to

[016-181]myself, except upon that one hypothesis, which I cannot resist, that

[016-182]the readings of the compass, somehow or other, have been wrong."

[016-183]He relapsed into silence. Then, brightening up, he added,--

[016-184]"But, thank God! at least we have now the satisfaction of knowing where

[016-185]we really are; we are no longer lost upon the wide Pacific; if only

[016-186]this hurricane will cease, long as the distance seems, we are on our

[016-187]proper course to the shores of America."

[016-188]The tone of confidence with which the youthful captain spoke had the

[016-189]effect of inspiring new hope into all who heard him; their spirits

[016-190]rose, and to their sanguine mood it seemed as if they were approaching

[016-191]to the end of all their troubles, and had hardly more to do than to

[016-192]await the turning of a tide to bring them into a glad proximity to port.
