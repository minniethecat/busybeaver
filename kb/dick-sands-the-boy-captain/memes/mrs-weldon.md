# Mrs. Weldon

The wife of James W. Weldon, a wealthy California shipowner (001-194), and mother of five-year-old Jack. As a passenger aboard the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*, she becomes the moral anchor of the party of survivors after the catastrophe at sea and during their subsequent [[africa|Africa]]n captivity.

## Role & Identity

- **Role**: Passenger aboard the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*; moral leader of the survivors
- **Affiliation**: Weldon family (wife of James W. Weldon)
- **First appearance**: Chapter 1 (PN 001-071)
- **Last appearance**: Chapter 51 (PN 051-004)

## Character Description

Mrs. Weldon is a woman of composure and quiet strength. When [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] expresses his concerns about her comfort aboard a whaling brig, she answers him with characteristic grace: "Certainly, [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]]," she answered, "but why do you trouble yourself?" (002-180). She submitted to the delays of the voyage "very philosophically, and did not utter a word of complaint" (006-041). Her presence commands respect — "she looked upon him" with an expression "rather of kindness than of contempt" (002-082) when dealing with the eccentric [[benedict|Cousin Benedict]].

Despite her position as a wealthy lady, she is compassionate. Looking at the rescued [[africa|Africa]]ns, she exclaims: "Poor things!" (005-040). She has acted as a maternal benefactor to [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: "For several years Mrs. Weldon had acted towards him" with kindness (003-155).

## Role in the Narrative

### Maritime Section: Trust and Crisis

Mrs. Weldon joins the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]* in [[new-zealand|New Zealand]] with her son Jack and [[benedict|Cousin Benedict]], eager to return to her husband in California. She "urged her request" (002-035) to take passage on the whaling brig rather than wait for a regular passenger ship. When the [[loss-of-captain-hull|whaleboat disaster]] strikes, "Mrs. Weldon sank upon her knees" (011-121) and "with great devoutness the lady offered up the prayer of commendation for the souls of the departing" (011-124).

In the critical moment after [[captain-hull|Hull]]'s death, she decisively supports [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]'s authority: "I wish every one on board to understand," she interposed, "that [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] is captain now" (011-183–184).

### African Section: Captivity and Endurance

When the party reaches [[africa|Africa]], Mrs. Weldon endures the march through the interior. Later, "Mrs. Weldon and the negroes were soon convinced" (024-125) of the reality of their situation. In the final escape sequence, "Mrs. Weldon in an agony" (051-004) endures the desperate final flight.

## Relationships

- **[[captain-hull]]** — [[captain-hull|Captain]] of the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*; [[captain-hull|Hull]] treated her with "courteous" deference (002-178)

## Fate

Mrs. Weldon survives the ordeal and is ultimately reunited with her husband James W. Weldon in California.

## Significance

Mrs. Weldon represents feminine [[courage|fortitude]] and moral authority in Verne's novel. Though placed in extreme danger, she never loses her composure or her faith. Her public endorsement of [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] — a fifteen-year-old apprentice — as captain is a pivotal moment that legitimizes the boy's authority and rallies the crew. She embodies maternal love, courage under duress, and the stabilizing influence of character in times of crisis.

> "I wish every one on board to understand," she interposed, "that Dick Sands is captain now. Orders must be taken from him, and they must be obeyed." (011-183–184)
