[002-001][[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] did everything in his power to repair the disappointment;

[002-002]he made every effort to engage a fresh gang; but it was too late; every

[002-003]available seaman had long since been carried off to the fisheries.

[002-004]Finding therefore that all hope of making good the deficiency in his

[002-005]cargo must be resigned, he was on the point of leaving Auckland, alone

[002-006]with his crew, when he was met by a request with which he felt himself

[002-007]bound to comply.

[002-008]It had chanced that James [[mrs-weldon|Weldon]], on one of those journeys which were

[002-009]necessitated by the nature of his business, had brought with him his

[002-010]wife, his son Jack, a child of five years of age, and a relation of the

[002-011]family who was generally known by the name of Cousin Benedict. Weldon

[002-012]had of course intended that his family should accompany him on his

[002-013]return home to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]; but little Jack was taken so seriously

[002-014]ill, that his father, whose affairs demanded his immediate return, was

[002-015]obliged to leave him behind at Auckland with his wife and Cousin

[002-016]Benedict.

[002-017]Three months had passed away, little Jack was convalescent, and Mrs.

[002-018]Weldon, weary of her long separation from her husband, was anxious to

[002-019]get home as soon as possible. Her readiest way of reaching San

[002-020]Francisco was to cross to Australia, and thence to take a passage in

[002-021]one of the vessels of the "Golden Age" Company, which run between

[002-022]Melbourne and the Isthmus of Panama: on arriving in Panama she would

[002-023]have to wait the departure of the next American steamer of the line

[002-024]which maintains a regular communication between the Isthmus and

[002-025]California. This route, however, involved many stoppages and changes,

[002-026]such as are always disagreeable and inconvenient for women and

[002-027]children, and Mrs. Weldon was hesitating whether she should encounter

[002-028]the journey, when she heard that her husband's vessel, the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]],"

[002-029]had arrived at Auckland. Hastening to Captain Hull, she begged him to

[002-030]take her with her little boy, Cousin Benedict, and Nan, an old negress

[002-031]who had been her attendant from her childhood, on board the "Pilgrim,"

[002-032]and to convey them to San Francisco direct.

[002-033]"Was it not over hazardous," asked the captain, "to venture upon a

[002-034]voyage of between 5000 and 6000 miles in so small a sailing-vessel?"

[002-035]But Mrs. Weldon urged her request, and Captain Hull, confident in the

[002-036]sea-going qualities of his craft, and anticipating at this season

[002-037]nothing but fair weather on either side of the equator, gave his

[002-038]consent.

[002-039]In order to provide as far as possible for the comfort of the lady

[002-040]during a voyage that must occupy from forty to fifty days, the captain

[002-041]placed his own cabin at her entire disposal.

[002-042]Everything promised well for a prosperous voyage. The only hindrance

[002-043]that could be foreseen arose from the circumstance that the "Pilgrim"

[002-044]would have to put in at Valparaiso for the purpose of unlading; but

[002-045]that business once accomplished, she would continue her way along the

[002-046]American coast with the assistance of the land breezes, which

[002-047]ordinarily make the proximity of those shores such agreeable quarters

[002-048]for sailing.

[002-049]Mrs. Weldon herself had accompanied her husband in so many voyages,

[002-050]that she was quite inured to all the makeshifts of a seafaring life,

[002-051]and was conscious of no misgiving in embarking upon a vessel of such

[002-052]small tonnage. She was a brave, high-spirited woman of about thirty

[002-053]years of age, in the enjoyment of excellent health, and for her the sea

[002-054]had no terrors. Aware that Captain Hull was an experienced man, in whom

[002-055]her husband had the utmost confidence, and knowing that his ship was a

[002-056]substantial craft, registered as one of the best of the American

[002-057]whalers, so far from entertaining any mistrust as to her safety, she

[002-058]only rejoiced in the opportuneness of the chance which seemed to offer

[002-059]her a direct and unbroken route to her destination.

[002-060]Cousin Benedict, as a matter of course, was to accompany her. He was

[002-061]about fifty; but in spite of his mature age it would have been

[002-062]considered the height of imprudence to allow him to travel anywhere

[002-063]alone. Spare, lanky, with a bony frame, with an enormous cranium, and a

[002-064]profusion of hair, he was one of those amiable, inoffensive _savants_

[002-065]who, having once taken to gold spectacles, appear to have arrived at a

[002-066]settled standard of age, and, however long they live afterwards, seem

[002-067]never to be older than they have ever been.

[002-068]Claiming a sort of kindredship with all the world, he was universally

[002-069]known, far beyond the pale of his own connexions, by the name of

[002-070]"Cousin Benedict." In the ordinary concerns of life nothing would ever

[002-071]have rendered him capable of shifting for himself; of his meals he

[002-072]would never think until they were placed before him; he had the

[002-073]appearance of being utterly insensible to heat or cold; he vegetated

[002-074]rather than lived, and might not inaptly be compared to a tree which,

[002-075]though healthy enough at its core, produces scant foliage and no fruit.

[002-076]His long arms and legs were in the way of himself and everybody else;

[002-077]yet no one could possibly treat him with unkindness. As M. Prudhomme

[002-078]would say, "if only he had been endowed with capability," he would have

[002-079]rendered a service to any one in the world; but helplessness was his

[002-080]dominant characteristic; helplessness was ingrained into his very

[002-081]nature; yet this very helplessness made him an object of kind

[002-082]consideration rather than of contempt, and Mrs. Weldon looked upon him

[002-083]as a kind of elder brother to her little Jack.

[002-084]It must not be supposed, however, that Cousin Benedict was either idle

[002-085]or unoccupied. On the contrary, his whole time was devoted to one

[002-086]absorbing passion for natural history. Not that he had any large claim

[002-087]to be regarded properly as a natural historian; he had made no

[002-088]excursions over the whole four districts of zoology, botany,

[002-089]mineralogy, and geology, into which the realms of natural history are

[002-090]commonly divided; indeed, he had no pretensions at all to be either a

[002-091]botanist, a mineralogist, or a geologist; his studies only sufficed to

[002-092]make him a zoologist, and that in a very limited sense. No Cuvier was

[002-093]he; he did not aspire to decompose animal life by analysis, and to

[002-094]recompose it by synthesis; his enthusiasm had not made him at all

[002-095]deeply versed in vertebrata, mollusca, or radiata; in fact, the

[002-096]vertebrata--animals, birds, reptiles, fishes--had had no place in his

[002-097]researches; the mollusca--from the cephalopoda to the bryozia--had had

[002-098]no attractions for him; nor had he consumed the midnight oil in

[002-099]investigating the radiata, the echmodermata, acalephæ, polypi, entozoa,

[002-100]or infusoria.

[002-101]No; Cousin Benedict's interest began and ended with the articulata; and

[002-102]it must be owned at once that his studies were very far from embracing

[002-103]all the range of the six classes into which "articulata" are

[002-104]subdivided; viz, the insecta, the mynapoda, the arachnida, the

[002-105]crustacea, the cinhopoda, and the anelides; and he was utterly unable

[002-106]in scientific language to distinguish a worm from a leech, an earwig

[002-107]from a sea-acorn, a spider from a scorpion, a shrimp from a

[002-108]frog-hopper, or a galley-worm from a centipede.

[002-109]To confess the plain truth, Cousin Benedict was an amateur

[002-110]entomologist, and nothing more.

[002-111][[entomology|Entomology]], it may be asserted, is a wide science; it embraces the

[002-112]whole division of the articulata; but our friend was an entomologist

[002-113]only in the limited sense of the popular acceptation of the word; that

[002-114]is to say, he was an observer and collector of insects, meaning by

[002-115]"insects" those articulata which have bodies consisting of a number of

[002-116]concentric movable rings, forming three distinct segments, each with a

[002-117]pair of legs, and which are scientifically designated as hexapods.

[002-118][Illustration: Cousin Benedict]

[002-119]To this extent was Cousin Benedict an entomologist; and when it is

[002-120]remembered that the class of insecta of which he had grown up to be the

[002-121]enthusiastic student comprises no less than ten[1] orders, and that of

[002-122]these ten the coleoptera and diptera alone include 30,000 and 60,000

[002-123]species respectively, it must be confessed that he had an ample field

[002-124]for his most persevering exertions.

[002-125][Footnote 1: These ten orders are (1) the orthoptera, _e.g._

[002-126]grasshoppers and crickets; (2) the neuroptera, _e.g._ dragon-flies; (3)

[002-127]the hymenoptera, _e.g._ bees, wasps, and ants; (4) the lepidoptera,

[002-128]_e.g._ butterflies and moths; (5) the hemiptera, _e.g._ cicadas and

[002-129]fleas; (6) the coleoptera, _e.g._ cockchafers and glow-worms; (7) the

[002-130]diptera, _e.g._ gnats and flies; (8) the rhipiptera, _e.g._ the

[002-131]stylops; (9) the parasites, _e.g._ the acarus; and (10) the thysanura,

[002-132]_e.g._ the lepisma and podura.]

[002-133]Every available hour did he spend in the pursuit of his favourite

[002-134]science: hexapods ruled his thoughts by day and his dreams by night.

[002-135]The number of pins that he carried thick on the collar and sleeves of

[002-136]his coat, down the front of his waistcoat, and on the crown of his hat,

[002-137]defied computation; they were kept in readiness for the capture of

[002-138]specimens that might come in his way, and on his return from a ramble

[002-139]in the country he might be seen literally encased with a covering of

[002-140]insects, transfixed adroitly by scientific rule.

[002-141]This ruling passion of his had been the inducement that had urged him

[002-142]to accompany Mr. and Mrs. Weldon to [[new-zealand|New Zealand]]. It had appeared to him

[002-143]that it was likely to be a promising district, and now having been

[002-144]successful in adding some rare specimens to his collection, he was

[002-145]anxious to get back again to San Francisco, and to assign them their

[002-146]proper places in his extensive cabinet.

[002-147]Besides, it never occurred to Mrs. Weldon to start without him. To

[002-148]leave him to shift for himself would be sheer cruelty. As a matter of

[002-149]course whenever Mrs. Weldon went on board the "Pilgrim," Cousin

[002-150]Benedict would go too.

[002-151]Not that in any emergency assistance of any kind could be expected from

[002-152]him; on the contrary, in the case of difficulty he would be an

[002-153]additional burden; but there was every reason to expect a fair passage

[002-154]and no cause of misgiving of any kind, so the propriety of leaving the

[002-155]amiable entomologist behind was never suggested.

[002-156]Anxious that she should be no impediment in the way of the due

[002-157]departure of the "Pilgrim" from Waitemata, Mrs. Weldon made her

[002-158]preparations with the utmost haste, discharged the servants which she

[002-159]had temporarily engaged at Auckland, and accompanied by little Jack and

[002-160]the old negress, and followed mechanically by Cousin Benedict, embarked

[002-161]on the 22nd of January on board the schooner.

[002-162]The amateur, however, kept his eye very scrupulously upon his own

[002-163]special box. Amongst his collection of insects were some very

[002-164]remarkable examples of new staphylins, a species of carnivorous

[002-165]coleoptera with eyes placed above their head; it was a kind supposed to

[002-166]be peculiar to New Caledonia. Another rarity which had been brought

[002-167]under his notice was a venomous spider, known among the Maoris as a

[002-168]"katipo;" its bite was asserted to be very often fatal. As a spider,

[002-169]however, belongs to the order of the arachnida, and is not properly an

[002-170]"insect," Benedict declined to take any interest in it. Enough for him

[002-171]that he had secured a novelty in his own section of research; the

[002-172]"Staphylin Neo-Zelandus" was not only the gem of his collection, but

[002-173]its pecuniary value baffled ordinary estimate; he insured his box at a

[002-174]fabulous sum, deeming it to be worth far more than all the cargo of oil

[002-175]and whalebone in the "Pilgrim's" hold.

[002-176]Captain Hull advanced to meet Mrs. Weldon and her party as they stepped

[002-177]on deck.

[002-178]"It must be understood, Mrs. Weldon," he said, courteously raising his

[002-179]hat, "that you take this passage entirely on your own [[duty|responsibility]]."

[002-180]"Certainly, Captain Hull," she answered; "but why do you ask?"

[002-181]"Simply because I have received no orders from Mr. Weldon," replied the

[002-182]captain.
