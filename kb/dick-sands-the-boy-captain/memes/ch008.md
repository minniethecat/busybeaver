[008-001]"Of course it is," said Howick, the boatswain; "only a quadruped has

[008-002]never yet been known to do it."

[008-003]Perhaps, however, the worthy fellow would have been amazed to hear that

[008-004]a certain Danish _savant_ once possesed a dog that could actually

[008-005]pronounce quite distinctly nearly twenty different words, demonstrating

[008-006]that the construction of the glottis, the aperture at the top of the

[008-007]windpipe, was adapted for the emission of regular sounds: of course the

[008-008]animal attached no meaning to the words it uttered any more than a

[008-009]parrot or a jay can comprehend their own chatterings.

[008-010][Illustration: "This [[dingo|Dingo]] is nothing out of the way."]

[008-011]Thus, unconsciously, Dingo had become the hero of the hour. On several

[008-012]separate occasions [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] repeated the experiment of spreading

[008-013]out the blocks before him, but invariably with the same result; the dog

[008-014]never failed, without the slightest hesitation, to pick out the two

[008-015]letters, leaving all the rest of the alphabet quite unnoticed.

[008-016]Cousin Benedict alone, somewhat ostentatiously, professed to take no

[008-017]interest in the circumstance.

[008-018]"You cannot suppose," he said to Captain Hull, after various

[008-019]repetitions of the trick, "that dogs are to be reckoned the only

[008-020]animals endowed with intelligence Rats, you know, will always leave a

[008-021]sinking ship, and beavers invariably raise their dams before the

[008-022]approach of a flood. Did not the horses of Nicomedes, Scanderberg and

[008-023]Oppian die of grief for the loss of their masters? Have there not been

[008-024]instances of donkeys with wonderful memories? Birds, too, have been

[008-025]trained to do the most remarkable things; they have been taught to

[008-026]write word after word at their master's dictation; there are cockatoos

[008-027]who can count the people in a room as accurately as a mathematician;

[008-028]and haven't you heard of the old Cardinal's parrot that he would not

[008-029]part with for a hundred gold crowns because it could repeat the

[008-030]Apostles' creed from beginning to end without a blunder? And [[entomology|insects]],"

[008-031]he continued, warming into enthusiasm, "how marvellously they vindicate

[008-032]the axiom--

[008-033]'In minimis maximus Deus!'

[008-034]Are not the structures of ants the very models for the architects of a

[008-035]city? Has the diving-bell of the aquatic argyroneta ever been surpassed

[008-036]by the invention of the most skilful student of mechanical art? And

[008-037]cannot fleas go through a drill and fire a gun as well as the most

[008-038]accomplished artilleryman? This Dingo is nothing out of the way. I

[008-039]suppose he belongs to some unclassed species of mastiff. Perhaps one

[008-040]day or other he may come to be identified as the 'canis alphabeticus'

[008-041]of [[new-zealand|New Zealand]]."

[008-042]The worthy entomologist delivered this and various similar harangues;

[008-043]but Dingo, nevertheless, retained his high place in the general

[008-044]estimation, and by the occupants of the forecastle was regarded as

[008-045]little short of a phenomenon. The feeling, otherwise universal, was not

[008-046]in any degree shared by [[negoro|Negoro]], and it is not improbable that the man

[008-047]would have been tempted to some foul play with the dog if the open

[008-048]sympathies of the crew had not kept him in check. More than ever he

[008-049]studiously avoided coming in contact in any way with the animal, and

[008-050][[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] in his own mind was quite convinced that since the incident

[008-051]of the letters, the cook's hatred of the dog had become still more

[008-052]intense.

[008-053]After continual alternations with long and wearisome calms the

[008-054]north-east wind perceptibly moderated, and on the both, Captain Hull

[008-055]really began to hope that such a change would ensue as to allow the

[008-056]schooner to run straight before the wind. Nineteen days had elapsed

[008-057]since the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" had left Auckland, a period not so long but that

[008-058]with a favourable breeze it might be made up at last. Some days however

[008-059]were yet to elapse before the wind veered round to the anticipated

[008-060]quarter.

[008-061]It has been already stated that this portion of the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] is almost

[008-062]always deserted. It is out of the line of the American and Australian

[008-063]steam-packets, and except a whaler had been brought into it by some

[008-064]such exceptional circumstances as the "Pilgrim," it was quite unusual

[008-065]to see one in this latitude.

[008-066]But, however void of traffic was the surface of the sea, to none but an

[008-067]unintelligent mind could it appear monotonous or barren of interest.

[008-068]The poetry of the ocean breathes forth in its minute and almost

[008-069]imperceptible changes. A marine plant, a tuft of seaweed lightly

[008-070]furrowing the water, a drifting spar with its unknown history, may

[008-071]afford unlimited scope--for the imagination; every little drop passing,

[008-072]in its process of evaporation, backwards and forwards from sea to sky,

[008-073]might perchance reveal its own special secret; and happy are those

[008-074]minds which are capable of a due appreciation of the mysteries of air

[008-075]and ocean.

[008-076][Illustration: Occasionally Dick Sands would take a pistol, and now and

[008-077]then a rifle.]

[008-078]Above the surface as well as below, the restless flood is ever teaming

[008-079]with animal life; and the passengers on board the "Pilgrim" derived no

[008-080]little amusement from watching great flocks of birds migrating

[008-081]northwards to escape the rigour of the polar winter, and ever and again

[008-082]descending in rapid flight to secure some tiny fish. Occasionally Dick

[008-083]Sands would take a pistol, and now and then a rifle, and, thanks to Mr.

[008-084][[mrs-weldon|Weldon]]'s former instructions, would bring down various specimens of the

[008-085]feathered tribe.

[008-086]Sometimes white petrels would congregate in considerable numbers near

[008-087]the schooner; and sometimes petrels of another species, with brown

[008-088]borders on their wings, would come in sight; now there would be flocks

[008-089]of damiers skimming the water; and now groups of penguins, whose clumsy

[008-090]gait appears so ludicrous on shore; but, as Captain Hull pointed out,

[008-091]when their stumpy wings were employed as fins, they were a match for

[008-092]the most rapid of fish, so that sailors have often mistaken them for

[008-093]bonitos.

[008-094]High over head, huge albatrosses, their outspread wings measuring ten

[008-095]feet from tip to tip, would soar aloft, thence to swoop down towards

[008-096]the deep, into which they plunged their beaks in search of food. Such

[008-097]incidents and scenes as these were infinite in their variety, and it

[008-098]was accordingly only for minds that were obtuse to the charms of nature

[008-099]that the voyage could be monotonous.

[008-100]On the day the wind shifted, Mrs. Weldon was walking up and down on the

[008-101]"Pilgrim's" stern, when her attention was attracted by what seemed to

[008-102]her a strange phenomenon. All of a sudden, far as the eye could reach,

[008-103]the sea had assumed a reddish hue, as if it were tinged with blood.

[008-104]Both Dick and Jack were standing close behind her, and she cried,--

[008-105]"Look, Dick, look! the sea is all red. Is it a sea-weed that is making

[008-106]the water so strange a colour?

[008-107]"No," answered Dick, "it is not a weed; it is what the sailors call

[008-108]whales' food; it is formed, I believe, of innumerable myriads of minute

[008-109]crustacea."

[008-110]"Crustacea they may be," replied Mrs. Weldon, "but they must be so

[008-111]small that they are mere insects. Cousin Benedict no doubt will like to

[008-112]see them."

[008-113]She called aloud,--

[008-114]"Benedict! Benedict! come here! we have a sight here to interest you."

[008-115]The amateur naturalist slowly emerged from his cabin followed by

[008-116]Captain Hull.

[008-117]"Ah! yes, I see!" said the captain; "whales' food; just the opportunity

[008-118]for you, Mr. Benedict; a chance not to be thrown away for studying one

[008-119]of the most curious of the crustacea."

[008-120]"Nonsense!" ejaculated Benedict contemptuously; "utter nonsense!"

[008-121]"Why? what do you mean, Mr. Benedict?" retorted the captain; "surely

[008-122]you, as an entomologist, must know that I am right in my conviction

[008-123]that these crustacea belong to one of the six classes of the

[008-124]articulata."

[008-125]The disdain of Cousin Benedict was expressed by a repeated sneer.

[008-126]"Are you not aware, sir, that my researches as an entomologist are

[008-127]confined entirely to the hexapoda?"

[008-128]Captain Hull, unable to repress a smile, only answered

[008-129]good-humouredly,--

[008-130]"I see, sir, your tastes do not lie in the same direction as those of

[008-131]the [[right-whale|whale]]."

[008-132]And turning to Mrs. Weldon, he continued,--

[008-133]"To whalemen, madam, this is a sight that speaks for itself. It is a

[008-134]token that we ought to lose no time in getting out our lines and

[008-135]looking to the state of our harpoons. There is game not far away."

[008-136]Jack gave vent to his astonishment.

[008-137]"Do you mean that great creatures like whales feed on such tiny things

[008-138]as these?"

[008-139]"Yes, my boy," said the captain; "and I daresay they are as nice to

[008-140]them as semolina and ground rice are to you.

[008-141]"When a whale gets into the middle of them he has nothing to do but to

[008-142]open his jaws, and, in a minute, hundreds of thousands of these minute

[008-143]creatures are inside the fringe or whalebone around his palate, and he

[008-144]is sure of a good mouthful."

[008-145]"So you see, Jack," said Dick, "the whale gets his shrimps without the

[008-146]trouble of shelling them."

[008-147]"And when he has just closed his snappers is the very time to give him

[008-148]a good taste of the harpoon," added Captain Hull.

[008-149]The words had hardly escaped the captain's lips when a shout from one

[008-150]of the sailors announced,--

[008-151]"A whale to larboard!"

[008-152]"There's the whale!" repeated the captain. All his professional

[008-153]instincts were aroused in an instant, and he hurried to the bow,

[008-154]followed in eager curiosity by all the stern passengers.

[008-155]Even Cousin Benedict loitered up in the rear, constrained, in spite of

[008-156]himself, to take a share in the general interest.

[008-157]There was no doubt about the matter. Four miles or so to windward an

[008-158]unusual commotion in the water betokened to experienced eyes the

[008-159]presence of a whale; but the distance was too great to permit a

[008-160]reasonable conjecture to be formed as to which species of those

[008-161]mammifers the creature belonged.

[008-162]Three distinct species are familiarly known. First there is the Right

[008-163]whale, which is ordinarily sought for in the northern fisheries. The

[008-164]average length of this cetacean is sixty feet, though it has been known

[008-165]to attain the length of eighty feet. It has no dorsal fin, and beneath

[008-166]its skin is a thick layer of blubber. One of these monsters alone will

[008-167]yield as much as a hundred barrels of oil.

[008-168]Then there is the Hump-back, a typical representative of the species

[008-169]"balænoptera," a definition which may at first sight appear to possess

[008-170]an interest for an entomologist, but which really refers to two white

[008-171]dorsal fins, each half as wide as the body, resembling a pair of wings,

[008-172]and in their formation similar to those of the flying-fish. It must be

[008-173]owned, however, that a flying whale would decidedly be a _rara avis_.

[008-174]Lastly, there is the Jubarte, commonly known as the Finback. It is

[008-175]provided with a dorsal fin, and in length not unfrequently is a match

[008-176]for the gigantic Right whale.

[008-177]While it was impossible to decide to which of the three species the

[008-178]whale in the distance really belonged, the general impression inclined

[008-179]to the belief that it was a jubarte.

[008-180]With longing eyes Captain Hull and his crew gazed at the object of

[008-181]general attraction. Just as irresistibly as it is said a clockmaker is

[008-182]drawn on to examine the mechanism of every clock which chance may throw

[008-183]in his way, so is a whaleman ever anxious to plunge his harpoon into

[008-184]any whale that he can get within his reach. The larger the game the

[008-185]more keen the excitement; and no elephant-hunter's eagerness ever

[008-186]surpasses the zest of the whale-fisher when once started in pursuit of

[008-187]the prey.

[008-188]To the crew the sight of the whale was the opening of an unexpected

[008-189]opportunity, and no wonder they were fired with the burning hope that

[008-190]even now they might do something to supply the deficiency of their

[008-191]meagre haul throughout the season.

[008-192]Far away as the creature still was, the captain's practised eye soon

[008-193]enabled him to detect various indications that satisfied him as to its

[008-194]true species. Amongst other things that arrested his attention, he

[008-195]observed a column of water and vapour ejected from the nostrils. "It

[008-196]isn't a right whale," he said; "if so, its spout would be smaller and

[008-197]it would rise higher in the air. And I do not think it is a hump-back.

[008-198]I cannot hear the hump-back's roar. Dick, tell me, what do you think

[008-199]about it?"
