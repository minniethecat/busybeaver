[022-001]It was agreed that there was no cause for delay. Each one took up the

[022-002]package that had been assigned him. [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]], with Hercules' help,

[022-003]mounted the horse, and Jack, with his miniature gun slung across his

[022-004]shoulder, was placed astride in front of her. Without a thought of

[022-005]acknowledging the kindness of the good-natured stranger in providing

[022-006]him so enjoyable a ride, the heedless little fellow declared himself

[022-007]quite capable of guiding the "gentleman's horse," and when to indulge

[022-008]him the bridle was put into his hand, he looked as proud as though he

[022-009]had been appointed leader of the whole [[slave-caravan|caravan]].

[022-010]CHAPTER XVI.

[022-011]THROUGH THE FOREST.

[022-012]Although there was no obvious cause for apprehension, it cannot be

[022-013]denied that it was with a certain degree of foreboding that [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]

[022-014]first entered that dense forest, through which for the next ten days

[022-015]they were all to wend their toilsome way.

[022-016]Mrs. Weldon, on the contrary, was full of confidence and hope. A woman

[022-017]and a mother, she might have been expected to be conscious of anxiety

[022-018]at the peril to which she might be exposing herself and her child; and

[022-019]doubtless she would have been sensible of alarm if her mind had not

[022-020]been fully satisfied upon two points; first, that the portion of the

[022-021]pampas they were about to traverse was little infested either by

[022-022]natives or by dangerous beasts; and secondly, that she was under the

[022-023]protection of a guide so trustworthy as she believed Harris to be.

[022-024]The entrance to the forest was hardly more than three hundred paces up

[022-025]the river. An order of march had been arranged which was to be observed

[022-026]as closely as possible throughout the journey. At the head of the troop

[022-027]were Harris and Dick Sands, one armed with his long gun, the other with

[022-028]his Remington; next came Bat and Austin, each carrying a gun and a

[022-029]cutlass, then Mrs. Weldon and Jack, on horseback, closely followed by

[022-030]Tom and old Nan, while Actæon with the fourth Remington, and Hercules

[022-031]with a huge hatchet in his waist-belt, brought up the rear. [[dingo|Dingo]] had

[022-032]no especial place in the procession, but wandered to and fro at his

[022-033]pleasure. Ever since he had been cast ashore Dick had noticed a

[022-034]remarkable change in the dog's behaviour; the animal was in a constant

[022-035]state of agitation, always apparently on the search for some lost

[022-036]scent, and repeatedly giving vent to a low growl, which seemed to

[022-037]proceed from grief rather than from rage.

[022-038]As for Cousin Benedict, his movements were permitted to be nearly as

[022-039]erratic as Dingo's; nothing but a leading-string could possibly have

[022-040]kept him in the ranks. With his tin box under his arm, and his

[022-041]butterfly net in his hand, and his huge magnifying-glass suspended from

[022-042]his neck, he would be sometimes far ahead, sometimes a long way behind,

[022-043]and at the risk of being attacked by some venomous snake, would make

[022-044]frantic dashes into the tall grass whenever he espied some attractive

[022-045]orthoptera or other insect which he thought might be honoured by a

[022-046]place in his collection.

[022-047]In one hour after starting Mrs. Weldon had called to him a dozen times

[022-048]without the slightest effect. At last she told him seriously that if he

[022-049]would not give up chasing the [[entomology|insects]] at a distance, she should be

[022-050]obliged to take possession of his tin box.

[022-051]"Take away my box!" he cried, with as much horror as if she had

[022-052]threatened to tear out his vitals.

[022-053]"Yes, your box and your net too!"

[022-054]"My box and my net! but surely not my spectacles!" almost shrieked the

[022-055]excited entomologist.

[022-056]"Yes, and your spectacles as well!" added Mrs. Weldon mercilessly; "I

[022-057]am glad you have reminded me of another means of reducing you to

[022-058]obedience!"

[022-059]The triple penalty of which he was thus warned had the effect of

[022-060]keeping him from wandering away for the best part of the next hour, but

[022-061]he was soon once more missing from the ranks; he was manifestly

[022-062]incorrigible; the deprivation of box, net, and spectacles would, it was

[022-063]acknowledged, be utterly without avail to prevent him from rambling.

[022-064]Accordingly it was thought better to let him have his own way,

[022-065]especially as Hercules volunteered to keep his eye upon him, and to

[022-066]endeavour to guard the worthy naturalist as carefully as he would

[022-067]himself protect some precious specimen of a lepidoptera. Further

[022-068]anxiety on his account was thus put to rest.

[022-069][Illustration: The way across the forest could scarcely be called a

[022-070]path.]

[022-071]In spite of Harris's confident assertion that they were little likely

[022-072]to be molested by any of the nomad Indians, the whole company rejoiced

[022-073]in feeling that they were well armed, and they resolved to keep in a

[022-074]compact body. The way across the forest could scarcely be called a

[022-075]path; it was, in fact, little more than the track of animals, and

[022-076]progress along it was necessarily very slow; indeed it seemed

[022-077]impossible, at the rate they started, to accomplish more than five or

[022-078]six miles in the course of twelve hours.

[022-079]The weather was beautifully fine; the sun ascended nearly to the

[022-080]zenith, and its rays, descending almost perpendicularly, caused a

[022-081]degree of heat which, as Harris pointed out, would have been

[022-082]unendurable upon the open plain, but was here pleasantly tempered by

[022-083]the shelter of the foliage.

[022-084]Most of the trees were quite strange to them. To an experienced eye

[022-085]they were such as were remarkable more for their character then for

[022-086]their size. Here, on one side, was the bauhinia, or mountain ebony;

[022-087]there, on the other, the molompi or pterocarpus, its trunk exuding

[022-088]large quantities of resin, and of which the strong light wood makes

[022-089]excellent oars or paddles; further on were fustics heavily charged with

[022-090]colouring matter, and guaiacums, twelve feet in diameter, surpassing

[022-091]the ordinary kind in magnitude, yet far inferior in quality.

[022-092]Dick Sands kept perpetually asking Harris to tell him the names of all

[022-093]these trees and plants.

[022-094]"Have you never been on the coast of South America before?" replied

[022-095]Harris, without giving the explicit information that was sought.

[022-096]"Never," said Dick; "never before. Nor do I recollect ever having seen

[022-097]any one who has."

[022-098]"But surely you have explored the coasts of Columbia or Patagonia,"

[022-099]Harris continued.

[022-100]Dick avowed that he had never had the chance.

[022-101]"But has Mrs. Weldon never visited these parts? Our countrymen, I know,

[022-102]are great travellers."

[022-103]"No," answered Mrs. Weldon; "my husband's business called him

[022-104]occasionally to [[new-zealand|New Zealand]], but I have accompanied him nowhere else.

[022-105]With this part of Lower Bolivia we are totally unacquainted."

[022-106]"Then, madam, I can only assure you that you will see a most remarkable

[022-107]country, in every way a very striking contrast to the regions of Peru,

[022-108]Brazil, and the Argentine republic. Its animal and vegetable products

[022-109]would fill a naturalist with unbounded wonder. May I not declare it a

[022-110]lucky chance that has brought you here?"

[022-111]"Do not say chance, Mr. Harris, if you please."

[022-112]"Well, then, madam; providence, if you prefer it," said Harris, with

[022-113]the air of a man incapable of recognizing the distinction.

[022-114]After finding that there was no one amongst them who was acquainted in

[022-115]any way with the country through which they were travelling, Harris

[022-116]seemed to exhibit an evident pleasure in pointing out and describing by

[022-117]name the various wonders of the forest. Had Cousin Benedict's

[022-118]attainments included a knowledge of botany he would have found himself

[022-119]in a fine field for researches, and might perchance have discovered

[022-120]novelties to which his own name could be appended in the catalogues of

[022-121]science. But he was no botanist; in fact, as a rule, he held all

[022-122]blossoms in aversion, on the ground that they entrapped insects into

[022-123]their corollæ, and poisoned them sometimes with venomous juices. New

[022-124]and rare insects, however, seemed hereabouts to be wanting.

[022-125]Occasionally the soil became marshy, and they all had to wend their way

[022-126]over a perfect network of tiny rivulets that were affluents of the

[022-127]river from which they had started. Sometimes these rivulets were so

[022-128]wide that they could not be passed without a long search for some spot

[022-129]where they could be forded; their banks were all very damp, and in many

[022-130]places abounded with a kind of reed, which Harris called by its proper

[022-131]name of papyrus.

[022-132]As soon as the marshy district had been passed, the forest resumed its

[022-133]original aspect, the footway becoming narrow as ever. Harris pointed

[022-134]out some very fine ebony-trees, larger than the common sort, and

[022-135]yielding a wood darker and more durable than what is ordinarily seen in

[022-136]the market. There were also more mango-trees than might have been

[022-137]expected at this distance from the sea; a beautiful white lichen

[022-138]enveloped their trunks like a fur; but in spite of their luxuriant

[022-139]foliage and delicious fruit, Harris said that there was not a native

[022-140]who would venture to propagate the species, as the superstition of the

[022-141]country is that "whoever plants a mango, dies!"

[022-142][Illustration: Occasionally the soil became marshy.]

[022-143]At noon a halt was made for the purpose of rest and refreshment. During

[022-144]the afternoon they arrived at some gently rising ground, not the first

[022-145]slopes of hills, but an insulated plateau which appeared to unite

[022-146]mountains and plains. Notwithstanding that the trees were far less

[022-147]crowded and more inclined to grow in detached groups, the numbers of

[022-148]herbaceous plants with which the soil was covered rendered progress no

[022-149]less difficult than it was before. The general aspect of the scene was

[022-150]not unlike an East Indian jungle. Less luxuriant indeed than in the

[022-151]lower valley of the river, the vegetation was far more abundant than

[022-152]that of the temperate zones either of the Old or New continents. Indigo

[022-153]grew in great profusion, and, according to Harris's representation, was

[022-154]the most encroaching plant in the whole country; no sooner, he said,

[022-155]was a field left untilled, than it was overrun by this parasite, which

[022-156]sprang up with the rank growth of thistles or nettles.

[022-157]One tree which might have been expected to be common in this part of

[022-158]the continent seemed entirely wanting. This was the caoutchouc. Of the

[022-159]various trees from which India-rubber is procured, such as the Ficus

[022-160]prinoides, the Castilioa elastica, the Cecropia peltata, the Callophora

[022-161]utilis, the Cameraria latifolia, and especially the Siphonia elastica,

[022-162]all of which abound in the provinces of South America, not a single

[022-163]specimen was to be seen. Dick had promised to show Jack an

[022-164]India-rubber-tree, and the child, who had conjured up visions of

[022-165]squeaking dolls, balls, and other toys growing upon its branches, was

[022-166]loud and constant in his expressions of disappointment.

[022-167]"Never mind, my little man," said Harris; "have patience, and you shall

[022-168]see hundreds of India-rubber-trees when you get to the hacienda."

[022-169]"And will they be nice and elastic?" asked Jack, whose ideas upon the

[022-170]subject were of the vaguest order.

[022-171]"Oh, yes, they will stretch as long as you like," Harris answered,

[022-172]laughing. "But here is something to amuse you," he added, and as he

[022-173]spoke, he gathered a fruit that looked as tempting as a peach.

[022-174]"You are quite sure that it is safe to give it him?" said Mrs. Weldon

[022-175]anxiously.

[022-176]"To satisfy you, madam, I will eat one first myself."

[022-177]The example he set was soon followed by all the rest. The fruit was a

[022-178]mango; that which had been so opportunely discovered was of the sort

[022-179]that ripens in March or April; there is a later kind which ripens in

[022-180]September. With his mouth full of juice, Jack pronounced that it was

[022-181]very nice, but did not seem to be altogether diverted from his sense of

[022-182]disappointment at not coming to an India-rubber-tree. Evidently the

[022-183]little man thought himself rather injured.

[022-184]"And Dick promised me some humming-birds too!" he murmured.

[022-185]"Plenty of humming-birds for you, when you get to the farm; lots of

[022-186]them where my brother lives," said Harris.
