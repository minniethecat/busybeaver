[019-001]Above the shore hovered a large number of screeching birds, mostly of

[019-002]the swallow tribe, their black plumage shot with steelly blue, and

[019-003]shading off to a light brown at the top of the head. Now and then a few

[019-004]partridges of a greyish colour rose on wing, their necks entirely bare

[019-005]of feathers: the fearless manner in which the various birds all allowed

[019-006]themselves to be approached made [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] and [[dick-sands|Dick]] both wonder if

[019-007]the shores upon which they had been thrown were not so deserted that

[019-008]the sound of fire-arms was not known.

[019-009]On the edge of the reefs some pelicans (of the species known as

[019-010]_pelicanus minor_) were busily filling their pouches with tiny fish,

[019-011]and some gulls coming in from the open sea began to circle round the

[019-012]wreck: with these exceptions not a living creature appeared in sight.

[019-013]Benedict, no doubt, could have discovered many entomological novelties

[019-014]amongst the foliage, but these could give no more information than the

[019-015]birds as to the name of their habitat. Neither north, nor south, nor

[019-016]towards the forest, was there trace of rising smoke, or any footprint

[019-017]or other sign to indicate the presence of a human being.

[019-018]Dick's surprise was very great. He knew that the proximity of a native

[019-019]would have made [[dingo|Dingo]] bark aloud; but the dog gave no warning; he was

[019-020]running backwards and forwards, his tail lowered and his nose close to

[019-021]the ground; now and again he uttered a deep growl.

[019-022]"Look at Dingo!" said Mrs. Weldon; "how strange he is! he seems to be

[019-023]trying to discover a lost scent."

[019-024]After watching the dog for a time, she spoke again:--

[019-025]"Look, too, at [[negoro|Negoro]]! he and the dog seem to be on the same purpose!"

[019-026]"As to Negoro," said Dick, "I cannot concern myself with him now; he

[019-027]must do as he pleases; I have no further control over him; his service

[019-028]expires with the loss of the ship."

[019-029]Negoro was in fact walking to and fro, surveying the shore with the air

[019-030]of a man who was trying to recall some past experience to his

[019-031]recollection. His dogged taciturnity was too well known for any one to

[019-032]think of questioning him; every one was accustomed to let him go his

[019-033]own way, and when Dick noticed that he had gone towards the little

[019-034]river, and had disappeared behind the cliff, he thought no more about

[019-035]him. Dingo likewise had quite forgotten his enemy, and desisted from

[019-036]his growling.

[019-037]The first necessity for the [[shipwreck|shipwrecked]] party was to find a temporary

[019-038]shelter where they might take some refreshment. There was no lack of

[019-039]provisions; independently of the resources of the land, the ebbing tide

[019-040]had left upon the rocks the great bulk of the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]'s" stores, and

[019-041]the negroes had already collected several kegs of biscuit, and a number

[019-042]of cases of preserved meat, besides a variety of other supplies. All

[019-043]that they rescued they carefully piled up above high-water mark. As

[019-044]nothing appeared to be injured by the sea-water, the victualling of the

[019-045]party all seemed to be satisfactorily secure for the interval which

[019-046]must elapse (and they all believed it would not be long,) before they

[019-047]reached one of the villages which they presumed were close at hand.

[019-048]Dick, moreover, took the precaution of sending Hercules to get a small

[019-049]supply of fresh water from the river hard by, and the good-natured

[019-050]fellow returned carrying a whole barrel-full on his shoulder.

[019-051][Illustration: Surveying the shore with the air of a man who was trying

[019-052]to recall some past experience]

[019-053]Plenty of fuel was lying about, and whenever they wanted to light a

[019-054]fire they were sure of having an abundance of dead wood and the roots

[019-055]of the old mangroves. Old Tom, an inveterate smoker, always carried a

[019-056]tinder box in his pocket; this had been too tightly fastened to be

[019-057]affected by the moisture, and could always produce a spark upon

[019-058]occasion.

[019-059]Still they must have a shelter. Without some rest it was impossible to

[019-060]start upon a tour of exploration; accordingly, all interests were

[019-061]directed towards ascertaining where the necessary repose could be

[019-062]obtained.

[019-063]The honour of discovering where the desired retreat could be found fell

[019-064]to the lot of little Jack. Trotting about at the foot of the cliff, he

[019-065]came upon one of those grottoes which are constantly being found

[019-066]hollowed out in the rock by the vehement action of the waves in times

[019-067]of [[storm-at-sea|tempest]].

[019-068]"Here, look here!" cried the child; "here's a place!"

[019-069]"Well done, Jack!" answered his mother; "your lucky discovery is just

[019-070]what we wanted. If we were going to stay here any time we should have

[019-071]to do the same as the Swiss Family Robinson, and name the spot after

[019-072]you!"

[019-073]It was hardly more than twelve or fourteen feet square, and yet the

[019-074]grotto seemed to Jack to be a gigantic cavern. But narrow as its limits

[019-075]were, it was capacious enough to receive the entire party. It was a

[019-076]great satisfaction to Mrs. Weldon to observe that it was perfectly dry,

[019-077]and as the moon was just about her first quarter there was no

[019-078]likelihood of a tide rising to the foot of the cliff. At any rate, it

[019-079]was resolved that they might take up their quarters there for a few

[019-080]hours.

[019-081]Shortly after one o'clock the whole party were seated upon a carpet of

[019-082]seaweed round a repast consisting of preserved meat, biscuit, and water

[019-083]flavoured with a few drops of rum, of which Bat had saved a quart

[019-084]bottle from the wreck. Even Negoro had returned and joined the group;

[019-085]probably he had not cared to venture alone along the bank of the stream

[019-086]into the forest. He sat listening, as it seemed indifferently, to the

[019-087]various plans for the future that were being discussed, and did not

[019-088]open his mouth either by way of remonstrance or suggestion.

[019-089]Dingo was not forgotten, and had his share of food duly given him

[019-090]outside the grotto, where he was keeping guard.

[019-091]When the meal was ended, Mrs. Weldon, passing her arms round Jack, who

[019-092]was lounging half asleep with excitement and fatigue at her side, was

[019-093]the first to speak.

[019-094]"My dear Dick," she said, "in the name of us all, let me thank you for

[019-095]the services you have rendered us in our tedious time of difficulty. As

[019-096]you have been our captain at sea, let me beg you to be our guide upon

[019-097]land. We shall have perfect confidence in your judgment, and await your

[019-098]instructions as to what our next proceedings shall be."

[019-099]All eyes were turned upon Dick. Even Negoro appeared to be roused to

[019-100]curiosity, as if eager to know what he had to say.

[019-101]Dick did not speak for some moments. He was manifestly pondering what

[019-102]step he should advise. After a while he said,--

[019-103]"My own impression, Mrs. Weldon, is that we have been cast ashore upon

[019-104]one of the least-frequented parts of the coast of Peru, and that we are

[019-105]near the borders of the Pampas. In that case I should conclude that we

[019-106]are at a considerable distance from any village. Now, I should

[019-107]recommend that we stay here altogether for the coming night. To-morrow

[019-108]morning, two of us can start off on an [[exploration|exploring]] expedition. I

[019-109]entertain but little doubt that natives will be met with within ten or

[019-110]a dozen miles."

[019-111]Mrs. Weldon looked doubtful. Plainly she thought unfavourably of the

[019-112]project of separating the party. She reflected for a considerable time,

[019-113]and then asked,--

[019-114]"And who is to undertake the task of exploring?"

[019-115]Prompt was Dick's answer:--

[019-116]"Tom and I."

[019-117]"And leave us here?" suggested the lady.

[019-118]"Yes; to take care of you, there will be Hercules, Bat, Actæon and

[019-119]Austin. Negoro, too, I presume, means to remain here," said Dick,

[019-120]glancing towards the cook.

[019-121]"Perhaps," replied Negoro, sparing as ever of his words.

[019-122]"We shall take Dingo," added Dick; "likely enough he may be useful."

[019-123]At the sound of his name the dog had entered the grotto. A short bark

[019-124]seemed to testify his approval of Dick's proposal.

[019-125]Mrs. Weldon was silent. She looked sad and thoughtful. It was hard to

[019-126]reconcile herself to the division of the party. She was aware that the

[019-127]separation would not be for long, but she could not suppress a certain

[019-128]feeling of nervousness. Was it not possible that some natives,

[019-129]attracted by the wreck, would assault them in hopes of plunder?

[019-130]Every argument he could think of, Dick brought forward to reassure the

[019-131]lady. He told her that the Indians were perfectly harmless, and

[019-132]entirely different to the savage tribes of [[africa|Africa]] and Polynesia; there

[019-133]was no reason to apprehend any mischief, even if they should chance to

[019-134]encounter them, which was itself extremely unlikely. No doubt the

[019-135]separation would have its inconveniences, but they would be

[019-136]insignificant compared with the difficulty of traversing the country

[019-137]_en masse_. Tom and he would have far greater freedom if they went

[019-138]alone, and could make their investigations much more thoroughly.

[019-139]Finally he promised that if within two days they failed to discover

[019-140]human habitation, they would return to the grotto forthwith.

[019-141]"I confess, however," he added, "that I have little expectation of

[019-142]being able to ascertain our true position, until I have penetrated some

[019-143]distance into the country."

[019-144]There was nothing in Dick's representations but what commanded Mrs.

[019-145]Weldon's assent as reasonable. It was simply her own nervousness, she

[019-146]acknowledged, that made her hesitate; but it was only with extreme

[019-147]reluctance that she finally yielded to the proposition.

[019-148]"And what, Mr. Benedict, is your opinion of my proposal?" said Dick,

[019-149]turning to the entomologist.

[019-150]"I?" answered Cousin Benedict, looking somewhat bewildered, "Oh, I am

[019-151]agreeable to anything. I dare say I shall find some specimens. I think

[019-152]I will go and look at once."

[019-153]"Take my advice, and don't go far away," replied Dick.

[019-154]"All right; I shall take care of myself."

[019-155]"And don't be bringing back a lot of mosquitoes," said old Tom

[019-156]mischievously.

[019-157]With his box under his arm, the naturalist left the grotto.

[019-158]Negoro followed almost immediately. He did not take the same direction

[019-159]as Benedict up the cliff, but for the second time bent his steps

[019-160]towards the river, and proceeded along its bank till he was out of

[019-161]sight.

[019-162]It was not long before Jack's exertions told upon him, and he fell into

[019-163]a sound sleep. Mrs. Weldon having gently laid him on Nan's lap,

[019-164]wandered out and made her way to the water's edge. She was soon joined

[019-165]by Dick and the negroes, who wanted to see whether it was possible to

[019-166]get to the "Pilgrim," and secure any articles that might be serviceable

[019-167]for future use. The reef on which the schooner had stranded was now

[019-168]quite dry, and the carcase of the vessel which had been partially

[019-169]covered at high water was lying in the midst of _debris_ of the most

[019-170]promiscuous character. The wide difference between high and low-water

[019-171]mark caused Dick Sands no little surprise. He knew that the tides on

[019-172]the shores of the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] were very inconsiderable; in his own mind,

[019-173]however, he came to the conclusion that the phenomenon was to be

[019-174]explained by the unusually high wind that had been blowing on the coast.

[019-175]Not without emotion could Mrs. Weldon, or indeed any of them, behold

[019-176]the unfortunate ship upon which they had spent so many eventful days,

[019-177]lying dismasted on her side. But there was little time for sentiment.

[019-178]If they wished to visit the hull before it finally went to pieces there

[019-179]must be no delay.

[019-180]Hoisting themselves by some loose rigging that was hanging from the

[019-181]deck, Dick and several of the negroes contrived to make their way into

[019-182]the interior of the hull. Dick left his men to gather together all they

[019-183]could in the way of food and drink from the store-room, and himself

[019-184]went straight to the stern cabin, into which the water had not

[019-185]penetrated. Here he found four excellent Purday's Remington rifles and

[019-186]a hundred cartridges; with these he determined to arm his party, in

[019-187]case they should be attacked by Indians. He also chose six of the

[019-188]strongest of the cutlasses that are used for slicing up dead whales;

[019-189]and did not forget the little toy gun which was Jack's special

[019-190]property. Unexpectedly he found a pocket-compass, which he was only too

[019-191]glad to appropriate. What a boon it would have been had he discovered

[019-192]it earlier! The ship's charts in the fore-cabin were too much injured

[019-193]by water to be of any further service. Nearly everything was either

[019-194]lost or spoiled, but the misfortune was not felt very acutely because

[019-195]there was ample provision for a few days, and it seemed useless to

[019-196]burden themselves with more than was necessary. Dick hardly needed Mrs.

[019-197]Weldon's advice to secure all the money that might be on board, but

[019-198]after the most diligent search he failed to discover more than five

[019-199]hundred dollars. This was a subject of perplexity. Mrs. Weldon herself

[019-200]had had a considerably larger sum than this, and [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] was known

[019-201]always to keep a good reserve in hand. There was but one way to solve

[019-202]the mystery. Some one had been beforehand to the wreck. It could not be

[019-203]any of the negroes, as not one of them had for a moment left the

[019-204]grotto. Suspicion naturally fell upon Negoro, who had been out alone

[019-205]upon the shore. Morose and cold-blooded as the man was, Dick hardly

[019-206]knew why he should suspect him of the crime of theft; nevertheless, he

[019-207]determined to cross-examine him, and, if need be, to have him searched,

[019-208]as soon as he came back.
