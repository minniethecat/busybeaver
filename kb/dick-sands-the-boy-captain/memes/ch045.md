[045-001]The report proved perfectly true. On the 29th of April the [[slave-caravan|caravan]] had

[045-002]reached the village of Chitambo, the doctor so unwell that he was

[045-003]carried on a litter. The following night he was in great pain, and

[045-004]after repeatedly murmuring in a low voice, "Oh dear, oh dear!" he fell

[045-005]into a kind of stupor. A short time afterwards he called up Suzi, and

[045-006]having asked for some medicine, told his attendant that he should not

[045-007]require anything more.

[045-008]"You can go now."

[045-009][[About]] four o'clock next morning, when an anxious visit was made to his

[045-010]room, the doctor was found kneeling by the bed-side, his head in his

[045-011]hands, in the attitude of prayer. Suzi touched him, but his forehead

[045-012]was icy with the coldness of death. He had died in the night.

[045-013]His body was carried by those who loved him, and in spite of many

[045-014]obstacles was brought to Zanzibar, whence, nine months after his death,

[045-015]it was conveyed to England. On the 12th of April, 1874, it was interred

[045-016]in Westminster Abbey, counted worthy to be deposited amongst those whom

[045-017]the country most delights to honour.

[045-018]CHAPTER XV.

[045-019]AN EXCITING CHASE.

[045-020]To say the truth, it was the very vaguest of hopes to which [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]

[045-021]had been clinging, yet it was not without some thrill of disappointment

[045-022]that she heard from the lips of old Alvez himself that Dr. Livingstone

[045-023]had died at a little village on Lake Bangweolo. There had appeared to

[045-024]be a sort of a link binding her to the civilized world, but it was now

[045-025]abruptly snapped, and nothing remained for her but to make what terms

[045-026]she could with the base and heartless [[negoro|Negoro]].

[045-027]On the 14th, the day appointed for the interview, he made his

[045-028]appearance at the hut, firmly resolved to make no abatement in the

[045-029]terms that he had proposed, Mrs. Weldon, on her part, being equally

[045-030]determined not to yield to the demand.

[045-031]"There is only one condition," she avowed, "upon which I will

[045-032]acquiesce. My husband shall not be required to come up the country

[045-033]here."

[045-034]Negoro hesitated; at length he said that he would agree to her husband

[045-035]being taken by ship to Mossamedes, a small port in the south of Angola,

[045-036]much frequented by slavers, whither also, at a date hereafter to be

[045-037]fixed, Alvez should send herself with Jack and Benedict; the

[045-038]stipulation was confirmed that the ransom should be 100,000 dollars,

[045-039]and it was further made part of the contract that Negoro should be

[045-040]allowed to depart as an honest man.

[045-041]Mrs. Weldon felt she had gained an important point in thus sparing her

[045-042]husband the necessity of a journey to Kazonndé, and had no

[045-043]apprehensions about herself on her way to Mossamedes, knowing that it

[045-044]was to the interest of Alvez and Negoro alike to attend carefully to

[045-045]her wants.

[045-046]Upon the terms of the covenant being thus arranged, Mrs. Weldon wrote

[045-047]such a letter to her husband as she knew would bring him with all speed

[045-048]to Mossamedes, but she left it entirely to Negoro to represent himself

[045-049]in whatever light he chose. Once in possession of the document, Negoro

[045-050]lost no time in starting on his errand. The very next morning, taking

[045-051]with him about twenty negroes, he set off towards the north, alleging

[045-052]to Alvez as his motive for taking that direction, that he was not only

[045-053]going to embark somewhere at the mouth of the Congo, but that he was

[045-054]anxious to keep as far as possible from the prison-houses of the

[045-055]Portuguese, with which already he had been involuntarily only too

[045-056]familiar.

[045-057]After his departure, Mrs. Weldon resolved to make the best of her

[045-058]period of imprisonment, aware that it could hardly be less than four

[045-059]months before he would return. She had no desire to go beyond the

[045-060]precincts assigned her, even had the privilege been allowed her; but

[045-061]warned by Negoro that Hercules was still free, and might at any time

[045-062]attempt a rescue, Alvez had no thought of permitting her any

[045-063]unnecessary [[freedom|liberty]]. Her life therefore soon resumed its previous

[045-064]monotony.

[045-065]The daily routine went on within the enclosure pretty much as in other

[045-066]parts of the town, the women all being employed in various labours for

[045-067]the benefit of their husbands and masters. The rice was pounded with

[045-068]wooden pestles; the maize was peeled and winnowed, previously to

[045-069]extracting the granulous substance for the drink which they call

[045-070]_mtyellé_; the sorghum had to be gathered in, the season of its

[045-071]ripening being marked by festive observances; there was a fragrant oil

[045-072]to be expressed from a kind of olive named the _mpafoo_; the cotton had

[045-073]to be spun on spindles, which were hardly less than a foot and a half

[045-074]in length; there was the bark of trees to be woven into textures for

[045-075]wearing; the manioc had to be dug up, and the cassava procured from its

[045-076]roots; and besides all this, there was the preparation of the soil for

[045-077]its future plantings, the usual productions of the country being the

[045-078]_moritsané_ beans, growing in pods fifteen inches long upon stems

[045-079]twenty feet high, the _arachides_, from which they procure a

[045-080]serviceable oil, the _chilobé_ pea, the blossoms of which are used to

[045-081]give a flavour to the insipid sorghum, cucumbers, of which the seeds

[045-082]are roasted as chestnuts, as well as the common crops of coffee, sugar,

[045-083]onions, guavas, and sesame.

[045-084]To the women's lot, too, falls the manipulation of all the fermented

[045-085]drinks, the _malafoo_, made from bananas, the _pombé_, and various

[045-086]other liquors. Nor should the care of all the domestic animals be

[045-087]forgotten; the cows that will not allow themselves to be milked unless

[045-088]they can see their calf, or a stuffed representative of it; the

[045-089]short-horned heifers that not unfrequently have a hump; the goats that,

[045-090]like slaves, form part of the currency of the country; the pigs, the

[045-091]sheep, and the poultry.

[045-092]The men, meanwhile, smoke their hemp or tobacco, hunt buffaloes or

[045-093]elephants, or are hired by the dealers to join in the slave-raids; the

[045-094]harvest of slaves, in fact, being a thing of as regular and periodic

[045-095]recurrence as the ingathering of the maize.

[045-096]In her daily strolls, Mrs. Weldon would occasionally pause to watch the

[045-097]women, but they only responded to her notice by a long stare or by a

[045-098]hideous grimace; a kind of natural instinct made them hate a white

[045-099]skin, and they had no spark of commiseration for the stranger who had

[045-100]been brought among them; Halima, however, was a marked exception, she

[045-101]grew more and more devoted to her mistress, and by degrees, the two

[045-102]became able to exchange many sentences in the native dialect.

[045-103]Jack generally accompanied his mother. Naturally enough he longed to

[045-104]get outside the enclosure, but still he found considerable amusement in

[045-105]watching the birds that built in a huge baobab that grew within; there

[045-106]were maraboos making their nests with twigs; there were

[045-107]scarlet-throated _souimangas_ with nests like weaver-birds; widow birds

[045-108]that helped themselves liberally to the thatch of the huts; _calaos_

[045-109]with their tuneful song; grey parrots, with bright red tails, called

[045-110]_roufs_ by the Manyuema, who apply the same name to their reigning

[045-111]chiefs; and insect-eating _drongos_, like grey linnets with large red

[045-112]beaks. Hundreds of butterflies flitted about, especially in the

[045-113]neighbourhood of the brooks; but these were more to the taste of Cousin

[045-114]Benedict than of little Jack; over and over again the child expressed

[045-115]his regret that he could not see over the walls, and more than ever he

[045-116]seemed to miss his friend [[dick-sands|Dick]], who had taught him to climb a mast, and

[045-117]who he was sure would have fine fun with him in the branches of the

[045-118]trees, which were growing sometimes to the height of a hundred feet.

[045-119][Illustration: The insufferable heat had driven all the residents

[045-120]within the depót indoors.]

[045-121]So long as the supply of [[entomology|insects]] did not fail, Benedict would have been

[045-122]contented to stay on without a murmur in his present quarters. True,

[045-123]without his glasses he worked at a disadvantage; but he had had the

[045-124]good fortune to discover a minute bee that forms its cells in the holes

[045-125]of worm-eaten wood, and a "sphex" that practises the craft of the

[045-126]cuckoo, and deposits its eggs in an abode not prepared by itself.

[045-127]Mosquitos abounded in swarms, and the worthy naturalist was so covered

[045-128]by their stings as to be hardly recognizable; but when Mrs. Weldon

[045-129]remonstrated with him for exposing himself so unnecessarily, he merely

[045-130]scratched the irritated places on his skin, and said--

[045-131]"It is their instinct, you know; it is their instinct."

[045-132]On the 17th of June an adventure happened to him which was attended

[045-133]with unexpected consequences. It was about eleven o'clock in the

[045-134]morning. The insufferable heat had driven all the residents within the

[045-135]dépôt indoors, and not a native was to be seen in the streets of

[045-136]Kazonndé. Mrs. Weldon was dozing; Jack was fast asleep. Benedict

[045-137]himself, sorely against his will, for he heard the hum of many an

[045-138]insect in the sunshine, had been driven to the seclusion of his cabin,

[045-139]and was falling into an involuntary siesta.

[045-140]Suddenly a buzz was heard, an insect's wing vibrating some fifteen

[045-141]thousand beats a second!

[045-142]"A hexapod!" cried Benedict, sitting up.

[045-143]Short-sighted though he was, his hearing was acute, and his perception

[045-144]made him thoroughly convinced that he was in proximity to some giant

[045-145]specimen of its kind. Without moving from his seat he did his utmost to

[045-146]ascertain what it was; he was determined not to flinch from the

[045-147]sharpest of stings if only he could get the chance of capturing it.

[045-148]Presently he made out a large black speck flitting about in the few

[045-149]rays of daylight that were allowed to penetrate the hut. With bated

[045-150]breath he waited in eager expectation. The insect, after long hovering

[045-151]above him, finally settled on his head. A smile of satisfaction played

[045-152]about his lips as he felt it crawling lightly through his hair. Equally

[045-153]fearful of missing or injuring it, he restrained his first impulse to

[045-154]grasp it in his hand.

[045-155]"I will wait a minute," he thought; "perhaps it may creep down my nose;

[045-156]by squinting a little perhaps I shall be able to see it."

[045-157]For some moments hope alternated with fear. There sat Benedict with

[045-158]what he persuaded himself was some new African hexapod perched upon his

[045-159]head, and agitated by doubts as to the direction in which it would

[045-160]move. Instead of travelling in the way he reckoned along his nose,

[045-161]might it not crawl behind his ears or down his neck, or, worse than

[045-162]all, resume its flight in the air?

[045-163]Fortune seemed inclined to favour him. After threading the entanglement

[045-164]of the naturalist's hair the insect was felt to be descending his

[045-165]forehead. With a [[courage|fortitude]] not unworthy of the Spartan who suffered his

[045-166]breast to be gnawed by a fox, nor of the Roman hero who plunged his

[045-167]hand into the red-hot coals, Benedict endured the tickling of the six

[045-168]small feet, and made not a motion that might frighten the creature into

[045-169]taking wing. After making repeated circuits of his forehead, it passed

[045-170]just between his eyebrows; there was a moment of deep suspense lest it

[045-171]should once more go upwards; but it soon began to move again; neither

[045-172]to the right nor to the left did it turn, but kept straight on over the

[045-173]furrows made by the constant rubbing of the spectacles, right along the

[045-174]arch of the cartilage till it reached the extreme tip of the nose. Like

[045-175]a couple of movable lenses, Benedict's two eyes steadily turned

[045-176]themselves inwards till they were directed to the proper point.

[045-177][Illustration: Before long the old black speck was again flitting just

[045-178]above his head. _Page_ 432.]

[045-179]"Good!" he whispered to himself.

[045-180]He was exulting at the discovery that what he had been waiting for so

[045-181]patiently was a rare specimen of the tribe of the Cicindelidæ, peculiar

[045-182]to the districts of Southern [[africa|Africa]].
