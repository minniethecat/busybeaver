The termite is one of the [[entomology|insects]] that captures the attention of [[benedict|Cousin Benedict]] during the [[africa|Africa]]n trek, reflecting the novel's [[entomology|entomological]] subplot and Jules Verne's fascination with natural history. The massive termite mounds of the [[africa|Africa]]n savanna serve both as landmarks and as objects of scientific wonder.

## In the Narrative

Termites appear in the [[africa|Africa]]n section of the novel as part of the landscape that the party traverses. For [[benedict|Cousin Benedict]], the entomo[[ships-log|log]]ist whose "comfortable museum at [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]" (015-031) had been left far behind, the [[africa|Africa]]n termites offer a new frontier of scientific observation.

The termite mounds, some reaching considerable heights, are among the features of the [[africa|Africa]]n interior that distinguish it from the [[san-francisco|South American]] landscape the party believed they were crossing. Like the [[elephant|elephant tracks]], the presence of termite architecture serves as an accumulating clue about their true location.

## Entomological Context

[[benedict|Benedict]]'s interest in termites connects to his broader passion for [[entomology|insects]], already established through his pursuit of the [[stag-beetle|Stag Beetle]] and other specimens. Termites represent a different order of insect life — social, architectural, and collaborative — in contrast to the solitary beetles that typically attract [[benedict|Benedict]]'s attention.

## Significance

Within the novel's structure, [[entomology|insects]] serve as [[benedict|Benedict]]'s lens through which the [[africa|Africa]]n landscape is observed. Where other characters see danger or hardship, [[benedict|Benedict]] sees entomo[[ships-log|log]]ical opportunity — a comic counterpoint that Verne uses to lighten the narrative's darker passages. The termite, like the [[stag-beetle|Stag Beetle]] and the [[tse-tse-fly|Tsetse Fly]], is part of this running entomo[[ships-log|log]]ical motif.
