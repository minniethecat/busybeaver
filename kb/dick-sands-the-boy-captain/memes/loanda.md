# Saint Paul de Loanda

**Saint Paul de Loanda** (modern Luanda) is the capital of [[angola|Angola]] and the principal port of the Portuguese colony on [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] coast of [[africa|Africa]]. In *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*, it appears repeatedly as a geographic benchmark — a distant point of relative safety that the stranded survivors measure themselves against as they navigate the dangers of the [[angola|Angola]]n interior.

## Geography

- **Type**: Port city
- **Real or fictional**: Real
- **Relevant to**: [[africa|Africa]]n interior journey, Portuguese colonial administration, Livingstone's trans-[[africa|Africa]]n expedition
- **First mentioned**: Chapter 27 (027-050)

Loanda is situated on [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] coast of [[angola|Angola]], which Verne describes as extending "from the [[congo|Zaire]] on the north to the Nourse on the south" (027-050). Together with [[benguela|Benguela]], it was one of the two chief ports of the Portuguese colony. Verne notes that at the time of the novel's setting, "the interior of the country had been almost entirely unexplored" (027-050), with very few travellers having ventured beyond the coastal settlements.

## Role in the Narrative

Loanda functions primarily as a symbol of civilization and safety that remains tantalizingly out of reach for the protagonists. After being stranded in the interior, the survivors repeatedly calculate their distance from Loanda as a measure of their predicament. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] estimates that Kazonndé "must be about 400 miles from S. Paul de Loanda, and consequently that it could hardly be more than 250 miles from the part of the [[coanza|Coanza]] where they were now encamped" (035-152). Despite the relative proximity, the journey remains impossible under their circumstances.

The Portuguese colonial presence at Loanda is depicted as ineffective against the interior [[slavery|slave trade]]. Verne observes that "the Portuguese authorities at Loanda and [[benguela|Benguela]] had practically no power to prevent it" (037-077), with the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]]s moving freely toward the interior while the coastal barracks were "crowded to overflowing with prisoners" (037-077). This institutional weakness is a recurring theme in the novel's critique of the [[slavery|slave trade]].

## Key Events at This Location

- **Livingstone's arrival (1854)** — David Livingstone reached "St. Paul de Loanda on the 31st of May, 1854" (027-068), becoming the first European explorer to cross the [[africa|Africa]]n continent from south to east. Verne references this achievement as a landmark in [[exploration|African exploration]].

- **[[negoro|Negoro]]'s imprisonment** — [[negoro|The Portuguese cook]] [[negoro|Negoro]] recounts being condemned "to end my days in the penitentiary at St. Paul de Loanda" (028-061), from which he eventually escaped. This backstory reveals [[negoro|Negoro]]'s criminal past and his connection to the [[angola|Angola]]n [[slavery|slave trade]].

- **Livingstone's second arrival (1854)** — After his trans-continental journey, Livingstone again "arrived at St. Paul de Loanda, having traversed the continent in about two years" (044-042), before setting off again along the [[coanza|Coanza River]].

## Description in the Text

Verne presents Loanda as part of a broader colonial landscape marked by the tension between official administration and the rampant [[slavery|slave trade]]. The missionary accounts and Portuguese trader reports "frequented the route from St. Paul de Loanda, by San Salvador to the [[congo|Zaire]]" (027-166), providing Europeans with knowledge of the interior routes.

> "The Portuguese authorities at Loanda and Benguela had practically no power to prevent it. The barracks on the shore were crowded to overflowing with prisoners, the few slave-ships that managed to elude the cruisers being quite inadequate to embark them." (037-077)

The city represents a paradox: a seat of colonial authority that cannot project its power beyond the coastline, leaving the interior to the mercies of [[slavery|slave trade]]rs like [[jose-antonio-alvez|Alvez]] and the clandestine networks that connect Loanda to inland markets like [[kazonnde|Kazonndé]].

## Historical Context

At the time of the novel's setting (1873–1874), Loanda had been a Portuguese possession for nearly three centuries. Its role in the transatlantic [[slavery|slave trade]] was well documented, though by the 1870s the trade had been officially abolished in Portuguese territories. Verne draws on contemporary accounts — notably those of David Livingstone and the Portuguese traders of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries (027-166) — to depict a colony where official prohibitions had little practical effect in the interior.

The city's connection to Livingstone's explorations was a point of contemporary interest for Verne's readers. Livingstone's trans-[[africa|Africa]]n journey (1852–1856) had made Loanda a household name among those following [[exploration|African exploration]], and Verne incorporates this historical context to lend authenticity to his fictional narrative.

Ultimately, [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] realizes that the escaping party might not "actually arrive at S. Paul de Loanda; but that would be immaterial, as they would be sure of finding help anywhere in the colonies of Lower Guinea" (047-173). The city thus serves its narrative purpose as a deferred destination — a symbol of safety that the characters never need to physically reach.
