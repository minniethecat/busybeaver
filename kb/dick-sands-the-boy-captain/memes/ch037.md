[037-002]_11th_.--Last night encamped under some large trees on the skirts of a

[037-003]forest. Several escaped prisoners recaptured; their punishment

[037-004]barbarously cruel. Loud roaring of lions and hyenas heard at nightfall,

[037-005]also snorting of hippopotamuses; probably some lake or water-course not

[037-006]far off. Tired, but could not sleep; heard a rustling in the grass;

[037-007]felt sure that something was going to attack me; what could I do? I had

[037-008]no gun. For [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]'s sake, must, if possible, preserve my life.

[037-009]The night was dark; no moon; two eyes gleamed upon me; I was about to

[037-010]utter a cry of alarm; fortunately, I suppressed it; the creature that

[037-011]had sprung to my feet was [[dingo|Dingo]]! The dog licked my hands all over,

[037-012]persisting in rubbing his neck against them, evidently to make me feel

[037-013]there; found a reed fastened to the well-known collar upon which the

[037-014]initials S.V. had so often awakened our curiosity; breaking open the

[037-015]reed, I took a note from inside; it was too dark for me to see to read

[037-016]it. I tried, by caressing Dingo, to detain him; but the dog appeared to

[037-017]know that his mission with me was at an end; he licked my hands

[037-018]affectionately, made a sudden bound, and disappeared in the long grass

[037-019]as mysteriously as he had come. The howling of the wild beasts

[037-020]increased. How I dreaded that the faithful creature would become their

[037-021]prey! No more sleep this night for me. It seemed that daylight would

[037-022]never dawn; at length it broke with the suddenness that marks a

[037-023]tropical morn. I was able cautiously to read my note; the handwriting,

[037-024]I knew at a glance, was that of Hercules; there were but a few lines in

[037-025]pencil:--

[037-026]"Mrs. Weldon and Jack carried away in a kitanda.

[037-027]"Harris and [[negoro|Negoro]] both with them. Mr. Benedict too. Only a few marches

[037-028]ahead, but cannot be communicated with at present. Found Dingo wounded

[037-029]by a gun-shot. Dear Mr. [[dick-sands|Dick]], do not despair; keep up your courage. I

[037-030]may help you yet.

[037-031]"Your ever true and faithful

[037-032]"HERCULES."

[037-033]As far as it went, this intelligence was satisfactory. A kitanda, I

[037-034]know, is a kind of litter made of dry grass, protected by a curtain,

[037-035]and carried on the shoulders of two men by a long bamboo. What a relief

[037-036]to know that Mrs. Weldon and Jack have been spared the miseries of this

[037-037]dreadful march! May I not indulge the hope of seeing them at Kazonndé?

[037-038]_12th_.--The prisoners getting more and more weary and worn out.

[037-039]Blood-stains on the way still more conspicuous. Many poor wretches are

[037-040]a mass of wounds. One poor woman for two days has carried her dead

[037-041]child, from which she refuses to be parted.

[037-042]_16th_.--Small-pox raging; the road strewn with corpses. Still ten days

[037-043]before we reach Kazonndé. Just passed a tree from which slaves who had

[037-044]died from hunger were hanging by the neck.

[037-045]_18th_.--Must not give in, but I am almost exhausted. Rains have

[037-046]ceased. We are to make what the dealers call _trikesa_, extra marches

[037-047]in the after-part of the day. Road very steep; runs through _nyassi_,

[037-048]tall grass of which the stalks scratch my face, and the seeds get under

[037-049]my tattered clothes and make my skin smart painfully. My boots

[037-050]fortunately are thick, and have not worn out. More slaves sick and

[037-051]abandoned to take their chance. Provisions running very short; soldiers

[037-052]and pagazis must be satisfied, otherwise they desert; consequently the

[037-053]slaves are all but starved. "They can eat each other," say the agents.

[037-054]A young slave, apparently in good health, dropped down dead. It made me

[037-055]think of Livingstone's description of how free-born men, reduced to

[037-056]slavery, will suddenly press their hand on their side, and die of a

[037-057]broken heart.

[037-058][Illustration: More slaves sick, and abandoned to take their chance.]

[037-059]_24th_.--Twenty captives, incapable any longer of keeping pace with the

[037-060]rest, put to death by the havildars, the Arab chief offering no

[037-061]opposition. Poor old Nan one of the victims of this horrible butchery.

[037-062]My foot struck her corpse as I passed, but I was not permitted to give

[037-063]her a decent burial. Poor old Nan! the first of the survivors of the

[037-064]"[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" to go to her long rest! Poor old Nan!

[037-065]Every night I watch for Dingo; but he never comes. Has Hercules nothing

[037-066]more to communicate? or has any mishap befallen him? If he is alive he

[037-067]will do what mortal strength can do to aid us.

[037-068]CHAPTER IX.

[037-069]KAZONNDÉ.

[037-070]By the 26th of May, when the [[slave-caravan|caravan]] reached Kazonndé the number of the

[037-071]slaves had diminished by more than half, so numerous had been the

[037-072]casualties along the road. But the dealers were quite prepared to make

[037-073]a market of their loss; the demand for slaves was very great, and the

[037-074]price must be raised accordingly.

[037-075]Angola at that time was the scene of a large negro-traffic, and as the

[037-076]caravans principally wended their way towards the interior, the

[037-077]Portuguese authorities at Loanda and Benguela had practically no power

[037-078]to prevent it. The barracks on the shore were crowded to overflowing

[037-079]with prisoners, the few slave-ships that managed to elude the cruisers

[037-080]being quite inadequate to embark the whole number for the Spanish

[037-081]colonies to America.

[037-082]Kazonndé, the point whence the caravans diverge to the various parts of

[037-083]the lake district, is situated three miles from the mouth of the

[037-084]Coanza, and is one of the most important _lakonis_, or markets of the

[037-085]province. The open marketplace where the slaves are exposed for sale is

[037-086]called the _chitoka_.

[037-087]All the larger towns of [[africa|Central Africa]] are divided into two distinct

[037-088]parts; one occupied by the Arab, Portuguese, or native merchants, and

[037-089]containing their slave-barracks; the other being the residence of the

[037-090]negro king, often a fierce drunken potentate, whose rule is a reign of

[037-091]terror, and who lives by subsidies allowed him by the traders.

[037-092]The commercial quarter of Kazonndé now belonged to José Antonio Alvez.

[037-093]It was his largest dépôt, although he had another at Bihé, and a third

[037-094]at Cassangé, where Cameron subsequently met him. It consisted of one

[037-095]long street, on each side of which were groups of flat-roofed houses

[037-096]called _tembés_, built of rough earth, and provided with square yards

[037-097]for cattle. The end of it opened into the _chitoka_, which was

[037-098]surrounded by the barracks. Above the houses some fine banyan-trees

[037-099]waved their branches, surmounted here and there by the crests of

[037-100]graceful palms. There was at least a score of birds of prey that

[037-101]hovered about the streets, and came down to perform the office of

[037-102]public scavengers. At no great distance flowed the Loohi, a river not

[037-103]yet explored, but which is supposed to be an affluent or sub-affluent

[037-104]of the Congo.

[037-105][Illustration: Adjoining the commercial quarter was the royal

[037-106]residence.]

[037-107]Adjoining the commercial quarter was the royal residence, nothing more

[037-108]nor less than a collection of dirty huts, extending over an area of

[037-109]nearly a square mile.

[037-110]Some of these huts were unenclosed; others were surrounded by a

[037-111]palisade of reeds, or by a hedge of bushy figs.

[037-112]In an enclosure within a papyrus fence were about thirty huts

[037-113]appropriated to the king's slaves, another group for his wives, and in

[037-114]the middle, almost hidden by a plantation of manioc, a _tembé_ larger

[037-115]and loftier than the rest, the abode of the monarch himself.

[037-116]He had sorely declined from the dignity and importance of his

[037-117]predecessors, and his army, which by the early Portuguese traders had

[037-118]been estimated at 20,000, now numbered less than 4000 men; no longer

[037-119]could he afford, as in the good old time, to order a sacrifice of

[037-120]twenty-five or thirty slaves at one offering.

[037-121]His name was Moené Loonga. Little over fifty, he was prematurely aged

[037-122]by drink and debauchery, and scarcely better than a maniac. His

[037-123]subjects, officers, and ministers, were all liable to be mutilated at

[037-124]his pleasure, and noses and ears, feet and hands, were cut off

[037-125]unsparingly whenever his caprice so willed it. His death would have

[037-126]been a cause of regret to no one, with the exception, perhaps, of

[037-127]Alvez, who was on very good terms with him. Alvez, moreover, feared

[037-128]that in the event of the present king's death, the succession of his

[037-129]chief wife, Queen Moena, might be disputed, and that his dominions

[037-130]would be invaded by a younger and more active neighbour, one of the

[037-131]kings of Ukusu, who had already seized upon some villages dependent on

[037-132]the government of Kazonndé, and who was in alliance with a rival trader

[037-133]named Tipo-Tipo, a man of pure Arab extraction, from whom Cameron

[037-134]afterwards received a visit at Nyangwé.

[037-135]To all intents and purposes Alvez was the real sovereign of the

[037-136]district, having fostered the vices of the brutalized king till he had

[037-137]him completely in his power. He was a man considerably advanced in

[037-138]years; he was not (as his name might imply) a white man, but had merely

[037-139]assumed his Portuguese title for purposes of business; his true name

[037-140]was Kendélé, and he was a pure negro by birth, being a native of Dondo

[037-141]on the Coanza. He had commenced life as a slave-dealer's agent, and was

[037-142]now on his way towards becoming a first-class trader; that is to say,

[037-143]he was a consummate rascal under the guise of an honest man. He it was

[037-144]whom Cameron met at the end of 1874 at Kilemba, the capital of Urua, of

[037-145]which Kasongo is chief, and with whose caravan he travelled to Bihé, a

[037-146]distance of seven hundred miles.

[037-147]It was midday when the caravan entered Kazonndé. The journey from the

[037-148]Coanza had lasted thirty-eight days, more than five weeks of misery as

[037-149]great as was within human power to endure. Amidst the noise of drums

[037-150]and coodoo-horns the slaves were conducted to the marketplace. The

[037-151]soldiers of the caravan discharged their guns into the air, and old

[037-152]Alvez' resident retinue responded with a similar salute. The bandits,

[037-153]than which the soldiers were nothing better, were delighted to meet

[037-154]again, and would celebrate their return by a season of riot and excess.

[037-155]The slaves, reduced to a total of about two hundred and fifty, were

[037-156]many of them almost dead from exhaustion; the forks were removed from

[037-157]their necks, though the chains were still retained, and the whole of

[037-158]them were driven into barracks that were unfit even for cattle, to

[037-159]await (in company with 1200 to 1500 other captives already there) the

[037-160]great market which would be held two days hence.

[037-161]The _pagazis_, after delivering their loads of ivory, would only stay

[037-162]to receive their payment of a few yards of calico or other stuff, and

[037-163]would then depart at once to join some other caravan.

[037-164]On being relieved from the forks which they had carried for so many

[037-165]weary days, Tom and his companions heartily wrung each other's hands,

[037-166]but they could not venture to utter one word of mutual encouragement.

[037-167]The three younger men, more full of life and vigour, had resisted the

[037-168]effects of the fatigue, but poor old Tom was nearly exhausted, and had

[037-169]the march been protracted for a few more days he must have shared Nan's

[037-170]fate and been left behind, a prey to the wild beasts.

[037-171]Upon their arrival all four were packed into a narrow cell, where some

[037-172]food was provided, and the door was immediately locked upon them.

[037-173]The _chitoka_ was now almost deserted, and Dick Sands was left there

[037-174]under the special charge of a havildar: he lost no opportunity of

[037-175]peering into every hut in the hope of catching a glimpse of Mrs.

[037-176]Weldon, who, if Hercules had not misinformed him, had come on hither

[037-177]just in front.

[037-178]But he was very much perplexed. He could well understand that Mrs.

[037-179]Weldon, if still a prisoner, would be kept out of sight, but why Negoro

[037-180]and Harris did not appear to triumph over him in his humiliation was

[037-181]quite a mystery to him. It was likely enough that the presence of

[037-182]either one or the other of them would be the signal for himself to be

[037-183]exposed to fresh indignity, or even to torture, but Dick would have

[037-184]welcomed the sight of them at Kazonndé, were it only as an indication

[037-185]that Mrs. Weldon and Jack were there also.
