# Freedom

The state of liberty from bondage, the condition that the rescued [[africa|Africa]]ns have won and that the captured protagonists desperately seek to regain in *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*.

## Definition

Freedom is the power to act, speak, or think without hindrance or restraint. In the context of the novel, freedom has specific legal and existential dimensions: freedom from chattel slavery, freedom of movement, and freedom from captivity.

## Role in the Novel

The rescued [[africa|Africa]]ns embody freedom hard-won. When [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] asks about their status, they confirm "that they had regained their freedom" (005-077). Old [[tom|Tom]]'s personal history traces the arc from enslavement to liberation: he was sold as a child, brought from [[africa|Africa]], and eventually "recovered his freedom" along with his companions (005-105).

The loss of freedom drives the novel's second half. Once captured by [[slavery|slave-trade]]rs, the party experiences the condition they had previously only understood abstractly. Their freedom becomes the central stake of every decision. The possibility of landing at [[valparaiso|Valparaiso]] represented for them "freedom" of movement and safety (003-072); now, freedom means something far more basic—escape from bondage.

The novel thus presents freedom as both a legal status (abolished by British and French law) and an existential condition (constantly threatened by the ongoing [[slavery|slave trade]]). The tension between these two states—freedom in law versus slavery in practice—is the novel's deepest moral concern.
