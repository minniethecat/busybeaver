# Exploration

The 19th-century European exploration of [[africa|Africa]]'s interior that serves as both historical backdrop and narrative framework in *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*.

## Definition

African exploration in the 19th century was the systematic European effort to map and document the continent's interior, driven by geographical curiosity, missionary zeal, commercial interests, and imperial ambition. By 1878, explorers like David Livingstone, Henry Morton Stanley, Richard Burton, John Hanning Speke, and James Grant had become household names.

## Role in the Novel

Verne situates the protagonists' journey within the context of African exploration. The novel references Livingstone's observations of [[slavery|slave-trade]] devastation: "Livingstone, upon returning to a district, immediately after one of these ruthless raids, said that he could never have recognized it" (026-196–198). The narrator also mentions "all other travellers, Grant, Speke, Burton, Cameron, Stanley" (026-199) as witnesses to the "theatre of the barbarous warfare" in [[africa|Central Africa]] (026-200).

The protagonists' own journey becomes an unintended exploration: [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] proposes to "start upon a tour of exploration" to find their way to safety (019-060). The novel's detailed geographical descriptions—rivers, plateaus, forest regions—reflect the era's hunger for knowledge about the "[[africa|Dark Continent]]."

[[harris|Harris]], the [[slavery|slave-trade]]r, cynically mocks the exploration pretense: "All this exploration, you know, is ruinous to our" trade (028-159), revealing how exploration and exploitation were intertwined.

## Historical Context

The novel references specific exploration milestones: "In March an exploration of the Rovouma was made" (044-083), and a published work titled "The Exploration of the" region (044-100). The account of "six weeks" during which explorers "made two separate explorations of" an area (044-115) reflects the methodical pace of the era's expeditions. The final chapter mentions "an exploration of a district of [[africa|Central Africa]]" (050-047), tying the narrative explicitly to the exploration tradition.

[[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] had previously asked about "the result of your explorations in [[new-zealand|New Zealand]]" (006-153), showing that the exploration theme extends beyond [[africa|Africa]]—it represents the broader 19th-century drive to map the unknown world.
