# Mossâmedes

**Mossâmedes** (modern Namibe) is a port city on the southern coast of [[angola|Angola]]. In the climactic final chapters of *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*, it becomes the focal point of the narrative — the destination that both the escaping survivors and the treacherous [[negoro|Negoro]] race toward, with freedom or captivity hanging in the balance.

## Geography

- **Type**: Port city
- **Real or fictional**: Real
- **First mentioned**: Chapter 28 (028-145)

Mossâmedes is described as "a small port in the south of [[angola|Angola]]" (045-035), situated geographically apart from the main slave-trading routes that passed through the northern ports. Its southern location made it strategically significant: while the northern ports of [[loanda|Loanda]] and [[benguela|Benguela]] were increasingly policed, Mossâmedes remained one of the last viable points for clandestine maritime activity on the [[angola|Angola]]n coast.

## Strategic Importance in the Slave Trade

The [[slavery|slave trade]]r [[harris|Harris]] explains that due to Portuguese authorities and English cruisers, the export of slaves had been "almost put a stop to" (028-145) at the northern ports. He identifies Mossâmedes as the critical exception:

> "Down to the south, near Mossamedes, is the only part where it can be attempted with any chance of success. To pass a caravan through Benguela or Loande is an utter impossibility; neither the governors nor the chefés will listen to a word of reason." (028-145–028-147)

This geographic reality shapes the entire final act of the novel. The [[slavery|slave trade]]r [[jose-antonio-alvez|Alvez]] uses Mossâmedes as his intended export point, and it is to this port that [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] is to be taken under [[negoro|Negoro]]'s escort.

## The Race to Mossâmedes

The final chapters of the novel revolve around a desperate race to reach Mossâmedes. [[negoro|Negoro]] negotiates a deal in which [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]'s husband would be "taken by ship to Mossamedes, a small port in the south of [[angola|Angola]], much frequented by slavers" (045-035), while she, [[jack-weldon|Jack]], and [[benedict|Cousin Benedict]] would be sent separately by Alvez. [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] perceives the danger: she had "apprehensions about herself on her way to Mossamedes, knowing that it was to the interest of Alvez and [[negoro|Negoro]] alike to attend carefully to her wants" (045-043).

She writes a letter designed to bring her husband "with all speed to Mossamedes" (045-048), but "left it entirely to [[negoro|Negoro]] to represent himself in whatever light he chose" (045-048). Once in possession of this letter, [[negoro|Negoro]] departs immediately with about twenty men, initiating the race.

[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] grasps the urgency with characteristic clarity:

> "We must get to Mossamedes before Negoro arrives; once there, we shall find that the Portuguese authorities will lend us their protection, and when old Alvez arrives to claim his 100,000 dollars — he shall receive a good thrashing for his pains." (047-162)

The party agrees that "it was most important that [[negoro|Negoro]]'s arrival at Mossamedes should be forestalled" (047-168). The plan to reach the coast by descending a river — something [[dick-sands|Dick]] had "so long contemplated" (047-168) — now converges on this single objective.

## Narrative Significance

Mossâmedes functions as the novel's ultimate MacGuffin — a destination that structures the entire final movement of the plot. It represents the thin line between rescue and catastrophe: if [[dick-sands|Dick]] and his party arrive first, they find Portuguese protection and reunion with [[james-weldon|Mr. Weldon]]; if [[negoro|Negoro]] arrives first, the trap closes. The port's dual character — a place of both potential rescue and [[slavery|slave-trade]] complicity — mirrors the moral ambiguity that Verne weaves throughout the novel's treatment of colonial [[africa|Africa]].

In a broader sense, Mossâmedes embodies the paradox of [[angola|Portuguese Angola]] itself: nominally a seat of European civilization and the rule of law, yet in practice a node in the networks of exploitation that the novel condemns. The "Portuguese authorities" at Mossâmedes who would "lend us their protection" (047-162) are the same authorities whose powerlessness against the [[slavery|slave trade]] Verne has documented throughout the narrative.

## Historical Context

Mossâmedes was founded by the Portuguese in 1840 and named after the district of Mossâmedes in Portugal. By the 1870s, when Verne's novel is set, it was a small but growing port, its remoteness making it attractive to those seeking to avoid the scrutiny of British anti-slavery patrols that concentrated on the more established ports to the north. Today the city is known as Namibe and remains an important port in southern [[angola|Angola]].
