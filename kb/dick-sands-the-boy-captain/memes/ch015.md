[015-001]No doubt there was much to cause concern in the general aspect of the

[015-002]weather; but there was one point on which they never failed to

[015-003]congratulate each other;--that the direction of the wind had never

[015-004]changed, and consequently must be carrying them in the desired course.

[015-005]Unless a storm should overtake them, they could continue their present

[015-006]navigation without peril, and with every prospect of finding a port

[015-007]upon the shore where they might put in. Such were their mutual and

[015-008]acknowledged hopes; but [[dick-sands|Dick]] secretly felt the misgiving lest, without

[015-009]a pilot, he might in his ignorance fail to find a harbour of refuge.

[015-010]Nevertheless, he would not suffer himself to meet trouble half-way, and

[015-011]kept up his spirits under the conviction that if difficulties came he

[015-012]should be strengthened to grapple with them or make his escape.

[015-013]Time passed on, and the 9th of March arrived without material change in

[015-014]the condition of the atmosphere. The sky remained heavily burdened, and

[015-015]the wind, which occasionally had abated for a few hours, had always

[015-016]returned with at least its former violence. The occasional rising of

[015-017]the mercury never encouraged Dick to anticipate a permanent improvement

[015-018]in the weather, and he discerned only too plainly that brighter times

[015-019]at present were not to be looked for.

[015-020]A startling alarm had more than once been caused by the sudden breaking

[015-021]of storms in which thunderbolts had seemed to fall within a few cables'

[015-022]lengths of the schooner. On these occasions the torrents of rain had

[015-023]been so heavy that the ship had appeared to be in the very midst of a

[015-024]whirlpool of vapour, and it was impossible to see a yard ahead.

[015-025]The "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" pitched and rolled frightfully. Fortunately [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]

[015-026]could bear the motion without much personal inconvenience, and

[015-027]consequently was able to devote her attention to her little boy, who

[015-028]was a miserable sufferer. Cousin Benedict was as undisturbed as the

[015-029]cockroaches he was investigating; he hardly noticed the increasing

[015-030]madness of either wind or wave, but went on with his studies as calmly

[015-031]as if he were in his own comfortable museum at [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]. Moreover,

[015-032]it was fortunate that the negroes did not suffer to any great degree

[015-033]from sea-sickness, and consequently were able to assist their captain

[015-034]in his arduous task, Dick was far too experienced a sailor himself to

[015-035]be inconvenienced by any oscillations of the vessel, however violent.

[015-036]The "Pilgrim" still made good headway, and Dick, although he was aware

[015-037]that ultimately it would probably be necessary again to shorten sail,

[015-038]was anxious to postpone making any alteration before he was absolutely

[015-039]obliged. Surely, he reasoned with himself, the land could not now be

[015-040]far away; he had calculated his speed; he had kept a diligent reckoning

[015-041]on the chart; surely, the shore must be almost in sight. He would not

[015-042]trust his crew to keep watch; he was aware how easily their

[015-043]inexperienced eyes would be misled, and how they might mistake a

[015-044]distant cloud-bank for the land they coveted to see; he kept watch for

[015-045]himself; his own gaze was ever fixed upon the horizon; and in the

[015-046]eagerness of his expectation he would repeatedly mount to the

[015-047]cross-trees to get a wider range of vision.

[015-048]But land was not to be seen.

[015-049]Next day as Dick was standing at the bow, alternately considering the

[015-050]canvas which his ship carried and the aspect presented by the sky, Mrs.

[015-051]Weldon approached him without his noticing her. She caught some

[015-052]muttered expressions of bewilderment that fell from his lips, and asked

[015-053]him whether he could see anything.

[015-054]He lowered the telescope which he had been holding in his hand, and

[015-055]answered,--

[015-056]"No, Mrs. Weldon, I cannot see anything; and it is this Hiat perplexes

[015-057]me so sorely. I cannot understand why we have not already come in sight

[015-058]of land. It is nearly a month since we lost our poor dear captain.

[015-059]There has been no delay in our progress; no stoppage in our rate of

[015-060]speed. I cannot make it out."

[015-061]"How far were we from land when we lost the captain?"

[015-062]"I am sure I am not far out in saying that we were scarcely more than

[015-063]4500 miles from the shores of America."

[015-064]"And at what rate have we been sailing?"

[015-065]"Not much less than nine score knots a day."

[015-066]"How long, then, do you reckon, Dick, we ought to be in arriving at the

[015-067]coast?"

[015-068]"Under six-and-twenty days," replied Dick.

[015-069]He paused before he spoke again, then added,--

[015-070]"But what mystifies me even more than our failing to sight the land is

[015-071]this: we have not come across a single vessel; and yet vessels without

[015-072]number are always traversing these seas."

[015-073]"But do you not think," inquired Mrs. Weldon, "that you have made some

[015-074]error in your reckoning? Is your speed really what you have supposed?"

[015-075]"Impossible, madam," replied Dick, with an air of dignity, "impossible

[015-076]that I should have fallen into error. The [[ships-log|log]] has been consulted,

[015-077]without fail, every half-hour. I am about to have it lowered now, and I

[015-078]will undertake to show you that we are at this present moment making

[015-079]ten miles an hour, which would give considerably over 200 miles a day."

[015-080]He then called out to Tom,--

[015-081]"Tom, lower the log!"

[015-082]The old man was quite accustomed to the duty. The log was fastened to

[015-083]the line and thrown overboard. It ran out regularly for about

[015-084]five-and-twenty fathoms, when all at once the line slackened in Tom's

[015-085]hand.

[015-086]"It is broken!" cried Tom; "the cord is broken!"

[015-087]"Broken?" exclaimed Dick: "good heavens! we have lost the log!"

[015-088]It was too true. The log was gone.

[015-089]Tom drew in the rope. Dick took it up and examined it. It had not

[015-090]broken at its point of union with the log; it had given way in the

[015-091]middle, at a place where the strands in some unaccountable way had worn

[015-092]strangely thin.

[015-093]Dick's agony of mind, in spite of his effort to be calm, was intensely

[015-094]great. A suspicion of foul play involuntarily occurred to him. He knew

[015-095]that the rope had been of first-rate make; he knew that it had been

[015-096]quite sound when used before; but he could prove nothing; he could only

[015-097]mourn over the loss which committed him to the sole remaining compass

[015-098]as his only guide.

[015-099]That compass, too, although he knew it not, was misleading him entirely!

[015-100]Mrs. Weldon sighed as she witnessed the grief which the loss manifestly

[015-101]caused poor Dick, but in purest sympathy she said nothing, and retired

[015-102]thoughtfully to her cabin.

[015-103]It was no longer possible to reckon the rate of progress, but there was

[015-104]no doubt that the "Pilgrim" continued to maintain at least her previous

[015-105]speed.

[015-106]Before another four-and-twenty hours had passed the barometer had

[015-107]fallen still lower, and the wind was threatening to rise to a velocity

[015-108]of sixty miles. Resolved to be on the safe side, Dick determined not

[015-109]only to strike the top-gallant and the main-top-mast, but to take in

[015-110]all the lower sails. Indeed, he began to be aware that no time was to

[015-111]be lost. The operation would not be done in a moment, and the storm was

[015-112]approaching. Dick made Tom take the helm; he ascended the shrouds with

[015-113]Bat, Austin, and Actæon, making Hercules stay on deck to slacken the

[015-114]halyards as required.

[015-115]By dint of arduous exertion, and at no little risk of being thrown

[015-116]overboard by the rolling of the ship, they succeeded in lowering the

[015-117]two masts; the fore-top-sail was then reefed, and the fore-sail

[015-118]entirely struck, so that the only canvas that the schooner carried was

[015-119]the reefed fore-top and the one stay. These, however, made her run with

[015-120]a terrific speed.

[015-121]Early on the morning of the 12th, Dick noted with alarm that the

[015-122]barometer had not ceased to fall, and now registered only 27.9°. The

[015-123][[storm-at-sea|tempest]] had continued to increase, till it was unsafe for the ship to

[015-124]carry any canvas at all. The order was given for the top-sail to be

[015-125]taken in, but it was too late; a violent gust carried the sail

[015-126]completely away, and Austin, who had made his way to the fore-top-yard,

[015-127]was struck by the flying sheet; and although he was not seriously hurt,

[015-128]he was obliged at once to return to deck.

[015-129]Dick Sands became more uneasy than ever; he was tortured by

[015-130]apprehensions of reefs outlying the shore, to which he imagined he must

[015-131]now be close; but he could discern no rocks to justify his fears, and

[015-132]returned to take his place at the helm.

[015-133]The next moment [[negoro|Negoro]] appeared on deck; he pointed mysteriously to the

[015-134]far-off horizon, as though he discerned some object, as a mountain,

[015-135]there; and looking round with a malevolent smile, immediately left the

[015-136]deck, and went back to his cabin.

[015-138]CHAPTER XII.

[015-139]HOPE REVIVED.

[015-140]The wind had now increased to a hurricane; it had veered to the

[015-141]south-west, and had attained a velocity little short of ninety miles an

[015-142]hour. On land, the most substantial of erections could with difficulty

[015-143]have withstood its violence, and a vessel anchored in a roadstead must

[015-144]have been torn from its moorings and cast ashore. The memorable storm

[015-145]that had devastated the Island of Guadaloupe on the 25th of July, 1825,

[015-146]when heavy cannon were lifted from their carriages, could scarcely have

[015-147]been more furious, and it was only her mobility before the blast and

[015-148]the solidity of her structure that gave the "Pilgrim" a hope of

[015-149]surviving the tempest.

[015-150]A few minutes after the topsail had been lost, the small jib was

[015-151]carried away. Dick Sands contemplated the possibility of throwing out a

[015-152]storm-jib, made of extra strong canvas, as a means of bringing the ship

[015-153]a little more under his control, but abandoned the idea as useless. It

[015-154]was, therefore, under bare poles that the "Pilgrim" was driven along;

[015-155]but in spite of the lack of canvas, the hull, masts, and rigging, gave

[015-156]sufficient purchase to the wind, and the progress of the schooner was

[015-157]prodigiously rapid; sometimes, indeed, she seemed to be literally

[015-158]lifted from the water, and scudded on, scarcely skimming its surface.

[015-159]The rolling was fearful. Enormous waves followed in quick succession,

[015-160]and as they travelled faster than the ship, there was the perpetual

[015-161]risk of one of them catching her astern. Without sail, there were no

[015-162]means of escaping that peril by increase of speed; the adroit

[015-163]management of the helm was the only chance of avoiding the hazardous

[015-164]shocks, and even this repeatedly failed.

[015-165]To prevent his being washed overboard Dick lashed himself to his place

[015-166]at the wheel by a rope round his waist, and made Tom and Bat keep close

[015-167]at hand, ready to give him assistance, in case of emergency. Hercules

[015-168]and Actæon, clinging to the bitt, kept watch at the bow. Mrs. Weldon

[015-169]and her party, at Dick's special request, remained inside the stern

[015-170]cabin, although the lady, for her own part, would much rather have

[015-171]stayed on deck; she had, however, yielded to the representation that

[015-172]she would thus be exposing herself to unnecessary danger.

[015-173]The hatchways were hermetically closed, and it was to be hoped that

[015-174]they would withstand the heavy sea that was dashing over them; only let

[015-175]one of them give way to the pressure, and the vessel must inevitably

[015-176]fill and founder. It was a matter of congratulation that the stowage

[015-177]had been done very carefully, so that notwithstanding all the lurchings

[015-178]of the ship, the cargo did not shift in the least.

[015-179]The heroic young commander had still further curtailed his periods of

[015-180]rest, and it was only at the urgent entreaty of Mrs. Weldon, who feared

[015-181]that he would exhaust himself by his vigilance, that he was induced to

[015-182]lie down for a few hours' sleep on the night of the 13th.

[015-183]After Tom and Bat had been left alone at the wheel they were, somewhat

[015-184]to their surprise, joined by Negoro, who very rarely came aft. He

[015-185]seemed inclined to enter into conversation, but found little

[015-186]encouragement to talk on the part either of Tom or his son. All at once

[015-187]a violent roll of the ship threw him off his feet, and he would have

[015-188]gone overboard if he had not been saved by falling against the binnacle.
