[021-001][[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] had hardly begun to express her thanks for the proposal

[021-002]when he said abruptly,--

[021-003]"Are these negroes your slaves?"

[021-004]"Slaves! sir," replied Mrs. Weldon, drawing herself up proudly; "we

[021-005]have no slaves in the United States. The south has now long followed

[021-006]the example of the north. [[slavery|Slavery]] is abolished."

[021-007]"I beg your pardon, madam. I had forgotten that the war of 1862 had

[021-008]solved that question. But seeing these fellows with you, I thought

[021-009]perhaps they might be in your service," he added, with a slight tone of

[021-010]irony.

[021-011]"We are very proud to be of any service to Mrs. Weldon," Tom interposed

[021-012]with dignity, "but we are no man's property. It is true I was sold for

[021-013]a slave when I was six years old; but I have long since had my freedom;

[021-014]and so has my son. Bat here, and all his friends, were born of free

[021-015]parents."

[021-016]"Ah! well then, I have to congratulate you," replied Harris, in a

[021-017]manner that jarred very sensibly upon Mrs. Weldon's feelings; but she

[021-018]said nothing.

[021-019]Harris added,--

[021-020]"I can assure you that you are as safe here in Bolivia as you would be

[021-021]in New England."

[021-022]He had not finished speaking, when Jack, followed by Nan, came out of

[021-023]the grotto. The child was rubbing his eyes, having only just awakened

[021-024]from his night's sleep. Catching sight of his mother, he darted towards

[021-025]her.

[021-026]"What a charming little boy!" exclaimed Harris.

[021-027]"He is my little son," said Mrs. Weldon, kissing the child by way of

[021-028]morning greeting.

[021-029]"Ah, madam, I am sure you must have suffered doubly on his account.

[021-030]Will the little man let me kiss him too?"

[021-031]But there was something in the stranger's appearance that did not take

[021-032]Jack's fancy, and he shrank back timidly to his mother's side.

[021-033]"You must excuse him, sir; he is very shy."

[021-034]"Never mind," said Harris; "we shall be better acquainted by-and-by.

[021-035]When we get to my brother's, he shall have a nice little pony to ride."

[021-036]But not even this tempting offer seemed to have any effect in coaxing

[021-037]Jack into a more genial mood. He kept fast hold of his mother's hand,

[021-038]and she, somewhat vexed at his behaviour, and anxious that no offence

[021-039]should be given to a man who appeared so friendly in his intentions,

[021-040]hastened to turn the conversation to another topic.

[021-041]Meantime [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] had been considering Harris's proposal. Upon the

[021-042]whole, the plan of making their way to the hacienda of San Felice

[021-043]seemed to commend itself to his judgment; but he could not conceal from

[021-044]himself that a journey of 200 miles across plains and forests, without

[021-045]any means of transport, would be extremely fatiguing. On expressing his

[021-046]doubts on this point, he was met with the reply,--

[021-047]"Oh, that can be managed well enough, young man; just round the corner

[021-048]of the cliff there I have a horse, which is quite at the disposal of

[021-049]the lady and her son; and by easy stages of ten miles or so a day, it

[021-050]will do the rest of us no harm to travel on foot. Besides," he added,

[021-051]"when I spoke of the journey being 200 miles, I was thinking of

[021-052]following, as I usually do, the course of the river; but by taking a

[021-053]short cut across the forest, we may reduce the distance by nearly

[021-054]eighty miles."

[021-055]Mrs. Weldon was about to say how grateful she was, but Harris

[021-056]anticipated her.

[021-057]"Not a word, madam, I beg you. You cannot thank me better than by

[021-058]accepting my offer. I confess I have never crossed this forest, but I

[021-059]am so much accustomed to the pampas that I have little fear of losing

[021-060]my way. The only difficulty is in the matter of provisions, as I have

[021-061]only supplied myself with enough to carry me on to San Felice."

[021-062]"As to provisions," replied Mrs. Weldon, "we have enough and to spare;

[021-063]and we shall be more than willing to share everything with you."

[021-064]"That is well," answered Harris; "then there can be no reason why we

[021-065]should not start at once."

[021-066]He was turning away with the intention of fetching his horse, when Dick

[021-067]Sands detained him. True to his seaman's instincts, the young sailor

[021-068]felt that he should be much more at his ease on the sea-shore than

[021-069]traversing the heart of an unknown forest.

[021-070]"Pardon me, Mr. Harris," he began, "but instead of taking so long a

[021-071]journey across the desert of Atacama, would it not be far better for us

[021-072]to follow the coast either northwards or southwards, until we reach the

[021-073]nearest seaport?"

[021-074]A frown passed over Harris's countenance.

[021-075]"I know very little about the coast," he answered; "but I know enough

[021-076]to assure you that there is no town to the north within 300 or 400

[021-077]miles."

[021-078]"Then why should we not go south?" persisted Dick.

[021-079]"You would then have to travel to Chili, which is almost as far; and,

[021-080]under your circumstances, I should not advise you to skirt the pampas

[021-081]of the Argentine Republic. For my own part, I could not accompany you."

[021-082]"But do not the vessels which ply between Chili and Peru come within

[021-083]sight of this coast?" interposed Mrs. Weldon.

[021-084]"No, madam; they keep out so far to sea that there would not be the

[021-085]faintest chance of your hailing one."

[021-086]"You seem to have another question to ask Mr. Harris," Mrs. Weldon

[021-087]continued, addressing Dick, who still looked rather doubtful.

[021-088]Dick replied that he was about to inquire at what port he would be

[021-089]likely to find a ship to convey their party to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]].

[021-090]"That I really cannot tell you, my young friend," rejoined Harris; "I

[021-091]can only repeat my promise that we will furnish you with the means of

[021-092]conveyance from San Felice to Atacama, where no doubt you will obtain

[021-093]all the information you require."

[021-094]"I hope you will not think that Dick is insensible to your kindness,

[021-095]Mr. Harris," said Mrs. Weldon, apologetically.

[021-096]"On the contrary," promptly observed Dick; "I fully appreciate it; I

[021-097]only wish we had been cast ashore upon a spot where we should have had

[021-098]no need to intrude upon his generosity."

[021-099]"I assure you, madam, it gives me unbounded pleasure to serve you in

[021-100]any way," said Harris; "it is, as I have told you, not often that I

[021-101]come in contact with any of my own countrymen."

[021-102]"Then we accept your offer as frankly as it is made," replied the lady,

[021-103]adding; "but I cannot consent to deprive you of your horse. I am a very

[021-104]good walker."

[021-105]"So am I," said Harris, with a bow, "and consequently I intend you and

[021-106]your little son to ride. I am used to long tramps through the pampas.

[021-107]Besides, it is not at all unlikely that we shall come across some of

[021-108]the workpeople belonging to the hacienda; if so, they will be able to

[021-109]give us a mount."

[021-110]Convinced that it would only be thwarting Mrs. Weldon's wishes to throw

[021-111]any further impediment in the way, Dick Sands suppressed his desire to

[021-112]raise fresh obstacles, and simply asked how soon they ought to start.

[021-113]"This very day, at once," said Harris quickly.

[021-114]"So soon?" asked Dick.

[021-115]"Yes. The rainy season begins in April, and the sooner we are at San

[021-116]Felice the better. The way through the forest is the safest as well as

[021-117]the shortest, for we shall be less likely to meet any of the nomad

[021-118]Indians, who are notorious robbers."

[021-119]Without making any direct reply, Dick proceeded to instruct the negroes

[021-120]to choose such of the provisions as were most easy of transport, and to

[021-121]make them up into packages, that every one might carry a due share.

[021-122]Hercules with his usual good nature professed himself willing to carry

[021-123]the entire load; a proposal, however, to which Dick would not listen

[021-124]for a moment.

[021-125]"You are a fine fellow, Hercules" said Harris, scrutinizing the giant

[021-126]with the eye of a connoisseur; "you would be worth something in the

[021-127]African market."

[021-128]"Those who want me now must catch me first," retorted Hercules, with a

[021-129]grin.

[021-130]The services of all hands were enlisted, and in a comparatively short

[021-131]time sufficient food was packed up to supply the party for about ten

[021-132]days' march.

[021-133]"You must allow us to show you what hospitality is in our power," said

[021-134]Mrs. Weldon, addressing her new acquaintance; "our breakfast will be

[021-135]ready in a quarter of an hour, and we shall be happy if you will join

[021-136]us."

[021-137]"It will give me much pleasure," answered Harris, gaily; "I will employ

[021-138]the interval in fetching my horse, who has breakfasted already."

[021-139]"I will accompany you," said Dick.

[021-140]"By all means, my young friend; come with me, and I will show you the

[021-141]lower part of the river."

[021-142]While they were gone, Hercules was sent in search of Cousin Benedict,

[021-143]who was wandering on the top of the cliff in quest of some wonderful

[021-144]insect, which, of course, was not to be found. Without asking his

[021-145]permission, Hercules unceremoniously brought him back to Mrs. Weldon,

[021-146]who explained how they were about to start upon a ten days' march into

[021-147]the interior of the country. The entomologist was quite satisfied with

[021-148]the arrangement, and declared himself ready for a march across the

[021-149]entire continent, as long as he was free to be adding to his collection

[021-150]on the way.

[021-151]Thus assured of her cousin's acquiescence in her plans; Mrs. Weldon

[021-152]proceeded to prepare such a substantial meal as she hoped would

[021-153]invigorate them all for the approaching journey.

[021-154]Harris and Dick Sands, meantime, had turned the corner of the cliff,

[021-155]and walked about 300 paces along the shore until they came to a tree to

[021-156]which a horse was tethered. The creature neighed as it recognized its

[021-157]master. It was a strong-built animal, of a kind that Dick had not seen

[021-158]before, although its long neck and crupper, short loins, flat shoulders

[021-159]and arched forehead indicated that it was of Arabian breed.

[021-160][Illustration: They came to a tree to which a horse was tethered.]

[021-161]"Plenty of strength here," Harris said, as after unfastening the horse,

[021-162]he took it by the bridle and began to lead it along the shore.

[021-163]Dick made no reply; he was casting a hasty glance at the forest which

[021-164]enclosed them on either hand; it was an unattractive sight, but he

[021-165]observed nothing to give him any particular ground for uneasiness.

[021-166]Turning round, he said abruptly,--

[021-167]"Did you meet a Portuguese last night, named [[negoro|Negoro]]?"

[021-168]"Negoro? who is Negoro?" asked Harris, in a tone of surprise.

[021-169]"He was our ship's cook; but he has disappeared."

[021-170]"Drowned, probably," said Harris indifferently.

[021-171]"No, he was not drowned; he was with us during the evening, but left

[021-172]afterwards; I thought perhaps you might have met him along the

[021-173]river-side, as you came that way."

[021-174]"No," said Harris, "I saw no one; if your cook ventured alone into the

[021-175]forest, most likely he has lost his way; it is possible we may pick him

[021-176]up upon our road."

[021-177]When they arrived at the grotto, they found breakfast duly prepared.

[021-178]Like the supper of the previous evening it consisted mainly of corned

[021-179]beef and biscuit. Harris did ample justice to the repast.

[021-180]"There is no fear of our starving as we go," he observed to Mrs.

[021-181]Weldon; "but I can hardly say so much for the unfortunate Portuguese,

[021-182]your cook, of whom my young friend here has been speaking."

[021-183]"Ah! has Dick been telling you about Negoro?" Mrs. Weldon said.

[021-184]Dick explained that he had been inquiring whether Mr. Harris had

[021-185]happened to meet him in the direction he had come.

[021-186]"I saw nothing of him," Harris repeated; "and as he has deserted you,

[021-187]you need not give yourselves any concern about him." And apparently

[021-188]glad to turn the subject, he said, "Now, madam, I am at your service;

[021-189]shall we start at once?"
