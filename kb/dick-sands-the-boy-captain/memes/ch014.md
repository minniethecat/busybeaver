[014-001]On the night of the 13th, he was so utterly worn-out that he was

[014-002]obliged to ask Tom to relieve him at the helm whilst he went down for a

[014-003]few hours' rest. Actæon and Hercules remained on watch on the

[014-004]forecastle.

[014-005]The night was very dark; the sky was covered with heavy clouds that had

[014-006]formed in the chill evening air, and the sails on the top-masts were

[014-007]lost in the obscurity. At the stern, the lamps on either side of the

[014-008]binnacle cast a faint reflection on the metal mountings of the wheel,

[014-009]leaving the deck generally in complete darkness.

[014-010]Towards three o'clock in the morning Tom was getting so heavy with

[014-011]sleepiness that he was almost unconscious. His eye, long fixed steadily

[014-012]on the compass, lost its power of vision, and he fell into a doze from

[014-013]which it would require more than a slight disturbance to arouse him.

[014-014]Meantime a light shadow glided stealthily along the deck. Creeping

[014-015]gradually up to the binnacle, [[negoro|Negoro]] put down something heavy that he

[014-016]had brought in his hand. He stole a keen and rapid glance at the dial

[014-017]of the compass, and made his way back, unseen and unheard as he had

[014-018]come.

[014-019]Almost immediately afterwards, Tom awakened from his slumber. His eye

[014-020]fell instinctively on the compass, and he saw in a moment that the ship

[014-021]was out of her proper course. By a turn of the helm he brought her head

[014-022]to what he supposed to be the east. But he was mistaken. During his

[014-023]brief interval of unconsciousness a piece of iron had been deposited

[014-024]beneath the magnetic needle, which by this means had been diverted

[014-025]thirty degrees to the right, and, instead of pointing due north,

[014-026]inclined far towards north-east.

[014-027]Consequently it came to pass that the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]," supposed by her young

[014-028]commander to be making good headway due east, was in reality, under the

[014-029]brisk north-west breeze, speeding along towards the south-east.

[014-030]CHAPTER XI.

[014-031]ROUGH WEATHER.

[014-032]During the ensuing week nothing particular occurred on board. The

[014-033]breeze still freshened, and the "Pilgrim" made on the average 160 miles

[014-034]every twenty-four hours. The speed was as great as could be expected

[014-035]from a craft of her size.

[014-036][[dick-sands|Dick]] grew more and more sanguine in his anticipations that it could not

[014-037]be long before the schooner would cross the track of the mail-packets

[014-038]plying between the eastern and western hemispheres. He had made up his

[014-039]mind to hail the first passing vessel, and either to transfer his

[014-040]passengers, or what perhaps would be better still, to borrow a few

[014-041]sailors, and, it might be, an officer to work the "Pilgrim" to shore.

[014-042]He could not help, however, a growing sense of astonishment, when day

[014-043]after day passed, and yet there was no ship to be signalled. He kept

[014-044]the most vigourous look-out, but all to no purpose. Three voyages

[014-045]before had he made to the [[right-whale|whale]]-fisheries, and his experience made him

[014-046]sure that he ought now to be sighting some English or American vessel

[014-047]on its way between the Equator and Cape Horn.

[014-048]Very different, however, was the true position of the "Pilgrim" from

[014-049]what Dick supposed; not only had the ship been carried far out of her

[014-050]direct course by currents, the force of which there were no means of

[014-051]estimating, but from the moment when the compass had been tampered with

[014-052]by Negoro, the steering itself had put the vessel all astray.

[014-053]Unconscious of both these elements of disturbance, Dick Sands was

[014-054]convinced that they were proceeding steadily eastwards, and was

[014-055]perpetually encouraging [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] and himself by the assurance that

[014-056]they must very soon arrive within view of the American coast; again and

[014-057]again asserting that his sole concern was for his passengers, and that

[014-058]for his own safety he had no anxiety.

[014-059]"But think, Dick," said the lady, "what a position you would have been

[014-060]in, if you had not had your passengers. You would have been alone with

[014-061]that terrible Negoro; you would have been rather alarmed then."

[014-062]"I should have taken good care to put it out of Negoro's power to do me

[014-063]any mischief, and then I should have worked the ship by myself,"

[014-064]answered the lad stoutly.

[014-065]His very pluck gave Mrs. Weldon renewed confidence. She was a woman

[014-066]with wonderful powers of endurance, and it was only when she thought of

[014-067]her little son that she had any feeling of despair; yet even this she

[014-068]endeavoured to conceal, and Dick's undaunted courage helped her.

[014-069]Although the youth of the apprentice did not allow him to pretend to

[014-070]any advanced scientific knowledge, he had the proverbial "weather-eye"

[014-071]of the sailor. He was not only very keen in noticing any change in the

[014-072]aspect of the sky, but he had learnt from [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]], who was a

[014-073]clever meteorologist, to draw correct conclusions from the indications

[014-074]of the barometer; the captain, indeed, having taken the trouble to make

[014-075]him learn by heart the general rules which are laid down in

[014-076]Vorepierre's _Dictionnaire Illustré_.

[014-077]There are seven of these rules:--

[014-078]1. If after a long period of fine weather the barometer falls suddenly

[014-079]and continuously, although the mercury may be descending for two or

[014-080]three days before there is an apparent change in the atmosphere, there

[014-081]will ultimately be rain; and the longer has been the time between the

[014-082]first depression and the commencement of the rain, the longer the rain

[014-083]may be expected to last.

[014-084]2. _Vice versâ_, if after a long period of wet weather the barometer

[014-085]begins to rise slowly and steadily, fine weather will ensue; and the

[014-086]longer the time between the first rising of the mercury and the

[014-087]commencement of the fine weather, the longer the fine weather may be

[014-088]expected to last.

[014-089]3. If immediately after the fall or rise of the mercury a change of

[014-090]weather ensues, the change will be of no long continuance.[1]

[014-091]4. A gradual rise for two or three days during rain forecasts fine

[014-092]weather; but if there be a fall immediately on the arrival of the fine

[014-093]weather, it will not be for long. This rule holds also conversely.

[014-094]5. In spring and autumn a sudden fall indicates rain; in the summer, if

[014-095]very hot, it foretells a storm. In the winter, after a period of steady

[014-096]frost, a fall prognosticates a change of wind with rain and hail;

[014-097]whilst a rise announces the approach of snow.

[014-098]6. Rapid oscillations of the mercury either way are not to be

[014-099]interpreted as indicating either wet or dry weather of any duration;

[014-100]continuance of either fair or foul weather is forecast only by a

[014-101]prolonged and steady rise or fall beforehand.

[014-102]7. At the end of autumn, after a period of wind and rain, a rise may be

[014-103]expected to be followed by north wind and frost.

[014-104]Not merely had Dick got these rules by rote, but he had tested them by

[014-105]his own observations, and had become singularly trustworthy in his

[014-106]forecasts of the weather. He made a point of consulting the barometer

[014-107]several times every day, and although to all appearances the sky

[014-108]indicated that the fine weather was settled, it did not escape his

[014-109]observation that on the 20th the mercury showed a tendency to fall.

[014-110]Dick knew that rain, if it came, would be accompanied by wind; an

[014-111]opinion in which he was very soon confirmed by the breeze freshening,

[014-112]till the air was displaced at the rate of nearly sixty feet a second,

[014-113]or more[1] than forty miles an hour; and he recognized the necessity of

[014-114]at once shortening sail. He had already used the precaution to take in

[014-115]the royal, the main-top-sail, and the flying jib, but he now at once

[014-116]resolved likewise to take in the top-gallant, and to have a couple of

[014-117]reefs in the foretop-sail.

[014-118][Footnote 1: This and several of the other rules are concisely

[014-119]concentrated in the couplet--

[014-120]Long foretold, long last;

[014-121]Short notice, soon past.]

[014-122]To an inexperienced crew, the last operation was far from easy; but

[014-123]there was no symptom of shrinking from it. Followed by Bat and Austin,

[014-124]Dick mounted the rigging of the foremast, and with little trouble got

[014-125]to the top-gallant. Had the weather been less unpromising he would have

[014-126]been inclined to leave the two yards as they were, but anticipating the

[014-127]ultimate necessity of being obliged to lower the mast, he unrigged

[014-128]them, and let them down to the deck; he knew well enough that in the

[014-129]event of the [[storm-at-sea|gale]] rising as he expected, the lowering of the mast as

[014-130]well as the shortening of sail would contribute to diminish the strain

[014-131]and stress upon the vessel.

[014-132]It was the work of two hours to get this preliminary operation over.

[014-133]There still remained the task of taking in the reefs in the top-sail.

[014-134]The "Pilgrim" in one respect differed from most modern vessels. She did

[014-135]not carry a double foretop, which would very much have diminished the

[014-136]difficulty attending the reefing. It was consequently necessary to

[014-137]proceed as before; to mount the rigging, by main force to haul in the

[014-138]flapping canvas, and to make the fastening secure. But critical and

[014-139]dangerous as the task was, it was successfully accomplished, and the

[014-140]three young men, having descended safely to the deck, had the

[014-141]satisfaction of seeing the schooner run easily before the wind, which

[014-142]had further increased till it was blowing a stiff gale.

[014-143]For three days the gale continued brisk and hard, yet without any

[014-144]variation in its direction. But all along the barometer was falling;

[014-145]the mercury sank to 28° without symptom of recovery. The sky was

[014-146]becoming overcast; clouds, thick and lowering, obscured the sun, and it

[014-147]was difficult to make out where it rose or where it set. Dick did his

[014-148]best to keep up his courage, but he could not disguise from himself

[014-149]that there was cause for uneasiness. He took no more rest than was

[014-150]absolutely necessary, and what repose he allowed himself he always took

[014-151]on deck; he maintained a calm exterior, but he was really tortured with

[014-152]anxiety.

[014-153][Illustration: For half an hour Negoro stood motionless.]

[014-154]Although the violence of the wind seemed to lull awhile, Dick did not

[014-155]suffer himself to be betrayed into any false security; he knew only too

[014-156]well what to expect, and after a brief interval of comparative quiet,

[014-157]the gale returned and the waves began to run very high.

[014-158][[About]] four o'clock one afternoon, Negoro (a most unusual thing for him)

[014-159]emerged from his kitchen, and skulked to the fore. [[dingo|Dingo]] was fast

[014-160]asleep, and did not make his ordinary growl by way of greeting to his

[014-161]enemy. For half an hour Negoro stood motionless, apparently surveying

[014-162]the horizon. The heavy waves rolled past; they were higher than the

[014-163]condition of the wind warranted; their magnitude witnessed to a storm

[014-164]passing in the west, and there was every reason to suspect that the

[014-165]"Pilgrim" might be caught by its violence.

[014-166]Negoro looked long at the water; he then raised his eyes and scanned

[014-167]the sky. Above and below he might have read threatening signs. The

[014-168]upper stratum of cloud was travelling far more rapidly than that

[014-169]beneath, an indication that ere long the masses of vapour would

[014-170]descend, and, coming in contact with the inferior current, would change

[014-171]the gale into a tempest, which probably would increase to a hurricane.

[014-172]It might be from ignorance or it might be from indifference, but there

[014-173]was no indication of alarm on the face of Negoro; on the other hand

[014-174]there might be seen a sort of smile curling on his lip. After thus

[014-175]gazing above him and around him, he clambered on to the bowsprit, and

[014-176]made his way by degrees to the very gammonings; again he rested and

[014-177]looked about him as if to explore the horizon; after a while he

[014-178]clambered back on deck, and soon stealthily retreated to his own

[014-179]quarters.
