[011-001]Meantime, just as had been expected, the [[right-whale|whale]] had risen to the surface

[011-002]of the water, the harpoon still fixed firmly in her side. She remained

[011-003]motionless, apparently waiting for her calf, which she had far

[011-004]out-distanced in her mad career. [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] ordered his men to pull

[011-005]towards her as rapidly as they could, and on getting close up, two of

[011-006]the sailors, following the captain's example, shipped their oars and

[011-007]took up the long lances with which the whale was now to be attacked.

[011-008]Howick held himself in readiness to sheer off quickly in the event of

[011-009]the finback making a turn towards the boat.

[011-010]"Now, my lads!" shouted the captain. "Look out! take a good aim! no

[011-011]false shots! Are you ready, Howick?"

[011-012]"Quite ready, captain," answered the boatswain, adding, "but it

[011-013]perplexes me altogether to see the brute so quiet all of a sudden."

[011-014]"It looks suspicious," said the captain; "but never mind; go on!

[011-015]straight ahead!"

[011-016]Captain Hull was becoming more excited every moment.

[011-017]During the time the boat was approaching, the whale had only turned

[011-018]round a little in the water without changing its position. It was

[011-019]evidently still looking for its calf, which was not to be seen by its

[011-020]side. All of a sudden it gave a jerk with its tail which carried it

[011-021]some few yards away.

[011-022]The men were all excited. Was the beast going to escape again? Was the

[011-023]fatiguing pursuit all to come over a second time? Must not the chase be

[011-024]abandoned? Would not the prize have to be given up?

[011-025]But no: the whale was not starting on another flight; it had merely

[011-026]turned so as to face the boat, and now rapidly beating the water with

[011-027]its enormous fins, it commenced a frantic dash forwards.

[011-028]"Look out, Howick, she's coming!" shouted Captain Hull.

[011-029]The skilful boatswain was all on the alert; the boat swerved, as if by

[011-030]instinct, so as to avoid the blow, and as the whale passed furiously

[011-031]by, she received three tremendous thrusts from the lances of the

[011-032]captain and the two men, who all endeavoured to strike at some vital

[011-033]part. There was a sudden pause. The whale spouted up two gigantic

[011-034]columns of blood and water, lashed its tail, and, with bounds and

[011-035]plunges that were terrible to behold, renewed its angry attack upon the

[011-036]boat.

[011-037]None but the most determined of whalemen could fail to lose their head

[011-038]under such an assault. Calm and collected, however, the crew remained.

[011-039]Once again did Howick adroitly sheer aside, and once again did the

[011-040]three lances do their deadly work upon the huge carcase as it rolled

[011-041]impetuously past; but this time, so great was the wave that was caused

[011-042]by the infuriated animal, that the boat was well-nigh full of water,

[011-043]and in imminent danger of being capsized.

[011-044]"Bale away, men!" cried the captain.

[011-045]Putting down their oars, the other sailors set to work baling with all

[011-046]their might. Captain Hull cut the harpoon-line, now no longer required,

[011-047]because the whale, maddened with pain and grief for the loss of its

[011-048]offspring, would certainly make no further attempt to escape, but would

[011-049]fight desperately to the very end.

[011-050]The finback was obviously bent on a third onslaught upon the boat,

[011-051]which, being in spite of all the men's exertions still more than half

[011-052]full of water, no longer answered readily to the rudder-oar.

[011-053]No one thought of flight. The swiftest boat could be overtaken in a

[011-054]very few bounds. There was no alternative but to face the encounter. It

[011-055]was not long in coming. Their previous good fortune failed them. The

[011-056]whale in passing caught the boat with such a violent blow from its

[011-057]dorsal fin, that the men lost their footing and the lances missed their

[011-058]mark.

[011-059]"Where's Howick?" screamed the captain in alarm.

[011-060]"Here I am, captain; all right!" replied the boatswain, who had

[011-061]scrambled to his feet only to find that the oar with which he had been

[011-062]steering was snapped in half.

[011-063]"The rudder's smashed," he said.

[011-064]"Take another, Howick; quick!" cried the captain.

[011-065]But scarcely had he time to replace the broken oar, when a bubbling was

[011-066]heard a few yards away from the boat, and the young whale made its

[011-067]appearance on the surface of the sea. Catching sight of it instantly,

[011-068]the mother made a fresh dash in its direction, the maternal instincts

[011-069]were aroused, and the contest must become more deadly than ever.

[011-070]Captain Hull looked towards the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]," and waved his signal

[011-071]frantically above his head. It was, however, with no hope of succour;

[011-072]he was only too well aware that no human efforts could effectually

[011-073]hasten the arrival of the ship. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] indeed had at once obeyed

[011-074]the first summons: already the wind was filling the sails, but in

[011-075]default of steam power her progress at best could not be otherwise than

[011-076]slow. Not only did Dick feel convinced that it would be a useless waste

[011-077]of time to lower a boat and come off with the negroes to the rescue,

[011-078]but he remembered the strict orders he had received on no account to

[011-079]quit the ship. Captain Hull, however, could perceive that the

[011-080]apprentice had had the aft-boat lowered, and was towing it along, so

[011-081]that it should be in readiness for a refuge as soon as they should get

[011-082]within reach.

[011-083][Illustration: The boat was well nigh full of water, and in imminent

[011-084]danger of being capsized]

[011-085]But the whale, close at hand, demanded attention that could ill be

[011-086]spared for the yet distant ship. Covering her young one with her body,

[011-087]she was manifestly designing another charge full upon the boat.

[011-088]"On your guard, Howick! sheer off!" bellowed the captain.

[011-089]But the order was useless. The fresh oar that the boatswain had taken

[011-090]to replace the broken one was considerably shorter, and consequently it

[011-091]failed in lever-power. There was, in fact, no helm for the boat to

[011-092]answer. The sailors saw the failure, and convinced that all was lost

[011-093]uttered one long, despairing cry that might have been heard on board

[011-094]the "Pilgrim." Another moment, and from beneath there came a tremendous

[011-095]blow from the monster's tail that sent the boat flying in the air. In

[011-096]fragments it fell back again into a sea that was lashed into fury by

[011-097]the angry flapping of the finback's fins.

[011-098]Was it not possible for the unfortunate men, bleeding and wounded as

[011-099]they were, still to save themselves by clinging to some floating spar?

[011-100]Captain Hull is indeed seen endeavouring to hoist the boatswain on to a

[011-101]drifting plank. But all in vain. There is no hope. The whale, writhing

[011-102]in the convulsions of death, returns yet once again to the attack; the

[011-103]waters around the struggling sailors seethe and foam. A brief turmoil

[011-104]follows as if there were the bursting of some vast waterspout.

[011-105]In a quarter of an hour afterwards, Dick Sands, with the negroes,

[011-106]reaches the scene of the catastrophe. All is still and desolate. Every

[011-107]living object has vanished. Nothing is visible except a few fragments

[011-108]of the whale-boat floating on the blood-stained water.

[011-110]CHAPTER IX.

[011-111]DICK'S PROMOTION.

[011-112]The first feeling experienced by those on board the "Pilgrim," after

[011-113]witnessing the terrible disaster was one of grief and horror at the

[011-114]fearful death that had befallen the victims. Captain Hull and his men

[011-115]had been swept away before their very eyes, and they had been powerless

[011-116]to assist. Not one was saved; the schooner had reached the spot too

[011-117]late to offer the least resistance to the attacks of the formidable

[011-118]sea-monster.

[011-119]When Dick and the negroes returned to the ship after their hopeless

[011-120]search, with only the corroboration of their sad foreboding that

[011-121]captain and crew had disappeared for ever, [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] sank upon her

[011-122]knees; little Jack knelt beside her crying bitterly; and Dick, old Nan,

[011-123]and all the negroes stood reverently around her whilst with great

[011-124]devoutness the lady offered up the prayer of commendation for the souls

[011-125]of the departing. All sympathized heartily with her supplications, nor

[011-126]was there any diminution of their fervour when she proceeded to implore

[011-127]that the survivors might have strength and courage for their own hour

[011-128]of need.

[011-129]The situation was indeed very grave. Here was the "Pilgrim" in the

[011-130]middle of the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]], hundreds of miles away from the nearest land,

[011-131]without captain, without crew, at the mercy of the wind and waves. It

[011-132]was a strange fatality that had brought the whale across their path; it

[011-133]was a fatality stranger still that had induced her captain, a man of no

[011-134]ordinary prudence, to risk even his life for the sake of making good a

[011-135]deficient cargo. It was an event almost unknown in the annals of

[011-136][[whaling|whale-fishing]] that not a single man in the whale-boat should escape

[011-137]alive; nevertheless, it was all too true; and now, of all those left on

[011-138]board, Dick Sands, the apprentice-boy of fifteen years of age, was the

[011-139]sole individual who had the slightest knowledge of the management of a

[011-140]ship; the negroes, brave and willing as they were, were perfectly

[011-141]ignorant of seamen's duties; and, to crown all, here was a lady with

[011-142]her child on board, for whose safety the commander of the vessel would

[011-143]be held responsible.

[011-144]Such were the facts which presented themselves to the mind of Dick as,

[011-145]with folded arms, he stood gazing gloomily at the spot where Captain

[011-146]Hull, his esteemed benefactor, had sunk to rise no more. The lad raised

[011-147]his eyes sadly; he scanned the horizon with the vain hope that he might

[011-148]perchance descry some passing vessel to which he could confide Mrs.

[011-149]Weldon and her son; for himself, his mind was made up; he had already

[011-150]resolved that nothing should induce him to quit the "Pilgrim" until he

[011-151]had exhausted every energy in trying to carry her into port.

[011-152]The ocean was all deserted. Since the disappearance of the whale

[011-153]nothing had broken the monotonous surface either of sea or sky. The

[011-154]apprentice, short as his experience was, knew enough to be aware that

[011-155]he was far out of the common track alike of merchantmen or whalers; he

[011-156]would not buoy himself up with false expectations; he would look his

[011-157]situation full and fairly in the face; he would do his best, and trust

[011-158]hopefully in guidance from the Power above.

[011-159]Thus absorbed in his meditations he did not observe that he was not

[011-160]alone. [[negoro|Negoro]], who had gone below immediately after the catastrophe,

[011-161]had again come back upon deck. What this mysterious character had felt

[011-162]upon witnessing the awful calamity it would be impossible to say.

[011-163]Although with his eye he had keenly taken in every detail of the

[011-164]melancholy spectacle, every muscle of, his face had remained unmoved;

[011-165]not a gesture, not a word betrayed the least emotion. Even if he had

[011-166]heard, he had taken no part, nor evinced the faintest interest in Mrs.

[011-167]Weldon's outpouring of prayer.

[011-168]He had made his way to the stern, where Dick Sands was pondering over

[011-169]the responsibilities of his own position, and stood looking towards the

[011-170]apprentice without interrupting his reverie.

[011-171]Catching sight of him, Dick roused himself in an instant, and said,--

[011-172]"You want to speak to me?"

[011-173]"I must speak either to the captain or the boatswain," answered the man.

[011-174]"Negoro," said Dick sharply, "you know as well as I do, that they are

[011-175]both drowned."

[011-176]"Then where am I to get my orders from?" asked the fellow insolently.

[011-177]"From me," promptly rejoined the apprentice.

[011-178]"From you! from a boy of fifteen?"

[011-179]"Yes, from me," repeated Dick, in a firm and resolute voice, looking at

[011-180]the man until he recoiled under his gaze. "From _me_."

[011-181]Mrs. Weldon had heard what passed.

[011-182]"I wish every one on board to understand," she interposed, "that Dick

[011-183]Sands is captain now. Orders must be taken from him, and they must be

[011-184]obeyed."
