[018-001]More than once another contingency occurred to his mind, and gave him

[018-002]some anxiety. He could not overlook the possibility of the wind

[018-003]changing all round. What should he do in such a case? He would of

[018-004]course endeavour by all means to get the schooner on by incessant

[018-005]tacking; but was there not the certainty of a most hazardous delay? and

[018-006]worse than this, was there not a likelihood of the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" being once

[018-007]again driven far out to sea?

[018-008]Happily these forebodings were not realized. The wind, after chopping

[018-009]about for several days, at one time blowing from the north, and at

[018-010]another from the south, finally settled down into a stiffish [[storm-at-sea|gale]] from

[018-011]the west, which did nothing worse than severely strain the masts.

[018-012]In this weary but hopeful endurance time passed on. The 5th of April

[018-013]had arrived. It was more than two months since the "Pilgrim" had

[018-014]quitted [[new-zealand|New Zealand]]; it was true that during the first three weeks of

[018-015]her voyage she had been impeded by protracted calms and contrary winds;

[018-016]but since that time her speed had been rapid, the very tempests had

[018-017]driven her forwards with unwonted velocity; she had never failed to

[018-018]have her bow towards the land, and yet land seemed as remote as ever;

[018-019]the coast line was retreating as they approached it. What could be the

[018-020]solution of the mystery?

[018-021]From the cross-trees one or other of the negroes was kept incessantly

[018-022]on the watch. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] himself, telescope in hand, would repeatedly

[018-023]ascend in the hope of beholding some lofty peak of the Andes emerging

[018-024]from the mists that hung over the horizon. But all in vain.

[018-025]False alarms were given more than once. Sometimes Tom, sometimes

[018-026]Hercules, or one of the others would be sure that a distant speck they

[018-027]had descried was assuredly a mountain ridge; but the vapours were

[018-028]continually gathering in such fantastic forms that their unexperienced

[018-029]eyes were soon deceived, and they seldom had to wait long before their

[018-030]fond delusion was all dispelled.

[018-031]At last, the expected longing was fulfilled. At eight o'clock one

[018-032]morning the mists seemed broken up with unusual rapidity, and the

[018-033]horizon was singularly clear. Dick had hardly gone aloft when his voice

[018-034]rung out,--

[018-035]"Land! Land ahead!"

[018-036]As if summoned by a spell, every one was on deck in an instant: Mrs.

[018-037][[mrs-weldon|Weldon]], sanguine of a speedy end to the general anxiety; little Jack,

[018-038]gratified at a new object of curiosity; Cousin Benedict, already

[018-039]scenting a new field for entomological investigation; old Nan; and the

[018-040]negroes, eager to set foot upon American soil; all, with the exception

[018-041]of [[negoro|Negoro]], all were on deck; but the cook did not stir from his

[018-042]solitude, or betray any sympathy with the general excitement.

[018-043]Whatever hesitation there might be at first soon passed away; one after

[018-044]another soon distinguished the shore they were approaching, and in half

[018-045]an hour there was no room for the most sceptical to doubt that Dick was

[018-046]right. There was land not far ahead.

[018-047]A few miles to the east there was a long low-lying coast; the chain of

[018-048]the Andes ought to be visible; but it was obscured, of course, by the

[018-049]intervening clouds.

[018-050]The "Pilgrim" bore down rapidly towards the land, and in a short time

[018-051]its configuration could be plainly made out. Towards the north-east the

[018-052]coast terminated in a headland of moderate height sheltering a kind of

[018-053]roadstead; on the south-east it stretched out in a long and narrow

[018-054]tongue. The Andes were still wanting to the scene; they must be

[018-055]somewhere in the background; but at present, strange to say, there was

[018-056]only a succession of low cliffs with some trees standing out against

[018-057]the sky. No human habitation, no harbour, not even an indication of a

[018-058]river-mouth, could anywhere be seen.

[018-059]The wind remained brisk, and the schooner was driving directly towards

[018-060]the land, with sails shortened as seemed desirable; but Dick realized

[018-061]to himself the fact that he was utterly incapable of altering her

[018-062]course. With eager eyes he scrutinized his situation. Straight ahead

[018-063]was a reef over which the waves were curling, and around which the surf

[018-064]must be tremendous. It could hardly be more than a mile away. The wind

[018-065]seemed brisker than before.

[018-066]After gazing awhile, Dick seemed to have come to a sudden resolution.

[018-067]He went quickly aft and took the helm. He had seen a little cove, and

[018-068]had made up his mind that he would try and make his way into it. He did

[018-069]not speak a word; he knew the difficulty of the task he had undertaken;

[018-070]he was aware from the white foam, that there was shallow water on

[018-071]either hand; but he kept the secret of the peril to himself, and sought

[018-072]no counsel in coming to his fixed resolve.

[018-073][[dingo|Dingo]] had been trotting up and down the deck. All at once he bounded to

[018-074]the fore, and broke out into a piteous howl. It roused Dick from his

[018-075]anxious cogitations. Was it possible that the animal recognized the

[018-076]coast? It almost seemed as if it brought back some painful associations.

[018-077]The howling of the dog had manifestly attracted Negoro's attention; the

[018-078]man emerged from his cabin, and, regardless of the dog, stood close to

[018-079]the netting; but although he gazed at the surf, it did not seem to

[018-080]occasion him any alarm. Mrs. Weldon, who was watching him, fancied she

[018-081]saw a flush rise to his face, which involuntarily suggested the thought

[018-082]to her mind that Negoro had seen the place before.

[018-083]Either she had no time or no wish to express what had struck her, for

[018-084]she did not mention it to Dick, who, at that moment, left the helm, and

[018-085]came and stood beside her.

[018-086]Dick looked as if he were taking a lingering farewell of the cove past

[018-087]which they were being carried beyond his power to help.

[018-088]In a few moments he turned round to Mrs. Weldon, and said quietly,--

[018-089]"Mrs. Weldon, I am disappointed. I hoped to get the schooner into

[018-090]yonder cove; but there is no chance now; if nothing is done, in half an

[018-091]hour she will be upon that reef. I have but one alternative left. I

[018-092]must run her aground. It will be utter destruction to the ship, but

[018-093]there is no choice. Your safety is the first and paramount

[018-094]consideration."

[018-095]"Do you mean that there is no other course to be taken, Dick?"

[018-096]"None whatever," said Dick decidedly.

[018-097]"It must be as you will," she said.

[018-098]Forthwith ensued the agitating preparations for [[shipwreck|stranding]]. Mrs. Weldon,

[018-099]Jack, Cousin Benedict, and Nan were provided with life belts, while

[018-100]Dick and the negroes made themselves ready for being dashed into the

[018-101]waves. Every precaution that the emergency admitted was duly taken.

[018-102]Mrs. Weldon was entrusted to the special charge of Hercules; Dick made

[018-103]himself responsible for doing all he could for little Jack; Cousin

[018-104]Benedict, who was tolerably calm, was handed over to Bat and Austin;

[018-105]while Actæon promised to look after Nan. Negoro's nonchalance implied

[018-106]that he was quite capable of shifting for himself.

[018-107]Dick had the forethought also to order about a dozen barrels of their

[018-108]cargo to be brought in front, so that when the "Pilgrim" struck, the

[018-109]oil escaping and floating on the waves would temporarily lull their

[018-110]fury, and make smoother water for the passage of the ship.

[018-111]After satisfying himself that there was no other measure to be taken to

[018-112]ameliorate the peril, Dick Sands returned to the helm. The schooner was

[018-113]all but upon the reef, and only a few cables' length from the shore;

[018-114]her starboard quarter indeed was already bathed in the seething foam,

[018-115]and any instant the keel might be expected to grate upon the

[018-116]under-lying rock. Presently a change of colour in the water was

[018-117]observed; it revealed a passage between the rocks. Dick gave the wheel

[018-118]a turn; he saw the chance of getting aground nearer to the shore than

[018-119]he had dared to hope, and he made the most of it. He steered the

[018-120]schooner right into the narrow channel; the sea was furious, and dashed

[018-121]vehemently upon the crags on either hand.

[018-122][Illustration: The sea was furious, and dashed vehemently upon the

[018-123]crags on either hand]

[018-124]"Now, my lads!" he cried to his crew, "now's your time; out with your

[018-125]oil! let it run!"

[018-126]Ready for the order, the negroes poured out the oil, and the raging

[018-127]waters were stilled as if by magic. A few moments more and perchance

[018-128]they would rage more vehemently than ever. But for the instant they

[018-129]were lulled.

[018-130]The "Pilgrim," meanwhile, had glided onwards, and made dead for the

[018-131]adjacent shore. There was a sudden shock. Caught by an enormous wave

[018-132]the schooner had been hurled aground; her masts had fallen, fortunately

[018-133]without injury to any one on board. But the vessel had parted

[018-134]amidships, and was foundering; the water was rushing irresistibly into

[018-135]the hold.

[018-136]The shore, however, was not half a cable's length away; there was a

[018-137]low, dark ridge of rocks that was united to the beach; it afforded

[018-138]ample means of rescue, and in less than ten minutes the "Pilgrim's"

[018-139]captain, crew, and passengers were all landed, with their lives, at the

[018-140]foot of the overhanging cliff.

[018-141]CHAPTER XIV.

[018-142]ASHORE.

[018-143]Thus, after a voyage of seventy-four days, the "Pilgrim" had stranded.

[018-144]Mrs. Weldon and her fellow-voyagers joined in thanksgiving to the kind

[018-145]Providence that had brought them ashore, not upon one of the solitary

[018-146]islands of Polynesia, but upon a solid continent, from almost any part

[018-147]of which there would be no difficulty in getting home.

[018-148]The ship was totally lost. She was lying in the surf a hopeless wreck,

[018-149]and few must be the hours that would elapse before she would be broken

[018-150]up in scattered fragments; it was impossible to save her.

[018-151]Notwithstanding that Dick Sands bewailed the loss of a valuable ship

[018-152]and her cargo to the owner, he had the satisfaction of knowing that he

[018-153]had been instrumental in [[rescue|saving]] what was far more precious, the lives

[018-154]of the owner's wife and son.

[018-155]It was impossible to do more than hazard a conjecture as to the part of

[018-156]the South American coast on which the "Pilgrim" had been cast. Dick

[018-157]imagined that it must be somewhere on the coast of Peru; after sighting

[018-158]Easter Island, he knew that the united action of the equatorial current

[018-159]and the brisk wind must have had the effect of driving the schooner far

[018-160]northward, and he formed his conclusion accordingly. Be the true

[018-161]position, however, what it might, it was all important that it should

[018-162]be accurately ascertained as soon as possible. If it were really in

[018-163]Peru, he would not be long in finding his way to one of the numerous

[018-164]ports and villages that lie along the coast.

[018-165]But the shore here was quite a desert. A narrow strip of beach, strewn

[018-166]with boulders, was enclosed by a cliff of no great height, in which, at

[018-167]irregular intervals, deep funnels appeared as chasms in the rock. Here

[018-168]and there a gentle slope led to the top.

[018-169][[About]] a quarter of a mile to the north was the mouth of a little river

[018-170]which had not been visible from the sea. Its banks were overhung by a

[018-171]number of "rhizophora," a species of mangrove entirely distinct from

[018-172]that indigenous to India. It was soon ascertained that the summit of

[018-173]the cliff was clothed by a dense forest, extending far away in

[018-174]undulations of verdure to the mountains in the background. Had Cousin

[018-175]Benedict been a botanist, he could not have failed to find a new and

[018-176]interesting field for his researches; there were lofty baobabs (to

[018-177]which an extraordinary longevity has often been erroneously ascribed),

[018-178]with bark resembling Egyptian syenite; there were white pines,

[018-179]tamarinds, pepper-plants of peculiar species, and numerous other plants

[018-180]unfamiliar to the eye of a native of the North; but, strange to say,

[018-181]there was not a single specimen of the extensive family of palms, of

[018-182]which more than a thousand varieties are scattered in profusion in so

[018-183]many quarters of the globe.
