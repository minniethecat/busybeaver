# Valparaíso

**Valparaíso** is a major port city on the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] coast of Chile. In *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*, it serves as the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*'s intended destination — the safe harbor toward which the ship was sailing when disaster struck, and the port the disabled vessel would never reach. It functions as a narrative counterpoint to the chaos that engulfs the voyage: the destination that represents normalcy, commerce, and the safe completion of a routine journey.

## Geography

- **Type**: Port city
- **Real or fictional**: Real
- **First mentioned**: Chapter 2 (002-044)

Valparaíso lies on the central Chilean coast, approximately 120 kilometers northwest of Santiago. In the 1870s, it was one of the busiest ports on the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] coast of South America, a crucial waypoint on the maritime trade routes connecting California, Oceania, and the South [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]]. The *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*'s regular route included this port, making it a familiar destination for [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] and his crew.

## The Pilgrim's Intended Destination

The *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]* was scheduled to call at Valparaíso after completing its whaling season in the southern [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]]. The ship "would have to put in at Valparaiso for the purpose of unlading" (002-044) before proceeding further on its commercial circuit. [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] anticipated "a week or two in a voyage from [[new-zealand|New Zealand]] to Valparaiso" (006-038), indicating the routine nature of the passage.

"After completing the unlading at Valparaiso," (006-020) the ship would continue its operations — a simple commercial errand that was never fulfilled. The port thus represents the normal world of commerce and safe harbor that the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*'s passengers expected: a quick stop to unload cargo, perhaps a brief stay ashore, and then onward to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]].

## A Beacon That Faded

After [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]]'s death and the disabling of the ship, Valparaíso becomes an increasingly distant hope. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] assures the passengers that he would "get competent officers to take the ship to Valparaiso" (017-142) or to "some other American port" (012-106). The young captain clings to the possibility of reaching the Chilean coast as a measure of his command.

> "I shall get competent officers to take the ship to Valparaiso — or to some other American port where we can find help." (017-142)

The crew predicted they "should sight the land at no great distance from Valparaiso" (013-155) — an optimistic calculation rendered meaningless by [[negoro|Negoro]]'s compass tampering. One passenger questioned whether the ship was "bound to go to Valparaiso if there is a nearer port which we could" reach (012-041), a practical concern that foreshadows the impossibility of reaching any port at all.

## Negoro's False Pretext

The treacherous cook [[negoro|Negoro]] "made no secret of his intention to land at Valparaiso" (003-072), using the port as a cover for his true intentions. His stated desire to disembark at Valparaíso was entirely plausible — it was, after all, the ship's next scheduled stop — which made it the perfect lie. In reality, [[negoro|Negoro]] was steering the ship toward the coast of [[africa|Africa]], and Valparaíso was nothing more than a convenient fiction.

## Narrative Significance

Valparaíso functions as the novel's "road not taken." Every reference to the port is tinged with dramatic irony: the reader knows — or comes to suspect — that the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]* will never reach Chile. The navigation challenges of "the coast between two oceans from [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] to Columbia" (012-066) that included Valparaíso's waters become irrelevant as the ship drifts ever southward and eastward.

The port also represents the divided intentions aboard the ship. For the honest passengers and crew, Valparaíso is the legitimate destination. For [[negoro|Negoro]], it is a smokescreen. The gap between what Valparaíso represents (safe harbor, commerce, civilization) and what actually awaits (shipwreck, captivity, the [[africa|Africa]]n [[slavery|slave trade]]) measures the scale of the novel's dramatic reversal.
