# Crocodile

A large carnivorous reptile inhabiting the rivers of central [[africa|Africa]], which becomes a direct and deadly threat to the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]] as they attempt to cross a blood-tinged river infested with the creatures.

## Overview

- **Type**: Species
- **Species**: *Crocodylus niloticus* (probable, based on [[africa|Africa]]n range)
- **Owner/Associated with**: None (wild animal)
- **Chapters**: 1, 36, 48

## Role in the Narrative

Crocodiles feature in one of the novel's most harrowing sequences: the crossing of a river by the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]]. The water is "tinged with blood" and crocodiles are visible beneath the bridge, presenting an immediate risk of being dragged under and devoured [036-131]. The scene is one of visceral horror — "a dozen or more crocodiles" strike at the flanks of the moving line, seizing women and children [036-157]. A character is seen examining "one of those that the crocodiles had got," presumably a body part or remnant of a victim [036-165].

The crocodile threat bookends the novel's [[africa|Africa]]n journey. An early, passing mention places crocodiles alongside other dangers of the sea [001-154]. In the final chapters, the scraping of "crocodile scales" on the riverbank, paired with hippopotamus snorting, serves as a persistent reminder that the waters remain lethal [048-104].

## Description in the Text

Verne presents crocodiles through their effects rather than detailed anatomical description. They are agents of sudden violence, striking from beneath the water's surface. The text emphasizes their numbers — "a dozen or more" [036-157] — and their indiscriminate predation. The blood-tinged water [036-131] suggests that the crocodiles have already fed, heightening the sense of danger.

> The water was tinged with blood; crocodiles seen under the bridge. [036-131]

## Historical/Natural History Context

The Nile crocodile (*Crocodylus niloticus*) was one of [[africa|Africa]]'s most feared predators in the 19th century, responsible for numerous human fatalities annually. European explorers' accounts frequently described terrifying river crossings where crocodiles lurked. Verne draws on this tradition, placing the crocodile attack as one of the most viscerally disturbing passages in the novel — a moment where the danger is not abstract but immediate and lethal.
