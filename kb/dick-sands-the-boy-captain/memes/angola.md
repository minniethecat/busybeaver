Angola is a Portuguese colony on the western coast of [[africa|Africa]] and serves as the primary [[africa|Africa]]n setting in the latter half of the novel. When the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]'s survivors are betrayed by [[negoro|Negoro]] and stranded on the [[africa|Africa]]n continent, they find themselves in "the terrible province of Angola" (027-159), a region deeply entangled in [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] [[slavery|slave trade]].

## Geography and Political Context

In the 1870s, Angola was one of three Portuguese provinces on the west coast of [[africa|Africa]], alongside [[congo|Congo]] and [[benguela|Benguela]] (027-047). The province is described as largely unknown to Europeans of the era:

> "Little was known of the province of Angola, except that it was the great slave-trading region of Western Africa" (027-144)

The region is situated south of the [[congo|Congo River]] basin and north of [[benguela|Benguela]]. Major rivers, including one described as "one of the principal rivers of Angola," serve as highways of traffic between the interior and the coast (034-168, 034-172).

## The Slave Trade

Angola's dark reputation in the novel stems from its role as a central hub of the transatlantic [[slavery|slave trade]]:

> "Slaves leave the coasts of Angola and Mozambique to transport them to various parts of the world" (005-070)

The [[slavery|slave trade]] dominates the economy of the region. [[slavery|Slave caravans]] regularly march from the interior to the coast, and the [[slave-caravan|caravan routes]] pass through the territory where the protagonists travel. Unlike the coastal regions, where Portuguese colonial administration maintains a nominal presence, the interior is controlled by native chiefs and [[slavery|slave trade]]rs who operate with impunity.

## Role in the Narrative

The revelation that the castaways have landed not in [[san-francisco|South America]] but in Angola marks the novel's most dramatic turning point. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] and his companions had been deceived by [[negoro|Negoro]], who deliberately wrecked the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]] on the [[africa|Africa]]n coast:

> "They were seated at the foot of a gigantic baobab, in the terrible province of Angola" (027-179)

The party's journey takes them across Angola's interior, through [[kazonnde|Kazonnde]], and toward the coast, [[navigation|navigating]] both hostile terrain and the ever-present threat of [[slavery|slave trade]]rs.

| Aspect | Bolivia (Expected) | Angola (Reality) |
|--------|-------------------|------------------|
| Political status | Independent republic | Portuguese colony |
| Slave trade | Abolished | Active and pervasive |

| Dangers | Natural obstacles | [[slavery|Slavers]] and hostile terrain |

[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] had been persuaded to "take Angola for Bolivia" (028-130), a [[treachery|deception]] that places the entire party in mortal danger.

## Significance

Angola represents the novel's engagement with one of the most pressing moral questions of Jules Verne's era: the ongoing [[atlantic-ocean|Atlantic]] [[slavery|slave trade]]. By setting the story's most harrowing sequences in this region, Verne drew his readers' attention to a continuing humanitarian crisis. The contrast between the freedom of the open sea (Part I) and the horror of the Angolan interior (Part II) structures the novel's moral geography.
