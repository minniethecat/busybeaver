[010-001]As [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] descended the rope-ladder and took his place in the

[010-002]front of the boat, [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] and all on board renewed their good

[010-003]wishes.

[010-004][[dingo|Dingo]] stood with his fore paws upon the taffrail, and appeared as much

[010-005]as any to be bidding the adventurous party farewell.

[010-006]When the boat pushed off, those who were left on board the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]"

[010-007]made their way slowly to the bows, from which the most extensive view

[010-008]was to be gained.

[010-009]The captain's voice came from the retreating boat,--

[010-010]"A sharp look-out, [[dick-sands|Dick]]; a sharp look-out; one eye on us, one on the

[010-011]ship!"

[010-012][Illustration: The Captain's voice came from the retreating boat. _Page

[010-013]72_]

[010-014][Illustration: "I must get you to keep your eye upon that man" _Page

[010-015]73._]

[010-016]"Ay, ay, sir," replied the apprentice.

[010-017]By his gestures the captain showed that he was under some emotion; he

[010-018]called out again, but the boat had made such headway that it was too

[010-019]far off for any words to be heard.

[010-020]Dingo broke out into a piteous howl.

[010-021]The dog was still standing erect, his eye upon the boat in the

[010-022]distance. To the sailors, ever superstitious, the howling was not

[010-023]reassuring. Even Mrs. Weldon was startled.

[010-024]"Why, Dingo, Dingo," she exclaimed, "this isn't the way to encourage

[010-025]your friends. Come here, sir; you must behave better than that!"

[010-026]Sinking down on all fours the animal walked slowly up to Mrs. Weldon,

[010-027]and began to lick her hand.

[010-028]"Ah!" muttered old Tom, shaking his head solemnly, "he doesn't wag his

[010-029]tail at all. A bad omen."

[010-030]All at once the dog gave a savage growl.

[010-031]As she turned her head, Mrs Weldon caught sight of [[negoro|Negoro]] making his

[010-032]way to the forecastle, probably actuated by the general spirit of

[010-033]curiosity to follow the maneuvers of the [[right-whale|whale]]-boat. He stopped and

[010-034]seized a handspike as soon as he saw the ferocious attitude of the dog.

[010-035]The lady was quite unable to pacify the animal, which seemed about to

[010-036]fly upon the throat of the cook, but Dick Sands called out loudly,--

[010-037]"Down, Dingo, down!"

[010-038]The dog obeyed; but it seemed to be with extreme reluctance that he

[010-039]returned to Dick's side; he continued to growl, as if still remembering

[010-040]his rage. Negoro had turned very pale, and having put down the

[010-041]handspike, made his way cautiously back to his own quarters.

[010-042]"Hercules," said Dick, "I must get you to keep your eye upon that man."

[010-043]"Yes, I will," he answered, significantly clenching his fists.

[010-044]Dick took his station at the helm, whence he kept an earnest watch upon

[010-045]the whale-boat, which under the vigourous plying of the seamen's oars

[010-046]had become little more than a speck upon the water.

[010-047]CHAPTER VIII.

[010-048]A CATASTROPHE.

[010-049]Experienced whaleman as he was, Captain Hull knew the difficulty of the

[010-050]task he had undertaken, he was alive to the importance of making his

[010-051]approach to the whale from the leeward, so that there should be no

[010-052]sound to apprize the creature of the proximity of the boat. He had

[010-053]perfect confidence in his boatswain, and felt sure that he would take

[010-054]the proper course to insure a favourable result to the enterprise.

[010-055]"We mustn't show ourselves too soon, Howick," he said.

[010-056]"Certainly not," replied Howick, "I am going to skirt the edge of the

[010-057]discoloured water, and I shall take good care to get well to leeward."

[010-058]"All right," the captain answered, and turning to the crew said, "now,

[010-059]my lads, as quietly as you can."

[010-060]Muffling the sound of their oars by placing straw in the rowlocks, and

[010-061]avoiding the least unnecessary noise, the men skilfully propelled the

[010-062]boat along the outline of the water tinged by the crustacea, so that

[010-063]while the starboard oars still dipped in the green and limpid sea, the

[010-064]larboard were in the deep-dyed waves, and seemed as though they were

[010-065]dripping with blood.

[010-066]"Wine on this side, water on that," said one of the sailors jocosely.

[010-067]"But neither of them fit to drink," rejoined the captain sharply, "so

[010-068]just hold your tongue!"

[010-069]Under Howick's guidance the boat now glided stealthily on to the greasy

[010-070]surface of the reddened waters, where she appeared to float as on a

[010-071]pool of oil. The whale seemed utterly unconscious of the attack that

[010-072]was threatening it, and allowed the boat to come nearer without

[010-073]exhibiting any sign of alarm.

[010-074][Illustration: The whale seemed utterly unconscious of the attack that

[010-075]was threatening it]

[010-076]The wide circuit which the captain had thought it advisable to take had

[010-077]the effect of considerably increasing the distance between his boat and

[010-078]the "Pilgrim," whilst the strange rapidity with which objects at sea

[010-079]become diminished in apparent magnitude, as if viewed through the wrong

[010-080]end of a telescope, made the ship look farther away than she actually

[010-081]was.

[010-082]Another half-hour elapsed, and at the end of it the captain found

[010-083]himself so exactly to leeward that the huge body of the whale was

[010-084]precisely intermediate between his boat and the "Pilgrim." A closer

[010-085]approach must now be made; every precaution must be used; but the time

[010-086]had come to get sufficiently near for the harpoon to be discharged.

[010-087]"Slowly, my men," said the captain, in a low voice; "slowly and softly!"

[010-088]Howick muttered something that implied that the whale had ceased

[010-089]blowing so hard, and that it was aware of their approach; the captain,

[010-090]upon this, enjoined the most perfect silence, but urged his crew

[010-091]onwards, until, in five or six minutes, they were within a cable's

[010-092]length of the finback. Erect at the stern the boatswain stood, and

[010-093]manoeuvred to get the boat as close as possible to the whale's left

[010-094]flank, while he made it an object of special care to keep beyond the

[010-095]reach of its formidable tail, one stroke of which could involve them

[010-096]all in instantaneous disaster.

[010-097]The manipulation of the boat thus left to the boatswain, the captain

[010-098]made ready for the arduous effort that was before him. At the extreme

[010-099]bow, harpoon in hand, with his legs somewhat astride so as to insure

[010-100]his equilibrium, he stood prepared to plunge his weapon into the mass

[010-101]that rose above the surface of the sea. By his side, coiled in a pail,

[010-102]and with one end firmly attached to the harpoon, was the first of the

[010-103]five lines which if the whale should dive to a considerable depth,

[010-104]would have to be joined end to end, one after another.

[010-105]"Are you ready, my lads?" said he, hardly above a whisper.

[010-106]"Ay, ay, sir," replied Howick, speaking as gently as his master, and

[010-107]giving a firmer grip to the rudder-oar that he held in his hands.

[010-108]"Then, alongside at once," was the captain's order, which was promptly

[010-109]obeyed, so that in a few minutes the boat was only about ten feet from

[010-110]the body of the whale. The animal did not move. Was it asleep? In that

[010-111]case there was hope that the very first stroke might be fatal. But it

[010-112]was hardly likely. Captain Hull felt only too sure that there was some

[010-113]different cause to be assigned for its remaining so still and

[010-114]stationary; and the rapid glances of the boatswain showed that he

[010-115]entertained the same suspicion. But it was no time for speculation; the

[010-116]moment for action had arrived, and no attempt was made on either hand

[010-117]to exchange ideas upon the subject.

[010-118]Captain Hull seized his weapon tightly by the shaft, and having poised

[010-119]it several times in the air, in order to make more sure of his aim, he

[010-120]gathered all his strength and hurled it against the side of the finback.

[010-121]"Backwater!" he shouted.

[010-122]The sailors pushed back with all their might, and the boat in an

[010-123]instant was beyond the range of the creature's tail.

[010-124]And now the immoveableness of the animal was at once accounted for.

[010-125]"See; there's a youngster!" exclaimed Howick.

[010-126]And he was not mistaken. Startled by the blow of the harpoon the

[010-127]monster had heeled over on to its side, and the movement revealed a

[010-128]young whale which the mother had been disturbed in the act of suckling.

[010-129]It was a discovery which made Captain Hull aware that the capture of

[010-130]the whale would be attended with double difficulty; he knew; that she

[010-131]would defend "her little one" (if such a term can be applied to a

[010-132]creature that was at least twenty feet long) with the most determined

[010-133]fury; yet having made what he considered a successful commencement of

[010-134]the attack, he would not be daunted, nor deterred from his endeavour to

[010-135]secure so fine a prize.

[010-136]The whale did not, as sometimes happens, make a precipitate dash upon

[010-137]the boat, a proceeding which necessitates the instant cutting of the

[010-138]harpoon-line, and an immediate retreat, but it took the far more usual

[010-139]course of diving downwards almost perpendicularly. It was followed by

[010-140]its calf; very soon, however, after rising once again to the surface

[010-141]with a sudden bound, it began swimming along under water with great

[010-142]rapidity.

[010-143]Before its first plunge Captain Hull and Howick had sufficient

[010-144]opportunity to observe that it was an unusually large balaenoptera,

[010-145]measuring at least eighty feet from head to tail, its colour being of a

[010-146]yellowish-brown, dappled with numerous spots of a darker shade.

[010-147]The pursuit, or what may be more aptly termed "the towing," of the

[010-148]whale had now fairly commenced. The sailors had shipped their oars, and

[010-149]the whale-boat darted like an arrow along the surface of the waves. In

[010-150]spite of the oscillation, which was very violent, Howick succeeded in

[010-151]maintaining equilibrium, and did not need the repeated injunctions with

[010-152]which the agitated captain urged his boatswain to be upon his guard.

[010-153]But fast as the boat flew along, she could not keep pace with the

[010-154]whale, and so rapidly did the line run out that except proper care had

[010-155]been taken to keep the bucket in which it was coiled filled with water,

[010-156]the friction against the edge of the boat would inevitably have caused

[010-157]it to take fire. The whale gave no indication of moderating its speed,

[010-158]so that the first line was soon exhausted, and the second had to be

[010-159]attached to its end, only to be run out with like rapidity. In a few

[010-160]minutes more it was necessary to join on the third line; it was evident

[010-161]that the whale had not been hit in a vital part, and so far from rising

[010-162]to the surface, the oblique direction of the rope indicated that the

[010-163]creature was seeking yet greater depths.

[010-164]"Confound it!" exclaimed the captain; "it seems as if the brute is

[010-165]going to run out all our line."

[010-166]"Yes; and see what a distance the animal is dragging us away from the

[010-167]'Pilgrim,'" answered Howick.

[010-168]"Sooner or later, however," said Captain Hull, "the thing must come to

[010-169]the surface; she is not a fish, you know."

[010-170]"She is [[rescue|saving]] her breath for the sake of her speed," said one of the

[010-171]sailors with a grin.

[010-172]But grin as he might, both he and his companions began to look serious

[010-173]when the fourth line had to be added to the third, and more serious

[010-174]still when the fifth was added to the fourth. The captain even began to

[010-175]mutter imprecations upon the refractory brute that was putting their

[010-176]patience to so severe a test.

[010-177]The last line was nearly all uncoiled, and the general consternation

[010-178]was growing very great, when there was observed to be a slight

[010-179]slackening in the tension.

[010-180]"Thank Heaven!" cried the captain; "the beast has tired herself out at

[010-181]last."

[010-182]Casting his eye towards the "Pilgrim," he saw at a glance that she

[010-183]could not be less than five miles to leeward. It was a long distance,

[010-184]but when, according to his arrangement, he had hoisted the flag on the

[010-185]boat-hook which was to be the signal for the ship to approach, he had

[010-186]the satisfaction of seeing that Dick Sands and the negroes at once

[010-187]began bracing the yards to get as near as possible to the wind. The

[010-188]breeze, however, blew only in short, unsteady puffs, and it was only

[010-189]too evident that the "Pilgrim" would have considerable difficulty in

[010-190]working her way to the whale-boat, even if she succeeded at last.
