# Kazonnde

A slave-trading settlement in the interior of [[angola|Angola]], where [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]], [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]], and their companions are marched by the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]] and held captive. The settlement serves as a hub for the region's slave market and is the site of the final crisis of Part II.

- **First mentioned**: The settlement is the destination of the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]], introduced in the middle chapters of Part II

## Role in the Narrative

Kazonnde represents the darkest point of the castaways' [[africa|Africa]]n ordeal. After the grueling march through [[angola|Angola]], the party is brought to this settlement where the [[slavery|slave trade]] operates openly. Here the major confrontations occur: [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] is separated from the group, [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] faces mortal danger, and the climactic escape is set in motion.

The settlement illustrates Verne's indictment of the ongoing [[slavery|slave trade]]. The [[slave-caravan|caravan]] routes lead to Kazonnde as the commercial center where captives are bought and sold, making it the tangible manifestation of the systematic cruelty that underpins the continent's depopulation (PN=026-091).

## Description in the Text

Kazonnde is depicted as the archetypal slave-trading post of the interior — a place where the brutality of the [[africa|Africa]]n [[slavery|slave trade]] is fully exposed. The settlement lies at the convergence of [[slave-caravan|caravan]] routes that bring slaves from deeper in the interior toward the coast. Verne describes the province of [[angola|Angola]], in which Kazonnde is situated, as "the terrible province of [[angola|Angola]]" and "worse than" anything that could have been anticipated (PN=027-179). As a semi-fictional settlement, Kazonnde synthesizes the real conditions of multiple Portuguese trading posts into a single, dramatic location.

## Historical Context

In 1878, the interior of [[angola|Angola]] remained a zone of active slave-trading despite international [[abolitionism|abolition]]. Portuguese colonial authorities in [[angola|Angola]], [[congo|Congo]], and [[benguela|Benguela]] (PN=027-047) nominally opposed the trade while local networks continued to supply slaves to coastal plantations and overseas markets. Verne's Kazonnde represents a composite of these real interior slave-collection points, dramatizing the distance between [[abolitionism|abolition]]ist ideals and the reality on the ground. The interior trade was so extensive that it continued, Verne wrote, "to depopulate the continent of [[africa|Africa]]" (PN=027-004).
