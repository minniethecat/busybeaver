[039-001]But it was not only on their heads that they made this extraordinary

[039-002]display of ornaments; the lobes of their ears were loaded till they

[039-003]reached their shoulders with a profusion of wooden pegs, open-work

[039-004]copper rings, grains of maize, or little gourds, which served the

[039-005]purpose of snuff-boxes; their necks, arms, wrists, legs, and ankles

[039-006]were a perfect mass of brass and copper rings, or sometimes were

[039-007]covered with a lot of bright buttons. Rows of red beads, called

[039-008]_sames-sames_, or _talakas_, seemed also very popular. As they had no

[039-009]pockets, they attached their knives, pipes and other articles to

[039-010]various parts of their body; so that altogether, in their holiday

[039-011]attire, the rich men of the district might not inappropriately be

[039-012]compared to walking shrines.

[039-013]With their teeth they had all played the strangest of vagaries; the

[039-014]upper and lower incisors had generally been extracted, and the others

[039-015]had been filed to points or carved into hooks, like the fangs of a

[039-016]rattle-snake. Their fingernails were allowed to grow to such an

[039-017]immoderate length as to render the hands well-nigh useless, and their

[039-018]swarthy skins were tattooed with figures of trees, birds, crescents and

[039-019]discs, or, not unfrequently, with those zigzag lines which Livingstone

[039-020]thinks he recognizes as resembling those observed in ancient Egyptian

[039-021]drawings. The tattooing is effected by means of a blue substance

[039-022]inserted into incisions previously made in the skin. Every child is

[039-023]tattooed in precisely the same pattern as his father before him, and

[039-024]thus it may always be ascertained to what family he belongs. Instead of

[039-025]carrying his armorial bearings upon his plate or upon the panels of his

[039-026]carriage, the African magnate wears them emblazoned on his own bosom!

[039-027]The garments that were usually worn were simply aprons of

[039-028]antelope-skins descending to the knees, but occasionally a short

[039-029]petticoat might be seen made of woven grass and dyed with bright

[039-030]colours. The ladies not unfrequently wore girdles of beads attached to

[039-031]green skirts embroidered with silk and ornamented with bits of glass or

[039-032]cowries, or sometimes the skirts were made of the grass cloth called

[039-033]_lambda_, which, in blue, yellow, or black, is so much valued by the

[039-034]people of Zanzibar.

[039-035]Garments of these pretensions, however, always indicated that the

[039-036]wearers belonged to the upper classes; the lower orders, such as the

[039-037]smaller dealers, as well as the slaves, had hardly any clothes at all.

[039-038]The women commonly acted as porters, and arrived at the market with

[039-039]huge baskets on their backs, which they secured by means of straps

[039-040]passed across the forehead. Having deposited their loads upon the

[039-041]chitoka, they turned out their goods, and then seated themselves inside

[039-042]the empty baskets.

[039-043]As the result of the extreme fertility of the country all the articles

[039-044]offered for sale were of a first-rate quality. There were large stores

[039-045]of rice, which had been grown at a profit a hundred times as great as

[039-046]the cost, and maize which, producing three crops in eight months,

[039-047]yielded a profit as large again as the rice. There were also sesame,

[039-048]Urua pepper stronger than Cayenne, manioc, nutmegs, salt, and palm-oil.

[039-049]In the market, too, were hundreds of goats, pigs and sheep, evidently

[039-050]of a Tartar breed, with hair instead of wool; and there was a good

[039-051]supply of fish and poultry. Besides all these there was an attractive

[039-052]display of bright-coloured pottery, the designs of which were very

[039-053]symmetrical.

[039-054]In shrill, squeaky voices, children were crying several varieties of

[039-055]native drinks; banana-wine, _pombé_, which, whatever it was, seemed to

[039-056]be in great demand; _malofoo_, a kind of beer compounded of bananas,

[039-057]and mead, a mixture of honey and water, fermented with malt.

[039-058]But the most prominent feature in the whole market was the traffic in

[039-059]stuffs and ivory. The pieces could be counted by thousands of the

[039-060]unbleached _mcrikani_ from Salem in Massachusetts, of the blue cotton,

[039-061]_kaniki_, thirty-four inches wide, and of the checked _sohari_, blue

[039-062]and black with its scarlet border. More expensive than these were lots

[039-063]of silk _diulis_, with red, green, or yellow grounds, which are sold in

[039-064]lengths of three yards, at prices varying from seven dollars to eighty,

[039-065]when they are interwoven with gold.

[039-066]The ivory had come from well-nigh every part of [[africa|Central Africa]], and was

[039-067]destined for Khartoom, Zanzibar, and Natal, many of the merchants

[039-068]dealing in this commodity exclusively.

[039-069]How vast a number of elephants must be slaughtered to supply this ivory

[039-070]may be imagined when it is remembered that over 200 tons, that is,

[039-071]1,125,000 lbs., are exported annually to Europe. Of this, much the

[039-072]larger share goes to England, where the Sheffield cutlery consumes

[039-073]about 382,500 lbs. From the West Coast of Africa alone the produce is

[039-074]nearly 140 tons.

[039-075]The average weight of a pair of tusks is 28 lbs., and the ordinary

[039-076]value of these in 1874 would be about 60_l_.; but here in Kazonndé were

[039-077]some weighing no less than 165 lbs., of that soft, translucent quality

[039-078]which retains its whiteness far better than the ivory from other

[039-079]sources.

[039-080]As already mentioned, slaves are not unfrequently used as current money

[039-081]amongst the African traders, but the natives themselves usually pay for

[039-082]their goods with Venetian glass beads, of which the chalk-white are

[039-083]called _catchokolos_, the black _bubulus_, and the red

[039-084]_sikunderetches_. Strung in ten rows, or _khetés_, these beads are

[039-085]twisted twice round the neck, forming what is called a _foondo_, which

[039-086]is always reckoned of considerable value.

[039-087]The usual measure by which they are sold is the _frasilah_, containing

[039-088]a weight of about 70 lbs. Livingstone, Cameron and Stanley always took

[039-089]care to be well provided with this kind of currency. In default of

[039-090]beads, the picé, a Zanzibar coin worth something more than a farthing,

[039-091]and _vioon-gooas,_ shells peculiar to the East Coast, are recognized as

[039-092]a medium of exchange in the market. Amongst the [[cannibalism|cannibal]] tribes a

[039-093]certain value is attached to human teeth, and at the lakoni some

[039-094]natives might be seen wearing strings of teeth, the owners of which

[039-095]they had probably, at some previous time, devoured. This species of

[039-096]currency, however, was falling rapidly into disuse.

[039-097]Towards the middle of the day the excitement of the market reached its

[039-098]highest pitch, and the uproar became perfectly deafening. The voices of

[039-099]the eager sellers mingled with those of indignant and overcharged

[039-100]customers; fights were numerous, and as there was an utter absence of

[039-101]any kind of police, no effort was made to restore peace or order

[039-102]amongst the unruly crowd.

[039-103]It was just noon when Alvez gave orders that the slaves he wished to

[039-104]dispose of should be placed on view. Thereupon nearly two thousand

[039-105]unfortunates were brought forward, many of whom had been confined in

[039-106]the dealer's barracks for several months. Most of the stock, however,

[039-107]had been so carefully attended to that they were in good condition, and

[039-108]it was only the last batch that looked as if they would be improved by

[039-109]another month's rest; but as the demand upon the East Coast was now

[039-110]very large, Alvez hoped to get a good price for all, and determined to

[039-111]part with even the last arrivals for whatever sum he could obtain.

[039-112]Amongst these latter, whom the havildars drove like a herd of cattle

[039-113]into the middle of the chitoka, were Tom and his three friends. They

[039-114]were closely chained, and rage and shame were depicted in their

[039-115]countenances.

[039-116]Bat passed a quick and scrutinizing gaze around him, and said to the

[039-117]others,--

[039-118]"I do not see Mr. [[dick-sands|Dick]]."

[039-119]Tom answered mournfully,--

[039-120]"Mr. Dick will be killed, if he is not dead already. Our only hope is

[039-121]that we may now all be bought in one lot; it will be a consolation to

[039-122]us if we can be all together."

[039-123]Tears rose to Bat's eyes as he thought of how his poor old father was

[039-124]likely to be sold, and carried away to wear out his days as a common

[039-125]slave.

[039-126]The sale now commenced. The agents of Alvez proceeded to divide the

[039-127]slaves, men, women and children, into lots, treating them in no respect

[039-128]better than beasts in a cattle-market. Tom and the others were paraded

[039-129]about from customer to customer, an agent accompanying them to proclaim

[039-130]the price demanded. Strong, intelligent-looking Americans, quite

[039-131]different to the miserable creatures brought from the banks of the

[039-132]Zambesi and Lualaba, they at once attracted the observation of the Arab

[039-133]and half-breed dealers. Just as though they were examining a horse, the

[039-134]buyers felt their limbs, turned them round and round, looked at their

[039-135]teeth, and finally tested their paces by throwing a stick to a distance

[039-136]and making them run to fetch it.

[039-137]All the slaves were subjected to similar humiliations; and ail alike,

[039-138]except the very young children, seemed deeply sensible of their

[039-139]degradation. The cruelty exhibited towards them was very vile. Coïmbra,

[039-140]who was half drunk, treated them with the utmost brutality; not that

[039-141]they had any reason to expect any gentler dealings at the hands of the

[039-142]new masters who might purchase them for ivory or any other commodity.

[039-143]Children were torn away from their parents, husbands from their wives,

[039-144]brothers from sisters, and without even the indulgence of a parting

[039-145]word, were separated never to meet again.

[039-146]The scenes that occur at such markets as this at Kazonndé are too

[039-147]heartrending to be described in detail.

[039-148]It is one of the peculiar requirements of the [[slavery|slave-trade]] that the two

[039-149]sexes should have an entirely different destination. In fact, the

[039-150]dealers who purchase men never purchase women. The women, who are

[039-151]required to supply the Mussulman harems, are sent principally to Arab

[039-152]districts to be exchanged for ivory; whilst the men, who are to be put

[039-153]to hard labour, are despatched to the coast, East and West, whence they

[039-154]are exported to the Spanish colonies, or to the markets of Muscat or

[039-155]Madagascar.

[039-156]To Tom and his friends the prospect of being transported to a slave

[039-157]colony was far better than that of being retained in some Central

[039-158]African province, where they could have no chance of regaining their

[039-159][[freedom|liberty]]; and the moment, to them, was accordingly one of great suspense.

[039-160]Altogether, things turned out for them better than they dared

[039-161]anticipate. They had at least the satisfaction of finding that as yet

[039-162]they were not to be separated. Alvez, of course, had taken good care to

[039-163]conceal the origin of this exceptional lot, and their own ignorance of

[039-164]the language thoroughly prevented them from communicating it; but the

[039-165]anxiety to secure so valuable a property rendered the competition for

[039-166]it very keen; the bidding rose higher and higher, until at length the

[039-167]four men were knocked down to a rich Arab dealer, who purposed in the

[039-168]course of a few days to take them to Lake Tanganyika, and thence to one

[039-169]of the deptôs of Zanzibar.

[039-170]This journey, it is true, would be for 1500 miles across the most

[039-171]unhealthy parts of Central Africa, through districts harassed by

[039-172]internal wars; and it seemed improbable that Tom could survive the

[039-173]hardships he must meet; like poor old Nan, he would succumb to fatigue;

[039-174]but the brave fellows did not suffer themselves to fear the future,

[039-175]they were only too happy to be still together; and the chain that bound

[039-176]them one to another was felt to be easier and lighter to bear.

[039-177]Their new master knew that it was for his own interest that his

[039-178]purchase should be well taken care of; he looked to make a substantial

[039-179]profit at Zanzibar, and sent them off at once to his own private

[039-180]barracks; consequently they saw no more of what transpired at Kazonndé.

[039-181]CHAPTER XI.

[039-182]A BOWL OF PUNCH.

[039-183]The afternoon was passing away, and it was now past four o'clock, when

[039-184]the sound of drums, cymbals, and a variety of native instruments was

[039-185]heard at the end of the main thoroughfare. The market was still going

[039-186]on with the same animation as before; half a day's screeching and

[039-187]fighting seemed neither to have wearied the voices nor broken the limbs

[039-188]of the demoniacal traffickers; there was a considerable number of

[039-189]slaves still to be disposed of, and the dealers were haggling over the

[039-190]remaining lots with an excitement of which a sudden panic on the London

[039-191]Stock Exchange could give a very inadequate conception.
