The Portuguese cook hired aboard the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]] at [[new-zealand|Auckland]] who conceals a dark past as a [[slavery|slave-trade]]r. Negoro becomes the novel's hidden antagonist, secretly sabotaging the voyage and ultimately delivering the survivors into the hands of the [[slavery|African slave trade]]. His treachery transforms the narrative from a sea voyage into a harrowing journey across [[angola|Angola]].

## Role & Identity

- **Role**: Cook aboard the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]; secret [[slavery|slave-trade]]r and antagonist
- **Affiliation**: [[slavery|African slave-trading network]]
- **First appearance**: Chapter 3 (003-053)
- **Last appearance**: Chapter 44 (approximately, death in [[africa|Africa]])

## Character Description

"Negoro looked about forty years of age" (003-064). He is described as "a Portuguese by birth, but speaking English perfectly" (003-053). His manner is marked by "his usual indifference" (004-039); he rarely mixes with the crew and maintains an air of sullen detachment. Physically, he is strong and menacing. When confronted, "Negoro frowned, bit his lip, sneered" (012-001) — a pattern of insolent defiance.

The dog [[dingo|Dingo]] exhibits "an unaccountable dislike to Negoro" (005-169) from their first encounter, "showing his teeth" (007-165) whenever the cook appears. This instinctive animosity, shared by [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] who feels a "strong feeling of distrust towards Negoro" (007-173), proves prophetic.

## Role in the Narrative

### Maritime Section: The Hidden Saboteur

Negoro joins the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]] at [[new-zealand|Auckland]] when the previous cook deserts. [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] hires him quickly, "only too glad to avoid delay" (003-056). From the first, Negoro behaves strangely. When the rescued [[africa|Africa]]ns are brought aboard, "Negoro had approached without being observed" (004-151) and manifested unease at their presence.

> "Negoro looked about forty years of age. He was thin, of medium height, and seemed to possess a constitution hardened by privation. He was a Portuguese by birth, but spoke English perfectly." (003-064)

After [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]]'s [[loss-of-captain-hull|tragic death]], Negoro's insolence emerges. He challenges [[dick-sands|Dick]]'s authority: "From you! from a boy of fifteen?" (011-178). His most critical act of sabotage occurs when "Negoro put down something heavy" near the binnacle — a piece of iron that deflects the [[compass|compass]] (014-015). He stands "motionless, apparently watching something out at sea" for half an hour (014-161). When the ship approaches land, "the next moment Negoro appeared on deck; he pointed" (015-133) — and then vanishes overboard before the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]] wrecks.

### African Section: The Slave-Trader Revealed

Negoro has reached [[africa|Africa]] ahead of the survivors and has been conspiring with [[harris|Harris]]. "The two men were [[harris|Harris]] and Negoro" (027-177) meeting in the [[africa|Africa]]n interior to coordinate the [[treachery|betrayal]]. Negoro's true nature as a [[slavery|slave-trade]]r is finally exposed. He is ultimately killed by [[dingo|Dingo]], who has tracked him across continents — [[dingo|the dog]] finally avenging an old wrong connected to the letters "S.V." found on [[dingos-collar|his collar]].

> "Negoro," said Dick sharply, "you know as well as I do, that they are both drowned." (011-174–175)

## Fate

Negoro is killed by [[dingo|Dingo]] in a climactic confrontation that reveals [[dingo|the dog]]'s long-held vendetta. The "S.V." on [[dingos-collar|Dingo's collar]] connects to Negoro's earlier crimes.

## Significance

Negoro is the archetype of concealed villainy — the traitor within. Where [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] represents trustworthy authority, Negoro represents corrupted authority, the trusted insider who betrays for profit. His role in the [[slavery|slave trade]] anchors the novel's moral geography: the maritime world of civilization gives way, through his machinations, to the [[africa|Africa]]n world of human trafficking from which he profits. His hostility toward [[dingo|Dingo]] and [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] marks him as the novel's moral antithesis.
