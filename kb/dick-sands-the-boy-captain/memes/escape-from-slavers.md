The escape from the slavers is the climactic sequence of Part II, in which [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] and his companions flee from the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]] of Ibn Hamish and make a desperate journey across [[angola|Angola]] toward the coast. The escape is made possible by the strength and courage of [[hercules|Hercules]], who had previously evaded capture and now works to free his friends.

## Background

After being betrayed by [[negoro|Negoro]] and stranded on the coast of [[angola|Angola]], the survivors of the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]] are captured by a [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]]. The [[slave-caravan|caravan]], led by the Arab trader Ibn Hamish, marches toward [[kazonnde|Kazonnde]], a major slave-trading center. The prisoners are kept under strict guard:

> "The guard over the prisoners was so strict that Dick Sands recognized the impossibility of attempting an escape for the present" (035-107)

[[hercules|Hercules]], who had earlier been separated from the party, has remained free and been following the [[slave-caravan|caravan]] at a distance, waiting for his opportunity.

## The Escape

[[hercules|Hercules]]' intervention is the turning point. Using his extraordinary strength and knowledge of the terrain, he creates a diversion that allows the prisoners to break free from the [[slave-caravan|caravan]]. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]], though weakened by the ordeal, organizes the flight and leads the party toward the coast.

> "Mrs. Weldon had herself been a witness of the escape of Hercules, but of course she had no idea of the means by which he had effected it" (042-121)

The escape is not without its costs. Several escaped prisoners are recaptured, and "their punishment was terrible" (037-003). The threat of recapture hangs over every step of the journey.

## The Journey to the Coast

The flight across [[angola|Angola]] is a test of endurance. The party must navigate:

- **Hostile terrain**: dense forests, rivers, and unfamiliar landscapes
- **Slave patrols**: armed guards searching for escaped prisoners
- **Exhaustion**: the prisoners are "getting more and more weary" (037-038)
- **Limited supplies**: food and water are scarce

Despite these obstacles, [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]' leadership and [[hercules|Hercules]]' physical strength keep the party moving toward the coast. The group's goal is to reach the [[atlantic-ocean|Atlantic Ocean]], where they hope to find a ship and return to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]].

## Resolution

The escape ultimately succeeds through a combination of courage, loyalty, and fortunate circumstances. The party reaches the coast and makes contact with Portuguese colonial authorities. The trader "heard of the escape of the captive" with fury (046-088), but the protagonists are beyond his reach.

## Significance

The escape from the slavers represents the novel's moral climax. It transforms the story from one of passive suffering into one of active resistance against the institution of [[slavery]]. [[hercules|Hercules]], the [[africa|Africa]]n crewman whose strength had earlier been a source of humor, becomes the agent of [[freedom|liberation]]. The sequence reinforces Jules Verne's [[abolitionism|abolition]]ist message while providing the adventure narrative's most suspenseful chapters.
