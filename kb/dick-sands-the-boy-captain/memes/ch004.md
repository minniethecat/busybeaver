[004-001]It was principally on [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]'s account that the [[captain-hull|Captain]] began to

[004-002]feel uneasiness, and he made up his mind that if he could hail a vessel

[004-003]proceeding to America he should advise his passengers to embark on her;

[004-004]unfortunately, however, he felt that they were still in a latitude far

[004-005]too much to the south to make it likely that they should sight a

[004-006]steamer going to Panama; and at that date, communication between

[004-007]Australia and the New World was much less frequent than it has since

[004-008]become.

[004-009]Still, nothing occurred to interrupt the general monotony of the voyage

[004-010]until the 2nd of February, the date at which our narrative commences.

[004-011]It was about nine o'clock in the morning of that day that [[dick-sands|Dick]] and

[004-012]little Jack had perched themselves together on the top-mast-yards. The

[004-013]weather was very clear, and they could see the horizon right round

[004-014]except the section behind them, hidden by the brigantine-sail on the

[004-015]main-mast. Below them, the bowsprit seemed to lie along the water with

[004-016]its stay-sails attached like three unequal wings; from the lads' feet

[004-017]to the deck was the smooth surface of the fore-mast; and above their

[004-018]heads nothing but the small top-sail and the top-mast. The schooner was

[004-019]running on the larboard tack as close to the wind as possible.

[004-020]Dick Sand was pointing out to Jack how well the ship was ballasted, and

[004-021]was trying to explain how it was impossible for her to capsize, however

[004-022]much she heeled to starboard, when suddenly the little fellow cried

[004-023]out,--

[004-024]"I can see something in the water!"

[004-025]"Where? what?" exclaimed Dick, clambering to his feet upon the yard.

[004-026]"There!" said the child, directing attention to the portion of the

[004-027]sea-surface that was visible between the stay-sails.

[004-028]Dick fixed his gaze intently for a moment, and then shouted out

[004-029]lustily,--

[004-030]"Look out in front, to starboard! There is something afloat. To

[004-031]windward, look out!"

[004-032]CHAPTER III.

[004-033]A RESCUE.

[004-034]At the sound of Dick's voice all the crew, in a moment, were upon the

[004-035]alert. The men who were not on watch rushed to the deck, and Captain

[004-036]Hull hurried from his cabin to the bows. Mrs. Weldon, Nan, and even

[004-037]Cousin Benedict leaned over the starboard taffrails, eager to get a

[004-038]glimpse of what had thus suddenly attracted the attention of the young

[004-039]apprentice. With his usual indifference, [[negoro|Negoro]] did not leave his

[004-040]cabin, and was the only person on board who did not share the general

[004-041]excitement.

[004-042]Speculations were soon rife as to what could be the nature of the

[004-043]floating object which could be discerned about three miles ahead.

[004-044]Suggestions of various character were freely made. One of the sailors

[004-045]declared that it looked to him only like an abandoned raft, but Mrs.

[004-046]Weldon observed quickly that if it were a raft it might be carrying

[004-047]some unfortunate [[shipwreck|shipwrecked]] men who must be rescued if possible.

[004-048]Cousin Benedict asserted that it was nothing more nor less than a huge

[004-049]sea-monster; but the captain soon arrived at the conviction that it was

[004-050]the hull of a vessel that had heeled over on to its side, an opinion

[004-051]with which Dick thoroughly coincided, and went so far as to say that he

[004-052]believed he could make out the copper keel glittering in the sun.

[004-053]"Luff, Bolton, luff!" shouted Captain Hull to the helmsman; "we will at

[004-054]any rate lose no time in getting alongside."

[004-055]"Ay, ay, sir," answered the helmsman, and the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" in an instant

[004-056]was steered according to orders.

[004-057]In spite, however, of the convictions of the captain and Dick, Cousin

[004-058]Benedict would not be moved from his opinion that the object of their

[004-059]curiosity was some huge cetacean.

[004-060]"It is certainly dead, then," remarked Mrs. Weldon; "it is perfectly

[004-061]motionless."

[004-062]"Oh, that's because it is asleep," said Benedict, who, although he

[004-063]would have willingly given up all the whales in the ocean for one rare

[004-064]specimen of an insect, yet could not surrender his own belief.

[004-065]"Easy, Bolton, easy!" shouted the captain when they were getting nearer

[004-066]the floating mass; "don't let us be running foul of the thing; no good

[004-067]could come from knocking a hole in our side; keep out from it a good

[004-068]cable's length."

[004-069]"Ay, ay, sir," replied the helmsman, in his usual cheery way; and by an

[004-070]easy turn of the helm the "Pilgrim's" course was slightly modified so

[004-071]as to avoid all fear of collision.

[004-072]The excitement of the sailors by this time had become more intense.

[004-073]Ever since the distance had been less than a mile all doubt had

[004-074]vanished, and it was certain that what was attracting their attention

[004-075]was the hull of a capsized ship. They knew well enough the established

[004-076]rule that a third of all salvage is the right of the finders, and they

[004-077]were filled with the hope that the hull they were nearing might contain

[004-078]an undamaged cargo, and be "a good haul," to compensate them for their

[004-079]ill-success in the last season.

[004-080]A quarter of an hour later and the "Pilgrim" was within half a mile of

[004-081]the deserted vessel, facing her starboard side. Water-logged to her

[004-082]bulwarks, she had heeled over so completely that it would have been

[004-083]next to impossible to stand upon her deck. Of her masts nothing was to

[004-084]be seen; a few ends of cordage were all that remained of her shrouds,

[004-085]and the try-sail chains were hanging all broken. On the starboard flank

[004-086]was an enormous hole.

[004-087]"Something or other has run foul of her," said Dick.

[004-088]"No doubt of that," replied the captain; "the only wonder is that she

[004-089]did not sink immediately."

[004-090]"Oh, how I hope the poor crew have been saved!" exclaimed Mrs Weldon.

[004-091]"Most probably," replied the captain, "they would all have taken to the

[004-092]boats. It is as likely as not that the ship which did the mischief

[004-093]would continue its course quite unconcerned."

[004-094]"Surely, you cannot mean," cried Mrs Weldon, "that any one could be

[004-095]capable of such inhumanity?"

[004-096]"Only too probable," answered Captain Hull, "unfortunately, such

[004-097]instances are very far from rare."

[004-098]He scanned the drifting ship carefully and continued,--

[004-099]"No, I cannot see any sign of boats here, I should guess that the crew

[004-100]have made an attempt to get to land, at such a distance as this,

[004-101]however, from America or from the islands of the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] I should be

[004-102]afraid that it must be hopeless."

[004-103]"Is it not possible," asked Mrs Weldon, "that some poor creature may

[004-104]still survive on board, who can tell what has happened?"

[004-105]"Hardly likely, madam; otherwise there would have been some sort of a

[004-106]signal in sight. But it is a matter about which we will make sure."

[004-107]The captain waved his hand a little in the direction in which he wished

[004-108]to go, and said quietly,--

[004-109]"Luff, Bolton, luff a bit!"

[004-110]The "Pilgrim" by this time was not much more than three cables' lengths

[004-111]from the ship, there was still no token of her being otherwise than

[004-112]utterly deserted, when Dick Sands suddenly exclaimed,--

[004-113]"Hark! if I am not much mistaken, that is a dog barking!"

[004-114]Every one listened attentively; it was no fancy on Dick's part, sure

[004-115]enough a stifled barking could be heard, as if some unfortunate dog had

[004-116]been imprisoned beneath the hatchways; but as the deck was not yet

[004-117]visible, it was impossible at present to determine the precise truth.

[004-118]Mrs Weldon pleaded,--

[004-119]"If it is only a dog, captain, let it be saved."

[004-120]"Oh, yes, yes, mamma, [[dingo|the dog]] must be saved!" cried little Jack; "I

[004-121]will go and get a bit of sugar ready for it."

[004-122][Illustration: Negoro had approached without being noticed by any one]

[004-123]"A bit of sugar, my child, will not be much for a starved dog."

[004-124]"Then it shall have my soup, and I will do without," said the boy, and

[004-125]he kept shouting, "Good dog! good dog!" until he persuaded himself that

[004-126]he heard the animal responding to his call.

[004-127]The vessels were now scarcely three hundred feet apart; the barking was

[004-128]more and more distinct, and presently a great dog was seen clinging to

[004-129]the starboard netting. It barked more desperately than ever.

[004-130]"Howick," said Captain Hull, calling to the boatswain, "heave to, and

[004-131]lower the small boat."

[004-132]The sails were soon trimmed so as to bring the schooner to a standstill

[004-133]within half a cable's length of the disabled craft, the boat was

[004-134]lowered, and the captain and Dick, with a couple of sailors, went on

[004-135]board. The dog kept up a continual yelping; it made the most vigourous

[004-136]efforts to retain its hold upon the netting, but perpetually slipped

[004-137]backwards and fell off again upon the inclining deck. It was soon

[004-138]manifest, however, that all the noise the creature was making was not

[004-139]directed exclusively towards those who were coming to its rescue, and

[004-140]Mrs. Weldon could not divest herself of the impression that there must

[004-141]be some survivors still on board. All at once the animal changed its

[004-142]gestures. Instead of the crouching attitude and supplicating whine with

[004-143]which it seemed to be imploring the compassion of those who were

[004-144]nearing it, it suddenly appeared to become bursting with violence and

[004-145]furious with rage.

[004-146]"What ails the brute?" exclaimed Captain Hull.

[004-147]But already the boat was on the farther side of the wrecked ship, and

[004-148]the captain was not in a position to see that Negoro the cook had just

[004-149]come on to the schooner's deck, or that it was obvious that it was

[004-150]against him that the dog had broken out in such obstreperous fury.

[004-151]Negoro had approached without being noticed by any one; he made his way

[004-152]to the forecastle, whence, without a word or look of surprise, he gazed

[004-153]a moment at the dog, knitted his brow, and, silent and unobserved as he

[004-154]had come, retired to his kitchen.

[004-155]As the boat had rounded the stern of the drifting hull, it had been

[004-156]observed that the one word "Waldeck" was painted on the aft-board, but

[004-157]that there was no intimation of the port to which the ship belonged. To

[004-158]Captain Hull's experienced eye, however, certain details of

[004-159]construction gave a decided confirmation to the probability suggested

[004-160]by her name that she was of American build.

[004-161]Of what had once been a fine brig of 500 tons burden this hopeless

[004-162]wreck was now all that remained. The large hole near the bows indicated

[004-163]the place where the disastrous shock had occurred, but as, in the

[004-164]heeling over, this aperture had been carried some five or six feet

[004-165]above the water, the vessel had escaped the immediate foundering which

[004-166]must otherwise have ensued; but still it wanted only the rising of a

[004-167]heavy swell to submerge the ship at any time in a few minutes.

[004-168]It did not take many more strokes to bring the boat close to the

[004-169]larboard bulwark, which was half out of the water, and Captain Hull

[004-170]obtained a view of the whole length of the deck. It was clear from end

[004-171]to end. Both masts had been snapped off within two feet of their

[004-172]sockets, and had been swept away with shrouds, stays, and rigging. Not

[004-173]a single spar was to be seen floating anywhere within sight of the

[004-174]wreck, a circumstance from which it was to be inferred that several

[004-175]days at least had elapsed since the catastrophe.

[004-176]Meantime the dog, sliding down from the taffrail, got to the centre

[004-177]hatchway, which was open. Here it continued to bark, alternately

[004-178]directing its eyes above deck and below.

[004-179]"Look at that dog!" said Dick; "I begin to think there must be somebody

[004-180]on board."

[004-181]"If so," answered the captain, "he must have died of hunger; the water

[004-182]of course has flooded the store-room."

[004-183]"No," said Dick; "that dog wouldn't look like that if there were nobody

[004-184]there alive."

[004-185][Illustration: The dog began to swim slowly and with manifest weakness

[004-186]towards the boat.]

[004-187]Taking the boat as close as was prudent to the wreck, the captain and

[004-188]Dick called and whistled repeatedly to the dog, which after a while let

[004-189]itself slip into the sea, and began to swim slowly and with manifest

[004-190]weakness towards the boat. As soon as it was lifted in, the animal,

[004-191]instead of devouring the piece of bread that was offered him, made its

[004-192]way to a bucket containing a few drops of fresh water, and began

[004-193]eagerly to lap them up.
