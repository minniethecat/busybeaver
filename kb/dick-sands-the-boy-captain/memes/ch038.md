[038-001]It disappointed him, too, that [[dingo|Dingo]] did not come back. Ever since the

[038-002]dog had brought him the first note, he had kept an answer written ready

[038-003]to send to Hercules, imploring him to look after [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]], and to

[038-004]keep him informed of everything. He began to fear that the faithful

[038-005]creature must be dead, perhaps perished in some attempt to reach

[038-006]himself; it was, however, quite possible that Hercules had taken the

[038-007]dog in some other direction, hoping to gain somedépôt in the interior.

[038-008]But so thoroughly had [[dick-sands|Dick]] persuaded himself that Mrs. Weldon had

[038-009]preceded him to Kazonndé that his disappointment became more and more

[038-010]keen when he failed to discover her. For a while he seemed to yield to

[038-011]despair, and sat down sorrowful and sick at heart.

[038-012]Suddenly a chorus of voices and trumpets broke upon his ear; he was

[038-013]startled into taking a new interest in what was going on.

[038-014]"Alvez! Alvez!" was the cry again and again repeated by the crowd.

[038-015]Here, then, was the great man himself about to appear. Was it not

[038-016]likely that Harris or [[negoro|Negoro]] might be with him?

[038-017]Dick stood erect and resolute, his eye vivid with expectation; he felt

[038-018]all eagerness to stand face to face with his betrayers; boy as he was,

[038-019]he was equal to cope with them both.

[038-020]The _kitanda_, which came in sight at the end of the street, was

[038-021]nothing more than a kind of hammock covered by a faded and ragged

[038-022]curtain. An old negro stepped out of it. His attendants greeted him

[038-023]with noisy acclamations.

[038-024]This, then, was the great trader, José Antonio Alvez.

[038-025]Immediately following him was his friend Coïmbra, son of the chief

[038-026]Coïmbra of Bihé, and, according to Cameron, the greatest blackguard in

[038-027]the province. This sworn ally of Alvez, this organizer of his

[038-028]slave-raids, this commander, worthy of his own horde of bandits, was

[038-029]utterly loathsome in his appearance, his flesh was filthily dirty, his

[038-030]eyes were bloodshot, his skin yellow, and his long hair all

[038-031]dishevelled. He had no other attire than a tattered shirt, a tunic made

[038-032]of grass, and a battered straw hat, under which his countenance

[038-033]appeared like that of some old hag.

[038-034]Alvez himself, whose clothes were like those of an old Turk the day

[038-035]after a carnival, was one degree more respectable in appearance than

[038-036]his satellite, not that his looks spoke much for the very highest class

[038-037]of African slave-dealers. To Dick's great disappointment, neither

[038-038]Harris nor Negoro was among his retinue.

[038-039]Both Alvez and Coïmbra shook hands with Ibn Hamish, the leader of the

[038-040][[slave-caravan|caravan]], and congratulated him on the success of the expedition. Alvez

[038-041]made a grimace on being told that half the slaves had died on the way,

[038-042]but on the whole he seemed satisfied; he could meet the demand that at

[038-043]present existed, and would lose no time in bartering the new arrival

[038-044]for ivory or _hannas_, copper in the shape of a St. Andrew's cross, the

[038-045]form in which the metal is exported in [[africa|Central Africa]].

[038-046]After complimenting the havildars upon the way in which they had done

[038-047]their work, the trader gave orders that the porters should be paid and

[038-048]dismissed. The conversations were carried on in a mixture of Portuguese

[038-049]and native idioms, in which the African element abounded so largely

[038-050]that a native of Lisbon would have been at a loss to understand them.

[038-051]Dick, of course, could not comprehend what was said, and it was only

[038-052]when he saw a havildar go towards the cell in which Tom and the others

[038-053]were confined, that he realized that the talk was about himself and his

[038-054]party.

[038-055]When the negroes were brought out, Dick came close up, being anxious to

[038-056]learn as much as he could of what was in contemplation. The old

[038-057]trader's eyes seemed to brighten as he glanced upon the three strapping

[038-058]young men who, he knew, would soon be restored to their full strength

[038-059]by rest and proper food. They at least would get a good price; as for

[038-060]poor old Tom, he was manifestly so broken down by infirmity and age,

[038-061]that he would have no value in the market.

[038-062]In a few words of broken English, which Alvez had picked up from some

[038-063]of his agents, he ironically gave them all a welcome.

[038-064]"Glad to see you!" he said, with a diabolical grin.

[038-065]Tom knew what he meant, and drew himself up proudly.

[038-066]"We are free men!" he protested, "free citizens of the United States!"

[038-067]"Yes, yes!" replied Alvez, grinning, "you are Americans; very glad to

[038-068]see you!"

[038-069]"Very glad to see you!" echoed Coïmbra, and walking up to Austin he

[038-070]felt his chest and shoulders, and then proceeded to open his mouth in

[038-071]order to examine his teeth.

[038-072]A blow from Austin's powerful fist sent the satellite staggering

[038-073]backwards.

[038-074]Some soldiers made a dash and seized the young negro, evidently ready

[038-075]to make him pay dearly for his temerity; but Alvez was by no means

[038-076]willing to have any injury done to his newly-acquired property, and

[038-077]called them off. He hardly attempted to conceal his amusement at

[038-078]Coïmbra's discomfiture, although the blow had cost him one of his front

[038-079]teeth.

[038-080]After he had recovered somewhat from the shock, Coïmbra stood scowling

[038-081]at Austin, as if mentally vowing vengeance on some future occasion.

[038-082]Dick Sands was now himself brought forward in the custody of a

[038-083]havildar. It was clear that Alvez had been told all about him, for

[038-084]after scanning him for a moment, he stammered out in his broken

[038-085]English,--

[038-086]"Ah! ah! the little Yankee!"

[038-087]"Yes," replied Dick; "I see you know who I am. What are you going to do

[038-088]with me and my friends?"

[038-089]"Yankee! little Yankee!" repeated the trader, who either did not or

[038-090]would not comprehend the meaning of Dick's question.

[038-091]Dick turned to Coïmbra and made the same inquiry of him; in spite of

[038-092]his degraded features, now still farther disfigured by being swollen

[038-093]from the blow, it was easy to recognize that he was not of native

[038-094]origin. He refused to answer a word, and only stared again with the

[038-095]vicious glare of malevolence.

[038-096]Meanwhile, Alvez had begun to talk to Ibn Hamish. Dick felt sure that

[038-097]they intended to separate him from the negroes, and accordingly took

[038-098]the opportunity of whispering a few words to them.

[038-099]"My friends, I have heard from Hercules. Dingo brought me a note from

[038-100]him, tied round his neck. He says Harris and Negoro have carried off

[038-101]Mrs. Weldon, Jack, and Mr. Benedict. He did not know where. Have

[038-102]patience, and we will find them yet."

[038-103][Illustration: With a yell and a curse, the American fell dead at his

[038-104]feet.]

[038-105]"And where's Nan?" muttered Tom, in a low voice.

[038-106]"Dead," replied Dick, and was about to add more, when a hand was laid

[038-107]upon his shoulder, and a voice that he knew too well exclaimed,--

[038-108]"Well, my young friend, how are you? I am glad to see you again."

[038-109]He turned round quickly. Harris stood before him.

[038-110]"Where is Mrs. Weldon?" asked Dick impetuously.

[038-111]"Ah, poor thing!" answered Harris, with an air of deep commiseration.

[038-112]"What! is she dead?" Dick almost shrieked; "where is her child?"

[038-113]"Poor little fellow!" said Harris, in the same mournful tone.

[038-114]These insinuations, that those in whose welfare he was so deeply

[038-115]interested had succumbed to the hardships of the journey, awoke in

[038-116]Dick's mind a sudden and irresistible desire for vengeance. Darting

[038-117]forwards he seized the cutlass that Harris wore in his belt, and

[038-118]plunged it into his heart.

[038-119]With a yell and a curse, the American fell dead at his feet.

[038-120]CHAPTER X.

[038-121]MARKET-DAY.

[038-122]So sudden was Dick's action that it had been impossible to parry his

[038-123]blow. Several of the natives rushed on him, and in all likelihood would

[038-124]have struck him down upon the spot had not Negoro arrived at that very

[038-125]moment. At a sign from him the natives drew back, and proceeded to

[038-126]raise and carry away Harris's corpse.

[038-127]Alvez and Coïmbra were urgent in their demand that Dick should

[038-128]forthwith be punished by death, but Negoro whispered to them that they

[038-129]would assuredly be the gainers by delay, and they accordingly contented

[038-130]themselves with ordering the youth to be placed under strict

[038-131]supervision.

[038-132]This was the first time that Dick had set eyes upon Negoro since he had

[038-133]left the coast; nevertheless, so heartbroken was he at the intelligence

[038-134]he had just received, that he did not deign to address a word to the

[038-135]man whom he knew to be the real author of all his misery. He cared not

[038-136]now what became of him.

[038-137]Loaded with chains, he was placed in the dungeon where Alvez was

[038-138]accustomed to confine slaves who had been condemned to death for mutiny

[038-139]or violence. That he had no communication with the outer world gave him

[038-140]no concern; he had avenged the death of those for whose safety he had

[038-141]felt himself responsible, and could now calmly await the fate which he

[038-142]could not doubt was in store for him; he did not dare to suppose that

[038-143]he had been temporarily spared otherwise than that he might suffer the

[038-144]cruellest tortures that native ingenuity could devise. That the

[038-145]"[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]'s" cook now held in his power the boy captain he so thoroughly

[038-146]hated was warrant enough that the sternest possible measure of

[038-147]vengeance would be exacted.

[038-148][Illustration: Accompanied by Coïmbra, Alvez himself was one of the

[038-149]first arrivals.]

[038-150]Two days later, the great market, the _lakoni_, commenced. Although

[038-151]many of the principal traders were there from the interior, it was by

[038-152]no means exclusively a slave-mart; a considerable proportion of the

[038-153]natives from the neighbouring provinces assembled to dispose of the

[038-154]various products of the country.

[038-155]Quite early the great _chitoka_ of Kazonndé was all alive with a

[038-156]bustling concourse of little under five thousand people, including the

[038-157]slaves of old Alvez, amongst whom were Tom and his three partners in

[038-158]adversity--an item by no means inconsiderable in the dealer's stock.

[038-159]Accompanied by Coïmbra, Alvez himself was one of the first arrivals. He

[038-160]was going to sell his slaves in lots to be conveyed in caravans into

[038-161]the interior. The dealers for the most part consisted of half-breeds

[038-162]from Ujiji, the principal market on Lake Tanganyika, whilst some of a

[038-163]superior class were manifestly Arabs.

[038-164]The natives that were assembled were of both sexes, and of every

[038-165]variety of age, the women in particular displaying an aptitude in

[038-166]making bargains that is shared by their sisters elsewhere of a lighter

[038-167]hue; and it may be said that no market of the most civilized region

[038-168]could be characterized by greater excitement or animation, for amongst

[038-169]the savages of Africa the customer makes his offer in equally noisy

[038-170]terms as the vendor.

[038-171]The _lakoni_ was always considered a kind of fète-day; consequently the

[038-172]natives of both sexes, though their clothing was scanty in extent, made

[038-173]a point of appearing in a most lavish display of ornaments. Their

[038-174]head-gear was most remarkable. The men had their hair arranged in every

[038-175]variety of eccentric device; some had it divided into four parts,

[038-176]rolled over cushions and fastened into a chignon, or mounted in front

[038-177]into a bunch of tails adorned with red feathers; others plastered it

[038-178]thickly with a mixture of red mud and oil similar to that used for

[038-179]greasing machinery, and formed it into cones or lumps, into which they

[038-180]inserted a medley of iron pins and ivory skewers; whilst the greatest

[038-181]dandies had a glass bead threaded upon every single hair, the whole

[038-182]being fastened together by a tattooing-knife driven through the

[038-183]glittering mass.

[038-184]As a general rule, the women preferred dressing their hair in little

[038-185]tufts about the size of a cherry, arranging it into the shape of a cap,

[038-186]with corkscrew ringlets on each side of the face. Some wore it simply

[038-187]hanging down their backs, others in French fashion, with a fringe

[038-188]across the forehead; but every _coiffure_, without exception, was

[038-189]daubed and caked either with the mixture of mud and grease, or with a

[038-190]bright red extract of sandal-wood called _nkola_.
