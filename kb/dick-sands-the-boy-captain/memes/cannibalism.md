# Cannibalism

The practice of eating human flesh that serves as the ultimate horror the protagonists must confront in the [[africa|Africa]]n interior of *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*.

## Definition

Cannibalism (anthropophagy) is the act of humans consuming the flesh or organs of other humans. In the 19th-century European imagination, cannibalism was the ultimate marker of savagery, and Verne employs it as the climax of the novel's representation of [[africa|Africa]]n danger.

## Role in the Novel

Cannibalism represents the extreme of the horrors awaiting the party in [[africa|Africa]]. Verne's narrator states plainly: "Only too true it is that cannibalism is still openly practised in" certain regions (040-135). The party discovers "traces of a widely-spread cannibalism amongst the inhabitants of" the interior (040-142). The ultimate confirmation comes when [[dick-sands|Dick]] has "reason to feel convinced that cannibalism had been" practised on victims they encounter (048-178).

The cannibalism theme heightens the stakes for the captured party. It transforms their journey from a geographical misadventure into a fight for survival against the most primal threat imaginable. The fear of being eaten adds urgency to every escape attempt.

## Historical Context

Verne's portrayal reflects genuine 19th-century accounts of ritual anthropophagy in parts of [[africa|Central Africa]], documented by explorers of the period. While modern scholarship recognizes these accounts as often exaggerated or misrepresented, they formed part of the era's conception of the [[africa|Africa]]n interior. The novel uses cannibalism both as a genuine historical reality and as a narrative device to heighten the dramatic stakes.
