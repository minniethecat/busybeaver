[006-001]But [[dingo|Dingo]], though unsociable, was not savage. Old Tom said that, on

[006-002]board the "Waldeck," he had noticed that the animal seemed to have a

[006-003]particular dislike to negroes; not that he actually attempted to do

[006-004]them any harm, only he uniformly avoided them, giving an impression

[006-005]that he must have been systematically ill-treated by the natives of

[006-006]that part of [[africa|Africa]] in which he had been found. During the ten days

[006-007]that had elapsed since the collision, Dingo had kept resolutely aloof

[006-008]from Tom and his companions; they could not tell what he had been

[006-009]feeding on; they only knew that, like themselves, he had suffered an

[006-010]excruciating thirst.

[006-011]Such had been the experience of the survivors of the "Waldeck." Their

[006-012]situation had been most critical. Even if they survived the pangs of

[006-013]want of food, the slightest [[storm-at-sea|gale]] or the most inconsiderable swell might

[006-014]at any moment have sunk the water-logged ship, and had it not been that

[006-015]calms and contrary winds had contributed to the opportune arrival of

[006-016]the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]," an inevitable fate was before them; their corpses must

[006-017]lie at the bottom of the sea.

[006-018][[captain-hull|Captain Hull]]'s act of humanity, however, would not be complete unless

[006-019]he succeeded in restoring the [[shipwreck|shipwrecked]] men to their homes. This he

[006-020]promised to do. After completing the unlading at Valparaiso, the

[006-021]"Pilgrim" would make direct for California, where, as [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]

[006-022]assured them, they would be most hospitably received by her husband,

[006-023]and provided with the necessary means for returning to Pennsylvania.

[006-024]The five men, who, as the consequence of the shipwreck, had lost all

[006-025]the savings of their last three years of toil, were profoundly grateful

[006-026]to their kind-hearted benefactors; nor, poor negroes as they were, did

[006-027]they utterly resign the hope that at some future time they might have

[006-028]it in their power to repay the debt which they owed their deliverers.

[006-029][Illustration: The good natured negroes were ever ready to lend a

[006-030]helping hand.]

[006-031]CHAPTER V.

[006-032]DINGO'S SAGACITY.

[006-033]Meantime the "Pilgrim" pursued her course, keeping as much as possible

[006-034]to the east, and before evening closed in the hull of the "Waldeck" was

[006-035]out of sight.

[006-036]Captain Hull still continued to feel uneasy about the constant

[006-037]prevalence of calms; not that for himself he cared much about the delay

[006-038]of a week or two in a voyage from [[new-zealand|New Zealand]] to Valparaiso, but he was

[006-039]disappointed at the prolonged inconvenience it caused to his lady

[006-040]passenger. Mrs. Weldon, however, submitted to the detention very

[006-041]philosophically, and did not utter a word of complaint.

[006-042]The captain's next care was to improvise sleeping accommodation for Tom

[006-043]and his four associates. No room for them could possibly be found in

[006-044]the crew's quarters, so that their berths had to be arranged under the

[006-045]forecastle; and as long as the weather continued fine, there was no

[006-046]reason why the negroes, accustomed as they were to a somewhat rough

[006-047]life, should not find themselves sufficiently comfortable.

[006-048]After this incident of the discovery of the wreck, life on board the

[006-049]"Pilgrim" relapsed into its ordinary routine. With the wind invariably

[006-050]in the same direction, the sails required very little shifting; but

[006-051]whenever it happened, as occasionally it would, that there was any

[006-052]tacking to be done, the good-natured negroes were ever ready to lend a

[006-053]helping hand; and the rigging would creak again under the weight of

[006-054]Hercules, a great strapping fellow, six feet high, who seemed almost to

[006-055]require ropes of extra strength made for his special use.

[006-056]Hercules became at once a great favourite with little Jack; and when

[006-057]the giant lifted him like a doll in his stalwart arms, the child fairly

[006-058]shrieked with delight.

[006-059]"Higher! higher! very high!" Jack would say sometimes.

[006-060]"There you are, then, Master Jack," Hercules would reply as he raised

[006-061]him aloft.

[006-062]"Am I heavy?" asked the child,

[006-063]"As heavy as a feather."

[006-064]"Then lift me higher still," cried Jack; "as high as ever you can

[006-065]reach."

[006-066]And Hercules, with the child's two feet supported on his huge palm,

[006-067]would walk about the deck with him like an acrobat, Jack all the time

[006-068]endeavouring, with vain efforts, to make him "feel his weight."

[006-069]Besides [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] and Hercules, Jack admitted a third friend to his

[006-070]companionship. This was Dingo. The dog, unsociable as he had been on

[006-071]board the "Waldeck," seemed to have found society more congenial to his

[006-072]tastes, and being one of those animals that are fond of children, he

[006-073]allowed Jack to do with him almost anything he pleased. The child,

[006-074]however, never thought of hurting the dog in any way, and it was

[006-075]doubtful which of the two had the greater enjoyment of their mutual

[006-076]sport. Jack found a live dog infinitely more entertaining than his old

[006-077]toy upon its four wheels, and his great delight was to mount upon

[006-078]Dingo's back, when the animal would gallop off with him like a

[006-079]race-horse with his jockey. It must be owned that one result of this

[006-080]intimacy was a serious diminution of the supply of sugar in the

[006-081]store-room. Dingo was the delight of all the crew excepting [[negoro|Negoro]], who

[006-082]cautiously avoided coming in contact with an animal who showed such

[006-083]unmistakable symptoms of hostility.

[006-084]The new companions that Jack had thus found did not in the least make

[006-085]him forget his old friend Dick Sands, who devoted all his leisure time

[006-086]to him as assiduously as ever. Mrs. Weldon regarded their intimacy with

[006-087]the greatest satisfaction, and one day made a remark to that effect in

[006-088]the presence of Captain Hull.

[006-089][Illustration: "There you are, then, Master Jack!"]

[006-090]"You are right, madam," said the captain cordially; "Dick is a capital

[006-091]fellow, and will be sure to be a first-rate sailor. He has an instinct

[006-092]which is little short of a genius; it supplies all deficiencies of

[006-093]theory. Considering how short an experience and how little instruction

[006-094]he has had, it is quite wonderful how much he knows about a ship."

[006-095]"Certainly for his age," assented Mrs. Weldon, "he is singularly

[006-096]advanced. I can safely say that I have never had a fault to find with

[006-097]him. I believe that it is my husband's intention, after this voyage, to

[006-098]let him have systematic training in navigation, so that he may be able

[006-099]ultimately to become a captain."

[006-100]"I have no misgivings, madam," replied the captain; "there is every

[006-101]reason to expect that he will be an honour to the service."

[006-102]"Poor orphan!" said the lady; "he has been trained in a hard school."

[006-103]"Its lessons have not been lost upon him," rejoined Captain Hull; "they

[006-104]have taught him the prime lesson that he has his own way to make in the

[006-105]world."

[006-106]The eyes of the two speakers turned as it were unwittingly in the

[006-107]direction where Dick Sands happened to be standing. He was at the helm.

[006-108]"Look at him now!" said the captain; "see how steadily he keeps his eye

[006-109]upon the fore; nothing distracts him from his duty; he is as much to be

[006-110]depended on as the most experienced helmsman. It was a capital thing

[006-111]for him that he began his training as a cabin-boy. Nothing like it.

[006-112]Begin at the beginning. It is the best of training for the merchant

[006-113]service."

[006-114]"But surely," interposed Mrs. Weldon, "you would not deny that in the

[006-115]navy there have been many good officers who have never had the training

[006-116]of which you are speaking?"

[006-117]"True, madam; but yet even some of the best of them have begun at the

[006-118]lowest step of the ladder. For instance, Lord Nelson."

[006-119]Just at this instant Cousin Benedict emerged from the stern-cabin, and

[006-120]completely absorbed, according to his wont, in his own pursuit, began

[006-121]to wander up and down the deck, peering into the interstices of the

[006-122]network, rummaging under the seats, and drawing his long fingers along

[006-123]the cracks in the floor where the tar had crumbled away.

[006-124]"Well, Benedict, how are you getting on?" asked Mrs Weldon.

[006-125]"I? Oh, well enough, thank you," he replied dreamily; "but I wish we

[006-126]were on shore."

[006-127]"What were you looking for under that bench?" said Captain Hull.

[006-128]"Insects, of course," answered Benedict; "I am always looking for

[006-129][[entomology|insects]]."

[006-130]"But don't you know, Benedict," said Mrs. Weldon, "that Captain Hull is

[006-131]far too particular to allow any vermin on the deck of his vessel?"

[006-132]Captain Hull smiled and said,--

[006-133]"Mrs Weldon is very complimentary; but I am really inclined to hope

[006-134]that your investigations in the cabins of the 'Pilgrim' will not be

[006-135]attended with much success."

[006-136]Cousin Benedict shrugged his shoulders in a manner that indicated that

[006-137]he was aware that the cabins could furnish nothing attractive in the

[006-138]way of insects.

[006-139]"However," continued the captain, "I dare say down in the hold you

[006-140]could find some cockroaches; but cockroaches, I presume, would be of

[006-141]little or no interest to you."

[006-142]"No interest?" cried Benedict, at once warmed into enthusiasm; "why,

[006-143]are they not the very orthoptera that roused the imprecations of Virgil

[006-144]and Horace? Are they not closely allied to the _Periplaneta orientalis_

[006-145]and the American Kakerlac, which inhabit--"

[006-146]"I should rather say infest," interrupted the captain.

[006-147]"Easy enough to see, sir," replied Benedict, stopping short with

[006-148]amazement, "that you are not an entomologist!"

[006-149]"I fear I must plead guilty to your accusation," said the captain

[006-150]good-humouredly.

[006-151]"You must not expect every one to be such an enthusiast in your

[006-152]favourite study as yourself." Mrs. Weldon interposed; "but are you not

[006-153]satisfied with the result of your explorations in New Zealand?"

[006-154]"Yes, yes," answered Benedict, with a sort of hesitating reluctance; "I

[006-155]must not say I was dissatisfied; I was really very delighted to secure

[006-156]that new staphylin which hitherto had never been seen elsewhere than in

[006-157]New California; but still, you know, an entomologist is always craving

[006-158]for fresh additions to his collection."

[006-159]While he was speaking, Dingo, leaving little Jack, who was romping with

[006-160]him, came and jumped on Benedict, and began to fawn on him.

[006-161]"Get away, you brute!" he exclaimed, thrusting the dog aside.

[006-162]"Poor Dingo! good dog!" cried Jack, running up and taking the animal's

[006-163]huge head between his tiny hands.

[006-164]"Your interest in cockroaches, Mr. Benedict," observed the captain,

[006-165]"does not seem to extend to dogs."

[006-166]"It isn't that I dislike dogs at all," answered Benedict; "but this

[006-167]creature has disappointed me."

[006-168]"How do you mean? You could hardly want to catalogue him with the

[006-169]diptera or hymenoptera?" asked Mrs Weldon laughingly.

[006-170]"Oh, not at all," replied Benedict, with the most unmoved gravity. "But

[006-171]I understood that he had been found on the West Coast of Africa, and I

[006-172]hoped that perhaps he might have brought over some African hemiptera in

[006-173]his coat; but I have searched his coat well, over and over again,

[006-174]without finding a single specimen. The dog has disappointed me," he

[006-175]repeated mournfully.

[006-176]"I can only hope," said the captain, "that if you had found anything,

[006-177]you were going to kill it instantly."

[006-178]Benedict looked with mute astonishment into the captain's face. In a

[006-179]moment or two afterwards, he said,--

[006-180]"I suppose, sir, you acknowledge that Sir John Franklin was an eminent

[006-181]member of your profession?"

[006-182]"Certainly; why?"

[006-183]"Because Sir John would never take away the life of the most

[006-184]insignificant insect; it is related of him that when he had once been

[006-185]incessantly tormented all day by a mosquito, at last he found it on the

[006-186]back of his hand and blew it off, saying, 'Fly away, little creature,

[006-187]the world is large enough for both you and me!'"

[006-188]"That little anecdote of yours, Mr. Benedict," said the captain,

[006-189]smiling, "is a good deal older than Sir John Franklin. It is told, in

[006-190]nearly the same words, about Uncle Toby, in Sterne's 'Tristram Shandy';

[006-191]only there it was not a mosquito, it was a common fly."

[006-192]"And was Uncle Toby an entomologist?" asked Benedict; "did he ever

[006-193]really live?"

[006-194]"No," said the captain, "he was only a character in a novel."
