# Courage

The quality of mind that enables one to face danger and adversity with resolve, the central moral virtue demonstrated by the protagonists of *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*.

## Definition

Courage is the ability to confront fear, pain, danger, uncertainty, or intimidation. In the novel, courage operates at multiple levels: physical courage in facing storms and wild animals, moral courage in standing up to [[slavery|slave-trade]]rs, and psycho[[ships-log|log]]ical courage in maintaining hope under desperate circumstances.

## Role in the Novel

Courage is the virtue most associated with [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]. The narrator establishes early that "[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] was truly courageous; he was one of the daring" (003-106). [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] affirms that [[dick-sands|Dick]] is "as modest as he is courageous. Yes; he may be trusted" (009-133).

During the storm, [[dick-sands|Dick]]'s courage becomes essential for the entire party: "[[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] could not conceal, and [[dick-sands|Dick]]'s undaunted courage helped her" (014-068). Even as he struggles privately, [[dick-sands|Dick]] "kept up his courage, and did not permit" despair to show (012-021). He makes "the best to keep up his courage, but he could not disguise from" himself the gravity of their situation (014-148).

Courage is presented as contagious and communal. The party resolves to "all do our best to prove our courage, and to show our devotion" (029-108). Yet courage has limits—even [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]], "lost much of her courage, and seemed" near breaking (032-164). The novel acknowledges that courage can be depleted and must be renewed.
