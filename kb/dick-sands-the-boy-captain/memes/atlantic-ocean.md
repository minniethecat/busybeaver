# Atlantic Ocean

The ocean that forms the western boundary of [[africa|Africa]] and serves as the geographical goal of the novel's overland journey. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]'s navigation error — rounding [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] into the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] instead of the Atlantic — sets the entire [[africa|Africa]]n ordeal in motion, and the survivors' eventual escape aims toward the Atlantic coast.

## Geography

- **Type**: Ocean
- **Real or fictional**: Real
- **Relevant to**: Ship voyage, overland trek, escape destination
- **First mentioned**: Chapter 1 (PN 001-209)

## Role in the Narrative

The Atlantic Ocean is both the intended destination of the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*'s original voyage and the geographical anchor of the novel's central tension. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] initially believes the ship is heading toward the Atlantic coast of South America; instead, [[negoro|Negoro]]'s sabotage directs them into the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]]. The narrator notes that "in the depths of the Atlantic and [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]]" the ship had been cast away [001-209], and that the distinction between the two oceans "was nowhere" more critical than in determining their true position [016-082].

When the travelers realize they are in [[africa|Africa]], the Atlantic becomes their destination. [[harris|Harris]]'s geographical lie rested on the impossible claim that they were approaching the Andes "towards the Atlantic" [024-011], when in fact they were deep in central [[africa|Africa]]. The discovery that they had "really entered the Atlantic" watershed [026-058] — marked by rivers flowing west — would have confirmed their location, had they known how to read the signs.

Throughout the overland trek, references to the Atlantic serve as a geographical compass. [[negoro|Negoro]]'s plan involves reaching "the coasts of the Atlantic" [027-030], and historical explorers are noted to have navigated the [[coanza|Coanza]] river until they "sighted the Atlantic" [027-090]. The "lake district" of central [[africa|Africa]] is described as connected "with the Atlantic Ocean" via its river systems [027-142]. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] resolves that he "would" reach the Atlantic [031-031], making it the fixed point of his strategic thinking.

The Atlantic also figures in the novel's thematic geography. The narrator notes that "on this side of the Atlantic" — meaning the eastern, [[africa|Africa]]n side — "the partisans of slavery" still operated [026-139], contrasting the ocean's western shores where the institution had been abolished.

## Key Events at This Location

- **[[navigation|Navigation]] error** — The *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]* is meant to sail the Atlantic but is diverted to the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] [016-082] (PN ref)
- **Geographical discovery** — The travelers realize the rivers flow toward the Atlantic, not the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] [026-058] (PN ref)
- **Escape planning** — [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] resolves to reach the Atlantic coast [031-031] (PN ref)
- **Final destination** — The Atlantic marks the boundary between captivity and freedom

## Description in the Text

Verne contrasts the Atlantic and [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] throughout the novel, using the former as a symbol of home and civilization. The Atlantic coast of [[africa|Africa]], while dangerous (home to [[slavery|slave trade]]rs and hostile terrain), represents the closest point of contact with the civilized world — the ocean across which ships sail to the Americas and Europe.

> Dick Sands resolved he would reach the Atlantic. [031-031]
