[029-001]"Yes, you are right," said Harris; "American-born slaves, with plenty

[029-002]of work in them, are rare articles, and very different to the miserable

[029-003]wretches we get up the country. But you never told me," he added,

[029-004]suddenly changing the subject, "whether you found any money on board

[029-005]the '[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]'!"

[029-006]"Oh, I rescued a few hundred dollars from the wreck, that was all,"

[029-007]said the Portuguese carelessly; "but I am expecting...." he stopped

[029-008]short.

[029-009]"What are you expecting?" inquired Harris eagerly.

[029-010]"Oh, nothing, nothing," said [[negoro|Negoro]], apparently annoyed that he had

[029-011]said so much, and immediately began talking of the means of securing

[029-012]the living prey which he had been taking so many pains to entrap.

[029-013]Harris informed him that on the Coanza, about ten miles distant, there

[029-014]was at the present time encamped a [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]], under the control of

[029-015]an Arab named Ibn Hamish; plenty of native soldiers were there on

[029-016]guard, and if [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] and his people could only be induced to travel

[029-017]in that direction, their capture would be a matter of very little

[029-018]difficulty. He said that of course Dick Sands' first thought would

[029-019]naturally be how to get back to the coast; it was not likely that he

[029-020]would venture a second time through the forest, but would in all

[029-021]probability try to make his way to the nearest river, and descend its

[029-022]course on a raft to the sea. The nearest river was undoubtedly the

[029-023]Coanza, so that he and Negoro might feel quite sure of meeting "their

[029-024]friends" upon its banks.

[029-025]"If you really think so," said Negoro, "there is not much time to be

[029-026]lost; whatever young Sands determines to do, he will do at once: he

[029-027]never lets the grass grow under his feet."

[029-028]"Let us start, then, this very moment, mate," was Harris's reply.

[029-029]Both rose to their feet, when they were startled by the same rustling

[029-030]in the papyrus which had previously aroused Negoro's fears. Presently a

[029-031]low growl was heard, and a large dog, showing his teeth, emerged from

[029-032]the bushes, evidently prepared for an attack.

[029-033]"It's [[dingo|Dingo]]!" exclaimed Harris.

[029-034]"Confound the brute! he shall not escape me this time," said Negoro.

[029-035]He caught up Harris's gun, and raising it to his shoulder, he fired

[029-036]just as the dog was in the act of springing at his throat. A long whine

[029-037]of pain followed the report, and Dingo disappeared again amongst the

[029-038]bushes that fringed the stream. Negoro was instantly upon his track,

[029-039]but could discover nothing beyond a few blood-stains upon the stalks of

[029-040]the papyrus, and a long crimson trail upon the pebbles on the bank.

[029-041]"I think I have done for the beast now," was Negoro's remark as he

[029-042]returned from his fruitless search.

[029-043]Harris, who had been a silent spectator of the whole scene, now asked

[029-044]coolly,--

[029-045]"What makes that animal have such an inveterate dislike to you?"

[029-046]"Oh, there is an old score to settle between us," replied the

[029-047]Portuguese.

[029-048]"What about?" inquired the American.

[029-049]Negoro made no reply, and finding him evidently disinclined to be

[029-050]communicative on the subject, Harris did not press the matter any

[029-051]further.

[029-052]A few moments later the two men were descending the stream, and making

[029-053]their way through the forest towards the Coanza.

[029-055]CHAPTER III.

[029-056]ON THE MARCH AGAIN.

[029-057]"[[africa|Africa]]! Africa!" was the terrible word that echoed and re-echoed in

[029-058]the mind of Dick Sands. As he pondered over the events of the preceding

[029-059]weeks he could now understand why, notwithstanding the rapid progress

[029-060]of the ship, the land seemed ever to be receding, and why the voyage

[029-061]had been prolonged to twice its anticipated length. It remained,

[029-062]however, a mystery inexplicable as before, how and when they had

[029-063]rounded Cape Horn and passed into another ocean. Suddenly the idea

[029-064]flashed upon him that the compass must have been tampered with; and he

[029-065]remembered the fall of the first compass; he recalled the night when he

[029-066]had been roused by Tom's cry of alarm that Negoro had fallen against

[029-067]the binnacle. As he recollected these circumstances he became more and

[029-068]more convinced that it was Negoro who was the mainspring of all the

[029-069]mischief; that it was he who had contrived the loss of the "Pilgrim,"

[029-070]and compromised the safety of all on board.

[029-071]What had been the career, what could be the motives of a man who was

[029-072]capable of such vile machinations?

[029-073]But shrouded in mystery as were the events of the past, the present

[029-074]offered a prospect equally obscure.

[029-075]Beyond the fact that he was in Africa and a hundred miles from the

[029-076]coast, Dick knew absolutely nothing. He could only conjecture that he

[029-077]was in the fatal province of Angola, and assured as he was that Harris

[029-078]had acted the traitor, he was led to the conclusion that he and Negoro

[029-079]had been playing into each other's hands. The result of the collision,

[029-080]he feared, might be very disastrous to the survivors of the "Pilgrim."

[029-081]Yet, in what manner would the odious stratagem be accomplished? Dick

[029-082]could well understand that the negroes would be sold for slaves; he

[029-083]could only too easily imagine that upon himself Negoro would wreak the

[029-084]vengeance he had so obviously been contemplating; but for [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]

[029-085]and the other helpless members of the party what fate could be in store?

[029-086]The situation was terrible, but yet Dick did not flinch; he had been

[029-087]appointed captain, and captain he would remain; Mrs. Weldon and her

[029-088]little son had been committed to his charge, and he was resolved to

[029-089]carry out his trust faithfully to the end.

[029-090]For several hours he remained wrapped in thought, pondering over the

[029-091]present and the future, weighing the evil chances against the good,

[029-092]only to be convinced that the evil much preponderated. At length he

[029-093]rose, firm, resolute, calm. The first glimmer of dawn was breaking upon

[029-094]the forest. All the rest of the party, except Tom, were fast asleep.

[029-095]Dick Sands crept softly up to the old negro, and whispered:--

[029-096]"Tom, you know now where we are!"

[029-097]"Yes, yes, Mr. Dick, only too well I know it. We are in Africa!"

[029-098]The old man sighed mournfully.

[029-099]"Tom," said Dick, in the same low voice, "you must keep this a secret;

[029-100]you must not say a word to let Mrs. Weldon or any of the others know."

[029-101]The old man murmured his assent, and Dick continued:--

[029-102]"It will be quite enough for them to learn that we have been betrayed

[029-103]by Harris, and that we must consequently practise extra care and

[029-104]watchfulness; they will merely think we are taking precautions against

[029-105]being surprised by nomad Indians. I trust to your good sense, Tom, to

[029-106]assist me in this."

[029-107]"You may depend upon me, Mr. Dick; and I can promise you that we will

[029-108]all do our best to prove our courage, and to show our devotion to your

[029-109]service."

[029-110][Illustration: "You must keep this a secret"]

[029-111]Thus assured of Tom's co-operation, Dick proceeded to deliberate upon

[029-112]his future line of action. He had every reason to believe that the

[029-113]treacherous American, startled by the traces of the slaves and the

[029-114]unexpected roaring of the lion, had taken flight before he had

[029-115]conducted his victims to the spot where they were to be attacked, and

[029-116]that consequently some hours might elapse before he would be joined by

[029-117]Negoro, who (to judge from Dingo's strange behaviour) had undoubtedly

[029-118]for the last few days been somewhere on their track.

[029-119]Here was a delay that might be turned to good account, and no time was

[029-120]to be lost in taking advantage of it to commence their return journey

[029-121]to the coast. If, as Dick had every reason to suppose, he was in

[029-122]Angola, he hoped to find, either north or south, some Portuguese

[029-123]settlement whence he could obtain the means of transporting his party

[029-124]to their several homes.

[029-125]But how was this return journey to be accomplished? It would be

[029-126]difficult, not to say imprudent, to retrace their footsteps through the

[029-127]forest; it would merely bring them to their starting-point, and would,

[029-128]moreover, afford an easy track for Negoro or his accomplices to follow.

[029-129]The safest and most secret means of reaching the coast would assuredly

[029-130]be by descending the course of some river. This would have to be

[029-131]effected by constructing a strong raft, from which the little party,

[029-132]well armed, might defend themselves alike from attacks either of the

[029-133]natives or of wild beasts, and which would likewise afford a

[029-134]comfortable means of transport for Mrs. Weldon and her little boy, who

[029-135]were now deprived of the use of Harris's horse. The negroes, it is

[029-136]true, would be only too pleased to carry the lady on a litter of

[029-137]branches, but this would be to occupy the services of two out of five,

[029-138]and under the circumstances it was manifestly advisable that all hands

[029-139]should be free to act on the defensive. Another great inducement

[029-140]towards the plan was that Dick Sands felt himself much more at home in

[029-141]travelling by water than by land, and was longing to be once again upon

[029-142]what to him was, as it were, his native element. He little dreamt that

[029-143]he was devising for himself the very plan that Harris, in his

[029-144]speculations, had laid down for him!

[029-145]The most urgent matter was now to find such a stream as would suit

[029-146]their purpose. Dick had several reasons for feeling sure that one

[029-147]existed in the neighbourhood. He knew that the little river, which fell

[029-148]into the Atlantic near the spot where the "Pilgrim" stranded, could not

[029-149]extend very far either to the north or east, because the horizon was

[029-150]bounded in both directions by the chain of mountains which he had taken

[029-151]for the Cordilleras. If the stream did not rise in those hills it must

[029-152]incline to the south, so that in either case Dick was convinced he

[029-153]could not be long in discovering it or one of its affluents. Another

[029-154]sign, which he recognized as hopeful, was that during the last few

[029-155]miles of the march the soil had become moist and level, whilst here and

[029-156]there the appearance of tiny rivulets indicated that an aqueous network

[029-157]existed in the subsoil. On the previous day, too, the caravan had

[029-158]skirted a rushing torrent, of which the waters were tinged with oxide

[029-159]of iron from its sloping banks.

[029-160]Dick's scheme was to make his way back as far as this stream, which

[029-161]though not navigable itself would in all probability empty itself into

[029-162]some affluent of greater importance. The idea, which he imparted to

[029-163]Tom, met with the old negro's entire approval.

[029-164]As the day dawned the sleepers, one by one, awoke. Mrs. Weldon laid

[029-165]little Jack in Nan's arms. The child was still dozing; the fever had

[029-166]abated, but he looked painfully white and exhausted after the attack.

[029-167]"Dick," said Mrs. Weldon, after looking round her, "where is Mr.

[029-168]Harris? I cannot see him."

[029-169]"Harris has left us," answered Dick very quietly.

[029-170]"Do you mean that he has gone on ahead?"

[029-171]"No, madam, I mean that he has left us, and gone away entirely: he is

[029-172]in league with Negoro."

[029-173]"In league with Negoro!" cried Mrs. Weldon, "Ah, I have had a fancy

[029-174]lately that there has been something wrong: but why? what can be their

[029-175]motive?"

[029-176]"Indeed I am unable to tell you," replied Dick; "I only know that we

[029-177]have no alternative but to return to the coast immediately if we would

[029-178]escape the two rascals."

[029-179][Illustration: "Harris has left us"]

[029-180]"I only wish I could catch them," said Hercules, who had overheard the

[029-181]conversation; "I would soon knock their heads together;" and he shook

[029-182]his two fists in giving emphasis to his words.

[029-183]"But what will become of my boy?" cried Mrs. Weldon, in tones of

[029-184]despondency; "I have been so sanguine in procuring him the comforts of

[029-185]San Felice."
