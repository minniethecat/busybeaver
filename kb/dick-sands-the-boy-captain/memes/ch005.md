[005-001]"The poor wretch is dying of thirst!" said [[dick-sands|Dick]].

[005-002]It soon appeared that [[dingo|the dog]] was very far from being engrossed with

[005-003]its own interests. The boat was being pushed back a few yards in order

[005-004]to allow the captain to ascertain the most convenient place to get

[005-005]alongside the "Waldeck," when the creature seized Dick by the jacket,

[005-006]and set up a howl that was almost human in its piteousness. It was

[005-007]evidently in a state of alarm that the boat was not going to return to

[005-008]the wreck. The dog's meaning could not be misunderstood. The boat was

[005-009]accordingly brought against the larboard side of the vessel, and while

[005-010]the two sailors lashed her securely to the "Waldeck's" cat-head,

[005-011][[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] and Dick, with the dog persistently accompanying them,

[005-012]clambered, after some difficulty, to the open hatchway between the

[005-013]stumps of the masts, and made their way into the hold. It was half full

[005-014]of water, but perfectly destitute of cargo, its sole contents being the

[005-015]ballast sand which had slipped to larboard, and now served to keep the

[005-016]vessel on her side.

[005-017]One glance was sufficient to convince the captain that there was no

[005-018]salvage to be effected.

[005-019]"There is nothing here; nobody here," he said.

[005-020]"So I see," said the apprentice, who had made his way to the extreme

[005-021]fore-part of the hold.

[005-022]"Then we have only to go up again," remarked the captain.

[005-023]They ascended the ladder, but no sooner did they reappear upon the deck

[005-024]than the dog, barking irrepressibly, began trying manifestly to drag

[005-025]them towards the stern.

[005-026]Yielding to what might be called the importunities of the dog, they

[005-027]followed him to the poop, and there, by the dim glimmer admitted by the

[005-028]sky-light, Captain Hull made out the forms of five bodies, motionless

[005-029]and apparently lifeless, stretched upon the floor.

[005-030]One after another, Dick hastily examined them all, and emphatically

[005-031]declared it to be his opinion, that not one or them had actually ceased

[005-032]to breathe; whereupon the captain did not lose a minute in summoning

[005-033]the two sailors to his aid, and although it was far from an easy task,

[005-034]he succeeded in getting the five unconscious men, who were all negroes,

[005-035]conveyed safely to the boat.

[005-036]The dog followed, apparently satisfied.

[005-037]With all possible speed the boat made its way back again to the

[005-038]"[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]," a girt-line was lowered from the mainyard, and the

[005-039]unfortunate men were raised to the deck.

[005-040]"Poor things!" said [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]], as she looked compassionately on the

[005-041]motionless forms.

[005-042]"But they are not dead," cried Dick eagerly; "they are not dead; we

[005-043]shall save them all yet!"

[005-044]"What's the matter with them?" asked Cousin Benedict, looking at them

[005-045]with utter bewilderment.

[005-046]"We shall hear all about them soon, I dare say," said the captain,

[005-047]smiling; "but first we will give them a few drops of rum in some water."

[005-048]Cousin Benedict smiled in return.

[005-049]"[[negoro|Negoro]]!" shouted the captain.

[005-050]At the sound of the name, the dog, who had hitherto been quite passive,

[005-051]growled fiercely, showed his teeth, and exhibited every sign of rage.

[005-052]The cook did not answer.

[005-053]"Negoro!" again the captain shouted, and the dog became yet more angry.

[005-054]At this second summons Negoro slowly left his kitchen, but no sooner

[005-055]had he shown his face upon the deck than the animal made a rush at him,

[005-056]and would unquestionably have seized him by the throat if the man had

[005-057]not knocked him back with a poker which he had brought with him in his

[005-058]hand.

[005-059]The infuriated beast was secured by the sailors, and prevented from

[005-060]inflicting any serious injury.

[005-061]"Do you know this dog?" asked the captain.

[005-062]"Know him? Not I! I have never set eyes on the brute in my life."

[005-063]"Strange!" muttered Dick to himself; "there is some mystery here. We

[005-064]shall see."

[005-065]CHAPTER IV.

[005-066]THE SURVIVORS OF THE "WALDECK."

[005-067]In spite of the watchfulness of the French and English cruisers, there

[005-068]is no doubt that the [[slavery|slave-trade]] is still extensively carried on in all

[005-069]parts of equatorial [[africa|Africa]], and that year after year vessels loaded

[005-070]with slaves leave the coasts of Angola and Mozambique to transport

[005-071]their living freight to many quarters even of the civilized world.

[005-072]Of this Captain Hull was well aware, and although he was now in a

[005-073]latitude which was comparatively little traversed by such slavers, he

[005-074]could not help almost involuntarily conjecturing that the negroes they

[005-075]had just found must be part of a slave-cargo which was on its way to

[005-076]some colony of the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]]; if this were so, he would at least have the

[005-077]satisfaction of announcing to them that they had regained their freedom

[005-078]from the moment that they came on board the "Pilgrim."

[005-079]Whilst these thoughts were passing through his mind, Mrs. Weldon,

[005-080]assisted by Nan and the ever active Dick Sands, was doing everything in

[005-081]her power to restore consciousness to the poor sufferers. The judicious

[005-082]administration of fresh water and a limited quantity of food soon had

[005-083]the effect of making them revive; and when they were restored to their

[005-084]senses it was found that the eldest of them, a man of about sixty years

[005-085]of age, who immediately regained his powers of speech, was able to

[005-086]reply in good English to all the questions that were put to him. In

[005-087]answer to Captain Hull's inquiry whether they were not slaves, the old

[005-088]negro proudly stated that he and his companions were all free American

[005-089]citizens, belonging to the state of Pennsylvania.

[005-090][Illustration: Mrs. Weldon, assisted by Nan and the ever active Dick

[005-091]Sands, was doing everything in her power to restore consciousness to

[005-092]the poor sufferers.]

[005-093]"Then, let me assure you, my friend," said the captain, "you have by no

[005-094]means compromised your [[freedom|liberty]] in having been brought on board the

[005-095]American schooner 'Pilgrim.'"

[005-096]Not merely, as it seemed, on account of his age and experience, but

[005-097]rather because of a certain superiority and greater energy of

[005-098]character, this old man was tacitly recognized as the spokesman of his

[005-099]party; he freely communicated all the information that Captain Hull

[005-100]required to hear, and by degrees he related all the details of his

[005-101]adventures.

[005-102]He said that his name was Tom, and that when he was only six years of

[005-103]age he had been sold as a slave, and brought from his home in Africa to

[005-104]the United States; but by the act of [[abolitionism|emancipation]] he had long since

[005-105]recovered his freedom. His companions, who were all much younger than

[005-106]himself, their ages ranging from twenty-five to thirty, were all

[005-107]free-born, their parents having been emancipated before their birth, so

[005-108]that no white man had ever exercised upon them the rights of ownership.

[005-109]One of them was his own son; his name was Bat (an abbreviation of

[005-110]Bartholomew); and there were three others, named Austin, Actæon, and

[005-111]Hercules. All four of them were specimens of that stalwart race that

[005-112]commands so high a price in the African market, and in spite of the

[005-113]emaciation induced by their recent sufferings, their muscular,

[005-114]well-knit frames betokened a strong and healthy constitution. Their

[005-115]manner bore the impress of that solid education which is given in the

[005-116]North American schools, and their speech had lost all trace of the

[005-117]"nigger-tongue," a dialect without articles or inflexions, which since

[005-118]the anti-slavery war has almost died out in the United States.

[005-119]Three years ago, old Tom stated, the five men had been engaged by an

[005-120]Englishman who had large property in South Australia, to work upon his

[005-121]estates near Melbourne. Here they had realized a considerable profit,

[005-122]and upon the completion of their engagement they determined to return

[005-123]with their savings to America. Accordingly, on the 5th of January,

[005-124]after paying their passage in the ordinary way, they embarked at

[005-125]Melbourne on board the "Waldeck." Everything went on well for seventeen

[005-126]days, until, on the night of the 22nd, which was very dark, they were

[005-127]run into by a great steamer. They were all asleep in their berths, but,

[005-128]roused by the shock of the collision, which was extremely severe, they

[005-129]hurriedly made their way on to the deck. The scene was terrible; both

[005-130]masts were gone, and the brig, although the water had not absolutely

[005-131]flooded her hold so as to make her sink, had completely heeled over on

[005-132]her side. Captain and crew had entirely disappeared, some probably

[005-133]having been dashed into the sea, others perhaps having saved themselves

[005-134]by clinging to the rigging of the ship which had fouled them, and which

[005-135]could be distinguished through the darkness rapidly receding in the

[005-136]distance. For a while they were paralyzed, but they soon awoke to the

[005-137]conviction that they were left alone upon a half-capsized and disabled

[005-138]hull, twelve hundred miles from the nearest land. Mrs. Weldon was loud

[005-139]in her expression of indignation that any captain should have the

[005-140]barbarity to abandon an unfortunate vessel with which his own

[005-141]carelessness had brought him into collision. It would be bad enough,

[005-142]she said for a driver on a public road, when it might be presumed that

[005-143]help would be forthcoming, to pass on unconcerned after causing an

[005-144]accident to another vehicle; but how much more shameful to desert the

[005-145]injured on the open sea, where the victims of his incompetence could

[005-146]have no chance of obtaining succour! Captain Hull could only repeat

[005-147]what he had said before, that incredibly atrocious as it might seem,

[005-148]such inhumanity was far from rare.

[005-149]On resuming his story, Tom said that he and his companions soon found

[005-150]that they had no means left for getting away from the capsized brig;

[005-151]both the boats had been crushed in the collision, so that they had no

[005-152]alternative except to await the appearance of a passing vessel, whilst

[005-153]the wreck was drifting hopelessly along under the action of the

[005-154]currents. This accounted for the fact of their being found so far south

[005-155]of their proper course.

[005-156]For the next ten days the negroes had subsisted upon a few scraps of

[005-157]food that they found in the stern cabin; but as the store room was

[005-158]entirely under water, they were quite unable to obtain a drop of

[005-159]anything to drink, and the freshwater tanks that had been lashed to the

[005-160]deck had been stove in at the time of the catastrophe. Tortured with

[005-161]thirst, the poor men had suffered agonies, and having on the previous

[005-162]night entirely lost consciousness, they must soon have died if the

[005-163]"Pilgrim's" timely arrival had not effected their rescue.

[005-164]All the outlines of Tom's narrative were fully confirmed by the other

[005-165]negroes; Captain Hull could see no reason to doubt it; indeed, the

[005-166]facts seemed to speak for themselves.

[005-167]One other survivor of the wreck, if he had been gifted with the power

[005-168]of speech, would doubtless have corroborated the testimony. This was

[005-169]the dog who seemed to have such an unaccountable dislike to Negoro.

[005-170]Dingo, as the dog was named, belonged to the fine breed of mastiffs

[005-171]peculiar to New Holland. It was not, however, from Australia, but from

[005-172]the coast of West Africa, near the mouth of the Congo, that the animal

[005-173]had come. He had been picked up there, two years previously, by the

[005-174]captain of the "Waldeck," who had found him wandering about and more

[005-175]than half starved. The initials S. V. engraved upon his collar were the

[005-176]only tokens that the dog had a past history of his own. After he had

[005-177]been taken on board the "Waldeck," he remained quite unsociable,

[005-178]apparently ever pining for some lost master, whom he had failed to find

[005-179]in the desert land where he had been met with.

[005-180]Larger than the dogs of the Pyrenees, Dingo was a magnificent example

[005-181]of his kind. Standing on his hind legs, with his head thrown back, he

[005-182]was as tall as a man. His agility and strength would have made him a

[005-183]sure match for a panther, and he would not have flinched at facing a

[005-184]bear. His fine shaggy coat was a dark tawny colour, shading off

[005-185]somewhat lighter round the muzzle, and his long bushy tail was as

[005-186]strong as a lion's. If he were made angry, no doubt he might become a

[005-187]most formidable foe, so that it was no wonder that Negoro did not feel

[005-188]altogether gratified at his reception.
