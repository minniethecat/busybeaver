# Nan

**Nan** is an elderly [[africa|Africa]]n American servant who has long served the [[mrs-weldon|Weldon]] household. She accompanies [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] and her young son [[jack-weldon|Jack]] on the ill-fated voyage of [[the-pilgrim|the *Pilgrim*]], caring for the child from the deck of the schooner through shipwreck and into the [[africa|Africa]]n interior. Her presence provides continuity, comfort, and an embodiment of quiet, uncomplaining loyalty throughout the ordeal.

## Role & Identity

- **Role**: Domestic servant and caregiver to [[jack-weldon|Jack Weldon]]
- **Affiliation**: [[mrs-weldon|Weldon]] household, [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]
- **First appearance**: Chapter 2 (002-030)
- **Last appearance**: Mentioned throughout the [[africa|Africa]]n journey

## Character Description

Nan is described as "an old negress" (002-030) who has been a faithful domestic servant to the [[mrs-weldon|Weldon]] family for many years. Her age and experience make her a stabilizing presence amid the chaos of the voyage, and her devotion to young Jack is absolute. Verne portrays her without condescension — she is simply one of the ship's company, sharing equally in its trials.

She shares the stern cabin with [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] and [[jack-weldon|Jack]] during the voyage: "[[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] was installed with Jack and old Nan" (003-032). Throughout the crossing, she assists [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] with domestic duties, frequently working alongside [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]], who was "ever active" in helping: "[[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] assisted by Nan and the ever active [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]" (001-077).

## During the Voyage

When a violent storm strikes the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*, Nan shares in the general alarm: "Jack was crying bitterly; and [[dick-sands|Dick]], old Nan" were among those affected by the terror of the [[storm-at-sea|gale]] (011-122). Despite her age, she proves willing to help in any way she can — "old Nan was equally ready to take" on whatever tasks the emergency demanded (013-105).

When the ship is in danger of foundering, [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] ensures that "Jack, [[benedict|Cousin Benedict]], and Nan were provided with life belts" (018-099). [[hercules|Hercules]]'s companion [[actaeon|Actæon]] "promised to look after Nan" during the crisis (018-105), recognizing that the elderly woman would need protection in the event of abandoning ship.

## In Africa

After the shipwreck on the [[africa|Africa]]n coast, Nan continues her role as Jack's caregiver without interruption. When the boy awakes from sleep, "Jack, followed by Nan, came out of" the improvised shelter (021-022). She remains constantly at the child's side: "[[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] having gently laid him on Nan's lap" (019-163).

During the grueling trek through the jungle, Nan travels alongside [[tom|Tom]] and the other survivors, enduring the same privations. She is seen resting "at the side of old Nan" as [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] watches over her little boy (023-109). As an elderly woman subjected to the same brutal march as the younger survivors, her [[courage|fortitude]] is remarkable.

## Fate

Nan survives the [[africa|Africa]]n ordeal and, it is implied, returns with the [[mrs-weldon|Weldon]] family to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]. Her fate is not individually dramatized — she simply endures, as she has always done, her presence a quiet constant through the narrative's storms.

## Significance

Though a minor character, Nan represents several important themes. Her steadfast care for Jack embodies the novel's valuation of loyalty and service. Her age — she is the oldest of the survivors — makes her endurance a testament to human resilience. And her presence among the rescued [[africa|Africa]]ns — herself an [[africa|Africa]]n American who has known servitude — creates a subtle bridge between the American domestic world and the [[africa|Africa]]n world of the [[slavery|slave trade]] that the novel condemns.

> "Mrs. Weldon assisted by Nan and the ever active Dick Sands" (001-077) — a line that captures Nan's role: always present, always helping, never the focus of attention but essential to the fabric of survival.
