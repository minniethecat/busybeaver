# Parrot

Multiple species of parrots feature in the novel, with the grey parrot (*Psittacus erithacus*) appearing most prominently as a noisy, quarrelsome colony that disturbs the travelers' passage through the [[africa|Africa]]n forest.

## Overview

- **Type**: Species
- **Species**: *Psittacus erithacus* (grey parrot), including red-tailed variety
- **Owner/Associated with**: None (wild birds)
- **Chapters**: 7, 8, 23, 24, 45

## Role in the Narrative

Parrots appear in two distinct contexts. Early in the novel, before the [[africa|Africa]]n setting is revealed, sailors discuss the talking abilities of parrots in passing conversation aboard the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*. One sailor notes that "parrots talk, and magpies" too, reflecting a common seafaring observation about the birds often kept as shipboard pets [007-184]. A second reference invokes "the old Cardinal's parrot" as an anecdote about avian mimicry [008-028].

The main appearance of parrots comes in Chapter 23, during the overland trek through the [[africa|Africa]]n forest. A "colony of grey parrots" is described as "noisy, quarrelsome, and restless" — their discordant outcry filling the forest and disturbing the travelers [023-041][023-053]. When the parrots finally fall silent, it is "to the general relief" of the party [023-060].

[[benedict|Cousin Benedict]], the entomo[[ships-log|log]]ist, becomes distracted by the parrots in Chapter 24, inquiring "about the beautiful parrots which he had been" observing [024-039]. This moment of scientific curiosity — aimed at birds rather than [[entomology|insects]] — shows [[benedict|Benedict]]'s easily diverted attention. Later, "the common grey parrots" are noted as abundant in the region [024-046].

In the novel's closing chapters, parrots reappear as part of the forest's rich avian life, "grey parrots, with bright red tails" calling alongside other birds with "tuneful song" [045-109].

## Description in the Text

Verne distinguishes the grey parrot from other species, noting both the "common grey parrots" [024-046] and the "grey parrots, with bright red tails" — a description matching the [[africa|Africa]]n grey parrot's distinctive crimson tail feathers [045-109]. The birds are characterized primarily by their vocal behavior: noisy, quarrelsome, and given to discordant collective outcries.

> A colony of grey parrots, a noisy, quarrelsome, and restless tribe. [023-041]

## Historical/Natural History Context

The [[africa|Africa]]n grey parrot (*Psittacus erithacus*) was well-known in Europe by 1878, having been traded as a pet since antiquity. Their remarkable mimicry abilities — the basis of the sailors' conversation and the Cardinal's parrot anecdote — were legendary. Grey parrots were frequently kept aboard ships as mascots and companions, making them a natural topic of conversation among Verne's sailors. The species' range across central and western [[africa|Africa]] matches the novel's setting precisely.
