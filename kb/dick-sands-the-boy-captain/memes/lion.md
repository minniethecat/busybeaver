The lion is one of the most feared predators encountered by the party during their trek across [[angola|Angola]]. Its roar, heard in the night, signals the transition from maritime danger to the terrestrial perils of the [[africa|Africa]]n interior — a world where humans are no longer the dominant species.

## In the Narrative

The party's first encounter with a lion occurs in the [[africa|Africa]]n forest, where [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] and his companions are traveling after the wreck of the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]. The encounter is sudden and terrifying:

> "A lion! a lion!" he shouted. (026-045)

The lion's roar that "had disturbed the" party (026-065) serves as a visceral reminder that they have entered a realm governed by different rules than the sea. On the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]], the danger was human — [[negoro|Negoro]]'s treachery. In [[africa|Africa]], the danger is primal and immediate.

## Context

Jules Verne uses the lion to dramatize the vulnerability of the European characters in the [[africa|Africa]]n interior. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]], who proved himself capable at sea, must now contend with threats for which [[navigation|seamanship]] provides no preparation. The lion serves as a narrative device that equalizes the party — no amount of courage or intelligence can face down an apex predator without weapons.

## Significance

The lion encounter is one of several moments that reinforce the novel's theme of human vulnerability in nature. Where other 19th-century adventure novels might glorify the hunting of big game, Verne presents the lion as a genuine threat that must be evaded rather than conquered. The animal is not a trophy but a force that underscores how far the party is from the civilized safety of [[san-francisco|San Francisco]].
