[013-001]The manoeuvre was simple enough. The main-sheet was gradually

[013-002]slackened, the great sail took the wind and added its powerful action

[013-003]to that of the fore-sails. The main-top-sail was next brought to bear;

[013-004]it was only clewed up, so that there was nothing to do except to pull

[013-005]the halyards, haul it aboard the tack, and unfurl it. But in pulling at

[013-006]the halyards the muscular energy of Hercules, which was supplemented by

[013-007]that of Actæon, not to forget little Jack, who had volunteered his

[013-008]assistance, proved to be overpowering, and the rope snapped in two. All

[013-009]three of them, of course, fell flat upon the deck; but fortunately

[013-010]neither of them was hurt, and Jack laughed heartily at his tumble as an

[013-011]excellent joke.

[013-012]"Up with you!" cried [[captain-hull|Captain]] [[dick-sands|Dick]]; "there's no harm done; splice the

[013-013]rope, and haul away more gently next time."

[013-014]It took but a few minutes to execute the order, and the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" was

[013-015]soon sailing away rapidly with her head to the east.

[013-016]"Well done, my friends!" said Dick, who had not left his post at the

[013-017]helm; "you will be first-rate sailors before the end of the voyage."

[013-018]"We shall do our best, I promise you, Captain Sands," replied Tom,

[013-019]making it a point to give the young commander his proper title.

[013-020][[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] also congratulated the new crew upon the success of their

[013-021]first attempt.

[013-022]"I believe it was Master Jack who broke that rope," said Hercules, with

[013-023]a sly twinkle in his eye; "he is very strong, I can tell you."

[013-024]Jack looked as though he thoroughly appreciated the compliment, and

[013-025]evidenced his satisfaction by giving his huge friend a hearty shake of

[013-026]the hand.

[013-027]There were still several sails that were not yet set. Running well

[013-028]before the wind as the "Pilgrim" was, Dick nevertheless felt that the

[013-029]gallant, royal, and stay-sails, if brought into service, would

[013-030]materially assist her progress, and he determined not to dispense with

[013-031]their help. The stay-sails could be hoisted from below, but to bring

[013-032]the gallant and royal into play demanded more experience than any of

[013-033]his crew had had. Knowing that he could not entrust the task to them,

[013-034]and yet resolved not to be baulked of his wish to set them, he

[013-035]undertook the task himself. He first put Tom to the helm, showing him

[013-036]how to keep the schooner's head in the right direction, and having

[013-037]placed the other four at the royal and top-gallant halyards, proceeded

[013-038]to mount the foremast.

[013-039][Illustration: All three of them fell flat upon the deck.]

[013-040]To clamber up the foreshrouds and the top-shrouds on to the cross-trees

[013-041]was mere child's play to the active apprentice. In a few minutes he had

[013-042]unfurled the top-gallant-sail, mounted to the royal-yard, unfurled the

[013-043]royal, again reached the cross-trees, and having caught hold of one of

[013-044]the starboard backstays, had descended to the deck; there he gave the

[013-045]necessary directions, and the two sails were made fast, and both yards

[013-046]braced.

[013-047]Nor did this content him. The stay-sails were set between the masts,

[013-048]and thus the "Pilgrim" was running along, crowded to the full, with all

[013-049]her canvas. The only additional sails which Dick could possibly have

[013-050]employed would have been some studding-sails to larboard, but as the

[013-051]setting of these was a matter of some difficulty, and they were not

[013-052]always readily struck in the case of a sudden squall, he contented

[013-053]himself without them.

[013-054]Again he took his place at the helm. The breeze was manifestly

[013-055]freshening, and the "Pilgrim," almost imperceptibly heeling to

[013-056]starboard, glided rapidly along the surface of the water, leaving

[013-057]behind her a wake, smooth and clean, that bore plain witness to the

[013-058]true adjustment of her water-line.

[013-059]"This is good progress, Mrs. Weldon," he said; "may Heaven grant the

[013-060]wind and weather may continue thus favourable!"

[013-061]The lady, in silence, shook the boy's hand; and then, worn-out with the

[013-062]excitement of the past hours, went to her cabin, where she lay down and

[013-063]fell into a troubled doze.

[013-064]The new crew remained on watch. They were stationed on the forecastle,

[013-065]in readiness to make any alteration which the sails might require, but

[013-066]the wind was so steady and unshifting that no need arose for their

[013-067]services.

[013-068]And Cousin Benedict? all this time, where was he? and what had he been

[013-069]doing?

[013-070]He was sitting in his cabin; he had a magnifying-glass in his hand and

[013-071]was studying an articulata of the order orthoptera, an insect of the

[013-072]Blattidae family; its characteristics are a roundish body, rather long

[013-073]wings, flat elytra, and a head hidden by the prothorax. He had been on

[013-074]deck at the time of the calamity; the ill-fated captain with the crew

[013-075]had been drowned before his very eyes; but he said nothing; not that he

[013-076]was unmoved; to think that he was not struck with horror would be to

[013-077]libel his kind and pitying nature. His sympathy was aroused, especially

[013-078]for his cousin; he pressed her hand warmly as if he would assure her of

[013-079]his truest commiseration; but he said nothing; he hurried off towards

[013-080]his cabin; and who shall deny that it was to devise some wonderfully

[013-081]energetic measures that he would take in consequence of this melancholy

[013-082]event?

[013-083]Passing the kitchen, however, he caught sight of [[negoro|Negoro]] in the act of

[013-084]crushing a blatta, an American species of cockroach. He broke out into

[013-085]a storm of invective, and in tones of indignation demanded the

[013-086]surrender of the insect, which Negoro made with cool contempt. In a

[013-087]moment Captain Hull and his partners in death were all forgotten; the

[013-088]enthusiast had secured a prize with which he hastened to his own little

[013-089]compartment, where he was soon absorbed in proving to his own

[013-090]satisfaction, in opposition to the opinion of other entomologists, that

[013-091]the blattae of the phoraspous species, which are remarkable for their

[013-092]colours, differ in their habits from blattae of the ordinary sort.

[013-093]For the remainder of the day perfect order reigned on board the

[013-094]"Pilgrim." Though they were unable to shake off the sickening feeling

[013-095]of horror roused by the frightful disaster, and felt that they had

[013-096]sustained a startling shock, all the passengers seemed mechanically to

[013-097]fall into their usual routine. Dick Sands, though avowedly at the

[013-098]wheel, seemed to be everywhere, with an eye for every thing, and his

[013-099]amateur crew obeyed him readily, and with the promptness of a willing

[013-100]activity.

[013-101]Negoro made no further overt attempt to question the young captain's

[013-102]authority, but remained shut up in his kitchen. Dick made no secret of

[013-103]his determination to place the cook in close confinement if he

[013-104]exhibited any future sign of insubordination. Hercules was ready to

[013-105]carry him off bodily to the hold, and old Nan was equally ready to take

[013-106]his place in the cooking department. Probably Negoro was aware of all

[013-107]this; at any rate he did not seem disposed to give any further cause of

[013-108]offence at present.

[013-109][Illustration: Jack evidenced his satisfaction by giving his huge

[013-110]friend a hearty shake of the hand.]

[013-111]As the day advanced the wind continued to freshen; but no shifting of

[013-112]the sails seemed necessary. The "Pilgrim" was running well. There was

[013-113]no need to diminish her spread of canvas. Masts as solid and rigging as

[013-114]strong as hers could stand a far heavier breeze.

[013-115]As a general rule, it is deemed prudent in case of a squall to shorten

[013-116]sail at night, and especially to take in gallants and royal; but the

[013-117]weather prospects now were all so promising and satisfactory that Dick

[013-118]persuaded himself he was under no necessity to take this precaution; he

[013-119]rather felt himself bound to take the strongest measures he could to

[013-120]expedite his reaching less unfrequented waters. He made up his mind,

[013-121]however, not to leave the deck at all that night.

[013-122]The young captain made every effort to get an approximate reckoning of

[013-123]the schooner's progress. He heaved [[ships-log|the log]] every half-hour and duly

[013-124]registered the result of each successive examination. There were two

[013-125]compasses on board; one in the binnacle, close under the eye of the

[013-126]helmsman, the other, an inverted compass, being attached to the rafters

[013-127]of the captain's cabin, so that without leaving his berth he could see

[013-128]whether the man in charge of the wheel was holding a proper course.

[013-129]Every vessel that is duly furnished for a lengthened voyage has always

[013-130]not only two compasses but two chronometers, one to correct the other.

[013-131]The "Pilgrim" was not deficient in this respect, and Dick Sands made a

[013-132]strong point of admonishing his crew that they should take especial

[013-133]care of the compasses, which under their present circumstances were of

[013-134]such supreme importance.

[013-135]A misfortune, however, was in store for them. On the night of the 12th,

[013-136]while Dick was on watch, the compass in the cabin became detached from

[013-137]its fastening and fell on the floor. The accident was not discovered

[013-138]until the following morning. Whether the metal ferule that had attached

[013-139]the instrument to the rafters had become rusty, or whether it had been

[013-140]worn away by additional friction it seemed impossible to settle. All

[013-141]that could be said was that the compass was broken beyond repair. Dick

[013-142]was extremely grieved at the loss; but he did not consider that any one

[013-143]was to be blamed for the mishap, and could only resolve for the future

[013-144]to take extra care of the compass in the binnacle.

[013-145]With the exception of this _contretemps_, everything appeared to go on

[013-146]satisfactorily on board. Mrs. Weldon, reassured by Dick's confidence,

[013-147]had regained much of her wonted calmness, and was besides ever

[013-148]supported by a sincere religious spirit. She and Dick had many a long

[013-149]conversation together. The ingenuous lad was always ready to take the

[013-150]kind and intelligent lady into his counsel, and day by day would point

[013-151]out to her on the chart the registers he made as the result of his dead

[013-152]reckoning; he would then try and satisfy her that under the prevailing

[013-153]wind there could be no doubt they must arrive at the coast of South

[013-154]America: moreover, he said that, unless he was much mistaken, they

[013-155]should sight the land at no great distance from Valparaiso.

[013-156]Mrs. Weldon had, in truth, no reason to question the correctness of

[013-157]Dick's representations; she owned that provided the wind remained in

[013-158]the same favourable quarter, there was every prospect of their reaching

[013-159]land in safety; nevertheless at times she could not resist the

[013-160]misgiving that would arise when she contemplated what might be the

[013-161]result of a change of wind or a breaking of the weather.

[013-162]With the light-heartedness that belonged to his age, Jack soon fell

[013-163]back into his accustomed pursuits, and was to be seen merrily running

[013-164]over the deck or romping with [[dingo|Dingo]]. At times, it is true, he missed

[013-165]the companionship of Dick; but his mother made him comprehend that now

[013-166]that Dick, was captain, his time was too much occupied to allow him;

[013-167]any leisure for play, and the child quite understood that he must not

[013-168]interrupt his old friend in his new duties.

[013-169][Illustration: A light shadow glided stealthily along the deck.]

[013-170]The negroes performed their work with intelligence, and seemed to make

[013-171]rapid progress in the art of [[navigation|seamanship]]. Tom had been unanimously

[013-172]appointed boatswain, and took one watch with Bat and Austin, the

[013-173]alternate watch being discharged by Dick himself with Hercules and

[013-174]Actæon. One of them steered so that the other two were free to watch at

[013-175]the bows. As a general rule Dick Sands managed to remain at the wheel

[013-176]all night; five or six hours' sleep in the daytime sufficed for him,

[013-177]and during the time when he was lying down he entrusted the wheel to

[013-178]Tom or Bat, who under his instructions had become very fair helmsmen.

[013-179]Although in these unfrequented waters there was little chance of

[013-180]running foul of any other vessel, Dick invariably took the precaution

[013-181]of lighting his signals, carrying a green light to starboard and a red

[013-182]light to port. His exertions, however, were a great strain upon him,

[013-183]and sometimes during the night his fatigue would induce a heavy

[013-184]drowsiness, and he steered, as it were, by instinct more than by

[013-185]attention.
