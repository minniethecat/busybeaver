# Monkey

Various species of monkeys feature in the novel's [[africa|Africa]]n forest passages, providing both comic relief (the chant-like howling of the "paternoster" chorus) and vivid similes for human appearance.

## Overview

- **Type**: Species (multiple varieties)
- **Species**: Howler monkeys (*Alouatta* sp.) and smaller species
- **Owner/Associated with**: None (wild animals)
- **Chapters**: 23, 40, 46, 48

## Role in the Narrative

Monkeys serve two distinct functions in the novel. First, they are part of the rich ambient soundscape of the [[africa|Africa]]n forest. The howler-monkey chorus of Chapter 23 — described as a "paternoster" chant — provides a moment of almost comic relief amid the tension of the journey [023-128]. The younger monkeys "join in" with the deeper voices, creating a layered, call-and-response sound [023-138].

Second, monkey characteristics are used as a vivid, unflattering simile for human appearance. A character is described as looking "like an old, decrepit monkey," with simian features used to convey age, cunning, and physical deterioration [040-012]. This visual comparison recurs when another group imitates movements "like monkeys" [046-159].

In the lighter moments of Chapter 48, a "group of monkeys" provides entertainment, described as "the young captain's great delight" [048-050]. These smaller monkeys are contrasted with the larger howler species.

## Description in the Text

Verne distinguishes between different monkey types encountered. The howler monkeys produce a "less than melodious" sound, performed as an "ordinary greeting" to travelers passing through their territory [023-128]. Their chant has an almost liturgical quality — the text calls it the chant of the monkey "paternosters," a comparison to the Lord's Prayer that lends the forest a cathedral-like quality [023-145].

Smaller monkeys appear later, distinguished as "belonging to a smaller kind" observed for their "weight" and size [048-053].

> "Less than melodious, performed by monkeys, as their ordinary greeting." [023-128]

## Historical/Natural History Context

By 1878, European naturalists had documented numerous [[africa|Africa]]n primate species, though live specimens were still exotic. Verne's howler-monkey chorus draws on explorer accounts of the haunting vocalizations that could be heard for miles through the forest canopy. The distinction between howler monkeys and smaller species shows Verne's characteristic attention to natural-history detail, even in a work of adventure fiction.
