[044-001][Illustration: Dr. Livingstone. _Page_ 408.]

[044-002]His next project was to cross the continent obliquely from south to

[044-003]west, but in this expedition he had resolved that he would risk no life

[044-004]but his own. Accompanied, therefore, by only a few natives, he started

[044-005]in the following June, and skirting the Kalahari desert entered

[044-006]Litoubarouba on the last day of the year; here he found the Bechuana

[044-007]district much ravaged by the Boers, the original Dutch colonists, who

[044-008]had formed the population of the Cape before it came into the

[044-009]possession of the English. After a fortnight's stay, he proceeded into

[044-010]the heart of the district of the Bamangonatos, and travelled

[044-011]continuously until the 23rd of May, when he arrived at Linyanté, and

[044-012]was received with much honour by Sekeletoo, who had recently become

[044-013]sovereign of the Makalolos. A severe attack of fever detained the

[044-014]traveller here for a period, but he made good use of the enforced rest

[044-015]by studying the manners of the country, and became for the first time

[044-016]sensible of its terrible sufferings in consequence of the [[slavery|slave-trade]].

[044-017]Descending the course of the Chobé to the Zambesi, he next entered

[044-018]Naniele, and after visiting Katonga and Libonta, advanced to the point

[044-019]of confluence of the Leeba with the Zambesi, where he determined upon

[044-020]ascending the former as far as the Portuguese possessions in the west;

[044-021]it was an undertaking, however, that required considerable preparation,

[044-022]so that it was necessary for him to return to Linyanté.

[044-023]On the 11th of November he again started. He was accompanied by

[044-024]twenty-seven Makalolos, and ascended the Leeba till, in the territory

[044-025]of the Balonda, he reached a spot where it received the waters of its

[044-026]tributary the Makondo.

[044-027]It was the first time a white man had ever penetrated so far.

[044-028]Proceeding on their way, they arrived at the residence of Shinté, the

[044-029]most powerful of the chieftains of the Balonda, by whom they were well

[044-030]received, and having met with equal kindness from Kateema, a ruler on

[044-031]the other side of the Leeba, they encamped, on the 20th of February,

[044-032]1853, on the banks of Lake Dilolo.

[044-033]Here it was that the real difficulty commenced; the arduous travelling,

[044-034]the attacks of the natives, and their exorbitant demands, the

[044-035]conspiracies of his own attendants and their desertions, would soon

[044-036]have caused any one of less energy to abandon his enterprise; but David

[044-037]Livingstone was not a man to be daunted; resolutely he persevered, and

[044-038]on the 4th of April reached the banks of the Coango, the stream that

[044-039]forms the frontier of the Portuguese possessions, and joins the Zaire

[044-040]on the north.

[044-041]Six days later he passed through Cassangé. Here it was that Alvez had

[044-042]seen him. On the 31st of May he arrived at St. Paul de Loanda, having

[044-043]traversed the continent in about two years.

[044-044]It was not long, however, before he was off again. Following the banks

[044-045]of the Coanza, the river which was to bring such trying experiences to

[044-046][[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] and his party, he reached the Lombé, and having met numbers

[044-047]of slave-caravans on his way, again passed through Cassange, crossed

[044-048]the Coango, and reached the Zambesi at Kewawa. By the 8th of the

[044-049]following June he was again at Lake Dilolo, and descending the river,

[044-050]he re-entered Linyanté. Here he stayed till the 3rd of November, when

[044-051]he commenced his second great journey, which was to carry him

[044-052]completely across [[africa|Africa]] from west to east.

[044-053]After visiting the famed Victoria Falls, the intrepid explorer quitted

[044-054]the Zambesi, and took a north-easterly route. The transit of the

[044-055]territory of the Batokas, a people brutalized by the inhalation of

[044-056]hemp; a visit to Semalemboni the powerful chief of the district; the

[044-057]passage of the Kafoni; a visit to king Mbourouma; an inspection of the

[044-058]ruins of Zumbo, an old Portuguese town; a meeting with the chief

[044-059]Mpendé, at that time at war with the Portuguese, these were the

[044-060]principal events of this journey, and on the 22nd of April, Livingstone

[044-061]left Teté, and having descended the river as far as its delta, reached

[044-062]Quilimané, just four years after his last departure from the Cape. On

[044-063]the 12th of July he embarked for the Mauritius, and on the 22nd of

[044-064]December, 1856, he landed in England after an absence of sixteen years.

[044-065][Illustration: With none to guide him except a few natives.]

[044-066]Loaded with honours by the Geographical Societies of London and Paris,

[044-067]brilliantly entertained by all ranks, it would have been no matter of

[044-068]surprise if he had surrendered himself to a well-earned repose; but no

[044-069]thought of permanent rest occurred to him, and on the 1st of March,

[044-070]1858, accompanied by his brother Charles, [[captain-hull|Captain]] Bedingfield, Dr.

[044-071]Kirk, Dr. Miller, Mr. Thornton, and Mr. Baines, he started again, with

[044-072]the intention of [[exploration|exploring]] the basin of the Zambesi, and arrived in due

[044-073]time at the coast of Mozambique.

[044-074]The party ascended the great river by the Kongone mouth; they were on

[044-075]board a small steamer named the "Ma-Robert," and reached Teté on the

[044-076]8th of September.

[044-077]During the following year they investigated the lower course of the

[044-078]Zambesi, and its left affluent the Shiré, and having visited Lake

[044-079]Shirwa, they explored the territory of the Manganjas, and discovered

[044-080]Lake Nyassa. In August, 1860, they returned to the Victoria Falls.

[044-081]Early in the following year, Bishop Mackenzie and his missionary staff

[044-082]arrived at the mouth of the Zambesi.

[044-083]In March an exploration of the Rovouma was made on board the "Pioneer,"

[044-084]the exploring party returning afterwards to Lake Nyassa, where they

[044-085]remained a considerable time. The 30th of January, 1862, was signalized

[044-086]by the arrival of Mrs. Livingstone, and by the addition of another

[044-087]steamer, the "Lady Nyassa;" but the happiness of reunion was very

[044-088]transient; it was but a short time before the enthusiastic Bishop

[044-089]Mackenzie succumbed to the unhealthiness of the climate, and on the

[044-090]27th of April Mrs. Livingstone expired in her husband's arms.

[044-091]A second investigation of the Rovouma soon followed and at the end of

[044-092]November the doctor returned to the Zambesi, and reascended the Shire.

[044-093]In the spring of 1803 he lost his companion Mr. Thornton, and as his

[044-094]brother and Dr. Kirk were both much debilitated, he insisted upon their

[044-095]return to Europe, while he himself returned for the third time to Lake

[044-096]Nyassa, and completed the hydrographical survey which already he had

[044-097]begun.

[044-098]A few months later found him once more at the mouth of the Zambesi;

[044-099]thence he crossed over to Zanzibar, and after five years' absence

[044-100]arrived in London, where he published his work, "The exploration of the

[044-101]Zambesi and its affluents."

[044-102]Still unwearied and insatiable in his longings, he was back again in

[044-103]Zanzibar at the commencement of 1866, ready to begin his fourth

[044-104]journey, this time attended only by a few sepoys and negroes.

[044-105]Witnessing on his way some horrible scenes which were perpetrated as

[044-106]the result of the prosecution of the slave-trade, he proceeded to

[044-107]Mokalaosé on the shores of Lake Nyassa, where nearly all his attendants

[044-108]deserted him, and returned to Zanzibar with the report that he was dead.

[044-109]Dr. Livingstone meanwhile was not only alive, but undaunted in his

[044-110]determination to visit the country between the two lakes Nyassa and

[044-111]Tanganyika. With none to guide him except a few natives, he crossed the

[044-112]Loangona, and in the following April discovered Lake Liemmba. Here he

[044-113]lay for a whole month hovering between life and death, but rallying a

[044-114]little he pushed on to the north shore of Lake Moero. Taking up his

[044-115]quarters at Cazembé for six weeks, he made two separate explorations of

[044-116]the lake, and then started farther northwards, intending to reach

[044-117]Ujiji, an important town upon Lake Tanganyika; overtaken, however, by

[044-118]floods, and again abandoned by his servants, he was obliged to retrace

[044-119]his steps. Six weeks afterwards he had made his way southwards to the

[044-120]great lake Bangweolo, whence once more he started towards Tanganyika.

[044-121]This last effort was most trying, and the doctor had grown so weak that

[044-122]he was obliged to be carried, but he reached Ujiji, where he was

[044-123]gratified by finding some supplies that had been thoughtfully forwarded

[044-124]to him by the Oriental Society at Calcutta.

[044-125][Illustration: "You are Dr. Livingstone, I presume?"]

[044-126]His great aim now was to ascend the lake, and reach the sources of the

[044-127]Nile. On the 21st of September he was at Bambarré, in the country of

[044-128]the [[cannibalism|cannibal]] Manyuema, upon the Lualaba, the river afterwards

[044-129]ascertained by Stanley to be the Upper Zaire or Congo. At Mamobela the

[044-130]doctor was ill for twenty-four days, tended only by three followers who

[044-131]continued faithful; but in July he made a vigorous effort, and although

[044-132]he was reduced to a skeleton, made his way back to Ujiji.

[044-133]During this long time no tidings of Livingstone reached Europe, and

[044-134]many were the misgivings lest the rumours of his death were only too

[044-135]true. He was himself, too, almost despairing as to receiving any help.

[044-136]But help was closer at hand than he thought. On the 3rd of November,

[044-137]only eleven days after his return to Ujiji, some gun shots were heard

[044-138]within half a mile of the lake. The doctor went out to ascertain whence

[044-139]they proceeded, and had not gone far before a white man stood before

[044-140]him.

[044-141]"You are Dr. Livingstone, I presume," said the stranger, raising his

[044-142]cap.

[044-143]"Yes, sir, I am Dr. Livingstone, and am happy to see you," answered the

[044-144]doctor, smiling kindly.

[044-145]The two shook each other warmly by the hand.

[044-146]The new arrival was Henry Stanley, the correspondent of the _New York

[044-147]Herald_, who had been sent out by Mr. Bennett, the editor, in search of

[044-148]the great African explorer. On receiving his orders in October, 1870,

[044-149]without a day's unnecessary delay he had embarked at Bombay for

[044-150]Zanzibar, and, after a journey involving considerable peril, had

[044-151]arrived safely at Ujiji.

[044-152]Very soon the two travellers found themselves on the best of terms, and

[044-153]set out together on an excursion to the north of Tanganyika. They

[044-154]proceeded as far as Cape Magala, and decided that the chief outlet of

[044-155]the lake must be an affluent of the Lualaba, a conclusion that was

[044-156]subsequently confirmed by Cameron.

[044-157]Towards the end of the year Stanley began to prepare to return.

[044-158]Livingstone accompanied him as far as Kwihara, and on the 3rd of the

[044-159]following March they parted.

[044-160]"You have done for me what few men would venture to do; I am truly

[044-161]grateful," said Livingstone.

[044-162]Stanley could scarcely repress his tears as he expressed his hope that

[044-163]the doctor might be spared to return to his friends safe and well.

[044-164]"Good-bye!" said Stanley, choked with emotion.

[044-165]"Good-bye!" answered the veteran feebly.

[044-166]Thus they parted, and in July, 1872, Stanley landed at Marseilles.

[044-167]Again David Livingstone resumed his researches in the interior.

[044-168]After remaining five months at Kwihara he gathered together a retinue

[044-169]consisting of his faithful followers Suzi, Chumah, Amoda, and Jacob

[044-170]Wainwright, and fifty-six men sent to him by Stanley, and lost no time

[044-171]in proceeding towards the south of Tanganyika. In the course of the

[044-172]ensuing month the [[slave-caravan|caravan]] encountered some frightful storms, but

[044-173]succeeded in reaching Moura. There had previously been an extreme

[044-174]drought, which was now followed by the rainy season, which entailed the

[044-175]loss of many of the beasts of burden, in consequence of the bites of

[044-176]the tzetsy.

[044-177]On the 24th of January they were at Chitounkwé, and in April, after

[044-178]rounding the east of Lake Bangweolo, they made their way towards the

[044-179]village of Chitambo. At this point it was that Livingstone had parted

[044-180]company with certain slave-dealers, who had carried the information to

[044-181]old Alvez that the missionary traveller would very likely proceed by

[044-182]way of Loanda to Kazonndé.

[044-183]But on the 13th of June, the very day before [[negoro|Negoro]] reckoned on

[044-184]obtaining from [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] the letter which should be the means of

[044-185]securing him a hundred thousand dollars, tidings were circulated in the

[044-186]district that on the 1st of May Dr. Livingstone had breathed his last.
