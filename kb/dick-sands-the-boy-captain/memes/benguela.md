# Benguela

**Benguela** is both a province and a port city on [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] coast of [[angola|Angola]], forming one of the three administrative divisions of Portuguese [[africa|West Africa]] alongside [[congo|Congo]] and [[angola|Angola]]. In *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*, it appears as a key geographic marker within the Portuguese colonial territory — a place where the authority of Lisbon theoretically extends but practically dissolves in the face of the interior [[slavery|slave trade]].

## Geography

- **Type**: Port city and province
- **Real or fictional**: Real
- **First mentioned**: Chapter 27 (027-047)

Verne describes the territory as comprising "three provinces, [[congo|Congo]], [[angola|Angola]], and Benguela" (027-047), noting that of this vast region "little was then known except the coast" (027-047). The land "extends from the [[congo|Zaire]] on the north to the Nourse on the south, and its chief towns are the ports of Benguela and of [[loanda|St. Paul de Loanda]]" (027-049). The interior had been "almost entirely unexplored" (027-050) at the time of the novel's events, with the Portuguese colonial presence concentrated almost exclusively along the coastline.

## Role in the Narrative

Benguela serves as the southern anchor of the [[angola|Angola]]n coastal zone through which the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]] operated. The Portuguese colonial administration at Benguela is depicted as impotent against the [[slavery|slave trade]]rs operating inland. Verne states plainly that "the Portuguese authorities at [[loanda|Loanda]] and Benguela had practically no power to prevent it" (037-077), with the coastal barracks "crowded to overflowing with prisoners" (037-077) while slave ships continued to elude naval patrols.

The region's geography also creates practical obstacles for the [[slavery|slave trade]]rs themselves. The trader [[harris|Harris]] explains that "to pass a [[slave-caravan|caravan]] through Benguela or Loande is an utter impossibility; neither the governors nor the chefés will listen to a word of reason" (028-147). This forces the [[slavery|slave trade]]r [[jose-antonio-alvez|Alvez]] to consider alternative routes "towards Nyangwe and the great lakes" (028-147), illustrating how the geography of the [[angola|Angola]]n coast shaped the [[slavery|slave trade]]'s [[ships-log|log]]istics.

## Role in African Exploration

Benguela was a significant waypoint in the European exploration of [[africa|Africa]]. The explorer Silva Porto "reached the port of St. Philip de Benguela, after a journey that had occupied three years and five months" (027-091), traversing regions devastated by war and the [[slavery|slave trade]] including "Kilemba, Urua, the sources of the Lomami, Ulanda, and Lovalé" (027-091) before crossing the [[coanza|Coanza River]].

Verney Lovett Cameron, who crossed [[africa|Africa]] from east to west a few years after the novel's setting, passed through Benguela on his trans-continental journey. The route "seems destined to become the chief highway of traffic between [[angola|Angola]] and the interior" (034-171), with steamers already plying the lower waters of the region's rivers.

## Description in the Text

Verne's depiction of Benguela and its surrounding region is colored by the grim realities of the [[slavery|slave trade]]. The reference to "an honest man in a Benguela penitentiary" (028-021) — delivered as dark humor by the [[slavery|slave trade]]r [[harris|Harris]] — underscores the reputation of the Portuguese colonial penal system.

> "The Portuguese authorities at Loanda and Benguela had practically no power to prevent it. The barracks on the shore were crowded to overflowing with prisoners, the few slave-ships that managed to elude the cruisers being quite inadequate to embark them." (037-077)

The region of southern [[angola|Angola]] near [[mossamedes|Mossâmedes]] was described as "the only part where it can be attempted with any chance of success" (028-147), referring to the export of slaves, as the governors at Benguela and [[loanda|Loanda]] had become less amenable to the trade.

## Historical Context

At the time of the novel's setting (1873–1874), Benguela had been a Portuguese colonial possession for centuries. The port of São Felipe de Benguela was established in 1617 and became an important center for the [[slavery|slave trade]] to Brazil. By the 1870s, when Verne's novel takes place, the transatlantic [[slavery|slave trade]] had been officially abolished in Portuguese territories (1836), though the internal [[africa|Africa]]n [[slavery|slave trade]] continued largely unchecked.

Verne's depiction draws on contemporary accounts of [[angola|Angola]]'s geography and the persistence of the [[slavery|slave trade]] despite official prohibitions. The explorer Cameron's journey through Benguela (1873–1875) was contemporary news when Verne was writing the novel, lending the geographic details a sense of immediacy for his French readers.
