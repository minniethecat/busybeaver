# Harris

An American who presents himself as a friendly guide in a fictional South American province but is in reality a [[slavery|slave-trade]]r conspiring with [[negoro|Negoro]]. Harris leads the party of shipwreck survivors deep into the [[africa|Africa]]n interior, all the while pretending they are crossing Bolivia toward civilization.

## Role & Identity

- **Role**: False guide posing as a resident of South America; [[slavery|slave-trade]]r conspiring with [[negoro|Negoro]]
- **Affiliation**: [[africa|Africa]]n slave-trading network; partner of [[negoro|Negoro]]
- **First appearance**: Chapter 20 (PN 020-171)
- **Last appearance**: Chapter 28 (flees after his treachery is exposed)

## Character Description

Harris introduces himself with smooth self-assurance: "My name is Harris. I was born in South Carolina" (020-171). He presents himself as an experienced traveler in the region, appearing eager to help the [[shipwreck|shipwrecked]] party. His manner is superficially genial — he calls little Jack "my young friend" (020-180) and exclaims "What a charming little boy!" (021-026) — but beneath his charm lies calculation.

When his story is questioned, "a frown passed over Harris's countenance" (021-074), revealing the anger beneath his friendly veneer. He is a practiced liar, answering [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]'s pointed questions with evasive assurances: "it is, as I have told you, a province but little frequented by travellers" (021-100).

## Role in the Narrative

### The False Guide

Harris appears on the shore after the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]* wrecks on the [[africa|Africa]]n coast. He claims the party has landed in Bolivia, in a region he knows well. He urges them to proceed inland immediately: "This very day, at once," said Harris quickly (021-113). When asked about [[negoro|Negoro]], Harris feigns ignorance: "[[negoro|Negoro]]? who is [[negoro|Negoro]]?" (021-168). He dismisses the missing cook as "Drowned, probably" (021-170).

### The March into Africa

Harris leads the party through the [[africa|Africa]]n jungle, inventing names for native plants and animals to sustain the illusion of South America. He points out features with reassurance: "there is not a native within a hundred miles of us" (022-153). When [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] increasingly suspects the truth, Harris grows evasive — "Harris could not deny" (024-152) the discrepancies accumulating in his story.

The conspiracy between the two men is confirmed: "Harris and [[negoro|Negoro]] must be in a conspiracy!" (026-069). The two are seen meeting together — "The two men were Harris and [[negoro|Negoro]]" (027-177). When his treachery is finally exposed, Harris flees; the section title foretells his fate: "Harris has left us" (001-135).

## Relationships

- **[[Negoro]]** — Partner in the slave-trading conspiracy; the two men coordinate the [[treachery|betrayal]] of the survivors
- **[[dick-sands]]** — Antagonist; [[dick-sands|Dick]]'s growing suspicion of Harris drives the narrative tension: "[[dick-sands|Dick]] becoming convinced that Harris was a traitor" (025-126)
- **[[mrs-weldon]]** — Deceived passenger; Harris charms her with his courteous manner (021-055)

## Fate

After his treachery is exposed by [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]], Harris flees. He is later killed in the course of the events that follow, paying for his crimes.

## Significance

Harris is the smooth-talking deceiver — the villain who works by charm and misdirection rather than open menace. Where [[negoro|Negoro]]'s villainy is sullen and menacing, Harris's is genial and persuasive, making him the more dangerous of the two. His false identity as an American guide in "South America" that is really [[africa|Africa]] is the novel's central [[treachery|deception]], the pivot on which the entire [[africa|Africa]]n section turns. He embodies the theme of betrayed trust: those who appear most helpful prove most treacherous.

> "Negoro? who is Negoro?" asked Harris, in a tone of surprise. "Drowned, probably," said Harris indifferently. (021-168–170)
