[001-001][[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] the Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]] by Jules Verne

[001-002][Redactor's Note: _Dick Sands the Boy Captain_ (Number V018 in the T&M

[001-003]numerical listing of Verne's works) is a translation of _Un capitaine

[001-004]de quinze ans_ (1878) by Ellen E. Frewer who also translated other

[001-005]Verne works. The current translation was published by Sampson & Low in

[001-006]England (1878) and Scribners in New York (1879) and was republished

[001-007]many times and included in Volume 8 of the Parke edition of _The Works

[001-008]of Jules Verne_ (1911). There is another translation published by

[001-009]George Munro (1878) in New York with the title _Dick Sand A Captain at

[001-010]Fifteen_.

[001-011]This work has an almost mechanical repetiveness in the continuing

[001-012]description of the day after day trials of sailing at sea. Thus the

[001-013]illustrations, of which there were 94 in the french edition, are all

[001-014]the more important in keeping up the reader's interest. The titles of

[001-015]the illustrations are given here as a prelude to a future fully

[001-016]illustrated edition.]

[001-190]CHAPTER I.

[001-191]THE "PILGRIM."

[001-192]On the 2nd of February, 1873, the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]," a tight little craft of

[001-193]400 tons burden, lay in lat. 43° 57', S. and long. 165° 19', W. She was

[001-194]a schooner, the property of James W. Weldon, a wealthy Californian

[001-195]ship-owner who had fitted her out at [[san-francisco|San Francisco]], expressly for the

[001-196]whale-fisheries in the southern seas.

[001-197]James Weldon was accustomed every season to send his whalers both to

[001-198]the Arctic regions beyond Behring Straits, and to the Antarctic Ocean

[001-199]below Tasmania and Cape Horn; and the "Pilgrim," although one of the

[001-200]smallest, was one of the best-going vessels of its class; her

[001-201]sailing-powers were splendid, and her rigging was so adroitly adapted

[001-202]that with a very small crew she might venture without risk within sight

[001-203]of the impenetrable ice-fields of the southern hemisphere: under

[001-204]skilful guidance she could dauntlessly thread her way amongst the

[001-205]drifting ice-bergs that, lessened though they were by perpetual shocks

[001-206]and undermined by warm currents, made their way northwards as far as

[001-207]the parallel of [[new-zealand|New Zealand]] or the Cape of Good Hope, to a latitude

[001-208]corresponding to which in the northern hemisphere they are never seen,

[001-209]having already melted away in the depths of the Atlantic and [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]]

[001-210]Oceans.

[001-211]For several years the command of the "Pilgrim" had been entrusted to

[001-212]Captain Hull, an experienced seaman, and one of the most dexterous

[001-213]harpooners in Weldon's service. The crew consisted of five sailors and

[001-214]an apprentice. This number, of course, was quite insufficient for the

[001-215]process of [[whaling|whale-fishing]], which requires a large contingent both for

[001-216]manning the whale-boats and for cutting up the whales after they are

[001-217]captured; but Weldon, following the example of other owners, found it

[001-218]more economical to embark at San Francisco only just enough men to work

[001-219]the ship to New Zealand, where, from the promiscuous gathering of

[001-220]seamen of well-nigh every nationality, and of needy emigrants, the

[001-221]captain had no difficulty in engaging as many whalemen as he wanted for

[001-222]the season. This method of hiring men who could be at once discharged

[001-223]when their services were no longer required had proved altogether to be

[001-224]the most profitable and convenient.

[001-225]The "Pilgrim" had now just completed her annual voyage to the Antarctic

[001-226]circle. It was not, however, with her proper quota of oil-barrels full

[001-227]to the brim, nor yet with an ample cargo of cut and uncut whalebone,

[001-228]that she was thus far on her way back. The time, indeed, for a good

[001-229]haul was past; the repeated and vigourous attacks upon the cetaceans

[001-230]had made them very scarce; the whale known as "the Right whale," the

[001-231]"Nord-kapper" of the northern fisheries, the "Sulpher-boltone" of the

[001-232]southern, was hardly ever to be seen; and latterly the whalers had had

[001-233]no alternative but to direct their efforts against the Finback or

[001-234]Jubarte, a gigantic mammal, encounter with which is always attended

[001-235]with considerable danger.

[001-236]So scanty this year had been the supply of whales that Captain Hull had

[001-237]resolved next year to push his way into far more southern latitudes;

[001-238]even, if necessary, to advance to the regions known as Clarie and

[001-239]Adélie Lands, of which the discovery, though claimed by the American

[001-240]navigator Wilkes, belongs by right to the illustrious Frenchman Dumont

[001-241]d'Urville, the commander of the "Astrolabe" and the "Zélee."

[001-242]The season had been exceptionally unfortunate for the "Pilgrim." At the

[001-243]beginning of January, almost in the height of the southern summer, long

[001-244]before the ordinary time for the whalers' return, Captain Hull had been

[001-245]obliged to abandon his fishing-quarters. His hired contingent, all men

[001-246]of more than doubtful character, had given signs of such

[001-247]insubordination as threatened to end in mutiny; and he had become aware

[001-248]that he must part company with them on the earliest possible

[001-249]opportunity. Accordingly, without delay, the bow of the "Pilgrim" was

[001-250]directed to the northwest, towards New Zealand, which was sighted on

[001-251]the 15th of January, and on reaching Waitemata, the port of Auckland,

[001-252]in the Hauraki Gulf, on the east coast of North Island, the whole of

[001-253]the gang was peremptorily discharged.

[001-254]The ship's crew were more than dissatisfied. They were angry. Never

[001-255]before had they returned with so meagre a haul. They ought to have had

[001-256]at least two hundred barrels more. The captain himself experienced all

[001-257]the mortification of an ardent sportsman who for the first time in his

[001-258]life brings home a half-empty bag; and there was a general spirit of

[001-259]animosity against the rascals whose rebellion had so entirely marred

[001-260]the success of the expedition.
