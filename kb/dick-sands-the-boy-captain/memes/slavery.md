The institution of human bondage and the transatlantic slave trade that forms the historical backdrop and central moral concern of the second part of *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*. When [[negoro|Negoro]]'s [[treachery|betrayal]] delivers the castaways to [[angola|Angola]], the narrative transforms from a sea adventure into an indictment of the ongoing [[africa|Africa]]n slave trade.

## Definition

In the context of the novel, "slavery" encompasses both the historic transatlantic slave trade and the ongoing internal [[africa|Africa]]n slave trade of the 19th century. As the narrator states, the "slave-trade" is an "abominable traffic" that, despite being "ostensibly forbidden" by 1878, "is still largely carried on, especially in [[africa|Central Africa]]" (026-079–083).

## Role in the Novel

The theme of slavery emerges early in the narrative when the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]] crew discovers five [[africa|Africa]]n passengers aboard a wrecked vessel. [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] immediately suspects them to be "part of a slave-cargo which was on its way to some [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] island" (005-075). The rescued [[africa|Africa]]ns — [[tom|Tom]], Bat, Austin, Actaeon, and [[hercules|Hercules]] — are revealed to be free men returning home after being liberated in the "anti-slavery war" in the United States (005-118).

Slavery becomes the dominant theme of the novel's second half when the party, betrayed by the slave-trader [[harris|Harris]], finds themselves in equatorial [[africa|Africa]] — "the land of slavery! in the very haunt of slave-drivers!" (026-072). The characters are captured and marched as [[slave-caravan|slaves themselves]], experiencing firsthand the horrors of the internal [[africa|Africa]]n slave trade.

> "The 'slave-trade' is an expression that ought never to have found its way into any human language." (026-079)

## Historical Context

Jules Verne devotes an extensive portion of Chapter 26 (the opening of Part II) to a historical survey of the slave trade, situating the novel within 19th-century [[abolitionism|abolition]]ist discourse:

| Period | Event |
|--------|-------|
| 15th century | Portuguese colonizers begin exchanging captured Moors for [[africa|Africa]]n slaves |
| Late 16th century | Slave trade "permanently established" across European colonial powers |
| 1751 | Quakers begin [[abolitionism|abolition]]ist movement in North America |
| 1807 | England prohibits the slave trade |
| 1814 | France prohibits the slave trade |
| 1820 | United States declares slave trade piracy |
| 1824 | Great Britain declares slave trade piracy |
| 1838 | 670,000 slaves freed in British colonies |
| 1848 | 260,000 slaves freed in French colonies |
| 1865 | American Civil War completes [[abolitionism|emancipation]] in North America |
| 1878 | Despite [[abolitionism|abolition]], "no less than 24,000 slaves annually reach the coast" (026-190) |

Despite these legal victories, Verne acknowledges that by 1878 the trade continues: "no less than 24,000 slaves annually reach the coast, a number that hardly represents a tenth part of those who are massacred" (026-190–191).

## Significance

Slavery is not merely a background element but the novel's organizing moral principle. The structure of the narrative — from the freedom of the sea to the horror of the [[africa|Africa]]n interior — maps onto the geography of the slave trade. The Portuguese slaver [[negoro|Negoro]] personifies the evil that Verne attributes to the institution, while [[hercules|Hercules]] and [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] represent the resistance against it. By devoting an entire chapter to the history of [[abolitionism|abolition]], Verne positions his adventure novel within the tradition of [[abolitionism|abolition]]ist literature.
