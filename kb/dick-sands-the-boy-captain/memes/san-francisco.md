The home port of the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]] and the destination of its ill-fated return voyage. San Francisco is owned by James W. [[mrs-weldon|Weldon]], who fitted out the schooner for the whaling trade, and it is the city where [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]], her son [[jack-weldon|Jack]], and Cousin [[benedict|Benedict]] all reside. Throughout the novel, San Francisco functions as a symbolic beacon of civilization and safety.

- **Type**: Port city

## Role in the Narrative

San Francisco is the home base of the [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]] and the destination toward which the entire narrative points. The schooner was fitted out by her owner "at San Francisco, expressly for the [[right-whale|whale]]-fisheries" (001-195). After the voyage from [[new-zealand|New Zealand]], [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] expects to "return home to San Francisco" (002-013), and the entire crew's objective is to "work our way back to San Francisco" (017-144).

The city represents safety, civilization, and home — the place that the castaways are desperately trying to reach. It contrasts at every turn with the [[africa|African wilderness]] into which they are betrayed. [[benedict|Benedict]] keeps his "own comfortable museum at San Francisco" (015-031), and even [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]], after the disaster, thinks of "arriving at San Francisco" as the benchmark of success (043-022).

## Description in the Text

Jules Verne does not provide extensive description of San Francisco itself, as none of the action takes place there. Rather, the city functions as a symbolic beacon — the point of return, the place of safety, the home that is always receding. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] promises to "work our way back to San Francisco" (017-144), and [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] is assured there will be "means of conveyance to San Francisco" (020-194).

> "I have every reason to believe I shall work our way back to San Francisco." — Dick Sands (017-144)

At the novel's end, the survivors finally do "arrive at San Francisco" (043-022), closing the geographic arc of the narrative.

## Historical Context

In 1878, San Francisco was the premier port of the American West Coast, a hub of the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] whaling industry, and a booming city in the decades following the Gold Rush. The [[mrs-weldon|Weldon]] family's ownership of a whaling schooner and their comfortable urban lifestyle reflects the mercantile wealth of Gilded Age San Francisco.
