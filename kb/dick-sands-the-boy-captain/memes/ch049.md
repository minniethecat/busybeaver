[049-001]A new cause of anxiety arose on the following evening, and made it

[049-002]necessary for them to take the most guarded measures of precaution. The

[049-003]river-bed had widened out into a kind of lagoon, and on the right side

[049-004]of this, built upon piles in the water, not only was there a collection

[049-005]of about thirty huts, but the fires gleaming under the thatch, made it

[049-006]evident that they were all inhabited. Unfortunately the only channel of

[049-007]the stream flowed close under the huts, the river elsewhere being so

[049-008]obstructed with rocks that navigation of any kind was impossible.

[049-009]Nothing was more probable than that the natives would have set their

[049-010]nets all across the piles, and if so, the canoe would be sure to be

[049-011]obstructed, and an alarm must inevitably be raised. Every caution

[049-012]seemed to be unavailing, because the canoe must follow the stream;

[049-013]however, in the lowest of whispers [[dick-sands|Dick]] ordered Hercules to keep clear

[049-014]as much as he could of the worm-eaten timber. The night was not very

[049-015]dark, which was equally an advantage and a disadvantage, as while it

[049-016]permitted those on board to steer as they wanted, it did not prevent

[049-017]them from being seen.

[049-018]The situation became more and more critical. [[About]] a hundred feet

[049-019]ahead, the channel was very contracted; two natives, gesticulating

[049-020]violently, were seen squatting on the pilework; a few moments more and

[049-021]their voices could be heard; it was obvious that they had seen the

[049-022]floating mass; apprehending that it was going to destroy their nets,

[049-023]they yelled aloud and shouted for assistance; instantly five or six

[049-024]negroes scrambled down the piles, and perched themselves upon the

[049-025]cross-beams.

[049-026]On board the canoe the profoundest silence was maintained. Dick only

[049-027]signalled his directions to Hercules, without uttering a word, while

[049-028]Jack performed his part by holding [[dingo|Dingo]]'s mouth tightly closed, to

[049-029]stop the low growlings which the faithful watch-dog seemed resolved to

[049-030]make; but fortunately every sound was overpowered by the rushing of the

[049-031]stream and the clamour of the negroes, as they hurriedly drew in their

[049-032]nets. If they should raise them in time, all might be well, but if, on

[049-033]the other hand, the canoe should get entangled, the consequences could

[049-034]hardly fail to be disastrous. The current in its narrow channel was so

[049-035]strong that Dick was powerless either to modify his course or to

[049-036]slacken it.

[049-037]Half a minute more, and the canoe was right under the woodwork, but the

[049-038]efforts of the natives had already elevated the nets so that the

[049-039]anticipated danger was happily escaped; but it chanced that in making

[049-040]its way through the obstacle, a large piece of the grass-thatch got

[049-041]detached. One of the negroes raised a sudden shout of alarm, and it

[049-042]seemed only too probable that he had caught a sight of the travellers

[049-043]below and was informing his companions. This apprehension, too, was

[049-044]only momentary; the current had changed almost to a rapid, and carried

[049-045]the canoe along with such velocity that the lacustrine village was

[049-046]quickly out of sight.

[049-047]"Steer to the left!" cried Dick, finding that the riverbed had again

[049-048]become clear.

[049-049]A stiff pull at the tiller made the craft fly in that direction.

[049-050]Dick went to the stern, and scanned the moonlit waters. All was

[049-051]perfectly still, no canoe was in pursuit; perhaps the natives had not

[049-052]one to use; but certain it was that when daylight dawned no vestige of

[049-053]an inhabitant was to be seen. Nevertheless Dick thought it prudent for

[049-054]a while to steer close under the shelter of the left-hand shore.

[049-055][Illustration: Instantly five or six negroes scrambled down the piles.]

[049-056]By the end of the next four days the aspect of the country had

[049-057]undergone a remarkable change, the jungle having given place to a

[049-058]desert as dreary as the Kalahari itself. The river appeared

[049-059]interminable, and it became a matter of serious consideration how to

[049-060]get a sufficiency of food. Fish was scarce, or at least hard to catch,

[049-061]and the arid soil provided no means of sustenance for antelopes, so

[049-062]that nothing was to be gained from the chase. Carnivorous animals also

[049-063]had quite disappeared, and the silence of the night was broken, not by

[049-064]the roar of wild beasts, but by the croaking of frogs in a discordant

[049-065]chorus, which Cameron has compared to the clanking of hammers and the

[049-066]grating of files in a ship-builder's yard.

[049-067]Far away both to the east and west the outlines of hills could be

[049-068]faintly discerned, but the shores on either hand were perfectly flat

[049-069]and devoid of trees. Euphorbias, it is true, grew in considerable

[049-070]numbers, but as they were only of the oil-producing species, and not

[049-071]the kind from which cassava or manioc is procured, they were useless in

[049-072]an alimentary point of view.

[049-073]Dick was becoming more and more perplexed, when Hercules happened to

[049-074]mention that the natives often eat young fern-fronds and the pith of

[049-075]the papyrus, and that before now he had himself been reduced to the

[049-076]necessity of subsisting on nothing better.

[049-077]"We must try them," said Dick.

[049-078]Both ferns and papyrus abounded on the banks, and a meal was prepared,

[049-079]the sweet soft pith of the papyrus being found very palatable. Jack in

[049-080]particular appeared to enjoy it extremely, but it was not in any way a

[049-081]satisfying diet.

[049-082]Thanks to Cousin Benedict, a fresh variety in the matter of food was

[049-083]found on the following day. Since the discovery of the "Hexapodes

[049-084]Benedictus" he had recovered his spirits, and, having fastened his

[049-085]prize safely inside his hat, he wandered about, as often as he had a

[049-086]chance, in his favourite pursuit of insect-hunting. As he was rummaging

[049-087]in the long grass, he put up a bird which flew but a very short

[049-088]distance. Benedict recognized it by its peculiar note, and, seeing Dick

[049-089]take his gun to aim at it, exclaimed,--

[049-090]"Don't fire, don't fire! that bird will be worth nothing for food among

[049-091]five of us."

[049-092]"It will be dinner enough for Jack," said Dick, who, finding that the

[049-093]bird did not seem in a hurry to make its escape, delayed his shot for a

[049-094]moment, without intending to be diverted from his purpose of securing

[049-095]it.

[049-096]"You mustn't fire," insisted Benedict, "it is an indicator; it will

[049-097]show you where there are lots of honey."

[049-098]Aware that a few pounds of honey would really be of more value than a

[049-099]little bird, Dick lowered his gun, and in company with the entomologist

[049-100]set off to follow the indicator, which seemed, by alternately flying

[049-101]and stopping, to be inviting them to come on, and they had but a little

[049-102]way to go before they observed several swarms of bees buzzing around

[049-103]some old stems hidden amongst the euphorbias. Notwithstanding

[049-104]Benedict's remonstrances against depriving the bees of the fruits of

[049-105]their industry, Dick instantly set to work, and without remorse

[049-106]suffocated them by burning dry grass underneath. Having secured a good

[049-107]amount of honey, he left the comb to the indicator as its share of the

[049-108]booty, and went back with his companion to the canoe.

[049-109]The honey was acceptable, but it did not do much to alleviate the

[049-110]cravings of hunger.

[049-111]Next day it happened that they had just stopped for their accustomed

[049-112]rest, when they observed that an enormous swarm of grasshoppers had

[049-113]settled at the mouth of a creek close by. Two or three deep they

[049-114]covered the soil, myriads and myriads of them adhering to every shrub.

[049-115]"The natives eat those grasshoppers," said Benedict, "and like them

[049-116]too."

[049-117]The remark produced an instant effect; all hands were busied in

[049-118]collecting them, and a large supply was quickly gathered: the canoe

[049-119]might have been filled ten times over.

[049-120]Grilled over a slow fire, they were found to be very palatable eating,

[049-121]and, spite of his qualms of conscience, Benedict himself made a hearty

[049-122]meal.

[049-123]But although the gnawings of absolute hunger were thus assuaged, all

[049-124]the travellers began to long most anxiously for the voyage to come to

[049-125]an end. The mode of transit indeed might be less exhausting to the

[049-126]bodily powers than a land march would have been, but the excessive heat

[049-127]by day, the damp mists at night, and the incessant attacks of

[049-128]mosquitoes, all combined to render the passage extremely trying. There

[049-129]was no telling how long it would last, and Dick was equally uncertain

[049-130]whether it might end in a few days, or be protracted for a month. The

[049-131]direction which the stream was taking was itself a subject of

[049-132]perplexity.

[049-133]A fresh surprise was now in store.

[049-134]As Jack, a few mornings afterwards, was standing at the bow peering

[049-135]through an aperture in the grass canopy above him, he suddenly turned

[049-136]round and cried,--

[049-137]"The sea! the sea!"

[049-138]Dick started forwards, and looked eagerly in the same direction.

[049-139]A large expanse of water was visible in the horizon, but after having

[049-140]surveyed it for a moment or two, he said,--

[049-141]"No, Jack, it is not the sea, it is a great river; it is running west,

[049-142]and I suppose this river runs into it. Perhaps it is the Zaire."

[049-143]"Let us hope it is," said [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] earnestly.

[049-144]Most cordially did Dick Sands re-echo her words, being well aware that

[049-145]at the mouth of that river were Portuguese villages, where a refuge

[049-146]might assuredly be found.

[049-147]For several succeeding days the canoe, still concealed by its covering,

[049-148]floated on the silvery surface of this new-found stream. On either side

[049-149]the banks became less arid, and there seemed everything to encourage

[049-150]the few survivors of the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" to believe that they would soon see

[049-151]the last of the perils and toils of their journey.

[049-152]They were too sanguine. Towards three o'clock on the morning of the

[049-153]18th, Dick, who was at his usual post at the bow, fancied he heard a

[049-154]dull rumbling towards the west. Mrs. Weldon, Jack, and Benedict were

[049-155]all asleep. Calling Hercules to him, he asked him whether he could not

[049-156]hear a strange noise. The night was perfectly calm, and not a breath of

[049-157]air was stirring. The negro listened attentively, and suddenly, his

[049-158]eyes sparkling with delight, exclaimed,--

[049-159]"Yes, captain, I hear the sea!"

[049-160]Dick shook his head and answered,--

[049-161]"It is not the sea, Hercules."

[049-162]"Not the sea!" cried the negro, "then what can it be?"

[049-163]"We must wait till daybreak," replied Dick, "and meanwhile we shall

[049-164]have to keep a sharp look-out."

[049-165]Hercules returned to his place, but only to continue listening with

[049-166]ever-increasing curiosity. The rumbling perceptibly increased till it

[049-167]became a continued roar.

[049-168]With scarcely any intervening twilight night passed into day. Just in

[049-169]front, scarcely more than half a mile ahead, a great mist was hanging

[049-170]over the river; it was not an ordinary fog, and when the sun rose, the

[049-171]light of the dawn caused a brilliant rainbow to arch itself from shore

[049-172]to shore.

[049-173]In a voice so loud that it awoke Mrs. Weldon, Dick gave his order to

[049-174]Hercules to steer for the bank:--

[049-175]"Quick, quick, Hercules! ashore! ashore! there are cataracts close

[049-176]ahead!"

[049-177]And so it was. Within little more than a quarter of a mile the bed of

[049-178]the river sank abruptly some hundred feet, and the foaming waters

[049-179]rushed down in a magnificent fall with irresistible velocity. A few

[049-180]minutes more and the canoe must have been swallowed in the deep abyss.

[049-181]CHAPTER XIX.

[049-182]AN ATTACK.

[049-183]The canoe inclined to the west readily enough; the fall in the

[049-184]river-bed was so sudden that the current remained quite unaffected by

[049-185]the cataract at a distance of three hundred yards.

[049-186]On the bank were woods so dense that sunlight could not penetrate the

[049-187]shade. Dick was conscious of a sad misgiving when he looked at the

[049-188]character of the territory through which they must necessarily pass. It

[049-189]did not seem practicable by any means to convey the canoe below the

[049-190]falls.
