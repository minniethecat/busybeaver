# Congo River

The **Congo River** (also called the Zaire or Lualaba) is one of [[africa|Africa]]'s greatest rivers, draining a vast basin in the heart of the continent. In *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*, it appears in the final chapters as the destination toward which the surviving party struggles — a river mouth that represents the promise of passage home after the nightmare of the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]].

## Geography

- **Type**: River
- **Real or fictional**: Real
- **First mentioned**: Chapter 7 (007-089)

Verne situates the Congo within [[africa|Central Africa]]'s geography with considerable precision, noting that it "unites the lake-district with [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] Ocean" over a course of nearly "2,900 miles, under the names of the Lualaba, Zaire, and Congo" (027-141). The river flows through "three provinces, Congo, [[angola|Angola]], and [[benguela|Benguela]]" (027-047), of which "little was then known" at the time of the novel's setting. Its vast watershed encompasses much of [[africa|Central Africa]], making it both a geographic landmark and a strategic route to the coast.

## Historical Exploration

The Congo's exploration history is woven into the narrative. The 1816 British expedition under [[captain-hull|Captain]] James Tuckey "ascended the Congo as far as the Yallala" falls (027-059), mapping the lower reaches of the river but failing to penetrate far into the interior. [[david-livingstone|David Livingstone]] "crossed the Coango, an affluent of the Congo" (027-066) during his trans-[[africa|Africa]]n journeys, and Livingstone believed that "by following its course, he should come to [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]]" (027-122) — a theory that would later be proven correct by [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]]'s epic descent of the river (1874–1877), which was occurring contemporaneously with Verne's writing of the novel.

The river also served as a base point for overland expeditions. Verne notes that a "starting-point was the mouth of the Congo" for one expedition (007-089), with the exit designed to be in the opposite direction.

## The Survivors' Destination

The Congo emerges as the survivors' objective in the novel's final act. After escaping from the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]], [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] leads the party toward the coast, hoping to reach the Congo's mouth. The group was "encamping on the banks of the Congo" (050-050), awaiting an opportunity to embark on a vessel that would carry them home.

The journey was arduous: they knew they "were still 120 miles from the coast" (050-107) as they made their way "to Emboma, at the mouth of the Congo" (051-039). The party expected to "embark somewhere at the mouth of the Congo" (045-053) for their passage home — a hope that sustains them through the final hardships of the journey.

> "They were still 120 miles from the coast — but every step brought them nearer to Emboma, at the mouth of the Congo, where a ship might be found." (050-107)

## The River System

The Congo's tributary network links it to the broader geography of the novel. The [[coanza|Coanza River]], which the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]] followed inland, is an affluent of the Congo system. The river thus connects the novel's two great geographic zones: the [[angola|Angola]]n interior where the [[slavery|slave trade]] operates, and [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] coast where [[rescue|salvation]] lies.

Verne's depiction reflects the state of geographic knowledge in the 1870s: the lower Congo was relatively well known, but the river's upper course — the Lualaba — remained a subject of intense speculation and controversy among geographers. Would it prove to be the headwaters of the Congo, or of the Nile? The question was not definitively resolved until Stanley's expedition, and Verne incorporates this uncertainty into the novel's geographic texture.
