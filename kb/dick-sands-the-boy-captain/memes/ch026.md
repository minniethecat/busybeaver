[026-001]But it was with a shudder that ran through his veins that he witnessed

[026-002]for himself the mutilated fragments of several human bodies, and saw,

[026-003]lying beside them, some broken forks, and some bits of iron chain.

[026-004]The sight of the gory remains made [[dingo|Dingo]] bark ferociously, and [[dick-sands|Dick]],

[026-005]who was most anxious that [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]'s attention should not be called

[026-006]to the discovery, had the greatest difficulty in driving him back; but

[026-007]fortunately the lady's mind was so engrossed with her patient, that she

[026-008]did not observe the commotion. Harris stood aloof; there was no one to

[026-009]notice the change that passed over his countenance, but the expression

[026-010]was almost diabolical in its malignity.

[026-011]Poor old Tom himself seemed perfectly spell-bound. With his hands

[026-012]clenched, his eyes dilated, and his breast heaving with emotion, he

[026-013]kept repeating without anything like coherence, the words,--

[026-014]"Forks! chains! forks! ... long ago ... remember ... too well ...

[026-015]chains!"

[026-016]"For Mrs. Weldon's sake, Tom, hold your tongue!" Dick implored him.

[026-017]Tom, however, was full with some remembrance of the past; he continued

[026-018]to repeat,--

[026-019]"Long ago ... forks ... chains!" until Dick led him out of hearing.

[026-020]A fresh halting-place was chosen a short distance further on, and

[026-021]supper was prepared. But the meal was left almost untasted; not so much

[026-022]that hunger had been overcome by fatigue, but because the indefinable

[026-023]feeling of uneasiness, that had taken possession of them all, had

[026-024]entirely destroyed all appetite.

[026-025][Illustration: The man was gone, and his horse with him.]

[026-026]Gradually the night became very dark. The sky was covered with heavy

[026-027]storm-clouds, and on the western horizon flashes of summer lightning

[026-028]now and then glimmered through the trees. The air was perfectly still;

[026-029]not a leaf stirred, and the atmosphere seemed so charged with

[026-030]electricity as to be incapable of transmitting sound of any kind.

[026-031]Dick, himself, with Austin and Bat in attendance, remained on guard,

[026-032]all of them eagerly straining both eye and ear to catch any light or

[026-033]sound that might disturb the silence and obscurity. Old Tom, with his

[026-034]head sunk upon his breast, sat motionless, as in a trance; he was

[026-035]gloomily revolving the awakened memories of the past. Mrs. Weldon was

[026-036]engaged with her sick child. Scarcely one of the party was really

[026-037]asleep, except indeed it might be Cousin Benedict, whose reasoning

[026-038]faculties were not of an order to carry him forwards into any future

[026-039]contingencies.

[026-040]Midnight was still an hour in advance, when the dull air seemed filled

[026-041]with a deep and prolonged roar, mingled with a peculiar kind of

[026-042]vibration.

[026-043]Tom started to his feet. A fresh recollection of his early days had

[026-044]struck him.

[026-045]"A lion! a lion!" he shouted.

[026-046]In vain Dick tried to repress him; but he repeated,--

[026-047]"A lion! a lion!"

[026-048]Dick Sands seized his cutlass, and, unable any longer to control his

[026-049]wrath, he rushed to the spot where he had left Harris lying.

[026-050]The man was gone, and his horse with him!

[026-051]All the suspicions that had been so long pent up within Dick's mind now

[026-052]shaped themselves into actual reality. A flood of light had broken in

[026-053]upon him. Now he was convinced, only too certainly, that it was not the

[026-054]coast of America at all upon which the schooner had been cast ashore!

[026-055]it was not Easter Island that had been sighted far away in the west!

[026-056]the compass had completely deceived him; he was satisfied now that the

[026-057]strong currents had carried them quite round Cape Horn, and that they

[026-058]had really entered the Atlantic. No wonder that quinquinas, caoutchouc,

[026-059]and other South American products, had failed to be seen. This was

[026-060]neither the Bolivian pampas nor the plateau of Atacama. They were

[026-061]giraffes, not ostriches, that had vanished down the glade; they were

[026-062]elephants that had trodden down the underwood; they were hippopotamuses

[026-063]that were lurking by the river; it was indeed the dreaded tzetsy that

[026-064]Cousin Benedict had so triumphantly discovered; and, last of all, it

[026-065]was a lion's roar that had disturbed the silence of the forest. That

[026-066]chain, that knife, those forks, were unquestionably the instruments of

[026-067]slave-dealers; and what could those mutilated hands be, except the

[026-068]relics of their ill-fated victims?

[026-069]Harris and [[negoro|Negoro]] must be in a conspiracy!

[026-070]It was with terrible anguish that Dick gnashed his teeth and muttered,--

[026-071]"Yes, it is too true; we are in [[africa|Africa]]! in equatorial Africa! in the

[026-072]land of slavery! in the very haunt of slave-drivers!"

[026-077]CHAPTER I.

[026-078]THE DARK CONTINENT.

[026-079]The "[[slavery|slave-trade]]" is an expression that ought never to have found its

[026-080]way into any human language. After being long practised at a large

[026-081]profit by such European nations as had possessions beyond the seas,

[026-082]this abominable traffic has now for many years been ostensibly

[026-083]forbidden; yet even in the enlightenment of this nineteenth century, it

[026-084]is still largely carried on, especially in Central Africa, inasmuch as

[026-085]there are several states, professedly Christian, whose signatures have

[026-086]never been affixed to the deed of [[abolitionism|abolition]].

[026-087]Incredible as it should seem, this barter of human beings still exists,

[026-088]and for the due comprehension of the second part of Dick Sands' story

[026-089]it must be borne in mind, that for the purpose of supplying certain

[026-090]colonies with slaves, there continue to be prosecuted such barbarous

[026-091]"man-hunts" as threaten almost to lay waste an entire continent with

[026-092]blood, fire, and pillage.

[026-093]The nefarious traffic as far as regards negroes does not appear to have

[026-094]arisen until the fifteenth century. The following are said to be the

[026-095]circumstances under which it had its origin. After being banished from

[026-096]Spain, the Mussulmans crossed the straits of Gibraltar and took refuge

[026-097]upon the shores of Africa, but the Portuguese who then occupied that

[026-098]portion of the coast persecuted the fugitives with the utmost severity,

[026-099]and having captured them in large numbers, sent them as prisoners into

[026-100]Portugal. They were thus the first nucleus of any African slaves that

[026-101]entered Western Europe since the commencement of the Christian era. The

[026-102]majority, however, of these Mussulmans were members of wealthy

[026-103]families, who were prepared to pay almost any amount of money for their

[026-104]release; but no ransom was exorbitant enough to tempt the Portuguese to

[026-105]surrender them; more precious than gold were the strong arms that

[026-106]should work the resources of their young and rising colonies. Thus

[026-107]baulked in their purpose of effecting a direct ransom of their captured

[026-108]relatives, the Mussulman families next submitted a proposition for

[026-109]exchanging them for a larger number of African negroes, whom it would

[026-110]be quite easy to procure. The Portuguese, to whom the proposal was in

[026-111]every way advantageous, eagerly accepted the offer; and in this way the

[026-112]slave-trade was originated in Europe.

[026-113]By the end of the sixteenth century this odious traffic had become

[026-114]permanently established; in principle it contained nothing repugnant to

[026-115]the semi-barbarous thought and customs then existing; all the great

[026-116]states recognized it as the most effectual means of colonizing the

[026-117]islands of the New World, especially as slaves of negro blood, well

[026-118]acclimatized to tropical heat, were able to survive where white men

[026-119]must have perished by thousands. The transport of slaves to the

[026-120]American colonies was consequently regularly effected by vessels

[026-121]specially built for that purpose, and large dépôts for this branch of

[026-122]commerce were established at various points of the African coast. The

[026-123]"goods" cost comparatively little in production, and the profits were

[026-124]enormous.

[026-125]Yet, after all, however indispensable it might be to complete the

[026-126]foundation of the trans-atlantic colonies, there was nothing to justify

[026-127]this shameful barter of human flesh and blood, and the voice of

[026-128]philanthropy began to be heard in protestation, calling upon all

[026-129]European governments, in the name of mercy and common humanity, to

[026-130]decree the abolition of the trade at once.

[026-131]In 1751, the Quakers put themselves at the head of the abolitionist

[026-132]movement in North America, that very land where, a hundred years later,

[026-133]the war of secession burst forth, in which the question of slavery bore

[026-134]the most conspicuous part. Several of the Northern States, Virginia,

[026-135]Connecticut, Massachusetts, and Pennsylvania prohibited the trade,

[026-136]liberating the slaves, in spite of the cost, who had been imported into

[026-137]their territories.

[026-138]The campaign, thus commenced, was not limited to a few provinces of the

[026-139]New World; on this side of the Atlantic, too, the partisans of slavery

[026-140]were subject to a vigourous attack. England and France led the van, and

[026-141]energetically beat up recruits to serve the righteous cause. "Let us

[026-142]lose our colonies rather than sacrifice our principles," was the

[026-143]magnanimous watchword that resounded throughout Europe, and

[026-144]notwithstanding the vast political and commercial interests involved in

[026-145]the question, it did not go forth in vain. A living impulse had been

[026-146]communicated to the [[freedom|liberation]]-movement. In 1807, England formally

[026-147]prohibited the slave-trade in her colonies; France following her

[026-148]example in 1814. The two great nations then entered upon a treaty on

[026-149]the subject, which was confirmed by Napoleon during the Hundred Days.

[026-150]Hitherto, however, the declaration was purely theoretical. Slave-ships

[026-151]continued to ply their illicit trade, discharging their living cargo at

[026-152]many a colonial port. It was evident that more resolute and practical

[026-153]measures must be taken to impress the enormity. Accordingly the United

[026-154]States in 1820, and Great Britain in 1824, declared the slave-trade to

[026-155]be an act of piracy and its perpetrators to be punishable with death.

[026-156]France soon gave in her adherence to the new treaty, but the Southern

[026-157]States of America, and the Spanish and Portuguese, not having signed

[026-158]the act of abolition, continued the importation of slaves at a great

[026-159]profit, and this in defiance of the recognized reciprocal right of

[026-160]visitation to verify the flags of suspected ships.

[026-161]But although the slave-trade by these measures was in a considerable

[026-162]measure reduced, it continued to exist; new slaves were not allowed,

[026-163]but the old ones did not recover their liberty. England was now the

[026-164]first to set a noble example. On the 14th of May, 1833, an Act of

[026-165]Parliament, by a munificent vote of millions of pounds, emancipated all

[026-166]the negroes in the British Colonies, and in August, 1838, 670,000

[026-167]slaves were declared free men. Ten years later, in 1848, the French

[026-168]Republic liberated the slaves in her colonies to the number of 260,000,

[026-169]and in 1859 the war which broke out between the Federals and

[026-170]Confederates in the United States finished the work of emancipation by

[026-171]extending it to the whole of North America.

[026-172]Thus, three great powers have accomplished their task of humanity, and

[026-173]at the present time the slave-trade is carried on only for the

[026-174]advantage of the Spanish and Portuguese colonies, or to supply the

[026-175]requirements of the Turkish or Arab populations of the East. Brazil,

[026-176]although she has not emancipated her former slaves, does not receive

[026-177]any new, and all negro children are pronounced free-born.

[026-178]In contrast, however, to all this, it is not to be concealed that, in

[026-179]the interior of Africa, as the result of wars between chieftains waged

[026-180]for the sole object of making captives, entire tribes are often reduced

[026-181]to slavery, and are carried off in caravans in two opposite directions,

[026-182]some westwards to the Portuguese colony of Angola, others eastwards to

[026-183]Mozambique. Of these miserable creatures, of whom a very small

[026-184]proportion ever reach their destination, some are despatched to Cuba or

[026-185]Madagascar, others to the Arab or Turkish provinces of Asia, to Mecca

[026-186]or Muscat. The French and English cruisers have practically very little

[026-187]power to control the iniquitous proceedings, because the extent of

[026-188]coast to be watched is so large that a strict and adequate surveillance

[026-189]cannot be maintained. The extent of the odious export is very

[026-190]considerable; no less than 24,000 slaves annually reach the coast, a

[026-191]number that hardly represents a tenth part of those who are massacred

[026-192]or otherwise perish by a deplorable end. After the frightful

[026-193]butcheries, the fields lie devastated, the smouldering villages are

[026-194]void of inhabitants, the rivers reek with bleeding corpses, and wild

[026-195]beasts take undisputed possession of the soil. Livingstone, upon

[026-196]returning to a district, immediately after one of these ruthless raids,

[026-197]said that he could never have recognized it for the same that he had

[026-198]visited only a few months previously; and all other travellers, Grant,

[026-199]Speke, Burton, Cameron, Stanley, describe the wooded plateau of Central

[026-200]Africa as the principal theatre of the barbarous warfare between chief

[026-201]and chief. In the region of the great lakes, throughout the vast

[026-202]district which feeds the market of Zanzibar, in Bornu and Fezzan,

[026-203]further south on the banks of the Nyassa and Zambesi, further west in

[026-204]the districts of the Upper Zaire, just traversed by the intrepid

[026-205]Stanley, everywhere there is the recurrence of the same scenes of ruin,

[026-206]slaughter, and devastation. Ever and again the question seems to be

[026-207]forced upon the mind whether slavery is not to end in the entire

[026-208]annihilation of the negro race, so that, like the Australian tribes of

[026-209]South Holland, it will become extinct. Who can doubt that the day must

[026-210]dawn which will herald the closing of the markets in the Spanish and

[026-211]Portuguese colonies, a day when civilized nations shall no longer

[026-212]tolerate the perpetration of this barbarous wrong?
