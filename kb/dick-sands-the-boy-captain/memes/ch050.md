[050-001]As they neared the shore, [[dingo|Dingo]] became intensely agitated. At first

[050-002][[dick-sands|Dick]] suspected that a wild beast or a native might be lurking in the

[050-003]papyrus, but it soon became obvious that the dog was excited by grief

[050-004]rather than by rage.

[050-005]"Dingo is crying," said Jack; "poor Dingo!" and the child laid his arms

[050-006]over the creature's neck.

[050-007]The dog, however, was too impatient to be caressed; bounding away, he

[050-008]sprang into the water, swam across the twenty feet that intervened

[050-009]between the shore, and disappeared in the grass.

[050-010]In a few moments the boat had glided on to a carpet of confervas and

[050-011]other aquatic plants, starting a few kingfishers and some snow-white

[050-012]herons. Hercules moored it to the stump of a tree, and the travellers

[050-013]went ashore.

[050-014]There was no pathway through the forest, only the trampled moss showed

[050-015]that the place had been recently visited either by animals or men.

[050-016][Illustration: Upon the smooth wood were two great letters in dingy

[050-017]red.]

[050-018]Dick took his gun and Hercules his hatchet, and they set out to search

[050-019]for Dingo. They had not far to go before they saw him with his nose

[050-020]close to the ground, manifestly following a scent; the animal raised

[050-021]his head for a moment, as if beckoning them to follow, and kept on till

[050-022]he reached an old sycamore-stump. Having called out to the rest of the

[050-023]party to join them, Dick made his way farther into the wood till he got

[050-024]up to Dingo, who was whining piteously at the entrance of a dilapidated

[050-025]hut.

[050-026]The rest were not long in following, and they all entered the hut

[050-027]together. The floor was strewn with bones whitened by exposure.

[050-028]"Some one has died here," said [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]].

[050-029]"Perhaps," added Dick, as if struck by a sudden thought, "it was

[050-030]Dingo's old master. Look at him! he is pointing with his paw."

[050-031]The portion of the sycamore-trunk which formed the farther side of the

[050-032]hut had been stripped of its bark, and upon the smooth wood were two

[050-033]great letters in dingy red almost effaced by time, but yet plain enough

[050-034]to be distinguished.

[050-035]"S. V.," cried Dick, as he looked where the dog's paw rested; "the same

[050-036]initials that Dingo has upon his collar. There can be no mistake. S. V."

[050-037]A small copper box, green with verdigris, caught his eye, and he picked

[050-038]it up. It was open, but contained a scrap of discoloured paper. The

[050-039]writing upon this consisted of a few sentences, of which only detached

[050-040]words could be made out, but they revealed the sad truth only too

[050-041]plainly.

[050-042]"Robbed by [[negoro|Negoro]]--murdered--Dingo--help--Negoro guide--120 miles from

[050-043]coast--December 3rd, 1871--write no more.

[050-044]"S. VERNON."

[050-045]Here was the clue to a melancholy story. Samuel Vernon, under the

[050-046]guidance of Negoro, and taking with him his dog Dingo, had set out on

[050-047]an exploration of a district of [[africa|Central Africa]]; he had taken a

[050-048]considerable quantity of money to procure the necessary supplies on the

[050-049]way, and this had excited the cupidity of his guide, who seized the

[050-050]opportunity, whilst they were encamping on the banks of the Congo, to

[050-051]assassinate his employer, and get possession of his property. Negoro,

[050-052]however, had not escaped; he had fallen into the hands of the

[050-053]Portuguese, by whom he was recognized as an agent of the slave-dealer

[050-054]Alvez, and condemned to spend the rest of his days in prison. He

[050-055]contrived after a while to make his escape, and, as has been already

[050-056]mentioned, found his way to [[new-zealand|New Zealand]], whence he had returned by

[050-057]securing an engagement on board the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]." Between the time when he

[050-058]was attacked by Negoro and the moment of his death, Vernon had managed

[050-059]to write the few brief lines of which the fragments still survived, and

[050-060]to deposit the document in the box from which the money had been

[050-061]stolen, and by a last effort had traced out his initials in blood upon

[050-062]the naked wood which formed the wall of the hut. For many days Dingo

[050-063]watched beside his master, and throughout that time his eyes were

[050-064]resting so perpetually upon the two crimson letters in front of him,

[050-065]that mere instinct seemed to fasten them indelibly on his memory.

[050-066]Quitting his watch one day, perhaps to pacify his hunger, the dog

[050-067]wandered to the coast, where he was picked up by the captain of the

[050-068]"Waldeck," afterwards to be transferred to the very ship on which his

[050-069]owner's murderer had been engaged as cook.

[050-070]All throughout this time poor Vernon's bones had been bleaching in the

[050-071]African forest, and the first resolution of Dick and Mrs. Weldon was to

[050-072]give the residue of his remains some semblance of a decent burial. They

[050-073]were just proceeding to their task when Dingo gave a furious growl, and

[050-074]dashed out of the hut; another moment, and a terrible shriek made it

[050-075]evident that he was in conflict with some dread antagonist.

[050-076]Hercules was quickly in pursuit, and the whole party followed in time

[050-077]to witness the giant hurl himself upon a man with whom already Dingo

[050-078]was in mortal combat.

[050-079][Illustration: The dog was griping the man by the throat]

[050-080]The dog was griping the man by the throat, the man was lifting his

[050-081]cutlass high above the head of the dog.

[050-082]That man was Negoro. The rascal, on getting his letter at Kazonndé,

[050-083]instead of embarking at once for America, had left his native escort

[050-084]for a while, and returned to the scene of his crime to secure the

[050-085]treasure which he had left buried at a little distance in a spot that

[050-086]he had marked. At this very moment he was in the act of digging up the

[050-087]gold he had concealed; some glistening coins scattered here and there

[050-088]betrayed his purpose; but in the midst of his labours he had been

[050-089]startled by the dashing forward of a dog; another instant, and the dog

[050-090]had fixed itself upon his throat, whilst he, in an agony of

[050-091]desperation, had drawn his cutlass and plunged it deep into the

[050-092]creature's side.

[050-093]Hercules came up at the very climax of the death-struggle.

[050-094]"You villain! you accursed villain! I have you now!" he cried, about to

[050-095]seize hold of his victim.

[050-096]But vengeance was already accomplished. Negoro gave no sign of life;

[050-097]death had overtaken him on the very scene of his guilt. Dingo, too, had

[050-098]received a mortal wound; he dragged himself back to the hut, lay down

[050-099]beside the remains of his master, and expired.

[050-100]The sad task of burying Vernon's bones, and laying his faithful dog

[050-101]beside them having been accomplished, the whole party was obliged to

[050-102]turn their thoughts to their own safety. Although Negoro was dead, it

[050-103]as very likely that the natives that he had taken with him were at no

[050-104]great distance, and would come to search for him.

[050-105]A hurried conference was held as to what steps had best be taken. The

[050-106]few words traceable on the paper made them aware that they were on the

[050-107]banks of the Congo, and that they were still 120 miles from the coast.

[050-108]The fall just ahead was probably the cataract of Memo, but whatever it

[050-109]was, no doubt it effectually barred their farther progress by water.

[050-110]There seemed no alternative but that they should make their way by one

[050-111]bank or the other a mile or two below the waterfall, and there

[050-112]construct a raft on which once again they could drift down the stream.

[050-113]The question that pressed for immediate settlement was which bank it

[050-114]should be. Here, on the left bank, would be the greater risk of

[050-115]encountering the negro escort of Negoro, while as to the farther shore

[050-116]they could not tell what obstacles it might present.

[050-117]Altogether Mrs. Weldon advocated trying the other side, but Dick

[050-118]insisted upon crossing first by himself to ascertain whether an advance

[050-119]by that route were really practicable.

[050-120]"The river is only about 100 yards wide," he urged; "I can soon get

[050-121]across. I shall leave Hercules to look after you all."

[050-122]Mrs. Weldon demurred for a while, but Dick seemed resolute, and as he

[050-123]promised to take his gun and not to attempt to land if he saw the least

[050-124]symptom of danger, she at last consented, but with so much reluctance

[050-125]that even after he had entered the canoe she said,--

[050-126]"I think, Dick, it would be really better for us all to go together."

[050-127]"No, Mrs. Weldon, indeed, no; I am sure it is best for me to go alone;

[050-128]I shall be back in an hour."

[050-129]"If it must be so, it must," said the lady.

[050-130]"Keep a sharp look-out, Hercules!" cried the youth cheerily, as he

[050-131]pushed off from the land.

[050-132]The strength of the current was by no means violent, but quite enough

[050-133]to make the direction of Dick's course somewhat oblique. The roar of

[050-134]the cataract reverberated in his ears, and the spray, wafted by the

[050-135]westerly wind, brushed lightly past his face, and he shuddered as he

[050-136]felt how near they must have been to destruction if he had relaxed his

[050-137]watch throughout the night.

[050-138]It took him hardly a quarter of an hour to reach the opposite bank, and

[050-139]he was just preparing to land when there arose a tremendous shout from

[050-140]about a dozen natives, who, rushing forward, began to tear away the

[050-141]canopy of grass with which the canoe was covered.

[050-142]Dick's horror was great. It would have been greater still if he had

[050-143]known that they were cannibals. They were the natives settled at the

[050-144]lacustrine village higher up the river. When the piece of thatch had

[050-145]been knocked off in passing the piles a glimpse had been caught of the

[050-146]passengers below, and aware that the cataract ahead must ultimately

[050-147]bring them to a standstill, the eager barbarians had followed them

[050-148]persistently day by day for the last eight days.

[050-149]Now they thought they had secured their prize, but loud was their yell

[050-150]of disappointment when on stripping off the thatch they found only one

[050-151]person, and that a mere boy, standing beneath it.

[050-152]Dick stood as calmly as he could at the bow, and pointed his gun

[050-153]towards the savages, who were sufficiently acquainted with the nature

[050-154]of fire-arms to make them afraid to attack him.

[050-155]Mrs. Weldon with the others, in their eagerness to watch Dick's

[050-156]movements, had remained standing upon the shore of the river, and at

[050-157]this instant were caught sight of by one of the natives, who pointed

[050-158]them out to his companions. A sudden impulse seized the whole of them,

[050-159]and they sprang into the canoe; there seemed to be a practised hand

[050-160]amongst them, which caught hold of the rudder-oar, and the little craft

[050-161]was quickly on its way back.

[050-162]Although he gave up all as now well-nigh lost, Dick neither moved nor

[050-163]spoke. He had one lingering hope yet left. Was it not possible even now

[050-164]that by sacrificing his own life he could save the lives of those that

[050-165]were entrusted to him?

[050-166]When the canoe had come near enough to the shore for his voice to be

[050-167]heard, he shouted with all his might,--

[050-168]"Fly, Mrs. Weldon; fly, all of you; fly for your lives!"

[050-169]But neither Mrs. Weldon nor Hercules stirred; they seemed rooted to the

[050-170]ground.

[050-171]"Fly, fly, fly!" he continued shouting.

[050-172]But though he knew they must hear him, yet he saw them make no effort

[050-173]to escape. He understood their meaning; of what avail was flight when

[050-174]the savages would be upon their track in a few minutes after?

[050-175]A sudden thought crossed his mind. He raised his gun and fired at the

[050-176]man who was steering; the bullet shattered the rudder-scull into

[050-177]fragments.

[050-178]The cannibals uttered a yell of terror. Deprived of guidance, the canoe

[050-179]was at the mercy of the current, and, borne along with increasing

[050-180]speed, was soon within a hundred feet of the cataract.
