# Storm at Sea

The violent meteoro[[ships-log|log]]ical phenomena at sea that serve as both obstacle and plot mechanism in *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*, driving the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]* far from its intended course.

## Definition

A storm at sea, in the novel's 1878 maritime context, encompasses gales, tempests, and hurricanes—progressively intensifying wind and wave conditions that threaten a sailing vessel's survival. The crew's ability to weather these storms tests their [[navigation|seamanship]] and vessel integrity.

## Role in the Novel

The storm sequence occupies a significant portion of the novel's first half. After [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]]'s death, [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] must navigate through increasingly violent weather. The barometer first indicates danger: "the gale into a tempest, which probably would increase to a hurricane" (014-171). The prediction proves accurate: "the wind had now increased to a hurricane; it had veered to the" southwest (015-140).

The storm's relentless duration is emphasized: "a week passed, and still the tempest showed no signs of abating" (016-076). The psycho[[ships-log|log]]ical toll mounts: "whither had this incessant hurricane driven them? why did not the" coast appear (016-091)? [[dick-sands|Dick]] brings the vessel "straight before the tempest" (016-057), the only safe course in extreme conditions.

The hurricane's role in the plot is crucial—it is the storm that drives the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]* off course: "north-eastwards by a tremendous hurricane to the very place" (028-126) where the [[slavery|slave-trade]]rs await. Without the storm, the party would never have reached [[africa|Africa]].

After landfall, the novel continues the storm motif: "of rain, hurricanes that no tree could resist" characterizes the [[africa|Africa]]n interior's climate (031-105). The storm at sea gives way to storms on land, maintaining the theme of nature's overwhelming power.
