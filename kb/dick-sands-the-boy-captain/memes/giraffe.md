# Giraffe

A species of large [[africa|Africa]]n ungulate that plays a pivotal role in the plot, serving as the incontrovertible evidence that the travelers are in [[africa|Africa]] — not South America — when [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] correctly identifies the animals [[harris|Harris]] tries to pass off as ostriches.

## Overview

- **Type**: Species
- **Species**: *Giraffa camelopardalis*
- **Owner/Associated with**: None (wild animal)
- **Chapters**: 24–30, 36

## Role in the Narrative

The giraffe herd appears at a moment of high dramatic tension, when [[harris|Harris]] is attempting to convince the party that they are traversing the South American pampas. When the travelers encounter a herd of large animals, [[harris|Harris]] claims they are ostriches, but [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] immediately identifies them as giraffes — an animal native to [[africa|Africa]], not the Americas. This moment destroys [[harris|Harris]]'s cover story and sets in motion the chain of revelations that follow [024-108].

The giraffe sighting is the narrative's turning point where the geographical [[treachery|deception]] begins to unravel. [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]'s objection — "there are no giraffes in America!" — underscores the impossibility of [[harris|Harris]]'s explanation [024-111]. [[harris|Harris]] attempts to salvage his lie by asserting the animals were actually ostriches [024-113]. This incident is referenced several times later as a landmark event in the journey [025-124][026-061][028-018].

During the [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]] march in later chapters, the herd again appears as a startled group fleeing the party's approach, a detail that reinforces the [[africa|Africa]]n savanna setting [030-119].

## Description in the Text

Verne portrays the giraffe as a large, unmistakable creature, tall enough that "an elephant or giraffe could have reared above" the tall grass [036-102]. The text emphasizes the giraffe's height and its status as a species well-known even to a young sailor. The contrast is clear: [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] recognizes giraffes on sight, while the treacherous [[harris|Harris]] tries desperately to deny what everyone has seen, first claiming they are ostriches [024-114], then pretending to acknowledge "an American ostrich" [024-127].

> "They were giraffes!" shouted Dick. [024-108]

## Historical/Natural History Context

In 1878, when Jules Verne published *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*, the giraffe was well-known to European audiences through zoo[[ships-log|log]]ical gardens and exploration narratives. The animal's exclusively [[africa|Africa]]n range was common knowledge among the educated public. Verne uses this familiarity to create a dramatic moment of recognition: the young hero's correct zoo[[ships-log|log]]ical knowledge directly contradicts the villain's geographical [[treachery|deception]].
