# Africa

The setting for all of Part II: the castaways of the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*, having been betrayed by [[negoro|Negoro]], find themselves on the coast of equatorial Africa instead of South America, and are forced to march into [[angola|Angola]]'s slave-trading interior.

## Geography

- **Type**: Continent
- **Real or fictional**: Real
- **Relevant to**: Part II / African interior / [[slavery|slave-trade]] regions of [[angola|Angola]]
- **First mentioned**: Chapter 1 (PN=001-138), though the continent becomes the explicit setting from Chapter 26 onward

## Role in the Narrative

Africa is the hidden destination to which [[negoro|Negoro]] steers the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*. After the [[right-whale|whale]] hunt disaster, the survivors believe they have reached South America, but [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] gradually realizes they are, in fact, in Africa — specifically in the Portuguese province of [[angola|Angola]], a region notorious in 1878 for the ongoing [[slavery|slave trade]] (PN=026-071). The revelation transforms the novel from a maritime adventure into a harrowing overland journey through a landscape of both natural peril and human cruelty (PN=026-072).

The narrative devotes over half the book to Africa. Verne uses it to expose the horrors of the internal [[slavery|slave trade]] — "the [[slavery|slave-trade]] is still extensively carried on, especially in Central Africa" (PN=026-084) — and to contrast the ideals of [[abolitionism|abolition]] with the brutal reality persisting in the Portuguese colonies (PN=027-047).

## Key Events at This Location

- **Arrival and landfall** — The castaways reach the coast believing they have landed in Bolivia (PN=026-071)
- **Recognition of the truth** — [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] discovers they are in equatorial Africa, in the land of slave-dealers (PN=026-067)
- **The march inland** — The party travels through [[angola|Angola]], encountering slavers and extreme hardship (PN=027-179)
- **Cameron's crossing recalled** — Verne references Cameron's recent east-to-west transit of Africa from 1873–1875 (PN=027-083)
- **Pursuit by [[slavery|slave-trade]]rs** — The party is menaced by [[slave-caravan|slave caravan]]s throughout the African interior (PN=005-070)

## Description in the Text

Verne describes Africa as "the land of slavery," "the very haunt of slave-dealers" (PN=026-072). The province of [[angola|Angola]] is characterized as dangerous and "fatal" (PN=027-042), a place where the ongoing [[slavery|slave trade]] had "laid almost waste an entire continent" (PN=026-091).

> "it is too true; we are in Africa! in equatorial Africa! in the land of slavery! in the very haunt of slave-dealers!" (PN=026-071–PN=026-072)

The continent is depicted through its rivers, its population displacements, and its trade routes — the [[coanza|Coanza River]] serving as a "highway of traffic between [[angola|Angola]] and the interior" (PN=034-172).

## Historical Context

In 1878, when Verne published this novel, equatorial Africa was still largely unmapped by Europeans. The [[slavery|slave trade]] was officially abolished but persisted through Portuguese colonial networks. The novel references the expeditions of Livingstone (who died in 1873) and Cameron (who crossed the continent 1873–1875), situating the story within contemporary geographical knowledge. The Portuguese colonies of [[angola|Angola]], [[congo|Congo]], and [[benguela|Benguela]] were known as centers of the ongoing illegal slave traffic (PN=027-047).
