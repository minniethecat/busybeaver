[032-001]"Termites, or whatever they are, they will have to turn out for us,"

[032-002]said [[dick-sands|Dick]].

[032-003]"But they will eat us up!" objected Benedict.

[032-004]"I can't help that," retorted Dick; "go we must, and go at once."

[032-005]"But stop a moment," continued the provoking naturalist; "stop, and

[032-006]tell me: I can't be wrong: I always thought that white ants could never

[032-007]be found elsewhere than in [[africa|Africa]]."

[032-008]"Come along, sir, I say; come along, quick!" shouted Dick, terrified

[032-009]lest [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] should have overheard him.

[032-010]They hurried on. A wind had risen; large spattering drops were now

[032-011]beginning to fall more heavily on the ground and in a few minutes it

[032-012]would be impossible to stand against the advancing [[storm-at-sea|tempest]]. The nearest

[032-013]of the accumulation of ant-hills was reached in time, and however

[032-014]dangerous their occupants might be, it was decided either to expel

[032-015]them, or to share their quarters. Each cone was formed of a kind of

[032-016]reddish clay, and had a single opening at its base. Hercules took his

[032-017]hatchet, and quickly enlarged the aperture till it would admit his own

[032-018]huge body. Not an ant made its appearance. Cousin Benedict expressed

[032-019]his extreme surprise. But the structure unquestionably was empty, and

[032-020]one after another the whole party made their way inside.

[032-021]The rain by this time was descending in terrific torrents, strong

[032-022]enough to extinguish, one would think, the most violent explosions of

[032-023]the electric fluid. But the travellers were secure in their shelter,

[032-024]and had nothing to fear for the present; their tenement was of greater

[032-025]stability than a tent or a native hut. It was one of those marvellous

[032-026]structures erected by little [[entomology|insects]], which to Cameron appeared even

[032-027]more wonderful than the upraising of the Egyptian pyramids by human

[032-028]hands. To use his own comparison, it might be likened to the

[032-029]construction of a Mount Everest, the loftiest of the Himalayan peaks,

[032-030]by the united labour of a nation.

[032-031]CHAPTER V.

[032-032]WHITE ANTS.

[032-033]The storm had now burst in full fury, and fortunate it was that a

[032-034]refuge had been found. The rain did not fall in separate drops as in

[032-035]temperate zones, but descended like the waters of a cataract, in one

[032-036]solid and compact mass, in a way that could only suggest the outpour of

[032-037]some vast aerial basin containing the waters of an entire ocean.

[032-038]Contrary, too, to the storms of higher latitudes, of which the duration

[032-039]seems ordinarily to be in inverse ratio to their violence, these

[032-040]African tempests, whatever their magnitude, often last for whole days,

[032-041]furrowing the soil into deep ravines, changing plains to lakes and

[032-042]brooks to torrents, and causing rivers to overflow and cover vast

[032-043]districts with their inundations. It is hard to understand whence such

[032-044]volumes of vapour and electric fluid can accumulate. The earth, upon

[032-045]these occasions, might almost seem to be carried back to the remote

[032-046]period which has been called "the diluvian age."

[032-047]Happily, the walls of the ant-hill were very thick; no beaver-hut

[032-048]formed of pounded earth could be more perfectly water-tight, and a

[032-049]torrent might have passed over it without a particle of moisture making

[032-050]its way through its substance.

[032-051]As soon as the party had taken possession of the tenement, a lantern

[032-052]was lighted, and they proceeded to examine the interior. The cone,

[032-053]which was about twelve feet high inside, was eleven feet wide at the

[032-054]base, gradually narrowing to a sugar-loaf top. The walls and partitions

[032-055]between the tiers of cells were nowhere less than a foot thick

[032-056]throughout.

[032-057]These wonderful erections, the result of the combined labour of

[032-058]innumerable insects, are by no means uncommon in the heart of Africa.

[032-059]Smeathman, a Dutch traveller of the last century, has recorded how he

[032-060]and four companions all at one time occupied the summit of one of them

[032-061]in Loundé. Livingstone noticed some made of red clay, of which the

[032-062]height varied from fifteen to twenty feet; and in Nyangwé, Cameron

[032-063]several times mistook one of these colonies for a native camp pitched

[032-064]upon the plain. He described some of these strange edifices as being

[032-065]flanked with small spires, giving them the appearance of a

[032-066]cathedral-dome.

[032-067]The reddish clay of which the ant-hill was composed could leave no

[032-068]doubt upon the mind of a naturalist that it had been formed by the

[032-069]species known as "termes bellicosus;" had it been made of grey or black

[032-070]alluvial soil, it might have been attributed to the "termes mordax" or

[032-071]"termes atrox," formidable names that must awaken anything but pleasure

[032-072]in the minds of all but enthusiast entomologists.

[032-073]In the centre was an open space, surrounded by roomy compartments,

[032-074]ranged one upon another, like the berths of a ship's cabin, and lined

[032-075]with the millions of cells that had been occupied by the ants. This

[032-076]central space was inadequate to hold the whole party that had now made

[032-077]their hurried resort to it, but as each of the compartments was

[032-078]sufficiently capacious to admit one person to occupy it in a sitting

[032-079]posture, Mrs. Weldon, Jack, Nan, and Cousin Benedict were exalted to

[032-080]the upper tier, Austin, Bat, and Actæon occupied the next story, whilst

[032-081]Tom and Hercules, and Dick Sands himself remained below.

[032-082]Dick soon found that the soil beneath his feet was beginning to get

[032-083]damp, and insisted upon having some of the dry clay spread over it from

[032-084]the base of the cone.

[032-085]"It is a long time," he said, "since we have slept with a roof over our

[032-086]heads; and I am anxious to make our refuge as secure as possible. It

[032-087]may be that we shall have to stay here for a whole day or more; on the

[032-088]first opportunity I shall go and explore; it may turn out that we are

[032-089]near the stream we are seeking; and perhaps we shall have to build a

[032-090]raft before we start again."

[032-091][Illustration: Cousin Benedict's curiosity was awakened.]

[032-092]Under his direction, therefore, Hercules took his hatchet, and

[032-093]proceeded to break down the lowest range of cells and to spread the

[032-094]dry, brittle clay of which they were composed a good foot thick over

[032-095]the damp floor, taking care not in any way to block up the aperture by

[032-096]which the fresh air penetrated into the interior.

[032-097]It was indeed fortunate that the termites had abandoned their home; had

[032-098]it swarmed with its multitudes of voracious Neuroptera, the ant-hill

[032-099]would have been utterly untenable for human beings. Cousin Benedict's

[032-100]curiosity was awakened, and he was intensely interested in the question

[032-101]of the evacuation, so that he proceeded at once to investigate, if he

[032-102]could, whether the emigration had been recent or otherwise. He took the

[032-103]lantern, and as the result of his scrutiny he soon discovered in a

[032-104]recess what he described as the termites' "storehouse," or the place

[032-105]where the indefatigable insects keep their provisions. It was a large

[032-106]cavity, not far from the royal cell, which, together with the cells for

[032-107]the reception of the young larvae, had been destroyed by Hercules in

[032-108]the course of his flooring operations. Out of this receptacle Benedict

[032-109]drew a considerable quantity of gum and vegetable juices, all in a

[032-110]state so liquid as to demonstrate that they had been deposited there

[032-111]quite recently.

[032-112]"They have only just gone," he exclaimed, with an air of authority, as

[032-113]if he imagined that some one was about to challenge his assertion.

[032-114]"We are not going to dispute your word, Mr. Benedict," said Dick; "here

[032-115]we are; we have taken their place, and shall be quite content for them

[032-116]to keep out of the way, without caring when they went, or where they

[032-117]have gone."

[032-118]"But we must care," retorted Benedict testily; "why they have gone

[032-119]concerns us a good deal; these juices make it evident, from the liquid

[032-120]state in which we find them, that the ants were here this morning, they

[032-121]have not only gone, but they have carried off their young larvae with

[032-122]them; they have been sagacious enough to take warning of some impending

[032-123]danger."

[032-124]"Perhaps they heard that we were coming," said Hercules, laughing.

[032-125]A look of withering scorn was the only answer that the entomologist

[032-126]deigned to give.

[032-127]"Yes, I say," repeated Hercules, "perhaps they heard that we were

[032-128]coming."

[032-129]"Pshaw!" said Benedict contemptuously; "do you imagine they would be

[032-130]afraid of you? they would reduce your carcase to a skeleton in no time,

[032-131]if they found it across their path."

[032-132]"No doubt, if I were dead," replied Hercules, "they could pick my bones

[032-133]pretty clean; but while I had the use of my limbs I think I could crush

[032-134]them by thousands."

[032-135]"Thousands!" ejaculated Benedict, with increasing warmth; "you think

[032-136]you could demolish thousands; but what if they were hundreds of

[032-137]thousands, millions, hundreds of millions? Alive as much as dead, I

[032-138]tell you, they wouldn't be long in consuming every morsel of you."

[032-139]During this brisk little discussion Dick Sands had been pondering over

[032-140]what Benedict had said. There was no doubt that the amateur naturalist

[032-141]was well acquainted with the habits of white ants, and if, as he

[032-142]affirmed, the insects had instinctively quitted their abode on account

[032-143]of some approaching danger, Dick asked himself whether it was safe or

[032-144]prudent for his party to remain. But the fury of the storm was still so

[032-145]great that all possibility of removing from the shelter seemed

[032-146]precluded for the present, and, without inquiring farther into the

[032-147]mystery, he merely said,

[032-148]"Although the ants, Mr. Benedict, have left us their provisions, we

[032-149]must not forget that we have brought our own. We will have our supper

[032-150]now, and to-morrow, when the storm is over, we will see what is to be

[032-151]done."

[032-152]Fatigue had not taken away the appetite of the energetic travellers,

[032-153]and they gladly set about the preparation of their meal. The

[032-154]provisions, of which they had enough for another two days, had not been

[032-155]injured by the rain. For some minutes the crunching of hard biscuit was

[032-156]the only sound to be heard; Hercules, in particular, seemed to pound

[032-157]away with his huge jaws as with a pair of millstones.

[032-158][Illustration: The naturalist now fairly mounted on a favourite hobby.]

[032-159]Mrs. Weldon was the only one of the party who ate little; and that

[032-160]little was only taken at Dick's earnest solicitation; he could not help

[032-161]noticing, with much concern, that although Jack seemed to be

[032-162]satisfactorily recovering, and, without sign of fever, was sleeping

[032-163]calmly enough on a bed made up of clothes spread out in one of the

[032-164]cells, yet his mother had lost much of her courage, and seemed

[032-165]preoccupied and depressed.

[032-166]Cousin Benedict did due honour to the simple evening repast; not on

[032-167]account of its quantity or quality, but because it gave him an

[032-168]opportunity of holding forth upon the subject of termites. He was much

[032-169]vexed that he had been unable to discover a single specimen in the

[032-170]deserted anthill with which he might illustrate his lecture, but

[032-171]notwithstanding this deficiency he continued to talk, heedless whether

[032-172]any one was listening.

[032-173]"They are wonderful insects," he said; "they belong to the order of the

[032-174]Neuroptera, which have the antennae longer than the head; their

[032-175]mandibles are well-developed, and the inferior pair of wings is

[032-176]generally as large as the superior. There are five families of them;

[032-177]the Panorpide, the Myrmellonide, the Hemerobiide, the Termitine, and

[032-178]the Perlide. I need hardly say that what we are now occupying is a

[032-179]dwelling of the Termitine."

[032-180]At this point Dick became all attention; he was anxious to ascertain

[032-181]whether this discovery of white ants had aroused any suspicion in

[032-182]Benedict's mind that they must be on African soil. The naturalist, now

[032-183]fairly mounted on a favourite hobby, went on with his discourse.

[032-184]"I am sorry not to have a specimen to show you, but these Termitine

[032-185]have four joints in the tarsi, and strong horny mandibles. The family

[032-186]includes, as genera, the Mantispa, the Raphidia, and the Termes, the

[032-187]last commonly known as white ants, amongst which are 'Termes fatalis,

[032-188]Termes lucifugans, Termes mordax,' and several others more or less

[032-189]rare."
