[012-001][[negoro|Negoro]] frowned, bit his lip, sneered, and having muttered something

[012-002]that was unintelligible, made his way back to his cabin.

[012-003]Meantime, the schooner under the freshening breeze had been carried

[012-004]beyond the shoal of the crustaceans. [[dick-sands|Dick]] cast his eye first at the

[012-005]sails, then along the deck, and seemed to become more and more alive to

[012-006]the weight of the [[duty|obligation]] that had fallen upon him; but his heart

[012-007]did not fail him; he was conscious that the hopes of the passengers

[012-008]centred in himself, and he was determined to let them see that he would

[012-009]do his best not to disappoint them.

[012-010]Although he was satisfied of his capability, with the help of the

[012-011]negroes, to manipulate the sails, he was conscious of a defect of the

[012-012]scientific knowledge which was requisite for properly controlling the

[012-013]ship's course. He felt the want of a few more years' experience. If

[012-014]only he had had longer practice he would, he thought, have been as able

[012-015]as [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] himself, to use the sextant, to take the altitude of

[012-016]the stars, to read the time from his chronometer; sun, moon, and

[012-017]planets, should have been his guides; from the firmament, as from a

[012-018]dial-plate, he would have gathered the teachings of his true position;

[012-019]but all this was beyond him as yet; his knowledge went no further than

[012-020]the use of [[ships-log|the log]] and compass, and by these alone he must be content

[012-021]to make his reckonings. But he kept up his courage, and did not permit

[012-022]himself for one moment to despair of ultimate success.

[012-023][[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] needed little penetration to recognize the thoughts which

[012-024]were passing in the mind of the resolute youth.

[012-025]"I see you have come to your decision, Dick," she said. "The command of

[012-026]the ship is in your hands; no fear but that you will do your duty; and

[012-027]Tom, and the rest of them, no doubt, will render you every assistance

[012-028]in their power."

[012-029]"Yes, Mrs. Weldon," rejoined Dick brightly; "and before long I shall

[012-030]hope to make them good seamen. If only the weather lasts fair,

[012-031]everything will go on well enough; and if the weather turns out bad, we

[012-032]must not despond; we will get safe ashore."

[012-033]He paused a moment and added reverently,--

[012-034]"God helping us."

[012-035]Mrs. Weldon proceeded to inquire whether he had any means of

[012-036]ascertaining the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]'s" present position. He replied that the

[012-037]ship's chart would at once settle that. Captain Hull had kept the

[012-038]reckoning accurately right up to the preceding day.

[012-039]"And what do you propose to do next?" she asked. "Of course you

[012-040]understand that in our present circumstances we are not in the least

[012-041]bound to go to Valparaiso if there is a nearer port which we could

[012-042]reach."

[012-043]"Certainly not," replied Dick; "and therefore it is my intention to

[012-044]sail due east, as by following that course we are sure to come upon

[012-045]some part of the American coast."

[012-046][Illustration: "Oh, we shall soon be on shore!"]

[012-047]"Do your best, Dick, to let us get ashore somewhere."

[012-048]"Never fear, madam," he answered; "as we get nearer land we shall be

[012-049]almost sure to fall in with a cruiser which will put us into the right

[012-050]track. If the wind does but remain in the north-west, and allow us to

[012-051]carry plenty of sail, we shall get on famously."

[012-052]He spoke with the cheery confidence of a good sailor who knows the good

[012-053]ship beneath his feet. He had moved off a few steps to go and take the

[012-054]helm, when Mrs. Weldon, calling him back, reminded him that he had not

[012-055]yet ascertained the true position of the schooner. Dick confessed that

[012-056]it ought to be done at once, and going to the captain's cabin brought

[012-057]out the chart upon which the ill-fated commander had marked the

[012-058]bearings the evening before. According to this dead-reckoning they were

[012-059]in lat. 43° 35', S., and long. 164° 13', W.; and as the schooner had

[012-060]made next to no progress during the last twenty-four eventful hours,

[012-061]the entry might fairly be accepted as representing approximately their

[012-062]present position.

[012-063]To the lady's inexperienced eye, as she bent over the outspread chart,

[012-064]it seemed that the land, as represented by the brown patch which

[012-065]depicted the continent of South America extending like a barrier

[012-066]between two oceans from Cape Horn to Columbia, was, after all, not so

[012-067]very far distant; the wide space of the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] was not so broad but

[012-068]that it would be quickly traversed.

[012-069]"Oh, we shall soon be on shore!" she said.

[012-070]But Dick knew better. He had acquaintance enough with the scale upon

[012-071]which the chart was constructed to be aware that the "Pilgrim" herself

[012-072]would have been a speck like a microscopic infusoria on the vast

[012-073]surface of that sea, and that hundreds and hundreds of weary miles

[012-074]separated her from the coast.

[012-075]No time was to be lost. Contrary winds had ceased to blow; a fresh

[012-076]north-westerly breeze had sprung up, and the _cirri_, or curl-cloud:

[012-077]overhead indicated that for some time at least the direction of the

[012-078]wind would be unchanged.

[012-079]Dick appealed to the negroes, and tried to make them appreciate the

[012-080]difficulty of the task that had fallen to his lot. Tom answered, in

[012-081]behalf of himself and all the rest, that they were not only willing,

[012-082]but anxious, to do all they could to assist him, saying that if their

[012-083]knowledge was small, yet their arms were strong, and added that they

[012-084]should certainly be obedient to every order he gave.

[012-085]"My friends," said Dick, addressing them in reply; "I shall make it a

[012-086]point of myself taking the helm as much as possible. But you know I

[012-087]must have my proper rest sometimes. No one can live without sleep. Now,

[012-088]Tom, I intend you to stand by me for the remainder of the day. I will

[012-089]try and make you understand how to steer by the aid of the compass. It

[012-090]is not difficult. You will soon learn. I shall have to leave you when I

[012-091]go to my hammock for an hour or two."

[012-092]"Is there nothing," said little Jack, "that I can learn to do?"

[012-093]"Oh yes, Jack; you shall keep the wind in order," answered Dick,

[012-094]smiling.

[012-095]"That I will!" cried the child, clapping his hands, while the mother

[012-096]drew him to her side.

[012-097]"And now, my men," was Dick's first order to his crew, "we must brace

[012-098]in the yards to sail fair. I will show you how."

[012-099]"All right, Captain Sands; we are at your service," said old Tom

[012-100]gravely.

[012-101][Illustration: "Oh yes, Jack; you shall keep the wind in order."]

[012-102]CHAPTER X.

[012-103]THE NEW CREW.

[012-104]Dick Sands, captain of the "Pilgrim," would not lose a moment in

[012-105]getting his ship under sail. His prime object was to land his

[012-106]passengers safely at Valparaiso or some other American port, and to

[012-107]accomplish his purpose it was in the first place necessary that he

[012-108]should ascertain the schooner's rate of speed and the direction that

[012-109]she was taking. This information was to be obtained readily enough by

[012-110]means of the log and compass, and the result of each day's observations

[012-111]would be entered regularly on the chart.

[012-112]The log on board was a patent log, with a dial-plate and screw, by

[012-113]means of which the distance that is travelled can be measured

[012-114]accurately for any definite time; it was an instrument so simple that

[012-115]the negroes were very soon taught its use. The slight error in the

[012-116]reckoning caused by the action of the currents could only be rectified

[012-117]by astronomical observations, which, as already has been stated, were

[012-118]beyond Dick's attainments to make.

[012-119]The idea more than once crossed Dick's mind whether he would not take

[012-120]the "Pilgrim" back again to [[new-zealand|New Zealand]]; the distance was considerably

[012-121]less than it was to America, and had the wind remained in the quarter

[012-122]whence it had been blowing so long, it is more than likely he would

[012-123]have determined to retrace his course. But as the wind had now veered

[012-124]to the north-west, and there was every probability that it was settled

[012-125]for a time, he came to the conclusion that he had better take advantage

[012-126]of it and persevere in making his way towards the east. Accordingly he

[012-127]lost no time in putting his ship before the wind.

[012-128]On a schooner the fore-mast usually carries four square sails; on the

[012-129]lower mast a fore-sail; on the top-mast a top-sail; on the top-gallant

[012-130]a top-gallant-sail and a royal. The main-mast carries only a main-sail

[012-131]and a top-sail. Between the masts upon the fore-stays can be hoisted a

[012-132]triple tier of triangular sails; while the bowsprit with its jib-boom

[012-133]will carry the three jibs.

[012-134]The jibs, the main-sail, the main-top-sail and the staysails are all

[012-135]managed with comparative ease, because they can be hoisted from the

[012-136]deck without the necessity of ascending the mast to let fly the

[012-137]robbins, by which they are fastened to the yards. With the sails on the

[012-138]fore-mast it is altogether a more difficult business. In order either

[012-139]to unfurl them, to take them in, or to reef them, it is necessary for a

[012-140]man to clamber up by the shrouds, either to the fore-top, or to the

[012-141]top-gallant cross-trees, and thence mounting by loose ropes, extended

[012-142]below the yards, to hold on by one hand whilst he does his work with

[012-143]the other. The operation requires alike the head and arm of an

[012-144]experienced mariner; and when a fresh breeze has been blowing, it is a

[012-145]casualty far from uncommon that a sailor, confused by the flapping of

[012-146]the canvas and the pitching of the vessel, should be blown overboard in

[012-147]the act. For the unpractised negroes the danger would necessarily be

[012-148]very great. However, the wind at present was very moderate, and the

[012-149]ship ploughed her way over the waves without any violent oscillations.

[012-150]At the time when Dick Sands, in obedience to the signal he received

[012-151]from Captain Hull, proceeded to make his way to the scene of the

[012-152]disaster, the "Pilgrim," as she lay to, was carrying only her jibs,

[012-153]main-sail, fore-sail, and fore-top-sail. In order, therefore, to put

[012-154]her as near as possible to the wind, it had been merely necessary to

[012-155]counter-brace the fore-sail yard, a manoeuvre in which the negroes had

[012-156]rendered all the assistance that was necessary. It was requisite now to

[012-157]do something more. To enable him to get straight before the wind Dick

[012-158]wanted to increase his sail, and was desirous of hoisting the

[012-159]top-gallant, the royal, the main-top-sail, and the stay-sails.

[012-160]He was himself standing at the wheel.

[012-161]"Now, my men," he shouted to the negroes; "I want your help. Do exactly

[012-162]as I tell you. Bear away, Tom!"

[012-163]Tom looked puzzled.

[012-164]"Bear away! unfasten that rope, I mean. And, Bat, come along; do the

[012-165]same as Tom."

[012-166]The men did what they were bidden.

[012-167]"That's right!" continued Dick, and calling to Hercules, said,--

[012-168]"Now, Hercules; a good strong pull!"

[012-169]To give such a direction to Hercules was somewhat imprudent; the

[012-170]rigging creaked again under his giant strength.

[012-171]"Gently, gently, my good fellow!" said Dick, laughing; "you will have

[012-172]the mast down."

[012-173]"I declare I hardly touched the rope," answered Hercules.

[012-174]"Well, next time, you must only pretend to touch it," said Dick; and,

[012-175]continuing his orders, shouted, "Now slacken! let fly! make fast! now

[012-176]brace in the yards! all right! that's capital!"

[012-177]The yards were loosened, the foresails turned slowly round, and,

[012-178]catching the breeze, gave a slight impetus to the ship. Dick's next

[012-179]orders were for the jib-sheets to be set free, and then he called the

[012-180]men to the stern.

[012-181]"Now," said he; "we must look to the main-mast; but take care,

[012-182]Hercules, not to have it down."

[012-183]"I will be as careful as possible, Mr. Dick," submissively replied

[012-184]Hercules, as though he were afraid to commit himself to any rash

[012-185]promise.
