# Whaling

The maritime industry of hunting [[right-whale|whale]]s for their oil, blubber, and [[right-whale|whale]]bone that forms the commercial backdrop of the [[mrs-weldon|Weldon]] family enterprise in *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*.

## Definition

Whaling, or "whale-fishing" as it was commonly termed in the 19th century, was the dangerous and profitable practice of hunting [[right-whale|whale]]s at sea. Whale oil was used for lighting and lubrication, while [[right-whale|whale]]bone (baleen) was a valuable material for products ranging from corset stays to umbrella ribs. The industry employed large crews on long voyages, typically lasting several years.

## Role in the Novel

The [[mrs-weldon|Weldon]] family fortune is built on whaling. James W. [[mrs-weldon|Weldon]], [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]'s husband, "was accustomed every season to send his [[right-whale|whale]]rs both to the Arctic and Antarctic seas" (001-197). The *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*, under [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]]'s command, is returning from a whaling expedition in the southern seas when the novel opens.

The whaling industry's decline provides key context. Whales had become scarce in accessible waters: "the [[right-whale|whale]] known as '[[right-whale|the Right whale]],' the object of repeated attacks, had become very scarce" (001-230). [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]]'s disappointing catch was the reason for his early return: "the supply of [[right-whale|whale]]s that [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] had procured had fallen far short of his expectations" (001-236). The ship carries "an ample cargo of cut and uncut [[right-whale|whale]]bone" but insufficient [[right-whale|whale]] oil to justify a full season (001-227).

The *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]* carries specialized equipment for the trade: "[[right-whale|whale]]-boats" for pursuit and harpooning, and facilities for "cutting up the [[right-whale|whale]]s on board" (001-215–216). [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] had difficulty "engaging as many [[right-whale|whale]]men as he wanted," reflecting the labor market pressures on the declining industry (001-221).

## Technical Details

The process of "whale-fishing" required substantial crew and equipment:
- **Whale-boats**: Small, fast boats launched from the main vessel for pursuit
- **Harpoons and lances**: For striking and killing the [[right-whale|whale]]
- **Cutting-in and trying-out**: Processing the carcass at sea—"cutting up the [[right-whale|whale]]s on board" (001-216)
- **Products**: Whale oil for lighting, [[right-whale|whale]]bone (baleen) for manufactured goods (001-227)

## Historical Context

By 1878, the whaling industry was in transition. The discovery of petroleum in Pennsylvania (1859) had begun to replace [[right-whale|whale]] oil for lighting. Overfishing had depleted [[right-whale|whale]] populations in traditional grounds. The *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]*'s disappointing season reflects these realities. Verne accurately portrays an industry in decline, where profit margins were shrinking and "[[right-whale|whale]]rs, so far from entertaining a" profitable return, faced uncertain futures (002-057).
