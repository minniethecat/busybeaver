# Pacific Ocean

The ocean traversed by the *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]* during the entirety of Part I. The schooner sails from [[auckland|Auckland]], [[new-zealand|New Zealand]], bound for [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] via [[valparaiso|Valparaiso]], crossing the Pacific's vast and largely empty expanse before the [[right-whale|whale]]-hunt disaster.

## Geography

- **Type**: Ocean
- **Real or fictional**: Real
- **Relevant to**: [[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]'s voyage / Part I setting / [[right-whale|whale]]-hunt disaster
- **First mentioned**: Chapter 1 (PN=001-209)

## Role in the Narrative

The Pacific Ocean is the setting for all the maritime action of Part I. The *[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]* crosses "the wide space of the Pacific" (PN=012-067) on what should be a routine whaling voyage. The ocean is described as "almost deserted" (PN=008-061) in the latitudes the schooner follows — a vast emptiness that isolates the crew and intensifies the crisis when it comes.

The stretch of the Pacific between [[new-zealand|New Zealand]] and South America becomes the theater for the novel's turning point: the [[right-whale|whale]] hunt that kills [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] and all but one of the crew, leaving the fifteen-year-old [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] in command. The ship becomes "a little speck in the midst of the Pacific" (PN=016-164), and the survivors' sense of isolation shapes the psycho[[ships-log|log]]ical tension of the narrative.

Verne treats the Pacific with poetic reverence, noting that "the poetry of the ocean breathes forth in its minute" (PN=008-068), while also emphasizing the practical dangers of the world's largest body of water — its distance, its deceptively calm surface, and its capacity to swallow a ship without a trace.

## Description in the Text

The Pacific is portrayed as both beautiful and treacherous. Verne notes that "this portion of the Pacific is almost deserted" (PN=008-061), underscoring the isolation of the ship. The vast expanse is so wide that "the wide space of the Pacific was not so broad but" that a competent navigator could find safe passage (PN=012-067). After the disaster, the ship becomes "but a little speck in the midst of the Pacific" (PN=016-164).

> "The ocean was all deserted. Since the departure from New Zealand, no sail had been sighted." (PN=011-152)

The Pacific also represents the frontier between the known and unknown worlds. The characters believe they are crossing toward South America; in reality, [[negoro|Negoro]] has altered their course, and the same Pacific waters carry them not to the American continent but toward the coast of [[africa|Africa]].

## Historical Context

In 1878, the Pacific was still a frontier ocean for commercial whaling. American whaling ships operated out of ports like [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] and [[nan|Nan]]tucket, hunting sperm [[right-whale|whale]]s across the Pacific for oil and [[right-whale|whale]]bone. The Pacific [[navigation|Navigation]] Company (PN=017-135) provided steamer service along the South American coast. Verne's descriptions reflect contemporary knowledge of Pacific currents, winds, and the geography of the whaling grounds in the southern hemisphere.
