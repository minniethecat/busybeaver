# Zanzibar

**Zanzibar** is an island off the coast of East [[africa|Africa]] that served as the principal hub of the 19th-century Indian Ocean [[slavery|slave trade]] and as a base for European exploration of the [[africa|Africa]]n interior. In *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*, it appears throughout the narrative as both a feared destination for captured slaves and a geographic reference point in Verne's incorporation of contemporary [[exploration|African exploration]] history.

## Geography

- **Type**: Island (and associated sultanate)
- **Real or fictional**: Real
- **First mentioned**: Chapter 26 (026-202)

Zanzibar lies in the Indian Ocean off the coast of present-day Tanzania. In the 19th century, the Sultanate of Zanzibar controlled not only the island itself but substantial territory on the East [[africa|Africa]]n mainland, including key trading posts that channeled slaves and ivory from the interior to the coast. Its geographic position made it the nexus between the [[africa|Africa]]n Great Lakes region and the maritime trade routes of the Indian Ocean.

## The Slave Trade

Zanzibar was the eastern terminus of the [[africa|Africa]]n [[slavery|slave trade]], complementing [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] routes that passed through [[angola|Angola]] on the western coast. Verne depicts it as one of the great slave-market destinations, alongside Khartoum and Natal. Captured slaves faced the prospect of being "sent to the markets of Khartoom, Natal, and Zanzibar" (035-067), while others might be dispatched to "Upper Egypt or Zanzibar" (034-193).

> "The caravan was destined for the district which feeds the market of Zanzibar, in Bornu and Fezzan." (026-202)

The slave-raiding operations — "razzias" — were "perpetrated by the officers of the Sultan of Zanzibar" (027-110), underscoring the institutional and state-sanctioned nature of the trade. The [[slave-caravan|caravan]]'s captives, after reaching the interior markets, could ultimately be "sent off to Zanzibar" (041-141) for export.

Verne notes the commercial sophistication of the Zanzibar trade, referencing "the picé, a Zanzibar coin worth something more than a French halfpenny" (039-090) — a detail that grounds the narrative in the real economics of the East [[africa|Africa]]n slave markets. Slaves were assessed by the "deptôs of Zanzibar" (039-169) and those deemed profitable were "sent them off at once" (039-179) for sale.

## Livingstone and European Exploration

Verne weaves the history of [[exploration|African exploration]] into the narrative through Zanzibar's role as a base for expeditions into the interior. David Livingstone's connection to the island appears repeatedly:

- **First expedition**: "Robert Moffat, a nephew of Livingstone, he started from Zanzibar" (027-080), connecting the great explorer's circle to the island.
- **Fourth expedition**: Livingstone was in "Zanzibar at the commencement of 1866, ready to begin his fourth" (044-103) journey into the interior.
- **Return and death**: Livingstone "crossed over to Zanzibar, and after five years' absence" (044-099) returned, but his porters ultimately "deserted him, and returned to Zanzibar with the report that he was dead" (044-108).
- **Final journey**: After Livingstone's death in 1873, his body "was brought to Zanzibar, whence, nine months after his death" (045-014) it was transported back to England for burial in Westminster Abbey.

The island also served other explorers: "Zanzibar, and the lake-district had been invaded by Speke and Grant" (043-158), two British explorers who confirmed Lake Victoria as the source of the Nile during expeditions launched from the Zanzibar coast (1860–1863).

## Geographic Reach

Zanzibar's influence extended across the entire East [[africa|Africa]]n interior through a network of trade routes connecting the Great Lakes region to the coast. Verne's references to "the people of Zanzibar" (039-034) as a distinct commercial class reflect the historical reality of Swahili-Arab traders who operated far into the interior, organizing the [[slave-caravan|caravan]]s that brought slaves and ivory to the coast. The parallel between Zanzibar's eastern trade and [[angola|Angola]]'s western trade structures the novel's geographic imagination: [[africa|Africa]] is depicted as a continent penetrated from both coasts by commercial networks that exploit its people.
