[003-001]"But my wish exonerates you," said [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]].

[003-002]"Besides," added [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]], "I am unable to provide you with the

[003-003]accommodation and the comfort that you would have upon a passenger

[003-004]steamer."

[003-005]"You know well enough, captain," remonstrated the lady "that my husband

[003-006]would not hesitate for a moment to trust his wife and child on board

[003-007]the '[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]].'"

[003-008]"Trust, madam! No! no more than I should myself. I repeat that the

[003-009]'Pilgrim' cannot afford you the comfort to which you are accustomed."

[003-010]Mrs. Weldon smiled.

[003-011]"Oh, I am not one of your grumbling travellers. I shall have no

[003-012]complaints to make either of small cramped cabins, or of rough and

[003-013]meagre food."

[003-014]She took her son by the hand, and passing on, begged that they might

[003-015]start forthwith.

[003-016]Orders accordingly were given; sails were trimmed; and after taking the

[003-017]shortest course across the gulf, the "Pilgrim" turned her head towards

[003-018]America.

[003-019]Three days later strong easterly breezes compelled the schooner to tack

[003-020]to larboard in order to get to windward. The consequence was that by

[003-021]the 2nd of February the captain found himself in such a latitude that

[003-022]he might almost be suspected of intending to round Cape Horn rather

[003-023]than of having a design to coast the western shores of the New

[003-024]Continent.

[003-025]Still, the sea did not become rough. There was a slight delay, but, on

[003-026]the whole, navigation was perfectly easy.

[003-027]CHAPTER II.

[003-028]THE APPRENTICE.

[003-029]There was no poop upon the "Pilgrim's" deck, so that Mrs. Weldon had no

[003-030]alternative than to acquiesce in the captain's proposal that she should

[003-031]occupy his own modest cabin.

[003-032]Accordingly, here she was installed with Jack and old Nan; and here she

[003-033]took all her meals, in company with the captain and Cousin Benedict.

[003-034]For Cousin Benedict tolerably comfortable sleeping accommodation had

[003-035]been contrived close at hand, while Captain Hull himself retired to the

[003-036]crew's quarter, occupying the cabin which properly belonged to the

[003-037]chief mate, but as already indicated, the services of a second officer

[003-038]were quite dispensed with.

[003-039]All the crew were civil and attentive to the wife of their employer, a

[003-040]master to whom they were faithfully attached. They were all natives of

[003-041]the coast of California, brave and experienced seamen, and united by

[003-042]tastes and habits in a common bond of sympathy. Few as they were in

[003-043]number, their work was never shirked, not simply from the sense of

[003-044]duty, but because they were directly interested in the profits of their

[003-045]undertaking; the success of their labours always told to their own

[003-046]advantage. The present expedition was the fourth that they had taken

[003-047]together; and, as it turned out to be the first in which they had

[003-048]failed to meet with success, it may be imagined that they were full of

[003-049]resentment against the mutinous whalemen who had been the cause of so

[003-050]serious a diminution of their ordinary gains.

[003-051][Illustration: [[negoro|Negoro]].]

[003-052]The only one on board who was not an American was a man who had been

[003-053]temporarily engaged as cook. His name was Negoro; he was a Portuguese

[003-054]by birth, but spoke English with perfect fluency. The previous cook had

[003-055]deserted the ship at Auckland, and when Negoro, who was out of

[003-056]employment, applied for the place, Captain Hull, only too glad to avoid

[003-057]detention, engaged him at once without inquiry into his antecedents.

[003-058]There was not the slightest fault to be found with the way in which the

[003-059]cook performed his duties, but there was something in his manner, or

[003-060]perhaps, rather in the expression of his countenance, which excited the

[003-061]Captain's misgivings, and made him regret that he had not taken more

[003-062]pains to investigate the character of one with whom he was now brought

[003-063]into such close contact.

[003-064]Negoro looked about forty years of age. Although he had the appearance

[003-065]of being slightly built, he was muscular; he was of middle height, and

[003-066]seemed to have a robust constitution; his hair was dark, his complexion

[003-067]somewhat swarthy. His manner was taciturn, and although, from

[003-068]occasional remarks that he dropped, it was evident that he had received

[003-069]some education, he was very reserved on the subjects both of his family

[003-070]and of his past life. No one knew where he had come from, and he

[003-071]admitted no one to his confidence as to where he was going, except that

[003-072]he made no secret of his intention to land at Valparaiso. His freedom

[003-073]from sea-sickness demonstrated that this could hardly be his first

[003-074]voyage, but on the other hand his complete ignorance of seamen's

[003-075]phraseology made it certain that he had never been accustomed to his

[003-076]present occupation. He kept himself aloof as much as possible from the

[003-077]rest of the crew, during the day rarely leaving the great cast-iron

[003-078]stove, which was out of proportion to the measurement of the cramped

[003-079]little kitchen; and at night, as soon as the fire was extinguished,

[003-080]took the earliest opportunity of retiring to his berth and going to

[003-081]sleep.

[003-082]It has been already stated that the crew of the "Pilgrim" consisted of

[003-083]five seamen and an apprentice. This apprentice was [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]].

[003-084]Dick was fifteen years old; he was a foundling, his unknown parents

[003-085]having abandoned him at his birth, and he had been brought up in a

[003-086]public charitable institution. He had been called Dick, after the

[003-087]benevolent passer-by who had discovered him when he was but an infant a

[003-088]few hours old, and he had received the surname of Sands as a memorial

[003-089]of the spot where he had been exposed, Sandy Hook, a point at the mouth

[003-090]of the Hudson, where it forms an entrance to the harbour of New York.

[003-091]As Dick was so young it was most likely he would yet grow a little

[003-092]taller, but it did not seem probable that he would ever exceed middle

[003-093]height, he looked too stoutly and strongly built to grow much. His

[003-094]complexion was dark, but his beaming blue eyes attested, with scarcely

[003-095]room for doubt, his Anglo-Saxon origin, and his countenance betokened

[003-096]energy and intelligence. The profession that he had adopted seemed to

[003-097]have equipped him betimes for fighting the battle of life.

[003-098]Misquoted often as Virgil's are the words

[003-099]"Audaces fortuna juvat!"

[003-100]but the true reading is

[003-101]"Audentes fortuna juvat!"

[003-102]and, slight as the difference may seem, it is very significant. It is

[003-103]upon the confident rather than the rash, the daring rather than the

[003-104]bold, that Fortune sheds her smiles; the bold man often acts without

[003-105]thinking, whilst the daring always thinks before he acts.

[003-106]And Dick Sands was truly courageous; he was one of the daring. At

[003-107]fifteen years old, an age at which few boys have laid aside the

[003-108]frivolities of childhood, he had acquired the stability of a man, and

[003-109]the most casual observer could scarcely fail to be attracted by his

[003-110]bright, yet thoughtful countenance. At an early period of his life he

[003-111]had realized all the difficulties of his position, and had made a

[003-112]resolution, from which nothing tempted him to flinch, that he would

[003-113]carve out for himself an honourable and independent career. Lithe and

[003-114]agile in his movements, he was an adept in every kind of athletic

[003-115]exercise; and so marvellous was his success in everything he undertook,

[003-116]that he might almost be supposed to be one of those gifted mortals who

[003-117]have two right hands and two left feet.

[003-118]Until he was four years old the little orphan had found a home in one

[003-119]of those institutions in America where forsaken children are sure of an

[003-120]asylum, and he was subsequently sent to an industrial school supported

[003-121]by charitable aid, where he learnt reading, writing, and arithmetic.

[003-122]From the days of infancy he had never deviated from the expression of

[003-123]his wish to be a sailor, and accordingly, as soon as he was eight, he

[003-124]was placed as cabin-boy on board one of the ships that navigate the

[003-125]Southern Seas. The officers all took a peculiar interest in him, and he

[003-126]received, in consequence, a thoroughly good grounding in the duties and

[003-127]discipline of a seaman's life. There was no room to doubt that he must

[003-128]ultimately rise to eminence in his profession, for when a child from

[003-129]the very first has been trained in the knowledge that he must gain his

[003-130]bread by the sweat of his brow, it is comparatively rare that he lacks

[003-131]the will to do so.

[003-132]Whilst he was still acting as cabin-boy on one of those

[003-133]trading-vessels, Dick attracted the notice of Captain Hull, who took a

[003-134]fancy to the lad and introduced him to his employer. Mr. Weldon at once

[003-135]took a lively interest in Dick's welfare, and had his education

[003-136]continued in [[san-francisco|San Francisco]], taking care that he was instructed in the

[003-137]doctrines of the Roman Catholic Church, to which his own family

[003-138]belonged.

[003-139]Throughout his studies Dick Sands' favourite subjects were always those

[003-140]which had a reference to his future profession; he mastered the details

[003-141]of the geography of the world; he applied himself diligently to such

[003-142]branches of mathematics as were necessary for the science of

[003-143]navigation; whilst for recreation in his hours of leisure, he would

[003-144]greedily devour every book of adventure in travel that came in his way.

[003-145]Nor did he omit duly to combine the practical with the theoretical; and

[003-146]when he was bound apprentice on board the "Pilgrim," a vessel not only

[003-147]belonging to his benefactor, but under the command of his kind friend

[003-148]Captain Hull, he congratulated himself most heartily, and felt that the

[003-149]experience he should gain in the southern [[right-whale|whale]]-fisheries could hardly

[003-150]fail to be of service to him in after-life. A first-rate sailor ought

[003-151]to be a first-rate fisherman too.

[003-152]It was a matter of the greatest pleasure to Dick Sands when he heard to

[003-153]his surprise that Mrs. Weldon was about to become a passenger on board

[003-154]the "Pilgrim." His devotion to the family of his benefactor was large

[003-155]and genuine. For several years Mrs. Weldon had acted towards him little

[003-156]short of a mother's part, and for Jack, although he never forgot the

[003-157]difference in their position, he entertained well-nigh a brother's

[003-158]affection. His friends had the satisfaction of being assured that they

[003-159]had sown the seeds of kindness on a generous soil, for there was no

[003-160]room to doubt that the heart of the orphan boy was overflowing with

[003-161]sincere gratitude. Should the occasion arise, ought he not, he asked,

[003-162]to be ready to sacrifice everything in behalf of those to whom he was

[003-163]indebted not only for his start in life, but for the knowledge of all

[003-164]that was right and holy?

[003-165]Confiding in the good principles of her protégé, Mrs. Weldon had no

[003-166]hesitation in entrusting her little son to his especial charge. During

[003-167]the frequent periods of leisure, when the sea was fair, and the sails

[003-168]required no shifting, the apprentice was never weary of amusing Jack by

[003-169]making him familiar with the practice of a sailor's craft; he made him

[003-170]scramble up the shrouds, perch upon the yards, and slip down the

[003-171]back-stays; and the mother had no alarm; her assurance of Dick Sands'

[003-172]ability and watchfulness to protect her boy was so complete that she

[003-173]could only rejoice in an occupation for him that seemed more than

[003-174]anything to restore the colour he had lost in his recent illness.

[003-175]Time passed on without incident; and had it not been for the constant

[003-176]prevalence of an adverse wind, neither passengers nor crew could have

[003-177]found the least cause of complaint. The pertinacity, however, with

[003-178]which the wind kept to the east could not do otherwise than make

[003-179]Captain Hull somewhat concerned; it absolutely prevented him from

[003-180]getting his ship into her proper course, and he could not altogether

[003-181]suppress his misgiving that the calms near the Tropic of Capricorn, and

[003-182]the equatorial current driving him on westwards, would entail a delay

[003-183]that might be serious.
