[031-001]But it soon became evident that elephants were not the only living

[031-002]creatures that had used this track. Human bones gnawed by beasts of

[031-003]prey, whole human skeletons, still wearing the iron fetters of slavery,

[031-004]everywhere strewed the ground. It was a scene only too common in

[031-005][[africa|Central Africa]], where like cattle driven to the slaughter, poor

[031-006]miserable men are dragged in caravans for hundreds of weary miles, to

[031-007]perish on the road in countless numbers beneath the trader's lash, to

[031-008]succumb to the mingled horrors of fatigue, privation, and disease, or,

[031-009]if provisions fail, to be butchered, without pity or remorse, by sword

[031-010]and gun.

[031-011]That slave-caravans had passed that way was too obvious to permit a

[031-012]doubt. For at least a mile, at almost every step [[dick-sands|Dick]] came in contact

[031-013]with the scattered bones; while ever and again huge goat suckers,

[031-014]disturbed by the approach of the travellers, rose with flapping wings,

[031-015]and circled round their heads.

[031-016]The youth's heart sank with secret dismay lest [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] should

[031-017]divine the meaning of this ghastly scene, and appeal to him for

[031-018]explanation, but fortunately she had again insisted on carrying her

[031-019]little patient, and although the child was fast asleep, he absorbed her

[031-020]whole attention. Nan was by her side, almost equally engrossed. Old Tom

[031-021]alone was fully alive to the significance of his surroundings, and with

[031-022]downcast eyes he mournfully pursued his march. Full of amazement, the

[031-023]other negroes looked right and left upon what might appear to them as

[031-024]the upheaval of some vast cemetery, but they uttered no word of inquiry

[031-025]or surprise.

[031-026]Meantime the bed of the stream had increased both in breadth and depth,

[031-027]and the rivulet had in a degree lost its character of a rushing

[031-028]torrent. This was a change which Dick Sands observed hopefully,

[031-029]interpreting it as an indication that it might itself become navigable,

[031-030]or would empty itself into some more important tributary of the

[031-031]Atlantic. His resolve was fixed: he would follow its course at all

[031-032]hazards. As soon, therefore, as he found that the elephant's track was

[031-033]quitting the water's edge, he made up his mind to abandon it, and had

[031-034]no hesitation in again resorting to the use of the axe. Once more,

[031-035]then, commenced the labour of cutting a way through the entanglement of

[031-036]bushes and creepers that were thick upon the soil. It was no longer

[031-037]forest through which they were wending their arduous path; trees were

[031-038]comparatively rare; only tall clumps of bamboos rose above the grass,

[031-039]so high, however, that even Hercules could not see above them, and the

[031-040]passage of the little troop could only have been discovered by the

[031-041]rustling in the stalks.

[031-042]In the course of the afternoon, the soil became soft and marshy. It was

[031-043]evident that the travellers were crossing plains that in a long rainy

[031-044]season must be inundated. The ground was carpeted with luxuriant mosses

[031-045]and graceful ferns, and the continual appearance of brown hematite

[031-046]wherever there was a rise in the soil, betokened the existence of a

[031-047]rich vein of metal beneath.

[031-048]Remembering what he had read in Dr. Livingstone's account of these

[031-049]treacherous swamps, Dick bade his companions take their footing warily.

[031-050]He himself led the way. Tom expressed his surprise that the ground

[031-051]should be so soaked when there had been no rain for some time.

[031-052]"I think we shall have a storm soon," said Bat,

[031-053]"All the more reason, then," replied Dick, "why we should get away from

[031-054]these marshes as quickly as possible. Carry Jack again, Hercules; and

[031-055]you, Bat and Austin, keep close to Mrs. Weldon, so as to be able to

[031-056]assist her if she wants your help. But take care, take care, Mr.

[031-057]Benedict!" he cried out in sudden alarm; "what are you doing, sir?"

[031-058]"I'm slipping in," was poor Benedict's helpless reply. He had trodden

[031-059]upon a kind of quagmire and, as though a trap had been opened beneath

[031-060]his feet, was fast disappearing into the slough. Assistance was

[031-061]immediately rendered, and the unfortunate naturalist was dragged out,

[031-062]covered with mud almost to his waist, but thoroughly satisfied because

[031-063]his precious box of specimens had suffered no injury. Actæon undertook

[031-064]for the future to keep close to his side, and endeavour to avoid a

[031-065]repetition of the mishap.

[031-066]The accident could not be said to be altogether free from unpleasant

[031-067]consequences. Air-bubbles in great numbers had risen to the surface of

[031-068]the mire from which Benedict had been extricated, and as they burst

[031-069]they disseminated an odious stench that was well-nigh intolerable. The

[031-070]passage of these pestilential districts is not unfrequently very

[031-071]dangerous, and Livingstone, who on several occasions waded through them

[031-072]in mud that reached to his breast, compares them to great sponges

[031-073]composed of black porous earth, in which every footstep causes streams

[031-074]of moisture to ooze out.

[031-075]For well nigh half a mile they had now to wend their cautious way

[031-076]across this spongy soil. Mrs. Weldon, ankle-deep in the soft mud, was

[031-077]at last compelled to come to a stand-still; and Hercules, Bat, and

[031-078]Austin, all resolved that she should be spared further discomfort, and

[031-079]insisted upon weaving some bamboos into a litter, upon which, after

[031-080]much reluctance to become such a burden, she was induced, with Jack

[031-081]beside her, to take her place.

[031-082]After the delay thus caused, the procession again started on its

[031-083]perilous route. Dick Sands continued to walk at the head, in order to

[031-084]test the stability of the footing; Action followed, holding Cousin

[031-085]Benedict firmly by the arm; Tom took charge of old Nan, who without his

[031-086]support would certainly have fallen into the quagmire; and the three

[031-087]other negroes carried the litter in the rear. It was a matter of the

[031-088]greatest difficulty to find a path that was sufficiently firm; the

[031-089]method they adopted was to pick their way as much as possible on the

[031-090]long rank grass that on the margin of the swamps was tolerably tough;

[031-091]but in spite of the greatest precaution, there was not one of them who

[031-092]escaped occasionally sinking up to his knees in slush.

[031-093]At about five o'clock they were relieved by finding themselves on

[031-094]ground of a more clayey character; it was still soft and porous below,

[031-095]but its surface was hard enough to give a secure foothold. There were

[031-096]watery pores that percolated the subsoil, and these gave evident

[031-097]witness to the proximity of a river-district.

[031-098]The heat would have been intolerably oppressive if it had not been

[031-099]tempered by some heavy storm-clouds which obstructed the direct

[031-100]influence of the sun's rays. Lightning was observed to be playing

[031-101]faintly about the sky, and there was now and again the low growl of

[031-102]distant thunder. The indications of a gathering storm were too manifest

[031-103]to be disregarded, and Dick could not help being very uneasy. He had

[031-104]heard of the extreme violence of African storms, and knew that torrents

[031-105]of rain, hurricanes that no tree could resist, and thunderbolt after

[031-106]thunderbolt were the usual accompaniment of these tempests. And here in

[031-107]this lowland desert, which too surely would be completely inundated,

[031-108]there would not be a tree to which they could resort for shelter, while

[031-109]it would likewise be utterly vain to hope to obtain a refuge by

[031-110]excavation, as water would be found only two feet below the surface.

[031-111][Illustration: Another brilliant flash brought the camp once again into

[031-112]relief.]

[031-113]After scrutinizing the landscape, however, he noticed some low

[031-114]elevations on the north that seemed to form the boundary of the marshy

[031-115]plain. A few trees were scattered along their summits; if his party

[031-116]could get no other shelter here, he hoped they would be able to find

[031-117]themselves free from any danger caused by the rising flood.

[031-118]"Push on, friends, push on!" he cried; "three miles more, and we shall

[031-119]be out of this treacherous lowland."

[031-120]His words served to inspire a fresh confidence, and in spite of all the

[031-121]previous fatigue, every energy was brought into play with renewed

[031-122]vigour. Hercules, in particular, seemed ready to carry the whole party,

[031-123]if it had been in his power.

[031-124]The storm was not long in beginning. The rising ground was still two

[031-125]miles away. Although the sun was above the horizon, the darkness was

[031-126]almost complete; the overhanging volumes of vapour sank lower and lower

[031-127]towards the earth, but happily the full force of the deluge which must

[031-128]ultimately come did not descend as yet. Lightning, red and blue,

[031-129]flashed on every side and appeared to cover the ground with a network

[031-130]of flame.

[031-131]Ever and again the little knot of travellers were in peril of being

[031-132]struck by the thunderbolts which, on that treeless plain, had no other

[031-133]object of attraction. Poor little Jack, who had been awakened by the

[031-134]perpetual crashes, buried his face in terror in Hercules' breast,

[031-135]anxious, however, not to distress his mother by any outward exhibition

[031-136]of alarm. The good-natured negro endeavoured to pacify him by promises

[031-137]that the lightning should not touch him, and the child, ever confident

[031-138]in the protection of his huge friend, lost something of his nervousness.

[031-139]But it could not be long before the clouds would burst and discharge

[031-140]the threatened down-pour.

[031-141]"What are we to do, Tom?" asked Dick, drawing up close to the negro's

[031-142]side.

[031-143]"We must make a rush for it; push on with all the speed we can."

[031-144]"But where?" cried Dick.

[031-145]"Straight on," was the prompt reply; "if the rain catches us here on

[031-146]the plain we shall all be drowned."

[031-147]"But where are we to go?" repeated Dick, in despair; "if only there

[031-148]were a hut! But look, look there!"

[031-149]A vivid flash of lightning had lit up the country, and Dick declared

[031-150]that he could see a camp which could hardly be more than a quarter of a

[031-151]mile ahead.

[031-152]The negro looked doubtful.

[031-153]"I saw it too," he assented: "but if it be a camp at all it would be a

[031-154]camp of natives; and to fall into that would involve us in a worse fate

[031-155]than the rain."

[031-156]Another brilliant flash brought the camp once again into relief; it

[031-157]appeared to be made up of about a hundred conical tents, arranged very

[031-158]symmetrically, each of them being from twelve to fifteen feet in

[031-159]height. It had the appearance, from a distance, of being deserted; if

[031-160]it were really so, it would afford just the shelter that was needed;

[031-161]otherwise, at all hazards, it must be most carefully avoided.

[031-162]"I will go in advance," said Dick, after a moment's reflection, "and

[031-163]reconnoitre it."

[031-164]"Let one of us, at least, go with you," replied Tom.

[031-165]"No, stay where you are; I shall be much less likely to be discovered

[031-166]if I go alone."

[031-167]Without another word, he darted off, and was soon lost in the sombre

[031-168]darkness that was only broken by the frequent lightning.

[031-169]Large drops of rain were now beginning to fall.

[031-170]Tom and Dick had been walking some little distance in advance of the

[031-171]rest of the party, who consequently had not overheard their

[031-172]conversation. A halt being made, Mrs. Weldon inquired what was the

[031-173]matter. Tom explained that a camp or village had been noticed a little

[031-174]way in front, and that the captain had gone forward to investigate it.

[031-175]Mrs. Weldon asked no further questions, but quietly waited the result.

[031-176]It was only a few minutes before Dick returned.

[031-177]"You may come on," he cried.

[031-178]"Is the camp deserted?" asked Tom.

[031-179]"It is not a camp at all; it is a lot of ant-hills!"

[031-180]"Ant-hills!" echoed Benedict, suddenly aroused into a state of

[031-181]excitement.

[031-182][Illustration: One after another, the whole party made their way inside]

[031-183]"No doubt of it, Mr. Benedict." replied Dick; "they are ant-hills

[031-184]twelve feet high at least: and I hope we shall be able to get into

[031-185]them."

[031-186]"Twelve feet!" the naturalist repeated; "they must be those of the

[031-187]termites, the white ants; there is no other insect that could make

[031-188]them. Wonderful architects are the termites."
