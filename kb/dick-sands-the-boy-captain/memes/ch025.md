[025-001]"A snake!" exclaimed [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] in alarm.

[025-002]"No, no, cousin, better than that! it was not a snake; I believe it was

[025-003]an orthoptera; I have it all right," he shouted triumphantly.

[025-004]"Then kill it quickly, sir; and let us go to sleep again in peace,"

[025-005]said Harris.

[025-006]"Kill it! not for the world! I must have a light, and look at it!"

[025-007][[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] indulged him, for reasons of his own, in getting a light.

[025-008]The entomologist carefully opened his hand and displayed an insect

[025-009]somewhat smaller than a bee, of a dull colour, streaked with yellow on

[025-010]the under portion of the body. He looked radiant with delight.

[025-011]"A diptera!" he exclaimed, half beside himself with joy, "a most famous

[025-012]diptera!"

[025-013]"Is it venomous?" asked Mrs. Weldon.

[025-014]"Not at all to men; it only hurts elephants and buffaloes."

[025-015]"But tell us its name! what is it?" cried Dick impetuously.

[025-016]The naturalist began to speak in a slow, oracular tone.

[025-017]"This insect is here a prodigy; it is an insect totally unknown in this

[025-018]country,--in America."

[025-019]"Tell us its name!" roared Dick.

[025-020]"It is a tzetzy, sir, a true tzetzy."

[025-021]Dick's heart sank like a stone. He was speechless. He did not, dared

[025-022]not, ask more. Only too well he knew where the tzetzy could alone be

[025-023]found. He did not close his eyes again that night.

[025-024]CHAPTER XVIII.

[025-025]A TERRIBLE DISCOVERY.

[025-026]The morning of the 18th dawned, the day on which, according to Harris's

[025-027]prediction, the travellers were to be safely housed at San Felice. Mrs.

[025-028]Weldon was really much relieved at the prospect, for she was aware that

[025-029]her strength must prove inadequate to the strain of a more protracted

[025-030]journey. The condition of her little boy, who was alternately flushed

[025-031]with fever, and pale with exhaustion, had begun to cause her great

[025-032]anxiety, and unwilling to resign the care of the child even to Nan his

[025-033]faithful nurse, she insisted upon carrying him in her own arms. Twelve

[025-034]days and nights, passed in the open air, had done much to try her

[025-035]powers of endurance, and the charge of a sick child in addition would

[025-036]soon break down her strength entirely.

[025-037]Dick Sands, Nan, and the negroes had all borne the march very fairly.

[025-038]Their stock of provisions, though of course considerably diminished,

[025-039]was still far from small. As for Harris, he had shown himself

[025-040]pre-eminently adapted for forest-life, and capable of bearing any

[025-041]amount of fatigue. Yet, strange to say, as he approached the end of the

[025-042]journey, his manner underwent a remarkable change; instead of

[025-043]conversing in his ordinary frank and easy way, he became silent and

[025-044]preoccupied, as if engrossed in his own thoughts. Perhaps he had an

[025-045]instinctive consciousness that "his young friend," as he was in the

[025-046]habit of addressing Dick, was entertaining hard suspicions about him.

[025-047]The march was resumed. The trees once again ceased to be crowded in

[025-048]impenetrable masses, but stood in clusters at considerable distances

[025-049]apart. Now, Dick tried to argue with himself, they must be coming to

[025-050]the true pampas, or the man must be designedly misleading them; and yet

[025-051]what motive could he have?

[025-052]Although during the earlier part of the day there occurred nothing that

[025-053]could be said absolutely to justify Dick's increasing uneasiness, two

[025-054]circumstances transpired which did not escape his observation, and

[025-055]which, he felt, might be significant. The first of these was a sudden

[025-056]change in [[dingo|Dingo]]'s behaviour. The dog, throughout the march, had

[025-057]uniformly run along with his nose upon the ground, smelling the grass

[025-058]and shrubs, and occasionally uttering a sad low whine; but to-day he

[025-059]seemed all agitation; he scampered about with bristling coat, with his

[025-060]head erect, and ever and again burst into one of those furious fits of

[025-061]barking, with which he had formerly been accustomed to greet [[negoro|Negoro]]'s

[025-062]appearance upon the deck of the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]."

[025-063]The idea that flitted across Dick's mind was shared by Tom.

[025-064]"Look, Mr. Dick, look at Dingo; he is at his old ways again," said he;

[025-065]"it is just as if Negoro...."

[025-066]"Hush!" said Dick to the old man, who continued in a lower voice,--

[025-067]"It is just as if Negoro had followed us; do you think it is likely?"

[025-068]"It might perhaps be to his advantage to follow us, if he doesn't know

[025-069]the country; but if he does know the country, why then...."

[025-070]Dick did not finish his sentence, but whistled to Dingo. The dog

[025-071]reluctantly obeyed the call.

[025-072]As soon as the dog was at his side, Dick patted him, repeating,--

[025-073]"Good dog! good Dingo! where's Negoro?"

[025-074]The sound of Negoro's name had its usual effect; it seemed to irritate

[025-075]the animal exceedingly, and he barked furiously, and apparently wanted

[025-076]to dash into the thicket.

[025-077]Harris had been an interested spectator of the scene, and now

[025-078]approached with a peculiar expression on his countenance, and inquired

[025-079]what they were saying to Dingo.

[025-080]"Oh, nothing much," replied Tom; "we were only asking him for news of a

[025-081]lost acquaintance."

[025-082]"Ah, I suppose you mean that Portuguese cook of yours."

[025-083]"Yes," answered Tom; "we fancied from Dingo's behaviour, that Negoro

[025-084]must be somewhere close at hand."

[025-085]"Why don't you send and search the underwood? perhaps the poor wretch

[025-086]is in distress."

[025-087]"No need of that, Mr. Harris; Negoro, I have no doubt, is quite capable

[025-088]of taking care of himself."

[025-089]"Well, just as you please, my young friend," said Harris, with an air

[025-090]of indifference.

[025-091]Dick turned away; he continued his endeavours to pacify Dingo, and the

[025-092]conversation dropped.

[025-093]The other thing that had arrested Dick's attention was the behaviour of

[025-094]the horse. If they had been as near the hacienda as Harris described,

[025-095]would not the animal have pricked up its ears, sniffed the air, and

[025-096]with dilated nostril, exhibited some sign of satisfaction, as being

[025-097]upon familiar ground?

[025-098]But nothing of the kind was to be observed; the horse plodded along as

[025-099]unconcernedly as if a stable were as far away as ever.

[025-100]Even Mrs. Weldon was not so engrossed with her child, but what she was

[025-101]fain to express her wonder at the deserted aspect of the country. No

[025-102]trace of a farm-labourer was anywhere to be seen! She cast her eye at

[025-103]Harris, who was in his usual place in front, and observing how he was

[025-104]looking first to the left, and then to the right, with the air of a man

[025-105]who was uncertain of his path, she asked herself whether it was

[025-106]possible their guide might have lost his way. She dared not entertain

[025-107]the idea, and averted her eyes, that she might not be harassed by his

[025-108]movements.

[025-109]After crossing an open plain about a mile in width, the travellers once

[025-110]again entered the forest, which resumed something of the same denseness

[025-111]that had characterized it farther to the west. In the course of the

[025-112]afternoon, they came to a spot which was marked very distinctly by the

[025-113]vestiges of some enormous animals, which must have passed quite

[025-114]recently. As Dick looked carefully about him, he observed that the

[025-115]branches were all torn off or broken to a considerable height, and that

[025-116]the foot-tracks in the trampled grass were much too large to be those

[025-117]either of jaguars or panthers. Even if it were possible that the prints

[025-118]on the ground had been made by ais or other taidigrades, this would

[025-119]fail to account in the least for the trees being broken to such a

[025-120]height. Elephants alone were capable of working such destruction in the

[025-121]underwood, but elephants were unknown in America. Dick was puzzled, but

[025-122]controlled himself so that he would not apply to Harris for any

[025-123]enlightenment; his intuition made him aware that a man who had once

[025-124]tried to make him believe that giraffes were ostriches, would not

[025-125]hesitate a second time to impose upon his credulity.

[025-126]More than ever was Dick becoming convinced that Harris was a traitor,

[025-127]and he was secretly prompted to tax him with his treachery. Still he

[025-128]was obliged to own that he could not assign any motive for the man

[025-129]acting in such a manner with the survivors of the "Pilgrim," and

[025-130]consequently hesitated before he actually condemned him for conduct so

[025-131]base and heartless. What could be done? he repeatedly asked himself. On

[025-132]board ship the boy captain might perchance have been able to devise

[025-133]some plan for the safety of those so strangely committed to his charge,

[025-134]but here on an unknown shore, he could only suffer from the burden of

[025-135]this [[duty|responsibility]] the more, because he was so utterly powerless to

[025-136]act.

[025-137]He made up his mind on one point. He determined not to alarm the poor

[025-138]anxious mother a moment before he was actually compelled. It was his

[025-139]carrying out this determination that explained why on subsequently

[025-140]arriving at a considerable stream, where he saw some huge heads,

[025-141]swollen muzzles, long tusks and unwieldy bodies rising from amidst the

[025-142]rank wet grass, he uttered no word and gave no gesture of surprise; but

[025-143]only too well he knew, at a glance, that he must be looking at a herd

[025-144]of hippopotamuses.

[025-145][Illustration: "Look here! here are hands, men's hands."]

[025-146]It was a weary march that day; a general feeling of depression spread

[025-147]involuntarily from one to another; hardly conscious to herself of her

[025-148]weariness, Mrs. Weldon was exhibiting manifest symptoms of lassitude;

[025-149]and it was only Dick's moral energy and sense of duty that kept him

[025-150]from succumbing to the prevailing dejection.

[025-151][[About]] four o'clock, Tom noticed something lying in the grass, and

[025-152]stooping down he picked up a kind of knife; it was of peculiar shape,

[025-153]being very wide and flat in the blade, while its handle, which was of

[025-154]ivory, was ornamented with a good deal of clumsy carving. He carried it

[025-155]at once to Dick, who, when he had scrutinized it, held it up to Harris,

[025-156]with the remark,--

[025-157]"There must be natives not far off."

[025-158]"Quite right, my young friend; the hacienda must be a very few miles

[025-159]away,--but yet, but yet...."

[025-160]He hesitated.

[025-161]"You don't mean that you are not sure of your way," said Dick sharply.

[025-162]"Not exactly that," replied Harris; "yet in taking this short cut

[025-163]across the forest, I am inclined to think I am a mile or so out of the

[025-164]way. Perhaps I had better walk on a little way, and look about me."

[025-165]"No; you do not leave us here," cried Dick firmly.

[025-166]"Not against your will; but remember, I do not undertake to guide you

[025-167]in the dark."

[025-168]"We must spare you the necessity for that. I can answer for it that

[025-169]Mrs. Weldon will raise no objection to spending another night in the

[025-170]open air. We can start off to-morrow morning as early as we like, and

[025-171]if the distance be only what you represent, a few hours will easily

[025-172]accomplish it."

[025-173]"As you please," answered Harris with cold civility.

[025-174]Just then, Dingo again burst out into a vehement fit of barking, and it

[025-175]required no small amount of coaxing on Dick's part to make him cease

[025-176]from his noise.

[025-177]It was decided that the halt should be made at once. Mrs. Weldon, as it

[025-178]had been anticipated, urged nothing against it, being preoccupied by

[025-179]her immediate attentions to Jack, who was lying in her arms, suffering

[025-180]from a decided attack of fever. The shelter of a large thicket had just

[025-181]been selected by Dick as a suitable resting-place for the night, when

[025-182]Tom, who was assisting in the necessary preparations, suddenly gave a

[025-183]cry of horror.

[025-184]"What is it, Tom?" asked Dick very calmly.

[025-185]"Look! look at these trees! they are spattered with blood! and look

[025-186]here! here are hands, men's hands, cut off and lying on the ground!"

[025-187]"What?" cried Dick, and in an instant was at his side.

[025-188]His presence of mind did not fail him; he whispered,--

[025-189]"Hush! Tom! hush! not a word!"
