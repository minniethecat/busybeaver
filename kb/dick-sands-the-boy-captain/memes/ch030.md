[030-001]"Master Jack will be all right enough, madam, when we get into a more

[030-002]healthy situation near the coast," said Tom.

[030-003]"But is there no farm anywhere near? no village? no shelter?" she

[030-004]pleaded.

[030-005]"None whatever, madam; I can only repeat that it is absolutely

[030-006]necessary that we make the best of our way back to the sea-shore."

[030-007]"Are you quite sure, [[dick-sands|Dick]], that Mr. Harris has deceived us?"

[030-008]Dirk felt that he should be glad to avoid any discussion on the

[030-009]subject, but with a warning glance at Tom, he proceeded to say that on

[030-010]the previous night he and Tom had discovered the American's treachery,

[030-011]and that if he had not instantly taken to his horse and fled he would

[030-012]have answered for his guilt with his life. Without, however, dwelling

[030-013]for a moment more than he could avoid upon the past, he hurried on to

[030-014]detail the means by which he now proposed to reach the sea, concluding

[030-015]by the assertion that he hoped a very few miles' march would bring them

[030-016]to a stream on which they might be able to embark.

[030-017][[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]], thoroughly ignoring her own weakness, professed her

[030-018]readiness not only to walk, but to carry Jack too. Bat and Austin at

[030-019]once volunteered to carry her in a litter; of this the lady would not

[030-020]hear, and bravely repeated her intention of travelling on foot,

[030-021]announcing her willingness to start without further delay. Dick Sands

[030-022]was only too glad to assent to her wish.

[030-023]"Let me take Master Jack," said Hercules; "I shall be out of my element

[030-024]if I have nothing to carry."

[030-025]The giant, without waiting for a reply, took the child from Nan's arms

[030-026]so gently that he did not even rouse him from his slumber.

[030-027]The weapons were next carefully examined, and the provisions, having

[030-028]been repacked into one parcel, were consigned to the charge of Actæon,

[030-029]who undertook to carry them on his back.

[030-030]Cousin Benedict, whose wiry limbs seemed capable of bearing any amount

[030-031]of fatigue, was quite ready to start. It was doubtful whether he had

[030-032]noticed Harris's disappearance; he was suffering from a loss which to

[030-033]him was of far greater importance. He had mislaid his spectacles and

[030-034]magnifying-glass. It had happened that Bat had picked them up in the

[030-035]long grass, close to the spot where the amateur naturalist had been

[030-036]lying, but acting on a hint from Dick Sands, he said nothing about

[030-037]them; in this way the entomologist, who, without his glasses could

[030-038]scarcely see a yard beyond his face, might be expected to be kept

[030-039]without trouble in the limits of the ranks, and having been placed

[030-040]between Actæon and Austin with strict injunctions not to leave their

[030-041]side, he followed them as submissively as a blind man in

[030-042]leading-strings.

[030-043]The start was made. But scarcely had the little troop advanced fifty

[030-044]yards upon their way, when Tom suddenly cried out,--

[030-045]"Where's [[dingo|Dingo]]?"

[030-046]With all the force of his tremendous lungs, Hercules gave a series of

[030-047]reverberating shouts:--

[030-048]"Dingo! Dingo! Dingo!"

[030-049]Not a bark could be distinguished in reply

[030-050]"Dingo! Dingo! Dingo!" again echoed in the air.

[030-051]But all was silence.

[030-052]Dick was intensely annoyed at the non-appearance of the dog; his

[030-053]presence would have been an additional safeguard in the event of any

[030-054]sudden surprise.

[030-055]"Perhaps he has followed Harris," suggested Tom.

[030-056]"Far more likely he is on the track of [[negoro|Negoro]]," rejoined Dick.

[030-057]"Then Negoro, to a dead certainty," said Hercules, "will put a bullet

[030-058]into his head."

[030-059]"It is to be hoped," replied Bat, "that Dingo will strangle him first."

[030-060]Dick Sands, disguising his vexation, said,

[030-061]"At any rate, we have no time to wait for the animal now: if he is

[030-062]alive, he will not fail to find us out. Move on, my lads! move on!"

[030-063]The weather was very hot; ever since daybreak heavy clouds had been

[030-064]gathering upon the horizon, and it seemed hardly likely that the day

[030-065]would pass without a storm. Fortunately the woods were sufficiently

[030-066]light to ensure a certain amount of freshness to the surface of the

[030-067]soil. Here and there were large patches of tall, rank grass enclosed by

[030-068]clumps of forest trees. In some places, fossilized trunks, lying on the

[030-069]ground, betokened the existence of one of the coal districts that are

[030-070]common upon the continent of [[africa|Africa]]. Along the glades the carpet of

[030-071]verdure was relieved by crimson stems and a variety of flowers;

[030-072]ginger-blossoms, blue and yellow, pale lobelias, and red orchids

[030-073]fertilized by the numerous [[entomology|insects]] that incessantly hovered about them.

[030-074]The trees did not grow in impenetrable masses of one species, but

[030-075]exhibited themselves in infinite variety. There was also a species of

[030-076]palm producing an oil locally much valued; there were cotton-plants

[030-077]growing in bushes eight or ten feet high, the cotton attached in long

[030-078]shreds to the ligneous stalks; and there were copals from which,

[030-079]pierced by the proboscis of certain insects, exudes an odorous resin

[030-080]that flows on to the ground and is collected by the natives. Then there

[030-081]were citrons and wild pomegranates and a score of other arborescent

[030-082]plants, all testifying to the fertility of this plateau of Central

[030-083]Africa. In many places, too, the air was fragrant with the odour of

[030-084]vanilla, though it was not possible to discover the shrub from which

[030-085]the perfume emanated.

[030-086]In spite of it being the dry season, so that the soil had only been

[030-087]moistened by occasional storms, all trees and plants were flourishing

[030-088]in great luxuriance. It was the time of year for fever, but, according

[030-089]to Dr. Livingstone's observation, the disorder may generally be cured

[030-090]by quitting the locality where it has been contracted. Dick expressed

[030-091]his hope that, in little Jack's case, the words of the great traveller

[030-092]would be verified, and in encouragement of this sanguine view, pointed

[030-093]out to Mrs. Weldon that although it was past the time for the

[030-094]periodical return of the fever, the child was still slumbering quietly

[030-095]in Hercules' arms.

[030-096]The march was continued with as much rapidity as was consistent with

[030-097]caution. Occasionally, where the bushes and brushwood had been broken

[030-098]down by the recent passage of men or beasts, progress was comparatively

[030-099]easy; but much more frequently, greatly to Dick's annoyance, obstacles

[030-100]of various sorts impeded their advance. Climbing plants grew in such

[030-101]inextricable confusion that they could only be compared to a ship's

[030-102]rigging involved in hopeless entanglement; there were creepers

[030-103]resembling curved scimitars, thickly covered with sharp thorns; there

[030-104]were likewise strange growths, like vegetable serpents, fifty or sixty

[030-105]feet long, which seemed to have a cruel faculty for torturing every

[030-106]passenger with their prickly spines. Axe in hand, the negroes had

[030-107]repeatedly to cut their road through these bewildering obstructions

[030-108]that clothed the trees from their summit to their base.

[030-109]Animal life was no less remarkable in its way than the vegetation.

[030-110]Birds in great variety flitted about in the ample foliage, secure from

[030-111]any stray shot from the little band, whose chief object it was to

[030-112]preserve its incognito. Guinea-fowls were seen in considerable numbers,

[030-113]francolins in several varieties, and a few specimens of the bird to

[030-114]which the Americans, in imitation of their note, have given the name of

[030-115]"whip-poor-will." If Dick had not had too much evidence in other ways

[030-116]to the contrary, he might almost have imagined himself in a province of

[030-117]the New World.

[030-118]Hitherto they had been unmolested by any dangerous wild beasts. During

[030-119]the present stage of their march a herd of giraffes, startled by their

[030-120]unexpected approach, rushed fleetly past; this time, however, without

[030-121]being represented as ostriches. Occasionally a dense cloud of dust on

[030-122]the edge of the prairie, accompanied by a sound like the roll of

[030-123]heavily-laden chariots, betokened the flight of a herd of buffaloes;

[030-124]but with these exceptions no animal of any magnitude appeared in view.

[030-125][Illustration: The march was continued with as much rapidity as was

[030-126]consistent with caution.]

[030-127]For about two miles Dick followed the course of the rivulet, in the

[030-128]hope that it would emerge into a more important stream, which would

[030-129]convey them without much difficulty or danger direct to the sea.

[030-130]Towards noon about three miles had been accomplished, and a halt was

[030-131]made for rest. Neither Negoro nor Harris had been seen, nor had Dingo

[030-132]reappeared. The encampment for the midday refreshment was made under

[030-133]the shelter of a clump of bamboos, which effectually concealed them

[030-134]all. Few words were spoken during the meal. Mrs. Weldon could eat

[030-135]nothing; she had again taken her little boy into her arms, and seemed

[030-136]wholly absorbed in watching him. Again and again Dick begged her to

[030-137]take some nourishment, urging upon her the necessity of keeping up her

[030-138]strength.

[030-139]"We shall not be long in finding a good current to carry us to the

[030-140]coast," said the lad brightly.

[030-141]Mrs. Weldon raised her eyes to his animated features. With so sanguine

[030-142]and resolute a leader, with such devoted servants as the five negroes

[030-143]in attendance, she felt that she ought not utterly to despair. Was she

[030-144]not, after all, on friendly soil? what great harm could Harris

[030-145]perpetrate against her or her belongings? She would hope still, hope

[030-146]for the best.

[030-147]Rejoiced as he was to see something of its former brightness return to

[030-148]her countenance, Dick nevertheless had scarcely courage steadily to

[030-149]return her searching gaze. Had she known the whole truth, he knew that

[030-150]her heart must fail her utterly.

[030-151]CHAPTER IV.

[030-152]ROUGH TRAVELLING.

[030-153]Just at this moment Jack woke up and put his arms round his mother's

[030-154]neck. His eyes were brighter, and there was manifestly no return of

[030-155]fever.

[030-156]"You are better, darling!" said Mrs. Weldon, pressing him tenderly to

[030-157]her.

[030-158]"Yes, mamma, I am better; but I am very thirsty."

[030-159]Some cold water was soon procured, which the child drank eagerly, and

[030-160]then began to look about him. His first inquiry was for his old

[030-161]friends, Dick and Hercules, both of whom approached at his summons and

[030-162]greeted him affectionately.

[030-163]"Where is the horse?" was the next question.

[030-164]"Gone away, Master Jack; I am your horse now," said Hercules.

[030-165]"But you have no bridle for me to hold," said Jack, looking rather

[030-166]disappointed.

[030-167]"You may put a bit in my mouth if you like, master Jack," replied

[030-168]Hercules, extending his jaws, "and then you may pull as hard as you

[030-169]please."

[030-170]"O, I shall not pull very hard," said Jack; "but haven't we nearly come

[030-171]to Mr. Harris's farm?"

[030-172]Mrs. Weldon assured the child that they should soon be where they

[030-173]wanted to be, and Dick, finding that the conversation was approaching

[030-174]dangerous ground, proposed that the journey should be now resumed. Mrs.

[030-175]Weldon assented; the encampment was forthwith broken up and the march

[030-176]continued as before.

[030-177][Illustration: It was a scene only too common in Central Africa]

[030-178]In order not to lose sight of the watercourse, it was necessary to cut

[030-179]a way right through the underwood: progress was consequently very slow;

[030-180]and a little over a mile was all that was accomplished in about three

[030-181]hours. Footpaths had evidently once existed, but they had all become

[030-182]what the natives term "dead," that is, they had become entirely

[030-183]overgrown with brushwood and brambles. The negroes worked away with a

[030-184]will; Hercules, in particular, who temporarily resigned his charge to

[030-185]Nan, wielded his axe with marvellous effect, all the time giving vent

[030-186]to stentorian groans and grunts, and succeeded in opening the woods

[030-187]before him as if they were being consumed by a devouring fire.

[030-188]Fortunately this heavy labour was not of very long duration.

[030-189]After about a mile, an opening of moderate width, converging towards

[030-190]the stream and following its bank, was discovered in the underwood. It

[030-191]was a passage formed by elephants, which apparently by hundreds must be

[030-192]in the habit of traversing this part of the forest. The spongy soil,

[030-193]soaked by the downpour of the rainy season, was everywhere indented

[030-194]with the enormous impressions of their feet.
