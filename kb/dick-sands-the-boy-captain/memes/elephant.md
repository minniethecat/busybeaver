The African elephant is one of the most significant animals encountered in the novel's second half, not merely as wildlife but as a landscape-shaping force whose trails provide passage through the dense [[africa|Africa]]n interior. The party follows "a passage formed by elephants, which apparently by hundreds" had trodden through the undergrowth (030-191).

## In the Narrative

The elephant first enters the narrative as part of a revealing moment of geographical confusion. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] observes elephant tracks and realizes they are not in [[san-francisco|South America]] — "elephants were unknown in America" (025-121). This is one of the accumulating clues that [[negoro|Negoro]]'s [[compass|compass sabotage]] has delivered them to [[africa|Africa]] rather than the intended destination.

> "Traces of elephants and hippopotamuses" were unmistakable evidence of Africa (028-020).

The elephants' trails become a practical asset for the party. A "passage formed by elephants, which apparently by hundreds" had created a natural road through otherwise impenetrable terrain (030-191). However, it soon becomes "evident that elephants were not the only living" creatures using these paths (031-001) — a grim foreshadowing of the dangers ahead.

| Symbolism | The power and scale of the [[africa|Africa]]n continent |

Jules Verne uses the elephant to accomplish multiple narrative purposes simultaneously: as a clue in the geographical mystery, as a practical element of the journey, and as a symbol of [[africa|Africa]]'s overwhelming scale compared to the vulnerable human travelers. The image of a "troop of a hundred or more elephants" (001-183) conveys a continent where humans are dwarfed by nature.
