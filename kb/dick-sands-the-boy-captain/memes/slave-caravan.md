# Slave Caravan

The organized overland transport of enslaved captives that the protagonists of *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]* encounter and are forced to join in equatorial [[africa|Africa]].

## Definition

A slave caravan was a coffle—a group of enslaved people chained together and marched overland by armed traders from the [[africa|Africa]]n interior to coastal markets. These caravans traversed vast distances, with captives "carried off in caravans in two opposite directions, some westwards to the Portuguese colony of [[angola|Angola]], others eastwards to Mozambique" (026-181–183).

## Role in the Novel

The caravan system represents the operational reality of the internal [[africa|Africa]]n [[slavery|slave trade]] that Verne condemns. The narrator notes that "caravans pass along under the very eye" of authorities who cannot or will not intervene (027-032). The region serves as a "rendezvous for all the caravans that journey in that portion" of [[africa|Africa]] (027-043).

The novel's protagonists are incorporated into a caravan when captured, experiencing the brutal march firsthand. This transforms the [[slavery|slave trade]] from an abstract historical phenomenon into an immediate, personal horror.

The caravan is also where the party's leadership dynamics shift. [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]], previously the captain, becomes the "appointed leader of the whole caravan" of captives (022-009), adapting his maritime leadership to a new and desperate context.

## Historical Context

By 1878, the caravan trade was the primary mechanism of the internal [[africa|Africa]]n [[slavery|slave trade]]. Verne estimates that "no less than 24,000 slaves annually reach the coast" via these caravans, a figure "that hardly represents a tenth part of those who are massacred" or die en route (026-190–191). Slave caravan routes crisscrossed [[africa|Central Africa]], converging on markets like [[zanzibar|Zanzibar]] in the east and the Portuguese colonies in the west.
