[027-001]It is hardly too much to say that another year ought to witness the

[027-002][[abolitionism|emancipation]] of every slave in the possession of Christian states. It

[027-003]seems only too likely that for years to come the Mussulman nations will

[027-004]continue to depopulate the continent of [[africa|Africa]]; to them is due the

[027-005]chief emigration of the natives, who, torn from their provinces, are

[027-006]sent to the eastern coast in numbers that exceed 40,000 annually. Long

[027-007]before the Egyptian expedition the natives of Sennaar were sold to the

[027-008]natives of Darfur and _vice versa_; and even Napoleon Buonaparte

[027-009]purchased a considerable number of negroes, whom he organized into

[027-010]regiments after the fashion of the mamelukes. Altogether it may be

[027-011]affirmed, that although four-fifths of the present century have passed

[027-012]away, slave-traffic in Africa has been increased rather than diminished.

[027-013]The truth is that Islamism really nurtures the [[slavery|slave-trade]]. In

[027-014]Mussulman provinces, the black slave has taken the place of the white

[027-015]slave of former times; dealers of the most questionable character bear

[027-016]their part in the execrable business, bringing a supplementary

[027-017]population to races which, unregenerated by their own labour, would

[027-018]otherwise diminish and ultimately disappear.

[027-019]As in the time of Buonaparte, these slaves often become soldiers; on

[027-020]the Upper Niger, for instance, they still form half the army of certain

[027-021]chieftains, under circumstances in which their lot is hardly, if at

[027-022]all, inferior to that of free men. Elsewhere, where the slave is not a

[027-023]soldier, he counts merely as current coin; and in Bornu and even in

[027-024]Egypt, we are told by William Lejean, an eye-witness, that officers and

[027-025]other functionaries have received their pay in this form.

[027-026]Such, then, appears to be the present actual condition of the

[027-027]slave-trade; and it is stern justice that compels the additional

[027-028]statement that there are representatives of certain great European

[027-029]powers who still favour the unholy traffic with an indulgent

[027-030]connivance, and whilst cruisers are watching the coasts of the Atlantic

[027-031]and of the Indian Ocean, kidnapping goes on regularly in the interior,

[027-032]caravans pass along under the very eyes of certain officials, and

[027-033]massacres are perpetrated in which frequently ten negroes are

[027-034]sacrificed in the capture of a single slave.

[027-035]It was the knowledge, more or less complete, of all this, that wrung

[027-036]from [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] his bitter and heart-rending cry:--

[027-037]"We are in Africa! in the very haunt of slave-drivers!"

[027-038]Too true it was that he found himself and his companions in a land

[027-039]fraught with such frightful peril. He could only tremble when he

[027-040]wondered on what part of the fatal continent the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" had

[027-041]stranded. Evidently it was at some point of the west coast, and he had

[027-042]every reason to fear that it was on the shores of Angola, the

[027-043]rendezvous for all the caravans that journey in that portion of Africa.

[027-044]His conjecture was correct; he really was in the very country that a

[027-045]few years later and with gigantic effort was to be traversed by Cameron

[027-046]in the south and Stanley in the north. Of the vast territory, with its

[027-047]three provinces, Congo, Angola, and Benguela, little was then known

[027-048]except the coast. It extends from the Zaire on the north to the Nourse

[027-049]on the south, and its chief towns are the ports of Benguela and of St.

[027-050]Paul de Loanda, the capital of the colony, which is a dependency of the

[027-051]kingdom of Portugal. The interior of the country had been almost

[027-052]entirely unexplored. Very few were the travellers who had cared to

[027-053]venture far inland, for an unhealthy climate, a hot, damp soil

[027-054]conducive to fever, a permanent warfare between the native tribes, some

[027-055]of which are cannibals, and the ill-feeling of the slave-dealers

[027-056]against any stranger who might endeavour to discover the secrets of

[027-057]their infamous craft, all combine to render the region one of the most

[027-058]hazardous in the whole of Equatorial Africa.

[027-059]It was in 1816 that Tuckey ascended the Congo as far as the Yellala

[027-060]Falls, a distance not exceeding 203 miles; but the journey was too

[027-061]short to give an accurate idea of the interior of the country, and

[027-062]moreover cost the lives of nearly all the officers and scientific men

[027-063]connected with the expedition.

[027-064]Thirty-seven years afterwards, Dr. Livingstone had advanced from the

[027-065]Cape of Good Hope to the Upper Zambesi; thence, with a fearlessness

[027-066]hitherto unrivalled, he crossed the Coango, an affluent of the Congo,

[027-067]and after having traversed the continent from the extreme south to the

[027-068]east he reached St. Paul de Loanda on the 31st of May, 1854, the first

[027-069]explorer of the unknown portions of the great Portuguese colony.

[027-070]Eighteen years elapsed, and two other bold travellers crossed the

[027-071]entire continent from east to west, and after encountering unparalleled

[027-072]difficulties, emerged, the one to the south, the other to the north of

[027-073]Angola.

[027-074]The first of these was Verney Lovett Cameron, a lieutenant in the

[027-075]British navy. In 1872, when serious doubts were entertained as to the

[027-076]safety of the expedition sent out under Stanley to the relief of

[027-077]Livingstone in the great lake district, Lieutenant Cameron volunteered

[027-078]to go out in search of the noble missionary explorer. His offer was

[027-079]accepted, and accompanied by Dr. Dillon, Lieutenant Cecil Murphy, and

[027-080]Robert Moffat, a nephew of Livingstone, he started from Zanzibar.

[027-081]Having passed through Ugogo, he met Livingstone's corpse, which was

[027-082]being borne to the eastern coast by his faithful followers. Unshaken in

[027-083]his resolve to make his way right across the continent, Cameron still

[027-084]pushed onwards to the west. He passed through Unyanyembe and Uganda,

[027-085]and reached Kawele, where he secured all Livingstone's papers. After

[027-086][[exploration|exploring]] Lake Tanganyika he crossed the mountains of Bambarre, and

[027-087]finding himself unable to descend the course of the Lualaba, he

[027-088]traversed the provinces devastated and depopulated by war and the

[027-089]slave-trade, Kilemba, Urua, the sources of the Lomami, Ulanda, and

[027-090]Lovalé, and having crossed the Coanza, he sighted the Atlantic and

[027-091]reached the port of St. Philip de Benguela, after a journey that had

[027-092]occupied three years and five months. Cameron's two companions, Dr.

[027-093]Dillon and Robert Moffat, both succumbed to the hardships of the

[027-094]expedition.

[027-095]The intrepid Englishman was soon to be followed into the field by an

[027-096]American, Mr. Henry Moreland Stanley. It is universally known how the

[027-097]undaunted correspondent of the _New York Herald_, having been

[027-098]despatched in search of Livingstone, found the veteran missionary at

[027-099]Ujiji, on the borders of Lake Tanganyika, on the 31st of October, 1871.

[027-100]But what he had undertaken in the course of humanity Stanley longed to

[027-101]continue in the interests of science, his prime object being to make a

[027-102]thorough investigation of the Lualaba, of which, in his first

[027-103]expedition, he had only been able to get a partial and imperfect

[027-104]survey. Accordingly, whilst Cameron was still deep in the provinces of

[027-105]Central Africa, Stanley started from Bagamoyo in November, 1874.

[027-106]Twenty-one months later he quitted Ujiji, which had been decimated by

[027-107]small-pox, and in seventy-four days accomplished the passage of the

[027-108]lake and reached Nyangwe, a great slave-market previously visited both

[027-109]by Livingstone and Cameron. He was also present at some of the horrible

[027-110]razzias, perpetrated by the officers of the Sultan of Zanzibar in the

[027-111]districts of the Marunzu and Manyuema.

[027-112]In order to be in a position to descend the Lualaba to its very mouth,

[027-113]Stanley engaged at Nyangwe 140 porters and nineteen boats. Difficulties

[027-114]arose from the very outset, and not only had he to contend with the

[027-115]cannibals of Ugusu, but, in order to avoid many unnavigable cataracts,

[027-116]he had to convey his boats many miles by land. Near the equator, just

[027-117]at the point where the Lualaba turns north-north-west, Stanley's little

[027-118]convoy was attacked by a fleet of boats, manned by several hundred

[027-119]natives, whom, however, he succeeded in putting to flight. Nothing

[027-120]daunted, the resolute American pushed on to lat. 20° N. and

[027-121]ascertained, beyond room for doubt, that the Lualaba was really the

[027-122]Upper Zaire or Congo, and that, by following its course, he should come

[027-123]directly to the sea.

[027-124]Beset with many perils was the way. Stanley was in almost daily

[027-125]collision with the various tribes upon the river-banks; on the 3rd of

[027-126]June, 1877, he lost one of his companions, Frank Pocock, at the passage

[027-127]of the cataracts of Massassa, and on the 18th of July he was himself

[027-128]carried in his boat into the Mbelo Falls, and escaped by little short

[027-129]of a miracle.

[027-130]On the 6th of August the daring adventurer arrived at the village of Ni

[027-131]Sanda, only four days from the sea; two days later he received a supply

[027-132]of provisions that had been sent by two Emboma merchants to Banza

[027-133]M'buko, the little coast-town where, after a journey of two years and

[027-134]nine months, fraught with every kind of hardship and privation, he

[027-135]completed his transit of the mighty continent. His toil told, at least

[027-136]temporarily, upon his years, but he had the grand satisfaction of

[027-137]knowing that he had traced the whole course of the Lualaba, and had

[027-138]ascertained, beyond reach of question, that as the Nile is the great

[027-139]artery of the north, and the Zambesi of the east, so Africa possesses

[027-140]in the west a third great river, which in a course of no less than 2900

[027-141]miles, under the names of the Lualaba, Zaire, and Congo, unites the

[027-142]lake district with the Atlantic Ocean.

[027-143]In 1873, however, the date at which the "Pilgrim" foundered upon the

[027-144]coast, very little was known of the province of Angola, except that it

[027-145]was the scene of the western slave-trade, of which the markets of Bihé,

[027-146]Cassanga, and Kazunde were the chief centres. This was the country in

[027-147]which Dick Sands now found himself, a hundred miles from shore, in

[027-148]charge of a lady exhausted with fatigue and anxiety, a half-dying

[027-149]child, and a band of negroes who would be a most tempting bait to the

[027-150]slave-driver.

[027-151]His last illusion was completely dispelled. He had no longer the

[027-152]faintest hope that he was in America, that land where little was to be

[027-153]dreaded from either native, wild beast, or climate; he could no more

[027-154]cherish the fond impression that he might be in the pleasant region

[027-155]between the Cordilleras and the coast, where villages are numerous and

[027-156]missions afford hospitable shelter to every traveller. Far, far away

[027-157]were those provinces of Bolivia and Peru, to which (unless a criminal

[027-158]hand had interposed) the "Pilgrim" would certainly have sped her way.

[027-159]No: too truly this was the terrible province of Angola; and worse than

[027-160]all, not the district near the coast, under the surveillance of the

[027-161]Portuguese authorities, but the interior of the country, traversed only

[027-162]by slave caravans, driven under the lash of the havildars.

[027-163]Limited, in one sense, was the knowledge that Dick Sands possessed of

[027-164]this land of horrors; but he had read the accounts that had been given

[027-165]by the missionaries of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, by the

[027-166]Portuguese traders who frequented the route from St. Paul de Loanda, by

[027-167]San Salvador to the Zaire, as well as by Dr. Livingstone in his travels

[027-168]in 1853, and consequently he knew enough to awaken immediate and

[027-169]complete despair in any spirit less indomitable than his own.

[027-170]Anyhow, his position was truly appalling.

[027-172]CHAPTER II.

[027-173]ACCOMPLICES.

[027-174]On the day following that on which Dick Sands and his party had made

[027-175]their last halt in the forest, two men met by appointment at a spot

[027-176]about three miles distant.

[027-177]The two men were Harris and [[negoro|Negoro]], the one lately landed from New

[027-178]Zealand, the other pursuing his wonted occupation of slave-dealer in

[027-179]the province of Angola. They were seated at the foot of an enormous

[027-180]banyan-tree, on the banks of a rushing torrent that streamed between

[027-181]tall borders of papyrus.

[027-182]After the conversation had turned awhile upon the events of the last

[027-183]few hours, Negoro said abruptly,--
