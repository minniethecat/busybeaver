[017-001]Easter Island, of which the true name is Vai-Hoo, was discovered by

[017-002]David in 1686 and visited by Cook and Lapérouse. It lies in lat. 27°

[017-003]S., and long. 112 E.; consequently, it was evident that during the

[017-004]raging of the [[storm-at-sea|hurricane]] the schooner had been driven northwards no less

[017-005]than fifteen degrees. Far away, however, as she was from shore, the

[017-006]wind could hardly fail within ten days to carry her within sight of

[017-007]land; and then, if the storm had worn itself out, (as probably it

[017-008]would,) the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]" would again hoist sail, and make her way into

[017-009]some port with safety. Anyhow, the discovery of his true position

[017-010]restored a spirit of confidence to [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]], and he anticipated the

[017-011]time when he should no longer be drifting helplessly before the storm.

[017-012]To say the truth, the "Pilgrim" had suffered very little from the

[017-013]prolonged fury of the weather. The damage she had sustained was limited

[017-014]to the loss of the topsail and the small jib, which could be easily

[017-015]replaced. The caulking of the seams remained thoroughly sound, and no

[017-016]drop of water had found its way into the hold. The pumps, too, were

[017-017]perfectly free. Dick Sands did not fear for the stability of his ship;

[017-018]his only anxiety was lest the weather should not moderate in time. Only

[017-019]let the wind subside, and the schooner once more would be under his

[017-020]control; but he never forgot that the ordering of the winds and waves

[017-021]were in the hands of the Great Disposer of all.

[017-022]CHAPTER XIII.

[017-023]LAND AT LAST.

[017-024]It was not long before Dick's sanguine expectations were partially

[017-025]realized, for on the very next day, which was the 27th, the barometer

[017-026]began to rise, not rapidly, but steadily, indicating that its elevation

[017-027]would probably continue. The sea remained exceedingly rough, but the

[017-028]violence of the wind, which had veered slightly towards the west, had

[017-029]perceptibly diminished. The tempest had passed its greatest fury, and

[017-030]was beginning to wear itself out.

[017-031]Not a sail, however, could yet be hoisted; the smallest show of canvas

[017-032]would have been carried away in an instant; nevertheless Dick hoped

[017-033]that before another twenty-four hours were over, the "Pilgrim" might be

[017-034]able to carry a storm-jib.

[017-035]In the course of the night the wind moderated still more and the

[017-036]pitching of the ship had so far diminished that the passengers began to

[017-037]reappear on deck. [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] was the first to leave her enforced

[017-038]imprisonment. She was anxious to speak to Dick, whom she might have

[017-039]expected to find looking pale and wan after his almost superhuman

[017-040]exertions and loss of sleep. But she was mistaken; however much the lad

[017-041]might suffer from the strain in after-years, at present he exhibited no

[017-042]symptoms of failing energy.

[017-043]"Well, [[captain-hull|Captain]] Dick, how are you?" she said, as she advanced towards

[017-044]him holding out her hand.

[017-045]Dick smiled.

[017-046]"You call me captain, Mrs. Weldon," he answered, "but you do not seem

[017-047]disposed to submit implicitly to captain's orders. Did I not direct you

[017-048]to keep to your cabin?"

[017-049][Illustration: "You have acquitted yourself like a man."]

[017-050]"You did," replied the lady; "but observing how much the storm had

[017-051]abated, I could not resist the temptation to disobey you."

[017-052]"Yes, madam, the weather is far more promising; the barometer has not

[017-053]fallen since yesterday morning, and I really trust the worst is over

[017-054]now."

[017-055]"Thank Heaven!" she replied, and after a few moments' silence, she

[017-056]added.--

[017-057]"But now, Dick, you must really take some rest; you may perhaps not

[017-058]know how much you require it; but it is absolutely necessary."

[017-059]"Rest!" the boy repeated; "rest! I want no rest. I have only done my

[017-060]duty, and it will be time enough for me to concern myself about my own

[017-061]rest, when I have seen my passengers in a place of safety."

[017-062]"You have acquitted yourself like a man," said Mrs. Weldon; "and you

[017-063]may be assured that my husband, like myself, will never forget the

[017-064]services you have rendered me. I shall urge upon him the request which

[017-065]I am sure he will not refuse, that you shall have your studies

[017-066]completed, so that you may be made a captain for the firm."

[017-067]Tears of gratitude rose to Dick's eyes. He deprecated the praise that

[017-068]was lavished upon him, but rejoiced in the prospect that seemed opening

[017-069]upon his future. Mrs. Weldon assured him that he was dear as a son to

[017-070]her, and pressed a gentle kiss upon his forehead. The lad felt that he

[017-071]was animated, if need be, to yet greater hardships in behalf of his

[017-072]benefactors, and resolved to prove himself even more worthy of their

[017-073]confidence.

[017-074]By the 29th, the wind had so far moderated that Dick thought he might

[017-075]increase the "Pilgrim's" speed by hoisting the foresail and topsail.

[017-076]"Now, my men, I have some work for you to-day," he said to the negroes

[017-077]when he came on deck at daybreak.

[017-078]"All right, captain," answered Hercules, "we are growing rusty for want

[017-079]of something to do."

[017-080]"Why didn't you blow with your big mouth?" said little Jack; "you could

[017-081]have beaten the wind all to nothing."

[017-082]Dick laughed, and said, "Not a bad idea, Jack; if ever we get becalmed,

[017-083]we must get Hercules to blow into the sails."

[017-084]"I shall be most happy," retorted the giant, and he inflated his huge

[017-085]checks till he was the very impersonation of Boreas himself.

[017-086]"But now to work!" cried Dick; "we have lost our topsail, and we must

[017-087]contrive to hoist another. Not an easy matter, I can tell you."

[017-088]"I dare say we shall manage it," replied Actæon.

[017-089]"We must do our best," said Tom.

[017-090]"Can't I help?" inquired Jack.

[017-091]"Of course you can," answered Dick; "run along to the wheel, and assist

[017-092]Bat."

[017-093]Jack strutted off, proud enough of his commission.

[017-094]Under Dick's directions, the negroes commenced their somewhat difficult

[017-095]task. The new topsail, rolled up, had first of all to be hoisted, and

[017-096]then to be made fast to the yard; but so adroitly did the crew carry

[017-097]out their orders, that in less than an hour the sail was properly set

[017-098]and flying with a couple of reefs. The foresail and second jib, which

[017-099]had been taken down before the tempest, were hoisted again, and before

[017-100]ten o'clock the "Pilgrim" was running along under the three sails which

[017-101]Dick considered were as much as it was prudent to carry. Even at her

[017-102]present speed, the schooner, he reckoned, would be within sight of the

[017-103]American shore in about ten days. It was an immense relief to him to

[017-104]find that she was no longer at the mercy of the waves, and when he saw

[017-105]the sails properly set he returned in good spirits to his post at the

[017-106]helm, not forgetting to thank the temporary helmsman for his services,

[017-107]nor omitting his acknowledgment to Master Jack, who received the

[017-108]compliment with becoming gravity.

[017-109]Although the clouds continued to travel all the next day with great

[017-110]rapidity they were very much broken, and alternately the "Pilgrim" was

[017-111]bathed in sunlight and enveloped in vapours, which rolled on towards

[017-112]the east. As the weather cleared, the hatchways were opened in order to

[017-113]ventilate the ship, and the outer air was allowed again to penetrate

[017-114]not only the hold, but the cabin and crew's quarters The wet sails were

[017-115]hung out to dry, the deck was washed down, for Dick Sands was anxious

[017-116]not to bring his ship into port without having "finished her toilet,"

[017-117]and he found that his crew could very well spare a few hours daily to

[017-118]get her into proper trim.

[017-119][Illustration: They both examined the outspread chart.]

[017-120]Notwithstanding the loss of [[ships-log|the log]], Dick had sufficient experience to

[017-121]be able to make an approximate estimate of the schooner's progress, and

[017-122]after having pointed out to Mrs. Weldon what he imagined was the

[017-123]"Pilgrim's" true position, he told her that it was his firm impression

[017-124]that land would be sighted in little more than a week.

[017-125]"And upon what part of South America do you reckon we are likely to

[017-126]find ourselves?" she asked.

[017-127]"That is more than I dare venture to promise," replied Dick; "but I

[017-128]should think somewhere hereabouts."

[017-129]He was pointing on the chart to the long shore-line of Chili and Peru.

[017-130]They both examined the outspread chart with still closer attention.

[017-131]"Here, you see," resumed Dick, "here is the island we have just left;

[017-132]we left it in the west; the wind has not shifted; we must expect to

[017-133]come in sight of land, pretty nearly due east of it. The coast has

[017-134]plenty of harbours. From any one of them you will be able easily to get

[017-135]to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]. You know, I dare say, that the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]] [[navigation|Navigation]]

[017-136]Company's steamers touch at all the principal ports. From any of them

[017-137]you will be sure to get direct passage to California."

[017-138]"But do you mean," asked Mrs. Weldon, "that you are not going yourself

[017-139]to take the schooner to San Francisco?"

[017-140]"Not direct," replied the young captain; "I want to see you safe on

[017-141]shore and satisfactorily on your homeward way. When that is done, I

[017-142]shall hope to get competent officers to take the ship to Valparaiso,

[017-143]where she will discharge her cargo, as Captain Hull intended; and

[017-144]afterwards I shall work our way back to San Francisco."

[017-145]"Ah, well; we will see all about that in due time." Mrs. Weldon said,

[017-146]smiling; and, after a short pause, added, "At one time, Dick, you

[017-147]seemed to have rather a dread of the shore."

[017-148]"Quite true," answered Dick; "but now I am in hopes we may fall in with

[017-149]some passing vessel; we want to have a confirmation as to our true

[017-150]position. I cannot tell you how surprised I am that we have not come

[017-151]across a single vessel. But when we near the land we shall be able to

[017-152]get a pilot."

[017-153]"But what will happen if we fail to get a pilot?" was Mrs. Weldon's

[017-154]inquiry. She was anxious to learn how far the lad was prepared to meet

[017-155]any emergency.

[017-156]With unhesitating promptness Dick replied,--

[017-157]"Why, then, unless the weather takes the control of the ship out of my

[017-158]hands, I must patiently follow the coast until I come to a harbour of

[017-159]refuge. But if the wind should freshen, I should have to adopt other

[017-160]measures."

[017-161]"What then, Dick, what then?" persisted Mrs. Weldon.

[017-162]The boy's brow knitted itself together in resolution, and he said

[017-163]deliberately,--

[017-164]"I should run the ship aground."

[017-165]Mrs. Weldon started.

[017-166]"However," Dick continued, "there is no reason to apprehend this. The

[017-167]weather has mended and is likely to mend. And why should we fear about

[017-168]finding a pilot? Let us hope all will be well."

[017-169]Mrs. Weldon at least had satisfied herself on one point. She had

[017-170]ascertained that although Dick did not anticipate disaster, yet he was

[017-171]prepared in the case of emergency to resort to measures from which any

[017-172]but the most experienced seaman would shrink.

[017-173]But although Dick's equanimity had been successful in allaying any

[017-174]misgivings on Mrs. Weldon's part, it must be owned that the condition

[017-175]of the atmosphere caused him very serious uneasiness.

[017-176]The wind remained uncomfortably high, and the barometer gave very

[017-177]ominous indications that it would ere long freshen still more. Dick

[017-178]dreaded that the time was about to return in which once again he must

[017-179]reduce his vessel to a state of bare poles; but so intense was his

[017-180]aversion to having his ship so wrested as it were from his own

[017-181]management, that he determined to carry the topsail till it was all but

[017-182]carried away by the force of the blast. Concerned, moreover, for the

[017-183]safety of his masts, the loss of which he acknowledged must be fatal,

[017-184]he had the shrouds well overhauled and the backstays considerably

[017-185]tightened.
