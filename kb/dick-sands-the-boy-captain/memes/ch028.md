[028-001]"Couldn't you manage to get that young fifteen-year-old any farther

[028-002]into the interior?"

[028-003]"No, indeed; it was a hard matter enough to bring him thus far; for the

[028-004]last few days his suspicions have been wide awake."

[028-005]"But just another hundred miles, you know," continued [[negoro|Negoro]], "would

[028-006]have finished the business off well, and those black fellows would have

[028-007]been ours to a dead certainty."

[028-008]"Don't I tell you, my dear fellow, that it was more than time for me to

[028-009]give them the slip?" replied Harris, shrugging his shoulders. "Only too

[028-010]well I knew that our young friend was longing to put a shot into my

[028-011]body, and that was a sugar-plum I might not be able to digest."

[028-012]The Portuguese gave a grunt of assent, and Harris went on,--

[028-013]"For several days I succeeded well enough. I managed to palm off the

[028-014]country as the forest of Atacama, which you may recollect I once

[028-015]visited; but when the youngster began to ask for gutta-percha and

[028-016]humming-birds, and his mother wanted quinquina-trees, and when that old

[028-017]fool of a cousin was bent on finding cocuyos, I was rather nonplussed.

[028-018]One day I had to swear that giraffes were ostriches, but the young

[028-019]captain did not seem to swallow the dose at all easily. Then we saw

[028-020]traces of elephants and hippopotamuses, which of course are as often

[028-021]seen in America as an honest man in a Benguela penitentiary; then that

[028-022]old nigger Tom discovered a lot of forks and chains left by some

[028-023]runaway slaves at the foot of a tree; but when, last of all, a lion

[028-024]roared,--and the noise, you know, is rather louder than the mewing of a

[028-025]cat,--I thought it was time to take my horse and decamp."

[028-026]Negoro repeated his expression of regret that the whole party had not

[028-027]been carried another hundred miles into the province.

[028-028]"It really cannot be helped," rejoined the American; "I have done the

[028-029]best I could; and I think, mate," he added confidentially, "that you

[028-030]have done wisely in following the [[slave-caravan|caravan]] at a good distance; that dog

[028-031]of theirs evidently owes you a grudge, and might prove an ugly

[028-032]customer."

[028-033]"I shall put a bullet into that beast's head before long," growled

[028-034]Negoro.

[028-035]"Take care you don't get one through your own first," laughed Harris;

[028-036]"that young Sands, I warn you, is a first-rate shot, and between

[028-037]ourselves, is rather a fine fellow of his kind."

[028-038]"Fine fellow, indeed!" sneered Negoro; "whatever he is, he is a young

[028-039]upstart, and I have a long score to wipe off against him;" and, as he

[028-040]spoke, an expression of the utmost malignity passed over his

[028-041]countenance.

[028-042]Harris smiled.

[028-043]"Well, mate," he said; "your travels have not improved your temper, I

[028-044]see. But come now, tell me what you have been doing all this time. When

[028-045]I found you just after the wreck, at the mouth of the Longa, you had

[028-046]only time to ask me to get this party, somehow or other, up into the

[028-047]country. But it is just upon two years since you left Cassange with

[028-048]that caravan of slaves for our old master Alvez. What have you been

[028-049]doing since? The last I heard of you was that you had run foul of an

[028-050]English cruiser, and that you were condemned to be hanged."

[028-051]"So I was very nearly," muttered Negoro.

[028-052]"Ah, well, that will come sooner or later," rejoined the American with

[028-053]philosophic indifference; "men of our trade can't expect to die quietly

[028-054]in our beds, you know. But were you caught by the English?"

[028-055]"No, by the Portuguese."

[028-056]"Before you had got rid of your cargo?"

[028-057]Negoro hesitated a moment before replying.

[028-058]"No," he said, presently, and added, "The Portuguese have changed their

[028-059]game: for a long time they carried on the trade themselves, but now

[028-060]they have got wonderfully particular; so I was caught, and condemned to

[028-061]end my days in the penitentiary at St. Paul de Loanda."

[028-062]"Confound it!" exclaimed Harris, "a hundred times better be hanged!"

[028-063]"I'm not so sure of that," the Portuguese replied, "for when I had been

[028-064]at the galleys about a fortnight I managed to escape, and got into the

[028-065]hold of an English steamer bound for [[new-zealand|New Zealand]]. I wedged myself in

[028-066]between a cask of water and a case of preserved meat, and so managed to

[028-067]exist for a month. It was close quarters, I can tell you, but I

[028-068]preferred to travel incognito rather than run the risk of being handed

[028-069]over again to the authorities at Loanda."

[028-070]"Well done!" exclaimed the American, "and so you had a free passage to

[028-071]the land of the Maoris. But you didn't come back in the same fashion?"

[028-072]"No; I always had a hankering to be here again at my old trade; but for

[028-073]a year and a half...."

[028-074]He stopped abruptly, and grasped Harris by the arm.

[028-075]"Hush," he whispered, "didn't you hear a rustling in that clump of

[028-076]papyrus?"

[028-077]In a moment Harris had caught up his loaded gun; and both men, starting

[028-078]to their feet, looked anxiously around them.

[028-079]"It was nothing," said Harris presently; "the stream is swollen by the

[028-080]storm, that is all; your two years' travelling has made you forget the

[028-081]sounds of the forest, mate. Sit down again, and go on with your story.

[028-082]When I know the past, I shall be better able to talk about the future."

[028-083]They reseated themselves, and Negoro went on,--

[028-084]"For a whole year and a half I vegetated at Auckland. I left the hold

[028-085]of the steamer without a dollar in my pocket, and had to turn my hand

[028-086]to every trade imaginable in order to get a living."

[028-087]"Poor fellow! I daresay you even tried the trade of being an honest

[028-088]man," put in the American.

[028-089]"Just so," said Negoro, "and in course of time the '[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]],' the

[028-090]vessel by which I came here, put in at Auckland. While she was waiting

[028-091]to take [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] and her party on board, I applied to the captain

[028-092]for a post, for I was once mate on board a slaver, and know something

[028-093]of [[navigation|seamanship]]. The 'Pilgrim's' crew was complete, but fortunately the

[028-094]ship's cook had just deserted; I offered to supply his place; in

[028-095]default of better my services were accepted, and in a few days we were

[028-096]out of sight of New Zealand."

[028-097]"I have heard something about the voyage from young Sands," said

[028-098]Harris, "but even now I can't understand how you reached here."

[028-099]"Neither does he," said Negoro, with a malicious grin. "I will tell you

[028-100]now, and you may repeat the story to your young friend if you like."

[028-101]"Well, go on," said Harris.

[028-102]"When we started," continued Negoro, "it was my intention to sail only

[028-103]as far as Chili: that would have brought me nearly half way to Angola;

[028-104]but three weeks after leaving Auckland, [[captain-hull|Captain Hull]] and all his crew

[028-105]were lost in chasing a [[right-whale|whale]], and I and the apprentice were the only

[028-106]seamen left on board."

[028-107]"Then why in the name of peace didn't you take command of the ship?"

[028-108]exclaimed Harris.

[028-109][Illustration: Both men, starting to their feet, looked anxiously

[028-110]around them.]

[028-111]"Because there were five strong niggers who didn't trust me; so, on

[028-112]second thoughts, I determined to keep my old post as cook."

[028-113]"Then do you mean to say that it was mere accident that brought you to

[028-114]the coast of [[africa|Africa]]?"

[028-115]"Not a bit of it; the only accident,--and a very lucky one it was--was

[028-116]meeting you on the very spot where we stranded. But it was my doing

[028-117]that we got so far. Young Sands understood nothing more of navigation

[028-118]than the use of [[ships-log|the log]] and compass. Well, one fine day, you

[028-119]understand, the log remained at the bottom of the sea, and one night

[028-120]the compass was tampered with, so that the 'Pilgrim,' scudding along

[028-121]before a [[storm-at-sea|tempest]], was carried altogether out of her course. You may

[028-122]imagine the young captain was puzzled at the length of the voyage; it

[028-123]would have bewildered a more experienced head than his. Before he was

[028-124]aware of it, we had rounded Cape Horn; I recognized it through the

[028-125]mist. Then at once I put the compass to rights again, and the 'Pilgrim'

[028-126]was carried north-eastwards by a tremendous hurricane to the very place

[028-127]I wanted. The island [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] took for Easter Island was really

[028-128]Tristan d'Acunha."

[028-129]"Good!" said Harris; "I think I understand now how our friends have

[028-130]been persuaded to take Angola for Bolivia. But they are undeceived now,

[028-131]you know," he added.

[028-132]"I know all about that," replied the Portuguese.

[028-133]"Then what do you intend to do?" said Harris.

[028-134]"You will see," answered Negoro significantly; "but first of all tell

[028-135]me something about our employer, old Alvez; how is he?"

[028-136]"Oh, the old rascal is well enough, and will be delighted to see you

[028-137]again," replied Harris.

[028-138]"Is he at the market at Bihé?"

[028-139]"No, he has been at his place at Kazonndé for a year or more."

[028-140]"And how does business go on?"

[028-141]"Badly enough, on this coast," said Harris; "plenty of slaves are

[028-142]waiting to be shipped to the Spanish colonies, but the difficulty is

[028-143]how to get them embarked. The Portuguese authorities on the one hand,

[028-144]and the English cruisers on the other, almost put a stop to exportation

[028-145]altogether; down to the south, near Mossamedes, is the only part where

[028-146]it can be attempted with any chance of success. To pass a caravan

[028-147]through Benguela or Loande is an utter impossibility; neither the

[028-148]governors nor the chefés[1] will listen to a word of reason. Old Alvez

[028-149]is therefore thinking of going in the other direction towards Nyangwe

[028-150]and Lake Tanganyika; he can there exchange his goods for slaves and

[028-151]ivory, and is sure to do a good business with Upper Egypt and the coast

[028-152]of Mozambique, which supplies Madagascar. But I tell you, Negoro," he

[028-153]added gravely, "I believe the time is coming when the [[slavery|slave-trade]] will

[028-154]come to an end altogether. The English missionaries are advancing into

[028-155]the interior. That fellow Livingstone, confound him! has finished his

[028-156]tour of the lakes, and is now working his way towards Angola; then

[028-157]there is another man named Cameron who is talking about crossing the

[028-158]continent from east to west, and it is feared that Stanley the American

[028-159]will do the same. All this exploration, you know, is ruinous to our

[028-160]business, and it is to our interest that not one of these travellers

[028-161]should be allowed to return to tell tales of us in Europe."

[028-162][Footnote 1: Subordinate Portuguese governors at secondary stations.]

[028-163]Harris spoke like a merchant embarrassed by a temporary commercial

[028-164]crisis. The atrocious scenes to which the slave-dealers are accustomed

[028-165]seems to render them impervious to all sense of justice or humanity,

[028-166]and they learn to regard their living merchandize with as small concern

[028-167]as though they were dealing with chests of tea or hogsheads of sugar.

[028-168]But Harris was right when he asserted that civilization must follow the

[028-169]wake of the intrepid pioneers of African discovery. Livingstone first,

[028-170]and after him, Grant, Speke, Burton, Cameron, Stanley, are the heroes

[028-171]whose names will ever be linked with the first dawnings of a brighter

[028-172]age upon the dark wilds of Equatorial Africa.

[028-173]Having ascertained that his accomplice had returned unscrupulous and

[028-174]daring as ever, and fully prepared to pursue his former calling as an

[028-175]agent of old Alvez the slave dealer, Harris inquired what he proposed

[028-176]doing with the survivors of the "Pilgrim" now that they were in his

[028-177]hands.

[028-178]"Divide them into two lots," answered Negoro, without a moment's

[028-179]hesitation, "one for the market, the other...."

[028-180]He did not finish his sentence, but the expression of his countenance

[028-181]was an index to the malignity of his purpose.

[028-182]"Which shall you sell?" asked the American.

[028-183]"The niggers, of course. The old one is not worth much, but the other

[028-184]four ought to fetch a good price at Kazonndé."
