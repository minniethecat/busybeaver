[023-001]And to say the truth, there was nothing extravagant in the way the

[023-002]child's anticipations had been raised, for in Bolivia humming-birds are

[023-003]found in great abundance. The Indians, who weave their plumage into all

[023-004]kinds of artistic designs, have bestowed the most poetical epithets

[023-005]upon these gems of the feathered race. They call them "rays of the

[023-006]sun," and "tresses of the day-star;" at one time they will describe

[023-007]them as "king of flowers," at another as "blossoms of heaven kissing

[023-008]blossoms of earth," or as "the jewel that reflects the sunbeam." In

[023-009]fact their imagination seems to have shaped a suitable distinction for

[023-010]almost every one of the 150 known species of this dazzling little

[023-011]beauty.

[023-012]But however numerous humming-birds might be expected to be in the

[023-013]Bolivian forest, they proved scarce enough at present, and Jack had to

[023-014]content himself with Harris's representations that they did not like

[023-015]solitude, but would be found plentifully at San Felice, where they

[023-016]would be heard all day long humming like a spinning-wheel. Already Jack

[023-017]said he longed to be there, a wish that was so unanimously echoed by

[023-018]all the rest, that they resolved that no stoppage should be allowed

[023-019]beyond what was absolutely indispensable.

[023-020]After a time the forest began to alter its aspect. The trees were even

[023-021]less crowded, opening now and then into wide glades. The soil, cropping

[023-022]up above its carpet of verdure, exhibited veins of rose granite and

[023-023]syenite, like plates of lapis lazuli; on some of the higher ground, the

[023-024]fleshy tubers of the sarsaparilla plant, growing in a hopeless

[023-025]entanglement, made progress a matter of still greater difficulty than

[023-026]in the narrow tracks of the dense forest.

[023-027]At sunset the travellers found that they had accomplished about eight

[023-028]miles from their starting-point. They could not prognosticate what

[023-029]hardships might be in store for them on future days, but it was certain

[023-030]that the experiences of the first day had been neither eventful nor

[023-031]very fatiguing. It was now unanimously agreed that they should make a

[023-032]halt for the night, and as little was to be apprehended from the

[023-033]attacks either of man or beast, it was considered unnecessary to form

[023-034]anything like a regular encampment. One man on guard, to be relieved

[023-035]every few hours, was presumed to be sufficient. Admirable shelter was

[023-036]offered by an enormous mango, the spreading foliage of which formed a

[023-037]kind of natural verandah, sweeping the ground so thoroughly that any

[023-038]one who chose could find sleeping-quarters in its very branches.

[023-039]Simultaneously with the halting of the party there was heard a

[023-040]deafening tumult in the upper boughs. The mango was the roosting place

[023-041]of a colony of grey parrots, a noisy, quarrelsome, and rapacious race,

[023-042]of whose true characteristics the specimens seen in confinement in

[023-043]Europe give no true conception. Their screeching and chattering were

[023-044]such a nuisance that [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] wanted to fire a shot into the middle

[023-045]of them, but Harris seriously dissuaded him, urging that the report of

[023-046]firearms would only serve to reveal their own presence, whilst their

[023-047]greatest safety lay in perfect silence.

[023-048]Supper was prepared. There was little need of cooking. The meal, as

[023-049]before, consisted of preserved meat and biscuit. Fresh water, which

[023-050]they flavoured with a few drops of rum, was obtained from an adjacent

[023-051]stream which trickled through the grass. By way of dessert they had an

[023-052]abundance of ripe mangoes, and the only drawback to their general

[023-053]enjoyment was the discordant outcry which the parrots kept up, as it

[023-054]were in protest against the invasion of what they held to be their own

[023-055]rightful domain.

[023-056]It was nearly dark when supper was ended. The evening shade crept

[023-057]slowly upwards to the tops of the trees, which soon stood out in sharp

[023-058]relief against the lighter background of the sky, while the stars, one

[023-059]by one, began to peep. The wind dropped, and ceased to murmur through

[023-060]the foliage; to the general relief, the parrots desisted from their

[023-061]clatter; and as Nature hushed herself to rest, she seemed to be

[023-062]inviting all her children to follow her example.

[023-063]"Had we not better light a good large fire?" asked Dick.

[023-064]"By no means," said Harris; "the nights are not cold, and under this

[023-065]wide-spreading mango the ground is not likely to be damp. Besides, as I

[023-066]have told you before, our best security consists in our taking care to

[023-067]attract no attention whatever from without."

[023-068][[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] interposed,--

[023-069]"It may be true enough that we have nothing to dread from the Indians,

[023-070]but is it certain that there are no dangerous quadrupeds against which

[023-071]we are bound to be upon our guard?"

[023-072]Harris answered,--

[023-073]"I can positively assure you, madam, that there are no animals here but

[023-074]such as would be infinitely more afraid of you than you would be of

[023-075]them."

[023-076]"Are there any woods without wild beasts?" asked Jack.

[023-077]"All woods are not alike, my boy," replied Harris; "this wood is a

[023-078]great park. As the Indians say, 'Es como el Pariso;' it is like

[023-079]Paradise."

[023-080]Jack persisted,--

[023-081]"There must be snakes, and lions, and tigers."

[023-082]"Ask your mamma, my boy," said Harris, "whether she ever heard of lions

[023-083]and tigers in America?"

[023-084]Mrs. Weldon was endeavouring to put her little boy at his ease on this

[023-085]point, when Cousin Benedict interposed, saying that although there were

[023-086]no lions or tigers, there were plenty of jaguars and panthers in the

[023-087]New World.

[023-088]"And won't they kill us?" demanded Jack eagerly, his apprehensions once

[023-089]more aroused.

[023-090]"Kill you?" laughed Harris; "why, your friend Hercules here could

[023-091]strangle them, two at a time, one in each hand!"

[023-092]"But, please, don't let the panthers come near me!" pleaded Jack,

[023-093]evidently alarmed.

[023-094]"No, no, Master Jack, they shall not come near you. I will give them a

[023-095]good grip first," and the giant displayed his two rows of huge white

[023-096]teeth.

[023-097]Dick Sands proposed that it should be the four younger negroes who

[023-098]should be assigned the task of keeping watch during the night, in

[023-099]attendance upon himself; but Actæon insisted so strongly upon the

[023-100]necessity of Dick's having his full share of rest, that the others were

[023-101]soon brought to the same conviction, and Dick was obliged to yield.

[023-102]Jack valiantly announced his intention of taking one watch, but his

[023-103]sleepy eyelids made it only too plain that he did not know the extent

[023-104]of his own fatigue.

[023-105]"I am sure there are wolves here," he said.

[023-106]"Only such wolves as [[dingo|Dingo]] would swallow at a mouthful," said Harris.

[023-107]"But I am sure there are wolves," he insisted, repeating the word

[023-108]"wolves" again and again, until he tumbled off to sleep against the

[023-109]side of old Nan. Mrs. Weldon gave her little son a silent kiss; it was

[023-110]her loving "good night."

[023-111]Cousin Benedict was missing. Some little time before, he had slipped

[023-112]away in search of "cocuyos," or fire-flies, which he had heard were

[023-113]common in South America.

[023-114]Those singular [[entomology|insects]] emit a bright bluish light from two spots on the

[023-115]side of the thorax, and their colours are so brilliant that they are

[023-116]used as ornaments for ladies' headdresses. Hoping to secure some

[023-117]specimens for his box, Benedict would have wandered to an unlimited

[023-118]distance; but Hercules, faithful to his undertaking, soon discovered

[023-119]him, and heedless of the naturalist's protestations and vociferations,

[023-120]promptly escorted him back to the general rendezvous.

[023-121]Hercules himself was the first to keep watch, but with this exception,

[023-122]the whole party, in another hour, were wrapped in peaceful slumber.

[023-124]CHAPTER XVII

[023-125]MISGIVINGS.

[023-126]Most travellers who have passed a night in a South American forest have

[023-127]been roused from their slumbers by a _matinée musicale_ more fantastic

[023-128]than melodious, performed by monkeys, as their ordinary greeting of the

[023-129]dawn. The yelling, chattering, screeching, howling, all unite to form a

[023-130]chorus almost unearthly in its hideousness.

[023-131]Amongst the various specimens of the numerous family of the quadrumana

[023-132]ought to be recognized the little marikina; the sagouin, with its

[023-133]parti-coloured face; the grey mora, the skin of which is used by the

[023-134]Indians for covering their gun-locks; the sapajou, with its singular

[023-135]tuft over the forehead, and, most remarkable of all, the guariba

[023-136](_Simia Beelzebul_) with its prehensile tail and diabolical countenance.

[023-137]At the first streak of daylight the senior member, as choragus, will

[023-138]start the key-note in a sonorous barytone, the younger monkeys join in

[023-139]tenor and alto, and the concert begins. But this morning there was no

[023-140]concert at all. There was nothing of the wonted serenade to break the

[023-141]silence of the forest. The shrill notes resulting from the rapid

[023-142]vibration of the hyoid bones of the throat were not to be heard.

[023-143]Indians would have been disappointed and perplexed; they are very fond

[023-144]of the flesh of the guariba when smoked and dried, and they would

[023-145]certainly have missed the chant of the monkey "paternosters;" but Dick

[023-146]Sands and his companions were unfamiliar with any of these things, and

[023-147]accordingly the singular quietude was to them a matter of no surprise.

[023-148]They all awoke much refreshed by their night's rest, which there had

[023-149]been nothing to disturb. Jack was by no means the latest in opening his

[023-150]eyes, and his first words were addressed to Hercules, asking him

[023-151]whether he had caught a wolf with his teeth. Hercules had to

[023-152]acknowledge that he had tasted nothing all night, and declared himself

[023-153]quite ready for breakfast. The whole party were unanimous in this

[023-154]respect, and after a brief morning prayer, breakfast was expeditiously

[023-155]served by old Nan. The meal was but a repetition of the last evening's

[023-156]supper, but with their appetites sharpened by the fresh forest air, and

[023-157]anxious to fortify themselves for a good day's march, they did not fail

[023-158]to do ample justice to their simple fare. Even Cousin Benedict, for

[023-159]once in his life at least, partook of his food as if it were not

[023-160]utterly a matter of indifference to him; but he grumbled very much at

[023-161]the restraint to which he considered himself subjected; he could not

[023-162]see the good of coming to such a country as this, if he were to be

[023-163]obliged to walk about with his hands in his pockets; and he protested

[023-164]that if Hercules did not leave him alone and permit him to catch

[023-165]fire-flies, there would be a bone to pick between them. Hercules did

[023-166]not look very much alarmed at the threat. Mrs. Weldon, however, took

[023-167]him aside, and telling him that she did not wish to deprive the

[023-168]enthusiast entirely of his favourite occupation, instructed him to

[023-169]allow her cousin as much [[freedom|liberty]] as possible, provided he did not lose

[023-170]sight of him.

[023-171]The morning meal was over, and it was only seven o'clock when the

[023-172]travellers were once more on their way towards the east, preserving the

[023-173]same marching-order as on the day before.

[023-174]The path was still through luxuriant forest. The vegetable kingdom

[023-175]reigned supreme. As the plateau was immediately adjacent to tropical

[023-176]latitudes, the sun's rays during the summer months descended

[023-177]perpendicularly upon the virgin soil, and the vast amount of heat thus

[023-178]obtained combined with the abundant moisture retained in the subsoil,

[023-179]caused vegetation to assume a character which was truly magnificent.

[023-180]Dick Sands could not overcome a certain sense of mystification. Here

[023-181]they were, as Harris told them, in the region of the pampas, a word

[023-182]which he knew in the Quichna dialect signifies "a plain;" but he had

[023-183]always read that these plains were characterized by a deficiency alike

[023-184]of water, of trees, and rocks; he had always understood that during the

[023-185]rainy season, thistles spring up in great abundance and grow until they

[023-186]form thickets that are well-nigh impenetrable; he had imagined that the

[023-187]few dwarf trees and prickly shrubs that exist during the summer only

[023-188]stamp the general scene with an aspect of yet more thorough bareness

[023-189]and desolation. But how different was everything to all this! The

[023-190]forest never ceased to stretch away interminably to the horizon. There

[023-191]were no tokens of the rough nakedness that he had expected. Dick seemed

[023-192]to be driven to the conclusion that Harris was right in describing this

[023-193]plateau of Atacama, which he had for his part most firmly believed to

[023-194]be a vast desert between the Andes and the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]], as a region that

[023-195]was quite exceptional in its natural features.
