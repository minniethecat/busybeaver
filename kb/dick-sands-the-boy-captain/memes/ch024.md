[024-001]It was not in [[dick-sands|Dick]]'s character to keep his reflections to himself. In

[024-002]the course of the morning he expressed his extreme surprise at finding

[024-003]the pampas answer so little to his preconceived ideas.

[024-004]"Have I not understood correctly," he said, "that the pampas is similar

[024-005]to the North American savannahs, only less marshy?"

[024-006]Harris replied that such was indeed a correct description of the pampas

[024-007]of Rio Colorado, and the Ilanos of Venezuela and the Orinoco.

[024-008]"But," he continued, "I own I am as much astonished as yourself at the

[024-009]character of this region; I have never crossed the plateau before, and

[024-010]I must confess it is altogether different to what you find beyond the

[024-011]Andes towards the Atlantic."

[024-012]"You don't mean that we are going to cross the Andes?" said Dick, in

[024-013]sudden alarm.

[024-014]Harris smiled.

[024-015]"No, no, indeed. With our limited means of transport such an

[024-016]undertaking would have been rash in the extreme. We had better have

[024-017]kept to the coast for ever rather than incur such a risk. Our

[024-018]destination, San Felice, is on this side of the range, and in order to

[024-019]reach it, we shall not have to leave the plateau, of which the greatest

[024-020]elevation is but little over 1500 feet."

[024-021]"And you say," Dick persisted, "that you have really no fear of losing

[024-022]your way in a forest such as this, a forest into which you have never

[024-023]set foot before?"

[024-024]"No fear whatever," Harris answered; "so accustomed am I to travelling

[024-025]of this kind, that I can steer my way by a thousand signs revealing

[024-026]themselves in the growth of the trees, and in the composition of the

[024-027]soil, which would never present themselves to your notice. I assure you

[024-028]that I anticipate no difficulties."

[024-029]This conversation was not heard by any of the rest of the party. Harris

[024-030]seemed to speak as frankly as he did fearlessly, and Dick felt that

[024-031]there might be, after all, no just grounds for any of his own

[024-032]misgivings.

[024-033]Five days passed by, and the 12th of April arrived without any special

[024-034]incident. Nine miles had been the average distance accomplished in a

[024-035]day; regular periods of rest had been taken, and, except that Jack's

[024-036]spirits had somewhat flagged, the fatigue did not seem to have

[024-037]interfered with the general good health of the travellers.

[024-038]First disappointed of his India-rubber-tree, and then of his humming

[024-039]birds, Jack had inquired about the beautiful parrots which he had been

[024-040]led to expect he should see in this wonderful forest. Where were the

[024-041]bright green macaws? where were the gaudy aras with their bare white

[024-042]cheeks and pointed tails, which seem never to light upon the ground?

[024-043]and where, too, were all the brilliant parroquets, with their feathered

[024-044]faces, and indeed the whole variety of those forest chatterers of which

[024-045]the Indians affirm that they speak the language of nations long extinct?

[024-046]It is true that there was no lack of the common grey parrots with

[024-047]crimson tails, but these were no novelty; Jack had seen plenty of them

[024-048]before, for owing to their reputation of being the most clever in

[024-049]mimickry of the Psittacidæ, they have been domesticated everywhere in

[024-050]both the Old and New worlds.

[024-051][Illustration: "Don't Fire!"]

[024-052]But Jack's dissatisfaction was nothing compared to Cousin Benedict's.

[024-053]In spite of being allowed to wander away from the rank, he had failed

[024-054]to discover a single insect which was worth the pursuit; not even a

[024-055]fire-fly danced at night; nature seemed to be mocking him, and his

[024-056]ill-humour increased accordingly.

[024-057]In this way the journey was continued for four days longer, and on the

[024-058]16th it was estimated that they must have travelled between eighty and

[024-059]ninety miles north-eastwards from the coast. Harris positively asserted

[024-060]that they could not be much more than twenty miles from San Felice, and

[024-061]that by pushing forwards they might expect in eight-and-forty hours to

[024-062]find themselves lodged in comfortable quarters.

[024-063]But although they had thus succeeded in traversing this vast

[024-064]table-land, they had not seen one human inhabitant. Dick was more than

[024-065]ever perplexed, and it was a subject of bitter regret to him that they

[024-066]had not stranded upon some more frequented part of the shore, near some

[024-067]village or plantation where [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] might long since have found a

[024-068]suitable refuge.

[024-069]Deserted, however, as the country apparently was by man, it had

[024-070]latterly shown itself much more abundantly tenanted by animals. Many a

[024-071]time a long, plaintive cry was heard, which Harris attributed to the

[024-072]tardigrades or sloths often found in wooded districts, and known by the

[024-073]name of "ais;" and in the middle of the dinner-halt on this day, a loud

[024-074]hissing suddenly broke upon the air which made Mrs. Weldon start to her

[024-075]feet in alarm.

[024-076]"A serpent!" cried Dick, catching up his loaded gun.

[024-077]The negroes, following Dick's example, were in a moment on the alert.

[024-078]"Don't fire!" cried Harris.

[024-079]There was indeed nothing improbable in the supposition that a "sucuru,"

[024-080]a species of boa, sometimes measuring forty feet in length, had just

[024-081]moved itself in the long grass at their side, but Harris affirmed that

[024-082]the "sucuru" never hisses, and declared that the noise had really come

[024-083]from animals of an entirely inoffensive character.

[024-084]"What animals?" asked Dick, always eager for information, which it must

[024-085]be granted Harris seemed always equally anxious to give.

[024-086]"Antelopes," replied Harris; "but, hush! not a sound, or you will

[024-087]frighten them away."

[024-088]"Antelopes!" cried Dick; "I must see them; I must get close to them."

[024-089]"More easily said than done," answered Harris, shaking his head; but

[024-090]Dick was not to be diverted from his purpose, and, gun in hand, crept

[024-091]into the grass. He had not advanced many yards before a herd of about a

[024-092]dozen gazelles, graceful in body, with short, pointed horns, dashed

[024-093]past him like a glowing cloud, and disappeared in the underwood without

[024-094]giving him time to take a shot.

[024-095]"I told you beforehand what you would have to expect," said Harris, as

[024-096]Dick, with a considerable sense of disappointment, returned to the

[024-097]party.

[024-098]Impossible, however, as it had been fairly to scrutinize the antelopes,

[024-099]such was hardly the case with another herd of animals, the

[024-100]identification of which led to a somewhat singular discussion between

[024-101]Harris and the rest.

[024-102][[About]] four o'clock on the afternoon of the same day, the travellers

[024-103]were halting for a few moments near an opening in the forest, when

[024-104]three or four large animals emerged from a thicket about a hundred

[024-105]paces ahead, and scampered off at full speed. In spite of what Harris

[024-106]had urged, Dick put his gun to his shoulder, and was on the very point

[024-107]of firing, when Harris knocked the rifle quickly aside.

[024-108]"They were giraffes!" shouted Dick.

[024-109]The announcement awakened the curiosity of Jack, who quickly scrambled

[024-110]to his feet upon the saddle on which he was lounging.

[024-111]"My dear Dick," said Mrs. Weldon, "there are no giraffes in America!"

[024-112][Illustration: A herd of gazelles dashed past him like a glowing cloud.]

[024-113]"Certainly not," cried Harris; "they were not giraffes, they were

[024-114]ostriches which you saw!"

[024-115]"Ostriches with four legs! that will never do! what do you say. Mrs.

[024-116]Weldon?"

[024-117]Mrs. Weldon replied that she had certainly taken the animals for

[024-118]quadrupeds, and all the negroes were under the same impression.

[024-119]Laughing heartily, Harris said it was far from an uncommon thing for an

[024-120]inexperienced eye to mistake a large ostrich for a small giraffe; the

[024-121]shape of both was so similar, that it often quite escaped observation

[024-122]as to whether the long necks terminated in a beak or a muzzle; besides,

[024-123]what need of discussion could there be when the fact was established

[024-124]that giraffes are unknown in the New World? The reasoning was plausible

[024-125]enough, and Mrs Weldon and the negroes were soon convinced. But Dick

[024-126]was far from satisfied.

[024-127]"I did not know that there was an American ostrich!" he again objected.

[024-128]"Oh, yes," replied Harris promptly, "there is a species called the

[024-129]nandu, which is very well known here; we shall probably see some more

[024-130]of them."

[024-131]The statement was correct; the nandu is common in the plains of South

[024-132]America, and is distinguished from the African ostrich by having three

[024-133]toes, all furnished with claws. It is a fine bird, sometimes exceeding

[024-134]six feet in height; it has a short beak, and its wings are furnished

[024-135]with blue-grey plumes. Harris appeared well acquainted with the bird,

[024-136]and proceeded to give a very precise account of its habits. In

[024-137]concluding his remarks, he again pressed upon Dick his most urgent

[024-138]request that he should abstain from firing upon any animal whatever. It

[024-139]was of the utmost consequence.

[024-140]Dick made no reply. He was silent and thoughtful. Grave doubts had

[024-141]arisen in his mind, and he could neither explain nor dispel them.

[024-142]When the march was resumed on the following day, Harris asserted his

[024-143]conviction that another four-and-twenty hours would bring them to the

[024-144]hacienda.

[024-145]"And there, madam," he said, addressing Mrs. Weldon, "we can offer you

[024-146]every essential comfort, though you may not find the luxuries of your

[024-147]own home in [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]."

[024-148]Mrs. Weldon repeated her expression of gratitude for the proffered

[024-149]hospitality, owning that she should now be exceedingly glad to reach

[024-150]the farm, as she was anxious about her little son, who appeared to be

[024-151]threatened with the symptoms of incipient fever.

[024-152]Harris could not deny that although the climate was usually very

[024-153]healthy, it nevertheless did occasionally produce a kind of

[024-154]intermittent fever during March and April.

[024-155]"But nature has provided the proper remedy," said Dick; and perceiving

[024-156]that Harris did not comprehend his meaning, he continued, "Are we not

[024-157]in the region of the quinquinas, the bark of which is notoriously the

[024-158]medicine with which attacks of fever are usually treated? for my part,

[024-159]I am amazed that we have not seen numbers of them already."

[024-160]"Ah! yes, yes; I know what you mean," answered Harris, after a moment's

[024-161]hesitation; "they are trees, however, not always easy to find; they

[024-162]rarely grow in groups, and in spite of their large leaves and fragrant

[024-163]red blossom, the Indians themselves often have a difficulty in

[024-164]recognizing them; the feature that distinguishes them most is their

[024-165]evergreen foliage."

[024-166]At Mrs. Weldon's request, Harris promised to point out the tree if he

[024-167]should see one, but added that when she reached the hacienda, she would

[024-168]be able to obtain some sulphate of quinine, which was much more

[024-169]efficacious than the unprepared bark.[1]

[024-170][Footnote 1: This bark was formerly, reduced to powder, known as

[024-171]"Pulvis Jesuiticus," because in the year 1649 the Jesuits in Rome

[024-172]imported a large quantity of it from their missionaries in South

[024-173]America.]

[024-174]The day passed without further incident. No rain had fallen at present,

[024-175]though the warm mist that rose from the soil betokened an approaching

[024-176]change of weather; the rainy season was certainly not far distant, but

[024-177]to travellers who indulged the expectation of being in a few hours in a

[024-178]place of shelter, this was not a matter of great concern.

[024-179][Illustration: A halt was made for the night beneath a grove of lofty

[024-180]trees.]

[024-181]Evening came, and a halt was made for the night beneath a grove of

[024-182]lofty trees. If Harris had not miscalculated, they could hardly be more

[024-183]than about six miles from their destination; so confirmed, however, was

[024-184]Dick Sands in his strange suspicions, that nothing could induce him to

[024-185]relax any of the usual precautions, and he particularly insisted upon

[024-186]the negroes, turn by turn, keeping up the accustomed watch.

[024-187]Worn out by fatigue, the little party were glad to lie down, but they

[024-188]had scarcely dropped off to sleep when they were aroused by a sharp cry.

[024-189]"Who's that? who's there? what's the matter?" exclaimed Dick, the first

[024-190]to rise to his feet.

[024-191]"It is I," answered Benedict's voice; "I am bitten. Something has

[024-192]bitten me."
