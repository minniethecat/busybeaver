[036-001]Exasperated by the rough attack that was made upon him, [[dick-sands|Dick]] had seized

[036-002]the havildar's gun and broken it, almost wrenching it from his hands,

[036-003]when several soldiers simultaneously assailed him, and would have

[036-004]struck him down and killed him upon the spot, had not one of the

[036-005]chiefs, an Arab of huge stature and ferocious countenance, interfered

[036-006]to stop them.

[036-007]This Arab was the Ibn Hamish of whom Harris had spoken to [[negoro|Negoro]]. He

[036-008]said a few words which Dick could not understand, and the soldiers,

[036-009]with manifest reluctance, relaxed their hold and retired. It was

[036-010]evident that although Dick was not to be permitted to hold any

[036-011]communication with the rest of his party, orders had been given that

[036-012]his life was to be protected.

[036-013][Illustration: The start was made.]

[036-014]It was now nine o'clock, and the beating of drums and the blowing of

[036-015]coodoo[1] horns gave the signal that the morning march was to be

[036-016]continued. Instantly chiefs, soldiers, porters, and slaves were upon

[036-017]their feet, and arranged themselves in their various groups with a

[036-018]havildar bearing a bright-coloured banner at their head.

[036-019][Footnote: Coodoo, a ruminant common in [[africa|Africa]].]

[036-020]The order was given; the start was made. A strange song was heard

[036-021]rising in the air. It was a song, not of the victors, but of the

[036-022]vanquished. The slaves were chanting an imprecation on their

[036-023]oppressors; and the burden of the chorus was that captured, tortured,

[036-024]slain--after death they would return and avenge their wrongs upon their

[036-025]murderers!

[036-026]CHAPTER VIII.

[036-027]NOTES BY THE WAY.

[036-028]The storm of the preceding evening had now passed away, but the sky was

[036-029]still cloudy and the weather far from settled. It was the 19th of

[036-030]April, the time of the _masika_, or second period of the rainy season,

[036-031]so that for the next two or three weeks the nights might be expected to

[036-032]be wet.

[036-033]On leaving the banks of the Coanza the [[slave-caravan|caravan]] proceeded due east.

[036-034]Soldiers marched at the head and in the rear, as well as upon the

[036-035]flanks of the troop; any escape of the prisoners, therefore, even if

[036-036]they had not been loaded with their fetters, would have been utterly

[036-037]impossible. They were all driven along without any attempt at order,

[036-038]the havildars using their whips unsparingly upon them whenever they

[036-039]showed signs of flagging. Some poor mothers could be seen carrying two

[036-040]infants, one on each arm, whilst others led by the hand naked children,

[036-041]whose feet were sorely cut by the rough ground over which they had trod.

[036-042]Ibn Hamish, the Arab who had interfered between Dick and the havildar,

[036-043]acted as commander to the caravan, and was here, there, and everywhere;

[036-044]not moved in the least by the sufferings of the captives, but obliged

[036-045]to be attentive to the importunities of the soldiers and porters, who

[036-046]were perpetually clamouring for extra rations, or demanding an

[036-047]immediate halt. Loud were the discussions that arose, and the uproar

[036-048]became positively deafening when the quarrelsome voices rose above the

[036-049]shrieks of the slaves, many of whom found themselves treading upon soil

[036-050]already stained by the blood of the ranks in front.

[036-051][Illustration: If ever the havildar strolled a few yards away, Bat took

[036-052]the opportunity of murmuring a few words of encouragement to this poor

[036-053]old father.]

[036-054]No chance again opened for Dick to get any communication with his

[036-055]friends, who had been sent to the van of the procession. Urged on by

[036-056]the whip they continued to march in single file, their heads in the

[036-057]heavy forks. If ever the havildar strolled a few yards away, Bat took

[036-058]the opportunity of murmuring a few words of encouragement to his poor

[036-059]old father, while he tried to pick out the easiest path for him, and to

[036-060]relax the pace to suit his enfeebled limbs. Large tears rolled down old

[036-061]Tom's cheeks when he found that his son's efforts only resulted in

[036-062]bringing down upon his back some sharp cuts of the havildar's whip.

[036-063]Actæon and Austin, subject to hardly less brutality, followed a few

[036-064]steps behind, but all four could not help feeling envious at the luck

[036-065]of Hercules, who might have dangers to encounter, but at least had his

[036-066][[freedom|liberty]].

[036-067]Immediately upon their capture, Tom had revealed to his companions the

[036-068]fact that they were in Africa, and informing them how they had been

[036-069]betrayed by Harris, made them understand that they had no mercy to

[036-070]expect.

[036-071]Old Nan had been placed amongst a group of women in the central ranks.

[036-072]She was chained to a young mother with two children, the one at the

[036-073]breast, the other only three years old, and scarcely able to walk.

[036-074]Moved by compassion, Nan took the little one into her own arms, thus

[036-075]not only [[rescue|saving]] it from fatigue, but from the blows it would very

[036-076]likely have received for lagging behind. The mother shed tears of

[036-077]gratitude, but the weight was almost too much for Nan's strength, and

[036-078]she felt as if she must break down under her self-imposed burden. She

[036-079]thought fondly of little Jack, and imagining him borne along in the

[036-080]arms of his weary mother, could not help asking herself whether she

[036-081]should ever see him or her kind mistress again.

[036-082]Far in the rear, Dick could not see the head of the caravan except

[036-083]occasionally, when the ground was rather on the rise. The voices of the

[036-084]agents and drivers, harsh and excited as they were, scarcely roused him

[036-085]from his melancholy reflections. His thoughts were not of himself nor

[036-086]of his own sufferings; his whole attention was absorbed in looking for

[036-087]some traces of [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]]'s progress; if she, too, was being taken to

[036-088]Kazonndé, her route must also lie this way. But he could discover no

[036-089]trace of her having been conducted by this line of march, and could

[036-090]only hope that she was being spared the cruelties which he was himself

[036-091]witnessing.

[036-092]The forest extended for about twenty miles to the east of the Coanza,

[036-093]but whether it was that the trees had been destroyed by the ravages of

[036-094][[entomology|insects]], or broken down before they had made their growth by being

[036-095]trampled on by elephants, they were growing much less thickly than in

[036-096]the immediate vicinity of the river. There were numbers of

[036-097]cotton-trees, seven or eight feet high, from which are manufactured the

[036-098]black-and-white striped stuffs that are worn in the interior of the

[036-099]province; but, upon the whole, progress was not much impeded either by

[036-100]shrubs or underwood. Occasionally the caravan plunged into jungles of

[036-101]reeds like bamboos, their stalks an inch in diameter, so tall that only

[036-102]an elephant or giraffe could have reared above them, and through which

[036-103]none excepting such as had a very intimate knowledge of the country

[036-104]could possibly have made their way.

[036-105]Starting every morning at daybreak they marched till noon, when an

[036-106]hour's halt was made. Packets of manioc were then unfastened, and doled

[036-107]out in sparing quantities among the slaves; sometimes, when the

[036-108]soldiers had plundered some village, a little goat's flesh or some

[036-109]sweet potatoes were added to the meal; but generally the fatigue,

[036-110]aggravated by inadequate rest, took away the appetite, and when

[036-111]meal-time arrived many of the slaves could hardly eat at all. During

[036-112]the first eight days' march from the Coanza no less than twenty

[036-113]unfortunate wretches had fallen upon the road, and had been left

[036-114]behind, a prey to the lions, panthers, and leopards that prowled in the

[036-115]wake. As Dick heard their roars in the stillness of the night, he

[036-116]trembled as he thought of Hercules. Nevertheless, had the opportunity

[036-117]offered itself, he would not for a moment have hesitated in making his

[036-118]own escape to the wilderness.

[036-119][Illustration: The caravan had been attacked on the flank by a dozen or

[036-120]more crocodiles.]

[036-121]The two hundred and fifty miles between the river and Kazonndé were

[036-122]accomplished in what the traders call marches of ten miles each,

[036-123]including the halts at night and midday. The journey cannot be better

[036-124]described than by a few rough notes that Dick Sands made upon his way.

[036-125]_April 25th_.--Saw a village surrounded with bamboo palisading, eight

[036-126]or nine feet high. Fields round planted with maize, beans, and sorghum.

[036-127]Two negroes captured, fifteen killed, rest took to flight.

[036-128]_26th_.--Crossed a torrent 150 yards wide. Bridge formed of trunks of

[036-129]trees and creepers. Piles nearly gave way; two women fastened to a

[036-130]fork; one of them, carrying a baby, fell into the water. Water quickly

[036-131]tinged with blood; crocodiles seen under bridge; risk of stepping into

[036-132]their very jaws.

[036-133]_28th_.--Crossed a forest of bauhinias; great trees, the iron-wood of

[036-134]the Portuguese. Heavy rain; ground sodden; marching difficult. Caught

[036-135]sight of Nan in the middle of caravan; she was toiling along with a

[036-136]black child in her arms; the woman with her limping, and blood

[036-137]trickling from her shoulder.

[036-138]_29th_.--Camp at night under a huge baobab, with white flowers and

[036-139]light green leaves. Lions and leopards roaring all night. A soldier

[036-140]fired at a panther. What has become of Hercules?

[036-141]_30th_.--Rainy season said to be over till November. First touch of

[036-142]African winter. Dew very heavy. Plains all flooded. Easterly winds:

[036-143]difficulty of respiration; susceptibility to fever. No trace of Mrs.

[036-144]Weldon; cannot tell whether she is ahead. Fear Jack may have a return

[036-145]of fever.

[036-146]_May 5th_.--Forced to march several stages across flooded plains, water

[036-147]up to the waist; many leeches sticking to the skin. Lotus and papyrus

[036-148]upon higher ground. Great heavy leaves, like cabbages, beneath the

[036-149]water, make many stumble as they walk. Saw large numbers of little

[036-150]fish, silurus-species; these are caught by the natives, and sold to the

[036-151]caravans.

[036-152]_7th_.--Plain still inundated. Last night, no halting-place to be

[036-153]found. Marched on through the darkness. Great misery. Except for Mrs.

[036-154]Weldon, life not worth having; for her sake must hold out. Loud cries

[036-155]heard. Saw, by the lightning, soldiers breaking large boughs from the

[036-156]resinous trees that emerged from the water. The caravan had been

[036-157]attacked on the flank by a dozen or more crocodiles; women and children

[036-158]seized and carried off to what Livingstone calls their "pasture-lands,"

[036-159]the holes where they deposit their prey until it is decomposed. Myself

[036-160]grazed by the scales of one of them. A slave close beside me torn out

[036-161]of the fork, which was snapped in half. How the poor fellow's cry of

[036-162]agony rings in my ear! This morning, twenty missing. Tom and the

[036-163]others, thank God! are still alive. They are on in front. Once Bat made

[036-164]a sharp turn, and Tom caught sight of me. Nothing to be seen of Nan;

[036-165]was she, poor creature, one of those that the crocodiles had got?

[036-166]_8th_.--After twenty-four hours in the water we have crossed the plain.

[036-167]We have halted on a hill. The sun helps to dry us. Nothing to eat

[036-168]except a little manioc and a few handfuls of maize. Only muddy water to

[036-169]drink. Impossible for Mrs. Weldon to survive these hardships; I hope

[036-170]from my heart that she has been taken some other way. Small-pox has

[036-171]broken out in the caravan; those that have it are to be left behind.

[036-172]_9th_.--Started at dawn. No stragglers allowed; sick and weary must be

[036-173]kept together by havildars' whip; the losses were considerable. Living

[036-174]skeletons all round. Rejoiced once more to catch sight of Nan. She was

[036-175]not carrying the child any longer; she was alone; the chain was round

[036-176]her waist, but she had the loose end thrown over her shoulder. I got

[036-177]close to her; suppose I am altered, as she did not know me. After I had

[036-178]called her by name several times she stared at me, and at last said,

[036-179]"Ah, Mr. Dick, is it you? you will not see me here much longer." Her

[036-180]cadaverous look pained my very soul, but I tried to speak hopefully.

[036-181]Poor Nan shook her head. "I shall never see my dear mistress again; no,

[036-182]nor master Jack; I shall soon die." Anxious to help her, I would gladly

[036-183]have carried the end of the chain which she had been obliged to bear

[036-184]because her fellow-prisoner was dead. A rough hand was soon upon my

[036-185]shoulder; a cruel lash had made Nan retreat to the general crowd,

[036-186]whilst, at the bidding of an Arab chief, I was hustled back to the very

[036-187]hindmost rank of the procession. I overheard the word Negoro, in a way

[036-188]that convinced me that it is under the direction of the Portuguese that

[036-189]I am subject to this hard indignity.
