# Abolitionism

The international movement to end the [[slavery|slave trade]] and emancipate enslaved people, presented by Jules Verne as a moral triumph of civilization in Chapter 26 of *[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]]: The Boy [[captain-hull|Captain]]*.

## Definition

Abolitionism was the political and social campaign to end the transatlantic [[slavery|slave trade]] and emancipate enslaved [[africa|Africa]]ns throughout European colonies and the Americas. In Verne's novel, the history of abolition provides the moral framework for understanding the horrors the protagonists face in equatorial [[africa|Africa]].

## Role in the Novel

The novel's extensive historical survey of abolition (Chapter 26, 026-079–211) serves to frame the [[slavery|slave trade]] as a criminal enterprise persisting despite decades of legal prohibition. Verne positions the abolitionist movement as one of civilization's great moral achievements, contrasting it with the "barbarous 'man-hunts'" that still occur in [[africa|Central Africa]] (026-091).

The rescued [[africa|Africa]]ns—[[tom|Tom]], Bat, Austin, Actaeon, and [[hercules|Hercules]]—are themselves products of abolition: they are free men who gained their [[freedom|liberty]] through "the anti-slavery war" in the United States (005-118). Their re-capture in [[africa|Africa]] represents the tragic irony that emancipation was not complete.

## Historical Development

The abolitionist movement began with the Quakers in 1751 in North America (026-131). Several Northern states—Virginia, Connecticut, Massachusetts, and Pennsylvania—prohibited the trade and "liberated the slaves, in spite of the cost" (026-135–136).

The campaign gained European momentum: England formally "prohibited the [[slavery|slave-trade]] in her colonies" in 1807, followed by France in 1814 (026-147–148). In 1820, the United States declared slave-trading an act of piracy "punishable with death," with Great Britain following in 1824 (026-153–155).

Emancipation proceeded in stages:
- 1833: British Parliament emancipated all slaves in British colonies
- 1838: 670,000 slaves declared free in British territories (026-166–167)
- 1848: French Republic liberated 260,000 slaves in its colonies (026-167–168)
- 1859–1865: American Civil War "finished the work of emancipation by extending it to the whole of North America" (026-169–171)

## Continuing Injustice

Despite these achievements, Verne acknowledges the incomplete nature of abolition. The [[slavery|slave trade]] continued "for the advantage of the Spanish and Portuguese colonies" and to supply "Turkish or Arab populations of the East" (026-173–175). Brazil, "although she has not emancipated her former slaves, does not receive any new" (026-176). The annual toll remained staggering: "no less than 24,000 slaves annually reach the coast, a number that hardly represents a tenth part of those who are massacred" (026-190–191).
