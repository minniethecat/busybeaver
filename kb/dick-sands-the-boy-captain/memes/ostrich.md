# Ostrich

A large flightless bird that becomes central to one of [[harris|Harris]]'s most desperate [[treachery|deception]]s. When the travelers encounter a herd of giraffes, the treacherous guide insists they are ostriches, attempting to maintain the fiction that the party is still in South America.

## Overview

- **Type**: Species
- **Species**: *Struthio camelus*
- **Owner/Associated with**: None (wild animal)
- **Chapters**: 24–26, 28, 30

## Role in the Narrative

The ostrich appears entirely through a lie — [[harris|Harris]]'s claim that the animals the party has seen are ostriches, not giraffes. This claim is central to his geographical [[treachery|deception]]: ostriches exist in both [[africa|Africa]] and South America (the rhea, or American ostrich), so identifying the animals as ostriches would preserve the fiction that the travelers are on the South American pampas rather than the [[africa|Africa]]n savanna.

[[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] immediately rejects this identification, having clearly seen giraffes. [[harris|Harris]] then attempts a dialectical retreat, first claiming "they were not giraffes, they were ostriches" [024-113], then conceding that they might be "a large ostrich" that an inexperienced eye might mistake for "a small giraffe" [024-120]. When pressed further, he invents "the American ostrich" as a distinct species [024-127], a desperate gambit that stretches credibility. Verne notes that the true American ostrich (rhea) differs from the [[africa|Africa]]n ostrich in having "three toes" rather than two [024-132].

The ostrich lie becomes a recurring motif in the novel's later chapters. The party repeatedly recalls "how [[harris|Harris]] had tried to make them believe that giraffes were ostriches" [025-124], and the incident is invoked as proof of his treachery [026-061][028-018]. In a later passage, actual ostriches do appear, "occasionally a dense cloud" of dust raised by birds "being represented as ostriches" [030-121].

## Description in the Text

Verne uses the ostrich mostly as a foil for the giraffe in the [[treachery|deception]] scene, but the distinction between [[africa|Africa]]n and American ostriches is given genuine natural-history attention. The [[africa|Africa]]n ostrich (*Struthio camelus*) is correctly noted as having two toes, while the South American rhea has three [024-132].

> Harris tried to make them believe that giraffes were ostriches. [025-124]

## Historical/Natural History Context

By 1878, the [[africa|Africa]]n ostrich (*Struthio camelus*) and the South American rhea (*Rhea americana*) were both well-documented by naturalists, and their anatomical differences — particularly the number of toes — were standard zoo[[ships-log|log]]ical knowledge. Verne's precise notation of the toe count (two vs. three) reflects the 19th-century natural-history education he aimed to embed in his adventure novels. The scene serves a double purpose: advancing the plot while teaching young readers a zoo[[ships-log|log]]ical fact.
