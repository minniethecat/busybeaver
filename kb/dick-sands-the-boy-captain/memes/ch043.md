[043-001]Nothing, however, transpired to give the least intimation that any of

[043-002]the prisoners were to be treated otherwise than they had been upon the

[043-003]journey from the Coanza. Excepting such as were retained for old Alvez'

[043-004]personal service, all the slaves had been sold, and the storehouses

[043-005]were now full of stuffs and ivory, the stuffs destined to be sent into

[043-006]the central provinces and the ivory to be exported. The establishment

[043-007]was thus no longer crowded as it had been, and [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]] and Jack

[043-008]were lodged in a different hut to Cousin Benedict. All three, however,

[043-009]took their meals together and were allowed a sufficient diet of mutton

[043-010]or goats'-flesh, vegetables, manioc, sorghum and native fruits. With

[043-011]the traders' servants they held no communication, but Halima, a young

[043-012]slave who had been told off to attend to Mrs. Weldon, evinced for her

[043-013]new mistress an attachment which, though rough, was evidently sincere.

[043-014][Illustration: He contented himself with the permission to go where he

[043-015]pleased within the limits of the palisade.]

[043-016]Old Alvez, who occupied the principal house in thedépôt, was rarely

[043-017]seen; whilst the non-appearance of either Harris or [[negoro|Negoro]] caused Mrs.

[043-018]Weldon much surprise and perplexity. In the midst of all her troubles,

[043-019]too, she was haunted by the thought of the anxiety her husband must be

[043-020]suffering on her account. Unaware of her having embarked on board the

[043-021]"[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]," at first he would have wondered at steamer after steamer

[043-022]arriving at [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] without her. After a while the "Pilgrim"

[043-023]would have been registered amongst the number of missing ships; and it

[043-024]was certain the intelligence would be forwarded to him by his

[043-025]correspondents, that the vessel had sailed from Auckland with his wife

[043-026]and child on board. What was he to imagine? he might refuse to believe

[043-027]that they had perished at sea, but he would never dream of their having

[043-028]been carried to [[africa|Africa]], and would certainly institute a search in no

[043-029]other direction than on the coast of America, or amongst the isles of

[043-030]the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]]. She had not the faintest hope of her whereabouts being

[043-031]discovered, and involuntarily her thoughts turned to the possibility of

[043-032]making an escape. She might well feel her heart sink within her at the

[043-033]bare idea; even if she should succeed in eluding the vigilance of the

[043-034]watch, there were two hundred miles of dense forest to be traversed

[043-035]before the coast could be reached; nevertheless, it revealed itself to

[043-036]her as her last chance, and failing all else, she resolved to hazard it.

[043-037]But, first of all, she determined, if it were possible, to discover the

[043-038]ultimate design of Negoro. She was not kept long in suspense. On the

[043-039]6th of June, just a week after the royal funeral, the Portuguese

[043-040]entered the depót, in which he had not set foot since his return, and

[043-041]made his way straight to the hut in which he knew he should find the

[043-042]prisoner. Benedict was out insect-hunting; Jack, under Halima's charge,

[043-043]was being taken for a walk. Mrs. Weldon was alone.

[043-044]Negoro pushed open the door, and said abruptly,--

[043-045]"Mrs. Weldon, I have come to tell you, that Tom and his lot have been

[043-046]sold for the Ujiji market; Nan died on her way here; and [[dick-sands|Dick Sands]] is

[043-047]dead too."

[043-048]Mrs. Weldon uttered a cry of horror.

[043-049]"Yes, Mrs. Weldon," he continued; "he has got what he deserved; he shot

[043-050]Harris, and has been executed for the murder. And here you are alone!

[043-051]mark this! alone and in my power!"

[043-052]What Negoro said was true; Tom, Bat, Actæon, and Austin had all been

[043-053]sent off that morning on their way to Ujiji.

[043-054]Mrs. Weldon groaned bitterly.

[043-055]Negoro went on.

[043-056]"If I chose, I could still further avenge upon you the ill-treatment I

[043-057]got on board that ship; but it does not suit my purpose to kill you.

[043-058]You and that boy of yours, and that idiot of a fly-catcher, all have a

[043-059]certain value in the market. I mean to sell you."

[043-060]"You dare not!" said Mrs. Weldon firmly; "you know you are making an

[043-061]idle threat; who do you suppose would purchase people of white blood?"

[043-062]"I know a customer who will give me the price I mean to ask," replied

[043-063]Negoro with a brutal grin.

[043-064]She bent down her head; only too well she knew that such things were

[043-065]possible in this horrid land.

[043-066]"Tell me who he is!" she said; "tell the name of the man who ..."

[043-067]"James Weldon," he answered slowly.

[043-068]"My husband!" she cried; "what do you mean?"

[043-069]"I mean what I say. I mean to make your husband buy you back at my

[043-070]price; and if he likes to pay for them, he shall have his son and his

[043-071]cousin too."

[043-072][Illustration: "I suppose Weldon will not mind coming to fetch you?"]

[043-073]"And when, and how, may I ask, do you propose to manage this?" replied

[043-074]Mrs. Weldon, forcing herself to be calm.

[043-075]"Here, and soon too. I suppose Weldon will not mind coming to fetch

[043-076]you."

[043-077]"He would not hesitate to come; but how could he know we are here?"

[043-078]"I will go to him. I have money that will take me to San Francisco."

[043-079]"What you stole from the 'Pilgrim'?" said Mrs. Weldon.

[043-080]"Just so," replied Negoro; "and I have plenty more I suppose when

[043-081]Weldon hears that you are a prisoner in Central Africa, he will not

[043-082]think much of a hundred thousand dollars."

[043-083]"But how is he to know the truth of your statement?"

[043-084]"I shall take him a letter from you. You shall represent me as your

[043-085]faithful servant, just escaped from the hands of savages."

[043-086]"A letter such as that I will never write; never," said Mrs. Weldon

[043-087]decisively.

[043-088]"What? what? you refuse?"

[043-089]"I refuse."

[043-090]She had all the natural cravings of a woman and a wife, but so

[043-091]thoroughly was she aware of the treachery of the man she had to deal

[043-092]with, that she dreaded lest, as soon as he had touched the ransom, he

[043-093]would dispose of her husband altogether.

[043-094]There was a short silence.

[043-095]"You will write that letter," said Negoro.

[043-096]"Never!" repeated Mrs. Weldon.

[043-097]"Remember your child!"

[043-098]Mrs. Weldon's heart beat violently, but she did not answer a word.

[043-099]"I will give you a week to think over this," hissed out Negoro.

[043-100]Mrs. Weldon was still silent.

[043-101]"A week! I will come again in a week; you will do as I wish, or it will

[043-102]be the worse for you."

[043-103]He gnashed his teeth, turned on his heel, and left the hut.

[043-104]CHAPTER XIV.

[043-105]A RAY OF HOPE.

[043-106]Mrs. Weldon's first feeling on being left alone was a sense of relief

[043-107]at having a week's respite. She had no trust in Negoro's honesty, but

[043-108]she knew well enough that their "marketable value" would secure them

[043-109]from any personal danger, and she had time to consider whether some

[043-110]compromise might be effected by which her husband might be spared the

[043-111]necessity of coming to Kazonndé. Upon the receipt of a letter from

[043-112]herself, he would not hesitate for a moment in undertaking the journey,

[043-113]but she entertained no little fear that after all perhaps her own

[043-114]departure might not be permitted; the slightest caprice on the part of

[043-115]Queen Moena would detain her as a captive, whilst as to Negoro, if once

[043-116]he should get the ransom he wanted, he would take no further pains in

[043-117]the matter.

[043-118]Accordingly, she resolved to make the proposition that she should be

[043-119]conveyed to some point upon the coast, where the bargain could be

[043-120]concluded without Mr. Weldon's coming up the country.

[043-121]She had to weigh all the consequences that would follow any refusal on

[043-122]her part to fall in with Negoro's demands. Of course, he would spend

[043-123]the interval in preparing for his start to America, and when he should

[043-124]come back and find her still hesitating, was it not likely that he

[043-125]would find scope for his revenge in suggesting that she must be

[043-126]separated from her child.

[043-127]The very thought sent a pang through her heart, and she clasped her

[043-128]little boy tenderly to her side.

[043-129]"What makes you so sad, mamma?" asked Jack.

[043-130]"I was thinking of your father, my child," she answered; "would you not

[043-131]like to see him?"

[043-132]"Yes, yes; is he coming here?"

[043-133]"No, my boy, he must not come here."

[043-134]"Then let us take Dick, and Tom, and Hercules, and go to him."

[043-135]Mrs. Weldon tried to conceal her tears.

[043-136]"Have you heard from papa?"

[043-137]"No."

[043-138]"Then why do you not write to him?"

[043-139]"Write to him?" repeated his mother, "that is the very thing I was

[043-140]thinking about."

[043-141]The child little knew the agitation that was troubling her mind.

[043-142]Meanwhile Mrs. Weldon had another inducement which she hardly ventured

[043-143]to own to herself for postponing her final decision. Was it absolutely

[043-144]impossible that her [[freedom|liberation]] should be effected by some different

[043-145]means altogether?

[043-146]A few days previously she had overheard a conversation outside her hut,

[043-147]and over this she had found herself continually pondering.

[043-148]Alvez and one of the Ujiji dealers, discussing the future prospects of

[043-149]their business, mutually agreed in denouncing the efforts that were

[043-150]being made for the suppression of the slave-traffic, not only by the

[043-151]cruisers on the coast, but by the intrusion of travellers and

[043-152]missionaries into the interior.

[043-153]Alvez averred that all these troublesome visitors ought to be

[043-154]exterminated forthwith.

[043-155]"But kill one, and another crops up," replied the dealer.

[043-156]"Yes, their exaggerated reports bring up a swarm of them," said Alvez.

[043-157]It seemed a subject of bitter complaint that the markets of Nyangwé,

[043-158]Zanzibar, and the lake-district had been invaded by Speke and Grant and

[043-159]others, and although they congratulated each other that the western

[043-160]provinces had not yet been much persecuted, they confessed that now

[043-161]that the travelling epidemic had begun to rage, there was no telling

[043-162]how soon a lot of European and American busy-bodies might be among

[043-163]them. Thedépôts at Cassange and Bihe had both been visited, and

[043-164]although Kazonndé had hitherto been left quiet, there were rumours

[043-165]enough that the continent was to be tramped over from east to west.[1]

[043-166][Footnote: This extraordinary feat was, it is universally known,

[043-167]subsequently accomplished by Cameron.]

[043-168]"And it may be," continued Alvez, "that that missionary fellow,

[043-169]Livingstone, is already on his way to us; if he comes there can be but

[043-170]one result; there must be freedom for all the slaves in Kazonndé."

[043-171]"Freedom for the slaves in Kazonndé!" These were the words which in

[043-172]connexion with Dr. Livingstone's name had arrested Mrs. Weldon's

[043-173]attention, and who can wonder that she pondered them over and over

[043-174]again, and ventured to associate them with her own prospects?

[043-175]Here was a ray of hope!

[043-176]The mere mention of Livingstone's name in association with this story

[043-177]seems to demand a brief survey of his career.

[043-178]Born on the 19th of March, 1813, David Livingstone was the second of

[043-179]six children of a tradesman in the village of Blantyre, in Lanarkshire.

[043-180]After two years' training in medicine and theology, he was sent out by

[043-181]the London Missionary Society, and landed at the Cape of Good Hope in

[043-182]1840, with the intention of joining Moffat in South Africa. After

[043-183][[exploration|exploring]] the country of the Bechuanas, he returned to Kuruman, and,

[043-184]having married Moffat's daughter, proceeded in 1843 to found a mission

[043-185]in the Mabotsa valley.

[043-186]After four years he removed to Kolobeng in the Bechuana district, 225

[043-187]miles north of Kuruman, whence, in 1849, starting off with his wife,

[043-188]three children, and two friends, Mr. Oswell and Mr. Murray, he

[043-189]discovered Lake Ngami, and returned by descending the course of the

[043-190]Zouga.

[043-191]The opposition of the natives had prevented his proceeding beyond Lake

[043-192]Ngami at his first visit, and he made a second with no better success.

[043-193]In a third attempt, however, he wended his way northwards with his

[043-194]family and Mr. Oswell along the Chobé, an affluent of the Zambesi, and

[043-195]after a difficult journey at length reached the district of the

[043-196]Makalolos, of whom the chief, named Sebituané, joined him at Linyanté.

[043-197]The Zambesi itself was discovered at the end of June, 1851, and the

[043-198]doctor returned to the Cape for the purpose of sending his family to

[043-199]England.
