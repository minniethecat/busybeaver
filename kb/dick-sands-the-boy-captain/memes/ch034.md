[034-001]As if struck by a sudden thought, Bat called out,--

[034-002]"Let me try and get outside. Perhaps I might dive and get through the

[034-003]opening."

[034-004]"I think I had better make that experiment myself," answered [[dick-sands|Dick]].

[034-005]"That you never shall," interposed Tom peremptorily; "you must let Bat

[034-006]go. It may not be possible to get back, and your presence is

[034-007]indispensable here. Think, sir, think of [[mrs-weldon|Mrs. Weldon]], and Master Jack,"

[034-008]he added in a lower tone.

[034-009]"Well, well," Dick assented, "if it must be so, Bat shall go."

[034-010]And turning to Bat, he continued,

[034-011]"Do not try to come back again; we will try, if we can, to follow you

[034-012]the same way; but if the top of the cone is still above water, knock

[034-013]hard on it with your hatchet, and we shall take it as a signal that we

[034-014]may break our way out. Do you understand?"

[034-015]"All right!" he said, "all right, sir."

[034-016]And after wringing his father's hand, he drew a long breath, and

[034-017]plunged into the water that filled the lower section of the ant-hill.

[034-018]It was an exploit that required considerable agility; the diver would

[034-019]have to find the orifice, make his way through it, and, without loss of

[034-020]a moment, let himself rise to the surface outside. Full half a minute

[034-021]elapsed, and Dick was making sure that the negro had been successful in

[034-022]his effort, when his black head emerged from the water. There was a

[034-023]general exclamation of surprise.

[034-024]"It is blocked up," gasped Bat, as soon as he had recovered breath

[034-025]enough to speak.

[034-026]"Blocked up?" cried Tom.

[034-027]"Yes," Bat affirmed; "I have felt all round the wall very carefully

[034-028]with my hand, and I am sure there is no hole left; I suppose the water

[034-029]has dissolved the clay."

[034-030]"If you cannot find a hole," exclaimed Hercules, "I can very soon make

[034-031]one;" and he was just about to plunge his hatchet into the side of the

[034-032]ant-hill, when Dick prevented him.

[034-033]"Stop, stop! you must not be in such a hurry!"

[034-034]He reflected for a few moments, and went on,--

[034-035]"We must be cautious; an impetuous step may be destruction; perhaps the

[034-036]water is over the top; if it is allowed to enter, then at once is an

[034-037]end of all."

[034-038]"But whatever we do," urged Tom, "must be done at once; there is no

[034-039]time to lose."

[034-040]He was right; the water had risen till it was quite six feet deep; none

[034-041]but Mrs. Weldon, Jack, Nan, and Cousin Benedict, who were lodged in the

[034-042]upper cells, were fairly above its surface.

[034-043]Dick now came to his determination. At about a foot above the

[034-044]water-level, that is, about seven feet from the ground, he resolved to

[034-045]bore a hole through the clay. If he should find himself in

[034-046]communication with the open air, he would have the proof he desired

[034-047]that the top of the cone was still uncovered; if, on the other hand, he

[034-048]should ascertain that he had pierced the wall below the surface of the

[034-049]external water, he would be prepared to plug up the hole

[034-050]instantaneously, and repeat the experiment higher up. It was true that

[034-051]the inundation might have risen even fifteen feet above the plain; in

[034-052]that case the worst had come, and there was no alternative but that

[034-053]they must all die of asphyxia.

[034-054]Carefully considering the chances of his undertaking, Dick calmly and

[034-055]steadily set about his task. The best instrument that suggested itself

[034-056]for his purpose was the ramrod of a gun, which, having a sort of

[034-057]corkscrew at the end for extracting the wadding, would serve as an

[034-058]auger. The hole would be very small, but yet large enough for the

[034-059]requisite test. Hercules showed him all the light he could by holding

[034-060]up the lantern. There were several candles left, so that they were not

[034-061]in fear of being altogether in darkness.

[034-062]The operation hardly took a minute; the ramrod passed through the clay

[034-063]without difficulty; a muffled sound was distinguished as of air-bubbles

[034-064]rushing through a column of water. As the air escaped, the water in the

[034-065]cone rose perceptibly. The hole had been pierced too low. A handful of

[034-066]clay was immediately forced into the orifice, which was thus

[034-067]effectually plugged; and Dick turned round quietly, and said,--

[034-068]"We must try again."

[034-069]The water had again become stationary, but its last rise had diminished

[034-070]the amount of breathing space by more than eight inches. The supply of

[034-071]oxygen was beginning to fail, respiration was becoming difficult, and

[034-072]the flame of the candle burned red and dim.

[034-073][[About]] a foot higher than the first hole, Dick now set about boring a

[034-074]second. The experiment might again prove a failure, and the water rise

[034-075]yet higher in the cone; but the risk must be run.

[034-076]Just as the auger was being inserted, a loud exclamation of delight was

[034-077]heard proceeding from Cousin Benedict's cell. Dick paused, and Hercules

[034-078]turned the lantern towards the excited naturalist, who seemed beaming

[034-079]with satisfaction.

[034-080]"Yes, yes; I see it all well enough," he cried; "I know now why the

[034-081]termites left their home; they were wide-awake; they were more clever

[034-082]than we are; they knew that the storm was coming!"

[034-083]Finding that this was all the worthy entomologist had to communicate,

[034-084]Dick, without comment, turned back again to his operation. Again the

[034-085]gurgling noise! again the water's upward rush! For the second time he

[034-086]had failed to effect an aperture to the outer air!

[034-087][Illustration: All fired simultaneously at the nearest boat.]

[034-088]The situation was to the last degree alarming. The water had all but

[034-089]reached Mrs. Weldon, and she was obliged to take her boy into her arms.

[034-090]Every one felt nearly stifled. A loud singing was heard in the ears,

[034-091]and the lantern showed barely any light at all. A few minutes more and

[034-092]the air would be incapable of supporting life. One chance alone

[034-093]remained. They must bore another hole at the very summit of the cone.

[034-094]Not that they were unaware of the imminent danger of this measure, for

[034-095]if the ant-hill were really submerged the water from below would

[034-096]immediately expel the remaining air and death must be instantaneous. A

[034-097]few brief words from Dick explained the emergency of the crisis. Mrs.

[034-098]Weldon recognized the necessity,--

[034-099]"Yes, Dick, do it; there is nothing else to be done."

[034-100]While she was speaking the light flickered out, and they were in total

[034-101]darkness.

[034-102]Mounted on the shoulders of Hercules, who was crouching in one of the

[034-103]side-cells, his head only just above water, Dick proceeded to force the

[034-104]ramrod into the clay, which at the vertex of the ant-hill was

[034-105]considerably harder and thicker than elsewhere.

[034-106]A strange mingling of hope and fear thrilled through Dick Sands as he

[034-107]applied his hand to make the opening which was to admit life and air,

[034-108]or the flood of death!

[034-109]The silence of the general expectation was broken by the noise of a

[034-110]sharp hissing; the water rose for eight inches, but all at once it

[034-111]ceased to rise; it had found its level. No need this time to close the

[034-112]orifice; the top of the ant-hill was higher than the top of the flood;

[034-113]and for the present, at least, they could all rejoice that their lives

[034-114]were spared!

[034-115]A general cheer, led by the stentorian voice of Hercules, involuntarily

[034-116]broke from the party; cutlasses were brought into action, and the clay

[034-117]crumbled away beneath the vigorous assault that was made upon it. The

[034-118]welcome air was admitted through the new-made aperture, bringing with

[034-119]it the first rays of the rising sun. The summit of the ant-hill once

[034-120]removed, it would be quite easy to clamber to the top, whence it was

[034-121]hoped they would soon get away to some high ground out of reach of the

[034-122]flood.

[034-123]Dick was the first to mount the summit; but a cry of dismay burst from

[034-124]his lips!

[034-125]A sound only too well known to travellers in [[africa|Africa]] broke upon his ear;

[034-126]that sound was the whizzing of arrows.

[034-127]Hardly a hundred yards away was a large encampment; whilst, in the

[034-128]water, quite close to the ant-hill where he stood, he saw some long

[034-129]boats full of natives. From one of these had come the volley of arrows

[034-130]which had greeted his appearance above the opening of the cone.

[034-131]To tell his people what had happened was the work of a moment. He

[034-132]seized his gun, and made Hercules, Bat, and Actæon take theirs, and all

[034-133]fired simultaneously at the nearest boat. Several of the natives were

[034-134]seen to fall; but shouts of defiance were raised, and shots were fired

[034-135]in return.

[034-136]Resistance was manifestly useless. What could they do against a hundred

[034-137]natives? they were assailed on every hand. In accordance with what

[034-138]seemed a preconcerted plan, they were carried off from the ant-hill

[034-139]with brutal violence, in two parties, without the chance of a farewell

[034-140]word or sign.

[034-141]Dick Sands saw that Mrs. Weldon, Jack, and Cousin Benedict were placed

[034-142]on board one boat, and were conveyed towards the camp, whilst he

[034-143]himself, with the five negroes and old Nan, was forced into another,

[034-144]and taken in a different direction. Twenty natives formed a body-guard

[034-145]around them, and five boats followed in their rear.

[034-146]Useless though it were, Dick and the negroes made one desperate attempt

[034-147]to maintain their freedom; they wounded several of their antagonists,

[034-148]and would doubtless have paid their lives as a penalty for their

[034-149]daring, if there had not been special orders given that they should be

[034-150]taken alive.

[034-151]The passage of the flood was soon accomplished. The boat had barely

[034-152]touched the shore, when Hercules with a tremendous bound sprang on to

[034-153]the land. Instantly two natives rushed upon him. The giant clave their

[034-154]skulls with the butt end of his gun, and made off. Followed though he

[034-155]was by a storm of bullets, he escaped in safety, and disappeared

[034-156]beneath the cover of the woods.

[034-157][Illustration: The giant clave their skulls with the butt end of his

[034-158]gun.]

[034-159]Dick Sands and the others were guarded to the shore, and fettered like

[034-160]slaves.

[034-161]CHAPTER VII.

[034-162]A SLAVE CARAVAN.

[034-163]The storm of the previous night, by swelling the tributaries of the

[034-164]Coanza, had caused the main river to overflow its banks. The inundation

[034-165]had entirely changed the aspect of the country, transforming the plain

[034-166]into a lake, where the peaks of a number of ant-hills were the sole

[034-167]objects that emerged above the watery expanse.

[034-168]The Coanza, which is one of the principal rivers of Angola, falls into

[034-169]the Atlantic about a hundred miles from the spot at which the "[[the-pilgrim|Pilgrim]]"

[034-170]was stranded. The stream, which a few years later was crossed by

[034-171]Cameron on his way to Benguela, seems destined to become the chief

[034-172]highway of traffic between Angola and the interior; steamers already

[034-173]ply upon its lower waters, and probably ten years will not elapse

[034-174]before they perform regular service along its entire course.

[034-175]Dick Sands had been quite right in searching northwards for the

[034-176]navigable stream he had been so anxious to find; the rivulet he had

[034-177]been following fell into the Coanza scarce a mile away, and had it not

[034-178]been for this unexpected attack he and his friends might reasonably

[034-179]have hoped to descend the river upon a raft, until they reached one of

[034-180]the Portuguese forts where steam vessels put in. But their fate was

[034-181]ordered otherwise.

[034-182]The camp which Dick had descried from the ant-hill was pitched upon an

[034-183]eminence crowned by an enormous sycamore-fig, one of those giant trees

[034-184]occasionally found in Central Africa, of which the spreading foliage

[034-185]will shelter some five hundred men. Some of the non-fruit-bearing kind

[034-186]of banyan-trees formed the background of the landscape.

[034-187]Beneath the shelter of the sycamore, the [[slave-caravan|caravan]] which had been

[034-188]referred to in the conversation between [[negoro|Negoro]] and Harris had just made

[034-189]a halt. Torn from their villages by the agents of the slave-dealer

[034-190]Alvez, the large troop of natives was on its way to the market of

[034-191]Kazonndé, thence to be sent as occasion required either to the west

[034-192]coast, or to Nyangwé, in the great lake district, to be dispersed into

[034-193]Upper Egypt or Zanzibar.
