# Immortality

In [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s dramatic vision, immortality — or, more precisely, radical life extension — is the practical demonstration that [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] works. If the [[life-force|Life Force]] is real, it should enable human beings to live longer than the "three score and ten" years allotted by traditional biology. The play's [[longevity|long-lived]] characters are the proof.

## Definition

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s "immortality" is not supernatural afterlife but biological and [[free-will|volition]]al: human beings, by [[free-will|will]]ing it strongly enough and long enough, can extend their lifespan indefinitely. This is [[lamarckism|Lamarckism]] applied to the most fundamental biological limit — the length of life itself. The burden of mortality, once lifted, transforms human character and society.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] frames the extension of human life as a logical consequence of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. If the [[life-force|Life Force]] works through trial and error, and if human [[free-will|will]] is an expression of the [[life-force|Life Force]], then humans who [[free-will|will]] to live longer should, over generations, achieve the capacity to do so.

The Preface does not belabor the mechanics; that is left to the play. But [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s framing makes clear that immortality is not a fantasy but a scientifically imaginable goal — the next step in human evolution.

### In the Play

The theme of immortality runs through the entire cycle. In Part I, [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]] discover that without death, life loses its urgency; [[the-serpent|the Serpent]]'s suggestion of birth introduces mortality and the need to reproduce. As [[eve|Eve]] exclaims: "How glorious! It [[free-will|will]] be a comfort to me every day now that the burden of immortality is lifted from you" (001-003) — early humans actually *want* death as release from the "burden of immortality" (001-003).

In Part II, the Barnabas brothers confront the "terror of eternity, of immortality. Think of it, man: to have no end!" (002-003). The prospect of living forever is terrifying to those conditioned to a short life.

In Part III, the political implications emerge. The Archbishop has achieved extended life, and the politician [[lubin|Lubin]] reacts with alarm: "the terrible burden of immortality" (003-011) — not just a personal burden but a political one, as immortal beings would disrupt the natural succession of power.

In Part IV, the [[longevity|long-lived]] have existed for centuries. Bolge Bluebin Barlow "puts on immortality" (005-006) as one might put on a coat — it has become accepted, almost mundane.

In Part V, the ultimate consequence: beings who have lived so long that the body is merely an "automaton," and consciousness is freed into contemplation. Immortality has transformed not just lifespan but the nature of being.

## Intellectual Context

The idea of radically extended human life has a long history:

- **Religious immortality**: Traditional Christianity promised eternal life in heaven, not on earth. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] inverts this: immortality should be achieved in this life, through biological means, not deferred to an afterlife.

- **The philosopher's stone**: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] explicitly connects his project to the alchemical quest for the elixir of life: "I exploit the eternal interest of the philosopher's stone which enables men to live forever" (P01-102).

- **Modern life extension**: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s vision anticipates contemporary transhumanist and life-extension movements, though his mechanism ([[free-will|will]]-driven [[lamarckism|Lamarckian evolution]]) differs from modern biomedical approaches.

## Dramatic Expression

The dramatic structure of *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* is organized around the progressive achievement of longer life. Each Part represents a leap forward in time, and with each leap, human lifespan extends. The play is a dramatized proof-of-concept: by showing us beings who have achieved immortality, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] argues that we too can achieve it — if we [[free-will|will]] it strongly enough.
