# Church of England

The established Protestant church of [[england|England]], headed by the monarch and governed by the Archbishop of Canterbury. In *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] treats the Church as an institution that grasped a profound theological truth — a God "without body, parts, or passions" — yet failed to live by it, lapsing into sectarian narrowness.

## Overview

Founded in 1534 when [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] broke with Rome, the Church of [[england|England]] had become by [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s time the spiritual arm of the British establishment — a national church tied to the crown, the aristocracy, and the Conservative Party. Its Thirty-Nine Articles of [[religion|Religion]] (1562) and its Book of Common Prayer defined its doctrine.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s relationship to the Church was complex. He admired its First Article — which defined God without anthropomorphic attributes — but despised its institutional cowardice during the Great War and its failure to embrace the universalism implicit in its own best theology.

## Shaw's Critique or Usage

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] defends the Church against simplistic charges of intellectual confusion. The problem was not the Church's doctrine but its failure to teach it:

> Our scornful young scientific and philosophic lions of today must not blame the Church of England for this confusion of thought. In 1562 the Church, in convocation in London "for the avoiding of diversities of opinions and for the establishment of consent touching true religion," proclaimed in their first utterance, and as an Article of Religion, that God is "without body, parts, or passions," or, as we say, an Elan Vital or Life Force. (P01-043)

The tragedy, for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], was that the people "imposed this idol in practice on the Church itself, in spite of the First Article" (P01-043). The Church possessed a theology compatible with [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] but lacked the courage to preach it.

His later judgment is harsher. In the section "The Danger of Reaction," [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] groups the Church with dwindling sects that must reform or perish:

> If dwindling sects like the Church of England, the Church of Rome, the Greek Church, and the rest, persist in trying to cramp the human mind within the limits of these grotesque perversions of natural truths and poetic metaphors, then they must be ruthlessly banished from the schools until they either perish in general contempt or discover the soul that is hidden in every dogma. (P01-088)

The remedy is universality — a test the Church fails:

> The test of a dogma is its universality. As long as the Church of England preaches a single doctrine that the Brahman, the Buddhist, the Mussulman, the Parsee, and all the other sectarians who are British subjects cannot accept, it has no legitimate place in the counsels of the British Commonwealth. (P01-089)

### In the Play

In Part II, the Church of [[england|England]] becomes a political football. Burge, the Liberal leader, scoffs at the notion that Tories are virtuous "because they are all members of the Church of [[england|England]]" (003-011). [[franklyn-barnabas|Franklyn Barnabas]] retorts that Churchmen are "a phalanx" — unified by a common creed — while Liberals are "a mob in which atheists are jostled by Plymouth Brethren" (003-011).

Later, Burge performs a cynical pivot: "Get rid of the establishment; get rid of the bishops; get rid of the candlesticks; get rid of the 39 articles; and the Church of [[england|England]] is just as good as any other Church" (003-011). It is pure political opportunism — the Church reduced to a bargaining chip.

## Historical Context

In the early twentieth century, the Church of [[england|England]] faced a crisis of identity. [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism had eroded biblical literalism; the Great War had exposed the failure of national churches to speak with moral authority; and the rise of [[atheism|secularism]], Fabian socialism, and Irish Catholic nationalism challenged its cultural monopoly.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], an Irish Protestant by background, knew the Church from both inside and outside. His critique reflects a broader Edwardian debate about disestablishment, the role of religion in education, and whether Christianity could survive the scientific revolution.
