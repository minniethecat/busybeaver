# William Shakespeare

The English playwright and poet (1564–1616), universally regarded as the greatest writer in the English language. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] invokes [[william-shakespeare]] frequently in the Preface to [[back-to-methuselah]], treating him not as a rival to be dismissed but as the supreme example of dramatic genius — and as a tragic case of talent constrained by the intellectual limitations of his era.

## Dramatis Personae

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] describes Shakespeare in the Preface as the greatest of the English playwrights who nevertheless labored under a fundamental handicap: the absence of a coherent religious or philosophical worldview. "[[eve|Eve]]r since Shakespeare, playwrights have been struggling with the same lack of religion" (P01-098). [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s treatment is admiring but diagnostic — he sees Shakespeare's achievement as proving what drama can do, while arguing that even Shakespeare could not fully transcend his age's conceptual poverty.

## Role in the Play

Shakespeare does not appear as a character in the play cycle, but his presence is felt throughout [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s dramatic argument. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] repeatedly uses Shakespeare as a benchmark for dramatic achievement, while insisting that his own generation of playwrights must aim higher — not in literary quality but in philosophical substance.

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] notes that "[[charles-darwin|Darwin]] could not read Shakespeare" (P01-018), using this as a pointed observation about the compartmentalization of genius rather than as criticism of either figure.

On the dramatic tradition: "[[eve|Eve]]r since Shakespeare, playwrights have been struggling with the same lack of religion; and many of them were forced to become mere panders and sensation-mongers" (P01-098).

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] remarks on Shakespeare's relation to tragedy: "Shakespeare did not make [[hamlet|Hamlet]] out of its final act but out of its first act" (P01-097), contrasting Shakespeare's method with [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s own evolutionary dramaturgy.

He lists Shakespeare among the pinnacles of English literature: "the whole body of English literature, from Shakespeare to the latest edition of the Encyclopaedia Britannica" (P01-026).

## Relationships

- [[george-bernard-shaw]]: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] saw himself as continuing and transcending the Shakespearean dramatic tradition by giving it philosophical substance
- [[creative-evolution]]: The philosophical framework [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] believed Shakespeare lacked
- [[back-to-methuselah]]: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s own attempt to write the kind of philosophically-grounded drama he believed Shakespeare could not

## Themes

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s references to Shakespeare consistently serve one argument: that even the greatest dramatic genius cannot fully realize his potential without a "religion" — by which [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] means a coherent philosophy of life and purpose. Shakespeare had unmatched literary gifts but lived in an age that offered only "the threadbare plots of the modern theatre" (P01-099). [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] presents [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] as the missing element that would have enabled Shakespeare to write not merely great plays but transformative ones.

## Real-World References

William Shakespeare (1564–1616) was baptized in Stratford-upon-Avon on 26 April 1564 and died there on 23 April 1616. His works include 39 plays, 154 sonnets, and several narrative poems. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] was a lifelong critic and admirer of Shakespeare, publishing *Shakes versus Shav* (1949) and frequently commenting on Shakespearean production. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s ambivalent relationship with Shakespeare was a defining feature of his critical writing.
