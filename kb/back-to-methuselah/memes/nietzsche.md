# Friedrich Nietzsche

The German philosopher [[nietzsche|Friedrich Nietzsche]] (1844–1900) appears at four points in [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s preface to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*, serving a dual role: he is both a genuine intellectual predecessor to [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] and a cautionary example of how historical reputation distorts the true genealogy of ideas.

## Role in Shaw's Argument

### The Misattribution Problem

Shaw's first reference to Nietzsche is in the same ironic passage where he mentions [[schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]:

> Thus Nietzsche, by the two or three who had come across his writings, was supposed to have been the first man to whom it occurred that mere morality and legality and urbanity lead nowhere, as if Bunyan had never written Badman. (P01-004)

The point is characteristically Shavian: Nietzsche's fashionable admirers credited him with originating an insight that was, in reality, centuries old. Shaw's larger argument — that the nineteenth century's sense of its own novelty was largely an illusion — applies to Nietzsche as much as to [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] or [[richard-wagner|Wagner]].

### The Will to Power

Shaw's most substantive engagement with Nietzsche comes in his discussion of scientific method and the [[life-force|Life Force]]:

> Nietzsche, for example, thinking out the great central truth of the Will to Power instead of cutting off mouse-tails, had no difficulty in concluding that the final objective of this Will was power over self, and that the seekers after power over others and material possessions were on a false scent. (P01-061)

This is Shaw at his most sympathetic to Nietzsche. The contrast is between Nietzsche's philosophical method — thinking out "the great central truth" — and the laboratory experimentalism Shaw associates with the [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinists]]. Nietzsche, in Shaw's reading, arrived at genuine insight because he asked the right questions, not because he accumulated data.

Crucially, Shaw interprets Nietzsche's "Will to Power" as power *over self* — a reading that aligns Nietzsche with Shaw's own doctrine of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]], in which the [[life-force|Life Force]] drives individuals toward self-mastery and higher consciousness.

### Good Europeans

In a passage on the failure of international politics, Shaw invokes Nietzsche's ideal:

> If the Western Powers had selected their allies in the Lamarckian manner intelligently, purposely, and vitally, ad majorem Dei gloriam, as what Nietzsche called good Europeans, there would have been a League of Nations and no war. (P01-080)

Nietzsche's "good Europeans" — cosmopolitan thinkers who transcended narrow nationalism — serve Shaw as a model for what intelligent, purposeful political action might look like. The passage is part of Shaw's broader argument that [[lamarckism|Lamarckian]] principles (intelligence, purpose, will) should guide human affairs, not the blind mechanics of [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]].

### The Superman

Shaw's final reference to Nietzsche places him in a lineage of creative genius that predates him by centuries:

> Michelangelo could not very well believe in Julius II or Leo X, or in much that they believed in; but he could paint the Superman three hundred years before Nietzsche wrote Also Sprach Zarathustra and Strauss set it to music. (P01-095)

The "Superman" (Übermensch) — Nietzsche's ideal of humanity transcending itself — is, for Shaw, not Nietzsche's invention but a perennial human aspiration. Michelangelo embodied it in art; Nietzsche articulated it in philosophy; Strauss expressed it in music. All three are expressions of the same [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] at work.

## Significance for Creative Evolution

Nietzsche's importance to Shaw lies primarily in two concepts:

1. **The Will to Power as Will to Self-Mastery**: Shaw reinterprets Nietzsche's central doctrine as an evolutionary principle — the drive of life toward higher forms, greater consciousness, and self-control. This aligns Nietzsche with [[schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] and [[samuel-butler|Samuel Butler]] as a precursor to Shaw's own [[life-force|Life Force]].

2. **The Superman as Evolutionary Ideal**: Nietzsche's Übermensch becomes, in Shaw's framework, the goal of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] — the being that humanity is striving to become, through willed effort over sufficient time.
