# Evil

The existence of suffering and evil in a world supposedly driven by a creative [[life-force|Life Force]] presents an obvious theological challenge. [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] addresses this directly in the Preface to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*, offering what he considers a decisive resolution: evil is the necessary by-product of a creative process that works through trial and error rather than omnipotent design.

## Definition

For [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], evil is not the result of divine malevolence or human sinfulness but the inevitable cost of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. The [[life-force|Life Force]] is not omnipotent in the traditional theological sense — it "must meanwhile struggle with matter and circumstance by the method of trial and error, then the world must be full of its unsuccessful experiments" (P01-067). Evil, in this view, is simply what unsuccessful experiments look like from the perspective of the suffering they cause.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] devotes a key passage to what he calls "the problem of evil," arguing that it "yields very easily to [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]" (P01-067). He rejects the Victorian theological framework in which suffering is either punishment for sin or part of a divine plan too mysterious for human comprehension.

Instead, he offers a naturalistic theodicy: "If all our calamities are either accidents or sincerely repented mistakes, there is no malice in the Cruelty of Nature and no Problem of Evil in the Victorian sense at all" (P01-067). The crucial shift is from intention to process — evil is not [[free-will|will]]ed by a malevolent creator; it is the debris left by a creative process that learns as it goes.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] illustrates with striking examples: "Christ may meet a tiger, or a High Priest arm-in-arm with a Roman Governor, and be the unfittest to survive under the circumstances. [[mozart|Mozart]] may have a genius that prevails against Emperors and Archbishops, and a lung that succumbs to some obscure and noxious property of foul air" (P01-067). The same creative process that produces [[mozart|Mozart]]s also produces, as a side effect, the conditions that kill them.

### In the Play

The play's [[longevity|long-lived]] beings embody the progressive overcoming of evil. Suffering, in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s dramatic universe, is what happens when the [[life-force|Life Force]] hasn't yet solved a problem. The Ancients of Part V, who have transcended bodily suffering, represent the endpoint of this process — evil overcome by evolutionary achievement rather than divine intervention.

## Key Passages

> The problem of evil yields very easily to Creative Evolution. If the driving power behind Evolution is omnipotent only in the sense that there seems no limit to its final achievement; and if it must meanwhile struggle with matter and circumstance by the method of trial and error, then the world must be full of its unsuccessful experiments. (P01-067)

> Christ may meet a tiger, or a High Priest arm-in-arm with a Roman Governor, and be the unfittest to survive under the circumstances. Mozart may have a genius that prevails against Emperors and Archbishops, and a lung that succumbs to some obscure and noxious property of foul air. (P01-067)

## Intellectual Context

The Problem of Evil has been central to Western theology since at least the Book of Job:

- **Augustinian theodicy**: Evil as privation — the absence of good rather than a positive force. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s naturalistic theodicy echoes this structure (evil as absence of creative achievement) while rejecting its supernatural framework.

- **Irenaean theodicy**: Evil as a necessary condition for soul-making — suffering develops moral character. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s version is biological rather than moral: suffering is the cost of creative experimentation.

- **Leibniz's "best of all possible worlds"**: Voltaire famously satirized this in *Candide*. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s theodicy avoids Leibniz's implausibility by denying omnipotence — the [[life-force|Life Force]] is doing the best it *can*, not the best that is logically possible.

## Dramatic Expression

The play does not dramatize evil as a metaphysical force — there is no Satan, no villain. Instead, evil appears as the incidental suffering caused by an incomplete evolutionary process: mortality, ignorance, limitation. The dramatic arc is the progressive overcoming of these evils through the [[longevity|extension of life]] and consciousness.
