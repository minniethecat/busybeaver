# Garden of Eden

The Garden of Eden serves as the setting for Part I of [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* and as a recurring symbolic reference throughout the Preface and the play. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] repurposes the Genesis story not as religious history but as a founding myth for his religion of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]], reinterpreting [[the-serpent|the Serpent]], [[adam|Adam]], and [[eve|Eve]] as figures in the first awakening of the [[life-force|Life Force]].

## Definition

For [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], the Garden of Eden is not a place but a metaphor for the moment when purely biological existence gave way to [[free-will|will]], choice, and creativity — the moment the [[life-force|Life Force]] first manifested in human consciousness. By "going back to the legend of the Garden of Eden" (P01-102), [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] claims the imaginative power of the Biblical narrative while stripping it of supernatural theology and replacing it with evolutionary philosophy.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] discusses Eden in the context of his deliberate choice of founding myths. He "abandon[s] the legend of Don Juan with its erotic associations, and go[es] back to the legend of the Garden of Eden" (P01-102). The Eden story has "held the imagination of men spellbound for centuries" (003-014) — its power as narrative is undeniable, regardless of its literal truth.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] is explicit about his method: he is making "a second legend of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] without distractions and embellishments" (P01-102). The Garden of Eden provides the raw material — the primordial garden, the first humans, the tempting [[the-serpent|Serpent]] — which [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] reinterprets in evolutionary terms.

He notes that "[[charles-darwin|Darwin]] discredited the Garden of Eden" (P01-071) as literal history, and that "Karl Marx had proclaimed in his Communist Manifesto" economic and political forces that made the Genesis story seem irrelevant. But [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] sees the Eden myth as surviving both [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] and Marx because it carries a truth deeper than literal accuracy.

### In the Play

The play opens with the stage direction: "The Garden of Eden. Afternoon. An immense serpent is sleeping" (001-001). This is not the Eden of Sunday School — [[the-serpent|the Serpent]] is not Satan but the first voice of creative consciousness, urging [[eve|Eve]] toward the act of conception that [[free-will|will]] set evolution in motion.

[[the-serpent|The Serpent]]'s wisdom is not evil but illuminating: she teaches [[eve|Eve]] that death and birth are linked — that mortality is the price of creativity. When [[eve|Eve]] accepts this, the [[life-force|Life Force]] enters human history, and evolution begins in earnest.

## Key Passages

> I abandon the legend of Don Juan with its erotic associations, and go back to the legend of the Garden of Eden. I exploit the eternal interest of the philosopher's stone which enables men to live forever. (P01-102)

> The fact that the tale of the Garden of Eden has survived and held the imagination of men spellbound for centuries, whilst hundreds of much more plausible and more recent stories have gone the way of all such tales, shows that there is something in it. (003-014)

> Somebody must take the Garden of Eden in hand and weed it properly. (P01-100)

## Intellectual Context

The Garden of Eden has a rich interpretive history:

- **Biblical Genesis**: The original narrative in Genesis 2–3, where God places [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]] in a garden, forbids the fruit of the Tree of Knowledge, and expels them after [[the-serpent|the Serpent]]'s temptation. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s reinterpretation is radical: [[the-serpent|the Serpent]] is the hero, knowledge is liberation, and the "Fall" is the beginning of human progress.

- **Milton's Paradise Lost**: John Milton's epic treatment of the Genesis story shaped the English literary imagination of Eden. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s comic, anti-authoritarian treatment is in deliberate contrast to Milton's solemnity.

- **[[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s challenge**: The theory of evolution made literal readings of Genesis untenable. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s response is not to discard the myth but to reinterpret it — the Garden becomes the stage for the first act of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].

## Dramatic Expression

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Garden of Eden is a comedy of ideas. [[the-serpent|The Serpent]] speaks in seductive, philosophical cadences; [[adam|Adam]] is a plodding materialist; [[eve|Eve]] is the first philosopher — curious, bold, [[free-will|will]]ing to risk death for the sake of creation. The tone is playful but the stakes are cosmic: what happens in this garden [[free-will|will]] determine the course of all subsequent evolution.
