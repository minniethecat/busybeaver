# Cain

The first child in [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*, and the first murderer. Cain's appearance in the play cycle — first as the violent son of [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]] in Part I, later as a figure who has been transformed by centuries of life — dramatizes [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s argument that even the most damaged human beings can be redeemed by the [[life-force|Life Force]].

## Dramatis Personae

Cain is introduced in Part I as the embodiment of mortality's darker possibilities. Once birth and death enter the world, violence follows — and Cain, who kills his brother [[abel|Abel]], is the proof. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Cain is not merely a villain but a demonstration of what happens when creative evolution goes wrong: the creative impulse, untutored by wisdom, becomes destructive.

## Role in the Play

### Part I: In the Beginning

Cain's murder of [[abel|Abel]] is the first demonstration that mortality has consequences beyond the biological. If people must die, some [[free-will|will]] decide who dies and when. Cain represents the primitive response to mortality — the attempt to master death by dealing it.

### Part V: As Far as Thought Can Reach

Cain reappears in the final Part as one of the figures conjured by the [[longevity|long-lived]] beings. Millennia of existence have changed him — he is no longer the violent youth of [[garden-of-eden|Eden]] but a being who has had time to outgrow his murderous beginnings. His transformation is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s argument that even the worst human impulses can be evolved beyond, given sufficient time.

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

Cain's confrontation with his parents after [[abel|Abel]]'s murder is the dramatic center of the latter part of Part I.

## Relationships

- [[adam]] — His father, whose cautious nature Cain's violence repudiates
- [[eve]] — His mother, whose creative courage takes a dark form in her son
- [[abel]] — His brother and victim

## Themes

Cain embodies the shadow side of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] — the possibility that the creative impulse, expressed through mortality and competition, becomes destructive. His redemption in Part V is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s argument that even this shadow can be overcome.
