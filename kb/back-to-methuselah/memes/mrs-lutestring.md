# Mrs. Lutestring

Mrs. Lutestring is the Domestic Minister in [[part-iii|Part III: The Thing Happens]], set in 2170 AD. She is one of the long-livers who discovered the truth of the [[conrad-barnabas|Barnabas]] longevity theory through direct experience — starting as a parlour maid in the Barnabas household, she is now 274 years old and one of the most formidable minds in government. Her journey from domestic servant to stateswoman is Shaw's most detailed portrait of how ordinary people can be transformed by extended lifespan.

## Dramatis Personae

Shaw lists her as: "Mrs. Lutestring, the Domestic Minister" (P02-016).

## Role in the Play

### Part III: The Thing Happens (2170 AD)

Mrs. Lutestring enters the President's parlor as a government colleague, but her first encounter with the Archbishop triggers mutual recognition. "Surely we have met, Mr. Archbishop. I remember your face," she says, before catching herself — "Ah, no: I remember now: it was someone else" (004-006-r283). The Archbishop experiences the same: "Something like a door opening continually and revealing you" (004-006-r284).

The truth emerges: Mrs. Lutestring was the Barnabas household's parlour maid — "A woman in a black dress and white apron, who opened the house door when people knocked or rang" (004-006-r330). She is now 274 years old, and the Archbishop is 283.

She recounts the horror of her early life with devastating clarity: poverty, drudgery, the dread of the workhouse, and the weekly escape of alcohol — "my only pleasure was looking forward to that poor little debauch. That is what saved me from suicide" (004-006-r362). But rest and the slow recuperation of her true biological age eventually revealed the truth: she was not growing old. She was growing younger.

Her testimony is the play's most powerful case for longevity. She married again at 101 — to a painter over 60 who died lamenting that "now that my foot is at last on the threshold of the temple I find that it is also the threshold of my tomb" (004-006-r371). She has watched everyone she loved die.

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

> "You, Mr. President, were born intoxicated with your own well-fed natural exuberance. You cannot imagine what alcohol was to an underfed poor woman." (004-006-r362)

> "Long life is complicated, and even terrible; but it is glorious all the same. I would no more change places with an ordinary woman than with a mayfly that lives only an hour." (004-006-r347)

> "Do you expect me to regard you as a completed soul? Oh, you may well be afraid of me. There are moments when your levity, your ingratitude, your shallow jollity, make my gorge rise so against you that if I could not remind myself that you are a child I should be tempted to doubt your right to live at all." (004-006-r378)

> "We all ended then like that. It is the highest creatures who take the longest to mature, and are the most helpless during their immaturity. I know now that it took me a whole century to grow up. I began my serious life when I was a hundred and twenty." (004-006-r396)

## Themes

Mrs. Lutestring is Shaw's proof that [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] is not only for geniuses like the Barnabas brothers. An uneducated, overworked domestic servant, given enough time to recover from exhaustion and poverty, becomes a stateswoman more capable than any short-lived politician. Her famous line — "It is the highest creatures who take the longest to mature, and are the most helpless during their immaturity" — expresses the play's central biological argument in personal terms.

Her contempt for the President's "shallow jollity" and her loneliness ("I have been very lonely sometimes" — 004-006-r366) complicate the utopian vision. Longevity is a gift, but it is also an ordeal. She has outlived her children, her husband, and her era, and she lives among people she regards as children.

## Real-World References

Mrs. Lutestring's account of poverty, Old Age Pensions, alcohol as escape, and the dread of the workhouse draws directly on Shaw's [[fabian-society|Fabian]] critique of late-Victorian and Edwardian social conditions. Her transformation from servant to minister is a parable of what Shaw believed universal provision and extended lifespan could achieve for the entire population.
