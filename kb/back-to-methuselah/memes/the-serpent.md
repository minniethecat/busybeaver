# The Serpent

The most radical revision in [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s reimagining of the [[garden-of-eden|Genesis]] story. Where the Biblical serpent is Satan in disguise — the tempter who brings sin and death into the world — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Serpent is the first voice of the [[life-force|Life Force]], the being whose "temptation" is not toward evil but toward creative evolution.

## Dramatis Personae

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s stage direction introduces the Serpent simply: "The [[garden-of-eden|Garden of Eden]]. Afternoon. An immense serpent is sleeping" (001-001). There is no suggestion of evil, no hint of demonic possession. The Serpent is a creature of the garden like [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]], distinguished not by moral corruption but by superior wisdom.

The Serpent knows what [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]] do not: that existence without creation is stasis, and stasis is death of a worse kind than mortality. By teaching [[eve|Eve]] that she can conceive — that she can make new life rather than merely perpetuate existing life — the Serpent sets evolution in motion.

## Role in the Play

### Part I: In the Beginning

The Serpent's dramatic function is to be the catalyst. Before the Serpent speaks, [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]] merely exist — they eat, they sleep, they tend the garden, they [[free-will|will]] live forever but have nothing to live for. After the Serpent speaks, everything changes.

The Serpent does not command or threaten. It simply reveals a possibility [[eve|Eve]] had not considered: that she can create new beings. The Serpent's "temptation" is not toward disobedience but toward creativity. What it offers is not forbidden fruit but the knowledge that life can be more than mere existence.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Serpent predicts its own multiplication: "there [[free-will|will]] be as many snakes in [[garden-of-eden|Eden]] as there are scales on my body" (001-003) — not as a threat but as a promise. The Serpent's wisdom [[free-will|will]] spread, and with it, the creative impulse [[free-will|will]] multiply.

### Dramatic Function

The Serpent is the [[life-force|Life Force]] in its purest dramatic form: a voice that speaks truth and lets the hearer decide. It does not force [[eve|Eve]] to conceive — it merely tells her that conception is possible. The choice is hers, and her choosing is the first act of free [[free-will|will]] in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s evolutionary drama.

## Themes

The Serpent embodies the creative wisdom that precedes and enables human agency. It knows but does not compel — it speaks truth and lets the hearer choose. This is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s model of how the [[life-force|Life Force]] operates: not through coercion but through revelation, leaving the creative act to the free [[free-will|will]] of the one who hears.

## Real-World References

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Serpent is a deliberate inversion of the Biblical serpent of [[garden-of-eden|Genesis]] 3. Where the Biblical serpent is cursed by God — "upon thy belly shalt thou go, and dust shalt thou eat all the days of thy life" — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Serpent is blessed by the evolutionary process it initiates. This inversion is the founding gesture of [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s reinterpretation of Christianity through [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].
