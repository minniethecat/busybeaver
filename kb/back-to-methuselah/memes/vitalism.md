# Vitalism

The philosophical doctrine that living organisms are animated by a principle fundamentally different from the forces governing inanimate matter — a "vital spark" or "élan vital" that cannot be reduced to physics and chemistry. [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] explicitly positioned [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] within the vitalist tradition, presenting it as the intellectual successor to "metaphysical Vitalism" and the alternative to mechanistic [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]].

## Definition

Vitalism holds that life possesses an organizing, purposive principle irreducible to mechanical causation. In [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s formulation, this principle is the [[life-force|Life Force]] — an immanent creative drive that shapes evolution toward ever higher forms of consciousness. Vitalism provides the philosophical framework that makes [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] intelligible: if life is not merely mechanism, then evolution can have direction, purpose, and creative power.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] situates his own position within a historical shift from mechanism to vitalism. He describes "the religion of metaphysical Vitalism" as having "been gaining the definite adherence of the masses" (P01-084) — a claim that may be more aspirational than descriptive, but that reveals his sense of which way the intellectual tide was turning.

The key passage traces the modern trajectory: "Nowadays, when we are turning in weary disgust and disillusion from [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]] and Mechanism to Vitalism and [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]], it is difficult to imagine how this new departure of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s could possibly have appealed to his contemporaries" (P01-035). Vitalism and [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] are paired — vitalism is the philosophical tradition, [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s specific contribution to it.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] acknowledges a simpler form of vitalism that predates his own: "this simple sort of Vitalism is always with us, and, like the belief in the philosopher's stone, refuses to be argued out of existence" (P01-094). But he distinguishes his own "religion of metaphysical Vitalism" as more sophisticated — a vitalism that has incorporated the insights (though not the conclusions) of post-[[darwinian|Darwinian]] biology.

### In the Play

The play dramatizes vitalism by showing life doing what mechanism cannot explain: extending itself through [[free-will|will]], transcending bodily limitation, achieving consciousness of increasingly subtle kinds. The Ancients of Part V are living refutations of the mechanistic hypothesis — they demonstrate that life is not merely matter in motion but something qualitatively different.

## Intellectual Context

Vitalism has a long and contested history:

- **Aristotle's entelechy**: Aristotle held that living things possess a purposive principle (entelechy) that guides their development toward their mature form. This is perhaps the earliest systematic vitalism.

- **Henri Bergson**: The French philosopher's *[[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]* (1907) was the most influential vitalist work of [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s time. Bergson's concept of the élan vital — a creative impulse driving evolution — is a direct source for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s [[life-force|Life Force]].

- **Hans Driesch**: The German embryologist's experiments with sea urchin embryos led him to posit an "entelechy" — a non-material organizing principle — reviving vitalism in early twentieth-century biology.

- **Mechanism vs. vitalism debate**: Throughout the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, biologists debated whether life could be fully explained by physics and chemistry. The rise of biochemistry and molecular biology after the 1930s largely settled this debate in favor of mechanism, though [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s vitalism was philosophical rather than strictly scientific.

## Dramatic Expression

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s vitalism is most powerfully dramatized in the contrast between the [[longevity|long-lived]] beings and ordinary mortals. The [[longevity|long-lived]] are not just older — they are qualitatively different, as if the vital principle in them has had time to develop capacities that ordinary humans never achieve. This is vitalism as theater: the [[life-force|Life Force]] made visible.
