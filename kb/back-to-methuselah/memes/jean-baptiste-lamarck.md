# Jean-Baptiste Lamarck

The French naturalist (1744–1829) whose theory of evolution by [[free-will|will]] and effort — that organisms change because they *want* to — provided [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] with an alternative to [[darwinian]] natural selection. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] devotes sustained attention to Lamarck in the Preface to [[back-to-methuselah]], presenting [[lamarckism|Lamarckian evolution]] as the scientific precursor to his own doctrine of [[creative-evolution]].

## Dramatis Personae

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] introduces Lamarck in the Preface as a figure of central importance for understanding the philosophical stakes of evolution. He singles Lamarck out explicitly: "I call your special attention to Lamarck, because later on there were Neo-Lamarckians as well as Neo-[[darwinian|Darwinians]]. I was a Neo-Lamarckian" (P01-021). Lamarck appears throughout the Preface not merely as a historical footnote but as the intellectual counterweight to [[charles-darwin]].

## Role in the Play

Lamarck does not appear as a character in the play cycle, but his ideas pervade the entire dramatic argument. The [[longevity|long-lived]] characters of the later Parts — who extend their lifespans through [[free-will|will]] and effort — embody Lamarckian principles in action. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] uses the giraffe example to illustrate Lamarck's theory: "How did he come by his long neck? Lamarck would have said, by wanting to get at the tender leaves high up on the tree, and trying until he succeeded in wishing the necessary length of neck into existence" (P01-046). This image of [[free-will|will]]-driven evolution animates the entire arc from [[adam]] and [[eve]] in Part I to the near-immortals of Part V.

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] summarizes Lamarck's fundamental proposition: "living organisms changed because they wanted to. As he stated it, the great factor in Evolution was use and disuse" (P01-021).

He distinguishes Lamarckian thought sharply from [[darwinian|Darwinian]] mechanism: "Though Lamarck's way, the way of life, [[free-will|will]], aspiration, and achievement, remained still possible, this newly shown way of hunger, death, stupidity, delusion, chance, and bare survival was also possible" (P01-047).

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] identifies the metaphysical dimension: Schopenhauer's treatise on *The World as Will* "is the metaphysical complement to Lamarck's natural history, as it demonstrates that the driving force behind Evolution is a [[free-will|will]]-to-live" (P01-032).

He also notes Lamarck's connection to the [[french-academy|French Academy]] and the idea that selection could "turn a pond full of amoebas into the [[french-academy|French Academy]]" (P01-047).

## Relationships

- [[charles-darwin]]: Lamarck's theory preceded and was largely eclipsed by [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s, but [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] regarded Lamarck's approach as philosophically richer
- [[samuel-butler]]: The Victorian writer who "ran up the Lamarckian flag to the topgallant peak" in opposition to [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] (P01-051)
- [[creative-evolution]]: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s own doctrine, built on Lamarckian foundations

## Themes

Lamarck embodies the central [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]ian theme of [[free-will|will]]-driven evolution. Against the "banishment of mind from the universe" that [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] attributed to [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism, [[lamarckism|Lamarckian evolution]] restores purpose, desire, and effort to the natural world (P01-051). This connects directly to [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s dramatic exploration of [[immortality|longevity]] as an act of [[free-will|will]] in [[back-to-methuselah]].

## Real-World References

Lamarck (1744–1829) was a French naturalist and early proponent of biological evolution. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s description of him as "a French soldier named Lamarck, who had beaten his musket into a microscope" (P01-020) refers to his transition from military service to scientific work. His major works include *[[philosophie-zoologique|Philosophie Zoologique]]* (1809). The "Neo-Lamarckians" [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] mentions were late 19th-century biologists who revived Lamarckian ideas in opposition to strict [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism.
