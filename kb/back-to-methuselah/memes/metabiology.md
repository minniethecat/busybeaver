# Metabiology

**Metabiology** is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s neologism for a science that transcends ordinary biology — not merely describing how organisms adapt and evolve, but accounting for the **purposive, creative impulse** that drives evolution toward higher complexity, longer life, and greater consciousness. It appears in the Preface to *[[Back to Methuselah]]* as [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s methodological self-justification for the play's central argument.

## Definition

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] defines metabiology as the scientific complement to [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]: where biology describes the mechanism of natural selection, metabiology studies the **[[free-will|will]] and direction** of the evolutionary process itself. It is "deductive biology" — reasoning forward from the observed fact of a creative impulse, rather than reasoning backward from fossils and adaptations (P01-100).

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] introduces metabiology in the closing pages of *The Infidel Half Century*. After critiquing [[darwinian|Darwinian]] orthodoxy for its mechanistic bias, he argues that a genuinely scientific account of evolution must include the "[[free-will|will]] to live" and the "creative impulse" as observable forces — not as mystical add-ons but as data requiring a theoretical framework. Metabiology is that framework: "fundamentally, a science of metabiology" (P01-100).

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] contrasts metabiology with the "mere collectors of anatomical or chemical facts" who dominated 19th-century science. These collectors, he argues, mistook description for explanation — cataloguing the products of evolution without accounting for the drive that produces them. Metabiology corrects this by placing purpose, [[free-will|will]], and creativity at the center of the evolutionary picture.

### In the Play

Though the term "metabiology" does not appear in the dramatic text, the entire five-part structure of *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* is an **exercise in metabiological thinking**. The play does not argue for [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] through scientific papers; it demonstrates it through dramatic action — showing humanity evolving from [[adam|Adam]]'s discovery of death through to the Ancients' transcendence of the body.

The Brothers Barnabas in Part II are practicing metabiology when they reason that a longer lifespan is both biologically necessary and morally imperative. Conrad's biology and [[franklyn-barnabas|Franklyn]]'s religion converge on the same conclusion: that human life *must* be extended, and that this extension is the next step in evolution (003-006).

## Key Passages

> "This is not mere Ruysdael-romance speculation: it is deductive biology, if there is such a science as biology. Fundamentally, a science of metabiology." — Shaw, Preface (P01-100)

## Intellectual Context

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] coined "metabiology" in explicit conversation with the biological debates of his time. The [[darwinian|Darwinian]] revolution had established natural selection as the dominant mechanism of evolution, but it left unanswered the question of *why* life tends toward complexity, consciousness, and what [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] saw as moral purpose. Metabiology is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s attempt to name a science that would answer that question.

The term echoes "metaphysics" — the study of what lies beyond physics — and positions metabiology as the study of what lies beyond descriptive biology. It also resonates with Bergson's concept of the *[[vitalism|élan vital]]*, though [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s version is more explicitly purposive and directional.

### Related Thinkers

- [[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]] — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s interlocutor; metabiology is defined against [[darwinian|Darwinian]] mechanism
- [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]] — [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]'s [[lamarckism|inheritance of acquired characteristics]] provides the biological mechanism for metabiology's purposive evolution
- Henri Bergson — Bergson's *[[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]* (1907) influenced [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s thinking about the life force
- [[samuel-butler|Samuel Butler]] — [[samuel-butler|Butler]]'s critique of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] anticipated [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s arguments

### Contrasting Concepts

- [[natural-selection|Natural Selection]] — Metabiology studies purpose; natural selection studies mechanism
- [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]] — The mechanistic orthodoxy that metabiology seeks to transcend
- [[vitalism]] — Related but distinct; metabiology is a proposed *science*, not a vitalist philosophy

## Dramatic Expression

Though metabiology is a methodological concept, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] dramatizes its implications throughout the play. The Ancient in Part V, who has moved beyond the body into pure thought, is the ultimate metabiological subject — a being whose evolution can no longer be described in purely biological terms. The entire arc from [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]]'s discovery of death to the year 31,920 is a metabiological demonstration: evolution does not merely happen to organisms; organisms *[[free-will|will]]* their own transformation.
