# Pygmalion

The mythological sculptor who appears among the figures conjured by the Oracle in Part IV. Pygmalion, who carved a statue so beautiful it came to life, represents the artistic dimension of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] — the maker whose creative act mirrors the [[life-force|Life Force]]'s cosmic creativity.

## Dramatis Personae

For [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], Pygmalion is a particularly significant mythological figure. The artist whose creation becomes life is a metaphor for the [[life-force|Life Force]] itself, which shapes living beings from matter as Pygmalion shaped Galatea from stone. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s own play *Pygmalion* (1913) had explored related themes of creation and transformation.

## Role in the Play

### Part IV: Tragedy of an Elderly Gentleman

Pygmalion appears briefly but carries symbolic weight — his presence argues that artists are evolutionary pioneers, their creative acts small-scale versions of the [[life-force|Life Force]]'s work.
