# William Haslam

William "Bill" Haslam is the young rector in [[part-ii|Part II: The Gospel of the Brothers Barnabas]], a charming but intellectually modest clergyman who becomes [[savvy-barnabas|Savvy Barnabas]]'s love interest. In [[part-iii|Part III]] it is revealed that he eventually became Archbishop Haslam — one of the long-livers who survived to 2170 AD by applying the Barnabas brothers' biological theory.

## Dramatis Personae

Shaw lists him simply as: "William 'Bill' Haslam" (P02-007).

## Role in the Play

### Part II: The Gospel of the Brothers Barnabas

Haslam is the new rector who calls on [[franklyn-barnabas|Franklyn Barnabas]], apologizing for being "an awful nuisance" — he has heard about the Barnabas family and finds Church people "mostly as dull as ditch-water" (003-006-r9). He freely admits he was "shoved into the Church" by his parents because "if there's a living in the family, or one's Governor knows a patron, one gets shoved into the Church" (003-006-r16).

His self-awareness is disarming. When Franklyn explains that he left the Church after realizing he was "walking with my own ignorance and self-conceit," Haslam counters: "If I thought I was going to live nine hundred and sixty years, I don't think I should stay in the Church" (003-006-r23). He admits the Church "chokes me off" and that when the bishop "lets off something more than usually out-of-date, the bird starts in my garden" — a bird that "keeps on singing 'Stick it or chuck it'" (003-006-r26, r28).

His romance with [[savvy-barnabas|Savvy Barnabas]] (Cynthia) blossoms through shared irreverence. Savvy calls him "Bill, short for William" (003-006-r56), and the two share cigarettes and laugh together at the political discussion. By the end of Part II, Haslam has been thoroughly exposed to the Barnabas theory of longevity — and, crucially, has the humility and honesty to absorb it, even while admitting he is not convinced (003-014-r99).

### Part III: The Thing Happens (2170 AD)

Haslam's true significance is revealed only in Part III, where [[barnabas|Barnabas]] — now over 300 years old — identifies the Archbishop as "Archbishop Haslam, the present man's last predecessor but four" (004-006-r136). Barnabas reveals that the Archbishop embodies five distinct identities: "Archbishop Haslam, Archbishop Stickit, President Dickenson, General Bullyboy and himself into the bargain; all five of them" (004-006-r210) — a practice of periodically faking death and assuming new identities to hide one's longevity.

The Archbishop's reunion with [[mrs-lutestring|Mrs. Lutestring]] — who was once the Barnabas household's parlour maid — forms one of the play's most poignant sequences. They recognize each other across two centuries and 274 years of shared experience.

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

> "If I thought I was going to live nine hundred and sixty years, I don't think I should stay in the Church." (003-006-r23)

> "It's no use. Were you convinced, Mr. Haslam?" / "About our being able to live three hundred years? Frankly no." (003-014-r98—r99)

> Haslam's recurring exclamation — "Priceless!" — punctuates the political discussions, marking him as an amused observer rather than an ideological combatant.

## Relationships

- [[franklyn-barnabas|Franklyn Barnabas]] — His parishioner and intellectual superior, whom he genuinely respects
- [[savvy-barnabas|Savvy Barnabas]] — His romantic interest, a kindred irreverent spirit
- [[conrad-barnabas|Conrad Barnabas]] — The theorist whose longevity book eventually transforms Haslam's life
- [[mrs-lutestring|Mrs. Lutestring]] — Formerly the Barnabas parlour maid; centuries later, his fellow long-liver in 2170 AD
- [[lubin|Lubin]] — The politician who initially ignores him ("Never heard of him. Is he any good?") (003-011-r128)

## Themes

Haslam embodies the [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] thesis in miniature: a man of modest intellect but genuine honesty, who lives long enough to grow beyond the limitations of his original character. The young rector who says "it won't happen to me: that's jolly sure" (003-014-r62) becomes the Archbishop who has outlived his initial self by centuries — proof that longevity enables growth even for those who lack genius.

His catchphrase "Priceless!" captures the comic detachment that [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] treats as both a survival mechanism and a spiritual resource. Haslam laughs at the absurdity of politics and the Church, and that laughter keeps him afloat until he can grow into something better.
