# The Bible

The sacred text of Judaism and Christianity, which [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] treats throughout *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* not as revealed truth but as a repository of powerful narratives that can be reinterpreted through [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s method is not to reject the Bible but to rewrite it — replacing supernatural theology with evolutionary philosophy while preserving the imaginative force of the stories.

## Shaw's Use

The play's opening — the [[garden-of-eden|Garden of Eden]] — is a direct revision of [[garden-of-eden|Genesis]] 2–3. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] "go[es] back to the legend of the [[garden-of-eden|Garden of Eden]]" (P01-102) not to debunk it but to claim its imaginative power for [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. The Bible's narratives have "held the imagination of men spellbound for centuries" (003-014) — a fact [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] exploits rather than denies.

## Relationship to Back to Methuselah

The five-play structure echoes the Biblical arc from [[garden-of-eden|Genesis]] to Revelation, recast in evolutionary terms. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s religion of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] needs "its legends, its parables, its miracles" (P01-093) — and the Bible provides the narrative template.
