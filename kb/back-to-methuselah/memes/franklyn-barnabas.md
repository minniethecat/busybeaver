# Franklyn Barnabas

The central intellectual figure of Parts II and III of *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*. As a biologist and visionary, Franklyn Barnabas articulates the scientific and philosophical case for [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]], and his practical project — the extension of human life — provides the dramatic engine for the play's middle sections.

## Dramatis Personae

Franklyn Barnabas is the eldest and most articulate of the Barnabas brothers, a family of independent thinkers who have dedicated themselves to the project of extending human life. Where his brother Conrad provides practical support and his brother (the clergyman) provides institutional cover, Franklyn is the theorist — the one who understands and articulates what they are attempting and why it matters.

## Role in the Play

### Part II: The Gospel of the Brothers Barnabas

Franklyn's intellectual dominance is established in his first scenes. He expounds the doctrine of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] with missionary zeal — not as abstract philosophy but as a practical program. Human beings can live longer because the [[life-force|Life Force]], working through human [[free-will|will]] and intelligence, can extend the biological limits that [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]] treats as fixed.

Franklyn confronts the political establishment through the character of [[joyce-burge|Joyce Burge]] and the clerisy through his own brother. He argues that Science must account for everything — "Science has to account for everything; and everything includes the Bible" (003-014) — a position that puts him at odds with both religious orthodoxy and mechanistic science.

### Part III: The Thing Happens

In Part III, Franklyn's project begins to bear fruit. The Archbishop of York has achieved extended life, and the political implications become apparent. Franklyn's role shifts from prophet to witness — he sees what he predicted coming to pass, and the play's attention shifts to how the world reacts.

### Dramatic Function

Franklyn is the intellectual conscience of the play — the character who articulates what [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] believes and who demonstrates, through his unwavering commitment, that [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] is not merely a theory but a calling.

## Themes

Franklyn embodies the marriage of scientific rigor and evolutionary faith that [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] advocates. He is not a mystic — he is a biologist. But his biology leads him to conclusions that traditional religion would recognize: that life has purpose, that [[free-will|will]] matters, and that death can be overcome. He is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s ideal: the scientist as prophet.

## Real-World References

Franklyn Barnabas is a composite figure, drawing on [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s own intellectual development, the Fabian tradition of scientifically-informed social reform, and perhaps figures like J.B.S. Haldane — British biologists who combined scientific expertise with bold philosophical speculation.
