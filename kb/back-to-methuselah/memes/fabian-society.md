# Fabian Society

The Fabian Society is a British socialist organization founded in 1884, dedicated to advancing socialist principles through gradual, democratic reform rather than revolutionary upheaval. [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] was one of its earliest and most influential members, and his Fabian commitments shaped the political dimension of his creative philosophy.

## Overview

Founded at 17 Osnaburgh Street, [[london|London]], the Fabian Society took its name from the Roman general Fabius Maximus Cunctator, known for his strategy of patience and attrition rather than head-on confrontation. This namesake encapsulated the Society's core method: the steady, incremental transformation of society through parliamentary democracy, municipal governance, and intellectual persuasion.

The Society's emblem — a wolf in sheep's clothing — and its motto expressed its strategic gradualism. Rather than forming a separate political party, the Fabians sought to permeate existing institutions, most notably the Labour Party, with socialist ideas. They published tracts, delivered lectures, and conducted research to build the intellectual case for socialism.

By the early twentieth century, the Fabian Society had become one of the most influential intellectual forces in British political life, laying the groundwork for the welfare state that would emerge after the Second World War. Its method of research-driven advocacy, orderly persuasion, and elite intellectual engagement distinguished it from the revolutionary socialism of continental [[europe]].

## Shaw's Involvement

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] joined the Fabian Society in September 1884, just months after its founding, and quickly became one of its most energetic members. He served on the executive committee, edited *Fabian Essays in Socialism* (1889), and wrote numerous Fabian tracts on topics ranging from rent and taxation to municipal socialism. His wit and rhetorical skill made him the Society's most visible public advocate.

> Between the hour when my warders, the four members of the Executive Committee of the Fabian Society, sat on me, and their usual hour of departure, the field was won. (Shaw, *Sixteen Self Sketches*)

In the Preface to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] recalls how the Fabian Society asked him to deliver a lecture on [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] as part of a series on "Prophets of the Nineteenth Century" — a direct acknowledgment of his standing as the Society's resident evolution critic:

> ...it was in the hope of making me clear the matter up that the Fabian Society, which was then organizing a series of lectures on Prophets of the Nineteenth Century, asked me to deliver a lecture on the prophet Darwin. I did so; and scraps of that lecture, which was never published, variegate these pages. (P01-006)

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Fabian [[political-science|politics]] permeate the political argument of the Preface. His critique of [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]] was not merely biological. He believed that natural selection, applied to society, justified inequality and war. The Fabian alternative — deliberate, cooperative reform — was the political expression of what [[creative-evolution]] would become philosophically.

> Neo-Darwinism in politics had produced a European catastrophe of a magnitude so appalling... it is still far from certain whether our civilization will survive it. (P01-007)

This passage, immediately following the Fabian reference, ties [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s evolutionary argument directly to the political crisis of the First World War, which he saw as the logical outcome of applying [[darwinian]] competition to international relations.

## Historical Context

The Fabian Society emerged during a period of intense socialist ferment in Britain. The 1880s saw widespread industrial unrest, the growth of trade unionism, and the founding of multiple socialist organizations including the Social Democratic Federation and the Independent Labour Party. The Fabians distinguished themselves by rejecting the Marxist doctrine of class war.

Key Fabian principles included:

- The "inevitability of gradualism" — social transformation occurs step by step, not through sudden rupture

- Permeation — influencing existing institutions and parties rather than creating new ones

- Municipal socialism — using local government as a laboratory for public ownership and service provision

- Research and education — producing detailed factual studies as the basis for policy

The Society's pragmatic, middle-class, intellectual approach to socialism was shaped by its early members, many of whom — like [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] — were writers, journalists, and civil servants rather than trade unionists or factory workers. This gave it an unusual character: a think tank that also functioned as a political pressure group, operating through persuasion rather than mobilization.

By the time [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] wrote *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* (1918–1920), the world had been convulsed by war and revolution. The Bolshevik Revolution of 1917 presented a stark alternative to Fabian gradualism, and [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s insistence on evolutionary rather than revolutionary change — in biology as in [[political-science|politics]] — can be read as a Fabian response to the revolutionary moment.
