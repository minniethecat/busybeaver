# Religion

In [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s philosophy, religion is redefined as [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] — "the genuinely scientific religion for which all wise men are now anxiously looking" (P01-018). [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] sought to replace what he saw as bankrupt traditional religion and soulless mechanistic science with a new faith grounded in the creative impulse of the [[life-force|Life Force]].

## Definition

For [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], religion is not supernatural belief but the recognition that evolution has direction, purpose, and creative power. Traditional religion had lost credibility because it rested on discredited miracles; mechanistic science had failed because it offered no meaning. [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] bridges the gap: it is "already a religion, and is indeed now unmistakeably the religion of the twentieth century, newly arisen from the ashes of pseudo-Christianity, of mere scepticism, and of the soulless affirmations and blind negations of the Mechanists and Neo-[[darwinian|Darwinians]]" (P01-093).

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] traces his own religious development from youthful atheism to the discovery of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] as a genuinely satisfying faith. He had "discarded the religion of my forefathers" (P01-003) as a young man, but found that without religion, people are "dominated and exploited" — "men are cowards when they have no religion" (P01-008).

He argues that giving someone a religion without also giving them political and economic education is dangerous: "What is the use of giving him a religion? A religion may make him hungry and penniless" (P01-009). Religion without material foundation breeds either fanaticism or despair.

The key insight is that [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] offers a new kind of religious experience: one that does not require the abandonment of reason. "[[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] is already a religion, and is indeed now unmistakeably the religion of the twentieth century" (P01-093). But it cannot become popular "until it has its legends, its parables, its miracles" (P01-093) — which is precisely what *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* provides.

### In the Play

The play functions as scripture for the religion of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. The [[garden-of-eden|Garden of Eden]] legend is repurposed: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] "exploit[s] the eternal interest of the philosopher's stone which enables men to live forever" (P01-102). The [[longevity|long-lived]] characters are not just dramatic inventions — they are saints and prophets of the new faith, demonstrating what the [[life-force|Life Force]] can achieve.

## Intellectual Context

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s religious thinking draws on several traditions:

- **Dissenting Protestantism**: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Irish Protestant background gave him familiarity with Biblical narrative and the habit of individual conscience, even as he rejected orthodox doctrine.

- **Nineteenth-century [[atheism|secularism]]**: Figures like Charles Bradlaugh and the secularist movement promoted atheism and rationalism. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] passed through this phase but found it insufficient.

- **[[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] as religion**: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] follows a tradition of thinkers who sought to sacralize evolution — including Henri Bergson, whose *[[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]* (1907) was both a philosophical treatise and, in its reception, almost a sacred text.

## Dramatic Expression

The five-play cycle functions as a new scripture: [[garden-of-eden|Genesis]] (Part I, the Garden), Exodus (Part II, the discovery of the doctrine), the trials of the faithful (Part III–IV), and Revelation (Part V, the transfiguration of humanity). [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] explicitly frames this as religious storytelling: "I now find myself inspired to make a second legend of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]" (P01-102).
