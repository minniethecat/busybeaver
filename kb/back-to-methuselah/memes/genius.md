# Genius

In [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s evolutionary philosophy, genius is not merely exceptional individual talent but the primary mechanism through which the [[life-force|Life Force]] advances. The genius — whether artist, scientist, or prophet — is the person through whom "the impulse that produces evolution is creative" manifests most intensely.

## Definition

Genius, for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], is evolutionary leadership. The genius sees further, [[free-will|will]]s more intensely, and creates more boldly than ordinary humanity. This is not a matter of genetic luck (the Neo-[[darwinian|Darwinian]] explanation) but of the [[life-force|Life Force]] working through a particularly receptive individual. The genius is evolution's experimental apparatus — the trial that, when successful, moves the species forward.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s concept of genius emerges most clearly in his discussion of the creative artist's role. He describes "the leaders among my own contemporaries (now veterans)" who "snatched at minor social problems rather than write entirely without any wider purpose than to win money and fame" (P01-099). The artist of genius cannot be content with mere entertainment — the [[life-force|Life Force]] drives them toward deeper purposes.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s own biographical account of *[[man-and-superman|Man and Superman]]* illustrates the point: "being then at the height of my invention and comedic talent, I decorated it too brilliantly and lavishly" (P01-101). Genius has its dangers — brilliance can obscure purpose. The later, sparer *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* is the work of a mature genius who has learned to subordinate decoration to doctrine.

The Preface is itself a work of genius in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s sense — not mere argument but creative persuasion, using every rhetorical and dramatic resource to advance the cause of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].

### In the Play

The play is populated with genius-figures: [[the-serpent|the Serpent]] (genius of insight), the Barnabas brothers (genius of scientific imagination), [[the-he-ancient|the He-Ancient]] (genius of wisdom). Each represents the [[life-force|Life Force]] achieving a new level of expression through an exceptional individual.

## Intellectual Context

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s concept of genius connects to several traditions:

- **Romantic genius**: The Romantic movement elevated the creative artist to near-divine status — the genius as one who channels transcendent forces. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] secularizes this: the genius channels the [[life-force|Life Force]], not God.

- **Carlyle's hero**: Thomas Carlyle's theory of history as driven by great men influenced [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], though [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s heroes are not warriors but creative evolutionaries.

- **Nietzsche's Übermensch**: Friedrich Nietzsche's concept of the overman — a being who transcends ordinary human limitations — parallels [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s [[longevity|long-lived]] geniuses, though [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s version is more comic and less tragic.

## Dramatic Expression

The Barnabas brothers are [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s most explicit genius-figures: men of science who see what others cannot, and who dedicate their lives to making that vision real. They are not merely intelligent — they are *driven* by something beyond themselves, which [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] would identify as the [[life-force|Life Force]] working through their exceptional capacities.
