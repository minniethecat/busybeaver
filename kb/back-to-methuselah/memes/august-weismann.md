# August Weismann

German biologist (1834–1914) and a principal architect of [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]], whose doctrine of the isolation of the germ-plasm severed any [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarckian]] link between organism and heredity. In the Preface to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*, [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] singles out Weismann as the biologist whose work most starkly illustrates the limitations of mechanistic [[darwinian|Darwinism]].

## Shaw's Critique

Shaw's treatment of Weismann in the Preface is extensive — he appears in at least nine separate paragraphs (P01-017 through P01-059) — more than any other biologist except [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] himself. Shaw's strategy is to use Weismann as a case study in how a clever scientist can be led astray by mechanistic assumptions.

### The Germ-Plasm Theory

Weismann's key doctrine held that the germ-plasm (hereditary material) is isolated from the rest of the organism — that acquired characteristics cannot be inherited. This was a direct repudiation of [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]'s theory of use-inheritance and, in Shaw's view, a catastrophic narrowing of evolutionary biology.

> …duration of individual life. Weismann, a very clever and suggestive… (P01-017)

### The Mouse Experiment

Shaw's most sustained attack on Weismann concerns his famous experiment on mice:

> Consider, for example, how Weismann set to work on that subject. (P01-055)

> And yet Weismann began to investigate the point… (P01-056)

> …is a traitor to science. For Weismann's experiment upon the mice was… (P01-059)

Shaw argues that Weismann designed his experiment to prove a foregone conclusion — that acquired traits cannot be inherited — and that his methodology was circular. This is part of Shaw's broader case that Neo-Darwinism was not scientific but ideological.

### The Cat and the Chick

Shaw cites two of Weismann's arguments as examples of mechanistic absurdity:

> Weismann insisted that the chick breaks… (P01-052)

> …it only on its own terms. If Weismann really thought that cats act… (P01-053)

For Shaw, these examples show that Weismann attributed conscious, purposive behavior to animals while denying them any real [[free-will|free will]] or creative agency — a contradiction at the heart of mechanistic biology.

### Weismann and the July Revolution

> …t me repeat the story told by Weismann of the July revolution in Paris… (P01-033)

Shaw recounts Weismann's anecdote about the 1830 July Revolution in Paris, using it to illustrate how biologists unconsciously introduce Lamarckian assumptions (environment affecting organism) even as they formally deny them.

## Historical Significance

Weismann's germ-plasm theory became a cornerstone of the Modern Evolutionary Synthesis. The rediscovery of Mendel's laws and the rise of population genetics in the early 20th century produced a theory even more mechanistically rigid than Darwin's original — exactly the outcome Shaw feared and fought against in his promotion of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].
