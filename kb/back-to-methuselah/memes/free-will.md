# Free Will

The capacity for conscious, purposeful choice that [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] identifies as the essential missing element in [[darwinian|Darwinian evolution]]ary theory. For [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], free will is not a metaphysical puzzle but the practical mechanism through which the [[life-force|Life Force]] operates — it is what makes [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] possible and what distinguishes [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s philosophy from the mechanistic determinism he attributed to [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]].

## Definition

Free will, in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s framework, is the human expression of the [[life-force|Life Force]]. It is not an illusion generated by complex neural activity, nor a supernatural gift from a deity, but the evolutionary achievement of organisms complex enough to participate consciously in their own development. When a human being wills something — to live longer, to create, to understand — that act of will is the [[life-force|Life Force]] working through that individual.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s argument for free will is primarily a negative one: he shows what happens when you deny it. [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism, he argues, leaves no room for will: "there is no place in [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism for free will, or any other sort of will" (P01-060). If evolution proceeds entirely through random variation and environmental selection, then human choice is an epiphenomenon — a by-product with no causal power.

This is, for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], not just philosophically wrong but practically disastrous. If people believe they have no free will, they will act as if they have no responsibility. The political consequences of mechanistic determinism were, in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s view, visible in the catastrophe of the First World War.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s positive case is that free will is the engine of evolution. The [[life-force|Life Force]] does not operate mysteriously from outside — it operates through the conscious choices of living beings. When [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]'s giraffe stretches for high leaves, it is exercising free will. When the Barnabas brothers dedicate their lives to extending human [[immortality|longevity]], they are exercising free will. Evolution is driven by countless such acts of will, accumulated across generations.

### In the Play

[[eve|Eve]]ry major turning point in the play is an act of free will: [[eve|Eve]] chooses to conceive; [[the-serpent|the Serpent]] chooses to speak; the Barnabas brothers choose to pursue [[immortality|longevity]]; the [[longevity|long-lived]] choose to transcend the body. These are not random events — they are deliberate, willed actions that change the course of evolution.

## Key Passages

> As there is no place in Darwinism for free will, or any other sort of will. (P01-060)

> The impulse that produces evolution is creative. (P01-016)

## Intellectual Context

The free will debate has deep philosophical roots:

- **Determinism**: The view that all events, including human choices, are determined by prior causes. In its scientific form, this is the view [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] attributed to [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]]: genes plus environment determine everything. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] rejected this as both false and dangerous.

- **Compatibilism**: The view that free will and determinism can coexist — we are free when we act according to our own desires, even if those desires are themselves determined. David Hume and, later, many analytic philosophers adopted this position. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s view is more robust: the will must have genuine creative power.

- **[[vitalism|Vitalism]]**: Henri Bergson and other vitalist philosophers argued that life cannot be reduced to mechanical processes — there is an [[vitalism|élan vital]], a creative impulse, that drives evolution. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s concept of free will is closely tied to this vitalist tradition.

- **[[lamarckism|Lamarckism]]**: The biological theory that makes free will evolutionarily significant. If acquired characteristics can be inherited, then acts of will — stretching for higher leaves, choosing to live longer — can be passed to offspring and become species traits.

## Dramatic Expression

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] dramatizes free will through a series of decisive choices. [[the-serpent|The Serpent]] chooses to speak the forbidden truth. [[eve|Eve]] chooses knowledge over safety. The Barnabas brothers choose the long, difficult work of extending human life. These choices are presented not as the inevitable outcomes of genetic programming and environmental conditioning, but as genuine acts of creative will — the [[life-force|Life Force]] choosing its own direction through human agency.
