# Natural Selection

[[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]]'s central mechanism of evolutionary change, which [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] both respected and critiqued. While acknowledging its explanatory power for certain classes of biological phenomena, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] argued that Natural Selection alone could not account for the full scope of evolution — particularly the emergence of consciousness, [[free-will|will]], and creative intelligence.

## Definition

Natural Selection is the process by which organisms with heritable traits better suited to their environment tend to survive and reproduce more successfully than those with less advantageous traits. Over many generations, this differential reproductive success shifts the distribution of traits within a population. [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] proposed this mechanism in *[[origin-of-species|On the Origin of Species]]* (1859) as the primary driver of evolutionary change.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s engagement with Natural Selection is nuanced. He does not deny that it occurs — he denies that it is *sufficient*. His critique operates on several levels.

First, he distinguishes [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] from the [[darwinian|Darwinians]]: "the conflict between the view of Evolution taken by the [[darwinian|Darwinians]] (though not altogether by [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] himself) and called Natural Selection, and that which is emerging, under the title of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]" (P01-018). [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] is more generous to [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] than to [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s followers.

Second, he grants Natural Selection its domain: "there is nothing to puzzle you in [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism" (P01-049) — the mechanism is simple, almost banal. A butterfly knows as much about Natural Selection as a scientist: "I heed know no more about [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism than a butterfly knows of a larva" (P01-053).

Third, he argues that Natural Selection fails to explain the most important phenomena. "[[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism proclaimed that our true relation is that of competitors and slaughterers" (P01-059), eliminating the cooperative and creative dimensions of life. "There is no place in [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism for free [[free-will|will]]" (P01-060) — and without free [[free-will|will]], human achievement becomes inexplicable.

### In the Play

The play dramatizes what Natural Selection cannot do: it cannot explain why [[the-serpent|the Serpent]] first urged [[eve|Eve]] toward conception, why the Barnabas brothers dedicated their lives to extending human [[immortality|longevity]], or why the [[longevity|long-lived]] beings of Part V have transcended bodily form. These are acts of [[free-will|will]] and intelligence — [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]], not mere selection.

## Key Passages

> The conflict between the view of Evolution taken by the Darwinians (though not altogether by Darwin himself) and called Natural Selection, and that which is emerging, under the title of Creative Evolution. (P01-018)

> There is nothing to puzzle you in Darwinism. (P01-049)

> Darwinism proclaimed that our true relation is that of competitors and slaughterers, and that we must treat the "lesser breeds without the law" as such. (P01-059)

> There is no place in Darwinism for free will, or any other sort of will. (P01-060)

## Intellectual Context

[[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s Natural Selection transformed biology in the nineteenth century and remains foundational. Key developments in its reception include:

- **[[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s own caution**: [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] himself was aware of difficulties with his theory, particularly the lack of a mechanism for heredity. He was more philosophically modest than many of his followers.

- **"Survival of the Fittest"**: Herbert Spencer's phrase, which [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] adopted in later editions of *Origin*, gave Natural Selection a competitive, sometimes brutal connotation that [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] found politically dangerous.

- **The modern evolutionary synthesis**: The integration of Natural Selection with Mendelian genetics produced [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]], which [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] attacked even more vigorously than classical [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism.

## Dramatic Expression

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s dramatic method refutes Natural Selection not by argument but by demonstration. The play shows evolution occurring through conscious choice: [[eve|Eve]] chooses to bear children, the Barnabas brothers choose to pursue [[immortality|longevity]], the [[longevity|long-lived]] choose to transcend the body. These are not random variations filtered by environment — they are acts of creative [[free-will|will]]. The play is a five-act dramatic proof that Natural Selection is not the whole story.
