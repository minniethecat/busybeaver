# Georges Cuvier

The French naturalist and zoologist (1769–1832), a towering figure in comparative anatomy and paleontology. In the Preface to [[back-to-methuselah]], [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] invokes [[georges-cuvier]] through Goethe's famous reaction to the 1830 debate at the [[french-academy]] — a debate that [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] presents as a pivotal moment in the history of evolutionary thought, predating [[charles-darwin]] by decades.

## Dramatis Personae

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] recounts the story "told by Weismann of the July revolution in Paris in 1830," when Goethe — then still living — was asked about the political upheaval but dismissed it entirely: "'Stuff!' said Goethe: 'I am not thinking of these people at all, but of the open rupture in the [[french-academy|French Academy]] between Cuvier and [[etienne-geoffroy-saint-hilaire|St. Hilaire]]. It is of the utmost importance to science'" (P01-033). Cuvier's position, as [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] summarizes it, was that there were four distinct species types, against [[etienne-geoffroy-saint-hilaire]]'s contention "that there was only one" (P01-033).

## Role in the Play

Cuvier does not appear as a character. His significance in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s argument is historical: he represents the pre-[[darwinian|Darwinian]] scientific establishment whose resistance to evolutionary unification illustrated that evolutionism "was the rage before Queen Victoria came to the throne" (P01-033). [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] uses the Cuvier–[[etienne-geoffroy-saint-hilaire|Geoffroy]] debate to demonstrate that evolutionary thinking was not invented by [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] but had a rich pre-[[darwinian|Darwinian]] history in which French naturalists played a leading role.

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s most significant reference places Cuvier at the center of the 1830 Academy debate: "The rupture Goethe meant was about Evolution, Cuvier contending that there were four species, and [[etienne-geoffroy-saint-hilaire|St. Hilaire]] that there was only one" (P01-033). This debate, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] argues, was more important than the political revolution happening simultaneously outside the Academy's doors.

## Relationships

- [[etienne-geoffroy-saint-hilaire]]: Cuvier's opponent in the 1830 [[french-academy|French Academy]] debate — Cuvier defended species fixity against [[etienne-geoffroy-saint-hilaire|Geoffroy]]'s evolutionary unity
- [[jean-baptiste-lamarck]]: Cuvier's older contemporary at the [[french-academy|French Academy]]; Cuvier opposed [[lamarckism|Lamarckian evolution]] as well
- [[french-academy]]: The institutional setting of the 1830 debate
- [[charles-darwin]]: The figure who would later synthesize and transform the debate in which Cuvier participated

## Themes

Cuvier embodies the tension between taxonomic rigor and evolutionary thinking. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] presents his debate with [[etienne-geoffroy-saint-hilaire|Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire]] not as a simple triumph of evolution over fixity, but as evidence that evolutionary questions were already central to scientific discourse long before [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]. The fact that Goethe — the supreme [[europe|Europe]]an intellectual — considered this debate more significant than the French Revolution of 1830 underscores [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s argument that ideas about life's origins and development are the most consequential of human inquiries.

## Real-World References

Georges Cuvier (1769–1832), known as the "founding father of paleontology," established the reality of extinction and developed the principle of the correlation of parts in comparative anatomy. His debate with [[etienne-geoffroy-saint-hilaire|Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire]] at the Académie des Sciences in 1830 was a defining intellectual event of the era, drawing the attention of Goethe and other [[europe|Europe]]an intellectuals. Cuvier's catastrophism — the theory that Earth's geological history was shaped by sudden, violent events — contrasted with the gradualism of [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]] and later [[charles-darwin|Darwin]].
