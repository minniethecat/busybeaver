# Part III Guide: The Thing Happens

[[part-iii|Part III: The Thing Happens]] is the pivot of [[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]] — the point where Shaw's evolutionary speculation leaves the realm of theory and enters history. Set on a summer afternoon in 2170 AD, it shows us a world where the Barnabas longevity theory has been silently vindicated, and a secret community of long-livers now occupies the highest offices of state.

## Setting

The official parlor of the President of the British Islands, 2170 AD. The British Empire has devolved into "the British Islands" — a diminished but still self-important political entity. The technology is modestly advanced (cinema records, electric conveniences), but the real transformation is biological: a handful of people have discovered they are living centuries longer than normal.

## Key Characters

### The Long-Livers

- [[william-haslam|Archbishop Haslam]] (283 years old) — Once the young rector Bill Haslam who courted Savvy Barnabas in Part II, now the Archbishop. He has survived by periodically faking his death and assuming a new identity. His reunion with Mrs. Lutestring forms the emotional heart of the Part.

- [[mrs-lutestring|Mrs. Lutestring]] (274 years old) — The Domestic Minister. She was once the Barnabas household's parlour maid. Her journey from exhausted domestic servant to formidable stateswoman is Shaw's most detailed portrait of what longevity can do for an "ordinary" person.

- [[barnabas|Barnabas]] (over 300 years old) — The long-lived remnant of the Barnabas family, now serving as Accountant General. Cynical, amused, and possessor of the cinema records that prove the long-livers' true identities.

### The Short-Lived

- [[burge-lubin|Burge-Lubin]] — The President, a blustering short-liver who combines the surnames of the two politicians from Part II (Burge and Lubin). He governs with "breezy assertion" and "shallow jollity" — exactly the kind of leader the long-livers regard as a child.

- [[confucius|Confucius]] — The Chinese Chief Secretary, a short-liver who has the long-livers' respect because he takes administration seriously. His presence signals Shaw's recognition that the East may be civilization's future.

## Thematic Structure

### 1. Recognition and Revelation

The Part builds toward two intertwined revelations:
- The Archbishop and Mrs. Lutestring recognize each other across two centuries
- Both are revealed to be long-livers who have been hiding their true ages

This recognition scene (004-006-r283 through r347) is among Shaw's most moving writing — two people who were young together, meeting again as ancient souls in young bodies.

### 2. The Critique of Short-Lived Politics

Mrs. Lutestring's indictment of the President is the play's most explicit political argument:
> "There are moments when your levity, your ingratitude, your shallow jollity, make my gorge rise so against you that if I could not remind myself that you are a child I should be tempted to doubt your right to live at all." (004-006-r378)

The point is clear: short-lived politicians are children playing with adult responsibilities. Only longevity can produce genuine political maturity.

### 3. The Cost of Longevity

Mrs. Lutestring's testimony also reveals what longevity costs:
- She has outlived all her children ("my children are all dead" — 004-006-r343)
- She married a great painter at 101, only to watch him die of old age before he could mature as an artist
- She endures profound loneliness among the "children" of the short-lived world

Shaw does not pretend longevity is easy. It is, as Mrs. Lutestring says, "complicated, and even terrible; but it is glorious all the same" (004-006-r347).

### 4. The Race Question

Part III introduces a racial dimension absent from earlier Parts. The President's dependence on "negresses and Chinamen" (Mrs. Lutestring's blunt 1920s language) suggests a shift in global power. The Archbishop and Mrs. Lutestring conclude that "if the white race is to be saved, our destiny is apparent" — meaning that the long-livers must take control before short-lived politicians cede everything to more competent short-lived administrators from other civilizations.
