# Creative Evolution (Bergson)

Henri Bergson's *L'Évolution créatrice* (1907), translated into English as *[[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]* (1911) — the philosophical treatise that gave [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] his central concept and much of his vocabulary. Bergson's argument that evolution is driven by an *[[vitalism|élan vital]]* — a creative [[life-force|vital impulse]] — provided [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] with the philosophical framework he needed to challenge mechanistic [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]].

## Shaw's Debt

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] is not identical to Bergson's, but the debt is acknowledged and substantial. Bergson provided the philosophical argument; [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] provided the dramatic demonstration. Together, they represent the vitalist alternative to mechanism.

## Relationship to Back to Methuselah

The play dramatizes what Bergson philosophized. Where Bergson argued that the [[vitalism|élan vital]] drives evolution toward higher consciousness, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] showed it happening — from the [[garden-of-eden|Garden of Eden]] to the far future, through five plays spanning tens of thousands of years.
