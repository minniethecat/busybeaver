# George Bernard Shaw: A Biographical Sketch

[[george-bernard-shaw|George Bernard Shaw]] (1856–1950) was an Irish playwright, critic, and polemicist — the leading English-language dramatist of his generation and the intellectual force behind [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] as a worldview. This page aggregates his biography from across the wiki.

## Early Life and Ireland

Shaw was born in [[dublin|Dublin]] in 1856 to a declining Protestant gentry family. His father was an unsuccessful merchant and alcoholic; his mother was a professional singer who left Dublin for London when Shaw was a young man. Shaw followed her to London in 1876, at age 20, and spent his twenties in poverty — writing five novels, all rejected by publishers, while educating himself in the British Museum reading room.

His Irish background informed his outsider perspective on English society. As he later wrote, Ireland gave him the critical distance to see England as it really was.

## Fabian Society and Political Awakening

In 1884, Shaw joined the newly formed [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]] — a socialist organization committed to gradual, constitutional reform rather than revolution. Shaw became the Fabians' most effective propagandist, writing tracts, delivering thousands of street-corner speeches, and editing ''Fabian Essays in Socialism'' (1889).

The Fabian years shaped Shaw's view that social progress required institutional transformation, not just individual moral improvement. His critique of capitalism, poverty, and the class system permeates all his plays, including [[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]].

## Career as Playwright and Critic

Shaw established himself as a music critic, art critic, and then theatre critic before turning to playwriting in the 1890s. His early plays — ''Widowers' Houses'', ''Mrs. Warren's Profession'', ''Arms and the Man'' — were "plays of ideas" that used the theatre as a platform for social criticism.

By the 1900s, Shaw was the most famous playwright in the English-speaking world. [[man-and-superman|Man and Superman]] (1903) introduced the [[life-force|Life Force]] as a dramatic concept. ''Major Barbara'' (1905), ''Pygmalion'' (1913), ''Heartbreak House'' (1919), and ''Saint Joan'' (1923) cemented his reputation.

## The Creative Evolution Years

The First World War shattered Shaw's progressive optimism. The spectacle of European civilization destroying itself convinced him that humanity was not morally mature enough for the technological power it possessed. His response was [[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]] (1921) — a five-play cycle spanning from the Garden of Eden to the year 31,920 AD, dramatizing his philosophy of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].

Shaw's intellectual debt to [[samuel-butler|Samuel Butler]] is crucial: Butler had argued decades earlier that Darwinian natural selection was philosophically inadequate, and that evolution must be understood as driven by will, habit, and purpose. Shaw extended Butler's argument into a full cosmological vision in which the [[life-force|Life Force]] learns through experimentation, and humanity is its most ambitious experiment yet.

## Later Life and Legacy

Shaw lived to 94 — long enough to see his early Fabian ideals partially realized in the British welfare state, and long enough to become a world-famous sage. He won the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1925. He died in 1950 at his home in Ayot St. Lawrence.

His prediction in ''Back to Methuselah'' — that humanity must learn to live longer in order to govern itself wisely — remains provocative. Whether or not the biology is plausible, the moral argument retains its force: we are, as [[mrs-lutestring|Mrs. Lutestring]] says, children governing children, and our politics reflects that immaturity.
