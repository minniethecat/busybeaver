# Hamlet

[[william-shakespeare|William Shakespeare]]'s tragedy of the Danish prince, representing the dramatic tradition that [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] inherited, admired, and deliberately departed from. [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]] asked the great questions — about mortality, meaning, and action — that [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] answered with [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].

## Shaw's Relationship

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] was a lifelong [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]] critic and admirer. The comment that "[[charles-darwin|Darwin]] could not read [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]]" (P01-018) is a backhanded compliment to both men: [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]]'s world is too rich for the narrow scientific mind, but [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s science is too important for the merely literary mind. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] aimed to combine both.
