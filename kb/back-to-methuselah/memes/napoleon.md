# Napoleon

Napoleon Bonaparte appears as a character in Part IV of *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* — a ghost from the short-lived past who encounters the [[longevity|long-lived]] beings of the future and is revealed as a figure of almost comic inadequacy. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] uses Napoleon to dramatize the obsolescence of traditional political ambition in a world transformed by [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].

## Dramatis Personae

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Napoleon is introduced with his most famous self-designation: "I am the Man of Destiny" (006-002). The claim, which once shook [[europe|Europe]], now lands with absurdity in a world where human beings live for centuries and [[political-science|politics]] has been transformed beyond recognition by biological achievement.

Napoleon's appearance in Part IV — set in 3000 AD — is one of [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s boldest dramatic inventions. The great man of history, the conqueror of [[europe|Europe]], is reduced to a curiosity: a short-lived being whose ambitions and achievements mean nothing to people who measure time in centuries.

## Role in the Play

### Part IV: Tragedy of an Elderly Gentleman

Napoleon appears among the figures conjured by the Oracle, alongside other historical personages. His characteristic self-assertion — "I am the Man of Destiny" — meets not with fear or awe but with gentle, amused condescension from the [[longevity|long-lived]].

The scene functions as [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s commentary on the nature of political greatness. If [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] succeeds in extending human life, the Napoleons of history [[free-will|will]] be seen as what they truly were: men of limited vision operating within the narrow constraints of a short life. Their ambitions — conquest, glory, empire — are the ambitions of beings who know they [[free-will|will]] die soon.

### Dramatic Function

Napoleon serves as a dramatic measuring-stick. By showing the great conqueror reduced to absurdity, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] demonstrates how radically the scale of human achievement changes when lifespan extends. What seems like world-historical significance within the horizon of a normal human life becomes, from the perspective of centuries, a footnote.

## Themes

Napoleon embodies the thesis that political ambition is a symptom of mortality. Men who know they [[free-will|will]] die in decades seek to leave their mark through conquest and domination. Men who expect to live for centuries have no need of such compensations — their ambition is evolutionary, not political.

## Real-World References

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821) was Emperor of the French and the dominant military and political figure of early nineteenth-century [[europe|Europe]]. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s choice of Napoleon as the emblem of obsolete political ambition is pointed: no figure in modern [[europe|Europe]]an history more perfectly represents the [[free-will|will]] to power expressed through military conquest. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s point is that even this supreme expression of the [[free-will|will]] to power is rendered meaningless by the biological transformation he envisions.
