# Discouragement

In [[Back to Methuselah]], discouragement is a **"deadly disease"** peculiar to short-lived humans — a fatal psycho-biological condition triggered by prolonged contact with the [[longevity|long-lived]]. It is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s most pointed dramatization of the evolutionary gap between ordinary humanity and the future race his [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] theory envisions.

## Definition

Discouragement is not melancholy or depression in the ordinary sense. It is a **species-level incompatibility**: short-lived people, when exposed to the presence, conversation, or achievements of the [[longevity|long-lived]], suffer a collapse of the [[free-will|will]] to live. Their organism simply cannot sustain itself in the face of beings whose capacities and perspectives make their own lives seem futile (005-003).

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Play

Discouragement is introduced in Part IV, when the [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]] — a short-lived visitor from [[baghdad|Baghdad]] — encounters Zoo, a [[longevity|long-lived]] guardian of [[ireland|Ireland]]. Zoo immediately recognizes that he is "already showing grave symptoms of that very distressing and usually fatal complaint" (005-006). The disease is treated matter-of-factly by the [[longevity|long-lived]]: it is a medical reality, not a metaphor.

Throughout the encounter, the [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]] oscillates between indignation, confusion, despair, and desperate self-assertion — the classic course of the disease. He boasts of his ancestry, his civilization, his progress — and Zoo's calm replies dismantle every one of his certainties. The [[longevity|long-lived]] do not intend to harm the short-lived; it is simply a "law of Nature that there is a fixed relation between conduct and length of life" (005-006).

By Part IV Act III, the [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]] has died of discouragement — despite the best efforts of the [[longevity|long-lived]] to protect him. [[the-oracle|The Oracle]], surveying his body, says simply: "Poor short-lived thing! What else could I do for you?" (007-003).

## Key Passages

> "There is a deadly disease called discouragement, against which short-lived people have to take very strict precautions. Intercourse with us puts too great a strain on them." — Zoo (005-003)

> "You are already showing grave symptoms of that very distressing and usually fatal complaint." — Zoo to the Elderly Gentleman (005-006)

> "You would die of discouragement in their presence in less than a day." — Zoo, warning the Elderly Gentleman about meeting other long-lived people (006-002)

> "If you stay with us you will die of discouragement." — Zoo to the Elderly Gentleman (007-003)

> "Poor short-lived thing! What else could I do for you?" — The Oracle, over the Elderly Gentleman's body (007-003)

## Intellectual Context

Discouragement is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s most radical dramatization of a central evolutionary argument: that there is no peaceful coexistence between different stages of human development. Just as the [[longevity|long-lived]] have evolved beyond romantic love, political ambition, and the fear of death, the short-lived are biologically incapable of sharing their world. The disease is not a punishment — it is a **symptom of evolutionary incompleteness**.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] reverses the expected hierarchy: it is the *short-lived* who are the fragile, endangered species, and the [[longevity|long-lived]] who must take precautions to protect them. This inversion is central to his critique of the complacency of ordinary human life.

### Related Thinkers

- [[samuel-butler|Samuel Butler]] — [[samuel-butler|Butler]]'s critique of [[darwinian|Darwinian]] mechanism influenced [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s view of evolution as a purposeful, [[free-will|will]]-driven process
- [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]] — The idea that organisms can adapt their constitution through [[free-will|will]] and effort

### Contrasting Concepts

- [[longevity]] — Discouragement is the pathology of insufficient [[immortality|longevity]]; the two concepts are inseparable
- [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] — Discouragement illustrates what happens when evolution stalls
- [[vitalism]] — The vital force that sustains life is precisely what fails in discouragement

## Dramatic Expression

[[the-elderly-gentleman|The Elderly Gentleman]]'s arc is the fullest depiction of discouragement. He arrives in [[ireland|Ireland]] full of pompous self-regard, boasting of his distinguished ancestry and the glories of short-lived civilization. Zoo's calm, factual replies — about the futility of his achievements, the bankruptcy of his political institutions, the childishness of his passions — progressively dismantle him. He tries dancing, singing, indignation, flattery, and philosophical oratory, each in turn, and each fails to bridge the gap.

The tragedy is that the [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]] is, by short-lived standards, an admirable man — intelligent, cultured, well-meaning. His death is not a punishment for vice but a natural consequence of his biological limits. He cannot help being what he is; and what he is cannot survive in the presence of what humanity may become.
