# Longevity

In [[Back to Methuselah]], [[immortality|longevity]] — the extension of the human lifespan to at least three hundred years — is not a mere fantasy of extended youth, but the **central biological precondition** of [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s entire philosophical project. Without sufficient time to accumulate experience, human beings cannot outgrow the "childish passions" that make wise government impossible.

## Definition

Longevity, in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s usage, means a human lifespan of **at least three centuries** — long enough for an individual to acquire genuine wisdom, to outlive the primitive impulses of youth, and to become capable of the kind of long-term thinking that responsible governance requires (003-006). It is distinct from mere immortality or agelessness: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s long-lived are not frozen in time; they mature through accumulated experience.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] argues throughout *The Infidel Half Century* that the brief span of human life — "threescore and ten" — is the hidden cause of nearly all political and moral failure. He contends that [[darwinian|Darwinian]] natural selection, if properly understood, points toward the purposeful extension of life as the next stage of evolution. His concept of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] depends on the organism's capacity to *[[free-will|will]]* its own transformation, and the first thing human beings should [[free-will|will]] is more time (P01-100).

### In the Play

The Brothers Barnabas, in Part II, arrive independently at the same figure: "at least three centuries" (003-006). Conrad, a professor of biology, frames it as a scientific necessity. [[franklyn-barnabas|Franklyn]], an ex-clergyman, frames it as a moral one: "Life is too short for men to take it seriously" (003-006).

By Part III (AD 2170), the first long-lived individuals have appeared. The Archbishop of York is over two centuries old, and [[confucius|Confucius]] — revealed to be still alive after 2,500 years — argues that the long-lived must organize and become "a great Power" (004-006). The Archbishop confronts the political establishment with the reality that long-lived citizens who "continue to work and produce" cannot simply be retired or dismissed (004-006).

In Part IV (AD 3000), [[ireland|Ireland]] has become a sanctuary of the long-lived. The contrast between the "short-lived" visitors and the "normal" long-lived population is dramatized through the [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]], who boasts of his "relative [[immortality|longevity]]" but is treated as a fragile, childish creature by Zoo, a long-lived guardian (005-006).

## Key Passages

> "The term of human life must be extended to at least three centuries." — Franklyn Barnabas (003-006)

> "If I thought I was going to live nine hundred and sixty years, I don't think I should stay in the Church." — Haslam, the Rector (003-006)

> "Brother: if that is so; if biology as you have worked at it, and religion as I have worked at it, both lead to the same conclusion — that the term of human life must be extended — then neither of us can be talking nonsense." (003-006)

> "The extension of life to three hundred years has provided the human race with capable leaders, and made short work of such childish stuff." — Zoo (005-006)

> "It is not enough to know what is good: you must be able to do it. They couldn't do it because they did not live long enough to find out how to do it, or to outlive the childish passions that prevented them from really wanting to do it." — Zoo (005-006)

## Intellectual Context

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s concept of [[immortality|longevity]] draws on several intellectual traditions. From [[lamarckism|Lamarckism]], he takes the idea that organisms can actively adapt to environmental demands — that the *[[free-will|will]]* to live longer could itself become an evolutionary force. From [[schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]], he borrows the concept of the Will as a metaphysical driving force, though he transforms it into a creative, life-affirming impulse. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] is directly contesting the [[darwinian|Darwinian]] orthodoxy of his day, which treated death as a neutral mechanism of natural selection.

### Related Thinkers

- [[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]] — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] contests [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s mechanism but accepts the evolutionary framework
- [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]] — The [[lamarckism|inheritance of acquired characteristics]] is central to [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s biology of [[immortality|longevity]]
- [[schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] — The concept of Will as a cosmic force

### Contrasting Concepts

- [[immortality]] — Longevity is finite (centuries, not eternity); it is earned through biological [[free-will|will]], not granted supernaturally
- [[natural-selection|Natural Selection]] — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] sees natural selection as an insufficient mechanism; purposeful [[immortality|longevity]] must replace it
- [[discouragement]] — The "[[discouragement|deadly disease]]" that kills short-lived people who cannot bear the presence of the long-lived

## Dramatic Expression

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] stages [[immortality|longevity]] not as a technological achievement but as a **biological and moral evolution**. The long-lived characters — the Archbishop, [[confucius|Confucius]], Zoo, [[the-he-ancient|the He-Ancient]] — are not superheroes. They are calmer, wiser, and more detached than the short-lived; they have outgrown romantic love, political ambition, and the fear of death. Their presence is literally dangerous to short-lived people, who contract "discouragement" and die when exposed to them for too long.

The play's five-part structure itself enacts the argument: from the [[garden-of-eden|Garden of Eden]] (where [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]] first discover death) to the year 31,920 AD (where the Ancients have evolved beyond the body itself), [[immortality|longevity]] is the invisible thread connecting each epoch.
