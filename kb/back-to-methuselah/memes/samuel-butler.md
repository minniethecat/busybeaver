# Samuel Butler

The English novelist, satirist, and critic (1835–1902) whose fierce opposition to [[charles-darwin]]'s theory of natural selection made him, in [[george-bernard-shaw]]'s telling, the great contrarian of Victorian evolutionary debate. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] devotes sustained, admiring attention to [[samuel-butler]] in the Preface to [[back-to-methuselah]], presenting him as the lonely voice who "ran up the [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian flag to the topgallant peak" (P01-051) — the intellectual forebear of [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s own [[creative-evolution]].

## Dramatis Personae

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] introduces Butler as the exception to the universal surrender to [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism: "[[eve|Eve]]ryone who had a mind to change, changed it. Only Samuel Butler, on whom [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] had acted homeopathically, reacted against him furiously; ran up the [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian flag to the topgallant peak; declared with penetrating accuracy that [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] had 'banished mind from the universe'; and even attacked [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s personal character" (P01-051). [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] acknowledges Butler's excessive reaction — attacking [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s character was unfair — while affirming the penetrating accuracy of Butler's critique.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] also notes Butler's isolation: "Nobody would listen to him. He was so completely submerged by the flowing tide of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism that he was hardly recognized as an opponent" (P01-051). This makes Butler a tragic-heroic figure in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s narrative — right about the fundamental issue, but dismissed by his contemporaries.

## Role in the Play

Butler does not appear as a character in the play cycle. His presence in the Preface is as an intellectual precursor — the man who saw, before anyone else, that [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism "banished mind from the universe" and who insisted on the [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian alternative that [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] would later develop into [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] credits Butler as "the pioneer of the reaction against [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism" who sought "to restore mind to the universe and religion to its place" (P01-082). And he recommends "Mr. Festing Jones's memoir of Samuel Butler to learn how completely even a saint can be misunderstood" (P01-047).

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

Butler's penetrating critique, as [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] quotes it: [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] had "banished mind from the universe" (P01-051).

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s own assessment: Butler "reacted against him furiously; ran up the [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian flag to the topgallant peak" (P01-051).

On Butler's religious significance: "Samuel Butler was the pioneer of the reaction against [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism... seeking to restore mind to the universe and religion to its place" (P01-082).

On Butler's educational background: "Samuel Butler was the pupil of a hopelessly unscientific public school and university" (P01-012) — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] noting that Butler's resistance to [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] was not based on scientific training but on philosophical insight.

## Relationships

- [[charles-darwin]]: Butler's great antagonist; [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] says [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] "acted homeopathically" on Butler
- [[jean-baptiste-lamarck]]: The scientific tradition Butler championed against [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism
- [[george-bernard-shaw]]: Butler's intellectual heir; [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] identified as a Neo-[[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian in Butler's tradition

## Themes

Butler represents the power of contrarian insight. Alone against the [[darwinian|Darwinian]] tide, he identified the central philosophical flaw in natural selection — its banishment of mind, purpose, and [[free-will|will]] from the evolutionary process. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] presents him as a prophet without honor, vindicated only later when [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] restored what [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism had removed. Butler's career illustrates [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s conviction that truth does not depend on popularity, and that the most important intellectual battles are often fought by isolated figures against overwhelming consensus.

## Real-World References

Samuel Butler (1835–1902) was an English novelist and critic, best known for the satirical novel *Erewhon* (1872) and the semi-autobiographical *The Way of All Flesh* (1903, published posthumously). His evolutionary writings include *Life and Habit* (1878), *Evolution, Old and New* (1879), and *Unconscious Memory* (1880), in which he developed a [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian critique of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]. Butler's attack on [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s character — referring to [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s treatment of earlier evolutionists — generated lasting controversy. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], while disagreeing with the personal attack, embraced Butler's [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian argument.
