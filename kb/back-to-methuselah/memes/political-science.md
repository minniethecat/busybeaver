# Political Science

In [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s analysis, political science is the most neglected branch of knowledge — and the most urgently needed. Throughout the Preface to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] argues that the failure to educate citizens in political science has produced a world of "cowards," "dominated and exploited" populations, and ultimately the catastrophe of the First World War.

## Definition

Political science, as [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] uses the term, is not an academic discipline but the practical knowledge required for democratic citizenship: understanding how power works, how institutions function, and how collective decisions are made. Without this knowledge, populations are helpless — dominated by those who do understand power, and incapable of the self-government that [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] demands.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s argument about political science emerges from his diagnosis of the causes of the First World War. He describes a [[europe|Europe]] where populations lacked even basic political understanding: "men do not carry with them a grain of political science, which is a very complicated science" (P01-009). The result is that even well-meaning people are helpless when confronted with political crises.

He presents the teaching of political science as an urgent necessity: "We must teach citizenship and political science at school. But must we? There is no must about it" (P01-010). The passive voice of "must" is telling — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] recognizes that those in power have little incentive to educate the population in the skills of self-government.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] notes the paradox that the working class, despite its numbers, is politically powerless because of ignorance: "nobody could make them understand what was happening" (P01-009). Political science is not a luxury of the educated elite — it is the essential tool of democratic self-defense.

He traces the political consequences of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism: "[[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism was so closely related to Capitalism" (P01-072) that it "had already brought political science into that unbreathable atmosphere" (P01-072). The survival-of-the-fittest ideology undermined the cooperative, educative dimensions of politics.

### In the Play

The political dimension is most explicit in Parts II and III, where the Barnabas brothers interact with the political establishment. Burge-[[lubin|Lubin]] and [[joyce-burge|Joyce Burge]] represent the political class — men of ambition and rhetoric but limited vision. The Barnabas brothers, with their scientific project of life extension, represent a different kind of political intervention: not electoral or rhetorical, but transformative. By changing the biological facts of human existence, they change the conditions under which politics operates.

## Intellectual Context

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s thinking about political science was shaped by:

- **Fabian socialism**: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] was a leading member of the [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]], which advocated gradual, democratic socialism achieved through education and persuasion rather than revolution. The Fabian emphasis on research, publication, and public education reflects [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s conviction that political science must be widely disseminated.

- **The First World War**: The war was, for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], the ultimate demonstration of the failure of political education. Populations that understood how international relations worked would not have been so easily led into slaughter.

- **Ibsen and the problem play**: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s dramatic method — using the stage as a forum for political and social argument — was influenced by Henrik Ibsen. The play that teaches political science is itself an act of political education.

## Dramatic Expression

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s characters frequently deliver what amount to political lectures. The Barnabas brothers' long speeches in Parts II and III are not dramatic flaws but deliberate pedagogy — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] is doing through drama what he argues schools should do through formal education: teaching political science to the public.
