# George Bernard Shaw

The Irish playwright, critic, and polemicist (1856–1950) whose play cycle [[back-to-methuselah]] (1921) presents his most sustained argument for [[creative-evolution]] — the doctrine that humanity must take charge of its own evolutionary future through [[free-will|will]] and effort. A committed [[fabian-society]] socialist and the leading English-language dramatist of his generation after [[william-shakespeare]].

## Dramatis Personae

Shaw appears throughout the Preface as a first-person narrator and polemicist, describing his own intellectual development and the genesis of his evolutionary philosophy. He recounts his discovery of the limitations of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism, his turn to [[jean-baptiste-lamarck]], and his decision to write [[back-to-methuselah]] as a dramatic parable of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. He situates himself explicitly in the [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian tradition: "I was a Neo-[[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian" (P01-021).

## Role in the Play

Shaw does not appear as a dramatic character in the cycle, but his authorial presence is felt on every page. The Preface is a direct address to the reader, and Shaw refers frequently to his own experience: "In my own activities as a playwright I found this state of things intolerable" (P01-100). He describes the decision to write the play: "Accordingly, in 1901, I took the legend of Don Juan in its [[mozart|Mozart]]ian form and made it a dramatic parable of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]" (P01-101).

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

Shaw diagnoses the problem of modern drama: "The fashionable theatre prescribed one serious subject: clandestine adultery: the dullest of all subjects for a serious author" (P01-100).

He defines his ambition: to give the theatre what [[william-shakespeare]] lacked — a coherent philosophical purpose grounded in the [[free-will|will]] to evolve.

On the intellectual climate: "[[eve|Eve]]ryone who had a mind to change, changed it. Only [[samuel-butler|Samuel Butler]], on whom [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] had acted homeopathically, reacted against him furiously; ran up the [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian flag to the topgallant peak" (P01-051).

## Relationships

- [[jean-baptiste-lamarck]]: Shaw's scientific inspiration; he identified as a Neo-[[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian
- [[william-shakespeare]]: The dramatic genius Shaw admired but sought to surpass in philosophical depth
- [[samuel-butler]]: Fellow [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian who ran up "the [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian flag" against [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] (P01-051)
- [[creative-evolution]]: Shaw's own philosophical doctrine, the core argument of the play
- [[back-to-methuselah]]: Shaw's magnum opus on evolutionary philosophy
- [[fabian-society]]: The socialist organization Shaw helped found and led

## Themes

Shaw synthesized [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian biology, Schopenhauerian metaphysics, and Fabian socialism into a unified philosophy of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. His central conviction was that human [[free-will|will]], properly directed, could drive evolutionary progress — and that the theatre was the ideal vehicle for communicating this vision. The Preface to [[back-to-methuselah]] is simultaneously a work of popular science, philosophical polemic, and theatrical manifesto.

## Real-World References

George Bernard Shaw (1856–1950) was born in [[dublin|Dublin]] and moved to [[london|London]] in 1876. He became a leading figure in the [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]], a music and drama critic, and the author of more than 60 plays. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1925. His major works include *[[man-and-superman|Man and Superman]]* (1903), *Major Barbara* (1905), *[[pygmalion|Pygmalion]]* (1913), *Heartbreak House* (1919), and *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* (1921). He is the only person to have won both a Nobel Prize and an Academy Award (for the screenplay of *[[pygmalion|Pygmalion]]*, 1938).
