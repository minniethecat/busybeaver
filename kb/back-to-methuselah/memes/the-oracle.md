# The Oracle

The Priestess of the [[temple-of-the-oracle|Temple of the Oracle]] in Part IV of *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*. As a [[longevity|long-lived]] being with the power to conjure figures from the short-lived past, the Oracle serves as a guide to the transformed world of 3000 AD and as a dramatic device for measuring the distance [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] has traveled.

## Dramatis Personae

The Oracle is one of the most enigmatic figures in the play. She possesses powers that seem supernatural — the ability to summon the dead, or at least their simulacra — but [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] presents these powers as the natural consequence of extended life and heightened consciousness. She is what human beings become when they have lived long enough.

## Role in the Play

### Part IV: Tragedy of an Elderly Gentleman

The Oracle serves as the dramatic mediator between the world of the short-lived and the world of the [[longevity|long-lived]]. When the [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]] visits her temple, she conjures figures from history — [[napoleon|Napoleon]], among others — to demonstrate the obsolescence of the short-lived perspective.

Her manner is detached, almost amused — she regards the short-lived with the gentle condescension of a being who has seen centuries pass. But there is no cruelty in her detachment; it is simply the perspective of one who has outgrown the urgencies that define a normal human life.

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

The Oracle's conjurations are the dramatic high points of Part IV.

## Relationships

- [[napoleon]] — One of the historical figures she conjures
- [[the-elderly-gentleman]] — The short-lived visitor she guides through the transformed world

## Themes

The Oracle embodies the cognitive and spiritual transformation that [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] imagines as the fruit of extended life. She is not merely old — she is qualitatively different, a being whose consciousness has developed capacities that short-lived humans cannot access.
