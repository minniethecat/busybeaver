# Timeline of Creative Evolution

This timeline traces the intellectual lineage of the [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] idea as it develops through the 19th and early 20th centuries, culminating in [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s [[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]] (1921).

## The Darwinian Revolution (1859–1880s)

The story begins with [[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]]'s [[origin-of-species|On the Origin of Species]] (1859), which established natural selection as the mechanism of evolutionary change. Darwin's theory transformed biology but also created a philosophical problem: if evolution proceeds by blind variation and selective retention, where is the purpose, the direction, the meaning?

Shaw's critique of [[darwinian|Darwinian]] orthodoxy would become the driving force behind his own evolutionary vision. He saw Darwinism as a philosophy of accident — ''circumstantial selection'', as he called it — that denied the creative impulse in living things.

See: [[natural-selection|Natural Selection]], [[darwinian|Darwinian]], [[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]].

## Lamarck's Shadow (1809–1900s)

[[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Jean-Baptiste Lamarck]] had proposed a very different evolutionary mechanism decades before Darwin: the inheritance of acquired characteristics. An organism's own efforts, its striving, its will — these could shape its descendants. Lamarck's vision was of evolution driven from within, not shaped from without.

Shaw embraced [[lamarckism|Lamarckism]] as the biological correlative of his own belief in human agency. Where Darwin saw organisms passively shaped by environment, Lamarck saw organisms actively shaping themselves. This was the biology Shaw needed for his philosophical commitment to the [[life-force|Life Force]].

See: [[lamarckism|Lamarckism]], [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Jean-Baptiste Lamarck]], [[vitalism|Vitalism]].

## Samuel Butler's Revolt (1870s–1902)

[[samuel-butler|Samuel Butler]] was the crucial bridge figure. A Victorian novelist and essayist, Butler read Darwin and recoiled — not from the science, but from the philosophy. He saw that Darwin had banished mind from nature, reducing life to mechanism. Butler's books ''Life and Habit'' (1878) and ''Evolution, Old and New'' (1879) argued that evolution was driven by the accumulated habits and memories of organisms — a form of Lamarckism updated with a theory of unconscious memory.

Butler is the direct intellectual ancestor of Shaw's evolutionary thinking. Shaw acknowledged this debt explicitly in the Preface to [[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]].

See: [[samuel-butler|Samuel Butler]], [[homeopathic-education|Homeopathic Education]].

## The Neo-Darwinian Synthesis (1900s–1920s)

While Shaw was developing his Creative Evolution philosophy, mainstream biology was moving in the opposite direction. August Weismann's doctrine of the isolation of the germ-plasm (reported as the experiments of [[august-weismann|Weismann]]) severed any Lamarckian link between organism and heredity. The rediscovery of Mendel's laws and the rise of population genetics produced the [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinian]] synthesis — a theory even more mechanistically rigid than Darwin's original.

Shaw saw this as scientific retrogression: a narrowing of biology to laboratory genetics that ignored the obvious facts of will, purpose, and creativity in living things. His Preface is, in large part, a polemic against [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]].

See: [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]], [[mechanism|Mechanism]], [[august-weismann|August Weismann]].

## Shaw's Synthesis: Back to Methuselah (1921)

Shaw's [[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]] was the artistic culmination of this intellectual trajectory. The play cycle dramatizes the Lamarckian-Butlerian theory of Creative Evolution across 33,000 years of imagined human history. The [[life-force|Life Force]] — Shaw's name for the purposive drive in evolution — learns through trial and error, creating ever more complex and conscious forms.

The play's central argument is simple: if Lamarck was right, then longevity is the key to human progress. Only by living long enough to accumulate wisdom and shed our childish impulses can humanity fulfill its evolutionary potential.
