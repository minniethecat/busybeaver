# Abel

The second son of [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]], murdered by his brother [[cain|Cain]]. Abel is the first victim of the violence that mortality enables.

His brief existence and violent end demonstrate what is at stake in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s evolutionary vision.

## Dramatis Personae

Abel appears only briefly in the play, but his murder is the dramatic hinge on which Part I turns. [[cain|Cain]]'s violence proves that mortality has consequences beyond the biological — it introduces killing into the human repertoire.

## Role in the Play

### Part I: In the Beginning

Abel's death at [[cain|Cain]]'s hands is the first demonstration that the creative impulse, when combined with mortality and competition, can become destructive. The [[life-force|Life Force]]'s experiment has produced not only birth but murder.

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

Abel's voice is heard only briefly before his death, but his absence echoes through the rest of the play.

## Themes

Abel represents the innocent cost of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] — the [[evil|suffering]] that occurs not through malice but through the trial-and-error process of a [[life-force|Life Force]] that learns as it goes.
