# Lamarckism

The evolutionary theory associated with [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Jean-Baptiste Lamarck]] (1744–1829), which [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] embraced and defended throughout his career. Lamarckism holds that organisms can pass on characteristics acquired through use, effort, and [[free-will|will]] — a view that allowed [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] to argue that evolution has purpose and direction, unlike the random, purposeless process described by [[darwinian|Darwinian]] natural selection.

## Definition

Lamarckism posits two main principles: (1) the use and disuse of organs — structures that are actively used become stronger and more developed, while those not used atrophy; and (2) the inheritance of acquired characteristics — changes acquired during an organism's lifetime can be transmitted to offspring. The classic example is the giraffe: by stretching to reach high leaves, each generation slightly elongates its neck, and this increment is passed down.

For [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], Lamarckism's crucial feature is that it makes *[[free-will|will]]* and *effort* into evolutionary forces. Organisms are not passive recipients of random mutations; they actively shape their own evolutionary destiny through striving.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] devotes considerable attention to [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]], treating him as a heroic figure in the history of evolutionary thought. He introduces [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]] dramatically: "after Waterloo, a French soldier named [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]" (P01-020) — the military context emphasizing that [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]] was a man of action as well as intellect.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] explicitly directs his reader's attention: "I call all your special attention to [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]], because later on there were unreconcilable differences of opinion as to his place in the history of science" (P01-021). He presents the [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian process as capable of producing "the most extravagant forms of organized life on [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian lines without the intervention of [[circumstantial-selection|Circumstantial Selection]]" (P01-023).

Most emphatically, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] insists: "Let us fix the Lamarckian evolutionary process well in our minds" (P01-024). This is not a casual aside — it is a deliberate instruction to the reader, signaling that Lamarckism is the scientific foundation upon which [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] rests.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] notes that Buffon "was the metaphysical complement to [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]'s natural history" (P01-032), situating [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]] within a broader tradition of French evolutionary thought that predated and (in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s view) surpassed [[charles-darwin|Darwin]].

### In the Play

The dramatic argument for Lamarckism is embodied in the entire trajectory of the play. The [[longevity|long-lived]] characters have *striven* to live longer — they have [[free-will|will]]ed it, worked for it, and passed the capacity on. This is Lamarckism dramatized: effort creates capacity, and capacity is inherited.

## Intellectual Context

Lamarckism has a complex history in biology:

- **[[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Jean-Baptiste Lamarck]]**: A French naturalist who served in the army before turning to science. His *[[philosophie-zoologique|Philosophie Zoologique]]* (1809) presented the first comprehensive theory of evolution, predating [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] by half a century.

- **Weismann's challenge**: August Weismann's germ plasm theory (1892) argued that the germ line is isolated from the body, making the inheritance of acquired characteristics impossible. Weismann famously cut off the tails of mice for twenty generations — and the offspring still had tails.

- **Lysenkoism**: In the Soviet Union, Trofim Lysenko promoted a version of Lamarckism as the officially sanctioned biology, suppressing Mendelian genetics. This political association damaged Lamarckism's scientific credibility, though [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s advocacy predates the Lysenko controversy.

- **Epigenetics (modern revival)**: Contemporary biology has discovered mechanisms — DNA methylation, histone modification, small RNA — by which acquired characteristics can influence gene expression across generations. While not a full vindication of classical Lamarckism, epigenetics has reopened questions that [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]] had declared settled.

## Dramatic Expression

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Lamarckism is most vividly dramatized in the development of the [[longevity|long-lived]] characters. In Part IV, a character describes how "the impulse that produces evolution is creative" — [[free-will|will]] and effort, passed down across generations, produce beings of [[immortality|extended lifespan]] and heightened consciousness. The Ancients of Part V represent the [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian endpoint: beings who have [[free-will|will]]ed themselves beyond bodily limitation entirely.
