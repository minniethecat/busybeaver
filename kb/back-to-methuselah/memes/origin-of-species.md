# On the Origin of Species

[[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]]'s *On the Origin of Species* (1859) — the book that transformed biology and whose philosophical implications [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] spent much of his career contesting. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s attitude toward the *Origin* is nuanced: he respects [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s scientific achievement while rejecting the philosophical interpretation placed upon it by the [[darwinian|Darwinians]].

## Shaw's Reading

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] distinguishes carefully between [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] and the [[darwinian|Darwinians]]. The *Origin* is a great work of science; what came after it — the elimination of purpose and [[free-will|will]] from the evolutionary process — is the error of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s followers, not (entirely) of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] himself.

## Relationship to Back to Methuselah

The play is, in one sense, a dramatic response to the *Origin* — an attempt to restore to evolution the creative, purposive dimension that [[darwinian|Darwinian]] theory (in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s view) had eliminated. Where the *Origin* described a world of passive adaptation, *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* dramatizes a world of active creative [[free-will|will]].
