# Richard Wagner

The German composer [[richard-wagner|Richard Wagner]] (1813–1883) appears in [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s preface to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* in two distinct contexts: as an example of how historical reputation exaggerates originality, and as a positive exemplar of what Shaw calls "Vitalist art."

## Role in Shaw's Argument

### The Illusion of Novelty

Shaw's first mention of Wagner is characteristically caustic. In a passage mocking the historical amnesia of the nineteenth century, he writes:

> People talked as if there had been no dramatic or descriptive music before Wagner; no impressionist painting before Whistler. (P01-004)

Wagner is grouped with Whistler and [[nietzsche|Nietzsche]] as figures whose admirers treated them as unprecedented innovators, when in reality — Shaw argues — they were working within long-established traditions. The point is not to diminish Wagner's achievement but to critique the cultural fashion that mistakes prominence for originality.

Shaw follows this with a self-deprecating observation about his own work: "I was finding that the surest way to produce an effect of daring innovation and originality was to revive the ancient attraction of long rhetorical speeches; to stick closely to the methods of Molière; and to lift characters bodily out of the pages of Charles Dickens" (P01-004).

### Vitalist Art

Shaw's second and more substantive reference to Wagner places him in the highest company:

> Compare Mozart's Magic Flute, Beethoven's Ninth Symphony, Wagner's Ring, all of them reachings-forward to the new Vitalist art, with the dreary pseudo-sacred oratorios and cantatas which were produced for no better reason than that Handel had formerly made splendid thunder in that way. (P01-094)

Here Wagner is not a target of irony but a positive exemplar. Shaw places the *Ring* cycle alongside [[mozart|Mozart]]'s *Magic Flute* and Beethoven's Ninth Symphony as works of "Vitalist art" — creative expressions that reach forward toward higher evolutionary expression.

The term "Vitalist art" is Shaw's own coinage, connecting Wagner's music to the philosophical tradition of [[vitalism|Vitalism]] and, by extension, to [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. For Shaw, great art is not merely beautiful — it is an expression of the [[life-force|Life Force]] striving toward greater complexity and consciousness.

## Significance for Creative Evolution

Wagner's importance to Shaw's argument lies in the concept of "Vitalist art." Shaw uses Wagner (alongside Mozart and Beethoven) to demonstrate that creative genius is not an accident or a random variation — it is the [[life-force|Life Force]] expressing itself through human creativity. The *Ring* cycle, in this reading, is not merely great music but an evolutionary act: humanity reaching beyond its present limits toward what [[nietzsche|Nietzsche]] would call the Superman and Shaw would call the goal of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].
