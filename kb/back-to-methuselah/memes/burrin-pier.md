# Burrin Pier

**Burrin Pier** is the opening setting of Part IV of [[Back to Methuselah]], "Tragedy of an [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]]." Located on the south shore of [[galway|Galway]] Bay in [[ireland|Ireland]], it is the point of entry for short-lived visitors to the [[longevity|long-lived]] colony — and the place where the [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]] first confronts the existential gulf between his kind and the future race.

## Location

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] describes it as "Burrin pier on the south shore of [[galway|Galway]] Bay in [[ireland|Ireland]], a region of stone-capped hills and granite fields" (005-001). The pier has "an ancient stone stump, about three feet thick and three feet high, used for securing ships by ropes to the shore, and called a bollard or holdfast" — a detail that anchors the scene in the material reality of a working pier.

### Real Location

Burrin (or Burren) is a real location on the south shore of [[galway|Galway]] Bay, part of the Burren region of County Clare — a stark, limestone landscape of unusual geological and botanical richness. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s description of "stone-capped hills and granite fields" matches the distinctive karst terrain of the area. In choosing this remote, ancient-feeling landscape for the entry point to the [[longevity|long-lived]] colony, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] emphasizes the separation between the short-lived world and the evolutionary future.

## Role in the Play

### Part IV: Tragedy of an Elderly Gentleman

The pier is the first location seen in Part IV (AD 3000). [[the-elderly-gentleman|The Elderly Gentleman]] sits on the bollard "with his head bowed and his face in his hands, sobbing" (005-001) — an image of utter desolation that immediately establishes the pathos of the short-lived condition.

The pier's function as a literal threshold — the boundary between sea and land, between the short-lived empire and the [[longevity|long-lived]] colony — makes it the perfect setting for the play's most sustained encounter between the two orders of humanity. From the pier, Zoo leads the [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]] toward the [[temple-of-the-oracle|Temple of the Oracle]], and toward his death from [[discouragement|discouragement]].

The working pier, with its "three or four full sacks" suggesting commerce, reminds us that the [[longevity|long-lived]] have not abandoned practical life — they have simply transcended the short-lived's anxious relationship to it.

## Significance

Burrin Pier represents the **liminal space** between two orders of human existence. It is neither fully part of the short-lived world ([[baghdad|Baghdad]], the capital the [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]] comes from) nor fully part of the [[longevity|long-lived]] world (the interior of [[ireland|Ireland]] and the Temple). It is the shore where the old humanity washes up and confronts what it has failed to become.

The pier's rugged, ancient character reinforces the theme of geological time that runs through Part V. The bollard — "ancient," "three feet thick" — has weathered centuries. It is permanent in a way that the sobbing [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]], in his Victorian finery, is not.
