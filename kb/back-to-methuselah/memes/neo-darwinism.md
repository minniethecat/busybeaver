# Neo-Darwinism

The modern synthesis of [[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]]'s theory of natural selection with Gregor Mendel's laws of genetic inheritance, which became the dominant evolutionary paradigm in the early twentieth century. [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] made Neo-Darwinism the primary intellectual antagonist of his philosophical system, attacking it with characteristic polemical force throughout the Preface to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*.

## Definition

Neo-Darwinism is the evolutionary theory that combines [[darwinian|Darwinian]] natural selection — the differential survival and reproduction of organisms based on heritable traits — with Mendelian particulate inheritance (genes). In this framework, evolution proceeds through random genetic mutations filtered by environmental selection, without any inherent direction, purpose, or creative impulse. It is this last point — the elimination of [[free-will|will]] and purpose from the evolutionary process — that made Neo-Darwinism intolerable to [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]].

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s attack on Neo-Darwinism is relentless and personal. He "habitually derided Neo-Darwinism as a ghastly idiocy" (P01-006) and traces its pernicious effects from biology into [[political-science|politics]] and ethics.

He makes a direct connection between Neo-[[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ist thinking and the catastrophe of the First World War: "Neo-Darwinism in [[political-science|politics]] had produced a [[europe|Europe]]an war" (P01-007). The logic is that if nature is merely a struggle in which the fittest survive, then nations are justified in competing ruthlessly — a "sound Neo-Darwinism" (P01-008) that leads to mass slaughter.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] particularly objects to the fatalism implicit in Neo-Darwinism: it "pointed out that death is not really the inevitable result of being unfit, but the essential condition of further progress" (P01-017). Against this glorification of death, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] offers [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]], in which life extends and consciousness expands.

He saw the intellectual tide turning in his favor: "Nowadays, when we are turning in weary disgust and disillusion from Neo-Darwinism and Mechanism to [[vitalism|Vitalism]] and [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]], it is difficult to imagine how this new departure of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s could possibly have appealed to his contemporaries" (P01-035).

### In the Play

The dramatic refutation of Neo-Darwinism is the Barnabas brothers' project: they seek to prove, through the practical extension of human life, that [[free-will|will]] and intelligence — not random mutation plus selection — are the drivers of human development. Part III (*The Thing Happens*) shows the first fruits of this refutation when the political leader Burge-[[lubin|Lubin]] learns that the Archbishop of York has lived well past the normal human span.

## Intellectual Context

Neo-Darwinism emerged from several converging developments:

- **August Weismann's germ plasm theory**: Weismann argued that the germ line (reproductive cells) is isolated from the soma (body cells), making the [[lamarckism|inheritance of acquired characteristics]] impossible — a direct challenge to [[lamarckism|Lamarckism]] and to [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s belief in [[free-will|will]]-driven evolution.

- **Mendelian genetics**: The rediscovery of Gregor Mendel's work on particulate inheritance around 1900 provided the mechanism of heredity that [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s original theory had lacked. The synthesis of Mendel and [[charles-darwin|Darwin]], associated with figures like R.A. Fisher, J.B.S. Haldane, and Sewall Wright, formalized Neo-Darwinism as a rigorous quantitative science.

- **Social [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism**: The application of "survival of the fittest" to human society, which [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] saw as Neo-Darwinism's most dangerous political consequence. The First World War was, in his view, the logical outcome of treating nations as competing organisms in a struggle for existence.

## Dramatic Expression

The play's structure is a deliberate dramatic counter-proof to Neo-Darwinism. If Neo-Darwinism were true, human life would have a fixed biological limit determined by random mutation and environmental selection. By showing human beings extending their lifespan through [[free-will|will]] and intelligence, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] dramatizes the failure of the Neo-[[darwinian|Darwinian]] framework. The [[longevity|long-lived]] characters of Parts IV and V are not "fitter" in any [[darwinian|Darwinian]] sense — they are more *[[free-will|will]]ed*, more *conscious*, more *purposeful*.
