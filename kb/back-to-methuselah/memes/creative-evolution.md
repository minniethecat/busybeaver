# Creative Evolution

The central philosophical doctrine of [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*: the thesis that evolution is driven by a purposeful creative impulse — the [[life-force|Life Force]] — rather than by random variation and natural selection alone. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] developed this idea in reaction to [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]], which he considered "a ghastly idiocy" that reduced life to mechanistic accident.

## Definition

Creative Evolution is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s name for the process by which the [[life-force|Life Force]] — an immanent, purposive drive — continually strives through trial and error to produce ever higher forms of life, culminating (but not ending) in the emergence of human intelligence and [[free-will|will]]. Unlike [[darwinian|Darwinian]] natural selection, which is passive and retrospective, Creative Evolution is active and forward-looking: "the impulse that produces evolution is creative" (P01-016). It is not omnipotent; it "must meanwhile struggle with matter and circumstance by the method of trial and error" (P01-067), and the world is consequently full of its unsuccessful experiments.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] uses the Preface ("The Infidel Half Century") to trace the intellectual history leading to Creative Evolution, distinguishing it sharply from [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s [[circumstantial-selection|Circumstantial Selection]] and situating it as the culmination of a tradition running from Buffon and [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]] through [[samuel-butler|Samuel Butler]] to the twentieth century.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] warns that "some are so entangled in the current confusion of Creative Evolution with [[circumstantial-selection|Circumstantial Selection]] by their historical ignorance that they are puzzled by any distinction between the two" (P01-018). He presents Creative Evolution as "the genuinely [[religion|scientific religion]] for which all wise men are now anxiously looking" (P01-018).

He traces the modern turn away from [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]]: "Nowadays, when we are turning in weary disgust and disillusion from [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]] and Mechanism to [[vitalism|Vitalism]] and Creative Evolution, it is difficult to imagine how this new departure of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s could possibly have appealed to his contemporaries" (P01-035).

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] directly addresses the problem of evil through Creative Evolution's lens: "the problem of evil yields very easily to Creative Evolution" (P01-067). If the [[life-force|Life Force]] works by trial and error, [[evil|suffering]] is not malice but the cost of experimentation.

### In the Play

In Part I, [[the-serpent|the Serpent]] in the [[garden-of-eden|Garden of Eden]] first articulates the creative impulse: the drive to conceive, to make, to become — the primal form of the [[life-force|Life Force]]. The opening lines dramatize the moment when mere biological existence gives way to creative [[free-will|will]].

In Part III (*The Thing Happens*), the biologist [[franklyn-barnabas|Franklyn Barnabas]] explains that all serious thought has been "converging rapidly upon Creative Evolution" (003-014). The Barnabas brothers serve as the intellectual mouthpieces for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s doctrine, arguing that human [[immortality|longevity]] is the next logical step the [[life-force|Life Force]] requires.

In Part IV (*Tragedy of an [[the-elderly-gentleman|Elderly Gentleman]]*), the [[longevity|long-lived]] characters embody the fruits of Creative Evolution — humans who have lived for centuries and developed new capacities. [[the-oracle|The Oracle]] and [[the-he-ancient|the He-Ancient]] exemplify what the [[life-force|Life Force]] can achieve when lifespan extends beyond the "three score and ten" limit.

In Part V (*As Far as Thought Can Reach*), the ultimate destination of Creative Evolution is shown: beings for whom the body is merely an "automaton" and the mind is freed into pure contemplation. The [[life-force|Life Force]] has carried humanity from protoplasm to disembodied intelligence.

## Key Passages

> Creative Evolution is already a religion, and is indeed now unmistakeably the religion of the twentieth century, newly arisen from the ashes of pseudo-Christianity, of mere scepticism, and of the soulless affirmations and blind negations of the Mechanists and Neo-Darwinians. (P01-093)

> Accordingly, in 1901, I took the legend of Don Juan in its Mozartian form and made it a dramatic parable of Creative Evolution. But being then at the height of my invention and comedic talent, I decorated it too brilliantly and lavishly. (P01-101)

> I now find myself inspired to make a second legend of Creative Evolution without distractions and embellishments. My sands are running out; the exuberance of 1901 has aged into the garrulity of 1930; and the war has been a stern intimation that the matter is not one to be trifled with. (P01-102)

> It is true that mere survival of the fittest in the struggle for existence plus sexual selection fail as hopelessly to account for Darwin's own life work as for my conquest of the bicycle; but who can prove that there are not other and more scientific factors in the problem? (P01-054)

## Intellectual Context

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Creative Evolution synthesizes several intellectual currents:

- **[[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian inheritance**: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] championed [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Jean-Baptiste Lamarck]]'s view that organisms can pass on characteristics acquired through effort and [[free-will|will]] — the "[[lamarckism|Lamarckian evolution]]ary process" producing "the most extravagant forms of organized life on [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian lines without the intervention of [[circumstantial-selection|Circumstantial Selection]]" (P01-023). He explicitly directs his reader: "Let us fix the [[lamarckism|Lamarckian evolution]]ary process well in our minds" (P01-024).

- **[[samuel-butler|Samuel Butler]]'s critique of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]**: [[samuel-butler|Butler]] argued that [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s mechanism of natural selection eliminated mind and purpose from the universe. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] adopted [[samuel-butler|Butler]]'s position that evolution must be driven by something more than accident.

- **Henri Bergson's [[vitalism|élan vital]]**: The French philosopher's concept of a [[life-force|vital impulse]] animating all life parallels [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s [[life-force|Life Force]], though [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] developed his views independently through dramatic work.

- **[[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]] as opponent**: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] defined Creative Evolution in opposition to "[[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]]" — the synthesis of [[darwinian|Darwinian]] natural selection with Mendelian genetics — which he considered a "ghastly idiocy" (P01-006) that reduced life to mechanical accident.

### Related Thinkers

- [[jean-baptiste-lamarck]] — French naturalist whose theory of acquired characteristics [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] embraced
- [[samuel-butler]] — Victorian author whose anti-[[darwinian|Darwinian]] writings influenced [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]
- [[charles-darwin]] — Naturalist whose theory [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] sought to supplement, not reject

### Contrasting Concepts

- [[circumstantial-selection]] — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s term for [[darwinian|Darwinian]] natural selection, which he viewed as passive and insufficient
- [[neo-darwinism]] — The modern synthesis [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] polemically opposed as "mechanistic"

## Dramatic Expression

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s dramatic method for expressing Creative Evolution is the parable. He acknowledges his first attempt in *[[man-and-superman|Man and Superman]]* (1901), where the Don Juan in Hell dream sequence "made it a dramatic parable of Creative Evolution" (P01-101). Dissatisfied with its embedding in a social comedy, he wrote *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* as "a second legend of Creative Evolution without distractions and embellishments" (P01-102).

The five-play cycle traces the [[life-force|Life Force]]'s arc: from the biological awakening in the [[garden-of-eden|Garden of Eden]] (Part I), through the intellectual discovery of the doctrine by twentieth-century thinkers (Part II–III), to the practical realization of extended life (Part IV), and finally to the transfiguration of humanity into beings of pure mind (Part V). The opening stage direction of Part I invokes [[garden-of-eden|Eden]] as origin-point, and each subsequent Part takes a leap forward in time, dramatizing the consequence of the [[life-force|Life Force]]'s incremental victories over mortality.

The comic tone throughout — even at the most portentous moments — is deliberate: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] believed that the religion of Creative Evolution needed "legends, parables, miracles" (P01-093) that Cabinet Ministers as well as villagers could apprehend.
