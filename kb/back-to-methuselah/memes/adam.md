# Adam

The first man in [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s dramatic reimagining of the [[garden-of-eden|Genesis]] story. In [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s version, Adam is not the noble patriarch of tradition but a cautious, somewhat plodding materialist — a man content with mere existence until [[eve|Eve]] and [[the-serpent|The Serpent]] persuade him that life without creation is not worth living.

His transformation from passive contentment to active participation in [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] is the dramatic engine of Part I of *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*.

## Dramatis Personae

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Adam is introduced as a creature of limited imagination who finds the [[garden-of-eden|Garden of Eden]] perfectly satisfactory. He is the voice of comfortable stasis, the original conservative — happy to eat, sleep, and tend the garden, asking nothing more of existence.

> Adam is digging in the middle of the garden. On his right, Eve sits on a stool in the shadow of a tree by the doorway, spinning flax. (002-001)

This stage picture — Adam working the earth, [[eve|Eve]] spinning — establishes their contrasting natures immediately: Adam is earthbound and practical, [[eve|Eve]] is patient and creative.

## Role in the Play

### Part I: In the Beginning

Adam's role in the opening act is to resist — and ultimately accept — [[the-serpent|the Serpent]]'s revelation. When [[the-serpent|the Serpent]] suggests to [[eve|Eve]] that birth is possible, Adam reacts with alarm, not enthusiasm. He is the voice of caution: life as it is seems good enough.

His most characteristic moment comes when he confronts the implications of [[the-serpent|the Serpent]]'s teaching:

> Adam! Eve! Eve's Voice! What is it, Adam? Adam! Come here. Quick. Something has happened. (001-003)

Adam's alarm is comic but genuine — he senses that his comfortable world is about to change forever.

### Across the Cycle

Adam, like [[eve|Eve]], is referenced across the full span of the Pentateuch:

| Part | Adam's Presence | Significance |
|------|----------------|-------------|
| Part I (Acts I–II) | Direct character | The reluctant pioneer: resistance to creative risk |
| Part II | Referenced by [[lubin|Lubin]] and Barnabas | Invoked as the original conservative whose inertia [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] must overcome (003-014) |

With 16 paragraph mentions, Adam is the second most-referenced person in the corpus, after [[eve|Eve]].

### Dramatic Function

Adam represents the inertial force that [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] must overcome. He is not evil — he is simply satisfied. His contentment with mere existence is the obstacle the [[life-force|Life Force]] must surmount to begin its creative work.

When he finally accepts the burden of mortality and creativity, human history begins.

In this sense, Adam is [[eve|Eve]]ryman — the voice of common-sense resistance to the difficult demands of creative life, which [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] identified with the [[darwinian|Darwinian]] worldview of passive acceptance.

## Relationships

- [[eve|Eve]] — His partner, who is bolder and more intellectually curious than he is; she leads, he follows
- [[the-serpent|The Serpent]] — The tempter who disrupts Adam's contented existence with the possibility of creative life
- [[cain|Cain]] — His firstborn son, who embodies the violent consequences of mortality

## Themes

Adam embodies the tension between stasis and creativity that is central to [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s philosophy. He is the original conservative — happy with things as they are — and his reluctant transformation is the first victory of the [[life-force|Life Force]] over mere satisfaction.

He also represents [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s critique of a certain kind of masculine limitation: Adam is practical, earthbound, and unimaginative, while [[eve|Eve]] is the true intellectual pioneer. This inversion of the Biblical hierarchy is one of [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s most pointed feminist gestures.
