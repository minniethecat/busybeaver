# Atheism

[[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s relationship to atheism is complex and revealing. He was himself an unbeliever in traditional Christianity, yet he titled his Preface "The Infidel Half Century" and argued that mere unbelief is as inadequate as the superstitions it replaces. Atheism, for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], is a transitional stage — necessary but insufficient — that must give way to the positive faith of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].

## Definition

Atheism, in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s usage, refers primarily to the rejection of traditional Christian theism — the God of miracles, divine intervention, and supernatural revelation. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] shared this rejection but argued that discarding God without replacing Him with something equally compelling leaves a spiritual vacuum that is filled by worse things: "mere scepticism," mechanistic materialism, and political fatalism.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] describes his own journey through unbelief. As a young man, he "had discarded the religion of my forefathers" (P01-003) — he was, by the standards of his time, an atheist. But he came to see this as a halfway position.

The Preface's title — "The Infidel Half Century" — is retrospective and critical. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] is naming the period of his own intellectual formation (roughly 1859–1914) as a time when disbelief was necessary but not sufficient. The infidels had cleared away the rubble of superstition but had built nothing in its place.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s positive case is that [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] fills the void atheism leaves:

> [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] is already a religion, and is indeed now unmistakeably the religion of the twentieth century, newly arisen from the ashes of pseudo-Christianity, of mere scepticism, and of the soulless affirmations and blind negations of the Mechanists and Neo-[[darwinian|Darwinians]] (P01-093)

Atheism — "mere scepticism" — is one of the ashes from which the new religion rises.

### In the Play

The Barnabas brothers represent the post-atheist position. They are not religious in any conventional sense — they are scientists. But their science has become a faith, and their laboratory is a kind of church. They have moved beyond mere unbelief to active, creative affirmation.

## Intellectual Context

Atheism in the Victorian and Edwardian periods took several forms:

- **Scientific naturalism**: Figures like T.H. Huxley ("[[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s Bulldog") promoted agnosticism as the intellectually honest position — we simply do not know whether God exists. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] moved beyond this to a positive evolutionary faith.

- **Secularism**: Charles Bradlaugh and the National Secular Society campaigned for the separation of church and state and the rights of unbelievers. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] sympathized with the [[political-science|politics]] but not the philosophy.

- **Nietzsche's "God is dead"**: Friedrich Nietzsche's proclamation that the death of God would lead to nihilism unless humanity created new values. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] is, in effect, his answer to the Nietzschean challenge.

## Dramatic Expression

None of [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s major characters are atheists in the simple sense — even the sceptical [[lubin|Lubin]] and Burge-[[lubin|Lubin]] are too worldly to be metaphysical unbelievers. The play's argument is not against belief but for a better object of belief: [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] rather than the "Nobodaddy" of traditional theism.
