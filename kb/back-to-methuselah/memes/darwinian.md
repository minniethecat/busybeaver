# Darwinian

A term [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]] uses with deliberate precision to distinguish the followers of [[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]] from [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] himself. In [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s analysis, the Darwinians — not [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] — are responsible for the mechanistic, purposeless interpretation of evolution that he attacks throughout the Preface to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*.

This distinction allows [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] to honor [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s achievement while rejecting [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism as a philosophy. With 23 paragraph mentions, "Darwinian" is the most frequently cited concept in the corpus.

## Definition

A "Darwinian," in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s lexicon, is an adherent of the school of thought that interprets [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s theory of natural selection as the complete and sufficient explanation of evolution — eliminating purpose, [[free-will|will]], and creative intelligence from the evolutionary process.

> The conflict between the view of Evolution taken by the Darwinians (though not altogether by Darwin himself) and called Natural Selection, and that which is emerging, under the title of Creative Evolution. (P01-018)

The parenthetical qualification "though not altogether by [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] himself" is the key to [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s entire rhetorical strategy: separate the master from his disciples, then demolish the disciples.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s treatment of the Darwinians is strategic and nuanced. He traces how [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism swept through Victorian intellectual life:

> The world was drowned by the flowing tide of Darwinism. (P01-051)

The Darwinians — figures like T.H. Huxley, Herbert Spencer, and the Neo-Darwinians of the early twentieth century — turned [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s cautious scientific hypothesis into a comprehensive worldview that excluded everything [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] valued: [[free-will|will]], purpose, creativity, and moral responsibility.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s attack on the Darwinians is not merely intellectual but moral. Their philosophy, he argues, produced political catastrophe:

> Neo-Darwinism in politics had produced a European war. (P01-007)

This claim — linking the intellectual error of mechanizing life to the practical horror of mechanized slaughter in [[europe|Europe]] — is the most rhetorically charged passage in the entire Preface.

### In the Play

The Barnabas brothers represent the anti-Darwinian position in its most constructive form. They do not deny [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s facts — they deny the Darwinian interpretation of those facts. Evolution happens, but not merely through the passive filtering of random variations. It happens through [[free-will|will]], intelligence, and the creative impulse that Darwinian theory cannot account for.

### Key Distinctions

| Term | [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s View | Period |
|------|------------|--------|
| [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] | Cautious naturalist, not responsible for [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism | 1859–1882 |
| Darwinians | Mechanistic interpreters who eliminated purpose from evolution | 1860s–1900 |
| Neo-Darwinians | Mathematical synthesis, "practically running current Evolutionism" (P01-006) | 1900–1920s |
| [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]ists | [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s preferred school — evolution has purpose and direction | 1921– |

## Intellectual Context

The Darwinians [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] opposed included:

- **T.H. Huxley**: "[[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s Bulldog," whose agnosticism represented the worldview [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] found inadequate
- **Herbert Spencer**: Coined "survival of the fittest"; applied evolutionary thinking to society
- **Neo-Darwinians**: Twentieth-century synthesis associated with Fisher and Haldane
- **[[charles-darwin|Darwin]] himself**: More cautious than his followers; never claimed natural selection was the sole mechanism

## Dramatic Expression

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s dramatic method is to show what the Darwinians leave out: the creative impulse that drives [[the-serpent|the Serpent]] to speak, [[eve|Eve]] to conceive, the Barnabas brothers to dedicate their lives to science, and the Ancients to transcend bodily form. These are not random variations — they are acts of [[free-will|will]].

The play *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* is a five-act dramatic argument that the Darwinian account of evolution is incomplete. Where [[circumstantial-selection|Circumstantial Selection]] sees only passive filtering, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] sees [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] — purposeful, [[free-will|will]]ed, and directional.
