# Man and Superman

[[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s 1903 play that first gave dramatic form to [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. It remains a landmark of twentieth-century theatre, though [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] himself came to regard it as a partial failure — the philosophy was there, but buried under too much brilliance.

## Shaw's Assessment

In the Preface to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] describes the 1901 composition of *Man and Superman* with characteristic self-criticism.

> Accordingly, in 1901, I took the legend of Don Juan in its Mozartian form and made it a dramatic parable of Creative Evolution. But being then at the height of my invention and comedic talent, I decorated it too brilliantly and lavishly. (P01-101)

The Don Juan in Hell dream sequence — often performed independently — contains the philosophical core. But [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] came to feel that its embedding in a social comedy diluted its impact.

> The effect was so vertiginous, apparently, that nobody noticed the new religion in the centre of the intellectual whirlpool. (P01-101)

## Relationship to Back to Methuselah

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] presents *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* as a deliberate correction — a stripped-down reworking of the same philosophical material.

> I now find myself inspired to make a second legend of Creative Evolution without distractions and embellishments. (P01-102)

The contrast between the two works forms a study in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s artistic development across three decades:

| Aspect | Man and Superman (1903) | [[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]] (1921) |
|--------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Length | Single play | Five-play Pentateuch |
| Method | Social comedy with philosophical interlude | Direct dramatic parable |
| Legend | Don Juan ([[mozart|Mozart]]ian) | [[garden-of-eden|Garden of Eden]] (Biblical) |
| Tone | Brilliant, vertiginous | Sparse, deliberate |
| Reception | Stage triumph | Mixed; long and demanding |
| [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Verdict | "decorated too brilliantly" (P01-101) | "the best I can do at my age" (P01-102) |

The later play is the mature, sober reworking of what *Man and Superman* first attempted. Where the 1903 play hides its [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] doctrine inside a comedy of manners, the 1921 cycle puts the doctrine on open display.

## Dramatic Structure and Philosophy

The play's most philosophically significant section is the Don Juan in Hell dream sequence (Act III), which dramatizes the tension between the mechanistic [[darwinian|Darwinian]] worldview and the creative [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian-Shavian alternative.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] uses the figure of [[don-juan-mozart|Don Juan]] — the legendary lover — as a mouthpiece for the [[life-force|Life Force]]. The pursuit of the beloved becomes a metaphor for the evolutionary drive toward higher forms of being. Juan's famous speech about the [[life-force|Life Force]] prefigures the arguments of the Barnabas brothers in *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*.

The dream sequence is surrounded by a drawing-room comedy about marriage, inheritance, and social convention. This framing was deliberate — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] wanted to smuggle philosophy into the commercial theater — but he later judged the frame as having overwhelmed the picture.

## Key Themes

- **[[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] as comedy**: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] treats the [[life-force|Life Force]] as a comic principle — irresistible, undignified, and triumphant over human pretension.

- **The limits of brilliance**: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s self-critique of the play is itself a theme: intellectual fireworks can obscure rather than illuminate the truth.

- **[[charles-darwin|Darwin]] vs. [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]], dramatized**: The Don Juan scene stages the debate between [[darwinian|Darwinian]] passivity and [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]'s purposeful [[free-will|will]].

- **The philosopher's task** in the theater: *Man and Superman* is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s first attempt to make the stage a pulpit for the religion of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* is his second, sparer attempt.

## Citation Summary

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] explicitly connects *Man and Superman* to the larger evolutionary argument through three key markers in the Preface:

- **P01-006**: He recalls publishing "my own view of evolution in a play called Man and Superman" in 1906 and finding that most people could not understand how he could be an Evolutionist without being a Neo-[[darwinian|Darwinian]].

- **P01-101**: He describes decorating it "too brilliantly and lavishly," noting that "nobody noticed the new religion in the centre of the intellectual whirlpool."

- **P01-102**: He announces the turn to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* as "a second legend of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] without distractions and embellishments."

These three passages trace [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s intellectual arc: from the ambitious theatrical experiment of middle age, through self-critical reassessment, to the sparer parables of his later years.
