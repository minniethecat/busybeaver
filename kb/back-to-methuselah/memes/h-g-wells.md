# H. G. Wells

The English writer (1866–1946), best known for his pioneering works of science fiction including *The Time Machine* (1895), *The War of the Worlds* (1898), and *The Shape of Things to Come* (1933). [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] invokes [[h-g-wells]] in the Preface to [[back-to-methuselah]] as a fellow explorer of evolutionary futures, even as he distinguishes his own [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian vision from Wells's more mechanistic depictions of human destiny.

## Dramatis Personae

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] references Wells in the Preface as a contemporary who, like himself, used fiction to explore the implications of evolutionary theory for human society. He refers to "that twentieth-century classic, Wells, a story of a race of men who..." (004-006), acknowledging Wells's imaginative engagement with the theme of human evolutionary transformation.

## Role in the Play

Wells does not appear as a character in the play cycle. His presence is felt as a parallel explorer — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s contemporary who approached similar themes of human future-evolution through the genre of scientific romance rather than philosophical drama. The two men knew each other through the [[fabian-society]], where both were active members, and their friendly rivalry stimulated [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s own thinking about the shape of humanity's evolutionary future.

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s reference to Wells in the Preface situates him among the modern writers who have grappled with evolutionary themes — a company that includes [[samuel-butler]] and [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] himself. The comparison is implicit but significant: where Wells imagined technological and biological transformation, [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] insisted on [[free-will|will]]-driven, [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian self-evolution.

## Relationships

- [[george-bernard-shaw]]: Fellow Fabian, fellow playwright-novelist exploring evolutionary futures; friendly rivals
- [[fabian-society]]: Both were prominent members of this socialist organization
- [[creative-evolution]]: Wells's evolutionary vision was more mechanistic than [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian alternative
- [[back-to-methuselah]]: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s direct contribution to the genre of evolutionary speculation that Wells helped establish

## Themes

Wells and [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] shared a Fabian faith in human progress and a conviction that evolutionary thinking must inform social policy. They diverged on method: Wells leaned toward technological and institutional solutions, while [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] insisted on the primacy of [[free-will|will]] and individual effort — the [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]]ian formula that organisms change because they *want* to. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s [[back-to-methuselah]] can be read partly as a response to Wells's evolutionary fictions, offering a rival vision of how humanity might transcend its biological limits.

## Real-World References

Herbert George Wells (1866–1946) was an English writer prolific in many genres, most famously science fiction. A socialist and Fabian, he was a friend and occasional antagonist of [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s within the [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]]. His works include *The Time Machine*, *The Island of Doctor Moreau*, *The Invisible Man*, *The War of the Worlds*, and *The Outline of History*. Wells's *The Shape of Things to Come* (1933) forecasts future human evolution in terms that parallel and contrast with [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s vision in [[back-to-methuselah]].
