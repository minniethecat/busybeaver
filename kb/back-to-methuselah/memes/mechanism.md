# Mechanism

**Mechanism** is the doctrine that all phenomena of life — including evolution, consciousness, and biological complexity — can be explained entirely by physical and chemical processes, without recourse to any purposeful force or vital principle. In [[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s preface to *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]*, Mechanism is the principal philosophical opponent to [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].

Shaw uses "Mechanism" and "the Mechanists" interchangeably, treating the doctrine as the intellectual foundation of [[neo-darwinism|Neo-Darwinism]] and the primary target of his evolutionary polemic.

## Shaw's Critique

### Mechanism as Despair

Shaw's fundamental objection to Mechanism is that it eliminates purpose from the universe. For the Mechanist, evolution proceeds by "senseless accident":

> According to the Neo-Darwinists, to the Mechanists, no hope whatever, because improvement can come only through some senseless accident which must, on the statistical average of accidents, be presently wiped out by another accident. (P01-015)

This is Shaw's central charge: Mechanism offers "no hope." If all change is accidental, there can be no direction, no progress, and no meaning — only the random drift of matter. Shaw contrasts this bleak vision with [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]], in which the [[life-force|Life Force]] purposefully drives organisms toward greater complexity and consciousness.

### The Turn from Mechanism

Shaw describes the intellectual mood of his time as a decisive break with the Mechanistic worldview:

> Nowadays, when we are turning in weary disgust and disillusion from Neo-Darwinism and Mechanism to Vitalism and Creative Evolution, it is difficult to imagine how this new departure of Darwin's could possibly have appealed to his contemporaries as exciting, agreeable, above all as hopeful. (P01-035)

The phrasing is telling: Mechanism once seemed "exciting" and "hopeful" to Darwin's contemporaries. Shaw's task is to explain why this apparent hopefulness was an illusion — and why the turn toward [[vitalism|Vitalism]] and [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] represents genuine intellectual progress.

### Paley's Watch

Shaw invokes the classic argument from design — Paley's watch — to expose what he sees as the dishonesty of the Mechanist position:

> If you found a watch, full of mechanism exquisitely adapted to produce a series of operations all leading to the fulfilment of one central purpose of measuring for mankind the march of the day and night, could you believe that it was not the work of a cunning artificer who had designed and contrived it all to that end? (P01-041)

The Mechanist must answer "yes" — must believe that apparent design is an illusion produced by [[circumstantial-selection|Circumstantial Selection]] operating over unlimited time. Shaw finds this answer intellectually dishonest, a form of special pleading that admits design everywhere except where it matters.

## The Mechanist-Vitalist Debate

In the closing pages of the preface, Shaw surveys the history of the Mechanist-Vitalist controversy among physiologists:

> The physiologists… were divided on the question into Mechanists and Vitalists. The Mechanists said that life is nothing but physical and chemical action; that they have demonstrated this in many cases of so-called vital phenomena; and that there is no reason to doubt that with improvements in scientific apparatus they would presently demonstrate it in all the rest. (P01-082)

Shaw's summary is even-handed but his sympathies are clear. He reports that the Anti-Mechanists (whom he wryly notes "objected to be called Vitalists (obviously the right name for them)") were caught in a conceptual muddle, and that "the New Vitalist" was "only half extricated from the Old Mechanist, objecting to be called either, and unable to give a clear lead in the new direction" (P01-082).

## Place in Shaw's Thought

Mechanism stands in Shaw's intellectual taxonomy as the negative pole — what [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] must overcome. Every major concept in Shaw's evolutionary philosophy is defined partly by its opposition to Mechanism:

| Concept | Relationship to Mechanism |
|---------|--------------------------|
| [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] | The purposeful alternative to mechanistic accident |
| [[life-force|Life Force]] | The driving Will that Mechanism denies |
| [[vitalism|Vitalism]] | The philosophical tradition that opposed Mechanism |
| [[lamarckism|Lamarckism]] | The evolutionary mechanism that requires will, not accident |
| [[circumstantial-selection|Circumstantial Selection]] | Shaw's term for the Mechanist's "design without a designer" |
