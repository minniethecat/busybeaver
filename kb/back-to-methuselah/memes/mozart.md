# Mozart

The Austrian composer (1756–1791) whose operatic treatment of the Don Juan legend — *Don Giovanni* (1787) — provided [[george-bernard-shaw]] with the narrative framework for his first sustained dramatic exploration of [[creative-evolution]]. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] invokes [[mozart]] multiple times in the Preface to [[back-to-methuselah]] as an exemplar of artistic genius and as the source of the "Mozartian form" in which he cast his evolutionary parable.

## Dramatis Personae

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] refers to Mozart in three distinct registers in the Preface. First, as an example of [[genius|creative genius]] whose gifts transcend circumstance: "Mozart may have a genius that prevails against Emperors and Archbishops, and a lung that succumbs to" tuberculosis (P01-067). Second, as a composer whose religious art achieved genuine greatness: "Compare Mozart's Magic Flute, Beethoven's Ninth Symphony" with the imitative art of a later, less religious age (P01-094). Third, and most personally, as the source of the dramatic form [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] adapted for [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].

## Role in the Play

Mozart does not appear as a character, but his *Don Giovanni* is the direct literary ancestor of [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s [[man-and-superman]] (1903). [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] explains in the Preface: "Accordingly, in 1901, I took the legend of Don Juan in its Mozartian form and made it a dramatic parable of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]" (P01-101). The Don Juan in Hell scene of *[[man-and-superman|Man and Superman]]* — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s first attempt to dramatize [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] — is a direct homage to and transformation of Mozart's operatic vision.

## Key Dialogue / Speeches

On genius and mortality: "Mozart may have a genius that prevails against Emperors and Archbishops, and a lung that succumbs to" the tubercle bacillus (P01-067) — [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s illustration that even the greatest creative force cannot always overcome circumstantial obstacles.

On art and religion: [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] invokes "Mozart's Magic Flute" alongside Beethoven's Ninth Symphony as examples of art in service of a living religion, contrasted with the hollow imitations of an age that has lost its faith (P01-094).

## Themes

Mozart represents for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] the ideal synthesis of art and religion — the artist whose work transcends entertainment to become a vessel for the deepest human questions. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s choice of Mozart's Don Juan as the vehicle for his first [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] parable was not arbitrary: Mozart, like [[william-shakespeare]], represented the pinnacle of artistic achievement, and [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] sought to show that even the greatest art could be enlisted in the service of evolutionary religion.

## Real-World References

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (1756–1791) composed over 600 works including symphonies, concertos, chamber music, and operas. His opera *Don Giovanni* (1787), with libretto by Lorenzo Da Ponte, retells the legend of Don Juan. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], a lifelong music critic and enthusiast, considered Mozart one of the supreme artistic geniuses. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s *[[man-and-superman|Man and Superman]]* (1903) explicitly reworks the Don Juan legend "in its Mozartian form" (P01-101), transforming the libertine into a philosopher of [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]].
