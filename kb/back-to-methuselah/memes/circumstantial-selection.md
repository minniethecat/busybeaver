# Circumstantial Selection

[[george-bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s preferred term for [[darwinian|Darwinian]] natural selection. By renaming it "Circumstantial Selection," [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] emphasizes what he saw as the theory's philosophical limitation: it attributes evolutionary change not to any creative impulse within organisms but merely to the passive filtering effect of external circumstances.

## Definition

Circumstantial Selection, as [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] uses the term, is the [[darwinian|Darwinian]] mechanism by which organisms better suited to their environment survive and reproduce at higher rates than those less suited. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] deliberately avoids the term "[[natural-selection|Natural Selection]]" to highlight his philosophical objection: there is nothing actively "selecting" in nature, only circumstances passively filtering.

The crucial distinction from [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] is that Circumstantial Selection has no direction, no purpose, and no creative power. It can explain which organisms persist but not why they exist in the first place.

## Shaw's Exposition

### In the Preface

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] devotes a substantial portion of the Preface to distinguishing Circumstantial Selection from [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]]. He structures his argument around a key distinction between [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] himself (cautious) and the [[darwinian|Darwinians]] (dogmatic) who turned a scientific hypothesis into a comprehensive worldview.

> Some are so entangled in the current confusion of Creative Evolution with Circumstantial Selection by their historical ignorance that they are puzzled by any distinction between the two. (P01-018)

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] traces how [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s theory swept through Victorian thought:

> The world was drowned by the flowing tide of Darwinism. (P01-051)

But he argues that [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism, in its pure form, eliminates purpose and [[free-will|will]] from the universe --- a philosophical error whose consequences he saw dramatized across [[europe|Europe]].

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] grants that Circumstantial Selection explains some phenomena:

> To Circumstantial Selection all mechanical and chemical reactions are possible, provided you accept the geologists' estimates of the great age of the earth, and therefore allow time enough for the circumstances to operate. (P01-054)

Yet he insists it "fail[s] as hopelessly to account for [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s own life work as for my conquest of the bicycle" (P01-054). The bicycle image is characteristically [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]ian --- deflating grand theory with a domestic example.

He notes that "there is no place in [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism for free [[free-will|will]], or any other sort of [[free-will|will]]" (P01-060), and for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], [[free-will|will]] is precisely what drives evolution forward.

### In the Play

The dramatic argument against Circumstantial Selection is embodied in the Barnabas brothers, particularly [[franklyn-barnabas|Franklyn]] and Conrad. Their position is that Science must account for everything --- including human [[free-will|will]], the impulse to create, and the fact that beings strive rather than merely adapt.

The [[longevity|long-lived]] characters of Parts IV and V are living proof, in [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s dramatic universe, that the [[life-force|Life Force]] has overcome the mere circumstantial filtering that would have kept human life at "three score and ten." [[eve|Eve]] in Part I, choosing birth and death over eternal stasis, is the first being to transcend Circumstantial Selection.

## Intellectual Context

Circumstantial Selection is [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s polemical rebranding with deep intellectual roots. The following table traces the lineage of the concept and [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s relationship to each figure:

| Thinker or School | Contribution | [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s Assessment |
|-------------------|-------------|-------------------|
| [[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]] | Natural selection as the mechanism of evolution | Respected as a cautious naturalist; not responsible for [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism |
| Herbert Spencer | Coined "survival of the fittest"; applied evolution to society | Objected to social-[[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ist implications |
| T.H. Huxley | "[[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s Bulldog"; agnosticism as worldview | Found his mechanistic universe philosophically inadequate |
| Neo-[[darwinian|Darwinians]] | Synthesis of selection with Mendelian genetics | "A ghastly idiocy" (P01-006) |
| [[samuel-butler|Samuel Butler]] | Anti-[[darwinian|Darwinian]] critique; evolution requires mind and purpose | Adopted and extended [[samuel-butler|Butler]]'s position |
| [[jean-baptiste-lamarck|Lamarck]] | Inheritance of acquired characteristics through effort and [[free-will|will]] | Championed as the true alternative to [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ism |

This table clarifies a key point of [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s rhetoric: his target is not [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] (whom he respects) but the [[darwinian|Darwinians]] who eliminated purpose from the evolutionary picture --- and whose ideas, he argues, "produced a [[europe|Europe]]an war" (P01-007) in [[europe|Europe]].

## Dramatic Expression

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] does not merely argue against Circumstantial Selection --- he dramatizes its insufficiency across the full arc of the Pentateuch.

The play sequence traces a movement from passive acceptance of circumstances to active creative [[free-will|will]]:

1. **Part I**: [[eve|Eve]] chooses birth over stasis --- the first transcendence of merely circumstantial existence

2. **Part II**: The Barnabas brothers formulate the case against [[darwinian|Darwinian]] passivity in [[england|England]]

3. **Part III**: The "thing" happens --- human lifespan extends beyond circumstantial limits

4. **Part IV**: Long-lived characters embody what [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] achieves beyond circumstances

5. **Part V**: Disembodied intelligence --- [[free-will|will]]'s ultimate triumph over circumstance

The entire arc of *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* is an extended counter-argument: if circumstances alone determined life, human beings would never have extended their lifespan. [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s comic method --- deflating grand theory with domestic examples like the bicycle --- is itself a dramatic argument against taking mechanistic explanations too seriously.

This is the same method [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]] had earlier deployed in *[[man-and-superman|Man and Superman]]*, whose Don Juan in Hell dream sequence made [[creative-evolution|Creative Evolution]] "a dramatic parable" (P01-101). But he judged that work "decorated too brilliantly and lavishly" --- *[[back-to-methuselah|Back to Methuselah]]* strips away the ornament to make the philosophical argument plain.

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s philosophical masterstroke is extending the argument from biology to [[political-science|politics]]. He traces a direct line from intellectual error to political catastrophe.

Mechanistic thinking about life produces mechanistic thinking about human beings --- and that, for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]], produced the mechanized slaughter of the First World War across [[europe|Europe]]. The intellectual stakes could not be higher.
