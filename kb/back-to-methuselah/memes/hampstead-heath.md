# Hampstead Heath

**Hampstead Heath** is the setting for the Barnabas brothers' study in Part II of [[Back to Methuselah]], "The Gospel of the Brothers Barnabas." From their windows overlooking the Heath toward [[london|London]], Conrad and [[franklyn-barnabas|Franklyn Barnabas]] formulate the play's central thesis: that human life must be extended to at least [[longevity|three hundred years]].

## Location

The Barnabas study windows "overlook Hampstead Heath towards [[london|London]]" (003-004), positioning the brothers at a physical and intellectual elevation above the city. The Heath — a large, ancient, semi-wild parkland in north [[london|London]] — provides a natural counterpoint to the urban civilization whose shortcomings the brothers diagnose.

### Real Location

Hampstead Heath has been public land since medieval times, spanning roughly 320 hectares of woodland, meadows, and ponds in the [[london|London]] Borough of Camden. In [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s time, it was a favored residence of artists, writers, and intellectuals — including [[h-g-wells|H.G. Wells]], who lived nearby. The area's associations with free-thinking and literary culture make it a fitting setting for [[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s intellectual revolutionaries.

## Role in the Play

### Part II: The Gospel of the Brothers Barnabas

The Heath functions primarily as a backdrop that establishes tone and class position. The Barnabas study is "well-furnished spacious" (003-003), suggesting professional comfort without ostentation — the home of thinking men, not social climbers. The view toward [[london|London]] places the brothers in a position of detached observation: they can see the city and its problems clearly, from a distance.

The Heath's natural beauty — "it being a fine afternoon in spring, the room is sunny" (003-004) — contrasts with the gravity of the conversation that takes place there. While [[london|London]] below them conducts its business as usual, the brothers are arriving at conclusions that would fundamentally alter human civilization.

## Significance

[[george-bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s choice of Hampstead Heath as [[conrad-barnabas|Conrad Barnabas]]'s residence carries deliberate intellectual associations. The area was known as a haven for unorthodox thinkers. Conrad, a professor of biology at [[jarrowfields-university|Jarrowfields University]], represents the scientific establishment in revolt against itself — and his Hampstead address signals membership in the intellectual vanguard of Edwardian [[england|England]].

The elevated vantage point is also thematic: the brothers look down on [[london|London]] physically, just as their ideas look down on conventional assumptions about human life from a higher evolutionary perspective. The spring season reinforces the theme of renewal and new beginnings.

## Notable Events

- Conrad and [[franklyn-barnabas|Franklyn Barnabas]] independently arrive at the conclusion that human life must be extended to "at least three centuries" (003-006)
- The Rector Haslam visits and unwittingly confirms the brothers' thesis: "It [[free-will|will]] last my time, anyhow" (003-006)
- [[lubin|Lubin]] and [[joyce-burge|Joyce Burge]] visit, bringing practical [[political-science|politics]] into collision with evolutionary theory
