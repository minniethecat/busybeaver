Growth and Decadence were the two fundamental rhythms Louis Sullivan identified as governing not only the life of civilizations and buildings but the inner life of the creative spirit itself. In 1886, he set out to give these rhythms poetic form in a remarkable essay titled "Inspiration," which became one of the most personal and revealing statements of his artistic philosophy.

## The Two Rhythms

Sullivan articulated the concept with characteristic clarity. He recognized these two forces as universal: "These two rhythms he called Growth and Decadence; and in 1886 he wished to say something about them" (015-040). For Sullivan, growth and decadence were not merely biological metaphors applied to architecture — they were the actual pulse of creative life, the systole and diastole of the spirit. Every living thing, every civilization, every artistic movement moved between these two poles.

>

> These two rhythms he called Growth and Decadence; and in 1886 he wished to say something about them — something that would give poetic form to what he felt stirring within his own creative life. (015-040)

The recognition of decay as a natural and inevitable phase of creative rhythm set Sullivan apart from the shallow optimism of his contemporaries. He did not deny decadence or seek to banish it; he accepted it as an integral part of the creative cycle, to be understood and expressed rather than suppressed.

## The Creative Cycle

For Sullivan, the poet's (and architect's) task was to live honestly within this rhythm: to experience growth as ecstasy and decadence as sorrow, without losing faith in the infinite source from which new growth springs. The rhythm could not be escaped, only embraced and expressed. This acceptance — the refusal to pretend that growth alone is the whole story — was, for Sullivan, the mark of creative maturity.

The artist who cannot acknowledge decadence cannot fully participate in growth, for the two are not opposites but phases of a single process. Sullivan's vision was fundamentally cyclical: every spring carries within it the seed of autumn, and every autumn prepares the ground for a new spring. The task of the creative spirit is to give honest expression to both moments.

## "Inspiration" — A Poetic Thesis

The essay Sullivan wrote to explore these rhythms was structured as a triptych. "He called his essay 'Inspiration.' The thesis fell into three parts: 'Growths: A Spring Song'; 'Decadence: An Autumn Reverie'; 'The Infinite: A Song of the Sea'; the transition from part to part effected by two interludes; the thought sustained to the point of rhapsody, in utterance, lyric and dramatic, of flowing prose" (015-040). The structure itself expressed the rhythm: spring growth giving way to autumnal decline, both resolved in the infinite vastness of the sea — a metaphor for the creative source that transcends both.

The essay's tripartite structure was not arbitrary but essential to Sullivan's meaning. Growth alone would be a half-truth; decadence alone would be despair. Only in the relation between the two — sustained by the infinite background against which both unfold — could the full truth of creative life be glimpsed. The sea, in Sullivan's metaphor, was the inexhaustible source from which new growth perpetually arises, even as old forms decay and return to it.

## Decadence as Cultural Diagnosis

Sullivan extended the concept of decadence from personal creative rhythm to cultural diagnosis. He saw in the architecture of his own day "a naked exhibitionism of charlatanry in the higher feudal and domineering culture, conjoined with expert salesmanship of the materials of decay" (015-082). Even as a young man visiting Boston, he had sensed it: "the city of Boston seemed a thing already in decay" (006-030). Decadence was not merely the absence of growth; it was an active force — the substitution of spurious imitation for genuine creation.

For Sullivan, the architectural decadence of his time was not a mystery. It was the direct consequence of a feudal culture that had lost its organic connection to life yet continued to dominate through imposition and authority. The "higher feudal and domineering culture" (015-082) maintained itself not through genuine creative power but through charlatanry — the expert marketing of dead forms to a public that had been taught to mistake imitation for art.

## Growth and the Democratic Spirit

Sullivan connected the rhythm of growth to his democratic philosophy. Feudal culture, he argued, was inherently decadent — it could only repeat and impose, never genuinely grow. Democratic culture, by contrast, was aligned with the growth principle: it arose from the people, responded to their life, and evolved organically as that life evolved. The architect who worked within a democratic framework participated in growth; the architect who merely reproduced feudal forms was complicit in decadence.

This was not merely an aesthetic judgment but a moral and political one. To build honestly — to create forms native to one's own time and people — was to participate in growth. To borrow from dead ages was to traffic in decadence. Sullivan's architectural practice was his attempt to align himself wholly with the growth principle, against the decadence he saw everywhere in the official architecture of his day.
