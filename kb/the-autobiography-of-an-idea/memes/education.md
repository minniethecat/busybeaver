## Definition

Education, as Sullivan experienced and later conceptualized it, encompassed both formal schooling and the broader process of self-cultivation through observation and experience. His mother "had not found time to acquire an 'education,' as it was then called and is still called" [001-014], establishing early in the autobiography a tension between institutional learning and lived wisdom.

## Formal Education and Its Limits

Sullivan received formal education at several institutions — the English High School in Boston, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, and the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris — but he consistently portrayed institutional learning as insufficient. His grandparents "did not bother about the child's education, for, being wise, he knew" that nature and experience were the true teachers [006-013]. Sullivan described his formal education in "small doses of formal routine education much as he might take a medicine" [006-033], a metaphor that captured his ambivalence.

## Self-Education Through Experience

The autobiography traces an alternative education acquired "partly through a process of absorption from his environment" [006-034]. This informal curriculum included the rhythms of nature, the discipline of work, and the direct observation of buildings and cities. A working mechanic's advice — "Ye got to have an education" [007-031] — highlighted the class dimension of Sullivan's thinking about learning: education was both a practical necessity and a social aspiration.

## Education as Moral Formation

Sullivan's grandmother believed that education carried moral weight, hoping her talks with young Louis "concerning the need of education, have a moral effect" [002-006]. This fusion of intellectual and moral development reflected Sullivan's broader philosophical idealism, in which learning was inseparable from character formation.
