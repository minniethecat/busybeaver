# English High School (Boston)

The English High School, located on Bedford Street in Boston, was the public secondary school Louis Sullivan entered in September 1870 at the age of fourteen (007-048). It was there, in a "rather old and dingy" building shared with the Latin High School, that Sullivan encountered Moses Woolson — the teacher whose "scientific" pedagogy and intense personality would become, in Sullivan's retrospective judgment, the single most important educational influence of his life.

## Overview

The English and Latin High Schools "were housed in a single building, rather old and dingy, on the south side of Bedford Street; a partition wall separating them, a single roof covering them" (009-014). The street front was granite, the side walls brick. Brick-paved yards served for the recess half-hour. Sullivan described it as "a barn-like, repellant structure fronting on a lane as narrow as the prevailing New England mind of its day" (009-014).

Sullivan was assigned to a second-floor room presided over by Moses Woolson, one of about forty boys. The room featured individual desks facing north, light from west and south windows, the master's platform at the west wall, and — memorably — "two large glass-paneled cabinets, one containing a collection of minerals, the other carefully prepared specimens of wood from all parts of the world" (009-016). These cabinets, and the spirit of systematic inquiry they represented, left a lasting impression.

## Key Figures

### Moses Woolson

Moses Woolson was, by Sullivan's account, an extraordinary teacher — "a blend of wild man and of poet" whose "plan of procedure was scientific to a degree, so far beyond the pedagogic attainments of his day that he stood unique, and was cordially hated by his craft as lambs might fear and hate a wolf" (009-027). Sullivan described the man not as a deep thinker or polished scholar, but as possessing "the art of teaching at his fingertips":

> "With great skill in intensive training he had brought them to this point within three months... Moses Woolson was not a deep thinker, nor was Moses Woolson erudite or scholarly, or polished in manners, or sedate. Rather was he a blend of wild man and of poet. But of a surety he had the art of teaching at his fingertips and his plan of procedure was scientific to a degree." (009-025, 009-027)

Woolson's one weakness was a volatile temper, but Sullivan forgave this: "his high strung, nervous makeup may be averred in part extenuation, for this very makeup was the source of his accomplishment and power: He surely gave in abundance, with overflowing hands, all that he had of the best to give" (009-027).

Woolson's classroom "partook of the nature of a university" (009-033). Professor Asa Gray of Harvard visited occasionally to lecture on botany. Woolson was "a sincere and ardent champion of womankind," and Gray's lectures unfailingly concluded with an exhortation for Women's Rights (009-033).

### Mr. Hale

In Sullivan's second year, he entered the Second Class under a sub-master named Hale — "a scholar and a gentleman, a shining light of conscientious, conventional, virtuous routine" (009-063). The contrast with Woolson was devastating. Sullivan wrote: "What was this tallow dip to the hot sun of Woolson? What could this mannikin accomplish? What could this respectable and approved lay figure do for one who had been trained intensively for a year by Moses Woolson? Let us therefore quickly draw the veil; and forget" (009-065).

## Role in Sullivan's Career

The English High School years were brief — roughly 1870 to 1872 — but their influence radiated through Sullivan's entire career. Woolson's training was the yardstick against which Sullivan measured all subsequent teachers and mentors.

At MIT, Sullivan reflected "with a sort of despair that neither immaculate Professor Ware nor sweaty, sallow, earnest Eugene Letang was a Moses Woolson. Ah, if but Moses Woolson had been versed in the story of architecture as he was in that of English Literature... what glowing flame would have come forth" (012-011).

Sullivan identified Woolson's training as the decisive factor in key career moments:

| Career Event | Woolson's Role |
|-------------|----------------|
| Surviving Frank Furness's trial week (1873) | "Moses Woolson followed him thereafter everywhere" — his training in accuracy and speed won Furness's approval (012-031) |
| Passing Beaux-Arts entrance exams (1874) | Woolson's discipline training: "Louis had the same capacity to absorb, and to value discipline, that Moses Woolson had to impart it" (009-040); Sullivan received his admission card (012-031) |
| Taking charge of Adler's office (1880) | "Louis put through this work with the efficiency of combined Moses Woolson and Beaux Arts training" (015-005n) |

Sullivan's philosophy of "annexing" valued influences to himself — "as he had done with Moses Woolson, Michelangelo, Richard Wagner, *et alii*" (015-005n) — placed Woolson first in a personal pantheon of great minds.

## Major Works or Achievements

The English High School's significance lies not in its buildings or institutional achievements but in the pedagogical episode that unfolded within its walls. Moses Woolson accomplished in one academic year what Sullivan described as a complete transformation:

> "By the end of the school year Moses Woolson through genius as a teacher, had turned a crudely promising boy into, so to speak, a mental athlete. He had brought order out of disorder, definition out of what was vague, superb alertness out of mere boyish ardor; had nurtured and concentrated all that was best in the boy; had made him consciously courageous and independent; had focused his powers of thought, feeling and action; had confirmed Louis's love of the great out of doors, as a source of inspiration; and had climaxed all by parting a great veil which opened to the view of this same boy, the wonderland of Poetry." (009-041)

The intellectual awakening Woolson catalyzed extended beyond literature and discipline into scientific observation. In the summer of 1872, "Louis botanized and mineralized with incessant ardor, and he saw what it signified that each thing should have a name, and what order and classification meant in the way of organized intelligence, and increased power of manipulation of things and thoughts. His insight into the relationship of function and structure deepened rapidly" (009-063). This insight — the relationship of function and structure — would become the germ of Sullivan's most famous architectural doctrine.

## Legacy

The English High School's legacy, as mediated through Sullivan, is the demonstration that one great teacher can alter the trajectory of a civilization. Sullivan's architectural philosophy of [[form-follows-function]] — the organizing principle of modernist architecture — can be traced in part to the observational discipline Woolson instilled: the habit of seeing that "each thing should have a name, and what order and classification meant" (009-063).

Sullivan's tribute to Woolson at the conclusion of the chapter is remarkable for its unqualified gratitude:

> "There may have been teachers and teachers, but for Louis Sullivan there was and could be only one. And now, in all too feeble utterance he pleads this token, remembrance, to the memory of that **one** long since passed on." (009-043)

In a life marked by bitter professional disappointment and acerbic critique of nearly every institution he encountered, Sullivan's reverence for Moses Woolson and the English High School stands as a singular exception — a debt he acknowledged until the end.
