# John Draper

John William Draper (1811--1882), American scientist, philosopher, and historian whose two-volume work *The Intellectual Development of Europe* (1862) provided Sullivan with an intellectual architecture that aligned his architectural thinking with the broad currents of human history.

## Biography

### Early Life and Career

John William Draper was born in St. Helens, Lancashire, England, in 1811. He studied at University College London before emigrating to the United States in 1832. He earned his medical degree from the University of Pennsylvania and became a professor of chemistry and natural philosophy at New York University. Draper was a pioneer in photochemistry and astronomical photography -- he took the first detailed photograph of the Moon and the first photograph of a solar spectrum.

### Major Works

Draper's most famous and controversial work, *History of the Intellectual Development of Europe* (1862), applied a scientific framework to the sweep of European civilization. The book organized European history into two grand epochs: the "Age of Faith" and the "Age of Reason." Draper argued that human progress consisted in the gradual emancipation of reason from the authority of faith -- a thesis that ignited the contemporary "war between science and religion" (013-025).

His other major work, *History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science* (1874), extended this argument into a more polemical register, becoming a bestseller and a touchstone of late-nineteenth-century secular thought.

## Relationship with Sullivan

Sullivan received Draper's *Intellectual Development of Europe* as a Christmas gift from his father (013-025), a rare gesture of intellectual patronage from the practical-minded Patrick Sullivan. Sullivan "read and reread with absorbing interest, passing over its controversial trend, for the 'war between science and religion' as it was called, was still raging" (013-025). This reading coincided with a period of intense solitary study in which Sullivan was "consciously now working out" (013-024) his architectural philosophy, and Draper's historical framework provided the breadth of reference that the young architect's introspection required.

What captivated Sullivan was not Draper's polemics but his structural framework. "The broad division of the work into an 'Age of faith,' and an 'Age of reason' held his interest, as he saw set forth the emergence and the growth of science as the spirit of man sought and found freedom in the open" (013-025). This framework aligned with Sullivan's own developing conviction "that man's spirit must be free that his powers may be free to accomplish in beneficence" (013-025).

Draper's panoramic history confirmed for Sullivan what he had already sensed in his architectural work: "that for centuries it had been the case that art had been belittled in superstitions called traditions -- and lived on by virtue of a thin and baseless faith" (013-025). John Edelmann's theory of suppressed functions -- which Sullivan had first encountered years earlier in Jenney's Chicago office (011-025) -- "recurred to him as broadly set forth, in confirmation, in Draper's heavy work" (013-025). The convergence of Edelmann's architectural diagnosis and Draper's historical panorama convinced Sullivan that the problems of architecture were not technical but spiritual and civilizational.

### Deepening the Task

Reading Draper expanded Sullivan's conception of his own work. He felt "that his thoughts concerning architecture must broaden into a perfected sympathy with mankind. That Man, past and present, must and would become more and more significant, would be found to have filled a greater role than any art, than any science" (013-025). Sullivan began to ask whether "the invisible spirit of mankind ... pervaded all things, all works, all civilizations, and informed them with the sense of actuality" (013-025). Draper's work thus set Sullivan on the path of investigating "what man now thought, and had thought through the centuries" (013-025) -- an investigation that would ultimately yield Sullivan's distinctive fusion of architectural practice and democratic philosophy.

## Significance

Draper's significance to Sullivan was catalytic rather than doctrinal. Sullivan did not adopt Draper's scientism wholesale; he "passed over its controversial trend" (013-025). Rather, Draper's sweeping historical framework gave Sullivan permission to think of architecture not as a technical craft operating within received traditions but as an expression of the spiritual condition of civilization itself. The idea that architectural form must emerge from the free spirit of man -- not from feudal or ecclesiastical inheritance -- owes a clear debt to Draper's narrative of emancipation.

The Christmas gift from Sullivan's father thus proved to be one of the most consequential books in Sullivan's intellectual development, ranking alongside Taine's *Philosophy of Art* and the works of Herbert Spencer as shaping forces in the architect's thought.
