## Definition

For Sullivan, aspiration was the upward-reaching quality of
the human spirit — the drive toward something higher, nobler,
and more beautiful than mere existence. In architecture, this
found expression in soaring forms, particularly the vertical
lines of the skyscraper [015-062].

## Aspiration in Architecture

Sullivan saw the tall building as an expression of collective
human aspiration. The skyscraper was not merely a practical
solution to urban density but a symbol of upward striving:

> the suggestion of slenderness and aspiration, the soaring quality as of a
> thing rising from the earth [015-062]

He believed architecture should interpret "the aspirations and
the heart's desire of the people" — making buildings into
expressions of collective hope and ambition [015-072].

## Aspiration and the Human Spirit

Sullivan extended aspiration beyond architecture to encompass
all human endeavor. He spoke of "the sincere, the wholesome
aspirations of our people and of our land" as the proper
foundation for a democratic architecture [015-089].

He perceived "the everlasting aspirations of mankind, the
ever-yearning" as a permanent feature of the human condition —
a force that architecture must acknowledge and serve [015-090].

## Sanity of Aspiration

Sullivan defended aspiration against charges of impracticality,
declaring:

> No dream, no aspiration, no prophecy can be saner [014-105]

For him, aspiration was not escapism but the sanest possible
response to the human condition — a recognition that humanity
is defined by its reach beyond the given [014-052].
