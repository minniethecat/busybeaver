# Adler and Sullivan Partnership (1881)

The founding of the architectural firm Adler & Sullivan on May 1, 1881, the professional partnership that launched Louis Sullivan's independent career and produced some of the most innovative buildings of late 19th-century America.

## Background

In 1879, Sullivan was working in the office of Major William Le Baron Jenney when his friend and foreman John Edelmann told him that Dankmar Adler had "cut loose from Burling, set up independently" and had "put through the New Central Music Hall" (013-027). Edelmann saw an opportunity. He arranged for Sullivan to meet Adler, who needed someone to run his office while he completed the Central Music Hall project.

Sullivan took the position, and the trial period proved successful. "Louis took hold and made things hum. All went well. The office was a busy hive. Here Louis was boss, there Adler was boss" (013-028). In the "course of Louis's daily working life, conditions were steadily improving. His engagements in offices grew longer, he began to prosper" (013-018). He "had passed the day of his majority, and was now looking out for himself" (013-018).

## The Event

### What Happened

The partnership was formalized in the spring of 1881. John Edelmann, "who meanwhile had entered into partnership with a man named Johnson, who did school work, sent for Louis to come over that evening — said he had something to say" (013-027). Edelmann conveyed that Adler was ready to make the arrangement permanent. Sullivan confronted Adler directly with the question: "How would you like to take me into partnership?" (013-029). Louis laughed at his own boldness (013-030), but Adler was serious.

Adler responded: "All right. Draw up a contract for five years, beginning first of May. First year you one third, after that, even" (013-031). "Louis drew up a brief memorandum on a sheet of office stationery, which Adler read over once and signed" (013-032). The terms were remarkably generous for a twenty-five-year-old — Sullivan would share equally in the firm's profits after the first year.

### Sullivan's Experience

The partnership's formal beginning was deeply significant for Sullivan. "On the first day of May, 1880, D. Adler & Co. moved into a fine suite of offices on the top floor of the Borden Block. On the first day of May, 1881, the firm of Adler & Sullivan, Architects, had its name on the entrance door" (013-033). Sullivan reflected that "after long years of ambitious dreaming and unremitting work, at the age of twenty-five, Louis H. Sullivan became a full-fledged architect before the world, with a reputation starting on its way, and in partnership with a man he had learned to respect and trust — Dankmar Adler" (013-033).

Now "Louis felt he had arrived at a point where he had a foothold, where he could make a beginning in the open world. Having come into its responsibilities, he would face it boldly" (013-034). This was the launching point for his program of "practical experimentation" that would produce the "form follows function" doctrine.

## Impact

### On Sullivan

The partnership freed Sullivan to experiment. "He could now, undisturbed, start on the course of practical experimentation he long had in mind, which was to make an architecture that fitted its functions" (013-034). The building business was "again under full swing, and a series of important mercantile structures came into the office, each one of which he treated experimentally, feeling his way toward a basic process, a grammar of his own" (013-035). Adler's engineering genius — he was the only man who "had had the intelligence to discern that the matter of acoustics is not a science but an art" (015-013) — complemented Sullivan's aesthetic vision. Together they built the Auditorium Building, the Transportation Building for the World's Columbian Exposition, and many other landmarks.

### Broader Significance

The Adler & Sullivan partnership, lasting from 1881 to 1895, produced some of the most important buildings in American architectural history. The firm's work defined the Chicago School of architecture and pioneered the modern skyscraper. Sullivan's design philosophy, developed during these partnership years, would influence a generation of architects including his most famous apprentice, Frank Lloyd Wright. The partnership's dissolution in 1895 — partly a consequence of the economic downturn triggered by the Panic of 1893 — marked the beginning of Sullivan's professional decline, but the work they produced together remains a cornerstone of American modernism.
