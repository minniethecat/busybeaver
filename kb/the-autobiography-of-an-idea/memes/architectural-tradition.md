# Architectural Tradition

Sullivan viewed architectural tradition with deep ambivalence: he recognized the accumulated wisdom embedded in historical forms but condemned the mindless replication of precedent as a betrayal of the living creative spirit. Tradition, in his polemical formulation, was "the velvet, the down of age" — comfortable, seductive, and ultimately suffocating (011-044).

## Definition

For Sullivan, tradition became a problem when it hardened into "superstition" — when architects accepted inherited forms without questioning their relevance to modern conditions. He distinguished between the vital tradition of craftsmanship and structural intelligence (which he respected) and the "inane traditions" of stylistic imitation (which he despised). The former transmitted living knowledge; the latter transmitted dead forms (013-025, 013-035).

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan's critique of tradition was shaped by the progressive spirit of nineteenth-century America — a young nation that defined itself against European precedent. It also drew from the rationalist architectural theory he encountered in Paris, particularly Viollet-le-Duc's argument that Gothic builders had solved structural problems honestly while Renaissance architects had merely copied appearances.

### Sullivan's Development

Sullivan's rebellion against tradition was personal before it was theoretical. As a young architect in Chicago, he confronted a profession dominated by the reproduction of European historical styles — Romanesque, Gothic, Renaissance, each applied as fashion dictated. His early work with Dankmar Adler gave him the technical confidence to question these conventions: if a steel frame could carry the load, why dress it in a masonry costume that lied about how the building stood?

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> Surely it was a finished landscape, invested with the velvet, the down of age and tradition (011-044).

> He saw that men settled in superstitions called traditions — and lived on by virtue of a law of inertia (013-025).

> He would not accept thoughtless acceptance of inane traditions, of puerile habits of uninquiring minds (013-035).

> No architectural dictum, or tradition, or superstition, or habit, should stand in the way of honest building (013-034).

### In Architecture

Sullivan's rejection of unthinking tradition is visible throughout his built work. The Auditorium Building, while employing Romanesque motifs, transformed them into something unprecedented through its massing and organic detail. The Wainwright Building abandoned historical quotation almost entirely, declaring instead a new vertical language derived from the steel frame's structural logic.

His most explicit repudiation of tradition as "ghostly m émoire" came in the Transportation Building at the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition — a defiantly polychromatic and unhistorical structure surrounded by the "White City's" neoclassical uniformity, which Sullivan saw as a cultural retreat into dead forms (015-086).

## Legacy

Sullivan's critique of tradition became a founding text of architectural modernism. His call to abandon "inane traditions" in favor of honest building resonated through the twentieth century, from the Bauhaus to the International Style — though Sullivan himself would likely have condemned the way modernism eventually became a tradition of its own, as unthinking in its replication as the Beaux-Arts classicism it replaced.
