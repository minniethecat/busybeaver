# Philadelphia

Philadelphia was the city where Louis Sullivan, at age seventeen, began his architectural career. It was here that he worked in the office of Frank Furness, encountered the intellectual culture of the Centennial Exposition, and first experienced the tension between American ambition and European tradition that would define his mature thought.

## In Sullivan's Life

### Arrival

Sullivan arrived in Philadelphia after a brief stopover in New York, "in due time, as they say" (010-028). He initially "lived with his grandpa and uncle in West Philadelphia" (010-037), establishing himself in the city while seeking entry into its architectural profession.

### Furness & Hewitt

Sullivan's first architectural employment was in the office of Furness & Hewitt, architects. Frank Furness, a Civil War veteran and Medal of Honor recipient, ran one of Philadelphia's most original and idiosyncratic practices. His assistant, Eugene Letang, was "a diplomé of the École National des Beaux Arts, Paris, and specifically an ancien of the atelier libre of Emil Vaudremer, architect, a winner of the Grand Prix de Rome" (010-019). Through Letang, Sullivan received his first exposure to the Beaux-Arts method — the rigorous architectural training system that he would later encounter firsthand in Paris.

Sullivan's experience at Furness & Hewitt was formative but brief. "His experience in the office of Furness & Hewitt, in Philadelphia, it seems, had given him an erroneous idea" (011-017) about the architectural profession — an idea that his later experience in Chicago would correct.

### Architectural Landscape

Philadelphia's built environment did not impress Sullivan. He "later found in New York and Philadelphia that almost all the buildings in these cities were of the same crassness of type; a singularly sordid, vulgar vernacular in architectural speech" (011-018). Coming from Boston, where he had already developed a critical eye, Sullivan saw Philadelphia's architecture as part of a broader eastern failure to produce an authentic American building language.

### The Centennial Exposition

Philadelphia in 1876 hosted the Centennial Exposition, a world's fair celebrating the hundredth anniversary of American independence. The Exposition was a watershed moment in Sullivan's narrative, introducing him to architectural possibilities that transcended local practice. It was here that he first grasped the scale and ambition of the international architectural stage — an awakening that would lead him, through a chain of events, to the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris.

### Musical Life

One hot summer evening in Philadelphia, Sullivan "had gone to the Academy of Music to hear a Thomas Concert" (011-027). The city's cultural institutions provided a dimension of refinement that complemented his architectural education, and Sullivan's lifelong engagement with music — particularly Wagner — found early nourishment in Philadelphia's concert halls.

## Significance

Philadelphia in Sullivan's autobiography is a city of beginnings and transitions. It was the site of his first professional employment, his first exposure to Beaux-Arts method, and his first recognition that American architecture was inadequate to its democratic promise. The city served as a necessary way station between the provincial world of his Boston childhood and the creative ferment of Chicago.
