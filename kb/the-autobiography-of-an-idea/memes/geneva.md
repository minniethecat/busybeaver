# Geneva

The Swiss city on the shores of Lake Geneva that served as the ancestral home of Louis Sullivan's maternal family, the Lists. Geneva was where Sullivan's grandmother Anna Mattheus was born, where his mother Andrienne and her siblings spent their early childhood, and where the family lived in prosperity before a financial catastrophe drove them to America.

## Location

A major city in southwestern Switzerland, situated at the western tip of Lake Geneva (Lac Léman), surrounded by the Alps and Jura mountains. In the nineteenth century, Geneva was a center of Calvinist culture, watchmaking, banking, and international diplomacy.

## In Sullivan's Life

### The List Family in Geneva

Sullivan's maternal grandmother, Anna Mattheus, "was Swiss-French, born in Geneva — where also her three children were born" (001-008). These three children were Andrienne (Louis's mother, born circa 1835), Jennie, and Jules. The family lived in a substantial residence: "a marble house with large grounds called La Maison des Paquis" (001-016).

Sullivan possessed a tangible artifact of this Genevan life: "a small oval card with perforated edge, on the plain field of which is drawn with colored pencils, a park-like view, with house half-hidden among the trees." On the back, in Anna Mattheus's handwriting, was the notation: "*Terrace de la Maison des Paquis faites par Andrienne en 1849*" — drawn by Andrienne at the age of fourteen (001-016).

### Henri List's Mysterious Past

Sullivan's grandfather, Henri List, arrived in Geneva under obscure circumstances. The "intervening years between his hegira and his arrival in Geneva, Switzerland, are a blank." There was "some lack of clearness as to his vocation in Geneva; was he a Professor of Greek in the University, or did he coach rich young English gentlemen through their university course?" In any event, "he was highly educated, and he prospered" (001-010).

Henri List met Anna Mattheus in Geneva. She was "considerably older than he" and "kept a store filled with a sumptuous stock of choice linens and laces." Family gossip had it that "he married her for her money" (001-016).

### Financial Ruin and Emigration

The family's Genevan prosperity ended catastrophically. Henri List "speculated and finally lent ear to the wiles of a Jew. He ventured his all. The enterprise strangely and suddenly lost its credit, and the house of List tottered and collapsed in irretrievable ruin." Anna List "borrowed money of her relatives to take the family to America, to forget the past and start anew in a strange land" (001-016).

The family reached Boston probably around 1850. Thus Geneva represented both the peak of the List family's European prosperity and the site of its collapse — a dual legacy that shaped the emotional inheritance Sullivan received from his mother.

## Cultural Significance

Geneva in Sullivan's narrative serves as a symbol of Old World cultivation abruptly lost. The marble house, the park-like grounds, young Andrienne's colored-pencil drawing — these fragments of elegance stand in poignant contrast to the family's immigrant life in Boston. The Genevan episode also exemplifies a recurring theme in Sullivan's autobiography: the role of chance, fate, and financial vicissitude in shaping lives.
