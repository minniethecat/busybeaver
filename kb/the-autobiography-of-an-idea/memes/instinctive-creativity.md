# Instinctive Creativity

Sullivan held that the most powerful creative faculty was not the trained intellect but the primal instinct that every child possesses — a direct, unmediated connection to the natural world that formal education too often suppressed rather than cultivated.

## Definition

Instinctive creativity, in Sullivan's theory, was the innate human capacity to respond to the world directly and form-givingly, without the intermediation of rules, precedents, or academic categories. The child who built a snow fort or arranged stones in a pattern was exercising the same creative instinct as the architect designing a cathedral — the difference was only in the sophistication of the means, not in the nature of the act (014-063, 014-065).

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan's valorization of instinct drew from Romantic celebrations of childhood innocence and from the Progressive-era interest in child development. It also reflected a pragmatic American suspicion of over-intellectualized systems — the belief that "book learning" could never substitute for native intuition.

### Sullivan's Development

Sullivan's own childhood provided the experiential basis for his theory. His early years on the farm were lived "almost wholly in the world of instinct" — a time when sensation and emotion dominated over intellect and analysis (004-010). The "unalloyed childhood instinct" he remembered was not a phase to be outgrown but a faculty to be preserved and cultivated (013-014).

His architectural education threatened to bury this instinct under layers of academic convention. At the École des Beaux-Arts, he encountered a system that valued precedent over intuition, formula over feeling. His rebellion against this system was, at root, a defense of the instinctive creativity he had known as a child.

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> He lived at that time almost wholly in the world of instinct. Whatever there was of intellect lay quiescent (004-010).

> He had an instinctive sense of physiognomy, and could read character in a face (010-028).

> What he saw in man was an unalloyed childhood instinct, an intuition and a childhood faith (013-014).

> The liberation of those instincts within us which are akin to the creative forces of nature (014-063).

> Our view the amazing world of instinct in the child whence arises genius (014-065).

> The recrudescent power of instinct, to direct this power into works of art (014-102).

### In Architecture

For Sullivan, the architect's task was not to suppress instinct in favor of training but to liberate it — to recover the child's capacity for fresh, unprejudiced seeing and to direct that capacity toward built form. His own design method, with its emphasis on organic growth and spontaneous expression, was a practical implementation of this philosophy. The ornament that flowed across his buildings was not calculated but *felt* — the product of an instinct trained by observation, not a formula learned by rote.

## Legacy

Sullivan's emphasis on instinctive creativity influenced progressive architectural education in the twentieth century, from the Bauhaus preliminary course to the experimental pedagogy of Black Mountain College. His insight — that creative power resides in faculties that formal education tends to suppress — remains a productive challenge to architectural training.
