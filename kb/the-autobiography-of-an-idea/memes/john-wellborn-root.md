# John Wellborn Root

John Wellborn Root (1850–1891), architect, partner to Daniel Burnham in the firm Burnham & Root, and designer of the Masonic Temple -- at the time the tallest building in the world and the structure that introduced the word "skyscraper" into common use (015-068). Sullivan admired Root deeply, describing him as a man who "had it in him to be great" (015-011).

## Biography

### Early Life

Root was born in Lumpkin, Georgia, and raised in the South before the Civil War. His father, Sidney Root, was a successful Atlanta businessman. Root studied civil engineering at New York University and later worked briefly in the office of John B. Snook before moving to Chicago in the aftermath of the Great Fire of 1871. He met Daniel Burnham in the office of Carter, Drake and Wight, and the two formed their partnership in 1873.

### Career

Root was the design genius of Burnham & Root. Sullivan's first impression of him -- on a visit to the Montauk Block, the firm's recently completed office building -- was vivid and detailed. Root was "redheaded, large bullet-headed, close-cropped, effervescent, witty, small-nosed, alert, debonair, a mind that sparkled, a keen sense of humor -- which Burnham lacked -- solidly put together, bull-necked, freckled, arms of iron, light blue sensuous eyes" (015-003). He was "a facile draftsman, quick to grasp ideas, and quicker to appropriate them; an excellent musician; well read on almost any subject; speaking English with easy exactitude of habit, ready and fluent on his feet, a man of quick-witted all-round culture which he carried easily and jauntily; and vain to the limit of the skies" (015-003).

Architecturally, Root's "mania was to be the first to do this or that or the other. He grasped at novelties like a child with new toys. He thought them efficacious and lovely -- then one by one he threw them away" (015-004). His tempering influence on Burnham's "megalomania" was limited; Root was "of the world, of the flesh, and considerably of the devil. His temperament was that of the well groomed freelance, never taking anything too seriously, wherein he differed from his ponderous partner, much as dragon fly and mastiff" (015-003).

His masterpiece was the Masonic Temple (1892), which at twenty-two stories "had raised its head far into the air, and the word 'skyscraper' came into use" (015-068). Root was appointed Consulting Architect for the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in the fall of 1890 (015-074). He did not live to see it built.

### Relationship with Sullivan

Sullivan met Root in the early 1880s and was immediately captivated: "At once he was attracted by Root's magnetic personality" (015-003). He recognized beneath Root's "superficial nonsense" a "man of power" and "took joy in him as a prospective and real stimulant in rivalry, as a mind with which it would be well worth while to clash wits in the promotion of an essentially common cause" (015-003).

Sullivan, true to his habit, "immediately annexed John Root to his collection of assets; or, if one so wills to put it -- to his menagerie of personalities great and small" (015-003). He saw Root as a kindred creative spirit in a profession increasingly dominated by businessmen.

Yet the two men differed fundamentally in temperament. Root once told Sullivan: "You take your art too seriously" (015-068) -- a rebuke that captured the distance between Root's cultivated insouciance and Sullivan's consuming passion. Sullivan noted the "friction of ideas between the artist and the merchant" in the Burnham-Root partnership, "a significant mismating which made him ponder" (015-010).

Root's death on January 15, 1891, at the age of forty-one, was a blow to Sullivan. A gathering of Fair architects met in February 1891, and "John Root was not there. In faith he could not come. He had made his rendezvous the month before. Grace-land was now his home. Soon above him would be reared a Celtic cross" (015-076). Sullivan wrote:

> "He departed this vale of tears, and this best of all possible worlds, 15 January, 1891, at the age of forty-one, leaving in Louis's heart and mind a deep sense of vacancy and loss. For John Root had it in him to be great, as Burnham had it in him to be big. John Wellborn Root in passing left a void in his wake." (015-011)

## Significance

Root's death at forty-one is one of the great "what-ifs" of American architectural history. Sullivan believed Root "had it in him to be great" (015-011) -- a higher compliment than he paid Burnham, who "had it in him to be big." Root's absence from the 1893 Fair left Burnham without his creative conscience, and the Fair's neoclassical turn -- which Sullivan bitterly opposed -- may have been partly a consequence of Root's death.

Root also designed the Rookery and the Monadnock Building (with its stark, undecorated northern half), structures that helped define the Chicago School's commercial aesthetic. His Masonic Temple, demolished in 1939, was for a brief period the tallest inhabited building on earth -- a concrete realization of the vertical impulse that Sullivan himself was exploring in the Wainwright and Guaranty Buildings.

Sullivan's portrait of Root captures a particular tragedy: a man of extraordinary gifts who "was so self-indulgent that there was risk he might never draw upon his underlying power" (015-005), and who died before he had fully realized what Sullivan believed was within him.
