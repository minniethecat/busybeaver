# Suppressed Functions

John Edelmann's theory that organisms, institutions, and societies harbor innate functional capacities that convention, tradition, or inadequate conditions systematically suppress — an idea that crystallized Sullivan's understanding of why architecture had lost its vitality and what must change to revive it.

## Definition

The theory of suppressed functions holds that living things possess latent capacities — functions — that remain unrealized not because they are absent but because they are actively suppressed by external constraints. Sullivan received the idea in dramatic terms. When John Edelmann explained the theory, "Louis, startled, saw in a flash that this meant the real clue to the mystery that lay behind the veil of appearances" (011-025). The explosive power of the concept came from the collision of two words: "when the word 'function' was detonated by the word 'suppressed,' a new, an immense idea came suddenly into being and lit up his inner and his outer world as one" (011-025). Under this illumination, "the world of men began to assume a semblance of form, and of function" (011-025).

The theory was not merely biological. It extended to any system — architectural, social, psychological — where innate capacities are held back by what Sullivan would later call "the dead weight of the 'consecrated wisdom of the ages' and its follies" (014-062). Suppression, in this view, was the mechanism by which institutions, traditions, and habits prevented living form from expressing its natural purpose.

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

The theory was John Edelmann's own, emerging from his "immense range of reading" that spanned "the great philosophers" and "the highest transcendentalisms of German metaphysics" (011-024). Edelmann was by Sullivan's account "a thinker, a profound thinker, a man of immense range of reading, a brain of extraordinary keenness, strong, vivid, that ranged in its operations from saturnine intelligence concerning men and their motives, to the highest transcendentalisms of German metaphysics" (011-024). As "an immediate psychologist, never before or since has Louis met his equal in vitality, in verity, and in perspicacity of thought" (011-024).

Edelmann's theory resonated with the evolutionary biology Sullivan was absorbing. Darwin's *Theory of Evolution* "seemed stupendous" to Sullivan (013-026), and Herbert Spencer's model of developmental progression from "an unorganized simple, through stages of growth and differentiation to a highly organized complex" (013-026) provided a framework for understanding what organisms could become if suppression were removed. John Draper's *Intellectual Development of Europe* reinforced the point historically, tracing "the emergence and the growth of science as the spirit of man sought and found freedom in the open" (013-025). Draper's survey confirmed that "John Edelmann's theory of suppressed functions, recurred to him as broadly set forth, in confirmation, in Draper's heavy work" (013-025).

### Sullivan's Development

Sullivan encountered Edelmann's theory during their time together at Major William Le Baron Jenney's architectural office in Chicago. The two had become "fast friends" (011-023), with Edelmann playing the voluble thinker and Sullivan the "practised listener" (011-024). Sullivan immediately grasped that the theory was larger than Edelmann himself perhaps realized: "Louis soon noticed that while he himself had a clear program in life, John had none. That all this talk, while of deep import to him, was for John merely luxurious self-indulgence and a luscious hour with parade of vanity" (011-026). Where Edelmann treated the idea as intellectual display, Sullivan took it as a life-orienting revelation.

The insight, however, did not deliver instant clarity. Sullivan discovered that "what he had assumed to be a single vast veil of mystery that might perhaps lift of a sudden, like a cloud, proved in experience to be a series of gossamer hangings that must slowly rise up one by one, in a grand transformation scene" (011-025). The concept of suppressed functions gave him the interpretive key; the work of unveiling would take decades.

The theory recurred to Sullivan years later — now in his early partnership with Dankmar Adler — as he read Draper and reflected on "the architectural art of his day." He had discovered "to his annoyance, that in the architectural art of his day, the spirit of man was not free, nor were his powers so liberated and so trained that he might create in beneficence. Not only this, but that for centuries it had been the case that art had been belittled in superstitions called traditions — and lived on by virtue of a thin and baseless faith" (013-025). Edelmann's theory explained the mechanism: architecture's vital functions — honest expression of purpose, organic form, creative freedom — had been suppressed by academic convention, stylistic precedent, and institutional habit.

Sullivan's mature recognition of his debt to Edelmann was unambiguous. Years later, reflecting on their friendship, Sullivan wrote: "The passing years have isolated and revealed John Edelmann, as unique in personality among fine and brilliant minds. Be assured he will not turn in his grave, unless in bliss, should he hear it said that he was the benefactor and Louis the parasite and profiteer" (011-032).

### In Architecture

The suppressed functions concept translated into Sullivan's architectural practice as an imperative to liberate. His resolve was clear: "no architectural dictum, or tradition, or superstition, or habit, should stand in the way" (013-034). He would make "an architecture that fitted its functions — a realistic architecture based on well defined utilitarian needs — that all practical demands of utility should be paramount as basis of planning and design" (013-034). A building, like an organism, possessed functions that demanded honest expression, not suppression beneath historical costume.

The practical application meant designing from the inside out, letting a building's structural and spatial requirements generate its form rather than imposing an external style. Sullivan's experimental vertical system with "slender piers, tending toward a masonry and iron combination" grew from functional needs — maximizing daylight and structural efficiency — rather than from stylistic preference (013-035). The result "upset all precedent" (013-035), precisely because it refused to suppress the building's actual functional logic under borrowed forms.

Sullivan generalized the concept beyond individual buildings to architecture as a cultural institution. The suppression of architectural functions was not accidental; it was systematic — enforced by schools, critics, and professional habit. His response was defiant: "He would brush them all aside, regardless of commentators" (013-034). The architectural act of "not suppressing" became a moral position, aligned with his broader conviction that "man's spirit must be free that his powers may be free to accomplish in beneficence" (013-025).

## Legacy

Edelmann's theory of suppressed functions is the intellectual seed from which Sullivan's central architectural principle grew. The insight that function must be liberated from suppression leads directly to the maxim that form follows function — not as a stylistic preference but as a moral imperative. In Sullivan's own narrative, the sequence is clear: first came Edelmann's theory (011-025); then came the recognition that architecture was suppressing its own functions under tradition (013-025); then came the resolve to "make an architecture that fitted its functions" and the formula "that form follows function" (013-034).

Beyond architecture, the concept holds broader significance. Sullivan applied it to civilization itself, noting "the free spirit of man — so long self-suppressed under the dead weight of the 'consecrated wisdom of the ages'" (014-062). The suppressed functions theory thus connects Sullivan's architectural practice to his democratic philosophy: both are liberation projects, concerned with identifying and releasing latent human capacities from institutional constraint.

Sullivan's acknowledgment of Edelmann's gift is unusually generous — "the benefactor and Louis the parasite and profiteer" (011-032) — and testifies to the theory's foundational role in his intellectual formation. The idea that functions can be suppressed, and that the work of art and life is to liberate them, runs like a spine through Sullivan's entire body of thought.
