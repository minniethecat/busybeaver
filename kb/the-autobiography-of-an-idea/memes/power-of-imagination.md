# Power of Imagination

Sullivan's view that imagination is the primary creative faculty of the human mind — the power by which individuals envision possibilities that do not yet exist and, joined with will, transform those visions into reality.

## Definition

For Sullivan, imagination is not idle fantasy but the fundamental instrument of human creation. He describes it as "the faculty of constructive imagination — without which the pursuit of architecture would be in vain" (012-029). Imagination is what allows one to see beyond the immediately given, to project what could be. Its opposite is conformity to habit and convention: Sullivan repeatedly pits imagination against the dead weight of tradition, positioning it as the active principle that makes genuine creation possible. In his mature formulation, the "immense growth in power of constructive imagination and of the will to do" (014-077) represents humanity's highest achievement.

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan's conception of imagination drew primarily from direct experience rather than formal philosophy. The years preceding his decision to become an architect were "essentially scatterbrained" — yet within this formless period, his "imagination, unknown to him as such" operated amid his "keen power of observation," his "insatiable hunger for knowledge at first hand," and his "utterly childlike flashes of intuition" (007-036). He would later recall a landscape "which awoke imagination" (012-009). This pre-intellectual immersion in nature grounded Sullivan's later conviction that imagination is born in direct sensory encounter with reality, not in abstraction.

### Sullivan's Development

Sullivan traces imagination through his own biography as a through-line. As a boy, he "had been busy in a world of his own, creating out of imagination those things which had appeared to him to be beautiful" — a domain "that imagination and will had bodied forth out of himself" (011-030). The childhood habit of world-building never left him. He reports that the "interrupted story of imagination and will, again renewed its deep refrain" (013-010) in his professional maturity. Crucially, Sullivan insists childhood imagination is not something to outgrow: "childhood, thus banished, remains sequestered within us unchanged" (010-002). The mature creator simply reconnects with the imagination that was always there.

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> "He had been busy in a world of his own, creating out of imagination those things which had appeared to him to be beautiful, and had transformed, to the limits of his capacity, this world of his own into a domain that was his own, that imagination and will had bodied forth out of himself." (011-030)

> "The immense growth in power of constructive imagination and of the will to do." (014-077)

> "He possessed, indeed, to a perceptible degree, the faculty of constructive imagination — without which the pursuit of architecture would be in vain." (012-029)

> "Man, not knowing himself, and none else knowing him, lived as a mirage, within a world of mirage which he fancied real. It was real for him; for such is the habit of man's imagination in playing tricks with him in his credulity." (014-006)

### In Architecture

Architecture is the arena where Sullivan's imagination was tested against physical reality. The architect must first envision a building in its entirety — its proportions, its ornament, its integration of function and form — and then through sustained labor bring that vision into material existence. This is what Sullivan means by "constructive imagination": the capacity to hold a clear mental image of a structure that does not yet exist and to guide it through to completion (012-029). His architectural practice was, by his own account, "of exemplar in the use of the imagination and the will, doggedly to carry out a program" (011-036). Imagination provides the vision; will provides the persistence to execute it.

Sullivan's buildings themselves testify to the "infinite fertility of man's imagination, evoked by his changing needs" (012-039) — each structure a unique solution arising from the marriage of imaginative vision and practical necessity.

## Legacy

Sullivan's elevation of imagination as the indispensable creative faculty influenced his most famous student, Frank Lloyd Wright, who extended the idea into his own philosophy of organic architecture. But beyond direct architectural lineage, Sullivan's insight belongs to a broader American tradition that prizes the individual's power to make something from nothing — the Emersonian self-reliance translated into the domain of building. His insistence that "man contains within himself limitless creative powers" (014-013) and that the city should give "vent to its wondrous imagination, its deep creative instinct, its romance" (014-091) remains a call to treat imagination not as ornament but as the engine of civilization itself.
