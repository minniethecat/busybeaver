## Definition

In Sullivan's philosophy, instinct represents the primal,
pre-rational source of creativity — a force most visible in
childhood, before education and social convention overlay it.
He saw instinct as the foundation of all genuine artistic
expression [004-009] [004-010].

## Instinct and Childhood

Sullivan located the purest form of instinct in the child.
The child lives "almost wholly in the world of instinct,"
responding directly to experience without the mediating
layers of abstract thought:

> almost wholly in the world of instinct. Whatever there was of intellect
> [004-010]

He saw childhood instinct as "an unalloyed childhood instinct,
an intuition and a childhood faith" — a state of pure, undiluted
engagement with the world [013-014].

## Creative Instinct

Sullivan identified instinct as the deep source of creative
power. The architect's task was not to suppress instinct but to
liberate and direct it:

> the liberation of those instincts within us which are akin to
> creative power [014-063]

He described "the amazing world of instinct in the child whence
arises genius" — connecting instinct directly to the highest
forms of human achievement [014-065].

## Instinct and Imagination

For Sullivan, instinct was inseparable from imagination. He spoke
of "imagination, its deep creative instinct, its romance" as a
unified force that drove artistic creation [014-091].

The recrudescent (re-emerging) power of instinct was something
to be "directed into worthy channels" rather than suppressed —
a central principle of his educational philosophy [014-102].
