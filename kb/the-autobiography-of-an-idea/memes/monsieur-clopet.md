# Monsieur Clopet

Monsieur Clopet, Sullivan's mathematics tutor in Paris during his preparation for the Ecole des Beaux-Arts entrance examinations in 1874, whose method of demonstration left a permanent imprint on Sullivan's approach to architecture.

## Biography

### Role at the Beaux-Arts

Monsieur Clopet was recommended to Sullivan by the American Legation in Paris as "the very best tutor in mathematics" (012-004) when Sullivan arrived with only six weeks to prepare for the Beaux-Arts entrance examinations. A "small dark man, who, to Louis's joy, spoke only French" (012-005), Clopet conducted a class of approximately twenty young men, mostly French, with a few students from other countries but "no Englanders, no other American" (012-009).

### Teaching Method

Clopet's method was distinctive and rigorous. In their first meeting, he took Sullivan's textbook on Descriptive Geometry, turned its pages, and then threw it into the waste basket with the declaration: "Our demonstrations shall be such as to admit of no exception!" (012-007). This dramatic gesture encapsulated Clopet's pedagogical philosophy: understanding came not from memorizing textbooks but from following demonstrations that were "so simple, so inclusive, so completely rounded as to preclude exception" (012-011).

The hallmark of Clopet's approach was the personal address: "Louis was especially pleased at the novelty of saying *je dis* -- 'I say' -- at the beginning of a demonstration. It humanized matters, brought them home, close up, a sort of challenge" (012-012). For Sullivan, the difference between the formal impersonal "The sum of the angles of any triangle equals two right angles" and Clopet's "I say the sum of the angles ..." was profound: "The former statement one may take or leave. The latter is a personal assertion and implies, 'I will show you'" (012-012).

Clopet's course covered mainly descriptive geometry and the science of arithmetic, with plane and solid geometry as incidentals. The class followed a rhythm: a half-hour of lecture, then recess in which Clopet "drew forth his pouch and his little book of rice papers; so did the others. There was sauntering, spectacular smoking and conversation" (012-013). After recess, students worked at the blackboard for the final half-hour.

## Relationship with Sullivan

Sullivan's six weeks under Clopet's tutelage were among the most intense of his life. He worked eighteen hours a day in his seventh-floor room, "a candle at each side, black coffee and wet towel as aids" (012-015), codifying Clopet's notes, memorizing a hundred new French vocabulary words daily, and reading history by the hour. At the midpoint, Sullivan's brain "seemed to be overcome by a fog. Everything was blurred as in a mist, his memory lost its grip" (012-015) -- a classic case of overtraining, which he remedied with three days of diversion.

Clopet's confidence in Sullivan was publicly expressed: "I don't know as to the rest of you, but there is one among you who will pass brilliantly in his mathematics, and he is an American" (012-016). This prediction was borne out in the entrance examination, where Sullivan, after an "heartbreaking crisis," suddenly "found himself actually *thinking* in terms of mathematics, and, accordingly, lost all fear, relaxed and let his mind go free" (012-022). The examining professor congratulated him: "I felicitate you, Monsieur Sullivan: you have the mathematical imagination which is rather rare" (012-022).

Sullivan was astonished: "of all things Louis might have said he did *not* possess, the mathematical imagination would head the list" (012-023). He credited Clopet's freedom of method and his own "enthusiasm at each beautiful demonstration, and the many pointed questions he asked of Monsieur Clopet" (012-023) for opening this unexpected dimension of his mind.

### The Enduring Lesson

Clopet's influence extended far beyond mathematics. Years later in Chicago, confronted with the seemingly insoluble engineering problem of the tall office building, Sullivan recalled: "This is what Monsieur Clopet really meant when he said to Louis in his Paris student days: 'Our demonstrations will be such as to admit of no exception.' Monsieur Clopet carried the principle no further than his mathematics, but Louis saw in a flash the immensity and minuteness of its application" (015-058). The principle that every problem contains and suggests its own solution -- and that the solution, once reached, is "invariably found to be simple in nature, basic, and clearly allied to common sense" (015-058) -- became a cornerstone of Sullivan's architectural method.

Sullivan also internalized the temporal scale of Clopet's method: "He saw that a 'Clopet demonstration' meant a matter of years of work and growth" (013-024). The architectural system Sullivan would spend decades developing was, in this sense, a Clopet demonstration writ large -- a "well reasoned process ... so simple, so inclusive, so completely rounded as to preclude exception" (012-011).

## Significance

Monsieur Clopet represents the convergence of French analytical rigor with Sullivan's native American empiricism. From Clopet, Sullivan absorbed not specific mathematical content -- he remained suspicious of abstraction, convinced that "all abstractions were assumptions -- that abstract truth was a mirage" (012-012) -- but a method: the conviction that understanding meant being able to demonstrate, personally and without appeal to external authority, why something was so.

The image of Clopet throwing the textbook into the waste basket became, in Sullivan's mental economy, a symbol of intellectual independence. Just as Clopet had taught mathematics without a book in sight, "making something teachable out of what at first seemed an abstraction in three dimensions" (012-011), Sullivan would teach himself to design buildings from first principles, letting each problem dictate its own unique solution.
