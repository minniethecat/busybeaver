Masonry — the art and technique of building with stone, brick, and mortar — represented for Sullivan both the ancient foundation of architecture and a construction method whose limits defined an era.

## The Masonry Period

Sullivan's early career coincided with the culmination of load-bearing masonry construction. His design for the Auditorium Building in Chicago represented what he called "the culmination of Louis's masonry 'period'" (015-044). This massive structure, "with its immense mass of ten stories, its tower, weighing thirty million pounds," exemplified both the achievements and the inherent constraints of solid masonry construction (015-054).

## Technical Limits and Innovation

By the late 1880s, Sullivan recognized that masonry had reached its practical limit for tall buildings. "Masonry construction, in so far as tall buildings were concerned, was a thing of the past, to be forgotten, that the mind might be free to face a new condition" (015-027). The Monadnock Building, a sixteen-story solid masonry office building by Burnham and Root, represented "the first and last word of its kind; a great word in its day, but its day vanished almost over night" (015-054). The inherent limitation was clear: "its ever thickening walls ate up ground and floor space as the height increased" (015-056).

## Transitional Forms

Sullivan's own work bridged the transition from masonry to steel-frame construction. His early experiments "led him to use slender piers, tending toward a masonry and iron combination, the beginnings of a vertical system" (013-035). This hybrid approach — masonry walls with iron reinforcement — pointed toward the steel skeleton frame that would liberate tall buildings from the tyranny of the load-bearing wall.
