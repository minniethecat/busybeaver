# Moses Woolson

Moses Woolson, teacher and master at the English High School in Boston, was the single most important educational influence on the young Louis Sullivan. Sullivan's tribute was unambiguous: "There may have been teachers and teachers, but for Louis Sullivan there was and could be only one" (009-040).

## Biography

### Early Life and Teaching

Little is recorded of Woolson's birth or early life outside Sullivan's memoir. He presided over a classroom of about forty boys on the second floor of the English High School in Boston, where Sullivan entered in the early 1870s. Sullivan described the room as "dingy rather than gloomy," with "individual desks in rows facing north" and "a long blackboard" on the east wall (009-016).

### Career and Method

Woolson was not a conventional educator. Sullivan's portrait is frank:

> "Moses Woolson was not a deep thinker, nor was Moses Woolson erudite or scholarly, or polished in manners, or sedate. Rather was he a blend of wild man and of poet. But of a surety he had the art of teaching at his fingertips and his plan of procedure was scientific to a degree, so far beyond the pedagogic attainments of his day that he stood unique, and was cordially hated by his craft as lambs might fear and hate a wolf." (009-032)

Sullivan described Woolson as "a 'human dynamo,' a man ninety-nine percent 'efficient'" whose "one weakness was a temper he all too often let escape him, but his high strung, nervous makeup may be averred in part extenuation, for this very makeup was the source of his accomplishment and power: He surely gave in abundance, with overflowing hands, all that he had of the best to give" (009-032).

Woolson's classroom "partook of the nature of a university" (009-035). Professor Asa Gray of Harvard came occasionally to lecture on botany, attending "out of regard for Moses Woolson's love of the science" (009-035). Woolson was also "a sincere and ardent champion of womankind," inserting perorations on "Women's Rights" into every lecture (009-035).

His taste and reach extended to European literature. When Woolson announced that "the best existing history of English literature was written by a Frenchman, one Hyppolite Taine," he provoked in Sullivan a transformative intellectual awakening: the concept of "culture" as "the genius of a people" -- "their innate qualities and powers of heart and mind" (009-037). Sullivan recorded this as a conversion experience: his "whole being floated o'er the sea to distant France" and the word "culture" became "a living word -- a sheer veil through which, at first, he could but dimly see" (009-038).

### Relationship with Sullivan

The Woolson-Sullivan relationship was intense and productive, though not warmly personal. Sullivan was clear: "for Moses Woolson, the master and the man, he felt neither love nor affection, and it is quite likely that the master felt much the same toward him. What he felt toward the man was a vast admiration, he felt the power and the vigor of his intense and prodigal personality" (009-040).

Sullivan's "object was to get every ounce of treasure out of Moses Woolson" (009-040). And treasure he got:

> "By the end of the school year Moses Woolson through genius as a teacher, had turned a crudely promising boy into, so to speak, a mental athlete. He had brought order out of disorder, definition out of what was vague, superb alertness out of mere boyish ardor; had nurtured and concentrated all that was best in the boy; had made him consciously courageous and independent; had focused his powers of thought, feeling and action; had confirmed Louis's love of the great out of doors, as a source of inspiration; and had climaxed all by parting a great veil which opened to the view of this same boy, the wonderland of Poetry." (009-039)

The gift was lasting. The "cumulative reciprocity" of that year of intensive training produced in Sullivan "a sense of obligation and of gratitude so heartfelt, so profound, that it has remained with him in constancy throughout the years" (009-040).

## Significance

Moses Woolson's importance to Sullivan's story cannot be overstated. Sullivan himself connected Woolson's training to every subsequent triumph. When Sullivan succeeded in Frank Furness's office despite a hostile first interview, he reflected: "without that elastic alertness and courage, that grimness Moses Woolson imparted, it is sure that Louis would not have broken through the barrier of contempt" (010-036). When he prepared for the Beaux-Arts entrance examinations in Paris in six weeks of eighteen-hour days, his self-reliance rested on the assurance: "Had he not been trained in discipline and self-discipline by Moses Woolson?" (012-004). When he made "things hum" in Adler's office, he "put through this work with the efficiency of combined Moses Woolson and Beaux Arts training" (013-028).

Sullivan even speculated that no other teacher could have compensated for Woolson's absence. At MIT, "neither immaculate Professor Ware nor sweaty, sallow, earnest Eugene Letang was a Moses Woolson" (010-026). The thought produced in Sullivan "a sort of despair": "Ah, if but Moses Woolson had been versed in the story of architecture as he was in that of English Literature, and had held the professorship; ah, what glowing flame would have come forth" (010-026).

Moses Woolson represents the ideal of the transformative teacher in Sullivan's autobiography -- the figure who does not merely impart knowledge but shapes the mind that receives it, equipping it for a lifetime of independent thought and creative work. Sullivan's lifelong gratitude to this single year of teaching testifies to Woolson's rare gift and to Sullivan's own capacity for recognizing and honoring his intellectual debts.
