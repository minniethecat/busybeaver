# Michelangelo

Michelangelo Buonarroti (1475--1564), the Italian Renaissance master whose Sistine Chapel frescoes became a decisive proving ground for Sullivan's confidence in his own powers of visual perception during his formative Paris years.

## Biography

### Early Life and Career

Michelangelo di Lodovico Buonarroti Simoni was born in Caprese, Italy, in 1475. A sculptor, painter, architect, and poet, he stands among the defining figures of the High Renaissance. His most celebrated works include the Pieta, the colossal David, and the fresco cycle on the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel in the Vatican -- an undertaking so immense that it required four years of solitary labor on scaffolding.

### The Sistine Chapel

The Sistine Chapel ceiling, completed between 1508 and 1512, depicts scenes from the Book of Genesis -- the Creation of Adam, the Fall, the Flood -- surrounded by prophets, sibyls, and ignudi. Decades later, Michelangelo returned to paint the altar wall with *The Last Judgment* (1536--1541), a vast and turbulent vision of the Second Coming. It was this latter work, and the question of its artistic decline relative to the ceiling, that brought Sullivan to Rome.

## Relationship with Sullivan

Sullivan's encounter with Michelangelo came not through direct study of art history but through a crisis of self-trust sparked by the French critic Hippolyte Taine. While preparing for the Beaux-Arts entrance examinations in Paris, Sullivan read Taine's *Philosophy of Art in Italy*, in which Taine asserted that Michelangelo's *Last Judgment* was "obviously done on momentum, as compared with the vigor of the ceiling" (012-033).

For Sullivan, who "had never trusted the care of his eyesight to anyone, nor did he now propose to entrust it in M. Taine's keeping," this claim was alarming. "He was averse to taking things on say-so. It was his pride that he could see" (012-033). The question was existential: if he could not detect so subtle a change in a great artist's work -- a change that Taine pronounced *obvious* -- what value did his own visual judgment possess? "The worth of his whole scheme seemed to rest in this delicate balance. It was vital. There must be no doubt. He must, beyond question, be sure of the quality of his eyesight" (012-033).

Sullivan traveled to Rome specifically to verify Taine's claim. Standing in the Sistine Chapel, he found that what Taine described as "obvious" was, in fact, immediately apparent to his own eye: "The Sistine Chapel! One steady sweep of the eye! It was easy -- oh, so easy! So self-evident! Thus a cumulating agony ended forever" (012-034). In the presence of Michelangelo's work, Sullivan experienced what he described as "that great and glorious personality ... power as he had seen it in the mountains, in the prairies, in the open sky, in the soul of man" (012-035).

## Significance

Michelangelo became one of the figures Sullivan "appropriated to himself" as a standard of greatness. Years later in Chicago, Sullivan would list Michelangelo alongside Moses Woolson, Richard Wagner, and John Root as personalities he had "annexed to his collection of assets -- or, if one so wills to put it -- to his menagerie of personalities great and small" (015-003). The Sistine Chapel episode was formative: it confirmed that Sullivan's own visual powers were reliable, that he could trust his eye -- a confidence essential to his later career as an architect who insisted on seeing for himself rather than accepting received opinion.

The Michelangelo episode also established a pattern that would recur throughout Sullivan's life: the journey undertaken to verify a claim personally, the refusal to accept authority on "say-so," and the conviction that direct visual experience -- not book learning -- was the true foundation of artistic judgment.
