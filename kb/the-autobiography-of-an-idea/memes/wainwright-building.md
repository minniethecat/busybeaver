# Wainwright Building

The Wainwright Building, completed in 1891 in St. Louis, Missouri, is a nine-story office structure designed by [[Louis H. Sullivan]] of the firm [[Adler & Sullivan]]. It is recognized as the first building to give authentic architectural expression to the new steel-frame form of construction in its exterior treatment (015-027), and stands as a landmark demonstration of Sullivan's principle that [[form-follows-function]].

## Overview

The Wainwright Building was commissioned at a pivotal moment in architectural history — the transition from load-bearing masonry to steel-frame construction. While the steel frame had first been applied by Holabird & Roche in the Tacoma Office Building in Chicago, it was Sullivan who recognized and articulated the revolutionary implications of the new engineering for architectural design. The Wainwright Building embodies Sullivan's conviction that the steel frame demanded "an equally revolutionary architectural mode" (015-027), and its vertical emphasis — continuous piers rising unbroken from base to cornice — established a compositional formula that would influence skyscraper design for generations.

> "He felt at once that the new form of engineering was revolutionary, demanding an equally revolutionary architectural mode. Masonry construction, in so far as tall buildings were concerned, was a thing of the past, to be forgotten, that the mind might be free to face and solve new problems in new functional forms." (015-027)

## Design

### Architect

Louis Sullivan designed the Wainwright Building as a partner in [[Adler & Sullivan]]. By this time, Sullivan had returned from his convalescence in Ocean Springs, Mississippi, refreshed and ready to work "with his old-time vim" (015-027). The building represents Sullivan at the height of his creative powers, applying his philosophy of [[form-follows-function]] to an entirely new structural type.

### Architectural Features

The Wainwright Building's most significant architectural innovation was its exterior treatment of the steel frame. Sullivan rejected the masonry-era language of thick piers, superimposed orders, and load-bearing expression. Instead, he emphasized vertical continuity — the piers rising unbroken from the second story to the cornice, expressing the steel frame's capacity for height without thickening walls at the base (015-027). Key features include:
- A pronounced vertical emphasis through continuous piers
- Rejection of superimposed classical orders in favor of functional expression
- Terracotta ornament concentrated at the spandrels and cornice, not structurally deceptive
- A clear tripartite division: base (entrance), shaft (office floors), and capital (cornice)

### Philosophy Embodied

Sullivan declared that "the old ideas of superimposition must give way before the sense of vertical continuity" (015-027). This was a direct application of the principle that form follows function: the steel skeleton's function of carrying load through a cage of columns and beams should be expressed in the building's exterior form, not hidden behind masonry facades imitating bearing walls. The building puts into practice Sullivan's broader conviction that "every problem contains and suggests its own solution" (015-028).

## History

### Commission & Construction

The Wainwright Building was commissioned in [[st-louis]] during a period when Sullivan's reputation had extended beyond Chicago. The city of St. Louis had recently been surpassed by Chicago in population and was eager to assert its own commercial prominence (015-068). Sullivan treated the commission as an opportunity to demonstrate his theories about the relationship between structure and architectural expression.

### Reception

The Wainwright Building was immediately recognized as a departure from convention. Sullivan himself noted it was in St. Louis that the steel frame "was given first authentic recognition and expression in the exterior treatment" (015-027). While eastern architects were "appalled" by the steel frame and "could make no contribution to it," Sullivan and his Chicago contemporaries "welcomed the steel frame and did something with it" (015-062).

## Significance

The Wainwright Building stands as a landmark in the history of skyscraper design. It demonstrated that the steel frame was not merely an engineering convenience but the basis for a new architectural language. The building's vertical emphasis — the "sense of vertical continuity" Sullivan insisted upon — would influence skyscraper design for generations, establishing a compositional formula (base, shaft, capital) that became the template for tall building design worldwide.

More fundamentally, the Wainwright Building embodied Sullivan's [[democracy|democratic]] architectural philosophy: the honest expression of structure was a democratic act, refusing to hide new technology behind the costumes of dead cultures. In this sense, the Wainwright — modest in scale but revolutionary in conception — was the architectural opposite of the [[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893|World's Columbian Exposition]] that would follow two years later, with its systematic concealment of structure behind classical scenography.
