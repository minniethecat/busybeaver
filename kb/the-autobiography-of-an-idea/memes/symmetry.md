Symmetry — the balanced arrangement of parts around a center or axis — entered Sullivan's consciousness through his father's passion for dance and became a recurring motif in his thinking about art, rhetoric, and philosophical systems.

## Symmetry in Dance

Sullivan's father, Patrick Sullivan, was a dancing master who had trained in London and Paris. "To him the art of dancing was a fine art of symmetry, of grace, of rhythm; but parallel to this ran a hunger for Nature's beauty" (001-012). This paternal appreciation for formal balance — the body in harmonious relation to space and music — provided the young Sullivan with his earliest exposure to aesthetic principles embodied in living movement rather than abstract theory.

## Symmetry as Power

Beyond aesthetics, Sullivan recognized symmetry as a source of rhetorical and social power. He observed that his maternal grandfather's preaching "gave pith, point and skilled direction to those collective aspirations and fears, which otherwise would have lacked symmetry and power" (003-020). Symmetry, in this sense, was not merely decorative order but structural coherence — the quality that transforms scattered impulses into compelling force.

## The Danger of Empty Symmetry

Sullivan also perceived the limitations of symmetry when it became an end in itself. He noted that certain philosophers "merely built an elaborate scaffolding, but no edifice within, and each was more concerned with the symmetry of his scaffold than with aught else" (011-031). This critique — echoed in his architectural philosophy — warned against the hollow formalism that prioritizes surface order over inner substance. True symmetry, for Sullivan, must arise from within, as the natural expression of a coherent idea, not as an externally imposed pattern.
