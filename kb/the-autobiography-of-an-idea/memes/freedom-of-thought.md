# Freedom of Thought

Sullivan's lifelong conviction that the human spirit must be liberated from convention, tradition, and external authority before genuine creative work — in architecture or any art — can flourish.

## Definition

For Sullivan, freedom of thought was not an abstraction but a practical prerequisite. He held that "man's spirit must be free that his powers may be free to accomplish in beneficence" (013-025). This conviction was not merely philosophical — it was born from his direct experience of architecture as a profession that, in his view, suppressed creative individuality under the weight of precedent and received wisdom. He discovered "to his annoyance, that in the architectural art of his day, the spirit of man was not free, nor were his powers so liberated and so trained that he might create in beneficence" (013-025). The remedy he demanded was absolute: "no architectural dictum, or tradition, or superstition, or habit, should stand in the way" (013-034).

Sullivan extended this principle beyond architecture to civilization itself. He saw that "the free spirit of man — so long self-suppressed under the dead weight of the 'consecrated wisdom of the ages' and its follies" was the force struggling to emerge in modern life (014-062). Freedom of thought, in his view, was the ground from which all genuine achievement must grow.

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan's commitment to intellectual freedom was shaped by his reading of John Draper's *Intellectual Development of Europe*, which traced "the emergence and the growth of science as the spirit of man sought and found freedom in the open" (013-025). Draper's broad division of European history into an "Age of Faith" and an "Age of Reason" reinforced Sullivan's emerging conviction that received authority — whether of religion or of architectural academies — suppressed the creative spirit. John Edelmann's "theory of suppressed functions" provided a complementary framework: the idea that social and institutional forces systematically suppress innate human capacities (011-025, 013-025). As Sullivan reflected, "for centuries it had been the case that art had been belittled in superstitions called traditions — and lived on by virtue of a thin and baseless faith" (013-025).

### Sullivan's Development

Sullivan's insistence on freedom of thought emerged early and grew through direct confrontation with institutional authority. As a schoolboy confronting his headmaster over a poem he considered "bunkum," the young Louis "spread out his views — so long suppressed; he pleaded for the open, for honesty of thought for the lifting of a veil that hid things, for freedom of thought, for the right of interpretation, for freedom of utterance. He passionately unbosomed his longings" (007-044). The headmaster, after hearing him out, called it "a pretty fine stump-speech" — an early validation that honest expression, even when defiant, could prevail (007-044).

At the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and later in practice, Sullivan saw how formal education could deaden rather than liberate creative instinct. His reading of Darwin and Spencer deepened his conviction that authentic growth came from within — from "the creative power and glory of man" (010-023) — not from imposed systems. By the time he formed his partnership with Dankmar Adler in 1880, Sullivan had resolved to "cast aside all pedantry, all the artificial teachings of the schools, the thoughtless acceptance of inane traditions, of puerile habits of uninquiring minds" (013-035). His faith "lay indeed in freedom" (010-023).

### In Architecture

Sullivan's demand for creative freedom translated directly into architectural practice. He insisted that "the architectural art to be of contemporary immediate value must be plastic; all senseless conventional rigidity must be taken out of it; it must intelligently serve — it must not suppress" (013-034). When his experimental vertical system using slender piers "upset all precedent," and contemporaries regarded him as "an iconoclast, a revolutionary," Sullivan was unmoved: "If a thousand proclaimed him wrong, the thousand could not change his course" (013-035). His buildings were to be demonstrations of freedom in action — forms growing "naturally out of the needs and expressing them frankly, and freshly" (013-034).

In his partnership with Adler, Sullivan secured the conditions for this freedom: each partner would "have initiative and final authority in his own field, without a sharp arbitrary line being drawn" (013-033). This arrangement was itself an architectural expression of his conviction that creative independence was non-negotiable.

## Legacy

Sullivan's demand for intellectual freedom in architecture prefigured the modernist rejection of historical styles. Yet his vision went beyond mere stylistic liberation. He saw freedom of thought as the basis for a democratic civilization: "Thus the living idea of man, the free spirit, master of his powers, shall find its form-image in a civilization which shall set forth the highest craftsmanship, the artistry of living joyously in stable equilibrium" (014-067). His insistence that creative work must arise from inner conviction rather than external authority — that the architect must be free to think — became an unspoken premise of twentieth-century architectural practice, even when his specific philosophical language fell out of fashion.
