# Boston

The city of Louis Sullivan's birth on September 3, 1856, where he spent his early childhood and received his first formal education, and whose social and cultural atmosphere he found confining.

## Location

Boston is situated on the Atlantic coast of Massachusetts, in New England. Sullivan was born at "22 South Bennett Street, Boston, Mass., U.S.A." in a city that in the mid-nineteenth century was already an established center of American culture, education, and commerce (001-005). The city lay approximately thirty miles south of the family farm near Newburyport (001-003).

## In Sullivan's Life

### First Encounter

Sullivan was born in Boston to an Irish father and a Swiss-French mother. His father, Patrick Sullivan, had reached Boston in 1847 after passage from London "in the good ship Unicorn" and "set up an academy" for teaching (001-016). His mother, Andrienne List, came from a family that had settled in the Boston area, where her own mother — Sullivan's maternal grandmother — gave "private lessons in French to certain brahminical circles" (003-010).

### Significance

Boston represented both Sullivan's origin and the world he would need to escape. As a small boy, he experienced the city as oppressive. Sullivan later wrote that "the miasma of the big city poisoned a small boy acutely sensitive to his surroundings. He mildewed; and the leaves and buds of ambition fell from him" (006-030). The city seemed "a thing already in decay" compared to the vibrant energy he had experienced in Newburyport summers (006-030).

He attended public schools in Boston, including the Rice Grammar School, before moving on to the English High School — a period he described with little enthusiasm. Boston winters were particularly grim: "As the Boston winter of '64 was groaning on its way to the tomb of all winters," his mother fell ill with diphtheria (006-032). Yet Boston also provided his earliest exposure to formal learning: he studied at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology briefly before departing for wider horizons (006-033).

After his grandfather's farm summers and the liberating experience of Newburyport, Sullivan returned to Boston each fall, each time finding the contrast more stark: "Newburyport departed—Boston came" (005-036).

### Sullivan's Description

> "As one might move a flourishing plant from the open to a dark cellar, and imprison it there, so the miasma of the big city poisoned a small boy acutely sensitive to his surroundings. He mildewed; and the leaves and buds of ambition fell from him." (006-030)

## Architectural Context

Nineteenth-century Boston was an established city of brick and granite, defined by its colonial and Federal-era architecture. Unlike Chicago's dynamic rebuilding, Boston represented tradition and settled order — the very constraints Sullivan would later argue architecture must transcend. The Boston City Hall and State House exemplified the formal neoclassicism that dominated American institutional architecture before the Chicago School's innovations.
