# Richard Wagner

Richard Wagner (1813–1883), German composer of operas and music dramas, whose sweeping orchestral works profoundly moved Louis Sullivan and became for him a symbol of the creative power of the free artistic spirit.

## Biography

### Early Life

Wilhelm Richard Wagner was born in Leipzig, Germany, in 1813. His early musical education was sporadic, but by his late teens he had committed himself to composition. He absorbed the influence of Beethoven and Weber while developing his own radical conception of opera as a Gesamtkunstwerk — a total work of art unifying music, drama, and visual spectacle. By the 1840s, Wagner had composed *Der fliegende Holländer*, *Tannhäuser*, and *Lohengrin*, works that would later electrify the young Louis Sullivan in a Philadelphia concert hall (011-027).

### Career

Wagner's mature career saw the creation of his monumental cycle *Der Ring des Nibelungen* (*Das Rheingold*, *Die Walküre*, *Siegfried*, *Götterdämmerung*), as well as *Tristan und Isolde* and *Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg*. His harmonic innovations — particularly the extended chromaticism of *Tristan* — pushed Western music toward the dissolution of traditional tonality. He built the Bayreuth Festspielhaus specifically for the performance of his works, a testament to his vision of art as a total, immersive experience. His music was still being introduced to American audiences in the 1870s and 1880s, when Sullivan first encountered it through the Chicago concerts of Hans Balatka (011-028).

### Relationship with Sullivan

Sullivan's discovery of Wagner was a revelation. One hot summer evening in Philadelphia, attending a Theodore Thomas concert at the Academy of Music, Sullivan "had become listless, when of a sudden came the first bars of a piece so fiery, that, startled, all alert, he listened in amazement to the end" (011-027). Consulting the program, he read: "Vorspiel, Third Act, Lohengrin — Richard Wagner." His immediate response was: "Who was Richard Wagner? Why had he never heard of him? He must look him up; for one could see at a glance that this piece was a work of genius" (011-027).

In Chicago, his friend John Edelmann took him to the North Side Turner Hall, where Hans Balatka and his orchestra "is introducing Wagner to Chicago" (011-028). There Sullivan heard the Pilgrims' Chorus — "and raved" (011-029). Week after week, he absorbed the Vorspiel to *Lohengrin*, to *Die Meistersinger*, to the *Flying Dutchman*, the *Ride of the Walkyrie*, the overture to *Tristan und Isolde*, Siegfried's *Tod*, and the "exquisite shimmering beauty of the Waldweben" (011-029). Sullivan emerged an "ardent Wagnerite" (011-030).

## Significance

For Sullivan, Wagner embodied a Mighty Personality — "a great Free Spirit, a Poet, a Master Craftsman, striding in power through a vast domain that was his own, that imagination and will had bodied forth out of himself" (011-030). Wagner's music lifted a veil, revealing "the enormous power of man to build as a mirage, the fabric of his dreams, and with his wand of toil to make them real" (011-030). Sullivan's heart was stirred and his courage "ten-folded in this raw city by the Great Lake in the West" (011-030).

Wagner's significance to Sullivan was not merely musical but philosophical: the composer modeled what the architect aspired to become — a creator who built from within, whose works were organic expressions of a single unifying vision. Sullivan later listed Wagner alongside Moses Woolson and Michelangelo as personalities he "appropriated to himself and considered as a part of himself" (015-003). Wagner represented the concentrated creative power that Sullivan sought to embody in architecture.
