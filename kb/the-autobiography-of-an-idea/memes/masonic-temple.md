# Masonic Temple (Chicago)

The Masonic Temple was a landmark skyscraper in Chicago designed by [[John Wellborn Root]] of the firm [[burnham-and-root]]. At the time of its completion in 1892, it was the tallest building in the world, and its name helped introduce the word "skyscraper" into the American vocabulary. (015-068)

## Overview

The Masonic Temple rose twenty-one stories at the corner of State and Randolph Streets in Chicago. It represented the peak of masonry construction for tall buildings — an engineering and architectural achievement that pushed load-bearing walls to their absolute limit. The building combined retail space at its base, office floors in its shaft, and a prominent roof garden and observation deck at its summit. (015-068)

## Design

### Architect

The Masonic Temple was designed by [[John Wellborn Root]], the creative force of the firm Burnham & Root. Root was a "redheaded, large bullet-headed" man of "keen sense of humor" — an artist who valued novelty and who, by Sullivan's assessment, "had it in him to be great." (015-003) His partner [[Daniel Burnham]], by contrast, was obsessed with "the feudal idea of power" and "the largest, the tallest, the most costly and sensational." (015-004) The Masonic Temple satisfied both partners' ambitions: Root's artistic drive and Burnham's megalomania. Though Root died in January 1891 at age forty-one, before the building's completion, his design endured as his most visible legacy. (015-011)

### Architectural Features

The Masonic Temple was a masonry skyscraper of remarkable height achieved through increasingly thick bearing walls at its base. Key features included:
- Twenty-one stories — the tallest occupied building in the world at completion (015-068)
- Load-bearing masonry construction, marking the extreme limit of the type
- A prominent roof garden and observation gallery
- Commercial arcade at street level
- Hybrid structure incorporating iron elements alongside masonry piers

### Philosophy Embodied

The Masonic Temple represented the last great assertion of masonry construction at its maximum height — and, paradoxically, its epitaph. Sullivan observed that as buildings pushed "higher and higher, until the Masonic Temple by John Root had raised its head far into the air," the word "skyscraper" came into use. (015-068) But the inherent economic and structural limits of masonry — ever-thickening walls that "ate up ground and floor space of ever increasing value" (015-057) — meant the Masonic Temple was a dead end. The future lay with the steel frame, which Sullivan would embrace in the [[Wainwright Building]].

## History

### Commission & Construction

The Masonic Temple was built during Chicago's great building boom of the early 1890s, when the city's population had surged to over a million, land values soared, and "its system of building had become known as the 'Chicago Construction.'" (015-068) The project reflected the "great enthusiasm and public spirit" of Chicago in 1890–1892, fueled by a collective dream "that their city should become the world's metropolis." (015-068)

### Reception

Upon completion, the Masonic Temple crowned Chicago's skyline and became a symbol of the city's ambition. Its height made it an international sensation. For Sullivan, however, the building also represented a cautionary tale about the limits of partnership between artist and merchant. He noted with concern the "friction of ideas between the artist and the merchant" in Burnham & Root — "a significant mismating which made him ponder." (015-010) Root's premature death in 1891 left the firm unbalanced, and Burnham's commercial ambitions would soon reshape the direction of American architecture at the [[World's Columbian Exposition]].

## Significance

The Masonic Temple was the last word in masonry skyscrapers — "its day vanished almost over night," as Sullivan wrote of the similar [[Monadnock Building]], "leaving it to stand as a symbol, as a solitary monument, marking the high tide of masonry construction as applied to commercial structures." (015-054) The building also crystallized the divergent paths of Chicago's two great architectural firms: Adler & Sullivan, pursuing a democratic architecture of functional expression, and Burnham & Root (later D.H. Burnham & Co.), pursuing scale, spectacle, and the classical revival that Sullivan would later denounce as having "died" the architectural art. (015-086)
