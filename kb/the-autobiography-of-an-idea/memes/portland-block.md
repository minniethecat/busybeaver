# Portland Block

Four-story commercial structure in post-fire Chicago whose study provided the young Sullivan with an early, unexpected lesson in the uncertain attribution of architectural work.

## Overview

The Portland Block was among the earliest substantial commercial buildings erected in Chicago after the [[Great Chicago Fire of 1871]]. Standing four stories in height, it was considered an outstanding example of post-fire construction and attracted the attention of young architects seeking to understand the emerging commercial style. Sullivan encountered the building during the aftermath of the devastating financial panic of 1873, when "in spite of the panic, there was stir; an energy that made him tingle to be in the game" [011-015]. He made a careful study of the building as part of his self-directed architectural education [011-016] [011-017].

The Portland Block was prominently located "at Washington and Dearborn Streets," constructed of "pressed brick and sandstone" [011-016]. It emerged during a period when Chicago was still recovering from the dual blows of "a great fire, and a panic in finance" — a load that Sullivan acknowledged "made load enough for any community to carry, but Chicago, hard hit, bore up bravely" [011-016].

## Sullivan's Study

In his autobiography, Sullivan recalled examining the Portland Block among Chicago's notable early buildings and being struck by its quality. "Outstanding among these was the Portland Block," he wrote, describing it as "a four-story structure of pronounced character" [011-016]. The building's architectural merit was such that Sullivan assumed it to be the work of a recognized master of the Chicago School. When he inquired, he was told the architect was "Jenney: Major Jenney; or in full, Major William Le Baron Jenney" [011-016]. Sullivan later characterized Jenney as "a free-and-easy cultured gentleman, but not an architect except by courtesy of terms," noting that his "true profession was that of engineer" and that he had been trained at the Ecole Polytechnique in France [011-018].

Sullivan "learned incidentally" a different truth: the Portland Block had in fact been designed not by Jenney but "by a clever draftsman named Cudell" [011-017]. "This gave him a shock," Sullivan wrote. "For he had supposed that all architects made buildings out of their own heads, not out of the heads of others" [011-017]. The discrepancy between reputed and actual authorship was an early education in the profession's less visible realities. Yet Sullivan drew a pragmatic lesson from the revelation: "the new knowledge cheered him in this hope: That he might some day make buildings out of his head for architects who did not have any heads of their own for such purpose" [011-017].

## Architectural Significance

The Portland Block exemplified the rapid rebuilding of Chicago's commercial district after the 1871 fire. Its four-story scale was typical of the immediate post-fire generation — substantial enough to signal the city's recovery, yet modest compared to the skyscrapers that would follow within a decade. The building's influence on Sullivan was methodological rather than stylistic: it taught him to look beyond surface reputation and to trust his own analytical eye when assessing architectural quality. The episode also introduced him to the informal studio culture of Chicago, where a draftsman like Cudell could produce work of such quality that it was publicly attributed to the firm principal — a dynamic Sullivan would later navigate himself as a designer within Adler & Sullivan [011-017]. Jenney's office itself was a convivial place where draftsmen addressed the Major as "our esteemed benefactor" [011-021], reflecting the blurred line between principal and staff that made the Portland Block misattribution possible.
