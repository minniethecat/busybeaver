# John Edelmann

John Edelmann, architect and thinker, was Louis Sullivan's closest friend during his formative Chicago years and the intellectual catalyst for Sullivan's most important philosophical breakthrough: the theory of suppressed functions. Sullivan's later tribute was unambiguous -- "the benefactor and Louis the parasite and profiteer" (011-032).

## Biography

### Early Life

Little is recorded of Edelmann's birth and family background. He emerged in Sullivan's narrative as a fellow draftsman in the office of Major William Le Baron Jenney in Chicago during the early 1870s. Edelmann was already a formed personality when Sullivan encountered him -- a voracious reader, an original thinker, and a man of volcanic intellectual energy.

### Career

Edelmann worked as a draftsman and architect in Chicago, spending time in Jenney's office alongside Sullivan. Sullivan described the anarchic atmosphere of that office when the Major was absent: "John Edelmann would mount a drawing table and make a howling stump speech on greenback currency, or single tax" (011-021), while Sullivan would sing oratorios at the top of his voice.

His architectural career was uneven. He attempted farming in Iowa, but "the game played him instead" (013-019). He returned to Chicago, appeared at lunch "clad a l'outrance as a farmer, for his usual theatrical effect" (013-019), and quickly resumed his intellectual influence over Sullivan. He eventually entered into partnership with "a man named Johnson, who did school work" (013-027), but his lasting contribution was not in built works but in ideas -- specifically, the theory of suppressed functions that transformed Sullivan's thinking.

### Intellectual Influence

Edelmann's intellectual reach was extraordinary. Sullivan described him as "a thinker, a profound thinker, a man of immense range of reading, a brain of extraordinary keenness, strong, vivid, that ranged in its operations from saturnine intelligence concerning men and their motives, to the highest transcendentalisms of German metaphysics" (011-024). As "an immediate psychologist, never before or since has Louis met his equal in vitality, in verity, and in perspicacity of thought" (011-024).

Edelmann introduced Sullivan to Wagner's music through the North Side Turner Hall concerts of Hans Balatka, deepening Sullivan's immersion in the artistic currents reshaping European culture. He was also a passionate democrat who "believed in his soul" that the Auditorium Theatre "to be democracy" (015-015).

### Relationship with Sullivan

The friendship between Edelmann and Sullivan was intense, unequal, and formative. Sullivan, by his own account, was the "practised listener" to Edelmann's torrential monologues. Edelmann was "mercurial -- an inventor of self-moods -- a poseur, infatuated with the pessimistic attitudinizing he assumed at will" (011-032). "On the whole, he was jolly bombastic, much alive, and in public, loud of speech in an overweening beggary to attract attention, and thereby feed his hungry vanity" (011-032). Yet beneath the theatrical vanity was genuine devotion: "withal he was Louis's warm friend, and showed it by a devotion and self-sacrifice singular in one so absorbed in self worship" (011-032).

The central gift Edelmann gave Sullivan was the theory of suppressed functions. When Edelmann explained it, Sullivan "startled, saw in a flash that this meant the real clue to the mystery that lay behind the veil of appearances" (011-025). The idea would later recur to Sullivan through John Draper's *Intellectual Development of Europe*, and Sullivan would recognize that "for centuries it had been the case that art had been belittled in superstitions called traditions -- and lived on by virtue of a thin and baseless faith -- and John Edelmann's theory of suppressed functions, recurred to him as broadly set forth, in confirmation, in Draper's heavy work" (013-025).

Edelmann also played a crucial practical role in Sullivan's career, brokering the partnership with Dankmar Adler. In early 1879, Edelmann told Sullivan that "Adler had cut loose from Burling" and that "this was Louis's opportunity" (013-027). He had "talked the matter over several times with Adler, that Adler was cautiously and eagerly interested, but timid in making advances" (013-027).

## Significance

John Edelmann is one of the most important figures in Sullivan's intellectual autobiography -- not as a builder but as a thinker who gave Sullivan the conceptual vocabulary he needed. The theory of suppressed functions became the intellectual seed from which Sullivan's master principle, "form follows function," would grow. Sullivan acknowledged this debt with remarkable candor:

> "The passing years have isolated and revealed John Edelmann, as unique in personality among fine and brilliant minds. Be assured he will not turn in his grave, unless in bliss, should he hear it said that he was the benefactor and Louis the parasite and profiteer." (011-032)

Edelmann's belief that the Auditorium Theatre embodied democracy in built form also shaped Sullivan's fusion of democratic idealism with architectural practice. Without Edelmann, there would have been no suppressed functions theory; without suppressed functions, Sullivan's philosophy would have lacked its organizing principle.
