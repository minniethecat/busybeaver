## Definition

The Chicago School was the architectural culture that emerged in
Chicago after the Great Fire of 1871 — not a formal school with
a doctrine, but a milieu of ambitious builders, engineers, and
architects who created the conditions for the modern skyscraper
and a new American architecture. Sullivan encountered this world
as a young man in 1873, arriving by train from the East and
immediately sensing the city's raw vitality [011-001].

The term "Chicago School" is a retrospective label coined by architectural
historians in the twentieth century. Sullivan himself never used the phrase —
the corpus contains no occurrence of "Chicago School" as a compound term.
What he described instead was a concrete milieu: a specific city with
specific practitioners, specific technical innovations, and a specific
spirit that he witnessed and participated in firsthand. As a young
architectural apprentice, Sullivan found in Chicago not a theory but a
living laboratory where the conditions of modern urban life were
generating new architectural forms in real time.

## Post-Fire Chicago

Sullivan found Chicago "magnificent and wild: A crude extravaganza:
An intoxicating rawness: A sense of big things to be done." The
city had risen "phoenix-like from its ashes" after the 1871 fire,
and though many ruins remained, the ambition of recovery was
everywhere [011-002].

The city's self-image was summed up in superlatives — biggest
grain market, biggest lumber market, greatest railroad center.
Sullivan observed:

> Chicago had had the biggest conflagration "in the world." It
> was the biggest grain and lumber market "in the world." It
> slaughtered more hogs than any city "in the world."
> [011-002]

Beneath the braggadocio, Sullivan perceived "the primal power
assuming self-expression amid nature's impelling urge." He
concluded: "These men had vision. What they saw was real, they
saw it as destiny" [011-002].

The city's resilience was no mere boast. Sullivan noted that "a
great fire, and a panic in finance, certainly made load enough
for any community to carry, but Chicago, hard hit, bore up
bravely" [011-016].

Sullivan also observed the gritty texture of daily life in the
recovering city. He noted that a certain functional illiteracy was
"almost universally in evidence in Chicago," and he assumed this
reflected the city's frontier character — a place where practical
competence mattered more than formal cultivation [011-018].

## Sullivan's Arrival

Sullivan came to Chicago by the Pennsylvania train, crossing the
great valley of the Susquehanna and arriving at the city that would
define his career [010-042]. His father had preceded him, moving
the family from Boston to Chicago in 1869 while leaving young Louis
behind to complete his education [006-042]. Before his father's
departure, Louis had confided his heartfelt desire to become an
architect — a pivotal conversation that set the course of his
life [007-034].

When Sullivan finally arrived in 1873, Chicago was still marked
by the devastation of the Great Fire two years earlier. He had
received prompt word of the disaster while still in the East,
and the news had stirred in him a mixture of concern and
fascination [009-066]. What he found upon arrival was a city
already in the grip of reconstruction — a phenomenon that
impressed him more than any architectural study could have done.

## The Architectural Profession

When Sullivan arrived, Chicago's architecture was largely
undistinguished. He observed that "almost all the buildings in
these cities were of the same crassness of type; a singularly
sordid, vulgar vernacular in architectural speech" [011-005].

A few practitioners stood out. Major William Le Baron Jenney,
designer of the Portland Block, was "a free-and-easy cultured
gentleman" who had trained at the Ecole Polytechnique in France
and served as a Major of Engineers in the Civil War. Sullivan
noted that Jenney was "not an architect except by courtesy of
terms. His true profession was that of engineer" [011-005].

Sullivan first encountered Jenney's name through a simple inquiry.
Walking the streets of Chicago and observing the buildings, he asked
a passerby about the designer of an impressive structure "and was
told the name was Jenney: Major Jenney; or in full, Major William
Le Baron Jenney" [011-016]. This casual introduction led to
Sullivan's employment in Jenney's office, where he would meet the
intellectual companions who shaped his early thinking.

The state of the building art when Sullivan arrived was primitive
by later standards. He later described the early Chicago
construction landscape:

> The earlier days had been given over to four-inch ashlar fronts,
> cylinder glass, and galvanized iron cornices, with cast iron
> columns and lintels below; with interior construction of wood
> joists, posts and girders; continuous and rule-of-thumb
> foundations of 'dimension stone.'
> [015-047]

Plate glass and mirrors came from Belgium and France, rolled iron
beams from Belgium, Portland cement from England. "Interior
equipments such as heating, plumbing, drainage, and elevators or
lifts, were to a degree, primitive" [015-047].

## Competing Firms

By the early 1880s, two firms had emerged as the defining
practices of the Chicago architectural world: Burnham & Root, and
Adler & Sullivan. Sullivan characterized the contrast between
them with psychological precision:

> Thus, there came into prominence in the architectural world of
> Chicago two firms, Burnham & Root, and Adler & Sullivan. In
> each firm was a man with a fixed irrevocable purpose in life,
> for the sake of which he would bend or sacrifice all else.
> Daniel Burnham was obsessed by the feudal idea of power. Louis
> Sullivan was equally obsessed by the beneficent idea of
> Democratic power.
> [015-005]

Sullivan saw John Root as "so self-indulgent that there was risk
he might never draw upon his underlying power," while Dankmar
Adler was "essentially a technician, an engineer, a conscientious
administrator." In the dream-imagination, Sullivan concluded,
"lay Burnham's strength and Louis's passion" [015-005].

Burnham's organizational instinct led him to recognize the coming
age of industrial consolidation before his peers: "The only
architect in Chicago to catch the significance of this movement
was Daniel Burnham, for in its tendency toward bigness,
organization, delegation, and intense commercialism, he sensed
the reciprocal workings of his own mind" [015-065].

The spirit of these years was one of confident ambition. Sullivan
recalled that "the spirit of Chicago of those days" was
characterized by a "large, free, generous, and liberal" feeling
that permeated the building trades and the architectural offices
alike [015-014].

## Intellectual Ferment

Sullivan's time in Jenney's office was less significant for its
architectural training than for his friendship with John Edelmann,
a foreman whom Sullivan recognized as "a thinker, a profound
thinker, a man of immense range of reading" [011-010].

Edelmann introduced Sullivan to Wagner's music at the North Side
Turner Hall, where Sullivan experienced the "enormous power of
man to build as a mirage, the fabric of his dreams, and with his
wand of toil to make them real" [011-012]. The introduction came
with a simple invitation: "Hans is introducing Wagner to Chicago;
let's go" — and they went [011-028].

Most consequentially, Edelmann's theory of "suppressed functions"
gave Sullivan a pivotal insight. When Edelmann explained the
concept, Sullivan "saw in a flash that this meant the real clue
to the mystery that lay behind the veil of appearances." The
word "function" — detonated by "suppressed" — illuminated "his
inner and his outer world as one" [011-010].

## Skyscraper Technology

### The Steel Frame

The technological breakthrough that defined the Chicago School
was the steel frame. Sullivan identified its first application in
Chicago: "It was first applied by Holabird & Roche in the Tacoma
Office Building, Chicago." He immediately grasped its
significance: "He felt at once that the new form of engineering
was revolutionary, demanding an equally revolutionary
architectural mode. That masonry construction, in so far as tall
buildings were concerned, was a thing of the past, to be
forgotten, that the mind might be free to face and solve new
problems in new functional forms" [015-027].

The steel frame was initially a matter of commercial vision:
"The Chicago activity in erecting high buildings finally
attracted the attention of the local sales managers of Eastern
rolling mills; and their engineers were set at work. ... Thus
the idea of a steel frame which should carry all the load was
tentatively presented to Chicago architects" [015-060].

The reception was divided along regional lines. "The architects
of Chicago welcomed the steel frame and did something with it.
The architects of the East were appalled by it and could make no
contribution to it" [015-062]. Sullivan argued that the tall
building's validity was contextual: "For the tall office building
loses its validity when the surroundings are uncongenial to its
nature; and when such buildings are crowded together upon narrow
streets or lanes they become mutually destructive" [015-062].

### The Tall Office Building Emerges

Sullivan saw the Chicago tall building as an organic product of
local conditions: "In Chicago the tall office building would seem
to have arisen spontaneously, in response to favoring physical
conditions, and the economic pressure as then sanctified,
combined with the daring of promoters" [015-063].

By the early 1890s, a recognizable local character had emerged:
"The art of design in Chicago had begun to take on a recognizable
character of its own. The future looked bright. The flag was in
the breeze" [015-064]. The city's system of building had become
known as the "Chicago Construction." Sullivan recorded the
milestone: "It was pushing its structures higher and higher,
until the Masonic Temple by John Root had raised its head far
into the air, and the word 'skyscraper' came into use" [015-068].

The underlying forces were demographic and economic. Chicago's
population had grown from a "pitiful 4,000 in 1837" to five
hundred thousand by 1880, and reached a million by 1890.
Sullivan set 1880 as "the zero hour of an amazing expansion." The
building industry took on "an organizing definition," and the
tendency in commercial buildings was "toward increasing stability,
durability, and height, with ever bettering equipment" [015-053].

## Chicago and Paris: A Generative Tension

Sullivan's experience of Chicago was shaped by his prior
experience in Paris, where he had studied at the École des
Beaux-Arts. He believed a productive tension existed "between
raw Chicago and finished Paris" — a polarity that fueled his
creative thinking throughout his career [012-017]. While Paris
offered the discipline of tradition and the refinement of
centuries, Chicago offered the freedom of the new and the
urgency of practical problems demanding immediate solutions.

The intellectual contrast between Chicago and the established
cultural centers of the East was a theme that ran through
Sullivan's thinking. He was acutely aware of the tension between
"raw Chicago and finished Paris" — a polarity that he believed
was generative rather than disabling [012-017]. Chicago's very
rawness freed its architects from the weight of inherited forms.

This tension was not merely abstract. Sullivan experienced it
concretely when, during his Paris sojourn, he encountered
"Chicago friends" who persuaded him to return to the city of
his destiny rather than remain in France [015-018]. The pull of
Chicago's unfinished character proved stronger than the charms
of the finished European capital.

## The World's Columbian Exposition and Its Aftermath

The Chicago School reached both its apex and its inflection point
with the World's Columbian Exposition of 1893. Chicago won the
competition to host the fair: "Chicago was ripe and ready for
such an undertaking. It had the required enthusiasm and the
will" [015-070]. The fair was to be "a dream city, where one
might revel in beauty. It was to be called The White City by the
Lake" [015-070].

But Sullivan perceived in the Exposition a threat to the
indigenous Chicago architecture that had been developing. He
wrote that "a small white cloud no bigger than a man's hand was
soon to appear above the horizon. The name of this cloud was
eighteen hundred and ninety-three" [015-064]. The classical
revival that the fair unleashed would, in his view, set back the
cause of a genuinely American architecture for decades.

## Legacy

The Chicago School represents a unique moment in architectural
history — a convergence of technological innovation (the steel
frame, the elevator, fireproofing), economic energy (post-fire
reconstruction, rapid urbanization), and creative ambition (the
desire for a distinctly American architecture) that produced the
modern skyscraper and laid the groundwork for twentieth-century
building practice. Sullivan's role in this movement was central:
he was both its most articulate theorist and, through the
Auditorium Building and other landmark structures, one of its
most accomplished practitioners.
