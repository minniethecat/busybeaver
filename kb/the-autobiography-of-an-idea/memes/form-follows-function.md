# Form Follows Function

Sullivan's architectural maxim that a building's external form should emerge organically from its internal purpose and structural needs, not from historical precedent or decorative convention.

## Definition

Sullivan crystallized this principle after "long contemplation of living things," arriving at the formula that "form follows function" — meaning in architectural practice that buildings designed from the inside out, expressing their utilitarian needs honestly, would naturally achieve authentic beauty (013-034). His conviction held that "the architectural art to be of contemporary immediate value must be plastic; all senseless conventional rigidity must be taken out of it; it must intelligently serve — it must not suppress" (013-034). He sought to make "an architecture that fitted its functions — a realistic architecture based on well defined utilitarian needs — that all practical demands of utility should be paramount as basis of planning and design; that no architectural dictum, or tradition, or superstition, or habit, should stand in the way" (013-034).

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan drew on a wide reading of contemporary scientific thought. Darwin's *Theory of Evolution* "seemed stupendous" to him, and Herbert Spencer's definition of progressive development "from an unorganized simple, through stages of growth and differentiation to a highly organized complex, seemed to fit his own case" (013-026). John Draper's *Intellectual Development of Europe* reinforced his conviction that "man's spirit must be free that his powers may be free to accomplish in beneficence" (013-025). He had discovered "to his annoyance, that in the architectural art of his day, the spirit of man was not free, nor were his powers so liberated and so trained that he might create in beneficence" (013-025).

### Sullivan's Development

The concept matured through Sullivan's partnership with Dankmar Adler beginning in 1880. At twenty-five, Sullivan became "a full-fledged architect before the world" (013-033) and resolved to "start on the course of practical experimentation he long had in mind" (013-034). John Edelmann's "theory of suppressed functions" provided an early intellectual framework (013-025), pointing Sullivan toward the insight that "the relationship of function and structure deepened rapidly" in his understanding (009-063). As Sullivan tested his ideas on actual buildings, his "conviction increased that architectural manipulation, as a homely art of discrimination, must give way to the new birth of an architecture of democracy, of the people, by the people, for the people" (013-035).

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> "He would put to the test a formula he had evolved, through long contemplation of living things, namely that form follows function, which would mean, in practice, that architecture might again become a living art, if this formula were but adhered to." (013-034)

> "In this wise the forms under his hand would grow naturally out of the needs and express them frankly, and freshly." (013-034)

> "He would brush them all aside, regardless of commentators." (013-034)

### In Architecture

Sullivan applied the principle experimentally across a series of mercantile structures in early 1880s Chicago. His immediate focus was maximizing daylight through "slender piers, tending toward a masonry and iron combination, the beginnings of a vertical system" — a method that "upset all precedent" (013-035). This systematic approach to design meant "the casting aside of all pedantry, of all the artificial teachings of the schools, of the thoughtless acceptance of inane traditions, of puerile habits of uninquiring minds" (013-035). Each building became a fresh application: "If a thousand proclaimed him wrong, the thousand could not change his course" (013-035).

## Legacy

Sullivan's maxim became the rallying cry of twentieth-century modernist architecture, influencing figures from Frank Lloyd Wright to the Bauhaus. Yet Sullivan insisted it was not mere utilitarianism — his vision encompassed "an architecture of democracy, of the people, by the people, for the people" (013-035), fusing democratic idealism with functional integrity. His philosophy "will be set forth in these closing chapters" of his autobiography (013-035), a testament to how deeply he identified with this principle as his life's intellectual work.
