Evolution as Sullivan understood it: not merely biological descent, but the unfolding of ideas, forms, and civilizations through progressive stages of growth and differentiation.

## Sullivan's Encounter with Evolutionary Thought

Louis Sullivan first encountered evolutionary theory through his reading of Darwin and Herbert Spencer during his formative years in Paris and Chicago. "In Darwin he found much food. The Theory of Evolution seemed stupendous" (013-026). Spencer's definition — "implying a progression from an unorganized simple, through stages of growth and differentiation to a highly organized complex" — resonated deeply with Sullivan's own intellectual development (013-026). He recognized in this framework a mirror of his own creative journey: "he had begun with a simple unorganized idea of *beneficent power*, and was beginning to see the enormous complexity growing out of it" (013-026).

## Evolution in Architectural Form

Sullivan applied the evolutionary metaphor to the development of architectural types. The tall commercial building, he argued, emerged through a process he described as "a flow, and which is frequently spoken of as evolution — a word fast losing its significance" (015-056). Just as biological organisms adapt to their environment, architectural forms evolve in response to new technologies and social needs. The steel frame rendered masonry construction obsolete for tall buildings, demanding "an equally revolutionary architectural mode" (015-027).

## From Biological to Cultural Evolution

For Sullivan, evolution was not confined to biology. The same principles of progressive differentiation applied to human society, art, and individual consciousness. A civilization's architecture, he believed, was the visible record of its evolutionary stage — a democracy demanded an architecture that expressed democratic ideals, not feudal forms dressed in modern materials.
