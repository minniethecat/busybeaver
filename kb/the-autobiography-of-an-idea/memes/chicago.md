# Chicago

The American city where Louis H. Sullivan established his architectural practice with Dankmar Adler and became a central figure in the birth of modern American architecture, rising phoenix-like from the Great Fire of 1871.

## Location

Chicago lies on the southwestern shore of Lake Michigan in northeastern Illinois, at the mouth of the Chicago River. In Sullivan's era, the city was a booming railroad hub and commercial center of the American Midwest, connected by rail to the eastern seaboard and the western frontier.

## In Sullivan's Life

### First Encounter

Sullivan arrived in Chicago in the early 1870s, taking the Pennsylvania train from the East. He first saw the great valley of the Susquehanna and the industrial landscape of Pittsburgh before arriving in the raw, ambitious city that would become his professional home (010-042). At his arrival, "personality meant little" in Chicago — the city was young, democratic, and defined by its furious energy of rebuilding (011-010).

### Significance

Chicago was the soil in which Sullivan's architectural philosophy grew. In the wake of the Great Fire of 1871, "Chicago had risen phoenix-like from its ashes," though "many ashes remained, and the sense of ruin was still blended with ambition of recovery" (011-012). Sullivan thought "it all magnificent and wild: A crude extravaganza: An intoxicating rawness" (011-012).

He found his first employment with Major William Le Baron Jenney, architect of the Portland Block, and later entered into partnership with Dankmar Adler in 1880. Here Sullivan developed and tested his architectural principles experimentally. The city's commercial boom provided a series of mercantile structures where Sullivan could work out "a basic process, a grammar of his own" — the immediate problem being "increased daylight, the maximum of daylight," which led him toward "slender piers, tending toward a masonry and iron combination, the beginnings of a vertical system" (013-035).

Chicago's booming economy and rapid reconstruction — despite the burden of "a bonded indebtedness that would have bankrupted any other city" — reflected a spirit that "bore up bravely" under the strain (011-016).

### Sullivan's Description

> "Chicago had risen phoenix-like from its ashes. But many ashes remained, and the sense of ruin was still blended with ambition of recovery. Louis thought it all magnificent and wild: A crude extravaganza: An intoxicating rawness." (011-012)

## Architectural Context

Chicago in the 1880s–1890s was the birthplace of the skyscraper and the Chicago School of architecture. Sullivan's office at 1600 Auditorium Tower became a laboratory for vertical architecture. Major buildings included the Portland Block (011-016), the Auditorium Building, and Sullivan's own experimental mercantile structures along the Chicago River branches (013-009).

Sullivan's last Chicago residence was at "Thirty-second Street and Michigan Avenue and Twenty-first Street, Chicago," on the southwest corner, where he lived during his final working years (015-002).
