# Charles Darwin

Charles Darwin (1809–1882), English naturalist and geologist, whose theory of evolution by natural selection, published in *On the Origin of Species* (1859), provided Louis Sullivan with a scientific foundation for his belief that form develops organically from within according to function.

## Biography

### Early Life

Charles Robert Darwin was born in Shrewsbury, England, in 1809. After an unremarkable education at Edinburgh and Cambridge, he accepted an invitation to serve as naturalist aboard HMS *Beagle* on a surveying expedition (1831–1836). His observations of geological formations, fossils, and the variation of species across the Galápagos Islands and South America planted the seeds of his evolutionary theory.

### Career

Darwin spent decades refining his theory, accumulating evidence from barnacles, pigeons, and botanical experiments. The publication of *On the Origin of Species* in 1859 transformed biology and placed humanity within the continuum of natural history. The book argued that species are not fixed but change over time through the mechanism of natural selection — variations that aid survival are preserved and accumulate, producing the branching tree of life. The implications extended far beyond biology, reshaping philosophy, theology, and social thought. Sullivan, reading Darwin in the 1870s as part of an intensive course in scientific literature, found that "In Darwin he found much food" (013-026).

### Relationship with Sullivan

Sullivan encountered Darwin alongside Spencer, Huxley, Tyndall, and "the Germans" in a self-directed reading program that opened before him "a new, an enormous world ... whose boundaries seemed destined to be limitless" (013-016). While Spencer provided a philosophical framework of progressive development, Darwin provided the biological evidence: living forms are not imposed from without but emerge through natural processes operating over vast spans of time. For Sullivan, "The Theory of Evolution seemed stupendous" (013-026). Darwin's work lent scientific authority to Sullivan's architectural intuition that buildings, like organisms, must develop their forms from inner necessity.

## Significance

Darwin's evolutionary biology was a cornerstone of Sullivan's organic architectural philosophy. The principle that form follows function — which Sullivan "had evolved, through long contemplation of living things" (013-034) — drew upon Darwin's demonstration that every feature of an organism serves a purpose, shaped by the demands of its environment. Darwin's vision of nature as a dynamic, creative process rather than a static catalogue of fixed types matched Sullivan's own rejection of frozen architectural styles. Where Darwin had shown that species evolved through adaptation, Sullivan argued that architectural forms must evolve through honest response to human needs. Darwin thus stood, alongside Spencer, as one of the two great scientific influences on Sullivan's thinking about organic growth.
