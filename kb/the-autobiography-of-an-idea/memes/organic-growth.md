# Organic Growth

Sullivan's understanding, drawn from Herbert Spencer's evolutionary philosophy, that all genuine development — personal, architectural, cultural — proceeds from an unorganized simple through progressive differentiation toward an organized complex, not by arbitrary imposition of form but by an internal logic of unfolding.

## Definition

Sullivan encountered Herbert Spencer's evolutionary theory as part of a systematic course of reading that also included Darwin, Huxley, and Tyndall (013-016). The central insight he took from Spencer was that development follows a progression "from an unorganized simple, through stages of growth and differentiation to a highly organized complex" (013-026). This became for Sullivan far more than an abstract biological concept. He recognized in it a description of his own intellectual trajectory. He writes that Spencer's definition "seemed to fit his own case, for he had begun with a simple unorganized idea of beneficent power, and was beginning to see the enormous complexity growing out of it" (013-026). Organic growth meant that ideas, like organisms, possess their own inner principle of development — they cannot be forced; they must be nurtured.

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan's exposure to evolutionary thought began in his Chicago years in the late 1870s, after his return from the Ecole des Beaux Arts in Paris. His father gave him a copy of John Draper's *The Intellectual Development of Europe*, a work that traced "the emergence and the growth of science as the spirit of man sought and found freedom in the open" (013-025). Draper's broad historical sweep reinforced Sullivan's conviction that "man's spirit must be free that his powers may be free to accomplish in beneficence" (013-025). From this foundation, Sullivan "set forth, with a new relish, upon a course of reading covering Spencer, Darwin, Huxley, Tyndall, and the Germans, and found a new, an enormous world opening before him" (013-016). Among these thinkers, Spencer's developmental philosophy resonated most deeply because it provided a systematic framework for growth that could be applied across domains.

### Sullivan's Development

Sullivan applied the growth metaphor to himself repeatedly. After his move from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology to work in Philadelphia, he notes that "Louis became freshened with new growth as a tree in spring, and a new world unfolded to his replenished wonder" (008-010). This personal identification with organic process intensified during his Chicago years when, reflecting on his main intellectual project, "he saw that a 'Clopet demonstration' meant a matter of years of work and growth" (013-024). The idea was elusive, and "seemed to recede and grow larger even as he grew abler to deal with it" (013-024). Sullivan came to understand that intellectual maturation cannot be rushed: "it needs a long long time, and a rich soil of life-experience, to enable a simple, single idea to grow to maturity and solid strength" (013-015). The growth model offered patience — the insight from Whitman that "Nature neither hastens nor delays" (013-015) — and the confidence that steady development would yield clarity.

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> "Spencer's definition implying a progression from an unorganized simple, through stages of growth and differentiation to a highly organized complex, seemed to fit his own case, for he had begun with a simple unorganized idea of beneficent power, and was beginning to see the enormous complexity growing out of it, and enriching its meaning while insistently demanding room and nurture for further growth, until it should reach that stage of clarity through the depths of which the original idea might again be clearly seen, and its primal power more fully understood." (013-026)

> "Meanwhile his days were for work; his nights for study, for reflection, and gradual formulation of ideas subsidiary to the main Idea he was consciously now working out alone. This form of solitude did not disturb him. He saw that a 'Clopet demonstration' meant a matter of years of work and growth." (013-024)

> "And so Louis became freshened with new growth as a tree in spring, and a new world unfolded to his replenished wonder." (008-010)

### In Architecture

The organic growth concept underlay Sullivan's architectural practice. His formula that form follows function was itself an evolutionary insight: buildings, like organisms, should grow naturally from their conditions rather than having external forms imposed on them. He resolved that "the forms under his hand would grow naturally out of the needs and express them frankly, and freshly" (013-034). His design method "meant the casting aside of all pedantry, of all the artificial teachings of the schools, of the thoughtless acceptance of inane traditions, of puerile habits of uninquiring minds" (013-035). This was architecture conceived as a living process of development — what he later called a conviction that "such creative energy cannot arise from a welter of pallid abstractions as a soil, nor can it thrive within the tyranny of any cut and dried system" — it must "seek and find its own befitting form" (014-067). Sullivan's own evolution from the study of Gothic cathedrals to the design of steel-frame skyscrapers embodied the principle: a progression from historical imitation toward the "highly organized complex" of a distinctively modern American architecture.

## Legacy

Sullivan's concept of organic growth provided the philosophical foundation for what Frank Lloyd Wright later called "organic architecture" — the idea that buildings, like living things, should develop from within according to their own nature rather than conforming to external styles. But Sullivan's ambition was larger than architecture. His autobiography itself is structured as a growth narrative, tracing a single idea — the power of man — from its "mystical beginning in so small a thing as a child's heart" (013-015) through progressive stages to mature expression. This conviction that all great achievements follow natural developmental pathways, that institutions and civilizations as well as individual minds must be allowed to grow organically rather than be forced into predetermined molds, remains Sullivan's deepest philosophical legacy.
