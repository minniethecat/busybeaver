# Dream

The Dream, in Sullivan's thought, was not a mere nighttime fancy but a mode of creative vision — the capacity to see beyond the actual toward what might be, and to hold that vision with sufficient conviction that it could eventually be built. Dreams bridged childhood intuition and mature architectural achievement, forming the psychological substrate from which all significant creative work emerged.

## The Child's Dream

Sullivan's autobiography is rich with childhood dreams. His mother, "lost in dreamland she played on and on," filling the house with music while the young Louis listened (001-020). He experienced "fleeting trances, the sudden dreaming within a dream" (003-006) — moments when ordinary consciousness gave way to deeper perception. As a very young child, "he dreamed. Vague day dreams they were, half formed, formless" (004-004), dreams that prefigured his later capacity for architectural vision. Even in these early years, he found the world "still uniquely beautiful" in "free admiration" (004-013), a dream-like quality of perception he would later strive to preserve.

>

> His mother, lost in dreamland she played on and on, filling the house with melody. The child listened as though spellbound, the sounds entering his very being. (001-020)

Sullivan understood these early experiences not as incidental memories but as formative events. The child's capacity for dreamlike perception — for seeing the world with fresh, wondering eyes — was, for Sullivan, the seed of the architect's later creative power. What distinguished the creative adult was precisely the preservation, not the abandonment, of this childhood faculty.

## Dreams and Creative Vision

Sullivan understood the creative process as continuous with dreaming. His own "dreams, his feelings, his romances, his visions, had been formless — imprisoned for lack of words" (009-020) until education gave them shape. He spoke of "the dreams of childhood — that form of mystical illumination" (010-002) as a resource the architect must learn to access. The creative imagination, dormant in many adults, could "at imagination's rousing call, again and again return" (010-005) — the dream faculty was not lost but sleeping, awaiting reawakening.

The English High School's landscape, "a sort of landscape which awoke imagination" (012-009), provided the setting in which his dreaming faculty found external corroboration. This interplay between inner vision and outer confirmation was, for Sullivan, the essential dynamic of creative development: the dream must find its answering reality, and reality must be transformed by the dream.

>

> His dreams, his feelings, his romances, his visions, had been formless — imprisoned for lack of words — until his education began to supply the language through which they could take shape. (009-020)

## Dream as Prophecy

For Sullivan, dreams had a prophetic dimension. He dreamed of figures "of whom he had dreamed in his childhood, of whom he prophesied in his youth" (012-034), suggesting that childhood vision anticipates mature achievement. The impulses that drive genuine creation are "akin to the dreams of childhood, and which, continuing on through life" (014-063), become the architect's most reliable guide.

>

> He dreamed of figures of whom he had dreamed in his childhood, of whom he prophesied in his youth — figures who seemed to stand at the threshold of his future, beckoning. (012-034)

Dream was thus not escapism but aspiration — the faculty through which the possible first becomes visible, and without which no great architecture can be conceived. Sullivan's insistence on the continuity between childhood dream and adult creation was a central theme of his autobiography: the architect who loses touch with the dreaming faculty loses the very source of creative power.

## Dream and the Democratic Spirit

Sullivan connected dreaming to the democratic ethos that underpinned his architectural theory. The child's dream was inherently democratic — free, uncoerced, arising from individual perception rather than inherited authority. The feudal world, by contrast, suppressed dreaming through imposition and hierarchy. Sullivan saw in the capacity to dream a political as well as a psychological faculty: to dream freely was to think freely, and to think freely was the foundation of democratic life.

The impulses that arise from authentic dreaming are "akin to the dreams of childhood" and continue "on through life" (014-063), providing a through-line from the child's first perceptions to the architect's mature work. This continuity of vision — the refusal to let the dreaming faculty be extinguished by convention or education — was, for Sullivan, the mark of the genuine creator.
