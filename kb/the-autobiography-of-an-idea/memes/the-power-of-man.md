# The Power of Man

At the core of Louis Sullivan's entire worldview lay a single, unwavering conviction: that humanity possesses an enormous, innate power — the power to create, to build, to think, and to shape its own destiny. This was not a conclusion Sullivan reached through abstract reasoning but a primal intuition that arose in childhood and grew throughout his life into the philosophical foundation of his architecture, his democratic faith, and his vision of civilization itself. "This steadfast belief in the power of man was an unalloyed childhood instinct, an intuition and a childhood faith which never for a day forsook him, but grew stronger, like an indwelling daemon" (013-014).

## Definition

For Sullivan, the power of man was not a single faculty but a comprehensive vision of human capability. It encompassed the constructive imagination that envisions what does not yet exist, the creative will that doggedly carries a program through to completion, the intelligence and "increased power of manipulation of things and thought" (009-063) that enables mastery over materials and ideas, and the spiritual freedom that allows all these capacities to operate "in beneficence" (013-025). Sullivan understood power not as domination over others — which he identified as the feudal perversion of power — but as the generative, constructive capacity inherent in every human being: "the enormous power of man to build as a mirage, the fabric of his dreams, and with his wand of toil to make them real" (011-030).

This conception of power was inseparable from Sullivan's democratic vision. Where feudal thought saw power as concentrated in masters and institutions, Sullivan saw it distributed through the multitudes, "a new power of the multitudes everywhere at work" (014-062), awaiting only the freedom to express itself. The architect's task, as he conceived it, was to give this democratic power its visible form — to build structures that embodied humanity's creative capacity rather than structures that asserted the dominion of wealth or tradition.

## Origins

### Childhood Instinct

The power of man was, for Sullivan, first and always a childhood reality — not a concept learned from books but a truth felt directly. "As day by day passed on, he saw power grow before his eyes, as each unsuspected and new world arose" (013-014). The young Sullivan's world was filled with evidence: the orchards bursting into bloom each spring, speaking of a beneficent power in nature; the great bridges and engineering works he later encountered; the "wondrous powers, mysterious hidden powers" (013-014) he sensed within himself. This was not naivety but a mode of direct perception that Sullivan never abandoned. He "came in a manner to worship man as a being, a presence containing wondrous powers, mysterious hidden powers, powers so varied as to surprise and bewilder him" (013-014).

The experience was not merely intellectual but emotional and spiritual. Sullivan describes himself "again, in wonder" feeling "the power of man" — and adds that "the thought struck deep" (008-005). This was a formative moment: the power of man was not something he reasoned his way toward but something that struck him with the force of revelation. The childhood conviction that human beings possess limitless creative capacities became, in Sullivan's own words, a "faith which never for a day forsook him" (013-014).

### Growth Through Observation

As Sullivan matured, the childhood intuition was continually reinforced by experience. He encountered confirming evidence everywhere: in the rebuilding of Chicago after the Great Fire of 1871, where the "eternal spirit of man rose to the height of the call of calamity" (010-013); in the "two great engineering works" — the St. Louis bridge and the Kentucky River cantilever — that demonstrated "the function of man, the power of man, the power of man to accomplish" (013-013); in his reading of Darwin and Spencer, which showed how nature itself progressed from simple to complex through inherent creative power (013-026); and in Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel, which revealed "the power of the open — of the free spirit of man striding abroad in the open" (012-035).

The concept coalesced around what Sullivan called an "idea of power" (013-014). He saw "the powers of nature and the powers of man coalesce in his vision into an *idea of power*" (013-014) — a synthesis that was, he noted, a "complete reversal and inversion of the commonly accepted intellectual and theological concept of the Nature of man" (013-014). Where traditional thought saw man as fallen and powerless without external redemption, Sullivan saw man as innately powerful — a "free spirit" and a "creator" (014-030) whose history was the progressive unfolding of that power through civilization.

### Role in Sullivan's Architectural Philosophy

The power of man was not merely a philosophical abstraction for Sullivan — it was the operating principle of his architectural practice. Buildings were to be expressions of human creative power, not monuments to inherited convention. His famous maxim that "form follows function" (013-034) was itself an expression of this conviction: because human creative power was real and generative, architecture could trust the organic unfolding of form from need, rather than imposing forms borrowed from the past.

Sullivan's experimental approach to commercial architecture in the early 1880s — developing a "vertical system" of slender piers that "upset all precedent" (013-035) — was a direct application of his faith in human power. If humanity possessed the capacity to create new forms appropriate to new conditions, then the architect's duty was to exercise that capacity, not to retreat into historical imitation. His insistence that "no architectural dictum, or tradition, or superstition, or habit, should stand in the way" (013-034) was a declaration that human creative power must not be suppressed by dead convention.

This placed Sullivan in direct opposition to the academic classicism of his era. Where the Ecole des Beaux-Arts trained architects to apply received forms, Sullivan trained himself to generate forms from first principles — from the needs of the program, the nature of the materials, and the creative intelligence of the designer. Architecture's foundation, he insisted, was "Man and his powers" (013-035), not the accumulated precedents of the past. Every building was an opportunity to demonstrate anew what [[form-follows-function|form follows function]] meant in practice — that human creative power, freely exercised, could produce works of authentic beauty without borrowing from any historical style.

### Connection to Self-Determination

For Sullivan, the power of man reached its fullest expression in self-determination — the capacity of individuals and peoples to shape their own destiny. The "power of man's self-determination" (014-076) was the ultimate fruit of freed human creative power, the point at which personal capacity merged with democratic ideals. Sullivan saw this as the great drama of modern civilization: "the living idea of man, the free spirit, master of his powers, shall find its form-image in a civilization which shall set forth the highest craftsmanship, the artistry of living joyously in stable equilibrium" (014-067).

This connection between personal power and democratic self-governance was explicit in Sullivan's thought. He contrasted his own faith in democratic power with the feudal conception that his rival Daniel Burnham embodied: "Daniel Burnham was obsessed by the feudal idea of power. Louis Sullivan was equally obsessed by the beneficent idea of Democratic power" (015-005). The feudal idea meant architecture as domination — buildings that asserted the power of the few over the many. The democratic idea meant architecture as liberation — buildings that enabled and expressed the creative power of all.

Sullivan's concept of [[creative-will]] was the executive dimension of this power — the dogged persistence that translated vision into reality. His concept of [[man-the-spirit]] was its spiritual dimension — the conviction that man is fundamentally a free, creative being whose powers are "sound to the core" (014-050). And his concept of [[sympathy-with-nature]] was its receptive dimension — the capacity to learn from nature's methods rather than imposing arbitrary human will. Together, these formed a complete philosophy: humanity possesses innate creative power; that power must be freed from feudal suppression; once freed, it naturally expresses itself in beneficent, democratic forms; and architecture is the visible record of that expression.

## Legacy

Sullivan's conviction in the power of man — a childhood intuition that "grew stronger, like an indwelling daemon" across a lifetime (013-014) — became the philosophical engine behind American modern architecture. His most famous student, Frank Lloyd Wright, carried forward the faith that human creative power, freely exercised in sympathy with nature, could produce a genuinely American architecture. Yet Sullivan's vision of human power was broader than any single architectural movement. His insistence that "man contains within himself limitless creative powers" (014-013) and that these powers must be directed toward "beneficent results that shall endure" (014-085) speaks to enduring questions about the purpose of creative work, the relationship between individual capacity and social responsibility, and the nature of democracy itself. His closing testament — that the city should give "vent to its wondrous imagination, its deep creative instinct, its romance" (014-091) — remains an unfinished invitation: to build a world that matches the power Sullivan saw in humanity from the beginning.
