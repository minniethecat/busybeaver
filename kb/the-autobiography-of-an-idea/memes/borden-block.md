# Borden Block

The Borden Block was a six-story high-grade office building in Chicago, completed in 1880. It was among the three large commissions that came into Dankmar Adler's office shortly after [[Louis H. Sullivan]] took charge — and it represented Sullivan's first major opportunity to put his architectural ideas into independent practice. (013-028)

## Overview

The Borden Block arrived at a critical juncture in Sullivan's career. In early 1879, at the urging of [[John Edelmann]], Sullivan had met with [[Dankmar Adler]] and agreed to take charge of Adler's office, with the understanding that partnership would follow if things went well. (013-027) (013-028) Soon after Sullivan took hold, three substantial commissions arrived simultaneously: the Borden Block (a six-story office building), an up-to-date theater, and a large residence. (013-028)

## Design

### Architect

Louis Sullivan, at age twenty-three, designed the Borden Block while serving as head of Dankmar Adler's office. Though Adler & Sullivan would not formally exist as a firm until May 1, 1881, the Borden Block was effectively Sullivan's first independent architectural statement — his "first fine opportunity," which he "used" to the fullest. (013-028) Sullivan found in Adler "a most congenial co-worker, open-minded, generous-minded" who gave him "every opportunity to go ahead on his own responsibility." (013-028)

### Architectural Features

As a six-story office building, the Borden Block represented the highest grade of commercial construction then emerging in Chicago. Sullivan applied his combined training from Moses Woolson's disciplined methods and the Ecole des Beaux-Arts to produce a building of exceptional quality. (013-028) The building featured:
- Six stories of high-grade commercial space
- Masonry construction typical of the pre-steel-frame era
- A top-floor suite that became the offices of D. Adler & Co. and, from May 1, 1881, of Adler & Sullivan, Architects (013-033)

### Philosophy Embodied

The Borden Block was Sullivan's first opportunity to implement his emerging architectural philosophy in practice. He "put through this work with the efficiency of combined Moses Woolson and Beaux Arts training." (013-028) Though still within the masonry tradition, the building allowed Sullivan to begin the "course of practical experimentation" he had long contemplated — "to make an architecture that fitted its functions — a realistic architecture based on well defined utilitarian needs." (013-034)

## History

### Commission & Construction

Sullivan "took hold and made things hum" after assuming leadership of Adler's office. (013-028) The Borden Block was one of three large orders that arrived in rapid succession, signaling the commercial revival of Chicago's building industry after the lingering effects of the Panic of 1873. Sullivan used the opportunity to demonstrate his capacity for managing significant commissions independently. (013-028)

### Reception

The success of the Borden Block and its companion commissions cemented the partnership between Sullivan and Adler. Adler, pleased with Sullivan's performance, initiated the formal partnership: "How would you like to take me into partnership?" (013-029) The contract, drafted by Sullivan on a sheet of office stationery and signed by Adler, began on May 1, 1880, with Sullivan receiving one-third in the first year and an equal share thereafter. (013-031) (013-032) On May 1, 1881, the firm moved into "a fine suite of offices on the top floor of the Borden Block aforesaid" and formally became Adler & Sullivan, Architects. (013-033)

## Significance

The Borden Block holds a unique place in architectural history as the building where Adler & Sullivan was born. It was on the top floor of this building that the partnership that would produce the [[Auditorium Building]], the [[Wainwright Building]], and other masterpieces of American architecture was formally constituted. At age twenty-five, Sullivan "became a full-fledged architect before the world, with a reputation starting on its way." (013-033) The Borden Block thus marks the threshold between Sullivan's long years of preparation and his emergence as a leading figure in the Chicago School of architecture.
