Frank Furness (1839–1912) was a Philadelphia architect and partner in the firm Furness & Hewitt (010-028), where Louis Sullivan began his professional career in 1873 (010-029). A Medal of Honor recipient from the Civil War, Furness was known for his forceful personality, distinctive red beard (010-029), and an architectural approach that Sullivan later characterized as making "buildings out of his head" (010-034).

## Biography

### Early Life and Military Service

Frank Furness was born in Philadelphia in 1839, the son of the Unitarian minister and abolitionist William Henry Furness. He served with distinction in the Union Army during the American Civil War, earning the Medal of Honor for his actions at the Battle of Trevilian Station in 1864. His military background informed the direct, no-nonsense manner that Sullivan encountered when he first entered Furness's office (010-029).

### Career and Furness & Hewitt

After the war, Furness established the architectural firm of Furness & Hewitt with George Hewitt in Philadelphia (010-028). The offices occupied the entire top floor of a new brick building at the northeast corner of Third Street and Chestnut (010-038). Within the partnership, Furness and Hewitt represented contrasting architectural sensibilities: Furness was the forceful, intuitive designer, while George Hewitt was described by Sullivan as "a slender, moustached person, pale and reserved" who specialized in Victorian Gothic church architecture, working "as though he held his elements by pincers" to produce what Sullivan dismissively called "decorous sublimities of inanity, as per the English current magazines" (010-034).

Furness's own architectural approach was radically different from Hewitt's academicism. According to Sullivan's later recollection, Frank Furness "made buildings out of his head" (010-034) — a phrase of high praise from Sullivan, who valued original, intuitive creation over formulaic reproduction. Furness affected English fashion, "wore loud plaids, and a scowl," and possessed "a marvelous red beard, beautiful in tone with each separate hair delicately crinkled from beginning to end" (010-029).

### Relationship with Sullivan

Sullivan's tenure at Furness & Hewitt began in characteristically direct fashion. When Louis arrived in Philadelphia, he "made up his mind that next day he would enter the employ of said Furness & Hewitt, they to have no voice in the matter, for his mind was made up" (010-029). He presented himself to Frank Furness and announced he had come to work there (010-029).

Furness, whom Sullivan's friend John Edelmann had described as a "dog-man" with "the growl of a dog and the heart of a dog" (010-031), was initially skeptical. He criticized Sullivan for ignorance and conceit (010-030), but was intrigued when Louis stood his ground, declaring that he "belonged to that rare class who were capable of learning, and desired to learn" (010-031). Furness relented and offered a trial.

The trial began with a week, then was extended (010-033). Sullivan's first task was to retrace a complete set of plans for a Savings Institution to be erected on Chestnut Street (010-033). His performance evidently satisfied Furness, who told him he could "stay as long as you like" (010-033). Sullivan's time at the firm, however, was cut short by economic circumstances: in November 1873, Furness regretfully told him, "the jig is up. There'll be no more building. The office now is running dry" (010-041). True to his character, Furness slipped money into Louis's hand as he left, saying, "You've done well, mighty well. I like you" (010-041).

## Significance

Frank Furness occupies a pivotal position in Sullivan's autobiography as the first practicing architect under whom Sullivan worked professionally (010-029). The experience at Furness & Hewitt shaped Sullivan's understanding of what an architect could be: a forceful, intuitive creator whose work came from personal vision rather than academic formula (010-034). Sullivan would later recall this period when, upon arriving in Chicago, he learned that buildings could be designed by draftsmen rather than by the named architect — a discovery that "gave him a shock. For he had supposed that all architects made buildings out of their own heads, not out of the heads of others. His experience in the office of Furness & Hewitt, in Philadelphia, it seems, had given him an erroneous idea" (011-017).

This "erroneous idea" — that the architect is a personal creative force — became the foundation of Sullivan's entire architectural philosophy (010-034). Furness, with his loud plaids and glorious red beard (010-029), his growl and his heart (010-031), his intuitive approach to building (010-034), and his personal integrity in letting Sullivan go with both money and praise (010-041), served as Sullivan's first model of what an architect should be.
