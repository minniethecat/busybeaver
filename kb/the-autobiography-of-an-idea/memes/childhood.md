# Childhood

Childhood, in Louis Sullivan's philosophical framework, was not merely a phase of life but the wellspring of all genuine creative power. Throughout *The Autobiography of an Idea*, Sullivan returns repeatedly to childhood as the source of instinct, imagination, and the authentic impulses that animate great architecture. His treatment of childhood is distinctive: it is neither nostalgic nor sentimental, but philosophical — an argument that the child's mode of perception is the template for the artist's and that the loss of childhood consciousness is the fundamental tragedy of conventional adulthood.

## Childhood as Creative Foundation

Sullivan's own father rose "alone and unaided, out of a childhood of poverty" [001-013], establishing a family narrative in which childhood hardship forged character. For Sullivan himself, the formative power of childhood lay not in deprivation but in its unalloyed instincts. He reflected that "the power of man was an unalloyed childhood instinct, an intuition and a childhood faith" [013-014], locating the origin of human creative capacity in the pre-rational consciousness of the child. In his own development, "in childhood his idols had been the big strong men" [013-013] — the engineers and builders whose works impressed upon him the dignity of construction.

This identification of creative power with childhood instinct is central to Sullivan's architectural philosophy. The architect who designs from convention rather than instinct has, in Sullivan's terms, lost contact with the childhood source of creativity. The authentic architect, by contrast, works from the same unalloyed impulse that animated the child's first encounters with the built world.

## Dreams and Sensibilities

Sullivan regarded the dreams of childhood as a form of "mystical illumination" [010-002] that the mature creator must preserve and cultivate. He insisted that one must "return again and again to his childhood's sensibilities and faith" [010-003], treating the child's mode of perception not as something to outgrow but as a faculty to be deliberately recovered.

This conviction extended to creative work in general: the impulses that drive artistic creation are "akin to the dreams of childhood, and which, continuing on through life" [014-063], constitute the deepest reservoir of imaginative power. The architect's task, in this light, is not to acquire new capacities but to recover and direct capacities that were present from the beginning. Education, properly understood, should protect and cultivate childhood sensibility rather than suppress it in favor of conventional knowledge.

## Memory and Return

The autobiography documents moments when Sullivan experienced a vivid return to childhood consciousness. He observed that "the delicate bloom of early childhood was passing, while the vigor of youth came on apace" [006-012], marking the transition with elegiac precision. He noted how adults, caught in habitual thought, were merely "regurgitating the thoughts of his childhood now become decayed and senile" [006-028] — a caution against allowing childhood insights to harden into dead convention.

The distinction Sullivan draws is crucial: childhood thought, kept alive and renewed, is the source of creativity; childhood thought, allowed to decay into unexamined habit, becomes the prison of convention. The same material — the impressions and insights of childhood — can become either the foundation of a living architecture or the dead weight of formula, depending on whether the adult mind engages it with fresh attention.

In moments of creative intensity, "his childhood would return to him with its freshness and its wonder" [015-069], a phenomenon Sullivan regarded as essential to the architect's continued vitality. He also looked back to figures "of whom he had dreamed in his childhood, of whom he prophesied in his youth" [012-034], suggesting that childhood vision anticipates mature achievement — that the child already contains, in germinal form, the full arc of the creative life.
