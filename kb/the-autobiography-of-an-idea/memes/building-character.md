# Building Character

Sullivan held that every genuine building, like every genuine person, must have character — an individual identity arising from within, not imposed from without. A building without character was a building without life, a mere envelope indifferent to its own existence.

## Definition

Building character, for Sullivan, meant the distinctive personality that emerges when a structure honestly expresses its unique conditions: its purpose, its site, its materials, its time, and the creative intelligence of its designer. Character was not a decorative afterthought but the natural result of integrity in conception and execution. Just as a river has "its special character" (008-012) and a person's character reveals itself through consistent action, a building's character reveals itself through the consistent logic of its form.

This concept directly opposed the nineteenth-century practice of applying interchangeable historical styles. A Gothic bank and a Renaissance library might differ in detailing, Sullivan argued, but neither possessed genuine character because neither arose from its own conditions — both were borrowed costumes.

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan's emphasis on character drew on Romantic notions of individuality and organic form. Nature was his constant reference: every tree, every river, every landscape possessed unmistakable character arising from its own nature. His grandfather's teaching — that one must look beyond surface appearances to understand the deeper character of things — was formative, as was his reading of Taine, who traced the character of artworks to the character of the civilizations that produced them.

### Sullivan's Development

Sullivan's sensitivity to character emerged early. Crossing the Hudson River for the first time, he was disappointed: "It seemed to lack what Louis had come to believe the character of a river" (008-006). The young Sullivan already expected landscapes to possess individuality — and judged them when they fell short.

His architectural education reinforced this instinct. Studying buildings of the past, he learned to see each as an expression of a specific people, place, and time — not as interchangeable examples of "style." A Romanesque church had Romanesque character because it was Romanesque in its bones, not because it wore Romanesque clothing.

In his own practice, "as buildings varying in character came under his hand, he extended to them his system of form and function" (013-035). Each building type — office tower, theater, tomb — had its own character because each served a different purpose and spoke a different emotional language.

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> It seemed to lack what Louis had come to believe the character of a river (008-006).

> Have you not, in your travels as a young man, observed the rivers each with its special character? (008-012).

> As buildings varying in character came under his hand, he extended to them his system of form and function (013-035).

> The great drama we herein have called the Modern... is in character so eye-opening as to constitute the first act in the drama of the universal brotherhood of man (014-078).

### In Architecture

Sullivan's buildings possess unmistakable character because each was conceived as an individual. The Auditorium Building is not interchangeable with the Wainwright Building; the Guaranty Building is not a copy of either. Each arose from a specific program, site, budget, and client — and each registers those conditions in its form.

His ornament was crucial to building character. Far from being a universal decorative language, Sullivan's ornament was tailored to each building's identity — geometric and masculine for commercial structures, organic and lyrical for cultural ones. The ornament was the building's voice, and every building had its own things to say.

The steel frame, which lesser architects treated as a generic armature for historical dress-up, became for Sullivan the very source of the modern building's character — its verticality, its structural honesty, its urban presence.

## Legacy

Sullivan's insistence on building character stands as a corrective to both historicist pastiche and modernist uniformity. His argument that every building should be an individual — that architecture is the creation of persons, not the application of formulas — resonates in contemporary movements that value place-specific, purpose-specific design over globalized repetition.
