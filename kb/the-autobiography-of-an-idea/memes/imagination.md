## Definition

Imagination, in Sullivan's thought, is the primal creative faculty through which the individual perceives and shapes reality. It operates not as idle fantasy but as a constructive power that "bodies forth" new realities from inner vision [011-030]. Sullivan traces this faculty to his earliest childhood, noting that his grandmother's sense of rhythm and color "had been acting on his imagination" from the beginning [001-015].

## Role in Creative Development

Sullivan saw imagination as the engine of all artistic and intellectual growth. In his own youth, "his imagination, unknown to him as such," expanded steadily through observation and experience [007-036]. This growth was not always matched by reality: "as usual his imagination had far outsped any possibility" [007-040], a tension that drove him toward architectural innovation.

## Imagination and Architectural Vision

In the professional realm, Sullivan described how imagination combined with will to produce concrete architectural results. The architect's task was to transform vision into structure, working in "that domain that was his own, that imagination and will had bodied forth out of the world of dreams" [011-030]. He saw in figures like major architects of Chicago "exemplar in the use of the imagination and the will, doggedly to carry forward their life work" [011-036].

## Imagination in Sullivan's Philosophy

Sullivan viewed imagination as a universal human endowment rather than a specialized artistic gift. It functioned as a bridge between the inner world of thought and the outer world of form: "from soul to soul, from imagination to imagination, from thought to thought" [009-031]. This democratic conception placed creative power within reach of every individual, aligning with his broader philosophical commitment to human potential.
