# Sublimity (Sullivan)

Sublimity, in Sullivan's lexicon, was not the Burkean terror of the infinite but the rapturous, life-affirming encounter with grandeur — natural and human — that disclosed to the young architect the "power of man" and the creative fecundity of the earth. It was, for Sullivan, the foundational aesthetic experience from which all genuine architecture must spring.

## Definition

Sullivan used "sublimity" to name moments of revelatory encounter: with mountains at dawn, with the Hudson River's Palisades, with the radiant patterning of the living earth. In each case, sublimity was not a property of the object beheld but of the transaction between beholder and beheld — a sudden, unpremeditated disclosure in which "a veil was lifted" (008-005) and the perceiver apprehended reality with undimmed clarity.

The concept carries three interwoven dimensions in Sullivan's usage:

| Dimension | Object | Significance |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Natural sublimity | Berkshire Hills, Hudson River, forest solitudes | Primary revelation of scale, power, and silence |
| Human sublimity | The locomotive, oratorios, architecture | Man's creative power as equal to nature's |
| Radiant sublimity | Earth's living fabric, the Free Spirit | The ongoing, gentle, fecund creativity of Life |

## Origins

### The Berkshire Revelation

Sullivan's first and defining encounter with sublimity occurred in the summer of his boyhood, traveling west from Boston with his Grandpa. He had begged to see "something big" — to escape the confines of a city he found suffocating (008-001). The experience came at dawn in the Berkshire Hills:

> "The true thrill of action began with the uprearing of imposing masses as Louis clung to the solid train now purring in the solitudes in ever-lengthening swings — deep valleys below — until, amid mists and pale moon gleaming, arose the mighty Berkshires, their summits faint and far, their immensities solemn, calm, seeming eternal in the ghostly fog in the mild shimmer, clad in forests, uttering great words, runic words revealing and withholding their secret to a young soul moving as a solitary visitant... amid the same echoes within and without, bereft of words to reply, a bare hush of being, as though through mists of mind and shimmer of hope, **sublimity**, in revelation, had come to *one* wholly unprepared." (008-005)

Two elements of this passage are crucial to Sullivan's mature philosophy. First, sublimity arrived unbidden — "wholly unprepared" — as genuine revelation must. Second, the experience disclosed not only the mountains' majesty but "the power of man" that had laid the rails and built the engines: "He had made a path, laid the rails, builded the engines that others might pass. Many saw engines and rails, and pathway, and *one* saw what lay behind them" (008-005).

### Forest Solitudes and the Hudson

The journey continued from Albany down the Hudson River by day-boat. Sullivan records that he "passed almost directly from the sublimity of forest solitudes to the grandeur of the lower Hudson" (009-010). The Palisades — "huge basalt crystals standing on end" — astounded him, as did his Grandpa's revelation that he had once taught French at West Point and swum the Hudson daily (009-010).

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan's sublimity draws on the Romantic tradition of the sublime — Wordsworth's encounters with mountain vastness, Emerson's transparent eyeball, Whitman's catalogues of democratic grandeur — but Sullivan's version is distinctively American in its fusion of natural wonder with technological awe. The locomotive is as sublime as the mountain it traverses; both testify to creative power.

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> "He entered the foothills and began winding among them, as with labored breath the engines, like heavy draft horses, began a steady pull... until, amid mists and pale moon gleaming, arose the mighty Berkshires, their summits faint and far, their immensities solemn, calm, seeming eternal in the ghostly fog in the mild shimmer, clad in forests, uttering great words, runic words revealing and withholding their secret." (008-005)

> "And Louis again, in wonder, felt the power of man. The thought struck deep, that what was bearing him along was solely the power of man; the living power to wish, to will, to do." (008-005)

> "It glories in these powers of earth and sky as in its own... It sees Life at work everywhere — Life, the mysterious, the companionable, the ineffable, the immensest and gentlest of powers, clothing the earth in a pattern of radiant sublimity, of tenderness, of fairy delicacy — ceaselessly at work." (014-033)

### In Architecture

Sullivan's concept of sublimity translated into architecture through the conviction that buildings, like mountains and locomotives, should disclose the power that made them. The steel frame should not be hidden behind masonry costumes; it should be expressed as "an equally revolutionary architectural mode" (015-027). The organic ornament that became Sullivan's signature was an attempt to capture nature's "radiant sublimity" in terracotta and iron — to make visible the "ceaselessly at work" creative impulse Sullivan saw in the natural world.

Three architectural principles derived from Sullivan's sublimity:

- **Structural honesty**: The steel frame, like the locomotive, is sublime in its own right and should be made visible — not hidden behind historical costumes
- **Organic ornament**: Nature's "fairy delicacy" and fecundity find echo in Sullivan's botanical ornament, which grows from the building as foliage from a tree
- **Vertical continuity**: The skyscraper's upward thrust echoes the Berkshires' "uprearing of imposing masses" — a sublime verticality expressive of human aspiration

## Legacy

Sullivan's sublimity entered the architectural bloodstream through his student [[Frank Lloyd Wright]], who made the encounter with landscape the originating moment of every design. The idea that a building should disclose rather than conceal — that structure is itself a form of sublime revelation — became a tenet of modernist architecture, even as later practitioners stripped away the spiritual language Sullivan considered essential.

The passage on the Free Spirit (014-033), with its vision of the earth "clothed in a pattern of radiant sublimity," represents Sullivan's most fully realized fusion of the aesthetic and the ethical. Beauty, power, and goodness converge in the sublime moment — an experience Sullivan believed was available to all, not reserved for the cultivated few. This democratic sublime — the locomotive as accessible grandeur, the skyscraper as the people's mountain — was Sullivan's distinctive contribution to aesthetic theory, and it remains perhaps the least understood aspect of his legacy.
