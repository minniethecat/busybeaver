## Definition

Spirit, in Sullivan's thought, was the vital, animating principle
that distinguishes living things from dead matter and great
architecture from mere building. He spoke of "the spirit of man"
as the irreducible core of human identity — that which thinks,
feels, aspires, and creates [015-010].

## Spirit in Architecture

Sullivan insisted that architecture must express spirit, not
merely satisfy practical requirements. He wrote that the
architect must be "a poet, a dreamer, a seer of visions" who
could perceive "the spirit that dwells within the facts"
[015-015].

> The building that has no spirit is dead. It may stand for a
> thousand years, but it will never live. The building that has
> spirit will live forever, though its stones crumble to dust.
> [015-018]

He saw the skyscraper not as a technical achievement but as "a
proud and soaring thing, rising in exultation" — an expression
of collective human spirit reaching upward [015-020].

## The Democratic Spirit

Sullivan tied spirit to his democratic philosophy. He believed
that every human being possessed spirit — "the spirit that is
in every man, woman, and child, waiting to be recognized,
waiting to be freed" [015-025]. A democratic architecture must
serve this universal spirit, not merely the powerful few.

He contrasted the "feudal spirit" of European architecture —
hierarchical, oppressive, serving lords and clergy — with the
democratic spirit that should animate American building. The
new architecture must express "the spirit of a free people"
[015-028].

## Spirit and Nature

Sullivan found spirit pervading all of life. He described America as "goal of the adventurous spirit, the great hospitable, open-armed land of the free" [001-015], linking individual vitality with national character. His grandmother "in great glee and high spirits sang to him about Le bon roi Dagobert" [001-018], demonstrating how the spirited temperament infused even domestic life. Sullivan himself would "remain patiently there, inspirited by the salt breeze" [001-025], showing his receptivity to nature's animating power.

## Spirit as Creative Force

In his mature thought, Sullivan connected spirit directly to creative action. He urged that one should approach a new country "in the same simplicity of spirit with which it is offered you" [007-007], applying the spiritual attitude to practical engagement with the world. The unfolding of language itself struck him as "ever more startling, ever more inspiriting" [007-039], suggesting that spirit was manifest in all genuine human expression — from words to buildings.
