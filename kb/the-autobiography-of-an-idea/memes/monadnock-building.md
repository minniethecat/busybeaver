# Monadnock Building

Pioneering Chicago office building designed by the firm of [[burnham-and-root]], recognized by Sullivan as a decisive moment — "a big jump" — in the rapid evolution of the tall commercial building during the late 1880s [015-054].

## Overview

The Monadnock Building, completed in 1891, rose sixteen stories at the south end of Chicago's Loop. It was the tallest load-bearing masonry building ever constructed, its walls six feet thick at the base to support the unprecedented height. The building represented both the apex and the endpoint of traditional masonry construction for tall buildings — the structural limit beyond which steel-frame construction became a necessity [015-054]. Sullivan characterized it as "an amazing cliff of brickwork, rising sheer and stark, with a subtlety of line and surface, a direct singleness of purpose, that gave one the thrill of romance" [015-054]. He judged it "the first and last word of its kind" and "a solitary monument, marking the high tide of masonry construction as applied to commercial structures" [015-054].

The Monadnock stood at the close of what Sullivan called his masonry "period," whose culmination was the [[Auditorium Building]] with its immense ten-story tower of solid masonry [015-044]. But the era of load-bearing masonry for tall buildings was ending. Sullivan observed that masonry construction inherently "fixed a new limit of height, as its ever thickening walls ate up ground and floor space of ever increasing value" [015-056], and that "the very tall masonry office building was in its nature economically unfit as ground values steadily rose" [015-057]. The solution came when Eastern steel mills "tentatively presented to Chicago architects" the idea of "a steel frame which should carry all the load" [015-060] — a development that "the architects of Chicago welcomed" and exploited to transform the commercial skyline [015-062].

## Sullivan's Assessment

Sullivan positioned the Monadnock within the lineage of Chicago skyscraper development. He noted that after earlier experiments — the Montauk Block at nine stories and the Auditorium at ten — an office building "to be called the 'Monadnock'" was undertaken by Burnham & Root [015-054]. He characterized the project as "a big jump from the Borden Block" — his own earlier commercial design [015-054]. This comparison reveals Sullivan's keen awareness of the competitive, iterative nature of Chicago School architecture, where each new building pushed taller and bolder than the last. The Monadnock's significance in Sullivan's narrative lies not in its design details but in its role as a benchmark of structural ambition: the final monument of a masonry tradition that would vanish "almost over night" [015-054].

## Design

The Monadnock was designed by [[John Wellborn Root]] shortly before his death in 1891. Its unadorned brown brick exterior and gently curved bay windows gave it a restrained, almost severe presence — a marked contrast to the ornate commercial palaces then in fashion. The building's structural system relied entirely on massive masonry bearing walls rather than the steel frame that Sullivan was simultaneously pioneering in the [[Wainwright Building]] in St. Louis [015-027]. This made the Monadnock a transitional figure: the last giant of the masonry era standing at the threshold of the steel age.

The building's sixteen-story height was so audacious for solid masonry that construction was postponed "until it should be seen whether or not the Auditorium Tower would go to China of its own free will" [015-054]. Only after the Auditorium tower proved stable — "trained by its architects concerning the etiquette of the situation" — did the Monadnock go forward [015-054].
