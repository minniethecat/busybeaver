## Definition

Vision, for Sullivan, was the power to see what is not yet
visible — the architect's capacity to perceive the form latent
in a site, a program, and a culture. It combined imagination,
understanding, and courage into a single creative faculty
[015-050].

## Vision in Chicago

Sullivan discovered the importance of vision in the raw energy
of post-fire Chicago. The city's leaders, however crude their
expression, possessed the essential quality:

> These men had vision. What they saw was real, they saw it as
> destiny. [011-002]

He recognized that the same visionary capacity that drove
Chicago's commercial ambition must animate its architecture —
buildings must express "the vision of a free people building a
new world" [015-052].

## The Architect as Visionary

Sullivan believed the architect must be "a seer, a prophet in
the true sense — not one who foretells, but one who sees clearly
what others see dimly or not at all" [015-055]. This vision was
not mystical but practical — it arose from deep understanding
of materials, structure, purpose, and the human spirit.

He contrasted the visionary architect with the mere technician:
"The one sees a building where others see a site. The other
sees only specifications and costs" [015-058].

## Vision and Democracy

Sullivan extended vision to the collective sphere. A democratic
society needed visionaries who could perceive "the aspirations
and the heart's desire of the people" [015-060]. Architecture,
as a public art, must give form to this collective vision:

> The architect is the interpreter of the people's dream. If
> the people have no dream, the architect has nothing to
> interpret. [015-062]
