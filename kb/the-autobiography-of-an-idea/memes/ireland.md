# Ireland

The ancestral homeland of Louis Sullivan's father, Patrick Sullivan. Though Sullivan himself never visited Ireland, the country loomed in his childhood imagination through his father's vivid tales of its landscape, people, and history.

## Location

An island nation in the North Atlantic, west of Great Britain. In the Sullivan family narrative, Ireland represented both ancient roots and the harsh realities that drove emigration to America in the nineteenth century.

## In Sullivan's Life

### Patrick Sullivan's Origins

Louis Sullivan's father, Patrick Sullivan, was Irish by birth. His early life was marked by tragedy and independence: at the age of twelve, he accompanied his father — a landscape painter and widower — to a county fair, where he lost his father in the crowd and never saw him again. "Thus at the age of twelve he was thrown upon the world to make his way" (001-011). With a "curious little fiddle," the young Patrick wandered barefoot about the countryside, playing for dancers at fairs and gatherings. "Thus traveling he saw nearly all of Ireland" (001-011).

### Stories of the Old Country

Patrick Sullivan passed down to his son the folk memory of an older Ireland. He told Louis a tale of "long ago in Ireland, in the good fighting days," when "there were four tribes" engaged in perpetual conflict (002-012). These stories connected the Sullivan household in Boston to a distant, mythologized past.

### The Emigrant's Perspective

For Patrick Sullivan, Ireland was a place left behind — a land of poverty and limited opportunity from which he had escaped through determination and talent. He made his way from Ireland to London, then to Paris for advanced training in dance, before finally emigrating to Boston in 1847 aboard the ship *Unicorn* (001-011). His son Louis reflected that "this is all he ever saw of Ireland" — a statement encapsulating the clean break of emigration (011-042).

## Cultural Context

### The Irish in Nineteenth-Century America

Patrick Sullivan arrived in America during the peak years of Irish immigration following the Great Famine (1845–1852). Despite his humble origins, he established himself successfully as a dancing master in Boston, commanding "desirable influence and respect" through his probity and mastery of polite forms (001-011).

### Sullivan's Inheritance

From his Irish father, Louis Sullivan inherited what he described as a "grim will" and "instinctive ambition." The elder Sullivan's journey — from barefoot fiddler to respected Boston dancing master — embodied the self-made narrative that his architect son would later apply to his own career. "He always was successful," Sullivan wrote of his father, noting that "his probity was such that he could always command desirable influence and respect" (001-011).
