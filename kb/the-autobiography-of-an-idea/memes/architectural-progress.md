# Architectural Progress

Sullivan believed architecture was not a fixed canon but a living process of continuous development. Like nature itself, building must progress — growing from simple forms to complex organisms, each generation advancing beyond the last rather than endlessly recycling historical styles.

## Definition

Architectural progress, in Sullivan's understanding, meant the steady advancement of building art through the cumulative application of human intelligence to new problems. Progress was not mere novelty — it was the organic unfolding of architectural form in response to changing conditions: new materials like steel, new building types like the skyscraper, new social realities like the modern city. Sullivan saw this as a law of nature applied to human creation: "a progression from an unorganized simple, through stages of growth and differentiation to a highly organized complex" (013-026).

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan's concept of progress drew heavily on evolutionary thought. Darwin's theory of evolution "seemed stupendous" to him, and Herbert Spencer's definition of progress — from simple homogeneity to complex heterogeneity — provided a framework he actively applied to his own intellectual development and to architecture itself (013-026). This was reinforced by his reading of Hippolyte Taine, who taught that art evolves as the expression of a people's changing life.

### Sullivan's Development

Sullivan's personal trajectory embodied his belief in progress. He was by nature "progressive, aggressive and impatient" — unwilling to stay where there was "no future" (010-027). His early education reflected the pattern: "rigid rules became plastic as he progressed, then they became fluent; gradually they disappeared" (007-039). This was not just a description of his studies but a model for how architectural tradition should work — rules mastered, internalized, and ultimately transcended.

His experience with great engineering works deepened this conviction. Watching "two great engineering works" — the St. Louis bridge and the Kentucky River cantilever — he saw "the function of man, the power of man, the power of man to accomplish" (013-013). These structures were not imitations of the past but bold solutions to unprecedented problems — exactly what architecture should be.

His intellectual growth followed the same pattern: "he had begun with a simple unorganized idea of beneficent power, and was beginning to see the enormous complexity growing out of it, and enriching it" (013-026).

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> He was progressive, aggressive and impatient. He wished to live in the stream of life. He wished to be impelled by the power of living (010-027).

> Rigid rules became plastic as he progressed, then they became fluent; gradually they disappeared (007-039).

> Spencer's definition implying a progression from an unorganized simple, through stages of growth and differentiation to a highly organized complex, seemed to fit his own case (013-026).

> His child-domain had seemed at first, and hopefully, to extend itself progressively into a larger world (009-022).

### In Architecture

Sullivan's commitment to progress manifested in his determination to "face and solve new problems in new functional forms" (015-027). The steel frame was not an occasion to apply Gothic or Renaissance detailing — it demanded an entirely new architectural language. His vertical system for tall buildings, developed experimentally across a series of commercial structures, was his answer: a grammar of form that was native to modern construction, not borrowed from the past.

He saw architectural history as "a spectacle, a processional of the races and the nations" (012-039) — not a catalogue of styles to be copied but evidence of architecture's continuous evolution. Each great civilization had created forms authentic to its own life; the modern age must do the same.

Crucially, Sullivan's idea of progress was organic, not mechanical. Architecture did not advance by discarding the past but by absorbing and transforming it — exactly as a living organism grows by assimilating nutrients into new tissue.

## Legacy

Sullivan's evolutionary model of architectural progress became foundational to modern architecture. His insistence that each era must create its own authentic forms — rather than borrowing from previous ones — directly influenced the modernist rejection of historical styles. Yet his organic understanding of progress, with its emphasis on continuity and growth rather than rupture, offers a more nuanced model than the "clean break" rhetoric of later modernism.
