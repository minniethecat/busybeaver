# Henry Hobson Richardson

Henry Hobson Richardson (1838–1886), the preeminent American architect of his generation, whose bold Romanesque buildings — particularly Trinity Church in Boston and the Marshall Field Wholesale Store in Chicago — Louis Sullivan admired as works of authentic creative power, calling him "sole giant of his day."

## Biography

### Early Life

Henry Hobson Richardson was born in 1838 at Priestly Plantation in Louisiana. He studied at Harvard College (graduating 1859) and then at the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris — the second American to attend, after Richard Morris Hunt. The American Civil War interrupted his plans, and he worked in the office of Théodore Labrouste before returning to the United States in 1865, settling in New York to begin his practice.

### Career

Richardson's breakthrough came with Trinity Church in Boston (1872–1877), the building whose tower so captivated the young Sullivan at MIT. The church's massive, sculptural Romanesque forms — a departure from the Gothic and Classical conventions of the era — established Richardson's signature style and national reputation. He went on to design the Marshall Field Wholesale Store in Chicago (1885–1887, demolished 1930), the Allegheny County Courthouse in Pittsburgh (1884–1888), and a series of public libraries, railroad stations, and private houses. His work introduced a boldly personal Romanesque idiom — now called Richardsonian Romanesque — characterized by rusticated stone, deep window reveals, Syrian arches, and an overarching sense of mass and weight. Richardson died prematurely in 1886 at the age of forty-seven, but his influence on American architecture was already immense.

### Relationship with Sullivan

Sullivan first encountered Richardson's work as a student at MIT in Boston. While his classmates absorbed the "Five Orders of Architecture" — which Sullivan came to see as "fairy tales of the long ago, now by the learned made rigid, mechanical and inane" (010-024) — Sullivan's thought "was mostly on the tower of the New Brattle Street Church, conceived and brought to light by the mighty Richardson, undoubtedly for Louis's special delight; for was not here a fairy tale indeed" (010-024). Richardson's tower offered what the classroom "Orders" could not: living architecture, red-blooded and real. Sullivan's faith "ever lay in the oft-seen creative power and glory of man" (010-023), and Richardson's work was the visible proof. Later, surveying the architectural landscape of America as a young man in Chicago, Sullivan observed that "scattered through the east were architects of book-attainment in fair number, and a few of marked personality and red blood — particularly one Henry Richardson, he of the strong arm and virile mind — sole giant of his day" (011-018). This assessment — "sole giant of his day" — placed Richardson in a category apart from all other contemporary American architects.

## Significance

Richardson represented to Sullivan the possibility of an architecture rooted not in book-learning but in personal creative power. The epithets Sullivan chose — "mighty," "strong arm," "virile mind," "sole giant" — reveal what he valued: architecture as the direct expression of a forceful, authentic personality. Unlike the "architects of book-attainment" who merely reproduced historical styles, Richardson created a living architecture that was unmistakably his own. Sullivan saw in Richardson's work the same quality he found in Wagner's music: a personality so powerful it could conjure its own domain into being. Richardson thus served as a crucial precedent and benchmark — the American architect who proved that authentic creative power could flourish on native soil, presaging Sullivan's own ambition to create an architecture organic to American democracy.
