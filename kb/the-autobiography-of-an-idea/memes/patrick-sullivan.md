# Patrick Sullivan

Patrick Sullivan (c. 1818--?), Irish dancing master, father of Louis Sullivan, whose "grim determination," physical discipline, and emotional reserve left a complex and lasting imprint on the architect's character.

## Biography

### Early Life in Ireland

Patrick Sullivan's origins were obscure, even to his son. "He had no secrets, but his memory did not extend much back of his twelfth year" (001-011). He claimed his father was "a landscape painter, a widower, and he an only child" (001-011). The two used to visit county fairs in Ireland together, but at one such fair the boy lost his father in the crowd and "never saw him again." At the age of twelve he was "thrown upon the world to make his way" (001-011).

With a "curious little fiddle," he wandered barefoot about the Irish countryside, "to fiddle here and there for those who wished to dance; and of dancing there was plenty" (001-011). This wandering life, which "must have covered a number of years" (001-011), brought him across nearly all of Ireland. The transition from itinerant fiddler to dancing master is obscure in Sullivan's account, but "his attention must have focused on dancing as an art" (001-011).

### Career as Dancing Master

Driven by "the grim determination of his character, his pride and his ambition" (001-011), Patrick Sullivan made his way to London and placed himself "under the tutelage of the best -- most fashionable -- masters" (001-011). He established his own academy in London, but "not content with this advance, which was successful, he must needs reach the heights of his art, and in Paris, the center of fashion, took instruction of the leading masters" (001-011).

Sullivan's son, writing decades later, paused to contextualize the social value of Patrick's profession: "In those days dancing was a social art of grace, of deportment, and of personal carriage ... an art of elegance that has passed with the days of elegance. Artificial it largely was, yet humanizing, and beneficent" (001-011).

Patrick Sullivan reached Boston in 1847, set up an academy, and "was successful. He always was successful" (001-016). "His probity was such that he could always command desirable influence and respect. He was familiar with polite forms" (001-016).

### Marriage and Family

In Boston, Patrick met Andrienne List, a young woman of Swiss-French and German descent, whose family had emigrated from Geneva. Sullivan's son speculated that what truly attracted Patrick was Andrienne's musical genius: "her sense of rhythm, of sweep, of accent, of the dance-cadence with its reinforcements and languishments, the *tempo rubato* -- was genius itself. He lost no time in marrying her as a business asset" (001-016). They married on August 14, 1852. On September 3, 1856, their son Louis Henri Sullivan was born at 22 South Bennett Street, Boston.

The assessment of Patrick's emotional life is stark: "She was lovable and he may have loved her. It is possible but hardly probable; for there is nothing in the record to show that he loved others, or that he loved himself. He was merely self-centered -- not even cold" (001-016). He was "moderate of habit; drank a little wine, smoked an occasional cigar, and was an enthusiast regarding hygiene" (001-016).

## Relationship with Sullivan

### The Regime of Discipline

Patrick Sullivan's relationship with his son was defined by a program of physical training that Louis later recounted with a mixture of awe and detachment. In Newburyport, when the family took summer quarters at a hotel, "Father" imposed a regime that contrasted sharply with the grandmother's sentimental indulgence:

"Not so with Father. There was no sentiment, no nonsense about him. He would not rave for thirty minutes over a single blossom; a brief moment of appreciation sufficed" (005-007). Patrick's program began at five in the morning, pulling his son from bed, leading him to the town pump for a cup of the coldest water, then setting him to run to establish "circulation" -- "again a new word" (005-007). After a brisk two-mile walk, they arrived at a sequestered pool, where Patrick "dexterously seized the neophyte, and, bracing himself, with a back-forward swing cast the youngster far out" to teach him "scientific swimming" (005-007).

Patrick's "dietary law was strict; simple foods, no coffee, no tea, no pastry, a little meat" (005-010). These disciplines, though severe, were not cruel: the young Sullivan recalled "the powerful heave and sink and heave of a fine swimmer, as he grasped his father's hair, and saw the bank approach" (005-007) with a child's exhilaration.

### The Father's Role in Sullivan's Vocation

When the young Louis confided his ambition to become an architect, Patrick "seemed pleased, greatly pleased, that his son's ambition was centering on something definite. He 'allowed,' as they used to say in New England, that Architecture was a great art, the mother of all the arts, and its practice a noble profession, adding a word or two about Michelangelo" (007-034). But Patrick characteristically offered a counter-proposal: Louis should become a "scientific farmer," equipped through an agricultural college education. The word "scientific" was "electrical" to the boy, and for a long moment Louis wavered before declaring: "No: I have made up my mind" (007-034).

This episode exemplifies Patrick's peculiar form of support: he encouraged without sentiment, offered alternatives without insistence, and let his son choose. It was also Patrick who gave Louis the Christmas gift of John Draper's *Intellectual Development of Europe* (013-025) -- a book that profoundly shaped the architect's developing worldview.

### The Irish Heritage

When the young Sullivan, around age twelve, asked about the name Sullivan "which seemed to coincide with shanty-Irish," his father told him "this tale: Long ago in Ireland, in the good fighting days, there were four tribes or clans of the O'Sullivans: The O'Sullivan-Moors, The O'Sullivan-Macs, and two others. That *We* were descended from the O'Sullivan-Moors, and that all four tribes were descended from a Spanish marauder, who ravished the west Irish coast and settled there. His name it appears was O'Soulyevoyne or something like that, which, translated, meant, 'The Prince with One Eye'" (002-012). The story was likely apocryphal -- a gift of narrative dignity from a father whose own origins had been swallowed by obscurity.

## Significance

Patrick Sullivan embodies one pole of the tension that shaped Louis Sullivan's character: discipline versus sentiment, will versus feeling, "grim determination" versus the "warmth of heart" the grandparents provided. Louis Sullivan inherited his father's "strong will, and obstinacy" (001-006) -- traits that served him through a career of creative independence and professional isolation.

The father's most profound gift may have been his emotional reserve itself. By refusing sentiment, Patrick Sullivan taught his son to rely on his own resources. The physical discipline of the morning swims, the dietary strictness, the insistence on self-reliance -- all prefigured the architect who would work eighteen-hour days in Paris, who would "arrive slowly though boldly through the years, by means of incessant thought, self correction, hard work and dogged perseverance" (013-036). If the grandparents gave Louis Sullivan the capacity to feel, Patrick Sullivan gave him the capacity to endure.
