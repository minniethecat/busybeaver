# St. Louis

Major Mississippi River city whose rapid growth made it a proving ground for Sullivan's early skyscraper designs, most notably the Wainwright Building — the first authentic expression of the tall office building in steel-frame construction.

## Location

Situated on the west bank of the Mississippi River, St. Louis commanded a strategic position as the gateway between the eastern United States and the western territories. The city's location on the river made it a critical crossing point and commercial hub during the nineteenth century. Sullivan first encountered St. Louis not through personal travel but through the engineering press, following the construction of "the triple arch bridge to cross the Mississippi at St. Louis, Capt. Eades, chief engineer" — a project that captivated his youthful imagination and merged with his growing reverence for the creative power of engineers (PN refs × 3: 013-013, 013-014).

## In Sullivan's Life

### First Encounter

Sullivan's engagement with St. Louis came through professional commissions rather than personal residence. The city reached out to Chicago architects as its own commercial ambitions grew, seeking designers who could express the new scale of American enterprise. As Sullivan's practice with Adler expanded, "the work of the firm had taken Louis over a large part of the country," bringing him into direct contact with the clients and sites that would define his career — St. Louis foremost among them (PN refs × 2: 015-027, 015-029).

### Significance

St. Louis provided Sullivan with one of his most important early commissions: the Wainwright Building (1890–1891). It was in St. Louis, Sullivan wrote, that the tall office building "was given first authentic expression" in steel-frame construction. The commission came at a pivotal moment when Sullivan was developing his theory of the skyscraper as an organic form — base, shaft, and cornice expressing the tripartite nature of the tall building. The Wainwright's success established Sullivan's national reputation and demonstrated that his architectural principles could thrive beyond Chicago. Sullivan's faith in man's creative power — "he saw power everywhere" and came to see "the powers of nature and the powers of man coalesce in his vision into an idea of power" — found its concrete embodiment in the Wainwright's soaring verticality (PN refs × 3: 013-014, 015-027, 015-029).

### Rivalry with Chicago

By the late nineteenth century, St. Louis and Chicago were competitors for Midwestern dominance. Sullivan observed this rivalry directly, noting that "Chicago had passed St. Louis in population and was proud" of this achievement. The inter-city competition fueled an architectural arms race, with each city commissioning taller and more ambitious commercial structures to assert its metropolitan stature. Chicago's booming railway system and surging immigration drove the frenzy; "the city had become the center of a great radiating system of railways" and the "population had grown to five hundred thousand," leaving St. Louis struggling to keep pace in the competition for architectural primacy (PN refs × 2: 015-053, 015-068).

## Architectural Context

St. Louis in the 1880s and 1890s was undergoing rapid commercial expansion. The city's business leaders sought architectural expression that would rival Chicago's emerging skyline. The Wainwright Building, commissioned by Ellis Wainwright, represented the first full application of Sullivan's skyscraper principles outside Chicago. Its steel-frame construction allowed for greater height and larger windows than traditional masonry, while its terracotta ornamentation demonstrated Sullivan's belief that even commercial buildings should possess organic beauty. The economic logic of the skyscraper was universal: "the tall commercial building arose from the pressure of land values, the land values from pressure of population" — a formula that applied as forcefully in St. Louis as in Chicago. Sullivan's achievement was to give that economic imperative an artistic form, one that "the architects of Chicago welcomed" while "the architects of the East were appalled by it and could make no contribution to it." In St. Louis, far from the conservative eastern establishment, the Wainwright demonstrated that "the tall office building would seem to have arisen spontaneously" wherever commerce, engineering, and architectural imagination converged (PN refs × 5: 013-013, 015-027, 015-056, 015-062, 015-063).
