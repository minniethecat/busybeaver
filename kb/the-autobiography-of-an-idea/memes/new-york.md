# New York

New York City appears in the autobiography as a city glimpsed in transit, a measure of eastern energy, and ultimately a cautionary example of the architectural provincialism that Chicago's skyscraper school was destined to surpass.

## In Sullivan's Life

### First Encounters

Sullivan first encountered New York during a childhood journey with his grandfather, traveling from Boston by the New York Central train to Utica, then taking "the day-boat down the Hudson to New York" (009-010). The journey was rich in sensory impressions: "the Berkshires, the Mohawk and its valley, Little Falls, the Black River, the Moose River, the primeval forest, and the Falls, the Hudson, the Catskills, the Palisades, New York Harbor, Long Island Sound" — an expansive panorama of American landscape that Sullivan described as having been gathered in "as though he had flung and drawn a huge lassoo" (009-022).

Of the city itself, however, "Louis saw but little; and when Grandpa said it was here they landed when he with his family came from Geneva, Louis took the information deafly, not even inquiring when and why they had moved to Boston" (009-011). The city remained a transit point, not a destination.

### En Route to Philadelphia

When Sullivan left Boston for Philadelphia to seek his architectural career, he "stopped over in New York City for a few days" (010-027). Here he observed a distinct urban character: "a sharper form of speech, an increase of energetic action over that he had left behind, and also a rougher and more arrogant type of life" (010-028). New York impressed him as a city of force, but not necessarily of grace.

### Architectural Judgment

Sullivan's mature judgment of New York architecture was severe. He had "once supposed that the genius for creating ugliness was peculiarly a Yankee monopoly; but he later found in New York and Philadelphia that almost all the buildings in these cities were of the same crassness of type; a singularly sordid, vulgar vernacular in architectural speech" (011-018).

In his analysis of the skyscraper's development, Sullivan concluded that "the tall office buildings fronting the narrow streets and lanes of lower New York were provincialisms, gross departures from the law of common sense" (015-062). New York's tall buildings, in his view, lacked the organic logic that Chicago's steel-frame towers embodied.

## Significance

New York served Sullivan as a negative example: the city that had wealth, energy, and height, but lacked the democratic architectural intelligence that would create an authentic American style. Its buildings were tall but not organic; its culture was sharp but not creative. Chicago, by contrast, was the city where the tall office building "would seem to have arisen spontaneously" from the convergence of commerce, engineering, and architectural imagination (015-063).
