# Ecole des Beaux-Arts

The Ecole Nationale des Beaux-Arts in Paris, where Louis Sullivan studied in 1874 as a young aspirant, was the world's most prestigious school of architecture whose systematic discipline profoundly shaped his analytical powers even as he came to reject its underlying philosophy.

## Overview

The Ecole des Beaux-Arts represented the summit of formal architectural education in the nineteenth century. Its curriculum centered on the theory of the plan (le plan), the mastery of historical styles, and a rigorous system of competitive projects (projets) judged by juries of academicians. For Sullivan, who entered as an ambitious nineteen-year-old from Chicago, it offered an intellectual discipline far beyond anything available in America. He discovered that "the entrance examinations were to begin in about six weeks" (012-004) and threw himself into preparation under Monsieur Clopet's tutelage. Though he passed the examinations and spent months immersed in the atelier of Emile Vaudremer, Sullivan ultimately concluded that "this Great School, in its perfect flower of technique, lacked the profound animus of a primal inspiration" (012-042).

The school became a lifelong reference point in Sullivan's thinking -- simultaneously the gold standard of intellectual rigor and the ultimate example of what he rejected in architecture: a closed system of dead forms imposed upon living creativity.

## Key Figures

- **Monsieur Clopet** -- Sullivan's mathematics tutor in Paris, "a small dark man" whose "demonstrations so broad as to admit of no exception" (012-007) taught Sullivan a new standard of intellectual clarity. Clopet's method -- "I say" (je dis) as the opening of each proof, a "sort of challenge" (012-012) -- impressed Sullivan as a model of rational inquiry.

- **Emile Vaudremer** -- Architect and patron of the atelier libre Sullivan joined. Vaudremer was a winner of the Grand Prix de Rome and represented the school's highest traditions. Eugene Letang, Sullivan's instructor at MIT, had been a "diplome of the Ecole Nationale des Beaux Arts" and specifically "an ancien of the atelier libre of Emil Vaudremer" (010-019).

- **Hippolyte Taine** (1828--1893) -- Though not directly associated with the Ecole, Taine's three volumes on the *Philosophy of Art* were discovered by Sullivan during his Paris studies and became essential to his intellectual development. From Taine, Sullivan derived the conviction that "the art of a people is a reflex or direct expression of the life of that people" (012-037), an insight that ultimately undermined the Beaux-Arts premise of a universal architectural language.

## Role in Sullivan's Career

Sullivan's path to the Beaux-Arts was deliberate. At MIT, he had already concluded that the school was "but a pale reflection of the Ecole des Beaux Arts; and he thought it high time that he go to headquarters to learn if what was preached there as a gospel, really signified glad tidings" (010-026). Frank Furness, the Philadelphia architect under whom Sullivan worked before Paris, reinforced this ambition, declaring that Sullivan "was an idiot to have wasted his time in a place where one was filled with sawdust" (010-029). Sullivan resolved "to see what architecture might be like in practice" (010-027) at the source.

After six weeks of intensive preparation working eighteen hours a day -- relying on the mental discipline Moses Woolson had given him in "the science and the art of thinking" (012-003) -- Sullivan passed the entrance examinations and was admitted. In Clopet's mathematics class, he encountered a method of thought so rigorous that it became a permanent standard: he "could no more forget it than he could his own name" (012-009). In the history examination, he discovered an approach that probed for "constructive imagination" rather than fact-recall (012-023).

Yet the Beaux-Arts ultimately disappointed him. After months in Vaudremer's atelier, he found that the school's "perfect flower of technique" was built upon a "fatal residuum of artificiality" (012-042). The theory of the plan, for all its brilliance, was "an abstraction, a method, a state of mind, that was local and specific; not universal" (012-042). Sullivan perceived that "beneath the law of the School lay a law which it ignored" -- "the law he had seen set forth in the stillness of the Sistine, which he saw everywhere in the open of life" (012-042). His experiences in Italy, especially the revelation of Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel, had taught him that true creative power transcended academic method.

## Major Achievements

- **Atelier system** — The Beaux-Arts established the atelier model of architectural education: students worked under a patron (master architect) in a studio setting, learning through competitive projects (projets) and rapid design exercises (esquisses). This system became the template for architectural education worldwide, and its influence persists in the studio-based pedagogy of contemporary architecture schools.

- **Theory of the plan** — The school's core intellectual contribution was the theory of the plan (le plan), which taught that a building's formal and spatial organization must be legible from its plan drawing alone. Sullivan mastered this discipline and acknowledged its power, even as he would later argue it was "an abstraction, a method, a state of mind, that was local and specific; not universal" (012-042).

- **Training of American architects** — The Beaux-Arts trained generations of American architects including Sullivan's own MIT instructor Eugene Letang (010-019) and many who would dominate the profession in the United States. Its prestige was such that "aspirants" from across the Western world sought its diploma, and its graduates led the leading architectural commissions of the era.

## Legacy

Sullivan's relationship to the Beaux-Arts remained dialectical throughout his career. He never denied the value of its intellectual training; he credited Clopet's geometry and the history examinations with teaching him a kind of thinking he could find nowhere else. But he came to see the school as the architectural embodiment of "feudal thought" -- a closed hierarchy of forms imposed by authority rather than derived from living conditions. His critique of the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition was, in part, a critique of the Beaux-Arts impulse: the imposition of European academic forms on American democratic soil. "The damage wrought by the World's Fair will last for half a century," he wrote (015-087), and behind that damage he saw the Beaux-Arts aesthetic as its enabling cause. Yet the systematic clarity Sullivan absorbed in Paris -- the habit of tracing every phenomenon to its first principles -- remained a permanent feature of his architectural thinking and writing.
