## Definition

Freedom, in Sullivan's autobiographical narrative, is both a condition of authentic living and a prerequisite for creative work. It manifests in physical liberty — the open landscape of the countryside — and in intellectual independence. Sullivan's faith "lay indeed in freedom" [010-023], a conviction that shaped his architectural practice and his philosophy of life.

## Freedom in Childhood

The young Sullivan experienced freedom most vividly in nature, away from adult constraints. He "climbed the gate and in new freedom, lightly traversing the down" [004-003], finding in the open fields a liberation from the formalities of home and school. His grandparents "allowed him the utmost freedom to go and come and do" [004-010], a permissiveness that nurtured his independent spirit. The farm at Newburyport became synonymous with escape: "Louis felt the hour of freedom was at hand" [009-021].

## Intellectual Freedom

As Sullivan matured, freedom took on an intellectual dimension. He sought "a new sort of freedom" [010-016] in his approach to architectural education and practice — a rejection of formulaic methods in favor of direct engagement with materials and forms. The vast spaces of the American landscape reinforced this: "here was freedom; here was expanse!" [008-009], he wrote of the countryside that surrounded his early life.

## Freedom and Creative Work

Sullivan linked freedom directly to the architectural act. "The song of Spring was the song of freedom" [010-023], and this seasonal metaphor extended to the architect's work — building as an act of liberation, not of conformity. He saw the struggle for freedom of thought as essential: the architect must seek "freedom of thought, for the right of free inquiry" [007-044], breaking through the "veil" of convention.
