The Renaissance in architecture, as Louis Sullivan understood it, was not a rebirth but a "violent outbreak" — a revival of Classical forms that, in his view, overwhelmed and contaminated the organic development of building traditions across Europe and America.

## Sullivan's Critique of the Renaissance

Sullivan held a characteristically strong opinion of the Renaissance and its architectural legacy. He described it as a force that "slowly spread westward, contaminating all that it touched, both at its source and outward" (015-086). This was no neutral historical observation; for Sullivan, the Renaissance represented the triumph of borrowed form over authentic expression — the very thing his own architectural philosophy opposed.

## The Classical Revival's Enduring Grip

Writing in the early twentieth century, Sullivan observed that the Renaissance influence had never truly loosened its hold on architectural practice. "Meanwhile the architectural generation immediately succeeding the Classic and Renaissance merchants, are seeking to secure a special immunity from the inroads of common sense, through a process of vaccination with the lymph of every known European style, period and accident" (015-088). He saw architects of his own day endlessly recycling historical forms rather than creating forms appropriate to modern life.

## A Church for Every Style

Sullivan noted with irony the stylistic eclecticism that Renaissance influence had fostered. "We have French, English and Italian Gothic, Classic and Renaissance for churches" (015-089). In his view, this menu of historical options was the antithesis of genuine architectural creation — a surrender of the architect's responsibility to express the living spirit of his own time and place.
