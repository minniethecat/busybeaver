## Definition

In Sullivan's philosophy, "the reality of man" is the authentic,
affirmative nature of human beings as creative, free spirits. This
reality stands in opposition to abstract, distorted, or degraded
conceptions of humanity that Sullivan saw pervading both
architectural practice and social thought
[014-003] [014-012] [014-023].

## Sullivan's Affirmation

Sullivan repeatedly affirms the "I am" — man's self-declared reality
as a foundation for authentic creative work:

> the affirmative "I am" that is man's reality [014-023]

He argued that architecture and society had become detached from this
reality, substituting abstractions for lived human experience
[012-042] [014-003].

## Reality vs. Abstraction

Sullivan drew a sharp contrast between immediate reality and
intellectual abstraction. His critique of academic education centered
on its detachment from lived experience:

> the intellect detached from reality — therefore detached from life [012-012]

For Sullivan, true understanding came from direct engagement with
the real, not from formal systems of thought that substituted
abstractions for concrete experience [011-011] [012-042].

## The Architect's Search

Sullivan described his own professional journey as a search for
"the reality of man":

> his search for the reality of man, in his profound faith [015-009]

This search led him to reject architectural styles that he saw as
expressing "inverted notions of reality" — forms that denied rather
than affirmed human creative power [014-084] [015-028].

## Reality and Creative Power

For Sullivan, recognizing man's reality meant recognizing his
creative powers. The denial of reality was simultaneously a denial
of creativity:

> other than the dream of the reality of man [014-072]

He saw the architect's task as giving form to this reality —
building structures that expressed rather than suppressed the
human spirit [015-086] [015-092].
