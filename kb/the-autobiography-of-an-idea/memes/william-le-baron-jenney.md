# William Le Baron Jenney

William Le Baron Jenney (1832--1907), Chicago architect and engineer, pioneer of metal-frame skyscraper construction, and Sullivan's first employer upon his arrival in Chicago in 1873.

## Biography

### Early Life and Education

Jenney was born in Fairhaven, Massachusetts, in 1832. He studied engineering at the Ecole Polytechnique in Paris, receiving a technical education that distinguished him from the "bookish and timid" architects Sullivan found elsewhere. He served through the American Civil War as a Major of Engineers, attached to General Sherman on the March to the Sea, earning the title "Major" by which he was universally known in Chicago.

### Career

After the war, Jenney established an architectural practice in Chicago. His office produced the Portland Block, a four-story structure of pressed brick and sandstone at Washington and Dearborn Streets, which Sullivan noted as one of the few buildings in post-fire Chicago that "showed talent in design, and a certain stability" (011-016). Jenney is historically credited as a pioneer of steel-frame construction, and his office became an incubator for the architects who would define the Chicago School -- Sullivan, Daniel Burnham, John Root, and others all passed through or were connected to his orbit.

Sullivan's assessment of Jenney was characteristically sharp: "The Major was a free-and-easy cultured gentleman, but not an architect except by courtesy of terms. His true profession was that of engineer" (011-018). Sullivan placed Jenney among those Chicago practitioners who were "intelligently conscientious in the interest of their clients" rather than among the "bookish and timid" (011-018).

## Relationship with Sullivan

Sullivan arrived in Chicago in 1873, in the midst of the financial panic that followed the Great Fire. Seeking employment, he inquired about the architect of the Portland Block and learned the name was Jenney -- "Major Jenney; or in full, Major William Le Baron Jenney" (011-016). He entered Jenney's office and found a workplace containing "work enough ... to keep five men busy and a boy" (011-021).

The office culture was anarchic and formative. In Jenney's frequent and prolonged absences, "bedlam reigned" (011-021). Sullivan and his colleague John Edelmann would mount drawing tables to deliver howling stump speeches on greenback currency or single tax, while Sullivan sang oratorio selections at the top of his voice. The office boy served as lookout: "'Cheese it, Cullies; the Boss!', which in high English signifies: 'Gentlemen, Major William Le Baron Jenney, our esteemed benefactor, approaches!'" (011-021). Jenney's entrance brought "sudden silence, sudden industry, intense concentration" (011-021).

It was in Jenney's office that Sullivan first met John Edelmann, whose theory of "suppressed functions" (011-025) and democratic idealism would profoundly shape Sullivan's developing architectural philosophy. The anarchic camaraderie of Jenney's drafting room was, in Sullivan's experience, the fertile soil from which the Chicago School's innovations would grow.

## Significance

Jenney's significance to Sullivan was primarily as an enabler rather than a teacher. As an engineer by training rather than a formal architect, Jenney represented a practical, construction-led approach that stood apart from the Beaux-Arts formalism Sullivan would later encounter -- and partly reject -- in Paris. The loose, creative, and intellectually vigorous atmosphere of Jenney's office gave Sullivan his first professional home and introduced him to the circle of young architects who would transform American building.

Historically, Jenney's importance lies in his pioneering use of metal-frame construction, which made the skyscraper possible. Sullivan's own later innovations in skyscraper form -- particularly the tripartite vertical division of the Wainwright and Guaranty Buildings -- rest on structural principles Jenney's generation first demonstrated.
