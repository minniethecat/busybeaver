# Daniel Burnham

Daniel Hudson Burnham (1846–1912), architect, urban planner, and partner in the Chicago firm Burnham & Root. In Sullivan's autobiographical framing, Burnham was his central foil: the man who "was obsessed by the feudal idea of power" while Sullivan "was equally obsessed by the beneficent idea of Democratic power" (015-005).

## Biography

### Early Life

Burnham was born in Henderson, New York, and raised in Chicago. He failed the entrance examinations for both Harvard and Yale, worked briefly as a retail clerk and mining speculator, and eventually found his way into architecture through an apprenticeship. His early career included work in the office of William Le Baron Jenney, the same firm where Sullivan and John Edelmann had worked.

### Career

In 1873, Burnham formed a partnership with John Wellborn Root, creating the firm Burnham & Root. The firm quickly established itself among Chicago's leading architectural practices, beginning with residences and expanding into commercial and institutional work. Burnham's role was the businessman and organizer; Root was the designer.

Sullivan first encountered Burnham when he was about eighteen, crossing paths at a residence Burnham & Root were building on Prairie Avenue for stockyards magnate John Sherman, Burnham's future father-in-law. Sullivan "found Burnham a sentimentalist, a dreamer, a man of fixed determination and strong will -- no doubt about that -- of large, wholesome, effective presence, a shade pompous, a mystic -- a Swedenborgian -- a man who readily opened his heart if one were sympathetic" (015-002).

Burnham articulated his vision candidly in that first meeting: "my idea is to work up a big business, to handle big things, deal with big business men, and to build up a big organization, for you can't handle big things unless you have an organization" (015-002).

Burnham's "megalomania concerning the largest, the tallest, the most costly and sensational, moved on in its sure orbit, as he painfully learned to use the jargon of big business" (015-004). Sullivan watched Burnham deploy flattery as a business tool: "Louis saw it done repeatedly, and at first was amazed at Burnham's effrontery, only to be more amazingly amazed at the drooling of the recipient. The method was crude but it worked" (015-004).

During the industrial merger wave of the late nineteenth century, "the only architect in Chicago to catch the significance of this movement was Daniel Burnham, for in its tendency toward bigness, organization, delegation, and intense commercialism, he sensed the reciprocal workings of his own mind" (015-065). Sullivan watched him grow "into a colossal merchandiser" (015-010).

Burnham's crowning achievement -- and, in Sullivan's view, his greatest betrayal -- was the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition. In the fall of 1890, "John Root was officially appointed consulting architect, and Daniel Burnham, Chief of Construction" (015-074). After Root's death in January 1891, Burnham consolidated control and orchestrated the Fair's adoption of neoclassical architecture, which Sullivan denounced as a feudal imposition that set American architecture back "for half a century" (015-087).

### Relationship with Sullivan

The Burnham-Sullivan relationship was a complex mix of friendship, rivalry, and philosophical opposition. Burnham was capable of genuine sentiment: he once said to Sullivan, "See! Louis, how beautiful the moon is, now, overhead, how tender. Something in her beauty suggests tears to me" (015-068). Yet he also delivered pragmatic advice that Sullivan found anathema: "It is not good policy to go much above the general level of intelligence" (015-068).

Sullivan's portrait contrasts the two men in structural terms. Burnham was "elephantine, tactless, and blurting" but "his purpose was fixed" (015-004). He "chose the easier way" -- the way of power, organization, and commercial success. Sullivan chose "the harder" -- the way of democracy, art, and creative independence.

> "Daniel Burnham was obsessed by the feudal idea of power. Louis Sullivan was equally obsessed by the beneficent idea of Democratic power. Daniel chose the easier way, Louis the harder. Each brooded incessantly." (015-005)

Burnham's practical advice -- "delegate, delegate, delegate" (015-010) -- epitomized for Sullivan the merchant's approach to architecture, antithetical to the artist's personal engagement with form.

## Significance

Daniel Burnham survives in architectural history as both a genuine visionary -- his 1909 Plan of Chicago shaped the modern city -- and as Sullivan's embodiment of everything wrong with American architecture. Sullivan's critique was not petty: Burnham genuinely represented a competing model of architectural practice, one organized around business logic, corporate scale, and stylistic eclecticism.

Burnham's "feudal" architecture of the 1893 Fair won the day in the short term, dominating American civic architecture for decades. But Sullivan's "democratic" alternative -- an indigenous American modernism -- proved the more enduring influence in the long run, shaping twentieth-century architecture through Frank Lloyd Wright and the modernist movement.

Sullivan's acknowledgment of Burnham's "dream-imagination" (015-005) as a genuine power, even as he rejected its direction, gives his portrait of his rival a lasting psychological depth beyond mere polemic.
