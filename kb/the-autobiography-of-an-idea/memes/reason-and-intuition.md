# Reason and Intuition

Sullivan rejected the false choice between reason and intuition. Throughout his life and work, he insisted that authentic creation demanded both: the disciplined rigor of rational thought and the spontaneous insight of intuitive perception, working together as complementary powers of the complete human being.

## Definition

For Sullivan, reason meant the capacity for systematic thought — the logical method he encountered in geometry, where "proof was a reasoned process based on certain facts or assertions," with the satisfying finality of Q.E.D. (009-030). Intuition was something more primal: the direct, unmediated knowing that children possess, "close to nature's heart, close to the strength of Earth" (014-095). His distinctive contribution was the conviction that these two modes of knowing were not opposites but partners. The architect needed reason to master technique, but needed intuition to grasp what was worth building in the first place.

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan's dual commitment to reason and intuition drew on several traditions. The Enlightenment's faith in reason reached him through his scientific and mathematical education in Boston. The Romantic counter-current — the belief that intuition accesses truths reason cannot — came through his direct experience of nature and his reading of Transcendentalist thought. His training in Paris, particularly his encounter with descriptive geometry under Monsieur Clopet, taught him that rigorous method and creative vision could be one.

### Sullivan's Development

The young Sullivan's mind was shaped by both forces from the beginning. His "fourteen living years had been filled to overflow with vivid episodes, with active thoughts, with dreams, mysteries, prophetic intuitions and rude industrious practicalities, all commingled" (009-011). This was not a contradiction but a description of his creative personality — the boy who could build a dam with "big men" practicality could also lie on his back dreaming for hours.

His education sharpened both capacities. Geometry delighted him "because of its nicety, its exactitude of relationships, its weird surprises — all like fairy tales, fairy tales which could be proved" (009-030). He acquired knowledge "partly through a series of shocks — frequently humiliating — which inverted his illusions into realities; partly through his own keen powers of observation, and perhaps something in the way of intuition" (006-034).

As he matured, intuition deepened into conviction. His "steadfast belief in the power of man was an unalloyed childhood instinct, an intuition and a childhood faith which never for a day forsook him, but grew stronger, like an indwelling daemon" (013-014). This faith was not anti-rational — it was the ground from which his rational inquiries drew their energy and direction.

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> He had acquired the education he possessed partly through a series of shocks — frequently humiliating — which inverted his illusions into realities; partly through his own keen powers of observation, and perhaps something in the way of intuition (006-034).

> This steadfast belief in the power of man was an unalloyed childhood instinct, an intuition and a childhood faith which never for a day forsook him (013-014).

> By intuition the child is mystic — close to nature's heart, close to the strength of Earth (014-095).

> Man's amazing power to do, should, in all decency and all reason, be coupled with Romance in the deed (008-008).

### In Architecture

Sullivan's architectural practice embodied the reason-intuition synthesis. His development of the vertical system for tall buildings was a rational response to the daylight problem — slender piers, masonry-and-iron combinations, structural logic. But the aesthetic form this system took was guided by intuition: the upward-reaching rhythm, the sense of aspirational energy, the conviction that a skyscraper should "look tall."

His critique of academic classicism was precisely that it had severed reason from intuition. The classical "Orders" had become "rigid, mechanical and inane" — reason ossified into formula, intuition banished entirely. "This line of reasoning amused Louis quaintly. It seemed to him romantic; much like a fairy tale" (010-024). Real architecture, he believed, required both the geometric clarity of the head and the "Romance" of the heart.

## Legacy

Sullivan's reconciliation of reason and intuition prefigures later attempts to bridge the "two cultures" of science and art. His insistence that technical mastery and creative vision are not enemies but necessary partners influenced architectural education far beyond his own practice. In an era that often pits algorithmic optimization against human intuition, Sullivan's integrated model remains urgently relevant.
