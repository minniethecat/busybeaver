## Definition

For Sullivan, personality meant the unique, individual character
that distinguishes one being from another. He applied this concept
both to human beings and to architecture, arguing that a building,
like a person, should possess a distinct and recognizable
personality [011-002] [011-003].

## Personality in Architecture

Sullivan believed that great buildings expressed personality —
they were not anonymous or interchangeable. He admired architectural
works that had "allure or style indicating personality":

> The word personality, in solitary and unique grandeur [011-002]

He contrasted this with mere functional structures that lacked
any expressive individual character [015-002].

## Men of Personality

Throughout his autobiography, Sullivan noted individuals of
"marked personality" who influenced his development. John Root
possessed a "magnetic personality" that attracted Sullivan, while
John Edelmann was "unique in personality among fine and brilliant
minds" [011-032] [015-003].

Sullivan's own unconsciousness of his personality was, paradoxically,
one of his defining traits:

> his unconsciousness of his own personality was remarkable to the point
> [011-023]

## Personality as Power

Sullivan described personality as a concentrated form of power.
In his description of Michelangelo, personality became a force
that transcended normal human limits:

> Here was that great and glorious personality. Here was power as he had seen
> [012-035]

He conceived of Man as "a vast personality, within which were
gathered all the powers" — a composite of creative, spiritual,
and intellectual forces [012-039] [012-040].
