# Massachusetts Institute of Technology

The Massachusetts Institute of Technology, where Louis Sullivan enrolled in 1872 at age sixteen, was his first formal exposure to architectural education -- an experience that left him feeling that the school was "but a polite introduction to the architectural Art" and drove him toward more rigorous training in Philadelphia and Paris.

## Overview

When Sullivan entered MIT in 1872, the School of Architecture was housed in Rogers Hall on Boylston and Berkeley streets in Boston, "adjoining, on the south, the Museum of Natural History" (010-017). The school was presided over by Professor William R. Ware of the Boston firm Ware & Van Brunt, whose important works included the Memorial Building at Harvard and the Worcester Railway Station (010-018). Eugene Letang, "a diplome of the Ecole Nationale des Beaux Arts, Paris," served as assistant (010-019). The program enrolled perhaps thirty students and followed a curriculum modeled on the Beaux-Arts system, with drawing, the Orders, and technical drafting at its core.

Sullivan stayed for only one academic year. His experience was one of gathering dissatisfaction: he found the teaching deadened by pedantry, the curriculum fixated on the letter rather than the spirit, and the entire enterprise "but a pale reflection of the Ecole des Beaux Arts" (010-026). The year taught him what he did not want from architectural education, setting in motion the trajectory that would take him first to Frank Furness's office in Philadelphia and then to Paris.

## Key Figures

- **William R. Ware** (1832--1915) -- Founder of MIT's architecture program and later founder of Columbia University's School of Architecture. Sullivan described him with ironic detachment: "neither immaculate Professor Ware nor sweaty, sallow, earnest Eugene Letang was a Moses Woolson" (010-026). Ware represented the academic establishment that Sullivan found wanting.

- **Eugene Letang** -- Ware's assistant, a French-trained architect described as "sallow earnestness itself; long and lean of face with a scanty student beard" (010-020). Letang was "a transplanted massier as it were of the atelier," a living emissary of the Beaux-Arts method whom Sullivan would later surpass by going to the source.

- **Moses Woolson** (1813--1894) -- Not an MIT figure, but Sullivan's constant point of comparison. Woolson, his teacher at Boston's English High School, represented genuine pedagogy: a teacher who gave "all that he had of the best to give" (009-026). For Sullivan, "there may have been teachers and teachers, but for Louis Sullivan there was and could be only one" (009-034). MIT's faculty failed this standard.

## Role in Sullivan's Career

Sullivan entered MIT at sixteen, "well settled in the Massachusetts Institute of Technology" (010-002) after two years living with the Tompson family in Wakefield. His grandfather Henri List had earlier expressed skepticism of formal education, warning that teachers "are not inspired. They are victims of routine" (008-012). The MIT experience confirmed this warning.

The course of study consisted of tracing the "Five Orders of Architecture" with drafting precision, learning about "diameters, modules, minutes, entablatures, columns, pilasters," and all the technical apparatus of classical architectural drawing (010-022). Sullivan performed faithfully: "He learned not only to draw but to draw very well" (010-022). But the intellectual content deadened him. The Orders "seemed to say, 'The book is closed; Art shall die'" (010-023). He reflected: "Why five orders? Why not one? Each of the five plainly tells a different story. Which one of them shall be sacrosanct?" (010-023).

The deeper problem was the absence of living ideas. At MIT, Sullivan felt "the need and the lack of a red-blooded explanation, of a valiant idea that should bring life to arouse his cemetery of orders and of styles" (010-025). The school substituted "the word" for "the spirit" (010-025). He "began to discover that this school was but a pale reflection of the Ecole des Beaux Arts" (010-026) and resolved to leave.

His departure was decisive and optimistic. He was "progressive, aggressive and impatient. He wished to live in the stream of life" (010-027). First he sought out the architectural offices of Philadelphia, where Frank Furness's explosive personality offered a dramatic contrast to academic Boston. When Furness asked why Sullivan had left MIT, Sullivan "said he had just come from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in Boston, at which Furness glared and said he (Louis) was an idiot to have wasted his time in a place where one was filled with sawdust, like a doll, and became a prig, a snob, and an ass" (010-029).

## Major Achievements

MIT's School of Architecture, founded in 1868, was the first formal architectural program in the United States and established the model for American architectural education. Under Ware's leadership it pioneered the integration of technical training with design instruction, preparing a generation of architects for professional practice. The program's emulation of the Beaux-Arts system -- with atelier instruction, the teaching of the Orders, and competitive projects -- became the template adopted by most American architecture schools.

## Legacy

Sullivan's brief MIT career illuminates a recurring tension in professional education: the gap between technical training and intellectual awakening. He did not deny the value of learning to draw well; his drafting skills became fundamental to his practice. But he refused to accept that mastering forms was equivalent to understanding architecture. The MIT experience -- combined with the contrasting model of Moses Woolson's teaching and Frank Furness's "red-blooded" practice -- crystallized Sullivan's conviction that genuine education must cultivate "the creative power and glory of man" (010-023) rather than merely transmit historical forms. This conviction would shape his later philosophy of architectural education and undergird his critique of the Beaux-Arts, even as he pursued its training in Paris.
