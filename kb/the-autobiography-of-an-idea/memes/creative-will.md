# Creative Will

Sullivan's view that will is the volitional complement to imagination — the steady, disciplined, and often stubborn force that converts visionary ideas into accomplished realities through sustained effort over time.

## Definition

For Sullivan, will is not mere obstinacy or brute strength of character but the practical engine of creative achievement. Where imagination supplies the vision of what could be, will supplies the persistence to realize it. The two are inseparable in Sullivan's thought: every major passage on imagination pairs it with will. He speaks of "imagination and will" as a compound noun, a single faculty with two aspects — the visionary and the executive. In his most concise formulation, "the immense growth in power of constructive imagination and of the will to do" (014-077) represents humanity's highest development. Will is what converts daydream into drawing, drawing into building, impulse into completed work. Its characteristic quality is doggedness: the capacity to continue when the initial inspiration has faded, to persist through obstacles, tedium, and self-doubt.

## Origins

### Inherited Disposition

Sullivan traces will to his paternal lineage. His father, Patrick Sullivan, possessed "the grim will, the instinctive ambition that had brought him, alone and unaided, out of a childhood of poverty" (001-013). Sullivan reports that at the mere age of two, he himself "had developed temper, strong will, and obstinacy" (001-016) — the raw, unrefined form of the executive force that would later be channeled into creative ends. The young Sullivan was also "a more stubborn will than is usual" (007-038), a trait that made him resist conventional schooling but would later sustain his architectural independence.

### The Exemplar: Bill Curtis

The critical transformation of Sullivan's understanding came through the athlete William B. Curtis. The "Great Chief" of the Lotus Club (011-033) — a consumptive who had rebuilt himself into a champion athlete — demonstrated will not as aimless stubbornness but as disciplined program. Sullivan records the effect precisely:

> "The effect of Bill Curtis upon Louis was not merely that of a magnificent athlete and man of brains, but primarily, and most valuably that of exemplar in the use of the imagination and the will, doggedly to carry out a program." (011-036)

This passage crystallizes Sullivan's mature concept: the pairing of imagination-and-will directed toward a defined program, pursued with dogged persistence. That Curtis was a consumptive who had "risen to become a great athlete" (011-036) made the lesson indelible — will was not a gift but an achievement.

### Sullivan's Development

Sullivan's own development as an architect was predicated on will. He reports that he "arrived slowly though boldly through the years, by means of incessant thought, self correction, hard work and dogged perseverance" (013-036). This was not a linear path but one of interruptions and renewals. After the economic depression and the Chicago fire, "the interrupted story of imagination and will, again renewed its deep refrain in arousing energy" (013-010). The pairing is always imagined as a story or refrain — a narrative carried forward by will across obstacles.

Sullivan also inherited from his father a model of pedagogical will. Patrick Sullivan, when explaining the curvature of the Earth to his son at Newburyport beach, "went at it bravely, patiently, doggedly, step by step; he even made diagrams on his drawing pad" (005-035). The adverb "doggedly" recurs across Sullivan's descriptions of effective action — it is the signature of will in operation.

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> "As piece after piece was deployed, before his open mind, he saw arise a Mighty Personality — a great Free Spirit, a Poet, a Master Craftsman, striding in power through a vast domain that was his own, that imagination and will had bodied forth out of himself." (011-030)

> "Here, indeed, had been lifted a great veil, revealing anew, refreshing as dawn, the enormous power of man to build as a mirage, the fabric of his dreams, and with his wand of toil to make them real." (011-030)

> "The effect of Bill Curtis upon Louis was not merely that of a magnificent athlete and man of brains, but primarily, and most valuably that of exemplar in the use of the imagination and the will, doggedly to carry out a program." (011-036)

> "The immense growth in power of constructive imagination and of the will to do." (014-077)

> "In a while the pulse of industry began the slow feeble beat of revival, and the interrupted story of imagination and will, again renewed its deep refrain in arousing energy." (013-010)

> "Quite the contrary, he arrived slowly though boldly through the years, by means of incessant thought, self correction, hard work and dogged perseverance." (013-036)

### In Architecture

Sullivan's buildings embody will in their very materiality. An architectural vision must be held steady across months and years of planning, negotiation, construction, and revision. Sullivan's insistence that the architect must master a "system of technique" (013-036) through relentless practice reflects his conviction that will is not merely the initial decision to act but the sustained discipline of craft. He "regarded every constructive human effort as an art" (013-017), meaning that even the mundane labor of producing working drawings, coordinating tradesmen, and supervising construction was infused with the same volitional energy as the initial creative vision. The "wand of toil" (011-030) is will's instrument: imagination conjures the vision, but will wields the wand.

## Legacy

Sullivan's pairing of imagination and will anticipated modern understandings of creative process — the insight that breakthrough ideas require not only insight but what psychologists now call "grit," the sustained passion and perseverance for long-term goals. His formulation is also distinctively democratic: the will to execute a program is available to anyone willing to cultivate it, not only to the innately gifted. The "enormous power of man to build as a mirage, the fabric of his dreams, and with his wand of toil to make them real" (011-030) makes no distinction between the architect, the composer, the athlete, or the teacher. Any human who pairs imagination with dogged will can build a domain of their own.
