# Montauk Block

The Montauk Block was a large nine-story office building in Chicago, designed by the firm [[burnham-and-root]]. Completed in 1882, it was among the tallest commercial structures of its era and a significant milestone in the development of the Chicago tall building. (015-054)

## Overview

Located in Chicago's central business district, the Montauk Block rose nine stories — an exceptional height for its day. It was built with solid masonry enclosing-and-supporting walls, the standard construction method before the advent of the steel frame. (015-054) The building housed the offices of Burnham & Root themselves, where [[John Wellborn Root]] worked on his designs and [[Daniel Burnham]] developed his ambitions for a "big business." (015-010)

## Design

### Architect

The Montauk Block was designed by [[John Wellborn Root]] of Burnham & Root. Root, whom Sullivan described as "effervescent, witty," an "excellent musician" and "well read on almost any subject," worked from his private office within the completed building. Sullivan visited Root there and observed him "at work designing an interesting detail of some building," drawing "with a rather heavy, rapid stroke" while chatting. (015-010)

### Architectural Features

As a masonry commercial building, the Montauk Block represented the standard of its period:
- Nine stories of load-bearing masonry enclosing-and-supporting walls (015-054)
- Iron columns and girders encased in fireproofing materials, reflecting advancing safety standards
- The building was regarded "with wonder" at its height — the benchmark by which later buildings were measured (015-054)
- It was among the buildings that established Burnham & Root as one of Chicago's two premier architectural firms, alongside Adler & Sullivan (015-005)

### Philosophy Embodied

The Montauk Block was, for Burnham & Root, both a workplace and a proving ground. It was within this building that Sullivan witnessed the dynamic tension between Root's artistic temperament and Burnham's commercial drive — a tension that would define the firm and, ultimately, American architecture. When Burnham told Root: "You ought to delegate that sort of thing. The only way to handle a big business is to *delegate, delegate, delegate*," Root sneered, and Burnham left "in something of a huff." (015-010) Sullivan "saw the friction of ideas between the artist and the merchant; a significant mismating which made him ponder." This observation deepened Sullivan's own conviction that architecture required the unified vision of a single creative will.

## History

### Commission & Construction

The Montauk Block was one of the early commissions that established Burnham & Root's reputation as builders of tall commercial structures. It was part of the wave of construction that followed Chicago's recovery from the Panic of 1873, as "the building industry took on an organizing definition" and "investors awakened to opportunity." (015-053)

### Reception

At nine stories, the Montauk Block was regarded with wonder when completed. It set the baseline for height in Chicago's commercial district, and its success spurred Burnham's ambition to go higher. The building's name became synonymous with the firm's early success, and it was here that Burnham formulated his vision of a practice that would "handle big things, deal with big business men." (015-002)

## Significance

The Montauk Block represents an important waypoint in the evolution of the tall commercial building. It demonstrated that nine-story masonry construction was feasible, establishing a baseline that the [[Auditorium Building]], the [[Monadnock Building]], and the [[Masonic Temple]] would successively surpass. For Sullivan, the building was most significant as the place where he observed firsthand the dynamics of the competing firm and began to clarify his own distinct philosophy — that architecture must be a "living art" guided by [[form-follows-function]], not by commercial ambition or stylistic novelty. (015-010) (013-034)
