# Adler & Sullivan

The architectural partnership of Dankmar Adler and Louis Sullivan, formed in Chicago in 1881, which became one of the two preeminent firms of the Chicago School and produced some of the most innovative commercial and civic buildings of the late nineteenth century.

## Overview

Adler & Sullivan represented a distinctive partnership between technical mastery and artistic vision. Dankmar Adler (born 1844) brought engineering genius, acoustical expertise, and business acumen; Louis Sullivan brought architectural imagination, a developing philosophy of organic form, and extraordinary talent as a designer (015-005). Together they created buildings that defined the Chicago School of architecture, including the Auditorium Building (completed 1889), the Wainwright Building in St. Louis (1891), and the Chicago Stock Exchange (1894). Sullivan described their complementary dynamic as one where each partner contributed what the other lacked: Adler handled structure, engineering, acoustics, and client relations, while Sullivan shaped the aesthetic and spatial conception of each work.

## Key Figures

- **Dankmar Adler** (1844--1900) -- Co-founder and engineering partner. Sullivan described him as "the only man living" who understood "that the matter of acoustics is not a science but an art" (015-013). Adler's technical brilliance made possible the firm's most ambitious projects, including the Auditorium Theatre's perfect acoustics and the structural innovations of the early skyscrapers. The strain of the firm's work "doubtless shortened Adler's life" (015-018).

- **Louis H. Sullivan** (1856--1924) -- Co-founder and design partner. Sullivan brought to the firm his Beaux-Arts training, his developing philosophy of organic architecture, and his conviction that "the architectural art to be of contemporary immediate value must be plastic" (013-034). His increasingly independent design sensibility would eventually carry him beyond the partnership.

- **John Edelmann** -- Foreman at Adler & Sullivan whose democratic idealism influenced Sullivan profoundly and who conceived the Auditorium as "in his soul to be democracy" (015-015).

## Role in Sullivan's Career

Sullivan's path to the partnership began in 1879 when he entered the office of Burling & Adler (013-020). He was immediately drawn to Adler's "broad serious face, and dark lustrous eyes that withheld nothing" (013-023). On May 1, 1880, the firm became D. Adler & Co. with Sullivan designing all ornament (013-032, 013-033). One year later, on May 1, 1881, the name became Adler & Sullivan, Architects (013-033).

The partnership gave Sullivan the platform to develop his architectural ideas at monumental scale. Where his earlier education at MIT and the Ecole des Beaux-Arts had been preparation, and his year with Frank Furness in Philadelphia had shown him "how, in effect, an actual building was brought about" (010-027), Adler & Sullivan provided the means to create. During this period Sullivan's "conviction increased that architectural manipulation, as a homely art of discrimination, must give way to the new birth of an architecture of democracy." The partnership represented the practical crucible in which his theoretical ideas were forged.

The firm's dissolution in 1895, after Adler departed to become consulting architect for a new company, marked a turning point in Sullivan's career. He continued practicing independently but never again commanded the scale of commissions that the partnership had attracted.

## Major Works

- **Auditorium Building** (1886--1889) -- A massive mixed-use structure combining a 4,250-seat theatre, hotel, and office tower, its "immense mass of ten stories, its tower, weighing thirty million pounds" made it a wonder of its age (015-054). Adler's acoustical design ensured perfect sightlines and sound for every patron; Edelmann "believed in his soul to be democracy" (015-015). The project's "unremitting strain" took four years and "doubtless shortened Adler's life" (015-016, 015-018).

- **Wainwright Building** (1890--1891) -- A nine-story office building in St. Louis where Sullivan applied "a system of vertical construction" to express the steel-frame structure in the exterior treatment (015-027). This became a foundational work of skyscraper design.

- Other important commissions included the Chicago Stock Exchange, the Schiller Building, and the Transportation Building at the World's Columbian Exposition of 1893 -- Sullivan's lone contribution to the Fair, which stood in defiant contrast to the prevailing classical revivalism.

## Legacy

Adler & Sullivan's work established the architectural vocabulary of the tall office building and demonstrated that commercial architecture could achieve artistic distinction. The partnership's influence extended through Frank Lloyd Wright, who worked as a draftsman in the office, and through the broader Chicago School. Sullivan's philosophy that "all senseless conventional rigidity must be taken out of" architecture, and his call for "the casting aside of all pedantry, of all the artificial teachings of the schools, of the thoughtless acceptance of inane traditions" (013-034, 013-035), found its practical expression in the firm's best buildings. The dissolution of the partnership in 1895 and Adler's death in 1900 marked the end of an era, but the buildings they created together remain touchstones of American architectural innovation.
