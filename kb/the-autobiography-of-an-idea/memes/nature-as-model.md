# Nature as Model

Sullivan's foundational belief that the architect should learn from nature's methods of organic development — where every form grows from within according to its inner purpose — rather than imitate historical styles or impose external decoration.

## Definition

Sullivan arrived at the conviction that architecture must follow nature's own creative logic: forms develop not by external imposition but by unfolding from an inner seed of purpose, exactly as a living organism grows. He expressed this insight most famously in his maxim that "form follows function" — a formula he had evolved "through long contemplation of living things" (013-034). For Sullivan, nature was not a catalogue of shapes to copy but a teacher of process: "In this wise the forms under his hand would grow naturally out of the needs and express them frankly, and freshly" (013-034). His architectural philosophy rested on the belief "that man's spirit must be free that his powers may be free to accomplish in beneficence" (013-025), a principle he saw embodied in the unimpeded development of every living thing.

## Origins

### Intellectual Sources

Sullivan drew on the major scientific currents of the nineteenth century. Darwin's *Theory of Evolution* "seemed stupendous" to him, and Herbert Spencer's definition of development — "a progression from an unorganized simple, through stages of growth and differentiation to a highly organized complex" — mapped directly onto Sullivan's own intellectual and creative development (013-026). He undertook "a course of reading covering Spencer, Darwin, Huxley, Tyndall, and the Germans," discovering "a new, an enormous world opening before him, a world whose boundaries seemed destined to be limitless in scope, in content, in diversity" (013-016).

John Draper's *Intellectual Development of Europe* reinforced Sullivan's belief in organic historical progress, tracing the emergence of science "as the spirit of man sought and found freedom in the open" (013-025). Sullivan also absorbed the scientific method itself as an instrument: "a relentless method whereby to arrive at the truth by tireless pursuit" (013-017), which he resolved to apply to architecture with "a craftsman's skill" (013-017).

### Sullivan's Development

Sullivan's nature-as-model philosophy grew from childhood experiences as much as from adult reading. As a boy in the Berkshire Hills, he experienced nature's power directly — "amid mists and pale moon gleaming, arose the mighty Berkshires, their summits faint and far, their immensities solemn, calm, seeming eternal" — an encounter that "struck deep" and first revealed that "the power of man" could coexist with nature's grandeur (008-005). This childhood revelation seeded a lifetime conviction: "This steadfast belief in the power of man was an unalloyed childhood instinct, an intuition and a childhood faith which never for a day forsook him, but grew stronger, like an indwelling daemon" (013-014).

As Sullivan matured, he "began to see the powers of nature and the powers of man coalesce in his vision into an ***idea** of power*" (013-014). He recognized this as "a complete reversal and inversion of the commonly accepted intellectual and theological concept of the Nature of man" (013-014). Sullivan understood that his idea "needs a long long time, and a rich soil of life-experience, to enable a simple, single idea to grow to maturity and solid strength," quoting Whitman's insight: "Nature neither hastens nor delays" (013-015). He wrote of himself in organic metaphor: "Louis became freshened with new growth as a tree in spring" (008-010), treating intellectual development as analogous to botanical renewal.

The Clopet demonstration model from his Paris training also shaped his thinking. Monsieur Clopet's geometry teaching — "our demonstrations shall be so broad as to admit of no exception" (012-007) — taught Sullivan that rigorous methods could yield universally applicable principles, a lesson he would later apply to architecture through the organic analogy.

## Expression

### In Sullivan's Words

> "He began to see the powers of nature and the powers of man coalesce in his vision into an ***idea** of power*. Then and only then he became aware that this idea was a *new idea* — a complete reversal and inversion of the commonly accepted intellectual and theological concept of the Nature of man." (013-014)

> "It needs a long long time, and a rich soil of life-experience, to enable a simple, single idea to grow to maturity and solid strength. ... the deep insight of Whitman is set forth in the line, 'Nature neither hastens nor delays.'" (013-015)

> "The Theory of Evolution seemed stupendous. Spencer's definition implying a progression from an unorganized simple, through stages of growth and differentiation to a highly organized complex, seemed to fit his own case, for he had begun with a simple unorganized idea of *beneficent power*, and was beginning to see the enormous complexity growing out of it." (013-026)

> "He would put to the test a formula he had evolved, through long contemplation of living things, namely that *form follows function*, which would mean, in practice, that architecture might again become a living art, if this formula were but adhered to." (013-034)

### In Architecture

Sullivan's nature-as-model philosophy translated into architectural practice through the principle that buildings must develop from the inside out. He insisted "that all practical demands of utility should be paramount as basis of planning and design; that no architectural dictum, or tradition, or superstition, or habit, should stand in the way" (013-034). Just as an organism's form is determined by its function, so architecture "must be *plastic*; all senseless conventional rigidity must be taken out of it; it must intelligently serve — it must not suppress" (013-034).

His early Chicago commercial buildings tested this organic method experimentally. The search for maximum daylight led to "slender piers, tending toward a masonry and iron combination, the beginnings of a vertical system" — a structural logic that grew from functional need rather than stylistic preference (013-035). Sullivan applied "his system of form and function" across varying building types. His conviction deepened "that architectural manipulation, as a homely art or a fine art must be rendered completely plastic to the mind and the hand of the designer; that materials and forms must yield to the mastery of his imagination and his will" (013-035). This was not the abandonment of artistic control but its reorientation — the architect, like nature, creates by working through inner principles rather than by applying surface decoration.

## Legacy

Sullivan's nature-as-model philosophy became the intellectual foundation of modern architecture. His insistence that buildings should grow organically from their purpose, that "form follows function," and that nature teaches process rather than pattern, shaped twentieth-century modernism from Frank Lloyd Wright's organic architecture to the functionalist traditions of European modernism. Yet Sullivan's vision was richer than mere functionalism: he sought the coalescence of nature's powers with human creative powers, an "*idea* of power" (013-014) that fused scientific method with poetic imagination. His reading of "Spencer, Darwin, Huxley, Tyndall, and the Germans" (013-016) placed architecture within the grand narrative of nineteenth-century evolutionary thought, and his conviction remained that "the world of expression is limitless; the theory so deep in idea, so rich in content, as to preclude any ending of its beneficent, all-inclusive power" (013-036).
