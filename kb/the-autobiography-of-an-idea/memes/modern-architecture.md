For Louis Sullivan, "the Modern" was not merely a chronological label but a world-historical drama — the long, turbulent emergence of democratic consciousness and industrial capability out of the feudal past, demanding an architecture adequate to its new conditions. He described this transformation in sweeping historical terms, seeing in it the central narrative of his age.

## The Modern Condition

Sullivan saw the modern era as a transformation of unprecedented scale and significance. "The great drama we herein have called the Modern, unique in the story of mankind, beginning with a small telescope, advancing to the radio, to the measurement of the stars, to the searching out of the utterly minute in Life's infinitude of variety, to enormous strides in developments of utility" (014-078). This was the context within which architecture had to find its new voice — not by borrowing from the past but by responding to the present.

The modern condition was defined by its break with feudal modes of thought. Sullivan described "serial collapses of age-long feudalism" from which "a new view of man" was arising (014-030). The feudal world had operated through imposition and inherited authority; the modern world, by contrast, demanded verification, inquiry, and democratic accountability.

## Democracy and Modern Thought

Sullivan traced a direct line from modernity to democracy. "In place of myopic ideas, democratic modern thought uses clear vision" (014-085). The feudal had been characterized by imposition and authority; the modern, by inquiry and verification. "Inquiry upon inquiry followed; invention upon invention, discovery upon discovery...the modern power grew and ramified" (014-076). For architecture, this meant that the age of received forms was over — the architect must think for himself.

> Glancing at our modern civilization we find on the surface a startling dissimilarity in its overt aspects as compared with the surface of things medieval. (014-062)

This dissimilarity was not superficial but structural: the feudal superstructure, built on inherited hierarchy and imposed authority, could not sustain the democratic forces that modern inquiry had unleashed. "The feudal concept of self-preservation" (014-047) had given way to a new imperative — the self-realization of the individual within a democratic framework.

## The Feudal Inheritance

Sullivan was acutely conscious of what modern architecture had to overcome. The feudal superstructure had "ever undermined itself" (014-070) through its own contradictions, yet its aesthetic habits persisted long after its political legitimacy had collapsed. Sullivan saw the persistence of historical styles in nineteenth-century architecture as a symptom of feudal consciousness surviving into the modern age — a "ghostly feudal scapegoat" (014-049) that the modern architect must finally exorcise through honest, democratic forms.

He traced how "the feudal super-power" operated through "groupings and summits" that had "no other purpose than to dominate" (014-071). Modern architecture, by contrast, must serve — must be accountable to the democratic community that builds and inhabits it.

## Technology as Catalyst

Modern architecture was made possible by modern industry. Sullivan recognized that "the Bessemer process itself was revolutionary, and the story of its early trials and tribulations, its ultimate success, form a special chapter in the bible of modern industry" (015-055). The steel frame, in particular, rendered traditional masonry construction obsolete and demanded an equally revolutionary architectural response — which Sullivan himself supplied in his tall office buildings.

> Thus the idea of a steel frame which should carry all the load was born. (015-060)

The steel frame liberated the architect from the structural constraints of masonry bearing walls. Sullivan noted with pride that "the architects of Chicago welcomed the steel frame and did something with it" (015-062), transforming a technical innovation into an architectural revolution. The tall office building — Sullivan's paradigmatic modern type — was the direct result of this technological breakthrough harnessed to democratic aspirations.

## The Architect's Task

The challenge of modern architecture, as Sullivan defined it, was to make form plastic to the designer's imagination and will. "Architectural manipulation, as a homely art or a fine art must be rendered completely plastic to the mind and the hand of the designer; that materials and forms must yield to the mastery of his imagination and his will; through this alone could modern conditions be met and faithfully expressed" (013-035). This was a radical proposition: the architect was not a curator of historical styles but a creative force shaping matter to meet modern life.

Sullivan argued that the modern architect must "think for himself" — a democratic imperative translated into design practice. The architect who merely reproduced historical forms was, in Sullivan's view, failing the fundamental demand of the age: to create forms native to the new conditions of democracy and industry.
