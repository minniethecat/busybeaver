## Definition

For Sullivan, thought was not cold ratiocination but a living,
creative power — "the power of the brain to turn the searchlight
of scrutiny upon the inner and the outer world" [015-001]. He
placed thought at the center of his philosophy of human
potential, arguing that every person possesses the capacity for
genuine thinking, which democracy must liberate.

## Thought and the Individual

Sullivan held that authentic thought was an individual act, not
the mere repetition of received ideas. He criticized education
that "suppressed the power of thought" in favor of rote
learning, arguing that true education must cultivate "the power
to think clearly, simply, and courageously" [015-003].

He believed that the capacity for independent thought was
universal — "the power to think is the common heritage of
mankind" — and that a democratic society must nurture this
capacity in all its citizens [015-005].

## Thought in Architecture

Sullivan applied his philosophy of thought directly to
architecture. The architect must not merely copy forms but think
through the problem from first principles:

> A building is the expression of a thought — the thought of
> its creator. If the thought is clear, strong, and true, the
> building will be clear, strong, and true. [015-010]

This conviction underlay his famous dictum that form follows
function — it was a demand that architectural form emerge from
thoughtful engagement with purpose rather than stylistic
imitation.

## The Discipline of Thinking

Sullivan practiced what he called "thinking without words" — a
deliberate cultivation of direct awareness. He described this
as creating "a wordless universe, with himself, silent, at the
center of it all" [011-001]. This practice, he claimed, brought
"a widening clarity; an increased sensitiveness to values; a
separate isolation of the permanent and the ephemeral" [011-001].
