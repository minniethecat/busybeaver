# Panic of 1873

The severe economic depression that began with the failure of Jay Cooke & Company in September 1873, plunging the United States into a prolonged downturn that lingered over Sullivan's arrival in Chicago and shaped the economic landscape of his early career.

## Background

The post-Civil War railroad boom had fueled a speculative economy built on credit and overexpansion. Jay Cooke & Company, the nation's premier banking house, had overextended itself financing the Northern Pacific Railway. When Cooke's firm collapsed on September 18, 1873, the panic spread rapidly through the American financial system. Banks failed, factories closed, and unemployment soared in what became known as the Long Depression, lasting in various forms until 1879.

## The Event

### What Happened

The Panic of 1873 triggered a nationwide economic collapse. In Chicago, the effects were pronounced but uneven. Sullivan arrived in the city in the midst of this depression and found a paradox: "In spite of the panic, there was stir; an energy that made him tingle to be in the game" (011-015). The city's rebuilding after the Great Fire of 1871 had created a kind of momentum that partially resisted the national downturn.

The crisis also struck Sullivan's family directly. His father Henri List's household had "collapsed and gone down. This time in fragments. Soon the farm was sold" (009-061). The financial strain was one factor pushing Sullivan toward an independent career path.

### Sullivan's Experience

Sullivan encountered the panic not through financial ruin but through its atmospheric effect on the city where he would build his career. The depression meant that architectural work was scarce — a reality he noted when "he bethought him he would enter the office of some architect" and found that "a few buildings showed talent in design, and a certain stability" (011-016). He had to navigate an economy where "there were still some buildings under way, or arranged for, on the momentum of the pre-panic boom (011-016).

In the Jenney office, Sullivan's foreman John Edelmann "would mount a drawing table and make a howling stump speech on greenback currency, or single tax" (011-021) — the monetary debates sparked by the depression were the intellectual background noise of Sullivan's early professional life. The economic instability reinforced Sullivan's sense that the architectural establishment of his day was built on shaky, "feudal" foundations.

## Impact

### On Sullivan

The Panic of 1873 shaped Sullivan's early career path indirectly. Coming to Chicago during hard times, he had to prove himself in a market where only the resourceful survived. The experience of making his way through economic adversity — moving from office to office, from the Jenney firm to independent practice and eventually partnership with Adler — forged the "dogged will" he would later identify as essential to creative achievement. The panic's legacy also appears in Sullivan's later warnings about speculative excess: "the hurricane swept away the pyramided paper structures of speculation. Its downpour washed away fancied gains" (015-083).

### Broader Significance

The Panic of 1873 was one of the most severe economic crises of the 19th century, and the depression it triggered lasted longer than any previous American downturn. It accelerated the concentration of capital and the rise of corporate power that would define the Gilded Age. For architecture, the gradual recovery set the stage for the building boom of the 1880s in which Sullivan would make his mark. "The old game began again with intensified fury, to come to a sudden halt in 1907" (015-085) — Sullivan lived to see the cycle repeat.
