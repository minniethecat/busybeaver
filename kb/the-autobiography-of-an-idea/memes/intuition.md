## Definition

In Sullivan's thought, intuition is a mode of direct knowing
that operates alongside and beyond reason. He associated it
with childhood perception, artistic insight, and the immediate
grasp of truth that precedes analytical understanding
[006-034] [007-036].

## Intuition and Childhood

Sullivan saw intuition as a natural faculty of childhood —
"an intuition and a childhood faith which needs no proof"
because it perceives directly:

> an unalloyed childhood instinct, an intuition and a childhood faith [013-014]

He described children as possessing "utterly childlike flashes
of intuition" and "welcoming of new worlds" — a capacity for
immediate, unprejudiced perception that education often dulled
[007-036] [009-011].

## Intuition and the Mystic

Sullivan connected intuition to mysticism — the direct
experience of unity with nature and spirit:

> by intuition the child is mystic — close to nature [014-095]

This mystical intuition gave the child (and the artist who
preserved it) access to truths that rational analysis alone
could not reach.

## Intuition in Creative Work

For Sullivan, intuition was essential to creative practice.
While reason analyzed and organized, intuition perceived
wholes and grasped relationships that escaped systematic
thought. His own creative method relied on "something in
the way of intuition" working in concert with observation
and reflection [006-034].
