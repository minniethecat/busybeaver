# Stoneham

Small Massachusetts town adjoining South Reading, connected by the Stoneham Road that formed the western boundary of Sullivan's childhood explorations and figured prominently in his memories of rural New England.

## Location

Stoneham lay west of South Reading (now Wakefield), Massachusetts, connected by the Stoneham Road — a thoroughfare that Sullivan described as running from South Reading to Stoneham across level land. The town was one of several rural communities, including Woburn, North Reading, and Saugus, that surrounded South Reading and formed the geography of Sullivan's early life. Sullivan introduces this geography at the very opening of his autobiography, guiding the reader along the route one would take after arriving at the station: "follows the first dirt road to the left... Straight ahead, running at right angles and terminating the road thus far traversed, was the main road from South Reading to Stoneham." This precise spatial orientation anchors the entire childhood narrative in a verifiable New England landscape (PN refs × 3: 002-001, 002-002, 006-032).

## In Sullivan's Life

### First Encounter

The Stoneham Road was part of Sullivan's earliest remembered landscape. In his autobiography, he situates his grandfather's farm in relation to it, describing "the main road from South Reading to Stoneham" with "level" land nearby. This road was one of the first named places in Sullivan's narrative, anchoring his childhood world in specific, local geography. The road also marked the starting point of his first great act of rebellion: "Without a word to anyone he set forth, following the Stoneham Road westward," escaping the hillside school to build his dam in a wooded ravine — the first assertion of the independent, creative will that would later define his architectural career (PN refs × 2: 002-002, 004-003).

### Significance

The Stoneham Road served as a recurring setting in Sullivan's autobiographical recollections. As a boy, he would follow the road westward on exploratory walks, absorbing the landscape that would later inform his sensitivity to natural forms. The road also carried the traffic of rural life — farmers, wagons, and the occasional "string of New England profanities" from passing teamsters. In one vivid memory, Sullivan recalled meeting a farmer friend on the Stoneham Road who was "setting forth in pomp and pride," a vignette that captures the road's role as the social artery of the farming community. The surrounding countryside was his classroom: "he made many tours of exploration; he called on many farmers and shoemakers" — even venturing into the stove foundry beside the railway depot, where a workman taught him the world of handicraft that became "the vestibule of the great world of art that he one day was to enter and explore" (PN refs × 3: 004-003, 004-016, 006-015).

### Sullivan's Description

> On the Stoneham road he met a farmer friend. (PN ref: 006-015)

## Architectural Context

Stoneham itself was not an architectural destination but part of the rural New England landscape that shaped Sullivan's formative sensibility. The contrast between this agrarian world and the urban intensity of Boston and later Chicago would become a central tension in Sullivan's thinking — the organic versus the mechanical, nature versus the grid. The Stoneham Road, as a named and remembered place, represents the grounding in specific locality that Sullivan believed all authentic architecture must possess. In his rural wanderings, Sullivan was already the nascent mystic: "beneath all the overlay the child was a mystic; inarticulate, wondering, believing" — a sensibility nourished by the fields and woods around Stoneham. The later change of South Reading's name to Wakefield, after the rattan magnate Cyrus Wakefield, marked the encroachment of commerce and industry upon this agrarian world — a transformation that Sullivan witnessed and that foreshadowed the larger tensions between tradition and modernity he would navigate as an architect (PN refs × 3: 004-003, 006-014, 006-040).
