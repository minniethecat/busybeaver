# Dankmar Adler

Dankmar Adler (1844–1900), architect and acoustical engineer, partner to Louis Sullivan in the firm Adler & Sullivan from 1880 to 1895. Sullivan described him as "essentially a technician, an engineer, a conscientious administrator, a large progressive judicial and judicious mind" (015-005) -- the indispensable practical counterweight to Sullivan's artistic intensity.

## Biography

### Early Life

Dankmar Adler was born in Germany in 1844 and immigrated to the United States with his family. He received training in engineering and architecture, developing both technical mastery and a broad, methodical intelligence that would later distinguish him from the more flamboyant designers of his era. By the 1870s he had established himself in Chicago's architectural community.

### Career

Adler's early practice included a partnership in the firm Burling & Adler. Sullivan's first encounter with him came through John Edelmann, who brought Louis to meet Adler at that office. When Louis was introduced, he saw "Adler whose broad serious face, and kindly brown efficient eyes" (013-023) made an immediate impression.

Adler's defining technical contribution was in acoustics -- a field he elevated from guesswork to art. Sullivan recorded that "the only man living, at the time, who had had the intelligence to discern that the matter of acoustics is not a science but an art -- as in fact all science is sterile until it rises to the level of art -- was Dankmar Adler" (015-013). Adler generously taught this art to Sullivan, and together they built a number of successful theatres. This expertise would culminate in their greatest joint project, the Auditorium Building (built 1885 to 1889), commissioned by Ferdinand W. Peck. "For four long years Dankmar Adler and his partner labored on this enormous, unprecedented work" (015-016), which seated 4,250 people and became a landmark of Chicago architecture.

Adler also designed the New Central Music Hall (1879), built in collaboration with promoter George A. Carpenter, and the Masonic Temple -- at the time Chicago's tallest building. His engineering grasp of the "Chicago Construction" system of iron and masonry made possible the structural innovations that Sullivan's aesthetic vision demanded.

### Relationship with Sullivan

The partnership between Adler and Sullivan began through John Edelmann's mediation. In early 1879, Edelmann told Sullivan that "Adler had cut loose from Burling, set up independently" and had work on hand, and that "this was Louis's opportunity." Edelmann explained that "Adler had all the strong points, but was feeble in design and knew it" (013-027). Adler was "cautiously and eagerly interested, but timid in making advances" (013-027).

When the two finally met, it was agreed that "Louis was to take charge of Adler's office, was to have a free hand, and, if all went well for a period, and they should get along together, there was something tangible in the background" (013-028). Sullivan found in Adler "a most congenial co-worker, open-minded, generous-minded, quick to perceive, thoroughgoing, warm in his enthusiasms, opening to Louis every opportunity to go ahead on his own responsibility, posting him on matters of building technique of which he had a complete grasp, and all in all treating Louis as a prize pet -- a treasure trove" (013-028). On May 1, 1880, the firm of D. Adler & Co. formally began, with Sullivan as partner.

Sullivan's assessment of the partnership dynamic was characteristically vivid:

> "Adler was essentially a technician, an engineer, a conscientious administrator, a large progressive judicial and judicious mind securing alike the confidence of conservative and radical, plenty of courage but lacking the dream-quality of Burnham; and such he must remain -- the sturdy wheel-horse of a tandem team of which Louis did the prancing." (015-005)

Adler was "Peck's man" on the Auditorium project, with Sullivan "rather dubious, but gradually came around -- conceding a superior aesthetic judgment -- which for him was in the nature of a miracle" (015-016). The personal warmth between them was real: Sullivan noted Adler's "elephantine" witticisms and the genuine friendship that sustained the partnership through years of intense work.

## Significance

Dankmar Adler represents the engineering and administrative dimension of the architectural partnership that produced some of Chicago's most significant buildings of the 1880s and early 1890s. Without Adler's technical mastery -- particularly in acoustics -- the Auditorium Building could not have succeeded. Without his business judgment and client relationships, Sullivan might never have had the platform to develop his radical design philosophy.

Sullivan's portrait is both respectful and candid: Adler "lacked sufficient imagination" -- "the imagination of the dreamer" (015-005) -- but this very limitation made the partnership effective. Adler provided the stable foundation upon which Sullivan built his visionary architecture. The partnership dissolved in 1895, and Adler died in 1900, but the buildings they created together remain monuments to a productive collaboration between technical mastery and artistic genius.
