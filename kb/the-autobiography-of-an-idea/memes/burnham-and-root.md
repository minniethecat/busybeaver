# Burnham & Root

The Chicago architectural firm of Daniel H. Burnham and John Wellborn Root, formed in 1873, which emerged alongside Adler & Sullivan as one of the two dominant practices of the Chicago School and became the organizing force behind the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition -- an event Sullivan regarded as a catastrophic triumph of feudal classicism over modern democratic architecture.

## Overview

Burnham & Root represented the establishment wing of Chicago architecture: commercially astute, technically accomplished, and oriented toward the centers of economic and social power. Sullivan described the symbiosis between the two leading firms: "there came into prominence in the architectural world of Chicago two firms, Burnham & Root, and Adler & Sullivan. In each firm was a man with a fixed irrevocable purpose in life, for the sake of which he would go it alone if necessary" (015-005). In Burnham & Root, that man was the flamboyant, red-headed John Root, the artistic partner; Daniel Burnham provided business organization and client cultivation. The dynamic was comparable to Adler & Sullivan's division of labor, but the firms diverged sharply in their architectural philosophy -- a divergence that became a dramatic public rupture at the World's Columbian Exposition.

## Key Figures

- **Daniel H. Burnham** (1846--1912) -- The business and organizational partner, later Chief of Construction for the World's Columbian Exposition. Sullivan first met Burnham during his eighteenth year, when Burnham introduced himself: "Glad to meet you. My name's Burnham: Daniel H. Burnham; my partner, John Root, is a wonder, a great artist" (015-002). Burnham was ambitious, politically adept, and possessed a "megalomania concerning the biggest, the tallest, the heaviest, the longest" (015-004). Sullivan acknowledged Burnham's "remarkable executive capacity" (015-080) while regarding his architectural vision as rudimentary. Burnham told Sullivan bluntly: "It is not necessary to take your art too seriously" (015-068).

- **John Wellborn Root** (1850--1891) -- The artistic partner, described by Sullivan as possessing "magnetic personality" but "not of Burnham's type, but redheaded, large, curly-haired, dreamy, with luminous eye" (015-003). Sullivan acknowledged Root's structural flair: his "mania was to be the first to do this or that or the other" (015-004), and he "grasped at novelties like a child with new toys" (015-004). Root's death in January 1891, just before the Fair's design began, removed the one architect who might have checked the classical revivalism Sullivan deplored. Sullivan noted pointedly that "John Root was not there. In faith he could not come. He had made his rendezvous the month before with Azrael" (015-076).

- **Richard Morris Hunt** (1827--1895) -- The "acknowledged dean of his profession" who chaired the architects' meeting for the Fair (015-077). His interruption -- "Hell, we haven't come out here on a missionary expedition. Let's get to work" (015-078) -- became emblematic of the no-nonsense, establishment-driven approach Sullivan resented.

## Role in Sullivan's Career

Sullivan's relationship with Burnham & Root was one of professional rivalry, grudging respect, and philosophical opposition. He visited Root's office in the Montauk Block, where he found John at work "designing an interesting detail of terra cotta" while Burnham entered with peremptory commands (015-010). Sullivan respected Root's boldness but recognized that Root "had a temper" and valued "social prestige" in ways Sullivan found compromising (015-011).

The critical turning point came with the World's Columbian Exposition. When Burnham was appointed Chief of Construction (015-074), he "selected five architects from the East and five from the West" to design the Fair's buildings (015-075). Sullivan served as secretary at the February 1891 meeting, taking notes as Burnham "was progressively and grossly apologizing to the Eastern men for the presence of their benighted brethren of the West" (015-077). Sullivan was given the Transportation Building -- a "golden doorway" on the periphery of the Court of Honor -- while the central commissions went to Hunt, McKim, Mead & White, and other Eastern classicists.

Sullivan came to see the entire Fair enterprise as the vehicle through which Burnham's organizational genius imposed a dead classical style on American architecture. "Burnham performed in a masterful way, displaying remarkable executive capacity" (015-080), but the result was architectural disaster: "The damage wrought by the World's Fair will last for half a century from its date" (015-087).

## Major Works

- **Montauk Block** (1882) -- The nine-story office building that established Chicago's skyscraper type; Sullivan noted that it "had reached the height of nine stories and was regarded with wonder" (015-054).

- **World's Columbian Exposition** (1893) -- Under Burnham's direction as Chief of Construction, the firm orchestrated the largest architectural project America had yet seen. Root was originally to serve as consulting architect but died before the work began (015-074, 015-076). The resulting White City of classical pavilions set American architecture on a course Sullivan deplored.

- **Rookery** (1888) — A landmark Chicago office building with Root's innovative structural design combining load-bearing masonry and iron framing.
- **Monadnock Building** (1891) — The sixteen-story masonry landmark that Sullivan called "the first and last word of its kind... an amazing cliff of brickwork, rising sheer and stark" (015-055); the high-water mark of load-bearing masonry construction.
- **Masonic Temple** (1892) — For a time the tallest building in the world, whose height gave the word "skyscraper" its currency (015-066).

## Legacy

Burnham & Root's legacy is twofold. On one hand, the firm contributed essential innovations to the Chicago School skyscraper and demonstrated the organizational capacity that large-scale commercial architecture demanded. Burnham's "Plan of Chicago" (1909) became a foundational document of American city planning. On the other hand, the firm -- and Burnham personally -- became, in Sullivan's narrative, the agent through which feudal classicism was imposed on American democratic architecture. Sullivan's critique was prophetic: the classical revival dominated American civic buildings for the next half-century, exactly as he predicted. The tension between Burnham's commercial pragmatism ("It is not necessary to take your art too seriously") and Sullivan's artistic intransigence remains a paradigmatic conflict in the profession.
