# Henri List

Henri List, the maternal grandfather of [[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]], was a German immigrant of enigmatic origins — "straight German of the Hessian type" (001-008) — whose life traced an arc from Catholic seminary rebel to professor of Greek at Geneva, and finally to a turbulent old age in Massachusetts marked by financial ruin and personal tragedy. Louis Sullivan's autobiography paints him with a mixture of affection, bemusement, and unsparing candor: a man "tainted with cupidity" yet capable of deep love for his wife Anna, whose death shattered him (001-010, 009-061).

## Biography

### Early Life and the Broken Priesthood

Henri List was "reticent as to his past," but family gossip held that "as a young man he was educated for the Catholic priesthood, rebelled at the job and ran away from home" (001-009). The years between his flight and his arrival in Geneva, Switzerland remained a blank to the family — a silence that Sullivan records without attempting to fill. In Geneva, he established himself as a scholar: "was he a Professor of Greek in the University, or did he coach rich young Englishmen in the intricacies of that language?" (001-010). The ambiguity itself becomes part of his character — a man whose past refused to resolve into fixed fact.

> "Henri List was reticent as to his past, but the family gossip had it that as a young man he was educated for the Catholic priesthood, rebelled at the job and ran away from home." (001-009)

### Life in Geneva and the List Daughters

In Geneva, Henri married Anna, a woman of steady character who became "his prop, his anchor, his life's mainstay" (009-041). Together they raised daughters, including Andrienne — Louis Sullivan's mother. Andrienne inherited her father's expressive features and her mother's resilience. "She had the typical eyelids, expressive hazel eyes, an oval face, features mobile" (001-008).

### "Tainted with Cupidity"

Sullivan's portrait of his grandfather is notably honest about his flaws:

> "There seems to be no doubt that Henri List was tainted with cupidity. He was always speculating in something or other — and always losing." (001-010)

This compulsion to speculate — to reach for wealth beyond his station — became the recurring theme of Henri's later life. It would eventually destroy the security he had built.

### The Summer of 1870

"Early in the summer of '70, Henri List felt an impelling desire to visit his second daughter, Jennie, whom he had not seen in a number of years" (008-002). Jennie had married a certain Walter Whittlesey, a contracting railway engineer, and they lived near Boston. This visit brought Henri to America, where the trajectory of his life would take its final, difficult turns.

The visit proved fateful. "For Henri List, conforming to custom, was gradually lured into speculation" (008-004). What began as a family reunion became "a nightmare" of financial entanglement, as Henri's old weakness for speculation reasserted itself on American soil.

### Collapse and Tragedy

"Early in 1871 Anna List, his wife, his prop, his anchor, his life's mainstay, was taken with her first and last illness" (009-041). Anna died on April 2, 1871, "aged sixty-three years, ten months and twenty-four days" (009-058). In the death notice, Sullivan detected "the cynic hand of Henri List" — a dryness of statement so precise it seemed to betray more than grief (009-059).

The final blow came soon after:

> "For the second time the house of Henri List had collapsed and gone down. This time in fragments. Soon the farm was sold." (009-061)

Julia, the fiery-haired servant who had been "cook and companion for nine long years, vanished in turmoil" (009-061). Henri List had lost his wife, his farm, and his household — all within a matter of months.

## Relationship with Sullivan

Henri List appears in Sullivan's autobiography primarily as a background figure — the source of his mother's lineage, the German-Hessian blood that balanced his father's Irish temperament. The young Louis observed his grandfather's financial disasters from the periphery, learning perhaps an early lesson in the danger of speculation and the fragility of domestic security.

Sullivan's treatment of Henri is characteristically layered: affection for the man who "loved flowers, his garden, his grape arbor, his books" tempers the blunt acknowledgment of his cupidity (001-010). The portrait is that of a complex, contradictory figure — a scholar who speculated, a rebel priest who never quite found his place, a loving husband whose "cynic hand" survived his wife's death.

## Significance

Henri List represents the European, scholarly, and unstable element in Sullivan's ancestry. Through Henri, Sullivan inherited not wealth but a certain intellectual lineage — the classical learning, the German precision, the restless ambition that refused to stay within the bounds of a single vocation. Henri's life, with its dramatic arcs of flight, achievement, love, and ruin, reads almost as a compressed foreshadowing of Sullivan's own: the grandson too would rebel against tradition (architectural rather than religious), know spectacular success and painful decline, and pour his final energies into written words rather than built structures.

| Trait | Henri List | Louis Sullivan |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Origin | German (Hessian) | American (Irish-German) |
| Rebellion | Fled Catholic priesthood | Rejected Beaux-Arts classicism |
| Vocation | Professor of Greek | Architect and philosopher |
| Weakness | Speculation, cupidity | (none explicitly paralleled) |
| Late life | Financial collapse, farm sold | Poverty, death in obscurity |
| Legacy | Grandfather of America's greatest architect | Father of the skyscraper |
