# Romance

Romance, in Sullivan's vocabulary, signified far more than sentimental love. It was the quality of heightened experience — the spirit of adventure, wonder, and creative vitality — that he believed must suffuse both life and architecture. When Romance was present, a building, a person, or a city came alive; when it was absent, mere mechanical existence remained. Throughout *The Autobiography of an Idea*, Romance functions as both a biographical theme and an architectural principle, connecting Sullivan's childhood receptivity to story and wonder with his mature conviction that buildings must embody the life-force of creative imagination.

## Romance as a Quality of Experience

Sullivan's earliest encounter with Romance came through storytelling. His mother's Irish servant "told tales of the old sod, fairy stories, and tales of wonder" that captivated the young Louis: "for here was Romance — and romance he could not withstand" [006-014]. This receptivity to Romance became a permanent feature of his character. He later reflected that the child's mind, "aside from the romance, was a budding sense of order and of time" [001-017], placing Romance alongside rationality as a fundamental mode of apprehending the world. His cousin Julia "gave out a perfume of romance, that radiated joy" [007-022], embodying the quality as a personal attribute. Under good teaching, even "grammar passed into romance; a dead book became a living one" [007-039] — Romance had the power to transform the mundane into the vital.

These early experiences established a pattern that Sullivan would later apply to architecture: Romance was not an addition to experience but a transformation of it. A thing became romantic not through decoration but through the intensity of its engagement with life. The fairy story, the radiant cousin, the animated grammar lesson — each demonstrated that Romance was a mode of attention, a way of perceiving the world with heightened receptivity.

## The Romance of Cities and the Sea

Sullivan's encounter with Chicago was framed in the language of Romance. He anticipated that "with clearsighted eyesight he would find a great romance there, also a great tragedy" [011-031], recognizing that Romance and tragedy were inseparable dimensions of genuine experience. His journey across the Atlantic prompted a lament for the lost romance of sail: "Where was the romance of the high seas? The end came when the clipper ship era passed" [011-042]. Even in Paris, amid the "surviving fragrance of romance" in the old bookstalls along the Seine [011-051], he sought the quality that animated places with meaning.

The pairing of Romance with tragedy in Sullivan's anticipation of Chicago is significant. He understood that Romance was not escapism — it was not the avoidance of difficulty but the capacity to find meaning and vitality within it. The great fire, the financial panics, the raw struggle of Chicago's growth — these were tragic, but they were also the material from which Romance was forged.

## Romance and Architecture

For Sullivan, architecture without Romance was lifeless. As a young man, he gazed upon certain buildings and felt their "lack of bigness, beauty and romance" [008-006] as a single impoverishment — the three qualities were, in his perception, inseparable. The architect's task was to create structures "full of glory, of adventure, of romance" [014-100], works that gave one "the thrill of romance" [015-054].

He "averred that romance need not die out; that there was still a vital power in the world" [015-002], positioning Romance as the life-force of creative work. This was not nostalgia for a lost past but an assertion that the modern world — with its steel frames, elevators, and commercial energies — was as capable of Romance as any previous era. The architect's responsibility was to perceive and express that capacity.

In his philosophy, the creative instinct itself was inseparable from "its romance" [014-091], and the highest achievement was work that expressed "the romance of goodwill toward men" [014-086] — a vision in which Romance, ethics, and architecture converged. This final formulation is striking: Romance culminates not in aesthetic pleasure alone but in a social vision, the romance of human solidarity expressed through built form.
