# The Tall Office Building

The tall office building was the central architectural problem of Chicago's building boom from 1880 onward — a new building type demanded by "the pressure of land values" and made possible only through the conjunction of the passenger elevator and the steel frame (015-057). For Louis Sullivan, it was not merely a technical challenge but a proving ground for his conviction that every genuine problem "contains and suggests its own solution" (015-028), and its resolution led directly to the formulation of his most enduring principle.

## Definition

The tall office building, as Sullivan understood it, was a commercial structure whose height exceeded what load-bearing masonry could economically support — a building type "pushing its structures higher and higher, until the Masonic Temple by John Root had raised its head far into the air, and the word 'skyscraper' came into use" (015-066). It was a building born of specifically modern conditions: concentrated urban population, rising ground values, the passenger elevator, and ultimately the steel skeleton frame. Sullivan distinguished the legitimate type from its debased imitations: the authentic version arose from "favoring physical conditions, and the economic pressure as then sanctified, combined with the daring of promoters" (015-063), while the copies — "the tall office buildings fronting the narrow streets and lanes of lower New York" — were "provincialisms, gross departures from the law of common sense" (015-062).

The tall office building was not merely a taller version of existing commercial architecture. It was an entirely new functional and structural problem requiring an equally new architectural expression. As Sullivan recognized with the Wainwright Building, "masonry construction, in so far as tall buildings were concerned, was a thing of the past, to be forgotten, that the mind might be free to face and solve new problems in new functional forms" (015-027).

## Origins

### The Structural Crisis

Before 1880, Chicago's commercial buildings relied on "solid masonry enclosing-and-supporting walls" (015-054). The Montauk Block, at nine stories, was "regarded with wonder" (015-054). The Auditorium Building followed with ten stories and a tower "weighing thirty million pounds, equivalent to twenty stories — a tower of solid masonry carried on a 'floating' foundation" (015-054). Burnham and Root then prepared plans for a sixteen-story solid masonry office building, the Monadnock, postponed "until it should be seen whether or not the Auditorium Tower would go to China of its own free will" (015-054). The Monadnock ultimately went ahead — "an amazing cliff of brickwork, rising sheer and stark" — and Sullivan would call it "the first and last word of its kind... marking the high tide of masonry construction as applied to commercial structures" (015-055).

But even as the Monadnock rose, masonry's inherent limits were undeniable. The ever-thickening walls "ate up ground and floor space of ever increasing value" (015-057). The very tall masonry office building was "in its nature economically unfit," its "unavoidably small window openings" unable to furnish adequate light (015-058). Thus arose the crisis:

> "Thus arose a crisis, a seeming impasse. What was to do? Architects made attempts at solutions by carrying the outer spans of floor loads on cast columns next to the masonry piers, but this method was of small avail, and of limited application as to height. The attempts, moreover, did not rest on any basic principle, therefore the squabblings as to priority are so much piffle." (015-058)

The real problem, Sullivan insisted, "had not been solved, because the solution had not been sought within the problem itself — within its inherent nature" (015-058).

### The Enabling Technologies

Two inventions broke the masonry height limit. The passenger elevator was essential: "an office building could not rise above stairway height without a means of vertical transportation" (015-057). Once developed for safety, speed, and control, it "removed the limit from the number of stories" (015-057).

The steel frame was the definitive breakthrough. The Bessemer process for mild steel had been limited to rails, with structural shapes still rolled from iron (015-056). But the mills had been producing structural shapes for bridge work, so "their own ground work thus was prepared. It was a matter of vision in salesmanship based upon engineering imagination and technique. Thus the idea of a steel frame which should carry *all* the load was tentatively presented to Chicago architects" (015-060). Sullivan called "the passion to *sell*" the impelling force: "The need was there, the capacity to satisfy was there, but contact was not there. Then came the flash of imagination which saw the single thing. The trick was turned; and there swiftly came into being something new under the sun" (015-061). The true steel-frame structure, he declared, "stands unique in the flowing of man and his works; a brilliant material example of man's capacity to satisfy his needs through the exercise of his natural powers" (015-061).

### Conditions in Chicago

The tall office building did not arise in a vacuum. Sullivan traced a chain of causation: "The tall commercial building arose from the pressure of land values, the land values from pressure of population, the pressure of population from external pressure" (015-057). Chicago's population grew from 500,000 in 1880 to a million in 1890 — "and this, from a pitiful 4,000 in 1837" (015-053). The city had become "the center of a great radiating system of railways" and "grew and flourished by virtue of pressure from without — the pressure of forest, field and plain, the mines of copper, iron and coal, and the human pressure of those who crowded in upon it from all sides seeking fortune" (015-053). Sullivan set 1880 as "the zero hour of an amazing expansion," by which time "manufacturing expanded with incredible rapidity, and the building industry took on an organizing definition. With the advance in land values, and a growing sense of financial stability, investors awakened to opportunity, and speculators and promoters were at high feast" (015-053). The tall office building "would seem to have arisen spontaneously, in response to favoring physical conditions, and the economic pressure as then sanctified, combined with the daring of promoters" (015-063).

## Expression

### Chicago vs. New York

Sullivan drew a sharp contrast between regional responses to the steel frame:

> "The architects of Chicago welcomed the steel frame and did something with it. The architects of the East were appalled by it and could make no contribution to it." (015-062)

This was not mere regional pride. Sullivan argued that the tall office building required surroundings congenial to its nature: "when such buildings are crowded together upon narrow streets or lanes they become mutually destructive" (015-062). The New York skyscrapers crammed into lower Manhattan violated the logic of the type. For Sullivan, the authentic tall building made "a powerful appeal to the architectural imagination where there is any" (015-062). Its essential aesthetic lay in "the element of loftiness, in the suggestion of slenderness and aspiration, the soaring quality as of a thing rising from the earth as a unitary utterance, Dionysian in beauty" (015-062). Failure to perceive this "has resulted in a throng of monstrosities, snobbish and maudlin or brashly insolent" — in either case "a defamation and denial of man's finest powers" (015-062). Chicago's system became known as the "Chicago Construction," and "the art of design in Chicago had begun to take on a recognizable character of its own" (015-068). Sullivan's Wainwright Building in St. Louis gave the steel frame "first authentic recognition and expression in the exterior treatment" (015-027).

### Toward "Form Follows Function"

The tall office building was the problem that forced Sullivan to articulate his most famous principle. The masonry crisis had shown that incremental fixes — cast columns beside piers — were "of small avail" (015-058). The solution required a different approach. Sullivan generalized his teacher Monsieur Clopet's mathematical rigor — "Our demonstrations will be such as to admit of no exception" — into a universal law: "every problem of whatsoever name or nature, contains and suggests its own solution; and, the solution reached, it is invariably found to be simple in nature, basic, and clearly allied to common sense" (015-058).

When the steel frame arrived, Sullivan "felt at once that the new form of engineering was revolutionary, demanding an equally revolutionary architectural mode" (015-027). "The old ideas of superimposition must give way before the sense of vertical continuity" (015-027). The building's exterior should no longer imitate masonry bearing walls but should express the steel cage directly. This reasoning flowed into the principle he had formulated "through long contemplation of living things, namely that form follows function" (013-034). In the tall office building, the function — the steel skeleton carrying loads in vertical continuity — must determine the form. The architectural expression must grow "naturally out of the needs and express them frankly, and freshly" (013-034). The building must stand as a direct, honest expression of its structural nature. This was the intellectual breakthrough the tall office building demanded.
