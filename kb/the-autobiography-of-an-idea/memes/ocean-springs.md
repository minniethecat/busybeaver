# Ocean Springs

A small village on the Mississippi Gulf Coast where Louis Sullivan retreated in 1890 to recover from nervous exhaustion and overwork, finding in its quiet beauty the peace that restored his health and later inspired him to purchase land and build a winter home.

## Location

Ocean Springs lies on the Mississippi Gulf Coast, approximately "eighty odd miles to the east of New Orleans" on the back of Biloxi Bay (015-018). In Sullivan's time it was a sleepy village accessible by rail, landlocked by Deer Island across the gentle bay waters.

## In Sullivan's Life

### First Encounter

Sullivan first came to Ocean Springs in the spring of 1890, brought there by friends after he had suffered a severe breakdown. Following the unremitting strain of architectural work that had claimed his partner Dankmar Adler's health and shortened his life, Sullivan had tried to recover first in central California, then Southern California — but "the climate irritated him" in both places (015-018). He was in San Diego when he learned "at four o'clock one morning, what a 'slight' earthquake should be," after which "friends arrived who persuaded him to go with them to Ocean Springs" (015-018).

### Significance

Ocean Springs proved to be Sullivan's place of healing. In contrast to the California climate that had aggravated his condition, the gentle Gulf Coast restored him: "Here in this haven, this peaceful quiescence, Louis's nerves, long taut with insomnia, yielded and renewed their life. In two weeks he was well and sound" (015-020).

The village charmed him with its timeless, unhurried quality. He saw "an undulating village all in bloom in softest sunshine, the gentle sparkle of the waters of a bay landlocked by Deer Island; a village sleeping as it had slept for generations with untroubled surface; a people soft-voiced, unconcerned, easy going, indolent" (015-019). The main street, "sheltered by ancient live oaks," held "the general store, the post office, the barber shop, the meat market" — a vision of American life before industrialization (015-019).

Sullivan's recovery was so complete and his affection for the place so strong that he determined to acquire land. A Michigan Yankee settler offered him "a fine piece of woodland" — "it's pretty wild, now. But if you clear it of pines and undergrowth the live oaks will show" (015-021). This purchase established Sullivan's winter retreat, where he would later write much of his autobiography, *The Autobiography of an Idea*, in the quiet solitude of the Gulf Coast.

## Architectural Context

Ocean Springs represented the antithesis of urban Chicago — no tall buildings, no commercial boom, no architectural ambition. There were "no 'booms,' no booming for a 'Greater Ocean Springs,' no factories, no anxious faces" (015-019). The village's vernacular architecture of wooden cottages under live oaks, its "one-man jail in the middle of the street," and its saloon near the depot spoke to a pre-industrial American building tradition that stood in quiet contrast to Sullivan's skyscraper practice. It was here, far from the architectural battlegrounds of Chicago, that Sullivan composed the reflective prose of his autobiography.
