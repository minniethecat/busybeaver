## Definition

Function, for Sullivan, was not merely utilitarian purpose but
the essential life-activity of a thing — its reason for being,
the "suppressed function" whose liberation gives rise to form.
The concept crystallized through his conversations with John
Edelmann in Jenney's Chicago office [011-010].

## Origins of the Concept

Sullivan's understanding of function was catalyzed by Edelmann's
theory of "suppressed functions." When Edelmann explained the
idea, Sullivan experienced a revelation:

> Louis was peculiarly subject to shock from unexpected explosion
> of a single word; and when the word "function" was detonated
> by the word "suppressed," a new, an immense idea came suddenly
> into being and lit up his inner and his outer world as one.
> [011-010]

This insight — that every living thing has innate functions
seeking expression — became the foundation of Sullivan's entire
architectural philosophy.

## Function and Form

Sullivan's most famous statement — "form follows function" —
emerged from this deeper understanding. He did not mean that
form should be mechanically determined by utility, but that
form should express the vital inner purpose of a thing:

> It is the pervading law of all things organic and inorganic,
> of all things physical and metaphysical, of all things human
> and all things superhuman, of all true manifestations of the
> head, of the heart, of the soul, that the life is recognizable
> in its expression, that form ever follows function. This is
> the law. [015-040]

## Beyond Utility

Sullivan's concept of function encompassed emotional and
spiritual needs as well as practical ones. The function of a
building included its role in the life of its inhabitants —
their aspirations, their sense of beauty, their need for
meaning. He wrote that architecture must serve "the functions
of the body, the mind, and the spirit" [015-042].
