# Auditorium Building

The Auditorium Building, completed in 1889 in [[chicago|Chicago]], was the first major commission of the firm [[adler-and-sullivan|Adler & Sullivan]] and remains [[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]]'s most celebrated work of his early masonry period. It combined a 4,250-seat theater, a luxury hotel, and an office tower within a single monumental structure, embodying Sullivan's emerging philosophy that [[form-follows-function|form follows function]] (015-015).

## Overview

The Auditorium Building was a massive mixed-use project at the corner of Michigan Avenue and Congress Street in Chicago. It rose ten stories with an additional tower equivalent in height to twenty stories, weighing thirty million pounds. The building rested on a "floating" raft foundation measuring 67 by 100 feet — an engineering innovation by [[dankmar-adler|Dankmar Adler]] that distributed the immense load across Chicago's soft soil (015-054).

> "Louis's heart went into this structure. It is old-time now, but its tower holds its head in the air, as a tower should. It was the culmination of Louis's masonry 'period.'" (015-044)

The project took four years to complete:

> "For four long years Dankmar Adler and his partner labored on this enormous, unprecedented work. Adler was Peck's man. As to Louis he was rather dubious, but gradually came around — conceding a superior aesthetic judgment — which for him was in the nature of a miracle." (015-016)

## Design

### Architect

The design was led by [[dankmar-adler|Dankmar Adler]] and [[louis-sullivan|Louis H. Sullivan]] of the firm [[adler-and-sullivan|Adler & Sullivan]]. Adler, twelve years Sullivan's senior, was the technical genius behind the project — a "sturdy wheel-horse of a tandem team" whose expertise in acoustics and structural engineering made the colossal undertaking feasible (015-005). Sullivan, only thirty when the project began, was responsible for the aesthetic design, gradually winning over the skeptical client [[ferdinand-peck|Ferdinand W. Peck]] with his "superior aesthetic judgment" (015-016).

### Philosophy Embodied

The Auditorium Building embodied several of Sullivan's core architectural principles. The building's three distinct functional zones — theater, hotel, and offices — were given unified expression through a coherent exterior form, reflecting the principle that [[form-follows-function|form follows function]]. The project's sponsor, Ferdinand W. Peck, believed the theater "in his soul to be democracy" — a civic hall for the multitude rather than an exclusive opera house — aligning with Sullivan's concept of [[democracy-in-architecture|democracy in architecture]] (015-015).

## History

### Commission & Construction

The Auditorium Building originated from a temporary experiment. In 1885, Ferdinand W. Peck approached Dankmar Adler about acoustics for a grand civic hall. Together they installed a vast temporary audience room in the old Exposition Building on the lake front, seating 6,200 persons, and presented a two-week season of grand opera with world-famous artists (015-013).

> "The effect was thrilling. An audience of 6,200 persons saw and heard; saw in clear line of vision; heard, even to the faintest pianissimo. No reverberation, no echo — the clear untarnished tone, of voice and instrument, reached all. The inference was obvious: a great permanent hall housed within a monumental structure must follow." (015-014)

The experiment's success convinced Chicago that a permanent monumental structure must be built. Construction took four years. The strain of the project "doubtless shortened Adler's life," while Sullivan himself collapsed from utter weariness at its completion and convalesced in California and Mississippi (015-016, 015-018).

### Reception

The drawings of the Auditorium Building attracted notice while still in progress, and upon completion the building was "regarded with wonder" (015-054). At the time, the Montauk Block had reached nine stories and was considered extraordinary; the Auditorium surpassed it with its immense ten-story mass and twenty-story-equivalent tower. The tower became a proving ground for tall masonry construction — its stability was watched closely by [[daniel-burnham|Burnham]] and [[john-wellborn-root|Root]], who delayed their sixteen-story Monadnock Building until it was clear "the Auditorium Tower would go to China of its own free will." The tower "politely declined to go to China," validating Adler's floating foundation technique (015-054).

## Significance

The Auditorium Building marked the high tide of masonry construction for commercial structures. It proved that enormous loads could be carried safely on floating raft foundations, enabling taller buildings throughout Chicago (015-054). It established Adler & Sullivan as a leading architectural firm in Chicago, alongside [[burnham-and-root|Burnham & Root]], and launched Sullivan's career as an architect who could handle monumental commissions (015-044).

> "Then came the Auditorium Building with its immense mass of ten stories, its tower, weighing thirty million pounds, equivalent to twenty stories — a tower of solid masonry carried on a floating raft foundation, the first of its kind, 67 × 100 feet in area." (015-054)

Today, though Sullivan considered it "old-time," the Auditorium Building remains a National Historic Landmark and a defining work of the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] of architecture.
