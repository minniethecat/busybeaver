# The Chicago School: A Collaborative Portrait

The Chicago School was not a school in any formal sense — no curriculum, no faculty, no diploma. It was an emergent phenomenon: a dense nexus of builders, engineers, and architects thrown together by the Great Fire of 1871, competing and collaborating in such close quarters that ideas circulated with unusual speed. What made Chicago different from New York, Boston, or Philadelphia was not any single genius but the *structure of collaboration itself*.

## Thesis

The Chicago School's creative fertility can be traced to three mutually reinforcing conditions: (1) the physical and economic blank slate created by the Great Fire, which suspended architectural convention and rewarded technical innovation; (2) a partnership culture in which architects like Adler & Sullivan and Burnham & Root combined complementary skills under conditions of unusual mutual trust; and (3) Sullivan's deliberate experimental method, which treated each new commercial commission as a fresh test of his evolving "form follows function" hypothesis. These three forces — destruction, collaboration, and experimentation — converged in Chicago between 1871 and 1893 with an intensity unmatched in any other American city.

## I. The Fire as Catalyst

The Great Fire of October 1871 destroyed more than 17,000 buildings across nearly four square miles of Chicago's commercial core ([[great-chicago-fire-1871]]). The destruction was catastrophic, but its architectural consequence was perversely liberating: it erased a cityscape of mediocre wood-frame construction and created an imperative to build — quickly, at scale, and with fire-resistant materials.

When Sullivan arrived in Chicago in 1873, he found a city already transforming itself. The rebuilding demanded structural iron and steel framing, elevators, and fireproofing — technologies that would become the skeleton of the skyscraper. More importantly, the urgency of reconstruction meant that established architectural conventions carried less weight. As Sullivan later reflected, Chicago's builders were forced to think from first principles.

The young Sullivan, fresh from his training with Frank Furness in Philadelphia and his year at the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris, entered this milieu at precisely the right moment. He was twenty-two years old, technically trained but not yet committed to any doctrine, and the city around him was conducting the largest architectural experiment in American history.

## II. The Partnership Model

What distinguished Chicago's architectural culture from that of older eastern cities was the prevalence and productivity of the partnership model. The two most consequential firms of the period — Adler & Sullivan and Burnham & Root — were structured around complementary dyads.

### Adler & Sullivan

Dankmar Adler was the engineer and acoustician; Louis Sullivan was the designer and theorist. Their partnership, formalized on May 1, 1881, was built on an unusual division of labor: Adler handled the engineering, business, and client relationships, while Sullivan was given "a free hand" over design (013-028). This arrangement — rare in an era when senior partners typically controlled design absolutely — gave Sullivan the liberty to conduct his "practical experimentation" across a series of commercial commissions (013-034).

Sullivan acknowledged the significance of this freedom. Without Adler's engineering expertise and business acumen, Sullivan's theoretical ambitions would have remained just that — theoretical. The partnership gave him a laboratory.

### Burnham & Root

Daniel Burnham was the organizer, the planner, the man who could manage the largest construction project in American history (the World's Columbian Exposition). John Wellborn Root was the designer — "redheaded, large bullet-headed, close-cropped, effervescent, witty... a mind that sparkled" by Sullivan's description (015-003). Together they designed some of Chicago's most innovative early skyscrapers, including the Montauk Block (1882) and the Monadnock Building (1891).

Sullivan met Root in the early 1880s and was immediately drawn to his "magnetic personality" (015-003). The two became friends, exchanging ideas across firm boundaries in a pattern of collegiality that would have been unusual in more stratified eastern architectural circles.

### Beyond the Duos

The partnership culture extended beyond these two famous firms. William Le Baron Jenney, the engineer-architect who designed the Home Insurance Building (1885) — widely considered the first metal-frame skyscraper — trained a generation of Chicago architects who moved freely between offices. John Edelmann, Sullivan's friend and intellectual sparring partner, contributed his "theory of suppressed functions" to Sullivan's developing philosophy (013-025), even though Edelmann was not himself an architect of major buildings.

This permeability of ideas — architects debating theory in each other's offices, engineers sharing structural innovations across firm lines — created what we might now call an *innovation ecosystem*. Ideas moved faster than buildings.

## III. Sullivan's Experimental Method

Sullivan did not arrive in Chicago with a fully-formed architectural philosophy. The principle of "form follows function" emerged gradually through trial and error on real buildings:

> "He would put to the test a formula he had evolved, through long contemplation of living things, namely that form follows function, which would mean, in practice, that architecture might again become a living art, if this formula were but adhered to." [013-034]

The key phrase is "put to the test." Sullivan treated each commercial commission — the Borden Block, the Rothschild Building, the Auditorium — as an experiment. His immediate technical focus was maximizing daylight through "slender piers, tending toward a masonry and iron combination, the beginnings of a vertical system" (013-035). This method, he noted with satisfaction, "upset all precedent" (013-035).

The experimental approach required both the freedom Adler provided and the competitive stimulus of neighboring firms. Sullivan knew what Burnham & Root and Jenney were building. He could see their structural innovations and respond with his own. Chicago's compact downtown — rebuilt almost from scratch after 1871 — made such mutual observation inevitable.

## IV. The Columbian Exposition: Culmination and Rupture

The World's Columbian Exposition of 1893 ([[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893]]) marked both the peak of Chicago's collaborative culture and its breaking point. Burnham, as Director of Works, assembled a team that included the best architects in the country. The resulting "White City" demonstrated what large-scale architectural coordination could achieve.

But the Exposition also imported a Beaux-Arts classicism that Sullivan regarded as a betrayal of Chicago's indigenous modernism. His own Transportation Building — a brilliantly colored, structurally expressive anomaly among the white classical palaces — was his defiant dissent. The Exposition, in Sullivan's view, "set American architecture back fifty years" by reasserting the very historical revivalism that Chicago's experimental culture had begun to overcome.

The irony is that the Exposition was made possible by the same collaborative machinery — Burnham's organizational genius, Root's early planning (cut short by his death in 1891), the pool of Chicago-trained draftsmen and engineers — that the Chicago School had created. The tool was Chicago's; the product was not.

## V. What Made Chicago Different

To understand why the Chicago School emerged in Chicago rather than New York or Boston, consider the structural differences:

| Factor | Chicago | Eastern Cities |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Architectural tradition | None (tabula rasa after 1871) | Established, institutionalized |
| Land values | High but uniform (grid plan) | Stratified, speculative |
| Client culture | Commercial pragmatists | Cultural institutions, old money |
| Partnership model | Common (Adler & Sullivan, Burnham & Root) | Rare (sole practitioners dominated) |
| Technical imperative | Fireproofing + elevators = skyscraper | Incremental adaptation of existing types |

Chicago had nothing to preserve and everything to build. Its commercial clients — department store owners, hotel operators, office developers — cared about function and cost, not architectural pedigree. Its architects, many of them engineers by training, were unembarrassed by technical innovation. And its partnership structure meant that design talent and technical expertise could combine in projects neither could achieve alone.

## Conclusion

The Chicago School was an accident of history — a convergence of destruction, immigration, commerce, and talent that could not have been planned. But within that accident, certain patterns are visible: the catalytic role of the Fire, the multiplicative effect of complementary partnerships, and the experimental discipline Sullivan brought to the design of individual buildings. These were not the achievements of a lone genius but of a *system* — an unusually open, competitive, and collaborative architectural culture that burned brightly for roughly two decades before being extinguished, in Sullivan's view, by the very Columbian Exposition that was its grandest expression.

The Chicago School's legacy is not a style but a method: the conviction that architecture should arise from genuine technical and functional needs, tested against the discipline of real buildings, and refined through the friction of talented peers pushing each other forward. That method outlasted the School itself, transmitted through Sullivan's writings to Frank Lloyd Wright and through Wright to the modern movement — a collaborative gift from a collaborative culture.
