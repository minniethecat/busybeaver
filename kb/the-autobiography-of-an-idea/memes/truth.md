## Definition

Truth, in Sullivan's philosophy, is the alignment between inner reality and outward expression. It is the standard against which both architecture and human character are measured. Sullivan praised those who were "too natural to lie or to equivocate" [010-015], establishing truthfulness as a moral as well as an aesthetic virtue. He believed that "a wholly truthful story of any human life" carried profound significance [009-011], a conviction that shaped the autobiographical project itself.

## Truth Against Convention

Sullivan consistently positioned truth in opposition to social and architectural convention. A childhood memory captures this tension: a relative "proclaimed the unholy truth that this was not like Grandmother's bread" [005-006], illustrating how truth-telling often violated decorum. In architecture, Sullivan rejected the academic formulae of the École des Beaux-Arts in favor of direct engagement with materials and structural facts, believing that "abstract truth was a mirage" [012-012] — only embodied, lived truth mattered.

## Truth in Architecture

For Sullivan, architectural truth meant that a building should honestly express its structure, function, and materials. The famous dictum "form follows function" was fundamentally a truth claim: exterior form should not deceive about interior purpose. His mentor Moses Woolson was a "truthful man" [010-036] who modeled the integration of character and craft, demonstrating that professional integrity and personal honesty were inseparable.

## Truth, Beauty, and the Democratic Ideal

Sullivan linked truth to his democratic philosophy. If every person possessed innate creative power, then truthful expression was both a right and a responsibility. The architect who built truthfully served the democratic community; the architect who built deceitfully betrayed it. This moralized conception of truth elevated architecture from a technical profession to a social mission.
