# Paris

The French capital where Louis Sullivan studied at the Ecole Nationale des Beaux-Arts for approximately one year in 1874–1875, an experience that profoundly shaped his architectural education and sense of creative identity.

## Location

Paris, the capital of France, situated on the River Seine in northern France. Sullivan lived on "the seventh floor of a rooming hotel, at the southeast corner Rue Monsieur le Prince and Rue Racine, in the Latin Quarter" — near the Boulevard Saint-Michel, the Odeon, the Luxembourg Palace and its gardens (012-002).

## In Sullivan's Life

### First Encounter

Sullivan arrived in Paris after nightfall, having traveled from "faraway Chicago" by steamship and rail (012-004). His first view of the city came from the heights of his seventh-floor perch: "From this lofty perch which he always reached on the run, two steps at a time, the City of Paris spread before him to the north" (012-002).

He had come with a specific purpose: to pass the entrance examinations for the Ecole Nationale des Beaux-Arts, and "specifically an ancien" — a former student of the school (010-019). An encounter with a "mighty man" who told "his tale of his life in Paris with Lefuel" had inspired Sullivan's ambition (010-027).

### Significance

Paris was the proving ground where Sullivan tested himself against the highest standards of European architectural training. After intensive preparation at the atelier of Monsieur Clopet, he passed the rigorous entrance examinations, which opened his eyes to "the immense difference in matter and manner, especially as applied to mathematics and history" between the French educational approach and the American one (012-030).

Paris offered Sullivan not just technical training but a model of urban civilization. He found "the city well ordered and cleanly, with architectural monuments everywhere" (012-018). In the Luxembourg Gardens he watched children at play "like flowers and the nurses stately flowers," and the "atmosphere was Paris; Paris was to be his home" (012-018).

Yet Paris also taught Sullivan the value of his own American identity. He came to see the contrast between "raw Chicago and finished Paris" not as a deficiency but as a dialectic (012-017). When his year of study concluded, he chose to return to Chicago rather than remain in Europe — a decision that defined his career. "He had come to Paris from faraway Chicago with that sole end in view; so he was glad" (012-004).

### Sullivan's Description

> "Wherever he went he found the city well ordered and cleanly, with architectural monuments everywhere... Paris seemed made for him. All was really new to him, but did not seem strange or alien. It was as though he had always lived there." (012-017) (012-018)

## Architectural Context

The Ecole des Beaux-Arts in the 1870s was the most prestigious architectural school in the world, teaching a rigorous classical curriculum grounded in the study of antiquity and the Renaissance. Baron Haussmann's recent transformation of Paris had created the modern boulevard system and unified urban fabric that Sullivan admired. The atelier system — where students worked under a practicing master architect — provided Sullivan with a model of practical mentorship that contrasted with American architectural training.
