## Overview

Lloyd's of London, the great maritime insurance market, appears in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s memoir
in a single, vivid image. Running for shelter into Bridport harbour — a basin "about
the size of a large drawing-room" — [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] finds that even this tiny port is equipped
with the full apparatus of British maritime commerce:

> It was near high water, so we had no fears for getting in, and we ran through
> the long, narrow bottleneck of an entrance, more like a canal than a harbour,
> to find ourselves in a basin about the size of a large drawing-room, but with
> everything complete — a Lloyd's office, a harbour master, and a signal station,
> a hotel, and all on the scale of the place. (024-003)

## Belloc's View

The Lloyd's office at Bridport represents, for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the thoroughness of British
maritime organisation. Every harbour, however small, was wired into the commercial
network that Lloyd's represented. The juxtaposition — a drawing-room-sized basin
with a Lloyd's office — is characteristic [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] humour: the grandeur of British
imperial commerce reduced to miniature, but still functioning.
