# Selsea Bill

## Overview

Selsea Bill (modern Selsey Bill) is a low headland on the West [[sussex|Sussex coast]] of [[england|England]], marking the western side of the entrance to the [[looe-stream]] — the inshore passage that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] calls "the gate into the [[sussex|Sussex]] [[the-sea|seas]]" [033-001]. It lies between the [[owers]] reefs to the south and [[chichester-harbour]] to the north.

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Selsea Bill was one of the enduring landmarks of his home waters. He had known it since boyhood, and after nearly forty [[time|years]] of sailing [[english-channel|the Channel]], it remained unchanged — a rare constancy in a world he saw transforming around him.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] describes the [[looe-stream]] as running "like a river in the midst of the [[the-sea|sea]] between the long-concealed flats and little rocks just under water at low tide of Selsea Bill and the reefs of the Owers outside" [033-002]. Navigating this passage required careful timing with the tide — "you had best take the Looe Stream on the flood" [033-002] — and [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] recounts making his way against the ebb until he had "Selsea Bill close before us" [033-002] before catching the flood through.

Later in the book, anchored under Folkestone Breakwater, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reflects on the familiar coast he has sailed for a lifetime:

> How well does a man get to know a bit of coast like this between Selsea and the Downs after half a life of lazy passages along it! It becomes as familiar as a street. [039-003]

Listing the unchanged landmarks, he includes "the low shelf of Selsea Bill, and the mark on the Mixen, and the two buoys of the Looe Stream" [039-008] — [[the-sea|sea]]-marks that had greeted him as a boy and still stood when he w[[corruption|rot]]e as an older man.
