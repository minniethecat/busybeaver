# Skomer

## Overview

Skomer ([[wales|Welsh]]: *Ynys Sgomer*) is a small island lying off the southwest coast of Pembrokeshire, [[wales|Wales]], just south of the Lleyn Peninsula. It stands at the northern entrance to Jack Sound, the narrow and notoriously difficult passage between the island and the mainland. To the sailor navigating the southern corner of [[wales|Wales]], Skomer is a landmark that must be reckoned with — a gatekeeper to the waters that lead toward [[milford-haven|Milford Haven]] and [[bristol-channel|the Bristol Channel]] beyond.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] notes that the name "Skomer" is Scandinavian in origin, like "[[bardsey-sound|Bardsey]]" and "sound" itself — a relic of the Norse pirates who sailed these waters in the eighth and ninth centuries [001-036]. The puzzle, as [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] observes, is how such names survived among a Celtic-speaking population that far outnumbered their brief Scandinavian visitors.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s encounter with Skomer came after the *[[nona|Nona]]* had passed through [[ramsey-sound|Ramsey]] Sound and entered the calm of Saint Bride's Bay. A more difficult trial awaited him: Jack Sound, "between Skomer and the mainland, seven miles to the south" [011-016].

But the wind had other plans. It fell dead calm, and the tide took charge. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s description of the ensuing drift is one of the most whimsical passages in the book:

> "The sea took charge and trolled the little boat along, keeping her just so far from the shore, as who should say, 'See, my child, how remarkable is the Island of Skomer! Come with me, I will show you every cranny of its outer shore.'" [011-024]

The *[[nona|Nona]]* drifted aimlessly, her tiller useless, her canvas hanging slack. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] found himself on what he called "a jolly little voyage of exploration" — an involuntary circumnavigation of Skomer that cost nothing and taught him "another few miles of shore" [011-026].

During this drift, the *[[nona|Nona]]* came close aboard a large ketch anchored off the Skomer shore. The solitary man on deck, smoking a pipe, was bound for Cork with a cargo of coal [011-025]. The encounter, brief and inconsequential, captures the companionship of the [[the-sea|sea]] — strangers exchanging a few words in the vast solitude of the [[wales|Welsh coast]].

By evening, the tide had returned the *[[nona|Nona]]* to where she started, at the mouth of Jack Sound. The wind rose again, and [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] pointed through the Sound toward [[milford-haven|Milford Haven]] as the late June darkness gathered.

## Geography & History

Skomer is now a national nature reserve, famous for its [[the-sea|sea]]bird colonies — puffins, Manx shearwaters, and razorbills. In [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s day, it was a quieter place, known mainly to fishermen and the few sailors who ventured into these difficult waters. The island's Scandinavian name is a reminder of a time when the Norse [[the-sea|sea]]-rovers left their linguistic mark on the western coasts of [[england|Britain]], a puzzle that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] himself reflected upon as he looked out over these waters.
