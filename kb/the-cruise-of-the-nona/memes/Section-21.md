[021-001]We came to Berry Head and opened Torbay.

[021-002]It is with Torbay as with the [[fowey|Fowey]] coast. I have known it only under such weathers as leave a hint of heaven: never have I opened Torbay in passing Berry Head but it was morning, with the [[youth|young]] [[the-sea|sea]] delighting in a leading breeze; and once, a draught to last forever, I came up under such a dawn and with so tender a [[death|dying]] crescent in the sky that I spent an hour in Paradise.

[021-003]What are those days of glory? They are not [[memory|memories]]: are they premonitions, or, are they visions?

[021-004]They are not memories, though perhaps Plato thought them so, and our modern pantheists, with [[william-wordsworth|Wordsworth]] for their spokesman here, called and believed them so.

[021-005]I will hope that they are premonitions, hints granted beforehand of a state to be attained. At the worst they are visions of such a state lying all about us, the home of the Blessed, which we are permitted to glimpse at for a moment, even those of us sad ones who may never reach that place.

[021-006]As for those who call such joy illusion, they are of that great *Schola Stultorum*, that new herd of fools peculiar to our day; they who have made the discovery that Something comes out of Nothing, who pour quarts out of pint-pots and give emptiness creative power.

[021-007]Neglect them, but ask yourself, as I do when such rare moods are granted, whether they be visions or premonitions, and hope that premonitions they may be. For certainly they are the supports of this life, and we creep from one to another like travellers from inn to inn, and when they fail us, the world will be no more endurable.

[021-008]Such thoughts I had—and have had repeatedly—in this run of water under a morning sky. It is inhabited, is all that air and sea, by something beneficent—at least, I have always found it so.
