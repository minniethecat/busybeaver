# Dreyfus Affair

## Overview

The Dreyfus Affair was a political scandal that convulsed France from 1894 to 1906, centred on the wrongful conviction of Captain Alfred [[alfred-dreyfus|Dreyfus]] for treason. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] treats the case in Section 28 not as a partisan but as a student of historical method, using it to illustrate the difficulty of establishing truth when human motives and contradictory testimony are involved.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] groups [[alfred-dreyfus|the Dreyfus case]] with the Affair of the Diamond Necklace and the Tichborne case as examples where "the difficulty of finding the solution lies in the discovery of motive, and in the estimate of truth proceeding from a group of witnesses" (028-032). In all three, he notes, "there is an opposing set of accounts, and of depositions contradictory the one to the other" (028-032).

## Belloc's Account

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes with the caution of one who lived through the controversy and suffered for his views. He reports that during his boyhood, "men eminent in the legal profession, men of the Tichbornes' social standing and religion, was as violently divided as were later men of the same capacity for judgment in the matter of [[alfred-dreyfus|the Dreyfus affair]]" (028-035).

> The Dreyfus case, after all these years, still rouses such passions that I tremble even to mention it. I have myself (so many years after!) been subject to mournful rebuke in public from the excellent pen of Dean Inge, who has reproached me in well-knit prose for being the only man in England who did not take the side of the accused. (028-038)

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] immediately rebuts this charge, citing "half a dozen prominent Englishmen who had the right to judge through a knowledge of European affairs, and who remained in doubt of [[alfred-dreyfus|Dreyfus]]' innocence, notably [[lord-russell-of-killowen|Lord Russell of Killowen]], the Lord Chief [[justice|Justice]], and Mr. Labouchère — and there were many others" (028-038).

Of his personal acquaintance, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] records that "most were for the innocence of [[alfred-dreyfus|Dreyfus]]: but the rest, fully competent also, were, and are, convinced of his guilt" (028-041).

## Historical Context

The Dreyfus Affair began in 1894 when Captain Alfred [[alfred-dreyfus|Dreyfus]], a Jewish artillery officer, was convicted of passing military secrets to Germany. The case split [[france|French]] society between [[alfred-dreyfus|Dreyfus]]ards (who believed in his innocence) and anti-[[alfred-dreyfus|Dreyfus]]ards. It exposed deep fissures in the [[france|French]] Third Republic — between army and republic, Church and state, and over the place of Jews in [[france|French]] society. [[alfred-dreyfus|Dreyfus]] was eventually exonerated in 1906.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] w[[corruption|rot]]e *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* in 1925, two decades after the Affair's resolution, yet his tone makes clear that the wounds were still raw. His own stance — doubting the certainty of [[alfred-dreyfus|Dreyfus]]'s innocence — placed him in a minority among English intellectuals of his generation.

## Significance

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of the Dreyfus Affair is remarkable for the historical claim with which he concludes. He argues that the case had a direct causal chain leading to the [[great-war|Great War]]:

> It is to the Dreyfus case that we owe the four years of war, 1914–18: for it destroyed the French Intelligence Bureau and so permitted the German surprise on Mons and [[battle-of-charleroi|Charleroi]]. (028-044)

This is a striking thesis: that a domestic political scandal crippled France's military intelligence apparatus, enabling the German advance through Belgium in August 1914. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] presents it not as speculation but as a "point of capital importance" (028-043) for the future historian.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s broader point is methodological. He predicts that "when the passions have died down, [[alfred-dreyfus|the Dreyfus case]] will remain for history very much what the Diamond Necklace has remained" (028-043): a popular legend "intellectually worth nothing" alongside a historian's task of "criticising that legend, but hardly of solving the problem" (028-043).
