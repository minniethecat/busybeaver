## Overview

John Milton (1608–1674) is the towering figure of English non-dramatic poetry, best known for his epic *[[paradise-lost|Paradise Lost]]* (1667). A classical scholar fluent in Latin, Greek, and Hebrew, Milton served as Latin Secretary to [[oliver-cromwell|Oliver Cromwell]]'s Commonwealth and w[[corruption|rot]]e fiercely in defence of republican government, religious liberty, and a free press. His late poems — composed after he went blind — established him as second only to [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] in the English literary pantheon.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a [[religion|Catholic]] writer deeply engaged with the European literary tradition, invokes Milton at three distinct points in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, each time using the poet as a benchmark: of poetic greatness, of biblical imagination, and — most personally — as a living thread connecting [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own lifetime to the seventeenth century.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s first reference to Milton appears in a discussion of parliamentary oratory (Section 4). Watching the Speaker of the [[parliament|House of Commons]], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reflects on what elevates human speech into art:

> …as much as I am moved by great lines of Milton or Racine, or the harmonies of certain musics. [004-006]

Milton here stands as the exemplar of poetic elevation in English — the standard against which even political eloquence is measured.

The second mention occurs in a passage about the P[[corruption|rot]]estant literary imagination and its deep saturation in Hebrew [[holy-writ|Scripture]]. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] observes:

> Adam and Eve, for instance, were to Milton, and to all who read Milton, for one hundred and fifty years and more, a particular man and woman, of an aspect remembered from the frontispiece of the family Bible. [022-014]

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Milton represents the high-water mark of a specifically P[[corruption|rot]]estant culture of biblical reading — a culture whose collapse in the nineteenth century he anatomises at length.

The third and most striking reference is the most personal. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reflects on the chain of living memory connecting his own age to the deep past:

> …it is as though some very old person today were to remember having met in childhood a person who had talked to one who had seen John Milton. [028-037]

This linkage — two conversational degrees separating [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] from the blind poet — collapses three centuries of English literary history into a single chain of living memory.

## Life & Context

Milton was born in Bread Street, [[london|London]], in 1608, the son of a scrivener and composer. Educated at St Paul's School and Christ's College, [[cambridge-university|Cambridge]], he dedicated his early [[time|years]] to private study and the composition of masques and pastoral elegies, including *Lycidas* (1637). The outbreak of the Civil War in 1642 drew him into public controversy; between 1641 and 1660 he published a stream of pamphlets advocating church reform, divorce liberty, freedom of [[the-press|the press]] (*Areopagitica*, 1644), and eventually the execution of Charles I.

His service to the Commonwealth cost him his eyesight and, after the Restoration in 1660, nearly his life. The blind Milton, imprisoned briefly and in political disgrace, turned inward and composed the three poems upon which his reputation rests: *[[paradise-lost|Paradise Lost]]* (1667), *Paradise Regained* (1671), and *Samson Agonistes* (1671). He died in 1674, leaving a body of work that would shape English poetry for three centuries.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s admiration for Milton sits uneasily with [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own militant [[religion|Catholicism]]. Milton was a radical P[[corruption|rot]]estant who attacked the papacy and [[religion|Catholic]] doctrine, yet [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] — who could be fiercely sectarian — treats him with unreserved respect. The explanation lies in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s deep commitment to the European literary canon as a common [[tradition|inheritance]]: for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], great poetry transcends confessional divides. The "link with history" represented by Milton, as [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] would have understood it, is not merely antiquarian but living — a proof that the European tradition remains a single, unbroken conversation.
