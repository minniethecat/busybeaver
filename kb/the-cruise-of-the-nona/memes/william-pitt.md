## Overview

William Pitt the Younger (1759–1806) was Prime Minister of Great [[england|Britain]] at twenty-four — the [[youth|young]]est holder of that office in British history — and served for nearly nineteen of his forty-six [[time|years]]. He led the nation through the [[france|French]] Revolutionary Wars, reorganised the national finances after the [[american-war-of-independence|American War of Independence]], and engineered the Act of Union with [[ireland|Ireland]] in 1800.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] invokes Pitt at a single concentrated point in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* — three times within the span of a single section — as the end-marker of an era of systematic [[corruption|political corruption]]. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Pitt is a historical pivot: the last great figure of the old regime of patronage, and the man under whom its logic reached its final expression.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s three references to Pitt appear in Section 39, within a sustained analysis of [[corruption|political corruption]] in eighteenth-century [[england|England]]. He opens the historical span with a sweeping judgment:

> From Walpole to the younger Pitt, from the South Sea Bubble to the Act of Union, public life was conducted universally upon a system of bribes. [039-032]

Pitt stands at the far end of this arc — the terminus of an era stretching from the first Prime Minister to the Act of Union with [[ireland|Ireland]], in which political allegiance was openly purchased.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] then refines his argument, insisting that the corruption of that age differed fundamentally from the commercialised venality of his own time. The eighteenth-century aristocrat was not a mercenary:

> You cannot imagine Walpole, for instance, or the younger Pitt, at either end of the period, taking money from a chance financial adventurer who should have come to him to get a new duty put on, or an old duty taken off. [039-036]

The third and most vivid reference is a thought-experiment that makes the same point by inversion:

> I cannot imagine a man going to the younger Pitt, and saying: "My brother who is your colleague in the Government and I have a project for fitting sailing-ships with such-and-such a device. If you will give us a contract…" [039-037]

The point is that the corruption of Pitt's era was structural and aristocratic — a system of patronage among gentlemen — not the transactional, commercial corruption of the modern democratic age. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], this distinction is crucial to [[knowledge|understanding]] what was lost when the old order gave way to the new.

## Life & Context

William Pitt was born in 1759, the second son of William Pitt the Elder, Earl of Chatham — the great war leader of the Seven Years' War. Educated at home by his father and at Pembroke College, [[cambridge-university|Cambridge]], Pitt entered [[parliament|Parliament]] at twenty-one and was appointed Prime Minister by George III at twenty-four.

His premiership was dominated by the struggle against Revolutionary France. Pitt built the coalitions that would eventually defeat Napoleon, though he did not live to see the final victory. The strain of office, compounded by heavy drinking, killed him in 1806. His epitaph, "the pilot that weathered the storm," captures the judgment of his contemporaries.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s interest in Pitt is diagnostic, not biographical. Pitt serves as a historical boundary-stone marking the end of the eighteenth-century political settlement — a world of aristocratic patronage that, for all its corruption, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] regards as having been more honest in its own terms than the commercialised politics that succeeded it. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], who served briefly as a Liberal MP (1906–1910), had direct experience of the modern political world he criticises, and his analysis of Pitt's era is implicitly a critique of his own.
