## Overview

"Holy Writ" is an archaic English term for the Bible, particularly common in [[religion|Catholic]] and High Anglican discourse. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] uses it in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* in a characteristically ironic passage that sets biblical counsel against the practical realities of [[the-sea|sea]]manship and human nature.

## Belloc's Reference

The reference appears in the context of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s extended meditation on fools, triggered by his experience with an incompetent [[wales|Welsh]] pilot who grounded the *[[nona|Nona]]* on the sands of Port Madoc:

> "I used to think that the irritation against fools was irrational and purposeless. Where it is written in Holy Writ that one should tolerate fools even with gladness, I thought that this was a general rule of conduct. But now I know it to be a counsel of perfection and, indeed, like so many things in the Old Testament, a counsel generally to be avoided." [006-002]

The passage is a perfect example of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s rhetorical method: start with a biblical text, appear to submit to it, then pivot to a worldly qualification that is both funnier and (he implies) truer than the original counsel. The irritation against fools, he concludes, is not a moral failing but "an instinct implanted in us ... a natural faculty of conservation" — and [[england|England]], which tolerates fools too much, suffers "many little disasters" as a result.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s wry treatment of Holy Writ here is not irreverent but characteristic of his [[religion|Catholic]] sensibility. He treats the Bible not as a fragile text to be handled with pieties but as a living document whose counsels can be weighed, qualified, and sometimes — in their extreme form — set aside by a man of experience. The distinction between a "counsel of perfection" and a general rule of conduct is itself a [[religion|Catholic]] one, rooted in the distinction between precepts and evangelical counsels.
