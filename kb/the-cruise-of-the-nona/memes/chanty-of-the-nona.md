# Chanty of the *Nona*

## Overview

The "Chanty of the *Nona*" is [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own contribution to the book's maritime atmosphere — a [[the-sea|sea]]-song composed during the voyage itself. He is cheerfully self-deprecating about its literary merit: "if any man say that it is a vile piece of verse, I am ready to agree with him, but if another man say that it heartens him, and fills him with the [[the-sea|sea]], why, I am ready to agree with him also" (012-006). It is one of the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s most endearing moments — [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] the poet declining to take himself seriously.

## Belloc's Account

The Chanty's composition spans three sections of the book. The tune and first verse were completed during the second windless day in Fishguard harbour (005-011), when [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] and his companion, frustrated by the calm, "took council together and determined that with the very first breath of air we could find we would run back to anchor and wait for a wind worth having" (005-010). The creative act fills the empty time.

The second and third verses were designed that night as [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] fell asleep (011-042), the rhythm of the verse perhaps influenced by the gentle motion of the boat at anchor. And the poem was finished "swaying in the cabin" at [[bideford-river|Bideford]] River (012-006), at the end of the voyage's first leg.

The distribution of the Chanty's composition across the narrative — beginning, middle, end — mirrors the book's structure. The poem is the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s internal echo, the song that accompanies the voyage.

## Belloc's Artistic Self-Deprecation

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s willingness to dismiss his own poem as "a vile piece of verse" is characteristic. He was, in [[truth|fact]], a considerable poet — his *Cautionary Tales for Children* (1907) and *The Bad Child's Book of Beasts* (1896) are classics of [[lighthouse|light]] verse, and his serious poetry has genuine merit. The self-deprecation is both sincere (the Chanty may well be a s[[lighthouse|light]] thing) and strategic — it disarms the critic while leaving the emotional effect intact.
