# Justice

## Overview

Justice for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is not primarily a legal concept — it is a moral and social virtue, the quality that distinguishes a healthy polity from a corrupt one. He invokes it most often not to praise but to mark its absence. "The aristocracy among men indifferent to honour and justice is so clear that I never pause to wonder at it" (010-018) — the sentence is characteristic: justice is named only to be declared missing.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of justice is inseparable from his critique of [[parliament|Parliament]]. The modern political class has abandoned the pursuit of justice in favour of the management of interests, and the result is a system in which "the doctrine of justice is [[fog|cloud]]ily remembered, but more as an inconvenience than a guide" (016-001).

## Belloc's Argument

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] makes his case through accumulation rather than systematic argument. He notes that even in a corrupt age, "the popular conception of justice" (015-039) retains a certain force — ordinary people still know what justice is and can recognise its violation. This residual moral sense is what makes [[corruption|political corruption]] possible: the corrupt know they are corrupt, which is why they must disguise their actions.

The most striking single passage concerns a judge: "He had charity, humility, and justice in equal poise" (017-002). This is [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s ideal of the just man — justice is not a cold abstraction but a personal quality, balanced with other virtues and expressed in conduct rather than doctrine.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] extends the critique to intellectual life. He warns against the use of [[knowledge|learning]] "to bolster up injustice or false doctrine" (022-021), recognising that the intellect can be as corrupt as the will. Justice is not merely a matter of right action but of right thinking — a point that connects his political and religious concerns.

## Key Passages

> The aristocracy among men indifferent to honour and justice is so clear that I never pause to wonder at it. (010-018)

Justice paired with honour as the twin foundations the powerful have abandoned.

> The doctrine of justice is cloudily remembered, but more as an inconvenience than a guide. (016-001)

The [[corruption|decay]] of justice as a living concept — remembered but no longer operative.

> He had charity, humility, and justice in equal poise. (017-002)

The just man as ideal type — justice integrated with the other virtues.

> Out of rottenness comes not strength; nor justice, nor truth, nor generosity out of corruption. (007-025)

Justice as incompatible with corruption — a moral absolute [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] insists upon.
