# The *Lily*

## Overview

The *Lily* is a [[london|London]] barge — one of the flat-bottomed working
vessels that carried cargo up and down the [[thames|Thames]] and its estuary.
She appears in the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s later pages as part of the working maritime
landscape, a reminder that the [[the-sea|sea]] [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sails is not just a
philosophical arena but a place where people earn their living.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s mention of the *Lily* is characteristically brief but laden with
implied respect. A cargo has been "salvaged" (041-021) by the barge — the
quotation marks around the word suggesting [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s wry awareness of the legal
and commercial complexities of salvage claims. In maritime law, salvage is a
formal right: the salvor who saves a vessel or cargo in [[pain|distress]] is entitled to
a reward proportionate to the value saved. The quotation marks may hint that
the *Lily*'s claim was contested, or merely that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] found the legal
formality amusing. The *Lily* is not romanticised; she is simply there, doing
her work, as barges have done for centuries.

## The Thames Sailing Barge

[[london|London]] barges (or [[thames|Thames]] [[london-barge|sailing barge]]s) were the lorries
of their age — flat-bottomed, sprit-rigged vessels that could navigate the
shallow [[tide|tidal]] waters of the [[thames|Thames]] estuary, the Medway, and the
coastal routes of [[kent|Kent]] and [[essex|Essex]]. The typical [[london-barge|Thames barge]]
was around 80–90 feet in length, with a beam of 20 feet, and drew as little
as three feet of water when unladen. This shallow draft was their genius: they
could lie aground on mudflats at low tide to load or discharge cargo without
the need for docks or wharves.

## Working Life

[[london-barge|Thames barge]]s were worked by small crews — often a skipper and a mate, sometimes
a boy as well — who lived aboard in cramped quarters beneath the stern. They
carried every conceivable cargo: bricks from the [[kent|Kentish]] brickfields,
timber from the Surrey docks, coal from the north-east colliers, grain from
the East Anglian ports, and manu[[truth|fact]]ured goods from [[london|London]]'s
warehouses. By [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s time the trade was in gentle decline, under pressure
from railways and steam-powered coasters, but hundreds of barges still worked
the estuary. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], sailing his small [[pilot-cutter|cutter]] *[[nona|Nona]]* through these same
waters, would have passed them routinely — workaday vessels whose presence marked
the continuity of an older maritime order.

## Contrast with the *Nona*

The *Lily* and the *[[nona|Nona]]* represent two different relationships with
the [[the-sea|sea]]. The *[[nona|Nona]]* is a leisure vessel, a vehicle for contemplation
and philosophical wandering. The *Lily* is a tool of [[trade|commerce]], her
every voyage determined by the economics of cargo and freight. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s
awareness of this contrast — and his respect for the working vessel — reflects
his broader sympathy for the dignity of labour and his distributist conviction
that the small independent operator, whether a barge skipper or a peasant farmer,
stood closer to a properly ordered society than any industrial combine.
