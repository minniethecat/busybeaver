## Overview

*The Spectator* is a British weekly magazine founded in 1828, covering politics, culture, and current affairs. By [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s time it was well established as a voice of educated opinion — the kind of periodical a donnish controversialist might write to when he wished to expose a rival's folly.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] mentions *The Spectator* twice in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, both times in contexts that illustrate his favourite theme: the absurdity of academic pretension.

The first reference appears in Section 11, during [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s extended attack on the scholarly habit of denying traditional authorship. He recounts a particularly egregious case of academic overreach — an [[oxford-university|Oxford]] don who claimed to have discovered concealed anagrams in classical texts proving, among other things, "that Euripides, when he was a little boy, w[[corruption|rot]]e the plays of Aeschylus." The hoaxer was exposed:

> "A stopper was put on him, however, by a man who wrote to the *Spectator* (of all papers), proving that the said don's name was but thinly concealed in an anagram of the opening lines of the *Iliad*, so that he must have written that excellent poem, not when he was a little boy, but long before he was born." (011-059)

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s parenthetical "(of all papers)" conveys amused surprise — it is the sort of hoax-exposure one might expect in a comic journal, not in the sober columns of *The Spectator*.

The second reference comes in Section 42, during [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s sustained assault on the false authority of academics. He is cataloguing absurdities solemnly pronounced by professors:

> "So I do when they tell me, as one professor solemnly told the world in the pages of, I think, the *Spectator* (but possibly it was another learned organ), that the French for 'very beautiful' was '*beaucoup belle*,' or that the phrase '*La volonté générale est toujours droite*,' means 'The general will is always right.'" (042-012)

Here *The Spectator* functions as a platform for learned nonsense — the professor uses its pages to broadcast an error that any schoolboy with elementary [[france|French]] could correct. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s studied [[doubt|uncertainty]] about which journal it was ("I think, the *Spectator* — but possibly it was another learned organ") implies that the organs of educated opinion are interchangeable in their capacity to disseminate foolishness.

## Connection to Belloc

Both references to *The Spectator* serve [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s larger argument about the unreliability of institutional authority. The periodical press, in his view, participates in the same machinery of credentialled error as the universities — it gives a platform to people whose titles and positions command deference, but whose claims dissolve under the scrutiny of common sense. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s tone is not angry but amused; he treats these absurdities as entertainment rather than outrage.
