# London Dock Strike

## Overview

The London Dock Strike of 1889 was a landmark in British labour history, in which tens of thousands of dock workers walked out demanding the "dockers' tanner" — sixpence an hour. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] witnessed the strike as a [[youth|young]] man of nineteen, and his [[memory|recollection]] of it in Section 31 is less a chronicle of the strike itself than a meditation on the fate of late-Victorian idealism.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] recalls "the great mobs that followed [[john-burns|John Burns]], and how I myself would go miles through the East End to hear him" (031-010). He remembers "that great whirlpool of men in Trafalgar Square on the most critical day when he and others accepted imprisonment" (031-010). The dominant note is not nostalgia but elegy.

## Belloc's Account

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sets the scene with vivid detail — a [[youth|young]] heir to a [[wealth|fortune]] standing "under the flaring naphtha lamps of a muddy [[london|London]] evening, calling out the new gospel and the promised land" (031-010), and the "eager, stupid, upturned faces of the men and women who had come there from bestial depths of the slums to hear him" (031-010).

His verdict on the idealism of 1889 is harsh: "Of all that enthusiasm and conviction nothing came; the mill turned noisily enough, but it ground no corn" (031-011). The humanitarian fervour of the dock strike era was, in his view, soon replaced by "a contemptible shrieking and raving called 'imperialism,' and that also went its way, and worked itself out to its ignoble end" (031-011).

> Of those years, one thing remains: the dock strike made Southampton. (031-012)

This is the pivot of the passage. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the one enduring physical consequence of the great dock strike was not the uplift of the [[london|London]] poor but the transformation of [[southampton|Southampton]] into a major transatlantic port.

> I see the monstrous ships of the Atlantic passage pass up and down the dredged channel to Southampton Water, and, as I look at them earning their millions for the few masters of such machines, I say to myself: "This was the fruit — here is the only fruit — of those nightly enthusiasms by the London Docks, and of the cry for freedom, and of the passionate belief that within a few years after 1889 the poverty of London would have passed into an evil memory." (031-012)

## Historical Context

The London Dock Strike of 1889 lasted five weeks and involved an estimated 100,000 workers. Led by Ben Tillett, Tom Mann, and John Burns, it won significant concessions — including the sixpence hourly rate — and inspired the formation of new trade unions among unskilled workers. A [[youth|young]] [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], freshly arrived in [[london|London]], was among the crowds who gathered to hear the strike leaders speak at mass meetings across the East End.

The strike's success led directly to the expansion of dock facilities at [[southampton|Southampton]], which could accommodate the new generation of deep-draught [[the-sea|ocean]] liners that the shallower [[london|London]] docks could not. In this narrow material sense, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s claim — "the dock strike made [[southampton|Southampton]]" — has a basis in [[truth|fact]].

## Significance

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of the dock strike embodies a recurring theme in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*: the gulf between the ideals that move men and the material consequences that survive them. The dock strike was fought for the dignity of the [[london|London]] poor, but its enduring physical legacy was a port that enriched "the few masters of such machines" (031-012). [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] contrasts "that time of my youth" — when "no one now has a creed" (031-010) — with the present, declaring "therefore, I call that time of my youth a better time" (031-010). The dock strike passage is, in this sense, as much about [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own loss of political [[religion|faith]] as about the strike itself.
