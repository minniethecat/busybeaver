## Overview

William Wordsworth (1770–1850) was the central figure of English Romanticism and Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death. Together with Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he launched the Romantic revolution in English poetry with *Lyrical Ballads* (1798). His greatest work, *The Prelude* (published posthumously in 1850), is a verse autobiography of unprecedented psychological depth.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] engages with Wordsworth not primarily as a poet but as a thinker — specifically as the English voice of pantheism, the belief that the divine infuses the natural world. In three separate passages of *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] uses Wordsworth to trace the arc and the limits of that modern creed.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s first mention of Wordsworth is perhaps the most intimate. Reflecting on the formative [[time|years]] of consciousness and the compatibility of development with innate gift, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes:

> That doctrine no more clashes with any proved historical, or probable prehistorical, process than the discovery that William Wordsworth grew up from childhood to manhood proves that he was never a poet, or that he never ceased to be a poet. [015-038]

The second reference is doctrinal. Discussing the difference between the [[religion|Catholic]] [[knowledge|understanding]] of the Fall and the modern rejection of it, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] names Wordsworth as the spokesman of pantheism:

> They are not memories, though perhaps Plato thought them so, and our modern pantheists, with Wordsworth for their spokesman here, called and believed them so. [021-004]

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the [[religion|Catholic]], this is a philosophical error — but one he treats with respect rather than dismissal, acknowledging the experiential truth from which the pantheist argument draws its force.

The third passage offers [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most pointed judgment. Analysing the decline of religious belief in nineteenth-century [[england|England]]:

> The reason, for instance, that the poet Wordsworth attained so ill-proportioned a popularity in the latter part of the nineteenth century in England was his pantheism. [035-028]

The word "ill-proportioned" is carefully chosen: [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not deny Wordsworth's poetic gifts but argues that his fame owed more to theological fashion than to literary merit alone.

## Life & Context

Born in Cockermouth, Cumberland, in 1770, Wordsworth grew up in the Lake District, a landscape that would furnish the imagery of his entire poetic career. Orphaned early, he was educated at Hawkshead and [[cambridge-university|Cambridge]]. A walking tour through revolutionary France in 1790 left him an ardent republican, though the Terror later tempered his radicalism.

His collaboration with Coleridge produced *Lyrical Ballads* (1798), whose famous preface declared poetry to be "the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings" arising from "emotion recollected in tranquillity." The later Wordsworth — settled at Rydal Mount, honoured, conservative — was a very different figure from the [[youth|young]] radical, and his long decline into Victorian respectability became a cautionary tale for later poets.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of Wordsworth reveals his characteristic method of literary criticism: to judge a writer not by aesthetic criteria alone but by the intellectual and religious consequences of their work. Wordsworth's pantheism, for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], was a half-step away from [[religion|Christianity]] — a reverence for nature that recognised the divine but refused to name it. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], who loved the [[the-sea|sea]] and the English coast with a passion equal to any Romantic's, understood the temptation but rejected the theology. His critique of Wordsworth is thus a kind of sympathetic dissent, spoken from inside the experience Wordsworth described.
