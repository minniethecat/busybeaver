Stephen Reynolds (1881–1919) was an English writer and social reformer whose close association with the fishing communities of the [[devon|Devon]] and [[cornwall|Cornish coast]]s made him a natural companion to [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]. He is known particularly for his book *A Poor Man's House* (1908), a vivid account of life in a Sidmouth fishing family, and for his tireless advocacy on behalf of working fishermen.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] introduces Reynolds with gravity and warmth. Returning from a Cornish town, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] had to make his way back to [[london|London]], and Reynolds undertook to bring the *[[nona|Nona]]* round the dangerous passage of Land's End:

> From that Cornish town I had the next morning to make my way back to London; and Stephen Reynolds, whom I met, got her round the land safely to the ports upon the southern side, whence later I resumed this cruise: Stephen Reynolds, that strongest-souled and most sincere of men, who desired and did good all his life [017-001].

The passage then deepens into a meditation on the world's neglect of its best men — a theme [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] returns to throughout the *[[nona|Nona]]*:

> It is the meeting with such men, and the comparison of their public label with their true function, of their false renown or lack of renown with their certain standing in the eyes of their Maker, which lead all wise men to a perfect contempt for the modern world [017-001].

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] asks whether anyone remembers Reynolds, and answers his own question with characteristic bleakness:

> Does anyone remember him now of those who are reading this? Perhaps one or two, perhaps no one. He loved the poor: he understood the sea. He was a brother and a support to sailing-men, and he had charity, humility, and justice in equal poise [017-002].

The passage concludes with a verdict on modernity itself: "our world is no longer fitted for governance by, nor even for advice from, its rare great men. It is fitted for governance by those who boast so exact an admixture of folly and of vice as makes them reasonably consonant with the stuff they have to govern" [017-002]. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Reynolds represents the kind of man whose very excellence guarantees obscurity — "the sooner they are out of it the better for them."

## Life & Context

Reynolds died in the 1918–19 influenza pandemic at the age of thirty-seven. His work with the [[devon|Devon]] fishing communities led to practical improvements in their working conditions, and he served on government committees concerned with the fishing industry during the [[great-war|First World War]]. To [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s point about the contrast between "public label" and "true function," Reynolds's reputation has indeed remained small — but among those who know his work, he is held in the high regard [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] predicted he would have in "the eyes of their Maker."
