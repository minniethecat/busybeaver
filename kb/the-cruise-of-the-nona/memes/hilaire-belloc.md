Hilaire Belloc (1870–1953) was a [[france|French]]-born British writer, poet, historian, sailor, and member of [[parliament|Parliament]]. *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, published in 1925, is one of his most personal works — a memoir of sailing combined with extended reflections on politics, religion, history, and literature.

## In the *Nona*

Belloc appears throughout the book as its narrator and p[[corruption|rot]]agonist. The *[[nona|Nona]]* is his own boat, and the entire narrative — from leaving [[holyhead|Holyhead Harbour]] in darkness to navigating the treacherous [[tide|tidal]] races of the [[wales|Welsh coast]] and the English Channel — is told in the first person:

> The Cruise of the Nona — the story of a cruise from Holyhead to the Wash, with reflections and judgments on life and letters, men and manners [P01-001].

The book takes its name from Belloc's vessel. He reflects on the choice of title, noting that he might equally have named it *The Cargo* — for the book is as much about the ideas and people he carries in his mind as about sailing:

> I might have called the whole book by the name of my boat, the Nona, and give the whole book the name of the boat, but I could not see why the name of the contents should suffer [P04-021].

> I gave the book a name: The Cruise of the Nona. Had I called it The Cargo, I might have better pleased the critics; for the book is stuffed with things that have nothing to do with the sea [P04-024].

The narrative opens dramatically, with Belloc taking the *[[nona|Nona]]* out from [[holyhead|Holyhead Harbour]] in darkness:

> It was a very dark night, with no moon, when I took the Nona out from Holyhead Harbour, having waited for the tide [001-001].

The book was published in 1925, after Belloc's career as a Liberal MP (1906–1910) had ended and his reputation as a man of letters was firmly established [046-001].

## Parliamentary Career

Belloc served as a Liberal Member of [[parliament|Parliament]] for Salford South from 1906 to 1910. His experience in the [[house-of-commons]] left him deeply disillusioned with parliamentary democracy. Throughout *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, he returns to the theme of [[house-of-commons|the Commons]]' [[corruption|decay]], documenting what he saw as its transformation from a deliberative body into a machine of "personal intrigue" — a critique grounded in his own observation "during the two [[parliament|Parliament]]s in which I sat" [007-028].

## Literary and Religious Identity

Belloc was one of the most prominent [[religion|Catholic]] voices in English letters during the early twentieth century, alongside his close friend [[g-k-chesterton]]. His historical works, including *The Path to Rome* (1902) and his studies of the [[france|French]] Revolution, established his reputation as a narrative historian of the first rank. His poetry — particularly his cautionary verses for children — remained widely read throughout his lifetime.

Throughout *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, Belloc's [[catholic-church|Catholicism]] informs his critique of modern [[england|England]], from the decline of the [[house-of-commons]] to what he saw as the spiritual emptiness of [[the-press|the press]] and the universities. Yet the dominant note of the book is not polemical but reflective — a man at [[the-sea|sea]], alone with his thoughts, taking stock of a life spent in letters and politics.
