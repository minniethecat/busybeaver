# Alderney Race

## Overview

Alderney Race — the passage between the island of Alderney and the Cap de la Hague on the [[france|French]] coast — is one of the most violent tide races in European waters. Currents can reach twelve knots at spring [[tide|tides]], and the overfalls can break a small vessel. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Alderney Race is one of the defining features of the English Channel: a "gatepost" of the narrow [[the-sea|sea]] that, together with [[portland-bill|Portland Bill]] on the English side, marks the boundary between the Atlantic Ocean and what he calls "the domestic pond" [028-004].

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] had passed through Alderney Race in the 1890s and remembered it vividly. By the time of the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s cruise, decades later, he had not been through it since — but the memory of its violence remained a benchmark against which other races were measured.

## In the *Nona*

Alderney Race appears early in the book as a point of reference for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s experience of rough water. Recounting a particularly violent passage off the [[wales|Welsh coast]] — where "the Earth-Shaker" pursued him "viciously" — he notes that the confused [[the-sea|seas]] were unlike anything he had seen, "not even in Alderney Race, which I had gone through" [001-014]. The comparison is significant: Alderney Race serves as [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s yardstick for extreme conditions. If a [[the-sea|sea]] is worse than Alderney, it is worth writing about.

In Section 11, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] catalogs the great tide races of [[english-channel|the Channel]] with something approaching enthusiasm. "Many races do I know, [[portland-bill|Portland Race]] and Alderney Race and Little Saint Alban's" [011-020] — the list reads like a connoisseur's inventory of maritime terrors. The [[wild-goose-race|Wild Goose]] Race, he notes, is in a class of its own: "a boat is taken up and pitched to heaven and let drop again, twirled round like a teetotum, thrown over on her side, banged off sideways with great stunning blows upon the cheekbone" [011-020]. But Alderney is the standard by which the others are judged.

The most sustained meditation on Alderney comes in Section 28, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reflects on the geography of the narrow [[the-sea|sea]]. "[[portland-bill|Portland Bill]] stands right out into [[english-channel|the Channel]] and challenges the Atlantic tide. It is a gatepost, with Alderney and the Hogue for gateposts on the other side" [028-004]. The image is architectural: [[english-channel|the Channel]] as a gateway, its pillars [[portland-bill|Portland]] on one side and Alderney and Cap de la Hague on the other. "Outside them you are really (in spite of names) in the air of [[the-sea|the ocean]], and look toward the Americas. Inside them is the domestic pond" [028-004]. Alderney Race is not merely a navigational [[danger|hazard]]; it is a geographical threshold — the line between [[the-sea|ocean]] and home.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] acknowledges that his knowledge of Alderney is dated. "I have not been through Alderney Race since the nineties, but I suppose it is still going strong" [028-011]. The casual understatement — "I suppose it is still going strong" — is typical of his treatment of natural forces. [[tide|Tide]]s do not age; the Race that terrified him as a [[youth|young]] man is still there, unchanged, indifferent to the decades that have passed.

## Geography & History

Alderney Race (Raz Blanchard in [[france|French]]) is the strait between Alderney, the northernmost of [[english-channel|the Channel]] Islands, and the Cotentin Peninsula of Normandy. The [[tide|tidal]] range in the Bay of Mont Saint-Michel — among the highest in Europe — drives enormous volumes of water through the narrow passage, creating currents of up to twelve knots and dangerous overfalls. The Race has been a [[danger|hazard]] to shipping since antiquity and remains challenging even for modern vessels. During the Second World War, Alderney was occupied by German forces and fortified as part of the Atlantic Wall. The island is now a British Crown Dependency, though geographically closer to France than to [[england|England]].
