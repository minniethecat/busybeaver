# Portsmouth

## Overview

Portsmouth is the great naval harbour of southern [[england|England]], standing at the eastern entrance of the [[solent|Solent]] where it opens into Spithead. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] names it sparingly but strategically: Portsmouth Harbour is the standard of comparison, the measure against which other harbours — past and present — are judged. In the geography of the *[[nona|Nona]]*, Portsmouth is not a place [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] visits so much as a fixed point in the mental chart of the south coast.

## In the *Nona*

Portsmouth appears twice in the book, in two very different contexts. In the great meditation on the [[solent|Solent]] waters, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] lists it among the harbours and havens that crowd the Hampshire shore:

> "Portsmouth Harbour right up to Fareham, and the Hamble to its bridge, and all the yard and wharfs of the Test and the Itchen" [031-001].

It is part of the dense geography of the "three-spoked lake" whose every entry and sounding a sailor might know — and yet, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] insists, still not truly possess unless he sees upon it the fullness of its past.

The second mention is more revealing. Writing of Pevensey, the Roman fortress whose harbour has utterly vanished, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reaches for Portsmouth as the analogy that makes the loss legible:

> "The place was a narrows, like those which give entry to the wide expanse of Portsmouth Harbour; and, as in that gulley between Portsmouth and Gosport today, there must then have raced through the gulley of Pevensey the gallop of the flood and of the ebb tides" [043-005].

Pevensey once was "what Portsmouth is" [043-008] — a vast sheltered harbour capable of holding a fleet. The comparison is [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s way of measuring historical depth: Portsmouth is the living thing against which the dead thing can be understood. And the implication is clear — what happened to Pevensey could happen again.

## Geography & History

Portsmouth has been the principal base of the [[the-fleet|Royal Navy]] since the twelfth century, its natural deep-water harbour making it one of the most strategically important ports in Europe. The harbour entrance is a narrow channel between Portsmouth and Gosport, through which the [[tide|tides]] race as [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] describes. The historic dockyard, home to Nelson's *Victory* and the *Mary Rose*, was still an active naval base in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s day. The comparison with Pevensey — a Roman harbour now dry land — carries an unspoken warning about the impermanence of even the greatest maritime installations.
