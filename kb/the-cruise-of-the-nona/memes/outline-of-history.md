## Overview

*The Outline of History* (1920) was H. G. Wells's attempt to tell the story of life and mankind from its origins to the present in a single accessible volume. It was an immense commercial success, selling millions of copies and making Wells wealthy. The book reflected the progressive, scientistic worldview of its author — a vision of human ascent from primordial slime to en[[lighthouse|light]]ened modernity that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] found philosophically shallow but professionally impressive in its execution.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] discusses *The Outline of History* in Section 40, within a sustained reflection on the economics of literary reputation. He has just praised Wells's *[[the-time-machine|The Time Machine]]* as "quite certainly of the first class" and "quite likely to endure." The contrast with the *Outline* is sharp:

> "That one which I hear has sold most largely has always seemed to me to be a mere repetition of what his audience already thought they knew, and of a philosophy which they already held: I mean his *Outline of History*. It was mainly a repetition of the popular mythology of today, of antiquated scientific jargon, full of the popular errors." (040-023)

Yet [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s criticism is not unqualified. He immediately adds praise for the book's technical virtues:

> "But it was clear, well-proportioned and vivacious, showing a rare economy in the use and placing of words. It was also (and that is high minor praise for any book purporting to deal with history) astonishingly accurate in details. The names were properly spelt; south was never written for north, nor east for west; the dates were all right."

This is pure [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]: ideological disagreement does not prevent him from acknowledging craftsmanship. The *Outline of History* propagated ideas [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] considered pernicious — the mythology of inevitable progress, the dismissal of religion as superstition — but it did so with professional competence. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s self-deprecating admission that he himself is "constitutionally inaccurate in such things" and "will write left for right and north for south as a matter of course" gives the praise a personal warmth.

His final judgment is succinct: "the book, with all its good and bad qualities, is obviously not of the first class, and equally obviously is not destined to endure."

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was himself a prolific historian — his *The Jews*, *The [[france|French]] Revolution*, and *A History of [[england|England]]* were widely read. His disagreement with Wells was not merely about [[truth|fact]]s but about the philosophy of history. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] believed history was shaped by Providence, tradition, and human will; Wells believed it was shaped by evolutionary forces and material conditions. The *Outline of History* represented everything [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] opposed intellectually, which makes his fair-minded assessment of its technical merits all the more noteworthy.
