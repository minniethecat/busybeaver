Alfred Dreyfus (1859–1935) was a [[france|French]] artillery officer of Jewish ancestry whose 1894 conviction for treason divided France and reverberated through European politics for decades. In [[the-cruise-of-the-nona|The Cruise of the Nona]], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] devotes a substantial portion of Section 28 to the Dreyfus case as one of three great historical puzzles turning on human motive.

## Belloc's Analysis

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] groups the Dreyfus case with the Affair of the Diamond Necklace and the Tichborne case as problems where "the difficulty of finding the solution lies in the discovery of motive" [028-032]. In each instance, two irreconcilable sets of testimony confronted one another, and the truth depended on weighing contradictory human accounts rather than physical evidence.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reflected on the enduring passions the case aroused:

> The Dreyfus case, after all these years, still rouses such passions that I tremble even to mention it. I have myself (so many years after!) been subject to mournful rebuke in public from the excellent pen of Dean Inge, who has reproached me in well-knit prose for being the only man in England who did not take the side of the accused [028-038].

## The Division of Opinion

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is careful to note that competent opinion remained divided long after the verdict. Men eminently qualified to judge — including legal professionals and those of Dreyfus's own social standing — were as violently divided on the Dreyfus affair as they had been on the Tichborne case [028-035].

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] himself was publicly rebuked by Dean Inge for being "the only man in [[england|England]] who did not take the side of the accused." He counters by citing prominent Englishmen who shared his doubt, notably [[lord-russell-of-killowen|Lord Russell of Killowen]], the Lord Chief [[justice|Justice]], and Mr. Labouchère [028-038]. He adds: "There are in [[england|England]] today two Englishmen whose wide knowledge of Europe and especially of Paris, and the [[france|French]] tongue and society, enable them to judge. They are both close friends of mine. One is for, the other against" [028-039].

## Motives on Both Sides

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] identifies two powerful but opposed motives operating in the case. On one side was the desire to preserve the Intelligence Department of the [[french-army|French Army]] intact against rival powers bent on its destruction. On the other was the overwhelming motive of freeing a man believed innocent, combined with the lesser motive of defending the immunity of a small, immensely wealthy clique of international financiers — one of whose members had, for the first and last time in the modern era, been challenged by the authority of a state [028-038].

## The Judges' Division

Even the bench itself was divided: a minority of the five judges — two out of three — were reportedly for acquittal [028-041]. Of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own intimate acquaintances who were on the spot and competent to judge, "most were for the innocence of Dreyfus: but the rest, fully competent also, were, and are, convinced of his guilt" [028-041].

## Belloc's Epistemological Caution

Throughout his discussion, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] maintains studied agnosticism about Dreyfus's actual guilt or innocence. He doubts whether any man could reach a certain conclusion who was not "on the bench in court, and physically confronted with the witnesses, and acquainted with all the documents" [028-041]. He predicts the case's ultimate historical fate:

> I believe that, when the passions have died down, the Dreyfus case will remain for history very much what the Diamond Necklace has remained, or the Tichborne case; that is, there will be a popular legend, intellectually worth nothing; and, for the historian, the task of criticising that legend, but hardly of solving the problem [028-043].

## Historical Significance

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] advances a striking thesis about the affair's geopolitical consequences:

> It is to the Dreyfus case that we owe the four years of war, 1914–18: for it destroyed the French Intelligence Bureau and so permitted the German surprise on Mons and Charleroi [028-044].

The internal convulsion of the Affair, in his analysis, fatally compromised [[french-army|French military]] preparedness at the critical moment — a judgment that connected his reflections on historical [[doubt|uncertainty]] directly to the catastrophe he had lived through.
