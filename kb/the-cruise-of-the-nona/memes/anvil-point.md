# Anvil Point

## Overview

Anvil Point is a headland on the Dorset coast of [[england|England]], situated near [[swanage-bay]] and marking the eastern approach to the [[poole]] harbour entry. It is a significant [[tide|tidal]] waypoint: rounding the Point at the right moment allows a sailor to carry a favourable tide much further than the usual six-hour window.

For [[hilaire-belloc]], Anvil Point had a personal significance beyond navigation. He regarded it as the western boundary of his "home [[the-sea|seas]]" — the stretch of the English Channel he knew as intimately as a garden.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] describes the [[tide|tidal]] advantage that Anvil Point offers the knowing sailor:

> You come round the Anvil Point, and there, if you are lucky, you catch another tide. There are many such points on the coast where you can carry the tide with you much longer than the due six hours or so, by coming into the midst of a fresh tide at the very end of the old one. [029-002]

He explains the mechanics: the main [[tide|tidal stream]] "seems to shoot past Anvil Point, towards the Island" [029-002], and a well-timed arrival at the top of the flood lets a vessel catch a second, [[youth|young]]er flood up past Old Harry and into [[poole]] harbour.

In a later passage, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] stakes a larger claim on the Point's significance:

> I am entering the home seas, which I count from the Anvil to the [[south-foreland|South Foreland]] as being my garden or back yard. [029-012]

He admits, with characteristic self-awareness, that his "estate" has expanded over the [[time|years]] — "I am not sure that [[portland-bill|Portland Bill]] is not now my gatepost, instead of the Anvil" [029-012] — but the original boundary, the Anvil, remains the point from which his intimate knowledge of [[english-channel|the Channel]] coast begins.
