# Landing of William of Orange

## Overview

The landing of William of Orange at Torbay on 5 November 1688 — the opening move of the "Glorious Revolution" — is one of the hinge events of English history. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], entering Torbay "all alone under so glad a morning" (022-019), it becomes an occasion for historical reflection of a particularly sardonic kind.

## Belloc's Account

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of the landing is characteristically double. He acknowledges its historical importance — "that other, that real, that known affair, the landing of the Prince of Orange" (022-019) — but he cannot resist the comedy of the event's details: the expedition financed with Dutch [[wealth|money]] secured upon taxes promised *beforehand* from the English (on their tobacco), the betrayal of James II by his own daughter and son-in-law, [[winston-churchill|Churchill]] (the future Duke of Marlborough) leaning "his handsome, villainous face over the dinner table" trying to lure James out for a ride into the waiting arms of the enemy.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s account focuses on the human texture of the event rather than its constitutional significance. James II dining at Andover with his daughter's husband, the Prince of Denmark — "that great bagful of stupidity" — repeating "Est-il possible!" to everything said, then excusing himself... and never returning. "That business on which they had excused themselves was treason" (022-024).

## Historical Context

The Glorious Revolution replaced the [[religion|Catholic]] James II with the P[[corruption|rot]]estant William III and Mary II, established parliamentary supremacy, and set [[england|England]] on a course that would lead, within a generation, to the Union with Scotland and the emergence of Great [[england|Britain]] as a European power. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], as a [[religion|Catholic]] and a student of the Stuart cause, viewed these events with an ambivalence that most English historians did not share.

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s readers in 1925, the Revolution was a settled [[truth|fact]] of national mythology. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s genius is to unsettle it — not by denying its importance but by insisting on the human comedy within the grand narrative.
