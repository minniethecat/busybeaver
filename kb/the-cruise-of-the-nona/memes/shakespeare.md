# Shakespeare

## Overview

Shakespeare appears in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* not as a biographical subject but as a cultural touchstone — the supreme example of what English literature achieved when it was still embedded in a [[religion|Catholic]] civilisation. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] invokes him three times, each in a different register: as evidence of [[england|England]]'s lost [[religion|Catholic]] culture, as a poet of [[death|mortality]], and as a master of the "violent stabs of emotion" that define poetry at its most intense.

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Shakespeare is never merely "the Bard" of patriotic cliché. He is a specific historical figure — "a writer who w[[corruption|rot]]e for and in a [[religion|Catholic]] [[england|England]], and a writer whose whole atmosphere is [[religion|Catholic]]" (015-027). This claim is central to [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s larger argument about English history: that the Reformation represented not progress but a catastrophic rupture, and that the greatest achievements of English culture belong to the world before that rupture.

## Belloc's Shakespeare

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most extended treatment of Shakespeare comes in a passage about the ignorance of [[religion|Catholic]] culture among educated Englishmen. He observes that the cultivated Englishman "is certain to have read Shakespeare" (015-027) — and yet remains oblivious to the [[religion|Catholic]] atmosphere that permeates the plays. The irony is deliberate: Shakespeare is universally admired, but the civilisation that produced him is treated as alien.

This is not merely a sectarian point. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is making a historical argument: you cannot understand Shakespeare without [[knowledge|understanding]] the world he inhabited, and that world was [[religion|Catholic]]. The "corporate tradition which made Europe" — "the matrix of such culture as we still retain" (015-027) — is the necessary context for everything Shakespeare w[[corruption|rot]]e. To read him without that context is to read him incompletely.

## The Poet of Mortality

In a passage on ageing and death, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] quotes Shakespeare in a different key: "we ripe and ripe, and then we [[corruption|rot]] and [[corruption|rot]]" (027-017). The reference — to *As You Like It* — serves [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s meditation on the indignity of resisting time. Shakespeare here is not the [[religion|Catholic]] poet of a lost civilisation but the universal poet of human [[death|mortality]], placed alongside Dante and Malherbe in a company of those who have told the truth about our condition.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s use of Shakespeare in this context is characteristic of his method: he reaches for the highest cultural authority available, not to display [[knowledge|erudition]] but to anchor a plain truth. That we ripen and [[corruption|rot]] is obvious to anyone who lives long enough; that Shakespeare said it first merely confirms that the obvious truths are also the deepest.

## Poetry and Its Boundary

The third Shakespearean reference is the most purely literary. "Poetry is only found in certain violent stabs of emotion such as Shakespeare and Keats can launch" (040-037), [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes, in the midst of a sustained reflection on the nature of poetry and prose. Shakespeare here is the exemplar of intensity — the poet who can concentrate an entire world of feeling into a single phrase.

But [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] immediately qualifies: poetry "often inhabits page upon page," as in [[paradise-lost|Milton's Fourth Book]]. Shakespeare represents one mode of poetic achievement — the [[lighthouse|light]]ning strike — but not the only one. This qualification is itself characteristically [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]ian: he refuses to let even Shakespeare become an idol.

## Shakespeare in Belloc's England

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s Shakespeare is ultimately a diagnostic tool. The [[truth|fact]] that educated Englishmen can "read Shakespeare" and yet understand nothing of the [[religion|Catholic]] civilisation that made him possible reveals, for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the depth of [[england|England]]'s cultural amnesia. [[england]] has forgotten its own history, and its greatest poet has become — in the deepest sense — unreadable.

This is a sombre conclusion, but [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] delivers it without despair. Shakespeare's work survives, and with it the possibility that a reader might one day recover the world it presupposes. The plays are still there, "in the midst of" a culture that no longer understands them — like the Temple Church in [[london]], still standing, but "dead."

## Key Passages

> He is certain to have read Shakespeare — a writer who wrote for and in a Catholic England, and a writer whose whole atmosphere is Catholic. (015-027)

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s central claim: Shakespeare belongs to the [[religion|Catholic]] civilisation [[england|England]] has forgotten.

> Shakespeare (another of those poets) also said that we ripe and ripe, and then we rot and rot. It is all in the Providence of God. (027-017)

The poet of [[death|mortality]], placed alongside Dante and Malherbe in a company of truth-tellers.

> Poetry is only found in certain violent stabs of emotion such as Shakespeare and Keats can launch, for it often inhabits page upon page. (040-037)

Shakespeare as the master of intensity — but not the only mode of poetic achievement.
