# American War of Independence

## Overview

The American War of Independence (1775–1783) appears in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s memoir not as a historical event in its own right but as his prime exhibit in a case against popular historiography. In Section 18, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] argues that the conventional English narrative of the war — colonies righteously rebelling against unjust taxation, [[england|England]] repenting of its error — is a "false legend" manu[[truth|fact]]ured for political convenience.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] introduces the American war immediately after dismissing "prehistory" as "a mere imposture" (018-021), suggesting that the same myth-making impulse operates on both the distant and the recent past.

## Belloc's Account

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] opens by stating the standard version with disarming precision:

> Millions of children are told that the American Colonies revolted because they were unjustly taxed by England; that after a long struggle they won, and that they won because they were right; because they had the same qualities as our own sacred selves, being of our own sacred blood; that the trouble ought never to have arisen, and was only due to the folly of a few misguided and pigheaded English gentlemen who happened then to hold political power. (018-021)

He then systematically dismantles this account. The war, he reminds his reader, "had been waged by the Mother Country in defence of the Colonies" (018-021) — a reference to the Seven Years' War that had secured the colonies' western frontier. The tax dispute arose from "long and difficult negotiations" over how to share that expense. And "certainly a majority of the colonists — and probably a large majority — were on the side of [[england|England]], and against the rebels" (018-021), a claim that challenges the Whig assumption of a united colonial cause.

Most strikingly, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] insists that "the final defeat of [[england|England]] was mainly due to the intervention of the [[france|French]], and especially to the more close concentration of the [[france|French]] fleet" (018-021). This is not revisionism for its own sake — it reflects the [[france|French]] naval victory at the Chesapeake in 1781, which trapped [[cornwall|Cornwall]]is at Yorktown.

## Historical Context

The American Revolutionary War began with the Battles of Lexington and Concord (1775) and ended with the Treaty of Paris (1783). France entered the war in 1778, followed by Spain (1779) and the Dutch Republic (1780), transforming a colonial rebellion into a global conflict. The [[france|French]] fleet under Admiral de Grasse defeated the British fleet at the Battle of the Chesapeake (September 1781), cutting off [[cornwall|Cornwall]]is's army and forcing his surrender at Yorktown — the decisive engagement [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] alludes to.

## Significance

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s deeper point is about the persistence of myth. The English, he claims, "continued to detest the Americans with the most vivid hatred for a hundred [[time|years]] afterwards" (018-021), and "the policy of pretending a friendship with America is quite recent, and only grew up after the strength of the new country was apparent, when the long agony of the [[american-civil-war|Civil War]] had turned in favour of the North" (018-021).

This passage, written in 1925, has a sharp contemporary edge. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sees the "false legend" of Anglo-American kinship as actively harmful: it leads English policymakers to assume American cooperation that never materialises. "The false legend is swallowed whole, and it is a very good example of how mythology can have a direct practical effect for ill" (018-021). The American War of Independence is, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s hands, not a story about 1776 but a warning about 1925.
