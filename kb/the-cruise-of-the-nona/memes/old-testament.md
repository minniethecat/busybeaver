## Overview

The Old Testament — the Hebrew Scriptures comprising the Law, the Prophets, and the Writings — forms the first part of the Christian Bible. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the Old Testament was not merely a collection of ancient texts but a theological battlefield: the way different Christian traditions handled these writings revealed, in his view, the fundamental difference between [[religion|Catholic]] and P[[corruption|rot]]estant cultures.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s two references to the Old Testament in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* are brief in themselves but open onto some of his most important arguments.

The first occurs in Section 6, during a comic passage about the folly of tolerating fools. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] recalls that [[holy-writ|Holy Writ]] counsels patience with fools, then immediately undercuts this with characteristic irony:

> "Where it is written in Holy Writ that one should tolerate fools even with gladness, I thought that this was a general rule of conduct. But now I know it to be a counsel of perfection and, indeed, like so many things in the Old Testament, a counsel generally to be avoided." (006-002)

This throwaway remark — treating an Old Testament precept as something to be "avoided" — is a small example of the [[religion|Catholic]] freedom with [[holy-writ|Scripture]] that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] contrasts with P[[corruption|rot]]estant literalism.

The second and far more substantial reference comes in Section 22, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reflects on the discovery of prehistoric human remains at Torquay by a [[religion|Catholic]] priest, Dr. McEnery. This discovery challenged the biblical chronology that dated Creation to 4004 BC — a chronology that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] insists was a P[[corruption|rot]]estant fixation, not a [[religion|Catholic]] one.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] argues that the shock of discovering human antiquity was felt almost exclusively in the P[[corruption|rot]]estant world, because:

> "in the Protestant culture, these Old Testament stories were *lived*. They had become flesh and bone of their readers. The very characters therein took on a divine air; the cruelties, the obscenities, the puerilities, equally with the occasional sublime actions and sayings of those Old Testament characters, became in men's minds the attributes of demigods." (022-013)

This is the heart of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s critique. The P[[corruption|rot]]estant North, having abandoned the interpretative authority of [[the-church|the Church]], had built its entire culture on a literal reading of [[holy-writ|the Bible]]. When geology and archaeology dismantled that literalism, the result was not a return to [[religion|Catholic]] tradition but "a bewilderment, a chaos, a disintegration." [[the-church|The Catholic Church]], by contrast, had always read the Old Testament allegorically and typologically — as "mystical, often allegorical, prophetic figurings of one great event that was to come: the Incarnation." Physical discoveries could not shake a religious culture that had never rested on physical literalism.

## Connection to Belloc

The Old Testament serves [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] as a diagnostic instrument. The way a culture handles these ancient texts reveals whether it possesses a living tradition of interpretation or is adrift on the private judgment of individual readers. P[[corruption|rot]]estant bibliolatry, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s analysis, made the Old Testament into a "rock of authority" that was bound to crumble — and when it crumbled, it took with it much of the moral and intellectual framework of Northern Europe. The [[religion|Catholic]], by contrast, could accept both the divinity of [[holy-writ|Scripture]] and the discoveries of [[knowledge|science]], because his [[religion|faith]] rested on the living Church, not on the dead letter.
