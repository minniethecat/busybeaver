## Overview

*A Crystal Age* (1887) is a utopian romance by W. H. Hudson (1841–1922), the Anglo-Argentine naturalist and writer. The novel imagines a future society living in harmonious simplicity, governed by a quasi-religious devotion to nature. It was Hudson's second novel, published [[time|years]] before he achieved fame as a nature writer with books like *Green Mansions* and *Far Away and Long Ago*.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] discusses *A Crystal Age* in Section 40, in a passage about the perversity of literary fame. He has been arguing that even when a writer becomes deservedly famous late in life, his earlier work rarely benefits:

> "Witness the late Mr. Hudson's *Crystal Age*. I came across it as a young man in a bookshop in the Euston Road on a winter evening. I took it up and began reading it, and I read it so long that the bookseller told me I must buy it, which I did." (040-023)

The anecdote is vivid and personal — the [[youth|young]] [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], browsing a secondhand bookshop on a winter evening, so absorbed by a random find that the proprietor has to remind him this is a shop, not a library. It is one of those moments of readerly discovery that every serious reader recognises.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] continues: "Now, towards the end of Mr. Hudson's life, he became deservedly famous as a writer upon the things of the natural world, and especially upon animals. But what made him famous was not his subject, though he was both accurate and learned; it was his manner of writing. One would have expected, therefore, that the *Crystal Age* would, upon his fame tardily arising, have a quiet and equal fame. It did not."

The lesson [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] draws is that the market for books has no memory and no logic. Hudson's late fame, earned through nature writing, did not transfer backward to his early fiction — even though it was the same sensibility, the same "manner of writing," that animated both. "I say that there are no laws governing these movements. They are capricious and incalculable."

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s affection for *A Crystal Age* is revealing. Hudson's utopian vision — a society governed by reverence for the natural order rather than by the machinery of progress — had affinities with [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own distributist politics and his suspicion of industrial modernity. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was not a utopian, but he understood the impulse that produced utopias: the desire to imagine a world ordered differently from the chaotic, unjust world we inhabit.
