Hubert Lyautey (1854–1934) was a Marshal of France and the first Resident-General of the [[france|French]] p[[corruption|rot]]ectorate in Morocco. In [[the-cruise-of-the-nona|The Cruise of the Nona]], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] presents Lyautey as the exemplar of a particular kind of achievement: constructive work built to last, executed outside the machinery of parliamentary politics.

## Historical Context

Lyautey served as Resident-General of Morocco from 1912 to 1925, pacifying the country and building the administrative infrastructure of the p[[corruption|rot]]ectorate. He was briefly Minister of War in 1916–17 before returning to Morocco. His reputation as a builder-administrator — as distinct from a mere conqueror — was precisely what attracted [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s admiration.

## Belloc's Portrait

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s reference to Lyautey occurs in Section 13, embedded in a broader argument about the nature of permanence in human institutions. "Lyautey's Morocco," [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes, "is an astonishing thing" [013-011]. What astonishes him is not the military conquest but the construction of a functioning administration — roads, courts, schools, public health — that promised to endure.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] grounds his admiration in two principles he believed Lyautey embodied:

> What Marshal Lyautey has done in the organisation of French Morocco has been based upon two foundations which, when they are absent from the effort to govern aliens — as one or both commonly are — make such government increasingly difficult; until, at last, it has to be abandoned. The first of these foundations is *working with* the alien people whom one administrates, not *against* them, nor even *above* them. The second foundation is the deliberate investment of one's own capital and energy *for the benefit of the alien governed* rather than for one's own benefit [013-015].

## A Building "by Itself"

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] characterises Lyautey's achievement as "a building by itself" — a structure raised by individual vision and energy rather than by the collective machinery of the [[france|French]] state [013-012]. This distinction is central to [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s political philosophy: his deep [[doubt|scepticism]] of parliamentary government and his corresponding admiration for men of action who build directly upon the land and its people.

## The Morocco Belloc Saw

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] visited Morocco himself and recorded his impressions:

> I carried away with me from that glimpse of the Atlas and of the treeless run of burnt land beyond vivid pictures in the mind: first of a vast tract of dead upland, utterly bare, between Oudja and the Moulouya [013-020].

He found in the [[france|French]] administration a genuine respect for Islamic civilisation that contrasted sharply with the contempt he had observed among less educated colonists. "The higher you went in the scale," he noted, "the greater was the liking for the Moor and for his civilisation, the greater the desire to understand him and to know his past, the greater the admiration of his art, his dignity, his [[religion|faith]]" [013-019].

## Contrast with Parliamentary Achievement

The Lyautey passage functions as a foil to [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s critique of democratic politics elsewhere in the book. Where parliamentary ministers come and go, leaving little trace, Lyautey — working at the periphery of empire, with considerable autonomy — created something tangible. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] saw in Lyautey's Morocco an illustration of a truth he considered universal: "There is no other working form of administration over multitudes of men, except the aristocratic" [013-014].

## The Great War Crisis

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] recounts a critical decision during the July crisis of 1914. The [[france|French]] government in Paris ordered the evacuation of the Moroccan interior, but Lyautey resisted:

> When war was already certain in the last days of July, 1914, the Government in Paris, the politicians, sent orders to the Resident-General that all troops should be withdrawn to the coast, the interior to be abandoned, and reconquest (if possible!) to be achieved after the Peace. We who know what those words "after the Peace" have come to mean, know also that it would have been the end of the French in Morocco [013-022].

Lyautey's defiance of this order preserved the p[[corruption|rot]]ectorate through the war, demonstrating — in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s view — the superiority of the man on the ground over the distant politician.
