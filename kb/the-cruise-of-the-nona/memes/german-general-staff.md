## Overview

The German General Staff — the *Großer Generalstab* — was the supreme military planning
body of the Prussian and later Imperial German Army. In [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s memoir, written after
the Great War, the General Staff appears as the architect of a miscalculation: a war
plan built on the assumption of a short, decisive campaign in the West, followed by
a prolonged Eastern campaign, all predicated on British neutrality.

## Belloc's View

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] addresses the German General Staff directly in Section 20, where he dismantles
the post-war apologetics that sought to exonerate Berlin from responsibility for the
war. He notes the persistence of pro-German sentiment in [[england|England]]:

> Those who plead in favour of Prussia and of the German General Staff (they are
> numerous in England today) still pretend, as do all the German writers, that the
> mobilisation ordered from Berlin was but a reply to the earlier orders issued from
> Saint Petersburg. (020-010)

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] rejects this argument, citing the institutional relationship between the
German General Staff and [[the-press|the press]]:

> There is the connection between the "Lokal Anzeiger" and the German General Staff.
> (020-011)

## The Mobilisation Scheme

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most vivid characterisation of the German General Staff is his comparison
of the Prussian mobilisation system to a weapon already aimed:

> The Prussian scheme of mobilisation may be compared to a loaded gun. If I know
> that a man has his gun loaded and he aims it at me, my then loading mine is not
> a provocation. (020-011)

This metaphor captures [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s argument: the German General Staff had designed a
mobilisation system in which preliminary steps were equivalent to other nations'
final orders, making the distinction between "mobilisation" and "war" artificial
in the Prussian case.

## The Strategic Miscalculation

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] identifies the German General Staff's fundamental error: they planned for a
Continental war only. The Staff believed that even if [[england|England]] intervened, the
immediate victory in the West would render [[the-sea|sea]] power irrelevant:

> Granted an immediate and certain German victory in the West (as a fact it failed),
> there would still be an Eastern campaign which the German General Staff believed
> to be necessarily prolonged and which they dreaded; blockade during that trial
> they dared not face. (020-013)

This passage reveals the General Staff's strategic blind spot: the failure to
anticipate that [[french-army|the French Army]] would survive the initial onslaught, and that the
[[the-fleet|Royal Navy]]'s blockade would prove decisive.

## In Belloc's World

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s analysis of the German General Staff reflects his broader view of
institutional hubris. As a military historian who had studied the campaigns of
the [[france|French]] Revolutionary and Napoleonic wars, he understood the General Staff's
planning tradition. His critique is not of Prussian military competence — which
he respected — but of the political miscalculation that assumed British neutrality
and [[france|French]] collapse. The German General Staff, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s telling, were masters
of operational planning who failed at strategic judgment.
