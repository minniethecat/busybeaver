## Overview

Thomas Henry Huxley (1825–1895) was the foremost public advocate of Charles Darwin's theory of evolution, earning the nickname "Darwin's Bulldog" for his tenacious defence of natural selection against both scientific and religious critics. A comparative anato[[fog|mist]] by training, Huxley was also one of the finest English prose writers of the Victorian age — a [[truth|fact]] that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], ever attentive to the craft of writing, singles out for praise in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s three references to Huxley span the book and reveal a complex admiration: for Huxley's willingness to suffer for inconvenient truths, for his place in the Victorian intellectual firmament, and for his mastery of English prose.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] first invokes Huxley in a passage about the cost of telling unwelcome truths. He observes that most men avoid the burden of proclaiming what the world does not wish to hear because to do so is to invite [[pain|suffering]]. Against this human tendency, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sets a single name:

> Huxley, who sacrificed himself entirely, both to false things which he believed to be true and to real things that were true, I never knew. [010-008]

The sentence is double-edged in a characteristically [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]ian way: Huxley's courage is honoured even as his beliefs are implicitly questioned. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a [[religion|Catholic]] apologist who knew what it meant to defend unpopular positions in public, clearly feels a kinship with Huxley's willingness to stand alone.

The second reference places Huxley in the distinguished company of the Victorian periodical press, that vanished world of serious intellectual journalism that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] laments:

> You saw in them the names of Tennyson or Browning, of Huxley and the Duke of Argyll, of Lord Salisbury, of Arnold. [039-013]

The third mention is the most telling. In a sustained reflection on the art of English prose, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] makes a striking confession:

> …so are even those quoted from Newman and (I am sorry to say) those quoted from the greatest of our modern prose writers, Huxley. [040-029]

The parenthetical "I am sorry to say" is pure [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] — an honest admission that the agnostic Huxley, rather than any [[religion|Catholic]] writer, earns the title of "greatest of our modern prose writers."

## Life & Context

Born in Ealing in 1825, Huxley was largely self-educated. He studied medicine at Charing Cross Hospital and, at twenty, sailed as assistant surgeon on HMS *Rattlesnake* to survey the waters around Australia and New Guinea. The marine specimens he collected and studied on that voyage established his scientific reputation.

His public career began in earnest at the 1860 [[oxford-university|Oxford]] debate, where he famously clashed with Bishop Samuel Wilberforce over Darwin's *Origin of Species*. From that moment until his death, Huxley was the public face of evolutionary [[knowledge|science]] in [[england|Britain]]. He coined the term "agnosticism" to describe his own philosophical position and w[[corruption|rot]]e prolifically on education, philosophy, and ethics. He died in 1895, having done more than any single figure to make evolutionary biology part of the common intellectual [[tradition|inheritance]] of the English-speaking world.

## Connection to Belloc

At first glance, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s admiration for Huxley is surprising. Huxley was a prominent agnostic; [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was a militant [[religion|Catholic]]. Yet [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] repeatedly names Huxley alongside [[john-henry-newman|Newman]] — the great [[religion|Catholic]] convert — as a master of English prose. The common ground is intellectual honesty. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] believed that clear writing was the outward sign of clear thinking, and in Huxley he found a writer who never blurred an argument or evaded a difficulty. That [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] could name an agnostic "the greatest of our modern prose writers" — and apologise for it — is a measure of his commitment to the craft of letters above sectarian allegiance.
