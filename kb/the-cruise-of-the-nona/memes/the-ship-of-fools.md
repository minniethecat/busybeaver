## Overview

*The Ship of Fools* (*Das Narrenschiff*), published in 1494 by the German humanist Sebastian Brant, is one of the foundational texts of Renaissance satire. Written in rhyming couplets and illustrated with woodcuts (some by the [[youth|young]] Albrecht Dürer), it depicts a ship laden with fools of every description sailing to the fools' paradise of Narragonia. The book was an immense success across Europe, translated into Latin, [[france|French]], English, and other vernaculars, and established the "fool literature" tradition that culminated in Erasmus's *[[the-praise-of-folly|The Praise of Folly]]*.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] mentions *The Ship of Fools* in Section 6, during a comic passage about his exasperation with a foolish [[wales|Welsh]] pilot who has been running the *[[nona|Nona]]* aground. This reflection on the nature of folly leads him to propose a modern revival of the Renaissance genre:

> "It is high time that a new book were written on Fools, like those of the sixteenth century, which are so excellent, and which you have in Latin, in German, in French, and in English: *The Praise of Folly*, *The Ship of Fools*, *The Fool's Paradise*, and others. If I had the leisure, it is one of the many hundred books which I should like to write myself: *The Fool, Sociological, Biological, and Pathological, with Many Portraits and an Appendix on the Newly Discovered Giant Fool of Aphasia*." (006-003)

The mock-scholarly title [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] invents for his proposed book — with its pseudo-scientific subdivisions and its "Newly Discovered Giant Fool of Aphasia" — is itself a joke in the Renaissance manner, using the apparatus of [[knowledge|learning]] to mock learned pretension. His category of modern fools would include the politician who stares blankly at his official papers, the "Fool of Inherited Money" who clings to his wealth "like bats in a steeple," and the academic who [[fog|mist]]akes the novel for the true.

But [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] admits he will never write the book: "As I have not the leisure (and never had it) I bequeath the idea to other and [[youth|young]]er and wealthier men, just as some [[time|years]] ago I bequeathed them the idea of a *Cads' Encyclopaedia*." The jest is characteristic — the huge ambitions, the self-mocking acknowledgment of incapacity, the aristocratic gesture of "bequeathing" an idea as though it were an estate.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s admiration for Renaissance satire is consistent with his broader intellectual loyalties. The sixteenth century — [[religion|Catholic]], European, learned but not pedantic, capable of laughing at folly without despising fools — represented for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] a cultural high point from which the modern world had declined. His own satirical writing, particularly his verse, belongs recognisably to this tradition, and his desire to write a modern *Ship of Fools* reveals his sense of himself as a Renaissance man born into the wrong century.
