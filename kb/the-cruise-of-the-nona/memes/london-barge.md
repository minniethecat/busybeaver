# London Barge

## Overview

The [[london|London]] barge — the spritsail-rigged, flat-bottomed working vessel of the [[thames|Thames]] estuary — is a recurring presence in the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s later chapters. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], approaching the [[thames|Thames]] and the end of his voyage, encounters these vessels not as curiosities but as fellow travellers, each one carrying its cargo with the patient competence of centuries.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s first barge reference is to the *[[the-lily|Lily]]*, a barge that has "salvaged" a cargo (041-021). The quotation marks around "salvaged" are telling — [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is amused by the legal language applied to what is, in essence, a neighbourly act of maritime assistance. The barge world operates by its own [[tradition|custom]]s, older and more organic than the Admiralty courts.

Later, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] observes "a [[london|London]] barge, a Norwegian ship with timber" (044-002) among the vessels he passes as he approaches the Wash. The barge is listed without special emphasis — simply one element in the working [[the-sea|seas]]cape, the ordinary maritime life that continues regardless of the philosophical reflections of a cruising yachtsman.

The [[london|London]] barge "seems to keep closer" to the land (041-023), [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] notes — an observation that is at once navigational and cultural. The bargemen know their waters intimately; they do not need the open [[the-sea|sea]] to make their living.

## Specifications & History

[[thames|Thames]] sailing barges were the dominant cargo carriers of the [[london|London]] River from the eighteenth century through the early twentieth. Flat-bottomed to sit upright on the mud at low tide, leeboarded to reduce draft, and rigged with a powerful spritsail, they could carry enormous loads with minimal crew. By [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s time, steam was overtaking sail, but barges still worked the estuary — a living link to an older maritime [[england|England]] that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the traditionalist, would have valued.
