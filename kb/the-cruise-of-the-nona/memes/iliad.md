# The Iliad

## Overview

[[homer|Homer]]'s *[[homer|Iliad]]* is the supreme epic of war — and for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], writing in the shadow of the Great War, it represents a mode of [[knowledge|understanding]] that modern strategists have lost. The ancients knew that battle was governed by chance and divine will; the moderns believed in plans.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s [[homer|Homer]]ic reference comes in Section 20, during his extended meditation on the origins and conduct of the Great War. Having argued that the war's course was governed by [[truth|fact]]ors "external to human direction" (020-011), he invokes the classical [[knowledge|understanding]]:

> It is with regard to the Great War as it is with regard to every set of campaigns in history; if you omit that which was not subject to any human will and intelligence, you omit nine tenths of the factors that make for the final result — and that is why every great captain has put his trust in Fortune and in a star, a destiny of his own. For great captains know what part Fortune plays in their trade. (020-013)

The "great captains" are [[homer|Homer]]'s heroes — Achilles, Hector, Odysseus — who live in a world where the gods intervene directly in battle and no outcome can be predicted with certainty. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is not suggesting that Athena actually fought at the Marne; he is arguing that the [[homer|Homer]]ic worldview, which acknowledges forces beyond human control, is more realistic than the modern one, which pretends to mastery.

## About the Work

The *[[homer|Iliad]]* is the oldest surviving work of Western literature, composed (or compiled) in the eighth century BC. Its twenty-four books recount a few weeks in the tenth year of the Trojan War, focusing on the wrath of Achilles and its consequences. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s generation, educated in the classics from childhood, [[homer|Homer]] was not an exotic text but a familiar presence — quoted, alluded to, and absorbed into the intellectual bloodstream.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s classicism is everywhere in the *[[nona|Nona]]*, though usually implicit. The book's maritime setting, its alternation between action and reflection, and its meditation on fate and character all owe something to the [[homer|Homer]]ic model — not as imitation but as [[tradition|inheritance]].
