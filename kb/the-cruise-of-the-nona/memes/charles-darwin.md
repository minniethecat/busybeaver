# Charles Darwin

**Charles Robert Darwin** (1809–1882) was an English naturalist, geologist,
and biologist, best known for his contributions to the [[knowledge|science]] of evolution.
His 1859 book *On the Origin of Species* introduced the theory of natural
selection, which proposed that species evolve over generations through the
differential survival of organisms with heritable traits best suited to their
environments.

## Key Works

Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* (1859) was followed by *The Descent of Man*
(1871), which applied evolutionary theory to human origins, and *The Expression
of the Emotions in Man and Animals* (1872). His earlier *Journal of Re[[the-sea|sea]]rches*
(1839), published as *The Voyage of the Beagle*, recounted his five-year
expedition aboard HMS *Beagle*, during which his observations of finches and
other wildlife on the [[galapagos-islands|Galápagos Islands]] provided crucial
evidence for his later theories.

## Belloc's Critique

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a devout [[religion|Catholic]], was a prominent critic of Darwinian evolution.
In *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* he dismisses Darwin's theory in characteristically
blunt terms:

> …exploded nonsense like Darwin's Natural Selection, and the imaginary evidence…
> — [022-020]

> The ruin of the Darwinian nonsense during the last…
> — [022-011]

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] viewed Darwinism as a philosophical error that had already begun to
collapse under scientific scrutiny by his own time — a position he shared
with other [[religion|Catholic]] intellectuals of the early twentieth century. His opposition
was not merely religious; he argued that Darwinism represented a broader
intellectual fashion that substituted plausible narrative for rigorous proof.
The "imaginary evidence" he mocks refers to what he saw as the theory's reliance
on extrapolation rather than observable data.

## Catholic Opposition to Darwinism

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s critique of Darwin formed part of a wider [[religion|Catholic]] intellectual
response to evolutionary theory. While the [[the-church|Catholic Church]] did not formally
condemn evolution as it had condemned [[galileo-galilei|Galileo]]'s astronomy,
many [[religion|Catholic]] thinkers — including [[john-henry-newman|John Henry Newman]] —
grappled with the theological implications of a theory that seemed to remove
divine purpose from the natural world. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s position was characteristically
polemical: he treated Darwinism not as a scientific theory to be debated on
its own terms but as a symptom of a broader intellectual [[corruption|decay]] that privileged
materialist explanations over metaphysical ones.
