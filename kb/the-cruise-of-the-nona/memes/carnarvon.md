# Carnarvonshire

## Overview

Carnarvonshire ([[wales|Welsh]]: *Sir Gaernarfon*) is the historic county of northwest [[wales|Wales]], comprising the Lleyn Peninsula and the mountainous mainland that stretches from the Menai Strait to the Conwy River. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Carnarvonshire is not a county to be explored but a point to be rounded — the headland that marks the entrance to [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]] and the beginning of the most dangerous passage of the entire cruise.

The Carnarvonshire coast is [[nona|the Nona]]'s gateway. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] first sights it under the glare of the [[lighthouse|light]]house at night, puts "the nose of the little ship round for the point of Carnarvonshire and the strait between the mainland and [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Island]], which is called [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]]" [001-002], and enters waters that will test him as no others do. In the geography of the *[[nona|Nona]]*, Carnarvonshire is the threshold between safe harbour and the open [[the-sea|sea]].

## In the *Nona*

The Carnarvonshire coast appears in the book's opening chapter, which is also its most harrowing. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] approaches from the north, rounding the point under sail as the wind freshens. His first description is practical — bearings, the shape of the land, the position of the [[lighthouse|light]]house — but the tone darkens quickly.

The gale that strikes the *[[nona|Nona]]* in [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]] transforms Carnarvonshire from a landmark into a refuge. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s goal becomes simple: "get round the point of Carnarvonshire into easier water right under the lee of the land" [001-012]. The shore, which had been scenery, becomes salvation. He watches the "points on the Carnarvonshire shore" as the *[[nona|Nona]]* struggles against wind and tide, measuring his progress in inches [001-014].

In the midst of the gale, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] looks at the coast close at hand and records a moment of strange detachment:

> "I looked at the Carnarvonshire coast there close at hand" and found that it stirred no emotion — not fear, not courage, not even a "right depreciation of this detestable little world" [001-017].

[[death|Death]] was close, the shore was close, and yet [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] felt nothing — a state of mind he analyses with the same precision he brings to his navigation. The Carnarvonshire coast, in this moment, is not a place but a condition: the nearness of safety that cannot be reached, the land that is so close and yet, in a gale, impossibly far.

In a later, calmer passage, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] looks back at "that coast between the point of Carnarvonshire and Portmadoc" and notes that it offers no shelter — "except a boat harbour or two, which could only be used in still weather" [003-001]. It is the verdict of experience: a coast he has learned the hard way, mile by mile.

## Geography & History

Carnarvonshire is dominated by the mountains of Snowdonia, which rise steeply from the [[the-sea|sea]] along its southern coast. The Lleyn Peninsula extends westward into the Irish Sea, and at its tip lies [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Island]], separated from the mainland by the treacherous [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]]. Historically, Carnarvonshire is the heartland of [[wales|Welsh]]-speaking [[wales|Wales]] and the site of Caernarfon Castle, built by Edward I in the late thirteenth century as part of his "iron ring" of fortresses. The county was renamed Caernarvonshire in the twentieth century and is now part of the modern county of Gwynedd.
