# Isle of Wight

## Overview

The Isle of Wight is a diamond-shaped island lying off the coast of Hampshire, separated from the mainland by [[solent|the Solent]] — the sheltered inland waterway that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] considers among the most history-dense stretches of [[the-sea|sea]] in [[england|Britain]]. For the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s cruise, the Wight is a landmark of the eastern Channel: its brilliant white chalk cliffs, the Needles rocks at its western tip, and the sheltered waters behind it form a recurring presence in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s navigation.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s interest in the island, however, extends beyond the nautical. In the opening pages of the book, he deploys the Isle of Wight in a characteristic historical aside — one that allows him to both celebrate his beloved [[sussex|Sussex]] and, reluctantly, yield the palm to its island neighbour. The Wight, he claims, was the last pagan kingdom in [[england|Britain]].

## In the *Nona*

The Isle of Wight makes its first appearance in the *[[nona|Nona]]* not as a navigational feature but as a historical curiosity. Meditating on the little kingdoms of early [[england|England]], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] notes "a king with two princes, his heirs, governing the Isle of Wight; all pagan when the rest of [[england|England]] had already gone [[religion|Catholic]]" [001-033]. It is a striking image — the island holding out against the tide of conversion, the old gods' last redoubt in the southern kingdom. He returns to the claim a moment later: "the truth is that not [[sussex|Sussex]] but the Isle of Wight was the last pagan spot in these islands, and here we of [[sussex|Sussex]] must reluctantly yield the palm" [001-035]. The concession is made with good humour — [[sussex|Sussex]] can still boast that its king was "the last of the kings in [[england|Britain]] to worship the Devil twelve hundred [[time|years]] ago" [001-035].

The Wight's navigational significance emerges gradually through the cruise narrative. Its western extremity — the Needles — are the most conspicuous [[the-sea|sea]]mark on the south coast. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] recounts an encounter with a sailor who, approaching from the west, failed to recognise them: "Are those the Needles?" the man shouted, "pointing fiercely eastward" [028-027]. The anecdote becomes a meditation on how familiarity shapes perception — the Needles, "the most conspicuous rocks, by their shape, in all the narrow [[the-sea|seas]]" [028-028], are invisible to those who approach them without the framework of local knowledge.

The "inland water behind the Wight" — [[solent|the Solent]] — earns [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s highest praise. Comparing natural harbours to artificial ones, he declares that "anyone would rather have the roads of Corfu, or the inland water behind the Wight than anything man ever made" [027-021]. It is a judgment that places [[solent|the Solent]] alongside the great natural harbours of the Mediterranean.

The waters around the Wight also feature in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s reflections on the double tide. The "gulder," as the locals call it — "the mystery of the Dorset and the Hampshire [[the-sea|seas]]" [014-015] — is a phenomenon that defies the conventional explanation. The theory that water enters through the Needles channel and then around by Spithead works for [[southampton|Southampton Water]], but Poole, which has no second entrance, experiences the same effect. The Wight's [[tide|tides]] remain, for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], one of those physical mysteries that keep the [[the-sea|sea]] interesting.

The Bay between Poole and the Wight is, for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], "so domestic and inward a thing that many would laugh to hear it called the [[the-sea|sea]] at all" [030-001]. But he immediately qualifies: "let them be as I was on that night and they will find it is the [[the-sea|sea]] right enough, and that it can raise a fine hubbub." The Wight's sheltered waters are deceptive — in the right conditions, they can match the violence of any open [[the-sea|sea]].

In Section 31, [[solent|the Solent]] becomes a lens through which [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] views all of British history. The Romans, the Saxons, the Normans, the Tudor fleets, the Dutch wars, the Napoleonic camps, the Victorian yachtsmen — "this patch of water has been packed with history as no other in [[england|Britain]], except [[thames|London River]]" [031-002]. The Wight is not merely an island; it is the sentinel of [[england|Britain]]'s most historic waterway.

## Geography & History

The Isle of Wight is roughly 23 miles by 13 miles, separated from Hampshire by [[solent|the Solent]] — a strait that is part drowned river valley, part [[tide|tidal]] channel. The island's chalk spine runs east to west, terminating in the Needles — three distinctive chalk stacks at the western extremity. Historically, the island was settled by the Jutes in the early Anglo-Saxon period and maintained a distinct royal line into the seventh century, when it was conquered by Wessex and forcibly Christianised. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s claim about it being the last pagan kingdom is historically plausible: the island's conversion to [[religion|Christianity]] is recorded as occurring around 686 CE, later than most of southern [[england|England]]. The island later became a favourite residence of Queen Victoria, whose Osborne House gave it a new identity as a royal retreat.
