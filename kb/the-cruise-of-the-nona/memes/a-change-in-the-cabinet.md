## Overview

*A Change in the Cabinet* (1909) is a political novel by Hilaire [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]. It is a satirical portrait of the Edwardian political world, written by a man who knew it from the inside — [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] served as a Liberal MP from 1906 to 1910. The novel follows the rise and fall of a cabinet minister, exposing the machinery of patronage, party intrigue, and the empty rhetoric of [[parliament|Westminster]].

## In *The Cruise of the Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] refers to *A Change in the Cabinet* in the context of a discussion about literary realism and the gap between how politicians actually speak and how novelists represent them. He describes a popular novelist who made her aristocratic characters "talk in his own house like Hansard" — delivering parliamentary-length sentences in domestic settings — and contrasts this with his own approach:

> "I myself once wrote a book about politicians, called *A Change in the Cabinet*. It was universally agreed in all that old world of professional politicians who were still educated men that my book, whether good or bad, was monstrously accurate to the way the people of that world talk and go on." [001-041]

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s point, however, is not to boast of his accuracy. He goes on to argue that the reading public will never accept such a book as realistic, because the public does not want realism — it wants a "pompous convention" that matches its preconceptions. This leads him to a broader defence of artifice in literature: "Literature has no business to be a mirror of life. We can see life for ourselves without literature."

## Connection to Belloc

The reference to *A Change in the Cabinet* is characteristic [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] — self-deprecating, meta-literary, and quietly polemical. He uses his own novel as a data point in an argument about the nature of literary truth, all the while aware that his readers are unlikely to have read it. The novel itself, though now obscure, was well received by [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s contemporaries; [[maurice-baring|Maurice Baring]], to whom *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* is dedicated, praised its wit and accuracy.
