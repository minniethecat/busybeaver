# Hungarian Soviet Republic

The Hungarian Soviet Republic — the brief communist regime established in Hungary under Béla Kun from March to August 1919 — appears in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* as an object lesson in the credulity of Western elites. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s reference captures his contempt for what he saw as the governing class's inability to distinguish real political forces from ephemeral ones.

## In the *Nona*

The reference occurs in Section 39, during [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s sweeping survey of the postwar transformation of English society. In a passage cataloguing the changes wrought by the Great War, he includes a barbed aside about the international scene: "the Jewish Communist revolution in Hungary would succeed. It was such men" who believed this, he writes [039-030]. The Hungarian Soviet Republic was, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s telling, a phantom — a regime that the Western press and political class briefly [[fog|mist]]ook for a permanent transformation, only to see it collapse within months when its internal contradictions and external enemies converged.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s choice of this example is not accidental. The Hungarian Soviet Republic was led by Béla Kun, a Jewish former journalist who had been radicalized as a prisoner of war in Russia. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] — whose anti-Semitic views on the relationship between Jews and revolutionary politics were publicly expressed throughout his career — the Hungarian episode illustrated a larger thesis about the instability of communist revolution and the folly of taking such movements at their own self-estimation. The *[[nona|Nona]]* reference is characteristically compressed, but it draws on a well of conviction [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] had been developing for decades.

## Wider Context

The Hungarian Soviet Republic lasted only 133 days before being overthrown by Romanian and Czechoslovak forces backed by the Entente powers. Its brief existence and sudden collapse made it, for conservative commentators like [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a perfect symbol of revolutionary hubris. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] returns to the theme of elite credulity repeatedly in the *[[nona|Nona]]* — the governing class's inability to see [[truth|reality]] clearly is one of the book's central preoccupations.
