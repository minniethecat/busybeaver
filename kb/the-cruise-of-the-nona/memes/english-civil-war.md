# English Civil War

## Overview

The English Civil Wars (1642–1651) appear in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* not as narrated history but as a ghostly presence on the water. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], sailing through [[solent|the Solent]], invokes the Civil War as part of the layered past that every sailor must see if he is truly to "possess" the piece of water he traverses. The war is a tragedy of kingship lost — Charles I imprisoned, his son's fleet impotently cruising offshore.

## Belloc's Account

The most sustained Civil War passage comes in Section 31, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reflects on [[solent|the Solent]]'s crowded history:

> You must see the last tragedy of the Civil Wars: the craft that might have taken Charles away from Southampton to freedom, and his young son cruising with the loyal fleet (which had declared for the King against the oligarchy) cruising just outside the Wight, but unable to save the King at Carisbrooke. (031-001)

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s language is carefully weighted. "The last tragedy of the Civil Wars" is not a battle but a near-miss — the boat that *might* have carried the King to freedom, the son who *might* have rescued him, the fleet that declared for the King but could not reach him. The tragedy lies in what did not happen.

Elsewhere, navigating the narrow passage under Hurst Castle on a stormy night, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] notes the spit of land "where Charles, the last true King of [[england|England]], lay a prisoner after having lost his power" (030-004). The epithet "the last true King of [[england|England]]" is characteristically [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]ian — a political judgment slipped into a sailing direction — and it signals where his sympathies lay.

## Historical Context

Charles I was held at Carisbrooke Castle on the [[wight|Isle of Wight]] from November 1647 to September 1648, following his surrender to the Scots and their delivery of him to [[parliament|Parliament]]. From Carisbrooke he negotiated secretly with the Scots, signed the "Engagement" promising Presbyterianism in [[england|England]], and thereby triggered the Second Civil War (1648). His execution followed in January 1649.

Hurst Castle, a Henrician fortress on a shingle spit in [[solent|the Solent]], held Charles I briefly in December 1648 after his transfer from Carisbrooke. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s description of the channel as "so narrow that even the *[[nona|Nona]]* could hardly beat through it" (030-004) underscores the isolation of the place.

The "[[youth|young]] son cruising with the loyal fleet" was Charles, Prince of [[wales|Wales]] (later Charles II), who in 1648 took command of the royalist fleet that had declared for the King and cruised off the Dutch coast and the [[wight|Isle of Wight]], unable to effect a rescue.

## Significance

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of the Civil War is significant for what it omits. He does not discuss causes, battles, or parliamentary politics. He presents the war as a tragedy of legitimate authority destroyed — "the last true King" — and as one layer in the deep historical palimpsest of [[solent|the Solent]]. The Civil War shares the water with Roman transports, Viking pirates, and modern [[the-sea|ocean]] liners, all visible to the sailor who "neglects the dimension of time" at his [[danger|peril]] (031-001).
