## Overview

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], wisdom is not abstract philosophy but the practical [[knowledge|understanding]] that comes from lived experience. He treats it as a rare quality that youth cannot possess and that only certain institutions—the [[the-sea|sea]], [[the-church|the Church]], deep tradition—can impart. Unlike knowledge, which can be acquired through study, wisdom in the *[[nona|Nona]]* is always embodied: it belongs to specific people ([[cardinal-manning|Cardinal Manning]]), specific forces (the [[the-sea|sea]]), and sometimes, with irony, to institutions (The [[time|Time]]s newspaper) [[010-012]][[014-022]].

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] draws a sharp contrast between wisdom and folly. Where folly de[[lighthouse|light]]s in nonsense and frivolity—he cites *[[alice-in-wonderland|Alice in Wonderland]]* as its literary monument—wisdom is grounded in the hard realities of human life. The [[the-sea|sea]] is his master teacher of this distinction [[012-005]][[014-022]].

## Belloc's Argument

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] develops his view of wisdom through four distinct contexts, each revealing a different facet of the concept. In his [[memory|recollection]] of [[cardinal-manning|Cardinal Manning]], wisdom is paired with sanctity as the deepest quality a person can possess—and one that youth, by its nature, cannot yet judge. He writes of "things so deep as sanctity and wisdom," acknowledging them as real even while confessing his [[youth|young]]er self was ill-equipped to measure them [[010-012]].

In a literary aside on *[[alice-in-wonderland|Alice in Wonderland]]*, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] draws a crucial distinction between two kinds of humour. There is "the humour which is founded upon folly"—the nonsense of Dodgson that de[[lighthouse|light]]ed the secure Victorian drawing-room—and "the wit that is founded upon wisdom." The former, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] predicts, will not survive the return of "terrors and heroisms"; the latter is built on something enduring [[012-005]].

The [[the-sea|sea]] provides [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most concentrated meditation on wisdom. "The [[the-sea|sea]], which teaches all wisdom," he writes, "certainly does not teach any man to despise human reason." What the [[the-sea|sea]] teaches is not [[doubt|scepticism]] but humility: the recognition that even sound reasoning from known premises can be overturned by "the irruption of other premises, unknown or not considered." In plain terms, "the [[the-sea|sea]] makes a man practical" [[014-022]].

Finally, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] deploys wisdom with ironic edge when describing the [[london|London]] press. He notes with dry amusement "the profound wisdom of The [[time|Time]]s" and "the piercing wit of the paper *[[punch|Punch]]*," recounting how William James praised these pillars of English establishment opinion. The irony is un[[fog|mist]]akable: the wisdom of [[the-press|newspapers]] is a received, comfortable, institutional sort—quite unlike the hard-won wisdom of the [[the-sea|sea]] [[040-016]].

## Key Passages

> The sea, which teaches all wisdom, certainly does not teach any man to despise human reason. I suppose there was never yet any Kantian fool or worser pragmatist who would not have been cured of his folly by half a week of moderate weather off the Onion. [[014-022]]

> ...the humour which is founded upon folly as contrasted with the wit that is founded upon wisdom. [[012-005]]

> I was, it may be said, too young to judge things so deep as sanctity and wisdom; but, on the other hand, youth has vision, especially upon elemental things. [[010-012]]
