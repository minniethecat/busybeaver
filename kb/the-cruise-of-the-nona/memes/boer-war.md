# Boer War

## Overview

The Second Boer War (1899–1902) appears in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s memoir not as a historical event but as a moral test. An unnamed English friend — "the only public man who talked conscientiously and rationally in this affair of the Boer War" (010-009) — predicted that the conflict would mark "the beginning of a decline in the financial credit of [[england|England]]."

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] uses the war to distinguish between two kinds of opposition: the principled critic who "did not confuse his conclusions with that disgusting antipathy to their own country which marked the most of those who p[[corruption|rot]]ested against the war" (010-009), and the anti-patriotic p[[corruption|rot]]ester who opposed the war out of contempt for [[england|England]] rather than love of truth.

## In the *Nona*

The Boer War passage in Section 10 is part of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s larger meditation on the difficulty of speaking truth to power. His unnamed friend — "he reasoned, he talked a sort of mathematical sense, and all his words were true; but he shall know no reward" (010-009) — is the archetype of the [[honour|honourable]] dissenter.
