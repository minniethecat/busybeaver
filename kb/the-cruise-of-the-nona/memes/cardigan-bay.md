Cardigan Bay is the great indentation of the [[wales|Welsh coast]] stretching from the Llŷn Peninsula in the north to Pembrokeshire in the south. For [[hilaire-belloc|Hilaire Belloc]], it was the long passage that followed his ordeal at [[bardsey-island|Bardsey]] — a seventy-mile run that tested the [[nona|Nona]] and her crew, and one of the defining open-water legs of the cruise recounted in [[the-cruise-of-the-nona|The Cruise of the Nona]].

## Strategic Position

Cardigan Bay occupies a strategic position in the geography of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s cruise. It is the great southward-opening bight that separates the rugged peninsulas of North [[wales|Wales]] — [[bardsey-island|Bardsey Island]] and the Llŷn — from the gentler coast of Pembrokeshire and, beyond it, [[bristol-channel|the Bristol Channel]].

For a small boat proceeding southward from [[holyhead|Holyhead]], the bay represented both opportunity and exposure. Its seventy-mile breadth offered a direct route south, avoiding the need to hug every indentation of the coast. But it also meant committing to open water for the better part of a day, with no harbour of refuge between [[bardsey-island|Bardsey]] and the St. David's Head peninsula.

## The Passage Across

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s crossing of Cardigan Bay came immediately after the dangerous passage of [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]]. He set out for "the passage across Cardigan Bay, a run of seventy-odd miles" [011-002] that would carry him from North [[wales|Wales]] toward the southern headlands and ultimately toward [[bideford-river|Bideford River]] in [[devon|Devon]].

The passage was long but relatively straightforward compared to the constricted [[tide|tidal]] passages that preceded it. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] described the moment of departure with matter-of-[[truth|fact]] navigational precision:

> We should be running into Cardigan Bay, past Bardsey, on the middle of the ebb [001-005].

This understated line, placed early in the book as a kind of预告, sets up the bay as the great open space that lay ahead — a seventy-mile expanse of water between the confined channels of North [[wales|Wales]] and the sheltered harbours of the south.

## Tidal Complexity

Like many of the waters [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] navigated, Cardigan Bay had its own complex [[tide|tidal]] patterns. He noted that the tide "ramified into branches, one branch coming up Cardigan Bay, which is called, I think, after some saint" [011-013].

The parenthetical "I think, after some saint" is characteristically [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]ian — precise about the physical phenomenon he was navigating, casually uncertain about the local nomenclature. For a sailor, the behaviour of the tide mattered more than the name on the chart.

## A Contemplative View

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most lyrical description of the bay comes not from the deck of [[nona|the Nona]] but from the shore. He recalled looking "over the northern corner of Cardigan Bay, seen from the silence and the height of the land" [005-006].

This rare moment of stillness — a view from land rather than [[the-sea|sea]], from height rather than waterline — reveals a different register in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s writing. From that vantage, the bay became not a navigational challenge but a landscape framed by the [[wales|Welsh mountains]] and the distant horizon. It is one of the few passages in the book where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] pauses to describe a place purely for its beauty, without the urgencies of tide and wind pressing upon the prose.
