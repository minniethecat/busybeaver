## Overview

The British Army appears in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s memoir primarily through the figure of
[[lord-haldane|Lord Haldane]], the Secretary of State for War whose reforms before 1914 created
the mobilisation framework that enabled British participation in the Continental
war. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of the Army is characteristic of his naval memoir: the
Fleet receives far more attention. Yet in Section 20, when [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] assesses who
"deserved well of their country" in the crisis of August 1914, the Army's
preparedness is central.

## Belloc's View

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] credits two men with preparing [[england|Britain]]'s armed forces for war: [[winston-churchill|Churchill]]
for [[the-fleet|the Navy]], and Haldane for the Army:

> One was Mr. Winston Churchill, who upon his own initiative mobilised the Fleet;
> and the other was Lord Haldane, who convinced his colleagues of the nature of
> the peril and who, it should be remembered, had made possible the cooperation
> of Britain in the war by his scheme for mobilisation. All that followed was
> based upon the work originally done by him at the War Office at a time when,
> to the great bulk of his countrymen, the idea of such a conflict was still
> quite unfamiliar and its enormous implications incredible. (020-014)

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s emphasis on Haldane's reforms — made before the war, when the idea of
Continental commitment seemed remote — reflects his respect for institutional
foresight. The War Office, under Haldane, transformed the British Army from a
colonial policing force into an expeditionary army capable of fighting alongside
the [[france|French]].

## In Belloc's World

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s relationship with the British Army was indirect. As a naval enthusiast
and maritime writer, his sympathies were with [[the-fleet|the Fleet]]. But as a military
historian who had written extensively on the Napoleonic campaigns, he understood
the importance of army reform. His praise for Haldane's work at [[great-war|the War]] Office is
notable because Haldane was a political figure whom [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] might otherwise have
opposed: a Liberal Imperialist. The credit [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] gives him is a mark of his
willingness to recognise institutional competence across party lines.
