## Overview

*Punch, or The London Charivari* was a British weekly magazine of humour and satire founded in 1841. For over a century it was a national institution, its cartoons and comic verses shaping the English sense of humour and its political commentary reflecting the prejudices of the educated middle class. By [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s time, *Punch* was as inseparable from the idea of Englishness as rainy Sundays and afternoon tea.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s single reference to *Punch* occurs in Section 40, embedded in a passage of layered irony. He is recalling a meeting at Rye with William James, the American philosopher and b[[corruption|rot]]her of Henry James. James, despite being a professional philosopher, chose to converse not about metaphysics but about the admirable qualities of [[england|England]]:

> "Also the piercing wit of the paper *Punch*, and the profound wisdom of *The Times*. I had just come back with a companion from foreign parts; I had been sleeping rough, and I was in no condition to take part in polite conversation, so I did all the listening, and, as I listened, I still considered how literary was Rye." (040-016)

The phrase "piercing wit" is, in context, gently mocking. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is reporting William James's opinions — the opinions of an intelligent foreigner charmed by English institutions — and the pairing of *Punch* with *[[the-times|The Times]]* as twin pillars of national greatness is too neat to be entirely serious. *Punch*'s humour was precisely the kind of safe, drawing-room comedy that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] associated with the sheltered [[england|England]] he both loved and mocked: the [[england|England]] of "the ease and unquestioned security" that he thought would not survive the return of "the terrors and the heroisms."

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own humour in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* is of a different kind — sharper, more polemical, rooted in the [[religion|Catholic]] and European tradition of satire rather than in the English tradition of amiable nonsense. His reference to *Punch* is thus a small cultural marker: this is what the English establishment finds witty; [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] himself aims at something older and less comfortable.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was himself a prolific humorist — his *Cautionary Tales for Children* and *The Bad Child's Book of Beasts* are classics of comic verse. But his humour always had an edge of moral seriousness that the genial satire of *Punch* lacked. The reference to *Punch* in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* is part of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s running commentary on the difference between the [[religion|Catholic]] and P[[corruption|rot]]estant cultures of Europe: the former capable of tragic wit, the latter content with safe pleasantry.
