# The Solent

## Overview

The Solent is the sheltered strait that separates the [[wight|Isle of Wight]] from the mainland of Hampshire. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], it is home — the waters of his own county, the "garden or back yard" of a lifetime's sailing. He enters it under dramatic circumstances, navigating the narrow passage under Hurst Castle on a stormy night, and once inside, the Solent becomes the stage for one of the book's most expansive meditations on history and the [[the-sea|sea]].

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] calls it "this three-spoked lake of the Solent, Spithead, and [[southampton|Southampton Water]]" [031-001]. It is a description that captures both geography and feeling: the Solent is not open [[the-sea|sea]] but an inland water, crowded with craft and with memory.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s entry into the Solent is one of the most technically vivid passages in the book. Running before a southerly wind on a blinding night off the Dorset coast, he makes for the North Channel under Hurst Castle — "a passage so narrow that even the *[[nona|Nona]]* could hardly beat through it" [030-004]. The directions are precise, the tension real: keep Hurst Light on the landward bow, pass it to starboard, skirt the land by half a cable, and then:

> "You will round the long spit of Hurst and find yourself inside the sheltered water of the Solent; all this if you have a leading wind" [030-004].

He succeeds. "In the Solent I carried the flood with me," he writes, "and, at the same time, the wind diminished and all rain ceased" [030-012]. Day breaks over the hills of his own county, and the riding [[lighthouse|light]]s of anchored yachts "swung and twinkled like a dance of stars" [030-012].

Once inside, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s gaze expands from the practical to the historical. In the book's most sustained meditation on a single piece of water, he argues that you cannot truly possess the Solent "unless you see moving upon it the fullness of its past" [031-001]. He summons the ghosts: the last tragedy of the [[english-civil-war|Civil Wars]], the great ships under sail making through Spithead, the pirate boats stranded on the Bramble, Alfred's men capturing Vikings, and the Roman fleet coming in by an August night.

> "Because of its size and of its security, because of its nearness to Europe, because of its many harbours, and one great harbour, this patch of water has been packed with history as no other in Britain, except London River" [031-001].

## Geography & History

The Solent is a strait approximately twenty miles long and between one and four miles wide, separating the [[wight|Isle of Wight]] from Hampshire. Its western entrance is guarded by the Needles, its eastern by Spithead. It has been a major shipping lane since prehistory, and its sheltered waters made it the natural centre of British yachting from the Victorian era onward. The Solent's double tide — water entering from both ends — is a hydrographic curiosity that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] discusses at length.
