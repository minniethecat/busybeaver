The [[alderney|Alderney Race]] (or the Race of Alderney) is a notorious [[tide|tidal]] passage in the [[english-channel|English Channel]], lying between the island of [[alderney|Alderney]] and the Cotentin Peninsula of France. It forms one of the three "gateposts" that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] identified as defining the boundary between the narrow [[the-sea|sea]] and the open Atlantic.

## Belloc's Knowledge of the Race

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] claimed direct experience of the [[alderney|Alderney Race]], though he admitted the memory was distant. "I have not been through [[alderney|Alderney Race]] since the nineties," he w[[corruption|rot]]e, "but I remember it clearly enough" [028-011]. He first mentioned it early in the book as a point of comparison for extreme [[the-sea|sea]] conditions. Describing the chaos off [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]], he reached for the only benchmark adequate to the experience:

> In a time much shorter than I had expected, with no lull in between, the steady run of sea which had been combing behind us turned into a confusion of huge tumbling pyramidical waves, leaping up, twisting, turning, and boiling in such a confusion as I had never seen, not even in Alderney Race, which I had gone through many years before when I was a boy [001-019].

## Among the Great Races

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] grouped the [[alderney|Alderney Race]] with the other major [[tide|tidal]] passages of his sailing world. "Many races do I know," he w[[corruption|rot]]e, "[[portland-race|Portland Race]] and [[alderney|Alderney Race]] and Little Saint Alban's and [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]]" [011-020]. This catalogue of dangerous waters served to establish his authority as a navigator who had tested himself against the worst conditions [[english-channel|the Channel]] could produce.

He returned to the theme in a later reflection on the varieties of maritime danger:

> I have not been through Alderney Race since the nineties, but I suppose it is still going strong. The Wild Goose Race you have already heard of — a very considerable thing. The Skerries also — I mean the one off Anglesey — is worthy to be saluted. And even little Saint Alban's, and Bardsey Sound [028-032].

## Geographical Context

Together with [[portland-bill|Portland Bill]] on the English side and the Hogue on the [[france|French]], [[alderney|Alderney]] forms the southern gatepost of the narrow [[the-sea|sea]]. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] described the arrangement precisely: "[[portland-bill|Portland Bill]] stands right out into [[english-channel|the Channel]] and challenges the Atlantic tide. It is a gatepost, with [[alderney|Alderney]] and the Hogue for gateposts on the other side" [028-004]. The [[alderney|Alderney Race]] is the hydraulic consequence of this constriction — the tide forced between island and mainland at speeds that can exceed ten knots.
