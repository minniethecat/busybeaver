[[portland-bill|Saint Alban's Race]] (often called "Little Saint Alban's" by [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]) is a [[tide|tidal]] race off Saint Alban's Head on the Dorset coast, between [[portland-bill|Portland Bill]] and [[anvil-point|Anvil Point]]. Though small compared to the great races of [[english-channel|the Channel]], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] devoted an unusual amount of attention to it — treating it as a familiar but irritating acquaintance.

## A Minor but Memorable Race

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was careful to calibrate the reader's expectations. "Even little Saint Alban's," he w[[corruption|rot]]e, "though it is a toy compared with the others I have spoken of... is still going strong" [028-011]. The race was not in the same league as [[portland-race|Portland]] or [[alderney-race|Alderney]], but it demanded respect.

He catalogued it among the races he knew personally: "Many races do I know, [[portland-bill|Portland Race]] and [[alderney|Alderney Race]] and Little Saint Alban's Race, of which you shall later hear" [011-020]. The promise to return to the subject was characteristically fulfilled in a later chapter.

## The Particular Annoyance

What distinguished [[portland-bill|Saint Alban's Race]] in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s mind was not its danger but its capacity to frustrate. "There is one thing about it which is a nuisance," he w[[corruption|rot]]e, "and that is [[portland-bill|Saint Alban's Race]]" [028-017]. The problem was that after the exertion of rounding [[portland-bill|Portland Bill]], a sailor expected relief — but instead found another obstacle: "When you have just beaten [[isle-of-portland|Portland]] in a blow... you are in no mood for Saint Alban's" [028-017].

The worst scenario was "half a night passing Saint Alban's with a leading wind — [[lighthouse|light]], but precisely in the wrong direction" [028-018], leaving a sailor becalmed within sight of the race's disturbed water.

## Belloc's Passage

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] did pass through the race at least once, memorialising the achievement in verse:

> "I made my passage through Saint Alban's Race
> With a leading wind and a bleeding face" [028-022]

The comic couplet, with its mock-heroic tone, captures the blend of genuine difficulty and self-deprecating humour that characterises [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of his own [[the-sea|sea]]manship.
