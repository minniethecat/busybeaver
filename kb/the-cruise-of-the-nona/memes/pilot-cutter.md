# Pilot Cutter

## Overview

The pilot cutter is, in a sense, the book's true hero. The [[nona|Nona]] herself is "cutter rigged" (001-009) — a detail [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] drops casually, as though any reader would know what that meant — and the cutter's qualities are the qualities [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] values most: simplicity, [[the-sea|sea]]worthiness, responsiveness to a single sailor's hand. The cutter is not the largest or the fastest or the most comfortable of vessels, but it is, for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the best — the boat that can go anywhere a skilled sailor dares to take it.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] mentions the cutter only once by name, but its presence suffuses the book. Every description of sailing — the helm answering to a touch, the boat working to windward, the careful negotiation of harbours and [[tide|tides]] — is a description of cutter sailing. The cutter is the medium through which [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] experiences the [[the-sea|sea]], and therefore the medium through which he experiences everything the [[the-sea|sea]] teaches him.

## The Cutter Rig

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s description of [[nona|the Nona]]'s specification is brief but precise: "from five to six feet of water, cutter rigged" (001-009). A cutter carries a single mast with a mainsail and two headsails — a rig that balances power and manoeuvrability, allowing a small crew (or a single sailor) to handle the boat in most conditions. It is the traditional rig of English pilot boats — the vessels that put harbour [[pilot|pilots]] aboard incoming ships — and its association with professional [[the-sea|sea]]manship is part of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s point.

The cutter rig is also beautiful. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not say this directly — he is too wary of aestheticism — but it is implicit in everything he writes about [[nona|the Nona]]'s motion through the water. A cutter "sailed well and was easily handled" (001-009): the conjunction of "well" and "easily" is the essence of the rig's appeal. It does what it is asked to do, without fuss, and the result is a harmony between sailor and boat that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] clearly finds deeply satisfying.

## The Cutter and Its Alternatives

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] contrasts the cutter implicitly with every other kind of vessel in the book. The [[channel-steamer|steamer]] is large, dirty, and anonymous — the cutter is small, clean-lined, and personal. The [[london-barge|London barge]] is flat-bottomed and sluggish — the cutter is deep-keeled and responsive. Even the [[fishing-boats|fishing boats]] [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] admires lack the cutter's versatility: they are designed for a single task, while the cutter can do anything the [[the-sea|sea]] demands.

This is not mere boat-snobbery. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s preference for the cutter is an expression of his larger philosophy: that the best way to do anything is in a vessel suited to a single person's capacities, dependent on his own energy and skill. The cutter is the poor man's boat — the boat of discomfort, dread, strong strain, "a life all moving" (P04-023) — and [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] would not have it any other way.

## The Cutter as Philosophy

In the Dedication, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] makes the philosophical stakes of his vessel choice explicit. The man who sails a small boat "dependent upon his own energy and skill, never achieving anything with that energy and skill save the perpetual repetition of calm and storm, danger undesired and somehow overcome" (P04-023) — that man "will be a poor man, and his voyage will be the parallel of the life of a poor man."

The cutter, in this account, is not merely a boat but a way of being in the world. It requires constant attention, rewards skill, punishes carelessness, and never arrives at a final destination. It is, in short, like life itself — "a string of happenings and thinkings, disconnected and without shape, meaningless, and yet full" (P04). [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not argue for the superiority of the cutter rig over other rigs; he argues for the superiority of a life lived at the scale of a single human being, in a vessel that can be handled by a single human hand.
