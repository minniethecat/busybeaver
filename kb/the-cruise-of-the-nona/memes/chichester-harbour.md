Chichester Harbour is a large natural harbour in West [[sussex|Sussex]] whose entry, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] observes, is controlled entirely by the tide. It encompasses Bosham, the ancient hamlet with a central place in the story of early [[england|England]]. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] mentions it in three separate contexts: as a site of Saxon justice, as an example of [[sussex|Sussex]]'s [[tide|tidal]]ly-restricted harbours, and as a familiar landmark on [[english-channel|the Channel]] passage he had known since boyhood.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] first mentions Chichester Harbour in a passage imagining the deep history of [[solent|the Solent]] waters, conjuring the Viking age:

> You must see the pirate boats of a thousand years ago stranded on the Bramble, because they did not know the water, and Alfred's men capturing them so, and taking them off to Chichester to be hanged: a proper end for all Vikings [031-021].

In Section 32, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] groups Chichester Harbour with other [[sussex|Sussex]] harbours that can only be used "by the favour of the tide," noting that all the [[sussex|Sussex]] harbours — "Chichester Harbour (which is also Bosham), Littlehampton, Shoreham, Newhaven, Folkestone" — are not to be approached at low tide "save for the work of man" [032-001]. This [[tide|tidal]] limitation forms part of his larger meditation on the insufficient opportunity granted to [[sussex|Sussex]] by its Creator: "opportunity enough to compel the use of a function, not enough opportunity for the fullness of that use" [032-001].

Returning to the harbour in Section 37, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] describes it as a familiar sight on [[english-channel|the Channel]] run he had sailed for nearly forty [[time|years]]:

> There is the same odd entry, somewhat shoal and difficult, to the branches of Chichester Harbour and Bosham, that famous hamlet in the story of England [037-005].

The harbour thus appears in three distinct temporal registers: the deep Saxon past, the geological present of tide and silt, and the personal memory of a lifetime's sailing.
