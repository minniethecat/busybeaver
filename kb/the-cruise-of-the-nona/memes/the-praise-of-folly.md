## Overview

*The Praise of Folly* (*Moriae Encomium*, 1511) is the most famous work of the Dutch humanist Desiderius Erasmus. Written during a stay with his friend Thomas More (whose name, *Morus*, puns on the Greek word for "fool"), the essay personifies Folly as a goddess who praises herself before an audience. Through her monologue, Erasmus satirises the follies of scholars, churchmen, princes, and common folk alike — a critique so sharp that it helped prepare the ground for the Reformation, though Erasmus himself remained a [[religion|Catholic]] reformer rather than a schismatic.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] mentions *The Praise of Folly* in the course of a meditation on fools and the irritation they provoke. After recounting a near-disastrous encounter with an incompetent [[wales|Welsh]] pilot who grounded the *[[nona|Nona]]* on the sands of Port Madoc, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] broadens his complaint into literary history:

> "It is high time that a new book were written on Fools, like those of the sixteenth century, which are so excellent, and which you have in Latin, in German, in French, and in English: *The Praise of Folly*, *The Ship of Fools*, *The Fool's Paradise*, and others." [006-003]

He goes on to sketch, in mock-serious tones, his own unwritten contribution to the genre: *The Fool, Sociological, Biological, and Pathological, with Many Portraits and an Appendix on the Newly Discovered Giant Fool of Aphasia* — and then bequeaths the idea to "[[youth|young]]er and wealthier men," just as he once bequeathed the idea of a *Cads' Encyclopaedia*.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s invocation of Erasmus is more than ornamental. The *Praise of Folly* tradition — learned satire that uses the figure of the fool to expose the pretensions of the wise — is one that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] himself practised throughout his career. His own *[[the-ship-of-fools|The Ship of Fools]]* (referenced in the same paragraph) and his many satirical essays belong to the same lineage. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] admired the sixteenth century as an age when strong doctrine and strong satire could coexist; his lament that "it is high time that a new book were written on Fools" is a lament for the loss of that synthesis.
