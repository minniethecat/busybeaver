The Wild Goose Race is a [[tide|tidal]] passage off the southern coast of [[wales|Wales]], somewhere in the tangled waters of Pembrokeshire. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] ranked it as the supreme danger of that coast and described it in terms of almost mythical terror — though he confessed he had only heard of it, never experienced it directly. Among all the [[tide|tidal]] [[danger|hazard]]s that populate the British and Irish coasts, the Wild Goose Race held a singular place in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s imagination, precisely because it remained something he knew only by report rather than by encounter.

## Worst of All

In cataloguing the [[danger|peril]]s of the [[wales|Welsh coast]], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reached a climax with the Wild Goose Race. He listed "Jack Sound and [[ramsey-sound|Ramsey]] Sound and the Bishop and his clerks, and, worst of all, Wild Goose Race" [011-018] — the emphatic "worst of all" setting it apart from even the formidable passages he had navigated personally.

He repeated the superlative in the same paragraph: "the tangle of the southern corner of [[wales|Wales]], Jack Sound and [[ramsey-sound|Ramsey]] Sound and the Bishop and his clerks, and, worst of all, Wild Goose Race" [011-018]. The repetition suggests that the Race had achieved, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s imagination, the status of a legendary [[danger|peril]]. He had earlier built toward this climax, describing the approach to the southern [[wales|Welsh]] corner as a progression through mounting danger — each passage worse than the last — until he reached "and, most terrible of all, Wild Goose Race" [011-017].

## Secondhand Knowledge

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was characteristically honest about the limits of his experience. "I write without knowledge of the Wild Goose Race," he admitted. "I have never been through it" [011-019]. This candour sets the Wild Goose Race apart from the other races he describes — [[portland-race|Portland]] he had felt; [[alderney-race|Alderney]] he remembered from the nineties; but the Wild Goose he only knew by report.

What he had heard was terrifying enough: "It seems that in Wild Goose Race a boat is taken up and pitched end over end like an empty barrel" [011-020]. The image of a boat tumbling helplessly was sufficiently vivid that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] felt no need to verify it personally. The simile — a vessel reduced to the weightlessness and passivity of an empty cask — is among the most violent images in all of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s writing about the [[the-sea|sea]], and it gains force precisely from being secondhand: the report of something so terrible that the teller's imagination has already done the work of terror.

## Later Echoes

The Wild Goose Race lingered in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s mind even as he discussed other passages. Referring to it again later, he noted "The Wild Goose Race you have already heard of — a very wicked place" [028-011]. The offhand epithet — "a very wicked place" — carries the same blend of dread and respect that marks all his writing about dangerous waters. By this point in the book, the reader has indeed encountered the Race in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s earlier catalogue of [[wales|Welsh]] [[danger|peril]]s; the casual reference assumes that the name alone now carries sufficient weight.

## In the Catalogue of Tidal Races

The Wild Goose Race belonged to a class of navigational [[danger|hazard]]s that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] treated with particular attention throughout *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*. Tidal races — places where currents converge or funnel through constrictions to create violent, chaotic [[the-sea|seas]] — appear repeatedly in his cruising narrative. He had felt the [[portland-race|Portland Race]] off the Dorset coast, remembered the [[alderney-race|Alderney Race]] from his Channel crossings, and navigated the narrower but still formidable [[ramsey-sound|Ramsey Sound]] and Jack Sound in [[wales|Wales]]. Each had its own character; the Wild Goose alone remained a blank on his personal chart, filled only by report.
