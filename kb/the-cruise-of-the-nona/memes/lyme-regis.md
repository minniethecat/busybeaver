Lyme Regis is an ancient harbour-town on the Dorset coast, between [[devon|Torbay]] to the west and the [[isle-of-portland|Isle of Portland]] to the east. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] used it as a regular port of call when sailing the [[nona|Nona]] along the south coast of [[england|England]]. Its small harbour, sheltered by the famous Cobb breakwater, had been a port of entry since the Middle Ages — a [[truth|fact]] that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] found quietly remarkable given its modest scale.

## A Regular Port of Call

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s connection to Lyme Regis was a matter of repeated visits. "My port, since I have been cruising on this coast, and often in [[nona|the Nona]], is Lyme Regis" [025-002], he w[[corruption|rot]]e. When sailing from the west, his standard route was straightforward: "When I leave Torbay, I run to Lyme Regis" [023-002].

This direct run from Torbay to Lyme Regis — cutting across the broad bight of Lyme Bay — was a passage [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] made many times. It lay on the coastal route toward [[portland-bill|Portland Bill]], the next major landmark eastward, and served as a natural stopping-point on the long passage from [[devon|Devon]] into the central Channel. For a small-boat sailor in [[nona|the Nona]], knowing every possible harbour of refuge was not a matter of convenience but of safety, and Lyme Regis was among the most reliable.

## The Mystery of Origins

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was fascinated by the question of why a harbour existed at Lyme Regis at all. "What produced Lyme Regis? How came there to be a harbour at all in such a place?" [025-003] he asked. The harbour, he conceded, was "but a small harbour" [025-003], yet it had functioned as "a port of entry all through the Middle Ages" [025-006].

He considered and rejected the explanation of a Roman or British road terminus: "There was something of a Roman road, or perhaps a British road, that came down to Lyme Regis, but that could not have produced a harbour" [025-005]. The road, in his view, was a consequence of the harbour, not its cause. The question engaged his deeper interest in how maritime settlements originate — a theme that runs through his reflections on [[bridport|Bridport]], [[dover|Dover]], and other ancient Channel ports.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] closed his meditation on the town with characteristic understatement. Having wrestled with the mystery of its origins and the persistence of its modest harbour through centuries of change, he concluded simply: "And so much for Lyme Regis" [025-008] — a half-dismissive, half-affectionate farewell that suggested he knew the town would outlast any theory he might propose about it.

## The Cobb

Though [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not name it directly in the passages he cites, the most distinctive feature of Lyme Regis harbour is the Cobb — a massive curved stone breakwater that has sheltered vessels since at least the fourteenth century. The harbour [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] entered in [[nona|the Nona]] was the same ancient refuge that had served medieval merchants, Elizabethan privateers, and eighteenth-century smugglers. Its survival into the twentieth century as a working harbour, when so many other small ports had silted up or been abandoned, was precisely the kind of historical continuity that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] valued.

## On the Coastal Route

Lyme Regis occupied a strategic position on [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s Channel cruising grounds. Lying roughly midway between the [[devon|Devon]] fishing ports and the Dorset headlands, it offered shelter in a stretch of coast that was otherwise exposed to the prevailing southwesterlies. The persistence of the port — small but enduring — exemplified the pattern of English maritime settlement that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] traced along the whole south coast: ancient harbours whose origins were often obscure but whose utility had never quite been exhausted.
