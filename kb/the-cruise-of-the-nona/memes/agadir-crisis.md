# Agadir Crisis

The Agadir Crisis of 1911 — a diplomatic confrontation between Germany, France, and [[england|Britain]] over [[france|French]] expansion in Morocco — appears in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* as a silent hinge in the chain of events leading to the Great War. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not narrate the crisis itself but invokes it as the moment after which British foreign policy acquired an irreversible momentum toward alliance with France.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s sole reference to the Agadir Crisis occurs in Section 20, embedded in his extended meditation on [[england|Britain]]'s decision to enter the Great War. Reviewing the arguments for and against British neutrality in August 1914, he notes that a web of informal commitments — military planning, diplomatic coordination, shared assumptions — had bound [[england|Britain]] to France despite the absence of any formal treaty:

> The general policy of the preceding years, especially after Agadir, had pointed to the same end; but the Government of this country had always, in the most insistent manner, affirmed and provided for its freedom of choice when the crisis should come. [020-005]

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s phrasing is precise. He does not claim that Agadir itself created an obligation. Rather, he identifies it as a watershed — the point after which British policy, whatever its public professions of freedom, had in practice aligned with France. The contradiction between stated freedom and actual commitment is the central irony of his analysis.

## Wider Context

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] visited [[france|French]] Morocco himself, and Section 13 of the *[[nona|Nona]]* contains a sustained portrait of [[hubert-lyautey|Marshal Lyautey]]'s colonial administration. His admiration for the [[france|French]] achievement in Morocco — "[[hubert-lyautey|Lyautey]]'s Morocco is an astonishing affair" — provides the background against which Agadir's significance can be understood. The crisis was fought over precisely the territory [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] later found so impressive: a [[france|French]] p[[corruption|rot]]ectorate built on the ruins of German diplomatic ambition.

In Section 20, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] traces the chain from Agadir through the 1911 German war tax and the [[france|French]] reintroduction of three-year military service to the final rupture of July 1914. The crisis appears in his narrative not as a discrete event but as the first in a sequence of escalations that made the Great War, if not inevitable, then increasingly difficult to avoid.

## Significance

The Agadir Crisis reference exemplifies [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s method in the *[[nona|Nona]]*: historical events enter the text not as subjects of narration but as nodes in an argument. The crisis matters to [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] because it illuminates the structure of Great Power decision-making — the way informal alignments acquire the force of treaties, and governments maintain the fiction of choice long after the choice has been made. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], writing in the mid-1920s, this was not academic history but the still-living prehistory of the catastrophe that had engulfed his world.
