# Battle of Hastings

## Overview

The Battle of Hastings is [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most frequently invoked historical event — appearing in twelve separate passages across the book, more than any other single occurrence. It functions at multiple levels: as a landmark in English history, as a feature of the coastal geography [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sails past, and — most distinctively — as a case study in the academic revisionism he so despises. "That the Battle of Hastings was not called the Battle of Hastings — although all the people who fought there called it the Battle of Hastings" (011-056): the absurdity of the claim is [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s entire argument.

But Hastings is more than a polemical instrument. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the battle is a living presence along the [[sussex|Sussex coast]]. He sails past the town repeatedly, notes the "odd broken breakwater" (042-002), and reflects on what the Norman Conquest meant for [[england|England]]. The battle that changed English history is also, for him, a feature of the familiar landscape — as concrete and as durable as the cliffs it was fought beneath.

## The Battle That Remade England

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] treats the Norman Conquest — of which Hastings was the decisive engagement — as the founding event of the [[england]] he knows. It brought [[england|England]] into the mainstream of European civilisation, replacing a peripheral Saxon kingdom with a Norman polity governed by [[france|French]]-speaking aristocrats and Latin-writing clerics. The [[battle-of-hastings|Battle of Hastings]] was the hinge on which this transformation turned.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not narrate the battle in detail — that is not his method. He assumes his reader knows what happened in 1066. What interests him is the battle's afterlife: how it is remembered, how it is disputed, and how its physical traces persist in the landscape. The breakwater at Hastings, "worn down under the influence of the [[the-sea|sea]]" (027-022), becomes an emblem of time's effect on all human works — even the mightiest.

## "Senlac" and the Debunkers

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most distinctive treatment of Hastings concerns its name. He reports with contempt that "the Battle of Hastings was not called the Battle of Hastings" (011-056) by certain scholars, who prefer "Senlac" — a term [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] treats as a pedantic invention. His objection is characteristically practical: "all the people who fought there called it the Battle of Hastings" (011-056). To rename it centuries later is not scholarship but arrogance.

The Senlac controversy appears again later in the book, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes of "the charlatanry of 'Senlac'" (043-001). The word "charlatanry" is carefully chosen: it suggests not mere error but fraud — the pretence of superior knowledge where no such knowledge exists. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the scholars who insist on "Senlac" are not correcting a [[fog|mist]]ake; they are inventing one, for the sake of seeming more learned than the common run of humanity.

## The Harbour That Disappeared

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most extended treatment of Hastings concerns not the battle but the harbour. "That harbour of Hastings has today quite disappeared" (042-003), he writes — a sober observation that opens into a meditation on geological time and human impermanence. The harbour that sheltered William the Conqueror's fleet has been "worn down by the [[the-sea|sea]]" (032-003), and "the breakwater at Hastings has failed" (039-007).

This is [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] at his most characteristic: a historical event becomes a geographical meditation, which becomes a philosophical reflection on time, [[corruption|decay]], and the limits of human effort. The battle that changed [[england|England]] was fought beside a harbour that no longer exists. What endures is not stone or earth but memory — and even memory, as the Senlac controversy shows, is perpetually contested.

## Key Passages

> That the Battle of Hastings was not called the Battle of Hastings — although all the people who fought there called it the Battle of Hastings? (011-056)

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s rhetorical trump card: the testimony of the participants against the theories of the scholars.

> That harbour of Hastings has today quite disappeared. The breakwater at Hastings has failed, but Fairlight gives a little shelter still. (042-003; 039-007)

The physical traces of the battle have been erased by the [[the-sea|sea]] — only memory remains.

> I hope that Hastings has reasserted its right to call its own battle by its own name. (042-007)

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s [[religion|faith]] that common sense will eventually prevail over pedantry.
