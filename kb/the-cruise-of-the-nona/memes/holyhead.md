# Holyhead

## Overview

Holyhead, on Holy Island off the western coast of Anglesey, is the port where *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* begins. The book's full subtitle — "The Story of a Cruise from Holyhead to the Wash, with Reflections and Judgments on Life and Letters, Men and Manners" [P05-002] — fixes Holyhead as the western terminus of a voyage whose arc spans the whole of the [[wales|Welsh]] and English coasts. It is the point of departure, the threshold between land and the [[the-sea|sea]]-road.

Holyhead is a working ferry port, not a place of romance. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] treats it practically: a harbour to slip out of at night, a name to compare with others in a discussion of artificial harbours. But as the starting point of the entire cruise, Holyhead holds a quiet structural importance in the book.

## In the *Nona*

The opening of the cruise is understated but evocative:

> "It was late in May, near midnight, the air being very warm and still, and the sky not covered but somewhat dim, with no moon, when I took the *Nona* out from Holyhead Harbour" [001-001].

The wind is so s[[lighthouse|light]] that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] must row the dinghy to pull the *[[nona|Nona]]* past the breakwater. They drift for an hour under the [[lighthouse|light]]house glare before a faint breeze stirs from over the Anglesey flats. The departure from Holyhead is not dramatic — it is quiet, warm, still — but it marks the beginning of everything that follows.

Holyhead reappears much later in the book, in a discussion of artificial harbours. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is praising [[portland-bill|Portland]] Harbour as one of the "very, very rare successes of man in his coaxing of the [[the-sea|sea]]" [027-002], and runs through the failures and partial successes for comparison. "Fishguard and Holyhead are open to certain winds" [027-002], he notes — functional but imperfect, not fully sheltered. It is the verdict of a sailor who has known the harbour and departed from it at midnight.

## Geography & History

Holyhead ([[wales|Welsh]]: *Caergybi*) is the largest town on Holy Island, Anglesey, and the principal ferry port between [[england|Britain]] and [[ireland|Ireland]]. Its harbour was improved in the nineteenth century with a substantial breakwater — one of the longest in [[england|Britain]] — to shelter the Irish mail packets. The port's exposure to certain winds, noted critically by [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], reflects the limitations of even major harbour engineering in an exposed position on the Irish Sea.
