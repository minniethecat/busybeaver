## Overview

The New Testament is the second major division of the Christian Bible, containing the four Gospels, the Acts of the Apostles, the Epistles, and the Book of Revelation. For Hilaire [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a devout [[religion|Catholic]], the New Testament was not merely a cultural arti[[truth|fact]] but divinely inspired scripture — a position that placed him at odds with the modernist biblical scholarship of his day.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not mention the "New Testament" by name in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, but [[the-bible|the Gospel]]s — particularly [[the-bible|the Gospel]] of Saint John — appear repeatedly in his arguments about scholarship, tradition, and the nature of evidence. In one key passage, he critiques the academic fashion for denying traditional authorship:

> "It is interesting to analyse the motives of this sort of thing. ... Whence springs this lust for saying that the Gospel of Saint John was not written by Saint John? that Homer was not written by Homer?" [039-013]

In a later chapter, he returns to the theme with greater force, defending the [[gospels|Fourth Gospel]]'s apostolic authorship against the sceptics:

> "A man who denies its apostolic authority, and who comes forward with a disconnected mass of guesswork, scrappy particularisms, and odd conjectures, should be regarded as the incongruous disturber of a judgment upon which millions of men through centuries had preserved a firm conclusion." [043-002]

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the New Testament is the supreme test case for his argument about tradition versus academic fashion. The sceptical scholarship that denied Saint John's authorship was, in his view, not [[knowledge|science]] but a prejudice — a preference for novelty over the accumulated wisdom of [[the-church|the Church]].

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s defence of traditional New Testament authorship was not an isolated opinion but part of his broader [[religion|Catholic]] apologetics. He believed that the modernist tendency to dissolve every traditional attribution into "committees" and "schools" and "later redactors" was a symptom of a deeper loss of [[religion|faith]] in the possibility of divine action in history. His arguments on this point appear in many of his historical and controversial works, including *Europe and [[the-church|the Faith]]* and *The Great Heresies*.
