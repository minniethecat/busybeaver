## Overview

*The Times* is the long-established [[london|London]] daily newspaper, founded in 1785. Throughout the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries it enjoyed a reputation as the newspaper of record — the voice of the British establishment, widely regarded (by itself and its readers) as a repository of profound wisdom on public affairs.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] mentions *The Times* twice in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, and on both occasions his tone is irreverent. He does not treat the newspaper as an authority but as a phenomenon to be observed — part of the furniture of English public life, and not always a reliable part.

The first reference is brief but pointed, in a passage discussing the reporting of [[alfred-dreyfus|the Dreyfus affair]]:

> "I defy anyone to make out from *The Times* what is really going on at the moment in any foreign country." (039-015)

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], who prided himself on his knowledge of European affairs and his [[france|French]] connections, *The Times* represented the insularity of English journalism — its inability to convey the texture of life beyond [[english-channel|the Channel]].

The second reference is more elaborately ironic. In Section 40, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] recalls meeting the philosopher William James at Rye. James, the great American pragmatist, was visiting his b[[corruption|rot]]her Henry James, and his conversation turned not to philosophy but to admiration for English institutions:

> "Here also did I meet his brother, William James, whose business in life it was to write about philosophy, but whose conversation when I met him turned principally upon the subtle diplomatic genius of the King of England then reigning; upon the stability of English institutions; the excellence of the English police, and the singular faculty shown by our institutions of permitting differences without conflict. Also the piercing wit of the paper *Punch*, and the profound wisdom of *The Times*." (040-016)

The pairing of "*[[punch|Punch]]*" and "*The Times*" — the comic weekly and the serious daily — and the attribution of "profound wisdom" to the latter, is characteristic [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] irony. He is reporting William James's opinions, not his own, but the list reads as a gentle satire of the things foreigners admire about [[england|England]]: the King, the police, the capacity for compromise, *[[punch|Punch]]*, *The Times*. It is a portrait of complacent English self-regard as seen through American eyes.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s references to *The Times* are part of his broader [[doubt|scepticism]] about institutional authority. Just as he [[fog|mist]]rusted academic credentials and scientific pretension, he [[fog|mist]]rusted the newspaper that spoke for the governing class. *The Times* might be "profoundly wise" by the standards of those who ran [[england|England]] — but [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a [[religion|Catholic]] outsider with a [[france|French]] education and a sailor's indifference to metropolitan opinion, was not among them.
