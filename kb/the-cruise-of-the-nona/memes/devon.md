# Devon

## Overview

Devon is the great southwestern county of [[england|England]], bounded by [[bristol-channel|the Bristol Channel]] to the north and the English Channel to the south. Its coastline — rugged cliffs, secret river mouths, and the deep-water harbour of [[plymouth|Plymouth Sound]] — forms a substantial portion of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s cruising ground. The *[[nona|Nona]]*'s voyage begins in Devon: [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sets out from [[bideford-river|Bideford]] River, on the county's northern coast, and the county accompanies him throughout the cruise as a point of departure and return.

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Devon is not merely geography but character. The "Devon [[the-sea|sea]]" has a quality all its own — a chill northern day[[lighthouse|light]] that drives out the warm [[memory|memories]] of Africa. The "Devon air" is something he invokes with almost sacramental feeling, a homecoming after the strangeness of foreign waters.

## In the *Nona*

Devon provides the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s point of origin. "So I set out at once from Devon with one companion," [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes, "and, finding all on board as I had left it, I set sail to leave [[bideford-river|Bideford]] River" [013-001]. It is a characteristically understated beginning — no fanfare, no grand declaration, simply a man and his boat setting out from a Devon harbour. His stated purpose is "to explore the outer [[the-sea|sea]] beyond [[hartland-point|Hartland]], which till then I had not seen" [013-001], though the cruise would carry him far beyond that modest ambition.

Devon's coastline appears throughout the narrative as a reference point. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sails "with our nose for the Atlantic headlands of the south, and for the corners of [[wales|Wales]], where they face the Cornish and the Devon [[the-sea|seas]] and [[ireland|Ireland]] also, beyond, with the great [[the-sea|ocean]] rolling in between" [008-001]. The Devon [[the-sea|seas]] face westward — toward [[cornwall|Cornwall]], toward [[ireland|Ireland]], toward the Atlantic — and this orientation shapes the cruise's geography.

The county's southern coast receives some of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s warmest descriptive prose. "It is a heavenly piece of coast, all this southern bulge of Devon, with its little secret rivers and untainted towns. The people are good and the land" [019-007]. Yet even this praise is qualified: "So, with reservations, is its [[the-sea|sea]] even beyond the Sound." The reservation is earned by experience — off Bolt Head, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] suffered "one of the two most abominable spills of wind I ever suffered" [019-007], a gale so violent it earned comparison with the storm off [[beachy-head|Beachy Head]].

The Devon coast also serves as the cruise's homing signal. In the closing stages, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] and his companion "ran along the coast to Devon for the Start" [019-024] — the familiar headland marking the approach to home waters. And when caught in [[danger|peril]]ous conditions, the natural instinct is to "scud down along the coast towards Devon, with enough [[the-sea|sea]] room to spare" [026-007]. Devon is safety, familiarity, the end of voyaging.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s philosophical musings return repeatedly to the Devon and [[cornwall|Cornwall]] peninsula. He puzzles over "why the entry into it, up to quite late in history, seems to have been from the north" [013-029] — a historical-geographical observation that opens into reflections on King Arthur, the Dark Ages, and the orientation of ancient Dumnonia toward the Celtic [[the-sea|seas]] rather than the Roman south.

The most personal Devon passage comes in a night meditation from the cabin of the *[[nona|Nona]]*: "the scent of the [[the-sea|sea]], and the Devon air. To the devil with all reasonings and counter-reasonings and abstractions and argufying: I am for sleep" [014-048]. After pages of navigation, history, and polemic, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] surrenders to the simple pleasure of a Devon night at anchor.

## Geography & History

Devon's coastline is among the most varied in [[england|Britain]]: the rugged cliffs of [[hartland-point|Hartland]] and the north coast facing the Atlantic, the softer southern shores of the English Channel, and the deep ria of [[plymouth|Plymouth Sound]] — one of the finest natural harbours in Europe. Historically, Devon was part of the Brythonic kingdom of Dumnonia, which also included [[cornwall|Cornwall]]. The county's maritime tradition is ancient: [[plymouth|Plymouth]] was the port from which Drake sailed against the Armada, and Devon men crewed the ships that built [[england|Britain]]'s naval supremacy. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s observation about entry from the north reflects the archaeological record — the Atlantic-facing harbours of the north coast were the primary points of contact during the early medieval period.
