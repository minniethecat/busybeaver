# Parliament

## Overview

Parliament occupies a peculiar position in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*. It is both the institution [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] served (as Liberal MP for Salford South, 1906–1910) and the institution whose decline provides his most sustained political critique. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes of Parliament not as an outsider denouncing a distant power but as a disillusioned insider who entered expecting honour and found corruption.

## Belloc's Critique

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s diagnosis of Parliament is concentrated in Section 39 but runs throughout the book. The central charge is that Parliament has been hollowed out by the commodification of honour: "The traffic in honours for the private advantage of the few" (039-022), "a man would take [[wealth|money]] or honours as the wages of voting for the policy of the rich" (039-036). The institution that once "was connected with honour and a social position worthy of a free man" (039-080) has become a marketplace.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s critique is structural, not merely moral. The system itself has been corrupted — "When once the tolerance of corruption has been established, the whole mass of the public life concerned is infected beyond remedy" (039-024). Parliament's decline is not the fault of any individual or party; it is the consequence of allowing honour to become a commodity.
