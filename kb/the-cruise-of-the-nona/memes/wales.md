# Wales

## Overview

Wales (Welsh: *Cymru*) is the mountainous country occupying the western promontory of the island of [[england|Britain]]. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Wales is not a destination but a passage — the long, dramatic coast that the *[[nona|Nona]]* must sail along as she makes her way from the Irish Sea to the English Channel. It is a coast of extremes: the "great hills of Wales" rising from the [[the-sea|sea]], the narrow [[tide|tidal]] sounds of the Pembrokeshire corner, and the lonely anchorages of a shoreline that, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s day, was still largely wild.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s Wales is a place of ancient memory. He thinks of "the dim stories of the ancients: the bards, and the chieftains of Wales" [003-003], of Roman roads and pre-Roman tracks, of a land that entered "the comity of Europe" only after long resistance [011-013]. The Welsh coast is the most challenging sailing in the entire cruise — and the most beautiful.

## In the *Nona*

Wales dominates the first half of the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s cruise. After departing [[holyhead|Holyhead]], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sails south along the Welsh coast, and his descriptions of the mountains are among the book's most powerful passages:

> "There is no corner of Europe that I know, not even the splendid amphitheatre standing in tiers of high Alpine wall around Udine, which so moves me with the awe and majesty of great things as does this mass of the northern Welsh mountains seen from the sea." [005-003]

These are not the observations of a tourist. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is a sailor navigating a difficult coast, and his admiration for the Welsh mountains is tempered by the practical [[danger|hazard]]s they present. The tide races, the sudden gales, the "tangle of the southern corner of Wales, Jack Sound and [[ramsey-sound|Ramsey]] Sound and the Bishop and his clerks, and, worst of all, [[wild-goose-race|Wild Goose]] Race" [011-018] — these are the realities of sailing Welsh waters.

The southern corner of Wales nearly defeated the *[[nona|Nona]]*. At [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] faced a gale that drove him back three times. At [[skomer|Skomer]], the tide took charge and drifted him on an aimless circumnavigation of the island. He sailed "with our nose for the Atlantic headlands of the south, and for the corners of Wales, where they face the Cornish and the [[devon|Devon]] [[the-sea|seas]] and [[ireland|Ireland]] also, beyond, with the great [[the-sea|ocean]] rolling in between" [008-001] — a sentence whose rhythm captures the scale of the waters he was navigating.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] also notes a curious cultural detail: the region of Pembrokeshire known as "Little [[england|England]] beyond Wales" [011-003]. Here, the Welsh language had retreated before English settlement, creating an enclave of English speech on the far side of the Celtic country. It is a reminder that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s Wales is a complex, layered place — not a romantic wilderness but a land shaped by centuries of migration and conflict.

The Welsh coast is also a place of solitude. At anchor in an unnamed bay, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] hears "far away in the midst of the [[the-sea|sea]]... the tolling of a bell" [007-007] — a sound whose source remains mysterious, carrying the suggestion of the drowned bells of Welsh legend. And throughout his passage of these waters, he is conscious of a deeper history: the Roman roads, the Celtic saints, the Scandinavian names that cling to islands and sounds, the long, slow process of civilization.

## Geography & History

Wales is a mountainous peninsula on the western side of [[england|Britain]], with a coastline of approximately 1,680 miles. Its highest peak, Snowdon (Yr Wyddfa), rises to 3,560 feet. The southern coast, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s cruise principally took place, is indented with deep bays and narrow [[tide|tidal]] sounds — [[ramsey-sound|Ramsey]] Sound, Jack Sound — that are notorious among sailors. Historically, Wales retained its independence from [[england|England]] until the conquest of Edward I in the late thirteenth century; its language and culture survived centuries of assimilation pressure, a resilience that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], as a [[religion|Catholic]] writing of a land with its own deep religious traditions, would have understood.
