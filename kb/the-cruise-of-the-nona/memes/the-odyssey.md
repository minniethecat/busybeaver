## Overview

The *[[homer|Odyssey]]* is the ancient Greek epic poem attributed to [[homer|Homer]], recounting the ten-year wanderings of Odysseus as he attempts to return home to Ithaca after the Trojan War. It is the foundational text of maritime literature — the first and greatest story of a man struggling against the [[the-sea|sea]]. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], whose own book is structured as a voyage narrative punctuated by reflections, the *[[homer|Odyssey]]* was part of the deep background of Western civilisation.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] makes only one direct reference to the *[[homer|Odyssey]]* in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, but it is revealing. In Section 41, during his long discourse on sailing equipment and practice, he pauses to praise the Admiralty sailing directions — the *[[channel-pilot|Channel Pilot]]* and its companions. After noting their exactness and reliability, he adds a characteristically [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]ian observation:

> "What gives me great pleasure in them is that they are also picturesque. The unknown authors let themselves out now and then, and write down charming little descriptive sentences praising the wooded heights above the sea, or sounding great notes of warning which have in them a reminiscence of the *Odyssey*." (041-036)

This is a striking compliment. The anonymous civil servants who compile the sailing directions are, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s reading, the heirs of [[homer|Homer]]. Their prose — practical, exact, yet occasionally touched by sublimity — carries forward the ancient tradition of writing about the [[the-sea|sea]] with truthful reverence. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] then quotes a passage from the [[pilot|Pilot]] that he has committed to memory:

> "But the mariner will do well to avoid this passage at the approach of the turn of the tide; or if the wind be rising, or darkness falling upon the sea."

He imagines this rendered into [[homer|Homer]]ic Greek hexameters — "telling how the goddess warned him of [[danger|peril]]... when the stream of Oceanos was turning, and the night was coming forward over the abyss." The *[[homer|Odyssey]]* is not a remote classic for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]; its spirit is alive in the prose of a government publication.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s reverence for [[homer|Homer]] runs throughout his work. In *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* he already has a featured page for [[homer]], and his allusion to the *[[homer|Odyssey]]* here reinforces the connection between the ancient epic and his own voyage narrative. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] saw himself as working in a continuous tradition — not as a "literary gent" producing fashionable books, but as a writer measuring himself against the permanent things. The *[[homer|Odyssey]]* is the permanent thing against which all [[the-sea|sea]]-writing must be measured.
