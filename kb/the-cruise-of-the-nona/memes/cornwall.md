# Cornwall

## Overview

Cornwall is the southwesternmost county of [[england|England]], a rugged peninsula jutting into the Atlantic at the meeting of the Celtic Sea and the English Channel. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Cornwall is the turning point — literally "going round the land" — the passage from the northern coast to the southern, from the Atlantic to [[english-channel|the Channel]], from the wild western [[the-sea|seas]] to the sheltered waters of home.

The Cornish coast holds some of the finest deep-water harbours in [[england|Britain]]: Falmouth, [[fowey|Fowey]], [[plymouth|Plymouth Sound]]. Yet [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is struck by a historical puzzle: why did the ancient kingdom of Dumnonia orient itself northward, toward the Celtic Sea, when its finest harbours face south toward the Continent? The question is typical of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] — a navigational observation that opens into a meditation on history, legend, and the deep patterns of human settlement.

## In the *Nona*

Cornwall enters the *[[nona|Nona]]* narrative most prominently in Section 18, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] confesses his failure to round the Land's End. "Three times have I set out from Saint Ives with the firm intention of passing the Longships, and putting her round up-Channel. Never have I done so. For the Seven Gods, or one or another of them, forbade it" [018-001]. It is a confession delivered with characteristic self-deprecation — the [[the-sea|seas]]oned sailor admitting that the gods of weather and chance have repeatedly denied him this particular passage.

The "turning of the point of Cornwall" [018-001] represents a symbolic threshold: the passage from the northern coast to the southern, from the Atlantic swell to the narrower waters of [[english-channel|the Channel]]. That [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] never makes this passage — that he sends the *[[nona|Nona]]* round the land instead — is one of the book's quiet ironies. The cruise that promises to circumnavigate the southwestern peninsula never quite completes that circuit.

Cornwall's coastline serves [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] as a theatre of navigational error. He recounts, "to my shame," misreading "the outline of a headland, not from [[the-sea|sea]] but from land" [018-006] — a [[fog|mist]]ake that, made at [[the-sea|sea]], could have been fatal. He records the case of the *Mohegan*, a ship that "struck the rocks called the Manacles, which are inside Black Head, in Cornwall" [018-013], despite [[lizard|the Lizard]] Light being "staring plain enough." The lesson is familiar to any sailor: familiarity breeds complacency, and complacency kills.

The peninsula's historical mystery preoccupies [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] in Section 13. The deep harbours of the south — Falmouth, [[fowey|Fowey]], [[plymouth|Plymouth]], the Yealm, Salcombe, the Dart, Teignmouth — seem designed by nature for continental trade. Yet "the legends, and even the history of Dumnonia are more connected with the north" [013-030]. Arthur's story is set on the northern coast; Cornwall communicated with Brittany not across the narrow Channel but by sailing "round the end of the land" [013-030]. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] leaves the puzzle unresolved, but the observation en[[wealth|riches]] his portrait of a coast that has always looked two ways at once.

Cornwall also provides some of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most evocative descriptive fragments. He knew "nothing of these harbours of North [[devon|Devon]] and Cornwall, not even by hearsay" [014-001] before setting out — approaching the coast as a discoverer rather than a tourist. The "Cornish [[the-sea|seas]]" face [[ireland|Ireland]] and the Atlantic beyond [008-001], and the weight of that [[the-sea|ocean]] is felt in every paragraph about the western approaches.

## Geography & History

Cornwall forms the southwestern tip of the British mainland, surrounded by the Celtic Sea to the north and west and the English Channel to the south. Its geology is granite and slate, its coastline indented with rias — drowned river valleys that form deep natural harbours. Historically, Cornwall was the heart of the Brythonic kingdom of Dumnonia, which maintained its distinct identity well into the early medieval period. The Cornish language, a Brythonic Celtic tongue closely related to [[wales|Welsh]] and Breton, survived as a community language into the eighteenth century. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s observation about the northern orientation of Dumnonia is borne out by archaeology: the early medieval trading posts and monastic settlements cluster along the north coast, facing the Irish Sea routes that connected Cornwall to [[wales|Wales]], [[ireland|Ireland]], and Brittany.
