# Oliver Cromwell

## Overview

Oliver Cromwell (1599–1658) was an English military and political leader who rose to power during the [[english-civil-war|Civil Wars]] and ruled as Lord P[[corruption|rot]]ector from 1653 until his death. His name appears in the *[[nona|Nona]]* not as a historical study but as a symbol — of conquest, of memory, and of the way [[tradition|the past]] refuses to stay buried.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] invokes Cromwell twice, and both references are charged with moral weight. The first comes early in the voyage, as he considers the deep history of the lands he sails past:

> I wondered, as I passed Fishguard and all that coast of massacre and pillage under Cromwell's leadership, how many of the men who read and write of those things have any conception of what they mean. (011-033)

The sentence is characteristically [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]ian: the historical reference is specific (Cromwell's Irish campaign of 1649–53), but the target is contemporary — the comfortable readers who treat historical atrocity as mere information. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], who had a deep [[religion|Catholic]] sensibility and a keen awareness of the Irish [[pain|suffering]] under English rule, Cromwell's name carries the weight of three centuries of unresolved grievance.

The second reference, in Section 37, is [[lighthouse|light]]er in tone but equally telling in its assumptions. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] speaks dismissively of "the serpent of books, or again, The Cromwell Road of Books" (037-001) — the relentless flow of publications that pile up without adding genuine [[knowledge|understanding]]. The metaphor is apt: Cromwell, like the serpent in Eden, is a figure whose story tempts us toward simplistic moralising rather than genuine historical comprehension.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s Cromwell is not the Lord P[[corruption|rot]]ector of Whig historiography — the champion of parliamentary liberty — but the man of Drogheda and Wexford, whose name haunts the Irish coast like a ghost.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s mother, Bessie Rayner Parkes, was of English P[[corruption|rot]]estant stock, but [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] himself was raised in the [[religion|Catholic]] [[religion|faith]] of his [[france|French]] father. His identification with [[ireland|Ireland]] — he visited frequently and w[[corruption|rot]]e extensively about Irish history — informs his view of Cromwell. For the [[religion|Catholic]] [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Cromwell represented the violent rupture of the [[reformation|English Reformation]] tradition, a wound that had never fully healed.
