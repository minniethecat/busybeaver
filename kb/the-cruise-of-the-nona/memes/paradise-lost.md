# Paradise Lost

## Overview

*Paradise Lost* (1667) is [[john-milton|John Milton]]'s epic poem of the Fall of Man — and for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], writing of poetry's special relationship with truth, it stands as the supreme example of the poet's license to exaggerate. The poet, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] argues, "may use what words he likes, and go to any extreme, or to any excess, so long only as he hits the mark" (040-020).

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s discussion of poetry in Section 40 is one of the book's most compressed and powerful statements of aesthetic principle. Having just survived the gybe off [[dungeness|Dungeness]], and having reflected on temperance as a virtue, he turns to consider the special case of poetic language:

> But the rhetorician wisely indulges in extravagance, and as for the poet, he may use what words he likes, and go to any extreme, or to any excess, so long only as he hits the mark. For it is with poetry as with love and with singing in tune. It is with poetry as with the sense of reality. It is with poetry as with the toothache. Either you have it, or you have it not. (040-020)

The series of similes — love, singing in tune, the sense of [[truth|reality]], toothache — is itself a piece of rhetorical extravagance. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is performing what he describes: using language to excess because excess is what the subject demands.

*Paradise Lost* is the implicit standard here. [[john-milton|Milton]]'s poem is nothing if not excessive — ten thousand lines of blank verse on the grandest possible subject, a poem that dares to put words in the mouth of God. If poetry fails when it fails to hit the mark, *Paradise Lost* succeeds because it never misses.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own poetry — [[lighthouse|light]]er in tone than [[john-milton|Milton]]'s but equally committed to formal precision — shows his debt to the English poetic tradition of which [[john-milton|Milton]] is the pinnacle. The *[[nona|Nona]]* itself, though prose, aspires to a poetic density of language and image that owes something to [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s lifelong engagement with verse.
