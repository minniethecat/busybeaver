Padstow is a harbour town on the north coast of [[cornwall|Cornwall]] whose shallow waters, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] notes, stand in apparent contradiction to its historical importance. In Section 14, he reflects on the paradox that [[cornwall|Cornwall]]'s wealth and power in the early Dark Ages were concentrated on the northern shore rather than on the deeper, more sheltered southern harbours.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] lists Padstow among the "shallow, small, and difficult places" of the north [[cornwall|Cornish coast]] that, paradoxically, dominated the history of Dumnonia:

> Yet the legends, and even the history of Dumnonia are more concerned with such shallow, small, and difficult places as Saint Ives, Padstow, and Bideford River. The wealth seems to have been more on the north than upon the south, for you will note that the pirate raids principally struck there, and the shrines also, the ancient shrines, are rather on the north than on the south [014-004].

The observation is part of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s larger reflection on the geography of early medieval [[cornwall|Cornwall]] — a landscape in which the [[the-sea|sea]]ts of government, the relics of the saints, and the story of Arthur were all oriented towards the northern coast, despite the southern harbours pointing directly at the Breton shore where [[cornwall|Cornwall]]'s cultural twin lay.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] notes that men sailing between [[cornwall|Cornwall]] and Brittany in the early Dark Ages "seem to sail round the end of the land — though the southern harbours are there, pointing right at the further shore" [014-005]. This geographical counter-intuition — that the more difficult northern ports held the greater share of power and sanctity — is characteristic of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s interest in the tension between physical geography and human history.
