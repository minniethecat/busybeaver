# Canterbury

## Overview

Canterbury is the ancient ecclesiastical capital of [[england|England]], its great cathedral the [[the-sea|sea]]t of the Archbishop and the destination of Chaucer's pilgrims. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a [[religion|Catholic]] writing in a P[[corruption|rot]]estant [[england|England]], Canterbury carries a particular weight: it is the site of Saint Thomas Becket's martyrdom, the symbolic centre of English [[religion|Catholicism]], and — in one of his most characteristic asides — the [[the-sea|sea]]t of a "dread emperor" under whom four pagan kings were once gathered.

Canterbury appears in the *[[nona|Nona]]* only glancingly, in historical digressions rather than navigational passages. The city lies inland, beyond the reach of the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s cruise. But it enters [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s thoughts whenever he sails past the [[kent|Kentish coast]] — the white cliffs and [[foreland|the Forelands]] are the shoreline of Canterbury's history.

## In the *Nona*

Canterbury's most vivid appearance comes in the opening pages, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] meditates on the little kingdoms of early [[england|England]]. "How pleasant it is to remember that there were four kings in [[kent|Kent]], grouped there under one dread emperor at Canterbury!" [001-033]. The exclamation mark is rare for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] — a sign of genuine de[[lighthouse|light]] in the image. The "dread emperor" is the Archbishop, and the four kings are the sub-kings of [[kent|Kentish]] legend, all pagans "when the rest of [[england|England]] had already gone [[religion|Catholic]]" [001-033].

The passage is part of a larger reflection on the pleasures of small kingdoms — Ithaca, Cephalonia, the little territory of the Athenians — and Canterbury serves as the English instance of a political arrangement that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a man of [[sussex|Sussex]] particularism, clearly finds congenial. The four kings under one emperor represent a kind of federated localism that appeals to his distributist instincts.

Canterbury reappears in Section 15, though indirectly. Discussing the nature of belief and miracle, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] references Saint Thomas of Canterbury — Thomas Becket, murdered in his own cathedral in 1170. He mentions a miracle attributed to Becket, but with characteristic [[doubt|scepticism]] about the miraculous: "I don't believe that" [015-033], he writes of one particular story. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s [[religion|Catholicism]] is of a particular kind — orthodox in doctrine but allergic to the pious exaggerations that have accumulated around the saints.

The darkest Canterbury reference comes in Section 38, as [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sails past the [[kent|Kentish]] corner. He invokes, in a single breath, "Saint Augustine, and [[julius-caesar|Julius Caesar]], and the men that murdered Saint Thomas, and so grew tails" [038-001]. The story that the citizens of Canterbury grew tails after Becket's murder — a divine punishment for their complicity — is one of those medieval legends that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] recounts with evident relish. It is the kind of detail that makes his historical asides memorable: the cosmic reduced to the comic, divine justice manifesting as a caudal appendage.

The city also earns a mention in Section 23, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] discusses the silted rivers of antiquity. "Some say that even Salisbury was reached from the [[the-sea|sea]], and Winchester. Certainly Exeter was; Arundel still can be by ships of some size" [023-004]. He notes that Canterbury, too, once had access from the [[the-sea|sea]] — the River Stour was navigable to the city in Roman and medieval times, though silting has long since closed that route. It is a reminder that Canterbury, though now an inland city, was once a port — a place where the [[the-sea|sea]] and [[the-church|the Church]] met.

## Geography & History

Canterbury stands on the River Stour in eastern [[kent|Kent]], approximately eight miles from the coast. It was the Roman city of Durovernum Cantiacorum and later [[london|the capital]] of the [[kent|Kentish]] kingdom. Saint Augustine established his mission there in 597 CE, founding the archbishopric that remains the primatial see of [[the-church|the Church]] of [[england|England]]. Thomas Becket's murder in the cathedral in 1170 made Canterbury one of the greatest pilgrimage destinations of medieval Europe, immortalised in Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales*. The cathedral is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s "dread emperor" allusion captures the medieval [[truth|reality]]: the Archbishop of Canterbury was, for centuries, among the most powerful figures in the realm, his authority extending over kings as well as clergy.
