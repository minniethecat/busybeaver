## Overview

*If Winter Comes* (1921) is a novel by the British writer A. S. M. Hutchinson (1879–1971). It was one of the great bestsellers of the early 1920s, particularly in the United States, where it became a cultural phenomenon. The novel, set in a quiet English town, tells the story of Mark Sabre, a man whose life unravels after he takes in a [[youth|young]] woman in [[pain|distress]] — a plot that allowed Hutchinson to explore themes of small-town hypocrisy, marital convention, and the gap between respectability and morality.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] mentions *If Winter Comes* in the course of a long reflection on the irrationality of the book market. He has been arguing that literary quality and commercial success have no causal relationship — that the market is "capricious and incalculable." As evidence, he cites the career of Hutchinson:

> "Mr. Hutchinson, for whose work I personally have (though I am afraid my judgment is not worth much in these things) a great liking, wrote among other books a book called *Once Aboard the Lugger*. It was exceedingly amusing, fresh, and, as I should have said, well worth anybody's buying. I do not know how many copies he sold of it, but I should imagine nothing very astonishing. A little while after he wrote a book called *If Winter Comes*, and it sold by the stack, and by the ton. One could see it piled up man-high upon the counters of shops in America, and it sold, and it sold, and it sold." [040-022]

The passage is characteristic [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] — simultaneously a piece of literary criticism, a meditation on the economics of writing, and a confession of his own bewilderment at the ways of the market. He likes Hutchinson's work genuinely; he notes without envy that the earlier book did modestly and the later one enormously; and he uses the contrast to drive home his point that "there are no laws governing these movements."

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s discussion of *If Winter Comes* is embedded in a longer passage that also mentions H. G. Wells's *[[the-time-machine|The Time Machine]]*, W. H. Hudson's *[[crystal-age|A Crystal Age]]*, and [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own experience of seeing his war articles sell sixty thousand copies a week, then stop selling for no discernible reason. The paragraph is a small treatise on the economics of authorship, written by a man who lived by his pen and had learned, with some bemusement, that the market was not a judge of merit.
