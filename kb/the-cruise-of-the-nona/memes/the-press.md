# The Press

## Overview

The press — by which [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] means the mass-circulation newspapers of Edwardian and Georgian [[england|Britain]] — is one of the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s recurring antagonists. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was himself a working journalist (he w[[corruption|rot]]e for the *Morning Post*, among others), and his critique of the press is grounded in insider knowledge: he knew how newspapers were made, and he distrusted what he knew.

## Belloc's View

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most sustained critique of the press appears in his discussion of evidence and belief in Section 18:

> We accept, in everything which does not immediately concern us, a bare newspaper statement made anonymously. We accept it as we accept the evidence of our own senses, or more; we accept it although it is unvouched for, unproved, untested. We accept it — to be brutal — because it is printed, and because it is printed on a large scale. (018-020)

The target here is not propaganda or deliberate falsehood but something subtler: the unearned authority of print. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] argues that modern men have developed a strange credulity toward the printed word — a willingness to believe anonymous statements that they would never accept from an individual. The press, by its very scale and anonymity, creates a simulacrum of authority.

This distrust of the press is connected to [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s broader critique of modern epistemology. Just as a sailor can [[fog|mist]]ake the Varne Buoy for a [[lighthouse|light]]ship in thick weather, so can a modern reader [[fog|mist]]ake a newspaper story for a [[truth|fact]]. The medium creates the illusion of certainty.

## Belloc and Fleet Street

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was a prolific contributor to the press — editor of the *Eye-Witness* (later the *New Witness*), columnist for the *Morning Post*, contributor to countless periodicals. His relationship with journalism was that of the insider-critic: he used the press while despising its influence. The *[[nona|Nona]]* itself, for all its literary ambition, was written by a man who understood deadlines, word counts, and the requirements of a general readership.
