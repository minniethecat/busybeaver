# Thames

## Overview

The Thames is [[england|England]]'s principal river, flowing from the Cotswold Hills through [[london|London]] to the North Sea. For sailors it is "London River" — the historic waterway that has carried the traffic of [[england|Britain]] since the beginning of recorded time. In [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s *[[nona|Nona]]*, the Thames is not a landscape feature to be explored but a threshold: the final passage the cruiser must cross before reaching home.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s relationship with the Thames is complex. He approaches it as a sailor who knows its dangers — the silted channels, the great ships, the treacherous [[tide|tides]] — but also as a writer who finds in its waters a metaphor for language itself. Words, he reflects, rise up from obscurity "like the little particles of ice which float to the surface of the Thames at the beginning of a frost, rising silently, floating up from the bottom of the river" [001-044]. The image is one of his most memorable: the river as the unconscious of English speech.

## In the *Nona*

The Thames appears in the *[[nona|Nona]]* narrative primarily as the cruise's eastern terminus. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] traces [[english-channel|the Channel]] coast westward from [[kent|Kent]], but the Thames estuary is the natural eastern boundary — the point where the English Channel becomes the North Sea. In Section 28, he notes the practical irrelevance of the Thames for most Channel sailors: "No man running down-Channel from the Thames need touch the Race: no man running up" [028-013]. [[portland-bill|Portland Race]], that great navigational terror, lies off the track for Thames traffic.

But [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most sustained meditation on the Thames comes in Section 37, where he reflects on the river as "London River" — the name sailors give it. He writes of "the getting across it slantwise in spite of the great ships and the way in which time has silted up that harbour" [037-002]. He notes the curious features of the Thamesmouth: the double tide, the shifting channels, and the way "the best of all the channels into the river is the one least used, because it would make ships go out of their way" [037-003]. Only the Dutchmen, he observes with approval, use the main mid-channel.

His most striking claim about the Thames is historical: "it was never shut for traffic since the beginning of recorded time … until. … Men always traded up and down it with a swarm of shipping since men first left memorial of their doings, *until* the fourth of August, 1914" [037-003]. [[great-war|The Great War]] closed the river that had been open since antiquity — a [[truth|fact]] [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] records with the weight of a man who has seen a world end.

The cruise's final passage brings [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] "round the corner of [[kent|Kent]] and across the Thames" [039-001]. Sitting in the cockpit, he studies his chart — though he knows "every inch (oh! come! say rather millimetres) of the coast I was to follow" [039-001]. The self-correction is quintessential [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]: precision asserted and then mocked in the same breath.

## Geography & History

The Thames rises in the Cotswolds and flows 215 miles to the North Sea. Its estuary has been a trading route since the Bronze Age. The Romans built Londinium at the river's lowest bridging point, and the Thames has been the commercial artery of [[england|England]] ever since. The [[tide|tidal]] reach extends to Teddington Lock, and the estuarine waters below [[london|London]] are notoriously complex: shifting sandbanks, double [[tide|tides]], and the constant traffic of one of the world's busiest waterways. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s observation about silting is geologically accurate — the Thames estuary has risen significantly since Roman times, leaving ports like Richborough stranded inland.
