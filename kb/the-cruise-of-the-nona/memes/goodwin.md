# Goodwin Sands

## Overview

The Goodwin Sands are a ten-mile stretch of shifting sandbanks lying off the coast of [[kent|Kent]], between the North and South [[foreland|Forelands]]. For centuries they have been among the most feared navigational [[danger|hazard]]s in the English Channel — a graveyard of ships that, according to legend, once formed the fertile island of Lomea, swallowed by the [[the-sea|sea]] in a great storm of the eleventh century. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the Goodwins are a constant presence on the eastern reach of his cruise: the dark counterpoint to the guiding [[lighthouse|light]]s of [[foreland|the Forelands]].

The Sands are doubly dangerous because they shift. Charts must be regularly updated, and even then the channels between the banks change with every great gale. The Goodwin Lightship — and [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s near-disastrous confusion of it with the Varne Buoy — provides one of the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s most vivid cautionary tales.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most personal encounter with the Goodwins comes not from the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s cruise but from an earlier voyage, recounted in Section 18 as a cautionary tale of navigational error. "The worst [[fog|mist]]ake of this sort I myself ever made was [[fog|mist]]aking the Varne Buoy for the South Goodwin Lightship" [018-017]. The weather was thick; he had been "drifting for many hours without much wind, and with no certitude of where I was except that I was somewhere in the Straits" [018-017]. Looking up-weather toward the northeast, he [[fog|mist]]ook one [[lighthouse|light]] for another — an error that, in those treacherous waters, could easily have been fatal.

The anecdote is characteristic [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]: self-deprecating, precise in its nautical detail, and willing to confess his own errors as warnings to others. The Varne Buoy and the South Goodwin Lightship are only a few miles apart, but confusing them means misplacing oneself on the chart by a margin that, in the narrow [[the-sea|seas]], leaves no room for recovery.

In the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s cruise, the Goodwins appear as the dark side of the home waters. In Section 36, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] describes running up for [[thames|London River]] with "the great [[lighthouse|light]] of [[foreland|the Forelands]] on the weather side, the Goodwin Lights on the lee" [036-001]. The [[lighthouse|light]]s stand "like the lamps of a street" — an image of domestic order that belies the danger beneath. The Goodwins are invisible, marked only by their [[lighthouse|light]]ships, but they are always there.

The Goodwins serve [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] as a geographical refrain in the closing chapters. "North of [[foreland|the Forelands]], north of the Goodwins, north of Long Nose: [[memory|memories]]" [037-001] — the place-names accumulating like the landmarks of a familiar journey. "The run between the [[kent|Kentish]] corner and the Goodwin Sands" [038-001] is the passage that marks the final approach to the [[thames|Thames]]. And in the book's concluding meditation, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] places the Goodwins among the unchanging marks of his world: "[[foreland|the Forelands]] Light and the Goodwins, are what I have always known" [039-007].

## Geography & History

The Goodwin Sands lie approximately six miles off the Deal coast in [[kent|Kent]], exposed at low water in places. They have claimed thousands of ships since the Middle Ages — the Great Storm of 1703 alone wrecked over fifty vessels on the Sands. The shifting nature of the banks means that channels through them are never permanent. Lightships have marked the Sands since the eighteenth century; the South Goodwin Lightship, which [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] references, was stationed at the southern end of the banks. During both World Wars, the Sands were used as an emergency landing ground for damaged aircraft. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s allusion to the legend of Lomea — the island supposedly destroyed by a storm — reflects a persistent local tradition that the Sands were once fertile farmland belonging to Earl Godwin.
