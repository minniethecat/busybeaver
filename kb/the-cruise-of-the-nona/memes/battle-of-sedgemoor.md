# Battle of Sedgemoor

The Battle of Sedgemoor (6 July 1685) was the final engagement of the Monmouth Rebellion — the attempt by James Scott, Duke of Monmouth, to seize the throne from his uncle James II. In *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] dismisses it with characteristic terseness, declaring bluntly that "Sedgemoor was not a battle" [024-009].

## In the *Nona*

The reference occurs in Section 24, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is sailing past Bridport on [[england|England]]'s south coast and reflecting on the Monmouth Rebellion's landing at [[lyme-regis|Lyme]]. His full judgment is that the rebel forces, hastily raised and without proper leadership, "must have been quite untrained" — and their culminating engagement at Sedgemoor was too one-sided to merit the name of a battle. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s dismissal is both a military assessment and a comment on the pathos of the rebellion: Monmouth, poorly funded and poorly led, never had a genuine chance.

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the Monmouth episode serves as a narrative foil to the successful landing of William of Orange three [[time|years]] later. Where Monmouth's rebels were a disorganized rabble defeated in a damp Somerset field, William's invasion — with its Dutch mercenaries and political backing — succeeded in transforming the English constitution. Sedgemoor marks the end of one kind of political action and the prelude to another.

## Wider Context

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s judgment of Sedgemoor reflects his broader interest in the mechanisms of political legitimacy and the conditions under which rebellions succeed or fail. Throughout the *[[nona|Nona]]*, he returns to the question of what makes authority legitimate — and the Monmouth Rebellion, in his telling, lacked every element that creates legitimacy: popular mandate, competent leadership, and adequate resources.
