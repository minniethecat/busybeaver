# Plymouth

## Overview

Plymouth Sound is one of the great natural harbours of [[england|England]] — a vast sheltered anchorage guarded by the breakwater, flanked by the Hoe, and steeped in naval history. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], it is a harbour of mixed feelings. He cannot deny its majesty, but its oversaturated fame — "Drake and the Armada... the perpetual reiteration" (019-006) — provokes his characteristic resistance to received opinion.

Yet Plymouth is also the setting for one of the book's most powerful narrative passages: the cruise of July 1914, when [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] set out from the Sound into a calm that would, within days, be broken by the outbreak of the Great War.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of Plymouth is structured around two arrivals and one departure. The first arrival is visual — the approach from the west, when "the career along the coast to Plymouth Sound was like a song" (019-004) and the *[[nona|Nona]]* "opened the majestic breadth of Plymouth Sound" (019-005). The language is unreservedly celebratory: the coast of [[devon|Devon]] is "a heavenly piece of coast" (019-006), its people good and its land uncrowded.

But then [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s contrariness surfaces. He is irritated by "exaggerated fame" (019-006), by the endless retelling of Drake's story, and confesses: "I would rather see Plymouth breakwater from the Sound, and so sail past, than look on the Sound from the Hoe. But I admit that this mood is evil, and that it is something of a perversion" (019-006). It is a characteristically honest moment — [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] acknowledging his own c[[corruption|rot]]chets.

The second Plymouth passage, in Section 19, is far graver. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sets out from Plymouth in late July 1914, unaware that the Great War is about to break. The Fleet had dispersed from Spithead; there was "nothing but rumour, nothing certain" (019-011). He had heard the priest at Salisbury pray for the murdered Archduke, but "nothing was further from my mind than war and armament" (019-026).

The departure from Plymouth Sound becomes a premonitory moment. In the calm, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] drifts to the Mewstone and back; a breeze finally rises; he rounds the Start at dawn; and then, alone upon the [[the-sea|sea]], "there passed me the vision I shall remember forever" (019-026) — an uncanny optical illusion in the morning haze that seems to him, in retrospect, an omen of the catastrophe about to engulf Europe.

The third reference, in Section 27, is practical: Plymouth is a good harbour but "does not wholly check the violence of a southerly wind" (027-020).

## Geography & History

Plymouth Sound has been a naval anchorage since at least the fifteenth century and was the traditional muster point for fleets before battle. The Hoe — the elevated promenade above the Sound — is famously associated with Sir Francis Drake's legendary game of bowls before engaging the [[spanish-armada|Spanish Armada]] in 1588. The breakwater, completed in 1841, p[[corruption|rot]]ects the Sound from southerly swells.
