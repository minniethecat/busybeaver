## Overview

*Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* (1865) is the most famous work of Charles Lutwidge Dodgson, who w[[corruption|rot]]e under the pseudonym Lewis Carroll. A mathematical don at Christ Church, [[oxford-university|Oxford]], Dodgson created a surreal dream-narrative that became one of the most widely read children's books in English. By [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s time, it had achieved the status of a cultural institution.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] discusses *Alice in Wonderland* in Section 12 of *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, in a passage that begins as praise for Dodgson's successful anonymity and turns into a sceptical assessment of the book's lasting value.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] admires Dodgson's strategy of concealment. Reflecting on his own youthful desire to publish under different names for different genres — "verse under one name, history under another, travel under another" — he notes that Dodgson alone among their contemporaries achieved this:

> "He had calculated well; and the immense fame of Lewis Carroll and of *Alice in Wonderland* left him private and secure." (012-004)

The mathematician could walk the streets unrecognised while his creation conquered the nurseries of [[england|England]]. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], who loathed the glass cage of publicity, this was an ideal arrangement.

But [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s praise is double-edged. He immediately questions whether *Alice*'s fame will endure:

> "*Alice in Wonderland* is a very remarkable book, as are, indeed, other books of Dodgson's, though not all. But a large element in its vast diffusion was its consonance with a particular 'drawing-room' mood of a particular day." (012-005)

He argues that the book's nonsense humour — "the humour which is founded upon folly as contrasted with the wit that is founded upon wisdom" — could only flourish in the sheltered security of late-Victorian [[england|England]], a period [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] regarded as abnormally peaceful. "When the terrors and the heroisms permanently return," he predicts, "the sawdust will run out of such things."

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of *Alice in Wonderland* reveals his broader literary-critical principles. He valued writing that engaged with permanent realities — danger, [[death|mortality]], [[religion|faith]], the [[the-sea|sea]] — over writing that depended on the transient moods of a particular social class. His [[doubt|scepticism]] about *Alice*'s longevity was, in retrospect, wrong: the book has far outlasted the drawing-room culture [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] thought it depended on. But his instinct — that truly great literature speaks to the permanent human condition rather than to passing fashion — is consistent with everything else he w[[corruption|rot]]e.
