# Milford Haven

## Overview

Milford Haven is a deep natural harbour on the southwestern coast of [[wales]], in Pembrokeshire. Formed by a drowned river valley, it extends inland as a long fjord-like inlet — one of the finest natural deep-water harbours in the British Isles. In [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s time it had "half dropped out of men's [[memory|memories]]" [011-018], lacking the industrial hinterland that might have revived its [[wealth|fortune]]s.

The harbour carries a heavy historical weight. It was here, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] notes, that two momentous events in British history took place — both of which he regards as calamities.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] approaches Milford Haven after a difficult passage through [[jack-sound]], fighting the full roar of the ebb tide. He and his companion ran quickly past [[skomer]] and [[skokholm]], hoping to round [[saint-annes-head]] and drop anchor in the shelter of the haven before darkness.

But at [[the-sea|sea]], "nothing is ever certain" [011-010]. A solid bank of fog rolled in from [[the-sea|the ocean]], enveloping the *[[nona]]* and forcing a tense, blind approach:

> It caught us like a cloak thrown over a man's head; and, before we knew where we were, we found ourselves steering hopelessly by the card, not daring to keep too much out, lest we should lose the mouth of the haven; not daring to keep too much in, lest we should strike. [011-010]

Guided only by the foghorn on [[saint-annes-head]] and the sound of water washing against rock, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] crept into the harbour in near-darkness. They dropped anchor in the first bay, trusting to luck and the known depths.

The next morning brought reflection as they sailed up the haven on the flood tide:

> We passed the time sailing slowly up that long fjord, Milford Haven, of which one reads so much in history, but which today, having no industrial hinterland, has half dropped out of men's memories. [011-018]

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] recalls that it was here "the first Tudor claimant, 'the foul usurper, Henry Tydder,' landed with his [[france|French]] mercenaries to try for the crown, which he grasped in two weeks on Bosworth Field" [011-018] — and here too that [[oliver-cromwell]]'s transports embarked for the conquest of [[ireland|Ireland]]. He wonders darkly what third curse might arise from this inlet.
