# Sussex

## Overview

Sussex is the southern English county that stretches along [[english-channel|the Channel]] coast from the [[kent|Kentish]] border in the east to Hampshire in the west. For Hilaire [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], it was home — the "dear county" whose waters he sailed more times than he could count, whose chalk cliffs he knew "every inch" of, and whose people, the "[[youth|young]] people of my own county of Sussex, and, indeed, of all rural [[england|England]]," he admired for their independence of mind [001-038].

Sussex is not merely the backdrop to [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s sailing life; it is the axis around which his voyages turn. The *[[nona|Nona]]* sailed from its harbours, cruised along its coast, and returned to its shelter. In [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s imagination, Sussex and the [[the-sea|sea]] are inseparable — the one shaping the character of the other.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s relationship with Sussex runs through the book like a current. Early on, he meditates on the county's history, noting the common belief that "Sussex was the last piece of English land to hold out against the heathen" [001-033]. He is sceptical — "the truth is that not Sussex but the [[wight|Isle of Wight]] was the last stronghold" [001-035] — but the very discussion shows how deeply the county's identity matters to him.

The Sussex coast appears and reappears as the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s familiar ground. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] describes the inshore current along the Bight of Sussex [014-013], the tricky navigation of its shore at night, where "a man who has been knocking about [[english-channel|the Channel]] at night, and does not quite know where he is, except that he is somewhere off the Sussex coast, may take Fair[[lighthouse|light]] in a haze for [[beachy-head|Beachy Head]]" [018-011]. These are the observations of a man who has learned the coast not from charts alone but from long experience.

Sussex is also the setting for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s friendships. He records an afternoon on the water "with my own dear county of Sussex" as the shared context of a conversation with an older friend — possibly [[maurice-baring|Maurice Baring]] [015-025]. And in one of the book's most personal passages, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] recalls an early memory from "one of the Sussex houses" where a political intrigue nearly played out [028-036].

As the cruise nears its end, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] turns toward home: "I have cruised up and down that garden wall of my own land so much that it stands in my mind as a perpetual come and go" [032-002]. The last days of the voyage carry him "round the corner of [[kent|Kent]] and across the [[thames|Thames]]" — but Sussex is the shore he has been sailing toward all along [039-001].

## Geography & History

Sussex was historically divided into East and West Sussex, with the South Downs forming a chalk spine running east to west. Its coast is a mixture of white cliffs, shingle beaches, and the great shallow bight that sailors must navigate with care. The county was the last part of [[england|England]] to be converted to [[religion|Christianity]] and long retained a fiercely independent character — traits [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] would have admired as both a [[religion|Catholic]] and a Sussex man.
