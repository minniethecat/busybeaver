# The Armistice (1918)

## Overview

The Armistice of 11 November 1918 brought the fighting of the Great War to an end — but for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], writing seven [[time|years]] later, it marks not a resolution but a beginning. The "comedy of small men in big places" (020-018) at the Paris Peace Conference, the blunders over Poland, the politicians who could not distinguish the Save from the Danube — all of this follows from the Armistice and colours [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s assessment of what the war achieved.

## Belloc's Account

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s extended treatment of the Armistice and its aftermath appears in Section 20, which is among the book's longest and most substantial passages of political reflection. He writes:

> As I write this, it is more than ten years since the dice of that "*chose aléatoire*" were thrown, and no one can even yet determine what interests in Europe have, upon the whole, most suffered; though (alas!) the larger lines of the result are already apparent. (020-016)

The [[france|French]] phrase — "a chancy thing" — is from General Boulanger, whom [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] had heard speak as a [[youth|young]] man. The admission that war is governed by chance, not plan, is the centre of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s critique of the politicians and generals who claimed to understand what they were doing.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s satire of the Paris peacemakers is withering but not bitter. He would prefer a comic treatment — "an amusing book full of truths, made up of anecdote without much comment" (020-017) — but the material resists pure comedy. Too much [[pain|suffering]] had flowed from the decisions made in those rooms.

## The Lost Certainties

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the war's deepest wound was not physical but intellectual. The belief that human reason could master events — the [[religion|faith]] of the nineteenth century — died somewhere between the Marne and [[treaty-of-versailles|Versailles]]. The Armistice was not a victory for reason but a truce between exhausted combatants, and the peace that followed was, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s judgment, a continuation of the war by other means.
