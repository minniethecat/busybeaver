# Westminster Parliament

The **[[parliament|Parliament]] of the United Kingdom** meets at the Palace of [[house-of-commons|Westminster]]
in [[london|London]]. It is the supreme legislative body of the United Kingdom,
comprising the Sovereign, the House of Lords, and the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]].
The modern parliamentary system evolved from the medieval *curia regis* and
was substantially shaped by the Reform Acts of the nineteenth century, which
progressively extended the franchise.

## Structure and Function

[[parliament|Parliament]]'s primary functions are legislation, scrutiny of the executive,
and the granting of supply (taxation and expenditure). The [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]],
as the elected chamber, holds primacy over the House of Lords, whose powers
were curtailed by the [[parliament|Parliament]] Acts of 1911 and 1949. Bills must pass both
Houses and receive Royal Assent before becoming law. The Palace of [[house-of-commons|Westminster]],
a Gothic Revival masterpiece by [[charles-barry|Charles Barry]] and
[[augustus-pugin|Augustus Pugin]], has been the [[the-sea|sea]]t of [[parliament|Parliament]] since
the medieval palace was destroyed by fire in 1834.

## Belloc's Connection

Hilaire [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] served as a Liberal Member of [[parliament|Parliament]] for Salford South
from 1906 to 1910. His experience in the [[house-of-commons|Commons]] colours
his writing in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*:

> …the Parliamentary poison eating up the Irish…
> — [007-030]

> …I saw it during the two Parliaments in which I sat…
> — [007-028]

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] grew disillusioned with parliamentary politics, viewing the system
as a machine that corrupted genuine reform. His experience as an MP convinced
him that the party system and the professionalisation of politics made real
change impossible from within. This conviction fed directly into his advocacy
for [[distributism|distributism]], a radical political philosophy that sought to
disperse political and economic power away from centralised institutions like
[[parliament|Parliament]] and toward local communities and small property holders.

## Belloc's Parliamentary Career

During his four [[time|years]] in [[house-of-commons|the Commons]], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] established a reputation as an
independent-minded reformer. He campaigned for the reform of party funding,
criticized the influence of wealthy donors, and advocated for the rights of
[[religion|Catholic]] schools. His most famous parliamentary intervention was his attack on
the [[house-of-commons|Establishment]]'s secret diplomacy, demanding that
foreign policy be conducted openly. His frustration with [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] — what
he called "the [[parliament|Parliament]]ary poison" — led him to decline to stand for
re-election in 1910, after just two [[parliament|Parliament]]s.
