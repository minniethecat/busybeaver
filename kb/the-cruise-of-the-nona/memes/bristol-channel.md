# Bristol Channel

## Overview

The Bristol Channel is the great funnel of water that separates South [[wales|Wales]] from the West Country of [[england|England]], opening westward into the Atlantic and narrowing eastward into the Severn Estuary. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], it is a working waterway — the [[the-sea|sea]]-road upon which he drifts, navigates in fog, and contemplates the mysteries of the [[tide|tides]]. It is not romanticised, but it is ever-present: the physical medium of several chapters of the cruise.

The Channel is famous for its [[tide|tides]], among the highest in the world. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] observes that its "enormous tide at the end of the funnel" is "reasonable enough, though it is a little odd that it should be so very much bigger at Chepstow than at Gloucester" [014-016]. This [[tide|tidal]] range — over fourteen metres at Avonmouth — shapes every sailor's experience of these waters.

## In the *Nona*

The Bristol Channel is the setting for two of the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s most vivid passages. The first occurs off [[lundy|Lundy]] Island, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] drifts becalmed in [[white-horse-race|the White Horse]] Race. He describes [[lundy|Lundy]] as "a sort of little breakwater set up in the midst of the Bristol Channel, offering security to all craft, coming up or down" [011-038]. But that security is tempered by the menace of the Race itself, whose "great swells passed us halfway to the height of our jaws" [011-038].

The second passage is more unnerving. [[fog|Fog]] descends on [[english-channel|the Channel]] while [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is under sail, and the great [[channel-steamer|steamer]]s become invisible [[danger|threat]]s:

> "To me it seemed as though every steamer in the Bristol Channel was atop of us" [013-028].

He hears the beat of propellers, the hooting of sirens, but in the white blankness of the [[fog|mist]] he cannot tell where danger lies. When the fog lifts, the [[the-sea|sea]] reveals only "a couple of outward bound tramps halfway to the horizon" [013-028] — the panic of the imagination far exceeding the [[truth|reality]].

In a later, calmer chapter, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] muses on the [[tide|tides]] of the Bristol Channel alongside those of Saint Malo and the [[sussex|Sussex]] rivers, marvelling at their inexplicable variety. The Channel's great heave is, for him, one of those things "in God's Providence" [014-016] that the sailor accepts without pretending to explain.

## Geography & History

The Bristol Channel extends approximately 120 kilometres from the Severn Estuary to a line between [[hartland-point|Hartland]] Point ([[devon|Devon]]) and St Govan's Head (Pembrokeshire). Its funnel shape amplifies the [[tide|tidal]] range to the second highest in the world. Historically, it has been one of [[england|Britain]]'s busiest shipping routes, serving the ports of Bristol, Cardiff, Newport, and Swan[[the-sea|sea]]. By [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s time, [[channel-steamer|steamer]]s regularly plied these waters between the Atlantic and Avonmouth.
