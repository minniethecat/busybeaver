# Lundy

## Overview

Lundy is an island in [[bristol-channel|the Bristol Channel]], lying approximately nineteen kilometres off the coast of [[devon|Devon]]. Small — barely five kilometres in length — yet rugged and distinctive, Lundy occupies a special place in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s taxonomy of islands. It is for him not merely a navigational landmark but a case study in a political philosophy of scale: the proposition that human beings are happier and better governed in small polities.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s encounter with Lundy is both practical and philosophical. He sails past it, struggles against its [[tide|tides]], and gazes upon it from the deck of the *[[nona|Nona]]* — but what stays with him is the idea of Lundy as a self-contained kingdom, one of several such island communities scattered around the British coast.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] first introduces Lundy in Section 1, in the context of a meditation on islands that begins with [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey]]. Reflecting on [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey]]'s tradition of electing its own king, he broadens the observation:

> All the islands around Britain tend to be little kingdoms of their own: Lundy is like that, and over and over again one finds similar islands owned by one man, living either alone like a beast or companioned like a patriarch with his family and his servants and tenants all round about him; and the truth is that man is happier in small kingdoms than in great. [001-030]

This is one of the book's central political assertions, and Lundy serves as its archetypal example. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s preference for the small over the great, the local over the imperial, finds its geographical expression in these offshore islands.

In Section 11, the *[[nona|Nona]]* makes an actual passage past Lundy. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sets a course "for Lundy and beyond, intending a passage through [[bristol-channel|the Bristol Channel]]" [011-034]. The sailing is memorable:

> Before morning, we raised Lundy Light full and clear. But the wind was failing us, and the tide turned against us, so that we endured, gazing at Lundy from the top of each passing wave, for what seemed an eternity. [011-035]

The frustration of being held in sight of the island by contrary wind and tide underscores a sailor's [[truth|reality]]: Lundy is a landmark to be passed, not always a destination to be reached. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reflects on the island's natural defences:

> All the water round Lundy is exactly made for security. [011-038]

The island's isolation is not a weakness but a form of strength — a physical expression of the independence [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] admired.

## Geography & History

Lundy lies at the mouth of [[bristol-channel|the Bristol Channel]], where the waters of the Severn Estuary meet the Atlantic. Its granite mass rises to over 140 metres, with steep cliffs on all sides making it naturally defensible. The island has a long history of quasi-independence: in the medieval period it was a stronghold of the Marisco family, who used it as a base for piracy, and it has changed hands among various private owners through the centuries.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] notes that Lundy is "larger than most" of the small British islands [011-039], distinguishing it from the mere rocks and islets that dot the coast. He also mentions the wooded coast between [[bideford-river|Bideford]] River and Lundy [011-043], evoking the [[devon|Devon]] shoreline from which the island is visible.
