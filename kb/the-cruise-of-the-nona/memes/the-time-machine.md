## Overview

*The Time Machine* (1895) is a [[knowledge|science]]-fiction novella by H. G. Wells, widely regarded as one of the founding texts of the genre. It tells the story of an unnamed [[time|Time]] Traveller who journeys to the year 802,701 and discovers a world divided between the gentle, childlike Eloi and the subterranean, predatory Morlocks — a grim parable of class division projected onto evolutionary time.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] discusses *The Time Machine* in Section 40, within a long meditation on the economics of literary reputation. The immediate context is his observation that there is no reliable relationship between the quality of a book and its sales. Wells serves as [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s prime example:

> "Mr. Wells wrote a book which is most remarkable, and will probably make a mark in English literature. It was called *The Time Machine*. When Henley (I think it was) was bringing it out by instalments, the appearance of each was, for me at least, and for very many others, the chief event of the month." (040-023)

The contrast [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] draws is between *The Time Machine* and Wells's later *Outline of History*, which sold vastly more copies but which [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] dismisses as "a mere repetition of what his audience already thought they knew... full of the popular errors." He continues:

> "At any rate, the book, with all its good and bad qualities, is obviously not of the first class, and equally obviously is not destined to endure, while *The Time Machine* is quite certainly of the first class, and is quite likely to endure." (040-023)

This is [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] the literary critic at his most confident. He distinguishes between books that confirm readers in their existing prejudices (the *Outline of History*) and books that create something new and permanent (*The Time Machine*). The judgment has proved prescient: *The Time Machine* has indeed endured while the *Outline of History* is largely forgotten.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s praise of *The Time Machine* is notable because he was, in most respects, temperamentally opposed to Wells's worldview. Wells was a progressive, a socialist, a believer in scientific rationalism — everything [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] [[fog|mist]]rusted. But [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s literary judgments were independent of his ideological commitments. He recognised first-class imaginative work when he saw it, even from a writer whose philosophy he rejected. This intellectual honesty — the willingness to praise an opponent's achievement — is one of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most attractive qualities as a critic.
