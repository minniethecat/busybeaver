# Portland Harbour

## Overview

Portland Harbour is a large artificial harbour on the Dorset coast of [[england|England]], sheltered behind the [[isle-of-portland]] and [[chesil-cove|the Chesil]] Beach. Built in the nineteenth century as a naval anchorage, it lies on the eastern side of the [[isle-of-portland|Portland]] peninsula, offering p[[corruption|rot]]ected water in contrast to the violent [[portland-race]] that rages off [[portland-bill|Portland Bill]] to the south. Completed in 1872, the harbour was one of the largest artificial harbours in the world at the time of its construction, designed to provide a secure anchorage for the [[the-fleet|Royal Navy]]'s Channel Fleet.

For [[hilaire-belloc]], who sailed the Dorset coast repeatedly in the *[[nona]]*, Portland Harbour represented a rare triumph of human engineering over the [[the-sea|sea]] — a stark contrast to the natural [[danger|peril]]s that surround it. In a book filled with accounts of dangerous waters and treacherous coasts, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s praise for Portland Harbour stands out as one of his few unqualified endorsements of human intervention in the maritime environment.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] approaches Portland Harbour from [[chesil-cove]], the "dreadful cove" on the outer side of [[chesil-cove|the Chesil]] Beach. He describes the strange experience of landing on the exposed western shore and walking only a few minutes to find the calm inner water:

> It is a strange feeling to leave one's little ship at anchor in the dreadful cove, to land as best one can on that steep shore, risking the dinghy under the pounding of the sea, pulling it up over the screeching stones beyond the mark of the tide and then, in a few minutes' walk, to be on the great placid expanse of the new Portland Harbour on the other side. [026-009]

He reflects on the contrast: "It is like coming indoors out of evil weather, into fire[[lighthouse|light]] out of the dark" [026-009]. The domestic simile — harbour as hearth — is carefully chosen. But to reach the harbour by [[the-sea|sea]] requires navigating past the dangers of [[portland-bill|Portland Bill]] and the Race — "you must run down the millrace of the tide along [[portland-bill|the Bill]] and [[danger|risk]] the buffeting of the Race" [026-009]. The harbour's shelter is earned, not given.

## A Rare Success

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s highest praise for the harbour comes in a direct assessment:

> Portland Harbour is one of the very, very rare successes of man in his coaxing of the sea. [027-001]

The repetition of "very" and the phrase "coaxing of the [[the-sea|sea]]" are both significant. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] did not generally admire large engineering works; he was suspicious of modern confidence in taming nature. But Portland Harbour earned his respect because it worked with the natural geography rather than against it.

He notes its successful design — "It has caught an angle and the defences have been made sure. It is deep, yet not too deep, and no gale alarms it" [027-009] — and observes that [[chesil-cove|the Chesil]] Beach itself provides natural p[[corruption|rot]]ection from the prevailing winds. The harbour succeeds, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s analysis, because its builders understood and exploited the existing topography rather than imposing a purely artificial solution upon it.
