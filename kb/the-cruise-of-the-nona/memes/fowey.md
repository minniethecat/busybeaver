# Fowey

## Overview

Fowey is a harbour town on the southern coast of [[cornwall|Cornwall]], situated at the mouth of the River Fowey. In [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s coastal geography of southern [[england|England]], Fowey holds a special significance as the westernmost of the great natural harbours of the English Channel — a line that runs eastward through [[plymouth|Plymouth]], Torbay, and [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]] to [[dover|Dover]].

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s praise for Fowey is characteristically emphatic yet precise. He does not treat it merely as a waypoint or anchorage but as an exemplar of what a harbour should be: a place of shelter shaped by nature rather than by engineers.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] first mentions Fowey in the context of [[cornwall|Cornwall]]'s distinctive coastline, listing it alongside Falmouth and the "miles of inland waterways" that distinguish the Cornish shore [013-030]. But his fullest treatment comes in Section 19, where he introduces Fowey with the weight of accumulated experience:

> Fowey, then, I say, is the most westerly of all those great harbours of the south coast of England. [019-003]

This superlative is not [[lighthouse|light]]ly given. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] had sailed into nearly every major harbour on the English south coast, and his judgment carries the authority of direct comparison. He elaborates:

> Fowey is the harbour of harbours, a perfect natural shelter, deep and winding, guarded by high ground on either side. [019-004]

The passage conveys a sailor's appreciation for a harbour that needs no breakwater, no dredging, no artificial maintenance — a gift of geography rather than of engineering. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] contrasts this with the engineered failures he critiques elsewhere, such as [[dover|Dover Harbour]].

In Section 21, he compares the Fowey coast with Torbay, noting that both are places he has known only under fair conditions — a sailor's admission that even the best harbour can be tested by weather one has not yet encountered there [021-002].

## Geography & History

Fowey lies on [[cornwall|Cornwall]]'s south coast, approximately 30 kilometres west of [[plymouth|Plymouth]]. The River Fowey opens into a deep, sheltered estuary with steep wooded banks on both sides, creating a natural harbour that has been in use since the medieval period. Historically, Fowey was a significant port for the export of tin and china clay, and its [[the-sea|sea]]faring tradition includes the "Fowey Gallants" — privateers who harried [[france|French]] shipping in the Hundred Years' War.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s mention of the "Fowey coast" [021-002] and its relationship to Torbay suggests that he sailed this stretch of [[english-channel|the Channel]] in settled weather, experiencing the [[cornwall|Cornish coast]]line at its most benign.
