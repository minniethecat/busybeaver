## Overview

The Book of Job is one of the [[wisdom|Wisdom]] books of the Old Testament. It tells the story of Job, a righteous man who loses everything — his wealth, his children, his health — and demands an explanation from God. When God finally answers, He does not explain; He overwhelms Job with a torrent of rhetorical questions about the creation of the world, the boundaries of the [[the-sea|sea]], and the mysteries of the natural order. The book is unique in [[holy-writ|the Bible]] for its refusal to offer a comfortable resolution: Job is restored to [[wealth|prosperity]], but his questions are never answered.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] invokes Job in the midst of a long passage about the [[tide|tides]] — one of his obsessive subjects — and the inadequacy of scientific explanations for them:

> "All this questioning sounds like the Book of Job; but, note you, that I, for my part, am with Job, and against the scientists. For Job, or God, or whoever it was who set the catechism, put the questions and was careful to avoid the answers, and for my part I will do the same, not only in the matter of the tides, but for the whole basketful of things on which the scientists have been pontificating with increasing uppishness for the last two hundred years." [017-007]

The passage is a small manifesto of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s epistemology. He admires the Book of Job not for its theology of [[pain|suffering]] but for its rhetorical method — the asking of unanswerable questions as a form of wisdom. The scientists, in his view, pretend to have answers they do not possess; Job, and [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] after him, prefer "the thing itself, [[truth|reality]] ... stated," without theorising. His loyalty is to the Admiralty tide tables, which tell you when the water will rise without pretending to explain why — and to the book of Job, which does the same for human [[pain|suffering]].

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s invocation of Job is characteristic of his [[religion|Catholic]] intellectual style. He was deeply suspicious of reductive explanations — scientific, historical, or political — that claimed to have solved mysteries that were, in his view, insoluble. The Book of Job provided a scriptural warrant for this suspicion: even God, when confronted with Job's demand for an explanation, preferred questions to answers.
