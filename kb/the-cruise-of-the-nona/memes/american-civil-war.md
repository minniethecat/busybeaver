# American Civil War

The American Civil War (1861–1865), fought between the Union and the Confederacy, appears in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* as a turning point in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s analysis of Anglo-American relations. It is not discussed in its own right but invoked as the fulcrum upon which British attitudes toward the United States shifted.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s sole explicit reference to the American Civil War occurs in Section 18, embedded in his extended critique of the "[[american-war-of-independence|American War of Independence]]" myth taught to English schoolchildren. He argues that English public opinion remained hostile to the United States for a century after the Revolution — and that this hostility ended only "when the long agony of the Civil War had turned in favour of the North" [018-540]. The phrasing is significant: [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] characterizes the war as an *agony* — a prolonged, bitter ordeal — and frames its outcome not as a foregone moral victory but as a contingent military result. This was the pivot, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] asserts, after which [[england|England]] found it expedient to invent a mythology of transatlantic kinship.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of the Civil War is entirely instrumental. It serves his larger argument that national mythologies are constructed retroactively to serve political convenience. The war itself — its causes, its battles, its leaders — receives no attention beyond this single reference.

## Wider Context

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s reference forms part of a passage in Section 18 that systematically deconstructs the Whig interpretation of Anglo-American history. Alongside the [[american-war-of-independence|American War of Independence]] and the Civil War, he mentions the [[france|French]] fleet's decisive role at Yorktown and the financial motives behind the Revolution — all [[truth|fact]]s he believed were suppressed by official pedagogy. The Civil War reference completes a triangular argument: the Revolution created the breach, a century of mutual contempt sustained it, and the Northern victory created the conditions for a manu[[truth|fact]]ured "special relationship."

## Belloc's Own Views

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was a prolific commentator on American affairs. He visited the United States several times and w[[corruption|rot]]e extensively about American society, politics, and foreign policy. His view of America was characteristically ambivalent: admiration for individual Americans combined with deep suspicion of American political mythology and what he perceived as a post-war project to subsume British identity within an Anglo-American construct. The reference to the Civil War in the *[[nona|Nona]]* reflects this suspicion — it is the moment, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s telling, when British convenience discovered American virtue.
