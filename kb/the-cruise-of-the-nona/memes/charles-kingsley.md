Charles Kingsley (1819–1875) was a Victorian clergyman, novelist, and controversialist — a figure of "violent fanatical genius," in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s phrase, whose personality was imprinted on the north [[devon|Devon coast]]. His novel *Westward Ho!* (1855) gave its name to the [[the-sea|seas]]ide town at the mouth of [[bideford-river|the Torridge]], and his muscular [[religion|Christianity]] made him one of the most distinctive voices of the mid-Victorian period.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] first invokes Kingsley while the *[[nona|Nona]]* lies at anchor off Appledore, waiting for the tide to carry her up the [[bideford-river|Bideford]] River:

> We brought up in the deep pool off Appledore, waiting for the tide to take us up to Bideford [011-053].

It is from this anchorage that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] surveys the landscape Kingsley made famous:

> All that bit of coast is Charles Kingsley, with his violent fanatical genius (for it was no less) and his power of imprinting himself upon the place. Fanatical men are not usually happy; but I think Kingsley must have been happy, because he had the tide under him all the time [011-054].

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] notes the "startling contrast between Appledore, on the business side of the river, and Instow opposite it" [011-060], a juxtaposition that mirrors the duality in Kingsley himself — the muscular Christian reformer and the romantic novelist.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s portrait is remarkably sympathetic despite their religious differences. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a [[religion|Catholic]], notes that Kingsley's cause was "the destruction of [[the-church|the Faith]]," yet he sees Kingsley as borne along by his times — "Everything in his own lifetime was going his way." He wryly observes that "the s[[lighthouse|light]]est accident the other way (such as [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s conversion) was an intolerable irritant to him" [011-054], a glancing reference to Kingsley's famous public quarrel with [[john-henry-newman]], which led to [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s *Apologia Pro Vita Sua*.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] then digresses on the comic fate of Kingsley's most famous place-name:

> You could get no better and, at the same time, no more comic criticism of that Kingsleian mood than what has happed to the name "Westward Ho," which Kingsley rendered famous. "Westward Ho" simply means the westerly of two landing-places. "Ho," or "Hoe," is a wharf: but that "Ho" has been spread broadcast throughout the world of English as a sort of "Halloa," or "Yoicks," to call the adventurous overseas [011-055].

In Section 41, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] returns to Kingsley — apologising for not having mentioned him at Appledore — to quote a sharp piece of literary criticism. When a woman showed Kingsley some verses, he pronounced: "Madam, there is poetry and there is verse; and verse is divided into two kinds — good verse and bad verse. What you have here shown me is not poetry, it is verse. It is not good verse, it is bad verse" [040-037]. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] uses this anecdote to illustrate the absolute boundary between poetry and non-poetry: "on the one side is It, and on the other is nothingness."

## Kingsley and Newman

The reference to [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s conversion as an "intolerable irritant" to Kingsley [011-054] alludes to one of the most famous literary quarrels of the Victorian era. In 1864, Kingsley publicly attacked [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s truthfulness, provoking [[john-henry-newman|Newman]] to write his *Apologia Pro Vita Sua* — widely regarded as one of the greatest spiritual autobiographies in the English language. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], who admired [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s prose as among the finest of the century, contrasts Kingsley's "violent fanatical genius" with what he saw as [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s steadier intellectual force.

## Legacy

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s assessment of Kingsley is ultimately generous: he grants him genius, however fanatical, and credits him with a rare power — "his power of imprinting himself upon the place." The north [[devon|Devon coast]], for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] as for generations of readers, is Kingsley's country. [[westward-ho]] and the [[bideford-river|Bideford]] River remain monuments to a writer who, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s phrase, "had the tide under him all the time."
