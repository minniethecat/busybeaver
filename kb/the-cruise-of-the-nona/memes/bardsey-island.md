[[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Island]] (Ynys Enlli in [[wales|Welsh]]) lies off the tip of the Llŷn Peninsula in North [[wales|Wales]], separated from the mainland by [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]] — a [[tide|tidal]] passage of considerable danger. For [[hilaire-belloc|Hilaire Belloc]], Bardsey was the site of a near-catastrophic experience that opens the narrative of [[the-cruise-of-the-nona|The Cruise of the Nona]].

## The Bardsey Passage

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s encounter with [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]] occurs in the very first chapter of the book and sets the tone for all that follows. He describes the strait between "the mainland and [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Island]], which is called [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]]" [001-002] as the scene of a desperate struggle. The danger was real:

> I had found myself, some days before, in the last of the darkness, in very great peril indeed off Bardsey, and utterly careless whether I lived or died; it was one of those moments which a man may perhaps go through once in a life and remember as something apart [001-019].

When [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] later lost his dinghy "during the tempest in [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]]" [011-002], the loss was attributed to the same wild weather that had nearly overwhelmed him there.

## Aftermath and Reflection

The exhaustion of [[shakespeare|the Bard]]sey passage was profound. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] woke late the following day, "for the fatigue of Bardsey passage was still on us, even after a long night's rest" [004-001]. The experience convinced him that even familiarity with danger did not diminish its impact — "I had supposed that the memory of the never-to-be-forgotten business of Bardsey would be an advantage" [015-001], but he found that past [[danger|peril]] offered little comfort in present difficulty.

## A Contemplative Shore

Yet Bardsey was not only a place of danger. After the struggle, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] "looked lazily over to Bardsey, with sunset on its streaming cliffs" [001-029], and reflected on the island's history and its name:

> Looking at Bardsey over an evening sea now less tumbled and under the dying gale, I thought also how odd it was that such Scandinavian names should have stuck to some places more than to others. These pirates of the eighth and ninth centuries were not many in number. They had no culture or traditions; no literature; no anything [001-034].

"Bardsey, they say, has a little community of holy men upon it" [001-030], he noted, associating the island with the early Celtic saints who had made it a place of pilgrimage. The island was reputed to be the burial place of twenty thousand saints — "and so again for Bardsey" [001-035], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] added, allowing the weight of that tradition to settle in the reader's mind against the evening [[the-sea|sea]].

## Bardsey Sound as a Benchmark

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] classed [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]] among the great [[tide|tidal]] races of his sailing world. "Many races do I know," he w[[corruption|rot]]e, listing "[[portland-race|Portland Race]] and [[alderney-race|Alderney Race]] and Little Saint Alban's and [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]]" [011-020]. The inclusion of Bardsey in this catalogue of dangerous waters placed it in the first rank of navigational challenges around the British coast.
