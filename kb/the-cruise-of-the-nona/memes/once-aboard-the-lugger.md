## Overview

*Once Aboard the Lugger* (1908) is a novel by the British writer A. S. M. Hutchinson (1879–1971). It was Hutchinson's early work, well received critically but not a spectacular commercial success. Hutchinson would later achieve enormous popularity with *[[if-winter-comes|If Winter Comes]]* (1921), which became one of the bestselling novels of its era.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] mentions *Once Aboard the Lugger* in Section 40, in the course of his meditation on the inexplicable economics of literary success. He is arguing that there is no reliable correlation between a book's quality and its sales, and Hutchinson serves as a case study:

> "Mr. Hutchinson, for whose work I personally have (though I am afraid my judgment is not worth much in these things) a great liking, wrote among other books a book called *Once Aboard the Lugger*. It was exceedingly amusing, fresh, and, as I should have said, well worth anybody's buying. I do not know how many copies he sold of it, but I should imagine nothing very astonishing." (040-022)

The contrast [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] draws is with Hutchinson's later *[[if-winter-comes|If Winter Comes]]*, which "sold by the stack, and by the ton. One could see it piled up man-high upon the counters of shops in America, and it sold, and it sold, and it sold." The better book — in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s judgment — sold modestly; the lesser book became a phenomenon.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s parenthetical modesty about his own critical judgment ("though I am afraid my judgment is not worth much in these things") is characteristic. He offers his opinion not as an authority but as a fellow practitioner of the "difficult trade of letters" — one who has observed the market's caprices from the inside and long since ceased to expect justice from it.

The title *Once Aboard the Lugger* has a nautical flavour appropriate to [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own milieu — a lugger being a type of sailing vessel — though the novel itself is not a [[the-sea|sea]] story but a romantic comedy. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s affection for it may owe something to the title's evocation of the sailing life he loved.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s discussion of Hutchinson is part of a larger argument about literary reputation that runs throughout Section 40. His willingness to praise a commercially unsuccessful book while dismissing a bestseller is consistent with his broader rejection of market logic as a guide to value — a rejection that extended from literature to politics to religion.
