# Homer

## Overview

Homer occupies a special place in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s imaginative economy. He is the poet at the origin of the European tradition — the figure whose very existence must be defended against the same academic debunking that questions the authorship of [[the-bible|the Gospel]]s and the name of the [[battle-of-hastings|Battle of Hastings]]. "That Homer was not written by Homer" (011-056): [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] places this claim second in his catalogue of scholarly absurdities, right after [[the-bible|the Gospel]] of Saint John.

But Homer is more than a polemical target for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]. He is a living presence — a poet whose work [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] knows intimately and cites naturally. When [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes of poetry as something that "inhabits page upon page" (040-037), Homer is the unspoken archetype: the poet who sustained epic intensity across thousands of lines, the master against whom all subsequent poets must be measured.

## Defending Homer

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s defence of Homeric authorship is part of his larger war against academic revisionism. The claim that "Homer was not written by Homer" is for him a perfect specimen of the debunking mentality: a conjecture advanced without evidence, maintained by institutional pressure, and impervious to the testimony of tradition.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not engage the Homeric Question on scholarly grounds. He dismisses it on moral grounds. The scholars who doubt Homer's existence are guilty of the same intellectual vice as those who doubt [[julius-caesar|Caesar]]'s authorship of the Gallic War or the traditional name of the [[battle-of-hastings|Battle of Hastings]] — a "lust" for saying that received truths are false. Against this lust, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sets the testimony of the entire European tradition, which has always known that Homer w[[corruption|rot]]e Homer.

This is not anti-intellectualism. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] respects scholarship — he praises "Rice Holmes' work, in which every movement of [[julius-caesar|Caesar]]'s... is clinched down with iron proof" (038). What he despises is scholarship that has detached itself from common sense and common tradition, that values novelty over truth, and that treats the accumulated wisdom of centuries as so much error to be corrected.

## Homer and the Sea

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s affinity for Homer runs deeper than polemic. Both are poets of the [[the-sea|sea]] — men who know what it is to be in a small boat, dependent on wind and tide, subject to forces beyond human control. When [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] describes the *[[nona|Nona]]* fighting through [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]], or drifting in a calm, or running before a gale, he is writing in the Homeric mode: narrative grounded in the physical [[truth|reality]] of maritime experience.

The *[[iliad|Iliad]]* and *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* are books [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] carries in his head as he sails. He does not need to cite them constantly; they are part of the mental furniture of a man formed by the classical tradition. When he writes of "the distant hosts of [[death|Death]] that march with muffled drum" (027), the echo of Homeric battle narrative is deliberate — a reminder that the oldest poetry remains the most powerful.

## Homer and the European Tradition

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Homer stands at the head of the European literary tradition — the pagan foundation upon which the Christian edifice was later built. He places Homer alongside [[the-bible|the Bible]] and [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] as one of the three great textual pillars of Western civilisation. Each represents a different moment in that civilisation's history: Homer, the classical-pagan origin; [[holy-writ|the Bible]], the Christian transformation; [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]], the vernacular flowering.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s ability to hold these three together — pagan, Christian, modern — without tension or apology is one of the most attractive features of his thought. He does not need to choose between Homer and [[holy-writ|the Bible]], or between [[holy-writ|the Bible]] and [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]]. They belong to the same continuous tradition, and to lose any one of them is to impoverish the whole.
