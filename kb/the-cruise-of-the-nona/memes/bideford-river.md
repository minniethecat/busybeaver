The Bideford River — properly the estuary of the River Torridge — is a [[tide|tidal]] harbour on the north coast of [[devon|Devon]]. For [[hilaire-belloc|Hilaire Belloc]], it was the satisfying conclusion of a challenging west-country passage and a place he described with more affection than almost any other English harbour in [[the-cruise-of-the-nona|The Cruise of the Nona]].

## The Passage from Wales

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s approach to Bideford River came at the end of a long leg from [[wales|Wales]]. After rounding [[lundy|Lundy]] and crossing the tumbling water between the island and the mainland [011-043], he set his course with evident relief:

> We put her nose round for Bideford River, with this pleasant little wind just abaft the beam [011-040].

The phrase "pleasant little wind" captures the shift in mood — from the tense navigation of the [[wales|Welsh]] passages to the easier sailing of the [[devon|Devon coast]]. After the ordeals of [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]] and the long crossing of [[cardigan-bay|Cardigan Bay]], Bideford represented arrival, rest, and the satis[[truth|fact]]ion of a passage completed.

## The Entrance

The entrance required careful timing of the tide. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] ran "straight for Bideford River, till we came to the fairway buoy" [011-052] and then waited "for the tide to take us up to Bideford" [011-053].

Like many of the harbours [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] frequented, local [[tide|tidal]] knowledge was essential — the estuary was navigable only on the flood, and a [[fog|mist]]imed approach would leave a vessel aground on the mudflats that fringe the Torridge.

> Beyond, intending a passage to Bideford River, and the ending of this book [011-034].

This line reveals that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] had conceived of Bideford as the narrative terminus from early in the voyage. The passage to Bideford was not merely a leg among others; it was the destination toward which the whole cruise had been tending.

## A Harbour of Character

Bideford River impressed [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] with its striking contrasts — between the "startling fecundity" of the southern shore and the northern shore's "barren" quality [011-060]. He also noted with dark humour "the deadly hatred with which the people of one bank regard the people of the other" [011-055].

This local peculiarity de[[lighthouse|light]]ed him. The division between the two shores of the Torridge — fertile south, barren north, and a population divided by ancient animosity — was exactly the sort of human detail that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] gathered as carefully as any navigational sounding.

> Another pleasing thing about Bideford River is the startling contrast between the southern shore and the northern [011-060].

## Arrival

Coming to anchor before the long bridge at Bideford [012-001], [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] declared the passage complete with a sense of finality:

> Bideford River is the end of my passage. Here am I in Bideford River, at anchor and come to harbour [012-002, 012-009].

The repetition — "at anchor and come to harbour" — has the cadence of a sailor savouring the moment. After the long miles from [[holyhead|Holyhead]], through the [[tide|tidal]] gates of [[wales|Wales]] and across the open water to [[devon|Devon]], [[nona|the Nona]] was still and her crew at rest beneath the old stone bridge.

## Commercial History

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] noted Bideford's place in the vanished maritime economy of the West Country, grouping it with "Saint Ives, Padstow, and Bideford River" as ports whose wealth "seems to have melted" [013-030].

This was part of his broader meditation on the decline of the small English harbour in the age of steam and rail. In an earlier century, Bideford had been a significant port — sending ships to the Newfoundland fisheries and trading across the Atlantic. By [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s time, the commerce had gone, leaving only the quiet beauty of the estuary and the memory of busier days.
