# Winston Churchill

## Overview

Winston Leonard Spencer Churchill (1874–1965) was a British statesman, soldier, and writer who served as First Lord of the Admiralty at the outbreak of the Great War. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], writing in 1925 with the benefit of hindsight, singles out Churchill for a decisive act that may have saved [[england|Britain]]: the pre-emptive mobilisation of [[the-fleet|the Fleet]].

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s judgment of Churchill appears in his extended meditation on the origins of the Great War (Section 20). Surveying the cascade of blunders and misjudgments that led to the catastrophe, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] identifies two men who "deserved well of their country at that moment" (020-014):

> Two men, I think, deserved well of their country at that moment, granted that an entry into the war sooner or later was inevitable. One was Mr. Winston Churchill, who upon his own initiative mobilised the Fleet; and the other was Lord Haldane, who convinced his colleagues of the nature of the peril.

The phrase "upon his own initiative" is crucial. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is describing a man who acted without waiting for consensus, who understood the gravity of the moment and made the decision that others would not or could not make. For a writer as skeptical of politicians as [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], this is high praise indeed.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] pairs Churchill with [[lord-haldane|Lord Haldane]], whose pre-war army reforms "made possible the cooperation of [[england|Britain]] in the war" (020-014). The pairing is strategic: Churchill at the Admiralty and Haldane at [[great-war|the War]] Office represented the two pillars of [[british-army|British military]] readiness, and both men acted when it mattered most.

## Life & Context

Churchill had become First Lord of the Admiralty in 1911, and in the [[time|years]] before the war he had overseen the conversion of the [[the-fleet|Royal Navy]] from coal to oil, the development of the Dreadnought programme, and the creation of the Royal Naval Air Service. His order to keep [[the-fleet|the Fleet]] concentrated after the 1914 summer manoeuvres — rather than dispersing as was [[tradition|custom]]ary — meant that when war came, the [[the-fleet|Royal Navy]] was already at battle stations.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] and Churchill were contemporaries — born the same year, 1874 — and moved in overlapping circles of [[london|London]] political and literary life, though there is no evidence of personal friendship between them. Churchill had crossed the floor from the Conservatives to the Liberals in 1904; [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] had been a Liberal MP himself from 1906 to 1910. By 1925, Churchill had rejoined the Conservatives as Chancellor of the Exchequer — a position he held when [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] published the *[[nona|Nona]]*.
