# Julius Caesar

## Overview

Julius Caesar appears in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* in two distinct guises: as a victim of academic pedantry and as a fellow-traveller along the English coast. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] first mentions him in a furious passage about scholarly debunking — the claim that "Julius Caesar's Gallic War was written by his tutor" (011-056) — and then returns to him at the [[kent|Kentish]] shore, offering "a few kind words to Saint Augustine, and Julius Caesar" (038-001) as he sails past the ancient landing places.

These two appearances, brief as they are, reveal a great deal about [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s historical imagination. Caesar is for him a real presence — a man who landed on the same beaches [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sails past, whose words can still be read, and whose integrity as an author is worth defending against the university-bred [[doubt|scepticism]] [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] so despises.

## Against the Debunkers

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s first mention of Caesar comes in the midst of a sustained polemic against academic revisionism. He lists, with mounting exasperation, the claims he has encountered: "that [[the-bible|the Gospel]] of Saint John was not written by Saint John? that [[homer|Homer]] was not written by [[homer|Homer]]? that the [[battle-of-hastings|Battle of Hastings]] was not called the [[battle-of-hastings|Battle of Hastings]]? ... that Julius Caesar's Gallic War was written by his tutor — and all the rest of the nonsense" (011-056).

The passage is comic in its accumulation, but the argument is serious. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sees the debunking impulse as a pathology of modern intellectual life — a "lust" (011-056) for saying that received truths are false, regardless of the evidence. The claim about Caesar's authorship is for him a perfect specimen: a conjecture with no basis in the sources, advanced solely because it contradicts tradition.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s defence of Caesar's authorship is not scholarly but moral. He trusts the chain of testimony — the people who were there, who read the documents, who knew the truth. "His secretary, who saw them, and read all the documents connected with them, gives us fifty thousand" (011-056): the specificity of the number is the point. Real history deals in particulars; academic debunking deals in abstractions.

## Landing on the Kentish Shore

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s second Caesar reference is gentler. Sailing past the [[kent|Kentish coast]], he offers "a few kind words" (038-001) to the men who have landed there — Caesar among them. The phrase is affectionate, almost domestic. Caesar is not here the conqueror of Gaul or the destroyer of the Republic, but simply a man who once beached his ships on the same shingle [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] now sails past.

This is characteristic of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s historical sensibility. The past is not distant; it is contiguous with the present, accessible through place. "I know not what that fascination is which attaches to seeing, touching, standing on the very site of some great business of [[tradition|the past]]" (040), [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes elsewhere in the book. Caesar matters not only as a figure in books but as a man who stood on English ground — ground [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] can still see from his cockpit.

## Caesar and the Classical Inheritance

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s invocation of Caesar is part of a larger pattern in the book: the constant presence of classical antiquity as a living reference. Caesar shares the [[kent|Kentish]] landfall with [[saint-augustine|Saint Augustine]], the Christian missionary who landed there six centuries later. Pagan and Christian, conqueror and saint — [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] places them side by side without tension, because both belong to the continuous European tradition he is defending.

This easy intimacy with antiquity is one of the book's most attractive features. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not need to explain who Caesar is; he assumes his reader knows, and he assumes that knowing Caesar is part of being civilised. The assumption itself is an argument — against the narrowing of education, against the forgetting of the classical past, against a modernity that treats Caesar as a specialist's concern rather than a common [[tradition|inheritance]].
