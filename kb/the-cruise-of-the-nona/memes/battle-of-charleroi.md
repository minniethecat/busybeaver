# Battle of Charleroi

## Overview

The Battle of Charleroi (21–23 August 1914) was one of the Battles of the Frontiers that opened the [[great-war|Great War]] on the Western Front. The German 2nd and 3rd Armies engaged the [[france|French]] 5th Army along the Sambre River near Charleroi, Belgium, resulting in a [[france|French]] defeat and a general retreat that would culminate at the Marne.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] references Charleroi twice in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, in two distinct contexts — once as a strategic benchmark in Prussian pre-war planning, and once as the direct consequence of a domestic political scandal a decade earlier.

## Belloc's Account

In Section 19, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] reconstructs what Prussia expected from the opening campaign. The calculation, revealed only after the war, was chilling in its confidence:

> Prussia firmly calculated on two things: first, the neutrality of England, and, secondly, a very complete and very rapid victory over the French, a decisive victory, destroying the whole of the French army within a month of the frontier crossing; a battle beginning as did Charleroi, but ending very differently from the Marne. (019-022)

The phrase is carefully chosen — "a battle beginning as did Charleroi" acknowledges the German tactical success at Charleroi (the [[france|French]] 5th Army was indeed driven back), while "ending very differently from the Marne" marks the point where the Prussian plan collapsed. Charleroi was the beginning Germany wanted; the Marne was the ending Germany got.

In Section 28, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] makes a more provocative claim: the German surprise at Charleroi was not merely a military success but the long-delayed consequence of the [[dreyfus-affair|Dreyfus Affair]], which "destroyed the [[france|French]] Intelligence Bureau and so permitted the German surprise on Mons and Charleroi" (028-044). On this reading, the battlefield defeat of August 1914 was prepared not in the German general staff but in the courtrooms and newspaper offices of the [[france|French]] Third Republic.

## Historical Context

The Battle of Charleroi was part of the larger Battle of the Frontiers (14–24 August 1914), in which France executed Plan XVII — a frontal offensive into Alsace-Lorraine — while Germany executed the Schlieffen Plan, swinging through Belgium in a vast wheeling movement. The [[france|French]] 5th Army under General Lanrezac was positioned to meet the German right wing but was caught by surprise, outnumbered, and driven into retreat. Lanrezac's decision to withdraw, though controversial, may have saved the [[france|French]] army from envelopment and made possible the eventual victory at the Marne.

## Significance

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s pairing of the two Charleroi references — strategic overconfidence in Section 19, institutional self-sabotage in Section 28 — forms a compact thesis about how wars are lost. Germany won at Charleroi because it had planned for exactly that battle; France lost because it had destroyed the intelligence service that might have warned of it. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] presents this not as tragic accident but as a chain of causation, running from the passions of the [[alfred-dreyfus|Dreyfus]] [[time|years]] to the German guns on the Sambre.
