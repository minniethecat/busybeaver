# Ireland

## Overview

Ireland is the large island lying to the west of Great [[england|Britain]], separated from it by the Irish Sea. Throughout *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] returns to Ireland not as a destination of his voyage — the *[[nona|Nona]]* never crosses the Irish Sea — but as a constant presence on the western horizon, both geographical and moral. From the [[wales|Welsh coast]], the mountains of Ireland are visible across the water, and this physical proximity becomes, for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a metaphor for an unresolved historical and spiritual relationship.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s treatment of Ireland is unusual among sailing memoirs: it is less a navigational reference than a meditation on nationhood, [[religion|faith]], and the consequences of political failure. The island functions in the text as "that most difficult of subjects, most intriguing of friends, most tenacious of enemies" [007-013], a presence that haunts the English con[[knowledge|science]].

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] first invokes Ireland in a theological context, describing the long conflict between [[england|England]] and Ireland as "essentially a theological struggle" [001-051]. This framing — that political conflicts are at root conflicts of doctrine — is characteristic of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s historical vision.

The most sustained meditation on Ireland comes in Section 7, when the tolling of a bell buoy off the [[wales|Welsh coast]] leads [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] to reflect on the "Sarn Badrig" — the Causeway of Saint Patrick — a submerged ridge stretching from [[wales|Wales]] toward Ireland:

> How right they were to call that sunken ridge pointing straight to Ireland "The Roman Road of Patrick"! And how its sudden cessation symbolises the break, the wound, the rupture, by which this island was cut off from that: more than two hundred years ago! [007-011]

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sees in this geological feature a symbol of a connection never completed — "that Causeway was never completed: that bridge was never built" [007-017] — and he lays the responsibility for this failure squarely on [[england|England]]. The passage builds to one of the book's most charged declarations:

> The mountains look upon the mountains: the mountains of Britain to the mountains of Ireland; from the east to the west, over a magic sea. But they look in wrath and remain sullen giants. Nor is the battle concluded. [007-014]

## Geography & History

Ireland lies approximately 80 kilometres west of Great [[england|Britain]] at the closest point, with the Irish Sea and St George's Channel between them. From the [[wales|Welsh coast]] near [[carnarvon|Carnarvonshire]], where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] sailed the *[[nona|Nona]]*, the mountains of Ireland are visible on clear days — a sight he records with the phrase "the mountains of [[england|Britain]] to the mountains of Ireland" [007-014].

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s historical perspective on Ireland is deeply [[religion|Catholic]] and counter-revisionist. He invokes Saint Patrick as a witness "to the more than natural quality of Ireland" [007-012], quoting the "Confessio" where Patrick writes of hearing "the voices of the Irish calling" in his dreams. He also touches on the [[oliver-cromwell|Cromwell]]ian conquest, describing it bluntly as "lust, loot, and massacre in Ireland under [[oliver-cromwell|Cromwell]]'s leadership" [011-033], and reflects on the political failures of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, including what he calls "the manifest betrayal of Ireland by the politicians" [007-023].

The passage ends on a note of unresolved tension: "the weight, the future consequence, of such a failure lies not upon Ireland, but (alas!) upon [[england|England]]" [007-017].
