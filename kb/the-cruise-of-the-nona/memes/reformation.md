# English Reformation

The English Reformation — the series of events in the 16th century by which [[the-church|the Church]] of [[england|England]] broke away from the authority of the Pope and the Roman [[the-church|Catholic Church]] — appears in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* as a historical dividing line. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a devout [[religion|Catholic]] and lifelong controversialist on religious questions, references the Reformation not as a theological event but as a political transformation that severed the popular connection between the English monarchy and its subjects.

## In the *Nona*

The sole reference occurs in Section 13, during [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s meditation on [[france|French]] colonial administration in Morocco. Contrasting the [[france|French]] approach to native governance with historical precedents, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] remarks parenthetically that "P[[corruption|rot]]estant [[england|England]] after the Reformation had destroyed popular monarchy" [013-014]. The construction is telling: [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not say the Reformation *changed* or *reformed* popular monarchy — he says it *destroyed* it. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the pre-Reformation English monarchy was a popular institution, embedded in the religious culture of the people; after the Reformation, the monarchy became a detached administrative apparatus.

This single line carries enormous weight in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s worldview. The "popular monarchy" he mourns is the medieval [[religion|Catholic]] kingship he idealized throughout his historical writings — a vision elaborated at length in works like *The Servile State* (1912) and *Europe and [[the-church|the Faith]]* (1920). The *[[nona|Nona]]* passage is a compressed fragment of a much larger thesis.

## Wider Context

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] places the Reformation reference within a discussion of [[france|French]] Morocco, but the argumentative logic connects it to his broader critique of modern governance. Section 13 examines France's mission in North Africa; the Reformation aside serves as a historical benchmark for what was lost in [[england|England]]'s own political development. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s admiration for [[france|French]] colonial policy — which he saw as preserving indigenous culture and [[religion|faith]] — implicitly contrasts with what he viewed as [[england|England]]'s destruction of its own [[religion|Catholic]] past.
