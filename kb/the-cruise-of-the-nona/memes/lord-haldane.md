# Lord Haldane

## Overview

Richard Burdon Haldane, 1st Viscount Haldane (1856–1928), was a British lawyer, philosopher, and statesman who served as Secretary of State for War from 1905 to 1912. His most significant legacy was the reorganisation of [[british-army|the British Army]] — creating the Territorial Force and the Expeditionary Force structure that would prove essential when the [[world-war-i|Great War]] broke out in 1914.

[[hilaire-belloc]] names Haldane as one of only two men who "deserved well of their country" [020-012] at the moment of [[england|Britain]]'s entry into the war, crediting him both for his prescient military reforms and for persuading his cabinet colleagues of the gravity of the German [[danger|threat]].

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] introduces Haldane in the context of a sustained analysis of the origins of the [[world-war-i|Great War]], arguing that while German militarism bore primary responsibility, the question of British intervention was not straightforward. He names two figures who rose to the moment:

> Two men, I think, deserved well of their country at that moment, granted that an entry into the war sooner or later was inevitable. One was Mr. Winston Churchill, who upon his own initiative mobilised the Fleet; and the other was Lord Haldane, who convinced his colleagues of the nature of the peril and who, it should be remembered, had made possible the cooperation of Britain in the war by his scheme for mobilisation. [020-012]

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] emphasises that Haldane's reforms were accomplished "at a time when, to the great bulk of his countrymen, the idea of such a conflict was still quite unfamiliar and its enormous implications incredible" [020-012] — a recognition of foresight that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], writing a decade after the [[armistice|Armistice]], clearly regarded as underappreciated.

## The Haldane Reforms

Haldane's tenure at [[great-war|the War]] Office (1905–1912) produced the most significant reorganisation of [[british-army|the British Army]] since the Cardwell Reforms of the 1870s. He created the British Expeditionary Force — a professional, rapidly deployable field army — and the Territorial Force, a part-time volunteer reserve. These structures were precisely what enabled [[england|Britain]] to commit an expeditionary force to France within days of the declaration of war in August 1914.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s praise is notable partly because he rarely praised politicians, and partly because Haldane's reputation was later damaged by unjust accusations of pro-German sympathy — Haldane had studied German philosophy and spoke the language fluently, [[truth|fact]]s that were used against him in the wartime press. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s 1925 assessment may be read as a partial rehabilitation, insisting that Haldane's pre-war efforts made him one of the few who "deserved well of their country."

## Philosophical Work

Beyond politics, Haldane was a serious philosopher and a leading exponent of British Idealism. He translated Schopenhauer and Hegel into English, and his Gifford Lectures, published as *The Reign of Relativity* (1921), explored the philosophical implications of Einstein's theories. This intellectual depth set him apart from most of his parliamentary contemporaries and may explain [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s respect — [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], himself a historian of ideas, recognised a mind of genuine philosophical range.
