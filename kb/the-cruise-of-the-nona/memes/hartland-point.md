Hartland Point is a bold headland on the north coast of [[devon|Devon]], marking the western entrance to [[bristol-channel|the Bristol Channel]]. For [[hilaire-belloc|Hilaire Belloc]], it was a place of singular importance in [[the-cruise-of-the-nona|The Cruise of the Nona]] — the boundary between the sheltered waters of [[english-channel|the Channel]] and the open Atlantic beyond, and a landmark that he invested with classical dignity and personal significance.

## The Point of Hercules

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] gave Hartland a pedigree reaching back to antiquity. "The Romans called it the Point of Hercules," he noted, connecting the [[devon|Devonshire]] headland to the ancient world [013-003]. The name reflected the scale of the place:

> The mass and strength and height of Hartland [013-003] were unlike anything else on that coast.

The invocation of Hercules — the god of strength and boundaries — was apt. Hartland marked the western limit of the known and sheltered world for a sailor coming down [[bristol-channel|the Bristol Channel]]. Beyond it lay the Atlantic, the long [[cornwall|Cornwall]] coast, and the open [[the-sea|sea]].

## Gateway to the Outer Sea

Hartland Point marked a navigational threshold. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] set sail from [[bideford-river|Bideford River]] with the express intention "to explore the outer [[the-sea|sea]] beyond Hartland, which till then I had not seen" [013-001].

The phrase "which till then I had not seen" is striking — [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the experienced Channel sailor, admitting that there was coast beyond Hartland that was new to him. The point was not merely a landmark but a frontier of his own experience.

His approach was dramatic. After "fetching up towards Hartland Point in two long boards" [013-002] through the night, he rounded the headland at day[[lighthouse|light]] and "opened the coast beyond" — a moment of revelation that he recalled vividly. The "two long boards" evoke the tedious, repetitive work of beating to windward in a small sailing boat, and the dawn rounding of the point as the reward for hours of patient labour.

## Savage Grandeur

What struck [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] most about Hartland was its aesthetic power. He w[[corruption|rot]]e of "the savage grandeur of Hartland" [013-004]:

> The noble isolation of Hartland, where the great seas were running [011-045].

The phrase "savage grandeur" captures both the physical scale of the cliffs and the emotional response they provoked. In a book full of descriptions of the English coast, Hartland received some of the most emphatic language — a measure of the impression it made on a sailor who had seen most of what the British shoreline had to offer.

Hartland belonged as much to [[the-sea|the ocean]] as to the land. The "great [[the-sea|seas]]" running past it were the Atlantic swells that had crossed three thousand miles of open water to break against this corner of [[devon|Devon]]. For a sailor in a small boat, the point was a place of power.

## The Wind off Hartland

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] recorded a memorable detail about the wind patterns around the headland. After leaving [[lundy|Lundy]] astern, he caught "a little slant of wind, from off Hartland Point" [011-040] that carried him toward [[bideford-river|Bideford]].

This small meteorological observation — the way a headland can generate its own local wind, deflecting the broader airflow — is typical of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s attentive [[the-sea|sea]]manship. For a sailor without an engine, such a "little slant of wind" could make the difference between a fair passage and a long beat.
