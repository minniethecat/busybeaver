# Treaty of Versailles

## Overview

The Treaty of Versailles hovers at the edge of *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* — rarely named directly, but everywhere present as the political context in which [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes. Signed in 1919, the treaty ended the [[great-war|Great War]] on terms that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] regarded as both vindictive and strategically foolish. His references to it are brief but charged, and they connect the treaty to the larger themes of the book: the failure of [[england|England]]'s governing class, the unreliability of [[the-press|the press]], and the danger of a politics disconnected from [[truth|reality]].

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] w[[corruption|rot]]e the book in the mid-1920s, when the consequences of Versailles were becoming visible: a humiliated Germany, a vindictive France, an exhausted [[england|England]], and an America that had retreated into isolation. His judgement, implicit throughout, is that the peace was as badly managed as the war — perhaps worse, since the war at least had been fought to a conclusion, while the peace seemed designed to ensure another war.

## The Political Context

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most direct engagement with the Versailles settlement comes in his discussions of the English political class. He describes "the prolongation of the war and the making of the peace" (020-001) as a single catastrophe — not two separate events but a continuum of failure. The same men who had drifted into war, who had mismanaged its conduct, and who had misled the public about its progress, were the men who negotiated the peace.

The result, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s analysis, was a treaty that satisfied no one. France got security without reconciliation; Germany got humiliation without destruction; [[england|England]] got exhaustion without gain. "I found the air to be filled with a kind of madness" (019-014) — [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s description of returning to [[london|London]] after a spell at [[the-sea|sea]] applies equally to the political atmosphere in which Versailles was negotiated.

## The Press and the Peace

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s critique of [[the-press|the press]] — a recurring theme throughout the book — is sharpest in connection with Versailles. The [[the-press|newspapers]], he argues, had misrepresented the war to the public; they now misrepresented the peace. The public was told that Germany had been crushed, that the terms were just, that a new era of international cooperation had begun. None of this was true, and [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] knew it.

His [[doubt|scepticism]] about [[the-press|the press]] is not a side issue but central to his analysis of Versailles. A treaty negotiated in secret, presented to the public through a compliant press, and never honestly debated in [[parliament]] — this, for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], was the very model of how modern government fails. The [[treaty-of-versailles|Treaty of Versailles]] was not an aberration but the logical outcome of the institutional failures he anatomises throughout the book.

## Belloc's Judgement

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not offer a detailed critique of the treaty's terms. That is not his genre. What he offers instead is something more valuable: a diagnosis of the political culture that produced the treaty. [[england]] was governed by men who "did not understand their own doom" (007-029); the same men, or their successors, negotiated the peace. The result was predictable.

His judgement is made explicit in a passage about nations and mortal choices: "in matters of life and death, a nation, like an individual, must choose" (020-006). [[england|England]] chose — or rather, its governors chose on its behalf — and the choice was wrong. The treaty that was supposed to secure peace ensured only an armed truce. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] does not say "I told you so," but the satis[[truth|fact]]ion is audible beneath the prose.
