# Battle of the Marne

The **First Battle of the Marne** was a major engagement early in the
[[world-war-i|First World War]], fought between 5–12 September 1914 along the
Marne River in [[france|France]]. It marked the failure of the German Schlieffen
Plan and the beginning of trench warfare on the Western Front.

## Strategic Context

In August 1914, German armies swept through [[belgium|Belgium]] and northern
[[france|France]] according to the Schlieffen Plan, which aimed to encircle
[[paris|Paris]] and knock France out of the war within six weeks. By early
September, the [[german-general-staff|German First Army]] under
[[alexander-von-kluck|von Kluck]] had reached the Marne River, within sight
of [[paris|Paris]]. The [[french-army|French]] commander,
[[joseph-joffre|General Joffre]], ordered a counter-offensive, supported by
the [[british-army|British Expeditionary Force]] under
[[john-french|Sir John French]].

## The Battle

The battle unfolded along a front stretching from [[paris|Paris]] to
[[verdun|Verdun]]. The decisive moment came when the German First and Second
Armies created a gap between them, which the [[british-army|BEF]] and the
[[french-army|French Fifth Army]] exploited. The Germans were forced to retreat
to the Aisne River, where they dug in. The subsequent "Race to the Sea" extended
the front to [[english-channel|the Channel]] coast, creating the trench system that would define the
[[world-war-i|Western Front]] for the next four [[time|years]].

## Belloc's Account

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] mentions the Battle of the Marne in several contexts within
*The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*:

> …just after the Battle of the Marne. It is in a little, somewhat …
> — [010-008]

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] visited the battlefield shortly after the engagement, and his
firsthand knowledge of the terrain informs his writing. The battle is
referenced geographically as well — the town of Châlons lies upon the Marne
River [P04-010], anchoring the narrative in real places [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] knew.
He contrasts the Marne with another major engagement of the opening weeks,
noting that [[battle-of-charleroi|Charleroi]] "began as did Charleroi, but
ended very differently" [019-022] — a reference to the [[truth|fact]] that Charleroi was
a [[france|French]] defeat, while the Marne was a decisive Allied victory.

## Aftermath and Significance

The Battle of the Marne saved [[paris|Paris]] and denied Germany the quick
victory its war plan required. With the German armies entrenched on the Aisne,
the [[world-war-i|war]] settled into a stalemate that would last four [[time|years]] and
cost millions of lives. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], writing during the war itself, the Marne
represented both the resilience of [[france|France]] and the awful dawning
realization that the conflict would not be resolved quickly. His proximity to
the events — as a journalist, a former soldier, and a man who knew the terrain —
gives his references a weight beyond the merely journalistic.
