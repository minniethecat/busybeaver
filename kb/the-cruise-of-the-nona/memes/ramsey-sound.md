Ramsey Sound is the [[tide|tidal]] strait separating Ramsey Island from the Pembrokeshire mainland in southwest [[wales|Wales]]. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] passed through it during his [[wales|Welsh]] cruise and described it as one of the most dramatic [[tide|tidal]] passages he encountered. The Sound lies in the southern corner of [[wales|Wales]], part of a concentration of navigational [[danger|hazard]]s that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] regarded as uniquely dangerous in British coastal waters.

## A Millrace of a Strait

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s description of Ramsey Sound is vivid and precise. He called it "the narrow millrace of a strait between Ramsey Island and the mainland" [011-011], capturing the sense of water forced through a constriction at dangerous speed. The metaphor of the millrace — water channelled into a narrow course, moving with unnatural velocity — recurs throughout his writing about [[tide|tidal]] passages, but nowhere more aptly than here.

The passage demanded "ticklish management" [011-013], the kind of close attention to helm and tide that separated experienced sailors from novices. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] confessed that the concentration required left no room for detached observation: with "this ticklish management of Ramsey Sound, I was too much taken up with the passage itself to note anything else" [011-013].

The physical sensation stayed with him: "the narrow roaring river of Ramsey Sound" [011-014] — a phrase that conveys both the confined channel and the noise of water in violent motion. He was struck by the question of why such a passage should exist at all: "Why on earth does it strike one as so much more grand and fearful than anything of the kind elsewhere?" [011-014].

## Emerging in Triumph

For all its danger, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s passage through Ramsey Sound ended well. He "came therefore grandly out of Ramsey Sound with a sweep and dignity" [011-016], the boat carried through by the tide and emerging into more open water beyond. The contrast between the constricted struggle within the Sound and the "grand" exit is characteristic of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s narrative rhythm — danger overcome, followed by relief and a moment of quiet pride in [[the-sea|sea]]manship well executed.

## Among the Welsh Perils

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] placed Ramsey Sound in the catalogue of [[danger|hazard]]s that made the southern corner of [[wales|Wales]] a uniquely dangerous cruising ground. He listed together "Jack Sound and Ramsey Sound and the Bishop and his clerks, and, worst of all, [[wild-goose-race|Wild Goose]] Race" [011-018] — a litany of navigational challenges that he attributed not to chance but to a kind of divine malevolence: "Someone here in these places, some early captain, first sailing offended the gods of the [[the-sea|sea]]" [011-018].

The memory of Ramsey stayed with him. Later in the book, encountering another tideway, he noted the "torrent of a tideway between Ramsey Island and the mainland" [027-022] as a point of comparison — evidence that the passage had impressed itself permanently on his navigational memory. It became one of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s fixed reference points for describing violent water, alongside the [[portland-race|Portland Race]] and the [[alderney-race|Alderney Race]].
