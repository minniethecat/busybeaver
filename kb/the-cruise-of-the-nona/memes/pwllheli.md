# Pwllheli

## Overview

Pwllheli is a small harbour town on the southern coast of the Lleyn Peninsula in North [[wales|Wales]]. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] approaches it on the opening night of the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s cruise, seeking shelter after departing [[holyhead|Holyhead]]. He describes it as "a curious little bottle harbour… with an odd entry up a sort of canal-like entrance" [003-001] — a place that, from the [[the-sea|sea]], barely announces itself as a harbour at all.

The Pwllheli episode is one of the *[[nona|Nona]]*'s most memorable comic set-pieces. It is not the harbour itself that matters — [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] spends barely a night there — but the encounter it provides with a local character whose misanthropic fury at the modern world provides a perfect counterpoint to [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own ambivalence about progress.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s approach to Pwllheli is narrated with the precision of a [[pilot|pilotage]] instruction. "There is no hole in the land along that coast between the point of [[carnarvon|Carnarvonshire]] and Portmadoc, except" Pwllheli [003-001]. The *[[nona|Nona]]* beats up toward it through the evening, "barely drifting under the [[lighthouse|light]]est of airs," until "a draft of tide took us in" [003-001]. It is the kind of entrance that rewards local knowledge — and punishes those who lack it.

At the mouth of the harbour, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] encounters a figure who will dominate the rest of the episode: a man on the quay "smoking a pipe and looking at us with an expression of profound disgust" [003-004]. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] leans over the rail to ask where they should lie in Pwllheli. The man "pulled the pipe out of his mouth and cursed Pwllheli loudly, in English, but in English so [[wales|Welsh]] that it reminded me of the famous story of the man who said, 'There will be folk in [[london|London]] today: one hundred and twenty thousand' " [003-004] — multiplying the population figure with Celtic extravagance.

The source of the man's grievance, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] discovers, is that "a financier had persuaded the people of Pwllheli to turn it into an earthly paradise, with a band upon an island, and gondolas or whatnot" [003-005] — and this just after the man had bought a cottage, so that he was "burdened with rates." It is a grievance that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] records with evident sympathy: the small harbour transformed by speculative development, the local man caught between the old world and the new.

The episode ends in characteristic anticlimax. "I think he hated all mankind as well as Pwllheli," [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] concludes, "and when we got into the broader water at the end of the entry and were moving up to the wharf in stately fashion, he gave me another surly sign to cast him off, which I did, and then, without a 'good evening,' he pulled for the shore" [003-006]. The *[[nona|Nona]]* is left to make her own way to the wharf — an unceremonious welcome to a town that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] would never, one suspects, make a point of visiting again.

## Geography & History

Pwllheli stands at the head of a shallow [[tide|tidal]] harbour on the south coast of the Lleyn Peninsula. Its name means "salt-water pool" in [[wales|Welsh]]. Historically a fishing port and shipbuilding centre, it became a [[the-sea|seas]]ide resort in the late nineteenth century — the transformation that so outraged [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s pipe-smoking informant. The harbour entrance is narrow and [[tide|tidal]]ly constrained, exactly as [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] describes. The town remains a centre of [[wales|Welsh]]-language culture and the unofficial capital of the Lleyn.
