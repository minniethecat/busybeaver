## Overview

John Henry Newman (1801–1890) was the most consequential English [[religion|Catholic]] of the nineteenth century. An [[oxford-university|Oxford]] fellow and Anglican clergyman who helped lead the [[oxford-university|Oxford]] Movement, his conversion to Roman [[religion|Catholicism]] in 1845 shook the English religious establishment. He was created a cardinal in 1879 and canonised a saint in 2019. His spiritual autobiography, *Apologia Pro Vita Sua* (1864), and his theological essay on the development of Christian doctrine remain landmarks of English religious prose.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a generation [[youth|young]]er, grew up in Newman's shadow. In *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]*, he invokes Newman three times: as the signal instance of [[religion|Catholic]] conversion, as a benchmark of English prose, and — with a touch of personal confession — as a writer whose own merits [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] feels compelled to judge honestly.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s first reference to Newman concerns conversion — the act of changing one's religious allegiance that [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] understood both intellectually and personally. Writing of the P[[corruption|rot]]estant establishment's deep anxiety about [[religion|Catholic]] resurgence, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] notes:

> …the slightest accident the other way (such as Newman's conversion) was an intolerable irritant to him. [011-054]

The word "intolerable" captures the scandal that Newman represented: not merely an individual changing churches but the intellectual leader of Anglican [[oxford-university|Oxford]] abandoning the Reformation settlement itself.

The second and third references occur within a long passage on the art of English prose in Section 40. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is making the case that good prose is characterised by clarity and honest statement, not by ornament, and he names Newman among the masters. Yet he adds a confession that no pious [[religion|Catholic]] hagiographer would make:

> You will get no better prose, for instance, than Newman's *Arians of the Fourth Century*. [040-034]

Even here, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s honesty compels a qualification. In the surrounding passage he compares Newman's prose unfavourably with certain other writers, noting that Newman, while always lucid, does not always achieve the highest pitch. The frankness is characteristic of [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] — literary judgment, for him, cannot be subordinated to piety, and even a saint's prose must be judged on its merits.

## Life & Context

Born in [[london|London]] in 1801, Newman was educated at Ealing and Trinity College, [[oxford-university|Oxford]], where he was elected a fellow of Oriel College in 1822. Ordained an Anglican priest in 1825, he became vicar of [[oxford-university|the University]] Church of St Mary the Virgin, where his sermons drew crowds. The [[oxford-university|Oxford]] Movement, which he led with John Keble and Edward Pusey, sought to recover the [[religion|Catholic]] [[tradition|heritage]] of [[the-church|the Church]] of [[england|England]].

His studies of the early Church fathers led him, gradually and against his own deepest inclinations, toward the conclusion that the Roman [[the-church|Catholic Church]] was the authentic successor of the apostolic Church. His conversion in 1845 was a national sensation. He founded the Birmingham Oratory and spent the remaining forty-five [[time|years]] of his life writing, teaching, and — after decades of suspicion from within the [[religion|Catholic]] hierarchy — receiving the cardinal's red hat from Pope Leo XIII. He was canonised by Pope Francis in 2019.

## Connection to Belloc

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Newman was a personal and intellectual ancestor. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own [[france|French]] [[religion|Catholic]] upbringing, his combative public style, and his conviction that history vindicated the [[religion|Catholic]] claim — all of these bear Newman's imprint. Yet [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s literary honesty compelled him to note that Newman's prose, while always lucid, was not always the best. This willingness to separate the saint from the stylist is quintessentially [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]ian: truth, even about a hero, must be told plainly, and a devotion to the craft of writing requires one to judge even the most revered authors by the standard of the sentence.
