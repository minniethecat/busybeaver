## Overview

James William Lowther (1855–1949), later 1st Viscount Ullswater, served as Speaker of the [[parliament|House of Commons]] from 1905 to 1921 — a tenure spanning the constitutional crisis of 1909–1911, the Great War, and the first [[time|years]] of the post-war settlement. His sixteen [[time|years]] in the Chair made him one of the longest-serving Speakers in British parliamentary history.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], who sat in [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] as a Liberal MP from 1906 to 1910, observed Lowther at the closest possible range. His three references to the Speaker in Section 4 of *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* are among the most admiring passages [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] ever devoted to a living politician. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], the management of the House was not merely a political skill but an art — and Lowther was its supreme practitioner.

## In the *Nona*

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s testimony is that of an eyewitness. All three references occur within a sustained meditation on the art of controlling large assemblies — a meditation prompted, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] tells us, by watching a man at a wharf manage a restless crowd. The memory leads him directly to [[parliament|Westminster]]:

> …the Speaker of my time (Mr. Lowther, as he then was) exercising the art through two Parliaments with a perfection which I had thought impossible, and which moved me, at the watching of it, as much as I am moved by great lines of Milton or Racine, or the harmonies of certain musics. [004-006]

The comparison to [[john-milton|Milton]] and Racine is not casual. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] is placing parliamentary procedure — the most mundane of political functions — on the same plane as the highest achievements of European art. The Speaker's chair, in this passage, becomes a kind of performance, and Lowther its virtuoso.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] emphasises the consistency of Lowther's command. Even the most difficult moments of the parliamentary day — moments when any lesser Speaker would have lost control — found Lowther equal to the test:

> …decisions during the day, Mr. Lowther never once failed. The House… [004-010]

The final reference is a judgment of the highest order. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] writes:

> I have no more doubt that Mr. Lowther, as I watched him day after day, displayed genius than I have a doubt that Mozart in music, or Houdon in sculpture, had genius. [004-011]

The word "genius" — applied to a politician rather than an artist — is as rare in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s vocabulary as it is deliberate. Lowther, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s memory, was not merely competent or even excellent; he belonged to that small company of men in whom a natural gift rises to the level of the inexplicable.

## Life & Context

James Lowther was born in 1855 into a Cumberland family with deep parliamentary roots — his great-grandfather had been Speaker before him. He entered [[parliament|Parliament]] as Conservative MP for Rutland in 1883, and after a junior ministerial post, was elected Speaker in 1905. His tenure witnessed the [[parliament|Parliament]] Act of 1911, which permanently reduced the power of the House of Lords, and the unprecedented strains of coalition government during the Great War. He retired from the Chair in 1921 and was raised to the peerage. He died in 1949 at the age of ninety-four.

## Connection to Belloc

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s admiration for Lowther is all the more striking for being directed at a political figure. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was, by temperament and conviction, a sceptic about parliamentary democracy. He distrusted politicians as a class and believed the [[parliament|House of Commons]] to be a fundamentally corrupt institution. Yet he made an exception for Lowther — a Speaker whose mastery of the House transcended the institution's limitations. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], Lowther was proof that even within a degraded political system, individual excellence could still flourish. The passage in which [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] names Lowther alongside [[john-milton|Milton]], Mozart, and Houdon is one of the most generous moments in a book not generally given to generosity toward politicians.
