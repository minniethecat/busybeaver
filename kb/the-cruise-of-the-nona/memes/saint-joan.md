# Saint Joan

## Overview

*Saint Joan: A Chronicle Play in Six Scenes and an Epilogue* (1923) is George Bernard Shaw's dramatic treatment of Joan of Arc. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] — who disagreed with Shaw on almost everything — it is remarkable as "the latest example" of a writer who genuinely understands what the [[religion|Catholic]] Faith is, even if he does not share it.

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s discussion of *Saint Joan* appears in Section 15, during a meditation on the boundary between [[religion|Catholic]] belief and the secular world:

> Now and then one comes across a man who talks or writes with some knowledge, if not of what lies beyond the boundary, at least of the boundary itself; but it is exceedingly rare. The latest example I know of the thing is the preface by Mr. Bernard Shaw, written for his play *Saint Joan*. Here there is a clear recognition of the boundary between the Faith and what lies outside: between the garden and the heath, or marsh. (015-029)

The metaphor — garden, heath, marsh — is [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s own, but he credits Shaw with recognising the boundary itself. For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], this is high praise. Shaw, the Fabian socialist and religious sceptic, had no sympathy for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s [[religion|Catholicism]], but he was intelligent enough to understand what he was rejecting. Most writers, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s view, could not even do that — they were simply ignorant of [[the-church|the Faith]] they dismissed.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] specifically notes Shaw's treatment of Joan's voices. Shaw "says that, having read the account, he is convinced that Joan did hear what she said she heard" (015-031). This willingness to take religious experience seriously — not as pathology but as phenomenon — distinguishes Shaw from the mass of secular writers who, in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s phrase, know nothing of "what lies beyond the boundary."

## About the Work

*Saint Joan* was Shaw's only tragedy and is widely considered his masterpiece. It premiered in 1923, two [[time|years]] before [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] published the *[[nona|Nona]]*. Shaw's Joan is a P[[corruption|rot]]estant before P[[corruption|rot]]estantism — an individualist who defies the institutional Church in the name of direct access to God. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] would have disagreed with this interpretation entirely, but he respects Shaw's willingness to engage with the question seriously.
