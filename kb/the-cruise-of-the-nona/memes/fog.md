## Overview

Fog appears in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* as one of the great elemental adversaries of the sailor. Unlike wind or tide, which can be measured and anticipated, fog strips away the navigator's primary sense — sight — and leaves him suspended in a world of disembodied sounds and imagined dangers. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s fog passages are among the most atmospheric in the book, blending precise observation with a quality of the uncanny.

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] distinguishes between ordinary [[the-sea|sea]] mist and the peculiarly solid, opaque fogs of the western coasts. His most extended treatment comes in a passage off the [[wales|Welsh coast]] where a "cloud rather than fog" descends upon the *[[nona|Nona]]*, so dense that it becomes a physical presence — "solid" and enveloping, transforming the familiar [[the-sea|seas]]cape into something alien and [[danger|threat]]ening.

## Fog in the Narrative

The most dramatic fog encounter occurs as [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] approaches the coast. A fog "came drifting up and enveloped" the boat, reducing visibility to nothing. In the calm that accompanied it, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] found himself straining to hear sounds that never came: "no voices, no swinging spars." The peculiar horror of the experience was not danger from collision but the erasure of the world itself — the [[the-sea|sea]], the shore, the sky all dissolved into a uniform grey.

Earlier in the narrative, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] catalogues fog among the "horrible great mists, fogs, vapours, malignant humours of the deep" that beset the mariner — placing it in a taxonomy of maritime [[danger|hazard]]s that includes mirages, false ground, and the terrible tide races of the [[wales|Welsh coast]]. Yet fog also serves a metaphorical purpose; in a later passage, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] invokes "mist and fog" as the condition of modern intellectual life, contrasting it with the clarity of older prophetic visions.

## Key Passages

> "Lord, what a tangle of dangers are here for the wretched mariner! Rocks and eddies and overfalls and shooting tides; currents and (as you shall shortly hear) horrible great mists, fogs, vapours, malignant humours of the deep, mirages, false ground, where the anchor will not hold, and foul ground, where the anchor holds forever." [011-017]

> "I say cloud rather than fog, because it was so solid and so opaque. It was not like the fogs of the land, which are damp and cold, but warm and dry, and of a texture more like wool than vapour." [011-029]

> "A fog came drifting up and enveloped us — I could see nothing. The wind had dropped, of course, with the fog. It gave us only enough way to keep steerage, and I could hear nothing — no voices, no swinging spars, no sound of oars." [011-030]

> "I have no doubt that had the fog lifted I should have had nothing to fear, but the imagination works strangely in such a solitude." [013-028]
