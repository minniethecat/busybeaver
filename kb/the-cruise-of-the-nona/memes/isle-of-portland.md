The Isle of Portland is a limestone island on the south coast of [[england|England]], connected to the mainland by [[chesil-cove|Chesil]] Beach — a shingle tombolo that makes it technically a tied island rather than a true island. For [[hilaire-belloc|Hilaire Belloc]], Portland was both a crucial navigational waypoint and a place of grim associations.

## The Western Approach

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] identified the [[the-sea|sea]] off Portland as one of those places upon which "a god has determined that there shall be [[danger|peril]]" [011-018]. He listed it alongside the [[bight-of-saint-malo|Bight of Saint Malo]] and the Straits of Messina as a location where multiple dangers accumulate:

> If it is an overfall or a race, then in that neighbourhood you will also have reefs, unaccountable thick weather, shifting soundings, bad holding, and all the rest of it. Such a place is the sea off Portland [011-018].

## Portland Bill

[[portland-bill|Portland Bill]], the southern tip of the island, stands "right out into [[english-channel|the Channel]] and challenges the Atlantic tide" [028-004]. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] was struck by its geological history — "[[portland-bill|Portland Bill]] was once an island" [026-008] — and by the evidence of millennia of human use: the worked quarries and the "mark of the historic centuries" [026-008].

He admitted to a long-standing irreverence toward the landmark, then confessed a change of heart:

> In my folly, for many years I used to call it "William," but I will do so no longer. For there is something awful about the snakelike descending point, and dreadful menace in the waters beyond [028-004].

## Sailing Past Portland

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s passages past Portland were frequent and often anxious. "The thing by me always dreaded" was [[portland-race|the Portland Race]] [028-001], though he once made the passage in unusually benign conditions: "I have once, and once only, gone through the tail-end of [[portland-bill|Portland Race]]; only the very tail-end. The [[the-sea|sea]] was of an oily calm" [028-003]. He knew this was exceptional — the normal state of the Race was "a terrible affair" [028-001].

On another occasion, entering from the west, he "made direct for [[portland-bill|Portland Bill]], cutting across the Bay" [023-001] in a small boat, a route that required careful timing of [[tide|tides]] and weather.

## Portland Harbour

Behind [[portland-bill|the Bill]] lies the great artificial expanse of [[portland-harbour|Portland Harbour]], which [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] called "one of the very, very few great artificial harbours of the world" [027-001]. He noted its unusual [[tide|tidal]] pattern — "four hours' ebb on the easterly shore of Portland and only three hours' flow" [014-014] — a peculiarity that fascinated him as a student of [[tide|tides]]. The harbour's construction was, for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], one of the great engineering achievements that marked the modern transformation of the British coastline.
