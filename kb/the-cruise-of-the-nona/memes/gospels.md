## Overview

The Gospels — Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John — are the four canonical accounts of the life and teachings of Jesus Christ, forming the core of the [[new-testament|New Testament]]. For Hilaire [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], a practising [[religion|Catholic]] writing in a predominantly P[[corruption|rot]]estant and increasingly sceptical [[england|England]], [[the-bible|the Gospel]]s were not merely ancient documents to be dissected by scholars but living testimonies whose authority rested on the continuous tradition of [[the-church|the Church]].

## Belloc's Reference

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s engagement with [[the-bible|the Gospel]]s in *The Cruise of [[nona|the Nona]]* is characteristically combative. He does not discuss their content so much as defend their authenticity against what he saw as the groundless [[doubt|scepticism]] of modern academic criticism.

The most sustained treatment appears in Section 11, where [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] attacks the academic habit of denying traditional authorship. He asks rhetorically:

> "Whence springs this lust for saying that the Gospel of Saint John was not written by Saint John? that Homer was not written by Homer? that the Battle of Hastings was not called the Battle of Hastings?" (011-056)

For [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]], this [[doubt|scepticism]] is not evidence-based but springs from vanity, the love of the marvellous, and "the perpetual substitution of hypothesis for [[truth|fact]]" — the three vices he identifies as driving academic foolishness.

In Section 42, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] returns to the Fourth Gospel at length, attacking the German scholar Wernle for presuming to judge the psychology of the evangelist:

> "It is in the matter of the Gospel of Saint John that this rubbish has accumulated its most monstrous heap." (042-020)

He argues that a scholar's linguistic expertise gives him no special authority to decide whether a text "cannot be that of an eyewitness" — that judgment belongs to common human experience, not to specialised [[knowledge|learning]]. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s counter-method is to assert the authority of tradition:

> "It is certainly so in the case of this Gospel of Saint John. A man who denies its apostolic authority... should be regarded as the incongruous disturber of a judgment upon which millions of men through centuries had preserved a firm conclusion." (042-021)

The other references are briefer but consistent. In Section 14, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] describes a wealthy P[[corruption|rot]]estant "Helper" who was "occupied in spreading [[the-bible|the Gospel]], under the reservation, of course, that it was not true" (014-035) — a satire of the modernist Christian who evangelises while discarding the supernatural content of the [[religion|faith]]. In Section 15, he notes simply that "the [[religion|Catholic]] admits all evidence" regarding [[the-bible|the Scriptures]], in contrast to those who approach them with predetermined [[doubt|scepticism]] (015-034).

## Connection to Belloc

The Gospels represent for [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] the central battleground between [[religion|Catholic]] tradition and modern unbelief. His defence of their authenticity is part of a larger argument that the [[the-church|Catholic Church]] is the true continuator of European civilisation. The academic critics, in his view, are provincial iconoclasts who do not understand the weight of what they are dismantling. His repeated return to [[the-bible|the Gospel]] of Saint John in particular may reflect the [[truth|fact]] that Johannine authorship was a flashpoint in early twentieth-century biblical criticism.
