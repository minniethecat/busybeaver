## Overview

The tide is the single most determining force in [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s narrative of coastal sailing. Every decision aboard the *[[nona|Nona]]* — when to depart, which harbour to enter, whether to press on or heave to — is governed by the state of the tide. [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] treats the tide not as an abstract [[the-sea|ocean]]ographic phenomenon but as a living presence: something that can be "vicious," kindly, or indifferent, depending on the circumstances of a given passage.

Throughout the memoir, [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] navigates the complex tidal waters of the [[wales|Welsh]] and English coasts: [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]], Jack Sound, [[portland-bill|Portland Race]], [[alderney|Alderney Race]], and the innumerable lesser races and overfalls that mark the western approaches of [[england|Britain]]. His descriptions of struggling against a foul tide, or being carried effortlessly by a fair one, form the dramatic backbone of the book.

## Belloc's Encounter with the Tide

[[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]]'s most harrowing encounter with the tide comes in [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Sound]], where a miscalculation of the turn nearly costs him his boat and his life. The tide turned against him just as he entered the narrow passage between the mainland and [[bardsey-sound|Bardsey Island]], creating a chaos of "tumbling pyramidical waves" that parted the dinghy painter and left the *[[nona|Nona]]* fighting for every yard of headway. It took four attempts before the boat slipped into an eddy beyond the tide race.

The experience left [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] with a profound respect for the tide's power. He catalogues the races he knows — "[[portland-bill|Portland Race]] and [[alderney|Alderney Race]] and [[saint-albans-race|Little Saint Alban's Race]]" — and those he wisely avoids, including "the very damnable races of the northern headlands." Yet he notes with characteristic wryness that not all races are hostile: "the race off the Start" has always treated him kindly.

## Key Passages

> "I reckoned that the tide would turn against us anywhere between six and seven in the morning, that it would turn back through the Sound about noon or one o'clock, and I calculated that we should be running into Cardigan Bay, past Bardsey, on the middle of the ebb." [001-005]

> "For I had done something or other to annoy the Earth-Shaker, and he pursued me viciously, making the tide turn just before we reached the mouth of the Sound. In a time much shorter than I had expected, with no lull in between, the steady run of sea which had been combing behind us, towering above the counter, but regular and normal to deal with, turned into a confusion of huge tumbling pyramidical waves, leaping up, twisting, turning, and boiling in such a confusion as I had never seen." [001-014]

> "Do not believe it when you read it in books that a race is made by anything so simple as a tide against a wind or a sudden precipice in the depths of the sea. It is made by the anger of those that rule the sea." [011-020]

> "The tide was at the neaps, there was little current, we had a straight course to steer." [011-040]
