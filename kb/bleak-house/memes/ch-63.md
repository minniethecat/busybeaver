[063-001] [[george-rouncewell|George]]’s Shooting Gallery is to let, and the stock is sold off, and

[063-002] George himself is at [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]] attending on [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester]] in his

[063-003] rides and riding very near his bridle-rein because of the uncertain

[063-004] hand with which he guides his horse. But not to-day is George so

[063-005] occupied. He is journeying to-day into the iron country farther north

[063-006] to look about him.

[063-007] As he comes into the iron country farther north, such fresh green

[063-008] woods as those of Chesney Wold are left behind; and coal pits and

[063-009] ashes, high chimneys and red bricks, blighted verdure, scorching

[063-010] fires, and a heavy never-lightening cloud of smoke become the

[063-011] features of the scenery. Among such objects rides the trooper,

[063-012] looking about him and always looking for something he has come to

[063-013] find.

[063-014] At last, on the black canal bridge of a busy town, with a clang of

[063-015] iron in it, and more fires and more smoke than he has seen yet, the

[063-016] trooper, swart with the dust of the coal roads, checks his horse and

[063-017] asks a workman does he know the name of Rouncewell thereabouts.

[063-018] “Why, master,” quoth the workman, “do I know my own name?”

[063-019] “’Tis so well known here, is it, comrade?” asks the trooper.

[063-020] “Rouncewell’s? Ah! You’re right.”

[063-021] “And where might it be now?” asks the trooper with a glance before

[063-022] him.

[063-023] “The bank, the factory, or the house?” the workman wants to know.

[063-024] “Hum! Rouncewell’s is so great apparently,” mutters the trooper,

[063-025] stroking his chin, “that I have as good as half a mind to go back

[063-026] again. Why, I don’t know which I want. Should I find Mr. Rouncewell

[063-027] at the factory, do you think?”

[063-028] “Tain’t easy to say where you’d find him—at this time of the day you

[063-029] might find either him or his son there, if he’s in town; but his

[063-030] contracts take him away.”

[063-031] And which is the factory? Why, he sees those chimneys—the tallest

[063-032] ones! Yes, he sees THEM. Well! Let him keep his eye on those

[063-033] chimneys, going on as straight as ever he can, and presently he’ll

[063-034] see ’em down a turning on the left, shut in by a great brick wall

[063-035] which forms one side of the street. That’s Rouncewell’s.

[063-036] The trooper thanks his informant and rides slowly on, looking about

[063-037] him. He does not turn back, but puts up his horse (and is much

[063-038] disposed to groom him too) at a public-house where some of

[063-039] Rouncewell’s hands are dining, as the ostler tells him. Some of

[063-040] Rouncewell’s hands have just knocked off for dinner-time and seem to

[063-041] be invading the whole town. They are very sinewy and strong, are

[063-042] Rouncewell’s hands—a little sooty too.

[063-043] He comes to a gateway in the brick wall, looks in, and sees a great

[063-044] perplexity of iron lying about in every stage and in a vast variety

[063-045] of shapes—in bars, in wedges, in sheets; in tanks, in boilers, in

[063-046] axles, in wheels, in cogs, in cranks, in rails; twisted and wrenched

[063-047] into eccentric and perverse forms as separate parts of machinery;

[063-048] mountains of it broken up, and rusty in its age; distant furnaces of

[063-049] it glowing and bubbling in its youth; bright fireworks of it

[063-050] showering about under the blows of the steam-hammer; red-hot iron,

[063-051] white-hot iron, cold-black iron; an iron taste, an iron smell, and a

[063-052] Babel of iron sounds.

[063-053] “This is a place to make a man’s head ache too!” says the trooper,

[063-054] looking about him for a counting-house. “Who comes here? This is very

[063-055] like me before I was set up. This ought to be my nephew, if

[063-056] likenesses run in families. Your servant, sir.”

[063-057] “Yours, sir. Are you looking for any one?”

[063-058] “Excuse me. Young Mr. Rouncewell, I believe?”

[063-059] “Yes.”

[063-060] “I was looking for your father, sir. I wish to have a word with him.”

[063-061] The young man, telling him he is fortunate in his choice of a time,

[063-062] for his father is there, leads the way to the office where he is to

[063-063] be found. “Very like me before I was set up—devilish like me!”

[063-064] thinks the trooper as he follows. They come to a building in the yard

[063-065] with an office on an upper floor. At sight of the gentleman in the

[063-066] office, Mr. George turns very red.

[063-067] “What name shall I say to my father?” asks the young man.

[063-068] George, full of the idea of iron, in desperation answers “Steel,” and

[063-069] is so presented. He is left alone with the gentleman in the office,

[063-070] who sits at a table with account-books before him and some sheets of

[063-071] paper blotted with hosts of figures and drawings of cunning shapes.

[063-072] It is a bare office, with bare windows, looking on the iron view

[063-073] below. Tumbled together on the table are some pieces of iron,

[063-074] purposely broken to be tested at various periods of their service, in

[063-075] various capacities. There is iron-dust on everything; and the smoke

[063-076] is seen through the windows rolling heavily out of the tall chimneys

[063-077] to mingle with the smoke from a vaporous Babylon of other chimneys.

[063-078] “I am at your service, Mr. Steel,” says the gentleman when his

[063-079] visitor has taken a rusty chair.

[063-080] “Well, Mr. Rouncewell,” George replies, leaning forward with his left

[063-081] arm on his knee and his hat in his hand, and very chary of meeting

[063-082] his brother’s eye, “I am not without my expectations that in the

[063-083] present visit I may prove to be more free than welcome. I have served

[063-084] as a dragoon in my day, and a comrade of mine that I was once rather

[063-085] partial to was, if I don’t deceive myself, a brother of yours. I

[063-086] believe you had a brother who gave his family some trouble, and ran

[063-087] away, and never did any good but in keeping away?”

[063-088] “Are you quite sure,” returns the ironmaster in an altered voice,

[063-089] “that your name is Steel?”

[063-090] The trooper falters and looks at him. His brother starts up, calls

[063-091] him by his name, and grasps him by both hands.

[063-092] “You are too quick for me!” cries the trooper with the tears

[063-093] springing out of his eyes. “How do you do, my dear old fellow? I

[063-094] never could have thought you would have been half so glad to see me

[063-095] as all this. How do you do, my dear old fellow, how do you do!”

[063-096] They shake hands and embrace each other over and over again, the

[063-097] trooper still coupling his “How do you do, my dear old fellow!” with

[063-098] his protestation that he never thought his brother would have been

[063-099] half so glad to see him as all this!

[063-100] “So far from it,” he declares at the end of a full account of what

[063-101] has preceded his arrival there, “I had very little idea of making

[063-102] myself known. I thought if you took by any means forgivingly to my

[063-103] name I might gradually get myself up to the point of [[document-and-letter|writing]] a

[063-104] letter. But I should not have been surprised, brother, if you had

[063-105] considered it anything but welcome news to hear of me.”

[063-106] “We will show you at home what kind of news we think it, George,”

[063-107] returns his brother. “This is a great day at home, and you could not

[063-108] have arrived, you bronzed old soldier, on a better. I make an

[063-109] agreement with my son Watt to-day that on this day twelvemonth he

[063-110] shall marry as pretty and as good a girl as you have seen in all your

[063-111] travels. She goes to Germany to-morrow with one of your nieces for a

[063-112] little polishing up in her education. We make a feast of the event,

[063-113] and you will be made the hero of it.”

[063-114] Mr. George is so entirely overcome at first by this prospect that he

[063-115] resists the proposed honour with great earnestness. Being overborne,

[063-116] however, by his brother and his nephew—concerning whom he renews his

[063-117] protestations that he never could have thought they would have been

[063-118] half so glad to see him—he is taken home to an elegant house in all

[063-119] the arrangements of which there is to be observed a pleasant mixture

[063-120] of the originally simple habits of the father and mother with such as

[063-121] are suited to their altered station and the higher fortunes of their

[063-122] children. Here Mr. George is much dismayed by the graces and

[063-123] accomplishments of his nieces that are and by the beauty of Rosa, his

[063-124] niece that is to be, and by the affectionate salutations of these

[063-125] young ladies, which he receives in a sort of dream. He is sorely

[063-126] taken aback, too, by the dutiful behaviour of his nephew and has a

[063-127] woeful consciousness upon him of being a scapegrace. However, there

[063-128] is great rejoicing and a very hearty company and infinite enjoyment,

[063-129] and Mr. George comes bluff and martial through it all, and his pledge

[063-130] to be present at the marriage and give away the bride is received

[063-131] with universal favour. A whirling head has Mr. George that night when

[063-132] he lies down in the state-bed of his brother’s house to think of all

[063-133] these things and to see the images of his nieces (awful all the

[063-134] evening in their floating muslins) waltzing, after the German manner,

[063-135] over his counterpane.

[063-136] The brothers are closeted next morning in the ironmaster’s room,

[063-137] where the elder is proceeding, in his clear sensible way, to show how

[063-138] he thinks he may best dispose of George in his business, when George

[063-139] squeezes his hand and stops him.

[063-140] “Brother, I thank you a million times for your more than brotherly

[063-141] welcome, and a million times more to that for your more than

[063-142] brotherly intentions. But my plans are made. Before I say a word as

[063-143] to them, I wish to consult you upon one family point. How,” says the

[063-144] trooper, folding his arms and looking with indomitable firmness at

[063-145] his brother, “how is my mother to be got to scratch me?”

[063-146] “I am not sure that I understand you, George,” replies the

[063-147] ironmaster.

[063-148] “I say, brother, how is my mother to be got to scratch me? She must

[063-149] be got to do it somehow.”

[063-150] “Scratch you out of her will, I think you mean?”

[063-151] “Of course I do. In short,” says the trooper, folding his arms more

[063-152] resolutely yet, “I mean—TO—scratch me!”

[063-153] “My dear George,” returns his brother, “is it so indispensable that

[063-154] you should undergo that process?”

[063-155] “Quite! Absolutely! I couldn’t be guilty of the meanness of coming

[063-156] back without it. I should never be safe not to be off again. I have

[063-157] not sneaked home to rob your children, if not yourself, brother, of

[063-158] your rights. I, who forfeited mine long ago! If I am to remain and

[063-159] hold up my head, I must be scratched. Come. You are a man of

[063-160] celebrated penetration and intelligence, and you can tell me how it’s

[063-161] to be brought about.”

[063-162] “I can tell you, George,” replies the ironmaster deliberately, “how

[063-163] it is not to be brought about, which I hope may answer the purpose as

[063-164] well. Look at our mother, think of her, recall her emotion when she

[063-165] recovered you. Do you believe there is a consideration in the world

[063-166] that would induce her to take such a step against her favourite son?

[063-167] Do you believe there is any chance of her consent, to balance against

[063-168] the outrage it would be to her (loving dear old lady!) to propose it?

[063-169] If you do, you are wrong. No, George! You must make up your mind to

[063-170] remain UNscratched, I think.” There is an amused smile on the

[063-171] ironmaster’s face as he watches his brother, who is pondering, deeply

[063-172] disappointed. “I think you may manage almost as well as if the thing

[063-173] were done, though.”

[063-174] “How, brother?”

[063-175] “Being bent upon it, you can dispose by will of anything you have the

[063-176] misfortune to inherit in any way you like, you know.”

[063-177] “That’s true!” says the trooper, pondering again. Then he wistfully

[063-178] asks, with his hand on his brother’s, “Would you mind mentioning

[063-179] that, brother, to your wife and family?”

[063-180] “Not at all.”

[063-181] “Thank you. You wouldn’t object to say, perhaps, that although an

[063-182] undoubted vagabond, I am a vagabond of the harum-scarum order, and

[063-183] not of the mean sort?”

[063-184] The ironmaster, repressing his amused smile, assents.

[063-185] “Thank you. Thank you. It’s a weight off my mind,” says the trooper

[063-186] with a heave of his chest as he unfolds his arms and puts a hand on

[063-187] each leg, “though I had set my heart on being scratched, too!”

[063-188] The brothers are very like each other, sitting face to face; but a

[063-189] certain massive simplicity and absence of usage in the ways of the

[063-190] world is all on the trooper’s side.

[063-191] “Well,” he proceeds, throwing off his disappointment, “next and last,

[063-192] those plans of mine. You have been so brotherly as to propose to me

[063-193] to fall in here and take my place among the products of your

[063-194] perseverance and sense. I thank you heartily. It’s more than

[063-195] brotherly, as I said before, and I thank you heartily for it,”

[063-196] shaking him a long time by the hand. “But the truth is, brother, I am

[063-197] a—I am a kind of a weed, and it’s too late to plant me in a regular

[063-198] garden.”

[063-199] “My dear George,” returns the elder, concentrating his strong steady

[063-200] brow upon him and smiling confidently, “leave that to me, and let me

[063-201] try.”

[063-202] George shakes his head. “You could do it, I have not a doubt, if

[063-203] anybody could; but it’s not to be done. Not to be done, sir! Whereas

[063-204] it so falls out, on the other hand, that I am able to be of some

[063-205] trifle of use to Sir Leicester Dedlock since his illness—brought on

[063-206] by family sorrows—and that he would rather have that help from our

[063-207] mother’s son than from anybody else.”

[063-208] “Well, my dear George,” returns the other with a very slight shade

[063-209] upon his open face, “if you prefer to serve in Sir Leicester

[063-210] Dedlock’s household brigade—”

[063-211] “There it is, brother,” cries the trooper, checking him, with his

[063-212] hand upon his knee again; “there it is! You don’t take kindly to that

[063-213] idea; I don’t mind it. You are not used to being officered; I am.

[063-214] Everything about you is in perfect order and discipline; everything

[063-215] about me requires to be kept so. We are not accustomed to carry

[063-216] things with the same hand or to look at ’em from the same point. I

[063-217] don’t say much about my garrison manners because I found myself

[063-218] pretty well at my ease last night, and they wouldn’t be noticed here,

[063-219] I dare say, once and away. But I shall get on best at Chesney Wold,

[063-220] where there’s more room for a weed than there is here; and the dear

[063-221] old lady will be made happy besides. Therefore I accept of Sir

[063-222] Leicester Dedlock’s proposals. When I come over next year to give

[063-223] away the bride, or whenever I come, I shall have the sense to keep

[063-224] the household brigade in ambuscade and not to manoeuvre it on your

[063-225] ground. I thank you heartily again and am proud to think of the

[063-226] Rouncewells as they’ll be founded by you.”

[063-227] “You know yourself, George,” says the elder brother, returning the

[063-228] grip of his hand, “and perhaps you know me better than I know myself.

[063-229] Take your way. So that we don’t quite lose one another again, take

[063-230] your way.”

[063-231] “No fear of that!” returns the trooper. “Now, before I turn my

[063-232] horse’s head homewards, brother, I will ask you—if you’ll be so

[063-233] good—to look over a letter for me. I brought it with me to send from

[063-234] these parts, as Chesney Wold might be a painful name just now to the

[063-235] person it’s written to. I am not much accustomed to correspondence

[063-236] myself, and I am particular respecting this present letter because I

[063-237] want it to be both straightforward and delicate.”

[063-238] Herewith he hands a letter, closely written in somewhat pale ink but

[063-239] in a neat round hand, to the ironmaster, who reads as follows:

[063-240] Miss [[esther-summerson|Esther Summerson]],

[063-241] A communication having been made to me by Inspector Bucket

[063-242] of a letter to myself being found among the papers of a

[063-243] certain person, I take the liberty to make known to you

[063-244] that it was but a few lines of instruction from abroad,

[063-245] when, where, and how to deliver an enclosed letter to a

[063-246] young and beautiful lady, then unmarried, in England. I

[063-247] duly observed the same.

[063-248] I further take the liberty to make known to you that it

[063-249] was got from me as a proof of handwriting only and that

[063-250] otherwise I would not have given it up, as appearing to

[063-251] be the most harmless in my possession, without being

[063-252] previously shot through the heart.

[063-253] I further take the liberty to mention that if I could have

[063-254] supposed a certain unfortunate gentleman to have been in

[063-255] existence, I never could and never would have rested until

[063-256] I had discovered his retreat and shared my last farthing

[063-257] with him, as my duty and my inclination would have equally

[063-258] been. But he was (officially) reported drowned, and

[063-259] assuredly went over the side of a transport-ship at night

[063-260] in an Irish harbour within a few hours of her arrival from

[063-261] the West Indies, as I have myself heard both from officers

[063-262] and men on board, and know to have been (officially)

[063-263] confirmed.

[063-264] I further take the liberty to state that in my humble

[063-265] quality as one of the rank and file, I am, and shall ever

[063-266] continue to be, your thoroughly devoted and admiring

[063-267] servant and that I esteem the qualities you possess above

[063-268] all others far beyond the limits of the present dispatch.

[063-269] I have the honour to be,

[063-270] GEORGE

[063-271] “A little formal,” observes the elder brother, refolding it with a

[063-272] puzzled face.

[063-273] “But nothing that might not be sent to a pattern young lady?” asks

[063-274] the younger.

[063-275] “Nothing at all.”

[063-276] Therefore it is sealed and deposited for posting among the iron

[063-277] correspondence of the day. This done, Mr. George takes a hearty

[063-278] farewell of the family party and prepares to saddle and mount. His

[063-279] brother, however, unwilling to part with him so soon, proposes to

[063-280] ride with him in a light open carriage to the place where he will

[063-281] bait for the night, and there remain with him until morning, a

[063-282] servant riding for so much of the journey on the thoroughbred old

[063-283] grey from Chesney Wold. The offer, being gladly accepted, is followed

[063-284] by a pleasant ride, a pleasant dinner, and a pleasant breakfast, all

[063-285] in brotherly communion. Then they once more shake hands long and

[063-286] heartily and part, the ironmaster turning his face to the smoke and

[063-287] fires, and the trooper to the green country. Early in the afternoon

[063-288] the subdued sound of his heavy military trot is heard on the turf in

[063-289] the avenue as he rides on with imaginary clank and jingle of

[063-290] accoutrements under the old elm-trees.
