[038-001] When our time came for returning to [[bleak-house|Bleak House]] again, we were

[038-002] punctual to the day and were received with an overpowering welcome. I

[038-003] was perfectly restored to health and strength, and finding my

[038-004] housekeeping keys laid ready for me in my room, rang myself in as if

[038-005] I had been a new year, with a merry little peal. “Once more, duty,

[038-006] duty, [[esther-summerson|Esther]],” said I; “and if you are not overjoyed to do it, more

[038-007] than cheerfully and contentedly, through anything and everything, you

[038-008] ought to be. That’s all I have to say to you, my dear!”

[038-009] The first few mornings were mornings of so much bustle and business,

[038-010] devoted to such settlements of accounts, such repeated journeys to

[038-011] and fro between the growlery and all other parts of the house, so

[038-012] many rearrangements of drawers and presses, and such a general new

[038-013] beginning altogether, that I had not a moment’s leisure. But when

[038-014] these arrangements were completed and everything was in order, I paid

[038-015] a visit of a few hours to [[london|London]], which something in the letter I had

[038-016] destroyed at [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]] had induced me to decide upon in my own

[038-017] mind.

[038-018] I made [[caddy-jellyby|Caddy Jellyby]]—her maiden name was so natural to me that I

[038-019] always called her by it—the pretext for this visit and wrote her a

[038-020] note previously asking the favour of her company on a little business

[038-021] expedition. Leaving home very early in the morning, I got to London

[038-022] by stage-coach in such good time that I got to Newman Street with the

[038-023] day before me.

[038-024] Caddy, who had not seen me since her wedding-day, was so glad and so

[038-025] affectionate that I was half inclined to fear I should make her

[038-026] husband jealous. But he was, in his way, just as bad—I mean as good;

[038-027] and in short it was the old story, and nobody would leave me any

[038-028] possibility of doing anything meritorious.

[038-029] The elder Mr. Turveydrop was in bed, I found, and Caddy was

[038-030] milling his chocolate, which a melancholy little boy who was an

[038-031] apprentice—it seemed such a curious thing to be apprenticed to the

[038-032] trade of dancing—was waiting to carry upstairs. Her father-in-law

[038-033] was extremely kind and considerate, Caddy told me, and they lived

[038-034] most happily together. (When she spoke of their living together, she

[038-035] meant that the old gentleman had all the good things and all the good

[038-036] lodging, while she and her husband had what they could get, and were

[038-037] poked into two corner rooms over the Mews.)

[038-038] “And how is your mama, Caddy?” said I.

[038-039] “Why, I hear of her, Esther,” replied Caddy, “through Pa, but I see

[038-040] very little of her. We are good friends, I am glad to say, but Ma

[038-041] thinks there is something absurd in my having married a

[038-042] dancing-master, and she is rather afraid of its extending to her.”

[038-043] It struck me that if Mrs. Jellyby had discharged her own natural

[038-044] duties and obligations before she swept the horizon with a telescope

[038-045] in search of others, she would have taken the best precautions

[038-046] against becoming absurd, but I need scarcely observe that I kept this

[038-047] to myself.

[038-048] “And your papa, Caddy?”

[038-049] “He comes here every evening,” returned Caddy, “and is so fond of

[038-050] sitting in the corner there that it’s a treat to see him.”

[038-051] Looking at the corner, I plainly perceived the mark of Mr. Jellyby’s

[038-052] head against the wall. It was consolatory to know that he had found

[038-053] such a resting-place for it.

[038-054] “And you, Caddy,” said I, “you are always busy, I’ll be bound?”

[038-055] “Well, my dear,” returned Caddy, “I am indeed, for to tell you a

[038-056] grand secret, I am qualifying myself to give lessons. Prince’s health

[038-057] is not strong, and I want to be able to assist him. What with

[038-058] schools, and classes here, and private pupils, AND the apprentices,

[038-059] he really has too much to do, poor fellow!”

[038-060] The notion of the apprentices was still so odd to me that I asked

[038-061] Caddy if there were many of them.

[038-062] “Four,” said Caddy. “One in-door, and three out. They are

[038-063] very good children; only when they get together they WILL

[038-064] play—children-like—instead of attending to their work. So the

[038-065] little boy you saw just now waltzes by himself in the empty kitchen,

[038-066] and we distribute the others over the house as well as we can.”

[038-067] “That is only for their steps, of course?” said I.

[038-068] “Only for their steps,” said Caddy. “In that way they practise, so

[038-069] many hours at a time, whatever steps they happen to be upon. They

[038-070] dance in the academy, and at this time of year we do figures at five

[038-071] every morning.”

[038-072] “Why, what a laborious life!” I exclaimed.

[038-073] “I assure you, my dear,” returned Caddy, smiling, “when the out-door

[038-074] apprentices ring us up in the morning (the bell rings into our room,

[038-075] not to disturb old Mr. Turveydrop), and when I put up the window and

[038-076] see them standing on the door-step with their little pumps under

[038-077] their arms, I am actually reminded of the Sweeps.”

[038-078] All this presented the art to me in a singular light, to be sure.

[038-079] Caddy enjoyed the effect of her communication and cheerfully

[038-080] recounted the particulars of her own studies.

[038-081] “You see, my dear, to save expense I ought to know something of the

[038-082] piano, and I ought to know something of the kit too, and consequently

[038-083] I have to practise those two instruments as well as the details of

[038-084] our [[professional-identity|profession]]. If Ma had been like anybody else, I might have had

[038-085] some little musical knowledge to begin upon. However, I hadn’t any;

[038-086] and that part of the work is, at first, a little discouraging, I must

[038-087] allow. But I have a very good ear, and I am used to drudgery—I have

[038-088] to thank Ma for that, at all events—and where there’s a will there’s

[038-089] a way, you know, Esther, the world over.” Saying these words, Caddy

[038-090] laughingly sat down at a little jingling square piano and really

[038-091] rattled off a quadrille with great spirit. Then she good-humouredly

[038-092] and blushingly got up again, and while she still laughed herself,

[038-093] said, “Don’t laugh at me, please; that’s a dear girl!”

[038-094] I would sooner have cried, but I did neither. I encouraged her and

[038-095] praised her with all my heart. For I conscientiously believed,

[038-096] dancing-master’s wife though she was, and dancing-mistress though in

[038-097] her limited ambition she aspired to be, she had struck out a natural,

[038-098] wholesome, loving course of industry and perseverance that was quite

[038-099] as good as a mission.

[038-100] “My dear,” said Caddy, delighted, “you can’t think how you cheer me.

[038-101] I shall owe you, you don’t know how much. What changes, Esther, even

[038-102] in my small world! You recollect that first night, when I was so

[038-103] unpolite and inky? Who would have thought, then, of my ever teaching

[038-104] people to dance, of all other possibilities and impossibilities!”

[038-105] Her husband, who had left us while we had this chat, now coming back,

[038-106] preparatory to exercising the apprentices in the ball-room, Caddy

[038-107] informed me she was quite at my disposal. But it was not my time yet,

[038-108] I was glad to tell her, for I should have been vexed to take her away

[038-109] then. Therefore we three adjourned to the apprentices together, and I

[038-110] made one in the dance.

[038-111] The apprentices were the queerest little people. Besides the

[038-112] melancholy boy, who, I hoped, had not been made so by waltzing alone

[038-113] in the empty kitchen, there were two other boys and one dirty little

[038-114] limp girl in a gauzy dress. Such a precocious little girl, with such

[038-115] a dowdy bonnet on (that, too, of a gauzy texture), who brought her

[038-116] sandalled shoes in an old threadbare velvet reticule. Such mean

[038-117] little boys, when they were not dancing, with string, and marbles,

[038-118] and cramp-bones in their pockets, and the most untidy legs and

[038-119] feet—and heels particularly.

[038-120] I asked Caddy what had made their parents choose this profession for

[038-121] them. Caddy said she didn’t know; perhaps they were designed for

[038-122] teachers, perhaps for the stage. They were all people in humble

[038-123] circumstances, and the melancholy boy’s mother kept a ginger-beer

[038-124] shop.

[038-125] We danced for an hour with great gravity, the melancholy child doing

[038-126] wonders with his lower extremities, in which there appeared to be

[038-127] some sense of enjoyment though it never rose above his waist. Caddy,

[038-128] while she was observant of her husband and was evidently founded upon

[038-129] him, had acquired a grace and self-possession of her own, which,

[038-130] united to her pretty face and figure, was uncommonly agreeable. She

[038-131] already relieved him of much of the instruction of these young

[038-132] people, and he seldom interfered except to walk his part in the

[038-133] figure if he had anything to do in it. He always played the tune. The

[038-134] affectation of the gauzy child, and her condescension to the boys,

[038-135] was a sight. And thus we danced an hour by the clock.

[038-136] When the practice was concluded, Caddy’s husband made himself ready

[038-137] to go out of town to a school, and Caddy ran away to get ready to go

[038-138] out with me. I sat in the ball-room in the interval, contemplating

[038-139] the apprentices. The two out-door boys went upon the staircase to put

[038-140] on their half-boots and pull the in-door boy’s hair, as I judged from

[038-141] the nature of his objections. Returning with their jackets buttoned

[038-142] and their pumps stuck in them, they then produced packets of cold

[038-143] bread and meat and bivouacked under a painted lyre on the wall. The

[038-144] little gauzy child, having whisked her sandals into the reticule and

[038-145] put on a trodden-down pair of shoes, shook her head into the dowdy

[038-146] bonnet at one shake, and answering my inquiry whether she liked

[038-147] dancing by replying, “Not with boys,” tied it across her chin, and

[038-148] went home contemptuous.

[038-149] “Old Mr. Turveydrop is so sorry,” said Caddy, “that he has not

[038-150] finished dressing yet and cannot have the pleasure of seeing you

[038-151] before you go. You are such a favourite of his, Esther.”

[038-152] I expressed myself much obliged to him, but did not think it

[038-153] necessary to add that I readily dispensed with this attention.

[038-154] “It takes him a long time to dress,” said Caddy, “because he is very

[038-155] much looked up to in such things, you know, and has a reputation to

[038-156] support. You can’t think how kind he is to Pa. He talks to Pa of an

[038-157] evening about the Prince Regent, and I never saw Pa so interested.”

[038-158] There was something in the picture of Mr. Turveydrop bestowing his

[038-159] deportment on Mr. Jellyby that quite took my fancy. I asked Caddy if

[038-160] he brought her papa out much.

[038-161] “No,” said Caddy, “I don’t know that he does that, but he talks to

[038-162] Pa, and Pa greatly admires him, and listens, and likes it. Of course

[038-163] I am aware that Pa has hardly any claims to deportment, but they get

[038-164] on together delightfully. You can’t think what good companions they

[038-165] make. I never saw Pa take snuff before in my life, but he takes one

[038-166] pinch out of Mr. Turveydrop’s box regularly and keeps putting it to

[038-167] his nose and taking it away again all the evening.”

[038-168] That old Mr. Turveydrop should ever, in the chances and changes of

[038-169] life, have come to the rescue of Mr. Jellyby from [[telescopic-philanthropy|Borrioboola-Gha]]

[038-170] appeared to me to be one of the pleasantest of oddities.

[038-171] “As to Peepy,” said Caddy with a little hesitation, “whom I was most

[038-172] afraid of—next to having any family of my own, Esther—as an

[038-173] inconvenience to Mr. Turveydrop, the kindness of the old gentleman to

[038-174] that child is beyond everything. He asks to see him, my dear! He lets

[038-175] him take the newspaper up to him in bed; he gives him the crusts of

[038-176] his toast to eat; he sends him on little errands about the house; he

[038-177] tells him to come to me for sixpences. In short,” said Caddy

[038-178] cheerily, “and not to prose, I am a very fortunate girl and ought to

[038-179] be very grateful. Where are we going, Esther?”

[038-180] “To the Old Street Road,” said I, “where I have a few words to say to

[038-181] the solicitor’s clerk who was sent to meet me at the coach-office on

[038-182] the very day when I came to London and first saw you, my dear. Now I

[038-183] think of it, the gentleman who brought us to your house.”

[038-184] “Then, indeed, I seem to be naturally the person to go with you,”

[038-185] returned Caddy.

[038-186] To the Old Street Road we went and there inquired at Mrs. Guppy’s

[038-187] residence for Mrs. Guppy. Mrs. Guppy, occupying the parlours and

[038-188] having indeed been visibly in danger of cracking herself like a nut

[038-189] in the front-parlour door by peeping out before she was asked for,

[038-190] immediately presented herself and requested us to walk in. She was an

[038-191] old lady in a large cap, with rather a red nose and rather an

[038-192] unsteady eye, but smiling all over. Her close little sitting-room was

[038-193] prepared for a visit, and there was a portrait of her son in it

[038-194] which, I had almost written here, was more like than life: it

[038-195] insisted upon him with such obstinacy, and was so determined not to

[038-196] let him off.

[038-197] Not only was the portrait there, but we found the original there too.

[038-198] He was dressed in a great many colours and was discovered at a table

[038-199] reading law-papers with his forefinger to his forehead.

[038-200] “Miss Summerson,” said Mr. Guppy, rising, “this is indeed an oasis.

[038-201] Mother, will you be so good as to put a chair for the other lady and

[038-202] get out of the gangway.”

[038-203] Mrs. Guppy, whose incessant smiling gave her quite a waggish

[038-204] appearance, did as her son requested and then sat down in a corner,

[038-205] holding her pocket handkerchief to her chest, like a fomentation,

[038-206] with both hands.

[038-207] I presented Caddy, and Mr. Guppy said that any friend of mine was

[038-208] more than welcome. I then proceeded to the object of my visit.

[038-209] “I took the liberty of sending you a note, sir,” said I.

[038-210] Mr. Guppy acknowledged the receipt by taking it out of his

[038-211] breast-pocket, putting it to his lips, and returning it to his pocket

[038-212] with a bow. Mr. Guppy’s mother was so diverted that she rolled her

[038-213] head as she smiled and made a silent appeal to Caddy with her elbow.

[038-214] “Could I speak to you alone for a moment?” said I.

[038-215] Anything like the jocoseness of Mr. Guppy’s mother just now, I think

[038-216] I never saw. She made no sound of laughter, but she rolled her head,

[038-217] and shook it, and put her handkerchief to her mouth, and appealed to

[038-218] Caddy with her elbow, and her hand, and her shoulder, and was so

[038-221] bedroom adjoining.

[038-223] a parent ever mindful of a son’s appiness. My mother, though highly

[038-224] exasperating to the feelings, is actuated by maternal dictates.”

[038-228] “I asked the favour of seeing you for a few moments here,” said I,
