# Krook
## Overview
Krook is the novel's most compressed allegorical figure — a rag-and-bone dealer who cannot read, yet hoards legal documents that would be valuable if anyone could decipher them. He is explicitly called "the Lord Chancellor" of his shop, making him a parodic double of the fog-bound judge in Chapter 1. His death by spontaneous combustion is the novel's most spectacular image of moral and physical decay converging.
## Role in the Novel
Krook's shop functions as a miniature Chancery: documents accumulate without resolution, value is buried under waste, and the whole structure is flammable. His death — reduced to "a smouldering suffocating vapour" — is simultaneously a gothic horror and a logical necessity. The novel has been arguing that accumulated corruption must ignite; Krook literalizes the metaphor.
