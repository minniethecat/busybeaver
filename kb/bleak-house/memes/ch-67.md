[067-001] Full seven happy years I have been the mistress of [[bleak-house|Bleak House]]. The

[067-002] few words that I have to add to what I have written are soon penned;

[067-003] then I and the unknown friend to whom I write will part for ever. Not

[067-004] without much dear remembrance on my side. Not without some, I hope,

[067-005] on his or hers.

[067-006] They gave my darling into my arms, and through many weeks I never

[067-007] left her. The little child who was to have done so much was born

[067-008] before the turf was planted on its father’s grave. It was a boy; and

[067-009] I, my husband, and my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]] gave him his father’s name.

[067-010] The help that my dear counted on did come to her, though it came, in

[067-011] the eternal wisdom, for another purpose. Though to bless and restore

[067-012] his mother, not his father, was the errand of this baby, its power

[067-013] was mighty to do it. When I saw the strength of the weak little hand

[067-014] and how its touch could heal my darling’s heart and raised hope

[067-015] within her, I felt a new sense of the goodness and the tenderness of

[067-016] God.

[067-017] They throve, and by degrees I saw my dear girl pass into my country

[067-018] garden and walk there with her infant in her arms. I was married

[067-019] then. I was the happiest of the happy.

[067-020] It was at this time that my guardian joined us and asked [[ada-clare|Ada]] when she

[067-021] would come home.

[067-022] “Both houses are your home, my dear,” said he, “but the older Bleak

[067-023] House claims priority. When you and my boy are strong enough to do

[067-024] it, come and take possession of your home.”

[067-025] Ada called him “her dearest cousin, John.” But he said, no, it must

[067-026] be guardian now. He was her guardian henceforth, and the boy’s; and

[067-027] he had an old association with the name. So she called him guardian,

[067-028] and has called him guardian ever since. The children know him by no

[067-029] other name. I say the children; I have two little daughters.

[067-030] It is difficult to believe that Charley (round-eyed still, and not at

[067-031] all grammatical) is married to a miller in our neighbourhood; yet so

[067-032] it is; and even now, looking up from my desk as I write early in the

[067-033] morning at my summer window, I see the very mill beginning to go

[067-034] round. I hope the miller will not spoil Charley; but he is very fond

[067-035] of her, and Charley is rather vain of such a match, for he is well to

[067-036] do and was in great request. So far as my small maid is concerned, I

[067-037] might suppose time to have stood for seven years as still as the mill

[067-038] did half an hour ago, since little Emma, Charley’s sister, is exactly

[067-039] what Charley used to be. As to Tom, Charley’s brother, I am really

[067-040] afraid to say what he did at school in ciphering, but I think it was

[067-041] decimals. He is apprenticed to the miller, whatever it was, and is a

[067-042] good bashful fellow, always falling in love with somebody and being

[067-043] ashamed of it.

[067-044] [[caddy-jellyby|Caddy Jellyby]] passed her very last holidays with us and was a dearer

[067-045] creature than ever, perpetually dancing in and out of the house with

[067-046] the children as if she had never given a dancing-lesson in her life.

[067-047] Caddy keeps her own little carriage now instead of hiring one, and

[067-048] lives full two miles further westward than Newman Street. She works

[067-049] very hard, her husband (an excellent one) being lame and able to do

[067-050] very little. Still, she is more than contented and does all she has

[067-051] to do with all her heart. Mr. Jellyby spends his evenings at her new

[067-052] house with his head against the wall as he used to do in her old one.

[067-053] I have heard that Mrs. Jellyby was understood to suffer great

[067-054] mortification from her daughter’s ignoble marriage and pursuits, but

[067-055] I hope she got over it in time. She has been disappointed in

[067-056] [[telescopic-philanthropy|Borrioboola-Gha]], which turned out a failure in consequence of the

[067-057] king of Borrioboola wanting to sell everybody—who survived the

[067-058] climate—for rum, but she has taken up with the rights of women to

[067-059] sit in [[parliament|Parliament]], and Caddy tells me it is a mission involving more

[067-060] correspondence than the old one. I had almost forgotten Caddy’s poor

[067-061] little girl. She is not such a mite now, but she is deaf and dumb. I

[067-062] believe there never was a better mother than Caddy, who learns, in

[067-063] her scanty intervals of leisure, innumerable deaf and dumb arts to

[067-064] soften the affliction of her child.

[067-065] As if I were never to have done with Caddy, I am reminded here of

[067-066] Peepy and old Mr. Turveydrop. Peepy is in the Custom House, and doing

[067-067] extremely well. Old Mr. Turveydrop, very apoplectic, still exhibits

[067-068] his deportment about town, still enjoys himself in the old manner, is

[067-069] still believed in in the old way. He is constant in his patronage of

[067-070] Peepy and is understood to have bequeathed him a favourite French

[067-071] clock in his dressing-room—which is not his [[inheritance-and-property|property]].

[067-072] With the first money we saved at home, we added to our pretty house

[067-073] by throwing out a little growlery expressly for my guardian, which we

[067-074] inaugurated with great splendour the next time he came down to see

[067-075] us. I try to write all this lightly, because my heart is full in

[067-076] drawing to an end, but when I write of him, my tears will have their

[067-077] way.

[067-078] I never look at him but I hear our poor dear [[richard-carstone|Richard]] calling him a

[067-079] good man. To Ada and her pretty boy, he is the fondest father; to me

[067-080] he is what he has ever been, and what name can I give to that? He is

[067-081] my husband’s best and dearest friend, he is our children’s darling,

[067-082] he is the object of our deepest love and veneration. Yet while I feel

[067-083] towards him as if he were a superior being, I am so familiar with him

[067-084] and so easy with him that I almost wonder at myself. I have never

[067-085] lost my old names, nor has he lost his; nor do I ever, when he is

[067-086] with us, sit in any other place than in my old chair at his side,

[067-087] Dame Trot, [[esther-summerson|Dame Durden]], Little Woman—all just the same as ever; and

[067-088] I answer, “Yes, dear guardian!” just the same.

[067-089] I have never known the wind to be in the east for a single moment

[067-090] since the day when he took me to the porch to read the name. I

[067-091] remarked to him once that the wind seemed never in the east now, and

[067-092] he said, no, truly; it had finally departed from that quarter on that

[067-093] very day.

[067-094] I think my darling girl is more beautiful than ever. The sorrow that

[067-095] has been in her face—for it is not there now—seems to have purified

[067-096] even its innocent expression and to have given it a diviner quality.

[067-097] Sometimes when I raise my eyes and see her in the black dress that

[067-098] she still wears, teaching my Richard, I feel—it is difficult to

[067-099] express—as if it were so good to know that she remembers her dear

[067-100] Esther in her prayers.

[067-101] I call him my Richard! But he says that he has two mamas, and I am

[067-102] one.

[067-103] We are not rich in the bank, but we have always prospered, and we

[067-104] have quite enough. I never walk out with my husband but I hear the

[067-105] people bless him. I never go into a house of any degree but I hear

[067-106] his praises or see them in grateful eyes. I never lie down at night

[067-107] but I know that in the course of that day he has alleviated pain and

[067-108] soothed some fellow-creature in the time of need. I know that from

[067-109] the beds of those who were past recovery, thanks have often, often

[067-110] gone up, in the last hour, for his patient ministration. Is not this

[067-111] to be rich?

[067-112] The people even praise me as the doctor’s wife. The people even like

[067-113] me as I go about, and make so much of me that I am quite abashed. I

[067-114] owe it all to him, my love, my pride! They like me for his sake, as I

[067-115] do everything I do in life for his sake.

[067-116] A night or two ago, after bustling about preparing for my darling and

[067-117] my guardian and little Richard, who are coming to-morrow, I was

[067-118] sitting out in the porch of all places, that dearly memorable porch,

[067-119] when Allan came home. So he said, “My precious little woman, what are

[067-120] you doing here?” And I said, “The moon is shining so brightly, Allan,

[067-121] and the night is so delicious, that I have been sitting here

[067-122] thinking.”

[067-123] “What have you been thinking about, my dear?” said Allan then.

[067-124] “How curious you are!” said I. “I am almost ashamed to tell you, but

[067-125] I will. I have been thinking about my old looks—such as they were.”

[067-126] “And what have you been thinking about THEM, my busy bee?” said

[067-127] Allan.

[067-128] “I have been thinking that I thought it was impossible that you COULD

[067-129] have loved me any better, even if I had retained them.”

[067-130] “‘Such as they were’?” said Allan, laughing.

[067-131] “Such as they were, of course.”

[067-132] “My dear Dame Durden,” said Allan, drawing my arm through his, “do

[067-133] you ever look in the glass?”

[067-134] “You know I do; you see me do it.”

[067-135] “And don’t you know that you are prettier than you ever were?”

[067-136] I did not know that; I am not certain that I know it now. But I know

[067-137] that my dearest little pets are very pretty, and that my darling is

[067-138] very beautiful, and that my husband is very handsome, and that my

[067-139] guardian has the brightest and most benevolent face that ever was

[067-140] seen, and that they can very well do without much beauty in me—even

[067-141] supposing—.
