[065-001] The term had commenced, and my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]] found an intimation from Mr.

[065-002] Kenge that the cause would come on in two days. As I had sufficient

[065-003] hopes of the will to be in a flutter about it, Allan and I agreed to

[065-004] go down to the court that morning. [[richard-carstone|Richard]] was extremely agitated and

[065-005] was so weak and low, though his illness was still of the mind, that

[065-006] my dear girl indeed had sore occasion to be supported. But she looked

[065-007] forward—a very little way now—to the help that was to come to her,

[065-008] and never drooped.

[065-009] It was at Westminster that the cause was to come on. It had come on

[065-010] there, I dare say, a hundred times before, but I could not divest

[065-011] myself of an idea that it MIGHT lead to some result now. We left home

[065-012] directly after breakfast to be at Westminster Hall in good time and

[065-013] walked down there through the lively streets—so happily and

[065-014] strangely it seemed!—together.

[065-015] As we were going along, planning what we should do for Richard and

[065-016] [[ada-clare|Ada]], I heard somebody calling “[[esther-summerson|Esther]]! My dear Esther! Esther!” And

[065-017] there was [[caddy-jellyby|Caddy Jellyby]], with her head out of the window of a little

[065-018] carriage which she hired now to go about in to her pupils (she had so

[065-019] many), as if she wanted to embrace me at a hundred yards’ distance. I

[065-020] had written her a note to tell her of all that my guardian had done,

[065-021] but had not had a moment to go and see her. Of course we turned back,

[065-022] and the affectionate girl was in that state of rapture, and was so

[065-023] overjoyed to talk about the night when she brought me the flowers,

[065-024] and was so determined to squeeze my face (bonnet and all) between her

[065-025] hands, and go on in a wild manner altogether, calling me all kinds of

[065-026] precious names, and telling Allan I had done I don’t know what for

[065-027] her, that I was just obliged to get into the little carriage and calm

[065-028] her down by letting her say and do exactly what she liked. Allan,

[065-029] standing at the window, was as pleased as Caddy; and I was as pleased

[065-030] as either of them; and I wonder that I got away as I did, rather than

[065-031] that I came off laughing, and red, and anything but tidy, and looking

[065-032] after Caddy, who looked after us out of the coach-window as long as

[065-033] she could see us.

[065-034] This made us some quarter of an hour late, and when we came to

[065-035] Westminster Hall we found that the day’s business was begun. Worse

[065-036] than that, we found such an unusual crowd in the [[chancery-court|Court of Chancery]]

[065-037] that it was full to the door, and we could neither see nor hear what

[065-038] was passing within. It appeared to be something droll, for

[065-039] occasionally there was a laugh and a cry of “Silence!” It appeared to

[065-040] be something interesting, for every one was pushing and striving to

[065-041] get nearer. It appeared to be something that made the professional

[065-042] gentlemen very merry, for there were several young counsellors in

[065-043] wigs and whiskers on the outside of the crowd, and when one of them

[065-044] told the others about it, they put their hands in their pockets, and

[065-045] quite doubled themselves up with laughter, and went stamping about

[065-046] the pavement of the Hall.

[065-047] We asked a gentleman by us if he knew what cause was on. He told us

[065-048] [[jarndyce-and-jarndyce|Jarndyce and Jarndyce]]. We asked him if he knew what was doing in it.

[065-049] He said really, no he did not, nobody ever did, but as well as he

[065-050] could make out, it was over. Over for the day? we asked him. No, he

[065-051] said, over for good.

[065-052] Over for good!

[065-053] When we heard this unaccountable answer, we looked at one another

[065-054] quite lost in amazement. Could it be possible that the will had set

[065-055] things right at last and that Richard and Ada were going to be rich?

[065-056] It seemed too good to be true. Alas it was!

[065-057] Our suspense was short, for a break-up soon took place in the crowd,

[065-058] and the people came streaming out looking flushed and hot and

[065-059] bringing a quantity of bad air with them. Still they were all

[065-060] exceedingly amused and were more like people coming out from a farce

[065-061] or a juggler than from a court of justice. We stood aside, watching

[065-062] for any countenance we knew, and presently great bundles of paper

[065-063] began to be carried out—bundles in bags, bundles too large to be got

[065-064] into any bags, immense masses of papers of all shapes and no shapes,

[065-065] which the bearers staggered under, and threw down for the time being,

[065-066] anyhow, on the Hall pavement, while they went back to bring out more.

[065-067] Even these clerks were laughing. We glanced at the papers, and seeing

[065-068] Jarndyce and Jarndyce everywhere, asked an official-looking person

[065-069] who was standing in the midst of them whether the cause was over.

[065-070] Yes, he said, it was all up with it at last, and burst out laughing

[065-071] too.

[065-072] At this juncture we perceived Mr. Kenge coming out of court with an

[065-073] affable dignity upon him, listening to [[mr-vholes|Mr. Vholes]], who was

[065-074] deferential and carried his own bag. Mr. Vholes was the first to see

[065-075] us. “Here is Miss Summerson, sir,” he said. “And [[allan-woodcourt|Mr. Woodcourt]].”

[065-076] “Oh, indeed! Yes. Truly!” said Mr. Kenge, raising his hat to me with

[065-077] polished politeness. “How do you do? Glad to see you. Mr. Jarndyce is

[065-078] not here?”

[065-079] No. He never came there, I reminded him.

[065-080] “Really,” returned Mr. Kenge, “it is as well that he is NOT here

[065-081] to-day, for his—shall I say, in my good friend’s absence, his

[065-082] indomitable singularity of opinion?—might have been strengthened,

[065-083] perhaps; not reasonably, but might have been strengthened.”

[065-084] “Pray what has been done to-day?” asked Allan.

[065-085] “I beg your pardon?” said Mr. Kenge with excessive urbanity.

[065-086] “What has been done to-day?”

[065-087] “What has been done,” repeated Mr. Kenge. “Quite so. Yes. Why, not

[065-088] much has been done; not much. We have been checked—brought up

[065-089] suddenly, I would say—upon the—shall I term it threshold?”

[065-090] “Is this will considered a genuine document, sir?” said Allan. “Will

[065-091] you tell us that?”

[065-092] “Most certainly, if I could,” said Mr. Kenge; “but we have not gone

[065-093] into that, we have not gone into that.”

[065-094] “We have not gone into that,” repeated Mr. Vholes as if his low

[065-095] inward voice were an echo.

[065-096] “You are to reflect, Mr. Woodcourt,” observed Mr. Kenge, using his

[065-097] silver trowel persuasively and smoothingly, “that this has been a

[065-098] great cause, that this has been a protracted cause, that this has

[065-099] been a complex cause. Jarndyce and Jarndyce has been termed, not

[065-100] inaptly, a monument of Chancery practice.”

[065-101] “And patience has sat upon it a long time,” said Allan.

[065-102] “Very well indeed, sir,” returned Mr. Kenge with a certain

[065-103] condescending laugh he had. “Very well! You are further to reflect,

[065-104] Mr. Woodcourt,” becoming dignified almost to severity, “that on the

[065-105] numerous difficulties, contingencies, masterly fictions, and forms of

[065-106] procedure in this great cause, there has been expended study,

[065-107] ability, eloquence, knowledge, intellect, Mr. Woodcourt, high

[065-108] intellect. For many years, the—a—I would say the flower of the bar,

[065-109] and the—a—I would presume to add, the matured autumnal fruits of

[065-110] the woolsack—have been lavished upon Jarndyce and Jarndyce. If the

[065-111] public have the benefit, and if the country have the adornment, of

[065-112] this great grasp, it must be paid for in money or money’s worth,

[065-113] sir.”

[065-114] “Mr. Kenge,” said Allan, appearing enlightened all in a moment.

[065-115] “Excuse me, our time presses. Do I understand that the whole [[inheritance-and-property|estate]]

[065-116] is found to have been absorbed in costs?”

[065-117] “Hem! I believe so,” returned Mr. Kenge. “Mr. Vholes, what do YOU

[065-118] say?”

[065-119] “I believe so,” said Mr. Vholes.

[065-120] “And that thus the suit lapses and melts away?”

[065-121] “Probably,” returned Mr. Kenge. “Mr. Vholes?”

[065-122] “Probably,” said Mr. Vholes.

[065-123] “My dearest life,” whispered Allan, “this will break Richard’s

[065-124] heart!”

[065-125] There was such a shock of apprehension in his face, and he knew

[065-126] Richard so perfectly, and I too had seen so much of his gradual

[065-127] [[ruin-and-decay|decay]], that what my dear girl had said to me in the fullness of her

[065-128] foreboding love sounded like a knell in my ears.

[065-129] “In case you should be wanting Mr. C., sir,” said Mr. Vholes, coming

[065-130] after us, “you’ll find him in court. I left him there resting himself

[065-131] a little. Good day, sir; good day, Miss Summerson.” As he gave me

[065-132] that slowly devouring look of his, while twisting up the strings of

[065-133] his bag before he hastened with it after Mr. Kenge, the benignant

[065-134] shadow of whose conversational presence he seemed afraid to leave, he

[065-135] gave one gasp as if he had swallowed the last morsel of his client,

[065-136] and his black buttoned-up unwholesome figure glided away to the low

[065-137] door at the end of the Hall.

[065-138] “My dear love,” said Allan, “leave to me, for a little while, the

[065-139] charge you gave me. Go home with this intelligence and come to Ada’s

[065-140] by and by!”

[065-141] I would not let him take me to a coach, but entreated him to go to

[065-142] Richard without a moment’s delay and leave me to do as he wished.

[065-143] Hurrying home, I found my guardian and told him gradually with what

[065-144] news I had returned. “Little woman,” said he, quite unmoved for

[065-145] himself, “to have done with the suit on any terms is a greater

[065-146] blessing than I had looked for. But my poor young cousins!”

[065-147] We talked about them all the morning and discussed what it was

[065-148] possible to do. In the afternoon my guardian walked with me to

[065-149] Symond’s Inn and left me at the door. I went upstairs. When my

[065-150] darling heard my footsteps, she came out into the small passage and

[065-151] threw her arms round my neck, but she composed herself directly and

[065-152] said that Richard had asked for me several times. Allan had found him

[065-153] sitting in the corner of the court, she told me, like a stone figure.

[065-154] On being roused, he had broken away and made as if he would have

[065-155] spoken in a fierce voice to the judge. He was stopped by his mouth

[065-156] being full of blood, and Allan had brought him home.

[065-157] He was lying on a sofa with his eyes closed when I went in. There

[065-158] were restoratives on the table; the room was made as airy as

[065-159] possible, and was darkened, and was very orderly and quiet. Allan

[065-160] stood behind him watching him gravely. His face appeared to me to be

[065-161] quite destitute of colour, and now that I saw him without his seeing

[065-162] me, I fully saw, for the first time, how worn away he was. But he

[065-163] looked handsomer than I had seen him look for many a day.

[065-164] I sat down by his side in silence. Opening his eyes by and by, he

[065-165] said in a weak voice, but with his old smile, “Dame Durden, kiss me,

[065-166] my dear!”

[065-167] It was a great comfort and surprise to me to find him in his low

[065-168] state cheerful and looking forward. He was happier, he said, in our

[065-169] intended marriage than he could find words to tell me. My husband had

[065-170] been a guardian angel to him and Ada, and he blessed us both and

[065-171] wished us all the joy that life could yield us. I almost felt as if

[065-172] my own heart would have broken when I saw him take my husband’s hand

[065-173] and hold it to his breast.

[065-174] We spoke of the future as much as possible, and he said several times

[065-175] that he must be present at our marriage if he could stand upon his

[065-176] feet. Ada would contrive to take him, somehow, he said. “Yes, surely,

[065-177] dearest Richard!” But as my darling answered him thus hopefully, so

[065-178] serene and beautiful, with the help that was to come to her so

[065-179] near—I knew—I knew!

[065-180] It was not good for him to talk too much, and when he was silent, we

[065-181] were silent too. Sitting beside him, I made a pretence of working for

[065-182] my dear, as he had always been used to joke about my being busy. Ada

[065-183] leaned upon his pillow, holding his head upon her arm. He dozed

[065-184] often, and whenever he awoke without seeing him, said first of all,

[065-185] “Where is Woodcourt?”

[065-186] Evening had come on when I lifted up my eyes and saw my guardian

[065-187] standing in the little hall. “Who is that, Dame Durden?” Richard

[065-188] asked me. The door was behind him, but he had observed in my face

[065-189] that some one was there.

[065-190] I looked to Allan for advice, and as he nodded “Yes,” bent over

[065-191] Richard and told him. My guardian saw what passed, came softly by me

[065-192] in a moment, and laid his hand on Richard’s. “Oh, sir,” said Richard,

[065-193] “you are a good man, you are a good man!” and burst into tears for

[065-194] the first time.

[065-195] My guardian, the picture of a good man, sat down in my place, keeping

[065-196] his hand on Richard’s.

[065-197] “My dear Rick,” said he, “the clouds have cleared away, and it is

[065-198] bright now. We can see now. We were all bewildered, Rick, more or

[065-199] less. What matters! And how are you, my dear boy?”

[065-200] “I am very weak, sir, but I hope I shall be stronger. I have to begin

[065-201] the world.”

[065-202] “Aye, truly; well said!” cried my guardian.

[065-203] “I will not begin it in the old way now,” said Richard with a sad

[065-204] smile. “I have learned a lesson now, sir. It was a hard one, but you

[065-205] shall be assured, indeed, that I have learned it.”

[065-206] “Well, well,” said my guardian, comforting him; “well, well, well,

[065-207] dear boy!”

[065-208] “I was thinking, sir,” resumed Richard, “that there is nothing on

[065-209] earth I should so much like to see as their house—Dame Durden’s and

[065-210] Woodcourt’s house. If I could be removed there when I begin to

[065-211] recover my strength, I feel as if I should get well there sooner than

[065-212] anywhere.”

[065-213] “Why, so have I been thinking too, Rick,” said my guardian, “and our

[065-214] little woman likewise; she and I have been talking of it this very

[065-215] day. I dare say her husband won’t object. What do you think?”

[065-216] Richard smiled and lifted up his arm to touch him as he stood behind

[065-217] the head of the couch.

[065-218] “I say nothing of Ada,” said Richard, “but I think of her, and have

[065-219] thought of her very much. Look at her! See her here, sir, bending

[065-220] over this pillow when she has so much need to rest upon it herself,

[065-221] my dear love, my poor girl!”

[065-222] He clasped her in his arms, and none of us spoke. He gradually

[065-223] released her, and she looked upon us, and looked up to heaven, and

[065-224] moved her lips.

[065-225] “When I get down to [[bleak-house|Bleak House]],” said Richard, “I shall have much to

[065-226] tell you, sir, and you will have much to show me. You will go, won’t

[065-227] you?”

[065-228] “Undoubtedly, dear Rick.”

[065-229] “Thank you; like you, like you,” said Richard. “But it’s all like

[065-230] you. They have been telling me how you planned it and how you

[065-231] remembered all Esther’s familiar tastes and ways. It will be like

[065-232] coming to the old Bleak House again.”

[065-233] “And you will come there too, I hope, Rick. I am a solitary man now,

[065-234] you know, and it will be a charity to come to me. A charity to come

[065-235] to me, my love!” he repeated to Ada as he gently passed his hand over

[065-236] her golden hair and put a lock of it to his lips. (I think he vowed

[065-237] within himself to cherish her if she were left alone.)

[065-238] “It was a troubled dream?” said Richard, clasping both my guardian’s

[065-239] hands eagerly.

[065-240] “Nothing more, Rick; nothing more.”

[065-241] “And you, being a good man, can pass it as such, and forgive and pity

[065-242] the dreamer, and be lenient and encouraging when he wakes?”

[065-243] “Indeed I can. What am I but another dreamer, Rick?”

[065-244] “I will begin the world!” said Richard with a light in his eyes.

[065-245] My husband drew a little nearer towards Ada, and I saw him solemnly

[065-246] lift up his hand to warn my guardian.

[065-247] “When shall I go from this place to that pleasant country where the

[065-248] old times are, where I shall have strength to tell what Ada has been

[065-249] to me, where I shall be able to recall my many faults and

[065-250] blindnesses, where I shall prepare myself to be a guide to my unborn

[065-251] child?” said Richard. “When shall I go?”

[065-252] “Dear Rick, when you are strong enough,” returned my guardian.

[065-253] “Ada, my darling!”

[065-254] He sought to raise himself a little. Allan raised him so that she

[065-255] could hold him on her bosom, which was what he wanted.

[065-256] “I have done you many wrongs, my own. I have fallen like a poor stray

[065-257] shadow on your way, I have married you to poverty and trouble, I have

[065-258] scattered your means to the winds. You will forgive me all this, my

[065-259] Ada, before I begin the world?”

[065-260] A smile irradiated his face as she bent to kiss him. He slowly laid

[065-261] his face down upon her bosom, drew his arms closer round her neck,

[065-262] and with one parting sob began the world. Not this world, oh, not

[065-263] this! The world that sets this right.

[065-264] When all was still, at a late hour, poor crazed [[miss-flite|Miss Flite]] came

[065-265] weeping to me and told me she had given her birds their liberty.
