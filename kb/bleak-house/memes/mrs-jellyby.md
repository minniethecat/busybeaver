# Mrs. Jellyby

## Overview

Mrs. Jellyby is the novel's most sustained satirical portrait of "telescopic philanthropy" — the habit of fixing one's benevolent attention on distant causes while neglecting the suffering within arm's reach. Obsessively devoted to the colonisation of Borrioboola-Gha, a fictional region of Africa, she conducts an enormous correspondence and dictates ceaselessly to her eldest daughter Caddy, all while her own house is in squalor, her children are unwashed and neglected, and her husband retreats into silent despair.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Jellyby appears early in the novel (Chapter 4) when Esther Summerson, Ada Clare, and Richard Carstone visit her at Thavies Inn before travelling to Bleak House. The visit is arranged by Mr. Kenge as a convenience — the Jellyby household is en route — but it serves a deeper narrative purpose: it is the first of several encounters (alongside Mrs. Pardiggle and Mr. Quale) that expose Esther to the varieties of misdirected benevolence that Dickens critiques throughout the novel.

Her most significant plot contribution is her relationship with her daughter Caddy. Caddy's misery as her mother's unpaid secretary — "I am only a drudge" — leads to her rebellion, her engagement to Prince Turveydrop, and her emergence as one of the novel's most satisfying character arcs. Mrs. Jellyby's neglect is thus generative: it produces Caddy's determination to create a functional household of her own.

## Background and History

Mrs. Jellyby is a woman of apparently comfortable middle-class means. Her husband, Mr. Jellyby, is a silent, defeated presence in the household — a man whose only response to domestic chaos is to rest his head against the wall in mute endurance. The source of the family's income is never specified, which is part of the satire: Mrs. Jellyby's philanthropy is made possible by resources she never questions and a household she never tends.

Her great project is the settlement of Borrioboola-Gha, a scheme to transplant British families to Africa to cultivate coffee and "civilise" the native population. The project involves thousands of letters, parliamentary lobbying, fundraising, and the recruitment of settlers — all conducted from her chaotic drawing-room while her own children fall down stairs unsupervised.

## Relationships

- **[[mr-jellyby|Mr. Jellyby]]**: Her husband, a ghost-like figure who has long since surrendered to his wife's monomania. His silent endurance is one of the novel's most quietly devastating portraits of a broken marriage.
- **[[caddy-jellyby|Caddy Jellyby]]**: Her eldest daughter, forced to serve as her mother's amanuensis, writing letters about Borrioboola-Gha until her fingers are stained with ink. Caddy's rebellion against this servitude drives her character development.
- **Peepy Jellyby**: Her youngest child, who falls down stairs, gets his head stuck in railings, and is generally neglected — a living indictment of his mother's priorities.
- **[[prince-turveydrop|Prince Turveydrop]]**: Her son-in-law, whose marriage to Caddy liberates her daughter from the Jellyby household.
- **[[esther-summerson|Esther Summerson]]**: Whose visit to the Jellyby household in Chapter 4 is the vehicle for the reader's introduction to the family.
- **[[mrs-pardiggle|Mrs. Pardiggle]]**: A fellow philanthropist whose domestic tyranny offers a complementary critique of organised charity.

## Key Scenes

Mrs. Jellyby's first appearance, in Chapter 4, is one of Dickens's most famous set-pieces. Esther, Ada, and Richard arrive at Thavies Inn to find a household in chaos — children tumbling, a drawing-room littered with papers — while Mrs. Jellyby sits at the centre, "a pretty very little woman" with "very good hair" and "a curious habit of looking a long way off" [004-113]. Her eyes, Esther notes, "had no more thought for the children than for the furniture":

> "Mrs. Jellyby had very good hair, but was too much occupied with her African duties to brush it." [004-113]

Mrs. Jellyby's explanation of her project to her visitors is a masterpiece of Dickensian monologue — she speaks of the climate of Borrioboola-Gha, the devotion required, the correspondence, the parliamentary interest — while around her the children descend into ever more dangerous and neglected predicaments [004-134, 004-148].

Caddy's rebellion, narrated later in the novel, provides the counterpoint. Her confession to Esther that "I am only a drudge" — writing letters about a place she will never see, for a cause she does not believe in — transforms the comedy of Chapter 4 into something more painful [005].

The eventual collapse of the Borrioboola-Gha scheme — the settlers are captured and sold into slavery by the local king, whom Mrs. Jellyby had previously praised — is reported with deadpan irony, and Mrs. Jellyby simply transfers her attention to the rights of women to sit in Parliament, demonstrating the infinite substitutability of causes that never require genuine self-scrutiny.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Jellyby is the locus classicus of Dickens's critique of "telescopic philanthropy" — a phrase he coined in this novel and that has entered the language. The critique is not of philanthropy itself but of its displacement: the Jellyby model of benevolence uses distant objects of charity to avoid the harder, closer, messier work of caring for the people one actually lives among.

The satirical method is structural rather than merely caricatural. Every detail of the Jellyby household — the sticky door-handle, the missing buttons, the tumbling Peepy — is a piece of evidence in Dickens's indictment. The case is built through accumulation, not through direct statement. Mrs. Jellyby is never explicitly called a bad mother; the facts are allowed to speak.

What makes the portrait more than topical satire (it was aimed at a specific strain of Victorian missionary fervour) is its psychological acuity. Mrs. Jellyby's philanthropy is not, at root, about Africa; it is about the gratifications of absorption, the pleasures of being wholly consumed by a great cause that never requires one to change one's actual life. Borrioboola-Gha is a screen behind which she can avoid the difficult particularities of love — the sticky door-handles, the missing buttons, the unhappy child.
