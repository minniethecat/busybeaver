[015-001] While we were in [[london|London]] [[john-jarndyce|Mr. Jarndyce]] was constantly beset by the

[015-002] crowd of excitable ladies and gentlemen whose proceedings had so much

[015-003] astonished us. Mr. Quale, who presented himself soon after our

[015-004] arrival, was in all such excitements. He seemed to project those two

[015-005] shining knobs of temples of his into everything that went on and to

[015-006] brush his hair farther and farther back, until the very roots were

[015-007] almost ready to fly out of his head in inappeasable philanthropy. All

[015-008] objects were alike to him, but he was always particularly ready for

[015-009] anything in the way of a testimonial to any one. His great power

[015-010] seemed to be his power of indiscriminate admiration. He would sit for

[015-011] any length of time, with the utmost enjoyment, bathing his temples in

[015-012] the light of any order of luminary. Having first seen him perfectly

[015-013] swallowed up in admiration of Mrs. Jellyby, I had supposed her to be

[015-014] the absorbing object of his devotion. I soon discovered my mistake

[015-015] and found him to be train-bearer and organ-blower to a whole

[015-016] procession of people.

[015-017] Mrs. Pardiggle came one day for a subscription to something, and with

[015-018] her, Mr. Quale. Whatever Mrs. Pardiggle said, Mr. Quale repeated to

[015-019] us; and just as he had drawn Mrs. Jellyby out, he drew Mrs. Pardiggle

[015-020] out. Mrs. Pardiggle wrote a letter of introduction to my guardian in

[015-021] behalf of her eloquent friend Mr. Gusher. With Mr. Gusher appeared

[015-022] Mr. Quale again. Mr. Gusher, being a flabby gentleman with a moist

[015-023] surface and eyes so much too small for his moon of a face that they

[015-024] seemed to have been originally made for somebody else, was not at

[015-025] first sight prepossessing; yet he was scarcely seated before Mr.

[015-026] Quale asked [[ada-clare|Ada]] and me, not inaudibly, whether he was not a great

[015-027] creature—which he certainly was, flabbily speaking, though Mr. Quale

[015-028] meant in intellectual beauty—and whether we were not struck by his

[015-029] massive configuration of brow. In short, we heard of a great many

[015-030] missions of various sorts among this set of people, but nothing

[015-031] respecting them was half so clear to us as that it was Mr. Quale’s

[015-032] mission to be in ecstasies with everybody else’s mission and that it

[015-033] was the most popular mission of all.

[015-034] Mr. Jarndyce had fallen into this company in the tenderness of his

[015-035] heart and his earnest desire to do all the good in his power; but

[015-036] that he felt it to be too often an unsatisfactory company, where

[015-037] benevolence took spasmodic forms, where charity was assumed as a

[015-038] regular uniform by loud professors and speculators in cheap

[015-039] notoriety, vehement in [[professional-identity|profession]], restless and vain in action,

[015-040] servile in the last degree of meanness to the great, adulatory of one

[015-041] another, and intolerable to those who were anxious quietly to help

[015-042] the weak from failing rather than with a great deal of bluster and

[015-043] self-laudation to raise them up a little way when they were down, he

[015-044] plainly told us. When a testimonial was originated to Mr. Quale by

[015-045] Mr. Gusher (who had already got one, originated by Mr. Quale), and

[015-046] when Mr. Gusher spoke for an hour and a half on the subject to a

[015-047] meeting, including two charity schools of small boys and girls, who

[015-048] were specially reminded of the widow’s mite, and requested to come

[015-049] forward with halfpence and be acceptable sacrifices, I think the wind

[015-050] was in the east for three whole weeks.

[015-051] I mention this because I am coming to [[harold-skimpole|Mr. Skimpole]] again. It seemed

[015-052] to me that his off-hand professions of childishness and carelessness

[015-053] were a great relief to my guardian, by contrast with such things, and

[015-054] were the more readily believed in since to find one perfectly

[015-055] undesigning and candid man among many opposites could not fail to

[015-056] give him pleasure. I should be sorry to imply that Mr. Skimpole

[015-057] divined this and was politic; I really never understood him well

[015-058] enough to know. What he was to my guardian, he certainly was to the

[015-059] rest of the world.

[015-060] He had not been very well; and thus, though he lived in London, we

[015-061] had seen nothing of him until now. He appeared one morning in his

[015-062] usual agreeable way and as full of pleasant spirits as ever.

[015-063] Well, he said, here he was! He had been bilious, but rich men were

[015-064] often bilious, and therefore he had been persuading himself that he

[015-065] was a man of [[inheritance-and-property|property]]. So he was, in a certain point of view—in his

[015-066] expansive intentions. He had been enriching his medical attendant in

[015-067] the most lavish manner. He had always doubled, and sometimes

[015-068] quadrupled, his fees. He had said to the doctor, “Now, my dear

[015-069] doctor, it is quite a delusion on your part to suppose that you

[015-070] attend me for nothing. I am overwhelming you with money—in my

[015-071] expansive intentions—if you only knew it!” And really (he said) he

[015-072] meant it to that degree that he thought it much the same as doing it.

[015-073] If he had had those bits of metal or thin paper to which mankind

[015-074] attached so much importance to put in the doctor’s hand, he would

[015-075] have put them in the doctor’s hand. Not having them, he substituted

[015-076] the will for the deed. Very well! If he really meant it—if his will

[015-077] were genuine and real, which it was—it appeared to him that it was

[015-078] the same as coin, and cancelled the obligation.

[015-079] “It may be, partly, because I know nothing of the value of money,”

[015-080] said Mr. Skimpole, “but I often feel this. It seems so reasonable! My

[015-081] butcher says to me he wants that little bill. It’s a part of the

[015-082] pleasant unconscious poetry of the man’s nature that he always calls

[015-083] it a ‘little’ bill—to make the payment appear easy to both of us. I

[015-084] reply to the butcher, ‘My good friend, if you knew it, you are paid.

[015-085] You haven’t had the trouble of coming to ask for the little bill. You

[015-086] are paid. I mean it.’”

[015-087] “But, suppose,” said my guardian, laughing, “he had meant the meat in

[015-088] the bill, instead of providing it?”

[015-089] “My dear Jarndyce,” he returned, “you surprise me. You take the

[015-090] butcher’s position. A butcher I once dealt with occupied that very

[015-093] honest friend?’ said I, naturally amazed by the question. ‘I like

[015-094] spring lamb!’ This was so far convincing. ‘Well, sir,’ says he, ‘I

[015-125] Summerson?”

[015-127] light-hearted manner and without the least embarrassment.

[015-128] “Oh, yes!” said I.

[015-129] “Coavinses has been arrested by the Great Bailiff,” said Mr.

[015-130] Skimpole. “He will never do violence to the sunshine any more.”

[015-131] It quite shocked me to hear it, for I had already recalled with

[015-133] the sofa that night wiping his head.

[015-134] “His successor informed me of it yesterday,” said Mr. Skimpole. “His

[015-135] successor is in my house now—in possession, I think he calls it. He

[015-136] came yesterday, on my blue-eyed daughter’s birthday. I put it to him,

[015-137] ‘This is unreasonable and inconvenient. If you had a blue-eyed

[015-138] daughter you wouldn’t like ME to come, uninvited, on HER birthday?’

[015-139] But he stayed.”

[015-140] Mr. Skimpole laughed at the pleasant absurdity and lightly touched

[015-141] the piano by which he was seated.

[015-142] “And he told me,” he said, playing little chords where I shall put

[015-143] full stops, “The Coavinses had left. Three children. No mother. And

[015-144] that Coavinses’ profession. Being unpopular. The rising Coavinses.

[015-145] Were at a considerable disadvantage.”

[015-146] Mr. Jarndyce got up, rubbing his head, and began to walk about. Mr.

[015-147] Skimpole played the melody of one of Ada’s favourite songs. Ada and I

[015-151] head, and beginning again, my guardian put his hand upon the keys and

[015-152] stopped Mr. Skimpole’s playing. “I don’t like this, Skimpole,” he

[015-153] said thoughtfully.

[015-154] Mr. Skimpole, who had quite forgotten the subject, looked up

[015-155] surprised.

[015-156] “The man was necessary,” pursued my guardian, walking backward and

[015-163] know more about this.”

[015-173] a house with barred windows, which he called Coavinses’ Castle. On

[015-178] “There was a follower, or an officer, or something, here,” said Mr.

[015-179] Jarndyce, “who is dead.”

[015-180] “Yes?” said the boy. “Well?”

[015-181] “I want to know his name, if you please?”

[015-182] “Name of Neckett,” said the boy.

[015-183] “And his address?”

[015-184] “Bell Yard,” said the boy. “Chandler’s shop, left hand side, name of

[015-185] Blinder.”

[015-186] “Was he—I don’t know how to shape the question—” murmured my

[015-187] guardian, “industrious?”

[015-188] “Was Neckett?” said the boy. “Yes, wery much so. He was never tired

[015-189] of watching. He’d set upon a post at a street corner eight or ten

[015-191] “He might have done worse,” I heard my guardian soliloquize. “He

[015-192] might have undertaken to do it and not done it. Thank you. That’s all

[015-200] perhaps both.

[015-202] miss. Three pair, if you please. Door right opposite the stairs.” And

[015-203] she handed me the key across the counter.

[015-205] that I knew what to do with it. As it could only be intended for the

[015-213] “No, sir,” said I; “I am going higher up.”

[015-237] “Charley,” said the boy, standing still to gaze at us.

[015-238] “Is Charley your brother?”

[015-239] “No. She’s my sister, Charlotte. Father called her Charley.”
