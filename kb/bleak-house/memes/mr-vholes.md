# Mr. Vholes
## Overview
Vholes is the novel's most chilling portrait of legal parasitism — a solicitor whose dull respectability masks his function as a drain on Richard's life and fortune. Dickens describes him drawing "sustenance out of the young man's very bones" (039), an image that literalizes Chancery's economic logic: the legal profession feeds on its clients, and the slower the case, the richer the feed.
## Role
Vholes presents himself as Richard's advocate while systematically consuming him. His repeated refrain — that he has a father and three daughters to support — is simultaneously a claim to respectability and a confession of motive. He is not a villain in Tulkinghorn's mould; he is something more terrifying: a functionary.
