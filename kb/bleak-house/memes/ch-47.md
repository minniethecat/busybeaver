[047-001] As [[allan-woodcourt|Allan Woodcourt]] and [[jo-crossing-sweeper|Jo]] proceed along the streets where the high

[047-002] church spires and the distances are so near and clear in the morning

[047-003] light that the city itself seems renewed by rest, Allan revolves in

[047-004] his mind how and where he shall bestow his companion. “It surely is a

[047-005] strange fact,” he considers, “that in the heart of a civilized world

[047-006] this creature in human form should be more difficult to dispose of

[047-007] than an unowned dog.” But it is none the less a fact because of its

[047-008] strangeness, and the difficulty remains.

[047-009] At first he looks behind him often to assure himself that Jo is still

[047-010] really following. But look where he will, he still beholds him close

[047-011] to the opposite houses, making his way with his wary hand from brick

[047-012] to brick and from door to door, and often, as he creeps along,

[047-013] glancing over at him watchfully. Soon satisfied that the last thing

[047-014] in his thoughts is to give him the slip, Allan goes on, considering

[047-015] with a less divided attention what he shall do.

[047-016] A breakfast-stall at a street-corner suggests the first thing to be

[047-017] done. He stops there, looks round, and beckons Jo. Jo crosses and

[047-018] comes halting and shuffling up, slowly scooping the knuckles of his

[047-019] right hand round and round in the hollowed palm of his left, kneading

[047-020] dirt with a natural pestle and mortar. What is a dainty repast to Jo

[047-021] is then set before him, and he begins to gulp the coffee and to gnaw

[047-022] the bread and butter, looking anxiously about him in all directions

[047-023] as he eats and drinks, like a scared animal.

[047-024] But he is so sick and miserable that even hunger has abandoned him.

[047-025] “I thought I was amost a-starvin, sir,” says Jo, soon putting down

[047-026] his food, “but I don’t know nothink—not even that. I don’t care for

[047-027] eating wittles nor yet for drinking on ’em.” And Jo stands shivering

[047-028] and looking at the breakfast wonderingly.

[047-029] Allan Woodcourt lays his hand upon his pulse and on his chest. “Draw

[047-030] breath, Jo!” “It draws,” says Jo, “as heavy as a cart.” He might add,

[047-031] “And rattles like it,” but he only mutters, “I’m a-moving on, sir.”

[047-032] Allan looks about for an apothecary’s shop. There is none at hand,

[047-033] but a tavern does as well or better. He obtains a little measure of

[047-034] wine and gives the lad a portion of it very carefully. He begins to

[047-035] revive almost as soon as it passes his lips. “We may repeat that

[047-036] dose, Jo,” observes Allan after watching him with his attentive face.

[047-037] “So! Now we will take five minutes’ rest, and then go on again.”

[047-038] Leaving the boy sitting on the bench of the breakfast-stall, with his

[047-039] back against an iron railing, Allan Woodcourt paces up and down in

[047-040] the early sunshine, casting an occasional look towards him without

[047-041] appearing to watch him. It requires no discernment to perceive that

[047-042] he is warmed and refreshed. If a face so shaded can brighten, his

[047-043] face brightens somewhat; and by little and little he eats the slice

[047-044] of bread he had so hopelessly laid down. Observant of these signs of

[047-045] improvement, Allan engages him in conversation and elicits to his no

[047-046] small wonder the adventure of the lady in the veil, with all its

[047-047] consequences. Jo slowly munches as he slowly tells it. When he has

[047-048] finished his story and his bread, they go on again.
