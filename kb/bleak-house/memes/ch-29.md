[029-001] [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]] is shut up, carpets are rolled into great scrolls in

[029-002] corners of comfortless rooms, bright damask does penance in brown

[029-003] holland, carving and gilding puts on mortification, and the Dedlock

[029-004] ancestors retire from the light of day again. Around and around the

[029-005] house the leaves fall thick, but never fast, for they come circling

[029-006] down with a dead lightness that is sombre and slow. Let the gardener

[029-007] sweep and sweep the turf as he will, and press the leaves into full

[029-008] barrows, and wheel them off, still they lie ankle-deep. Howls the

[029-009] shrill wind round Chesney Wold; the sharp rain beats, the windows

[029-010] rattle, and the chimneys growl. Mists hide in the avenues, veil the

[029-011] points of view, and move in funeral-wise across the rising grounds.

[029-012] On all the house there is a cold, blank smell like the smell of a

[029-013] little church, though something dryer, suggesting that the dead and

[029-014] buried Dedlocks walk there in the long nights and leave the flavour

[029-015] of their graves behind them.

[029-016] But the house in town, which is rarely in the same mind as Chesney

[029-017] Wold at the same time, seldom rejoicing when it rejoices or mourning

[029-018] when it mourns, excepting when a Dedlock dies—the house in town

[029-019] shines out awakened. As warm and bright as so much state may be, as

[029-020] delicately redolent of pleasant scents that bear no trace of winter

[029-021] as hothouse flowers can make it, soft and hushed so that the ticking

[029-022] of the clocks and the crisp burning of the fires alone disturb the

[029-023] stillness in the rooms, it seems to wrap those chilled bones of Sir

[029-024] Leicester’s in rainbow-coloured wool. And [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester]] is glad to

[029-025] repose in dignified contentment before the great fire in the library,

[029-026] condescendingly perusing the backs of his books or honouring the fine

[029-027] arts with a glance of approbation. For he has his pictures, ancient

[029-028] and modern. Some of the Fancy Ball School in which art occasionally

[029-029] condescends to become a master, which would be best catalogued like

[029-030] the miscellaneous articles in a sale. As “Three high-backed chairs, a

[029-031] table and cover, long-necked bottle (containing wine), one flask, one

[029-032] Spanish female’s costume, three-quarter face portrait of Miss Jogg

[029-033] the model, and a suit of armour containing Don Quixote.” Or “One

[029-034] stone terrace (cracked), one gondola in distance, one Venetian

[029-035] senator’s dress complete, richly embroidered white satin costume with

[029-036] profile portrait of Miss Jogg the model, one Scimitar superbly

[029-037] mounted in gold with jewelled handle, elaborate Moorish dress (very

[029-038] rare), and Othello.”

[029-039] [[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]] comes and goes pretty often, there being [[inheritance-and-property|estate]]

[029-040] business to do, leases to be renewed, and so on. He sees my Lady

[029-041] pretty often, too; and he and she are as composed, and as

[029-042] indifferent, and take as little heed of one another, as ever. Yet it

[029-043] may be that my Lady fears this Mr. Tulkinghorn and that he knows it.

[029-044] It may be that he pursues her doggedly and steadily, with no touch of

[029-045] compunction, remorse, or pity. It may be that her beauty and all the

[029-046] state and brilliancy surrounding her only gives him the greater zest

[029-047] for what he is set upon and makes him the more inflexible in it.

[029-048] Whether he be cold and cruel, whether immovable in what he has made

[029-049] his duty, whether absorbed in love of power, whether determined

[029-050] to have nothing hidden from him in ground where he has burrowed

[029-051] among secrets all his life, whether he in his heart despises the

[029-052] splendour of which he is a distant beam, whether he is always

[029-053] treasuring up slights and offences in the affability of his gorgeous

[029-054] clients—whether he be any of this, or all of this, it may be that my

[029-055] Lady had better have five thousand pairs of fashionable eyes upon

[029-056] her, in distrustful vigilance, than the two eyes of this rusty lawyer

[029-057] with his wisp of neckcloth and his dull black breeches tied with

[029-058] ribbons at the knees.

[029-059] Sir Leicester sits in my Lady’s room—that room in which Mr.

[029-060] Tulkinghorn read the affidavit in [[jarndyce-and-jarndyce|Jarndyce and Jarndyce]]—particularly

[029-061] complacent. My Lady, as on that day, sits before the fire with her

[029-062] screen in her hand. Sir Leicester is particularly complacent because

[029-063] he has found in his newspaper some congenial remarks bearing directly

[029-064] on the floodgates and the framework of society. They apply so happily

[029-065] to the late case that Sir Leicester has come from the library to my

[029-066] Lady’s room expressly to read them aloud. “The man who wrote this

[029-067] article,” he observes by way of preface, nodding at the fire as if he

[029-068] were nodding down at the man from a mount, “has a well-balanced

[029-069] mind.”

[029-070] The man’s mind is not so well balanced but that he bores my Lady,

[029-071] who, after a languid effort to listen, or rather a languid

[029-072] resignation of herself to a show of listening, becomes distraught and

[029-073] falls into a contemplation of the fire as if it were her fire at

[029-074] Chesney Wold, and she had never left it. Sir Leicester, quite

[029-075] unconscious, reads on through his double eye-glass, occasionally

[029-076] stopping to remove his glass and express approval, as “Very true

[029-077] indeed,” “Very properly put,” “I have frequently made the same remark

[029-078] myself,” invariably losing his place after each observation, and

[029-079] going up and down the column to find it again.

[029-080] Sir Leicester is reading with infinite gravity and state when the

[029-081] door opens, and the Mercury in powder makes this strange

[029-082] announcement, “The young man, my Lady, of the name of Guppy.”

[029-083] Sir Leicester pauses, stares, repeats in a killing voice, “The young

[029-084] man of the name of Guppy?”

[029-085] Looking round, he beholds the young man of the name of Guppy, much

[029-086] discomfited and not presenting a very impressive letter of

[029-087] introduction in his manner and appearance.

[029-088] “Pray,” says Sir Leicester to Mercury, “what do you mean by

[029-089] announcing with this abruptness a young man of the name of Guppy?”

[029-090] “I beg your pardon, Sir Leicester, but my Lady said she would see the

[029-091] young man whenever he called. I was not aware that you were here, Sir

[029-092] Leicester.”

[029-093] With this apology, Mercury directs a scornful and indignant look at

[029-094] the young man of the name of Guppy which plainly says, “What do you

[029-095] come calling here for and getting ME into a row?”

[029-096] “It’s quite right. I gave him those directions,” says my Lady. “Let

[029-097] the young man wait.”

[029-098] “By no means, my Lady. Since he has your orders to come, I will not

[029-099] interrupt you.” Sir Leicester in his gallantry retires, rather

[029-100] declining to accept a bow from the young man as he goes out and

[029-101] majestically supposing him to be some shoemaker of intrusive

[029-102] appearance.

[029-103] [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]] looks imperiously at her visitor when the servant has

[029-104] left the room, casting her eyes over him from head to foot. She

[029-105] suffers him to stand by the door and asks him what he wants.

[029-106] “That your ladyship would have the kindness to oblige me with a

[029-107] little conversation,” returns Mr. Guppy, embarrassed.

[029-108] “You are, of course, the person who has written me so many [[document-and-letter|letters]]?”

[029-109] “Several, your ladyship. Several before your ladyship condescended to

[029-110] favour me with an answer.”

[029-111] “And could you not take the same means of rendering a Conversation

[029-112] unnecessary? Can you not still?”

[029-113] Mr. Guppy screws his mouth into a silent “No!” and shakes his head.

[029-114] “You have been strangely importunate. If it should appear, after all,

[029-115] that what you have to say does not concern me—and I don’t know how

[029-116] it can, and don’t expect that it will—you will allow me to cut you

[029-117] short with but little ceremony. Say what you have to say, if you

[029-118] please.”

[029-119] My Lady, with a careless toss of her screen, turns herself towards

[029-120] the fire again, sitting almost with her back to the young man of the

[029-121] name of Guppy.

[029-122] “With your ladyship’s permission, then,” says the young man, “I will

[029-123] now enter on my business. Hem! I am, as I told your ladyship in my

[029-124] first letter, in the law. Being in the law, I have learnt the habit

[029-125] of not committing myself in writing, and therefore I did not mention

[029-126] to your ladyship the name of the firm with which I am connected and

[029-127] in which my standing—and I may add income—is tolerably good. I may

[029-128] now state to your ladyship, in confidence, that the name of that firm

[029-129] is [[kenge-and-carboy|Kenge and Carboy]], of Lincoln’s Inn, which may not be altogether

[029-130] unknown to your ladyship in connexion with the case in [[chancery-court|Chancery]] of

[029-131] Jarndyce and Jarndyce.”

[029-132] My Lady’s figure begins to be expressive of some attention. She has

[029-133] ceased to toss the screen and holds it as if she were listening.

[029-134] “Now, I may say to your ladyship at once,” says Mr. Guppy, a little

[029-135] emboldened, “it is no matter arising out of Jarndyce and Jarndyce

[029-136] that made me so desirous to speak to your ladyship, which conduct I

[029-137] have no doubt did appear, and does appear, obtrusive—in fact, almost

[029-138] blackguardly.”

[029-139] After waiting for a moment to receive some assurance to the contrary,

[029-140] and not receiving any, Mr. Guppy proceeds, “If it had been Jarndyce

[029-141] and Jarndyce, I should have gone at once to your ladyship’s

[029-142] solicitor, Mr. Tulkinghorn, of the Fields. I have the pleasure of

[029-143] being acquainted with Mr. Tulkinghorn—at least we move when we meet

[029-144] one another—and if it had been any business of that sort, I should

[029-145] have gone to him.”

[029-146] My Lady turns a little round and says, “You had better sit down.”

[029-147] “Thank your ladyship.” Mr. Guppy does so. “Now, your ladyship”—Mr.

[029-148] Guppy refers to a little slip of paper on which he has made small

[029-149] notes of his line of argument and which seems to involve him in the

[029-150] densest obscurity whenever he looks at it—“I—Oh, yes!—I place

[029-151] myself entirely in your ladyship’s hands. If your ladyship was to

[029-152] make any complaint to Kenge and Carboy or to Mr. Tulkinghorn of the

[029-153] present visit, I should be placed in a very disagreeable situation.

[029-154] That, I openly admit. Consequently, I rely upon your ladyship’s

[029-155] honour.”

[029-156] My Lady, with a disdainful gesture of the hand that holds the screen,

[029-157] assures him of his being worth no complaint from her.

[029-158] “Thank your ladyship,” says Mr. Guppy; “quite satisfactory.

[029-159] Now—I—dash it!—The fact is that I put down a head or two here of

[029-160] the order of the points I thought of touching upon, and they’re

[029-161] written short, and I can’t quite make out what they mean. If your

[029-162] ladyship will excuse me taking it to the window half a moment, I—”

[029-163] Mr. Guppy, going to the window, tumbles into a pair of love-birds, to

[029-164] whom he says in his confusion, “I beg your pardon, I am sure.” This

[029-165] does not tend to the greater legibility of his notes. He murmurs,

[029-166] growing warm and red and holding the slip of paper now close to his

[029-167] eyes, now a long way off, “C.S. What’s C.S. for? Oh! C.S.! Oh, I

[029-168] know! Yes, to be sure!” And comes back enlightened.

[029-169] “I am not aware,” says Mr. Guppy, standing midway between my Lady and

[029-170] his chair, “whether your ladyship ever happened to hear of, or to

[029-171] see, a young lady of the name of Miss [[esther-summerson|Esther Summerson]].”

[029-172] My Lady’s eyes look at him full. “I saw a young lady of that name not

[029-173] long ago. This past autumn.”

[029-174] “Now, did it strike your ladyship that she was like anybody?” asks

[029-175] Mr. Guppy, crossing his arms, holding his head on one side, and

[029-176] scratching the corner of his mouth with his memoranda.

[029-177] My Lady removes her eyes from him no more.

[029-178] “No.”

[029-179] “Not like your ladyship’s family?”

[029-180] “No.”

[029-181] “I think your ladyship,” says Mr. Guppy, “can hardly remember Miss

[029-182] Summerson’s face?”

[029-183] “I remember the young lady very well. What has this to do with me?”

[029-184] “Your ladyship, I do assure you that having Miss Summerson’s image

[029-185] imprinted on my ’eart—which I mention in confidence—I found, when I

[029-186] had the honour of going over your ladyship’s mansion of Chesney Wold

[029-187] while on a short out in the county of Lincolnshire with a friend,

[029-188] such a resemblance between Miss Esther Summerson and your ladyship’s

[029-189] own portrait that it completely knocked me over, so much so that I

[029-190] didn’t at the moment even know what it WAS that knocked me over. And

[029-191] now I have the honour of beholding your ladyship near (I have often,

[029-192] since that, taken the liberty of looking at your ladyship in your

[029-193] carriage in the park, when I dare say you was not aware of me, but I

[029-194] never saw your ladyship so near), it’s really more surprising than I

[029-195] thought it.”

[029-196] Young man of the name of Guppy! There have been times, when ladies

[029-197] lived in strongholds and had unscrupulous attendants within call,

[029-198] when that poor life of yours would NOT have been worth a minute’s

[029-199] purchase, with those beautiful eyes looking at you as they look at

[029-200] this moment.

[029-201] My Lady, slowly using her little hand-screen as a fan, asks him again

[029-202] what he supposes that his taste for likenesses has to do with her.

[029-203] “Your ladyship,” replies Mr. Guppy, again referring to his paper, “I

[029-204] am coming to that. Dash these notes! Oh! ‘Mrs. Chadband.’ Yes.” Mr.

[029-205] Guppy draws his chair a little forward and seats himself again. My

[029-206] Lady reclines in her chair composedly, though with a trifle less of

[029-207] graceful ease than usual perhaps, and never falters in her steady

[029-208] gaze. “A—stop a minute, though!” Mr. Guppy refers again. “E.S.

[029-209] twice? Oh, yes! Yes, I see my way now, right on.”

[029-210] Rolling up the slip of paper as an instrument to point his speech

[029-211] with, Mr. Guppy proceeds.

[029-212] “Your ladyship, there is a mystery about Miss Esther Summerson’s

[029-213] birth and bringing up. I am informed of that fact because—which I

[029-214] mention in confidence—I know it in the way of my [[professional-identity|profession]] at Kenge

[029-215] and Carboy’s. Now, as I have already mentioned to your ladyship, Miss

[029-216] Summerson’s image is imprinted on my ’eart. If I could clear this

[029-217] mystery for her, or prove her to be well related, or find that having

[029-218] the honour to be a remote branch of your ladyship’s family she had a

[029-219] right to be made a party in Jarndyce and Jarndyce, why, I might make

[029-220] a sort of a claim upon Miss Summerson to look with an eye of more

[029-221] dedicated favour on my proposals than she has exactly done as yet. In

[029-222] fact, as yet she hasn’t favoured them at all.”

[029-223] A kind of angry smile just dawns upon my Lady’s face.

[029-224] “Now, it’s a very singular circumstance, your ladyship,” says Mr.

[029-225] Guppy, “though one of those circumstances that do fall in the way of

[029-226] us professional men—which I may call myself, for though not

[029-227] admitted, yet I have had a present of my articles made to me by Kenge

[029-228] and Carboy, on my mother’s advancing from the principal of her little

[029-229] income the money for the stamp, which comes heavy—that I have

[029-230] encountered the person who lived as servant with the lady who brought

[029-231] Miss Summerson up before [[john-jarndyce|Mr. Jarndyce]] took charge of her. That lady

[029-232] was a Miss Barbary, your ladyship.”

[029-233] Is the dead colour on my Lady’s face reflected from the screen which

[029-234] has a green silk ground and which she holds in her raised hand as if

[029-235] she had forgotten it, or is it a dreadful paleness that has fallen on

[029-236] her?

[029-237] “Did your ladyship,” says Mr. Guppy, “ever happen to hear of Miss

[029-238] Barbary?”

[029-239] “I don’t know. I think so. Yes.”

[029-240] “Was Miss Barbary at all connected with your ladyship’s family?”

[029-241] My Lady’s lips move, but they utter nothing. She shakes her head.

[029-242] “NOT connected?” says Mr. Guppy. “Oh! Not to your ladyship’s

[029-243] knowledge, perhaps? Ah! But might be? Yes.” After each of these

[029-244] interrogatories, she has inclined her head. “Very good! Now, this

[029-245] Miss Barbary was extremely close—seems to have been extraordinarily

[029-246] close for a female, females being generally (in common life at least)

[029-247] rather given to conversation—and my witness never had an idea

[029-248] whether she possessed a single relative. On one occasion, and only

[029-249] one, she seems to have been confidential to my witness on a single

[029-250] point, and she then told her that the little girl’s real name was not

[029-251] Esther Summerson, but Esther Hawdon.”

[029-252] “My God!”

[029-253] Mr. Guppy stares. Lady Dedlock sits before him looking him through,

[029-254] with the same dark shade upon her face, in the same attitude even to

[029-255] the holding of the screen, with her lips a little apart, her brow a

[029-256] little contracted, but for the moment dead. He sees her consciousness

[029-257] return, sees a tremor pass across her frame like a ripple over water,

[029-258] sees her lips shake, sees her compose them by a great effort, sees

[029-259] her force herself back to the knowledge of his presence and of what

[029-260] he has said. All this, so quickly, that her exclamation and her dead

[029-261] condition seem to have passed away like the features of those

[029-262] long-preserved dead bodies sometimes opened up in tombs, which,

[029-263] struck by the air like lightning, vanish in a breath.

[029-264] “Your ladyship is acquainted with the name of Hawdon?”

[029-265] “I have heard it before.”

[029-266] “Name of any collateral or remote branch of your ladyship’s family?”

[029-267] “No.”

[029-268] “Now, your ladyship,” says Mr. Guppy, “I come to the last point of

[029-269] the case, so far as I have got it up. It’s going on, and I shall

[029-270] gather it up closer and closer as it goes on. Your ladyship must

[029-271] know—if your ladyship don’t happen, by any chance, to know

[029-272] already—that there was found dead at the house of a person named

[029-273] [[krook|Krook]], near [[chancery-lane|Chancery Lane]], some time ago, a law-writer in great

[029-274] distress. Upon which law-writer there was an inquest, and which

[029-275] law-writer was an anonymous character, his name being unknown. But,

[029-276] your ladyship, I have discovered very lately that that law-writer’s

[029-277] name was Hawdon.”

[029-278] “And what is THAT to me?”

[029-279] “Aye, your ladyship, that’s the question! Now, your ladyship, a queer

[029-280] thing happened after that man’s death. A lady started up, a disguised

[029-281] lady, your ladyship, who went to look at the scene of action and went

[029-282] to look at his grave. She hired a crossing-sweeping boy to show it

[029-283] her. If your ladyship would wish to have the boy produced in

[029-284] corroboration of this statement, I can lay my hand upon him at any

[029-285] time.”

[029-286] The wretched boy is nothing to my Lady, and she does NOT wish to have

[029-287] him produced.

[029-288] “Oh, I assure your ladyship it’s a very queer start indeed,” says Mr.

[029-289] Guppy. “If you was to hear him tell about the rings that sparkled on

[029-290] her fingers when she took her glove off, you’d think it quite

[029-291] romantic.”

[029-292] There are diamonds glittering on the hand that holds the screen. My

[029-293] Lady trifles with the screen and makes them glitter more, again with

[029-294] that expression which in other times might have been so dangerous to

[029-295] the young man of the name of Guppy.

[029-296] “It was supposed, your ladyship, that he left no rag or scrap behind

[029-297] him by which he could be possibly identified. But he did. He left a

[029-298] bundle of old letters.”

[029-299] The screen still goes, as before. All this time her eyes never once

[029-300] release him.

[029-301] “They were taken and secreted. And to-morrow night, your ladyship,

[029-302] they will come into my possession.”

[029-303] “Still I ask you, what is this to me?”

[029-304] “Your ladyship, I conclude with that.” Mr. Guppy rises. “If you think

[029-305] there’s enough in this chain of circumstances put together—in the

[029-306] undoubted strong likeness of this young lady to your ladyship, which

[029-307] is a positive fact for a jury; in her having been brought up by Miss

[029-308] Barbary; in Miss Barbary stating Miss Summerson’s real name to be

[029-309] Hawdon; in your ladyship’s knowing both these names VERY WELL; and in

[029-310] Hawdon’s dying as he did—to give your ladyship a family interest in

[029-311] going further into the case, I will bring these papers here. I don’t

[029-312] know what they are, except that they are old letters: I have never

[029-313] had them in my possession yet. I will bring those papers here as soon

[029-314] as I get them and go over them for the first time with your ladyship.

[029-315] I have told your ladyship my object. I have told your ladyship that I

[029-316] should be placed in a very disagreeable situation if any complaint

[029-317] was made, and all is in strict confidence.”

[029-318] Is this the full purpose of the young man of the name of Guppy, or

[029-319] has he any other? Do his words disclose the length, breadth, depth,

[029-320] of his object and suspicion in coming here; or if not, what do they

[029-321] hide? He is a match for my Lady there. She may look at him, but he

[029-322] can look at the table and keep that witness-box face of his from

[029-323] telling anything.

[029-324] “You may bring the letters,” says my Lady, “if you choose.”

[029-325] “Your ladyship is not very encouraging, upon my word and honour,”

[029-326] says Mr. Guppy, a little injured.

[029-327] “You may bring the letters,” she repeats in the same tone, “if

[029-328] you—please.”

[029-329] “It shall be done. I wish your ladyship good day.”

[029-330] On a table near her is a rich bauble of a casket, barred and clasped

[029-331] like an old strong-chest. She, looking at him still, takes it to her

[029-332] and unlocks it.

[029-333] “Oh! I assure your ladyship I am not actuated by any motives of that

[029-334] sort,” says Mr. Guppy, “and I couldn’t accept anything of the kind. I

[029-335] wish your ladyship good day, and am much obliged to you all the

[029-336] same.”

[029-337] So the young man makes his bow and goes downstairs, where the

[029-338] supercilious Mercury does not consider himself called upon to leave

[029-339] his Olympus by the hall-fire to let the young man out.

[029-340] As Sir Leicester basks in his library and dozes over his newspaper,

[029-341] is there no influence in the house to startle him, not to say to make

[029-342] the very trees at Chesney Wold fling up their knotted arms, the very

[029-343] portraits frown, the very armour stir?

[029-344] No. Words, sobs, and cries are but air, and air is so shut in and

[029-345] shut out throughout the house in town that sounds need be uttered

[029-346] trumpet-tongued indeed by my Lady in her chamber to carry any faint

[029-347] vibration to Sir Leicester’s ears; and yet this cry is in the house,

[029-348] going upward from a wild figure on its knees.

[029-349] “O my child, my child! Not dead in the first hours of her life, as my

[029-350] cruel sister told me, but sternly nurtured by her, after she had

[029-351] renounced me and my name! O my child, O my child!”
