[027-001] [[george-rouncewell|Mr. George]] has not far to ride with folded arms upon the box, for

[027-002] their destination is Lincoln’s Inn Fields. When the driver stops his

[027-003] horses, Mr. George alights, and looking in at the window, says,

[027-004] “What, [[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]]’s your man, is he?”

[027-005] “Yes, my dear friend. Do you know him, Mr. George?”

[027-006] “Why, I have heard of him—seen him too, I think. But I don’t know

[027-007] him, and he don’t know me.”

[027-008] There ensues the carrying of Mr. Smallweed upstairs, which is done to

[027-009] perfection with the trooper’s help. He is borne into Mr.

[027-010] Tulkinghorn’s great room and deposited on the Turkey rug before the

[027-011] fire. Mr. Tulkinghorn is not within at the present moment but will be

[027-012] back directly. The occupant of the pew in the hall, having said thus

[027-013] much, stirs the fire and leaves the triumvirate to warm themselves.

[027-014] Mr. George is mightily curious in respect of the room. He looks up at

[027-015] the painted ceiling, looks round at the old law-books, contemplates

[027-016] the portraits of the great clients, reads aloud the names on the

[027-017] boxes.

[027-018] “‘[[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester Dedlock]], Baronet,’” Mr. George reads thoughtfully.

[027-019] “Ha! ‘Manor of [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]].’ Humph!” Mr. George stands looking at

[027-020] these boxes a long while—as if they were pictures—and comes back to

[027-021] the fire repeating, “Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet, and Manor of

[027-022] Chesney Wold, hey?”

[027-023] “Worth a mint of money, Mr. George!” whispers Grandfather Smallweed,

[027-024] rubbing his legs. “Powerfully rich!”

[027-025] “Who do you mean? This old gentleman, or the Baronet?”

[027-026] “This gentleman, this gentleman.”

[027-027] “So I have heard; and knows a thing or two, I’ll hold a wager. Not

[027-028] bad quarters, either,” says Mr. George, looking round again. “See the

[027-029] strong-box yonder!”

[027-030] This reply is cut short by Mr. Tulkinghorn’s arrival. There is no

[027-031] change in him, of course. Rustily drest, with his spectacles in his

[027-032] hand, and their very case worn threadbare. In manner, close and dry.

[027-033] In voice, husky and low. In face, watchful behind a blind; habitually

[027-034] not uncensorious and contemptuous perhaps. The peerage may have

[027-035] warmer worshippers and faithfuller believers than Mr. Tulkinghorn,

[027-036] after all, if everything were known.

[027-037] “Good morning, Mr. Smallweed, good morning!” he says as he comes in.

[027-038] “You have brought the sergeant, I see. Sit down, sergeant.”

[027-039] As Mr. Tulkinghorn takes off his gloves and puts them in his hat, he

[027-040] looks with half-closed eyes across the room to where the trooper

[027-041] stands and says within himself perchance, “You’ll do, my friend!”

[027-042] “Sit down, sergeant,” he repeats as he comes to his table, which is

[027-043] set on one side of the fire, and takes his easy-chair. “Cold and raw

[027-044] this morning, cold and raw!” Mr. Tulkinghorn warms before the bars,

[027-045] alternately, the palms and knuckles of his hands and looks (from

[027-046] behind that blind which is always down) at the trio sitting in a

[027-047] little semicircle before him.

[027-048] “Now, I can feel what I am about” (as perhaps he can in two senses),

[027-049] “Mr. Smallweed.” The old gentleman is newly shaken up by Judy to bear

[027-050] his part in the conversation. “You have brought our good friend the

[027-051] sergeant, I see.”

[027-052] “Yes, sir,” returns Mr. Smallweed, very servile to the lawyer’s

[027-053] wealth and influence.

[027-054] “And what does the sergeant say about this business?”

[027-055] “Mr. George,” says Grandfather Smallweed with a tremulous wave of his

[027-056] shrivelled hand, “this is the gentleman, sir.”

[027-057] Mr. George salutes the gentleman but otherwise sits bolt upright and

[027-058] profoundly silent—very forward in his chair, as if the full

[027-059] complement of regulation appendages for a field-day hung about him.

[027-060] Mr. Tulkinghorn proceeds, “Well, George—I believe your name is

[027-061] George?”

[027-062] “It is so, Sir.”

[027-063] “What do you say, George?”

[027-064] “I ask your pardon, sir,” returns the trooper, “but I should wish to

[027-065] know what YOU say?”

[027-066] “Do you mean in point of reward?”

[027-067] “I mean in point of everything, sir.”

[027-068] This is so very trying to Mr. Smallweed’s temper that he suddenly

[027-069] breaks out with “You’re a brimstone beast!” and as suddenly asks

[027-070] pardon of Mr. Tulkinghorn, excusing himself for this slip of the

[027-071] tongue by saying to Judy, “I was thinking of your grandmother, my

[027-072] dear.”

[027-073] “I supposed, sergeant,” Mr. Tulkinghorn resumes as he leans on one

[027-074] side of his chair and crosses his legs, “that Mr. Smallweed might

[027-075] have sufficiently explained the matter. It lies in the smallest

[027-076] compass, however. You served under Captain Hawdon at one time, and

[027-077] were his attendant in illness, and rendered him many little services,

[027-078] and were rather in his confidence, I am told. That is so, is it not?”

[027-079] “Yes, sir, that is so,” says Mr. George with military brevity.

[027-080] “Therefore you may happen to have in your possession

[027-081] something—anything, no matter what; accounts, instructions, orders,

[027-082] a letter, anything—in Captain Hawdon’s [[document-and-letter|writing]]. I wish to compare

[027-083] his writing with some that I have. If you can give me the

[027-084] opportunity, you shall be rewarded for your trouble. Three, four,

[027-085] five, guineas, you would consider handsome, I dare say.”

[027-086] “Noble, my dear friend!” cries Grandfather Smallweed, screwing up his

[027-087] eyes.

[027-088] “If not, say how much more, in your conscience as a soldier, you can

[027-089] demand. There is no need for you to part with the writing, against

[027-090] your inclination—though I should prefer to have it.”

[027-091] Mr. George sits squared in exactly the same attitude, looks at the

[027-092] painted ceiling, and says never a word. The irascible Mr. Smallweed

[027-093] scratches the air.

[027-094] “The question is,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn in his methodical, subdued,

[027-095] uninterested way, “first, whether you have any of Captain Hawdon’s

[027-096] writing?”

[027-097] “First, whether I have any of Captain Hawdon’s writing, sir,” repeats

[027-098] Mr. George.

[027-099] “Secondly, what will satisfy you for the trouble of producing it?”

[027-100] “Secondly, what will satisfy me for the trouble of producing it,

[027-101] sir,” repeats Mr. George.

[027-102] “Thirdly, you can judge for yourself whether it is at all like that,”

[027-103] says Mr. Tulkinghorn, suddenly handing him some sheets of written

[027-104] paper tied together.

[027-105] “Whether it is at all like that, sir. Just so,” repeats Mr. George.

[027-106] All three repetitions Mr. George pronounces in a mechanical manner,

[027-107] looking straight at Mr. Tulkinghorn; nor does he so much as glance at

[027-108] the affidavit in [[jarndyce-and-jarndyce|Jarndyce and Jarndyce]], that has been given to him

[027-109] for his inspection (though he still holds it in his hand), but

[027-110] continues to look at the lawyer with an air of troubled meditation.

[027-111] “Well?” says Mr. Tulkinghorn. “What do you say?”

[027-112] “Well, sir,” replies Mr. George, rising erect and looking immense, “I

[027-113] would rather, if you’ll excuse me, have nothing to do with this.”

[027-114] Mr. Tulkinghorn, outwardly quite undisturbed, demands, “Why not?”

[027-115] “Why, sir,” returns the trooper. “Except on military compulsion, I am

[027-116] not a man of business. Among civilians I am what they call in

[027-117] Scotland a ne’er-do-weel. I have no head for papers, sir. I can stand

[027-118] any fire better than a fire of cross questions. I mentioned to Mr.

[027-119] Smallweed, only an hour or so ago, that when I come into things of

[027-120] this kind I feel as if I was being smothered. And that is my

[027-121] sensation,” says Mr. George, looking round upon the company, “at the

[027-122] present moment.”

[027-123] With that, he takes three strides forward to replace the papers on

[027-124] the lawyer’s table and three strides backward to resume his former

[027-125] station, where he stands perfectly upright, now looking at the ground

[027-126] and now at the painted ceiling, with his hands behind him as if to

[027-127] prevent himself from accepting any other document whatever.

[027-128] Under this provocation, Mr. Smallweed’s favourite adjective of

[027-129] disparagement is so close to his tongue that he begins the words “my

[027-130] dear friend” with the monosyllable “brim,” thus converting the

[027-131] possessive pronoun into brimmy and appearing to have an impediment in

[027-132] his speech. Once past this difficulty, however, he exhorts his dear

[027-133] friend in the tenderest manner not to be rash, but to do what so

[027-134] eminent a gentleman requires, and to do it with a good grace,

[027-135] confident that it must be unobjectionable as well as profitable. Mr.

[027-136] Tulkinghorn merely utters an occasional sentence, as, “You are the

[027-137] best judge of your own interest, sergeant.” “Take care you do no harm

[027-138] by this.” “Please yourself, please yourself.” “If you know what you

[027-139] mean, that’s quite enough.” These he utters with an appearance of

[027-140] perfect indifference as he looks over the papers on his table and

[027-141] prepares to write a letter.

[027-142] Mr. George looks distrustfully from the painted ceiling to the

[027-143] ground, from the ground to Mr. Smallweed, from Mr. Smallweed to Mr.

[027-144] Tulkinghorn, and from Mr. Tulkinghorn to the painted ceiling again,

[027-145] often in his perplexity changing the leg on which he rests.

[027-146] “I do assure you, sir,” says Mr. George, “not to say it offensively,

[027-147] that between you and Mr. Smallweed here, I really am being smothered

[027-148] fifty times over. I really am, sir. I am not a match for you

[027-149] gentlemen. Will you allow me to ask why you want to see the captain’s

[027-150] hand, in the case that I could find any specimen of it?”

[027-151] Mr. Tulkinghorn quietly shakes his head. “No. If you were a man of

[027-152] business, sergeant, you would not need to be informed that there are

[027-153] confidential reasons, very harmless in themselves, for many such

[027-154] wants in the [[professional-identity|profession]] to which I belong. But if you are afraid of

[027-155] doing any injury to Captain Hawdon, you may set your mind at rest

[027-156] about that.”

[027-157] “Aye! He is dead, sir.”

[027-158] “IS he?” Mr. Tulkinghorn quietly sits down to write.

[027-159] “Well, sir,” says the trooper, looking into his hat after another

[027-160] disconcerted pause, “I am sorry not to have given you more

[027-161] satisfaction. If it would be any satisfaction to any one that I

[027-162] should be confirmed in my judgment that I would rather have nothing

[027-163] to do with this by a friend of mine who has a better head for

[027-164] business than I have, and who is an old soldier, I am willing to

[027-165] consult with him. I—I really am so completely smothered myself at

[027-166] present,” says Mr. George, passing his hand hopelessly across his

[027-167] brow, “that I don’t know but what it might be a satisfaction to me.”

[027-168] Mr. Smallweed, hearing that this authority is an old soldier, so

[027-169] strongly inculcates the expediency of the trooper’s taking counsel

[027-170] with him, and particularly informing him of its being a question of

[027-171] five guineas or more, that Mr. George engages to go and see him. Mr.

[027-172] Tulkinghorn says nothing either way.

[027-173] “I’ll consult my friend, then, by your leave, sir,” says the trooper,

[027-174] “and I’ll take the liberty of looking in again with the final answer

[027-175] in the course of the day. Mr. Smallweed, if you wish to be carried

[027-176] downstairs—”

[027-177] “In a moment, my dear friend, in a moment. Will you first let me

[027-178] speak half a word with this gentleman in private?”

[027-179] “Certainly, sir. Don’t hurry yourself on my account.” The trooper

[027-180] retires to a distant part of the room and resumes his curious

[027-181] inspection of the boxes, strong and otherwise.

[027-182] “If I wasn’t as weak as a brimstone baby, sir,” whispers Grandfather

[027-183] Smallweed, drawing the lawyer down to his level by the lapel of his

[027-184] coat and flashing some half-quenched green fire out of his angry

[027-185] eyes, “I’d tear the writing away from him. He’s got it buttoned in

[027-186] his breast. I saw him put it there. Judy saw him put it there. Speak

[027-187] up, you crabbed image for the sign of a walking-stick shop, and say

[027-188] you saw him put it there!”

[027-189] This vehement conjuration the old gentleman accompanies with such a

[027-190] thrust at his granddaughter that it is too much for his strength, and

[027-191] he slips away out of his chair, drawing Mr. Tulkinghorn with him,

[027-192] until he is arrested by Judy, and well shaken.

[027-193] “Violence will not do for me, my friend,” Mr. Tulkinghorn then

[027-194] remarks coolly.

[027-195] “No, no, I know, I know, sir. But it’s chafing and

[027-196] galling—it’s—it’s worse than your smattering chattering magpie of a

[027-197] grandmother,” to the imperturbable Judy, who only looks at the fire,

[027-198] “to know he has got what’s wanted and won’t give it up. He, not to

[027-199] give it up! HE! A vagabond! But never mind, sir, never mind. At the

[027-200] most, he has only his own way for a little while. I have him

[027-201] periodically in a vice. I’ll twist him, sir. I’ll screw him, sir. If

[027-202] he won’t do it with a good grace, I’ll make him do it with a bad one,

[027-203] sir! Now, my dear Mr. George,” says Grandfather Smallweed, winking at

[027-204] the lawyer hideously as he releases him, “I am ready for your kind

[027-205] assistance, my excellent friend!”

[027-206] Mr. Tulkinghorn, with some shadowy sign of amusement manifesting

[027-207] itself through his self-possession, stands on the hearth-rug with his

[027-208] back to the fire, watching the disappearance of Mr. Smallweed and

[027-209] acknowledging the trooper’s parting salute with one slight nod.

[027-210] It is more difficult to get rid of the old gentleman, Mr. George

[027-211] finds, than to bear a hand in carrying him downstairs, for when he is

[027-212] replaced in his conveyance, he is so loquacious on the subject of the

[027-213] guineas and retains such an affectionate hold of his button—having,

[027-214] in truth, a secret longing to rip his coat open and rob him—that

[027-215] some degree of force is necessary on the trooper’s part to effect a

[027-216] separation. It is accomplished at last, and he proceeds alone in

[027-217] quest of his adviser.

[027-218] By the cloisterly Temple, and by Whitefriars (there, not without a

[027-219] glance at Hanging-Sword Alley, which would seem to be something in

[027-220] his way), and by Blackfriars Bridge, and Blackfriars Road, Mr. George

[027-221] sedately marches to a street of little shops lying somewhere in that

[027-222] ganglion of roads from Kent and Surrey, and of streets from the

[027-223] bridges of [[london|London]], centring in the far-famed elephant who has lost

[027-224] his castle formed of a thousand four-horse coaches to a stronger iron

[027-225] monster than he, ready to chop him into mince-meat any day he dares.

[027-226] To one of the little shops in this street, which is a musician’s

[027-227] shop, having a few fiddles in the window, and some Pan’s pipes and a

[027-228] tambourine, and a triangle, and certain elongated scraps of music,

[027-229] Mr. George directs his massive tread. And halting at a few paces from

[027-230] it, as he sees a soldierly looking woman, with her outer skirts

[027-231] tucked up, come forth with a small wooden tub, and in that tub

[027-232] commence a-whisking and a-splashing on the margin of the pavement,

[027-233] Mr. George says to himself, “She’s as usual, washing greens. I never

[027-234] saw her, except upon a baggage-waggon, when she wasn’t washing

[027-235] greens!”

[027-236] The subject of this reflection is at all events so occupied in

[027-237] washing greens at present that she remains unsuspicious of Mr.

[027-238] George’s approach until, lifting up herself and her tub together when

[027-239] she has poured the water off into the gutter, she finds him standing

[027-240] near her. Her reception of him is not flattering.

[027-241] “George, I never see you but I wish you was a hundred mile away!”

[027-242] The trooper, without remarking on this welcome, follows into the

[027-243] musical-instrument shop, where the lady places her tub of greens upon

[027-244] the counter, and having shaken hands with him, rests her arms upon

[027-245] it.

[027-246] “I never,” she says, “George, consider Matthew Bagnet safe a minute

[027-247] when you’re near him. You are that restless and that roving—”

[027-248] “Yes! I know I am, Mrs. Bagnet. I know I am.”

[027-249] “You know you are!” says Mrs. Bagnet. “What’s the use of that? WHY

[027-250] are you?”

[027-251] “The nature of the animal, I suppose,” returns the trooper

[027-252] good-humouredly.

[027-253] “Ah!” cries Mrs. Bagnet, something shrilly. “But what satisfaction

[027-254] will the nature of the animal be to me when the animal shall have

[027-255] tempted my Mat away from the musical business to New Zealand or

[027-256] Australey?”

[027-257] Mrs. Bagnet is not at all an ill-looking woman. Rather large-boned, a

[027-258] little coarse in the grain, and freckled by the sun and wind which

[027-259] have tanned her hair upon the forehead, but healthy, wholesome, and

[027-260] bright-eyed. A strong, busy, active, honest-faced woman of from

[027-261] forty-five to fifty. Clean, hardy, and so economically dressed

[027-262] (though substantially) that the only article of ornament of which she

[027-263] stands possessed appear’s to be her wedding-ring, around which her

[027-264] finger has grown to be so large since it was put on that it will

[027-265] never come off again until it shall mingle with Mrs. Bagnet’s dust.

[027-266] “Mrs. Bagnet,” says the trooper, “I am on my parole with you. Mat

[027-267] will get no harm from me. You may trust me so far.”

[027-268] “Well, I think I may. But the very looks of you are unsettling,” Mrs.

[027-269] Bagnet rejoins. “Ah, George, George! If you had only settled down and

[027-270] married Joe Pouch’s widow when he died in North America, SHE’D have

[027-271] combed your hair for you.”

[027-272] “It was a chance for me, certainly,” returns the trooper half

[027-273] laughingly, half seriously, “but I shall never settle down into a

[027-274] respectable man now. Joe Pouch’s widow might have done me good—there

[027-275] was something in her, and something of her—but I couldn’t make up my

[027-276] mind to it. If I had had the luck to meet with such a wife as Mat

[027-277] found!”

[027-278] Mrs. Bagnet, who seems in a virtuous way to be under little reserve

[027-279] with a good sort of fellow, but to be another good sort of fellow

[027-280] herself for that matter, receives this compliment by flicking Mr.

[027-281] George in the face with a head of greens and taking her tub into the

[027-282] little room behind the shop.

[027-283] “Why, Quebec, my poppet,” says George, following, on invitation, into

[027-284] that department. “And little Malta, too! Come and kiss your Bluffy!”

[027-285] These young ladies—not supposed to have been actually christened by

[027-286] the names applied to them, though always so called in the family from

[027-287] the places of their birth in barracks—are respectively employed on

[027-288] three-legged stools, the younger (some five or six years old) in

[027-289] learning her letters out of a penny primer, the elder (eight or nine

[027-290] perhaps) in teaching her and sewing with great assiduity. Both hail

[027-291] Mr. George with acclamations as an old friend and after some kissing

[027-292] and romping plant their stools beside him.

[027-293] “And how’s young Woolwich?” says Mr. George.

[027-294] “Ah! There now!” cries Mrs. Bagnet, turning about from her saucepans

[027-295] (for she is cooking dinner) with a bright flush on her face. “Would

[027-296] you believe it? Got an engagement at the theayter, with his father,

[027-297] to play the fife in a military piece.”

[027-298] “Well done, my godson!” cries Mr. George, slapping his thigh.

[027-299] “I believe you!” says Mrs. Bagnet. “He’s a Briton. That’s what

[027-300] Woolwich is. A Briton!”

[027-301] “And Mat blows away at his bassoon, and you’re respectable civilians

[027-302] one and all,” says Mr. George. “Family people. Children growing up.

[027-303] Mat’s old mother in Scotland, and your old father somewhere else,

[027-304] corresponded with, and helped a little, and—well, well! To be sure,

[027-305] I don’t know why I shouldn’t be wished a hundred mile away, for I

[027-306] have not much to do with all this!”

[027-307] Mr. George is becoming thoughtful, sitting before the fire in the

[027-308] whitewashed room, which has a sanded floor and a barrack smell and

[027-309] contains nothing superfluous and has not a visible speck of dirt or

[027-310] dust in it, from the faces of Quebec and Malta to the bright tin pots

[027-312] thoughtful, sitting here while Mrs. Bagnet is busy, when Mr. Bagnet

[027-313] and young Woolwich opportunely come home. Mr. Bagnet is an

[027-314] ex-artilleryman, tall and upright, with shaggy eyebrows and whiskers

[027-315] like the fibres of a coco-nut, not a hair upon his head, and a torrid

[027-316] complexion. His voice, short, deep, and resonant, is not at all

[027-317] unlike the tones of the instrument to which he is devoted. Indeed

[027-318] there may be generally observed in him an unbending, unyielding,

[027-319] brass-bound air, as if he were himself the bassoon of the human

[027-320] orchestra. Young Woolwich is the type and model of a young drummer.

[027-321] Both father and son salute the trooper heartily. He saying, in due

[027-322] season, that he has come to advise with Mr. Bagnet, Mr. Bagnet

[027-323] hospitably declares that he will hear of no business until after

[027-324] dinner and that his friend shall not partake of his counsel without

[027-325] first partaking of boiled pork and greens. The trooper yielding to

[027-326] this invitation, he and Mr. Bagnet, not to embarrass the domestic

[027-327] preparations, go forth to take a turn up and down the little street,

[027-328] which they promenade with measured tread and folded arms, as if it

[027-329] were a rampart.

[027-330] “George,” says Mr. Bagnet. “You know me. It’s my old girl that

[027-331] advises. She has the head. But I never own to it before her.

[027-332] Discipline must be maintained. Wait till the greens is off her mind.

[027-333] Then we’ll consult. Whatever the old girl says, do—do it!”

[027-334] “I intend to, Mat,” replies the other. “I would sooner take her

[027-335] opinion than that of a college.”

[027-336] “College,” returns Mr. Bagnet in short sentences, bassoon-like. “What

[027-337] college could you leave—in another quarter of the world—with

[027-338] nothing but a grey cloak and an umbrella—to make its way home to

[027-339] Europe? The old girl would do it to-morrow. Did it once!”

[027-340] “You are right,” says Mr. George.

[027-341] “What college,” pursues Bagnet, “could you set up in life—with two

[027-343] sand—and the rest of the change out of sixpence in money? That’s

[027-344] what the old girl started on. In the present business.”

[027-345] “I am rejoiced to hear it’s thriving, Mat.”

[027-346] “The old girl,” says Mr. Bagnet, acquiescing, “saves. Has a stocking

[027-347] somewhere. With money in it. I never saw it. But I know she’s got it.

[027-348] Wait till the greens is off her mind. Then she’ll set you up.”

[027-349] “She is a treasure!” exclaims Mr. George.

[027-350] “She’s more. But I never own to it before her. Discipline must be

[027-354] girl said it wouldn’t do; intention good, but want of flexibility;

[027-358] George remarks that she looks as fresh as a rose and as sound as an

[027-359] apple.

[027-360] “The old girl,” says Mr. Bagnet in reply, “is a thoroughly fine

[027-361] woman. Consequently she is like a thoroughly fine day. Gets finer as

[027-362] she gets on. I never saw the old girl’s equal. But I never own to it

[027-363] before her. Discipline must be maintained!”

[027-364] Proceeding to converse on indifferent matters, they walk up and down

[027-365] the little street, keeping step and time, until summoned by Quebec

[027-366] and Malta to do justice to the pork and greens, over which Mrs.

[027-367] Bagnet, like a military chaplain, says a short grace. In the

[027-368] distribution of these comestibles, as in every other household duty,
