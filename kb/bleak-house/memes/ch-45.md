[045-001] One morning when I had done jingling about with my baskets of keys,

[045-002] as my beauty and I were walking round and round the garden I happened

[045-003] to turn my eyes towards the house and saw a long thin shadow going in

[045-004] which looked like [[mr-vholes|Mr. Vholes]]. [[ada-clare|Ada]] had been telling me only that

[045-005] morning of her hopes that [[richard-carstone|Richard]] might exhaust his ardour in the

[045-006] [[chancery-court|Chancery]] suit by being so very earnest in it; and therefore, not to

[045-007] damp my dear girl’s spirits, I said nothing about Mr. Vholes’s

[045-008] shadow.

[045-009] Presently came Charley, lightly winding among the bushes and tripping

[045-010] along the paths, as rosy and pretty as one of Flora’s attendants

[045-011] instead of my maid, saying, “Oh, if you please, miss, would you step

[045-012] and speak to [[john-jarndyce|Mr. Jarndyce]]!”

[045-013] It was one of Charley’s peculiarities that whenever she was charged

[045-014] with a message she always began to deliver it as soon as she beheld,

[045-015] at any distance, the person for whom it was intended. Therefore I saw

[045-016] Charley asking me in her usual form of words to “step and speak” to

[045-017] Mr. Jarndyce long before I heard her. And when I did hear her, she

[045-018] had said it so often that she was out of breath.

[045-019] I told Ada I would make haste back and inquired of Charley as we went

[045-020] in whether there was not a gentleman with Mr. Jarndyce. To which

[045-021] Charley, whose grammar, I confess to my [[secret-and-shame|shame]], never did any credit

[045-022] to my educational powers, replied, “Yes, miss. Him as come down in

[045-023] the country with Mr. Richard.”

[045-024] A more complete contrast than my guardian and Mr. Vholes I suppose

[045-025] there could not be. I found them looking at one another across a

[045-026] table, the one so open and the other so close, the one so broad and

[045-027] upright and the other so narrow and stooping, the one giving out what

[045-028] he had to say in such a rich ringing voice and the other keeping it

[045-029] in in such a cold-blooded, gasping, fish-like manner that I thought I

[045-030] never had seen two people so unmatched.

[045-031] “You know Mr. Vholes, my dear,” said my guardian. Not with the

[045-032] greatest urbanity, I must say.

[045-033] Mr. Vholes rose, gloved and buttoned up as usual, and seated himself

[045-034] again, just as he had seated himself beside Richard in the gig. Not

[045-035] having Richard to look at, he looked straight before him.

[045-036] “Mr. Vholes,” said my guardian, eyeing his black figure as if he were

[045-037] a bird of ill omen, “has brought an ugly report of our most

[045-038] unfortunate Rick.” Laying a marked emphasis on “most unfortunate” as

[045-039] if the words were rather descriptive of his connexion with Mr.

[045-040] Vholes.

[045-041] I sat down between them; Mr. Vholes remained immovable, except that

[045-042] he secretly picked at one of the red pimples on his yellow face with

[045-043] his black glove.

[045-044] “And as Rick and you are happily good friends, I should like to

[045-045] know,” said my guardian, “what you think, my dear. Would you be so

[045-046] good as to—as to speak up, Mr. Vholes?”

[045-047] Doing anything but that, Mr. Vholes observed, “I have been saying

[045-048] that I have reason to know, Miss Summerson, as Mr. C.’s professional

[045-049] adviser, that Mr. C.’s circumstances are at the present moment in an

[045-050] embarrassed state. Not so much in point of amount as owing to the

[045-051] peculiar and pressing nature of liabilities Mr. C. has incurred and

[045-052] the means he has of liquidating or meeting the same. I have staved

[045-053] off many little matters for Mr. C., but there is a limit to staving

[045-054] off, and we have reached it. I have made some advances out of pocket

[045-055] to accommodate these unpleasantnesses, but I necessarily look to

[045-056] being repaid, for I do not pretend to be a man of capital, and I have

[045-057] a father to support in the Vale of Taunton, besides striving to

[045-058] realize some little independence for three dear girls at home. My

[045-059] apprehension is, Mr. C.’s circumstances being such, lest it should

[045-060] end in his obtaining leave to part with his commission, which at all

[045-061] events is desirable to be made known to his connexions.”

[045-062] Mr. Vholes, who had looked at me while speaking, here emerged into

[045-063] the silence he could hardly be said to have broken, so stifled was

[045-064] his tone, and looked before him again.

[045-065] “Imagine the poor fellow without even his present resource,” said my

[045-066] guardian to me. “Yet what can I do? You know him, [[esther-summerson|Esther]]. He would

[045-067] never accept of help from me now. To offer it or hint at it would be

[045-068] to drive him to an extremity, if nothing else did.”

[045-069] Mr. Vholes hereupon addressed me again.

[045-070] “What Mr. Jarndyce remarks, miss, is no doubt the case, and is the

[045-071] difficulty. I do not see that anything is to be done. I do not say

[045-072] that anything is to be done. Far from it. I merely come down here

[045-073] under the seal of confidence and mention it in order that everything

[045-074] may be openly carried on and that it may not be said afterwards that

[045-075] everything was not openly carried on. My wish is that everything

[045-076] should be openly carried on. I desire to leave a good name behind me.

[045-077] If I consulted merely my own interests with Mr. C., I should not be

[045-078] here. So insurmountable, as you must well know, would be his

[045-079] objections. This is not a professional attendance. This can he

[045-080] charged to nobody. I have no interest in it except as a member of

[045-081] society and a father—AND a son,” said Mr. Vholes, who had nearly

[045-082] forgotten that point.

[045-083] It appeared to us that Mr. Vholes said neither more nor less than the

[045-084] truth in intimating that he sought to divide the responsibility, such

[045-085] as it was, of knowing Richard’s situation. I could only suggest that

[045-086] I should go down to Deal, where Richard was then stationed, and see

[045-087] him, and try if it were possible to avert the worst. Without

[045-088] consulting Mr. Vholes on this point, I took my guardian aside to

[045-089] propose it, while Mr. Vholes gauntly stalked to the fire and warmed

[045-090] his funeral gloves.

[045-091] The fatigue of the journey formed an immediate objection on my

[045-092] guardian’s part, but as I saw he had no other, and as I was only too

[045-093] happy to go, I got his consent. We had then merely to dispose of Mr.

[045-094] Vholes.

[045-095] “Well, sir,” said Mr. Jarndyce, “Miss Summerson will communicate with

[045-096] Mr. Carstone, and you can only hope that his position may be yet

[045-097] retrievable. You will allow me to order you lunch after your journey,

[045-098] sir.”

[045-099] “I thank you, Mr. Jarndyce,” said Mr. Vholes, putting out his long

[045-100] black sleeve to check the ringing of the bell, “not any. I thank you,

[045-101] no, not a morsel. My digestion is much impaired, and I am but a poor

[045-102] knife and fork at any time. If I was to partake of solid food at this

[045-103] period of the day, I don’t know what the consequences might be.

[045-104] Everything having been openly carried on, sir, I will now with your

[045-105] permission take my leave.”

[045-106] “And I would that you could take your leave, and we could all take

[045-107] our leave, Mr. Vholes,” returned my guardian bitterly, “of a cause

[045-108] you know of.”

[045-109] Mr. Vholes, whose black dye was so deep from head to foot that it had

[045-110] quite steamed before the fire, diffusing a very unpleasant perfume,

[045-111] made a short one-sided inclination of his head from the neck and

[045-112] slowly shook it.

[045-113] “We whose ambition it is to be looked upon in the light of

[045-114] respectable practitioners, sir, can but put our shoulders to the

[045-115] wheel. We do it, sir. At least, I do it myself; and I wish to think

[045-116] well of my professional brethren, one and all. You are sensible of an

[045-117] obligation not to refer to me, miss, in communicating with Mr. C.?”

[045-118] I said I would be careful not to do it.

[045-119] “Just so, miss. Good morning. Mr. Jarndyce, good morning, sir.” Mr.

[045-120] Vholes put his dead glove, which scarcely seemed to have any hand in

[045-121] it, on my fingers, and then on my guardian’s fingers, and took his

[045-122] long thin shadow away. I thought of it on the outside of the coach,

[045-123] passing over all the sunny landscape between us and [[london|London]], chilling

[045-124] the seed in the ground as it glided along.

[045-125] Of course it became necessary to tell Ada where I was going and why I

[045-126] was going, and of course she was anxious and distressed. But she was

[045-127] too true to Richard to say anything but words of pity and words of

[045-128] excuse, and in a more loving spirit still—my dear devoted girl!—she

[045-129] wrote him a long letter, of which I took charge.

[045-130] Charley was to be my travelling companion, though I am sure I wanted

[045-131] none and would willingly have left her at home. We all went to London

[045-132] that afternoon, and finding two places in the mail, secured them. At

[045-133] our usual bed-time, Charley and I were rolling away seaward with the

[045-134] Kentish [[document-and-letter|letters]].

[045-135] It was a night’s journey in those coach times, but we had the mail to

[045-136] ourselves and did not find the night very tedious. It passed with me

[045-137] as I suppose it would with most people under such circumstances. At

[045-138] one while my journey looked hopeful, and at another hopeless. Now I

[045-139] thought I should do some good, and now I wondered how I could ever

[045-140] have supposed so. Now it seemed one of the most reasonable things in

[045-141] the world that I should have come, and now one of the most

[045-142] unreasonable. In what state I should find Richard, what I should say

[045-143] to him, and what he would say to me occupied my mind by turns with

[045-144] these two states of feeling; and the wheels seemed to play one tune

[045-145] (to which the burden of my guardian’s letter set itself) over and

[045-146] over again all night.

[045-147] At last we came into the narrow streets of Deal, and very gloomy they

[045-148] were upon a raw misty morning. The long flat beach, with its little

[045-149] irregular houses, wooden and brick, and its litter of capstans, and

[045-150] great boats, and sheds, and bare upright poles with tackle and

[045-151] blocks, and loose gravelly waste places overgrown with grass and

[045-152] weeds, wore as dull an appearance as any place I ever saw. The sea

[045-153] was heaving under a thick white [[fog-motif|fog]]; and nothing else was moving but

[045-154] a few early ropemakers, who, with the yarn twisted round their

[045-155] bodies, looked as if, tired of their present state of existence, they

[045-156] were spinning themselves into cordage.

[045-157] But when we got into a warm room in an excellent hotel and sat down,

[045-158] comfortably washed and dressed, to an early breakfast (for it was too

[045-159] late to think of going to bed), Deal began to look more cheerful. Our

[045-160] little room was like a ship’s cabin, and that delighted Charley very

[045-161] much. Then the fog began to rise like a curtain, and numbers of ships

[045-162] that we had had no idea were near appeared. I don’t know how many

[045-163] sail the waiter told us were then lying in the downs. Some of these

[045-164] vessels were of grand size—one was a large Indiaman just come home;

[045-165] and when the sun shone through the clouds, making silvery pools in

[045-166] the dark sea, the way in which these ships brightened, and shadowed,

[045-167] and changed, amid a bustle of boats pulling off from the shore to

[045-168] them and from them to the shore, and a general life and motion in

[045-169] themselves and everything around them, was most beautiful.

[045-170] The large Indiaman was our great attraction because she had come into

[045-171] the downs in the night. She was surrounded by boats, and we said how

[045-172] glad the people on board of her must be to come ashore. Charley was

[045-173] curious, too, about the voyage, and about the heat in India, and the

[045-174] serpents and the tigers; and as she picked up such information much

[045-175] faster than grammar, I told her what I knew on those points. I told

[045-176] her, too, how people in such voyages were sometimes wrecked and cast

[045-177] on rocks, where they were saved by the intrepidity and humanity of

[045-178] one man. And Charley asking how that could be, I told her how we knew

[045-179] at home of such a case.

[045-180] I had thought of sending Richard a note saying I was there, but it

[045-181] seemed so much better to go to him without preparation. As he lived

[045-182] in barracks I was a little doubtful whether this was feasible, but we

[045-183] went out to reconnoitre. Peeping in at the gate of the barrack-yard,

[045-184] we found everything very quiet at that time in the morning, and I

[045-185] asked a sergeant standing on the guardhouse-steps where he lived. He

[045-186] sent a man before to show me, who went up some bare stairs, and

[045-187] knocked with his knuckles at a door, and left us.

[045-188] “Now then!” cried Richard from within. So I left Charley in the

[045-189] little passage, and going on to the half-open door, said, “Can I come

[045-190] in, Richard? It’s only Dame Durden.”

[045-191] He was writing at a table, with a great confusion of clothes, tin

[045-192] cases, books, boots, brushes, and portmanteaus strewn all about the

[045-193] floor. He was only half dressed—in plain clothes, I observed, not in

[045-194] uniform—and his hair was unbrushed, and he looked as wild as his

[045-195] room. All this I saw after he had heartily welcomed me and I was

[045-196] seated near him, for he started upon hearing my voice and caught me

[045-197] in his arms in a moment. Dear Richard! He was ever the same to me.

[045-198] Down to—ah, poor poor fellow!—to the end, he never received me but

[045-199] with something of his old merry boyish manner.

[045-200] “Good heaven, my dear little woman,” said he, “how do you come here?

[045-201] Who could have thought of seeing you! Nothing the matter? Ada is

[045-202] well?”

[045-203] “Quite well. Lovelier than ever, Richard!”

[045-204] “Ah!” he said, leaning back in his chair. “My poor cousin! I was

[045-205] writing to you, Esther.”

[045-206] So worn and haggard as he looked, even in the fullness of his

[045-207] handsome youth, leaning back in his chair and crushing the closely

[045-208] written sheet of paper in his hand!

[045-209] “Have you been at the trouble of writing all that, and am I not to

[045-210] read it after all?” I asked.

[045-211] “Oh, my dear,” he returned with a hopeless gesture. “You may read it

[045-212] in the whole room. It is all over here.”

[045-213] I mildly entreated him not to be despondent. I told him that I had

[045-214] heard by chance of his being in difficulty and had come to consult

[045-215] with him what could best be done.

[045-216] “Like you, Esther, but useless, and so NOT like you!” said he with a

[045-217] melancholy smile. “I am away on leave this day—should have been gone

[045-218] in another hour—and that is to smooth it over, for my selling out.

[045-219] Well! Let bygones be bygones. So this calling follows the rest. I

[045-220] only want to have been in the church to have made the round of all

[045-221] the professions.”

[045-222] “Richard,” I urged, “it is not so hopeless as that?”

[045-223] “Esther,” he returned, “it is indeed. I am just so near disgrace as

[045-224] that those who are put in authority over me (as the catechism goes)

[045-225] would far rather be without me than with me. And they are right.

[045-226] Apart from debts and duns and all such drawbacks, I am not fit even

[045-227] for this employment. I have no care, no mind, no heart, no soul, but

[045-228] for one thing. Why, if this bubble hadn’t broken now,” he said,

[045-229] tearing the letter he had written into fragments and moodily casting

[045-230] them away, by driblets, “how could I have gone abroad? I must have

[045-231] been ordered abroad, but how could I have gone? How could I, with my

[045-232] experience of that thing, trust even Vholes unless I was at his

[045-233] back!”

[045-234] I suppose he knew by my face what I was about to say, but he caught

[045-235] the hand I had laid upon his arm and touched my own lips with it to

[045-236] prevent me from going on.

[045-237] “No, Dame Durden! Two subjects I forbid—must forbid. The first is

[045-238] John Jarndyce. The second, you know what. Call it madness, and I tell

[045-239] you I can’t help it now, and can’t be sane. But it is no such thing;

[045-240] it is the one object I have to pursue. It is a pity I ever was

[045-241] prevailed upon to turn out of my road for any other. It would be

[045-242] wisdom to abandon it now, after all the time, anxiety, and pains I

[045-243] have bestowed upon it! Oh, yes, true wisdom. It would be very

[045-244] agreeable, too, to some people; but I never will.”

[045-245] He was in that mood in which I thought it best not to increase his

[045-246] determination (if anything could increase it) by opposing him. I took

[045-247] out Ada’s letter and put it in his hand.

[045-248] “Am I to read it now?” he asked.

[045-249] As I told him yes, he laid it on the table, and resting his head upon

[045-250] his hand, began. He had not read far when he rested his head upon his

[045-251] two hands—to hide his face from me. In a little while he rose as if

[045-252] the light were bad and went to the window. He finished reading it

[045-253] there, with his back towards me, and after he had finished and had

[045-254] folded it up, stood there for some minutes with the letter in his

[045-255] hand. When he came back to his chair, I saw tears in his eyes.

[045-256] “Of course, Esther, you know what she says here?” He spoke in a

[045-257] softened voice and kissed the letter as he asked me.

[045-258] “Yes, Richard.”

[045-259] “Offers me,” he went on, tapping his foot upon the floor, “the little

[045-260] [[inheritance-and-property|inheritance]] she is certain of so soon—just as little and as much as

[045-261] I have wasted—and begs and prays me to take it, set myself right

[045-262] with it, and remain in the service.”

[045-263] “I know your welfare to be the dearest wish of her heart,” said I.

[045-264] “And, oh, my dear Richard, Ada’s is a noble heart.”

[045-265] “I am sure it is. I—I wish I was dead!”

[045-266] He went back to the window, and laying his arm across it, leaned his

[045-267] head down on his arm. It greatly affected me to see him so, but I

[045-268] hoped he might become more yielding, and I remained silent. My

[045-269] experience was very limited; I was not at all prepared for his

[045-270] rousing himself out of this emotion to a new sense of injury.

[045-271] “And this is the heart that the same John Jarndyce, who is not

[045-272] otherwise to be mentioned between us, stepped in to estrange from

[045-273] me,” said he indignantly. “And the dear girl makes me this generous

[045-274] offer from under the same John Jarndyce’s roof, and with the same

[045-275] John Jarndyce’s gracious consent and connivance, I dare say, as a new

[045-276] means of buying me off.”

[045-277] “Richard!” I cried out, rising hastily. “I will not hear you say such

[045-278] shameful words!” I was very angry with him indeed, for the first time

[045-279] in my life, but it only lasted a moment. When I saw his worn young

[045-280] face looking at me as if he were sorry, I put my hand on his shoulder

[045-281] and said, “If you please, my dear Richard, do not speak in such a

[045-282] tone to me. Consider!”

[045-283] He blamed himself exceedingly and told me in the most generous manner

[045-284] that he had been very wrong and that he begged my pardon a thousand

[045-285] times. At that I laughed, but trembled a little too, for I was rather

[045-286] fluttered after being so fiery.

[045-287] “To accept this offer, my dear Esther,” said he, sitting down beside

[045-288] me and resuming our conversation, “—once more, pray, pray forgive

[045-289] me; I am deeply grieved—to accept my dearest cousin’s offer is, I

[045-290] need not say, impossible. Besides, I have letters and papers that I

[045-291] could show you which would convince you it is all over here. I have

[045-292] done with the red coat, believe me. But it is some satisfaction, in

[045-293] the midst of my troubles and perplexities, to know that I am pressing

[045-294] Ada’s interests in pressing my own. Vholes has his shoulder to the

[045-295] wheel, and he cannot help urging it on as much for her as for me,

[045-296] thank God!”

[045-297] His sanguine hopes were rising within him and lighting up his

[045-298] features, but they made his face more sad to me than it had been

[045-299] before.

[045-300] “No, no!” cried Richard exultingly. “If every farthing of Ada’s

[045-301] little fortune were mine, no part of it should be spent in retaining

[045-302] me in what I am not fit for, can take no interest in, and am weary

[045-303] of. It should be devoted to what promises a better return, and should

[045-304] be used where she has a larger stake. Don’t be uneasy for me! I shall

[045-305] now have only one thing on my mind, and Vholes and I will work it. I

[045-306] shall not be without means. Free of my commission, I shall be able to

[045-307] compound with some small usurers who will hear of nothing but their

[045-308] bond now—Vholes says so. I should have a balance in my favour

[045-309] anyway, but that would swell it. Come, come! You shall carry a letter

[045-310] to Ada from me, Esther, and you must both of you be more hopeful of

[045-311] me and not believe that I am quite cast away just yet, my dear.”

[045-312] I will not repeat what I said to Richard. I know it was tiresome, and

[045-313] nobody is to suppose for a moment that it was at all wise. It only

[045-314] came from my heart. He heard it patiently and feelingly, but I saw

[045-315] that on the two subjects he had reserved it was at present hopeless

[045-316] to make any representation to him. I saw too, and had experienced in

[045-317] this very interview, the sense of my guardian’s remark that it was

[045-318] even more mischievous to use persuasion with him than to leave him as

[045-319] he was.

[045-320] Therefore I was driven at last to asking Richard if he would mind

[045-321] convincing me that it really was all over there, as he had said, and

[045-322] that it was not his mere impression. He showed me without hesitation

[045-323] a correspondence making it quite plain that his retirement was

[045-324] arranged. I found, from what he told me, that Mr. Vholes had copies

[045-325] of these papers and had been in consultation with him throughout.

[045-326] Beyond ascertaining this, and having been the bearer of Ada’s letter,

[045-327] and being (as I was going to be) Richard’s companion back to London,

[045-328] I had done no good by coming down. Admitting this to myself with a

[045-329] reluctant heart, I said I would return to the hotel and wait until he

[045-330] joined me there, so he threw a cloak over his shoulders and saw me to

[045-331] the gate, and Charley and I went back along the beach.

[045-332] There was a concourse of people in one spot, surrounding some naval

[045-333] officers who were landing from a boat, and pressing about them with

[045-334] unusual interest. I said to Charley this would be one of the great

[045-335] Indiaman’s boats now, and we stopped to look.

[045-336] The gentlemen came slowly up from the waterside, speaking

[045-337] good-humouredly to each other and to the people around and glancing

[045-338] about them as if they were glad to be in England again. “Charley,

[045-339] Charley,” said I, “come away!” And I hurried on so swiftly that my

[045-340] little maid was surprised.

[045-341] It was not until we were shut up in our cabin-room and I had had time

[045-342] to take breath that I began to think why I had made such haste. In

[045-343] one of the sunburnt faces I had recognized Mr. [[allan-woodcourt|Allan Woodcourt]], and I

[045-344] had been afraid of his recognizing me. I had been unwilling that he

[045-345] should see my altered looks. I had been taken by surprise, and my

[045-346] courage had quite failed me.

[045-347] But I knew this would not do, and I now said to myself, “My dear,

[045-348] there is no reason—there is and there can be no reason at all—why

[045-349] it should be worse for you now than it ever has been. What you were

[045-350] last month, you are to-day; you are no worse, you are no better. This

[045-351] is not your resolution; call it up, Esther, call it up!” I was in a

[045-352] great tremble—with running—and at first was quite unable to calm

[045-353] myself; but I got better, and I was very glad to know it.

[045-354] The party came to the hotel. I heard them speaking on the staircase.

[045-355] I was sure it was the same gentlemen because I knew their voices

[045-356] again—I mean I knew Mr. Woodcourt’s. It would still have been a

[045-357] great relief to me to have gone away without making myself known, but

[045-358] I was determined not to do so. “No, my dear, no. No, no, no!”

[045-359] I untied my bonnet and put my veil half up—I think I mean half down,

[045-360] but it matters very little—and wrote on one of my cards that I

[045-361] happened to be there with Mr. Richard Carstone, and I sent it in to

[045-362] Mr. Woodcourt. He came immediately. I told him I was rejoiced to be

[045-363] by chance among the first to welcome him home to England. And I saw

[045-364] that he was very sorry for me.

[045-365] “You have been in shipwreck and peril since you left us, Mr.

[045-366] Woodcourt,” said I, “but we can hardly call that a misfortune which

[045-367] enabled you to be so useful and so brave. We read of it with the

[045-368] truest interest. It first came to my knowledge through your old
