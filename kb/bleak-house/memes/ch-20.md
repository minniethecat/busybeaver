[020-001] The long vacation saunters on towards term-time like an idle river

[020-002] very leisurely strolling down a flat country to the sea. Mr. Guppy

[020-003] saunters along with it congenially. He has blunted the blade of his

[020-004] penknife and broken the point off by sticking that instrument into

[020-005] his desk in every direction. Not that he bears the desk any ill will,

[020-006] but he must do something, and it must be something of an unexciting

[020-007] nature, which will lay neither his physical nor his intellectual

[020-008] energies under too heavy contribution. He finds that nothing agrees

[020-009] with him so well as to make little gyrations on one leg of his stool,

[020-010] and stab his desk, and gape.

[020-011] [[kenge-and-carboy|Kenge and Carboy]] are out of town, and the articled clerk has taken

[020-012] out a shooting license and gone down to his father’s, and Mr. Guppy’s

[020-013] two fellow-stipendiaries are away on leave. Mr. Guppy and Mr. [[richard-carstone|Richard]]

[020-014] Carstone divide the dignity of the office. But Mr. Carstone is for

[020-015] the time being established in Kenge’s room, whereat Mr. Guppy chafes.

[020-016] So exceedingly that he with biting sarcasm informs his mother, in the

[020-017] confidential moments when he sups with her off a lobster and lettuce

[020-018] in the Old Street Road, that he is afraid the office is hardly good

[020-019] enough for swells, and that if he had known there was a swell coming,

[020-020] he would have got it painted.

[020-021] Mr. Guppy suspects everybody who enters on the occupation of a stool

[020-022] in Kenge and Carboy’s office of entertaining, as a matter of course,

[020-023] sinister designs upon him. He is clear that every such person wants

[020-024] to depose him. If he be ever asked how, why, when, or wherefore, he

[020-025] shuts up one eye and shakes his head. On the strength of these

[020-026] profound views, he in the most ingenious manner takes infinite pains

[020-027] to counterplot when there is no plot, and plays the deepest games of

[020-028] chess without any adversary.

[020-029] It is a source of much gratification to Mr. Guppy, therefore, to find

[020-030] the new-comer constantly poring over the papers in Jarndyce and

[020-031] Jarndyce, for he well knows that nothing but confusion and failure

[020-032] can come of that. His satisfaction communicates itself to a third

[020-033] saunterer through the long vacation in Kenge and Carboy’s office, to

[020-034] wit, Young Smallweed.

[020-035] Whether Young Smallweed (metaphorically called Small and eke Chick

[020-036] Weed, as it were jocularly to express a fledgling) was ever a boy is

[020-037] much doubted in Lincoln’s Inn. He is now something under fifteen and

[020-038] an old limb of the law. He is facetiously understood to entertain a

[020-039] passion for a lady at a cigar-shop in the neighbourhood of [[chancery-court|Chancery]]

[020-040] Lane and for her sake to have broken off a contract with another

[020-041] lady, to whom he had been engaged some years. He is a town-made

[020-042] article, of small stature and weazen features, but may be perceived

[020-043] from a considerable distance by means of his very tall hat. To become

[020-044] a Guppy is the object of his ambition. He dresses at that gentleman

[020-045] (by whom he is patronized), talks at him, walks at him, founds

[020-046] himself entirely on him. He is honoured with Mr. Guppy’s particular

[020-047] confidence and occasionally advises him, from the deep wells of his

[020-048] experience, on difficult points in private life.

[020-049] Mr. Guppy has been lolling out of window all the morning after trying

[020-050] all the stools in succession and finding none of them easy, and after

[020-051] several times putting his head into the iron safe with a notion of

[020-052] cooling it. Mr. Smallweed has been twice dispatched for effervescent

[020-053] drinks, and has twice mixed them in the two official tumblers and

[020-054] stirred them up with the ruler. Mr. Guppy propounds for Mr.

[020-055] Smallweed’s consideration the paradox that the more you drink the

[020-056] thirstier you are and reclines his head upon the window-sill in a

[020-057] state of hopeless languor.

[020-058] While thus looking out into the shade of Old Square, Lincoln’s Inn,

[020-059] surveying the intolerable bricks and mortar, Mr. Guppy becomes

[020-060] conscious of a manly whisker emerging from the cloistered walk below

[020-061] and turning itself up in the direction of his face. At the same time,

[020-062] a low whistle is wafted through the Inn and a suppressed voice cries,

[020-063] “Hip! Gup-py!”

[020-064] “Why, you don’t mean it!” says Mr. Guppy, aroused. “Small! Here’s

[020-065] Jobling!” Small’s head looks out of window too and nods to Jobling.

[020-066] “Where have you sprung up from?” inquires Mr. Guppy.

[020-067] “From the market-gardens down by Deptford. I can’t stand it any

[020-068] longer. I must enlist. I say! I wish you’d lend me half a crown. Upon

[020-069] my soul, I’m hungry.”

[020-070] Jobling looks hungry and also has the appearance of having run to

[020-071] seed in the market-gardens down by Deptford.

[020-072] “I say! Just throw out half a crown if you have got one to spare. I

[020-073] want to get some dinner.”

[020-074] “Will you come and dine with me?” says Mr. Guppy, throwing out the

[020-075] coin, which Mr. Jobling catches neatly.

[020-076] “How long should I have to hold out?” says Jobling.

[020-077] “Not half an hour. I am only waiting here till the enemy goes,”

[020-078] returns Mr. Guppy, butting inward with his head.

[020-079] “What enemy?”

[020-080] “A new one. Going to be articled. Will you wait?”

[020-081] “Can you give a fellow anything to read in the meantime?” says Mr.

[020-082] Jobling.

[020-083] Smallweed suggests the law list. But Mr. Jobling declares with much

[020-084] earnestness that he “can’t stand it.”

[020-085] “You shall have the paper,” says Mr. Guppy. “He shall bring it down.

[020-086] But you had better not be seen about here. Sit on our staircase and

[020-087] read. It’s a quiet place.”

[020-088] Jobling nods intelligence and acquiescence. The sagacious Smallweed

[020-089] supplies him with the newspaper and occasionally drops his eye upon

[020-090] him from the landing as a precaution against his becoming disgusted

[020-091] with waiting and making an untimely departure. At last the enemy

[020-092] retreats, and then Smallweed fetches Mr. Jobling up.

[020-093] “Well, and how are you?” says Mr. Guppy, shaking hands with him.

[020-094] “So, so. How are you?”

[020-095] Mr. Guppy replying that he is not much to boast of, Mr. Jobling

[020-096] ventures on the question, “How is SHE?” This Mr. Guppy resents as a

[020-097] liberty, retorting, “Jobling, there ARE chords in the human mind—”

[020-098] Jobling begs pardon.

[020-099] “Any subject but that!” says Mr. Guppy with a gloomy enjoyment of his

[020-100] injury. “For there ARE chords, Jobling—”

[020-101] Mr. Jobling begs pardon again.

[020-102] During this short colloquy, the active Smallweed, who is of the

[020-103] dinner party, has written in legal characters on a slip of paper,

[020-104] “Return immediately.” This notification to all whom it may concern,

[020-105] he inserts in the letter-box, and then putting on the tall hat at the

[020-106] angle of inclination at which Mr. Guppy wears his, informs his patron

[020-107] that they may now make themselves scarce.

[020-108] Accordingly they betake themselves to a neighbouring dining-house, of

[020-109] the class known among its frequenters by the denomination slap-bang,

[020-110] where the waitress, a bouncing young female of forty, is supposed to

[020-111] have made some impression on the susceptible Smallweed, of whom it

[020-112] may be remarked that he is a weird changeling to whom years are

[020-113] nothing. He stands precociously possessed of centuries of owlish

[020-114] wisdom. If he ever lay in a cradle, it seems as if he must have lain

[020-115] there in a tail-coat. He has an old, old eye, has Smallweed; and he

[020-116] drinks and smokes in a monkeyish way; and his neck is stiff in his

[020-117] collar; and he is never to be taken in; and he knows all about it,

[020-118] whatever it is. In short, in his bringing up he has been so nursed by

[020-119] Law and Equity that he has become a kind of fossil imp, to account

[020-120] for whose terrestrial existence it is reported at the public offices

[020-121] that his father was John Doe and his mother the only female member of

[020-122] the Roe family, also that his first long-clothes were made from a

[020-123] blue bag.

[020-124] Into the dining-house, unaffected by the seductive show in the window

[020-125] of artificially whitened cauliflowers and poultry, verdant baskets of

[020-126] peas, coolly blooming cucumbers, and joints ready for the spit, Mr.

[020-127] Smallweed leads the way. They know him there and defer to him. He has

[020-128] his favourite box, he bespeaks all the papers, he is down upon bald

[020-129] patriarchs, who keep them more than ten minutes afterwards. It is of

[020-130] no use trying him with anything less than a full-sized “bread” or

[020-131] proposing to him any joint in cut unless it is in the very best cut.

[020-132] In the matter of gravy he is adamant.

[020-133] Conscious of his elfin power and submitting to his dread experience,

[020-134] Mr. Guppy consults him in the choice of that day’s banquet, turning

[020-135] an appealing look towards him as the waitress repeats the catalogue

[020-136] of viands and saying “What do YOU take, Chick?” Chick, out of the

[020-137] profundity of his artfulness, preferring “veal and ham and French

[020-138] beans—and don’t you forget the stuffing, Polly” (with an unearthly

[020-139] cock of his venerable eye), Mr. Guppy and Mr. Jobling give the like

[020-140] order. Three pint pots of half-and-half are superadded. Quickly the

[020-141] waitress returns bearing what is apparently a model of the Tower of

[020-142] Babel but what is really a pile of plates and flat tin dish-covers.

[020-143] Mr. Smallweed, approving of what is set before him, conveys

[020-144] intelligent benignity into his ancient eye and winks upon her. Then,

[020-145] amid a constant coming in, and going out, and running about, and a

[020-146] clatter of crockery, and a rumbling up and down of the machine which

[020-147] brings the nice cuts from the kitchen, and a shrill crying for more

[020-148] nice cuts down the speaking-pipe, and a shrill reckoning of the cost

[020-149] of nice cuts that have been disposed of, and a general flush and

[020-150] steam of hot joints, cut and uncut, and a considerably heated

[020-151] atmosphere in which the soiled knives and tablecloths seem to break

[020-152] out spontaneously into eruptions of grease and blotches of beer, the

[020-153] legal triumvirate appease their appetites.

[020-154] Mr. Jobling is buttoned up closer than mere adornment might require.

[020-155] His hat presents at the rims a peculiar appearance of a glistening

[020-156] nature, as if it had been a favourite snail-promenade. The same

[020-157] phenomenon is visible on some parts of his coat, and particularly at

[020-158] the seams. He has the faded appearance of a gentleman in embarrassed

[020-159] circumstances; even his light whiskers droop with something of a

[020-160] shabby air.

[020-161] His appetite is so vigorous that it suggests spare living for some

[020-162] little time back. He makes such a speedy end of his plate of veal and

[020-163] ham, bringing it to a close while his companions are yet midway in

[020-164] theirs, that Mr. Guppy proposes another. “Thank you, Guppy,” says Mr.

[020-165] Jobling, “I really don’t know but what I WILL take another.”

[020-166] Another being brought, he falls to with great goodwill.

[020-167] Mr. Guppy takes silent notice of him at intervals until he is half

[020-168] way through this second plate and stops to take an enjoying pull at

[020-169] his pint pot of half-and-half (also renewed) and stretches out his

[020-170] legs and rubs his hands. Beholding him in which glow of contentment,

[020-171] Mr. Guppy says, “You are a man again, Tony!”

[020-172] “Well, not quite yet,” says Mr. Jobling. “Say, just born.”

[020-173] “Will you take any other vegetables? Grass? Peas? Summer cabbage?”

[020-174] “Thank you, Guppy,” says Mr. Jobling. “I really don’t know but what I

[020-175] WILL take summer cabbage.”

[020-176] Order given; with the sarcastic addition (from Mr. Smallweed) of

[020-177] “Without slugs, Polly!” And cabbage produced.

[020-178] “I am growing up, Guppy,” says Mr. Jobling, plying his knife and fork

[020-179] with a relishing steadiness.

[020-180] “Glad to hear it.”

[020-181] “In fact, I have just turned into my teens,” says Mr. Jobling.

[020-182] He says no more until he has performed his task, which he achieves as

[020-183] Messrs. Guppy and Smallweed finish theirs, thus getting over the

[020-184] ground in excellent style and beating those two gentlemen easily by a

[020-185] veal and ham and a cabbage.

[020-186] “Now, Small,” says Mr. Guppy, “what would you recommend about

[020-187] pastry?”

[020-188] “Marrow puddings,” says Mr. Smallweed instantly.

[020-189] “Aye, aye!” cries Mr. Jobling with an arch look. “You’re there, are

[020-190] you? Thank you, Mr. Guppy, I don’t know but what I WILL take a marrow

[020-191] pudding.”

[020-192] Three marrow puddings being produced, Mr. Jobling adds in a pleasant

[020-193] humour that he is coming of age fast. To these succeed, by command of

[020-194] Mr. Smallweed, “three Cheshires,” and to those “three small rums.”

[020-195] This apex of the entertainment happily reached, Mr. Jobling puts up

[020-196] his legs on the carpeted seat (having his own side of the box to

[020-197] himself), leans against the wall, and says, “I am grown up now,

[020-198] Guppy. I have arrived at maturity.”

[020-199] “What do you think, now,” says Mr. Guppy, “about—you don’t mind

[020-200] Smallweed?”

[020-201] “Not the least in the world. I have the pleasure of drinking his good

[020-202] health.”

[020-203] “Sir, to you!” says Mr. Smallweed.

[020-204] “I was saying, what do you think NOW,” pursues Mr. Guppy, “of

[020-205] enlisting?”

[020-206] “Why, what I may think after dinner,” returns Mr. Jobling, “is one

[020-207] thing, my dear Guppy, and what I may think before dinner is another

[020-208] thing. Still, even after dinner, I ask myself the question, What am I

[020-209] to do? How am I to live? Ill fo manger, you know,” says Mr. Jobling,

[020-210] pronouncing that word as if he meant a necessary fixture in an

[020-211] English stable. “Ill fo manger. That’s the French saying, and

[020-212] mangering is as necessary to me as it is to a Frenchman. Or more so.”

[020-213] Mr. Smallweed is decidedly of opinion “much more so.”

[020-214] “If any man had told me,” pursues Jobling, “even so lately as when

[020-215] you and I had the frisk down in Lincolnshire, Guppy, and drove over

[020-216] to see that house at Castle Wold—”

[020-217] Mr. Smallweed corrects him—[[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]].

[020-218] “Chesney Wold. (I thank my honourable friend for that cheer.) If any

[020-219] man had told me then that I should be as hard up at the present time

[020-220] as I literally find myself, I should have—well, I should have

[020-221] pitched into him,” says Mr. Jobling, taking a little rum-and-water

[020-222] with an air of desperate resignation; “I should have let fly at his

[020-223] head.”

[020-224] “Still, Tony, you were on the wrong side of the post then,”

[020-225] remonstrates Mr. Guppy. “You were talking about nothing else in the

[020-226] gig.”

[020-227] “Guppy,” says Mr. Jobling, “I will not deny it. I was on the wrong

[020-228] side of the post. But I trusted to things coming round.”

[020-229] That very popular trust in flat things coming round! Not in their

[020-230] being beaten round, or worked round, but in their “coming” round! As

[020-231] though a lunatic should trust in the world’s “coming” triangular!

[020-232] “I had confident expectations that things would come round and be all

[020-233] square,” says Mr. Jobling with some vagueness of expression and

[020-234] perhaps of meaning too. “But I was disappointed. They never did. And

[020-235] when it came to creditors making rows at the office and to people

[020-236] that the office dealt with making complaints about dirty trifles of

[020-237] borrowed money, why there was an end of that connexion. And of any

[020-238] new professional connexion too, for if I was to give a reference

[020-239] to-morrow, it would be mentioned and would sew me up. Then what’s a

[020-240] fellow to do? I have been keeping out of the way and living cheap

[020-241] down about the market-gardens, but what’s the use of living cheap

[020-242] when you have got no money? You might as well live dear.”

[020-243] “Better,” Mr. Smallweed thinks.

[020-244] “Certainly. It’s the fashionable way; and fashion and whiskers have

[020-245] been my weaknesses, and I don’t care who knows it,” says Mr. Jobling.

[020-246] “They are great weaknesses—Damme, sir, they are great. Well,”

[020-247] proceeds Mr. Jobling after a defiant visit to his rum-and-water,

[020-248] “what can a fellow do, I ask you, BUT enlist?”

[020-249] Mr. Guppy comes more fully into the conversation to state what, in

[020-250] his opinion, a fellow can do. His manner is the gravely impressive

[020-251] manner of a man who has not committed himself in life otherwise than

[020-252] as he has become the victim of a tender sorrow of the heart.

[020-253] “Jobling,” says Mr. Guppy, “myself and our mutual friend Smallweed—”

[020-254] Mr. Smallweed modestly observes, “Gentlemen both!” and drinks.

[020-255] “—Have had a little conversation on this matter more than once since

[020-256] you—”

[020-257] “Say, got the sack!” cries Mr. Jobling bitterly. “Say it, Guppy. You

[020-258] mean it.”

[020-259] “No-o-o! Left the Inn,” Mr. Smallweed delicately suggests.

[020-260] “Since you left the Inn, Jobling,” says Mr. Guppy; “and I have

[020-261] mentioned to our mutual friend Smallweed a plan I have lately thought

[020-262] of proposing. You know Snagsby the stationer?”

[020-263] “I know there is such a stationer,” returns Mr. Jobling. “He was not

[020-264] ours, and I am not acquainted with him.”

[020-265] “He IS ours, Jobling, and I AM acquainted with him,” Mr. Guppy

[020-266] retorts. “Well, sir! I have lately become better acquainted with him

[020-267] through some accidental circumstances that have made me a visitor of

[020-268] his in private life. Those circumstances it is not necessary to offer

[020-269] in argument. They may—or they may not—have some reference to a

[020-270] subject which may—or may not—have cast its shadow on my existence.”

[020-271] As it is Mr. Guppy’s perplexing way with boastful misery to tempt his

[020-272] particular friends into this subject, and the moment they touch it,

[020-273] to turn on them with that trenchant severity about the chords in the

[020-274] human mind, both Mr. Jobling and Mr. Smallweed decline the pitfall by

[020-275] remaining silent.

[020-276] “Such things may be,” repeats Mr. Guppy, “or they may not be. They

[020-277] are no part of the case. It is enough to mention that both Mr. and

[020-278] Mrs. Snagsby are very willing to oblige me and that Snagsby has, in

[020-279] busy times, a good deal of copying work to give out. He has all

[020-280] [[mr-tulkinghorn|Tulkinghorn]]’s, and an excellent business besides. I believe if our

[020-281] mutual friend Smallweed were put into the box, he could prove this?”

[020-282] Mr. Smallweed nods and appears greedy to be sworn.

[020-283] “Now, gentlemen of the jury,” says Mr. Guppy, “—I mean, now,

[020-284] Jobling—you may say this is a poor prospect of a living. Granted.

[020-285] But it’s better than nothing, and better than enlistment. You want

[020-286] time. There must be time for these late affairs to blow over. You

[020-287] might live through it on much worse terms than by [[document-and-letter|writing]] for

[020-288] Snagsby.”

[020-289] Mr. Jobling is about to interrupt when the sagacious Smallweed checks

[020-290] him with a dry cough and the words, “Hem! Shakspeare!”

[020-291] “There are two branches to this subject, Jobling,” says Mr. Guppy.

[020-292] “That is the first. I come to the second. You know [[krook|Krook]], the

[020-293] Chancellor, across the lane. Come, Jobling,” says Mr. Guppy in his

[020-294] encouraging cross-examination-tone, “I think you know Krook, the

[020-295] Chancellor, across the lane?”

[020-296] “I know him by sight,” says Mr. Jobling.

[020-297] “You know him by sight. Very well. And you know little Flite?”

[020-298] “Everybody knows her,” says Mr. Jobling.

[020-299] “Everybody knows her. VERY well. Now it has been one of my duties of

[020-300] late to pay Flite a certain weekly allowance, deducting from it the

[020-301] amount of her weekly rent, which I have paid (in consequence of

[020-302] instructions I have received) to Krook himself, regularly in her

[020-303] presence. This has brought me into communication with Krook and into

[020-304] a knowledge of his house and his habits. I know he has a room to let.

[020-305] You may live there at a very low charge under any name you like, as

[020-306] quietly as if you were a hundred miles off. He’ll ask no questions

[020-307] and would accept you as a tenant at a word from me—before the clock

[020-308] strikes, if you chose. And I tell you another thing, Jobling,” says

[020-309] Mr. Guppy, who has suddenly lowered his voice and become familiar

[020-310] again, “he’s an extraordinary old chap—always rummaging among a

[020-311] litter of papers and grubbing away at teaching himself to read and

[020-312] write, without getting on a bit, as it seems to me. He is a most

[020-313] extraordinary old chap, sir. I don’t know but what it might be worth

[020-314] a fellow’s while to look him up a bit.”

[020-315] “You don’t mean—” Mr. Jobling begins.

[020-316] “I mean,” returns Mr. Guppy, shrugging his shoulders with becoming

[020-317] modesty, “that I can’t make him out. I appeal to our mutual friend

[020-318] Smallweed whether he has or has not heard me remark that I can’t make

[020-319] him out.”

[020-320] Mr. Smallweed bears the concise testimony, “A few!”

[020-321] “I have seen something of the [[professional-identity|profession]] and something of life,

[020-322] Tony,” says Mr. Guppy, “and it’s seldom I can’t make a man out, more

[020-323] or less. But such an old card as this, so deep, so sly, and secret

[020-324] (though I don’t believe he is ever sober), I never came across. Now,

[020-325] he must be precious old, you know, and he has not a soul about him,

[020-326] and he is reported to be immensely rich; and whether he is a

[020-327] smuggler, or a receiver, or an unlicensed pawnbroker, or a

[020-328] money-lender—all of which I have thought likely at different

[020-329] times—it might pay you to knock up a sort of knowledge of him. I

[020-330] don’t see why you shouldn’t go in for it, when everything else

[020-331] suits.”

[020-332] Mr. Jobling, Mr. Guppy, and Mr. Smallweed all lean their elbows on

[020-333] the table and their chins upon their hands, and look at the ceiling.

[020-334] After a time, they all drink, slowly lean back, put their hands in

[020-335] their pockets, and look at one another.

[020-336] “If I had the energy I once possessed, Tony!” says Mr. Guppy with a

[020-337] sigh. “But there are chords in the human mind—”

[020-338] Expressing the remainder of the desolate sentiment in rum-and-water,

[020-339] Mr. Guppy concludes by resigning the adventure to Tony Jobling and

[020-340] informing him that during the vacation and while things are slack,

[020-341] his purse, “as far as three or four or even five pound goes,” will be

[020-342] at his disposal. “For never shall it be said,” Mr. Guppy adds with

[020-343] emphasis, “that William Guppy turned his back upon his friend!”

[020-344] The latter part of the proposal is so directly to the purpose that

[020-345] Mr. Jobling says with emotion, “Guppy, my trump, your fist!” Mr.

[020-346] Guppy presents it, saying, “Jobling, my boy, there it is!” Mr.

[020-347] Jobling returns, “Guppy, we have been pals now for some years!” Mr.

[020-348] Guppy replies, “Jobling, we have.”

[020-349] They then shake hands, and Mr. Jobling adds in a feeling manner,

[020-350] “Thank you, Guppy, I don’t know but what I WILL take another glass

[020-351] for old acquaintance sake.”

[020-352] “Krook’s last lodger died there,” observes Mr. Guppy in an incidental

[020-353] way.

[020-354] “Did he though!” says Mr. Jobling.

[020-355] “There was a verdict. Accidental death. You don’t mind that?”

[020-356] “No,” says Mr. Jobling, “I don’t mind it; but he might as well have

[020-357] died somewhere else. It’s devilish odd that he need go and die at MY

[020-358] place!” Mr. Jobling quite resents this liberty, several times

[020-359] returning to it with such remarks as, “There are places enough to die

[020-360] in, I should think!” or, “He wouldn’t have liked my dying at HIS

[020-361] place, I dare say!”

[020-362] However, the compact being virtually made, Mr. Guppy proposes to

[020-363] dispatch the trusty Smallweed to ascertain if Mr. Krook is at home,

[020-364] as in that case they may complete the negotiation without delay. Mr.
