[025-001] There is disquietude in Cook’s Court, Cursitor Street. Black

[025-002] suspicion hides in that peaceful region. The mass of Cook’s Courtiers

[025-003] are in their usual state of mind, no better and no worse; but Mr.

[025-004] Snagsby is changed, and his little woman knows it.

[025-005] For Tom-all-Alone’s and Lincoln’s Inn Fields persist in harnessing

[025-006] themselves, a pair of ungovernable coursers, to the chariot of Mr.

[025-007] Snagsby’s imagination; and Mr. Bucket drives; and the passengers are

[025-008] [[jo-crossing-sweeper|Jo]] and [[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]]; and the complete equipage whirls though the

[025-009] law-stationery business at wild speed all round the clock. Even in

[025-010] the little front kitchen where the family meals are taken, it rattles

[025-011] away at a smoking pace from the dinner-table, when Mr. Snagsby pauses

[025-012] in carving the first slice of the leg of mutton baked with potatoes

[025-013] and stares at the kitchen wall.

[025-014] Mr. Snagsby cannot make out what it is that he has had to do with.

[025-015] Something is wrong somewhere, but what something, what may come of

[025-016] it, to whom, when, and from which unthought of and unheard of quarter

[025-017] is the puzzle of his life. His remote impressions of the robes and

[025-018] coronets, the stars and garters, that sparkle through the

[025-019] surface-dust of Mr. Tulkinghorn’s chambers; his veneration for the

[025-020] mysteries presided over by that best and closest of his customers,

[025-021] whom all the [[inns-of-court|Inns of Court]], all [[chancery-lane|Chancery Lane]], and all the legal

[025-022] neighbourhood agree to hold in awe; his remembrance of Detective Mr.

[025-023] Bucket with his forefinger and his confidential manner, impossible to

[025-024] be evaded or declined, persuade him that he is a party to some

[025-025] dangerous secret without knowing what it is. And it is the fearful

[025-026] peculiarity of this condition that, at any hour of his daily life, at

[025-027] any opening of the shop-door, at any pull of the bell, at any

[025-028] entrance of a messenger, or any delivery of a letter, the secret may

[025-029] take air and fire, explode, and blow up—Mr. Bucket only knows whom.

[025-030] For which reason, whenever a man unknown comes into the shop (as many

[025-031] men unknown do) and says, “Is Mr. Snagsby in?” or words to that

[025-032] innocent effect, Mr. Snagsby’s heart knocks hard at his guilty

[025-033] breast. He undergoes so much from such inquiries that when they are

[025-034] made by boys he revenges himself by flipping at their ears over the

[025-035] counter and asking the young dogs what they mean by it and why they

[025-036] can’t speak out at once? More impracticable men and boys persist in

[025-037] walking into Mr. Snagsby’s sleep and terrifying him with

[025-038] unaccountable questions, so that often when the cock at the little

[025-039] dairy in Cursitor Street breaks out in his usual absurd way about the

[025-040] morning, Mr. Snagsby finds himself in a crisis of nightmare, with his

[025-041] little woman shaking him and saying “What’s the matter with the man!”

[025-042] The little woman herself is not the least item in his difficulty. To

[025-043] know that he is always keeping a secret from her, that he has under

[025-044] all circumstances to conceal and hold fast a tender double tooth,

[025-045] which her sharpness is ever ready to twist out of his head, gives Mr.

[025-046] Snagsby, in her dentistical presence, much of the air of a dog who

[025-047] has a reservation from his master and will look anywhere rather than

[025-048] meet his eye.

[025-049] These various signs and tokens, marked by the little woman, are not

[025-050] lost upon her. They impel her to say, “Snagsby has something on his

[025-051] mind!” And thus suspicion gets into Cook’s Court, Cursitor Street.

[025-052] From suspicion to jealousy, Mrs. Snagsby finds the road as natural

[025-053] and short as from Cook’s Court to [[chancery-court|Chancery]] Lane. And thus jealousy

[025-054] gets into Cook’s Court, Cursitor Street. Once there (and it was

[025-055] always lurking thereabout), it is very active and nimble in Mrs.

[025-056] Snagsby’s breast, prompting her to nocturnal examinations of Mr.

[025-057] Snagsby’s pockets; to secret perusals of Mr. Snagsby’s [[document-and-letter|letters]]; to

[025-058] private researches in the day book and ledger, till, cash-box, and

[025-059] iron safe; to watchings at windows, listenings behind doors, and a

[025-060] general putting of this and that together by the wrong end.

[025-061] Mrs. Snagsby is so perpetually on the alert that the house becomes

[025-062] ghostly with creaking boards and rustling garments. The ’prentices

[025-063] think somebody may have been murdered there in bygone times. Guster

[025-064] holds certain loose atoms of an idea (picked up at Tooting, where

[025-065] they were found floating among the [[childhood-and-orphanhood|orphans]]) that there is buried

[025-066] money underneath the cellar, guarded by an old man with a white

[025-067] beard, who cannot get out for seven thousand years because he said

[025-068] the Lord’s Prayer backwards.

[025-069] “Who was Nimrod?” Mrs. Snagsby repeatedly inquires of herself. “Who

[025-070] was that lady—that creature? And who is that boy?” Now, Nimrod being

[025-071] as dead as the mighty hunter whose name Mrs. Snagsby has

[025-072] appropriated, and the lady being unproducible, she directs her mental

[025-073] eye, for the present, with redoubled vigilance to the boy. “And who,”

[025-074] quoth Mrs. Snagsby for the thousand and first time, “is that boy? Who

[025-075] is that—!” And there Mrs. Snagsby is seized with an inspiration.

[025-076] He has no respect for Mr. Chadband. No, to be sure, and he wouldn’t

[025-077] have, of course. Naturally he wouldn’t, under those contagious

[025-078] circumstances. He was invited and appointed by Mr. Chadband—why,

[025-079] Mrs. Snagsby heard it herself with her own ears!—to come back, and

[025-080] be told where he was to go, to be addressed by Mr. Chadband; and he

[025-081] never came! Why did he never come? Because he was told not to come.

[025-082] Who told him not to come? Who? Ha, ha! Mrs. Snagsby sees it all.

[025-083] But happily (and Mrs. Snagsby tightly shakes her head and tightly

[025-084] smiles) that boy was met by Mr. Chadband yesterday in the streets;

[025-085] and that boy, as affording a subject which Mr. Chadband desires to

[025-086] improve for the spiritual delight of a select congregation, was

[025-087] seized by Mr. Chadband and threatened with being delivered over to

[025-088] the police unless he showed the reverend gentleman where he lived and

[025-089] unless he entered into, and fulfilled, an undertaking to appear in

[025-090] Cook’s Court to-morrow night, “to—mor—row—night,” Mrs. Snagsby

[025-091] repeats for mere emphasis with another tight smile and another tight

[025-092] shake of her head; and to-morrow night that boy will be here, and

[025-093] to-morrow night Mrs. Snagsby will have her eye upon him and upon some

[025-094] one else; and oh, you may walk a long while in your secret ways (says

[025-095] Mrs. Snagsby with haughtiness and scorn), but you can’t blind ME!

[025-096] Mrs. Snagsby sounds no timbrel in anybody’s ears, but holds her

[025-097] purpose quietly, and keeps her counsel. To-morrow comes, the savoury

[025-098] preparations for the Oil Trade come, the evening comes. Comes Mr.

[025-099] Snagsby in his black coat; come the Chadbands; come (when the gorging

[025-100] vessel is replete) the ’prentices and Guster, to be edified; comes at

[025-101] last, with his slouching head, and his shuffle backward, and his

[025-102] shuffle forward, and his shuffle to the right, and his shuffle to the

[025-103] left, and his bit of fur cap in his muddy hand, which he picks as if

[025-104] it were some mangy bird he had caught and was plucking before eating

[025-105] raw, Jo, the very, very tough subject Mr. Chadband is to improve.

[025-106] Mrs. Snagsby screws a watchful glance on Jo as he is brought into the

[025-107] little drawing-room by Guster. He looks at Mr. Snagsby the moment he

[025-108] comes in. Aha! Why does he look at Mr. Snagsby? Mr. Snagsby looks at

[025-109] him. Why should he do that, but that Mrs. Snagsby sees it all? Why

[025-110] else should that look pass between them, why else should Mr. Snagsby

[025-111] be confused and cough a signal cough behind his hand? It is as clear

[025-112] as crystal that Mr. Snagsby is that boy’s father.

[025-113] “Peace, my friends,” says Chadband, rising and wiping the oily

[025-114] exudations from his reverend visage. “Peace be with us! My friends,

[025-115] why with us? Because,” with his fat smile, “it cannot be against us,

[025-116] because it must be for us; because it is not hardening, because it is

[025-117] softening; because it does not make war like the hawk, but comes home

[025-118] unto us like the dove. Therefore, my friends, peace be with us! My

[025-119] human boy, come forward!”

[025-120] Stretching forth his flabby paw, Mr. Chadband lays the same on Jo’s

[025-121] arm and considers where to station him. Jo, very doubtful of his

[025-122] reverend friend’s intentions and not at all clear but that something

[025-123] practical and painful is going to be done to him, mutters, “You let

[025-124] me alone. I never said nothink to you. You let me alone.”

[025-125] “No, my young friend,” says Chadband smoothly, “I will not let you

[025-126] alone. And why? Because I am a harvest-labourer, because I am a

[025-127] toiler and a moiler, because you are delivered over unto me and are

[025-128] become as a precious instrument in my hands. My friends, may I so

[025-129] employ this instrument as to use it to your advantage, to your

[025-130] profit, to your gain, to your welfare, to your enrichment! My young

[025-131] friend, sit upon this stool.”

[025-132] Jo, apparently possessed by an impression that the reverend gentleman

[025-133] wants to cut his hair, shields his head with both arms and is got

[025-134] into the required position with great difficulty and every possible

[025-135] manifestation of reluctance.

[025-136] When he is at last adjusted like a lay-figure, Mr. Chadband, retiring

[025-137] behind the table, holds up his bear’s-paw and says, “My friends!”

[025-138] This is the signal for a general settlement of the audience. The

[025-139] ’prentices giggle internally and nudge each other. Guster falls into

[025-140] a staring and vacant state, compounded of a stunned admiration of Mr.

[025-141] Chadband and pity for the friendless outcast whose condition touches

[025-142] her nearly. Mrs. Snagsby silently lays trains of gunpowder. Mrs.

[025-143] Chadband composes herself grimly by the fire and warms her knees,

[025-144] finding that sensation favourable to the reception of eloquence.

[025-145] It happens that Mr. Chadband has a pulpit habit of fixing some member

[025-146] of his congregation with his eye and fatly arguing his points with

[025-147] that particular person, who is understood to be expected to be moved

[025-148] to an occasional grunt, groan, gasp, or other audible expression of

[025-149] inward working, which expression of inward working, being echoed by

[025-150] some elderly lady in the next pew and so communicated like a game of

[025-151] forfeits through a circle of the more fermentable sinners present,

[025-152] serves the purpose of parliamentary cheering and gets Mr. Chadband’s

[025-153] steam up. From mere force of habit, Mr. Chadband in saying “My

[025-154] friends!” has rested his eye on Mr. Snagsby and proceeds to make that

[025-155] ill-starred stationer, already sufficiently confused, the immediate

[025-156] recipient of his discourse.

[025-157] “We have here among us, my friends,” says Chadband, “a Gentile and a

[025-158] heathen, a dweller in the tents of Tom-all-Alone’s and a mover-on

[025-159] upon the surface of the earth. We have here among us, my friends,”

[025-160] and Mr. Chadband, untwisting the point with his dirty thumb-nail,

[025-161] bestows an oily smile on Mr. Snagsby, signifying that he will throw

[025-162] him an argumentative back-fall presently if he be not already down,

[025-163] “a brother and a boy. Devoid of parents, devoid of relations, devoid

[025-164] of flocks and herds, devoid of gold and silver and of precious

[025-165] stones. Now, my friends, why do I say he is devoid of these

[025-166] possessions? Why? Why is he?” Mr. Chadband states the question as if

[025-167] he were propounding an entirely new riddle of much ingenuity and

[025-168] merit to Mr. Snagsby and entreating him not to give it up.

[025-169] Mr. Snagsby, greatly perplexed by the mysterious look he received

[025-170] just now from his little woman—at about the period when Mr. Chadband

[025-171] mentioned the word parents—is tempted into modestly remarking, “I

[025-172] don’t know, I’m sure, sir.” On which interruption Mrs. Chadband

[025-173] glares and Mrs. Snagsby says, “For [[secret-and-shame|shame]]!”

[025-174] “I hear a voice,” says Chadband; “is it a still small voice, my

[025-175] friends? I fear not, though I fain would hope so—”

[025-176] “Ah—h!” from Mrs. Snagsby.

[025-177] “Which says, ‘I don’t know.’ Then I will tell you why. I say this

[025-178] brother present here among us is devoid of parents, devoid of

[025-179] relations, devoid of flocks and herds, devoid of gold, of silver, and

[025-180] of precious stones because he is devoid of the light that shines in

[025-181] upon some of us. What is that light? What is it? I ask you, what is

[025-182] that light?”

[025-183] Mr. Chadband draws back his head and pauses, but Mr. Snagsby is not

[025-184] to be lured on to his destruction again. Mr. Chadband, leaning

[025-185] forward over the table, pierces what he has got to follow directly

[025-186] into Mr. Snagsby with the thumb-nail already mentioned.

[025-187] “It is,” says Chadband, “the ray of rays, the sun of suns, the moon

[025-188] of moons, the star of stars. It is the light of Terewth.”

[025-189] Mr. Chadband draws himself up again and looks triumphantly at Mr.

[025-190] Snagsby as if he would be glad to know how he feels after that.

[025-191] “Of Terewth,” says Mr. Chadband, hitting him again. “Say not to me

[025-192] that it is NOT the lamp of lamps. I say to you it is. I say to you, a

[025-193] million of times over, it is. It is! I say to you that I will

[025-194] proclaim it to you, whether you like it or not; nay, that the less

[025-195] you like it, the more I will proclaim it to you. With a

[025-196] speaking-trumpet! I say to you that if you rear yourself against it,

[025-197] you shall fall, you shall be bruised, you shall be battered, you

[025-198] shall be flawed, you shall be smashed.”

[025-199] The present effect of this flight of oratory—much admired for its

[025-200] general power by Mr. Chadband’s followers—being not only to make Mr.

[025-201] Chadband unpleasantly warm, but to represent the innocent Mr. Snagsby

[025-202] in the light of a determined enemy to virtue, with a forehead of

[025-203] brass and a heart of adamant, that unfortunate tradesman becomes yet

[025-204] more disconcerted and is in a very advanced state of low spirits and

[025-205] false position when Mr. Chadband accidentally finishes him.

[025-206] “My friends,” he resumes after dabbing his fat head for some

[025-207] time—and it smokes to such an extent that he seems to light his

[025-208] pocket-handkerchief at it, which smokes, too, after every dab—“to

[025-209] pursue the subject we are endeavouring with our lowly gifts to

[025-210] improve, let us in a spirit of love inquire what is that Terewth to

[025-211] which I have alluded. For, my young friends,” suddenly addressing the

[025-212] ’prentices and Guster, to their consternation, “if I am told by the

[025-213] doctor that calomel or castor-oil is good for me, I may naturally ask

[025-214] what is calomel, and what is castor-oil. I may wish to be informed of

[025-215] that before I dose myself with either or with both. Now, my young

[025-216] friends, what is this Terewth then? Firstly (in a spirit of love),

[025-217] what is the common sort of Terewth—the working clothes—the

[025-218] every-day wear, my young friends? Is it deception?”

[025-219] “Ah—h!” from Mrs. Snagsby.

[025-220] “Is it suppression?”

[025-221] A shiver in the negative from Mrs. Snagsby.

[025-222] “Is it reservation?”

[025-223] A shake of the head from Mrs. Snagsby—very long and very tight.

[025-224] “No, my friends, it is neither of these. Neither of these names

[025-225] belongs to it. When this young heathen now among us—who is now, my

[025-226] friends, asleep, the seal of indifference and perdition being set

[025-227] upon his eyelids; but do not wake him, for it is right that I should

[025-228] have to wrestle, and to combat and to struggle, and to conquer, for

[025-229] his sake—when this young hardened heathen told us a story of a cock,

[025-230] and of a bull, and of a lady, and of a sovereign, was THAT the

[025-231] Terewth? No. Or if it was partly, was it wholly and entirely? No, my

[025-232] friends, no!”

[025-233] If Mr. Snagsby could withstand his little woman’s look as it enters

[025-234] at his eyes, the windows of his soul, and searches the whole

[025-235] tenement, he were other than the man he is. He cowers and droops.

[025-236] “Or, my juvenile friends,” says Chadband, descending to the level of

[025-237] their comprehension with a very obtrusive demonstration in his

[025-238] greasily meek smile of coming a long way downstairs for the purpose,

[025-239] “if the master of this house was to go forth into the city and there

[025-240] see an eel, and was to come back, and was to call unto him the

[025-241] mistress of this house, and was to say, ‘Sarah, rejoice with me, for

[025-242] I have seen an elephant!’ would THAT be Terewth?”

[025-243] Mrs. Snagsby in tears.

[025-244] “Or put it, my juvenile friends, that he saw an elephant, and

[025-245] returning said ‘Lo, the city is barren, I have seen but an eel,’

[025-246] would THAT be Terewth?”

[025-247] Mrs. Snagsby sobbing loudly.

[025-248] “Or put it, my juvenile friends,” said Chadband, stimulated by the

[025-249] sound, “that the unnatural parents of this slumbering heathen—for

[025-250] parents he had, my juvenile friends, beyond a doubt—after casting

[025-251] him forth to the wolves and the vultures, and the wild dogs and the

[025-252] young gazelles, and the serpents, went back to their dwellings and

[025-253] had their pipes, and their pots, and their flutings and their

[025-254] dancings, and their malt liquors, and their butcher’s meat and

[025-255] poultry, would THAT be Terewth?”

[025-256] Mrs. Snagsby replies by delivering herself a prey to spasms, not an

[025-257] unresisting prey, but a crying and a tearing one, so that Cook’s

[025-258] Court re-echoes with her shrieks. Finally, becoming cataleptic, she

[025-259] has to be carried up the narrow staircase like a grand piano. After

[025-260] unspeakable suffering, productive of the utmost consternation, she is

[025-261] pronounced, by expresses from the bedroom, free from pain, though

[025-262] much exhausted, in which state of affairs Mr. Snagsby, trampled and

[025-263] crushed in the piano-forte removal, and extremely timid and feeble,

[025-264] ventures to come out from behind the door in the drawing-room.

[025-265] All this time Jo has been standing on the spot where he woke up, ever

[025-266] picking his cap and putting bits of fur in his mouth. He spits them

[025-267] out with a remorseful air, for he feels that it is in his nature to

[025-268] be an unimprovable reprobate and that it’s no good HIS trying to keep

[025-269] awake, for HE won’t never know nothink. Though it may be, Jo, that

[025-270] there is a history so interesting and affecting even to minds as near

[025-271] the brutes as thine, recording deeds done on this earth for common

[025-272] men, that if the Chadbands, removing their own persons from the

[025-273] light, would but show it thee in simple reverence, would but leave it

[025-274] unimproved, would but regard it as being eloquent enough without

[025-275] their modest aid—it might hold thee awake, and thou might learn from

[025-276] it yet!

[025-277] Jo never heard of any such book. Its compilers and the Reverend

[025-278] Chadband are all one to him, except that he knows the Reverend

[025-279] Chadband and would rather run away from him for an hour than hear him

[025-280] talk for five minutes. “It an’t no good my waiting here no longer,”

[025-281] thinks Jo. “Mr. Snagsby an’t a-going to say nothink to me to-night.”

[025-282] And downstairs he shuffles.

[025-283] But downstairs is the charitable Guster, holding by the handrail of

[025-284] the kitchen stairs and warding off a fit, as yet doubtfully, the same

[025-285] having been induced by Mrs. Snagsby’s screaming. She has her own

[025-286] supper of bread and cheese to hand to Jo, with whom she ventures to

[025-287] interchange a word or so for the first time.

[025-288] “Here’s something to eat, poor boy,” says Guster.

[025-289] “Thank’ee, mum,” says Jo.

[025-290] “Are you hungry?”

[025-291] “Jist!” says Jo.

[025-292] “What’s gone of your father and your mother, eh?”

[025-293] Jo stops in the middle of a bite and looks petrified. For this orphan

[025-294] charge of the Christian saint whose shrine was at Tooting has patted

[025-295] him on the shoulder, and it is the first time in his life that any

[025-296] decent hand has been so laid upon him.

[025-297] “I never know’d nothink about ’em,” says Jo.

[025-298] “No more didn’t I of mine,” cries Guster. She is repressing symptoms

[025-299] favourable to the fit when she seems to take alarm at something and

[025-300] vanishes down the stairs.

[025-301] “Jo,” whispers the law-stationer softly as the boy lingers on the

[025-302] step.

[025-303] “Here I am, Mr. Snagsby!”

[025-304] “I didn’t know you were gone—there’s another half-crown, Jo. It was

[025-305] quite right of you to say nothing about the lady the other night when

[025-306] we were out together. It would breed trouble. You can’t be too quiet,

[025-307] Jo.”

[025-308] “I am fly, master!”

[025-309] And so, good night.

[025-310] A ghostly shade, frilled and night-capped, follows the law-stationer

[025-311] to the room he came from and glides higher up. And henceforth he

[025-312] begins, go where he will, to be attended by another shadow than his

[025-313] own, hardly less constant than his own, hardly less quiet than his

[025-314] own. And into whatsoever atmosphere of secrecy his own shadow may

[025-315] pass, let all concerned in the secrecy beware! For the watchful Mrs.

[025-316] Snagsby is there too—bone of his bone, flesh of his flesh, shadow of

[025-317] his shadow.
