# Jo
## Overview
Jo is the novel's moral test case — a crossing-sweeper who knows nothing of his origins, his name, or the Lord's Prayer, and whose death is the direct consequence of a society that has no place for him. "He is not one of Mrs. Pardiggle's Tockahoopo Indians... he is not a genuine foreign-grown savage; he is the ordinary home-made article" (047-015).
## Role in the Novel
Jo connects the novel's disparate worlds. It is through Jo that the smallpox travels from Tom-all-Alone's to Esther's bedside. His testimony about the veiled lady at Hawdon's grave advances the detective plot. His death — with the Lord's Prayer on his lips, taught to him at the last moment — is one of Victorian fiction's most famous death scenes, and its irony ("He is dead. Dead, your Majesty") is devastating.
