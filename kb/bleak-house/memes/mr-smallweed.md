# Mr. Smallweed

## Overview

Mr. Smallweed — also known as Grandfather Smallweed — is the aged, avaricious patriarch of a family of moneylenders. Confined to a wheelchair, alternately throwing cushions at his senile wife and demanding " brimstone and treacle" from his granddaughter Judy, he is one of Dickens's most repulsive comic creations. Beneath his grotesque exterior, however, lies a shrewd and ruthless financial operator who has profited from the Chancery system and now circles the dying Krook's property and the Jarndyce secrets like a vulture.

## Role in the Novel

Smallweed represents the parasitic ecosystem that the Court of Chancery fosters. Unlike the passive victims of Jarndyce and Jarndyce — Richard Carstone, Miss Flite, Gridley — Smallweed has learned to feed on the legal process itself. He buys up debts, trades in inside information, and positions himself to acquire the bundle of letters that will ultimately expose Lady Dedlock's secret. His role in the plot accelerates dramatically after Krook's spontaneous combustion: Smallweed takes possession of the dead man's papers, discovers Hawdon's letters, and sells them to Tulkinghorn — becoming the mercenary link between the novel's two great repositories of dangerous knowledge.

He is also the uncle of Mr. Jobling (Weevle) and the grandfather of Bart and Judy Smallweed, whom he has trained in his own image of grasping, prematurely aged greed.

## Background and History

Smallweed came from humble origins — he began as a clerk and rose through relentless acquisition and usury. The family history, narrated with savage irony by Dickens in Chapter 21, traces how the Smallweeds evolved over generations into human embodiments of compound interest: every natural impulse or affection has been extinguished, replaced by the instinct to calculate and accumulate. His physical decrepitude — shrivelled legs, a wheelchair, the need to be fed and propped up — is presented as a direct consequence of this spiritual bankruptcy.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-smallweed|Mrs. Smallweed]]**: His wife, now senile and reduced to a state of infantile helplessness, whom he abuses by throwing cushions at her head whenever she speaks. Their marriage is a savage parody of companionship.
- **[[judy-smallweed|Judy Smallweed]]**: His granddaughter and carer, who has been raised to share his values and whose own youth has been crushed into a premature middle age of avarice and servitude.
- **[[bart-smallweed|Bart Smallweed]]**: His grandson, a clerk at Kenge and Carboy, similarly warped by the family's financial obsessions.
- **[[mr-jobling|Mr. Jobling (Weevle)]]**: His nephew, a law-writer who lodges in Krook's building and acts as Smallweed's agent in securing the dead man's papers.
- **[[krook|Krook]]**: Smallweed is Krook's brother-in-law (though this relationship is distant and purely businesslike). After Krook's death, Smallweed takes possession of his property.
- **[[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]]**: Smallweed sells Hawdon's letters to Tulkinghorn, setting the final act of the tragedy in motion.
- **[[guppy|Mr. Guppy]]**: Acquaintance through the law-writers' network; Smallweed treats him with contemptuous familiarity.

## Key Scenes

Smallweed is introduced in Chapter 20, where he is wheeled into George Rouncewell's shooting gallery to demand repayment of a debt. His entrance is a tour de force of grotesque physical description [020-052]. The scene establishes his method: relentless, wheedling pressure backed by the threat of legal action.

Chapter 21 provides the Smallweed family history — a sustained piece of satirical genealogy that traces the "gradual assimilation of the human Smallweeds to the habits of the money-market" [021].

Following Krook's death by spontaneous combustion (Chapter 32), Smallweed descends on the rag-and-bottle shop to claim the deceased's effects. It is in sorting through Krook's accumulated hoard of papers that Smallweed discovers the packet of Captain Hawdon's love letters from Lady Dedlock — the physical evidence of her pre-marital affair. Smallweed, recognising their value but unable to read their full significance, sells them to Tulkinghorn:

> "Mr. Smallweed is decidedly of opinion 'much the best thing she could do.'" [020-203]

In Chapter 62, the dying Smallweed is visited by Inspector Bucket, who extracts from him the final information needed to close the case — a scene that mirrors his own predatory visits to debtors throughout the novel.

## Character Analysis

Grandfather Smallweed is the novel's most concentrated embodiment of what the Chancery system produces when its logic is internalised and embraced rather than suffered. If Richard Carstone is destroyed by his hope of a Chancery fortune, Smallweed is preserved — but at the cost of his humanity — by feeding on the desperation Chancery creates. His physical condition is explicitly allegorical: the shrivelled legs, the wheelchair, the perpetually agitated state are Dickens's way of literalising the spiritual deformity of avarice.

The comedy of Smallweed — the "shake me up, Judy!" ritual, the cushions thrown at Mrs. Smallweed's head — is deliberately discomforting. Dickens makes the reader laugh at a monster, then interrogates why we laugh. The Smallweed household, with its senile grandmother, its prematurely aged grandchildren, and its tyrannical patriarch, is a funhouse-mirror reflection of the Victorian patriarchal family, distorted to reveal its latent cruelties.

The name itself is significant: "small weed" suggests a plant that is both insignificant and parasitic — a weed that chokes other growth. Yet Smallweed is far from insignificant in the plot. He is arguably the novel's most efficient agent of destruction: it is his sale of the letters, more than Tulkinghorn's investigation or Hortense's bullet, that activates the tragic machinery. The moneylender, not the lawyer, is the fated link in the chain.
