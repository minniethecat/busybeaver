[046-001] Darkness rests upon Tom-All-Alone’s. Dilating and dilating since the

[046-002] sun went down last night, it has gradually swelled until it fills

[046-003] every void in the place. For a time there were some dungeon lights

[046-004] burning, as the lamp of life hums in Tom-all-Alone’s, heavily,

[046-005] heavily, in the nauseous air, and winking—as that lamp, too, winks

[046-006] in Tom-all-Alone’s—at many horrible things. But they are blotted

[046-007] out. The moon has eyed Tom with a dull cold stare, as admitting some

[046-008] puny emulation of herself in his desert region unfit for life and

[046-009] blasted by volcanic fires; but she has passed on and is gone. The

[046-010] blackest nightmare in the infernal stables grazes on Tom-all-Alone’s,

[046-011] and Tom is fast asleep.

[046-012] Much mighty speech-making there has been, both in and out of

[046-013] [[parliament|Parliament]], concerning Tom, and much wrathful disputation how Tom

[046-014] shall be got right. Whether he shall be put into the main road by

[046-015] constables, or by beadles, or by bell-ringing, or by force of

[046-016] figures, or by correct principles of taste, or by high church, or by

[046-017] low church, or by no church; whether he shall be set to splitting

[046-018] trusses of polemical straws with the crooked knife of his mind or

[046-019] whether he shall be put to stone-breaking instead. In the midst of

[046-020] which dust and noise there is but one thing perfectly clear, to wit,

[046-021] that Tom only may and can, or shall and will, be reclaimed according

[046-022] to somebody’s theory but nobody’s practice. And in the hopeful

[046-023] meantime, Tom goes to perdition head foremost in his old determined

[046-024] spirit.

[046-025] But he has his revenge. Even the winds are his messengers, and they

[046-026] serve him in these hours of darkness. There is not a drop of Tom’s

[046-027] corrupted blood but propagates [[disease-and-contagion|infection]] and contagion somewhere. It

[046-028] shall pollute, this very night, the choice stream (in which chemists

[046-029] on analysis would find the genuine nobility) of a Norman house, and

[046-030] his Grace shall not be able to say nay to the infamous alliance.

[046-031] There is not an atom of Tom’s slime, not a cubic inch of any

[046-032] pestilential gas in which he lives, not one obscenity or degradation

[046-033] about him, not an ignorance, not a wickedness, not a brutality of his

[046-034] committing, but shall work its retribution through every order of

[046-035] society up to the proudest of the proud and to the highest of the

[046-036] high. Verily, what with tainting, plundering, and spoiling, Tom has

[046-037] his revenge.

[046-038] It is a moot point whether Tom-all-Alone’s be uglier by day or by

[046-039] night, but on the argument that the more that is seen of it the more

[046-040] shocking it must be, and that no part of it left to the imagination

[046-041] is at all likely to be made so bad as the reality, day carries it.

[046-042] The day begins to break now; and in truth it might be better for the

[046-043] national glory even that the sun should sometimes set upon the

[046-044] British dominions than that it should ever rise upon so vile a wonder

[046-045] as Tom.

[046-046] A brown sunburnt gentleman, who appears in some inaptitude for sleep

[046-047] to be wandering abroad rather than counting the hours on a restless

[046-048] pillow, strolls hitherward at this quiet time. Attracted by

[046-049] curiosity, he often pauses and looks about him, up and down the

[046-050] miserable by-ways. Nor is he merely curious, for in his bright dark

[046-051] eye there is compassionate interest; and as he looks here and there,

[046-052] he seems to understand such wretchedness and to have studied it

[046-053] before.

[046-054] On the banks of the stagnant channel of mud which is the main street

[046-055] of Tom-all-Alone’s, nothing is to be seen but the crazy houses, shut

[046-056] up and silent. No waking creature save himself appears except in one

[046-057] direction, where he sees the solitary figure of a woman sitting on a

[046-058] door-step. He walks that way. Approaching, he observes that she has

[046-059] journeyed a long distance and is footsore and travel-stained. She

[046-060] sits on the door-step in the manner of one who is waiting, with her

[046-061] elbow on her knee and her head upon her hand. Beside her is a canvas

[046-062] bag, or bundle, she has carried. She is dozing probably, for she

[046-063] gives no heed to his steps as he comes toward her.

[046-064] The broken footway is so narrow that when [[allan-woodcourt|Allan Woodcourt]] comes to

[046-065] where the woman sits, he has to turn into the road to pass her.

[046-066] Looking down at her face, his eye meets hers, and he stops.

[046-067] “What is the matter?”

[046-068] “Nothing, sir.”

[046-069] “Can’t you make them hear? Do you want to be let in?”

[046-070] “I’m waiting till they get up at another house—a lodging-house—not

[046-071] here,” the woman patiently returns. “I’m waiting here because there

[046-072] will be sun here presently to warm me.”

[046-073] “I am afraid you are tired. I am sorry to see you sitting in the

[046-074] street.”

[046-075] “Thank you, sir. It don’t matter.”

[046-076] A habit in him of speaking to the poor and of avoiding patronage or

[046-077] condescension or childishness (which is the favourite device, many

[046-078] people deeming it quite a subtlety to talk to them like little

[046-079] spelling books) has put him on good terms with the woman easily.

[046-080] “Let me look at your forehead,” he says, bending down. “I am a

[046-081] doctor. Don’t be afraid. I wouldn’t hurt you for the world.”

[046-082] He knows that by touching her with his skilful and accustomed hand he

[046-083] can soothe her yet more readily. She makes a slight objection,

[046-084] saying, “It’s nothing”; but he has scarcely laid his fingers on the

[046-085] wounded place when she lifts it up to the light.

[046-086] “Aye! A bad bruise, and the skin sadly broken. This must be very

[046-087] sore.”

[046-088] “It do ache a little, sir,” returns the woman with a started tear

[046-089] upon her cheek.

[046-090] “Let me try to make it more comfortable. My handkerchief won’t hurt

[046-091] you.”

[046-092] “Oh, dear no, sir, I’m sure of that!”

[046-093] He cleanses the injured place and dries it, and having carefully

[046-094] examined it and gently pressed it with the palm of his hand, takes a

[046-095] small case from his pocket, dresses it, and binds it up. While he is

[046-096] thus employed, he says, after laughing at his establishing a surgery

[046-097] in the street, “And so your husband is a brickmaker?”

[046-098] “How do you know that, sir?” asks the woman, astonished.

[046-099] “Why, I suppose so from the colour of the clay upon your bag and on

[046-100] your dress. And I know brickmakers go about working at piecework in

[046-101] different places. And I am sorry to say I have known them cruel to

[046-102] their wives too.”

[046-103] The woman hastily lifts up her eyes as if she would deny that her

[046-104] injury is referable to such a cause. But feeling the hand upon her

[046-105] forehead, and seeing his busy and composed face, she quietly drops

[046-106] them again.

[046-107] “Where is he now?” asks the surgeon.

[046-108] “He got into trouble last night, sir; but he’ll look for me at the

[046-109] lodging-house.”

[046-110] “He will get into worse trouble if he often misuses his large and

[046-111] heavy hand as he has misused it here. But you forgive him, brutal as

[046-112] he is, and I say no more of him, except that I wish he deserved it.

[046-113] You have no young child?”

[046-114] The woman shakes her head. “One as I calls mine, sir, but it’s

[046-115] Liz’s.”

[046-116] “Your own is dead. I see! Poor little thing!”

[046-117] By this time he has finished and is putting up his case. “I suppose

[046-118] you have some settled home. Is it far from here?” he asks,

[046-119] good-humouredly making light of what he has done as she gets up and

[046-120] curtsys.

[046-121] “It’s a good two or three and twenty mile from here, sir. At Saint

[046-122] Albans. You know Saint Albans, sir? I thought you gave a start like,

[046-123] as if you did.”

[046-124] “Yes, I know something of it. And now I will ask you a question in

[046-125] return. Have you money for your lodging?”

[046-126] “Yes, sir,” she says, “really and truly.” And she shows it. He tells

[046-127] her, in acknowledgment of her many subdued thanks, that she is very

[046-128] welcome, gives her good day, and walks away. Tom-all-Alone’s is still

[046-129] asleep, and nothing is astir.

[046-130] Yes, something is! As he retraces his way to the point from which he

[046-131] descried the woman at a distance sitting on the step, he sees a

[046-132] ragged figure coming very cautiously along, crouching close to the

[046-133] soiled walls—which the wretchedest figure might as well avoid—and

[046-134] furtively thrusting a hand before it. It is the figure of a youth

[046-135] whose face is hollow and whose eyes have an emaciated glare. He is so

[046-136] intent on getting along unseen that even the apparition of a stranger

[046-137] in whole garments does not tempt him to look back. He shades his face

[046-138] with his ragged elbow as he passes on the other side of the way, and

[046-139] goes shrinking and creeping on with his anxious hand before him and

[046-140] his shapeless clothes hanging in shreds. Clothes made for what

[046-141] purpose, or of what material, it would be impossible to say. They

[046-142] look, in colour and in substance, like a bundle of rank leaves of

[046-143] swampy growth that rotted long ago.

[046-144] Allan Woodcourt pauses to look after him and note all this, with a

[046-145] shadowy belief that he has seen the boy before. He cannot recall how

[046-146] or where, but there is some association in his mind with such a form.

[046-147] He imagines that he must have seen it in some hospital or refuge,

[046-148] still, cannot make out why it comes with any special force on his

[046-149] remembrance.

[046-150] He is gradually emerging from Tom-all-Alone’s in the morning light,

[046-151] thinking about it, when he hears running feet behind him, and looking

[046-152] round, sees the boy scouring towards him at great speed, followed by

[046-153] the woman.

[046-154] “Stop him, stop him!” cries the woman, almost breathless. “Stop him,

[046-155] sir!”

[046-156] He darts across the road into the boy’s path, but the boy is quicker

[046-157] than he, makes a curve, ducks, dives under his hands, comes up

[046-158] half-a-dozen yards beyond him, and scours away again. Still the woman

[046-159] follows, crying, “Stop him, sir, pray stop him!” Allan, not knowing

[046-160] but that he has just robbed her of her money, follows in chase and

[046-161] runs so hard that he runs the boy down a dozen times, but each time

[046-162] he repeats the curve, the duck, the dive, and scours away again. To

[046-163] strike at him on any of these occasions would be to fell and disable

[046-164] him, but the pursuer cannot resolve to do that, and so the grimly

[046-165] ridiculous pursuit continues. At last the fugitive, hard-pressed,

[046-166] takes to a narrow passage and a court which has no thoroughfare.

[046-167] Here, against a hoarding of decaying timber, he is brought to bay and

[046-168] tumbles down, lying gasping at his pursuer, who stands and gasps at

[046-169] him until the woman comes up.

[046-170] “Oh, you, [[jo-crossing-sweeper|Jo]]!” cries the woman. “What? I have found you at last!”

[046-171] “Jo,” repeats Allan, looking at him with attention, “Jo! Stay. To be

[046-172] sure! I recollect this lad some time ago being brought before the

[046-173] coroner.”

[046-174] “Yes, I see you once afore at the inkwhich,” whimpers Jo. “What of

[046-175] that? Can’t you never let such an unfortnet as me alone? An’t I

[046-176] unfortnet enough for you yet? How unfortnet do you want me fur to be?

[046-177] I’ve been a-chivied and a-chivied, fust by one on you and nixt by

[046-178] another on you, till I’m worritted to skins and bones. The inkwhich

[046-179] warn’t MY fault. I done nothink. He wos wery good to me, he wos; he

[046-180] wos the only one I knowed to speak to, as ever come across my

[046-181] crossing. It ain’t wery likely I should want him to be inkwhiched. I

[046-182] only wish I wos, myself. I don’t know why I don’t go and make a hole

[046-183] in the water, I’m sure I don’t.”

[046-184] He says it with such a pitiable air, and his grimy tears appear so

[046-185] real, and he lies in the corner up against the hoarding so like a

[046-186] growth of fungus or any unwholesome excrescence produced there in

[046-187] neglect and impurity, that Allan Woodcourt is softened towards him.

[046-188] He says to the woman, “Miserable creature, what has he done?”

[046-189] To which she only replies, shaking her head at the prostrate figure

[046-190] more amazedly than angrily, “Oh, you Jo, you Jo. I have found you at

[046-191] last!”

[046-192] “What has he done?” says Allan. “Has he robbed you?”

[046-193] “No, sir, no. Robbed me? He did nothing but what was kind-hearted by

[046-194] me, and that’s the wonder of it.”

[046-195] Allan looks from Jo to the woman, and from the woman to Jo, waiting

[046-196] for one of them to unravel the riddle.

[046-197] “But he was along with me, sir,” says the woman. “Oh, you Jo! He was

[046-198] along with me, sir, down at Saint Albans, ill, and a young lady, Lord

[046-199] bless her for a good friend to me, took pity on him when I durstn’t,

[046-200] and took him home—”

[046-201] Allan shrinks back from him with a sudden horror.

[046-202] “Yes, sir, yes. Took him home, and made him comfortable, and like a

[046-203] thankless monster he ran away in the night and never has been seen or

[046-204] heard of since till I set eyes on him just now. And that young lady

[046-205] that was such a pretty dear caught his illness, lost her beautiful

[046-206] looks, and wouldn’t hardly be known for the same young lady now if it

[046-207] wasn’t for her angel temper, and her pretty shape, and her sweet

[046-208] voice. Do you know it? You ungrateful wretch, do you know that this

[046-209] is all along of you and of her goodness to you?” demands the woman,

[046-210] beginning to rage at him as she recalls it and breaking into

[046-211] passionate tears.

[046-212] The boy, in rough sort stunned by what he hears, falls to smearing

[046-213] his dirty forehead with his dirty palm, and to staring at the ground,

[046-214] and to shaking from head to foot until the crazy hoarding against

[046-215] which he leans rattles.

[046-216] Allan restrains the woman, merely by a quiet gesture, but

[046-217] effectually.

[046-218] “[[richard-carstone|Richard]] told me—” He falters. “I mean, I have heard of this—don’t

[046-219] mind me for a moment, I will speak presently.”

[046-220] He turns away and stands for a while looking out at the covered

[046-221] passage. When he comes back, he has recovered his composure, except

[046-222] that he contends against an avoidance of the boy, which is so very

[046-223] remarkable that it absorbs the woman’s attention.

[046-224] “You hear what she says. But get up, get up!”

[046-225] Jo, shaking and chattering, slowly rises and stands, after the manner

[046-226] of his tribe in a difficulty, sideways against the hoarding, resting

[046-227] one of his high shoulders against it and covertly rubbing his right

[046-228] hand over his left and his left foot over his right.

[046-229] “You hear what she says, and I know it’s true. Have you been here

[046-230] ever since?”

[046-231] “Wishermaydie if I seen Tom-all-Alone’s till this blessed morning,”

[046-232] replies Jo hoarsely.

[046-233] “Why have you come here now?”

[046-234] Jo looks all round the confined court, looks at his questioner no

[046-235] higher than the knees, and finally answers, “I don’t know how to do

[046-236] nothink, and I can’t get nothink to do. I’m wery poor and ill, and I

[046-237] thought I’d come back here when there warn’t nobody about, and lay

[046-238] down and hide somewheres as I knows on till arter dark, and then go

[046-239] and beg a trifle of Mr. Snagsby. He wos allus willin fur to give me

[046-240] somethink he wos, though Mrs. Snagsby she was allus a-chivying on

[046-241] me—like everybody everywheres.”

[046-242] “Where have you come from?”

[046-243] Jo looks all round the court again, looks at his questioner’s knees

[046-244] again, and concludes by laying his profile against the hoarding in a

[046-245] sort of resignation.

[046-246] “Did you hear me ask you where you have come from?”

[046-247] “Tramp then,” says Jo.

[046-248] “Now tell me,” proceeds Allan, making a strong effort to overcome his

[046-249] repugnance, going very near to him, and leaning over him with an

[046-250] expression of confidence, “tell me how it came about that you left

[046-251] that house when the good young lady had been so unfortunate as to

[046-252] pity you and take you home.”

[046-253] Jo suddenly comes out of his resignation and excitedly declares,

[046-254] addressing the woman, that he never known about the young lady, that

[046-255] he never heern about it, that he never went fur to hurt her, that he

[046-256] would sooner have hurt his own self, that he’d sooner have had his

[046-257] unfortnet ed chopped off than ever gone a-nigh her, and that she wos

[046-258] wery good to him, she wos. Conducting himself throughout as if in his

[046-259] poor fashion he really meant it, and winding up with some very

[046-260] miserable sobs.

[046-261] Allan Woodcourt sees that this is not a sham. He constrains himself

[046-262] to touch him. “Come, Jo. Tell me.”

[046-263] “No. I dustn’t,” says Jo, relapsing into the profile state. “I

[046-264] dustn’t, or I would.”

[046-265] “But I must know,” returns the other, “all the same. Come, Jo.”

[046-266] After two or three such adjurations, Jo lifts up his head again,

[046-267] looks round the court again, and says in a low voice, “Well, I’ll

[046-268] tell you something. I was took away. There!”

[046-269] “Took away? In the night?”

[046-270] “Ah!” Very apprehensive of being overheard, Jo looks about him and

[046-271] even glances up some ten feet at the top of the hoarding and through

[046-272] the cracks in it lest the object of his distrust should be looking

[046-273] over or hidden on the other side.

[046-274] “Who took you away?”

[046-275] “I dustn’t name him,” says Jo. “I dustn’t do it, sir.

[046-276] “But I want, in the young lady’s name, to know. You may trust me. No

[046-277] one else shall hear.”

[046-278] “Ah, but I don’t know,” replies Jo, shaking his head fearfully, “as

[046-279] he DON’T hear.”

[046-280] “Why, he is not in this place.”

[046-281] “Oh, ain’t he though?” says Jo. “He’s in all manner of places, all at

[046-282] wanst.”

[046-283] Allan looks at him in perplexity, but discovers some real meaning and

[046-284] good faith at the bottom of this bewildering reply. He patiently

[046-285] awaits an explicit answer; and Jo, more baffled by his patience than

[046-286] by anything else, at last desperately whispers a name in his ear.

[046-287] “Aye!” says Allan. “Why, what had you been doing?”

[046-288] “Nothink, sir. Never done nothink to get myself into no trouble,

[046-289] ’sept in not moving on and the inkwhich. But I’m a-moving on now. I’m

[046-290] a-moving on to the berryin ground—that’s the move as I’m up to.”

[046-291] “No, no, we will try to prevent that. But what did he do with you?”

[046-292] “Put me in a horsepittle,” replied Jo, whispering, “till I was

[046-293] discharged, then giv me a little money—four half-bulls, wot you may

[046-294] call half-crowns—and ses ‘Hook it! Nobody wants you here,’ he ses.

[046-295] ‘You hook it. You go and tramp,’ he ses. ‘You move on,’ he ses.

[046-296] ‘Don’t let me ever see you nowheres within forty mile of [[london|London]], or

[046-297] you’ll repent it.’ So I shall, if ever he doos see me, and he’ll see

[046-298] me if I’m above ground,” concludes Jo, nervously repeating all his

[046-299] former precautions and investigations.

[046-300] Allan considers a little, then remarks, turning to the woman but

[046-301] keeping an encouraging eye on Jo, “He is not so ungrateful as you

[046-302] supposed. He had a reason for going away, though it was an

[046-303] insufficient one.”

[046-304] “Thankee, sir, thankee!” exclaims Jo. “There now! See how hard you

[046-305] wos upon me. But ony you tell the young lady wot the genlmn ses, and

[046-306] it’s all right. For YOU wos wery good to me too, and I knows it.”

[046-307] “Now, Jo,” says Allan, keeping his eye upon him, “come with me and I

[046-308] will find you a better place than this to lie down and hide in. If I

[046-309] take one side of the way and you the other to avoid observation, you

[046-310] will not run away, I know very well, if you make me a promise.”

[046-311] “I won’t, not unless I wos to see HIM a-coming, sir.”

[046-312] “Very well. I take your word. Half the town is getting up by this

[046-313] time, and the whole town will be broad awake in another hour. Come

[046-314] along. Good day again, my good woman.”

[046-315] “Good day again, sir, and I thank you kindly many times again.”

[046-316] She has been sitting on her bag, deeply attentive, and now rises and

[046-317] takes it up. Jo, repeating, “Ony you tell the young lady as I never

[046-318] went fur to hurt her and wot the genlmn ses!” nods and shambles and

[046-319] shivers, and smears and blinks, and half laughs and half cries, a

[046-320] farewell to her, and takes his creeping way along after Allan

[046-321] Woodcourt, close to the houses on the opposite side of the street. In

[046-322] this order, the two come up out of Tom-all-Alone’s into the broad

[046-323] rays of the sunlight and the purer air.
