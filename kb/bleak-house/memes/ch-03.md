[003-001] I have a great deal of difficulty in beginning to write my portion of

[003-002] these pages, for I know I am not clever. I always knew that. I can

[003-003] remember, when I was a very little girl indeed, I used to say to my

[003-004] doll when we were alone together, “Now, Dolly, I am not clever, you

[003-005] know very well, and you must be patient with me, like a dear!” And so

[003-006] she used to sit propped up in a great arm-chair, with her beautiful

[003-007] complexion and rosy lips, staring at me—or not so much at me, I

[003-008] think, as at nothing—while I busily stitched away and told her every

[003-009] one of my secrets.

[003-010] My dear old doll! I was such a shy little thing that I seldom dared

[003-011] to open my lips, and never dared to open my heart, to anybody else.

[003-012] It almost makes me cry to think what a relief it used to be to me

[003-013] when I came home from school of a day to run upstairs to my room and

[003-014] say, “Oh, you dear faithful Dolly, I knew you would be expecting me!”

[003-015] and then to sit down on the floor, leaning on the elbow of her great

[003-016] chair, and tell her all I had noticed since we parted. I had always

[003-017] rather a noticing way—not a quick way, oh, no!—a silent way of

[003-018] noticing what passed before me and thinking I should like to

[003-019] understand it better. I have not by any means a quick understanding.

[003-020] When I love a person very tenderly indeed, it seems to brighten. But

[003-021] even that may be my vanity.

[003-022] I was brought up, from my earliest remembrance—like some of the

[003-023] princesses in the fairy stories, only I was not charming—by my

[003-024] godmother. At least, I only knew her as such. She was a good, good

[003-025] woman! She went to church three times every Sunday, and to morning

[003-026] prayers on Wednesdays and Fridays, and to lectures whenever there

[003-027] were lectures; and never missed. She was handsome; and if she had

[003-028] ever smiled, would have been (I used to think) like an angel—but she

[003-029] never smiled. She was always grave and strict. She was so very good

[003-030] herself, I thought, that the badness of other people made her frown

[003-031] all her life. I felt so different from her, even making every

[003-032] allowance for the differences between a child and a woman; I felt so

[003-033] poor, so trifling, and so far off that I never could be unrestrained

[003-034] with her—no, could never even love her as I wished. It made me very

[003-035] sorry to consider how good she was and how unworthy of her I was, and

[003-036] I used ardently to hope that I might have a better heart; and I

[003-037] talked it over very often with the dear old doll, but I never loved

[003-038] my godmother as I ought to have loved her and as I felt I must have

[003-039] loved her if I had been a better girl.

[003-040] This made me, I dare say, more timid and retiring than I naturally

[003-041] was and cast me upon Dolly as the only friend with whom I felt at

[003-042] ease. But something happened when I was still quite a little thing

[003-043] that helped it very much.

[003-044] I had never heard my mama spoken of. I had never heard of my papa

[003-045] either, but I felt more interested about my mama. I had never worn a

[003-046] black frock, that I could recollect. I had never been shown my mama’s

[003-047] grave. I had never been told where it was. Yet I had never been

[003-048] taught to pray for any relation but my godmother. I had more than

[003-049] once approached this subject of my thoughts with Mrs. Rachael, our

[003-050] only servant, who took my light away when I was in bed (another very

[003-051] good woman, but austere to me), and she had only said, “[[esther-summerson|Esther]], good

[003-052] night!” and gone away and left me.

[003-053] Although there were seven girls at the neighbouring school where I

[003-054] was a day boarder, and although they called me little Esther

[003-055] Summerson, I knew none of them at home. All of them were older than

[003-056] I, to be sure (I was the youngest there by a good deal), but there

[003-057] seemed to be some other separation between us besides that, and

[003-058] besides their being far more clever than I was and knowing much more

[003-059] than I did. One of them in the first week of my going to the school

[003-060] (I remember it very well) invited me home to a little party, to my

[003-061] great joy. But my godmother wrote a stiff letter declining for me,

[003-062] and I never went. I never went out at all.

[003-063] It was my birthday. There were holidays at school on other

[003-064] birthdays—none on mine. There were rejoicings at home on other

[003-065] birthdays, as I knew from what I heard the girls relate to one

[003-066] another—there were none on mine. My birthday was the most melancholy

[003-067] day at home in the whole year.

[003-068] I have mentioned that unless my vanity should deceive me (as I know

[003-069] it may, for I may be very vain without suspecting it, though indeed I

[003-070] don’t), my comprehension is quickened when my affection is. My

[003-071] disposition is very affectionate, and perhaps I might still feel such

[003-072] a wound if such a wound could be received more than once with the

[003-073] quickness of that birthday.

[003-074] Dinner was over, and my godmother and I were sitting at the table

[003-075] before the fire. The clock ticked, the fire clicked; not another

[003-076] sound had been heard in the room or in the house for I don’t know how

[003-077] long. I happened to look timidly up from my stitching, across the

[003-078] table at my godmother, and I saw in her face, looking gloomily at me,

[003-079] “It would have been far better, little Esther, that you had had no

[003-080] birthday, that you had never been born!”

[003-081] I broke out crying and sobbing, and I said, “Oh, dear godmother, tell

[003-082] me, pray do tell me, did Mama die on my birthday?”

[003-083] “No,” she returned. “Ask me no more, child!”

[003-084] “Oh, do pray tell me something of her. Do now, at last, dear

[003-085] godmother, if you please! What did I do to her? How did I lose her?

[003-086] Why am I so different from other children, and why is it my fault,

[003-087] dear godmother? No, no, no, don’t go away. Oh, speak to me!”

[003-088] I was in a kind of fright beyond my grief, and I caught hold of her

[003-089] dress and was kneeling to her. She had been saying all the while,

[003-090] “Let me go!” But now she stood still.

[003-092] midst of my vehemence. I put up my trembling little hand to clasp

[003-093] hers or to beg her pardon with what earnestness I might, but withdrew

[003-100] forgiven her”—but her face did not relent—“the wrong she did to me,

[003-102] know—than any one will ever know but I, the sufferer. For yourself,

[003-105] your head, according to what is written. Forget your mother and leave

[003-106] all other people to forget her who will do her unhappy child that

[003-107] greatest kindness. Now, go!”

[003-108] She checked me, however, as I was about to depart from her—so frozen

[003-110] are the preparations for a life begun with such a shadow on it. You

[003-111] are different from other children, Esther, because you were not born,

[003-112] like them, in common sinfulness and wrath. You are set apart.”

[003-113] I went up to my room, and crept to bed, and laid my doll’s cheek

[003-114] against mine wet with tears, and holding that solitary friend upon my

[003-115] bosom, cried myself to sleep. Imperfect as my understanding of my

[003-116] sorrow was, I knew that I had brought no joy at any time to anybody’s

[003-117] heart and that I was to no one upon earth what Dolly was to me.

[003-118] Dear, dear, to think how much time we passed alone together

[003-119] afterwards, and how often I repeated to the doll the story of my

[003-120] birthday and confided to her that I would try as hard as ever I could

[003-121] to repair the fault I had been born with (of which I confessedly felt

[003-122] guilty and yet innocent) and would strive as I grew up to be

[003-123] industrious, contented, and kind-hearted and to do some good to some

[003-124] one, and win some love to myself if I could. I hope it is not

[003-125] self-indulgent to shed these tears as I think of it. I am very

[003-126] thankful, I am very cheerful, but I cannot quite help their coming to

[003-127] my eyes.

[003-128] There! I have wiped them away now and can go on again properly.

[003-129] I felt the distance between my godmother and myself so much more

[003-130] after the birthday, and felt so sensible of filling a place in her

[003-131] house which ought to have been empty, that I found her more difficult

[003-132] of approach, though I was fervently grateful to her in my heart, than

[003-133] ever. I felt in the same way towards my school companions; I felt in

[003-134] the same way towards Mrs. Rachael, who was a widow; and oh, towards

[003-135] her daughter, of whom she was proud, who came to see her once a

[003-136] fortnight! I was very retired and quiet, and tried to be very

[003-137] diligent.

[003-138] One sunny afternoon when I had come home from school with my books

[003-139] and portfolio, watching my long shadow at my side, and as I was

[003-140] gliding upstairs to my room as usual, my godmother looked out of the

[003-141] parlour-door and called me back. Sitting with her, I found—which was

[003-142] very unusual indeed—a stranger. A portly, important-looking

[003-143] gentleman, dressed all in black, with a white cravat, large gold

[003-144] watch seals, a pair of gold eye-glasses, and a large seal-ring upon

[003-145] his little finger.

[003-146] “This,” said my godmother in an undertone, “is the child.” Then she

[003-147] said in her naturally stern way of speaking, “This is Esther, sir.”

[003-148] The gentleman put up his eye-glasses to look at me and said, “Come

[003-149] here, my dear!” He shook hands with me and asked me to take off my

[003-150] bonnet, looking at me all the while. When I had complied, he said,

[003-151] “Ah!” and afterwards “Yes!” And then, taking off his eye-glasses and

[003-154] Upon that, my godmother said, “You may go upstairs, Esther!” And I

[003-155] made him my curtsy and left him.

[003-164] stone at her!’”

[003-165] I was stopped by my godmother’s rising, putting her hand to her head,

[003-166] and crying out in an awful voice from quite another part of the book,

[003-167] “‘Watch ye, therefore, lest coming suddenly he find you sleeping. And

[003-168] what I say unto you, I say unto all, Watch!’”

[003-169] In an instant, while she stood before me repeating these words, she
