# Mrs. Snagsby

## Overview

Mrs. Snagsby is the formidable wife of the law-stationer Mr. Snagsby. A woman of "vinegary" temperament, she rules the Snagsby household — and her husband — with a combination of sharp reproaches, financial control, and a relentless suspicion that Mr. Snagsby is concealing dark secrets from her. Her jealousy, though comic in its excess, functions as a narrative engine: it is her pursuit of the "secret" that draws Jo the crossing-sweeper into the Snagsby household and, indirectly, into the orbit of Tulkinghorn's investigation.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Snagsby is primarily a comic figure, but her role in the plot is serious. Her conviction that Mr. Snagsby is the father of Jo — a suspicion entirely unfounded — leads her to interrogate the household, upbraid her husband, and ultimately involve Inspector Bucket, all of which connects Jo's testimony at the inquest to the larger machinery of Tulkinghorn's inquiry. Her obsessive jealousy thus functions as an accidental detective impulse: in her determination to uncover her husband's supposed secret, she inadvertently exposes the real secrets that others are trying to hide.

She is also, through her financial management of the stationery business, a counter-image to the novel's other wives. Unlike Mrs. Bagnet, whose competence is paired with affection and trust, Mrs. Snagsby's efficiency is weaponised — her control of the accounts is a form of domestic tyranny.

## Background and History

Mrs. Snagsby was the niece of Peffer, the previous proprietor of the Cook's Court stationery business. Her marriage to Mr. Snagsby was arranged on the understanding that he would inherit the shop. She thus occupies a position of structural superiority in the marriage: the business is hers by blood, and Mr. Snagsby is, in effect, her manager. This power imbalance, combined with her naturally suspicious temperament, has produced a marriage of perpetual surveillance and evasion.

She is childless, which intensifies her attention on her husband and her household. The maid Guster — a workhouse foundling prone to epileptic fits — is a particular target of her contradictory impulses: Mrs. Snagsby alternately bullies and protects her, unable to sustain either kindness or cruelty consistently.

## Relationships

- **[[mr-snagsby|Mr. Snagsby]]**: Her husband, whom she alternately reproaches and clings to. The marriage is defined by her jealousy and his timidity — a closed loop in which his evasions fuel her suspicions and her suspicions provoke further evasions.
- **Guster**: The Snagsbys' maid-of-all-work, whom Mrs. Snagsby treats with erratic harshness born of her own frustrations.
- **[[jo-crossing-sweeper|Jo]]**: The street urchin whose presence in the household — brought there by Mr. Snagsby out of a vague sense of charity — crystallises Mrs. Snagsby's suspicions. She is convinced that Jo is her husband's illegitimate child.
- **[[inspector-bucket|Inspector Bucket]]**: Whom she consults about her husband's "secret," unwittingly feeding information into Bucket's investigation.
- **[[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]]**: To whom she defers as a personage of legal authority, and whose summonses to her husband deepen her unease.

## Key Scenes

Mrs. Snagsby is introduced in Chapter 10 as an extension of her husband — "Mr. and Mrs. Snagsby are not only one bone and one flesh but one voice" [010-051]. This sardonic opening immediately establishes the nature of their union: not a partnership but an absorption, with Mrs. Snagsby as the dominant element.

Her suspicion is given sustained comic development throughout the novel's middle chapters. When Jo is brought to Cook's Court, Mrs. Snagsby's interrogation of the boy is a set-piece of Dickensian comedy:

> "Mrs. Snagsby is jealous and inquisitive and that Mr. Snagsby is henpecked." [010-113]

The comedy reaches its peak in Chapter 11, where she confronts Mr. Snagsby about Jo — "that boy," as she calls him, refusing to name the supposed evidence of her husband's infidelity — and drives the stationer to a state of guilty bewilderment over a crime he has not committed [011-149].

During the long vacation, Mrs. Snagsby's jealousy acquires a new dimension: she becomes convinced that her husband's frequent absences from the shop are assignations, when in fact they are unwilling errands for Inspector Bucket [019-092]. The irony — that her husband's "secret" is not an affair but a murder investigation — is one of Dickens's most sustained comic structures.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Snagsby is a study in the corrosive effects of powerlessness disguised as power. She dominates her husband absolutely — the household finances, the shop accounts, even his diet and leisure hours are under her control — yet this dominance brings her no satisfaction, because she suspects (correctly) that Mr. Snagsby's compliance is not love but fear. The jealousy she directs at him is, at root, a demand for the one thing her position cannot compel: genuine affection freely given.

The comic mechanism of the Snagsby marriage — her relentless investigations producing precisely the evasions they are designed to uncover — is an echo, in the domestic sphere, of the novel's larger critique of Chancery. Like the Jarndyce suit, Mrs. Snagsby's inquiries consume all the resources they touch and produce nothing of value. The "secret" she pursues does not exist; her investigation is its own justification, perpetuating itself indefinitely.

Yet Dickens does not make Mrs. Snagsby merely monstrous. Her childlessness, her inherited shop rather than chosen home, her husband's emotional retreat into vague goodwill — these are circumstances that invite sympathy even as her behaviour invites laughter. She is, like many Dickensian comic figures, a real person trapped in an unreal situation.
