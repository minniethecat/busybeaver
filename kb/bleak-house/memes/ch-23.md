[023-001] We came home from Mr. Boythorn’s after six pleasant weeks. We were

[023-002] often in the park and in the woods and seldom passed the lodge where

[023-003] we had taken shelter without looking in to speak to the keeper’s

[023-004] wife; but we saw no more of [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]], except at church on

[023-005] Sundays. There was company at [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]]; and although several

[023-006] beautiful faces surrounded her, her face retained the same influence

[023-007] on me as at first. I do not quite know even now whether it was

[023-008] painful or pleasurable, whether it drew me towards her or made me

[023-009] shrink from her. I think I admired her with a kind of fear, and I

[023-010] know that in her presence my thoughts always wandered back, as they

[023-011] had done at first, to that old time of my life.

[023-012] I had a fancy, on more than one of these Sundays, that what this lady

[023-013] so curiously was to me, I was to her—I mean that I disturbed her

[023-014] thoughts as she influenced mine, though in some different way. But

[023-015] when I stole a glance at her and saw her so composed and distant and

[023-016] unapproachable, I felt this to be a foolish weakness. Indeed, I felt

[023-017] the whole state of my mind in reference to her to be weak and

[023-018] unreasonable, and I remonstrated with myself about it as much as I

[023-019] could.

[023-020] One incident that occurred before we quitted Mr. Boythorn’s house, I

[023-021] had better mention in this place.

[023-022] I was walking in the garden with [[ada-clare|Ada]] when I was told that some one

[023-023] wished to see me. Going into the breakfast-room where this person was

[023-024] waiting, I found it to be the French maid who had cast off her shoes

[023-025] and walked through the wet grass on the day when it thundered and

[023-026] lightened.

[023-027] “Mademoiselle,” she began, looking fixedly at me with her too-eager

[023-028] eyes, though otherwise presenting an agreeable appearance and

[023-029] speaking neither with boldness nor servility, “I have taken a great

[023-030] liberty in coming here, but you know how to excuse it, being so

[023-031] amiable, mademoiselle.”

[023-032] “No excuse is necessary,” I returned, “if you wish to speak to me.”

[023-033] “That is my desire, mademoiselle. A thousand thanks for the

[023-034] permission. I have your leave to speak. Is it not?” she said in a

[023-035] quick, natural way.

[023-036] “Certainly,” said I.

[023-037] “Mademoiselle, you are so amiable! Listen then, if you please. I have

[023-038] left my Lady. We could not agree. My Lady is so high, so very high.

[023-039] Pardon! Mademoiselle, you are right!” Her quickness anticipated what

[023-040] I might have said presently but as yet had only thought. “It is not

[023-041] for me to come here to complain of my Lady. But I say she is so high,

[023-042] so very high. I will not say a word more. All the world knows that.”

[023-043] “Go on, if you please,” said I.

[023-044] “Assuredly; mademoiselle, I am thankful for your politeness.

[023-045] Mademoiselle, I have an inexpressible desire to find service with a

[023-046] young lady who is good, accomplished, beautiful. You are good,

[023-047] accomplished, and beautiful as an angel. Ah, could I have the honour

[023-048] of being your domestic!”

[023-049] “I am sorry—” I began.

[023-050] “Do not dismiss me so soon, mademoiselle!” she said with an

[023-051] involuntary contraction of her fine black eyebrows. “Let me hope a

[023-052] moment! Mademoiselle, I know this service would be more retired than

[023-053] that which I have quitted. Well! I wish that. I know this service

[023-054] would be less distinguished than that which I have quitted. Well! I

[023-055] wish that, I know that I should win less, as to wages here. Good. I

[023-056] am content.”

[023-057] “I assure you,” said I, quite embarrassed by the mere idea of having

[023-058] such an attendant, “that I keep no maid—”

[023-059] “Ah, mademoiselle, but why not? Why not, when you can have one so

[023-060] devoted to you! Who would be enchanted to serve you; who would be so

[023-061] true, so zealous, and so faithful every day! Mademoiselle, I wish

[023-062] with all my heart to serve you. Do not speak of money at present.

[023-063] Take me as I am. For nothing!”

[023-064] She was so singularly earnest that I drew back, almost afraid of her.

[023-065] Without appearing to notice it, in her ardour she still pressed

[023-066] herself upon me, speaking in a rapid subdued voice, though always

[023-067] with a certain grace and propriety.

[023-068] “Mademoiselle, I come from the South country where we are quick and

[023-069] where we like and dislike very strong. My Lady was too high for me; I

[023-070] was too high for her. It is done—past—finished! Receive me as your

[023-071] domestic, and I will serve you well. I will do more for you than you

[023-072] figure to yourself now. Chut! Mademoiselle, I will—no matter, I will

[023-073] do my utmost possible in all things. If you accept my service, you

[023-074] will not repent it. Mademoiselle, you will not repent it, and I will

[023-075] serve you well. You don’t know how well!”

[023-076] There was a lowering energy in her face as she stood looking at me

[023-077] while I explained the impossibility of my engaging her (without

[023-078] thinking it necessary to say how very little I desired to do so),

[023-079] which seemed to bring visibly before me some woman from the streets

[023-080] of Paris in the reign of terror.

[023-081] She heard me out without interruption and then said with her pretty

[023-082] accent and in her mildest voice, “Hey, mademoiselle, I have received

[023-083] my answer! I am sorry of it. But I must go elsewhere and seek what I

[023-084] have not found here. Will you graciously let me kiss your hand?”

[023-085] She looked at me more intently as she took it, and seemed to take

[023-086] note, with her momentary touch, of every vein in it. “I fear I

[023-087] surprised you, mademoiselle, on the day of the storm?” she said with

[023-088] a parting curtsy.

[023-089] I confessed that she had surprised us all.

[023-090] “I took an oath, mademoiselle,” she said, smiling, “and I wanted to

[023-091] stamp it on my mind so that I might keep it faithfully. And I will!

[023-092] Adieu, mademoiselle!”

[023-093] So ended our conference, which I was very glad to bring to a close. I

[023-094] supposed she went away from the village, for I saw her no more; and

[023-095] nothing else occurred to disturb our tranquil summer pleasures until

[023-096] six weeks were out and we returned home as I began just now by

[023-097] saying.

[023-098] At that time, and for a good many weeks after that time, [[richard-carstone|Richard]] was

[023-099] constant in his visits. Besides coming every Saturday or Sunday and

[023-100] remaining with us until Monday morning, he sometimes rode out on

[023-101] horseback unexpectedly and passed the evening with us and rode back

[023-102] again early next day. He was as vivacious as ever and told us he was

[023-103] very industrious, but I was not easy in my mind about him. It

[023-104] appeared to me that his industry was all misdirected. I could not

[023-105] find that it led to anything but the formation of delusive hopes in

[023-106] connexion with the suit already the pernicious cause of so much

[023-107] sorrow and ruin. He had got at the core of that mystery now, he told

[023-108] us, and nothing could be plainer than that the will under which he

[023-109] and Ada were to take I don’t know how many thousands of pounds must

[023-110] be finally established if there were any sense or justice in the

[023-111] [[chancery-court|Court of Chancery]]—but oh, what a great IF that sounded in my

[023-112] ears—and that this happy conclusion could not be much longer

[023-113] delayed. He proved this to himself by all the weary arguments on that

[023-114] side he had read, and every one of them sunk him deeper in the

[023-115] infatuation. He had even begun to haunt the court. He told us how he

[023-116] saw [[miss-flite|Miss Flite]] there daily, how they talked together, and how he did

[023-117] her little kindnesses, and how, while he laughed at her, he pitied

[023-118] her from his heart. But he never thought—never, my poor, dear,

[023-119] sanguine Richard, capable of so much happiness then, and with such

[023-120] better things before him—what a fatal link was riveting between his

[023-121] fresh youth and her faded age, between his free hopes and her caged

[023-122] birds, and her hungry garret, and her wandering mind.

[023-123] Ada loved him too well to mistrust him much in anything he said or

[023-124] did, and my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]], though he frequently complained of the east

[023-125] wind and read more than usual in the growlery, preserved a strict

[023-126] silence on the subject. So I thought one day when I went to [[london|London]] to

[023-127] meet [[caddy-jellyby|Caddy Jellyby]], at her solicitation, I would ask Richard to be in

[023-128] waiting for me at the coach-office, that we might have a little talk

[023-129] together. I found him there when I arrived, and we walked away arm in

[023-130] arm.

[023-131] “Well, Richard,” said I as soon as I could begin to be grave with

[023-132] him, “are you beginning to feel more settled now?”

[023-133] “Oh, yes, my dear!” returned Richard. “I’m all right enough.”

[023-134] “But settled?” said I.

[023-135] “How do you mean, settled?” returned Richard with his gay laugh.

[023-136] “Settled in the law,” said I.

[023-137] “Oh, aye,” replied Richard, “I’m all right enough.”

[023-138] “You said that before, my dear Richard.”

[023-139] “And you don’t think it’s an answer, eh? Well! Perhaps it’s not.

[023-140] Settled? You mean, do I feel as if I were settling down?”

[023-141] “Yes.”

[023-142] “Why, no, I can’t say I am settling down,” said Richard, strongly

[023-143] emphasizing “down,” as if that expressed the difficulty, “because one

[023-144] can’t settle down while this business remains in such an unsettled

[023-145] state. When I say this business, of course I mean the—forbidden

[023-146] subject.”

[023-147] “Do you think it will ever be in a settled state?” said I.

[023-148] “Not the least doubt of it,” answered Richard.

[023-149] We walked a little way without speaking, and presently Richard

[023-150] addressed me in his frankest and most feeling manner, thus: “My dear

[023-151] [[esther-summerson|Esther]], I understand you, and I wish to heaven I were a more constant

[023-152] sort of fellow. I don’t mean constant to Ada, for I love her

[023-153] dearly—better and better every day—but constant to myself.

[023-154] (Somehow, I mean something that I can’t very well express, but you’ll

[023-155] make it out.) If I were a more constant sort of fellow, I should have

[023-156] held on either to Badger or to [[kenge-and-carboy|Kenge and Carboy]] like grim death, and

[023-157] should have begun to be steady and systematic by this time, and

[023-158] shouldn’t be in debt, and—”

[023-159] “ARE you in debt, Richard?”

[023-160] “Yes,” said Richard, “I am a little so, my dear. Also, I have taken

[023-161] rather too much to billiards and that sort of thing. Now the murder’s

[023-162] out; you despise me, Esther, don’t you?”

[023-163] “You know I don’t,” said I.

[023-164] “You are kinder to me than I often am to myself,” he returned. “My

[023-165] dear Esther, I am a very unfortunate dog not to be more settled, but

[023-166] how CAN I be more settled? If you lived in an unfinished house, you

[023-167] couldn’t settle down in it; if you were condemned to leave everything

[023-168] you undertook unfinished, you would find it hard to apply yourself to

[023-169] anything; and yet that’s my unhappy case. I was born into this

[023-170] unfinished contention with all its chances and changes, and it began

[023-171] to unsettle me before I quite knew the difference between a suit at

[023-172] law and a suit of clothes; and it has gone on unsettling me ever

[023-173] since; and here I am now, conscious sometimes that I am but a

[023-174] worthless fellow to love my confiding cousin Ada.”

[023-175] We were in a solitary place, and he put his hands before his eyes and

[023-176] sobbed as he said the words.

[023-177] “Oh, Richard!” said I. “Do not be so moved. You have a noble nature,

[023-178] and Ada’s love may make you worthier every day.”

[023-179] “I know, my dear,” he replied, pressing my arm, “I know all that. You

[023-180] mustn’t mind my being a little soft now, for I have had all this upon

[023-181] my mind for a long time, and have often meant to speak to you, and

[023-182] have sometimes wanted opportunity and sometimes courage. I know what

[023-183] the thought of Ada ought to do for me, but it doesn’t do it. I am too

[023-184] unsettled even for that. I love her most devotedly, and yet I do her

[023-185] wrong, in doing myself wrong, every day and hour. But it can’t last

[023-186] for ever. We shall come on for a final hearing and get judgment in

[023-187] our favour, and then you and Ada shall see what I can really be!”

[023-188] It had given me a pang to hear him sob and see the tears start out

[023-189] between his fingers, but that was infinitely less affecting to me

[023-190] than the hopeful animation with which he said these words.

[023-191] “I have looked well into the papers, Esther. I have been deep in them

[023-192] for months,” he continued, recovering his cheerfulness in a moment,

[023-193] “and you may rely upon it that we shall come out triumphant. As to

[023-194] years of delay, there has been no want of them, heaven knows! And

[023-195] there is the greater probability of our bringing the matter to a

[023-196] speedy close; in fact, it’s on the paper now. It will be all right at

[023-197] last, and then you shall see!”

[023-198] Recalling how he had just now placed Messrs. Kenge and Carboy in the

[023-199] same category with Mr. Badger, I asked him when he intended to be

[023-200] articled in Lincoln’s Inn.

[023-201] “There again! I think not at all, Esther,” he returned with an

[023-202] effort. “I fancy I have had enough of it. Having worked at Jarndyce

[023-203] and Jarndyce like a galley slave, I have slaked my thirst for the law

[023-204] and satisfied myself that I shouldn’t like it. Besides, I find it

[023-205] unsettles me more and more to be so constantly upon the scene of

[023-206] action. So what,” continued Richard, confident again by this time,

[023-207] “do I naturally turn my thoughts to?”

[023-208] “I can’t imagine,” said I.

[023-209] “Don’t look so serious,” returned Richard, “because it’s the best

[023-210] thing I can do, my dear Esther, I am certain. It’s not as if I wanted

[023-211] a [[professional-identity|profession]] for life. These proceedings will come to a termination,

[023-212] and then I am provided for. No. I look upon it as a pursuit which is

[023-213] in its nature more or less unsettled, and therefore suited to my

[023-214] temporary condition—I may say, precisely suited. What is it that I

[023-215] naturally turn my thoughts to?”

[023-216] I looked at him and shook my head.

[023-217] “What,” said Richard, in a tone of perfect conviction, “but the

[023-218] army!”

[023-219] “The army?” said I.

[023-220] “The army, of course. What I have to do is to get a commission;

[023-221] and—there I am, you know!” said Richard.

[023-222] And then he showed me, proved by elaborate calculations in his

[023-223] pocket-book, that supposing he had contracted, say, two hundred

[023-224] pounds of debt in six months out of the army; and that he contracted

[023-225] no debt at all within a corresponding period in the army—as to which

[023-226] he had quite made up his mind; this step must involve a saving of

[023-227] four hundred pounds in a year, or two thousand pounds in five years,

[023-228] which was a considerable sum. And then he spoke so ingenuously and

[023-229] sincerely of the sacrifice he made in withdrawing himself for a time

[023-230] from Ada, and of the earnestness with which he aspired—as in thought

[023-231] he always did, I know full well—to repay her love, and to ensure her

[023-232] happiness, and to conquer what was amiss in himself, and to acquire

[023-233] the very soul of decision, that he made my heart ache keenly, sorely.

[023-234] For, I thought, how would this end, how could this end, when so soon

[023-235] and so surely all his manly qualities were touched by the fatal

[023-236] [[ruin-and-decay|blight]] that ruined everything it rested on!

[023-237] I spoke to Richard with all the earnestness I felt, and all the hope

[023-238] I could not quite feel then, and implored him for Ada’s sake not to

[023-239] put any trust in Chancery. To all I said, Richard readily assented,

[023-240] riding over the court and everything else in his easy way and drawing

[023-241] the brightest pictures of the character he was to settle into—alas,

[023-242] when the grievous suit should loose its hold upon him! We had a long

[023-243] talk, but it always came back to that, in substance.

[023-244] At last we came to Soho Square, where Caddy Jellyby had appointed to

[023-245] wait for me, as a quiet place in the neighbourhood of Newman Street.

[023-246] Caddy was in the garden in the centre and hurried out as soon as I

[023-247] appeared. After a few cheerful words, Richard left us together.

[023-248] “Prince has a pupil over the way, Esther,” said Caddy, “and got the

[023-249] key for us. So if you will walk round and round here with me, we can

[023-250] lock ourselves in and I can tell you comfortably what I wanted to see

[023-251] your dear good face about.”

[023-252] “Very well, my dear,” said I. “Nothing could be better.” So Caddy,

[023-253] after affectionately squeezing the dear good face as she called it,

[023-254] locked the gate, and took my arm, and we began to walk round the

[023-255] garden very cosily.

[023-256] “You see, Esther,” said Caddy, who thoroughly enjoyed a little

[023-257] confidence, “after you spoke to me about its being wrong to marry

[023-258] without Ma’s knowledge, or even to keep Ma long in the dark

[023-259] respecting our engagement—though I don’t believe Ma cares much for

[023-260] me, I must say—I thought it right to mention your opinions to

[023-261] Prince. In the first place because I want to profit by everything you

[023-262] tell me, and in the second place because I have no secrets from

[023-263] Prince.”

[023-264] “I hope he approved, Caddy?”

[023-265] “Oh, my dear! I assure you he would approve of anything you could

[023-266] say. You have no idea what an opinion he has of you!”

[023-267] “Indeed!”

[023-268] “Esther, it’s enough to make anybody but me jealous,” said Caddy,

[023-269] laughing and shaking her head; “but it only makes me joyful, for you

[023-270] are the first friend I ever had, and the best friend I ever can have,

[023-271] and nobody can respect and love you too much to please me.”

[023-272] “Upon my word, Caddy,” said I, “you are in the general conspiracy to

[023-273] keep me in a good humour. Well, my dear?”

[023-274] “Well! I am going to tell you,” replied Caddy, crossing her hands

[023-275] confidentially upon my arm. “So we talked a good deal about it, and

[023-276] so I said to Prince, ‘Prince, as Miss Summerson—’”

[023-277] “I hope you didn’t say ‘Miss Summerson’?”

[023-328] hopeful pupil—a stubborn little girl with a sulky forehead, a deep

[023-338] leisure in the intervals of a light collation, and his dressing-case,

[023-339] brushes, and so forth, all of quite an elegant kind, lay about.

[023-340] “Father, Miss Summerson; Miss Jellyby.”

[023-342] high-shouldered bow. “Permit me!” Handing chairs. “Be seated!”

[023-343] Kissing the tips of his left fingers. “Overjoyed!” Shutting his eyes

[023-344] and rolling. “My little retreat is made a paradise.” Recomposing

[023-345] himself on the sofa like the second gentleman in Europe.

[023-355] of snuff.

[023-359] My dear father, may I beg you to prepare your mind for what I am

[023-363] lunacy! Or what is this?”

[023-365] lady, and we are engaged.”

[023-366] “Engaged!” cried Mr. Turveydrop, reclining on the sofa and shutting

[023-367] out the sight with his hand. “An arrow launched at my brain by my own

[023-368] child!”

[023-369] “We have been engaged for some time, father,” faltered Prince, “and

[023-370] Miss Summerson, hearing of it, advised that we should declare the

[023-371] fact to you and was so very kind as to attend on the present

[023-372] occasion. Miss Jellyby is a young lady who deeply respects you,
