[026-001] Wintry morning, looking with dull eyes and sallow face upon the

[026-002] neighbourhood of Leicester Square, finds its inhabitants unwilling to

[026-003] get out of bed. Many of them are not early risers at the brightest of

[026-004] times, being birds of night who roost when the sun is high and are

[026-005] wide awake and keen for prey when the stars shine out. Behind dingy

[026-006] blind and curtain, in upper story and garret, skulking more or less

[026-007] under false names, false hair, false titles, false jewellery, and

[026-008] false histories, a colony of brigands lie in their first sleep.

[026-009] Gentlemen of the green-baize road who could discourse from personal

[026-010] experience of foreign galleys and home treadmills; spies of strong

[026-011] governments that eternally quake with weakness and miserable fear,

[026-012] broken traitors, cowards, bullies, gamesters, shufflers, swindlers,

[026-013] and false witnesses; some not unmarked by the branding-iron beneath

[026-014] their dirty braid; all with more cruelty in them than was in Nero,

[026-015] and more crime than is in Newgate. For howsoever bad the devil can be

[026-016] in fustian or smock-frock (and he can be very bad in both), he is a

[026-017] more designing, callous, and intolerable devil when he sticks a pin

[026-018] in his shirt-front, calls himself a gentleman, backs a card or

[026-019] colour, plays a game or so of billiards, and knows a little about

[026-020] bills and promissory notes, than in any other form he wears. And in

[026-021] such form Mr. Bucket shall find him, when he will, still pervading

[026-022] the tributary channels of Leicester Square.

[026-023] But the wintry morning wants him not and wakes him not. It wakes Mr.

[026-024] [[george-rouncewell|George]] of the shooting gallery and his familiar. They arise, roll up

[026-025] and stow away their mattresses. Mr. George, having shaved himself

[026-026] before a looking-glass of minute proportions, then marches out,

[026-027] bare-headed and bare-chested, to the pump in the little yard and anon

[026-028] comes back shining with yellow soap, friction, drifting rain, and

[026-029] exceedingly cold water. As he rubs himself upon a large jack-towel,

[026-030] blowing like a military sort of diver just come up, his hair curling

[026-031] tighter and tighter on his sunburnt temples the more he rubs it so

[026-032] that it looks as if it never could be loosened by any less coercive

[026-033] instrument than an iron rake or a curry-comb—as he rubs, and puffs,

[026-034] and polishes, and blows, turning his head from side to side the more

[026-035] conveniently to excoriate his throat, and standing with his body well

[026-036] bent forward to keep the wet from his martial legs, Phil, on his

[026-037] knees lighting a fire, looks round as if it were enough washing for

[026-038] him to see all that done, and sufficient renovation for one day to

[026-039] take in the superfluous health his master throws off.

[026-040] When Mr. George is dry, he goes to work to brush his head with two

[026-041] hard brushes at once, to that unmerciful degree that Phil,

[026-042] shouldering his way round the gallery in the act of sweeping it,

[026-043] winks with sympathy. This chafing over, the ornamental part of Mr.

[026-044] George’s toilet is soon performed. He fills his pipe, lights it, and

[026-045] marches up and down smoking, as his custom is, while Phil, raising a

[026-046] powerful odour of hot rolls and coffee, prepares breakfast. He smokes

[026-047] gravely and marches in slow time. Perhaps this morning’s pipe is

[026-048] devoted to the memory of Gridley in his grave.

[026-049] “And so, Phil,” says George of the shooting gallery after several

[026-050] turns in silence, “you were dreaming of the country last night?”

[026-051] Phil, by the by, said as much in a tone of surprise as he scrambled

[026-052] out of bed.

[026-053] “Yes, guv’ner.”

[026-054] “What was it like?”

[026-055] “I hardly know what it was like, guv’ner,” said Phil, considering.

[026-056] “How did you know it was the country?”

[026-057] “On account of the grass, I think. And the swans upon it,” says Phil

[026-058] after further consideration.

[026-059] “What were the swans doing on the grass?”

[026-060] “They was a-eating of it, I expect,” says Phil.

[026-061] The master resumes his march, and the man resumes his preparation of

[026-062] breakfast. It is not necessarily a lengthened preparation, being

[026-063] limited to the setting forth of very simple breakfast requisites for

[026-064] two and the broiling of a rasher of bacon at the fire in the rusty

[026-065] grate; but as Phil has to sidle round a considerable part of the

[026-066] gallery for every object he wants, and never brings two objects at

[026-067] once, it takes time under the circumstances. At length the breakfast

[026-068] is ready. Phil announcing it, Mr. George knocks the ashes out of his

[026-069] pipe on the hob, stands his pipe itself in the chimney corner, and

[026-070] sits down to the meal. When he has helped himself, Phil follows suit,

[026-071] sitting at the extreme end of the little oblong table and taking his

[026-072] plate on his knees. Either in humility, or to hide his blackened

[026-073] hands, or because it is his natural manner of eating.

[026-074] “The country,” says Mr. George, plying his knife and fork; “why, I

[026-075] suppose you never clapped your eyes on the country, Phil?”

[026-076] “I see the marshes once,” says Phil, contentedly eating his

[026-077] breakfast.

[026-078] “What marshes?”

[026-079] “THE marshes, commander,” returns Phil.

[026-080] “Where are they?”

[026-081] “I don’t know where they are,” says Phil; “but I see ’em, guv’ner.

[026-082] They was flat. And miste.”

[026-083] Governor and commander are interchangeable terms with Phil,

[026-084] expressive of the same respect and deference and applicable to nobody

[026-085] but Mr. George.

[026-086] “I was born in the country, Phil.”

[026-087] “Was you indeed, commander?”

[026-088] “Yes. And bred there.”

[026-089] Phil elevates his one eyebrow, and after respectfully staring at his

[026-090] master to express interest, swallows a great gulp of coffee, still

[026-091] staring at him.

[026-092] “There’s not a bird’s note that I don’t know,” says Mr. George. “Not

[026-093] many an English leaf or berry that I couldn’t name. Not many a tree

[026-094] that I couldn’t climb yet if I was put to it. I was a real country

[026-095] boy, once. My good mother lived in the country.”

[026-096] “She must have been a fine old lady, guv’ner,” Phil observes.

[026-097] “Aye! And not so old either, five and thirty years ago,” says Mr.

[026-098] George. “But I’ll wager that at ninety she would be near as upright

[026-099] as me, and near as broad across the shoulders.”

[026-100] “Did she die at ninety, guv’ner?” inquires Phil.

[026-101] “No. Bosh! Let her rest in peace, God bless her!” says the

[026-102] trooper. “What set me on about country boys, and runaways, and

[026-103] good-for-nothings? You, to be sure! So you never clapped your eyes

[026-104] upon the country—marshes and dreams excepted. Eh?”

[026-105] Phil shakes his head.

[026-106] “Do you want to see it?”

[026-107] “N-no, I don’t know as I do, particular,” says Phil.

[026-108] “The town’s enough for you, eh?”

[026-109] “Why, you see, commander,” says Phil, “I ain’t acquainted with

[026-110] anythink else, and I doubt if I ain’t a-getting too old to take to

[026-111] novelties.”

[026-112] “How old ARE you, Phil?” asks the trooper, pausing as he conveys his

[026-113] smoking saucer to his lips.

[026-114] “I’m something with a eight in it,” says Phil. “It can’t be eighty.

[026-115] Nor yet eighteen. It’s betwixt ’em, somewheres.”

[026-116] Mr. George, slowly putting down his saucer without tasting its

[026-117] contents, is laughingly beginning, “Why, what the deuce, Phil—” when

[026-118] he stops, seeing that Phil is counting on his dirty fingers.

[026-119] “I was just eight,” says Phil, “agreeable to the parish calculation,

[026-120] when I went with the tinker. I was sent on a errand, and I see him

[026-121] a-sittin under a old buildin with a fire all to himself wery

[026-122] comfortable, and he says, ‘Would you like to come along a me, my

[026-123] man?’ I says ‘Yes,’ and him and me and the fire goes home to

[026-124] Clerkenwell together. That was April Fool Day. I was able to count up

[026-125] to ten; and when April Fool Day come round again, I says to myself,

[026-126] ‘Now, old chap, you’re one and a eight in it.’ April Fool Day after

[026-127] that, I says, ‘Now, old chap, you’re two and a eight in it.’ In

[026-128] course of time, I come to ten and a eight in it; two tens and a eight

[026-129] in it. When it got so high, it got the upper hand of me, but this is

[026-130] how I always know there’s a eight in it.”

[026-131] “Ah!” says Mr. George, resuming his breakfast. “And where’s the

[026-132] tinker?”

[026-133] “Drink put him in the hospital, guv’ner, and the hospital put him—in

[026-134] a glass-case, I HAVE heerd,” Phil replies mysteriously.

[026-135] “By that means you got promotion? Took the business, Phil?”

[026-136] “Yes, commander, I took the business. Such as it was. It wasn’t much

[026-137] of a beat—round Saffron Hill, Hatton Garden, Clerkenwell, Smiffeld,

[026-138] and there—poor neighbourhood, where they uses up the kettles till

[026-139] they’re past mending. Most of the tramping tinkers used to come and

[026-140] lodge at our place; that was the best part of my master’s earnings.

[026-141] But they didn’t come to me. I warn’t like him. He could sing ’em a

[026-142] good song. I couldn’t! He could play ’em a tune on any sort of pot

[026-143] you please, so as it was iron or block tin. I never could do nothing

[026-144] with a pot but mend it or bile it—never had a note of music in me.

[026-145] Besides, I was too ill-looking, and their wives complained of me.”

[026-146] “They were mighty particular. You would pass muster in a crowd,

[026-147] Phil!” says the trooper with a pleasant smile.

[026-148] “No, guv’ner,” returns Phil, shaking his head. “No, I shouldn’t. I

[026-149] was passable enough when I went with the tinker, though nothing to

[026-150] boast of then; but what with blowing the fire with my mouth when I

[026-151] was young, and spileing my complexion, and singeing my hair off, and

[026-152] swallering the smoke, and what with being nat’rally unfort’nate in

[026-153] the way of running against hot metal and marking myself by sich

[026-154] means, and what with having turn-ups with the tinker as I got older,

[026-155] almost whenever he was too far gone in drink—which was almost

[026-156] always—my beauty was queer, wery queer, even at that time. As to

[026-157] since, what with a dozen years in a dark forge where the men was

[026-158] given to larking, and what with being scorched in a accident at a

[026-159] gas-works, and what with being blowed out of winder case-filling at

[026-160] the firework business, I am ugly enough to be made a show on!”

[026-161] Resigning himself to which condition with a perfectly satisfied

[026-162] manner, Phil begs the favour of another cup of coffee. While drinking

[026-163] it, he says, “It was after the case-filling blow-up when I first see

[026-164] you, commander. You remember?”

[026-165] “I remember, Phil. You were walking along in the sun.”

[026-166] “Crawling, guv’ner, again a wall—”

[026-167] “True, Phil—shouldering your way on—”

[026-168] “In a night-cap!” exclaims Phil, excited.

[026-169] “In a night-cap—”

[026-170] “And hobbling with a couple of sticks!” cries Phil, still more

[026-171] excited.

[026-172] “With a couple of sticks. When—”

[026-173] “When you stops, you know,” cries Phil, putting down his cup and

[026-174] saucer and hastily removing his plate from his knees, “and says to

[026-175] me, ‘What, comrade! You have been in the wars!’ I didn’t say much to

[026-176] you, commander, then, for I was took by surprise that a person so

[026-177] strong and healthy and bold as you was should stop to speak to such a

[026-178] limping bag of bones as I was. But you says to me, says you,

[026-179] delivering it out of your chest as hearty as possible, so that it was

[026-180] like a glass of something hot, ‘What accident have you met with? You

[026-181] have been badly hurt. What’s amiss, old boy? Cheer up, and tell us

[026-182] about it!’ Cheer up! I was cheered already! I says as much to you,

[026-183] you says more to me, I says more to you, you says more to me, and

[026-184] here I am, commander! Here I am, commander!” cries Phil, who has

[026-185] started from his chair and unaccountably begun to sidle away. “If a

[026-186] mark’s wanted, or if it will improve the business, let the customers

[026-187] take aim at me. They can’t spoil MY beauty. I’M all right. Come on!

[026-188] If they want a man to box at, let ’em box at me. Let ’em knock me

[026-189] well about the head. I don’t mind. If they want a light-weight to be

[026-190] throwed for practice, Cornwall, Devonshire, or Lancashire, let ’em

[026-191] throw me. They won’t hurt ME. I have been throwed, all sorts of

[026-192] styles, all my life!”

[026-193] With this unexpected speech, energetically delivered and accompanied

[026-194] by action illustrative of the various exercises referred to, Phil

[026-195] Squod shoulders his way round three sides of the gallery, and

[026-196] abruptly tacking off at his commander, makes a butt at him with his

[026-197] head, intended to express devotion to his service. He then begins to

[026-198] clear away the breakfast.

[026-199] Mr. George, after laughing cheerfully and clapping him on the

[026-200] shoulder, assists in these arrangements and helps to get the gallery

[026-201] into business order. That done, he takes a turn at the dumb-bells,

[026-202] and afterwards weighing himself and opining that he is getting “too

[026-203] fleshy,” engages with great gravity in solitary broadsword practice.

[026-204] Meanwhile Phil has fallen to work at his usual table, where he screws

[026-205] and unscrews, and cleans, and files, and whistles into small

[026-206] apertures, and blackens himself more and more, and seems to do and

[026-207] undo everything that can be done and undone about a gun.

[026-208] Master and man are at length disturbed by footsteps in the passage,

[026-209] where they make an unusual sound, denoting the arrival of unusual

[026-210] company. These steps, advancing nearer and nearer to the gallery,

[026-211] bring into it a group at first sight scarcely reconcilable with any

[026-212] day in the year but the fifth of November.

[026-213] It consists of a limp and ugly figure carried in a chair by two

[026-214] bearers and attended by a lean female with a face like a pinched

[026-215] mask, who might be expected immediately to recite the popular verses

[026-216] commemorative of the time when they did contrive to blow Old England

[026-217] up alive but for her keeping her lips tightly and defiantly closed as

[026-218] the chair is put down. At which point the figure in it gasping, “O

[026-219] Lord! Oh, dear me! I am shaken!” adds, “How de do, my dear friend,

[026-220] how de do?” Mr. George then descries, in the procession, the

[026-221] venerable Mr. Smallweed out for an airing, attended by his

[026-222] granddaughter Judy as body-guard.

[026-223] “Mr. George, my dear friend,” says Grandfather Smallweed, removing

[026-224] his right arm from the neck of one of his bearers, whom he has nearly

[026-225] throttled coming along, “how de do? You’re surprised to see me, my

[026-226] dear friend.”

[026-227] “I should hardly have been more surprised to have seen your friend in

[026-228] the city,” returns Mr. George.

[026-229] “I am very seldom out,” pants Mr. Smallweed. “I haven’t been out for

[026-230] many months. It’s inconvenient—and it comes expensive. But I longed

[026-231] so much to see you, my dear Mr. George. How de do, sir?”

[026-232] “I am well enough,” says Mr. George. “I hope you are the same.”

[026-233] “You can’t be too well, my dear friend.” Mr. Smallweed takes him by

[026-234] both hands. “I have brought my granddaughter Judy. I couldn’t keep

[026-235] her away. She longed so much to see you.”

[026-236] “Hum! She bears it calmly!” mutters Mr. George.

[026-237] “So we got a hackney-cab, and put a chair in it, and just round the

[026-238] corner they lifted me out of the cab and into the chair, and carried

[026-239] me here that I might see my dear friend in his own establishment!

[026-240] This,” says Grandfather Smallweed, alluding to the bearer, who has

[026-241] been in danger of strangulation and who withdraws adjusting his

[026-242] windpipe, “is the driver of the cab. He has nothing extra. It is by

[026-243] agreement included in his fare. This person,” the other bearer, “we

[026-244] engaged in the street outside for a pint of beer. Which is twopence.

[026-245] Judy, give the person twopence. I was not sure you had a workman of

[026-246] your own here, my dear friend, or we needn’t have employed this

[026-247] person.”

[026-248] Grandfather Smallweed refers to Phil with a glance of considerable

[026-249] terror and a half-subdued “O Lord! Oh, dear me!” Nor in his

[026-250] apprehension, on the surface of things, without some reason, for

[026-251] Phil, who has never beheld the apparition in the black-velvet cap

[026-252] before, has stopped short with a gun in his hand with much of the air

[026-253] of a dead shot intent on picking Mr. Smallweed off as an ugly old

[026-254] bird of the crow species.

[026-255] “Judy, my child,” says Grandfather Smallweed, “give the person his

[026-256] twopence. It’s a great deal for what he has done.”

[026-257] The person, who is one of those extraordinary specimens of human

[026-258] fungus that spring up spontaneously in the western streets of [[london|London]],

[026-259] ready dressed in an old red jacket, with a “mission” for holding

[026-260] horses and calling coaches, received his twopence with anything but

[026-261] transport, tosses the money into the air, catches it over-handed, and

[026-262] retires.

[026-263] “My dear Mr. George,” says Grandfather Smallweed, “would you be so

[026-264] kind as help to carry me to the fire? I am accustomed to a fire, and

[026-265] I am an old man, and I soon chill. Oh, dear me!”

[026-266] His closing exclamation is jerked out of the venerable gentleman by

[026-267] the suddenness with which Mr. Squod, like a genie, catches him up,

[026-268] chair and all, and deposits him on the hearth-stone.

[026-269] “O Lord!” says Mr. Smallweed, panting. “Oh, dear me! Oh, my stars! My

[026-270] dear friend, your workman is very strong—and very prompt. O Lord, he

[026-271] is very prompt! Judy, draw me back a little. I’m being scorched in

[026-272] the legs,” which indeed is testified to the noses of all present by

[026-273] the smell of his worsted stockings.

[026-274] The gentle Judy, having backed her grandfather a little way from the

[026-275] fire, and having shaken him up as usual, and having released his

[026-276] overshadowed eye from its black-velvet extinguisher, Mr. Smallweed

[026-277] again says, “Oh, dear me! O Lord!” and looking about and meeting Mr.

[026-278] George’s glance, again stretches out both hands.

[026-279] “My dear friend! So happy in this meeting! And this is your

[026-280] establishment? It’s a delightful place. It’s a picture! You never

[026-281] find that anything goes off here accidentally, do you, my dear

[026-282] friend?” adds Grandfather Smallweed, very ill at ease.

[026-283] “No, no. No fear of that.”

[026-284] “And your workman. He—Oh, dear me!—he never lets anything off

[026-285] without meaning it, does he, my dear friend?”

[026-286] “He has never hurt anybody but himself,” says Mr. George, smiling.

[026-287] “But he might, you know. He seems to have hurt himself a good deal,

[026-288] and he might hurt somebody else,” the old gentleman returns. “He

[026-289] mightn’t mean it—or he even might. Mr. George, will you order him to

[026-290] leave his infernal fire-arms alone and go away?”

[026-291] Obedient to a nod from the trooper, Phil retires, empty-handed, to

[026-292] the other end of the gallery. Mr. Smallweed, reassured, falls to

[026-293] rubbing his legs.

[026-294] “And you’re doing well, Mr. George?” he says to the trooper, squarely

[026-295] standing faced about towards him with his broadsword in his hand.

[026-296] “You are prospering, please the Powers?”

[026-297] Mr. George answers with a cool nod, adding, “Go on. You have not come

[026-298] to say that, I know.”

[026-299] “You are so sprightly, Mr. George,” returns the venerable

[026-300] grandfather. “You are such good company.”

[026-301] “Ha ha! Go on!” says Mr. George.

[026-302] “My dear friend! But that sword looks awful gleaming and sharp. It

[026-303] might cut somebody, by accident. It makes me shiver, Mr. George.

[026-304] Curse him!” says the excellent old gentleman apart to Judy as the

[026-306] and might think of paying off old scores in this murdering place. I

[026-309] Mr. George, returning, folds his arms, and looking down at the old

[026-310] man, sliding every moment lower and lower in his chair, says quietly,

[026-311] “Now for it!”

[026-312] “Ho!” cries Mr. Smallweed, rubbing his hands with an artful chuckle.

[026-313] “Yes. Now for it. Now for what, my dear friend?”

[026-314] “For a pipe,” says Mr. George, who with great composure sets his

[026-315] chair in the chimney-corner, takes his pipe from the grate, fills it

[026-316] and lights it, and falls to smoking peacefully.

[026-317] This tends to the discomfiture of Mr. Smallweed, who finds it so

[026-318] difficult to resume his object, whatever it may be, that he becomes

[026-319] exasperated and secretly claws the air with an impotent

[026-323] watery; and, over and above this, as he continues, while he claws, to

[026-325] becomes such a ghastly spectacle, even in the accustomed eyes of

[026-330] distress he utters enforced sounds like a paviour’s rammer.

[026-331] When Judy has by these means set him up again in his chair, with a

[026-332] white face and a frosty nose (but still clawing), she stretches out

[026-333] her weazen forefinger and gives Mr. George one poke in the back. The

[026-334] trooper raising his head, she makes another poke at her esteemed

[026-335] grandfather, and having thus brought them together, stares rigidly at

[026-336] the fire.

[026-337] “Aye, aye! Ho, ho! U—u—u—ugh!” chatters Grandfather Smallweed,

[026-338] swallowing his rage. “My dear friend!” (still clawing).

[026-339] “I tell you what,” says Mr. George. “If you want to converse with me,

[026-340] you must speak out. I am one of the roughs, and I can’t go about and

[026-341] about. I haven’t the art to do it. I am not clever enough. It don’t

[026-342] suit me. When you go winding round and round me,” says the trooper,

[026-343] putting his pipe between his lips again, “damme, if I don’t feel as

[026-344] if I was being smothered!”

[026-345] And he inflates his broad chest to its utmost extent as if to assure

[026-346] himself that he is not smothered yet.

[026-347] “If you have come to give me a friendly call,” continues Mr. George,

[026-348] “I am obliged to you; how are you? If you have come to see whether

[026-349] there’s any [[inheritance-and-property|property]] on the premises, look about you; you are

[026-350] welcome. If you want to out with something, out with it!”

[026-351] The blooming Judy, without removing her gaze from the fire, gives her

[026-352] grandfather one ghostly poke.

[026-353] “You see! It’s her opinion too. And why the devil that young woman

[026-354] won’t sit down like a Christian,” says Mr. George with his eyes

[026-355] musingly fixed on Judy, “I can’t comprehend.”

[026-356] “She keeps at my side to attend to me, sir,” says Grandfather

[026-357] Smallweed. “I am an old man, my dear Mr. George, and I need some

[026-358] attention. I can carry my years; I am not a brimstone poll-parrot”

[026-359] (snarling and looking unconsciously for the cushion), “but I need

[026-360] attention, my dear friend.”

[026-361] “Well!” returns the trooper, wheeling his chair to face the old man.

[026-362] “Now then?”

[026-363] “My friend in the city, Mr. George, has done a little business with a

[026-364] pupil of yours.”

[026-365] “Has he?” says Mr. George. “I am sorry to hear it.”

[026-366] “Yes, sir.” Grandfather Smallweed rubs his legs. “He is a fine young

[026-367] soldier now, Mr. George, by the name of Carstone. Friends came
