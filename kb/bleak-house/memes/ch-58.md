[058-001] Still impassive, as behoves its breeding, the Dedlock town house

[058-002] carries itself as usual towards the street of dismal grandeur. There

[058-003] are powdered heads from time to time in the little windows of the

[058-004] hall, looking out at the untaxed powder falling all day from the sky;

[058-005] and in the same conservatory there is peach blossom turning itself

[058-006] exotically to the great hall fire from the nipping weather out of

[058-007] doors. It is given out that my Lady has gone down into Lincolnshire,

[058-008] but is expected to return presently.

[058-009] Rumour, busy overmuch, however, will not go down into Lincolnshire.

[058-010] It persists in flitting and chattering about town. It knows that that

[058-011] poor unfortunate man, [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester]], has been sadly used. It hears,

[058-012] my dear child, all sorts of shocking things. It makes the world of

[058-013] five miles round quite merry. Not to know that there is something

[058-014] wrong at the Dedlocks’ is to augur yourself unknown. One of the

[058-015] peachy-cheeked charmers with the skeleton throats is already apprised

[058-016] of all the principal circumstances that will come out before the

[058-017] Lords on Sir Leicester’s application for a bill of divorce.

[058-018] At Blaze and Sparkle’s the jewellers and at Sheen and Gloss’s the

[058-019] mercers, it is and will be for several hours the topic of the age,

[058-020] the feature of the century. The patronesses of those establishments,

[058-021] albeit so loftily inscrutable, being as nicely weighed and measured

[058-022] there as any other article of the stock-in-trade, are perfectly

[058-023] understood in this new fashion by the rawest hand behind the counter.

[058-024] “Our people, Mr. Jones,” said Blaze and Sparkle to the hand in

[058-025] question on engaging him, “our people, sir, are sheep—mere sheep.

[058-026] Where two or three marked ones go, all the rest follow. Keep those

[058-027] two or three in your eye, Mr. Jones, and you have the flock.” So,

[058-028] likewise, Sheen and Gloss to THEIR Jones, in reference to knowing

[058-029] where to have the fashionable people and how to bring what they

[058-030] (Sheen and Gloss) choose into fashion. On similar unerring

[058-031] principles, Mr. Sladdery the librarian, and indeed the great farmer

[058-032] of gorgeous sheep, admits this very day, “Why yes, sir, there

[058-033] certainly ARE reports concerning [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]], very current indeed

[058-034] among my high connexion, sir. You see, my high connexion must talk

[058-035] about something, sir; and it’s only to get a subject into vogue with

[058-036] one or two ladies I could name to make it go down with the whole.

[058-037] Just what I should have done with those ladies, sir, in the case of

[058-038] any novelty you had left to me to bring in, they have done of

[058-039] themselves in this case through knowing Lady Dedlock and being

[058-040] perhaps a little innocently jealous of her too, sir. You’ll find,

[058-041] sir, that this topic will be very popular among my high connexion. If

[058-042] it had been a speculation, sir, it would have brought money. And when

[058-043] I say so, you may trust to my being right, sir, for I have made it my

[058-044] business to study my high connexion and to be able to wind it up like

[058-045] a clock, sir.”

[058-046] Thus rumour thrives in the capital, and will not go down into

[058-047] Lincolnshire. By half-past five, post meridian, Horse Guards’ time,

[058-048] it has even elicited a new remark from the Honourable Mr. Stables,

[058-049] which bids fair to outshine the old one, on which he has so long

[058-050] rested his colloquial reputation. This sparkling sally is to the

[058-051] effect that although he always knew she was the best-groomed woman in

[058-052] the stud, he had no idea she was a bolter. It is immensely received

[058-053] in turf-circles.

[058-054] At feasts and festivals also, in firmaments she has often graced, and

[058-055] among constellations she outshone but yesterday, she is still the

[058-056] prevalent subject. What is it? Who is it? When was it? Where was it?

[058-057] How was it? She is discussed by her dear friends with all the

[058-058] genteelest slang in vogue, with the last new word, the last new

[058-059] manner, the last new drawl, and the perfection of polite

[058-060] indifference. A remarkable feature of the theme is that it is found

[058-061] to be so inspiring that several people come out upon it who never

[058-062] came out before—positively say things! William Buffy carries one of

[058-063] these smartnesses from the place where he dines down to the House,

[058-064] where the Whip for his party hands it about with his snuff-box to

[058-065] keep men together who want to be off, with such effect that the

[058-066] Speaker (who has had it privately insinuated into his own ear under

[058-067] the corner of his wig) cries, “Order at the bar!” three times without

[058-068] making an impression.

[058-069] And not the least amazing circumstance connected with her being

[058-070] vaguely the town talk is that people hovering on the confines of Mr.

[058-071] Sladdery’s high connexion, people who know nothing and ever did know

[058-072] nothing about her, think it essential to their reputation to pretend

[058-073] that she is their topic too, and to retail her at second-hand with

[058-074] the last new word and the last new manner, and the last new drawl,

[058-075] and the last new polite indifference, and all the rest of it, all at

[058-076] second-hand but considered equal to new in inferior systems and to

[058-077] fainter stars. If there be any man of [[document-and-letter|letters]], art, or science among

[058-078] these little dealers, how noble in him to support the feeble sisters

[058-079] on such majestic crutches!

[058-080] So goes the wintry day outside the Dedlock mansion. How within it?

[058-081] Sir Leicester, lying in his bed, can speak a little, though with

[058-082] difficulty and indistinctness. He is enjoined to silence and to rest,

[058-083] and they have given him some opiate to lull his pain, for his old

[058-084] enemy is very hard with him. He is never asleep, though sometimes he

[058-085] seems to fall into a dull waking doze. He caused his bedstead to be

[058-086] moved out nearer to the window when he heard it was such inclement

[058-087] weather, and his head to be so adjusted that he could see the driving

[058-088] snow and sleet. He watches it as it falls, throughout the whole

[058-089] wintry day.

[058-090] Upon the least noise in the house, which is kept hushed, his hand is

[058-091] at the pencil. The old housekeeper, sitting by him, knows what he

[058-092] would write and whispers, “No, he has not come back yet, Sir

[058-093] Leicester. It was late last night when he went. He has been but a

[058-094] little time gone yet.”

[058-095] He withdraws his hand and falls to looking at the sleet and snow

[058-096] again until they seem, by being long looked at, to fall so thick and

[058-097] fast that he is obliged to close his eyes for a minute on the giddy

[058-098] whirl of white flakes and icy blots.

[058-099] He began to look at them as soon as it was light. The day is not yet

[058-100] far spent when he conceives it to be necessary that her rooms should

[058-101] be prepared for her. It is very cold and wet. Let there be good

[058-102] fires. Let them know that she is expected. Please see to it yourself.

[058-103] He writes to this purpose on his slate, and Mrs. Rouncewell with a

[058-104] heavy heart obeys.

[058-105] “For I dread, [[george-rouncewell|George]],” the old lady says to her son, who waits below

[058-106] to keep her company when she has a little leisure, “I dread, my dear,

[058-107] that my Lady will never more set foot within these walls.”

[058-108] “That’s a bad presentiment, mother.”

[058-109] “Nor yet within the walls of [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]], my dear.”

[058-110] “That’s worse. But why, mother?”

[058-111] “When I saw my Lady yesterday, George, she looked to me—and I may

[058-112] say at me too—as if the step on the Ghost’s Walk had almost walked

[058-113] her down.”

[058-114] “Come, come! You alarm yourself with old-story fears, mother.”

[058-115] “No I don’t, my dear. No I don’t. It’s going on for sixty year that I

[058-116] have been in this family, and I never had any fears for it before.

[058-117] But it’s breaking up, my dear; the great old Dedlock family is

[058-118] breaking up.”

[058-119] “I hope not, mother.”

[058-120] “I am thankful I have lived long enough to be with Sir Leicester in

[058-121] this illness and trouble, for I know I am not too old nor too useless

[058-122] to be a welcomer sight to him than anybody else in my place would be.

[058-123] But the step on the Ghost’s Walk will walk my Lady down, George; it

[058-124] has been many a day behind her, and now it will pass her and go on.”

[058-125] “Well, mother dear, I say again, I hope not.”

[058-126] “Ah, so do I, George,” the old lady returns, shaking her head and

[058-127] parting her folded hands. “But if my fears come true, and he has to

[058-128] know it, who will tell him!”

[058-129] “Are these her rooms?”

[058-130] “These are my Lady’s rooms, just as she left them.”

[058-131] “Why, now,” says the trooper, glancing round him and speaking in a

[058-132] lower voice, “I begin to understand how you come to think as you do

[058-133] think, mother. Rooms get an awful look about them when they are

[058-134] fitted up, like these, for one person you are used to see in them,

[058-135] and that person is away under any shadow, let alone being God knows

[058-136] where.”

[058-137] He is not far out. As all partings foreshadow the great final one,

[058-138] so, empty rooms, bereft of a familiar presence, mournfully whisper

[058-139] what your room and what mine must one day be. My Lady’s state has a

[058-140] hollow look, thus gloomy and abandoned; and in the inner apartment,

[058-141] where Mr. Bucket last night made his secret perquisition, the traces

[058-142] of her dresses and her ornaments, even the mirrors accustomed to

[058-143] reflect them when they were a portion of herself, have a desolate and

[058-144] vacant air. Dark and cold as the wintry day is, it is darker and

[058-145] colder in these deserted chambers than in many a hut that will barely

[058-146] exclude the weather; and though the servants heap fires in the grates

[058-147] and set the couches and the chairs within the warm glass screens that

[058-148] let their ruddy light shoot through to the furthest corners, there is

[058-149] a heavy cloud upon the rooms which no light will dispel.

[058-150] The old housekeeper and her son remain until the preparations are

[058-151] complete, and then she returns upstairs. Volumnia has taken Mrs.

[058-152] Rouncewell’s place in the meantime, though pearl necklaces and rouge

[058-153] pots, however calculated to embellish Bath, are but indifferent

[058-154] comforts to the invalid under present circumstances. Volumnia, not

[058-155] being supposed to know (and indeed not knowing) what is the matter,

[058-156] has found it a ticklish task to offer appropriate observations and

[058-159] her kinsman’s eyes, and one exasperating whisper to herself of, “He

[058-160] is asleep.” In disproof of which superfluous remark Sir Leicester has

[058-161] indignantly written on the slate, “I am not.”

[058-163] housekeeper, Volumnia sits at a table a little removed,

[058-165] listens for the returning steps that he expects. In the ears of his

[058-166] old servant, looking as if she had stepped out of an old

[058-167] picture-frame to attend a summoned Dedlock to another world, the

[058-169] He has been under his valet’s hands this morning to be made

[058-170] presentable and is as well got up as the circumstances will allow. He

[058-171] is propped with pillows, his grey hair is brushed in its usual

[058-172] manner, his linen is arranged to a nicety, and he is wrapped in a

[058-173] responsible dressing-gown. His eye-glass and his watch are ready to

[058-174] his hand. It is necessary—less to his own dignity now perhaps than

[058-175] for her sake—that he should be seen as little disturbed and as much

[058-176] himself as may be. Women will talk, and Volumnia, though a Dedlock,

[058-177] is no exceptional case. He keeps her here, there is little doubt, to

[058-178] prevent her talking somewhere else. He is very ill, but he makes his

[058-179] present stand against distress of mind and body most courageously.

[058-183] undisguisable yawns. Finding it impossible to suppress those yawns by

[058-184] any other process than conversation, she compliments Mrs. Rouncewell

[058-185] on her son, declaring that he positively is one of the finest figures

[058-186] she ever saw and as soldierly a looking person, she should think, as

[058-187] what’s his name, her favourite Life Guardsman—the man she dotes on,

[058-188] the dearest of creatures—who was killed at Waterloo.

[058-189] Sir Leicester hears this tribute with so much surprise and stares

[058-190] about him in such a confused way that Mrs. Rouncewell feels it

[058-191] necessary to explain.

[058-192] “Miss Dedlock don’t speak of my eldest son, Sir Leicester, but my

[058-193] youngest. I have found him. He has come home.”

[058-194] Sir Leicester breaks silence with a harsh cry. “George? Your son

[058-195] George come home, Mrs. Rouncewell?”

[058-196] The old housekeeper wipes her eyes. “Thank God. Yes, Sir Leicester.”

[058-197] Does this discovery of some one lost, this return of some one so long

[058-198] gone, come upon him as a strong confirmation of his hopes? Does he

[058-199] think, “Shall I not, with the aid I have, recall her safely after

[058-200] this, there being fewer hours in her case than there are years in

[058-201] his?”

[058-202] It is of no use entreating him; he is determined to speak now, and he

[058-203] does. In a thick crowd of sounds, but still intelligibly enough to be

[058-204] understood.

[058-205] “Why did you not tell me, Mrs. Rouncewell?”

[058-206] “It happened only yesterday, Sir Leicester, and I doubted your being

[058-207] well enough to be talked to of such things.”

[058-214] Mrs. Rouncewell, not a little alarmed by his disregard of the

[058-216] “Where in London?”

[058-217] Mrs. Rouncewell is constrained to admit that he is in the house.

[058-218] “Bring him here to my room. Bring him directly.”
