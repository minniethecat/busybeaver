[035-001] I lay ill through several weeks, and the usual tenor of my life

[035-002] became like an old remembrance. But this was not the effect of time

[035-003] so much as of the change in all my habits made by the helplessness

[035-004] and inaction of a sick-room. Before I had been confined to it many

[035-005] days, everything else seemed to have retired into a remote distance

[035-006] where there was little or no separation between the various stages of

[035-007] my life which had been really divided by years. In falling ill, I

[035-008] seemed to have crossed a dark lake and to have left all my

[035-009] experiences, mingled together by the great distance, on the healthy

[035-010] shore.

[035-011] My housekeeping duties, though at first it caused me great anxiety to

[035-012] think that they were unperformed, were soon as far off as the oldest

[035-013] of the old duties at Greenleaf or the summer afternoons when I went

[035-014] home from school with my portfolio under my arm, and my childish

[035-015] shadow at my side, to my godmother’s house. I had never known before

[035-016] how short life really was and into how small a space the mind could

[035-017] put it.

[035-018] While I was very ill, the way in which these divisions of time became

[035-019] confused with one another distressed my mind exceedingly. At once a

[035-020] child, an elder girl, and the little woman I had been so happy as, I

[035-021] was not only oppressed by cares and difficulties adapted to each

[035-022] station, but by the great perplexity of endlessly trying to reconcile

[035-023] them. I suppose that few who have not been in such a condition can

[035-024] quite understand what I mean or what painful unrest arose from this

[035-025] source.

[035-026] For the same reason I am almost afraid to hint at that time in my

[035-027] disorder—it seemed one long night, but I believe there were both

[035-028] nights and days in it—when I laboured up colossal staircases, ever

[035-029] striving to reach the top, and ever turned, as I have seen a worm in

[035-030] a garden path, by some obstruction, and labouring again. I knew

[035-031] perfectly at intervals, and I think vaguely at most times, that I was

[035-032] in my bed; and I talked with Charley, and felt her touch, and knew

[035-033] her very well; yet I would find myself complaining, “Oh, more of

[035-034] these never-ending stairs, Charley—more and more—piled up to the

[035-035] sky’, I think!” and labouring on again.

[035-036] Dare I hint at that worse time when, strung together somewhere in

[035-037] great black space, there was a flaming necklace, or ring, or starry

[035-038] circle of some kind, of which I was one of the beads! And when my

[035-039] only prayer was to be taken off from the rest and when it was such

[035-040] inexplicable agony and misery to be a part of the dreadful thing?

[035-041] Perhaps the less I say of these sick experiences, the less tedious

[035-042] and the more intelligible I shall be. I do not recall them to make

[035-043] others unhappy or because I am now the least unhappy in remembering

[035-044] them. It may be that if we knew more of such strange afflictions we

[035-045] might be the better able to alleviate their intensity.

[035-046] The repose that succeeded, the long delicious sleep, the blissful

[035-047] rest, when in my weakness I was too calm to have any care for myself

[035-048] and could have heard (or so I think now) that I was dying, with no

[035-049] other emotion than with a pitying love for those I left behind—this

[035-050] state can be perhaps more widely understood. I was in this state when

[035-051] I first shrunk from the light as it twinkled on me once more, and

[035-052] knew with a boundless joy for which no words are rapturous enough

[035-053] that I should see again.

[035-054] I had heard my [[ada-clare|Ada]] crying at the door, day and night; I had heard her

[035-055] calling to me that I was cruel and did not love her; I had heard her

[035-056] praying and imploring to be let in to nurse and comfort me and to

[035-057] leave my bedside no more; but I had only said, when I could speak,

[035-058] “Never, my sweet girl, never!” and I had over and over again reminded

[035-059] Charley that she was to keep my darling from the room whether I lived

[035-060] or died. Charley had been true to me in that time of need, and with

[035-061] her little hand and her great heart had kept the door fast.

[035-062] But now, my sight strengthening and the glorious light coming every

[035-063] day more fully and brightly on me, I could read the [[document-and-letter|letters]] that my

[035-064] dear wrote to me every morning and evening and could put them to my

[035-065] lips and lay my cheek upon them with no fear of hurting her. I could

[035-066] see my little maid, so tender and so careful, going about the two

[035-067] rooms setting everything in order and speaking cheerfully to Ada from

[035-068] the open window again. I could understand the stillness in the house

[035-069] and the thoughtfulness it expressed on the part of all those who had

[035-070] always been so good to me. I could weep in the exquisite felicity of

[035-071] my heart and be as happy in my weakness as ever I had been in my

[035-072] strength.

[035-073] By and by my strength began to be restored. Instead of lying, with so

[035-074] strange a calmness, watching what was done for me, as if it were done

[035-075] for some one else whom I was quietly sorry for, I helped it a little,

[035-076] and so on to a little more and much more, until I became useful to

[035-077] myself, and interested, and attached to life again.

[035-078] How well I remember the pleasant afternoon when I was raised in bed

[035-079] with pillows for the first time to enjoy a great tea-drinking with

[035-080] Charley! The little creature—sent into the world, surely, to

[035-081] minister to the weak and sick—was so happy, and so busy, and stopped

[035-082] so often in her preparations to lay her head upon my bosom, and

[035-083] fondle me, and cry with joyful tears she was so glad, she was so

[035-084] glad, that I was obliged to say, “Charley, if you go on in this way,

[035-085] I must lie down again, my darling, for I am weaker than I thought I

[035-086] was!” So Charley became as quiet as a mouse and took her bright face

[035-087] here and there across and across the two rooms, out of the shade into

[035-088] the divine sunshine, and out of the sunshine into the shade, while I

[035-089] watched her peacefully. When all her preparations were concluded and

[035-090] the pretty tea-table with its little delicacies to tempt me, and its

[035-091] white cloth, and its flowers, and everything so lovingly and

[035-092] beautifully arranged for me by Ada downstairs, was ready at the

[035-093] bedside, I felt sure I was steady enough to say something to Charley

[035-094] that was not new to my thoughts.

[035-095] First I complimented Charley on the room, and indeed it was so fresh

[035-096] and airy, so spotless and neat, that I could scarce believe I had

[035-097] been lying there so long. This delighted Charley, and her face was

[035-098] brighter than before.

[035-099] “Yet, Charley,” said I, looking round, “I miss something, surely,

[035-100] that I am accustomed to?”

[035-101] Poor little Charley looked round too and pretended to shake her head

[035-102] as if there were nothing absent.

[035-103] “Are the pictures all as they used to be?” I asked her.

[035-104] “Every one of them, miss,” said Charley.

[035-105] “And the furniture, Charley?”

[035-106] “Except where I have moved it about to make more room, miss.”

[035-107] “And yet,” said I, “I miss some familiar object. Ah, I know what it

[035-108] is, Charley! It’s the looking-glass.”

[035-109] Charley got up from the table, making as if she had forgotten

[035-110] something, and went into the next room; and I heard her sob there.

[035-111] I had thought of this very often. I was now certain of it. I could

[035-112] thank God that it was not a shock to me now. I called Charley back,

[035-113] and when she came—at first pretending to smile, but as she drew

[035-114] nearer to me, looking grieved—I took her in my arms and said, “It

[035-115] matters very little, Charley. I hope I can do without my old face

[035-116] very well.”

[035-117] I was presently so far advanced as to be able to sit up in a great

[035-118] chair and even giddily to walk into the adjoining room, leaning on

[035-119] Charley. The mirror was gone from its usual place in that room too,

[035-120] but what I had to bear was none the harder to bear for that.

[035-121] My [[john-jarndyce|guardian]] had throughout been earnest to visit me, and there was

[035-122] now no good reason why I should deny myself that happiness. He came

[035-123] one morning, and when he first came in, could only hold me in his

[035-124] embrace and say, “My dear, dear girl!” I had long known—who could

[035-125] know better?—what a deep fountain of affection and generosity his

[035-126] heart was; and was it not worth my trivial suffering and change to

[035-127] fill such a place in it? “Oh, yes!” I thought. “He has seen me, and

[035-128] he loves me better than he did; he has seen me and is even fonder of

[035-129] me than he was before; and what have I to mourn for!”

[035-130] He sat down by me on the sofa, supporting me with his arm. For a

[035-131] little while he sat with his hand over his face, but when he removed

[035-132] it, fell into his usual manner. There never can have been, there

[035-133] never can be, a pleasanter manner.

[035-134] “My little woman,” said he, “what a sad time this has been. Such an

[035-135] inflexible little woman, too, through all!”

[035-136] “Only for the best, guardian,” said I.

[035-137] “For the best?” he repeated tenderly. “Of course, for the best. But

[035-138] here have Ada and I been perfectly forlorn and miserable; here has

[035-139] your friend [[caddy-jellyby|Caddy]] been coming and going late and early; here has

[035-140] every one about the house been utterly lost and dejected; here has

[035-141] even poor [[richard-carstone|Rick]] been writing—to ME too—in his anxiety for you!”

[035-142] I had read of Caddy in Ada’s letters, but not of Richard. I told him

[035-143] so.

[035-144] “Why, no, my dear,” he replied. “I have thought it better not to

[035-145] mention it to her.”

[035-146] “And you speak of his writing to YOU,” said I, repeating his

[035-147] emphasis. “As if it were not natural for him to do so, guardian; as

[035-148] if he could write to a better friend!”

[035-149] “He thinks he could, my love,” returned my guardian, “and to many a

[035-150] better. The truth is, he wrote to me under a sort of protest while

[035-154] Jarndyce has warped him out of himself and perverted me in his eyes.

[035-155] I have known it do as bad deeds, and worse, many a time. If two

[035-156] angels could be concerned in it, I believe it would change their

[035-157] nature.”

[035-158] “It has not changed yours, guardian.”

[035-172] a pyramid, in memory of Chancery’s transcendent wickedness.”

[035-173] “IS it possible, guardian,” I asked, amazed, “that Richard can be

[035-174] suspicious of you?”

[035-175] “Ah, my love, my love,” he said, “it is in the subtle poison of such

[035-176] abuses to breed such diseases. His blood is infected, and objects

[035-177] lose their natural aspects in his sight. It is not HIS fault.”

[035-178] “But it is a terrible misfortune, guardian.”

[035-179] “It is a terrible misfortune, little woman, to be ever drawn within

[035-181] little and little he has been induced to trust in that rotten reed,

[035-183] around him. But again I say with all my soul, we must be patient with

[035-186] I could not help expressing something of my wonder and regret that

[035-187] his benevolent, disinterested intentions had prospered so little.

[035-190] young creatures might be friends instead of distrustful foes and that

[035-191] we might so far counter-act the suit and prove too strong for it. But

[035-192] it was too much to expect. Jarndyce and Jarndyce was the curtain of

[035-193] Rick’s cradle.”

[035-194] “But, guardian, may we not hope that a little experience will teach

[035-195] him what a false and wretched thing it is?”

[035-196] “We WILL hope so, my Esther,” said Mr. Jarndyce, “and that it may not

[035-198] are not many grown and matured men living while we speak, good men

[035-210] He had supported me, as at first, all this time, and his tenderness

[035-211] was so precious to me that I leaned my head upon his shoulder and

[035-212] loved him as if he had been my father. I resolved in my own mind in

[035-213] this little pause, by some means, to see Richard when I grew strong

[035-215] “There are better subjects than these,” said my guardian, “for such a

[035-218] When shall Ada come to see you, my love?”

[035-219] I had been thinking of that too. A little in connexion with the

[035-220] absent mirrors, but not much, for I knew my loving girl would be

[035-221] changed by no change in my looks.

[035-222] “Dear guardian,” said I, “as I have shut her out so long—though

[035-223] indeed, indeed, she is like the light to me—”

[035-224] “I know it well, Dame Durden, well.”

[035-225] He was so good, his touch expressed such endearing compassion and

[035-230] “I think I should like to have my own way a little longer, guardian.

[035-231] It would be best to be away from here before I see her. If Charley

[035-232] and I were to go to some country lodging as soon as I can move, and

[035-233] if I had a week there in which to grow stronger and to be revived by

[035-234] the sweet air and to look forward to the happiness of having Ada with

[035-235] me again, I think it would be better for us.”

[035-236] I hope it was not a poor thing in me to wish to be a little more used

[035-238] ardently to see, but it is the truth. I did. He understood me, I was
