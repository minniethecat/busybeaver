# Social Class

## Overview

*Bleak House* maps the entire class structure of mid-Victorian England, from Sir Leicester Dedlock's ancestral estate at Chesney Wold to Jo the crossing-sweeper's patch of mud at the threshold of Tom-all-Alone's. Dickens' radical claim is that this hierarchy is not natural but constructed — and that it is maintained by the same institutional logic (precedent, property, procedure) that drives Chancery. The novel traces the connections that the class system works to obscure: the way Sir Leicester's comfort depends on Jo's misery, and the way both are entangled in the same legal and economic order.

## In the Novel

The Dedlocks occupy the apex: Sir Leicester, whose baronetcy is "more important than the universe" (002-020), and Lady Dedlock, whose "deadly-lively" boredom is the affect of a class with nothing to do. Below them, the professional classes — lawyers (Tulkinghorn, Vholes, Kenge), doctors (Woodcourt), military men (George Rouncewell) — mediate between aristocrats and the poor. The "shabby genteel" (Mr. Skimpole, Harold Skimpole) complicate the ladder with their cultivated irresponsibility.

At the bottom: Jo, the crossing-sweeper, whose ignorance of everything — including his own name and the Lord's Prayer — is not a personal failure but a structural condition. "He is not one of Mrs. Pardiggle's Tockahoopo Indians; he is not one of Mrs. Jellyby's lambs... he is not a genuine foreign-grown savage; he is the ordinary home-made article" (047-015). The savage irony of "home-made" is Dickens at his most politically pointed: Jo is the product of English civilization, not a failure of it.

The novel's intricate plot connections — Esther's illegitimacy linking her to both the Dedlock aristocracy and the Hawdon underclass, the smallpox that travels from Tom-all-Alone's to Esther's bedside — insist that no class is isolated. Disease, like the fog, is democratic.

## Dickens' Treatment

The novel's class critique operates through connection rather than contrast. Rather than simply juxtaposing rich and poor (a standard Victorian trope), Dickens traces the causal chains that bind them. Sir Leicester's gout is treated by the same medical profession that cannot save Jo. The brickmaker's baby dies while Mrs. Pardiggle distributes tracts. The novel's narrative structure — alternating between panoramic satire and intimate confession — is itself a formal argument that the macro and the micro, the systemic and the personal, cannot be separated.

## Related Characters

- **Sir Leicester Dedlock** — Aristocratic patriarch, embodiment of landed power
- **Lady Dedlock** — Boredom as class symptom; secret transgression
- **Jo** — The "home-made savage," product of English civilization
- **Mr. Tulkinghorn** — The lawyer who polices class boundaries
- **Mr. Rouncewell** — The ironmaster, representing a new industrial class
- **Mrs. Pardiggle** — Middle-class charity as class violence
