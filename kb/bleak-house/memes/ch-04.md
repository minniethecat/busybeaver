[004-001] We were to pass the night, Mr. Kenge told us when we arrived in his

[004-002] room, at Mrs. Jellyby’s; and then he turned to me and said he took it

[004-003] for granted I knew who Mrs. Jellyby was.

[004-004] “I really don’t, sir,” I returned. “Perhaps Mr. Carstone—or Miss

[004-005] Clare—”

[004-006] But no, they knew nothing whatever about Mrs. Jellyby. “In-deed! Mrs.

[004-007] Jellyby,” said Mr. Kenge, standing with his back to the fire and

[004-008] casting his eyes over the dusty hearth-rug as if it were Mrs.

[004-009] Jellyby’s biography, “is a lady of very remarkable strength of

[004-010] character who devotes herself entirely to the public. She has devoted

[004-011] herself to an extensive variety of public subjects at various times

[004-012] and is at present (until something else attracts her) devoted to the

[004-013] subject of Africa, with a view to the general cultivation of the

[004-014] coffee berry—AND the natives—and the happy settlement, on the banks

[004-015] of the African rivers, of our superabundant home population. Mr.

[004-016] Jarndyce, who is desirous to aid any work that is considered likely

[004-017] to be a good work and who is much sought after by philanthropists,

[004-018] has, I believe, a very high opinion of Mrs. Jellyby.”

[004-019] Mr. Kenge, adjusting his cravat, then looked at us.

[004-020] “And Mr. Jellyby, sir?” suggested [[richard-carstone|Richard]].

[004-021] “Ah! Mr. Jellyby,” said Mr. Kenge, “is—a—I don’t know that I can

[004-022] describe him to you better than by saying that he is the husband of

[004-023] Mrs. Jellyby.”

[004-024] “A nonentity, sir?” said Richard with a droll look.

[004-025] “I don’t say that,” returned Mr. Kenge gravely. “I can’t say that,

[004-026] indeed, for I know nothing whatever OF Mr. Jellyby. I never, to my

[004-027] knowledge, had the pleasure of seeing Mr. Jellyby. He may be a very

[004-028] superior man, but he is, so to speak, merged—merged—in the more

[004-029] shining qualities of his wife.” Mr. Kenge proceeded to tell us that

[004-030] as the road to [[bleak-house|Bleak House]] would have been very long, dark, and

[004-031] tedious on such an evening, and as we had been travelling already,

[004-032] [[john-jarndyce|Mr. Jarndyce]] had himself proposed this arrangement. A carriage would

[004-033] be at Mrs. Jellyby’s to convey us out of town early in the forenoon

[004-034] of to-morrow.

[004-035] He then rang a little bell, and the young gentleman came in.

[004-036] Addressing him by the name of Guppy, Mr. Kenge inquired whether Miss

[004-037] Summerson’s boxes and the rest of the baggage had been “sent round.”

[004-038] Mr. Guppy said yes, they had been sent round, and a coach was waiting

[004-039] to take us round too as soon as we pleased.

[004-040] “Then it only remains,” said Mr. Kenge, shaking hands with us, “for

[004-041] me to express my lively satisfaction in (good day, Miss Clare!) the

[004-042] arrangement this day concluded and my (GOOD-bye to you, Miss

[004-043] Summerson!) lively hope that it will conduce to the happiness, the

[004-044] (glad to have had the honour of making your acquaintance, Mr.

[004-045] Carstone!) welfare, the advantage in all points of view, of all

[004-046] concerned! Guppy, see the party safely there.”

[004-047] “Where IS ‘there,’ Mr. Guppy?” said Richard as we went downstairs.

[004-048] “No distance,” said Mr. Guppy; “round in Thavies Inn, you know.”

[004-049] “I can’t say I know where it is, for I come from Winchester and am

[004-050] strange in [[london|London]].”

[004-051] “Only round the corner,” said Mr. Guppy. “We just twist up [[chancery-court|Chancery]]

[004-052] Lane, and cut along Holborn, and there we are in four minutes’ time,

[004-053] as near as a toucher. This is about a London particular NOW, ain’t

[004-054] it, miss?” He seemed quite delighted with it on my account.

[004-055] “The [[fog-motif|fog]] is very dense indeed!” said I.

[004-056] “Not that it affects you, though, I’m sure,” said Mr. Guppy, putting

[004-057] up the steps. “On the contrary, it seems to do you good, miss,

[004-058] judging from your appearance.”

[004-059] I knew he meant well in paying me this compliment, so I laughed at

[004-060] myself for blushing at it when he had shut the door and got upon the

[004-061] box; and we all three laughed and chatted about our inexperience and

[004-062] the strangeness of London until we turned up under an archway to our

[004-063] destination—a narrow street of high houses like an oblong cistern to

[004-064] hold the fog. There was a confused little crowd of people,

[004-065] principally children, gathered about the house at which we stopped,

[004-066] which had a tarnished brass plate on the door with the inscription

[004-067] JELLYBY.

[004-068] “Don’t be frightened!” said Mr. Guppy, looking in at the

[004-069] coach-window. “One of the young Jellybys been and got his head

[004-070] through the area railings!”

[004-071] “Oh, poor child,” said I; “let me out, if you please!”

[004-072] “Pray be careful of yourself, miss. The young Jellybys are always up

[004-073] to something,” said Mr. Guppy.

[004-074] I made my way to the poor child, who was one of the dirtiest little

[004-075] unfortunates I ever saw, and found him very hot and frightened and

[004-076] crying loudly, fixed by the neck between two iron railings, while a

[004-077] milkman and a beadle, with the kindest intentions possible, were

[004-078] endeavouring to drag him back by the legs, under a general impression

[004-079] that his skull was compressible by those means. As I found (after

[004-080] pacifying him) that he was a little boy with a naturally large head,

[004-081] I thought that perhaps where his head could go, his body could

[004-082] follow, and mentioned that the best mode of extrication might be to

[004-083] push him forward. This was so favourably received by the milkman and

[004-084] beadle that he would immediately have been pushed into the area if I

[004-085] had not held his pinafore while Richard and Mr. Guppy ran down

[004-086] through the kitchen to catch him when he should be released. At last

[004-087] he was happily got down without any accident, and then he began to

[004-088] beat Mr. Guppy with a hoop-stick in quite a frantic manner.

[004-089] Nobody had appeared belonging to the house except a person in

[004-090] pattens, who had been poking at the child from below with a broom; I

[004-091] don’t know with what object, and I don’t think she did. I therefore

[004-092] supposed that Mrs. Jellyby was not at home, and was quite surprised

[004-093] when the person appeared in the passage without the pattens, and

[004-094] going up to the back room on the first floor before [[ada-clare|Ada]] and me,

[004-095] announced us as, “Them two young ladies, Missis Jellyby!” We passed

[004-096] several more children on the way up, whom it was difficult to avoid

[004-097] treading on in the dark; and as we came into Mrs. Jellyby’s presence,

[004-098] one of the poor little things fell downstairs—down a whole flight

[004-099] (as it sounded to me), with a great noise.

[004-100] Mrs. Jellyby, whose face reflected none of the uneasiness which we

[004-101] could not help showing in our own faces as the dear child’s head

[004-102] recorded its passage with a bump on every stair—Richard afterwards

[004-103] said he counted seven, besides one for the landing—received us with

[004-104] perfect equanimity. She was a pretty, very diminutive, plump woman of

[004-105] from forty to fifty, with handsome eyes, though they had a curious

[004-106] habit of seeming to look a long way off. As if—I am quoting Richard

[004-107] again—they could see nothing nearer than Africa!

[004-108] “I am very glad indeed,” said Mrs. Jellyby in an agreeable voice, “to

[004-109] have the pleasure of receiving you. I have a great respect for Mr.

[004-110] Jarndyce, and no one in whom he is interested can be an object of

[004-111] indifference to me.”

[004-112] We expressed our acknowledgments and sat down behind the door, where

[004-113] there was a lame invalid of a sofa. Mrs. Jellyby had very good hair

[004-114] but was too much occupied with her African duties to brush it. The

[004-115] shawl in which she had been loosely muffled dropped onto her chair

[004-116] when she advanced to us; and as she turned to resume her seat, we

[004-117] could not help noticing that her dress didn’t nearly meet up the back

[004-118] and that the open space was railed across with a lattice-work of

[004-119] stay-lace—like a summer-house.

[004-120] The room, which was strewn with papers and nearly filled by a great

[004-121] [[document-and-letter|writing]]-table covered with similar litter, was, I must say, not only

[004-122] very untidy but very dirty. We were obliged to take notice of that

[004-123] with our sense of sight, even while, with our sense of hearing, we

[004-124] followed the poor child who had tumbled downstairs: I think into the

[004-125] back kitchen, where somebody seemed to stifle him.

[004-126] But what principally struck us was a jaded and unhealthy-looking

[004-127] though by no means plain girl at the writing-table, who sat biting

[004-128] the feather of her pen and staring at us. I suppose nobody ever was

[004-129] in such a state of ink. And from her tumbled hair to her pretty feet,

[004-130] which were disfigured with frayed and broken satin slippers trodden

[004-131] down at heel, she really seemed to have no article of dress upon her,

[004-132] from a pin upwards, that was in its proper condition or its right

[004-133] place.

[004-134] “You find me, my dears,” said Mrs. Jellyby, snuffing the two great

[004-135] office candles in tin candlesticks, which made the room taste

[004-136] strongly of hot tallow (the fire had gone out, and there was nothing

[004-137] in the grate but ashes, a bundle of wood, and a poker), “you find me,

[004-138] my dears, as usual, very busy; but that you will excuse. The African

[004-139] project at present employs my whole time. It involves me in

[004-140] correspondence with public bodies and with private individuals

[004-141] anxious for the welfare of their species all over the country. I am

[004-142] happy to say it is advancing. We hope by this time next year to have

[004-143] from a hundred and fifty to two hundred healthy families cultivating

[004-144] coffee and educating the natives of [[telescopic-philanthropy|Borrioboola-Gha]], on the left bank

[004-145] of the Niger.”

[004-146] As Ada said nothing, but looked at me, I said it must be very

[004-147] gratifying.

[004-148] “It IS gratifying,” said Mrs. Jellyby. “It involves the devotion of

[004-149] all my energies, such as they are; but that is nothing, so that it

[004-150] succeeds; and I am more confident of success every day. Do you know,

[004-151] Miss Summerson, I almost wonder that YOU never turned your thoughts

[004-152] to Africa.”

[004-153] This application of the subject was really so unexpected to me that I

[004-154] was quite at a loss how to receive it. I hinted that the climate—

[004-155] “The finest climate in the world!” said Mrs. Jellyby.

[004-156] “Indeed, ma’am?”

[004-157] “Certainly. With precaution,” said Mrs. Jellyby. “You may go into

[004-158] Holborn, without precaution, and be run over. You may go into

[004-159] Holborn, with precaution, and never be run over. Just so with

[004-160] Africa.”

[004-161] I said, “No doubt.” I meant as to Holborn.

[004-162] “If you would like,” said Mrs. Jellyby, putting a number of papers

[004-163] towards us, “to look over some remarks on that head, and on the

[004-164] general subject, which have been extensively circulated, while I

[004-165] finish a letter I am now dictating to my eldest daughter, who is my

[004-166] amanuensis—”

[004-167] The girl at the table left off biting her pen and made a return to

[004-168] our recognition, which was half bashful and half sulky.

[004-169] “—I shall then have finished for the present,” proceeded Mrs.

[004-170] Jellyby with a sweet smile, “though my work is never done. Where are

[004-171] you, [[caddy-jellyby|Caddy]]?”

[004-172] “‘Presents her compliments to Mr. Swallow, and begs—’” said Caddy.

[004-173] “‘And begs,’” said Mrs. Jellyby, dictating, “‘to inform him, in

[004-174] reference to his letter of inquiry on the African project—’ No,

[004-175] Peepy! Not on my account!”

[004-176] Peepy (so self-named) was the unfortunate child who had fallen

[004-177] downstairs, who now interrupted the correspondence by presenting

[004-178] himself, with a strip of plaster on his forehead, to exhibit his

[004-179] wounded knees, in which Ada and I did not know which to pity

[004-180] most—the bruises or the dirt. Mrs. Jellyby merely added, with the

[004-181] serene composure with which she said everything, “Go along, you

[004-182] naughty Peepy!” and fixed her fine eyes on Africa again.

[004-183] However, as she at once proceeded with her dictation, and as I

[004-184] interrupted nothing by doing it, I ventured quietly to stop poor

[004-185] Peepy as he was going out and to take him up to nurse. He looked very

[004-186] much astonished at it and at Ada’s kissing him, but soon fell fast

[004-187] asleep in my arms, sobbing at longer and longer intervals, until he

[004-188] was quiet. I was so occupied with Peepy that I lost the letter in

[004-189] detail, though I derived such a general impression from it of the

[004-190] momentous importance of Africa, and the utter insignificance of all

[004-191] other places and things, that I felt quite ashamed to have thought so

[004-192] little about it.

[004-193] “Six o’clock!” said Mrs. Jellyby. “And our dinner hour is nominally

[004-194] (for we dine at all hours) five! Caddy, show Miss Clare and Miss

[004-195] Summerson their rooms. You will like to make some change, perhaps?

[004-196] You will excuse me, I know, being so much occupied. Oh, that very bad

[004-197] child! Pray put him down, Miss Summerson!”

[004-198] I begged permission to retain him, truly saying that he was not at

[004-199] all troublesome, and carried him upstairs and laid him on my bed. Ada

[004-200] and I had two upper rooms with a door of communication between. They

[004-201] were excessively bare and disorderly, and the curtain to my window

[004-202] was fastened up with a fork.

[004-203] “You would like some hot water, wouldn’t you?” said Miss Jellyby,

[004-204] looking round for a jug with a handle to it, but looking in vain.

[004-205] “If it is not being troublesome,” said we.

[004-206] “Oh, it’s not the trouble,” returned Miss Jellyby; “the question is,

[004-207] if there IS any.”

[004-209] that I must confess it was a little miserable, and Ada was half

[004-210] crying. We soon laughed, however, and were busily unpacking when Miss

[004-211] Jellyby came back to say that she was sorry there was no hot water,

[004-212] but they couldn’t find the kettle, and the boiler was out of order.

[004-213] We begged her not to mention it and made all the haste we could to

[004-216] bed, and our attention was distracted by the constant apparition of

[004-218] doors. It was impossible to shut the door of either room, for my

[004-219] lock, with no knob to it, looked as if it wanted to be wound up; and

[004-221] smoothness, it was attended with no effect whatever on the door.

[004-224] Riding Hood while I dressed; which they did, and were as quiet as

[004-225] mice, including Peepy, who awoke opportunely before the appearance of

[004-226] the wolf.

[004-228] Wells” on it lighted up in the staircase window with a floating wick,

[004-232] degree, in short, that we all sat coughing and crying with the

[004-236] that he had washed his hands in a pie-dish and that they had found

[004-237] the kettle on his dressing-table, and he made Ada laugh so that they
