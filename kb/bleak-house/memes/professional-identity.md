# Professional Identity

## Overview

*Bleak House* is a novel of professions. Lawyers (Tulkinghorn, Vholes, Kenge, Guppy), doctors (Woodcourt, the medical students), military men (George Rouncewell), industrialists (Mr. Rouncewell the ironmaster), and civil servants populate its world. Dickens uses professional identity as a moral diagnostic: the same profession that corrupts Tulkinghorn into a predator of secrets can elevate Woodcourt into a healer who crosses class boundaries. The novel asks not whether a profession is good but what kind of person it makes.

## In the Novel

The legal profession is the novel's most comprehensively indicted. Tulkinghorn is the lawyer as predator — a "miser" of secrets who "treasured up" knowledge for leverage (036-188). Vholes is the lawyer as parasite, drawing "sustenance out of the young man's very bones" (039). Kenge is the lawyer as bureaucrat, cheerfully processing other people's ruin. Only John Jarndyce, who refuses to practise the law he studied, escapes contamination.

Medicine is the novel's counter-profession. Allan Woodcourt's practice takes him from Tom-all-Alone's to the Dedlock estate; his profession genuinely serves rather than extracts. Mr. Rouncewell the ironmaster represents a third model: industrial production, which creates value rather than redistributing it. The novel's professional taxonomy maps onto its moral taxonomy with unusual clarity.

## Dickens' Treatment

Dickens' treatment of professional identity is not anti-professional but pro-reform. The novel condemns professions that have become self-serving systems (law) and celebrates those that remain oriented toward service (medicine, to a degree). The critical variable is not the profession itself but its relationship to power: law serves power, medicine serves bodies.
