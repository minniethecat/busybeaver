[013-001] We held many consultations about what [[richard-carstone|Richard]] was to be, first

[013-002] without [[john-jarndyce|Mr. Jarndyce]], as he had requested, and afterwards with him,

[013-003] but it was a long time before we seemed to make progress. Richard

[013-004] said he was ready for anything. When Mr. Jarndyce doubted whether he

[013-005] might not already be too old to enter the Navy, Richard said he had

[013-006] thought of that, and perhaps he was. When Mr. Jarndyce asked him what

[013-007] he thought of the Army, Richard said he had thought of that, too, and

[013-008] it wasn’t a bad idea. When Mr. Jarndyce advised him to try and decide

[013-009] within himself whether his old preference for the sea was an ordinary

[013-010] boyish inclination or a strong impulse, Richard answered, Well he

[013-011] really HAD tried very often, and he couldn’t make out.

[013-012] “How much of this indecision of character,” Mr. Jarndyce said to me,

[013-013] “is chargeable on that incomprehensible heap of uncertainty and

[013-014] procrastination on which he has been thrown from his birth, I don’t

[013-015] pretend to say; but that [[chancery-court|Chancery]], among its other sins, is

[013-016] responsible for some of it, I can plainly see. It has engendered or

[013-017] confirmed in him a habit of putting off—and trusting to this, that,

[013-018] and the other chance, without knowing what chance—and dismissing

[013-019] everything as unsettled, uncertain, and confused. The character of

[013-020] much older and steadier people may be even changed by the

[013-021] circumstances surrounding them. It would be too much to expect that a

[013-022] boy’s, in its formation, should be the subject of such influences and

[013-023] escape them.”

[013-024] I felt this to be true; though if I may venture to mention what I

[013-025] thought besides, I thought it much to be regretted that Richard’s

[013-026] education had not counteracted those influences or directed his

[013-027] character. He had been eight years at a public school and had learnt,

[013-028] I understood, to make Latin verses of several sorts in the most

[013-029] admirable manner. But I never heard that it had been anybody’s

[013-030] business to find out what his natural bent was, or where his failings

[013-031] lay, or to adapt any kind of knowledge to HIM. HE had been adapted to

[013-032] the verses and had learnt the art of making them to such perfection

[013-033] that if he had remained at school until he was of age, I suppose he

[013-034] could only have gone on making them over and over again unless he had

[013-035] enlarged his education by forgetting how to do it. Still, although I

[013-036] had no doubt that they were very beautiful, and very improving, and

[013-037] very sufficient for a great many purposes of life, and always

[013-038] remembered all through life, I did doubt whether Richard would not

[013-039] have profited by some one studying him a little, instead of his

[013-040] studying them quite so much.

[013-041] To be sure, I knew nothing of the subject and do not even now know

[013-042] whether the young gentlemen of classic Rome or Greece made verses to

[013-043] the same extent—or whether the young gentlemen of any country ever

[013-044] did.

[013-046] be. Except that I am quite sure I don’t want to go into the Church,

[013-049] “I don’t know that, sir!” replied Richard. “I am fond of boating.

[013-050] Articled clerks go a good deal on the water. It’s a capital

[013-051] [[professional-identity|profession]]!”
