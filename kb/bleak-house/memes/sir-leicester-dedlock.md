# Sir Leicester Dedlock
## Overview
Sir Leicester Dedlock is the novel's portrait of aristocratic decay — a man whose baronetcy is "more important than the universe" but whose world is crumbling. His genuine grief at Lady Dedlock's exposure and death redeems him from mere satirical type: the aristocrat who values honour over property, and who proves capable of love even when everything his class has taught him insists on condemnation.
## Role in the Novel
Sir Leicester's defining moment is his response to Lady Dedlock's flight. Rather than repudiating her, he suffers a stroke while insisting on her innocence: "I desire that my wife should be found" (056). His collapse literalizes the death of the aristocratic order he represents — not with a bang but with a paralysis.
