# Inheritance and Property

## Overview

The literal subject of the Jarndyce lawsuit is inheritance — who gets what when the original testator's intentions have been lost in procedural fog. But *Bleak House* treats inheritance as a problem that extends far beyond property law. What do parents pass to children? The novel's answer is unsettling: Esther inherits her mother's shame, Richard inherits the Chancery suit's corrosive hope, and Sir Leicester inherits a social order he cannot sustain. Inheritance is the novel's master trope for the transmission of damage across generations.

## In the Novel

The Jarndyce will — the document whose meaning has been buried under "tens of thousands of Chancery folio-pages" (001-157) — is the absent centre of the legal plot. Its disappearance is itself the point: the mechanism for transmitting property has become the mechanism for destroying it. By the time the suit is resolved, "the whole estate was found to have been entirely absorbed in costs" (065).

Esther's inheritance is more ambiguous. She receives no property from her mother (Lady Dedlock) or father (Captain Hawdon), but she inherits John Jarndyce's benevolent guardianship — and, eventually, the new Bleak House. This is presented as the novel's only functional inheritance: property given freely, by a living benefactor, outside the machinery of Chancery.

The Rouncewell family provides a counter-narrative: Mr. Rouncewell the ironmaster represents a self-made industrial class that neither inherits nor litigates but produces. His offer to educate George's son represents an alternative model of transmission — skill and industry rather than land and precedent.

## Dickens' Treatment

Dickens' treatment of inheritance is radical in its implications: the traditional mechanism for transmitting wealth and status (the will, the estate, the family name) is shown to be fundamentally broken. The novel does not propose a political alternative — Dickens was no revolutionary — but it insists that the existing system produces only ruin. John Jarndyce's gift of the new Bleak House to Esther is the novel's utopian counter-image: an inheritance that is actually a gift, given in love rather than extracted by law.
