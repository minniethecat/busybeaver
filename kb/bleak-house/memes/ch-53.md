[053-001] Mr. Bucket and his fat forefinger are much in consultation together

[053-002] under existing circumstances. When Mr. Bucket has a matter of this

[053-003] pressing interest under his consideration, the fat forefinger seems

[053-004] to rise, to the dignity of a familiar demon. He puts it to his ears,

[053-005] and it whispers information; he puts it to his lips, and it enjoins

[053-006] him to [[secret-and-shame|secrecy]]; he rubs it over his nose, and it sharpens his scent;

[053-007] he shakes it before a guilty man, and it charms him to his

[053-008] destruction. The Augurs of the Detective Temple invariably predict

[053-009] that when Mr. Bucket and that finger are in much conference, a

[053-010] terrible avenger will be heard of before long.

[053-011] Otherwise mildly studious in his observation of human nature, on the

[053-012] whole a benignant philosopher not disposed to be severe upon the

[053-013] follies of mankind, Mr. Bucket pervades a vast number of houses and

[053-014] strolls about an infinity of streets, to outward appearance rather

[053-015] languishing for want of an object. He is in the friendliest condition

[053-016] towards his species and will drink with most of them. He is free with

[053-017] his money, affable in his manners, innocent in his conversation—but

[053-018] through the placid stream of his life there glides an under-current

[053-019] of forefinger.

[053-020] Time and place cannot bind Mr. Bucket. Like man in the abstract, he

[053-021] is here to-day and gone to-morrow—but, very unlike man indeed, he is

[053-022] here again the next day. This evening he will be casually looking

[053-023] into the iron extinguishers at the door of [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester Dedlock]]’s

[053-024] house in town; and to-morrow morning he will be walking on the leads

[053-025] at [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]], where erst the old man walked whose ghost is

[053-026] propitiated with a hundred guineas. Drawers, desks, pockets, all

[053-027] things belonging to him, Mr. Bucket examines. A few hours afterwards,

[053-028] he and the Roman will be alone together comparing forefingers.

[053-029] It is likely that these occupations are irreconcilable with home

[053-030] enjoyment, but it is certain that Mr. Bucket at present does not go

[053-031] home. Though in general he highly appreciates the society of Mrs.

[053-032] Bucket—a lady of a natural detective genius, which if it had been

[053-033] improved by professional exercise, might have done great things, but

[053-034] which has paused at the level of a clever amateur—he holds himself

[053-035] aloof from that dear solace. Mrs. Bucket is dependent on their lodger

[053-036] (fortunately an amiable lady in whom she takes an interest) for

[053-037] companionship and conversation.

[053-038] A great crowd assembles in Lincoln’s Inn Fields on the day of the

[053-039] funeral. Sir Leicester Dedlock attends the ceremony in person;

[053-040] strictly speaking, there are only three other human followers, that

[053-041] is to say, Lord Doodle, William Buffy, and the debilitated cousin

[053-042] (thrown in as a make-weight), but the amount of inconsolable

[053-043] carriages is immense. The peerage contributes more four-wheeled

[053-044] affliction than has ever been seen in that neighbourhood. Such is the

[053-045] assemblage of armorial bearings on coach panels that the Herald’s

[053-046] College might be supposed to have lost its father and mother at a

[053-047] blow. The Duke of Foodle sends a splendid pile of dust and ashes,

[053-048] with silver wheel-boxes, patent axles, all the last improvements, and

[053-049] three bereaved worms, six feet high, holding on behind, in a bunch of

[053-050] woe. All the state coachmen in [[london|London]] seem plunged into mourning; and

[053-051] if that dead old man of the rusty garb be not beyond a taste in

[053-052] horseflesh (which appears impossible), it must be highly gratified

[053-053] this day.

[053-054] Quiet among the undertakers and the equipages and the calves of so

[053-055] many legs all steeped in grief, Mr. Bucket sits concealed in one of

[053-056] the inconsolable carriages and at his ease surveys the crowd through

[053-057] the lattice blinds. He has a keen eye for a crowd—as for what

[053-058] not?—and looking here and there, now from this side of the carriage,

[053-059] now from the other, now up at the house windows, now along the

[053-060] people’s heads, nothing escapes him.

[053-061] “And there you are, my partner, eh?” says Mr. Bucket to himself,

[053-062] apostrophizing Mrs. Bucket, stationed, by his favour, on the steps of

[053-063] the deceased’s house. “And so you are. And so you are! And very well

[053-064] indeed you are looking, Mrs. Bucket!”

[053-065] The procession has not started yet, but is waiting for the cause of

[053-066] its assemblage to be brought out. Mr. Bucket, in the foremost

[053-067] emblazoned carriage, uses his two fat forefingers to hold the lattice

[053-070] still occupied with Mrs. B. “There you are, my partner, eh?” he

[053-071] murmuringly repeats. “And our lodger with you. I’m taking notice of

[053-072] you, Mrs. Bucket; I hope you’re all right in your health, my dear!”

[053-076] they fly with him on that sudden journey?—and until the procession

[053-084] narrow track of blood which keeps the other in the watchful state

[053-085] expressed in every hair of his head! But it is all one to both;

[053-086] neither is troubled about that.
