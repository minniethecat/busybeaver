[005-001] Although the morning was raw, and although the [[fog-motif|fog]] still seemed

[005-002] heavy—I say seemed, for the windows were so encrusted with dirt that

[005-003] they would have made midsummer sunshine dim—I was sufficiently

[005-004] forewarned of the discomfort within doors at that early hour and

[005-005] sufficiently curious about [[london|London]] to think it a good idea on the part

[005-006] of Miss Jellyby when she proposed that we should go out for a walk.

[005-007] “Ma won’t be down for ever so long,” she said, “and then it’s a

[005-008] chance if breakfast’s ready for an hour afterwards, they dawdle so.

[005-009] As to Pa, he gets what he can and goes to the office. He never has

[005-010] what you would call a regular breakfast. Priscilla leaves him out the

[005-011] loaf and some milk, when there is any, overnight. Sometimes there

[005-012] isn’t any milk, and sometimes the cat drinks it. But I’m afraid you

[005-013] must be tired, Miss Summerson, and perhaps you would rather go to

[005-014] bed.”

[005-015] “I am not at all tired, my dear,” said I, “and would much prefer to

[005-016] go out.”

[005-017] “If you’re sure you would,” returned Miss Jellyby, “I’ll get my

[005-018] things on.”

[005-019] [[ada-clare|Ada]] said she would go too, and was soon astir. I made a proposal to

[005-020] Peepy, in default of being able to do anything better for him, that

[005-021] he should let me wash him and afterwards lay him down on my bed

[005-022] again. To this he submitted with the best grace possible, staring at

[005-023] me during the whole operation as if he never had been, and never

[005-024] could again be, so astonished in his life—looking very miserable

[005-025] also, certainly, but making no complaint, and going snugly to sleep

[005-026] as soon as it was over. At first I was in two minds about taking such

[005-027] a liberty, but I soon reflected that nobody in the house was likely

[005-028] to notice it.

[005-029] What with the bustle of dispatching Peepy and the bustle of getting

[005-030] myself ready and helping Ada, I was soon quite in a glow. We found

[005-031] Miss Jellyby trying to warm herself at the fire in the [[document-and-letter|writing]]-room,

[005-032] which Priscilla was then lighting with a smutty parlour candlestick,

[005-033] throwing the candle in to make it burn better. Everything was just as

[005-034] we had left it last night and was evidently intended to remain so.

[005-035] Below-stairs the dinner-cloth had not been taken away, but had been

[005-036] left ready for breakfast. Crumbs, dust, and waste-paper were all over

[005-037] the house. Some pewter pots and a milk-can hung on the area railings;

[005-038] the door stood open; and we met the cook round the corner coming out

[005-039] of a public-house, wiping her mouth. She mentioned, as she passed us,

[005-040] that she had been to see what o’clock it was.

[005-041] But before we met the cook, we met [[richard-carstone|Richard]], who was dancing up and

[005-042] down Thavies Inn to warm his feet. He was agreeably surprised to see

[005-043] us stirring so soon and said he would gladly share our walk. So he

[005-044] took care of Ada, and Miss Jellyby and I went first. I may mention

[005-045] that Miss Jellyby had relapsed into her sulky manner and that I

[005-046] really should not have thought she liked me much unless she had told

[005-047] me so.

[005-048] “Where would you wish to go?” she asked.

[005-049] “Anywhere, my dear,” I replied.

[005-050] “Anywhere’s nowhere,” said Miss Jellyby, stopping perversely.

[005-051] “Let us go somewhere at any rate,” said I.

[005-052] She then walked me on very fast.

[005-053] “I don’t care!” she said. “Now, you are my witness, Miss Summerson, I

[005-054] say I don’t care—but if he was to come to our house with his great,

[005-055] shining, lumpy forehead night after night till he was as old as

[005-056] Methuselah, I wouldn’t have anything to say to him. Such ASSES as he

[005-057] and Ma make of themselves!”

[005-058] “My dear!” I remonstrated, in allusion to the epithet and the

[005-059] vigorous emphasis Miss Jellyby set upon it. “Your duty as a child—”

[005-060] “Oh! Don’t talk of duty as a child, Miss Summerson; where’s Ma’s duty

[005-061] as a parent? All made over to the public and Africa, I suppose! Then

[005-062] let the public and Africa show duty as a child; it’s much more their

[005-063] affair than mine. You are shocked, I dare say! Very well, so am I

[005-064] shocked too; so we are both shocked, and there’s an end of it!”

[005-065] She walked me on faster yet.

[005-066] “But for all that, I say again, he may come, and come, and come, and

[005-067] I won’t have anything to say to him. I can’t bear him. If there’s any

[005-068] stuff in the world that I hate and detest, it’s the stuff he and Ma

[005-069] talk. I wonder the very paving-stones opposite our house can have the

[005-070] patience to stay there and be a witness of such inconsistencies and

[005-071] contradictions as all that sounding nonsense, and Ma’s management!”

[005-072] I could not but understand her to refer to Mr. Quale, the young

[005-073] gentleman who had appeared after dinner yesterday. I was saved the

[005-074] disagreeable necessity of pursuing the subject by Richard and Ada

[005-075] coming up at a round pace, laughing and asking us if we meant to run

[005-076] a race. Thus interrupted, Miss Jellyby became silent and walked

[005-077] moodily on at my side while I admired the long successions and

[005-078] varieties of streets, the quantity of people already going to and

[005-079] fro, the number of vehicles passing and repassing, the busy

[005-080] preparations in the setting forth of shop windows and the sweeping

[005-081] out of shops, and the extraordinary creatures in rags secretly

[005-082] groping among the swept-out rubbish for pins and other refuse.

[005-083] “So, cousin,” said the cheerful voice of Richard to Ada behind me.

[005-084] “We are never to get out of [[chancery-court|Chancery]]! We have come by another way to

[005-085] our place of meeting yesterday, and—by the Great Seal, here’s the

[005-086] old lady again!”

[005-087] Truly, there she was, immediately in front of us, curtsying, and

[005-088] smiling, and saying with her yesterday’s air of patronage, “The wards

[005-089] in Jarndyce! Ve-ry happy, I am sure!”

[005-090] “You are out early, ma’am,” said I as she curtsied to me.

[005-091] “Ye-es! I usually walk here early. Before the court sits. It’s

[005-092] retired. I collect my thoughts here for the business of the day,”

[005-093] said the old lady mincingly. “The business of the day requires a

[005-094] great deal of thought. Chancery justice is so ve-ry difficult to

[005-095] follow.”

[005-096] “Who’s this, Miss Summerson?” whispered Miss Jellyby, drawing my arm

[005-097] tighter through her own.

[005-098] The little old lady’s hearing was remarkably quick. She answered for

[005-099] herself directly.

[005-100] “A suitor, my child. At your service. I have the honour to attend

[005-101] court regularly. With my documents. Have I the pleasure of addressing

[005-102] another of the youthful parties in Jarndyce?” said the old lady,

[005-103] recovering herself, with her head on one side, from a very low

[005-104] curtsy.

[005-105] Richard, anxious to atone for his thoughtlessness of yesterday,

[005-106] good-naturedly explained that Miss Jellyby was not connected with the

[005-107] suit.

[005-108] “Ha!” said the old lady. “She does not expect a judgment? She will

[005-109] still grow old. But not so old. Oh, dear, no! This is the garden of

[005-110] Lincoln’s Inn. I call it my garden. It is quite a bower in the

[005-111] summer-time. Where the birds sing melodiously. I pass the greater

[005-112] part of the long vacation here. In contemplation. You find the long

[005-113] vacation exceedingly long, don’t you?”

[005-114] We said yes, as she seemed to expect us to say so.

[005-115] “When the leaves are falling from the trees and there are no more

[005-116] flowers in bloom to make up into nosegays for the Lord Chancellor’s

[005-117] court,” said the old lady, “the vacation is fulfilled and the sixth

[005-118] seal, mentioned in the Revelations, again prevails. Pray come and see

[005-119] my lodging. It will be a good omen for me. Youth, and hope, and

[005-120] beauty are very seldom there. It is a long, long time since I had a

[005-121] visit from either.”

[005-122] She had taken my hand, and leading me and Miss Jellyby away, beckoned

[005-123] Richard and Ada to come too. I did not know how to excuse myself and

[005-124] looked to Richard for aid. As he was half amused and half curious and

[005-125] all in doubt how to get rid of the old lady without offence, she

[005-126] continued to lead us away, and he and Ada continued to follow, our

[005-127] strange conductress informing us all the time, with much smiling

[005-128] condescension, that she lived close by.

[005-129] It was quite true, as it soon appeared. She lived so close by that we

[005-130] had not time to have done humouring her for a few moments before she

[005-131] was at home. Slipping us out at a little side gate, the old lady

[005-132] stopped most unexpectedly in a narrow back street, part of some

[005-133] courts and lanes immediately outside the wall of the inn, and said,

[005-134] “This is my lodging. Pray walk up!”

[005-135] She had stopped at a shop over which was written KROOK, RAG AND

[005-136] BOTTLE WAREHOUSE. Also, in long thin letters, KROOK, DEALER IN MARINE

[005-137] STORES. In one part of the window was a picture of a red paper mill

[005-138] at which a cart was unloading a quantity of sacks of old rags. In

[005-139] another was the inscription BONES BOUGHT. In another, KITCHEN-STUFF

[005-140] BOUGHT. In another, OLD IRON BOUGHT. In another, WASTE-PAPER BOUGHT.

[005-141] In another, LADIES’ AND GENTLEMEN’S WARDROBES BOUGHT. Everything

[005-142] seemed to be bought and nothing to be sold there. In all parts of the

[005-146] shop had in several little particulars the air of being in a legal

[005-147] neighbourhood and of being, as it were, a dirty hanger-on and

[005-148] disowned relation of the law. There were a great many ink bottles.

[005-149] There was a little tottering bench of shabby old volumes outside the

[005-150] door, labelled “Law Books, all at 9d.” Some of the inscriptions I

[005-151] have enumerated were written in law-hand, like the papers I had seen

[005-152] in [[kenge-and-carboy|Kenge and Carboy]]’s office and the letters I had so long received

[005-153] from the firm. Among them was one, in the same writing, having

[005-154] nothing to do with the business of the shop, but announcing that a

[005-155] respectable man aged forty-five wanted engrossing or copying to

[005-157] [[krook|Krook]], within. There were several second-hand bags, blue and red,

[005-159] crackled parchment scrolls and discoloured and dog’s-eared

[005-161] there must have been hundreds huddled together as old iron, had once

[005-166] fancy, as Richard whispered to Ada and me while we all stood looking

[005-168] clean, were the bones of clients, to make the picture complete.

[005-170] by the wall of Lincoln’s Inn, intercepting the light within a couple

[005-174] short, cadaverous, and withered, with his head sunk sideways between

[005-175] his shoulders and the breath issuing in visible smoke from his mouth

[005-176] as if he were on fire within. His throat, chin, and eyebrows were so

[005-177] frosted with white hairs and so gnarled with veins and puckered skin

[005-179] snow.

[005-180] “Hi, hi!” said the old man, coming to the door. “Have you anything to

[005-181] sell?”

[005-182] We naturally drew back and glanced at our conductress, who had been

[005-183] trying to open the house-door with a key she had taken from her

[005-184] pocket, and to whom Richard now said that as we had had the pleasure

[005-185] of seeing where she lived, we would leave her, being pressed for

[005-186] time. But she was not to be so easily left. She became so

[005-187] fantastically and pressingly earnest in her entreaties that we would

[005-188] walk up and see her apartment for an instant, and was so bent, in her

[005-189] harmless way, on leading me in, as part of the good omen she desired,

[005-190] that I (whatever the others might do) saw nothing for it but to

[005-191] comply. I suppose we were all more or less curious; at any rate, when

[005-192] the old man added his persuasions to hers and said, “Aye, aye! Please

[005-193] her! It won’t take a minute! Come in, come in! Come in through the

[005-194] shop if t’other door’s out of order!” we all went in, stimulated by

[005-195] Richard’s laughing encouragement and relying on his protection.

[005-196] “My landlord, Krook,” said [[miss-flite|the little old lady]], condescending to him

[005-197] from her lofty station as she presented him to us. “He is called

[005-198] among the neighbours the Lord Chancellor. His shop is called the

[005-199] [[court-of-chancery-institution|Court of Chancery]]. He is a very eccentric person. He is very odd. Oh,

[005-200] I assure you he is very odd!”

[005-201] She shook her head a great many times and tapped her forehead with

[005-202] her finger to express to us that we must have the goodness to excuse

[005-203] him, “For he is a little—you know—M!” said the old lady with great

[005-204] stateliness. The old man overheard, and laughed.

[005-205] “It’s true enough,” he said, going before us with the lantern, “that

[005-206] they call me the Lord Chancellor and call my shop Chancery. And why

[005-207] do you think they call me the Lord Chancellor and my shop Chancery?”

[005-208] “I don’t know, I am sure!” said Richard rather carelessly.

[005-209] “You see,” said the old man, stopping and turning round, “they—Hi!

[005-210] Here’s lovely hair! I have got three sacks of ladies’ hair below, but

[005-211] none so beautiful and fine as this. What colour, and what texture!”

[005-212] “That’ll do, my good friend!” said Richard, strongly disapproving of

[005-213] his having drawn one of Ada’s tresses through his yellow hand. “You

[005-214] can admire as the rest of us do without taking that liberty.”

[005-215] The old man darted at him a sudden look which even called my

[005-216] attention from Ada, who, startled and blushing, was so remarkably

[005-217] beautiful that she seemed to fix the wandering attention of the

[005-218] little old lady herself. But as Ada interposed and laughingly said

[005-231] mind. I go to see my noble and learned brother pretty well every day,

[005-232] when he sits in the Inn. He don’t notice me, but I notice him.

[005-233] There’s no great odds betwixt us. We both grub on in a muddle. Hi,

[005-234] Lady Jane!”

[005-235] A large grey cat leaped from some neighbouring shelf on his shoulder

[005-236] and startled us all.

[005-237] “Hi! Show ’em how you scratch. Hi! Tear, my lady!” said her master.

[005-238] The cat leaped down and ripped at a bundle of rags with her tigerish

[005-240] “She’d do as much for any one I was to set her on,” said the old man.

[005-241] “I deal in cat-skins among other general matters, and hers was

[005-253] “[[jarndyce-and-jarndyce|Jarndyce and Jarndyce]]. The great suit, Krook,” returned his lodger.

[005-259] Chancellor!”
