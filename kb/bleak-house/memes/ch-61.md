[061-001] The days when I frequented that miserable corner which my dear girl

[061-002] brightened can never fade in my remembrance. I never see it, and I

[061-003] never wish to see it now; I have been there only once since, but in

[061-004] my memory there is a mournful glory shining on the place which will

[061-005] shine for ever.

[061-006] Not a day passed without my going there, of course. At first I found

[061-007] [[harold-skimpole|Mr. Skimpole]] there, on two or three occasions, idly playing the piano

[061-008] and talking in his usual vivacious strain. Now, besides my very much

[061-009] mistrusting the probability of his being there without making [[richard-carstone|Richard]]

[061-010] poorer, I felt as if there were something in his careless gaiety too

[061-011] inconsistent with what I knew of the depths of [[ada-clare|Ada]]’s life. I clearly

[061-012] perceived, too, that Ada shared my feelings. I therefore resolved,

[061-013] after much thinking of it, to make a private visit to Mr. Skimpole

[061-014] and try delicately to explain myself. My dear girl was the great

[061-015] consideration that made me bold.

[061-016] I set off one morning, accompanied by Charley, for Somers Town. As I

[061-017] approached the house, I was strongly inclined to turn back, for I

[061-018] felt what a desperate attempt it was to make an impression on Mr.

[061-019] Skimpole and how extremely likely it was that he would signally

[061-020] defeat me. However, I thought that being there, I would go through

[061-021] with it. I knocked with a trembling hand at Mr. Skimpole’s

[061-022] door—literally with a hand, for the knocker was gone—and after a

[061-023] long parley gained admission from an Irishwoman, who was in the area

[061-024] when I knocked, breaking up the lid of a water-butt with a poker to

[061-025] light the fire with.

[061-026] Mr. Skimpole, lying on the sofa in his room, playing the flute a

[061-027] little, was enchanted to see me. Now, who should receive me, he

[061-028] asked. Who would I prefer for mistress of the ceremonies? Would I

[061-029] have his Comedy daughter, his Beauty daughter, or his Sentiment

[061-030] daughter? Or would I have all the daughters at once in a perfect

[061-031] nosegay?

[061-032] I replied, half defeated already, that I wished to speak to himself

[061-033] only if he would give me leave.

[061-034] “My dear Miss Summerson, most joyfully! Of course,” he said, bringing

[061-035] his chair nearer mine and breaking into his fascinating smile, “of

[061-036] course it’s not business. Then it’s pleasure!”

[061-037] I said it certainly was not business that I came upon, but it was not

[061-038] quite a pleasant matter.

[061-039] “Then, my dear Miss Summerson,” said he with the frankest gaiety,

[061-040] “don’t allude to it. Why should you allude to anything that is NOT a

[061-041] pleasant matter? I never do. And you are a much pleasanter creature,

[061-042] in every point of view, than I. You are perfectly pleasant; I am

[061-043] imperfectly pleasant; then, if I never allude to an unpleasant

[061-044] matter, how much less should you! So that’s disposed of, and we will

[061-045] talk of something else.”

[061-046] Although I was embarrassed, I took courage to intimate that I still

[061-047] wished to pursue the subject.

[061-048] “I should think it a mistake,” said Mr. Skimpole with his airy laugh,

[061-049] “if I thought Miss Summerson capable of making one. But I don’t!”

[061-050] “Mr. Skimpole,” said I, raising my eyes to his, “I have so often

[061-051] heard you say that you are unacquainted with the common affairs of

[061-052] life—”

[061-053] “Meaning our three banking-house friends, L, S, and who’s the junior

[061-054] partner? D?” said Mr. Skimpole, brightly. “Not an idea of them!”

[061-055] “—That perhaps,” I went on, “you will excuse my boldness on that

[061-056] account. I think you ought most seriously to know that Richard is

[061-057] poorer than he was.”

[061-058] “Dear me!” said Mr. Skimpole. “So am I, they tell me.”

[061-059] “And in very embarrassed circumstances.”

[061-060] “Parallel case, exactly!” said Mr. Skimpole with a delighted

[061-061] countenance.

[061-062] “This at present naturally causes Ada much secret anxiety, and as I

[061-063] think she is less anxious when no claims are made upon her by

[061-064] visitors, and as Richard has one uneasiness always heavy on his mind,

[061-065] it has occurred to me to take the liberty of saying that—if you

[061-066] would—not—”

[061-067] I was coming to the point with great difficulty when he took me by

[061-068] both hands and with a radiant face and in the liveliest way

[061-069] anticipated it.

[061-070] “Not go there? Certainly not, my dear Miss Summerson, most assuredly

[061-071] not. Why SHOULD I go there? When I go anywhere, I go for pleasure. I

[061-072] don’t go anywhere for pain, because I was made for pleasure. Pain

[061-073] comes to ME when it wants me. Now, I have had very little pleasure at

[061-074] our dear Richard’s lately, and your practical sagacity demonstrates

[061-075] why. Our young friends, losing the youthful poetry which was once so

[061-076] captivating in them, begin to think, ‘This is a man who wants

[061-077] pounds.’ So I am; I always want pounds; not for myself, but because

[061-078] tradespeople always want them of me. Next, our young friends begin to

[061-079] think, becoming mercenary, ‘This is the man who HAD pounds, who

[061-080] borrowed them,’ which I did. I always borrow pounds. So our young

[061-081] friends, reduced to prose (which is much to be regretted), degenerate

[061-082] in their power of imparting pleasure to me. Why should I go to see

[061-083] them, therefore? Absurd!”

[061-084] Through the beaming smile with which he regarded me as he reasoned

[061-085] thus, there now broke forth a look of disinterested benevolence quite

[061-086] astonishing.

[061-087] “Besides,” he said, pursuing his argument in his tone of

[061-088] light-hearted conviction, “if I don’t go anywhere for pain—which

[061-089] would be a perversion of the intention of my being, and a monstrous

[061-090] thing to do—why should I go anywhere to be the cause of pain? If I

[061-091] went to see our young friends in their present ill-regulated state of

[061-092] mind, I should give them pain. The associations with me would be

[061-093] disagreeable. They might say, ‘This is the man who had pounds and who

[061-094] can’t pay pounds,’ which I can’t, of course; nothing could be more

[061-095] out of the question! Then kindness requires that I shouldn’t go near

[061-096] them—and I won’t.”

[061-097] He finished by genially kissing my hand and thanking me. Nothing but

[061-098] Miss Summerson’s fine tact, he said, would have found this out for

[061-099] him.

[061-100] I was much disconcerted, but I reflected that if the main point were

[061-101] gained, it mattered little how strangely he perverted everything

[061-102] leading to it. I had determined to mention something else, however,

[061-103] and I thought I was not to be put off in that.

[061-104] “Mr. Skimpole,” said I, “I must take the liberty of saying before I

[061-105] conclude my visit that I was much surprised to learn, on the best

[061-106] authority, some little time ago, that you knew with whom that poor

[061-107] boy left [[bleak-house|Bleak House]] and that you accepted a present on that

[061-108] occasion. I have not mentioned it to my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]], for I fear it would

[061-109] hurt him unnecessarily; but I may say to you that I was much

[061-110] surprised.”

[061-111] “No? Really surprised, my dear Miss Summerson?” he returned

[061-112] inquiringly, raising his pleasant eyebrows.

[061-113] “Greatly surprised.”

[061-114] He thought about it for a little while with a highly agreeable and

[061-115] whimsical expression of face, then quite gave it up and said in his

[061-116] most engaging manner, “You know what a child I am. Why surprised?”

[061-117] I was reluctant to enter minutely into that question, but as he

[061-118] begged I would, for he was really curious to know, I gave him to

[061-119] understand in the gentlest words I could use that his conduct seemed

[061-120] to involve a disregard of several moral obligations. He was much

[061-121] amused and interested when he heard this and said, “No, really?” with

[061-122] ingenuous simplicity.

[061-123] “You know I don’t intend to be responsible. I never could do it.

[061-124] Responsibility is a thing that has always been above me—or below

[061-125] me,” said Mr. Skimpole. “I don’t even know which; but as I understand

[061-126] the way in which my dear Miss Summerson (always remarkable for her

[061-127] practical good sense and clearness) puts this case, I should imagine

[061-128] it was chiefly a question of money, do you know?”

[061-129] I incautiously gave a qualified assent to this.

[061-130] “Ah! Then you see,” said Mr. Skimpole, shaking his head, “I am

[061-131] hopeless of understanding it.”

[061-132] I suggested, as I rose to go, that it was not right to betray my

[061-133] guardian’s confidence for a bribe.

[061-134] “My dear Miss Summerson,” he returned with a candid hilarity that was

[061-135] all his own, “I can’t be bribed.”

[061-136] “Not by Mr. Bucket?” said I.

[061-137] “No,” said he. “Not by anybody. I don’t attach any value to money. I

[061-138] don’t care about it, I don’t know about it, I don’t want it, I don’t

[061-139] keep it—it goes away from me directly. How can I be bribed?”

[061-140] I showed that I was of a different opinion, though I had not the

[061-141] capacity for arguing the question.

[061-142] “On the contrary,” said Mr. Skimpole, “I am exactly the man to be

[061-143] placed in a superior position in such a case as that. I am above the

[061-144] rest of mankind in such a case as that. I can act with philosophy in

[061-145] such a case as that. I am not warped by prejudices, as an Italian

[061-146] baby is by bandages. I am as free as the air. I feel myself as far

[061-147] above suspicion as Caesar’s wife.”

[061-148] Anything to equal the lightness of his manner and the playful

[061-149] impartiality with which he seemed to convince himself, as he tossed

[061-150] the matter about like a ball of feathers, was surely never seen in

[061-151] anybody else!

[061-152] “Observe the case, my dear Miss Summerson. Here is a boy received

[061-153] into the house and put to bed in a state that I strongly object to.

[061-154] The boy being in bed, a man arrives—like the house that Jack built.

[061-155] Here is the man who demands the boy who is received into the house

[061-156] and put to bed in a state that I strongly object to. Here is a

[061-157] bank-note produced by the man who demands the boy who is received

[061-158] into the house and put to bed in a state that I strongly object to.

[061-159] Here is the Skimpole who accepts the bank-note produced by the man

[061-160] who demands the boy who is received into the house and put to bed in

[061-161] a state that I strongly object to. Those are the facts. Very well.

[061-162] Should the Skimpole have refused the note? WHY should the Skimpole

[061-163] have refused the note? Skimpole protests to Bucket, ‘What’s this for?

[061-164] I don’t understand it, it is of no use to me, take it away.’ Bucket

[061-165] still entreats Skimpole to accept it. Are there reasons why Skimpole,

[061-166] not being warped by prejudices, should accept it? Yes. Skimpole

[061-167] perceives them. What are they? Skimpole reasons with himself, this is

[061-168] a tamed lynx, an active police-officer, an intelligent man, a person

[061-169] of a peculiarly directed energy and great subtlety both of conception

[061-170] and execution, who discovers our friends and enemies for us when they

[061-171] run away, recovers our [[inheritance-and-property|property]] for us when we are robbed, avenges us

[061-172] comfortably when we are murdered. This active police-officer and

[061-173] intelligent man has acquired, in the exercise of his art, a strong

[061-174] faith in money; he finds it very useful to him, and he makes it very

[061-175] useful to society. Shall I shake that faith in Bucket because I want

[061-176] it myself; shall I deliberately blunt one of Bucket’s weapons; shall

[061-177] I positively paralyse Bucket in his next detective operation? And

[061-178] again. If it is blameable in Skimpole to take the note, it is

[061-179] blameable in Bucket to offer the note—much more blameable in Bucket,

[061-180] because he is the knowing man. Now, Skimpole wishes to think well of

[061-181] Bucket; Skimpole deems it essential, in its little place, to the

[061-182] general cohesion of things, that he SHOULD think well of Bucket. The

[061-183] state expressly asks him to trust to Bucket. And he does. And that’s

[061-184] all he does!”

[061-185] I had nothing to offer in reply to this exposition and therefore took

[061-186] my leave. Mr. Skimpole, however, who was in excellent spirits, would

[061-187] not hear of my returning home attended only by “Little Coavinses,”

[061-188] and accompanied me himself. He entertained me on the way with a

[061-189] variety of delightful conversation and assured me, at parting, that

[061-190] he should never forget the fine tact with which I had found that out

[061-191] for him about our young friends.

[061-192] As it so happened that I never saw Mr. Skimpole again, I may at once

[061-193] finish what I know of his history. A coolness arose between him and

[061-194] my guardian, based principally on the foregoing grounds and on his

[061-195] having heartlessly disregarded my guardian’s entreaties (as we

[061-196] afterwards learned from Ada) in reference to Richard. His being

[061-197] heavily in my guardian’s debt had nothing to do with their

[061-198] separation. He died some five years afterwards and left a diary

[061-199] behind him, with [[document-and-letter|letters]] and other materials towards his life, which

[061-200] was published and which showed him to have been the victim of a

[061-201] combination on the part of mankind against an amiable child. It was

[061-202] considered very pleasant reading, but I never read more of it myself

[061-203] than the sentence on which I chanced to light on opening the book. It

[061-204] was this: “Jarndyce, in common with most other men I have known, is

[061-205] the incarnation of selfishness.”

[061-206] And now I come to a part of my story touching myself very nearly

[061-207] indeed, and for which I was quite unprepared when the circumstance

[061-208] occurred. Whatever little lingerings may have now and then revived in

[061-209] my mind associated with my poor old face had only revived as

[061-210] belonging to a part of my life that was gone—gone like my infancy or

[061-211] my childhood. I have suppressed none of my many weaknesses on that

[061-212] subject, but have written them as faithfully as my memory has

[061-213] recalled them. And I hope to do, and mean to do, the same down to the

[061-214] last words of these pages, which I see now not so very far before me.

[061-215] The months were gliding away, and my dear girl, sustained by the

[061-216] hopes she had confided in me, was the same beautiful star in the

[061-217] miserable corner. Richard, more worn and haggard, haunted the court

[061-218] day after day, listlessly sat there the whole day long when he knew

[061-219] there was no remote chance of the suit being mentioned, and became

[061-220] one of the stock sights of the place. I wonder whether any of the

[061-221] gentlemen remembered him as he was when he first went there.

[061-222] So completely was he absorbed in his fixed idea that he used to avow

[061-223] in his cheerful moments that he should never have breathed the fresh

[061-224] air now “but for [[allan-woodcourt|Woodcourt]].” It was only Mr. Woodcourt who could

[061-225] occasionally divert his attention for a few hours at a time and rouse

[061-226] him, even when he sunk into a lethargy of mind and body that alarmed

[061-227] us greatly, and the returns of which became more frequent as the

[061-228] months went on. My dear girl was right in saying that he only pursued

[061-229] his errors the more desperately for her sake. I have no doubt that

[061-230] his desire to retrieve what he had lost was rendered the more intense

[061-231] by his grief for his young wife, and became like the madness of a

[061-232] gamester.

[061-233] I was there, as I have mentioned, at all hours. When I was there at

[061-234] night, I generally went home with Charley in a coach; sometimes my

[061-235] guardian would meet me in the neighbourhood, and we would walk home

[061-236] together. One evening he had arranged to meet me at eight o’clock. I

[061-237] could not leave, as I usually did, quite punctually at the time, for

[061-238] I was working for my dear girl and had a few stitches more to do to

[061-239] finish what I was about; but it was within a few minutes of the hour

[061-240] when I bundled up my little work-basket, gave my darling my last kiss

[061-241] for the night, and hurried downstairs. Mr. Woodcourt went with me, as

[061-242] it was dusk.

[061-243] When we came to the usual place of meeting—it was close by, and Mr.

[061-244] Woodcourt had often accompanied me before—my guardian was not there.

[061-245] We waited half an hour, walking up and down, but there were no signs

[061-246] of him. We agreed that he was either prevented from coming or that he

[061-247] had come and gone away, and Mr. Woodcourt proposed to walk home with

[061-248] me.

[061-249] It was the first walk we had ever taken together, except that very

[061-250] short one to the usual place of meeting. We spoke of Richard and Ada

[061-251] the whole way. I did not thank him in words for what he had done—my

[061-252] appreciation of it had risen above all words then—but I hoped he

[061-253] might not be without some understanding of what I felt so strongly.

[061-254] Arriving at home and going upstairs, we found that my guardian was

[061-255] out and that Mrs. Woodcourt was out too. We were in the very same

[061-256] room into which I had brought my blushing girl when her youthful

[061-257] lover, now her so altered husband, was the choice of her young heart,

[061-258] the very same room from which my guardian and I had watched them

[061-259] going away through the sunlight in the fresh bloom of their hope and

[061-260] promise.

[061-261] We were standing by the opened window looking down into the street

[061-262] when Mr. Woodcourt spoke to me. I learned in a moment that he loved

[061-263] me. I learned in a moment that my scarred face was all unchanged to

[061-264] him. I learned in a moment that what I had thought was pity and

[061-265] compassion was devoted, generous, faithful love. Oh, too late to know

[061-266] it now, too late, too late. That was the first ungrateful thought I

[061-267] had. Too late.

[061-268] “When I returned,” he told me, “when I came back, no richer than when

[061-269] I went away, and found you newly risen from a sick bed, yet so

[061-270] inspired by sweet consideration for others and so free from a selfish

[061-271] thought—”

[061-272] “Oh, Mr. Woodcourt, forbear, forbear!” I entreated him. “I do not

[061-273] deserve your high praise. I had many selfish thoughts at that time,

[061-274] many!”

[061-275] “Heaven knows, beloved of my life,” said he, “that my praise is not a

[061-276] lover’s praise, but the truth. You do not know what all around you

[061-277] see in [[esther-summerson|Esther Summerson]], how many hearts she touches and awakens,

[061-278] what sacred admiration and what love she wins.”

[061-279] “Oh, Mr. Woodcourt,” cried I, “it is a great thing to win love, it is

[061-280] a great thing to win love! I am proud of it, and honoured by it; and

[061-281] the hearing of it causes me to shed these tears of mingled joy and

[061-282] sorrow—joy that I have won it, sorrow that I have not deserved it

[061-283] better; but I am not free to think of yours.”

[061-284] I said it with a stronger heart, for when he praised me thus and when

[061-285] I heard his voice thrill with his belief that what he said was true,

[061-286] I aspired to be more worthy of it. It was not too late for that.

[061-287] Although I closed this unforeseen page in my life to-night, I could

[061-288] be worthier of it all through my life. And it was a comfort to me,

[061-289] and an impulse to me, and I felt a dignity rise up within me that was

[061-290] derived from him when I thought so.

[061-291] He broke the silence.

[061-292] “I should poorly show the trust that I have in the dear one who will

[061-293] evermore be as dear to me as now”—and the deep earnestness with

[061-294] which he said it at once strengthened me and made me weep—“if, after

[061-295] her assurance that she is not free to think of my love, I urged it.

[061-296] Dear Esther, let me only tell you that the fond idea of you which I

[061-297] took abroad was exalted to the heavens when I came home. I have

[061-298] always hoped, in the first hour when I seemed to stand in any ray of

[061-299] good fortune, to tell you this. I have always feared that I should

[061-300] tell it you in vain. My hopes and fears are both fulfilled to-night.

[061-301] I distress you. I have said enough.”

[061-302] Something seemed to pass into my place that was like the angel he

[061-303] thought me, and I felt so sorrowful for the loss he had sustained! I

[061-304] wished to help him in his trouble, as I had wished to do when he

[061-305] showed that first commiseration for me.

[061-306] “Dear Mr. Woodcourt,” said I, “before we part to-night, something is

[061-307] left for me to say. I never could say it as I wish—I never

[061-308] shall—but—”

[061-309] I had to think again of being more deserving of his love and his

[061-310] affliction before I could go on.

[061-311] “—I am deeply sensible of your generosity, and I shall treasure its

[061-312] remembrance to my dying hour. I know full well how changed I am, I

[061-313] know you are not unacquainted with my history, and I know what a

[061-314] noble love that is which is so faithful. What you have said to me

[061-315] could have affected me so much from no other lips, for there are none

[061-316] that could give it such a value to me. It shall not be lost. It shall

[061-317] make me better.”

[061-318] He covered his eyes with his hand and turned away his head. How could

[061-319] I ever be worthy of those tears?

[061-320] “If, in the unchanged intercourse we shall have together—in tending

[061-321] Richard and Ada, and I hope in many happier scenes of life—you ever

[061-322] find anything in me which you can honestly think is better than it

[061-323] used to be, believe that it will have sprung up from to-night and

[061-324] that I shall owe it to you. And never believe, dear dear Mr.

[061-325] Woodcourt, never believe that I forget this night or that while my

[061-326] heart beats it can be insensible to the pride and joy of having been

[061-327] beloved by you.”

[061-328] He took my hand and kissed it. He was like himself again, and I felt

[061-329] still more encouraged.

[061-330] “I am induced by what you said just now,” said I, “to hope that you

[061-331] have succeeded in your endeavour.”

[061-332] “I have,” he answered. “With such help from Mr. Jarndyce as you who

[061-333] know him so well can imagine him to have rendered me, I have

[061-334] succeeded.”

[061-335] “Heaven bless him for it,” said I, giving him my hand; “and heaven

[061-336] bless you in all you do!”

[061-337] “I shall do it better for the wish,” he answered; “it will make me

[061-338] enter on these new duties as on another sacred trust from you.”

[061-339] “Ah! Richard!” I exclaimed involuntarily, “What will he do when you

[061-340] are gone!”

[061-341] “I am not required to go yet; I would not desert him, dear Miss

[061-342] Summerson, even if I were.”

[061-343] One other thing I felt it needful to touch upon before he left me. I

[061-344] knew that I should not be worthier of the love I could not take if I

[061-345] reserved it.

[061-346] “Mr. Woodcourt,” said I, “you will be glad to know from my lips

[061-347] before I say good night that in the future, which is clear and bright

[061-348] before me, I am most happy, most fortunate, have nothing to regret or

[061-349] desire.”

[061-350] It was indeed a glad hearing to him, he replied.

[061-351] “From my childhood I have been,” said I, “the object of the untiring

[061-352] goodness of the best of human beings, to whom I am so bound by every

[061-353] tie of attachment, gratitude, and love, that nothing I could do in

[061-354] the compass of a life could express the feelings of a single day.”

[061-355] “I share those feelings,” he returned. “You speak of Mr. Jarndyce.”

[061-356] “You know his virtues well,” said I, “but few can know the greatness

[061-357] of his character as I know it. All its highest and best qualities

[061-358] have been revealed to me in nothing more brightly than in the shaping

[061-359] out of that future in which I am so happy. And if your highest homage

[061-360] and respect had not been his already—which I know they are—they

[061-361] would have been his, I think, on this assurance and in the feeling it

[061-362] would have awakened in you towards him for my sake.”

[061-363] He fervently replied that indeed indeed they would have been. I gave

[061-364] him my hand again.

[061-365] “Good night,” I said, “Good-bye.”

[061-366] “The first until we meet to-morrow, the second as a farewell to this

[061-367] theme between us for ever.”

[061-368] “Yes.”

[061-369] “Good night; good-bye.”

[061-370] He left me, and I stood at the dark window watching the street. His

[061-371] love, in all its constancy and generosity, had come so suddenly upon

[061-372] me that he had not left me a minute when my fortitude gave way again

[061-373] and the street was blotted out by my rushing tears.

[061-374] But they were not tears of regret and sorrow. No. He had called me

[061-375] the beloved of his life and had said I would be evermore as dear to

[061-376] him as I was then, and I felt as if my heart would not hold the

[061-377] triumph of having heard those words. My first wild thought had died

[061-378] away. It was not too late to hear them, for it was not too late to be

[061-379] animated by them to be good, true, grateful, and contented. How easy

[061-380] my path, how much easier than his!
