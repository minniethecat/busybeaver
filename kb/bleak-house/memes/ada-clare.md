# Ada Clare

## Overview

Ada Clare is introduced as "my beautiful Ada" in Esther's narrative — a young woman of sweetness, loyalty, and, as the novel reveals, considerable moral courage. While often read as Richard's adjunct (the loving wife who cannot save him), Ada's decision to secretly marry Richard against her guardian's wishes is an act of significant autonomy. Her quiet endurance after Richard's death is the novel's most understated portrait of resilience.

## Role in the Novel

Ada arrives at Bleak House alongside Esther and Richard. Her beauty and virtue are established early, but the novel complicates the initial portrait: Ada is not merely passive. Her secret marriage to Richard — undertaken without Jarndyce's knowledge — is a dangerous act that asserts her right to choose her own fate, even at the cost of her guardian's approval.

After Richard's death, Ada returns to Bleak House with her infant son. Esther's final description of her — "my Ada, still my Ada" — acknowledges the permanence of her loss while affirming her capacity to endure. She is not the novel's most vivid character, but she is arguably its bravest.

## Relationships

- **Richard Carstone** — Husband; her love persists through his destruction
- **John Jarndyce** — Guardian; their relationship survives her secret marriage
- **Esther Summerson** — Closest friend and fellow ward
- **Infant son** — Richard's posthumous child; named after his father

## Significance

Ada is easily underestimated. The novel's critics have sometimes dismissed her as a sentimental type, but her actions — secret marriage, endurance of Richard's decline, widowhood, motherhood — constitute a quiet assertion of agency that the novel's more dramatic characters (Lady Dedlock, Tulkinghorn) obscure. She represents the domestic virtues the novel affirms, but without the self-effacement that complicates Esther's narrative position.
