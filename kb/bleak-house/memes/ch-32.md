[032-001] It is night in Lincoln’s Inn—perplexed and troublous valley of the

[032-002] shadow of the law, where suitors generally find but little day—and

[032-003] fat candles are snuffed out in offices, and clerks have rattled down

[032-004] the crazy wooden stairs and dispersed. The bell that rings at nine

[032-005] o’clock has ceased its doleful clangour about nothing; the gates are

[032-006] shut; and the night-porter, a solemn warder with a mighty power of

[032-007] sleep, keeps guard in his lodge. From tiers of staircase windows

[032-008] clogged lamps like the eyes of Equity, bleared Argus with a

[032-009] fathomless pocket for every eye and an eye upon it, dimly blink at

[032-010] the stars. In dirty upper casements, here and there, hazy little

[032-011] patches of candlelight reveal where some wise draughtsman and

[032-012] conveyancer yet toils for the entanglement of real [[inheritance-and-property|estate]] in meshes

[032-013] of sheep-skin, in the average ratio of about a dozen of sheep to an

[032-014] acre of land. Over which bee-like industry these benefactors of their

[032-015] species linger yet, though office-hours be past, that they may give,

[032-016] for every day, some good account at last.

[032-017] In the neighbouring court, where the Lord Chancellor of the rag and

[032-018] bottle shop dwells, there is a general tendency towards beer and

[032-019] supper. Mrs. Piper and Mrs. Perkins, whose respective sons, engaged

[032-020] with a circle of acquaintance in the game of hide and seek, have been

[032-021] lying in ambush about the by-ways of [[chancery-lane|Chancery Lane]] for some hours and

[032-022] scouring the plain of the same thoroughfare to the confusion of

[032-023] passengers—Mrs. Piper and Mrs. Perkins have but now exchanged

[032-024] congratulations on the children being abed, and they still linger on

[032-025] a door-step over a few parting words. Mr. [[krook|Krook]] and his lodger, and

[032-026] the fact of Mr. Krook’s being “continually in liquor,” and the

[032-027] testamentary prospects of the young man are, as usual, the staple of

[032-028] their conversation. But they have something to say, likewise, of the

[032-029] Harmonic Meeting at the Sol’s Arms, where the sound of the piano

[032-030] through the partly opened windows jingles out into the court, and

[032-031] where Little Swills, after keeping the lovers of harmony in a roar

[032-032] like a very Yorick, may now be heard taking the gruff line in a

[032-033] concerted piece and sentimentally adjuring his friends and patrons to

[032-034] “Listen, listen, listen, tew the wa-ter fall!” Mrs. Perkins and Mrs.

[032-035] Piper compare opinions on the subject of the young lady of

[032-036] professional celebrity who assists at the Harmonic Meetings and who

[032-037] has a space to herself in the manuscript announcement in the window,

[032-038] Mrs. Perkins possessing information that she has been married a year

[032-039] and a half, though announced as Miss M. Melvilleson, the noted siren,

[032-040] and that her baby is clandestinely conveyed to the Sol’s Arms every

[032-041] night to receive its natural nourishment during the entertainments.

[032-042] “Sooner than which, myself,” says Mrs. Perkins, “I would get my

[032-043] living by selling lucifers.” Mrs. Piper, as in duty bound, is of the

[032-044] same opinion, holding that a private station is better than public

[032-045] applause, and thanking heaven for her own (and, by implication, Mrs.

[032-046] Perkins’) respectability. By this time the pot-boy of the Sol’s Arms

[032-047] appearing with her supper-pint well frothed, Mrs. Piper accepts that

[032-048] tankard and retires indoors, first giving a fair good night to Mrs.

[032-049] Perkins, who has had her own pint in her hand ever since it was

[032-050] fetched from the same hostelry by young Perkins before he was sent to

[032-051] bed. Now there is a sound of putting up shop-shutters in the court

[032-052] and a smell as of the smoking of pipes; and shooting stars are seen

[032-053] in upper windows, further indicating retirement to rest. Now, too,

[032-054] the policeman begins to push at doors; to try fastenings; to be

[032-055] suspicious of bundles; and to administer his beat, on the hypothesis

[032-056] that every one is either robbing or being robbed.

[032-057] It is a close night, though the damp cold is searching too, and there

[032-058] is a laggard mist a little way up in the air. It is a fine steaming

[032-059] night to turn the slaughter-houses, the unwholesome trades, the

[032-060] sewerage, bad water, and burial-grounds to account, and give the

[032-061] registrar of deaths some extra business. It may be something in the

[032-062] air—there is plenty in it—or it may be something in himself that is

[032-063] in fault; but Mr. Weevle, otherwise Jobling, is very ill at ease. He

[032-064] comes and goes between his own room and the open street door twenty

[032-065] times an hour. He has been doing so ever since it fell dark. Since

[032-066] the Chancellor shut up his shop, which he did very early to-night,

[032-067] Mr. Weevle has been down and up, and down and up (with a cheap tight

[032-068] velvet skull-cap on his head, making his whiskers look out of all

[032-069] proportion), oftener than before.

[032-070] It is no phenomenon that Mr. Snagsby should be ill at ease too, for

[032-071] he always is so, more or less, under the oppressive influence of the

[032-072] secret that is upon him. Impelled by the mystery of which he is a

[032-073] partaker and yet in which he is not a sharer, Mr. Snagsby haunts what

[032-074] seems to be its fountain-head—the rag and bottle shop in the court.

[032-075] It has an irresistible attraction for him. Even now, coming round by

[032-076] the Sol’s Arms with the intention of passing down the court, and out

[032-077] at the [[chancery-court|Chancery]] Lane end, and so terminating his unpremeditated

[032-078] after-supper stroll of ten minutes’ long from his own door and back

[032-079] again, Mr. Snagsby approaches.

[032-080] “What, Mr. Weevle?” says the stationer, stopping to speak. “Are YOU

[032-081] there?”

[032-082] “Aye!” says Weevle, “Here I am, Mr. Snagsby.”

[032-083] “Airing yourself, as I am doing, before you go to bed?” the stationer

[032-084] inquires.

[032-085] “Why, there’s not much air to be got here; and what there is, is not

[032-086] very freshening,” Weevle answers, glancing up and down the court.

[032-087] “Very true, sir. Don’t you observe,” says Mr. Snagsby, pausing to

[032-088] sniff and taste the air a little, “don’t you observe, Mr. Weevle,

[032-089] that you’re—not to put too fine a point upon it—that you’re rather

[032-090] greasy here, sir?”

[032-091] “Why, I have noticed myself that there is a queer kind of flavour in

[032-092] the place to-night,” Mr. Weevle rejoins. “I suppose it’s chops at the

[032-093] Sol’s Arms.”

[032-094] “Chops, do you think? Oh! Chops, eh?” Mr. Snagsby sniffs and tastes

[032-095] again. “Well, sir, I suppose it is. But I should say their cook at

[032-096] the Sol wanted a little looking after. She has been burning ’em, sir!

[032-097] And I don’t think”—Mr. Snagsby sniffs and tastes again and then

[032-098] spits and wipes his mouth—“I don’t think—not to put too fine a

[032-099] point upon it—that they were quite fresh when they were shown the

[032-100] gridiron.”

[032-101] “That’s very likely. It’s a tainting sort of weather.”

[032-102] “It IS a tainting sort of weather,” says Mr. Snagsby, “and I find it

[032-103] sinking to the spirits.”

[032-104] “By [[george-rouncewell|George]]! I find it gives me the horrors,” returns Mr. Weevle.

[032-105] “Then, you see, you live in a lonesome way, and in a lonesome room,

[032-106] with a black circumstance hanging over it,” says Mr. Snagsby, looking

[032-107] in past the other’s shoulder along the dark passage and then falling

[032-108] back a step to look up at the house. “I couldn’t live in that room

[032-109] alone, as you do, sir. I should get so fidgety and worried of an

[032-110] evening, sometimes, that I should be driven to come to the door and

[032-111] stand here sooner than sit there. But then it’s very true that you

[032-112] didn’t see, in your room, what I saw there. That makes a difference.”

[032-113] “I know quite enough about it,” returns Tony.

[032-114] “It’s not agreeable, is it?” pursues Mr. Snagsby, coughing his cough

[032-115] of mild persuasion behind his hand. “Mr. Krook ought to consider it

[032-116] in the rent. I hope he does, I am sure.”

[032-117] “I hope he does,” says Tony. “But I doubt it.”

[032-118] “You find the rent too high, do you, sir?” returns the stationer.

[032-119] “Rents ARE high about here. I don’t know how it is exactly, but the

[032-120] law seems to put things up in price. Not,” adds Mr. Snagsby with his

[032-121] apologetic cough, “that I mean to say a word against the [[professional-identity|profession]] I

[032-122] get my living by.”

[032-123] Mr. Weevle again glances up and down the court and then looks at the

[032-124] stationer. Mr. Snagsby, blankly catching his eye, looks upward for a

[032-125] star or so and coughs a cough expressive of not exactly seeing his

[032-126] way out of this conversation.

[032-127] “It’s a curious fact, sir,” he observes, slowly rubbing his hands,

[032-128] “that he should have been—”

[032-129] “Who’s he?” interrupts Mr. Weevle.

[032-130] “The deceased, you know,” says Mr. Snagsby, twitching his head and

[032-131] right eyebrow towards the staircase and tapping his acquaintance on

[032-132] the button.

[032-133] “Ah, to be sure!” returns the other as if he were not over-fond of

[032-134] the subject. “I thought we had done with him.”

[032-135] “I was only going to say it’s a curious fact, sir, that he should

[032-136] have come and lived here, and been one of my writers, and then that

[032-137] you should come and live here, and be one of my writers too. Which

[032-138] there is nothing derogatory, but far from it in the appellation,”

[032-139] says Mr. Snagsby, breaking off with a mistrust that he may have

[032-140] unpolitely asserted a kind of proprietorship in Mr. Weevle, “because

[032-141] I have known writers that have gone into brewers’ houses and done

[032-142] really very respectable indeed. Eminently respectable, sir,” adds Mr.

[032-143] Snagsby with a misgiving that he has not improved the matter.

[032-144] “It’s a curious coincidence, as you say,” answers Weevle, once more

[032-145] glancing up and down the court.

[032-146] “Seems a fate in it, don’t there?” suggests the stationer.

[032-147] “There does.”

[032-148] “Just so,” observes the stationer with his confirmatory cough. “Quite

[032-149] a fate in it. Quite a fate. Well, Mr. Weevle, I am afraid I must bid

[032-150] you good night”—Mr. Snagsby speaks as if it made him desolate to go,

[032-151] though he has been casting about for any means of escape ever since

[032-152] he stopped to speak—“my little woman will be looking for me else.

[032-153] Good night, sir!”

[032-154] If Mr. Snagsby hastens home to save his little woman the trouble of

[032-155] looking for him, he might set his mind at rest on that score. His

[032-156] little woman has had her eye upon him round the Sol’s Arms all this

[032-157] time and now glides after him with a pocket handkerchief wrapped over

[032-158] her head, honouring Mr. Weevle and his doorway with a searching

[032-159] glance as she goes past.

[032-160] “You’ll know me again, ma’am, at all events,” says Mr. Weevle to

[032-161] himself; “and I can’t compliment you on your appearance, whoever you

[032-162] are, with your head tied up in a bundle. Is this fellow NEVER

[032-163] coming!”

[032-164] This fellow approaches as he speaks. Mr. Weevle softly holds up his

[032-165] finger, and draws him into the passage, and closes the street door.

[032-166] Then they go upstairs, Mr. Weevle heavily, and Mr. Guppy (for it is

[032-167] he) very lightly indeed. When they are shut into the back room, they

[032-168] speak low.

[032-169] “I thought you had gone to Jericho at least instead of coming here,”

[032-170] says Tony.

[032-171] “Why, I said about ten.”

[032-172] “You said about ten,” Tony repeats. “Yes, so you did say about ten.

[032-173] But according to my count, it’s ten times ten—it’s a hundred

[032-174] o’clock. I never had such a night in my life!”

[032-175] “What has been the matter?”

[032-176] “That’s it!” says Tony. “Nothing has been the matter. But here have I

[032-177] been stewing and fuming in this jolly old crib till I have had the

[032-178] horrors falling on me as thick as hail. THERE’S a blessed-looking

[032-179] candle!” says Tony, pointing to the heavily burning taper on his

[032-180] table with a great cabbage head and a long winding-sheet.

[032-181] “That’s easily improved,” Mr. Guppy observes as he takes the snuffers

[032-182] in hand.

[032-183] “IS it?” returns his friend. “Not so easily as you think. It has been

[032-184] smouldering like that ever since it was lighted.”

[032-185] “Why, what’s the matter with you, Tony?” inquires Mr. Guppy, looking

[032-186] at him, snuffers in hand, as he sits down with his elbow on the

[032-187] table.

[032-188] “William Guppy,” replies the other, “I am in the downs. It’s this

[032-189] unbearably dull, suicidal room—and old Boguey downstairs, I

[032-190] suppose.” Mr. Weevle moodily pushes the snuffers-tray from him with

[032-191] his elbow, leans his head on his hand, puts his feet on the fender,

[032-192] and looks at the fire. Mr. Guppy, observing him, slightly tosses his

[032-193] head and sits down on the other side of the table in an easy

[032-194] attitude.

[032-195] “Wasn’t that Snagsby talking to you, Tony?”

[032-196] “Yes, and he—yes, it was Snagsby,” said Mr. Weevle, altering the

[032-197] construction of his sentence.

[032-198] “On business?”

[032-199] “No. No business. He was only sauntering by and stopped to prose.”

[032-200] “I thought it was Snagsby,” says Mr. Guppy, “and thought it as well

[032-201] that he shouldn’t see me, so I waited till he was gone.”

[032-202] “There we go again, William G.!” cried Tony, looking up for an

[032-203] instant. “So mysterious and secret! By George, if we were going to

[032-204] commit a murder, we couldn’t have more mystery about it!”

[032-205] Mr. Guppy affects to smile, and with the view of changing the

[032-206] conversation, looks with an admiration, real or pretended, round the

[032-207] room at the Galaxy Gallery of British Beauty, terminating his survey

[032-208] with the portrait of [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]] over the mantelshelf, in which she

[032-209] is represented on a terrace, with a pedestal upon the terrace, and a

[032-210] vase upon the pedestal, and her shawl upon the vase, and a prodigious

[032-211] piece of fur upon the shawl, and her arm on the prodigious piece of

[032-212] fur, and a bracelet on her arm.

[032-213] “That’s very like Lady Dedlock,” says Mr. Guppy. “It’s a speaking

[032-214] likeness.”

[032-215] “I wish it was,” growls Tony, without changing his position. “I

[032-216] should have some fashionable conversation, here, then.”

[032-217] Finding by this time that his friend is not to be wheedled into a

[032-218] more sociable humour, Mr. Guppy puts about upon the ill-used tack and

[032-219] remonstrates with him.

[032-220] “Tony,” says he, “I can make allowances for lowness of spirits, for

[032-221] no man knows what it is when it does come upon a man better than I

[032-222] do, and no man perhaps has a better right to know it than a man who

[032-223] has an unrequited image imprinted on his ’eart. But there are bounds

[032-224] to these things when an unoffending party is in question, and I will

[032-225] acknowledge to you, Tony, that I don’t think your manner on the

[032-226] present occasion is hospitable or quite gentlemanly.”

[032-227] “This is strong language, William Guppy,” returns Mr. Weevle.

[032-228] “Sir, it may be,” retorts Mr. William Guppy, “but I feel strongly

[032-229] when I use it.”

[032-230] Mr. Weevle admits that he has been wrong and begs Mr. William Guppy

[032-231] to think no more about it. Mr. William Guppy, however, having got the

[032-232] advantage, cannot quite release it without a little more injured

[032-233] remonstrance.

[032-234] “No! Dash it, Tony,” says that gentleman, “you really ought to be

[032-235] careful how you wound the feelings of a man who has an unrequited

[032-236] image imprinted on his ’eart and who is NOT altogether happy in those

[032-237] chords which vibrate to the tenderest emotions. You, Tony, possess in

[032-238] yourself all that is calculated to charm the eye and allure the

[032-239] taste. It is not—happily for you, perhaps, and I may wish that I

[032-240] could say the same—it is not your character to hover around one

[032-241] flower. The ole garden is open to you, and your airy pinions carry

[032-242] you through it. Still, Tony, far be it from me, I am sure, to wound

[032-243] even your feelings without a cause!”

[032-244] Tony again entreats that the subject may be no longer pursued, saying

[032-245] emphatically, “William Guppy, drop it!” Mr. Guppy acquiesces, with

[032-246] the reply, “I never should have taken it up, Tony, of my own accord.”

[032-247] “And now,” says Tony, stirring the fire, “touching this same bundle

[032-248] of [[document-and-letter|letters]]. Isn’t it an extraordinary thing of Krook to have

[032-249] appointed twelve o’clock to-night to hand ’em over to me?”

[032-250] “Very. What did he do it for?”

[032-251] “What does he do anything for? HE don’t know. Said to-day was his

[032-252] birthday and he’d hand ’em over to-night at twelve o’clock. He’ll

[032-253] have drunk himself blind by that time. He has been at it all day.”

[032-254] “He hasn’t forgotten the appointment, I hope?”

[032-255] “Forgotten? Trust him for that. He never forgets anything. I saw him

[032-259] cap on the chair-back, and stood turning them over before the fire. I

[032-262] and Bibo being drunk when he died, or something or other. He has been

[032-264] “And you are to go down at twelve?”

[032-265] “At twelve. And as I tell you, when you came it seemed to me a

[032-266] hundred.”

[032-269] “Read! He’ll never read. He can make all the letters separately, and

[032-289] “Chimney on fire!”

[032-290] “Ah!” returns Mr. Guppy. “See how the soot’s falling. See here, on my

[032-294] little way upstairs, and a little way downstairs. Comes back and says

[032-296] Mr. Snagsby about their cooking chops at the Sol’s Arms.

[032-336] forming with his lips only the words, “What do you know?”

[032-345] mantelshelf and replies, “Tony, you are asked to leave that to the

[032-353] resounding from towers of various heights, in tones more various than

[032-354] their situations. When these at length cease, all seems more

[032-356] whispering is that it seems to evoke an atmosphere of silence,

[032-357] haunted by the ghosts of sound—strange cracks and tickings, the

[032-360] snow. So sensitive the two friends happen to be that the air is full
