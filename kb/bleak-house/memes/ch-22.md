[022-001] Allegory looks pretty cool in Lincoln’s Inn Fields, though the

[022-002] evening is hot, for both [[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]]’s windows are wide open, and

[022-003] the room is lofty, gusty, and gloomy. These may not be desirable

[022-004] characteristics when November comes with [[fog-motif|fog]] and sleet or January

[022-005] with ice and snow, but they have their merits in the sultry long

[022-006] vacation weather. They enable Allegory, though it has cheeks like

[022-007] peaches, and knees like bunches of blossoms, and rosy swellings for

[022-008] calves to its legs and muscles to its arms, to look tolerably cool

[022-009] to-night.

[022-010] Plenty of dust comes in at Mr. Tulkinghorn’s windows, and plenty more

[022-011] has generated among his furniture and papers. It lies thick

[022-012] everywhere. When a breeze from the country that has lost its way

[022-013] takes fright and makes a blind hurry to rush out again, it flings as

[022-014] much dust in the eyes of Allegory as the law—or Mr. Tulkinghorn, one

[022-015] of its trustiest representatives—may scatter, on occasion, in the

[022-016] eyes of the laity.

[022-017] In his lowering magazine of dust, the universal article into which

[022-018] his papers and himself, and all his clients, and all things of earth,

[022-019] animate and inanimate, are resolving, Mr. Tulkinghorn sits at one of

[022-020] the open windows enjoying a bottle of old port. Though a hard-grained

[022-021] man, close, dry, and silent, he can enjoy old wine with the best. He

[022-022] has a priceless bin of port in some artful cellar under the Fields,

[022-023] which is one of his many secrets. When he dines alone in chambers, as

[022-024] he has dined to-day, and has his bit of fish and his steak or chicken

[022-025] brought in from the coffee-house, he descends with a candle to the

[022-026] echoing regions below the deserted mansion, and heralded by a remote

[022-027] reverberation of thundering doors, comes gravely back encircled by an

[022-028] earthy atmosphere and carrying a bottle from which he pours a radiant

[022-029] nectar, two score and ten years old, that blushes in the glass to

[022-030] find itself so famous and fills the whole room with the fragrance of

[022-031] southern grapes.

[022-032] Mr. Tulkinghorn, sitting in the twilight by the open window, enjoys

[022-033] his wine. As if it whispered to him of its fifty years of silence and

[022-034] seclusion, it shuts him up the closer. More impenetrable than ever,

[022-035] he sits, and drinks, and mellows as it were in [[secret-and-shame|secrecy]], pondering at

[022-036] that twilight hour on all the mysteries he knows, associated with

[022-037] darkening woods in the country, and vast blank shut-up houses in

[022-038] town, and perhaps sparing a thought or two for himself, and his

[022-039] family history, and his money, and his will—all a mystery to every

[022-040] one—and that one bachelor friend of his, a man of the same mould and

[022-041] a lawyer too, who lived the same kind of life until he was

[022-042] seventy-five years old, and then suddenly conceiving (as it is

[022-043] supposed) an impression that it was too monotonous, gave his gold

[022-044] watch to his hair-dresser one summer evening and walked leisurely

[022-045] home to the Temple and hanged himself.

[022-046] But Mr. Tulkinghorn is not alone to-night to ponder at his usual

[022-047] length. Seated at the same table, though with his chair modestly and

[022-048] uncomfortably drawn a little way from it, sits a bald, mild, shining

[022-049] man who coughs respectfully behind his hand when the lawyer bids him

[022-050] fill his glass.

[022-051] “Now, Snagsby,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, “to go over this odd story

[022-052] again.”

[022-053] “If you please, sir.”

[022-054] “You told me when you were so good as to step round here last

[022-055] night—”

[022-056] “For which I must ask you to excuse me if it was a liberty, sir; but

[022-057] I remember that you had taken a sort of an interest in that person,

[022-058] and I thought it possible that you might—just—wish—to—”

[022-059] Mr. Tulkinghorn is not the man to help him to any conclusion or to

[022-060] admit anything as to any possibility concerning himself. So Mr.

[022-061] Snagsby trails off into saying, with an awkward cough, “I must ask

[022-062] you to excuse the liberty, sir, I am sure.”

[022-063] “Not at all,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn. “You told me, Snagsby, that you

[022-064] put on your hat and came round without mentioning your intention to

[022-065] your wife. That was prudent I think, because it’s not a matter of

[022-066] such importance that it requires to be mentioned.”

[022-067] “Well, sir,” returns Mr. Snagsby, “you see, my little woman is—not

[022-068] to put too fine a point upon it—inquisitive. She’s inquisitive. Poor

[022-069] little thing, she’s liable to spasms, and it’s good for her to have

[022-070] her mind employed. In consequence of which she employs it—I should

[022-071] say upon every individual thing she can lay hold of, whether it

[022-072] concerns her or not—especially not. My little woman has a very

[022-073] active mind, sir.”

[022-074] Mr. Snagsby drinks and murmurs with an admiring cough behind his

[022-075] hand, “Dear me, very fine wine indeed!”

[022-076] “Therefore you kept your visit to yourself last night?” says Mr.

[022-077] Tulkinghorn. “And to-night too?”

[022-078] “Yes, sir, and to-night, too. My little woman is at present in—not

[022-079] to put too fine a point on it—in a pious state, or in what she

[022-080] considers such, and attends the Evening Exertions (which is the name

[022-081] they go by) of a reverend party of the name of Chadband. He has a

[022-082] great deal of eloquence at his command, undoubtedly, but I am not

[022-083] quite favourable to his style myself. That’s neither here nor there.

[022-084] My little woman being engaged in that way made it easier for me to

[022-085] step round in a quiet manner.”

[022-086] Mr. Tulkinghorn assents. “Fill your glass, Snagsby.”

[022-087] “Thank you, sir, I am sure,” returns the stationer with his cough of

[022-088] deference. “This is wonderfully fine wine, sir!”

[022-089] “It is a rare wine now,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn. “It is fifty years

[022-090] old.”

[022-091] “Is it indeed, sir? But I am not surprised to hear it, I am sure. It

[022-092] might be—any age almost.” After rendering this general tribute to

[022-093] the port, Mr. Snagsby in his modesty coughs an apology behind his

[022-094] hand for drinking anything so precious.

[022-095] “Will you run over, once again, what the boy said?” asks Mr.

[022-096] Tulkinghorn, putting his hands into the pockets of his rusty

[022-097] smallclothes and leaning quietly back in his chair.

[022-098] “With pleasure, sir.”

[022-099] Then, with fidelity, though with some prolixity, the law-stationer

[022-100] repeats [[jo-crossing-sweeper|Jo]]’s statement made to the assembled guests at his house. On

[022-101] coming to the end of his narrative, he gives a great start and breaks

[022-102] off with, “Dear me, sir, I wasn’t aware there was any other gentleman

[022-103] present!”

[022-104] Mr. Snagsby is dismayed to see, standing with an attentive face

[022-105] between himself and the lawyer at a little distance from the table, a

[022-106] person with a hat and stick in his hand who was not there when he

[022-107] himself came in and has not since entered by the door or by either of

[022-108] the windows. There is a press in the room, but its hinges have not

[022-109] creaked, nor has a step been audible upon the floor. Yet this third

[022-110] person stands there with his attentive face, and his hat and stick in

[022-111] his hands, and his hands behind him, a composed and quiet listener.

[022-112] He is a stoutly built, steady-looking, sharp-eyed man in black, of

[022-113] about the middle-age. Except that he looks at Mr. Snagsby as if he

[022-114] were going to take his portrait, there is nothing remarkable about

[022-115] him at first sight but his ghostly manner of appearing.

[022-116] “Don’t mind this gentleman,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn in his quiet way.

[022-117] “This is only Mr. Bucket.”

[022-118] “Oh, indeed, sir?” returns the stationer, expressing by a cough that

[022-119] he is quite in the dark as to who Mr. Bucket may be.

[022-120] “I wanted him to hear this story,” says the lawyer, “because I have

[022-121] half a mind (for a reason) to know more of it, and he is very

[022-122] intelligent in such things. What do you say to this, Bucket?”

[022-123] “It’s very plain, sir. Since our people have moved this boy on, and

[022-124] he’s not to be found on his old lay, if Mr. Snagsby don’t object to

[022-125] go down with me to Tom-all-Alone’s and point him out, we can have him

[022-126] here in less than a couple of hours’ time. I can do it without Mr.

[022-127] Snagsby, of course, but this is the shortest way.”

[022-128] “Mr. Bucket is a detective officer, Snagsby,” says the lawyer in

[022-129] explanation.

[022-130] “Is he indeed, sir?” says Mr. Snagsby with a strong tendency in his

[022-131] clump of hair to stand on end.

[022-132] “And if you have no real objection to accompany Mr. Bucket to the

[022-133] place in question,” pursues the lawyer, “I shall feel obliged to you

[022-134] if you will do so.”

[022-135] In a moment’s hesitation on the part of Mr. Snagsby, Bucket dips down

[022-136] to the bottom of his mind.

[022-137] “Don’t you be afraid of hurting the boy,” he says. “You won’t do

[022-138] that. It’s all right as far as the boy’s concerned. We shall only

[022-139] bring him here to ask him a question or so I want to put to him, and

[022-140] he’ll be paid for his trouble and sent away again. It’ll be a good

[022-141] job for him. I promise you, as a man, that you shall see the boy sent

[022-142] away all right. Don’t you be afraid of hurting him; you an’t going to

[022-143] do that.”

[022-144] “Very well, Mr. Tulkinghorn!” cries Mr. Snagsby cheerfully. And

[022-145] reassured, “Since that’s the case—”

[022-146] “Yes! And lookee here, Mr. Snagsby,” resumes Bucket, taking him aside

[022-147] by the arm, tapping him familiarly on the breast, and speaking in a

[022-148] confidential tone. “You’re a man of the world, you know, and a man of

[022-149] business, and a man of sense. That’s what YOU are.”

[022-150] “I am sure I am much obliged to you for your good opinion,” returns

[022-151] the stationer with his cough of modesty, “but—”

[022-152] “That’s what YOU are, you know,” says Bucket. “Now, it an’t necessary

[022-153] to say to a man like you, engaged in your business, which is a

[022-154] business of trust and requires a person to be wide awake and have his

[022-155] senses about him and his head screwed on tight (I had an uncle in

[022-156] your business once)—it an’t necessary to say to a man like you that

[022-157] it’s the best and wisest way to keep little matters like this quiet.

[022-158] Don’t you see? Quiet!”

[022-159] “Certainly, certainly,” returns the other.

[022-160] “I don’t mind telling YOU,” says Bucket with an engaging appearance

[022-161] of frankness, “that as far as I can understand it, there seems to be

[022-162] a doubt whether this dead person wasn’t entitled to a little

[022-163] [[inheritance-and-property|property]], and whether this female hasn’t been up to some games

[022-164] respecting that property, don’t you see?”

[022-165] “Oh!” says Mr. Snagsby, but not appearing to see quite distinctly.

[022-166] “Now, what YOU want,” pursues Bucket, again tapping Mr. Snagsby on

[022-167] the breast in a comfortable and soothing manner, “is that every

[022-168] person should have their rights according to justice. That’s what YOU

[022-169] want.”

[022-170] “To be sure,” returns Mr. Snagsby with a nod.

[022-171] “On account of which, and at the same time to oblige a—do you call

[022-172] it, in your business, customer or client? I forget how my uncle used

[022-173] to call it.”

[022-174] “Why, I generally say customer myself,” replies Mr. Snagsby.

[022-175] “You’re right!” returns Mr. Bucket, shaking hands with him quite

[022-176] affectionately. “—On account of which, and at the same time to

[022-177] oblige a real good customer, you mean to go down with me, in

[022-178] confidence, to Tom-all-Alone’s and to keep the whole thing quiet ever

[022-179] afterwards and never mention it to any one. That’s about your

[022-180] intentions, if I understand you?”

[022-181] “You are right, sir. You are right,” says Mr. Snagsby.

[022-182] “Then here’s your hat,” returns his new friend, quite as intimate

[022-183] with it as if he had made it; “and if you’re ready, I am.”

[022-184] They leave Mr. Tulkinghorn, without a ruffle on the surface of his

[022-185] unfathomable depths, drinking his old wine, and go down into the

[022-186] streets.

[022-187] “You don’t happen to know a very good sort of person of the name of

[022-188] Gridley, do you?” says Bucket in friendly converse as they descend

[022-189] the stairs.

[022-190] “No,” says Mr. Snagsby, considering, “I don’t know anybody of that

[022-191] name. Why?”

[022-192] “Nothing particular,” says Bucket; “only having allowed his temper to

[022-193] get a little the better of him and having been threatening some

[022-194] respectable people, he is keeping out of the way of a warrant I have

[022-195] got against him—which it’s a pity that a man of sense should do.”

[022-196] As they walk along, Mr. Snagsby observes, as a novelty, that however

[022-197] quick their pace may be, his companion still seems in some

[022-198] undefinable manner to lurk and lounge; also, that whenever he is

[022-199] going to turn to the right or left, he pretends to have a fixed

[022-200] purpose in his mind of going straight ahead, and wheels off, sharply,

[022-201] at the very last moment. Now and then, when they pass a

[022-202] police-constable on his beat, Mr. Snagsby notices that both the

[022-203] constable and his guide fall into a deep abstraction as they come

[022-204] towards each other, and appear entirely to overlook each other, and

[022-205] to gaze into space. In a few instances, Mr. Bucket, coming behind

[022-206] some under-sized young man with a shining hat on, and his sleek hair

[022-207] twisted into one flat curl on each side of his head, almost without

[022-208] glancing at him touches him with his stick, upon which the young man,

[022-209] looking round, instantly evaporates. For the most part Mr. Bucket

[022-210] notices things in general, with a face as unchanging as the great

[022-211] mourning ring on his little finger or the brooch, composed of not

[022-212] much diamond and a good deal of setting, which he wears in his shirt.

[022-213] When they come at last to Tom-all-Alone’s, Mr. Bucket stops for a

[022-214] moment at the corner and takes a lighted bull’s-eye from the

[022-215] constable on duty there, who then accompanies him with his own

[022-216] particular bull’s-eye at his waist. Between his two conductors, Mr.

[022-217] Snagsby passes along the middle of a villainous street, undrained,

[022-218] unventilated, deep in black mud and corrupt water—though the roads

[022-219] are dry elsewhere—and reeking with such smells and sights that he,

[022-220] who has lived in [[london|London]] all his life, can scarce believe his senses.

[022-221] Branching from this street and its heaps of ruins are other streets

[022-222] and courts so infamous that Mr. Snagsby sickens in body and mind and

[022-223] feels as if he were going every moment deeper down into the infernal

[022-224] gulf.

[022-225] “Draw off a bit here, Mr. Snagsby,” says Bucket as a kind of shabby

[022-226] palanquin is borne towards them, surrounded by a noisy crowd. “Here’s

[022-227] the fever coming up the street!”

[022-228] As the unseen wretch goes by, the crowd, leaving that object of

[022-229] attraction, hovers round the three visitors like a dream of horrible

[022-230] faces and fades away up alleys and into ruins and behind walls, and

[022-231] with occasional cries and shrill whistles of warning, thenceforth

[022-232] flits about them until they leave the place.

[022-233] “Are those the fever-houses, Darby?” Mr. Bucket coolly asks as he

[022-234] turns his bull’s-eye on a line of stinking ruins.

[022-235] Darby replies that “all them are,” and further that in all, for

[022-236] months and months, the people “have been down by dozens” and have

[022-237] been carried out dead and dying “like sheep with the rot.” Bucket

[022-238] observing to Mr. Snagsby as they go on again that he looks a little

[022-239] poorly, Mr. Snagsby answers that he feels as if he couldn’t breathe

[022-240] the dreadful air.

[022-241] There is inquiry made at various houses for a boy named Jo. As few

[022-242] people are known in Tom-all-Alone’s by any Christian sign, there is

[022-243] much reference to Mr. Snagsby whether he means Carrots, or the

[022-244] Colonel, or Gallows, or Young Chisel, or Terrier Tip, or Lanky, or

[022-245] the Brick. Mr. Snagsby describes over and over again. There are

[022-246] conflicting opinions respecting the original of his picture. Some

[022-247] think it must be Carrots, some say the Brick. The Colonel is

[022-248] produced, but is not at all near the thing. Whenever Mr. Snagsby and

[022-249] his conductors are stationary, the crowd flows round, and from its

[022-250] squalid depths obsequious advice heaves up to Mr. Bucket. Whenever

[022-251] they move, and the angry bull’s-eyes glare, it fades away and flits

[022-252] about them up the alleys, and in the ruins, and behind the walls, as

[022-253] before.

[022-254] At last there is a lair found out where Toughy, or the Tough Subject,

[022-255] lays him down at night; and it is thought that the Tough Subject may

[022-256] be Jo. Comparison of notes between Mr. Snagsby and the proprietress

[022-257] of the house—a drunken face tied up in a black bundle, and flaring

[022-258] out of a heap of rags on the floor of a dog-hutch which is her

[022-259] private apartment—leads to the establishment of this conclusion.

[022-260] Toughy has gone to the doctor’s to get a bottle of stuff for a sick

[022-261] woman but will be here anon.

[022-262] “And who have we got here to-night?” says Mr. Bucket, opening another

[022-263] door and glaring in with his bull’s-eye. “Two drunken men, eh? And

[022-264] two women? The men are sound enough,” turning back each sleeper’s arm

[022-265] from his face to look at him. “Are these your good men, my dears?”

[022-266] “Yes, sir,” returns one of the women. “They are our husbands.”

[022-267] “Brickmakers, eh?”

[022-268] “Yes, sir.”

[022-269] “What are you doing here? You don’t belong to London.”

[022-270] “No, sir. We belong to Hertfordshire.”

[022-271] “Whereabouts in Hertfordshire?”

[022-272] “Saint Albans.”

[022-273] “Come up on the tramp?”

[022-274] “We walked up yesterday. There’s no work down with us at present, but

[022-275] we have done no good by coming here, and shall do none, I expect.”

[022-276] “That’s not the way to do much good,” says Mr. Bucket, turning his

[022-277] head in the direction of the unconscious figures on the ground.

[022-278] “It an’t indeed,” replies the woman with a sigh. “Jenny and me knows

[022-279] it full well.”

[022-280] The room, though two or three feet higher than the door, is so low

[022-281] that the head of the tallest of the visitors would touch the

[022-282] blackened ceiling if he stood upright. It is offensive to every

[022-283] sense; even the gross candle burns pale and sickly in the polluted

[022-284] air. There are a couple of benches and a higher bench by way of

[022-285] table. The men lie asleep where they stumbled down, but the women sit

[022-286] by the candle. Lying in the arms of the woman who has spoken is a

[022-287] very young child.

[022-288] “Why, what age do you call that little creature?” says Bucket. “It

[022-289] looks as if it was born yesterday.” He is not at all rough about it;

[022-290] and as he turns his light gently on the infant, Mr. Snagsby is

[022-291] strangely reminded of another infant, encircled with light, that he

[022-292] has seen in pictures.

[022-293] “He is not three weeks old yet, sir,” says the woman.

[022-294] “Is he your child?”

[022-295] “Mine.”

[022-296] The other woman, who was bending over it when they came in, stoops

[022-297] down again and kisses it as it lies asleep.

[022-298] “You seem as fond of it as if you were the mother yourself,” says Mr.

[022-299] Bucket.

[022-300] “I was the mother of one like it, master, and it died.”

[022-301] “Ah, Jenny, Jenny!” says the other woman to her. “Better so. Much

[022-302] better to think of dead than alive, Jenny! Much better!”

[022-303] “Why, you an’t such an unnatural woman, I hope,” returns Bucket

[022-304] sternly, “as to wish your own child dead?”

[022-305] “God knows you are right, master,” she returns. “I am not. I’d stand

[022-306] between it and death with my own life if I could, as true as any

[022-307] pretty lady.”

[022-308] “Then don’t talk in that wrong manner,” says Mr. Bucket, mollified

[022-309] again. “Why do you do it?”

[022-310] “It’s brought into my head, master,” returns the woman, her eyes

[022-311] filling with tears, “when I look down at the child lying so. If it

[022-312] was never to wake no more, you’d think me mad, I should take on so. I

[022-313] know that very well. I was with Jenny when she lost hers—warn’t I,

[022-314] Jenny?—and I know how she grieved. But look around you at this

[022-315] place. Look at them,” glancing at the sleepers on the ground. “Look

[022-316] at the boy you’re waiting for, who’s gone out to do me a good turn.

[022-317] Think of the children that your business lays with often and often,

[022-318] and that YOU see grow up!”

[022-319] “Well, well,” says Mr. Bucket, “you train him respectable, and he’ll

[022-320] be a comfort to you, and look after you in your old age, you know.”

[022-321] “I mean to try hard,” she answers, wiping her eyes. “But I have been

[022-322] a-thinking, being over-tired to-night and not well with the ague, of

[022-323] all the many things that’ll come in his way. My master will be

[022-324] against it, and he’ll be beat, and see me beat, and made to fear his

[022-325] home, and perhaps to stray wild. If I work for him ever so much, and

[022-326] ever so hard, there’s no one to help me; and if he should be turned

[022-327] bad ‘spite of all I could do, and the time should come when I should

[022-328] sit by him in his sleep, made hard and changed, an’t it likely I

[022-329] should think of him as he lies in my lap now and wish he had died as

[022-330] Jenny’s child died!”

[022-331] “There, there!” says Jenny. “Liz, you’re tired and ill. Let me take

[022-332] him.”

[022-333] In doing so, she displaces the mother’s dress, but quickly readjusts

[022-334] it over the wounded and bruised bosom where the baby has been lying.

[022-335] “It’s my dead child,” says Jenny, walking up and down as she nurses,

[022-336] “that makes me love this child so dear, and it’s my dead child that

[022-337] makes her love it so dear too, as even to think of its being taken

[022-338] away from her now. While she thinks that, I think what fortune would

[022-339] I give to have my darling back. But we mean the same thing, if we

[022-340] knew how to say it, us two mothers does in our poor hearts!”

[022-341] As Mr. Snagsby blows his nose and coughs his cough of sympathy, a

[022-342] step is heard without. Mr. Bucket throws his light into the doorway

[022-343] and says to Mr. Snagsby, “Now, what do you say to Toughy? Will HE

[022-344] do?”

[022-345] “That’s Jo,” says Mr. Snagsby.

[022-346] Jo stands amazed in the disk of light, like a ragged figure in a

[022-347] magic-lantern, trembling to think that he has offended against the

[022-348] law in not having moved on far enough. Mr. Snagsby, however, giving

[022-349] him the consolatory assurance, “It’s only a job you will be paid for,

[022-350] Jo,” he recovers; and on being taken outside by Mr. Bucket for a

[022-351] little private confabulation, tells his tale satisfactorily, though

[022-352] out of breath.

[022-353] “I have squared it with the lad,” says Mr. Bucket, returning, “and

[022-354] it’s all right. Now, Mr. Snagsby, we’re ready for you.”

[022-355] First, Jo has to complete his errand of good nature by handing over

[022-356] the physic he has been to get, which he delivers with the laconic

[022-357] verbal direction that “it’s to be all took d’rectly.” Secondly, Mr.

[022-358] Snagsby has to lay upon the table half a crown, his usual panacea for

[022-359] an immense variety of afflictions. Thirdly, Mr. Bucket has to take Jo

[022-360] by the arm a little above the elbow and walk him on before him,

[022-361] without which observance neither the Tough Subject nor any other

[022-362] Subject could be professionally conducted to Lincoln’s Inn Fields.

[022-363] These arrangements completed, they give the women good night and come

[022-364] out once more into black and foul Tom-all-Alone’s.

[022-365] By the noisome ways through which they descended into that pit, they

[022-366] gradually emerge from it, the crowd flitting, and whistling, and

[022-367] skulking about them until they come to the verge, where restoration
