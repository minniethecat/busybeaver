[012-001] It has left off raining down in Lincolnshire at last, and Chesney

[012-002] Wold has taken heart. Mrs. Rouncewell is full of hospitable cares,

[012-003] for [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester]] and my Lady are coming home from Paris. The

[012-004] fashionable intelligence has found it out and communicates the glad

[012-005] tidings to benighted England. It has also found out that they will

[012-006] entertain a brilliant and distinguished circle of the ELITE of the

[012-007] BEAU MONDE (the fashionable intelligence is weak in English, but a

[012-008] giant refreshed in French) at the ancient and hospitable family seat

[012-009] in Lincolnshire.

[012-010] For the greater honour of the brilliant and distinguished circle, and

[012-011] of [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]] into the bargain, the broken arch of the bridge in

[012-012] the park is mended; and the water, now retired within its proper

[012-013] limits and again spanned gracefully, makes a figure in the prospect

[012-014] from the house. The clear, cold sunshine glances into the brittle

[012-015] woods and approvingly beholds the sharp wind scattering the leaves

[012-016] and drying the moss. It glides over the park after the moving shadows

[012-017] of the clouds, and chases them, and never catches them, all day. It

[012-018] looks in at the windows and touches the ancestral portraits with bars

[012-019] and patches of brightness never contemplated by the painters. Athwart

[012-020] the picture of my Lady, over the great chimney-piece, it throws a

[012-021] broad bend-sinister of light that strikes down crookedly into the

[012-022] hearth and seems to rend it.

[012-023] Through the same cold sunshine and the same sharp wind, my Lady and

[012-024] Sir Leicester, in their travelling chariot (my Lady’s woman and Sir

[012-025] Leicester’s man affectionate in the rumble), start for home. With a

[012-026] considerable amount of jingling and whip-cracking, and many plunging

[012-027] demonstrations on the part of two bare-backed horses and two centaurs

[012-028] with glazed hats, jack-boots, and flowing manes and tails, they

[012-029] rattle out of the yard of the Hôtel Bristol in the Place Vendôme and

[012-030] canter between the sun-and-shadow-chequered colonnade of the Rue de

[012-031] Rivoli and the garden of the ill-fated palace of a headless king and

[012-032] queen, off by the Place of Concord, and the Elysian Fields, and the

[012-033] Gate of the Star, out of Paris.

[012-034] Sooth to say, they cannot go away too fast, for even here my Lady

[012-035] Dedlock has been bored to death. Concert, assembly, opera, theatre,

[012-036] drive, nothing is new to my Lady under the worn-out heavens. Only

[012-037] last Sunday, when poor wretches were gay—within the walls playing

[012-038] with children among the clipped trees and the statues in the Palace

[012-039] Garden; walking, a score abreast, in the Elysian Fields, made more

[012-040] Elysian by performing dogs and wooden horses; between whiles

[012-041] filtering (a few) through the gloomy Cathedral of Our Lady to say a

[012-042] word or two at the base of a pillar within flare of a rusty little

[012-043] gridiron-full of gusty little tapers; without the walls encompassing

[012-044] Paris with dancing, love-making, wine-drinking, tobacco-smoking,

[012-045] tomb-visiting, billiard card and domino playing, quack-doctoring, and

[012-046] much murderous refuse, animate and inanimate—only last Sunday, my

[012-047] Lady, in the desolation of Boredom and the clutch of Giant Despair,

[012-048] almost hated her own maid for being in spirits.

[012-049] She cannot, therefore, go too fast from Paris. Weariness of soul lies

[012-050] before her, as it lies behind—her Ariel has put a girdle of it round

[012-051] the whole earth, and it cannot be unclasped—but the imperfect remedy

[012-052] is always to fly from the last place where it has been experienced.

[012-053] Fling Paris back into the distance, then, exchanging it for endless

[012-054] avenues and cross-avenues of wintry trees! And, when next beheld, let

[012-055] it be some leagues away, with the Gate of the Star a white speck

[012-056] glittering in the sun, and the city a mere mound in a plain—two dark

[012-057] square towers rising out of it, and light and shadow descending on it

[012-058] aslant, like the angels in Jacob’s dream!

[012-059] Sir Leicester is generally in a complacent state, and rarely bored.

[012-060] When he has nothing else to do, he can always contemplate his own

[012-061] greatness. It is a considerable advantage to a man to have so

[012-062] inexhaustible a subject. After reading his [[document-and-letter|letters]], he leans back in

[012-063] his corner of the carriage and generally reviews his importance to

[012-064] society.

[012-065] “You have an unusual amount of correspondence this morning?” says my

[012-066] Lady after a long time. She is fatigued with reading. Has almost read

[012-067] a page in twenty miles.

[012-068] “Nothing in it, though. Nothing whatever.”

[012-069] “I saw one of [[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]]’s long effusions, I think?”

[012-070] “You see everything,” says Sir Leicester with admiration.

[012-071] “Ha!” sighs my Lady. “He is the most tiresome of men!”

[012-072] “He sends—I really beg your pardon—he sends,” says Sir Leicester,

[012-073] selecting the letter and unfolding it, “a message to you. Our

[012-074] stopping to change horses as I came to his postscript drove it out of

[012-075] my memory. I beg you’ll excuse me. He says—” Sir Leicester is so

[012-076] long in taking out his eye-glass and adjusting it that my Lady looks

[012-077] a little irritated. “He says ‘In the matter of the right of way—’ I

[012-078] beg your pardon, that’s not the place. He says—yes! Here I have it!

[012-079] He says, ‘I beg my respectful compliments to my Lady, who, I hope,

[012-080] has benefited by the change. Will you do me the favour to mention (as

[012-081] it may interest her) that I have something to tell her on her return

[012-082] in reference to the person who copied the affidavit in the [[chancery-court|Chancery]]

[012-083] suit, which so powerfully stimulated her curiosity. I have seen

[012-084] him.’”

[012-085] My Lady, leaning forward, looks out of her window.

[012-086] “That’s the message,” observes Sir Leicester.

[012-087] “I should like to walk a little,” says my Lady, still looking out of

[012-088] her window.

[012-089] “Walk?” repeats Sir Leicester in a tone of surprise.

[012-090] “I should like to walk a little,” says my Lady with unmistakable

[012-091] distinctness. “Please to stop the carriage.”

[012-092] The carriage is stopped, the affectionate man alights from the

[012-093] rumble, opens the door, and lets down the steps, obedient to an

[012-094] impatient motion of my Lady’s hand. My Lady alights so quickly and

[012-095] walks away so quickly that Sir Leicester, for all his scrupulous

[012-096] politeness, is unable to assist her, and is left behind. A space of a

[012-097] minute or two has elapsed before he comes up with her. She smiles,

[012-098] looks very handsome, takes his arm, lounges with him for a quarter of

[012-099] a mile, is very much bored, and resumes her seat in the carriage.

[012-100] The rattle and clatter continue through the greater part of three

[012-101] days, with more or less of bell-jingling and whip-cracking, and more

[012-102] or less plunging of centaurs and bare-backed horses. Their courtly

[012-103] politeness to each other at the hotels where they tarry is the theme

[012-104] of general admiration. Though my Lord IS a little aged for my Lady,

[012-105] says Madame, the hostess of the Golden Ape, and though he might be

[012-106] her amiable father, one can see at a glance that they love each

[012-107] other. One observes my Lord with his white hair, standing, hat in

[012-108] hand, to help my Lady to and from the carriage. One observes my Lady,

[012-109] how recognisant of my Lord’s politeness, with an inclination of her

[012-110] gracious head and the concession of her so-genteel fingers! It is

[012-111] ravishing!

[012-112] The sea has no appreciation of great men, but knocks them about like

[012-113] the small fry. It is habitually hard upon Sir Leicester, whose

[012-114] countenance it greenly mottles in the manner of sage-cheese and in

[012-115] whose aristocratic system it effects a dismal revolution. It is the

[012-116] Radical of Nature to him. Nevertheless, his dignity gets over it

[012-117] after stopping to refit, and he goes on with my Lady for Chesney

[012-118] Wold, lying only one night in [[london|London]] on the way to Lincolnshire.

[012-119] Through the same cold sunlight, colder as the day declines, and

[012-120] through the same sharp wind, sharper as the separate shadows of bare

[012-121] trees gloom together in the woods, and as the Ghost’s Walk, touched

[012-122] at the western corner by a pile of fire in the sky, resigns itself to

[012-123] coming night, they drive into the park. The rooks, swinging in their

[012-124] lofty houses in the elm-tree avenue, seem to discuss the question of

[012-125] the occupancy of the carriage as it passes underneath, some agreeing

[012-126] that Sir Leicester and my Lady are come down, some arguing with

[012-127] malcontents who won’t admit it, now all consenting to consider the

[012-128] question disposed of, now all breaking out again in violent debate,

[012-129] incited by one obstinate and drowsy bird who will persist in putting

[012-130] in a last contradictory croak. Leaving them to swing and caw, the

[012-131] travelling chariot rolls on to the house, where fires gleam warmly

[012-132] through some of the windows, though not through so many as to give an

[012-133] inhabited expression to the darkening mass of front. But the

[012-134] brilliant and distinguished circle will soon do that.

[012-135] Mrs. Rouncewell is in attendance and receives Sir Leicester’s

[012-136] customary shake of the hand with a profound curtsy.

[012-137] “How do you do, Mrs. Rouncewell? I am glad to see you.”

[012-138] “I hope I have the honour of welcoming you in good health, Sir

[012-139] Leicester?”

[012-140] “In excellent health, Mrs. Rouncewell.”

[012-141] “My Lady is looking charmingly well,” says Mrs. Rouncewell with

[012-142] another curtsy.

[012-143] My Lady signifies, without profuse expenditure of words, that she is

[012-144] as wearily well as she can hope to be.

[012-145] But Rosa is in the distance, behind the housekeeper; and my Lady, who

[012-146] has not subdued the quickness of her observation, whatever else she

[012-147] may have conquered, asks, “Who is that girl?”

[012-148] “A young scholar of mine, my Lady. Rosa.”

[012-149] “Come here, Rosa!” [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]] beckons her, with even an appearance

[012-150] of interest. “Why, do you know how pretty you are, child?” she says,

[012-151] touching her shoulder with her two forefingers.

[012-152] Rosa, very much abashed, says, “No, if you please, my Lady!” and

[012-153] glances up, and glances down, and don’t know where to look, but looks

[012-154] all the prettier.

[012-155] “How old are you?”

[012-156] “Nineteen, my Lady.”

[012-157] “Nineteen,” repeats my Lady thoughtfully. “Take care they don’t spoil

[012-158] you by flattery.”

[012-159] “Yes, my Lady.”

[012-160] My Lady taps her dimpled cheek with the same delicate gloved fingers

[012-161] and goes on to the foot of the oak staircase, where Sir Leicester

[012-162] pauses for her as her knightly escort. A staring old Dedlock in a

[012-163] panel, as large as life and as dull, looks as if he didn’t know what

[012-164] to make of it, which was probably his general state of mind in the

[012-165] days of Queen Elizabeth.

[012-166] That evening, in the housekeeper’s room, Rosa can do nothing but

[012-167] murmur Lady Dedlock’s praises. She is so affable, so graceful, so

[012-168] beautiful, so elegant; has such a sweet voice and such a thrilling

[012-169] touch that Rosa can feel it yet! Mrs. Rouncewell confirms all this,

[012-170] not without personal pride, reserving only the one point of

[012-171] affability. Mrs. Rouncewell is not quite sure as to that. Heaven

[012-172] forbid that she should say a syllable in dispraise of any member of

[012-173] that excellent family, above all, of my Lady, whom the whole world

[012-174] admires; but if my Lady would only be “a little more free,” not quite

[012-175] so cold and distant, Mrs. Rouncewell thinks she would be more

[012-176] affable.

[012-177] “’Tis almost a pity,” Mrs. Rouncewell adds—only “almost” because it

[012-178] borders on impiety to suppose that anything could be better than it

[012-179] is, in such an express dispensation as the Dedlock affairs—“that my

[012-180] Lady has no family. If she had had a daughter now, a grown young

[012-181] lady, to interest her, I think she would have had the only kind of

[012-182] excellence she wants.”

[012-183] “Might not that have made her still more proud, grandmother?” says

[012-184] Watt, who has been home and come back again, he is such a good

[012-185] grandson.

[012-186] “More and most, my dear,” returns the housekeeper with dignity, “are

[012-187] words it’s not my place to use—nor so much as to hear—applied to

[012-188] any drawback on my Lady.”

[012-189] “I beg your pardon, grandmother. But she is proud, is she not?”

[012-190] “If she is, she has reason to be. The Dedlock family have always

[012-191] reason to be.”

[012-192] “Well,” says Watt, “it’s to be hoped they line out of their

[012-193] prayer-books a certain passage for the common people about pride and

[012-194] vainglory. Forgive me, grandmother! Only a joke!”

[012-195] “Sir Leicester and Lady Dedlock, my dear, are not fit subjects for

[012-196] joking.”

[012-197] “Sir Leicester is no joke by any means,” says Watt, “and I humbly ask

[012-198] his pardon. I suppose, grandmother, that even with the family and

[012-199] their guests down here, there is no objection to my prolonging my

[012-200] stay at the Dedlock Arms for a day or two, as any other traveller

[012-201] might?”

[012-202] “Surely, none in the world, child.”

[012-203] “I am glad of that,” says Watt, “because I have an inexpressible

[012-204] desire to extend my knowledge of this beautiful neighbourhood.”

[012-205] He happens to glance at Rosa, who looks down and is very shy indeed.

[012-206] But according to the old superstition, it should be Rosa’s ears that

[012-207] burn, and not her fresh bright cheeks, for my Lady’s maid is holding

[012-208] forth about her at this moment with surpassing energy.

[012-209] My Lady’s maid is a Frenchwoman of two and thirty, from somewhere in

[012-210] the southern country about Avignon and Marseilles, a large-eyed brown

[012-211] woman with black hair who would be handsome but for a certain feline

[012-212] mouth and general uncomfortable tightness of face, rendering the jaws

[012-213] too eager and the skull too prominent. There is something indefinably

[012-214] keen and wan about her anatomy, and she has a watchful way of looking

[012-215] out of the corners of her eyes without turning her head which could

[012-216] be pleasantly dispensed with, especially when she is in an ill humour

[012-217] and near knives. Through all the good taste of her dress and little

[012-218] adornments, these objections so express themselves that she seems to

[012-219] go about like a very neat she-wolf imperfectly tamed. Besides being

[012-220] accomplished in all the knowledge appertaining to her post, she is

[012-221] almost an Englishwoman in her acquaintance with the language;

[012-222] consequently, she is in no want of words to shower upon Rosa for

[012-223] having attracted my Lady’s attention, and she pours them out with

[012-224] such grim ridicule as she sits at dinner that her companion, the

[012-225] affectionate man, is rather relieved when she arrives at the spoon

[012-226] stage of that performance.

[012-227] Ha, ha, ha! She, Hortense, been in my Lady’s service since five years

[012-228] and always kept at the distance, and this doll, this puppet,

[012-229] caressed—absolutely caressed—by my Lady on the moment of her

[012-230] arriving at the house! Ha, ha, ha! “And do you know how pretty you

[012-231] are, child?” “No, my Lady.” You are right there! “And how old are

[012-232] you, child! And take care they do not spoil you by flattery, child!”

[012-233] Oh, how droll! It is the BEST thing altogether.

[012-234] In short, it is such an admirable thing that Mademoiselle Hortense

[012-235] can’t forget it; but at meals for days afterwards, even among her

[012-236] countrywomen and others attached in like capacity to the troop of

[012-237] visitors, relapses into silent enjoyment of the joke—an enjoyment

[012-238] expressed, in her own convivial manner, by an additional tightness of

[012-239] face, thin elongation of compressed lips, and sidewise look, which

[012-240] intense appreciation of humour is frequently reflected in my Lady’s

[012-241] mirrors when my Lady is not among them.

[012-242] All the mirrors in the house are brought into action now, many of

[012-243] them after a long blank. They reflect handsome faces, simpering

[012-244] faces, youthful faces, faces of threescore and ten that will not

[012-245] submit to be old; the entire collection of faces that have come to

[012-246] pass a January week or two at Chesney Wold, and which the fashionable

[012-247] intelligence, a mighty hunter before the Lord, hunts with a keen

[012-248] scent, from their breaking cover at the Court of St. James’s to their

[012-249] being run down to death. The place in Lincolnshire is all alive. By

[012-250] day guns and voices are heard ringing in the woods, horsemen and

[012-251] carriages enliven the park roads, servants and hangers-on pervade the

[012-252] village and the Dedlock Arms. Seen by night from distant openings in

[012-253] the trees, the row of windows in the long drawing-room, where my

[012-254] Lady’s picture hangs over the great chimney-piece, is like a row of

[012-255] jewels set in a black frame. On Sunday the chill little church is

[012-256] almost warmed by so much gallant company, and the general flavour of

[012-257] the Dedlock dust is quenched in delicate perfumes.

[012-258] The brilliant and distinguished circle comprehends within it no

[012-259] contracted amount of education, sense, courage, honour, beauty, and

[012-260] virtue. Yet there is something a little wrong about it in despite of

[012-261] its immense advantages. What can it be?

[012-262] Dandyism? There is no King [[george-rouncewell|George]] the Fourth now (more the pity) to

[012-263] set the dandy fashion; there are no clear-starched jack-towel

[012-264] neckcloths, no short-waisted coats, no false calves, no stays. There

[012-265] are no caricatures, now, of effeminate exquisites so arrayed,

[012-266] swooning in opera boxes with excess of delight and being revived by

[012-267] other dainty creatures poking long-necked scent-bottles at their

[012-268] noses. There is no beau whom it takes four men at once to shake into

[012-269] his buckskins, or who goes to see all the executions, or who is

[012-270] troubled with the self-reproach of having once consumed a pea. But is

[012-271] there dandyism in the brilliant and distinguished circle

[012-272] notwithstanding, dandyism of a more mischievous sort, that has got

[012-273] below the surface and is doing less harmless things than

[012-274] jack-towelling itself and stopping its own digestion, to which no

[012-275] rational person need particularly object?

[012-276] Why, yes. It cannot be disguised. There ARE at Chesney Wold this

[012-277] January week some ladies and gentlemen of the newest fashion, who

[012-278] have set up a dandyism—in religion, for instance. Who in mere

[012-279] lackadaisical want of an emotion have agreed upon a little dandy talk

[012-280] about the vulgar wanting faith in things in general, meaning in the

[012-281] things that have been tried and found wanting, as though a low fellow

[012-282] should unaccountably lose faith in a bad shilling after finding it

[012-283] out! Who would make the vulgar very picturesque and faithful by

[012-284] putting back the hands upon the clock of time and cancelling a few

[012-285] hundred years of history.

[012-286] There are also ladies and gentlemen of another fashion, not so new,

[012-287] but very elegant, who have agreed to put a smooth glaze on the world

[012-288] and to keep down all its realities. For whom everything must be

[012-289] languid and pretty. Who have found out the perpetual stoppage. Who

[012-290] are to rejoice at nothing and be sorry for nothing. Who are not to be

[012-291] disturbed by ideas. On whom even the fine arts, attending in powder

[012-292] and walking backward like the Lord Chamberlain, must array themselves

[012-293] in the milliners’ and tailors’ patterns of past generations and be

[012-294] particularly careful not to be in earnest or to receive any impress

[012-295] from the moving age.

[012-296] Then there is my Lord Boodle, of considerable reputation with his

[012-297] party, who has known what office is and who tells Sir Leicester

[012-298] Dedlock with much gravity, after dinner, that he really does not see

[012-299] to what the present age is tending. A debate is not what a debate

[012-300] used to be; the House is not what the House used to be; even a

[012-301] Cabinet is not what it formerly was. He perceives with astonishment

[012-302] that supposing the present government to be overthrown, the limited

[012-303] choice of the Crown, in the formation of a new ministry, would lie

[012-304] between Lord Coodle and Sir Thomas Doodle—supposing it to be

[012-305] impossible for the Duke of Foodle to act with Goodle, which may be

[012-306] assumed to be the case in consequence of the breach arising out of

[012-307] that affair with Hoodle. Then, giving the Home Department and the

[012-308] leadership of the House of Commons to Joodle, the Exchequer to

[012-309] Koodle, the Colonies to Loodle, and the Foreign Office to Moodle,

[012-310] what are you to do with Noodle? You can’t offer him the Presidency of

[012-311] the Council; that is reserved for Poodle. You can’t put him in the

[012-312] Woods and Forests; that is hardly good enough for Quoodle. What

[012-313] follows? That the country is shipwrecked, lost, and gone to pieces

[012-314] (as is made manifest to the patriotism of Sir Leicester Dedlock)

[012-315] because you can’t provide for Noodle!

[012-316] On the other hand, the Right Honourable William Buffy, M.P., contends

[012-317] across the table with some one else that the shipwreck of the

[012-318] country—about which there is no doubt; it is only the manner of it

[012-319] that is in question—is attributable to Cuffy. If you had done with

[012-320] Cuffy what you ought to have done when he first came into [[parliament|Parliament]],

[012-321] and had prevented him from going over to Duffy, you would have got

[012-322] him into alliance with Fuffy, you would have had with you the weight

[012-323] attaching as a smart debater to Guffy, you would have brought to bear

[012-324] upon the elections the wealth of Huffy, you would have got in for

[012-325] three counties Juffy, Kuffy, and Luffy, and you would have

[012-326] strengthened your administration by the official knowledge and the

[012-327] business habits of Muffy. All this, instead of being as you now are,

[012-328] dependent on the mere caprice of Puffy!

[012-329] As to this point, and as to some minor topics, there are differences

[012-330] of opinion; but it is perfectly clear to the brilliant and

[012-331] distinguished circle, all round, that nobody is in question but

[012-332] Boodle and his retinue, and Buffy and HIS retinue. These are the

[012-333] great actors for whom the stage is reserved. A People there are, no

[012-334] doubt—a certain large number of supernumeraries, who are to be

[012-335] occasionally addressed, and relied upon for shouts and choruses, as

[012-336] on the theatrical stage; but Boodle and Buffy, their followers and

[012-337] families, their heirs, executors, administrators, and assigns, are

[012-338] the born first-actors, managers, and leaders, and no others can

[012-339] appear upon the scene for ever and ever.

[012-340] In this, too, there is perhaps more dandyism at Chesney Wold than the

[012-341] brilliant and distinguished circle will find good for itself in the

[012-342] long run. For it is, even with the stillest and politest circles, as

[012-343] with the circle the necromancer draws around him—very strange

[012-344] appearances may be seen in active motion outside. With this

[012-345] difference, that being realities and not phantoms, there is the

[012-346] greater danger of their breaking in.

[012-347] Chesney Wold is quite full anyhow, so full that a burning sense of

[012-348] injury arises in the breasts of ill-lodged ladies’-maids, and is not

[012-349] to be extinguished. Only one room is empty. It is a turret chamber of

[012-350] the third order of merit, plainly but comfortably furnished and

[012-351] having an old-fashioned business air. It is Mr. Tulkinghorn’s room,

[012-352] and is never bestowed on anybody else, for he may come at any time.

[012-353] He is not come yet. It is his quiet habit to walk across the park

[012-354] from the village in fine weather, to drop into this room as if he had

[012-355] never been out of it since he was last seen there, to request a

[012-356] servant to inform Sir Leicester that he is arrived in case he should

[012-357] be wanted, and to appear ten minutes before dinner in the shadow of

[012-358] the library-door. He sleeps in his turret with a complaining

[012-359] flag-staff over his head, and has some leads outside on which, any

[012-360] fine morning when he is down here, his black figure may be seen

[012-361] walking before breakfast like a larger species of rook.

[012-362] Every day before dinner, my Lady looks for him in the dusk of the

[012-363] library, but he is not there. Every day at dinner, my Lady glances

[012-364] down the table for the vacant place that would be waiting to receive

[012-365] him if he had just arrived, but there is no vacant place. Every night

[012-366] my Lady casually asks her maid, “Is Mr. Tulkinghorn come?”

[012-367] Every night the answer is, “No, my Lady, not yet.”

[012-368] One night, while having her hair undressed, my Lady loses herself in

[012-369] deep thought after this reply until she sees her own brooding face in

[012-370] the opposite glass, and a pair of black eyes curiously observing her.

[012-371] “Be so good as to attend,” says my Lady then, addressing the

[012-372] reflection of Hortense, “to your business. You can contemplate your

[012-373] beauty at another time.”

[012-374] “Pardon! It was your Ladyship’s beauty.”

[012-375] “That,” says my Lady, “you needn’t contemplate at all.”
