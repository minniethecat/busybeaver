[044-001] My [[john-jarndyce|guardian]] called me into his room next morning, and then I told him

[044-002] what had been left untold on the previous night. There was nothing to

[044-003] be done, he said, but to keep the secret and to avoid another such

[044-004] encounter as that of yesterday. He understood my feeling and entirely

[044-005] shared it. He charged himself even with restraining [[harold-skimpole|Mr. Skimpole]] from

[044-006] improving his opportunity. One person whom he need not name to me, it

[044-007] was not now possible for him to advise or help. He wished it were,

[044-008] but no such thing could be. If her mistrust of the lawyer whom she

[044-009] had mentioned were well-founded, which he scarcely doubted, he

[044-010] dreaded discovery. He knew something of him, both by sight and by

[044-011] reputation, and it was certain that he was a dangerous man. Whatever

[044-012] happened, he repeatedly impressed upon me with anxious affection and

[044-013] kindness, I was as innocent of as himself and as unable to influence.

[044-014] “Nor do I understand,” said he, “that any doubts tend towards you, my

[044-015] dear. Much suspicion may exist without that connexion.”

[044-016] “With the lawyer,” I returned. “But two other persons have come into

[044-017] my mind since I have been anxious. Then I told him all about Mr.

[044-018] Guppy, who I feared might have had his vague surmises when I little

[044-019] understood his meaning, but in whose silence after our last interview

[044-020] I expressed perfect confidence.”

[044-021] “Well,” said my guardian. “Then we may dismiss him for the present.

[044-022] Who is the other?”

[044-023] I called to his recollection the French maid and the eager offer of

[044-024] herself she had made to me.

[044-025] “Ha!” he returned thoughtfully. “That is a more alarming person than

[044-026] the clerk. But after all, my dear, it was but seeking for a new

[044-027] service. She had seen you and [[ada-clare|Ada]] a little while before, and it was

[044-028] natural that you should come into her head. She merely proposed

[044-029] herself for your maid, you know. She did nothing more.”

[044-030] “Her manner was strange,” said I.

[044-031] “Yes, and her manner was strange when she took her shoes off and

[044-032] showed that cool relish for a walk that might have ended in her

[044-033] death-bed,” said my guardian. “It would be useless self-distress and

[044-034] torment to reckon up such chances and possibilities. There are very

[044-035] few harmless circumstances that would not seem full of perilous

[044-036] meaning, so considered. Be hopeful, little woman. You can be nothing

[044-037] better than yourself; be that, through this knowledge, as you were

[044-038] before you had it. It is the best you can do for everybody’s sake. I,

[044-039] sharing the secret with you—”

[044-040] “And lightening it, guardian, so much,” said I.

[044-041] “—will be attentive to what passes in that family, so far as I can

[044-042] observe it from my distance. And if the time should come when I can

[044-043] stretch out a hand to render the least service to one whom it is

[044-044] better not to name even here, I will not fail to do it for her dear

[044-045] daughter’s sake.”

[044-046] I thanked him with my whole heart. What could I ever do but thank

[044-047] him! I was going out at the door when he asked me to stay a moment.

[044-048] Quickly turning round, I saw that same expression on his face again;

[044-049] and all at once, I don’t know how, it flashed upon me as a new and

[044-050] far-off possibility that I understood it.

[044-051] “My dear [[esther-summerson|Esther]],” said my guardian, “I have long had something in my

[044-052] thoughts that I have wished to say to you.”

[044-053] “Indeed?”

[044-054] “I have had some difficulty in approaching it, and I still have. I

[044-055] should wish it to be so deliberately said, and so deliberately

[044-056] considered. Would you object to my [[document-and-letter|writing]] it?”

[044-057] “Dear guardian, how could I object to your writing anything for ME to

[044-058] read?”

[044-059] “Then see, my love,” said he with his cheery smile, “am I at this

[044-060] moment quite as plain and easy—do I seem as open, as honest and

[044-061] old-fashioned—as I am at any time?”

[044-062] I answered in all earnestness, “Quite.” With the strictest truth, for

[044-063] his momentary hesitation was gone (it had not lasted a minute), and

[044-064] his fine, sensible, cordial, sterling manner was restored.

[044-065] “Do I look as if I suppressed anything, meant anything but what I

[044-066] said, had any reservation at all, no matter what?” said he with his

[044-067] bright clear eyes on mine.

[044-068] I answered, most assuredly he did not.

[044-069] “Can you fully trust me, and thoroughly rely on what I profess,

[044-070] Esther?”

[044-071] “Most thoroughly,” said I with my whole heart.

[044-072] “My dear girl,” returned my guardian, “give me your hand.”

[044-073] He took it in his, holding me lightly with his arm, and looking down

[044-074] into my face with the same genuine freshness and faithfulness of

[044-075] manner—the old protecting manner which had made that house my home

[044-076] in a moment—said, “You have wrought changes in me, little woman,

[044-077] since the winter day in the stage-coach. First and last you have done

[044-078] me a world of good since that time.”

[044-079] “Ah, guardian, what have you done for me since that time!”

[044-080] “But,” said he, “that is not to be remembered now.”

[044-081] “It never can be forgotten.”

[044-082] “Yes, Esther,” said he with a gentle seriousness, “it is to be

[044-083] forgotten now, to be forgotten for a while. You are only to remember

[044-084] now that nothing can change me as you know me. Can you feel quite

[044-085] assured of that, my dear?”

[044-086] “I can, and I do,” I said.

[044-087] “That’s much,” he answered. “That’s everything. But I must not take

[044-088] that at a word. I will not write this something in my thoughts until

[044-089] you have quite resolved within yourself that nothing can change me as

[044-090] you know me. If you doubt that in the least degree, I will never

[044-091] write it. If you are sure of that, on good consideration, send

[044-092] Charley to me this night week—‘for the letter.’ But if you are not

[044-093] quite certain, never send. Mind, I trust to your truth, in this thing

[044-094] as in everything. If you are not quite certain on that one point,

[044-095] never send!”

[044-096] “Guardian,” said I, “I am already certain, I can no more be changed

[044-097] in that conviction than you can be changed towards me. I shall send

[044-098] Charley for the letter.”

[044-099] He shook my hand and said no more. Nor was any more said in reference

[044-100] to this conversation, either by him or me, through the whole week.

[044-101] When the appointed night came, I said to Charley as soon as I was

[044-102] alone, “Go and knock at Mr. Jarndyce’s door, Charley, and say you

[044-103] have come from me—‘for the letter.’” Charley went up the stairs, and

[044-104] down the stairs, and along the passages—the zig-zag way about the

[044-105] old-fashioned house seemed very long in my listening ears that

[044-106] night—and so came back, along the passages, and down the stairs, and

[044-107] up the stairs, and brought the letter. “Lay it on the table,

[044-108] Charley,” said I. So Charley laid it on the table and went to bed,

[044-109] and I sat looking at it without taking it up, thinking of many

[044-110] things.

[044-111] I began with my overshadowed childhood, and passed through those

[044-112] timid days to the heavy time when my aunt lay dead, with her resolute

[044-113] face so cold and set, and when I was more solitary with Mrs. Rachael

[044-114] than if I had had no one in the world to speak to or to look at. I

[044-115] passed to the altered days when I was so blest as to find friends in

[044-116] all around me, and to be beloved. I came to the time when I first saw

[044-117] my dear girl and was received into that sisterly affection which was

[044-118] the grace and beauty of my life. I recalled the first bright gleam of

[044-119] welcome which had shone out of those very windows upon our expectant

[044-120] faces on that cold bright night, and which had never paled. I lived

[044-121] my happy life there over again, I went through my illness and

[044-122] recovery, I thought of myself so altered and of those around me so

[044-123] unchanged; and all this happiness shone like a light from one central

[044-124] figure, represented before me by the letter on the table.

[044-125] I opened it and read it. It was so impressive in its love for me, and

[044-126] in the unselfish caution it gave me, and the consideration it showed

[044-127] for me in every word, that my eyes were too often blinded to read

[044-128] much at a time. But I read it through three times before I laid it

[044-129] down. I had thought beforehand that I knew its purport, and I did. It

[044-130] asked me, would I be the mistress of [[bleak-house|Bleak House]].

[044-131] It was not a love letter, though it expressed so much love, but was

[044-132] written just as he would at any time have spoken to me. I saw his

[044-133] face, and heard his voice, and felt the influence of his kind

[044-134] protecting manner in every line. It addressed me as if our places

[044-135] were reversed, as if all the good deeds had been mine and all the

[044-136] feelings they had awakened his. It dwelt on my being young, and he

[044-137] past the prime of life; on his having attained a ripe age, while I

[044-138] was a child; on his writing to me with a silvered head, and knowing

[044-139] all this so well as to set it in full before me for mature

[044-140] deliberation. It told me that I would gain nothing by such a marriage

[044-141] and lose nothing by rejecting it, for no new relation could enhance

[044-142] the tenderness in which he held me, and whatever my decision was, he

[044-143] was certain it would be right. But he had considered this step anew

[044-144] since our late confidence and had decided on taking it, if it only

[044-145] served to show me through one poor instance that the whole world

[044-146] would readily unite to falsify the stern prediction of my childhood.

[044-147] I was the last to know what happiness I could bestow upon him, but of

[044-148] that he said no more, for I was always to remember that I owed him

[044-149] nothing and that he was my debtor, and for very much. He had often

[044-150] thought of our future, and foreseeing that the time must come, and

[044-151] fearing that it might come soon, when Ada (now very nearly of age)

[044-152] would leave us, and when our present mode of life must be broken up,

[044-153] had become accustomed to reflect on this proposal. Thus he made it.

[044-154] If I felt that I could ever give him the best right he could have to

[044-155] be my protector, and if I felt that I could happily and justly become

[044-156] the dear companion of his remaining life, superior to all lighter

[044-157] chances and changes than death, even then he could not have me bind

[044-158] myself irrevocably while this letter was yet so new to me, but even

[044-159] then I must have ample time for reconsideration. In that case, or in

[044-160] the opposite case, let him be unchanged in his old relation, in his

[044-161] old manner, in the old name by which I called him. And as to his

[044-162] bright Dame Durden and little housekeeper, she would ever be the

[044-163] same, he knew.

[044-164] This was the substance of the letter, written throughout with a

[044-165] justice and a dignity as if he were indeed my responsible guardian

[044-166] impartially representing the proposal of a friend against whom in his

[044-167] integrity he stated the full case.

[044-168] But he did not hint to me that when I had been better looking he had

[044-169] had this same proceeding in his thoughts and had refrained from it.

[044-170] That when my old face was gone from me, and I had no attractions, he

[044-171] could love me just as well as in my fairer days. That the discovery

[044-172] of my birth gave him no shock. That his generosity rose above my

[044-173] disfigurement and my [[inheritance-and-property|inheritance]] of [[secret-and-shame|shame]]. That the more I stood in

[044-174] need of such fidelity, the more firmly I might trust in him to the

[044-175] last.

[044-176] But I knew it, I knew it well now. It came upon me as the close of

[044-177] the benignant history I had been pursuing, and I felt that I had but

[044-178] one thing to do. To devote my life to his happiness was to thank him

[044-179] poorly, and what had I wished for the other night but some new means

[044-180] of thanking him?

[044-181] Still I cried very much, not only in the fullness of my heart after

[044-182] reading the letter, not only in the strangeness of the prospect—for

[044-183] it was strange though I had expected the contents—but as if

[044-184] something for which there was no name or distinct idea were

[044-185] indefinitely lost to me. I was very happy, very thankful, very

[044-186] hopeful; but I cried very much.

[044-187] By and by I went to my old glass. My eyes were red and swollen, and I

[044-188] said, “Oh, Esther, Esther, can that be you!” I am afraid the face in

[044-189] the glass was going to cry again at this reproach, but I held up my

[044-190] finger at it, and it stopped.

[044-191] “That is more like the composed look you comforted me with, my dear,

[044-192] when you showed me such a change!” said I, beginning to let down my

[044-193] hair. “When you are mistress of Bleak House, you are to be as

[044-194] cheerful as a bird. In fact, you are always to be cheerful; so let us

[044-195] begin for once and for all.”

[044-196] I went on with my hair now, quite comfortably. I sobbed a little

[044-197] still, but that was because I had been crying, not because I was

[044-198] crying then.

[044-199] “And so Esther, my dear, you are happy for life. Happy with your best

[044-200] friends, happy in your old home, happy in the power of doing a great

[044-201] deal of good, and happy in the undeserved love of the best of men.”

[044-202] I thought, all at once, if my guardian had married some one else, how

[044-203] should I have felt, and what should I have done! That would have been

[044-204] a change indeed. It presented my life in such a new and blank form

[044-205] that I rang my housekeeping keys and gave them a kiss before I laid

[044-206] them down in their basket again.

[044-207] Then I went on to think, as I dressed my hair before the glass, how

[044-208] often had I considered within myself that the deep traces of my

[044-209] illness and the circumstances of my birth were only new reasons why I

[044-210] should be busy, busy, busy—useful, amiable, serviceable, in all

[044-211] honest, unpretending ways. This was a good time, to be sure, to sit

[044-212] down morbidly and cry! As to its seeming at all strange to me at

[044-213] first (if that were any excuse for crying, which it was not) that I

[044-214] was one day to be the mistress of Bleak House, why should it seem

[044-215] strange? Other people had thought of such things, if I had not.

[044-216] “Don’t you remember, my plain dear,” I asked myself, looking at the

[044-217] glass, “what Mrs. [[allan-woodcourt|Woodcourt]] said before those scars were there about

[044-218] your marrying—”

[044-219] Perhaps the name brought them to my remembrance. The dried remains of

[044-220] the flowers. It would be better not to keep them now. They had only

[044-221] been preserved in memory of something wholly past and gone, but it

[044-222] would be better not to keep them now.

[044-223] They were in a book, and it happened to be in the next room—our

[044-224] sitting-room, dividing Ada’s chamber from mine. I took a candle and

[044-225] went softly in to fetch it from its shelf. After I had it in my hand,

[044-226] I saw my beautiful darling, through the open door, lying asleep, and

[044-227] I stole in to kiss her.

[044-228] It was weak in me, I know, and I could have no reason for crying; but

[044-229] I dropped a tear upon her dear face, and another, and another. Weaker

[044-230] than that, I took the withered flowers out and put them for a moment

[044-231] to her lips. I thought about her love for [[richard-carstone|Richard]], though, indeed,

[044-232] the flowers had nothing to do with that. Then I took them into my own

[044-233] room and burned them at the candle, and they were dust in an instant.

[044-234] On entering the breakfast-room next morning, I found my guardian just

[044-235] as usual, quite as frank, as open, and free. There being not the

[044-236] least constraint in his manner, there was none (or I think there was

[044-237] none) in mine. I was with him several times in the course of the

[044-238] morning, in and out, when there was no one there, and I thought it

[044-239] not unlikely that he might speak to me about the letter, but he did

[044-240] not say a word.

[044-241] So, on the next morning, and the next, and for at least a week, over

[044-242] which time Mr. Skimpole prolonged his stay. I expected, every day,

[044-243] that my guardian might speak to me about the letter, but he never

[044-244] did.

[044-245] I thought then, growing uneasy, that I ought to write an answer. I

[044-246] tried over and over again in my own room at night, but I could not

[044-247] write an answer that at all began like a good answer, so I thought

[044-248] each night I would wait one more day. And I waited seven more days,

[044-249] and he never said a word.

[044-250] At last, Mr. Skimpole having departed, we three were one afternoon

[044-251] going out for a ride; and I, being dressed before Ada and going down,

[044-252] came upon my guardian, with his back towards me, standing at the

[044-253] drawing-room window looking out.

[044-254] He turned on my coming in and said, smiling, “Aye, it’s you, little

[044-255] woman, is it?” and looked out again.

[044-256] I had made up my mind to speak to him now. In short, I had come down

[044-257] on purpose. “Guardian,” I said, rather hesitating and trembling,

[044-258] “when would you like to have the answer to the letter Charley came

[044-259] for?”

[044-260] “When it’s ready, my dear,” he replied.

[044-261] “I think it is ready,” said I.

[044-262] “Is Charley to bring it?” he asked pleasantly.

[044-263] “No. I have brought it myself, guardian,” I returned.

[044-264] I put my two arms round his neck and kissed him, and he said was this

[044-265] the mistress of Bleak House, and I said yes; and it made no

[044-266] difference presently, and we all went out together, and I said

[044-267] nothing to my precious pet about it.
