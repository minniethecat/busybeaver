[021-001] In a rather ill-favoured and ill-savoured neighbourhood, though one

[021-002] of its rising grounds bears the name of Mount Pleasant, the Elfin

[021-003] Smallweed, christened Bartholomew and known on the domestic hearth as

[021-004] Bart, passes that limited portion of his time on which the office and

[021-005] its contingencies have no claim. He dwells in a little narrow street,

[021-006] always solitary, shady, and sad, closely bricked in on all sides like

[021-007] a tomb, but where there yet lingers the stump of an old forest tree

[021-008] whose flavour is about as fresh and natural as the Smallweed smack of

[021-009] youth.

[021-010] There has been only one child in the Smallweed family for several

[021-011] generations. Little old men and women there have been, but no child,

[021-012] until Mr. Smallweed’s grandmother, now living, became weak in her

[021-013] intellect and fell (for the first time) into a childish state. With

[021-014] such infantine graces as a total want of observation, memory,

[021-015] understanding, and interest, and an eternal disposition to fall

[021-016] asleep over the fire and into it, Mr. Smallweed’s grandmother has

[021-017] undoubtedly brightened the family.

[021-018] Mr. Smallweed’s grandfather is likewise of the party. He is in a

[021-019] helpless condition as to his lower, and nearly so as to his upper,

[021-020] limbs, but his mind is unimpaired. It holds, as well as it ever held,

[021-021] the first four rules of arithmetic and a certain small collection of

[021-022] the hardest facts. In respect of ideality, reverence, wonder, and

[021-023] other such phrenological attributes, it is no worse off than it used

[021-024] to be. Everything that Mr. Smallweed’s grandfather ever put away in

[021-025] his mind was a grub at first, and is a grub at last. In all his life

[021-026] he has never bred a single butterfly.

[021-027] The father of this pleasant grandfather, of the neighbourhood of

[021-028] Mount Pleasant, was a horny-skinned, two-legged, money-getting

[021-029] species of spider who spun webs to catch unwary flies and retired

[021-030] into holes until they were entrapped. The name of this old pagan’s

[021-031] god was Compound Interest. He lived for it, married it, died of it.

[021-032] Meeting with a heavy loss in an honest little enterprise in which all

[021-033] the loss was intended to have been on the other side, he broke

[021-034] something—something necessary to his existence, therefore it

[021-035] couldn’t have been his heart—and made an end of his career. As his

[021-036] character was not good, and he had been bred at a charity school in a

[021-037] complete course, according to question and answer, of those ancient

[021-038] people the Amorites and Hittites, he was frequently quoted as an

[021-039] example of the failure of education.

[021-040] His spirit shone through his son, to whom he had always preached of

[021-041] “going out” early in life and whom he made a clerk in a sharp

[021-042] scrivener’s office at twelve years old. There the young gentleman

[021-043] improved his mind, which was of a lean and anxious character, and

[021-044] developing the family gifts, gradually elevated himself into the

[021-045] discounting [[professional-identity|profession]]. Going out early in life and marrying late, as

[021-046] his father had done before him, he too begat a lean and

[021-047] anxious-minded son, who in his turn, going out early in life and

[021-048] marrying late, became the father of Bartholomew and Judith Smallweed,

[021-049] twins. During the whole time consumed in the slow growth of this

[021-050] family tree, the house of Smallweed, always early to go out and late

[021-051] to marry, has strengthened itself in its practical character, has

[021-052] discarded all amusements, discountenanced all story-books,

[021-053] fairy-tales, fictions, and fables, and banished all levities

[021-054] whatsoever. Hence the gratifying fact that it has had no child born

[021-055] to it and that the complete little men and women whom it has produced

[021-056] have been observed to bear a likeness to old monkeys with something

[021-057] depressing on their minds.

[021-058] At the present time, in the dark little parlour certain feet below

[021-059] the level of the street—a grim, hard, uncouth parlour, only

[021-060] ornamented with the coarsest of baize table-covers, and the hardest

[021-061] of sheet-iron tea-trays, and offering in its decorative character no

[021-062] bad allegorical representation of Grandfather Smallweed’s

[021-063] mind—seated in two black horsehair porter’s chairs, one on each side

[021-064] of the fire-place, the superannuated Mr. and Mrs. Smallweed while

[021-065] away the rosy hours. On the stove are a couple of trivets for the

[021-066] pots and kettles which it is Grandfather Smallweed’s usual occupation

[021-067] to watch, and projecting from the chimney-piece between them is a

[021-068] sort of brass gallows for roasting, which he also superintends when

[021-069] it is in action. Under the venerable Mr. Smallweed’s seat and guarded

[021-070] by his spindle legs is a drawer in his chair, reported to contain

[021-071] [[inheritance-and-property|property]] to a fabulous amount. Beside him is a spare cushion with

[021-072] which he is always provided in order that he may have something to

[021-073] throw at the venerable partner of his respected age whenever she

[021-074] makes an allusion to money—a subject on which he is particularly

[021-075] sensitive.

[021-095] unbecoming state of cap, but the necessary exertion recoils on Mr.

[021-104] their two porter’s chairs, like a couple of sentinels long forgotten

[021-105] on their post by the Black Serjeant, Death.

[021-106] Judy the twin is worthy company for these associates. She is so

[021-107] indubitably sister to Mr. Smallweed the younger that the two kneaded

[021-108] into one would hardly make a young person of average proportions,

[021-109] while she so happily exemplifies the before-mentioned family likeness

[021-110] to the monkey tribe that attired in a spangled robe and cap she might

[021-111] walk about the table-land on the top of a barrel-organ without

[021-112] exciting much remark as an unusual specimen. Under existing

[021-113] circumstances, however, she is dressed in a plain, spare gown of

[021-114] brown stuff.

[021-115] Judy never owned a doll, never heard of Cinderella, never played at

[021-116] any game. She once or twice fell into children’s company when she was

[021-117] about ten years old, but the children couldn’t get on with Judy, and

[021-118] Judy couldn’t get on with them. She seemed like an animal of another

[021-119] species, and there was instinctive repugnance on both sides. It is

[021-120] very doubtful whether Judy knows how to laugh. She has so rarely seen

[021-121] the thing done that the probabilities are strong the other way. Of

[021-122] anything like a youthful laugh, she certainly can have no conception.

[021-123] If she were to try one, she would find her teeth in her way,

[021-124] modelling that action of her face, as she has unconsciously modelled

[021-125] all its other expressions, on her pattern of sordid age. Such is

[021-126] Judy.

[021-127] And her twin brother couldn’t wind up a top for his life. He knows no

[021-128] more of Jack the Giant Killer or of Sinbad the Sailor than he knows

[021-129] of the people in the stars. He could as soon play at leap-frog or at

[021-130] cricket as change into a cricket or a frog himself. But he is so much

[021-131] the better off than his sister that on his narrow world of fact an

[021-132] opening has dawned into such broader regions as lie within the ken of

[021-133] Mr. Guppy. Hence his admiration and his emulation of that shining

[021-134] enchanter.

[021-135] Judy, with a gong-like clash and clatter, sets one of the sheet-iron

[021-136] tea-trays on the table and arranges cups and saucers. The bread she

[021-137] puts on in an iron basket, and the butter (and not much of it) in a

[021-138] small pewter plate. Grandfather Smallweed looks hard after the tea as

[021-139] it is served out and asks Judy where the girl is.

[021-140] “Charley, do you mean?” says Judy.

[021-141] “Hey?” from Grandfather Smallweed.

[021-142] “Charley, do you mean?”

[021-146] water, over the water to Charley!” and becomes quite energetic about

[021-148] recovered his late exertion.

[021-153] “No?” returns the old man. “Why not?”
