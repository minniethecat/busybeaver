[030-001] [[richard-carstone|Richard]] had been gone away some time when a visitor came to pass a

[030-002] few days with us. It was an elderly lady. It was Mrs. [[allan-woodcourt|Woodcourt]], who,

[030-003] having come from Wales to stay with Mrs. Bayham Badger and having

[030-004] written to my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]], “by her son Allan’s desire,” to report that

[030-005] she had heard from him and that he was well “and sent his kind

[030-006] remembrances to all of us,” had been invited by my guardian to make a

[030-007] visit to [[bleak-house|Bleak House]]. She stayed with us nearly three weeks. She took

[030-008] very kindly to me and was extremely confidential, so much so that

[030-009] sometimes she almost made me uncomfortable. I had no right, I knew

[030-010] very well, to be uncomfortable because she confided in me, and I felt

[030-011] it was unreasonable; still, with all I could do, I could not quite

[030-012] help it.

[030-013] She was such a sharp little lady and used to sit with her hands

[030-014] folded in each other looking so very watchful while she talked to me

[030-015] that perhaps I found that rather irksome. Or perhaps it was her being

[030-016] so upright and trim, though I don’t think it was that, because I

[030-017] thought that quaintly pleasant. Nor can it have been the general

[030-018] expression of her face, which was very sparkling and pretty for an

[030-019] old lady. I don’t know what it was. Or at least if I do now, I

[030-020] thought I did not then. Or at least—but it don’t matter.

[030-021] Of a night when I was going upstairs to bed, she would invite me

[030-022] into her room, where she sat before the fire in a great chair;

[030-023] and, dear me, she would tell me about Morgan ap-Kerrig until I

[030-024] was quite low-spirited! Sometimes she recited a few verses from

[030-025] Crumlinwallinwer and the Mewlinnwillinwodd (if those are the right

[030-026] names, which I dare say they are not), and would become quite fiery

[030-027] with the sentiments they expressed. Though I never knew what they

[030-028] were (being in Welsh), further than that they were highly eulogistic

[030-029] of the lineage of Morgan ap-Kerrig.

[030-030] “So, Miss Summerson,” she would say to me with stately triumph,

[030-031] “this, you see, is the fortune inherited by my son. Wherever my son

[030-032] goes, he can claim kindred with Ap-Kerrig. He may not have money, but

[030-033] he always has what is much better—family, my dear.”

[030-034] I had my doubts of their caring so very much for Morgan ap-Kerrig in

[030-035] India and China, but of course I never expressed them. I used to say

[030-036] it was a great thing to be so highly connected.

[030-037] “It IS, my dear, a great thing,” Mrs. Woodcourt would reply. “It has

[030-038] its disadvantages; my son’s choice of a wife, for instance, is

[030-039] limited by it, but the matrimonial choice of the royal family is

[030-040] limited in much the same manner.”

[030-041] Then she would pat me on the arm and smooth my dress, as much as to

[030-042] assure me that she had a good opinion of me, the distance between us

[030-043] notwithstanding.

[030-044] “Poor Mr. Woodcourt, my dear,” she would say, and always with some

[030-045] emotion, for with her lofty pedigree she had a very affectionate

[030-046] heart, “was descended from a great Highland family, the MacCoorts of

[030-047] MacCoort. He served his king and country as an officer in the Royal

[030-048] Highlanders, and he died on the field. My son is one of the last

[030-049] representatives of two old families. With the blessing of heaven he

[030-050] will set them up again and unite them with another old family.”

[030-051] It was in vain for me to try to change the subject, as I used to try,

[030-052] only for the sake of novelty or perhaps because—but I need not be so

[030-053] particular. Mrs. Woodcourt never would let me change it.

[030-054] “My dear,” she said one night, “you have so much sense and you look

[030-055] at the world in a quiet manner so superior to your time of life that

[030-056] it is a comfort to me to talk to you about these family matters of

[030-057] mine. You don’t know much of my son, my dear; but you know enough of

[030-058] him, I dare say, to recollect him?”

[030-059] “Yes, ma’am. I recollect him.”

[030-060] “Yes, my dear. Now, my dear, I think you are a judge of character,

[030-061] and I should like to have your opinion of him.”

[030-062] “Oh, Mrs. Woodcourt,” said I, “that is so difficult!”

[030-063] “Why is it so difficult, my dear?” she returned. “I don’t see it

[030-064] myself.”

[030-065] “To give an opinion—”

[030-066] “On so slight an acquaintance, my dear. THAT’S true.”

[030-067] I didn’t mean that, because Mr. Woodcourt had been at our house a

[030-068] good deal altogether and had become quite intimate with my guardian.

[030-069] I said so, and added that he seemed to be very clever in his

[030-070] [[professional-identity|profession]]—we thought—and that his kindness and gentleness to Miss

[030-071] Flite were above all praise.

[030-072] “You do him justice!” said Mrs. Woodcourt, pressing my hand. “You

[030-073] define him exactly. Allan is a dear fellow, and in his profession

[030-074] faultless. I say it, though I am his mother. Still, I must confess he

[030-075] is not without faults, love.”

[030-076] “None of us are,” said I.

[030-077] “Ah! But his really are faults that he might correct, and ought to

[030-078] correct,” returned the sharp old lady, sharply shaking her head. “I

[030-079] am so much attached to you that I may confide in you, my dear, as a

[030-080] third party wholly disinterested, that he is fickleness itself.”

[030-081] I said I should have thought it hardly possible that he could have

[030-082] been otherwise than constant to his profession and zealous in the

[030-083] pursuit of it, judging from the reputation he had earned.

[030-084] “You are right again, my dear,” the old lady retorted, “but I don’t

[030-085] refer to his profession, look you.”

[030-086] “Oh!” said I.

[030-087] “No,” said she. “I refer, my dear, to his social conduct. He is

[030-088] always paying trivial attentions to young ladies, and always has

[030-089] been, ever since he was eighteen. Now, my dear, he has never really

[030-090] cared for any one of them and has never meant in doing this to do any

[030-091] harm or to express anything but politeness and good nature. Still,

[030-092] it’s not right, you know; is it?”

[030-093] “No,” said I, as she seemed to wait for me.

[030-094] “And it might lead to mistaken notions, you see, my dear.”

[030-095] I supposed it might.

[030-096] “Therefore, I have told him many times that he really should be more

[030-097] careful, both in justice to himself and in justice to others. And he

[030-098] has always said, ‘Mother, I will be; but you know me better than

[030-099] anybody else does, and you know I mean no harm—in short, mean

[030-100] nothing.’ All of which is very true, my dear, but is no

[030-101] justification. However, as he is now gone so far away and for an

[030-102] indefinite time, and as he will have good opportunities and

[030-103] introductions, we may consider this past and gone. And you, my dear,”

[030-104] said the old lady, who was now all nods and smiles, “regarding your

[030-105] dear self, my love?”

[030-106] “Me, Mrs. Woodcourt?”

[030-107] “Not to be always selfish, talking of my son, who has gone to seek

[030-108] his fortune and to find a wife—when do you mean to seek YOUR fortune

[030-109] and to find a husband, Miss Summerson? Hey, look you! Now you blush!”

[030-110] I don’t think I did blush—at all events, it was not important if I

[030-111] did—and I said my present fortune perfectly contented me and I had

[030-112] no wish to change it.

[030-113] “Shall I tell you what I always think of you and the fortune yet to

[030-114] come for you, my love?” said Mrs. Woodcourt.

[030-115] “If you believe you are a good prophet,” said I.

[030-116] “Why, then, it is that you will marry some one very rich and very

[030-117] worthy, much older—five and twenty years, perhaps—than yourself.

[030-118] And you will be an excellent wife, and much beloved, and very happy.”

[030-119] “That is a good fortune,” said I. “But why is it to be mine?”

[030-120] “My dear,” she returned, “there’s suitability in it—you are so busy,

[030-121] and so neat, and so peculiarly situated altogether that there’s

[030-122] suitability in it, and it will come to pass. And nobody, my love,

[030-123] will congratulate you more sincerely on such a marriage than I

[030-124] shall.”

[030-125] It was curious that this should make me uncomfortable, but I think it

[030-126] did. I know it did. It made me for some part of that night

[030-127] uncomfortable. I was so ashamed of my folly that I did not like to

[030-128] confess it even to [[ada-clare|Ada]], and that made me more uncomfortable still. I

[030-129] would have given anything not to have been so much in the bright old

[030-130] lady’s confidence if I could have possibly declined it. It gave me

[030-131] the most inconsistent opinions of her. At one time I thought she was

[030-132] a story-teller, and at another time that she was the pink of truth.

[030-133] Now I suspected that she was very cunning, next moment I believed her

[030-134] honest Welsh heart to be perfectly innocent and simple. And after

[030-135] all, what did it matter to me, and why did it matter to me? Why could

[030-136] not I, going up to bed with my basket of keys, stop to sit down by

[030-137] her fire and accommodate myself for a little while to her, at least

[030-138] as well as to anybody else, and not trouble myself about the harmless

[030-139] things she said to me? Impelled towards her, as I certainly was, for

[030-140] I was very anxious that she should like me and was very glad indeed

[030-141] that she did, why should I harp afterwards, with actual distress and

[030-142] pain, on every word she said and weigh it over and over again in

[030-143] twenty scales? Why was it so worrying to me to have her in our house,

[030-144] and confidential to me every night, when I yet felt that it was

[030-145] better and safer somehow that she should be there than anywhere else?

[030-146] These were perplexities and contradictions that I could not account

[030-147] for. At least, if I could—but I shall come to all that by and by,

[030-148] and it is mere idleness to go on about it now.

[030-149] So when Mrs. Woodcourt went away, I was sorry to lose her but was

[030-150] relieved too. And then [[caddy-jellyby|Caddy Jellyby]] came down, and Caddy brought

[030-151] such a packet of domestic news that it gave us abundant occupation.

[030-152] First Caddy declared (and would at first declare nothing else) that I

[030-153] was the best adviser that ever was known. This, my pet said, was no

[030-154] news at all; and this, I said, of course, was nonsense. Then Caddy

[030-155] told us that she was going to be married in a month and that if Ada

[030-156] and I would be her bridesmaids, she was the happiest girl in the

[030-157] world. To be sure, this was news indeed; and I thought we never

[030-158] should have done talking about it, we had so much to say to Caddy,

[030-159] and Caddy had so much to say to us.

[030-160] It seemed that Caddy’s unfortunate papa had got over his

[030-161] bankruptcy—“gone through the Gazette,” was the expression Caddy

[030-162] used, as if it were a tunnel—with the general clemency and

[030-163] commiseration of his creditors, and had got rid of his affairs in

[030-164] some blessed manner without succeeding in understanding them, and had

[030-165] given up everything he possessed (which was not worth much, I should

[030-166] think, to judge from the state of the furniture), and had satisfied

[030-167] every one concerned that he could do no more, poor man. So, he had

[030-168] been honourably dismissed to “the office” to begin the world again.

[030-169] What he did at the office, I never knew; Caddy said he was a

[030-170] “custom-house and general agent,” and the only thing I ever

[030-171] understood about that business was that when he wanted money more

[030-172] than usual he went to the docks to look for it, and hardly ever found

[030-173] it.

[030-174] As soon as her papa had tranquillized his mind by becoming this shorn

[030-175] lamb, and they had removed to a furnished lodging in Hatton Garden

[030-176] (where I found the children, when I afterwards went there, cutting

[030-177] the horse hair out of the seats of the chairs and choking themselves

[030-178] with it), Caddy had brought about a meeting between him and old Mr.

[030-179] Turveydrop; and poor Mr. Jellyby, being very humble and meek, had

[030-180] deferred to Mr. Turveydrop’s deportment so submissively that they had

[030-181] become excellent friends. By degrees, old Mr. Turveydrop, thus

[030-182] familiarized with the idea of his son’s marriage, had worked up his

[030-183] parental feelings to the height of contemplating that event as being

[030-184] near at hand and had given his gracious consent to the young couple

[030-185] commencing housekeeping at the academy in Newman Street when they

[030-186] would.

[030-187] “And your papa, Caddy. What did he say?”

[030-188] “Oh! Poor Pa,” said Caddy, “only cried and said he hoped we might get

[030-189] on better than he and Ma had got on. He didn’t say so before Prince,

[030-190] he only said so to me. And he said, ‘My poor girl, you have not been

[030-191] very well taught how to make a home for your husband, but unless you

[030-192] mean with all your heart to strive to do it, you had better murder

[030-193] him than marry him—if you really love him.’”

[030-194] “And how did you reassure him, Caddy?”

[030-195] “Why, it was very distressing, you know, to see poor Pa so low and

[030-196] hear him say such terrible things, and I couldn’t help crying myself.

[030-197] But I told him that I DID mean it with all my heart and that I hoped

[030-198] our house would be a place for him to come and find some comfort in

[030-199] of an evening and that I hoped and thought I could be a better

[030-200] daughter to him there than at home. Then I mentioned Peepy’s coming

[030-201] to stay with me, and then Pa began to cry again and said the children

[030-202] were Indians.”

[030-203] “Indians, Caddy?”

[030-204] “Yes,” said Caddy, “wild Indians. And Pa said”—here she began to

[030-205] sob, poor girl, not at all like the happiest girl in the world—“that

[030-206] he was sensible the best thing that could happen to them was their

[030-207] being all tomahawked together.”

[030-208] Ada suggested that it was comfortable to know that Mr. Jellyby did

[030-209] not mean these destructive sentiments.

[030-210] “No, of course I know Pa wouldn’t like his family to be weltering in

[030-211] their blood,” said Caddy, “but he means that they are very

[030-212] unfortunate in being Ma’s children and that he is very unfortunate in

[030-213] being Ma’s husband; and I am sure that’s true, though it seems

[030-214] unnatural to say so.”

[030-215] I asked Caddy if Mrs. Jellyby knew that her wedding-day was fixed.

[030-216] “Oh! You know what Ma is, [[esther-summerson|Esther]],” she returned. “It’s impossible to

[030-217] say whether she knows it or not. She has been told it often enough;

[030-218] and when she IS told it, she only gives me a placid look, as if I was

[030-219] I don’t know what—a steeple in the distance,” said Caddy with a

[030-220] sudden idea; “and then she shakes her head and says ‘Oh, Caddy,

[030-221] Caddy, what a tease you are!’ and goes on with the Borrioboola

[030-222] [[document-and-letter|letters]].”

[030-223] “And about your wardrobe, Caddy?” said I. For she was under no

[030-224] restraint with us.

[030-225] “Well, my dear Esther,” she returned, drying her eyes, “I must do the

[030-226] best I can and trust to my dear Prince never to have an unkind

[030-227] remembrance of my coming so shabbily to him. If the question

[030-228] concerned an outfit for Borrioboola, Ma would know all about it and

[030-229] would be quite excited. Being what it is, she neither knows nor

[030-230] cares.”

[030-231] Caddy was not at all deficient in natural affection for her mother,

[030-232] but mentioned this with tears as an undeniable fact, which I am

[030-233] afraid it was. We were sorry for the poor dear girl and found so much

[030-234] to admire in the good disposition which had survived under such

[030-235] discouragement that we both at once (I mean Ada and I) proposed a

[030-236] little scheme that made her perfectly joyful. This was her staying

[030-237] with us for three weeks, my staying with her for one, and our all

[030-238] three contriving and cutting out, and repairing, and sewing, and

[030-239] saving, and doing the very best we could think of to make the most of

[030-240] her stock. My guardian being as pleased with the idea as Caddy was,

[030-241] we took her home next day to arrange the matter and brought her out

[030-242] again in triumph with her boxes and all the purchases that could be

[030-243] squeezed out of a ten-pound note, which Mr. Jellyby had found in the

[030-244] docks I suppose, but which he at all events gave her. What my

[030-245] guardian would not have given her if we had encouraged him, it would

[030-246] be difficult to say, but we thought it right to compound for no more

[030-247] than her wedding-dress and bonnet. He agreed to this compromise, and

[030-248] if Caddy had ever been happy in her life, she was happy when we sat

[030-249] down to work.

[030-250] She was clumsy enough with her needle, poor girl, and pricked her

[030-251] fingers as much as she had been used to ink them. She could not help

[030-252] reddening a little now and then, partly with the smart and partly

[030-253] with vexation at being able to do no better, but she soon got over

[030-254] that and began to improve rapidly. So day after day she, and my

[030-255] darling, and my little maid Charley, and a milliner out of the town,

[030-256] and I, sat hard at work, as pleasantly as possible.

[030-257] Over and above this, Caddy was very anxious “to learn housekeeping,”

[030-258] as she said. Now, mercy upon us! The idea of her learning

[030-259] housekeeping of a person of my vast experience was such a joke that I

[030-260] laughed, and coloured up, and fell into a comical confusion when she

[030-261] proposed it. However, I said, “Caddy, I am sure you are very welcome

[030-262] to learn anything that you can learn of ME, my dear,” and I showed

[030-263] her all my books and methods and all my fidgety ways. You would have

[030-264] supposed that I was showing her some wonderful inventions, by her

[030-265] study of them; and if you had seen her, whenever I jingled my

[030-266] housekeeping keys, get up and attend me, certainly you might have

[030-267] thought that there never was a greater imposter than I with a blinder

[030-268] follower than Caddy Jellyby.

[030-269] So what with working and housekeeping, and lessons to Charley, and

[030-270] backgammon in the evening with my guardian, and duets with Ada, the

[030-271] three weeks slipped fast away. Then I went home with Caddy to see

[030-272] what could be done there, and Ada and Charley remained behind to take

[030-273] care of my guardian.

[030-274] When I say I went home with Caddy, I mean to the furnished lodging in

[030-275] Hatton Garden. We went to Newman Street two or three times, where

[030-276] preparations were in progress too—a good many, I observed, for

[030-277] enhancing the comforts of old Mr. Turveydrop, and a few for putting

[030-278] the newly married couple away cheaply at the top of the house—but

[030-279] our great point was to make the furnished lodging decent for the

[030-280] wedding-breakfast and to imbue Mrs. Jellyby beforehand with some

[030-281] faint sense of the occasion.

[030-282] The latter was the more difficult thing of the two because Mrs.

[030-283] Jellyby and an unwholesome boy occupied the front sitting-room (the

[030-284] back one was a mere closet), and it was littered down with

[030-285] waste-paper and Borrioboolan documents, as an untidy stable might be

[030-286] littered with straw. Mrs. Jellyby sat there all day drinking strong

[030-287] coffee, dictating, and holding Borrioboolan interviews by

[030-288] appointment. The unwholesome boy, who seemed to me to be going into a

[030-289] decline, took his meals out of the house. When Mr. Jellyby came home,

[030-290] he usually groaned and went down into the kitchen. There he got

[030-291] something to eat if the servant would give him anything, and then,

[030-292] feeling that he was in the way, went out and walked about Hatton

[030-293] Garden in the wet. The poor children scrambled up and tumbled down

[030-294] the house as they had always been accustomed to do.

[030-295] The production of these devoted little sacrifices in any presentable

[030-296] condition being quite out of the question at a week’s notice, I

[030-297] proposed to Caddy that we should make them as happy as we could on

[030-298] her marriage morning in the attic where they all slept, and should

[030-299] confine our greatest efforts to her mama and her mama’s room, and a

[030-300] clean breakfast. In truth Mrs. Jellyby required a good deal of

[030-301] attention, the lattice-work up her back having widened considerably

[030-302] since I first knew her and her hair looking like the mane of a

[030-303] dustman’s horse.

[030-304] Thinking that the display of Caddy’s wardrobe would be the best means

[030-305] of approaching the subject, I invited Mrs. Jellyby to come and look

[030-306] at it spread out on Caddy’s bed in the evening after the unwholesome

[030-307] boy was gone.

[030-308] “My dear Miss Summerson,” said she, rising from her desk with her

[030-309] usual sweetness of temper, “these are really ridiculous preparations,

[030-310] though your assisting them is a proof of your kindness. There is

[030-311] something so inexpressibly absurd to me in the idea of Caddy being

[030-312] married! Oh, Caddy, you silly, silly, silly puss!”

[030-313] She came upstairs with us notwithstanding and looked at the clothes

[030-314] in her customary far-off manner. They suggested one distinct idea to

[030-315] her, for she said with her placid smile, and shaking her head, “My

[030-316] good Miss Summerson, at half the cost, this weak child might have

[030-317] been equipped for Africa!”

[030-318] On our going downstairs again, Mrs. Jellyby asked me whether this

[030-319] troublesome business was really to take place next Wednesday. And on

[030-320] my replying yes, she said, “Will my room be required, my dear Miss

[030-321] Summerson? For it’s quite impossible that I can put my papers away.”

[030-322] I took the liberty of saying that the room would certainly be wanted

[030-323] and that I thought we must put the papers away somewhere. “Well, my

[030-324] dear Miss Summerson,” said Mrs. Jellyby, “you know best, I dare say.

[030-325] But by obliging me to employ a boy, Caddy has embarrassed me to that

[030-326] extent, overwhelmed as I am with public business, that I don’t know

[030-327] which way to turn. We have a Ramification meeting, too, on Wednesday

[030-328] afternoon, and the inconvenience is very serious.”

[030-329] “It is not likely to occur again,” said I, smiling. “Caddy will be

[030-330] married but once, probably.”

[030-331] “That’s true,” Mrs. Jellyby replied; “that’s true, my dear. I suppose

[030-332] we must make the best of it!”

[030-333] The next question was how Mrs. Jellyby should be dressed on the

[030-334] occasion. I thought it very curious to see her looking on serenely

[030-335] from her writing-table while Caddy and I discussed it, occasionally

[030-336] shaking her head at us with a half-reproachful smile like a superior

[030-337] spirit who could just bear with our trifling.

[030-338] The state in which her dresses were, and the extraordinary confusion

[030-339] in which she kept them, added not a little to our difficulty; but at

[030-340] length we devised something not very unlike what a common-place

[030-341] mother might wear on such an occasion. The abstracted manner in which

[030-342] Mrs. Jellyby would deliver herself up to having this attire tried on

[030-343] by the dressmaker, and the sweetness with which she would then

[030-344] observe to me how sorry she was that I had not turned my thoughts to

[030-345] Africa, were consistent with the rest of her behaviour.

[030-346] The lodging was rather confined as to space, but I fancied that if

[030-347] Mrs. Jellyby’s household had been the only lodgers in Saint Paul’s or

[030-348] Saint Peter’s, the sole advantage they would have found in the size

[030-349] of the building would have been its affording a great deal of room to

[030-350] be dirty in. I believe that nothing belonging to the family which it

[030-351] had been possible to break was unbroken at the time of those

[030-352] preparations for Caddy’s marriage, that nothing which it had been

[030-353] possible to spoil in any way was unspoilt, and that no domestic

[030-354] object which was capable of collecting dirt, from a dear child’s knee

[030-355] to the door-plate, was without as much dirt as could well accumulate

[030-356] upon it.

[030-357] Poor Mr. Jellyby, who very seldom spoke and almost always sat when he

[030-358] was at home with his head against the wall, became interested when he

[030-359] saw that Caddy and I were attempting to establish some order among

[030-360] all this waste and ruin and took off his coat to help. But such

[030-361] wonderful things came tumbling out of the closets when they were

[030-362] opened—bits of mouldy pie, sour bottles, Mrs. Jellyby’s caps,

[030-363] letters, tea, forks, odd boots and shoes of children, firewood,

[030-364] wafers, saucepan-lids, damp sugar in odds and ends of paper bags,

[030-365] footstools, blacklead brushes, bread, Mrs. Jellyby’s bonnets, books

[030-366] with butter sticking to the binding, guttered candle ends put out

[030-367] by being turned upside down in broken candlesticks, nutshells,

[030-368] heads and tails of shrimps, dinner-mats, gloves, coffee-grounds,

[030-369] umbrellas—that he looked frightened, and left off again. But he came

[030-370] regularly every evening and sat without his coat, with his head

[030-371] against the wall, as though he would have helped us if he had known
