[001-001] [[london|London]]. Michaelmas term lately over, and the Lord Chancellor sitting

[001-002] in Lincoln’s Inn Hall. Implacable November weather. As much mud in

[001-003] the streets as if the waters had but newly retired from the face of

[001-004] the earth, and it would not be wonderful to meet a Megalosaurus,

[001-005] forty feet long or so, waddling like an elephantine lizard up Holborn

[001-006] Hill. Smoke lowering down from chimney-pots, making a soft black

[001-007] drizzle, with flakes of soot in it as big as full-grown

[001-008] snowflakes—gone into mourning, one might imagine, for the death of

[001-009] the sun. Dogs, undistinguishable in mire. Horses, scarcely better;

[001-010] splashed to their very blinkers. Foot passengers, jostling one

[001-011] another’s umbrellas in a general [[disease-and-contagion|infection]] of ill temper, and losing

[001-012] their foot-hold at street-corners, where tens of thousands of other

[001-013] foot passengers have been slipping and sliding since the day broke

[001-014] (if this day ever broke), adding new deposits to the crust upon crust

[001-015] of mud, sticking at those points tenaciously to the pavement, and

[001-016] accumulating at compound interest.

[001-017] Fog everywhere. Fog up the river, where it flows among green aits and

[001-018] meadows; [[fog-motif|fog]] down the river, where it rolls defiled among the tiers

[001-019] of shipping and the waterside pollutions of a great (and dirty) city.

[001-020] Fog on the Essex marshes, fog on the Kentish heights. Fog creeping

[001-021] into the cabooses of collier-brigs; fog lying out on the yards and

[001-022] hovering in the rigging of great ships; fog drooping on the gunwales

[001-023] of barges and small boats. Fog in the eyes and throats of ancient

[001-024] Greenwich pensioners, wheezing by the firesides of their wards; fog

[001-025] in the stem and bowl of the afternoon pipe of the wrathful skipper,

[001-026] down in his close cabin; fog cruelly pinching the toes and fingers of

[001-027] his shivering little ’prentice boy on deck. Chance people on the

[001-028] bridges peeping over the parapets into a nether sky of fog, with fog

[001-029] all round them, as if they were up in a balloon and hanging in the

[001-030] misty clouds.

[001-031] Gas looming through the fog in divers places in the streets, much as

[001-032] the sun may, from the spongey fields, be seen to loom by husbandman

[001-033] and ploughboy. Most of the shops lighted two hours before their

[001-034] time—as the gas seems to know, for it has a haggard and unwilling

[001-035] look.

[001-036] The raw afternoon is rawest, and the dense fog is densest, and the

[001-037] muddy streets are muddiest near that leaden-headed old obstruction,

[001-038] appropriate ornament for the threshold of a leaden-headed old

[001-039] corporation, Temple Bar. And hard by Temple Bar, in Lincoln’s Inn

[001-040] Hall, at the very heart of the fog, sits the Lord High Chancellor in

[001-041] his [[chancery-court|High Court of Chancery]].

[001-042] Never can there come fog too thick, never can there come mud and mire

[001-043] too deep, to assort with the groping and floundering condition which

[001-044] this High [[court-of-chancery-institution|Court of Chancery]], most pestilent of hoary sinners, holds

[001-045] this day in the sight of heaven and earth.

[001-046] On such an afternoon, if ever, the Lord High Chancellor ought to be

[001-047] sitting here—as here he is—with a foggy glory round his head,

[001-048] softly fenced in with crimson cloth and curtains, addressed by a

[001-049] large advocate with great whiskers, a little voice, and an

[001-050] interminable brief, and outwardly directing his contemplation to the

[001-051] lantern in the roof, where he can see nothing but fog. On such an

[001-052] afternoon some score of members of the High Court of Chancery bar

[001-053] ought to be—as here they are—mistily engaged in one of the ten

[001-054] thousand stages of an endless cause, tripping one another up on

[001-055] slippery precedents, groping knee-deep in technicalities, running

[001-056] their goat-hair and horsehair warded heads against walls of words and

[001-057] making a pretence of equity with serious faces, as players might. On

[001-058] such an afternoon the various solicitors in the cause, some two or

[001-059] three of whom have inherited it from their fathers, who made a

[001-060] fortune by it, ought to be—as are they not?—ranged in a line, in a

[001-061] long matted well (but you might look in vain for truth at the bottom

[001-062] of it) between the registrar’s red table and the silk gowns, with

[001-063] bills, cross-bills, answers, rejoinders, injunctions, affidavits,

[001-064] issues, references to masters, masters’ reports, mountains of costly

[001-065] nonsense, piled before them. Well may the court be dim, with wasting

[001-066] candles here and there; well may the fog hang heavy in it, as if it

[001-067] would never get out; well may the stained-glass windows lose their

[001-068] colour and admit no light of day into the place; well may the

[001-069] uninitiated from the streets, who peep in through the glass panes in

[001-070] the door, be deterred from entrance by its owlish aspect and by the

[001-071] drawl, languidly echoing to the roof from the padded dais where the

[001-072] Lord High Chancellor looks into the lantern that has no light in it

[001-073] and where the attendant wigs are all stuck in a fog-bank! This is the

[001-074] Court of Chancery, which has its decaying houses and its blighted

[001-075] lands in every shire, which has its worn-out lunatic in every

[001-076] madhouse and its dead in every churchyard, which has its ruined

[001-077] suitor with his slipshod heels and threadbare dress borrowing and

[001-078] begging through the round of every man’s acquaintance, which gives to

[001-079] monied might the means abundantly of wearying out the right, which so

[001-080] exhausts finances, patience, courage, hope, so overthrows the brain

[001-081] and breaks the heart, that there is not an honourable man among its

[001-082] practitioners who would not give—who does not often give—the

[001-083] warning, “Suffer any wrong that can be done you rather than come

[001-084] here!”

[001-085] Who happen to be in the Lord Chancellor’s court this murky afternoon

[001-086] besides the Lord Chancellor, the counsel in the cause, two or three

[001-087] counsel who are never in any cause, and the well of solicitors before

[001-088] mentioned? There is the registrar below the judge, in wig and gown;

[001-089] and there are two or three maces, or petty-bags, or privy purses, or

[001-090] whatever they may be, in legal court suits. These are all yawning,

[001-091] for no crumb of amusement ever falls from [[jarndyce-and-jarndyce|Jarndyce and Jarndyce]] (the

[001-092] cause in hand), which was squeezed dry years upon years ago. The

[001-093] short-hand writers, the reporters of the court, and the reporters of

[001-094] the newspapers invariably decamp with the rest of the regulars when

[001-095] Jarndyce and Jarndyce comes on. Their places are a blank. Standing on

[001-096] a seat at the side of the hall, the better to peer into the curtained

[001-097] sanctuary, is a little mad old woman in a squeezed bonnet who is

[001-098] always in court, from its sitting to its rising, and always expecting

[001-099] some incomprehensible judgment to be given in her favour. Some say

[001-100] she really is, or was, a party to a suit, but no one knows for

[001-101] certain because no one cares. She carries some small litter in a

[001-102] reticule which she calls her documents, principally consisting of

[001-103] paper matches and dry lavender. A sallow prisoner has come up, in

[001-104] custody, for the half-dozenth time to make a personal application “to

[001-105] purge himself of his contempt,” which, being a solitary surviving

[001-106] executor who has fallen into a state of conglomeration about accounts

[001-107] of which it is not pretended that he had ever any knowledge, he is

[001-108] not at all likely ever to do. In the meantime his prospects in life

[001-109] are ended. Another ruined suitor, who periodically appears from

[001-110] Shropshire and breaks out into efforts to address the Chancellor at

[001-111] the close of the day’s business and who can by no means be made to

[001-112] understand that the Chancellor is legally ignorant of his existence

[001-113] after making it desolate for a quarter of a century, plants himself

[001-114] in a good place and keeps an eye on the judge, ready to call out “My

[001-115] Lord!” in a voice of sonorous complaint on the instant of his rising.

[001-116] A few lawyers’ clerks and others who know this suitor by sight linger

[001-117] on the chance of his furnishing some fun and enlivening the dismal

[001-118] weather a little.

[001-119] Jarndyce and Jarndyce drones on. This scarecrow of a suit has, in

[001-120] course of time, become so complicated that no man alive knows what it

[001-121] means. The parties to it understand it least, but it has been

[001-122] observed that no two Chancery lawyers can talk about it for five

[001-123] minutes without coming to a total disagreement as to all the

[001-124] premises. Innumerable children have been born into the cause;

[001-125] innumerable young people have married into it; innumerable old people

[001-126] have died out of it. Scores of persons have deliriously found

[001-127] themselves made parties in Jarndyce and Jarndyce without knowing how

[001-128] or why; whole families have inherited legendary hatreds with the

[001-129] suit. The little plaintiff or defendant who was promised a new

[001-130] rocking-horse when Jarndyce and Jarndyce should be settled has grown

[001-131] up, possessed himself of a real horse, and trotted away into the

[001-132] other world. Fair wards of court have faded into mothers and

[001-133] grandmothers; a long procession of Chancellors has come in and gone

[001-134] out; the legion of bills in the suit have been transformed into mere

[001-135] bills of mortality; there are not three Jarndyces left upon the earth

[001-136] perhaps since old Tom Jarndyce in despair blew his brains out at a

[001-137] coffee-house in [[chancery-lane|Chancery Lane]]; but Jarndyce and Jarndyce still drags

[001-138] its dreary length before the court, perennially hopeless.

[001-139] Jarndyce and Jarndyce has passed into a joke. That is the only good

[001-140] that has ever come of it. It has been death to many, but it is a joke

[001-141] in the [[professional-identity|profession]]. Every master in Chancery has had a reference out

[001-142] of it. Every Chancellor was “in it,” for somebody or other, when he

[001-143] was counsel at the bar. Good things have been said about it by

[001-144] blue-nosed, bulbous-shoed old benchers in select port-wine committee

[001-145] after dinner in hall. Articled clerks have been in the habit of

[001-146] fleshing their legal wit upon it. The last Lord Chancellor handled it

[001-147] neatly, when, correcting Mr. Blowers, the eminent silk gown who said

[001-148] that such a thing might happen when the sky rained potatoes, he

[001-149] observed, “or when we get through Jarndyce and Jarndyce, Mr.

[001-150] Blowers”—a pleasantry that particularly tickled the maces, bags, and

[001-151] purses.

[001-152] How many people out of the suit Jarndyce and Jarndyce has stretched

[001-153] forth its unwholesome hand to spoil and corrupt would be a very wide

[001-154] question. From the master upon whose impaling files reams of dusty

[001-155] warrants in Jarndyce and Jarndyce have grimly writhed into many

[001-156] shapes, down to the copying-clerk in the Six Clerks’ Office who has

[001-157] copied his tens of thousands of Chancery folio-pages under that

[001-158] eternal heading, no man’s nature has been made better by it. In

[001-159] trickery, evasion, procrastination, spoliation, botheration, under

[001-160] false pretences of all sorts, there are influences that can never

[001-161] come to good. The very solicitors’ boys who have kept the wretched

[001-162] suitors at bay, by protesting time out of mind that Mr. Chizzle,

[001-163] Mizzle, or otherwise was particularly engaged and had appointments

[001-164] until dinner, may have got an extra moral twist and shuffle into

[001-165] themselves out of Jarndyce and Jarndyce. The receiver in the cause

[001-166] has acquired a goodly sum of money by it but has acquired too a

[001-167] distrust of his own mother and a contempt for his own kind. Chizzle,

[001-168] Mizzle, and otherwise have lapsed into a habit of vaguely promising

[001-169] themselves that they will look into that outstanding little matter

[001-170] and see what can be done for Drizzle—who was not well used—when

[001-171] Jarndyce and Jarndyce shall be got out of the office. Shirking and

[001-172] sharking in all their many varieties have been sown broadcast by the

[001-173] ill-fated cause; and even those who have contemplated its history

[001-174] from the outermost circle of such evil have been insensibly tempted

[001-175] into a loose way of letting bad things alone to take their own bad

[001-176] course, and a loose belief that if the world go wrong it was in some

[001-177] off-hand manner never meant to go right.

[001-178] Thus, in the midst of the mud and at the heart of the fog, sits the

[001-179] Lord High Chancellor in his High Court of Chancery.

[001-180] “Mr. Tangle,” says the Lord High Chancellor, latterly something

[001-181] restless under the eloquence of that learned gentleman.

[001-182] “Mlud,” says Mr. Tangle. Mr. Tangle knows more of Jarndyce and

[001-183] Jarndyce than anybody. He is famous for it—supposed never to have

[001-184] read anything else since he left school.

[001-185] “Have you nearly concluded your argument?”

[001-186] “Mlud, no—variety of points—feel it my duty tsubmit—ludship,” is

[001-187] the reply that slides out of Mr. Tangle.

[001-188] “Several members of the bar are still to be heard, I believe?” says

[001-189] the Chancellor with a slight smile.

[001-190] Eighteen of Mr. Tangle’s learned friends, each armed with a little

[001-191] summary of eighteen hundred sheets, bob up like eighteen hammers in a

[001-192] pianoforte, make eighteen bows, and drop into their eighteen places

[001-193] of obscurity.

[001-194] “We will proceed with the hearing on Wednesday fortnight,” says the

[001-195] Chancellor. For the question at issue is only a question of costs, a

[001-196] mere bud on the forest tree of the parent suit, and really will come

[001-197] to a settlement one of these days.

[001-198] The Chancellor rises; the bar rises; the prisoner is brought forward

[001-199] in a hurry; the man from Shropshire cries, “My lord!” Maces, bags,

[001-200] and purses indignantly proclaim silence and frown at the man from

[001-201] Shropshire.

[001-202] “In reference,” proceeds the Chancellor, still on Jarndyce and

[001-203] Jarndyce, “to the young girl—”

[001-204] “Begludship’s pardon—boy,” says Mr. Tangle prematurely. “In

[001-205] reference,” proceeds the Chancellor with extra distinctness, “to the

[001-206] young girl and boy, the two young people”—Mr. Tangle crushed—“whom

[001-207] I directed to be in attendance to-day and who are now in my private

[001-208] room, I will see them and satisfy myself as to the expediency of

[001-209] making the order for their residing with their uncle.”

[001-210] Mr. Tangle on his legs again. “Begludship’s pardon—dead.”

[001-211] “With their”—Chancellor looking through his double eye-glass at the

[001-212] papers on his desk—“grandfather.”

[001-213] “Begludship’s pardon—victim of rash action—brains.”

[001-214] Suddenly a very little counsel with a terrific bass voice arises,

[001-215] fully inflated, in the back settlements of the fog, and says, “Will

[001-216] your lordship allow me? I appear for him. He is a cousin, several

[001-217] times removed. I am not at the moment prepared to inform the court in

[001-218] what exact remove he is a cousin, but he IS a cousin.”

[001-219] Leaving this address (delivered like a sepulchral message) ringing in

[001-220] the rafters of the roof, the very little counsel drops, and the fog

[001-221] knows him no more. Everybody looks for him. Nobody can see him.

[001-222] “I will speak with both the young people,” says the Chancellor anew,

[001-223] “and satisfy myself on the subject of their residing with their

[001-224] cousin. I will mention the matter to-morrow morning when I take my

[001-225] seat.”

[001-226] The Chancellor is about to bow to the bar when the prisoner is

[001-227] presented. Nothing can possibly come of the prisoner’s conglomeration

[001-228] but his being sent back to prison, which is soon done. The man from

[001-229] Shropshire ventures another remonstrative “My lord!” but the

[001-230] Chancellor, being aware of him, has dexterously vanished. Everybody

[001-231] else quickly vanishes too. A battery of blue bags is loaded with

[001-232] heavy charges of papers and carried off by clerks; the little mad old

[001-233] woman marches off with her documents; the empty court is locked up.

[001-234] If all the injustice it has committed and all the misery it has

[001-235] caused could only be locked up with it, and the whole burnt away in a

[001-236] great funeral pyre—why so much the better for other parties than the

[001-237] parties in Jarndyce and Jarndyce!
