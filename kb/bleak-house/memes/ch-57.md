[057-001] I had gone to bed and fallen asleep when my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]] knocked at the

[057-002] door of my room and begged me to get up directly. On my hurrying to

[057-003] speak to him and learn what had happened, he told me, after a word or

[057-004] two of preparation, that there had been a discovery at [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester]]

[057-005] Dedlock’s. That my mother had fled, that a person was now at our door

[057-006] who was empowered to convey to her the fullest assurances of

[057-007] affectionate protection and forgiveness if he could possibly find

[057-008] her, and that I was sought for to accompany him in the hope that my

[057-009] entreaties might prevail upon her if his failed. Something to this

[057-010] general purpose I made out, but I was thrown into such a tumult of

[057-011] alarm, and hurry and distress, that in spite of every effort I could

[057-012] make to subdue my agitation, I did not seem, to myself, fully to

[057-013] recover my right mind until hours had passed.

[057-014] But I dressed and wrapped up expeditiously without waking Charley or

[057-015] any one and went down to Mr. Bucket, who was the person entrusted

[057-016] with the secret. In taking me to him my guardian told me this, and

[057-017] also explained how it was that he had come to think of me. Mr.

[057-018] Bucket, in a low voice, by the light of my guardian’s candle, read to

[057-019] me in the hall a letter that my mother had left upon her table; and I

[057-020] suppose within ten minutes of my having been aroused I was sitting

[057-021] beside him, rolling swiftly through the streets.

[057-022] His manner was very keen, and yet considerate when he explained to me

[057-023] that a great deal might depend on my being able to answer, without

[057-024] confusion, a few questions that he wished to ask me. These were,

[057-025] chiefly, whether I had had much communication with my mother (to whom

[057-026] he only referred as [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]]), when and where I had spoken with

[057-027] her last, and how she had become possessed of my handkerchief. When I

[057-028] had satisfied him on these points, he asked me particularly to

[057-029] consider—taking time to think—whether within my knowledge there was

[057-030] any one, no matter where, in whom she might be at all likely to

[057-031] confide under circumstances of the last necessity. I could think of

[057-032] no one but my guardian. But by and by I mentioned Mr. Boythorn. He

[057-033] came into my mind as connected with his old chivalrous manner of

[057-034] mentioning my mother’s name and with what my guardian had informed me

[057-035] of his engagement to her sister and his unconscious connexion with

[057-036] her unhappy story.

[057-037] My companion had stopped the driver while we held this conversation,

[057-038] that we might the better hear each other. He now told him to go on

[057-039] again and said to me, after considering within himself for a few

[057-040] moments, that he had made up his mind how to proceed. He was quite

[057-041] willing to tell me what his plan was, but I did not feel clear enough

[057-042] to understand it.

[057-043] We had not driven very far from our lodgings when we stopped in a

[057-044] by-street at a public-looking place lighted up with gas. Mr. Bucket

[057-045] took me in and sat me in an arm-chair by a bright fire. It was now

[057-046] past one, as I saw by the clock against the wall. Two police

[057-047] officers, looking in their perfectly neat uniform not at all like

[057-048] people who were up all night, were quietly [[document-and-letter|writing]] at a desk; and the

[057-049] place seemed very quiet altogether, except for some beating and

[057-050] calling out at distant doors underground, to which nobody paid any

[057-051] attention.

[057-052] A third man in uniform, whom Mr. Bucket called and to whom he

[057-053] whispered his instructions, went out; and then the two others advised

[057-054] together while one wrote from Mr. Bucket’s subdued dictation. It was

[057-055] a description of my mother that they were busy with, for Mr. Bucket

[057-056] brought it to me when it was done and read it in a whisper. It was

[057-057] very accurate indeed.

[057-058] The second officer, who had attended to it closely, then copied it

[057-059] out and called in another man in uniform (there were several in an

[057-060] outer room), who took it up and went away with it. All this was done

[057-061] with the greatest dispatch and without the waste of a moment; yet

[057-062] nobody was at all hurried. As soon as the paper was sent out upon its

[057-063] travels, the two officers resumed their former quiet work of writing

[057-064] with neatness and care. Mr. Bucket thoughtfully came and warmed the

[057-065] soles of his boots, first one and then the other, at the fire.

[057-066] “Are you well wrapped up, Miss Summerson?” he asked me as his eyes

[057-067] met mine. “It’s a desperate sharp night for a young lady to be out

[057-068] in.”

[057-069] I told him I cared for no weather and was warmly clothed.

[057-070] “It may be a long job,” he observed; “but so that it ends well, never

[057-071] mind, miss.”

[057-072] “I pray to heaven it may end well!” said I.

[057-073] He nodded comfortingly. “You see, whatever you do, don’t you go and

[057-074] fret yourself. You keep yourself cool and equal for anything that may

[057-075] happen, and it’ll be the better for you, the better for me, the

[057-076] better for Lady Dedlock, and the better for Sir Leicester Dedlock,

[057-077] Baronet.”

[057-078] He was really very kind and gentle, and as he stood before the fire

[057-079] warming his boots and rubbing his face with his forefinger, I felt a

[057-080] confidence in his sagacity which reassured me. It was not yet a

[057-081] quarter to two when I heard horses’ feet and wheels outside. “Now,

[057-082] Miss Summerson,” said he, “we are off, if you please!”

[057-083] He gave me his arm, and the two officers courteously bowed me out,

[057-084] and we found at the door a phaeton or barouche with a postilion and

[057-085] post horses. Mr. Bucket handed me in and took his own seat on the

[057-086] box. The man in uniform whom he had sent to fetch this equipage then

[057-087] handed him up a dark lantern at his request, and when he had given a

[057-088] few directions to the driver, we rattled away.

[057-089] I was far from sure that I was not in a dream. We rattled with great

[057-090] rapidity through such a labyrinth of streets that I soon lost all

[057-091] idea where we were, except that we had crossed and re-crossed the

[057-092] river, and still seemed to be traversing a low-lying, waterside,

[057-094] basins, high piles of warehouses, swing-bridges, and masts of ships.

[057-095] At length we stopped at the corner of a little slimy turning, which

[057-096] the wind from the river, rushing up it, did not purify; and I saw my

[057-097] companion, by the light of his lantern, in conference with several

[057-098] men who looked like a mixture of police and sailors. Against the

[057-099] mouldering wall by which they stood, there was a bill, on which I

[057-100] could discern the words, “Found Drowned”; and this and an inscription

[057-102] our visit to that place.

[057-103] I had no need to remind myself that I was not there by the indulgence

[057-104] of any feeling of mine to increase the difficulties of the search, or

[057-105] to lessen its hopes, or enhance its delays. I remained quiet, but

[057-106] what I suffered in that dreadful spot I never can forget. And still

[057-107] it was like the horror of a dream. A man yet dark and muddy, in long

[057-108] swollen sodden boots and a hat like them, was called out of a boat

[057-114] know and defer to) went in with the others at a door and left me in

[057-115] the carriage, while the driver walked up and down by his horses to

[057-116] warm himself. The tide was coming in, as I judged from the sound it

[057-123] darkened his lantern, and once more took his seat. “Don’t you be

[057-124] alarmed, Miss Summerson, on account of our coming down here,” he

[057-125] said, turning to me. “I only want to have everything in train and to

[057-135] he alighted and went back after a shadowy female figure that flitted
