[009-001] I don’t know how it is I seem to be always [[document-and-letter|writing]] about myself. I

[009-002] mean all the time to write about other people, and I try to think

[009-003] about myself as little as possible, and I am sure, when I find myself

[009-004] coming into the story again, I am really vexed and say, “Dear, dear,

[009-005] you tiresome little creature, I wish you wouldn’t!” but it is all of

[009-006] no use. I hope any one who may read what I write will understand that

[009-007] if these pages contain a great deal about me, I can only suppose it

[009-008] must be because I have really something to do with them and can’t be

[009-009] kept out.

[009-010] My darling and I read together, and worked, and practised, and found

[009-011] so much employment for our time that the winter days flew by us like

[009-012] bright-winged birds. Generally in the afternoons, and always in the

[009-013] evenings, [[richard-carstone|Richard]] gave us his company. Although he was one of the

[009-014] most restless creatures in the world, he certainly was very fond of

[009-015] our society.

[009-016] He was very, very, very fond of [[ada-clare|Ada]]. I mean it, and I had better say

[009-017] it at once. I had never seen any young people falling in love before,

[009-018] but I found them out quite soon. I could not say so, of course, or

[009-019] show that I knew anything about it. On the contrary, I was so demure

[009-020] and used to seem so unconscious that sometimes I considered within

[009-021] myself while I was sitting at work whether I was not growing quite

[009-022] deceitful.

[009-023] But there was no help for it. All I had to do was to be quiet, and I

[009-024] was as quiet as a mouse. They were as quiet as mice too, so far as

[009-025] any words were concerned, but the innocent manner in which they

[009-026] relied more and more upon me as they took more and more to one

[009-027] another was so charming that I had great difficulty in not showing

[009-028] how it interested me.

[009-029] “Our dear [[esther-summerson|little old woman]] is such a capital old woman,” Richard

[009-030] would say, coming up to meet me in the garden early, with his

[009-031] pleasant laugh and perhaps the least tinge of a blush, “that I can’t

[009-032] get on without her. Before I begin my harum-scarum day—grinding away

[009-033] at those books and instruments and then galloping up hill and down

[009-034] dale, all the country round, like a highwayman—it does me so much

[009-035] good to come and have a steady walk with our comfortable friend, that

[009-036] here I am again!”

[009-037] “You know, Dame Durden, dear,” Ada would say at night, with her head

[009-038] upon my shoulder and the firelight shining in her thoughtful eyes, “I

[009-039] don’t want to talk when we come upstairs here. Only to sit a little

[009-040] while thinking, with your dear face for company, and to hear the wind

[009-041] and remember the poor sailors at sea—”

[009-042] Ah! Perhaps Richard was going to be a sailor. We had talked it over

[009-043] very often now, and there was some talk of gratifying the inclination

[009-044] of his childhood for the sea. [[john-jarndyce|Mr. Jarndyce]] had written to a relation

[009-045] of the family, a great [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester Dedlock]], for his interest in

[009-046] Richard’s favour, generally; and Sir Leicester had replied in a

[009-047] gracious manner that he would be happy to advance the prospects of

[009-048] the young gentleman if it should ever prove to be within his power,

[009-049] which was not at all probable, and that my Lady sent her compliments

[009-050] to the young gentleman (to whom she perfectly remembered that she was

[009-051] allied by remote consanguinity) and trusted that he would ever do his

[009-052] duty in any honourable [[professional-identity|profession]] to which he might devote himself.

[009-053] “So I apprehend it’s pretty clear,” said Richard to me, “that I shall

[009-054] have to work my own way. Never mind! Plenty of people have had to do

[009-055] that before now, and have done it. I only wish I had the command of a

[009-056] clipping privateer to begin with and could carry off the Chancellor

[009-057] and keep him on short allowance until he gave judgment in our cause.

[009-058] He’d find himself growing thin, if he didn’t look sharp!”

[009-059] With a buoyancy and hopefulness and a gaiety that hardly ever

[009-060] flagged, Richard had a carelessness in his character that quite

[009-061] perplexed me, principally because he mistook it, in such a very odd

[009-062] way, for prudence. It entered into all his calculations about money

[009-063] in a singular manner which I don’t think I can better explain than by

[009-064] reverting for a moment to our loan to [[harold-skimpole|Mr. Skimpole]].

[009-065] Mr. Jarndyce had ascertained the amount, either from Mr. Skimpole

[009-066] himself or from Coavinses, and had placed the money in my hands with

[009-067] instructions to me to retain my own part of it and hand the rest to

[009-068] Richard. The number of little acts of thoughtless expenditure which

[009-069] Richard justified by the recovery of his ten pounds, and the number

[009-070] of times he talked to me as if he had saved or realized that amount,

[009-071] would form a sum in simple addition.

[009-072] “My prudent Mother Hubbard, why not?” he said to me when he wanted,

[009-073] without the least consideration, to bestow five pounds on the

[009-074] brickmaker. “I made ten pounds, clear, out of Coavinses’ business.”

[009-075] “How was that?” said I.

[009-076] “Why, I got rid of ten pounds which I was quite content to get rid of

[009-077] and never expected to see any more. You don’t deny that?”

[009-078] “No,” said I.

[009-079] “Very well! Then I came into possession of ten pounds—”

[009-080] “The same ten pounds,” I hinted.

[009-081] “That has nothing to do with it!” returned Richard. “I have got ten

[009-082] pounds more than I expected to have, and consequently I can afford to

[009-083] spend it without being particular.”

[009-084] In exactly the same way, when he was persuaded out of the sacrifice

[009-085] of these five pounds by being convinced that it would do no good, he

[009-086] carried that sum to his credit and drew upon it.

[009-087] “Let me see!” he would say. “I saved five pounds out of the

[009-088] brickmaker’s affair, so if I have a good rattle to [[london|London]] and back in

[009-089] a post-chaise and put that down at four pounds, I shall have saved

[009-090] one. And it’s a very good thing to save one, let me tell you: a penny

[009-091] saved is a penny got!”

[009-092] I believe Richard’s was as frank and generous a nature as there

[009-093] possibly can be. He was ardent and brave, and in the midst of all his

[009-094] wild restlessness, was so gentle that I knew him like a brother in a

[009-095] few weeks. His gentleness was natural to him and would have shown

[009-096] itself abundantly even without Ada’s influence; but with it, he

[009-097] became one of the most winning of companions, always so ready to be

[009-098] interested and always so happy, sanguine, and light-hearted. I am

[009-099] sure that I, sitting with them, and walking with them, and talking

[009-100] with them, and noticing from day to day how they went on, falling

[009-101] deeper and deeper in love, and saying nothing about it, and each

[009-102] shyly thinking that this love was the greatest of secrets, perhaps

[009-103] not yet suspected even by the other—I am sure that I was scarcely

[009-104] less enchanted than they were and scarcely less pleased with the

[009-105] pretty dream.

[009-106] We were going on in this way, when one morning at breakfast Mr.

[009-107] Jarndyce received a letter, and looking at the superscription, said,

[009-108] “From Boythorn? Aye, aye!” and opened and read it with evident

[009-109] pleasure, announcing to us in a parenthesis when he was about

[009-110] half-way through, that Boythorn was “coming down” on a visit. Now who

[009-111] was Boythorn, we all thought. And I dare say we all thought too—I am

[009-112] sure I did, for one—would Boythorn at all interfere with what was

[009-113] going forward?

[009-114] “I went to school with this fellow, Lawrence Boythorn,” said Mr.

[009-115] Jarndyce, tapping the letter as he laid it on the table, “more than

[009-116] five and forty years ago. He was then the most impetuous boy in the

[009-117] world, and he is now the most impetuous man. He was then the loudest

[009-118] boy in the world, and he is now the loudest man. He was then the

[009-119] heartiest and sturdiest boy in the world, and he is now the heartiest

[009-120] and sturdiest man. He is a tremendous fellow.”

[009-121] “In stature, sir?” asked Richard.

[009-122] “Pretty well, Rick, in that respect,” said Mr. Jarndyce; “being some

[009-123] ten years older than I and a couple of inches taller, with his head

[009-124] thrown back like an old soldier, his stalwart chest squared, his

[009-125] hands like a clean blacksmith’s, and his lungs! There’s no simile for

[009-126] his lungs. Talking, laughing, or snoring, they make the beams of the

[009-127] house shake.”

[009-128] As Mr. Jarndyce sat enjoying the image of his friend Boythorn, we

[009-129] observed the favourable omen that there was not the least indication

[009-130] of any change in the wind.

[009-131] “But it’s the inside of the man, the warm heart of the man, the

[009-132] passion of the man, the fresh blood of the man, Rick—and Ada, and

[009-133] little Cobweb too, for you are all interested in a visitor—that I

[009-134] speak of,” he pursued. “His language is as sounding as his voice. He

[009-135] is always in extremes, perpetually in the superlative degree. In his

[009-136] condemnation he is all ferocity. You might suppose him to be an ogre

[009-137] from what he says, and I believe he has the reputation of one with

[009-138] some people. There! I tell you no more of him beforehand. You must

[009-139] not be surprised to see him take me under his protection, for he has

[009-140] never forgotten that I was a low boy at school and that our

[009-141] friendship began in his knocking two of my head tyrant’s teeth out

[009-142] (he says six) before breakfast. Boythorn and his man,” to me, “will

[009-143] be here this afternoon, my dear.”

[009-144] I took care that the necessary preparations were made for Mr.

[009-145] Boythorn’s reception, and we looked forward to his arrival with some

[009-146] curiosity. The afternoon wore away, however, and he did not appear.

[009-147] The dinner-hour arrived, and still he did not appear. The dinner was

[009-148] put back an hour, and we were sitting round the fire with no light

[009-149] but the blaze when the hall-door suddenly burst open and the hall

[009-150] resounded with these words, uttered with the greatest vehemence and

[009-151] in a stentorian tone: “We have been misdirected, Jarndyce, by a most

[009-152] abandoned ruffian, who told us to take the turning to the right

[009-153] instead of to the left. He is the most intolerable scoundrel on the

[009-154] face of the earth. His father must have been a most consummate

[009-155] villain, ever to have such a son. I would have had that fellow shot

[009-156] without the least remorse!”

[009-157] “Did he do it on purpose?” Mr. Jarndyce inquired.

[009-158] “I have not the slightest doubt that the scoundrel has passed his

[009-159] whole existence in misdirecting travellers!” returned the other. “By

[009-160] my soul, I thought him the worst-looking dog I had ever beheld when

[009-161] he was telling me to take the turning to the right. And yet I stood

[009-162] before that fellow face to face and didn’t knock his brains out!”

[009-163] “Teeth, you mean?” said Mr. Jarndyce.

[009-164] “Ha, ha, ha!” laughed Mr. Lawrence Boythorn, really making the whole

[009-165] house vibrate. “What, you have not forgotten it yet! Ha, ha, ha! And

[009-166] that was another most consummate vagabond! By my soul, the

[009-167] countenance of that fellow when he was a boy was the blackest image

[009-168] of perfidy, cowardice, and cruelty ever set up as a scarecrow in a

[009-169] field of scoundrels. If I were to meet that most unparalleled despot

[009-170] in the streets to-morrow, I would fell him like a rotten tree!”

[009-171] “I have no doubt of it,” said Mr. Jarndyce. “Now, will you come

[009-172] upstairs?”

[009-173] “By my soul, Jarndyce,” returned his guest, who seemed to refer to

[009-174] his watch, “if you had been married, I would have turned back at the

[009-175] garden-gate and gone away to the remotest summits of the Himalaya

[009-176] Mountains sooner than I would have presented myself at this

[009-177] unseasonable hour.”

[009-178] “Not quite so far, I hope?” said Mr. Jarndyce.

[009-179] “By my life and honour, yes!” cried the visitor. “I wouldn’t be

[009-180] guilty of the audacious insolence of keeping a lady of the house

[009-181] waiting all this time for any earthly consideration. I would

[009-182] infinitely rather destroy myself—infinitely rather!”

[009-183] Talking thus, they went upstairs, and presently we heard him in his

[009-184] bedroom thundering “Ha, ha, ha!” and again “Ha, ha, ha!” until the

[009-185] flattest echo in the neighbourhood seemed to catch the contagion and

[009-186] to laugh as enjoyingly as he did or as we did when we heard him

[009-187] laugh.

[009-188] We all conceived a prepossession in his favour, for there was a

[009-189] sterling quality in this laugh, and in his vigorous, healthy voice,

[009-190] and in the roundness and fullness with which he uttered every word he

[009-191] spoke, and in the very fury of his superlatives, which seemed to go

[009-192] off like blank cannons and hurt nothing. But we were hardly prepared

[009-193] to have it so confirmed by his appearance when Mr. Jarndyce presented

[009-194] him. He was not only a very handsome old gentleman—upright and

[009-195] stalwart as he had been described to us—with a massive grey head, a

[009-196] fine composure of face when silent, a figure that might have become

[009-197] corpulent but for his being so continually in earnest that he gave it

[009-198] no rest, and a chin that might have subsided into a double chin but

[009-199] for the vehement emphasis in which it was constantly required to

[009-200] assist; but he was such a true gentleman in his manner, so

[009-201] chivalrously polite, his face was lighted by a smile of so much

[009-202] sweetness and tenderness, and it seemed so plain that he had nothing

[009-203] to hide, but showed himself exactly as he was—incapable, as Richard

[009-204] said, of anything on a limited scale, and firing away with those

[009-205] blank great guns because he carried no small arms whatever—that

[009-206] really I could not help looking at him with equal pleasure as he sat

[009-207] at dinner, whether he smilingly conversed with Ada and me, or was led

[009-208] by Mr. Jarndyce into some great volley of superlatives, or threw up

[009-209] his head like a bloodhound and gave out that tremendous “Ha, ha, ha!”

[009-210] “You have brought your bird with you, I suppose?” said Mr. Jarndyce.

[009-211] “By heaven, he is the most astonishing bird in Europe!” replied the

[009-212] other. “He IS the most wonderful creature! I wouldn’t take ten

[009-213] thousand guineas for that bird. I have left an annuity for his sole

[009-214] support in case he should outlive me. He is, in sense and attachment,

[009-215] a phenomenon. And his father before him was one of the most

[009-216] astonishing birds that ever lived!”

[009-217] The subject of this laudation was a very little canary, who was so

[009-218] tame that he was brought down by Mr. Boythorn’s man, on his

[009-219] forefinger, and after taking a gentle flight round the room, alighted

[009-220] on his master’s head. To hear Mr. Boythorn presently expressing the

[009-221] most implacable and passionate sentiments, with this fragile mite of

[009-222] a creature quietly perched on his forehead, was to have a good

[009-223] illustration of his character, I thought.

[009-224] “By my soul, Jarndyce,” he said, very gently holding up a bit of

[009-225] bread to the canary to peck at, “if I were in your place I would

[009-226] seize every master in [[chancery-court|Chancery]] by the throat to-morrow morning and

[009-227] shake him until his money rolled out of his pockets and his bones

[009-228] rattled in his skin. I would have a settlement out of somebody, by

[009-229] fair means or by foul. If you would empower me to do it, I would do

[009-230] it for you with the greatest satisfaction!” (All this time the very

[009-231] small canary was eating out of his hand.)

[009-232] “I thank you, Lawrence, but the suit is hardly at such a point at

[009-233] present,” returned Mr. Jarndyce, laughing, “that it would be greatly

[009-234] advanced even by the legal process of shaking the bench and the whole

[009-235] bar.”

[009-236] “There never was such an infernal cauldron as that Chancery on the

[009-237] face of the earth!” said Mr. Boythorn. “Nothing but a mine below it

[009-238] on a busy day in term time, with all its records, rules, and

[009-239] precedents collected in it and every functionary belonging to it

[009-240] also, high and low, upward and downward, from its son the

[009-241] Accountant-General to its father the Devil, and the whole blown to

[009-242] atoms with ten thousand hundredweight of gunpowder, would reform it

[009-243] in the least!”

[009-244] It was impossible not to laugh at the energetic gravity with which he

[009-245] recommended this strong measure of reform. When we laughed, he threw

[009-246] up his head and shook his broad chest, and again the whole country

[009-247] seemed to echo to his “Ha, ha, ha!” It had not the least effect in

[009-248] disturbing the bird, whose sense of security was complete and who

[009-249] hopped about the table with its quick head now on this side and now

[009-250] on that, turning its bright sudden eye on its master as if he were no

[009-251] more than another bird.

[009-252] “But how do you and your neighbour get on about the disputed right of

[009-253] way?” said Mr. Jarndyce. “You are not free from the toils of the law

[009-254] yourself!”

[009-255] “The fellow has brought actions against ME for trespass, and I have

[009-256] brought actions against HIM for trespass,” returned Mr. Boythorn. “By

[009-257] heaven, he is the proudest fellow breathing. It is morally impossible

[009-258] that his name can be Sir Leicester. It must be Sir Lucifer.”

[009-259] “Complimentary to our distant relation!” said my guardian laughingly

[009-260] to Ada and Richard.

[009-261] “I would beg Miss Clare’s pardon and Mr. Carstone’s pardon,” resumed

[009-262] our visitor, “if I were not reassured by seeing in the fair face of

[009-263] the lady and the smile of the gentleman that it is quite unnecessary

[009-264] and that they keep their distant relation at a comfortable distance.”

[009-265] “Or he keeps us,” suggested Richard.

[009-266] “By my soul,” exclaimed Mr. Boythorn, suddenly firing another volley,

[009-267] “that fellow is, and his father was, and his grandfather was, the

[009-268] most stiff-necked, arrogant imbecile, pig-headed numskull, ever, by

[009-269] some inexplicable mistake of Nature, born in any station of life but

[009-270] a walking-stick’s! The whole of that family are the most solemnly

[009-271] conceited and consummate blockheads! But it’s no matter; he should

[009-272] not shut up my path if he were fifty baronets melted into one and

[009-273] living in a hundred Chesney Wolds, one within another, like the ivory

[009-274] balls in a Chinese carving. The fellow, by his agent, or secretary,

[009-275] or somebody, writes to me ‘Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet, presents

[009-276] his compliments to Mr. Lawrence Boythorn, and has to call his

[009-277] attention to the fact that the green pathway by the old

[009-278] parsonage-house, now the [[inheritance-and-property|property]] of Mr. Lawrence Boythorn, is Sir

[009-279] Leicester’s right of way, being in fact a portion of the park of

[009-280] [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]], and that Sir Leicester finds it convenient to close up

[009-281] the same.’ I write to the fellow, ‘Mr. Lawrence Boythorn presents his

[009-282] compliments to Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet, and has to call HIS

[009-283] attention to the fact that he totally denies the whole of Sir

[009-284] Leicester Dedlock’s positions on every possible subject and has to

[009-285] add, in reference to closing up the pathway, that he will be glad to

[009-286] see the man who may undertake to do it.’ The fellow sends a most

[009-287] abandoned villain with one eye to construct a gateway. I play upon

[009-288] that execrable scoundrel with a fire-engine until the breath is

[009-289] nearly driven out of his body. The fellow erects a gate in the night.

[009-290] I chop it down and burn it in the morning. He sends his myrmidons to

[009-291] come over the fence and pass and repass. I catch them in humane man

[009-292] traps, fire split peas at their legs, play upon them with the

[009-293] engine—resolve to free mankind from the insupportable burden of the

[009-294] existence of those lurking ruffians. He brings actions for trespass;

[009-295] I bring actions for trespass. He brings actions for assault and

[009-296] battery; I defend them and continue to assault and batter. Ha, ha,

[009-297] ha!”

[009-298] To hear him say all this with unimaginable energy, one might have

[009-299] thought him the angriest of mankind. To see him at the very same

[009-300] time, looking at the bird now perched upon his thumb and softly

[009-301] smoothing its feathers with his forefinger, one might have thought

[009-302] him the gentlest. To hear him laugh and see the broad good nature of

[009-303] his face then, one might have supposed that he had not a care in the

[009-304] world, or a dispute, or a dislike, but that his whole existence was a

[009-305] summer joke.

[009-306] “No, no,” he said, “no closing up of my paths by any Dedlock! Though

[009-307] I willingly confess,” here he softened in a moment, “that Lady

[009-308] Dedlock is the most accomplished lady in the world, to whom I would

[009-309] do any homage that a plain gentleman, and no baronet with a head

[009-310] seven hundred years thick, may. A man who joined his regiment at

[009-311] twenty and within a week challenged the most imperious and

[009-312] presumptuous coxcomb of a commanding officer that ever drew the

[009-313] breath of life through a tight waist—and got broke for it—is not

[009-314] the man to be walked over by all the Sir Lucifers, dead or alive,

[009-315] locked or unlocked. Ha, ha, ha!”

[009-316] “Nor the man to allow his junior to be walked over either?” said my

[009-317] guardian.

[009-318] “Most assuredly not!” said Mr. Boythorn, clapping him on the shoulder

[009-319] with an air of protection that had something serious in it, though he

[009-320] laughed. “He will stand by the low boy, always. Jarndyce, you may

[009-321] rely upon him! But speaking of this trespass—with apologies to Miss

[009-322] Clare and Miss Summerson for the length at which I have pursued so

[009-325] “I think not, Esther?” said Mr. Jarndyce.

[009-326] “Nothing, guardian.”

[009-327] “Much obliged!” said Mr. Boythorn. “Had no need to ask, after even my

[009-328] slight experience of Miss Summerson’s forethought for every one about

[009-329] her.” (They all encouraged me; they were determined to do it.) “I

[009-333] I saw him so often in the course of the evening, which passed very

[009-336] at a little distance from the piano listening to the music—and he

[009-337] had small occasion to tell us that he was passionately fond of music,

[009-338] for his face showed it—that I asked my guardian as we sat at the

[009-340] “No,” said he. “No.”

[009-341] “But he meant to be!” said I.

[009-342] “How did you find out that?” he returned with a smile. “Why,

[009-344] what was in my thoughts, “there is something so tender in his manner,

[009-345] after all, and he is so very courtly and gentle to us, and—”

[009-346] Mr. Jarndyce directed his eyes to where he was sitting as I have just

[009-348] I said no more.

[009-349] “You are right, little woman,” he answered. “He was all but married

[009-351] “Did the lady die?”

[009-352] “No—but she died to him. That time has had its influence on all his

[009-355] “I think, guardian, I might have supposed so. But it is easy to say

[009-357] “He has never since been what he might have been,” said Mr. Jarndyce,

[009-364] was awakened by Mr. Boythorn’s lusty snoring; and I tried to do that

[009-365] very difficult thing, imagine old people young again and invested

[009-366] with the graces of youth. But I fell asleep before I had succeeded,

[009-367] and dreamed of the days when I lived in my godmother’s house. I am

[009-368] not sufficiently acquainted with such subjects to know whether it is
