# Marriage and Property

## Overview

Marriage in *Bleak House* is shadowed by property. Every marriage plot in the novel is entangled with the Jarndyce suit or with the economic realities that Chancery represents. The novel critiques the marriage market — the traffic in women that reduces matrimony to a financial transaction — while also affirming marriage as the site of the novel's only durable happiness. The tension between these two treatments is one of the novel's most productive ambiguities.

## In the Novel

The novel presents a spectrum of marriages and near-marriages. At one pole: the Dedlocks' marriage, a property arrangement hollowed out by secret and shame. Sir Leicester's genuine love for his wife is revealed too late — after her exposure and flight. At the other pole: Esther and Allan Woodcourt, whose marriage is enabled by John Jarndyce's renunciation of his own claim to Esther. Jarndyce's gift is the novel's only successful property transfer — and it is a transfer of a person from one man to another, however benevolent the intention.

Between these poles: Caddy Jellyby's marriage to Prince Turveydrop, a modest happiness achieved by escape from her mother's telescopic philanthropy; the tragic Miss Flite, whose decades of Chancery waiting have precluded marriage entirely; and the Bagnets, a working-class couple whose domestic harmony is the novel's moral baseline. Ada Clare's secret marriage to Richard Carstone — undertaken without her guardian's knowledge — is a gesture of romantic autonomy that proves futile when Richard dies, leaving Ada with an infant and the depleted Jarndyce legacy.

## Dickens' Treatment

The novel's marriage politics are characteristically double. Dickens is savage about the marriage market — the way women are priced and traded — but his solution is individual virtue rather than structural change. John Jarndyce's renunciation is presented as noble rather than patriarchal. Esther's happiness is purchased at the cost of her narrative self-effacement: the novel's final words are "I have been very happy," spoken by a narrator who has systematically downplayed her own desires throughout.

Yet the novel also insists, through counter-examples, that marriage need not be a market transaction. The Bagnets' domestic economy, the Rouncewells' productive partnership, and even the reformed Caddy Jellyby's modest contentment suggest that marriage can be a site of genuine mutuality — if it is insulated from the property logic that Chancery represents.
