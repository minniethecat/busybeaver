[056-001] Impassive, as behoves its high breeding, the Dedlock town house

[056-002] stares at the other houses in the street of dismal grandeur and gives

[056-003] no outward sign of anything going wrong within. Carriages rattle,

[056-004] doors are battered at, the world exchanges calls; ancient charmers

[056-005] with skeleton throats, and peachy cheeks that have a rather ghastly

[056-006] bloom upon them seen by daylight, when indeed these fascinating

[056-007] creatures look like Death and the Lady fused together, dazzle the

[056-008] eyes of men. Forth from the frigid mews come easily swinging

[056-009] carriages guided by short-legged coachmen in flaxen wigs, deep sunk

[056-010] into downy hammercloths, and up behind mount luscious Mercuries

[056-011] bearing sticks of state and wearing cocked hats broadwise, a

[056-012] spectacle for the angels.

[056-013] The Dedlock town house changes not externally, and hours pass before

[056-014] its exalted dullness is disturbed within. But Volumnia the fair,

[056-015] being subject to the prevalent complaint of boredom and finding that

[056-016] disorder attacking her spirits with some virulence, ventures at

[056-017] length to repair to the library for change of scene. Her gentle

[056-018] tapping at the door producing no response, she opens it and peeps in;

[056-019] seeing no one there, takes possession.

[056-020] The sprightly Dedlock is reputed, in that grass-grown city of the

[056-021] ancients, Bath, to be stimulated by an urgent curiosity which impels

[056-022] her on all convenient and inconvenient occasions to sidle about with

[056-023] a golden glass at her eye, peering into objects of every description.

[056-024] Certain it is that she avails herself of the present opportunity of

[056-025] hovering over her kinsman’s [[document-and-letter|letters]] and papers like a bird, taking a

[056-026] short peck at this document and a blink with her head on one side at

[056-027] that document, and hopping about from table to table with her glass

[056-028] at her eye in an inquisitive and restless manner. In the course of

[056-029] these researches she stumbles over something, and turning her glass

[056-030] in that direction, sees her kinsman lying on the ground like a felled

[056-031] tree.

[056-032] Volumnia’s pet little scream acquires a considerable augmentation of

[056-033] reality from this surprise, and the house is quickly in commotion.

[056-034] Servants tear up and down stairs, bells are violently rung, doctors

[056-035] are sent for, and [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]] is sought in all directions, but not

[056-036] found. Nobody has seen or heard her since she last rang her bell. Her

[056-037] letter to [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester]] is discovered on her table, but it is

[056-038] doubtful yet whether he has not received another missive from another

[056-039] world requiring to be personally answered, and all the living

[056-040] languages, and all the dead, are as one to him.

[056-041] They lay him down upon his bed, and chafe, and rub, and fan, and put

[056-042] ice to his head, and try every means of restoration. Howbeit, the day

[056-043] has ebbed away, and it is night in his room before his stertorous

[056-044] breathing lulls or his fixed eyes show any consciousness of the

[056-045] candle that is occasionally passed before them. But when this change

[056-046] begins, it goes on; and by and by he nods or moves his eyes or even

[056-047] his hand in token that he hears and comprehends.

[056-048] He fell down, this morning, a handsome stately gentleman, somewhat

[056-049] infirm, but of a fine presence, and with a well-filled face. He lies

[056-050] upon his bed, an aged man with sunken cheeks, the decrepit shadow of

[056-051] himself. His voice was rich and mellow and he had so long been

[056-052] thoroughly persuaded of the weight and import to mankind of any word

[056-053] he said that his words really had come to sound as if there were

[056-054] something in them. But now he can only whisper, and what he whispers

[056-055] sounds like what it is—mere jumble and jargon.

[056-056] His favourite and faithful housekeeper stands at his bedside. It is

[056-057] the first act he notices, and he clearly derives pleasure from it.

[056-058] After vainly trying to make himself understood in speech, he makes

[056-059] signs for a pencil. So inexpressively that they cannot at first

[056-060] understand him; it is his old housekeeper who makes out what he wants

[056-061] and brings in a slate.

[056-062] After pausing for some time, he slowly scrawls upon it in a hand that

[056-063] is not his, “[[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]]?”

[056-064] No, she tells him; he is in [[london|London]]. He was taken ill in the library

[056-065] this morning. Right thankful she is that she happened to come to

[056-066] London and is able to attend upon him.

[056-067] “It is not an illness of any serious consequence, Sir Leicester. You

[056-068] will be much better to-morrow, Sir Leicester. All the gentlemen say

[056-069] so.” This, with the tears coursing down her fair old face.

[056-070] After making a survey of the room and looking with particular

[056-071] attention all round the bed where the doctors stand, he writes, “My

[056-072] Lady.”

[056-073] “My Lady went out, Sir Leicester, before you were taken ill, and

[056-074] don’t know of your illness yet.”

[056-075] He points again, in great agitation, at the two words. They all try

[056-076] to quiet him, but he points again with increased agitation. On their

[056-077] looking at one another, not knowing what to say, he takes the slate

[056-078] once more and writes “My Lady. For God’s sake, where?” And makes an

[056-079] imploring moan.

[056-080] It is thought better that his old housekeeper should give him Lady

[056-081] Dedlock’s letter, the contents of which no one knows or can surmise.

[056-082] She opens it for him and puts it out for his perusal. Having read it

[056-083] twice by a great effort, he turns it down so that it shall not be

[056-084] seen and lies moaning. He passes into a kind of relapse or into a

[056-085] swoon, and it is an hour before he opens his eyes, reclining on his

[056-086] faithful and attached old servant’s arm. The doctors know that he is

[056-087] best with her, and when not actively engaged about him, stand aloof.

[056-088] The slate comes into requisition again, but the word he wants to

[056-089] write he cannot remember. His anxiety, his eagerness, and affliction

[056-090] at this pass are pitiable to behold. It seems as if he must go mad in

[056-091] the necessity he feels for haste and the inability under which he

[056-092] labours of expressing to do what or to fetch whom. He has written the

[056-093] letter B, and there stopped. Of a sudden, in the height of his

[056-094] misery, he puts Mr. before it. The old housekeeper suggests Bucket.

[056-095] Thank heaven! That’s his meaning.

[056-096] Mr. Bucket is found to be downstairs, by appointment. Shall he come

[056-097] up?

[056-098] There is no possibility of misconstruing Sir Leicester’s burning wish

[056-099] to see him or the desire he signifies to have the room cleared of

[056-100] every one but the housekeeper. It is speedily done, and Mr. Bucket

[056-101] appears. Of all men upon earth, Sir Leicester seems fallen from his

[056-102] high [[inheritance-and-property|estate]] to place his sole trust and reliance upon this man.

[056-103] “Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet, I’m sorry to see you like this. I

[056-104] hope you’ll cheer up. I’m sure you will, on account of the family

[056-105] credit.”

[056-106] Sir Leicester puts her letter in his hands and looks intently in his

[056-107] face while he reads it. A new intelligence comes into Mr. Bucket’s

[056-108] eye as he reads on; with one hook of his finger, while that eye is

[056-109] still glancing over the words, he indicates, “Sir Leicester Dedlock,

[056-110] Baronet, I understand you.”

[056-111] Sir Leicester writes upon the slate. “Full forgiveness. Find—” Mr.

[056-112] Bucket stops his hand.

[056-113] “Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet, I’ll find her. But my search after

[056-114] her must be begun out of hand. Not a minute must be lost.”

[056-115] With the quickness of thought, he follows Sir Leicester Dedlock’s

[056-116] look towards a little box upon a table.

[056-117] “Bring it here, Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet? Certainly. Open it

[056-118] with one of these here keys? Certainly. The littlest key? TO be sure.

[056-119] Take the notes out? So I will. Count ’em? That’s soon done. Twenty

[056-120] and thirty’s fifty, and twenty’s seventy, and fifty’s one twenty, and

[056-121] forty’s one sixty. Take ’em for expenses? That I’ll do, and render an

[056-122] account of course. Don’t spare money? No I won’t.”

[056-123] The velocity and certainty of Mr. Bucket’s interpretation on all

[056-124] these heads is little short of miraculous. Mrs. Rouncewell, who holds

[056-125] the light, is giddy with the swiftness of his eyes and hands as he

[056-126] starts up, furnished for his journey.

[056-127] “You’re [[george-rouncewell|George]]’s mother, old lady; that’s about what you are, I

[056-128] believe?” says Mr. Bucket aside, with his hat already on and

[056-129] buttoning his coat.

[056-130] “Yes, sir, I am his distressed mother.”

[056-131] “So I thought, according to what he mentioned to me just now. Well,

[056-132] then, I’ll tell you something. You needn’t be distressed no more.

[056-133] Your son’s all right. Now, don’t you begin a-crying, because what

[056-134] you’ve got to do is to take care of Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet,

[056-135] and you won’t do that by crying. As to your son, he’s all right, I

[056-136] tell you; and he sends his loving duty, and hoping you’re the same.

[056-137] He’s discharged honourable; that’s about what HE is; with no more

[056-138] imputation on his character than there is on yours, and yours is a

[056-139] tidy one, I’LL bet a pound. You may trust me, for I took your son. He

[056-140] conducted himself in a game way, too, on that occasion; and he’s a

[056-141] fine-made man, and you’re a fine-made old lady, and you’re a mother

[056-142] and son, the pair of you, as might be showed for models in a caravan.

[056-143] Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet, what you’ve trusted to me I’ll go

[056-144] through with. Don’t you be afraid of my turning out of my way, right

[056-145] or left, or taking a sleep, or a wash, or a shave till I have found

[056-146] what I go in search of. Say everything as is kind and forgiving on

[056-147] your part? Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet, I will. And I wish you

[056-148] better, and these family affairs smoothed over—as, Lord, many other

[056-149] family affairs equally has been, and equally will be, to the end of

[056-150] time.”

[056-151] With this peroration, Mr. Bucket, buttoned up, goes quietly out,

[056-152] looking steadily before him as if he were already piercing the night

[056-153] in quest of the fugitive.

[056-154] His first step is to take himself to Lady Dedlock’s rooms and look

[056-155] all over them for any trifling indication that may help him. The

[056-156] rooms are in darkness now; and to see Mr. Bucket with a wax-light in

[056-157] his hand, holding it above his head and taking a sharp mental

[056-158] inventory of the many delicate objects so curiously at variance with

[056-159] himself, would be to see a sight—which nobody DOES see, as he is

[056-160] particular to lock himself in.

[056-161] “A spicy boudoir, this,” says Mr. Bucket, who feels in a manner

[056-162] furbished up in his French by the blow of the morning. “Must have

[056-163] cost a sight of money. Rum articles to cut away from, these; she must

[056-164] have been hard put to it!”

[056-165] Opening and shutting table-drawers and looking into caskets and

[056-166] jewel-cases, he sees the reflection of himself in various mirrors,

[056-167] and moralizes thereon.

[056-168] “One might suppose I was a-moving in the fashionable circles and

[056-169] getting myself up for Almack’s,” says Mr. Bucket. “I begin to think I

[056-170] must be a swell in the Guards without knowing it.”

[056-171] Ever looking about, he has opened a dainty little chest in an inner

[056-172] drawer. His great hand, turning over some gloves which it can

[056-173] scarcely feel, they are so light and soft within it, comes upon a

[056-174] white handkerchief.

[056-175] “Hum! Let’s have a look at YOU,” says Mr. Bucket, putting down the

[056-176] light. “What should YOU be kept by yourself for? What’s YOUR motive?

[056-177] Are you her ladyship’s property, or somebody else’s? You’ve got a

[056-178] mark upon you somewheres or another, I suppose?”

[056-179] He finds it as he speaks, “[[esther-summerson|Esther Summerson]].”

[056-180] “Oh!” says Mr. Bucket, pausing, with his finger at his ear. “Come,

[056-181] I’ll take YOU.”

[056-182] He completes his observations as quietly and carefully as he has

[056-183] carried them on, leaves everything else precisely as he found it,

[056-184] glides away after some five minutes in all, and passes into the

[056-185] street. With a glance upward at the dimly lighted windows of Sir

[056-186] Leicester’s room, he sets off, full-swing, to the nearest

[056-187] coach-stand, picks out the horse for his money, and directs to be

[056-188] driven to the shooting gallery. Mr. Bucket does not claim to be a

[056-189] scientific judge of horses, but he lays out a little money on the

[056-190] principal events in that line, and generally sums up his knowledge of

[056-191] the subject in the remark that when he sees a horse as can go, he

[056-192] knows him.

[056-193] His knowledge is not at fault in the present instance. Clattering

[056-194] over the stones at a dangerous pace, yet thoughtfully bringing his

[056-195] keen eyes to bear on every slinking creature whom he passes in the

[056-196] midnight streets, and even on the lights in upper windows where

[056-197] people are going or gone to bed, and on all the turnings that he

[056-198] rattles by, and alike on the heavy sky, and on the earth where the

[056-199] snow lies thin—for something may present itself to assist him,

[056-200] anywhere—he dashes to his destination at such a speed that when he

[056-201] stops the horse half smothers him in a cloud of steam.

[056-202] “Unbear him half a moment to freshen him up, and I’ll be back.”

[056-203] He runs up the long wooden entry and finds the trooper smoking his

[056-204] pipe.

[056-205] “I thought I should, George, after what you have gone through, my

[056-206] lad. I haven’t a word to spare. Now, honour! All to save a woman.

[056-207] Miss Summerson that was here when Gridley died—that was the name, I

[056-208] know—all right—where does she live?”

[056-209] The trooper has just come from there and gives him the address, near

[056-210] Oxford Street.

[056-211] “You won’t repent it, George. Good night!”

[056-212] He is off again, with an impression of having seen Phil sitting by

[056-213] the frosty fire staring at him open-mouthed, and gallops away again,

[056-214] and gets out in a cloud of steam again.

[056-215] [[john-jarndyce|Mr. Jarndyce]], the only person up in the house, is just going to bed,

[056-216] rises from his book on hearing the rapid ringing at the bell, and

[056-217] comes down to the door in his dressing-gown.

[056-218] “Don’t be alarmed, sir.” In a moment his visitor is confidential with

[056-219] him in the hall, has shut the door, and stands with his hand upon the

[056-220] lock. “I’ve had the pleasure of seeing you before. Inspector Bucket.

[056-221] Look at that handkerchief, sir, Miss Esther Summerson’s. Found it

[056-222] myself put away in a drawer of Lady Dedlock’s, quarter of an hour

[056-223] ago. Not a moment to lose. Matter of life or death. You know Lady

[056-224] Dedlock?”

[056-225] “Yes.”

[056-226] “There has been a discovery there to-day. Family affairs have come

[056-227] out. Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet, has had a fit—apoplexy or

[056-228] paralysis—and couldn’t be brought to, and precious time has been

[056-229] lost. Lady Dedlock disappeared this afternoon and left a letter for

[056-230] him that looks bad. Run your eye over it. Here it is!”

[056-231] Mr. Jarndyce, having read it, asks him what he thinks.

[056-232] “I don’t know. It looks like suicide. Anyways, there’s more and more

[056-233] danger, every minute, of its drawing to that. I’d give a hundred

[056-234] pound an hour to have got the start of the present time. Now, Mr.

[056-235] Jarndyce, I am employed by Sir Leicester Dedlock, Baronet, to follow

[056-236] her and find her, to save her and take her his forgiveness. I have

[056-237] money and full power, but I want something else. I want Miss

[056-238] Summerson.”

[056-239] Mr. Jarndyce in a troubled voice repeats, “Miss Summerson?”

[056-240] “Now, Mr. Jarndyce”—Mr. Bucket has read his face with the greatest

[056-241] attention all along—“I speak to you as a gentleman of a humane

[056-242] heart, and under such pressing circumstances as don’t often happen.

[056-243] If ever delay was dangerous, it’s dangerous now; and if ever you

[056-244] couldn’t afterwards forgive yourself for causing it, this is the

[056-245] time. Eight or ten hours, worth, as I tell you, a hundred pound

[056-246] apiece at least, have been lost since Lady Dedlock disappeared. I am

[056-247] charged to find her. I am Inspector Bucket. Besides all the rest

[056-248] that’s heavy on her, she has upon her, as she believes, suspicion of

[056-249] murder. If I follow her alone, she, being in ignorance of what Sir

[056-250] Leicester Dedlock, Baronet, has communicated to me, may be driven to

[056-251] desperation. But if I follow her in company with a young lady,

[056-252] answering to the description of a young lady that she has a

[056-253] tenderness for—I ask no question, and I say no more than that—she

[056-254] will give me credit for being friendly. Let me come up with her and

[056-255] be able to have the hold upon her of putting that young lady for’ard,

[056-256] and I’ll save her and prevail with her if she is alive. Let me come

[056-257] up with her alone—a hard matter—and I’ll do my best, but I don’t

[056-258] answer for what the best may be. Time flies; it’s getting on for one

[056-259] o’clock. When one strikes, there’s another hour gone, and it’s worth

[056-260] a thousand pound now instead of a hundred.”

[056-261] This is all true, and the pressing nature of the case cannot be

[056-262] questioned. Mr. Jarndyce begs him to remain there while he speaks to

[056-263] Miss Summerson. Mr. Bucket says he will, but acting on his usual

[056-264] principle, does no such thing, following upstairs instead and keeping

[056-265] his man in sight. So he remains, dodging and lurking about in the

[056-266] gloom of the staircase while they confer. In a very little time Mr.

[056-267] Jarndyce comes down and tells him that Miss Summerson will join him

[056-268] directly and place herself under his protection to accompany him

[056-269] where he pleases. Mr. Bucket, satisfied, expresses high approval and

[056-270] awaits her coming at the door.

[056-271] There he mounts a high tower in his mind and looks out far and wide.

[056-272] Many solitary figures he perceives creeping through the streets; many

[056-273] solitary figures out on heaths, and roads, and lying under haystacks.

[056-274] But the figure that he seeks is not among them. Other solitaries he

[056-275] perceives, in nooks of bridges, looking over; and in shadowed places

[056-276] down by the river’s level; and a dark, dark, shapeless object

[056-277] drifting with the tide, more solitary than all, clings with a

[056-278] drowning hold on his attention.

[056-279] Where is she? Living or dead, where is she? If, as he folds the

[056-280] handkerchief and carefully puts it up, it were able with an enchanted

[056-281] power to bring before him the place where she found it and the

[056-282] night-landscape near the cottage where it covered the little child,

[056-283] would he descry her there? On the waste where the brick-kilns are

[056-284] burning with a pale blue flare, where the straw-roofs of the wretched

[056-285] huts in which the bricks are made are being scattered by the wind,

[056-286] where the clay and water are hard frozen and the mill in which the

[056-287] gaunt blind horse goes round all day looks like an instrument of

[056-288] human torture—traversing this deserted, blighted spot there is a

[056-289] lonely figure with the sad world to itself, pelted by the snow and

[056-290] driven by the wind, and cast out, it would seem, from all

[056-291] companionship. It is the figure of a woman, too; but it is miserably

[056-292] dressed, and no such clothes ever came through the hall and out at

[056-293] the great door of the Dedlock mansion.
