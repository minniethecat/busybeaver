[014-001] [[richard-carstone|Richard]] left us on the very next evening to begin his new career, and

[014-002] committed [[ada-clare|Ada]] to my charge with great love for her and great trust in

[014-003] me. It touched me then to reflect, and it touches me now, more

[014-004] nearly, to remember (having what I have to tell) how they both

[014-005] thought of me, even at that engrossing time. I was a part of all

[014-006] their plans, for the present and the future. I was to write Richard

[014-007] once a week, making my faithful report of Ada, who was to write to

[014-008] him every alternate day. I was to be informed, under his own hand, of

[014-009] all his labours and successes; I was to observe how resolute and

[014-010] persevering he would be; I was to be Ada’s bridesmaid when they were

[014-011] married; I was to live with them afterwards; I was to keep all the

[014-012] keys of their house; I was to be made happy for ever and a day.

[014-013] “And if the suit SHOULD make us rich, [[esther-summerson|Esther]]—which it may, you

[014-014] know!” said Richard to crown all.

[014-015] A shade crossed Ada’s face.

[014-016] “My dearest Ada,” asked Richard, “why not?”

[014-017] “It had better declare us poor at once,” said Ada.

[014-018] “Oh! I don’t know about that,” returned Richard, “but at all events,

[014-019] it won’t declare anything at once. It hasn’t declared anything in

[014-020] heaven knows how many years.”

[014-021] “Too true,” said Ada.

[014-022] “Yes, but,” urged Richard, answering what her look suggested rather

[014-023] than her words, “the longer it goes on, dear cousin, the nearer it

[014-024] must be to a settlement one way or other. Now, is not that

[014-025] reasonable?”

[014-026] “You know best, Richard. But I am afraid if we trust to it, it will

[014-027] make us unhappy.”

[014-028] “But, my Ada, we are not going to trust to it!” cried Richard gaily.

[014-029] “We know it better than to trust to it. We only say that if it SHOULD

[014-030] make us rich, we have no constitutional objection to being rich. The

[014-031] court is, by solemn settlement of law, our grim old [[john-jarndyce|guardian]], and we

[014-032] are to suppose that what it gives us (when it gives us anything) is

[014-033] our right. It is not necessary to quarrel with our right.”

[014-034] “No,” said Ada, “but it may be better to forget all about it.”

[014-035] “Well, well,” cried Richard, “then we will forget all about it! We

[014-036] consign the whole thing to oblivion. Dame Durden puts on her

[014-037] approving face, and it’s done!”

[014-038] “Dame Durden’s approving face,” said I, looking out of the box in

[014-039] which I was packing his books, “was not very visible when you called

[014-040] it by that name; but it does approve, and she thinks you can’t do

[014-041] better.”

[014-042] So, Richard said there was an end of it, and immediately began, on no

[014-043] other foundation, to build as many castles in the air as would man

[014-044] the Great Wall of China. He went away in high spirits. Ada and I,

[014-045] prepared to miss him very much, commenced our quieter career.

[014-046] On our arrival in [[london|London]], we had called with Mr. Jarndyce at Mrs.

[014-047] Jellyby’s but had not been so fortunate as to find her at home. It

[014-048] appeared that she had gone somewhere to a tea-drinking and had taken

[014-049] Miss Jellyby with her. Besides the tea-drinking, there was to be some

[014-050] considerable speech-making and letter-[[document-and-letter|writing]] on the general merits

[014-051] of the cultivation of coffee, conjointly with natives, at the

[014-052] Settlement of [[telescopic-philanthropy|Borrioboola-Gha]]. All this involved, no doubt,

[014-053] sufficient active exercise of pen and ink to make her daughter’s part

[014-054] in the proceedings anything but a holiday.

[014-055] It being now beyond the time appointed for Mrs. Jellyby’s return, we

[014-056] called again. She was in town, but not at home, having gone to Mile

[014-057] End directly after breakfast on some Borrioboolan business, arising

[014-058] out of a society called the East London Branch Aid Ramification. As I

[014-059] had not seen Peepy on the occasion of our last call (when he was not

[014-060] to be found anywhere, and when the cook rather thought he must have

[014-061] strolled away with the dustman’s cart), I now inquired for him again.

[014-062] The oyster shells he had been building a house with were still in the

[014-063] passage, but he was nowhere discoverable, and the cook supposed that

[014-064] he had “gone after the sheep.” When we repeated, with some surprise,

[014-065] “The sheep?” she said, Oh, yes, on market days he sometimes followed

[014-066] them quite out of town and came back in such a state as never was!

[014-067] I was sitting at the window with my guardian on the following

[014-068] morning, and Ada was busy writing—of course to Richard—when Miss

[014-069] Jellyby was announced, and entered, leading the identical Peepy, whom

[014-070] she had made some endeavours to render presentable by wiping the dirt

[014-071] into corners of his face and hands and making his hair very wet and

[014-072] then violently frizzling it with her fingers. Everything the dear

[014-073] child wore was either too large for him or too small. Among his other

[014-074] contradictory decorations he had the hat of a bishop and the little

[014-075] gloves of a baby. His boots were, on a small scale, the boots of a

[014-076] ploughman, while his legs, so crossed and recrossed with scratches

[014-077] that they looked like maps, were bare below a very short pair of

[014-078] plaid drawers finished off with two frills of perfectly different

[014-079] patterns. The deficient buttons on his plaid frock had evidently been

[014-080] supplied from one of Mr. Jellyby’s coats, they were so extremely

[014-081] brazen and so much too large. Most extraordinary specimens of

[014-082] needlework appeared on several parts of his dress, where it had been

[014-083] hastily mended, and I recognized the same hand on Miss Jellyby’s. She

[014-084] was, however, unaccountably improved in her appearance and looked

[014-085] very pretty. She was conscious of poor little Peepy being but a

[014-086] failure after all her trouble, and she showed it as she came in by

[014-087] the way in which she glanced first at him and then at us.

[014-088] “Oh, dear me!” said my guardian. “Due east!”

[014-089] Ada and I gave her a cordial welcome and presented her to Mr.

[014-090] Jarndyce, to whom she said as she sat down, “Ma’s compliments, and

[014-091] she hopes you’ll excuse her, because she’s correcting proofs of the

[014-092] plan. She’s going to put out five thousand new circulars, and she

[014-093] knows you’ll be interested to hear that. I have brought one of them

[014-094] with me. Ma’s compliments.” With which she presented it sulkily

[014-095] enough.

[014-096] “Thank you,” said my guardian. “I am much obliged to Mrs. Jellyby.

[014-097] Oh, dear me! This is a very trying wind!”

[014-098] We were busy with Peepy, taking off his clerical hat, asking him if

[014-099] he remembered us, and so on. Peepy retired behind his elbow at first,

[014-100] but relented at the sight of sponge-cake and allowed me to take him

[014-101] on my lap, where he sat munching quietly. Mr. Jarndyce then

[014-102] withdrawing into the temporary growlery, Miss Jellyby opened a

[014-103] conversation with her usual abruptness.

[014-104] “We are going on just as bad as ever in Thavies Inn,” said she. “I

[014-105] have no peace of my life. Talk of Africa! I couldn’t be worse off if

[014-106] I was a what’s-his-name—man and a brother!”

[014-107] I tried to say something soothing.

[014-108] “Oh, it’s of no use, Miss Summerson,” exclaimed Miss Jellyby, “though

[014-109] I thank you for the kind intention all the same. I know how I am

[014-110] used, and I am not to be talked over. YOU wouldn’t be talked over if

[014-111] you were used so. Peepy, go and play at Wild Beasts under the piano!”

[014-112] “I shan’t!” said Peepy.

[014-113] “Very well, you ungrateful, naughty, hard-hearted boy!” returned Miss

[014-114] Jellyby with tears in her eyes. “I’ll never take pains to dress you

[014-115] any more.”

[014-116] “Yes, I will go, [[caddy-jellyby|Caddy]]!” cried Peepy, who was really a good child and

[014-117] who was so moved by his sister’s vexation that he went at once.

[014-118] “It seems a little thing to cry about,” said poor Miss Jellyby

[014-119] apologetically, “but I am quite worn out. I was directing the new

[014-120] circulars till two this morning. I detest the whole thing so that

[014-121] that alone makes my head ache till I can’t see out of my eyes. And

[014-122] look at that poor unfortunate child! Was there ever such a fright as

[014-123] he is!”

[014-124] Peepy, happily unconscious of the defects in his appearance, sat on

[014-125] the carpet behind one of the legs of the piano, looking calmly out of

[014-126] his den at us while he ate his cake.

[014-127] “I have sent him to the other end of the room,” observed Miss

[014-128] Jellyby, drawing her chair nearer ours, “because I don’t want him to

[014-129] hear the conversation. Those little things are so sharp! I was going

[014-130] to say, we really are going on worse than ever. Pa will be a bankrupt

[014-131] before long, and then I hope Ma will be satisfied. There’ll be nobody

[014-132] but Ma to thank for it.”

[014-133] We said we hoped Mr. Jellyby’s affairs were not in so bad a state as

[014-134] that.

[014-135] “It’s of no use hoping, though it’s very kind of you,” returned Miss

[014-136] Jellyby, shaking her head. “Pa told me only yesterday morning (and

[014-137] dreadfully unhappy he is) that he couldn’t weather the storm. I

[014-138] should be surprised if he could. When all our tradesmen send into our

[014-139] house any stuff they like, and the servants do what they like with

[014-140] it, and I have no time to improve things if I knew how, and Ma don’t

[014-141] care about anything, I should like to make out how Pa is to weather

[014-142] the storm. I declare if I was Pa, I’d run away.”

[014-143] “My dear!” said I, smiling. “Your papa, no doubt, considers his

[014-144] family.”

[014-145] “Oh, yes, his family is all very fine, Miss Summerson,” replied Miss

[014-146] Jellyby; “but what comfort is his family to him? His family is

[014-147] nothing but bills, dirt, waste, noise, tumbles downstairs, confusion,

[014-148] and wretchedness. His scrambling home, from week’s end to week’s end,

[014-149] is like one great washing-day—only nothing’s washed!”

[014-150] Miss Jellyby tapped her foot upon the floor and wiped her eyes.

[014-151] “I am sure I pity Pa to that degree,” she said, “and am so angry with

[014-152] Ma that I can’t find words to express myself! However, I am not going

[014-153] to bear it, I am determined. I won’t be a slave all my life, and I

[014-154] won’t submit to be proposed to by Mr. Quale. A pretty thing, indeed,

[014-155] to marry a philanthropist. As if I hadn’t had enough of THAT!” said

[014-156] poor Miss Jellyby.

[014-157] I must confess that I could not help feeling rather angry with Mrs.

[014-158] Jellyby myself, seeing and hearing this neglected girl and knowing

[014-159] how much of bitterly satirical truth there was in what she said.

[014-160] “If it wasn’t that we had been intimate when you stopped at our

[014-161] house,” pursued Miss Jellyby, “I should have been ashamed to come

[014-162] here to-day, for I know what a figure I must seem to you two. But as

[014-163] it is, I made up my mind to call, especially as I am not likely to

[014-164] see you again the next time you come to town.”

[014-165] She said this with such great significance that Ada and I glanced at

[014-166] one another, foreseeing something more.

[014-167] “No!” said Miss Jellyby, shaking her head. “Not at all likely! I know

[014-168] I may trust you two. I am sure you won’t betray me. I am engaged.”

[014-169] “Without their knowledge at home?” said I.

[014-170] “Why, good gracious me, Miss Summerson,” she returned, justifying

[014-171] herself in a fretful but not angry manner, “how can it be otherwise?

[014-172] You know what Ma is—and I needn’t make poor Pa more miserable by

[014-173] telling HIM.”

[014-174] “But would it not be adding to his unhappiness to marry without his

[014-175] knowledge or consent, my dear?” said I.

[014-176] “No,” said Miss Jellyby, softening. “I hope not. I should try to make

[014-177] him happy and comfortable when he came to see me, and Peepy and the

[014-178] others should take it in turns to come and stay with me, and they

[014-179] should have some care taken of them then.”

[014-180] There was a good deal of affection in poor Caddy. She softened more

[014-181] and more while saying this and cried so much over the unwonted little

[014-182] home-picture she had raised in her mind that Peepy, in his cave under

[014-183] the piano, was touched, and turned himself over on his back with loud

[014-184] lamentations. It was not until I had brought him to kiss his sister,

[014-185] and had restored him to his place on my lap, and had shown him that

[014-186] Caddy was laughing (she laughed expressly for the purpose), that we

[014-187] could recall his peace of mind; even then it was for some time

[014-188] conditional on his taking us in turns by the chin and smoothing our

[014-189] faces all over with his hand. At last, as his spirits were not equal

[014-190] to the piano, we put him on a chair to look out of window; and Miss

[014-191] Jellyby, holding him by one leg, resumed her confidence.

[014-192] “It began in your coming to our house,” she said.

[014-193] We naturally asked how.

[014-194] “I felt I was so awkward,” she replied, “that I made up my mind to be

[014-195] improved in that respect at all events and to learn to dance. I told

[014-196] Ma I was ashamed of myself, and I must be taught to dance. Ma looked

[014-197] at me in that provoking way of hers as if I wasn’t in sight, but I

[014-198] was quite determined to be taught to dance, and so I went to Mr.

[014-199] Turveydrop’s Academy in Newman Street.”

[014-200] “And was it there, my dear—” I began.

[014-201] “Yes, it was there,” said Caddy, “and I am engaged to Mr. Turveydrop.

[014-202] There are two Mr. Turveydrops, father and son. My Mr. Turveydrop is

[014-203] the son, of course. I only wish I had been better brought up and was

[014-204] likely to make him a better wife, for I am very fond of him.”

[014-205] “I am sorry to hear this,” said I, “I must confess.”

[014-206] “I don’t know why you should be sorry,” she retorted a little

[014-207] anxiously, “but I am engaged to Mr. Turveydrop, whether or no, and he

[014-208] is very fond of me. It’s a secret as yet, even on his side, because

[014-209] old Mr. Turveydrop has a share in the connexion and it might break

[014-210] his heart or give him some other shock if he was told of it abruptly.

[014-211] Old Mr. Turveydrop is a very gentlemanly man indeed—very

[014-212] gentlemanly.”

[014-213] “Does his wife know of it?” asked Ada.

[014-214] “Old Mr. Turveydrop’s wife, Miss Clare?” returned Miss Jellyby,

[014-215] opening her eyes. “There’s no such person. He is a widower.”

[014-216] We were here interrupted by Peepy, whose leg had undergone so much on

[014-217] account of his sister’s unconsciously jerking it like a bell-rope

[014-218] whenever she was emphatic that the afflicted child now bemoaned his

[014-219] sufferings with a very low-spirited noise. As he appealed to me for

[014-220] compassion, and as I was only a listener, I undertook to hold him.

[014-221] Miss Jellyby proceeded, after begging Peepy’s pardon with a kiss and

[014-222] assuring him that she hadn’t meant to do it.

[014-223] “That’s the state of the case,” said Caddy. “If I ever blame myself,

[014-224] I still think it’s Ma’s fault. We are to be married whenever we can,

[014-225] and then I shall go to Pa at the office and write to Ma. It won’t

[014-226] much agitate Ma; I am only pen and ink to HER. One great comfort is,”

[014-227] said Caddy with a sob, “that I shall never hear of Africa after I am

[014-228] married. Young Mr. Turveydrop hates it for my sake, and if old Mr.

[014-229] Turveydrop knows there is such a place, it’s as much as he does.”

[014-230] “It was he who was very gentlemanly, I think!” said I.

[014-231] “Very gentlemanly indeed,” said Caddy. “He is celebrated almost

[014-232] everywhere for his deportment.”

[014-233] “Does he teach?” asked Ada.

[014-234] “No, he don’t teach anything in particular,” replied Caddy. “But his

[014-235] deportment is beautiful.”

[014-236] Caddy went on to say with considerable hesitation and reluctance that

[014-237] there was one thing more she wished us to know, and felt we ought to

[014-238] know, and which she hoped would not offend us. It was that she had

[014-239] improved her acquaintance with [[miss-flite|Miss Flite]], the little crazy old lady,

[014-240] and that she frequently went there early in the morning and met her

[014-241] lover for a few minutes before breakfast—only for a few minutes. “I

[014-242] go there at other times,” said Caddy, “but Prince does not come then.

[014-243] Young Mr. Turveydrop’s name is Prince; I wish it wasn’t, because it

[014-244] sounds like a dog, but of course he didn’t christen himself. Old Mr.

[014-245] Turveydrop had him christened Prince in remembrance of the Prince

[014-246] Regent. Old Mr. Turveydrop adored the Prince Regent on account of his

[014-247] deportment. I hope you won’t think the worse of me for having made

[014-248] these little appointments at Miss Flite’s, where I first went with

[014-253] you to go with me, Miss Summerson; but if you would,” said Caddy, who

[014-254] had said all this earnestly and tremblingly, “I should be very

[014-255] glad—very glad.”

[014-257] Flite’s that day. We had told him of our former visit, and our

[014-258] account had interested him; but something had always happened to

[014-259] prevent our going there again. As I trusted that I might have

[014-260] sufficient influence with Miss Jellyby to prevent her taking any very

[014-261] rash step if I fully accepted the confidence she was so willing to

[014-262] place in me, poor girl, I proposed that she and I and Peepy should go

[014-263] to the academy and afterwards meet my guardian and Ada at Miss

[014-264] Flite’s, whose name I now learnt for the first time. This was on

[014-307] detained her, and to receive my excuses, sir,” said I.

[014-308] “Oh, dear!” said he.

[014-309] “And pray,” I entreated, “do not allow me to be the cause of any more

[014-335] “Distinguished,” said Mr. Turveydrop, “by Miss Summerson’s presence.”

[014-340] admired.”

[014-343] At this command, or by this gracious permission, the lesson went on.

[014-344] Prince Turveydrop sometimes played the kit, dancing; sometimes played

[014-345] the piano, standing; sometimes hummed the tune with what little

[014-346] breath he could spare, while he set a pupil right; always

[014-347] conscientiously moved with the least proficient through every step

[014-348] and every part of the figure; and never rested for an instant. His

[014-352] censorious countenance. “Yet would you believe that it’s HIS name on

[014-355] “He wouldn’t let his son have any name if he could take it from him,”

[014-359] deport him! Transport him would be better!”

[014-361] give lessons in deportment now?”

[014-362] “Now!” returned the old lady shortly. “Never did.”
