# Richard Carstone

## Overview

Richard Carstone is the novel's primary victim of Chancery — not of the court's direct action but of the corrosive hope it inspires. Bright, charming, and constitutionally incapable of sustained effort, Richard drifts from profession to profession (medicine, law, the army) while waiting for the Jarndyce suit to make effort unnecessary. His decline, drawn across the novel's full span, is Dickens' most thorough case study in institutional damage.

## Role in the Novel

Richard arrives at Bleak House with Esther and Ada as a ward of Chancery. Unlike Esther, who builds a life outside the suit's shadow, and Ada, who loves Richard unconditionally, Richard cannot resist the suit's gravitational pull. "The uncertainties and delays of the Chancery suit had imparted to his character a certain irresolution" (017-023).

His relationship with Mr. Vholes — the vampiric solicitor who draws "sustenance out of the young man's very bones" (039) — is the novel's most explicit image of Chancery's parasitic economy. Richard's secret marriage to Ada, undertaken against Jarndyce's wishes, is an act of romantic autonomy that proves futile: he dies shortly after the suit's resolution, which consumes the entire estate in costs.

## Relationships

- **Ada Clare** — Wife and cousin; her love is the one constant in his decline
- **John Jarndyce** — Guardian; his warnings go unheeded
- **Esther Summerson** — Fellow ward; she watches his decline with grief
- **Mr. Vholes** — The solicitor who feeds on Richard's Chancery hopes

## Significance

Richard is the novel's argument that Chancery does not merely fail to deliver justice — it actively destroys those who hope in it. His tragedy is not that he is weak (though he is) but that the system is designed to exploit weakness. The "dead sea of the Chancery suit and all the ashy fruit it cast ashore" (037-688) is, finally, Richard himself.
