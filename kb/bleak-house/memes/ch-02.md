[002-001] It is but a glimpse of the world of fashion that we want on this same

[002-002] miry afternoon. It is not so unlike the [[chancery-court|Court of Chancery]] but that we

[002-003] may pass from the one scene to the other, as the crow flies. Both the

[002-004] world of fashion and the [[court-of-chancery-institution|Court of Chancery]] are things of precedent

[002-005] and usage: oversleeping Rip Van Winkles who have played at strange

[002-006] games through a deal of thundery weather; sleeping beauties whom the

[002-007] knight will wake one day, when all the stopped spits in the kitchen

[002-008] shall begin to turn prodigiously!

[002-009] It is not a large world. Relatively even to this world of ours, which

[002-010] has its limits too (as your Highness shall find when you have made

[002-011] the tour of it and are come to the brink of the void beyond), it is a

[002-012] very little speck. There is much good in it; there are many good and

[002-013] true people in it; it has its appointed place. But the evil of it is

[002-014] that it is a world wrapped up in too much jeweller’s cotton and fine

[002-015] wool, and cannot hear the rushing of the larger worlds, and cannot

[002-016] see them as they circle round the sun. It is a deadened world, and

[002-017] its growth is sometimes unhealthy for want of air.

[002-018] My [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]] has returned to her house in town for a few days

[002-019] previous to her departure for Paris, where her ladyship intends to

[002-020] stay some weeks, after which her movements are uncertain. The

[002-021] fashionable intelligence says so for the comfort of the Parisians,

[002-022] and it knows all fashionable things. To know things otherwise were to

[002-023] be unfashionable. My Lady Dedlock has been down at what she calls, in

[002-024] familiar conversation, her “place” in Lincolnshire. The waters are

[002-025] out in Lincolnshire. An arch of the bridge in the park has been

[002-026] sapped and sopped away. The adjacent low-lying ground for half a mile

[002-027] in breadth is a stagnant river with melancholy trees for islands in

[002-028] it and a surface punctured all over, all day long, with falling rain.

[002-029] My Lady Dedlock’s place has been extremely dreary. The weather for

[002-030] many a day and night has been so wet that the trees seem wet through,

[002-031] and the soft loppings and prunings of the woodman’s axe can make no

[002-032] crash or crackle as they fall. The deer, looking soaked, leave

[002-033] quagmires where they pass. The shot of a rifle loses its sharpness in

[002-034] the moist air, and its smoke moves in a tardy little cloud towards

[002-035] the green rise, coppice-topped, that makes a background for the

[002-036] falling rain. The view from my Lady Dedlock’s own windows is

[002-037] alternately a lead-coloured view and a view in Indian ink. The vases

[002-038] on the stone terrace in the foreground catch the rain all day; and

[002-039] the heavy drops fall—drip, drip, drip—upon the broad flagged

[002-040] pavement, called from old time the Ghost’s Walk, all night. On

[002-041] Sundays the little church in the park is mouldy; the oaken pulpit

[002-042] breaks out into a cold sweat; and there is a general smell and taste

[002-043] as of the ancient Dedlocks in their graves. My Lady Dedlock (who is

[002-044] childless), looking out in the early twilight from her boudoir at a

[002-045] keeper’s lodge and seeing the light of a fire upon the latticed

[002-046] panes, and smoke rising from the chimney, and a child, chased by a

[002-047] woman, running out into the rain to meet the shining figure of a

[002-048] wrapped-up man coming through the gate, has been put quite out of

[002-049] temper. My Lady Dedlock says she has been “bored to death.”

[002-050] Therefore my Lady Dedlock has come away from the place in

[002-051] Lincolnshire and has left it to the rain, and the crows, and the

[002-052] rabbits, and the deer, and the partridges and pheasants. The pictures

[002-053] of the Dedlocks past and gone have seemed to vanish into the damp

[002-054] walls in mere lowness of spirits, as the housekeeper has passed along

[002-055] the old rooms shutting up the shutters. And when they will next come

[002-056] forth again, the fashionable intelligence—which, like the fiend, is

[002-057] omniscient of the past and present, but not the future—cannot yet

[002-058] undertake to say.

[002-059] [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester Dedlock]] is only a baronet, but there is no mightier

[002-060] baronet than he. His family is as old as the hills, and infinitely

[002-061] more respectable. He has a general opinion that the world might get

[002-062] on without hills but would be done up without Dedlocks. He would on

[002-063] the whole admit nature to be a good idea (a little low, perhaps, when

[002-064] not enclosed with a park-fence), but an idea dependent for its

[002-065] execution on your great county families. He is a gentleman of strict

[002-066] conscience, disdainful of all littleness and meanness and ready on

[002-067] the shortest notice to die any death you may please to mention rather

[002-068] than give occasion for the least impeachment of his integrity. He is

[002-069] an honourable, obstinate, truthful, high-spirited, intensely

[002-070] prejudiced, perfectly unreasonable man.

[002-071] Sir Leicester is twenty years, full measure, older than my Lady. He

[002-072] will never see sixty-five again, nor perhaps sixty-six, nor yet

[002-073] sixty-seven. He has a twist of the gout now and then and walks a

[002-074] little stiffly. He is of a worthy presence, with his light-grey hair

[002-075] and whiskers, his fine shirt-frill, his pure-white waistcoat, and his

[002-076] blue coat with bright buttons always buttoned. He is ceremonious,

[002-077] stately, most polite on every occasion to my Lady, and holds her

[002-078] personal attractions in the highest estimation. His gallantry to my

[002-079] Lady, which has never changed since he courted her, is the one little

[002-080] touch of romantic fancy in him.

[002-081] Indeed, he married her for love. A whisper still goes about that she

[002-082] had not even family; howbeit, Sir Leicester had so much family that

[002-083] perhaps he had enough and could dispense with any more. But she had

[002-084] beauty, pride, ambition, insolent resolve, and sense enough to

[002-085] portion out a legion of fine ladies. Wealth and station, added to

[002-086] these, soon floated her upward, and for years now my Lady Dedlock has

[002-087] been at the centre of the fashionable intelligence and at the top of

[002-088] the fashionable tree.

[002-089] How Alexander wept when he had no more worlds to conquer, everybody

[002-090] knows—or has some reason to know by this time, the matter having

[002-091] been rather frequently mentioned. My Lady Dedlock, having conquered

[002-092] HER world, fell not into the melting, but rather into the freezing,

[002-093] mood. An exhausted composure, a worn-out placidity, an equanimity of

[002-094] fatigue not to be ruffled by interest or satisfaction, are the

[002-095] trophies of her victory. She is perfectly well-bred. If she could be

[002-096] translated to heaven to-morrow, she might be expected to ascend

[002-097] without any rapture.

[002-098] She has beauty still, and if it be not in its heyday, it is not yet

[002-099] in its autumn. She has a fine face—originally of a character that

[002-100] would be rather called very pretty than handsome, but improved into

[002-101] classicality by the acquired expression of her fashionable state. Her

[002-102] figure is elegant and has the effect of being tall. Not that she is

[002-103] so, but that “the most is made,” as the Honourable Bob Stables has

[002-104] frequently asserted upon oath, “of all her points.” The same

[002-105] authority observes that she is perfectly got up and remarks in

[002-106] commendation of her hair especially that she is the best-groomed

[002-107] woman in the whole stud.

[002-108] With all her perfections on her head, my Lady Dedlock has come up

[002-109] from her place in Lincolnshire (hotly pursued by the fashionable

[002-110] intelligence) to pass a few days at her house in town previous to her

[002-111] departure for Paris, where her ladyship intends to stay some weeks,

[002-112] after which her movements are uncertain. And at her house in town,

[002-113] upon this muddy, murky afternoon, presents himself an old-fashioned

[002-114] old gentleman, attorney-at-law and eke solicitor of the High Court of

[002-115] Chancery, who has the honour of acting as legal adviser of the

[002-116] Dedlocks and has as many cast-iron boxes in his office with that name

[002-117] outside as if the present baronet were the coin of the conjuror’s

[002-118] trick and were constantly being juggled through the whole set. Across

[002-119] the hall, and up the stairs, and along the passages, and through the

[002-120] rooms, which are very brilliant in the season and very dismal out of

[002-121] it—fairy-land to visit, but a desert to live in—the old gentleman

[002-122] is conducted by a Mercury in powder to my Lady’s presence.

[002-123] The old gentleman is rusty to look at, but is reputed to have made

[002-124] good thrift out of aristocratic marriage settlements and aristocratic

[002-125] wills, and to be very rich. He is surrounded by a mysterious halo of

[002-126] family confidences, of which he is known to be the silent depository.

[002-127] There are noble mausoleums rooted for centuries in retired glades of

[002-128] parks among the growing timber and the fern, which perhaps hold fewer

[002-129] noble secrets than walk abroad among men, shut up in the breast of

[002-130] [[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]]. He is of what is called the old school—a phrase

[002-131] generally meaning any school that seems never to have been young—and

[002-132] wears knee-breeches tied with ribbons, and gaiters or stockings. One

[002-133] peculiarity of his black clothes and of his black stockings, be they

[002-134] silk or worsted, is that they never shine. Mute, close, irresponsive

[002-135] to any glancing light, his dress is like himself. He never converses

[002-136] when not professionally consulted. He is found sometimes, speechless

[002-137] but quite at home, at corners of dinner-tables in great country

[002-138] houses and near doors of drawing-rooms, concerning which the

[002-139] fashionable intelligence is eloquent, where everybody knows him and

[002-140] where half the Peerage stops to say “How do you do, Mr. Tulkinghorn?”

[002-141] He receives these salutations with gravity and buries them along with

[002-142] the rest of his knowledge.

[002-143] Sir Leicester Dedlock is with my Lady and is happy to see Mr.

[002-144] Tulkinghorn. There is an air of prescription about him which is

[002-145] always agreeable to Sir Leicester; he receives it as a kind of

[002-146] tribute. He likes Mr. Tulkinghorn’s dress; there is a kind of tribute

[002-147] in that too. It is eminently respectable, and likewise, in a general

[002-148] way, retainer-like. It expresses, as it were, the steward of the

[002-149] legal mysteries, the butler of the legal cellar, of the Dedlocks.

[002-150] Has Mr. Tulkinghorn any idea of this himself? It may be so, or it may

[002-151] not, but there is this remarkable circumstance to be noted in

[002-152] everything associated with my Lady Dedlock as one of a class—as one

[002-153] of the leaders and representatives of her little world. She supposes

[002-154] herself to be an inscrutable Being, quite out of the reach and ken of

[002-155] ordinary mortals—seeing herself in her glass, where indeed she looks

[002-156] so. Yet every dim little star revolving about her, from her maid to

[002-157] the manager of the Italian Opera, knows her weaknesses, prejudices,

[002-158] follies, haughtinesses, and caprices and lives upon as accurate a

[002-159] calculation and as nice a measure of her moral nature as her

[002-160] dressmaker takes of her physical proportions. Is a new dress, a new

[002-161] custom, a new singer, a new dancer, a new form of jewellery, a new

[002-162] dwarf or giant, a new chapel, a new anything, to be set up? There are

[002-163] deferential people in a dozen callings whom my Lady Dedlock suspects

[002-164] of nothing but prostration before her, who can tell you how to manage

[002-165] her as if she were a baby, who do nothing but nurse her all their

[002-166] lives, who, humbly affecting to follow with profound subservience,

[002-167] lead her and her whole troop after them; who, in hooking one, hook

[002-168] all and bear them off as Lemuel Gulliver bore away the stately fleet

[002-169] of the majestic Lilliput. “If you want to address our people, sir,”

[002-170] say Blaze and Sparkle, the jewellers—meaning by our people Lady

[002-171] Dedlock and the rest—“you must remember that you are not dealing

[002-172] with the general public; you must hit our people in their weakest

[002-173] place, and their weakest place is such a place.” “To make this

[002-174] article go down, gentlemen,” say Sheen and Gloss, the mercers, to

[002-175] their friends the manufacturers, “you must come to us, because we

[002-176] know where to have the fashionable people, and we can make it

[002-177] fashionable.” “If you want to get this print upon the tables of my

[002-178] high connexion, sir,” says Mr. Sladdery, the librarian, “or if you

[002-179] want to get this dwarf or giant into the houses of my high connexion,

[002-180] sir, or if you want to secure to this entertainment the patronage of

[002-181] my high connexion, sir, you must leave it, if you please, to me, for

[002-182] I have been accustomed to study the leaders of my high connexion,

[002-183] sir, and I may tell you without vanity that I can turn them round my

[002-184] finger”—in which Mr. Sladdery, who is an honest man, does not

[002-185] exaggerate at all.

[002-186] Therefore, while Mr. Tulkinghorn may not know what is passing in the

[002-187] Dedlock mind at present, it is very possible that he may.

[002-188] “My Lady’s cause has been again before the Chancellor, has it, Mr.

[002-189] Tulkinghorn?” says Sir Leicester, giving him his hand.

[002-190] “Yes. It has been on again to-day,” Mr. Tulkinghorn replies, making

[002-191] one of his quiet bows to my Lady, who is on a sofa near the fire,

[002-192] shading her face with a hand-screen.

[002-193] “It would be useless to ask,” says my Lady with the dreariness of the

[002-194] place in Lincolnshire still upon her, “whether anything has been

[002-195] done.”

[002-196] “Nothing that YOU would call anything has been done to-day,” replies

[002-197] Mr. Tulkinghorn.

[002-198] “Nor ever will be,” says my Lady.

[002-199] Sir Leicester has no objection to an interminable Chancery suit. It

[002-200] is a slow, expensive, British, constitutional kind of thing. To be

[002-201] sure, he has not a vital interest in the suit in question, her part

[002-202] in which was the only [[inheritance-and-property|property]] my Lady brought him; and he has a

[002-203] shadowy impression that for his name—the name of Dedlock—to be in a

[002-204] cause, and not in the title of that cause, is a most ridiculous

[002-205] accident. But he regards the Court of Chancery, even if it should

[002-206] involve an occasional delay of justice and a trifling amount of

[002-207] confusion, as a something devised in conjunction with a variety of

[002-208] other somethings by the perfection of human wisdom for the eternal

[002-209] settlement (humanly speaking) of everything. And he is upon the whole

[002-210] of a fixed opinion that to give the sanction of his countenance to

[002-211] any complaints respecting it would be to encourage some person in the

[002-212] lower classes to rise up somewhere—like Wat Tyler.

[002-213] “As a few fresh affidavits have been put upon the file,” says Mr.

[002-214] Tulkinghorn, “and as they are short, and as I proceed upon the

[002-215] troublesome principle of begging leave to possess my clients with any

[002-216] new proceedings in a cause”—cautious man Mr. Tulkinghorn, taking no

[002-217] more responsibility than necessary—“and further, as I see you are

[002-218] going to Paris, I have brought them in my pocket.”

[002-219] (Sir Leicester was going to Paris too, by the by, but the delight of

[002-220] the fashionable intelligence was in his Lady.)

[002-221] Mr. Tulkinghorn takes out his papers, asks permission to place them

[002-222] on a golden talisman of a table at my Lady’s elbow, puts on his

[002-223] spectacles, and begins to read by the light of a shaded lamp.

[002-224] “‘In Chancery. Between [[john-jarndyce|John Jarndyce]]—’”

[002-225] My Lady interrupts, requesting him to miss as many of the formal

[002-226] horrors as he can.

[002-227] Mr. Tulkinghorn glances over his spectacles and begins again lower

[002-228] down. My Lady carelessly and scornfully abstracts her attention. Sir

[002-229] Leicester in a great chair looks at the file and appears to have a

[002-230] stately liking for the legal repetitions and prolixities as ranging

[002-231] among the national bulwarks. It happens that the fire is hot where my

[002-232] Lady sits and that the hand-screen is more beautiful than useful,

[002-233] being priceless but small. My Lady, changing her position, sees the

[002-234] papers on the table—looks at them nearer—looks at them nearer

[002-235] still—asks impulsively, “Who copied that?”

[002-236] Mr. Tulkinghorn stops short, surprised by my Lady’s animation and her

[002-237] unusual tone.

[002-238] “Is it what you people call law-hand?” she asks, looking full at him

[002-239] in her careless way again and toying with her screen.

[002-240] “Not quite. Probably”—Mr. Tulkinghorn examines it as he speaks—“the

[002-241] legal character which it has was acquired after the original hand was

[002-242] formed. Why do you ask?”

[002-243] “Anything to vary this detestable monotony. Oh, go on, do!”

[002-244] Mr. Tulkinghorn reads again. The heat is greater; my Lady screens her

[002-245] face. Sir Leicester dozes, starts up suddenly, and cries, “Eh? What

[002-246] do you say?”

[002-247] “I say I am afraid,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, who had risen hastily,

[002-248] “that Lady Dedlock is ill.”

[002-249] “Faint,” my Lady murmurs with white lips, “only that; but it is like

[002-250] the faintness of death. Don’t speak to me. Ring, and take me to my

[002-251] room!”

[002-252] Mr. Tulkinghorn retires into another chamber; bells ring, feet

[002-253] shuffle and patter, silence ensues. Mercury at last begs Mr.

[002-254] Tulkinghorn to return.

[002-255] “Better now,” quoth Sir Leicester, motioning the lawyer to sit down

[002-256] and read to him alone. “I have been quite alarmed. I never knew my

[002-257] Lady swoon before. But the weather is extremely trying, and she

[002-258] really has been bored to death down at our place in Lincolnshire.”
