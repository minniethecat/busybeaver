[037-001] If the secret I had to keep had been mine, I must have confided it to

[037-002] [[ada-clare|Ada]] before we had been long together. But it was not mine, and I did

[037-003] not feel that I had a right to tell it, even to my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]], unless

[037-004] some great emergency arose. It was a weight to bear alone; still my

[037-005] present duty appeared to be plain, and blest in the attachment of my

[037-006] dear, I did not want an impulse and encouragement to do it. Though

[037-007] often when she was asleep and all was quiet, the remembrance of my

[037-008] mother kept me waking and made the night sorrowful, I did not yield

[037-009] to it at another time; and Ada found me what I used to be—except, of

[037-010] course, in that particular of which I have said enough and which I

[037-011] have no intention of mentioning any more just now, if I can help it.

[037-012] The difficulty that I felt in being quite composed that first evening

[037-013] when Ada asked me, over our work, if the family were at the house,

[037-014] and when I was obliged to answer yes, I believed so, for [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]]

[037-015] had spoken to me in the woods the day before yesterday, was great.

[037-016] Greater still when Ada asked me what she had said, and when I replied

[037-017] that she had been kind and interested, and when Ada, while admitting

[037-018] her beauty and elegance, remarked upon her proud manner and her

[037-019] imperious chilling air. But Charley helped me through, unconsciously,

[037-020] by telling us that Lady Dedlock had only stayed at the house two

[037-021] nights on her way from [[london|London]] to visit at some other great house in

[037-022] the next county and that she had left early on the morning after we

[037-023] had seen her at our view, as we called it. Charley verified the adage

[037-024] about little pitchers, I am sure, for she heard of more sayings and

[037-025] doings in a day than would have come to my ears in a month.

[037-026] We were to stay a month at Mr. Boythorn’s. My pet had scarcely been

[037-027] there a bright week, as I recollect the time, when one evening after

[037-028] we had finished helping the gardener in watering his flowers, and

[037-029] just as the candles were lighted, Charley, appearing with a very

[037-030] important air behind Ada’s chair, beckoned me mysteriously out of the

[037-031] room.

[037-032] “Oh! If you please, miss,” said Charley in a whisper, with her eyes

[037-033] at their roundest and largest. “You’re wanted at the Dedlock Arms.”

[037-034] “Why, Charley,” said I, “who can possibly want me at the

[037-035] public-house?”

[037-036] “I don’t know, miss,” returned Charley, putting her head forward and

[037-037] folding her hands tight upon the band of her little apron, which she

[037-038] always did in the enjoyment of anything mysterious or confidential,

[037-039] “but it’s a gentleman, miss, and his compliments, and will you please

[037-040] to come without saying anything about it.”

[037-041] “Whose compliments, Charley?”

[037-042] “His’n, miss,” returned Charley, whose grammatical education was

[037-043] advancing, but not very rapidly.

[037-044] “And how do you come to be the messenger, Charley?”

[037-045] “I am not the messenger, if you please, miss,” returned my little

[037-046] maid. “It was W. Grubble, miss.”

[037-047] “And who is W. Grubble, Charley?”

[037-048] “Mister Grubble, miss,” returned Charley. “Don’t you know, miss? The

[037-049] Dedlock Arms, by W. Grubble,” which Charley delivered as if she were

[037-050] slowly spelling out the sign.

[037-051] “Aye? The landlord, Charley?”

[037-052] “Yes, miss. If you please, miss, his wife is a beautiful woman, but

[037-053] she broke her ankle, and it never joined. And her brother’s the

[037-054] sawyer that was put in the cage, miss, and they expect he’ll drink

[037-055] himself to death entirely on beer,” said Charley.

[037-056] Not knowing what might be the matter, and being easily apprehensive

[037-057] now, I thought it best to go to this place by myself. I bade Charley

[037-058] be quick with my bonnet and veil and my shawl, and having put them

[037-059] on, went away down the little hilly street, where I was as much at

[037-060] home as in Mr. Boythorn’s garden.

[037-064] iron vessel (it looked as heavy), preceded me along the sanded

[037-069] don’t know which, and I doubt if many people did) hanging from his

[037-072] who never seemed to consider himself cozily dressed for his own

[037-073] fire-side without his hat and top-boots, but who never wore a coat

[037-074] except at church.

[037-088] “Always the same dear girl!” said Richard just as heartily as before.

[037-115] as well as anything else, for a time. I don’t know that I shall care

[037-122] “Indeed?”

[037-123] “Yes. I have run over to look after my—my [[chancery-court|Chancery]] interests before

[037-124] the long vacation,” said Richard, forcing a careless laugh. “We are

[037-125] beginning to spin along with that old suit at last, I promise you.”

[037-126] No wonder that I shook my head!

[037-127] “As you say, it’s not a pleasant subject.” Richard spoke with the

[037-128] same shade crossing his face as before. “Let it go to the four winds

[037-129] for to-night. Puff! Gone! Who do you suppose is with me?”

[037-130] “Was it [[harold-skimpole|Mr. Skimpole]]’s voice I heard?”

[037-131] “That’s the man! He does me more good than anybody. What a

[037-132] fascinating child it is!”

[037-134] answered, no, nobody. He had been to call upon the dear old

[037-135] infant—so he called Mr. Skimpole—and the dear old infant had told

[037-136] him where we were, and he had told the dear old infant he was bent on

[037-137] coming to see us, and the dear old infant had directly wanted to come

[037-139] sordid expenses—but thrice his weight in gold,” said Richard. “He is

[037-140] such a cheery fellow. No worldliness about him. Fresh and

[037-141] green-hearted!”

[037-181] other part, and with less trouble.

[037-182] They both walked back with me, and Mr. Skimpole leaving us at the

[037-186] knew it. It was a very transparent business, that meeting as cousins

[037-196] Ah me! What Richard would have been without that [[ruin-and-decay|blight]], I never
