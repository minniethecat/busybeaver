[033-001] Now do those two gentlemen not very neat about the cuffs and buttons

[033-002] who attended the last coroner’s inquest at the Sol’s Arms reappear in

[033-003] the precincts with surprising swiftness (being, in fact, breathlessly

[033-004] fetched by the active and intelligent beadle), and institute

[033-005] perquisitions through the court, and dive into the Sol’s parlour, and

[033-006] write with ravenous little pens on tissue-paper. Now do they note

[033-007] down, in the watches of the night, how the neighbourhood of [[chancery-court|Chancery]]

[033-008] Lane was yesterday, at about midnight, thrown into a state of the

[033-009] most intense agitation and excitement by the following alarming and

[033-010] horrible discovery. Now do they set forth how it will doubtless be

[033-011] remembered that some time back a painful sensation was created in the

[033-012] public mind by a case of mysterious death from opium occurring in the

[033-013] first floor of the house occupied as a rag, bottle, and general

[033-014] marine store shop, by an eccentric individual of intemperate habits,

[033-015] far advanced in life, named [[krook|Krook]]; and how, by a remarkable

[033-016] coincidence, Krook was examined at the inquest, which it may be

[033-017] recollected was held on that occasion at the Sol’s Arms, a

[033-018] well-conducted tavern immediately adjoining the premises in question

[033-019] on the west side and licensed to a highly respectable landlord, Mr.

[033-020] James [[george-rouncewell|George]] Bogsby. Now do they show (in as many words as possible)

[033-021] how during some hours of yesterday evening a very peculiar smell was

[033-022] observed by the inhabitants of the court, in which the tragical

[033-023] occurrence which forms the subject of that present account

[033-024] transpired; and which odour was at one time so powerful that Mr.

[033-025] Swills, a comic vocalist professionally engaged by Mr. J. G. Bogsby,

[033-026] has himself stated to our reporter that he mentioned to Miss M.

[033-027] Melvilleson, a lady of some pretensions to musical ability, likewise

[033-028] engaged by Mr. J. G. Bogsby to sing at a series of concerts called

[033-029] Harmonic Assemblies, or Meetings, which it would appear are held at

[033-030] the Sol’s Arms under Mr. Bogsby’s direction pursuant to the Act of

[033-031] George the Second, that he (Mr. Swills) found his voice seriously

[033-032] affected by the impure state of the atmosphere, his jocose expression

[033-033] at the time being that he was like an empty post-office, for he

[033-034] hadn’t a single note in him. How this account of Mr. Swills is

[033-035] entirely corroborated by two intelligent married females residing in

[033-036] the same court and known respectively by the names of Mrs. Piper and

[033-037] Mrs. Perkins, both of whom observed the foetid effluvia and regarded

[033-038] them as being emitted from the premises in the occupation of Krook,

[033-039] the unfortunate deceased. All this and a great deal more the two

[033-040] gentlemen who have formed an amicable partnership in the melancholy

[033-041] catastrophe write down on the spot; and the boy population of the

[033-042] court (out of bed in a moment) swarm up the shutters of the Sol’s

[033-043] Arms parlour, to behold the tops of their heads while they are about

[033-044] it.

[033-045] The whole court, adult as well as boy, is sleepless for that night,

[033-046] and can do nothing but wrap up its many heads, and talk of the

[033-047] ill-fated house, and look at it. [[miss-flite|Miss Flite]] has been bravely rescued

[033-048] from her chamber, as if it were in flames, and accommodated with a

[033-049] bed at the Sol’s Arms. The Sol neither turns off its gas nor shuts

[033-050] its door all night, for any kind of public excitement makes good for

[033-051] the Sol and causes the court to stand in need of comfort. The house

[033-052] has not done so much in the stomachic article of cloves or in

[033-053] brandy-and-water warm since the inquest. The moment the pot-boy heard

[033-054] what had happened, he rolled up his shirt-sleeves tight to his

[033-055] shoulders and said, “There’ll be a run upon us!” In the first outcry,

[033-056] young Piper dashed off for the fire-engines and returned in triumph

[033-057] at a jolting gallop perched up aloft on the Phoenix and holding on to

[033-058] that fabulous creature with all his might in the midst of helmets and

[033-059] torches. One helmet remains behind after careful investigation of all

[033-060] chinks and crannies and slowly paces up and down before the house in

[033-061] company with one of the two policemen who have likewise been left in

[033-062] charge thereof. To this trio everybody in the court possessed of

[033-063] sixpence has an insatiate desire to exhibit hospitality in a liquid

[033-064] form.

[033-065] Mr. Weevle and his friend Mr. Guppy are within the bar at the Sol and

[033-066] are worth anything to the Sol that the bar contains if they will only

[033-067] stay there. “This is not a time,” says Mr. Bogsby, “to haggle about

[033-068] money,” though he looks something sharply after it, over the counter;

[033-069] “give your orders, you two gentlemen, and you’re welcome to whatever

[033-070] you put a name to.”

[033-071] Thus entreated, the two gentlemen (Mr. Weevle especially) put names

[033-072] to so many things that in course of time they find it difficult to

[033-073] put a name to anything quite distinctly, though they still relate to

[033-074] all new-comers some version of the night they have had of it, and of

[033-075] what they said, and what they thought, and what they saw. Meanwhile,

[033-076] one or other of the policemen often flits about the door, and pushing

[033-077] it open a little way at the full length of his arm, looks in from

[033-078] outer gloom. Not that he has any suspicions, but that he may as well

[033-079] know what they are up to in there.

[033-080] Thus night pursues its leaden course, finding the court still out of

[033-081] bed through the unwonted hours, still treating and being treated,

[033-082] still conducting itself similarly to a court that has had a little

[033-083] money left it unexpectedly. Thus night at length with slow-retreating

[033-084] steps departs, and the lamp-lighter going his rounds, like an

[033-085] executioner to a despotic king, strikes off the little heads of fire

[033-086] that have aspired to lessen the darkness. Thus the day cometh,

[033-087] whether or no.

[033-088] And the day may discern, even with its dim [[london|London]] eye, that the court

[033-089] has been up all night. Over and above the faces that have fallen

[033-090] drowsily on tables and the heels that lie prone on hard floors

[033-091] instead of beds, the brick and mortar physiognomy of the very court

[033-092] itself looks worn and jaded. And now the neighbourhood, waking up and

[033-093] beginning to hear of what has happened, comes streaming in, half

[033-094] dressed, to ask questions; and the two policemen and the helmet (who

[033-095] are far less impressible externally than the court) have enough to do

[033-096] to keep the door.

[033-097] “Good gracious, gentlemen!” says Mr. Snagsby, coming up. “What’s this

[033-098] I hear!”

[033-099] “Why, it’s true,” returns one of the policemen. “That’s what it is.

[033-100] Now move on here, come!”

[033-101] “Why, good gracious, gentlemen,” says Mr. Snagsby, somewhat promptly

[033-102] backed away, “I was at this door last night betwixt ten and eleven

[033-103] o’clock in conversation with the young man who lodges here.”

[033-104] “Indeed?” returns the policeman. “You will find the young man next

[033-105] door then. Now move on here, some of you.”

[033-106] “Not hurt, I hope?” says Mr. Snagsby.

[033-107] “Hurt? No. What’s to hurt him!”

[033-108] Mr. Snagsby, wholly unable to answer this or any question in his

[033-109] troubled mind, repairs to the Sol’s Arms and finds Mr. Weevle

[033-110] languishing over tea and toast with a considerable expression on him

[033-111] of exhausted excitement and exhausted tobacco-smoke.

[033-112] “And Mr. Guppy likewise!” quoth Mr. Snagsby. “Dear, dear, dear! What

[033-113] a fate there seems in all this! And my lit—”

[033-114] Mr. Snagsby’s power of speech deserts him in the formation of the

[033-115] words “my little woman.” For to see that injured female walk into the

[033-116] Sol’s Arms at that hour of the morning and stand before the

[033-117] beer-engine, with her eyes fixed upon him like an accusing spirit,

[033-118] strikes him dumb.

[033-119] “My dear,” says Mr. Snagsby when his tongue is loosened, “will you

[033-120] take anything? A little—not to put too fine a point upon it—drop of

[033-121] shrub?”

[033-122] “No,” says Mrs. Snagsby.

[033-123] “My love, you know these two gentlemen?”

[033-124] “Yes!” says Mrs. Snagsby, and in a rigid manner acknowledges their

[033-125] presence, still fixing Mr. Snagsby with her eye.

[033-126] The devoted Mr. Snagsby cannot bear this treatment. He takes Mrs.

[033-127] Snagsby by the hand and leads her aside to an adjacent cask.

[033-128] “My little woman, why do you look at me in that way? Pray don’t do

[033-129] it.”

[033-130] “I can’t help my looks,” says Mrs. Snagsby, “and if I could I

[033-131] wouldn’t.”

[033-132] Mr. Snagsby, with his cough of meekness, rejoins, “Wouldn’t you

[033-133] really, my dear?” and meditates. Then coughs his cough of trouble and

[033-134] says, “This is a dreadful mystery, my love!” still fearfully

[033-135] disconcerted by Mrs. Snagsby’s eye.

[033-136] “It IS,” returns Mrs. Snagsby, shaking her head, “a dreadful

[033-137] mystery.”

[033-138] “My little woman,” urges Mr. Snagsby in a piteous manner, “don’t for

[033-139] goodness’ sake speak to me with that bitter expression and look at me

[033-140] in that searching way! I beg and entreat of you not to do it. Good

[033-141] Lord, you don’t suppose that I would go spontaneously combusting any

[033-142] person, my dear?”

[033-143] “I can’t say,” returns Mrs. Snagsby.

[033-144] On a hasty review of his unfortunate position, Mr. Snagsby “can’t

[033-145] say” either. He is not prepared positively to deny that he may have

[033-146] had something to do with it. He has had something—he don’t know

[033-147] what—to do with so much in this connexion that is mysterious that it

[033-148] is possible he may even be implicated, without knowing it, in the

[033-149] present transaction. He faintly wipes his forehead with his

[033-150] handkerchief and gasps.

[033-151] “My life,” says the unhappy stationer, “would you have any objections

[033-152] to mention why, being in general so delicately circumspect in your

[033-153] conduct, you come into a wine-vaults before breakfast?”

[033-154] “Why do YOU come here?” inquires Mrs. Snagsby.

[033-155] “My dear, merely to know the rights of the fatal accident which has

[033-156] happened to the venerable party who has been—combusted.” Mr. Snagsby

[033-157] has made a pause to suppress a groan. “I should then have related

[033-158] them to you, my love, over your French roll.”

[033-159] “I dare say you would! You relate everything to me, Mr. Snagsby.”

[033-160] “Every—my lit—”

[033-161] “I should be glad,” says Mrs. Snagsby after contemplating his

[033-162] increased confusion with a severe and sinister smile, “if you would

[033-163] come home with me; I think you may be safer there, Mr. Snagsby, than

[033-164] anywhere else.”

[033-165] “My love, I don’t know but what I may be, I am sure. I am ready to

[033-166] go.”

[033-167] Mr. Snagsby casts his eye forlornly round the bar, gives Messrs.

[033-168] Weevle and Guppy good morning, assures them of the satisfaction with

[033-169] which he sees them uninjured, and accompanies Mrs. Snagsby from the

[033-170] Sol’s Arms. Before night his doubt whether he may not be responsible

[033-171] for some inconceivable part in the catastrophe which is the talk of

[033-172] the whole neighbourhood is almost resolved into certainty by Mrs.

[033-173] Snagsby’s pertinacity in that fixed gaze. His mental sufferings are

[033-174] so great that he entertains wandering ideas of delivering himself up

[033-175] to justice and requiring to be cleared if innocent and punished with

[033-176] the utmost rigour of the law if guilty.

[033-177] Mr. Weevle and Mr. Guppy, having taken their breakfast, step into

[033-178] Lincoln’s Inn to take a little walk about the square and clear as

[033-179] many of the dark cobwebs out of their brains as a little walk may.

[033-180] “There can be no more favourable time than the present, Tony,” says

[033-181] Mr. Guppy after they have broodingly made out the four sides of the

[033-182] square, “for a word or two between us upon a point on which we must,

[033-183] with very little delay, come to an understanding.”

[033-184] “Now, I tell you what, William G.!” returns the other, eyeing his

[033-185] companion with a bloodshot eye. “If it’s a point of conspiracy, you

[033-186] needn’t take the trouble to mention it. I have had enough of that,

[033-187] and I ain’t going to have any more. We shall have YOU taking fire

[033-188] next or blowing up with a bang.”

[033-189] This supposititious phenomenon is so very disagreeable to Mr. Guppy

[033-190] that his voice quakes as he says in a moral way, “Tony, I should have

[033-191] thought that what we went through last night would have been a lesson

[033-192] to you never to be personal any more as long as you lived.” To which

[033-193] Mr. Weevle returns, “William, I should have thought it would have

[033-194] been a lesson to YOU never to conspire any more as long as you

[033-195] lived.” To which Mr. Guppy says, “Who’s conspiring?” To which Mr.

[033-196] Jobling replies, “Why, YOU are!” To which Mr. Guppy retorts, “No, I

[033-197] am not.” To which Mr. Jobling retorts again, “Yes, you are!” To which

[033-198] Mr. Guppy retorts, “Who says so?” To which Mr. Jobling retorts, “I

[033-199] say so!” To which Mr. Guppy retorts, “Oh, indeed?” To which Mr.

[033-200] Jobling retorts, “Yes, indeed!” And both being now in a heated state,

[033-201] they walk on silently for a while to cool down again.

[033-202] “Tony,” says Mr. Guppy then, “if you heard your friend out instead of

[033-203] flying at him, you wouldn’t fall into mistakes. But your temper is

[033-204] hasty and you are not considerate. Possessing in yourself, Tony, all

[033-205] that is calculated to charm the eye—”

[033-206] “Oh! Blow the eye!” cries Mr. Weevle, cutting him short. “Say what

[033-207] you have got to say!”

[033-208] Finding his friend in this morose and material condition, Mr. Guppy

[033-209] only expresses the finer feelings of his soul through the tone of

[033-210] injury in which he recommences, “Tony, when I say there is a point on

[033-211] which we must come to an understanding pretty soon, I say so quite

[033-212] apart from any kind of conspiring, however innocent. You know it is

[033-213] professionally arranged beforehand in all cases that are tried what

[033-214] facts the witnesses are to prove. Is it or is it not desirable that

[033-215] we should know what facts we are to prove on the inquiry into the

[033-216] death of this unfortunate old mo—gentleman?” (Mr. Guppy was going to

[033-217] say “mogul,” but thinks “gentleman” better suited to the

[033-218] circumstances.)

[033-219] “What facts? THE facts.”

[033-220] “The facts bearing on that inquiry. Those are”—Mr. Guppy tells them

[033-221] off on his fingers—“what we knew of his habits, when you saw him

[033-222] last, what his condition was then, the discovery that we made, and

[033-223] how we made it.”

[033-224] “Yes,” says Mr. Weevle. “Those are about the facts.”

[033-225] “We made the discovery in consequence of his having, in his eccentric

[033-226] way, an appointment with you at twelve o’clock at night, when you

[033-227] were to explain some [[document-and-letter|writing]] to him as you had often done before on

[033-228] account of his not being able to read. I, spending the evening with

[033-229] you, was called down—and so forth. The inquiry being only into the

[033-232] “No!” returns Mr. Weevle. “I suppose not.”

[033-233] “And this is not a conspiracy, perhaps?” says the injured Guppy.

[033-234] “No,” returns his friend; “if it’s nothing worse than this, I

[033-235] withdraw the observation.”

[033-236] “Now, Tony,” says Mr. Guppy, taking his arm again and walking him

[033-237] slowly on, “I should like to know, in a friendly way, whether you

[033-238] have yet thought over the many advantages of your continuing to live

[033-239] at that place?”

[033-240] “What do you mean?” says Tony, stopping.

[033-241] “Whether you have yet thought over the many advantages of your

[033-242] continuing to live at that place?” repeats Mr. Guppy, walking him on

[033-243] again.

[033-244] “At what place? THAT place?” pointing in the direction of the rag and

[033-245] bottle shop.

[033-246] Mr. Guppy nods.

[033-247] “Why, I wouldn’t pass another night there for any consideration that

[033-248] you could offer me,” says Mr. Weevle, haggardly staring.

[033-249] “Do you mean it though, Tony?”

[033-250] “Mean it! Do I look as if I mean it? I feel as if I do; I know that,”

[033-251] says Mr. Weevle with a very genuine shudder.

[033-252] “Then the possibility or probability—for such it must be

[033-253] considered—of your never being disturbed in possession of those

[033-254] effects lately belonging to a lone old man who seemed to have no

[033-255] relation in the world, and the certainty of your being able to find

[033-259] “Certainly not. Talk in that cool way of a fellow’s living there?”

[033-260] cries Mr. Weevle indignantly. “Go and live there yourself.”

[033-261] “Oh! I, Tony!” says Mr. Guppy, soothing him. “I have never lived

[033-264] “You are welcome to it,” rejoins his friend, “and—ugh!—you may make

[033-266] “Then you really and truly at this point,” says Mr. Guppy, “give up

[033-268] “You never,” returns Tony with a most convincing steadfastness, “said

[033-270] While they are so conversing, a hackney-coach drives into the square,

[033-271] on the box of which vehicle a very tall hat makes itself manifest to

[033-272] the public. Inside the coach, and consequently not so manifest to the

[033-273] multitude, though sufficiently so to the two friends, for the coach

[033-274] stops almost at their feet, are the venerable Mr. Smallweed and Mrs.

[033-275] Smallweed, accompanied by their granddaughter Judy.

[033-276] An air of haste and excitement pervades the party, and as the tall

[033-277] hat (surmounting Mr. Smallweed the younger) alights, Mr. Smallweed

[033-278] the elder pokes his head out of window and bawls to Mr. Guppy, “How

[033-279] de do, sir! How de do!”

[033-280] “What do Chick and his family want here at this time of the morning,

[033-281] I wonder!” says Mr. Guppy, nodding to his familiar.

[033-282] “My dear sir,” cries Grandfather Smallweed, “would you do me a

[033-283] favour? Would you and your friend be so very obleeging as to carry me

[033-284] into the public-house in the court, while Bart and his sister bring

[033-285] their grandmother along? Would you do an old man that good turn,

[033-286] sir?”

[033-287] Mr. Guppy looks at his friend, repeating inquiringly, “The

[033-288] public-house in the court?” And they prepare to bear the venerable

[033-289] burden to the Sol’s Arms.

[033-290] “There’s your fare!” says the patriarch to the coachman with a fierce

[033-298] no worse aggravation of his symptoms, however, than the utterance of

[033-305] poll-parrot! Sit down!”

[033-306] This little apostrophe to Mrs. Smallweed is occasioned by a

[033-307] propensity on the part of that unlucky old lady whenever she finds

[033-309] accompanying herself with a chattering noise, as in a witch dance. A

[033-310] nervous affection has probably as much to do with these

[033-311] demonstrations as any imbecile intention in the poor old woman, but

[033-312] on the present occasion they are so particularly lively in connexion

[033-313] with the Windsor arm-chair, fellow to that in which Mr. Smallweed is

[033-314] seated, that she only quite desists when her grandchildren have held

[033-315] her down in it, her lord in the meanwhile bestowing upon her, with

[033-316] great volubility, the endearing epithet of “a pig-headed jackdaw,”

[033-317] repeated a surprising number of times.

[033-318] “My dear sir,” Grandfather Smallweed then proceeds, addressing Mr.

[033-319] Guppy, “there has been a calamity here. Have you heard of it, either

[033-320] of you?”

[033-321] “Heard of it, sir! Why, we discovered it.”

[033-322] “You discovered it. You two discovered it! Bart, THEY discovered it!”

[033-323] The two discoverers stare at the Smallweeds, who return the

[033-324] compliment.

[033-325] “My dear friends,” whines Grandfather Smallweed, putting out both his

[033-326] hands, “I owe you a thousand thanks for discharging the melancholy

[033-327] office of discovering the ashes of Mrs. Smallweed’s brother.”

[033-328] “Eh?” says Mr. Guppy.

[033-332] eccentric. Unless he has left a will (which is not at all likely) I

[033-334] after the [[inheritance-and-property|property]]; it must be sealed up, it must be protected. I

[033-335] have come down,” repeats Grandfather Smallweed, hooking the air

[033-336] towards him with all his ten fingers at once, “to look after the

[033-337] property.”

[033-338] “I think, Small,” says the disconsolate Mr. Guppy, “you might have

[033-339] mentioned that the old man was your uncle.”

[033-340] “You two were so close about him that I thought you would like me to

[033-341] be the same,” returns that old bird with a secretly glistening eye.

[033-342] “Besides, I wasn’t proud of him.”

[033-343] “Besides which, it was nothing to you, you know, whether he was or

[033-344] not,” says Judy. Also with a secretly glistening eye.

[033-345] “He never saw me in his life to know me,” observed Small; “I don’t

[033-353] Krook was Mrs. Smallweed’s only brother; she had no relation but

[033-354] Krook, and Krook had no relation but Mrs. Smallweed. I am speaking of

[033-355] your brother, you brimstone black-beetle, that was seventy-six years

[033-357] Mrs. Smallweed instantly begins to shake her head and pipe up,

[033-358] “Seventy-six pound seven and sevenpence! Seventy-six thousand bags of

[033-365] you dog, you brimstone barker!” Here Mr. Smallweed, wrought up to the

[033-368] at the old lady with such force as he can muster and then dropping
