# Spontaneous Combustion

## Overview

Krook's death by spontaneous combustion in Chapter 32 is the novel's most extreme image — a rag-and-bone dealer who hoards legal documents literally consumed by his own accumulated corruption. Dickens defended the scientific possibility of the phenomenon in the novel's preface, engaging in a public controversy with the physiologist George Henry Lewes. The episode crystallizes the novel's insistence that moral and physical decay are continuous: what Chancery does slowly to its suitors, spontaneous combustion does instantly to Krook.

## In the Novel

Krook, the "Lord Chancellor" of his rag-and-bone shop (a parodic double of the real Lord Chancellor in Chapter 1), is found reduced to "a smouldering suffocating vapour" and "a dark greasy coating on the walls and ceiling" (032). His death is discovered by Guppy and Jobling, and the scene is narrated with grotesque precision — the cat, Lady Jane, hissing at the remains; the "thick yellow liquor" dripping from the windowsill; the "small burnt patch of flooring."

The episode is rich in symbolic resonance. Krook's shop, full of "waste paper" from Chancery, is a miniature of the Court itself: both are sites where documents accumulate without resolution. Krook's illiteracy — he cannot read the legal papers he hoards — parallels Chancery's functional illiteracy: the court processes documents it cannot properly comprehend.

## Dickens' Treatment

Dickens insisted on the literal possibility of spontaneous combustion, citing historical cases in the preface. But the episode's power is metaphorical. Krook combusts because the novel's logic demands it: accumulated corruption, denied any outlet, must eventually ignite. It is the narrative equivalent of Miss Flite's caged birds awaiting "Judgement" — a violent release that reveals the system's internal pressure.
