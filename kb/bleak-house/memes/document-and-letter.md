# Documents and Letters

## Overview

*Bleak House* is a novel saturated with writing. Legal writing — "tens of thousands of Chancery folio-pages" (001-157) — consumes lives without producing justice. Personal writing — Lady Dedlock's letters, Captain Hawdon's handwriting, Esther's narrative — reveals what the legal system conceals. The novel's dual narration is itself a formal argument that the act of writing can be either an instrument of institutional power or a vehicle for personal truth, depending on who holds the pen.

## In the Novel

Legal documents are the novel's anti-life principle. The Jarndyce affidavit consumes generations of lawyers who copy it without understanding it. Krook hoards legal waste paper that he cannot read — his illiteracy is a grotesque parody of Chancery's functional illiteracy. The Court processes documents endlessly without ever reaching meaning.

Against this, personal documents carry dangerous truth. Lady Dedlock's recognition of Captain Hawdon's handwriting on a legal document is the catalyst for the entire detective plot. Esther's narrative — the document we are reading — is an act of self-construction against the official narratives that would define her (as bastard, as disgrace, as ward). The novel stages a war between two kinds of writing: the institutional writing that buries truth, and the personal writing that excavates it.

## Dickens' Treatment

The novel's form embodies its argument about documents. Esther's first-person chapters are "written" by her; the omniscient chapters are "written" by Dickens' institutional satirist. The alternation between these two kinds of writing is the novel's deepest structural argument: personal testimony and institutional critique need each other. Neither alone is adequate to tell the story of *Bleak House*.
