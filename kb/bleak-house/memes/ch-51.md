[051-001] When [[allan-woodcourt|Mr. Woodcourt]] arrived in [[london|London]], he went, that very same day, to

[051-002] [[mr-vholes|Mr. Vholes]]’s in Symond’s Inn. For he never once, from the moment when

[051-003] I entreated him to be a friend to [[richard-carstone|Richard]], neglected or forgot his

[051-004] promise. He had told me that he accepted the charge as a sacred

[051-005] trust, and he was ever true to it in that spirit.

[051-006] He found Mr. Vholes in his office and informed Mr. Vholes of his

[051-007] agreement with Richard that he should call there to learn his

[051-008] address.

[051-009] “Just so, sir,” said Mr. Vholes. “Mr. C.’s address is not a hundred

[051-010] miles from here, sir, Mr. C.’s address is not a hundred miles from

[051-011] here. Would you take a seat, sir?”

[051-012] Mr. Woodcourt thanked Mr. Vholes, but he had no business with him

[051-013] beyond what he had mentioned.

[051-014] “Just so, sir. I believe, sir,” said Mr. Vholes, still quietly

[051-015] insisting on the seat by not giving the address, “that you have

[051-016] influence with Mr. C. Indeed I am aware that you have.”

[051-017] “I was not aware of it myself,” returned Mr. Woodcourt; “but I

[051-018] suppose you know best.”

[051-019] “Sir,” rejoined Mr. Vholes, self-contained as usual, voice and all,

[051-020] “it is a part of my professional duty to know best. It is a part of

[051-021] my professional duty to study and to understand a gentleman who

[051-022] confides his interests to me. In my professional duty I shall not be

[051-023] wanting, sir, if I know it. I may, with the best intentions, be

[051-024] wanting in it without knowing it; but not if I know it, sir.”

[051-025] Mr. Woodcourt again mentioned the address.

[051-026] “Give me leave, sir,” said Mr. Vholes. “Bear with me for a moment.

[051-027] Sir, Mr. C. is playing for a considerable stake, and cannot play

[051-028] without—need I say what?”

[051-029] “Money, I presume?”

[051-030] “Sir,” said Mr. Vholes, “to be honest with you (honesty being my

[051-031] golden rule, whether I gain by it or lose, and I find that I

[051-032] generally lose), money is the word. Now, sir, upon the chances of Mr.

[051-033] C.’s game I express to you no opinion, NO opinion. It might be highly

[051-034] impolitic in Mr. C., after playing so long and so high, to leave off;

[051-035] it might be the reverse; I say nothing. No, sir,” said Mr. Vholes,

[051-036] bringing his hand flat down upon his desk in a positive manner,

[051-037] “nothing.”

[051-038] “You seem to forget,” returned Mr. Woodcourt, “that I ask you to say

[051-039] nothing and have no interest in anything you say.”

[051-040] “Pardon me, sir!” retorted Mr. Vholes. “You do yourself an injustice.

[051-041] No, sir! Pardon me! You shall not—shall not in my office, if I know

[051-042] it—do yourself an injustice. You are interested in anything, and in

[051-043] everything, that relates to your friend. I know human nature much

[051-044] better, sir, than to admit for an instant that a gentleman of your

[051-045] appearance is not interested in whatever concerns his friend.”

[051-046] “Well,” replied Mr. Woodcourt, “that may be. I am particularly

[051-047] interested in his address.”

[051-048] “The number, sir,” said Mr. Vholes parenthetically, “I believe I have

[051-049] already mentioned. If Mr. C. is to continue to play for this

[051-050] considerable stake, sir, he must have funds. Understand me! There are

[051-051] funds in hand at present. I ask for nothing; there are funds in hand.

[051-052] But for the onward play, more funds must be provided, unless Mr. C.

[051-053] is to throw away what he has already ventured, which is wholly and

[051-054] solely a point for his consideration. This, sir, I take the

[051-055] opportunity of stating openly to you as the friend of Mr. C. Without

[051-056] funds I shall always be happy to appear and act for Mr. C. to the

[051-057] extent of all such costs as are safe to be allowed out of the [[inheritance-and-property|estate]],

[051-058] not beyond that. I could not go beyond that, sir, without wronging

[051-059] some one. I must either wrong my three dear girls or my venerable

[051-060] father, who is entirely dependent on me, in the Vale of Taunton; or

[051-061] some one. Whereas, sir, my resolution is (call it weakness or folly

[051-062] if you please) to wrong no one.”

[051-063] Mr. Woodcourt rather sternly rejoined that he was glad to hear it.

[051-064] “I wish, sir,” said Mr. Vholes, “to leave a good name behind me.

[051-065] Therefore I take every opportunity of openly stating to a friend of

[051-066] Mr. C. how Mr. C. is situated. As to myself, sir, the labourer is

[051-067] worthy of his hire. If I undertake to put my shoulder to the wheel, I

[051-068] do it, and I earn what I get. I am here for that purpose. My name is

[051-069] painted on the door outside, with that object.”

[051-070] “And Mr. Carstone’s address, Mr. Vholes?”

[051-071] “Sir,” returned Mr. Vholes, “as I believe I have already mentioned,

[051-072] it is next door. On the second story you will find Mr. C.’s

[051-073] apartments. Mr. C. desires to be near his professional adviser, and I

[051-074] am far from objecting, for I court inquiry.”

[051-075] Upon this Mr. Woodcourt wished Mr. Vholes good day and went in search

[051-076] of Richard, the change in whose appearance he began to understand now

[051-077] but too well.

[051-078] He found him in a dull room, fadedly furnished, much as I had found

[051-079] him in his barrack-room but a little while before, except that he was

[051-080] not [[document-and-letter|writing]] but was sitting with a book before him, from which his

[051-081] eyes and thoughts were far astray. As the door chanced to be standing

[051-082] open, Mr. Woodcourt was in his presence for some moments without

[051-083] being perceived, and he told me that he never could forget the

[051-084] haggardness of his face and the dejection of his manner before he was

[051-085] aroused from his dream.

[051-086] “Woodcourt, my dear fellow,” cried Richard, starting up with extended

[051-087] hands, “you come upon my vision like a ghost.”

[051-088] “A friendly one,” he replied, “and only waiting, as they say ghosts

[051-089] do, to be addressed. How does the mortal world go?” They were seated

[051-090] now, near together.

[051-091] “Badly enough, and slowly enough,” said Richard, “speaking at least

[051-092] for my part of it.”

[051-093] “What part is that?”

[051-094] “The [[chancery-court|Chancery]] part.”

[051-095] “I never heard,” returned Mr. Woodcourt, shaking his head, “of its

[051-096] going well yet.”

[051-097] “Nor I,” said Richard moodily. “Who ever did?” He brightened again in

[051-098] a moment and said with his natural openness, “Woodcourt, I should be

[051-099] sorry to be misunderstood by you, even if I gained by it in your

[051-100] estimation. You must know that I have done no good this long time. I

[051-101] have not intended to do much harm, but I seem to have been capable of

[051-102] nothing else. It may be that I should have done better by keeping out

[051-103] of the net into which my destiny has worked me, but I think not,

[051-104] though I dare say you will soon hear, if you have not already heard,

[051-105] a very different opinion. To make short of a long story, I am afraid

[051-106] I have wanted an object; but I have an object now—or it has me—and

[051-107] it is too late to discuss it. Take me as I am, and make the best of

[051-108] me.”

[051-109] “A bargain,” said Mr. Woodcourt. “Do as much by me in return.”

[051-110] “Oh! You,” returned Richard, “you can pursue your art for its own

[051-111] sake, and can put your hand upon the plough and never turn, and can

[051-112] strike a purpose out of anything. You and I are very different

[051-113] creatures.”

[051-114] He spoke regretfully and lapsed for a moment into his weary

[051-115] condition.

[051-116] “Well, well!” he cried, shaking it off. “Everything has an end. We

[051-117] shall see! So you will take me as I am, and make the best of me?”

[051-118] “Aye! Indeed I will.” They shook hands upon it laughingly, but in

[051-119] deep earnestness. I can answer for one of them with my heart of

[051-120] hearts.

[051-121] “You come as a godsend,” said Richard, “for I have seen nobody here

[051-122] yet but Vholes. Woodcourt, there is one subject I should like to

[051-123] mention, for once and for all, in the beginning of our treaty. You

[051-124] can hardly make the best of me if I don’t. You know, I dare say, that

[051-125] I have an attachment to my cousin [[ada-clare|Ada]]?”

[051-126] Mr. Woodcourt replied that I had hinted as much to him. “Now pray,”

[051-127] returned Richard, “don’t think me a heap of selfishness. Don’t

[051-128] suppose that I am splitting my head and half breaking my heart over

[051-129] this miserable Chancery suit for my own rights and interests alone.

[051-130] Ada’s are bound up with mine; they can’t be separated; Vholes works

[051-131] for both of us. Do think of that!”

[051-132] He was so very solicitous on this head that Mr. Woodcourt gave him

[051-133] the strongest assurances that he did him no injustice.

[051-134] “You see,” said Richard, with something pathetic in his manner of

[051-135] lingering on the point, though it was off-hand and unstudied, “to an

[051-136] upright fellow like you, bringing a friendly face like yours here, I

[051-137] cannot bear the thought of appearing selfish and mean. I want to see

[051-138] Ada righted, Woodcourt, as well as myself; I want to do my utmost to

[051-139] right her, as well as myself; I venture what I can scrape together to

[051-140] extricate her, as well as myself. Do, I beseech you, think of that!”

[051-141] Afterwards, when Mr. Woodcourt came to reflect on what had passed, he

[051-142] was so very much impressed by the strength of Richard’s anxiety on

[051-143] this point that in telling me generally of his first visit to

[051-144] Symond’s Inn he particularly dwelt upon it. It revived a fear I had

[051-145] had before that my dear girl’s little property would be absorbed by

[051-146] Mr. Vholes and that Richard’s justification to himself would be

[051-147] sincerely this. It was just as I began to take care of [[caddy-jellyby|Caddy]] that the

[051-148] interview took place, and I now return to the time when Caddy had

[051-149] recovered and the shade was still between me and my darling.

[051-150] I proposed to Ada that morning that we should go and see Richard. It

[051-151] a little surprised me to find that she hesitated and was not so

[051-152] radiantly willing as I had expected.

[051-153] “My dear,” said I, “you have not had any difference with Richard

[051-154] since I have been so much away?”

[051-155] “No, [[esther-summerson|Esther]].”

[051-156] “Not heard of him, perhaps?” said I.

[051-157] “Yes, I have heard of him,” said Ada.

[051-158] Such tears in her eyes, and such love in her face. I could not make

[051-159] my darling out. Should I go to Richard’s by myself? I said. No, Ada

[051-160] thought I had better not go by myself. Would she go with me? Yes, Ada

[051-161] thought she had better go with me. Should we go now? Yes, let us go

[051-162] now. Well, I could not understand my darling, with the tears in her

[051-163] eyes and the love in her face!

[051-165] chill rain fell at intervals. It was one of those colourless days

[051-167] dust rose at us, the smoke swooped at us, nothing made any compromise

[051-169] quite out of place in the rugged streets, and I thought there were

[051-170] more funerals passing along the dismal pavements than I had ever seen

[051-171] before.

[051-174] likely to be far out, my love, if we go in that direction,” said I.

[051-175] So to Chancery Lane we went, and there, sure enough, we saw it

[051-179] said, perhaps that was Mr. Vholes’s office in the corner there. And

[051-180] it really was.

[051-182] the one, and my darling was going for the other; and my darling was

[051-192] There never was such a good fellow as Woodcourt is. He finds time to

[051-195] so fresh, so sensible, so earnest, so—everything that I am not, that

[051-198] “God bless him,” I thought, “for his truth to me!”
