# Mr. Snagsby

## Overview

Mr. Snagsby is a timid, well-meaning law-stationer who runs a shop in Cook's Court, Cursitor Street. He supplies legal forms and stationery to the offices around Chancery Lane, including Mr. Tulkinghorn's chambers. His meek temperament and fear of his domineering wife make him a sympathetic figure, but his passive role as a conduit for legal documents entangles him in Tulkinghorn's secret investigation — and eventually in the murder inquiry that follows.

## Role in the Novel

Snagsby functions as a structural pivot between the novel's public and private worlds. Through his shop, legal documents and secrets flow between Tulkinghorn, Krook, and the law-writers; through his domestic life, Dickens paints a comic but poignant portrait of a henpecked husband. Snagsby's most significant narrative contribution is his unwitting role in the chain of events linking Tulkinghorn to Nemo (Captain Hawdon) and Lady Dedlock: he employed Nemo as a law-writer, identified the hand that copied the Jarndyce affidavit, and later became the person to whom Guster brought Jo the crossing-sweeper. His testimony before the coroner at Nemo's inquest and his later involvement with Inspector Bucket during the Tulkinghorn murder investigation place him at the center of the novel's two great mysteries without any agency of his own.

## Background and History

Mr. Snagsby is a middle-aged tradesman who inherited his law-stationery business from a deceased uncle, Peffer. He married his wife, the niece of Peffer, partly out of convenience and expectation, and the marriage has defined his domestic existence ever since. The shop at Cook's Court is described as perpetually dim and close, smelling of parchment and dust — a reflection of Snagsby's own circumscribed life. He is a man of modest means but honest dealing, respected by his neighbours if not especially noticed by them.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-snagsby|Mrs. Snagsby]]**: His wife, a sharp-tongued, suspicious woman who rules the household and the accounts. Snagsby lives in perpetual dread of her displeasure, and her jealousy over his supposed secrets — particularly regarding Jo — drives much of the novel's domestic comedy.
- **[[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]]**: Snagsby supplies stationery to Tulkinghorn's chambers and is deferential to the lawyer. Tulkinghorn treats him as a mere functionary, extracting information without ever confiding in him.
- **[[guppy|Mr. Guppy]]**: A former clerk at Kenge and Carboy who occasionally visits Snagsby's shop and is connected through the law-writers' network.
- **[[krook|Krook]]**: Snagsby's landlord; the rag-and-bottle shop below Krook's is part of the same building, and Snagsby tolerates Krook's eccentricities as a matter of business.
- **[[inspector-bucket|Inspector Bucket]]**: During the Tulkinghorn murder investigation, Bucket enlists Snagsby as an unwilling guide through the London underworld, dragging him on nocturnal expeditions that terrify the mild stationer.
- **Nemo ([[captain-hawdon|Captain Hawdon]])**: Snagsby employed the anonymous law-writer known as Nemo, and his identification of Nemo's handwriting on the Jarndyce affidavit is a critical link in Tulkinghorn's chain of evidence against Lady Dedlock.
- **[[jo-crossing-sweeper|Jo]]**: The street urchin whom Snagsby is reluctantly drawn into sheltering and whose testimony before the coroner he is compelled to attend.
- **Guster**: The Snagsbys' maid-of-all-work, a foundling from the workhouse prone to fits, whom Snagsby treats with quiet kindness.

## Key Scenes

Snagsby first appears in Chapter 10, where Dickens introduces Cook's Court and the law-stationer's shop in richly atmospheric detail [010-002]. His domestic subjugation is immediately established through the "bone and flesh of one substance" imagery that binds him to Mrs. Snagsby [010-051].

His most significant narrative intervention occurs when Tulkinghorn shows him a legal document and asks if he recognises the handwriting:

> "This proper name, so used by Mr. Snagsby, has before now sharpened the wit of the legal profession." [010-067]

Snagsby identifies the hand as that of Nemo, the law-writer who copied the document — a seemingly trivial act of professional recognition that sets in motion Tulkinghorn's pursuit of Lady Dedlock's secret.

During the coroner's inquest into Nemo's death (Chapter 11), Snagsby is called as a witness, and Jo's testimony brings the mysterious connection between the dead law-writer and the veiled lady into public record. Snagsby's discomfort at being associated with these dark proceedings is palpable throughout.

In the aftermath of Tulkinghorn's murder, Inspector Bucket commandeers Snagsby as a reluctant companion through the slums of Tom-all-Alone's, a night journey that reduces the timid stationer to a state of bewildered terror [054].

## Character Analysis

Snagsby embodies Dickens's critique of how the Chancery system ensnares the innocent and the powerless. He is a man whose defining characteristic is his desire for a quiet life — yet the tentacles of Jarndyce and Jarndyce reach even into his modest shop. Unlike Richard Carstone or Miss Flite, Snagsby is not a direct party to the suit; he is merely its stationer. Yet he cannot escape the moral and physical contamination that the legal machinery spreads.

Dickens renders Snagsby with a blend of comedy and pathos. The running joke about "not to put too fine a point upon it" — Snagsby's verbal tic, a phrase he uses to soften every statement — is both a character tag and a structural metaphor for his entire life: a man who rounds off every sharp edge to avoid conflict, yet finds himself dragged into the sharpest conflict of all. His relationship with Mrs. Snagsby is one of Dickens's most sustained comic portraits of marital dynamics, but beneath the humour lies a genuine sadness: the Snagsbys are childless, and their domestic life is a sterile ritual of mutual misunderstanding.

The stationer's night journey with Bucket is arguably his most thematically significant scene. Led through the labyrinthine slums, Snagsby confronts the human cost of the legal and social systems he has unwittingly served. He sees the fever-ridden Jo, the desperate Jenny, and the squalor of Tom-all-Alone's — a world whose existence he has spent his life not putting too fine a point upon. The episode transforms him from a comic type into a moral witness.
