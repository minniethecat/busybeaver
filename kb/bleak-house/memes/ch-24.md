[024-001] As soon as [[richard-carstone|Richard]] and I had held the conversation of which I have

[024-002] given an account, Richard communicated the state of his mind to Mr.

[024-003] Jarndyce. I doubt if my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]] were altogether taken by surprise

[024-004] when he received the representation, though it caused him much

[024-005] uneasiness and disappointment. He and Richard were often closeted

[024-006] together, late at night and early in the morning, and passed whole

[024-007] days in [[london|London]], and had innumerable appointments with Mr. Kenge, and

[024-008] laboured through a quantity of disagreeable business. While they were

[024-009] thus employed, my guardian, though he underwent considerable

[024-010] inconvenience from the state of the wind and rubbed his head so

[024-011] constantly that not a single hair upon it ever rested in its right

[024-012] place, was as genial with [[ada-clare|Ada]] and me as at any other time, but

[024-013] maintained a steady reserve on these matters. And as our utmost

[024-014] endeavours could only elicit from Richard himself sweeping assurances

[024-015] that everything was going on capitally and that it really was all

[024-016] right at last, our anxiety was not much relieved by him.

[024-017] We learnt, however, as the time went on, that a new application was

[024-018] made to the Lord Chancellor on Richard’s behalf as an infant and a

[024-019] ward, and I don’t know what, and that there was a quantity of

[024-020] talking, and that the Lord Chancellor described him in open court as

[024-021] a vexatious and capricious infant, and that the matter was adjourned

[024-022] and readjourned, and referred, and reported on, and petitioned about

[024-023] until Richard began to doubt (as he told us) whether, if he entered

[024-024] the army at all, it would not be as a veteran of seventy or eighty

[024-025] years of age. At last an appointment was made for him to see the Lord

[024-026] Chancellor again in his private room, and there the Lord Chancellor

[024-027] very seriously reproved him for trifling with time and not knowing

[024-028] his mind—“a pretty good joke, I think,” said Richard, “from that

[024-029] quarter!”—and at last it was settled that his application should be

[024-030] granted. His name was entered at the Horse Guards as an applicant for

[024-031] an ensign’s commission; the purchase-money was deposited at an

[024-032] agent’s; and Richard, in his usual characteristic way, plunged into a

[024-033] violent course of military study and got up at five o’clock every

[024-034] morning to practise the broadsword exercise.

[024-035] Thus, vacation succeeded term, and term succeeded vacation. We

[024-036] sometimes heard of [[jarndyce-and-jarndyce|Jarndyce and Jarndyce]] as being in the paper or out

[024-037] of the paper, or as being to be mentioned, or as being to be spoken

[024-038] to; and it came on, and it went off. Richard, who was now in a

[024-039] professor’s house in London, was able to be with us less frequently

[024-040] than before; my guardian still maintained the same reserve; and so

[024-041] time passed until the commission was obtained and Richard received

[024-042] directions with it to join a regiment in Ireland.

[024-043] He arrived post-haste with the intelligence one evening, and had a

[024-044] long conference with my guardian. Upwards of an hour elapsed before

[024-045] my guardian put his head into the room where Ada and I were sitting

[024-046] and said, “Come in, my dears!” We went in and found Richard, whom we

[024-047] had last seen in high spirits, leaning on the chimney-piece looking

[024-048] mortified and angry.

[024-049] “Rick and I, Ada,” said Mr. Jarndyce, “are not quite of one mind.

[024-050] Come, come, Rick, put a brighter face upon it!”

[024-051] “You are very hard with me, sir,” said Richard. “The harder because

[024-052] you have been so considerate to me in all other respects and have

[024-053] done me kindnesses that I can never acknowledge. I never could have

[024-054] been set right without you, sir.”

[024-055] “Well, well!” said Mr. Jarndyce. “I want to set you more right yet. I

[024-056] want to set you more right with yourself.”

[024-057] “I hope you will excuse my saying, sir,” returned Richard in a fiery

[024-058] way, but yet respectfully, “that I think I am the best judge about

[024-059] myself.”

[024-060] “I hope you will excuse my saying, my dear Rick,” observed Mr.

[024-061] Jarndyce with the sweetest cheerfulness and good humour, “that it’s

[024-062] quite natural in you to think so, but I don’t think so. I must do my

[024-063] duty, Rick, or you could never care for me in cool blood; and I hope

[024-064] you will always care for me, cool and hot.”

[024-065] Ada had turned so pale that he made her sit down in his reading-chair

[024-066] and sat beside her.

[024-067] “It’s nothing, my dear,” he said, “it’s nothing. Rick and I have only

[024-068] had a friendly difference, which we must state to you, for you are

[024-069] the theme. Now you are afraid of what’s coming.”

[024-070] “I am not indeed, cousin John,” replied Ada with a smile, “if it is

[024-071] to come from you.”

[024-072] “Thank you, my dear. Do you give me a minute’s calm attention,

[024-073] without looking at Rick. And, little woman, do you likewise. My dear

[024-074] girl,” putting his hand on hers as it lay on the side of the

[024-075] easy-chair, “you recollect the talk we had, we four when the little

[024-076] woman told me of a little love affair?”

[024-077] “It is not likely that either Richard or I can ever forget your

[024-078] kindness that day, cousin John.”

[024-079] “I can never forget it,” said Richard.

[024-080] “And I can never forget it,” said Ada.

[024-081] “So much the easier what I have to say, and so much the easier for us

[024-082] to agree,” returned my guardian, his face irradiated by the

[024-083] gentleness and honour of his heart. “Ada, my bird, you should know

[024-084] that Rick has now chosen his [[professional-identity|profession]] for the last time. All that

[024-085] he has of certainty will be expended when he is fully equipped. He

[024-086] has exhausted his resources and is bound henceforward to the tree he

[024-087] has planted.”

[024-088] “Quite true that I have exhausted my present resources, and I am

[024-089] quite content to know it. But what I have of certainty, sir,” said

[024-090] Richard, “is not all I have.”

[024-091] “Rick, Rick!” cried my guardian with a sudden terror in his manner,

[024-092] and in an altered voice, and putting up his hands as if he would have

[024-093] stopped his ears. “For the love of God, don’t found a hope or

[024-094] expectation on the family curse! Whatever you do on this side the

[024-095] grave, never give one lingering glance towards the horrible phantom

[024-096] that has haunted us so many years. Better to borrow, better to beg,

[024-097] better to die!”

[024-098] We were all startled by the fervour of this warning. Richard bit his

[024-099] lip and held his breath, and glanced at me as if he felt, and knew

[024-100] that I felt too, how much he needed it.

[024-101] “Ada, my dear,” said Mr. Jarndyce, recovering his cheerfulness,

[024-102] “these are strong words of advice, but I live in [[bleak-house|Bleak House]] and have

[024-103] seen a sight here. Enough of that. All Richard had to start him in

[024-104] the race of life is ventured. I recommend to him and you, for his

[024-105] sake and your own, that he should depart from us with the

[024-106] understanding that there is no sort of contract between you. I must

[024-107] go further. I will be plain with you both. You were to confide freely

[024-108] in me, and I will confide freely in you. I ask you wholly to

[024-109] relinquish, for the present, any tie but your relationship.”

[024-110] “Better to say at once, sir,” returned Richard, “that you renounce

[024-111] all confidence in me and that you advise Ada to do the same.”

[024-112] “Better to say nothing of the sort, Rick, because I don’t mean it.”

[024-113] “You think I have begun ill, sir,” retorted Richard. “I HAVE, I

[024-114] know.”

[024-115] “How I hoped you would begin, and how go on, I told you when we spoke

[024-116] of these things last,” said Mr. Jarndyce in a cordial and encouraging

[024-117] manner. “You have not made that beginning yet, but there is a time

[024-118] for all things, and yours is not gone by; rather, it is just now

[024-119] fully come. Make a clear beginning altogether. You two (very young,

[024-120] my dears) are cousins. As yet, you are nothing more. What more may

[024-121] come must come of being worked out, Rick, and no sooner.”

[024-122] “You are very hard with me, sir,” said Richard. “Harder than I could

[024-123] have supposed you would be.”

[024-124] “My dear boy,” said Mr. Jarndyce, “I am harder with myself when I do

[024-125] anything that gives you pain. You have your remedy in your own hands.

[024-126] Ada, it is better for him that he should be free and that there

[024-127] should be no youthful engagement between you. Rick, it is better for

[024-128] her, much better; you owe it to her. Come! Each of you will do what

[024-129] is best for the other, if not what is best for yourselves.”

[024-130] “Why is it best, sir?” returned Richard hastily. “It was not when we

[024-131] opened our hearts to you. You did not say so then.”

[024-132] “I have had experience since. I don’t blame you, Rick, but I have had

[024-133] experience since.”

[024-134] “You mean of me, sir.”

[024-135] “Well! Yes, of both of you,” said Mr. Jarndyce kindly. “The time is

[024-136] not come for your standing pledged to one another. It is not right,

[024-137] and I must not recognize it. Come, come, my young cousins, begin

[024-138] afresh! Bygones shall be bygones, and a new page turned for you to

[024-139] write your lives in.”

[024-140] Richard gave an anxious glance at Ada but said nothing.

[024-141] “I have avoided saying one word to either of you or to [[esther-summerson|Esther]],” said

[024-142] Mr. Jarndyce, “until now, in order that we might be open as the day,

[024-143] and all on equal terms. I now affectionately advise, I now most

[024-144] earnestly entreat, you two to part as you came here. Leave all else

[024-145] to time, truth, and steadfastness. If you do otherwise, you will do

[024-146] wrong, and you will have made me do wrong in ever bringing you

[024-147] together.”

[024-148] A long silence succeeded.

[024-149] “Cousin Richard,” said Ada then, raising her blue eyes tenderly to

[024-150] his face, “after what our cousin John has said, I think no choice is

[024-151] left us. Your mind may be quite at ease about me, for you will leave

[024-152] me here under his care and will be sure that I can have nothing to

[024-153] wish for—quite sure if I guide myself by his advice. I—I don’t

[024-154] doubt, cousin Richard,” said Ada, a little confused, “that you are

[024-155] very fond of me, and I—I don’t think you will fall in love with

[024-156] anybody else. But I should like you to consider well about it too, as

[024-157] I should like you to be in all things very happy. You may trust in

[024-158] me, cousin Richard. I am not at all changeable; but I am not

[024-159] unreasonable, and should never blame you. Even cousins may be sorry

[024-160] to part; and in truth I am very, very sorry, Richard, though I know

[024-161] it’s for your welfare. I shall always think of you affectionately,

[024-162] and often talk of you with Esther, and—and perhaps you will

[024-163] sometimes think a little of me, cousin Richard. So now,” said Ada,

[024-164] going up to him and giving him her trembling hand, “we are only

[024-165] cousins again, Richard—for the time perhaps—and I pray for a

[024-166] blessing on my dear cousin, wherever he goes!”

[024-167] It was strange to me that Richard should not be able to forgive my

[024-168] guardian for entertaining the very same opinion of him which he

[024-169] himself had expressed of himself in much stronger terms to me. But it

[024-170] was certainly the case. I observed with great regret that from this

[024-171] hour he never was as free and open with Mr. Jarndyce as he had been

[024-172] before. He had every reason given him to be so, but he was not; and

[024-173] solely on his side, an estrangement began to arise between them.

[024-174] In the business of preparation and equipment he soon lost himself,

[024-175] and even his grief at parting from Ada, who remained in Hertfordshire

[024-176] while he, Mr. Jarndyce, and I went up to London for a week. He

[024-177] remembered her by fits and starts, even with bursts of tears, and at

[024-178] such times would confide to me the heaviest self-reproaches. But in a

[024-179] few minutes he would recklessly conjure up some undefinable means by

[024-180] which they were both to be made rich and happy for ever, and would

[024-181] become as gay as possible.

[024-182] It was a busy time, and I trotted about with him all day long, buying

[024-183] a variety of things of which he stood in need. Of the things he would

[024-184] have bought if he had been left to his own ways I say nothing. He was

[024-185] perfectly confidential with me, and often talked so sensibly and

[024-186] feelingly about his faults and his vigorous resolutions, and dwelt so

[024-187] much upon the encouragement he derived from these conversations that

[024-188] I could never have been tired if I had tried.

[024-189] There used, in that week, to come backward and forward to our lodging

[024-190] to fence with Richard a person who had formerly been a cavalry

[024-191] soldier; he was a fine bluff-looking man, of a frank free bearing,

[024-192] with whom Richard had practised for some months. I heard so much

[024-193] about him, not only from Richard, but from my guardian too, that I

[024-194] was purposely in the room with my work one morning after breakfast

[024-195] when he came.

[024-196] “Good morning, [[george-rouncewell|Mr. George]],” said my guardian, who happened to be

[024-197] alone with me. “Mr. Carstone will be here directly. Meanwhile, Miss

[024-198] Summerson is very happy to see you, I know. Sit down.”

[024-199] He sat down, a little disconcerted by my presence, I thought, and

[024-200] without looking at me, drew his heavy sunburnt hand across and across

[024-201] his upper lip.

[024-202] “You are as punctual as the sun,” said Mr. Jarndyce.

[024-203] “Military time, sir,” he replied. “Force of habit. A mere habit in

[024-204] me, sir. I am not at all business-like.”

[024-205] “Yet you have a large establishment, too, I am told?” said Mr.

[024-206] Jarndyce.

[024-207] “Not much of a one, sir. I keep a shooting gallery, but not much of a

[024-208] one.”

[024-209] “And what kind of a shot and what kind of a swordsman do you make of

[024-210] Mr. Carstone?” said my guardian.

[024-211] “Pretty good, sir,” he replied, folding his arms upon his broad chest

[024-212] and looking very large. “If Mr. Carstone was to give his full mind to

[024-213] it, he would come out very good.”

[024-214] “But he don’t, I suppose?” said my guardian.

[024-215] “He did at first, sir, but not afterwards. Not his full mind. Perhaps

[024-216] he has something else upon it—some young lady, perhaps.” His bright

[024-217] dark eyes glanced at me for the first time.

[024-218] “He has not me upon his mind, I assure you, Mr. George,” said I,

[024-219] laughing, “though you seem to suspect me.”

[024-220] He reddened a little through his brown and made me a trooper’s bow.

[024-221] “No offence, I hope, miss. I am one of the roughs.”

[024-222] “Not at all,” said I. “I take it as a compliment.”

[024-223] If he had not looked at me before, he looked at me now in three or

[024-224] four quick successive glances. “I beg your pardon, sir,” he said to

[024-225] my guardian with a manly kind of diffidence, “but you did me the

[024-226] honour to mention the young lady’s name—”

[024-227] “Miss Summerson.”

[024-228] “Miss Summerson,” he repeated, and looked at me again.

[024-229] “Do you know the name?” I asked.

[024-230] “No, miss. To my knowledge I never heard it. I thought I had seen you

[024-231] somewhere.”

[024-232] “I think not,” I returned, raising my head from my work to look at

[024-233] him; and there was something so genuine in his speech and manner that

[024-234] I was glad of the opportunity. “I remember faces very well.”

[024-235] “So do I, miss!” he returned, meeting my look with the fullness of

[024-236] his dark eyes and broad forehead. “Humph! What set me off, now, upon

[024-237] that!”

[024-238] His once more reddening through his brown and being disconcerted by

[024-239] his efforts to remember the association brought my guardian to his

[024-240] relief.

[024-241] “Have you many pupils, Mr. George?”

[024-242] “They vary in their number, sir. Mostly they’re but a small lot to

[024-243] live by.”

[024-244] “And what classes of chance people come to practise at your gallery?”

[024-245] “All sorts, sir. Natives and foreigners. From gentlemen to

[024-246] ’prentices. I have had Frenchwomen come, before now, and show

[024-247] themselves dabs at pistol-shooting. Mad people out of number, of

[024-248] course, but THEY go everywhere where the doors stand open.”

[024-249] “People don’t come with grudges and schemes of finishing their

[024-250] practice with live targets, I hope?” said my guardian, smiling.

[024-251] “Not much of that, sir, though that HAS happened. Mostly they come

[024-252] for skill—or idleness. Six of one, and half-a-dozen of the other. I

[024-253] beg your pardon,” said Mr. George, sitting stiffly upright and

[024-254] squaring an elbow on each knee, “but I believe you’re a [[chancery-court|Chancery]]

[024-255] suitor, if I have heard correct?”

[024-256] “I am sorry to say I am.”

[024-257] “I have had one of YOUR compatriots in my time, sir.”

[024-258] “A Chancery suitor?” returned my guardian. “How was that?”

[024-259] “Why, the man was so badgered and worried and tortured by being

[024-260] knocked about from post to pillar, and from pillar to post,” said Mr.

[024-261] George, “that he got out of sorts. I don’t believe he had any idea of

[024-262] taking aim at anybody, but he was in that condition of resentment and

[024-263] violence that he would come and pay for fifty shots and fire away

[024-264] till he was red hot. One day I said to him when there was nobody by

[024-265] and he had been talking to me angrily about his wrongs, ‘If this

[024-266] practice is a safety-valve, comrade, well and good; but I don’t

[024-267] altogether like your being so bent upon it in your present state of

[024-268] mind; I’d rather you took to something else.’ I was on my guard for a

[024-269] blow, he was that passionate; but he received it in very good part

[024-270] and left off directly. We shook hands and struck up a sort of

[024-271] friendship.”

[024-272] “What was that man?” asked my guardian in a new tone of interest.

[024-273] “Why, he began by being a small Shropshire farmer before they made a

[024-274] baited bull of him,” said Mr. George.

[024-275] “Was his name Gridley?”

[024-276] “It was, sir.”

[024-277] Mr. George directed another succession of quick bright glances at me

[024-278] as my guardian and I exchanged a word or two of surprise at the

[024-279] coincidence, and I therefore explained to him how we knew the name.

[024-280] He made me another of his soldierly bows in acknowledgment of what he

[024-281] called my condescension.

[024-282] “I don’t know,” he said as he looked at me, “what it is that sets me

[024-283] off again—but—bosh! What’s my head running against!” He passed one

[024-284] of his heavy hands over his crisp dark hair as if to sweep the broken

[024-285] thoughts out of his mind and sat a little forward, with one arm

[024-286] akimbo and the other resting on his leg, looking in a brown study at

[024-287] the ground.

[024-288] “I am sorry to learn that the same state of mind has got this Gridley

[024-289] into new troubles and that he is in hiding,” said my guardian.

[024-290] “So I am told, sir,” returned Mr. George, still musing and looking on

[024-291] the ground. “So I am told.”

[024-292] “You don’t know where?”

[024-293] “No, sir,” returned the trooper, lifting up his eyes and coming out

[024-294] of his reverie. “I can’t say anything about him. He will be worn out

[024-295] soon, I expect. You may file a strong man’s heart away for a good

[024-296] many years, but it will tell all of a sudden at last.”

[024-297] Richard’s entrance stopped the conversation. Mr. George rose, made me

[024-298] another of his soldierly bows, wished my guardian a good day, and

[024-299] strode heavily out of the room.

[024-300] This was the morning of the day appointed for Richard’s departure. We

[024-301] had no more purchases to make now; I had completed all his packing

[024-302] early in the afternoon; and our time was disengaged until night, when

[024-303] he was to go to Liverpool for Holyhead. Jarndyce and Jarndyce being

[024-304] again expected to come on that day, Richard proposed to me that we

[024-305] should go down to the court and hear what passed. As it was his last

[024-306] day, and he was eager to go, and I had never been there, I gave my

[024-307] consent and we walked down to Westminster, where the court was then

[024-308] sitting. We beguiled the way with arrangements concerning the [[document-and-letter|letters]]

[024-309] that Richard was to write to me and the letters that I was to write

[024-310] to him and with a great many hopeful projects. My guardian knew where

[024-311] we were going and therefore was not with us.

[024-312] When we came to the court, there was the Lord Chancellor—the same

[024-313] whom I had seen in his private room in Lincoln’s Inn—sitting in

[024-314] great state and gravity on the bench, with the mace and seals on a

[024-315] red table below him and an immense flat nosegay, like a little

[024-316] garden, which scented the whole court. Below the table, again, was a

[024-317] long row of solicitors, with bundles of papers on the matting at

[024-318] their feet; and then there were the gentlemen of the bar in wigs and

[024-319] gowns—some awake and some asleep, and one talking, and nobody paying

[024-320] much attention to what he said. The Lord Chancellor leaned back in

[024-321] his very easy chair with his elbow on the cushioned arm and his

[024-322] forehead resting on his hand; some of those who were present dozed;

[024-323] some read the newspapers; some walked about or whispered in groups:

[024-324] all seemed perfectly at their ease, by no means in a hurry, very

[024-325] unconcerned, and extremely comfortable.

[024-326] To see everything going on so smoothly and to think of the roughness

[024-327] of the suitors’ lives and deaths; to see all that full dress and

[024-328] ceremony and to think of the waste, and want, and beggared misery it

[024-329] represented; to consider that while the sickness of hope deferred was

[024-330] raging in so many hearts this polite show went calmly on from day to

[024-331] day, and year to year, in such good order and composure; to behold

[024-332] the Lord Chancellor and the whole array of practitioners under him

[024-333] looking at one another and at the spectators as if nobody had ever

[024-334] heard that all over England the name in which they were assembled was

[024-335] a bitter jest, was held in universal horror, contempt, and

[024-336] indignation, was known for something so flagrant and bad that little

[024-337] short of a miracle could bring any good out of it to any one—this

[024-338] was so curious and self-contradictory to me, who had no experience of

[024-339] it, that it was at first incredible, and I could not comprehend it. I

[024-340] sat where Richard put me, and tried to listen, and looked about me;

[024-341] but there seemed to be no reality in the whole scene except poor

[024-342] little [[miss-flite|Miss Flite]], the madwoman, standing on a bench and nodding at

[024-343] it.

[024-344] Miss Flite soon espied us and came to where we sat. She gave me a

[024-345] gracious welcome to her domain and indicated, with much gratification

[024-346] and pride, its principal attractions. Mr. Kenge also came to speak to

[024-347] us and did the honours of the place in much the same way, with the

[024-348] bland modesty of a proprietor. It was not a very good day for a

[024-349] visit, he said; he would have preferred the first day of term; but it

[024-350] was imposing, it was imposing.

[024-351] When we had been there half an hour or so, the case in progress—if I

[024-352] may use a phrase so ridiculous in such a connexion—seemed to die out

[024-353] of its own vapidity, without coming, or being by anybody expected to

[024-354] come, to any result. The Lord Chancellor then threw down a bundle of

[024-355] papers from his desk to the gentlemen below him, and somebody said,

[024-356] “Jarndyce and Jarndyce.” Upon this there was a buzz, and a laugh, and

[024-357] a general withdrawal of the bystanders, and a bringing in of great

[024-358] heaps, and piles, and bags and bags full of papers.

[024-359] I think it came on “for further directions”—about some bill of

[024-360] costs, to the best of my understanding, which was confused enough.

[024-361] But I counted twenty-three gentlemen in wigs who said they were “in

[024-362] it,” and none of them appeared to understand it much better than I.

[024-363] They chatted about it with the Lord Chancellor, and contradicted and

[024-364] explained among themselves, and some of them said it was this way,

[024-365] and some of them said it was that way, and some of them jocosely

[024-366] proposed to read huge volumes of affidavits, and there was more

[024-367] buzzing and laughing, and everybody concerned was in a state of idle

[024-368] entertainment, and nothing could be made of it by anybody. After an

[024-369] hour or so of this, and a good many speeches being begun and cut

[024-370] short, it was “referred back for the present,” as Mr. Kenge said, and

[024-371] the papers were bundled up again before the clerks had finished

[024-372] bringing them in.

[024-373] I glanced at Richard on the termination of these hopeless proceedings

[024-374] and was shocked to see the worn look of his handsome young face. “It

[024-375] can’t last for ever, Dame Durden. Better luck next time!” was all he
