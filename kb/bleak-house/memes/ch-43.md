[043-001] It matters little now how much I thought of my living mother who had

[043-002] told me evermore to consider her dead. I could not venture to

[043-003] approach her or to communicate with her in [[document-and-letter|writing]], for my sense of

[043-004] the peril in which her life was passed was only to be equalled by my

[043-005] fears of increasing it. Knowing that my mere existence as a living

[043-006] creature was an unforeseen danger in her way, I could not always

[043-007] conquer that terror of myself which had seized me when I first knew

[043-008] the secret. At no time did I dare to utter her name. I felt as if I

[043-009] did not even dare to hear it. If the conversation anywhere, when I

[043-010] was present, took that direction, as it sometimes naturally did, I

[043-011] tried not to hear: I mentally counted, repeated something that I

[043-012] knew, or went out of the room. I am conscious now that I often did

[043-013] these things when there can have been no danger of her being spoken

[043-014] of, but I did them in the dread I had of hearing anything that might

[043-015] lead to her betrayal, and to her betrayal through me.

[043-016] It matters little now how often I recalled the tones of my mother’s

[043-017] voice, wondered whether I should ever hear it again as I so longed to

[043-018] do, and thought how strange and desolate it was that it should be so

[043-019] new to me. It matters little that I watched for every public mention

[043-020] of my mother’s name; that I passed and repassed the door of her house

[043-021] in town, loving it, but afraid to look at it; that I once sat in the

[043-022] theatre when my mother was there and saw me, and when we were so wide

[043-023] asunder before the great company of all degrees that any link or

[043-024] confidence between us seemed a dream. It is all, all over. My lot has

[043-025] been so blest that I can relate little of myself which is not a story

[043-026] of goodness and generosity in others. I may well pass that little and

[043-027] go on.

[043-028] When we were settled at home again, [[ada-clare|Ada]] and I had many conversations

[043-029] with my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]] of which [[richard-carstone|Richard]] was the theme. My dear girl was

[043-030] deeply grieved that he should do their kind cousin so much wrong, but

[043-031] she was so faithful to Richard that she could not bear to blame him

[043-032] even for that. My guardian was assured of it, and never coupled his

[043-033] name with a word of reproof. “Rick is mistaken, my dear,” he would

[043-034] say to her. “Well, well! We have all been mistaken over and over

[043-035] again. We must trust to you and time to set him right.”

[043-036] We knew afterwards what we suspected then, that he did not trust to

[043-037] time until he had often tried to open Richard’s eyes. That he had

[043-038] written to him, gone to him, talked with him, tried every gentle and

[043-039] persuasive art his kindness could devise. Our poor devoted Richard

[043-040] was deaf and blind to all. If he were wrong, he would make amends

[043-041] when the [[chancery-court|Chancery]] suit was over. If he were groping in the dark,

[043-042] he could not do better than do his utmost to clear away those

[043-043] clouds in which so much was confused and obscured. Suspicion and

[043-044] misunderstanding were the fault of the suit? Then let him work the

[043-045] suit out and come through it to his right mind. This was his

[043-046] unvarying reply. [[jarndyce-and-jarndyce|Jarndyce and Jarndyce]] had obtained such possession

[043-047] of his whole nature that it was impossible to place any consideration

[043-048] before him which he did not, with a distorted kind of reason, make a

[043-049] new argument in favour of his doing what he did. “So that it is even

[043-050] more mischievous,” said my guardian once to me, “to remonstrate with

[043-051] the poor dear fellow than to leave him alone.”

[043-052] I took one of these opportunities of mentioning my doubts of Mr.

[043-053] [[harold-skimpole|Skimpole]] as a good adviser for Richard.

[043-054] “Adviser!” returned my guardian, laughing, “My dear, who would advise

[043-055] with Skimpole?”

[043-056] “Encourager would perhaps have been a better word,” said I.

[043-057] “Encourager!” returned my guardian again. “Who could be encouraged by

[043-058] Skimpole?”

[043-059] “Not Richard?” I asked.

[043-060] “No,” he replied. “Such an unworldly, uncalculating, gossamer

[043-061] creature is a relief to him and an amusement. But as to advising or

[043-062] encouraging or occupying a serious station towards anybody or

[043-063] anything, it is simply not to be thought of in such a child as

[043-064] Skimpole.”

[043-065] “Pray, cousin John,” said Ada, who had just joined us and now looked

[043-066] over my shoulder, “what made him such a child?”

[043-067] “What made him such a child?” inquired my guardian, rubbing his head,

[043-068] a little at a loss.

[043-069] “Yes, cousin John.”

[043-070] “Why,” he slowly replied, roughening his head more and more, “he is

[043-071] all sentiment, and—and susceptibility, and—and sensibility,

[043-072] and—and imagination. And these qualities are not regulated in him,

[043-073] somehow. I suppose the people who admired him for them in his youth

[043-074] attached too much importance to them and too little to any training

[043-075] that would have balanced and adjusted them, and so he became what he

[043-076] is. Hey?” said my guardian, stopping short and looking at us

[043-077] hopefully. “What do you think, you two?”

[043-078] Ada, glancing at me, said she thought it was a pity he should be an

[043-079] expense to Richard.

[043-080] “So it is, so it is,” returned my guardian hurriedly. “That must not

[043-081] be. We must arrange that. I must prevent it. That will never do.”

[043-082] And I said I thought it was to be regretted that he had ever

[043-083] introduced Richard to [[mr-vholes|Mr. Vholes]] for a present of five pounds.

[043-084] “Did he?” said my guardian with a passing shade of vexation on his

[043-085] face. “But there you have the man. There you have the man! There is

[043-086] nothing mercenary in that with him. He has no idea of the value of

[043-087] money. He introduces Rick, and then he is good friends with Mr.

[043-088] Vholes and borrows five pounds of him. He means nothing by it and

[043-089] thinks nothing of it. He told you himself, I’ll be bound, my dear?”

[043-090] “Oh, yes!” said I.

[043-091] “Exactly!” cried my guardian, quite triumphant. “There you have the

[043-092] man! If he had meant any harm by it or was conscious of any harm in

[043-093] it, he wouldn’t tell it. He tells it as he does it in mere

[043-094] simplicity. But you shall see him in his own home, and then you’ll

[043-095] understand him better. We must pay a visit to Harold Skimpole and

[043-096] caution him on these points. Lord bless you, my dears, an infant, an

[043-097] infant!”

[043-098] In pursuance of this plan, we went into [[london|London]] on an early day and

[043-099] presented ourselves at Mr. Skimpole’s door.

[043-100] He lived in a place called the Polygon, in Somers Town, where there

[043-101] were at that time a number of poor Spanish refugees walking about in

[043-102] cloaks, smoking little paper cigars. Whether he was a better tenant

[043-103] than one might have supposed, in consequence of his friend Somebody

[043-104] always paying his rent at last, or whether his inaptitude for

[043-105] business rendered it particularly difficult to turn him out, I don’t

[043-106] know; but he had occupied the same house some years. It was in a

[043-107] state of dilapidation quite equal to our expectation. Two or three of

[043-108] the area railings were gone, the water-butt was broken, the knocker

[043-109] was loose, the bell-handle had been pulled off a long time to judge

[043-110] from the rusty state of the wire, and dirty footprints on the steps

[043-111] were the only signs of its being inhabited.

[043-112] A slatternly full-blown girl who seemed to be bursting out at the

[043-113] rents in her gown and the cracks in her shoes like an over-ripe berry

[043-114] answered our knock by opening the door a very little way and stopping

[043-115] up the gap with her figure. As she knew Mr. Jarndyce (indeed Ada and

[043-116] I both thought that she evidently associated him with the receipt of

[043-117] her wages), she immediately relented and allowed us to pass in. The

[043-118] lock of the door being in a disabled condition, she then applied

[043-119] herself to securing it with the chain, which was not in good action

[043-120] either, and said would we go upstairs?

[043-121] We went upstairs to the first floor, still seeing no other furniture

[043-122] than the dirty footprints. Mr. Jarndyce without further ceremony

[043-123] entered a room there, and we followed. It was dingy enough and not at

[043-124] all clean, but furnished with an odd kind of shabby luxury, with a

[043-125] large footstool, a sofa, and plenty of cushions, an easy-chair, and

[043-126] plenty of pillows, a piano, books, drawing materials, music,

[043-127] newspapers, and a few sketches and pictures. A broken pane of glass

[043-128] in one of the dirty windows was papered and wafered over, but there

[043-129] was a little plate of hothouse nectarines on the table, and there was

[043-130] another of grapes, and another of sponge-cakes, and there was a

[043-131] bottle of light wine. Mr. Skimpole himself reclined upon the sofa in

[043-132] a dressing-gown, drinking some fragrant coffee from an old china

[043-133] cup—it was then about mid-day—and looking at a collection of

[043-134] wallflowers in the balcony.

[043-135] He was not in the least disconcerted by our appearance, but rose and

[043-136] received us in his usual airy manner.

[043-137] “Here I am, you see!” he said when we were seated, not without some

[043-138] little difficulty, the greater part of the chairs being broken. “Here

[043-139] I am! This is my frugal breakfast. Some men want legs of beef and

[043-140] mutton for breakfast; I don’t. Give me my peach, my cup of coffee,

[043-141] and my claret; I am content. I don’t want them for themselves, but

[043-142] they remind me of the sun. There’s nothing solar about legs of beef

[043-143] and mutton. Mere animal satisfaction!”

[043-144] “This is our friend’s consulting-room (or would be, if he ever

[043-145] prescribed), his sanctum, his studio,” said my guardian to us.

[043-146] “Yes,” said Mr. Skimpole, turning his bright face about, “this is the

[043-147] bird’s cage. This is where the bird lives and sings. They pluck his

[043-148] feathers now and then and clip his wings, but he sings, he sings!”

[043-149] He handed us the grapes, repeating in his radiant way, “He sings! Not

[043-150] an ambitious note, but still he sings.”

[043-151] “These are very fine,” said my guardian. “A present?”

[043-152] “No,” he answered. “No! Some amiable gardener sells them. His man

[043-153] wanted to know, when he brought them last evening, whether he should

[043-154] wait for the money. ‘Really, my friend,’ I said, ‘I think not—if

[043-155] your time is of any value to you.’ I suppose it was, for he went

[043-156] away.”

[043-157] My guardian looked at us with a smile, as though he asked us, “Is it

[043-158] possible to be worldly with this baby?”

[043-159] “This is a day,” said Mr. Skimpole, gaily taking a little claret in a

[043-160] tumbler, “that will ever be remembered here. We shall call it Saint

[043-161] Clare and Saint Summerson day. You must see my daughters. I have a

[043-162] blue-eyed daughter who is my Beauty daughter, I have a Sentiment

[043-163] daughter, and I have a Comedy daughter. You must see them all.

[043-164] They’ll be enchanted.”

[043-165] He was going to summon them when my guardian interposed and asked him

[043-166] to pause a moment, as he wished to say a word to him first. “My dear

[043-167] Jarndyce,” he cheerfully replied, going back to his sofa, “as many

[043-168] moments as you please. Time is no object here. We never know what

[043-169] o’clock it is, and we never care. Not the way to get on in life,

[043-170] you’ll tell me? Certainly. But we DON’T get on in life. We don’t

[043-171] pretend to do it.”

[043-172] My guardian looked at us again, plainly saying, “You hear him?”

[043-173] “Now, Harold,” he began, “the word I have to say relates to Rick.”

[043-174] “The dearest friend I have!” returned Mr. Skimpole cordially. “I

[043-175] suppose he ought not to be my dearest friend, as he is not on terms

[043-176] with you. But he is, I can’t help it; he is full of youthful poetry,

[043-177] and I love him. If you don’t like it, I can’t help it. I love him.”

[043-178] The engaging frankness with which he made this declaration really had

[043-179] a disinterested appearance and captivated my guardian, if not, for

[043-180] the moment, Ada too.

[043-181] “You are welcome to love him as much as you like,” returned Mr.

[043-182] Jarndyce, “but we must save his pocket, Harold.”

[043-183] “Oh!” said Mr. Skimpole. “His pocket? Now you are coming to what I

[043-184] don’t understand.” Taking a little more claret and dipping one of the

[043-185] cakes in it, he shook his head and smiled at Ada and me with an

[043-186] ingenuous foreboding that he never could be made to understand.

[043-187] “If you go with him here or there,” said my guardian plainly, “you

[043-188] must not let him pay for both.”

[043-189] “My dear Jarndyce,” returned Mr. Skimpole, his genial face irradiated

[043-190] by the comicality of this idea, “what am I to do? If he takes me

[043-191] anywhere, I must go. And how can I pay? I never have any money. If I

[043-192] had any money, I don’t know anything about it. Suppose I say to a

[043-193] man, how much? Suppose the man says to me seven and sixpence? I know

[043-194] nothing about seven and sixpence. It is impossible for me to pursue

[043-195] the subject with any consideration for the man. I don’t go about

[043-196] asking busy people what seven and sixpence is in Moorish—which I

[043-197] don’t understand. Why should I go about asking them what seven and

[043-198] sixpence is in Money—which I don’t understand?”

[043-199] “Well,” said my guardian, by no means displeased with this artless

[043-200] reply, “if you come to any kind of journeying with Rick, you must

[043-201] borrow the money of me (never breathing the least allusion to that

[043-202] circumstance), and leave the calculation to him.”

[043-203] “My dear Jarndyce,” returned Mr. Skimpole, “I will do anything to

[043-204] give you pleasure, but it seems an idle form—a superstition.

[043-205] Besides, I give you my word, Miss Clare and my dear Miss Summerson, I

[043-206] thought Mr. Carstone was immensely rich. I thought he had only to

[043-207] make over something, or to sign a bond, or a draft, or a cheque, or a

[043-208] bill, or to put something on a file somewhere, to bring down a shower

[043-209] of money.”

[043-210] “Indeed it is not so, sir,” said Ada. “He is poor.”

[043-211] “No, really?” returned Mr. Skimpole with his bright smile. “You

[043-212] surprise me.”

[043-213] “And not being the richer for trusting in a rotten reed,” said my

[043-214] guardian, laying his hand emphatically on the sleeve of Mr.

[043-215] Skimpole’s dressing-gown, “be you very careful not to encourage him

[043-216] in that reliance, Harold.”

[043-217] “My dear good friend,” returned Mr. Skimpole, “and my dear Miss

[043-218] Simmerson, and my dear Miss Clare, how can I do that? It’s business,

[043-219] and I don’t know business. It is he who encourages me. He emerges

[043-220] from great feats of business, presents the brightest prospects before

[043-221] me as their result, and calls upon me to admire them. I do admire

[043-222] them—as bright prospects. But I know no more about them, and I tell

[043-223] him so.”

[043-224] The helpless kind of candour with which he presented this before us,

[043-225] the light-hearted manner in which he was amused by his innocence, the

[043-226] fantastic way in which he took himself under his own protection and

[043-227] argued about that curious person, combined with the delightful ease

[043-228] of everything he said exactly to make out my guardian’s case. The

[043-229] more I saw of him, the more unlikely it seemed to me, when he was

[043-230] present, that he could design, conceal, or influence anything; and

[043-231] yet the less likely that appeared when he was not present, and the

[043-232] less agreeable it was to think of his having anything to do with any

[043-233] one for whom I cared.

[043-234] Hearing that his examination (as he called it) was now over, Mr.

[043-235] Skimpole left the room with a radiant face to fetch his daughters

[043-236] (his sons had run away at various times), leaving my guardian quite

[043-237] delighted by the manner in which he had vindicated his childish

[043-238] character. He soon came back, bringing with him the three young

[043-239] ladies and Mrs. Skimpole, who had once been a beauty but was now a

[043-240] delicate high-nosed invalid suffering under a complication of

[043-241] disorders.

[043-242] “This,” said Mr. Skimpole, “is my Beauty daughter, Arethusa—plays

[043-243] and sings odds and ends like her father. This is my Sentiment

[043-244] daughter, Laura—plays a little but don’t sing. This is my Comedy

[043-245] daughter, Kitty—sings a little but don’t play. We all draw a little

[043-246] and compose a little, and none of us have any idea of time or money.”

[043-247] Mrs. Skimpole sighed, I thought, as if she would have been glad to

[043-248] strike out this item in the family attainments. I also thought that

[043-249] she rather impressed her sigh upon my guardian and that she took

[043-250] every opportunity of throwing in another.

[043-251] “It is pleasant,” said Mr. Skimpole, turning his sprightly eyes from

[043-252] one to the other of us, “and it is whimsically interesting to trace

[043-253] peculiarities in families. In this family we are all children, and I

[043-254] am the youngest.”

[043-255] The daughters, who appeared to be very fond of him, were amused by

[043-256] this droll fact, particularly the Comedy daughter.

[043-257] “My dears, it is true,” said Mr. Skimpole, “is it not? So it is, and

[043-258] so it must be, because like the dogs in the hymn, ‘it is our nature

[043-259] to.’ Now, here is Miss Summerson with a fine administrative capacity

[043-260] and a knowledge of details perfectly surprising. It will sound very

[043-261] strange in Miss Summerson’s ears, I dare say, that we know nothing

[043-262] about chops in this house. But we don’t, not the least. We can’t cook

[043-263] anything whatever. A needle and thread we don’t know how to use. We

[043-264] admire the people who possess the practical wisdom we want, but we

[043-265] don’t quarrel with them. Then why should they quarrel with us? Live

[043-266] and let live, we say to them. Live upon your practical wisdom, and

[043-267] let us live upon you!”

[043-268] He laughed, but as usual seemed quite candid and really to mean what

[043-269] he said.

[043-270] “We have sympathy, my roses,” said Mr. Skimpole, “sympathy for

[043-271] everything. Have we not?”

[043-272] “Oh, yes, papa!” cried the three daughters.

[043-273] “In fact, that is our family department,” said Mr. Skimpole, “in this

[043-274] hurly-burly of life. We are capable of looking on and of being

[043-275] interested, and we DO look on, and we ARE interested. What more can

[043-276] we do? Here is my Beauty daughter, married these three years. Now I

[043-277] dare say her marrying another child, and having two more, was all

[043-278] wrong in point of political economy, but it was very agreeable. We

[043-279] had our little festivities on those occasions and exchanged social

[043-280] ideas. She brought her young husband home one day, and they and their

[043-281] young fledglings have their nest upstairs. I dare say at some time or

[043-282] other Sentiment and Comedy will bring THEIR husbands home and have

[043-283] THEIR nests upstairs too. So we get on, we don’t know how, but

[043-284] somehow.”

[043-285] She looked very young indeed to be the mother of two children, and I

[043-286] could not help pitying both her and them. It was evident that the

[043-287] three daughters had grown up as they could and had had just as little

[043-288] haphazard instruction as qualified them to be their father’s

[043-289] playthings in his idlest hours. His pictorial tastes were consulted,

[043-290] I observed, in their respective styles of wearing their hair, the

[043-291] Beauty daughter being in the classic manner, the Sentiment daughter

[043-292] luxuriant and flowing, and the Comedy daughter in the arch style,

[043-293] with a good deal of sprightly forehead, and vivacious little curls

[043-294] dotted about the corners of her eyes. They were dressed to

[043-295] correspond, though in a most untidy and negligent way.

[043-296] Ada and I conversed with these young ladies and found them

[043-297] wonderfully like their father. In the meanwhile Mr. Jarndyce (who had

[043-298] been rubbing his head to a great extent, and hinted at a change in

[043-299] the wind) talked with Mrs. Skimpole in a corner, where we could not

[043-300] help hearing the chink of money. Mr. Skimpole had previously

[043-301] volunteered to go home with us and had withdrawn to dress himself for

[043-302] the purpose.

[043-303] “My roses,” he said when he came back, “take care of mama. She is

[043-304] poorly to-day. By going home with Mr. Jarndyce for a day or two, I

[043-305] shall hear the larks sing and preserve my amiability. It has been

[043-306] tried, you know, and would be tried again if I remained at home.”

[043-307] “That bad man!” said the Comedy daughter.

[043-308] “At the very time when he knew papa was lying ill by his wallflowers,

[043-309] looking at the blue sky,” Laura complained.

[043-310] “And when the smell of hay was in the air!” said Arethusa.

[043-311] “It showed a want of poetry in the man,” Mr. Skimpole assented, but

[043-312] with perfect good humour. “It was coarse. There was an absence of the

[043-313] finer touches of humanity in it! My daughters have taken great

[043-314] offence,” he explained to us, “at an honest man—”

[043-315] “Not honest, papa. Impossible!” they all three protested.

[043-316] “At a rough kind of fellow—a sort of human hedgehog rolled up,” said

[043-317] Mr. Skimpole, “who is a baker in this neighbourhood and from whom we

[043-318] borrowed a couple of arm-chairs. We wanted a couple of arm-chairs,

[043-319] and we hadn’t got them, and therefore of course we looked to a man

[043-320] who HAD got them, to lend them. Well! This morose person lent them,

[043-321] and we wore them out. When they were worn out, he wanted them back.

[043-322] He had them back. He was contented, you will say. Not at all. He

[043-323] objected to their being worn. I reasoned with him, and pointed out

[043-324] his mistake. I said, ‘Can you, at your time of life, be so

[043-325] headstrong, my friend, as to persist that an arm-chair is a thing to

[043-326] put upon a shelf and look at? That it is an object to contemplate, to

[043-327] survey from a distance, to consider from a point of sight? Don’t you

[043-328] KNOW that these arm-chairs were borrowed to be sat upon?’ He was

[043-329] unreasonable and unpersuadable and used intemperate language. Being

[043-330] as patient as I am at this minute, I addressed another appeal to him.

[043-331] I said, ‘Now, my good man, however our business capacities may vary,

[043-332] we are all children of one great mother, Nature. On this blooming

[043-333] summer morning here you see me’ (I was on the sofa) ‘with flowers

[043-334] before me, fruit upon the table, the cloudless sky above me, the air

[043-335] full of fragrance, contemplating Nature. I entreat you, by our common

[043-336] brotherhood, not to interpose between me and a subject so sublime,

[043-337] the absurd figure of an angry baker!’ But he did,” said Mr. Skimpole,

[043-338] raising his laughing eyes in playful astonishment; “he did interpose

[043-339] that ridiculous figure, and he does, and he will again. And therefore

[043-340] I am very glad to get out of his way and to go home with my friend

[043-341] Jarndyce.”

[043-342] It seemed to escape his consideration that Mrs. Skimpole and the

[043-343] daughters remained behind to encounter the baker, but this was so old

[043-344] a story to all of them that it had become a matter of course. He took

[043-345] leave of his family with a tenderness as airy and graceful as any

[043-346] other aspect in which he showed himself and rode away with us in

[043-347] perfect harmony of mind. We had an opportunity of seeing through some

[043-348] open doors, as we went downstairs, that his own apartment was a

[043-349] palace to the rest of the house.

[043-350] I could have no anticipation, and I had none, that something very

[043-351] startling to me at the moment, and ever memorable to me in what

[043-352] ensued from it, was to happen before this day was out. Our guest was

[043-353] in such spirits on the way home that I could do nothing but listen to

[043-354] him and wonder at him; nor was I alone in this, for Ada yielded to

[043-355] the same fascination. As to my guardian, the wind, which had

[043-356] threatened to become fixed in the east when we left Somers Town,

[043-357] veered completely round before we were a couple of miles from it.

[043-358] Whether of questionable childishness or not in any other matters, Mr.

[043-359] Skimpole had a child’s enjoyment of change and bright weather. In no

[043-360] way wearied by his sallies on the road, he was in the drawing-room

[043-361] before any of us; and I heard him at the piano while I was yet

[043-362] looking after my housekeeping, singing refrains of barcaroles and

[043-363] drinking songs, Italian and German, by the score.

[043-364] We were all assembled shortly before dinner, and he was still at the

[043-365] piano idly picking out in his luxurious way little strains of music,

[043-366] and talking between whiles of finishing some sketches of the ruined

[043-367] old Verulam wall to-morrow, which he had begun a year or two ago and
