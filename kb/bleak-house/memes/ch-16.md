[016-001] My [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]] is restless, very restless. The astonished

[016-002] fashionable intelligence hardly knows where to have her. To-day she

[016-003] is at [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]]; yesterday she was at her house in town; to-morrow

[016-004] she may be abroad, for anything the fashionable intelligence can with

[016-005] confidence predict. Even [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester]]’s gallantry has some trouble

[016-006] to keep pace with her. It would have more but that his other faithful

[016-007] ally, for better and for worse—the gout—darts into the old oak

[016-008] bed-chamber at Chesney Wold and grips him by both legs.

[016-009] Sir Leicester receives the gout as a troublesome demon, but still a

[016-010] demon of the patrician order. All the Dedlocks, in the direct male

[016-011] line, through a course of time during and beyond which the memory of

[016-012] man goeth not to the contrary, have had the gout. It can be proved,

[016-013] sir. Other men’s fathers may have died of the rheumatism or may have

[016-014] taken base contagion from the tainted blood of the sick vulgar, but

[016-015] the Dedlock family have communicated something exclusive even to the

[016-016] levelling process of dying by dying of their own family gout. It has

[016-017] come down through the illustrious line like the plate, or the

[016-018] pictures, or the place in Lincolnshire. It is among their dignities.

[016-019] Sir Leicester is perhaps not wholly without an impression, though he

[016-020] has never resolved it into words, that the angel of death in the

[016-021] discharge of his necessary duties may observe to the shades of the

[016-022] aristocracy, “My lords and gentlemen, I have the honour to present to

[016-023] you another Dedlock certified to have arrived per the family gout.”

[016-024] Hence Sir Leicester yields up his family legs to the family disorder

[016-025] as if he held his name and fortune on that feudal tenure. He feels

[016-026] that for a Dedlock to be laid upon his back and spasmodically

[016-027] twitched and stabbed in his extremities is a liberty taken somewhere,

[016-028] but he thinks, “We have all yielded to this; it belongs to us; it has

[016-029] for some hundreds of years been understood that we are not to make

[016-030] the vaults in the park interesting on more ignoble terms; and I

[016-031] submit myself to the compromise.”

[016-032] And a goodly show he makes, lying in a flush of crimson and gold in

[016-033] the midst of the great drawing-room before his favourite picture of

[016-034] my Lady, with broad strips of sunlight shining in, down the long

[016-035] perspective, through the long line of windows, and alternating with

[016-036] soft reliefs of shadow. Outside, the stately oaks, rooted for ages in

[016-037] the green ground which has never known ploughshare, but was still a

[016-038] chase when kings rode to battle with sword and shield and rode

[016-039] a-hunting with bow and arrow, bear witness to his greatness. Inside,

[016-040] his forefathers, looking on him from the walls, say, “Each of us was

[016-041] a passing reality here and left this coloured shadow of himself and

[016-042] melted into remembrance as dreamy as the distant voices of the rooks

[016-043] now lulling you to rest,” and bear their testimony to his greatness

[016-044] too. And he is very great this day. And woe to Boythorn or other

[016-045] daring wight who shall presumptuously contest an inch with him!

[016-046] My Lady is at present represented, near Sir Leicester, by her

[016-047] portrait. She has flitted away to town, with no intention of

[016-048] remaining there, and will soon flit hither again, to the confusion of

[016-049] the fashionable intelligence. The house in town is not prepared for

[016-050] her reception. It is muffled and dreary. Only one Mercury in powder

[016-051] gapes disconsolate at the hall-window; and he mentioned last night to

[016-052] another Mercury of his acquaintance, also accustomed to good society,

[016-053] that if that sort of thing was to last—which it couldn’t, for a man

[016-054] of his spirits couldn’t bear it, and a man of his figure couldn’t be

[016-055] expected to bear it—there would be no resource for him, upon his

[016-056] honour, but to cut his throat!

[016-057] What connexion can there be between the place in Lincolnshire, the

[016-058] house in town, the Mercury in powder, and the whereabout of [[jo-crossing-sweeper|Jo]] the

[016-059] outlaw with the broom, who had that distant ray of light upon him

[016-060] when he swept the churchyard-step? What connexion can there have been

[016-061] between many people in the innumerable histories of this world who

[016-062] from opposite sides of great gulfs have, nevertheless, been very

[016-063] curiously brought together!

[016-064] Jo sweeps his crossing all day long, unconscious of the link, if any

[016-065] link there be. He sums up his mental condition when asked a question

[016-066] by replying that he “don’t know nothink.” He knows that it’s hard to

[016-067] keep the mud off the crossing in dirty weather, and harder still to

[016-068] live by doing it. Nobody taught him even that much; he found it out.

[016-069] Jo lives—that is to say, Jo has not yet died—in a ruinous place

[016-070] known to the like of him by the name of Tom-all-Alone’s. It is a

[016-071] black, dilapidated street, avoided by all decent people, where the

[016-072] crazy houses were seized upon, when their [[ruin-and-decay|decay]] was far advanced, by

[016-073] some bold vagrants who after establishing their own possession took

[016-074] to letting them out in lodgings. Now, these tumbling tenements

[016-075] contain, by night, a swarm of misery. As on the ruined human wretch

[016-076] vermin parasites appear, so these ruined shelters have bred a crowd

[016-077] of foul existence that crawls in and out of gaps in walls and boards;

[016-078] and coils itself to sleep, in maggot numbers, where the rain drips

[016-079] in; and comes and goes, fetching and carrying fever and sowing more

[016-080] evil in its every footprint than Lord Coodle, and Sir Thomas Doodle,

[016-081] and the Duke of Foodle, and all the fine gentlemen in office, down to

[016-082] Zoodle, shall set right in five hundred years—though born expressly

[016-083] to do it.

[016-084] Twice lately there has been a crash and a cloud of dust, like the

[016-085] springing of a mine, in Tom-all-Alone’s; and each time a house has

[016-086] fallen. These accidents have made a paragraph in the newspapers and

[016-087] have filled a bed or two in the nearest hospital. The gaps remain,

[016-088] and there are not unpopular lodgings among the rubbish. As several

[016-089] more houses are nearly ready to go, the next crash in Tom-all-Alone’s

[016-090] may be expected to be a good one.

[016-091] This desirable [[inheritance-and-property|property]] is in [[chancery-court|Chancery]], of course. It would be an

[016-092] insult to the discernment of any man with half an eye to tell him so.

[016-093] Whether “Tom” is the popular representative of the original plaintiff

[016-094] or defendant in [[jarndyce-and-jarndyce|Jarndyce and Jarndyce]], or whether Tom lived here when

[016-095] the suit had laid the street waste, all alone, until other settlers

[016-096] came to join him, or whether the traditional title is a comprehensive

[016-097] name for a retreat cut off from honest company and put out of the

[016-098] pale of hope, perhaps nobody knows. Certainly Jo don’t know.

[016-099] “For I don’t,” says Jo, “I don’t know nothink.”

[016-100] It must be a strange state to be like Jo! To shuffle through the

[016-101] streets, unfamiliar with the shapes, and in utter darkness as to the

[016-102] meaning, of those mysterious symbols, so abundant over the shops, and

[016-103] at the corners of streets, and on the doors, and in the windows! To

[016-104] see people read, and to see people write, and to see the postmen

[016-105] deliver [[document-and-letter|letters]], and not to have the least idea of all that

[016-106] language—to be, to every scrap of it, stone blind and dumb! It must

[016-107] be very puzzling to see the good company going to the churches on

[016-108] Sundays, with their books in their hands, and to think (for perhaps

[016-109] Jo DOES think at odd times) what does it all mean, and if it means

[016-110] anything to anybody, how comes it that it means nothing to me? To be

[016-111] hustled, and jostled, and moved on; and really to feel that it would

[016-112] appear to be perfectly true that I have no business here, or there,

[016-113] or anywhere; and yet to be perplexed by the consideration that I AM

[016-114] here somehow, too, and everybody overlooked me until I became the

[016-115] creature that I am! It must be a strange state, not merely to be told

[016-116] that I am scarcely human (as in the case of my offering myself for a

[016-117] witness), but to feel it of my own knowledge all my life! To see the

[016-118] horses, dogs, and cattle go by me and to know that in ignorance I

[016-119] belong to them and not to the superior beings in my shape, whose

[016-120] delicacy I offend! Jo’s ideas of a criminal trial, or a judge, or a

[016-121] bishop, or a government, or that inestimable jewel to him (if he only

[016-122] knew it) the Constitution, should be strange! His whole material and

[016-123] immaterial life is wonderfully strange; his death, the strangest

[016-124] thing of all.

[016-125] Jo comes out of Tom-all-Alone’s, meeting the tardy morning which is

[016-126] always late in getting down there, and munches his dirty bit of bread

[016-127] as he comes along. His way lying through many streets, and the houses

[016-128] not yet being open, he sits down to breakfast on the door-step of the

[016-129] Society for the Propagation of the Gospel in Foreign Parts and gives

[016-130] it a brush when he has finished as an acknowledgment of the

[016-131] accommodation. He admires the size of the edifice and wonders what

[016-132] it’s all about. He has no idea, poor wretch, of the spiritual

[016-133] destitution of a coral reef in the Pacific or what it costs to look

[016-134] up the precious souls among the coco-nuts and bread-fruit.

[016-135] He goes to his crossing and begins to lay it out for the day. The

[016-136] town awakes; the great tee-totum is set up for its daily spin and

[016-137] whirl; all that unaccountable reading and writing, which has been

[016-138] suspended for a few hours, recommences. Jo and the other lower

[016-139] animals get on in the unintelligible mess as they can. It is

[016-140] market-day. The blinded oxen, over-goaded, over-driven, never guided,

[016-141] run into wrong places and are beaten out, and plunge red-eyed and

[016-142] foaming at stone walls, and often sorely hurt the innocent, and often

[016-143] sorely hurt themselves. Very like Jo and his order; very, very like!

[016-144] A band of music comes and plays. Jo listens to it. So does a dog—a

[016-145] drover’s dog, waiting for his master outside a butcher’s shop, and

[016-146] evidently thinking about those sheep he has had upon his mind for

[016-147] some hours and is happily rid of. He seems perplexed respecting three

[016-148] or four, can’t remember where he left them, looks up and down the

[016-149] street as half expecting to see them astray, suddenly pricks up his

[016-150] ears and remembers all about it. A thoroughly vagabond dog,

[016-151] accustomed to low company and public-houses; a terrific dog to sheep,

[016-152] ready at a whistle to scamper over their backs and tear out mouthfuls

[016-153] of their wool; but an educated, improved, developed dog who has been

[016-154] taught his duties and knows how to discharge them. He and Jo listen

[016-155] to the music, probably with much the same amount of animal

[016-156] satisfaction; likewise as to awakened association, aspiration, or

[016-157] regret, melancholy or joyful reference to things beyond the senses,

[016-158] they are probably upon a par. But, otherwise, how far above the human

[016-159] listener is the brute!

[016-160] Turn that dog’s descendants wild, like Jo, and in a very few years

[016-161] they will so degenerate that they will lose even their bark—but not

[016-162] their bite.

[016-163] The day changes as it wears itself away and becomes dark and drizzly.

[016-164] Jo fights it out at his crossing among the mud and wheels, the

[016-165] horses, whips, and umbrellas, and gets but a scanty sum to pay for

[016-166] the unsavoury shelter of Tom-all-Alone’s. Twilight comes on; gas

[016-167] begins to start up in the shops; the lamplighter, with his ladder,

[016-168] runs along the margin of the pavement. A wretched evening is

[016-169] beginning to close in.

[016-170] In his chambers [[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]] sits meditating an application to the

[016-171] nearest magistrate to-morrow morning for a warrant. Gridley, a

[016-172] disappointed suitor, has been here to-day and has been alarming. We

[016-173] are not to be put in bodily fear, and that ill-conditioned fellow

[016-174] shall be held to bail again. From the ceiling, foreshortened

[016-175] Allegory, in the person of one impossible Roman upside down, points

[016-176] with the arm of Samson (out of joint, and an odd one) obtrusively

[016-177] toward the window. Why should Mr. Tulkinghorn, for such no reason,

[016-178] look out of window? Is the hand not always pointing there? So he does

[016-179] not look out of window.

[016-180] And if he did, what would it be to see a woman going by? There are

[016-181] women enough in the world, Mr. Tulkinghorn thinks—too many; they are

[016-182] at the bottom of all that goes wrong in it, though, for the matter of

[016-183] that, they create business for lawyers. What would it be to see a

[016-184] woman going by, even though she were going secretly? They are all

[016-185] secret. Mr. Tulkinghorn knows that very well.

[016-186] But they are not all like the woman who now leaves him and his house

[016-187] behind, between whose plain dress and her refined manner there is

[016-188] something exceedingly inconsistent. She should be an upper servant by

[016-189] her attire, yet in her air and step, though both are hurried and

[016-190] assumed—as far as she can assume in the muddy streets, which she

[016-191] treads with an unaccustomed foot—she is a lady. Her face is veiled,

[016-192] and still she sufficiently betrays herself to make more than one of

[016-193] those who pass her look round sharply.

[016-194] She never turns her head. Lady or servant, she has a purpose in her

[016-195] and can follow it. She never turns her head until she comes to the

[016-196] crossing where Jo plies with his broom. He crosses with her and begs.

[016-197] Still, she does not turn her head until she has landed on the other

[016-198] side. Then she slightly beckons to him and says, “Come here!”

[016-199] Jo follows her a pace or two into a quiet court.

[016-200] “Are you the boy I’ve read of in the papers?” she asked behind her

[016-201] veil.

[016-202] “I don’t know,” says Jo, staring moodily at the veil, “nothink about

[016-203] no papers. I don’t know nothink about nothink at all.”

[016-204] “Were you examined at an inquest?”

[016-205] “I don’t know nothink about no—where I was took by the beadle, do

[016-206] you mean?” says Jo. “Was the boy’s name at the inkwhich Jo?”

[016-207] “Yes.”

[016-208] “That’s me!” says Jo.

[016-209] “Come farther up.”

[016-210] “You mean about the man?” says Jo, following. “Him as wos dead?”

[016-211] “Hush! Speak in a whisper! Yes. Did he look, when he was living, so

[016-212] very ill and poor?”

[016-213] “Oh, jist!” says Jo.

[016-214] “Did he look like—not like YOU?” says the woman with abhorrence.

[016-215] “Oh, not so bad as me,” says Jo. “I’m a reg’lar one I am! You didn’t

[016-216] know him, did you?”

[016-217] “How dare you ask me if I knew him?”

[016-218] “No offence, my lady,” says Jo with much humility, for even he has

[016-219] got at the suspicion of her being a lady.

[016-220] “I am not a lady. I am a servant.”

[016-221] “You are a jolly servant!” says Jo without the least idea of saying

[016-222] anything offensive, merely as a tribute of admiration.

[016-223] “Listen and be silent. Don’t talk to me, and stand farther from me!

[016-224] Can you show me all those places that were spoken of in the account I

[016-225] read? The place he wrote for, the place he died at, the place where

[016-226] you were taken to, and the place where he was buried? Do you know the

[016-227] place where he was buried?”

[016-228] Jo answers with a nod, having also nodded as each other place was

[016-229] mentioned.

[016-230] “Go before me and show me all those dreadful places. Stop opposite to

[016-231] each, and don’t speak to me unless I speak to you. Don’t look back.

[016-232] Do what I want, and I will pay you well.”

[016-233] Jo attends closely while the words are being spoken; tells them off

[016-234] on his broom-handle, finding them rather hard; pauses to consider

[016-235] their meaning; considers it satisfactory; and nods his ragged head.

[016-236] “I’m fly,” says Jo. “But fen larks, you know. Stow hooking it!”

[016-237] “What does the horrible creature mean?” exclaims the servant,

[016-238] recoiling from him.

[016-239] “Stow cutting away, you know!” says Jo.

[016-240] “I don’t understand you. Go on before! I will give you more money

[016-241] than you ever had in your life.”

[016-242] Jo screws up his mouth into a whistle, gives his ragged head a rub,

[016-243] takes his broom under his arm, and leads the way, passing deftly with

[016-244] his bare feet over the hard stones and through the mud and mire.

[016-245] Cook’s Court. Jo stops. A pause.

[016-246] “Who lives here?”

[016-247] “Him wot give him his writing and give me half a bull,” says Jo in a

[016-248] whisper without looking over his shoulder.

[016-249] “Go on to the next.”

[016-250] [[krook|Krook]]’s house. Jo stops again. A longer pause.

[016-251] “Who lives here?”

[016-252] “HE lived here,” Jo answers as before.

[016-253] After a silence he is asked, “In which room?”

[016-254] “In the back room up there. You can see the winder from this corner.

[016-255] Up there! That’s where I see him stritched out. This is the

[016-256] public-ouse where I was took to.”

[016-257] “Go on to the next!”

[016-258] It is a longer walk to the next, but Jo, relieved of his first

[016-259] suspicions, sticks to the forms imposed upon him and does not look

[016-260] round. By many devious ways, reeking with offence of many kinds, they

[016-261] come to the little tunnel of a court, and to the gas-lamp (lighted

[016-262] now), and to the iron gate.

[016-263] “He was put there,” says Jo, holding to the bars and looking in.

[016-264] “Where? Oh, what a scene of horror!”

[016-265] “There!” says Jo, pointing. “Over yinder. Among them piles of bones,

[016-266] and close to that there kitchin winder! They put him wery nigh the

[016-267] top. They was obliged to stamp upon it to git it in. I could unkiver

[016-268] it for you with my broom if the gate was open. That’s why they locks

[016-269] it, I s’pose,” giving it a shake. “It’s always locked. Look at the

[016-270] rat!” cries Jo, excited. “Hi! Look! There he goes! Ho! Into the

[016-271] ground!”

[016-272] The servant shrinks into a corner, into a corner of that hideous

[016-273] archway, with its deadly stains contaminating her dress; and putting

[016-274] out her two hands and passionately telling him to keep away from her,

[016-275] for he is loathsome to her, so remains for some moments. Jo stands

[016-276] staring and is still staring when she recovers herself.

[016-277] “Is this place of abomination consecrated ground?”

[016-278] “I don’t know nothink of consequential ground,” says Jo, still

[016-279] staring.

[016-280] “Is it blessed?”

[016-281] “Which?” says Jo, in the last degree amazed.

[016-282] “Is it blessed?”

[016-283] “I’m blest if I know,” says Jo, staring more than ever; “but I

[016-284] shouldn’t think it warn’t. Blest?” repeats Jo, something troubled in

[016-285] his mind. “It an’t done it much good if it is. Blest? I should think

[016-286] it was t’othered myself. But I don’t know nothink!”

[016-287] The servant takes as little heed of what he says as she seems to take

[016-288] of what she has said herself. She draws off her glove to get some

[016-289] money from her purse. Jo silently notices how white and small her

[016-290] hand is and what a jolly servant she must be to wear such sparkling

[016-291] rings.

[016-292] She drops a piece of money in his hand without touching it, and

[016-293] shuddering as their hands approach. “Now,” she adds, “show me the

[016-294] spot again!”

[016-295] Jo thrusts the handle of his broom between the bars of the gate, and

[016-296] with his utmost power of elaboration, points it out. At length,

[016-297] looking aside to see if he has made himself intelligible, he finds

[016-298] that he is alone.

[016-299] His first proceeding is to hold the piece of money to the gas-light

[016-300] and to be overpowered at finding that it is yellow—gold. His next is

[016-301] to give it a one-sided bite at the edge as a test of its quality.

[016-302] His next, to put it in his mouth for safety and to sweep the

[016-303] step and passage with great care. His job done, he sets off for

[016-304] Tom-all-Alone’s, stopping in the light of innumerable gas-lamps to

[016-305] produce the piece of gold and give it another one-sided bite as a

[016-306] reassurance of its being genuine.

[016-307] The Mercury in powder is in no want of society to-night, for my Lady

[016-308] goes to a grand dinner and three or four balls. Sir Leicester is

[016-309] fidgety down at Chesney Wold, with no better company than the gout;

[016-310] he complains to Mrs. Rouncewell that the rain makes such a monotonous

[016-311] pattering on the terrace that he can’t read the paper even by the

[016-312] fireside in his own snug dressing-room.

[016-313] “Sir Leicester would have done better to try the other side of the

[016-314] house, my dear,” says Mrs. Rouncewell to Rosa. “His dressing-room is

[016-315] on my Lady’s side. And in all these years I never heard the step upon

[016-316] the Ghost’s Walk more distinct than it is to-night!”
