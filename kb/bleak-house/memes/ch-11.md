[011-001] A touch on the lawyer’s wrinkled hand as he stands in the dark room,

[011-002] irresolute, makes him start and say, “What’s that?”

[011-003] “It’s me,” returns the old man of the house, whose breath is in his

[011-004] ear. “Can’t you wake him?”

[011-005] “No.”

[011-006] “What have you done with your candle?”

[011-007] “It’s gone out. Here it is.”

[011-008] [[krook|Krook]] takes it, goes to the fire, stoops over the red embers, and

[011-009] tries to get a light. The dying ashes have no light to spare, and his

[011-010] endeavours are vain. Muttering, after an ineffectual call to his

[011-011] lodger, that he will go downstairs and bring a lighted candle from

[011-012] the shop, the old man departs. [[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]], for some new reason

[011-013] that he has, does not await his return in the room, but on the stairs

[011-014] outside.

[011-015] The welcome light soon shines upon the wall, as Krook comes slowly up

[011-016] with his green-eyed cat following at his heels. “Does the man

[011-017] generally sleep like this?” inquired the lawyer in a low voice. “Hi!

[011-018] I don’t know,” says Krook, shaking his head and lifting his eyebrows.

[011-019] “I know next to nothing of his habits except that he keeps himself

[011-020] very close.”

[011-021] Thus whispering, they both go in together. As the light goes in, the

[011-022] great eyes in the shutters, darkening, seem to close. Not so the eyes

[011-023] upon the bed.

[011-024] “God save us!” exclaims Mr. Tulkinghorn. “He is dead!” Krook drops

[011-025] the heavy hand he has taken up so suddenly that the arm swings over

[011-026] the bedside.

[011-027] They look at one another for a moment.

[011-028] “Send for some doctor! Call for [[miss-flite|Miss Flite]] up the stairs, sir. Here’s

[011-029] poison by the bed! Call out for Flite, will you?” says Krook, with

[011-030] his lean hands spread out above the body like a vampire’s wings.

[011-031] Mr. Tulkinghorn hurries to the landing and calls, “Miss Flite! Flite!

[011-032] Make haste, here, whoever you are! Flite!” Krook follows him with his

[011-033] eyes, and while he is calling, finds opportunity to steal to the old

[011-034] portmanteau and steal back again.

[011-035] “Run, Flite, run! The nearest doctor! Run!” So Mr. Krook addresses a

[011-036] crazy little woman who is his female lodger, who appears and vanishes

[011-037] in a breath, who soon returns accompanied by a testy medical man

[011-038] brought from his dinner, with a broad, snuffy upper lip and a broad

[011-039] Scotch tongue.

[011-040] “Ey! Bless the hearts o’ ye,” says the medical man, looking up at

[011-041] them after a moment’s examination. “He’s just as dead as Phairy!”

[011-042] Mr. Tulkinghorn (standing by the old portmanteau) inquires if he has

[011-043] been dead any time.

[011-044] “Any time, sir?” says the medical gentleman. “It’s probable he wull

[011-045] have been dead aboot three hours.”

[011-046] “[[About]] that time, I should say,” observes a dark young man on the

[011-047] other side of the bed.

[011-048] “Air you in the maydickle prayfession yourself, sir?” inquires the

[011-049] first.

[011-050] The dark young man says yes.

[011-051] “Then I’ll just tak’ my depairture,” replies the other, “for I’m nae

[011-052] gude here!” With which remark he finishes his brief attendance and

[011-053] returns to finish his dinner.

[011-054] The dark young surgeon passes the candle across and across the face

[011-055] and carefully examines the law-writer, who has established his

[011-056] pretensions to his name by becoming indeed No one.

[011-057] “I knew this person by sight very well,” says he. “He has purchased

[011-058] opium of me for the last year and a half. Was anybody present related

[011-059] to him?” glancing round upon the three bystanders.

[011-060] “I was his landlord,” grimly answers Krook, taking the candle from

[011-061] the surgeon’s outstretched hand. “He told me once I was the nearest

[011-062] relation he had.”

[011-063] “He has died,” says the surgeon, “of an over-dose of opium, there is

[011-064] no doubt. The room is strongly flavoured with it. There is enough

[011-065] here now,” taking an old tea-pot from Mr. Krook, “to kill a dozen

[011-066] people.”

[011-067] “Do you think he did it on purpose?” asks Krook.

[011-068] “Took the over-dose?”

[011-069] “Yes!” Krook almost smacks his lips with the unction of a horrible

[011-070] interest.

[011-071] “I can’t say. I should think it unlikely, as he has been in the habit

[011-072] of taking so much. But nobody can tell. He was very poor, I suppose?”

[011-073] “I suppose he was. His room—don’t look rich,” says Krook, who might

[011-074] have changed eyes with his cat, as he casts his sharp glance around.

[011-075] “But I have never been in it since he had it, and he was too close to

[011-076] name his circumstances to me.”

[011-077] “Did he owe you any rent?”

[011-078] “Six weeks.”

[011-079] “He will never pay it!” says the young man, resuming his examination.

[011-080] “It is beyond a doubt that he is indeed as dead as Pharaoh; and to

[011-081] judge from his appearance and condition, I should think it a happy

[011-082] release. Yet he must have been a good figure when a youth, and I dare

[011-083] say, good-looking.” He says this, not unfeelingly, while sitting on

[011-084] the bedstead’s edge with his face towards that other face and his

[011-085] hand upon the region of the heart. “I recollect once thinking there

[011-086] was something in his manner, uncouth as it was, that denoted a fall

[011-087] in life. Was that so?” he continues, looking round.

[011-088] Krook replies, “You might as well ask me to describe the ladies whose

[011-089] heads of hair I have got in sacks downstairs. Than that he was my

[011-090] lodger for a year and a half and lived—or didn’t live—by

[011-091] law-[[document-and-letter|writing]], I know no more of him.”

[011-092] During this dialogue Mr. Tulkinghorn has stood aloof by the old

[011-093] portmanteau, with his hands behind him, equally removed, to all

[011-094] appearance, from all three kinds of interest exhibited near the

[011-095] bed—from the young surgeon’s professional interest in death,

[011-096] noticeable as being quite apart from his remarks on the deceased as

[011-097] an individual; from the old man’s unction; and the little crazy

[011-098] woman’s awe. His imperturbable face has been as inexpressive as his

[011-099] rusty clothes. One could not even say he has been thinking all this

[011-100] while. He has shown neither patience nor impatience, nor attention

[011-101] nor abstraction. He has shown nothing but his shell. As easily might

[011-102] the tone of a delicate musical instrument be inferred from its case,

[011-103] as the tone of Mr. Tulkinghorn from his case.

[011-104] He now interposes, addressing the young surgeon in his unmoved,

[011-105] professional way.

[011-106] “I looked in here,” he observes, “just before you, with the

[011-107] intention of giving this deceased man, whom I never saw alive, some

[011-108] employment at his trade of copying. I had heard of him from my

[011-109] stationer—Snagsby of Cook’s Court. Since no one here knows anything

[011-110] about him, it might be as well to send for Snagsby. Ah!” to the

[011-111] little crazy woman, who has often seen him in court, and whom he has

[011-112] often seen, and who proposes, in frightened dumb-show, to go for the

[011-113] law-stationer. “Suppose you do!”

[011-114] While she is gone, the surgeon abandons his hopeless investigation

[011-115] and covers its subject with the patchwork counterpane. Mr. Krook and

[011-116] he interchange a word or two. Mr. Tulkinghorn says nothing, but

[011-117] stands, ever, near the old portmanteau.

[011-118] Mr. Snagsby arrives hastily in his grey coat and his black sleeves.

[011-119] “Dear me, dear me,” he says; “and it has come to this, has it! Bless

[011-120] my soul!”

[011-121] “Can you give the person of the house any information about this

[011-122] unfortunate creature, Snagsby?” inquires Mr. Tulkinghorn. “He was in

[011-123] arrears with his rent, it seems. And he must be buried, you know.”

[011-124] “Well, sir,” says Mr. Snagsby, coughing his apologetic cough behind

[011-125] his hand, “I really don’t know what advice I could offer, except

[011-126] sending for the beadle.”

[011-127] “I don’t speak of advice,” returns Mr. Tulkinghorn. “I could

[011-128] advise—”

[011-129] “No one better, sir, I am sure,” says Mr. Snagsby, with his

[011-130] deferential cough.

[011-131] “I speak of affording some clue to his connexions, or to where he

[011-132] came from, or to anything concerning him.”

[011-133] “I assure you, sir,” says Mr. Snagsby after prefacing his reply with

[011-134] his cough of general propitiation, “that I no more know where he came

[011-135] from than I know—”

[011-136] “Where he has gone to, perhaps,” suggests the surgeon to help him

[011-137] out.

[011-138] A pause. Mr. Tulkinghorn looking at the law-stationer. Mr. Krook,

[011-139] with his mouth open, looking for somebody to speak next.

[011-140] “As to his connexions, sir,” says Mr. Snagsby, “if a person was to

[011-141] say to me, ‘Snagsby, here’s twenty thousand pound down, ready for you

[011-142] in the Bank of England if you’ll only name one of ’em,’ I couldn’t do

[011-143] it, sir! About a year and a half ago—to the best of my belief, at

[011-144] the time when he first came to lodge at the present rag and bottle

[011-145] shop—”

[011-146] “That was the time!” says Krook with a nod.

[011-147] “About a year and a half ago,” says Mr. Snagsby, strengthened, “he

[011-148] came into our place one morning after breakfast, and finding my

[011-149] little woman (which I name Mrs. Snagsby when I use that appellation)

[011-173] were in a condition to do it.”

[011-174] “Hadn’t you better see,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn to Krook, “whether he

[011-175] had any papers that may enlighten you? There will be an inquest, and

[011-176] you will be asked the question. You can read?”

[011-177] “No, I can’t,” returns the old man with a sudden grin.

[011-178] “Snagsby,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, “look over the room for him. He will

[011-179] get into some trouble or difficulty otherwise. Being here, I’ll wait

[011-180] if you make haste, and then I can testify on his behalf, if it should

[011-181] ever be necessary, that all was fair and right. If you will hold the

[011-182] candle for Mr. Snagsby, my friend, he’ll soon see whether there is

[011-183] anything to help you.”

[011-184] “In the first place, here’s an old portmanteau, sir,” says Snagsby.

[011-185] Ah, to be sure, so there is! Mr. Tulkinghorn does not appear to have

[011-186] seen it before, though he is standing so close to it, and though

[011-187] there is very little else, heaven knows.

[011-188] The marine-store merchant holds the light, and the law-stationer

[011-189] conducts the search. The surgeon leans against the corner of the

[011-190] chimney-piece; Miss Flite peeps and trembles just within the door.

[011-191] The apt old scholar of the old school, with his dull black breeches

[011-192] tied with ribbons at the knees, his large black waistcoat, his

[011-193] long-sleeved black coat, and his wisp of limp white neckerchief tied

[011-194] in the bow the peerage knows so well, stands in exactly the same

[011-195] place and attitude.

[011-196] There are some worthless articles of clothing in the old portmanteau;

[011-197] there is a bundle of pawnbrokers’ duplicates, those turnpike tickets

[011-198] on the road of poverty; there is a crumpled paper, smelling of opium,

[011-199] on which are scrawled rough memoranda—as, took, such a day, so many

[011-200] grains; took, such another day, so many more—begun some time ago, as

[011-201] if with the intention of being regularly continued, but soon left

[011-202] off. There are a few dirty scraps of newspapers, all referring to

[011-203] coroners’ inquests; there is nothing else. They search the cupboard

[011-204] and the drawer of the ink-splashed table. There is not a morsel of an

[011-205] old letter or of any other writing in either. The young surgeon

[011-206] examines the dress on the law-writer. A knife and some odd halfpence

[011-207] are all he finds. Mr. Snagsby’s suggestion is the practical

[011-208] suggestion after all, and the beadle must be called in.

[011-209] So the little crazy lodger goes for the beadle, and the rest come out

[011-210] of the room. “Don’t leave the cat there!” says the surgeon; “that

[011-211] won’t do!” Mr. Krook therefore drives her out before him, and she

[011-212] goes furtively downstairs, winding her lithe tail and licking her

[011-213] lips.

[011-214] “Good night!” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, and goes home to Allegory and

[011-215] meditation.

[011-217] inhabitants assemble to discuss the thing, and the outposts of the

[011-218] army of observation (principally boys) are pushed forward to Mr.

[011-219] Krook’s window, which they closely invest. A policeman has already

[011-220] walked up to the room, and walked down again to the door, where he

[011-221] stands like a tower, only condescending to see the boys at his base

[011-222] occasionally; but whenever he does see them, they quail and fall

[011-223] back. Mrs. Perkins, who has not been for some weeks on speaking terms

[011-224] with Mrs. Piper in consequence for an unpleasantness originating in

[011-225] young Perkins’ having “fetched” young Piper “a crack,” renews her

[011-226] friendly intercourse on this auspicious occasion. The potboy at the

[011-227] corner, who is a privileged amateur, as possessing official knowledge

[011-228] of life and having to deal with drunken men occasionally, exchanges

[011-229] confidential communications with the policeman and has the appearance

[011-230] of an impregnable youth, unassailable by truncheons and unconfinable

[011-231] in station-houses. People talk across the court out of window, and

[011-232] bare-headed scouts come hurrying in from [[chancery-lane|Chancery Lane]] to know what’s

[011-233] the matter. The general feeling seems to be that it’s a blessing Mr.

[011-234] Krook warn’t made away with first, mingled with a little natural

[011-235] disappointment that he was not. In the midst of this sensation, the

[011-236] beadle arrives.

[011-237] The beadle, though generally understood in the neighbourhood to be a

[011-238] ridiculous institution, is not without a certain popularity for the

[011-239] moment, if it were only as a man who is going to see the body. The

[011-240] policeman considers him an imbecile civilian, a remnant of the

[011-241] barbarous watchmen times, but gives him admission as something that

[011-242] must be borne with until government shall abolish him. The sensation

[011-243] is heightened as the tidings spread from mouth to mouth that the

[011-244] beadle is on the ground and has gone in.

[011-245] By and by the beadle comes out, once more intensifying the sensation,

[011-246] which has rather languished in the interval. He is understood to be

[011-248] coroner and jury anything whatever respecting the deceased. Is

[011-251] Mrs. Green’s son “was a law-writer his-self and knowed him better

[011-258] Policeman seen to smile to potboy. Public loses interest and
