[041-001] [[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]] arrives in his turret-room a little breathed by the

[041-002] journey up, though leisurely performed. There is an expression on his

[041-003] face as if he had discharged his mind of some grave matter and were,

[041-004] in his close way, satisfied. To say of a man so severely and strictly

[041-005] self-repressed that he is triumphant would be to do him as great an

[041-006] injustice as to suppose him troubled with love or sentiment or any

[041-007] romantic weakness. He is sedately satisfied. Perhaps there is a

[041-008] rather increased sense of power upon him as he loosely grasps one of

[041-009] his veinous wrists with his other hand and holding it behind his back

[041-010] walks noiselessly up and down.

[041-011] There is a capacious [[document-and-letter|writing]]-table in the room on which is a pretty

[041-012] large accumulation of papers. The green lamp is lighted, his

[041-013] reading-glasses lie upon the desk, the easy-chair is wheeled up to

[041-014] it, and it would seem as though he had intended to bestow an hour or

[041-015] so upon these claims on his attention before going to bed. But he

[041-016] happens not to be in a business mind. After a glance at the documents

[041-017] awaiting his notice—with his head bent low over the table, the old

[041-018] man’s sight for print or writing being defective at night—he opens

[041-019] the French window and steps out upon the leads. There he again walks

[041-020] slowly up and down in the same attitude, subsiding, if a man so cool

[041-021] may have any need to subside, from the story he has related

[041-022] downstairs.

[041-023] The time was once when men as knowing as Mr. Tulkinghorn would walk

[041-024] on turret-tops in the starlight and look up into the sky to read

[041-025] their fortunes there. Hosts of stars are visible to-night, though

[041-026] their brilliancy is eclipsed by the splendour of the moon. If he be

[041-027] seeking his own star as he methodically turns and turns upon the

[041-028] leads, it should be but a pale one to be so rustily represented

[041-029] below. If he be tracing out his destiny, that may be written in other

[041-030] characters nearer to his hand.

[041-031] As he paces the leads with his eyes most probably as high above his

[041-032] thoughts as they are high above the earth, he is suddenly stopped in

[041-033] passing the window by two eyes that meet his own. The ceiling of his

[041-034] room is rather low; and the upper part of the door, which is opposite

[041-035] the window, is of glass. There is an inner baize door, too, but the

[041-036] night being warm he did not close it when he came upstairs. These

[041-037] eyes that meet his own are looking in through the glass from the

[041-038] corridor outside. He knows them well. The blood has not flushed into

[041-039] his face so suddenly and redly for many a long year as when he

[041-040] recognizes [[lady-dedlock|Lady Dedlock]].

[041-041] He steps into the room, and she comes in too, closing both the doors

[041-042] behind her. There is a wild disturbance—is it fear or anger?—in her

[041-043] eyes. In her carriage and all else she looks as she looked downstairs

[041-044] two hours ago.

[041-045] Is it fear or is it anger now? He cannot be sure. Both might be as

[041-046] pale, both as intent.

[041-047] “Lady Dedlock?”

[041-048] She does not speak at first, nor even when she has slowly dropped

[041-049] into the easy-chair by the table. They look at each other, like two

[041-050] pictures.

[041-051] “Why have you told my story to so many persons?”

[041-052] “Lady Dedlock, it was necessary for me to inform you that I knew it.”

[041-053] “How long have you known it?”

[041-054] “I have suspected it a long while—fully known it a little while.”

[041-055] “Months?”

[041-056] “Days.”

[041-057] He stands before her with one hand on a chair-back and the other in

[041-058] his old-fashioned waistcoat and shirt-frill, exactly as he has stood

[041-059] before her at any time since her marriage. The same formal

[041-060] politeness, the same composed deference that might as well be

[041-061] defiance; the whole man the same dark, cold object, at the same

[041-062] distance, which nothing has ever diminished.

[041-063] “Is this true concerning the poor girl?”

[041-064] He slightly inclines and advances his head as not quite understanding

[041-065] the question.

[041-066] “You know what you related. Is it true? Do her friends know my story

[041-067] also? Is it the town-talk yet? Is it chalked upon the walls and cried

[041-068] in the streets?”

[041-069] So! Anger, and fear, and [[secret-and-shame|shame]]. All three contending. What power this

[041-070] woman has to keep these raging passions down! Mr. Tulkinghorn’s

[041-071] thoughts take such form as he looks at her, with his ragged grey

[041-072] eyebrows a hair’s breadth more contracted than usual under her gaze.

[041-073] “No, Lady Dedlock. That was a hypothetical case, arising out of Sir

[041-074] Leicester’s unconsciously carrying the matter with so high a hand.

[041-075] But it would be a real case if they knew—what we know.”

[041-076] “Then they do not know it yet?”

[041-077] “No.”

[041-078] “Can I save the poor girl from injury before they know it?”

[041-079] “Really, Lady Dedlock,” Mr. Tulkinghorn replies, “I cannot give a

[041-080] satisfactory opinion on that point.”

[041-081] And he thinks, with the interest of attentive curiosity, as he

[041-082] watches the struggle in her breast, “The power and force of this

[041-083] woman are astonishing!”

[041-084] “Sir,” she says, for the moment obliged to set her lips with all the

[041-085] energy she has, that she may speak distinctly, “I will make it

[041-086] plainer. I do not dispute your hypothetical case. I anticipated it,

[041-087] and felt its truth as strongly as you can do, when I saw Mr.

[041-088] Rouncewell here. I knew very well that if he could have had the power

[041-089] of seeing me as I was, he would consider the poor girl tarnished by

[041-090] having for a moment been, although most innocently, the subject of my

[041-091] great and distinguished patronage. But I have an interest in her, or

[041-092] I should rather say—no longer belonging to this place—I had, and if

[041-093] you can find so much consideration for the woman under your foot as

[041-094] to remember that, she will be very sensible of your mercy.”

[041-095] Mr. Tulkinghorn, profoundly attentive, throws this off with a shrug

[041-096] of self-depreciation and contracts his eyebrows a little more.

[041-097] “You have prepared me for my exposure, and I thank you for that too.

[041-098] Is there anything that you require of me? Is there any claim that I

[041-099] can release or any charge or trouble that I can spare my husband in

[041-100] obtaining HIS release by certifying to the exactness of your

[041-101] discovery? I will write anything, here and now, that you will

[041-102] dictate. I am ready to do it.”

[041-103] And she would do it, thinks the lawyer, watchful of the firm hand

[041-104] with which she takes the pen!

[041-105] “I will not trouble you, Lady Dedlock. Pray spare yourself.”

[041-106] “I have long expected this, as you know. I neither wish to spare

[041-107] myself nor to be spared. You can do nothing worse to me than you have

[041-108] done. Do what remains now.”

[041-109] “Lady Dedlock, there is nothing to be done. I will take leave to say

[041-110] a few words when you have finished.”

[041-111] Their need for watching one another should be over now, but they do

[041-112] it all this time, and the stars watch them both through the opened

[041-113] window. Away in the moonlight lie the woodland fields at rest, and

[041-114] the wide house is as quiet as the narrow one. The narrow one! Where

[041-115] are the digger and the spade, this peaceful night, destined to add

[041-116] the last great secret to the many secrets of the Tulkinghorn

[041-117] existence? Is the man born yet, is the spade wrought yet? Curious

[041-118] questions to consider, more curious perhaps not to consider, under

[041-119] the watching stars upon a summer night.

[041-120] “Of repentance or remorse or any feeling of mine,” Lady Dedlock

[041-121] presently proceeds, “I say not a word. If I were not dumb, you would

[041-122] be deaf. Let that go by. It is not for your ears.”

[041-123] He makes a feint of offering a protest, but she sweeps it away with

[041-124] her disdainful hand.

[041-125] “Of other and very different things I come to speak to you. My jewels

[041-126] are all in their proper places of keeping. They will be found there.

[041-127] So, my dresses. So, all the valuables I have. Some ready money I had

[041-128] with me, please to say, but no large amount. I did not wear my own

[041-129] dress, in order that I might avoid observation. I went to be

[041-130] henceforward lost. Make this known. I leave no other charge with

[041-131] you.”

[041-132] “Excuse me, Lady Dedlock,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, quite unmoved. “I am

[041-133] not sure that I understand you. You want—”

[041-134] “To be lost to all here. I leave [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]] to-night. I go this

[041-135] hour.”

[041-136] Mr. Tulkinghorn shakes his head. She rises, but he, without moving

[041-137] hand from chair-back or from old-fashioned waistcoat and shirt-frill,

[041-138] shakes his head.

[041-139] “What? Not go as I have said?”

[041-140] “No, Lady Dedlock,” he very calmly replies.

[041-141] “Do you know the relief that my disappearance will be? Have you

[041-142] forgotten the stain and blot upon this place, and where it is, and

[041-143] who it is?”

[041-144] “No, Lady Dedlock, not by any means.”

[041-145] Without deigning to rejoin, she moves to the inner door and has it in

[041-146] her hand when he says to her, without himself stirring hand or foot

[041-147] or raising his voice, “Lady Dedlock, have the goodness to stop and

[041-148] hear me, or before you reach the staircase I shall ring the

[041-149] alarm-bell and rouse the house. And then I must speak out before

[041-150] every guest and servant, every man and woman, in it.”

[041-151] He has conquered her. She falters, trembles, and puts her hand

[041-152] confusedly to her head. Slight tokens these in any one else, but when

[041-153] so practised an eye as Mr. Tulkinghorn’s sees indecision for a moment

[041-154] in such a subject, he thoroughly knows its value.

[041-155] He promptly says again, “Have the goodness to hear me, Lady Dedlock,”

[041-156] and motions to the chair from which she has risen. She hesitates, but

[041-157] he motions again, and she sits down.

[041-158] “The relations between us are of an unfortunate description, Lady

[041-159] Dedlock; but as they are not of my making, I will not apologize for

[041-160] them. The position I hold in reference to [[sir-leicester-dedlock|Sir Leicester]] is so well

[041-161] known to you that I can hardly imagine but that I must long have

[041-162] appeared in your eyes the natural person to make this discovery.”

[041-163] “Sir,” she returns without looking up from the ground on which her

[041-164] eyes are now fixed, “I had better have gone. It would have been far

[041-165] better not to have detained me. I have no more to say.”

[041-166] “Excuse me, Lady Dedlock, if I add a little more to hear.”

[041-167] “I wish to hear it at the window, then. I can’t breathe where I am.”

[041-168] His jealous glance as she walks that way betrays an instant’s

[041-169] misgiving that she may have it in her thoughts to leap over, and

[041-170] dashing against ledge and cornice, strike her life out upon the

[041-171] terrace below. But a moment’s observation of her figure as she stands

[041-172] in the window without any support, looking out at the stars—not

[041-173] up—gloomily out at those stars which are low in the heavens,

[041-174] reassures him. By facing round as she has moved, he stands a little

[041-175] behind her.

[041-176] “Lady Dedlock, I have not yet been able to come to a decision

[041-177] satisfactory to myself on the course before me. I am not clear what

[041-178] to do or how to act next. I must request you, in the meantime, to

[041-179] keep your secret as you have kept it so long and not to wonder that I

[041-180] keep it too.”

[041-181] He pauses, but she makes no reply.

[041-182] “Pardon me, Lady Dedlock. This is an important subject. You are

[041-183] honouring me with your attention?”

[041-184] “I am.”

[041-185] “Thank you. I might have known it from what I have seen of your

[041-186] strength of character. I ought not to have asked the question, but I

[041-187] have the habit of making sure of my ground, step by step, as I go on.

[041-188] The sole consideration in this unhappy case is Sir Leicester.”

[041-189] “Then why,” she asks in a low voice and without removing her gloomy

[041-190] look from those distant stars, “do you detain me in his house?”

[041-191] “Because he IS the consideration. Lady Dedlock, I have no occasion to

[041-192] tell you that Sir Leicester is a very proud man, that his reliance

[041-193] upon you is implicit, that the fall of that moon out of the sky would

[041-194] not amaze him more than your fall from your high position as his

[041-195] wife.”

[041-196] She breathes quickly and heavily, but she stands as unflinchingly as

[041-197] ever he has seen her in the midst of her grandest company.

[041-198] “I declare to you, Lady Dedlock, that with anything short of this

[041-199] case that I have, I would as soon have hoped to root up by means of

[041-200] my own strength and my own hands the oldest tree on this [[inheritance-and-property|estate]] as to

[041-201] shake your hold upon Sir Leicester and Sir Leicester’s trust and

[041-202] confidence in you. And even now, with this case, I hesitate. Not that

[041-203] he could doubt (that, even with him, is impossible), but that nothing

[041-204] can prepare him for the blow.”

[041-205] “Not my flight?” she returned. “Think of it again.”

[041-206] “Your flight, Lady Dedlock, would spread the whole truth, and a

[041-207] hundred times the whole truth, far and wide. It would be impossible

[041-208] to save the family credit for a day. It is not to be thought of.”

[041-209] There is a quiet decision in his reply which admits of no

[041-210] remonstrance.

[041-211] “When I speak of Sir Leicester being the sole consideration, he and

[041-212] the family credit are one. Sir Leicester and the baronetcy, Sir

[041-213] Leicester and Chesney Wold, Sir Leicester and his ancestors and his

[041-214] patrimony”—Mr. Tulkinghorn very dry here—“are, I need not say to

[041-215] you, Lady Dedlock, inseparable.”

[041-216] “Go on!”

[041-217] “Therefore,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, pursuing his case in his jog-trot

[041-218] style, “I have much to consider. This is to be hushed up if it can

[041-219] be. How can it be, if Sir Leicester is driven out of his wits or laid

[041-220] upon a death-bed? If I inflicted this shock upon him to-morrow

[041-221] morning, how could the immediate change in him be accounted for? What

[041-222] could have caused it? What could have divided you? Lady Dedlock, the

[041-223] wall-chalking and the street-crying would come on directly, and you

[041-224] are to remember that it would not affect you merely (whom I cannot at

[041-225] all consider in this business) but your husband, Lady Dedlock, your

[041-226] husband.”

[041-227] He gets plainer as he gets on, but not an atom more emphatic or

[041-228] animated.

[041-229] “There is another point of view,” he continues, “in which the case

[041-230] presents itself. Sir Leicester is devoted to you almost to

[041-231] infatuation. He might not be able to overcome that infatuation, even

[041-232] knowing what we know. I am putting an extreme case, but it might be

[041-233] so. If so, it were better that he knew nothing. Better for common

[041-234] sense, better for him, better for me. I must take all this into

[041-235] account, and it combines to render a decision very difficult.”

[041-236] She stands looking out at the same stars without a word. They are

[041-237] beginning to pale, and she looks as if their coldness froze her.

[041-238] “My experience teaches me,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, who has by this

[041-239] time got his hands in his pockets and is going on in his business

[041-240] consideration of the matter like a machine. “My experience teaches

[041-241] me, Lady Dedlock, that most of the people I know would do far better

[041-242] to leave marriage alone. It is at the bottom of three fourths of

[041-243] their troubles. So I thought when Sir Leicester married, and so I

[041-244] always have thought since. No more about that. I must now be guided

[041-245] by circumstances. In the meanwhile I must beg you to keep your own

[041-246] counsel, and I will keep mine.”

[041-247] “I am to drag my present life on, holding its pains at your pleasure,

[041-248] day by day?” she asks, still looking at the distant sky.

[041-249] “Yes, I am afraid so, Lady Dedlock.”

[041-250] “It is necessary, you think, that I should be so tied to the stake?”

[041-251] “I am sure that what I recommend is necessary.”

[041-252] “I am to remain on this gaudy platform on which my miserable

[041-253] deception has been so long acted, and it is to fall beneath me when

[041-254] you give the signal?” she said slowly.

[041-255] “Not without notice, Lady Dedlock. I shall take no step without

[041-256] forewarning you.”

[041-257] She asks all her questions as if she were repeating them from memory

[041-258] or calling them over in her sleep.

[041-259] “We are to meet as usual?”

[041-260] “Precisely as usual, if you please.”

[041-261] “And I am to hide my guilt, as I have done so many years?”

[041-262] “As you have done so many years. I should not have made that

[041-263] reference myself, Lady Dedlock, but I may now remind you that your

[041-264] secret can be no heavier to you than it was, and is no worse and no

[041-265] better than it was. I know it certainly, but I believe we have never

[041-266] wholly trusted each other.”

[041-267] She stands absorbed in the same frozen way for some little time

[041-268] before asking, “Is there anything more to be said to-night?”

[041-269] “Why,” Mr. Tulkinghorn returns methodically as he softly rubs his

[041-270] hands, “I should like to be assured of your acquiescence in my

[041-271] arrangements, Lady Dedlock.”

[041-272] “You may be assured of it.”

[041-273] “Good. And I would wish in conclusion to remind you, as a business

[041-274] precaution, in case it should be necessary to recall the fact in any

[041-275] communication with Sir Leicester, that throughout our interview I

[041-276] have expressly stated my sole consideration to be Sir Leicester’s

[041-277] feelings and honour and the family reputation. I should have been

[041-278] happy to have made Lady Dedlock a prominent consideration, too, if

[041-279] the case had admitted of it; but unfortunately it does not.”

[041-280] “I can attest your fidelity, sir.”

[041-281] Both before and after saying it she remains absorbed, but at length

[041-282] moves, and turns, unshaken in her natural and acquired presence,

[041-283] towards the door. Mr. Tulkinghorn opens both the doors exactly as he

[041-284] would have done yesterday, or as he would have done ten years ago,

[041-285] and makes his old-fashioned bow as she passes out. It is not an

[041-286] ordinary look that he receives from the handsome face as it goes into

[041-287] the darkness, and it is not an ordinary movement, though a very

[041-288] slight one, that acknowledges his courtesy. But as he reflects when

[041-289] he is left alone, the woman has been putting no common constraint

[041-290] upon herself.

[041-291] He would know it all the better if he saw the woman pacing her own

[041-292] rooms with her hair wildly thrown from her flung-back face, her hands

[041-293] clasped behind her head, her figure twisted as if by pain. He would

[041-294] think so all the more if he saw the woman thus hurrying up and down

[041-295] for hours, without fatigue, without intermission, followed by the

[041-296] faithful step upon the Ghost’s Walk. But he shuts out the now chilled

[041-297] air, draws the window-curtain, goes to bed, and falls asleep. And

[041-298] truly when the stars go out and the wan day peeps into the

[041-299] turret-chamber, finding him at his oldest, he looks as if the digger

[041-300] and the spade were both commissioned and would soon be digging.

[041-301] The same wan day peeps in at Sir Leicester pardoning the repentant

[041-302] country in a majestically condescending dream; and at the cousins

[041-303] entering on various public employments, principally receipt of

[041-304] salary; and at the chaste Volumnia, bestowing a dower of fifty

[041-305] thousand pounds upon a hideous old general with a mouth of false

[041-306] teeth like a pianoforte too full of keys, long the admiration of Bath

[041-307] and the terror of every other community. Also into rooms high in the

[041-308] roof, and into offices in court-yards, and over stables, where

[041-309] humbler ambition dreams of bliss, in keepers’ lodges, and in holy

[041-310] [[marriage-and-property|matrimony]] with Will or Sally. Up comes the bright sun, drawing

[041-311] everything up with it—the Wills and Sallys, the latent vapour in the

[041-312] earth, the drooping leaves and flowers, the birds and beasts and

[041-313] creeping things, the gardeners to sweep the dewy turf and unfold

[041-314] emerald velvet where the roller passes, the smoke of the great

[041-315] kitchen fire wreathing itself straight and high into the lightsome

[041-316] air. Lastly, up comes the flag over Mr. Tulkinghorn’s unconscious

[041-317] head cheerfully proclaiming that Sir Leicester and Lady Dedlock are

[041-318] in their happy home and that there is hospitality at the place in

[041-319] Lincolnshire.
