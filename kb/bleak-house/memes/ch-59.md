[059-001] It was three o’clock in the morning when the houses outside [[london|London]]

[059-002] did at last begin to exclude the country and to close us in with

[059-003] streets. We had made our way along roads in a far worse condition

[059-004] than when we had traversed them by daylight, both the fall and the

[059-005] thaw having lasted ever since; but the energy of my companion never

[059-006] slackened. It had only been, as I thought, of less assistance than

[059-007] the horses in getting us on, and it had often aided them. They had

[059-008] stopped exhausted half-way up hills, they had been driven through

[059-009] streams of turbulent water, they had slipped down and become

[059-010] entangled with the harness; but he and his little lantern had been

[059-011] always ready, and when the mishap was set right, I had never heard

[059-012] any variation in his cool, “Get on, my lads!”

[059-013] The steadiness and confidence with which he had directed our journey

[059-014] back I could not account for. Never wavering, he never even stopped

[059-015] to make an inquiry until we were within a few miles of London. A very

[059-016] few words, here and there, were then enough for him; and thus we

[059-017] came, at between three and four o’clock in the morning, into

[059-018] Islington.

[059-019] I will not dwell on the suspense and anxiety with which I reflected

[059-020] all this time that we were leaving my mother farther and farther

[059-021] behind every minute. I think I had some strong hope that he must be

[059-022] right and could not fail to have a satisfactory object in following

[059-023] this woman, but I tormented myself with questioning it and discussing

[059-024] it during the whole journey. What was to ensue when we found her and

[059-025] what could compensate us for this loss of time were questions also

[059-026] that I could not possibly dismiss; my mind was quite tortured by long

[059-027] dwelling on such reflections when we stopped.

[059-028] We stopped in a high-street where there was a coach-stand. My

[059-029] companion paid our two drivers, who were as completely covered with

[059-030] splashes as if they had been dragged along the roads like the

[059-031] carriage itself, and giving them some brief direction where to take

[059-032] it, lifted me out of it and into a hackney-coach he had chosen from

[059-033] the rest.

[059-034] “Why, my dear!” he said as he did this. “How wet you are!”

[059-035] I had not been conscious of it. But the melted snow had found its way

[059-036] into the carriage, and I had got out two or three times when a fallen

[059-037] horse was plunging and had to be got up, and the wet had penetrated

[059-038] my dress. I assured him it was no matter, but the driver, who knew

[059-039] him, would not be dissuaded by me from running down the street to his

[059-040] stable, whence he brought an armful of clean dry straw. They shook it

[059-041] out and strewed it well about me, and I found it warm and

[059-042] comfortable.

[059-043] “Now, my dear,” said Mr. Bucket, with his head in at the window after

[059-044] I was shut up. “We’re a-going to mark this person down. It may take a

[059-045] little time, but you don’t mind that. You’re pretty sure that I’ve

[059-046] got a motive. Ain’t you?”

[059-047] I little thought what it was, little thought in how short a time I

[059-048] should understand it better, but I assured him that I had confidence

[059-049] in him.

[059-050] “So you may have, my dear,” he returned. “And I tell you what! If you

[059-051] only repose half as much confidence in me as I repose in you after

[059-052] what I’ve experienced of you, that’ll do. Lord! You’re no trouble at

[059-053] all. I never see a young woman in any station of society—and I’ve

[059-054] seen many elevated ones too—conduct herself like you have conducted

[059-055] yourself since you was called out of your bed. You’re a pattern, you

[059-056] know, that’s what you are,” said Mr. Bucket warmly; “you’re a

[059-057] pattern.”

[059-058] I told him I was very glad, as indeed I was, to have been no

[059-059] hindrance to him, and that I hoped I should be none now.

[059-060] “My dear,” he returned, “when a young lady is as mild as she’s game,

[059-061] and as game as she’s mild, that’s all I ask, and more than I expect.

[059-062] She then becomes a queen, and that’s about what you are yourself.”

[059-063] With these encouraging words—they really were encouraging to me

[059-064] under those lonely and anxious circumstances—he got upon the box,

[059-065] and we once more drove away. Where we drove I neither knew then nor

[059-066] have ever known since, but we appeared to seek out the narrowest and

[059-067] worst streets in London. Whenever I saw him directing the driver, I

[059-068] was prepared for our descending into a deeper complication of such

[059-069] streets, and we never failed to do so.

[059-070] Sometimes we emerged upon a wider thoroughfare or came to a larger

[059-071] building than the generality, well lighted. Then we stopped at

[059-072] offices like those we had visited when we began our journey, and I

[059-073] saw him in consultation with others. Sometimes he would get down by

[059-074] an archway or at a street corner and mysteriously show the light of

[059-075] his little lantern. This would attract similar lights from various

[059-076] dark quarters, like so many insects, and a fresh consultation would

[059-077] be held. By degrees we appeared to contract our search within

[059-078] narrower and easier limits. Single police-officers on duty could now

[059-079] tell Mr. Bucket what he wanted to know and point to him where to go.

[059-080] At last we stopped for a rather long conversation between him and one

[059-081] of these men, which I supposed to be satisfactory from his manner of

[059-082] nodding from time to time. When it was finished he came to me looking

[059-083] very busy and very attentive.

[059-084] “Now, Miss Summerson,” he said to me, “you won’t be alarmed whatever

[059-085] comes off, I know. It’s not necessary for me to give you any further

[059-086] caution than to tell you that we have marked this person down and

[059-087] that you may be of use to me before I know it myself. I don’t like to

[059-088] ask such a thing, my dear, but would you walk a little way?”

[059-089] Of course I got out directly and took his arm.

[059-090] “It ain’t so easy to keep your feet,” said Mr. Bucket, “but take

[059-091] time.”

[059-092] Although I looked about me confusedly and hurriedly as we crossed the

[059-093] street, I thought I knew the place. “Are we in Holborn?” I asked him.

[059-094] “Yes,” said Mr. Bucket. “Do you know this turning?”

[059-095] “It looks like [[chancery-lane|Chancery Lane]].”

[059-096] “And was christened so, my dear,” said Mr. Bucket.

[059-097] We turned down it, and as we went shuffling through the sleet, I

[059-098] heard the clocks strike half-past five. We passed on in silence and

[059-099] as quickly as we could with such a foot-hold, when some one coming

[059-100] towards us on the narrow pavement, wrapped in a cloak, stopped and

[059-101] stood aside to give me room. In the same moment I heard an

[059-102] exclamation of wonder and my own name from [[allan-woodcourt|Mr. Woodcourt]]. I knew his

[059-103] voice very well.

[059-104] It was so unexpected and so—I don’t know what to call it, whether

[059-105] pleasant or painful—to come upon it after my feverish wandering

[059-106] journey, and in the midst of the night, that I could not keep back

[059-107] the tears from my eyes. It was like hearing his voice in a strange

[059-108] country.

[059-109] “My dear Miss Summerson, that you should be out at this hour, and in

[059-110] such weather!”

[059-111] He had heard from my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]] of my having been called away on some

[059-112] uncommon business and said so to dispense with any explanation. I

[059-113] told him that we had but just left a coach and were going—but then I

[059-114] was obliged to look at my companion.

[059-115] “Why, you see, Mr. Woodcourt”—he had caught the name from me—“we

[059-116] are a-going at present into the next street. Inspector Bucket.”

[059-117] Mr. Woodcourt, disregarding my remonstrances, had hurriedly taken off

[059-118] his cloak and was putting it about me. “That’s a good move, too,”

[059-119] said Mr. Bucket, assisting, “a very good move.”

[059-120] “May I go with you?” said Mr. Woodcourt. I don’t know whether to me

[059-121] or to my companion.

[059-122] “Why, Lord!” exclaimed Mr. Bucket, taking the answer on himself. “Of

[059-123] course you may.”

[059-124] It was all said in a moment, and they took me between them, wrapped

[059-125] in the cloak.

[059-126] “I have just left [[richard-carstone|Richard]],” said Mr. Woodcourt. “I have been sitting

[059-127] with him since ten o’clock last night.”

[059-128] “Oh, dear me, he is ill!”

[059-129] “No, no, believe me; not ill, but not quite well. He was depressed

[059-130] and faint—you know he gets so worried and so worn sometimes—and [[ada-clare|Ada]]

[059-131] sent to me of course; and when I came home I found her note and came

[059-132] straight here. Well! Richard revived so much after a little while,

[059-133] and Ada was so happy and so convinced of its being my doing, though

[059-134] God knows I had little enough to do with it, that I remained with him

[059-135] until he had been fast asleep some hours. As fast asleep as she is

[059-136] now, I hope!”

[059-137] His friendly and familiar way of speaking of them, his unaffected

[059-138] devotion to them, the grateful confidence with which I knew he had

[059-139] inspired my darling, and the comfort he was to her; could I separate

[059-140] all this from his promise to me? How thankless I must have been if it

[059-141] had not recalled the words he said to me when he was so moved by the

[059-142] change in my appearance: “I will accept him as a trust, and it shall

[059-143] be a sacred one!”

[059-144] We now turned into another narrow street. “Mr. Woodcourt,” said Mr.

[059-145] Bucket, who had eyed him closely as we came along, “our business

[059-146] takes us to a law-stationer’s here, a certain Mr. Snagsby’s. What,

[059-147] you know him, do you?” He was so quick that he saw it in an instant.

[059-148] “Yes, I know a little of him and have called upon him at this place.”

[059-149] “Indeed, sir?” said Mr. Bucket. “Then you will be so good as to let

[059-150] me leave Miss Summerson with you for a moment while I go and have

[059-151] half a word with him?”

[059-152] The last police-officer with whom he had conferred was standing

[059-153] silently behind us. I was not aware of it until he struck in on my

[059-154] saying I heard some one crying.

[059-155] “Don’t be alarmed, miss,” he returned. “It’s Snagsby’s servant.”

[059-156] “Why, you see,” said Mr. Bucket, “the girl’s subject to fits, and has

[059-157] ’em bad upon her to-night. A most contrary circumstance it is, for I

[059-158] want certain information out of that girl, and she must be brought to

[059-159] reason somehow.”

[059-160] “At all events, they wouldn’t be up yet if it wasn’t for her, Mr.

[059-161] Bucket,” said the other man. “She’s been at it pretty well all night,

[059-162] sir.”

[059-163] “Well, that’s true,” he returned. “My light’s burnt out. Show yours a

[059-164] moment.”

[059-165] All this passed in a whisper a door or two from the house in which I

[059-166] could faintly hear crying and moaning. In the little round of light

[059-167] produced for the purpose, Mr. Bucket went up to the door and knocked.

[059-168] The door was opened after he had knocked twice, and he went in,

[059-169] leaving us standing in the street.

[059-170] “Miss Summerson,” said Mr. Woodcourt, “if without obtruding myself on

[059-171] your confidence I may remain near you, pray let me do so.”

[059-172] “You are truly kind,” I answered. “I need wish to keep no secret of

[059-173] my own from you; if I keep any, it is another’s.”

[059-174] “I quite understand. Trust me, I will remain near you only so long as

[059-175] I can fully respect it.”

[059-176] “I trust implicitly to you,” I said. “I know and deeply feel how

[059-177] sacredly you keep your promise.”

[059-178] After a short time the little round of light shone out again, and Mr.

[059-179] Bucket advanced towards us in it with his earnest face. “Please to

[059-180] come in, Miss Summerson,” he said, “and sit down by the fire. Mr.

[059-181] Woodcourt, from information I have received I understand you are a

[059-182] medical man. Would you look to this girl and see if anything can be

[059-183] done to bring her round. She has a letter somewhere that I

[059-184] particularly want. It’s not in her box, and I think it must be about

[059-185] her; but she is so twisted and clenched up that she is difficult to

[059-186] handle without hurting.”

[059-187] We all three went into the house together; although it was cold and

[059-188] raw, it smelt close too from being up all night. In the passage

[059-189] behind the door stood a scared, sorrowful-looking little man in a

[059-190] grey coat who seemed to have a naturally polite manner and spoke

[059-191] meekly.

[059-192] “Downstairs, if you please, Mr. Bucket,” said he. “The lady will

[059-193] excuse the front kitchen; we use it as our workaday sitting-room. The

[059-194] back is Guster’s bedroom, and in it she’s a-carrying on, poor thing,

[059-195] to a frightful extent!”

[059-196] We went downstairs, followed by Mr. Snagsby, as I soon found the

[059-197] little man to be. In the front kitchen, sitting by the fire, was Mrs.

[059-198] Snagsby, with very red eyes and a very severe expression of face.

[059-199] “My little woman,” said Mr. Snagsby, entering behind us, “to

[059-200] wave—not to put too fine a point upon it, my dear—hostilities for

[059-201] one single moment in the course of this prolonged night, here is

[059-202] Inspector Bucket, Mr. Woodcourt, and a lady.”

[059-203] She looked very much astonished, as she had reason for doing, and

[059-204] looked particularly hard at me.

[059-205] “My little woman,” said Mr. Snagsby, sitting down in the remotest

[059-206] corner by the door, as if he were taking a liberty, “it is not

[059-207] unlikely that you may inquire of me why Inspector Bucket, Mr.

[059-208] Woodcourt, and a lady call upon us in Cook’s Court, Cursitor Street,

[059-209] at the present hour. I don’t know. I have not the least idea. If I

[059-210] was to be informed, I should despair of understanding, and I’d rather

[059-211] not be told.”

[059-212] He appeared so miserable, sitting with his head upon his hand, and I

[059-213] appeared so unwelcome, that I was going to offer an apology when Mr.

[059-214] Bucket took the matter on himself.

[059-215] “Now, Mr. Snagsby,” said he, “the best thing you can do is to go

[059-216] along with Mr. Woodcourt to look after your Guster—”

[059-217] “My Guster, Mr. Bucket!” cried Mr. Snagsby. “Go on, sir, go on. I

[059-218] shall be charged with that next.”

[059-219] “And to hold the candle,” pursued Mr. Bucket without correcting

[059-220] himself, “or hold her, or make yourself useful in any way you’re

[059-221] asked. Which there’s not a man alive more ready to do, for you’re a

[059-222] man of urbanity and suavity, you know, and you’ve got the sort of

[059-223] heart that can feel for another. Mr. Woodcourt, would you be so good

[059-224] as see to her, and if you can get that letter from her, to let me

[059-225] have it as soon as ever you can?”

[059-226] As they went out, Mr. Bucket made me sit down in a corner by the fire

[059-227] and take off my wet shoes, which he turned up to dry upon the fender,

[059-228] talking all the time.

[059-229] “Don’t you be at all put out, miss, by the want of a hospitable look

[059-230] from Mrs. Snagsby there, because she’s under a mistake altogether.

[059-231] She’ll find that out sooner than will be agreeable to a lady of her

[059-232] generally correct manner of forming her thoughts, because I’m a-going

[059-234] and shawls in his hand, himself a pile of wet, he turned to Mrs.

[059-235] Snagsby. “Now, the first thing that I say to you, as a married woman

[059-236] possessing what you may call charms, you know—‘Believe Me, if All

[059-237] Those Endearing,’ and cetrer—you’re well acquainted with the song,

[059-238] because it’s in vain for you to tell me that you and good society are

[059-239] strangers—charms—attractions, mind you, that ought to give you

[059-240] confidence in yourself—is, that you’ve done it.”

[059-241] Mrs. Snagsby looked rather alarmed, relented a little and faltered,

[059-242] what did Mr. Bucket mean.

[059-243] “What does Mr. Bucket mean?” he repeated, and I saw by his face that

[059-244] all the time he talked he was listening for the discovery of the

[059-245] letter, to my own great agitation, for I knew then how important it

[059-246] must be; “I’ll tell you what he means, ma’am. Go and see Othello

[059-247] acted. That’s the tragedy for you.”

[059-248] Mrs. Snagsby consciously asked why.

[059-249] “Why?” said Mr. Bucket. “Because you’ll come to that if you don’t

[059-250] look out. Why, at the very moment while I speak, I know what your

[059-257] Mrs. Snagsby appeared to understand the reference better than I did

[059-260] and no other; and the law-writer that you know of was mixed up in the
