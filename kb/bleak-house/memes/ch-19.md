[019-001] It is the long vacation in the regions of [[chancery-lane|Chancery Lane]]. The good

[019-002] ships Law and Equity, those teak-built, copper-bottomed,

[019-003] iron-fastened, brazen-faced, and not by any means fast-sailing

[019-004] clippers are laid up in ordinary. The Flying Dutchman, with a crew of

[019-005] ghostly clients imploring all whom they may encounter to peruse their

[019-006] papers, has drifted, for the time being, heaven knows where. The

[019-007] courts are all shut up; the public offices lie in a hot sleep.

[019-008] Westminster Hall itself is a shady solitude where nightingales might

[019-009] sing, and a tenderer class of suitors than is usually found there,

[019-010] walk.

[019-011] The Temple, [[chancery-court|Chancery]] Lane, Serjeants’ Inn, and Lincoln’s Inn even

[019-012] unto the Fields are like tidal harbours at low water, where stranded

[019-013] proceedings, offices at anchor, idle clerks lounging on lop-sided

[019-014] stools that will not recover their perpendicular until the current of

[019-015] Term sets in, lie high and dry upon the ooze of the long vacation.

[019-016] Outer doors of chambers are shut up by the score, messages and

[019-017] parcels are to be left at the Porter’s Lodge by the bushel. A crop of

[019-018] grass would grow in the chinks of the stone pavement outside

[019-019] Lincoln’s Inn Hall, but that the ticket-porters, who have nothing to

[019-020] do beyond sitting in the shade there, with their white aprons over

[019-021] their heads to keep the flies off, grub it up and eat it

[019-022] thoughtfully.

[019-023] There is only one judge in town. Even he only comes twice a week to

[019-024] sit in chambers. If the country folks of those assize towns on his

[019-025] circuit could see him now! No full-bottomed wig, no red petticoats,

[019-026] no fur, no javelin-men, no white wands. Merely a close-shaved

[019-027] gentleman in white trousers and a white hat, with sea-bronze on the

[019-028] judicial countenance, and a strip of bark peeled by the solar rays

[019-029] from the judicial nose, who calls in at the shell-fish shop as he

[019-030] comes along and drinks iced ginger-beer!

[019-031] The bar of England is scattered over the face of the earth. How

[019-032] England can get on through four long summer months without its

[019-033] bar—which is its acknowledged refuge in adversity and its only

[019-034] legitimate triumph in prosperity—is beside the question; assuredly

[019-035] that shield and buckler of Britannia are not in present wear. The

[019-036] learned gentleman who is always so tremendously indignant at the

[019-037] unprecedented outrage committed on the feelings of his client by the

[019-038] opposite party that he never seems likely to recover it is doing

[019-039] infinitely better than might be expected in Switzerland. The learned

[019-040] gentleman who does the withering business and who blights all

[019-041] opponents with his gloomy sarcasm is as merry as a grig at a French

[019-042] watering-place. The learned gentleman who weeps by the pint on the

[019-043] smallest provocation has not shed a tear these six weeks. The very

[019-044] learned gentleman who has cooled the natural heat of his gingery

[019-045] complexion in pools and fountains of law until he has become great in

[019-046] knotty arguments for term-time, when he poses the drowsy bench with

[019-047] legal “chaff,” inexplicable to the uninitiated and to most of the

[019-048] initiated too, is roaming, with a characteristic delight in aridity

[019-049] and dust, about Constantinople. Other dispersed fragments of the same

[019-050] great palladium are to be found on the canals of Venice, at the

[019-051] second cataract of the Nile, in the baths of Germany, and sprinkled

[019-052] on the sea-sand all over the English coast. Scarcely one is to be

[019-053] encountered in the deserted region of Chancery Lane. If such a lonely

[019-054] member of the bar do flit across the waste, and come upon a prowling

[019-055] suitor who is unable to leave off haunting the scenes of his anxiety,

[019-056] they frighten one another and retreat into opposite shades.

[019-057] It is the hottest long vacation known for many years. All the young

[019-058] clerks are madly in love, and according to their various degrees,

[019-059] pine for bliss with the beloved object, at Margate, Ramsgate, or

[019-060] Gravesend. All the middle-aged clerks think their families too large.

[019-061] All the unowned dogs who stray into the [[inns-of-court|Inns of Court]] and pant about

[019-062] staircases and other dry places seeking water give short howls of

[019-063] aggravation. All the blind men’s dogs in the streets draw their

[019-064] masters against pumps or trip them over buckets. A shop with a

[019-065] sun-blind, and a watered pavement, and a bowl of gold and silver fish

[019-066] in the window, is a sanctuary. Temple Bar gets so hot that it is, to

[019-067] the adjacent Strand and Fleet Street, what a heater is in an urn, and

[019-068] keeps them simmering all night.

[019-069] There are offices about the Inns of Court in which a man might be

[019-070] cool, if any coolness were worth purchasing at such a price in

[019-071] dullness; but the little thoroughfares immediately outside those

[019-072] retirements seem to blaze. In Mr. [[krook|Krook]]’s court, it is so hot that

[019-073] the people turn their houses inside out and sit in chairs upon the

[019-074] pavement—Mr. Krook included, who there pursues his studies, with his

[019-075] cat (who never is too hot) by his side. The Sol’s Arms has

[019-076] discontinued the Harmonic Meetings for the season, and Little Swills

[019-077] is engaged at the Pastoral Gardens down the river, where he comes out

[019-078] in quite an innocent manner and sings comic ditties of a juvenile

[019-079] complexion calculated (as the bill says) not to wound the feelings of

[019-080] the most fastidious mind.

[019-081] Over all the legal neighbourhood there hangs, like some great veil of

[019-082] rust or gigantic cobweb, the idleness and pensiveness of the long

[019-083] vacation. Mr. Snagsby, law-stationer of Cook’s Court, Cursitor

[019-084] Street, is sensible of the influence not only in his mind as a

[019-085] sympathetic and contemplative man, but also in his business as a

[019-086] law-stationer aforesaid. He has more leisure for musing in Staple Inn

[019-087] and in the Rolls Yard during the long vacation than at other seasons,

[019-088] and he says to the two ’prentices, what a thing it is in such hot

[019-089] weather to think that you live in an island with the sea a-rolling

[019-090] and a-bowling right round you.

[019-091] Guster is busy in the little drawing-room on this present afternoon

[019-092] in the long vacation, when Mr. and Mrs. Snagsby have it in

[019-093] contemplation to receive company. The expected guests are rather

[019-094] select than numerous, being Mr. and Mrs. Chadband and no more. From

[019-095] Mr. Chadband’s being much given to describe himself, both verbally

[019-096] and in [[document-and-letter|writing]], as a vessel, he is occasionally mistaken by strangers

[019-097] for a gentleman connected with navigation, but he is, as he expresses

[019-098] it, “in the ministry.” Mr. Chadband is attached to no particular

[019-099] denomination and is considered by his persecutors to have nothing so

[019-100] very remarkable to say on the greatest of subjects as to render his

[019-101] volunteering, on his own account, at all incumbent on his conscience;

[019-102] but he has his followers, and Mrs. Snagsby is of the number. Mrs.

[019-103] Snagsby has but recently taken a passage upward by the vessel,

[019-104] Chadband; and her attention was attracted to that Bark A 1, when she

[019-105] was something flushed by the hot weather.

[019-106] “My little woman,” says Mr. Snagsby to the sparrows in Staple Inn,

[019-107] “likes to have her religion rather sharp, you see!”

[019-108] So Guster, much impressed by regarding herself for the time as the

[019-109] handmaid of Chadband, whom she knows to be endowed with the gift of

[019-110] holding forth for four hours at a stretch, prepares the little

[019-111] drawing-room for tea. All the furniture is shaken and dusted, the

[019-112] portraits of Mr. and Mrs. Snagsby are touched up with a wet cloth,

[019-113] the best tea-service is set forth, and there is excellent provision

[019-114] made of dainty new bread, crusty twists, cool fresh butter, thin

[019-115] slices of ham, tongue, and German sausage, and delicate little rows

[019-116] of anchovies nestling in parsley, not to mention new-laid eggs, to be

[019-117] brought up warm in a napkin, and hot buttered toast. For Chadband is

[019-118] rather a consuming vessel—the persecutors say a gorging vessel—and

[019-119] can wield such weapons of the flesh as a knife and fork remarkably

[019-120] well.

[019-121] Mr. Snagsby in his best coat, looking at all the preparations when

[019-122] they are completed and coughing his cough of deference behind his

[019-123] hand, says to Mrs. Snagsby, “At what time did you expect Mr. and Mrs.

[019-124] Chadband, my love?”

[019-125] “At six,” says Mrs. Snagsby.

[019-126] Mr. Snagsby observes in a mild and casual way that “it’s gone that.”

[019-127] “Perhaps you’d like to begin without them,” is Mrs. Snagsby’s

[019-128] reproachful remark.

[019-129] Mr. Snagsby does look as if he would like it very much, but he says,

[019-130] with his cough of mildness, “No, my dear, no. I merely named the

[019-131] time.”

[019-132] “What’s time,” says Mrs. Snagsby, “to eternity?”

[019-133] “Very true, my dear,” says Mr. Snagsby. “Only when a person lays in

[019-134] victuals for tea, a person does it with a view—perhaps—more to

[019-135] time. And when a time is named for having tea, it’s better to come up

[019-136] to it.”

[019-137] “To come up to it!” Mrs. Snagsby repeats with severity. “Up to it! As

[019-138] if Mr. Chadband was a fighter!”

[019-139] “Not at all, my dear,” says Mr. Snagsby.

[019-140] Here, Guster, who had been looking out of the bedroom window, comes

[019-141] rustling and scratching down the little staircase like a popular

[019-142] ghost, and falling flushed into the drawing-room, announces that Mr.

[019-143] and Mrs. Chadband have appeared in the court. The bell at the inner

[019-144] door in the passage immediately thereafter tinkling, she is

[019-145] admonished by Mrs. Snagsby, on pain of instant reconsignment to her

[019-146] patron saint, not to omit the ceremony of announcement. Much

[019-147] discomposed in her nerves (which were previously in the best order)

[019-148] by this threat, she so fearfully mutilates that point of state as to

[019-149] announce “Mr. and Mrs. Cheeseming, least which, Imeantersay,

[019-150] whatsername!” and retires conscience-stricken from the presence.

[019-151] Mr. Chadband is a large yellow man with a fat smile and a general

[019-152] appearance of having a good deal of train oil in his system. Mrs.

[019-153] Chadband is a stern, severe-looking, silent woman. Mr. Chadband moves

[019-154] softly and cumbrously, not unlike a bear who has been taught to walk

[019-155] upright. He is very much embarrassed about the arms, as if they were

[019-156] inconvenient to him and he wanted to grovel, is very much in a

[019-157] perspiration about the head, and never speaks without first putting

[019-158] up his great hand, as delivering a token to his hearers that he is

[019-159] going to edify them.

[019-160] “My friends,” says Mr. Chadband, “peace be on this house! On the

[019-161] master thereof, on the mistress thereof, on the young maidens, and on

[019-162] the young men! My friends, why do I wish for peace? What is peace? Is

[019-163] it war? No. Is it strife? No. Is it lovely, and gentle, and

[019-164] beautiful, and pleasant, and serene, and joyful? Oh, yes! Therefore,

[019-165] my friends, I wish for peace, upon you and upon yours.”

[019-166] In consequence of Mrs. Snagsby looking deeply edified, Mr. Snagsby

[019-167] thinks it expedient on the whole to say amen, which is well received.

[019-168] “Now, my friends,” proceeds Mr. Chadband, “since I am upon this

[019-169] theme—”

[019-170] Guster presents herself. Mrs. Snagsby, in a spectral bass voice and

[019-171] without removing her eyes from Chadband, says with dreadful

[019-172] distinctness, “Go away!”

[019-173] “Now, my friends,” says Chadband, “since I am upon this theme, and in

[019-174] my lowly path improving it—”

[019-175] Guster is heard unaccountably to murmur “one thousing seven hundred

[019-176] and eighty-two.” The spectral voice repeats more solemnly, “Go away!”

[019-177] “Now, my friends,” says Mr. Chadband, “we will inquire in a spirit of

[019-178] love—”

[019-179] Still Guster reiterates “one thousing seven hundred and eighty-two.”

[019-180] Mr. Chadband, pausing with the resignation of a man accustomed to be

[019-181] persecuted and languidly folding up his chin into his fat smile,

[019-182] says, “Let us hear the maiden! Speak, maiden!”

[019-183] “One thousing seven hundred and eighty-two, if you please, sir. Which

[019-184] he wish to know what the shilling ware for,” says Guster, breathless.

[019-185] “For?” returns Mrs. Chadband. “For his fare!”

[019-186] Guster replied that “he insistes on one and eightpence or on

[019-187] summonsizzing the party.” Mrs. Snagsby and Mrs. Chadband are

[019-188] proceeding to grow shrill in indignation when Mr. Chadband quiets the

[019-189] tumult by lifting up his hand.

[019-190] “My friends,” says he, “I remember a duty unfulfilled yesterday. It

[019-191] is right that I should be chastened in some penalty. I ought not to

[019-192] murmur. Rachael, pay the eightpence!”

[019-193] While Mrs. Snagsby, drawing her breath, looks hard at Mr. Snagsby, as

[019-194] who should say, “You hear this apostle!” and while Mr. Chadband glows

[019-195] with humility and train oil, Mrs. Chadband pays the money. It is Mr.

[019-196] Chadband’s habit—it is the head and front of his pretensions

[019-197] indeed—to keep this sort of debtor and creditor account in the

[019-198] smallest items and to post it publicly on the most trivial occasions.

[019-199] “My friends,” says Chadband, “eightpence is not much; it might justly

[019-200] have been one and fourpence; it might justly have been half a crown.

[019-201] O let us be joyful, joyful! O let us be joyful!”

[019-202] With which remark, which appears from its sound to be an extract in

[019-203] verse, Mr. Chadband stalks to the table, and before taking a chair,

[019-204] lifts up his admonitory hand.

[019-205] “My friends,” says he, “what is this which we now behold as being

[019-206] spread before us? Refreshment. Do we need refreshment then, my

[019-207] friends? We do. And why do we need refreshment, my friends? Because

[019-208] we are but mortal, because we are but sinful, because we are but of

[019-209] the earth, because we are not of the air. Can we fly, my friends? We

[019-210] cannot. Why can we not fly, my friends?”

[019-211] Mr. Snagsby, presuming on the success of his last point, ventures to

[019-212] observe in a cheerful and rather knowing tone, “No wings.” But is

[019-213] immediately frowned down by Mrs. Snagsby.

[019-214] “I say, my friends,” pursues Mr. Chadband, utterly rejecting and

[019-215] obliterating Mr. Snagsby’s suggestion, “why can we not fly? Is it

[019-216] because we are calculated to walk? It is. Could we walk, my friends,

[019-217] without strength? We could not. What should we do without strength,

[019-218] my friends? Our legs would refuse to bear us, our knees would double

[019-219] up, our ankles would turn over, and we should come to the ground.

[019-220] Then from whence, my friends, in a human point of view, do we derive

[019-221] the strength that is necessary to our limbs? Is it,” says Chadband,

[019-222] glancing over the table, “from bread in various forms, from butter

[019-223] which is churned from the milk which is yielded unto us by the cow,

[019-224] from the eggs which are laid by the fowl, from ham, from tongue, from

[019-225] sausage, and from such like? It is. Then let us partake of the good

[019-226] things which are set before us!”

[019-227] The persecutors denied that there was any particular gift in Mr.

[019-228] Chadband’s piling verbose flights of stairs, one upon another, after

[019-229] this fashion. But this can only be received as a proof of their

[019-230] determination to persecute, since it must be within everybody’s

[019-231] experience that the Chadband style of oratory is widely received and

[019-232] much admired.

[019-233] Mr. Chadband, however, having concluded for the present, sits down at

[019-234] Mr. Snagsby’s table and lays about him prodigiously. The conversion

[019-235] of nutriment of any sort into oil of the quality already mentioned

[019-236] appears to be a process so inseparable from the constitution of this

[019-237] exemplary vessel that in beginning to eat and drink, he may be

[019-238] described as always becoming a kind of considerable oil mills or

[019-239] other large factory for the production of that article on a wholesale

[019-240] scale. On the present evening of the long vacation, in Cook’s Court,

[019-241] Cursitor Street, he does such a powerful stroke of business that the

[019-242] warehouse appears to be quite full when the works cease.

[019-243] At this period of the entertainment, Guster, who has never recovered

[019-244] her first failure, but has neglected no possible or impossible means

[019-245] of bringing the establishment and herself into contempt—among which

[019-246] may be briefly enumerated her unexpectedly performing clashing

[019-247] military music on Mr. Chadband’s head with plates, and afterwards

[019-248] crowning that gentleman with muffins—at which period of the

[019-249] entertainment, Guster whispers Mr. Snagsby that he is wanted.

[019-250] “And being wanted in the—not to put too fine a point upon it—in the

[019-251] shop,” says Mr. Snagsby, rising, “perhaps this good company will

[019-252] excuse me for half a minute.”

[019-253] Mr. Snagsby descends and finds the two ’prentices intently

[019-254] contemplating a police constable, who holds a ragged boy by the arm.

[019-255] “Why, bless my heart,” says Mr. Snagsby, “what’s the matter!”

[019-256] “This boy,” says the constable, “although he’s repeatedly told to,

[019-257] won’t move on—”

[019-258] “I’m always a-moving on, sar,” cries the boy, wiping away his grimy

[019-259] tears with his arm. “I’ve always been a-moving and a-moving on, ever

[019-260] since I was born. Where can I possibly move to, sir, more nor I do

[019-261] move!”

[019-262] “He won’t move on,” says the constable calmly, with a slight

[019-263] professional hitch of his neck involving its better settlement in his

[019-264] stiff stock, “although he has been repeatedly cautioned, and

[019-265] therefore I am obliged to take him into custody. He’s as obstinate a

[019-266] young gonoph as I know. He WON’T move on.”

[019-267] “Oh, my eye! Where can I move to!” cries the boy, clutching quite

[019-268] desperately at his hair and beating his bare feet upon the floor of

[019-269] Mr. Snagsby’s passage.

[019-270] “Don’t you come none of that or I shall make blessed short work of

[019-271] you!” says the constable, giving him a passionless shake. “My

[019-272] instructions are that you are to move on. I have told you so five

[019-273] hundred times.”

[019-274] “But where?” cries the boy.

[019-275] “Well! Really, constable, you know,” says Mr. Snagsby wistfully, and

[019-276] coughing behind his hand his cough of great perplexity and doubt,

[019-277] “really, that does seem a question. Where, you know?”

[019-278] “My instructions don’t go to that,” replies the constable. “My

[019-279] instructions are that this boy is to move on.”

[019-280] Do you hear, [[jo-crossing-sweeper|Jo]]? It is nothing to you or to any one else that the

[019-284] be-all and the end-all of your strange existence upon earth. Move on!

[019-285] You are by no means to move off, Jo, for the great lights can’t at

[019-286] all agree about that. Move on!

[019-287] Mr. Snagsby says nothing to this effect, says nothing at all indeed,

[019-289] direction. By this time Mr. and Mrs. Chadband and Mrs. Snagsby,

[019-291] never left the end of the passage, the whole household are assembled.

[019-292] “The simple question is, sir,” says the constable, “whether you know

[019-293] this boy. He says you do.”

[019-298] there’s any harm; perhaps on the contrary, constable.” To whom the

[019-337] square it fur to give me change, and then a young man he thieved

[019-361] Now, Jo’s improbable story concerning the lady and the sovereign has

[019-362] awakened more or less the curiosity of all the company. Mr. Guppy,

[019-363] who has an inquiring mind in matters of evidence and who has
