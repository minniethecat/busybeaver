[017-001] [[richard-carstone|Richard]] very often came to see us while we remained in [[london|London]] (though

[017-002] he soon failed in his letter-[[document-and-letter|writing]]), and with his quick abilities,

[017-003] his good spirits, his good temper, his gaiety and freshness, was

[017-004] always delightful. But though I liked him more and more the better I

[017-005] knew him, I still felt more and more how much it was to be regretted

[017-006] that he had been educated in no habits of application and

[017-007] concentration. The system which had addressed him in exactly the same

[017-008] manner as it had addressed hundreds of other boys, all varying in

[017-009] character and capacity, had enabled him to dash through his tasks,

[017-010] always with fair credit and often with distinction, but in a fitful,

[017-011] dazzling way that had confirmed his reliance on those very qualities

[017-012] in himself which it had been most desirable to direct and train. They

[017-013] were good qualities, without which no high place can be meritoriously

[017-014] won, but like fire and water, though excellent servants, they were

[017-015] very bad masters. If they had been under Richard’s direction, they

[017-016] would have been his friends; but Richard being under their direction,

[017-017] they became his enemies.

[017-018] I write down these opinions not because I believe that this or any

[017-019] other thing was so because I thought so, but only because I did think

[017-020] so and I want to be quite candid about all I thought and did. These

[017-021] were my thoughts about Richard. I thought I often observed besides

[017-022] how right my [[john-jarndyce|guardian]] was in what he had said, and that the

[017-023] uncertainties and delays of the [[chancery-court|Chancery]] suit had imparted to his

[017-024] nature something of the careless spirit of a gamester who felt that

[017-025] he was part of a great gaming system.

[017-026] Mr. and Mrs. Bayham Badger coming one afternoon when my guardian was

[017-027] not at home, in the course of conversation I naturally inquired after

[017-028] Richard.

[017-029] “Why, Mr. Carstone,” said Mrs. Badger, “is very well and is, I assure

[017-030] you, a great acquisition to our society. Captain Swosser used to say

[017-031] of me that I was always better than land a-head and a breeze a-starn

[017-032] to the midshipmen’s mess when the purser’s junk had become as tough as

[017-033] the fore-topsel weather earrings. It was his naval way of mentioning

[017-034] generally that I was an acquisition to any society. I may render the

[017-035] same tribute, I am sure, to Mr. Carstone. But I—you won’t think me

[017-036] premature if I mention it?”

[017-037] I said no, as Mrs. Badger’s insinuating tone seemed to require such

[017-038] an answer.

[017-039] “Nor Miss Clare?” said Mrs. Bayham Badger sweetly.

[017-040] [[ada-clare|Ada]] said no, too, and looked uneasy.

[017-041] “Why, you see, my dears,” said Mrs. Badger, “—you’ll excuse me

[017-042] calling you my dears?”

[017-043] We entreated Mrs. Badger not to mention it.

[017-044] “Because you really are, if I may take the liberty of saying so,”

[017-045] pursued Mrs. Badger, “so perfectly charming. You see, my dears, that

[017-046] although I am still young—or Mr. Bayham Badger pays me the

[017-047] compliment of saying so—”

[017-048] “No,” Mr. Badger called out like some one contradicting at a public

[017-049] meeting. “Not at all!”

[017-050] “Very well,” smiled Mrs. Badger, “we will say still young.”

[017-051] “Undoubtedly,” said Mr. Badger.

[017-052] “My dears, though still young, I have had many opportunities of

[017-053] observing young men. There were many such on board the dear old

[017-054] Crippler, I assure you. After that, when I was with Captain Swosser

[017-055] in the Mediterranean, I embraced every opportunity of knowing and

[017-056] befriending the midshipmen under Captain Swosser’s command. YOU never

[017-057] heard them called the young gentlemen, my dears, and probably would

[017-058] not understand allusions to their pipe-claying their weekly accounts,

[017-059] but it is otherwise with me, for blue water has been a second home to

[017-060] me, and I have been quite a sailor. Again, with Professor Dingo.”

[017-061] “A man of European reputation,” murmured Mr. Badger.

[017-062] “When I lost my dear first and became the wife of my dear second,”

[017-063] said Mrs. Badger, speaking of her former husbands as if they were

[017-064] parts of a charade, “I still enjoyed opportunities of observing

[017-065] youth. The class attendant on Professor Dingo’s lectures was a large

[017-066] one, and it became my pride, as the wife of an eminent scientific man

[017-067] seeking herself in science the utmost consolation it could impart, to

[017-068] throw our house open to the students as a kind of Scientific

[017-069] Exchange. Every Tuesday evening there was lemonade and a mixed

[017-070] biscuit for all who chose to partake of those refreshments. And there

[017-071] was science to an unlimited extent.”

[017-072] “Remarkable assemblies those, Miss Summerson,” said Mr. Badger

[017-073] reverentially. “There must have been great intellectual friction

[017-074] going on there under the auspices of such a man!”

[017-075] “And now,” pursued Mrs. Badger, “now that I am the wife of my dear

[017-076] third, Mr. Badger, I still pursue those habits of observation which

[017-077] were formed during the lifetime of Captain Swosser and adapted to new

[017-078] and unexpected purposes during the lifetime of Professor Dingo. I

[017-079] therefore have not come to the consideration of Mr. Carstone as a

[017-080] neophyte. And yet I am very much of the opinion, my dears, that he

[017-081] has not chosen his [[professional-identity|profession]] advisedly.”

[017-082] Ada looked so very anxious now that I asked Mrs. Badger on what she

[017-083] founded her supposition.

[017-084] “My dear Miss Summerson,” she replied, “on Mr. Carstone’s character

[017-085] and conduct. He is of such a very easy disposition that probably he

[017-086] would never think it worth while to mention how he really feels, but

[017-087] he feels languid about the profession. He has not that positive

[017-088] interest in it which makes it his vocation. If he has any decided

[017-089] impression in reference to it, I should say it was that it is a

[017-090] tiresome pursuit. Now, this is not promising. Young men like Mr.

[017-091] [[allan-woodcourt|Allan Woodcourt]] who take it from a strong interest in all that it can

[017-092] do will find some reward in it through a great deal of work for a

[017-093] very little money and through years of considerable endurance and

[017-094] disappointment. But I am quite convinced that this would never be the

[017-095] case with Mr. Carstone.”

[017-096] “Does Mr. Badger think so too?” asked Ada timidly.

[017-097] “Why,” said Mr. Badger, “to tell the truth, Miss Clare, this view of

[017-098] the matter had not occurred to me until Mrs. Badger mentioned it. But

[017-099] when Mrs. Badger put it in that light, I naturally gave great

[017-100] consideration to it, knowing that Mrs. Badger’s mind, in addition to

[017-101] its natural advantages, has had the rare advantage of being formed by

[017-102] two such very distinguished (I will even say illustrious) public men

[017-103] as Captain Swosser of the Royal Navy and Professor Dingo. The

[017-104] conclusion at which I have arrived is—in short, is Mrs. Badger’s

[017-105] conclusion.”

[017-106] “It was a maxim of Captain Swosser’s,” said Mrs. Badger, “speaking in

[017-107] his figurative naval manner, that when you make pitch hot, you cannot

[017-108] make it too hot; and that if you only have to swab a plank, you

[017-109] should swab it as if Davy Jones were after you. It appears to me that

[017-110] this maxim is applicable to the medical as well as to the nautical

[017-111] profession.”

[017-112] “To all professions,” observed Mr. Badger. “It was admirably said by

[017-113] Captain Swosser. Beautifully said.”

[017-114] “People objected to Professor Dingo when we were staying in the north

[017-115] of Devon after our marriage,” said Mrs. Badger, “that he disfigured

[017-116] some of the houses and other buildings by chipping off fragments of

[017-117] those edifices with his little geological hammer. But the professor

[017-118] replied that he knew of no building save the Temple of Science. The

[017-119] principle is the same, I think?”

[017-120] “Precisely the same,” said Mr. Badger. “Finely expressed! The

[017-121] professor made the same remark, Miss Summerson, in his last illness,

[017-122] when (his mind wandering) he insisted on keeping his little hammer

[017-123] under the pillow and chipping at the countenances of the attendants.

[017-124] The ruling passion!”

[017-125] Although we could have dispensed with the length at which Mr. and

[017-126] Mrs. Badger pursued the conversation, we both felt that it was

[017-127] disinterested in them to express the opinion they had communicated to

[017-128] us and that there was a great probability of its being sound. We

[017-129] agreed to say nothing to Mr. Jarndyce until we had spoken to Richard;

[017-130] and as he was coming next evening, we resolved to have a very serious

[017-131] talk with him.

[017-132] So after he had been a little while with Ada, I went in and found my

[017-133] darling (as I knew she would be) prepared to consider him thoroughly

[017-134] right in whatever he said.

[017-135] “And how do you get on, Richard?” said I. I always sat down on the

[017-136] other side of him. He made quite a sister of me.

[017-137] “Oh! Well enough!” said Richard.

[017-138] “He can’t say better than that, [[esther-summerson|Esther]], can he?” cried my pet

[017-139] triumphantly.

[017-140] I tried to look at my pet in the wisest manner, but of course I

[017-141] couldn’t.

[017-142] “Well enough?” I repeated.

[017-143] “Yes,” said Richard, “well enough. It’s rather jog-trotty and

[017-144] humdrum. But it’ll do as well as anything else!”

[017-145] “Oh! My dear Richard!” I remonstrated.

[017-146] “What’s the matter?” said Richard.

[017-147] “Do as well as anything else!”

[017-148] “I don’t think there’s any harm in that, Dame Durden,” said Ada,

[017-149] looking so confidingly at me across him; “because if it will do as

[017-150] well as anything else, it will do very well, I hope.”

[017-151] “Oh, yes, I hope so,” returned Richard, carelessly tossing his hair

[017-152] from his forehead. “After all, it may be only a kind of probation

[017-153] till our suit is—I forgot though. I am not to mention the suit.

[017-154] Forbidden ground! Oh, yes, it’s all right enough. Let us talk about

[017-155] something else.”

[017-156] Ada would have done so willingly, and with a full persuasion that we

[017-157] had brought the question to a most satisfactory state. But I thought

[017-158] it would be useless to stop there, so I began again.

[017-159] “No, but Richard,” said I, “and my dear Ada! Consider how important

[017-160] it is to you both, and what a point of honour it is towards your

[017-161] cousin, that you, Richard, should be quite in earnest without any

[017-162] reservation. I think we had better talk about this, really, Ada. It

[017-163] will be too late very soon.”

[017-164] “Oh, yes! We must talk about it!” said Ada. “But I think Richard is

[017-165] right.”

[017-166] What was the use of my trying to look wise when she was so pretty,

[017-167] and so engaging, and so fond of him!

[017-168] “Mr. and Mrs. Badger were here yesterday, Richard,” said I, “and they

[017-169] seemed disposed to think that you had no great liking for the

[017-170] profession.”

[017-171] “Did they though?” said Richard. “Oh! Well, that rather alters the

[017-172] case, because I had no idea that they thought so, and I should not

[017-173] have liked to disappoint or inconvenience them. The fact is, I don’t

[017-174] care much about it. But, oh, it don’t matter! It’ll do as well as

[017-175] anything else!”

[017-176] “You hear him, Ada!” said I.

[017-177] “The fact is,” Richard proceeded, half thoughtfully and half

[017-178] jocosely, “it is not quite in my way. I don’t take to it. And I get

[017-179] too much of Mrs. Bayham Badger’s first and second.”

[017-180] “I am sure THAT’S very natural!” cried Ada, quite delighted. “The

[017-181] very thing we both said yesterday, Esther!”

[017-182] “Then,” pursued Richard, “it’s monotonous, and to-day is too like

[017-183] yesterday, and to-morrow is too like to-day.”

[017-184] “But I am afraid,” said I, “this is an objection to all kinds of

[017-185] application—to life itself, except under some very uncommon

[017-186] circumstances.”

[017-187] “Do you think so?” returned Richard, still considering. “Perhaps! Ha!

[017-188] Why, then, you know,” he added, suddenly becoming gay again, “we

[017-189] travel outside a circle to what I said just now. It’ll do as well as

[017-190] anything else. Oh, it’s all right enough! Let us talk about something

[017-191] else.”

[017-192] But even Ada, with her loving face—and if it had seemed innocent and

[017-193] trusting when I first saw it in that memorable November [[fog-motif|fog]], how much

[017-194] more did it seem now when I knew her innocent and trusting

[017-195] heart—even Ada shook her head at this and looked serious. So I

[017-196] thought it a good opportunity to hint to Richard that if he were

[017-197] sometimes a little careless of himself, I was very sure he never

[017-198] meant to be careless of Ada, and that it was a part of his

[017-199] affectionate consideration for her not to slight the importance of a

[017-200] step that might influence both their lives. This made him almost

[017-201] grave.

[017-202] “My dear Mother Hubbard,” he said, “that’s the very thing! I have

[017-203] thought of that several times and have been quite angry with myself

[017-204] for meaning to be so much in earnest and—somehow—not exactly being

[017-205] so. I don’t know how it is; I seem to want something or other to

[017-206] stand by. Even you have no idea how fond I am of Ada (my darling

[017-207] cousin, I love you, so much!), but I don’t settle down to constancy

[017-208] in other things. It’s such uphill work, and it takes such a time!”

[017-209] said Richard with an air of vexation.

[017-210] “That may be,” I suggested, “because you don’t like what you have

[017-211] chosen.”

[017-212] “Poor fellow!” said Ada. “I am sure I don’t wonder at it!”

[017-213] No. It was not of the least use my trying to look wise. I tried

[017-214] again, but how could I do it, or how could it have any effect if I

[017-215] could, while Ada rested her clasped hands upon his shoulder and while

[017-216] he looked at her tender blue eyes, and while they looked at him!

[017-217] “You see, my precious girl,” said Richard, passing her golden curls

[017-218] through and through his hand, “I was a little hasty perhaps; or I

[017-219] misunderstood my own inclinations perhaps. They don’t seem to lie in

[017-220] that direction. I couldn’t tell till I tried. Now the question is

[017-221] whether it’s worth-while to undo all that has been done. It seems

[017-222] like making a great disturbance about nothing particular.”

[017-223] “My dear Richard,” said I, “how CAN you say about nothing

[017-224] particular?”

[017-225] “I don’t mean absolutely that,” he returned. “I mean that it MAY be

[017-226] nothing particular because I may never want it.”

[017-227] Both Ada and I urged, in reply, not only that it was decidedly

[017-228] worth-while to undo what had been done, but that it must be undone. I

[017-229] then asked Richard whether he had thought of any more congenial

[017-230] pursuit.

[017-231] “There, my dear Mrs. Shipton,” said Richard, “you touch me home. Yes,

[017-232] I have. I have been thinking that the law is the boy for me.”

[017-233] “The law!” repeated Ada as if she were afraid of the name.

[017-234] “If I went into Kenge’s office,” said Richard, “and if I were placed

[017-235] under articles to Kenge, I should have my eye on the—hum!—the

[017-236] forbidden ground—and should be able to study it, and master it, and

[017-237] to satisfy myself that it was not neglected and was being properly

[017-238] conducted. I should be able to look after Ada’s interests and my own

[017-239] interests (the same thing!); and I should peg away at Blackstone and

[017-240] all those fellows with the most tremendous ardour.”

[017-241] I was not by any means so sure of that, and I saw how his hankering

[017-242] after the vague things yet to come of those long-deferred hopes cast

[017-243] a shade on Ada’s face. But I thought it best to encourage him in any

[017-244] project of continuous exertion, and only advised him to be quite sure

[017-245] that his mind was made up now.

[017-246] “My dear Minerva,” said Richard, “I am as steady as you are. I made a

[017-247] mistake; we are all liable to mistakes; I won’t do so any more, and

[017-248] I’ll become such a lawyer as is not often seen. That is, you know,”

[017-249] said Richard, relapsing into doubt, “if it really is worth-while,

[017-250] after all, to make such a disturbance about nothing particular!”

[017-251] This led to our saying again, with a great deal of gravity, all that

[017-252] we had said already and to our coming to much the same conclusion

[017-253] afterwards. But we so strongly advised Richard to be frank and open

[017-254] with Mr. Jarndyce, without a moment’s delay, and his disposition was

[017-255] naturally so opposed to concealment that he sought him out at once

[017-256] (taking us with him) and made a full avowal. “Rick,” said my

[017-257] guardian, after hearing him attentively, “we can retreat with honour,

[017-258] and we will. But we must be careful—for our cousin’s sake, Rick, for

[017-259] our cousin’s sake—that we make no more such mistakes. Therefore, in

[017-260] the matter of the law, we will have a good trial before we decide. We

[017-261] will look before we leap, and take plenty of time about it.”

[017-262] Richard’s energy was of such an impatient and fitful kind that he

[017-263] would have liked nothing better than to have gone to Mr. Kenge’s

[017-264] office in that hour and to have entered into articles with him on the

[017-265] spot. Submitting, however, with a good grace to the caution that we

[017-266] had shown to be so necessary, he contented himself with sitting down

[017-267] among us in his lightest spirits and talking as if his one unvarying

[017-268] purpose in life from childhood had been that one which now held

[017-269] possession of him. My guardian was very kind and cordial with him,

[017-270] but rather grave, enough so to cause Ada, when he had departed and we

[017-271] were going upstairs to bed, to say, “Cousin John, I hope you don’t

[017-272] think the worse of Richard?”

[017-273] “No, my love,” said he.

[017-274] “Because it was very natural that Richard should be mistaken in such

[017-275] a difficult case. It is not uncommon.”

[017-276] “No, no, my love,” said he. “Don’t look unhappy.”

[017-277] “Oh, I am not unhappy, cousin John!” said Ada, smiling cheerfully,

[017-278] with her hand upon his shoulder, where she had put it in bidding him

[017-279] good night. “But I should be a little so if you thought at all the

[017-280] worse of Richard.”

[017-281] “My dear,” said Mr. Jarndyce, “I should think the worse of him only

[017-282] if you were ever in the least unhappy through his means. I should be

[017-283] more disposed to quarrel with myself even then, than with poor Rick,

[017-284] for I brought you together. But, tut, all this is nothing! He has

[017-285] time before him, and the race to run. I think the worse of him? Not

[017-286] I, my loving cousin! And not you, I swear!”

[017-287] “No, indeed, cousin John,” said Ada, “I am sure I could not—I am

[017-288] sure I would not—think any ill of Richard if the whole world did. I

[017-289] could, and I would, think better of him then than at any other time!”

[017-290] So quietly and honestly she said it, with her hands upon his

[017-291] shoulders—both hands now—and looking up into his face, like the

[017-292] picture of truth!

[017-293] “I think,” said my guardian, thoughtfully regarding her, “I think it

[017-294] must be somewhere written that the virtues of the mothers shall

[017-295] occasionally be visited on the children, as well as the sins of the

[017-296] father. Good night, my rosebud. Good night, little woman. Pleasant

[017-297] slumbers! Happy dreams!”

[017-298] This was the first time I ever saw him follow Ada with his eyes with

[017-299] something of a shadow on their benevolent expression. I well

[017-300] remembered the look with which he had contemplated her and Richard

[017-301] when she was singing in the firelight; it was but a very little while

[017-302] since he had watched them passing down the room in which the sun was

[017-303] shining, and away into the shade; but his glance was changed, and

[017-304] even the silent look of confidence in me which now followed it once

[017-305] more was not quite so hopeful and untroubled as it had originally

[017-306] been.

[017-307] Ada praised Richard more to me that night than ever she had praised

[017-308] him yet. She went to sleep with a little bracelet he had given her

[017-309] clasped upon her arm. I fancied she was dreaming of him when I kissed

[017-310] her cheek after she had slept an hour and saw how tranquil and happy

[017-311] she looked.

[017-312] For I was so little inclined to sleep myself that night that I sat up

[017-313] working. It would not be worth mentioning for its own sake, but I was

[017-314] wakeful and rather low-spirited. I don’t know why. At least I don’t

[017-315] think I know why. At least, perhaps I do, but I don’t think it

[017-316] matters.

[017-317] At any rate, I made up my mind to be so dreadfully industrious that I

[017-318] would leave myself not a moment’s leisure to be low-spirited. For I

[017-319] naturally said, “Esther! You to be low-spirited. YOU!” And it really

[017-320] was time to say so, for I—yes, I really did see myself in the glass,

[017-321] almost crying. “As if you had anything to make you unhappy, instead

[017-322] of everything to make you happy, you ungrateful heart!” said I.

[017-323] If I could have made myself go to sleep, I would have done it

[017-324] directly, but not being able to do that, I took out of my basket some

[017-325] ornamental work for our house (I mean [[bleak-house|Bleak House]]) that I was busy

[017-326] with at that time and sat down to it with great determination. It was

[017-327] necessary to count all the stitches in that work, and I resolved to

[017-328] go on with it until I couldn’t keep my eyes open, and then to go to

[017-329] bed.

[017-330] I soon found myself very busy. But I had left some silk downstairs in

[017-331] a work-table drawer in the temporary growlery, and coming to a stop

[017-332] for want of it, I took my candle and went softly down to get it. To

[017-333] my great surprise, on going in I found my guardian still there, and

[017-334] sitting looking at the ashes. He was lost in thought, his book lay

[017-335] unheeded by his side, his silvered iron-grey hair was scattered

[017-336] confusedly upon his forehead as though his hand had been wandering

[017-337] among it while his thoughts were elsewhere, and his face looked worn.

[017-338] Almost frightened by coming upon him so unexpectedly, I stood still

[017-339] for a moment and should have retired without speaking had he not, in

[017-340] again passing his hand abstractedly through his hair, seen me and

[017-341] started.

[017-342] “Esther!”

[017-343] I told him what I had come for.

[017-344] “At work so late, my dear?”

[017-345] “I am working late to-night,” said I, “because I couldn’t sleep and

[017-346] wished to tire myself. But, dear guardian, you are late too, and look

[017-347] weary. You have no trouble, I hope, to keep you waking?”

[017-348] “None, little woman, that YOU would readily understand,” said he.

[017-349] He spoke in a regretful tone so new to me that I inwardly repeated,

[017-350] as if that would help me to his meaning, “That I could readily

[017-351] understand!”

[017-352] “Remain a moment, Esther,” said he, “You were in my thoughts.”

[017-353] “I hope I was not the trouble, guardian?”

[017-354] He slightly waved his hand and fell into his usual manner. The change

[017-355] was so remarkable, and he appeared to make it by dint of so much

[017-356] self-command, that I found myself again inwardly repeating, “None

[017-357] that I could understand!”

[017-358] “Little woman,” said my guardian, “I was thinking—that is, I have

[017-359] been thinking since I have been sitting here—that you ought to know

[017-360] of your own history all I know. It is very little. Next to nothing.”

[017-361] “Dear guardian,” I replied, “when you spoke to me before on that

[017-362] subject—”

[017-363] “But since then,” he gravely interposed, anticipating what I meant to

[017-364] say, “I have reflected that your having anything to ask me, and my

[017-365] having anything to tell you, are different considerations, Esther. It

[017-366] is perhaps my duty to impart to you the little I know.”

[017-367] “If you think so, guardian, it is right.”

[017-368] “I think so,” he returned very gently, and kindly, and very

[017-369] distinctly. “My dear, I think so now. If any real disadvantage can

[017-370] attach to your position in the mind of any man or woman worth a

[017-371] thought, it is right that you at least of all the world should not

[017-372] magnify it to yourself by having vague impressions of its nature.”

[017-373] I sat down and said after a little effort to be as calm as I ought to

[017-374] be, “One of my earliest remembrances, guardian, is of these words:

[017-375] ‘Your mother, Esther, is your disgrace, and you were hers. The time

[017-376] will come, and soon enough, when you will understand this better, and

[017-377] will feel it too, as no one save a woman can.’” I had covered my face

[017-378] with my hands in repeating the words, but I took them away now with a
