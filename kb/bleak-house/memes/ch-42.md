[042-001] From the verdant undulations and the spreading oaks of the Dedlock

[042-002] [[inheritance-and-property|property]], [[mr-tulkinghorn|Mr. Tulkinghorn]] transfers himself to the stale heat and

[042-003] dust of [[london|London]]. His manner of coming and going between the two places

[042-004] is one of his impenetrabilities. He walks into [[chesney-wold|Chesney Wold]] as if it

[042-005] were next door to his chambers and returns to his chambers as if he

[042-006] had never been out of Lincoln’s Inn Fields. He neither changes his

[042-007] dress before the journey nor talks of it afterwards. He melted out of

[042-008] his turret-room this morning, just as now, in the late twilight, he

[042-009] melts into his own square.

[042-010] Like a dingy London bird among the birds at roost in these pleasant

[042-011] fields, where the sheep are all made into parchment, the goats into

[042-012] wigs, and the pasture into chaff, the lawyer, smoke-dried and faded,

[042-013] dwelling among mankind but not consorting with them, aged without

[042-014] experience of genial youth, and so long used to make his cramped nest

[042-015] in holes and corners of human nature that he has forgotten its

[042-016] broader and better range, comes sauntering home. In the oven made by

[042-017] the hot pavements and hot buildings, he has baked himself dryer than

[042-018] usual; and he has in his thirsty mind his mellowed port-wine half a

[042-019] century old.

[042-020] The lamplighter is skipping up and down his ladder on Mr.

[042-021] Tulkinghorn’s side of the Fields when that high-priest of noble

[042-022] mysteries arrives at his own dull court-yard. He ascends the

[042-023] door-steps and is gliding into the dusky hall when he encounters, on

[042-024] the top step, a bowing and propitiatory little man.

[042-025] “Is that Snagsby?”

[042-026] “Yes, sir. I hope you are well, sir. I was just giving you up, sir,

[042-027] and going home.”

[042-028] “Aye? What is it? What do you want with me?”

[042-029] “Well, sir,” says Mr. Snagsby, holding his hat at the side of his

[042-030] head in his deference towards his best customer, “I was wishful to

[042-031] say a word to you, sir.”

[042-032] “Can you say it here?”

[042-033] “Perfectly, sir.”

[042-034] “Say it then.” The lawyer turns, leans his arms on the iron railing

[042-035] at the top of the steps, and looks at the lamplighter lighting the

[042-036] court-yard.

[042-037] “It is relating,” says Mr. Snagsby in a mysterious low voice, “it is

[042-038] relating—not to put too fine a point upon it—to the foreigner,

[042-039] sir!”

[042-040] Mr. Tulkinghorn eyes him with some surprise. “What foreigner?”

[042-041] “The foreign female, sir. French, if I don’t mistake? I am not

[042-042] acquainted with that language myself, but I should judge from her

[042-043] manners and appearance that she was French; anyways, certainly

[042-044] foreign. Her that was upstairs, sir, when Mr. Bucket and me had the

[042-045] honour of waiting upon you with the sweeping-boy that night.”

[042-046] “Oh! Yes, yes. Mademoiselle Hortense.”

[042-047] “Indeed, sir?” Mr. Snagsby coughs his cough of submission behind his

[042-048] hat. “I am not acquainted myself with the names of foreigners in

[042-049] general, but I have no doubt it WOULD be that.” Mr. Snagsby appears

[042-050] to have set out in this reply with some desperate design of repeating

[042-051] the name, but on reflection coughs again to excuse himself.

[042-052] “And what can you have to say, Snagsby,” demands Mr. Tulkinghorn,

[042-053] “about her?”

[042-054] “Well, sir,” returns the stationer, shading his communication with

[042-055] his hat, “it falls a little hard upon me. My domestic happiness is

[042-056] very great—at least, it’s as great as can be expected, I’m sure—but

[042-057] my little woman is rather given to jealousy. Not to put too fine a

[042-058] point upon it, she is very much given to jealousy. And you see, a

[042-059] foreign female of that genteel appearance coming into the shop, and

[042-060] hovering—I should be the last to make use of a strong expression if

[042-061] I could avoid it, but hovering, sir—in the court—you know it

[042-062] is—now ain’t it? I only put it to yourself, sir.”

[042-063] Mr. Snagsby, having said this in a very plaintive manner, throws in a

[042-064] cough of general application to fill up all the blanks.

[042-065] “Why, what do you mean?” asks Mr. Tulkinghorn.

[042-066] “Just so, sir,” returns Mr. Snagsby; “I was sure you would feel it

[042-067] yourself and would excuse the reasonableness of MY feelings when

[042-068] coupled with the known excitableness of my little woman. You see, the

[042-069] foreign female—which you mentioned her name just now, with quite a

[042-070] native sound I am sure—caught up the word Snagsby that night, being

[042-071] uncommon quick, and made inquiry, and got the direction and come at

[042-072] dinner-time. Now Guster, our young woman, is timid and has fits, and

[042-073] she, taking fright at the foreigner’s looks—which are fierce—and at

[042-074] a grinding manner that she has of speaking—which is calculated to

[042-075] alarm a weak mind—gave way to it, instead of bearing up against it,

[042-076] and tumbled down the kitchen stairs out of one into another, such

[042-077] fits as I do sometimes think are never gone into, or come out of, in

[042-078] any house but ours. Consequently there was by good fortune ample

[042-079] occupation for my little woman, and only me to answer the shop. When

[042-080] she DID say that Mr. Tulkinghorn, being always denied to her by his

[042-081] employer (which I had no doubt at the time was a foreign mode of

[042-082] viewing a clerk), she would do herself the pleasure of continually

[042-083] calling at my place until she was let in here. Since then she has

[042-084] been, as I began by saying, hovering, hovering, sir”—Mr. Snagsby

[042-085] repeats the word with pathetic emphasis—“in the court. The effects

[042-086] of which movement it is impossible to calculate. I shouldn’t wonder

[042-087] if it might have already given rise to the painfullest mistakes even

[042-088] in the neighbours’ minds, not mentioning (if such a thing was

[042-089] possible) my little woman. Whereas, goodness knows,” says Mr.

[042-090] Snagsby, shaking his head, “I never had an idea of a foreign female,

[042-091] except as being formerly connected with a bunch of brooms and a baby,

[042-092] or at the present time with a tambourine and earrings. I never had, I

[042-093] do assure you, sir!”

[042-094] Mr. Tulkinghorn had listened gravely to this complaint and inquires

[042-095] when the stationer has finished, “And that’s all, is it, Snagsby?”

[042-096] “Why yes, sir, that’s all,” says Mr. Snagsby, ending with a cough

[042-097] that plainly adds, “and it’s enough too—for me.”

[042-098] “I don’t know what Mademoiselle Hortense may want or mean, unless she

[042-099] is mad,” says the lawyer.

[042-100] “Even if she was, you know, sir,” Mr. Snagsby pleads, “it wouldn’t be

[042-101] a consolation to have some weapon or another in the form of a foreign

[042-102] dagger planted in the family.”

[042-103] “No,” says the other. “Well, well! This shall be stopped. I am sorry

[042-104] you have been inconvenienced. If she comes again, send her here.”

[042-105] Mr. Snagsby, with much bowing and short apologetic coughing, takes

[042-106] his leave, lightened in heart. Mr. Tulkinghorn goes upstairs, saying

[042-107] to himself, “These women were created to give trouble the whole earth

[042-108] over. The mistress not being enough to deal with, here’s the maid

[042-109] now! But I will be short with THIS jade at least!”

[042-110] So saying, he unlocks his door, gropes his way into his murky rooms,

[042-111] lights his candles, and looks about him. It is too dark to see much

[042-112] of the Allegory overhead there, but that importunate Roman, who is

[042-113] for ever toppling out of the clouds and pointing, is at his old work

[042-114] pretty distinctly. Not honouring him with much attention, Mr.

[042-115] Tulkinghorn takes a small key from his pocket, unlocks a drawer in

[042-116] which there is another key, which unlocks a chest in which there is

[042-117] another, and so comes to the cellar-key, with which he prepares to

[042-118] descend to the regions of old wine. He is going towards the door with

[042-119] a candle in his hand when a knock comes.

[042-120] “Who’s this? Aye, aye, mistress, it’s you, is it? You appear at a

[042-121] good time. I have just been hearing of you. Now! What do you want?”

[042-122] He stands the candle on the chimney-piece in the clerk’s hall and

[042-123] taps his dry cheek with the key as he addresses these words of

[042-124] welcome to Mademoiselle Hortense. That feline personage, with her

[042-125] lips tightly shut and her eyes looking out at him sideways, softly

[042-126] closes the door before replying.

[042-127] “I have had great deal of trouble to find you, sir.”

[042-128] “HAVE you!”

[042-129] “I have been here very often, sir. It has always been said to me, he

[042-130] is not at home, he is engage, he is this and that, he is not for

[042-131] you.”

[042-132] “Quite right, and quite true.”

[042-133] “Not true. Lies!”

[042-134] At times there is a suddenness in the manner of Mademoiselle Hortense

[042-135] so like a bodily spring upon the subject of it that such subject

[042-136] involuntarily starts and falls back. It is Mr. Tulkinghorn’s case at

[042-137] present, though Mademoiselle Hortense, with her eyes almost shut up

[042-138] (but still looking out sideways), is only smiling contemptuously and

[042-139] shaking her head.

[042-140] “Now, mistress,” says the lawyer, tapping the key hastily upon the

[042-141] chimney-piece. “If you have anything to say, say it, say it.”

[042-142] “Sir, you have not use me well. You have been mean and shabby.”

[042-143] “Mean and shabby, eh?” returns the lawyer, rubbing his nose with the

[042-144] key.

[042-145] “Yes. What is it that I tell you? You know you have. You have

[042-146] attrapped me—catched me—to give you information; you have asked me

[042-147] to show you the dress of mine my Lady must have wore that night, you

[042-148] have prayed me to come in it here to meet that boy. Say! Is it not?”

[042-149] Mademoiselle Hortense makes another spring.

[042-150] “You are a vixen, a vixen!” Mr. Tulkinghorn seems to meditate as he

[042-151] looks distrustfully at her, then he replies, “Well, wench, well. I

[042-152] paid you.”

[042-153] “You paid me!” she repeats with fierce disdain. “Two sovereign! I

[042-154] have not change them, I re-fuse them, I des-pise them, I throw them

[042-155] from me!” Which she literally does, taking them out of her bosom as

[042-156] she speaks and flinging them with such violence on the floor that

[042-157] they jerk up again into the light before they roll away into corners

[042-158] and slowly settle down there after spinning vehemently.

[042-159] “Now!” says Mademoiselle Hortense, darkening her large eyes again.

[042-160] “You have paid me? Eh, my God, oh yes!”

[042-161] Mr. Tulkinghorn rubs his head with the key while she entertains

[042-162] herself with a sarcastic laugh.

[042-163] “You must be rich, my fair friend,” he composedly observes, “to throw

[042-164] money about in that way!”

[042-165] “I AM rich,” she returns. “I am very rich in hate. I hate my Lady, of

[042-166] all my heart. You know that.”

[042-167] “Know it? How should I know it?”

[042-168] “Because you have known it perfectly before you prayed me to give you

[042-169] that information. Because you have known perfectly that I was

[042-170] en-r-r-r-raged!” It appears impossible for mademoiselle to roll the

[042-171] letter “r” sufficiently in this word, notwithstanding that she

[042-172] assists her energetic delivery by clenching both her hands and

[042-173] setting all her teeth.

[042-174] “Oh! I knew that, did I?” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, examining the wards

[042-175] of the key.

[042-176] “Yes, without doubt. I am not blind. You have made sure of me because

[042-177] you knew that. You had reason! I det-est her.” Mademoiselle Hortense folds her

[042-178] arms and throws this last remark at him over one of her shoulders.

[042-179] “Having said this, have you anything else to say, mademoiselle?”

[042-180] “I am not yet placed. Place me well. Find me a good condition! If you

[042-181] cannot, or do not choose to do that, employ me to pursue her, to

[042-182] chase her, to disgrace and to dishonour her. I will help you well,

[042-183] and with a good will. It is what YOU do. Do I not know that?”

[042-184] “You appear to know a good deal,” Mr. Tulkinghorn retorts.

[042-185] “Do I not? Is it that I am so weak as to believe, like a child, that

[042-186] I come here in that dress to rec-eive that boy only to decide a

[042-187] little bet, a wager? Eh, my God, oh yes!” In this reply, down to the

[042-188] word “wager” inclusive, mademoiselle has been ironically polite and

[042-189] tender, then as suddenly dashed into the bitterest and most defiant

[042-190] scorn, with her black eyes in one and the same moment very nearly

[042-191] shut and staringly wide open.

[042-192] “Now, let us see,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, tapping his chin with the

[042-193] key and looking imperturbably at her, “how this matter stands.”

[042-194] “Ah! Let us see,” mademoiselle assents, with many angry and tight

[042-195] nods of her head.

[042-196] “You come here to make a remarkably modest demand, which you have

[042-197] just stated, and it not being conceded, you will come again.”

[042-198] “And again,” says mademoiselle with more tight and angry nods. “And

[042-199] yet again. And yet again. And many times again. In effect, for ever!”

[042-200] “And not only here, but you will go to Mr. Snagsby’s too, perhaps?

[042-201] That visit not succeeding either, you will go again perhaps?”

[042-202] “And again,” repeats mademoiselle, cataleptic with determination.

[042-203] “And yet again. And yet again. And many times again. In effect, for

[042-204] ever!”

[042-205] “Very well. Now, Mademoiselle Hortense, let me recommend you to take

[042-206] the candle and pick up that money of yours. I think you will find it

[042-207] behind the clerk’s partition in the corner yonder.”

[042-208] She merely throws a laugh over her shoulder and stands her ground

[042-209] with folded arms.

[042-210] “You will not, eh?”

[042-211] “No, I will not!”

[042-212] “So much the poorer you; so much the richer I! Look, mistress, this

[042-213] is the key of my wine-cellar. It is a large key, but the keys of

[042-214] prisons are larger. In this city there are houses of correction

[042-215] (where the treadmills are, for women), the gates of which are very

[042-216] strong and heavy, and no doubt the keys too. I am afraid a lady of

[042-217] your spirit and activity would find it an inconvenience to have one

[042-218] of those keys turned upon her for any length of time. What do you

[042-219] think?”

[042-220] “I think,” mademoiselle replies without any action and in a clear,

[042-221] obliging voice, “that you are a miserable wretch.”

[042-222] “Probably,” returns Mr. Tulkinghorn, quietly blowing his nose. “But I

[042-223] don’t ask what you think of myself; I ask what you think of the

[042-224] prison.”

[042-225] “Nothing. What does it matter to me?”

[042-226] “Why, it matters this much, mistress,” says the lawyer, deliberately

[042-227] putting away his handkerchief and adjusting his frill; “the law is so

[042-228] despotic here that it interferes to prevent any of our good English

[042-229] citizens from being troubled, even by a lady’s visits against his

[042-230] desire. And on his complaining that he is so troubled, it takes hold

[042-231] of the troublesome lady and shuts her up in prison under hard

[042-232] discipline. Turns the key upon her, mistress.” Illustrating with the

[042-233] cellar-key.

[042-234] “Truly?” returns mademoiselle in the same pleasant voice. “That is

[042-235] droll! But—my faith!—still what does it matter to me?”

[042-236] “My fair friend,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, “make another visit here, or

[042-237] at Mr. Snagsby’s, and you shall learn.”

[042-238] “In that case you will send me to the prison, perhaps?”

[042-239] “Perhaps.”

[042-240] It would be contradictory for one in mademoiselle’s state of

[042-241] agreeable jocularity to foam at the mouth, otherwise a tigerish

[042-242] expansion thereabouts might look as if a very little more would make

[042-243] her do it.

[042-244] “In a word, mistress,” says Mr. Tulkinghorn, “I am sorry to be

[042-245] unpolite, but if you ever present yourself uninvited here—or

[042-246] there—again, I will give you over to the police. Their gallantry is

[042-247] great, but they carry troublesome people through the streets in an

[042-248] ignominious manner, strapped down on a board, my good wench.”

[042-249] “I will prove you,” whispers mademoiselle, stretching out her hand,

[042-250] “I will try if you dare to do it!”

[042-251] “And if,” pursues the lawyer without minding her, “I place you in

[042-252] that good condition of being locked up in jail, it will be some time

[042-253] before you find yourself at liberty again.”

[042-254] “I will prove you,” repeats mademoiselle in her former whisper.

[042-255] “And now,” proceeds the lawyer, still without minding her, “you had

[042-256] better go. Think twice before you come here again.”

[042-257] “Think you,” she answers, “twice two hundred times!”

[042-258] “You were dismissed by your lady, you know,” Mr. Tulkinghorn

[042-259] observes, following her out upon the staircase, “as the most

[042-260] implacable and unmanageable of women. Now turn over a new leaf and

[042-261] take warning by what I say to you. For what I say, I mean; and what I

[042-262] threaten, I will do, mistress.”

[042-263] She goes down without answering or looking behind her. When she is

[042-264] gone, he goes down too, and returning with his cobweb-covered bottle,

[042-265] devotes himself to a leisurely enjoyment of its contents, now and

[042-266] then, as he throws his head back in his chair, catching sight of the

[042-267] pertinacious Roman pointing from the ceiling.
