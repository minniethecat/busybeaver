## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Fisher belongs to the small constellation of servants who populate the earliest pages of the novel, when [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is still an infant and the world is perceived through a child's physical sensations. As housekeeper at [[five-elms|Five Elms]] — the home of [[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]] and [[uncle-edward|Uncle Edward]] — she is one of the first figures outside the immediate family whom [[mary-olivier|Mary]] learns to navigate, and her portrayal embodies the child's unanalytic fascination with adult bodies. For the infant [[mary-olivier|Mary]], Mrs. Fisher is a source of comfort, peppermint balls, and a certain alarming physical magnificence.

Her narrative function is to supply domestic warmth in a household that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] instinctively understands to be emotionally ambiguous — a place where people "pretended" to like each other. Mrs. Fisher's kind, twinkling face and her fat, simmering smile offer [[mary-olivier|Mary]] an uncomplicated welcome that contrasts with the strained affections of [[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]] and [[uncle-edward|Uncle Edward]].

## Biography

Mrs. Fisher first appears in the servants' hall at [[five-elms|Five Elms]], standing with Rose, Annie, Cook, and Giles the footman, all of them staring at the infant [[mary-olivier|Mary]] "in a queer, kind way" (003-157). She is the housekeeper, responsible for the household's daily running, and her special charge during [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s visits is the child herself.

During [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s stay at [[five-elms|Five Elms]], it is Mrs. Fisher who "dressed and undressed you" (004-008). The child's consciousness registers her primarily through physical impressions: "Mrs. Fisher was large and rather like [[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]], only softer and more bulging. Her round face had a high red polish on it always, and when she saw you coming her eyes twinkled, and her red forehead and her big cheeks and her mouth smiled all together a fat, simmering smile" (004-011). The description is affectionate and slightly comic, the child's eye noting surface and volume without moral judgement.

She also provides distraction. When post-lunch restlessness threatens, "Mrs. Fisher took you up into her bedroom to see her dress" (004-023). The bedroom has "a smell of rotten apples" that "oozed through the rosebud pattern on the walls" (004-024). Mrs. Fisher gives [[mary-olivier|Mary]] peppermint balls to suck while she changes, and the child watches the ritual transformation: "You watched for the moment when the brooch with the black tomb and the weeping willow on it was undone and Mrs. Fisher's chin came out first by the open collar and Mrs. Fisher began to swell. When she stood up in her petticoat and bodice she was enormous; her breasts and hips and her great arms shook as she walked about the room" (004-024). The brooch — a [[funerals|mourning]] brooch with its black tomb and weeping willow — is a small, telling detail: even the comfortably bulging Mrs. Fisher carries a private grief.

When [[mary-olivier|Mary]] leaves [[five-elms|Five Elms]], she is "sorry when she said goodbye to [[uncle-edward|Uncle Edward]] and [[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]] and Mrs. Fisher" (004-025). But later, when she is older and has learned the family's emotional politics, she is made to understand that she ought to dislike these figures. Pressed by Roddy, she cries: "'I hate Pidgeon. I hate [[uncle-edward|Uncle Edward]] and [[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]]. I hate Mrs. Fisher'" (004-060). The declaration, like all coerced avowals in this novel, rings hollow; the real feeling — the child's unjudging acceptance of the large, kind, peppermint-giving housekeeper — persists beneath it.

## Relationships

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: Mrs. Fisher is one of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest caretakers outside her immediate family. The child finds her physically fascinating and emotionally benign; the forced rejection of her in Chapter 4 is one of the novel's first instances of a child being made to adopt adult antipathies.

**[[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]]**: As [[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]]'s housekeeper, Mrs. Fisher operates within the [[five-elms|Five Elms]] household hierarchy. She is described as resembling [[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]] physically — "large and rather like [[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]], only softer and more bulging" — suggesting a parallel between the mistress and her servant that the child's eye registers but cannot analyse.
