## Role in the Novel

Mr. Batty belongs to the clerical trio — Batty, Farmer, [[mr-propart|Propart]] — who define the religious landscape of the Olivier family's [[ilford|Ilford]] years. His function is to embody a version of clerical manhood against which the Olivier children, particularly [[mark-olivier|Mark]], measure their father and their own emerging scepticism. Where [[mr-propart|Mr. Propart]] is faintly comic and [[mr-farmer|Mr. Farmer]] is physically unsettling, Mr. Batty is merely ordinary — and it is his ordinariness that provokes the sharpest critique, for it suggests that the Church's authority rests on men no more remarkable than anyone else.

## Biography

Mr. Batty is introduced as "the Vicar of [[barkingside|Barkingside]], who took his two boys out fishing and taught them to tie trout-flies, and was so nice to them that they were actually proud of them" (009-032). The detail of the trout-flies, concrete and homely, establishes him as a father who is competent in the small masculine arts. The phrase "actually proud of them" registers [[mark-olivier|Mark]]'s surprise: paternal pride, in the Olivier household, is not a given.

His daughter Isabel is a childhood friend of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s: "when [[isabel-batty|Isabel Batty]] came to tea. Isabel was almost as good as going to [[ilford|Ilford]]" (010-011). The Batty household is one of the destinations [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is allowed: "It was all right about her [[god-and-religion|religion]] if she went to Mr. Batty's [[church-and-chapel|church]] or [[mr-farmer|Mr. Farmer]]'s or [[mr-propart|Mr. Propart]]'s" (012-003). The equivalence — any [[church-and-chapel|church]] will do, so long as it is not [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s Calvinist [[church-and-chapel|chapel]] — reveals the family's religious nominalism.

Mr. Batty is a visitor to the Olivier household: "or Mr. Batty would come in alone" (013-004), suggesting a social intimacy that extends beyond the clerical call. But [[mark-olivier|Mark]]'s judgement is severe: he cannot reconcile the vicar's ordinariness with his office. "He had read missionary books about people like Mr. Batty and [[mr-propart|Mr. Propart]] and their surprising conversions" (013-102). The missionaries' tales, with their dramatic transformations, make the [[ilford|Ilford]] clergy seem tame by comparison.

At the pivotal party scene, Mr. Batty stands among the disapproving adults: "the [[mr-propart|Propart]]s and the Farmers and Mr. Batty, all stiff and disapproving" (024-036), aligned with the forces of convention against [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s emerging independence.

## Relationships

**[[mark-olivier|Mark Olivier]]**: The most important relationship. [[mark-olivier|Mark]] "began trying to behave like Mr. Batty" (009-034) in his determination to be good to his own children someday. But he also sees through the vicar: "It was funny when you thought of Mr. Batty and [[mr-propart|Mr. Propart]] with their little surplices" (015-041). The surplice that should signify sacerdotal authority becomes, in [[mark-olivier|Mark]]'s vision, merely a costume.

**[[isabel-batty|Isabel Batty]]**: His daughter, a childhood friend of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s who appears in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s private catalogue of be[[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]d figures (013-044).

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: Mr. Batty is a background figure in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s religious formation — one of the clerical faces that populate a world of conventional piety from which [[mary-olivier|Mary]] eventually departs.
