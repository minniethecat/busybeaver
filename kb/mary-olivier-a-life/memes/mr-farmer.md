## Role in the Novel

Mr. Farmer is the third member of the [[ilford|Ilford]] clerical trio — Batty, [[mr-propart|Propart]], Farmer — and the one rendered in the most physically specific terms. His role is to embody a version of clerical masculinity that is unthreatening but also unappealing, a man whose physical presence provokes in the adolescent [[mary-olivier|Mary]] a visceral discomfort that the narrative records with clinical precision.

## Biography

Mr. Farmer first appears as a model of domestic devotion: "Mr. Farmer, the curate of [[church-and-chapel|St. Mary's Chapel]], who walked up and down the room all night with the baby" (009-032). The image of the curate pacing with an infant is endearing — a man who performs childcare, who does not delegate the night-watch to his wife. But the endearment does not survive [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s adolescence.

The Farmers are regular visitors to the Olivier household: "Sometimes Mr. and [[mrs-farmer|Mrs. Farmer]] would come in, and Mr. Farmer would play chess with [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] while [[mrs-farmer|Mrs. Farmer]] talked to [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]" (013-004). The chess game with [[emilius-olivier|Emilius Olivier]] places Mr. Farmer in the masculine sphere of the drawing room, parallel to the women's domestic conversation. It is a ritual of provincial sociability, unremarkable in its content but significant in its regularity.

As [[mary-olivier|Mary]] grows older, Mr. Farmer's physical presence becomes oppressive. At a social call, she registers "his close, hatchet nose and straggly beard. Every now and then his small, greenish eyes lifted and looked at you" (016-173). The description — "hatchet nose," "straggly beard," "small, greenish eyes" — is rendered with a precision that borders on cruelty. The verb "looked at you" carries a charge of unwanted attention; the second-person "you" implicates the reader in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s discomfort.

The Farmers' visits become occasions of dread. "Even [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] owned that Mr. Farmer never knew when it was time to go" (016-177), a small social failure — staying too long — that marks him as lacking the intuitive sense of occasion that governs provincial politeness. When Mr. Farmer looks at [[mary-olivier|Mary]] "as if he wanted to say something and couldn't" (016-182), the unarticulated intention is more unsettling than any spoken word could be.

The darkest moment comes when [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] threatens to have Mr. Farmer examine [[mary-olivier|Mary]]: "Because Mr. Farmer will want to examine you" (016-186). The verb "examine" — with its clinical and ecclesiastical overtones — blurs the boundary between pastoral care and physical intrusion. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s desperate wish to "do something to stop Mr. Farmer and [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]" (016-193) captures the adolescent's powerlessness against adult collusion.

At the party that marks a turning point in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s social standing, Mr. Farmer is among the disapproving adults: "the [[mr-propart|Propart]]s and the Farmers and [[mr-batty|Mr. Batty]], all stiff and disapproving" (024-036). He has moved from being a background figure to being an agent of communal judgement.

## Relationships

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: The relationship is defined by [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s physical revulsion and his unreadable attention. Mr. Farmer's "greenish eyes" that "lifted and looked at you" (016-173) represent the adult world's capacity for scrutiny without communication. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s discomfort is never named as anything more than discomfort, but its intensity is disproportionate to its occasion.

**[[mrs-farmer|Mrs. Farmer]]**: His wife, who "was always having babies" (016-174) and whose soft drawl (016-172) is rendered with phonetic precision on the existing wiki page. The Farmers are a matched pair in the Olivier family's social calendar.

**[[emilius-olivier|Emilius Olivier]] ([[emilius-olivier|Papa]])**: Mr. Farmer's chess partner. The chess games place Mr. Farmer in the category of acceptable male visitors — men with whom [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] can pass an evening without friction.

**[[caroline-olivier|Caroline Olivier]] ([[caroline-olivier|Mamma]])**: [[mrs-farmer|Mrs. Farmer]]'s conversational companion and, more darkly, the parent who threatens [[mary-olivier|Mary]] with Mr. Farmer's examination.

## Characterisation

Mr. Farmer is the most physically specific of the [[ilford|Ilford]] clergy, and the specificity is uniformly unflattering. The "hatchet nose," "straggly beard," and "small, greenish eyes" form a composite that is at once precise and repellent. Sinclair's technique here is focalisation without mitigation: everything we see of Mr. Farmer is filtered through [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s consciousness, and [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s consciousness has no interest in finding him attractive or even tolerable. The result is a portrait that is unfair in the way adolescent perception is unfair — true to the experience, if not necessarily to the man.

The examination threat (016-186) is the scene's most disturbing moment, and it hinges on the ambiguity of "examine." Does [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] mean a medical examination? A religious one? The word hovers between the two, and the hovering is what makes it sinister. Mr. Farmer, as curate, has the authority to examine [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s soul; as a man who "looked at you," he has an interest in examining her body. The conflation of the two is the horror of the scene.
