## Description

Five Elms is the Olivier family's home in [[morfe|Morfe]], [[morfe|Yorkshire]] — a substantial old house with a "long line of red-tiled roof" and a "flat brown face," set in grounds that include a garden, a sumach tree by the front door, and views of the surrounding countryside. [[PN=008-058]] It is the primary setting for Books II through IV of the novel, and its rooms — the nursery, the drawing-room, the library, the spare room — become the topography of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s inner world.

The house's name suggests rootedness and tradition (the five elm trees), but the novel repeatedly hints at its vulnerability: "Some day they'll run a street over Five Elms." [[PN=022-027]] This tension between permanence and threat mirrors the family's own precarious social position.

## Significance in the Narrative

Five Elms is more than setting; it is a character in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s psychological development. The nursery staircase where she imagines [[funerals|coffin]]s; the library (very small) where she discovers philosophy; the garden where [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] and [[uncle-victor|Uncle Victor]] walk and talk; the front door where [[mark-olivier|Mark]] and [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] plant the sumach tree — each space is saturated with emotional memory. [[PN=024-035]] [[PN=019-060]]

The move from [[ilford|Ilford]] to Five Elms marks the transition from [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest infancy to her childhood proper, and the house becomes the container of family dynamics: [[emilius-olivier|Papa]]'s authority, [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s withdrawal, [[mark-olivier|Mark]]'s companionship, [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s solitude. [[PN=008-054]] [[morfe|Morfe]] itself — the village, the Green, the surrounding landscape — offers [[mary-olivier|Mary]] a freedom unavailable within the house's walls. [[PN=024-019]]

## Key Passages

> She saw Five Elms for the first time: the long line of its old red-tiled roof, its flat brown face; the three rows of narrow windows, four at the bottom, with the front door at the end of the row.
> [[PN=008-058]]

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s first sight of the house establishes its physical presence — old, solid, brown, long — which will become the visual ground of her childhood memories.

> "Some day they'll run a street over Five Elms."
> [[PN=022-027]]

A casual remark that registers the fragility of the world [[mary-olivier|Mary]] inhabits: even Five Elms, apparently permanent, is subject to change and erasure.
