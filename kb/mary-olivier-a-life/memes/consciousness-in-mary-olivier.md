# Consciousness in Mary Olivier

**Consciousness** in *[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]: A Life* operates on
multiple registers: as the narrative's primary medium (the novel is
relentlessly focalised through [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s perceptions), as a
psychological theme (the quality and content of awareness across the phases
of a life), and as a philosophical problem (what it means to be conscious of
one[[self-and-identity|self]], of others, and of [[god-and-religion|God]]). Sinclair's modernist technique — the
sustained interior monologue, the absence of authorial commentary — makes
consciousness the novel's subject as much as its method.

## Childhood Self-Consciousness

The novel's opening chapters establish a child's consciousness as intensely
embodied and egocentric. The cot, the iron railing, the curtain — objects
are animated by the child's awareness, and the boundary between [[self-and-identity|self]] and
world is permeable. This early consciousness is marked by vivid sensory
registration but also by the first stirrings of the [[self-and-identity|self]] as a social object:
the dawning awareness of being seen, of being important or unimportant to
others.

The mature [[mary-olivier|Mary]] reflects on this childhood self-consciousness
with ironic precision:

> Her consciousness of being important was intense, exquisite.
> [[PN=017-044]]

The irony lies in the retrospective knowledge that this "importance" was
precarious — dependent on the fleeting attention of adults, vulnerable to
the arrival of a sibling or the withdrawal of parental favour.

## The Consciousness of Others

Sinclair's novel is remarkable for its attention to the *difference* between
consciousnesses. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] becomes aware that other minds are not
simply versions of her own — that they have their own texture, their own
limits, their own opacity. This awareness crystallises around the figures of
[[daniel-olivier|Dan]] and [[uncle-victor|Uncle Victor]].

[[daniel-olivier|Dan]], the father, is characterised by a quality of *unconsciousness* — a
dullness of awareness that paradoxically draws the family's attention to him:

> Their minds fastened on Dan, to get immunity for themselves out of his
> unconsciousness.
> [[PN=025-078]]

The family's "minds" fasten on [[daniel-olivier|Dan]] precisely because his lack of awareness
absolves them of their own. His unconsciousness is a kind of shelter. Yet
the horror is that he might become aware:

> He would be like them, and his consciousness would put a sharper edge on
> theirs.
> [[PN=025-081]]

Consciousness here is figured as a blade — something that cuts, that
sharpens, that makes [[suffering-in-mary-olivier|suffering]] keener. To be unconscious is to be protected;
to be conscious is to be exposed.

[[uncle-victor|Uncle Victor]] represents a different register of
consciousness — educated, reflective, but nonetheless limited:

> In spite of his heavy hand she was aware that Uncle Victor's consciousness
> of these things was different from hers.
> [[PN=025-117]]

The difference between consciousnesses — between [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s and
[[uncle-victor|Uncle Victor]]'s, between hers and [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s,
between hers and [[mark-olivier|Mark]]'s — is one of the novel's persistent
preoccupations. No two minds apprehend the same event in the same way.

## Consciousness of God

The novel's most compressed statement on consciousness comes late, in a
sentence that fuses psychological and theological registers:

> The flash point of freedom was your consciousness of God.
> [[PN=040-046]]

"Flash point" — the temperature at which a vapour ignites — is an
extraordinary metaphor for the [[time-in-mary-olivier|moment]] when consciousness becomes freedom.
The phrase "consciousness of [[god-and-religion|God]]" is deliberately ambiguous: it is both
consciousness *[[home-in-mary-olivier|belonging]] to* [[god-and-religion|God]] (a Spinozist immanence) and consciousness
*directed toward* [[god-and-religion|God]] (a theistic intentionality). In either [[reading-in-mary-olivier|reading]], the
sentence asserts that freedom is not an external condition but a quality of
awareness — a way of being conscious that transforms the conditions it cannot
change.

## Modernist Interiority

Sinclair's narrative method — the sustained interior monologue, the absence
of an omniscient narrator, the refusal to step outside [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s
perceptual field — makes *[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]* a landmark of modernist psychological
fiction. The novel does not describe consciousness from outside; it enacts
consciousness from within. The reader is given no vantage point from which to
judge [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s perceptions because there is no vantage point
outside them. This technique, which Sinclair developed contemporaneously with
Dorothy Richardson and before Virginia Woolf, makes consciousness not merely
a theme but the novel's fundamental ontology — the only [[reality-in-mary-olivier|reality]] to which the
reader has access.
