# Time in Mary Olivier

**Time** in *[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]: A Life* is not a neutral
medium in which events occur but a primary dimension of consciousness,
experienced differently across the phases of a life. May Sinclair traces
time's phenomenology — its felt texture, its variable speed, its relation
to [[memory-in-mary-olivier|memory]] and desire — with the precision she
brings to every register of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s inner experience. The
novel moves between two poles of temporal experience: the plenitude of
time-as-opportunity, stretched open before a self with work to do, and the
erosion of time-as-suffering, tearing past a self imprisoned in
circumstance. Between these poles lies the Spinozist vision of eternity —
the attempt to see things not as they pass in time but "under the form of
eternity."

## Temporal Plenitude

The novel's most sustained evocation of temporal abundance comes during
[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s mature years at Morfe, when the departure of
[[roddy-olivier|Roddy]] and the settling of domestic arrangements open a
space for intellectual work:

> Time stretched out behind and before you, time to read, to make music,
> to make poems in, to translate Euripides, while Mamma looked after her
> flowers in the garden.
> [[PN=031-007]]

The syntax performs its meaning: the comma-spliced list of infinitives —
"to read, to make music, to make poems in, to translate Euripides" —
accumulates without closure, each item opening onto the next in a rhythm of
inexhaustible possibility. Time here is not measured but inhabited, a
medium capacious enough for all of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s vocations. The
detail of [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] absorbed in her gardening — "sowing and
planting and weeding with a fixed, vehement passion" — secures this temporal
abundance by removing [[mary-olivier|Mary]] from domestic obligation. Time
opens when the mother's attention is elsewhere.

## Sub Specie Aeternitatis

The novel's most philosophically ambitious treatment of time draws on
Spinoza's distinction between duration — the succession of moments in which
things come to be and pass away — and eternity, the timeless order of
necessary connections grasped by reason. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s reading of
[[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] provides the conceptual framework, but the novel
tests that framework against the data of lived experience. The articulation
comes in a passage of extraordinary conceptual density:

> Perhaps those were the perfect things, the things that would never pass
> away; they would stay forever, beautiful as you had seen them, fixed in
> their moment of perfection, wearing the very air and light of it forever.
>
> You would see them sub specie ceternitatis.
> Under the form of eternity.
> [[PN=036-116]] [[PN=036-117]]

The passage moves from tentative speculation ("Perhaps") through
affirmation to the authority of Latin — "sub specie ceternitatis" — and
then translates that authority back into English, as if the insight must be
earned in the vernacular. What is "fixed" in eternity is not an abstract
truth but a concrete moment — the "very air and light" of a particular
time, preserved against loss.

The Spinozist framework allows [[mary-olivier|Mary]] to reinterpret her own
history: losses are transformed when seen under the form of eternity because
what was perfect in a moment cannot be undone by what comes after. The
moment with [[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]] in the cornfield, the
moment with [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]] at the piano — these
survive unchanged by the disappointments that followed:

> If Maurice Jourdain had never come back she would always have seen him
> standing in the cornfield. If Lindley Vickers had never come back she
> wouldn't have seen him with Nannie Learoyd in the schoolhouse lane; the
> moment when he held her hands in the drawing-room, standing by the piano,
> would have been their one eternal moment.
> [[PN=036-119]]

The logic is counterfactual but the feeling is real: what "would have been"
their eternal moment in fact is — the conditional mood registers the
possibility of a vision that survives empirical disappointment. The past
that never ended is the past seen *sub specie aeternitatis*.

## The Critique of Eternity

Yet the novel does not rest in the Spinozist consolation. Immediately after
the vision of things under the form of eternity, [[mary-olivier|Mary]]
subjects her own philosophical habits to critique:

> She had spent most of her time in the passionate pursuit of things under
> the form of eternity, regardless of their actual behaviour in time.
> [[PN=031-253]]

"Regardless of their actual behaviour in time" — the phrase is quietly
devastating. The pursuit of eternity has been a form of evasion, a refusal
to attend to the temporal world in its concrete particularity. The
admission registers a turn in the novel's intellectual trajectory: from the
young woman who read [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] as escape to the mature
woman who recognises that a philosophy that discounts time discounts life.

## Time as Suffering

Against the expansiveness of intellectual time, the novel sets the
constriction of time-as-suffering — time experienced not as opportunity but
as force, tearing past a self immobilised in grief or obligation. The return
from travel to the fixity of house and duty:

> It would be you sitting still while time tore by, as it used to tear by
> at Morfe before Richard came, and in the three years after he had gone.
> [[PN=040-006]]

"You sitting still while time tore by" — the image is stark in its
asymmetry: the self fixed, time in violent motion. The verb "tore" is the
novel's most forceful rendering of temporal experience, and it recurs —
time "tore by" at Morfe before [[richard-nicholson|Richard]] arrived, and
"tore by" again after he left. Suffering is inseparable from this sense of
time as a force that happens *to* you rather than a medium you inhabit.

## Temporal Expansion

The novel offers one sustained counter-image to time-as-suffering: the
experience of travel, in which the rapid succession of new places produces a
paradoxical expansion of duration:

> After all that ecstasy of space, that succession of heavenly places with
> singing names: Carcassone and Vezelay; Rome and Florence and San
> Gimignano; Marseilles and Arles and Avignon; filling up time, stretching
> it out, making a long life out of one year.
> [[PN=040-004]]

The place-names accumulate in a litany — "Carcassone and Vezelay; Rome and
Florence and San Gimignano" — each name a portal to a different moment,
each moment expanding the felt duration of the year. The phrase "ecstasy of
space" is precisely chosen: ek-stasis, standing outside oneself, the
displacement of travel as a mode of inhabiting time more fully. "Making a
long life out of one year" names the deepest ambition of temporal
experience — not merely to endure time but to *make* it, to shape duration
into a life dense with experience.
