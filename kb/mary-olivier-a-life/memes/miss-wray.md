## Role and Significance

Miss Wray is a minor character associated with Clevehead, appearing in the novel as a teacher figure who both disciplines and assists the young [[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]. Her name carries a geographical identifier — "Miss Wray of Clevehead" — which anchors her within the regional landscape of the novel and distinguishes her as a person of some local standing. Her interactions with [[mary-olivier|Mary]] reveal the tensions between institutional [[school|education]] and [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s fiercely independent intellectual development.

## Key Appearances

### The Prize-Giving

Miss Wray appears at a school prize-giving ceremony alongside other pupils: "Carrie and little Lena and Kate; Miss Wray with her pale face and angry eyes" [022-002]. Her "pale face" and "angry eyes" suggest either strain or dissatisfaction — the description contrasts with the ceremonial occasion and hints at an inner life that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] registers but does not fully understand. At this same event, when [[mary-olivier|Mary]] receives a prize, Miss Wray remarks that she "knew it was the one" [[mary-olivier|Mary]] deserved [022-021] — a comment that reveals a more perceptive and perhaps supportive side beneath the severe exterior.

### Books from Clevehead

During a later period, when [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is confined or isolated, books arrive that "Miss Wray of Clevehead had procured for her" [027-001]. This act of procuring and sending reading material marks Miss Wray as more than a disciplinarian; she becomes, however distantly, a facilitator of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s intellectual growth. The books themselves are placed on a table by [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s window, where they serve as a connection to the broader world of ideas that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] craves.

## Character and Pedagogy

Miss Wray embodies a particular type of educator in late Victorian fiction: the unmarried woman teacher whose professional identity is entangled with personal disappointment (the "angry eyes") but who nonetheless retains a commitment to the intellectual development of her pupils. Her dual role — critical at the prize-giving, generative in procuring books — mirrors the ambivalent portrayal of formal [[school|education]] throughout the novel, where institutional settings often frustrate [[mary-olivier|Mary]] even as individual teachers (Miss Wray, [[miss-lambert|Miss Lambert]], [[miss-thompson|Miss Thompson]]) open doors to knowledge.
