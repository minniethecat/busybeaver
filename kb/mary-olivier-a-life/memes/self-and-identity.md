## Overview

"Self" in _[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]: A Life_ is the novel's most contested term. The plot of Mary's development is a plot about selfhood: its formation against the pressures of family and religion, its defence against the claims of others, and its eventual dissolution into something larger. The novel moves between two apparently contradictory positions — that the self is "sacred, inviolable" and must be defended, and that "there were no independent, separate entities, no sacred, inviolable selves" — and the tension between them is productive rather than paralysing.

## The Sacred, Inviolable Self

Mary's early understanding of selfhood is oppositional. She conceives of her family as "powerful, but independent and separate entities, in themselves sacred and inviolable, working against you from the outside" [031-258]. Against them, she posits "yourself as a somewhat less powerful, but still independent and separate entity, a sacred, inviolable self, struggling against them for completer freedom and detachment" [031-258].

This model of selfhood is heroic but exhausting — a continuous battle, "crushed down, but always getting up and going on again; fighting a more and more successful battle for your own" [031-258]. It is the self of adolescence: defined by what it resists, dependent on the oppositions that give it shape.

## Selves Are Sacred

The novel's most explicit statement on selfhood comes through Richard Nicholson: "Selves are sacred. You ought to adore them. Anybody's self. Catty's … I used to wonder what the sin against the Holy Ghost was. They told you nobody knew what it was. I know. It's that. Not adoring the self in people. Hating it. Trying to crush it" [029-183].

This is a radical ethical claim: the self is not merely to be respected but adored. The theological language — "sacred," "adore," "the sin against the Holy Ghost" — transposes the problem of selfhood from psychology into theology. To violate another's self is not merely cruel but blasphemous. Richard's formulation includes the self of anyone — "Catty's" — not only the selves of the obviously worthy.

## The Dissolution of Separate Selves

The mature Mary arrives at a position that dissolves the oppositional model. "It was not so. There were no independent, separate entities, no sacred, inviolable selves. They were one immense organism and you were part of it; you were nothing that they had not been before you. It was no good struggling. You were caught in the net; you couldn't get out" [031-258].

This is the Spinozist turn: the recognition that separate selfhood is an illusion born of inadequate knowledge. The family Mary has fought against is not external to her — she is "nothing that they had not been before you." The insight is simultaneously a defeat (the heroic struggle is futile) and a liberation (the burden of maintaining a separate self is lifted).

## Self of Your Self

The novel's final register of selfhood is the language of mystical union: "Your self willed. It was free to will … God's will in your will. Self of your self. Reality of reality" [037-146]. Here the self is not abolished but fulfilled — it becomes more itself by becoming aligned with something larger. The self that wills in concert with God's will is the self that is finally free.

This position does not simply cancel the earlier "sacred, inviolable self". It recontextualises it: the self is sacred *because* it is the site where God's will can operate, not because it is an independent entity. The "real self" that Mary cannot bear to lose [040-060] is the self that knows reality — a self constituted by its relation to what is not itself.
