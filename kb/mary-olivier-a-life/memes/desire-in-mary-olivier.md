# Desire in Mary Olivier

**Desire** in *[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]: A Life* is the most
persistent and the most problematic force in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s inner
life. May Sinclair traces desire across its objects — romantic, intellectual,
aesthetic — and across its registers: as bodily sensation, as philosophical
concept, as torment and as vocation. The novel's treatment of desire is
unusually comprehensive because it refuses to separate the intellectual from
the erotic, the hunger for [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] from the hunger for
[[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]]. Both are expressions of the same
fundamental energy — what Spinoza called *conatus*, the striving of each
thing to persist in its being — and both pose the same problem: how to
desire without being destroyed by desire.

## The Discovery of Desire

Desire enters [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s consciousness not as a concept but as
a bodily disturbance — something felt before it is named. The adolescent
[[mary-olivier|Mary]], lying awake at night, confronts a restlessness
that resists understanding:

> It gnawed, stopping a minute, beginning again, only to be appeased by
> reverie, by the running tap.
>
> That was desire. It must be.
> [[PN=028-055]] [[PN=028-056]]

The naming is tentative — "It must be" — as if the concept has to be
inferred from the sensation rather than applied to it. The "gnawing" is
physical and insistent, and the remedies — "reverie, the running tap" — are
stopgaps that acknowledge the desire without satisfying it. The passage is
characteristic of Sinclair's method: psychological states are rendered with
somatic precision, and theoretical understanding follows bodily registration.

The discovery is immediately pursued through a cascade of literary
references — the Greek ἱμερος, Sophocles's Antigone, Swinburne's "tears of
desire" — as if [[mary-olivier|Mary]] can only grasp her own experience by
recruiting the whole tradition that has named it before her. The "silly
song" Minna Acroyd sang — "Sighing and sad for desire of the bee"
[[PN=028-061]] — marks the gap between the literary tradition of desire and
its felt reality: how could anyone sing such a thing? The question registers
the inadequacy of conventional languages for the experience they purport to
describe.

## Romantic Desire

The novel's treatment of romantic desire centres on [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s
relationship with [[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]], in which desire is
inseparable from idealisation and self-sacrifice. When
[[maurice-jourdain|Maurice]]'s letter arrives, [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s
response is at once somatic and conceptual:

> When she said "Maurice Jourdain" she could feel her voice throb in her
> body like the string of a violin. When she thought of Maurice Jourdain
> the stir renewed itself in a vague, exquisite vibration.
> [[PN=027-051]]

The metaphor of the violin string is precise: desire is not a separate
faculty but a vibration transmitted through the whole self, resonating in
voice and body. The aesthetic register — "exquisite" — marks desire as
pleasurable even in its intensity. Yet the same passage reveals desire's
tendency toward self-abnegation:

> She was mainly aware of a surpassing tenderness and a desire to immolate
> herself, in some remarkable and noble fashion, for Maurice Jourdain.
> [[PN=027-052]]

"Immolate" — the word is violent, sacrificial, and it registers the danger
in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s desire: the willingness to destroy the self
for the beloved. The novel will trace this impulse through to its necessary
correction: the choice of [[self-and-identity|self]] over sacrifice, of
vocation over [[maurice-jourdain|Maurice]].

## Desire and the Body

Sinclair's most radical treatment of desire locates it squarely in the body.
The connection between intellectual and physical longing is made explicit
in a moment of startling candour:

> A sharp and tender pang went through her. It was like desire; like the
> feeling you had when you thought of babies: painful and at the same time
> delicious.
> [[PN=030-132]]

The comparison of an emotional response to [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]]'s
illness with the desire for children is remarkable for its period — desire
is acknowledged as a bodily reality that pervades experience beyond the
romantic context. "Painful and at the same time delicious" is the novel's
most economical formulation of desire's double nature: it wounds and
pleases in the same instant.

This somatic dimension of desire finds its complement in a passage that
explicitly seeks freedom from it:

> She would be free from the restlessness and the endless idiotic reverie
> of desire.
> [[PN=028-148]]

"Endless idiotic reverie" — the judgement is harsh and self-directed. Desire
that does not issue in action becomes a loop, a "reverie" that consumes
energy without producing anything. The desire for freedom from desire is one
of the novel's deepest tensions, linking [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s personal
struggle to the philosophical traditions — Stoic, Spinozist, Buddhist — that
have made the mastery of desire the condition of wisdom.

## Intellectual Desire

The novel's most distinctive contribution to the literature of desire is its
insistence on the reality and intensity of intellectual desire — the hunger
for knowledge that is as urgent and as bodily as romantic longing. At Morfe,
deprived of intellectual companionship, [[mary-olivier|Mary]] confronts
desire in its most abstract and most consuming form:

> If, before it killed you, you could kill the desire to know another
> mind, a luminous, fiery crystal, to see it turn, shining and flashing.
> To talk to it, to listen to it, to love the human creature it belonged
> to.
> [[PN=033-075]]

The desire is for a *mind* — "a luminous, fiery crystal" — but it is also
inescapably for a *person*, "the human creature it belonged to." The
movement from mind to creature to love traces the trajectory of
intellectual desire toward its object: one wants to know a mind, but knowing
a mind means loving the person whose mind it is. The passage acknowledges
that the sharpest desire is not for ideas in the abstract but for the
consciousness of another — the desire for [[solitude-in-mary-olivier|
solitude]] to be broken by a presence equal to one's own.

The same hunger is expressed more directly in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s
account of her intellectual formation:

> "I wanted to know things — to know what's real and what isn't, and what's
> at the back of everything, and whether there is anything there or not."
> [[PN=029-080]]

The progression — from "what's real" to "what's at the back of everything"
to "whether there is anything there or not" — moves from epistemology to
metaphysics to the existential question that underlies both. Desire for
knowledge is finally desire for reassurance: the hope that there is
something, that the search is not in vain.
