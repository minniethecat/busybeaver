## Description

Back Lane is a narrow lane in [[morfe|Morfe]] that runs behind Mr. Horn's yard, connecting to the orchard of [[five-elms|Five Elms]] on one side and the field track to the schoolhouse on the other. [[PN=028-036]] It is accessed through a narrow passage or through the orchard, and from it "you could get up through the fields to the schoolhouse lane without seeing people" — a geography of avoidance that Mary learns to navigate deliberately. [[PN=028-036]]

The lane is part of the intricate back-network of [[morfe|Morfe]] — the alternative routes that bypass the public spaces of the Green and the High Row. It connects to the Bottom Lane, the Rathdale fields, and the river, forming part of a longer walking route: "Down the Back Lane and through the gap in the lower fields, along the flagged path to the Bottom Lane and through the Rathdale fields to the river." [[PN=036-051]]

## Significance in the Narrative

Back Lane functions as [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s geography of retreat. When she wants to avoid encounters — with Mr. and Mrs. Belk on the High Row, with the village gaze — she turns into Back Lane. [[PN=028-036]] It is the spatial equivalent of her inner withdrawal: a way of moving through the world unseen.

The lane is also the site of village transgression. Nannie Learoyd, the village girl, "slink[s] about the Back Lane waiting for young Horn to come out to her" after dark. [[PN=031-152]] Mary sees Nannie there, and Nannie's smile of trust — "as if she trusted you not to tell" — implicates Mary in the lane's secret life. The image recurs: "As if nothing had happened she hung about Horn's yard and the Back Lane, waiting for young Horn." [[PN=031-245]] Back Lane becomes associated with sexual desire, secrecy, and the persistence of village patterns that outlast individual shame.

The lane serves Mary as an exit from domestic obligation as well: "When old Martha brought the message for her to go to tea with Miss Kendal, Mary slunk out through the orchard into the Back Lane" — an evasion of social demands through the back geography of the village. [[PN=033-073]]

## Key Passages

> From the Back Lane you could get up through the fields to the schoolhouse lane without seeing people.
> [[PN=028-036]]

The lane's defining function: a route that permits movement without social encounter. In a village where everyone knows everyone's business, Back Lane is the infrastructure of privacy.

> She saw her after dark slinking about the Back Lane waiting for young Horn to come out to her.
> [[PN=031-152]]

The lane at night becomes a site of covert desire. Nannie Learoyd's "slinking" is both furtive and knowing — a performance of secrecy that the whole village can see.

> Down the Back Lane and through the gap in the lower fields, along the flagged path to the Bottom Lane and through the Rathdale fields to the river.
> [[PN=036-051]]

The lane is a node in Mary's walking network — the back-way that eventually leads to the river, to the wider landscape beyond the village's social mesh.
