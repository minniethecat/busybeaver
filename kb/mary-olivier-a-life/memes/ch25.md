## Chapter 25

### I

[029-001]She waited.

[029-002]Mamma and Mark had turned their backs to her as they clung together. But there was his sparrow-brown hair, clipped close into the nape of his red-brown neck. If only Mamma wouldn’t cry like that﻿—

[029-003]“Mark﻿—”

[029-004]“Is that Minky?”

[029-005]They held each other and let go in one tick of the clock, but she had stood a long time seeing his eyes arrested in their rush of recognition. Disappointed.

[029-006]The square dinner-table stretched itself into an immense white space between her and Mark. It made itself small again for Mark and Mamma. Across the white space she heard him saying things: about Dan meeting him at Tilbury, and poor Victor coming to Liverpool Street, and Cox’s. Last night he had stayed at Ilford, he had seen Bella and Edward and Pidgeon and Mrs. Fisher and the Proparts. “Do you remember poor Edward and his sheep? And Mary’s lamb!”

[029-007]Mark hadn’t changed, except that he was firmer and squarer, and thinner, because he had had fever. And his eyes﻿—He was staring at her with his disappointed eyes.

[029-008]She called to him. “You don’t know me a bit, Mark.”

[029-009]He laughed. “I thought I’d see somebody grown up. Victor said Mary was dreadfully mature. What did he mean?”

[029-010]Mamma said she was sure she didn’t know.

[029-011]“What do you do with yourself all day, Minky?”

[029-012]“Nothing much. Read﻿—work﻿—play tennis with Mr. Sutcliffe.”

[029-013]“Mr.﻿—Sutcliffe?”

[029-014]“Never mind Mr. Sutcliffe. Mark doesn’t want to hear about him.”

[029-015]“Is there a Mrs. Sutcliffe?”

[029-016]“Yes.”

[029-017]“Does she play?”

[029-018]“No. She’s too old. Much older than he is.”

[029-019]“That’ll do, Mary.”

[029-020]Mamma’s eyes blinked. Her forehead was pinched with vexation. Her foot tapped on the floor.

[029-021]Mark’s eyes kept up their puzzled stare.

[029-022]“What’s been happening?” he said. “What’s the matter? Everywhere I go there’s a mystery. There was a mystery at Ilford. About Dan. And about poor Charlotte. I come down here and there’s a mystery about some people called Sutcliffe. And a mystery about Mary.” He laughed again. “Minky seems to be in disgrace, as if she’d done something.﻿ ﻿… It’s awfully queer. Mamma’s the only person something hasn’t happened to.”

[029-023]“I should have thought everything had happened to me,” said Mamma.

[029-024]“That makes it queerer.”

[029-025]Mamma went up with Mark into his room. Papa’s room. You could hear her feet going up and down in it, and the squeaking wail of the wardrobe door as she opened and shut it.

[029-026]She waited, listening. When she heard her mother come downstairs she went to him.

[029-027]Mark didn’t know that the room had been Papa’s room. He didn’t know that she shivered when she saw him sitting on the bed. She had stood just there where Mark’s feet were and watched Papa die. She could feel the basin slipping, slipping from the edge of the bed.

[029-028]Mark wasn’t happy. There was something he missed, something he wanted. She had meant to say, “It’s all right. Nothing’s happened. I haven’t done anything,” but she couldn’t think about it when she saw him sitting there.

[029-029]“Mark﻿—what is it?”

[029-030]“I don’t know, Minky.”

[029-031]“I know. You’ve come back, and it isn’t like what you thought it would be.”

[029-032]“No,” he said, “it isn’t.﻿ ﻿… I didn’t think it would be so awful without Papa.”

### II

[029-033]The big package in the hall had been opened. The tiger’s skin lay on the drawing-room carpet.

[029-034]Mark was sorry for the tiger.

[029-035]“He was only a young cat. You’d have loved him, Minky, if you’d seen him, with his shoulders down﻿—very big cat﻿—shaking his haunches at you, and his eyes shining and playing; cat’s eyes, sort of swimming and shaking with his fun.”

[029-036]“How did you feel?”

[029-037]“Beastly mean to go and shoot him when he was happy and excited.”

[029-038]“Five years without any fighting.﻿ ﻿… Anything else happen?”

[029-039]“No. No polo. No fighting. Only a mutiny in the battery once.”

[029-040]“What was it like?”

[029-041]“Oh, it just tumbled into the office and yelled and waved jabby things and made faces at you till you nearly burst with laughing.”

[029-042]“You laughed?” Mamma said. “At a mutiny?”

[029-043]“Anybody would. Minky’d have laughed if she’d been there. It frightened them horribly because they didn’t expect it. The poor things never know when they’re being funny.”

[029-044]“What happened,” said Mary, “to the mutiny?”

[029-045]“That.”

[029-046]“Oh﻿—Mark﻿—” She adored him.

[029-047]She went to bed, happy, thinking of the tiger and the mutiny. When Catty called her in the morning she jumped out of bed, quickly, to begin another happy day. Everything was going to be interesting, to be exciting.

[029-048]At any minute anything might happen, now that Mark had come home.

### III

[029-049]“Mark, are you coming?”

[029-050]She was tired of waiting on the flagstones, swinging her stick. She called through the house for him to come. She looked through the rooms, and found him in the study with Mamma. When they saw her they stopped talking suddenly, and Mamma drew herself up and blinked.

[029-051]Mark shook his head. After all, he couldn’t come.

[029-052]Mamma wanted him. Mamma had him. As long as they lived she would have him. Mamma and Mark were happy together; their happiness tingled, you could feel it tingling, like the happiness of lovers. They didn’t want anybody but each other. You existed for them as an object in some unintelligible time and in a space outside their space. The only difference was that Mark knew you were there and Mamma didn’t.

[029-053]She chose the Garthdale road. Yesterday she had gone that way with Mamma and Mark. She had not talked to him, for when she talked the pinched, vexed look came into Mamma’s face though she pretended she hadn’t heard you. Every now and then Mark had looked at her over his shoulder and said, “Poor Minx.” It was as if he said, “I’m sorry, but you see how it is. I can’t help it.”

[029-054]And just here, where the moor track touched the road, she had left them, clearing the watercourses, and had gone up towards Karva.

[029-055]She had looked back and seen them going slowly towards the white sickle of the road, Mark very upright, taut muscles held in to his shortened stride; Mamma pathetic and fragile, in her shawl, moving with a stiff, self-hypnotised air.

[029-056]Her love for them was a savage pang that cut her eyes and drew her throat tight.

[029-057]Then suddenly she had heard Mark whooping, and she had run back, whooping and leaping, down the hill to walk with them again.

[029-058]She turned back now, at the sickle. Perhaps Mark would come to meet her.

[029-059]He didn’t come. She found them sitting close on the drawing-room sofa; the tea-table was pushed aside; they were looking at Mark’s photographs. She came and stood by them to see.

[029-060]Mark didn’t look up or say anything. He went on giving the photographs to Mamma, telling her the names. “Dicky Carter. Man called St. John. Man called Bibby﻿—Jonas Bibby. Allingham. Peters. Gunning, Stobart Hamilton. Sir George Limond, Colonel Robertson.”

[029-061]Photographs of women. Mamma’s fingers twitched as she took them, one by one. Women with smooth hair and correct, distinguished faces. She looked at each face a long time; her mouth half-smiled, half-pouted at them. She didn’t hand on the photographs to you, but laid them down on the sofa, one by one, as if you were not there.

[029-062]A youngish woman in a black silk gown; Mrs. Robertson, the Colonel’s wife. A girl in a white frock; Mrs. Dicky Carter, she had nursed Mark through his fever. [029-063]“Lady Limond.” Mark had stayed with them at Simla.

[029-064]“Oh. Very handsome face.”

[029-065]“Very handsome.”

[029-066]You could see by Mark’s face that he didn’t care about Lady Limond.

[029-067]Mamma had turned again to the girl in the white frock who had nursed him.

[029-068]“Are those all, Mark?”

[029-069]“Those are all.”

[029-070]She took off her glasses and closed her eyes. Her face was smooth now: her hands were quiet. She had him. She would always have him.

[029-071]But when he went away for a fortnight to stay with the man called St. John, she was miserable till he had come back, safe.

### IV

### V

[029-117]“And mine haven’t begun.”

Taught to sit on our wills, to be afraid of our selves and not trust them for a single minute.﻿ ﻿… Mamma was glad when I was jilted, because that was one for me.”

[029-215]“Were you jilted?”

[029-216]“Yes. She thought it would make me humble. I always was. I am. I’m afraid of my self now. I can’t trust it. I keep on asking people what they think when I ought to know.﻿ ﻿… But I’m going to stop all that. I’m going to fight.”

[029-217]“Fight little Mamma?”

[029-218]“No. Myself. The bit of me that claws on to her and can’t get away. My body’ll stay here and take care of her all her life, but my self will have got away. It’ll get away from all of them. It’s got bits of them sticking to it, bits of Mamma, bits of Papa, bits of Roddy, bits of Aunt Charlotte. Bits of you, Mark. I don’t want to get away from you, but I shall have to. You’d kick me down and stamp on me if you thought it would please Mamma. There mayn’t be much left when I’m done, but at least it’ll be me.”

[029-219]“Mad. Quite mad, Minx. You ought to be married.”

[029-220]“And leave little Mamma?﻿ ﻿… I’ll race you from the bridge to the top of the hill.”

[029-221]He raced her. He wasn’t really angry. Deep down inside him he knew.

### VII

[029-222]November, and Mark’s last morning. He had got promotion. He was going back to India with a new battery. He would be stationed at Poona, a place he hated. Nothing ever happened as he wanted it to happen.

[029-223]She was in Papa’s room, helping him to pack. The wardrobe door gave out its squeaking wail again and again as he opened it and threw his things on to the bed. Her mother had gone away because she couldn’t bear to see them, his poor things.

[029-224]They were all folded now and pressed down into the boxes and portmanteaus. She sat on the bed with Mark’s sword across her knees, rubbing vaseline on the blade. Mark came and stood before her, looking down at her.

[029-225]“Minky, I don’t like going away and leaving Mamma with you.﻿ ﻿… When I went before you promised you’d be kind to her.”

[029-226]“What do I do?”

[029-227]There was a groove down the middle of the blade for the blood to run in.

[029-228]“Do? You do nothing. Nothing. You don’t talk to her. You don’t want to talk to her. You behave as if she wasn’t there.”

[029-229]The blade was blunt. It would have to be sharpened before Mark took it into a battle. Mark’s eyes hurt her. She tried to fix her attention on the blade.

[029-230]“What makes you?”

[029-231]“I don’t know,” she said. “Whatever it is it was done long ago.”

[029-232]“She hasn’t got anybody,” he said. “Roddy’s gone. Dan’s no good to her. She won’t have anybody but you.”

[029-233]“I know, Mark. I shall never go away and leave her.”

[029-234]“Don’t talk about going away and leaving her!”

[029-235]He didn’t want her to see him off at the train. He wanted to go away alone, after he had said goodbye to Mamma. He didn’t want Mamma to be left by herself after he had gone.

[029-236]They stood together by the shut door of the drawing-room. She and her mother stood between Mark and the door. She had said goodbye a minute ago, alone with him in Papa’s room. But there was something they had missed﻿—

[029-237]She thought: “We must get it now, this minute. He’ll say goodbye to Mamma last. He’ll kiss her last. But I must kiss him again, first.”

[029-238]She came to him, holding up her face. He didn’t see her; but when his arm felt her hand it jerked up and pushed her out of his way, as he would have pushed anything that stood there between him and Mamma.
