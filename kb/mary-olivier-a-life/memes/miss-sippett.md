## Role and Significance

Miss Sippett is a minor character who appears as [[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]'s piano teacher. She is the sister of [[mr-sippett|Mr. Sippett]], the boys' tutor, and thus belongs to the small network of instructors who rotate through the Olivier household at [[five-elms|Five Elms]]. Her role is modest — she arrives to give [[mary-olivier|Mary]] her weekly [[music-in-mary-olivier|music]] lesson — but her presence contributes to the novel's portrait of a Victorian girl's [[school|education]] as a patchwork of tutors, governesses, and visiting teachers, each bringing a distinct temperament to the nursery.

## Key Appearances

### The Piano Lesson

Miss Sippett's primary appearance in the novel occurs in the context of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s early [[school|education]]: "for an hour after breakfast before Miss Sippett came to give her her [[music-in-mary-olivier|music]] lesson" [011-027]. This regular appointment structures a portion of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s week, the piano representing one of the expected accomplishments for a middle-class girl. The piece [[mary-olivier|Mary]] learns is "Violetta," which she finds "beautiful" — a rare moment of genuine aesthetic pleasure in an otherwise regimented curriculum [011-026].

### A Lenient Presence

The narrator notes that Miss Sippett was more lenient than [[miss-thompson|Miss Thompson]], the other instructor [011-027]. This comparison — brief but telling — suggests a teacher who allowed [[mary-olivier|Mary]] some measure of freedom or encouragement, contrasting with the stricter pedagogical methods that prevailed elsewhere in the Olivier household. Whether Miss Sippett noticed the Greek Accidence that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] kept hidden during lessons remains uncertain: "But whether Miss Sippett saw the Accidence or not she…" [011-028]. The trailing sentence implies a tolerance, or at least a willing blindness, that distinguished her from other authority figures.

## The Sippett Family in the Olivier Household

Miss Sippett's brother, [[mr-sippett|Mr. Sippett]], serves as tutor to the Olivier boys — teaching Rodney Latin with the memorable phrase "Mensa: a table" [005-012][005-040] and discussing the philosophy of numbers with [[mary-olivier|Mary]] [008-037]. Together, the Sippett siblings represent a modest family enterprise of tutoring, their combined presence suggesting that the Oliviers valued continuity and trusted recommendation even in the hiring of part-time instructors. Miss Sippett's role — teaching [[music-in-mary-olivier|music]] rather than Latin — reflects the gendered division of [[school|education]]al labour, in which female accomplishments (piano, drawing, French conversation) were the province of female teachers.
