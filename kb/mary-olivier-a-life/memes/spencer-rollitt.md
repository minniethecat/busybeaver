## Role in the Novel

Spencer Rollitt is one of the fixed points of the [[morfe|Morfe]] social landscape — a figure who appears periodically across the novel's span to register, through his steady disapproval and eventual grudging acceptance, the shifting terms on which [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is judged by her community. He belongs to the world of the [[church-and-chapel|church]], the parish, and respectable country society: the world that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] inhabits physically but never fully occupies.

His structural function is to embody the normative gaze. Rollitt "looked at you with his hard, blue eyes" and "his mouth closed tight with a snap when he saw you coming" (028-003). He disapproves of [[mary-olivier|Mary]] — first for childish transgressions (playing hide-and-seek in his garden), later for her unmarried state, perhaps for her intellectualism, perhaps simply for being the kind of woman who attracts disapproval. His judgement is impersonal, institutional: it is the judgement of the community made flesh in one hard, handsome face.

In the novel's final pages, Rollitt reappears in a transformed role: he recommends [[mary-olivier|Mary]] for a position as [[richard-nicholson|Richard Nicholson]]'s secretary (036-041). The recommendation is a pivot point — the man who disapproved of her for twenty years suddenly approves, not because [[mary-olivier|Mary]] has changed but because [[richard-nicholson|Richard Nicholson]], a man of standing, has asked. The irony is characteristic of Sinclair's treatment of social reputation: approval and disapproval are equally arbitrary, equally external to [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s real self.

## Biography

Spencer Rollitt is introduced in a sustained descriptive passage that is among the most vivid character sketches in the novel. He is "hard and handsome" with a "large face, square-cut, clean-shaved, bare of any accent except its eyebrows" (024-294). His mouth is "a thin straight line hardly visible in its sunburn," and his "small blue eyes" stand "still in the sunburn, hard and cold" (024-294). To express heartiness — an emotion his face cannot register — he must "fall back on gesture, on the sudden flash of white teeth" and draw in his breath "sharply, between the straight, close lips, with a sound: 'Fivv‑vv!'" (024-294).

The description is rendered through [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s appraising gaze, which notes the paradox of his appearance: "Under his small handsome nose his mouth and chin together made one steep, straight line. This lower face, flat and naked, without lips, stretched like another forehead" (024-296). Yet Sinclair undercuts the severity with a single, surprising detail: "Mr. Spencer Rollitt's hair curled and clustered out at the back of his head in perfect innocence" (024-296). The innocent curls against the hard face — the detail is characteristic of Sinclair's technique, which finds in physical description a moral complexity that abstract commentary could not supply.

Rollitt's social position is established by the company he keeps. He is listed among the regular attendees of [[church-and-chapel|church]] and parish functions: "[[mrs-waugh|Mrs. Waugh]] and [[miss-frewin|Miss Frewin]], and the Kendals; Mr. Spencer Rollitt, and [[louisa-wright|Miss Louisa Wright]] who had had a disappointment; and [[mrs-heron|old Mrs. Heron]]" (024-354). The catalogue of the ageing and the disappointed situates Rollitt in a world of diminished expectations and fixed routines.

He is the uncle of [[harry-craven|Harry Craven]], and it is through this connection that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] first incurs his disapproval: "He had disapproved of you ever since you played hide-and-seek in his garden with his nephew" (028-003). The disapproval persists and deepens: "He thought it served you right to be jilted" (028-003) — a reference to [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s broken engagement or romantic disappointment.

The turning point comes decades later, when Rollitt recommends [[mary-olivier|Mary]] to [[richard-nicholson|Richard Nicholson]]. "He was proud because [[richard-nicholson|Richard Nicholson]] had asked him about a secretary and he had recommended you. Funny that people could go on disapproving of you for twenty years, and then suddenly approve because of [[richard-nicholson|Richard Nicholson]]" (036-041). [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s reflection on the arbitrariness of social approval is rendered without bitterness — the word "funny" captures a detachment that is the fruit of long experience.

In his final appearance, Rollitt stretches himself straight, looks away over [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s head, and draws in his breath with his characteristic "Fivv‑vv‑vv" as he asks after [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]. The passage closes with a summing-up: "His thoughts were hidden behind his bare, wooden face. He was a just and cautious man. He wouldn't accept any statement outside the Bible without proof" (039-009). This is Rollitt's epitaph in the novel: just, cautious, wooden-faced, Bible-bound — a representative of a world that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] has long since outgrown but can regard, at the last, with something approaching equanimity.

## Relationships

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: Rollitt's relationship to [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is defined by disapproval — first for a childhood game, then for her unmarried state and social position. His eventual recommendation of her to [[richard-nicholson|Richard Nicholson]] marks a shift that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] receives with detached amusement rather than vindication. The arc from disapproval to approval is, she recognises, a function of social mechanisms that have little to do with her actual merits.

**[[harry-craven|Harry Craven]]**: Rollitt's nephew, through whom [[mary-olivier|Mary]] first enters Rollitt's field of vision — and disapproval. Craven's tennis-party appearance (027-138) anchors Rollitt in the web of [[morfe|Morfe]] family connections.

**[[richard-nicholson|Richard Nicholson]]**: The figure whose request transforms Rollitt's attitude toward [[mary-olivier|Mary]]. Nicholson's standing is sufficient to override twenty years of disapproval — a measure of Nicholson's social weight and of the superficiality of Rollitt's earlier judgement.

**[[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]]**: Rollitt exists in the same [[morfe|Morfe]] social orbit as [[lindley-vickers|Vickers]], though the two men represent different poles of that world: Rollitt the institutional pillar, [[lindley-vickers|Vickers]] the restless intellectual. Rollitt's world is one [[mary-olivier|Mary]] must escape; [[lindley-vickers|Vickers]]'s is one she briefly inhabits before losing it.

## Characterisation

Sinclair's portrait of Rollitt is built almost entirely through physical description rendered in free indirect discourse. The technique is notably external: unlike characters who are presented through [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s sympathetic imagination ([[catty|Catty]], [[jenny|Jenny]], [[mark-olivier|Mark]]), Rollitt is seen only from the outside. His "bare, wooden face" is never penetrated; his thoughts remain "hidden" (039-009). This externality is the point — Rollitt is opaque to [[mary-olivier|Mary]] because he represents a mode of being (institutional, conventional, Bible-bound) that her consciousness cannot inhabit.

The descriptive detail of the curls — "curled and clustered out at the back of his head in perfect innocence" (024-296) — is the one softening touch in an otherwise severe portrait. It functions as a kind of narrative mercy: even the hardest face has its innocent feature. The detail also anticipates the novel's final judgement of Rollitt as "a just and cautious man" (039-009) — not warm, not generous, but not malicious either.

The repeated motif of the "Fivv‑vv" sound — Rollitt's substitute for expressed emotion — becomes a kind of signature, a tic that defines him as much as any action or speech. It appears at his introduction (024-294) and again at his leave-taking (039-009), bookending his presence in the novel.

## Selected Passages

> Mr. Spencer Rollitt. Hard and handsome. Large face, square-cut, clean-shaved, bare of any accent except its eyebrows, its mouth a thin straight line hardly visible in its sunburn. Small blue eyes standing still in the sunburn, hard and cold. (024-294)

The staccato sentences — "Hard and handsome." — mimic the hardness they describe. Sinclair uses parataxis here as a descriptive tool: each feature is presented as a discrete, unsoftened fact, the syntax as unforgiving as the face.

> Yet Mr. Spencer Rollitt's hair curled and clustered out at the back of his head in perfect innocence. (024-296)

The "yet" is crucial — a turn in the middle of the description that complicates everything that has come before. The curls are "innocent" in a way the face is not, and the juxtaposition saves the portrait from caricature.

> His thoughts were hidden behind his bare, wooden face. He was a just and cautious man. He wouldn't accept any statement outside the Bible without proof. (039-009)

The final assessment balances negative capability ("his thoughts were hidden") with positive qualities ("just and cautious"). The Bible clause is ambiguous — it can be read as a limitation (his epistemology is constrained by scripture) or as a form of intellectual integrity (he requires proof). The ambiguity is deliberate and characteristic of Sinclair's mature narrative voice.
