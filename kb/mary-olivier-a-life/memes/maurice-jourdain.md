## Overview

Maurice Jourdain is the most significant romantic figure in [[mary-olivier]]'s adult life. Introduced when [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is a young woman, he reappears years later, leading to an engagement that collapses under the weight of ambivalence, intellectual tension, and the gravitational pull of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s devotion to her mother. He represents the possibility of an intellectual and emotional partnership that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] ultimately cannot sustain.

## First Meeting

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s first encounter with Maurice Jourdain occurs outdoors, in a moment of unexpected intimacy [020-008]:

> "And all this time," she said, "I don't know your name."
> "Maurice. Maurice Jourdain. I know yours — Mary Olivier. I like it." [020-008]

Their exchange is playful and immediate, with [[mary-olivier|Mary]] complaining about [[emilius-olivier|her father]]'s teasing and Maurice responding with wry sympathy: "Fathers will do these cruel things. It's a way they have" [020-008]. This first meeting lodges itself permanently in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s memory — she later recalls "him standing in the field path" and hearing him say "Talk to me. I'm alive. I'm here. I'll listen. I'll never misunderstand" [025-025].

She notes his physical details with characteristic precision: "his worn eyelids; his narrow, yellowish teeth" [025-025].

## The Letter and Its Revelation

Years later, the reappearance of Maurice Jourdain shatters [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s composure. The shock comes not from seeing him but from discovering a letter he has written — not to her, but to [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] [027-009]:

> Maurice Jourdain. Maurice Jourdain. What on earth was he writing to Mamma for?
> Five minutes ago she had been quiet and happy, reading Kant's Critique of Pure Reason. Now her heart beat like a hammer, staggering with its own blows. [027-009]

The letter reveals a hidden history — that Maurice has been in communication with her mother, that [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] has played a role in the relationship [[mary-olivier|Mary]] thought was entirely her own. When [[mary-olivier|Mary]] confronts her mother about comparing Maurice to [[emilius-olivier|Papa]], her mother asks incredulously: "You surely don't compare Maurice Jourdain with your father?" [027-041]. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s response — "He's [[god-and-religion|faith]]ful. [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] was [[god-and-religion|faith]]ful. I'm [[god-and-religion|faith]]ful too" — draws a parallel that disturbs her mother: "Faithful! To a horrid man like that!" [027-041].

## The Engagement

The engagement happens with a curious lack of drama, in late August, after days of tense walks during which Maurice "would slouch along beside you, for miles, morosely" [027-090]. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] describes the period with detachment:

> You knew it would happen some day; but you thought of it as happening tomorrow or the day after rather than today. At three o'clock you started for a walk, never knowing how you might come back, and at five you found yourself sitting at tea in the orchard, safe. [027-090]

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s behaviour during their courtship is notably unromantic — she breaks away to "run tearing along the road, to jump watercourses, to climb trees and grin down at him through the branches" [027-090]. The engagement itself crystallises "just after he had not been pleased at all" with one of these outbursts [027-090].

## The End

The engagement does not survive. By October, [[mary-olivier|Mary]] "began to wonder when he would come again" [027-100]. When [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] receives another letter from Maurice, she looks "sad and at the same time happy" [027-105]. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] reflects later on his criticism of her: that he "nagged at you about your hair, about philosophy" and that he had called her ugly [027-170]. She analyses the dissolution with philosophical detachment:

> As far as she could see through the working of his dark mind, first he had cared for her, cared violently. Then he had not cared. That would be because he cared for some other woman. There were two of them. The girl and the married woman. [027-170]

## Significance

Maurice Jourdain occupies a pivotal position in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s emotional history. He is the man who bridges her intellectual and romantic lives — she finds him "in the Critique of Pure Reason," in the "Sonata Appassionata," in "the solemn beauty of [[karva|Karva]] under the moon" [027-052]. She imagines his mind as "a big room, filled from end to end with light" [027-052].

After the engagement ends, the community's pity becomes a burden: "They all thought the same thing: that you wanted Maurice Jourdain and that you were unhappy because you hadn't got him" [027-170]. Yet [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s own feelings are more complex — she knows that "he knew you didn't want to marry him any more than he wanted to marry you" [027-170]. Their relationship, in its failure, clarifies the shape of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s solitude and her ultimate incapacity to subordinate her inner life to another person.
