## Overview

Dorsy Heron is the niece of [[mrs-heron|old Mrs. Heron]] and a recurring presence in the social world of [[morfe|Morfe]]. At twenty-seven she is considered past youth — "Dorsy was old too, twenty-seven" [024-355] — yet she carries herself with a quiet dignity, "tall and straight" [025-038], her face a "shy hare's face trying to look austere" [025-038].

## Character and Appearance

Dorsy is defined by her shyness. Her "bright, shy eyes, like a hare" [024-355] and her "shy hare's eyes" that "tried to look away and couldn't" [025-041] mark her as physically present at gatherings but emotionally guarded. When she blushes, her "little high, red nose got redder" [025-041].

Despite her reserve, she is recognised as "sweet and gentle and unselfish" [029-121]. Her presence at [[morfe|Morfe]] gatherings — drawing-room evenings at the Kendals', [[church-and-chapel|church]] services, country walks — is a steady fixture. She listens to [[music-in-mary-olivier|music]] with "soft and quiet" eyes [025-047], and people are "sorry for you because you had been jilted" when they look at her [028-004], suggesting a history of romantic disappointment that colours her present.

## Social Role

Dorsy moves within the close-knit circle of [[morfe|Morfe]] society alongside figures like [[miss-kendal|Miss Kendal]], [[louisa-wright|Louisa Wright]], and [[mr-sutcliffe|Mr. Sutcliffe]]. At the dance, [[roddy-olivier|Roddy Olivier]] dances five times with her — "because he liked her" [029-118]. She is the subject of quiet marital speculation: after a gathering with [[mr-sutcliffe|Mr. Sutcliffe]], [[mary-olivier|Mary]] reflects that "it would be well if he could marry Dorsy" [030-179].

Her later appearances take on a sombre note. She takes "pills and arsenic" from [[dr-charles|Dr. Charles]] [031-270], and the community wonders "what poor Dorsy would do if anything should happen to [[mrs-heron|Mrs. Heron]]" [033-013]. Two days after this speculation, Dorsy appears "with her tale; her nose was red and her hare's eyes were red" [033-014] — a moment of grief or crisis that the narrative registers without fully explaining.

## Significance

Dorsy embodies the quiet pathos of the unmarried woman in late-Victorian rural society — too old for youthful romance, too gentle to demand attention, yet fully aware of her own predicament. Her "shy hare's face" and gentle manner make her a sympathetic figure who earns the community's concern without ever becoming central to the novel's action.
