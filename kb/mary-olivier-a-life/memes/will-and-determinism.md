## Overview

The problem of will — whether it is free or determined — runs through _[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]: A Life_ as a philosophical and existential question. The novel moves through three distinct frameworks: the Calvinist theology of predestination, in which the will is bound by divine decree; the [[baruch-spinoza|Spinozist]] position that freedom consists in understanding necessity, not in libertarian choice; and Mary's mature synthesis, which inverts the Spinozist formula by treating free will as more fundamental than determinism. The "flash point of freedom" [040-046] is the novel's name for the moment when these frameworks converge in lived experience.

## Calvinist Predestination

Mary's earliest encounter with the problem of will is theological. The Calvinism of her household teaches that the will is powerless to achieve goodness: "You must pray for grace. You are not able to be good of your own will and in your own strength" [005-025]. The doctrine of original sin renders the unaided will corrupt, and Mary internalises this judgement: "God hates selfishness and self-will" [003-078]. The child's experience of will is thus shaped by guilt — to will is to sin, and the only acceptable will is surrender.

This theological framework is reinforced by family dynamics. Mamma's will operates through emotional coercion rather than open command, and Mary's developing sense of her own will is inseparable from her resistance to her mother's. The Calvinist war on self-will becomes indistinguishable from the maternal demand for conformity.

## Waves and Waves of Will

Against the theological constraint of the will, the novel sets moments of spontaneous volition that feel radically free. In a passage of visionary intensity, Mary experiences the will as an impersonal force moving through her: "Willing was this. Waves and waves of will, coming on and on, making your will, driving it through empty time … 'The time of time': that was the Self. … Time where nothing happens except this. Where nothing happens except God's will. God's will in your will. Self of your self. Reality of reality" [037-146].

This experience dissolves the distinction between self and God, will and necessity. "God's will in your will" is neither compulsion nor submission but alignment: the self wills most freely when it wills what God wills. The "waves and waves of will" are not chosen by the conscious self — they arrive unbidden — yet their arrival is experienced not as violation but as liberation.

## The Flash Point of Freedom

The novel's mature philosophical position is articulated in a passage of sustained argument: "If you were part of God your will was God's will at the moment when you really willed. There was always a point when you knew it: the flash point of freedom. You couldn't mistake your flash when it came. You couldn't doubt away that certainty of freedom any more than you could doubt away the certainty of necessity and determination" [040-046].

The "flash point of freedom" is the moment in which the will knows itself as free — not as a logical deduction but as an experiential certainty that is as undeniable as the certainty of causation. The novel does not deny that "from the outside" actions are "part of the show of existence, the illusion of separation from God" [040-046]. But "from the inside" — from the first-person perspective of the willing agent — freedom is irreducible.

## Free Will as Reality

The novel's most striking claim inverts the Spinozist hierarchy of reality and illusion: "Free will was the reality underneath the illusion of necessity" [040-046]. For [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], necessity is the reality and free will the illusion born of ignorance of causes. Mary's position reverses this: the experience of freedom, grounded in the "consciousness of God" [040-046], discloses what is most real, while the world of determined causes is the surface.

This is not a refutation of Spinoza so much as a phenomenological reinterpretation. The "flash point of freedom" is not a metaphysical claim about uncaused causes but a report on the texture of lived experience — the quality of consciousness when it apprehends itself as participating in divine will.
