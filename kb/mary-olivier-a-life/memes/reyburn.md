## Description

Reyburn is a market town near [[morfe|Morfe]], connected by rail to [[durlingham|Durlingham]] and [[london|London]]. It functions as the transport gateway for the Olivier family's [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] life — the station where they arrive, the town where they shop, the point of connection between the rural village and the wider world. [[PN=022-011]] [[PN=024-017]]

The town is reached from [[morfe|Morfe]] by bus or by walking down Reyburn hill, and its station is the setting for departures and returns that mark the rhythm of family life. [[PN=037-025]] [[PN=033-078]]

## Transit Hub

Reyburn station is the portal through which characters pass between the enclosed world of [[morfe|Morfe]] and the outside. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s arrival in [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] is framed by the journey "from King's Cross to Reyburn for [[morfe|Morfe]]" — the station name the first sign of arrival in a new life. [[PN=022-011]] The road to Reyburn Station is a recurring narrative path: "three, going along the road to Reyburn Station, going in the train from Reyburn to [[durlingham|Durlingham]]." [[PN=025-131]]

The train "moving out of Reyburn station" is experienced as an ending — "It was awful to think" — the physical departure matching emotional severance. [[PN=033-078]] Characters settle "into their seats in the Reyburn train," the familiar ritual of travel through a familiar station. [[PN=033-126]]

## Market and Commerce

Reyburn is also a market town where practical business is conducted. [[daniel-olivier|Dan]] goes into Reyburn to give notice, [[catty|Catty]] shops there and collects the mail, and the shepherds return from Reyburn — the town as the commercial centre serving the rural hinterland. [[PN=031-366]] [[PN=032-024]] [[PN=031-078]]

Ned's driving into Reyburn market is a detail of rural life: the weekly rhythm of buying and selling that connects the village economy to the wider region. [[PN=031-061]]

## Social Crossroads

Reyburn brings together people from different social worlds. At [[funerals]], people come from Reyburn and [[durlingham|Durlingham]] to [[morfe|Morfe]] — the railway making possible the gathering of a wider community. [[PN=029-111]] The invitation "I say, aren't you coming to Reyburn?" captures the town's role as a destination for social outings, a place to go when one leaves the village. [[PN=031-180]]

## Key Passages

> …it would be King's Cross to Reyburn for Morfe.
> [[PN=022-011]]

The railway itinerary that maps [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s world — [[london|London]] terminus to [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] station to village — each name a step further into rural life.

> The train was moving out of Reyburn station. It was awful to think…
> [[PN=033-078]]

The station as site of emotional significance — the train's movement away from Reyburn marking a departure from home, the "awful" registering the pain of separation.

> People had come from Reyburn and Durlingham. A hollow square of faces.
> [[PN=029-111]]

Reyburn as a source of community — the railway bringing mourners from the nearby towns, the "hollow square" suggesting both the formal arrangement of [[funerals|funeral]] ritual and the emotional emptiness of the occasion.
