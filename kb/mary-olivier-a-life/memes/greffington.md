## Description

Greffington is a location near [[morfe|Morfe]] comprising two elements: **Greffington Edge**, a line of cliffs and hills that forms the horizon of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s landscape, and **Greffington Hall**, the country estate of a higher social class. [[PN=024-006]] The Edge is visible from the Olivier family home and serves as a fixed landmark in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s consciousness — "the shoulder line of Greffington Edge was fixed across the open window." [[PN=030-079]]

The Edge changes with light and season: its cliffs turn violet at evening, thorn-trees flower on its slopes in spring, and clouds crawl over its summit. [[PN=024-033]] [[PN=033-024]] [[PN=034-017]]

## Greffington Edge as Landscape

The Edge is described through sensory precision: "dim white groves, magic rings, a world of thorns and moonshine." [[PN=033-024]] It is a place of solitary walking and reflection — [[mary-olivier|Mary]] lies down among the grass on Greffington Edge, walks its fields with Roddy, and dreams there. [[PN=028-085]]

For Roddy, Greffington Edge provokes aversion: "Roddy hated Greffington Edge." [[PN=026-112]] For [[mary-olivier|Mary]], it becomes associated with the contemplation of her philosophical interests and her reading — she borrows the *[[london|London]] Library Catalogue* at Greffington Hall. [[PN=031-251]]

The fields below the Edge, beside the beck and the mown fields, are bleached with summer light — part of the larger landscape that includes [[karva|Karva]] and the moor. [[PN=036-053]] [[PN=036-062]]

## Greffington Hall and Social Hierarchy

Greffington Hall represents the social world that judges and excludes the Olivier family. The Hall is the site of a pivotal humiliation: [[emilius-olivier|Papa]]'s drunkenness at a dinner party there. [[PN=024-309]] [[PN=024-312]] The incident is remembered years later: "Your father was drunk at Greffington Hall. He will never be asked again." [[PN=024-312]]

The Hall is owned by people of a higher class — "The people who own the Hall" — and later tenants take it for summer [[christmas|holiday]]s, signalling the family's permanent exclusion from that world. [[PN=024-106]] [[PN=036-009]] [[PN=036-015]]

When the Hall is let to tenants, it occasions [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s encounter with [[lindley-vickers]] and the intrusion of romantic feeling into her solitary landscape. [[PN=036-009]]

## Key Passages

> "Greffington Edge. You aren't listening."
> [[PN=024-006]]

The line that introduces Greffington — spoken by [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]] to [[mary-olivier|Mary]], establishing the Edge as a shared point of reference in their landscape and conversation.

> The cliffs of Greffington Edge were violet now.
> [[PN=024-033]]

Sinclair's characteristic colour notation, marking the Edge's transformation under evening light — part of the novel's sustained attention to the visual texture of landscape.

> The thorn-trees flowered on Greffington Edge: dim white groves, magic rings, a world of thorns and moonshine.
> [[PN=033-024]]

The Edge rendered as enchanted space — the "magic rings" and "world of thorns" suggesting both the beauty and the danger of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s inner landscape.

> "Your father was drunk at Greffington Hall. He will never be asked again."
> [[PN=024-312]]

The Hall as site of social judgment — the definitive statement of the Olivier family's exclusion from the gentry world, and the enduring shame that [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] must carry.
