## Overview

Catty is the young servant in the Olivier household and the niece of [[jenny|old Jenny]]. A Wesleyan Methodist, she is defined by her capacity for [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]] and her warm, singing presence in the kitchen and nursery. Throughout [[mary-olivier]]'s earliest years, Catty serves as [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s primary caretaker — a role she holds with fierce devotion, later declaring: "Master Roddy was [[jenny|Jenny]]'s child and you was always mine" [037-050].

## Role in the Household

Catty works alongside her aunt [[jenny|Jenny]] in the Olivier family home at [[five-elms|Five Elms]]. She moves between the kitchen — where she dries plates, sings hymns, and prepares meals — and the nursery, where she tends to [[mary-olivier|Mary]]. Her presence is marked by sound and warmth: "Through the barred windows of the basement she could hear Catty singing in the pantry" [008-087].

Her Wesleyan [[god-and-religion|faith]] is integral to her identity. On [[social-gatherings|New Year]]'s Eve, Catty and [[jenny|Jenny]] go to the [[church-and-chapel|Wesleyan Chapel]] at [[ilford|Ilford]] "to sing the [[social-gatherings|New Year]] in" [008-023]. Catty's folk theology shapes [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s early imagination, particularly the ritual of opening the window at midnight "to let the Old Year go out and the [[social-gatherings|New Year]] come in" — a failure to do so would mean "something dreadful would happen" [008-023].

## Temperament and Faith

Catty's defining quality is her exuberant [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]. "Catty was full of [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]. She [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]d her relations; she [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]d the people at the [[church-and-chapel|Wesleyan Chapel]]" [009-095]. This [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]] extends to [[mary-olivier|Mary]] through physical affection and playful rituals: Catty would follow up [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s nursery rhymes with verses of her own, and [[mary-olivier|Mary]] knew without question that "Catty [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]d you. There was never the shadow of a doubt about it" [009-093].

Her happiness is visibly expressed: "Catty was happy when she sang and danced the dishcloth round and round the yellow pie-dish" [008-090]. This warmth stands in quiet contrast to the emotional austerity of the Olivier household under [[caroline-olivier|Caroline Olivier]].

Catty also shares in the household's small vanities: her purchase of a new hat for Easter Sunday is one of the associative details that fill [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s mind when she tries to focus on the crown of thorns during Bible reading [008-086].

## Relationship with Mary

Catty's devotion to [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is the most sustained and unconditional affection the child receives outside of [[mark-olivier|Mark]]. She wakes [[mary-olivier|Mary]] each morning at seven o'clock [010-027], is the one [[mary-olivier|Mary]] waits for in the dark [010-025], and sits with her through illness. The relationship is tactile and reassuring: "With Catty and [[jenny|Jenny]] you could measure the [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]] they had for you. The more they [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]d you, the safer you were" [009-106].

This bond proves durable. Decades later, Catty reaffirms it with the simple declaration that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] was always hers [037-050] — a claim of emotional ownership that no other character in the novel makes so unambiguously.
