## Role and Significance

Miss Horn is a minor character in the novel, the sister of [[young-horn|Young Horn]] and daughter of Mr. Horn, the [[morfe|Morfe]] grocer. She appears in the Maturity section (Book IV) as an intermediary figure who reveals to [[mary-olivier|Mary]] certain information about her family's discussions. Her parlour serves as a setting for a moment of social revelation rooted in [[morfe|Morfe]]'s small-town dynamics.

## Key Appearances

### The Letter in the Parlour (Book IV)

The most significant scene involving Miss Horn occurs when she waits for [[mary-olivier|Mary]] at the house door and takes her into her parlour [032-057]. There, an envelope lies on the maroon cloth of her table, and Miss Horn covers it with her hand — a gesture that suggests both discretion and the weight of its contents [032-058]. The parlour itself strikes [[mary-olivier|Mary]] as an "extraordinary" space, its existence extending back to earlier periods of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s life in [[morfe|Morfe]] [032-067]. Even as she presents the envelope, Miss Horn maintains a composed front, facing [[mary-olivier|Mary]] steadily; [[mrs-waugh|Mrs. Waugh]] later remarks on Miss Horn's "extraordinary" conduct in this episode [032-070].

### The Horn Household

Miss Horn belongs to the Horn family, whose presence is woven through the fabric of [[morfe|Morfe]] life. Her father, Mr. Horn, is the village grocer, whose yard and granary are familiar landmarks [024-349][028-001]. Her brother, [[young-horn|Young Horn]], figures in the subplot involving [[nannie-learoyd|Nannie Learoyd]], who waits for him in the Back Lane [031-152][031-222]. Miss Horn occupies a quieter but still defined position within this family — she keeps the parlour, receives visitors at the house door, and participates in the circulation of social knowledge that characterises [[morfe|Morfe]]'s close community.

## Character and Social Position

Miss Horn's conduct in the parlour scene suggests a woman accustomed to discretion and to managing delicate social situations. She waits at the door rather than sending a servant, indicating personal investment in the encounter; she covers the envelope with her hand, controlling the pace of disclosure. The repeated use of "extraordinary" to describe both her parlour and her conduct underscores her ambiguous position — she is at once ordinary (a grocer's daughter in a small village) and singular (the keeper of a parlour that "went back" through [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s memory of [[morfe|Morfe]]) [032-067].

## Connections to the Olivier Circle

Miss Horn's connection to [[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]] runs through the overlapping social circuits of [[morfe|Morfe]]: the [[church-and-chapel|chapel]], the grocer's yard, and the informal networks by which family news travels. Her role as a bearer of significant information — the envelope on the maroon cloth — places her at a node in the novel's larger pattern of communication and concealment. Like many of [[morfe|Morfe]]'s minor residents, she serves as a conduit through which the emotional pressures bearing on the Olivier family become visible to the wider community.
