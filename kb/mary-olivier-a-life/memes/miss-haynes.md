## Role and Significance

Miss Haynes is a governess at [[five-elms|Five Elms]] whose brief tenure in the Olivier household leaves a lasting impression on the young [[mary-olivier|Mary]]. She belongs to the long succession of governesses and teachers who pass through the Olivier children's lives — each one embodying a different mode of authority, affection, or limitation. Miss Haynes stands out for her gentleness and for the abruptness of her departure, which exposes the precariousness of a governess's position and the emotional cost of such ruptures for the children in their charge.

## Key Appearances

### Arrival and First Impressions

When Miss Haynes first appears, [[mary-olivier|Mary]] registers her "little gentle, tender hands" — a physical detail that immediately distinguishes her from the more severe governesses who have preceded her [021-022]. The narrator records Miss Haynes's hope that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] "would be happy" — a concern for the child's emotional wellbeing that sets her apart from instructors who prioritise discipline and accomplishment. This brief introduction establishes Miss Haynes as a figure of potential warmth in the cold institutional architecture of the Olivier nursery.

### The Dismissal

The most significant scene involving Miss Haynes is her sudden dismissal. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] enters "the white curtained room where Miss Haynes knelt by her trunk, packing" [021-079]. The image of the governess kneeling — a posture of supplication or defeat — before her open trunk conveys the abruptness and humiliation of her departure. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s response is immediate and involuntary: she cries out "Miss Haynes" and declares "I'm not expelled" [021-080] — a confusion that reveals how deeply she identifies with the governess's fate, as if dismissal were contagious. The scene crystallises one of the novel's recurring themes: the vulnerability of women who depend on employment in other people's households, and the emotional violence that such dependency can inflict.

## Character and the Governess Archetype

Miss Haynes belongs to a recognisable Victorian literary type — the gentle governess whose kindness to children contrasts with the harshness of the household that employs her. Her "tender hands" and her hope for [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s happiness mark her as emotionally available in ways that [[caroline-olivier|Caroline Olivier]], [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s own mother, is not. Yet her powerlessness — she can be dismissed at will, sent to kneel before her trunk — underscores the governess's liminal position: neither family member nor servant, she is expendable. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s identification with Miss Haynes (her cry of "I'm not expelled") suggests an early recognition of this shared vulnerability.
