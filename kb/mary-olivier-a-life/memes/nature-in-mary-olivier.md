# Nature in Mary Olivier

## Overview

Nature permeates Mary Olivier's world as both sensory refuge and philosophical ground. From childhood gardens to Yorkshire landscapes, the natural world offers Mary a form of experience that stands apart from the emotional turbulence of family life. Spinoza's doctrine of *natura naturans* and *natura naturata* — God as Nature creating and Nature created — provides the intellectual framework through which Mary understands her relationship to the physical world.

## Gardens and Early Sensory Memory

Mary's earliest memories are entangled with the garden at Five Elms. The garden flowers "stood still, straight up in the grey earth" (003-105), possessing a stillness and permanence that the human world lacked. She "loved best the flowers that you stooped down to look at and the flowers that were not Mamma's: the small crumpled poppy by the edge of the field, and the ears of the wild rye that ran up your sleeve and tickled you, and the speedwell, striped like the blue eyes of Meta, the wax doll" (003-108). This preference for wild, unowned flowers over cultivated ones reflects Mary's deeper resistance to possession and control.

The sumach tree, given to Mamma by Mark, becomes an emotional landmark. Mary watched it daily: "Every day you went out on to the steps to see if the sumach tree had grown" (003-117). The tree's fate intertwines with family dynamics — Papa's displeasure, Mark's gift, Mamma's care — making it a silent witness to the household's emotional weather.

## Animals as Companions

Animals provide Mary with relationships uncomplicated by human demands. Tibby the dog and Sarah the cat are sources of simple affection. The cat Sarah, "a white cat with a pink nose and pink lips and pink pads under her paws" (003-053), is described with a precision of observation characteristic of Mary's attention to the physical world. Dank's animal book is listed among Mary's early joys, and the garden flowers are noted to be "not like the animals" (003-106), suggesting that animals occupied a distinct and valued category in her childhood imagination.

## Landscape as Refuge

The Yorkshire landscape — "wide fields, flat and still, like water, flooded with the thin, clear light; grey earth, shot delicately with green blades, shimmering" (008-056) — offers a visual tranquillity that contrasts with the claustrophobia of family life. When Mary turns "from the quiet fields," something vital "would be gone" (015-008). The landscape's capacity to hold and restore her is a recurring motif. Sensory memory of landscape persists: "When you shut your eyes you could still see Bertha's scarlet frock on the white bridge path and smell the wet earth at the edges of the ford" (015-030).

## Spinozist Nature

Spinoza's philosophy gives Mary an intellectual vocabulary for what she already experiences intuitively. The *Encyclopaedia* explains: "'God, as the infinite substance, with its infinity of attributes is the *natura naturans*. As the infinity of modes under which his attributes are manifested, he is the *natura naturata*'" (016-014). Mary synthesises this: "Nature naturing would be the cause, and Nature natured would be the effect. God was both" (016-015). This pantheistic vision dissolves the boundary between the divine and the natural, making every tree and field a manifestation of God — a radical alternative to the Calvinist doctrine of original sin, which held that "the fault and corruption of the Nature of every man" (016-212) made the natural world itself fallen.
