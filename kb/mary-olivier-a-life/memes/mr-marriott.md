## Role in the Novel

Mr. Marriott never appears directly as a speaking character; he is present entirely through report and gossip — a disembodied spiritual force whose influence over [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]] rattles the Olivier family's fragile equilibrium. He is a minister in [[morfe|Morfe]], unmarried, and the object of [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]'s intense religious devotion: she attends his [[church-and-chapel|church]], writes him letters, and proclaims him "the wisest and the holiest man she ever knew" (016-059). The family responds to this attachment with alarm and mockery, suspecting that [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]'s religiosity masks an emotional — perhaps romantic — fixation. Mr. Marriott thus functions as a kind of absent centre around which the novel's themes of religious enthusiasm, repressed desire, and family surveillance crystallise.

Structurally, the Marriott episodes belong to the novel's middle books — Infancy and Adolescence — and are tightly bound to [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]'s narrative: her illness, her withdrawal from family life, and her eventual breakdown. Mr. Marriott is the magnet that draws her away from the Oliviers, just as other figures draw [[mary-olivier|Mary]] away from them; he is part of the novel's quiet insistence that the paths away from the family hearth are many, and not all of them lead outward into freedom.

## Biography

Mr. Marriott is first mentioned in connection with [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]'s mysterious illness during [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s infancy. "The illness had something to do with being married and going continually to Mr. Marriott's [[church-and-chapel|church]] and calling on Mr. Marriott and writing letters to him about [[god-and-religion|religion]]" (012-003). The narrator, filtering [[emilius-olivier|Papa]]'s perspective, observes the incongruity: "You couldn't say [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]] was not religious. But [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] said he would believe in her [[god-and-religion|religion]] if she went to [[mr-batty|Mr. Batty]]'s [[church-and-chapel|church]] or [[mr-farmer|Mr. Farmer]]'s or [[mr-propart|Mr. Propart]]'s. They had all got wives and Mr. Marriott hadn't. [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] had forbidden [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]] to go any more to Mr. Marriott's [[church-and-chapel|church]]" (012-003).

The crisis that follows involves several members of the extended family. "Mr. Marriott had written a nice letter to [[uncle-victor|Uncle Victor]], and [[uncle-victor|Uncle Victor]] had taken [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] to see him, and the doctor had come to see [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]] and she had been sent to bed" (012-004). The nature of the "nice letter" is never divulged; what matters is that the family treats Mr. Marriott's correspondence as a symptom of a wider disturbance requiring medical intervention.

By the time of the party in Chapter 17, Mr. Marriott has become a figure of wry social calculation: "Everybody had come except [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]; even Mr. Marriott, though he was supposed to be afraid of [[social-gatherings|parties]]. (You couldn't ask [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]] because of Mr. Marriott.)" (017-049). The parenthetical aside captures the family's settled narrative: [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]'s devotion to Marriott has rendered her socially unreliable, and Marriott's presence is an awkward necessity, a concession to propriety that nobody quite knows how to manage.

Years later, during [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]'s final illness in Chapter 31, the letters continue: "She could see [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]] sitting up in the big white bed and saying 'Love [[god-and-religion|God]] and you'll be happy,' as she scribbled letters to Mr. Marriott and hid them under the bedclothes" (031-268). The image of the sick woman hiding letters under her bedclothes suggests both secrecy and shame — a religious devotion that dare not show itself openly, infantilised by the family's disapproval.

In a late, cruel passage, [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] dismisses the whole cult of Marriott with medicalised mockery: "They may say what they like. It isn't the piano-tuner. It isn't the man who does the clocks. They know who it is. It isn't that Marriott man. I've found out something about him they don't know. He's got a false stomach. It goes by clockwork" (022-055). The "false stomach" — a prosthetic or medical appliance — is offered as a kind of proof that Marriott is not what [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]] imagines; the family weaponises intimate bodily knowledge against spiritual authority.

## Relationships

**[[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]**: The central relationship, though entirely mediated through report. [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]'s fixation on Mr. Marriott — her attendance at his [[church-and-chapel|church]], her letters, her declaration that he is "the wisest and holiest man" — is the object of persistent family anxiety. [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] forbids her from attending his [[church-and-chapel|church]]; the family treats her devotion as a symptom of illness. Whether Mr. Marriott himself reciprocates or even knows the extent of her attachment is never disclosed.

**[[uncle-victor|Uncle Victor]]**: Acts as intermediary during the early crisis, receiving Mr. Marriott's "nice letter" and taking [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] to see him (012-004). His role suggests that the family initially hoped for a negotiated settlement to the Charlotte-Marriott problem.

**[[emilius-olivier|Papa]] ([[emilius-olivier|Emilius Olivier]])**: Forbids [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]] from attending Mr. Marriott's [[church-and-chapel|church]], framing the issue in terms of propriety — Marriott is unmarried and therefore an unsuitable spiritual director for a married woman. His ban is the proximate cause of [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]'s crisis.

## Significance

Mr. Marriott is one of several absent religious figures in *[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]* — alongside [[dr-martineau|Dr. Martineau]], [[mr-propart|Mr. Propart]], and the various ministers who populate the novel's ecclesiastical landscape — whose authority is simultaneously invoked and undermined. What distinguishes him is the erotic subtext: the family's anxiety about [[aunt-charlotte|Aunt Charlotte]]'s devotion to an unmarried minister carries an unmistakable charge of sexual suspicion, never stated directly but legible in every prohibition and every sneer. He is a ghost at the edges of the Olivier family's emotional life, a reminder that the spiritual and the sexual are never far apart in the world May Sinclair depicts.
