## Description

Agaye is [[mr-sutcliffe|Mr. Sutcliffe]]'s garden and house in the South of France — "a pink and white house on the terrace" where the Sutcliffes spend their winters and where [[mr-sutcliffe|Mr. Sutcliffe]] finds his happiness. [[PN=030-061]] [[PN=030-039]] It is reached via the Paris-Lyons-Mediterranée express, passing through Dijon, Avignon, Toulon, and Cannes — a litany of Continental names that for Mary represent an enchanted alternative to the emotional confinement of her life in England. [[PN=030-061]]

The name itself — Agaye — exerts a phonetic spell on Mary: "Only the sounds, Agaye, rose up out of the other sounds, like a song, a slender foreign song, bright and clear, that you could sing without knowing what it meant." [[PN=030-061]] The place exists for her primarily as sound and promise before it exists as geography.

## Significance in the Narrative

Agaye is the novel's most concentrated symbol of desire and its renunciation. Mary's anticipated journey there with the Sutcliffes — stitched into being through the making of new underclothing — represents everything she wants: escape from family obligation, travel, beauty, and time with [[mr-sutcliffe|Mr. Sutcliffe]]. [[PN=030-061]]

The crisis of renunciation forms one of the novel's most extended psychological sequences. When Mary realizes that going to Agaye would mean leaving her brother [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]], the conflict is rendered with physical immediacy: "The thought of not going to Agaye dragged at her waist and made her feel weak, suddenly, as if she were trying to stand after an illness." [[PN=030-078]] Her prayer — "Make me not want to go to Agaye" — is answered by a moment of clarity: "She was not going to Agaye. She didn't want to go." [[PN=030-081]] [[PN=030-084]]

The renunciation is neither simple nor complete. Later, [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]] himself will say: "You oughtn't to have come back from Agaye" — suggesting that her sacrifice was understood and perhaps regretted by the brother for whom she made it. [[PN=031-284]] And after [[mr-sutcliffe|Mr. Sutcliffe]]'s death, the refrain becomes: "He'll be happier in his garden at Agaye." [[PN=031-343]]

Agaye also serves as a site of disillusionment. Mary observes that [[mr-sutcliffe|Mr. Sutcliffe]] confides: "I'm not really happy, Mary, till I'm pottering about my garden at Agaye" — but then admits that "you aren't really happy at Agaye" either, revealing that the promised place cannot deliver the happiness it seems to offer. [[PN=030-039]] [[PN=030-044]]

## Key Passages

> Only the sounds, Agaye, rose up out of the other sounds, like a song, a slender foreign song, bright and clear, that you could sing without knowing what it meant.
> [[PN=030-061]]

The name of the place becomes the place itself — a linguistic talisman. Mary's desire for Agaye is in part a desire for pure aesthetic experience, unmoored from practical knowledge.

> She had never wanted anything in her life as she wanted to go to Agaye with the Sutcliffes.
> [[PN=030-067]]

The superlative structure — "never wanted anything...as she wanted" — establishes this as the peak of Mary's desiring life, the point against which all other longings are measured.

> Make me not want to go to Agaye.
> [[PN=030-081]]

The prayer is startling in its precision: Mary does not ask for the wisdom to renounce, or the strength to bear renunciation. She asks to be relieved of desire itself — a characteristically Spinozist solution.

> She was not going to Agaye. She didn't want to go.
> [[PN=030-084]]

The double statement — first the fact, then the transformed desire — records a genuine inner change. Mary's "exquisite security and clarity and joy" that follows is one of the novel's most sustained accounts of grace.
