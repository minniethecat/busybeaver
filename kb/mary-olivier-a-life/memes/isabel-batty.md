## Role in the Novel

Isabel Batty appears in the novel's earliest chapters, during the infancy and early childhood sections that establish [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s developing consciousness. She is one of the real children who populate the periphery of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s imaginative world — a world in which dolls, real babies, and imaginary companions blur together in a single category of "children" over whom [[mary-olivier|Mary]] exercises a tender, maternal sovereignty.

Isabel's significance lies in her place within [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s taxonomy of be[[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]d objects. She is listed among [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s "seven" children: "There was Isabel Batty, and [[mrs-farmer|Mrs. Farmer]]'s red-haired baby, and the little girl at the turnpike; and [[catty|Catty]]'s baby that her aunt let her undress, and [[mrs-propart|Mrs. Propart]]'s baby, and Minna Acroyd's baby, and the railway girl's baby" (013-044). This catalogue demonstrates the fluid boundary in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s young mind between real children (Isabel), dolls, and the babies of neighbours — all are objects of a nurturing impulse that will later be redirected into intellectual and creative channels.

Her father, [[mr-batty|Mr. Batty]], is described as "the tall man by the [[church-and-chapel|church]]yard side, who called his daughter Isabel his 'pretty one'" (009-032). This brief portrait links Isabel to the world of [[morfe|Morfe]]'s [[church-and-chapel|church]] and its associated figures — [[mr-propart|Mr. Propart]], [[uncle-edward|Uncle Edward]] — who will later feature in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s horrified recognition of adult sexuality through [[bertha-mitchison|Bertha Mitchison]]'s revelations.

Isabel is ultimately one of the things [[mary-olivier|Mary]] must give up as she crosses the threshold from infancy into childhood proper. "She must give them all up. She must keep Isabel and the red-haired baby" (013-056) — the attempt to retain two of her seven children marks the beginning of a lifelong pattern of renunciation.

## Biography

Isabel Batty is introduced when [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is very young — the infancy chapters cover [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest years at [[five-elms|Five Elms]]. She is the daughter of [[mr-batty|Mr. Batty]], "the tall man by the [[church-and-chapel|church]]yard side" (009-032), a figure who, like many of the minor adult characters in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s early world, is defined by a single salient trait: his affectionate nickname for his daughter.

Isabel comes to tea at the Olivier house, and during one such visit [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s mother asks a pointed question: "Would you run away from Isabel if you saw her lying in her little [[funerals|coffin]]?" (010-016). The question, bizarre and unsettling to an adult reader, is presented as a natural part of the infant [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s moral [[school|education]]. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] replies that she would not run away. This episode, like the similar one involving the dead baby at the turnpike, is part of the novel's pervasive association of childhood with [[death-in-mary-olivier|mortality]] — an association that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] internalises without apparent distress.

Isabel reappears in the catalogue of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s seven children in chapter 013. By this point, [[mary-olivier|Mary]] has been told she is too old for dolls: "[[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] said she must give up her dolls" (013-043). The dolls are to be given to [[mrs-farmer|Mrs. Farmer]]'s red-haired baby, who "had no papa." [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s grief at this forced renunciation is acute, and her attempt to preserve two of her seven children — "She must keep Isabel and the red-haired baby" (013-056) — represents a last stand against the pressures of growing up.

After this point, Isabel Batty disappears from the novel. She belongs exclusively to the world of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s infancy, and her vanishing marks the closure of that world.

## Relationships

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: Isabel is one of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest companions and, more importantly, one of the objects of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s intense, possessive [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]. The relationship is not reciprocal in any realistic sense — [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s "children" exist primarily in her own imagination — but it is psychologically real. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s grief at having to give up Isabel is, proportionately, as intense as her later grief at losing [[jimmy|Jimmy]] or being betrayed by [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]].

**[[mr-batty|Mr. Batty]]**: Isabel's father, the "tall man by the [[church-and-chapel|church]]yard side" who calls her his "pretty one" (009-032). His presence by the [[church-and-chapel|church]]yard — the liminal space between the living and the dead — associates Isabel with the novel's persistent theme of childhood [[death-in-mary-olivier|mortality]]. [[mr-batty|Mr. Batty]], like [[mr-propart|Mr. Propart]] and [[uncle-edward|Uncle Edward]], is one of the dignified, slightly absurd male figures whose sexual lives [[mary-olivier|Mary]] will later find impossible to reconcile with their public personas.

**[[mrs-farmer|Mrs. Farmer]]**: The recipient of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s discarded dolls. [[mrs-farmer|Mrs. Farmer]]'s baby — the "red-haired baby" — is the only one of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s seven children she is allowed to keep, along with Isabel. The connection between Isabel and the red-haired baby (both "kept," both cherished) reinforces the equivalence in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s mind between real children and imaginary ones.

## Characterisation

Isabel Batty is a presence almost entirely without attributes. She has no described physical features, no speech, no actions beyond coming to tea. This absence of individuation is itself meaningful: in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s infant consciousness, people are not sharply distinguished from each other or from the world of objects and imaginings. Isabel exists on the same ontological plane as dolls and the "railway girl's baby" — all are recipients of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]], and all must eventually be relinquished.

The single detail attached to Isabel — her father's nickname for her, "his pretty one" — is borrowed, not original. We know Isabel not through herself but through her father's perception of her, much as the infant [[mary-olivier|Mary]] knows the world through the mediating perceptions of adults.

The "little [[funerals|coffin]]" episode (010-016) is characteristic of the novel's treatment of childhood. [[caroline-olivier|Caroline Olivier]]'s question — "Would you run away from Isabel if you saw her lying in her little [[funerals|coffin]]?" — is both morbid and pedagogical, part of a regime of emotional training that equips [[mary-olivier|Mary]] to face the [[funerals|death]]s that punctuate her childhood ([[emilius-olivier|Papa]], the dead baby at the turnpike, her own dolls' imagined [[funerals|death]]s). Isabel becomes, in this moment, not a companion but a thought-experiment, a vehicle for moral instruction.

## Selected Passages

> Mr. Batty, the tall man by the churchyard side, who called his daughter Isabel his "pretty one." (009-032)

The appositional construction — "the tall man by the [[church-and-chapel|church]]yard side" — embeds [[mr-batty|Mr. Batty]] in the landscape of [[morfe|Morfe]]'s [[church-and-chapel|church]] and its graveyard. The nickname "his pretty one" is tender but also possessive; Isabel is defined by her father's affection rather than by any quality of her own.

> "Would you run away from Isabel if you saw her lying in her little coffin?" (010-016)

The question, posed by [[caroline-olivier|Caroline Olivier]] to the infant [[mary-olivier|Mary]], encapsulates the novel's distinctive combination of maternal solicitude and morbid pedagogy. The phrase "her little [[funerals|coffin]]" — the adjective "little" mirroring Isabel's smallness — makes [[funerals|death]] intimate and domestic, something that happens to people you know and [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]].

> She had seven. There was Isabel Batty, and Mrs. Farmer's red-haired baby, and the little girl at the turnpike; and Catty's baby that her aunt let her undress, and Mrs. Propart's baby, and Minna Acroyd's baby, and the railway girl's baby. (013-044)

The catalogue form is characteristic of Sinclair's rendering of childhood consciousness. The seven "children" are listed without hierarchy, real and imagined, living and dead, in a single paratactic chain. The syntax itself performs the fluid boundary between categories that defines [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s infant world.

> She must give them all up. She must keep Isabel and the red-haired baby. (013-056)

The shift from "all" to "Isabel and the red-haired baby" enacts the process of renunciation and compromise. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] cannot keep everything, so she bargains: two out of seven. The choice of Isabel (a real child) and the red-haired baby (presumably also real) suggests that the living children, at least, can be retained — not as possessions but as memories, as figures in the mental landscape [[mary-olivier|Mary]] carries forward into childhood and beyond.
