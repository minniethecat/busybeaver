## Definition in Context

Poetry represents the hidden, vulnerable core of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s creative identity. Unlike philosophy, which she discusses openly with [[mark-olivier|Mark]] and pursues systematically, poetry is her secret—a private joy she guards from her mother's disapproval and even from her own intellectual self-critique. The novel presents poetry as [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest and most authentic mode of self-expression, predating her philosophical awakening and surviving its disappointments [[011-024]].

The novel's treatment of poetry is shaped by gender. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s brothers are permitted creative expression; her own poems must be hidden. When her Dorset-inspired poetry is described as "Dorian poetry perished," the phrase captures both a specific loss and a broader pattern of feminine artistic suppression [[025-016]].

> "If God knew how happy I am writing poetry he'd make the earth open and swallow me up." [[011-024]]

## Key Passages

### The Secret Joy of Poetry

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest explicit reference to poetry occurs in Book II (Childhood), when she is still embedded in the Calvinist framework that equates happiness with sin. The intensity of her joy in writing poetry is itself proof of its forbiddenness within [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s theological economy [[011-024]].

### Poetry Among Mary's Joys

[[mary-olivier|Mary]] lists poetry alongside trees, animals, [[music-in-mary-olivier|music]], and running races as her deepest pleasures. The catalogue places poetry in the domain of embodied, sensory experience rather than abstract intellectual activity, distinguishing it from her philosophical pursuits [[011-032]].

> "She loved best: trees and animals and poetry and music and running races and eating and drinking cinnamon toast." [[011-032]]

### Dorian Poetry Perished

A passing reference with enormous weight. The phrase appears in connection with [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s Dorset-inspired verses, which are described as having "perished." The loss is not explained—it is simply recorded, as one records a [[death-in-mary-olivier|death]]. The passive construction ("perished") refuses to assign blame, but the context—[[mark-olivier|Mark]]'s dismissiveness, [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s disapproval—is unambiguous [[025-016]].

> "To him music is dead and Dorian poetry perished—" [[025-016]]

### Poetry as Translation

Late in the novel, [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s creative identity is described in terms of translation rather than original composition. The shift from "writing poetry" to "translating" suggests both accommodation and continuity—the poetic impulse survives, transformed [[036-106]].

> "From the start that you're a poet translating; not the other way." [[036-106]]

## Character Perspectives

### Mary Olivier

For [[mary-olivier|Mary]], poetry is the form creativity takes before it has a name. In childhood it is indistinguishable from play; in adolescence it becomes secret; in maturity it is suppressed and then transmuted. Her relationship with poetry maps onto her broader struggle for self-ownership against [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s demands for renunciation [[011-024]].

### Caroline Olivier (Mamma)

[[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] never comments on [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s poetry directly, but the logic of her disapproval is clear. Poetry, like philosophy, is a form of self-assertion incompatible with the feminine ideal of self-effacement. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] hides her poems from [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] as she hides her philosophical books [[011-024]].

### Mark Olivier

[[mark-olivier|Mark]]'s role in the "perishing" of Dorian poetry is ambiguous but implicated. As [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s intellectual confidant, he might have been an ally; instead, his dismissal of the Dorset verses as adolescent effusions contributes to their loss. His own literary tastes—Byron, Shelley—are more conventional than [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s direct lyricism [[025-016]].

## Narrative Significance

Poetry functions as a submerged current throughout the novel, surfacing at moments of emotional intensity and then receding. Its suppression parallels [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s broader trajectory of self-renunciation under family pressure, and its persistence—as "translation"—suggests resilience rather than defeat.

The novel's own form—a poetic bildungsroman rendered in free indirect discourse—enacts the synthesis [[mary-olivier|Mary]] seeks. Her consciousness, rendered on the page, is itself a kind of poetry: attentive, rhythmic, attuned to the sensory world. What is "perished" as verse survives as perception.
