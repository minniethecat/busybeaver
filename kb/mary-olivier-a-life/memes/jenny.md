## Overview

Jenny — "old Jenny" as she is invariably called — is the Olivier family's nurse and one of the most constant presences in [[mary-olivier]]'s earliest years. A Wesleyan Methodist, she cares for [[mary-olivier|Mary]] from infancy through childhood, and her worn, creased face and tired rocking-chair form part of the sensory texture of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest memories.

## Role in the Nursery

From [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s very first remembered moments, Jenny is present. The nightly ritual of bedtime revolves around her [002-003]:

> Every night, when Jenny had gone away with the doll and the donkey, you hunched up the blanket and the stiff white counterpane to hide the curtain and you played with the knob in the green painted iron railing of the cot. [002-003]

She carries [[mary-olivier|Mary]] into rooms, sits in the rocking-chair by the nursery fire, and though her presence is steady, it is not indulgent. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] notes that "old Jenny didn't want her to be the baby" [002-039]. Jenny's face, etched by years, has "tiny creases and creases" [002-040], and she is "too tired to listen" [002-041] to a child's chatter.

Despite her weariness, [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s attachment is profound: "[[mary-olivier|Mary]] [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]d old Jenny next to [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] and [[mark-olivier|Mark]]" [002-042].

## Character and Faith

Jenny is a Wesleyan Methodist, and her [[god-and-religion|faith]] is central to her identity. Her niece [[catty]] is also a Wesleyan, and together they represent a religious tradition distinct from the Olivier family's rigid [[god-and-religion|Calvinism]]. Jenny's happiness is bound up with her [[god-and-religion|faith]] [002-039]:

> But Jenny was happy because she was going to wear the grey silk dress and live in Mr. Spall's cottage and talk to him about Jesus. [002-039]

Her later years see her married to [[mr-spall|Mr. Spall]], a fellow Wesleyan, and installed in his cottage — a modest but contented retirement from the Olivier household. The grey silk dress trimmed with black lace, laid out on the spare-room bed and inspected by [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] and [[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]] with disapproving shakes of the head, marks her transition to a life outside service [002-039].

## Relationship with the Children

Jenny's affections are particular. [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]] is explicitly designated as "Jenny's child," just as [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is [[catty]]'s. [[catty|Catty]] later explains: "Master Roddy was Jenny's child and you was always mine" [037-050]. This distribution of loyalties among the servants mirrors the complex web of attachments within the family.

[[mary-olivier|Mary]] recalls with regret her childhood treatment of Jenny [037-040]:

> "I must go and see Jenny's grave before I leave. I wish I hadn't teased her so to love me." [037-040]

This late, elegiac reflection — occurring when [[mary-olivier|Mary]] passes the [[london|City of London]] Cemetery by train — reveals the depth of the childhood attachment and the guilt that accompanies the memory.

## Significance

Jenny represents the world of servants whose lives are interwoven with the Olivier family yet remain distinct. Her Wesleyan [[god-and-religion|faith]], her quiet endurance, and her eventual modest happiness with [[mr-spall|Mr. Spall]] provide a counterpoint to the emotional austerity of the Olivier household. In the novel's taxonomy of [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]], Jenny occupies a specific place in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s hierarchy of attachments — below [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] and [[mark-olivier|Mark]], but firmly present in the foundational layer of her emotional life.
