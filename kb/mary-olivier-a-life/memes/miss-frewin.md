## Overview

Miss Frewin is a governess who lives with [[mrs-waugh|Mrs. Waugh]] in [[morfe|Morfe]], forming one half of a closely coupled pair — "[[mrs-waugh|Mrs. Waugh]] and Miss Frewin" appear together so frequently that they function as a compound social entity in [[mary-olivier]]'s observations of the community.

## Life with Mrs. Waugh

Miss Frewin resides in the house that "belonged to [[mrs-waugh|Mrs. Waugh]] and Miss Frewin" [024-140]. The two women are so inseparable that [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s mother is "angry with [[mrs-waugh|Mrs. Waugh]] and Miss Frewin for making [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] go there" [022-012] when [[mary-olivier|Mary]] falls ill after a visit. The pairing extends to their mannerisms: both are observed "with their heads on one side, considering her" [032-094] — a synchronized posture of genteel appraisal.

## Social Presence

At [[morfe|Morfe]] gatherings, Miss Frewin is a regular presence. She "arched her eyes and smiled, with little nodding bows" [024-147] — a "sidelong and slow" bow [024-149] that suggests careful social performance. She opens a "black silk bag" [024-161] at gatherings, a detail that fixes her in the material texture of [[morfe|Morfe]] drawing rooms.

At the dance, she is partnered with [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]]: "he danced: with Miss Frewin and [[louisa-wright|Miss Louisa Wright]], because he didn't like it" [029-118]. She is also among those who know about [[dorsy|Dorsy]]'s romantic disappointment — "[[mrs-waugh|Mrs. Waugh]] and Miss Frewin knew it" [028-001] — which places her firmly within the village's network of shared, unspoken knowledge.

## Significance

Miss Frewin belongs to the governess class that populates the margins of [[morfe|Morfe]] society — educated enough to teach, dependent enough to live in another woman's house, visible enough at gatherings to be noticed but never central. She and [[mrs-waugh|Mrs. Waugh]] together represent a particular type of female household that the novel registers without either celebrating or condemning.
