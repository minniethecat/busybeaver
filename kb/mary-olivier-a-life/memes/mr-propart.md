## Overview

Mr. Propart is the curate at [[church-and-chapel|Aldborough Hatch Church]] and one of the clerical figures who populate the religious landscape of [[mary-olivier]]'s childhood. Unlike the more severe religious influences in her life, Mr. Propart is mild, faintly comic, and oddly ineffectual — a clergyman who seems relieved when his duties are done.

## Clerical Manner

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest impression of Mr. Propart is of his "nice clean-shaved face" [008-095] as he reads the Passion narrative and preaches "about [[god-and-religion|God]]'s mercy and his justice." His delivery, however, is notably disengaged: "He did it all in a soothing, inattentive voice; and when he had finished he went quick into the vestry as if he were glad it was all over" [008-095]. At the [[church-and-chapel|church]] gate afterwards, "he didn't look as if Good Friday mattered very much" [008-095]. This portrait of a clergyman going through the motions is one of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest intuitions that the official [[god-and-religion|religion]] of her world may be hollow.

He is also evasive when faced with theological questions. When [[mary-olivier|Mary]] asks him what Pantheism is, "Mr. Propart smiled when you asked him and said it was a difficult question" [016-007] — a retreat from intellectual engagement that contrasts with [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s own urgent philosophical enquiries.

## Family and Social Standing

Mr. Propart is married to [[mrs-propart|Mrs. Propart]], who plays the "March in Scipio after Evening Service" [011-021], and has two sons, Humphrey and Arthur. The boys are described as "tame and domestic because they were always going about with Mr. Propart and talking to him as if they liked it" [009-032] — an arrangement so unusual in the Olivier children's experience that [[mark-olivier|Mark]] finds it remarkable.

Mr. Propart's one attempt at physical daring ends in farce: "[[mark-olivier|Mark]] had once seen Mr. Propart trying to jump a ditch on the Aldborough Road. It was ridiculous. Humphrey and Arthur had to grab him by the arms and pull him over" [009-032]. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s immediate thought is that her father [[emilius-olivier|Emilius Olivier]] "couldn't jump ditches any more than Mr. Propart; but then he knew he couldn't, and as [[mark-olivier|Mark]] said, he had the jolly good sense not to try" [009-032].

## The Pupils and the Books

Mr. Propart takes pupils at Aldborough Hatch, and their presence prevents the Olivier children from attending [[church-and-chapel|church]] there after they have had chickenpox [008-004]. He is a visitor to the Olivier household — "sometimes it would be the Proparts" [013-004] — and on one occasion "came into the library and stood and looked at the books" [019-072], borrowing a volume. The books "must have been" taken by him, as [[mary-olivier|Mary]] later notes they were never returned [019-077].
