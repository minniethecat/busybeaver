## Role in the Novel

Jem Alderson is a peripheral figure in *Mary Olivier: A Life*, serving as Roddy Olivier's point of contact and companion in Canadian emigration. He represents the practical, working-class world beyond the Olivier family's genteel poverty — a farmer's son who builds a life in Montreal and, in doing so, provides a destination for Roddy's escape from England. Jem functions as a minor but crucial narrative device: his existence in Canada makes Roddy's emigration materially possible [026-003].

## Biography

Jem Alderson is the son of a farming family in the Morfe area, his brother Ben Alderson owning a substantial farm near Montreal, Canada. When Roddy Olivier decides to emigrate to Canada to become a farmer, Jem is already planning to travel there in March [026-003].

Jem accompanies Roddy on the train journey to the port of departure, and the two travel together. Uncle Victor Olivier arranges for two hundred pounds to be paid into Jem Alderson's bank account in Montreal, suggesting that Jem was entrusted with both Roddy's passage money and his initial settlement funds [026-064]. Mary recalls seeing Roddy go off, "pleased and excited, with Jem Alderson in the train" [026-097].

After arriving in Canada, Jem appears to have looked after Roddy responsibly. Mary later reflects that "Jem Alderson must have taken mighty good care of him" [030-113], and that "Jem Alderson had taken care of him" [030-114]. The repetition of this sentiment — a rare instance in a novel where care is acknowledged and appreciated — underscores Jem's reliability despite his minor role.

From Canada, Roddy writes suggesting that Mamma should also emigrate, and Mamma at one point declares "Jem Alderson would" take Roddy in [031-057]. Roddy himself "had always meant to go out to Jem Alderson when he had learnt enough from Ned," suggesting that Jem Alderson remained a fixed point in Roddy's Canadian plans even after his initial farming apprenticeship elsewhere [031-368].

## Relationships

- **[[roddy-olivier]]**: Travel companion and Canadian contact. Jem provides the practical bridge between Roddy's Morfe life and his new life as a farmer in Canada.
- **[[uncle-victor]]**: Victor Olivier arranges the financial transfer to Jem Alderson's Canadian bank account, indicating trust placed in Jem as a reliable intermediary.

## Themes

Jem Alderson embodies the theme of emigration and the escape from England that runs through the novel's middle sections. His existence in Canada — running a farm, sending for others to join him — represents an alternative to the constrained English social world that confines Mary and Roddy. His quiet reliability contrasts with the emotional turbulence of the Olivier household, and the phrase "Jem Alderson must have taken mighty good care of him" stands out as one of the novel's few unequivocal endorsements of another person's character.
