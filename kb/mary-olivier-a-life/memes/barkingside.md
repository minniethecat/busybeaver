## Description

Barkingside is a village near [[ilford|Ilford]] that forms part of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest remembered world. It is connected to [[ilford|Ilford]] by Ley Street — a road that "swerved slightly towards Barkingside" — and by the fields and plantations between the two settlements. [[PN=008-055]] [[PN=009-010]]

The village has a [[church-and-chapel|church]], a Vicarage, and small shops — the components of a settled rural parish life — and its bells are among the sounds that define [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s auditory childhood, ringing alongside the bells of [[ilford|Ilford]] and the distant bells of the [[city-of-london|City of London]]. [[PN=008-029]]

## Church and Parish Life

The [[church-and-chapel|church]] at Barkingside is the Olivier family's place of Sunday worship. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] goes to [[church-and-chapel|church]] at Barkingside with [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]], "kneeling close" — a detail that captures the physical intimacy of mother and child in the formal setting of the parish service. [[PN=009-081]]

But the [[church-and-chapel|church]] is also a place of exclusion: [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is "not allowed to go to the [[church-and-chapel|church]] at Barkingside for fear of giving whooping-cough" — the childhood illness barring her from the communal space of worship. [[PN=008-004]] This early experience of being kept apart from the congregation foreshadows the pattern of isolation and exclusion that marks [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s later life.

[[mr-batty|Mr. Batty]], the Vicar of Barkingside, is a figure in the parish social world — he calls his daughter Isabella, a detail that marks the Vicarage as a site of domestic as well as clerical life. [[PN=009-032]] The family makes calls at "Barkingside Vicarage" as part of the round of parish social visits. [[PN=009-077]]

## Shops and Walks

The little shops of Barkingside, reached via Ley Street, are a destination for childhood walks — part of the small, bounded geography of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s infancy. [[PN=008-050]] Three tall poplars stand at the point where Ley Street turns toward the village, marking the threshold between [[ilford|Ilford]] and Barkingside as a landmark in the child's mental map. [[PN=008-055]]

The question "Supposing Barkingside church and Aldborough Hatch [[church-and-chapel|church]]…" captures the way the child maps her world through the institutions of parish life — [[church-and-chapel|church]]es as the fixed points of a known geography. [[PN=013-102]]

## Key Passages

> …the bells of Ilford, the bells of Barkingside, and far beyond the flats and the fields the bells of the City of London.
> [[PN=008-029]]

The auditory geography of childhood — bells layered in space, each with its own distance and character, mapping the world from the local to the distant.

> …not allowed to go to the church at Barkingside for fear of giving whooping-cough…
> [[PN=008-004]]

The child excluded from the congregation — illness as the first experience of being set apart from the community that will become a pattern of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s life.

> …down Ley Street to Barkingside where the little shops were…
> [[PN=008-050]]

The child's geography — the street leading to the village, the shops as destination, the small world rendered in its fullness.
