## Overview

Miss Thompson is the first governess employed by [[caroline-olivier|Caroline Olivier]] to teach the Olivier children. She comes on weekday mornings from ten to twelve, and her presence — quiet, thin, and shadowed by the [[funerals|death]] of her mother — leaves a lasting impression on [[mary-olivier]], for whom she becomes entangled with the child's first apprehensions of [[death-in-mary-olivier|mortality]].

## Appearance and Manner

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earliest description of Miss Thompson is filtered through her mother's voice: "[[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] called her Miss Thompson" [005-004]. The child observes that "Miss Thompson's face was so light and thin it might slip through your fingers if you touched it" [005-005] — an image of fragility that prefigures the revelation about her mother's [[funerals|death]].

Miss Thompson teaches the children "reading, writing, and the multiplication table" [005-012], and her moral framework is straightforward: "you were good if you knew your lessons and sat up straight" [005-039]. But the lessons are overshadowed by a deeper, unspoken lesson — the fact of [[funerals|death]].

## The Dead Mother

The central fact about Miss Thompson, in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s consciousness, is that "her mother was dead" [005-012]. This information, imparted by Caroline as a reason for good behaviour, becomes an obsession for [[mary-olivier|Mary]]: "She kept on thinking about Miss Thompson's mother. Miss Thompson's mother was dead. Miss Thompson's mother was dead and buried in a cemetery" [005-036].

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s fear transforms the governess: "Every time Miss Thompson came near her she saw the white stream of the graveyard flowing down after her" [005-037]. The dead mother follows Miss Thompson into the [[school|schoolroom]], and [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s pity for her — "[[mary-olivier|Mary]] was sorry for Miss Thompson" [005-034] — is inseparable from her terror. When Miss Thompson leaves the household, the departure is abrupt: "before they had time to be naughty Miss Thompson had gone" [005-041].

## Later Echoes

Miss Thompson's mother returns to [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s mind much later, when she thinks about [[funerals|death]] in [[morfe|Morfe]]: "somebody's body was cold and stiff like Miss Thompson's mother" [008-039]. The governess herself disappears from the narrative, but the lesson she inadvertently taught — that [[funerals|death]] is real, that it touches the people you know — stays with [[mary-olivier|Mary]] permanently.
