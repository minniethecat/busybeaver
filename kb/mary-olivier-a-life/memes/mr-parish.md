## Role in the Novel

Mr. Parish is the Olivier family's coachman, a figure so integral to the household's daily functioning that he becomes almost invisible — seen through the characters' eyes only when his services are contextually significant. Yet his wagonette is the vehicle of nearly every important departure and return in the novel's [[ilford|Ilford]] years: the Easter visit to the cemetery, [[mark-olivier|Mark]] and [[daniel-olivier|Dan]]'s leave-taking for school, [[aunt-lavvy|Aunt Lavvy]]'s departure, [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s own journey home from school. If trains and railway stations mark the larger arcs of separation in this novel, Mr. Parish's wagonette marks the local ones — the short, charged journeys between home and station, home and cemetery, that constitute the emotional geography of childhood.

Sinclair's rendering of Mr. Parish is characteristic of her treatment of servants: he is given no interiority, no speech beyond the functional, yet his repeated, rhythmic appearances — his high hat, his descent from the box, his shouldering of trunks — accrue a quiet dignity. He is the novel's embodiment of dependable service, the human infrastructure that makes the family's emotional life possible.

## Biography

Mr. Parish first appears driving the family to the cemetery on Easter Saturday: "They drove in Mr. Parish's wagonette, and called for [[aunt-lavvy|Aunt Lavvy]] at [[uncle-victor|Uncle Victor]]'s tall white house at the bottom of [[ilford|Ilford High Street]]" (008-100). The wagonette stops at the railway-crossing at Manor Park; [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] instructs Mr. Parish "to drive round to the Leytonstone side and wait for them there at the big gates" (008-104). The instruction establishes his role: he waits, he conveys, he reappears when needed.

His most emotionally charged appearance comes when [[mark-olivier|Mark]] and [[daniel-olivier|Dan]] are leaving for school. "Outside in the road Mr. Parish was descending from the box of his wagonette" (009-068). [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] checks his watch; he is going with them to [[school|Chelmsted]]. After the leave-taking, [[mary-olivier|Mary]] and Roddy "had stopped their ears so as not to hear the wheels of Mr. Parish's wagonette taking [[mark-olivier|Mark]] and [[daniel-olivier|Dan]] away" (009-078). The wagonette's wheels become, in this moment, the audible sign of loss — a sound that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] actively tries to block out.

Mr. Parish's wagonette also conveys happier journeys. "Or Mr. Parish's wagonette would be ordered, and [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] and [[mary-olivier|Mary]] would put on their best clothes very quick and go up to [[london|London]] with him" (009-078). These [[london|London]] outings — to St. Paul's, Maskelyne and Cooke's, the National Gallery — are among the rare moments of uncomplicated pleasure in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s childhood, and Mr. Parish is their unremarked facilitator.

A small, vivid detail fixes his presence in the child's imagination: "'You see Mr. Parish's high 'at a driving in his wagonette'" (010-005). The high hat — the formal accoutrement of a coachman — is also a portent: "When he put the black top on his wagonette it looked like a hearse" (010-006). The child's association of the wagonette with [[funerals|funeral]]s is not incidental; many of the journeys it makes are to or from cemeteries, stations where [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]d ones depart, houses where the bereaved wait.

In the novel's later pages, Mr. Parish remains the family's driver. "Aware of Mr. Parish shouldering the trunk, she turned into the open dining-room" (021-103). The shouldering of trunks — [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s, [[mark-olivier|Mark]]'s, [[daniel-olivier|Dan]]'s, [[aunt-lavvy|Aunt Lavvy]]'s — is Mr. Parish's recurring gesture, the physical sign of his role as the bearer of the family's transitions.

## Relationships

**The Olivier Family**: Mr. Parish serves the household as coachman, his duties spanning the full range of the family's movements — cemetery visits, railway-station departures, [[london|London]] outings, and the transport of visitors like [[aunt-lavvy|Aunt Lavvy]].

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: Through [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s childhood consciousness, Mr. Parish's wagonette becomes freighted with emotional significance — its wheels signalling loss, its high hat suggesting [[death-in-mary-olivier|mortality]]. He himself remains a kind, silent presence at the periphery of her childhood.
