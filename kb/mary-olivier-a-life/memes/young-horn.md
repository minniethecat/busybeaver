## Overview

Young Horn is the son of Mr. Horn, the [[morfe|Morfe]] grocer, and a labourer whose covert romantic involvement with [[nannie-learoyd|Nannie Learoyd]] becomes one of [[mary-olivier]]'s most troubling moral discoveries. He appears only through the lens of what [[mary-olivier|Mary]] witnesses from windows and lanes — a figure of illicit desire whose actions force her to reckon with the gulf between adult behaviour and the moral order she has been taught.

## The Nannie Learoyd Affair

[[mary-olivier|Mary]] first becomes aware of young Horn when she sees [[nannie-learoyd|Nannie Learoyd]]'s face pressed against the kitchen windowpane, "waiting for young Horn to come out to her" [031-152]. The image is furtive and unsettling. Later, from her vantage point at the back of the house, [[mary-olivier|Mary]] sees Nannie "sitting on the stone stairs of Horn's granary, waiting for young Horn to come round the corner of his father's house" [031-222].

What [[mary-olivier|Mary]] witnesses next is a fixed, almost sculptural vision of intimacy: "It would be [[nannie-learoyd|Nannie Learoyd]] and young Horn. They were fixed in one block together by the stone wall" [031-226]. The phrase "fixed in one block" conveys both the physical closeness of the couple and the way the image becomes lodged, immovable, in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s consciousness.

## Moral Reckoning

The relationship between Nannie and young Horn is contrasted, to Horn's detriment, with [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]]'s simultaneous involvement with Nannie. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s brother [[daniel-olivier|Dan]] describes Horn as "a man who was no better than young Horn, who had shown her that he didn't care for her by going to Nannie" [031-246] — a tangled assessment in which Horn figures both as seducer and as the baseline against which other men are measured.

For [[mary-olivier|Mary]], the discovery is painful because she cannot tell her mother what she has seen, and because Nannie's "trusting smile" [031-245] as she waits for Horn reveals a vulnerability that makes moral judgment feel inadequate.
