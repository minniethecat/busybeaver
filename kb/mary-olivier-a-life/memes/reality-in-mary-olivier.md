## Overview

"Reality" in _[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]: A Life_ is the novel's master philosophical term. It operates on three levels: the metaphysical (what is ultimately real, opposed to what merely appears), the perceptual (moments when ordinary experience yields to something more vivid and true), and the existential (the question of whether reality can be lost, and what it would cost to keep it). Across these registers, the novel draws on [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s identification of God with Nature — "facts are but appearances, laws the reality" — while pushing toward a more personal, experiential account of what it means to know reality.

## Appearances and Laws

Mary's first intellectual encounter with the concept of reality comes through her reading of the French psychologist Théodule Ribot, whose laws of heredity offer a new lens on her family. "You could see why Ribot was so excited about his laws of Heredity: 'They it is that are real … facts are but appearances, laws the reality'" [031-255]. This formulation — that the regularities underlying phenomena are more real than the phenomena themselves — maps onto the Spinozist framework she has begun to absorb. For fifteen years, she has been "trying to find out the reality — if there was any reality — that hid behind appearances, piggishly obtuse to the interest of appearances themselves" [031-253].

The philosophical discovery is liberating in one respect and impoverishing in another. It liberates Mary from the tyranny of immediate family dynamics: "the sheer interest of it made you forget about Papa and Mamma and Aunt Charlotte; it kept you from thinking about yourself" [031-255]. But it also distorts her relationship to the sensory world, as she has "cared for nothing in them but their beauty, and its exciting play on her emotions" [031-253].

## Reality Breaking Through

The novel distinguishes between thinking *about* reality and being *aware* of it. In the garden at night, after Richard Nicholson has left, Mary experiences the dissolution of the barrier that ordinarily separates her from the real: "For twenty-three years something had come between her and reality. She could see what it was now. She had gone through life wanting things, wanting people, clinging to the thought of them, not able to keep off them and let them go" [040-052].

This "reality breaking through, if only in flashes coming and going" [040-051] contrasts with Mary's earlier solely intellectual approach: "She had never been aware of it before; she had only thought about and about it, about Substance, the Thing-in-itself, Reality, God. Thinking was not being aware" [040-051]. The distinction between conceptual knowledge and experiential acquaintance is one the novel treats as philosophically serious.

## The Impermanence of the Real

Reality in the novel has a temporal dimension: it appears in flashes rather than as a permanent possession. In a moment of shared stillness, "these supernaturally clear things had reality, a unique but impermanent reality. It would last as long as you sat there and would go when you went" [037-020]. The most intense encounters with the real are defined by their transience — to take them up and examine them is to lose the quality that made them real: "If you took it up and looked at it the clearness, the unique, impermanent reality would be gone, and you would never get it again" [037-024].

## Reality and the Self

The novel's most radical claim comes in its linking of reality to selfhood. When Mary asks herself whether she could give up her experience of reality to regain Richard, the answer is unambiguous: "Knowing reality is knowing that you can't lose it" [040-061]. To lose reality would be to lose "my real self" — a self that is not separable from its apprehension of what is real [040-060]. This is the endpoint of the Spinozist thread: the self that knows reality is the "Self of your self. Reality of reality" [037-146], a self whose freedom consists in its consciousness of God.
