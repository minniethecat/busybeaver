Wanstead Flats is an expanse of open grassland and commons in East London, forming part of the southern tip of Epping Forest. In the novel, it appears as a landmark on the route between Ilford and central London, crossed by the Olivier children during family journeys.

## A Landmark on the Journey

Roddy excitedly identifies Wanstead Flats during a wagonette journey, marking it as a significant waypoint on the route to London.[^1] The open landscape of the Flats contrasts with the built-up areas of Ilford and the City, representing a threshold between suburban and urban space.

> Roddy whispered excitedly that they were in Wanstead Flats. [008-105]

The journey across Wanstead Flats generates excitement in the children, suggesting the Flats represent adventure and the approach to the larger world of London beyond.[^2] For Mary, the crossing of this open space may also carry symbolic weight, as the Flats lie adjacent to the City of London Cemetery — a juxtaposition of open life and the city of the dead.

> Across Wanstead Flats." He was excited. [008-113]

## Geography and Views

From the upper windows of Chadwell Grange, one can look beyond Aldborough Hatch towards Wanstead Flats and the City of London Cemetery.[^3] This panoramic vista connects the suburban domestic world of Ilford with the open commons and the distant cemetery, framing the geography of Mary's childhood.

> From the top-storey windows of Chadwell Grange you could look beyond Aldborough Hatch towards Wanstead Flats and the City of London Cemetery. [010-031]

## Historical Context

In the late Victorian period, Wanstead Flats was a well-known open space used for recreation, grazing, and public gatherings. It was the site of significant protests in the 19th century over enclosure and public access rights. By the time of the novel's setting (1860s–1890s), it had been secured as public open space under the Epping Forest Act, preserving it from development.
