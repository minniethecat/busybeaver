# Music in Mary Olivier

**Music** functions in [[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]] as both a narrative
device and a thematic symbol of transcendent experience. From [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s
childhood piano lessons with [[miss-sippett|Miss Sippett]] to the adult [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s
consuming engagement with Chopin, music provides one of the novel's
primary modes of access to states of consciousness beyond language
and rational thought.

## Music and Childhood

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s musical [[school|education]] begins in the domestic sphere of
[[five-elms|Five Elms]]. [[miss-sippett|Miss Sippett]], her music teacher, visits to
give lessons, and [[mary-olivier|Mary]] learns to approach the piano with a mixture
of discipline and emotional intensity. The lessons are among the few
structured activities in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s childhood that connect her to a world
beyond the Olivier household:

> "Miss Sippett came to give her her music lesson. She was always careful
> to play her very best for Miss Sippett."
> [011-027]

Music occupies a category distinct from other childhood pursuits.
Sinclair groups it with "poetry and music and running races and
playing games" [011-032] — activities that engage the body and the
imagination simultaneously, in contrast to the abstract labor of
schoolwork or the emotional demands of family life.

## Music and Religion

For the young [[mary-olivier|Mary]], music and [[god-and-religion|religion]] are intimately connected.
She perceives music as the most accessible dimension of religious
experience:

> "The nicest part of religion was music, and knowing how the world was
> made."
> [011-008]

This association between music and the sacred persists throughout
the novel, even as [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s formal religious belief erodes. Music
becomes a secular channel for experiences that religious language
can no longer contain — a "noble, serious, passionate" [017-076]
alternative to doctrinal [[god-and-religion|Christianity]].

## Mamma and the Forbidden Music

A pivotal moment in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s musical development involves her mother's
response to her playing. [[caroline-olivier|Caroline Olivier]] "hated it
when you played her music" [017-074], a prohibition that transforms
certain pieces into objects of guilty fascination. The tension between
[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s musical ambition and her mother's possessiveness mirrors the
larger pattern of their relationship, in which Caroline's [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]] operates
as a form of control.

The "noble, serious, passionate music" [017-076] that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is drawn to
represents everything her mother's domestic ideology cannot accommodate:
intensity, ambition, and a form of self-expression that is not mediated
by familial obligation.

## Music as Consciousness

Sinclair uses musical experience to render states of consciousness that
resist straightforward narration. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s thoughts move in "a sort of
syncopated time" to "the beat of the music" [017-079], and she lies
"awake at night thinking of the music" [025-005]. The music "beat on
the thick stone walls" [025-008] of her inner life, becoming a metaphor
for the novel's central concern with the texture and rhythm of
subjective experience.

## The Chopin Performance

In a late scene, a character named Prichard performs Chopin's
"Fontana Polonaise," "straining the little music room" [022-003]
with the force of the performance. This moment crystallizes music's
function in the novel: it is a force that exceeds containment,
that "strains" the domestic spaces in which it is performed, much
as [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s consciousness strains against the limitations of her
environment.

## Music and the Death of Art

The novel's most elegiac passage linking music and [[death-in-mary-olivier|mortality]] appears
in its later sections:

> "And with him music is dead and Dorian poetry perished."
> [025-016]

This lament connects music to a broader meditation on the fragility
of art and the [[death-in-mary-olivier|mortality]] of those who create and sustain it. Music,
which had seemed to offer access to the eternal, is revealed as
contingent — dependent on living performers, listeners, and the
cultural conditions that make aesthetic experience possible.
