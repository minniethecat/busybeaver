## Overview

Old Mrs. Heron is a fixture of the [[morfe|Morfe]] community, living with her niece [[dorsy|Dorsy Heron]] in a "dark cottage that crouched under the hill" [033-005]. She belongs to the generation of the elderly whom [[mary-olivier]] observes with a mixture of pity and apprehension — part of the landscape of ageing and [[funerals|death]] that surrounds [[morfe|Morfe]]'s younger inhabitants.

## Life at Morfe

Mrs. Heron is first listed among the "old" residents of [[morfe|Morfe]]: "old Mrs. Heron. They were all old" [024-354]. Her cottage is a regular stop for afternoon visits, where [[dorsy|Dorsy]] reads the newspaper to her. She is one of the community members for whom prayers are offered — "for all prisoners and captives; for women labouring of child; for old Mrs. Heron; for [[dorsy|Dorsy Heron]]" [028-154].

Her social world is small but consistent: she is linked to the Kendals, to [[louisa-wright|Louisa Wright]], to the Waughs — the network of [[morfe|Morfe]] residents whose lives intersect in drawing rooms, at [[church-and-chapel|church]], and on the village green.

## Illness and Death

Mrs. Heron breaks her thigh, and the injury precipitates a crisis. The community gathers in sympathy: "They were sorry for Mrs. Heron. They wondered what poor [[dorsy|Dorsy]] would do if anything should happen to Mrs. Heron" [033-013]. The question is not only about care but about [[dorsy|Dorsy]]'s entire situation — her dependence on her aunt, her lack of other prospects.

Mrs. Heron dies "of a stroke in the first week after [[christmas|Christmas Day]]" [037-002]. Her [[funerals|death]], coming at the year's turning point, is one of the markers by which [[mary-olivier|Mary]] measures the relentless passage of time in [[morfe|Morfe]]. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] reflects later that someone she knows "thinks she'll die like Mrs. Heron" [037-134] — a phrase in which Mrs. Heron's [[funerals|death]] becomes a benchmark, a template for understanding other losses.

## Significance

Mrs. Heron represents the elderly generation whose decline and passing form the background rhythm of [[morfe|Morfe]] life. Her [[funerals|death]] is not a turning point in the plot but a fact of existence — quiet, inevitable, and mourned in the restrained way of a community accustomed to its losses.
