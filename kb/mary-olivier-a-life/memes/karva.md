## Description

Karva is a hill near [[morfe|Morfe]], distinguished by its "high, purple mound" and the moorland that surrounds it. [[PN=024-017]] It rises above the fields on the south side of the village, a landmark visible from the schoolhouse lane and the walking routes that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] and [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]] follow. [[PN=026-093]]

The hill is part of a larger landscape system that includes [[greffington|Greffington Edge]] and the moor — "the half-ring of moor that lay under Karva." [[PN=024-333]] It is a working landscape as well as a scenic one: sheep move slowly up its slopes, their bleating carried down by the hill's acoustics. [[PN=024-342]]

## Significance in the Narrative

Karva is the site of one of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s most significant philosophical experiences. Walking over Karva with [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]], she listens as he "turns it all into a play" — transforming the landscape into narrative, just as she transforms it into philosophy. [[PN=026-097]]

Under moonlight, Karva becomes associated with the sublime: "She was in the solemn beauty of Karva under the moon. In the *Critique of Pure Reason*." [[PN=027-052]] The juxtaposition — landscape beauty and [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s philosophical text — captures the novel's characteristic fusion of sensuous experience and intellectual life.

[[mary-olivier|Mary]] goes up Karva to look for lost things — physical and metaphorical — and lies on her back under the hill dreaming her "Dream-Play," the creative work that expresses her inner life. [[PN=028-043]] [[PN=032-015]]

## Physical Freedom and Health

Karva functions as a measure of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s physical well-being. When she is recovering from illness, the ability to "walk up the lower slopes of Karva without panting" marks her return to health. [[PN=030-144]] The hill is the destination of walks with her father in his final years, a shared activity that briefly restores connection. [[PN=030-147]] [[PN=031-033]]

## The South Slope Fields

The fields on the southern slope of Karva have names that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] can recite by heart — part of her deep knowledge of the local landscape, a geography of belonging. [[PN=034-020]] Yet Karva is also a place she sometimes avoids — "Not up Karva because of her secret happiness" — when her inner state is too precious to risk disturbance by the outer world. [[PN=036-054]]

## Key Passages

> …the high, purple mound of Karva Hill.
> [[PN=024-017]]

The novel's first description of Karva, establishing its visual dominance in the landscape through colour — "purple" — the same hue that marks the novel's attention to light and atmosphere.

> She was in the solemn beauty of Karva under the moon. In the *Critique of Pure Reason*.
> [[PN=027-052]]

The fusion of landscape and philosophy that defines [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s experience of Karva — natural beauty and intellectual labour are not opposed but continuous.

> Lying on her back under Karva, she dreamed her "Dream-Play."
> [[PN=032-015]]

Karva as creative space — the physical posture of lying under the hill enabling the imaginative work that matters most to [[mary-olivier|Mary]].
