## Role in the Novel

Mr. Sippett belongs to the small company of educators who furnish [[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]'s early intellectual life. He is the boys' tutor — Rodney's tutor, strictly speaking — but his accidental presence in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s world is one of those quiet hinges on which a mind turns. "If it had not been for Mr. Sippett and 'Mensa: a table,' she would have known as much as Roddy" (005-040): the throwaway line records a genuine debt. Mr. Sippett, by overhearing and absorbing his Wednesday-morning Latin lessons, gains her first foothold in the classical [[school|education]] from which her sex would otherwise exclude her.

He is also, through his sister, the conduit to [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s [[music-in-mary-olivier|music]]al [[school|education]] — or rather, to the one tune she loathes: "Violetta," which "of all hateful and disgusting tunes the most disgusting and the most hateful was" (011-026). The Sippetts thus span the range of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s early learning: Latin and arithmetic on one side (Mr. Sippett, welcome), piano on the other ([[miss-sippett|Miss Sippett]], endured). Together they represent the patchwork, ad-hoc quality of Victorian female [[school|education]] — a lesson here, a lesson there, whatever the household can afford or whatever a brother leaves behind.

## Biography

Mr. Sippett appears first in Chapter 5, during [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s infancy at [[ilford|Ilford]]: "Every Wednesday at half-past eleven the boys' tutor, Mr. Sippett, looked in and taught Rodney 'Mensa: a table'" (005-012). The bare facts — Wednesday, half-past eleven, "Mensa: a table" — are given with the precision of a child's memory, where the rhythm of the week is fixed by its small events. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is not Mr. Sippett's pupil; she is present by accident, a younger sibling in the room. But she listens.

The listening pays off. "By midsummer [[mary-olivier|Mary]] could read the books that [[daniel-olivier|Dank]] read. If it had not been for Mr. Sippett and 'Mensa: a table,' she would have known as much as Roddy" (005-040). The construction is characteristic of Sinclair's free indirect discourse: the child's rivalry with her brother ("known as much as Roddy") is inseparable from her gratitude to the tutor who, without intending it, gave her the means to compete.

Mr. Sippett also teaches arithmetic, and his explanations become entangled with [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s nighttime metaphysical speculations: "At night when you lay on your back in the dark you thought about being born and about arithmetic and [[god-and-religion|God]]. The sacred number three went into eighteen sixty-nine and didn't come out again; so did seven. She liked numbers that fitted like that with no loose ends left over. Mr. Sippett said there were things you could do with the loose ends of numbers to make them fit. That was fractions. Supposing there was somewhere in the world a number that simply wouldn't fit? Mr. Sippett said there was no such number" (008-037). The child's meditation moves from arithmetic to metaphysics — the loose ends of numbers, the problem of fitting — and it is Mr. Sippett's offhand assurance ("there was no such number") that provides the foundation for her trust in an orderly, intelligible universe.

Mr. Sippett's sister enters the narrative as [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s piano teacher. "Of all hateful and disgusting tunes the most disgusting and the most hateful was 'Violetta,' which Mr. Sippett's sister taught her. But if [[mark-olivier|Mark]] would promise to make [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] let her learn Greek she would play it to him twenty times running" (011-026). [[miss-sippett|Miss Sippett]] is the price [[mary-olivier|Mary]] pays for the hope of Greek: endure Violetta, and [[mark-olivier|Mark]] may intercede with [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]].

After [[mark-olivier|Mark]] departs for [[school|Chelmsted]], he leaves [[mary-olivier|Mary]] his Greek Accidence and Smith's Classical Dictionary. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] teaches herself Greek in the hour before [[miss-sippett|Miss Sippett]]'s lesson, and "she was always careful to leave the Accidence open where [[miss-sippett|Miss Sippett]] could see it and realise that she was not a stupid little girl" (011-027). But "whether [[miss-sippett|Miss Sippett]] saw the Accidence or not she always behaved as if it wasn't there" (011-028). [[miss-sippett|Miss Sippett]]'s refusal to acknowledge the Greek book is a minor cruelty — the tutor who will not see what her pupil can do — and it prefigures the larger pattern of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s intelligence being overlooked or denied by those in authority over her.

## Relationships

**Roddy ([[roddy-olivier|Rodney Olivier]])**: Mr. Sippett's actual pupil, whose Latin lessons [[mary-olivier|Mary]] overhears and absorbs. The asymmetry of their [[school|education]] — Rodney gets a tutor, [[mary-olivier|Mary]] gets to listen — is the quiet engine of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s intellectual hunger.

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: The unofficial pupil whose debt to Mr. Sippett is genuine but qualified: he gave her "Mensa," but she gave herself everything that followed from it.

**[[miss-sippett|Miss Sippett]]**: His sister, the piano teacher whose "Violetta" [[mary-olivier|Mary]] loathes and who refuses to acknowledge the Greek Accidence open on the table. The Sippett siblings thus divide neatly into welcome and unwelcome instruction — the intellect and the drudgery, the mind and the fingers.

**[[mark-olivier|Mark Olivier]]**: Leaves [[mary-olivier|Mary]] his Greek Accidence when he departs for [[school|Chelmsted]], the gift that makes [[miss-sippett|Miss Sippett]]'s indifference visible and painful.
