## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Farmer belongs to the constellation of clerical families — the Battys, the [[mr-propart|Propart]]s, the Farmers — that form the Olivier family's social circle at [[ilford|Ilford]]. She appears in the childhood and early adolescence chapters as a recurring minor figure: calling at the house, attending [[social-gatherings|parties]], producing babies, and contributing her soft drawl to the chorus of provincial voices. Her function is primarily atmospheric — she is part of the social furniture against which [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s more intense inner life is set.

In the religious crisis of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s adolescence, Mrs. Farmer takes on a sharper outline. She is among those who whisper, "[[mary-olivier|Mary]] is an Infidel" (016-005), and the soft drawl that once seemed merely provincial now carries the edge of communal judgement. She represents the decent, conventional, unreflective [[god-and-religion|Christianity]] against which [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s philosophical searching defines itself.

## Biography

Mrs. Farmer is introduced through her husband: "[[mr-farmer|Mr. Farmer]], the curate of [[church-and-chapel|St. Mary's Chapel]], who walked up and down the room all night with the baby" (009-032). The detail of the curate walking the baby — performing domestic labour usually assigned to the mother or nurse — establishes the Farmers as a household where traditional roles are slightly softened, where the husband is involved in childcare to a degree unusual in Victorian middle-class families.

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s childhood visits to Mrs. Farmer are remembered as pleasures: "You did forget sometimes when nice things happened; when you went to see Mrs. Farmer's baby undressed" (010-011). The baby, "red-haired" (013-044), is a fixture of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s early consciousness — one of the "seven" be[[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]d figures in her private catalogue, alongside [[isabel-batty|Isabel Batty]], [[mark-olivier|Mark]], [[daniel-olivier|Dank]], and "the three very little babies you made up out of your head" (013-044). The real baby and the imagined ones blur together in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s inner world, a reminder of the intensity with which she invests in objects of affection.

The Farmers are regular visitors to the Olivier household. "Sometimes Mr. and Mrs. Farmer would come in, and [[mr-farmer|Mr. Farmer]] would play chess with [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] while Mrs. Farmer talked to [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] about how troublesome and independent the tradespeople were, and how hard it was to get servants and to keep them" (013-004). The division of labour is gendered and class-bound: the men play chess, the women discuss household management. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s narrative voice — "[[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] listened to Mrs. Farmer as if she were saying something wonderful and exciting" — registers the gap between the content of the conversation and [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s absorption in it, a gap that measures the narrowness of [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s intellectual world.

Mrs. Farmer's speech is distinguished by a "soft drawl" that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] reproduces with phonetic precision: "'And a resident plumber. Yes, [[caroline-olivier|Mrs. Olivier]], you really wou-ould'" (016-172). The stretched vowel — "wou-ould" — captures the drawl and, implicitly, the leisure that produces it. This is the voice of a woman with time to draw out her vowels, time to discuss the servant problem at length, time to call and stay to tea.

The social calls continue into [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s adolescence, but the tone darkens. "Mr. and Mrs. Farmer had called and stayed to tea. Across the room you could see his close, hatchet nose and straggly beard. Every now and then his small, greenish eyes lifted and looked at you" (016-173). The description of [[mr-farmer|Mr. Farmer]] — "hatchet nose," "straggly beard," "small, greenish eyes" — is rendered with a physical distaste that colours the whole Farmer household. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s earlier pleasure in visiting Mrs. Farmer's baby now seems remote: "Impossible that you had ever enjoyed going to Mrs. Farmer's to see the baby. It was like something that had happened to somebody else, a long time ago" (016-174).

The estrangement is not personal but developmental. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] has outgrown babies — and, by extension, the world of Mrs. Farmer, whose life is defined by the cycle of pregnancy, infant care, and domestic management. "Mrs. Farmer was always having babies, and always asking you to go and see them. She couldn't understand that as you grew older you left off caring about babies" (016-174). The inability to understand — "she couldn't understand" — is a quiet indictment of the imaginative limitations of provincial womanhood.

In the religious crisis precipitated by [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s flirtation with pantheism, Mrs. Farmer joins the whispering chorus: "She could hear [[aunt-bella|Aunt Bella]] talking to [[uncle-edward|Uncle Edward]], and Mrs. Farmer and [[mrs-propart|Mrs. Propart]] whispering: '[[mary-olivier|Mary]] is an Infidel'" (016-005). The whisper is the weapon of the powerless — the women who cannot act directly against [[mary-olivier|Mary]] register their disapproval through the sanctioned channels of gossip. Mrs. Farmer's whisper, like her drawl, is a social performance: it signals membership in the community of the respectable while excluding [[mary-olivier|Mary]] from it.

## Relationships

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: Mrs. Farmer is a figure from [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s childhood whose significance fades as [[mary-olivier|Mary]] matures. The baby visits, once a source of pleasure, become incomprehensible to the adolescent [[mary-olivier|Mary]]. The relationship charts [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s movement away from the domestic and the conventional — the world Mrs. Farmer represents — toward the intellectual and the spiritual.

**[[caroline-olivier|Caroline Olivier]] ([[caroline-olivier|Mamma]])**: Mrs. Farmer is one of [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s social peers, and their conversations about servants and tradespeople place both women firmly in the world of Victorian middle-class domesticity. [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s absorption in these conversations (013-004) is one measure of her distance from [[mary-olivier|Mary]].

**[[emilius-olivier|Emilius Olivier]] ([[emilius-olivier|Papa]])**: Mrs. Farmer's husband plays chess with [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] — a masculine activity that parallels the women's domestic discourse. The Farmers and the Oliviers are paired couples in the social rituals of [[ilford|Ilford]].

**[[mr-farmer|Mr. Farmer]]**: Mrs. Farmer's husband, the curate of [[church-and-chapel|St. Mary's Chapel]], is the more sharply delineated of the pair. His physical description — "hatchet nose," "straggly beard," "small, greenish eyes" (016-173) — is rendered with a specificity that Mrs. Farmer, characterised chiefly by her drawl and her fecundity, never receives. The asymmetry in characterisation reflects the novel's gendered distribution of narrative attention: men, even minor clergymen, are drawn in sharper lines than their wives.

## Characterisation

Mrs. Farmer is rendered through a handful of externalised details — her drawl, her babies, her whisper — that together form a type-portrait of the provincial clergyman's wife. Sinclair does not grant her interiority, and the absence is significant. Mrs. Farmer is not interesting enough to imagine from the inside; she belongs to the world [[mary-olivier|Mary]] must leave, and the narrative's refusal to enter her consciousness is a formal enactment of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s own indifference.

The single moment of phonetic transcription — "wou-ould" (016-172) — is the most vivid characterisation Mrs. Farmer receives. The stretched vowel is at once comic and damning: it captures the drawl and, implicitly, the intellectual vacuity it expresses. In a novel that takes language — reading, writing, philosophy, poetry — as its central preoccupation, a character defined by how she draws out her vowels is already judged.

## Selected Passages

> Mamma listened to Mrs. Farmer as if she were saying something wonderful and exciting. (013-004)

The simile — "as if she were saying something wonderful and exciting" — is devastating in its quietness. The subjunctive "as if" marks the gap between the actual content of Mrs. Farmer's speech (servants, tradespeople) and [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s reception of it. The irony is directed not at Mrs. Farmer but at [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]], whose intellectual horizon is so narrow that a discussion of the servant problem can absorb her entirely.

> Mrs. Farmer was always having babies, and always asking you to go and see them. She couldn't understand that as you grew older you left off caring about babies. (016-174)

The sentence moves from the habitual ("always having babies, and always asking") to the epiphanic ("you left off caring"). The second-person "you" generalises [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s experience — this is what happens when one grows older, when one's interests shift from the domestic to the intellectual, when one leaves the world of Mrs. Farmer behind.
