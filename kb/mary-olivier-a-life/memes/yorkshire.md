## Description

Yorkshire is the county in which [[morfe|Morfe]] is situated — the regional setting that provides the novel's most fully realised landscape. It appears in the novel through small, concrete details: "the hot buttered Yorkshire cakes that were never served at the proper time," a domestic marker of regional identity that links food, place, and the rhythms of household life. [[PN=009-058]]

## Regional Identity

Yorkshire in the novel is less a described place than an atmosphere — the landscape of moor, hill, and field that shapes the sensory world of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s maturity. The county name signals a move away from the [[london|London]]-adjacent world of [[ilford|Ilford]] and [[barkingside|Barkingside]] into a different register of rural life: rougher, more exposed, more beautiful.

The Yorkshire cakes detail is characteristic of Sinclair's method — regional identity conveyed not through extended description but through a single concrete particular, the cake that is never served at the right time, the small domestic disappointment that carries the weight of place.
