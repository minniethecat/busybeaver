## Description

Funerals appear in the novel not as discrete narrated events but as objects of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s childhood terror — a recurring motif that haunts her earliest consciousness. The black funeral horses, the plumed hearses, the coffins that appear in her dreams: these images constitute one of the novel's most persistent and psychologically charged patterns. [[PN=008-133]]

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s fear of funerals is treated by the adults around her as a tiresome affectation — "[[jenny|Jenny]] didn't believe that a big girl, nine next birthday, could really be afraid of funerals" — but the novel takes it seriously as a manifestation of existential anxiety. [[PN=010-011]] The funeral is [[death-in-mary-olivier|death]] made visible, and [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s inability to look away from it marks her as different from the other children, who seem to accept adult reassurance.

## Key Passages

> Outside the cemetery gates, in the white road, the black funeral horses tossed their heads and neighed, and the black plumes quivered on the hearses.
> [[PN=008-133]]

The visual contrast — black on white, stillness and motion, life (the neighing horses) and [[death-in-mary-olivier|death]] (the hearses they draw) — compresses the funeral's恐怖 into a single vivid image.

> Please God, keep me from dreaming about coffins and funerals and ghosts and skeletons and corpses.
> [[PN=008-138]]

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s bedtime prayer, added "after the blessings, so that [[god-and-religion|God]] couldn't forget." The catalogue — coffins, funerals, ghosts, skeletons, corpses — escalates in specificity, revealing the child's mind working through the components of [[death-in-mary-olivier|death]] one by one.

> Jenny didn't believe that a big girl, nine next birthday, could really be afraid of funerals. She thought you were only trying to be tiresome.
> [[PN=010-011]]

The adult dismissal of childhood fear is rendered with clinical precision. [[jenny|Jenny]]'s disbelief is not cruelty but category error: she cannot imagine that a child's terror might be genuine.

## Narrative Significance

The funeral motif serves several functions in the novel's psychological architecture. It establishes [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s difference from the normative childhood of her siblings — she sees what they do not see, fears what they accept. It prefigures the actual [[death-in-mary-olivier|death]]s she will later confront ([[mark-olivier|Mark]]'s, her parents'). And it introduces the theme of inadequate adult response: the world [[mary-olivier|Mary]] inhabits cannot accommodate the intensity of her inner experience.

The motif also connects to the novel's broader treatment of [[god-and-religion|religion]]. The funerals [[mary-olivier|Mary]] fears are Christian funerals, promising resurrection — but what the child registers is not the promise but the corpse, not the assurance but the black plumes.
