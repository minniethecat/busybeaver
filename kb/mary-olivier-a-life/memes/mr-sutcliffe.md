## Role in the Novel

Mr. Sutcliffe appears in the [[morfe|Morfe]] chapters as a figure of neighbourly decency and unthreatening masculine attention. His role is structural: he provides a setting — the Sutcliffes' house, tennis court, [[social-gatherings|parties]] — in which [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s social self develops in young adulthood, and an occasion for [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s anxiety about her daughter's independence. The Sutcliffes are among the first [[morfe|Morfe]] households [[mary-olivier|Mary]] enters on her own terms, and the freedom of that entry is charged with both possibility and unease.

## Biography

Mr. Sutcliffe first enters the narrative as a name on [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s lips: "My sister — Mr. Sutcliffe" (024-090). He is described as "handsome with his bony, high nose that had a little white mark on the bridge" (024-091). This single physical detail — the white mark on the nose — is characteristic of Sinclair's portraiture: the face is reduced to one arresting feature, and the feature carries no symbolic weight beyond the fact of its having been noticed.

The Sutcliffes' house "hid in the thick trees" (024-226) at the bottom of the hill, its seclusion lending it a quality of sheltered case. From the hayfield, [[mary-olivier|Mary]] can see "the Sutcliffes' tennis court; an emerald green square set in the grey field" (028-094) — an image of cultivated leisure inserted into the working landscape. Mr. Sutcliffe plays tennis with [[mary-olivier|Mary]], though "if Mr. Sutcliffe asked you to play singles you couldn't very well say No" (028-095), a formulation that registers the constraint buried in the invitation.

At one of the Sutcliffes' social evenings he enters with a companion: "He had come in late, with Dr. Charles, and stood at the door a minute before he sat down" (025-043). He positions himself where "he could see her" (025-048), and his voice sounds "miles away between the drumming of her heart and the wonderful throbbing sweetness" (025-049) as she plays piano — the romantic syntax is [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s, and it invests the scene with an intensity that Mr. Sutcliffe himself may not warrant.

Later, he takes her into the house after tennis, showing her photographs of the son in [[india|India]]: "when it was all over, Mr. Sutcliffe took her back into the house, through the long room where the tea was laid" (028-102). His comment on her hair — "It curls at the tips... I like it. Makes you look as if you'd been out in the rain" (028-120) — is at once intimate and innocuous, the kind of attention a married neighbour can safely offer.

## Relationships

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: Mr. Sutcliffe is a figure in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s [[morfe|Morfe]] social world — a tennis partner, a host, an adult male presence whose attention is flattering without being dangerous. [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s suspicion of the relationship (029-013, 029-015) exceeds any evidence of impropriety, and the gap between [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s anxiety and the ordinariness of the social contact measures the narrowness of the world [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] would keep [[mary-olivier|Mary]] in.

**[[mrs-sutcliffe|Mrs. Sutcliffe]]**: Mr. Sutcliffe's wife appears as a benign presence who "would wave her hand and nod to you" (028-101) when [[mary-olivier|Mary]] jumps the tennis net. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s question — "Is there a [[mrs-sutcliffe|Mrs. Sutcliffe]]?" (029-015) — is pointed: the existence of a wife should neutralise [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s suspicions, but it does not. [[mrs-sutcliffe|Mrs. Sutcliffe]]'s sewing [[social-gatherings|parties]] (028-156) and the [[sutcliffes-dance|dance at the Sutcliffes]]' (029-101) form the calendar of [[morfe|Morfe]] social life.

## Characterisation

Mr. Sutcliffe is rendered chiefly through his effect on [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s sensorium: the drumming heart, the throbbing sweetness. Sinclair does not give us his interiority — he is seen, not known — and the asymmetry of the portraiture is the point. What matters is not who Mr. Sutcliffe is but what his attention does to [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s sense of herself. The narrative voice, focalised through [[mary-olivier|Mary]], makes the ordinary extraordinary, investing a neighbour's tennis invitation with the texture of romance. Whether the romance exists anywhere but in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s perception is a question the text leaves open.

## Selected Passages

> "Mr. Sutcliffe — if you'd known a girl when she was a child, you wouldn't want to marry her when she grew up, would you?" (028-123)

[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s question, with its artful indirection, is the closest the scene comes to acknowledging the current of feeling that runs beneath the tennis and the tea. The question is ostensibly hypothetical, but its real referent is transparent. Mr. Sutcliffe's response is not recorded; the chapter break intervenes.
