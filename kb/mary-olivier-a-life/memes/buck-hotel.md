## Description

The Buck Hotel is the public house in [[morfe|Morfe]] situated along the High Row, where [[emilius-olivier|Papa]] goes "regularly, about six o'clock in the evening." [[PN=024-348]] It is the destination of his daily shuffle from [[five-elms|Five Elms]], and [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]] must go and bring him back "towards dinnertime." [[PN=024-348]]

The hotel is more than a drinking place: it is a node of village information and economic connection. Its landlord functions as an intermediary in the community, telling Uncle Victor about Farmer Alderson's brother Ben who "had a big farm somewhere near Montreal" — the information that leads to [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]]'s emigration to [[canada|Canada]]. [[PN=026-003]]

## Significance in the Narrative

The Buck Hotel is the physical locus of [[emilius-olivier|Papa]]'s alcoholism and the family's unspoken shame. [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] "would never admit that he went to the Buck Hotel" — the denial is itself the acknowledgment, a ritual of refusal that structures the family's relationship to Papa's drinking. [[PN=024-350]]

For [[mary-olivier|Mary]], the hour between Papa's departure and Roddy's retrieval of him becomes a window of liberation: "There might be a whole hour before Roddy fetched him from the Buck Hotel. If you could only reach the last movement, the two thundering chords, and then — the Presto." [[PN=025-007]] The Buck Hotel, paradoxically, enables Mary's musical practice — Papa's absence creates the space for her art.

The hotel also functions in the novel's plotting of [[daniel-olivier|Dan]]'s decline. "If Mr. Horn had looked the other way when he saw you coming you would have known that Dan was in the Buck Hotel" — the averted gaze is the village's sign language for family disgrace. [[PN=031-014]] Dan stays there, dines there, and the hotel becomes associated with his deterioration.

Yet the Buck Hotel is also the source of [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]]'s eventual escape. The landlord's information about Canada farming sets in motion the chain of events that will send Roddy to Montreal — a paradoxical gift from the place of family shame. [[PN=026-003]]

## Key Passages

> Regularly, about six o'clock in the evening, he shuffled out of the house and along the High Row to the Buck Hotel, and towards dinnertime Roddy had to go and bring him back.
> [[PN=024-348]]

The rhythm of Papa's drinking is rendered as ritual: the shuffle, the High Row, the retrieval. Roddy's role as his father's keeper is established in this daily pattern.

> But she would never admit that he went to the Buck Hotel.
> [[PN=024-350]]

Mamma's denial is the family's central fiction. The sentence's simplicity — "But she would never admit" — carries the weight of years of performed ignorance.

> There might be a whole hour before Roddy fetched him from the Buck Hotel. If you could only reach the last movement, the two thundering chords, and then — the Presto.
> [[PN=025-007]]

Mary's relationship to the Buck Hotel is opportunistic: Papa's absence is her creative presence. The hour of his drinking is the hour of her Beethoven.
