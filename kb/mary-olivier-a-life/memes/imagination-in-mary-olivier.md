# Imagination in Mary Olivier

**Imagination** in *[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]: A Life* functions as both
refuge and vocation — the inner capacity that enables
[[mary-olivier|Mary]] to survive the constraints of her domestic world
and that ultimately becomes the foundation of her identity as a writer and
translator. May Sinclair traces imagination not as a transcendent faculty
but as a practice, something exercised in childhood games of pretending and
cultivated in adult acts of making. The novel's attention to the workings
of imagination — its pleasures and its disappointments — constitutes one of
its most sustained psychological investigations.

## Childhood Pretending

Imagination enters the novel as a child's strategy for managing a world that
offers insufficient freedom. The young [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s games of
pretending are not mere play but early exercises in self-making — the
creation of worlds in which she possesses agency:

> Then, walking very fast and never looking back, she pretended that she had
> gone out by herself.
> [[PN=008-053]]

The pretence is layered: [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is not simply imagining a
different scenario but performing an identity — the child who has gone out
alone, independent and unobserved. The detail "never looking back" marks the
commitment imagination requires, the willed suspension of the real.
Throughout her childhood, such games recur as a counterpoint to the
constraints imposed by [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]]'s vigilance and the
family's domestic economy.

This habit of imaginative world-making extends into adolescence and becomes
the means by which [[mary-olivier|Mary]] sustains relationships across
absence:

> She couldn't tell him that she pretended he was there when he was not
> there; that she created situations.
> [[PN=020-058]]

The verb "created" is significant — what the child called pretending the
adolescent names as creation. The passage marks the transition from
imagination as escape to imagination as a form of making, the nascent
artistic impulse that will later find expression in
[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s [[poetry-in-mary-olivier|poetry]] and translations.

## Roddy's Imagination: Adventure and Departure

Sinclair extends her investigation of imagination beyond
[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s consciousness to that of her brother
[[roddy-olivier|Roddy]], whose imaginative response to emigration offers a
contrastive case. Faced with the prospect of leaving for Canada, Roddy
transforms uncertainty into adventure:

> He was turning it all into an adventure, his imagination playing round and
> round it.
> [[PN=026-097]]

The phrase "playing round and round it" captures the iterative, almost
compulsive quality of imagination — it circles its object, working it over,
transforming it. For Roddy, imagination is a defence against fear; the
voyage becomes "an adventure" because he needs it to be one. The passage
also registers the fragility of this transformation: Roddy is "sick and
couldn't eat his breakfast" on the morning of departure, the body refusing
the consolation imagination offers.

## Imagination and Vocation

For [[mary-olivier|Mary]], imagination matures into vocation — the
conviction that her inner life of making is not merely a private refuge
but the work she is meant to do. When [[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]]
proposes marriage, she refuses by appealing to a deeper commitment:

> "I'll work for you all my life. I shall never want to do anything else.
> It's what I always wanted. When I was a child I used to imagine myself
> doing it for you. It was a sort of game I played."
> [[PN=027-182]]

[[maurice-jourdain|Maurice]]'s response — "It's a sort of game you're
playing now, my poor Mary" [[PN=027-183]] — exposes the gulf between
[[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s understanding of imagination as the most serious
thing in her life and the world's reduction of it to childishness. The
rejection of [[maurice-jourdain|Maurice]]'s proposal is, in this sense, a
defence of imagination itself — a refusal to subordinate the life of making
to the life of domestic obligation. In choosing the "game" over the man,
[[mary-olivier|Mary]] chooses her [[self-and-identity|self]].

## The Hunger for Another Mind

In the years at Morfe after the loss of the Sutcliffes, imagination becomes
not only a practice of making but a practice of survival — the inner
capacity that staves off the "death" of boredom:

> She envied her youth its capacity for daydreaming, for imagining
> interminable communions.
> [[PN=033-076]]

"Interminable communions" — the phrase is precise in its dual register:
communion as intellectual companionship (the desire to "know another mind,
a luminous, fiery crystal" [[PN=033-075]]) and communion in a near-religious
sense, the imagination furnishing the presence that reality withholds. Yet
the envy directed at her younger self marks a distance: the adult
[[mary-olivier|Mary]] knows that daydreaming is "better than nothing"
but not enough. Imagination that finds no outward form —
[[writing-in-mary-olivier|writing]], [[poetry-in-mary-olivier|poetry]],
translation — becomes "brilliant hallucinations of a mental hunger," a
consuming rather than a sustaining force.

## Imagination Realised: The Poet's Life

The novel's final books trace imagination's movement from private refuge to
public form. [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s [[poetry-in-mary-olivier|poems]] are
accepted for publication; she works as a translator; the inner life that
began as childhood pretending acquires the solidity of print. The
transformation is registered in a passage that gathers the novel's various
strands of imaginative activity into a single, encompassing affirmation:

> "When I'm making music I think there's nothing but music in all the
> world; when I'm doing philosophy I think there's nothing but philosophy
> in all the world; when I'm writing verses I think there's nothing but
> writing in all the world."
> [[PN=031-208]]

The repeated structure — "when I'm... I think there's nothing but..." —
performs the immersive quality of imaginative attention. Each activity
absorbs the whole self; imagination is not a faculty one exercises but a
mode of being in which the world contracts to the work at hand. The passage
is the novel's most complete affirmation of the life of making, and it is no
accident that it follows [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s refusal of
[[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]] — the choice of vocation over
marriage is vindicated in the fullness of imaginative absorption.
