# Freedom in Mary Olivier

## Overview

Freedom is both a philosophical problem and a lived experience in *Mary Olivier: A Life*. Mary grapples with the tension between Spinoza's determinism — the doctrine that everything follows necessarily from the divine nature — and Kant's assertion of moral freedom: "You can because you ought." Her personal experience of freedom is spatial, psychological, and ultimately theological: "The flash point of freedom was your consciousness of God" (040-046).

## "Precious Freedom!"

Freedom is first experienced as physical and spatial release. "When you had got through the gate you were free" (024-332); the landscape participates in this freedom: "The moor and the high mound of the hill were free; they had slipped from the net of the walls" (024-333). These images associate freedom with escape from enclosure — gates, walls, nets — and entry into open space. "Precious freedom!" (024-203) is an exclamation that registers its value precisely because it is rare and hard-won.

Yet freedom is incomplete as long as the emotional self remains bound: "The part that cared was not free" (024-205). Attachment, care, love — these bind Mary to others and make her unfree even when physically liberated.

## Kant versus Spinoza

The novel stages the philosophical conflict between determinism and autonomy. The radical doubt of Mary's intellectual crisis encompasses everything: "For all you knew there was no Reality, no God, no freedom, no immortality. Only doing your duty" (030-003). Kant's categorical imperative — "'You can because you ought.' Kant, when you got to the bottom of him, was no more exciting than Mamma. 'Du kannst, weil du sollst'" (030-003) — is dismissed as a philosophical version of maternal moral authority. Kantian freedom, the power to obey the moral law one gives oneself, collapses into the childhood experience of Mamma's commands.

Against this, Mary eventually arrives at a position that inverts the Spinozist formula: "Free will was the reality underneath the illusion of necessity. The flash point of freedom was your consciousness of God" (040-046). Where Spinoza sees necessity as reality and free will as illusion born of ignorance of causes, Mary's mature position treats the experience of freedom — grounded in consciousness of God — as more fundamental than determinism.

## Freedom and the Self

Freedom is tied to the conception of selfhood: "You had thought of yourself as a somewhat less powerful, but still independent and separate entity, a sacred, inviolable self, struggling against them for completer freedom and detachment" (031-258). The self is defined by its struggle for freedom *from* others — from family, from social expectations, from "them." This struggle is ongoing; freedom is not a state achieved but a condition continually contested.

## Freedom from Desire

The desire for freedom extends inward: "She would be free from the restlessness and the endless idiotic reverie of desire" (028-148). Psychological freedom — freedom from one's own compulsive patterns — is as important as freedom from external constraint. This connects to the Spinozist ideal of freedom through understanding: to know the causes of one's desires is to be free from their tyranny.
