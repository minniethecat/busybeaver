## Description

Durlingham is an industrial town connected by rail to [[reyburn|Reyburn]] and [[london|London]]. It functions in the novel as a site of practical necessity — work, shopping, medical appointments — contrasting with the scenic and contemplative landscape of [[morfe|Morfe]], [[greffington|Greffington Edge]], and [[karva|Karva]]. [[PN=025-131]]

The town is home to works and factories: [[daniel-olivier|Dan]], the Olivier family's loyal helper, is taken into the works at Durlingham at age seventeen, a point of entry into the world of industrial employment. [[PN=024-366]] Durlingham is also where [[mary-olivier|Mary]] consults an oculist about her eyesight. [[PN=028-168]]

## Transit and Commerce

Durlingham's primary narrative function is as a transit point. The train route runs [[reyburn|Reyburn]] → Durlingham → King's Cross, making the town a waystation on journeys between the rural setting and [[london|London]]. [[PN=025-131]] [[PN=033-079]] It is also a shopping destination: [[mary-olivier|Mary]] and her companions go into Durlingham to buy clothes and supplies that cannot be obtained in [[morfe|Morfe]]. [[PN=030-062]] [[PN=033-084]] [[PN=033-091]]

The platform at Durlingham station is the site of the novel's most explicit engagement with public intellectual life — posters announce Professor Lee Ramsden's lectures on philosophy, a detail that underscores the distance between the metropolitan world of ideas and the provincial reality of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s life. [[PN=033-103]]

## Social World

The social reach of Durlingham extends into the village: people come from [[reyburn|Reyburn]] and Durlingham to attend [[funerals|funeral]]s at [[morfe|Morfe]], forming "a hollow square of faces." [[PN=029-111]] The teachers of St. Paul's [[school|School]]s, Durlingham, appear in the narrative through a presentation ceremony, marking the town's institutional connections to the local [[school|education]]al world. [[PN=033-111]]

[[daniel-olivier|Dan]]'s overnight trip to Durlingham — "He was away all night" — hints at the town's role as a place where the constraints of village life are loosened. [[PN=031-140]] And the detail of cycling from Durlingham to [[morfe|Morfe]] suggests the town's proximity and the ease with which its resources can be reached. [[PN=037-013]]

## Key Passages

> …in the train from Reyburn to Durlingham, from Durlingham to King's Cross.
> [[PN=025-131]]

The train journey that maps the geography of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s world — from the rural village through the industrial town to the imperial metropolis — each station marking a different register of experience.

> But the oculist in Durlingham said it was her glasses.
> [[PN=028-168]]

A characteristic narrative compression: Durlingham is the place where practical problems — eyesight, health, logistics — find their resolution, in contrast to the emotional and philosophical life centring on [[morfe|Morfe]].

> Posters on the platform at Durlingham announced in red letters that Professor Lee Ramsden…
> [[PN=033-103]]

The railway platform as the interface between the local and the intellectual world — the posters a reminder of the life of the mind that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] craves but can only access at a distance.
