## Description

Renton is a village in [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], near [[morfe|Morfe]], encompassing Renton Moor, Renton Church, and Renton Lodge. It is embedded in the network of grey stone walls that knit the Yorkshire landscape together: "Where you thought the net had ended it flung out a grey rope over the purple back of Renton, the green shoulder of Greffington." [[PN=024-330]]

The village is reached from [[morfe|Morfe]] by Jackson's yard and the fields — a route that Mary takes to avoid passing [[greffington-hall|Greffington Hall]] when it is occupied by strangers taking it for the summer holidays. [[PN=036-009]] Renton Moor, with Rathdale fields leading up to it, is one of the walking destinations in the landscape Mary inhabits during her maturity, though she avoids certain paths because of their association with [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]], [[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]], [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]], and [[mark-olivier|Mark]]. [[PN=036-053]]

The Vicar of Renton appears briefly as a figure in the local social landscape, wanting to see Mary — one of the many village clergymen who populate the novel's Yorkshire world. [[PN=024-292]]

## Significance in the Narrative

Renton's most charged narrative function is as the source of the tolling bell. At two o'clock, the bell of Renton Church begins to toll for [[emilius-olivier|Papa]]'s funeral, punctuating the formal rhythms of mourning with its insistent presence. [[PN=025-135]] The bell tolls again at a moment of intense emotional confrontation between Mary and [[richard-nicholson|Richard Nicholson]], marking another kind of ending. [[PN=030-211]]

The Renton landscape — moor, church, lodge — forms part of the differentiated Yorkshire geography that the novel maps with care. Unlike the childhood geography of [[ley-street|Ley Street]] and [[ilford|Ilford]], Renton belongs to Mary's adult consciousness: it is a place she navigates deliberately, choosing routes based on emotional association. Her decision to go "round to Renton by Jackson's yard and the fields so as not to see" Greffington Hall reveals the landscape as a map of avoidance and preservation — certain views must be protected from contamination by other people's occupancy. [[PN=036-009]]

Renton Lodge appears in the social geography of the neighborhood: "the two Acroyd girls from Renton Lodge" attend a tennis party at the Vicarage, anchoring the place in the routines of village sociability. [[PN=027-138]]

## Key Passages

> Where you thought the net had ended it flung out a grey rope over the purple back of Renton, the green shoulder of Greffington.
> [[PN=024-330]]

The image of stone walls as a net thrown over the landscape — and Renton as one of the knots in that net — captures the way the Yorkshire landscape is both natural and constructed, a topography shaped by centuries of human labor.

> At two o'clock the bell of Renton Church began to toll. Her mother sat up in a stiff, self-conscious attitude and opened the Church Service. The bell went on tolling. For Papa.
> [[PN=025-135]]

The tolling bell is the sound of formal mourning — impersonal and relentless. Mamma's stiff posture and the opened prayer book set human ritual against the bell's mechanical marking of time and loss.

> She would go round to Renton by Jackson's yard and the fields so as not to see it.
> [[PN=036-009]]

The route to Renton becomes a detour around pain — Mary shapes her physical path through the landscape to protect herself from the sight of a house occupied by others.
