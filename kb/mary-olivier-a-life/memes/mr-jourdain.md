## Role in the Novel

[[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]] occupies a unique position in the novel's architecture of [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]. He is neither a childhood friend like [[jimmy|Jimmy Ponsonby]] nor a family member; he is the first man who pursues [[mary-olivier|Mary]] as an adult, and his pursuit — ardent, intellectual, ultimately disappointing — serves as the novel's most extended study of romantic [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]'s promise and its inevitable failure to deliver what it seems to offer. The Jourdain episode spans several chapters (20–27) and constitutes one of the novel's major emotional arcs.

Jourdain matters to [[mary-olivier|Mary]] not primarily as a suitor but as a mind. He shares her passion for philosophy; he is "the only person she could think of as having a real life at all like her own" (027-052). His withdrawal — the gradual discovery that the man with "the crystal mind, shining and flashing" was, perhaps, a projection — is one of the novel's central disillusionments, a necessary step in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s long [[school|education]] in the difference between what [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]] promises and what it delivers.

## Biography

[[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]] first appears in the Essex fields, walking with [[mary-olivier|Mary]] and [[mark-olivier|Mark]]. "'Maurice. [[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]]. I know yours — [[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]. I like it'" (020-008). The introduction is direct, personal, and marked by an attention to [[mary-olivier|Mary]] that distinguishes him from the other young men of her acquaintance.

Their relationship develops through letters and visits. When [[mary-olivier|Mary]] goes to school, she tells him: "'Mr. Jourdain, I am going to school'" (020-063). The announcement, offered as if it were important that he know, suggests the intimacy already established. Jourdain writes to her; he goes to Paris, and his letter from there becomes a talisman: "Mysterious, measured sounds came through the open window. That was the sea. She got up and looked out. The deep-walled garden lay under the window, black and clear like a well. Calais was over there. And Paris. Mr. Jourdain had written to say he was going to Paris. She had his letter" (021-026).

His courtship, when it comes, is persistent. [[aunt-lavvy|Aunt Lavvy]] reports: "'He's been to see me nine times in one week. Nine times'" (022-058). [[uncle-victor|Uncle Victor]] intervenes to "get him away before he could speak," but Jourdain "wouldn't take 'No' for an answer" (022-058). The persistence suggests genuine feeling, yet the novel's retrospective view — [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s later understanding — complicates this.

The peak of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s feeling for Jourdain is rendered in a remarkable passage of interior monologue in Chapter 27. "When she said '[[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]]' she could feel her voice throb in her body like the string of a violin" (027-051). She imagines him as the secret sharer of her real life: "He was in the songs she made, he was in the 'Sonata Appassionata'; he was in the solemn beauty of [[karva|Karva]] under the moon. In the Critique of Pure Reason she caught the bright passing of his mind" (027-052). The passage is one of the novel's most lyric celebrations of intellectual and romantic fusion — and its very intensity prepares the ground for the fall.

When Jourdain finally visits, the reality is less than the anticipation. The visit is a formal affair, hedged about by family. And the aftermath is a slow, painful deflation. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] comes to see that "There had been two [[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]]s, the one who said, 'I'll understand. I'll never lose my temper'; the one with the crystal mind, shining and flashing, the mind like a big room filled from end to end with light. But he had never existed" (027-052). The sentence is devastating in its finality: the man she [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]d was a fiction.

Her mother's judgement, characteristically blunt, confirms the novel's verdict: "'I'd rather have seen you in your [[funerals|coffin]] than married to [[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]]'" (027-188). And yet the novel does not endorse this harshness entirely. When [[mrs-draper|Mrs. Draper]] writes in a postscript that Jourdain has called asking for news of [[mary-olivier|Mary]], adding "'I think he would like to know that that wretched affair has not made her unhappy'" (027-188), there is a suggestion of something genuine in him — a tenderness that survives the romantic failure.

## Relationships

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: The central romantic relationship of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s young womanhood. Jourdain represents the promise of a [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]] that would be at once intellectual and passionate — a promise the novel ultimately judges to be illusory.

**[[caroline-olivier|Caroline Olivier]]**: [[caroline-olivier|Mamma]] is fiercely opposed to Jourdain, whom she regards as an unworthy match. Her opposition, rooted partly in class anxiety and partly in genuine protective instinct, contributes to the relationship's failure.

**[[uncle-victor|Uncle Victor]]**: The family gatekeeper who initially prevents Jourdain from speaking to [[mary-olivier|Mary]], then later relents and allows the formal visit.
