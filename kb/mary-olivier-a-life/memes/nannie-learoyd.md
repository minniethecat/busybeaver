## Role in the Novel

Nannie Learoyd occupies a small but narratively potent role in *[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]*. She is the vessel through which [[mary-olivier|Mary]], at a crucial moment in her adolescence, encounters the raw fact of adult sexuality — and, more painfully, the discovery that [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]], the man she [[love-in-mary-olivier|love]]s, is implicated in it. Learoyd's function is catalytic: she precipitates a crisis in [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s understanding of desire, transforming it from a vague, idealised abstraction into something bodily, furtive, and ugly.

The revelation occurs in a single, devastating sequence — chapter 31, the "Back Lane" episode — that ranks among the most compressed and powerful scenes in the novel. [[mary-olivier|Mary]], walking home through the dark lane, comes upon a man and woman "fixed in one block, their faces tilted backwards, their bodies motionless. The woman's arms were round the man's neck, his arms round her waist" (031-226). She expects it to be Nannie Learoyd and young Horn, the labourer Nannie has been meeting. When the couple breaks apart, she sees instead: "Nannie Learoyd and [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]]" (031-227). The substitution is devastating. [[lindley-vickers|Vickers]], the man whose intellectual companionship [[mary-olivier|Mary]] treasures, whose hands she has held "in the drawing-room, standing by the piano" (036-119), is entangled with the "naughty" girl of the Back Lane.

In the novel's later retrospective catalogue, Learoyd's name surfaces again: "If [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]] had never come back she wouldn't have seen him with Nannie Learoyd in the schoolhouse lane; the moment when he held her hands in the drawing-room, standing by the piano, would have been their one eternal moment" (036-119). The conditional structure — if he had never come back — reveals the wound: [[lindley-vickers|Vickers]]'s return destroyed the perfection of the memory by attaching his name, irrevocably, to Nannie Learoyd's.

## Biography

Nannie Learoyd is introduced through [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s window: "Something crossing the window pane made her start and turn. Nannie Learoyd's face, looking in" (031-152). The face at the window is an intrusion — the outside world pressing against [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s domestic space. The description that follows is at once vivid and judgemental: "Naughty Nannie. You could see her big pink cheeks and her scarlet mouth and her eyes sliding and peering. Poor pretty, naughty Nannie" (031-152).

The epithets "naughty" and "poor pretty" establish the town's view of Learoyd — a girl whose sexuality is public knowledge, whose beauty ("big pink cheeks," "scarlet mouth") is excessive, uncontained, and therefore suspect. The detail that she "smiled when she met you on the Green, as if she trusted you not to tell how you saw her after dark slinking about the Back Lane waiting for young Horn to come out to her" (031-152) implicates [[mary-olivier|Mary]] in a pact of silence — [[mary-olivier|Mary]] knows Nannie's secret, and Nannie knows she knows.

The "Back Lane" episode unfolds with the inexorability of nightmare. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] goes out through the orchard and sees "Nannie Learoyd sitting on the stone stairs of Horn's granary, waiting for young Horn" (031-222). The image of waiting — passive, patient, sexualised — is repeated and intensified. At the lane's last crook, she encounters the couple embracing, and the revelation that the man is not young Horn but [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]] shatters the expected narrative.

The aftermath is rendered in a single, searing sentence: "She lay awake all night. Her brain, incapable of thought, kept turning round and round, showing her on an endless rolling screen the images of Lindley and Nannie Learoyd, clinging together, loosening, swinging apart, clinging together" (031-228). The "endless rolling screen" is an image of traumatic repetition — the mind unable to process, only to replay. The verbs "clinging together, loosening, swinging apart" reproduce the rhythm of the encounter itself, the body's motion becoming the mind's torment.

## Relationships

**[[mary-olivier|Mary Olivier]]**: Learoyd's primary narrative function is to wound [[mary-olivier|Mary]] — not deliberately, but through the simple fact of her entanglement with [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]]. [[mary-olivier|Mary]] does not hate Learoyd (the epithet "poor pretty, naughty Nannie" carries something closer to pity); what torments her is the defilement of her ideal. Learoyd is not a rival but a revelation — the proof that [[lindley-vickers|Vickers]] inhabits a world of bodily desire from which [[mary-olivier|Mary]] is excluded.

**[[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]]**: The relationship is presented entirely through [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s traumatised perspective. We never learn what [[lindley-vickers|Vickers]] felt for Learoyd, whether the encounter was a casual liaison or something more. The novel's refusal to explain or contextualise is itself a narrative choice: [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s knowledge is radically incomplete, and the incompleteness is the source of her suffering.

**[[young-horn|Young Horn]]**: The labourer for whom Learoyd waits on the granary stairs (031-222). His displacement by [[lindley-vickers|Vickers]] in the Back Lane encounter is the surprise that upends [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s world. Horn exists as a name only — a marker of the class dimension that complicates the sexual drama.

## Characterisation

Sinclair presents Learoyd exclusively through [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s focalised perspective. We never enter Learoyd's consciousness; we see only her face at the window, her body on the granary stairs, her arms around [[lindley-vickers|Vickers]]'s neck. This externality is thematically significant. Learoyd is not a subject but an object — of town gossip, of male desire, of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s horrified fascination. The narrative technique reproduces this objectification while simultaneously, through the epithet "poor pretty," registering its injustice.

The language used to describe Learoyd — "naughty," "slinking," "peering" — draws on a vocabulary of transgression and surveillance. Learoyd is always being watched (by [[mary-olivier|Mary]], by the town), and her transgressions are always sexual. The description "her big pink cheeks and her scarlet mouth" (031-152) uses colour to code excess and availability: the pink and scarlet are the colours of the body displayed, in contrast to the muted, respectable tones of the ladies [[mary-olivier|Mary]] encounters at [[church-and-chapel|church]].

In the retrospective passage (036-119), Learoyd's name appears as a kind of scar — the mark of a wound that has not fully healed. The grammar of the sentence ("If [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]] had never come back she wouldn't have seen him with Nannie Learoyd") suggests that the memory remains as vivid in middle age as it was on the sleepless night after the discovery.

## Selected Passages

> Nannie Learoyd's face, looking in. Naughty Nannie. You could see her big pink cheeks and her scarlet mouth and her eyes sliding and peering. Poor pretty, naughty Nannie. (031-152)

The face at the window is a classic Gothic image — the outside intruding on the inside, the uncanny breaking into the domestic. The repetition of "naughty" and the shift from "naughty" to "poor pretty, naughty" enacts the movement of [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s judgement from condemnation to something more complex: pity mixed with fascination.

> They were fixed in one block, their faces tilted backwards, their bodies motionless. The woman's arms were round the man's neck, his arms round her waist. There was something about the queer back-tilted faces — queer and ugly. (031-226)

The description freezes the couple into a sculptural tableau — "fixed in one block" suggests stone, monumentality, the inhuman. The repetition of "queer" and the final judgement "ugly" mark [[mary-olivier|Mary]]'s recoil. What she sees is not erotic but grotesque, and the grotesquerie is bound up with the revelation that the man is [[lindley-vickers|Vickers]].

> She lay awake all night. Her brain, incapable of thought, kept turning round and round, showing her on an endless rolling screen the images of Lindley and Nannie Learoyd, clinging together, loosening, swinging apart, clinging together. (031-228)

The "endless rolling screen" is an image of cinema avant la lettre — a mechanical, involuntary projection that the mind cannot stop. The syntax enacts the repetition it describes, the participial phrases cycling through the same three actions without resolution. It is one of the novel's most powerful representations of traumatic memory.
