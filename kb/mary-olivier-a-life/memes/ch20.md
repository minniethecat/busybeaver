## Chapter 20

### I

[024-001]The scent of hay came through the open window of her room. Clearer and finer than the hay smell of the Essex fields.

[024-002]She shut her eyes to live purely in that one sweet sense; and opened them to look at the hill, the great hill heaved up against the east.

[024-003]You had to lean far out of the window to see it all. It came on from the hidden north, its top straight as a wall against the sky. Then the long shoulder, falling and falling. Then the thick trees. A further hill cut the trees off from the sky.

[024-004]Roddy was saying something. Sprawling out from the corner of the window-seat, he stared with sulky, unseeing eyes into the little room.

[024-005]“Roddy, what did you say that hill was?”

[024-006]“Greffington Edge. You aren’t listening.”

[024-007]His voice made a jagged tear in the soft, quiet evening.

[024-008]“And the one beyond it?”

[024-009]“Sarrack. Why can’t you listen?”

[024-010]Greffington Edge. Sarrack. Sarrack.

[024-011]Green fields coming on from the north, going up and up, netted in with the strong net of the low grey walls that held them together, that kept them safe. Above them thin grass, a green bloom on the grey face of the hill. Above the thin grass a rampart of grey cliffs.

[024-012]Roddy wouldn’t look at the hill.

[024-013]“I tell you,” he said, “you’ll loathe the place when you’ve lived a week in it.”

[024-014]The thick, rich trees were trying to climb the Edge, but they couldn’t get higher than the netted fields.

[024-015]The lean, ragged firs had succeeded. No. Not quite. They stood out against the sky, adventurous mountaineers, roped together, leaning forward with the effort.

[024-016]“It’s Mamma’s fault,” Roddy was saying. “Papa would have gone anywhere, but she would come to this damned Morfe.”

[024-017]“Don’t. Don’t﻿—” Her mind beat him off, defending her happiness. He would kill it if she let him. Coming up from Reyburn on the front seat of the Morfe bus, he had sulked. He smiled disagreeable smiles while the driver pointed with his whip and told her the names of the places. Renton Moor. Renton Church. Morfe, the grey village, stuck up on its green platform under the high, purple mound of Karva Hill.

[024-018]Garthdale in front of it, Rathdale at its side, meeting in the fields below its bridge.

[024-019]Morfe was beautiful. She loved it with love at first sight, faithless to Ilford.

[024-020]Straight, naked houses. Grey walls of houses, enclosing the wide oblong Green. Dark grey stone roofs, close-clipped lest the wind should lift them. On the Green two grey stone pillar fountains; a few wooden benches; telegraph poles. Under her window a white road curling up to the platform. Straight, naked houses, zigzagging up beside it. Down below, where the white road came from, the long grey raking bridge, guarded by a tall ash-tree.

[024-021]Roddy’s jabbing voice went on and on:

[024-022]“I used to think Mamma was holy and unselfish. I don’t think so any more. She says she wants to do what Papa wants and what we want; but she always ends by doing what she wants herself. It’s all very well for her. As long as she’s got a garden to poke about in she doesn’t care how awful it is for us.”

[024-023]She hated Roddy when he said things like that about Mamma.

[024-024]“I don’t suppose the little lamb thought about it at all. Or if she did she thought we’d like it.”

[024-025]She didn’t want to listen to Roddy’s grumbling. She wanted to look and look, to sniff up the clear, sweet, exciting smell of the fields.

[024-026]The roofs went crisscrossing up the road﻿—straight﻿—slant﻿—straight. They threw delicate violet-green shadows on to the sage-green field below. That long violet-green pillar was the shadow of the ash-tree by the bridge.

[024-027]The light came from somewhere behind the village, from a sunset you couldn’t see. It made the smooth hill fields shine like thin velvet, stretched out, clinging to the hills.

[024-028]“Oh, Roddy, the light’s different. Different from Ilford. Look﻿—”

[024-029]“I’ve been looking for five weeks,” Roddy said. “You haven’t, that’s all. I was excited at first.”

[024-030]He got up. He stared out of the window, not seeing anything.

[024-031]“I didn’t mean what I said about Mamma. Morfe makes you say things. Soon it’ll make you mean them. You wait.”

[024-032]She was glad when he had left her.

[024-033]The cliffs of Greffington Edge were violet now.

### II

[024-034]At night, when she lay in bed in the strange room, the Essex fields began to haunt her; the five trees, the little flying trees, low down, low down; the straight, narrow paths through the corn, where she walked with Mark, with Jimmy, with Mr. Jourdain; Mr. Jourdain, standing in the path and saying: “Talk to me. I’m alive. I’m here. I’ll listen.”

[024-035]Mark and Mamma planting the sumach tree by the front door; Papa saying it wouldn’t grow. It had grown up to the dining-room windowsill.

[024-036]Aunt Bella and Uncle Edward; the Proparts and the Farmers and Mr. Batty, all stiff and disapproving; not nearly so nice to you as they used to be and making you believe it was your fault.

[024-037]The old, beautiful drawing-room. The piano by the door.

[024-038]Dan staggering down the room at Mark’s party. Mark holding her there, in his arms.

[024-039]Dawn, and Dr. Draper’s carriage waiting in the road beside the mangold fields. And Aunt Charlotte carried out, her feet brushing the flagstones.

[024-040]She mustn’t tell them. Mamma couldn’t bear it. Roddy couldn’t bear it. Aunt Charlotte was Papa’s sister. He must never know.

[024-041]The sound of the brushing feet made her heart ache.

[024-042]She was glad to wake in the small, strange room. It had taken a snip off Mamma’s and Papa’s room on one side of the window, and a snip off the spare room on the other. That made it a funny T shape. She slept in the tail of the T, in a narrow bed pushed against the wall. When you sat up you saw the fat trees trying to get up the hill between the washstand and the chest of drawers.

[024-043]This room would never be taken from her, because she was the only one who was small enough to fit the bed.

[024-044]She would be safe there with her hill.

### III

[024-045]The strange houses fascinated her. They had the simplicity and the precision of houses in a very old engraving. On the west side of the Green they made a long straight wall. Morfe High Row. An open space of cobblestones stretched in front of it. The marketplace.

[024-046]Sharp morning light picked out the small black panes of the windows in the white crisscross of their frames, and the long narrow signs of the King’s Head and the Farmer’s Arms, black on grey. The plaster joints of the walls and the dark net of earth between the cobbles showed thick and clear as in a very old engraving. The west side had the sky behind it and the east side had the hill.

[024-047]Grey-white cart roads slanted across the Green, cutting it into vivid triangular grass-plots. You went in and out of Morfe through the open corners of its Green. Her father’s house stood at the southwest corner, by itself. A projecting wing at that end of the High Row screened it from the marketplace.

[024-048]The strange houses excited her.

[024-049]Wonderful, unknown people lived in them. You would see them and know what they were like: the people in the tall house with the rusty stones, in the bright green ivy house with the white doors, in the small grey, humble houses, in the big, important house set at the top of the Green, with the three long rows of windows, the front garden and the iron gate.

[024-050]People you didn’t know. You would be strange and exciting to them as they were strange and exciting to you. They might say interesting things. There might be somebody who cared about Plato and Spinoza.

[024-051]Things would happen that you didn’t know. Anything might happen any minute.

[024-052]If you knew what was happening in the houses now﻿—some of them had hard, frightening faces. Dreadful things might have happened in them. Her father’s house had a good, simple face. You could trust it.

[024-053]Five windows in the rough grey wall, one on each side of the white door, three above. A garden at the side, an orchard at the back. In front a cobbled square marked off by a line of thin stones set in edgeways.

[024-054]A strange house, innocent of unhappy memories.

[024-055]Catty stood at the door, looking for her. She called to her to come in to breakfast.

### IV

[024-056]Papa was moving restlessly about the house. His loose slippers shuffled on the stone flags of the passages.

[024-057]Catty stopped gathering up the breakfast cups to listen.

[024-058]Catty was not what she used to be. Her plump cheeks were sunk and flattened. Some day she would look like Jenny.

[024-059]Papa stood in the doorway. He looked round the small dining-room as if he were still puzzled by its strangeness. Papa was not what he used to be. A streak of grey hair showed above each ear. Grey patches in his brown beard. Scarlet smears in the veined sallow of his eyes. His bursting, violent life had gone. He went stooping and shuffling. The house was too small for Papa. He turned in it as a dog turns in his kennel, feeling for a place to stretch himself.

[024-060]He said, “Where’s your mother? I want her.”

[024-061]Mary went to find her.

[024-062]She knew the house: the flagged passage from the front door. The dining-room on the right. The drawing-room on the left. In there the chairs and tables drew together to complain of Morfe. View of the blacksmith’s house and yard from the front window. From the side window Mamma’s garden. Green grass-plot. Trees at the far end. Flowers in the borders: red roses, cream roses, Canterbury bells, white and purple, under the high walls. In a corner an elder bush frothing greenish white on green.

[024-063]Behind the dining-room Papa’s tight den. Stairs where the passage turned to the left behind the drawing-room. Glass door at the end, holding the green of the garden, splashed with purple, white and red. The kitchen here in a back wing like a rough barn run out into the orchard.

[024-064]Upstairs Catty’s and Cook’s room in the wing; Papa’s dressing-room above the side passage; Roddy’s room above Papa’s den. Then the three rooms in front. The one above the drawing-room was nearly filled with the yellow birchwood wardrobe and bed. The emerald green of the damask was fading into the grey.

[024-065]Her mother was there, sitting in the window-seat, reading the fourteenth chapter of St. John.

[024-066]“Let not your heart be troubled: ye believe in God, believe also in me. In my Father’s house are many mansions﻿—”

[024-067]Mamma was different, too, as if she had shrunk through living in the cramped rooms. She raised her head. The head of a wounded bird, very gentle.

[024-068]“Why are you sitting up here all alone?”

[024-069]“Because sometimes I want to be alone.”

[024-070]“Shall I spoil the aloneness?”

[024-071]“Not if you’re a good girl and keep quiet.”

[024-072]Mary sat on the bed and waited till the chapter should be ended.

[024-073]She thought: “She talks to me still as though I were a child. What would she say if I told her about Aunt Charlotte? She wouldn’t know what it was really like. She wasn’t there.

[024-074]“I shall never tell her.”

[024-075]She was thrilled at the thought of her grown-up hardness, her grown-up silence, keeping her mother safe.

[024-076]Mamma looked up and smiled; the chapter was ended; they went downstairs.

[024-077]Papa stood in the doorway of his den and called to Mamma in a queer low voice.

[024-078]The letters﻿—

[024-079]She went into the dining-room and waited﻿—ten minutes﻿—twenty.

[024-080]Her mother came to her there. She sat down in her armchair by the window-seat where the old workbasket stood piled with socks ready for darning. She took a sock and drew it over her hand, stretching it to find the worn places. Mary took its fellow and began to darn it. The coarse wool, scraping her fingertips, sent through her a little light, creeping, disagreeable shock.

[024-081]She was afraid to look at her mother’s face.

[024-082]“Well, Mary﻿—poor Aunt Charlotte might have been carried away in her coffin, and we shouldn’t have known if it had been left to you to tell us.”

[024-083]“I didn’t because I thought it would frighten you.”

[024-084]Mamma was not frightened. They couldn’t have told her what it was really like.

[024-085]Papa’s slippers shuffled in the passage. Mamma left off darning to listen as Catty had listened.

### V

[024-086]On Greffington Edge.

[024-088]Roddy walked slowly, trying not to meet him at the cattle-gate. The tall man walked faster, and they met. Roddy opened the gate.

[024-135]Roddy had never said anything like that before.

[024-136]“I’ll stick to you, anyhow,” she said.

[024-137]“It’s no use your sticking. I shan’t be here. I shall have to clear out and do something,” he said.

[024-138]On his face there was a look of fear.

### VI

[024-139]She was excited because they were going to the ivy house for tea. It looked so pretty and so happy with its green face shining in the sun. Nothing could take from her her belief in happiness hiding behind certain unknown doors. It hid behind the white doors of the ivy house. When you went in something wonderful would happen.

[024-140]The ivy house belonged to Mrs. Waugh and Miss Frewin.

[024-141]The photographs in Mamma’s old album showed how they looked when they and Mamma were young. Modest pose of dropped arms, holding mushroom hats in front of them as a protection, the narrow ribbons dangling innocently. Ellen Frewin, small and upright, slender back curved in to the set of shawl and crinoline, prim head fixed in the composure of gentle disdain, small mouth saying always “Oh,” Meta, the younger sister, very tall, head bent in tranquil meditation, her mantle slanting out from the fall of the thin shoulders.

[024-142]They rose up in the small, green lighted drawing-room. Their heads bent forward to kiss.

[024-143]Ellen Waugh: the photographed face still keeping its lifted posture of gentle disdain, the skin stretched like a pale tight glove, a slight downward swelling of the prim oval, like the last bulge of a sucked peppermint ball, the faded mouth still making its small “oh.” She was the widow of a clergyman.

[024-144]Meta, a beautiful nose leaping out at you in a high curve; narrow, delicate cheeks thinned away so that they seemed part of the nose; sweet rodent mouth smiling up under its tip; blurred violet eyes arching vaguely.

[024-145]Princess gowns stiffened their shawl and crinoline gestures.

[024-146]“So this is Mary. She’s not like her mother, Caroline. Meta, can you see any likeness?”

[024-147]Miss Frewin arched her eyes and smiled, without looking at you.

[024-148]“I can’t say I do.”

[024-149]Their heads made little nodding bows as they talked. Miss Frewin’s bow was sidelong and slow, Mrs. Waugh’s straight and decisive.

[024-150]“She’s not like Rodney,” Mrs. Waugh said. “And she’s not like Emilius. Who is she like?”

[024-151]Mary answered. “I’m rather like Dan and a good bit like Mark. But I’m most of all like myself.”

[024-152]Mrs. Waugh said “Oh.” Her mouth went on saying it while she looked at you.

[024-153]“She is not in the least like Mark,” Mamma said.

[024-154]They settled down, one on each side of Mamma, smiling at her with their small, faded mouths as you smile at people you love and are happy with. You could see that Mamma was happy, too, sitting between them, safe.

[024-155]Mrs. Waugh said, “I see you’ve got Blenkiron in again?”

[024-156]“Well, he’s left his ladder in the yard. I suppose that means he’ll mend the kitchen chimney some time before winter.”

[024-157]“The Yorkshire workmen are very independent,” Mrs. Waugh said.

[024-158]“They scamp their work like the rest. You’d need a resident carpenter, and a resident glazier, and a resident plumber﻿—”

[024-159]“Yes, Caroline, you would indeed.”

[024-160]Gentle voices saying things you had heard before in the drawing-room at Five Elms.

[024-161]Miss Frewin had opened a black silk bag that hung on her arm, and taken out a minute pair of scissors and a long strip of white stuff with a stitched pattern on it. [024-162]“Do you like doing it?”

[024-163]“Yes.”

[024-164]She thought: “What a fool she must think me. As if she’d do it if she didn’t like it.”

[024-165]The arching eyes and twitching mouth smiled at your foolishness.

[024-166]Mrs. Waugh’s voice went on. It came smoothly, hardly moving her small, round mouth. That was her natural voice. Then suddenly it rose, like a voice that calls to you to get up in the morning.

[024-167]“Well, Mary﻿—so you’ve left school. Come home to be a help to your mother.”

[024-168]A high, false cheerfulness, covering disapproval and reproach.

[024-169]Their gentleness was cold to her and secretly inimical. They had asked her because of Mamma. They didn’t really want her.

[024-170]Half-past six. It was all over. They were going home across the Green.

[024-171]“Mary, I wish you could learn to talk without affectation. Telling Mrs. Waugh you ‘looked like yourself’! If you could only manage to forget yourself.”

[024-172]Your self? Your self? Why should you forget it? You had to remember. They would kill it if you let them.

[024-173]What had it done? What was it that they should hate it so? It had been happy and excited about them, wondering what they would be like. And quiet, looking on and listening, in the strange, green-lighted, green-dark room, crushed by the gentle, hostile voices.

[024-174]Would it always have to stoop and cringe before people, hushing its own voice, hiding its own gesture?

[024-175]It crouched now, stung and beaten, hiding in her body that walked beside her mother with proud feet, and small lifted head.

### VII

[024-222]“Not to anybody.”

[024-223]“Not to anybody who wouldn’t like it. Unless they make me. Will that do?”

[024-224]“I suppose it’ll have to.”

[024-225]Mamma held her face up, like a child, to be kissed.

### VIII

[024-226]The Sutcliffes’ house hid in the thick trees at the foot of Greffington Edge. You couldn’t see it. You could pretend it wasn’t there. You could pretend that Mr. Sutcliffe and Mrs. Sutcliffe were not there. You could pretend that nothing had happened.

[024-227]There were other houses.

### IX

[024-228]The long house at the top of the Green was gay with rows of pink and white sun-blinds stuck out like attic roofs. The poplars in the garden played their play of falling rain.

[024-229]You waited in the porch, impatient for the opening of the door.

[024-230]“Mamma﻿—what will it be like?”

[024-231]Mamma smiled a naughty, pretty smile. She knew what it would be like.

[024-232]There was a stuffed salmon in a long glass case in the hall. He swam, over a brown plaster river bed, glued to a milk-blue plaster stream.

[024-328]Stone walls. A wild country, caught in the net of the stone walls.
