Rathdale is a dale or valley near Morfe in Yorkshire, forming part of the rural landscape that Mary explores during her walks in the later sections of the novel. It lies adjacent to Garthdale, with the two dales meeting in the fields below the bridge at Morfe.

## Geography and Relation to Garthdale

Rathdale sits at the side of Garthdale, the two valleys converging in the fields below the bridge at Morfe.[^1] This geography places Rathdale within walking distance of Morfe village, accessible via field paths and lanes that Mary frequently traverses. The name "Rathdale" follows the Yorkshire dale-naming convention, suggesting a small side-valley off the main dale system.

> Garthdale in front of it, Rathdale at its side, meeting in the fields below its bridge. [024-018]

## Mary's Walks

Rathdale features in Mary's walking routes around Morfe. One route takes her "down the Back Lane and through the gap in the lower fields, along the flagged path to the Bottom Lane and through the Rathdale fields to the river."[^2] Another walk goes "up the Rathdale fields to Renton Moor,"[^3] indicating that Rathdale connects to the upland moorland landscape beyond the cultivated valley floor.

> Down the Back Lane and through the gap in the lower fields, along the flagged path to the Bottom Lane and through the Rathdale fields to the river. [036-051]

> Up the Rathdale fields to Renton Moor. [036-053]

## Social Encounters

Dr. Charles Kendal makes a comment about Mary's thin face, saying "Not in Rathdale, I'll be bound,"[^4] suggesting that Rathdale has a reputation for healthy air or that Mary's pallor is incongruent with the robust outdoor life associated with the dale.

> He hoped she liked Rathdale. [024-093]
> "Not in Rathdale, I'll be bound." [024-264]

## Significance

Rathdale represents the Yorkshire landscape that becomes Mary's refuge in maturity — a world of fields, rivers, dales, and moors that contrasts with the confined suburban spaces of her Ilford childhood. Together with Garthdale and Renton Moor, Rathdale forms part of the named rural geography that grounds Mary's experience of freedom and solitude in the novel's later sections.
