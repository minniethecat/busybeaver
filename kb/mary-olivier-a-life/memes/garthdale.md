## Description

Garthdale is a dale near [[morfe|Morfe]], lying "under the immense wall of Greffington Edge." [[PN=026-112]] It sits in relation to Rathdale, the two meeting "in the fields below [Morfe's] bridge." [[PN=024-018]] The Garthdale road runs up from the village, a route that [[mary-olivier|Mary]] and [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]] take together, and the dale is part of the network of valleys and ridges — Garthdale, Rathdale, [[renton|Renton Moor]], Greffington Edge, [[karva|Karva Hill]] — that constitute the topographical texture of Mary's Yorkshire world.

The Garthdale road is described as "a white highway curving like a sickle" — one of the novel's recurring images of landscape as both beautiful and threatening instrument. [[PN=024-356]]

## Significance in the Narrative

Garthdale Road is the setting for social encounters that punctuate Mary's solitary walking life. On the Garthdale road, Mary and [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]] encounter Mrs. Waugh, one of the village figures who populate the Yorkshire social landscape. [[PN=024-356]]

The dale takes on symbolic weight through its relation to Greffington Edge. "Down in the darkness beyond the fields Garthdale lay like a ditch under the immense wall of Greffington Edge" — the valley as ditch, the edge as wall, the topography rendered as architecture of enclosure and threat. [[PN=026-112]] This is the landscape as Mary experiences it in moments of psychological pressure: not picturesque but confining, the high edges pressing down on the low dales.

Garthdale is also part of Mary's deliberate route-avoidance in later years. When she walks "up the Rathdale fields to Renton Moor," she explicitly does not go "on the Garthdale Road" — the Garthdale road is associated with memories of [[lindley-vickers|Lindley Vickers]], [[maurice-jourdain|Maurice Jourdain]], [[roddy-olivier|Roddy]], and [[mark-olivier|Mark]] — figures whose loss makes certain paths unbearable. [[PN=036-053]]

## Key Passages

> Garthdale in front of it, Rathdale at its side, meeting in the fields below its bridge.
> [[PN=024-018]]

The dales are rendered in spatial relation to each other and to the bridge — a geography of convergence that maps the village's setting among the Yorkshire valleys.

> Down in the darkness beyond the fields Garthdale lay like a ditch under the immense wall of Greffington Edge.
> [[PN=026-112]]

The simile — "like a ditch" — strips the dale of pastoral charm. Under Greffington Edge, Garthdale becomes a trench, a place of darkness and enclosure rather than openness and freedom.

> Not on the Garthdale Road... Because of Lindley Vickers and Maurice Jourdain; and Roddy and Mark.
> [[PN=036-053]]

The Garthdale road joins the geography of loss: a path that cannot be walked because of the ghosts that walk it. Mary's avoidance maps grief onto the Yorkshire landscape.
