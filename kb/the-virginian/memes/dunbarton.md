# Dunbarton, New Hampshire

**Dunbarton, New Hampshire** is the home of [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s great-aunt — the last surviving bearer of the Stark name — and the site of one of the novel's most pivotal conversations. When Molly flees [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] after learning of [[the-virginian|the Virginian]]'s role in the [[hanging-of-steve|hanging of Steve]], it is to Dunbarton, not [[bennington|Bennington]], that she turns for wisdom. The great-aunt's counsel — born of her own lost love, a ship's captain who never returned — authorizes Molly to choose passion over propriety, love over station.

## Role in the Novel

Dunbarton appears sparingly but decisively. During Molly's summer visits, [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]] would take her by the hand and, "after looking with fond intentness at her," pronounce her a true Stark (005-021). These visits build the emotional foundation for [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]]'s climactic role: when Molly, torn between love for the Virginian and fear of social disgrace, returns East, it is [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]] who gives her permission to choose love.

The great-aunt's own story — she loved a man "brave and handsome" who was lost at sea before she could give him her answer — is the novel's most poignant miniature. "If I had — if — perhaps I should have lost him; but it would have been after — ah, well!" (034-028). This confession, delivered in the quiet of the Dunbarton house, releases Molly from the bondage of social expectation.

## Key Scenes

### The Summer Visits

Each summer, Molly traveled to Dunbarton to pay "her established family visit to the last survivors of her connection who bore the name of Stark" (005-021). These visits, described with nostalgic warmth, establish Dunbarton as a repository of family memory and inherited character.

### The Great-Aunt's Confession (Chapter XXXIV)

Molly returns to Dunbarton in crisis, having written the Virginian a farewell letter. The great-aunt, now "an old, old woman," perceives the change in Molly immediately. Her advice — "When you cannot help it a moment longer, then listen to nothing but that" — is the novel's most direct endorsement of love over convention. She speaks from experience: her own failure to commit cost her everything. Molly must not repeat the mistake.

## Themes & Analysis

### Ancestral Wisdom

Dunbarton represents the ancestral Stark legacy — the lineage of strong women that Molly carries into the West. The great-aunt's counsel is not merely personal advice but the distilled wisdom of generations. Molly's choice to follow it is an affirmation of her Stark blood.

### Permission to Love

The Dunbarton scenes address a question the novel has been asking since Molly's first appearance: can a "lady" love a [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]? The great-aunt's answer is an unambiguous yes — provided the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] is worthy. Her blessing transforms Molly's social anxiety into moral clarity.
