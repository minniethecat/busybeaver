# Mrs. Wood

**Mrs. Wood** is [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Stark Wood]]'s mother, a conventional [[bennington|Bennington, Vermont]] matron whose shock at her daughter's engagement to the Virginian embodies the novel's central cultural tension between Eastern propriety and the untamed West. Though she never appears in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]], her letters and the reactions they provoke in Molly make her a significant offstage presence whose values must be confronted and overcome.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Wood is the voice of [[bennington|Bennington]] — the settled, conventional Eastern world that Molly leaves behind and to which, in some sense, she can never fully return. She appears solely through letters and through the reactions she provokes in Molly, yet her influence permeates the novel's final third. When Molly sends home the letter announcing her engagement, Mrs. Wood's response embodies everything the novel's Western characters are not: decorous, fearful of social disgrace, and fundamentally unable to comprehend a life outside Vermont's settled expectations.

> "A savage with knives and pistols!" she wailed. (034-???)

Her horror is comical yet also serves a serious narrative purpose: it forces Molly to confront, explicitly and painfully, the gap between how the Virginian is and how [[bennington|Bennington]] would perceive him. The scenes in which Molly imagines taking the Virginian to Vermont — the doorbells, the drawing-rooms, the secret appraising eyes — are among the novel's most acute social observations.

## Key Relationships

### Molly Stark Wood

Mrs. Wood loves her daughter but cannot understand her. When Molly went to teach school in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]], Mrs. Wood worried about the "dreadful place" and sent her books to sustain her mind (022-???). Her concern is genuine, but it is the concern of a woman who cannot conceive that her daughter might find meaning, let alone love, in a world without parlors.

### Sarah Bell

Molly's elder sister Sarah is Mrs. Wood's ally in [[bennington|Bennington]] orthodoxy. After the engagement letter arrives, it is Sarah who reads the Virginian's letter and quotes his admission that he has "never killed for pleasure or profit" back at Molly as damning evidence (036-???). Together, mother and elder sister constitute the tribunal of Eastern judgment that Molly must defy.

### The Virginian

Though Mrs. Wood and the Virginian never meet in the novel's primary timeline, they correspond. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s letter to Mrs. Wood is a pivotal moment — his attempt to speak honestly to the mother of the woman he loves. Mrs. Wood cannot appreciate the candor; she sees only confirmation of her worst fears.

### Great-Aunt at Dunbarton

The great-aunt at [[dunbarton|Dunbarton]] provides the counterweight to Mrs. Wood. Where Mrs. Wood wails, [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]] reads the Virginian's letter carefully and pronounces, "The man appears to have written to me himself" (034-???). The contrast between these two older women — one panicked, one perceptive — dramatizes the novel's argument that wisdom does not necessarily reside in conventional respectability.

## Key Scenes

### The Letter of Engagement

Mrs. Wood receives eleven pages from Molly (plus a postscript on the twelfth) attempting to break the news of her engagement. "Its effect was the usual effect of remarks designed to break a thing gently. It merely made Mrs. Wood's head swim, and filled her with a sickening dread" (034-???). The letter's very length and care testify to Molly's anxiety about her mother's judgment.

### The Virginian's Letter

> "The man appears to have written to me himself," said Mrs. Wood. (034-???)

This single line, delivered after Mrs. Wood receives the Virginian's direct communication, captures her bewilderment. A man who writes — and writes plainly and honestly — is not quite the "savage with knives and pistols" she had imagined. The letter forces a crack in her preconceptions.

### The Great-Aunt's Intervention

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s letter is forwarded to [[dunbarton|Dunbarton]], where the old lady reads it with attention and writes back to Molly with words "like balm among the harsh stings Molly was receiving" (036-???). Meanwhile, "her days were full of hurts; and there was no one by to kiss the hurts away." The support of [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]] and the silence of Mrs. Wood together define Molly's emotional isolation during this period.

## Themes

Mrs. Wood represents the social conservatism that the novel neither wholly condemns nor endorses. Her fears are not groundless — she has never seen [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]], never met the Virginian, and has only her daughter's bewildering accounts to guide her. The novel's generosity lies in allowing her to be wrong without being villainous, a mother whose love is genuine but whose vision is limited by the small horizons of a small Vermont town.
