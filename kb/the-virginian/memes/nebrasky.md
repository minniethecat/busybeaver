# Nebrasky

**Nebrasky** is a young [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] employed at the [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]] in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. He appears primarily in the Goose Egg dance chapter (X) as one of the many cowpunchers smitten with the new schoolmarm, [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]], and becomes a target of [[honey-wiggin|Honey Wiggin]]'s teasing humor for his awkward romantic overtures.

## Introduction

Nebrasky is first introduced among the roster of cowpunchers at [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]]'s [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]], listed alongside [[honey-wiggin|Honey Wiggin]], Dollar Bill, and Chalkeye—men drawn from "farms and cities, from Maine and from California" by the "romance of American adventure" [006-005]. His name, derived from the state of Nebraska, marks him as one of the many young men who came West to find work and adventure on the cattle ranges.

## The Goose Egg Dance

Nebrasky's principal appearance in the novel occurs at the Goose Egg social gathering, where the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s assemble around a roasting steer and the prospect of dancing with [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]. When [[honey-wiggin|Honey Wiggin]] begins his round of public teasing, Nebrasky becomes one of his prime targets.

[[honey-wiggin|Wiggin]] calls out Nebrasky for having offered [[molly-stark-wood|the schoolmarm]] a caged canary as a gift, despite her explicit instruction not to:

> "Nebrasky and the boys was tellin' me they'd missed yu' off the range," again interposed Wiggin. "Say, Nebrasky, who have yu' offered your canary to the schoolmarm said you mustn't give her?" [010-012]

Nebrasky's response captures his character in a single image: he "grinned wretchedly" [010-013]. The phrase conveys the deep embarrassment of a young man whose romantic gesture has been publicly exposed and mocked, yet who lacks either the wit to deflect the attack or the anger to resent it.

His illiteracy is noted in passing: "It was well known that he could not write his name" [010-015], a detail that underscores his humble origins and the social distance between the cowpunchers and the educated schoolmarm from Vermont.

### Romantic Optimism

Despite the public humiliation, Nebrasky clings to a shred of romantic hope. Later in the same scene, when [[honey-wiggin|Wiggin]] catalogs the long list of suitors who have failed with [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]], Nebrasky ventures a timid defense:

> "I think she encourages a man some," said poor Nebrasky. [010-025]

The narrator's adjective "poor" signals the sympathy extended to this young [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]], whose hopefulness is both endearing and clearly doomed. [[honey-wiggin|Wiggin]] immediately punctures it: "Encourages? Because she lets yu' teach her how to shoot" [010-026].

## Character Significance

Nebrasky is a minor figure in the novel, but his brief appearance serves multiple narrative purposes. He exemplifies the ordinary [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]—young, unlettered, socially awkward—whose romantic aspirations toward [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] highlight both her desirability and her fundamental unattainability for men of his station. His wretched grin and stubborn hope humanize the bunkhouse community, reminding the reader that behind the leather chaps and spurs are young men with the same vulnerabilities as any other.

In the novel's social hierarchy, Nebrasky occupies the lowest rung among the named [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s: he is neither a leader like the Virginian nor a skilled verbal combatant like [[honey-wiggin|Honey Wiggin]], but a follower—well-meaning, ineffectual, and ultimately sympathetic in his small defeats.
