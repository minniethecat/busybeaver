# Balaam

**Balaam** is the owner of a ranch on Butte Creek and one of *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*'s most unambiguously contemptible characters. Unlike [[trampas]], whose villainy has a certain dark charisma, Balaam is portrayed as petty, vindictive, and cruel — a man whose mistreatment of animals serves as the novel's shorthand for moral bankruptcy. His name, drawn from the biblical Balaam who struck his donkey, is no accident: the episode in which he brutalizes the horse [[pedro|Pedro]] is a direct moral indictment, and [[the-virginian]]'s violent intervention on the animal's behalf is one of the novel's most satisfying moments of frontier justice.

## Role in the Novel

Balaam serves as an object lesson in the kind of man the West should not produce. If the Virginian represents the ideal synthesis of strength and restraint, Balaam represents strength without conscience — the bully who uses his power over the weak (animals, employees, anyone smaller) to indulge his rages. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s judgment is delivered early and definitively:

> "A man that will do what they claim Balaam does to a hawss when he's mad, ain't fit to be called human."
> （004-063）

This condemnation — delivered before the reader ever meets Balaam — frames every subsequent appearance. Balaam is not a complex villain; he is a brute, and the novel treats him accordingly. His function is to demonstrate that the frontier code judges men by how they treat the powerless, not merely by how they face their equals.

Structurally, Balaam provides the occasion for one of the novel's most dramatic set pieces: the Virginian's confrontation with him over the abused horse [[pedro|Pedro]]. This scene allows Wister to dramatize the code's animal ethic — the belief that cruelty to a horse is a reliable index of a man's soul.

## Key Relationships

### The Virginian

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s contempt for Balaam is immediate and total. When he is sent to Balaam's ranch to retrieve two borrowed horses that Balaam has failed to return, the Virginian must navigate the tension between his duty to [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]] and his personal disgust. His discovery of Balaam's abuse of [[pedro|Pedro]] transforms a routine errand into a moral confrontation.

### Judge Henry

Balaam's relationship with [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]] is purely transactional and reveals Balaam's petty dishonesty. He borrows two horses from the Judge and neglects to return them, forcing the Judge to send a written reminder. Balaam's reaction to this "dunning reminder" — resentment rather than shame — is characteristic: "Balaam was ready to be very disagreeable now" (025-018).

### Mrs. Balaam

Mrs. Balaam appears only through correspondence — she exchanges letters with [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] and her mother, forming part of the thin social network of [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] women. Her existence humanizes Balaam slightly, or at least complicates the picture: that such a man should have a wife who corresponds with ladies of quality suggests the social realities of a sparsely populated territory.

### Shorty

Balaam briefly employs [[shorty|Shorty]], and their interactions underscore both men's limitations — Balaam's pettiness and [[shorty|Shorty]]'s helplessness. When [[shorty|Shorty]] learns that Balaam has sold [[pedro|Pedro]], his distress is genuine: "You've sure not sold [[pedro|Pedro]]?" (026-082). [[shorty|Shorty]], for all his weakness, cared about the horse; Balaam cared about nothing but his own convenience.

## Key Scenes

### The Horses at the Creek (Chapter XXV)

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]] arrives at Balaam's ranch and witnesses the rancher's casual brutality from across the stream:

> "For Balaam had led some horses to the water, and was lashing them heavily because they would not drink."
> （025-010）

The image is carefully chosen: Balaam whips horses for refusing water, a perversion of the pastoral ideal. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]], watching from concealment, forms his judgment before any words are exchanged.

### The Reminder Letter

Balaam's reception of [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]]'s letter requesting the return of his horses reveals the man's pettiness:

> "As Balaam read the reminder, he wished that he had sent the horses before. Balaam could not but excuse the 'dunning reminder,' — but he was ready to be disagreeable to somebody at once."
> （025-015）

The phrasing is ironic: Balaam "could not but excuse" the reminder (it was justified), yet his immediate reaction is to inflict his bad temper on others. This is the psychology of the bully — incapable of accepting fault, always seeking a target.

### The Pedro Episode (Chapter XXVI)

Chapter XXVI is titled "Balaam and [[pedro|Pedro]]," and the episode is the moral center of Balaam's arc. [[pedro|Pedro]] is a horse that Balaam has brutalized; the Virginian, discovering the animal's injuries, intervenes with characteristic quiet force. The biblical echo is deliberate: in Numbers 22, Balaam's donkey speaks to protest its beating; in Wister's [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]], the Virginian speaks for [[pedro|Pedro]].

The episode's resolution — the Virginian purchasing [[pedro|Pedro]] from Balaam — is less a transaction than an expropriation. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] takes the horse because Balaam has forfeited any moral right to own it.

## Themes & Analysis

### Cruelty as Character Index

Wister employs Balaam to advance the novel's most consistent ethical claim: that a man's treatment of animals reveals his essential nature. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] states this explicitly: Balaam's abuse of horses proves he "ain't fit to be called human" (004-063). This judgment is never qualified or complicated — Balaam is exactly what his cruelty suggests, and the novel offers no redemption for him.

### The Biblical Name

The choice of "Balaam" as the character's name is unusually direct for Wister, whose biblical allusions tend toward the subtle. In the Old Testament, Balaam is a prophet who strikes his donkey and is rebuked by the animal, which God enables to speak. By naming his horse-abusing rancher Balaam, Wister invites the reader to see the [[pedro|Pedro]] episode as a moral fable: the abused animal cannot speak, but the Virginian speaks for it, and his judgment carries the weight of divine sanction.

### Petty Tyranny

Balaam is a study in petty tyranny — the cruelty of the small man with small power. Unlike [[trampas|Trampas]], whose villainy operates on a grand scale (rustling, murder), Balaam's sins are quotidian: borrowing without returning, lashing out when inconvenienced, taking his frustrations out on creatures that cannot fight back. The novel's contempt for him is correspondingly more intimate: [[trampas|Trampas]] must be killed; Balaam need only be exposed.
