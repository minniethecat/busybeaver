# Bishop of Wyoming

The **Bishop of Wyoming** is an unnamed Episcopal clergyman who appears in the novel's later chapters as a representative of organized religion's reach into the frontier. His visit to [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]], where he delivers a sermon to the assembled [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s and ranch hands, provides one of the novel's most subtly humorous commentaries on the relationship between institutional Christianity and the informal moral codes of the West.

## Role in the Novel

The Bishop appears briefly but significantly during his visit to [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]]. His presence occasions one of the novel's characteristic juxtapositions: the formal, institutional morality of the Eastern church set against the unwritten but fiercely observed ethical code of the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s. The Bishop's sermon, delivered to an audience that includes men who have hung [[rustler-gang|rustlers]] and faced down armed enemies, creates a moment of collision between two worlds that normally operate in parallel.

The Bishop is treated respectfully — Wister has no interest in caricaturing the clergy — but the reader is left to contemplate the distance between Sunday morning in a [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] ranch house and the decisions men make on the open range. The Bishop represents "civilization" in its institutional form, just as [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] represents it in its domestic form and [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]] in its legal form.

## Key Scenes

### The Sermon at Sunk Creek

The Bishop's sermon is the centerpiece of his brief appearance. Delivered to [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s whose exposure to church ritual is minimal at best, it becomes an occasion for the narrator to observe how Western men respond to formal religion: with outward respect but inward independence. The scene anticipates the novel's final vision of [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]], where church and schoolhouse stand alongside the saloon and the corral as markers of a society in transition.

## Significance

The Bishop of Wyoming, though a minor figure, completes the novel's portrait of the forces that were settling and "civilizing" the frontier. Alongside [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]] (law), [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] (education and domesticity), and [[the-railroad|the railroad]] (commerce), the Bishop represents the institutional infrastructure that would transform [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] from open range to settled territory. His presence at Sunk Creek signals that even this remote outpost is not beyond the reach of organized society.
