# Mrs. Flynt

**Mrs. Flynt** is a minor but memorable character in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*, serving as the resident social commentator of [[hoosic-falls|Hoosic Falls]], New York. As the wife of the Baptist minister, she occupies a position of moral authority in the community, which she exercises chiefly through pointed gossip about [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s social standing and conduct.

## Social Enforcer of Hoosic Falls

Mrs. Flynt first appears in the novel's extended introduction ("To the Reader"), where she is immediately established as the voice of conventional social judgment. When the townspeople of [[bennington|Bennington]] wonder whether [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]], who earns her living teaching music, is truly a "lady," it is Mrs. Flynt who articulates the criticism:

> "I dare say he had a great-grandmother quite as good as hers," remarked Mrs. Flynt, the wife of the Baptist minister. (P02-577)

The "he" in question is [[sam-bannett|Sam Bannett]], Molly's persistent suitor, and Mrs. Flynt's remark is a direct challenge to the Stark family's claim to social distinction. Her comment reveals the petty class anxieties that animate [[hoosic-falls|Hoosic Falls]] society.

## Conflict with the Rector

Mrs. Flynt's exchange with the Episcopal rector of Hoosic—who is a friend of Molly's—provides one of the novel's sharpest satirical moments:

> "That's entirely possible," returned the Episcopal rector of Hoosic, "only we don't happen to know who she was." The rector was a friend of Molly's. After this little observation, Mrs. Flynt said no more, but continued her purchases in the store where she and the rector had happened to find themselves together. Later she stated to a friend that she had always thought the Episcopal Church a snobbish one, and now she knew it. (P02-578)

This brief scene captures Mrs. Flynt's character in miniature: she gossips, she is rebuffed, she retreats—and then she retaliates by expanding her criticism to encompass an entire denomination. The rector's dry defense of Molly and Mrs. Flynt's reflexive anti-Episcopal prejudice together illustrate the petty sectarian and social divisions of small-town Eastern life that Molly will eventually leave behind.

## Fear for Molly's Looks

As Molly continues to resist [[sam-bannett|Sam Bannett]]'s advances and considers her move to the frontier, Mrs. Flynt interprets her behavior through the lens of physical appearance:

> her friends thought that she was overworked, and Mrs. Flynt feared she was losing her looks. (P02-581)

This observation, though ostensibly concerned, subtly reinforces Mrs. Flynt's worldview: a woman's value lies in her marriageability, and declining to marry the most eligible man in town is a form of self-destruction.

## The Letter Incident

When news of Molly's life in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] reaches [[bennington|Bennington]] through her letters home, Mrs. Flynt is denied direct access to the information—a slight she does not forget:

> Mrs. Flynt, when this story was repeated to her (she had not been invited in to hear the letter), remarked that she had always felt that Molly Wood must be a little vulgar, ever since she began to go about giving music lessons like any ordinary German. (P02-878)

Excluded from the inner circle, Mrs. Flynt retaliates with the most cutting judgment available to her: that Molly is "a little vulgar." The comparison to "any ordinary German" reveals the nativist and class prejudices underlying [[hoosic-falls|Hoosic Falls]] respectability.

## The Rustler Rumor

When rumors reach [[bennington|Bennington]] that Molly is involved with a "rustler" in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]], Mrs. Flynt again serves as the voice of dire speculation:

> Mrs. Flynt feared she had married a Mormon.
> （022-231）

The rumor—absurd on its face—illustrates how thoroughly the Eastern gossips misunderstand the Western world Molly now inhabits. The word "rustler" accumulates increasingly lurid meanings as it travels east, and Mrs. Flynt's leap to "Mormon" is the comic culmination of this process.

## Epilogue: Final Revenge

In the novel's closing pages, Mrs. Flynt gets the last word—or tries to:

> Mrs. Flynt took her revenge by sowing broadcast her thankfulness that poor Sam Bannett had been Molly's rejected suitor. He had done so much better for himself. Sam had married a rich Miss Van Scootzer, of the second families of Troy; and with their combined riches this happy couple still inhabit the most expensive residence in Hoosic Falls.
> （029-193）

The narrator's deadpan reporting of this "revenge" is quietly devastating. Mrs. Flynt measures success in real estate and social standing—precisely the values Molly has left behind. The reader, who has witnessed the depth of Molly's experience in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]], understands how hollow Mrs. Flynt's triumph truly is.

## Character Significance

Mrs. Flynt is one of Wister's most effective minor characters, serving as a satirical portrait of small-town Eastern respectability. She embodies the gossip, class anxiety, and moral conventionality that the novel's Western setting exists to escape. Her judgments—on Molly's ancestry, her looks, her conduct, her suitors—are consistently rendered ironic by the narrator's understated presentation, making her a figure of comedy even as she functions as a serious obstacle in Molly's social world.

> **Thematic note**: Mrs. Flynt represents what the novel calls "the rubbish heap" of the world—those who "wondered how Miss Wood could go about from house to house teaching the piano, and she a lady." Her presence in the narrative sharpens the contrast between the constricted social world of the East and the liberating possibilities of the frontier.
