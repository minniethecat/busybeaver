# The Cheyenne Club

The **Cheyenne Club** was the social institution of the [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] cattle aristocracy during the open-range era depicted in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. Mentioned as part of the great cattle boom when steers "jumped to seventy-five," the Club is invoked alongside the counties of the cattle country — "Johnson County, and Natrona, and Converse, and others, to say nothing of the Cheyenne Club" (009-002) — as one of the institutions that celebrated the industry's prosperity.

## The Cattle Aristocracy

The Cheyenne Club was the gathering place for the territory's most powerful cattlemen — men like [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]], who combined Western operations with Eastern connections and Eastern standards of comfort. The Club represented the cattle barons' aspiration to gentility: it was a place where men who spent their days on horseback could dine in formal clothes, discuss Eastern politics, and display the refinement that distinguished them from the ordinary cowhand.

The Club's mention in the novel is brief but significant. It is named alongside the counties of [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] as if it were a jurisdiction in its own right — a recognition that power in the territory was exercised through social institutions as well as political ones.

## Historical Reality

Unlike many of the novel's fictional institutions, the Cheyenne Club was real. Founded in 1880, it served as the headquarters of the [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] Stock Growers Association and the social hub of the cattle aristocracy that Wister knew from his summers in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]]. The Club's membership included the most powerful men in the territory, and its building — with its wine cellars, reading rooms, and formal dining room — was a deliberate importation of Eastern club culture to the frontier.

The historical Cheyenne Club was a symbol of both the cattle boom's wealth and its fragility. The disastrous winter of 1886–87, which destroyed much of the open-range cattle industry, also marked the beginning of the Club's decline. By the time Wister published *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]* in 1902, the Cheyenne Club — like the open range itself — was already a memory.
