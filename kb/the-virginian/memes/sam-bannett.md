# Sam Bannett

**Sam Bannett** is a wealthy young man from [[hoosic-falls|Hoosic Falls]], New York, who serves as [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s persistent suitor in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. He represents the conventional Eastern life of financial comfort and social respectability that Molly decisively rejects when she chooses to go west—and ultimately when she chooses the Virginian over all that Sam Bannett embodies.

## The Persistent Suitor

Sam Bannett is introduced early in the novel as the man that [[hoosic-falls|Hoosic Falls]] society considers the obvious match for [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]. The townspeople "all with one voice declared that Sam Bannett was good enough for anybody who did fancy embroidery at five cents a letter" ([[TOC|P02]]-576). The implication is clear: Molly, who works for a living teaching music and doing embroidery, should be grateful for the attention of a man of Bannett's prospects.

Molly, however, firmly and repeatedly rejects him:

> Molly Wood continued cheerfully to embroider the handkerchiefs, make the preserves, teach the pupils—and firmly to reject Sam Bannett. (P02-580)

Her rejection of Bannett is not merely personal; it is a rejection of the entire social framework that would measure her worth by whom she marries. Even Molly's own mother cannot understand it:

> "She wouldn't ride with poor Sam Bannett, and after all he is a suitable person."
> （012-017）

Mrs. Wood's voice here represents the settled Eastern consensus: Sam Bannett is suitable, wealthy, respectable — what more could a young woman want? But the very quality that makes him "suitable" to [[hoosic-falls|Hoosic Falls]] is what makes him insufficient to Molly.

## The Railroad Station Encounter

When Molly departs for the West, Sam Bannett makes one last attempt. At [[the-railroad|the railroad]] station, just as she is leaving:

> But something caused her eyes to open; and there before her stood Sam Bannett, asking if he might accompany her so far as Rotterdam Junction. (P02-610)

This moment—Bannett appearing unbidden at the station, hoping to extend his pursuit even as Molly is physically leaving his world—epitomizes his inability to accept her rejection. Molly refuses him once more, and [[the-railroad|the train]] carries her away from [[bennington|Bennington]] and from the life Bannett represents.

## A Contrast to the Virginian

Bannett's role in the novel is largely defined by contrast with the Virginian. The narrator makes this explicit when describing the effect the Virginian has on Molly:

> Sam Bannett did not have it in him to look as this man could look, when the cold lustre of his eyes grew hot with internal fire. (P02-922)

Where Bannett is safe, conventional, and financially comfortable, the Virginian is dangerous, magnetic, and mysterious. The narrator reflects on this contrast with wry self-awareness:

> How is it that this girl could cry at having to tell Sam Bannett she could not think of him, and then treat another lover as she treated the Virginian? I cannot tell you, having never (as I said before) been a woman myself.
> （025-188）

## Molly's Return Visit

When Molly returns to [[bennington|Bennington]] for a visit after her time at [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]], the Bannetts host a lavish dinner in her [[frontier-honor|honor]]:

> The Bannetts at Hoosic Falls offered a large specimen to Molly—a dinner (perhaps I should say a banquet) of twenty-four. And Sam Bannett of course took her to drive more than once.
> （025-190）

During these drives, Sam renews his suit with a plaintive directness:

> "I don't forget you," said Sam. "Do you remember me? Or is it out of sight out of mind?"
> （022-027）

His question cuts to the heart of their relationship. For Sam, proximity is everything; for Molly, who has now known the West and the Virginian, Sam has become a memory belonging to a life she has already left behind. The narrator confirms the outcome with characteristic understatement:

> Was it? At all events, Sam failed in his suit.
> （022-030）

These scenes, in which Molly revisits familiar places while her mind is clearly elsewhere—dwelling on [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] and the Virginian—underscore how irrevocably she has left her old life behind. The drives with Sam Bannett only confirm what she already knows.

## Epilogue: Sam Bannett's Fate

The novel provides a brief epilogue for Sam Bannett in its closing pages. [[mrs-flynt|Mrs. Flynt]], the chief gossip of [[hoosic-falls|Hoosic Falls]], takes satisfaction in reporting that:

> Mrs. Flynt took her revenge by sowing broadcast her thankfulness that poor Sam Bannett had been Molly's rejected suitor. He had done so much better for himself. Sam had married a rich Miss Van Scootzer, of the second families of Troy; and with their combined riches this happy couple still inhabit the most expensive residence in Hoosic Falls.
> （029-193）

This conclusion is satisfying in its symmetry: Sam Bannett marries within his own social world, to material success and conventional happiness, while Molly finds something altogether different—and, the novel strongly implies, far more meaningful—in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] with the Virginian.

## Character Significance

Sam Bannett functions in the novel as the embodiment of the life Molly rejects. He is not portrayed as a villain—indeed, he is treated with a certain sympathy as "poor Sam Bannett"—but as a representative of everything conventional, safe, and ultimately insufficient about Eastern society. His wealth, his persistence, and his eventual marriage to a rich woman of good family all mark him as a success by the standards of [[hoosic-falls|Hoosic Falls]]. That Molly wants none of it is the measure of her difference from her surroundings, and the justification for her journey west.
