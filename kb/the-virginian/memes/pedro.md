# Pedro

**Pedro** is a buckskin cow-pony — first the beloved mount of the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] [[shorty|Shorty]], then the victim of [[balaam|Balaam]]'s cruelty. In a novel where "a man's treatment of animals reveals his essential nature" (see [[the-code-of-the-west|The Code of the West]]), Pedro is more than livestock: he is a moral test that [[balaam|Balaam]] fails catastrophically and [[shorty|Shorty]], for all his weakness, passes with tenderness.

## Role in the Novel

Pedro appears in the novel as a moral barometer. The horse himself is innocent — a gentle, well-trained animal who trusts his master. But the men around him reveal themselves through their treatment of him.

[[shorty|Shorty]], despite his weaknesses (gambling, drifting, falling under [[trampas|Trampas]]'s influence), has gentled Pedro with patience and affection. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] notes this early, using it as evidence that [[shorty|Shorty]] still has good in him:

> "Shorty is kind to animals," he said. "He has gentled that hawss Pedro he bought with his first money. Gentled him wonderful. When a man is kind to dumb animals, I always say he had got some good in him."
> （018-026）

This judgment is vindicated when we see [[shorty|Shorty]] with Pedro — the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] has taught the horse to shake hands with both forehooves, a trick that speaks to patience and mutual trust rather than dominance:

> "Shake!" he said to his pony, who lifted his forefoot quietly and put it in his master's hand. ... "Now the other hoof," said Shorty; and the horse and master shook hands with their left. "I learned him that," said the cowboy, with pride and affection.
> （026-036）

[[balaam|Balaam]], by contrast, brutalizes Pedro when angered — whipping him, overworking him, and ultimately selling him without a thought for the animal's welfare. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s condemnation of [[balaam|Balaam]] on precisely these grounds ("A man that will do what they claim [[balaam|Balaam]] does to a hawss when he's mad, ain't fit to be called human," 004-063) is delivered before the reader witnesses the abuse, establishing Pedro's mistreatment as the definitive proof of [[balaam|Balaam]]'s character.

## Key Relationships

### Shorty

The bond between [[shorty|Shorty]] and Pedro is one of the novel's few moments of uncomplicated tenderness. [[shorty|Shorty]], who has lost his money to [[trampas|Trampas]] at poker, still has Pedro — and the care he lavishes on the horse suggests the better man he might have been under different circumstances. When [[shorty|Shorty]] learns [[balaam|Balaam]] has sold Pedro, his distress is genuine: "You've sure not sold Pedro?" (026-082).

### Balaam

[[balaam|Balaam]]'s treatment of Pedro is the novel's definitive moral indictment of the man. The biblical echo of [[balaam|Balaam]]'s name — the prophet who struck his donkey in Numbers 22 — makes the parallel explicit: [[balaam|Balaam]] brutalizes the innocent creature in his power, and the creature cannot speak in its own defense.

### The Virginian

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s rescue of Pedro from [[balaam|Balaam]] is an act of moral enforcement, not charity. He purchases the horse not because he needs another mount but because [[balaam|Balaam]] has forfeited the right to own any animal. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] speaks for the voiceless, a role the novel consistently assigns to its hero.

## Key Scenes

### The Handshake (Chapter XXVI)

[[shorty|Shorty]] demonstrates Pedro's training to [[balaam|Balaam]] — a performance of trust and affection that [[balaam|Balaam]], characteristically, sees only in terms of its monetary value:

> "Drybone's had no circus this season. Maybe they'd buy tickets to see Pedro. He's good for that, anyway."
> （026-039）

[[balaam|Balaam]]'s inability to see beauty in the horse's gentleness — his reduction of the animal to a commodity or a circus act — distills his moral poverty into a single line.

### The Rescue

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s confrontation with [[balaam|Balaam]] over Pedro's abuse is one of the novel's most satisfying scenes of frontier justice. Unlike the violent climax with [[trampas|Trampas]], this confrontation is resolved through economic power (the Virginian simply buys the horse) backed by moral authority. [[balaam|Balaam]] cannot refuse because he knows, and the Virginian knows, and the reader knows, that he is in the wrong.

## Themes & Analysis

### The Animal Ethic

Pedro is the novel's clearest vehicle for what might be called the "animal ethic" of the Western code. The belief that cruelty to animals is a reliable index of a man's soul runs throughout the novel, and Pedro's story — from [[shorty|Shorty]]'s gentleness to [[balaam|Balaam]]'s brutality to the Virginian's intervention — is its most fully realized expression.

### The Voiceless Victim

Pedro cannot speak. The novel repeatedly places voiceless or silenced figures at the center of its moral drama, and the hero's role is consistently to speak or act on their behalf. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] speaks for Pedro as surely as he speaks for the code itself.
