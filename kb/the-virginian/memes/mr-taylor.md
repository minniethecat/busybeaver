# Mr. Taylor

**Mr. Taylor** is a [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] homesteader and family man whose determination to build a schoolhouse and hire a proper teacher from the East brings [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] to [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]]. In a novel dominated by [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s, outlaws, and violent codes of [[frontier-honor|honor]], Taylor represents the arriving force of domestic settlement — the quiet, stubborn, family-centered civilization that will ultimately transform the frontier more completely than any gunfighter ever could.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Taylor is the novel's embodiment of the civilizing impulse that follows the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] frontier. Where the Virginian represents the code of the trail and the cattle range, Taylor represents fences, schoolhouses, and the education of "offspring." His importance to the plot is causal: it is Taylor's committee that corresponds with [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] in Vermont and hires her to teach at [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]], setting the entire romantic plot in motion.

When the Virginian and the narrator encounter Taylor on the trail, the contrast is immediate and deliberate. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] has just been explaining the justice of a hanging; Taylor arrives with news of a schoolhouse:

> Importance came into Mr. Taylor's countenance. "Bear Creek is going to build a schoolhouse," said he.
> （004-094）

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s response — "Goodness gracious! What's that for?" — is only half in jest. Taylor and the Virginian occupy different [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] frontiers, and Wister lets the reader see that both will leave their mark.

Taylor's seriousness about education is the novel's signal that [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] is no longer a transient [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] camp but a community with permanence in mind:

> "Bear Creek," he said, "don't want the experience they had over at Calef. We must not hire an ignoramus."
> （005-005）

His insistence on quality — "Nor we don't want no gad-a-way flirt" (005-007) — leads directly to [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s arrival, making Taylor the unwitting architect of the novel's central romance.

## Key Relationships

### The Virginian

Taylor's trailside conversation with [[the-virginian]] in Chapter IV establishes the thematic counterpoint between the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]'s world and the settler's. Taylor tells the Virginian about the schoolhouse; the Virginian tells Taylor about [[uncle-hughey|Uncle Hughey]]'s latest marriage. Both men laugh, but they are laughing from different worlds:

> "Get married yourself," suggested Mr. Taylor.
> "Me! I ain't near reached the marriageable age." (004-099–100)

Taylor's suggestion is prophetic, though neither man knows it yet.

### Molly Wood

Taylor does not interact extensively with [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] after her arrival, but his correspondence and the committee's decision to hire her are the proximate cause of her journey west. His wife [[mrs-taylor|Mrs. Taylor]] becomes Molly's closest neighbor and confidante, with the Taylors building Molly's cabin next to their own (005-038).

### Mrs. Taylor

Taylor's marriage to [[mrs-taylor|Mrs. Taylor]] represents the settled domestic life that the novel treats as the frontier's desirable endpoint. The couple appears together at key community events, including the Swinton barbecue and the dance, always functioning as a unit — the stable household against which the novel's romantic turbulence plays out.

## Key Scenes

### The Trailside Encounter (Chapter IV)

Taylor's first appearance establishes both his character and his narrative function. Riding up to the Virginian and narrator on the trail, he is described as "a sedate-looking traveller riding upon an equally sober horse" (004-090). His sobriety contrasts with the Virginian's laconic humor, but the two men treat each other with mutual respect — the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] and the settler recognizing each other as legitimate inhabitants of the same contested ground.

### The Schoolhouse Committee (Chapter V)

Taylor's role as the spokesman for [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]]'s educational ambitions occupies the opening of Chapter V. The committee's deliberation — "[[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] ain't going to be hasty about a schoolmarm" (005-001) — is both comic and sincere. Taylor takes the matter "seriously" because, as a family man, "the problem of educating his children could appear to him in no light except a sober one" (005-005). The passage is Wister's acknowledgment that the frontier's future belongs to men like Taylor, not to men like [[trampas|Trampas]].

### The Dance at Swinton's (Chapter X)

Taylor appears among the assembled families at the Swinton barbecue and dance, a communal event that marks [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s full entry into [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] society. His presence, alongside his wife and children, signals the stable domestic world that forms the backdrop for the Virginian's courtship.

## Themes & Analysis

### The Civilizing Impulse

Mr. Taylor embodies what the historian Frederick Jackson Turner called the "civilizing" phase of frontier settlement. He follows the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s, the prospectors, and the trail-drivers, bringing with him children, schoolhouses, and the expectation of permanence. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] himself will eventually join this world — becoming a partner in a ranch, marrying, and settling — but Taylor is already there, and his presence helps define the future toward which the novel moves.

### Seriousness as Virtue

Taylor is not a witty or charismatic figure, but Wister treats his earnestness with respect. In a novel where charm often masks danger ([[trampas|Trampas]]) and silence masks depth (the Virginian), Taylor's transparent seriousness is a different kind of honesty — the honesty of a man who knows exactly what he wants and works steadily toward it. "[[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] don't want the experience they had over at Calef" is not a joke, and Taylor does not deliver it as one.
