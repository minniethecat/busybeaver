# Dr. MacBride

**The Reverend Dr. Alexander MacBride** is a Presbyterian missionary whose visit to [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]] in Chapter XIX provides the novel with both comic relief and theological critique. Pompous, humorless, and armed with a "copious sheaf" of sermons, MacBride is the antithesis of the frontier virtues the novel celebrates — and his presence serves, by contrast, to illuminate the genuine spirituality of [[the-virginian|the Virginian]] and the wisdom of [[bishop-of-wyoming|the Bishop]] of [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]]. Chapter XIX is titled "Dr. Macbride Begs Pardon," a phrase that encapsulates the man: he apologizes with a manner that "rendered forgiveness well-nigh impossible" (019-042).

## Role in the Novel

Dr. MacBride functions as a theological foil — a representative of the kind of religion the novel rejects in favor of the more humane, practical faith embodied by [[bishop-of-wyoming|the Bishop]] of [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]]. His sermon to the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s is the novel's most sustained critique of dogmatic Christianity:

> Stone upon stone he built the black cellar of his theology, leaving out its beautiful park and the sunshine of its garden. ... wrath he spoke of, and never once of love.
> （020-012）

The narrator's observation that "it was the bishop's way, I knew well, to hold [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s by homely talk of their special hardships and temptations" directly contrasts MacBride's approach with the pastoral wisdom of the territory's bishop. MacBride preaches damnation to men who need compassion; the bishop preaches forgiveness to men who need hope.

Structurally, MacBride's visit serves a crucial plot function: his presence fills the foreman's house, forcing the Virginian to seek other quarters — which leads to the nighttime scene at [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]]'s office where the Virginian's confrontation with [[trampas|Trampas]] becomes inevitable. The Judge's cleverness in housing MacBride separately is revealed when the narrator realizes:

> I saw why the foreman's house had been empty to receive Dr. MacBride and me. And I saw that the Judge had been very clever indeed.
> （020-017）

## Key Relationships

### Judge Henry

[[judge-henry|Judge Henry]] hosts MacBride with characteristic diplomacy, managing the minister's difficult personality while quietly arranging matters to serve his own purposes. The Judge's dry humor — "That is where I've landed him!" — reveals his strategic mind even in matters of hospitality.

### The Virginian

MacBride and [[the-virginian|the Virginian]] occupy opposite poles of the novel's spiritual spectrum. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]], who has told [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] that he believes in a God who is "satisfied" if a man does his best (018-035), represents an unlettered but genuine faith. MacBride represents theological sophistication without spiritual warmth. They never directly debate, but the novel leaves no doubt which man's religion it prefers.

### Molly Wood

[[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s assessment of MacBride is characteristically sharp. At dinner, she observes: "Do you know, when I first heard him I thought his voice was hearty. But if you listen, you'll find it's merely militant. He never really meets you with it. He's off on his hill watching the battlefield the whole time" (019-025). It is one of the novel's finest pieces of character analysis, delivered by a woman who reads people as carefully as she reads books.

### The Bishop of Wyoming

Though [[bishop-of-wyoming|the Bishop]] does not appear in MacBride's chapters, he is invoked by the narrator as the standard against which MacBride fails. The bishop "held [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s by homely talk"; MacBride lectures them on predestination. The contrast is explicit and devastating.

### The Narrator

The narrator shares a room with MacBride in the foreman's house and serves as the reader's witness to the minister's personality. His observations — wry, generous, but ultimately damning — shape the reader's judgment of MacBride more than any direct argument could.

## Key Scenes

### The Dinner at Sunk Creek (Chapter XIX)

MacBride's first evening at the Henrys' dinner table establishes his character through a series of small, telling moments. His "heavy ha-ha's" produce "a dreadfully cavernous effect" (019-025). When [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]] tells a story, MacBride "laid his 'ha-ha,' like a heavy stone, upon the gaiety" (019-027). His response to the narrator's tale of frontier justice — "Pardon me. I cannot accept such a view, sir" (019-045) — reveals a man incapable of understanding the moral complexity of the world he has entered.

### The Sermon to the Cowboys (Chapter XX)

MacBride's Sunday sermon is the novel's most direct theological statement. Preaching to [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s about predestination — telling men who live by their wits and their courage that their actions are irrelevant to their salvation — MacBride demonstrates a catastrophic failure of pastoral imagination. The narrator's lengthy critique of the sermon (020-012) is one of the novel's most explicitly philosophical passages.

> Having told them this, he invited them to glorify the Creator of the scheme. Even if damned, they must praise the person who had made them expressly for damnation.
> （020-012）

## Themes & Analysis

### Dogma vs. Pastoral Care

MacBride represents the failure of dogma disconnected from human reality. His theology is internally consistent but pastorally bankrupt — it answers questions the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s are not asking and ignores the ones they are. The novel's preference for the bishop's approach — practical, forgiving, attentive to "special hardships and temptations" — is unambiguous.

### The "Middlin' Man of God"

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s earlier remark about preachers — "keep me from a middlin' man of God" (018-035) — is recalled by MacBride's visit. MacBride is not evil; he is middling. He means well, works hard, and entirely fails to reach the people he has come to save. The novel's judgment is not that MacBride is a hypocrite but that he is inadequate — and inadequacy in a man of God, the Virginian suggests, is worse than inadequacy in a doctor or a lawyer, because souls are at stake.
