## Location

Medicine Bow is a small railroad town in south-central Wyoming Territory, situated along the Union Pacific line. When the narrator arrives, it consists of just twenty-nine buildings: "one coal shute, one water tank, the station, one store, two eating-houses, one billiard hall, two tool-houses, one feed stable, and twelve others that for one reason and another I shall not name" (002-024). The town lies on the edge of the great cattle country, serving as both a transportation hub and a gathering place for [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s, drummers, and travelers passing between East and West.

## Role in the Novel

Medicine Bow functions as the novel's threshold — the liminal space where Eastern civilization meets Western reality. It is here that the narrator first encounters the Virginian, witnesses the famous "smile" confrontation with [[trampas|Trampas]], and receives his initiation into the codes and customs of the cattle country. The town itself is neither fully civilized nor fully wild; its saloon hosts both orderly beer-drinking and deadly poker-table standoffs, its hotel cannot guarantee a bed, and its eating-house lady flirts with departing [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s.

Wister uses Medicine Bow to establish the novel's central contrasts: the Eastern narrator's discomfort and fascination, the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]'s natural mastery of this environment, and the ever-present possibility that ordinary social gatherings can turn violent. As the narrator observes, "So far my hours at Medicine Bow had seemed to glide beneath a sunshine of merriment, of easygoing jocularity. This was suddenly gone, like the wind changing to north in the middle of a warm day" (002-154).

## Key Scenes

### The Narrator's Arrival

The narrator arrives at Medicine Bow to find his baggage lost and himself stranded: "I stood deserted among crates and boxes, blankly holding my check, hungry and forlorn" (001-004). His irritation at "what a forsaken hole this is" is interrupted by the Virginian's drawling conversation with [[uncle-hughey|Uncle Hughey]] on the platform — an exchange that immediately establishes the novel's distinctive voice and the Virginian's irresistible charm.

### The Smile Confrontation

Medicine Bow's saloon is the setting for the novel's defining moment: the Virginian's poker-table confrontation with [[trampas|Trampas]]. When [[trampas|Trampas]] calls him a "son-of-a------," the Virginian lays his pistol on the table and quietly insists, "When you call me that, smile!" (002-176). The scene transforms the town from mere backdrop into an arena where the code of the West is publicly enacted and enforced.

### The Baby-Swap Prank

Later, Medicine Bow becomes the staging ground for the Virginian's most elaborate prank: swapping babies at a dance, which "turned Medicine Bow upside down" (025-013). This comic episode reveals the town's dual nature — a place of both deadly seriousness and anarchic humor.

### Uncle Hughey's Wedding

The town serves as a recurring site for departures and arrivals, most memorably [[uncle-hughey|Uncle Hughey]]'s repeated matrimonial expeditions: "The word had come over the wires from Laramie: '[[uncle-hughey|Uncle Hughey]] has made it this time. Expect him on today's number two.' And Medicine Bow had expected him" (004-024).

## Real-World Context

Medicine Bow is a real town in Carbon County, Wyoming, founded in the 1860s as a watering stop on the Union Pacific Railroad. The town today remains small (population under 300) and celebrates its literary heritage through the Virginian Hotel and various historical markers. Wister visited Medicine Bow during his Wyoming travels in the 1880s and 1890s, and his fictional portrayal — while dramatically heightened — reflects the actual character of [[the-railroad|the railroad]]-town frontier. The name derives from the Native American practice of gathering mountain mahogany wood for bow-making, which gave the nearby Medicine Bow River and mountains their names.
