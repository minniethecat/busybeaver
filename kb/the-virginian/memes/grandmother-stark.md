# Grandmother Stark

**Grandmother Stark** is the ancestress of [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Stark Wood]] in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. Though she never appears as a living character, her presence is felt throughout the novel through a colonial miniature portrait that Molly hangs on the wall of her cabin at [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]], and through the inherited spirit of pride and independence that she represents. She serves as a silent moral anchor for Molly amid the disorienting world of the [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] frontier.

## The Miniature Portrait

Molly brings the miniature of Grandmother Stark with her from [[bennington|Bennington, Vermont]], along with "a little money, a few clothes," and "a colonial miniature" ([[TOC|P02]]-608). It is one of her few possessions and clearly among the most treasured. The portrait hangs prominently on the wall of Molly's cabin at [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]], where it becomes a recurring visual motif in the novel's domestic scenes.

The portrait is described in the novel's closing pages as showing a figure who is "rosy, blue, and flaxen, not quite familiar, not quite smiling" (027-255). This description is given at the moment when the Virginian notices the resemblance between Molly and her ancestress:

> He now noticed the miniature of Grandmother Stark. "You are awful like her," he whispered. "You're cert'nly awful like her. May I kiss you too, ma'am?"
> （027-260）

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s recognition of the physical resemblance—and his courtly request to kiss the portrait as he has just kissed Molly—links past and present, East and West, in one of the novel's most tender moments.

## Symbol of Independence

Grandmother Stark represents an inherited spirit of female independence that Molly carries into the frontier. When Molly first travels west, the narrator explicitly names Grandmother Stark as the force animating her courage:

> If the ancestors that we carry shut up inside us take turns in dictating to us our actions and our state of mind, undoubtedly Grandmother Stark was empress of Molly's spirit upon this Monday. (P02-608)

This same spirit surfaces when Molly faces the advances of the stage driver who proposes to her during the journey: "Independence and Grandmother Stark shining in her eye" ([[TOC|P02]]-614). When the Virginian makes a suggestive remark early in their acquaintance, "Grandmother Stark flashed awake deep within the spirit of her descendant, and she made a haughty declaration of war" ([[TOC|P02]]-809).

## The Dedicated Chapter

Chapter XXVII bears the title "Grandmother Stark," making explicit the thematic importance of this ancestress. In this chapter, Molly has a confrontation with [[mrs-taylor|Mrs. Taylor]] about her relationship with the Virginian—a confrontation that drives Molly to retreat to her cabin and seek comfort from the portrait:

> There, on her wall still, was the miniature, the little silent ancestress; and upon this face the girl's glance rested. It was as if she appealed to Grandmother Stark for support and comfort across the hundred years which lay between them.
> （027-091）

This moment of silent communion between Molly and her ancestress across a century underscores the novel's broader themes: the continuity of values between East and West, the persistence of family pride in a raw new world, and the way inherited standards both constrain and sustain the individual.

## Thematic Significance

### Social Class and Pride

Grandmother Stark embodies the Stark family's claim to gentility—a claim that the gossips of [[hoosic-falls|Hoosic Falls]] attempt to undermine. [[mrs-flynt|Mrs. Flynt]], the Baptist minister's wife, remarks that "I dare say he had a great-grandmother quite as good as hers" ([[TOC|P02]]-577), referring to [[sam-bannett|Sam Bannett]]'s ancestry. This chain of gossip—from Grandmother Stark's status to Molly's refusal to marry [[sam-bannett|Sam Bannett]]—runs through the Eastern sections of the novel, establishing the social world that Molly leaves behind.

### East and West

The miniature of Grandmother Stark is a piece of the East that Molly transplants to the West. It hangs on her cabin wall as a reminder of where she comes from and what she stands for, even as her new life reshapes her. In the novel's final domestic scene, the portrait presides silently over the union of Molly and the Virginian, suggesting that the Eastern values Grandmother Stark represents have not been discarded but integrated into a new, Western synthesis:

> So they remained long, the fair head nesting in the great arms, and the black head laid against it, while over the silent room presided the little Grandmother Stark in her frame, rosy, blue, and flaxen, not quite familiar, not quite smiling.
> （027-255）

### Ancestral Legacy

The narrator's theory of ancestral influence—that "the ancestors that we carry shut up inside us take turns in dictating to us our actions and our state of mind"—is one of the novel's most explicit statements about character formation. Grandmother Stark is the mechanism by which Wister explains Molly's otherwise surprising combination of gentility and grit: she is a lady because her ancestress was a lady, and she is courageous because her ancestress was courageous.
