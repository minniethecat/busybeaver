# Bennington, Vermont

**Bennington, Vermont** is the Eastern hometown of [[molly-stark-wood]], serving throughout *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]* as the symbolic counterpoint to [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]]'s wild frontier. Where [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek]] and [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]] represent the raw, untamed West, Bennington embodies the settled, civilized East — the world of drawing rooms, social hierarchies, and "people who wondered how [[molly-stark-wood|Miss Wood]] could go about from house to house teaching the piano, and she a lady" (005-023). Molly's journey from Bennington to [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] is the novel's central geographic metaphor for its heroine's transformation from proper Vermont schoolteacher to the wife of a [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] cattleman.

## Role in the Novel

### The Eastern Counterpoint

Bennington functions as the novel's embodied argument for what the West is *not*. Its social world is defined by inherited status, rigid propriety, and the quiet desperation of people who have never tested themselves against anything more dangerous than social disapproval. The town's representative figure — [[sam-bannett|Sam Bannett]], the wealthy suitor "good enough for anybody who did fancy embroidery at five cents a letter" (005-023) — is everything the Virginian is not: safe, predictable, and fundamentally unworthy of Molly's adventurous spirit.

### Molly's Origin and Destination

Bennington shapes Molly even as she leaves it. Her pride in her Stark ancestry — she "traced direct descent from the historic lady whose name she bore, that Molly Stark who was not a widow after the battle" (005-021) — is a Bennington inheritance that she carries into the West. The miniature portrait of the original Molly Stark, her "most precious possession," connects her to a lineage of strong women who chose brave men. In this sense, Bennington gives Molly the very qualities that enable her to thrive at [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]].

### The Return

Molly's return visit to Bennington in Chapter XXXIV is the novel's most sustained examination of the gap between East and West. "Bennington opened its arms to its venturesome daughter" (034-015), but the embrace is complicated. Molly has been changed by the West; she no longer fits comfortably into the world she left. The great-aunt at [[dunbarton|Dunbarton]] perceives this change immediately: "You have changed very much" (034-018). Bennington, in Molly's absence, has become a place to visit rather than a place to live — a museum of her former self.

## Key Scenes

### The Departure (Chapter XI)

Molly's train journey from Bennington to the West is the novel's most lyrical passage of physical transition. As she travels, the familiar landscapes of New England give way to the increasingly alien terrain of the frontier — "trees were extraordinarily scarce" (011-012) — until, stepping off [[the-railroad|the train]] at Rock Creek, "she had really attained the unknown" (011-012). The departure from Bennington is not merely geographical but existential: Molly is leaving behind the only world she has ever known.

### Letters from Bear Creek

Throughout the novel, Molly's letters home to Bennington function as a narrative device for measuring her transformation. The letters grow longer, more vivid, more self-assured as Molly grows into her Western life. The Bennington recipients — [[mrs-wood|Mrs. Wood]], the Flynts, [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]] — receive these dispatches from another world with a mixture of fascination and unease. "I hate her to be where such things can happen," [[mrs-wood|Mrs. Wood]] declares upon hearing of the swapped-baby incident (018-012), a reaction that captures Bennington's fundamental incomprehension of frontier life.

### The Return Visit (Chapter XXXIV)

Molly's visit home is occasioned by her crisis of faith in the Virginian — her discovery that he has participated in a hanging. She flees West for East, civilization for safety, only to discover that the East cannot shelter her from her own heart. The great-aunt's counsel — "When you cannot help it a moment longer, then listen to nothing but that" (034-028) — authorizes Molly to choose love over propriety. Bennington, ironically, is where Molly decides to return to [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]].

## Themes & Analysis

### East vs. West

Bennington embodies everything Wister's novel critiques about Eastern society: its obsession with class, its horror of violence, its preference for comfort over authenticity. But Wister is too nuanced a writer to make the critique simple. Bennington also produces [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] — a woman of courage, intelligence, and independence who proves herself the Virginian's equal. The town that nurtured her cannot be entirely dismissed.

### The Meaning of Home

Bennington is the first of several "homes" Molly inhabits in the novel — preceding [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] and, ultimately, the ranch she will share with the Virginian. Each home demands something different of her: Bennington demands propriety, [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] demands resilience, and the ranch demands love. Molly's capacity to belong to all three, to carry Bennington's values into [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] without being constrained by Bennington's limitations, is the measure of her character.
