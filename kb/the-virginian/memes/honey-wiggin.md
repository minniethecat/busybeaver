# Honey Wiggin

**Honey Wiggin** is a [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] employed at the [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]] in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. He is characterized by his voluble, teasing wit and serves as an unofficial social commentator and comedian among the cowpunchers, frequently needling his fellow [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s at gatherings and providing comic relief through his running commentary on the romantic pursuits directed at the new schoolmarm, [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]].

## Identity and Role

Honey Wiggin is introduced among the cowpunchers at [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]]'s [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]], listed alongside [[nebrasky|Nebrasky]], Dollar Bill, and Chalkeye—men who "bore names of various denominations" and "came from farms and cities, from Maine and from California" [006-005]. While the Virginian represents the disciplined, capable ideal of the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]], Wiggin embodies its irrepressible social energy—always ready with a quip, a piece of gossip, or a teasing remark directed at whichever cowpuncher happens to be in his sights.

His nickname "Honey" suggests an ironic sweetness that belies his sharp tongue. He is described as "agile" in his verbal attacks, "stooping to fresh prey" as he moves from one target to the next [010-016].

## Social Role at Sunk Creek

### Gathering at the Bunkhouse

The narrator first encounters Wiggin indirectly, overhearing the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s' laughter from the bunkhouse after his own awkward attempts at ranch work. One evening, passing by the bunkhouse after dark, the narrator finds "Honey Wiggin and the rest of the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s were gathered inside it" [006-009], suggesting that Wiggin was a central figure in the bunkhouse's social life.

### The Goose Egg Dance

Wiggin's most extended appearance comes at the Goose Egg dance in Chapter X. As the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s gather at the Taylors' for a social occasion that revolves around [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s presence, Wiggin positions himself as master of ceremonies and unofficial commentator:

> "Well, boys," said Wiggin, "I expect it will be Miss Schoolmarm says who's number one tonight." [010-008]

He proceeds to work through the assembled cowpunchers one by one, publicly humiliating each with comic stories of their failed romantic overtures toward [[molly-stark-wood|the schoolmarm]]. He teases [[nebrasky|Nebrasky]] about offering a caged canary as a gift, recites the tale of [[bokay-baldy|Bokay Baldy]]'s ill-fated knitted slippers with "pink cabbages on 'em" [010-016], and catalogs the long list of suitors who have "got their tumble"—from a stage-driver to a horse thief with a secret wife [010-022].

When the Virginian arrives and delivers a stern rebuke to the men for their behavior, calling them out for having "hit town too frequent for any of us to play Sunday on the balance of the gang" [010-037], Wiggin becomes the first to accept the criticism:

> "Shut your rank mouth," said Wiggin to him, amiably. "I don't care whether he knows her or if he done it on principle. I'll accept the roundin' up he gave us—and say! You'll swallo' your dose, too! Us boys'll stand in with him in this." [010-039]

This moment reveals Wiggin's underlying decency beneath the teasing exterior: he recognizes the justice of the Virginian's rebuke and rallies the other [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s to accept it.

### Views on Women

Wiggin confesses a certain discomfort around "good women," telling the assembled [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s:

> "I've always kind o' kep' away from them good women. Don't seem to think of anything to chat about to 'em. The only folks I'd say she encourages is the school kids. She kisses them." [010-026]

This admission, delivered in his characteristic drawl, reveals a shyness that his constant talking otherwise conceals. It also distinguishes him sharply from the Virginian, whose pursuit of [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] will define the novel's romantic arc.

## The Wedding

Wiggin reappears in the novel's climactic wedding chapter (XXXV) as one of three friends—alongside [[scipio-le-moyne|Scipio Le Moyne]] and [[lin-mclean|Lin McLean]]—who intercept the Virginian and his bride as they arrive in town. The three men are eager to drink the Virginian's health but also, as the narrator notes, "behind their gaiety the Virginian read some other purpose" [035-105].

When the Virginian prepares to face [[trampas|Trampas]] in the final confrontation, Wiggin and the others offer to handle the matter themselves:

> Honey Wiggin now made the bridegroom a straight offer. "We'll take this thing off your hands," said he. [035-140]

Their offer is a mark of loyalty and friendship: they understand what the Virginian must do and would spare him the burden if they could. Later, as the Virginian walks toward the street to meet [[trampas|Trampas]], he sees "Wiggin, walking with McLean and [[scipio-le-moyne|Scipio]]. They were watching the town to see that his friends made no foul play" [035-298].

> "We're giving you a clear field," said Wiggin. [035-299]

This final act—ensuring a fair fight by watching [[trampas|Trampas]]'s allies—demonstrates the [[the-code-of-the-west|cowboy code]] of [[frontier-honor|honor]] in action and Wiggin's role as a loyal member of the Sunk Creek community.

## Character Significance

Honey Wiggin functions in the novel as a representative of the ordinary [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]: neither a hero like the Virginian nor a villain like [[trampas|Trampas]], but a man of the bunkhouse—gregarious, occasionally crude, yet fundamentally decent. His teasing humor provides much of the novel's comic relief, particularly in the Goose Egg dance chapter, while his acceptance of the Virginian's moral authority at that same gathering foreshadows the community's broader recognition of the Virginian's exceptional character.

In the wedding chapter, Wiggin's offer to confront [[trampas|Trampas]] and his subsequent role in ensuring a fair fight place him among the Virginian's trusted circle. He is, in the novel's moral scheme, one of the good men—not grand or heroic, but reliable when it matters.
