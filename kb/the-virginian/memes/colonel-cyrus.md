# Colonel Cyrus Jones

**Colonel Cyrus Jones** is the proprietor of an eating palace in Omaha, Nebraska, where the narrator unexpectedly encounters the Virginian during the latter's journey east as deputy foreman on a cattle shipment. A Confederate veteran with "gray flowery mustaches," Colonel Cyrus is one of the novel's most memorable minor characters — a figure whose theatrical presence and frontier vocabulary evoke an earlier era of the American West.

## Role in the Novel

Colonel Cyrus Jones appears in a single extended scene — the narrator's morning stop at his Omaha eating palace — yet his presence is so vivid that he is remembered and discussed by the characters long after his death. He serves multiple narrative functions: as comic relief during the narrator's cross-country journey, as a representative of the older, more colorful frontier that was already vanishing, and as a marker of the passage of time and the professionalization of the West.

The eating palace itself is a frontier institution — part restaurant, part theater, part relic of a world where a Confederate uniform could still be worn as a badge of identity rather than a costume. Colonel Cyrus shouts orders to his cook through a hole, enforces his ticket policy with military precision, and serves as a one-man embodiment of a West that [[the-railroad|the railroad]] was already rendering obsolete.

## Key Scenes

### The Morning at the Eating Palace

The narrator, traveling from a sleeping-car toward a bath, passes by Colonel Cyrus Jones's establishment and is stopped by the sound of "the language of Colonel Cyrus Jones":

> "Too many brain workers breakfasting before yu' came in, professor," he shouted. "Missionary ate the last leg off me just now. Brown the wheat!"

This encounter brings the narrator face to face with a kind of speech and a kind of character he has not encountered since leaving [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] — the Colonel's vocabulary "met me like the breeze of the plains." Inside, he finds the Virginian seated at a table alone, and the two share breakfast in this unlikely setting.

### The Telegraph Pole

Colonel Cyrus Jones's fate is revealed later by [[scipio-le-moyne|Scipio Le Moyne]], who briefly worked impersonating the Colonel at the eating palace after the original's death: "He met a Cheyenne telegraph pole in seventy-four, and was buried. But his palace was doin' big business, and he had been a kind of attraction, and so they always keep a live bear outside, and some poor fello', fixed up like the Colonel used to be, inside" (015-???).

This macabre detail — a man killed by a telegraph pole, the very symbol of the civilization that was replacing his world — is one of the novel's most darkly ironic moments. The eating palace continues without him, but the "poor fello'" who impersonates him can never truly replace the original. The Colonel has become a brand, a performance, a ghost haunting the institution he created.

## Significance

Colonel Cyrus Jones is a representative of a particular type in Western literature: the larger-than-life frontier character whose personal eccentricity cannot be replicated by the standardized institutions that follow. His death by telegraph pole is almost symbolic — the communications technology of the new era literally striking down a representative of the old one. When [[scipio-le-moyne|Scipio]] describes his brief tenure as the Colonel's impersonator as "a turruble mean position," he is acknowledging that authentic frontier character cannot be performed; it can only be remembered.
