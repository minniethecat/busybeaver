# Great-Aunt at Dunbarton

The **great-aunt at [[dunbarton|Dunbarton]]** is [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Stark Wood]]'s elderly relative in [[dunbarton|Dunbarton, New Hampshire]], and the sole member of Molly's Eastern family who perceives the Virginian's worth. In a novel structured around the clash between Eastern propriety and Western character, the great-aunt is a crucial figure: she bridges the divide, applying old-fashioned wisdom rather than new-fashioned snobbery to the question of whom her grand-niece should marry.

## Role in the Novel

The great-aunt provides the moral counterweight to [[mrs-wood|Mrs. Wood]] and Sarah Bell. Where [[mrs-wood|Molly's mother]] and sister react to the engagement with horror, the old lady at Dunbarton reads the Virginian's letter with intellectual curiosity and open-mindedness. Her verdict — conveyed in the single observation that the man "appears to have written to me himself" — signals a different mode of judgment, one based on direct engagement with the evidence rather than prejudgment based on social categories.

The novel positions her as the true inheritor of the Stark family spirit. She is the keeper of the miniature portrait of the original Molly Stark, and her connection to the family's Revolutionary-era ancestor gives her judgments a weight that [[mrs-wood|Mrs. Wood]]'s conventional anxieties cannot match. When she declares, after looking at the Virginian's photograph in frontier dress, that "the Starks had always preferred a man who was a man," she is aligning the Virginian with a long tradition of masculine competence that predates and transcends the parlor proprieties of late-nineteenth-century Vermont.

## Key Relationships

### Molly Stark Wood

Molly visits the great-aunt each summer in [[dunbarton|Dunbarton]]. Their bond deepens when Molly reaches twenty and faces family pressure to marry [[sam-bannett|Sam Bannett]]: "It was at this time also that her face grew a little paler... It was at this time, too, that she grew very intimate with that great-aunt over at [[dunbarton|Dunbarton]], and from her received much comfort and strengthening" (008-???). The great-aunt is the only family member who treats Molly's independence as a virtue rather than a problem.

### The Virginian

Though the great-aunt and the Virginian never meet, her reading of his letter constitutes the novel's most important offstage act of judgment. She alone among the Eastern relations takes him on his own terms, reading his words without filtering them through social prejudice. Her approval, when it comes, carries the authority of the Stark lineage — if the keeper of the family's history accepts the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]], then the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] must be worthy.

### Mrs. Wood

The contrast between the great-aunt and [[mrs-wood|Mrs. Wood]] is deliberate and sharp. Both are elderly women, both love Molly, but one panics while the other reflects. The great-aunt's letters to Molly are "balm among the harsh stings" she receives from [[bennington|Bennington]], a reminder that family can be a source of support rather than judgment.

## Key Scenes

### Looking at the Photograph

During one of Molly's visits to [[dunbarton|Dunbarton]], the great-aunt sees the Virginian's photograph in frontier dress. Her reaction — a dry observation about whether "there are days when he does not kill people" — is characteristically sharp but not condemnatory. When Molly protests that "He never killed anybody!" the great-aunt lets the matter rest, reserving judgment for more complete evidence.

### Reading the Virginian's Letter

> "The man appears to have written to me himself."

This line, the great-aunt's response to receiving the Virginian's letter, is one of the novel's most economically powerful moments. In eight words, she acknowledges the Virginian's agency, his literacy, and his directness — all qualities that [[mrs-wood|Mrs. Wood]]'s hysteria had obscured.

### The Final Verdict

Looking at the Virginian after the wedding, the great-aunt is "once more able to declare that the Starks had always preferred a man who was a man" (034-011). The verdict is simultaneously personal and ancestral: in blessing Molly's choice, the old lady redeems the promise of the Stark name.

## Themes

The great-aunt embodies the novel's argument that wisdom is not distributed according to geography or sophistication. She lives in a small New Hampshire town, far from the centers of fashion, yet she sees more clearly than anyone in [[bennington|Bennington]]. Her approval of the Virginian represents the possibility of reconciliation between East and West — not on the East's terms, but on terms of mutual recognition and respect.
