# General Stark

**General Stark** is the late father of [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Stark Wood]] in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. A Revolutionary War general descended from the New Hampshire Stark family, he appears only in memory and family lore, but his military legacy and the pride of his lineage profoundly shape Molly's character, her sense of identity, and her family's expectations.

## Family and Descent

General Stark's daughter [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] could, the narrator notes, have belonged to "any number of those patriotic societies" had she wished—"the Boston Tea Party, the Ethan Allen Ticonderogas, the Green Mountain Daughters, the Saratoga Sacred Circle, the Confederated Colonial Chatelaines" [008-005]. Her descent from General Stark is the first of two reasons given for why "Miss Mary Stark Wood was not a usual young lady" [008-004].

This lineage carries weight at [[bennington|Bennington, Vermont]], where the Stark family maintains its social standing. [[mrs-wood|Molly's mother]] frequently invokes the General's legacy, telling her daughter: "My dear, you're getting more like the General's wife every year you live" [008-006]—a comparison that Molly deflects with humor about the family nose. The narrator notes that "the Starks have always been punctual" [008-010], a small detail that suggests the military discipline of the General's household has become family tradition.

Molly herself consults two objects before dressing for tea: "the miniature of General Stark" and a photograph of the Virginian [008-011]. The juxtaposition is significant: the General represents her heritage and duty; the Virginian represents her future and desire. The tension between these two loyalties drives much of the novel's romantic plot.

## The General's Legacy

General Stark's military service—carrying "knives and pistols owing to the necessities of his career" [029-082]—is invoked by [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]] at [[dunbarton|Dunbarton]] in defense of the Virginian. When [[mrs-wood|Mrs. Wood]] expresses alarm at her daughter's engagement to a Western cowpuncher, [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]] reminds her that the General himself had been no stranger to weapons and frontier violence, suggesting that the Stark family's own history contains more roughness than its [[bennington|Bennington]] gentility might acknowledge.

The portrait of General Stark hangs at [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]]'s home in [[dunbarton|Dunbarton]], where it serves as a silent arbiter of the family's judgment on new arrivals. At the visit described in Chapter XXXVI, [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]] shows the Virginian the family portrait:

> "There he is," she said, showing the family portrait. "And a rough time he must have had of it now and then. New Hampshire was full of fine young men in those days. But nowadays most of them have gone away to seek their fortunes in the West. Do they find them, I wonder?" [036-076]

The great-aunt's commentary draws a parallel between the General's generation—men who fought and built in the East—and the Virginian's generation, who seek their fortunes in the West. The implied question is whether the West still produces men of the General's caliber.

## Character Significance

General Stark, though deceased before the novel's action begins, exerts a powerful gravitational pull on the narrative. His legacy represents:

1. **The weight of Eastern tradition**: Molly's pride in her descent is genuine, and her family's invocation of the General's name serves as a constant reminder of the social expectations she must navigate.

2. **A bridge between East and West**: The great-aunt's comparison of the General's rough frontier experience to the Virginian's life in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] suggests that the virtues of courage and capability transcend geography and generation.

3. **The patriarchal standard**: [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] is implicitly measured against the General throughout the courtship narrative. Molly's choice of the Virginian must [[frontier-honor|honor]], not dis[[frontier-honor|honor]], the Stark name—a burden that the Virginian understands and accepts.

The General's miniature, which Molly consults alongside the Virginian's photograph, symbolizes the reconciliation she must achieve between her inherited identity and her chosen life.
