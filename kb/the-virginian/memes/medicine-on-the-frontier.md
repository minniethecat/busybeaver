# Medicine on the Frontier

**Medicine on the frontier** appears in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]* as one of the markers of the distance between [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] Territory and the settled East. Doctors are few and far between; folk knowledge and self-reliance fill the gaps; and the precariousness of frontier life is nowhere more evident than in the absence of reliable medical care.

## Dr. Barker at Drybone

The only named physician in the novel is Dr. Barker, who practices at [[drybone|Drybone]]. His name appears in connection with Mrs. Dow, "upon whom Dr. Barker at [[drybone|Drybone]] had enjoined total abstinence from all excitement" (018-781). The prescription — complete avoidance of excitement — is both medically vague and practically impossible on the frontier, where excitement comes in the form of baby-swapping pranks, cattle stampedes, and the ordinary hazards of Western life.

Dr. Barker's location at [[drybone|Drybone]], a rough settlement known for its hog-ranch and poker games, suggests the marginal status of professional medicine in the territory. A doctor serves where he can, not where he might choose, and his patients include women prone to "fits" — a catch-all diagnosis that reflects the era's limited medical vocabulary.

## Folk Medicine and Self-Reliance

The frontier's medical culture is largely one of self-reliance. When [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s are injured, they rely on rest, whiskey, and the body's own healing powers. The novel's many violent episodes — fistfights, gunshot wounds, riding accidents — are treated without recourse to doctors. The implication is that on the frontier, one either recovers or one does not; professional medicine is a luxury of the settled East.

## Medicine as Civilization

The absence of reliable medical care is one of the ways Wister marks the frontier as incomplete. [[sunk-creek-ranch|Judge Henry's ranch]], the most civilized outpost in the novel, still has no resident doctor. When [[mrs-taylor|Mrs. Taylor]] worries about [[molly-stark-wood|the schoolmarm]]'s health, she can offer only folk wisdom and neighborly concern. The frontier's lack of doctors, like its lack of churches and schools, is a sign of its immaturity — a condition that the novel's narrative of settlement and domestication is meant to remedy.
