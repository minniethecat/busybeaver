# Bokay Baldy

**Bokay Baldy** is a [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] at the [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]] in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. His principal claim to notoriety among the cowpunchers is a legendary romantic misadventure: an attempt to impress the new schoolmarm [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] with a pair of hand-knitted slippers—an episode that [[honey-wiggin|Honey Wiggin]] recounts with relish at the Goose Egg dance.

## Identity

Bokay Baldy is introduced as one of the cowpunchers at [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]]'s [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]], part of the community of young men drawn West by the "romance of American adventure" [006-005]. His nickname "Bokay" may derive from "bouquet," an ironic moniker for a rough [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]], while "Baldy" likely refers to his appearance.

His limited presence in the narrative centers entirely on a single comic anecdote that has become bunkhouse legend.

## The Slipper Episode

At the Goose Egg dance in Chapter X, [[honey-wiggin|Honey Wiggin]] singles out Baldy as his next target, launching into the well-known story of Baldy's failed courtship gift:

> "Why, if here ain't Bokay Baldy!" cried the agile Wiggin, stooping to fresh prey. "Found them slippers yet, Baldy? Tell yu' boys, that was turruble sad luck Baldy had. Did yu' hear about that? Baldy, yu' know, he can stay on a tame horse most as well as the schoolmarm. But just you give him a pair of young knittin'-needles and see him make 'em sweat! He worked an elegant pair of slippers with pink cabbages on 'em for Miss Wood." [010-016]

The story, as [[honey-wiggin|Wiggin]] tells it, continues with Baldy purchasing the slippers at [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]] and then growing anxious on the way to deliver them:

> "Baldy he bought 'em. And on the road to her cabin there at the Taylors' he got thinkin' they might be too big, and he got studyin' what to do." [010-018]

Baldy protests mildly—"[[About]] half that ain't straight" [010-019]—but his defense is characteristically mild and ineffectual against [[honey-wiggin|Wiggin]]'s relentless comic energy. The story of the slippers has become such established bunkhouse lore that even [[trampas|Trampas]] invokes it in a different context, noting that Molly "leaves Baldy sit on the fence while she and [[lin-mclean|Lin McLean]]—" [010-029].

## Lin McLean's Gift

A brief glimpse of Baldy appears in Chapter XI, where [[lin-mclean|Lin McLean]], departing the ranch, leaves a parcel beside the sleeping [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]:

> From the saddlebag, he brought a parcel, and lightly laying this beside Bokay Baldy, he mounted and was gone. When Baldy awoke... [011-020]

This small moment suggests that despite his status as the butt of jokes, Baldy is not without friends among the cowpunchers.

## Character Significance

Bokay Baldy's role in the novel is emblematic: he represents the comic futility of the ordinary [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]'s romantic aspirations toward [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]. Like [[nebrasky|Nebrasky]] with his canary, Baldy with his slippers becomes a cautionary tale circulated in the bunkhouse, reinforcing the social hierarchy that places the educated Eastern schoolmarm beyond the reach of common cowpunchers. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]], by contrast, will succeed where Baldy and all the others fail—a difference that defines the Virginian's exceptional status.

Baldy's mild protest against [[honey-wiggin|Wiggin]]'s embellished version of events—"[[About]] half that ain't straight"—is his one moment of agency in the narrative, and it is characteristically gentle. He lacks the verbal firepower to compete with [[honey-wiggin|Wiggin]] and the social standing to command respect. He endures his role as comic relief with quiet resignation.
