# Andrew Bell

**Andrew Bell** is a minor character in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*, appearing as [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s brother-in-law through his marriage to her sister Sarah. He appears in the novel's Eastern scenes and serves as a sensible, plain-spoken masculine voice in the family discussions about Molly's mysterious life in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]].

## Family Role

Andrew Bell is introduced as the husband of Sarah Wood, Molly's sister—making him part of the extended Wood family in [[bennington|Bennington, Vermont]]. His position as an in-law gives him a certain detachment from the family's anxieties about Molly, allowing him to make observations that the blood relatives might not voice.

When the family receives a letter from Molly describing the baby-swapping incident at [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]], Andrew Bell's response is characteristically direct:

> "I wish I could have been there," said her son-in-law, Andrew Bell.
> （018-872）

This simple statement—wishing he could have witnessed the comic chaos—reveals his appreciation for the humor and vitality of the Western episodes that so trouble the more conventional members of Molly's family. His wife Sarah, by contrast, focuses on what the letter does not say: "She does not mention who played the trick" (018-873).

## Reading Between the Lines

Andrew Bell's most significant contribution to the novel comes during a family discussion after Molly's return visit to [[bennington|Bennington]]. As the family reflects on her changed demeanor, Sarah observes that Molly seems preoccupied:

> "Oh, just somehow that she was thinking."—"Thinking?"—"Well, I believe she has something on her mind."—"You mean a man," said Andrew Bell.—"A man, Andrew?"—"Yes, Mrs. Wood, that's what Sarah always means."
> （022-227）

This exchange reveals several things at once: Andrew Bell's bluntness, his understanding of his wife's conversational indirection, and his willingness to name what the rest of the family dances around. It is Andrew Bell who first articulates what everyone suspects—that Molly has fallen in love with someone in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]].

## The Rustler Rumor

Andrew Bell also serves as the conduit through which the most alarming rumor about Molly reaches the family:

> Somebody said to Andrew Bell that they heard Miss Molly Wood was engaged to marry a *rustler*.
> （022-228）

The word "rustler"—with all its ominous and ambiguous Western connotations—enters the Bell household through Andrew, though the narrator is careful to note that he is merely the messenger: "somebody said to Andrew Bell." The rumor, arriving in this secondhand fashion, precipitates the family's crisis over Molly's Western life.

## Character Significance

Andrew Bell is a functional character, not a deeply developed one. He serves the narrative by providing a plain-spoken, masculine counterpoint to the more anxious feminine voices in Molly's family circle. His willingness to wish he could have seen the [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] baby-swapping, his blunt identification of Molly's romantic preoccupation, and his role as the bearer of the rustler rumor all contribute to moving the Eastern plot forward.

He also represents the ordinary domestic man in Wister's moral landscape—neither heroic like the Virginian nor comic like [[james-westfall|James Westfall]], but sensible, observant, and content in his settled Eastern life. His presence reminds us of the world Molly has left and the conventional expectations she has chosen to defy.
