# Lin McLean

**Lin McLean** is a young [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] who moves through the margins of *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]* before becoming the protagonist of Owen Wister's next novel, *Lin McLean* (1897). In this novel, he appears primarily in the [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]] and [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] chapters, where his presence adds texture to the social world of the [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s and provides a foil to the Virginian's more serious temperament.

## Role in the Novel

Lin McLean first appears in the narrative during the Virginian's account of a bronco-busting expedition to the Yellowstone:

> the expedition ended in an untravelled corner of the Yellowstone Park, near Pitchstone Canyon, where he and young Lin McLean and others were witnesses of a sad and terrible drama that has been elsewhere chronicled.
> （007-006）

This brief mention establishes him as part of the Virginian's circle — a younger member of the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] fraternity who shares in its adventures and its dangers. The "sad and terrible drama" alluded to is presumably material Wister would develop in his subsequent *Lin McLean* stories.

His most significant appearance in *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]* occurs during the [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] barbecue episode, where a traveller gossips that "Lin McLean had told him she was 'away up in G'" — referring to the new schoolmarm from Vermont:

> a casual traveller from the north gossiped of Bear Creek, and the fences up there, and the farm crops, the Westfalls, and the young schoolmarm from Vermont, for whom the Taylors had built a cabin next door to theirs. The traveller had not seen her, but Mrs. Taylor and all the ladies thought the world of her, and Lin McLean had told him she was "away up in G."
> （009-031）

This passing remark places Lin among the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s who have taken notice of [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s arrival, and his casual assessment of her quality hints at the broader community's interest in the Eastern newcomer.

During the climactic baby-switching episode at the barbecue, Lin McLean appears as part of the comic chaos. [[mrs-taylor|Mrs. Taylor]], searching for her missing infant, cries out:

> "Ask Lin McLean. Ask him that sets bulls on folks and steals slippers, what he's done with our innocent lambs, mixing them up with other people's coughing, unhealthy brats."
> （011-013）

The absurdity of the accusation — Lin McLean kidnapping babies — is part of the episode's broad comedy, and his presence among the assembled cast reinforces the community texture of [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]].

## Key Relationships

### The Virginian

Lin McLean is portrayed as a friend and junior associate of the Virginian, part of the younger generation of [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s who look up to him. When the Virginian feels jealousy over Lin's attention to [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]], he does not act on it — instead finding a brotherhood in an unexpected place:

> The Virginian had been ready to look at Lin McLean with a hostile eye; but finding him now beside the barrel, he felt a brotherhood between himself and Lin, and his hostility had taken a new and whimsical direction.
> （010-097）

This moment at the whiskey barrel outside the dance reveals the code's pragmatic wisdom: shared hardship and shared amusement count for more than romantic rivalry among men who work together.

Lin McLean also appears in the tense prelude to the final duel. When the Virginian and Molly arrive in town on the eve of their wedding, Lin is among the three friends who greet them:

> Turning a corner into the town's chief street, where stood the hotel, the bank, the drug store, the general store, and the seven saloons, they were hailed heartily. Here were three friends—Honey Wiggin, Scipio Le Moyne, and Lin McLean—all desirous of drinking the Virginian's health, if his lady—would she mind?
> （035-105）

His presence at this critical juncture, and the gallant deference to Molly, shows that Lin has grown from the sour young man at the barbecue into a loyal member of the Virginian's circle, one who will stand by his friend when the stakes are highest.

### Molly Wood

Lin McLean's assessment of [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] — "away up in G" — is the novel's first external evaluation of her quality as a schoolteacher. The [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] vernacular rating establishes that Molly has been noticed, and favorably, by the community she has come to serve.

## Beyond the Novel

Lin McLean's significance extends beyond *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. Wister published *Lin McLean* in 1897, a collection of interconnected stories making Lin the central figure of his own narrative. Readers familiar with Wister's broader canon would recognize Lin as more than a background figure — he is, in a sense, the Virginian's literary younger brother, a character whose own adventures in love, work, and moral growth parallel and complement the story told here.
