# Hoosic Falls

**Hoosic Falls** is a small town in upstate New York in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*, serving as [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s hometown and the primary setting for the novel's Eastern scenes. It represents the constricted, gossip-driven society that Molly decisively leaves behind when she ventures west to teach school at [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]], [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]].

## Molly's Hometown

Hoosic Falls is where [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] grows up, teaches music, and endures the scrutiny of a small-town community that measures her worth by the man she marries. The town's social order is enforced by figures like [[mrs-flynt|Mrs. Flynt]], the Baptist minister's wife, who dispenses judgment on everything from Molly's ancestry to her decision to earn her own living.

The town's social geography is revealed through its landmarks: the Hoosic Bridge, the lodge gate, the valley of the "little Hoosic," and Mount Anthony. These places — revisited by Molly on her return visit from [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] — are freighted with memory and lost possibility. When Molly drives with [[sam-bannett|Sam Bannett]] to the Hoosic Bridge and gazes at the view, she is measuring the familiar landscape against the remembered vastness of [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]]: "With a little sagebrush, that spot would look something like a place I know on [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]]" (025-190).

## Hoosic Junction

The railroad junction where Molly changes trains on her journey west — and where she glimpses the "up-train bound back to her home" with its familiar conductor and engineer — is a threshold point. It is here that her courage nearly fails her: "she shut her eyes against this glimpse of the familiar things that she was leaving" ([[TOC|P02]]-610). Later, in moments of emotional intensity with the Virginian, Molly becomes "suddenly severe, as she had done at Hoosic Junction" ([[TOC|P02]]-809), suggesting that the Junction has become a psychological marker — the place where she learned to guard herself against overwhelming feeling.

## Social World

The social world of Hoosic Falls is defined by its judgments. "Public opinion went on being indignant over Molly's conduct" ([[TOC|P02]]-580) when she refuses to marry [[sam-bannett|Sam Bannett]] and chooses instead to work. "The most rising young man in Hoosic Falls" is the standard against which Molly's choices are measured — and found inexplicable. The Bannetts offer "a dinner (perhaps I should say a banquet) of twenty-four" (025-190), a display of provincial affluence that underscores the world Molly has outgrown.

The novel's final mention of Hoosic Falls belongs to [[mrs-flynt|Mrs. Flynt]]'s epilogue: [[sam-bannett|Sam Bannett]] and his rich wife "still inhabit the most expensive residence in Hoosic Falls" (029-193). The town remains exactly what it was — prosperous, respectable, and utterly unchanged — while Molly has been transformed by her years in the West.

## Contrast with the West

Hoosic Falls serves throughout the novel as a foil to the [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] cattle country. Where [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] measures people by what they can do, Hoosic Falls measures them by their grandmothers. Where the West offers silence, space, and the possibility of reinvention, Hoosic Falls offers gossip, constraint, and the suffocating weight of other people's opinions. Molly's journey from one world to the other is the novel's central movement, and Hoosic Falls is the world she is right to leave.
