# The Wedding of the Virginian and Molly Wood

The **wedding of [[the-virginian]] and [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Stark Wood]]** is the culminating event of *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*, the narrative resolution toward which the novel's twin threads — the Western adventure and the Eastern romance — have been straining since Molly's first appearance at [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]]. The marriage represents more than a personal happy ending: it is Wister's symbolic argument that the best of Eastern civilization (Molly's refinement, education, and moral seriousness) and the best of Western character (the Virginian's courage, [[frontier-honor|honor]], and competence) can be joined in a union that ennobles both.

## Role in the Novel

### The Romantic Payoff

From the moment the Virginian first encounters Molly — the Eastern schoolteacher who has "quite suddenly decided that she would try her hand at instructing the new generation" of [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] (005-017) — the novel has been building toward their union. The wedding is the fulfillment of a courtship conducted across a cultural divide as wide as the continent: the Virginian, who cannot spell, courting the woman who reads Jane Austen to her class in a one-room [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] schoolhouse.

### The Social Argument

Wister, an Eastern aristocrat who fell in love with the West, uses the marriage to make a social argument. Molly's great-aunt at [[dunbarton|Dunbarton]], who once lost her own love to social convention, counsels Molly to ignore the voices that say she is "marrying below her station." The wedding is thus an act of rebellion against Eastern snobbery and an affirmation of the frontier's democratic promise: a man should be judged by his character, not his pedigree.

### The Civilizing Union

The marriage also serves the novel's larger theme of civilization advancing westward. Molly brings Eastern culture — books, music, domestic order — to the raw frontier, while the Virginian provides the strength and competence that make civilization possible. Their union is Wister's vision of the American future: a synthesis of East and West, refinement and strength, law and freedom.

## Key Scenes

### The Great-Aunt's Counsel

Before the wedding can occur, Molly must overcome her crisis of faith. Her discovery that the Virginian participated in [[hanging-of-steve|the hanging of Steve]] sends her fleeing back East, where [[great-aunt-dunbarton|the great-aunt]] delivers the novel's most explicit endorsement of love over social convention: "When you cannot help it a moment longer, then listen to nothing but that" (034-028). This permission — granted by the oldest and wisest member of the Stark family — authorizes the wedding.

### The Final Duel's Aftermath

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]] kills [[trampas|Trampas]] in a street duel shortly before the wedding — a fact that Molly must accept as the price of loving a man who lives by the code. The novel does not flinch from this juxtaposition: the man Molly marries is a man who has killed. Her acceptance of this truth is the final test of her love.

## Themes & Analysis

### Marriage as Civilization

The novel treats marriage not merely as a romantic conclusion but as a civilizing act. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]], for all his virtues, is incomplete without Molly — his courage lacks refinement, his [[frontier-honor|honor]] lacks domestic purpose. Molly, for all her cultivation, is incomplete without him — her refinement lacks vitality, her education lacks a world to shape. Together, they form a complete social unit: the foundation of the settled, civilized West that will follow the open range.

### The Transformation of the Virginian

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s arc is from solitary [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] to partnered citizen. The wedding marks the completion of this transformation. He has proven himself capable of violence (against [[trampas|Trampas]]), justice (against [[steve|Steve]]), and love (for Molly). The marriage signals that his education — in reading, in feeling, in the responsibilities of adulthood — is complete.
