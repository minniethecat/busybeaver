# Cheyenne

**Cheyenne** is the capital and principal city of [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] Territory in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. It serves as the urban and commercial anchor of the novel's world — a place where politics are conducted, business is transacted, and the cattle barons gather at the celebrated Cheyenne Club. As the eastern gateway to the territory, Cheyenne is the city through which civilization flows into the cattle country and through which the cattle country's wealth flows back east.

## The Gateway to Wyoming

Cheyenne's significance is established in the novel's opening pages, where it is named as the destination at the end of [[the-railroad|the railroad]] line:

> Had you left New York or San Francisco at ten o'clock this morning, by noon the day after tomorrow you could step out at Cheyenne. There you would stand at the heart of the world that is the subject of my picture, yet you would look around you in vain for the reality. (P02-564)

This passage — part of the novel's celebrated elegy for the vanished frontier — positions Cheyenne as both a real place and a threshold to a world already gone. The railroad has made Cheyenne accessible; what lies beyond it, in the narrator's time, has already faded into memory.

## The Cheyenne Club

[[the-cheyenne-club|The Cheyenne Club]] is mentioned as the social center of the [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] cattle aristocracy. During the great cattle boom, when steers had "jumped to seventy-five" dollars a head, "Johnson County, and Natrona, and Converse, and others, to say nothing of the Cheyenne Club, had been jumping over the moon for some weeks" (009-002). The Club represents the Eastern-inflected social ambitions of the cattle barons — men who have made their fortunes on the range but seek the refinements of urban society. [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]], as one of the territory's leading men, is a member of this world.

## Politics and Commerce

Cheyenne is the seat of territorial politics. When [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]] has business to conduct — "Write to Cheyenne and say that if they will hold a meeting next Monday week, I will..." (024-093) — it is through Cheyenne that his influence is exercised. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]], as the Judge's foreman, handles much of this correspondence, writing "such communications with ease" in a businesslike style that contrasts with his unease in more personal written expression.

[[balaam|Balaam]], the novel's villain, remembers "certain politics soon to be rife in Cheyenne" (026-137) as a motive for his journey — a reminder that Cheyenne is the arena where territorial power is contested and distributed.

## A Place of Memory

For the Virginian, Cheyenne is associated with the memory of his lost friend [[steve|Steve]]. He recalls "a Thanksgiving visit to town that he had made with [[steve|Steve]]" when "we was just colts then" (024-112), describing their "coltish doings" in the perfect fellowship of youth. This Cheyenne memory — of a time before [[steve|Steve]]'s fall, before the hanging — is one of the Virginian's rare moments of unguarded nostalgia.

## Rings from the East

In the novel's closing movement, as the Virginian prepares for his wedding to [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]], Cheyenne is considered and rejected as a source of wedding rings:

> Rings were to be had in Cheyenne, and a still greater choice in Denver; and so far as either of these towns his affairs would have permitted him to travel. But he was set upon having rings from the East.
> （034-007）

[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s insistence on Eastern rings — "from the best place in the country" — is telling. Cheyenne, for all its territorial importance, is not good enough for Molly. The rings must come from the East, just as Molly herself came from the East, bringing with her the standards and refinements that the Virginian associates with her world.

## Historical Cheyenne

Unlike many of the novel's fictional place-names (Sunk Creek, [[rawhide|Rawhide]], [[drybone|Drybone]]), Cheyenne is a real city, founded in 1867 as a station on the Union Pacific Railroad. [[the-cheyenne-club|The Cheyenne Club]] was a real institution, home to the [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] Stock Growers Association and the social hub of the cattle aristocracy that Wister knew firsthand. The novel's depiction of Cheyenne reflects its historical role as the political and commercial capital of the [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] cattle kingdom during the open-range era of the 1870s and 1880s.
