# Rawhide

**Rawhide** is a frontier mining settlement in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] Territory, mentioned throughout Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]* as a site of gold excitement and a destination for the drifting population of the frontier. It appears principally in the early chapters of the novel, serving as a symbol of the transient, get-rich-quick mentality that stands in contrast to the settled, code-driven life of the cattle ranches.

## Location and Character

Rawhide is a mining camp rather than an established town, defined by the speculative energy of a gold rush. Its name suggests the raw, unprocessed nature of frontier life — "rawhide" being the untanned leather of the cattle country, but here applied to a place where men come seeking fortune rather than building community. The settlement lies within traveling distance of [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]], as characters pass between the two locations on the stage and railroad.

## In the Novel

### The Stagecoach Journey

Rawhide first enters the novel during the narrator's stagecoach journey from [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]] to [[sunk-creek-ranch|Judge Henry's ranch]]. [[scipio-le-moyne|Scipio Le Moyne]], one of the passengers, is said to have "Rawhide much on his brain" (001-022), suggesting that the mining excitement is the talk of the territory and a magnet for men of uncertain purpose.

The passengers on the stage discuss the new gold finds freely:

> They told me of the new gold excitement at Rawhide, and supposed it would bring up the Northern Pacific; and when I explained the millions owed to this road's German bondholders, they were of opinion that a German would strike it richer at Rawhide.
> （001-020）

This exchange captures the speculative fever that grips such settlements: everything is measured against the gold find, and even [[the-railroad|the railroad]]'s complex finances are reduced to whether a German bondholder might do better to abandon his claims and head for Rawhide.

### Transition Point

For several characters, Rawhide functions as a way station or transition point. Men pass through it, hear rumors there, or use it as a reference point — "We'll all howl when we get to Rawhide" (001-038) — without necessarily settling. This transience is part of the settlement's character: unlike the ranches, which represent permanence and belonging, Rawhide is a place one passes through on the way to somewhere else, or a place one talks about without ever quite reaching.

### Contrast with Sunk Creek

The gold excitement at Rawhide provides an implicit contrast with the cattle economy centered on [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]]. Gold mining is speculative, individual, and destructive of the landscape; cattle ranching, as practiced by [[judge-henry|Judge Henry]], is steady, communal, and integrated with the land. Men drawn to Rawhide are "looking around" — [[scipio-le-moyne|Scipio]]'s phrase — rather than building anything lasting. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]], by contrast, is deeply rooted at Sunk Creek, his identity inseparable from the ranch and its work.

## Thematic Significance

Rawhide represents one pole of the Western experience that Wister maps: the transient, fortune-seeking, individualistic West of mining camps and boomtowns. This is the West of short-term thinking and rootless men — the West that the novel's hero, and the novel's moral scheme, ultimately reject in favor of the settled, [[frontier-honor|honor]]able life of the cattle ranch.

The name itself is suggestive. "Rawhide" is the untamed, unprocessed state of things — exactly what the novel argues the West must transform into something more refined. The journey from Rawhide to Sunk Creek is, in miniature, the journey the novel traces from frontier chaos to civilized order.
