# Uncle Hughey

**Uncle Hughey** is the novel's resident comic figure — an elderly man with a talent for matrimony whose appearances bracket the narrative from the opening chapter to the baby-switching episode. Described with the vividness of a cockatoo "enraged by insult," Uncle Hughey is, in his small way, one of Wister's most memorable creations: ridiculous, irrepressible, and oddly endearing.

## Role in the Novel

Uncle Hughey opens the novel. Before the reader meets the Virginian, before the narrator loses his trunk, before [[trampas|Trampas]] or [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] or any of the novel's grand themes have been introduced, there is Uncle Hughey — standing on the platform at [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]] in his Sunday clothes, en route to yet another wedding. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s teasing of him is the first sustained dialogue in the book, and it establishes at once the Virginian's wit, his gift for deadpan humor, and his essential kindness (he teases Uncle Hughey, but never cruelly).

Structurally, Uncle Hughey is the novel's choral figure. He appears at the beginning (Chapter I), reappears midway through in triumph (the wedding departure at [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]]), and pops up at community gatherings (the baby-switching episode in Chapter XXIV). He is never central to the plot, but he is always there — a reminder, in a novel full of moral seriousness, that life is also ridiculous.

## Key Relationships

### The Virginian

The opening chapter's comic byplay between Uncle Hughey and [[the-virginian|the Virginian]] is the novel's first demonstration of the hero's character. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s teasing is precise and affectionate — he draws Uncle Hughey out, amplifies his absurdity, and then lets him go with his dignity (mostly) intact:

> "Why, your Sunday clothes told me, Uncle Hughey. They are speakin' mighty loud o' nuptials."
> （001-007）

When Uncle Hughey brandishes the returned ring — "There! See that! That's my ring she sent me back, being too unstrung for marriage" — the Virginian's response combines mock concern with genuine warmth:

> "Oh, don't go to get married again, Uncle Hughey! What's the use o' being married?"
> （001-020）

### The Narrator

The narrator observes Uncle Hughey both directly (at the station) and through community report (the wedding departure, the baby-switching episode). His descriptions of Uncle Hughey are among the novel's most visually vivid passages:

> Have you ever seen a cockatoo — the white kind with the topknot — enraged by insult? The bird erects every available feather upon its person. So did Uncle Hughey seem to swell, clothes, mustache, and woolly white beard.
> （001-026）

### The Bride(s)

Uncle Hughey's brides are never named, but they form a comic chain of jiltings and acceptances that culminate in his triumphant departure from [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]] with "an actual wife on his arm. She could easily have been his granddaughter" (004-052). The age gap is noted without judgment — Uncle Hughey's marriages are presented as one of the frontier's inexplicable but harmless phenomena.

## Key Scenes

### The Platform Encounter (Chapter I)

Uncle Hughey's first appearance is, simultaneously, the novel's first scene. Standing on the [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]] platform in his wedding clothes, accused by the Virginian of being en route to yet another nuptial, Uncle Hughey protests, shrieks, brandishes the returned ring, and finally boards the eastbound train in a fury of ruffled dignity. The scene is pure comedy, but it also does important narrative work: it introduces the Virginian as a man who can joke with an old man without cruelty, and it establishes the informal, teasing register of frontier social life.

### The Triumphant Wedding (Chapter IV)

Uncle Hughey reappears in triumph: he has found a bride, and he departs [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]] in a shower of rice, "shrieking to the horses and the bride waving unabashed adieus" (004-052). [[the-virginian|The Virginian]], watching, is "doubled up with laughing" — one of the few times in the novel that the hero is described as laughing without reservation. The scene provides a moment of uncomplicated joy in a book that will soon turn to hangings, rustling, and gunfights.

### The Baby-Switching Episode (Chapter XXIV)

Uncle Hughey appears among the assembled [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] families during the chaotic baby-switching episode, arriving "with his wife" to sort out the confusion (024-016). His presence at this community crisis — simultaneously domestic and absurd — is fitting: Uncle Hughey belongs wherever [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]]'s families gather, a permanent fixture of the community's collective life.

## Themes & Analysis

### Comedy as Counterpoint

Uncle Hughey's function is tonal. In a novel that treats [[frontier-honor|honor]], violence, and moral seriousness with high respect, Uncle Hughey provides the necessary comic counterpoint — a reminder that the frontier is not only a theater of heroic action but also a place where old men in Sunday clothes chase after young brides and get rice thrown at them by [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s. His presence prevents the novel from taking itself too seriously.

### The Persistence of Life

Uncle Hughey's serial matrimony — he is jilted, he returns the ring, he tries again — is, in its comic way, a celebration of resilience. The same quality that makes the Virginian persist through three years of courtship, that makes [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] endure the hardships of frontier teaching, is present in Uncle Hughey's refusal to give up on marriage. He is ridiculous, but he is also indomitable.
