# The Goose Egg Barbecue and Dance

The **Goose Egg barbecue and dance** is the great social event of Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*, hosted by the Stanton Brothers at their [[goose-egg|Goose Egg Ranch]] on [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] during the cattle boom when steers "jumped to seventy-five." It brings together the scattered population of the cattle country — [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s, ranchers, families — for a night of feasting, dancing, and courtship that drives much of the novel's central action.

## The Occasion

The barbecue is a celebration of prosperity. "On the strength of this vigorous price of seventy-five, the Stanton Brothers were giving a barbecue at the Goose Egg outfit" (009-002). The whole neighborhood is invited, "and would come forty miles to a man." [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] rides a hundred and eighteen miles — a journey that would take a full day — drawn not merely by the prospect of feasting but by the news that a young schoolmarm from Vermont would be present.

## The Feast

Wister's description of the barbecue emphasizes its abundance and its elemental simplicity. A steer is roasted whole over an open fire, "the bed of flame under it showed steadily brighter against the dusk that was beginning to veil the lowlands" (009-016). Inside the Goose Egg kitchen, "many small delicacies were preparing." The "busy hosts went and came, while men stood and men lay near the fire-glow."

The scene is deliberately epic in its scale and its sensory richness: the smell of roasting beef, the glow of the fire against the [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] dusk, the gathering of men from across the vast territory. It is a feast in the Homeric mode, transposed to the American West.

## The Dance

After the feast comes the dance, and with it the novel's most sustained comic performance. [[honey-wiggin|Honey Wiggin]], "enjoying himself" with an audience of [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s, systematically catalogs the failed romantic overtures of every man present toward [[molly-stark-wood|the schoolmarm]] — beginning with a stage driver, proceeding through a foreman and two deputy marshals, and ending with "a horse thief — awful good-lookin'" who, it later emerged, "had a Mrs. Horse-thief camped on Poison Spider" (010-022).

Into this atmosphere of comic courtship arrives the Virginian — late, dressed in the new clothes he has bought for the occasion, and radiating a quiet authority that changes the dynamics of the room. His stern rebuke to the assembled [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s for their behavior — and [[honey-wiggin|Honey Wiggin]]'s surprising acceptance of that rebuke — marks the moment when the Virginian establishes his claim not merely on [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s attention but on the respect of the entire [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] community.

## Legacy

The Goose Egg barbecue and dance sets in motion the novel's central romantic arc. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s courtship of [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]] begins here, amid the "small delicacies" of the Stanton Brothers' kitchen and the "fiddles tuning" for the first dance. The baby-swapping prank that will occupy Chapter XVIII is precipitated by the gathering. And the novel's vision of the cattle country as a genuine community — not merely a collection of isolated ranches — is most fully realized at the Goose Egg.
