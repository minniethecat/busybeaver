# Drybone

**Drybone** is a frontier settlement in [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] Territory in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. It appears as a minor but recurring location — a rough way station on the routes connecting [[the-railroad|the railroad]] towns to the cattle country. The settlement's name, with its suggestion of aridity and desolation, captures the harshness of its environment and the transience of its population.

## Location

Drybone lies approximately twenty-five miles from [[balaam|Balaam]]'s Butte Creek Ranch, a distance that the cow-pony [[pedro|Pedro]] could cover "in three hours and not know he done it" (026-041). It is situated along the stage and wagon routes that connect the more settled railroad towns like [[medicine-bow|Medicine Bow]] and Cheyenne to the ranches of the interior. The Judge drives the narrator to [[the-railroad|the railroad]] "across the [[bow-leg|Bow Leg Mountains]], and south through [[balaam|Balaam]]'s Ranch and Drybone to Rock Creek" (004-071), establishing Drybone as a node in the territory's transportation network.

## Character

Drybone is portrayed as a rough place — the kind of frontier settlement where men gamble away their wages and a woman traveling alone is uneasy. [[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]], on her stagecoach journey to [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]], passes through Drybone at night, and "nor did the voices that she heard at Drybone reassure her" ([[TOC|P02]]-614). The implication is clear: Drybone is not a place for respectable women, especially not at night.

The settlement is home to a "hog-ranch" — a frontier term for a low establishment combining saloon, gambling, and worse. It is here that the Virginian spends "a little comfortable money" (009-030) after the beef roundup, and it is here that word of the Goose Egg barbecue and the new schoolmarm reaches him from a casual traveler.

## Dr. Barker

Drybone is notable as the location of Dr. Barker's medical practice. The doctor is mentioned as the physician who "had enjoined total abstinence from all excitement" upon Mrs. Dow, who is prone to "fits" (018-781). This single reference sketches a picture of Drybone as a place that, however rough, supports at least the rudiments of civilized infrastructure — a doctor, a mail wagon, travelers passing through.

## Shorty's Loss

One of the novel's darker moments passes through Drybone. [[shorty|Shorty]], the gentle yellow-haired [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] who loves his horse [[pedro|Pedro]], stops at Drybone on his way to deliver a letter:

> He had passed through Drybone on his way, and at Drybone there had been a game of poker. Shorty's money was now in the pocket of Trampas.
> （026-026）

This loss — the gambling away of his small earnings at Drybone's tables — sets in motion the chain of events that leads to [[shorty|Shorty]]'s sale of [[pedro|Pedro]] to [[balaam|Balaam]] and, ultimately, to the horse's death. Drybone functions here as a place of moral hazard, where the rootless frontier culture of gambling and drink preys on the vulnerable.

## Thematic Significance

Drybone, like [[rawhide|Rawhide]], represents the unsettled frontier — a place of transience, gambling, and uncertain voices in the night. It is the kind of place the novel's heroes pass through but do not stay in. The settlement's function in the narrative is to mark the boundary between civilization and the raw frontier: it has a doctor, a mail service, and roads connecting it to larger towns, but it also has a hog-ranch, poker games that strip men of their last dollar, and an atmosphere that makes a lone woman traveler uneasy.

The name "Drybone" suggests both the harshness of the environment and a kind of gallows humor characteristic of Western place-naming — as if the place is so inhospitable that nothing but dry bones remains.
