# James Westfall

**James Westfall** is a young rancher living near [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. He appears principally in Chapter XVIII ("Would You Be a Parson?"), where he and his wife become the unwitting victims of [[lin-mclean|Lin McLean]]'s baby-swapping prank following the Goose Egg dance. His role, though brief, anchors one of the novel's most celebrated comic set-pieces.

## The Baby-Swapping Episode

James Westfall and his wife Lizzie attend the Goose Egg dance with their infant son Alfred. After the festivities—during which [[lin-mclean|Lin McLean]] has been amusing himself by various pranks—the Westfalls drive home through the night, a journey of nine miles. When they arrive and unwrap their bundled child, they discover a stranger in the blankets.

The discovery unfolds with mounting comic horror. Mrs. Westfall is first to realize something is wrong:

> "Well, they won't bite. Whose are they? Where have you stowed ourn?"
> （018-769）

James's initial calm—"Well, they won't bite"—dissolves when his wife catalogues the offense:

> "Ask Lin McLean. Ask him that sets bulls on folks and steals slippers, what he's done with our innocent lambs, mixing them up with other people's coughing, unhealthy brats. That's Charlie Taylor in Alfred's clothes... and the other one that's been put in Christopher's new quilts is not even a bub—bub—boy!"
> （018-772）

## James's Laughter

James Westfall's response to this domestic catastrophe is one of the novel's most human moments:

> As this crime against society loomed clear to James Westfall's understanding, he sat down on the nearest piece of furniture, and heedless of his wife's tears and his exchanged children, broke into unregenerate laughter.
> （018-772）

His laughter is attributed to nervous release—"Doubtless after his sharp alarm about the bear, he was unstrung"—but it also reveals something essential about his character: an ability to see the absurdity in catastrophe that his wife, in her maternal outrage, cannot share. The narrator notes dryly that "his lady, however, promptly restrung him."

## The Pursuit of Justice

Once properly "restrung" by his wife, James Westfall becomes a willing participant in the pursuit of justice:

> by the time they had repacked the now clamorous changelings, and were rattling on their way to the Taylors', he began to share her outraged feelings properly, as a husband and a father should; but when he reached the Taylors' and learned from Miss Wood that at this house a child had been unwrapped whom nobody could at all identify... James Westfall whipped up his horses and grew almost as thirsty for revenge as was his wife.
> （018-772）

The phrase "almost as thirsty for revenge as was his wife" is characteristic Wister irony: James is doing his husbandly duty by being outraged, but the narrator implies that his heart isn't entirely in it—or wasn't, until the scale of McLean's mischief became clear.

## The Sorting Out

At the Swintons', where the affected families gather at dawn, James Westfall contributes one of the episode's most quoted lines. Assessing the chaos [[lin-mclean|Lin McLean]] has created, he observes that McLean had not merely "swapped the duds; he had shuffled the whole doggone deck" (018-783). This image of McLean as a card sharp dealing chaos among the infants has become one of the novel's durable comic phrases.

The Westfalls eventually recover their child: "Mrs. Westfall found Christopher at once in the green shawl of Anna Maria Dow"—but the sorting requires a "ladies' caucus" from which the men, including James, are excluded.

## Character and Significance

James Westfall is a minor figure, but his brief appearance adds texture to the [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] community Wister portrays. He is a young husband, still more lover than father ("being as yet more their wives' lovers than their children's parents"), capable of laughter even in domestic crisis—a quality that distinguishes him from the more rigidly outraged mothers of [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]]. His presence in the novel contributes to the sense of [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] as a functioning community of families, not merely a backdrop for the Virginian's heroic narrative.

The episode also serves a structural function: the baby-swapping aftermath provides the setting for the Virginian's confession that he, not [[lin-mclean|Lin McLean]], was the mastermind of the prank—a confession delivered with characteristic timing and charisma that disarms the assembled fathers and earns their reluctant admiration.
