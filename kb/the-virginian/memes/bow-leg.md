# Bow Leg Mountains

The **Bow Leg Mountains** are a mountain range in south-central [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] Territory in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. They form the physical and symbolic horizon of the novel's principal setting: Sunk Creek flows from their western side, and in the folds of these mountains lies [[sunk-creek-ranch|Judge Henry's ranch]]. The mountains are a constant presence in the landscape of the novel, visible from the ranch, from the trail, and from the surrounding cattle country.

## Geography

The Bow Leg Mountains are introduced as a landmark during the narrator's journey to [[sunk-creek-ranch|Judge Henry's ranch]]:

> We crossed a river in the forenoon, and far to the north of us we saw the Bow Leg Mountains, pale in the bright sun. Sunk Creek flowed from their western side, and deep in the folds of these mountains was the Judge's ranch.
> （004-058）

The mountains' relationship to [[sunk-creek-ranch|Sunk Creek Ranch]] is intimate and defining: the ranch exists within them, not merely beside them. The creek that gives the ranch its name originates in these mountains, flowing westward out of their canyons. The mountains thus serve as both a physical boundary — marking the ranch's territory — and a source of the water that makes the ranch possible.

## The Bow Leg Canyon

Sunk Creek emerges from its canyon through the Bow Leg before reaching the ranch proper (006-004). This canyon functions as a kind of threshold: beyond it lies the ranch's cultivated home quarter; within it, the wilder country of the mountains. The canyon's mention at the boundary between ranch and wilderness reinforces the novel's larger theme of domestication and settlement.

## Symbolic Function

Throughout the novel, the Bow Leg Mountains are part of the visual backdrop against which the action unfolds. They are "pale in the bright sun" during the day, and they form the horizon that the [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s see from their saddles. Their presence is constant but unobtrusive — like the mountains themselves, a fixed point in a landscape of movement and change.

The mountains also help define the novel's sense of scale. The distances are vast, the mountains "far to the north," and yet Sunk Creek's course ties the ranch to this distant range, creating a geography that is both expansive and intimately connected. The [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]]s' knowledge of this geography — which canyon leads where, which stream flows from which mountain — is part of their professional competence and their belonging to the land.

## Name

The name "Bow Leg" is characteristic of Western place-naming: descriptive, earthy, and unromantic. A "bow leg" is a leg that curves outward, and the name presumably describes the shape of the mountains or a particular formation within them. Like "[[rawhide|Rawhide]]" and "Sunk Creek," the name belongs to the plain-spoken vernacular of the cattle country, where places are named for what they look like or what happens there, not for abstract ideals.
