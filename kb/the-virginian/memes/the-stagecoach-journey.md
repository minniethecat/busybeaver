# Molly Wood's Stagecoach Journey

**[[molly-stark-wood|Molly Wood]]'s stagecoach journey** from [[the-railroad|the railroad]] to her new teaching post at [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] is one of the pivotal events in Owen Wister's *[[the-virginian|The Virginian]]*. Spanning some thirty hours of rough travel through [[medicine-bow|Wyoming]] Territory, the journey serves as Molly's rite of passage into the frontier — a trial by discomfort, danger, and unwelcome male attention that tests the same [[grandmother-stark|Grandmother Stark]] courage that brought her west. It culminates in the flooded ford at South Fork, where the Virginian lifts her from a sinking stagecoach onto his horse, inaugurating the novel's central romance.

## The Journey Begins

Molly departs from [[the-railroad|the railroad]] at dawn, "the stage drove away into the sagebrush, with her as its only passenger" ([[TOC|P02]]-614). She carries little: "a little money, a few clothes, and in her mind a rigid determination neither to be a burden to her mother nor to give in to that mother's desires." Behind her are [[bennington|Bennington]], [[hoosic-falls|Hoosic Falls]], [[sam-bannett|Sam Bannett]], and the world she has chosen to leave.

## The First Driver

The first stage driver — a "frank-looking boy" — is not dangerous but importunate. After a harrowing incident in which his "virgin to harness" team nearly overturns the stage in a gully, and impressed by Molly's composure — she "sat mute and unflinching beside the driver" — he proposes marriage:

> he invited her earnestly to be his wife during many of the next fifteen miles, and told her of his snug cabin and his horses and his mine. (P02-614)

Molly handles him with characteristic grace: she moves inside the stage, and when they part at Point of Rocks, "she answered sweetly that she would try" to remember him, and gave him her hand. The narrator notes drily that "Molly Stark, in her day, was not a New Woman" — she combines feminine courtesy with an immovable independence.

## The Second Driver

The second driver is another matter entirely. "He was not a frank-looking boy, and he had been taking whiskey. All night long he took it, while his passenger, helpless and sleepless inside the lurching stage, sat as upright as she possibly could." The stage passes through [[drybone|Drybone]] at night, and "nor did the voices that she heard at [[drybone|Drybone]] reassure her."

## South Fork

At dawn, the stage reaches the South Fork river. The drunken driver bungles the ford:

> Two wheels sank down over an edge, and the canvas toppled like a descending kite. The ripple came sucking through the upper spokes... (P02-614)

At this moment of crisis, a tall rider appears. He takes Molly onto his horse, tells her "to cheer up, and its being all right," and then — with characteristic delicacy — "gently withdrew, leaving her to become herself again."

## Significance

The stagecoach journey is Molly's introduction to the frontier — its roughness, its dangers, and its unexpected gallantry. It tests her courage and proves it equal to the challenge. And at South Fork, it delivers her into the orbit of the man who will define her life. The journey from [[bennington|Bennington]] to [[bear-creek|Bear Creek]] is, in miniature, the journey from East to West that the novel celebrates.
