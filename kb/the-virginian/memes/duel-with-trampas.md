## Summary

After five years of growing enmity, the Virginian and [[trampas|Trampas]] face each other on the main street at sundown. [[trampas|Trampas]] has issued a public ultimatum: "Get out by sundown" (035-174). [[the-virginian|The Virginian]], with his bride Molly waiting in the hotel, chooses to meet the threat rather than flee. When [[trampas|Trampas]] draws, the Virginian fires once. A wind seems to blow [[trampas|Trampas]]'s sleeve off his arm, and he pitches forward. It is over. The novel's long conflict is resolved, and with it, the Virginian's claim to the moral authority he has embodied throughout the story.

## Context

The duel is the culmination of a conflict that began with the poker-table confrontation in Chapter 2 and escalated through the novel's major events: [[trampas|Trampas]]'s humiliation at [[the-dance|the barbecue dance]], his resentment of the Virginian's authority during the cattle gather, his association with the [[rustler-gang|rustlers]], and the killing of [[shorty|Shorty]]. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] tells Molly, "I had to explain myself to [[trampas|Trampas]] a right smart while ago" (035-084), but the explanation has never been accepted, and [[trampas|Trampas]]'s hatred has only deepened with time.

The immediate trigger is the Virginian's return to town with Molly, on the eve of their wedding journey. [[trampas|Trampas]], emboldened by whiskey and the presence of his faction, delivers his ultimatum in the saloon. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]]'s response is characteristic: measured, reluctant, and final.

## Key Participants

- **[[the-virginian]]** — Now a prosperous man, soon to be married, he faces [[trampas|Trampas]] not as a young [[the-cowpuncher|cowboy]] proving himself but as a settled member of the community defending its order. He acts without anger and with full moral clarity.
- **[[trampas]]** — The antagonist, whose hatred of the Virginian has consumed him for five years. In the end, he is a diminished figure — drunk, cornered by his own ultimatum, facing a man he can neither outdraw nor outlive.
- **[[molly-stark-wood]]** — Waiting in the hotel, unaware of what is happening on the street below, she represents everything the Virginian is fighting to protect — and everything he stands to lose.
- **The bishop** — His conversation with the Virginian before the duel provides the novel's final moral framing. When the bishop says "I should run away from [[trampas|Trampas]]" (035-205) and the Virginian replies "if you was me, seh, and not a bishop, would you run away from [[trampas|Trampas]]?" (035-208), the bishop's silence is its own answer.
- **[[scipio-le-moyne|Scipio Le Moyne]]** — Present as witness, he vouches for the Virginian's conduct: "Three of us saw his gun out" (035-312), confirming that the Virginian fired in self-defense.

## Narrative

The scene is constructed with remarkable economy. [[trampas|Trampas]] issues his threat in the saloon. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] walks out into the street. He speaks with the bishop, whose counsel — "Thou shalt not kill" (035-213) — is met with the Virginian's unanswerable reply: "Make it plain to [[trampas|Trampas]], and there'll be no killin'" (035-214). He visits Molly in the hotel, telling her only, "There is some waiting still before us. I wish you did not have to wait alone. But it will not be long" (035-245).

### The Friends' Vigil

As the Virginian walks through the town, his friends sense the gravity of what is to come. Scipio speaks the collective feeling:

> "It's a turruble shame," sighed Scipio, "that he couldn't have postponed it."
> （035-178）

And as the Virginian prepares for the confrontation, his friends move into position behind him:

> "This race will not be pulled," said McLean.
>
> "Be with you at the finish," said Scipio.
>
> And they passed on.
> （035-300–035-302）

This quiet exchange — cowpunchers speaking in the language of horse racing — is the code's version of a blessing. They will not interfere, but they will bear witness, and they will stand by their friend.

### The Virginian's Private Moment

Before walking into the street, the Virginian pauses alone. Wister gives us the most intimate glimpse of his hero in the entire novel:

> The Virginian, for precaution, did not walk out of the front door of the hotel. He went through back ways, and paused once. Against his breast he felt the wedding ring where he had it suspended by a chain from his neck. His hand went up to it, and he drew it out and looked at it. He took it off the chain, and his arm went back to hurl it from him as far as he could. But he stopped and kissed it with one sob, and thrust it in his pocket.
> （035-304）

This is the moment of ultimate choice. He considers throwing the ring away — abandoning the marriage, the future, everything he has built — rather than risk making Molly a widow. But he cannot. He kisses it, pockets it, and walks out to meet his fate.

Wister cuts between the Virginian's preparations and [[trampas|Trampas]]'s deterioration. [[trampas|Trampas]] drinks, paces, checks the sun. "Something had changed. He looked everywhere, and feeling it everywhere, wondered what this could be. Then he knew: it was the sun that had gone entirely behind the mountains, and he drew out his pistol" (035-303).

The gunfight itself takes a single paragraph. [[trampas|Trampas]] fires and misses. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] fires once:

> A wind seemed to blow his sleeve off his arm, and he replied to it, and saw Trampas pitch forward. He saw Trampas raise his arm from the ground and fall again, and lie there this time, still. A little smoke was rising from the pistol on the ground, and he looked at his own, and saw the smoke flowing upward out of it.
> （035-306）

Two fingers twitch, then cease. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] stands looking down at his enemy, then turns away.

## Aftermath

[[scipio-le-moyne|Scipio]]'s immediate testimony — "Three of us saw his gun out" — establishes the legal and moral facts: the Virginian fired in self-defense against a man who drew first. The community accepts this verdict without question. [[the-virginian|The Virginian]] returns to the hotel and tells Molly simply: "I have killed [[trampas|Trampas]]" (035-316).

Molly's response completes her arc. Where once she would have recoiled in horror, she now receives the news with the gravity it demands. Her acceptance signals that she has fully internalized the code's terms — she can love a man who has killed because she understands why he had to.

## Themes

The duel is the novel's resolution in miniature. It validates the Virginian's entire way of life: the code that demanded he face [[trampas|Trampas]] rather than flee, the restraint that gave his enemy every chance to back down, and the lethal competence that ensured, when the moment came, that justice prevailed. The bishop's silence is the novel's final word on the matter — when formal institutions are absent and moral clarity is demanded, the code provides what law cannot.
