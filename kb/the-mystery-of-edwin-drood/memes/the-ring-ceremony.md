# The Ring Ceremony

The **Ring Ceremony** is a pivotal event in Chapter 11 of *[[the-mystery-of-edwin-drood|The Mystery of Edwin Drood]]*, in which [[mr-grewgious]] — acting as guardian to [[rosa-bud]] — presents [[edwin-drood]] with [[rosa-bud|Rosa]]'s mother's betrothal ring and delivers a solemn charge that will reverberate through the remainder of the novel. The scene takes place in [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]]'s chambers at [[staple-inn|Staple Inn]], [[london|London]], following an elaborate dinner party attended only by [[edwin-drood|Edwin]] and [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]]'s clerk, [[mr-bazzard]].

## The Presentation of the Ring

After dinner, [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]] leads [[edwin-drood|Edwin]] through a long, meticulously formal preamble about the nature of true love — what he calls his "picture" of the true lover as "ever impatient to be in the presence or vicinity of the beloved object of his [[love|affection]]s" [011-081]. He then rises, unlocks a secret drawer in his escritoire, and retrieves "an ordinary ring-case made for a single ring" [011-108].

The ring itself carries deep personal history: "this rose of diamonds and rubies delicately set in gold, was a ring belonging to Miss [[rosa-bud|Rosa]]'s mother" [011-109]. [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]] reveals that [[rosa-bud|Rosa]]'s mother — who drowned early in her marriage — received the ring from her husband "when they first plighted their faith to one another" [011-111]. The ring thus binds three generations: [[rosa-bud|Rosa]]'s parents, [[rosa-bud|Rosa]] and [[edwin-drood|Edwin]], and — by implication — the unresolved grief [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]] still carries for [[rosa-bud|Rosa]]'s mother.

## The Solemn Charge

[[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]] does not simply hand over the ring; he frames the transfer as a moral test. "Your placing it on her finger," he tells [[edwin-drood|Edwin]], "will be the solemn seal upon your strict fidelity to the living and the dead" [011-113]. But the true weight of the scene comes in what follows — one of the novel's most quoted passages:

> "If anything should be amiss, if anything should be even slightly wrong, between you; if you should have any secret consciousness that you are committing yourself to this step for no higher reason than because you have long been accustomed to look forward to it; then, I charge you once more, by the living and by the dead, to bring that ring back to me!" [011-115]

The charge is striking for its psychological penetration. [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]] does not command [[edwin-drood|Edwin]] to marry [[rosa-bud|Rosa]]; he commands [[edwin-drood|Edwin]] to be honest with himself — to examine whether his betrothal springs from genuine love or mere long habit. At this moment, [[edwin-drood|Edwin]]'s face shows "some trouble" and his hand shows "some indecision" [011-112].

## The Comic Interruption

The gravity of the moment is characteristically undercut by Dickensian comedy. At the very instant of the charge, [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]]'s clerk Bazzard — who has been asleep throughout — "awoke himself by his own snoring; and, as is usual in such cases, sat apoplectically staring at vacancy, as defying vacancy to accuse him of having been asleep" [011-116]. The juxtaposition of high moral drama with clerical somnolence is a hallmark of Dickens's method, preventing the scene from tipping into melodrama.

## The Ring's Afterlife

The ring does not leave [[edwin-drood|Edwin]]'s consciousness. In Chapter 13, as he and [[rosa-bud|Rosa]] walk by the river and agree to end their betrothal, "his right hand was in his breast, seeking the ring; but he checked it" [013-041]. The narrative voice returns obsessively to this detail: "[[edwin-drood|Edwin Drood]]'s right hand closed again upon the ring in its little case, and again was checked by the consideration: 'It is certain, now, that I am to give it back to him; then why should I tell her of it?'" [013-063].

After the betrothal is dissolved, the question of the ring's return hangs over the novel. [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]], alone after [[edwin-drood|Edwin]]'s departure, paces his rooms and murmurs: "Her ring. Will it come back to me? My mind hangs about her ring very uneasily tonight" [011-126]. His unease proves prophetic — [[edwin-drood|Edwin]] never returns the ring, and the ring's fate becomes entangled with [[edwin-drood|Edwin]]'s [[death|disappearance]] in the great storm [014-132].

## Thematic Significance

The ring ceremony establishes the moral architecture of the novel. [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]]'s charge — "by the living and by the dead, bring that ring back to me" — elevates a betrothal gift into an instrument of conscience. The ring tests not only [[edwin-drood|Edwin]]'s fidelity to [[rosa-bud|Rosa]] but his fidelity to truth itself. When [[edwin-drood|Edwin]] chooses to be honest with [[rosa-bud|Rosa]] (Chapter 13) and dissolve the engagement, he is in some sense following [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]]'s charge before it crushes him. The ring's failure to return — like [[edwin-drood|Edwin]]'s failure to reappear — becomes one of the novel's central lacunae, a physical emblem of [[mystery|the mystery]] Dickens left unfinished.
