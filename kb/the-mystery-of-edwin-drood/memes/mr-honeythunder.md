# Mr. Honeythunder

## Character Overview

Mr. Honeythunder is a bombastic [[london|London]] philanthropist whose "tremendous voice" [017-004] and institutional self-righteousness make him one of Dickens's most savagely satirized characters. He first enters the novel through his calling card, delivered to [[minor-canon-corner|Minor Canon Corner]]: "'It's from Mr. Honeythunder, of course,' said the old lady" [006-013]. The card identifies him as a professional philanthropist, a self-appointed guardian of public virtue whose "public character of Professor of Philanthropy" [006-041] demands deference from all who encounter him.

Dickens's portrait is unsparing. Mr. Honeythunder represents everything the novel distrusts about institutional benevolence: its self-regard, its coerciveness, its substitution of noise for genuine moral action. He is philanthropy without love — a "Haven of Philanthropy" that serves primarily as a haven for its proprietor's ego.

## The Dinner at Minor Canon Corner

Mr. Honeythunder's most extended scene comes in Chapter 6, when he descends upon [[minor-canon-corner|Minor Canon Corner]] for dinner. [[mr-crisparkle|Mr. Crisparkle]] receives him "with a troubled mind" [006-072], and the ensuing meal proves his misgivings justified. "Mr. Honeythunder expanded into an inflammatory address" [006-073], his conversation indistinguishable from a public lecture, his attention fixed entirely on his own pronouncements.

The narrator captures the collective relief when "the departure of Mr. Honeythunder began to impend" [006-075], and afterward Cloisterham feels "quiet and peaceful after Mr. Honeythunder's departure" [007-030]. [[neville-landless|Neville Landless]] observes that "'But for Mr. Honeythunder's eloquence, I might have had'" [007-051] a different outcome — a recognition that the philanthropist's words, far from helping, actively harm those they claim to serve.

Mr. Honeythunder's habit of demanding agreement is consistent: "'Eh?' demanded Mr. Honeythunder" [006-068], and when met with levity, his response is characteristically humorless: "'Ay; I never see a joke,' Mr. Honeythunder frowningly retorted. 'A joke is wasted on me'" [006-070]. The inability to laugh — to see oneself from the outside — is, for Dickens, the surest marker of moral incapacity.

## The Philanthropic Profession

Mr. Honeythunder's "public character" [006-041] is not a private virtue publicly expressed but a performance sustained through professional apparatus. His London office serves as the headquarters from which he directs his philanthropic operations, and his wards — [[neville-landless|Neville]] and [[helena-landless|Helena Landless]] — are treated less as individuals than as exhibits in his moral portfolio.

The narrator's parenthetical description of [[luke-honeythunder|Luke Honeythunder]] — the "Lukely deceased brother (In Philanthropy), Luke Honeythunder" [006-035] — captures the essence of Honeythunder's character: even family bereavement is subordinated to the public identity of the philanthropist. The dead brother exists only in parentheses, his entire significance reduced to his relationship to Philanthropy.

## Confrontation with Crisparkle

In Chapter 17, [[mr-crisparkle|Crisparkle]] seeks "audience of Mr. Honeythunder" [017-001] at his London office. The encounter is a study in contrasting moral styles: [[mr-crisparkle|Crisparkle]]'s quiet, persistent decency against [[mr-honeythunder|Honeythunder]]'s theatrical denunciation. [[mr-crisparkle|Crisparkle]] has come to defend [[neville-landless|Neville]], and [[mr-honeythunder|Honeythunder]]'s response — "a young man" [017-015], as if that answered everything — exemplifies his substitution of categories for judgment.
