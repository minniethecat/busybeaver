Miss Ferdinand is a minor character in Charles Dickens's unfinished novel *The Mystery of Edwin Drood*. She is a student at Miss Twinkleton's seminary for young ladies, the Nuns' House in Cloisterham.

Miss Ferdinand is repeatedly singled out for her unruly conduct. In the classroom, Miss Twinkleton publicly calls her out as "apparently incorrigible" and assigns her the punishment of writing out the first four fables of Monsieur La Fontaine "in the original language" [009-014]. Her misbehaviour continues; on the same day she "got into new trouble by surreptitiously clapping on a paper moustache at dinnertime, and going through the motions of aiming a water-bottle at Miss Giggles, who drew a tablespoon in defence" [009-015].

Miss Ferdinand's spirited nature extends beyond the classroom. During the end-of-term festivities at the Nuns' House, she is described as "the daring Miss Ferdinand" who "had even surprised the company with a sprightly solo on the comb" [013-001]. In a later chapter, Miss Twinkleton notes that "Miss Ferdinand is at present weighed down by an incubus" of writing out her La Fontaine punishment, though the penalty is eventually remitted at the intercession of Mr. Grewgious [009-089].
