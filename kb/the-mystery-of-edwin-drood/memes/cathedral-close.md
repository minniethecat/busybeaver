The Cathedral Close is the precinct immediately surrounding Cloisterham Cathedral, a space that occupies an important position in the geography of *The Mystery of Edwin Drood*. Neither fully sacred nor fully secular, the Close is a zone of transition where characters cross paths, exchange information, and advance the novel's intrigue.

In Chapter 10, Mr. Crisparkle is described "tracing his steps towards the Cathedral Close," where he walks while thinking over the troubling developments concerning Neville Landless and Edwin Drood [010-085]. The Close here serves as a place of solitary reflection, its quiet avenues providing a setting for the inward drama of conscience and decision.

The Close also functions as a habitual promenade. In Chapter 12, the narrator notes that a certain character "often takes an airing in the Cathedral Close and thereabout," finding in its orderly precincts a congenial setting for leisurely movement and social observation [012-001]. Like the Cathedral itself, the Close represents the ordered, visible surface of Cloisterham life — a space of propriety and decorum that stands in implicit contrast to the darker currents running beneath the town's placid exterior.

## Significance

The Cathedral Close is one of several interstitial spaces in the novel — like the Gate House, the Crypt, and the weir — that occupy the boundary between different orders of experience. In walking through the Close, characters move between the sacred space of the Cathedral and the mundane world of the town, a passage that mirrors the novel's larger thematic concern with the relationship between public respectability and private truth.
