Miss Brobity is a minor character in Charles Dickens's unfinished novel *The Mystery of Edwin Drood*, introduced indirectly through the reminiscences of Mr. Thomas Sapsea during a conversation with John Jasper in Chapter 4. She was the mistress of what Sapsea calls "the other parallel establishment down town" — a rival school to the Nuns' House [004-030].

According to Sapsea's self-aggrandising account, Miss Brobity "showed a passion for attending my sales" on half-holidays and vacation time, and "admired my style" to such a degree that "my style became traceable in the dictation-exercises of Miss Brobity's pupils" [004-030]. The relationship culminated in marriage. When Sapsea proposed, Miss Brobity was so overwhelmed with what he describes as "a species of Awe" that "she was able to articulate only the two words, 'O Thou!' meaning myself" [004-032].

After their marriage, Sapsea disposed of the parallel establishment by private contract. He notes that his wife "never could, and she never did, find a phrase satisfactory to her perhaps-too-favourable estimate of my intellect. To the very last (feeble action of liver), she addressed me in the same unfinished terms" [004-032]. The parenthetical mention of "feeble action of liver" suggests she died of liver disease. The portrait that emerges through Sapsea's pompous narration is that of a woman whose genuine admiration was filtered through her husband's limitless vanity.
