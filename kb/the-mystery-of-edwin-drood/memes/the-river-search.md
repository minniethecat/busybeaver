Following Edwin Drood's disappearance after Christmas Eve, a systematic search of the river and weir at Cloisterham becomes the first major investigatory action. The river search represents the community's attempt to resolve the mystery through physical evidence — an effort that produces hauntingly incomplete results.

## The Search Operation

Under the direction of the authorities and with the Reverend Crisparkle's involvement, the river is dragged from the Weir downstream. The search party methodically combs the water and riverbanks, looking for any trace of Edwin — his body, his clothing, his watch, or the distinctive gold pin that he was known to wear. Dickens describes the operation with a journalist's precision, noting the practical difficulties of searching a tidal river with strong currents and murky waters [015-060].

## The Discovery

The search yields only ambiguous items: Edwin's watch is found in the weir, but his body is not. This partial recovery deepens rather than resolves the mystery. The watch — stopped, water-damaged, and stripped from its owner — becomes a piece of forensic evidence without a conclusive narrative. It could indicate drowning, foul play, or deliberate misdirection. Dickens deliberately withholds certainty, using the river's opacity as a metaphor for the limits of empirical investigation [015-075].

## Aftermath and Suspicion

The inconclusive results of the river search shift attention to Neville Landless, who was the last person seen with Edwin at the weir. The physical evidence — or lack thereof — feeds a climate of suspicion in Cloisterham, where prejudice against Neville's foreign background and passionate temperament predisposes the community to see guilt rather than ambiguity. The river search thus becomes not only an investigatory event but a turning point in the novel's exploration of evidence, certainty, and social judgment [016-010].
