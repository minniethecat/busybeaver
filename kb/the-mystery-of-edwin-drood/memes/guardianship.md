## Overview

Guardianship is a legal institution that shapes the central relationships of *The Mystery of Edwin Drood*. Mr. Grewgious serves as Rosa Bud's guardian under her father's will, a role that carries both legal obligations and moral weight. The novel explores guardianship not merely as a legal arrangement but as a form of surrogate parenthood — one that protects Rosa from unwanted suitors while binding her to her late father's wishes regarding her betrothal to Edwin Drood [003-008, 007-002].

## Grewgious as Guardian

Rosa describes Grewgious to the Minor Canon: "She made him our guardian, and he was a miserly wretch who grudged us food to eat and clothes to wear" [007-007] — referring to her earlier guardian before Grewgious assumed the role. The contrast is deliberate: Grewgious, though outwardly dry and angular, proves to be a protector of genuine tenderness. He keeps Rosa's father's ring in his strong-box, honoring his dead friend's trust [009-005].

## Legal and Emotional Dimensions

Guardianship in the novel operates on two levels. Legally, it gives Grewgious authority over Rosa's marriage: "until then, my guardian and trustee" [008-007]. Emotionally, it makes him a surrogate father figure who intervenes at critical moments. Rosa credits his intercession for her ability to break the betrothal: "owing to the intercession of your guardian, Mr. Grewgious" [009-089]. When Rosa is distressed after Edwin's disappearance, her guardian comes down from London: "when all at once my guardian came down, to prepare for my leaving here" [013-039].

## The Betrothal as Guardianship's Burden

The betrothal between Edwin and Rosa was arranged by their fathers, and Grewgious, as Rosa's guardian, is bound by the will's terms. Yet he never forces Rosa to comply. Instead, he facilitates the mutual release, acting as a guardian in the truest sense: one who protects his ward's happiness above legal technicalities [013-056, 013-061, 013-063].
