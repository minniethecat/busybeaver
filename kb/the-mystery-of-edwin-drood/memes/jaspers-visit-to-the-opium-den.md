The novel opens with one of Dickens' most atmospheric sequences: John Jasper emerging from an opium stupor in a squalid East End den, surrounded by the "haggard" figures of fellow addicts, attended by the mysterious Princess Puffer. This opening event establishes both Jasper's secret double life and the novel's preoccupation with altered states of consciousness.

## The Den

Dickens' depiction of the opium den draws on his own documented visits to London's East End opium establishments. The den is a cramped, foul-smelling room where bodies lie in various states of narcotic oblivion — "a choked and rusty old fellow" beside "a Chinaman, a Lascar, and a haggard woman." The atmosphere is one of suspended animation, a space outside normal social time where identities dissolve [001-010].

## Jasper's Condition

Jasper is discovered twitching and murmuring in his opium sleep, his words unintelligible but clearly freighted with guilt and obsession. Upon waking, he is immediately concerned with concealment — adjusting his appearance, checking the time, calculating his return to Cloisterham. The juxtaposition of his respectable cathedral identity (choirmaster, nephew, guardian) with this degraded setting establishes the novel's central theme of psychological duality [001-030].

## Princess Puffer

The woman who tends to Jasper — known as Princess Puffer — is one of Dickens' most vivid minor creations. She is both grotesque and shrewd, a figure who extracts what she can from her customers while nursing private knowledge. Her relationship with Jasper goes beyond the commercial: she knows something about him, or suspects something, that gives her a kind of power. This power dynamic, established in the opening scene, will prove crucial to the novel's eventual unraveling [001-050].
