# Mr. Bazzard

## Character Overview

Mr. Bazzard is [[mr-grewgious|Mr. Grewgious]]'s clerk at [[staple-inn|Staple Inn]], a quiet, efficient functionary whose secret life as an aspiring playwright provides one of the novel's most delicately handled comic strains. He first appears in Chapter 11, summoned by [[mr-grewgious|Mr. Grewgious]]'s call: "'Now, Bazzard,' said Mr. Grewgious, on the entrance of his clerk" [011-012]. Bazzard's reply — "'Mr. Drood,' said Bazzard" [011-013] — establishes his manner: correct, economical, faintly ironic.

Bazzard's tragedy, titled *The Thorn of Anxiety*, is never written — and this is the point. His creative ambition exists entirely in the conditional tense, a masterpiece perpetually deferred. In this he resembles many of Dickens's would-be artists, but with a crucial difference: Bazzard seems genuinely at peace with his unwritten opus. When [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]] asks if the tragedy is advancing, Bazzard's response is philosophical: "'in that case I don't care if I do' " [011-033] — his indifference to fame as quietly dignified as his competence at his desk.

## Service to Mr. Grewgious

Bazzard's professional relationship with [[mr-grewgious|Mr. Grewgious]] is characterized by mutual, if understated, respect. [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]] acknowledges his clerk's care: "'I have so good a fire; but Mr. Bazzard has taken care of me'" [011-021]. Bazzard's deadpan correction — "'No I haven't,' said Mr. Bazzard at the door" [011-022] — reveals a relationship in which truth-telling is permitted, even welcomed, between employer and employee.

Bazzard performs his duties with practiced precision: "Bazzard, after drawing out the round table" [011-035], hosting the dinner that [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]] offers to [[edwin-drood|Edwin Drood]], [[john-jasper|Jasper]], and [[neville-landless|Neville Landless]]. He returns "accompanied by two waiters from the hotel" [011-061], a clerk who can summon a dinner party as efficiently as he can file a document. [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]]'s sudden exclamation — "'Bazzard!'" [011-066] — and the clerk's steady response — "'I follow you, sir,' returned Bazzard; who had done his work of conducting the dinner" [011-067] — encapsulate their dynamic: the employer impulsive, the clerk unflappable.

## The Unwritten Tragedy

The title *The Thorn of Anxiety* reveals as much about Bazzard as any completed work could. It suggests a sensibility drawn to grand emotional subjects — anxiety, thorns, suffering — while the unwritten state of the work suggests an artist for whom the dream suffices. In a novel about the gap between public appearance and private truth, Bazzard is an exception: there is no gap. He is exactly what he appears to be, and his unwritten tragedy is not a failure but a form of self-knowledge.

[[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]] drinks to his clerk: "'I drink to you, Bazzard; Mr. Edwin, success to Mr. Bazzard!'" [011-068]. The toast is sincere, and Bazzard's acceptance of it — without false modesty, without inflated hope — marks him as one of the novel's most balanced characters.
