The Tilted Wagon is a roadside inn located on the top of a hill, appearing in Chapter 15 of *The Mystery of Edwin Drood*. The establishment caters primarily to horses and cattle — visitors seeking human breakfast are unusual enough to cause considerable delay, as the inn's domestic machinery must be laboriously realigned from its customary indifference to the preparation of tea, toast, and bacon [015-002].

Dickens describes the Tilted Wagon with a comically exhaustive catalogue of its deficiencies. The ground before the door is puddled with damp hoofs and trodden straw; the landlady is seen slapping a moist baby in the bar; cheese sits aground on a shelf alongside a mouldy tablecloth; the bread weeps crumbs; and the family linen, half-washed and half-dried, lies about in public view. Everything to drink is served in mugs, and "everything else was suggestive of a rhyme to mugs" [015-003].

The inn's painted sign promises "good entertainment for Man and Beast," but the narrator drily observes that it "hardly kept its painted promise" [015-003]. The Tilted Wagon exemplifies Dickens's talent for rendering minor locations with extravagant comic energy, transforming a mere roadside stop into a miniature world of cheerful squalor.

## Location in the Novel

The Tilted Wagon appears during a journey sequence in Chapter 15 ("Impeached"), when characters are traveling in the vicinity of Cloisterham. Its exact geographical position is not specified beyond being on a hilltop, but it serves as a waypoint on the road to or from the cathedral town.
