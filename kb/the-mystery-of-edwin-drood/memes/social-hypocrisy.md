Social hypocrisy is one of Dickens' great themes, and *The Mystery of Edwin Drood* gives it a particularly sharp treatment. Cloisterham — the ancient cathedral town — presents a facade of Christian piety and communal harmony that conceals prejudice, self-interest, and a profound indifference to truth. The novel's mystery plot is inseparable from this social critique: the town's rush to judgment after Edwin's disappearance reveals how quickly respectable society converts suspicion into certainty when the suspect is an outsider.

## The Cathedral as Mask

Cloisterham Cathedral dominates the town physically and symbolically, but Dickens consistently suggests that its religious authority is hollow. The cathedral's daily round of services provides the rhythm of community life, yet the most pious-seeming of its officers — John Jasper — is the novel's darkest figure. The cathedral's music, which should uplift the soul, becomes associated with Jasper's opium trance and his obsessive desire for Rosa. The institution that represents moral order is, in practice, a stage on which hypocrisy performs itself [001-080].

## Mr. Sapsea and Respectable Stupidity

Mr. Sapsea, the auctioneer and mayor, is Dickens' most concentrated satire of respectable hypocrisy. Pompous, self-satisfied, and intellectually vacant, Sapsea embodies the conviction that social position is equivalent to moral worth. His monumental self-regard — epitomized by the epitaph he composes for his wife, praising himself — makes him comic but also dangerous: his judgment carries weight in Cloisterham, and his prejudices help condemn Neville Landless. Sapsea demonstrates that hypocrisy does not require intelligence — only the unshakeable belief in one's own virtue [004-050].

## Philanthropy as Fashion

The novel's subplot about Honeythunder's philanthropic society exposes a different form of hypocrisy: the use of charitable causes as platforms for self-display. The philanthropists who gather at Honeythunder's meetings speak loudly of their concern for the oppressed while demonstrating no genuine empathy for actual suffering individuals. Neville Landless, their supposed beneficiary, finds their attentions coercive rather than caring. Dickens, always suspicious of institutional benevolence, suggests that organized philanthropy can be another form of moral theater [017-030].
