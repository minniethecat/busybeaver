# Durdles

## Character Overview

Durdles is the stonemason of [[cloisterham-cathedral|Cloisterham Cathedral]], a man who knows every stone, every crypt, every hidden corner of the ancient building. "Durdles is a stonemason; chiefly in the gravestone, tomb, and monument way" [004-048], the narrator explains, and he "leads a hazy, gipsy sort of life" [004-049], carrying his dinner in a bundle and sleeping where he can. His intimate knowledge of the cathedral's physical structure makes him a potentially crucial witness — or participant — in the novel's central mystery.

Dickens draws him with characteristic sympathy for the working-class eccentric: drunken, rough-spoken, and possessed of a craftsman's deep, embodied knowledge that the educated characters lack. "Cloisterham knows me as Durdles, Mr. Jasper" [004-072], he declares, asserting a local identity that is both proud and precarious.

The townspeople call him "Stony Durdles" [004-071] — a nickname that fuses the man with his material, as if years among the tombstones have petrified him into something half-human. His relationship with [[deputy|Deputy]], the street boy who throws stones at him to keep him from drinking, provides the novel's richest vein of dark comedy. But beneath the comedy lies serious narrative function: Durdles knows the crypt where [[edwin-drood|Edwin]] may have met his end, and his tour of it with [[john-jasper|Jasper]] is one of the novel's most atmospheric scenes.

## The Crypt Tour

Durdles's most significant scene comes in Chapters 4 and 5, where [[john-jasper|Jasper]] invites him for a glass of wine and questions him about the cathedral crypt. [[john-jasper|Jasper]]'s interest is intense and peculiar — he wants to understand the quicklime, the hidden spaces, the acoustics of the buried chambers. "Durdles knows where it's to be put, Mr. Jasper" [004-063], the stonemason says of the crypt key, his knowledge both professional and, in the context of the novel's mystery, ominous.

The scene is thick with foreshadowing. [[john-jasper|Jasper]] asks about the "old 'uns" buried in the crypt and receives Durdles's professional assessment: they are "all a muchness, I expect. They all belong to monuments of one kind or another" [004-070]. The stonemason's indifference to the dead — they are simply material for his trade — contrasts sharply with [[john-jasper|Jasper]]'s obsessive interest in the crypt's potential for concealment.

Durdles describes his solitary crypt work with matter-of-fact precision: "You get among them Tombs, and the old coffins, and the old bones, and the old dust" [004-057]. Mr. Jasper "knows what Durdles means" — the bitterness, the isolation, "down in the crypt among the old coffins and the old bones" [004-059]. The exchange establishes the crypt as a space of death and secrecy, with Durdles as its reluctant gatekeeper.

## The Stone-Throwing Ritual

The relationship between Durdles and [[deputy|Deputy]] is one of Dickens's most inventive comic creations. "The relations between Durdles and Deputy are of a capricious kind" [005-021], the narrator observes with characteristic understatement. For a small payment, Deputy pelts Durdles with stones whenever he sees the stonemason drinking — an arrangement that is "agreed upon, as a caution to Durdles to stand clear if he can" [005-011].

Durdles emerges from the Travellers' Twopenny "dinner-bundle and all, leaning against the iron railings" [005-001], a picture of alcoholic dissolution that is at once comic and pathetic. His protestations to Deputy — "Own brother, sir" [005-024], claiming kinship with the boy who torments him — are met with the child's single form of contradiction: "Yer lie, I did" [005-023].

The stone-throwing serves a double narrative purpose. On the surface, it is pure Dickensian comedy: the drunken stonemason pelted by the street urchin, their "capricious" relations [005-021] playing out on [[cloisterham|Cloisterham]]'s nighttime streets. But the stones are also a form of surveillance — Deputy watches Durdles, and Durdles watches the crypt. In a novel about watching and being watched, the stone-throwing ritual is one link in a chain of observation that runs from the cathedral tower to the opium den.

## Craftsmanship and Knowledge

Durdles possesses a form of knowledge that the novel's educated characters cannot access. "When Durdles puts a touch or a finish upon his work, no one in Cloisterham can approach him" [004-065]. His expertise is tactile, spatial, and irreducibly physical — he knows the cathedral not as an abstraction but as a body of stone, "to a eighth of a inch" [004-053].

This knowledge makes him valuable to [[john-jasper|Jasper]], who questions him with an intensity that disturbs even Durdles: "Why, Durdles!" exclaims Jasper, looking on him in astonishment [004-067]. The choirmaster's interest in the crypt's quicklime — a substance used to accelerate decomposition — has suggested to generations of readers that [[john-jasper|Jasper]] is planning a murder that will leave no trace.
