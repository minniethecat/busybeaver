Peartree is a minor character in Charles Dickens's unfinished novel *The Mystery of Edwin Drood*. He is a member of the Eight Club, a London dining society, and a surgeon affiliated with the Royal College of Surgeons [024-007].

Peartree is described as attending "the poor gratis whenever they want him, and is not the parish doctor" [024-007]. The narrator, Mr. Sapsea, notes that Peartree had been "in India with the soldiers" and describes him physically as "a brown hulking sort of revolutionary subject" [024-009].

Within the Eight Club, Peartree maintains a "sickly sort of feebleminded alliance" with another member named Kimber [024-008]. When Kimber's goods were auctioned off, Peartree purchased several lots which later appeared in Kimber's lodgings, suggesting a quiet act of charity disguised as a loan [024-009]. During the club's discussion of a stranger who mistook Sapsea for a clergyman, Peartree is the first to declare the young man a fool [024-017], joining the general mockery that ultimately drives Sapsea to withdraw from the club [024-021].
