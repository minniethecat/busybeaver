## Overview

The betrothal of Edwin Drood and Rosa Bud was arranged by their late fathers, who "fast friends" in life, bound their infant children to each other in a kind of posthumous wish-fulfillment. The betrothal grew out of Edwin's father's "year of mental distress" following his wife's death, "soothed by his fast friend" (Rosa's father), who proposed the union of their children [003-048]. What was intended as a bond of love becomes, for both parties, a source of discomfort — a "false position" from which neither can freely escape until Mr. Grewgious facilitates their release.

## The Arrangement

The betrothal was formalized in Rosa's father's will, a certified copy of which Mr. Grewgious transmits to Edwin. As Grewgious explains: the two young people "can only be betrothed in marriage" under the will's terms [009-074]. Edwin, upon hearing the story of the betrothal repeated aloud, reacts with a mixture of humor and discomfort: "By George! [...] Every boy in the eight clubs would have my portrait for the sign of The Betrothed's Head" [003-048].

## Rosa's Unease

Rosa finds herself "in a false position altogether as to her marriage engagement" [009-016]. She is never free from uneasiness with Edwin, and the betrothal weighs on her as an obligation she has not chosen. Her discomfort is amplified by Jasper's unsettling attention — she is betrothed to one man while being watched obsessively by another. Grewgious, observing her distress, becomes the agent of her liberation.

## The Mutual Release

In Chapter 13, Edwin and Rosa confront their shared unhappiness. Edwin begins: "You are not truly happy in our engagement; I am not truly happy in it" [013-023]. Rosa, weeping, agrees. They dissolve the betrothal by mutual consent — a rare moment of honest emotional reckoning in the novel. Grewgious later confirms: Rosa, "though so long betrothed, and so long recognising their position to be insupportable" is now free [015-079].

The timing is critical: the mutual release occurs on the very day of Edwin's disappearance, making it one of the last known events of his life. The betrothal's dissolution thus becomes a key element in the mystery — it eliminates Edwin's marriage as a motive for Jasper (who desired Rosa), while simultaneously deepening the question of what happened to Edwin after he left Rosa that night.
