# Deputy

## Character Overview

Deputy is a [[cloisterham|Cloisterham]] street boy employed to shadow the stonemason [[durdles|Durdles]] and throw stones at him whenever he attempts to drink [005-014], [005-016]. He is based at the Travellers' Twopenny lodging house, as the keeper explains: "When we're chock full and the Travellers is all bedded, and nobody else comes knocking at the door, the said young man will be." The man's name "is named Deputy" [005-017], reflecting his function — he deputizes for the absent supervision that [[durdles|Durdles]] cannot provide for himself.

His role is nominally comic — the stone-throwing ritual is one of the novel's most distinctive running gags — but, like everything in *[[edwin-drood|Edwin Drood]]*, the comedy shades into something darker. Deputy is a child of the streets, and his violent, ritualized relationship with [[durdles|Durdles]] reflects the casual [[violence|brutality]] of Victorian working-class life.

He also gives [[princess-puffer|the opium woman]] her unforgettable nickname: "[[princess-puffer|Princess Puffer]]." His eye for the grotesque and his irreverent naming capture something essential about Dickens's own narrative method.

## The Stone-Throwing Arrangement

The arrangement between Deputy and [[durdles|Durdles]] is established in Chapter 5, when [[john-jasper|Jasper]] encounters them on the street. [[durdles|Durdles]] explains the terms with a nod: "'Deputy,' says Durdles" [005-014], and Deputy assents [005-016]. The boy's function is both practical and punitive — he pelts [[durdles|Durdles]] with stones to keep him from the bottle, a form of community supervision rendered as street theater.

The "relations between Durdles and Deputy are of a capricious kind" [005-021], the narrator notes, capturing the unstable equilibrium of their bond. Deputy's single form of polite contradiction — "Yer lie, I did" [005-023] — marks him as a child whose entire social repertoire has been learned on the streets. His shrill response to [[durdles|Durdles]]'s call — "Widdy!" [005-056] — and his catch of the halfpenny payment [005-058] complete the portrait of a child whose life is conducted entirely in the currency of transactions and threats.

## Appearances in the Novel

Deputy's stone-throwing is a recurring motif across the novel's middle chapters. In Chapter 12, [[durdles|Durdles]] "rushes at Deputy, collars him, and tries to bring him back" [012-116], only to receive the boy's characteristic rebuttal: "Yer lie, I didn't!" [012-120]. Deputy protests his innocence with the claim that he had "only jist come out for a breath of fresh air" [012-122], followed by a sharp whistle [012-125] — his signature sound, half bird-call and half defiance.

In Chapter 18, Deputy's identification is succinct: "Deputy. Travellers' Twopenny, 'cross the road" [018-039], and he enters "preceded by a flying oyster-shell" [018-090] — the projectile that is his calling card. His final significant appearance comes in Chapter 23, where his recognition of [[princess-puffer]] as "Princess Puffer" connects [[london|London]]'s East End to [[cloisterham|Cloisterham]]'s streets, making him an unexpected agent of the novel's geographical and thematic convergence.

## Dickens's Street Children

Deputy belongs to a long line of Dickensian street children — the Artful Dodger's country cousin, transplanted from [[london|London]] to the cathedral town. Unlike Oliver Twist, Deputy is not rescued; unlike Jo the crossing-sweeper, he does not die. He simply persists, a permanent feature of [[cloisterham|Cloisterham]]'s nighttime streets, his stones and whistles a reminder that the cathedral town's respectability has an unruly underside.
