The Eight Club is a London dining society in Charles Dickens's unfinished novel *The Mystery of Edwin Drood*. Founded by Mr. Thomas Sapsea, the club is built around a numerical conceit of the number eight: it consists of eight members, meets at eight o'clock during eight months of the year, plays eight games of four-handed cribbage at eightpence per game, and its frugal supper consists of eight portions of each dish — "eight rolls, eight mutton chops, eight pork sausages, eight baked potatoes, eight marrowbones, with eight toasts" [024-003].

The club's known members include its founder Mr. Sapsea, a dancing-master named Kimber, and Peartree, a surgeon of the Royal College of Surgeons [024-007]. Kimber is described as "a commonplace, hopeful sort of man, wholly destitute of dignity or knowledge of the world" [024-004], while Peartree and Kimber share a "sickly sort of feebleminded alliance" [024-008].

The Eight Club serves as a narrative vehicle for Dickens to expose Sapsea's pompous character. During a weekly meeting, Sapsea takes offence when the other members mock a stranger who mistook him for a clergyman. Rising in indignation, Sapsea delivers a characteristically self-important speech and withdraws from the club, declaring: "Gentlemen, until then you cease to be the Eight Club, and must make the best you can of becoming the Seven" [024-022].
