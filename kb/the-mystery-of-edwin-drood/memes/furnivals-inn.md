# Furnival's Inn

## Overview

Furnival's Inn is a London inn of court near Holborn, significant in the novel as the location of [[mr-grewgious|Mr. Grewgious]]'s habitual dining place and, later, as the refuge where [[rosa-bud|Rosa Bud]] finds safety after fleeing [[john-jasper|John Jasper]]. Grewgious tells Rosa that "Furnival's is fireproof, and specially watched and lighted" [020-093]—a description that makes it the architectural antithesis of Jasper's dark Cathedral gatehouse.

## In the Novel

Furnival's Inn first appears as the location of Grewgious's daily routine: "Three hundred days in the year, at least, he crossed over to the hotel in Furnival's Inn for his dinner, and after dinner crossed back again" [011-010]. The inn is thus the site of Grewgious's most consistent social ritual—a small domestic act that testifies to his need for order and regularity.

When [[rosa-bud|Rosa]] flees [[cloisterham|Cloisterham]] and arrives in London, Grewgious arranges for her to stay at Furnival's Inn. He promises her "the prettiest chamber in Furnival's" [020-035] and "ran across to Furnival's without his hat, to give his various directions" [020-040], his urgency betraying the depth of his concern. Later, Grewgious "led her by the hand (with a certain stately awkwardness, as if he were going to walk a minuet) across Holborn, and into Furnival's Inn" [020-087].

For Rosa, the inn becomes something approaching a home: "Some passing idea of living, fireproof, up a good many stairs in Furnival's Inn for the rest of her life, was the only thing in the nature of a plan that occurred to her" [022-056]. The word "fireproof" is both literal and metaphorical—Furnival's Inn is proof against the fires that Jasper represents. It is also at Furnival's that [[mr-tartar|Mr. Tartar]] appears, "walking up and down Furnival's Inn, checking himself when he saw them coming, and advancing towards them" [022-101], suggesting the possibility of a new romantic future for Rosa.

## Significance

Furnival's Inn represents safety through institutional solidity. Unlike the Gothic architecture of [[cloisterham-cathedral|Cloisterham Cathedral]]—ancient, shadowy, full of crypts and hidden passages—Furnival's Inn is modern, fireproof, watched, and lighted. It is the architecture of the law rather than the architecture of faith, and in a novel where faith-based institutions fail (the Cathedral shelters a murderer), the legal institution provides genuine protection.
