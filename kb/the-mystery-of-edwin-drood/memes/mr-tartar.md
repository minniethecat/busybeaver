# Mr. Tartar

## Character Overview

Mr. Tartar is a retired naval officer whose introduction in Chapter 17 offers one of the novel's most vivid character sketches. When [[neville-landless|Neville Landless]] encounters him, Tartar introduces himself with the directness of his profession: "'My name is Tartar'" [017-126]. He lives next door to [[neville-landless|Neville]] in [[london|London]] — "Tartar, corresponding set, next door" [017-128] — in chambers maintained with naval precision.

Tartar represents a model of disciplined masculinity that contrasts sharply with the novel's other male figures. Where [[john-jasper|Jasper]] is secretive and sensual, Tartar is open and self-controlled. Where [[mr-honeythunder|Mr. Honeythunder]] is bombastic, Tartar is quietly competent. His rooms are shipshape, his physical courage unquestionable, and his friendship with [[neville-landless|Neville]] provides the younger man with something he has lacked since arriving in England: a model of adult male behavior that is neither predatory nor performative.

## The Window Rescue

Tartar's most dramatic moment comes in Chapter 17, when he demonstrates his physical courage with startling matter-of-factness. [[neville-landless|Neville]] watches in alarm: "'Where are you going Mr. Tartar? You'll be dashed to pieces!'" [017-142]. Tartar's response — "with a wave of his hand" [017-145] — dismisses the danger while acknowledging the concern. He moves through vertical space — windows, ledges, the exterior of the building — with the ease of a man who has spent his life in ships' rigging, where the ground is never stable and survival depends on sure-footedness.

This physical fearlessness is inseparable from Tartar's moral character. He does what needs doing without drama, without self-congratulation, without the "tremendous voice" that characterizes [[mr-honeythunder|Mr. Honeythunder]]'s every intervention. His courage is not a performance but a habit.

## Friendship with Neville

Tartar's bond with [[neville-landless|Neville]] develops across the novel's later chapters. In Chapter 21, a voice calls out: "'Give me another instant! Tartar!'" [021-021], and Tartar's recognition — "'My old master!' said Mr. Tartar" [021-024] — reveals an unexpected backstory: the retired naval officer once served under Grewgious in some long-ago capacity.

The connection deepens when Tartar's swimming ability is invoked: "'Swimming, you know!' said Mr. Tartar" [021-026], and Grewgious supplies the context: "imagine Mr. Tartar, when he was the smallest of midshipmen" [021-030], learning from the older man. Tartar's confession — "'as I was his fag!' said Mr. Tartar. 'But the truth being that he'" [021-031] — establishes a history of mentorship that now comes full circle. The naval officer who once learned from [[mr-grewgious|Grewgious]] now models for [[neville-landless|Neville]] what honorable manhood looks like.

Grewgious's interest in Tartar's present circumstances — "the pleasure of seeing Mr. Tartar's name as tenant of the top set" [021-035] — and Tartar's confirmation — "'Yes, sir,' returned Mr. Tartar. 'You are right so far'" [021-036] — close the circle of respect between the novel's most admirable male characters. In a novel shadowed by [[john-jasper|Jasper]]'s dark double life, Tartar offers the alternative: a man who is exactly what he appears to be.
