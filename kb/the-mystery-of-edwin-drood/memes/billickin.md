Mrs. Billickin is the keeper of a London lodging-house where Rosa Bud takes rooms after leaving Cloisterham, appearing in Chapter 22 of *The Mystery of Edwin Drood*. She is introduced by the narrator with the characteristically Dickensian observation that her name obscures her identity "as to sex or condition" — a name that tells the reader nothing of who or what she is, and yet proves perfectly suited to its bearer [022-065].

The distinguishing features of Mrs. Billickin's character are her pride in her establishment and her elaborate manner of speech. She comes "languishing" to answer the door, and upon recognising Mr. Grewgious, greets him with a formality that borders on the ceremonious [022-066]–[022-067]. Her conversation is marked by grand pronouncements and evasive qualifications: "I will not deceive you; far from it," she declares, before proceeding to enumerate the various shortcomings of her own accommodation in terms that suggest she considers them evidence of its superiority [022-071].

When Rosa is shown into the front parlour, Mrs. Billickin impresses her into the conversation with a gravity that seems to demand acknowledgment of the solemnity of the occasion [022-074]. At one point she places her hand upon her heart — a gesture that, in Mrs. Billickin's repertoire, signals the profoundest sincerity [022-075]. Her exchanges with Mr. Grewgious, in which she responds to his dry legal manner with ever-mounting solemnity, are among the novel's most purely comic passages [022-077]–[022-080].

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Billickin appears only in Chapter 22, but her scene is memorable. She provides a moment of comic relief in the novel's darker second half, and her lodging-house serves as Rosa's London refuge. Her verbal duels with the imperturbable Mr. Grewgious showcase Dickens's gift for comic dialogue between characters of incompatible rhetorical styles.
