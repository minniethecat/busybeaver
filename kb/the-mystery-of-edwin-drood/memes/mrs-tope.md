Mrs. Tope is the wife of the verger of Cloisterham Cathedral and a minor but warmly drawn character in *The Mystery of Edwin Drood*. She first appears in Chapter 2, when Deputy hails her arrival at Jasper's gate with characteristic irreverence: "And, Lord! here's Mrs. Tope! Lovelier than ever!" [002-053]. She is affectionately nicknamed "Pussy" — a fact she blushingly acknowledges when Deputy teases her about it [002-056].

Mrs. Tope appears most prominently in Chapter 16, when John Jasper falls ill and is tended by both Mr. and Mrs. Tope at their home. She is described as "tearful" in her concern for Jasper, urging him to rest and pressing him to take wine and jelly [016-001]–[016-008]. Her solicitude is almost maternal, and Dickens presents her as a figure of simple, unaffected goodness — the kind of minor domestic character at which he excelled.

The narrator, with characteristic irony, notes that Mrs. Tope would have found Jasper's cryptic remarks "highly mysterious" [016-009]. She also appears in Chapter 14, where she and Mr. Tope are described "daintily sticking sprigs of holly into the cushion-covers" in preparation for Christmas at Minor Canon Corner [014-002]. These small domestic details give Mrs. Tope a vividness that belies her minor role.

## Relationship to Other Characters

Mrs. Tope is the wife of the Cathedral verger, Mr. Tope, and the two are often mentioned as a pair. Her relationship with Jasper is one of deferential care — she is a servant of the Cathedral establishment and treats Jasper with the respect due to the choirmaster, but her kindness toward him in his illness seems genuinely personal rather than merely professional. Deputy's teasing familiarity with her suggests she is a well-known and well-liked figure in the Cathedral close.
