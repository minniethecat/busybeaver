# The Travellers' Twopenny

## Overview

The Travellers' Twopenny is a low lodging house in [[cloisterham|Cloisterham]], situated across what was once the Monastery vineyard from the main town. It is the home of [[deputy|Deputy]] and other street children, and represents the novel's most sustained depiction of Cloisterham's hidden poverty—the underside of the Cathedral town's respectable surface.

## Dickens' Description

Dickens devotes one of his most elaborate descriptive passages to the Travellers' Twopenny, presenting it as "a house all warped and distorted, like the morals of the travellers, with scant remains of a latticework porch over the door, and also of a rustic fence before its stamped-out garden" [005-059]. The architecture mirrors the inhabitants: both are "warped and distorted," both have lost whatever original form they once possessed.

The house's inhabitants are described with characteristic Dickensian grotesquerie: "by reason of the travellers being so bound to the premises by a tender sentiment (or so fond of having a fire by the roadside in the course of the day), that they can never be persuaded or threatened into departure, without violently possessing themselves of some wooden forget-me-not, and bearing it off" [005-059]. The "tender sentiment" is, of course, poverty—the travellers cannot leave because they cannot afford to, and the "wooden forget-me-not" they carry away is a pathetic fragment of the home they will never have.

## In the Novel

The Travellers' Twopenny serves as [[deputy|Deputy]]'s base of operations. He describes himself as "manservant up at the Travellers' Twopenny in Gas Works Garding" [005-017], explaining that "all us manservants at Travellers' Lodgings is named Deputy" [005-017]. The lodging house thus provides the novel's most explicit depiction of child labor and urban poverty—the street children who survive by serving the adult travellers.

The house appears at several key moments. [[durdles|Durdles]] and [[john-jasper|Jasper]] pass it during their nocturnal crypt exploration: "We can't help going round by the Travellers' Twopenny, if we go the short way" [005-032]. Later, [[edwin-drood|Edwin Drood]] and [[mr-crisparkle|Mr. Crisparkle]] pass "the red windows of the Travellers' Twopenny" [012-053] as they walk through the Monks' Vineyard. On the night of Edwin's disappearance, Deputy goes "away in the direction of the Travellers' Lodging House" [014-086]. The house's recurrent presence at moments of nocturnal movement makes it a geographical constant in the novel's nighttime world.

## The Monks' Vineyard

The Travellers' Twopenny is located on the site of what was once the Monastery vineyard, a detail that reinforces the novel's theme of institutional decay. The monks who once cultivated the vineyard are gone; the vineyard has become a lodging house for the destitute. The transformation from sacred space to space of poverty is one of the novel's quietest and most devastating historical observations.

## Significance

The Travellers' Twopenny is the novel's most vivid representation of Cloisterham's hidden poverty. While the Cathedral, the Nuns' House, and Minor Canon Corner represent the town's respectable face, the Twopenny represents the economic reality that respectability conceals. Its "warped and distorted" architecture is a physical manifestation of the social warping that poverty produces.
