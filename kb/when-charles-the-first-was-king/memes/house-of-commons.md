# House of Commons

The House of Commons was the elected lower chamber of the English Parliament, composed of representatives from the counties (knights of the shire) and boroughs (burgesses). During the reign of Charles I, the Commons emerged as the principal forum for constitutional opposition to royal policies, driven by a cadre of Puritan lawyers and country gentlemen whose legal expertise and moral conviction made them formidable opponents of the Crown.

## Overview

By the 17th century, the Commons had acquired — largely by custom and precedent — the exclusive right to initiate taxation. Charles I's attempts to raise revenue without parliamentary consent — through Ship Money, forced loans, and the revival of feudal dues — were therefore not merely unpopular but, in the Commons' view, unconstitutional. The struggle between the King's claim to rule by prerogative and the Commons' claim to control the purse strings was the constitutional heart of the Civil War.

## Structure

The Commons consisted of approximately 500 members: two knights from each of the 40 English counties, and two burgesses from each of the roughly 200 parliamentary boroughs. The franchise was restricted to property-owning males — the famous "forty-shilling freeholders" in the counties — but it was still broader than any other representative assembly in Europe. The Speaker, elected by the members, presided over debates but was expected to be the Commons' servant rather than the Crown's.

## Role in the Civil War

The Commons was the engine of Parliamentary resistance. It was the Commons that impeached Strafford, passed the Grand Remonstrance, and asserted control over the militia. When Charles I entered the chamber with armed men to arrest the Five Members in January 1642 — an act of unprecedented royal intrusion — he triggered the final breach that made war inevitable. The novel observes the three-way split within the Commons: "the King, the Presbyterians, and the Independents" ((023-006)), a division that would eventually allow the Army to purge the House of its Presbyterian majority.

## In the Narrative

The Commons features in the novel as part of the distant political drama that the Yorkshire characters observe from afar. News of its proceedings — the impeachments, the Remonstrances, the King's intrusion — reaches Darrington slowly, filtered through rumour and partisan interpretation. This distance is deliberate: the novel is about how great events are experienced by those who have no power to shape them.

## Historical Significance

The triumph of the Commons in the Civil War established the constitutional principle that the Crown cannot govern without Parliament's consent — a principle that, after the interruption of the Restoration and the crisis of 1688, became the foundation of English and later British constitutional government.
