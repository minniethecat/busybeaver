# Royalist

A political and military allegiance during the English Civil War (1642–1651), denoting those who supported King Charles I against the forces of Parliament. Royalists were also known as Cavaliers or the King's Men.

## Definition

In 17th-century England, a Royalist was one who upheld the authority of the Crown and opposed the Parliamentary cause. Royalism was rooted in the belief that the King ruled by divine right, and that rebellion against the sovereign was both a sin and a crime. The cause was encapsulated in its watchword: loyalty to the King above all else ((013-054)).

## Historical Context

The Royalist cause drew support from the nobility, the established clergy, and rural gentry — particularly in the north and west of England. The movement coalesced around Charles I's personal rule and his resistance to Parliamentary reforms. The King's failed attempt to arrest five MPs in 1642 precipitated open conflict, with Royalist forces rallying at Nottingham. The war would ultimately end in Royalist defeat, culminating in the execution of Charles I at Whitehall in 1649 ((013-054)).

## In the Novel

The novel *When Charles the First Was King* portrays Royalism through the eyes of its Yorkshire characters. The narrator, a simple farmer, is drawn into the Royalist cause through the influence of his friends. Royalist sentiment in the novel is not monolithic — characters express varying degrees of conviction, from passionate devotion to cautious pragmatism.

Parson Drumbleforth articulates the core Royalist position most clearly, declaring himself "a Royalist to my last breath and to the last throb of my heart" while nevertheless acknowledging that the King "hath not been well advised" in certain matters ((013-011)). This nuanced position — personal loyalty to the Crown combined with recognition of grievances — contrasts with the more ardent Royalism of Philip Lisle, who views Drumbleforth as "a fainthearted Royalist at the best" ((013-012)).

The practical dangers of Royalist allegiance in Parliamentarian-leaning towns are vividly depicted when the characters ride into Pontefract "to see how certain of our acquaintance were disposed towards the Royalist cause" — described as "a perilous thing to do" given the townspeople's Parliamentary sympathies ((014-020)).

The narrator's own attitude is more pragmatic: he counsels a companion to "be whatever you please, Royalist or Roundhead, but at present, for the love of Heaven, give us something to eat" — grounding the ideological conflict in the immediate realities of hunger and survival ((016-006)).
