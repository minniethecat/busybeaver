# Cavalier

A term for the aristocratic supporters of King Charles I during the English Civil War. Originally a derogatory Spanish word (caballero), it was embraced by the Royalists as a badge of honour, connoting gallantry, elegance, and unwavering loyalty to the Crown.

## Definition

The Cavalier ideal combined martial valour with courtly refinement. Cavaliers were known for their flowing hair, elaborate dress, and swashbuckling manner — a deliberate contrast to the sober, close-cropped Roundheads. In the novel, a character is described leaning from his horse to accept a white rose, lifting it "to his lips, like the gallant cavalier he was, and placed it above his heart" ((029-062)).

## Historical Context

The Cavalier image was cultivated by the Royalist cause as a recruiting tool and cultural identity. The Cavalier poets — Robert Herrick, Richard Lovelace, Sir John Suckling — celebrated love, honour, and loyalty to the King in verse. On the battlefield, Cavalier cavalry under Prince Rupert were famous for their dashing charges, though their indiscipline could prove costly. The term remains embedded in English cultural memory as shorthand for a lost world of aristocratic grace and doomed nobility.

## In the Novel

The novel presents Cavaliers through the admiring eyes of the yeoman narrator. Philip Lisle embodies the Cavalier ideal — he "was a tall, fine man" who "looked like the sort that order and command other men naturally," with "slender and elegantly" proportioned features ((019-007)). When a young woman asks the narrator "And who was your cavalier?" ((019-007)), the term carries its full romantic and martial weight.

Social tensions between Cavaliers and common folk surface in the novel. A character sneers dismissively: "Oh, I know what these grand Cavalier soldiers are!" ((029-021)), expressing the resentment that ordinary people sometimes felt toward the aristocratic Royalist officers.
