# English Civil War

The English Civil War (1642–1651) was the series of armed conflicts between the forces of King Charles I (the Royalists or Cavaliers) and Parliament (the Parliamentarians or Roundheads) that resulted in the temporary overthrow of the monarchy and the establishment of the Commonwealth under Oliver Cromwell. It is the defining historical event of the novel *When Charles the First Was King*.

## Overview

The war was not a single conflict but three distinct campaigns: the First Civil War (1642–1646), which ended in Royalist defeat and the King's surrender; the Second Civil War (1648), a brief Royalist rising crushed by the New Model Army; and the Third Civil War (1650–1651), which culminated in Charles II's defeat at Worcester. The novel's action takes place during the First Civil War, as seen through the eyes of Yorkshire Royalists who must choose between loyalty to the Crown and the practical concerns of survival.

The war grew from long-standing tensions over taxation, religion, and the limits of royal authority. Charles I's eleven-year Personal Rule (1629–1640), during which he governed without Parliament, his imposition of Ship Money, and his Laudian religious reforms had alienated broad sections of English society. The immediate trigger was the King's attempt to arrest five MPs in the Commons chamber in January 1642 — an act that convinced many that the King could not be trusted with absolute power.

## Background

The political crisis that led to war had deep roots. James I had asserted the divine right of kings, but his son Charles I pursued it with a rigidity that made compromise impossible. The King married a Catholic princess (Henrietta Maria), appointed an archbishop (Laud) whose liturgical reforms struck many as crypto-Papist, and attempted to impose Anglican worship on Presbyterian Scotland — triggering the Bishops' Wars of 1639–1640.

In Yorkshire, the narrator observes that at the time "there were really three forces fighting for the supremacy in England — the King, the Presbyterians, and the Independents" ((023-006)). This three-sided struggle meant that the war was never simply a binary conflict. Moderate Parliamentarians and moderate Royalists often had more in common with each other than with the radicals on their own side.

## Narrative Account

The novel presents the war from the perspective of ordinary Yorkshire people caught up in events beyond their control. William Dale describes himself as one whose "lot it has been to live in somewhat troublous times and be dragged into the concerns thereof sorely against his will" ((001-009)). His narrative voice — a plain yeoman farmer's — strips the war of its political abstractions and reveals it as a matter of loyalty, fear, and survival.

The war enters the narrator's world gradually. First come the political discussions at Darrington, where Parson Drumbleforth declares his Royalist principles ((013-011)). Then come the recruitment drives: Philip Lisle persuades William to join the King's forces ((013-032)). Finally comes the war itself — the march to Pontefract ((014-020)), the letters from the front ((018-035)), and the crushing impact of Marston Moor, where "the Ironsides broke our ranks" ((026-019)).

## Outcome and Consequences

The war ended in total Parliamentarian victory. Charles I was tried by a Parliamentary court and executed on 30 January 1649 — the "fatal scaffold at Whitehall" that haunts the novel's Royalist characters ((013-054)). The monarchy was abolished and England became a Commonwealth under Cromwell. But the Restoration of Charles II in 1660 demonstrated that the constitutional questions raised by the war — the proper limits of royal power, the rights of Parliament, the role of religion in public life — could not be resolved by military victory alone.

For the novel's Yorkshire characters, the war's outcome is tragic. They backed the losing side, and though the novel does not show the aftermath in detail, the reader understands that their world — the world of yeoman farmers, country parsons, and Cavalier gentlemen — was transformed by the conflict they lived through.

## Historical Accuracy

The novel is a work of historical fiction, not a history. Its author, Joseph Smith Fletcher (writing as "the author of *When Charles the First Was King*"), was a Yorkshire journalist and novelist who wrote with deep knowledge of the region's history and geography. The broad historical outline — the battles, the political divisions, the religious tensions — is accurate. The fictional characters (William Dale, Philip Lisle, the Drumbleforths) are inventions who interact with real historical figures and events in ways that illuminate rather than distort the historical record.
