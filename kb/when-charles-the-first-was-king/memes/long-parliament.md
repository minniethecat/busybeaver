# Long Parliament

The Long Parliament, summoned by Charles I in November 1640 out of financial desperation, became the instrument of his downfall. It sat — with interruptions — for twenty years, outlasting the King it opposed, the Commonwealth that succeeded him, and the Protectorate that followed, until it was finally dissolved in March 1660 on the eve of the Restoration.

## Overview

The Parliament earned its name by refusing to be dissolved. Having been summoned because the King needed money to fight the Scots, it immediately set about dismantling the apparatus of royal absolutism: the Earl of Strafford was impeached and executed, the Star Chamber and High Commission were abolished, Ship Money was declared illegal, and the Triennial Act required that Parliament be summoned at least every three years. By the time the King attempted to arrest five of its members in January 1642, Parliament had become — in its own eyes — the guardian of English liberties against royal tyranny.

## Structure

The Long Parliament consisted of the House of Commons (elected by property-owning freeholders) and the House of Lords (the peers of the realm and the bishops). The Commons was dominated by Puritans and common lawyers — men like John Pym and John Hampden whose constitutional expertise and religious conviction made them formidable opponents of the Crown. The Lords was more divided, with many peers ultimately siding with the King.

## Role in the Civil War

Parliament's refusal to surrender control of the militia to the King was the immediate cause of war. Throughout the conflict, Parliament raised armies, levied taxes, and conducted diplomacy — effectively functioning as a rival government. The novel notes the complexity of Parliamentary politics: "at this time there were really three forces fighting for the supremacy in England — the King, the Presbyterians, and the Independents" ((023-006)). Parliament contained both Presbyterians (who wanted a state church) and Independents (who wanted toleration), and the struggle between these factions was as bitter as the war against the King.

## In the Narrative

Parliament appears as a distant but powerful force in the novel. The Royalist characters of Yorkshire are aware of its proceedings but view them through the lens of Royalist propaganda and local loyalties. The narrator notes the slow arrival of news: "in our parts we knew very little of the discussion between the King and the Parliament" ((011-012)). This provincial perspective — the war as experienced by men who hear of Parliament's doings weeks after the fact — is one of the novel's most effective historical techniques.

## Historical Significance

The Long Parliament established principles that would define English constitutional government: that taxation requires parliamentary consent, that the King's ministers are accountable to Parliament, and that the monarch is subject to law. Though the Restoration reversed many of its specific acts, the constitutional settlement of 1688–1689 fulfilled much of its programme.
