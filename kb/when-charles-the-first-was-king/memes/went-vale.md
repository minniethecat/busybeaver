# Went Vale

Went Vale is a pastoral valley in Yorkshire that serves as the emotional heart of *When Charles the First Was King*. It is the place where the young [[william-dale|William Dale]] first encounters [[rose-lisle|Rose Lisle]] and [[philip-lisle|Philip Lisle]], and the landscape to which the narrator repeatedly returns in memory and action throughout the novel. As a fictionalised version of the real Went Valley, it represents the rural ideal that the Civil War threatens to destroy.

## Overview

Went Vale is introduced in the novel's opening chapters as the countryside lying between the narrator's farm at Dale's Field and the road to Wentbridge. Its meadows, woods, and the old sheepfold within it form the backdrop for the novel's most idyllic scenes — the childhood meeting with the Lisle children, the narrator's bird's-nesting expeditions, and later, his romantic walks with Rose. The valley's pastoral character is deliberately contrasted with the violence and upheaval of the approaching war.

## Geographic Context

Went Vale lies below Dale's Field, between the farmstead and the village of Wentbridge: "As I ran across the meadows that lie between Dale's Field and the head of Went Vale" ((003-022)). The valley is anchored by landmarks that recur throughout the narrative: the old elm tree above the sheepfold with the stormcock's nest ((003-020)), the road to Wentbridge where the narrator finds the Lisle children on the bridge ((024-009)), and the mill at Wentbridge ((003-023)).

The valley's proximity to Darrington and Pontefract places it squarely within the novel's Yorkshire geography, a patch of rural beauty situated between the domestic space of Dale's Field and the wider world of market towns and military conflict.

## In the Narrative

Went Vale is the setting for some of the novel's most significant personal moments:

- **The First Meeting**: The narrator's childhood encounter with Rose Lisle occurs at the old sheepfold in Went Vale. The stormcock's nest in the elm tree above it draws the young William there, where he finds Rose — a meeting that shapes the entire course of his life ((003-020)).

- **A Romantic Landscape**: Years later, the narrator walks with Rose through the meadows leading towards Went Vale on the evening before his midnight ride to York: "Unconsciously my feet turned in the direction I had taken that spring morning long years before, when I set out for the stormcock's nest" ((024-003)). The valley becomes a touchstone of memory and love.

- **The Road to Wentbridge**: The road along the valley floor is the route by which the narrator travels to and from home, and the place where he later encounters Philip Lisle and Rose on the bridge ((024-009)).

## Historical Significance

The name "Went Vale" is a fictionalised version of the real Went Valley (or Went Vale), the valley of the River Went in Yorkshire's West Riding. The real Wentbridge was an important crossing point on the Great North Road, and the surrounding countryside — a mix of farmland, woodland, and small villages — closely matches the narrator's descriptions. By using real geographical names (Went, Wentbridge) while creating a fictionalised setting, the author achieves the novel's characteristic blend of historical authenticity and narrative freedom.
