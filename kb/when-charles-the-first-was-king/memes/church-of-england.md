# Church of England

The Church of England, established by Henry VIII's break with Rome in 1534, was the official state church of England during the Civil War. Its doctrine of non-resistance to the sovereign and its bishops' seats in the House of Lords made it a pillar of royal authority — and a target of Parliamentarian reformers who sought to abolish episcopacy and remodel the Church along Presbyterian or Independent lines.

## Overview

The Church occupied a middle ground between Roman Catholicism and continental Protestantism — a "via media" that satisfied neither Puritans nor Papists. Its liturgy, codified in the Book of Common Prayer, was in English; its theology was broadly Calvinist; but its governance remained episcopal, with bishops appointed by the Crown. This episcopal structure, and the Church's teaching that resistance to the sovereign was sinful, made the established clergy among the King's most reliable supporters.

## Structure

The Church was governed by the two Archbishops (Canterbury and York) and 26 bishops who sat in the House of Lords. Below them were the parish clergy — rectors and vicars who served the approximately 9,000 parishes of England. The parish was the basic unit of English religious and social life, and men like Parson Drumbleforth of Darrington were the face of the Church for most Englishmen.

## Role in the Civil War

The Church aligned overwhelmingly with the King. Its clergy preached obedience to the sovereign from their pulpits, and many — like Parson Drumbleforth — were Royalists "to the last throb of my heart" ((013-011)). Parliament, dominated by Puritans who wished to abolish episcopacy, viewed the Church hierarchy as a pillar of royal absolutism. The abolition of bishops from the House of Lords (1642) and the eventual proscription of the Prayer Book were among the war's most significant religious outcomes.

## In the Narrative

The Church is a constant presence in the novel's world. William Dale attends Divine service at Darrington Church every Sunday. Parson Drumbleforth's sermons and conversations shape the moral understanding of the younger characters. The Parson's Royalism is explicitly grounded in the Church's teaching: "I am a Royalist... for so I am commanded by my conscience and my reason" ((013-011)). The novel shows the Church not as an abstract institution but as the fabric of parish life — the weekly rhythm of worship, the familiar figure of the parson, the shared prayer book that bound the community together.

## Historical Significance

The Civil War transformed the Church of England. Episcopacy was abolished (1642), the Prayer Book was banned (1645), and many Anglican clergy were ejected from their livings. The Restoration of 1660 restored the Church but on terms that accepted greater diversity of Protestant practice. The war's religious legacy was the principle — however imperfectly realised — that the English state could not enforce uniformity of worship.
