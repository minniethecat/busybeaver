# Thomas Fairfax

Sir Thomas Fairfax (1612–1671) was a Yorkshire-born Parliamentarian general who rose to become commander-in-chief of the New Model Army. Known to his troops as "Black Tom" for his dark complexion and battlefield prowess, he led Parliament's forces to decisive victories at Marston Moor and Naseby, though he opposed the trial and execution of Charles I.

## Overview

Fairfax was one of the few senior Parliamentarian commanders who was also a Yorkshireman — a fact that gave the northern campaign a bitter, almost civil-war-within-a-civil-war quality, as neighbours and former friends found themselves on opposing sides. His military skill, particularly in cavalry command, made him the natural choice for commander-in-chief when the New Model Army was formed in 1645. Yet he remained a moderate in politics, opposing the radical Independents and refusing to sit as a judge at the King's trial.

## Role in the Narrative

Fairfax appears in the novel as a formidable opponent — the Parliamentarian commander whose forces threaten the Royalist hold on Yorkshire. The novel notes the three-sided struggle between "the King, the Presbyterians, and the Independents" ((023-006)), and Fairfax, as a moderate Parliamentarian, occupies a complex position within that conflict.

His victory at Marston Moor, where "our right wing had been equally broken by Cromwell and Fairfax" ((026-017)), is the military event that most directly affects the narrator and his companions. The breaking of the Royalist ranks by the combined forces of Fairfax and Cromwell marks the moment when the war in the north is effectively lost.

## Biographical Details

Born at Denton Hall in Yorkshire's West Riding, Fairfax came from a family with a strong military tradition. He studied at Cambridge and saw military service in the Netherlands before the Civil War. His early successes in Yorkshire established his reputation, and he was appointed commander-in-chief of the New Model Army at age 33. His victories at Naseby (1645) effectively decided the First Civil War. After the war, he opposed the King's execution and resigned his command rather than lead an invasion of Scotland. He lived quietly after the Restoration, his reputation for personal honour largely intact.

## Historical Context

Fairfax represents the moderate Parliamentarian tradition — men who fought to limit the King's prerogative but who never intended to abolish the monarchy or execute the monarch. His career illustrates the tragedy of the Civil War: men of principle on both sides who found themselves driven by events beyond their control. As a Yorkshireman fighting Yorkshiremen, his presence in the novel underlines the divided loyalties that made the Civil War a conflict not just between regions but within communities.
