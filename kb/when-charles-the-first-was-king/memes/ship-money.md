# Ship Money

Ship Money was a medieval tax originally levied on coastal counties to fund naval defence in time of war. Under Charles I, it was controversially extended to inland counties and collected annually during peacetime without Parliamentary approval, becoming a central grievance of the Parliamentarian cause.

## Definition

Traditionally, Ship Money required coastal towns to furnish ships (or their cash equivalent) for the navy. Charles I's innovation was twofold: he extended the levy to inland shires like Yorkshire, and he collected it year after year as a permanent revenue stream, bypassing Parliament's control of taxation. John Hampden's famous refusal to pay and subsequent trial (1637) made Ship Money a national cause célèbre.

## Historical Context

The Ship Money controversy epitomized the constitutional crisis of Charles I's Personal Rule. The King's lawyers argued that the royal prerogative permitted taxation for national defence without Parliamentary consent. Hampden's lawyers countered that only Parliament could lawfully impose taxes. Though the Crown won the case narrowly (7–5), the trial galvanized opposition and contributed directly to the summoning of the Long Parliament in 1640, which declared Ship Money illegal.

## In the Novel

In Yorkshire, news of distant constitutional disputes reaches the countryside slowly and incompletely. The narrator observes that "in our parts we knew very little of the discussion between the King and the Parliament, for news travelled but slowly" ((011-012)). When political discussions do occur, Ship Money features alongside the Star Chamber as an example of royal overreach. An old gentleman at a local gathering warns that "if the King hath the people against him, his army will stand him in little stead" ((013-016)), citing taxation grievances including Ship Money.
