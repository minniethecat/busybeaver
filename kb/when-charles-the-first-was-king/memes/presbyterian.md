# Presbyterian

A major religious and political faction during the English Civil War period, Presbyterians sought to reform the Church of England along Calvinist lines, replacing episcopal government (rule by bishops) with a system of presbyteries — representative councils of ministers and elders.

## Definition

Presbyterianism in 17th-century England was both a theological position and a political programme. Its adherents opposed Archbishop Laud's High Church Anglicanism and the King's ecclesiastical policies, but they also opposed the more radical religious Independents who favoured congregational autonomy. The Presbyterians became the dominant party in the Long Parliament after 1643, allied with the Scots through the Solemn League and Covenant.

## Historical Context

The Presbyterian party reached its zenith between 1643 and 1647, controlling Parliament and directing the war effort. However, their insistence on a uniform national church and their intolerance of dissenting Protestant sects created new conflicts. The novel captures this moment of Presbyterian dominance and its challenges: "now, however, rose up a new party, which combated the cruel intolerance of the Presbyterians, and finally vanquished it" ((023-005)), referring to the Independents who eventually supplanted Presbyterian control.

## In the Novel

The novel portrays the shifting political landscape as news reaches the Royalist characters of divisions among their enemies. The narrator reports that "at this time there were really three forces fighting for the supremacy in England — the King, the Presbyterians, and the Independents" ((023-006)), showing how the Civil War was as much a struggle within the Parliamentary camp as between King and Parliament.
