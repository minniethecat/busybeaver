# Earl of Strafford

Thomas Wentworth, 1st Earl of Strafford (1593–1641), was Charles I's ablest and most ruthless minister. A Yorkshireman by birth, he served as Lord Deputy of Ireland before being recalled to advise the King during the crisis with Parliament. His impeachment, trial, and execution by act of attainder in May 1641 — the King reluctantly signing his death warrant — shocked England and radicalized the political conflict between Crown and Parliament.

## Overview

Strafford began his career as a critic of the Crown but became Charles I's most effective servant. His philosophy of "Thorough" — efficient, centralised government answerable only to the King — made him the most feared man in England. His impeachment by the Long Parliament was a political trial rather than a criminal one: Parliament could not prove treason against him by ordinary legal standards, so it passed a bill of attainder instead. The King's decision to sign Strafford's death warrant — an act he would later describe as his greatest sin — haunted Charles for the rest of his life.

## Role in the Narrative

Strafford is referenced in the political discussions that frame the novel's action. The Parson's observation that "his Majesty hath not been well advised" ((013-011)) echoes the widespread contemporary judgment that the King's advisors — Strafford among them — had steered the Crown toward disaster. Strafford's execution serves as a grim precedent for the King's own fate, and his memory hangs over the Royalist cause as both a warning and a martyrdom.

## Biographical Details

Born at Wentworth Woodhouse in Yorkshire's West Riding, he was educated at Cambridge and the Inns of Court. Elected to Parliament in 1614, he initially opposed the Crown's policies before switching allegiance to royal service. As Lord Deputy of Ireland (1632–1640), he pursued the policy of "Thorough" with characteristic ruthlessness, raising an Irish army that Parliament feared would be used against England. His impeachment and execution in 1641 made him the first major casualty of the constitutional crisis.

## Historical Context

Strafford's execution was a watershed moment. It demonstrated that Parliament was willing to kill the King's closest servants and that the King could be compelled to consent. To Royalists, Strafford was a martyr to parliamentary vindictiveness; to Parliamentarians, he was a would-be tyrant who got what he deserved. His death deepened the mutual distrust that made armed conflict inevitable, and his ghost haunted both sides throughout the war.
