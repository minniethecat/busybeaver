# Parson Drumbleforth

Parson Drumbleforth is the clergyman of Darrington and one of the novel's most nuanced voices. An unwavering Royalist in principle, he nevertheless possesses the intellectual honesty to acknowledge the King's errors — a combination that distinguishes him from less reflective partisans.

## Overview

The Parson is a fixture of the narrator's world from childhood. He sups weekly at Dale's Field, "discoursing upon the crops and news from London," and his sermons and conversations shape the moral understanding of the younger characters. He represents the established Church of England — neither Puritan nor Papist — and his Royalism is rooted in the traditional Anglican doctrine of obedience to the sovereign.

## Role in the Narrative

Parson Drumbleforth delivers the novel's most articulate statement of Royalist principle, one that acknowledges complexity within loyalty. He declares: "I am a Royalist to my last breath and to the last throb of my heart, for so I am commanded by my conscience and my reason. And yet I do think that in this matter his Majesty hath not been well advised and will ultimately suffer" ((013-011)). This combination of steadfast loyalty and honest criticism makes him a moral touchstone.

His political analysis is sharp. He observes that "if the King hath the people against him, his army will stand him in little stead" ((013-016)), voicing the concern that royal policies — particularly Ship Money and the Star Chamber — were alienating the very people whose support the Crown needed most.

Philip Lisle regards the Parson as "a fainthearted Royalist at the best" ((013-012)), but the novel suggests that Drumbleforth's careful reasoning may be wiser than Philip's hot-blooded enthusiasm. The Parson's willingness to admit that "Saul was anything but a blessing to his people" ((013-017)) — likening Charles to the flawed biblical king — shows a mind that places truth above party.

## Biographical Details

The Parson of Darrington Church, where the Dale family attends Divine service every Sunday. He is a regular visitor to Dale's Field and a trusted figure in the community. His social position as a clergyman gives him authority, but his manner is warm and familiar — he sups with farmers and discourses on both spiritual and worldly matters with equal ease.

## Historical Context

Parson Drumbleforth represents the predicament of the Anglican clergy during the Civil War. The Church of England's doctrine of non-resistance to the sovereign made most clergymen natural Royalists, yet many were also aware of the King's shortcomings. After the Parliamentarian victory, many Anglican clergy were ejected from their livings — a fate the reader may sense hanging over Drumbleforth as the narrative progresses. His voice embodies the moderate, thoughtful Royalism that was ultimately swept aside by the more radical forces on both sides of the conflict.
