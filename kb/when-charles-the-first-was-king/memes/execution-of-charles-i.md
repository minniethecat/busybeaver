# Execution of Charles I

The execution of Charles I took place on 30 January 1649 on a scaffold erected outside the Banqueting House at the Palace of Whitehall. It was the first and only public execution of an English monarch, an event that the novel repeatedly invokes as the tragic endpoint of the conflict: "the fatal scaffold at Whitehall" ((013-054)).

## Overview

After his defeat in the First Civil War and the brief Royalist rising of the Second Civil War, Charles I was tried by a specially constituted High Court of Justice on the charge of high treason against the people of England. The King refused to recognise the court's authority — "I would know by what power I am called hither" — but was nonetheless condemned to death. On a bitterly cold January morning, he walked from St James's Palace to Whitehall, delivered a brief speech on the scaffold asserting that he died a "martyr of the people," and was beheaded with a single stroke of the axe.

## Background

The trial of the King followed years of failed negotiation. Charles's duplicity — he had negotiated with the Scots, with the Irish, and with foreign powers while ostensibly seeking a settlement with Parliament — had convinced the Army leadership that he could never be trusted. The Army's purge of Parliament (Pride's Purge, December 1648) removed moderate MPs who favoured continued negotiation, leaving a "Rump Parliament" willing to try the King.

## Narrative Account

The execution lies beyond the chronological scope of the novel's narrative, but it is invoked repeatedly as the defining tragedy toward which events are moving. Parson Drumbleforth declares his Royalism "to the last throb of my heart" while acknowledging that "his Majesty hath not been well advised and will ultimately suffer" ((013-011)). The narrator observes that the King's fate was "the watchword of the cause" ((013-054)) — the one outcome that every Royalist both feared and, in some deep sense, expected.

## Outcome and Consequences

The execution transformed Charles from a defeated and divisive monarch into a martyr. The *Eikon Basilike*, purportedly Charles's spiritual autobiography, became an instant bestseller and helped turn public opinion against the regicides. The abolition of the monarchy proved temporary: the Restoration of 1660 brought Charles II to the throne, but on terms that acknowledged Parliament's constitutional role. The execution established — and the Restoration confirmed — that English monarchy was not absolute.

## Historical Accuracy

The novel's references to the execution are brief but accurate. The "fatal scaffold at Whitehall" ((013-054)) was indeed erected outside the Banqueting House, and Charles did deliver a speech from the scaffold. The novelist's use of the execution as a looming fatal event — always in the background, always approaching — is historically justified: from 1645 onward, it was increasingly clear to contemporaries that reconciliation was unlikely and that the conflict would end in some form of royal tragedy.
