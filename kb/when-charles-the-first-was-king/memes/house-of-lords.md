# House of Lords

The House of Lords was the upper chamber of the English Parliament, composed of the hereditary peers of the realm and (until their abolition in 1642) the bishops of the Church of England. As the traditional council of the Crown, the Lords occupied a constitutional position between the King and the Commons — a position that the Civil War rendered untenable.

## Overview

The Lords was, by its nature, the more conservative house. Its members held their seats by birth rather than election, and the peerage as a class had a vested interest in the social hierarchy over which the King presided. Yet the Lords was not uniformly Royalist: many peers resented Charles I's personal rule, and a significant minority sided with Parliament. The war split the peerage as it split the country.

## Structure

The House consisted of the Lords Temporal (the hereditary peers — dukes, marquises, earls, viscounts, and barons) and the Lords Spiritual (the 26 bishops of the Church of England). The bishops were expelled from the Lords in February 1642, and the House itself was abolished by the Commons in March 1649 following the King's execution — though it was restored, along with the monarchy, in 1660.

## Role in the Civil War

The Lords attempted to mediate between King and Commons, but its middle position became impossible as the conflict escalated. Many peers — including the [[marquis-of-newcastle]] — joined the King and fought in his armies. A smaller number remained at Westminster and provided a veneer of constitutional legitimacy to the Parliamentary cause. The Lords' eventual abolition in 1649 was the logical culmination of the war's assault on the traditional social order.

## Key Figures

- **[[marquis-of-newcastle]]** — Royalist peer who raised an army for the King
- **[[earl-of-strafford]]** — Impeached and executed by Parliament in 1641
- **[[thomas-fairfax]]** — Peer who sided with Parliament

## In the Narrative

The Lords appears only indirectly in the novel — as part of the political world that the Yorkshire characters inhabit but do not directly engage. The presence of noble names (Newcastle, Fairfax, Strafford) among the characters and references reminds the reader that the war was, in part, a conflict within the governing class itself.

## Historical Significance

The abolition of the House of Lords in 1649 was one of the Civil War's most radical acts — a repudiation of hereditary legislative power that was without precedent. Its restoration in 1660 demonstrated that the peerage remained indispensable to the English constitution, but the Lords never again claimed co-equal legislative authority with the Commons.
