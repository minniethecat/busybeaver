# Roundhead

A term for supporters of the Parliament of England during the English Civil War (1642–1651), who opposed the Royalist forces of King Charles I. The name originated as a derogatory term for the close-cropped hair of Puritan apprentices, contrasting with the flowing locks of the Cavaliers.

## Definition

In the context of 17th-century England, a Roundhead was one who sided with Parliament against the King. The term encompassed a broad coalition: Puritans who sought religious reform, merchants and townspeople who resented royal taxation, and those who believed Parliamentary authority should check monarchical power. In the novel, the division is starkly personal — the narrator urges a companion to "be whatever you please, Royalist or Roundhead, but at present, for the love of Heaven, give us something to eat and drink" ((016-006)), grounding the ideological conflict in immediate human need.

## Historical Context

The Roundhead cause drew its strength from the towns, the merchant classes, and the more radical Protestant sects. London and the southeast were predominantly Parliamentarian, as were many market towns like Pontefract, where "taking them as a whole, [the townspeople] were on the side of the Parliament" ((014-020)). The Parliamentarian forces were eventually reorganized into the New Model Army under Sir Thomas Fairfax and Oliver Cromwell, whose Ironsides cavalry proved decisive at Marston Moor and Naseby.

## In the Novel

The novel portrays the Parliamentarian presence as a constant reality in the narrator's Yorkshire world. The townsfolk of Pontefract are largely Parliamentarian, and the Royalist characters must navigate this hostile territory cautiously. Master Oldthwaite, a local figure, "was a Parliamentarian and supported that cause to his utmost" ((013-022)), showing how the political divide cut through local communities.

The military threat of the Roundhead forces is felt throughout the narrative. Jack Drumbleforth, eager for battle, declares "It shall go hard today if we three do not split some Roundhead skulls" ((025-071)) before a major engagement, illustrating the personal animosity between the two sides.

The Parliamentarian magistrates actively work against Royalist recruitment: Ben Tuckett warns that "they were determined, being strong Parliamentarians, to put a stop to your recruiting tactics" ((016-029)). A magistrate in the marketplace concerns the Royalist characters, as "there is a strong party amongst the magistrates who are Parliamentarians to the backbone" ((014-028)).
