# Pontefract Castle

Pontefract Castle is both a real historical fortress and a central location in *When Charles the First Was King*. Situated on a rocky prominence near the narrator's home at Dale's Field, the Castle dominates the landscape and the narrative alike — its two sieges (Chapters 30–31) and eventual surrender (Chapter 38) marking the collapse of Royalist resistance in Yorkshire.

## Overview

The narrator describes Pontefract Castle as "one of the strongest fortresses in England, being almost impregnable on account of its situation. It was built upon a great rock, from the summits of which are to be seen many miles of fair country" ((030-015)). This imposing medieval stronghold, known colloquially as "Pomfret," had been a royal castle since the Norman period — the place where Richard II was imprisoned and murdered in 1400. By the Civil War, it remained one of the most strategically important fortifications in the north of England.

## Geographic Context

Pontefract Castle stands on a rocky hill overlooking the town of Pontefract, approximately four miles from the narrator's farm at Dale's Field. Its elevated position made it a dominant feature of the landscape: "standing at my door of a clear evening, and looking east and northeast, I can behold the two great hills" ((001-006)). The Castle's situation on a great rock made direct assault extremely difficult, contributing to its ability to withstand siege for five months.

The Castle's proximity to the narrator's home means that its fate directly affects the Dale family and their neighbours. When the Parliamentarian forces invest the Castle, the war ceases to be a distant affair and becomes an immediate, daily reality.

## In the Narrative

### The First Siege (Chapter 30)

The investment of Pontefract Castle by Parliamentarian forces under Colonel Sands marks the beginning of active warfare in the narrator's immediate vicinity. News arrives that "Colonel Sands fell in with a foraging party from the Castle yesterday, and killed some, captured others, took all the cattle, and made forty horse prisoners" ((030-011)). The narrator and his companions, being Royalists, regard the siege with alarm: "though we had expected that Pontefract Castle would be besieged sooner or later, there had been such delay in the commencement of active operations that we had begun to think the enemy were never coming to decisive action" ((030-012)).

### The Second Siege (Chapter 31)

The second siege intensifies the pressure on the garrison. Through the siege chapters, the novel offers a ground-level view of siege warfare — the diminishing supplies, the failed hopes of relief, and the psychological toll on the defenders. [[ben-tuckett|Ben Tuckett]]'s lament that the Roundheads have occupied his house in the Marketplace ((038-014)) captures the civilian cost of the military standoff.

### The Surrender (Chapter 38)

After five months of siege, with the Royalist cause collapsing after the defeat at Naseby, the garrison has no choice but to yield. General Poyntz, the Parliamentarian commander, sends word that "the King's army had been totally defeated at Naseby" ((038-021)). The narrator records the grim conclusion: "So now our last hopes were fled, and there was naught for it but to make an honourable surrender. We had defended the Castle for a space of five months, and during that time we had slain over a thousand of the enemy" ((038-025)).

The Castle's fall marks the effective end of Royalist military power in Yorkshire and the close of the novel's military chapters.

## Historical Significance

Historically, Pontefract Castle was one of the most important Royalist garrisons in the north. It withstood multiple sieges between 1644 and 1649, becoming the last Royalist stronghold in England to surrender — holding out even after the execution of Charles I. The novel compresses the historical timeline somewhat but faithfully captures the Castle's formidable defences, its strategic importance, and the grim determination of its garrison.

After the Civil War, Parliament ordered the Castle "slighted" — deliberately demolished to prevent further military use. The narrator's description of it as "now reduced to ruins" ((030-015)) reflects this post-war reality, lending historical authenticity to the fictional memoir.

## Notable Features

- **The Great Rock** — the natural foundation on which the Castle is built, making it "almost impregnable"
- **The Towers and Battlements** — commanding views over miles of countryside
- **The Keep** — the central stronghold
- **The Castle Mill** — mentioned as the site of a skirmish and prisoner capture ((038-020))
