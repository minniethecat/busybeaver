# John Hampden

John Hampden (1595–1643) was a Buckinghamshire gentleman and MP whose refusal to pay Ship Money in 1637 made him one of the most famous men in England. His trial established him as the pre-eminent symbol of constitutional resistance to royal absolutism, and his death from wounds received at the Battle of Chalgrove Field made him a Parliamentarian martyr.

## Overview

Hampden's fame rested on a single act of principled defiance: his refusal to pay the 20 shillings of Ship Money that Charles I had imposed without parliamentary consent. Though he lost the legal case (seven judges to five), the trial transformed him from a country gentleman into a national figure. His cousin Oliver Cromwell said of him that "he was a man of the most absolute integrity." His death in 1643, early in the war, deprived Parliament of one of its most respected moderate voices.

## Role in the Narrative

Hampden appears in the novel as a name invoked in political discussions — a symbol of the Parliamentary cause that the Royalist characters oppose. The novel notes the complex politics of the period: "at this time there were really three forces fighting for the supremacy in England — the King, the Presbyterians, and the Independents" ((023-006)). Hampden, as a moderate, represented a Parliamentary position that many Englishmen might have accepted had the war not pushed both sides toward extremes.

## Biographical Details

Born in Buckinghamshire to a wealthy gentry family, educated at Oxford and the Inner Temple. Elected MP in 1621. His Ship Money case (R v Hampden, 1637) was the constitutional test case of Charles I's prerogative taxation. He was one of the Five Members whom Charles attempted to arrest in the Commons chamber in January 1642 — the act that made armed conflict almost inevitable. Served as a colonel in the Parliamentarian army and died of wounds sustained at Chalgrove Field.

## Historical Context

Hampden's significance lies in what he represented rather than what he achieved. His principled resistance to Ship Money gave constitutional opposition to the Crown a respectable, gentlemanly face. His early death meant that he was not present for the radicalisation of the Parliamentary cause — the rise of the Independents, the New Model Army, the King's trial — and he has therefore been remembered as a moderate who might have prevented the excesses of the Revolution. Whether this is true or merely the romance of "what might have been" is debated, but his symbolic importance to the Parliamentarian cause is beyond question.
