# Parliamentarian

A political allegiance during the English Civil War denoting those who supported the cause of Parliament against King Charles I. While often used interchangeably with "Roundhead," the term Parliamentarian emphasizes the constitutional and political dimension of the conflict rather than its religious or cultural aspects.

## Definition

A Parliamentarian believed that the King's authority should be limited by law and that Parliament — not the Crown alone — should govern England. This position was rooted in centuries of English constitutional tradition, amplified by resistance to Charles I's personal rule, his imposition of taxes without Parliamentary consent, and his use of prerogative courts such as the Star Chamber.

## Historical Context

The Parliamentarian cause emerged from decades of tension between Crown and Parliament. Charles I's eleven-year Personal Rule (1629–1640), during which he governed without summoning Parliament, galvanized opposition. The King's attempt to arrest five MPs in the House of Commons in January 1642 marked the point of no return. Parliamentarian forces, initially loosely organized, eventually triumphed through the creation of the New Model Army and the military leadership of Fairfax and Cromwell.

## In the Novel

The novel depicts the Parliamentarian presence as deeply embedded in Yorkshire society. Master Oldthwaite is explicitly identified as a Parliamentarian who "supported that cause to his utmost" ((013-022)). The townspeople of Pontefract, "taking them as a whole, were on the side of the Parliament" ((014-020)), making it perilous for Royalist characters to travel there openly.

The Parliamentarian magistrates are portrayed as a formidable local force: "there is a strong party amongst the magistrates who are Parliamentarians to the backbone" ((014-028)). Ben Tuckett warns the Royalist protagonists that local Parliamentarians "were determined, being strong Parliamentarians, to put a stop to your recruiting tactics" ((016-029)).

Philip Lisle's letter from the front describes an early Parliamentarian defeat: "we did vanquish the Parliamentarians with very great ease" at Powick Bridge, though he notes that Edgehill "was a matter of different complexion" ((018-035)), foreshadowing the eventual Parliamentarian military ascendancy.
