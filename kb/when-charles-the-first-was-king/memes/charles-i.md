# Charles I

Charles I (1600–1649) was King of England, Scotland, and Ireland from 1625 until his execution in 1649. His belief in the divine right of kings, his contentious relationship with Parliament, and his insistence on royal prerogative precipitated the English Civil War. He is the titular figure of the novel *When Charles the First Was King*.

## Overview

Charles Stuart ascended the throne in 1625, inheriting both his father James I's belief in absolute monarchy and mounting political tensions over taxation and religion. His eleven-year Personal Rule (1629–1640), during which he governed without summoning Parliament, his imposition of Ship Money, and his use of the Star Chamber court created the grievances that eventually led to armed conflict. After Royalist defeat in the Civil War, he was tried by a Parliamentary court, condemned to death, and executed on a scaffold outside the Banqueting House at Whitehall — an event that the novel references as a defining moment of the era.

## Role in the Narrative

Though Charles I never appears directly as a speaking character in the novel's Yorkshire scenes, his presence looms over every event. The Royalist characters' loyalty is defined in relation to him. Parson Drumbleforth declares himself "a Royalist to my last breath" on the principle that "so I am commanded by my conscience and my reason" ((013-011)), encapsulating the moral duty to the Crown that motivates the King's supporters. The novel's title itself establishes Charles I as the era's defining figure.

The King's military fortunes directly affect the Yorkshire characters: they learn of his raising of the Royal Standard at Nottingham, follow news of his army's movements, and fight in his cavalry. His ultimate fate — the "fatal scaffold at Whitehall" ((013-054)) — is invoked early in the narrative as the tragic endpoint toward which events are moving.

## Biographical Details

Born at Dunfermline Palace, Scotland, the second son of James VI of Scotland (later James I of England). Became heir apparent after the death of his elder brother Henry in 1612. Married Henrietta Maria of France in 1625. His reign was marked by conflicts with Parliament over taxation (Ship Money, Tonnage and Poundage), religion (Laudian reforms, perceived Catholic sympathies), and royal prerogative (use of Star Chamber). Defeated in the First Civil War (1642–1646), he surrendered to the Scots in 1646, was handed over to Parliament in 1647, escaped briefly in 1647–1648 triggering the Second Civil War, and was tried and executed in January 1649.

## Key Appearances

Though absent from direct scenes, Charles I is referenced throughout the novel:
- The Royal Standard is raised at Nottingham, marking war's beginning ((017-036))
- His strategy and army movements shape Yorkshire campaigns
- His execution at Whitehall is foretold as "the watchword of the cause" ((013-054))
- Local Royalist recruitment efforts are undertaken in his name

## Historical Context

Charles I's historical significance extends beyond his death. His execution shocked Europe and established the precedent that a monarch could be held accountable to law. The subsequent Commonwealth under Cromwell proved short-lived, and the Restoration of Charles II in 1660 restored the monarchy — but on terms that acknowledged Parliament's constitutional role. The novel is set during the period when Charles's fate still hung in the balance, and his subjects had to choose sides in a conflict that would define England's political future.
