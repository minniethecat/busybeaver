# Star Chamber

The Court of Star Chamber was a prerogative court that sat at the Palace of Westminster, originally established to ensure fair justice against powerful defendants, but by the reign of Charles I it had become a symbol of royal absolutism and arbitrary justice.

## Definition

The Star Chamber derived its authority from the royal prerogative rather than common law. Unlike ordinary courts, it could act without a jury and impose punishments including fines, imprisonment, pillory, and mutilation — though not death. Under Charles I, it was used to enforce royal policy and to silence political and religious dissent.

## Historical Context

Under the Stuart kings, and particularly during Charles I's Personal Rule (1629–1640), the Star Chamber became increasingly associated with political repression. It was used to prosecute Puritans who published pamphlets critical of the established church, and to enforce the King's proclamations as if they were law. The court's association with Archbishop Laud's religious policies made it a primary target of Parliamentary reform. The Long Parliament abolished the Star Chamber in July 1641, an act seen as one of the great constitutional victories of the Parliamentarian cause.

## In the Novel

The Star Chamber appears in the novel as a byword for royal oppression. During a political discussion in the Darrington churchyard, a voice from the crowd asks pointedly: "What, to the Star Chamber? Would he find justice there?" ((013-019)) — the implication being that the court was an instrument of injustice, not a place where honest men could expect fair treatment.

The court is mentioned alongside other grievances against Charles I's rule. In a local discussion about the King's policies, an old farmer at Master Oldthwaite's house links the Star Chamber with Ship Money as examples of royal overreach, expressing the common view that "if the King hath the people against him, his army will stand him in little stead" ((013-016)). The reference connects the Star Chamber to the broader pattern of arbitrary government that drove England toward civil war.
