# Treason

Treason — specifically high treason — was the most serious crime in seventeenth-century English law, carrying the penalty of hanging, drawing, and quartering. During the period covered by *When Charles the First Was King*, the concept of treason underwent a radical transformation: Parliament, which had traditionally used treason charges against the King's enemies, turned the charge against the King's own ministers and ultimately against Charles I himself. This inversion of treason's meaning lies at the heart of the novel's political drama.

## Definition and Law

Under the Treason Act of 1351 (25 Edward III), still in force during the Civil War, high treason encompassed compassing or imagining the death of the King, levying war against the King, or adhering to the King's enemies. During the Tudor period, the scope of treason had been expanded by successive statutes, and by the seventeenth century the charge had become a powerful political weapon. The key ambiguity — one exploited by both sides during the Civil War — was whether "levying war against the King" meant war against the King's person or war against the King's authority as exercised through Parliament.

## In the Novel

The novel engages with treason at several levels. Philip Lisle's recruitment of soldiers for the King ((013-032)) — an act of loyalty from one perspective — is treason from Parliament's. The Royalists are labelled "traitors" by the Parliamentarians throughout the novel, and the recurring tension between legal legitimacy and military power shapes characters' decisions.

The narrator's own position is precarious: by carrying despatches for [[captain-trevor|Captain Trevor]] and later serving in the Royalist garrison at [[pontefract-castle|Pontefract Castle]], he commits acts that Parliament would certainly judge treasonable. The Vicar of Darrington, [[parson-drumbleforth|Parson Drumbleforth]], attempts to navigate a middle path — loyal to the King but troubled by Royalist excesses ((013-011)) — illustrating the moral complexity that the treason question imposed on ordinary people.

The concept's most dramatic presence in the novel comes in the London chapters, when the narrator witnesses the trial and execution of Charles I. The King is tried in the name of the people for "high treason against the realm" — a charge that, under traditional law, was a legal impossibility, since the King was the source of law. The narrator, watching from [[master-goodfellow|Master Goodfellow]]'s side at Westminster Hall ((042-013)), witnesses the moment when the old understanding of treason is replaced by a new one.

## Historical Context

The Earl of Strafford, Charles I's chief minister, was executed for treason in 1641 — the first time a King's minister had been condemned by Parliament for acts done in the King's service. Archbishop Laud followed in 1645. The culminating act was the trial of Charles I himself in January 1649, when the King was charged with "treason and other high crimes" against the people of England. The prosecution argued that the King had levied war "against his Parliament and people," inverting the traditional understanding that the King embodied the realm.

This transformation of treason — from a crime against the monarch to a crime against the people as represented by Parliament — was one of the Civil War's most enduring legacies. It established the principle, fundamental to later constitutional development, that sovereignty resided not in the King alone but in the King-in-Parliament.
