# Master Goodfellow

Master Goodfellow is a fictional character in *When Charles the First Was King* who serves as the narrator's host and guide during the London chapters of the novel. A verger at Westminster Abbey, he provides [[william-dale|William Dale]] with lodging, companionship, and a Royalist perspective on the capital during the momentous winter of 1648–49, when Charles I was tried and executed.

## Overview

Though a minor character in terms of narrative space, Master Goodfellow plays a crucial structural role: he is the narrator's anchor in London, a city otherwise alien and overwhelming to the Yorkshireman William Dale. His position as verger of Westminster Abbey places him at the physical and symbolic heart of the conflict between King and Parliament. His quiet Royalist loyalty — maintained while serving an institution that Parliament now controls — mirrors the predicament of many ordinary Englishmen caught between opposing allegiances.

## Role in the Narrative

Master Goodfellow enters the story when William Dale arrives in London to seek compensation for the despoiling of his property. He is introduced through a recommendation: "this Master Goodfellow with whom I had taken up my lodging was a verger at the great Abbey of Westminster, and had been recommended to me as a good Royalist by our Vicar, who had known him in times past" ((042-004)).

Goodfellow provides Dale not only with a room but with companionship and orientation in the bewildering city. On the morning of 21 January 1649, the two men rise early together and make their way to Westminster Hall: "Master Goodfellow and I rose early and made our way to the entrance of the great hall, where his Majesty was to appear before the men who had constituted themselves his judges" ((042-013)). Goodfellow thus serves as the narrator's witness-partner at one of the most consequential events in English history — the trial of Charles I.

After the King's execution on 30 January, Dale remains at Goodfellow's house for a few more days, attending to business: "on the 2nd of February, being the third day after the King's execution, I said farewell to Master Goodfellow and his wife and set forth upon my homeward journey" ((044-041)). Even after leaving, the narrator carries a piece of Goodfellow with him: he has picked up the habit of pipe-smoking, "having been inducted into it by Master Goodfellow" ((045-032)).

## Biographical Details

Master Goodfellow is a married man living in Westminster; his wife, Mistress Goodfellow, is mentioned alongside him. He holds the office of verger — a lay attendant — at Westminster Abbey, one of the most politically and religiously significant churches in England. His Royalist sympathies, vouched for by the Dale family's Vicar, mark him as part of the quiet network of loyalty that persisted in London even under Parliamentarian control.

## Historical Context

As a verger of Westminster Abbey, Master Goodfellow occupies a historically precise position. Westminster Abbey was a Royal Peculiar — directly under the monarch's authority, not that of any bishop — making it a natural haven for Royalist sentiment. During the Civil War and Interregnum, the Abbey was purged of its dean and chapter, but lay officers like vergers often remained in post, maintaining the fabric and routines of the church under radically changed circumstances. Goodfellow's ability to remain in his position while quietly harbouring Royalist sympathies reflects the compromises many ordinary people made to survive the revolutionary years.
