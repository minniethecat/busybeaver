# William Dale

William Dale is the narrator and protagonist of *When Charles the First Was King*. A yeoman farmer of modest means but firm principles, he tells his story as "a plain chronicle of the doings of a simple farmer, whose lot it has been to live in somewhat troublous times and be dragged into the concerns thereof sorely against his will" ((001-009)).

## Overview

William Dale begins his narrative by firmly establishing his identity and his place in the world: "I, William Dale, yeoman, was born in the year of grace 1621" ((001-005)). His attachment to his ancestral home — "a right good house of stone" built by his grandfather ((001-005)) — defines his character. He is not a man who seeks adventure or glory; rather, he is pulled into the great events of his time through loyalty to his friends and a sense of duty. His perspective as a yeoman — neither gentry nor peasant — gives the novel its distinctive grounding in the everyday realities of 17th-century rural life.

## Role in the Narrative

As first-person narrator, William Dale is both participant and observer. He describes his beloved Yorkshire countryside with a farmer's eye, recounts battles with a soldier's candour, and reflects on political events with a plain man's common sense. His voice is honest, sometimes humorous, and deeply human. He loves Rose Lisle but steps aside for his friend Philip; he fears battle but fights bravely; he is a Royalist by circumstance and loyalty rather than ideology.

His yeoman identity shapes every aspect of his narration. "Of all parts of this broad-acred land there is none which I so much love or admire as that in which the greater portion of my life hath been spent" ((001-003)) — this deep sense of place provides the novel's moral and emotional centre.

## Biographical Details

Born in 1621 at Dale's Field, a stone farmhouse near Darrington in Yorkshire's West Riding. Son of a yeoman farmer whose family had worked the land for generations. His mother Susannah was the daughter of Master Richard Challoner, a corn-miller of Ackworth. William grew up attending Darrington Church and riding to Pontefract market with his father. He had an older sister, and a close relationship with Jacob, the family's serving-man. His childhood was shaped by the rhythms of farming life, the local feud with the Watsons of Castle Hill, and the sermons of Parson Drumbleforth.

## Key Appearances

As narrator, William appears in every chapter. Key moments include:
- His childhood at Dale's Field and first encounters with Philip Lisle and Rose ((001-003))
- Political discussions at Darrington that draw him toward the Royalist cause ((013-011))
- His decision to join the King's forces despite his natural reluctance
- Riding into Pontefract to gauge Royalist support ((014-020))
- Military service and battle experiences, including Marston Moor
- His enduring love for his home and land throughout the war

## Historical Context

William Dale represents the yeoman class that formed the backbone of rural England. Yeomen like William owned their land, served in parish offices, and possessed the property qualification to vote. They were neither gentry nor labourers — an independent middle class whose allegiance was courted by both sides in the Civil War. William's narrative voice, with its plain speech and practical wisdom, embodies the yeoman perspective on events that historians usually describe from the viewpoint of kings and generals.
