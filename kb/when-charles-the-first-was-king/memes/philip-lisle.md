# Philip Lisle

Philip Lisle is the novel's archetypal Cavalier — a tall, handsome gentleman whose charm, courage, and passionate Royalism drive much of the narrative's action. The narrator William Dale describes him as a man who "looked like the sort that order and command other men naturally" ((019-007)).

## Overview

Philip Lisle is introduced early as a figure of romance and daring. Known as "Black Phil" for his dark features — "Philip Lisle he was called once upon a time, but now Black Phil, by reason of his dark complexion" — he is both admired and, among Parliamentarian sympathisers, feared. His fervent Royalism makes him a natural recruiter for the King's cause, persuading William Dale and others to join the fight. His courtship of Rose and his friendship with William form the emotional core of the novel's personal drama.

## Role in the Narrative

Philip is the catalyst who draws the reluctant William Dale into the Civil War. His appeal to join the King's forces stirs William's heart: "my heart had felt a great desire to go to the wars, and I was tempted to say so at the time" ((013-032)). Philip's eloquence and conviction make the Royalist cause feel personal and urgent.

He is also a man of action. As a cavalry officer, he serves under Prince Rupert and fights at Edgehill and Marston Moor. His letter from the front describes the early skirmish at Powick Bridge — "we did vanquish the Parliamentarians with very great ease" — but also the sobering reality of Edgehill, which "was a matter of different complexion, and showed me what war is really like" ((018-035)).

Philip's relationship with Parson Drumbleforth reveals a tension in his character: he dismisses the cautious parson as "a fainthearted Royalist at the best" ((013-012)), showing an impatience with moderation that is both his strength and his flaw.

## Biographical Details

A gentleman of the West Riding, Philip Lisle moves in higher social circles than the yeoman narrator. His "slender and elegantly proportioned" build distinguishes him from the sturdy Yorkshire farmers. He has a sister, Rose, whom William Dale secretly loves. His reputation as "Black Phil" suggests both his dark good looks and a certain notoriety — he is known and talked about beyond his immediate circle.

## Historical Context

Philip Lisle represents the Cavalier ideal that was both the strength and weakness of the Royalist cause. His gallantry and personal magnetism attracted followers, but his impatience with caution and compromise reflected the aristocratic culture that struggled to match the discipline of Cromwell's Ironsides. As a fictional character, he embodies the romance and tragedy of the Royalist cause — a cause that inspired deep personal loyalty even as it marched toward defeat.
