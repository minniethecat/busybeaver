# Pikeman

A pikeman was an infantry soldier of the 17th century armed with a pike — a long spear typically 16 to 18 feet in length — whose primary role was to defend musketeers from cavalry charges. Pikemen formed the defensive backbone of both Royalist and Parliamentarian infantry regiments during the English Civil War.

## Definition

The pike was a simple but effective weapon: a long ash shaft tipped with a steel point. Pikemen stood in dense formations, bracing the butts of their pikes against the ground to present a hedge of steel points against charging cavalry. By the Civil War period, the ratio of musketeers to pikemen in a typical regiment was roughly 2:1, reflecting the growing dominance of firearms, but the pike remained essential for protection while musketeers reloaded.

## Historical Context

The pikeman's role evolved from the medieval Scottish schiltron and Swiss pike squares of the Renaissance. In English armies, pikemen were typically the stronger and taller recruits, as the pike required physical strength to wield effectively. The weapon declined after the Civil War with the introduction of the bayonet, which allowed musketeers to defend themselves against cavalry without dedicated pike support.

## In the Novel

The novel's military passages include pikemen in their supporting role. When Captain Flood leads a sortie from Pontefract Castle, he "went out with fifty musketeers" ((032-010)) — the pikemen who would have accompanied them are present by implication, standing guard against Royalist cavalry while the musketeers advanced. The ordinary soldier's experience is part of the novel's authentic texture.
