# Yeoman

In 17th-century England, a yeoman was a farmer who owned and cultivated his own land, occupying a social position between the gentry and the labouring classes. Yeomen were typically freeholders with sufficient property to vote in county elections, forming the backbone of rural English society.

## Definition

A yeoman was defined by land ownership: he worked his own freehold rather than tenanted land. The yeomanry constituted an independent class of small landowners who were neither aristocratic nor poor. William Dale, the novel's narrator, introduces himself plainly: "I, William Dale, yeoman, was born in the year of grace 1621" ((001-005)). This self-identification immediately establishes his social standing — respectable, rooted in the land, but not gentry.

## Historical Context

The yeomanry were a distinctly English phenomenon. Unlike the peasantry of continental Europe, English yeomen owned their land and possessed legal rights. They served as churchwardens, constables, and jurymen in their parishes, forming the administrative backbone of local government. The Civil War divided the yeomanry: some followed their local gentry into the Royalist camp, while others, particularly in Puritan-leaning areas, supported Parliament. The yeoman's independence of mind is a recurring theme in the novel.

## In the Novel

The novel is narrated by a yeoman, and this perspective shapes the entire narrative. William Dale's yeoman identity means he approaches great events with practical, grounded sensibilities. He describes his life as "a plain chronicle of the doings of a simple farmer, whose lot it has been to live in somewhat troublous times and be dragged into the concerns thereof sorely against his will" ((001-009)).

The yeoman's attachment to place is profound. Dale declares that of "all parts of this broad-acred land there is none which I so much love or admire as that in which the greater portion of my life hath been spent" ((001-003)) — his beloved Yorkshire. His house is "a right good house of stone" built by his grandfather ((001-005)), representing generations of continuity on the land.

The yeoman's social world includes the Watsons of Castle Hill, who "were yeomen like ourselves, and had been on the land and in deadly feud with the Dales for many a century" ((002-013)) — showing that yeomen families maintained their own local histories and rivalries independent of the great national conflict.
