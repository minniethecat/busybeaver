# Parliamentarian Army

The Parliamentarian Army was the military force raised by the English Parliament during the First and Second Civil Wars (1642–1649). Initially a collection of regional militias and volunteers, it was reorganised in 1645 into the New Model Army — a professional, centralised fighting force that proved decisive in defeating the Royalists. The Parliamentarian Army appears throughout *When Charles the First Was King* as the antagonist force besieging [[pontefract-castle|Pontefract Castle]] and threatening the Royalist world of the narrator.

## Origins and Structure

When the Civil War began in 1642, Parliament controlled London, the navy, and the wealthier south and east of England. Its initial military forces were raised through the ancient system of Trained Bands — local militias organised by county. These forces, while numerous, were often reluctant to serve far from their home counties. The creation of the New Model Army in 1645, under the command of [[thomas-fairfax|Sir Thomas Fairfax]], replaced this patchwork with a single national force of 22,000 men — professionally officered, regularly paid, and capable of sustained campaigning anywhere in the kingdom.

## In the Novel

The Parliamentarian Army is the ever-present adversary in the novel's Yorkshire setting. The narrator refers to them as "Roundheads," the popular Royalist epithet, and describes their dominance of the region: "they are surrounding him, and you will have your work set" ((024-042)). The Parliamentarian siege of Pontefract Castle — which occupies Chapters 30–31 and culminates in the surrender of Chapter 38 — is the military centrepiece of the novel's second half.

The Parliamentarian commander in the Pontefract sector is General Poyntz, who "sent a drum to the Castle with a letter for Sir William Lowther, in which it was informed us that the King's army had been totally defeated at Naseby" ((038-021)). The novel captures the psychological toll of siege warfare on the Royalist defenders: "We were now indeed in a sad plight. The help which had been so constantly promised to us, and the thought of which had lifted up our hearts in the struggle, came not" ((038-024)).

The narrator's encounter with [[oliver-cromwell|General Cromwell]] after Marston Moor provides a rare direct view of a Parliamentarian leader. Cromwell, while courteous to his prisoner, represents the implacable force that the Royalists cannot overcome.

## Key Figures

- [[thomas-fairfax|Sir Thomas Fairfax]] — Commander-in-Chief of the New Model Army
- [[oliver-cromwell|Oliver Cromwell]] — Lieutenant-General of Horse, later Lord Protector
- General Poyntz — Commander of Parliamentarian forces at Pontefract

## The New Model Army

The New Model Army represented a revolutionary departure in English military history. Unlike earlier forces, it recruited nationally, paid its soldiers regularly, and promoted officers on merit rather than social rank. Its discipline and effectiveness transformed the military balance, enabling the decisive victories at Naseby (1645) and the reduction of Royalist strongholds including Pontefract. After the war, the Army became a political force in its own right, driving the trial and execution of Charles I and dominating English politics until the Restoration in 1660.
