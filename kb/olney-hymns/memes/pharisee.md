## Definition

The [[Pharisee]] in the Olney Hymns serves less as a historical figure and more as a moral type — the person who trusts in his own righteousness and looks down on others. [[John Newton]] uses the Pharisee as the foil for the penitent sinner: where the Pharisee boasts of his merits, the true believer pleads only [[grace]].

## Biblical Foundation

The Pharisees were a Jewish religious sect known for strict adherence to the law and oral tradition. Jesus repeatedly confronted their hypocrisy: "Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you clean the outside of the cup and the plate, but inside they are full of greed and self-indulgence" (Matthew 23:25). The parable of the Pharisee and the tax collector (Luke 18:9–14) is the classic biblical portrait — the Pharisee prays "God, I thank you that I am not like other men," while the tax collector can only beat his breast and cry "God, be merciful to me, a sinner."

## In the Olney Hymns

### Christ's Warning

[[john-newton|Newton]] draws directly from Christ's own warning about Pharisaic influence:

> "The leaven of the Pharisees
> Beware," the Saviour said.
> (094-002)

The metaphor of leaven — a small amount that permeates the whole lump — captures how self-righteousness spreads. [[john-newton|Newton]] treats Pharisaism not as a historical curiosity but as a present spiritual danger: the leaven of self-congratulation can corrupt the whole Christian life.

### The Pharisee and the Sinner

[[john-newton|Newton]] contrasts the Pharisee's self-assessment with the sinner's plea. The Pharisee who "most did owe" (098-004) thought he owed God the least — his debt, in his own eyes, was small, and his credits were large. But Christ's parable reverses the calculus: the one who recognizes the size of his debt is the one who loves much.

### Newton's Personal Application

For [[john-newton|Newton]], the Pharisee was not a distant figure but a mirror. His own conversion from slave trader to pastor was built on the recognition that he was not the Pharisee in the parable — he was the tax collector. The hymns constantly warn against the subtle drift toward self-righteousness that can overtake even the most sincere believer.

## The Pharisee as a Hymnodic Archetype

The Pharisee occupies a distinctive place in the moral vocabulary of eighteenth-century English hymnody. Unlike the abstract personification of vices (Pride, Envy, Sloth) common in earlier religious verse, the Pharisee is a concrete biblical character whose traits — religious punctiliousness masking spiritual pride — were instantly recognizable to Newton's congregation.

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s treatment is notable for its restraint. He does not caricature the Pharisee as a cartoon villain; rather, he presents him as a warning that can be seen in any devout heart. The hymn "Beware of the Leaven of the Pharisees" (Book I, Hymn 94) is not directed outward at hypocrites but inward at the singer: the one who sings the warning is the one most at risk.

This use of the Pharisee as an archetype rather than a historical referent aligns with the broader Evangelical homiletic tradition. John Bunyan's characters in *The Pilgrim's Progress* — Mr. Worldly-Wiseman, Talkative, Ignorance — function similarly as portable moral categories. Newton, who admired Bunyan, brings the same approach into hymn form.

## Historical Context: Anti-Pharisaism in Eighteenth-Century Evangelicalism

The Evangelical Revival of the eighteenth century, in which Newton played a significant part, was in many respects a reaction against what its leaders perceived as the "Pharisaism" of the established church. George Whitefield and John Wesley alike denounced a religion of outward form without inward transformation — precisely what they identified as the leaven of the Pharisees.

Newton, writing hymns for his rural Olney parish, participated in this critique but with characteristic pastoral gentleness. His anti-Pharisaism is not a polemic against rivals but a diagnostic tool for self-examination. The question his Pharisee hymns press upon the singer is not "Who out there is a Pharisee?" but "Is the leaven working in me?"
