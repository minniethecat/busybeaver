## Summary

**The Flood** (Genesis 6–9) is the universal deluge by which God judged a world "filled with violence" (Genesis 6:11), destroying all humanity except [[Noah]] and his family, who were preserved in the ark. In the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], the Flood operates on two theological registers simultaneously: as a warning that divine judgment is real and inescapable for those outside the ark, and as a type of [[salvation]] — the believer, like [[noah|Noah]], is carried safely through the waters of judgment by a divinely appointed means of deliverance.

[[john-newton|John Newton]] draws on the Flood narrative to reinforce the urgency of repentance and the sufficiency of Christ as the antitype of [[noah|Noah]]'s ark. The waters that destroy the unbelieving world are the same waters that bear the ark to safety — a paradox that runs through the hymns' treatment of judgment and [[grace]].

## Biblical Account

The Flood narrative occupies Genesis 6–9, with New Testament reflections in Matthew 24:37–39, Hebrews 11:7, and 1 Peter 3:20–22:

| Passage | Content | Hymn Application |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Genesis 6:5–7 | God's grief over human wickedness | The reality and justice of divine judgment |
| Genesis 7:17–24 | The waters prevail; all flesh dies | The Flood as type of final judgment |
| Genesis 8:1–3 | God remembers [[noah|Noah]]; the waters recede | Divine faithfulness within judgment |
| Hebrews 11:7 | [[noah|Noah]]'s faith condemned the world | [[faith|Faith]] as the differentiating factor |
| 1 Peter 3:20–21 | The Flood as type of baptism | Waters of judgment become waters of salvation |

The hymns treat the Flood as historical event and theological type without separating the two. [[noah|Noah]] was really saved; the world was really judged; and both realities prefigure Christ's work.

## In the Olney Hymns

The Flood appears in the hymns in three distinct theological registers:

### The Flood as Judgment

The most direct treatment places the Flood alongside other biblical judgments as a warning to the unrepentant:

> A few were saved in Noah's ark,
> For many millions drown'd.
> — [318-005]

The arithmetic is sobering: "a few" saved, "many millions" lost. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s point is not to speculate about numbers but to underscore the narrowness of the way of salvation. The same point appears in the New Testament: "few there be that find it" (Matthew 7:14).

### The Flood as Type of Atonement

Several hymns use "flood" imagery for Christ's atoning blood — the waters of judgment become the waters of cleansing:

> If wash'd in that atoning flood,
> We are no more our own.
> — [027-004]

> The sinners plunged beneath that flood
> Lose all their guilty stains.
> — [079-001]

This is a remarkable theological inversion: the Flood that destroyed the world becomes a type of the blood that saves sinners. The same waters that drowned the disobedient cleanse the believing. The hymn-writers are drawing on the rich typological tradition in which [[noah|Noah]]'s ark prefigures Christ — the one safe vessel in the waters of divine judgment.

### The Flood as Spiritual Trial

Beyond the Genesis narrative, "flood" imagery in the hymns describes the believer's experience of overwhelming trial:

> When the great water-floods prevail,
> Leave not my trembling soul!
> — [260-001]

> Through floods and flames the passage lies,
> To your divine abode.
> — [332-002]

Here the Flood functions metaphorically — the believer passes through waters of affliction on the way to glory, just as [[israel|Israel]] passed through the Red Sea and [[noah|Noah]] through the deluge. The typology extends from Genesis to Exodus to the individual spiritual life.

## Theological Significance

The Flood's theological weight in the hymns lies in its demonstration that judgment and salvation are two sides of the same divine action. The waters that destroyed the world saved [[noah|Noah]]; the cross that condemns sin saves the sinner; the second coming that brings wrath to the impenitent brings glory to the believing. The hymns refuse to separate what God has joined — every warning of judgment contains an implied invitation to enter the ark.

> While they perish in the flood,
> You that bear my holy mark,
> Safely reach the shores of rest.
> — [208-003]

The "holy mark" — the seal of the Spirit (Ephesians 1:13) — is what distinguishes those who perish from those who are saved. It is not human merit but divine marking that makes the difference.
