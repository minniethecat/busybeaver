## Definition

[[Prayer]] is the believer's direct address to God — the soul's conversation with its Maker. In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] portrays prayer not as a formal religious duty but as the Christian's native breath: the instinctive cry of a heart that knows its need and its [[saviour|Saviour]].

[[john-newton|Newton]] understood prayer from experience. His Preface records his own "humble prayer to the [[saviour|Lord]] for his blessing upon it" (P02-005), and he requested others "to strive mightily with their prayers to God for me" (P02-006).

## Biblical Foundation

Scripture commands believers to "pray without ceasing" (1 Thessalonians 5:17) and to bring every request to God "by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving" (Philippians 4:6). Christ taught His disciples to pray (Matthew 6:9–13) and promised that whatever they asked in His name would be done (John 14:13–14). The author of Hebrews invites believers to "draw near with confidence to the throne of grace" (Hebrews 4:16).

## In the Olney Hymns

### Prayer as the Sinner's Refuge

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns present prayer as the desperate sinner's first resort:

> Sought thy mercy-seat by prayer;
> Mercy heard and set him free.
> (010-004)

The "mercy-seat" imagery draws from the Old Testament — the golden cover of the ark where atonement was made. The sinner who approaches God's mercy-seat in prayer finds not judgment but release. The sequence is essential: prayer seeks, mercy hears, freedom follows.

### The Prayer of Faith

[[john-newton|Newton]] emphasizes that effective prayer is prayer joined to [[faith]]:

> The prayer of faith will still prevail
> For all the wants of life.
> (015-002)

[[faith|Faith]] is what gives prayer its access. The prayer of faith reaches God when every other channel is dry. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s image of "creature-streams" running dry underscores the point: when human resources fail, the prayer of faith still flows.

### Christ's Intercession

[[john-newton|Newton]] grounds the believer's confidence in prayer not in the quality of the praying but in the worthiness of the intercessor. Believers pray through Christ:

> The incense of his prayers and tears
> Perfumes the holy place.
> (019-008)

Christ's own prayers and tears ascend like incense, and the believer's prayers ascend with His. The metaphor of incense comes from Revelation 8:3–4, where the prayers of the saints rise before God. [[john-newton|Newton]] adds the specific note that Christ's prayers and tears are the fragrant medium through which the believer's prayers become acceptable.

### Prayer in Hymn-Singing

For [[john-newton|Newton]], congregational hymn-singing was itself an act of prayer. The hymns are prayers set to meter — they teach the believer what to ask for and how to ask. "Dear [[saviour|Lord]], while we in prayer are found, / Do thou the manna give" (016-007) — the hymn becomes the prayer it describes.
