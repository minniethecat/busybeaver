## Summary

[[John (Apostle)]] was the son of Zebedee and brother of [[james-apostle|James]], one of the inner circle of Jesus' Twelve Apostles alongside [[peter|Peter]] and [[james-apostle|James]]. Known as "the disciple whom [[saviour|Jesus]] loved" (John 13:23), he is traditionally identified as the author of the Fourth Gospel, three Epistles, and the Book of Revelation. His distinctive theological vocabulary — light, life, love, truth, witness — pervades the New Testament and deeply informs the language of the Olney Hymns.

[[john-newton|Newton]] draws heavily on John's writings, particularly the themes of divine love ("God is love," 1 John 4:8), the vision of the heavenly city (Revelation 21–22), and the testimony of the beloved disciple to the person and work of Christ.

## Biblical Role

John was a fisherman on the Sea of Galilee when [[saviour|Jesus]] called him and [[james-apostle|James]] to follow (Matthew 4:21–22). The two brothers, whom [[saviour|Jesus]] nicknamed "Sons of Thunder" (Mark 3:17), belonged to the inner circle that witnessed the Transfiguration, the raising of Jairus's daughter, and the agony in [[gethsemane|Gethsemane]].

At the Last Supper, John reclined close to [[saviour|Jesus]] (John 13:23). He alone of the Twelve stood near the cross, where [[saviour|Jesus]] entrusted [[mary-magdalene|Mary]] to his care (John 19:26–27). After the [[resurrection|Resurrection]], John ran with [[peter|Peter]] to the empty tomb and "saw and believed" (John 20:8).

According to tradition, John spent his later years in Ephesus, where he wrote the Gospel and Epistles that bear his name, and was exiled to Patmos where he received the Revelation.

## Writings and Theological Vocabulary

John's Gospel emphasizes the divinity of Christ, opening with the majestic declaration that "the Word was God" (John 1:1). His first Epistle develops the themes of love, light, and fellowship with God. Revelation presents the apocalyptic vision of Christ's triumph and the new [[heaven|heavens]] and new earth.

John's distinctive vocabulary — *light*, *life*, *love*, *truth*, *witness*, *glory*, *abide* — supplies the raw material for much of Newton's hymnodic imagery. When Newton sings of "light shining out of darkness" or "abiding in Christ," he is drawing, consciously or not, on the Johannine lexicon.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns resonate with Johannine themes across four dimensions:

### Divine Love

"God is love" (1 John 4:8) undergirds hymns like "How sweet the name of [[saviour|Jesus]] sounds" (PN-057). For Newton as for John, love is not merely an attribute of God but his very nature. The cross is the demonstration of that love: "In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son" (1 John 4:10). Newton's hymns of divine love are essentially meditations on this Johannine text.

### Heavenly Vision

The imagery of the New [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] in Revelation 21–22 shapes [[john-newton|Newton]]'s descriptions of [[heaven]] as a place of eternal light and joy, where "the Lamb is all the light" (cf. Revelation 21:23). John's vision of the river of life, the tree of life, and the eradication of tears, death, and pain provides the eschatological vocabulary for Newton's hymns of longing and hope.

### Witness and Testimony

John's emphasis on bearing witness to what he "heard" and "saw with our eyes" (1 John 1:1) matches [[john-newton|Newton]]'s own experiential approach to hymnody. Newton does not write as a speculative theologian but as a witness — one who has seen, heard, and tasted the grace of God. The Johannine category of *martyria* (witness) shapes Newton's self-understanding as a hymn-writer.

### Light and Darkness

The Johannine contrast between light and darkness (John 1:5) appears throughout the hymns as a metaphor for conversion and sanctification. The sinner is in darkness; Christ is the light; conversion is the passage from one to the other. Newton's most famous hymn, "Amazing Grace," concludes with the hope of being "bright shining as the sun" — a Johannine image of final glorification.

## Theological Significance

John's distinctive contribution is his synthesis of Christ's person (fully divine, fully human) with the believer's experience (abiding in Christ, walking in the light). The Reformers, whom [[john-newton|Newton]] admired, found in John the clearest biblical warrant for justification by faith and the assurance of salvation. John's vision of the Lamb enthroned in Revelation supplies the eschatological horizon against which all of [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns of hope are set. For Newton, John was not merely an apostle but a model: the one who saw most deeply because he loved most intimately.
