## Definition

Holiness in the Olney Hymns has a double reference: it is first an attribute of God — His absolute moral purity, separation from sin, and perfect righteousness — and second the calling of every believer to be "set apart" and progressively conformed to the divine character. [[john-newton|Newton]] never separates these two: because God is holy, His people must be holy; but because they cannot sanctify themselves, Christ's holiness is imputed to them by faith.

## Biblical Basis

The Olney Hymns draw on the full biblical doctrine of holiness, from the Levitical command to the New Testament's declaration of Christ's sanctifying work:

| Passage | Theme | Hymn Connection |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Leviticus 19:2 | "You shall be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy" | The fundamental link between divine holiness and human obligation |
| Isaiah 6:3 | "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts" | The angelic worship that defines God's essential character |
| 1 Peter 1:15–16 | "As He who called you is holy, you also be holy" | The New Testament reiteration of the Levitical command |
| Ephesians 5:25–27 | Christ gave Himself "that He might sanctify her" | Holiness as Christ's gift to the Church |
| Hebrews 12:14 | "Without holiness no one will see the Lord" | The necessity of holiness for final salvation |

God's command to Israel — "You shall be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy" (Leviticus 19:2) — establishes the fundamental link between divine holiness and human obligation. Yet the New Testament also presents holiness as Christ's gift to the Church: "He gave Himself for her, that He might sanctify her, having cleansed her" (Ephesians 5:25–26).

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] treats holiness across the full spectrum of soteriology, from its ground in God's character to its consummation in glory.

### God's Holiness as the Foundation

The Lord is "holy in all His ways" (cf. PN-015). The holiness of God underscores the gravity of sin and the necessity of atonement. Newton never allows the believer to forget that the God who saves is the God who is "of purer eyes than to behold evil" (Habakkuk 1:13). Grace does not lower God's standard; it meets it in Christ.

### Holiness Imputed: Christ Our Righteousness

Through faith, the believer is clothed in Christ's righteousness. "His holiness my beauty is" — this echoes the imputation of Christ's active obedience, a doctrine [[john-newton|Newton]] shared with the Reformed tradition. The believer's standing before God rests not on personal attainment but on the holiness of Another, credited by faith alone. This is the Reformation doctrine of *alien righteousness* — a righteousness *extra nos*, outside ourselves — transposed into hymnody.

### Progressive Sanctification: The Believer's Growth

[[john-newton|Newton]] portrays the Christian life as a gradual growth in holiness — "from strength to strength," "from grace to [[glory|glory]]." This is the work of the Holy Spirit, who "sanctifies and seals" the believer. Newton is careful to avoid both perfectionism (the claim to sinless perfection in this life) and antinomianism (the denial that holiness is necessary). The believer grows, but the growth is slow, uneven, and entirely dependent on grace.

### Holiness and Assurance: The Evidence of Faith

A growing desire for holiness is evidence of genuine faith. [[john-newton|Newton]] counsels believers to examine themselves not by perfection but by the direction of their desires: "Do I long to be holy? Am I grieved by my remaining sin?" The presence of this longing, even amidst failure, is a mark of the new birth. The person who is content with sin has reason to doubt; the person who hates it, even while struggling against it, has reason for assurance.

### Holiness Consummated: The Hope of Glory

Newton consistently directs the believer's gaze forward to the day when sanctification will be complete — when the believer will be "presented faultless before the presence of his glory" (Jude 24). In heaven, holiness will no longer be a struggle but a delight, no longer partial but perfect. This eschatological hope sustains the believer in the slow work of present sanctification.
