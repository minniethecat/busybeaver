## Definition

**Gospel** (from Old English *godspel*, "good news"; Greek εὐαγγέλιον, *euangelion*), in the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], is the glad announcement of [[salvation]] accomplished by [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] and freely offered to sinners. It is not merely the message that begins the Christian life; it is the sustaining [[truth]] that governs the whole of it. [[john-newton|John Newton]], whose own conversion was dramatically marked by a storm at sea and a recalled gospel promise, treats the gospel as the power source of genuine piety — what he called "a gospel conversation" (P02-003). For Newton, the gospel was not a one-time transaction but the abiding atmosphere in which the believer lives, thinks, and grows.

The gospel's twin aspects — objective proclamation and subjective experience — run through the entire Olney collection. It is both the message preached from pulpit and page, and the inward reality tasted by the believing heart.

## Biblical Basis

The hymns reflect the New Testament's rich theology of the gospel:

| Passage | Theme | Hymn Expression |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Romans 1:16 | The gospel as "the power of God unto salvation" | Gospel power that transforms hearts |
| 1 Corinthians 15:1–4 | Christ died for our sins, was buried, rose again | The gospel's core facts affirmed throughout |
| Galatians 1:6–9 | There is no other gospel | The gospel's exclusive sufficiency |
| Mark 1:15 | "Repent and believe the gospel" | The gospel's call to response |

The [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]] emphasize that the gospel is both a message to be believed and a power to be experienced — it is not enough to affirm it doctrinally; one must live in its [[grace]].

## In the Olney Hymns

The gospel appears across the hymns as the defining reality of the Christian's existence:

### The Gospel Proclaimed

The hymns present the gospel as a universal offer, calling all who hear to respond:

> A warning to all / Wherever the gospel shall come; / O hasten and yield.
> — [008-002]

> Thus, where the gospel's preach'd, / And sinners come to hear.
> — [107-003]

Newton's own preaching voice echoes through these lines. The gospel proclamation carries urgency — it is a message with eternal consequences, and delay is spiritually perilous. The call to "hasten and yield" (008-002) reflects the evangelical conviction that the gospel demands an immediate response of [[faith]] and [[repentance]].

### The Gospel Field

This proclaimed gospel is not a narrow gate but a rich territory of provision, open to all:

> Here, in the fair gospel field, / Wells of free salvation stand.
> — [058-002]

The metaphor of the "gospel field" suggests spaciousness, abundance, and accessibility. Wells dug in a field are open to all who thirst — a picture of the gospel's free offer. This image pairs naturally with the Olney hymns' repeated emphasis on [[grace]] as unmerited and freely given.

### The Gospel Walk

True reception of the gospel manifests not only in hearing but in transformed conduct — a "gospel-walk":

> And witnessed by a gospel-walk, / Will not a true profession prove.
> — [097-005]

The term "gospel-walk" is Newton's compact phrase for the integration of doctrine and life. A profession of faith without corresponding conduct is hollow; the gospel remakes the whole person. This echoes the New Testament emphasis that [[faith]] without works is dead.

### The Gospel Throne

The gospel opens access to God's presence — a theme Newton connects to the Old Testament type of Jacob's ladder:

> How Jacob's ladder suit / To the gospel throne of grace; / We are at the foot removed.
> — [009-004]

Here the "gospel throne" is a throne of [[grace]], not of judgment. The believer approaches with confidence because the gospel has reconciled the sinner to God. The imagery of Jacob's ladder — bridging heaven and earth — finds its fulfillment in the gospel's mediation through [[saviour|Christ]].

### Gospel Opposition

Yet this access is contested. The hymns acknowledge that [[satan|Satan]] actively opposes the gospel's reception:

> Too many, who the gospel hear, / Whom Satan blinds and so deceives.
> — [097-004]

Spiritual blindness is presented not as an intellectual deficiency but as a work of the enemy. The gospel must be accompanied by inward illumination if it is to be received — a theme that runs through Newton's theology of effectual calling.

### The Gospel Received

When opposition is overcome and the gospel is personally received, the moment marks a decisive turning point:

> Thus I, when the gospel proclaim'd / The Saviour's great love to lost man.
> — [034-004]

This first-person testimony captures the movement from hearing to believing — the gospel transitions from external message to internal possession. The "Saviour's great love" is the gospel's content; the "I" who receives it is the gospel's target.

### Gospel Peace

The inward fruit of receiving the gospel is a tranquility that surpasses circumstances:

> The balmy dew of gospel peace.
> — [023-004]

The image of "balmy dew" suggests gentleness, refreshment, and divine origin — peace descends from above, not manufactured by human effort. This "gospel peace" is the subjective experience of objective reconciliation.

### The Gospel Attended

The hymns therefore urge not passive exposure but a responsive, attentive hearing:

> Make us timely wise / Thy gospel to attend, / That we may live a life of faith.
> — [102-007]

To "attend" the gospel is to give it weight, to arrange one's life around its claims. The prayerful tone — "Make us timely wise" — acknowledges that even this attentiveness is a gift of [[grace]].
