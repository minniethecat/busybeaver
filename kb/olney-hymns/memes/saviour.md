## Definition

**Saviour** (Greek: σωτήρ, *sōtēr*) is the preeminent title for Jesus Christ in the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], denoting his role as the divine deliverer who rescues sinners from guilt, death, and eternal condemnation through his atoning death on the cross. The term appears more frequently than any other Christological title in the collection — more than "Redeemer," "Lord," or "Friend" — reflecting [[john-newton|John Newton]]'s and [[william-cowper|William Cowper]]'s Evangelical conviction that salvation is exclusively the work of Christ, received by [[faith]] alone.

In eighteenth-century Evangelical usage, "Saviour" carried the full weight of substitutionary atonement: Christ bore the punishment due to sinners, satisfying divine [[justice]] while extending divine [[mercy]]. The hymns consistently portray the Saviour as both transcendent (seated in glory, sovereign over creation) and immanent (near to the brokenhearted, responsive to prayer).

## Biblical Basis

The title "Saviour" rests on a broad scriptural foundation that [[john-newton|Newton]] and [[william-cowper|Cowper]] would have known intimately:

| Passage | Context | Hymn Connection |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Luke 2:11 | "Unto you is born this day...a Saviour, which is Christ the Lord" | The incarnation as the Saviour's entry into human history |
| Titus 2:13–14 | "Looking for that blessed hope...our great God and Saviour Jesus Christ; who gave himself for us" | The Saviour's self-giving death as the ground of redemption |
| 2 Peter 3:18 | "Grow in grace, and in the knowledge of our Lord and Saviour Jesus Christ" | The ongoing relationship between the believer and the Saviour |
| Isaiah 43:11 | "I, even I, am the LORD; and beside me there is no saviour" | The Old Testament background: Yahweh as the sole deliverer of [[israel|Israel]] |
| Acts 4:12 | "Neither is there salvation in any other" | The exclusivity of Christ as Saviour, a recurrent theme in the hymns |

The Old Testament portrays God himself as the Saviour of [[israel|Israel]] (Isaiah 43:11; 45:21), and the hymns seamlessly identify Jesus with this divine role. When [[john-newton|Newton]] writes "God the Saviour dwells in thee" ([058-005]), he echoes the Hebrew Scriptures' insistence that salvation belongs to the Lord alone.

## In the Olney Hymns

The word "Saviour" appears in over 50 of the 348 Olney Hymns, making it the collection's most pervasive Christological term. The hymns deploy it in three distinct theological registers:

### The Saviour's Death

The cross stands at the center of the Saviour's work. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns return repeatedly to the crucifixion as the decisive act of salvation:

> The cross on which the Saviour died
> And conquer'd for his saints,
> This is the tree by faith applied,
> Which sweetens all complaints.
> — [013-005]

Here the cross is both the instrument of death and the site of victory — the Saviour "conquers" by dying. This paradox runs through the collection: the bleeding, dying Saviour is simultaneously the triumphant, reigning Saviour.

### The Saviour's Pardon

Personal experience of forgiveness dominates the hymns' treatment of the Saviour. The speaker typically recounts a moment of conversion or reassurance when the Saviour's pardoning word was heard:

> Struck dumb, and left without a plea,
> I heard my gracious Saviour say,
> "Know, Satan, I this sinner free —
> I died to take his sins away."
> — [077-003]

> Sweet was the time when first I felt
> The Saviour's pard'ning blood
> Applied, to cleanse my soul from guilt,
> And bring me home to God.
> — [043-001]

The Saviour's pardon is always *spoken* — it comes as a word that silences accusation and creates assurance. This reflects the Evangelical emphasis on the preached word as the means by which Christ's benefits are applied.

### The Saviour's Presence

Beyond the once-for-all work of atonement, the hymns portray the Saviour as an abiding presence — one who "shines," "cheers," "appears," and "passes by":

> While thus I lay distress'd, I saw
> The Saviour passing by;
> To him, though fill'd with shame and awe,
> I raised my mournful cry.
> — [082-005]

> Saviour, shine, and cheer my soul;
> Bid my dying hopes revive;
> Make my wounded spirit whole;
> Far away the tempter drive.
> — [044-001]

These verses reflect the ongoing, experiential dimension of salvation: the Saviour who died in the past continues to act in the present, sustaining the believer through trial and temptation.

## Newton vs. Cowper

The two authors of the Olney Hymns share a common Evangelical vocabulary but deploy "Saviour" with distinct emotional registers.

**[[john-newton|John Newton]]** (1725–1807) draws on his own dramatic conversion — from slave-ship captain to Anglican clergyman — to portray the Saviour as the one who rescues the utterly undeserving. His hymns emphasize the *surprise* of grace: the Saviour appears when least expected, and his choice of sinners defies all human expectation of merit. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s "Saviour" is a sovereign, intervening deliverer whose grace is "wondrous" precisely because it is unearned:

> For, when they least expect his aid,
> The Saviour will appear.
> — [006-001]

**[[william-cowper|William Cowper]]** (1731–1800), who struggled throughout his life with severe depression and religious melancholy, writes of the Saviour with a more aching, introspective tone. His hymns often place the Saviour in tension with the speaker's own unworthiness, and the note of assurance is harder-won. Where [[john-newton|Newton]]'s Saviour breaks in from outside, [[william-cowper|Cowper]]'s Saviour must contend with the darkness within:

> Pensive, doubting, fearful heart,
> Hear what Christ the Saviour says;
> Every word should joy impart,
> Change thy mourning into praise.
> — [063-001]

The contrast is not a disagreement in theology but a difference in spiritual temperament — and the hymns are richer for containing both voices.
