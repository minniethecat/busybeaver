## Summary

The Resurrection of [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] is the central miracle of the Christian faith: on the third day after his [[crucifixion]], Jesus rose bodily from the grave, conquering sin and death. This event is the vindication of Christ's claims, the foundation of Christian hope, and the guarantee of the future resurrection of all believers. In the Olney Hymns, the Resurrection is treated both as a historical fact and as the source of present spiritual life — the risen [[Jesus]] is not merely a past figure but a living [[Saviour]] who "snatch'd me from the grave" (PN-062-001).

## Biblical Account

All four Gospels record the Resurrection ([[Matthew]] 28, [[Mark]] 16, [[Luke]] 24, [[John]] 20–21). After Jesus' death on the cross, his body was laid in a tomb belonging to Joseph of Arimathea. On the first day of the week, [[Mary Magdalene]] and other women found the stone rolled away and the tomb empty. An angel announced, "He is not here; he is risen."

Jesus appeared to Mary Magdalene, to the disciples on the road to Emmaus, to the eleven apostles in the upper room, and later to over five hundred witnesses (1 Corinthians 15:6). His resurrection body was physical — he ate, walked, and was touched — yet also glorified, able to appear and disappear at will. Forty days later, he [[Ascension|ascended]] into heaven.

The [[New Testament]] connects Christ's resurrection to the future "resurrection of the dead" (John 5:28–29, 1 Corinthians 15), when all who are in Christ will likewise rise to eternal life.

## In the Olney Hymns

The Olney Hymns present the Resurrection as both a completed historical event and a present spiritual reality. [[John Newton]]'s hymnody repeatedly joins Christ's death and resurrection as a single redemptive act:

> A dying, risen Jesus,
> Seen by the eye of faith.
> — [PN-062-005]

The compound adjective "dying, risen" captures the inseparable link between cross and empty tomb. The resurrection is the believer's ground of confidence:

> That dear Lord
> Who died and is risen
> His pity afford.
> — [PN-250-001]

[[john-newton|Newton]] grounds the general resurrection explicitly in Christ's own words:

> "The resurrection of the dead,
> The life of all who believe."
> — [PN-116-001]

This "resurrection of the dead" is not a vague hope but a direct promise from Jesus (John 11:25). The hymn develops the theme further:

> Our life and resurrection thou,
> Our hope, our joy, our all.
> — [PN-116-007]

Here Christ himself is identified as "resurrection" — not merely the agent of resurrection but its very substance. The personal application is vivid:

> He snatched me from the grave,
> To tell to all around me
> His pow'r to save.
> — [PN-062-001]

The rescue from the grave is both spiritual (conversion) and eschatological (final resurrection). The general judgment scene reinforces this:

> The sinner, sleeping in his grave,
> Shall at my voice awake.
> — [PN-116-003]

This echoes John 5:28–29, where Jesus declares that the dead will hear his voice and rise. The same eschatological vision appears elsewhere:

> In their silent graves now lie:
> Graves will open at his cry.
> — [PN-143-001]

## Theological Significance

In the Olney Hymns, the Resurrection functions as the pivot of Christian experience. It is the proof that Christ's death was efficacious (Romans 4:25: "raised for our justification"), the source of the believer's new life ("snatch'd from the grave"), and the guarantee of future bodily resurrection.

The hymns reflect the standard evangelical use of Christ's resurrection as a type of the believer's spiritual regeneration — dying to sin and rising to new life (Romans 6:4). But [[john-newton|Newton]]'s distinctive contribution is the *presentness* of the risen Christ: Jesus is not a dead founder to be remembered but a living [[Saviour]] to be encountered. The "dying, risen Jesus" (PN-062-005) is both the historical [[saviour|Redeemer]] and the present [[saviour|Lord]].
