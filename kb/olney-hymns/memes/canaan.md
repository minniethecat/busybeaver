## Summary

[[Canaan]] is the land promised by God to [[Abraham]] and his descendants (Genesis 12:7), conquered under [[Joshua]] after the Exodus from [[Egypt]], and settled by the twelve tribes of [[Israel]]. In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] uses Canaan in its typological sense — as a figure of [[Heaven]], the "promised land" toward which the believer journeys through the wilderness of this life.

The historical Canaan was inhabited by various peoples (the Canaanites, Hittites, Amorites, etc.) whom [[israel|Israel]] was commanded to dispossess. The conquest is depicted in the Book of Joshua as a holy war — God's judgment on corrupt nations and his gift of the land to his covenant people.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s treatment of Canaan follows the standard Puritan typology: the Exodus from [[Egypt]] is conversion; the wilderness journey is the Christian life; and Canaan is Heaven. The anticipation of entering Canaan mirrors the believer's hope of glory:

> To enter Canaan soon they hoped;
> But quickly they were brought to stand.
> — [PN-049-002]

The reference is to [[israel|Israel]]'s failure at Kadesh-barnea (Numbers 13–14), where unbelief delayed entry into the land by forty years. The lesson is clear: unbelief postpones blessing.

The conquest itself is invoked as a divine act of judgment and gift:

> Joshua, by God's command,
> Invaded Canaan's guilty land;
> Gibeon, unlike the rest, believed.
> — [PN-021-001]

The story of Gibeon (Joshua 9) — the one Canaanite city that made peace with [[israel|Israel]] through a ruse — illustrates the possibility of mercy even within judgment. [[john-newton|Newton]] also draws on the story of the Canaanite woman (Matthew 15:21–28):

> Yet although from Canaan sprung,
> Though a dog herself she knew,
> She by faith obtained her plea.
> — [PN-088-005]

The Canaanite woman — outside the covenant, a "dog" in Jewish terminology — receives healing for her daughter through persistent faith. The point is not ethnic but theological: [[Faith]], not ancestry, determines access to [[Grace]].

## Canaan as Type of Heaven

The typological reading of Canaan as Heaven is deeply embedded in Christian hymnody. Just as [[israel|Israel]] crossed the Jordan to enter the promised land, so the believer crosses death to enter glory. [[john-newton|Newton]] uses this typology sparingly but effectively — Canaan is always *ahead*, the object of hope, not yet possessed.
