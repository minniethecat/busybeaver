## Identity

**Satan** (Hebrew: "the Adversary"; Greek: "the Accuser") is the chief opponent of God and His people in Scripture. He appears in the Old Testament as the serpent who tempted Eve (Genesis 3), the accuser in the heavenly court (Job 1–2), and the adversary of Israel (1 Chronicles 21:1; Zechariah 3:1–2). In the New Testament, he is identified as "the tempter" (Matthew 4:3), "the evil one" (Matthew 13:19), "a murderer from the beginning" and "the father of lies" (John 8:44), and "the god of this world" (2 Corinthians 4:4). His defeat was accomplished at the cross, where Christ "disarmed the rulers and authorities and put them to open shame" (Colossians 2:15), and his final destruction is certain (Revelation 20:10).

In the Olney Hymns, Satan is a pervasive presence — not as a mythological bogeyman but as a real spiritual adversary whose attacks are experienced in the believer's daily life. [[John Newton]]'s treatment of Satan is characteristically pastoral: Satan accuses, tempts, and threatens, but he is always a defeated enemy whose power is broken by Christ.

## Biblical Narrative

Satan's biblical trajectory moves from accuser to defeated foe. In the Old Testament, he operates within God's permission: he tests Job's faith (Job 1:6–12), he incites David to sin (1 Chronicles 21:1), and he stands to accuse Joshua the high priest (Zechariah 3:1). In the New Testament, his hostility intensifies: he tempts Christ in the wilderness (Matthew 4:1–11), enters Judas Iscariot (Luke 22:3), and wages war against the Church (Ephesians 6:11–12). But the decisive turning point is the cross, where Christ "destroy[ed] the one who has the power of death, that is, the devil" (Hebrews 2:14). The book of Revelation shows his final end: "thrown into the lake of fire and sulfur" (Revelation 20:10).

## In the Olney Hymns

[[John Newton]] portrays Satan as a real and active adversary, but always within a framework of Christ's ultimate victory. Five themes dominate:

**Satan as Accuser.** Newton draws on the legal imagery of Zechariah 3 and Revelation 12, presenting Satan as the believer's prosecutor before God's throne:

> With Satan, my accuser, near,
> My spirit trembled when I saw
> — [PN-077-001]

The legal metaphor is precise: Satan "accuses" and the believer "trembles." But the accusation does not stand, because Christ the Advocate silences every charge.

> At this rebuke the tempter fled;
> — [PN-077-005]

The rebuke is Christ's — not the believer's own argument. Satan is silenced not by human righteousness but by divine intervention.

**Satan as Tempter.** Newton depicts temptation as Satan's primary mode of attack on believers, using the serpentine imagery of Genesis 3:

> As the serpent raised by Moses
> — [PN-061-001]

The bronze serpent (Numbers 21:8–9) becomes a type of Christ: just as the Israelites looked at the raised serpent and lived, so believers look to the crucified Christ and are healed from Satan's venom. The tempter's wiles are real but resistible:

> In vain the tempter spread his wiles,
> — [PN-043-003]

**Satan as Defeated Foe.** Every mention of Satan's power is immediately qualified by Christ's superior power:

> Though I am weak, and Satan strong,
> — [PN-048-003]

The line continues with the resolution: Christ is stronger. Newton never leaves the believer in a state of helpless terror — the admission of weakness is the preamble to a declaration of Christ's sufficiency.

> Now, Satan, thou hast lost thy aim;
> — [PN-077-008]

The triumphant tone is rooted in the accomplished work of Christ. Satan "lost his aim" at Calvary; his present attacks are the thrashing of a defeated enemy.

**Satan and the World.** Newton frequently pairs Satan with "the world" as joint adversaries:

> The world and Satan join their strength,
> — [PN-024-004]

This coupling reflects the Johannine triad of enemies (the world, the flesh, and the devil) and underscores the believer's need for supernatural aid. The alliance of world and Satan makes the Christian life impossible without grace.

> Deliver'd thus from Satan's snare,
> — [PN-091-006]

Deliverance is always passive — the believer does not escape by skill but is "delivered" by another.

**Satan under Divine Sovereignty.** Newton, ever the Calvinist, never allows Satan independent power. Satan operates only within God's permission:

> Thus Satan for a season reigns,
> — [PN-101-004]

The key word is "season" — Satan's reign is bounded by divine decree. It has both a beginning and, more importantly, an end.

## Theological Significance

In Newton's hymnody, Satan serves a paradoxical pastoral purpose: he is the enemy whose attacks drive believers to Christ. The recognition of Satan's reality leads not to morbid fascination but to dependent faith. Newton's treatment avoids both extremes — neither denying the adversary's existence nor granting him more attention than Christ. Satan appears only to be dismissed; he accuses only to be silenced; he tempts only to be overcome. The practical effect is to deepen the believer's reliance on Christ's finished work and intercessory ministry.
