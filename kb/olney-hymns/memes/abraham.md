## Identity

**Abraham** (originally Abram) is the founding patriarch of the [[Israel]]ite people. Called by God to leave his homeland in Ur of the Chaldees, he journeyed to [[Canaan]] where God promised to make his descendants a great nation (Genesis 12:1–3). His name was changed from Abram ("exalted father") to Abraham ("father of many nations") as a sign of this covenant.

Abraham is known for his faith and obedience — most notably in the binding of [[isaac|Isaac]] (Genesis 22), where he demonstrated willingness to sacrifice his promised son at God's command. This act became a defining example of trusting faith in both Jewish and Christian tradition, and it resonates deeply through the Olney Hymns as a type of God's own sacrifice of his Son.

## Biblical Context

Abraham's story spans Genesis 12–25. Key events include:

- **The Call** (Genesis 12:1–9): God calls Abram to leave Ur. He promises land, descendants, and blessing — promises reiterated throughout the narrative.
- **The Covenant** (Genesis 15, 17): God formalizes the covenant. Circumcision becomes its sign. Abram's name is changed to Abraham. Through his offspring all nations shall be blessed.
- **Sodom and Gomorrah** (Genesis 18–19): Abraham intercedes for the righteous. His bold prayer demonstrates concern for divine justice.
- **The Binding of Isaac** (Genesis 22): The Akedah — Abraham's supreme test of faith. On Mount Moriah, God provides a ram as substitute.

Abraham appears throughout Scripture as the paradigmatic person of faith. Paul cites him as justified by faith (Romans 4, Galatians 3). James points to his works as faith's evidence (2:21–24). He holds a central place in Hebrews 11's "hall of faith."

Jesus himself declares, "Abraham rejoiced to see my day" (John 8:56), connecting the patriarch directly to the gospel promise.

## In the Olney Hymns

Abraham is directly referenced in Book I, Hymn 105, where [[John Newton]] describes the death of the righteous believer:

> In Abraham's bosom safely placed,
> Enjoys perpetual rest.
> (105-002)

The image of "Abraham's bosom" draws from Luke 16:22, where the poor man Lazarus is carried there after death — signifying the blessed resting place of the faithful departed. For Newton, this was not mere metaphor but a tangible hope for his congregation.

### Covenant and the Patriarchal Sequence

The Book I hymns on the Patriarchs form a deliberate sequence in Newton's design of the Olney Hymns. Following the scriptural order — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph — these hymns walk the congregation through the covenant narrative as it unfolds in Genesis. Abraham receives the most sustained treatment, which is fitting: he is the recipient of the foundational covenant promise from which all subsequent patriarchal hymns derive their meaning.

Newton's approach to the Abraham narrative is governed by his hermeneutical conviction that the Old Testament is a "picture book of Christ" (*Authentic Narrative*, 1764). The promise to Abraham — "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" — is read not as a future hope still pending but as a promise already fulfilled in Christ's first coming and distributed to the nations through gospel preaching. Every Olney hymn that touches on Abraham therefore carries an implicit missionary impulse: the blessing promised to the patriarch has reached even rural Buckinghamshire.

### Abraham's Bosom: Death and Comfort in Newton's Pastoral Theology

The phrase "Abraham's bosom" (Luke 16:22) held particular pastoral significance for Newton. As a parish minister who buried many of his congregants — and who had himself faced death at sea countless times — Newton understood the existential weight carried by images of the afterlife. "Abraham's bosom" is not a doctrinal abstraction; it is the destination of real people Newton had baptized, married, and buried.

In Hymn 105 of Book I, Newton draws on the traditional association of Abraham's bosom with the intermediate state — the resting place of the faithful soul between death and resurrection. The comfort offered is simple but profound: the believer who dies in Christ does not go to a place of uncertainty but to a place of welcome, where the father of the faithful himself receives incoming souls. For a rural congregation facing high mortality rates, this image carried pastoral power that theological treatises could not match.

### Faith and Providence in the Abraham Hymns

Newton consistently uses Abraham to exemplify the life of faith — a sinner justified not by works, but by trusting in God's promises. In this, the patriarch becomes a mirror for every believer in the Olney congregation: called from a distant land, walking by faith, and resting finally in the bosom of divine mercy.

Abraham also features through the hymns that reflect on the Patriarchs. The [[genesis|Book of Genesis]] narratives of Abraham's call, his covenant with God, and his test of faith each receive dedicated treatment in Book I hymns that draw out lessons of faith and providence.

## Theological Significance

Abraham's faith was not a single act but a lifelong pattern. He believed God's promise of descendants when he was childless (Genesis 15:6), trusted God's provision on Moriah (Genesis 22:8), and died "not having received the things promised, but having seen them and greeted them from afar" (Hebrews 11:13). This progressive, forward-looking faith made him the ideal model for Newton's Calvinistic theology of grace — salvation depends not on human achievement but on trusting the divine promise.

The covenant with Abraham is the root from which the entire redemptive history grows. God's promise — "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" (Genesis 12:3) — finds its fulfillment in Christ, Abraham's seed (Galatians 3:16). The Olney Hymns, with their Christ-centered reading of the Old Testament, implicitly trace this line whenever they invoke the patriarchal narratives.
