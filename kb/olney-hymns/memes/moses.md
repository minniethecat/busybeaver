## Identity

**Moses** (Hebrew: מֹשֶׁה, *Mōšeh*) was the deliverer of [[israel|Israel]] from [[egypt|Egypt]], the mediator of the Law at [[Sinai]], and the greatest prophet of the Old Covenant. In the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], Moses is never a standalone subject — he always appears in relation to something else: his uplifted hands bringing victory over Amalek, his rod striking the rock, his trembling before the thundering law, or the insufficiency of his ministry compared to Christ's.

This is deliberate. For [[john-newton|John Newton]] and [[william-cowper|William Cowper]], Moses represents the law — holy, good, and necessary, yet unable to save. [[eve|Eve]]rything Mosaic in the hymns points beyond itself to Christ, who fulfills what Moses could only foreshadow.

## Biblical Context

Moses dominates Exodus through Deuteronomy, and his presence echoes through the entire Old Testament. The hymns draw on five key Mosaic episodes:

| Episode | Scripture | Typological Meaning |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| The uplifted hands | Exodus 17:8–13 | Intercessory prayer sustains the church's warfare |
| The bronze serpent | Numbers 21:4–9 | Christ lifted up for sin's healing (John 3:14–15) |
| The striking of the rock | Exodus 17:1–7; Numbers 20:1–13 | Christ stricken, from whose side flow living waters |
| [[sinai|Sinai]] and the law | Exodus 19–20 | The terror of the law contrasted with the gentleness of grace |
| The Red Sea crossing | Exodus 14 | [[faith|Faith]]'s passage through apparently impassable obstacles |

Each episode is read typologically — Moses' historical actions prefigure Christ's redemptive work. The hymns never treat the type as autonomous; it is always "slight" and "faint" compared to the antitype (128-003).

## In the Olney Hymns

Moses appears in at least ten hymns, always in a typological or contrastive role:

### The Uplifted Hands

The most developed Mosaic image in the collection comes from Exodus 17, where [[israel|Israel]] prevails against Amalek only while Moses' hands remain raised in prayer:

> While Moses stood with arms spread wide,
> Success was found on Isr'el's side;
> But when through weariness they fail'd,
> That moment Amalek prevail'd.
> — [201-004]

[[john-newton|Newton]] draws a direct ecclesiological application: the church's success in spiritual warfare depends on sustained intercessory prayer. When prayer falters, the enemy advances:

> We now of fleets and armies vaunt,
> And ships and men prepare;
> But men like Moses most we want,
> To save the state by pray'r.
> — [207-005]

The application is pointed — national security depends less on military preparedness than on "men like Moses" who prevail with God on their knees. This was a politically resonant claim in 1779, with Britain at war with the American colonies.

### The Smitten Rock

The rock that yielded water when struck by Moses' rod (Exodus 17:6) becomes a type of Christ's crucifixion:

> In Moses' rod a type they saw
> Of His severe and fiery law;
> The smitten rock prefigured Him
> From whose pierced side all blessings stream.
> — [128-002]

The comparison then immediately notes the inadequacy of the type:

> But ah! the types were all too faint
> His sorrows or His worth to paint;
> Slight was the stroke of Moses' rod,
> But He endured the wrath of God.
> — [128-003]

This self-conscious inadequacy of Old Testament types is characteristic of the hymns' hermeneutic: the type teaches truly, but the antitype exceeds it infinitely.

### The Bronze Serpent

Hymn 61 develops the typology of Numbers 21, where Moses lifted up a bronze serpent to heal those bitten by fiery serpents:

> As the serpent raised by Moses
> Heal'd the burning serpent's bite,
> Jesus thus himself discloses
> To the wounded sinner's sight.
> — [061-001]

This is one of the most explicit Christological typologies in the collection, directly following John 3:14–15: "As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up."

### Sinai and the Law

Moses at [[sinai|Sinai]] represents the terror of the law, deliberately contrasted with the gentleness of the gospel:

> The holy Moses quaked and fear'd
> When Sinai's thund'ring law he heard:
> But reigning grace, with accents mild,
> Speaks to the sinner as a child.
> — [158-003]

The contrast is not between a bad old covenant and a good new one — it is between two modes of divine speech: thunder and gentleness, law and [[grace]]. Moses heard the thunder; the believer hears the gentle voice.

## Theological Significance

Moses functions in the Olney Hymns as the hinge between law and gospel. [[eve|Eve]]ry Mosaic reference makes the same fundamental point: the law came through Moses, but [[grace]] and truth came through [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] (John 1:17). The hymns never denigrate Moses — he is "holy Moses" (158-003) — but they consistently insist that his ministry was preparatory and provisional.

This reflects the classic Reformed law-gospel distinction: the law commands and condemns; the gospel promises and saves. Moses delivers the law from [[sinai|Sinai]]; Christ delivers the gospel from [[crucifixion|Calvary]]. Both are divine; only the second saves.
