## Summary

The Creation is the foundational divine act by which God brought the universe, the earth, and all living things into existence. Described in [[Genesis]] 1–2, it establishes God as the sovereign [[Creator]] and sets the stage for the entire biblical narrative. In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] and [[William Cowper]] reference Creation both as evidence of God's power and wisdom, and as a metaphor for the believer's spiritual transformation — the "new creation" of the soul.

## Biblical Account

The biblical account of Creation occupies the opening chapters of [[Genesis]] (1:1–2:3). God speaks the universe into being over six days: light (Day 1), sky and waters (Day 2), land and vegetation (Day 3), sun, moon, and stars (Day 4), fish and birds (Day 5), and land animals and humanity (Day 6). On the seventh day, God rests, establishing the pattern of the [[Sabbath]].

Key elements of the Creation narrative include: God creating *ex nihilo* (out of nothing) by His word alone; the declaration that creation is "very good"; humanity made in God's image; and the赋予 of dominion over the earth.

## In the Olney Hymns

The Olney Hymns reference Creation in eight passages, spanning both the physical universe and spiritual regeneration:

> The whole creation homage paid,
> And own'd him Lord of all.
> — [PN-001-001]

[[john-newton|Newton]] opens Book I by grounding the hymn's Christology in creation's acknowledgment of its [[saviour|Lord]]. The vastness of creation serves as a backdrop for God's majesty:

> He form'd the vast creation's plan,
> And searches all our hearts.
> — [PN-140-001]

Here creation's design and God's intimate knowledge of human hearts are paired, emphasizing both transcendence and immanence. The hymns also employ creation imagery in eschatological contexts:

> See the creation wrapt in flames,
> First kindled by his ire.
> — [PN-219-004]

This apocalyptic vision portrays creation's end — consumed by fire at the final judgment — mirroring its beginning by divine word:

> Th' Almighty's efficacious word
> Which could the vast creation raise!
> — [PN-221-001]

Most significantly, [[john-newton|Newton]] develops the "new creation" motif as a metaphor for conversion and spiritual renewal:

> The new creation of the soul
> Does now no less his power control.
> — [PN-221-005]

This theme recurs, with the renewed heart compared to a freshly created world:

> A new creation of our own;
> All appears real, though yet unknown.
> — [PN-239-002]

[[william-cowper|Cowper]] echoes this imagery more gently:

> The whole creation wears a smile.
> — [PN-272-002]

## Theological Significance

In the Olney Hymns, Creation serves a dual theological function. First, it establishes God's absolute sovereignty — the One who spoke the universe into being is worthy of worship and obedience. Second, and more distinctively, Creation provides the controlling metaphor for [[Regeneration]] and spiritual renewal. Just as God once called light out of darkness in the physical realm, so He calls the sinner from spiritual death to new life. This "new creation" theology, rooted in 2 Corinthians 5:17, is one of the most characteristic themes of [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymnody.

The movement from original Creation (PN-001-001) through creation's judgment (PN-219-004) to the new creation of the soul (PN-221-005) traces a complete theological arc: Creation → Fall → [[redemption|Redemption]] → New Creation — the fourfold pattern of evangelical theology.
