## Bethel in Scripture

Bethel, meaning "House of God," is the place where the patriarch Jacob, fleeing from Esau, slept with a stone for a pillow and received the vision of a ladder connecting earth to heaven (Genesis 28:10–19). Though the name Bethel does not appear verbatim in the Olney Hymns corpus, its theological significance is vividly present in Newton's treatment of Jacob's encounter.

## Jacob's Ladder and the Olney Hymns

Book I, Hymn 9 — **Jacob's Ladder** (based on Genesis 28:12) — captures the Bethel moment directly. Newton presents the vision as a promise that God is present with His people wherever they go: "If the Lord our leader be, / We may follow without fear; / East or west, by land or sea, / Home with him is every where."

## Spiritual Significance

For the Evangelical tradition represented by Newton and Cowper, Bethel symbolizes the truth that any place can become a sanctuary through divine encounter. Jacob's stone pillow and the vision of angels ascending and descending prefigure Christ as the true ladder between heaven and earth (John 1:51). The hymn's emphasis on God's omnipresence — "God is there, and he's their home" — makes Bethel less a geographical location than a spiritual reality accessible to every believer.

## Bethel in the History of Israel

Bethel's significance in the biblical narrative extends well beyond Jacob's vision. After the conquest of [[Canaan]], Bethel became one of the principal sanctuary cities of the northern kingdom. The Ark of the Covenant was kept there during the period of the Judges (Judges 20:26–28). Samuel made it a station on his annual circuit of judgment (1 Samuel 7:16).

However, Bethel's sacred history is shadowed by its later corruption. After the division of the kingdom, Jeroboam I established one of his two golden calves at Bethel (1 Kings 12:28–33), turning the "House of God" into a center of idolatrous worship. The prophets Amos and Hosea denounced Bethel's sanctuary — Amos for its empty ritualism (Amos 4:4, 5:5) and Hosea with the bitter pun "Beth-aven" ("house of wickedness") in place of Bethel ("house of God") (Hosea 4:15, 10:5).

This duality — Bethel as both the site of divine promise and the cautionary tale of corrupted worship — hovers over Newton's use of the Jacob narrative. His Hymn 9 focuses entirely on the Genesis 28 vision without touching the later history. Newton's instinct is pastoral: he wants the singer to encounter the God who meets Jacob in his flight, not to rehearse the centuries-later failure of Israelite worship. The promise of God's presence is the song's singular aim.

## "Home with Him Is Every Where": Newton's Theology of Place

The closing line of Hymn 9 — "Home with him is every where" — encapsulates a distinctive strain in Newton's pastoral theology. As a man who had lived in ships' cabins, African shore stations, Liverpool counting-houses, and finally an Olney vicarage, Newton knew the disorientation of frequent displacement. His hymns repeatedly dissolve the distinction between sacred and secular space: the believer's true sanctuary is not a building but the presence of God.

This makes Bethel, for Newton, the paradigmatic sacred site — precisely because it was not a temple or a shrine but a patch of ground where a fugitive laid his head on a stone. The God of Bethel meets His people not in consecrated architecture but in the ordinary places of their flight and rest.
