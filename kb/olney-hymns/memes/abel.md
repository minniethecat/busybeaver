## Summary

[[Abel]] was the second son of [[Adam]] and [[Eve]], a shepherd whose offering of the firstborn of his flock was accepted by God, while his brother [[Cain]]'s offering of crops was rejected. In jealous rage, [[cain|Cain]] murdered Abel — the first death and first martyrdom in human history. Abel's blood "cried out from the ground" (Genesis 4:10), and the New Testament holds him up as the prototypical righteous sufferer whose blood prefigures the blood of [[saviour|Christ]], which "speaks a better word than the blood of Abel" (Hebrews 12:24).

In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] uses Abel as a type of Christ and of the persecuted Church: just as [[cain|Cain]] killed Abel because God approved Abel's sacrifice, so the world hates true believers whose worship God accepts.

## Biblical Narrative

Abel's story occupies Genesis 4:1–16. He is introduced as "a keeper of sheep" (unlike [[cain|Cain]], "a tiller of the ground"). Both brothers bring offerings to the [[saviour|Lord]]: [[cain|Cain]] brings fruit of the ground; Abel brings firstborn of his flock with their fat portions. "The [[saviour|Lord]] had regard for Abel and his offering, but for [[cain|Cain]] and his offering he had no regard" (Genesis 4:4–5).

The text does not explicitly state why Abel's offering was accepted, but Hebrews 11:4 provides the New Testament interpretation: "By faith Abel offered to God a more acceptable sacrifice than [[cain|Cain]]." Abel's faith — not the material of his offering — made the difference.

[[cain|Cain]] lures Abel into the field and kills him. When God confronts [[cain|Cain]] ("Where is Abel your brother?"), [[cain|Cain]]'s reply — "Am I my brother's keeper?" — has become proverbial for moral evasion. God curses [[cain|Cain]], and Abel's blood cries out for justice.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] uses Abel's murder to illustrate the persecution of the righteous by the wicked, a theme with deep resonance in his own life and ministry:

> By Cain's own hand good Abel died,
> Because the Lord approved his faith.
> — [PN-002-004]

The cause of Abel's death — divine approval of his faith — is also the cause of his blessedness. The world's hatred is, paradoxically, evidence of God's favor. [[john-newton|Newton]] extends this typology to the Church:

> Of Abels whom the Cains have kill'd!
> — [PN-002-007]

The plural — "Abels" — universalizes the type. [[eve|Eve]]ry believer who suffers for righteousness' sake is an Abel. And every persecutor is a [[cain|Cain]]. The Christological application is explicit:

> His blood far better things than Abel's cries —
> Obtains his murderers' peace.
> — [PN-002-008]

Christ's blood does what Abel's could not: Abel's blood cried for justice; Christ's blood obtains mercy even for his murderers. The comparison — "far better things" — is a direct echo of Hebrews 12:24, where Christ's sprinkled blood "speaks a better word than the blood of Abel."

[[john-newton|Newton]] also draws a psychological parallel:

> So Cain murder'd Abel,
> And trembled like me.
> — [PN-247-002]

The speaker identifies with [[cain|Cain]] — the trembling murderer — not Abel. This characteristic [[john-newton|Newton]]ian move (the worst sinner is the best candidate for grace) transforms the Abel-[[cain|Cain]] story from a morality tale into a gospel narrative: if [[cain|Cain]] could be forgiven, anyone can.

## Theological Significance

Abel is the first link in a chain of redemptive typology that runs throughout Scripture: Abel → Isaac (bound for sacrifice) → Passover lamb → suffering servant (Isaiah 53) → Christ. His acceptable sacrifice by faith, his unjust death, and his crying blood all prefigure the cross, where the ultimate innocent victim's blood speaks a final word — not of vengeance, but of peace.
