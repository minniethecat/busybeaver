The prophet Elijah appears prominently in three Book I [[index-of-hymns]] by [[john-newton]] and is referenced across both books of the [[Olney-Hymns-Title]]. His life exemplifies the power of [[prayer]], dependence on divine provision, and the continuity of [[power]] across dispensations — [[index-of-concepts]] central to Evangelical piety.

## Elijah in Newton's Hymns

Three hymns are devoted to Elijah's story. **Elijah Fed by Ravens** (Book I, Hymn 35, 1 Kings 17:6) presents the prophet's miraculous sustenance during famine as a model for believers to trust God in every distress. **Elijah's Prayer** (Book I, Hymn 14 in the later sequence, 1 Kings 18) dramatizes the confrontation on Mount Carmel, where Elijah's [[faith]]-filled appeal to [[heaven]] draws down fire. **Elijah's Mantle** (Book I, Hymn 19, 2 Kings 2:11–14) treats the passing of prophetic authority to Elisha, with the refrain "Where is Elijah's God?" becoming a plea for revival in Newton's own day.

## Theological Themes

Newton uses Elijah as more than a historical figure. In the poet's hands, the ravens that fed Elijah become proof that "creatures only can fulfill / Their great [[creator]]'s holy will." The widow's inexhaustible oil and meal demonstrate that "God the store maintained." Most powerfully, in **Elijah's Mantle**, the cry "Where is Elijah's God, the [[saviour]]?" becomes a prayer for "another Pentecost" — linking the Old Testament prophet directly to the ongoing work of the [[spirit]].

## Legacy in the Hymns

Elijah's example of prayer in drought ("When Elijah prayed, it rained, / After three long years of drought") appears in Book II as evidence that "the Lord is still the same." Newton also invokes Elijah in a hymn on the death of a minister, praying that "double portions of thy [[grace]] / To us who stay be sent" — the very blessing Elisha sought from his departing master.
