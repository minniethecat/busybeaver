## Definition

Atonement is the theological doctrine that Christ's death on the cross satisfied God's justice, paid the penalty for sin, and reconciled believers to God. It is the central work of redemption, whereby Christ's blood covers sin and secures forgiveness for those who trust in him.

## Biblical Basis

The Old Testament sacrificial system prefigured atonement through the blood of animals offered on the altar (Leviticus 17:11). The New Testament declares Christ as the fulfillment: "whom God put forward as a propitiation by his blood" (Romans 3:25). 1 John 2:2 affirms that Christ "is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world."

## In the Olney Hymns

Newton proclaimed the atonement as the sole ground of the sinner's confidence: "His blood a full atonement makes, And cries aloud, 'Forgive'" [PN=170-004]. This hymn — John Newton's "The Sinner's Prayer" — presents Christ's atoning blood as the complete and sufficient basis for pardon, requiring no supplement of human merit. Newton's emphasis on the fullness of the atonement reflects his Evangelical conviction that the cross leaves nothing for the sinner to add.
