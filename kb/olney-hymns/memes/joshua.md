## Identity

[[Joshua]], son of Nun, was the successor of [[Moses]] and the leader who brought the Israelites across the [[jordan-river|Jordan]] into [[canaan|Canaan]], conquering the land as recorded in the Book of Joshua. His name in Hebrew — *Yehoshua* — means "Yahweh is salvation," the same root as the name [[Jesus]]. In the Olney Hymns, [[john-newton|Newton]] treats Joshua both as a historical figure and as a type pointing forward to [[saviour|Christ]].

## Biblical Context

Joshua first appears as [[Moses]]' military commander (Exodus 17:9–14) at the battle against Amalek. He served as Moses' assistant and was one of the two faithful spies (with [[Caleb]]) who brought back a good report from Canaan (Numbers 14:6–9). God commissioned him to lead Israel after Moses' death (Joshua 1:1–9), and under his leadership the Israelites crossed the Jordan, conquered [[jericho|Jericho]], and settled the land. The Book of Joshua records the military campaigns and the division of the land among the tribes.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[John Newton]] draws on Joshua's story in three distinct hymns, published in 1778, each illuminating a different facet of his typological significance.

### Joshua as a Type of Christ: The Gibeon Episode

In Hymn 21, "Gibeon," Joshua is the instrument of divine judgment who nonetheless becomes a picture of the Saviour:

> When Joshua, by God's command,
> Invaded Canaan's guilty land,
> Gibeon, unlike the nations round,
> Submission made, and mercy found.
> — [PN-021-001]

Newton reads the Gibeon narrative typologically: Joshua spares those who submit, just as Christ receives all who come to him in humility. The hymn's final stanza makes the application explicit — "Jesus invitations sends, / Treating with rebels as his friends." The typological move is characteristic of Newton's hermeneutic: the historical Joshua's actions prefigure the spiritual work of the greater Joshua, Jesus Christ. Just as the Gibeonites found mercy by submitting to Joshua's authority, so sinners find mercy by submitting to Christ's.

### The Division of Labour: Joshua and Moses at Amalek

In Hymn 66, "Moses and Amalek," Joshua appears in his first biblical role as battlefield commander:

> While Joshua led the armed bands
> Of Israel forth to war;
> Moses apart with lifted hands
> Engaged in humble pray'r.
> — [PN-066-001]

The division of labour is the point: Joshua with the sword, Moses with uplifted hands. Newton uses this to teach that human effort alone cannot prevail — it is the intercession of a mediator (prefiguring Christ) that secures the victory. The battle is won not by Joshua's generalship but by Moses' prayer, and Moses' upheld hands point forward to Christ's intercession at the right hand of God. The believer's warfare — like Israel's — is won not by human strength but by the intercession of a greater mediator.

### Humility Under Rebuke: The Defeat at Ai

A third allusion to Joshua appears in Hymn 145, where Newton references Joshua falling on his face after the defeat at Ai (Joshua 7:6). Following Israel's defeat — caused by Achan's hidden sin — Joshua tears his clothes and falls facedown before the ark. Newton uses the episode to teach humility under divine rebuke: even the great leader must learn that victory depends not on past success but on present obedience. The lesson is pastoral: when God's people suffer unexpected defeat, the response is not to question God's faithfulness but to search the camp for what has been hidden.
