# Joseph

## Identity

Joseph, the eleventh son of Jacob and firstborn of Rachel, is one of the most fully narrated figures in Genesis
(chapters 37–50). Sold into slavery by his brothers, falsely accused in Potiphar's house, imprisoned, and finally
exalted to rule Egypt, Joseph's story is the Bible's most sustained meditation on divine providence working through
human evil for redemptive ends.

## Biblical Narrative

Joseph's life follows the pattern of descent and exaltation: the beloved son becomes a slave, the slave becomes
a prisoner, the prisoner becomes a ruler. At every stage, the narrator insists that "the Lord was with Joseph."
His eventual revelation to his brothers — "You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (Genesis 50:20) —
is the theological climax of the entire patriarchal narrative.

## In the Olney Hymns

The Olney Hymns reference Joseph directly in at least two hymns: "Joseph Made Known to His Brethren" and the
account of the Egyptians coming to Joseph for grain. Newton reads Joseph typologically — as a figure of Christ,
the rejected brother who becomes the savior of his people.

> To Joseph the Egyptians went (PN=001-025)
> When Joseph his brethren beheld (PN=001-027)

## Theological Significance

Joseph's story provided Newton with a powerful template for his own autobiography: the former slave-trader who
became a minister of the gospel, seeing God's hand in the darkest chapters of his past. The Joseph narrative
is, for Newton, proof that providence is not a doctrine to be debated but a reality to be lived.

## Newton vs. Cowper

Newton's engagement with Joseph is direct and typological. Cowper does not treat Joseph separately but absorbs
the Joseph-pattern (descent into darkness, unexpected deliverance) into his broader theology of affliction —
the conviction that God's purposes are worked out precisely where they seem most absent.
