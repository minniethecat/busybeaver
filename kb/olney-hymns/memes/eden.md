## Summary

[[Eden]] was the garden "eastward in Eden" (Genesis 2:8) where God placed [[Adam]] and [[Eve]], the first humans. A place of perfect harmony between God, humanity, and creation, Eden contained the tree of life and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. After [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]]'s disobedience — the [[fall-of-man|Fall]] — they were expelled from Eden, and a cherubim with a flaming sword guarded its entrance.

In the Olney Hymns, Eden appears as a memory of lost innocence and, implicitly, as a promise of restored paradise. [[John Newton]] uses Eden to frame the arc of redemption: what was lost in the first [[adam|Adam]] is restored — and surpassed — in [[saviour|Christ]], the Second [[adam|Adam]].

## In the Olney Hymns

Eden appears at the very opening of the Olney Hymns, in [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymn on [[adam|Adam]]:

> He dwelt in Eden's garden, stored
> With sweets beyond compare.
> — [PN-001-002]

The language of abundance — "stored with sweets" — establishes Eden as the baseline of human flourishing. [[fall-of-man|The Fall]] is measured by the distance from Eden:

> To Adam, soon as he transgress'd,
> Thus Eden bloom'd in vain:
> Not Paradise itself could shield
> The sinner from his pain.
> — [PN-173-007]

The pathos of this couplet is striking: Eden's beauty ("bloom'd in vain") could not protect [[adam|Adam]] from the consequences of his own sin. Paradise is no shield against guilt. The final reference returns to Eden as dwelling-place:

> Eden was Adam's dwelling-place
> While blest with innocence.
> — [PN-236-002]

The conditional — "while blest with innocence" — makes explicit what the Fall narrative implies: Eden was conditional on obedience. Once innocence was lost, Eden was lost. The restoration of paradise is the work of the "Second [[adam|Adam]]":

> The second Adam saw
> Oppress'd with woe, to set us free.
> — [PN-236-004]

## Eden and Paradise

In biblical theology, Eden is the archetype of Paradise — the state of perfect communion with God. The Bible's narrative arc moves from a garden (Eden, Genesis 2) to a garden-city (the New [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], Revelation 21–22), where the tree of life reappears and "there shall no longer be any curse." The Olney Hymns, while focused on the present experience of grace, gesture toward this restored paradise.
