## Definition

Wisdom in the Olney Hymns spans the full biblical range: it is an attribute of God, a gift of grace, and a practical
discipline of the believer. Wisdom is not cleverness but Christlikeness — the skill of living in accordance with
God's order. Newton's hymns especially present wisdom as something prayed for, received, and exercised in the
ordinary circumstances of the Christian life.

## Biblical Basis

The hymns draw extensively on the wisdom literature, especially Proverbs' personification of Wisdom and James's
promise that God gives wisdom generously to those who ask. Solomon's prayer for wisdom (1 Kings 3) is a recurring
reference point — wisdom as the supreme gift, valued above riches or power.

## In the Olney Hymns

Wisdom appears across all three books as both divine attribute and human aspiration. The believer prays for wisdom
to navigate affliction, to discern providence, and to walk worthy of the gospel calling.

### True Wisdom and Its Counterfeits

The Olney Hymns are attentive to the difference between divine wisdom and its
persuasive imitations. Newton, drawing on both biblical categories and his own
unusual spiritual history — a self-educated former slave-ship captain who had
sampled both the world's learning and the gospel's simplicity — is particularly
alert to wisdom that wears a false face.

#### The Limits of Human Wisdom

The hymns acknowledge that merely human wisdom has real but strictly bounded
value. The preacher's art, the scholar's systems, the moralist's maxims — these
"pass for wisdom in the schools" (134-001) and may even contain genuine insight.
Yet Newton's own testimony is that they proved powerless to change his heart: "I
strove my passion to control, but still the tyrant reigned within my soul"
(134-001). The schools could diagnose the disease but could not supply the cure.
Elsewhere the hymns press this limit further: when contemplating the magnitude of
divine providence, even the heavens' own testimony falls short — "all the stars
proclaim their wisdom cannot reach" (222-003) the full scope of God's ways.
Creation displays wisdom, but a wisdom that points beyond itself to its
inscrutable source.

#### Wisdom's Fragility in Human Hands

Even wisdom rightly received can be mishandled. In a striking couplet, Newton
observes that "a fever or a blow can shake our wisdom's boasted rule" (054-004).
The physical frailty of the human frame — illness, age, accident — can undo in a
moment the accumulated prudence of a lifetime. This is not an argument against
pursuing wisdom but a caution against trusting in it: wisdom is a gift to be held
with open hands, not a possession to be banked. The same note sounds in the
eschatological counsel that "your wisdom is to be prepared before the summons
comes" (176-004). The truest wisdom is not the mastery of life's complexities but
readiness for life's end.

#### Wisdom Learned at the Feet of Another

The positive model is discipleship. Newton pictures believers gathered "at
Elisha's feet" where "they grace and wisdom learned" (039-002). Wisdom is
acquired not in solitude but in company — at the feet of a master, in the
fellowship of fellow-learners. The posture matters: sitting at another's feet is
an act of humility, a recognition that wisdom must be received before it can be
exercised. This undergirds the hymns' consistent emphasis on prayer as the
posture of wisdom: the believer who asks God for wisdom, like Solomon, has
already begun to be wise.

#### Walking in Wisdom's Ways

The hymns' most characteristic image for wisdom-in-action is simply walking: "we
walk in wisdom's ways" (166-003). The metaphor is deliberately unspectacular.
Wisdom is not ecstasy or extraordinary insight but steady, directional movement
along a known path. The cripple healed by the gospel "runs in wisdom's ways"
(113-003) — the same path, but now traversed with the energy of new life. The
union of divine grace and human responsibility is captured in this image: God
gives the path and the power to walk it; the believer rises and walks.

## Newton vs. Cowper

Newton's treatment is practical and pastoral: wisdom for daily living, sought at the throne of grace.
Cowper's is more existential: wisdom to understand the ways of God in the midst of suffering — the question
not of how to live well but of how to make sense of living at all. Cowper never resolves the tension;
he places it before God and waits.
