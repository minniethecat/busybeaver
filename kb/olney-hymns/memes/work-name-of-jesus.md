## Overview

"The Name of Jesus" (Book I, Hymn 57) is among [[John Newton]]'s most enduring and widely sung hymns. Opening with the immortal line "How sweet the name of Jesus sounds / In a believer's ear," the hymn unfolds as an extended meditation on the manifold virtues of Christ's name — it soothes sorrow, heals wounds, drives away fear, and is the believer's "shield and hiding-place."

The hymn belongs to the long Christian tradition of devotion to the Holy Name, rooted in the biblical promise that "at the name of Jesus every knee should bow" (Philippians 2:10). [[john-newton|Newton]]'s treatment combines intense personal piety with doctrinal substance: the name of [[Jesus]] is sweet because of *who* Jesus is and *what* he has done.

## Authorship

[[John Newton]] composed this hymn for Book I of the Olney Hymns, which contains hymns "On Select Texts of Scripture." The scriptural anchor is Song of Solomon 1:3 — "Thy name is as ointment poured forth" — but [[john-newton|Newton]] weaves in New Testament themes of Christ's exaltation and mediatorial work.

The hymn's meter (CM, 8.6.8.6) allows it to be paired with numerous tunes. It has appeared in hundreds of hymnals since its first publication in 1779 and remains in active use.

## Scripture Reference

- **Primary**: Song of Solomon 1:3 ("Thy name is as ointment poured forth")
- **Supporting**: Philippians 2:9–11 (the name above every name); Psalm 61:3 (a strong tower); Proverbs 18:10 (the name of the [[saviour|Lord]] is a strong tower)

## Theological Themes

The hymn is structured as a series of epithets and functions of Jesus' name:

| Function | Imagery |
|----------|---------|
| Soothing | "It soothes his sorrows, heals his wounds" |
| Protection | "Shield and hiding-place" |
| Provision | "Manna to the hungry soul" |
| [[peace|Rest]]oration | "[[peace|Rest]] to the weary" |
| Guidance | "My shepherd, husband, friend" |

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s pastoral instinct is evident: the hymn is not abstract theology but applied comfort. Each attribute of Jesus' name answers a specific human need — sorrow, fear, hunger, weariness, loneliness.
