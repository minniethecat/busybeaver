## Definition

In the Olney Hymns, [[Creator]] refers to God as the sovereign author of all existence — the one who "form'd the mighty whole / And kindled darkness into day" (PN-081-005). The doctrine of creation is never abstract: it is the ground of both worship and trust. Because God made everything, everything belongs to him and serves his will. The hymns treat creation as a book to be read (the "book of nature"), a testimony to divine power, and a school of trust in divine [[providence|Providence]].

## Biblical Basis

The Olney Hymns draw on the full biblical witness to God as Creator, from Genesis to the New Testament:

| Passage | Theme | Hymn Connection |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Genesis 1:1 | "In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth" | Foundation of all creation hymns |
| Psalm 19:1 | "The heavens declare the glory of God" | The book of nature as revelation |
| Psalm 33:6–9 | "By the word of the Lord were the heavens made" | Creation by divine *fiat* |
| Isaiah 40:26–28 | The everlasting God, the Creator of the ends of the earth | Creator's power as ground of trust |
| Romans 1:20 | The invisible things "clearly seen" from creation | Creation as universal testimony |
| Colossians 1:16 | "All things were created by him, and for him" | Christ as agent of creation |

The foundational text is Genesis 1:1: "In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth." The Psalms expand creation into worship — "The heavens declare the glory of God" (Psalm 19:1). The New Testament declares that creation was *through* Christ: "All things were created by him, and for him" (Colossians 1:16). Romans 1:20 teaches that creation leaves humanity without excuse, for "the invisible things of him from the creation of the world are clearly seen."

## In the Olney Hymns

[[John Newton]] treats creation under three aspects: as a source of *praise*, as a school of *providence*, and as a pattern of *new creation*.

### Creation as Praise

The praise aspect is clearest in Hymn 33, a spring meditation:

> Hark! the birds with artless lays,
> Warble their Creator's praise!
> Where, in winter, all was snow,
> Now the flow'rs in clusters grow.
> — [PN-033-001]

Creation is a choir. The birds do not sing for their own sake but instinctively "warble their Creator's praise" — a model for the believer, who alone among creatures can offer *intelligent* worship. The seasonal renewal of spring becomes an "emblem of the spring of grace."

### The Book of Nature and the Need for Grace

The providence aspect appears in Hymn 81, "The Book of Creation":

> The book of nature open lies,
> With much instruction stored:
> But till the Lord anoints our eyes
> We cannot read a word.
> — [PN-081-001]

Creation is a book, but it requires a Teacher. Newton's point is both the *sufficiency* of creation as revelation and the *necessity* of grace to interpret it. The natural world is filled with "instruction stored," but fallen human perception cannot access it without divine illumination. This is a characteristically Evangelical epistemology: general revelation is real but requires special revelation (and regenerating grace) to be rightly understood.

### New Creation: The Greater Work

The same hymn treats regeneration as a new creation — and a greater work than the original:

> Though self-destroy'd, O Lord, we are,
> Yet let us feel what thou canst do;
> Thy word the ruin can repair,
> And all our hearts create anew.
> — [PN-081-006]

The Creator who spoke light out of darkness can speak new life into a dead heart. The parallel is deliberate: the same power that made the world remakes the sinner. Newton implies that new creation is the *greater* work, for it overcomes not nothingness but active rebellion.

### Creatures as Servants

In Hymn 129, Newton draws the practical conclusion: if God is Creator, his creatures obey him without fail — and so should we trust him alone rather than depend on created things:

> Thus creatures only can fulfill
> Their great Creator's holy will;
> And when his servants need their aid,
> His purposes must be obey'd.
> — [PN-129-004]

The Creator's sovereignty over his creation is the ultimate ground of the believer's confidence. If the inanimate creation and the animal kingdom obey their Maker without resistance, how much more should redeemed humanity — who have experienced the Creator's grace as well as his power — trust his government of their lives?
