## Overview

"[[adam|Adam]]" is the opening hymn of Book I of the Olney Hymns. Placed first by [[John Newton]]'s editorial design, it establishes the foundational theological framework for the entire collection: humanity's original state of innocence in [[Eden]], the catastrophic [[fall-of-man|Fall]], the loss of God's image, and the promise of redemption through the "Second [[adam|Adam]]," [[saviour|Jesus Christ]].

The hymn follows the classic Reformed narrative arc: [[creation|Creation]] → Fall → Promise → [[redemption|Redemption]]. [[john-newton|Newton]] begins with [[adam|Adam]]'s original dignity ("On man, in his own image made"), traces the descent into sin, and closes with the triumphant note that what [[adam|Adam]] lost, Christ restores — and more.

## Authorship

[[John Newton]] (1725–1807), the former slave trader turned Anglican clergyman, wrote this hymn for the inaugural position in the Olney Hymns (1779). As the opening piece, it carries programmatic weight: [[john-newton|Newton]] intended the collection to trace the full arc of Christian experience, and "[[adam|Adam]]" anchors that arc in the biblical narrative of fall and redemption.

[[john-newton|Newton]] composed the hymn in Common Meter (8.6.8.6), the most frequently used meter in the collection, allowing it to be sung to familiar tunes.

## Scripture Reference

The hymn draws primarily from [[Genesis]] 1–3 ([[creation|Creation]] and Fall) and Romans 5:12–21 ([[adam|Adam]] as type of Christ). Key allusions include:

- "On man, in his own image made" — Genesis 1:26–27
- "When [[adam|Adam]] fell he quickly lost / God's image" — Genesis 3:6–7
- The implicit "Second [[adam|Adam]]" — Romans 5:14–19, 1 Corinthians 15:45

## Theological Themes

The hymn works through several interconnected theological themes:

| Theme | Development |
|-------|------------|
| **Original Righteousness** | Man made in God's image, dwelling in [[eden|Eden]] |
| **[[fall-of-man|The Fall]]** | [[adam|Adam]]'s disobedience → loss of divine image |
| **Original [[sin|Sin]]** | All humanity shares [[adam|Adam]]'s fallen condition |
| **Second [[adam|Adam]]** | Christ as the one who restores what [[adam|Adam]] lost |
| **Superior [[redemption|Redemption]]** | What Christ gives exceeds what [[adam|Adam]] forfeited |

The controlling idea is that the Fall is real and devastating, but redemption in Christ is more glorious than the original creation — a theme that runs throughout [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymnody.
