## Identity

**Isaac** is the son of [[Abraham]] and Sarah, the child of promise born when his parents were advanced in age (Genesis 21:1–7). His name means "he laughs," reflecting both Sarah's initial disbelief and later joy at his birth. Isaac is the second of the three patriarchs of [[Israel]], standing between Abraham and [[Jacob]].

Isaac's life is marked by several key moments: the binding on Mount Moriah where Abraham's faith was tested (Genesis 22), his marriage to Rebekah arranged through divine providence (Genesis 24), and the renewal of God's covenant promise to him (Genesis 26:2–5). Compared to Abraham and Jacob, Isaac's narrative in Genesis is briefer — he is often depicted as a transitional figure who faithfully maintains the covenant promises received from his father and passes them to his sons.

## Biblical Context

Isaac's story occupies Genesis 21–35, with key episodes:

- **The Promised Birth** (Genesis 21): Born to Abraham (age 100) and Sarah (age 90). His birth fulfills God's covenant promise of descendants.
- **The Binding** (Genesis 22): Abraham's supreme test of faith on Mount Moriah. Isaac carries the wood for his own sacrifice — a detail long read by Christian interpreters as prefiguring Christ carrying the cross.
- **Marriage to Rebekah** (Genesis 24): Abraham's servant, guided by prayer, finds Rebekah at the well. The story emphasizes God's providential guidance in covenant relationships.
- **The Blessing of Jacob** (Genesis 27): In old age, Isaac is deceived into bestowing the patriarchal blessing on Jacob rather than Esau, shaping the destiny of Israel.

Isaac appears less frequently than Abraham in New Testament references, but is consistently named alongside Abraham and Jacob in the patriarchal triad (Matthew 8:11, Acts 3:13, Hebrews 11:9, 17–20). His near-sacrifice is cited in Hebrews 11:17–19 as an act of resurrection faith — Abraham believed God could raise Isaac from the dead.

## In the Olney Hymns

Isaac has no direct named reference in the corpus of the Olney Hymns. However, he is present through the hymns that reflect on the patriarchal narratives of [[genesis|Genesis]]. The [[exodus|Exodus]] and [[israel|Israel]] hymns that trace God's faithfulness to the covenant people implicitly draw on the line of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

The binding of Isaac (Genesis 22), while not directly quoted, resonates with [[John Newton]]'s theological emphasis on substitutionary sacrifice — a theme central to Book I hymns on Scripture doctrine. For Newton, the ram caught in the thicket points toward Christ the substitute, and Isaac's willing submission mirrors Christ's obedience unto death.

Isaac's role as recipient of the covenant promise (Genesis 26:2–5) provides the link between Abraham's calling and Jacob's formation of the twelve tribes. The "fear of Isaac" — a divine title used by Jacob (Genesis 31:42) — signifies the God who was personally known and reverenced by the second patriarch, a God whose promises span generations.

## Theological Significance

Isaac is the least active of the three patriarchs. Unlike Abraham who journeyed and Jacob who wrestled, Isaac primarily stayed and dug wells — re-opening his father's wells (Genesis 26:18) and digging new ones. This pattern of receiving and renewing what was inherited makes Isaac a distinctive model: the faithful steward of covenant promise rather than the pioneer.

The Akedah gives Isaac his greatest theological weight. Christian typology has long seen in Isaac a figure of Christ: the beloved son, offered by his father, carrying the wood of his own sacrifice, and restored alive. The Olney Hymns, saturated with the language of substitutionary atonement, draw on this typological reading even when Isaac is not named directly.
