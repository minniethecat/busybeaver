## Identity

**David** (Hebrew: דָּוִד, *Dāwīd*; "beloved") was the second king of [[israel|Israel]], the author of many Psalms, and the recipient of God's covenant promise that his throne would endure forever (2 Samuel 7:12–16). In the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], David appears more frequently than any other Old Testament figure — not as a remote historical character but as a mirror for every believer's experience of faith, failure, and restoration.

[[john-newton|John Newton]] and [[william-cowper|William Cowper]] draw on David's life at three key moments: his victory over Goliath as a young shepherd (1 Samuel 17), his grievous sin with Bathsheba and subsequent repentance (2 Samuel 11–12; Psalm 51), and his role as the father of the Messianic line — "David's Son and David's [[saviour|Lord]]" (161-001).

## Biblical Context

David's story spans 1 Samuel 16 through 1 Kings 2, with his Psalms filling the Psalter. The Olney Hymns concentrate on four episodes:

| Episode | Scripture | Hymn Theme |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| David and Goliath | 1 Samuel 17 | [[faith|Faith]] triumphing over impossible odds |
| Saul's pursuit of David | 1 Samuel 23–24 | Divine deliverance in extremity |
| David's fall with Bathsheba | 2 Samuel 11–12 | The danger of spiritual complacency |
| David as Messianic ancestor | Matthew 1:1 | Christ as "David's Son and David's [[saviour|Lord]]" |

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s treatment of David is notably pastoral — he reads David's story not as a morality tale but as a case study in [[grace]]. David the giant-killer and David the adulterer are the same man, and the same [[God]] who gave him victory over Goliath restored him after his fall.

## In the Olney Hymns

David appears in at least eight hymns, serving three distinct rhetorical purposes:

### David as Model of Faith

The young David facing Goliath with only "a pebble from the brook" (017-001) becomes the paradigm of faith that trusts God rather than human resources. [[john-newton|Newton]] applies this directly to the believer's spiritual warfare:

> Satan will not be braved
> By such a worm as I;
> Then let me learn with David
> To trust in the Most High —
> To plead the name of Jesus,
> And use the sling of prayer.
> — [028-006]

The "sling of prayer" is a characteristically [[john-newton|Newton]]ian image — the believer's weapon seems as feeble as David's sling, yet it prevails because God empowers it. Saul's offer of armour (028-001–002) represents the temptation to rely on human means rather than divine power.

### David as Cautionary Example

[[john-newton|Newton]] does not idealize David. Hymn 29 opens with a sober assessment of David's fall:

> How David, when by sin deceived,
> From bad to worse went on!
> For when the Holy Spirit's grieved,
> Our strength and guard are gone.
> — [029-001]

Yet the conclusion is not despair but warning tempered with hope:

> Let those who think they stand, beware,
> For David stood before:
> Nor let the fallen soul despair,
> For [[mercy]] can restore.
> — [029-008]

This double movement — "beware" and "despair not" — captures the Evangelical balance: genuine assurance coexists with genuine vigilance because the same David who fell was also the David whom God restored.

### David as Messianic Pointer

The title "Son of David" appears in the New Testament as a Messianic designation (Matthew 1:1; 21:9), and the hymns pick up this connection. Hymn 161 addresses Christ with the paradox:

> O David's Son and David's Lord!
> From age to age thou art the same.
> — [161-001]

Christ is both David's descendant (Son) and David's sovereign ([[saviour|Lord]]) — a paradox that the New Testament itself highlights (Mark 12:35–37) and that the hymns deploy to affirm Christ's divinity.

## Theological Significance

David occupies a unique place in Evangelical typology because he is simultaneously a historical figure, a moral example (both positive and negative), and a type of Christ. The hymns hold these three dimensions in tension:

- **Historical**: David is treated as a real person whose actions had real consequences — [[john-newton|Newton]] never reduces him to a mere symbol.
- **Exemplary**: David's faith and David's failure are both held up as relevant to the believer's experience. He is not a stained-glass saint but a fellow sinner sustained by grace.
- **Typological**: David points forward to Christ — his victory over Goliath prefigures Christ's victory over sin and death; his kingship anticipates Christ's eternal reign.

This threefold reading reflects the broader Evangelical hermeneutic in which the Old Testament is read Christologically without denying its historical referent.
