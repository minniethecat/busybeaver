## Summary

[[Israel]] is the covenant people of God in the [[Old Testament]] — the descendants of [[Abraham]], [[Isaac]], and [[Jacob]] (who was renamed Israel). Chosen by God to be a "kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exodus 19:6), Israel received the Law at [[Sinai]], was sustained by manna in the wilderness, and was led into the promised land of [[Canaan]]. In the Olney Hymns, Israel functions both as a historical community and as a type of the [[Church]] — God's New Testament people.

[[John Newton]] draws extensively on Israel's history as a pattern of God's dealings with his people: the giving of manna, the fiery law at [[sinai|Sinai]], the provision in the wilderness, and the tension between faith and unbelief all serve as object lessons for Christian experience.

## Biblical Foundation

Israel's story spans the Pentateuch and Historical Books: the patriarchal promises (Genesis 12–50), the Exodus from [[Egypt]] (Exodus 1–15), the covenant at [[Sinai]] (Exodus 19–24), the wilderness wanderings (Numbers), the conquest of [[Canaan]] (Joshua), the monarchy (Samuel–Kings), the exile, and the return.

The name "Israel" means "he who strives with God," given to [[jacob|Jacob]] after he wrestled with the angel at Peniel (Genesis 32:28). The collective "children of Israel" (Bnei Yisrael) became the standard biblical designation for the covenant nation.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] references Israel in five hymn passages, each drawing on a distinct episode of Israel's history:

> Manna to Israel well supplied
> The want of other bread.
> — [PN-015-001]

The manna in the wilderness (Exodus 16) becomes a type of Christ, the true bread from heaven. [[john-newton|Newton]] connects Israel's physical sustenance with the believer's spiritual nourishment:

> The manna, favour'd Israel's meat,
> Was gather'd day by day.
> — [PN-016-001]

The daily gathering of manna teaches dependence on God's provision — a central [[john-newton|Newton]]ian theme:

> Israel must live by faith and hope,
> And not on sight depend.
> — [PN-015-007]

The giving of the Law at [[sinai|Sinai]] is evoked with awe:

> When Israel heard the fiery law
> From Sinai's top proclaimed.
> — [PN-018-001]

And Israel's identity is rooted in its divine King:

> 'Twas Israel's God and King
> Who sent him to the fight.
> — [PN-017-002]

## Israel as Type of the Church

In Reformed covenant theology — which [[john-newton|Newton]] shared — Israel is understood typologically: the Old Testament people of God prefigure the New Testament [[Church]]. The manna points to the Eucharist, the Exodus to baptism, the wilderness journey to the Christian life, and [[canaan|Canaan]] to [[Heaven]]. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns consistently read Israel's story through this Christ-centered lens.

This typological reading means that hymns "about" Israel are ultimately about the Church — and about the individual believer's journey from bondage (sin) through wilderness (this life) to the promised land (glory).
