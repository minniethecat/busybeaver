## Definition

Communion describes the intimate fellowship that believers enjoy with God and with one another through [[jesus]]. It encompasses both the vertical dimension of personal communion with the [[saviour]] and the horizontal dimension of shared spiritual fellowship within the [[church]]. This two-fold communion is made possible by the indwelling [[spirit]].

## Biblical Basis

1 John 1:3 declares that the apostolic message is proclaimed "so that you too may have fellowship with us; and indeed our fellowship is with the Father and with his Son [[jesus]]." Acts 2:42 describes the early church devoting themselves to "the [[disciples]]' teaching and the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers."

## In the Olney Hymns

The [[Olney-Hymns-Title]] celebrate communion as the sweet bond uniting believers. "That they may meet, And in sweet communion join" [PN=212-003] describes the gathering of God's [[index-of-persons]] in [[worship]] as a foretaste of [[heaven]] fellowship. The hymns also portray communion with Christ as the source of [[joy]] that transcends earthly experience: "And possess in sweet communion, Joys which earth cannot afford" [PN=335-001]. This language reflects [[insight-newton-cowper]]'s vision of gathered worship as a real participation in divine fellowship.

## Historical Context

In eighteenth-century English Evangelicalism, "sweet communion" was a distinctive phrase carrying deep spiritual significance. [[john-newton]] and [[william-cowper]] ministered at Olney during a period when hymn-singing was itself a controversial innovation in Anglican worship — metrical psalms were traditional, but "[[index-of-hymns]] of human composure" faced resistance. The Olney Hymns' emphasis on the sweetness of communal worship was a deliberate affirmation of the legitimacy and spiritual value of congregational hymnody. Newton's parish gathered for [[prayer]] meetings where hymns like those in the Olney collection were sung, and the language of "sweet communion" captured the experiential warmth of these gatherings.
