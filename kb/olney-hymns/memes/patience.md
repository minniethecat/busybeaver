## Definition

[[Patience]] is the Spirit-wrought capacity to endure delay, suffering, and provocation without murmuring against God or retaliating against others. It is both an attribute of God — who is "slow to anger" (Exodus 34:6) — and a fruit of the Spirit in the believer (Galatians 5:22). In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] treats patience as the quiet companion of [[faith]] and [[hope]], forged in the furnace of affliction.

## Biblical Foundation

James writes that "the testing of your faith produces patience" (James 1:3), and [[paul|Paul]] traces the chain from suffering through patience to hope (Romans 5:3–4). God's own patience toward sinners is the model: "The [[saviour|Lord]] is not slow to fulfill his promise as some count slowness, but is patient toward you" (2 Peter 3:9). The believer is called to mirror this divine patience both toward God's timing and toward others' failings.

## In the Olney Hymns

### Patience Tested to Extremity

[[john-newton|Newton]] does not present patience as an easy virtue. His hymns acknowledge the point at which patience seems exhausted:

> Till hope and patience ceased!
> (082-004)

The context is the believer's struggle against indwelling sin — a fight so prolonged that both hope and patience appear to fail. Yet the very confession of this near-failure is itself an act of faith. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s pastoral genius lies in giving voice to the believer's lowest moments without leaving him there.

### Impatience as the Native Condition

[[john-newton|Newton]] is equally honest about the believer's natural impatience. He contrasts the grace God creates with the impatience that returns when grace seems withdrawn:

> Alas! impatience is its name
> When it returns to me.
> (067-003)

The confession is personal and specific. The "me" at the end of the couplet makes clear this is autobiography, not abstract theology. The hymn-writer knows impatience from the inside.

### God's Patience with Sinners

The hymns also celebrate God's patience toward rebellious sinners. [[john-newton|Newton]], reflecting on his own pre-conversion life, acknowledges:

> Thy patience scorned, thy power defied.
> (121-002)

God's patience is the background condition of every conversion. The sinner who scorned and defied God was not struck down — he was waited for. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s own story of radical conversion gave this doctrine personal urgency.

### Patience and Faith Together

[[john-newton|Newton]] frequently pairs patience with [[faith]] as companion graces. In his vision of the Christian life, "faith and patience, love and zeal" (213-006) mark the mature believer. These are not isolated virtues but an interconnected fabric — patience sustains faith when sight fails, and faith gives patience its reason to wait.
