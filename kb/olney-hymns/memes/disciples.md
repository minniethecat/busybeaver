## Definition

The [[Disciples]] were the twelve men called by [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] to follow him during his earthly ministry, to learn from him, and to become "fishers of men" (Matthew 4:19). In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] uses the disciples not as stained-glass saints but as relatable figures whose fears and failures mirror every believer's experience.

## Biblical Foundation

Jesus chose twelve disciples — [[Peter]], Andrew, James, [[John]], Philip, Bartholomew, Thomas, Matthew, James son of Alphaeus, Thaddaeus, Simon the Zealot, and Judas Iscariot (Matthew 10:2–4). They witnessed His miracles, heard His teaching, and were commissioned to carry the gospel to the nations (Matthew 28:19–20). After Christ's ascension and the coming of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, they became the foundational leaders of the early [[Church]].

## In the Olney Hymns

### Crossing the Lake

[[john-newton|Newton]] draws on the Gospel accounts of the disciples' experiences to illustrate the believer's journey:

> When the disciples crossed the lake
> With but one loaf on board.
> (094-001)

The reference is to Mark 8:14–21, where the disciples worry about having only one loaf of bread — forgetting that Christ had just fed thousands with far less. [[john-newton|Newton]] uses this incident to expose the believer's tendency to forget past mercies when facing present needs.

### Tossed by Storms

The disciples' storm-tossed boat becomes a metaphor for the Christian life:

> We, like the disciples, are tossed,
> By storms on a perilous deep.
> (114-003)

[[john-newton|Newton]] identifies the hymn-singer with the disciples in the boat. The storms are real — affliction, doubt, temptation — but Christ is in the boat. The disciples' experience is not a historical curiosity but the believer's present reality.

### Christ's Care for His Own

The overarching theme in [[john-newton|Newton]]'s treatment of the disciples is Christ's faithful care despite their weakness. When the disciples grieve during Christ's apparent absence, [[john-newton|Newton]] draws the lesson that "though he remain awhile concealed" (114-001), he is never truly absent from his people.
