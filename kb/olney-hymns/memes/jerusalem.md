## Summary

[[Jerusalem]] is the city chosen by God as the site of the [[Temple]] — the dwelling place of his name (1 Kings 11:36). It was [[david|David]]'s capital, Solomon's building project, the goal of pilgrimage, the scene of the prophets' ministry, and the place where [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] was crucified, buried, and raised. Destroyed by Babylon in 586 BC and by Rome in AD 70, Jerusalem endures in Christian theology as a type of the heavenly city — the "New Jerusalem" of Revelation 21.

In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] uses Jerusalem in two registers: as the historical city of Christ's passion, and as the eschatological city of God's protection and final dwelling with his people.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s use of Jerusalem ranges from the historical to the typological. Christ's journey toward crucifixion is depicted with poignant detail:

> When, hasting to Jerusalem,
> He march'd before the rest!
> — [PN-196-001]

Jesus "hasting" — eager, determined — toward the city where he would die (Luke 9:51: "he set his face to go to Jerusalem"). The emotional resonance is of a willing sacrifice. Jerusalem also represents the object of God's protective care:

> "So will I guard Jerusalem."
> — [PN-072-001]

This echoes Zechariah's prophecy of Jerusalem as a city protected by God himself. [[john-newton|Newton]] then asks the theological question:

> And what then is Jerusalem —
> This darling object of his care?
> — [PN-072-002]

The implied answer is the [[Church]] — the Jerusalem that matters is not the geographical city but the community of the redeemed. This typological reading is reinforced by the pilgrimage motif:

> When once to Jerusalem come,
> The treasure and train shall be secure.
> — [PN-034-002]

Jerusalem as the goal of pilgrimage — the place of arrival, security, and rest — merges into the heavenly Jerusalem, the ultimate destination of the Christian journey.

## Jerusalem and Zion

In the Olney Hymns, [[Zion]] and Jerusalem are closely related but distinct. [[zion|Zion]] is specifically the mountain of God's dwelling, the city of God — more theological than geographical. Jerusalem is the historical city that contains [[zion|Zion]] but also the broader city of pilgrimage, suffering, and consummation. Together they represent the Church militant (Jerusalem, on pilgrimage) and the Church triumphant ([[zion|Zion]], at rest).
