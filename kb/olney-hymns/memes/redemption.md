## Definition

[[Redemption]] is the deliverance of sinners from bondage to sin and death through the payment of a ransom price — the precious blood of [[saviour|Jesus Christ]]. In biblical language, to "redeem" means to buy back, to liberate by payment.

The New Testament declares that Christ "gave himself as a ransom for all" (1 Timothy 2:6) and that believers are "bought with a price" (1 Corinthians 6:20). In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] — himself a former slave trader turned abolitionist — writes of redemption with profound personal resonance: he who once trafficked in human bondage now sings of the blood-bought liberty of the soul.

## Biblical Foundation

Redemption is a thread running through all of Scripture. In the Old Testament, God redeemed [[israel|Israel]] from [[egypt|Egypt]]ian slavery by the blood of the Passover lamb (Exodus 12). The kinsman-redeemer (*goel*) in Ruth and the levitical redemption laws prefigure Christ's work.

The prophets foretold a greater redemption — not from political oppression but from sin itself (Isaiah 44:22). In the New Testament, Christ is the fulfillment: "In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of our trespasses" (Ephesians 1:7). His death is the ransom price (Mark 10:45), and His resurrection is the receipt of payment accepted.

## In the Olney Hymns

### Redemption as Security

Redemption is a dominant theme throughout [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns. The language of blood, ransom, purchase, and deliverance recurs constantly:

> Redemption is his shield and tower;
> He sees his Saviour always near.
> (048-003)

Redemption is not a distant theological concept but the believer's immediate security — a "shield and tower" against every assault of guilt and fear. The redeemed sinner stands behind an impenetrable defense.

### Redemption as Personal Possession

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s doctrine of redemption is emphatically particular and substitutionary:

> In his great redemption shine:
> Mighty Jesus! give me leave
> To call thee mine.
> (078-002)

The personal possessive — "mine" — is the heartbeat of [[john-newton|Newton]]'s redemption theology. Redemption is not merely a cosmic transaction but a personal claim: this [[saviour|Saviour]] is my [[saviour|Saviour]]; this blood was shed for me.

### Redemption's Purchase Price

[[john-newton|Newton]] grounds assurance in the objective reality of Christ's payment. The believer's confidence rests not on subjective feeling but on the transaction accomplished at [[crucifixion|Calvary]]:

> He rests assured,
> In that great redemption price
> I see the whole secured.
> (279-005)

The "redemption price" is Christ's blood — a payment sufficient to secure the whole salvation of all who believe. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s use of commercial language reflects his era's familiarity with slave markets and ransom payments.

### Redemption and Justice

For [[john-newton|Newton]], redemption is not arbitrary generosity but the harmonious operation of divine justice and mercy. In the cross, "justice smiled and mercy flowed" — redemption satisfies every demand of God's holy law while pouring out grace upon the guilty. This theme appears throughout the hymns: Christ's blood pays the debt justice requires, so mercy can be extended without compromising righteousness.
