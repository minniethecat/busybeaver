## Summary

[[Forgiveness]] is the divine act by which God cancels the debt of sin and restores the sinner to fellowship with himself. It is the heart of the Christian gospel: "In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins" (Ephesians 1:7). In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] treats forgiveness not as a theological abstraction but as the personal experience that transforms the believer's relationship with God — the removal of guilt that makes [[Peace]] and [[Joy]] possible.

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s own biography gave him unique authority on this theme. As a former slave trader who experienced profound conviction of sin and subsequent assurance of pardon, his forgiveness hymns carry the weight of personal testimony: "The load of sin is felt, and much forgiveness known" (PN-098-005).

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s treatment of forgiveness follows a consistent pattern: the sinner's guilt is real, the pardon is entirely of grace, and the experience of forgiveness produces love and gratitude:

> The Lord forgiveness spoke.
> — [PN-029-007]

The reference is to [[david|David]]'s forgiveness after his sin with Bathsheba (2 Samuel 12:13). [[john-newton|Newton]] draws the parallel: as [[david|David]] found pardon, so may every penitent sinner. The sheer wonder of forgiveness is never taken for granted:

> That one so vile could prove,
> Yet now forgiveness find.
> — [PN-098-001]

The hymn dramatizes the paradox: the sinner's vileness makes forgiveness more astonishing, not less available. [[john-newton|Newton]] insists that a deep sense of sin is the prerequisite for appreciating grace:

> The load of sin is felt,
> And much forgiveness known;
> Then the heart of course will melt.
> — [PN-098-005]

This "felt" forgiveness — forgiveness not merely believed but experienced — is characteristically [[john-newton|Newton]]ian. He also prays for others to find the same mercy:

> Let them find forgiveness too.
> — [PN-145-004]

And connects the awareness of needing forgiveness with humility toward others:

> Like her you knew
> How much you forgiveness need.
> — [PN-098-004]

## The Ground of Forgiveness

In evangelical theology, forgiveness is grounded in the atoning death of [[saviour|Christ]]. God does not simply overlook sin — he forgives on the basis of Christ's satisfaction of divine [[Justice]]. The cross is where "grace and justice join" (see [[justice]]), making forgiveness both free to the sinner and costly to God.

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns reflect this structure: the sinner comes with nothing, pleads nothing, and receives everything. Forgiveness is not earned by [[Repentance]] but accompanied by it — the repentant heart is the *recipient*, not the *cause*, of pardon.
