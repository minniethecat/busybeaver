## Definition

Intercession is Christ's continuing work in heaven, where he acts as advocate and mediator for believers. Having completed his atoning work on the cross, Christ now "ever lives to make intercession" (Hebrews 7:25), presenting his sacrifice and praying for his people before the Father's throne.

## Biblical Basis

Romans 8:34 declares that "Christ Jesus is the one who died — more than that, who was raised — who is at the right hand of God, who indeed is interceding for us." Hebrews 7:25 affirms that Christ "is able to save to the uttermost those who draw near to God through him, since he always lives to make intercession for them." The doctrine assures believers that their salvation is secured not only by Christ's past death but by his present advocacy.

## In the Olney Hymns

Newton found deep confidence in Christ's intercessory work. "In my Saviour's intercession Therefore I will still confide" [PN=293-005] expresses the believer's reliance on Christ's heavenly pleading rather than on personal merit. This hymn connects intercession with the free confession of sin — "Lord, accept my free confession, I have sinn'd" [PN=293-005] — showing that Newton understood intercession as the ground on which a penitent sinner may approach God without fear.

## Related Concepts

Christ's intercession is inseparable from his other saving works. [[atonement]] provides the finished sacrifice that intercession pleads before the Father — the cross and the throne are two aspects of one priestly ministry. [[prayer]] is the believer's participation in Christ's intercessory pattern. [[salvation]] is secured "to the uttermost" precisely because Christ ever lives to intercede. [[faith]] rests on the assurance that Christ's present advocacy guarantees the believer's final acceptance.
