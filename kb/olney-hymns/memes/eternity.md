## Definition

Eternity is the realm of existence beyond temporal limits — the unending duration that belongs to God by nature and into which every human soul will enter at death. As a theological concept, it confronts believers with both the brevity of earthly life and the weight of everlasting consequences that depend upon one's relationship with God through Christ.

## Biblical Basis

Ecclesiastes 3:11 declares that God "has put eternity into man's heart," suggesting an innate human awareness of the infinite. John 3:16 contrasts two eternal destinies: believers receive "eternal life" while those who reject Christ "perish." The New Testament consistently presents the believer's hope as grounded in the promise of eternal blessedness in God's presence (2 Corinthians 4:17–18).

## In the Olney Hymns

The Olney Hymns call believers to live with eternity in view. Newton urged: "Teach me, henceforth, how to live With eternity in view" [PN=142-003], recognizing that an eternal perspective transforms daily conduct. The brevity of life sharpens this urgency: "Teach us, Lord, each one to think Vast eternity is near, I am standing on the brink" [PN=144-003]. Yet eternity is also the horizon of gratitude, as believers will praise God "In time and to eternity, To owe thee more and more" [PN=308-007]. For Newton, eternity was not a distant abstraction but the immediate framework within which every soul stands.

## Related Concepts

Eternity shapes the Olney Hymns' treatment of several theological themes. [[judgment]] is the event that determines one's eternal state — the hymns' urgency about eternity is inseparable from their warnings about the Day of Judgment. [[heaven]] is the blessed side of eternity, the destination of the redeemed. [[hope]] in the Olney Hymns is predominantly eschatological — hope directed toward the eternal inheritance. [[time]] — or more precisely, the brevity of earthly time — is the foil against which eternity's vastness is measured.
