## Location

The **Temple** stood on Mount Moriah in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], the same site where [[abraham|Abraham]] was commanded to sacrifice [[isaac|Isaac]] (2 Chronicles 3:1). The First Temple was constructed under [[solomon|Solomon]]'s reign (c. 957 BC) following plans given to [[david|David]], Solomon's father. It was the permanent successor to the portable Tabernacle that had accompanied [[israel|Israel]] through the wilderness.

The Temple complex included the Holy Place and the Holy of Holies — the innermost sanctuary where the Ark of the Covenant rested and where the high priest entered once a year on the Day of Atonement. Destroyed by the Babylonians in 586 BC, rebuilt under Zerubbabel (c. 516 BC), and extensively expanded by Herod the Great, the Second Temple was destroyed by the Romans in AD 70.

## Biblical Significance

The Temple was the center of Israel's religious life — the place of sacrifice, festival pilgrimage, and priestly ministry. It represented God's dwelling among his people (1 Kings 8:10–13). At its dedication, Solomon's prayer acknowledged that "heaven and the highest heaven cannot contain you; how much less this house that I have built" (1 Kings 8:27). The Temple was thus a sacrament of presence — a tangible sign pointing beyond itself to the transcendent God who condescended to dwell with his people.

In the New Testament, the Temple is the setting for key events in the life of Jesus: his presentation as an infant (Luke 2), his teaching and cleansing of the money-changers (Matthew 21:12–13), and the rending of the Temple veil at his crucifixion (Matthew 27:51). That torn veil signified the end of the old order — access to God was no longer mediated through priests and curtains but opened directly through Christ's flesh (Hebrews 10:19–20).

Christian theology extends the Temple theme in two directions: the individual believer as a temple of the Holy Spirit (1 Corinthians 6:19), and the [[church]] collectively as God's temple (Ephesians 2:21). The heavenly Temple appears in Revelation as the dwelling place of God where worship is eternal (Revelation 7:15).

## In the Olney Hymns

The Temple appears in Book I, Hymn 96, where [[John Newton]] reflects on God's illuminating presence among his people:

> And if thou make thy temple shine,
> Yet, self-abased, will I repine.
> (096-006)

Newton uses the image of the Temple shining with God's glory to express the believer's posture of humility. Even when favored with divine brightness — when the temple shines — the worshiper remains "self-abased" before God. This is characteristically Newtonian: doctrine pressed into the service of piety.

The Temple is also implicitly present in the Olney Hymns through the broader themes of worship, sacrifice, and divine presence. The hymns that speak of approaching God's throne, offering spiritual sacrifices, and dwelling in God's house all draw on biblical Temple imagery. For [[John Newton]] and [[william-cowper|William Cowper]], the Temple was not merely an archaeological artifact — it was a living theological symbol, signifying access to God through Christ, the true temple (John 2:19–21).

## Theological Symbolism

The Temple in Scripture operates at multiple levels: historical building, national shrine, and eschatological hope. The Olney Hymns engage primarily with the third — the Temple as a type of the believer's communion with God. When Newton writes of God making "thy temple shine," he is not describing architectural restoration but spiritual illumination: God's presence brightening the gathered church.

This symbolic reading reflects the broader Evangelical hermeneutic: Old Testament institutions find their fulfillment in Christ and their application in the experience of the believer. The physical Temple was destroyed; the spiritual temple — the church indwelt by the Spirit — endures.
