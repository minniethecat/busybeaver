## Identity

[[Eve]] was the first woman, created by God from [[Adam]]'s rib as a "helper fit for him" (Genesis 2:18). As the mother of all living (Genesis 3:20), she holds a unique place in the biblical narrative. Deceived by the serpent, she ate the forbidden fruit and gave some to [[adam|Adam]], participating in the fall of humanity. Yet the promise of redemption — that her Seed would crush the serpent's head (Genesis 3:15) — was given in the very context of her transgression. In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] treats Eve within the broader framework of the fall, focusing on the grace that overcomes human sin.

## Biblical Context

Eve's story unfolds in Genesis 2–4. Formed from [[adam|Adam]]'s side, she was brought to him as his companion and wife. The serpent targeted her with the temptation to eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, promising, "You will be like God, knowing good and evil" (Genesis 3:5). She ate and gave to [[adam|Adam]]. After their eyes were opened, God pronounced judgment: pain in childbearing, submission to her husband, and eventual death. Yet in the midst of judgment came the protevangelium — the first gospel promise: "I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and her offspring; he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel" (Genesis 3:15). Eve later bore [[Cain]], [[Abel]], Seth, and other children.

## In the Olney Hymns

The Olney Hymns do not mention Eve by name, but she is present wherever [[adam|Adam]] and the fall are treated. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s Hymn 1 ("[[adam|Adam]]") opens the collection by describing the fall's consequences — "When [[adam|Adam]] fell he quickly lost / The image of his God" [PN-1001-001] — and Eve is the unnamed partner in this event. The garden imagery of Hymn 95 ("The Garden") likewise recalls the primal pair:

> Eden was Adam's dwelling-place,
> A garden stored with sweets;
> But he who dwelt in Paradise
> Transgressed and was cast out.
> — [PN-2236-003]

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s emphasis on unasked grace — "grace, unasked, his heart subdued" [PN-1001-006] — applies equally to Eve. Neither sought mercy; both received it. The promise of the Seed (Genesis 3:15) was pronounced in Eve's hearing, making her the first recipient of the gospel promise. The New Testament would later call Christ the "second [[adam|Adam]]" (Romans 5, 1 Corinthians 15); by extension, the Church is understood as the second Eve — the bride of Christ, just as Eve was the bride of [[adam|Adam]].
