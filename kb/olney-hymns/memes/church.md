## Identity

**The Church** (Greek: ἐκκλησία, *ekklēsia*; "assembly" or "called-out ones") is the community of all who belong to [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] through [[faith]] — the assembly of the redeemed across all times and places, gathered by [[grace]] and destined for glory. In the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], the Church is never referred to as an institution, a denomination, or a building. It is always a *people* — those "ransomed" by Christ's blood, planted in God's garden, and journeying toward "the church above."

[[john-newton|John Newton]] and [[william-cowper|William Cowper]] were clergymen in the Church of England, yet their hymns treat the Church in consistently spiritual rather than institutional terms. The hymns describe the Church as Christ's bride (137-002), God's garden (103-001, 236-006), and a mixed assembly of wheat and tares awaiting final separation (086-001). This reflects the Evangelical emphasis on the invisible church — the true spiritual body of believers known fully only to God.

## Biblical Basis

The New Testament uses a rich variety of images for the Church, and the hymns draw on most of them:

| Image | Scripture | Hymn Expression |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| The Body of Christ | 1 Corinthians 12:27; Ephesians 1:22–23 | Christ as Head; believers as members |
| The Bride of Christ | Ephesians 5:25–27; Revelation 19:7 | "His church, his chosen bride" (137-002) |
| God's Garden / Vineyard | John 15:1–8; 1 Corinthians 3:6–9 | "The church a garden is" (103-001) |
| The Building / Temple | Ephesians 2:19–22; 1 Peter 2:4–5 | "The church is on him built" (078-001) |
| Wheat and Tares | Matthew 13:24–30 | "The wheat and tares together grow" (086-001) |
| The Seven Churches | Revelation 2–3 | Letters to Sardis, Ephesus (136-001, 138-001) |

The hymns move freely between these images, often mixing metaphors within a single stanza — the Church is simultaneously a garden *and* a building *and* a bride because no single image exhausts its nature.

## In the Olney Hymns

The Church appears in at least a dozen hymns, addressed through four central themes:

### Christ as the Church's Foundation

The most fundamental claim about the Church is that it rests on Christ alone:

> Jesus Christ, the Lord's Anointed,
> Who his blood for sinners spilt,
> Is the Stone by God appointed,
> And the church is on him built.
> — [078-001]

The "Stone" imagery draws on Isaiah 28:16 and 1 Peter 2:6 — Christ as the cornerstone, the only sure foundation. The Church is not built on human effort, apostolic succession, or institutional continuity but on the person and work of Christ alone.

### The Church as Mixed Assembly

The hymns refuse to idealize the visible church. Within its earthly membership, genuine believers and nominal professors coexist — and only the final judgment will separate them:

> Though in the outward church below
> The wheat and tares together grow,
> Jesus ere long will weed the crop,
> And pluck the tares in anger up.
> — [086-001]

This is a direct allusion to Jesus' parable of the wheat and tares (Matthew 13:24–30). [[john-newton|Newton]]'s pastoral realism is evident: the mixed character of the visible church is not a cause for despair but a reminder that final judgment belongs to Christ alone. The believer's task is not to purify the church — Christ will do that "ere long" — but to ensure that he himself is wheat and not a tare.

### The Church as Garden

Two hymns develop the image of the Church as God's cultivated garden:

> The church a garden is,
> In which believers stand
> Like ornamental trees
> Planted by God's own hand!
> His Spirit waters all their roots,
> And every branch abounds with fruits.
> — [103-001]

> His church as a fair garden stands,
> Which walls of love enclose;
> Each tree is planted by his hands,
> And by his blessing grows.
> — [236-006]

The garden imagery emphasizes divine initiative (God plants), divine sustenance (the Spirit waters), and divine protection ("walls of love"). The Church is not a wild forest but a cultivated garden — ordered, tended, and fruitful. Individual believers are "trees" whose fruitfulness depends entirely on the Gardener's care.

### The Church Addressed in Warning

Hymns 136–138 draw on Christ's letters to the seven churches in Revelation 2–3, applying them directly to eighteenth-century congregations:

> "Write to Sardis," (saith the Lord,)
> And write what he declares...
> "Thou art call'd a living church,
> But thou art cold and dead."
> — [138-001]

The application is bold: the words addressed to a first-century Asian congregation are addressed to [[john-newton|Newton]]'s and [[william-cowper|Cowper]]'s own hearers. "Thou art called a living church, but thou art cold and dead" — the gap between reputation and reality is the constant danger of institutional Christianity.

## Historical Context

In 1779, when the Olney Hymns was published, the Church of England was the established church, but Evangelical clergy like [[john-newton|Newton]] were a minority within it, often viewed with suspicion by the ecclesiastical hierarchy. The hymns' consistent emphasis on the spiritual rather than institutional nature of the Church reflects this marginal position: [[john-newton|Newton]] and [[william-cowper|Cowper]] were loyal Anglicans who believed that belonging to the true Church was a matter of regeneration, not registration.

The Olney parish itself was a center of Evangelical revival, with [[john-newton|Newton]]'s preaching drawing hearers from surrounding villages. The hymns were written for this congregation — ordinary Buckinghamshire laborers, farmers, and tradespeople — and the images of the Church as garden, flock, and family would have resonated with their daily experience in ways that abstract ecclesiology could not.
