## Definition

**Power**, in the theological vocabulary of the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], denotes the sovereign, effectual might of God by which he creates, saves, sustains, and ultimately delivers his people. It is intimately linked with [[grace]] — divine power is the executive force of divine favor. The hymns consistently portray God's power not as abstract omnipotence but as the specific strength that rescues helpless sinners and keeps them secure.

For [[john-newton|John Newton]], the doctrine of divine power was deeply personal. A former slave-ship captain who had nearly perished at sea, Newton knew what it meant to be powerless and delivered by a strength beyond his own. His hymns never treat power as a theoretical attribute; they invoke it as the believer's only reliable resource in a world of danger and spiritual opposition.

## Biblical Basis

The Olney Hymns root the theme of divine power in Scripture's consistent testimony that strength belongs to God alone:

| Passage | Theme | Hymn Expression |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Psalm 62:11 | "Power belongs to God" | "The Lord is our power, The Lord will provide" (007-007) |
| Romans 1:16 | The gospel as "the power of God unto salvation" | "He alone has power to save" (023-001) |
| Ephesians 1:19–20 | The immeasurable greatness of his power toward believers | "Fresh strength they gain to run their race" (025-003) |
| 1 Corinthians 1:24 | Christ as "the power of God" | Power displayed in Christ's saving work |

The hymns emphasize that power is not shared with human agencies — it is God's exclusive prerogative in the work of [[salvation]].

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|John Newton]]'s hymns deploy the word "power" across multiple dimensions of the believer's experience:

### Power to Save

The central claim is that only God possesses saving power, which he exercises through Christ. This is the gospel's distinctive claim:

> Seed he view'd, and felt His power to save.
> — [001-006]

> He alone has power to save, And claims the glory as his due.
> — [023-001]

The repetition of "power to save" across hymns 001 and 023 underscores its foundational role. Salvation is not a cooperative venture between God and man; it is an exercise of divine power from start to finish. The second line adds a polemical edge: because salvation is entirely God's work, the [[glory]] belongs entirely to him.

### Power in Creation and Providence

This saving power is not confined to redemption — it is the same power displayed in the natural order and daily provision:

> Bless'd proofs of power and grace divine / That meet us in the world.
> — [006-005]

> It fell, and own'd the power divine, / (Grace can with ease remove).
> — [026-002]

The phrase "proofs of power and grace divine" (006-005) pairs the two attributes — power and [[grace]] — as twin witnesses in creation. Every mercy in the natural world testifies to the God who both orders and redeems. The second citation (026-002) applies power to the removal of obstacles: what blocks the believer's path falls before divine power as effortlessly as [[grace]] removes guilt.

### Power Against Rebellion

Divine power does not merely create and sustain — it also confronts human defiance, not only to judge but to subdue and transform:

> That poor rebel, Lord, behold, / Scorn thy grace, thy power defy.
> — [010-003]

The rebel who "scorns grace" and "defies power" is depicted as spiritually suicidal — rejecting the only forces that can save him. The verse is a plea for divine intervention against hardened unbelief. It reflects Newton's Calvinist conviction that only sovereign power can overcome the will's resistance.

### Power Displayed in Judgment and Mercy

The tension between power-as-threat and power-as-mercy is captured in the story of [[joseph|Joseph]] and his brothers:

> For Joshua had no power to spare, / If he had known from whence they were.
> — [021-004]

This reference to Joshua's treatment of the Gibeonites (Joshua 9) becomes a lesson in limited human power. Human authorities lack the full knowledge needed for perfect judgment; only God's power is exercised with complete wisdom. The subtext is that believers rest in divine power precisely because human power is insufficient.

### Power in Weakness

Yet this sovereign power is not experienced as domination but as strength given to the weak. Human inability is set against divine sufficiency:

> Men have not power or skill / With troubled souls to bear.
> — [025-006]

This candid assessment of human limitation is the ground of the hymn's confidence: because power belongs to God and not to man, the troubled soul looks beyond human helpers to the divine source. The pastoral implication is that ministers and friends should not pretend to a power they do not possess.

### Power that Preserves

Divine power guarantees the believer's security, forming an unassailable defense:

> The Lord is our power, / The Lord will provide.
> — [007-007]

> Are safe from all their power; / Fresh strength they gain to run their race.
> — [025-003]

The first couplet (007-007) pairs power with provision — the God who defends also supplies. The second (025-003) contrasts hostile human power ("their power") with divine empowerment: the persecuted are not merely protected but strengthened, gaining "fresh strength" to continue the race. This is power not as removal from danger but as enablement within it.

### Power that Quickens

Beyond protection, divine power also animates the believer from within — awakening dormant spiritual faculties and directing them toward God:

> Lord, quicken all our drowsy powers / To flee to thee and live.
> — [005-008]

The word "drowsy" captures the natural spiritual lethargy that afflicts even believers. The prayer is for divine power to rouse what is sluggish, directing renewed energy toward the one right object — flight to God. "Quicken" carries the sense of both awakening and making alive; it is a prayer for revival.
