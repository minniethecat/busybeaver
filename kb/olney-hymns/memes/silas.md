## Summary

[[Silas]] (also called Silvanus) was a Jewish Christian leader in the Jerusalem church and a companion of the Apostle [[paul|Paul]] on his second missionary journey. He is first mentioned in Acts 15:22 as one of the "leading men among the brothers" chosen to deliver the Jerusalem Council's decree to Antioch. He later joined [[paul|Paul]]'s mission through Asia Minor and Greece, enduring imprisonment and persecution with him at Philippi (Acts 16:19–40).

In the Olney Hymns, though [[Silas]] is not directly named, the themes he exemplifies — missionary companionship, fearless witness, and singing hymns in prison at midnight — resonate deeply with [[john-newton|Newton]]'s own experience and hymnody.

## Biblical Role

[[Silas]] emerges in Acts 15 as a trusted delegate of the Jerusalem church, sent with [[paul|Paul]] and Barnabas to communicate the council's decision that Gentile converts need not be circumcised. After Barnabas and [[paul|Paul]] parted ways over [[mark|John Mark]], [[paul|Paul]] chose [[Silas]] as his new traveling companion (Acts 15:40).

The most dramatic episode of [[Silas]]'s ministry occurred at Philippi. After [[paul|Paul]] cast a spirit of divination out of a slave girl, he and [[Silas]] were beaten with rods and imprisoned. "About midnight [[paul|Paul]] and [[Silas]] were praying and singing hymns to God, and the prisoners were listening" (Acts 16:25). An earthquake opened the prison doors, and the jailer and his household were converted.

[[Silas]] later appears with [[paul|Paul]] at Thessalonica (Acts 17:4), Berea (Acts 17:10), and Corinth (Acts 18:5). He is named as co-sender of the Thessalonian letters (1 Thessalonians 1:1; 2 Thessalonians 1:1). In 1 Peter 5:12, [[peter|Peter]] names "Silvanus" as the one through whom he writes — suggesting [[Silas]] also served as Peter's amanuensis.

## In the Olney Hymns

Though never directly named, [[Silas]]'s midnight hymn-singing at Philippi stands behind [[john-newton|Newton]]'s conviction that praise is the Christian's weapon in affliction. [[john-newton|Newton]] himself knew imprisonment and peril at sea, and his hymns consistently link suffering with song:

> Though dark be my way, since He is my guide,
> 'Tis mine to obey, 'tis His to provide.
> — [PN-022-003]

The imprisoned [[Silas]], singing in the darkness while his feet were fastened in stocks, embodies exactly the faith that [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns seek to cultivate: praise is not the fruit of comfortable circumstances but the exercise of trust when circumstances are at their worst.

## Theological Significance

[[Silas]] demonstrates that the apostolic mission was not a solitary endeavor but a shared labor. His partnership with [[paul|Paul]] and his role as amanuensis for [[peter|Peter]] underscore the collaborative character of early Christian ministry. His midnight hymnody at Philippi also shows that Christian worship is not confined to sacred spaces — a prison cell, like the hold of a slave ship, can become a sanctuary through praise.
