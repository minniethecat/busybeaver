## Location

**Mount Sinai** (Hebrew: סִינַי, *Sīnay*; also called **Horeb**) is the mountain in the Sinai Peninsula where, according to Exodus 19–20, God descended in fire and gave the Law to [[moses|Moses]]. Its exact location is uncertain — the traditional site of Jabal Musa ("Mountain of [[moses|Moses]]") in the southern Sinai has been identified since the fourth century — but its theological location in the biblical narrative is precise: Sinai stands at the boundary between [[egypt|Egypt]] and [[canaan|Canaan]], between slavery and promise, between the old life and the new.

In the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], Sinai is never described as a place one might visit. It is always a *sound* — thunder, the fiery law, the voice that makes even [[moses|Moses]] quake. Sinai is what the law sounds like.

## Biblical Significance

Sinai is the mountain of the covenant. Here God gave the Ten Commandments (Exodus 20), the Book of the Covenant (Exodus 21–23), and the instructions for the Tabernacle (Exodus 25–31). Here also [[israel|Israel]] committed its defining apostasy — the golden calf (Exodus 32), fashioned even while the glory of God was visible on the mountain above them.

|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| The giving of the Law | Exodus 19–20 | God's holy standard, revealing human sinfulness |
| The golden calf | Exodus 32 | [[israel|Israel]]'s inability to keep the law even at the mountain where it was given |
| Elijah at Horeb | 1 Kings 19 | The still small voice — grace after the thunder |

The New Testament explicitly contrasts Sinai and [[zion|Zion]] (Hebrews 12:18–24), and the Olney Hymns develop this contrast at length. Sinai is the mountain that *cannot* be touched without dying; [[zion|Zion]] is the mountain where God dwells with his people in grace.

## In the Olney Hymns

Sinai appears in the hymns almost exclusively as a foil — the holy terror from which [[grace]] delivers:

### The Fiery Law

The defining Sinai moment is the giving of the law amid fire, smoke, and thunder:

> When Israel heard the fiery law
> From Sinai's top proclaim'd,
> Their hearts seem'd full of holy awe,
> Their stubborn spirits tamed.
> — [018-001]

But the "taming" is temporary. The very next stanza exposes [[israel|Israel]]'s fickleness:

> Yet, as forgetting all they knew,
> Ere forty days were past,
> With blazing Sinai still in view,
> A molten calf they cast.
> — [018-002]

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s point is devastating: even with Sinai visibly blazing before them, [[israel|Israel]] worshiped a calf. The law cannot change the heart — it can only command and condemn. The golden calf episode is not an anomaly but a demonstration of the law's inherent limitation.

### Moses at Sinai

[[eve|Eve]]n the mediator of the law trembles before it:

> The holy Moses quaked and fear'd
> When Sinai's thund'ring law he heard:
> But reigning grace, with accents mild,
> Speaks to the sinner as a child.
> — [158-003]

The contrast is stark: [[moses|Moses]] — "holy [[moses|Moses]]" — quakes; the sinner — any sinner — hears a gentle voice. The law terrifies even the lawgiver; grace comforts even the transgressor. This is the fundamental Sinai-[[zion|Zion]] antithesis that structures the hymns' soteriology.

### Sinai and the Rod

[[moses|Moses]]' rod, the instrument of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian plagues and the Red Sea parting, is associated with Sinai as the symbol of divinely delegated power:

> When Moses waved his mystic rod,
> What wonders follow'd while he spoke!
> Firm as a wall the waters stood,
> Or gush'd in rivers from the rock.
> — [157-001]

Yet the hymns immediately qualify:

> But what could Moses' rod have done
> Had he not been divinely sent?
> The power was from the Lord alone,
> And Moses but the instrument.
> — [157-003]

The rod is not magic; it is ministry. Sinai's power is not its own — it is the power of the God who descends upon it.

## Theological Significance

Sinai represents a fundamental theological category in the Olney Hymns: the law in its holiness, goodness, and inability to save. The hymns never disparage the law — it is "fiery" and "holy" — but they insist that the law can only do what law does: command, expose, and condemn. It cannot give life.

This is the classic Reformed law-gospel hermeneutic: the law drives the sinner to Christ by revealing the depth of sin and the impossibility of self-justification. Sinai's thunder prepares the ear for [[zion|Zion]]'s gentle voice. Without Sinai, grace would be cheap; without [[zion|Zion]], Sinai would be despair. The hymns hold both mountains together.
