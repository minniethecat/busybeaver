## Identity

[[Adam]] was the first man, created by God in His own image and placed in the Garden of [[Eden]]. As the federal head of humanity, his disobedience in eating the forbidden fruit brought sin and death upon all his descendants. The New Testament contrasts Adam with [[saviour|Christ]] as the "second Adam" whose obedience brings life (Romans 5:12–19). In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] uses Adam as the archetype of fallen humanity — and of grace received without asking.

## Biblical Context

Adam's story spans Genesis 1–5. Created from dust and given dominion over creation, he dwelt in [[eden|Eden]] with [[Eve]], walking in confidence with God. After transgressing God's command regarding the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, Adam hid from God, cast blame on [[eve|Eve]] (and implicitly on God Himself: "the woman whom You gave me"), and was expelled from the garden. Despite his sin, God provided covering and promised a [[saviour|Redeemer]] — the "Seed" who would crush the serpent's head (Genesis 3:15).

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] opens his collection with Adam — Hymn 1 of Book I — establishing the theological foundation for all that follows:

> When Adam fell he quickly lost
> The image of his God;
> His heart from God and truth estranged —
> His conscience filled with dread!
> — [PN-1001-001]

The fall transforms Adam from one who walked confidently with God to one who flees and hides. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s Adam is not merely the first sinner but [[eve|Eve]]ryman — our representative and our mirror:

> To Adam, soon as he transgressed,
> Thus spoke the Lord, "Where art thou?"
> — [PN-1173-007]

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s key insight about Adam is the nature of his restoration: "grace, unasked, his heart subdued." Adam did not seek mercy — he blamed and hid. Yet grace came unbidden, and by faith Adam "viewed the promised Seed" — Christ. This is the pattern of salvation [[john-newton|Newton]] sees repeated in every believer.

The garden imagery returns in [[john-newton|Newton]]'s "The Garden" (Hymn 95), where [[eden|Eden]] serves as a place of contemplation:

> Eden was Adam's dwelling-place,
> A garden stored with sweets.
> — [PN-2236-002]
