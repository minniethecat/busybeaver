## Identity

[[Cain]] was the firstborn son of [[Adam]] and [[Eve]], a tiller of the ground whose offering of crops was rejected by God while his brother [[Abel]]'s offering of the firstborn of his flock was accepted. In jealous rage, Cain murdered [[abel|Abel]] — the first murder in human history. God marked Cain and sent him out as a fugitive, yet also placed a protective mark upon him. In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] uses Cain as the type of the unregenerate sinner who envies and persecutes the righteous.

## Biblical Context

Cain's story occupies Genesis 4:1–16. When God "had regard for [[abel|Abel]] and his offering" but not for Cain's, Cain's face fell with anger. God warned him: "[[sin|Sin]] is crouching at the door. Its desire is contrary to you, but you must rule over it" (Genesis 4:7). Cain ignored the warning, lured [[abel|Abel]] into the field, and killed him. Confronted by God — "Where is [[abel|Abel]] your brother?" — Cain uttered the evasive reply: "Am I my brother's keeper?" (Genesis 4:9). God cursed Cain from the ground and made him a restless wanderer, yet set a mark on him so none would kill him. The New Testament holds Cain up as a warning: "We should not be like Cain, who was of the evil one and murdered his brother" (1 John 3:12).

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] uses Cain-[[abel|Abel]] typology to explore persecution and grace:

> By Cain's own hand good Abel died,
> Because the Lord approved his faith.
> — [PN-1002-004]

The cause of [[abel|Abel]]'s death — divine approval of his faith — is the very evidence of his blessedness. [[john-newton|Newton]] draws a stark contrast:

> Such was the wicked murderer Cain,
> Who slew his righteous brother.
> — [PN-1002-005]

But [[john-newton|Newton]] makes a surprising move: the speaker identifies with Cain, not [[abel|Abel]]. In Hymn 247, the trembling murderer becomes a candidate for grace:

> So Cain murdered Abel,
> And trembled like me.
> — [PN-3247-002]

This is characteristically [[john-newton|Newton]]ian: even Cain — the archetypal murderer — is not beyond the reach of grace. If the blood of Christ speaks "better things than [[abel|Abel]]'s" (Hebrews 12:24), then even Cain's sin falls within the scope of redemption.
