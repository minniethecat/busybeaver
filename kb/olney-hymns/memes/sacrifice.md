## Definition

In the Olney Hymns, [[Sacrifice]] is understood through the lens of biblical typology: the animal sacrifices of the Old Testament — Cain and Abel's offerings, the Passover lamb, the Levitical system — are not ends in themselves but *figures* and *shadows* that point forward to the one true sacrifice of [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] on the cross. The hymns treat the sacrificial system as a divinely-given "glass" through which believers under the old covenant could, by faith, see the coming Redeemer.

## Biblical Basis

The sacrificial theme runs from Genesis through Revelation, and the Olney Hymns draw on multiple strata of the biblical witness:

| Passage | Sacrificial Theme | Hymn Connection |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Genesis 4:3–8 | Abel's acceptable offering vs Cain's rejected one | Hymn 2: "The sacrifice the Lord ordain'd" |
| Exodus 12:1–13 | The Passover lamb and blood on the doorposts | Hymn 132: "The paschal sacrifice" |
| Leviticus 16 | The Day of Atonement — two goats, blood-sprinkled mercy seat | Typological background for Christ's once-for-all offering |
| Isaiah 53:7 | The suffering servant "brought as a lamb to the slaughter" | Christ as the willing victim |
| Hebrews 9:22 | "Without shedding of blood is no remission" | The necessity of blood atonement |
| Hebrews 10:10–14 | Christ "offered one sacrifice for sins for ever" | The finality and sufficiency of the cross |

Abel's acceptable offering (Genesis 4:4), the institution of the Passover (Exodus 12), and the Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16) establish the principle that sin requires a blood sacrifice for forgiveness. The New Testament declares these to be "a shadow of the good things to come" (Hebrews 10:1), fulfilled in Christ who "offered one sacrifice for sins for ever" (Hebrews 10:12).

## In the Olney Hymns

The Olney Hymns develop the theme of sacrifice along two complementary lines: the insufficiency of human offerings and the all-sufficiency of Christ's blood.

### Cain's Rejected Offering: The Divine Pattern

[[John Newton]] opens the collection with the story of [[Cain]] and Abel in Hymn 2:

> The sacrifice the Lord ordain'd
> In type of the Redeemer's blood
> Self-righteous reasoning Cain disdain'd,
> And thought his own first-fruits as good.
> — [PN-002-002]

The contrast is sharp: God's appointed sacrifice (pointing to Christ) versus human self-righteousness. Cain's offering was rejected not because it lacked quality but because it ignored the divine pattern — a pattern that, for [[john-newton|Newton]], is the gospel in embryo. The "sacrifice the Lord ordain'd" is the only acceptable approach to God; human-devised religion, however sincere, cannot substitute for divinely-appointed atonement.

### The Paschal Type and its Antitype

[[William Cowper]] draws the same typological lesson from the Passover in Hymn 132:

> The paschal sacrifice,
> And blood-be-sprinkled door,
> Seen with enlighten'd eyes,
> And once applied with power,
> Would teach the need of other blood
> To reconcile an angry God.
> — [PN-132-002]

The "other blood" is Christ's. The paschal lamb saved Israel from the destroyer, but it could not reconcile them to God — only the antitype could. Cowper's phrase "seen with enlighten'd eyes" is crucial: the types were always present in Scripture, but only faith — eyes opened by grace — could read them aright. The Old Testament believer who looked *through* the sacrifice to the coming Redeemer was saved by the same blood as the New Testament believer, though the revelation was dimmer.

### The Believer's Comfort

Cowper also expresses the personal, devotional response to the sacrifice in Hymn 58:

> Comfortable thoughts arise
> From the bleeding sacrifice.
> — [PN-058-001]

The sacrifice is not merely a doctrine to be assented to; it is the ground of the believer's comfort. The bleeding of Christ produces the healing of the conscience. In the Olney Hymns, the cross is never merely a theological abstraction — it is the place where guilty consciences find peace, where "comfortable thoughts" displace the terrors of the law.

### Sacrifice and the Life of Faith

The hymns also apply the theme of sacrifice to the believer's own life. Newton frequently draws the implication that if Christ has offered the one sufficient sacrifice, the believer's response is not to offer further atoning sacrifices but to present himself as a "living sacrifice" (Romans 12:1) — a life of grateful obedience, not meritorious self-denial. The cross ends all attempts to purchase divine favour; what remains is the sacrifice of praise and thanksgiving.
