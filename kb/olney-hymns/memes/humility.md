## Definition

Humility in the Olney Hymns is not a natural disposition but a spiritual grace — the honest acknowledgment before God that one is a sinner saved by mercy alone, possessing nothing that was not received as a gift. It is the opposite of pride, which [[john-newton|Newton]] identifies as the root sin, and it is the characteristic posture of every true believer: "God opposes the proud but gives [[grace|grace]] to the humble" (James 4:6).

## Biblical Basis

The call to humility runs through both Testaments, finding its supreme expression in the incarnation and death of Christ:

| Passage | Theme | Hymn Connection |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Micah 6:8 | "Walk humbly with your God" | The Old Testament summary of true religion |
| Isaiah 66:2 | "This is the one to whom I will look: he who is humble and contrite in spirit" | God's regard for the lowly |
| Philippians 2:5–8 | Christ "humbled Himself by becoming obedient to the point of death" | The supreme pattern of humility |
| James 4:6 | "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble" | The divine economy of grace |
| 1 Peter 5:5–6 | "Clothe yourselves with humility … humble yourselves under the mighty hand of God" | The apostolic command |

The supreme biblical example of humility is Christ Himself, who "humbled Himself by becoming obedient to the point of death" (Philippians 2:8). His incarnation, servanthood, and crucifixion are the pattern for Christian humility: not thinking less of oneself but thinking of oneself less, and counting others more significant.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s treatment of humility is shaped by his own biography. The former slave trader who became a pastor never forgot that he was "the chief of sinners" shown mercy.

### Humility as Self-Knowledge

[[john-newton|Newton]] insists that true humility begins with honest self-assessment — "I am what I am by the [[grace|grace]] of God" (cf. 1 Corinthians 15:10). His hymns repeatedly juxtapose the sinner's unworthiness with God's mercy. This is not false modesty but theological realism: the person who grasps the holiness of God and the depth of their own sin cannot be proud. Newton's famous self-description — "I am a great sinner, and Christ is a great Saviour" — captures the experiential root of his teaching on humility.

### Humility and Prayer

The prayers in the Olney Hymns are almost always cast from the posture of humble dependence. The speaker does not demand; he pleads. He does not stand; he kneels. Newton models prayer not as the assertion of rights but as the supplication of need. The humble petitioner approaches God not with a list of merits but with the single plea: "God be merciful to me, a sinner" (Luke 18:13).

### The Grace-Humility Circuit

In [[john-newton|Newton]]'s theology, humility is both the precondition for receiving grace and its product. The proud heart cannot see its need; the humbled heart sees nothing else. "Grace first inscribed the humble name on Christ's own heart" (cf. PN-189). This creates what might be called a "grace-humility circuit": grace produces humility, and humility opens the heart to receive more grace. Pride interrupts this circuit; humility sustains it.

### Humility in Suffering

[[john-newton|Newton]] counsels that affliction is God's instrument for cultivating humility. When the believer is brought low by trial, pride is mortified and dependence on God deepened. This is a hard providence but a kind one: God wounds the pride that would destroy the soul. Newton's own afflictions — the memory of his slave-trading years, the loss of his wife, the struggles of pastoral ministry — were, in his view, divine surgeries to extract the tumor of pride.

### The Social Dimension: Humility Toward Others

Newton also insists that humility toward God must express itself in humility toward others. The believer who claims to be humble before God but treats fellow believers with condescension or contempt has not understood the gospel. The cross levels all distinctions: at the foot of the cross, the slave trader and the respectable churchgoer stand on the same ground — sinners saved by grace alone.
