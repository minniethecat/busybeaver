## Definition

[[Peace]] in the biblical sense is far more than the absence of conflict — it is the positive state of wholeness, harmony, and well-being captured by the Hebrew word *shalom*.

For the Christian, peace has three dimensions: peace with God through justification (Romans 5:1), the peace of God that guards the heart (Philippians 4:7), and peace with others as far as it depends on oneself (Romans 12:18).

In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] writes of peace as the fruit of Christ's work — a gift given, not a condition achieved. [[john-newton|Newton]] himself declared, "I am a friend of peace" (P02-003), a conviction forged through his own turbulent life from slave trader to pastor.

## Biblical Foundation

Christ is repeatedly called the "Prince of Peace" (Isaiah 9:6), and His parting gift to His disciples was peace: "Peace I leave with you; my peace I give to you. Not as the world gives do I give to you" (John 14:27).

[[paul|Paul]] opens nearly every epistle with "[[grace|Grace]] to you and peace from God our Father." The foundation of Christian peace is justification: "Therefore, since we have been justified by faith, we have peace with God through our [[saviour|Lord]] [[saviour|Jesus Christ]]" (Romans 5:1). This peace is objective — a legal status — but also subjective, experienced as the Spirit's fruit (Galatians 5:22).

## In the Olney Hymns

### Peace as Spiritual Thriving

Peace is a recurring note throughout [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns, always grounded in Christ's work:

> And throve, with peace and comfort filled.
> (011-001)

The word "throve" is telling — peace is not merely a static possession but the soil in which the Christian life flourishes. Where peace is present, the soul grows; where peace is absent, the soul languishes.

### Peace Through Faith

[[john-newton|Newton]] connects peace with the healing ministry of Christ. In Hymn 14, reflecting on the woman who touched Christ's garment, he writes:

> Was answered, "Daughter, go in peace:
> Thy faith hath made thee whole."
> (014-004)

Peace and wholeness are inseparable. The woman's faith brings both physical healing and spiritual peace — a single word from Christ accomplishes what no human effort could.

### Peace Won by Christ's Death

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s most profound treatment of peace locates it at the cross. Christ's death achieves what no human diplomacy could:

> Obtains his murderers' peace with God,
> And gains them mansions in the skies.
> (002-008)

The paradox is staggering — Christ purchases peace for those who killed Him. His intercessory prayer from the cross secures the very peace His persecutors did not seek.

### Remembered Peace

[[john-newton|Newton]] is realistic about the Christian's experience of peace. It is not uninterrupted tranquility but a peace maintained through conflict:

> What peaceful hours I once enjoyed!
> How sweet their memory still!
> (003-003)

The hymns that speak of peace often do so from within the storm — peace is not the absence of waves but the presence of Christ in the boat.

### Counterfeit Peace

[[john-newton|Newton]] also warns against a false peace built on self-deception. In Hymn 21, he describes the Gibeonites who "gain'd their peace by framing lies" (021-004) — a peace secured by deceit rather than truth. True peace can never be built on pretense.
