## Summary

[[paul|Paul the Apostle]], originally Saul of Tarsus, was a [[Pharisee]] and persecutor of the early [[Church]] who encountered the risen [[saviour|Christ]] on the road to Damascus (Acts 9) and was transformed into Christianity's greatest missionary and theologian. Author of thirteen New Testament epistles, Paul articulated the doctrines of justification by [[Faith]], union with Christ, and the life of the Spirit that form the theological backbone of evangelicalism.

In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] references Paul in three distinct roles: as an example of suffering witness (imprisoned with Silas, Acts 16), as an illustration of spiritual conflict (the name of Jesus overpowering evil spirits, Acts 19), and implicitly as the theologian whose writings shaped [[john-newton|Newton]]'s understanding of [[Grace]].

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] draws on the dramatic story of Paul and Silas imprisoned in Philippi (Acts 16) to illustrate joy in suffering:

> Paul and Silas thus confined,
> Thou didst make their prison ring.
> — [PN-123-001]

[[sin|Sin]]ging hymns at midnight in a jail cell — this image of defiant worship in affliction resonated deeply with [[john-newton|Newton]], who knew imprisonment and deprivation. The power of Jesus' name is illustrated through Paul's ministry:

> And naming Jesus preach'd by Paul,
> They charged the spirit to depart.
> — [PN-124-002]

The reference is to the sons of Sceva episode (Acts 19:13–16), where Jewish exorcists attempt to use Jesus' name without faith, crying: "Jesus I know, and Paul I know; but who are you?" (PN-124-003). The demons' recognition of Paul — "Paul I know" — is ironic testimony to apostolic authority:

> The evil spirit answered then,
> "Jesus I know, and Paul I know."
> — [PN-124-003]

[[john-newton|Newton]] also alludes to Paul's legal trials:

> If Paul in Caesar's court must stand,
> He need not fear.
> — [PN-125-001]

Paul before Caesar (Acts 25–26) becomes a type of the believer before the judgment seat — confident not in personal merit but in the advocacy of Christ.

## Paul and Newton

The affinity between Paul and [[john-newton|Newton]] runs deep. Both were "blasphemers" converted by grace (1 Timothy 1:13); both understood themselves as the chief of sinners shown mercy; both became ministers of the gospel they once opposed. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s favorite self-designation — "the old African blasphemer" — echoes Paul's "I was a blasphemer." [[john-newton|Newton]]'s theology of grace is, at its core, Pauline: salvation is entirely of God, from election to glorification, leaving no room for human boasting.
