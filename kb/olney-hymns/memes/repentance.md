## Summary

[[Repentance]] is the act of turning from sin to God — a change of mind (*metanoia*), heart (godly sorrow), and life (amendment). It is, with [[Faith]], one of the two essential responses to the Gospel: "Repent and believe the good news" (Mark 1:15). In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] portrays repentance as both a divine gift ("Thou alone canst give repentance," PN-061-005) and an urgent human duty ("While yet for repentance there's room!" PN-008-002).

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s treatment of repentance avoids two extremes: it is neither a meritorious work that earns [[Forgiveness]], nor an optional extra that can be dispensed with. It is the *instrument* by which the sinner receives pardon — not the cause, but the channel.

## In the Olney Hymns

The urgency of repentance is [[john-newton|Newton]]'s most characteristic note:

> Yield to the call
> While yet for repentance there's room!
> Your season will soon be past.
> — [PN-008-002]

The temporal pressure — "while yet there's room" — reflects the evangelical conviction that the offer of mercy has an expiration: death or the hardening of the heart. Repentance cannot be indefinitely postponed.

Yet [[john-newton|Newton]] is equally insistent that true repentance is a gift of God, not a human achievement:

> Thou alone canst give repentance,
> Thou alone our souls canst heal.
> — [PN-061-005]

This paradox — repentance is commanded yet also given — is resolved in the Reformed doctrine of effectual calling: God's sovereign [[Grace]] enables the very response it demands. The hymn prays for what it commands:

> O pray for repentance
> And life-giving faith
> Before the dark winter of death.
> — [PN-242-005]

[[john-newton|Newton]] distinguishes between genuine repentance and its counterfeits. Fear of punishment alone is not enough:

> Nor can my repentance,
> Extorted by fear,
> Reverse the just sentence above.
> — [PN-247-003]

A repentance "extorted by fear" — mere terror of consequences without a change of heart — cannot satisfy divine justice. True repentance involves sorrow for sin *as sin*, not merely sorrow for sin's penalties (2 Corinthians 7:10).

## Repentance and Conversion

In Puritan and evangelical theology, repentance is the "turning" pole of conversion — turning *from* sin, while faith turns *to* Christ. The two are inseparable: there is no faith without repentance, and no genuine repentance without faith. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns hold these together: the call to repent is always accompanied by the promise that Christ receives repentant sinners.
