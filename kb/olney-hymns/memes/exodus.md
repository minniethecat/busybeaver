## The Exodus in the Olney Hymns

The Exodus from Egypt stands as one of the most powerful types of redemption in the Olney Hymns. For John Newton and William Cowper, the journey of Israel from bondage through the wilderness to the Promised Land mirrors the Christian's passage from sin through sanctification to eternal rest.

Six Book I hymns draw directly from Exodus narratives. **The Bitter Waters** (Hymn 13, Exodus 15:23–25) uses the bitter spring at Marah as a metaphor for the soul's desert trials. **Manna** (Hymn 15, Exodus 16:18) and **Manna Hoarded** (Hymn 16, Exodus 16:20) explore divine provision and the danger of hoarding grace. **The Golden Calf** (Hymn 18, Exodus 32) warns against idolatry even after receiving God's law.

## Theological Typology

The Exodus provides a complete typological framework. **Jehovah-Rophi** (Hymn 14, Exodus 15) presents God as the healer of His people. **Jehovah-Nissi** (Hymn 17, Exodus 17:15) portrays God as the banner under which spiritual battles are fought. The tabernacle and its furnishings, the manna, the water from the rock — all become spiritual metaphors for Christ and the means of grace.

## Newton's Personal Exodus

Newton, who once commanded a slave ship and experienced his own spiritual deliverance, understood the Exodus narrative viscerally. His hymns on Exodus themes carry a personal urgency that transforms the ancient story into present-tense devotion.

The parallel between Newton's own life and the Exodus pattern is striking. As a young man, Newton was enslaved — not in Egypt but in West Africa, serving under a cruel mistress after being abandoned by his ship. His rescue in 1748, dispatched by his father's search party, gave him a personal experience of deliverance that colored his reading of every biblical liberation narrative. When Newton wrote of Israel's bondage in Egypt, he was writing of a reality he had tasted.

His hymn "The Bitter Waters" (Book I, Hymn 13) draws on Exodus 15:23–25, where Moses throws a log into the bitter waters of Marah and they become sweet. Newton's application is experiential: the believer, journeying through a spiritual desert, finds that every bitter providence can be sweetened by the cross of Christ — the true tree that makes bitter waters drinkable.

## The Passover and the Lord's Supper

Though the Olney Hymns do not contain dedicated communion hymns — Newton's Anglican context assigned the Eucharist to the *Book of Common Prayer* liturgy — the Passover typology runs beneath the surface of multiple Exodus hymns. The blood of the lamb that shelters Israel from the destroyer (Exodus 12) is implicitly the blood of Christ that shelters the believer from divine judgment. Newton's restraint in making this connection explicit reflects his respect for liturgical boundaries: he expected his congregation to recognize the typology from Scripture reading and preaching without his hymns needing to displace the Prayer Book.

## Significance

Newton's hymns on Exodus themes transform the ancient story into present-tense devotion. The wilderness between Egypt and Canaan is, for the Olney congregation, the space between conversion and glory — a space they inhabit each Lord's Day as they sing. The manna is not merely Israel's bread but Christ the Bread of Life; the water from the rock is not merely Horeb's spring but the Spirit poured out. This christological reading of Exodus, while common in Reformed preaching, achieves in Newton's hymns a poetic immediacy that sermons alone could not sustain.
