## Definition

In the [[Olney-Hymns-Title]], "law" refers primarily to the moral law of God — the commandments revealed through [[moses]] at [[sinai]]. The law is holy, just, and good, but its primary function in evangelical theology is to convict sinners of their guilt and drive them to [[jesus]] for [[mercy]]. The law condemns; [[gospel]] saves.

## Biblical Basis

Exodus 20 records the giving of the Ten Commandments at Sinai. [[paul]] declares that "the law is holy, and the commandment holy, and just, and good" (Romans 7:12), while also teaching that "the law was our schoolmaster to bring us unto Christ" (Galatians 3:24). The law reveals sin but cannot itself provide the [[power]] to overcome it.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton]] presents the giving of the law with dramatic force: "When [[israel]] heard the fiery law From Sinai's top proclaim'd" [PN=018-001], evoking the terror of the theophany. The law exposes human guilt — "We knew not what a law we broke, How holy, just, and pure" [PN=109-005]. The remedy lies not in law-keeping but in Christ's substitutionary work: "His blood a full [[atonement]] makes" [PN=109-006], satisfying the law's demands. The gospel proclaims that Christ "might the law fulfill, Bleed and suffer in our stead" [PN=178-003]. For the believer, the law's condemning power is broken: Christ "died to set me free From the law and sin and thee!" [PN=197-005]. The law speaks with "a fiery language" [PN=224-003] that drives sinners to the refuge of [[grace]].
