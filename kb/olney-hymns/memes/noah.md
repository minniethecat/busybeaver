## Identity

[[Noah]] was a righteous man, blameless in his generation, who "walked with God" (Genesis 6:9). When God determined to destroy all flesh because of its corruption, Noah "found favor in the eyes of the [[saviour|Lord]]" and was instructed to build an ark for the saving of his household. He is described in the New Testament as a "preacher of righteousness" (2 Peter 2:5) and an exemplar of faith: "By faith Noah, being warned by God concerning events as yet unseen, in reverent fear constructed an ark for the saving of his household" (Hebrews 11:7). In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] uses Noah's ark as an image of salvation's narrowness — few are saved while multitudes perish.

## Biblical Context

Noah's story occupies Genesis 6–9. In a world filled with violence and corruption, Noah alone was found righteous. God commanded him to build an ark of gopher wood, 300 cubits long, and to bring into it his family (wife, three sons Shem, Ham, and Japheth, and their wives) along with pairs of every living creature. [[flood|The Flood]] came, covering even the highest mountains, and all flesh outside the ark perished. After 150 days the waters receded, the ark rested on Mount Ararat, and Noah sent out a raven and a dove to test for dry land. Upon disembarking, Noah built an altar and offered sacrifice to God, who set the rainbow as a covenant sign — a promise never again to destroy the earth by flood.

## In the Olney Hymns

The Olney Hymns contain one direct reference to Noah, in the context of warning against presumption:

> But numbers are no mark
> That men will right be found;
> A few were saved in Noah's ark,
> For many millions drowned.
> — [PN-3077-005]

This verse appears in Hymn 77 of Book III, which warns against trusting in the multitude — "So many surely cannot be wrong, / And miss a happy end." [[john-newton|Newton]] counters this with Noah: the majority perished; only eight souls were saved. The ark is a type of Christ — the sole vessel of salvation in a world under judgment.

[[flood|The Flood]] narrative also lies behind [[john-newton|Newton]]'s references to divine judgment and mercy. The rainbow-covenant (Genesis 9) is not directly cited in the hymns, but the theme of God's faithfulness to His promises — even after judgment — runs throughout [[john-newton|Newton]]'s corpus. Noah's sacrifice after the flood (Genesis 8:20–21) prefigures the acceptable sacrifice of Christ, whose blood does not merely delay judgment but satisfies it fully.
