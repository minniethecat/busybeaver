## Identity

William Cowper (1731–1800) was one of the most popular English poets of the 18th century and, with [[john-newton|John Newton]], co-author of the Olney Hymns (P01-001). Cowper contributed 67 of the 348 hymns — fewer than [[john-newton|Newton]] — but his contributions are among the most beloved, including "[[light-shining-out-of-darkness|God Moves in a Mysterious Way]]" and "There Is a Fountain Filled with Blood."

## In the Olney Hymns

Cowper's hymns are marked by profound emotional depth and personal struggle. Unlike [[john-newton|Newton]], who wrote from the perspective of dramatic conversion, Cowper wrestled throughout his life with severe depression and religious melancholy. His hymns often reflect the experience of affliction and the search for comfort in darkness.

The collaboration between [[john-newton|Newton]] and Cowper grew from their close friendship. [[john-newton|Newton]] had hoped for more contributions from Cowper, but "my dear friend was prevented, by a long and affecting indisposition, from affording me any further assistance" (P02-001). Cowper's mental health struggles limited his output, but his existing hymns are masterpieces of devotional poetry.

## Partnership with Newton

[[john-newton|Newton]] and Cowper's friendship was the catalyst for the Olney Hymns. [[john-newton|Newton]] described the project as "a monument, to perpetuate the remembrance of an intimate and endeared friendship" (P02-001). Their contrasting personalities — [[john-newton|Newton]] the converted sinner full of confidence in grace, Cowper the lifelong sufferer clinging to faith despite darkness — give the collection its remarkable range of emotional and theological perspective.

The final attribution "[[john-newton|John Newton]] and William Cowper" (336-050) confirms their joint authorship.

## Literary Significance

Beyond the Olney Hymns, Cowper was a major literary figure whose poems influenced Wordsworth and the Romantic movement. His hymn-writing represents a fusion of polished literary craft with evangelical devotion. His hymns demonstrate that "perspicuity, simplicity, and ease" — [[john-newton|Newton]]'s stated goals for the collection — could coexist with genuine poetic art.
