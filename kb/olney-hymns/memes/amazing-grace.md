## Overview

**"Amazing Grace"** (titled "Faith's Review and Expectation" in the 1779 [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]]) is [[john-newton|John Newton]]'s most enduring hymn and one of the most widely sung songs in the English language. It is Hymn 41 of Book I, placed among the hymns "On the Scripture" and explicitly keyed to 1 Chronicles 17:16–17 — [[david|David]]'s prayer of astonished gratitude: "Who am I, O [[saviour|Lord]] God, and what is mine house, that thou hast brought me hitherto?"

The hymn traces the full arc of [[salvation]] as [[john-newton|Newton]] experienced it: from the moment of awakening ("I once was lost, but now am found"), through the sustaining work of [[grace]] amid "dangers, toils, and snares," to the final confidence that "God, who call'd me here below, will be for ever mine." It is simultaneously [[john-newton|Newton]]'s autobiography in miniature and every believer's spiritual itinerary.

## Text

The hymn's six stanzas move through past, present, and future — a structure that mirrors the biblical pattern of remembrance, trust, and hope:

| Stanza | Temporal Focus | Theme |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| I | Past → Present | Conversion: "I once was lost, but now am found" |
| II | Past | [[grace|Grace]] as teacher: "Twas grace that taught my heart to fear" |
| III | Present | [[perseverance|Perseverance]]: "Through many dangers, toils, and snares" |
| IV | Present → Future | Promise: "His word my hope secures" |
| V | Future (death) | Victory: "I shall possess, within the veil" |
| VI | Eschatological | Eternal security: "God...will be for ever mine" |

The movement is chiastic: the first stanza reaches back to the moment of conversion, the middle stanzas dwell in the present journey, and the final stanzas stretch forward to death and beyond. [[grace|Grace]] is the thread that runs through every stanza — past grace (I–II), present grace (III–IV), and future grace (V–VI).

The first line is often misquoted as "Amazing grace, how sweet the sound" — the 1779 text reads "Amazing grace! (how sweet the sound!)" with parentheses that heighten the exclamatory quality. The parenthetical "(how sweet the sound!)" is [[john-newton|Newton]] stepping back from his narrative to comment on it — a characteristic Evangelical move in which testimony and doxology are inseparable.

## Authorship

[[john-newton|John Newton]] (1725–1807) wrote "Amazing Grace" to accompany a sermon on 1 Chronicles 17:16–17 for his New Year's Day service, likely January 1, 1773, at St. Peter and St. [[paul|Paul]] Church in Olney, Buckinghamshire. The hymn is deeply autobiographical:

- **"A wretch like me"**: [[john-newton|Newton]]'s self-designation reflects his former life as a slave-ship captain. He never softened his assessment of his pre-conversion state — the "wretch" was not rhetorical exaggeration but honest self-knowledge.
- **"Was blind, but now I see"**: The blindness is both moral and spiritual. As a young man, [[john-newton|Newton]] was a professed atheist who mocked religion. His conversion during a near-fatal storm at sea in 1748 was experienced as the literal gift of sight.
- **"Dangers, toils, and snares"**: [[john-newton|Newton]]'s life included impressment into the Royal Navy, flogging, enslavement in West Africa, and years in the Atlantic slave trade. The "dangers" were not metaphorical.

The hymn's placement in Book I (hymns "On the Scripture") is significant: [[john-newton|Newton]] presents his own conversion narrative as an illustration of the biblical text. [[david|David]]'s astonished "Who am I?" becomes [[john-newton|Newton]]'s "Amazing grace!" — the Old Testament king's wonder at divine condescension is re-voiced by the eighteenth-century pastor.

## Meter and Music

The hymn is written in **Common Meter** (CM: 8.6.8.6) — the most widely used meter in English hymnody, also used for "O God, Our Help in Ages Past" and "[[joy|Joy]] to the World." The alternating lines of eight and six syllables create a rhythm of statement and response, expansion and contraction, that suits the hymn's movement between testimony and reflection.

The tune most commonly associated with "Amazing Grace" today — "New Britain" — is a nineteenth-century American melody of uncertain origin, first paired with [[john-newton|Newton]]'s text in William Walker's *Southern Harmony* (1835). [[john-newton|Newton]] himself never heard it; in his day, the hymn would have been sung to a psalm tune or one of the common-meter tunes in use at Olney.

## Theological Themes

"Amazing Grace" is a compressed summa of [[john-newton|Newton]]'s soteriology. Every major element of his theology of grace is present:

| Theme | Stanza | Expression |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| **Sovereign grace** | I–II | [[grace|Grace]] is the sole agent of salvation — it "saved," "taught," and "relieved" |
| **Total depravity** | I | "A wretch like me" — the natural state without grace |
| **[[perseverance|Perseverance]]** | III–IV | [[grace|Grace]] leads, grace secures — the believer will not finally fall away |
| **Assurance** | IV–VI | God's promise secures; the outcome is certain |
| **Eschatological hope** | V–VI | Death is not the end; the "veil" will be passed |

The hymn contains no imperative verbs, no exhortations, and no direct address to the congregation. It is entirely testimonial — [[john-newton|Newton]] declares what God has done for him. This first-person structure reflects the Evangelical conviction that doctrine becomes credible only when it becomes experience.

## Historical Notes

Since its publication in 1779, "Amazing Grace" has traveled far beyond its Olney origins. It became a staple of American camp meetings in the nineteenth century, an anthem of the Civil Rights movement in the twentieth (Mahalia Jackson's 1947 recording was a landmark), and is now sung in thousands of churches worldwide every Sunday. The irony is profound: a hymn written by a former slave trader has become one of the most beloved songs in African American Christianity — a testament, [[john-newton|Newton]] would insist, not to the author but to the grace of which he wrote.
