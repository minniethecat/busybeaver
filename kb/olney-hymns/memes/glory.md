# Glory

## Definition

Glory in the Olney Hymns denotes the weighty, radiant majesty of God — both the intrinsic splendor of the divine nature
and the reflection of that splendor in creation, providence, and redemption. To glorify God is not to add to his glory
but to acknowledge and proclaim it.

## Biblical Basis

The hymnody draws on the full biblical range of glory: the Shekinah cloud in Exodus, the glory filling Solomon's temple,
the transfigured Christ, and the eschatological vision of the New Jerusalem lit by the glory of the Lamb.
The root concept — Hebrew *kavod* (weight, honor) — carries through the hymns' insistence that God's glory is
a substantive reality, not a poetic metaphor.

## In the Olney Hymns

Glory appears as a refrain across all three books: the glory of God in creation, the glory of Christ in redemption,
and the believer's destiny to share in that glory. The doxological impulse — "to God be the glory" — is not a formulaic
closing but the theological center of Newton's hymn-writing.

> And wisdom, grace, and glory meet (PN=001-043)

## Newton vs. Cowper

Newton's treatment of glory is characteristically doxological: declarative praise rising from conviction.
Cowper approaches glory through the lens of contrast — the glory of God set against the darkness of the human heart,
making the divine radiance not less certain but more necessary. Both agree that glory belongs to God alone.

## Hymn Excerpts

> The praise and the glory be thine (PN=001-020)
> And raise thee in glory to reign (PN=001-029)

## Thematic Motifs

Beyond its doxological refrain, glory in the Olney Hymns functions across three
interwoven threads: glory as divine self-attestation, glory as the standard that
surpasses all earthly splendor, and glory as the believer's destined end.

### Glory as Divine Self-Attestation

The hymns consistently present glory not as an attribute God merely possesses but
as the visible weight of his presence — a reality that both reveals and protects.
When Newton recalls the wilderness pilgrimage, he pictures "the cloud and fire"
hovering over Israel's camp as "a glory and a cov'ring, showing that the Lord is
near" (060-003). The same Exodus imagery informs his reading of Gideon's signs:
the miraculous fleece demonstrates that God "alone has power to save, and claims
the glory as his own" (023-001). In both cases, glory is God's way of making
himself unmistakably present — it is self-attestation, not self-advertisement.

### Glory Surpassing Earthly Splendor

The Queen of Sheba's visit to Solomon provides the hymns' most vivid illustration
of glory's incomparability. Newton retells the story to make a theological point:
if Solomon's temporal glory so exceeded report that the queen "cried with a
pleasing surprise" on seeing it (034-001), and if "the glory of Solomon shone in
every object she viewed" (034-002), how much more will the glory of Christ — of
whom Solomon was but a type — surpass all human expectation? This typological
move turns a historical episode into an argument for the transcendent superiority
of divine glory: earthly magnificence is at best a shadow, and at worst a
distraction, from the true glory revealed in Christ.

### Glory in Redemption's Arc

The hymns trace glory through the full arc of redemption. It begins at "the
eternal throne" where "the God of glory reigns" (059-001), descends in the
incarnation where Christ "bore our sins and pains," and reaches forward to the
believer's hope to "in glory shine" beyond death (033-007). This trajectory is
not merely sequential but intensifying: the glory of creation is real but
partial; the glory of redemption is fuller; the glory of the new creation will be
complete. Newton insists that Christians should "maintain in view his glory as
our end" (027-006), making glory not only the origin and means but the very
telos of salvation.
