## Identity

John Newton (1725–1807) was an Anglican clergyman and hymn-writer who, together with [[william-cowper|William Cowper]], composed the Olney Hymns, first published in 1779 (P01-001). Newton was the principal contributor, writing the majority of the 348 hymns. His most famous hymn, "[[amazing-grace|Amazing Grace]]," reflects his own dramatic conversion from slave trader to Christian minister.

## In the Olney Hymns

Newton's hymns draw deeply from his personal experience of radical grace. As a former slave ship captain who experienced conversion during a violent storm at sea, he understood salvation as rescue from the deepest sin. His preface to the collection, signed from Olney, Bucks, on February 15, 1779 (P02-007), explains his pastoral purpose: the hymns were composed "for the use of plain people" and aimed at "promoting the faith and comfort of sincere Christians" (P02-001).

Newton's theological perspective was Calvinistic, emphasizing God's sovereign grace in salvation. He acknowledged controversy around his views but defended them as essential to his spiritual life: "The views I have received of the doctrines of grace are essential to my peace; I could not live comfortably a day or an hour without them" (P02-003).

## Pastoral Ministry

Newton served as curate of Olney, Buckinghamshire, from 1764 to 1780. The Olney Hymns were originally written for weekly prayer meetings in his parish. Newton dedicated the collection "more particularly to my dear friends in the parish and neighborhood of Olney" (P02-005). His pastoral heart is evident throughout — he saw hymn-writing as an extension of his ministry, aiming at his congregation's "growth and establishment in the grace of our God and [[saviour|Saviour]]" (P02-006).

## Legacy

Newton's influence extends far beyond Olney. His hymns, particularly "[[amazing-grace|Amazing Grace]]," have become among the most sung in the English language. The co-authorship with [[william-cowper|Cowper]] is recorded as "John Newton and [[william-cowper|William Cowper]]" (336-050). Newton's life story — from slave trader to abolitionist and minister — embodies the very theology of grace his hymns proclaim.
