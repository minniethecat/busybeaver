## Definition

Despair is a state of profound hopelessness in which the soul perceives no possibility of relief or redemption. In Christian theology, despair is distinguished from ordinary sadness by its spiritual dimension — it is the condition of one who believes God has abandoned them, or that their sins place them beyond the reach of mercy. In the Olney Hymns, [[john-newton|Newton]] treats despair with remarkable pastoral nuance: it is a real danger for the unconverted, a past condition from which Christ has delivered the believer, and an ongoing temptation that faith must continually resist.

## Biblical Basis

The Olney Hymns draw on a rich biblical vocabulary of despair, hope, and deliverance:

| Passage | Theme | Hymn Connection |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Psalm 42:5 | "Why art thou cast down, O my soul? hope thou in God" | The psalmist's self-counsel against despair |
| Psalm 88 | The darkest psalm — no resolution, only lament | The biblical precedent for honest cries from darkness |
| 2 Corinthians 4:8–9 | "Perplexed, but not in despair … cast down, but not destroyed" | The distinction between affliction and despair |
| Hebrews 6:18–19 | Hope as "an anchor of the soul, both sure and stedfast" | The opposite of despair |
| Romans 8:38–39 | Nothing can separate us from the love of God | The ultimate answer to despair |

Psalm 42:5 captures the psalmist's struggle: "Why art thou cast down, O my soul? and why art thou disquieted in me? hope thou in God." The Apostle Paul affirms that believers may be "perplexed, but not in despair" (2 Corinthians 4:8), teaching that faith provides a ground of hope even in the darkest circumstances.

## In the Olney Hymns

Despair appears throughout the Olney Hymns in four distinct registers: as a past deliverance, as a present danger, as the fate of the impenitent, and as a pastoral warning.

### Deliverance from Despair

[[John Newton]] vividly portrays the sinner delivered from the brink:

> The sinner who, by grace subdued,
> When near despair, thy mercy sought,
> And found the promise seal'd with blood,
> To sweeten every bitter thought.
> — [PN-010-004]

The phrase "near despair" is pastorally precise: the sinner approached the edge but did not fall over, because "mercy heard and set him free." Newton's own biography — his near-drowning, his terror in storms at sea, his conviction of sin — gives these lines autobiographical weight. He knew what it was to be at the point of despair and to be rescued.

### Christ's Rescue of the Despairing

Christ's coming brings the decisive remedy for despair:

> The pitying eye
> Relieved me from despair;
> He saw me at the point to die,
> And saved the sinner's prayer.
> — [PN-233-007]

The "pitying eye" of Christ sees the despairing sinner at the precise moment of extremity. Newton's theology here is Calvinistic in substance but pastorally tender in expression: the initiative is entirely Christ's — he sees, he relieves, he saves. The despairing sinner contributes nothing but the "sinner's prayer," and even that is elicited by grace.

### The Horror of Eternal Despair

The hymns do not flinch from the eschatological dimension. For the impenitent, despair is eternal:

> Their horror and despair,
> When they see the open'd book.
> — [PN-245-003]

The "open'd book" is the book of judgment (Revelation 20:12). Newton presents this not with relish but with solemn warning. The distinction is absolute: for the believer, despair is a temporary trial; for the unbeliever, it is an eternal state. The horror of the damned is described as suffering "with a long despairing groan" (PN-055-005) — "long" because it never ends.

### The Believer's Security Against Despair

Yet the hymns also fortify the believer against despair. Newton's repeated theme is that the Christian's hope rests not on subjective feeling but on objective fact — the finished work of Christ. The believer may *feel* despair but cannot finally *be* in despair, because "the city has a sure defence" (PN-072-005). The ground of hope is external to the self: the blood of Christ, the promise of God, the intercession of the ascended Lord.
