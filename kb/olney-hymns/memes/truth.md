# Truth

## Definition

Truth in the Olney Hymns is first and foremost a property of God — his word, his promises, his character — before it is
anything the believer possesses. Newton in particular grounds assurance not in subjective feeling but in the objective
reality of divine truth: what God has said stands, regardless of the believer's wavering perception.

## Biblical Basis

The hymns' theology of truth draws on the Johannine identification of Christ as the Truth, the Psalmist's confidence
that God's word is a lamp and a light, and the prophetic insistence that the word of the Lord endures forever.
Truth is not merely propositional accuracy but covenantal faithfulness.

## In the Olney Hymns

Truth functions as an anchor across the collection. In Book I, scriptural narrative is presented as divinely guaranteed truth.
In Book II, truth counters the fluctuations of experience. In Book III, Cowper's wrestling with despair makes truth
not a comfortable possession but a lifeline grasped in darkness.

> The truths by which the soul is fed (PN=001-036)

## Newton vs. Cowper

Newton holds truth with the confidence of one who has been seized by it. Cowper holds truth with the desperation of one
who fears losing it. For Newton, truth is a foundation; for Cowper, truth is a handhold. Both are biblical responses,
and both appear in the Olney corpus as complementary witnesses.

## Hymn Excerpts

> His heart from God and truth estranged (PN=001-003)

The estrangement from truth is the condition of fallen humanity; the restoration to truth is the work of grace.

## Truth in the Life of the Believer

The Olney Hymns do not treat truth as an abstraction to be contemplated but as a
living force that acts upon the soul. The hymns deploy a range of vivid metaphors
to describe how truth operates in the believer's experience: truth as nourishing
food, truth as irresistible force, and truth as planted seed.

### Truth as Soul's Sustenance

The most intimate image is that of feeding: "the truths by which the soul is
fed" (016-004). This is more than a metaphor of instruction — it casts truth as
spiritual nutrition without which the soul starves. Newton extends the image
through the believer's inconsistent appetite: "one hour upon the truth I feed,
the next I know not what I need" (130-003). The problem is not that truth is
scarce but that the believer's hunger wavers. This honesty about spiritual
inconstancy is characteristic of the Olney voice — truth is steady food, but the
eater is unsteady.

### Truth as Irresistible Force

A complementary image casts truth as active power: "the force of truth, how
great! When enemies confess" (020-003). Here truth is not passive content to be
received but an agent that compels acknowledgment even from opponents. The same
note sounds in the prayer that hearers would "feel the force of sacred truth
while the gospel call we hear" (150-004). Truth has a weight — the Hebrew root
*kavod* hovers in the background — that presses upon the conscience and demands
response.

### Truth as Planted Seed

The agricultural metaphor runs through the hymns' prayers for ministry: "let the
seed of sacred truth fall in a fruitful place" (149-001). This image shifts the
emphasis from truth's present force to its future fruitfulness. Truth sown now
may lie dormant for a season, but where the soil is prepared — "by thy Spirit,
and by thy truth" (151-005) — it will bear a harvest. Newton, who knew something
about seeds lying long in the ground (his own conversion came years after his
mother's early instruction), invests this metaphor with autobiographical weight.

### Truth and God's Faithfulness

Underlying all three images is the conviction enunciated in the divine pledge:
"My truth the great design ensures" (071-003). Truth is ultimately secured not by
the believer's grasp of it but by God's commitment to his own purposes. This
promise anchors the hymns' confidence: what God has spoken in truth, he will "all
his truth perform" (146-005). The performance of truth — its translation from
word to deed, from promise to fulfillment — is the arc of redemptive history, and
the believer's assurance rests entirely within it.
