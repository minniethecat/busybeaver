## Summary

The [[Ascension]] marks the moment when the risen [[saviour|Christ]], forty days after his resurrection, was taken up into heaven and seated at the right hand of God the Father (Acts 1:9–11; Mark 16:19). It is the climactic event of Christ's earthly ministry: his humiliation complete, his exaltation begun. In the Olney Hymns, the ascension is not treated as a separate doctrinal locus but as the inseparable consequence of the [[resurrection|Resurrection]] — the risen Lord *must* ascend, for his work on earth is finished and his heavenly reign must begin.

## Biblical Account

The ascension is recorded in Luke 24:50–53 and Acts 1:6–11. Jesus led his disciples to [[bethany|Bethany]], blessed them, and was "carried up into heaven." Two angels appeared to the disciples and declared that "this same Jesus, which is taken up from you into heaven, shall so come in like manner as ye have seen him go."

| Passage | Content | Significance |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| Luke 24:50–53 | Jesus blesses the disciples and ascends | The ascension as the fitting close of the incarnation |
| Acts 1:6–11 | The ascension witnessed; angels' promise of return | The ascension as a forward-looking event |
| Ephesians 1:20–22 | Christ seated at God's right hand, above all rule | Exaltation and enthronement |
| Romans 8:34 | Christ at the right hand of God, interceding | The ascended Lord as advocate |
| 1 Corinthians 15:25 | He must reign until all enemies are subdued | The ascension inaugurates Christ's reign |
| Hebrews 4:14–16 | A great high priest passed into the heavens | The ascension as priestly entrance |

The ascension thus looks both backward (confirming the completed work of redemption) and forward (anticipating the [[second-coming|Second Coming]]). The New Testament epistles develop the ascension's theological significance: Christ is now seated at God's right hand (Ephesians 1:20–22), interceding for his people (Romans 8:34), and reigning until all his enemies are subdued (1 Corinthians 15:25).

## In the Olney Hymns

The ascension appears in the Olney Hymns primarily through the language of exaltation, enthronement, and heavenly intercession. [[John Newton]] presents the ascension not as a departure that leaves the church orphaned but as an installation that secures the church's life.

### The Ascended Lord as Provider

Newton's Hymn 134, "Looking Unto Jesus," draws the believer's gaze upward through the entire arc of redemption — from cross to resurrection to throne:

> To look to Jesus as he rose,
> Confirms my faith, disarms my foes;
> Satan I shame and overcome,
> By pointing to my Saviour's tomb.
>
> Exalted on his glorious throne,
> I see him make my cause his own;
> Then all my anxious cares subside,
> For Jesus lives, and will provide.
> — [PN-134-005], [PN-134-006]

The progression is deliberate: the risen Lord ascends to his throne, and from that throne he "makes my cause his own" — the ascended Christ is not distant but engaged, advocating for his people. The ascension transforms him from the object of faith to the active sustainer of faith. The believer's confidence rests not on a dead teacher or a risen but absent Lord, but on a living, enthroned, interceding King.

### The Descent and Ascent: One Divine Movement

In Hymn 62, Newton presents the ascension as the fitting end of the incarnation:

> By the Father's eye approved,
> Lo, a voice is heard from heaven,
> "Sinners, this is my Beloved,
> For your ransom freely given."
>
> Angels with their eyes pursued him
> When he left his glorious throne;
> With astonishment they view'd him
> Put the form of servant on.
> — [PN-062-003], [PN-062-004]

The movement is downward then upward: the Son leaves his throne to take the form of a servant, and having completed his work, returns to that throne. The angels' astonishment — first at the descent, then at the ascent — frames the incarnation and ascension as two halves of one divine movement. The ascension is not an afterthought but the necessary completion of the incarnation: the one who descended must ascend, for his descent was for a purpose now accomplished.

### The Practical Import of the Ascension

For Newton, the ascension is not a speculative doctrine but a practical anchor. Because Christ is ascended, the believer knows: (1) the work of redemption is complete — nothing remains to be added; (2) the ascended Lord intercedes — the believer has a friend at court; (3) the same Jesus will return — history has a destination. The ascension thus underwrites assurance, prayer, and hope.
