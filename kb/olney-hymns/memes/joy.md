## Definition

[[Joy]] is a fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22), a deep and enduring gladness rooted not in circumstances but in the believer's union with [[saviour|Jesus Christ]]. Unlike mere happiness — which depends on happenings — Christian joy persists through sorrow, trial, and even persecution.

The psalmist declares, "In your presence there is fullness of joy" (Psalm 16:11), and Nehemiah says, "The joy of the [[saviour|Lord]] is your strength" (Nehemiah 8:10). In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] portrays joy as both a present possession and a future hope — the believer has joy now, but its fullness awaits heaven.

## Biblical Foundation

Joy runs through Scripture from beginning to end. The Old Testament commands [[israel|Israel]] to "rejoice before the [[saviour|Lord]] your God" (Deuteronomy 12:12). The Psalms ring with joyful praise. In the New Testament, joy is the keynote of the Gospel — the angel announces "good news of great joy" (Luke 2:10).

Christ tells His disciples, "These things I have spoken to you, that my joy may be in you, and that your joy may be full" (John 15:11). [[paul|Paul]], writing from prison, commands: "Rejoice in the [[saviour|Lord]] always; again I will say, rejoice" (Philippians 4:4).

## In the Olney Hymns

### Joy Through Tears

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns are suffused with joy — but it is a distinctly Christian joy, forged in the crucible of trial:

> But crops of joy they reap at length
> From what they sow in tears.
> (024-004)

The image of harvesting joy from tear-watered fields is quintessentially [[john-newton|Newton]]ian. Joy and sorrow are not opposites but partners — sorrow is the soil in which joy grows. The farmer who sows in tears does not bypass grief but passes through it, and on the far side finds a harvest he could not have anticipated.

### Joy in Christ's Pardon

The source of this joy is always Christ:

> To read my pardon sealed,
> And from thy joy to draw my strength.
> (032-007)

Assurance of pardon — reading one's name in the book of life — is the wellspring of Christian joy. [[john-newton|Newton]] draws strength not from his own resolutions but from Christ's joy. The believer's strength is derivative; it flows from a source outside himself.

### The Believer's Former Joy

[[john-newton|Newton]] is honest about the human heart's tendency to wander from its true joy. Before conversion, the sinner found pleasure in things that now repel:

> He flees, which was before his joy,
> And thinks to hide amidst the trees.
> (001-004)

This is [[adam|Adam]] in [[Eden]] — hiding from God among the trees — but it is also every sinner's story. What was once joy becomes shame; what was once delight becomes dread. The hymn charts the reorientation of the affections that conversion accomplishes.

### Joy in Present Peace

Not all joy is deferred to the future. [[john-newton|Newton]] knows a joy rooted in present communion:

> What peaceful hours I once enjoyed!
> How sweet their memory still!
> (003-003)

The hymn's speaker looks back on seasons of sweet fellowship with God — not as a permanent possession but as a remembered taste that fuels present longing. The memory of past joy becomes a goad to renewed seeking.

### Joy in Future Hope

Joy culminates in eternity. [[john-newton|Newton]], reflecting on his own approaching death, wrote of his desire to "finish my course with joy" (P02-006) — an echo of [[paul|Paul]]'s words in Acts 20:24. For the believer, joy is not merely a present experience but a future destination. [[eve|Eve]]ry foretaste along the way is a pledge of the fullness to come.
