## Definition

**Spirit**, in the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], refers primarily to the Holy Spirit — the third person of the Trinity, sent by the Father and the Son to indwell, teach, and transform believers. The hymns portray the Spirit as the active agent of sanctification: he convicts of sin, comforts in distress, revives the fainting [[soul]], and makes [[faith]] effectual. [[john-newton|John Newton]] treats the Spirit's work as essential and experiential — not a doctrine to be defended but a presence to be enjoyed.

Newton's own spiritual biography shaped his pneumatology. His conversion at sea was not merely a change of mind but a supernatural encounter — the Spirit breaking through spiritual deadness. His hymns consistently present the Spirit as the one who makes objective [[grace]] subjectively real in the believer's heart.

## Biblical Basis

The Olney Hymns draw on the New Testament's rich teaching on the Holy Spirit:

| Passage | Theme | Hymn Expression |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| John 14:26 | The Spirit as teacher and reminder of Christ's words | Spirit-led understanding in the hymns' prayers |
| Romans 8:26 | The Spirit helps our weakness, intercedes for us | "Make my wounded spirit whole" (044-001) |
| Galatians 5:22–23 | The fruit of the Spirit | Spiritual graces woven throughout the hymns |
| Ephesians 1:13–14 | The Spirit as seal and guarantee of inheritance | Assurance through the Spirit's witness |

The hymns reflect the Reformed conviction that the Spirit applies the benefits of Christ's redemption to the individual believer — without the Spirit, [[grace]] remains external; with the Spirit, grace becomes internal and transformative.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] depicts the Spirit's work through concrete pastoral imagery, organized here by the Spirit's distinct roles:

### The Spirit as Comforter and Healer

The Spirit revives and heals the troubled believer, restoring what sin and sorrow have broken:

> Make my wounded spirit whole; / Far away the tempter drive.
> — (044-001)

The image of a "wounded spirit" captures the inward damage that only the Spirit can repair. The petition is twofold: healing ("make whole") and protection ("drive the tempter far away"). The Holy Spirit is both physician and guardian — he mends the past wound and prevents the next assault.

### The Spirit as Reviver

When the believer's inward life flags, the Spirit brings renewal:

> Whene'er my feeble spirit faints — / At once my soul revives.
> — (004-006)

The word "feeble" is honest about the believer's native weakness. Spiritual vitality is not self-generated; it comes from outside, and it comes "at once" — the Spirit's reviving work is immediate and effective.

### The Spirit as Advocate Against Doubt

When [[satan|Satan]] suggests that grace is insufficient, the Spirit counters with inward testimony:

> But when such suggestions / Our spirits have plied, / This answers all questions.
> — (007-006)

The "suggestions" are satanic insinuations — doubts about God's love, the sufficiency of Christ's work, the reality of personal [[faith]]. The Spirit's answer is not a logical argument but an inward witness that "answers all questions" by redirecting the believer to the finished work of the [[saviour]] (Romans 8:16 rendered in hymnic form).

### The Spirit as Transformer

The Spirit draws the affections heavenward, away from earthly attachments:

> Our spirits cleave no more to earth, / But long for worlds above.
> — (032-004)

The verb "cleave" indicates a bond that has been broken. Before the Spirit's work, the believer's affections were glued to earthly things; the Spirit loosens that grip and redirects desire upward.

### The Spirit as Exalting Force

The Spirit elevates the mind beyond its natural horizons:

> Which make the spirit mount on high, / And leave the world behind.
> — (042-005)

To "mount on high" is to share, in measure, the heavenly perspective, seeing earthly trials and joys in their true proportion.

### The Spirit as Pilgrim's Companion

The Spirit cheers and guides the believer through life's pilgrimage:

> Let thy love my spirit cheer; / As my Guide, my Guard, my Stay.
> — (031-006)

Three titles — Guide, Guard, Stay — summarize the Spirit's comprehensive care. He leads, protects, and supports.

### The Human Spirit in the Hymns

The hymns also explore the human spirit as the seat of devotion, grief, and longing. The indwelling Spirit works upon the human spirit, making it the theater of sanctification:

> But ah! my inmost spirit mourns, / And well my eyes with tears can tell.
> — (030-004)

Here the human spirit is the locus of sorrow — not the Holy Spirit but the believer's own inward self, grieving over sin or affliction. The honesty of such passages testifies to the Olney Hymns' refusal to spiritualize away genuine pain.

### The Spirit that Produces Awe

The Spirit's work on unbelievers is equally attested — he subdues stubborn hearts:

> Filled with holy awe, / Their stubborn spirits tamed.
> — (018-001)

This describes the Spirit's convicting work on those outside the faith. "Stubborn spirits tamed" is a picture of effectual calling — the Spirit overcoming resistance not by violence but by the weight of holy awe.
