## Definition

[[Sin]] is any want of conformity unto or transgression of the law of God (1 John 3:4). It is both an inherited condition (original sin, from [[Adam]]'s fall) and a personal act of willful rebellion. The Bible declares that "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" (Romans 3:23) and that "the wages of sin is death" (Romans 6:23).

In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] treats sin with unflinching honesty — not as a distant abstraction but as the daily reality of the believer's heart, the very thing that makes grace necessary and precious.

## Biblical Foundation

Sin enters the biblical narrative in Genesis 3 with [[Adam]] and [[Eve]]'s transgression in [[Eden]]. From that point, sin spreads — [[Cain]] murders [[Abel]] (Genesis 4), humanity grows so corrupt that God sends the Flood (Genesis 6–8), and even after the covenant with [[Abraham]], [[israel|Israel]] repeatedly rebels.

The Old Testament sacrificial system provisionally covers sin; the prophets anticipate a final remedy. The New Testament declares that Christ is that remedy: "Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!" (John 1:29). His death atones for sin; His resurrection conquers its penalty.

## In the Olney Hymns

### The Fall and Its Consequences

Sin is the condition [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns assume on every page. Hymn 1 opens with the fall:

> But, oh by sin how quickly changed —
> His honour forfeited —
> His heart from God and truth estranged —
> His conscience filled with dread!
> (001-003)

This is [[john-newton|Newton]]'s anthropology in four lines: sin brings loss of honour, estrangement from God and truth, and a conscience tormented by dread. The fall is not merely [[adam|Adam]]'s story but every person's autobiography.

### Hatred of Sin

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s treatment of sin is marked by a holy hatred of what sin has done to Christ:

> I hate the sins that made thee mourn,
> And drove thee from my breast.
> (003-005)

The believer's hatred of sin is not abstract moralism but personal devotion — sin is hated because it wounded Christ. This is the logic of the cross applied to sanctification: love for the [[saviour|Saviour]] produces loathing for the sin that cost Him His life.

### The Believer's Ongoing Struggle

[[john-newton|Newton]] never treats sin as something the believer has left entirely behind. The hymns acknowledge that the Christian continues to wrestle with indwelling sin. This is not despair but honesty — the same honesty that marked [[john-newton|Newton]]'s own self-assessment as "the chief of sinners" saved by grace.

The awareness of sin never drives [[john-newton|Newton]] to despair because grace always meets the sinner at the point of confession. The hymn that begins with the deepest groaning ends with the highest praise. Sin is the canvas on which grace paints its masterpiece.

### Sin in the Preface

In his Preface, [[john-newton|Newton]] disclaims any pretense of literary perfection, insisting he has not "written a single line with an intention either to flatter or to offend" (P02-001). This humility about his own work reflects his deeper conviction: even the best human efforts are tainted by sin and require divine grace to accomplish any good.
