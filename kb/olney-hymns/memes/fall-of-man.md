## Summary

The Fall of Man is the pivotal event in [[Genesis]] 3 in which [[Adam]] and [[Eve]], the first humans, disobeyed God's command by eating the forbidden fruit in the Garden of [[Eden]], at the instigation of the serpent. This act of rebellion introduced sin, suffering, and death into the human condition — what theologians call "original sin." In the Olney Hymns, the Fall is the dark backdrop against which the drama of [[Redemption]] unfolds. [[John Newton]] in particular uses the Fall to frame the human predicament: [[adam|Adam]]'s transgression explains why every person needs a [[Saviour]].

## Biblical Account

The Fall narrative occupies [[Genesis]] 3. The serpent, described as "more crafty than any beast of the field," deceives [[eve|Eve]] into eating the fruit of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, which God had forbidden. [[eve|Eve]] shares the fruit with [[adam|Adam]], who also eats. Their eyes are opened, they become aware of their nakedness, and they hide from God.

God's judgment follows: the serpent is cursed to crawl on its belly; the woman is told she will bear children in pain; and the man is told the ground is cursed because of him — he will eat by toil and sweat until he returns to dust. [[adam|Adam]] and [[eve|Eve]] are expelled from [[eden|Eden]], and a cherubim with a flaming sword guards the way to the tree of life.

Theologically, the Fall introduces "original sin" — the doctrine that all humanity inherits a fallen nature from [[adam|Adam]] (Romans 5:12–21). This sets up the need for a "second [[adam|Adam]]" — [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] — whose obedience undoes [[adam|Adam]]'s disobedience.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] opens Book I with a hymn on [[adam|Adam]] that immediately establishes the Fall as the human condition:

> When Adam fell he quickly lost
> God's image, which he once possessed.
> — [PN-002-001]

This loss of the divine image is the catastrophe from which all other spiritual problems flow. The contrast between [[eden|Eden]]ic bliss and post-lapsarian suffering is starkly drawn:

> 'Twas not thus before the fall:
> What but pain and thorn and…
> — [PN-056-004]

The hymn connects the thorns of fallen creation with the crown of thorns worn by Christ, linking Fall and [[redemption|Redemption]] typologically. [[john-newton|Newton]] also notes the immediacy of [[adam|Adam]]'s transgression:

> To Adam, soon as he transgress'd,
> The promised seed was given.
> — [PN-173-007]

The "promised seed" (Genesis 3:15, the *protoevangelium*) is the first glimmer of gospel hope — even in judgment, God promises a [[saviour|Redeemer]]. The serpent's role is not minimized:

> The serpent sin's envenom'd sting
> Has poisoned all my frame.
> — [PN-126-001]

And again:

> Yet pierced by sin, the serpent's tooth,
> I mourn the anguish of my wound.
> — [PN-305-001]

The serpent's venom is a vivid metaphor for the corruption of sin passed down from [[adam|Adam]]. The fallen state is universal:

> Nor let the fallen soul despair,
> For Mercy can forgive.
> — [PN-029-008]

And [[john-newton|Newton]]'s Christology explicitly sees Jesus as the healer of fallen humanity:

> Thus Jesus pities fallen man,
> And heals the wounds that sin has made.
> — [PN-099-001]

The [[eden|Eden]]-fall-redemption arc is completed by the "second [[adam|Adam]]" typology:

> The second Adam saw
> Oppress'd with woe, to set us free.
> — [PN-236-004]

## Theological Significance

The Fall is the linchpin of evangelical soteriology and it functions as such in the Olney Hymns. Without the Fall, there is no need for a [[Saviour]]; without original sin, [[Grace]] is mere assistance rather than rescue. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns repeatedly trace a three-part movement: the goodness of original creation, the catastrophe of the Fall, and the greater glory of redemption in [[saviour|Christ]].

The Fall also provides the emotional register for many hymns — the sense of unworthiness, the lament over indwelling sin, and the longing for restoration all presuppose a primal loss. The "[[eden|Eden]]" that was [[adam|Adam]]'s dwelling-place (PN-236-002) becomes, typologically, the [[Heaven]] that awaits the redeemed.
