## Summary

[[Egypt]] is the land where the [[Israel|Israelites]] lived for over four hundred years, first as honored guests under [[Joseph]]'s administration and later as oppressed slaves. The [[Exodus]] from Egypt — God's deliverance through the plagues, Passover, and Red Sea crossing — is the foundational salvation event of the Old Testament. In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] uses Egypt typologically: as [[israel|Israel]] was delivered from physical bondage, so the believer is delivered from spiritual bondage by [[saviour|Christ]].

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] makes the Exodus the controlling metaphor for conversion:

> When Israel was from Egypt freed,
> The Lord, who brought them out indeed,
> Then led them through the wilderness.
> — [PN-049-001]

The movement is tripartite: Egypt (bondage) → deliverance → wilderness (Christian life). Egypt is decisively *behind*. [[john-newton|Newton]] applies this personally:

> From Egypt lately freed
> By the Redeemer's grace,
> A rough and thorny path I tread.
> — [PN-283-001]

"Lately freed" — the conversion is fresh, the memory of bondage vivid. [[john-newton|Newton]] also invokes the Joseph typology:

> To Joseph the Egyptians went;
> To Jesus I make known my case.
> — [PN-011-004]

As Egyptians came to Joseph for grain during famine, so the sinner comes to Christ for the bread of life.
