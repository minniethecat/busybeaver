## Identity

**Jesus** (Jesus of Nazareth, Jesus Christ) is the central figure of Christianity — the incarnate Son of God, the promised Messiah, and the Saviour of sinners. His name "Jesus" (Hebrew: *Yeshua*) means "Yahweh saves," and "Christ" (Greek: *Christos*) is the title "Anointed One," equivalent to the Hebrew "Messiah."

Jesus lived in first-century Judea, taught the Kingdom of God, performed miracles, was crucified under Pontius Pilate, and rose from the dead on the third day. Christians confess him as fully God and fully man — the second person of the Trinity — whose death atones for sin and whose resurrection guarantees eternal life for all who believe.

In the 18th-century Evangelical context of the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], the person and work of Jesus Christ was the heartbeat of personal piety. [[John Newton]]'s hymns are saturated with devotion to Jesus as Saviour, Friend, Advocate, and Lord. As Newton writes in his preface, the hymns aim at "growth and establishment in the grace of our God and [[saviour|Saviour]]" (P02-006).

## Biblical Context

Jesus's life and ministry are recorded in the four Gospels. Key events include:

- **Incarnation** (Matthew 1, Luke 1–2): The eternal Son of God "became flesh and dwelt among us" (John 1:14), born of the Virgin Mary in Bethlehem.
- **Ministry** (Matthew 4–25, Mark 1–13, Luke 4–21, John 2–17): Three years of teaching, healing, and miracle-working throughout Galilee and Judea.
- **Crucifixion** (Matthew 27, Mark 15, Luke 23, John 19): Executed under Roman authority, bearing the sins of the world as the substitutionary sacrifice.
- **Resurrection** (Matthew 28, Mark 16, Luke 24, John 20–21): Raised bodily from the dead, appearing to his disciples over forty days.
- **Ascension** (Acts 1, Hebrews 7:25): Exalted to God's right hand, where he lives to intercede for his people.

The New Testament epistles develop the significance of Jesus's work — justification by faith (Romans 5), reconciliation (2 Corinthians 5:18–19), redemption (Ephesians 1:7), and his ongoing heavenly priesthood (Hebrews 4:14–16). He will return in glory as judge and king (Revelation 19:11–16).

## In the Olney Hymns

Jesus is the most frequently named person in the Olney Hymns. [[John Newton]] and [[william-cowper|William Cowper]] invoke his name in virtually every dimension of his person and work.

**Jesus as Saviour and Friend**:

> Jesus, the Saviour, is his name,
> He freely loves and without blame.
> (030-001)

Newton's hymnody presents Jesus as the approachable, loving friend of sinners — not a distant judge but a present helper. This intimacy is a hallmark of Evangelical piety: the believer addresses Jesus directly, not through priestly mediation (021-005, 031-001, 033-001).

**Jesus as Sacrifice and Priest**:

> Thus Jesus fell; but, oh! his blood
> Far dearer held than ours.
> (002-008)

The cross is central. Jesus died as a substitute for sinners (012-006, 013-005), and now lives as the believer's high priest and advocate in heaven (019-005, 028-006). Newton's Calvinistic theology emphasizes that Christ's death was effectual — it actually secures salvation, it does not merely make it possible.

**Jesus in Daily Experience**:

From guidance through trials (027-008, 025-008) to protection from enemies (024-003), Jesus is portrayed as the believer's constant companion. [[William Cowper|Cowper]]'s preface hymn (P02) testifies that his own deliverance from despair came through knowing "the grace of our God and Saviour" (P02-006).

The name of Jesus receives a dedicated hymn (Book I, Hymn 57), where every office of Christ is unfolded: prophet, priest, king, shepherd, brother, friend. The Olney Hymns consistently direct the singer's affections not toward abstract doctrines but toward the person of Jesus himself. As Newton writes, "How sweet the name of Jesus sounds / In a believer's ear" — the experiential knowledge of Christ is the summit of Evangelical devotion.
