Solomon, the son of [[david]] and king of [[israel]], appears in the [[Olney-Hymns-Title]] primarily through [[john-newton]]'s extended meditation on the Queen of Sheba's visit. In Newton's poetic treatment, Solomon's [[wisdom]] and [[glory]] become a typological shadow of [[jesus]] — the "greater than Solomon."

## Solomon in the Olney Hymns

Book I, Hymn 34 — **The Queen of Sheba** (based on 1 Kings 10 / 2 Chronicles 9) — uses the queen's journey to [[jerusalem]] as an elaborate allegory of the soul coming to Christ. The queen, drawn by "a distant report / Of Solomon's glory and fame," finds that reality far exceeds reputation: "How much what I see with my eyes / Surpasses the rumour I heard!" Newton extends the metaphor through six stanzas, culminating in the declaration: "I saw, and transported I cried, / 'A greater than Solomon thou!'"

## Typological Significance

For Newton, Solomon's surpassing of the queen's expectations becomes the pattern for every believer's discovery of Christ. The "hard questions" she brought find "ready solution"; her "doubt and hard questions" are met with restored [[peace]]. The hymn moves from historical narrative to personal testimony: "Beholding me poor and distressed, / His bounty supplied all my wants."

## David's Charge to Solomon

Book I, Hymn 20 — **David's Charge to Solomon** (1 Chronicles 28:9) — shifts from Solomon as type to Solomon as recipient of paternal wisdom. Newton uses David's dying charge to his son as a New Year's address to young [[index-of-persons]], urging them to "know thou the God of thy father, and serve him with a perfect heart."
