## Summary

[[Jacob]] was the son of [[Isaac]] and [[Rebekah]], the twin brother of [[Esau]], and the grandson of [[Abraham]]. Through a life marked by conflict, deception, and eventual transformation, Jacob became the father of the twelve tribes and the namesake of the nation of [[Israel]] — the name God gave him after he "struggled with God and with men and overcame" (Genesis 32:28).

In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] focuses almost exclusively on one episode: Jacob's dream of a ladder reaching from earth to heaven at [[Bethel]] (Genesis 28:10–22). This vision, in which angels ascend and descend, becomes for [[john-newton|Newton]] a powerful type of [[saviour|Christ]] as the mediator who connects heaven and earth.

## Biblical Narrative

Jacob's story spans Genesis 25–50. He is born grasping Esau's heel (hence the name Ya'akov, "heel-grabber" or "supplanter"). He acquires Esau's birthright for a bowl of stew and, with Rebekah's help, deceives the blind Isaac to receive the patriarchal blessing intended for Esau. Fleeing Esau's wrath, he has the dream of the ladder at Bethel, where God renews the Abrahamic covenant with him.

After twenty years serving his uncle Laban in Haran (marrying Leah and Rachel, fathering twelve sons and a daughter), Jacob returns to [[canaan|Canaan]]. On the night before meeting Esau, he wrestles with a mysterious divine being at the ford of Jabbok. He prevails and is renamed [[israel|Israel]] — "he who strives with God." His later years are dominated by the story of his son [[Joseph]], and he dies in [[egypt|Egypt]], blessing his sons with prophetic words over each tribe.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] devotes an entire hymn (Book I, Hymn 9) to Jacob's ladder, reading it Christologically:

> When from Esau Jacob fled,
> Though his pillow was a stone,
> There he saw a ladder raised.
> — [PN-009-001]

The stone pillow and the open sky — [[john-newton|Newton]] emphasizes the contrast between Jacob's physical discomfort and the glory of the vision. The ladder is explicitly Christological:

> Never king like Jacob slept,
> For he lay at heaven's gate.
> — [PN-009-002]

Jacob's sleep at Bethel ("house of God") prefigures the believer's rest in Christ, the true gate of heaven (John 1:51, where Jesus applies the ladder image to himself). God speaks directly to the patriarch:

> "Fear not, Jacob, thou art mine,
> And my presence shall be thine."
> — [PN-009-003]

The covenant promise — "thou art mine" — is the gospel in miniature. [[john-newton|Newton]] applies the ladder to the believer's present experience:

> Well does Jacob's ladder suit
> To the gospel scheme.
> — [PN-009-004]

[[eve|Eve]]ry step of the ladder — election, calling, justification, sanctification, glorification — is a rung by which God descends to us and we ascend to him, all through Christ.

## Jacob as Israel

The name change from Jacob ("supplanter") to [[israel|Israel]] ("striver with God") signals transformation. Jacob the schemer becomes [[israel|Israel]] the patriarch. This pattern — a morally compromised person claimed and renamed by grace — is characteristically attractive to [[john-newton|Newton]], who saw his own story in similar terms.
