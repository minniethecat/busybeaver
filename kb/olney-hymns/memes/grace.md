## Definition

**Grace** (Greek: χάρις, *charis*; Latin: *gratia*) is the unmerited favor and supernatural power of God freely given to humanity for salvation and spiritual life. In the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], grace is not merely a doctrine to be affirmed but the lived experience that defines every believer's standing before God. It is undeserved, sovereign, effectual, and persevering — God initiates and completes the work of [[salvation]] entirely by grace.

[[john-newton|John Newton]], drawing from his Calvinistic convictions and his dramatic conversion from slave-ship captain to clergyman, regarded grace as essential to spiritual peace: "The views I have received of the doctrines of grace are essential to my peace; I could not live comfortably a day or an hour without them" (P02-003).

For [[john-newton|Newton]], grace answered the question his own life posed: how can the chief of sinners be accepted by a holy God?

## Biblical Basis

The doctrine of grace is foundational to the New Testament, particularly in the [[paul|Paul]]ine epistles, and the Olney Hymns reflect this scriptural depth:

| Passage | Theme | Hymn Expression |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Ephesians 2:8–9 | [[salvation|Salvation]] by grace through faith, not works | "But grace, unask'd, his heart subdued" (001-006) |
| Titus 2:11–14 | The grace of God that brings salvation has appeared to all | "the grace of our God and [[saviour|Saviour]]" (P02-006) |
| Romans 5:20 | Where sin abounded, grace did much more abound | "Grace reigns, to pardon crimson sin" (327-003) |
| 2 Corinthians 12:9 | "My grace is sufficient for thee" | Grace as sustaining power in affliction |
| John 1:16–17 | Grace and truth came through [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] | "Grace, which, like the [[saviour|Lord]], the giver" (060-002) |

The hymns consistently portray grace as mediated entirely through [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] — specifically through his atoning death. Grace is not an abstract divine attribute but the specific favor that flows from "a bleeding [[Saviour]] seen by faith" (042-003).

## In the Olney Hymns

Grace pervades the Olney Hymns as both theological anchor and devotional comfort. The word appears in dozens of hymns, deployed in four interconnected dimensions:

### Saving Grace

Grace as the agent of conversion — it precedes, enables, and completes the sinner's turning to God:

> But grace, unask'd, his heart subdued,
> And all his guilt forgave.
> — [001-006]

This is the Calvinist emphasis on prevenient grace: God acts before any human response. The sinner does not decide and then receive grace; grace decides and then transforms the sinner.

### Sustaining Grace

Grace as ongoing divine provision — the believer's daily dependence on a supply that never fails:

> In Jesus is our store,
> Grace issues from his throne;
> Whoever can ask for more —
> Let him his want make known.
> — [315-006]

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s metaphor of God's "magazine of grace" (011-004) — a military storehouse — captures the inexhaustible character of this supply.

### Comforting Grace

Grace as a word of assurance in affliction, trial, and spiritual darkness:

> Grace, triumphant in the throne,
> Scorns a rival or a frown;
> Grace will all our hopes complete,
> Lay its crown at Jesus' feet.
> — [316-001]

### Transformative Grace

Grace changes human nature — it is not merely a legal declaration but a renovating power:

> Grace is a plant, where'er it grows,
> Of pure and heav'nly root;
> It buds, and blossoms, and brings forth
> Celestial, pleasant fruit.
> — [149-002]

The botanical metaphor (grace as a living plant, not a static possession) reflects the Evangelical insistence that genuine grace produces visible transformation — a changed life is the evidence of a changed status.

## Newton vs. Cowper

The two hymn-writers share a common theology of grace but express it through profoundly different spiritual temperaments, shaped by their contrasting biographies.

**[[john-newton|John Newton]]** (1725–1807) experienced grace as dramatic rescue. His years as a slave-ship captain, followed by a near-fatal storm at sea and subsequent conversion, gave him an unshakable conviction that grace reaches the utterly undeserving. His hymns ring with the astonishment of the pardoned:

> O'erwhelm'd and confounded by grace:
> "Poor sinner, I know thee full well,"
> — [012-005]

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s grace is *expansive* — it floods the soul, overwhelms resistance, and turns the chief of sinners into a preacher of righteousness. His characteristic note is triumphant assurance.

**[[william-cowper|William Cowper]]** (1731–1800) experienced grace as tenuous refuge. Plagued by severe depression and periodic conviction of his own damnation, [[william-cowper|Cowper]] wrote of grace from the position of one clinging to it in darkness. His hymns acknowledge that grace can feel absent even while theologically present:

> Grace alone can cure our ills,
> Sweeten all our pains;
> Heal the soul of all its ills
> And wash out all its stains.
> — [147-004] (Cowper)

Where [[john-newton|Newton]] declares, [[william-cowper|Cowper]] pleads. Where [[john-newton|Newton]]'s grace is a broad river, [[william-cowper|Cowper]]'s is a narrow beam of light in a dark room. The hymns are richer for containing both registers — [[john-newton|Newton]]'s confidence prevents [[william-cowper|Cowper]]'s introspection from becoming despair, and [[william-cowper|Cowper]]'s honesty prevents [[john-newton|Newton]]'s assurance from becoming presumption.

## Hymn Excerpts

> Grace, which, like the Lord, the giver,
> Never fails from age to age;
> This from thirsting souls shall ever
> Be to parched ground like a stream,
> Whence a thousand hopes, well founded,
> Shall spring up on every side:
> Milk and honey have abounded,
> Since the Saviour was revealed.
> — [060-002]

> Grace bid me live, and taught my tongue
> To raise the vocal prayer;
> Through all my life, by grace made strong,
> I'll trust a Saviour's care.
> — [301-007]

> Grace reigns, to pardon crimson sin —
> Grace tills the soil, and sows the seed;
> Grace finishes what grace begins,
> And brings the soul to heaven indeed.
> — [327-003–004]
