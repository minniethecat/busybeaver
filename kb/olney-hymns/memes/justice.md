## Summary

[[Justice]] is the divine attribute by which God upholds moral order, rendering to every person according to their deeds. In Christian theology, God's justice is both retributive (punishing sin) and distributive (vindicating the righteous). The tension between divine justice and divine [[Mercy]] is one of the great theological problems resolved at the cross of [[saviour|Christ]], where "grace and justice join" (PN-323-004).

In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] treats justice not as an abstract philosophical problem but as an existential crisis: the sinner, confronted with God's righteous standard, has nothing to offer. This "law-work" — the conviction of sin that prepares the heart for [[Grace]] — is central to [[john-newton|Newton]]'s evangelical scheme.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s treatment of justice moves through a characteristic progression: terror → despair → gospel relief. The sinner's first encounter with divine justice is frightening:

> O sinner, say, What can you to justice pay?
> Tremble, lest, when life is past, You be lost.
> — [PN-145-003]

The rhetorical question ("What can you to justice pay?") has an implied answer: nothing. The sinner is bankrupt before God's righteous demands. This existential terror intensifies:

> If justice pursue, What heart can endure?
> — [PN-247-001]

Yet [[john-newton|Newton]] never leaves the sinner in despair. Justice and mercy meet in the redemptive work of Christ:

> Love, with awful justice mix'd,
> In his great redemption work.
> — [PN-078-002]

And the triumphant resolution:

> If in condemnation Strict justice takes place,
> It shines in salvation, Through no merit of ours.
> — [PN-247-007]

The paradox is that justice — which seemed to threaten condemnation — actually "shines" in salvation. At the cross, justice is satisfied and mercy is extended:

> Let us wonder, grace and justice
> Join, and point to mercy's store.
> — [PN-323-004]

## Theological Framework

The Reformed theological tradition that shaped [[john-newton|Newton]] understands justice as an essential divine attribute. God *must* punish sin; justice is not arbitrary but intrinsic to his character. This creates the problem of the Gospel: how can a just God forgive sinners?

The answer is penal substitutionary atonement: Christ bears the penalty of sin, satisfying divine justice, so that God can be "just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus" (Romans 3:26). [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns dramatize this transaction: the sinner trembles before justice, discovers his inability to pay, and flees to Christ — where justice and mercy meet.
