## Summary

[[Esau]] was the firstborn twin son of [[isaac|Isaac]] and [[rebekah|Rebekah]], born moments before his brother [[jacob|Jacob]] (Genesis 25:24–26). As the elder, he held the birthright — the double portion and family headship — but he "despised" it, trading it to [[jacob|Jacob]] for bread and lentil stew (Genesis 25:29–34). Later, [[jacob|Jacob]] deceived their blind father [[isaac|Isaac]] and received the blessing meant for [[Esau]], completing the reversal prophesied at their birth: "the older shall serve the younger" (Genesis 25:23).

[[Esau]]'s story is one of profane disregard for spiritual privilege. The New Testament holds him up as a cautionary example: "See that no one is... unholy like [[esau|Esau]], who sold his birthright for a single meal" (Hebrews 12:16).

In the Olney Hymns, [[john-newton|Newton]] alludes to [[Esau]] in the context of warnings against worldliness and the folly of choosing present gratification over eternal inheritance.

## Biblical Narrative

[[Esau]] is introduced as a "skillful hunter, a man of the field," while [[jacob|Jacob]] was "a quiet man, dwelling in tents" (Genesis 25:27). [[isaac|Isaac]] loved [[Esau]] for his game; [[rebekah|Rebekah]] loved [[jacob|Jacob]].

The pivotal episode occurs when [[Esau]] returns exhausted from hunting and finds [[jacob|Jacob]] cooking stew. "Let me eat some of that red stew, for I am exhausted," he says. [[jacob|Jacob]] demands the birthright in exchange. [[Esau]]'s reply — "I am about to die; of what use is a birthright to me?" (Genesis 25:32) — reveals his fatal flaw: he values the immediate over the eternal.

The stolen blessing (Genesis 27) compounds the tragedy. [[Esau]]'s anguished cry — "Have you but one blessing, my father?" — is one of Scripture's most poignant moments. Though [[Esau]] later reconciles with [[jacob|Jacob]] (Genesis 33), his descendants, the Edomites, became perennial adversaries of [[israel|Israel]].

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] references [[Esau]] as an emblem of spiritual carelessness, drawing one of the collection's most searching pastoral warnings:

> Let not the world's enchanting bowl
> Prevail on thee to sell thy soul.
> — [PN-088-008]

The "enchanting bowl" evokes [[Esau]]'s stew — a small, momentary satisfaction purchased at the cost of eternal ruin. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s warning is pastoral: believers must guard against the temptation to trade spiritual birthrights (peace of conscience, assurance, hope of glory) for fleeting worldly pleasures. The word "enchanting" is significant: the bowl exerts a spell-like power, and only vigilance — the word of God breaking the enchantment — can protect the soul.

### Election Illustrated

The contrast between [[Esau]] and [[jacob|Jacob]] also illustrates the doctrine of election: God's choice of [[jacob|Jacob]] over [[Esau]] "though they were not yet born and had done nothing either good or bad" (Romans 9:11) demonstrates that grace rests on divine will, not human merit. For Newton, this is not grounds for speculation but for humility: if God's choice depended on foreseen merit, no one would be chosen.

### Esau as Pastoral Mirror

Newton's use of Esau functions as a diagnostic tool. The believer is implicitly asked: "What would you trade your birthright for?" The answer reveals the heart. Esau traded for stew; others trade for reputation, comfort, or respectability. The object differs but the transaction is the same — the eternal exchanged for the temporal.

## Theological Significance

[[Esau]] is the paradigmatic "profane" person in Scripture — not irreligious in a formal sense, but indifferent to sacred things. His fault was not hostility toward God but the practical atheism of appetite: he let hunger decide his destiny. For [[john-newton|Newton]], this is a pastoral diagnostic: the true believer values the birthright; the nominal professor sells it for stew. The Esau test is not doctrinal orthodoxy but practical priority — what do you reach for when you are hungry?
