## Summary

[[Hope]] in the Christian sense is not wishful thinking or uncertain optimism but a confident expectation grounded in the character and promises of God. It is one of the three theological virtues alongside [[Faith]] and [[Love]] (1 Corinthians 13:13). In the Olney Hymns, hope is the believer's lifeline through spiritual darkness — the anticipation of final deliverance that sustains the soul in present trial.

[[John Newton]]'s treatment of hope is characteristically experiential: hope is not a theory but a felt reality, "an anchor of the soul, both sure and steadfast" (Hebrews 6:19). He pairs hope with its opposite (fear, despair) to dramatize the believer's oscillation between confidence and doubt — and the ultimate triumph of hope in [[saviour|Christ]].

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] presents hope as a hard-won conviction, often contrasted with fear:

> Like her, with hopes and fears we come,
> To touch the Saviour's hem.
> — [PN-014-006]

The woman with the issue of blood (Mark 5) becomes a type of the believer — approaching Christ with a mixture of hope and fear, yet received with mercy. The enemy of hope is spiritual discouragement:

> He tells us we're weak,
> Our hope is in vain,
> The good that we seek
> We ne'er shall obtain.
> — [PN-007-008]

These words are placed in the mouth of the Tempter — the voice that whispers despair and undermines confidence. [[john-newton|Newton]] counters this by grounding hope not in the believer's performance but in Christ's sufficiency:

> With Christ on our side —
> We hope to die shouting,
> The Lord will provide.
> — [PN-007-008]

The phrase "die shouting" captures the robust, almost defiant quality of [[john-newton|Newton]]ian hope — it is a confidence that persists even through death. The foundation of this hope is Christ's presence, not the believer's feelings:

> Nor sink in hopeless fear;
> For, when they least expect it,
> Their Saviour will appear.
> — [PN-006-001]

[[john-newton|Newton]]'s pastoral genius is evident: he acknowledges the real experience of "hopeless fear" while redirecting the gaze to the coming [[Saviour]].

## Hope and Assurance

In Puritan and evangelical theology, hope is closely related to assurance — the confidence that one is truly in a state of [[Grace]]. [[john-newton|Newton]]'s hymns navigate the tension between the "full assurance of hope" (Hebrews 6:11) and the honest acknowledgment of doubt. Hope is not the absence of uncertainty but the decision to trust God's promises despite uncertainty.
