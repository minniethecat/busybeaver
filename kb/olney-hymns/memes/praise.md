## Definition

**Praise**, in the [[Olney-Hymns-Title|Olney Hymns]], is the glad, vocal acknowledgment of God's nature and deeds — the response of a [[grace]]-filled heart to its benefactor. It is both a present duty and an eternal destiny: the believer praises God now for [[salvation]] and will praise him forever in glory. [[john-newton|John Newton]] presents praise not as a liturgical formality but as the spontaneous overflow of a heart that has tasted divine goodness.

The Olney Hymns themselves are acts of praise. The collection was compiled for Newton's parishioners at Olney to sing together — congregational praise was the organizing purpose of the entire work. Every hymn, whatever its specific theme, participates in the broader act of rendering glory to God.

## Biblical Basis

The hymns ground the call to praise in the consistent witness of Scripture:

| Passage | Theme | Hymn Expression |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Psalm 150:6 | "Let everything that has breath praise the Lord" | "I will praise thee every day" (058-001) |
| Hebrews 13:15 | "The fruit of lips that acknowledge his name" | "Accept the praise I bring" (057-005) |
| Psalm 103:1–2 | "Bless the Lord, O my soul, and forget not all his benefits" | Praise for daily mercies throughout the hymns |
| Revelation 5:12–13 | Eternal praise to the Lamb | "When I see thee as thou art I'll praise thee as I ought" (057-006) |

Praise in the Olney Hymns is thoroughly theocentric — it is directed to God, for God's own sake, and only secondarily for the benefit of the worshiper.

## In the Olney Hymns

Praise permeates the Olney Hymns as both explicit exhortation and implicit posture. Several dimensions emerge:

### Daily Praise

[[john-newton|Newton]] ties praise to the regular rhythm of morning and evening devotion:

> Soon as the morn the light reveal'd, His praises tuned my tongue.
> — [043-002]

> I will praise thee every day, Now thine anger's turn'd away.
> — [058-001]

Daily praise is the fruit of daily [[grace]]. The first citation (043-002) links praise to the rising of the sun — as regular as morning [[light]], so regular should be the believer's thanksgiving. The second (058-001) anchors daily praise in the settled reality of reconciliation: God's anger has been turned away, so every day is a day for praise.

### The Vow of Perpetual Praise

The believer commits to a lifetime — and beyond — of praising God:

> And his praises shall prolong, / While I live, my joyful tongue.
> — [058-003]

The vow of lifelong praise is not a burden but a joy. The phrase "joyful tongue" insists that authentic praise is never merely dutiful; it arises from delight in its object.

### Praise that Asks for Acceptance

The hymns recognize that human praise, offered by sinful lips, requires divine acceptance:

> Accept the praise I bring.
> — [057-005]

This brief petition acknowledges the asymmetry of worship: the creature offers praise, but only the Creator can receive it rightly. The request for acceptance is itself an act of [[faith]] — trusting that God welcomes the imperfect worship of sincere hearts.

### Praise Despite Affliction

The hymns insist that praise is not contingent on favorable circumstances:

> Sweetly spent in pray'r and praise.
> — [044-003]

The adverb "sweetly" is striking — the time spent in prayer and praise is not grim duty but genuine sweetness, even when outward circumstances are bitter. This transforms praise from a fair-weather activity into a constant discipline and delight.

### Praise from Experience

The hymns root praise in testimony — what God has done is recounted as ground for worship:

> And many can say to his praise, / He sends them by ravens their food.
> — [035-003]

The allusion to Elijah fed by ravens (1 Kings 17) personalizes divine provision: what God did for Elijah, he continues to do for his people. Praise is not generic religious sentiment but specific gratitude for specific deliverances. "Many can say to his praise" — testimony is inherently corporate, building the [[faith]] of the whole congregation.

### Praise in Life and Death

Praise is so essential that the prospect of death is framed as the loss of the capacity to praise:

> For who can praise thee in the grave, / Or sing the triumphs of thy grace?
> — [045-005]

This echoes the Old Testament psalmic logic (Psalm 6:5, 30:9): the living praise God; the dead are silent. The plea for continued life is at root a plea for continued opportunity to glorify God. [[grace]] is the content of praise — "the triumphs of thy grace" — and praising it is the believer's highest employment.

### Eternal Praise

The highest expression of praise awaits the believer's entrance into glory:

> When I see thee as thou art / I'll praise thee as I ought.
> — [057-006]

This couplet captures the eschatological horizon of all Olney Hymn praise. Present praise is real but incomplete — limited by finitude, sin, and partial sight. The beatific vision ("when I see thee as thou art") will perfect praise ("as I ought"). Until then, all earthly worship is rehearsal for heaven.

### The Insufficiency of Human Praise

The hymns acknowledge that even the best human praise falls short:

> The praise and the glory be thine.
> — [008-005]

This concluding line of a longer meditation on human frailty redirects all honor to God. The context is the brevity and vanity of human acclaim — "its favour and praise are but breath" (008-003). Human praise is fleeting and ultimately worthless; divine praise is the only praise that matters.
