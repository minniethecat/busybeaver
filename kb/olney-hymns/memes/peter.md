## Identity

[[peter|Peter the Apostle]], originally named Simon, was a Galilean fisherman called by [[saviour|Jesus Christ]] to be a "fisher of men" (Matthew 4:19). Renamed Peter ("rock") by Christ, he became the foremost of the twelve apostles — bold in confession ("You are the Christ, the Son of the living God"), impulsive in action (stepping out of the boat onto stormy waters), and yet weak in the hour of trial (denying Christ three times). In the Olney Hymns, [[John Newton]] devotes multiple hymns to Peter's experiences, seeing in him the pattern of every believer: called by grace, sinking through unbelief, and restored by Christ's look.

## Biblical Context

Peter dominates the Gospel narratives and the first half of Acts. A fisherman from Bethsaida, he was called with his brother Andrew at the Sea of Galilee (Matthew 4:18–20). His key moments include: the catch of fish (Luke 5:1–11), walking on water (Matthew 14:28–31), the great confession at Caesarea Philippi (Matthew 16:16–19), the Transfiguration (Matthew 17:1–8), the threefold denial and the rooster's crow (Matthew 26:69–75), the restoration by Christ at the Sea of Galilee (John 21:15–19), the Pentecost sermon (Acts 2), the healing of the lame man (Acts 3), and his miraculous release from prison (Acts 12). Tradition holds he was martyred in Rome under Nero, crucified upside down at his own request.

## In the Olney Hymns

[[john-newton|Newton]] devotes at least four hymns specifically to Peter: Hymns 87 (Walking on Water), 91 ([[sin|Sin]]ning and Repenting), 122 (Released from Prison), and Hymn 10 of Book II (Casting the Gospel Net). Peter's experience on the stormy sea becomes a paradigm of faith and doubt:

> To Peter on the waves he came,
> And gave him instant peace;
> He bade him rise, and come to him,
> And made the tempest cease.
> — [PN-1087-003]

[[john-newton|Newton]] personalizes Peter's cry: "[[saviour|Lord]], save me, or I sink":

> Peter's request was mine;
> Lord, call me down, I long to prove
> That I am truly thine.
> — [PN-1087-004]

Peter's denial and repentance (Hymn 91) receives extended treatment:

> When Peter boasted, soon he fell,
> Yet was by grace restored;
> His case should be regarded well
> By all who fear the Lord.
> — [PN-1091-001]

[[john-newton|Newton]] draws the application to every believer:

> As I, like Peter, vows have made,
> Yet acted Peter's part;
> So conscience, like the cock, upbraids
> My vile and wandering heart.
> — [PN-1091-009]

Peter's miraculous prison release (Hymn 122) becomes an image of spiritual liberation:

> Peter, when in prison cast,
> Though by soldiers kept with care —
> Every door wide open flew;
> Peter thought he dreamed at first.
> — [PN-1122-002]

And his night of fruitless fishing, followed by Christ's command to cast the net again (Book II, Hymn 10), illustrates the difference between human effort and divine blessing:

> When Peter, through the tedious night,
> Had often cast his net in vain;
> But at the Lord's command he cast,
> And soon enclosed a numerous shoal.
> — [PN-2010-001]
