# Summer

Summer in *The Getting of Wisdom* is the season of return — to home, to childhood landscapes, to the self that exists outside the Ladies' College. The Australian summer, with its characteristic heat and languor, provides the atmospheric backdrop for Laura Rambotham's holidays and forms a sensory contrast with the enclosed, regulated world of the school. Summer is, in the novel's emotional geography, the season of freedom, however temporary and however complicated that freedom proves to be.

## Summer Heat and Australian Identity

The Australian summer heat permeates the novel's descriptive passages, establishing a distinctively colonial atmosphere. The "scorching" sun, the dust of country roads, the relief of shade — these sensory details ground the novel in its Australian setting and distinguish it from the English boarding-school tradition against which Richardson was writing. Summer is the season in which Australia is most itself, and Laura's experience of summer is inseparable from her experience of Australian identity.

The contrast between the College's regulated interior and the sun-drenched freedom of the Australian summer landscape is one of the novel's structuring oppositions. At school, Laura's body is disciplined — uniformed, supervised, constrained. In summer, the body asserts its own rhythms: the languor of hot afternoons, the energy of cool mornings, the physical freedom of outdoor spaces.

## Summer Holidays

The summer holidays represent the longest period Laura spends away from the College, and they are consequently the period in which the distance between her school self and her home self becomes most apparent. During summer, Laura must negotiate the expectations of her family alongside the habits and attitudes she has acquired at school — a negotiation that is never entirely successful. The summer holiday, rather than providing simple respite, becomes another arena of performance and judgment.

The novel's summer passages are marked by a particular quality of attention — a heightened awareness of sensory detail, of landscape, of the physical world that the College's institutional architecture obscures. Summer is the season in which Laura is most connected to the natural world and, paradoxically, most aware of her estrangement from it. The girl who climbs trees and runs through paddocks at home is not the girl who navigates the College's social hierarchies — and the summer holiday forces Laura to confront the gap between these two selves.

## Summer and Childhood

Summer is also the season most closely associated with Laura's childhood — the period before the College, before the acquisition of the "wisdom" that gives the novel its title. Summer landscapes evoke memories of a younger, less guarded self: the Laura who played with her brothers, who knew nothing of social class or the tyranny of peer judgment, who was at home in her body and in the world. Each summer return is thus shadowed by the awareness of what has been lost — the innocence that cannot be recovered, the childhood that recedes with each passing year.

The novel's treatment of summer participates in the broader literary tradition of the season as a time of transformation and heightened emotion. Summer is when things happen — when the constraints of routine loosen, when encounters occur that would be impossible within the school's regulated calendar, when the self is exposed to experiences that will only be understood in retrospect.

## Seasonal Rhythm

The seasonal cycle in *The Getting of Wisdom* — summer holidays, autumn term, winter routines, spring examinations — structures the narrative's temporal architecture. Each season carries its own emotional register: autumn is the season of new beginnings and fresh anxieties, winter the season of endurance and consolidation, spring the season of anticipation and conclusion. But summer is the season of truth — the time when the defenses constructed during the school year are tested against the realities of home, family, and the self that exists beyond the College's walls.
