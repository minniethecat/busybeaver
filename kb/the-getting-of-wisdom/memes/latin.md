# Latin

Latin in "The Getting of Wisdom" is a cipher for educational privilege. Laura's ignorance of the language upon entering the Ladies' College exposes her provincial background, while her eventual study of it marks her academic advancement. The language thus functions as a class marker within the novel's educational economy.

## In the Novel

Latin makes its first appearance as a test Laura fails. When Miss Chapman asks "Wherever did you learn Latin?" (PN [[005-054]]), Laura is forced to confess "that she had never learnt Latin at all" (PN [[005-055]]) — a moment of exposure that reveals the gap between Laura's quick intelligence and her rudimentary preparation. By the second year, however, Laura has advanced sufficiently to "begin Latin" (PN [[010-002]]), a milestone rewarded with a place in the second class. The subject's difficulty escalates: by the upper forms, "Latin was no longer bounded by the elementary grammar" (PN [[013-002]]) but had become a serious academic discipline, alongside Euclid and other advanced subjects. Laura's capacity to translate Latin — "You know Latin, don't you? Just give us a hand" (PN [[022-007]]) — becomes, by the novel's later chapters, a mark of the competence she has achieved even as she fails her public examinations.

## Key Passages

> "Wherever did you learn Latin?" — [[005-054]], Miss Chapman's question exposes Laura's educational deficits

> She confessed that she had never learnt Latin at all. — [[005-055]], a moment of humiliating honesty

> She began Latin; and as a reward was allowed to mount a form. — [[010-002]], academic advancement as institutional recognition

> "You know Latin, don't you? Just give us a hand." — [[022-007]], Laura's hard-won competence acknowledged by her peers

## Historical Context

In the late-Victorian educational system, Latin was the gatekeeper of serious scholarship — a requirement for university matriculation and a marker of class status. For girls' schools like the Ladies' College, offering Latin was itself a claim to intellectual seriousness, distinguishing the institution from finishing schools that emphasized accomplishments over scholarship. Laura's progress from ignorance to competence in Latin thus tracks her broader educational trajectory.
