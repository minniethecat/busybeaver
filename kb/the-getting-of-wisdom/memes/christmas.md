# Christmas

Christmas serves as a structural punctuation in "The Getting of Wisdom," marking the novel's annual rhythm and providing the occasion for Laura's returns home. Each Christmas holiday measures Laura's growing distance from her family and her progressive socialization into the codes of the Ladies' College.

## In the Novel

The novel's timeline is organized around the Christmas break. Laura's first return home occurs early in the narrative (PN [[010-025]]), and her behavior during this visit reveals how the school has already begun to reshape her: she behaves with an uncharacteristic reserve, newly conscious of the gulf between her family's rusticity and the college's polish. The second Christmas (PN [[020-008]]) brings a significant change — her room at school is changed, marking her advancement in the school's hierarchy. By the third Christmas (PN [[021-004]]), Laura has become "one of the few permitted" students, enjoying privileges she could not have imagined at the novel's opening. The Christmas season after the matriculation examinations (PN [[022-005]]) brings yet another shift in the social landscape, as students who have passed their examinations move on. The final Christmas holiday (PN [[019-010]]) arrives after weeks of anxious waiting for examination results.

## Key Passages

> When she went home at Christmas it went with her, still in the bottom of her box. — [[010-025]], Laura's first Christmas return, carrying an unopened gift that symbolizes her changed relationship to home

> Shortly after Christmas, her room was changed for the better one. — [[020-008]], institutional advancement marked by the holiday calendar

> Since Christmas, she was one of the few permitted students. — [[021-004]], Christmas as a threshold to privilege

> Weeks had still to run till the Christmas holidays. She drew twenty-one days on her calendar. — [[019-010]], the anxious countdown to release

## Significance

Christmas functions thematically as a hinge between two worlds — the family home and the Ladies' College. Each return home reveals a Laura further removed from her origins, more fluent in the school's language and less comfortable in her family's. The holiday thus becomes a measure of the novel's central transformation: Laura's "getting of wisdom" is also a getting of distance from everything she once was.
