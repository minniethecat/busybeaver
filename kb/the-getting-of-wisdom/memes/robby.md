# Robby (Mr. Shepherd)

Robby — familiarly known by his family but formally the Reverend Mr. Shepherd — is the young curate at St Stephen's-on-the-Hill, and the object of one of Laura Rambotham's most revealing adolescent crushes during her years at the Ladies' College. His significance lies not in his own personality (which the novel renders with characteristic irony) but in what Laura's infatuation reveals about her capacity to transform reality through imagination — the same faculty that drives her storytelling and her musical passion.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Shepherd first appears in Laura's consciousness not as a person but as a solution to a social problem: "someone had to be loved if you were to be able to hold up your head with the rest" (PN [[016-001]]). Laura and several of her friends fix upon the curate precisely because "no personal contact was necessary" — enabling "immense ardour, without needing either to humble your pride or call invention to your aid: the worship took place from afar" (PN [[016-001]]). The narrator notes that he is "quite attractive, in a lean, ascetic fashion, with his spiritual blue eyes, and the plain gold cross that dangled from his black watch-ribbon" (PN [[016-001]]).

Laura discovers a principle that governs much of her subsequent behaviour: "if you imagine a thing with sufficient force, you can induce your imagining to become reality" (PN [[016-002]]). By sheer will, she "worked herself up into an attack of love, which was genuine enough to make her redden when Mr. Shepherd was spoken of" (PN [[016-002]]).

The curate's holy calling becomes entangled with Laura's genuine religious feeling: "her feelings for Mr. Shepherd were soon mystically jumbled up with her piety: the eastward slant for the Creed, and the Salutation at the Sacred Name, seemed not alone homage due to the Deity, but also a kind of minor homage offered to and accepted by Mr. Shepherd" (PN [[016-002]]).

## The Crush as Social Mechanism

Laura's infatuation with Mr. Shepherd is not, the novel makes clear, a matter of spontaneous affection. It is a carefully constructed performance — so carefully constructed that Laura herself loses sight of its constructedness. The crush serves multiple functions: it provides Laura with a normative adolescent experience that she can discuss with her friends, it channels her religious feeling into a socially legible form, and it allows her to participate in the school's culture of romantic fantasy without risking the exposure that real social interaction would entail.

The fact that Laura's crush is deliberately manufactured does not, in the novel's accounting, make it less real. Richardson's psychological acuity is most evident here: the recognition that performed emotion, sustained with sufficient conviction, becomes indistinguishable from genuine feeling. Laura's "attack of love" is both a fiction and a fact, and the novel refuses to resolve the paradox.

## The Visit to the Shepherd Household (Ch. XVI)

The crisis of Laura's infatuation comes during her monthly-holiday visit to the Shepherd household in Prahran. There, she encounters Mr. Shepherd not as the distant object of romantic-religious veneration but as a domestic presence — a man whose "voice — it went on and on — sounded, at such close quarters, both harsh and rasping" (PN [[016-039]]) through the thin ceiling. The curate's spiritual glamour dissipates in the ordinary context of family meals, thin walls, and the mundane routines of clerical domesticity.

The visit does not so much destroy Laura's crush as recontextualize it. She continues to perform her infatuation for social purposes — "to make her redden when Mr. Shepherd was spoken of, and to enjoy being teased about him" (PN [[016-025]]) — but the performance has been hollowed out. The gap between the imagined curate and the actual man has been exposed, and Laura's capacity to sustain the former in the face of the latter marks both the resilience and the limitation of her imaginative powers.

## Character

Mr. Shepherd is rendered almost entirely through Laura's perception, which means the reader sees him as she does — first as an idealized figure of spiritual glamour, then as a disappointing reality. He is "lean, ascetic" in appearance, with the "plain gold cross" that marks his clerical identity. His relationship with Laura is conducted entirely at a distance: he is unaware of her feelings, and their interactions are limited to the most formal social exchanges. This distance is essential to his function in the novel — he is not a character in his own right so much as a screen onto which Laura projects her need for an object of romantic devotion.

## Significance

Robby Shepherd represents one of the novel's most sophisticated treatments of the relationship between imagination and reality. Laura's capacity to generate genuine emotion for an imagined object is both her greatest gift (it is the same capacity that fuels her musical expression) and her greatest vulnerability (it allows her to be deceived by her own fabrications). The curate's function in the narrative is to make this doubleness visible: he is the test case for Laura's theory that "if you imagine a thing with sufficient force, you can induce your imagining to become reality."
