# Loneliness

Loneliness in *The Getting of Wisdom* is not merely the absence of company but a profound emotional isolation that defines Laura Rambotham's experience of growing up. She is "lonely" even when surrounded by others — a "bottled-up child" whose inner world separates her from her peers.

## In the Novel

Loneliness is Laura's constant companion through the novel. It begins with her social estrangement as a newcomer and deepens through her complex relationships. When her friendship with Pin falters, "Laura retired in lonely dudgeon to the beach" (PN=019-057) — loneliness as chosen retreat. Her growing attachment to Evelyn Souttar is explained by her nature as "a lonely, bottled-up child" who returns liking "with interest" when someone shows her kindness (PN=022-031). The novel's most intense loneliness comes after Evelyn's departure: Laura feels "guilty, careworn, lonely" and "shrank into her shell" (PN=024-004), experiencing a solitude so complete that "for a time she felt as lonely as she had done in those unthinkable days when she was still the poor little green 'new chum'" (PN=024-019). At night "the loneliness returned, and was only the more intense because, for some hours on end, she had been able to forget it" (PN=024-021).

## Key Passages

> Laura's friendship with Evelyn is rooted in her loneliness: "She began to return the liking, with interest, after the manner of a lonely, bottled-up child. And everything about Evelyn made it easy to grow fond of her." (PN=022-031)

> After Evelyn's departure, loneliness becomes overwhelming: "It seemed as if everybody and everything were ranged against her; and guilty, careworn, lonely, she shrank into her shell." (PN=024-004)

> The return to the school without Evelyn rekindles early isolation: "Laura was in a strange room, with strange, indifferent girls; and for a time she felt as lonely as she had done in those unthinkable days when she was still the poor little green 'new chum.'" (PN=024-019)

> Loneliness returns at night with renewed force: "But at night the loneliness returned, and was only the more intense because, for some hours on end, she had been able to forget it." (PN=024-021)

> Laura's chosen solitude: "Laura retired in lonely dudgeon to the beach." (PN=019-057)

## Characters

**Laura** is the novel's primary vessel for loneliness. Her experience moves through phases: the shock of initial isolation, the desperate attachment to friends who alleviate it (Evelyn), and the eventual, more mature recognition that loneliness is an inextricable part of her temperament. **Evelyn Souttar** serves as the antidote to Laura's loneliness — her "careless, happy, laughing sunniness" (PN=022-031) provides the warmth Laura craves, which makes her departure all the more devastating.

## Historical Context

Richardson's treatment of childhood loneliness reflects emerging early-twentieth-century psychological understandings of the inner life of children. The "bottled-up child" motif echoes Victorian and Edwardian ideals of emotional restraint, where children — particularly girls — were expected to manage their feelings privately. The boarding school setting of Ladies' College intensifies this dynamic, as children are removed from family comfort and left to navigate emotional life largely on their own.
