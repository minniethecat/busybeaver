# Ormond College

Ormond College, the Presbyterian residential college of the University of Melbourne, appears in "The Getting of Wisdom" alongside Trinity College as part of the university world whose "coloured blazers" and boat-race rivalries define the masculine social sphere toward which the Ladies' College curriculum is ultimately directed.

## In the Novel

Ormond College appears in Chapter XIV, at the picnic scene, where "some Trinity and Ormond men with coloured blazers" (PN [[014-026]]) join the group of schoolgirls. The mention of both colleges — Trinity (Anglican) and Ormond (Presbyterian) — maps the denominational structure of the University of Melbourne's residential system onto the social geography of the novel. The "coloured blazers" function as visual markers of institutional and religious identity: to know which blazer belongs to which college is to possess the cultural literacy of Melbourne's educated classes.

For Laura, the Ormond men are figures of distant fascination — part of a world she can observe but not enter. The university sphere, with its colleges, lectures, degrees, and social rituals, represents a form of intellectual life to which the Ladies' College is merely preparatory, and which only the most exceptional of its graduates (M. P., Mary Pidwall) will actually attain.

## Historical Context

Ormond College was founded in 1881 as the Presbyterian residential college of the University of Melbourne, nine years after Trinity College's establishment. Together, the two colleges dominated the university's residential life in the 1890s, and their denominational identities — Anglican and Presbyterian — reflected the sectarian divisions that structured Melbourne's Protestant establishment. The "boat-race" rivalry between Trinity and Ormond, mentioned in the novel (PN [[014-048]]), was an established feature of university social life, and the schoolgirls' alignment with one college or the other ("We church girls always wear Trinity colours," PN [[014-048]]) mapped their own religious affiliations onto the masculine world of university sport.

## Significance

Ormond College's brief appearance in the novel serves a structural purpose: along with Trinity, it defines the limit of the Ladies' College world. The university colleges represent genuine intellectual life, professional aspiration, and a social freedom that the school's "church girls" can admire but not access. The "coloured blazers" are visual shorthand for a masculine world of higher learning that exists in parallel to the feminine world of "accomplishments" — a world that Laura, in her closing fantasy, briefly imagines herself entering.
