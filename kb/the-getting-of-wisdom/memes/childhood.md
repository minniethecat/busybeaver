# Childhood

Childhood in "The Getting of Wisdom" is not a fixed developmental stage but a lost country — the world Laura Rambotham inhabits before the Ladies' College, before social consciousness, before the discovery that one's self is subject to the judgment of others. The novel's opening chapters, set in and around the Rambotham household at Warrenega, establish childhood as a state of relative plenitude that the subsequent narrative will systematically dismantle.

## Warrenega: The Childhood World

The early chapters depict Laura's childhood as a world of sensory richness and emotional security. The garden at Warrenega, with its "two summer-houses, a loom, and a bush" (PN [[002-030]]), is an Edenic space where Laura's imagination can roam freely. Her relationships with her siblings — Pin, Leppie, and the others — are governed by the logic of play rather than the logic of status. Her mother's love, though anxious and sometimes oppressive, is unconditional in a way that the approval of peers and teachers will never be.

The departure for Melbourne — the train journey with Mother, the arrival at the Ladies' College, the first night in an unfamiliar bed — is the expulsion from this childhood Eden. Richardson renders the break with a precision that owes something to the Romantic tradition of the lost childhood (Wordsworth's "shades of the prison-house") but also to a more modern, psychologically acute understanding of how social institutions dismantle the childhood self.

## The Loss of Childhood

Laura's childhood self is dismantled not all at once but through an accumulation of small violences. Lilith Gordon's interrogations, the school's dress code, the public ranking of academic performance, the discovery that one's mother's occupation is a source of shame rather than pride — each of these experiences peels away a layer of the childhood self and replaces it with something more socially functional but less authentic.

The novel is unusual among Bildungsromane in the clarity with which it sees this process as a loss rather than a gain. Laura does not grow up; she is grown over — her childhood self buried beneath layers of social performance that protect her from the judgment of others but also separate her from her own desires and perceptions. The novel's final image — Laura on the dock, watching the "handsome Trinity man" — captures this doubleness: she has become a young woman capable of navigating the social world, but at the cost of a self that could experience that world without irony.

## Childhood Memory

The novel is structured around the persistence of childhood memory. Laura's letters home, her dreams of Warrenega, her moments of unguarded feeling — all testify to the continuing presence of the childhood self beneath the social carapace she has constructed. The novel's psychological realism lies in its recognition that childhood is never simply left behind; it is the baseline against which all subsequent experience is measured, the touchstone of authenticity in a world of performance.

## Significance

Childhood in "The Getting of Wisdom" functions as a normative ideal — a standard of authentic selfhood against which the deformations of social life are measured. Richardson's achievement is to render this ideal without sentimentality: Laura's childhood is not idyllic in any simple sense (her family is poor, her mother anxious, her father absent), but it is *hers* — a self that has not yet learned to be ashamed. The novel's coming-of-age narrative is the story of that self's gradual loss, and the "wisdom" of the title is inseparable from the knowledge of what has been sacrificed to acquire it.
