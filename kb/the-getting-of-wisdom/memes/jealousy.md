# Jealousy

Jealousy in "The Getting of Wisdom" is one of the primary emotional forces that structure relations among the girls at the [[ladies-college]]. It appears in multiple registers — as hungry watching, as competitive exclusion, as the poison of intimacy — and it is experienced both by [[laura-rambotham]] and by those around her with an intensity that reveals the precariousness of social standing in the school's hothouse atmosphere.

## The Gaze of Envy

> Laura meanwhile looking on, from a high, uncomfortable chair, with a somewhat hungry envy. — [[008-011]]

Laura's first experience of envy is observational and passive. Watching the older girl Marina navigate the adult world of grocery orders, cheque-writing, and household management, Laura sits perched on a high chair in a cooperative store, excluded by youth and inexperience from the competence she observes. The envy is "hungry" — a word that suggests appetite, need, the ache of an unfulfilled wanting. It is a form of desire that has no object except the condition of being someone else, someone older, someone at home in the world.

This scene establishes the pattern that will recur throughout the novel: Laura watching others from the outside, wanting what they have, unable to articulate her want except through the intensity of her gaze.

## The Jealous Clique

> As for the boarders who sat in this form, they made up a jealous little clique, and it was some time before the younger couple could discover the secret bond. — [[013-003]]

When Laura and [[tilly]] are promoted to a higher form, they encounter a new social configuration: a "jealous little clique" of older girls who guard their territory against interlopers. The secret bond that unites them turns out to be a coded game of biblical reference, a private language that excludes newcomers by design. Jealousy here is not an individual emotion but a group property — the collective guarding of social capital against those who might dilute or claim it.

Laura's exclusion from this clique is doubly painful: she is excluded not only from friendship but from knowledge, the "secret bond" that she cannot crack. The jealousy of the clique is defensive, territorial — a wall erected against the threat of shared intimacy.

## Envy of the Successful

> The Church girls were wild with envy. — [[016-005]]

When Laura receives an invitation to visit the home of [[mr-shepherd]], the Church girls — [[maria-morell]], [[kate-horner]], and their circle — react with undisguised envy. Their response is described as "wild with envy," and they subject Laura to a campaign of innuendo and warning designed to poison her pleasure in the invitation. "These saints are oftenest bad 'uns," Kate Horner gobbles [[016-007]], and the girls urge Laura to beware of being left alone with Mr. Shepherd in the dark.

This episode reveals how envy operates as a social weapon: the envious cannot bear another's good fortune and seek to destroy it through insinuation and sabotage. The Church girls cannot have what Laura has, so they attempt to make what she has worthless.

## Laura's Own Jealousy

> Laura was not only jealous, she was extraordinarily suspicious. — [[023-036]]

Laura herself is no stranger to jealousy. Her attachment to [[evelyn-souttar]] is marked by possessiveness and fear of displacement, and her "extraordinarily suspicious" nature [[023-036]] causes her to interpret innocent interactions as threats. When [[tilly]] calls her "a jealous little cub" [[018-023]], the charge stings because it is true — Laura wants to be the sole object of her friends' attention, and she resents any sharing of that attention with others.

Laura's jealousy is the shadow side of her capacity for fierce attachment. She loves intensely and therefore fears loss intensely; her hunger for belonging makes every friendship a precarious possession, vulnerable to theft by any newcomer. The novel does not condemn Laura's jealousy but renders it as a natural consequence of her emotional intensity and her long history of exclusion.

## The Jealous God

> ...was mindful not to scrimp his portion, remembering that the Lord our God is a jealous God. — [[024-033]]

The novel's final reference to jealousy invokes the divine: the God of the Old Testament is "a jealous God," demanding exclusive devotion. This theological frame elevates jealousy from petty schoolgirl emotion to cosmic principle — the anger of a sovereign who will brook no rival. It is a fitting conclusion to the novel's treatment of the emotion, suggesting that jealousy, however destructive, is also a form of love that cannot bear division.
