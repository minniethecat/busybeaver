# Food

Food in "The Getting of Wisdom" surfaces as a quiet index of power, comfort, and social distinction. Though never the novel's central concern, eating and its regulation punctuate the narrative, revealing the bodily dimension of Laura's education — what she consumes, and what is denied her, speaks to the school's broader control over its students.

## In the Novel

The theme appears first in the domestic context of the Rambotham household, where Laura's mother uses the denial of food as a disciplinary tool: "It's a very good thing you're going away, for I should have to deny you your food" (PN [[001-062]]). At the Ladies' College, food becomes a marker of institutional adequacy — Laura writes to Mother that "the food is very nice for dinner" (PN [[006-038]]), a reassurance that also reveals her awareness of the school's material conditions. Her mother's later anxiety that Laura is "not getting proper food" (PN [[019-010]]) becomes grounds for complaint, the maternal concern for bodily nourishment contrasting with the school's concern for moral formation. The food at home, described in Laura's holiday visit, is characterized by an overwhelming smell: "it even got into the food" (PN [[019-025]]), the domestic cooking's pungency marking the distance between the Rambothams' rough comfort and the college's institutional refinement.

Eating also serves as a social stage. Mealtimes at the college are communal rituals where hierarchy is enacted — who sits where, who gets what, how one eats — and Laura, perpetually anxious about her performance, navigates these occasions with the same alert self-consciousness she brings to every other social arena.

## Key Passages

> "It's a very good thing you're going away, for I should have to deny you your food." — [[001-062]], maternal discipline expressed through the control of eating

> "The food is very nice for dinner for there is plenty and it is enough." — [[006-038]], Laura's letter home — reassurance that also reveals institutional adequacy as a concern

> If Mother said she was not getting proper food, that would be enough to put a stop to the whole business. — [[019-010]], food as a legitimate ground for maternal intervention

> It even got into the food. — [[019-025]], the domestic environment's saturation of even the most basic experience

## Significance

Food in the novel operates at the intersection of body and institution. The school controls what students eat; mothers worry about their children's nourishment; and the quality of food marks the line between the college's gentility and the Rambothams' provincial coarseness. Laura's experience of food is thus inseparable from her experience of class — what she eats, and how, is one more arena in which she must learn to perform a belonging she does not yet feel.
