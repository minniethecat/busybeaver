# Honesty

**Honesty** in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]] operates as a contested moral ideal, one that [[laura-rambotham]] is raised to revere but finds increasingly difficult to sustain within the social dynamics of [[ladies-college]]. The novel traces Laura's movement from the simple moral framework of her childhood toward a more complex understanding in which truth-telling, politeness, and storytelling exist in perpetual tension.

## The Moral Inheritance

Laura arrives at school with a clear, mother-instilled model of honesty. [[mother]] articulates this code in a letter of reproach:

> [006-018] That is telling an untruth and I hope you are not going to be led away by the examples of bad girls. I have always brought you children up to be straightforward and I am astonished at you beginning fibbing as soon as you get away from home. Fibbing soon leads to something worse.

This passage establishes honesty as a family value and frames Laura's school experience as a moral trial. Mother's warning — "fibbing soon leads to something worse" — hangs over the narrative, providing an ironic counterpoint to the social necessity of concealment and self-presentation that school life demands.

## The Social Cost of Truthfulness

As Laura matures, she discovers that strict honesty carries social penalties at [[ladies-college]]. The narrator observes the psychological complexity beneath the moral binary:

> [012-036] Did she now think back over the half-hour spent in Number One, and act honest Injun with herself, she had to admit that her companions' indignant and horrified aversion to the crime had not been hers, let alone their decent indifference towards the criminal.

The phrase "act honest Injun with herself" captures the novel's insistence that honesty is first and foremost an inward virtue — and a difficult one. Laura's inability to feel the "proper, ladylike" responses of her peers reveals a gap between performed rectitude and authentic feeling.

## Honesty as Currency: The M.P. Episode

The novel's most sustained treatment of honesty comes in Chapter 20, during Laura's campaign to win the approval of [[mary-pidwall]], the school prefect. Laura recognizes that M.P. judges character by a single standard:

> [020-011] Mary's friendship was extended to none but those who had a lofty moral standard; and truthfulness and honesty were naturally the head virtues on her list.

Laura adapts her strategy accordingly, learning to "say what she meant, above all, mean what she said, and stick manfully to it" (020-011). But the performance of honesty for M.P.'s benefit is itself a kind of calculation — honesty deployed as a tactical tool rather than a genuine virtue.

The philosophical center of the chapter arrives in a remarkable dormitory conversation. Laura, sitting on the edge of her bed in her nightgown, poses a genuinely subversive question: what if truth is merely a social convention?

> [020-030] "I say, M. P., if everybody told stories, and everybody knew everybody else was telling them, then truth wouldn't be any good any more at all, would it? If nobody used it?"

Cupid's response — "As if truth were a soap!" (020-032) — deflates the earnestness of the discussion while also acknowledging Laura's point: truth is being treated as a commodity, a practical tool rather than a transcendent absolute. M.P.'s appeal to the Bible as the foundation of truth (020-037) is met with Laura's tentative counterargument about Jonah and the whale (020-040), a moment that reveals Laura's intellectual inheritance from her skeptical father.

Cupid ultimately provides the pragmatic resolution:

> [020-042] "Truth has got to be — and honesty, too. If it didn't exist, there couldn't be any state, or laws, or any social life. It's one of the things that makes men different from animals."

Laura eagerly seizes on this instrumental justification: "Then truth's a useful thing. Oh, and that's probably what it means, too, when you say: Honesty is the best Policy" (020-043). Her reduction of the highest virtue to mere policy shocks M.P., who declares Laura's ideas "simply dreadful" (020-046). Yet the novel leaves the question unresolved — Cupid herself advises Laura to "keep your ideas to yourself: they're too crude for this elegant world" (020-049), a verdict that is less a moral judgment than a social one.

## Honesty and the Boundary with Politeness

The novel's most acute treatment of honesty comes when [[mother]] confronts Laura about the conflict between manners and truthfulness:

> [024-016] "Well, if a child of mine doesn't know the difference between being polite and telling stories," said Mother, completely outraged, "then, all I can say is, it's a ... a great shame!"

This moment crystallizes a central paradox: the very social codes that Laura has been taught to uphold — [[manners]], politeness, ladylike behavior — depend on a strategic departure from absolute honesty. Mother cannot countenance Laura's claim that a "good, plain, straightforward, thumping" lie (018-068) might sometimes be socially necessary. The novel does not resolve this contradiction but exposes it as a genuine moral dilemma of coming of age.

## The Inward Virtue

Despite the philosophical complexity, the novel does affirm a core of genuine honesty in Laura's character. At the end, the narrator offers this assessment:

> [022-041] far from that: she was honest and straightforward, too proud, or, it might be, too little of a dissembler to hide her real feelings.

This passage complicates the picture: Laura's honesty is not simply a virtue but also a limitation — "too little of a dissembler" — that makes her ill-suited to the social world she must navigate. Her truthfulness, like her manners, becomes another site of the fundamental tension between self and society that defines her coming of age.
