# Laura Rambotham

Laura Tweedle Rambotham is the central character of Henry Handel Richardson's "The Getting of Wisdom" — a spirited, imaginative country girl who arrives at an elite Melbourne boarding school and must navigate the fraught social territory between her rural upbringing and the polished world of colonial respectability.

## Role in the Novel

Laura is both protagonist and narrative lens: the novel follows her from childhood at home through her school years at the Ladies' College. She is introduced as the imaginative storyteller "Wondrous Fair" among her siblings, spinning fairy tales in the garden (PN [[001-001]]–[[001-006]]). Her journey to Melbourne marks a rupture — from the freedom of country life to the suffocating formality of institutional education. Throughout the novel, Laura oscillates between defiance and conformity, pride and shame, as she learns to navigate the social codes of her new world. She is present in every chapter, and her consciousness — her perceptions, mortifications, and small triumphs — drives the narrative forward.

## Character Traits

Laura is defined by several enduring traits that surface repeatedly across the novel. She is **imaginative and creative** — the "Wondrous Fair" persona of her garden storytelling reveals a gift for narrative invention, later channeled into her musical talent (PN [[001-002]]–[[001-011]]). She is **proud and defiant**, refusing to be diminished by authority or social pressure: when scolded, she answers "in a proud voice" (PN [[001-011]]), and when told the rules of her new school she reacts with rebellious silence (PN [[001-035]]). She is also **sensitive to humiliation** — the novel's central drama is Laura's struggle with the shame of being an outsider, from her homemade clothes (PN [[001-023]]–[[001-028]]) to her ignorance of social customs. Her **musical ability** becomes her one source of distinction and self-worth within the school's hierarchy.

## Key Scenes

Laura's character is revealed through a series of defining moments. **The garden storytelling (Ch. I)** establishes her as a natural fabulist and defines her relationship with her siblings — she is the leader, the creator, the one who commands attention (PN [[001-001]]–[[001-015]]). **The dress scene (Ch. I)** introduces the theme of social inadequacy: her mother makes a dress "too short" for fashion, and Laura's defiant "I don't care" masks genuine distress (PN [[001-023]]–[[001-035]]).

**Arrival at the Ladies' College (Ch. II–III)** marks the beginning of her social education — she is bewildered by the rules, the hierarchies, and the casual cruelties of her peers. **The music examination** represents her moment of triumph: her piano performance earns her rare approval from Miss Chapman and establishes her sole claim to distinction within the school's ranking system (PN [[005-063]]–[[005-064]]).

Her later **friendship with Evelyn Souttar (Ch. XXII)** reveals her capacity for intense attachment and her vulnerability to disappointment when that friendship proves unequal (PN [[022-005]]–[[022-025]]).

## Relationship to Laura

As the protagonist, Laura serves as the focal point through which all other characters are filtered. Her central relationship is with herself — the novel is fundamentally a portrait of a consciousness learning to understand its own desires, deceptions, and compromises. Her mother ("Pin" to the children) represents the world she leaves behind — loving but socially inadequate. Miss Chapman embodies institutional authority and moral judgment. Evelyn represents the allure of social acceptance — and its ultimate hollowness. Each relationship teaches Laura something about the gap between who she is and who she must appear to be.

## Significance

Laura functions thematically as a study in colonial identity formation. She is caught between multiple worlds: the British cultural inheritance embodied by the Ladies' College, the raw Australian actuality of her country origins, and the emerging colonial middle-class aspiration to respectability. Her musical gift — genuine, European, cultured — becomes the bridge that allows her to cross between these worlds, but at the cost of suppressing other aspects of herself. The novel's title is doubly ironic: what Laura acquires at school is not wisdom in any philosophical sense, but rather the hard-won understanding of social artifice — "the getting of wisdom" is a process of learning to dissemble, to conform, to survive.

> "I don't mind if you call me Laura today, as it's the last day." — [[001-015]], Laura granting permission to her siblings, revealing her self-awareness of the "Wondrous Fair" persona

> "I don't care," said Laura rebelliously, though she was on the brink of tears. — [[001-035]], the quintessential Laura response: defiance masking vulnerability

> "As long as the coach rolled down the main street Laura sat bolt upright at the window." — [[003-001]], Laura's proud arrival in Melbourne, her first step into a larger world

> "Laura began to model herself more and more on those around her; to grasp that the unpardonable sin is to be unlike other people." — [[010-001]], the painful education in conformity that defines her school years

> "just as she rode high on this wave of approbation, Laura suffered another of those drops in the esteem of her fellows." — [[022-005]], the cycle of social triumph and fall that shapes Laura's emotional life

> "Laura, walking at their side, listened to their words with the deepest interest, and with the reverence she had." — [[025-007]], the final chapter: Laura, on the verge of leaving school, absorbing the wisdom of her elders
