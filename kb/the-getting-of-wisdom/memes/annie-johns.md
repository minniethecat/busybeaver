# Annie Johns

Annie Johns is a schoolmate at the Ladies' College whose fate becomes inextricably bound with Laura's moral education. When Laura, caught up in the social dynamics of the classroom, drafts a satirical "public expulsion letter" addressed to Annie, she sets in motion a chain of events that culminates in Annie's actual expulsion and leaves Laura with a guilt that persists long after the event itself has faded from the school's collective memory.

Annie Johns is thus both a character in her own right and a symbol — of the damage that social cruelty can inflict, and of the moral reckoning that follows.

## Role in the Novel

Annie Johns enters the narrative in Chapter 12 as the target of a classroom drama. The scene is set by Miss Blount, the secretary, who "popped her head in" (PN [[012-004]]) with some piece of news that ignites the students' appetite for scandal. Laura, eager to demonstrate her verbal facility and to secure her place in the school's social world, composes a mock-legal document: "Herewith, Miss Annie Johns, I publicly expel you from the school" (PN [[012-021]]). The letter is satirical in intent — a performance of wit designed to impress Laura's peers — but its consequences are devastatingly real.

What follows is one of the novel's most compressed moral sequences. Annie is expelled; Laura is left to wonder "where Annie Johns was now, and what she was doing" (PN [[012-036]]); and in her dreams, Laura experiences a transposition of guilt: "she dreamed that not Annie Johns but she was being expelled" (PN [[012-039]]). The dream is the novel's most direct representation of moral imagination — Laura, for the first time, placing herself in another's position and feeling the weight of what she has done.

## Character Traits

Annie Johns herself is given relatively little direct characterization — she is defined primarily by what happens to her rather than by what she does. This narrative choice is itself significant: Annie is, in the novel's moral economy, more symbol than person.

Her expulsion serves as the occasion for Laura's moral awakening rather than as an event with its own independent emotional weight.

What little we know of Annie comes filtered through Laura's consciousness: she is a figure of curiosity ("burning with curiosity, from Annie Johns to Mr. Strachey," PN [[012-016]]), a target of wit, and ultimately, in Laura's retrospective imagination, a haunting presence who reappears in dreams. She is, in a sense, the novel's most important minor character — not because of who she is but because of what her treatment reveals about the society that expels her.

## Key Scenes

**The Letter (Ch. XII)**: Laura drafts the expulsion letter with all the performative flair of a student showing off: "Herewith, Miss Annie Johns, I publicly expel you from the school" (PN [[012-021]]). The language is mock-legal, the tone arch — Laura demonstrating her mastery of a certain kind of verbal performance that, in the school's culture, counts as cleverness. The gap between the letter's playful intent and its eventual consequence is the novel's most pointed critique of the moral blindness that social performance can induce.

**The Aftermath (Ch. XII)**: In the scene's aftermath, Laura "wondered where Annie Johns was now" (PN [[012-036]]). The wondering is important — it is Laura's first step toward moral imagination, the recognition that Annie has a life beyond the school's walls, a life that Laura's clever letter has disrupted. Richardson notes that Laura "understood Annie Johns' motive better than anyone else" (PN [[012-038]]) — a claim that positions Laura as someone capable of moral insight, even if that insight comes too late to prevent harm.

**The Dream (Ch. XII)**: Laura dreams "that not Annie Johns but she was being expelled" (PN [[012-039]]). The dream is the novel's most concentrated image of moral reciprocity — the self experiencing the fate it has inflicted on another. That the dream wakes Laura with a cry is evidence that her conscience, however belatedly, is functioning.

**The Echo (Ch. XVIII)**: The memory of Annie Johns resurfaces in Chapter 18, when Laura reflects on the possibility of being "stood up and expelled, as Annie Johns had been" (PN [[018-045]]). The years have not erased the event; it remains a live possibility in Laura's imagination, a template for social destruction that could, she now understands, be applied to anyone — including herself.

## Relationship to Laura

Laura's relationship with Annie Johns is almost entirely one-directional: Laura acts, Annie suffers. There is no mutuality, no exchange, no recognition between them — only the asymmetry of wit and its target, cleverness and its victim. This asymmetry is precisely the point. The Annie Johns episode is the novel's most direct treatment of what it means to use another person as material for social performance — to treat someone's life as the raw stuff of one's own wit. Laura's later guilt, her dreams, her wondering — these are not redemption but they are recognition, and in Richardson's moral universe, recognition is the necessary precondition for growth.

## Significance

Annie Johns functions thematically as the novel's moral center of gravity. Her expulsion is the event around which Laura's ethical education organizes itself — the moment when cleverness collides with consequence, when performance gives way to guilt. Through Annie, Richardson poses the novel's most difficult question: can the damage done by social cruelty ever be undone, or does moral growth consist not in undoing but in remembering?

That Annie herself remains a shadowy figure — more symbol than character — is both a narrative limitation and a thematic necessity. She must remain somewhat abstract for the weight to fall where Richardson wants it: not on Annie's experience of injustice but on Laura's experience of having committed it. In this, Annie Johns is the novel's most potent absence — the person whose reality Laura must learn to imagine, precisely because she failed to imagine it when it mattered.
