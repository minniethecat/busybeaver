# Church

**Church** attendance and religious observance form a persistent backdrop to life at the [[ladies-college]] in *The Getting of Wisdom*, structuring the weekly rhythm of institutional existence and providing a stage for the display of social conformity. The novel treats the church not primarily as a site of spiritual transformation but as an arena in which social hierarchies are reinforced, public piety is performed, and the gap between outward observance and inward belief is made visible. For [[laura-rambotham]], the rituals of churchgoing become yet another domain in which she must learn to navigate expectations that are never fully explained.

## Sunday as Institutional Rhythm

The church punctuates the school week with a regularity that mirrors the broader disciplinary structure of institutional life. Sunday brings a distinct temporal pattern: "between breakfast and church" [[008-050]] the boarders occupy an interval of supervised leisure, the service itself providing the day's central obligation. This rhythm is presented as immutable, one of the fixed points around which the fluid social world of the schoolgirls organizes itself. Church attendance is not optional but compulsory, not personal but collective — a fact that fundamentally shapes how religion is experienced by the characters.

> The boarders sat opposite the boarders in church every Sunday morning. [[012-005]]

This arrangement is telling: even in the sacred space of the church, the school's internal divisions are preserved. The boarders face each other, not the congregation at large, making the service as much an exercise in mutual surveillance as in worship. The architecture of church seating reproduces the architecture of school discipline.

## St. Stephen's-on-the-Hill

The specific congregation that serves as the school's religious home, St. Stephen's-on-the-Hill, appears as a distinct social institution with its own hierarchies and personalities. Laura and her schoolfellows "attended morning and evening service at St. Stephen's-on-the-Hill" [[016-044]], a routine that embeds them within the wider religious community of [[melbourne]] while simultaneously marking their difference as schoolgirls. The church is presided over by Mr. Shepherd, a figure whose presence in "the vestry after evening service" [[017-067]] hints at the institutional structures that organize religious life beyond the visible rituals of worship.

Richardson's depiction of church life is notable for what it does not show. There are no moments of ecstatic conversion or profound spiritual insight within the church's walls. Instead, the novel presents a religion of habit and obligation, a dutiful observance that satisfies institutional expectations without necessarily transforming the inner lives of its practitioners.

## Church as Social Obligation

One of the novel's more subversive treatments of religious observance emerges in Laura's casual admission: "I can go to another church quite well. I even think I'd rather" [[004-051]]. The statement, made in conversation with Mrs. Gurley, reveals that for Laura, church affiliation is a matter of personal preference rather than doctrinal conviction — a startling confession that nearly results in catastrophe when "had she denied religion altogether, the look Mrs. Gurley gave her" [[004-052]] indicates the gravity of such apparent impiety.

This exchange illuminates the novel's broader treatment of religion: it is not that Laura is irreligious, but that she fails to understand the performative dimension of religious identity. Church attendance is not simply about worship; it is about appearing to worship in the right way, at the right time, with the right congregation. Laura's naive suggestion that churches are interchangeable misses the point entirely, exposing her ignorance of the social codes that govern religious life.

> At any rate when in church, she was a sincerely religious little girl. [[016-002]]

Richardson is careful here to distinguish between Laura's private religiosity and her public performance. The qualifier "when in church" acknowledges that sincerity is situational, while "little girl" underscores the developmental dimension: Laura's religious feelings are sincere but immature, genuine but unformed, a distinction that the institutional church is ill-equipped to recognize or nurture.

## Religious Identity and Belonging

Laura's evolving relationship to churchgoing parallels her broader struggle for social acceptance. Church attendance marks her as a respectable member of the school community, but it also exposes her to scrutiny. The "lip-service" against which she must guard herself, being "mindful not to scrimp in lip-service, or to shirk the minutest ceremonies" [[024-033]], describes a religious practice that has become indistinguishable from the performance of social conformity. The rituals of worship have merged with the rituals of belonging.

By the novel's later stages, Laura's religious practice becomes entangled with her personal crises. Her determination to "settle down to practise religion after the glib and shallow mode" [[024-053]] suggests a yearning for authentic spiritual experience that the institutional church has failed to provide. The phrase "glib and shallow mode" is damning: it indicts not Laura but the religious culture that has reduced faith to rote observance.
