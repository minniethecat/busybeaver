# Hope

A pervasive but fragile emotional force in the novel — hope drives characters' actions and sustains them through difficulty, but it is frequently shadowed by disappointment and the gap between expectation and reality.

## In the Novel

Hope in *The Getting of Wisdom* operates on multiple levels. Mother's hopes for Laura's education and moral development form the novel's opening motivation: she hopes Laura will "be all right" at the boarding school despite her youth and sensitivity (PN=002-052). These maternal hopes are tied to social aspiration — Mother refuses to send her children to a State School "not if I have to beg the money to send them elsewhere" (PN=002-052). Laura herself harbors hopes for admiration and belonging, as seen in her careful presentation to Mrs. Gurley where she "hoped the admiration, thus subtly expressed in the form of surprise, would flatter Mrs. Gurley" (PN=004-041). The novel tracks how hope evolves from naive childhood expectations to the more complex, often disappointed hopes of adolescence.

## Key Passages

> Mother's anxious hope for Laura on the eve of her departure: "It was all she could do to keep up... I do hope she'll be all right. She seems such a child to be sending off like this. Yet what else could I do? To a State School, I've always said it, my children shall never go — not if I have to beg the money to send them elsewhere." (PN=002-052)

> Laura's hopeful strategy to please Mrs. Gurley backfires: "She hoped the admiration, thus subtly expressed in the form of surprise, would flatter Mrs. Gurley, as a kind of co-proprietor; but it was evident that it did nothing of the sort: the latter seemed to have gone deaf and dumb, and marched on up the stairs, her hands clasped at her waist, her eyes fixed ahead, like a walking stone-statue." (PN=004-041)

> A moment of relief: "Then, however, something happened which held a ray of hope." (PN=004-049)

> Laura's prayerful hope in a moment of social anxiety: "Oh, I hope she won't ask me. What shall I do if she does?" (PN=007-058)

## Characters

**Mother** is the character most defined by hope — her ambitions for Laura drive the novel's central premise. She projects hopes onto Laura that Laura struggles to fulfill. **Laura** experiences hope most acutely in its disappointment; her hopes for friendship, recognition, and belonging are frequently dashed. **Godmother** represents pragmatic hope, expressing the wish that the children "would not be kept waiting" (PN=004-019), embodying a more worldly, less idealistic form of caring.

## Historical Context

The novel's treatment of hope reflects late-Victorian and Edwardian values around social mobility through education. Mother's hope that her children avoid State Schools reflects the 1890s Australian class consciousness. The concept of "hope" for a daughter in this period was tied almost exclusively to education, moral character, and marriage prospects — public achievement being largely unavailable to women.
