# Courage

**Courage** appears in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]] not as a heroic virtue but as a desperate necessity — something characters summon in moments of social and moral crisis. The novel distinguishes between the "Dutch courage" of performance and the deeper moral courage required for honesty and self-disclosure. Through its treatment of courage, the novel advances a psychologically nuanced argument: that courage and cowardice are not opposites but often coexist within the same person, and that the social performance of boldness frequently masks inner fear.

## Courage of Despair

The novel's most striking formulation of courage comes not in triumph but in desperation. When cornered, a character turns "with the courage of despair" [005-031]. This phrase captures the paradoxical nature of courage in the school's social world: it emerges not from strength but from having nothing left to lose. The courage of despair is the courage of the trapped animal — not a moral achievement but a survival mechanism.

This formulation also suggests a critique of the school's disciplinary culture. When students are pushed to their limits, their courage is not cultivated but extracted — wrung from them by circumstances rather than developed through moral education. The novel implies that an institution genuinely concerned with character formation would foster courage grounded in conviction, not courage born of desperation.

## Dutch Courage and Social Performance

Laura's need for courage is most acute in moments of social exposure. The narrator observes:

> [018-051] …never had more need of Dutch courage than Laura in this moment.

The reference to "Dutch courage" — courage derived from alcohol — is metaphorical here, suggesting that Laura requires artificial fortification to face social judgment. The courage needed is not physical bravery but the ability to withstand shame and exposure. The term "Dutch courage" carries an implicit critique: courage that must be artificially induced is not genuine courage at all, but a temporary bypass of fear rather than its genuine overcoming.

## Moral Courage and Confession

The novel presents moral courage — the courage to confess, to tell the truth — as the most difficult form. Laura reflects that "she would never have the courage to confess" [018-040], acknowledging the gap between her knowledge of what is right and her ability to act on it. This failure of courage is not condemned but presented with empathy as a universal human limitation.

The distinction between knowing and doing is crucial to the novel's moral psychology. Laura knows she should confess; she understands the moral imperative; but knowledge alone does not generate courage. The novel thus implicitly critiques any educational philosophy that assumes moral instruction automatically produces moral behavior. Teaching students what is right does not give them the courage to do what is right — that requires something the school cannot provide through its methods of precept and punishment.

## Boldness and Social Courage

The novel uses "boldness" as a related but distinct form of social courage — the willingness to transgress social norms openly. Maria Morell is described as having "boldness of speech" [017-068], yet the narrator notes that "for all her boldness of speech she was at heart a coward" [017-068], introducing the novel's key insight about the gap between performed and genuine courage. Similarly, another character is noted to have been "often very bold of speech" [020-017], suggesting that verbal boldness functions as social currency at the school.

This boldness can be transgressive in unsettling ways: Laura is "unpleasantly impressed by the boldness of her behaviour" [009-053], showing that the school's social code treats boldness as a form of boundary violation. At the same time, social conformity suppresses courage: students fear "crushing complaints — were any bold enough to offer them" [007-002], and the narrator notes that "no one was bold enough to put an open question" [023-034] about sensitive matters. Boldness is thus both admired and policed — a precarious social stance that requires constant calibration.

The social function of boldness reveals the school's contradictory values. Boldness of speech is rewarded with social attention, yet boldness that challenges institutional authority is suppressed. The result is a student culture that valorizes a superficial version of courage — the courage to gossip, to transgress minor rules, to speak provocatively — while leaving genuine moral courage uncultivated and unrewarded.

## Encouragement and Its Limits

The novel plays on the etymological connection between courage and encouragement. Laura is "encouraged, put on her mettle" [013-017] by attention and recognition. The "machinery of invitation and encouragement" [015-021] at the school suggests that courage can be socially cultivated — but the novel remains ambivalent about whether institutional encouragement produces genuine courage or merely its performance. The word "machinery" is telling: encouragement here is not a personal gift but an institutional mechanism, something cranked into operation rather than genuinely offered.

The same machinery that encourages can also coerce. When encouragement becomes systematic, it risks becoming indistinguishable from pressure — students perform courage because they are expected to, not because they have genuinely found it within themselves. The novel's skepticism about institutional encouragement extends its broader critique of the school: even the virtues the school claims to cultivate may be merely performed rather than internalized.

## The Cowardice Beneath

The novel's most penetrating insight into courage is its exposure of the cowardice that often lies beneath bold exteriors. Maria Morell's case is paradigmatic: "for all her boldness of speech she was at heart a coward" [017-068]. This observation extends the novel's critique of social performance — what looks like courage may be no more than the absence of a challenge. The real test of courage in the novel is not bold speech but the willingness to face consequences, and on this measure, most characters fall short.

The novel's coupling of courage and cowardice within the same character resists moral simplification. There are no pure heroes or villains in its moral universe — only people who are brave in some contexts and cowardly in others. This psychological realism is one of the novel's most modern features, anticipating the twentieth century's more complex understanding of moral character. Courage, in Richardson's treatment, is not a stable trait but a situational achievement, always threatened by the cowardice that lies just beneath the surface of social performance.
