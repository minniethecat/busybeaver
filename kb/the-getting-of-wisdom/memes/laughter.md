# Laughter

**Laughter** is one of the most pervasive and analytically significant phenomena in *The Getting of Wisdom*, functioning as a primary mechanism through which social inclusion and exclusion are enacted, hierarchies are reinforced, and the boundaries of acceptable behaviour are policed. The novel tracks laughter across its full emotional range — from the warm, affiliative laughter of genuine amusement to the cruel, exclusionary laughter that marks its target as an outsider. For [[laura-rambotham]], learning to navigate the laughter of others, and to control her own vulnerability to it, constitutes a central thread of her social education at the [[ladies-college]].

## The Taxonomy of Teasing

Richardson distinguishes carefully between varieties of teasing laughter, mapping a spectrum from affectionate ribbing to sustained cruelty. When "Bertha only laughed at this, in a teasing way" [[007-042]], the qualifier "in a teasing way" signals a mode of laughter that hovers ambiguously between play and aggression. The tease occupies a liminal space in schoolgirl culture: it can serve as an invitation to intimacy or as a test that the recipient is destined to fail.

The distinction between laughing *at* and laughing *with* — a distinction that Laura herself struggles to grasp — is consistently blurred in the novel's social world. The girls' laughter is frequently mobilized as a weapon of exclusion, yet its wielders rarely acknowledge its aggressive function, preferring to frame it as harmless amusement. This denial of intent is itself a form of power: the target of the laughter is doubly injured, first by the mockery and then by the implication that her distress is itself risible.

> She laughed till she could laugh no more, and then she threw back her head and laughed anew, at the remembrance of Laura's face. [[005-015]] [[005-035]]

These two moments, separated in the text but linked in their structure, illustrate the self-perpetuating quality of cruel laughter. The laughter feeds on its own memory; the recollection of the victim's distress becomes a renewable resource of amusement. Laura's face — her visible reaction — is the catalyst, making clear that the laughter's pleasure derives not from wit but from the spectacle of another's discomfort.

## Laughter as Social Performance

The novel's early chapters establish laughter as a mode of performance that Laura fails to master. "Cousin Grace laughed boisterously" [[004-005]], and "Cousin Grace laughed the wide laugh that displayed" [[004-008]] — the descriptions emphasize the theatrical quality of this laughter, its function as a display of personality and social confidence. Laughter here is not a spontaneous response to humour but a deliberate self-presentation, a way of occupying social space assertively.

This performative dimension is equally visible in the school setting, where "everybody pushed, and talked, and laughed, in a hurry to leave the hall" [[005-009]]. Laughter here merges with physical movement and speech into a generalised display of group vitality. To participate in this collective laughter is to belong; to be excluded from it, or worse, to be its object, is to be cast out. Laura's "two little brothers laughed approval" [[001-019]] at her antics, establishing early in the narrative the pattern by which Laura's behaviour is subject to the judgment of an audience whose laughter confers or withholds acceptance.

## The Laughingstock

Laura's vulnerability to becoming an object of laughter reaches its apotheosis in her status as "the laughingstock of the school" [[023-017]]. This phrase marks the extreme end of the laughter-as-exclusion spectrum, where mockery becomes so pervasive that it attaches to the person rather than to any particular action or trait. To be a laughingstock is to have one's identity defined by the laughter of others, to become legible only as an object of amusement.

> It made her the laughingstock of the school. What schemes she tried! [[023-017]]

The exclamation that follows — "What schemes she tried!" — captures the pathos of Laura's situation. She does not passively accept her role as object of ridicule; she actively, desperately, and unsuccessfully attempts to escape it. Her schemes are futile not because they lack ingenuity but because the laughter that defines her has ceased to be a response to anything she does. It has become a habit of the group, a self-sustaining ritual in which Laura's actual behaviour is merely the occasion, not the cause.

## Nervous Laughter and Self-Protection

The novel also attends to the nervous, defensive laughter that characters deploy to manage uncomfortable situations. When "Evelyn laughed a little at this, but with an" [[022-067]] hesitation or qualification that the text signals but does not fully specify, Richardson registers the subtle graduation between confident social laughter and its more anxious variants. Laughter can be a weapon, but it can also be a shield — a way of deflecting attention, diffusing tension, or signaling that one is not to be taken too seriously.

For Laura, the challenge is compounded by her inability to distinguish these registers. She experiences all laughter directed at her as threatening, missing the moments when laughter might serve as an overture to friendship. This interpretive failure is not presented as a character flaw but as a consequence of her social position: having been so often the target of cruel laughter, Laura has learned to expect cruelty, and this expectation becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy.

> The others burst out laughing at their friend's bafflement. [[005-032]]

Here laughter is directed not at an outsider but at one of the group's own members, and its function is accordingly different. This is the laughter of the in-group, laughter that reinforces solidarity even as it momentarily singles out one member for amusement. Laura observes such exchanges from outside, unable to participate but acutely aware of the social bond they represent. The laughter of the group is a door that remains closed to her.
