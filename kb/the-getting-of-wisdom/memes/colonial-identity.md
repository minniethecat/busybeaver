# Colonial Identity: Between England and Australia

The Ladies' College stands in [[melbourne]], but its cultural orientation is toward England. Its curriculum is English. Its Church is the Church of England. Its maps are maps of England. Its standard of judgment — of what constitutes proper speech, proper manners, proper knowledge — is English. And Laura Rambotham, the scholarship girl from rural Victoria, is measured against this standard and found wanting.

This is the colonial condition as [[the-getting-of-wisdom]] depicts it: not a political relationship but a cultural one, operative at the level of daily experience rather than formal governance. The novel's Australia is a British colony in the 1890s, before Federation, and the "Mother Country" ([[007-045]]) is an absent presence whose authority is felt everywhere — in the classroom, in the chapel, in the unspoken assumptions about what counts as civilisation and who gets to count as civilised.

## The Argument

The colonial identity tension in the novel operates through three interlocking mechanisms:

1. **Cultural orientation**: The school's curriculum, religion, and social norms are all oriented toward England, treating Australia as a provincial periphery.
2. **Knowledge as colonial hierarchy**: What counts as knowledge — the geography of England, the history of Britain, the literature of the English canon — is defined by the imperial centre, and Australian experience is excluded from the category.
3. **Internalised inferiority**: Laura and her classmates have absorbed the colonial hierarchy so thoroughly that they experience their own Australianness as a deficit — something to be overcome or concealed.

## Cultural Orientation

The geography-lesson episode in Chapter VII is the novel's most concentrated treatment of colonial cultural orientation. The pupils "were required to copy the outline of the map of England" ([[007-033]]), and Laura is called upon to locate Stafford on "a huge map of England" slung over an easel ([[007-045]]). The map is "seared and scored with boundary-lines, black and bristling with names" — an image of dense, authoritative knowledge that Laura cannot access.

The irony is layered. Laura knows England imaginatively — "from hearsay, just how England looked; that she could see, in fancy, its evergreen grass, thick hedges, and spreading trees; its never-dry rivers; its hoary old cathedrals" — but her imaginative knowledge, derived from reading and hearsay, cannot be translated into the cartographic precision the classroom demands. The map of England is both familiar (as a mental image) and alien (as an instrument of examination), and Laura's failure before it crystallises the colonial subject's double relationship to the imperial centre: intimately known and structurally excluded.

The Church of England functions similarly. Mrs. Gurley's declaration — "I myself, am an Episcopalian, and I expect those gels, who belong to the Church of England, to attend it, with me" ([[004-053]]) — establishes denominational lines that are also cultural lines. The Church of England is the default, the establishment, the place where respectable people go. Laura, arriving from a religiously mixed rural background, must learn this code just as she must learn the codes of dress, speech, and deportment.

## Knowledge as Colonial Hierarchy

The novel is explicit about the colonial structure of knowledge at the school. The curriculum is imported from England; the textbooks are English; the examinations test knowledge of English geography and English history and English literature. Australian content — Australian geography, Australian history, Australian literature — is simply absent. The school educates its pupils to know a country they have never seen, while systematically ignoring the country they inhabit.

This absence is not incidental. It is structural — a feature of the colonial education system that Richardson, who was herself educated at Presbyterian Ladies' College in Melbourne before traveling to Leipzig to study music, knew from the inside. The Ladies' College is not training its pupils to be Australians; it is training them to be colonial Englishwomen, oriented toward the imperial centre and embarrassed by their own locality.

The cost of this orientation falls heaviest on Laura. She arrives at the school with rich knowledge of her own — knowledge of the Australian landscape, of her family's life in rural Victoria, of a world that is simply not represented in the school's curriculum. This knowledge is not merely devalued; it is invisible. The school has no category for it, no examination that can test it, no map on which it can be located. Laura's indigenous knowledge is not false; it is simply irrelevant, and its irrelevance is one of the school's most effective instruments of exclusion.

## Internalised Inferiority

The most insidious effect of the colonial cultural orientation is its internalisation by the colonial subjects themselves. Laura does not merely experience the English standard as external pressure; she has absorbed it as a standard of judgment she applies to herself. Her embarrassment about her clothes, her speech, her manners, her family — all of these are measured against an English ideal that she has never directly encountered but that the school has taught her to revere.

[[colonial]] documents how this internalised inferiority operates across multiple dimensions: linguistic (Laura's Australian speech is marked as provincial), sartorial (her clothes are wrong by English standards), cultural (her knowledge is local rather than metropolitan), and social (her family lacks the English gentility that the school values). Each dimension reinforces the others, creating a comprehensive experience of inadequacy that no amount of academic success could dispel.

The novel's treatment of internalised inferiority is psychologically acute. Laura does not rebel against the English standard; she tries to meet it. Her deceptions — the invented letters, the embellished stories about her family — are attempts to construct a version of herself that might pass the English test. That these attempts fail is not simply a consequence of Laura's incompetence at deception; it is a consequence of the impossibility of the task. No amount of performance can transform a colonial subject into an English one, because colonial identity is not a costume to be changed but a structural position to be inhabited.

## Melbourne as Colonial Metropolis

[[melbourne]] — "marvellous Melbourne" of the 1890s boom — occupies an ambiguous position in this colonial dynamic. It is the metropolitan centre of Australian experience, the place where Laura encounters wealth, fashion, and culture for the first time. But it is also a provincial outpost relative to London — a "colonial city" whose own claims to civilisation are derivative of the imperial centre.

The novel captures this double character of colonial Melbourne through Laura's experience of it. The city dazzles her, but it also judges her — and its judgments are calibrated according to standards imported from a place she has never seen. Melbourne is both the location of Laura's education and one of the forces from which she needs to be educated — a city that embodies the colonial condition without transcending it.

## Beyond the Binary

Richardson does not resolve the colonial identity tension. Laura does not discover a proud Australian identity; she does not reject English culture; she does not transcend the binary. The novel ends with her still suspended between the two worlds, "unclear what was to come next" ([[025-005]]).

This lack of resolution reflects the historical moment. Federation was still a few years away; the cultural nationalism of the *Bulletin* school was in its early stages; the question of what an Australian identity might look like, independent of British models, was open and contested. Richardson, writing in 1910 but setting her novel in the 1890s, captures this moment of indeterminacy — a colonial society that had not yet imagined itself as a nation, and whose cultural institutions were still oriented toward an imperial centre that its own inhabitants had never seen.

## Conclusion

The colonial identity strand of the novel is not its most visible theme, but it is one of its most structurally significant. The tension between English aspiration and Australian reality shapes the school's curriculum, the social hierarchy it enforces, and the self-understanding of every character. Laura's experience of this tension is personal — it manifests as shame, as anxiety, as the feeling of never quite belonging — but its sources are structural. The colonial condition is not something Laura can overcome through individual effort; it is something she must live within, and the novel's contribution is to make that condition visible — to show how thoroughly it shapes the world of the Ladies' College, and how little room it leaves for anyone who, like Laura, cannot quite fit.
