# Toorak

Toorak is the wealthiest and most socially prestigious suburb of Melbourne referenced in Henry Handel Richardson's "The Getting of Wisdom." It appears in the novel not as a described place but as a social signifier — the address that confers upon its resident an unquestioned authority in the school's social hierarchy.

## In the Novel

Toorak enters the novel through the character of Lucy, a younger boarder at the Ladies' College. The narrator introduces her with a direct statement of the suburb's social meaning: "In the group was a girl called Lucy, young but very saucy; for she lived at Toorak, and came of one of the best families in Melbourne" (PN 011-013). The word "for" is telling — Lucy's sauciness is excused, even enabled, by her address. She is "not as old as Laura by two years, but was already feared and respected for the fine scorn of her opinions" (PN 011-013).

Lucy's social power is demonstrated in the exchange where she mocks Lilith Gordon's claim about an uncle's promised gold watch. When Lilith reveals the uncle keeps a newspaper, "Lucy of Toorak could not recover from her amusement. 'An uncle who keeps a newspaper! A newspaper! Well, I'm glad none of my uncles are so rummy'" (PN 011-030). The repeated phrase "Lucy of Toorak" (PN 011-015) echoes the aristocratic naming convention — the place becomes part of the person's identity and authority.

## Key Passages

> In the group was a girl called Lucy, young but very saucy; for she lived at Toorak, and came of one of the best families in Melbourne. — (011-013), Toorak as a shorthand for social supremacy

> But Lucy of Toorak could not recover from her amusement. — (011-030), the name and suburb become synonymous with the character's dismissive power

## Place as Prestige: The Semiotics of Suburban Address

Richardson's use of Toorak illustrates a distinctive feature of late-Victorian Melbourne society: the encoding of social status in suburban geography. In a colonial city without an established aristocracy, where old-world titles and inherited estates were scarce, the suburb one lived in became a portable coat of arms. Toorak, by the 1890s, had consolidated its identity as Melbourne's most exclusive residential district — a meaning Richardson assumes her readers will recognize without explanation.

The novel's treatment of suburban address as social currency is notably sophisticated. Lucy does not need to describe Toorak, boast about her house, or enumerate her family's possessions. The single word "Toorak" suffices. It operates as a compressed social narrative, instantly legible to every girl in the school: here is someone whose family has arrived, whose position is established, whose scorn carries weight. The suburb name becomes a form of social magic — an utterance that transforms the speaker's standing.

## The Geography of Scorn

Lucy's Toorak address grants her what Laura, from rural Warrenega, can never possess: the license to dismiss. The phrase "Lucy of Toorak" appears at the very moment of her most cutting mockery — the repetition of place and name together binding geography to social power. Richardson's narrator uses the formulation with just enough ironic distance to signal that this conflation of person and postcode is, at some level, absurd. Yet the irony does not undo the power: Lucy's scorn is real in its effects, and her victims feel it.

For Laura, who exists at the opposite end of the novel's suburban hierarchy — from nameless rural Warrenega to Toorak, the apex — Lucy's authority is both enviable and unattainable. Laura can change her clothes, improve her accent, win a literary prize, but she cannot change her address. The novel's geography is, in this sense, its most honest sociology: some distances cannot be crossed by effort alone.
