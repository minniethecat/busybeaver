# Cousin Grace

Cousin Grace is Laura Rambotham's older cousin, "of at least twice Laura's age" [004-001], who serves as the girl's companion and escort on the journey from her country home to the Ladies' College in Melbourne. She appears only in Chapter IV — the chapter of Laura's arrival — but her brief presence establishes the emotional register of the novel's opening movement: the journey from the familiar to the unknown, mediated by someone who knows both worlds but belongs to neither.

## Role in the Novel

Cousin Grace's narrative function is that of the transitional figure. She is the bridge between Laura's country childhood and her school life, the last familiar face before the door of the College closes behind Laura. She appears on the railway platform, accompanies Laura through the streets of Melbourne, delivers her to the school, and then vanishes from the novel entirely. Her role is structurally compact but emotionally significant: she is the witness to Laura's crossing, the person who sees the old Laura for the last time before the College begins its work of transformation.

## Character Traits

Grace is defined by her laughter — a "boisterous" laugh that punctuates nearly every one of her appearances. She "laughed boisterously" [004-005] at Laura's naive questions; she "laughed the wide laugh" [004-008] that disconcerted the child; she "only laughed till it seemed" [004-029] the sound would never stop. This laughter is not cruel, but it is not entirely kind either — it is the laughter of someone who knows more than she is saying, who finds the innocence of childhood amusing from the superior vantage of adulthood.

Grace also possesses a kind of worldly knowingness that Laura lacks. She "kept up the conversation" [004-002] on the platform with adult ease, and she understands the social codes of the school world — she "made a sign with her eyes to Cousin Grace to say no more" [004-018] when Laura's godmother signals discretion. In the face of authority, however, Grace's confidence collapses: "Cousin Grace was hushed at last" [004-023], and she "ceased to giggle" [004-020] when the door of the College opens. She is, ultimately, as vulnerable to institutional intimidation as Laura herself.

## Key Scenes

**The Railway Platform (Ch. IV)**: Grace's first appearance establishes her relationship to Laura: she is the older, knowing cousin, Laura the naive child she is escorting. "Laura was not sure whether Cousin Grace spoke in jest or earnest" [004-003] — the uncertainty is characteristic of their dynamic, Grace always slightly ahead of Laura's understanding.

**The Walk to the College (Ch. IV)**: Walking "between Godmother and Cousin Grace" [004-019], Laura is physically flanked by the two adult women who will deliver her to her new life. The "asphalted path" they walk is the threshold between the familiar and the unknown. Grace continues to laugh [004-024], but as they approach the College, even her irrepressible humour begins to falter.

**At the College Door (Ch. IV)**: The moment of arrival is marked by silence — Grace "was hushed at last" [004-023], her laughter extinguished by the gravity of the institution. This is the novel's first lesson in the power of the school: it can silence even the most irrepressible voice, sober even the most determined levity.

**The Final Warning (Ch. IV)**: Grace's last words to Laura are a prediction dressed as a joke: "It'll be bread and water for you" [004-027]. The line is delivered with Grace's characteristic laughter, but it contains a kernel of truth — a warning about the privations of school life that Laura's experience will, in its own way, confirm.

## Relationship to Laura

Grace is Laura's last link to her pre-College life, but she is not a source of comfort. Her laughter, her knowingness, her adult composure — all of these serve to emphasize Laura's own smallness and uncertainty rather than to reassure her. Grace is not cruel, but she is careless; her teasing "disconcerted" [004-008] Laura without any apparent awareness of the child's distress. In this, Grace prefigures the social dynamic Laura will encounter at the College: a world of people who know more than she does, who laugh at jokes she does not understand, and who cannot be counted on to protect her from her own vulnerability.

## Significance

Cousin Grace functions thematically as the novel's first ambassador from the adult world — a world that finds childish innocence amusing, that speaks in codes Laura cannot decipher, and that ultimately delivers the child to the institution without looking back. Her laughter, which fills the chapter of Laura's arrival, is the sound of the world Laura is leaving behind: careless, confident, and utterly indifferent to the trials that await her. When Grace laughs her last laugh and disappears from the novel, Laura is left alone — and the real story begins.
