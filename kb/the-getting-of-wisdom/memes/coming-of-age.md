# Coming of Age

"The Getting of Wisdom" belongs to the tradition of the Bildungsroman — the novel of formation or education — but it revises the genre's conventions in ways that reflect both its Australian setting and its female protagonist. Laura Rambotham's coming-of-age is not a triumphant progress toward self-realization but a painful negotiation with social forces that constrain and deform the self even as they shape it.

## The Female Bildungsroman

The classic Bildungsroman — Goethe's "Wilhelm Meister," Dickens's "David Copperfield" — traces a young man's journey from provincial obscurity to mature self-understanding, typically through a combination of education, travel, romantic experience, and professional establishment. Richardson adapts this template for a female protagonist in colonial Australia, and the adaptations are revealing. Laura's sphere of action is not the world but the school; her education is not in philosophy or law but in "accomplishments" designed to secure a husband; her travels are not to London or Paris but from Warrenega to Melbourne; her coming-of-age culminates not in marriage or professional success but in the ambiguous achievement of having learned "to keep her own counsel" (PN [[025-067]]).

The genre's constraints are themselves the point: the female Bildungsroman cannot offer the expansive resolution of its male counterpart because the world it depicts offers no such resolution. Laura's wisdom, such as it is, is the wisdom of limitation — the knowledge of what cannot be achieved, of doors that are closed, of selves that must be suppressed.

## The Price of Knowledge

The novel's epigraph, from Nietzsche's "Thus Spoke Zarathustra," warns: "Whoever is to attain to wisdom must first suffer the pain of disillusion." Laura's coming-of-age is structured around a series of disillusionments: with her own social position, with the possibility of genuine friendship across class lines, with the value of the "accomplishments" the school teaches, and with the moral economy of a world that rewards performance over substance. Each disillusion is a step toward the "wisdom" of the title — a wisdom that consists not in the acquisition of positive knowledge but in the shedding of illusions.

The novel's final chapters, set after Laura's return from the Christmas holiday, mark the completion of her coming-of-age. She has learned to see the school and its inhabitants with a clarity that is indistinguishable from disenchantment. She knows who she is and who she is not; she knows what she can achieve and what will forever be beyond her. This knowledge — the "wisdom" of the title — is presented not as triumphant but as elegiac: the price of growing up is the loss of the self that could still believe in the possibility of a different world.

## Significance

Richardson's contribution to the Bildungsroman tradition is a novel that refuses the genre's consolations. Laura's coming-of-age is not a success story but a survival story, and the "wisdom" she gains is not the wisdom of self-fulfillment but the wisdom of self-protection. In this, "The Getting of Wisdom" anticipates the twentieth-century novel of female adolescence — from Jean Rhys to Elena Ferrante — that would make the constrained Bildungsroman a genre of its own.
