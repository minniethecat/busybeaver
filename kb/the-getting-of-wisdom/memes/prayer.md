# Prayer

**Prayer** functions in *The Getting of Wisdom* as a site of tension between private interiority and public performance, between authentic spiritual longing and institutionalized religious routine. The novel traces [[laura-rambotham]]'s evolving relationship with prayer — from the dutiful recitations of childhood, through the communal observances of boarding-school life, to the desperate, transactional bargains of personal crisis. Throughout, Richardson maintains a complex double vision: prayer is treated neither as mere superstition nor as uncomplicated piety, but as a genuine human response to vulnerability that is nonetheless shaped, and sometimes distorted, by the institutional forms in which it is practiced.

## The Architecture of Communal Prayer

At the [[ladies-college]], prayer structures the daily rhythm of institutional life. The boarders "assembled in the dining-hall for prayers and breakfast" [[007-002]] each morning, a ritual that combines spiritual observance with the practical organization of the school day. This conflation of the sacred and the administrative is characteristic of the novel's treatment of religion: prayer is never allowed to be purely spiritual; it is always embedded in the material and social realities of institutional existence.

The form of these communal prayers is significant. Richardson notes the "extempore prayer, and a long one" [[005-068]] that Laura endures on her first evening at the school, observing that "Laura did not hear much of it" [[005-068]]. The length and spontaneity of the prayer — the very qualities that presumably signal its sincerity — paradoxically render it inaccessible to the child who is its supposed beneficiary. The extempore prayer, designed to be more authentic than rote recitation, becomes merely a different kind of performance.

> We have prayers in the morning and the evening. [[006-038]]

This matter-of-fact statement, offered in explanation of school routine, captures the novel's understated critique of institutional religion. Prayer is presented as a schedulable activity, equivalent in status to making one's bed or preparing lessons — a habit rather than a spiritual practice. The very regularity that is supposed to cultivate devotion may instead inoculate against it.

## Public Piety and Private Doubt

The novel repeatedly contrasts the public face of prayer with what it conceals. Morning prayers at the school are attended by an audience whose thoughts are elsewhere: "fuller even than at morning prayers; for then there was always an" [[012-009]] element of distraction or inattention that the formal setting cannot overcome. The architectural arrangement of the prayer hall, like that of the [[church]], enforces a visible conformity of posture and attention while remaining powerless to compel genuine spiritual engagement.

Laura's own relationship to communal prayer is marked by a characteristic earnestness that leaves her vulnerable. Her private anxieties about her mother's health drive her to passionate private devotion, yet this authentic spiritual impulse finds no support in the school's religious routine. The disjunction between Laura's inner religious life and the external forms provided by the institution is one of the novel's subtlest and most poignant themes.

> She proffered long and glowing prayers, which vied with one another in fervour. [[024-025]]

The language here — "vied with one another" — is telling. Even in her most private spiritual moments, Laura experiences prayer as a form of competition, an internalized extension of the social rivalries that dominate her waking life at school. Prayer, which should offer respite from the performance of self, becomes merely another arena for performance.

## Prayer as Transaction

In moments of personal crisis, Laura's prayer takes on a distinctly transactional character. Her most fervent prayers are those in which she bargains with God, promising devotion in exchange for intervention. "Her part prayer to Him had been to save Mother" [[024-053]] reveals the desperation that drives this bargaining, while the phrase "her part" acknowledges the implicit deal: Laura will offer something if God will deliver something in return. This transactional spirituality is presented not as a failure of faith but as a developmentally appropriate — and deeply human — response to powerlessness.

Yet Richardson does not mock this crude theology. Instead, she traces its roots in the devotional practices that the school itself promotes, with their emphasis on petitionary prayer and their implicit promise that sincere devotion will be rewarded. When those rewards fail to materialize, Laura is left to grapple with the consequences, a spiritual crisis that the institution is ill-equipped to address.

> But Laura, knowing very well that the Lord's Prayer was the only one she had ever been taught. [[024-033]]

This moment of recognition — that Laura's religious education has given her form without content, ritual without understanding — crystallizes the novel's critique of institutional religion. She knows the words but not their meaning; she can perform devotion but cannot inhabit it. The prayer she has been taught is a shell, and her own attempts to fill it with genuine feeling have been met with indifference.

## The Solitude of Private Devotion

After the public rituals of the school day, Laura retreats to the privacy of her room: "Laura sped to her room" [[005-074]]. This movement from communal to private space maps onto a deeper psychological movement, from performed piety to authentic spiritual experience. It is alone, in the darkness of her bedroom, that Laura's most genuine prayers take place — prayers that are never witnessed, never evaluated, and never folded back into the social economy of school life.

The novel's treatment of private prayer thus offers a muted hope. If the institutional forms of religion have largely failed Laura, the impulse to prayer — the reaching outward of a frightened child toward something larger than herself — survives intact. That the novel declines to validate or invalidate the object of this reaching is one of its most artful ambiguities, leaving open the question of whether prayer is answered while affirming its psychological reality for the one who prays.
