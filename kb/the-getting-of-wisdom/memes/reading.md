# Reading

Reading in "The Getting of Wisdom" functions as a contested territory. The school prescribes and polices it — Sunday books, approved letters, Bible chapters — while Laura's own appetite for reading exceeds and subverts these constraints, becoming a marker of the imaginative vitality that distinguishes her from her more compliant classmates.

## In the Novel

Laura arrives at the Ladies' College already a reader: she "was very fond of reading" (PN [[008-051]]), and her literary knowledge — her familiarity with Scott, her ability to recite poetry — is one of the few distinctions she brings from home. The school's response to reading, however, is primarily regulatory. Sunday reading is restricted to approved "Sunday books" (PN [[008-052]]), and letters home are subject to scrutiny: Maria Moreton writes to Mother expressing shame at Laura's "nonsense" correspondence (PN [[006-015]]). The governess reads while Laura sits in solitude (PN [[005-011]]), modeling a kind of withdrawn, impersonal literacy that contrasts with Laura's eager engagement. The institutional regulation of reading — controlling what enters the mind — mirrors the school's broader project of controlling behavior.

Reading thus becomes a site of both conformity and resistance. Laura reads the Bible with competitive audacity (PN [[013-017]]); she reads letters with hungry attention; she reads the social world itself as a text to be deciphered. Her failure as a student is never a failure of intelligence but of discipline — the gap between her quick, omnivorous reading of experience and the school's demand for slow, regulated absorption.

## Key Passages

> The elderly governess left, who was reading. She, Laura, in her solitude, had been reading too — though not from a book. — [[005-011]], Laura's reading of the social world contrasted with institutional literacy

> Laura was very fond of reading, and as she dressed that morning she planned a little scheme of Sunday reading. — [[008-051]], the private imagination organizing itself around books

> It must be a Sunday book. — [[008-052]], the school's regulation of what may be read

> Maria Moreton wrote to Mother: "I am ashamed of you for writing such nonsense to her." — [[006-015]], the policing of written expression as an extension of reading control

## Historical Context

In the late-Victorian girls' school, reading was a carefully supervised activity. The "Sunday book" tradition — limiting Sabbath reading to explicitly religious or morally improving works — was widespread, reflecting anxieties about the potentially corrupting influence of fiction on young female minds. Laura's fondness for reading places her within a broader cultural shift toward female literacy, while the school's regulatory response reflects the persistent institutional fear of unsupervised imagination.
