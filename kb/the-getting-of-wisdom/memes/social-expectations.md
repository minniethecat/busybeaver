## The Invisible Curriculum

> For her new friends belonged socially to the best set in the school. — [[009-007]]

The Ladies' College teaches more than Latin and arithmetic. Beneath the formal curriculum runs an invisible curriculum of social expectations: how to dress, how to speak, when to display ability and when to conceal it, whom to befriend and whom to avoid. Laura, arriving from a modest provincial home, knows none of this. Her education in social expectations is the novel's real subject.

The phrase "the best set" recurs throughout the novel as a marker of social hierarchy. Belonging to the "best set" is not about academic achievement or moral character; it is about family wealth, social standing, and the performance of refinement. Laura's efforts to penetrate this set drive much of the plot.

## Class and Its Markers

> It was not the size of your home, or the social position you occupied in the town, that mattered. — [[015-025]]

Richardson captures the fine gradations of Melbourne's class system with sociological precision. The school contains "the daughters of poor ministers at reduced fees; and the spoilt heiresses of wealthy wool-brokers and squatters." Class is marked by everything from clothing to speech patterns to the contents of one's lunch basket.

Laura's acute awareness of class difference — her shame at Mother's embroidery work, her invention of a more distinguished family background — reflects a society in which social position is both rigidly maintained and anxiously policed. The novel's irony is that Laura's class anxiety is itself a marker of her class position: the truly secure do not fret about security.

## The Code of Feminine Modesty

> It evidenced a want of good-fellowship, and made you very glad the little prig had afterwards come to grief: if you had abilities that others had not you concealed them, instead of parading them under people's noses. — [[009-055]]

The social expectations governing gender are particularly constraining for Laura. The code demands that girls conceal intellectual ability, avoid competition, and prioritize romantic eligibility over academic achievement. When Laura offers to accompany Lilith Gordon's song at the piano, she violates this code: her ability is seen not as helpfulness but as self-promotion.

This is the novel's sharpest critique of feminine socialization: the qualities that might lead to genuine achievement — curiosity, ambition, self-confidence — are systematically punished, while the qualities of pleasingness and self-effacement are rewarded.

## The Outsider's Education

> Not that the social banishment she now suffered was new to her. — [[019-001]]

Laura experiences social expectations primarily through their violation. Each of her major crises — the faux pas with Mrs. Gurley, the piano incident, the Shepherd scandal — teaches her a rule she did not know existed. Her education in social expectations is thus an education in punishment, and the novel documents it with forensic precision.

The knowledge she gains is bitter: social expectations are arbitrary, inconsistently enforced, and designed to preserve existing hierarchies. Yet she must learn them to survive.

## Social Life as Performance

> It's one of the things you have to go through, I suppose. — [[020-042]]

By the novel's end, Laura has internalized enough of the social code to function within it, but she has not been converted. She performs the expected behaviors — church attendance, polite conversation, the appearance of modesty — while maintaining an inner distance. The "getting of wisdom" includes the knowledge that social life is a performance and that authenticity must often be sacrificed to survival.
