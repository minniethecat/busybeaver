# Sarah

Sarah is the maidservant of the Rambotham household, a figure of bustling domestic authority whose plain speech and tireless work anchor the home life that Laura departs in the novel's opening chapters.

## Role in the Novel

Sarah appears throughout the first two chapters, managing the household's practical affairs during the lead-up to Laura's departure for Ladies' College. She irons, packs, bathes the younger children, tends the garden, and mediates between Mother and the children [001-051]. As a servant, she occupies the boundary between the family's private life and the working world, and her speech patterns — colloquial, direct, sometimes sharp — introduce the novel's attention to class and vernacular [001-056].

## Character Traits

Sarah is defined by her industry and her direct manner of speaking. She is perpetually busy: "even the busy Sarah" works late into the evening [001-069]. She describes Laura as "high-stomached" — proud and unwilling to ask for things — a characterization that captures Laura's emerging personality from an outsider's perspective [001-049]. Sarah's speech is marked by colloquialisms: she calls Pin "res'vor" (reservoir) for the child's tearfulness [001-056], and she threatens to "turn the hose" on the children when they linger too long in the bath [001-068].

## Key Scenes

On the morning of Laura's departure, Sarah is found "roping a battered tin box" in the passage, preparing luggage [002-015]. She looks in on the children as they dress [002-007] and shakes her head when Pin tries to delay: "Missis'll be after you directly" [002-010]. In the garden, Sarah applies herself to tying up stray plants and drives off interruptions [001-050]. Her presence is constant, grounding the chapter in physical, practical reality.

## Relationship to Laura

Sarah's relationship to Laura is that of a household authority figure who is neither parent nor sibling. She comments on Laura's character with the freedom of a servant who has known the child from infancy: calling her "high-stomached" and scornful [001-056]. Laura, for her part, seems to accept Sarah's judgments without resentment — Sarah is simply part of the furniture of home. The relationship illuminates the class structure of the Rambotham household: Sarah is subordinate in status but holds a kind of domestic moral authority.

## Significance

Sarah embodies the working-class presence in the Rambotham household, a register of speech and attitude distinct from the middle-class aspirations represented by Mother and the school. Her language — "res'vor," "high-stomached," "awfly" — introduces the novel's attentiveness to Australian vernacular. Sarah also represents the world of practical necessity that Laura is leaving behind as she enters the more rarified social environment of Ladies' College.

## In the Text

> Sarah called her "high-stomached," to decide not to ask for something you wanted because you were too proud to own to the want. [001-049]

> "Let that be, can't you?" was Sarah's word, when a child begged for something it would be troublesome to give. [001-052]
