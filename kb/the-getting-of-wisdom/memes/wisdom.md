# Wisdom

**Wisdom** is the title concept of [[the-getting-of-wisdom]], and the novel's central question is what kind of wisdom Laura Rambotham acquires through her years at the Ladies' College. The biblical epigraph and the novel's narrative trajectory suggest that wisdom is not identical with formal education — it is something harder-won and more ambivalent.

## The Biblical Frame

The novel opens with an epigraph from Proverbs: "Wisdom is the principal thing; therefore get wisdom: and with all thy getting get understanding" (PN=P02-001). This framing establishes wisdom as a moral and spiritual pursuit, not merely an intellectual one. A later biblical quotation — "I wisdom dwell with prudence and find out knowledge of witty inventions" (PN=005-072) — reinforces the association of wisdom with prudence, suggesting that wisdom involves practical judgment as much as abstract knowledge.

## Wisdom as Institutional Claim

The school presents itself as the dispenser of wisdom. The governesses are "mercilessly secure in their own wisdom" (PN=009-001), and Laura initially accepts this claim. But the novel gradually undermines it — the wisdom of the institution proves to be narrow, rule-bound, and disconnected from the moral complexities Laura actually faces. The governesses' "wisdom" is a form of institutional authority, not genuine understanding.

## Wisdom Through Experience

Laura's actual acquisition of wisdom comes not from lessons but from experience — often painful experience. Each moral crisis, each social humiliation, each failure adds to her store of understanding. The novel's title is in this sense ironic: Laura does not "get" wisdom in the way the biblical epigraph intends. What she gets is a hard, disillusioned knowledge of how the social world works, and of her own limitations within it.

## Wisdom and Disillusionment

The novel's closing pages are explicit about the costs of wisdom. Laura leaves school "with the uncomfortable sense of being a square peg, which fitted into none of the round holes of her world; the wisdom she had got, the experience she was richer by, had, in the process, unfitted her for the life she was to lead" (PN=025-032). This is the novel's most devastating insight: wisdom, far from being an unambiguous good, can alienate you from the world you must inhabit. The "getting of wisdom" is also a loss — of innocence, of belonging, of the capacity for unreflective happiness.

## Wisdom and Others

The novel distributes wisdom unevenly across its characters. Evelyn, at eighteen, possesses "all the accumulated wisdom of eighteen" (PN=022-042) and can clarify matters about which Laura is still ignorant. Pin develops "a sturdy independence" and ceases "to look up to Laura as a prodigy of wisdom" (PN=019-033). The suggestion is that wisdom is relative — each character possesses the wisdom appropriate to their age and station, and the tragedy is that this wisdom rarely transfers between people or across social boundaries.

## Key Passages

> The biblical epigraph frames the entire novel: "Wisdom is the principal thing; therefore get wisdom: and with all thy getting get understanding" (PN=P02-001)

> The governesses embody a false wisdom: they are "mercilessly secure in their own wisdom" (PN=009-001)

> Evelyn as a figure of worldly wisdom: she possesses "all the accumulated wisdom of eighteen" (PN=022-042)

> Pin's maturation: she ceases "to look up to Laura as a prodigy of wisdom" (PN=019-033)

> The novel's devastating conclusion: "the wisdom she had got, the experience she was richer by, had, in the process, unfitted her for the life she was to lead" (PN=025-032)

## Characters

**Laura Rambotham** is the novel's wisdom-seeker, whose journey the title frames as a philosophical quest. **Evelyn Souttar** represents worldly wisdom — the easy, practical knowledge that comes from social confidence. **Pin** undergoes her own wisdom journey, developing "a sturdy independence" (PN=019-033) that parallels Laura's growth. **The governesses** represent a false wisdom — institutional authority masquerading as genuine understanding.

## Historical Context

Richardson's use of the biblical wisdom tradition reflects the novel's engagement with late-Victorian educational philosophy, which often framed schooling in moral and spiritual terms. The Proverbs epigraph places the novel in conversation with a long tradition of wisdom literature, even as the narrative undercuts the epigraph's optimistic claim. The irony — that wisdom "unfits" rather than prepares Laura for life — reflects early modernist skepticism about the Victorian faith in education as moral improvement.

## Critical Perspective

The novel's title has been the subject of extensive critical commentary. Most readings emphasize its irony: Laura "gets" wisdom, but not the kind the biblical epigraph promises. Some critics read the title as a reference to the German Bildungsroman tradition — the novel of education — which Richardson both invokes and subverts. Where the traditional Bildungsroman ends with the protagonist's successful integration into society, *The Getting of Wisdom* ends with alienation, questioning whether education can ever deliver what it promises.
