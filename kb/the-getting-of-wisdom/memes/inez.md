# Inez

Inez is a student at the Ladies' College who appears throughout the novel as Bertha Ramsey's closest friend. Described as "pretty" and "lazy," she possesses a "deep, slow voice" that marks her speech as distinctive among the schoolgirls. Her friendship with Bertha — teasing, intimate, and enduring — forms one of the novel's most stable social pairings, a relationship Laura Rambotham alternately admires, participates in, and resents.

## Role in the Novel

Inez first enters the narrative through Laura's early letter home: "in my own class their names are Inez and Bertha they are very nice" [006-026]. From this introduction, she remains a steady presence through the middle chapters of the novel, always paired with Bertha — the two names linked so closely they become almost a compound entity: "Inez and Bertha," "Bertha and Inez." Inez's role is simultaneously slight and significant: she rarely drives the plot, but her presence marks the emotional geography of the school. She is the friend Bertha turns to, the one whose "deep, slow voice" [007-040] punctuates conversations, and the object of Laura's "peculiar grudge" [009-010] — a resentment Laura herself cannot fully justify.

## Character Traits

Inez is characterized by a combination of physical attractiveness and a certain emotional indolence. The retrospective catalogue of schoolmates in Chapter 22 remembers her as "pretty, lazy Inez" [022-029] — two adjectives that capture both her advantage and her disposition. Her voice is her most distinctive feature: "deep" and "slow" [007-040], it carries a weight that contrasts with the higher, faster voices around her. Inez is also capable of a sharp observational humour: she "chimed in from the other side" [007-039] during conversations, and Laura notes that Inez "could not help giggling" [007-052] at moments of tension. Her prettiness gives her social standing, but it also makes her a target of Laura's complicated feelings — Laura who is "awfully ashamed" [007-059] of the infantile associations of childhood friendship, and who sees in Inez a rival for the kind of mature companionship she craves.

## Key Scenes

**Introduction at Evening Tea (Ch. VII)**: Laura first describes Inez to her family in a letter home, grouping her with Bertha as "very nice and not at all stuck up" [006-026]. The introduction establishes Inez within a friendship pair — she will almost never appear alone.

**The Drawing Lesson (Ch. VII)**: In a classroom scene rich with social texture, Inez delivers the line "It's all very well for you" [007-040] in her characteristic deep, slow voice. The moment captures the easy intimacy between the two friends — teasing, knowing, affectionate. Later, Inez "leaned across behind" [007-042] to draw Laura into the circle of their banter, a small gesture of inclusion that nonetheless positions Laura as an outsider being granted temporary access.

**The Verandah Conversation (Ch. VII)**: After the drawing lesson, Inez "came up to Laura's side" [007-046] on the verandah, initiating a private conversation. She "looked round to see that Bertha was not within hearing" [007-049] before speaking — a detail that reveals the small politics of friendship, the way even the closest pairs maintain secrets and side-conversations. Inez tells Laura something about Bertha that Laura had not known, a confidence that momentarily elevates Laura into the inner circle.

**The Expulsion Letter Scene (Ch. XII)**: Inez sits behind Laura and Tilly during the dramatic confrontation over Annie Johns, paired as always with Bertha [012-010]. Her presence in this scene is atmospheric rather than active — she is part of the audience to the crisis, her reaction absorbed into the collective response of the schoolgirls.

**Laura's Retrospective (Ch. XXII)**: In a passage that distills character to essence, Laura remembers "pretty, lazy Inez" alongside "perky Tilly" and "rough, teasing Bertha" [022-029]. The summary is affectionate but also reductive — Inez, in Laura's memory, becomes a type, her individuality flattened into the two adjectives that defined her.

## Relationship to Laura

Laura's relationship with Inez is complicated by feelings Laura does not fully understand. She appreciates Inez's prettiness and social ease, but she also "bore Inez a peculiar grudge" [009-006]. The grudge is revealing precisely because it is "peculiar" — Laura cannot name its source. Partly it is the intimacy Inez shares with Bertha, a friendship so easy and natural that it throws Laura's own social awkwardness into relief. Partly it is Inez's prettiness, which Laura envies without admitting it. And partly it is Inez's laziness — the way she glides through school life without apparent effort — which offends Laura's more anxious, aspirational nature.

At one point, Laura "was separated from Inez and Bertha by the width of the room" [009-015] — a physical distance that doubles as emotional metaphor. Laura is never quite inside the circle that Inez and Bertha inhabit; she can observe it, occasionally be drawn into it, but always remains at one remove.

## Significance

Inez functions in the novel as an emblem of a particular kind of schoolgirl femininity: attractive without being beautiful, lazy without being stupid, socially adept without being ambitious. She and Bertha together model a friendship based on physical proximity and shared experience rather than intellectual or emotional affinity. For Laura — who craves intensity, who wants her friendships to *mean* something — Inez is at once enviable and incomprehensible. How can someone be so effortlessly social? So unbothered? So content with a friend like Bertha and a life bounded by the school's routines?

The answer, the novel suggests, is that Inez represents a mode of being that Laura cannot access: the capacity to take school life at face value, to accept its friendships and rivalries without needing them to add up to something larger. Inez is not a protagonist of her own story — but unlike Laura, she does not need to be.
