# Mrs. Strachey

**Mrs. Strachey** is the wife of Mr. Strachey, the co-administrator of the Ladies' College alongside Mrs. Gurley. She occupies a distinctive social role within the school — neither teacher nor disciplinarian, but a gracious hostess whose drawing-room provides a rare space of cultivated gentility for selected students.

## Drawing-Room Hostess

Mrs. Strachey's primary function is hosting drawing-room evenings for a rotating batch of invited boarders. These events represent one of the few opportunities for the girls to experience a domestic social setting within the institutional confines of the College:

> "Laura, ignorant of social customs, found herself included in the batch of girls invited to Mrs. Strachey's drawing-room." (PN [[009-017]])

The drawing-room itself serves as a space where the rigid hierarchy of the school temporarily softens — though not without its own codes of behaviour that can equally confound the uninitiated.

## Social Mediation

Mrs. Strachey possesses a talent for smoothing over awkward social moments. When conversation falters among the assembled schoolgirls, she steps in:

> "Mrs. Strachey came to the rescue. 'It's so hard to get young people to talk among themselves, isn't it?'" (PN [[009-024]])

Her interventions are gentle rather than commanding — a marked contrast to Mrs. Gurley's regime of fear. Yet her quiet authority is effective; "a half turn of Mrs. Strachey's head subdued her" is all it takes to quiet an unruly girl (PN [[009-043]]).

## Musical Patronage

Music figures prominently in Mrs. Strachey's drawing-room. She presides over musical performances by the pupils, expressing the era's characteristic ambivalence about accomplished young women:

> "Mrs. Strachey looked doubtfully at the thin, sallow girl... 'Perhaps you might try,' said Mrs. Strachey, with the ingrained distrust of the amateur for the professional." (PN [[009-029]]–[[009-033]])

When a performance succeeds, her relief is palpable:

> "Mrs. Strachey looked relieved. 'Very nice indeed.'" (PN [[009-035]])

The drawing-room grand piano, on which selected students may practice, represents a particular honour: "practice on the grand piano in Mrs. Strachey's drawing-room — an honour, it was understood" (PN [[021-004]]).

## Role in School Life

Mrs. Strachey appears alongside her husband at school rituals. At the close of evening prayers:

> "The prayer at an end, Mr. and Mrs. Strachey bowed vaguely in several directions and went out." (PN [[005-069]])

She and Mr. Strachey together form the administrative couple who supplement Mrs. Gurley's governance with a more domestic, socially conventional presence. While Mrs. Gurley rules through fear and discipline, the Stracheys represent the respectable face of the institution — the world of drawing-rooms, polite conversation, and cultivated accomplishment that the College ostensibly exists to prepare its pupils for.

## Comparison with Mrs. Gurley

| Aspect | Mrs. Strachey | Mrs. Gurley |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Domain | Drawing-room | Dining hall, classrooms |
| Authority style | Gentle correction | Intimidation and fear |
| Interaction mode | Hostess to guests | Commander to subordinates |
| Atmosphere | Cultivated gentility | Rigid discipline |
| Physical presence | Unobtrusive | Imposing, majestically proportioned |
