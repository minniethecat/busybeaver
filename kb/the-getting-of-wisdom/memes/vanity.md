# Vanity

Vanity in *The Getting of Wisdom* is not merely personal pride in one's appearance — it is a social currency. At the Ladies' College, how one looks determines where one stands, and Laura Rambotham learns this lesson keenly. Yet her relationship with vanity is characteristically ambivalent: she possesses the impulse without the discipline to cultivate it into social advantage.

## In the Novel

Laura has "her share of natural vanity" (014-016), but she is "too impatient to do more than cast a perfunctory glance at her reflected self." This impatience sets her apart from girls who invest hours in cultivating their appearance as a tool of social advancement. Her vanity flares when she receives validation — when a classmate thinks her "very pretty" (011-004), "it was not in Laura's nature to remain indifferent to such a tribute." Yet she cannot sustain the effort required to convert natural gifts into social capital.

The connection between appearance and class is explicit: Laura knows that "good birth and an aristocratic appearance would not avail her, did the damaging fact leak out that Mother worked for her living" (011-036). Physical appearance can mask social origins, but only imperfectly. For Laura, prettiness is at once a potential asset and a source of danger — it draws attention that might lead to the exposure of her circumstances.

The school's approach to appearance is punitive. Laura's dress gives her figure a "square, board-like appearance" (024-043), erasing individual shape in favour of institutional conformity. The uniform is an instrument of vanity's suppression, reducing all students to the same unflattering silhouette.

Among Laura's peers, prettiness operates as an explicit currency. Inez "was so pretty that Laura never tired of looking at her" (009-006), and the physical descriptions of Evelyn Souttar and Lilith Gordon emphasise their beauty as inseparable from their social power. Even Laura's schoolmates at her first arrival whisper: "Isn't she pretty? What black eyes! What lovely curls!" (003-041) — a chorus of appraisal that establishes appearance as the first thing noticed and judged.

When Laura first appears as an author in the *Literary Society* proceedings (022-005), her vanity finds a new outlet in intellectual rather than physical display. This transformation — from seeking approval through looks to seeking it through literary achievement — marks one of the novel's quiet developments in Laura's character.

## The Ambivalence of Vanity

Laura's relationship with her own appearance is marked by contradiction. She is capable of intense self-consciousness — noting "a change that had come to pass in her appearance" (019-049) when returning home — yet she will not do the work of cultivation that her peers undertake. When her companions discuss which among them is prettiest, Tilly declares that "May's the pretty one — and has such a jolly figure" (014-042), a ranking from which Laura is notably absent. Laura's vanity is thus a source of pain without becoming a source of power.

## Characters

- **Laura Rambotham** — possesses natural vanity but lacks the patience or motivation to cultivate appearance as a social tool; her prettiness draws attention without securing her position
- **Inez** — "so pretty that Laura never tired of looking at her" (009-006); beauty as effortless social asset
- **Lilith Gordon** and **Evelyn Souttar** — represent the social elite whose appearance and bearing signal their status
- **May** — designated "the pretty one" by peer consensus (014-042)
- **Laura's mother** — her working-class status is the fact that Laura most fears being revealed by her appearance
