# Deception

Deception in *The Getting of Wisdom* takes the form of Laura Rambotham's compulsive storytelling — a survival mechanism that begins in childhood and escalates into elaborate fabrications at school, blurring the line between creative imagination and moral transgression. What starts as a child's fib becomes, at the Ladies' College, Laura's most effective tool for gaining the social acceptance she craves.

## In the Novel

Laura's relationship with truth is complicated from the start. As a child, she tells "a lie" to her sister Pin (002-037), and the pattern intensifies at the Ladies' College. Even before she reaches the school, Mrs. Gurley warns her: "I am astonished at you beginning fibbing as soon as you get away from home. Fibbing soon leads to something worse" (006-018). The governess's prophecy proves partially true — Laura's fabrications do escalate, though not into criminality but into a sophisticated social performance.

Isolated and struggling to fit in, Laura discovers that "if you imagine a thing with sufficient force, you can induce your imagining to become reality" (016-002). This insight becomes both a creative and a destructive tool. She begins telling her classmates elaborate romantic tales, and "the plunge once taken, she had no more trouble with her story" (017-064). Her audience proves "very greedy for more love-story" (018-039), and Laura, "consulting its tastes, during the week that followed, she concocted a new story" (017-067).

The moral ambiguity of Laura's fabrications is underscored by the narrator's observation that there is a "difference between merely making up a story about people, and laying hands on... what wasn't yours" (019-009). Laura's deceptions, however elaborate, remain in the realm of imagination — a distinction the novel treats with surprising sympathy. When confronted about her stories, Laura's internal response is dismissive: "they were merely a few little fibs" (020-027).

Her fabrications extend inward as well: "by no sign did she indicate what she really was — a very unhappy girl" (020-006). This is perhaps Laura's most consequential deception — the performance of contentment that conceals her isolation and misery.

## The Storytelling Cycle

Laura's deceptions follow a recurring pattern at the Ladies' College:

1. **Invention** — Laura concocts a romantic or dramatic narrative, drawing on her reading and imagination
2. **Performance** — She tells the story to her eager classmates, who "swallowed her tales uncritically" (017-065)
3. **Escalation** — Audience demand drives her to produce ever more elaborate fabrications: "she concocted a new story, even more thrilling than the first" (017-067)
4. **Exposure** — A "garbled version of the story" (018-069) eventually circulates beyond her control, threatening her reputation

This cycle reveals Laura's fundamental predicament: her storytelling is her only reliable source of social power, yet it is also the mechanism most likely to destroy the acceptance it wins.

## Characters

- **Laura Rambotham** — the central fabricator, whose storytelling is both her social currency and her emotional shield; her deceptions range from small social pretenses to elaborate romantic narratives
- **Mrs. Gurley** — the governess who warns Laura about fibbing, representing the institutional moral code Laura violates
- **Pin** — Laura's sister, to whom she tells her first recorded lie (002-037)
- **Laura's classmates** — eager consumers of her romantic fabrications, "hungry for romance" (017-065), whose appetite drives the escalation of Laura's deceptions
