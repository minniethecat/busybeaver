# Miss Blount

Miss Blount is the secretary at the Ladies' College, a member of the school's administrative staff whose single dramatic appearance — bursting into a classroom with evidence of cheating — triggers one of the novel's most significant set pieces. She is a minor character in terms of page time, but her actions catalyze the Annie Johns expulsion-letter scandal, a crisis that tests the moral character of every student involved.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Blount appears in a single chapter but her role is pivotal. During an ordinary school day, she "entered with a paper in her hand, and with extreme surprise depicted on her face" [012-004], interrupting the routine with news that will convulse the school's social order. The paper she carries is an examination that has been tampered with — evidence of cheating that will lead to the interrogation of Annie Johns and the moral crisis that follows.

Miss Blount's function in the narrative is that of the messenger: she delivers the evidence that sets the plot in motion, but the precise nature of her involvement thereafter is left deliberately unclear. She is not a teacher, not a student, but occupies the ambiguous space of the school's administrative apparatus — close enough to the machinery of discipline to be its instrument, distant enough from the students to be its face.

## Character Traits

The single detail Richardson gives us about Miss Blount's inner life is the most important one: her face shows "extreme surprise" [012-004] at what she has discovered. This is not the satisfaction of a disciplinarian catching a wrongdoer, nor the cold professionalism of a bureaucrat processing a case. It is surprise — a human reaction that suggests Miss Blount did not expect to find cheating among the students, and perhaps does not want to be the one who has found it.

Later, when the investigation reaches its climax, Miss Blount's reaction to Dr. Pughson's verdict is carefully noted. The narrative implies a tension between her institutional role — to report wrongdoing — and her human response to the consequences of that reporting. She is, in this sense, a figure of the institutional conscience: the person who must do the institution's work while feeling the human cost of that work.

## Key Scenes

**The Dramatic Entrance (Ch. XII)**: Miss Blount's single significant scene begins with her entrance into a classroom — "Miss Blount entered with a paper in her hand, and with extreme surprise depicted on her face" [012-004]. The detail of the paper — physical, held in her hand — anchors the scene in material reality. This is not abstract suspicion but concrete evidence. The surprise on her face is crucial: it tells us that what she has found is genuinely unexpected, and it establishes her as a reluctant messenger rather than an eager accuser.

**The Aftermath (Ch. XXIV)**: Miss Blount reappears briefly in the examination chapter, her presence a reminder of the earlier scandal. The narrative notes her continued involvement in the school's disciplinary machinery [024-044], but does not dramatize her response in detail. Her silence in these later scenes is itself significant — the institutional instrument receding back into the background once its dramatic function has been served.

## Relationship to Laura

Laura has no personal relationship with Miss Blount; the secretary is a figure of institutional authority, distant from the students' social world. But Laura is present in the classroom when Miss Blount makes her entrance, and the scandal that follows directly affects Laura's social circle — Annie Johns is a classmate, and the moral questions the scandal raises about loyalty, honesty, and institutional justice are questions Laura wrestles with throughout the novel. Miss Blount, in this sense, represents the institutional gaze that Laura both fears and respects: the power of the adult world to judge, to discover, to punish.

## Significance

Miss Blount embodies the institutional dimension of the school's moral order. Unlike Mrs. Gurley or Mr. Strachey, whose authority is personal and pervasive, Miss Blount's authority is procedural — she carries papers, delivers evidence, and retreats into the background. She represents the machinery of institutional judgment, the impersonal process by which wrongdoing is discovered and punished. Her "extreme surprise" humanizes this machinery without disabling it: she feels the weight of what she must do, but she does it anyway.

In a novel so deeply concerned with the moral education of its protagonist, Miss Blount serves as a reminder that institutions, too, have a moral character — composed not of rules and policies alone, but of the human beings who must enforce them.
