# Rivalry

Rivalry permeates the social world of [[ladies-college]] in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]], shaping relationships between students and driving the plot's central conflicts. For [[laura-rambotham]], rivalry operates on multiple fronts: she competes academically against her peers, vies socially for acceptance and status, and struggles internally between her natural impulses and the demands of institutional conformity. Richardson portrays rivalry not as an aberration but as a structural feature of the girls' school, embedded in its systems of [[examination]], prizes, and social hierarchy.

## Academic Rivalry and the Examination System

The apparatus of formal education institutionalizes rivalry through examinations and prizes. Laura arrives at the school already alert to academic standing. The narrator notes that her mother "laid tremendous weight on prizes and examinations, as offering the one means of getting on in the world" [[024-003]]. This maternal attitude converts academic achievement into a moral imperative, and failure into a threat to future survival.

The examination becomes the primary theater of academic rivalry. Laura participates eagerly in the system, recognizing that "ten extra marks added to an examination paper, in this easy fashion" [[015-022]] could shift the balance of achievement. The prefect system adds another competitive layer, as Laura, "as prefect of her class," is entrusted with oversight of a written examination [[020-022]], her authority dependent on continued academic performance.

As the university examination approaches, rivalry intensifies to an almost unbearable pitch:

> "little else was spoken of but the approaching examinations" [[024-020]]

The collective obsession narrows the girls' world to a single competitive horizon. Laura's anxiety is rendered in explicitly competitive terms: "she was behindhand with her work" [[023-053]]; she "had never before failed to pass an examination, having always lightly skimmed the surface" [[024-003]]. Her self-assessment is entirely relative — she measures herself against both her own past performance and the anticipated performance of her peers.

## Social Rivalry and the Clique System

Beyond the formal structures of academic competition, social rivalry governs the informal life of the school. The formation of exclusive friendship groups creates a hierarchy of belonging:

> "they made up a jealous little clique" [[013-003]]

The word "jealous" captures the emotional temperature of these social formations. Friendship at the Ladies' College is not merely about affection but about possession and exclusion. Laura, often on the outside of such cliques, experiences their power as a form of social violence.

The rivalry for social standing expresses itself most painfully in the drawing-room episode, where Laura's attempt to assist [[lilith-gordon]] at the piano is interpreted as competitive self-promotion. The resulting social punishment teaches Laura that rivalry must be conducted covertly — open competition is a violation of the school's unwritten codes. The narrator reflects:

> "from this time forward, she would rival the rest in lukewarmness" [[024-053]]

This sentence captures the paradox of social rivalry at the school: the most successful competitors are those who appear not to compete at all. Laura's education in rivalry is ultimately an education in concealment.

## Jealousy and Possessiveness in Friendship

Richardson portrays the emotional texture of rivalry through the corrosive effects of jealousy on friendship. Laura's relationship with [[lilith-gordon]] is repeatedly disrupted by possessive suspicion:

> "Laura was not only jealous, she was extraordinarily suspicious" [[023-036]]

The phrase registers the emotional intensity that rivalry generates. Laura subjects Lilith to "a rigorous cross-examination" [[023-036]], demanding to know where she has been and with whom she has spent her time. This jealous surveillance mirrors the institutional surveillance to which Laura herself is subject, suggesting that rivalry reproduces the structures of power it ostensibly resists.

The rivalry between Laura and her friend [[bertha-ramsey]] takes a different form. Laura observes that Bertha has "learned to be the latter's rival in an adherence to bald fact" [[021-002]]. Here rivalry is conducted through competing claims to truthfulness and authenticity — a battle over who can claim the moral high ground. The deterioration of Laura's manners under the pressure of rivalry is noted: "The amenity of her manners suffered, too: though she kept up appearances as best she could" [[021-002]].

## Rivalry with the Self

Richardson extends the concept of rivalry beyond interpersonal competition to encompass Laura's internal conflicts. The novel's central drama involves Laura's rivalry with her own nature — her struggle to reconcile her genuine impulses with the self she must perform to survive. The examination represents not merely competition against others but a test of self-mastery:

> "if you got happily through the public examination" [[024-035]]

The adverb "happily" carries the weight of contingency — success is never assured, and the self can betray its own ambitions. Laura prays, "only let me pass my examinations, Lord, and there is nothing I will not do" [[024-029]], bargaining with divine power in the language of competitive desperation.

## The Costs of Rivalry

The novel's treatment of rivalry is ultimately ambivalent. Academic competition gives Laura one of her few avenues for distinction and self-worth, yet it also isolates her from peers who resent her success. Social rivalry teaches her the codes of her world but at the cost of spontaneity and honesty. The narrator's observation that Laura will "rival the rest in lukewarmness" [[024-053]] is both a survival strategy and a small tragedy — the passionate, earnest girl of the early chapters has learned to hide her fire.

In the novel's closing movement, returning from the examination, Laura walks the garden paths with the knowledge that one phase of rivalry is over. But the competitive logic she has internalized will accompany her beyond the school gates, part of the ambiguous [[wisdom]] she has acquired.
