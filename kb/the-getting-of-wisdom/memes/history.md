# History

History in "The Getting of Wisdom" serves a double function. As a school subject, it is the discipline that defeats Laura — the examination she fails, the knowledge she cannot discipline herself to acquire. As a thematic concern, it represents the past that shapes her without her comprehension: the history of her family, of colonial Australia, of the British inheritance that the Ladies' College is designed to transmit.

## In the Novel

Laura's relationship with history as a subject is characterized by the gap between her interest and her discipline. She is capable of engagement — she reads Scott, she absorbs narratives — but the systematic study required for the examination eludes her. The history examination becomes the novel's climactic failure: "her mind a blank" (PN [[024-044]]), Laura confronts the consequences of her accumulated neglect. History thus becomes the subject that most clearly exposes the limits of Laura's mode of learning — quick, impressionistic, driven by interest rather than method — and the demands of institutional education.

At a deeper level, the novel itself is concerned with history: the history of the Rambotham family (the father's death, the mother's sacrifices), the history of colonial Australia (the British educational models transplanted to Melbourne), and the history Laura is herself living (the "getting of wisdom" as a historical process of identity formation). The subject Laura fails is thus also the subject the novel is about.

## Key Passages

> The history examination — Laura's mind a blank — the failure that crystallizes the novel's critique of how students are measured (PN [[024-044]])

## Historical Context

History education in the 1890s was overwhelmingly British history — kings, queens, parliaments, and wars. For Australian students, this meant learning the history of a country most would never visit, as preparation for a cultural identity that was always already secondary. Laura's failure in history can be read as a quiet rebellion against this colonial curriculum: she cannot make herself care about what the school insists she should care about.
