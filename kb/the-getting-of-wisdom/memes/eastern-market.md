# Eastern Market

The Eastern Market was one of Melbourne's principal nineteenth-century marketplaces, located on the eastern side of the central business district. In "The Getting of Wisdom," it appears as part of the urban landscape that the Ladies' College students traverse — a waypoint in the geography of colonial Melbourne's commercial and social life.

## In the Novel

The Eastern Market is referenced in connection with Collins Street [[014-026]] [[014-092]], Melbourne's premier shopping thoroughfare. For the students of the Ladies' College, the market and its surrounding streets represent the world beyond the school gates — a world of commerce, display, and social observation where the hierarchies of the school are momentarily suspended in favor of the broader hierarchies of the city.

The market appears during an outing when Laura, Tilly, and Bob — at loose ends and struggling with the social awkwardness of the afternoon — wander into the building:

> Collins Street was now as empty as a bush road. The young people went into Bourke Street, where, for want of something better to do, they entered the Eastern Market and strolled about inside. The noise that rose from the livestock, on ground floor and upper storey, was earsplitting: pigs grunted; cocks crowed, turkeys gobbled, parrots shrieked; while rough human voices echoed and reechoed under the lofty roof. There was a smell, too, an extraordinary smell, composed of all the individual smells of all these living things: of fruit and vegetables, fresh and decayed; of flowers, and butter, and grain; of meat, and fish, and strong cheeses; of sawdust sprinkled with water, and freshly wet pavements—one great complicated smell, the piquancy of which made Laura sniff like a spaniel. But after a very few minutes Tilly, whose temper was still short, called it a "vile stink" and clapped her handkerchief to her nose, and so they hurried out, past many enticing little side booths hidden in dark corners on the ground floor, such as a woman without legs, a double-headed calf, and the like. [[014-092]]

The market passage is one of the novel's most vivid set-pieces of sensory description — sound, smell, and spectacle combined to render the commercial life of colonial Melbourne in full material detail. The "enticing little side booths" [[014-092]] with their human curiosities suggest the market's function as a site of popular entertainment as well as commerce, while the hurried exit prompted by Tilly's disgust marks the class sensibilities that govern the girls' experience of urban space.

The market's presence in the novel is topographical rather than dramatic: it helps establish the texture of late-nineteenth-century Melbourne, a city whose commercial infrastructure — markets, arcades, department stores — was expanding rapidly during the period of the novel's setting. The trio then proceeds to a Waxworks Exhibition [[014-093]], continuing their drift through the city's commercial entertainments. For Laura, who comes from rural Warrenega [[003-037]], the Eastern Market and Collins Street together represent the metropolitan world she has entered — a world of abundance, anonymity, and the intoxicating possibility of being someone other than who she is.

## Historical Context

The Eastern Market was established in the 1840s and operated throughout the nineteenth century as one of Melbourne's principal fresh produce and general markets. It was located on the corner of Exhibition and Bourke Streets, in the eastern section of the city grid. By the 1890s, when "The Getting of Wisdom" is set, the market was a well-established institution, surrounded by the shops, hotels, and commercial premises that served a growing colonial city. It was demolished in the 1960s, making Richardson's reference to it — with its "lofty roof" [[014-092]] and "earsplitting" noise — a preserved trace of a lost Melbourne.

## Significance

Like Collins Street [[014-026]], the Eastern Market functions in the novel as a coordinate in the geography of urban experience — a place that is real enough to ground the fiction in historical Melbourne but that carries symbolic weight as part of the city's commercial heart. Richardson's rendering of the market's "great complicated smell" [[014-092]] is a reminder of the novel's documentary ambition: to capture not just the social hierarchies of colonial Melbourne but its material and sensory texture as well. For Richardson's contemporary readers, the mention of the Eastern Market would have evoked a specific, familiar location; for later readers, it is a reminder of the novel's documentary value as a record of colonial Melbourne's urban fabric.
