# Mr. Shepherd

Mr. Shepherd is the clergyman father of three schoolmates at the Ladies' College — Isabella, Maisie, and Olive Shepherd — whose household becomes the setting for one of the novel's most socially charged episodes: Laura's monthly-holiday visit in Chapter XVI, where her web of fabricated stories about her own family background collides with the unpretentious domesticity of a real Melbourne clergyman's home.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Shepherd appears primarily in Chapter XVI, where Laura, having spun elaborate fictions about her family's wealth and social standing ("lies Laura had spun round Mr. Shepherd, a holy man, a clergyman, and a personal friend of Mother's into the bargain," PN [[019-035]]), must spend a day in his household. His presence in the novel is structural rather than dramatic: he represents the respectable, religious, middle-class Melbourne family that Laura simultaneously envies and fabricates for herself. His "holy calling and spiritual appearance stood him in small stead" (PN [[016-025]]) against Laura's critical eye — she finds him ordinary, his sermonizing dull, his household shabby in its respectability.

The irony of Mr. Shepherd's role is that Laura has used him as the raw material for her most ambitious social fictions — claiming personal friendship between him and her mother — yet the reality of his domestic life, when she finally encounters it, is profoundly deflating. He is not the distinguished ecclesiastic of her imaginings but a man whose "voice — it went on and on — sounded, at such close quarters, both harsh and rasping" (PN [[016-039]]).

## Character

Mr. Shepherd is defined by his **vocation**: he is a clergyman first and a father second, and his household revolves around the rhythms of parish life. He "clapped on his shovel-hat and started, with long strides, for his class" (PN [[016-038]]), the broad-brimmed clerical hat marking his professional identity. His **domestic presence** is patriarchal in the Victorian mode — he presides over the household but does not manage it, leaving the anxious work of hospitality to his wife. His voice, heard through the "thin ceiling," becomes an oppressive background drone during Laura's visit, a reminder of the religious propriety she is performing rather than inhabiting.

## Key Scene: The Monthly Holiday Visit (Ch. XVI)

Laura's visit to the Shepherd household is the longest sustained domestic scene outside the Ladies' College in the novel. Mr. Shepherd's home in Prahran — "a quiet, shady street" (PN [[016-018]]) — is modest but respectable, with a "thin ceiling" through which his sermonizing voice penetrates. The visit exposes the gap between Laura's social pretensions and the modest reality of even a "good" Melbourne family: the Shepherds are pious, hospitable, and fundamentally ordinary. Mr. Shepherd himself, with his long-windedness and his clerical hat, becomes for Laura a symbol of the respectable world she has tried to enter through fiction — a world that, seen up close, proves as confining as the one she left behind.

## Significance

Mr. Shepherd serves a dual function in the novel's social taxonomy. As a clergyman, he represents the institutional authority of the Anglican Church in colonial Melbourne — the religious establishment that the Ladies' College, with its "church girls" and Sunday observances, is designed to serve. As a father, he represents the patriarchal structure of the Victorian family, where even the most anxious mother defers to her husband's calling and schedule. His home is a way-station in Laura's social education: she learns there that respectability is not glamorous, that the families she has envied are themselves constrained by money, manners, and the thin ceilings through which fathers' voices drone.
