# Cruelty

Cruelty in *The Getting of Wisdom* is rarely spectacular. It is not the cruelty of physical violence or institutional oppression but the cruelty of words, of laughter, of exclusion — the small, daily violences that compose the social life of the Ladies' College. The novel's moral intelligence lies in its recognition that these small cruelties are more damaging, and more revealing, than any grand injustice. Laura Rambotham is both victim and perpetrator of cruelty, and her education consists, in part, of learning to recognize the moral weight of what she does to others.

## The Architecture of Teasing

Teasing is the novel's primary mode of cruelty. Laura is teased about her name, her clothes, her accent, her family — every aspect of her identity that marks her as different from the wealthy boarders who set the College's social tone. The teasing takes the form of nicknames ("Laura Tweedle Rambotham"), of knowing laughter, of the exclusionary dynamics that define schoolgirl society. Richardson's rendering of this teasing is notable for its precision: she captures not only the cruelty itself but the social logic that sustains it — the way teasing functions as a bonding mechanism for the group at the expense of the individual.

The novel understands that the most effective cruelty is public. Teasing requires an audience; laughter requires witnesses. Laura's humiliation is always performed before a gallery of her peers, and the presence of that gallery is what makes the cruelty so difficult to resist. To stand up to one's tormentors would require a moral courage that Laura, at twelve, does not yet possess — and the novel does not pretend otherwise.

## Laura as Perpetrator

Laura is not merely a victim of cruelty. The Annie Johns episode — in which Laura drafts a satirical "public expulsion letter" that contributes to Annie's actual expulsion — is the novel's most concentrated treatment of Laura's capacity for cruelty. The letter is witty, performative, and devastating; it demonstrates that Laura has internalized the school's culture of verbal cruelty and can deploy it with skill. The guilt that follows, the dream in which Laura imagines herself expelled, is the novel's acknowledgment that cruelty has consequences — for the perpetrator as well as the victim.

Laura's cruelty to Annie Johns is not exceptional but representative. It is the cruelty of someone who has been taught, by the example of her peers, that wit at another's expense is a form of social currency. The novel's treatment of this episode refuses easy moralizing: Laura is not a monster but a child operating within a social system that rewards cruelty and punishes weakness. Her moral growth consists not in becoming incapable of cruelty but in learning to recognize it for what it is.

## The Moral Economy of Cruelty

Richardson's novel maps a moral economy in which cruelty circulates like currency. The powerful deploy it against the weak; the weak, when they gain temporary power, deploy it against those weaker still. Laura, positioned near the bottom of the College's social hierarchy, is a frequent recipient of cruelty; but when she finds someone lower still — Annie Johns, or the "new girl" who replaces her as the target of teasing — she participates in the cruelty with an eagerness that reveals how thoroughly she has absorbed the system's logic.

The novel's insight is that cruelty is structural rather than individual. It is not that the girls at the Ladies' College are unusually cruel — they are ordinary children in an environment that channels ordinary human aggression into particular forms. The College's enclosed world, its rigid hierarchy, its lack of adult intervention in the daily social lives of its students — these conditions create a laboratory in which cruelty flourishes. Laura's moral education consists of recognizing this structure and, however imperfectly, beginning to resist it.

## Significance

Cruelty in *The Getting of Wisdom* serves as the dark underside of the "wisdom" that Laura acquires. To become wise, in the novel's terms, is to understand not only how the social world works but what it costs — to recognize that one's own survival has been purchased at the expense of others, and to carry that knowledge as a permanent moral burden. The novel's final vision is not redemptive; Laura does not transcend cruelty or achieve moral purity. She simply knows, by the end, what cruelty is — and that knowledge, painful and incomplete as it may be, is the beginning of moral adulthood.
