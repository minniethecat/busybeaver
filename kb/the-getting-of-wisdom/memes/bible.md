# Bible

The Bible in "The Getting of Wisdom" is less a sacred text than a social instrument — a tool of institutional discipline, a marker of respectability, and, for Laura Rambotham, a stage for competitive performance. Its presence throughout the novel illuminates the gap between the school's professed religiosity and the actual texture of its spiritual life.

## In the Novel

The Bible appears in the daily routines of the Ladies' College: teachers advance "with open Bibles" (PN [[005-069]]), students memorize chapters by heart (PN [[018-014]]), and church attendance is compulsory. Laura's own relationship to the text is characteristically theatrical — "there was no more audacious Bible-reader in the class than she" (PN [[013-017]]) — and her engagement with Scripture is driven by the desire to perform rather than to understand. The Bible also serves as a physical instrument of discipline: the verger raps "impish knuckles" with it (PN [[008-053]]), and Mrs. Gurley holds it open in her hand as she declaims moral pronouncements (PN [[013-004]]). This convergence of text and violence captures the novel's ironic treatment of institutional religion: the Word is a weapon before it is a comfort.

## Key Passages

> Teachers to advance with open Bibles. — [[005-069]], the institutional ritual of Scripture

> The big girl lent her a Bible, and, since it was not a hard one, Laura got through pretty comfortably. — [[005-071]], Laura's pragmatic approach to piety

> There was no more audacious Bible-reader in the class than she. — [[013-017]], Laura's competitive relationship with Scripture

> She was in the garden, getting her Bible chapter by heart, when Maria came to find her. — [[018-014]], piety as rote discipline

## Historical Context

The Bible's prominence in the novel reflects the central role of Scripture in late-Victorian and Edwardian education. In the Australian colonial context, compulsory Bible study served dual purposes: moral formation and the reinforcement of British cultural identity. The novel's treatment of this piety is characteristic of Richardson's irony — the Bible is everywhere but genuine religious feeling is rare, and Laura's "audacious" reading is admired for its performance value rather than its spiritual depth.
