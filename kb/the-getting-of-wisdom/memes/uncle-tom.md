# Uncle Tom

Uncle Tom is a peripheral family figure in "The Getting of Wisdom," mentioned in passing as part of the Rambotham family network. His brief appearance contributes to the novel's portrait of the provincial world from which Laura departs for the Ladies' College.

## Role in the Novel

Uncle Tom's presence is minimal but telling. He represents the family connections that Laura's mother mobilizes in the effort to support her children — the network of relations and obligations that constitute provincial life. Like the other Rambotham relatives who appear fleetingly in the novel, Uncle Tom belongs to a world Laura is progressively leaving behind. His name carries a secondary resonance: the American abolitionist novel "Uncle Tom's Cabin" was widely read in the late-Victorian period, and the echo of that title adds a faint literary dimension to this minor figure.

## Key Passages

> The reference to Uncle Tom in the context of family arrangements — a name that surfaces in the network of relations Laura's mother navigates

## Significance

Uncle Tom functions as part of the novel's background texture — one of those family names that populate the opening chapters and establish the density of Laura's pre-school world. His very ordinariness is significant: he is not extraordinary enough to require development, which is precisely the point. The Ladies' College will teach Laura to leave such ordinary relations behind.
