# Colonial Society

The society depicted in "The Getting of Wisdom" is that of colonial Melbourne in the 1890s — a world suspended between English models and Australian realities, where social status is anxiously policed through dress, deportment, and the thousand small signals that distinguish the respectable from the merely aspiring.

## Melbourne's Social Hierarchy

Richardson renders colonial society with the precision of a social anatomist. At the apex stand families like the Shepherds — clerical, respectable, connected to the Anglican establishment — and below them the various strata of the commercial and professional classes who send their daughters to the Ladies' College. The school itself is a second-tier institution, and the novel never lets the reader forget this: its pupils are not the daughters of the Melbourne elite but the children of families who can afford "accomplishments" without being able to afford the social confidence that genuine wealth confers. The students themselves are characterized collectively as "these young colonials" [[009-020]], a designation that places them firmly within the colonial social order — energetic, high-spirited, but ultimately subject to the metropolitan authority represented by figures like Mr. Strachey, whose "dignified air of detachment" [[009-020]] embodies the English model against which colonial life is measured.

Laura Rambotham, coming from rural Warrenega [[003-037]] with a mother who earns her living by needlework, occupies the precarious lower boundary of this social world. Her presence at the school is made possible by her musical talent — the one accomplishment that might elevate her — but her lack of the other markers of respectability (fashionable clothes, a leisured mother, a father of any description) makes her permanently vulnerable to the kind of social anatomizing that Lilith Gordon and her ilk practice with instinctive cruelty.

## The Colonial Condition

The novel's title — taken from a Nietzsche epigraph that warns against the pain of wisdom's acquisition — gestures toward a specifically colonial coming-of-age. To grow up in colonial Melbourne is to grow up in a derivative culture: the books are English, the fashions are English, the "Home" that everyone refers to is England, and the accomplishments that the Ladies' College teaches are those that might, in a pinch, pass muster in an English drawing-room. Laura's discovery that her mother takes in sewing is devastating not only because it reveals the family's poverty but because it locates the Rambothams outside the charmed circle of colonial respectability — the circle defined by not working with one's hands. The darning of stockings becomes a source of social shame [[010-037]] precisely because it is the kind of visible, manual labor that colonial gentility exists to disavow:

> But what she would like more than anything to know was, why the mending of the stockings at home should not be Sarah's work? Why must it just be Mother—her mother alone—who made herself so disagreeably conspicuous, and not merely by darning the stockings, but, what was a still greater grievance, by not even darning them well? [[010-037]]

Laura's dress, too, marks her class position with humiliating clarity. Her mother's "homemade, picturesque style of dress" [[010-009]] — fashioned from improbable fabrics bought as bargains in Melbourne — makes Laura an outsider in a world where clothing functions as an index of social standing:

> Those were awful times when she went, say, to Melbourne, and bought as a bargain a whole roll of cloth of an impossible colour, which had to be utilised to the last inch; or when she unearthed, from an old trunk, some antiquated garment to be cut up and reshaped—a Paisley shawl, a puce ball-dress, even an old pair of green rep curtains. [[010-009]]

Richardson, who herself attended the Presbyterian Ladies' College in Melbourne before leaving for musical study in Leipzig, wrote from intimate knowledge of this world. The novel's critique of colonial society is sharper for its insider's perspective: it knows exactly how much a dress costs, what a "Sunday book" [[008-052]] signifies, and why the phrase "going Home" carries the weight of cultural cringe.

## Significance

Colonial society in the novel is both the object of satire and the source of genuine pathos. The social rituals that Laura must learn — the correct way to dress, to speak, to spend a Sunday — are absurd in their rigidity, yet the pain of failing to master them is real. Richardson's achievement is to show how a society that most of its members experience as natural and inevitable is, in fact, a fragile construction maintained through constant, anxious effort — the effort of mothers making dresses [[010-009]], of clergymen donning shovel-hats, of schoolgirls policing one another's wardrobes with the dedication of customs inspectors. The "self-conscious, emotionally shy young colonials" [[012-005]] of Class Two are the products of this anxious social machinery — shy precisely because they are never quite sure whether they measure up.
