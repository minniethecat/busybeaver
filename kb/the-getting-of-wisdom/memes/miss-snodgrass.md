# Miss Snodgrass

Miss Snodgrass is one of the assistant governesses at the Ladies' College, a member of the school's teaching staff who occupies a position in the middle tier of the institutional hierarchy — below Mrs. Gurley and Miss Chapman, above the students, and forever negotiating the precarious authority that this middle position entails.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Snodgrass appears in the background of school scenes, one of several governesses whose collective presence constitutes the institutional apparatus that governs the girls' daily lives. She is part of a staff that includes Miss Hicks, Miss Zielinski, Miss Day, and others — a corps of unmarried women whose professional identities are subsumed into the school's regime of surveillance and instruction. The novel offers little individual characterization of these governesses, treating them instead as components of the institutional machinery. This treatment is itself significant: the governesses are not individuals to the girls but functions — the function of watching, judging, correcting, and reporting. Miss Snodgrass's name, with its suggestion of severity and rectitude ("snod" being an archaic word for smooth or trim), contributes to this functional characterization.

## Character Traits

Miss Snodgrass is defined less by individual traits than by her **institutional role**. She is part of the apparatus of surveillance and instruction that defines the school's relationship to its students. Her authority is real but limited — she can correct and report but cannot make major decisions, which remain the province of Mrs. Gurley and Miss Chapman. This intermediate position, shared by all the assistant governesses, creates a particular dynamic: the governesses must enforce rules they did not create, maintain discipline through borrowed authority, and navigate the gap between the school's stated values and the practical realities of managing dozens of adolescent girls.

## Key Scenes

Miss Snodgrass appears in the collective scenes of school life — the classroom, the dining hall, the supervised evening study. Her individual actions are not singled out for narrative attention; she is part of the institutional texture, the background of adult authority against which the girls' dramas unfold.

## Relationship to Laura

Laura relates to Miss Snodgrass as she relates to most of the governesses — with the wary deference required by the school's hierarchy, combined with the private resistance that is Laura's characteristic response to authority. The governesses are not figures of intimate relationship for Laura but figures of institutional constraint — the human faces of a system that judges her and finds her wanting.

## Significance

Miss Snodgrass and her fellow assistant governesses represent the novel's attention to **institutional structure** as a subject in its own right. By populating the school with named staff members who are nevertheless denied individual characterization, Richardson creates a portrait of an institution in which individuals are subsumed into functions — a system that processes girls into young ladies through the coordinated labor of women whose own individuality has been similarly erased. The governesses are, in a sense, what the girls might become: unmarried women of uncertain social standing, dependent on the institution for their livelihood, their identities absorbed into their professional roles.

## In the Text

Miss Snodgrass is mentioned among the school's staff in the novel's early chapters. Her name appears in the roster of governesses who collectively constitute the institutional authority under which Laura and her classmates live.
