# Peter

Peter is a minor figure encountered at the Warreneen railway station in the novel's opening chapters — a yellow-haired boy whose presence at Laura's departure provides one of the novel's early comic moments and helps establish the provincial world Laura is leaving.

## Role in the Novel

Peter appears only in Chapter III, at the railway station where Laura and her mother await the train to Melbourne. He is introduced as part of a group: "'To boardin'-school? Peter! D'you hear?'" (PN [[003-009]]), an exclamation that registers the novelty of Laura's departure in the small community. Peter responds to the news with visible discomfort — "Peter, who avoided looking at Laura" (PN [[003-014]]) — and his embarrassment is amplified when he blurts out an awkward question: "'Are you going to write an essay?' said the yellow-haired Peter so suddenly that Laura started" (PN [[003-022]]). His redness deepens as the adults discuss Laura's undertaking, and "Peter, growing still redder, volunteered the remark that he had heard it was very hard" (PN [[003-023]]). Laura observes that "it was impossible for Peter to take offence" (PN [[003-025]]), and he ultimately retreats — "followed by Peter" (PN [[003-028]]) — into the background of the farewell scene.

## Character Traits

Peter is characterized by his **awkwardness** — he avoids Laura's gaze, blushes deeply, and blurts out questions whose answers embarrass him. His **yellow hair** serves as his only physical descriptor, repeated in the narrative. His **provincial curiosity** — asking about essays, about boarding-school — registers the gap between the small world of Warreneen and the larger world Laura is entering. He is, above all, a figure of the ordinary — the boy who stays behind while Laura goes forward.

## Significance

Peter's brief appearance serves a structural function: he embodies the provincial world Laura is leaving, a world of awkward boys and simple questions and small railway stations. His embarrassment at Laura's departure mirrors the larger embarrassment that Laura herself will feel at the Ladies' College — but in Peter, it is innocent and unmasked, while Laura will learn to conceal her own provincial origins behind a veneer of acquired sophistication.
