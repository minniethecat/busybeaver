# Isabella Shepherd

Miss Isabella Shepherd is a divinity student at Trinity College — "the sister of her divinity" (PN [[016-010]]) — who enters Laura's life in Chapter 16 and quickly becomes one of the novel's most warmly drawn adult figures. Unlike the school's teachers, whose authority is exercised through surveillance and judgment, Isabella offers Laura a different model of adult womanhood: religious but not severe, smiling but not superficial, capable of both admonishment and genuine warmth. She is, as Laura later recognizes, "a staunch ally" (PN [[017-067]]).

## Role in the Novel

Isabella appears in the latter half of the novel, during a phase of Laura's school life when she is beginning to look beyond the school's walls. As a divinity student at Trinity College, Isabella occupies an intermediate position — neither fully within the school's hierarchy nor entirely outside it — that gives her a unique perspective on Laura's world. She is connected to the school through her sister Olive Shepherd, but her own life at Trinity places her in a different social and intellectual context.

Her role is that of a bridge figure: between school and college, between girlhood and womanhood, between religious piety and worldly warmth. She "smiles warmly" (PN [[016-012]]), "ogles" her books, speaks in "stage-whispers" (PN [[016-019]]), and lets down her hair at night while reflecting on the day (PN [[016-049]]). These domestic, human details — the hair, the biscuits, the smile — make Isabella more accessible than the school's formal authority figures. She is an adult who has not lost the capacity for warmth.

## Character Traits

Isabella's defining quality is her smile. It "beams out" (PN [[016-050]]), it is offered "warmly" (PN [[016-012]]), it persists even in moments of mild reproach. This smile is not the empty pleasantness of social convention; it is, in Richardson's treatment, a genuine expression of Isabella's disposition — a face turned toward the world with an openness that the novel's other adults rarely permit themselves.

Yet Isabella is not merely warm. She is also capable of admonishment — "Really, Maisie!" (PN [[016-030]]) — and of the quiet exercise of authority. She teaches a Sunday school class (PN [[016-044]]), works on sermons (PN [[016-040]]), and rises in the middle of the night to find herself "dressing by the light of a single candle" (PN [[016-045]]). Her piety is genuine but not oppressive; it coexists with biscuits, stroking soles (PN [[016-048]]), and the easy intimacy of shared domestic space.

## Key Scenes

**The First Meeting (Ch. XVI)**: Laura's first encounter with Isabella establishes her immediately as a figure of warmth. Isabella "smiled warmly, and ogled the books" (PN [[016-012]]), inviting Laura into the study with a combination of intellectual curiosity and personal welcome. The scene is notable for its absence of the tension that characterizes Laura's interactions with the school's teachers — Isabella does not assess or judge; she invites.

**The Night Scene (Ch. XVI)**: In one of the novel's most intimate domestic vignettes, Laura wakes "in the middle of the night, to find Isabella dressing by the light of a single candle" (PN [[016-045]]). The image — Isabella preparing for some unnamed religious observance while Laura watches from bed — creates a sense of shared but differentiated space. Isabella's world includes mysteries (the candle, the dressing, the unspoken purpose) that Laura can observe but not yet enter. Biscuits, a man's feet being stroked (PN [[016-048]]), and Isabella letting down her hair (PN [[016-049]]) complete the scene's texture of domestic intimacy.

**The Ally Declared (Ch. XVII)**: Laura "had early turned Miss Isabella into a staunch ally of her own" (PN [[017-067]]). This sentence, brief and declarative, marks a significant development in Laura's social education: the recognition that adult allies can be cultivated, that warmth can be strategic, and that the world beyond the school contains people who might be on her side.

## Relationship to Laura

Isabella relates to Laura with a warmth that the novel's other adults rarely offer. She does not surveil, like Miss Day; she does not judge, like Mrs. Gurley; she does not demand, like the school's curriculum. Instead, she smiles, invites, and includes — making space for Laura in her world without requiring Laura to be anything other than what she is.

For Laura, Isabella represents a possible future: a version of adult womanhood that combines intellectual seriousness (the divinity studies, the sermons) with personal warmth (the smile, the biscuits, the easy intimacy). She is proof that growing up need not mean becoming like Miss Day or Mrs. Gurley — that adulthood can include laughter and late-night conversations and the smell of sermons being composed by candlelight.

## Significance

Isabella Shepherd functions thematically as the novel's most positive model of adult femininity. She is religious but not joyless; she is authoritative but not authoritarian; she is womanly but not in the constrained, performative way that the school's culture demands. Through Isabella, Richardson offers a glimpse of an alternative — a way of being female and adult that integrates intellect, faith, and warmth without sacrificing any of them. That Isabella exists outside the school's walls (at Trinity College) is itself significant: the novel suggests that the qualities she embodies may not be cultivable within the institution that has shaped Laura's school years.

## In the Text

> Isabella's smile beamed out once more. — [[016-050]], a characteristic moment in which Isabella's defining quality — her radiant warmth — is rendered as both a physical fact and a moral attribute
