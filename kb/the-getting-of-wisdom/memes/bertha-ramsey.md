# Bertha Ramsey

Bertha Ramsey is a schoolmate at the Ladies' College whose defining characteristic — a tendency to burst into tears — makes her one of the novel's most humanly drawn minor figures. She is closely associated with her chum Inez, and together they form one of the school's visible friendship pairs: Bertha the emotional one, Inez the one with the "deep, slow voice" (PN [[007-040]]). For Laura, Bertha is at different times a companion, a source of comparison, and a reminder that friendship itself can take forms she does not fully understand.

## Role in the Novel

Bertha enters the narrative in Chapter 7, when Laura first notes her at evening tea (PN [[007-034]]). She is immediately placed within a friendship dyad: "Inez and Bertha they are very nice and not at all stuck up" (PN [[006-026]]). This pairing — Bertha and Inez, Inez and Bertha — persists through the middle chapters of the novel, a stable formation in the shifting social landscape of the school.

Bertha's role in the novel's most dramatic set piece — the Annie Johns expulsion letter incident — is characteristic. As the scandal unfolds, "Bertha burst into tears and hid her face" (PN [[012-014]]), and her "purry crying went steadily on" (PN [[012-019]]) in the background of the scene's mounting tension. She is not an actor in the drama but a reactor — her tears providing an emotional soundtrack to events she cannot control. By the scene's end, "Bertha, whose tears had dried as rapidly as they had sprung" (PN [[012-026]]), has moved on, her emotional weather having passed as quickly as it arrived.

## Character Traits

Bertha's defining trait is her emotional transparency. Where other characters in the novel hide their feelings behind social masks, Bertha wears hers on the surface — crying, laughing, teasing with an immediacy that the novel presents as both endearing and slightly comic. She is "heavy of foot" (PN [[007-061]]) when winded, given to "dealing out a couple of hearty slaps" (PN [[007-060]]), and capable of being "awfully ashamed" (PN [[007-059]]) of the infantile associations of her friendship with Inez.

Richardson's physical descriptions of Bertha emphasize a kind of warm solidity. She is not delicate or refined; she is substantial, present, her emotions as physical as her body. This quality — the refusal or inability to be anything other than what she is — makes Bertha one of the novel's least guarded characters, and perhaps one of its most honest.

## Key Scenes

**Evening Tea Introduction (Ch. VII)**: Laura first encounters Bertha "at evening tea; her name was Bertha Ramsey" (PN [[007-034]]). The scene establishes Bertha through drawing — or rather, through her self-deprecating claim that she "can't draw a stroke" if anyone watches her. This small moment of vulnerability, offered without self-pity, is Bertha in miniature: aware of her limitations, open about them, untroubled by the need to pretend otherwise.

**The Drawing Lesson (Ch. VII)**: In a scene that economically sketches the friendship between Bertha and Inez, Bertha draws while Inez comments in her "deep, slow voice" (PN [[007-040]]): "It's all very well for you." The exchange — teasing, intimate, slightly competitive — captures the texture of school friendship, its small rivalries and loyalties woven together so tightly they cannot be separated.

**The Expulsion Letter (Ch. XII)**: When the Annie Johns crisis reaches its peak, Bertha's response is immediate and physical: she "burst into tears" (PN [[012-014]]). Her crying continues through the scene, a "purry" sound (PN [[012-019]]) that provides the emotional undertone to the formal drama of accusation and judgment. By the scene's end, her tears have dried "as rapidly as they had sprung" (PN [[012-026]]) — a detail that captures both the superficiality and the sincerity of Bertha's emotional life.

## Relationship to Laura

Laura's relationship with Bertha is less intense than her bonds with Tilly or her fascination with Evelyn Souttar, but it is in some ways more instructive. Through Bertha, Laura sees a mode of being that is foreign to her: unguarded, emotionally immediate, capable of crying without shame and stopping without resentment. Laura, who agonizes over every social slight and hoards her humiliations, cannot understand how Bertha can cry one moment and laugh the next. The gap between Laura's intensity and Bertha's emotional fluency is one of the novel's quiet measures of Laura's difference — and her loneliness.

## Significance

Bertha Ramsey functions thematically as a representative of a particular kind of female friendship: bodily, emotionally transparent, built on shared experience rather than shared ambition. She and Inez together model a form of intimacy that Laura can observe but not fully inhabit — a friendship based not on aspiration or emulation but on simple presence. For a novel so concerned with the codes and hierarchies of school society, Bertha offers a counter-example: a way of being social that does not depend on climbing or performing.

In the novel's later chapters, Bertha reappears in Laura's retrospective summary of her school friendships: "rough, teasing Bertha; pretty, lazy Inez; perky Tilly" (PN [[022-029]]). The adjective "rough" is not unkind — it is accurate, and in Bertha's case, it doubles as a form of affection. She is rough the way honest things are rough: unpolished, present, real.
