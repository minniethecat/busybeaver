# Social Standing

Social standing is a pervasive concern in *The Getting of Wisdom*, governing the
relationships among students at the [[ladies-college]] and reflecting the broader
class structure of colonial Melbourne.

## Status at the College

Within the school, a finely calibrated social hierarchy determines each girl's
place. Laura's new friends belong "socially to the best set in the school"
[009-007], while Laura herself, despite her eventual "acceptance socially by even
the most exclusive" [011-035], remains conscious of her precarious position. The
narrator notes that the school contains "an exceedingly mixed society" [011-035],
with the "three professions alone" providing a measure of respectability.

## The Measure of Worth

Social standing is assessed through material signs: "the size of your home, or
the social position you occupied" [015-025]. Laura, coming from a modest cottage
in [[prahran]] with a widowed mother who takes in sewing, is acutely aware of the
gap between her circumstances and those of her wealthier classmates.

## Belonging and Exclusion

The pain of social exclusion is a recurring motif. Laura suffers "social
banishment" [019-001] at the hands of her peers, experiencing firsthand the
cruelty that underpins the school's social order. The question of "who belonged to
the society" [021-001] is one that Laura must continually navigate.

## Social Standing and Character

The novel critiques the equation of social position with personal worth. Laura,
despite her modest background, possesses qualities — intelligence, imagination,
loyalty — that surpass those of many socially superior classmates. Yet the school
system consistently values the accident of birth over the substance of character.
