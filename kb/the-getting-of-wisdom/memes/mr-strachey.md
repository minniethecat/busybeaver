# Mr. Strachey

Mr. Strachey is, alongside Mrs. Gurley, one of the two principal administrators of Ladies' College. An imposing physical presence — "over six feet in his stockings" [009-020] — he manages student discipline, parental complaints, and formal social occasions with a manner that combines authority with social awkwardness.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Strachey appears throughout the novel as a figure of administrative authority. Students and parents alike bring complaints to him: Laura's mother threatens to write to him about the "tone of the College" [006-015], and parents complain to him about "want of proper supervision" [009-004]. He presides over formal events, hosts students in his rooms to examine his collections, and participates in the examination discipline process. His role is that of the public face of the institution — less intimate than Mrs. Gurley's maternal authority, but equally invested in the school's order and reputation.

## Character Traits

Mr. Strachey is physically imposing: the girls perceive him as standing "over six feet in his stockings" [009-020]. His manner is formal and deliberate: he speaks with "extreme deliberation" [009-048] and can be "unpleasantly impressed" by students who breach decorum [009-053]. Yet he also has a social awkwardness that makes him, in certain scenes, nearly sympathetic: it takes him "a minute to recover from his astonishment" when Laura makes an unexpected remark to him [009-046]. He hosts students in his rooms to show them his collections — a gesture that mingles pedagogy with social obligation [009-022].

## Key Scenes

Mr. Strachey figures prominently in the College's social ritual of inviting students to his drawing-room [009-022]. When Laura, seated next to him at such an event, makes a bold remark, he is visibly taken aback [009-046]. During the formal examination proceedings, he enters the room "in an ominous hush" and strides to his desk, an entrance that terrifies the assembled students [012-011]. Laura stares at him "much as a rabbit stares at the snake" [012-015]. In the final examination scene, he strides into the room and interrupts Dr. Pughson at his papers [024-048].

## Relationship to Laura

Laura's encounters with Mr. Strachey are marked by her characteristic blend of boldness and anxiety. At the drawing-room event, she speaks to him with a frankness that startles him [009-046], and she is mortified by his reaction: "Mr. Strachey had been so unpleasantly impressed" [009-053]. During the examination crisis, his presence fills her with dread [012-015]. Mr. Strachey thus becomes another figure before whom Laura must navigate the treacherous waters of social expectation and institutional power.

## Significance

Mr. Strachey represents the masculine, institutional face of Ladies' College authority, complementing Mrs. Gurley's more maternal governance. His awkwardness — the deliberateness of his speech, his astonishment at Laura's forwardness — humanizes the institution and suggests that the adults who wield power over the students are themselves uncertain, fallible, and bound by social codes they did not create. The novel's portrayal of Mr. Strachey is part of its broader critique of the emotional sterility of institutional education.

## In the Text
