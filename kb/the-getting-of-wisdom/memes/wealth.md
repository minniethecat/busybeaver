# Wealth

**Wealth** functions as an invisible but decisive social marker in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]], structuring the hierarchy of Ladies' College and shaping Laura Rambotham's experience as an outsider. The novel's treatment of wealth is closely tied to its depiction of [[class]] distinction in colonial Melbourne. As the narrator observes, "loud as money made itself in this young community" [011-035], wealth is both conspicuous and defining.

## Wealth and Social Hierarchy at Ladies' College

The student body at Ladies' College is stratified by family wealth. The narrator describes "the spoilt heiresses of wealthy wool-brokers and squatters" [007-002] who occupy the top of the school's social order. These girls' status is established before they arrive — "the report of her father's wealth had preceded her" [010-004] — demonstrating that wealth functions as a form of reputation that precedes the individual. A student's financial background arrives at the school before she does, shaping the social reception she will receive.

Yet the narrator also notes that Laura's sense of class injury may be misplaced. In a passage that broadens the novel's social critique beyond Laura's personal grievances, the narrator observes:

> [010-008] But the question of money was, after all, trifling, compared with the infinitely more important one of class.

This distinction between money and class is crucial: wealth alone does not determine social standing. "Class distinctions were observed as strictly as though you got away from the vastly rich" [011-035], suggesting that birth, manners, and comportment matter as much as financial resources. The phrase "infinitely more important" is deliberately hyperbolic, underscoring the narrator's insistence that class — a cultural and social formation — operates on a different order of significance than mere money.

The novel's analysis of the money-class distinction reflects the particular dynamics of colonial Australian society, where new wealth from wool and land speculation coexisted uneasily with British class hierarchies imported to the colonies. The "young community" [011-035] of Melbourne was still negotiating the relationship between financial and social standing, and the school microcosm reproduces this uncertainty.

## The Culture of Pocket-Money

The novel's attention to the small economies of school life reveals the pervasiveness of wealth as a social marker. Laura's anxiety about pocket-money begins even before she arrives at school — her mother's casual remark that "I can't give you a penny of pocket-money to take to school" [002-028] establishes financial precarity as a defining condition of Laura's school experience.

At school, the contrast with students who have "a bottomless supply of pocket-money" [010-006] is stark. The adjective "bottomless" captures the apparently infinite resources of the wealthy students from Laura's perspective — a perception that may be as important as the reality. When money goes missing — "scholars who left small sums of money in their jacket pockets" [012-008] — the theft becomes a major social crisis, "more particularly since the money had been taken" [012-016] from girls across the social spectrum.

The narrator notes that a father's wealth is measured in its practical effects: "Oh, he's got plenty of money" [011-022] is the casual marker of a family's standing, while the darker observation that "she had lots of money and he had none" [017-035] shows how financial inequality shapes even romantic prospects. Money mediates not just what students can buy but who they can become.

## Poverty and Its Knowledge

Laura's family's financial struggles give her a "discriminating acquaintance with poverty that made her feel differently" [012-036] from her wealthier classmates. The narrator describes how "trouble, poverty and night-watches had scored" [002-053] Laura's mother's face — poverty is written on the body, visible and inescapable. The triad of "trouble, poverty and night-watches" links material deprivation with emotional distress and physical exhaustion, presenting poverty as a total condition rather than a simple lack of money.

Mother's desperate resolution — "not if I have to beg the money to send them elsewhere" [002-052] — shows the sacrifices that underwrite Laura's education. The word "beg" strips away any pretense of middle-class respectability: Mother is prepared to humble herself completely for her children's futures.

The financial pressure reaches its climax when Mother struggles to pay "the school-fees for another year. The money which, by hook and by crook" [024-003] she has gathered, and when Mother confronts Laura: "your only return for all the money I've spent on you, then I wish" [024-011] — the unfinished sentence carrying the weight of disappointed investment. The fact that Mother's sentence is left grammatically incomplete is itself significant: the full consequences of Laura's failure are too painful to articulate.

## The Wealthy as Cultural Others

Laura's perspective on wealth is shaped by her own family's precarious financial position. The narrator notes that Laura's mother "had no wealth of words at her disposal" [021-028], extending the metaphor of poverty beyond the financial to the cultural realm. This linguistic poverty — the inability to articulate experience richly — is presented as a form of deprivation as real as the lack of money. The wealthy are sometimes dismissed by the girls as culturally inferior — Tilly's "wealthy relations were a 'rude, stupid set'" [015-001] — suggesting that old money and new money distinctions operate even among the students.

> [023-038] …it meant wealth, youth, beauty, and an unbounded…

This fragment captures the aspirational triad — wealth, youth, beauty — that defines the social ideal at Ladies' College and that Laura, as an outsider, can only observe from a distance. The word "unbounded" is cut off, its object unspecified, mirroring Laura's own incomplete access to the world of wealth and privilege. She can see the triad but cannot complete it — cannot fill in what "unbounded" leads to.

## Wealth and Moral Judgment

The novel does not simply equate wealth with corruption. Instead, it depicts a more nuanced social landscape: "some of the wealthiest, too, were aware" [011-035] of the social dynamics at play. Wealth brings awareness as well as privilege, and the novel's treatment of the subject resists simple moral binaries. The wealthy are not uniformly villainous, nor are the poor uniformly virtuous — the novel's moral vision is too sophisticated for such reductions.

This nuance extends to the novel's treatment of its own social world. "Loud as money made itself" [011-035] acknowledges the conspicuousness of wealth while the qualifying "as" structure suggests that money's loudness, however undeniable, is not the whole story. Other forces — class, manners, education, character — speak in different registers.

## The True Riches: Experience

The novel's epilogue introduces a significant redefinition of wealth. The narrator reflects that Laura, looking back, felt that "the experience she was richer by, had, in the process of" [025-032] her education been worth the cost. This metaphorical wealth — richness of experience — stands in contrast to the financial wealth that so dominates school life. The novel's title, [[the-getting-of-wisdom]], suggests that wisdom itself is the ultimate wealth, one that cannot be purchased with pocket-money.

This redefinition is not merely sentimental. By translating wealth from the financial to the experiential register, the novel offers Laura — and readers in comparable circumstances — a way of valuing their lives that does not depend on material resources. The wisdom she gains through suffering and social exclusion is, in the novel's final accounting, a form of wealth more durable than the pocket-money of her wealthier classmates.
