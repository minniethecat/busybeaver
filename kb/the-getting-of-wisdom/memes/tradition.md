# Tradition

Tradition in *The Getting of Wisdom* functions as both a stabilising force and a
constraint. The novel examines how inherited customs — from the "traditional
method" of school-life to the deeper "inherited instincts and traditions"
[011-035] of class and culture — shape the experience of its characters.

## School Traditions

The [[ladies-college]] is governed by unwritten traditions as much as by formal
rules. Laura's difficulty in adapting to school life stems partly from her
ignorance of these traditions — the codes of dress, speech, and behaviour that
her classmates absorb unthinkingly. The "traditional method of purchasing each
book" [005-057] and other customary practices mark the boundary between insider
and outsider.

## Inherited Traditions and Class

The narrator observes that "inherited instincts and traditions were not so easily
subdued" [011-035], noting the persistence of class-based behaviours even in the
ostensibly democratic setting of the school. Laura, coming from a modest home in
[[prahran]], finds that traditions of class and breeding are invisible barriers
she cannot easily cross.

## Tradition versus Individuality

Throughout the novel, Laura's individuality chafes against the weight of
tradition. Her imaginative storytelling, her unconventional family background, and
her reluctance to conform all place her in tension with traditional expectations.
The novel suggests that tradition, while providing structure, can also stifle the
individual spirit.

## Colonial Tradition

Set in late nineteenth-century Melbourne, the novel depicts a colonial society
negotiating its relationship with British traditions. The school imports English
models of education and social organisation, applying them — sometimes
awkwardly — to Australian conditions. This theme of inherited cultural tradition
connects to the novel's broader exploration of [[australian-identity]].
