# Servants

Domestic servants appear throughout "The Getting of Wisdom" as a silent, observing class — present at the margins of every domestic scene, essential to the functioning of both the Ladies' College and the private households the novel depicts, yet rarely granted interiority or voice. Their significance lies in what their presence reveals about the social world the novel anatomizes.

## Servants at the Ladies' College

The Ladies' College operates on the labor of servants whose work is everywhere visible but whose personhood is rendered invisible by the conventions of the institution. Mary, the maid-of-all-work, is one of the few servants granted a name and a page in the novel's economy of attention. She appears in moments of domestic intimacy — helping with wardrobes, attending to the students' physical needs — and her presence marks the boundary between the students' world of "accomplishment" and the manual labor that makes that world possible.

The students' treatment of servants is an index of character. Laura, coming from a household where her mother's needlework blurs the line between "lady" and "worker," is more attuned to servants' humanity than her classmates, for whom servants are part of the furniture of respectable life. When Laura's own mother is revealed to earn her living by sewing, the horror of her classmates registers not only the fact of paid work but its association with the servant class — the class that handles clothes but does not own them.

## Servants in the Rambotham Household

Old Anne, the Rambotham family's servant at Warrenega, represents a different model of domestic service — the rural, almost familial retainer whose long association with the family gives her a status distinct from the anonymous maids of Melbourne. Her presence in the opening chapters establishes the Rambothams' tenuous claim to gentility: they have a servant (and therefore are not servants themselves), but only just — one old woman in a weatherboard cottage, not a staff of uniformed maids. Old Anne's relationship with Laura is affectionate rather than merely functional, and her role in the household blurs the line between domestic labor and familial care.

## Servants and Class Anxiety

The presence of servants in the novel functions as a class-signaling device. The Shepherd household in Prahran, though modest by the standards of Toorak, maintains servants — a fact that Mrs. Shepherd's anxious management makes visible. The "little saucepan" she "fetched" (PN [[016-041]]) for the children's supper represents a moment where the mistress of the house performs labor that would normally fall to a servant, and her doing so signals both maternal care and the family's economic constraints. In the world of the novel, having servants is the minimum condition of respectability; doing servants' work is the mark of having fallen below it.

## Significance

Servants in "The Getting of Wisdom" function as what the critic Bruce Robbins has called "the servant's hand" — the labor that makes gentility possible but must remain invisible for gentility to maintain its aura of effortlessness. Richardson, whose own family experienced downward mobility after her father's death, writes about servants with an awareness of the fragility of the boundary between "having" and "being" a servant — an awareness that Laura, whose mother sews for money, cannot afford to forget.
