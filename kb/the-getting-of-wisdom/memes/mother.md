# Mother (Pin Rambotham)

Laura Rambotham's mother — known to her children by the affectionate diminutive "Pin" — is one of the novel's most quietly tragic figures: a widowed country woman who sends her daughter to an elite Melbourne boarding school with all the love she possesses and none of the social knowledge that love requires. She is the source of Laura's deepest shame and her most enduring attachment, and her presence haunts the novel even in the long stretches when she is physically absent.

## Role in the Novel

Mother appears most prominently in the novel's opening chapters, where she prepares Laura for her departure to school. The dress-making scene — Mother sewing Laura's school clothes herself, producing garments that are "too short" for fashion — establishes the central dynamic of their relationship within the novel's social economy: Mother's love is expressed through labor, but the products of that labor mark Laura as poor and provincial. After Laura's departure for the Ladies' College, Mother recedes into the background, reappearing through Laura's letters home (in which Laura embellishes and distorts her experiences) and through the occasional visit. Her most significant reappearance occurs through the embroidery incident — when Bob discovers Laura's embroidered handkerchief and exposes the fact that Laura's mother "takes in sewing" (PN [[014-081]]), transforming a private act of maternal care into a public marker of social shame.

## Character Traits

Mother is defined by her **love** and her **social inadequacy** — two qualities that, in the world of the novel, are tragically intertwined. She loves Laura with an intensity that the novel registers without sentimentality: her care is expressed in the dresses she sews, the letters she writes, the sacrifices she makes to afford the school fees. But her love is blind to the social codes that govern the world she is sending Laura into. She does not know that the dresses are too short, that taking in sewing is a mark of poverty rather than a respectable occupation, that the world of the Ladies' College operates according to rules she has never learned. Her ignorance is not a moral failing but a social fact, and the novel's treatment of her is marked by a complex blend of sympathy and critique: she means well and does harm, and the two are inseparable.

Her nickname — "Pin" — is both affectionate and diminutive. It suggests the closeness of the family circle and the smallness of the world she inhabits. In the context of the Ladies' College, with its elaborate hierarchies and its cultivation of "finished" young ladies, "Pin" is a name from another world entirely — the world Laura has left behind and can never fully return to.

## Key Scenes

**The Dress-Making Scene (Ch. I)**: Mother sews Laura's school clothes herself, an act of maternal love that produces garments immediately recognizable as inadequate. Laura's first taste of the shame that will define her school experience comes not from a cruel schoolmate but from her mother's loving hands — a bitter irony that sets the novel's emotional tone (PN [[001-023]]–[[001-035]]).

**The Letters**: Throughout the novel, Laura writes to her mother from school, and these letters — in which Laura embellishes her social successes and conceals her humiliations — reveal the growing distance between mother and daughter. Laura is learning to perform for her mother as she learns to perform for everyone else.

**The Embroidery Exposure (Ch. XIV)**: When Bob discovers Laura's embroidered handkerchief and the fact that her mother "takes in sewing" becomes public knowledge, Mother's occupation — the needlework by which she supports her family — is transformed into a mark of shame. The scene is devastating because it attacks not Laura directly but through her mother: the one person Laura loves without reservation becomes the source of her deepest humiliation (PN [[014-081]]).

## Relationship to Laura

Laura's relationship with her mother is the novel's emotional foundation — and its most painful study in the costs of social mobility. Laura loves her mother, but she is also ashamed of her. She writes to her faithfully but fills her letters with fabrications. She misses her but cannot bear the thought of her visiting the school, where her social inadequacy would be on display. This ambivalence — love and shame intertwined beyond the possibility of separation — is the novel's most psychologically acute portrait of the colonial class experience: the child who loves her origins and must transcend them, whose very success at the school depends on learning to hide the mother who made that success possible.

## Significance

Mother represents the **human cost of social aspiration**. The novel does not condemn her — her love for Laura is genuine, her sacrifices real — but neither does it sentimentalize her. Her social inadequacy is a fact, and its consequences for Laura are real and painful. In Mother, Richardson captures the tragedy of the colonial family: the parent who wants more for her child than she can prepare her for, the child who must learn to despise the parent she loves in order to survive in a world that despises her origins. Mother is not the villain of Laura's story, but neither is she its hero; she is its precondition, the ground from which Laura must rise and from which she can never fully separate.

## In the Text

> "The trouble her dear mother has had to get her things ready!" — [[004-033]], Mrs. Gurley's patronizing report of Mother's words, the maternal sacrifice refracted through institutional condescension

> Filled with pride in Mother's accomplishments and Lilith's appreciation of them, Laura expanded. — [[011-012]], the rare moment when maternal pride and social acceptance align

> "Laura wriggled out of the one she had on, and stood stiffly and ungraciously, with her arms held like pokers from her sides, while Mother on her knees arranged the length." — [[001-032]], the dress-making scene: maternal love expressed through labor, received as humiliation

> "Laura sat down and fell to with appetite, but also with a side-glance at the generous pile of bread and meat growing under Mother's hands." — [[002-021]], Mother's care as provider, her love shaped into the ordinary abundance of the family table

> "Be a good girl and learn well, and be a comfort to your mother." — [[004-004]], Godmother's parting words that condense the entire burden placed on Laura: to succeed, to repay, to be worthy of her mother's sacrifice
