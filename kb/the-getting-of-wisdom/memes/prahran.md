# Prahran

Prahran is a Melbourne suburb that appears in "The Getting of Wisdom" as the location of Laura Rambotham's godmother's house — a place of partial refuge from the rigors of boarding-school life, offering Laura brief respites of relative freedom and a glimpse of adult social life beyond the school walls.

## In the Novel

Prahran is introduced as the home of Laura's godmother, who hosts Laura for weekend visits. The first visit is described in Chapter IV: "Laura's godmother; she lived at Prahran, and it was at her house that Laura was to spend her Saturdays and Sundays" (PN [[004-001]]). These visits become a regular feature of Laura's school life — "from Saturday till Monday—at Prahran" (PN [[008-001]]) — and offer a contrast to the institutional discipline of the Ladies' College. At Prahran, Laura experiences a more adult social environment, dressing in her newer clothes and mingling with visitors (PN [[010-021]]). The suburb represents a threshold space between the school's total institution and the wider world of Melbourne society.

## Key Passages

> Laura's godmother; she lived at Prahran, and it was at her house that Laura was to spend her Saturdays and Sundays. — [[004-001]], introducing Prahran as a weekend destination

> The month before, she had been to spend the week-end — from Saturday till Monday — at Prahran. — [[008-001]], establishing the regularity of Laura's visits

> On the coming Saturday as well at Prahran, and in her new dress, under her godmother's roof, she might perhaps meet visitors. — [[010-021]], Prahran as a stage for Laura's social aspirations

## Significance

Prahran serves a dual function in the novel. On the narrative level, it provides Laura with a physical escape from the College's oppressive routines — a place where different rules apply and where she can glimpse the adult world she is being educated to enter. On the thematic level, Prahran represents the incomplete nature of Laura's social integration: even at her godmother's house, she is a guest rather than a member, navigating another set of social expectations that are only marginally less demanding than the school's. The suburb — then a genteel middle-class area south-east of the city — locates Laura's godmother within a specific social geography that would have been legible to Richardson's contemporary readers.

## Historical Context

Prahran in the 1890s was an established middle-class suburb of Melbourne, known for its shopping district along Chapel Street and its substantial Victorian-era housing. It was a world away from the rural Victoria of Laura's origins, but also distinct from the institutional formality of East Melbourne where the Ladies' College was situated. The weekend visits from a boarding school to a relative's suburban home were a common pattern in the lives of country girls being educated in the city.
