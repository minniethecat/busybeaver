# Trinity College

Trinity College, the University of Melbourne residential college, appears in the novel as a symbol of the world beyond the Ladies' College — a masculine domain of higher learning, coloured blazers, and boat-race rivalries that represents the intellectual and social destination toward which the school's most ambitious students aspire.

## In the Novel

Trinity College is referenced in two key contexts within the novel, each illuminating a different aspect of its symbolic function. First, it appears at the picnic scene (Ch. XIV) as part of the social panorama: "some Trinity and Ormond men with coloured blazers" mingle with the schoolgirls, their presence marking the event as a site of cross-institutional social performance (PN [[014-026]]). The college colours become a point of school loyalty: "We church girls always wear Trinity colours at the boat-race" (PN [[014-048]]), linking religious affiliation, institutional identity, and social display.

Later, after the public examination, Laura reflects that she might herself be "going on to Trinity, where she intended to take out a degree" (PN [[025-008]]) — a projection of intellectual ambition that marks her distance from the school's more conventional graduates and aligns her with the novel's most accomplished figure, M. P. (Mary Pidwall), who has already gone up to the university.

## Key Passages

> Some Trinity and Ormond men with coloured blazers had joined the group of girls. — [[014-026]], the university as social spectacle, male students as objects of feminine attention

> She was going on to Trinity, where she intended to take out a degree. — [[025-008]], Laura's projection of her own intellectual future in the novel's closing pages

## Significance

Trinity College functions in the novel as a symbol of the gendered structure of colonial education. The Ladies' College prepares its students for marriage, not for degrees; its curriculum emphasizes accomplishments (music, deportment) over scholarship. Trinity, by contrast, represents genuine intellectual life — the world of lectures, examinations worth passing, and learning pursued for its own sake. That M. P., the school's most intellectually formidable student, goes on to Trinity marks it as the destination for those rare girls who refuse to be contained by the school's limited ambitions for them. Laura's closing fantasy of following her there signals her partial escape from the school's social logic — she has learned enough to want something beyond it.

Historically, Trinity College was founded in 1872 as the first residential college of the University of Melbourne, affiliated with the Anglican Church. Its association with the social elite of colonial Victoria would have been unmistakable to Richardson's contemporary readers. The "Trinity colours" referenced in the novel — and their connection to "church girls" — reflect the actual sectarian divisions that structured Melbourne's educational landscape in the 1890s, with Trinity (Anglican) and Ormond (Presbyterian) colleges functioning as markers of denominational as well as social identity.

## Historical Context

In the 1890s, the University of Melbourne was the only university in Victoria and one of only a handful in Australia. Women had been admitted to its degree programs since 1881, but female undergraduates remained a small and socially conspicuous minority. Trinity College itself did not admit women as residents until the 1970s, meaning that Laura's fantasy of "going on to Trinity" would have meant attending as a non-resident student — an arrangement that marked her as belonging neither to the college's social life nor to the conventional feminine trajectory of her classmates. The novel's final image of Laura watching the Trinity men at the dock, with "a handsome Trinity man" laying his straw hat on her luggage (PN [[025-003]]), is ambiguous: is she entering their world, or still watching it from the outside?
