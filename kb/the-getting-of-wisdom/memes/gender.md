# Gender

**Gender** in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]] functions as both an explicit subject of critique and an implicit structural constraint on every aspect of [[laura-rambotham]]'s experience at [[ladies-college]]. The novel interrogates the intellectual and social limitations imposed on young women in late-nineteenth-century Melbourne, most memorably through the figure of Miss Hicks and her indictment of the "woman's brain."

## The "Woman's Brain" Critique

The novel's most direct engagement with gender and intellect comes through the governess Miss Hicks, who delivers a devastating assessment of a classmate that Laura cannot help but apply to herself:

> [009-012] "I'll tell you what it is, Inez," she said; "you're blessed with a real woman's brain: vague, slippery, inexact, interested only in the personal aspect of a thing. You can't concentrate your thoughts, and, worst of all, you've no curiosity — about anything that really matters."

Laura's reaction transforms this external judgment into a moment of painful self-recognition:

> [009-013] Vague, slippery, inexact, interested only in the personal — every word struck home. Had Miss Hicks set out to describe her, in particular, she could not have done it more accurately.

The narrator records Laura's resistance to the label with striking directness:

> [009-013] She did not want to have a woman's brain, thank you; not one of that sort; and she smarted for the whole class.

The final phrase — "she smarted for the whole class" — elevates Laura's private humiliation into a collective, gendered wound. Miss Hicks's verdict that "you make me ashamed to belong to the same sex" (009-012) frames intellectual deficiency not as an individual failing but as a sex-linked characteristic, an internalized judgment that Laura carries forward into every subsequent intellectual endeavor.

## The Forbidden Curriculum: Knowledge, Sex, and the Bible

The novel's portrayal of gender education — or its conspicuous absence — reaches its fullest expression in Chapter 13. The school's official curriculum is deliberately sanitized:

> [013-018] it was necessary to obtain information on matters of sex; for girls most of whom were well across the threshold of womanhood the subject had an invincible fascination.

The mechanism by which this knowledge is obtained is remarkable: the Bible itself becomes contraband. "The girls were thrown thus upon the Book of Books for their contraband knowledge, since it was the only frankly outspoken piece of literature allowed within the College walls" (013-018). The irony is sharp — the one text the school sanctions as morally unimpeachable becomes the vehicle for exactly the knowledge the school wishes to suppress.

The narrator describes the consequences of this illicit self-education with unflinching clarity:

> [013-019] Such knowledge as they possessed was a strange jumble, picked up at random: in one direction they were well primed; in another, supremely ignorant. Thus, though they received lectures on what was called "Physiology," and for these were required to commit to memory the name of every bone and artery in the body, yet all that related to a woman's special organs and chief natural function was studiously ignored.

The absurdity of a physiology curriculum that teaches every bone and artery while omitting the female reproductive system is presented without comment — the facts speak for themselves. The narrator notes that the resulting ignorance produces "fancies woven by quite big girls, for instance, round the physical feat of bringing a child into the world, would have supplied material for a volume of fairytales" (013-019).

The moral consequence of this clandestine knowledge is a warped perspective:

> [013-021] This dabbling in the illicit — it had little in common with the opener grime of the ordinary schoolboy — did not even widen the outlook of these girls. For it was something to hush up and keep hidden away, to have qualms, even among themselves, about knowing; and, like all knowledge that fungus-like shrinks from the sun, it was stunted and unlovely.

The metaphor of fungus — knowledge that grows only in darkness, becoming "stunted and unlovely" — is one of the novel's most powerful images of how institutional silence about female sexuality produces not innocence but distortion.

## Woman as Prey

The culmination of this distorted education is a vision of heterosexual relations rendered in stark, archetypal terms:

> [013-022] For out of it all rose the vague, crude picture of woman as the prey of man. Man was animal, a composite of lust and cruelty, with no aim but that of brutally taking his pleasure: something monstrous, yet to be adored; annihilating, yet to be sought after; something to flee and, at the same time, to entice, with every art at one's disposal.

This passage captures the impossible double bind of emerging female sexuality: the imperative to simultaneously flee and entice, to fear and to worship. The "vague, crude" quality of this picture underscores how little formal education or adult guidance the girls receive about their own sexual development.

Laura initially participates in this culture of clandestine knowledge with enthusiasm. The narrator notes that "always a very inquisitive little girl, she was now agog to learn new lore" (013-023) and that "her mind, in this direction, was like a clean but highly sensitised plate" (013-023). Yet a distinction soon emerges:

> [013-024] But that was all; a step farther, and she ran her head against a stone wall. For the invisible yeast that brought this ferment of natural curiosity to pass, was the girls' intense interest in the opposite sex: a penned-up interest that clamoured for an outlet; an interest which, in the life of these prospective mothers, had already usurped the main place.

Laura, who "had so far had scant experience of boys of a desirable age, nor any liking for such as she had known" (013-024), remains outside this current. She absorbs the forbidden knowledge eagerly but lacks the erotic motivation that drives her peers — a difference that marks her as both intellectually precocious and developmentally behind, and which sets up the novel's later treatment of her relationship with the opposite sex.

## The Ladylike Imperative

The gender expectations placed on Laura are inseparable from the codes of [[manners]] and [[class]]. [[mother]] articulates the connection directly:

> [006-033] I do want you to remember that you are a lady though you are poor and must behave in a ladylike way.

The conjunction of "poor" and "lady" reveals the class anxiety at the heart of gender performance. To be "ladylike" is to perform a class position that the Rambotham family's economic circumstances cannot naturally sustain. The novel suggests that gender discipline, for a girl of Laura's station, is indistinguishable from the labor of class aspiration. Laura is told by Mother that she was once a "little woman" (002-011), expected to grow up quickly; the school's gender order presses her to be simultaneously sexually ignorant and socially sophisticated — another impossible double bind.

The novel's treatment of gender culminates not in resolution but in exposure: the system produces girls who know the names of every bone in the human body but nothing of their own reproductive function, who are trained to be "ladylike" while absorbing a vision of men as brutal predators, and who are told to be both intellectually curious (to succeed at their studies) and intellectually limited (to remain "womanly"). Laura's inability to reconcile these contradictions is not presented as a personal failure but as an indictment of the system itself.
