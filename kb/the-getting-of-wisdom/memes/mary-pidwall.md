# Mary Pidwall

Mary Pidwall is a senior student at Ladies' College, sixteen years old at the time of the novel's events. She is intelligent, morally serious, and exerts a powerful influence over younger students — particularly Laura Rambotham, who both admires and fears her.

## Role in the Novel

Mary Pidwall appears in the later chapters of the novel as a figure of scholastic and moral authority among the students. She is one of the oldest girls at the College, and her impending visit to Laura's room becomes a source of intense anxiety for Laura across an entire week [018-039]. Mary's role is that of the respected elder: she knows Laura's secrets, judges her conduct, and represents the standard of behaviour Laura aspires to but cannot consistently meet.

## Character Traits

Mary is characterized by a quiet authority that does not require assertion. The narrative describes her as "one of those people" who inspire a peculiar dread — not through cruelty, but through the weight of their moral expectation [018-026]. Laura "hardly dared to lift her eyes in Mary Pidwall's presence" [020-009]. Mary is also studious: she and Cupid are seen studying together, suggesting she belongs to the serious, academically inclined set at the College [023-006].

## Key Scenes

Mary's most significant appearance is her visit to Laura, an event that dominates Laura's consciousness for an entire week beforehand [018-039]. The visit itself is not dramatized in detail, but its effect on Laura is profound: Mary knows something about Laura that Laura wishes kept private [020-009]. In a later scene, Laura repeats something she had said about Mary Pidwall, suggesting a breach of confidence that troubles her [022-041]. Mary also appears in the examination period alongside Cupid, absorbed in study [023-006].

## Relationship to Laura

Laura's relationship to Mary Pidwall is one of anxious reverence. Mary occupies the position Laura herself might aspire to — respected, academically capable, morally upright — but the gap between them fills Laura with shame and dread. The relationship captures the novel's theme of social and moral surveillance: at Ladies' College, one is always being watched and judged, and Mary Pidwall embodies the judging gaze.

## Significance

Mary Pidwall represents the moral seriousness of the senior schoolgirl world — a world where reputation, conduct, and the opinion of one's peers carry immense weight. She is less a fully realized character than a narrative function: the embodiment of the standard Laura fears she cannot meet. Her presence in the novel also illustrates the hierarchical structure of student society at Ladies' College, where age and academic standing confer authority.

## In the Text

> But Mary Pidwall was one of those people — there are such — at the mere thought of whom poor Laura's soul sickened with a kind of nervous dread. [018-026]
