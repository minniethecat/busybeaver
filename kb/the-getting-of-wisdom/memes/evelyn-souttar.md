# Evelyn Souttar

Evelyn Souttar appears in the latter part of Henry Handel Richardson's "The Getting of Wisdom" as a figure of effortless social grace — pretty, well-liked, and indifferent to the hierarchies that torment Laura. Her friendship with Laura becomes one of the novel's most emotionally complex relationships, illuminating Laura's capacity for devotion and her vulnerability to social aspiration.

## Role in the Novel

Evelyn is introduced relatively late in the novel as "a very pretty girl, called Evelyn, with twinkling brown eyes" (PN [[018-070]]). She enters Laura's life after the "Shepherd debacle" — a social humiliation from which Laura has not yet recovered — and becomes the focus of Laura's intense emotional energy (PN [[022-005]]). Their friendship develops into a shared bedroom arrangement, and Laura's attachment to Evelyn becomes consuming: she rearranges her habits to avoid or seek Evelyn's company, experiences jealousy, and discovers in herself a capacity for devotion that exceeds anything the rather indifferent Evelyn returns.

## Character Traits

Evelyn is defined above all by her **effortlessness**. She is "also the only girl in the school who did not try to be popular" (PN [[022-005]]), and it is precisely this lack of striving that makes her magnetic. She is **physically beautiful** with "twinkling brown eyes," and everything about her "made it easy to grow fond of her" (PN [[022-031]]). She is **casually indifferent** to the academic and social competitions that consume other students — she "studied where she chose" (PN [[022-006]]) and refused Latin without apparent anxiety about the consequences. She can be **playful and teasing**: when Laura studies obsessively she catches her and says "Don't" with amusement rather than reproach (PN [[022-023]]). When Laura confesses to reading her letters, Evelyn only laughs — "Evelyn was more tickled than before" (PN [[022-035]]) — revealing both her emotional security and the asymmetry of their attachment.

## Key Scenes

**First appearance (Ch. XVIII)** introduces Evelyn as a visual impression — pretty, twinkling, set apart from the other girls by her self-possession (PN [[018-070]]). **The bedroom arrangement (Ch. XXII)** marks the intensification of their friendship: Laura moves into Evelyn's room, a change that "came as a great surprise" to her and that transforms her daily life (PN [[022-013]]). **Laura's surveillance (Ch. XXII)** reveals the asymmetry of their attachment — Laura "avoided the hours when Evelyn was there, getting up earlier" in a pattern of anxious avoidance and pursuit (PN [[022-021]]–[[022-022]]). **The letter-reading confession (Ch. XXII)** is the emotional climax: when Laura admits to reading Evelyn's private correspondence, she expects anger; instead Evelyn laughs, and this laughter, more than any rebuke, underscores the gap between Laura's intensity and Evelyn's ease (PN [[022-033]]–[[022-035]]).

## Relationship to Laura

Evelyn represents for Laura the possibility of social belonging — not through effort or achievement, but through simple proximity to grace. Laura's attachment to Evelyn is romantic in intensity, though not necessarily in kind: it is the devotion of someone who has never been fully accepted to someone who has never needed to try. The relationship is fundamentally unequal. Laura rearranges her life around Evelyn; Evelyn barely notices. This asymmetry is the relationship's painful lesson: that social acceptance, when it comes, may not bring the fulfillment Laura imagined. Evelyn is not cruel — she is simply insufficiently invested, and this insufficiency is itself a kind of wound.

## Significance

Evelyn functions thematically as an object of desire — not romantic desire in the conventional sense, but the desire for social grace, for the ease of belonging that Laura has never experienced. She embodies what Laura cannot achieve through talent or effort: the natural right to occupy space without apology. In this sense, Evelyn is less a fully realized character than a mirror in which Laura sees her own lack. The relationship's asymmetry — Laura's intensity met with Evelyn's casualness — reflects the novel's broader theme: that "the getting of wisdom" often means learning to live with unequal attachments, with wanting more than can be returned.
