# Examinations

The examination system in "The Getting of Wisdom" is the ultimate mechanism of institutional judgment — a formalized ordeal that measures, ranks, and determines the futures of the school's students. For Laura Rambotham, the examinations become the narrative's culminating crisis, exposing the gap between her intellectual self-conception and her actual performance with devastating clarity.

## In the Novel

Examinations structure the novel's final movement. Laura, who has always considered herself intellectually superior to her classmates — who has privately mocked their academic limitations and prided herself on her quick understanding — approaches the examinations with the casual confidence of one who has "always lightly skimmed through all examinations hitherto" (PN [[024-003]]).

But the midwinter test-examinations deliver a brutal correction: "she was behindhand with her work" (PN [[023-059]]), and her results mark her as precisely the kind of mediocre student she has always disdained. The public (matriculation) examination represents the final reckoning.

Despite desperate last-minute cramming — including a moment of panic in which Laura contemplates stealing an examination paper (PN [[024-044]]) — she faces the history examination with a mind "blank" and emerges knowing she has failed. The verdict: she has not passed her examinations, and her university ambitions are, for the present, foreclosed (PN [[025-003]]–[[025-005]]).

The examinations thus function as the novel's mechanism of consequences — the point at which Laura's accumulated failures of discipline, her social preoccupations, and her intellectual overconfidence converge into a single, irreversible judgment.

## Key Passages

> She was behindhand with her work, she who had never before failed to pass an examination, having always lightly skimmed through all examinations hitherto. — [[024-003]], the shock of self-recognition

> Let me only pass my examinations, Lord, and there is nothing I will not do for You. — [[024-029]], Laura's desperate bargain with the divine, the transparency of instrumental piety

> The public examination had proved as anticipated, child's play. — [[025-003]], the narrator's devastating irony: the examination was easy, and Laura failed it anyway

## Characters

**Laura Rambotham** is the central subject of the examination narrative: her failure is the novel's most painful lesson in the gap between self-image and reality. **M. P. (Mary Pidwall)** represents the counter-example — the genuinely brilliant student who passes her examinations with distinction and proceeds to the university, embodying the academic achievement Laura cannot match. **Miss Chapman** and the other teachers function as the examination system's agents, administering its judgments with the impersonal authority of institutional standards. **The Principal** appears in the aftermath of the public examination to deliver the verdict, his presence lending institutional weight to Laura's private disappointment.

## Significance

The examinations are thematically rich in several dimensions.

As a **plot mechanism**, they provide the novel with its climactic test: after two years of social education, Laura must face an academic judgment, and she fails — a failure that paradoxically marks her growth, since the Laura of Chapter I would have been incapable of recognizing a failure as her own.

As a **social commentary**, the examinations expose the class dimensions of educational assessment: Laura's failure is not purely intellectual but is bound up with her social distractions, her lack of cultural capital, and the school's own indifference to cultivating genuine scholarship in its female students.

As a **psychological portrait**, the examination scenes reveal Laura's characteristic pattern: overconfidence followed by panic, a desperate attempt to cheat or bargain her way out of difficulty, and finally a bitter self-knowledge that is the closest the novel comes to genuine wisdom.

The irony of the examination plot is that Laura's failure may be her salvation. The novel's final pages suggest a future beyond formal education — a world in which Laura, freed from the school's ranking systems, might discover something the Ladies' College could never teach: that intelligence and institutional success are not the same thing, and that the latter is not necessarily worth having.
