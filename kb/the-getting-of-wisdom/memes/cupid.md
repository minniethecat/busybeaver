# Cupid Stanhope

Cupid Stanhope — known universally by her nickname — is a senior student at the Ladies' College whose position at the apex of the school's social hierarchy makes her a figure of both emulation and exclusion for younger girls like Laura Rambotham. As co-leader of the Literary Society alongside Mary Pidwall, Cupid embodies the convergence of social prestige and institutional authority that defines the school's internal aristocracy.

## Role in the Novel

Cupid appears in the novel's later chapters as a background figure of considerable social weight. She is mentioned as one of the "leading members" of the Literary Society (PN [[021-001]]), the selective student organization whose membership serves as a marker of social acceptance. Her status as a prefect gives her formal authority within the school's governance structure, while her social standing — signaled by her nickname, which suggests affection rather than mockery — gives her informal influence that may be even more significant. She presides over the Literary Society's meetings and represents, for Laura, another rung on the ladder of social aspiration that the novel charts with such painful precision.

## Character Traits

Cupid's most distinctive characteristic is her **nickname**, which the novel records without irony. Unlike the nicknames assigned to Laura ("Wondrous Fair" by her siblings, various less flattering epithets by her schoolmates), Cupid's nickname is a mark of social affection — a diminutive that signals belonging rather than exclusion. She is associated with **social prestige** rather than intellectual achievement: where Mary Pidwall represents the academic elite, Cupid represents the social elite, and their co-leadership of the Literary Society suggests the alliance of these two forms of power within the school's hierarchy. Her role as **prefect** adds institutional authority to her social standing, making her a figure whom younger girls must both respect and seek to please.

## Key Scenes

**The Literary Society (Ch. XXI)**: Cupid is introduced as a co-leader of the Literary Society, the selective student organization that Laura is invited to join. The invitation — extended by Cupid and Mary — represents Laura's social arrival, a recognition that she has achieved sufficient status to be admitted to the school's unofficial elite (PN [[021-001]]). Cupid's presence lends the invitation its social weight: to be approved by Cupid is to be approved by the school's social establishment.

## Relationship to Laura

Laura relates to Cupid primarily as a distant figure of authority and prestige — not an intimate or an enemy, but someone whose judgment matters and whose approval carries weight. Cupid is not directly cruel to Laura, nor is she particularly kind; she is simply above her in the social order, and her elevation is itself a form of social fact that Laura must navigate. The invitation to the Literary Society, extended through Cupid, is one of the few unambiguous social victories Laura achieves — a moment when the hierarchy that has so often excluded her briefly opens to admit her.

## Significance

Cupid functions thematically as a representation of the **social elite as institution**. She is not individually interesting — the novel gives her no psychological depth, no distinctive voice, no memorable scene — and this absence of individuality is precisely her significance. Cupid matters not because of who she is but because of what she represents: the convergence of social prestige and institutional authority that constitutes the school's ruling class. Her nickname, which might in another context signal intimacy, here signals the opposite — the easy familiarity of those who have never had to fight for belonging.
