# Speech

Speech in *The Getting of Wisdom* is never merely communication. It is performance, social currency, and a site of constant judgment. Laura Rambotham's relationship to speech — her accented vowels, her hesitant delivery, her occasional bursts of reckless candor — tracks her progress through the social world of the Ladies' College as surely as her academic marks. To speak well is to belong; to speak poorly, or too boldly, or in the wrong accent, is to mark oneself as an outsider.

## Speech as Class Marker

From her first days at the College, Laura is made aware that her speech marks her. Her colonial Australian accent, her "loud and ironic manner of speech" acquired from her mother [[004-001]], and her unfamiliarity with the verbal codes of the wealthier boarders all conspire to identify her as different. The question "What's your father?" is a question about class, but it is also a question posed in a particular linguistic register — one that Laura must learn to inhabit if she is to survive socially.

The novel draws attention to the varieties of speech that circulate within the College: the refined English of the headmistress, the broad Australian of the servants, the affected gentility of the aspiring middle-class girls, and the careless confidence of the truly wealthy. Laura's speech lies somewhere between these poles — she can mimic refinement when necessary, but her natural register betrays her origins. The gap between what she wants to say and what she knows how to say is one of the novel's persistent preoccupations.

## Speech Day

Speech Day at the Ladies' College is the culminating public event of the academic year, the occasion on which the students' verbal accomplishments are displayed before an audience of parents and dignitaries. "When speech day was over" [[019-013]], the school year effectively ends, and the cycle of performance and judgment resets. Speech Day crystallizes the novel's treatment of speech as performance: the girls recite, deliver, and perform, their words carefully scripted and their delivery carefully rehearsed.

The irony of Speech Day is that it celebrates the kind of speech that least resembles genuine communication. The girls perform words that are not their own, in a manner that is not natural, for an audience whose judgment matters enormously. It is speech as theater, and like all theater at the College, it conceals as much as it reveals.

## The Tyranny of Eloquence

Laura's relationship to speech is complicated by her awareness of her own verbal limitations. "A prompting to speech invariably tied her tongue" [[015-030]] — she is, for much of the novel, someone who wants to speak but cannot, whose interior life is rich but whose verbal expression is constrained. The novel's free indirect discourse gives us access to Laura's unspoken thoughts precisely because her spoken words so often fail her.

Yet Laura is also capable of bold speech — the cruel wit of the Annie Johns expulsion letter, the "boldness of speech" that characterizes certain moments of social confidence [[017-068]]. These moments of verbal daring are double-edged: they win Laura attention and, occasionally, admiration, but they also expose her to the consequences of speaking unwisely. The novel understands speech as a risk — to speak is to expose oneself to judgment, and Laura, more than most, cannot afford to be judged.

## Speech and Silence

The novel's treatment of speech is inseparable from its treatment of silence. Laura's silences — her blushing muteness when questioned about her father, her wordless suffering under teasing, her inability to defend herself verbally — are as significant as her speech. Silence in the novel is not absence but presence: it is the space in which social pressure is most acutely felt, the medium through which exclusion operates.

"In your speech, your talk with others, you must learn to be careful" [[021-044]] — this injunction, directed at Laura, captures the novel's understanding of speech as a discipline. To speak is to navigate a minefield of social expectations; to remain silent is to cede the field entirely. Laura must learn not only what to say but when, how, and to whom — a curriculum more demanding than any academic subject the College offers.

## Significance

Speech in *The Getting of Wisdom* functions as both theme and method. Thematically, the novel charts Laura's progress from verbal naivety to a hard-won competence in the language of her social world — a competence that is never complete, never secure, but real enough to allow her survival. Methodologically, the novel's own speech — its free indirect discourse, its rendering of Australian vernacular, its attention to the nuances of spoken language — embodies the very concerns it explores.

The "wisdom" of the title is, in part, the wisdom of knowing what to say and when to say it — the practical wisdom of social speech, acquired not from books but from the painful experience of speaking wrongly and suffering the consequences.
