# Shame

Shame in "The Getting of Wisdom" is not a passing emotion but the novel's central affective condition — the burning awareness of social inadequacy that marks Laura Rambotham as an outsider and fuels her desperate, often self-destructive attempts to belong to a world that continuously judges her and finds her wanting.

## In the Novel

Shame operates in the novel as both theme and narrative engine. From the first chapter, when Laura's mother makes her a dress "too short" for fashion and Laura insists "I don't care" while on the brink of tears (PN [[001-023]]–[[001-035]]), shame is established as the emotion that cannot be admitted — and therefore the one that drives all action. The novel's episodic structure is organized around a series of shaming events: the trunk inspection by Mrs. Gurley (PN [[004-044]]–[[004-053]]), the exposure of her mother's occupation as a needlewoman (Ch. XI), the embroidery incident with Bob (PN [[014-081]]), the scandal of the Shepherd episode (Ch. XX), and the bitter disappointment of failing her examinations (Ch. XXIV–XXV). Each episode is a lesson in the gap between Laura's self-conception and her social reality. The cumulative effect is a portrait of childhood as a prolonged state of mortification — a condition from which "the getting of wisdom" offers only partial escape.

Shame is also the novel's primary social mechanism. It is not merely what Laura feels but what the institution deploys: Mrs. Gurley's freezing glances, Lilith Gordon's interrogations, and the school's elaborate ranking systems all weaponize shame as a disciplinary tool. The novel demonstrates how shame — unlike guilt, which attaches to actions — attaches to identity itself: Laura is not ashamed of what she has done but of who she is.

## Key Passages

> Another second, and the shameful tears might have found their way to her eyes. — [[004-057]], Laura under Mrs. Gurley's inspection, the body betraying what the will would conceal

> "Why, Laura, you're not ashamed of it, are you? — that mother of yours takes in sewing?" — [[019-040]], the casual cruelty of exposure, shame named aloud for the first time

> The first shameful stab over, she felt the better for having spoken. — [[020-019]], after confessing her role in the Shepherd affair, the paradoxical relief of acknowledged shame

## Characters

Shame attaches most powerfully to **Laura Rambotham**, for whom it is the default emotional response to almost every social situation. Her mother — "Pin" — is herself a figure of shame: her occupation as a seamstress (the novel's euphemism is "needlework"), her ignorance of social codes, and her physical distance from the school all mark her as the source of Laura's social deficiency. **Lilith Gordon** functions as shame's primary agent, her curiosity and condescension designed to expose the gap between Laura's pretensions and her reality. **Evelyn Souttar** represents the longed-for escape from shame — social acceptance so complete that it promises to erase the stigma of origins — but the friendship's collapse only deepens Laura's mortification. **Mrs. Gurley** embodies institutional shame: her chilly smile and methodical inspections turn social judgment into a quasi-religious ritual of unworthiness.

## Historical Context

The novel's treatment of shame is inseparable from its late-Victorian colonial setting. In 1890s Melbourne, the colonial middle class was engaged in an anxious project of social self-fashioning, importing British class distinctions and the cult of respectability to a society that lacked the entrenched aristocracy to sustain them. The Ladies' College, with its uniforms, its forms, and its elaborate codes of conduct, was an engine of this project — a machine for transforming colonial girls into recognizably "English" young ladies. Shame, in this context, was the mechanism that enforced compliance: the fear of being exposed as not-quite-respectable-enough drove the entire enterprise. Laura's particular shame — the daughter of a country widow who takes in sewing — maps precisely onto colonial anxieties about status, origins, and the ever-present fear of being "found out."

## Critical Perspective

Literary critics have read shame in "The Getting of Wisdom" through several lenses. Feminist readings emphasize how shame operates as a technology of gender: the school's surveillance of girls' bodies, clothes, and comportment reproduces patriarchal norms through internalized self-policing. Postcolonial readings locate Laura's shame in the experience of the colonial subject who has internalized metropolitan standards and finds herself perpetually deficient. Bildungsroman readings treat shame as the necessary pain of the coming-of-age process — the crucible through which the self is forged. All three perspectives agree on the novel's central insight: that shame, for all its pain, produces knowledge. Laura's humiliations teach her what the school's explicit curriculum cannot — the social codes that govern the world she must enter.
