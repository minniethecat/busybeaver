# Conformity

Conformity is the gravitational force of the Ladies' College social universe. Every aspect of school life — from the clothes one wears to the books one reads on Sunday [[008-052]] to the families one claims as connections — is governed by unwritten codes that the students enforce with a vigilance that makes explicit rules seem almost irrelevant. Laura Rambotham's education is, above all, an education in the demands and costs of conformity.

## The Codes of School Life

The Ladies' College operates through a dense web of social codes. Dress is perhaps the most visible: the length of a skirt, the quality of a fabric, the fashionableness of a cut — these are not trivial matters of personal taste but indices of class position, scrutinized by classmates with the precision of semioticians. Laura's first crisis is provoked by the length of her dress: "All the other girls will have dresses down to the tops of their boots, and they'll laugh at me, and call me a baby" [[001-035]]. Speech is another: the right way to address a teacher, the correct tone for a classroom answer, the acceptable topics for conversation at evening tea. Religious observance, leisure activities, even the choice of "Sunday books" for approved reading [[008-052]] — all are subject to codes that distinguish the knowing from the ignorant, the insider from the interloper. The very fact that the College is "nonconformist" [[004-050]] — a religious identity — embeds conformity into the institution's founding character:

> "Why, what is this?" asked Mrs. Gurley freezingly, and held up to view—with the tips of her fingers, Laura thought—a small, black Prayer Book. "Pray, are you not a dissenter?"—For the College was nonconformist. [[004-050]]

Laura arrives at the school innocent of these codes, and her innocence is the source of her suffering. She wears the wrong clothes, says the wrong things, reveals the wrong facts about her family. Her violations of the code are not acts of rebellion but failures of knowledge: she does not know what she does not know. The novel's tragedy is that by the time she learns the codes well enough to comply with them, she has also learned their arbitrariness — a knowledge that makes conformity impossible as anything other than performance.

## The Cost of Non-Conformity

The novel is unsparing in its depiction of the social penalties for non-conformity. Laura's exposure as the daughter of a needlewoman is not merely embarrassing but socially catastrophic: it confirms everything her classmates have suspected about her unfitness for their company. The punishment is not formal (there is no rule against having a mother who works) but informal and all the more devastating for it: exclusion from the circle of acceptable friendship, the loss of the social standing she has worked so desperately to construct.

> The real reason of her pleasurable absorption was, she supposed, that she had understood Annie Johns' motive better than anyone else. Well, she had had no business to understand—that was the long and the short of it: nice-minded girls found such a thing impossible, and turned incuriously away. And her companions had been quick to recognise her difference of attitude, or they would never have dared to accuse her of sympathy with the thief, or to doubt her chorusing assertion with a sneer. [[012-038]]

Laura's desire to conform is not cynical but desperate — a survival instinct. She longs "to see eye to eye with her comrades" [[012-038]] and to "be made to feel as she ought to feel, in a proper, ladylike way" [[012-038]]. Her prayer is not for individuality but for its erasure: that she might "be preserved from having thoughts that were different from other people's" [[012-038]].

## Conformity and Selfhood

The novel's deepest insight into conformity is its recognition that successful conformity requires not just outward compliance but inward transformation — a remodeling of the self to fit the available social categories. Laura cannot simply pretend to be the kind of girl the school approves; she must, in some measure, become that girl. The cost of this transformation is the loss of the childhood self, and the novel's ambivalent ending — Laura running free through the park on leaving school [[025-049]] — reflects the uncertainty of whether the self that has survived is still recognizably Laura:

> She went out from school with the uncomfortable sense of being a square peg, which fitted into none of the round holes of her world; the wisdom she had got, the experience she was richer by, had, in the process of equipping her for life, merely seemed to disclose her unfitness. [[025-032]]

## Significance

Conformity in "The Getting of Wisdom" is not a moral failure but a social fact — the condition of survival in an institution that has no room for individuality except the kind that can be performed within the narrow bounds of acceptable self-presentation. From the first crisis of dress length [[001-035]] to the last anxious longing to be "proper" [[012-038]], Laura's story is a testament to the weight of social expectation. Richardson's critique is directed not at the girls who enforce the codes (they are, after all, children themselves) but at the society that has produced such codes and such children — a colonial culture so anxious about its own status that it teaches its daughters to police one another with the ruthlessness of gatekeepers guarding a door that leads nowhere in particular.
