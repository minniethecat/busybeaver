# Education

Education is the explicit subject and implicit critique at the heart of "The Getting of Wisdom." The novel maps two parallel educations: the formal curriculum of the Ladies' College — academic subjects, musical accomplishments, deportment — and the unofficial education in social survival that Laura Rambotham receives from her peers and from the institution itself. The tension between these two educations, and the cost at which Laura acquires her "wisdom," constitutes the novel's central drama.

## In the Novel

Education is omnipresent in the novel as both setting and theme. The Ladies' College curriculum structures Laura's daily life: lessons, practice, examinations, and the ranking systems that measure every girl against her peers. The "high-road of education" (PN [[013-003]]) is a path Laura travels with mixed results — distinguished in music, adequate in academic subjects, always aware that formal achievement is only part of what the school values. Alongside and often in tension with formal learning is the social education: learning to dress correctly, to modulate one's voice, to navigate the invisible hierarchies of the dining hall and the dormitory, to dissemble one's true feelings in the service of social survival.

The financial dimension of education is also present. Laura's mother has "hoarded up for Laura's education" (PN [[024-003]]), and this sacrifice weighs on Laura — her education is not a right but a privilege purchased at cost. The novel's ending, which finds Laura at the end of her school career contemplating "what she would do when she had finished her education" (PN [[015-023]]), leaves open the question of what, exactly, she has learned — and what of value the school has actually taught her.

## Key Passages

> I'm going to school tomorrow, and it's a shame. — [[001-032]], Laura's premonitory lament before departing for the Ladies' College

> The money her mother had scraped together and hoarded up for Laura's education was now coming to an end. — [[024-003]], the economic reality underlying Laura's privileged position

> She thought of the girls whom she had seen leave school when she was still lower down, and how they had looked going away. — [[015-023]], Laura's contemplation of what lies beyond education

> "The lesson went home; Laura began to model herself more and more on those around her; to grasp that the unpardonable sin is to vary from the common mould." — [[010-001]], the social curriculum asserting itself over the academic one

> "She became a quick, adaptable pupil, with a parrot-like memory, and at the end of the school year delighted Mother's heart with a couple of highly gilt volumes." — Ch. X, the triumph of conformity: Laura learns to produce the results the system rewards

## Characters

Laura is the primary subject of the novel's education theme, but other characters illuminate different aspects. **Miss Chapman** represents institutional education personified — competent, rule-bound, incapable of recognizing the individual needs behind the student. **Evelyn Souttar** embodies the product of successful social education: effortlessly graceful, indifferent to academic striving, naturally in possession of what Laura must labor to acquire. **Laura's mother** represents the sacrifice that underwrites education — the "hoarded" money that purchases Laura's opportunity and the emotional cost of sending a child away to be transformed.

## Historical Context

In late nineteenth-century Australia, secondary education for girls was a relatively new phenomenon. Schools like the Presbyterian Ladies' College (the real-world model for the novel's Ladies' College) were part of a broader movement to provide middle-class girls with an education that combined academic subjects with the "accomplishments" (music, drawing, languages) that marked a young lady's suitability for marriage and social life. The tension between genuine intellectual development and social credentialing was inherent in these institutions from the start — and it is precisely this tension that Richardson's novel anatomizes with such precision.

## Critical Perspective

Richardson's treatment of education is fundamentally ironic. The novel's title invokes the Biblical proverb of wisdom as a divine gift, but what Laura acquires at school is something more ambiguous: the wisdom of knowing how to dissemble, how to suppress one's authentic self in the service of social survival. This is education as deformation rather than formation — a critique that aligns Richardson with contemporary progressive educational reformers and with the broader modernist skepticism toward institutional authority. The novel leaves unresolved whether Laura's education has been a net gain or loss — whether "the getting of wisdom" is growth or diminishment.
