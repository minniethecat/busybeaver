# Lucy

Lucy — known throughout the College as "Lucy of Toorak" — is a young but socially formidable student at the Ladies' College, distinguished by her quick wit and her willingness to deploy it at the expense of others. Her four appearances in Chapter XI are enough to establish her as one of the novel's sharpest minor portraits: a girl whose verbal facility gives her a social power disproportionate to her age or standing.

## Role in the Novel

Lucy appears exclusively in Chapter XI, in a group scene that brings together several of the novel's minor schoolgirl characters. She is "young but very saucy" [011-013], a description that captures both her youth — she is not one of the senior girls — and her social confidence. In the hierarchy of the College, Lucy belongs to the middle ranks: too young to lead, too sharp to follow quietly. Her nickname — "Lucy of Toorak" — gives her a territorial identity, anchoring her to a specific, fashionable Melbourne suburb. This is a marker of both class and social aspiration: Toorak is a place that connotes wealth and status, and Lucy carries that connotation like a banner.

## Character Traits

Lucy's defining characteristic is her wit, which is inseparable from her cruelty. She is "saucy" [011-013] — a word that suggests both impudence and charm, the kind of verbal boldness that amuses even as it stings. Her laugh is physically distinctive: her "nose came down" [011-015] when she laughed, a detail that makes her mirth seem almost predatory, her face transforming with the pleasure of mockery.

Richardson is precise about the social function of Lucy's wit. She makes "the butt of young Lucy's wit" [011-031] of those less socially adept than herself — a phrase that reveals both the aggressiveness of her humour and its social utility. By turning others into targets, Lucy consolidates her own position; her wit is a weapon, and she uses it strategically.

## Key Scenes

**Introduction in the Group (Ch. XI)**: Lucy is introduced as part of a social group on the school grounds: "In the group was a girl called Lucy, young but very saucy" [011-013]. The phrasing — "a girl called Lucy" — is deliberately casual, as if her reputation has preceded her and everyone already knows who she is.

**The Laugh (Ch. XI)**: "Lucy of Toorak laughed: her nose came down" [011-015]. The physical detail of the nose coming down transforms her laugh from a sound into a visual image — a face momentarily distorted by amusement, predatory and pleased.

**Recovery and Redirection (Ch. XI)**: After failing to land a joke, "Lucy of Toorak could not recover from her amazement" [011-030]. The line captures both her confidence — she expects her wit to land — and her vulnerability: when it does not, she is genuinely disoriented. The social world she navigates so skillfully can, in an instant, become foreign to her.

**The Butt of Wit (Ch. XI)**: The narrator notes that Laura, at this stage, was "the butt of young Lucy's wit" [011-031]. This is Lucy in her element: identifying the socially vulnerable and targeting them with a precision that is both entertaining and wounding. For Laura, who is already struggling to find her place, Lucy's mockery is another reminder of her own social inadequacy.

## Relationship to Laura

Lucy's relationship to Laura is one-directional: Lucy mocks, Laura endures. Laura is "the butt of young Lucy's wit" [011-031] at a time when she is still learning the social codes of the College, still uncertain of her footing. Lucy, young as she is, has already mastered the art of social aggression — she knows how to identify weakness and exploit it for the entertainment of the group. For Laura, who craves acceptance and dreads exposure, Lucy represents the cruelty latent in school society: the casual wounding that passes for wit, the mockery that masquerades as play.

Yet Lucy is not a villain. She is simply a schoolgirl who has discovered that verbal skill is a form of power, and who uses that power in the way that schoolgirls do — to climb, to exclude, to entertain. The novel does not condemn her; it simply observes her, with the same cool precision it brings to all its portraits of the social world.

## Significance

Lucy of Toorak embodies a particular mode of female social power: wit as weapon, mockery as method. She is young, which makes her sharpness all the more striking — she has learned early that in the competitive social world of the Ladies' College, verbal facility is a surer path to status than kindness. Her surname is never given; she is "Lucy of Toorak," identified by her fashionable suburb rather than her family — a girl who has already learned to brand herself with the markers of class and place.

In the novel's taxonomy of schoolgirl types, Lucy occupies the position of the wit: the girl whose social standing depends on the continual performance of cleverness, whose friendships are mediated by the constant threat that she might turn her sharpness on the friend. She is vivid, memorable, and slightly frightening — a reminder that the school world Laura enters is not only strange but dangerous, full of girls who have learned to wield words as weapons.
