# Melbourne

Melbourne is the primary urban setting of Henry Handel Richardson's "The Getting of Wisdom" — a city in the late 1880s or early 1890s that embodies the aspirations and anxieties of colonial Australian society. For Laura Rambotham, arriving from the country, Melbourne represents both opportunity and estrangement.

## In the Novel

Melbourne is present in the novel less as a described landscape than as a pervasive atmosphere of judgment and social expectation. It is the destination of Laura's journey from home — the train journey in Chapter II ends with the conductor's announcement: "Melbourne" (PN [[002-018]]). Through the train window, Laura sees "the broad white streets of East Melbourne" bordered by thick greenery (PN [[008-010]]), a glimpse of a world more ordered and imposing than anything she has known. The city is the site of the Ladies' College, of the godmother's house in Prahran, and of the shopping expeditions that measure Laura's social adequacy — her mother's trips "to Melbourne" to buy dress fabric as "a bargain" contrast with the fashionable standards Laura encounters at school (PN [[010-009]]).

## Key Passages

> The train slowed, and a voice called: "Melbourne." — [[002-018]], the arrival that marks Laura's entry into a new world

> This was Melbourne. At the nearer end of the platform stood a cluster of people, their eyes scanning the carriage windows. — [[003-044]], Laura's first impression of the city

> The broad white streets of East Melbourne; at their side was the thick, green foliage of the Fitzroy Gardens. — [[008-010]], a rare descriptive passage locating the school in the city's geography

## Significance

Melbourne in the novel functions as both setting and symbol. It represents the metropolitan center against which Laura's country origins are measured and found wanting. The city's institutions — the Ladies' College, the churches, the social rituals — embody the British-derived colonial culture that demands conformity and polish. At the same time, Melbourne is not London: it is a colonial city, aspirational rather than established, and this aspirational quality mirrors Laura's own struggle to become something she is not yet. The novel's city is the Melbourne of "Marvellous Melbourne" — the boom-era capital of the 1880s — though the novel's social world registers the economic anxieties that followed the crash of the early 1890s.

## Historical Context

In the late nineteenth century, Melbourne was one of the largest and wealthiest cities in the British Empire, enriched by the Victorian gold rush and land speculation. "Marvellous Melbourne" was a city of grand public buildings, broad streets, and a fiercely status-conscious middle class. The Ladies' College model — private secondary education for girls — was a relatively recent import from England, reflecting the growing demand for "accomplished" daughters who could marry well and maintain social position. The geographic references in the novel — East Melbourne, Prahran, St Kilda, Collins Street — map onto real locations that remain identifiable in modern Melbourne.
