# Anxiety

Anxiety is the emotional condition that governs [[laura-rambotham]]'s experience from her first moments at the [[ladies-college]] to her final reflections on what she has become. It appears not as a clinical diagnosis but as a pervasive atmosphere — a state of heightened vigilance, anticipatory dread, and somatic distress that Richardson renders with unsparing psychological precision. Laura's anxiety is both cause and consequence of her social precariousness, driving her to desperate measures while marking her, ever more deeply, as an outsider.

## The Entry into Fear

Anxiety enters the novel even before Laura reaches the College. On the eve of her departure, her mother Pin "began to sniff, in sheer nervous anxiety" [[001-032]], and the emotion transfers from mother to daughter like an infection. Laura's fear of the unknown — the school, the other girls, the judgment she will face — is established before she has any concrete experience of what awaits her. The train journey, the arrival at the College gates, the first glimpse of Mrs. Gurley — each stage of her initiation is shadowed by anticipatory dread.

The "dreaded moment" of separation from her godmother [[004-032]] and the "dreaded looks" that Mrs. Gurley directs at her over spectacles [[004-046]] confirm that Laura's anxiety is not neurotic excess but rational response to a genuinely threatening environment.

## The Architecture of Terror

> In the case of Mrs. Gurley, familiarity had never been known to breed contempt. She was possessed of what was little short of genius, for ruling through fear. — [[007-002]]

The College itself is organized around anxiety as a technology of control. Mrs. Gurley's "hard, unflagging eye" [[007-002]] creates a regime of perpetual surveillance in which the girls police themselves. The "crippling fear that governed their relations with the Principal" [[009-021]] extends to every adult authority figure: even at a gathering intended for pleasure, the girls sit rigid on the edges of their chairs, unable to relax or speak naturally.

This anxiety operates at every level of Laura's experience. She "went in fear and trembling lest the truth should come to light" about her mother's occupation as a needlewoman [[011-036]], living "in hourly dread of Lilith betraying her." The fear of exposure — of being revealed as someone whose mother works — transforms Laura into "a porcupine, ready to erect her quills at a touch" [[011-036]].

## The Dream of Expulsion

> For that night she started up, with a cry, from dreaming that not Annie Johns but she was being expelled; that an army of spear-like first fingers was marching towards her, and that, try as she would, she could not get her limp, heavy legs to bear her to the schoolroom door. — [[012-039]]

The nightmare of being expelled in [[annie-johns]]'s place reveals how deeply anxiety has penetrated Laura's unconscious life. The dream recurs, its imagery of pointing fingers and paralyzed limbs capturing the essence of her waking fears: judgment, exclusion, and the inability to escape the consequences of who she is. The dream takes the most feared outcome of school discipline — expulsion — and applies it to Laura herself, suggesting that even in sleep she cannot escape the conviction of her own vulnerability.

## Anxiety and the Body

Richardson renders Laura's anxiety in physical terms. Her heart beats irregularly, her throat constricts, her face flushes scarlet. In moments of acute stress, "the shameful tears might have found their way to her eyes" [[004-057]] despite her determination to conceal emotion. The body betrays what the will would hide, and this betrayal becomes another source of anxiety — the fear not only of what will happen, but of how she will reveal herself in the happening.

The somatic character of Laura's anxiety distinguishes it from mere worry: it is embedded in her physical being, a condition of the self that cannot be reasoned away or disciplined into submission. It is, in this sense, a realistic portrait of adolescent anxiety as a total experience — cognitive, emotional, and bodily.

## Survival Through Anxiety

Anxiety is not, however, merely debilitating. It heightens Laura's perception, sharpens her attention to nuance, and motivates the constant self-monitoring that eventually enables her to decode the school's social codes. The anxiety-driven vigilance that makes her miserable also makes her observant — and observation is the first step toward understanding. In this sense, Laura's anxiety is both wound and tool, a condition that damages her but also equips her for the difficult education in social reality that the novel chronicles.
