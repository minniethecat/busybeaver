# Fear

**Fear** is the emotional undercurrent of [[the-getting-of-wisdom]], operating at every level of Laura Rambotham's experience: fear of authority figures, fear of social exposure, fear of the future, and the deep existential fear that surfaces in her recurring nightmares.

## Fear as Institutional Governance

The Ladies' College operates through fear as its primary mechanism of control. The narrator's description of the head governess is definitive:

> [007-002] Mrs. Gurley was possessed of what was little short of genius, for ruling through fear.

This is not presented as incidental personality — it is a structural claim. The narrator elaborates with a catalogue of the school's population that emphasizes the scope of Mrs. Gurley's dominion: "these half-hundred girls, of all ages and degrees: gentle and common; ruly and unruly, children hardly out of the nursery, and girls well over the brink of womanhood … the daughters of poor ministers at reduced fees; and the spoilt heiresses of wealthy wool-brokers and squatters." The rhetorical climax — "Mrs. Gurley was equal to them all" (007-002) — establishes fear not as a failure of the institution but as its operational principle.

The effect on Laura is immediate and physical. After the catastrophic incident in which Laura innocently taps Mrs. Gurley on the arm to ask about a French lesson:

> [007-006] "How dare you interrupt me — when I am speaking!" — she hissed, punctuating her words with the ominous head-shakes and pauses. "The first thing, miss, for you to do, will be, to take a course of lessons, in manners. Your present ones, may have done well enough, in the outhouse, to which you have evidently belonged. They will not do, here, in the company of your betters."

The word "outhouse" — with its suggestion of rustic squalor — is deliberately chosen to wound. And it does: "The iron of the thrust went deep down into her soul: no one had ever yet cast a slur upon her home" (007-007). Laura retreats to a lavatory and cries herself "nearly sick" (007-007). From that day forward, "she had never of her own free will approached Mrs. Gurley again" (007-007).

The narrator later confirms the persistence of this institutional terror:

> [009-021] it was impossible for the pupils to throw off, at will, the crippling fear that governed their relations with the Principal.

The word "crippling" is precise — fear does not merely modify behavior but disables capacity. It is not a transient emotion but a "governing" condition, as structural as the school timetable.

## Social Fear and Class Exposure

Beyond institutional fear, Laura experiences a more intimate social fear — the terror of exposure and judgment. The narrator describes the specific vulnerability that Laura's class position creates:

> [011-036] the circumstance of Mother being a woman made things ten times worse: it meant that she had to work for her living — "and the whole school would think the less of her for being so." She lived in fear and trembling lest the truth should come to light — this truth that she had so laboriously kept hidden — and she would then be "a pariah indeed."

This passage, from the moment after Laura has impulsively revealed to Lilith Gordon that her mother does embroidery for money, captures the layered structure of social fear: it is not simply fear of discovery but fear of the consequences of discovery — becoming a "pariah." The phrase "fear and trembling" carries a quasi-religious weight, suggesting that social judgment functions as a secular Last Judgment for these girls.

## Fear and the Body

The novel renders fear not as an abstract emotion but as a physical condition. Laura's response to Mrs. Gurley is visceral:

> [007-003] In a very short time, there was no more persistent shrinker from the ice of this gaze than little Laura. In the presence of Mrs. Gurley the child had a difficulty in getting her breath.

The phrase "difficulty in getting her breath" is almost clinical — fear manifests as a respiratory impairment. Later, when Laura must re-enter the dining hall to retrieve a forgotten book, the narrator describes her "in a twitter lest the dreaded eyes should turn her way" (007-007). The word "twitter" — suggesting the agitated movement of a small bird — reduces Laura to animal panic.

## Fear and Stoicism

The novel presents an important counter-movement to fear in Laura's determination to conceal it. From early in the narrative, Laura shows "an instinct that made her chary of showing fear, or pain, or grief" (004-026). This instinctive stoicism — the refusal to display vulnerability — becomes a defining feature of her character. The irony is that this very concealment of fear produces the isolation and loneliness that generate more fear. Laura is trapped in a cycle: fear → concealment → isolation → more fear.

## The Recurring Nightmare

Fear takes its most vivid form in Laura's recurring dream:

> [012-039] that night she started up, with a cry, from dreaming that not Annie Johns but she was being expelled; that an army of spear-like first fingers was marching towards her.

The narrator adds that "this dream often returned" (012-040), and later its familiar form reappears:

> [018-045] she had the familiar dream that she was being "stood up" and expelled, as Annie Johns had been: thousands of tongues shouted her guilt; she was hunted like a wallaby.

The dream condenses Laura's deepest fears — public shame, expulsion, persecution — into a single recurring image. The progression from "fingers" to "tongues" and the simile "hunted like a wallaby" (with its specific Australian reference, reducing the human to native prey) suggests that fear, in Laura's unconscious, expands rather than diminishes with time.

## Fear of the Future

In the novel's final chapters, fear takes a new form: anxiety about the future. Laura is "beset by nervous fears about the future" (024-003), as the protected world of school gives way to the uncertainties of adult life. This fear is compounded by academic pressure — she "had never before failed to pass an examination" (024-003) and cannot afford another year of fees. The fear that began as institutional and social now becomes existential: the question is no longer "will I be shamed?" but "what will become of me?"

The novel's arc of fear — from institutional terror through social dread to existential anxiety — traces Laura's development from child to young adult. What changes is not the intensity of fear but its object: the terrifying Mrs. Gurley is replaced by the terrifying open future. And Laura's characteristic response — the stoic refusal to show what she feels — remains constant to the end.
