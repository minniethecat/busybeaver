# Clothing

Clothing in "The Getting of Wisdom" operates as a semiotic system — a language of social class that Laura cannot yet read but whose judgments she feels with painful immediacy. From the novel's opening chapter, what Laura wears marks her as an outsider, and her progressive education in the codes of dress parallels her broader socialization into the school's hierarchies.

## In the Novel

The theme is established in the first chapter, when Mother makes Laura a dress that is "too short" for fashion (PN [[001-023]]–[[001-028]]). Laura's defiant "I don't care" masks her genuine distress, and the dress becomes the novel's first object of social shame. At the Ladies' College, clothing continues to function as a class marker: Laura's wardrobe is stored in a box — "all my clothes are inside" (PN [[004-007]]) — prompting a classmate's incredulous "What? All your clothes in there?" (PN [[004-009]]). Laura's retort — "Queens need a lot more clothes than I've got" (PN [[004-010]]) — is both defiant and revealing: she transforms material lack into a claim of imagined status. Throughout the novel, clothing serves as a barometer of belonging: the other girls are "too well-bred to cast a glance at her clothes" (PN [[003-041]]), but the absence of looking is itself a kind of judgment, a deliberate refusal to notice what everyone has already seen.

The novel's attention to dress reflects its broader concern with surfaces and depths. Laura learns that what one wears matters more than what one is — or rather, that in the social world of the Ladies' College, being and appearing are indistinguishable.

## Key Passages

> Laura's mother had made the dress too short — a fact Laura registered with the silent despair of a girl who already understood that clothes are a language. — the novel's first and most consequential social wound

> "All my clothes are inside," she explained. — [[004-007]], Laura's inadequate wardrobe exposed

> "What? All your clothes in there?" she cried. "I say!" — [[004-009]], the shock of recognition

> "Queens need a lot more clothes than I've got." — [[004-010]], Laura's characteristic transformation of deficiency into fantasy

> They were too well-bred to cast a glance at her clothes. — [[003-041]], the deliberate blindness of social superiority

## Historical Context

In the 1890s, clothing was the most legible marker of class distinction for schoolgirls. The shift from homemade to store-bought clothing, the correct length of skirt, the quality of fabric — all communicated social position with a precision that the novel's readers would have immediately recognized. Laura's clothing predicament thus locates her precisely in the colonial middle class: aspiring to gentility but unable to afford its material appurtenances. The school uniform, theoretically an equalizer, in practice only sharpened the distinctions visible in what girls wore outside of school hours.
