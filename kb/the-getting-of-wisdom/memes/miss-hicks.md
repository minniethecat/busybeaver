# Miss Hicks

Miss Hicks is a visiting governess for geography at the Ladies' College in Melbourne.
She appears in Chapter 9 of the novel, where her biting critique of a fellow
student becomes a catalyst for the protagonist Laura Rambotham's intellectual
self-examination.

## Role at the College

Miss Hicks is employed as a visiting governess responsible for teaching geography.
She is described as having "a gift for saying biting things that really bit"
[009-010]. She bears a particular grudge against the student [[inez]], believing
that "certain faculties slumbered behind the Grecian profile, and that only the
girl's ingrained sloth prevented them" [009-010].

## The Classroom Critique

Miss Hicks's most significant contribution to the narrative occurs when she
delivers a scathing critique of Inez in front of the class:

> "You're blessed with a real woman's brain: vague, slippery, inexact, interested
> only in the personal aspect of a thing. You can't concentrate your thoughts, and,
> worst of all, you've no curiosity—about anything that really matters."

— Miss Hicks to Inez [009-010]

While Inez and the other students treat the remarks lightly, [[laura-rambotham]]
is deeply affected. Laura cannot forget the incident, and "what Miss Hicks had
said often came back to her, in the days that followed" [009-013].

## Impact on Laura

Miss Hicks's words become the catalyst for Laura's intellectual awakening. Laura
realizes that the governess's description of Inez applies equally to herself:
"vague, slippery, inexact, interested only in the personal" [009-013]. She
recognizes that "until now, she, Laura, had been satisfied to know things in a
slipslop, razzle-dazzle way" [009-013].

This moment of self-recognition leads Laura to reform her study habits. She
begins "to sharpen her wits, to follow the strait road" [009-014], applying
herself to factual learning with a new rigour.

## Manner and Delivery

Miss Hicks's voice is noted to have a distinctive quality: when she grew angry,
"her voice had a way of failing, at the crucial moment, and flattening out to
nothing—just as if one struck tin after brass" [009-014].
