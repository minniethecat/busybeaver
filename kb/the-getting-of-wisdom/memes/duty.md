# Duty

Duty is a recurring theme in *The Getting of Wisdom*, operating at multiple
levels: the institutional duties of school life, the moral duties imposed by
social convention, and the personal duties felt by [[laura-rambotham]] toward her
family.

## School Duty

At the [[ladies-college]], duty structures daily life. The junior governess is
"on early duty" [007-001], responsible for waking the boarders at six o'clock.
Morning prayers are attended in a "bounden-duty kind of way" [005-040] by
students who perform the motions without genuine devotion. The narrative captures
the mechanical quality of institutional obligation, where outward compliance masks
inward detachment.

## Moral Duty and Tale-Bearing

The concept of duty takes on a moral dimension in the episode of the school theft.
Laura, having witnessed suspicious behaviour, feels it is "the tale-maker's
bounden duty to do" [017-067] what is expected — to report what she has seen. This
framing of duty as a moral imperative creates tension between loyalty to peers and
obligation to authority.

## Duty and Family

Laura's sense of duty toward her [[mother]] and siblings in [[prahran]] informs
her behaviour at school. The sacrifices made for her education create an implicit
obligation to succeed, adding pressure to her academic and social performance.

## Duty versus Inclination

Throughout the novel, Laura navigates the conflict between what she feels she
ought to do and what she wishes to do. The teachers "feel it a duty to clip and
prune at her" [022-032], while Laura resists the constraints that duty imposes.
This tension between personal desire and social obligation is one of the novel's
central conflicts.
