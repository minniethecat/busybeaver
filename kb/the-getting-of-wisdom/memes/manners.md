# Manners

Manners in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]] operate as a hidden curriculum at [[ladies-college]] — a system of codes and prohibitions that the school enforces but never explicitly teaches. For [[laura-rambotham]], the demand to behave in a "ladylike" manner becomes a persistent source of anxiety, as the standard of correct conduct proves to be both exacting and opaque. Richardson uses the motif of manners to explore the intersection of [[class]], [[gender]], and institutional power in colonial Australian society.

## The Rambotham Family's Pride in Manners

Laura arrives at the Ladies' College confident in her family's standards of conduct. The Rambothams, despite their limited means, pride themselves on their breeding. Mother is described by the school staff as possessing "ladylike manners" that "soon made them feel at home with her" [[003-041]]. This maternal model establishes manners as an inheritance — something passed down within the family and worn as a mark of distinction.

The family's self-image is built on the conviction that good manners compensate for modest circumstances:

> "if there was one thing she, Mother, all of them, prided themselves on, it was the good manners that had been instilled into them since their infancy." — [[004-048]]

This passage captures the Rambotham creed: manners as moral capital, a resource that class position cannot easily supply but family discipline can. The phrase "instilled into them since their infancy" emphasizes the depth and duration of this training — manners are not acquired but absorbed, part of the very fabric of selfhood.

## The School's Correction

The Ladies' College immediately challenges Laura's confidence in her manners. [[mrs-gurley]], the headmistress, delivers a devastating public rebuke:

> "The first thing, miss, for you to do, will be, to take a course of lessons, in manners. Your present ones, may have done well enough, for the colony." — [[007-006]]

The reprimand is devastating on multiple levels. It publicly shames Laura before her peers. It dismisses the Rambotham family's most cherished possession — their manners — as provincial and inadequate. And it introduces a colonial dimension: the school, representing English metropolitan standards, judges colonial manners as inherently deficient. The phrase "for the colony" is a casual cruelty that links Laura's social failure to the broader cultural insecurity of [[australian-identity]].

The rebuke initiates Laura into a world where manners are a moving target. She can never be certain she has mastered the code because the code is never explicitly stated. As the narrator observes after a later transgression:

> "No one of course vouchsafed to explain her the nature of her offence." — [[009-056]]

The word "vouchsafed" is ironic: those who possess the knowledge of correct conduct guard it jealously, treating it as a mystery into which the unworthy cannot be initiated. Laura has committed "a twofold breach of school etiquette" [[009-056]], but she must deduce the nature of her offense from the silence that surrounds it.

## The Internalization of Manners

The demand for correct manners extends beyond outward behavior to prescribed emotional responses. Laura is expected not merely to act correctly but to feel correctly:

> "she did not feel as she ought to feel, in a proper, ladylike way — and especially did she seem quite unable to work herself up into a romantic mood." — [[012-038]]

This passage reveals that "ladylike" is not only a behavioral standard but an affective one. Laura's emotional life is subject to the same disciplinary pressure as her table manners and her posture. The phrase "work herself up" suggests the performative labor required — correct feeling is something to be produced, not spontaneously experienced.

The cumulative effect of this pressure is a divided self. Laura learns to maintain a surface of compliance while her inner life diverges increasingly from the required model. The narrator notes the deterioration:

> "The amenity of her manners suffered, too: though she kept up appearances as best she could." — [[021-002]]

The word "amenity" — suggesting pleasantness, agreeableness — captures what manners are supposed to produce: a frictionless social surface. When Laura's manners "suffer," what is lost is not merely correctness but the appearance of ease, the quality that makes social interaction comfortable for others.

## The Return Home and the Shifting Standard

The instability of the manners code becomes most apparent when Laura returns home. [[mother]], the very source of Laura's original training in manners, now finds her daughter wanting:

> "You used to have nice, ladylike manners. Now, you've no more idea how to behave than a country bumpkin." — [[024-014]]

Laura is caught between two incompatible standards. The school has reshaped her conduct, but the transformation has rendered her unacceptable to the very family that sent her there. Mother's accusation that Laura can no longer distinguish "between being polite and telling stories" [[024-016]] crystallizes the moral problem at the heart of manners: when does social performance become deception?

The novel's most devastating summary of Laura's predicament comes near its close:

> "Was it then impossible, they asked themselves, for Laura Rambotham to do anything in a decorous and ladylike way? Must she at every step put her foot through the brittle web of convention?" — [[024-025]]

The image of the "brittle web" captures the paradox of manners in Richardson's world. The code is fragile — a single misstep can tear it — yet it is also a web, an entanglement from which escape is impossible. Laura's tragedy is not that she lacks manners but that she cannot inhabit them naturally. She experiences the code as an external imposition rather than an internalized second nature, and this gap between performance and selfhood marks her as perpetually suspect.

## Manners and the Social Order

Richardson's treatment of manners connects individual behavior to the broader structures of [[social-expectations]] and [[class]]. The demand to behave "in a ladylike way" — a phrase that recurs throughout the novel [[006-033]] — merges gender discipline with class aspiration. For a young woman of Laura's station, situated precariously between the genteel poverty of her family and the solid middle-class world of the school, manners are a form of survival. They are the currency in which social acceptance is purchased, and Laura, for all her efforts, never quite accumulates enough.

The novel's closing wisdom is bleak: manners, the very quality the Rambothams most prized, turn out to be a game whose rules are determined by those already in possession of power. Learning this truth is part of the education in disillusionment that constitutes Laura's getting of [[wisdom]].
