# Fitzroy Gardens

The Fitzroy Gardens are a notable public garden in East Melbourne, adjacent to the Ladies' College in Henry Handel Richardson's "The Getting of Wisdom." Though referenced only once in the novel, the gardens contribute to the distinctive sense of place that marks Laura's first experience of the city.

## In the Novel

The Fitzroy Gardens appear in a single, vivid descriptive passage when Laura leaves the College for her first monthly holiday with Marina, her godmother's daughter. Walking through East Melbourne, Laura takes in the scene: "Round them stretched the broad white streets of East Melbourne; at their side was the thick, exotic greenery of the Fitzroy Gardens; on the brow of the hill rose the massive proportions of the Roman Catholic Cathedral" (PN 008-010). The gardens' "thick, exotic greenery" is juxtaposed with the ordered whiteness of the streets and the imposing architecture of the cathedral, creating a layered urban landscape. The word "exotic" is significant — for Laura, coming from the dry rural landscape of Warrenega, this lush, cultivated vegetation is as foreign and impressive as the city itself.

## The Colonial Garden

The Fitzroy Gardens belong to a particular nineteenth-century colonial type: the public garden as transplanted European landscape. Their "exotic" greenery — a word that, for Richardson's contemporary readers, would have carried the full weight of imperial botany — signals vegetation imported from elsewhere: English oaks, European shrubs, species that required the careful irrigation and cultivation absent from the native Australian bush. The gardens are not an Australian landscape but a European one reconstructed on Australian soil.

This horticultural displacement parallels Laura's own situation at the College. She too has been transplanted, taken from the "native" landscape of Warrenega and set down in cultivated Melbourne soil. The gardens' thriving exoticism — their success at being not-native — offers an implicit model for what Laura is being asked to become: a successfully transplanted colonial subject, thriving in imported soil.

## A Threshold Space

The Fitzroy Gardens occupy a significant spatial position in the novel: they lie at the boundary between the College enclosure and the wider city. Laura glimpses them not from within the school grounds — she is inside the College walls for almost all of her daily life — but during a moment of licensed departure, her first monthly holiday. The gardens are thus associated with the threshold between institutional confinement and urban freedom, between the all-female world of the school and the mixed, public world of the street.

That the gardens appear only once in the novel, and never as a place the girls enter or occupy, underscores their function as scenery rather than setting. They are seen, not inhabited — a visual marker of the cultivated world beyond the College walls, a world Laura may observe but does not yet belong to. In this respect the gardens are kin to all the novel's outdoor spaces: framed by Laura's perception, colored by her yearning, and always at one remove from her grasp.
