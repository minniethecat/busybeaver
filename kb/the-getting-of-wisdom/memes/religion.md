## Religion as Institutional Routine

> We have prayers in the morning and the evening and prayers again when the day scholers come. — Laura's letter to Mother, [[Ch-V]]

Religious observance structures the school day at the Ladies' College. Morning and evening prayers bracket the boarders' waking hours; a third prayer session follows the arrival of day-scholars. Sunday brings the extended ritual of church attendance, Bible class, and the week's longest stretch of supervised idleness.

Laura describes these routines matter-of-factly in her letters home — they are conditions of school life, neither embraced nor resisted, simply endured. The novel's treatment of religion is notable for its absence of genuine spiritual content: characters pray, sing hymns, and memorize verses, but no character experiences religious feeling.

## Mrs. Gurley's Evangelical Judgment

> Had she denied religion altogether, the look Mrs. Gurley bent on her could not have been more annihilating. — [[004-052]]

When Laura, new to the school, innocently suggests she might attend a different church "for a change," [[mrs-gurley]]'s response is annihilating. The episode is revealing: religion at the College is not about belief but about conformity and institutional authority. Laura's casualness about denominational affiliation is read as a challenge to Gurley's jurisdiction.

The irony is layered: Laura is not irreligious — she says her prayers, she knows her Bible verses — but religion as Gurley administers it is a test of submission, not faith. The "annihilating" look punishes not heresy but independence.

## The Evening Hymn Ritual

> Let us sing in the hundred and fifty-seventh hymn. — [[Ch-VII]]

The evening hymn is a set piece of institutional theater. [[miss-chapman]] plays the piano "as indecisive as her manner"; the Principal reads Paul's epistles "to a continual nervous movement of the left leg"; an extempore prayer drones on while senior girls whisper and Miss Zielinski startles at a mouse. The scene is Richardson at her most satirical: religious ritual emptied of reverence, performed by rote.

After the prayer, Mr. and Mrs. Strachey "bowed vaguely in several directions" — a gesture that captures the perfunctory quality of the entire exercise. The teachers advance with open Bibles, and the girls recite verses they have memorized minutes before.

## Laura's Personal Prayers

> Tossing off her clothes, she gabbled ardently through her own prayers, drew the blankets up over her head, and pretended to be asleep. — [[Ch-V]]

Laura's private religious practice is distinguished from the institutional kind by its sincerity — she prays "ardently" — but also by its brevity. She "gabbles" through prayers as she might gabble through a lesson; religion has not penetrated her inner life in any meaningful way. The prayer she says when Mrs. Gurley confronts Lilith Gordon is described as "a little prayer" — a superstitious reflex rather than an act of faith.

## The Hollow Close

> Settle down to practise religion after the glib and shallow mode in which it was cultivated by those around her. — [[024-053]]

In the novel's closing chapters, Laura contemplates the life ahead of her and imagines herself "settling down to practise religion" in the manner of her peers. The verb "practise" is chosen with care: religion is a performance, a social competence to be acquired like piano-playing or deportment. The "glib and shallow mode" in which it is cultivated around her is both an indictment of the school's religious culture and a recognition that Laura will likely conform to it.

Richardson's final word on religion is characteristically double: the religious framework of the College is hollow and coercive, yet Laura, having survived it, will probably replicate it. The "getting of wisdom" includes the knowledge that institutions survive not through belief but through performance.
