# Performance

Performance is the governing metaphor of social life in *The Getting of Wisdom*. From Laura Rambotham's first day at the Ladies' College to the final Speech Day that closes her school career, the novel presents social existence as a continuous performance — a series of roles assumed, rehearsed, and enacted before an audience of peers whose judgment is absolute and whose approval is never secure. Laura's education is, above all, an education in performance: she must learn to play the part of the young lady, the accomplished student, the desirable friend, the dutiful daughter — and to manage the gaps between these roles and the self that performs them.

## The College as Theater

The Ladies' College is structured as a theater of social performance. The dining hall, the classrooms, the dormitories, the garden — each space is a stage on which the girls enact their social identities before an audience of their peers. The novel's attention to gesture, costume, and setting — Laura's clothes, her blushes, the arrangement of bodies at meals and in recreation — reflects this theatrical understanding of school life. To be seen is to be judged; to be judged is to be found wanting or, occasionally, to be approved.

The College's formal events — Speech Day, the annual concert, the examination results — are performances in a more literal sense, but the novel insists that these formal performances are merely intensifications of the everyday theatricality that governs school life. Every meal, every study hour, every conversation in the garden is a performance, and the stakes are always the same: social survival.

## Laura as Performer

Laura approaches the College as a stage on which she must perform without a script. She does not know the lines, cannot read the cues, and misjudges the audience. Her early attempts at self-presentation — the stories she tells about her family, the attitudes she adopts toward her schoolwork, the friendships she pursues — are performances that fail, sometimes spectacularly, because she has not yet learned the conventions of the College's social theater.

Over the course of the novel, Laura becomes a more competent performer. She learns what to say and what to conceal, when to speak and when to be silent, how to present a version of herself that will survive the scrutiny of her peers. But this competence comes at a cost: the performance becomes so habitual that Laura herself can no longer distinguish between the role and the self that plays it. The "wisdom" she acquires is, in part, the wisdom of the performer — the knowledge that identity is always, to some degree, a construction, and that authenticity may be the most difficult performance of all.

## The Audience

The novel's treatment of performance is inseparable from its treatment of the social gaze. Laura is watched — by her peers, by the governesses, by Mrs. Gurley, by her mother on her visits home — and her performances are shaped by the demands of these overlapping audiences. The novel's free indirect discourse gives us access to Laura's interior experience, but it also reminds us that this interior experience is constantly shaped by the awareness of being observed.

The most painful moments in the novel are those in which Laura's performance fails — when she is caught in a lie, exposed in a pretense, seen to be something other than what she has claimed. These moments of exposure are the novel's equivalent of the theatrical catastrophe: the moment when the mask slips and the performer stands revealed.

## Authenticity and Its Costs

The novel poses, without fully answering, the question of whether authentic selfhood is possible in a world that demands performance. Laura's moments of genuine spontaneity — her musical gift, her occasional bursts of honest speech, her private griefs — suggest that there is a self beneath the performance, but the novel is skeptical about whether this self can survive in public. To be authentic at the Ladies' College is to be vulnerable; to be vulnerable is to be hurt. Performance, however costly, is also protective.

The novel's final image — of Laura leaving the College, entering a world beyond its walls that will demand new performances, new roles, new scripts — suggests that the problem of performance is not confined to school life. It is, rather, the condition of social existence itself. The "getting of wisdom" is, in the end, the getting of the knowledge that one is always, in some sense, performing — and that the best one can hope for is to perform well enough to survive, and honestly enough to recognize the performance for what it is.
