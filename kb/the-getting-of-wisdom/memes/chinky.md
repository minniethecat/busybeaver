# Laura Chin (Chinky)

Laura Chin — invariably referred to by the nickname "Chinky" throughout the novel — is a student at the Ladies' College whose presence in the narrative illuminates one of the novel's most uncomfortable truths: the casual, unexamined racism of late-Victorian colonial society, reproduced without comment within the microcosm of the school.

## Role in the Novel

Chinky appears as a member of Laura Rambotham's social circle, one of the named schoolmates who populate the novel's middle chapters. Her role is that of a minor character — present in group scenes, mentioned in passing, never the focus of sustained narrative attention. Yet her very ordinariness is significant: she is neither persecuted nor celebrated for her difference, merely absorbed into the school's social fabric under a nickname that marks her Chinese ancestry as her defining characteristic. She first appears by name during an evening of charity sewing [[014-095]]:

> Laura had been at work for weeks on a coarse, red flannel petticoat, and as a rule was under constant reprimand for her idleness. On this night, having separated herself from Tilly, she sat down beside a girl with a very long plait of hair and small, narrow eyes, who went by the name of "Chinky." Chinky was always making up to her, and could be relied on to cover her silence. [[014-095]]

The novel's narrator offers no commentary on this naming practice, presenting it as simply part of the school's social landscape — a narrative silence that is itself a form of historical documentation.

## Character Traits

Chinky's most notable characteristic is her **nickname**, which the novel deploys without apparent irony or critique. In the context of 1890s colonial Melbourne — a city shaped by the gold rushes that had drawn Chinese immigrants to Victoria, and by the racial anxieties that had produced the White Australia policy's precursors — the casual racialization of a Chinese-Australian student is historically legible even as it is ethically troubling. Chinky's acceptance of the nickname, and the school's acceptance of her, suggests a complex social reality: inclusion is possible, but it is inclusion on the majority's terms, marked by a diminutive that ensures difference is never forgotten.

Despite the racialized nickname, the narrative grants Chinky small moments of personhood. She is "rather pretty, in her way" [[015-011]], and her gentle, unassuming nature is established through her interactions with Laura:

> She would have shrunk from putting an open question of this kind to her intimates; but Chinky could be trusted. For she garnered the few words Laura vouchsafed her, as gratefully as Lazarus his crumbs; and a mark of confidence, such as this, would sustain her for days. [[015-005]]

The metaphor of Lazarus and crumbs — the grateful recipient of the smallest kindness — positions Chinky as the perpetual supplicant in her social relationships, her gratitude for Laura's attention a measure of her marginal status.

## Key Scenes

As a minor character, Chinky appears primarily in the background of group scenes — the dining hall, the common room, the school grounds. Her presence is part of the novel's documentary texture, the accumulation of named individuals that gives the Ladies' College its sense of being a real social world rather than a stage for Laura's solitary drama.

In one of her few foregrounded moments, Chinky provides an image for Laura's eyes — "like sloes" [[015-029]] — a small act of imaginative observation that briefly individuates her:

> Laura had already fancied that the boy looked at her with interest. This was not improbable; for she had her best hat on, which made her eyes seem very dark—"like sloes," Chinky said, though neither of them had any clear idea what a sloe was. [[015-029]]

Yet even this moment of individuation is undercut by the phrase "neither of them had any clear idea what a sloe was" — a gentle undermining of Chinky's one attempt at poetic language. Later, when Laura is humiliated at the cricket match, it is Chinky who appears with attempted consolation:

> "Laura … Laura!"—and there was Chinky, in her best white hat. [[015-054]]

## Relationship to Laura

Chinky relates to Laura as one schoolmate among many — neither a close friend nor an antagonist, but part of the social environment that Laura must learn to navigate. Her role in the social circle is explicitly marked as marginal: "Except Chinky—and Chinky did not count" [[015-023]]. This dismissal, presented as Laura's free indirect thought, reveals the social logic of the school: Chinky exists within the group but does not fully belong to it, her presence acknowledged but her significance denied. Laura's unreflective acceptance of this dismissal — and of Chinky's racialized nickname — is part of the novel's unsparing portrait of colonial social norms: even the outsider Laura, so sensitive to her own exclusion, participates without thought in the casual racism that marks another girl as permanently other.

## Significance

Chinky's significance is primarily **historical and critical**. For the contemporary reader, her presence in the novel — and the narrator's untroubled deployment of her nickname — offers a window into the racial attitudes of late-Victorian colonial Australia that the novel does not critique because it cannot see them. She is evidence of what the novel takes for granted: the racial hierarchy that structures colonial society as thoroughly as the class hierarchy that structures the school, and that is as invisible to its beneficiaries as water is to fish. The Chinese presence in the colony — marked by the "leprous Chinaman's hut" [[003-032]] and the "Chinese camps" [[013-020]] that dot the landscape — forms the broader context within which Chinky's nickname is rendered unremarkable. Reading Chinky now requires a double vision — seeing both the character Richardson drew and the world that made such a character possible, unremarked and unremarkable.

## In the Text

References to Chinky appear in the middle chapters among the constellation of named schoolmates. The novel offers no sustained portrait or quoted speech beyond brief exchanges [[015-004]] [[015-005]] [[015-011]] [[015-029]] [[015-054]] [[015-058]] — her presence is documentary rather than dramatic.
