# Mr. Repton

Mr. Repton is the visiting elocution master at the Ladies' College in Melbourne.
He is a minor but memorably drawn character whose physical appearance and rumoured
past provide a note of comic relief in the novel's school setting.

## Teaching at the College

Mr. Repton teaches elocution to Class Two. His lessons consist of reading aloud
in "a sonorous voice" — on the day he appears in the narrative, he is reading a
chapter of *Handy Andy* to the class [012-005]. He "underlined his points heavily"
in performance, though the students regarded his histrionic style with a mixture
of amusement and superiority.

## Physical Appearance and Reputation

Mr. Repton is chiefly distinguished by his legs, which are "generally admitted
to be the handsomest in the school" [012-005]. The girls of Class Two compete for
"the best view of them beneath the desk." The narrator notes the general
"abandonment of attitude" during his lessons, with students lounging and
sprawling in ungraceful postures.

A rumour circulates among the students that Mr. Repton was once an actor — a
report lent weight by his "very curly hair" [012-005]. Class Two enjoys
"picturing the comely limbs in the tights of a Hamlet or Othello," and invents
for him "a lurid life outside the College walls."

The narrator adds a note of irony: despite these romantic student fantasies,
Mr. Repton and his "sonsy wife sat opposite the boarders in church every Sunday
morning, the embodiment of the virtuous commonplace" [012-005].

## Classroom Management

Mr. Repton is described as "not a pattern disciplinarian" [012-005]. His
students feel free to adopt relaxed postures during his lessons. The narrator
also notes his bemusement at the students' reactions to his "friendly overtures,"
which the girls habitually construe "into meaning something they should not mean."
