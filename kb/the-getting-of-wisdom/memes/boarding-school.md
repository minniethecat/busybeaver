# Boarding School

The boarding school is the total institution that contains and shapes Laura Rambotham's adolescence in "The Getting of Wisdom." The Ladies' College is not merely a setting but an active force — a closed society with its own hierarchies, rituals, and punishments that functions as a crucible in which Laura's character is tested, deformed, and ultimately tempered.

## The Ladies' College as Boarding School

The Ladies' College, as depicted in the novel, is a second-rate institution that serves the lower rungs of Melbourne's respectable classes — families who cannot afford the more prestigious Presbyterian Ladies' College but who can nonetheless pay for their daughters to acquire the "accomplishments" that mark a young lady. As a boarding school, it removes its pupils from their families and immerses them in an alternative social world governed by peer reputation, teacherly authority, and the relentless scrutiny of communal life.

The physical layout of the school — dormitories, dining hall, classrooms, gardens — creates distinct zones of surveillance and freedom. The boarders' bedrooms become sites of intimacy and cruelty, confession and exposure; the dining hall is a theater of public ranking, where where one sits and with whom one eats are indices of social standing; the classrooms are contested spaces where academic performance and social performance intersect, often to the detriment of the former.

## Boarding School Life

The novel renders boarding-school life with ethnographic precision: the "between breakfast and church" intervals (PN [[008-050]]), the supervised "stroll about in Sunday fashion" (PN [[008-054]]), the evening study hours, the letters to and from home, the sharing of food parcels, the rituals of bedtime and rising. Each of these routines constitutes what Erving Goffman would later theorize as the "mortification of the self" in total institutions — the process by which an individual's prior identity is stripped away and replaced with one defined by the institution's categories.

For Laura, this process is especially brutal. She arrives with the identity of a clever, cherished, unconventional child and is rapidly re-categorized as a poorly dressed, socially awkward, country girl whose mother takes in sewing. The boarding school's tendency to amplify social differences — to make visible the economic hierarchies that might be less obvious in a day-school setting — makes it the perfect narrative engine for Richardson's class critique.

## Significance

The boarding school in "The Getting of Wisdom" is a microcosm of colonial Melbourne society, replicating in miniature the class distinctions, gender expectations, and religious observances of the adult world. It is also, paradoxically, a site of freedom — a place where Laura escapes her mother's anxious love and the limitations of rural Warrenega, and where she discovers, however painfully, the contours of her own character. The school's double function — as prison and as portal — makes it one of the novel's most richly ambivalent creations.
