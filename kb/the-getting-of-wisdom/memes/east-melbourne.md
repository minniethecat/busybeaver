# East Melbourne

East Melbourne is the inner-city neighborhood in which the Ladies' College is situated in Henry Handel Richardson's "The Getting of Wisdom." It provides the immediate physical setting for the school, framing both the constrained routine of the boarders and Laura's fleeting moments of liberty.

## In the Novel

East Melbourne first appears as the backdrop to Laura's first walk outside the College gates, when she departs with her godmother's daughter Marina for a monthly holiday. "Round them stretched the broad white streets of East Melbourne; at their side was the thick, exotic greenery of the Fitzroy Gardens; on the brow of the hill rose the massive proportions of the Roman Catholic Cathedral" (PN 008-010). The description is one of the novel's rare landscape passages, capturing the ordered spaciousness of the suburb — a world utterly unlike Laura's country home.

The streets of East Melbourne also frame the boarders' daily routine. Their supervised exercise takes the form of "the winding of the snaky line about the streets and parks of East Melbourne, which constituted the boarders' daily exercise" (PN 012-022). This image of a long, serpentine column of identically dressed girls winding through the suburb's grid illustrates the institutional discipline that governs every aspect of their lives.

## Key Passages

> Round them stretched the broad white streets of East Melbourne; at their side was the thick, exotic greenery of the Fitzroy Gardens. — (008-010), the neighborhood revealed in Laura's first moment of freedom

> It was too late after this for the winding of the snaky line about the streets and parks of East Melbourne, which constituted the boarders' daily exercise. — (012-022), the daily ritual that maps school discipline onto the suburb's geography

## The Architecture of Respectability

East Melbourne's physical description — "broad white streets," "massive proportions of the Roman Catholic Cathedral" — is not neutral observation but a vocabulary of colonial respectability. The whiteness of the streets suggests order, cleanliness, and prosperity; the cathedral's massiveness speaks to institutional permanence. For Laura, arriving from a rural landscape of hills and gullies, this built environment is itself a kind of education: it teaches her, wordlessly, what the city values and how it arranges its hierarchies in stone and asphalt.

The architecture of East Melbourne thus mirrors the architecture of the College: both are structures designed to shape behavior through spatial arrangement. The broad streets impose a grid logic on movement — one walks along prescribed paths — just as the College's timetable imposes temporal order on each day. The suburb and the school are continuous: both are sites of discipline, one civic and one institutional, both dedicated to producing orderly colonial subjects.

## Freedom and Its Boundaries

Laura's experience of East Melbourne on her first monthly holiday is described through the paradoxical phrase "a prisoner at large" — language that captures the suburb's dual nature. She is free of the College walls, walking the broad streets without supervision, yet she is not truly free. She remains within the College's sphere of influence, visible to anyone who might report on her behavior, bound by the expectations of conduct that the suburb itself embodies.

The "snaky line" of boarders on their daily walk (PN 012-022) is the visual emblem of this bounded freedom: the girls move through public space but remain tethered to the school, their serpentine formation a living chain that connects them back to the College gates. East Melbourne's streets are thus neither wholly public nor wholly private — they form a zone of supervised release, a space where the school's authority extends beyond its walls through the mechanism of the supervised walk.
