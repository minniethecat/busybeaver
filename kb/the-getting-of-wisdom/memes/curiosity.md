# Curiosity

**Curiosity** is a central psychological motif in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]], manifesting as a double-edged trait in the protagonist [[laura-rambotham]]. Throughout the novel, curiosity oscillates between the intellectual aspiration encouraged by education and the "morbid curiosity" that the school environment fosters in its students. Laura is described from the outset as "always a very inquisitive little girl" [013-023], and her "straight, inquisitive black eyes" [008-042] serve as a physical marker of her probing nature.

## Intellectual Curiosity and Its Frustration

Laura arrives at Ladies' College with a natural intellectual curiosity, but the educational system fails to nurture it. The narrator observes:

> [009-012] … and, worst of all, you've no curiosity—about anything that really matters.

This critique, directed at Laura by her own conscience, reflects the school's inability to cultivate genuine intellectual engagement. Laura's realization that "she, too, had no intelligent curiosity" [009-013] marks a moment of painful self-awareness about the limitations of her education. Crucially, the narrator distinguishes between mere inquisitiveness and genuine intellectual engagement: "she was incapable of a vulgar inquisitiveness. But Laura, though she lacked the intelligence to know this, was not" [009-013] — she understands there is a higher form of curiosity she has not been taught to access.

The narrator concludes bluntly:

> [009-014] Intellectual curiosity could not, she discovered, be taught.

This statement encapsulates the novel's critique of rote learning and its failure to inspire authentic intellectual growth. The verb "discovered" positions this insight as Laura's own hard-won realization rather than an externally imposed judgment. It is a discovery about the limits of institutional education — what schools can transmit (facts, manners, examination techniques) versus what they cannot (genuine intellectual engagement, the desire to know for its own sake). Laura's conversation with a fellow student at a later point in the novel, where she is described as having "a very inquiring mind, and seemed always trying to learn something" [015-025], shows that her natural inquisitiveness persists despite the school's stifling methods, but it remains unfocused and untrained.

## Morbid Curiosity and Social Surveillance

In contrast to intellectual curiosity, the social environment at Ladies' College breeds what the narrator calls "a morbid curiosity" [007-054]. The girls' intense interest in one another's backgrounds, secrets, and scandals becomes a form of social surveillance. Laura's own eyes are described as "saucers of curiosity" [013-007] as she absorbs the gossip and social dynamics around her.

This morbid curiosity is explicitly linked to the culture of secrecy and scandal at the school. The narrator describes how rumors "travelled, burning with curiosity, from Annie Johns to Mr. Strachey" [012-016], showing how curiosity functions as the engine of the school's gossip economy. The metaphor of burning — "burning with curiosity" — suggests both intensity and destructiveness: curiosity here is not an illuminating flame but a consuming one. The public nature of school discipline exacerbates this dynamic: Laura is acutely aware of "advertising to the curious what had taken place" [009-052] whenever a student's transgression becomes known.

The distinction between intellectual and morbid curiosity maps onto the novel's broader critique of the school's moral education. Where intellectual curiosity might lead students toward genuine knowledge and self-understanding, the school environment channels their natural inquisitiveness into the surveillance of one another's moral failings. Curiosity becomes not a path to wisdom but a tool of social control.

## Curiosity and the Stranger's Gaze

The novel extends the motif of curiosity beyond the school walls. When Laura first travels to Melbourne, a fellow passenger's "good-natured curiosity, however, was not to be done" [003-006] — a benign but intrusive form of interest that mirrors the school's own culture. The qualifier "good-natured" is significant: curiosity in the novel is rarely neutral, always carrying a moral valence. Even well-intentioned curiosity can feel invasive to its object.

More pointedly, when Laura faces "the cold and curious looks that met her" [005-035] upon first entering the school dining hall, curiosity becomes indistinguishable from judgment and exclusion. The adjective "cold" strips curiosity of its association with warmth and interest, revealing its capacity to objectify and alienate. Laura, already self-conscious about her social position, experiences the curiosity of others as a form of hostile appraisal.

## Natural Curiosity and Coming of Age

The novel also depicts a "ferment of natural curiosity" [013-024] that characterizes the girls' passage through adolescence. This natural inquisitiveness about relationships, identity, and the adult world is neither wholly intellectual nor morbid, but forms a necessary part of their maturation. The word "ferment" suggests something alive, bubbling, organic — a natural process that the school's artificial constraints cannot entirely suppress.

Laura turns "incuriously away" [012-038] only when something seems impossible to her; otherwise, her curiosity is relentless, driving her to seek out knowledge: "curiosity got the better of her, and she set out in quest of her" [019-004] describes an episode where Laura's need to know overcomes hesitation. The phrase "got the better of her" positions curiosity as a force stronger than Laura's own will — something that possesses her rather than something she chooses.

The novel's treatment of curiosity culminates in its depiction of how the adult world regards this youthful inquisitiveness. The school authorities observe Laura's romantic attachments "with an amused curiosity. The governesses also smiled" [023-024] — a condescending perspective that suggests curiosity, like the girls themselves, is something to be managed rather than nurtured. The governesses' "amused" distance mirrors the novel's own ironic narrative voice, which observes Laura's intensities with a mixture of sympathy and detachment.

## Curiosity and Laura's Dignity

A subtle but significant aspect of the novel's treatment of curiosity is its relationship to Laura's sense of self. When confronted, "Laura's face took on a curious air of dignity" [003-010] — the word "curious" here operates in both its meanings: strange and inquisitive. Laura's dignity, like her curiosity, is something she must construct for herself, and the curious quality of that dignity suggests its fragility in an environment that values conformity over inquiry.

This doubleness — curiosity as both strength and vulnerability — extends throughout the novel. Laura's inquisitive nature makes her perceptive, imaginative, and alive to the world in ways her more complacent classmates are not. But it also exposes her to social injury, makes her the object of others' morbid interest, and leaves her perpetually unsatisfied with the answers the school provides. The novel ultimately suggests that curiosity, like the [[wisdom]] of its title, is something one must learn to bear rather than something one simply acquires.
