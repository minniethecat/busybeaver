# Tilly

Tilly is a schoolmate at the Ladies' College who occupies a distinctive position in Laura Rambotham's social world — neither an intimate friend nor an antagonist, but a consistent presence whose occasional kindness and whose cousin Bob provide pivotal moments in Laura's education in the codes of school society.

## Role in the Novel

Tilly appears primarily in the middle chapters of the novel, associated with the social events and excursions that punctuate school life. She is notable for being one of the few girls who does not actively participate in Laura's social exclusion — though neither does she champion her cause. Her cousin Bob, introduced through her, becomes the agent of one of Laura's most painful experiences: the embroidery incident in which Laura's mother's occupation is exposed to male scrutiny (PN [[014-081]]). Tilly thus functions as a bridge figure — connecting Laura to the world beyond the school's walls, for better and worse.

## Character Traits

Tilly is characterized by a kind of unassuming ordinariness that sets her apart from the novel's more vividly drawn personalities. She is neither a leader like Lilith Gordon nor a beloved figure like Evelyn Souttar; she occupies the middle ground of school society, friendly to Laura without being her champion, present at key moments without driving them. This quality — her ordinariness — is itself thematically significant: in a novel populated by extremes of cruelty and kindness, Tilly represents the vast middle of social life, the people who are not the source of our deepest wounds or our greatest joys but who nevertheless shape our experience through their quiet presence.

## Key Scenes

**The Social Gatherings**: Tilly appears in the middle chapters as part of the social fabric of the school, participating in the excursions and informal gatherings that form the texture of Laura's school life. Her presence is noted but not emphasized — she is part of the background against which Laura's more dramatic relationships unfold.

**The Bob Incident (Ch. XIV)**: Tilly's cousin Bob, introduced during a school outing, becomes the agent of one of Laura's defining humiliations. When Bob discovers Laura's embroidered handkerchief — a product of her mother's needlework — and shares this knowledge in a way that exposes Laura's family circumstances, the shame that Laura has worked so hard to conceal becomes public (PN [[014-081]]). Tilly's connection to Bob makes her, however unwittingly, the conduit through which Laura's private shame enters the male social world — a moment that crystallizes the novel's treatment of class, gender, and the inescapability of origins.

## Relationship to Laura

Tilly relates to Laura with a casual friendliness that is neither the intense attachment Laura craves nor the active cruelty she fears. For Laura, who experiences social relationships primarily through the extremes of passion and mortification, Tilly's ordinariness is both a relief and a disappointment — proof that friendship can be unremarkable, and that unremarkable friendship is not what Laura is looking for. Tilly's cousin Bob, by contrast, becomes a figure of pure threat — the male gaze that Laura has been taught to fear, now trained on the evidence of her family's poverty.

## Significance

Tilly functions thematically as a representative of the novel's quieter social truths. Not every relationship is transformative; not every person is a lesson. Tilly is there, and her being there matters — not as a dramatic force but as part of the social ecosystem in which more dramatic forces operate. Through Bob, she also serves as a reminder that the novel's social world extends beyond the school's walls: the boys, the families, the networks of kinship and acquaintance that connect the enclosed world of the Ladies' College to the larger colonial society in which it is embedded.

## In the Text

> The child felt, would not have shamed a Bishop, the Principal drew near and addressed her. — [[012-021]], a rare moment of adult acknowledgment in which Tilly's ordinary presence is subsumed into the larger social scene
