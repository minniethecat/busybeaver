# Sydney

Sydney appears as a fleeting toponym in "The Getting of Wisdom," mentioned as the onward destination of the English mail steamer that Laura Rambotham watches from the beach during the Christmas holidays. The reference is incidental to geography but significant to the novel's thematic patterning of exclusion and longing.

## In the Novel

The mention occurs in Chapter XIX, as Laura lies on the beach during the summer break, daydreaming and composing her romantic fictions. She watches a ship on the horizon and imagines it sailing "to some unknown, fairer world," before puncturing her own fantasy with the deflating recognition that "it was only the English mail going on to Sydney" (PN [[019-060]]). This brief geographical name-drop does double work: it grounds the scene in the actual shipping routes of colonial Australia — where the English mail steamer would call at Melbourne before proceeding to Sydney — and it reinforces the Cinderella motif that runs through Laura's consciousness, her sense of being left behind while others journey toward brighter destinations.

## Key Passage

> Sometimes she rose to her elbow to follow a ship just inside the horizon; and it pleased her to think that this great boat was sailing off, with a load of lucky mortals, to some unknown, fairer world, while she, a poor Cinderella, had to stop behind — even though she knew it was only the English mail going on to Sydney. — [[019-060]]

## Significance

Sydney's function in the novel is almost entirely symbolic. As a named destination beyond Melbourne — further along the route, more distant — it represents the world of movement and possibility from which Laura, lying immobilised on the sand, feels cut off. The juxtaposition of romantic fantasy ("some unknown, fairer world") with mundane reality ("only the English mail") is characteristic of Richardson's ironic treatment of Laura's imaginative excesses, and the name "Sydney" anchors this irony in a specific Australian geography.

## Historical Context

In the 1890s, the Melbourne–Sydney steamer route was a well-established colonial shipping lane. The "English mail" referred to the regular steamship service carrying postal mail from Britain to the Australian colonies, typically calling at Albany, Adelaide, Melbourne, and Sydney. For Richardson's contemporary readers, "the English mail going on to Sydney" would have been a familiar and unremarkable piece of maritime routine — which is precisely the point of the deflation.
