# The Getting of Wisdom

"The Getting of Wisdom" is the novel that contains all the characters, events, and themes documented in this wiki. Published in 1910, it is the second novel by Henry Handel Richardson (the pen name of Ethel Florence Lindesay Richardson) and remains one of the enduring works of Australian literature — a sharp, psychologically acute portrait of a girl's education in the social codes of late-Victorian colonial society.

## About

The novel was first published in 1910 by William Heinemann in London, after Richardson had established her literary reputation in Europe with "Maurice Guest" (1908). It draws heavily on Richardson's own experiences as a student at the Presbyterian Ladies' College in Melbourne from 1883 to 1887 — a biographical connection that places the novel within the tradition of semi-autobiographical fiction that was gaining prominence in the Edwardian period. The novel follows Laura Tweedle Rambotham from her rural home through her years at an elite boarding school, charting her struggles with social conformity, her musical talent, and her slow, often painful acquisition of the "wisdom" of the title — a wisdom that turns out to consist largely of learning to dissemble and compromise.

The novel is structured around the school year, with chapters corresponding to terms, examinations, holidays, and the social rhythms of boarding-school life. Its narrative perspective stays closely aligned with Laura's consciousness, rendering her experiences with an immediacy that was unusual for the period and that anticipates the free indirect discourse of modernist fiction. The novel's 25 chapters trace a trajectory from innocence through experience to a qualified, ironic maturity — a structure that both honors and subverts the Bildungsroman tradition.

## In the Novel

As the containing work itself, "The Getting of Wisdom" is the ultimate source of every passage, character reference, and thematic observation in this wiki. It spans approximately two years in Laura's life, beginning with her departure from her country home and ending with her departure from the school — a narrative arc that maps a complete cycle of arrival, education, and release. The cast includes over thirty named characters, from the formidable Mrs. Gurley to the mercurial Evelyn Souttar, and the settings range from the claustrophobic interiors of the Ladies' College to the open streets of Melbourne and its suburbs.

## Literary Style and Technique

Richardson's style in this novel is notable for its **ironic distance**: the narrator observes Laura's passions and humiliations with a cool clarity that neither sentimentalizes childhood nor dismisses its real pain. The prose is precise and observational, owing something to the naturalist tradition that Richardson absorbed during her years in Germany, where she studied music and encountered the European literary avant-garde. The novel's use of **free indirect discourse** — blending Laura's perceptions with the narrator's judgments — creates a double vision that is the book's signature effect: we feel Laura's mortification from the inside even as we recognize, from the outside, its place in a larger social pattern.

The novel's **structure** is episodic rather than tightly plotted, reflecting the rhythms of school life — terms, examinations, holidays — rather than a conventional dramatic arc. This episodic quality, which some contemporary reviewers criticized as formless, now reads as one of the novel's strengths: it captures the way childhood time is experienced as a series of discrete intensities rather than a continuous narrative.

## The Nietzsche Epigraph

The novel bears an epigraph from Nietzsche's "Thus Spoke Zarathustra": "Wisdom is a woman and loves only a warrior." This epigraph, placed at the head of the novel, signals Richardson's engagement with the European philosophical currents that were transforming literary culture at the turn of the century. The Nietzschean reference frames Laura's story not as a sentimental tale of girlhood but as a contest — a struggle for self-mastery in which the "getting" of wisdom requires not passive receptivity but active conquest. The irony, of course, is that Laura is no warrior: her weapons are evasion, fantasy, and music, and her victories are ambiguous at best.

## Connection to Themes

The novel's title is multiply ironic. What Laura "gets" is not wisdom in any classical or philosophical sense, but rather a sharp education in the gap between appearance and reality, self and society, aspiration and achievement. The central themes — **education as socialization**, **the cost of conformity**, **the role of art (music) as both escape and achievement**, **colonial identity formation**, **the economy of shame and pride** — are all refracted through Laura's experience. The novel participates in the tradition of the Bildungsroman but subverts its central premise: Laura does grow, but what she grows into is not transcendent self-knowledge but a pragmatic accommodation to social reality. The novel's final image — Laura watching the Trinity men at the dock — leaves her future radically open: she has acquired neither wisdom nor happiness, but she has acquired the capacity to want something beyond what the school could offer.

## Critical Reception and Legacy

At its publication in 1910, "The Getting of Wisdom" received respectful but not effusive reviews. It was praised for its psychological acuity and its vivid rendering of school life, but its episodic structure and ironic tone puzzled critics accustomed to more sentimental treatments of childhood. The novel's reputation grew slowly through the twentieth century, aided by the rising prestige of Australian literature and the postwar reevaluation of women's writing. It is now widely regarded as a classic of Australian fiction and a foundational text in the tradition of the girls' school story — though one that, characteristically for Richardson, both honors and subverts the conventions of the genre.
