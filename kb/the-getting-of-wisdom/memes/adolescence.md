# Adolescence

Adolescence in "The Getting of Wisdom" is not merely a biographical stage but the novel's essential subject. It is the period of life when the self becomes visible to itself as a social object, subject to the judgment of others, and when the gap between who one is and who one wishes to be becomes the central existential problem.

## The Adolescent Condition

Richardson's novel captures adolescence with a psychological acuity that was unusual for its time. Laura's experience — the alternation between grandiosity and self-loathing, the desperate need for peer approval, the fabrication of alternative selves, the confusion of religious feeling with romantic longing — anticipates the developmental psychology that would not be formalized until decades later.

The novel understands adolescence as a period of radical instability: Laura is never quite one thing or another, but always in transition between states, and the instability is both her suffering and her potential.

The Ladies' College provides the ideal setting for an anatomy of adolescence because it concentrates the social pressures that define the adolescent experience. Removed from her family, surrounded by peers who serve as both audience and judges, Laura is forced to construct a social self under conditions of maximum visibility. Every gesture, every item of clothing, every story told about one's family is subject to the scrutiny of a public that is at once intimate and pitiless. This is adolescence as a theater of exposure, and Richardson's rendering of it — the blushes, the stammered lies, the "hourly dread" of discovery — is as precise as it is painful.

## The Body

The novel attends to the adolescent body with a frankness that borders on the transgressive for its period. Laura's physical self-consciousness — her awareness of her clothes, her complexion, her "ears and cheeks on fire" (PN [[014-038]]) — is a constant presence in the narrative. The body in adolescence is a traitor: it blushes when one wishes to appear composed, it wears the wrong clothes when one wishes to appear fashionable, it betrays one's origins and one's anxieties in ways that words can be marshaled to conceal. Laura's body is also the site of her musical gift — the hands at the piano, the ear that hears with "instinctive rightness" — and this doubleness captures the novel's understanding of adolescence as a period when the body is simultaneously a source of shame and a vehicle of authentic expression.

## Social Experimentation

Laura's adolescence is characterized by a series of experiments in identity construction. She tries on different selves — the sophisticated young lady, the devout church girl, the romantic heroine, the intellectual aspirant — with an energy that is both creative and desperate. Each experiment is tested against the reactions of her peers and modified accordingly; none is entirely successful, but the cumulative effect is the construction of a self that, however compromised and defensive, is recognizably Laura's own.

The novel's understanding of adolescence as a period of identity experimentation — of trying on selves until one finds one that fits, or until one exhausts the possibilities — anticipates the developmental theories of Erik Erikson and his successors by half a century.

## Significance

"The Getting of Wisdom" is one of the earliest novels in English to take female adolescence seriously as a literary subject. Richardson's achievement is to render the adolescent experience without the condescension that characterized much contemporary fiction about young women — the assumption that the storms of adolescence are merely a phase to be outgrown. Instead, the novel insists on the formative significance of adolescent experience: the self that emerges from the Ladies' College is not the "real" self that was temporarily obscured by adolescent turbulence but a new self, constructed from the materials that adolescence provided, for better and worse. The "wisdom" of the title is adolescent wisdom — the knowledge that comes from having survived the passage from childhood to adulthood with enough of the self intact to tell the story.
