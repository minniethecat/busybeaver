# Presbyterian Ladies' College

The Presbyterian Ladies' College (PLC) in East Melbourne is the historical institution behind the fictional Ladies' College of "The Getting of Wisdom." Richardson's attendance there as a boarder from 1883 to 1887 provided the experiential foundation for the novel's most indelible creation: the school as a total institution, a theater of social cruelty, and the unlikely forge of female consciousness.

## Richardson at PLC

Ethel Florence Lindesay Richardson (who would later publish as Henry Handel Richardson) entered PLC as a boarder at the age of thirteen, following her family's move from various Victorian country towns to Melbourne. Like her fictional counterpart Laura Rambotham, Richardson was a gifted musician whose talent provided both a source of distinction and a mark of difference from her more conventionally accomplished classmates. Also like Laura, Richardson came from a family of precarious respectability — her father, a doctor, had died of syphilis when she was nine, leaving the family in reduced circumstances that made her position at an elite school perpetually anxious.

The novel's famous epigraph from Nietzsche — "Whoever is to attain to wisdom must first suffer the pain of disillusion" — speaks to the autobiographical dimension of Richardson's material. She knew, from the inside, what it meant to be the clever, unconventional, socially insecure girl in an institution designed to produce young ladies of a very different type.

## The School

PLC was founded in 1875 as one of Melbourne's earliest secondary schools for girls, part of the wave of institution-building that followed the Victorian Education Act of 1872. Unlike the fictional Ladies' College — which the novel takes pains to distinguish from "the other, the real, the original Ladies' College" (PN [[004-003]]) — PLC was a genuinely prestigious institution, associated with the Presbyterian establishment that dominated Melbourne's commercial and professional elite. Richardson's transformation of PLC into the second-rate Ladies' College of the novel was an artistic choice rather than a documentary record: the fictional school's mediocrity sharpens the novel's satire of colonial aspirations and makes Laura's social vulnerability more acute.

## The Transformation

The relationship between PLC and the novel's Ladies' College is a study in the alchemy of autobiographical fiction. Richardson preserves the school's essential character — its social hierarchies, its curriculum of "accomplishments," its religious observances, its complex ecology of teachers and students — while altering its prestige, its specific location, and many of its personnel. The result is a fictional institution that is more effective as a vehicle for social critique than a faithful portrait would have been: the Ladies' College's second-rate status makes visible the class anxieties that a genuinely elite school might have rendered invisible through sheer social confidence.

## Significance

PLC's transformation into the Ladies' College exemplifies Richardson's method as a novelist: the transmutation of lived experience into fiction through strategic alteration and heightening. The school that emerges in the novel is not PLC but something more — an institution that is also an argument, a place whose specific details (the classrooms, the gardens, the Sunday books, the thin ceilings) serve a vision of colonial society that is at once intimate and comprehensive. For readers interested in the biographical background of "The Getting of Wisdom," PLC is the necessary starting point; for readers interested in the novel as art, the distance between PLC and the Ladies' College is the measure of Richardson's creative achievement.
