## The Schoolgirl Romantic Culture

> Such was Class Two's youthful contribution to the romance of school life. — [[012-005]]

The Ladies' College harbors an elaborate romantic subculture. Girls cultivate crushes on the curate, on visiting masters like Mr. Repton (whose handsome legs are the object of "enthusiastic admiration"), and on each other's male relatives. This romantic culture is at once innocent and knowing — the girls are "hungry for romance" yet "bluntly" realistic about the practical conduct of their lives [[017-065]].

Richardson captures the doubleness of adolescent romance: it is both deeply felt and self-consciously performative, a rehearsal for adult life conducted with the earnestness of children who know they are playing at being adults.

## Bob: The Romantic Object

[[bob]], Tilly's handsome cousin, is the novel's primary romantic catalyst. With his "liquid eyes" and "blanched-almond teeth," Bob represents a kind of masculine beauty that Laura has not previously encountered in her provincial world. Her infatuation with him — brief, intense, humiliating — follows the classic arc of adolescent romance: idealization, pursuit, rejection.

> Having once seen him close at hand, she knew how desirable he was. — [[015-003]]

The Bob episode is significant partly because it fails. Laura does not know how to perform the romantic role expected of her — she cannot flirt, cannot deploy feminine charm, cannot make herself interesting to a young man. Romance, like school, demands skills she has not been taught.

## Romance as Fiction

> It should be a romance of Venice, with abundant murder and mystery in it, and a black, black villain, such as her soul loved. — [[021-009]]

Laura's story for the [[literary-society]] is explicitly a romance — "a romance of Venice, with abundant murder and mystery." The story's success suggests that Laura's facility with romantic narrative exceeds her facility with romantic experience. She can invent compelling fictions of love and danger; she cannot, or will not, live them.

The novel thus draws a sharp distinction between romance as literary genre and romance as lived experience. Laura's triumph in the former domain only highlights her struggle in the latter.

## Romance and Truth

> As soon as you put pen to paper, provided you kept one foot planted on probability, you might lie as hard as you liked: indeed, the more vigorously you lied, the louder would be your hearers' applause. — [[021-044]]

The novel's most radical insight about romance concerns its relationship to truth. In conversation, romance — "drawing the longbow" — is punished as lying. On paper, it is rewarded as art. This double standard illuminates Laura's predicament: the qualities that make her a gifted storyteller are the same qualities that make her a social failure.

## Romance as Horizon

> The thoughts of all were fixed, with an intentness that varied only in degree, on the great consummation which, as planned in these young minds, should come to pass without fail directly the college-doors closed behind them. — [[015-023]]

For Laura's schoolfellows, romance — specifically marriage — is the horizon toward which all their education tends. An engaged classmate is "looked up to with tremendous reverence, as one set apart, oiled and anointed." Laura is a heretic in this regard: she cannot limit her hopes to marriage, cannot see romance as life's culminating event. The future she imagines is vast and indeterminate — "it might contain anything — from golden slippers to a Jacob's ladder."
