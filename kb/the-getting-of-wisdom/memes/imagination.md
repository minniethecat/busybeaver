# Imagination

**Imagination** is a defining trait of [[laura-rambotham]]'s character in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]], functioning simultaneously as her greatest gift and her most dangerous liability. Throughout the novel, Laura's "imagination had wreaked itself" [010-009] on the world around her, transforming mundane school life into rich narrative but also leading her into the moral ambiguities of [[deception]]. The narrator explicitly notes that Laura and her siblings are "the daughters of an imaginative mother" [010-009], suggesting that this trait is inherited and that its sources lie in the family home rather than the school.

## Imagination as Compensation

Laura's imaginative faculty develops partly as compensation for the limitations of her circumstances. The narrator observes the discrepancy between Mother's idealized vision and reality: "it had crumbled" [009-013] — referring to the educational aspirations that existed "except in Mother's imagination." Imagination here is associated with hope and aspiration, but also with a certain unreliability. What exists only in imagination is, by definition, not real — and the novel is persistently attentive to the gap between imagined and actual circumstances.

> [010-009] …balked in other outlets, this imagination had wreaked itself on their creators.

This passage reveals the compensatory nature of Laura's imagination: denied other forms of expression and recognition, her creative faculty turns inward and finds outlets in storytelling and fabrication. The verb "wreaked" is significant — imagination is not a gentle solace but a force that acts upon its possessor with a kind of violence. It is something done *to* Laura, not simply something she does. The word "balked" further emphasizes the frustration that channels imagination into its more destructive forms: imagination becomes an outlet for energies the school's narrow world cannot otherwise accommodate.

## The Power of Imagined Reality

The novel makes a startling claim about the causal power of imagination. Laura makes "the following discovery: that, if you imagine a thing with sufficient force, you can induce it to happen" [016-002]. This almost magical belief in the efficacy of imagination reveals both Laura's psychological intensity and her tendency to blur the boundary between inner and outer reality. The same passage also shows the limits of this power — imagination can induce but not guarantee outcomes.

Laura's belief in imagination's causal power connects to the novel's broader interest in the relationship between mental states and material outcomes. Her academic success or failure is repeatedly linked to her imaginative projections: "in imagination she saw the fifty-five gaining" [018-044] — the examination score she desperately needs. Imagination here functions as a form of visualization, a mental rehearsal of desired outcomes. But the novel is too psychologically acute to endorse any simple "power of positive thinking" message; Laura's imagined victories often fail to materialize, and the gap between what she imagines and what she achieves is one of the novel's recurring sources of pathos.

## Fancy and Embellishment

The novel uses "fancy" as a related but lighter form of imaginative activity. Laura can "embellish them in fancy" [003-041] — her stories and her sense of self — and the narrator notes that she gives "rein to her fancy" [011-058], picturing idealized futures. Laura is described as capable of trimming "the edge of her tale with a few fancy stitches, in a way she had not been above doing" [022-035], showing that even as she matures, her impulse to imaginatively embellish persists. This aspect of imagination links it to Laura's fabrications and the culture of storytelling at the school.

The metaphor of "fancy stitches" — drawn from needlework — domesticates imagination, positioning it as a feminine craft rather than a dangerous faculty. Yet the narrator's observation that Laura had "not been above doing" this suggests a moral judgment: embellishment, even in its lighter forms, shades into dishonesty. The novel never resolves the tension between imagination as creative gift and imagination as moral failing, leaving the reader to navigate the same ambiguity that Laura herself cannot escape.

## Imagination and Moral Warping

The novel does not treat imagination as innocent. The narrator explicitly links Laura's imaginative faculty to moral distortion:

> [021-002] …trifles warped both her imagination and her sympathies: under the…

The verb "warped" is crucial — imagination here is not neutral creativity but a force that can distort perception and sympathy. This darker aspect of imagination connects to Laura's fabrications and the [[secret]] culture at Ladies' College. The preoccupation with "trifles" suggests that the school's narrow social world channels imagination into morally dubious channels rather than healthy creative expression.

The pairing of "imagination and her sympathies" is particularly significant: what warps one warps the other. Imagination and sympathy are linked faculties in the novel's moral psychology — the ability to imagine others' experiences is the foundation of compassion. When imagination is warped by trivial concerns, sympathy is warped alongside it. The novel thus offers an implicit argument for why the content of imaginative life matters: imagination trained on "trifles" produces moral distortion, while imagination directed toward genuine human complexity might produce genuine understanding.

## Imagination versus Unimaginative Dullness

The novel presents the absence of imagination not as neutrality but as a moral failing. The narrator speaks of "that intolerance, that unimaginative dullness, which makes a person incapable of seeing any side of a question but one" [019-009]. Here, lack of imagination is directly equated with intolerance — an inability to perceive complexity or empathize with others. Laura, for her part, feels contempt for "her unimaginative way of seeing and saying things" [020-016] in others, valuing imaginative perception as a superior mode of engagement with the world.

This defense of imagination as a moral faculty — as the basis of tolerance and perspective-taking — is one of the novel's most sophisticated arguments. Richardson anticipates twentieth-century psychology's insight that empathy requires imagination: to understand another person's experience, one must be able to imagine it. The "unimaginative dullness" the narrator condemns is not merely an aesthetic failing but an ethical one: people who cannot imagine others' perspectives cannot treat them justly.

## Imagination and Ambition

Laura's imagination also fuels her [[ambition]]. The narrator notes that "in imagination she saw the fifty-five gaining" [018-044] — her creative faculty projects her into desired futures, visualizing academic success. This forward-looking imagination sustains her through the disappointments of school life but also sets her at odds with the reality of her social position. The gap between imagined futures and real outcomes becomes one of the novel's sources of pathos.
