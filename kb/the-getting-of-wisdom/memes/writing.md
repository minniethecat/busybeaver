# Writing

Writing in "The Getting of Wisdom" is never a neutral act. Whether Laura is composing letters home, copying out sermons in her best hand, or penning the prescriptions of school rules, the act of writing becomes a medium through which her identity is produced, judged, and disciplined by others.

## In the Novel

The novel opens with a written artifact: Mother's writing on a piece of paper (PN [[002-015]]), establishing writing as a medium of maternal authority and connection. Laura's own writing quickly becomes a site of institutional concern — Maria Moreton writes to Mother expressing shame at Laura's "nonsense" (PN [[006-015]]), and Laura herself is "quite capable of writing posthaste to Mrs. Gurley or Miss Chapman" (PN [[009-004]]), suggesting writing as a vehicle for complaint and self-advocacy. In a rare moment of pleasure, Laura "had even enjoyed writing, in her best hand, the prescriptions" of school rules (PN [[009-017]]), her careful penmanship a performance of the compliance she cannot genuinely feel. The sermon-copying episode reveals writing as discipline: Robby "can't read his own writing" (PN [[016-037]]), while Laura watches a sermon being written "like copperplate" (PN [[016-040]]), the beautiful handwriting contrasting with the rote religious content.

Writing thus oscillates between self-expression and self-suppression. Laura's letters home are monitored; her compositions are judged; her handwriting is a tool of institutional discipline. Yet writing also offers one of the few avenues for distinction — a well-written letter, an elegant hand — and Laura, always alert to opportunities for recognition, learns to deploy it strategically.

## Key Passages

> A paper, which bore, in Mother's writing, the words. — [[002-015]], writing as maternal presence across distance

> "I am ashamed of you for writing such nonsense to her." — [[006-015]], the institutional policing of written expression

> She was quite capable of writing posthaste to Mrs. Gurley or Miss Chapman. — [[009-004]], writing as a tool of self-advocacy

> She had even enjoyed writing, in her best hand, the prescriptions of school life. — [[009-017]], the ambiguous pleasure of disciplined writing

## Historical Context

In the Edwardian girls' school, composition and penmanship were central to the curriculum — not merely as skills but as moral disciplines. Beautiful handwriting signaled character; careless writing suggested a careless soul. The monitoring of students' letters home was standard practice, reflecting both protective concern and institutional anxiety about what students might say to parents. Laura's experience of writing as a field of both surveillance and self-display is characteristic of the period's educational culture.
