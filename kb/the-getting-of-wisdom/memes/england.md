# England

England functions in "The Getting of Wisdom" not as a visited place but as a pervasive cultural presence — the distant imperial centre whose maps, church, and traditions structure the world of the Ladies' College. It appears in two distinct contexts: as a geography lesson object and as a religious affiliation.

## In the Novel

England enters the narrative first through religion. Mrs. Gurley, the Principal's wife, corrects Laura's assumption about church attendance: "I myself, am an Episcopalian, and I expect those gels, who belong to the Church of England, to attend it, with me" (PN [[004-053]]). This early exchange situates the Church of England as the default religious identity of the school's establishment — a marker of the Anglican ethos that pervades the Ladies' College as an institution modelling itself on English public-school traditions.

The second and more sustained appearance is the geography-lesson episode in Chapter VII. The pupils are required to copy the outline of the map of England (PN [[007-033]]), and Laura is later called upon to locate Stafford on "a huge map of England" slung over an easel (PN [[007-045]]). She fails spectacularly, standing "stupid and confused" before a map "seared and scored with boundary-lines, black and bristling with names." The irony is pointed: Laura knows England imaginatively — "from hearsay, just how England looked; that she could see, in fancy, its evergreen grass, thick hedges, and spreading trees; its never-dry rivers; its hoary old cathedrals" — but cannot translate this romantic vision into cartographic knowledge. The episode crystallises a central tension of colonial education: a curriculum centred on a country the pupils have never seen, taught through abstractions that have no connection to their lived Australian experience.

## Key Passages

> I myself, am an Episcopalian, and I expect those gels, who belong to the Church of England, to attend it, with me. — [[004-053]], Mrs. Gurley establishing denominational lines

> One day at geography, the pupils were required to copy the outline of the map of England. — [[007-033]], the geography lesson that humiliates Laura

> In this exigency, it did not help her that she knew, from hearsay, just how England looked; that she could see, in fancy, its evergreen grass, thick hedges, and spreading trees; its never-dry rivers; its hoary old cathedrals; its fogs, and sea-mists, and over-populous cities. — [[007-045]], the gap between Laura's imaginative knowledge and institutional demands

## Significance

England in the novel embodies the colonial cultural dynamic that shaped late-nineteenth-century Australian education. The Ladies' College teaches English geography, enforces Anglican observance, and implicitly measures its pupils against an English standard — yet Laura, the country girl from rural Victoria, can only access this England through hearsay and imagination. Her failure at the map is not intellectual inadequacy but a failure of translation: the "green grass, thick hedges, and spreading trees" of her mental picture belong to a different kind of knowledge than the "boundary-lines, black and bristling with names" demanded by the classroom. Richardson, who herself attended Presbyterian Ladies' College in Melbourne before studying music in Leipzig, knew firsthand the colonial subject's ambiguous relationship to English cultural authority.
