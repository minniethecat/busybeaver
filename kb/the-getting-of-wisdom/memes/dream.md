# Dream

The imagery of **dreams and dreaming** pervades [[the-getting-of-wisdom]], serving as the novel's primary metaphor for Laura Rambotham's inner life. Dreams function in three distinct registers: as imaginative escape, as nocturnal terror, and as a mode of perception that blurs the boundary between inner and outer reality.

## The Sea of Dreams

The novel's dream imagery begins with imaginative transport. Laura, embarking on the journey to school, "embarked thus upon her sea of dreams, Laura set sail and was speedily borne away" (PN=003-041). This "sea of dreams" is a space of freedom — a realm where Laura's imagination can operate without the constraints of social reality. The metaphor recurs when Laura passes through unfamiliar streets "in a kind of dream" (PN=004-012), suggesting that the school world itself has a dreamlike quality, unreal and disorienting.

## The Recurring Nightmare

Dreaming takes its darkest form in Laura's recurring nightmare of expulsion. She starts up "with a cry, from dreaming that not Annie Johns but she was being expelled; that an army of spear-like first fingers was marching towards her" (PN=012-039). The narrator states plainly: "this dream often returned" (PN=012-040). The nightmare consolidates Laura's deepest anxieties — public exposure, social judgment, exclusion — into a single image of pointing fingers and marching persecution. It returns again: "she had the familiar dream that she was being 'stood up' and expelled, as Annie Johns had been: thousands of tongues shouted her guilt; she was hunted like a wallaby" (PN=018-045). The escalation from "spear-like fingers" to "thousands of tongues" and being "hunted like a wallaby" shows how Laura's social anxiety intensifies over time.

## Dream as Dislocation

In the novel's closing section, dream imagery marks Laura's disorientation as she leaves school. "The first few days were like a bad dream" (PN=024-019), suggesting that the transition from school to the wider world is itself a kind of nightmare. The history she studies seems populated by "faraway, dreamlike countries" (PN=024-046), as if the solid world of facts and dates has dissolved into the insubstantiality of dream. This framing connects the school experience to a broader meditation on reality and perception: Laura's entire education has been, in some sense, dreamlike — a constructed world that she must now leave behind.

## Dreaming and Social Ambition

The novel also uses dream in a more conventional sense — as daydream or aspiration. Laura can "indulge such a dream" (PN=011-058) only in private moments, when the pressure of social performance is suspended. The connection between dreaming and ambition is implicit: to dream is to imagine a self beyond one's current circumstances. But the novel remains skeptical about whether such dreams can be realized — Laura's dreams of social acceptance are repeatedly undercut by reality. In the same passage describing Miss Chapman's private ambitions (PN=011-058), the narration reveals that "thoughts of this kind sank to their proper level" when Mrs. Gurley appeared, suggesting that dreams and social reality exist in permanent, uneasy tension.

## Key Passages

> The novel's opening dream metaphor: Laura "embarked thus upon her sea of dreams, Laura set sail and was speedily borne away" (PN=003-041)

> The recurring nightmare crystallizes: "she started up with a cry, from dreaming that not Annie Johns but she was being expelled; that an army of spear-like first fingers was marching towards her. And this dream often returned." (PN=012-039–012-040)

> The nightmare intensifies: "she had the familiar dream that she was being 'stood up' and expelled, as Annie Johns had been: thousands of tongues shouted her guilt; she was hunted like a wallaby" (PN=018-045)

> After leaving school, reality dissolves into nightmare: "The first few days were like a bad dream" (PN=024-019)

> The wider world becomes unreal: history study takes place in "faraway, dreamlike countries" (PN=024-046)

## Characters

**Laura Rambotham** is the sole dreamer in the novel — her dreams, both waking and sleeping, provide the reader with unique access to her subconscious. The recurring nightmare reveals her deepest fear: social exclusion and public judgment. **Annie Johns** appears within the nightmare as a symbolic figure — the girl who was actually expelled, whose fate Laura fears will become her own. **Miss Chapman** provides a counterpoint: her private daydreams of ambition (PN=011-058) mirror Laura's own.

## Historical Context

Richardson's use of dream imagery reflects the early twentieth-century literary interest in psychological realism and the subconscious. Published in 1910, the novel predates the full flowering of Freudian influence on literature, but its treatment of the recurring nightmare as a window into repressed anxiety participates in the era's growing fascination with the inner life. The school setting intensifies the psychological pressure: in the enclosed world of Ladies' College, Laura has no escape from the social dynamics that generate her nightmares.

## Critical Perspective

The dream motif in *The Getting of Wisdom* has been read as part of Richardson's broader project of representing female interiority. Where the male coming-of-age novel of the period often focuses on external adventure and achievement, Richardson turns inward, using dream and nightmare to map the emotional landscape of a girl's education. The recurring nightmare of expulsion can be read as a specifically female anxiety — the fear of being judged, exposed, and cast out from a community, which parallels the social policing of female behavior in Edwardian society.
