# Ambition

Ambition is a central thematic concept in *The Getting of Wisdom*, manifesting
in both the protagonist [[laura-rambotham]]'s personal aspirations and her
[[mother]]'s hopes for her daughter's social advancement.

## Mother's Ambitions

Laura's mother harbours grand ambitions for her daughter's future. The narrator
notes that "Mother's ambitions knew no bounds" [009-004], reflecting a driving
force behind Laura's placement at the prestigious [[ladies-college]]. These
aspirations are shaped by the family's modest circumstances in [[prahran]] and the
mother's desire for Laura to transcend them.

## Laura's Personal Ambition

Laura's own ambition is first mentioned in connection with a "former ambition"
[004-011], suggesting childhood dreams that predate her school experience. At the
College, her ambition takes on new forms — she aspires to literary achievement
and social acceptance among her peers.

The narrative notes that despite the
"stillness of the great house, her ambition did not seem wholly out of reach"
[011-058], indicating the tension between aspiration and reality.

## Ambition and Social Class

Laura's ambitions intersect with questions of [[social-expectations]] and class.
Her desire to rise socially is complicated by her awareness of her family's
position. The theme echoes the novel's broader exploration of social mobility in
colonial Melbourne.

## Ambition as Moral Question

The novel treats ambition with ambivalence. While Laura's aspirations are
presented sympathetically, the text also hints at the costs of unchecked ambition
— particularly the strain it places on her relationship with her family and her
own sense of [[identity]].
