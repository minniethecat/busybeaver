# Forgiveness

Forgiveness in *The Getting of Wisdom* is less a practice than an absence — the thing that is needed but not given, the moral repair that the novel's social world cannot provide. Laura Rambotham's guilt over the Annie Johns affair, her strained relationship with her mother, her betrayals of and by her friends — these injuries accumulate without resolution, and the novel offers no easy redemption. Forgiveness, when it appears at all, appears obliquely: in a moment of unexpected kindness, in the simple passage of time, in the quiet recognition that certain wounds cannot be healed but can be borne.

## The Unforgiven Injury

The Annie Johns episode is the novel's central case study in the problem of forgiveness. Laura's satirical expulsion letter contributes to Annie's actual expulsion — an injury for which Laura never apologizes and for which she is never forgiven. The guilt persists, surfacing in dreams and in moments of anxious recollection, but there is no mechanism in the novel's social world for addressing it. Annie disappears from the narrative; Laura is left with a guilt that has no object and therefore no resolution.

The novel's refusal to provide closure for the Annie Johns episode is thematically significant. Forgiveness, in Richardson's world, is not something that can be assumed or expected. It requires a confrontation between perpetrator and victim that the social circumstances of the College do not permit. Laura and Annie never speak after the expulsion; the injury stands, unaddressed and irreparable, as a permanent feature of Laura's moral landscape.

## Religious Forgiveness and Its Limits

The novel's treatment of religious forgiveness is characteristically ambivalent. Laura prays, attends church, and is exposed to a Christian framework in which forgiveness is central — "forgive us our trespasses, as we forgive those who trespass against us." But this framework proves inadequate to the social realities of the College. Religious forgiveness is abstract, cosmic, disembodied; what Laura needs is forgiveness from the specific person she has injured, and that forgiveness is not forthcoming.

The novel's skepticism toward religious solutions is consistent with its broader treatment of organized religion. Prayer offers Laura comfort but not absolution; church attendance provides structure but not moral guidance. The gap between religious doctrine and social practice — between the ideal of forgiveness and the reality of unforgiven injury — is one of the novel's quiet but persistent themes.

## Forgiveness and Time

If forgiveness does not arrive through confrontation or religion, the novel suggests, it may arrive — or something like it — through the passage of time. Laura's guilt over Annie Johns does not disappear, but it recedes; the intensity of her remorse diminishes as new experiences overlay the old. Time does not heal the wound, exactly, but it renders it bearable — a scar rather than an open injury.

This is the novel's most realistic treatment of moral repair. In the absence of institutional or religious mechanisms for forgiveness, time does what it can. Laura does not become innocent; she becomes, simply, older — and the wisdom of age consists, in part, of learning to live with the knowledge of one's own capacity for harm.

## Forgiveness and Love

The novel's treatment of maternal forgiveness offers a partial counterpoint to the social unforgiveness of the College. Laura's mother, for all her faults — her "grand ambitions," her sharp tongue, her inability to understand her daughter's inner life — offers a form of love that absorbs injury without demanding reparation. The mother's love is not forgiveness in any formal sense, but it is a kind of acceptance that the College's social world cannot provide.

This maternal acceptance, however imperfect, is the closest the novel comes to a model of forgiveness. It is not earned or deserved; it is simply given, as part of the brute fact of familial love. Whether Laura recognizes this gift, or can accept it, is another question — and one the novel leaves open.
