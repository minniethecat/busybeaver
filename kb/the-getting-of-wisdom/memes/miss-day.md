# Miss Day

Miss Day is a teacher at the Ladies' College whose presence in the novel is defined by surveillance and judgment. With her "stiff, black hair" and "lynx-eyes" (PN [[005-073]]), she represents the institutional gaze under which Laura and her schoolmates live — watching, assessing, and occasionally intervening in their conduct. Unlike the more sympathetic or distant authority figures in the novel, Miss Day is persistently present, a figure whose authority is exercised not through grand pronouncements but through observation, sniffing, and the quiet deployment of disapproval.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Day first appears in Chapter 5 as one of the teachers who presides over the classroom where Laura endures her early social trials. When asked about Laura's situation, Miss Day responds with studied indifference: "I'm sure I don't know" (PN [[005-039]]). This non-answer — delivered without malice but without warmth — establishes Miss Day's characteristic mode: she is not cruel, but she is not kind; she is simply the institution made flesh, concerned with order and propriety above individual feeling.

Her role extends across multiple chapters as a figure of surveillance. She stands "on the first landing, watching with lynx-eyes to see that none of the girls lingered" (PN [[005-073]]). She "spies" Laura (PN [[007-008]]) and "demands an explanation" — always watching, always requiring accounts. When Laura fails to meet institutional standards, it is Miss Day who pronounces judgment: "Well, it looks as if you had never been taught to do anything" (PN [[008-003]]). This sentence, delivered as observation rather than cruelty, captures the particular wound that institutional authority can inflict — a wound made not of anger but of the calm assumption of inadequacy.

## Character Traits

Miss Day is defined by a physicality that mirrors her institutional function. Her "stiff, black hair" (PN [[005-039]]) suggests rigidity; her "full bust" (PN [[007-012]]) gives her judgments a physical weight; her "sniff" (PN [[010-036]]) is a compressed form of moral judgment, requiring no words. Richardson draws her as a figure whose body and role are perfectly aligned: she is the school's disciplinary function given human form, her physical presence as unyielding as the rules she enforces.

Yet there are moments when Miss Day's humanity flickers through the institutional mask. When she observes Laura's poorly mended clothes, she " exclaimed in horror at the condition they were in" (PN [[010-033]]). The horror is genuine — a moment when professional scrutiny gives way to something closer to personal feeling. And when she is "on leave" (PN [[011-038]]), her absence is felt as a kind of liberation by the students — proof that even Miss Day, for all her institutional solidity, is a person with a life beyond the school's walls.

## Key Scenes

**The Landing Vigil (Ch. V)**: Miss Day stands "on the first landing, watching with lynx-eyes to see that none of the girls lingered" (PN [[005-073]]). This image — the teacher as watcher, the landing as panoptic position — is one of the novel's quiet emblems of institutional power. Miss Day does not need to speak or punish; her presence alone, her watching, is enough to shape behavior.

**The Pronouncement (Ch. VIII)**: Examining Laura's work, Miss Day delivers the crushing assessment: "Well, it looks as if you had never been taught to do anything" (PN [[008-003]]). The sentence is devastating precisely because of its calm — it is not an accusation but a finding, delivered with the impersonal authority of someone who simply states what is. For Laura, who has come to the school precisely to be taught, the comment strikes at the foundation of her aspirations.

**The Night Off (Ch. XI)**: Miss Day's absence — "Miss Day was also on leave" (PN [[011-038]]) — creates a different kind of space in the school, one in which the usual surveillance is suspended and the remaining authority figures operate with a lighter touch. Her absence is itself a character note: the school without Miss Day is a different school, proof of how thoroughly her presence defines its atmosphere.

## Relationship to Laura

Miss Day relates to Laura not as an individual but as an instance — a student to be watched, assessed, and corrected. For Laura, who craves individual recognition (even negative recognition), Miss Day's impersonal gaze is both a wound and a lesson. It teaches Laura that institutions do not love or hate — they simply observe and judge according to their own internal standards. The question Miss Day implicitly asks of Laura is not "who are you?" but "do you meet the standard?" — and Laura, for much of the novel, does not.

Yet Miss Day is not simply an antagonist. Her vigilance, however uncomfortable, is part of the structure that makes the school function. Laura's education is not only in Latin and literature but in learning to live under institutional authority — and Miss Day is her primary instructor in that second, unspoken curriculum.

## Significance

Miss Day functions thematically as the human face of institutional authority. She is not evil or corrupt; she is simply doing her job as she understands it, which is to maintain order and enforce standards. The novel's treatment of her is notably even-handed — Richardson allows Miss Day her moments of genuine concern (the horror at Laura's mending) alongside her moments of cold judgment. In this, Miss Day represents a truth about institutional life: that the people who enforce its rules are themselves shaped by those rules, their humanity both constrained and expressed through the roles they occupy.
