# Australian Identity

Australian identity in "The Getting of Wisdom" is less a theme than an atmosphere — the unspoken condition that shapes the novel's characters, institutions, and values without ever becoming an explicit subject of discussion. Richardson, writing in 1910 about the 1890s, captures a colonial society at the moment of its anxious self-formation, suspended between English models and Australian experience.

## The Colonial Frame

The novel's characters live in a society that is structurally derivative. The Ladies' College curriculum is English; the books the students read are English; the "Home" to which the colonial elite orient themselves is England. The school's religion is the Church of England; its social codes are imported from English models of gentility; its aspirations — for its students, for itself — are measured against English standards. Yet the novel is set in Melbourne, not London, and its characters are Australian, not English. The gap between the cultural model and the lived reality produces an undertow of anxiety that the novel registers without ever foregrounding. The students themselves are identified simply as "these young colonials" [[009-020]] [[017-065]], their very designation marking the derivative status of their society. In the elocution lesson, Class Two is described as "the self-conscious, emotionally shy young colonials they were" [[012-005]] — self-consciousness and emotional shyness being precisely the marks of a people not yet at ease in their own cultural skin.

Laura's own ambiguous class position — respectable enough for the Ladies' College but not respectable enough to be safe there — mirrors the colony's own ambiguous status. Australia is not England, however much it may wish to be; it is a society of recent settlement, recent wealth, and uncertain status, where the markers of class and breeding that function automatically in the metropolitan context must be anxiously produced and policed.

## The Cultural Cringe

The phrase "cultural cringe" was coined decades after Richardson's novel, but the phenomenon it describes is already fully formed in "The Getting of Wisdom." The colonial subject who measures herself against metropolitan standards and finds herself wanting — this is Laura's experience in miniature, and it is also the experience of the society that produced both Laura and the school that judges her. The novel's satire of the Ladies' College is, at a deeper level, a satire of a society that has invested so heavily in the project of being English that it has forgotten how to be itself.

> The air, Australian air, met them like a prickling shower-bath. [[014-023]]

The very air the characters breathe is marked as Australian — the national adjective attached to the atmosphere itself, as though even the natural world must carry the label of its colonial identity. When the schoolgirls attend the cricket match, Richardson observes them as "a representative gathering of young Australia, bathed in the brilliant March light" [[015-026]] — the schoolgirls are not merely spectators at a game but representatives of a nation in the making.

> In the girls' eyes, Mr. Strachey stood over six feet in his stocking-soles. He had also a most arrogant way of looking down his nose, and of tugging, intolerantly, at his long, drooping moustache. There was little need for him to assume the frigid contemptuousness of Mrs. Gurley's manner: his mere presence, the very unseeingness of his gaze, inspired awe. He quelled the high spirits of these young colonials by his dignified air of detachment. [[009-020]]

## An Australian Literature

"The Getting of Wisdom" is itself an assertion of Australian cultural identity — a novel that takes Australian experience seriously as literary material, that renders colonial Melbourne with the same ethnographic precision that an English novelist might bring to London or the provinces. Richardson, who left Australia as a young woman and wrote the novel in Europe, was engaged in the quintessential colonial literary act: writing "home" from a distance, transforming the experience of colonial childhood into art that could stand alongside the metropolitan tradition it both emulated and challenged. The novel's Nietzsche epigraph [[012-002]], with its cosmopolitan intellectual credentials, is a declaration that an Australian novel can traffic in the same ideas as its European counterparts — that the story of a schoolgirl in Melbourne is as worthy of serious literary treatment as the story of a young man in London or Paris.

> For, crass realists though these young colonials were, and bluntly as they faced facts, they were none the less just as hungry for romance as the most insatiable novel-reader. [[017-065]]

## Significance

Australian identity in the novel is not a theme to be analyzed but a condition to be inhabited. Richardson does not tell us what it means to be Australian; she shows us what it feels like to live in a society that is still in the process of becoming itself, where identity — personal and national — is less an inheritance than a project. The novel renders this condition through its characters' designation as "colonials" [[009-020]] [[012-005]] [[017-065]] and through the Australian air itself [[014-023]] — a national atmosphere that is both natural and culturally constructed. In this, "The Getting of Wisdom" is not only a novel about growing up but a novel about the growing up of a culture — a coming-of-age story in which the protagonist and her society are engaged in the same painful, uncertain work of self-construction.
