# Failure

**Failure** recurs throughout [[the-getting-of-wisdom]] as both an event and a self-definition. Laura Rambotham experiences failure in multiple domains — social, romantic, academic — and each instance deepens her sense of being fundamentally unsuited to the world she inhabits.

## The Pattern of Failure

Laura's experience of failure is cumulative. After a disastrous social outing, "it came over her with a rush what a miserable failure the day had been" (PN=014-094). The narrator notes that even after the initial sting subsides, "a soreness at her own failure was the abiding result" (PN=015-002). This pattern — failure followed not by recovery but by lingering self-reproach — characterizes Laura's emotional life throughout the novel.

## Romantic Failure

The novel is particularly acute in its portrayal of Laura's failures in the romantic sphere. Despite her careful study of her companions' behavior, "she remained where the other sex was concerned a disheartening failure" (PN=015-024). The word "disheartening" is significant — it suggests not merely that Laura fails, but that her failures progressively erode her confidence. When a classmate witnesses one of her romantic setbacks, Laura responds with coldness, aware that the other girl has "no doubt also been a witness of her failure" (PN=015-056).

## Failure of Moral Courage

Beyond social and romantic failure, Laura repeatedly fails in the moral domain. Her resolve to confess her fabrications collapses: "on Monday her purpose failed her" (PN=018-035), and by Thursday "she not only failed to own up, but indulged anew" (PN=018-038). This moral failure — knowing what is right and being unable to do it — produces the guilt that shadows much of the novel's middle section.

## Academic Failure

The most consequential failure in the novel is academic. Laura "had never before failed to pass an examination" (PN=024-003), but the prospect of failing her final exams threatens everything: "she could not afford in the event of a failure, to pay the school-fees for another year" (PN=024-003). The narrator describes how Laura lies awake "haunted by the prospect of failure" (PN=024-022), turning the pages of her Bible in desperation. This crisis marks a turning point — failure is no longer a social embarrassment but an existential threat.

## Failure and Self-Knowledge

The novel's treatment of failure is ultimately productive. Laura's repeated failures force her toward self-knowledge, however painful. Her inability to project consequences — "her wonted inability to project her thoughts beyond the moment" — means she is "so unthinking of possible failure" (PN=021-009) that each setback arrives as a fresh shock. Learning to anticipate failure, and to live with it, is part of the wisdom she acquires.

## Key Passages

> The abiding sting of social failure: "it came over her with a rush what a miserable failure the day had been... a soreness at her own failure was the abiding result" (PN=014-094–015-002)

> Romantic failure as chronic condition: "she remained where the other sex was concerned a disheartening failure" (PN=015-024)

> Moral failure compounds: "on Monday her purpose failed her... by Thursday she not only failed to own up, but indulged anew" (PN=018-035–018-038)

> Academic crisis: she "had never before failed to pass an examination" and "could not afford in the event of a failure, to pay the school-fees for another year" (PN=024-003)

> Failure and self-ignorance: her "wonted inability to project her thoughts beyond the moment" meant she was "so unthinking of possible failure" (PN=021-009)

## Characters

**Laura Rambotham** is defined by her failures as much as by her successes. Her sensitivity to failure distinguishes her from more resilient characters. **Mother** represents the stakes of failure — her "ambitions knew no bounds" (PN=009-004) and Laura's academic failure would devastate those ambitions. **Evelyn Souttar** provides a counterpoint: her easy success in social and academic spheres throws Laura's struggles into sharper relief.

## Historical Context

The novel's treatment of failure reflects the high-stakes nature of education for girls in late-Victorian society. For a girl like Laura, academic failure was not merely embarrassing — it could determine her entire future, closing off the limited professional and social opportunities available. The examination system, imported from British models, was particularly unforgiving, and the novel captures the psychological toll of this pressure.

## Critical Perspective

Richardson's portrayal of failure has been read as part of her broader critique of institutional education. Laura's failures are not simply personal shortcomings — they reveal the inadequacy of a system that judges all students by the same narrow standard. The novel suggests that the real failure belongs to the school, which cannot accommodate Laura's particular gifts or understand her particular struggles.
