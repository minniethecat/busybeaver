# Identity

Identity is the novel's deepest thematic concern: Laura Rambotham's passage through the Ladies' College is fundamentally a struggle to construct a self that can survive the conflict between who she is (a poor girl from the country, a gifted musician, a mother's daughter) and who she wishes to be (a young lady of accomplishment, a social success, an independent being). The novel traces this struggle with unsparing psychological acuity, showing how identity is forged not in solitude but in the crucible of social relations.

## The Problem of the Self

Laura arrives at the Ladies' College with an identity that is at once coherent and precarious. She is her mother's daughter — clever, musical, cherished — and she is a child of Warrenega, with all the limitations that rural origin implies in the eyes of her Melbourne schoolmates. The collision between her inherited sense of self and the school's social categories produces the novel's central drama: Laura must either remake herself in the image the school demands or find a way to survive with her original self intact.

The novel's answer is characteristically unsentimental: Laura does both and neither. She learns to perform the version of herself that the school requires — fabricating a family history, affecting social confidence, dissimulating about her mother's occupation — but these performances come at a cost. The self that performs is not the self that feels, and the gap between them becomes a source of guilt, anxiety, and, ultimately, a kind of bitter wisdom.

## The False Self

Laura's fabrications are not merely lies; they are experiments in identity construction. When she tells her classmates that her mother is a woman of leisure, that her family has social connections, that Mr. Shepherd is a "personal friend," she is not simply deceiving others — she is trying on a self that might be acceptable, testing whether performance can substitute for substance. The novel's treatment of these fabrications is complex: it condemns them as morally corrosive (the guilt Laura feels is real) while also acknowledging their psychological necessity. In a social world that judges by surfaces, the surface is all one has to work with.

## Artistic Identity

Laura's musical talent offers an alternative route to identity — one based on genuine accomplishment rather than social performance. Her ability at the piano is the one aspect of her self that cannot be reduced to class position: it is real, demonstrable, and recognized by the institution (however grudgingly). The novel's treatment of Laura's musicianship — from her early lessons in Warrenega to her performances at the school concert — traces the emergence of an identity grounded in authentic ability. Yet even artistic identity proves fragile: Laura's failure at the public examination (Ch. XXV) suggests that talent alone cannot secure one's place in a world where social forces are always in play.

## Significance

"The Getting of Wisdom" is, at its core, a novel about the difficulty of being a person — of assembling a coherent self from the conflicting materials of inheritance, desire, and social pressure. Richardson's innovation, in the context of the early-twentieth-century Bildungsroman, is to locate this struggle not in grand existential moments but in the daily texture of school life: the clothes one wears, the letters one writes, the stories one tells about one's family. Identity, the novel suggests, is not discovered but constructed — and the materials available for its construction are never of one's own choosing.
