# Miss Chapman

Miss Chapman is the head governess at the Ladies' College in Henry Handel Richardson's "The Getting of Wisdom" — a figure who embodies the institutional values of the school: propriety, discipline, and a careful hierarchy of feminine accomplishments.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Chapman serves as the primary mediating authority between the students — particularly Laura — and the higher powers of the school (Mrs. Gurley, the principal). She is first introduced when Laura arrives at the college, and becomes a recurring figure in the daily life of the school: supervising meals, overseeing evening study, and adjudicating the small dramas of boarding-school life. She is "neither loved nor feared" (PN [[005-040]]), occupying the uneasy middle ground of a functionary who must enforce rules she did not make. Her role in Laura's story is particularly significant as the gatekeeper of musical performance — it is Miss Chapman who plays the piano accompaniment for school concerts and who can grant or withhold musical approval.

## Character Traits

Miss Chapman is defined by her **propriety and caution**. She "liked to have her opinions corroborated" (PN [[005-060]]) and is more concerned with avoiding disruption than with justice or warmth. She **twiddles her chain** in moments of uncertainty — a nervous gesture that Laura observes and that humanizes the otherwise remote figure (PN [[007-018]]). She has **genuine musical discernment** — her piano playing and her assessment of Laura's musical ability represent perhaps the only arena in which she exercises independent judgment rather than institutional reflex. Her relationship with Laura is ambivalent: she recognizes Laura's talent but is also quick to notice her faults, particularly what she perceives as "laziness" in Laura's approach to non-musical subjects (PN [[007-020]]).

## Key Scenes

**The first tea (Ch. V)** establishes Miss Chapman's role as overseer of school ritual and her position in the social hierarchy: when Laura is uncertain where to sit, the refrain is "you'd better ask Miss Chapman" (PN [[005-037]]–[[005-039]]). **The music examination (Ch. V)** reveals her in her element — at the piano, playing accompaniment for a visiting examiner, competent and absorbed (PN [[005-063]]–[[005-064]]). **Evening supervision (Ch. VII)** shows her vulnerability: "Elderly Miss Chapman twiddled her chain" as she attempts to manage students who have learned exactly how far they can push against her authority (PN [[007-018]]). **Walking duty (Ch. VI)** pairs her with Miss Day as the two governesses shepherd the girls, a ritual of surveillance that defines the school's approach to student freedom (PN [[006-007]]).

## Relationship to Laura

Miss Chapman represents for Laura the ambivalence of institutional approval. She can confer status through musical recognition, but her approval is always conditional — contingent on Laura's compliance with school norms. She is both obstacle and audience: someone Laura must navigate around in daily life, and someone whose opinion of her musical ability matters deeply. The relationship is not warm — Miss Chapman is "elderly," remote, more function than person — but it is consequential. Through Miss Chapman, Laura learns that the adult world's judgment is both powerful and arbitrary.

## Significance

Miss Chapman embodies the institutional type of the governess in late-Victorian colonial education — a figure neither servant nor equal, whose authority derives entirely from the institution she serves. Her characterization illustrates the novel's critique of the school system: that it produces functionaries rather than educators, and that its moral supervision is more concerned with appearances than with genuine development. Her musical ability is the one crack in this institutional facade — a suggestion that beneath the chain-twiddling propriety, there might once have been a person with passions and talents of her own.

## In the Text

> Miss Chapman, the head governess, was neither loved nor feared, which left her in a position of some difficulty with the bolder spirits among the girls. — [[005-040]], introducing Miss Chapman's uneasy middle position in the school hierarchy

> "I don't think she's lazy," said Miss Chapman. "At least she takes great pains with her music." — [[007-020]], Miss Chapman's one consistent defense of Laura
