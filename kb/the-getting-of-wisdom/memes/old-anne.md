# Old Anne

Old Anne is a servant in the Rambotham household, appearing briefly in the novel's early chapters. Her presence marks the Rambothams' position on the lower edge of middle-class respectability — genteel enough to employ domestic help, but not wealthy enough to escape the intimacy of shared domestic space that characterizes their household.

## Role in the Novel

Old Anne functions as part of the domestic landscape of Laura's childhood. In a household without a father, where Mother struggles to maintain appearances on limited means, a servant like Old Anne occupies an ambiguous position — neither family member nor mere employee, but something in between. Her name — "Old Anne" rather than the more formal "Anne" or "Mrs. X" — suggests familiarity and long service. She belongs to the world Laura leaves behind when she departs for the Ladies' College, a world of provincial intimacies that the school's institutional formality will progressively erase.

## Key Passages

> Old Anne surfaces in the domestic context of the Rambotham household — a presence registered more through the texture of daily life than through specific actions or speech

## Significance

Like the other domestic figures in the Rambotham household, Old Anne contributes to the novel's early establishment of Laura's social position. The family has a servant, but not many servants; they are middle-class, but barely; they belong to a world of small-town intimacies that the Ladies' College — with its separate spheres for students, teachers, and servants — will render unrecognizable.
