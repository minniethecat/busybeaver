# Secret

The motif of the **secret** runs through [[the-getting-of-wisdom]] as a structuring principle of the social and moral universe of Ladies' College. From the "sacred secret" of childhood games to the "shameful secret" and "disgraceful secret" that haunt Laura, secrets function as currency in the girls' social economy and as markers of moral transgression.

## Secrets as Social Currency

At Ladies' College, the sharing of secrets creates and tests bonds of intimacy. When a schoolmate tells Laura "Can you keep a secret, Laura?" (016-049), the question is both an overture of friendship and a test of loyalty. The phrase "a dead secret" recurs throughout the novel, as in the urgent whisper: "it's a dead secret; you must swear you won't tell" (017-044). Secrets create an economy of trust in which disclosure and betrayal are constant possibilities.

The sharing of confidences also provokes jealousy, as when Laura reacts furiously to a rival's intimacy:

> [023-023] "What business has she to have secrets with you?" said Laura furiously.

This outburst occurs in the context of Laura's intense, possessive attachment to [[evelyn-souttar]]. When the birdlike Lolo calls Evelyn aside with "It's a secret. You must come over here" (023-009), Laura stands frozen — "all five fingers of her right hand were thrust through the holes of the netting, and held oddly and unconsciously outspread; she stood on one leg, and with her other foot rubbed up and down behind her ankle; mouth and brow were sullen, her black eyes bent wrathfully on her faithless friend" (023-012). The secret is not merely excluded knowledge but a visible wound — the physical detail of Laura's posture renders her exclusion as bodily as it is emotional.

## The Shameful Secret and Moral Weight

Several characters carry secrets with heavy moral weight. Bertha is described as having "the shameful secret in the background, of a mother" (007-061) — a disclosure that marks social difference and vulnerability. The vagueness of the phrase ("of a mother" — what about the mother?) mirrors the school's culture of knowing silence: everyone knows something is wrong, but no one speaks it plainly.

Laura herself harbors a "disgraceful secret" after failing her examinations:

> [024-003] Mother did not know or understand anything about "tests"; and Laura had no idea of enlightening her. She held her peace, and throughout the holidays hugged her disgraceful secret to her, untold.

The verb "hugged" is striking — the secret is not merely concealed but held close, a source of private misery that Laura will not or cannot share. The narrator details the material stakes of this secret: "The money which, by hook and by crook, had been scraped together and hoarded up for Laura's education was now coming to an end" (024-003). The secret is not merely emotional but economic: to reveal it would be to expose the fragility of the entire Rambotham enterprise.

## The Sacred Secret of Childhood

The novel's earliest reference to secrets appears in a childhood context — "the sacred secret, and forbidden formula" (002-033) — suggesting that the ritual of secret-keeping begins in the imaginative play of childhood. This early pattern prefigures the more consequential secrets of adolescence, where the stakes shift from play to survival.

The childhood pattern of secret-sharing also carries forward into Laura's creative life. As the narrator notes, Laura "bosomed herself of dark little secrets; and finally did what she would" (022-032). The phrase "bosomed herself" — turning secrets inward, making them part of the self — captures the psychological interiority that the culture of secrecy produces.

## The Performance of Secrecy and the Chinky Affair

The novel's most elaborate treatment of secrecy as performance comes in Chapter 17, when Laura returns from her visit to Mr. Robby's house and must invent material to satisfy her audience. The narrator observes:

> [017-007] Laura was in a pretty pickle. Did she tell the plain truth, state the pedestrian facts — and this she would have been capable of doing with some address … But did she confess this, confess also that, save for a handful of monosyllables, her only exchange of words with him had been a line of Virgil; and, still more humbling, that she had liked his wife and sister better than himself: did this come to light, she would forfeit every sou of the prestige the visit had lent and yet promised to lend her.

The secret here is not a hidden transgression but a hidden absence — Laura has nothing to tell, and this nothingness is itself the shameful truth. So she manufactures secrets, performing a knowingness she does not possess, and the performance succeeds precisely because her audience craves the spice of illicit knowledge more than it values truth. The secret, in this episode, is revealed to be a theatrical genre as much as a category of information — what matters is not what you know but how convincingly you can perform having something to hide.
