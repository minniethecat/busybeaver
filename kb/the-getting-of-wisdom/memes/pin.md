# Pin

Pin is Laura Rambotham's younger sibling, a child whose tearfulness and attachment to home life form the backdrop of Laura's childhood before she departs for Ladies' College.

## Role in the Novel

Pin appears exclusively in the opening chapters set in the Rambotham household, before Laura leaves for Melbourne. As the younger sibling, Pin serves to establish the domestic world Laura is about to leave behind — a world of shared bedrooms, baths, and sibling rivalry [001-006]. Pin's presence helps define Laura's own character: Laura is the elder, the one preparing to go out into the wider world, while Pin remains in the nursery [002-001].

## Character Traits

Pin is defined by emotional sensitivity and a tendency to cry. Sarah the servant calls Pin "res'vor" — short for reservoir — on account of Pin's seemingly inexhaustible tears [001-056]. Unlike Laura, who is described as "high-stomached" and proud, Pin is softer, more vulnerable, and more openly dependent on the family's care [001-049]. Physically, Pin wears hair "flapping at their backs" in the same style as Laura [001-052].

## Key Scenes

In the opening chapter, Pin appears in the garden alongside Laura as their mother prepares them [001-006]. On the morning of Laura's departure, Pin urges Laura to come along: "Come on. Sarah's going to give me my bath when she's finished" [002-004]. Pin weeps during the preparations for Laura's departure, described as "tireless, snuffly weeping," which hardens Laura's own resolve [002-046]. Their father teases Pin: "pipin' your eye like that? Why, you're just made of water" [002-042].

## Relationship to Laura

Pin is Laura's closest childhood companion by proximity, but the sisters are temperamental opposites. Where Laura is proud and determined, Pin is tearful and dependent. Laura's response to Pin's weeping — setting her own "lips tight" — foreshadows the emotional self-discipline Laura will need at Ladies' College [002-046]. Pin represents the childhood vulnerability that Laura must shed.

## Significance

As a minor character confined to the domestic opening, Pin functions as a foil to Laura. Pin's open emotionality contrasts with Laura's emerging self-control, and Pin's remaining at home underscores Laura's solitary journey into the world beyond. The nickname "res'vor" — a small linguistic detail — also captures the novel's ear for the vernacular of the household.
