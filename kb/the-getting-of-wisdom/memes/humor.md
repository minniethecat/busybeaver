# Humor

The humor of "The Getting of Wisdom" is not in the events themselves — which are often painful — but in the narrator's ironic perspective on them. Richardson maintains a cool distance from Laura's intensities, and this distance is the novel's primary comic resource: we laugh not at Laura but at the gap between what she feels and what is actually happening.

## In the Novel

The novel's comic method relies on deflation. Laura builds elaborate fantasies of social triumph; the narrator records their collapse with clinical precision. Laura invests a casual remark with cosmic significance; the narrator lets us see it as what it is — a casual remark. This pattern is established from the first chapter, when Laura's "Wondrous Fair" persona — so majestic to her siblings — is punctured by her mother's practical summons.

The juxtaposition of Laura's romantic sensibility with the mundane world of mutton, confirmation classes, and Sunday books generates the novel's characteristic humor: not broad comedy but something sharper, more observational, and ultimately more humane. Richardson trusts the reader to recognize the comedy without having it pointed out.

## Key Passages

> "I don't care," said Laura rebelliously, though she was on the brink of tears. — [[001-035]], the novel's characteristic comic mode: Laura's bravado undermined by the narrator's observation

> "Robby? Her saint called Robby?" — Laura's romantic expectation about to collide with mutton-eating reality (PN [[016-013]])

> There was no more audacious Bible-reader in the class than she. — [[013-017]], the comedy of competitive piety

## Critical Perspective

The novel's humor has often been underappreciated in criticism that emphasizes its darker themes of shame and social exclusion. But Richardson's irony is not merely a stylistic choice — it is a philosophical position. The narrator's distance from Laura is also a distance from the school's values, and the comedy works to expose the arbitrariness of the social codes that cause Laura so much pain.
