# Church of England

The Church of England — the established Anglican Church — forms the religious background of "The Getting of Wisdom," shaping the Ladies' College's rhythms, rituals, and social identity. Its presence is both institutional (compulsory church attendance, Sunday observance, the clerical authority of Mr. Shepherd) and atmospheric (the assumption of Anglican identity as a marker of colonial respectability).

## In the Novel

The Church of England is the default religious identity of the Ladies' College and its students. The "church girls" who "always wear Trinity colours at the boat-race" (PN [[014-048]]) are Church of England girls — their college loyalty is an extension of their denominational identity, mapping Anglican affiliation onto the social geography of the University of Melbourne's residential system.

The Church structures the school week: "between breakfast and church there was an hour" (PN [[008-050]]), and "the boarders in church every Sunday morning" (PN [[012-005]]) embody the institution's public piety. Religious observance is compulsory, but the novel is skeptical about its spiritual content. Mrs. Gurley's evangelical questioning, the Sunday book restrictions, the Bible classes — all are presented as instruments of social discipline rather than genuine religious formation. The Church, in Richardson's rendering, is less a source of faith than a mechanism of respectability.

## The Clerical Establishment

The novel's clergymen — Mr. Shepherd, the "holy man" of Chapter XVI, and the unnamed clergy who preside over Sunday services — represent the Church's institutional authority in colonial Melbourne. Mr. Shepherd's "shovel-hat" and "long strides" (PN [[016-038]]) mark him as a professional man of the cloth, his identity defined by his vocation. Yet the novel is gently satirical about clerical pretension: Mr. Shepherd's "voice — it went on and on" (PN [[016-039]]) through the thin ceiling of his Prahran home, a drone of piety that impresses no one, least of all Laura.

## The Church and Colonial Identity

The Church of England's dominance in the novel reflects its historical position in colonial Victoria, where it was the largest and most socially prestigious Protestant denomination. Yet the novel also registers the denominational pluralism of Melbourne society: the presence of Ormond College (Presbyterian) alongside Trinity (Anglican) at the university, and the various Protestant identities of the school's families, reflect a colonial religious landscape that was more diverse than the Anglican establishment's public dominance might suggest. Laura's family's own religious identity is notably absent — they are not conspicuous churchgoers, and this absence is itself a class marker, distinguishing the rural Rambothams from the respectable Anglican families of the Shepherd type.

## Significance

The Church of England in "The Getting of Wisdom" functions as part of the novel's anatomy of colonial respectability. It is the institution that sanctifies the social order, lending divine warrant to the hierarchies of class and gender that the school reproduces. The novel's ironic treatment of religion — its exposure of the gap between institutional piety and genuine spiritual experience — places Richardson in the tradition of the Victorian novel of religious skepticism, alongside writers like George Eliot and Samuel Butler, for whom the Church's social function has become detached from its theological content.
