# Nature

Nature in "The Getting of Wisdom" defines the space beyond institutional walls — the garden at home, the countryside around Warreneen, the outdoor strolls at the college. It represents, for Laura, a realm of comparative freedom: a world not yet wholly organized by the hierarchies and regulations that govern the school's interiors.

## In the Novel

The novel opens in nature — the Rambotham garden where Laura, as "Wondrous Fair," spins stories for her siblings beneath the trees. This garden is the domain of imagination, a space where Laura commands attention and creates worlds. The transition to the Ladies' College is, in part, a transition from outdoors to indoors — from the freedom of the garden to the regulation of the classroom. At the college, nature is permitted only in supervised doses: the Sunday stroll "in Sunday fashion" (PN [[008-054]]), the garden where Laura retreats to memorize Bible chapters (PN [[018-014]]). The college's grounds — "thick hedges, and spreading trees" (PN [[007-045]]) — are beautiful but bounded, nature tamed and enclosed as a setting for institutional life.

Nature also carries moral connotations. Laura's "nature" — her essential character — is described as being "outside the bounds of nature" (PN [[007-051]]), a phrase that collapses the distinction between natural setting and innate disposition. To be "natural" at the Ladies' College is to belong without striving; to be "unnatural" is to be Laura — always trying, always failing, always visibly performing an ease she cannot feel.

## Key Passages

> The garden storytelling (Ch. I) — Laura as "Wondrous Fair" among her siblings, the natural world as the setting for unfettered imagination

> To stroll about in Sunday fashion. — [[008-054]], the institutionalization of outdoor leisure

> The college grounds: thick hedges, and spreading trees. — [[007-045]], nature enclosed and regulated

> "Outside the bounds of nature." — [[007-051]], the collapse of natural setting and natural character

## Significance

The nature/culture opposition structures much of the novel's thematic work. Laura arrives from the countryside — natural, unformed, "outside the bounds" of institutional culture — and is progressively shaped into a social being. The cost of this shaping is registered in her distance from the natural world she once inhabited: by the novel's end, the garden of childhood is a memory, and Laura has learned to navigate the indoor world of social performance with a fluency that is itself a kind of loss.
