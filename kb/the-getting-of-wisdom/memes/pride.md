## Family Pride

> Every morning early, Mother brushed and twisted, with a kind of grim pride, these silky ringlets round her finger. — [[001-052]]

Pride enters the novel through family. Mother takes "grim pride" in Laura's hair; Laura herself is proud of her curls, tossing them when strangers admire them. The family prides itself on qualities Laura will later learn to hide: "being 'stuck-up'" is Mother's reputation in the neighborhood [[003-016]].

This early pride is innocent, unselfconscious. It is the pride of belonging to a family, of possessing something distinctive. The novel's tragedy is partly the story of what happens to this pride when it meets institutional life.

## The Overthrow of Pride

> And yet, painful as was this fresh overthrow of her pride, it was neither the worst nor the most lasting result of the incident. — [[009-055]]

Laura's offer to accompany [[lilith-gordon]]'s song at Mrs. Strachey's drawing-room event is a pivotal moment in the novel's treatment of pride. Laura acts from genuine helpfulness — she can play the accompaniment and Lilith cannot — but the school interprets her action as showing off, as a refusal to conceal ability as the code demands.

The "overthrow of pride" that follows teaches Laura a lesson the novel regards as deeply ambivalent: that ability must be hidden, that excellence invites punishment. It is the first major wound to Laura's self-regard, and it reshapes her behavior for the remainder of her school career.

## Pride in Mother's Work

> Filled with pride in Mother's accomplishments and Lilith's appreciation of them, Laura fell asleep that night without a qualm. — [[011-012]]

When Lilith Gordon praises Mother's embroidery, Laura experiences a complex pride — pride in Mother's skill, pride in peer recognition, but also the relief of having family labor reframed as art rather than necessity. This moment of uncomplicated pride is rare in the novel; it depends on external validation and is thus inherently fragile.

## Pride and the Bob Episode

> Had she possessed the power to captivate him, she would cheerfully have put her pride in her pocket. — [[015-003]]

The [[bob]] episode introduces romantic pride. Laura's inability to attract Bob's sustained attention wounds her pride, but the wound is different from those inflicted by the school: it is private, not public; it concerns desirability rather than achievement. Laura would "cheerfully" sacrifice pride for romantic success — a calculation that reveals pride's hierarchy in her value system.

## The Body's Pride

> She could not help admiring their proficiency in the art of pleasing, even though she felt a little abashed by the open pride they took in their growing charms. — [[015-023]]

Richardson contrasts Laura's intellectual and artistic pride with her peers' bodily pride. [[bertha-ramsey]] takes frank gratification in her developing figure; [[tilly]] lives to reduce her waist measurement; [[inez]] has graduated beyond artificial aids to a natural grace. Laura observes this bodily pride from outside, "half-fascinated, half-repelled" — she cannot participate in it, but she cannot dismiss it either.

## Pride Transformed: The Literary Society

> Said Laura, almost blind with pride and pleasure. — [[016-022]]

Laura's admission to the [[literary-society]] represents pride's positive transformation. Here, her abilities are recognized rather than punished; her creativity finds an institutional home. The pride she feels at this moment — "almost blind with pride and pleasure" — is earned through work, not inherited through family or performed through social skill.
