# Colonial Identity

The students at the Ladies' College are repeatedly described as "young colonials" — a phrase that captures their position between British cultural inheritance and Australian colonial reality. This identity is not merely a demographic label but a psychological condition: the students are self-conscious about their distance from the metropolitan centre, yet possessed of a pragmatic energy that is distinctly colonial. The tension between British aspiration and Australian actuality shapes their comportment, their ambitions, and their sense of their place in the world.

## In the Novel

Richardson uses the term "young colonials" at key moments to frame the students' collective identity. The headmaster, Mr. Strachey, "quelled the high spirits of these young colonials by his dignified air of detachment" (009-020) — the Englishman imposing order on colonial exuberance. During an elocution lesson, the students, "like the self-conscious, emotionally shy young colonials they were, felt half amused by, half-superior to the histrionic display" of their visiting master (012-005). Their awkward self-consciousness is a colonial trait — neither fully British nor fully Australian, they are caught between cultures.

Yet these same young colonials possess a pragmatic realism that distinguishes them. They are "crass realists though these young colonials were, and bluntly as they faced facts, they were none the less just as hungry for romance as the most insatiable novel-reader" (017-065). This duality — practical and romantic, blunt and hungry for beauty — defines the colonial character Richardson portrays. The "young colonials" can mock pretension while secretly longing for the cultural richness of the old world.

The physical setting reinforces colonial identity. When the students take their holiday, "The air, Australian air, met them like a prickling" (014-023) — the sensory shock of the native environment contrasting with the European interiors of the school. The "representative gathering of young Australia, bathed in the brilliant March sunshine" (015-026) at a school event presents the colonial generation in full display, sunlit and distinct from their English models.

Laura's own background embodies the colonial condition. She comes from "a bookless house — like most Australian houses of its kind" (008-051), a detail that places her family within the cultural poverty of the colonial middle class. The "English mail going on to Sydney" (019-060) serves as a recurring reminder of the metropolitan centre's gravitational pull — news, culture, and validation all arrive by ship from England.

## Colonial Education

The Ladies' College itself is a colonial institution — importing British curricula, British governesses, and British standards to shape the daughters of the colonial middle class. The students read "Green's History of the English People" (024-037), absorbing a version of history that positions England as the centre and the colonies as periphery. Monsieur Boehmer's "ridiculous English" (024-042) adds a further layer — even the European culture the school aspires to impart comes through an imperfect colonial filter.

## Characters

- **Laura Rambotham** — a colonial subject navigating British institutional culture; her "bookless" home and modest background represent the colonial middle class
- **Mr. Strachey** — the English headmaster whose "detachment" represents metropolitan authority over colonial subjects
- **Mr. Repton** — the visiting elocution master whose theatricality provokes the colonial self-consciousness of his students
- **The students of Class Two** — collectively embody the "young colonial" identity, at once skeptical and yearning
