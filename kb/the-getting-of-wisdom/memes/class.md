# Class

Social class in *The Getting of Wisdom* operates as an invisible but pervasive hierarchy that determines students' social standing, friendships, and self-worth at the Ladies' College. For Laura Rambotham, class is not an abstract concept but a daily reality — it governs where she sits, whom she befriends, how she is treated by governesses, and ultimately how she sees herself.

## In the Novel

Social class permeates every aspect of life at the Ladies' College. Laura enters the school conscious of her family's modest means — her mother works as a needlewoman, a fact she desperately conceals from her peers. Upon arrival, she is told bluntly: "Father didn't give you a penny of pocket-money to take to school" (002-028), marking her economic difference from classmates before lessons even begin.

The school's internal ranking system mirrors the class structure of 1890s Melbourne: students are placed in "lowest" and "upper" classes academically, but the true hierarchy is social. Laura sits "bottom" of the lowest class upon arrival (007-022), and her classmates' social positions are marked by family connections, wealth, and appearance. She quickly learns that "good birth and an aristocratic appearance would not avail her, did the damaging fact leak out that Mother worked for her living" (011-036).

The class consciousness extends beyond the school gates into the language and assumptions Laura has absorbed from her colonial upbringing. She associates certain terms "only with the lowest class of Irish agricultural labourers" (007-051), revealing the class prejudices she has internalised even as she herself occupies a precarious social position.

Money talks loudly within the school community. As the narrator observes, "loud as money made itself in this young community, it was yet a more vital matter that you had not to work for it" (011-035). Laura's peers have "a bottomless supply of pocket-money" (010-006), funding subscriptions and social activities from which she is excluded. Yet the narrator notes that "the question of money was, after all, trifling, compared with the infinitely more important one: whether or not she would ever succeed in making a friend" (010-008).

Laura's class-fortunes shift with her social successes and failures. When she wins approval through her storytelling, it brings "a change for the better in her class-fortunes" (009-016), showing how class and social acceptance are intertwined. Her eventual promotion to the second class (010-002) is both an academic milestone and a social advance.

## Colonial Class Context

The class system at the Ladies' College reflects the peculiarities of late-nineteenth-century colonial society. Laura is one of the "young colonials" whose families have made their way in Australia, and the school's social hierarchy mirrors the aspirations of a colonial middle class striving to replicate English standards. The governesses themselves embody an ambiguous class position — educated women who must work for a living, occupying a status not unlike the one Laura fears inheriting from her mother.

Miss Chapman, the head governess, represents authority derived from position rather than birth — "neither so hard-looking nor so terrible as some of the others" (005-040), yet firmly in command. The younger governesses laugh at Laura on her first evening (005-009), demonstrating how even those on the margins of respectability police the class boundaries against those lower still.

## Characters

- **Laura Rambotham** — acutely conscious of her lower social position; her mother's employment as a needlewoman is a source of shame she hides from schoolmates; her lack of pocket-money and modest background separate her from the school's social mainstream
- **Inez** and **Bertha** — Laura's early friends who, like her, occupy low positions in the class hierarchy and are "well content" with their status (009-008)
- **Lilith Gordon** and **Evelyn Souttar** — represent the upper social tier that Laura aspires to join, distinguished by family wealth and social confidence
- **Miss Chapman** — the head governess, embodying institutional authority that transcends and reinforces class distinctions
