# Teasing

**Teasing** in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]] ranges from the casual banter of schoolgirls to a more threatening mode of social aggression. The novel uses teasing to expose the power dynamics of its social world — who may tease whom, and what forms of teasing are permitted or transgressive.

## Teasing Among Schoolgirls

Within the Ladies' College, teasing operates as a form of social bonding with a sharp edge. The narrator records the casual cruelty of classroom banter: "'I bet Godmother's asking her not to wallop you too often,' the tease had just begun afresh" (PN=004-031). This scene captures the rhythm of school teasing — persistent, unserious on the surface, but capable of wounding. Older students intervene — "Stop teasing the kid" (PN=005-031) and "You're teasing that kid again" (PN=005-058) — but the intervention itself reinforces the hierarchy: Laura is "the kid," defined by her vulnerability to teasing.

## The Joking Tease

Not all teasing is cruel. The narrator describes a lighter form: Bertha "only laughed at this, in a teasing way" (PN=007-042), suggesting that teasing can also be a mode of affectionate interaction. The distinction between harmful and harmless teasing is one the novel leaves for the reader to discern — it depends entirely on the power relation between teaser and teased.

## The Male Tease

The novel's most unsettling portrait of teasing appears outside the school, in the figure of "the Uncle" who "turned out to be a type that struck instant terror into Laura: a full-fledged male tease" (PN=014-050). This adult male tease represents a different order of threat — not the rough-and-tumble of schoolgirl banter but something more predatory. The narrator notes that "Tilly was so obviously sulky that the tease could not let her escape him thus" (PN=014-083), revealing the dynamic of the tease who feeds on his victim's discomfort.

## Teasing and Affection

The novel complicates the picture by showing how teasing can be a vehicle for emerging romantic feeling. Laura "gradually worked herself up into an attack of love, which was genuine enough to make her redden when Mr. Shepherd was spoken of, and to enjoy being teased about him" (PN=016-002). Here teasing becomes a form of social recognition — to be teased about a crush is to have that crush acknowledged. The enjoyment of teasing marks a step in Laura's social development.

## Vulnerability to Teasing

Not all characters are equally vulnerable. Pin provides a counterexample: "so long as you did not tease her about her legs, there was no limit to what you might say to her" (PN=019-024). Each person has their specific vulnerability, and the social art consists in knowing where the line is drawn. Laura, by contrast, is vulnerable across many fronts — her family, her fabrications, her social standing — making her an easy target for teasing of all kinds.

## Key Passages

> Classroom teasing as a rite of passage: "'I bet Godmother's asking her not to wallop you too often,' the tease had just begun afresh" (PN=004-031)

> The limits of intervention: "Stop teasing the kid" (PN=005-031) — the phrase itself reinforces Laura's subordinate status

> The menace of the adult male tease: "turned out to be a type that struck instant terror into Laura: a full-fledged male tease" (PN=014-050)

> Teasing as romantic recognition: Laura "enjoy being teased about him" — a sign of social development (PN=016-002)

> Pin's sturdy resistance: "so long as you did not tease her about her legs, there was no limit to what you might say to her" (PN=019-024)

## Characters

**Laura Rambotham** is the novel's primary target of teasing — her sensitivity, her fabrications, and her uncertain social position make her vulnerable. **Pin** provides a model of resilience: she has one vulnerability but is otherwise impervious. **The Uncle** (PN=014-050) embodies the most threatening form of teasing — the adult male who uses teasing as a weapon of humiliation. **Bertha** represents teasing's lighter register — playful, affectionate, without cruelty.

## Historical Context

The culture of teasing in the novel reflects the norms of late-Victorian and Edwardian boarding schools, where teasing and bullying were often tolerated as part of the "character-building" experience. The specifically gendered dimension — the "male tease" as a distinct threat — reflects the period's anxieties about male-female social interaction and the vulnerability of young women in mixed company.

## Critical Perspective

Richardson's treatment of teasing has been noted for its subtle gradation of social power. Teasing in the novel is never just "fun" — it always reveals who has power and who lacks it. The "male tease" episode has drawn particular critical attention as an early literary representation of what later criticism would identify as gendered harassment, rendered with a psychological insight unusual for its period.
