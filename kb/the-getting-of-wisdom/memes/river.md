# River

**River** and water imagery in *The Getting of Wisdom* functions as a symbolic counterpoint to the enclosed, regulated world of the [[ladies-college]], carrying associations of freedom, natural vitality, and the childhood landscape that [[laura-rambotham]] has left behind. Though the river occupies relatively little narrative space compared to the school's interiors, its appearances are strategically placed and thematically charged, offering glimpses of a world beyond institutional walls and a mode of experience unmediated by social performance.

## The Yarra and the Melbourne Landscape

The novel's geographical imagination is organised around the contrast between the provincial landscapes of Laura's childhood and the urban environment of [[melbourne]]. The Yarra River, winding through the city, serves as a persistent reminder of the natural world within the built environment. Laura's journey to Melbourne brings her past "the watery flats where Chinamen were fording" [[003-003]], a landscape in which water and labour combine to create a distinctly Australian pastoral scene, far removed from the manicured propriety of the Ladies' College.

The river appears not as a site of recreation or beauty but as a working waterway, part of the economic geography that sustains the city. This refusal to sentimentalize the natural world is characteristic of Richardson's realist aesthetic: the Yarra is not a picturesque backdrop but a functional element of the urban landscape, its "watery flats" [[003-003]] indifferent to the romantic expectations that a more conventional novelist might impose upon them.

> There was an intoxicating sense of freedom in being locked in, alone, with the window down. [[003-038]]

This moment, though not explicitly about the river, captures the quality of liberation that the novel associates with movement through the landscape. The train journey that brings Laura to Melbourne is charged with a sense of possibility that the school itself will systematically extinguish. The "intoxicating sense of freedom" belongs to the world of travel and transition, not to the world of institutional routine.

## Water as Symbol of Freedom

Water imagery throughout the novel sustains an association with liberty, flow, and natural impulse that stands in direct opposition to the rigidity of institutional life. Laura's childhood is marked by "the red earth riddled by creeklets of running water" [[024-004]], a landscape in which water moves freely, carving its own paths through the resistant earth. This image of natural agency — water that finds its own way, that cannot be contained — serves as an implicit metaphor for the self that Laura struggles to preserve within the school's disciplinary regime.

The creeklets that "riddle" the earth suggest a landscape of hidden passages and secret flows, a natural world that operates according to its own logic rather than the imposed order of human institutions. It is a vision of the Australian landscape as a place of organic complexity, irreducible to the simple categories that the school seeks to impose on experience. Laura's memory of this landscape provides a psychological resource that the school cannot access or control.

> Be a crybaby, do. [[001-056]]

The taunt, directed at the young Laura on account of her "perpetual wateriness," links emotional expression to the element of water in a way that prefigures the novel's broader symbolic scheme. To be watery is to be emotionally unregulated, to leak feeling in ways that violate the norms of composure that the school will later demand. The river and its associated imagery thus come to stand for everything in Laura's nature that the institution seeks to discipline: spontaneity, emotional transparency, and a refusal to be contained.

## Creek and Stream: The Memory of Childhood

The smaller waterways of the novel — creeks, streams, and rivulets — carry particular associations with Laura's pre-institutional life. The "creeklets of running water" [[024-004]] that mark the landscape of her childhood represent a world of natural freedom that the school has displaced but not erased. These waterways persist in memory, a subterranean current beneath the dry surface of institutional existence.

The movement from creek to college maps the trajectory of Laura's journey: from the fluid, organic, and personal to the rigid, artificial, and impersonal. The water that runs freely through the childhood landscape becomes, in the school, domesticated and controlled — flowers placed "in water for her" [[001-061]], the rituals of washing, the supervised propriety of the bathroom. Water, like Laura herself, has been institutionalized.

> She spent the evening in watery dudgeon on her pillow. [[001-068]]

The phrase "watery dudgeon" — tears of offended dignity — again links water with emotional authenticity. Laura's tears are the one form of expression the school cannot fully police, the irreducible residue of a self that refuses to be entirely socialized. The "watery" quality that was once a source of shame becomes, in the novel's symbolic economy, a sign of genuine feeling in a world of performance.

## River Versus School Confinement

The most profound opposition the novel establishes is between the river's freedom of movement and the school's regime of confinement. The Ladies' College is a place of walls, schedules, and supervised spaces; the river is a place of flow, change, and unregulated encounter. Laura's school existence is defined by enclosure — the classroom, the dormitory, the dining hall — while the river suggests a world of open horizons and unscripted possibility.

This opposition is not merely spatial but existential. The school demands that Laura become a certain kind of person: accomplished, decorous, socially adept. The river, by contrast, demands nothing; it simply is, a presence that precedes and exceeds the categories of social judgment. In a novel so concerned with the performance of self, the river offers an image of being without performance, of existence without justification.
