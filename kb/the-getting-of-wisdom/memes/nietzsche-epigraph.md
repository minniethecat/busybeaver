# Nietzsche Epigraph

Throughout "The Getting of Wisdom," Henry Handel Richardson prefaces several chapters with epigraphs drawn from the works of Friedrich Nietzsche. These quotations serve as a philosophical counterpoint to the narrative, linking Laura Rambotham's struggles with social conformity and self-definition to Nietzsche's critiques of morality, education, and the development of the individual will.

## About

Nietzsche's presence in an Australian novel of 1910 is itself significant — a mark of Richardson's cosmopolitan intellectual formation and her ambition to transcend the generic conventions of the school story. Richardson lived and worked in Europe (primarily Germany and England) and was well-read in contemporary German philosophy at a time when Nietzsche's ideas were only beginning to circulate in the English-speaking world. The use of Nietzschean epigraphs signals her literary ambition — to frame an ostensibly domestic story of a schoolgirl's education within a broader philosophical inquiry into the nature of selfhood, morality, and social convention. The epigraphs appear at the heads of multiple chapters, functioning as interpretive keys that invite the reader to read Laura's experiences through a Nietzschean lens: her struggles with the school's moral authority, her pride and shame, her artistic ambition, and her ultimate accommodation to social reality.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Epigraphs

The Nietzsche epigraphs appear at the beginning of at least five chapters:

- **Chapter XII** (PN [[012-002]]): Sets a philosophical frame for Laura's developing social awareness and the tensions between individual desire and collective expectation
- **Chapter XVII** (PN [[017-002]]): Contextualizes Laura's growing capacity for self-deception and her need for social validation
- **Chapter XVIII** (PN [[018-002]]): Frames the introduction of new characters and shifting social dynamics
- **Chapter XX** (PN [[020-002]]): Accompanies the Shepherd scandal, the novel's most direct confrontation with moral hypocrisy
- **Chapter XXII** (PN [[022-002]]): Frames Laura's intense friendship with Evelyn Souttar and the emotional education it provides

The consistent placement of the epigraphs — always at the chapter opening — creates a ritual of philosophical framing that elevates the narrative beyond mere school story into something approaching moral philosophy conducted through fiction.

## Connection to Themes

The Nietzschean epigraphs reinforce and complicate several of the novel's central themes:

**Self-overcoming**: Nietzsche's concept of the will to transcend one's given circumstances and inherited values mirrors Laura's trajectory as she struggles to define herself against the school's homogenizing pressure. Her journey is not toward conventional wisdom — the school's stated goal — but toward a hard-won understanding of the gap between social performance and authentic selfhood.

**Herd morality**: Nietzsche's critique of the morality of the herd — the unthinking conformity to collective values — resonates with the novel's depiction of the Ladies' College as a mechanism of social reproduction. The school does not teach girls to think; it teaches them to belong. Laura's resistance to this pressure, however partial and compromised, marks her as a Nietzschean figure in embryo.

**Art as redemption**: Nietzsche's valorization of artistic creation as a mode of authentic self-expression aligns with Laura's musical gift. Her piano-playing is not merely an "accomplishment" but the one domain in which she is genuinely herself — absorbed, passionate, beyond the reach of social judgment. The epigraphs invite us to read Laura's music as a form of Zarathustran self-creation rather than a decorative feminine skill.

**The uses of suffering**: Nietzsche's insistence that suffering can be productive — that the greatest growth comes through difficulty — provides a framework for understanding the novel's refusal to sentimentalize Laura's pain. Her humiliations are not merely to be endured but to be metabolized; they are the raw material of whatever wisdom she eventually acquires.

## Critical Significance

The Nietzsche epigraphs have been the subject of considerable scholarly attention. Some critics argue that they represent Richardson's most direct intervention in the narrative, establishing an ironic distance between the narrator's cool observation and the philosophical stakes of Laura's experience. Others contend that the epigraphs function defensively — that Richardson, writing a "school story" that risked being dismissed as women's fiction, used Nietzsche to claim intellectual seriousness for her project. Still others read the epigraphs as genuinely integral to the novel's meaning: the Nietzschean frame transforms Laura's story from a Bildungsroman (in which the protagonist is formed by experience) into something closer to a genealogy (in which the protagonist's "wisdom" is exposed as a construction of power, not a natural development).

The epigraphs also participate in the novel's engagement with European intellectual culture. At a time when Australian literature was often dismissed as provincial, Richardson's deployment of Nietzsche — the most radical European philosopher of the age — was a declaration of cosmopolitan ambition. The epigraphs insist that Laura's story, set in a Melbourne boarding school, is as philosophically serious as any novel of European manners.
