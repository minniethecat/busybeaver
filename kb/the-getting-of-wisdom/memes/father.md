# Father (Mr. Rambotham)

Laura Rambotham's father is a structuring absence in *The Getting of Wisdom* — deceased before the novel's opening, he exists only in memory and in the economic consequences of his death, yet his absence shapes every dimension of Laura's experience at the [[ladies-college]]. The family's reduced circumstances, the [[mother]]'s struggle to manage alone, and Laura's vulnerability to the question "What's your father?" all trace back to this original loss.

## Death and Its Consequences

Mr. Rambotham's death has left the family in precarious financial circumstances. The narrator notes the mother's lament: "If only her father had been alive to help!" [[002-055]]. The absence of a father's income and status places the Rambothams at a disadvantage in the socially stratified world of colonial Melbourne, where a family's position depended heavily on the father's profession and wealth.

The mother's response — "I'm sure no father livin' could do more than you" [[002-056]] — is spoken in reference to her own efforts on Laura's behalf, but it inadvertently highlights the impossibility of fully compensating for the absent parent. The mother's "grand ambitions" for Laura's education are, in part, an attempt to secure for her daughter the social position that a living father might have provided.

## "What's Your Father?"

The question "What's your father?" — posed to Laura by her schoolmates at the Ladies' College — becomes a recurring motif of social interrogation. When Laura is asked "What's your name and what's your father?" [[005-031]], the question exposes her vulnerability: she has no father to name, no paternal profession to claim, no inherited status to deploy. In a school culture where girls are identified by their fathers' occupations and social standing, Laura's paternal absence is a social liability.

The awkwardness of this question is compounded by the fact that Laura cannot offer the respectable professional identity — doctor, lawyer, clergyman, wealthy pastoralist — that would secure her standing among her peers. The report of Evelyn Souttar's "father's wealth" [[010-004]] and the envy directed toward "those whose fathers did nothing more active than" manage investments [[011-036]] establish the social hierarchy against which Laura must measure herself — and in which she cannot compete.

## The Paternal Figure in School Culture

Fathers appear throughout the novel as figures of authority, wealth, and social definition. Bob "stood in awe of their father" [[008-026]], while another girl fears "what my father'd say, if he knew I'd been in" some transgression [[012-033]]. These references to living fathers — present, disciplining, defining — underscore Laura's isolation. She has no father to invoke as a source of authority or protection, no paternal reputation to trade on.

The most pointed reference comes through Bob, who is "still smarting from his father's banter" [[014-089]], a reminder that even the discomforts of having a father are a form of presence that Laura lacks. The "Uncle" who replaces the father figure in the household — present in the same passage — does not fill the gap but rather emphasizes it.

## Social Status and Paternal Identity

The novel's treatment of fathers is inseparable from its critique of colonial social hierarchy. A girl "whose father was once a German" fears being "looked down on" [[011-055]], while another boasts of a "most distinguished family" whose father "was a lord or a baronet or something" [[017-037]]. The retort "Oh, never mind the father" [[017-039]] is the novel's most dismissive treatment of paternal status — but it is spoken by a character secure enough to dismiss what Laura can never afford to ignore.

Laura's father is also invoked indirectly through the question of her origins. The teasing she endures — about her name, her clothes, her manners — is ultimately teasing about class, and class in the novel's world is determined largely by fathers. To have no father is, in a sense, to have no class identity at all — or rather, to have an identity defined entirely by lack.

## Significance

The absent father functions in *The Getting of Wisdom* as both a practical and symbolic force. Practically, his death explains the family's financial strain and the mother's determination to secure Laura's education. Symbolically, his absence represents the social vulnerability that Laura must navigate throughout the novel — the sense of being exposed, undefined, without the paternal armor that protects her wealthier classmates from the worst rigors of social judgment.

Laura's eventual emergence into a self-authored identity — however partial and compromised — is the novel's answer to the question of what replaces the father. The "wisdom" of the title is, in part, the knowledge that one must construct one's own social self, without the inherited certainties that a living father might have provided.
