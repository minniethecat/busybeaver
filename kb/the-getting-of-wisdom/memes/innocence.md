# Innocence

Innocence is a significant thematic concept in *The Getting of Wisdom*,
representing both Laura Rambotham's initial naivety and the broader transition
from childhood to experience that defines the novel's coming-of-age arc.

## The Innocence of the Heart

Early in her time at the [[ladies-college]], Laura acts from "the innocence of
her heart" [018-027]. She is guileless, unguarded, and unaware of the social
codes that govern the school. This innocence leads her into difficulties — she
speaks and acts without calculation in an environment where every gesture is
scrutinised.

## Innocence and Accusation

The theme of innocence takes on legal overtones in the school theft episode.
Laura "strove in vain to prove her innocence" [019-009] when wrongly suspected,
experiencing "the conviction of one's own innocence" [007-002] as both a comfort
and a burden. Being innocent does not protect her from the consequences of
appearing guilty — a hard lesson in the gap between truth and perception.

## Loss of Innocence

Laura's trajectory through the novel is, in part, a chronicle of innocence lost.
Her encounters with [[miss-hicks]]'s sharp critique, the machinations of her
classmates, and the school's disciplinary apparatus all contribute to a gradual
stripping away of her childhood guilelessness. By the novel's end, Laura has
gained worldly wisdom at the cost of a certain innocence.

## Innocence and Knowledge

The novel poses an implicit question about the relationship between innocence and
education. The "facts" that the school values — the kind that earned Laura her
place in class — are presented as morally neutral but existentially diminishing.
Gaining knowledge, the novel suggests, involves losing something as well.
