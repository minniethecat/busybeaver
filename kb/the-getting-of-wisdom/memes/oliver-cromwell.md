# Oliver Cromwell

Oliver Cromwell is referenced in "The Getting of Wisdom" as part of the historical curriculum at the Ladies' College. His presence in the novel illuminates the content of colonial education — specifically, the transmission of British history as the foundation of Australian schoolgirls' knowledge of the world.

## Role in the Novel

Cromwell appears as a subject of study rather than a character — one of the historical figures whose deeds Laura is expected to learn and reproduce for examinations. His inclusion in the curriculum reflects the assumptions of late-Victorian colonial education: that the history that mattered was British history, that the figures worth studying were English figures, and that Australian schoolgirls should know more about seventeenth-century England than about their own continent's past. Laura's engagement with Cromwell — like her engagement with much of the curriculum — is shaped by the tension between her quick intelligence and her resistance to institutional forms of learning.

## Historical Context

In the 1890s, Australian education was still largely an extension of British education. History curricula centered on English monarchs, parliaments, and wars. Cromwell occupied a particularly charged position in this narrative — regicide, Puritan, Lord Protector — and his ambivalent legacy made him a subject that required careful handling by schoolmistresses concerned with propriety. His presence in the Ladies' College curriculum says as much about the school's commitment to conventional British historical narratives as it does about Cromwell himself.
