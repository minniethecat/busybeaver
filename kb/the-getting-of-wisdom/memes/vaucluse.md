# Vaucluse

Vaucluse is the name of Bertha Ramsey's family home, mentioned in "The Getting of Wisdom" as the site of an unspoken "shameful secret" about Bertha's mother. Like many house-names in colonial Melbourne, "Vaucluse" gestures toward European cultural capital while marking the aspirational domestic architecture of the Victorian middle class.

## Significance in the Novel

The house is mentioned in connection with Bertha Ramsey, one of Laura's schoolmates. The narrator notes that the girls know of "a shameful secret" attached to Bertha's mother — something connected to Vaucluse (PN [[007-055]]) — but the nature of the secret is never specified. This reticence is characteristic of the novel's treatment of adult transgression: it exists as atmosphere, as something the girls sense without fully understanding.

The house-name itself is significant. "Vaucluse" — evoking the Provençal valley of Petrarch's beloved Laura — is typical of the aspirational naming practices of colonial Melbourne's middle class, who borrowed European toponyms to lend their residences a borrowed distinction.

## Key Passages

> The "shameful secret" about Bertha's mother was connected to Vaucluse. — [[007-055]]
