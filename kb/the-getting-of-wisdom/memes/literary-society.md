# Literary Society

The Literary Society at the Ladies' College is a selective student organization that appears in the later chapters of "The Getting of Wisdom." Membership in the Society functions as a marker of social acceptance and intellectual standing among the school's boarders — and Laura's admission to it marks a significant moment in her social ascent within the school's hierarchy, one that proves to be both a triumph and a prelude to the novel's most painful disappointment.

## In the Novel

The Literary Society is introduced in Chapter XXI when Laura is "invited to join the boarders' Literary Society" (PN [[021-001]]). The invitation is significant because membership is restricted: the Society is presided over by senior students Cupid (Cupid Stanhope) and Mary (M. P.), both figures of considerable prestige within the school. Admission is not automatic, and Laura's invitation "augured well" for her social standing (PN [[021-003]]), signaling that despite her earlier humiliations — the dress incident, the exposure of her mother's occupation, the various social missteps that marked her first year — she has achieved a measure of recognition among her peers.

The Society also provides the crucial setting for Laura's first meaningful encounter with Evelyn Souttar. "A member of the Literary Society was the girl with the twinkly brown eyes" (PN [[022-005]]) — the narrative's introduction of Evelyn links her immediately to the Society's elite social space. Their acquaintance, begun under the Society's auspices, develops into the novel's central relationship, and the Society thus functions as the mechanism through which Laura's social ascent and her emotional vulnerability become entwined. The Society's literary character — its ostensible purpose of reading, discussing, and cultivating literary taste — recedes almost entirely behind its social function: it is not what the Society does that matters, but who belongs to it.

## Hierarchy and Membership

The Society's leadership — Cupid and Mary — reflects the school's broader hierarchies. Cupid, whose nickname suggests both affection and a certain dismissiveness, represents the social elite, while Mary (Mary Pidwall, M. P.) represents intellectual achievement. Together they embody the two paths to status within the school: popularity and scholarship. That the Society includes both suggests its function as a site where these hierarchies converge and where girls like Laura — who belongs fully to neither category — must negotiate their position.

Laura's admission to the Society is itself a marker of her social progress. The girl who arrived at the College marked as an outsider by her homemade clothes and country manners has, by the novel's later chapters, acquired enough social competence to be recognized by the school's elite. But the Society's acceptance is conditional and fragile — as Laura discovers when her friendship with Evelyn collapses and she finds herself, once again, on the outside of the social world she had briefly inhabited.

## Key Passages

> Laura was invited to join the boarders' Literary Society; of which Cupid and Mary were the leading members. — [[021-001]], the invitation that signals Laura's social arrival

> Hence, her admission to the Literary Society augured well. — [[021-003]], the narrator's measured assessment, the careful "augured well" rather than a definitive endorsement

> A member of the Literary Society was the girl with the twinkly brown eyes — Evelyn Souttar, it turned out, was her name. — [[022-005]], the setting that brings Laura into Evelyn's orbit

## Thematic Significance

The Literary Society represents one of the few student-governed spaces within the school's rigid adult-controlled hierarchy — a domain where peer approval, rather than staff assessment, determines status. Its existence points to the school's implicit acknowledgment that girls require some sphere of autonomous social organization, even as its activities remain carefully bounded by institutional norms (literary discussion being a safely "improving" pursuit).

For Laura, membership in the Society represents a form of validation that academic and musical achievement alone cannot provide: it means being recognized by the very girls who had once mocked or ignored her. But the novel's characteristic irony is that this validation proves hollow. The Society gives Laura access to Evelyn, and Evelyn gives Laura her most intense experience of friendship — and her most devastating experience of its failure. The social prestige that the Society confers turns out to be as fragile as every other form of status Laura has pursued.

The Society's literary character also invites reflection on the novel's treatment of culture and class. Literature — the reading, discussing, and appreciation of books — was central to the Victorian ideal of the cultivated young lady, and the Society's existence reflects the school's investment in this ideal. But the novel offers almost no detail about the Society's actual literary activities, implying that its cultural function is secondary to its social one: what matters is not what the girls read but who is reading it together.

## Historical Basis

Literary societies were common features of secondary schools and colleges in the late nineteenth century, reflecting the Victorian belief in the improving power of literature and organized debate. In the context of a girls' school like the Ladies' College, a literary society would have been one of the few sanctioned forms of student self-governance and intellectual expression — a space where the "accomplishments" of the curriculum could be exercised in a semi-autonomous setting. The historical Presbyterian Ladies' College, on which the novel's school is based, maintained similar student organizations, though records of their specific activities are scarce.
