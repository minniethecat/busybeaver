# Sacrifice

**Sacrifice** in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]] centers on the economic and emotional costs borne by [[mother]] to secure [[laura-rambotham]]'s education at [[ladies-college]]. Laura's gradual recognition of this sacrifice — and her failure to adequately honor it through academic achievement — constitutes one of the novel's most quietly devastating narrative arcs. The novel treats sacrifice not as a straightforward virtue but as a complex transaction whose costs are distributed unevenly and whose returns are never guaranteed.

## The Cost of Education

The material reality of Laura's privileged education is exposed only late in the novel, when she returns home and confronts the consequences of her academic struggles:

> [024-003] The money which, by hook and by crook, had been scraped together and hoarded up for Laura's education was now coming to an end; as it was, the next six months would mean a terrible pinching and screwing. The other children, too, were growing day by day more costly; their little minds and bodies clamoured for a larger share of attention.

The phrase "by hook and by crook" underscores the precariousness of the Rambotham family's financial position. Laura's education represents a collective family sacrifice, one that weighs on her siblings as well as on her mother. The narrator's economic language — "pinching and screwing," "scraped together and hoarded up" — emphasizes that sacrifice is not an abstract moral concept but a material reality measured in pennies saved and comforts foregone.

The final sentence of the passage introduces a crucial dimension: the siblings' "little minds and bodies clamoured for a larger share of attention." The verb "clamoured" positions the other children not as passive beneficiaries of Mother's sacrifice but as active claimants, their needs competing with Laura's education for scarce resources. Sacrifice is thus revealed as a zero-sum economy: what is given to Laura is taken from her siblings, and Mother must adjudicate between competing claims.

## Awakening to Gratitude

Laura's awareness of the sacrifice made on her behalf is not innate but forced upon her:

> [024-003] And Laura's eyes were rudely opened to the struggle Mother had had to make both ends meet, while her firstborn was acquiring wisdom; for Mother spoke of it herself, spoke openly of her means and resources, perhaps with some idea of rousing in Laura a gratitude that had so far been dormant.

The narrator's choice of "rudely opened" emphasizes the unpleasantness of Laura's awakening and the implication that her gratitude "had so far been dormant." The phrase "acquiring wisdom" — echoing the novel's title — acquires an ironic resonance when weighed against the financial and emotional cost of that acquisition. What kind of wisdom is it that requires ignorance of its own cost?

Mother's strategy of speaking "openly of her means and resources" is identified by the narrator as a deliberate pedagogical tactic — an attempt to "rouse" gratitude through disclosure. But the word "perhaps" introduces uncertainty about Mother's motives, and the metaphor of dormant gratitude suggests something that, once awakened, may not behave as expected.

But the novel immediately undercuts any simple redemptive narrative:

> [024-004] Laura was much too fast entangled in her own troubles, to have leisure for such a costly feeling as gratitude; and Mother's outspokenness only added a fresh weight to her pack.

Gratitude itself is described as a "costly feeling" — another expense Laura cannot afford. The psychological realism here is sharp: awareness of sacrifice does not automatically produce appropriate gratitude. Laura is "guilty, careworn, lonely" [024-004], and the knowledge of what her education has cost only deepens her isolation. The metaphor of the "pack" — a burden carried on one's back — transforms gratitude from a virtue into a weight, something that makes movement harder rather than easier.

## The Economics of Obligation

The novel presents sacrifice as creating a web of obligation that Laura instinctively resists. The narrator observes that Laura "was never at ease under an obligation; she had to pay the coin back" [011-004], suggesting that even as a recipient of sacrifice, she experiences obligation as a debt demanding repayment. This insight complicates the moral economy of the novel: sacrifice given freely by the giver may be experienced as a burden by the recipient.

The metaphor of "coin" extends the economic framing of sacrifice. Obligation is currency, and Laura's instinct is always to repay — to clear the account — rather than to live in a state of indebtedness. This psychological trait makes her especially vulnerable to the burden of maternal sacrifice. A more complacent child might accept sacrifice as her due; Laura experiences it as a loan she may never be able to repay.

[[mother]] reminds Laura of her broader debts in a letter of reproach:

> [009-003] "If it hadn't been for her supplying you with books and things I couldn't have sent you to school at all."

This reference to Godmother as benefactor situates Laura's education within a web of female economic interdependence. Laura's failure to achieve examination success is not merely a personal disappointment but a breach of obligation to this wider network. The chain of obligation extends outward: Mother sacrifices for Laura, Godmother enables Mother, and Laura's task is to make all of it worthwhile through achievement — a burden she cannot bear.

## Sacrifice's Failed Return

Mother's sacrifice is presented as an investment that fails to yield its expected return. In a devastating confrontation, Mother says:

> [024-011] "All I say is, big girls as you are, you deserve to have the nonsense whipped out of you. As for you, Laura, if this is your only return for all the money I've spent on you, then I wish from my heart you'd never seen the inside of that Melbourne school."

The language of "return" on "money spent" makes explicit the economic logic underlying the sacrifice: Mother has invested, and she expects a dividend. Laura's failure to produce that dividend — through examination success, improved manners, or even appropriate gratitude — represents not just a personal disappointment but a breach of the implied contract between parent and child. The unfinished sentence at [024-011] ("then I wish…") leaves the full force of Mother's regret unspoken but powerfully present.

The novel's refusal to complete Mother's sentence is one of its most effective narrative choices. What does Mother wish? That Laura had never been born? That she had never sacrificed? That she had sent her to a different school? The incompletion forces the reader to supply the ending — and the available completions are all devastating.

## Childish Self-Sacrifice and Adult Reality

The novel also glimpses the psychology of self-sacrifice in Laura's earlier life. As a child, Laura performs acts of self-sacrifice with a self-dramatizing pleasure that the narrator gently mocks — she is "lost in the pleasure of self-sacrifice" [001-056]. The gap between this childish performance and the real, adult sacrifice demanded by Mother's financial strain charts Laura's moral growth across the novel.

The phrase "pleasure of self-sacrifice" is devastating in its precision. The child's self-sacrifice is pleasurable precisely because it costs nothing real — it is a performance of virtue without material consequences. Adult sacrifice, by contrast, offers no such pleasure: it is measured in "pinching and screwing" [024-003], in nights of worry, in faces "scored" [002-053] by poverty and exhaustion. Laura's journey from the childish pleasure of performed sacrifice to the adult burden of received sacrifice is one of the novel's most compressed moral educations.
