# Friendship

Friendship in "The Getting of Wisdom" is not the sentimental refuge from institutional cruelty that the boarding-school genre might lead one to expect. Instead, it is the arena in which social hierarchies are most intimately reproduced — a field of shifting alliances, strategic calculations, and repeated betrayals that teaches Laura Rambotham harder lessons than any classroom ever could.

## In the Novel

Laura arrives at the Ladies' College with no experience of peer friendship — her social world has been limited to her siblings, to whom she relates as the imaginative leader of their small kingdom. The novel traces her painful education in the codes of school friendship through a series of relationships, each of which follows a similar arc: intense initial attachment, gradual exposure of inequality, and final disappointment.

The pattern is established early: Laura "had more than once begun a friendship" with characteristic intensity, only to find herself excluded from the inner circle (PN [[001-052]]). Her experience as "third in the friendship between Inez and Bertha" (PN [[009-006]]) — a position of tolerated proximity rather than genuine inclusion — becomes the template for her subsequent relationships. Each friendship teaches Laura something about the gap between what she wants (unconditional acceptance) and what the school's social economy permits (conditional, hierarchical association). The novel's most painful insight is that friendship, far from transcending social class, is the medium through which class distinctions are most keenly felt.

Laura's friendship with **Evelyn Souttar** represents both the high point and the collapse of her social aspirations. Evelyn — pretty, wealthy, effortlessly popular — embodies everything Laura wants to be, and for a brief period her attention seems to promise entry into the school's social elite. But the friendship proves asymmetrical: Evelyn distributes her affections widely while Laura invests everything.

When the inevitable disappointment comes, Laura is left with the bitter knowledge that "her present dear friendship had no more strength to endure than any of its predecessors" (PN [[023-057]]). The novel's closing pages suggest that Laura has begun to understand something her school could not teach: that the capacity for friendship requires a self sturdy enough to survive its loss.

## Key Passages

> She had more than once begun a friendship with the words: "Have you ever seen a girl with hair the colour of new copper?" — [[001-052]], Laura's signature mode of approach: intensity, imagination, the offer of a story

> She was privileged to be third in the friendship between Inez and Bertha — a favour which she had thus far enjoyed without a break. — [[009-006]], the comedy and pathos of the excluded third

> Laura had but a small talent for friendship; she did not grasp the constant give-and-take that it demands. — [[022-029]], the narrator's devastating assessment

## Characters

**Laura Rambotham** is the novel's student of friendship — a poor student, by the narrator's own admission, who brings too much intensity to relationships that others treat with casual flexibility. **Evelyn Souttar** represents the object of Laura's most intense attachment: the girl whose casual affection Laura mistakes for intimacy and whose inevitable withdrawal teaches the novel's hardest lesson.

**Lilith Gordon** is the anti-friend: her curiosity about Laura is voracious but never affectionate, and her exposure of Laura's secrets is the novel's purest example of social predation disguised as interest. **Inez and Bertha** function as a portrait of the exclusive dyad — the friendship that admits a third only as audience, never as equal.

**Mary Pidwall (M. P.)** offers the novel's one example of friendship grounded in respect rather than need: her relationship with Laura, though less passionate than the Evelyn attachment, suggests an alternative model based on intellectual parity rather than social aspiration.

## Historical Context

The culture of intense same-sex friendship — what Victorian and Edwardian discourse termed "romantic friendship" — provides the historical frame for the novel's treatment of the theme. In the 1890s, passionate attachments between schoolgirls were at once encouraged (as preparation for marriage, as moral influence) and surveilled (as potentially excessive, as a diversion from proper developmental trajectories).

The Ladies' College, with its dormitories, its shared bedrooms, and its constant proximity of female bodies, was a hothouse for such attachments — and the novel captures with painful precision the economy of jealousy, exclusion, and longing that this hothouse produced. Richardson's narrator maintains an ironic distance from Laura's passions, but the irony does not diminish the real pain they cause: the novel understands that for a girl in Laura's position, friendship is not a luxury but a lifeline, and its failure is a genuine catastrophe.

## Critical Perspective

Critics have read the novel's treatment of friendship through several interpretive lenses. Queer readings attend to the erotic charge of Laura's attachments — the intensity of her feeling for Evelyn, the charged language of jealousy and possession — and locate the novel within a tradition of boarding-school narratives that encode same-sex desire as "friendship." Feminist readings emphasize the structural constraints on girls' relationships: in a world where women's futures depend entirely on marriage, friendship between girls is both a training ground for romantic attachment and a site of resistance to it. Psychoanalytic readings treat Laura's pattern of intense attachment and inevitable disappointment as a repetition of the primary loss — her father's death — and her friendships as attempts to recover an impossible wholeness.
