## The Fashionable Promenade

> They reached the "block," that strip of Collins Street which forms the fashionable promenade. Here the road was full of cabs and carriages, and there was a great crowd on the pavement. — [[014-026]]

Collins Street's "block" is Melbourne's social theater. On Saturday mornings, the city's fashionable set promenades here — meeting friends, shopping at the library, eating ices at the confectioner's, buying fruit at the corner fruit-shop. The street is filled with "high-collared young dudes," Trinity and Ormond men with colored hatbands, ladies with parcels, and countless schoolgirls.

For Laura, the block represents a world of adult social performance she can observe but not yet inhabit. She walks as Tilly's "lame and unnecessary third," a spectator of flirtation rather than a participant.

## The Shopping Ritual

Shopping on Collins Street is not mere commerce but social ritual. Tilly swings her shoulders "in a somewhat provocative fashion," conscious of her fine eyes and modish clothes. She ogles passersby out of the corner of her eye while maintaining an appearance of absorption in conversation with Laura. The goal of the morning's promenade — to "unhook" a male acquaintance from a doorway group — is achieved through "glance and smile combined."

Richardson captures the doubleness of the shopping expedition: it is ostensibly about consumption (books, ices, fruit) but actually about being seen, about displaying oneself for male and female appraisal alike.

## The Eastern Market

> Collins Street was now as empty as a bush road. The young people went into Bourke Street, where, for want of something better to do, they entered the Eastern Market. — [[014-091]]

When the Collins Street promenade empties, Tilly, Bob, and Laura retreat to the Eastern Market — a sensory chaos of livestock, produce, and human noise. The market offers a counterpoint to Collins Street's fashionability: it is "ear-splitting," filled with "an extraordinary smell, composed of all the individual smells of all these living things."

Laura sniffs "like a spaniel" at the piquancy, but Tilly, whose temper is short and whose concerns are elsewhere, calls it a "vile stink." The contrast in their responses captures the difference between Laura's curiosity about the world and Tilly's single-minded focus on social performance.

## Bob's Appearance

The Collins Street scene culminates in Bob's appearance — the handsome cousin Laura has been hoping to encounter. But the meeting is a disappointment: Bob's attention is entirely on Tilly. He pulls at the curls on Tilly's neck; Laura sits "sandwiched between the other two," invisible.

> It came over her with a rush what a miserable failure the day had been. — [[014-091]]

Collins Street thus becomes the stage for one of Laura's characteristic humiliations: public, observed, and grounded in her inability to perform the feminine role that Tilly inhabits so effortlessly.

## Collins Street and Social Hierarchy

The block is stratified by invisible lines of class and status. Lucy "of Toorak" derives her authority from her address; the Trinity and Ormond men's colored hatbands mark their university affiliations. Everyone on Collins Street is performing an identity encoded in clothing, address, and manner. Laura, in her outdated clothes and provincial accent, is acutely aware of what she lacks — and what she lacks is legible to everyone who sees her.
