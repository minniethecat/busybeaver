# Sunday

Sunday in "The Getting of Wisdom" is the institutional Sabbath — a day when the Ladies' College's religious machinery is on full display. Between compulsory church attendance and supervised "Sunday fashion" strolling, the day reveals both the school's commitment to conventional piety and the students' varied responses to its demands.

## In the Novel

Sunday structures the school week as a day apart. The routine is rigid: "between breakfast and church" the students assemble, and then process to the church where they sit as a body — "the boarders in church every Sunday morning, the embodiment of the school's piety" (PN [[012-005]]). Laura's first Sunday at the school (PN [[008-050]]) introduces her to the rituals of institutional religion: Sunday school prizes in glass bookcases (PN [[008-051]]), the selection of "Sunday books" for approved reading (PN [[008-052]]), and the supervised "stroll about in Sunday fashion" in the garden (PN [[008-054]]).

On subsequent Sundays, Laura discovers the social dimension of church attendance — "fingering, in an agony of nervousness," the possibility of public performance (PN [[010-020]]). The day also becomes associated with bodily shame: Laura recalls "the fact of the next day being Sunday — ears and cheeks on fire" (PN [[014-038]]), linking the Sabbath to the novel's broader themes of exposure and embarrassment.

## Key Passages

> The next day was Sunday. Between breakfast and church there was an hour. — [[008-050]], the rhythm of the institutional Sabbath

> It must be a Sunday book. — [[008-052]], the approved culture of the Sabbath — reading policed for piety

> The boarders in church every Sunday morning, the embodiment of the school's piety. — [[012-005]], students as institutional display

> On several subsequent Sundays, Laura fingered, in an agony of nervousness, the possibility of distinction. — [[010-020]], the social anxiety beneath religious observance

## Significance

Sunday in the novel functions as a lens through which to examine the school's religious culture. The day's regimentation — approved books, supervised strolling, compulsory church — reveals an institution more concerned with the forms of piety than with its substance. Laura's own experience of Sunday is characteristically ambivalent: she is neither the devout scholar of the Bible class nor the indifferent truant, but something more complicated — a girl who "fingers" at distinction, even in the house of God.
