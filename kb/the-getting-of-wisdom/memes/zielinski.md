# Miss Zielinski

Miss Zielinski is the governess at the Ladies' College in "The Getting of Wisdom," responsible for the supervision of the younger boarders. Sharp, observant, and not above sarcasm, she serves as both a minor antagonist and an inadvertent catalyst for Laura's musical development.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Zielinski appears primarily in the early chapters of the novel, where she functions as a figure of institutional authority in the junior section of the school. Her first interaction with Laura establishes the dynamic: when Laura arrives asking for a shelf, Miss Zielinski replies without looking up (PN [[005-041]]–[[005-042]]). She later scolds Laura for staying upstairs (PN [[005-049]]), but also makes the crucial observation that Laura "can say some poetry" and "has read Scott" (PN [[007-016]]). Most significantly, it is Miss Zielinski who facilitates Laura's access to the piano and who — in a shrill whisper — remarks that Laura's father "had taught her" music (PN [[005-068]]), indirectly revealing the origins of Laura's talent to the school community.

## Character Traits

Miss Zielinski is **sharp-tongued and direct**: she "lost no opportunity of making a sarcastic remark" (PN [[005-060]]). She is also **observant**, noting Laura's unusual abilities — her poetry recitation and reading of Scott — even while maintaining a pose of indifference. Her **crossness** (PN [[005-049]]) reflects the institutional governess type: overworked, underappreciated, and quick to suppress any sign of disorder. Yet her recognition of Laura's talent suggests a more discerning intelligence beneath the gruff exterior.

## Key Passages

> Miss Zielinski — she was the governess in the junior house — looked up. — [[005-042]], Laura's introduction to the governess

> "What are you staying upstairs for?" asked Miss Zielinski crossly. — [[005-049]], the governess's characteristic tone

> She was startled to hear Miss Zielinski say, in a shrill whisper: "Her father taught her." — [[005-068]], the revelation of Laura's musical origins

> "She can say some poetry," said Miss Zielinski. "And she's read Scott." — [[007-016]], the governess's recognition of Laura's literary accomplishments

## Significance

Miss Zielinski represents one of the novel's functional institutional figures — neither villain nor ally, but a sharp-eyed observer whose casual remarks have outsized consequences. Her mention of Laura's father as a music teacher plants the seed of Laura's musical identity in the school's consciousness, making her one of the novel's agents of revelation.
