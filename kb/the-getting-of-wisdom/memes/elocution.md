# Elocution

Elocution in "The Getting of Wisdom" is more than a school subject — it is a theater of social performance where Laura Rambotham's intellectual gifts and social awkwardness collide, and where the Ladies' College's curriculum of feminine accomplishment is revealed as training in display rather than genuine education.

## In the Novel

Elocution appears as a formal subject at the Ladies' College, taught by Mr. Repton, the "visiting-master for this branch of study" (PN [[012-005]]). The subject encompasses recitation, deportment, and the art of speaking well — accomplishments deemed essential for young ladies in colonial Melbourne society. Mr. Repton's lessons are occasions for social performance as much as pedagogical instruction: students are evaluated not only on their delivery but on their bearing, their confidence, and their ability to command the room.

The elocution classroom becomes the setting for one of the novel's key dramatic sequences — the Annie Johns expulsion letter scene — where Mr. Repton's authority as visiting master intersects with the school's disciplinary machinery. The fact that this crisis unfolds during an elocution lesson is thematically significant: elocution is the art of saying things well, and the letter Laura is compelled to write is an exercise in saying something terrible with elegant form.

## Laura and Elocution

For Laura, elocution represents both opportunity and exposure. Her natural intelligence and verbal fluency make her a capable reciter, but her social insecurity — her fear of being seen, judged, and found wanting — makes the performative demands of the subject a source of anxiety. The elocution classroom replicates in miniature the larger social theater of the school: those who perform well are rewarded with approval; those who falter are exposed to ridicule. Laura's relationship to elocution mirrors her relationship to the school itself — she has the raw materials for success but cannot master the unwritten rules that govern their deployment.

## Significance

Elocution in the novel functions as a synecdoche for the Ladies' College curriculum as a whole. The subject's emphasis on performance, display, and the "correct" way to speak encodes the class anxieties of colonial Melbourne society: to speak well is to signal membership in the respectable classes, and to recite with feeling is to demonstrate the sensibility that marks a young lady. Yet the novel consistently ironizes this project — the students who excel at elocution are not necessarily the most thoughtful or most intelligent, and the art of "saying things well" is shown to be compatible with saying things that are false, cruel, or empty.
