# Holiday

The holidays in *The Getting of Wisdom* are not merely intervals between school terms but crucial narrative spaces in which the distance between Laura Rambotham's two worlds — the Ladies' College and the family home — becomes measurable. Each holiday marks a return to a world that has grown slightly more foreign, and each return to school marks a re-entry into a social environment that has shifted in her absence. The monthly holiday rhythm structures the novel's temporal architecture and charts Laura's progressive estrangement from her origins.

## The Monthly Holiday Rhythm

The College's calendar provides for a "monthly holiday — from Saturday till Monday" [[008-001]], a brief window of freedom that Laura initially anticipates with eagerness. The rhythm of these monthly respites punctuates the school year, offering Laura the illusion that she can move between her two worlds without friction. Laura "would sometimes spend a monthly holiday" at her godmother's house [[004-001]], an arrangement that places her in an intermediate social space — neither fully at home nor fully at school.

The brevity of these holidays — merely two nights away from the College — underscores their insufficiency. They are long enough to remind Laura of what she has left behind but too short to allow any genuine reintegration into family life. Each holiday is a taste of freedom that makes the return to school more painful, a glimpse of a world in which she is known rather than judged.

## The Midwinter Holidays

The longer midwinter holidays mark a more significant transition. After the "midwinter holidays" in August, Laura "was promoted to the second class" [[010-002]], linking the holiday period with academic advancement and suggesting that the breaks between terms are also periods of invisible growth. Laura returns from the midwinter holidays changed — not by anything that happened at home but by the accumulated experience of the preceding months, which the holiday pause allows to settle into her sense of self.

During these longer holidays at home, "she gave her sister and brothers" the benefit of her new knowledge [[010-003]] — a reversal of the earlier dynamic in which Laura was the ignorant one. The holiday thus becomes a stage on which Laura can perform her new sophistication, however awkwardly, for an audience that knows her old self. The performance is seldom entirely successful; her siblings' responses reveal the gap between Laura's school persona and the sister they remember.

## Holiday as Unmasking

The holidays also function as occasions of unmasking. Laura's mother, observing her daughter after a term at school, is described as watching her with an acute awareness of the changes the College has wrought — changes that are not always welcome. The holiday strips away the protective anonymity of school life and exposes Laura to a gaze that predates her carefully constructed school identity.

The "first holidays" after Laura's enrollment are particularly revealing: "At home, during those first holidays, she gave her sister and brothers" accounts of school life [[010-003]], but the accounts themselves are performances — edited, embellished, designed to impress. The holiday thus becomes another theater of self-presentation, different from the school's theater but no less demanding. Laura is never quite at rest, never quite herself, because the self she has become at school cannot be easily translated back into the language of home.

## Holiday at Godmother's

The holidays spent at Godmother's house represent yet another variant of the holiday experience. Godmother's house is neither school nor home — it is a third space, defined by a different set of expectations and a different quality of attention. "After a holiday at Godmother's, she received" news or gifts or instructions [[010-021]], the Godmother serving as an intermediary figure who bridges the gap between Laura's two worlds without belonging fully to either.

The Godmother's house offers Laura a kind of holiday from the holiday — a space in which she is neither daughter nor student but something in between, a visitor whose status is provisional and whose obligations are correspondingly light. These holidays at Godmother's are, in a sense, the novel's most peaceful interludes, precisely because they lack the emotional stakes of both home and school.

## The Winter Holidays and Romance

The "winter holidays" bring a new dimension to Laura's holiday experience: the possibility of romance. "At home during the winter holidays, his name chanced to cross her mind" [[016-003]] — the "his" being a young man Laura has encountered, and the holiday providing the leisure for romantic speculation. The holiday thus becomes the space in which Laura's emotional life, constrained by the routines and surveillance of the College, can expand into new territories.

Isabella's invitation — "tell us when you have another holiday" [[016-050]] — links the holiday to the social world beyond the College, suggesting that holidays are not merely returns to the old but openings toward the new. Each holiday carries the possibility of encounters that the College, with its enclosed female world, cannot provide.

## Significance

The holidays in *The Getting of Wisdom* serve as the novel's breathing spaces — the intervals in which the tensions accumulated during school terms can be registered, if not resolved. They are also the sites of the novel's most acute explorations of the divided self: Laura at home is never quite the Laura of the College, and Laura at the College can never fully inhabit the self that home remembers. The holiday, far from offering rest, exposes this division, making it visible to Laura and to those who observe her.
