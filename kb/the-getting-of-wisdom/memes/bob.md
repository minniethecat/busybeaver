# Bob

**Bob** is Tilly Macnamara's handsome cousin, a young man of about nineteen who becomes the focus of Laura Rambotham's first experience with romantic attraction and social failure. Though his appearances in the novel are concentrated in a few chapters, his impact on Laura's self-understanding is profound.

## Appearance and Demeanour

Bob is described in strikingly handsome terms that set him apart from the ordinary boys the schoolgirls typically encounter:

> "Bob was a beautiful youth of seventeen or eighteen, with liquid brown eyes and a careless, laughing manner." (PN [[014-006]])

His physical appeal makes him a desirable catch among the schoolgirls, with specific details of his appearance — his eyes, his teeth, his smile — noted with the intense attention of adolescent admiration. Laura observes his "liquid eyes" and "blanched-almond teeth" (PN [[015-003]]), features that make it hard for her to dismiss him from her mind even after their encounter ends badly.

## The Outing with Tilly

Bob first enters Laura's life when Tilly Macnamara arranges an outing. Tilly informs Laura:

> "My cousin Bob's awfully gone on you." (PN [[014-001]])

The preparation for the meeting reveals the elaborate social choreography expected of adolescent girls. Tilly coaches Laura on every detail — she must not wear her brown hat because "Bob can't stand brown" (PN [[014-008]]), and must wear blue because "Bob loves blue" (PN [[014-011]]). Tilly even applies vaseline to Laura's eyebrows, explaining that it makes them grow (PN [[014-058]]–[[014-059]]).

## The Picnic Scene

The meeting takes place at a picnic outing where Bob is accompanied by his father, referred to as "pater." The scene unfolds in a series of awkward exchanges that reveal Laura's complete inability to perform the expected social role:

| Moment | Laura's Response | Expected Response |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Bob's arrival | Unable to speak | Flirtatious banter |
| Bob's compliment about gloves | Blushes, says nothing | Witty reply |
| Sitting together at picnic | Stiff, silent | Easy conversation |
| Bob's wink | Goes crimson | Playful acknowledgment |

> "Laura and Bob went different shades of crimson." (PN [[014-065]])

Bob himself is not without awkwardness. When his father embarrasses him, he murmurs "Oh, chuck it, pater!" in disgust (PN [[014-067]]), revealing the familiar tension between a young man and an overbearing parent.

## Laura's Failure and Its Aftermath

After the outing, Bob's verdict is damning: he finds Laura "dull as ditchwater" (PN [[015-002]]). For Laura, this judgment cuts deeply — not because she had desired Bob's attention in the first place, but because she had failed at a social performance that others seemed to master effortlessly:

> "She had not wanted Bob, had never asked him to be 'gone' on her. And the circumstance that, because he had singled her out approvingly, she was expected to worm herself into his favour, seemed to her of a monstrous injustice." (PN [[014-034]])

Laura's introspection after the failure reveals her fundamental outsider status. She studies her more successful classmates — Maria Morell, Bertha Ramsey, Inez — and recognizes that they possess an art of romantic performance that she lacks entirely. "She remained where the other sex was concerned a disheartening failure" (PN [[015-021]]).

## Later References

The incident haunts Laura. In Chapter XVII, her "maladroit handling of Bob" still rankles, and she resolves to "kill that smile" that plays on Tilly's lips whenever the subject is mentioned (PN [[017-008]]). Kate Horner later references the episode dismissively, noting that when she "took her out to see my cousin Bob, she couldn't say boo to a goose" (PN [[018-063]]).

## Significance

Bob serves as a narrative device through which Richardson explores the painful gap between Laura's genuine self and the social expectations placed upon adolescent girls. Laura's inability to flirt, to perform, to play the game of romantic attraction — all of this marks her as different, as an outsider in a culture that values these skills above academic achievement. Bob is less a character in his own right than a mirror in which Laura sees her own social inadequacy reflected.
