# Piano

The **piano** serves as one of the central symbols of ability, identity, and social positioning in *The Getting of Wisdom*. For [[laura-rambotham]], musical talent represents her most significant — and perhaps her only — natural advantage within the competitive social world of the [[ladies-college]]. Unlike the accomplishments of her wealthier peers, which are often shown to be superficial or purchased, Laura's pianistic ability is presented as an authentic gift, a form of expression that bypasses the social artifice in which she otherwise struggles [[009-022]]. Yet the novel is careful to show that even genuine talent is not immune to the distorting pressures of institutional life and adolescent social competition.

## The Grand Piano at Mrs. Strachey's

The physical instrument itself becomes a charged object in the novel's early chapters. Laura's access to "the grand piano in Mrs. Strachey's drawing-room" [[021-004]] marks her initiation into a world of middle-class accomplishment and aspiration. The setting is significant: the piano occupies the domestic space of the headmistress, not a public concert hall, underscoring how musical performance at the Ladies' College is simultaneously intimate and evaluative. Laura's "morning practice" [[021-004]] on this instrument places her literally within the headmistress's domain, a spatial arrangement that mirrors the scrutiny under which all her talents will be held.

> Laura rose and went to the piano, without seeking advice from Polly or being confused by the trying noise that Chinky made. [[009-034]]

This moment of unselfconscious competence contrasts sharply with Laura's usual social anxiety. At the keyboard, she does not need to navigate the bewildering codes of schoolgirl society; the instrument provides a structured, rule-governed space in which she can excel without pretense [[009-017]].

## Musical Talent as Social Currency

Richardson portrays musical ability as a peculiar kind of social asset — one that can confer status but also invite resentment. Laura's skill at the piano distinguishes her from peers who have "an ingrained distrust of the unmusical" [[009-033]]. The Saturday evening gatherings in "an empty music-room" [[021-010]] reveal the dual function of musical performance: it is both entertainment and social proving-ground. Laura's performances earn her temporary recognition, but the novel is attentive to the fragility of this esteem. Talent alone cannot secure lasting acceptance in a community that values conformity above accomplishment.

The hymnbook distributed at school assemblies, "one with music in it being handed to Miss Ch---" [[005-063]], further illustrates how musical ability is woven into the fabric of institutional life. Music at the Ladies' College serves religious observance, public ceremony, and private recreation, making pianistic skill a versatile if unstable form of cultural capital.

## Practice and Discipline

The discipline required for musical mastery provides a counterpoint to Laura's often chaotic social education. Where Laura struggles to learn the unwritten rules of schoolgirl society, her application to the piano is methodical and effective. The novel draws an implicit parallel between the repetitive discipline of musical practice and the rote "application" with which Laura "applied herself to reach the school" standard [[009-015]]. Both require patient repetition; both demand submission to external authority. Yet only the piano rewards her efforts with visible improvement and genuine pleasure.

This contrast between musical discipline and social bewilderment points to one of the novel's deeper insights: that institutional education values certain forms of learning while remaining blind to others. Laura can master a difficult piece of music through solitary effort, but the curriculum offers no equivalent path to mastering the intricate social choreography of boarding-school life.

## Music as Emotional Refuge

Beyond its social functions, the piano offers Laura an emotional outlet that the rigid institutional environment otherwise denies her. The act of playing allows for a kind of authentic self-expression unavailable in the dining hall or the classroom. Richardson uses musical performance to reveal dimensions of Laura's character — sensitivity, concentration, emotional depth — that remain invisible to her schoolfellows and teachers alike. The piano becomes, paradoxically, both the most public demonstration of Laura's abilities and her most private emotional resource.

> Laura was the least embarrassed: she was not, as the others were, disconcerted by the silence which descended as she rose. [[009-022]]

This composure at the instrument signals something essential about Laura's character. Where social interaction produces anxiety and missteps, musical performance produces calm and competence. The piano reveals a version of Laura that the school's ordinary social mechanisms cannot perceive.
