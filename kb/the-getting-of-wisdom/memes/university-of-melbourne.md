# University of Melbourne

The University of Melbourne is the unseen destination toward which the novel's most ambitious characters — and the narrative itself — implicitly aspire. Though never directly depicted, it functions as the symbolic summit of the educational hierarchy: the place where learning is pursued for its own sake, where degrees are "taken out" rather than accomplishments displayed, and where the gendered divisions that structure the Ladies' College curriculum begin, however imperfectly, to dissolve.

## In the Novel

The university appears in the novel only through its residential colleges — Trinity and Ormond — and through the figure of M. P. (Mary Pidwall), the school's most intellectually accomplished student, who "had gone up to the university" (PN [[025-008]]). For Laura, the university represents the possibility of a life beyond the school's narrow horizons: "She was going on to Trinity, where she intended to take out a degree" (PN [[025-008]]). This fantasy of university education is both a genuine aspiration (Laura is intellectually capable of university study) and a social gesture (to "go up to the university" is to claim membership in the educated class).

The university's absence from the novel's direct representation is itself significant. The Ladies' College world is one from which the university is visible but not accessible — a place where "church girls" can watch the coloured blazers at the boat-race but cannot themselves participate. The novel's gender critique is sharpest here: the education that Laura receives is designed not to prepare her for further study but to equip her with the "accomplishments" that will secure a husband. The university, by contrast, offers knowledge without reference to the marriage market.

## Historical Context

The University of Melbourne was founded in 1853 and by the 1890s was the only university in Victoria. It admitted women to degree programs from 1881, well ahead of many English institutions, though female undergraduates remained a small minority. The residential colleges — Trinity (Anglican, 1872) and Ormond (Presbyterian, 1881) — provided accommodation and social life for male students from the Protestant establishment. Neither admitted women as residents until the 1970s, meaning that women like M. P. who "went up to the university" attended as non-resident students, excluded from the college life that constituted much of the university's social experience.

## Significance

The University of Melbourne in the novel functions as the symbolic outside of the Ladies' College world — the place where education might become something other than the production of marriageable young ladies. That Laura's fantasy of attending is presented as plausible (she is intellectually capable) but uncertain (the novel does not confirm that she will go) captures the novel's ambivalence about the possibilities available to its protagonist. The university represents a genuine alternative to the constrained feminine trajectory of the Ladies' College, but it is an alternative that, like the "handsome Trinity man" on the dock, Laura can only watch from a distance.
