# Garden

The garden is a recurring setting in [[the-getting-of-wisdom]], functioning as both a physical space and a symbolic landscape where the emotional life of [[laura-rambotham]] unfolds. From the garden at her family home in the opening chapters to the ornamental grounds of [[ladies-college]], gardens provide Laura with spaces of retreat, play, and social navigation. Richardson uses the garden to mark transitions in Laura's inner world, from childhood innocence to the fraught social awareness of adolescence.

## The Garden at Rambotham Home

Laura's earliest significant moments occur in the garden of the family home. On the eve of her departure for school, she retreats to the garden seeking solace:

> Laura returned to the garden. The two little boys came up to her. — [[001-036]]

The garden here is a space of intimate family connection, where Laura interacts freely with her brothers [[frank]] and [[joey]]. It is also the place where she composes herself for public view, "in a becoming attitude by the garden-gate" [[001-038]], suggesting an early awareness of self-presentation. The garden-gate marks the threshold between the private world of home and the public world beyond.

In a moment of anxiety about the coming separation, Laura "made her way to a secluded part of the garden on the farther side of the deep verandah" [[001-050]], using the garden's hidden corners to process her emotions in solitude. The garden at Rambotham thus functions as an emotional refuge, a pattern that will continue at the Ladies' College.

## The Summerhouse as Sanctuary

The family garden contains two summerhouses that feature significantly in Laura's imaginative life. On her final morning at home, she "ran down the garden to the summerhouse. This, the size of a goodly room" [[002-011]], where she reads with a peculiar absorption, as if trying to absorb every detail before leaving. The summerhouse represents the world of private fantasy and reading that Laura must soon exchange for institutional life.

Before departing, Laura makes a ceremonial circuit of her childhood landscape: "on the heels of this she made a round of the garden, bidding goodbye to things and places" [[002-030]]. The two summerhouses where she "had played house" are given specific mention, underscoring their role as stages for her childhood imagination. This farewell tour is a ritual of [[childhood]]'s end, the garden bearing witness to the passage from one phase of life to another.

## The Ladies' College Grounds

At the [[ladies-college]], the garden takes on new functions. It becomes a space for regulated social interaction: "in the afternoon after school we walk two and two" [[006-007]]. The garden path is where alliances are formed and social hierarchies enacted. Laura writes to her [[mother]] describing her discovery that "by the corner of the garden a paling is lose and the boys come" [[006-007]], revealing the garden's boundary as a site of transgressive contact with the outside world.

The garden also serves as the setting for the school's theatrical self-fashioning. Miss Blount, the English teacher, stages historical scenes for her pupils: "such as 'Not Angles but Angels'" [[007-022]], converting the garden into an impromptu theater of [[education]] and imperial mythology. Meanwhile, the more athletic girls call out, "who's on for a race up the garden?" [[007-060]], using the grounds for physical competition and display.

## Social Performance and Exclusion

The garden path is where social status is displayed and measured. Laura observes with envy how certain girls "could link arms and walk the garden with complacency" [[009-007]], a privilege of popularity that she herself struggles to attain. The phrase "with complacency" captures the self-satisfaction of those securely positioned within the school's social hierarchy.

On Sundays, the garden's function shifts toward decorous display: "the boys were dispatched to the garden, to stroll about in Sunday fashion" [[008-054]], and Laura herself passes "through the garden-gate, and turned to walk townwards" [[008-010]], the gate now marking her first taste of freedom during her initial weeks at the school. The garden-gate at the Ladies' College, like its counterpart at the Rambotham home, is a threshold between constraint and liberty.

## Garden as Emotional Landscape

Richardson frequently uses the garden to register Laura's emotional state. After a particularly painful humiliation, Laura "retreated to the bottom of the garden to give vent to her feelings" [[010-012]], the far end of the grounds providing the privacy that the communal life of the school otherwise denies. This echoes her earlier retreat to "a secluded part of the garden" at home [[001-050]], establishing a pattern that runs throughout the novel: when wounded, Laura seeks the garden's farthest reaches.

In the novel's final chapter, returning from the university examination, Laura walks "the well-known paths of the garden once again" [[025-005]], the familiar grounds now charged with the accumulated memory of her school years. The garden has become a palimpsest of her emotional history — a place that has witnessed her humiliations, her small triumphs, and the slow, painful acquisition of [[wisdom]].
