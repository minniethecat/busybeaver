## Overview

Music plays a central role in the social rituals of the boarding school that Laura Rambotham attends. Musical performance—particularly on the piano—serves as a marker of middle-class refinement and feminine accomplishment, and Laura's encounters with the institution's musical expectations reveal both her naivety and the rigid social codes governing the school (009-017).

## Music as Social Ritual

Evening gatherings at the school are structured around musical practice. After supper, hymnbooks "with music in it" are distributed, and the community participates in collective singing, reinforcing shared values through musical worship (005-063). This ritualized use of music establishes a baseline of communal expectation against which individual musical performances are judged.

## The Drawing-Room Performance

A pivotal episode occurs when Laura is invited to Mrs. Strachey's drawing-room and is expected to bring and perform a piece of music. Unaware of the social conventions, Laura "hastily select[s] a volume of music" and follows the other girls (009-019). Her choice of Thalberg—a composer of showy, virtuosic salon pieces—is later condemned by Mrs. Gurley as "a gross impertinence, in profaning the ears of the Principal and Mrs. Strachey with Thalberg's music" (009-053).

## Classical Taste vs. Popular Music

The episode reveals a cultural hierarchy in musical taste. Mrs. Strachey gently suggests Laura "will soon get to like classical music also," speaking "gravely, and in all good faith" (009-043). Her comment reflects an ingrained assumption that classical music represents cultivated taste, which all properly educated young ladies should acquire. Laura herself is described as one for whom Mrs. Strachey feels "the ingrained distrust of the unmusical" (009-033).

## Music Masters and Education

Formal musical instruction forms part of the school curriculum, though the music-masters are regarded with less prestige than other faculty members (016-001). This hierarchy among teachers mirrors the broader social valuation of musical accomplishment: it is expected of refined young women, yet the instructors who impart it occupy a lower rung in the school's professional order.

## Personal Indifference

Despite the social weight placed on musical taste, Laura herself is notably unaffected by the aesthetic judgment passed on her performance: "The matter of the music touched Laura little: if they preferred their dull old exercises to what she had offered them, so much the worse for them" (009-054). Her indifference to the musical critique stands in contrast to the sting she feels from the "reproach cast on her manners"—revealing that for Laura, social acceptance matters more than artistic validation.
