# Society

Hugo's *Les Misérables* is, from its preface onward, a work of social indictment. "So long as there shall exist, by virtue of law and custom, a social condemnation which creates hells in the midst of civilization," Hugo writes, "books like this cannot be useless." The novel's title — "The Wretched" — names not individuals but a class: those whom society has produced and then abandoned.

## Hugo's Exposition

The Bishop's social philosophy, which Hugo clearly endorses, is stated with remarkable directness:

> (004-016) "Teach those who are ignorant as many things as possible; society is culpable, in that it does not afford instruction gratis; it is responsible for the night which it produces. This soul is full of shadow; sin is therein committed. The guilty one is not the person who has committed the sin, but the person who has created the shadow."

This is a radical claim: society, not the individual criminal, bears primary responsibility for crime. The argument is not that individuals lack free will but that society systematically deprives people of the conditions — education, opportunity, dignity — necessary for moral flourishing. To punish the products of this deprivation without addressing its causes is, in Hugo's view, itself a form of injustice.

Hugo extends this critique to economic structures. The law, he writes, "sells" the air that God gives freely (004-007). The market for labor, the price of bread, the cost of shelter — all are structured to extract profit from the poor while condemning them for the poverty these structures produce. Fantine is destroyed not by one villain but by an entire system: the lover who abandons her, the Thénardiers who exploit her, the factory that dismisses her, the police who arrest her.

## Development Through the Narrative

The novel's panoramic scope is itself an argument about society. Hugo interrupts the narrative for extended essays on Waterloo, the Paris sewers, argot (slang), and the convent — each a window into a different aspect of the social order. The barricade scenes in Volume IV represent society in microcosm: revolutionaries, workers, students, and the poor converge in a space where the normal hierarchies are temporarily suspended.

The Thénardiers serve as society's mirror. Their cruelty and greed are not presented as innate evil but as the desperate logic of those who have been brutalized by poverty. Thénardier is what Jean Valjean might have become without the Bishop's mercy — a man whom society has taught that only exploitation pays.

Javert's suicide implicitly indicts the social order he served. When Javert discovers that the law, the one structure that gave his life meaning, is morally bankrupt, he has no other identity to fall back on. Society created Javert as surely as it created the criminals he pursued.

## Historical and Social Context

*Les Misérables* was published in 1862, a period of intense social debate in France. The July Monarchy, the 1848 Revolution, and the Second Empire under Napoleon III had each proposed different answers to the "social question" — the problem of mass poverty in an industrializing society. Hugo, in exile on Guernsey, wrote from the perspective of one who had seen revolutions succeed and fail, and who had come to believe that structural change, not mere charity, was required.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **individual-virtue** — the claim that poverty is the result of personal failing; Hugo's entire novel refutes this
- **charity-alone** — what Hugo calls the "amelioration" approach; necessary but insufficient without structural change
- **revolution** — the violent response to social injustice; Hugo's attitude is ambivalent but ultimately sympathetic
- **the-law** — the mechanism by which society enforces its inequalities
