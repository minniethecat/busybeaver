# Sin

Hugo's treatment of sin is one of the most radical aspects of *Les Misérables*. He does not deny the reality of sin — the novel is full of genuinely evil acts — but he insists on tracing sin to its source in social deprivation. "The guilty one," Bishop Myriel declares, "is not the person who has committed the sin, but the person who has created the shadow" (004-016). This is not a denial of individual responsibility; it is a redistribution of responsibility to include society itself.

## Hugo's Exposition

The Bishop's social theology of sin is the novel's moral foundation:

> (004-016) "Teach those who are ignorant as many things as possible; society is culpable, in that it does not afford instruction gratis; it is responsible for the night which it produces. This soul is full of shadow; sin is therein committed. The guilty one is not the person who has committed the sin, but the person who has created the shadow."

This passage rewrites the Christian doctrine of original sin. The "shadow" in which sin occurs is not the inherited stain of Adam but the concrete darkness of ignorance, poverty, and exclusion created by social structures. The primary sinner is not the thief who steals bread but the society that made bread unavailable.

Hugo is careful not to dissolve personal responsibility entirely. Valjean's theft is a sin; his nineteen years in the galleys represent, in part, the natural consequence of his actions. But the disproportion between the sin and its punishment — nineteen years for a loaf of bread — reveals a society more interested in vengeance than in justice or rehabilitation.

The Bishop's teaching on sin culminates in his treatment of the death penalty. The guillotine is not justice but society's own sin — the premeditated, calculated taking of a life that belongs only to God. "Death belongs to God alone. By what right do men touch that unknown thing?" (004-032).

## Development Through the Narrative

The novel's trajectory traces sin's transformation through mercy. Valjean begins as a sinner (the theft), is punished as a sinner (the galleys), emerges hardened (the theft from Petit Gervais), and then — through the Bishop's unmerited mercy — begins the long process of becoming a saint. The key theological claim is that sin is not an ontological state but a condition that can be transformed. No one in Hugo's universe is irredeemable — not even Thénardier, who is offered, and refuses, the chance at transformation that Valjean accepted.

The contrasting treatment is the Thénardiers, who represent sin that has become structural — not the sin of desperation but the sin of systematic exploitation. They are what Valjean might have become: poverty, followed by crime, followed by the hardening of the heart that makes mercy impossible. Yet Hugo refuses to condemn even them absolutely; the social analysis that explains their condition always tempers the moral judgment.

## Historical and Social Context

The Catholic doctrine of sin in 19th-century France emphasized individual culpability and sacramental confession. Hugo's social reinterpretation — sin as socially produced, society as the primary sinner — was a direct challenge to this framework. It aligned him more with socialist thinkers who saw poverty as a structural problem than with the Church, which (in Hugo's view) too often treated poverty as an occasion for charity rather than a demand for justice.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **virtue** — not, for Hugo, the mere absence of sin but active love and justice
- **innocence** — the condition of those (like Fantine) who have been sinned against but have not sinned
- **grace** — the divine force that transforms sin into sanctity, operating through human agents like the Bishop
- **the-law** — which punishes sin without understanding it
