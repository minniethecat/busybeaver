# The Elephant of the Bastille

The elephant of the Bastille is one of Hugo's most memorable images — a forty-foot plaster model of a bronze elephant that Napoleon intended to build on the site of the demolished Bastille prison. The bronze was never cast. The plaster model decayed, inhabited by rats and, in Hugo's novel, by the street child Gavroche.

## In the Narrative
Gavroche lives inside the elephant's leg — his "apartment" is a space within the decaying monument, furnished with a straw mattress and protected from the elements by the elephant's hollow body. The image is simultaneously comic (a child living in an elephant) and devastating (the empire's abandoned ambition becomes the only shelter available to the empire's abandoned child).

## Hugo's Description
Hugo describes the elephant with the precision of an architectural historian and the imagination of a surrealist. It is a monument to imperial hubris, rotting in place, and the child who lives inside it is the living reproach to the society that built monuments instead of schools.

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance
The elephant embodies **imperial decay** — the grandiose projects of power that, when abandoned, serve only the rats and the street children that the empire created and forgot.
