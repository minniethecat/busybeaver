# Fantine

Fantine is *Les Misérables*'s most concentrated argument. Her story — a young woman destroyed not by villainy but by an indifferent social order — is Hugo's indictment of a society that manufactures misery and then punishes those who bear it. She appears only in Books III–V of Volume I, dying before the novel is a quarter finished, but her shadow falls across every subsequent page.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Fantine (surname unknown; "Fantine" may be a nickname)
- **Role**: Tragic figure — the novel's case study in socially-produced suffering
- **First Appearance**: Volume I, Book III, Chapter I — "The Year 1817" (038-001)
- **Social Position**: Grisette (working-class Parisian woman, not quite a prostitute) → abandoned mother → factory worker → prostitute → corpse
- **Key Relationships**: Félix Tholomyès (lover who abandoned her), Cosette (daughter), the Thénardiers (Cosette's exploiters), Jean Valjean (the mayor who failed to protect her and the man who promised to care for her child)

## Character Arc

Fantine's trajectory is a downward line, plotted with remorseless logic. She begins as a young grisette in Paris, in love with a bourgeois student named Félix Tholomyès. The "love" is, on his side, a dalliance; Fantine, naive and trusting, does not understand this until he abandons her — leaving her pregnant with Cosette.

Unable to find work in Paris with an illegitimate child, Fantine returns to her hometown of Montreuil-sur-Mer, leaving Cosette with the Thénardiers — an innkeeper family who will systematically exploit both mother and child for the rest of the novel. Fantine finds work in Madeleine's factory, but when her illegitimate child is discovered, she is dismissed — not by Madeleine (who knows nothing of it) but by the factory forewoman, acting on the cruel logic of "respectability."

What follows is Hugo's anatomy of destruction. Fantine sells her hair, then her teeth, then her body — each sale a step down a ladder that has no bottom. The money goes to the Thénardiers, who invent ever-more-urgent crises (Cosette needs a winter dress! Cosette is ill!) to extort more from a woman who has nothing left.

When Javert arrests Fantine for striking a bourgeois who insulted her, Valjean (as Madeleine) intervenes — too late to save her life but in time to receive her dying plea: "Take care of my child." His promise to the dying Fantine binds him to Cosette and structures the remainder of the novel.

## Key Actions

- **Abandonment by Tholomyès** (Book III, Chapter IX) — the original wound: left with a child and no resources
- **Entrusting Cosette to the Thénardiers** (Book IV, Chapter I) — the fatal decision, made in desperation, that will destroy her
- **Dismissal from the factory** (Book V, Chapter VIII) — the point of no return: respectable employment lost
- **Sale of her hair and teeth** (Book V, Chapter X) — the body dismantled piece by piece for the sake of a child she cannot protect
- **Deathbed scene** (Book V, Chapter XIII) — Fantine dies believing Cosette is near, while Valjean, now revealed as a convict, is being arrested by Javert

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo's treatment of Fantine is unsparing and accusatory. The narrative voice, usually expansive and digressive, becomes tight and prosecutorial when describing her suffering:

> (039-018) Injustice had made her peevish, and misery had made her ugly. Nothing remained to her except her beautiful hair and her beautiful teeth.

The accumulation of detail — the two napoleons for her hair, the ten for her teeth, the gradual transformation of a beautiful young woman into a skeletal figure the doctor says "will not live through the night" — is Hugo's rhetorical strategy. He is not asking for pity; he is building a case.

## Symbolic / Thematic Role

Fantine embodies **the victim** — not of individual cruelty (though there is plenty) but of a social system that makes her vulnerable and then punishes her vulnerability. She is the human face of Hugo's argument that poverty is not a moral failing but a structural condition, and that society's treatment of the poor is the true crime.
