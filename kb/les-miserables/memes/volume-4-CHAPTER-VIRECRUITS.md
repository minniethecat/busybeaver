[276-001]The band augmented every moment. Near the Rue des Billettes, a man of lofty stature, whose hair was turning gray, and whose bold and daring mien was remarked by Courfeyrac, [[enjolras|Enjolras]], and Combeferre, but whom none of them knew, joined them. [[gavroche|Gavroche]], who was occupied in singing, whistling, humming, running on ahead and pounding on the shutters of the shops with the butt of his triggerless pistol; paid no attention to this man.

[276-002]It chanced that in the Rue de la Verrerie, they passed in front of Courfeyrac’s door.

[276-003]“This happens just right,” said Courfeyrac, “I have forgotten my purse, and I have lost my hat.”

[276-004]He quitted the mob and ran up to his quarters at full speed. He seized an old hat and his purse.

[276-005]He also seized a large square coffer, of the dimensions of a large valise, which was concealed under his soiled linen.

[276-006]As he descended again at a run, the portress hailed him:—

[276-007]“Monsieur de Courfeyrac!”

[276-008]“What’s your name, portress?”

[276-009]The portress stood bewildered.

[276-010]“Why, you know perfectly well, I’m the concierge; my name is Mother Veuvain.”

[276-011]“Well, if you call me Monsieur de Courfeyrac again, I shall call you Mother de Veuvain. Now speak, what’s the matter? What do you want?”

[276-012]“There is some one who wants to speak with you.”

[276-013]“Who is it?”

[276-014]“I don’t know.”

[276-015]“Where is he?”

[276-016]“In my lodge.”

[276-017]“The devil!” ejaculated Courfeyrac.

[276-018]“But the person has been waiting your return for over an hour,” said the portress.

[276-019]At the same time, a sort of pale, thin, small, freckled, and youthful artisan, clad in a tattered blouse and patched trousers of ribbed velvet, and who had rather the air of a girl accoutred as a man than of a man, emerged from the lodge and said to Courfeyrac in a voice which was not the least in the world like a woman’s voice:—

[276-020]“Monsieur [[marius-pontmercy|Marius]], if you please.”

[276-021]“He is not here.”

[276-022]“Will he return this evening?”

[276-023]“I know nothing about it.”

[276-024]And Courfeyrac added:—

[276-025]“For my part, I shall not return.”

[276-026]The young man gazed steadily at him and said:—

[276-027]“Why not?”

[276-028]“Because.”

[276-029]“Where are you going, then?”

[276-030]“What business is that of yours?”

[276-031]“Would you like to have me carry your coffer for you?”

[276-032]“I am going to the barricades.”

[276-033]“Would you like to have me go with you?”

[276-034]“If you like!” replied Courfeyrac. “The street is free, the pavements belong to every one.”

[276-035]And he made his escape at a run to join his friends. When he had rejoined them, he gave the coffer to one of them to carry. It was only a quarter of an hour after this that he saw the young man, who had actually followed them.

[276-036]A mob does not go precisely where it intends. We have explained that a gust of wind carries it away. They overshot Saint-Merry and found themselves, without precisely knowing how, in the Rue Saint-Denis.
