# Conscience

In *Les Misérables*, conscience is not a passive internal voice but an active tribunal — the "inner tribunal" (013-001) before which every character must stand. Hugo positions conscience as the divine law made personal, the mechanism by which grace enters a hardened soul, and the only court whose verdict truly matters.

## Hugo's Exposition

Hugo develops his theology of conscience most explicitly through Bishop Myriel. In the chapter "What He Believed," the narrator states the Bishop's conviction:

> (013-001) The conscience of the just man should be accepted on its word. Moreover, granted the few faculties which he possessed, he considered that in the silence of the inner tribunal of the conscience are known only to the tomb, where the soul arrives stripped of all pretense.

For Myriel, conscience functions as a kind of direct line to divine truth — it bypasses human institutions entirely. This is why the Bishop can pardon Valjean without consulting any earthly authority: his conscience tells him that the silver was not stolen but reclaimed for a higher purpose.

The Conventionist G——, in his deathbed debate with the Bishop, pushes this idea to its logical conclusion. When the Bishop appeals to "conscience" as a counterweight to revolutionary violence, the dying man retorts that the Bishop's conscience is too comfortable — it has never had to make the terrible choices that history forces upon those who act. This confrontation establishes conscience not as a simple guide but as something that must be tested against reality.

## Development Through the Narrative

Jean Valjean's entire moral trajectory is a drama of conscience. After the Bishop's pardon, Valjean experiences what Hugo calls "a troubled and uneasy conscience, which has arrived on the brink of a precipice" (025-021). The theft of Petit Gervais's coin — committed almost automatically, out of ingrained habit — becomes the catalyst for his conversion precisely because his newly awakened conscience cannot bear it.

The most extended treatment comes in the chapter "A Tempest in a Skull" (Book VII, Chapter III), where Valjean spends an entire night wrestling with the choice between self-preservation and revelation at Arras. Hugo describes conscience as a courtroom in which the self is simultaneously prosecutor, defendant, and judge:

> (027-083) His conscience weighed in turn these two men placed before it — the Bishop and the convict. The Bishop, like a great light, the convict, like a great shadow.

Valjean's decision to confess — to "do justice" to himself — is the novel's supreme example of conscience operating as action, not mere sentiment. It costs him everything in worldly terms and gains him, in Hugo's reckoning, his soul.

Javert provides the counterpoint: a man whose conscience has been entirely outsourced to the legal code. When Javert finds himself unable to arrest Valjean after being spared at the barricade, his conscience — for the first time — conflicts with the law. Unable to reconcile the two, he chooses the Seine. Hugo presents this not as conscience failing but as the tragedy of a conscience that never learned to function independently of rules.

## Historical and Social Context

Hugo's elevation of individual conscience reflects both Catholic moral theology (the examination of conscience before confession) and the Romantic reaction against Enlightenment rationalism. Where the philosophes had sought universal principles discoverable by reason, Hugo insists that the deepest moral truths are felt, not deduced — and that society's laws, far from embodying reason, systematically violate the conscience of anyone who looks at them honestly.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **law** — the external code; conscience is law internalized and transformed by love
- **temptation** — what conscience struggles against; the dramatic tension of moral choice
- **legalism** — conscience's enemy; the substitution of rules for moral perception
- **sin** — the darkness that conscience illuminates and, through grace, can overcome
