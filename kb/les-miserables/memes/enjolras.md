# Enjolras

Enjolras is *Les Misérables*'s portrait of revolutionary purity. He is not a person so much as an idea made flesh: the Republic incarnate. (176-014) Hugo introduces him as "a charming young man, who was capable of being terrible." His death on the barricade, serene before the firing squad, is the novel's definitive image of political martyrdom.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Enjolras (first name only; Hugo never gives him a surname)
- **Role**: Revolutionary leader — the logic of the Republic
- **First Appearance**: Volume III, Book IV, Chapter I — "A Group Which Barely Missed Becoming Historic" (176-014)
- **Social Position**: Wealthy only son → student revolutionary → leader of the Friends of the ABC → commander of the barricade → executed by firing squad
- **Key Relationships**: Combeferre (the guide, the philosophy to his logic), Courfeyrac (the center, the warmth to his coldness), Grantaire (the sceptic who worships him), [[marius-pontmercy]] (the latecomer to the revolution), [[gavroche]] (the spirit of Paris who serves his command)

## Character Arc

Enjolras is the novel's figure of the absolute. (176-016) By the side of Enjolras, "who represented the logic of the Revolution," stands Combeferre, who represents its philosophy. Together they form the mind of the insurrection: Enjolras the implacable will, Combeferre the humane intelligence.

Hugo draws Enjolras in the language of marble and light. He is "a charming young man," with a blue eye, a severe and beautiful face, and a voice that commands. (176-015) He is wealthy, an only son, educated — and he has given all of it to the cause. His mother, he tells Marius, is the Republic. (180-033)

His leadership at the barricade is absolute. He organizes defenses, inspects weapons, posts sentries, delivers orations. He can be terrifying in his coldness: when a man attempts to flee the barricade, Enjolras executes him without hesitation and without pleasure. Justice, for Enjolras, is a function of the ideal, not of feeling.

His relationship with Grantaire is the novel's most charged dynamic. (176-048) Grantaire "admired, loved, and venerated" Enjolras — a sceptic who believes in nothing except the man who believes in everything. Enjolras disdains Grantaire's drunkenness and cynicism, regarding him with "a little lofty pity." (176-051)

At the end, when the barricade falls and Enjolras stands against the wall to be shot, Grantaire wakes from his drunken stupor and asks to die beside him. Enjolras takes his hand. The revolutionary and the sceptic, the believer and the one who can believe only in the believer, die together — the novel's final word on the relationship between faith and doubt.

## Key Actions

- **Founding and leading the Friends of the ABC** (Volume III) — Enjolras organizes the revolutionary society that will fight at the barricade (176-010)
- **Orchestrating the insurrection** (Volume IV) — Enjolras plans the rising of June 5, 1832, surveying the city and preparing his forces (228-001)
- **Commanding the barricade** (Volume IV) — Enjolras directs the defense at the Rue de la Chanvrerie with military precision and unyielding resolve
- **Execution at the barricade** (Volume IV) — Enjolras dies facing a firing squad with serene indifference, his hand in Grantaire's

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo's treatment of Enjolras is remarkable for its ambivalence. He admires the revolutionary — the courage, the purity, the refusal to compromise — but he also registers something cold, something inhuman in this "priest of the ideal." The revolution needs Enjolras, Hugo implies, but the world cannot be built by Enjolras alone.

> (176-016) By the side of Enjolras, who represented the logic of the Revolution, Combeferre represented its philosophy.

The distinction is crucial. Logic without philosophy is terror; philosophy without logic is impotence. Enjolras is necessary but not sufficient.

## Symbolic / Thematic Role

Enjolras embodies **the revolutionary ideal** in its purest, most uncompromising form. He is the argument that political change requires absolute commitment — that the future will not be built by the half-hearted or the self-interested. But he also represents the novel's quiet warning: that purity of principle, untempered by ordinary human warmth, can become a kind of coldness.

His death with Grantaire is Hugo's reconciliation of opposites. The believer embraces the sceptic, and the two die as one — a vision of a revolution that includes even the doubters, a Republic broad enough for faith and doubt alike.
