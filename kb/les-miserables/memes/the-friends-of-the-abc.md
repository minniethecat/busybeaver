# The Friends of the ABC

The Friends of the ABC (*Les Amis de l'ABC*) — the name is a bilingual pun on *abaissés*, "the abased" or "the lowly" — are the revolutionary society whose members form the novel's collective hero. Led by the charismatic [[enjolras|Enjolras]], they are students, workers, and dreamers united by the conviction that [[justice]] demands sacrifice. Their debates in the Café Musain and their collective death at [[the-barricade]] constitute Hugo's most sustained portrait of political friendship — the argument that those who share a vision of the future form a bond as sacred as any family tie.

## Members

Hugo individuates each member of the Friends with extraordinary care — each is a type, but each is also a person. Their diversity of character is the group's strength:

| Member | Role | Distinguishing Trait |
|--------|------|---------------------|
| [[enjolras|Enjolras]] | Leader | "A charming young man, capable of being terrible" — the priest of the revolution, beautiful and absolute |
| Combeferre | Philosopher | The thinker who complements Enjolras's action; believes revolution should be guided by reason, not passion |
| Courfeyrac | Center | The warm heart of the group; Marius's introduction to the Friends comes through him |
| Jean Prouvaire | Poet | Gentle, tender, called "Jehan" — loves flowers, medieval poetry, and the people; dies crying "Vive la France! Vive l'avenir!" |
| Feuilly | Worker | A fan-maker, self-educated, and the group's internationalist; "he had but one thought: to deliver the world" |
| Bahorel | Peasant brawler | A law student who never studies, a fighter who laughs at danger; embodies rural strength |
| Lesgle (Bossuet) | The unlucky one | Bald at twenty-five, perpetually unfortunate, unfailingly cheerful |
| Joly | Hypochondriac medical student | Studious and anxious; his hypochondria is a form of attentiveness |
| Grantaire | Skeptic | The cynic who believes in nothing — except Enjolras; his final act is asking to die beside the believer |

## In the Narrative

The Friends appear throughout Volumes III and IV, their debates and camaraderie providing the novel's most sustained portrait of political friendship. Hugo shows them not as fanatics but as young men — drinking, arguing, falling in love, making jokes — whose political conviction is woven into the texture of everyday life. Their meetings at the Café Musain are the novel's vision of what democratic politics looks like at human scale: argument, laughter, conviction, and the willingness to die for what one believes.

At [[the-barricade]], they are destroyed almost to a man. Only [[marius-pontmercy|Marius]], carried unconscious through the sewers by [[jean-valjean|Valjean]], survives. Hugo's narration of their deaths is elegiac — each death is individuated, each life honored:

- Enjolras is executed against the wall of the Corinthe wine-shop, refusing a blindfold
- Combeferre is bayoneted while tending a wounded man
- Courfeyrac calls out to a friend as he falls
- Jean Prouvaire is taken prisoner and executed separately, weeping for the future
- Grantaire asks permission to die beside Enjolras: "Long live the Republic! I am one of them."

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo's portrait of the Friends is loving and elegiac. He presents their sacrifice not as futile (though the uprising fails) but as essential to the arc of [[progress]]:

> Hugo's narrator: the barricade is not a defeat; it is a testimony. Every generation that rises and falls for justice prepares the ground for the generation that will rise and succeed. The Friends of the ABC are the seed that will become the Republic.

The group's name — the pun on *abaissés* — is Hugo's political thesis in miniature: the friends of the alphabet are the friends of the lowly. Knowledge and justice are inseparable. Those who can read must speak for those who cannot.

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance

The Friends embody **revolutionary idealism** — the conviction that the future is worth dying for, and that friendship among those who share that conviction is itself a form of politics. Three symbolic dimensions:

- **The collective as hero**: No single member of the Friends is the novel's protagonist, but together they function as a collective character — the people organized, the conscience of a generation. Their individual deaths are subsumed into their collective meaning.
- **Youth as political force**: Hugo insists that the Friends' youth is not a weakness but a strength. They have not been compromised by age, by pragmatism, by the slow erosion of ideals. Their willingness to die is their purity.
- **Friendship as politics**: The Friends' political work is inseparable from their friendships — drinking at the Musain, debating philosophy, caring for one another. Hugo's vision of revolution is not the guillotine but the café: argument, solidarity, and the refusal to abandon one another.
