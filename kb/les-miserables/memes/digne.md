# Digne

Digne — Digne-les-Bains, a small town in the French Alps — is where *Les Misérables* begins. Before Paris, before the barricade, before the sewers, there is Digne: a provincial diocese where a saintly bishop lives in a converted hospital and practices a Christianity so radical that it transforms everyone who encounters it.

## Geography
- **Location**: Digne-les-Bains, Alpes-de-Haute-Provence, in the foothills of the French Alps
- **Type**: Provincial town and episcopal see
- **Period**: 1806–1821
- **Associated Characters**: Bishop Myriel, his sister Baptistine, his housekeeper Madame Magloire, Jean Valjean (who passes through and is transformed)

## In the Narrative
Digne occupies only Book I of Volume I — the first sixty pages of a fifteen-hundred-page novel. But those pages establish the moral universe against which everything that follows will be measured. It is in Digne that Valjean steals the Bishop's silver; it is in Digne that the Bishop pardons him and gives him the candlesticks. The entire novel unfolds from this provincial encounter.

## Hugo's Description
Hugo's Digne is sketched in broad strokes — the mountains, the hospital, the modest episcopal residence. The town itself matters less than what happens there: the demonstration that sanctity is possible, that mercy is real, that transformation is not a metaphor.

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance
Digne embodies **provincial grace** — the possibility, in a small place far from the centers of power, of a goodness so radical that it changes everything. The novel begins in Digne and never leaves it, spiritually speaking.
