# Notre-Dame de Paris

Notre-Dame is the architectural soul of Paris, though it appears in *Les Misérables* only in glimpses. Hugo, who had made the cathedral the protagonist of his earlier novel *Notre-Dame de Paris* (1831), invokes it here as the silent witness to the city's transformations — the medieval monument that outlasts revolutions, restorations, and barricades.

## In the Narrative
Notre-Dame appears in the background of the novel's Paris scenes — a presence rather than a setting. Its significance is symbolic: it represents the accumulation of history, the deep time of the city against which the novel's political dramas play out.

## Hugo's Description
Hugo's relationship with Notre-Dame is the subject of an entire earlier novel. Here, the cathedral is treated with the reverence of an established symbol — it needs no introduction. Its stones have absorbed centuries of Parisian life, and the novel's characters, whether they notice it or not, live in its shadow.

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance
Notre-Dame embodies **history** — the deep, accumulated past that outlasts every political regime and judges every generation's claims to novelty.
