# Montfermeil

Montfermeil is a village east of Paris whose name, in *Les Misérables*, is synonymous with exploitation. It is here that the Thénardiers run their inn — the "Sergeant of Waterloo" — and here that Cosette, aged eight, fetches water in the dark forest.

## In the Narrative
The Montfermeil chapters (Book III of Volume II) contain the novel's most famous image of childhood suffering: Cosette, a tiny figure, staggering under the weight of a bucket of water in the night forest. Valjean's appearance — he takes the bucket, buys her a doll, and purchases her freedom — is the novel's most direct act of rescue.

## Hugo's Description
Hugo's Montfermeil is sketched economically — the inn, the forest, the road to Paris. The village matters not for itself but for what happens there: the transfer of Cosette from the Thénardiers' world of exploitation to Valjean's world of protective love.

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance
Montfermeil embodies **rescue** — the place where mercy intervenes in suffering and changes a life.
