# The Marriage of Marius and Cosette

Marius and Cosette's wedding is the novel's domestic resolution — the future that every prior act of sacrifice (Fantine's, Valjean's, Éponine's, the revolutionaries') has made possible. It is intentionally ordinary: a bourgeois wedding, a happy couple, a grandfather reconciled.

## In the Narrative
The wedding occupies Book XV of Volume V. Valjean, having secured Cosette's future, withdraws — revealing his past to Marius and requesting to be excluded from their domestic life. His presence at the wedding, in the background, is the novel's final image of self-sacrificing love.

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance
The wedding embodies **ordinary happiness** — the life that the novel's martyrs died to make possible, and Hugo's insistence that extraordinary sacrifice finds its justification in the ordinary good it enables.
