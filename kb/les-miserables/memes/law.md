# Law

In *Les Misérables*, "law" is almost never used as a term of approval. Hugo draws a sharp distinction between human law — the code enforced by police, courts, and the guillotine — and divine law, which operates through conscience and mercy. The novel's central argument is that these two systems are not merely different but fundamentally opposed: human law, in punishing without understanding, perpetuates the injustice it claims to remedy.

## Hugo's Exposition

The novel's critique of law is established early, through Bishop Myriel's encounter with the guillotine. After witnessing an execution, the Bishop is shattered:

> (004-032) "I did not think that it was so monstrous. It is wrong to become absorbed in the divine law to such a degree as not to perceive human law. Death belongs to God alone. By what right do men touch that unknown thing?"

The guillotine is, for Hugo, the logical endpoint of a legal system that treats human beings as objects to be processed. He calls it "the concretion of the law" (004-030), a phrase that collapses the abstraction of legal theory into the brutal physical reality of the killing machine. The scaffold "is not neutral, and it does not permit you to remain neutral" — Hugo insists that every citizen is complicit in what the law does in their name.

The Bishop's debate with the Conventionist G—— sharpens the distinction between types of law:

> (010-064) "The judge speaks in the name of justice; the priest speaks in the name of pity, which is nothing but a more lofty justice."

This is the novel's legal philosophy in one sentence. Human law (the judge's domain) deals in categories, rules, and punishments. Divine law (the priest's domain, but accessible to all through conscience) deals in persons, circumstances, and transformation. The "more lofty justice" of pity does not abolish law but transcends it.

## Development Through the Narrative

Jean Valjean's nineteen-year sentence for stealing a loaf of bread is the novel's emblem of law's cruelty. The punishment is so disproportionate to the crime that it creates rather than corrects a criminal. Valjean enters the bagne as a young man who committed a desperate act to feed his sister's children; he emerges as a hardened convict who has learned to hate society because society's law has shown him nothing but hate.

Javert embodies legal absolutism. For Javert, the law is not a human construction subject to error but a metaphysical reality: "Javert, personified justice, light, and truth in their celestial function" (068-020). His tragedy is that he cannot accommodate the possibility that a convict might become a saint — the law's categories are fixed, irreversible, and total. When Valjean spares his life at the barricade, Javert's legal framework shatters. Unable to reconcile gratitude with duty, he chooses death.

The Champmathieu trial (Book VII) dramatizes the law's epistemological blindness. The court is prepared to condemn an innocent man because the "evidence" fits, and only Valjean's self-revelation — an act that the law can neither require nor comprehend — prevents the miscarriage of justice. Hugo's point is that this is not an aberration but the normal operation of a system that substitutes procedure for truth.

## Historical and Social Context

Hugo wrote at a time when France's penal system was undergoing reform, but the bagne — the prison-hulk system — remained a living memory. The novel's detailed descriptions of the galleys, the chain-gang, and the conditions at Toulon draw on Hugo's own research and prison visits. His 1829 novel *The Last Day of a Condemned Man* had already established him as a leading voice against capital punishment, and *Les Misérables* extends that critique to the entire structure of criminal justice.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **mercy** — what the law lacks; the capacity to see the person behind the crime
- **conscience** — the internal tribunal that, unlike law, can weigh circumstances
- **justice** — in Hugo's usage, a higher standard that law systematically fails to meet
- **grace** — divine intervention that breaks the law's chain of cause and effect
