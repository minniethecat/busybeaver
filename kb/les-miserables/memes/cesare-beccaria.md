# Dei Delitti e delle Pene (On Crimes and Punishments)

Cesare Beccaria's *Dei Delitti e delle Pene* (1764) is the Enlightenment's foundational text against torture and capital punishment — a work that Hugo invokes at four crucial points across *Les Misérables* as the intellectual authority behind his own argument that the state's power to punish must be bounded by reason and humanity.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Author** | Cesare Beccaria (1738–1794), Italian criminologist and philosopher |
| **Published** | 1764, Livorno (Leghorn), Italy |
| **Original Language** | Italian |
| **Type** | Philosophical treatise on legal reform |
| **Core Argument** | Punishment should be proportional, public, and swift — not cruel; the death penalty is neither necessary nor just; torture is barbaric and epistemologically worthless |

## Appearance in Les Misérables

Beccaria is invoked at four structural points in the novel, each reinforcing Hugo's critique of the French penal system:

1. **Book I, Chapter IV** — The narrator, describing society's relationship to the guillotine, aligns the Bishop's horror at capital punishment with Beccaria's philosophical case:
> (004-030) Others execrate it, like Beccaria. The guillotine is the concretion of the law; it is called vindicate; it is not neutral, and it does not permit you to remain neutral.

2. **Volume IV** — Louis-Philippe is described annotating Beccaria [225-020], a detail that establishes the king as an enlightened reformer in theory — a contrast to the system's continued barbarity in practice.

3. **The barricade section** — The narrator notes that the government's refusal to commute four death sentences hides behind Beccaria's authority while violating his principles [226-050].

4. **The novel's conclusion** — Hugo's final invocation is elegiac: Beccaria is dead, and the reactionary jurist Farinace lives on [367-008] — a lament that the Enlightenment's legal reforms remain incomplete.

## Hugo's Purpose

Hugo's invocation of Beccaria serves a precise rhetorical function: it positions the novel's critique of the bagne, the guillotine, and the yellow passport within a respectable philosophical tradition. By aligning his fictional Bishop and his authorial voice with Beccaria, Hugo insists that his argument is not sentimental but rational — not the indulgence of a novelist but the reasoned conclusion of Enlightenment criminology.

Hugo's twist on Beccaria is theological: where Beccaria argues from utility (the death penalty does not deter), Hugo argues from transcendence ("Death belongs to God alone"). The two arguments converge on the same practical conclusion — abolish the guillotine — but Hugo's foundations are ultimately religious rather than rationalist.

## Summary of Content

Beccaria's treatise, barely a hundred pages, made a case that was radical in 1764:

- Punishment exists to deter, not to avenge — its purpose is prevention, not retribution
- The certainty of punishment deters more effectively than its severity
- Torture extracts confessions, not truth — it punishes the accused before conviction
- The death penalty is neither a right of the state (the social contract does not grant it) nor a necessity (life imprisonment deters equally well)

The treatise was immediately influential across Europe, translated into French by André Morellet (with a reorganized structure that Voltaire praised). It shaped the penal reforms of the late 18th century and remained the touchstone for abolitionist arguments throughout the 19th.
