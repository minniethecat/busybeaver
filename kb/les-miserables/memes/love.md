# Love

Love in *Les Misérables* is not a sentiment but a force — the one power capable of breaking the cycles of poverty, punishment, and despair that society produces. Hugo presents love in several distinct but related forms: maternal love (Fantine's sacrifice for Cosette), charitable love (the Bishop's `caritas`), romantic love (Marius and Cosette), and redemptive love (Valjean's devotion to Cosette). Each form operates as a counterforce to the novel's great antagonists — injustice, the law, and human cruelty.

## Hugo's Exposition

Hugo's theology of love is most concentrated in the Bishop Myriel chapters. The Bishop's love is not sentimental — it is a structured, theological commitment to see Christ in every person, especially the most degraded:

> (010-064) "The judge speaks in the name of justice; the priest speaks in the name of pity, which is nothing but a more lofty justice."

This "more lofty justice" — pity, mercy, caritas — is Hugo's translation of Christian agape into social action. The Bishop loves the poor not as objects of charity but as equals who reveal the face of God. His love is costly: it takes him into the condemned man's cell, onto the scaffold, and finally to the moment of pardoning the thief who stole his silver.

The novel's preface frames love as a political act. To "destroy the social condemnation which creates hells in the midst of civilization" requires, Hugo argues, not just structural change but a revolution of the heart.

## Development Through the Narrative

Fantine's story is the novel's definitive treatment of maternal love. She sells her hair, her teeth, and finally her body — all to send money to the daughter she has entrusted to the Thénardiers. Hugo presents this not as degradation but as a kind of martyrdom: love literally strips Fantine of everything, including her identity. Her death, with Valjean's promise to care for Cosette, completes the transfer of maternal love from one guardian to another.

Marius and Cosette's romance — often criticized as the novel's weakest element — serves a structural purpose. Romantic love, in Hugo's scheme, is the form of love most vulnerable to selfishness and possessiveness. Marius nearly fails the test: his jealousy of Valjean and his initial willingness to abandon the barricade represent love turning inward. His eventual acceptance of Valjean — and Cosette's — completes his moral education.

Jean Valjean's love for Cosette is the novel's supreme example of redemptive love. It is not biological (Cosette is not his daughter) and not romantic; it is a love chosen, a love that gives him a reason to live after the galleys have taken everything else. His withdrawal at the novel's end — removing himself from Cosette's life so she can marry without the shadow of his past — completes the arc: love that began as a gift received (from the Bishop) becomes a gift given at the ultimate cost.

## Historical and Social Context

Hugo wrote during a period when French social thought was increasingly focused on "the social question" — how to address mass poverty in an industrializing society. Catholic social teaching emphasized charity as an individual virtue; socialist thinkers emphasized structural reform. Hugo's treatment of love bridges both: he insists on structural change (the novel's preface is a political manifesto) while also insisting that structures alone cannot produce justice — love must animate them.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **indifference** — love's opposite; the failure to see another's suffering, epitomized by the society that ignores Fantine
- **exploitation** — love's perversion; what the Thénardiers practice, using others for gain
- **legalism** — love's antagonist; Javert's inability to love is inseparable from his worship of the law
- **possessiveness** — love's corruption; Marius's early jealousy, the emotional greed that mistakes ownership for devotion
