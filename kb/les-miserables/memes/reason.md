# Reason

Hugo inherited the Enlightenment's commitment to reason while mounting a Romantic critique of its limits. In *Les Misérables*, reason is not rejected — the novel's political arguments, its social analysis, its detailed research into the penal system and the sewers all testify to Hugo's respect for rational inquiry — but it is systematically shown to be insufficient. The deepest moral truths, Hugo insists, are not reached by reasoning but by conscience, mercy, and love.

## Hugo's Exposition

The Conventionist G—— represents reason at its most formidable. His arguments are logical, historical, and devastating; the Bishop, who has come to condemn him, finds himself out-argued. The Conventionist's defense of the Revolution is a masterpiece of Enlightenment reasoning — it proceeds from principles, cites evidence, and draws conclusions that the Bishop cannot refute on rational grounds.

But the Bishop's response, when it comes, is not an argument. It is a gesture: he kneels before the dying Conventionist and asks his blessing. This is not a concession that reason is wrong — it is a demonstration that reason is not enough. The Bishop respects the Conventionist's arguments, but the moral reality of the situation (a dying man who has devoted his life to human progress, however brutally) exceeds what argument can capture.

> (010-050) "And conscience," added the Bishop.

This one word — not "logic" or "reason" but "conscience" — is the novel's epistemological keystone. Conscience is reason plus love; it is the faculty that knows what reason alone cannot.

## Development Through the Narrative

Javert represents reason perverted into legalism — the reduction of moral judgment to the mechanical application of rules. His tragedy is that his reasoning is impeccable given his premises; the flaw is in the premises themselves, which exclude mercy and transformation. When he encounters a fact that his system cannot process (Valjean's mercy), his reason collapses — he has no other faculty to fall back on.

Jean Valjean's decision at Arras is presented as a triumph of conscience over reason. Every rational argument supports letting Champmathieu be condemned: Valjean is doing more good as Mayor Madeleine than he could ever do as a prisoner, the workers of Montreuil-sur-Mer depend on him, Cosette needs him. His decision to reveal himself is, by any rational calculus, a mistake. Hugo presents it as the most moral act in the novel.

## Historical and Social Context

The tension between Enlightenment rationalism and Romantic intuition was a defining intellectual conflict of Hugo's era. The Revolution had claimed to govern by reason (the Cult of Reason, the Committee of Public Safety's rationalizations of terror); the Romantics responded by insisting on emotion, nature, and individual genius as sources of truth. Hugo occupies an unusual position — politically progressive (like the rationalists) but epistemologically Romantic, trusting conscience over syllogism.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **conscience** — reason's necessary supplement; the faculty that knows what reasoning cannot
- **superstition** — what reason rightly combats; Hugo is an Enlightenment figure in his rejection of unreasoning belief
- **fanaticism** — reason's dark twin; what happens when reason becomes absolute and excludes mercy
- **faith** — not reason's opponent but its partner; the Bishop's faith is rational, but it exceeds reason

## Key Passages

> Valjean's night at Arras (027-083): "His conscience weighed in turn these two men" — the moment reason yields to conscience

> Javert's suicide: the collapse of reason when it encounters a fact it cannot categorize
