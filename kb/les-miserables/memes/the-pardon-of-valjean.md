# The Pardon of Jean Valjean

The pardon is the event from which everything in *Les Misérables* follows. Valjean, released from the galleys after nineteen years, has been turned away from every inn in Digne. The Bishop takes him in, feeds him, gives him a bed. Valjean, in the night, steals the Bishop's silver and flees. The gendarmes catch him and bring him back. And the Bishop — impossibly, inexplicably — tells the police that the silver was a gift and adds the silver candlesticks.

## In the Narrative
The pardon occupies only a few pages (Book II, Chapter XII) but its weight is incalculable. "Jean Valjean, my brother," the Bishop says, "you no longer belong to evil, but to good. It is your soul that I am buying from you."

## Hugo's Commentary
Hugo presents the pardon not as sentiment but as theology. The Bishop's lie to the police is a truth of a higher order — the recognition that Valjean's soul is worth more than silver, that mercy transcends law, that transformation is possible.

## Key Moments
- The theft of the silver — Valjean's final act as a convict
- The pardon — the Bishop's claim that the silver was a gift
- "It is your soul that I am buying from you" — the novel's foundational sentence

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance
The pardon is the novel's **origin event** — the Big Bang from which the entire moral universe of *Les Misérables* expands. Every subsequent act of mercy (Valjean's rescue of Cosette, his sparing of Javert) is an echo of this moment.
