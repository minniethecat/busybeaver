# Thénardier

Thénardier is *Les Misérables*'s most unambiguously evil character. Unlike [[javert]], the principled antagonist, Thénardier represents greed incarnate.

For him, other human beings exist only as instruments of profit. His exploitation of [[fantine]] and [[cosette]] drives the novel's central tragedy, and his persistence makes him the shadow that [[jean-valjean]] can never fully escape.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Thénardier (first name never given; aliases include Jondrette, Fabantou, Genflot)
- **Role**: Antagonist — the embodiment of selfishness and exploitation
- **First Appearance**: Volume I, Book IV, Chapter I — "One Mother Meets Another Mother" (037-001)
- **Social Position**: Innkeeper (Montfermeil) → failed businessman → slum landlord and criminal gang leader (Paris) → self-exiled slave-trader in America
- **Key Relationships**: Madame Thénardier (wife and partner in exploitation), [[cosette]] (foster child turned servant), [[fantine]] (extorted for years), [[eponine]] and Azelma (daughters), [[gavroche]] (son he abandoned)

## Character Arc

Thénardier is introduced at his inn in Montfermeil. Hugo describes him as a "small, thin, pale, angular, bony, rickety man" — but the moral description matters more. He is a figure of pure, calculating avarice.

When Fantine entrusts Cosette to him, Thénardier sees not a child but an income stream. He raises his price from seven francs a month to twelve to fifteen, and when Fantine falls into arrears, he keeps the child as unpaid labor. (037-062)

His genius lies in weaponizing a mother's love. Every letter to Fantine announces a new crisis requiring payment: Cosette has no winter clothes, Cosette is ill, Cosette will die without medicine. These crises are entirely fabricated. (039-004) "A fine favor she is doing us!" he exclaims.

When [[jean-valjean]] arrives to rescue Cosette, Thénardier recognizes an opportunity. He pursues Valjean through the streets, attempting to extort one final payment, and later becomes obsessed with the idea that Valjean's wealth is somehow owed to him.

In Paris, Thénardier reinvents himself as "Jondrette" at the Gorbeau tenement. By Hugo's supreme irony, he attempts to rob Valjean without recognizing him as the man who took Cosette from Montfermeil. The ambush is foiled by [[marius-pontmercy]], who overhears the plot through a hole in the wall, and by [[javert]], who arrests the gang.

But Thénardier escapes. He always escapes. In the novel's final pages, he attempts to sell Marius information about Valjean's past. His slander, intended to destroy Valjean's reputation, has the opposite effect: it reveals to Marius that Valjean was a hero, not a criminal, and that Marius owes his life to the man he has been avoiding.

Thénardier's final act is to accept money from Marius and depart for America. There, Hugo notes with bitter irony, he becomes a slave-trader — the logical terminus of a life spent treating human beings as property.

## Key Actions

- **Taking in Cosette to exploit her** (Volume I, Book IV) — Thénardier accepts Fantine's child and payment, then systematically transforms the arrangement into exploitation (037-062)
- **Extorting Fantine** (Volume I, Book V) — a sustained campaign of fraudulent demands for money, driving Fantine to sell her hair, teeth, and finally her body
- **Attempted robbery at the Gorbeau tenement** (Volume II, Book VIII) — the ambush of Jean Valjean, foiled by Marius and Javert
- **Revealing Valjean's heroism by accident** (Volume V, Book IX) — Thénardier's attempted blackmail inadvertently proves Valjean's virtue

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo introduces the Thénardiers with a rhetorical question that frames the entire characterization:

> (038-002) Who were these Thénardiers?

The answer is an anatomy of mediocrity curdling into evil. Thénardier is a man who has "read a little, thought little, and prided himself on being a liberal." His intelligence is deployed entirely in the service of fraud.

Hugo presents him not as demonic but as perfectly ordinary — a man who has never once considered the moral claims of anyone but himself.

## Symbolic / Thematic Role

Thénardier embodies **greed without conscience** — the dark twin of the capitalist spirit that Valjean channels into prosperity for others. Where Valjean's wealth liberates, Thénardier's profit-seeking imprisons.

He is the novel's argument that poverty can produce not sympathy but predation. His survival to the end, unpunished and unrepentant, is Hugo's final realist touch: in this world, the Thénardiers often win.
