# Gavroche

Gavroche is Hugo's portrait of the Paris *gamin* — the street child who belongs to no one, lives by his wits, and becomes the novel's most vivid symbol of the revolutionary spirit. Thrown away by his parents (the [[thenardier]]s), he claims the streets as his home and the city's outcasts as his family. His death on the barricade, singing as he collects bullets under fire, is one of the most celebrated scenes in nineteenth-century literature.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Gavroche Thénardier (abandoned; his surname is almost never used)
- **Role**: Symbolic figure — the gamin as the embodiment of Paris itself
- **First Appearance**: Volume III, Book I, Chapter I — "The Space Between Two Fires" (159-018)
- **Social Position**: Abandoned child → street urchin (gamin) → de facto guardian of two younger boys → revolutionary combatant → martyr of the barricade
- **Key Relationships**: Monsieur and Madame Thénardier (parents who abandoned him), [[eponine]] and Azelma (sisters), two nameless younger boys (whom he shelters without knowing they are his brothers), the revolutionaries at the barricade

## Character Arc

Gavroche enters the narrative almost as a force of nature. Hugo introduces him as part of his extended meditation on the Paris gamin — a type, a social phenomenon, a creature who belongs entirely to the city. (159-018) Hugo asks: "Why was he called Little Gavroche?" and the answer is that he simply *is* — named by the streets, defined by his own irrepressible vitality.

He is the abandoned son of the Thénardiers, discarded as casually as they discard everything that does not generate profit. But unlike his parents, Gavroche is a figure of pure generosity. When he encounters two younger boys shivering on the street — boys he does not recognize as his own brothers — he takes them in, shares his bread, and gives them shelter in the hollow belly of the elephant statue on the Place de la Bastille. (250-003) He is "always shivering gayly," a child whose poverty cannot extinguish his spirit.

His trajectory accelerates toward the barricade. On the night of June 5, 1832, Gavroche joins the insurrection at the Rue de la Chanvrerie. He is not an ideologue — he has no political theory — but he recognizes injustice with unerring instinct. At the barricade, he becomes a combatant, a scout, a supplier of ammunition, and the comic relief whose humor is indistinguishable from courage.

His death is the novel's most famous scene. When the defenders run out of cartridges, Gavroche climbs over the barricade into the street, ignoring enemy fire, singing a mocking song as he strips cartridges from the bodies of fallen soldiers. A bullet strikes him; he rises, keeps singing. A second bullet silences him. Hugo frames his death with the language of apotheosis: "He lay face down on the pavement, and moved no more. That little great soul had taken flight." (278-086)

## Key Actions

- **Sheltering two lost boys** (Volume III) — Gavroche takes in two younger street children, giving them his bread and his shelter in the elephant, unaware they are his own brothers (250-003)
- **Exposing his father's criminality** (Volume III) — Gavroche witnesses Thénardier's attempted robbery and intervenes to protect the victim
- **Joining the barricade** (Volume IV) — Gavroche becomes a combatant at the Rue de la Chanvrerie, serving as scout and ammunition-runner
- **Dying while collecting cartridges** (Volume IV) — the novel's iconic martyrdom: Gavroche, under fire, singing as he gathers bullets for the revolution

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo's portrait of Gavroche in the opening of Volume III is one of the novel's great digressions — a seventy-page essay on the Paris gamin that establishes Gavroche as both a specific child and a universal type. The gamin, Hugo argues, is the purest expression of Paris: irreverent, resourceful, unkillable, and fundamentally generous.

> (159-018) Why was he called Little Gavroche? Probably because his father was called Jondrette.

The mock-explanation captures Gavroche's essential condition: he is named by contingency, defined by no one's intention, belonging entirely to himself and the streets.

## Symbolic / Thematic Role

Gavroche embodies **the spirit of Paris** — not the Paris of monuments and manners but the Paris of the streets, the people, the unkillable vitality that rises again after every suppression. He is the neglected child who becomes the soul of the revolution, the discard who proves indispensable. His death is Hugo's argument that the revolution is justified not by political theory but by the existence of children like Gavroche — children whom society has abandoned and who nonetheless give everything for the hope of a better world. He represents the [[progress]] that cannot be stopped, the [[justice]] that the powerful cannot extinguish.
