# The Conventionary G——

The dying Conventionist who appears in a single chapter — "The Bishop in the Presence of an Unknown Light" — is one of the novel's most important characters. His deathbed debate with Bishop Myriel is the theological and philosophical center of *Les Misérables*, the moment when orthodox Catholic faith and secular revolutionary conviction are forced to reckon with each other.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: G—— (Hugo deliberately withholds the full name; the character represents a type, not an individual)
- **Role**: Revolutionary conscience — the secular saint whom the Bishop learns to respect
- **First Appearance**: Volume I, Book I, Chapter X (010-001)
- **Social Position**: Former member of the National Convention, regicide, living in obscurity and dying alone
- **Key Relationships**: Bishop Myriel (theological interlocutor)

## Character Arc

The Conventionist does not change — he is dying, and his convictions are fixed. It is the Bishop who is transformed by the encounter. The chapter's structure is dialectical: the Bishop arrives full of righteous condemnation for the man who voted to kill a king; the Conventionist, calm and dying, defends the Revolution not as a political program but as a moral necessity — "the brutalities of progress are called revolutions" (010-094). The Bishop, after initial fury, kneels to ask the old revolutionary's blessing.

## Hugo's Commentary

> (010-094) "Yes, the brutalities of progress are called revolutions. When they are over, this fact is recognized — that the human race has been treated harshly, but that it has progressed."

Hugo's ambivalence is structured into the scene: the Conventionist's argument is compelling, but the Bishop's final gesture (kneeling) suggests that moral recognition transcends argument. The novel does not declare a winner — it insists that both men, the believer and the revolutionary, are necessary.

## Key Actions

- **The debate** — the Conventionist defends the Revolution, acknowledges its "brutalities," and insists that progress is real
- **The Bishop's reversal** — Myriel, who came to condemn, kneels to receive a blessing

## Notable Quotes

> (010-094) "The brutalities of progress are called revolutions."
> (010-050) "And conscience," added the Bishop.
