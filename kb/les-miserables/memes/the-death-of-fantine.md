# The Death of Fantine

Fantine's death is the novel's most compressed tragedy — and its most generative promise. Dying in the hospital at Montreuil-sur-Mer, destroyed by poverty, exploitation, and disease, Fantine's last coherent thought is of Cosette. Valjean, now revealed as a convict, promises the dying woman to care for her child.

## In the Narrative
The deathbed scene (Book V, Chapters XII–XIII) brings together the novel's three registers: the personal (Fantine's love for Cosette), the institutional (the hospital, the police), and the spiritual (Valjean's promise, which has the character of a vow before God).

Fantine dies believing Cosette is near — Valjean has lied to comfort her. Javert arrives to arrest Valjean. The scene's cruelty (Javert's implacable presence at a deathbed) and its mercy (Valjean's promise) are held in the same frame.

## Key Moments
- Fantine's last words — of Cosette, of spring, of the future she will not see
- Valjean's promise — the vow that structures the remainder of the novel

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance
Fantine's death embodies **the promise** — the obligation that the living owe to the dead, the chain of mercy that extends across generations.
