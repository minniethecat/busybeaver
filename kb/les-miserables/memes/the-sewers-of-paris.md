# The Sewers of Paris

The sewers of Paris — the subject of one of Hugo's most famous digressions — are the city's hidden digestive system. Valjean's flight through the sewers with the wounded Marius on his back is the novel's most harrowing physical journey: a descent into the underworld that becomes the path to salvation.

## Geography
- **Location**: Beneath the streets of Paris, extending from central Paris to the Seine
- **Type**: Urban sewer system — an underground network of tunnels carrying the city's waste
- **Period**: June 6, 1832 (the novel's sewer scene)
- **Associated Characters**: Jean Valjean (who carries Marius through the sewers), Thénardier (who claims to have "saved" Marius)

## In the Narrative
After the barricade falls, Valjean carries the unconscious Marius into the sewers — the only escape route Javert cannot follow. The journey through darkness, filth, and waist-deep water is a symbolic death and rebirth: Valjean descends into the underworld (like Orpheus, like Christ) and emerges carrying the future (Marius).

Hugo's digression on the sewers (Book II of Volume V) is a complete history of the Parisian waste system, from medieval open drains to the modern network. The thesis: a civilization is measured by what it hides.

## Hugo's Description
Hugo describes the sewers as "the conscience of the city" — the hidden infrastructure where everything the city would rather not see (waste, corpses, crime) flows underground. The digression is both technical (dimensions, gradients, construction history) and philosophical (what does it mean that Paris hides its filth beneath monuments?).

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance
The sewers embody **the hidden** — society's underside, the filth civilization produces and buries, the truth that flows beneath the monuments. Valjean's passage through them is his final ordeal before redemption.
