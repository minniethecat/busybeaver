# Ignorance

Hugo's most radical claim about ignorance is that it is not an individual failing but a social crime. When Bishop Myriel declares that "society is culpable, in that it does not afford instruction gratis; it is responsible for the night which it produces" (004-016), he is not making a modest proposal for educational reform. He is indicting the entire social order. Ignorance, in Hugo's scheme, is the "night" — the darkness in which all other sins become possible — and society, by keeping the poor uneducated, actively produces the crimes it then punishes.

## Hugo's Exposition

The Bishop's philosophy of ignorance is stated with the directness of a manifesto:

> (004-016) "Teach those who are ignorant as many things as possible; society is culpable, in that it does not afford instruction gratis; it is responsible for the night which it produces. This soul is full of shadow; sin is therein committed. The guilty one is not the person who has committed the sin, but the person who has created the shadow."

The metaphor of "night" and "shadow" is crucial. Ignorance is not merely the absence of knowledge; it is an active condition — a darkness that makes moral perception impossible. The person who sins in darkness is not excused (the sin is real) but the primary responsibility lies with whoever created the darkness.

Hugo does not romanticize ignorance. The uneducated characters in the novel are not noble savages; they are genuinely damaged by their lack of knowledge. But Hugo insists that this damage is inflicted, not innate. The solution is not charity but instruction — structural provision of education that would make moral agency genuinely possible for everyone.

## Development Through the Narrative

Jean Valjean's ignorance is both social and moral. Before the galleys, he is a peasant who can barely read; after nineteen years, he has learned only hatred. The Bishop's pardon is, in this sense, an act of education — it teaches Valjean something he did not know: that mercy exists, that transformation is possible. Valjean's subsequent self-education (he becomes a reader, a thinker, a mayor) is the novel's demonstration that ignorance is reversible given the right conditions.

Fantine's ignorance is of a different kind. She is not uneducated in the literal sense — she can read and write — but she is ignorant of the social forces arrayed against her. She does not understand that her lover will abandon her, that the Thénardiers will exploit her, that the factory will dismiss her. This is ignorance as structural vulnerability: the condition of those who lack the social knowledge to protect themselves.

The Thénardiers represent ignorance that has hardened into something worse. They are not merely uneducated; they have learned — from the society that has brutalized them — that only exploitation pays. Their ignorance is the darkness that the Bishop warned about, now become active malice.

## Historical and Social Context

Universal education was a live political issue in 19th-century France. The Guizot law of 1833 required every commune to maintain a primary school, but implementation was uneven and largely excluded the poorest. Hugo's insistence on free, universal education aligned him with the progressive republican tradition. His argument that ignorance produces crime was influential in French penal reform debates.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **knowledge** — what education provides; in Hugo, not mere information but moral capacity
- **education** — the structural solution to ignorance; the state's first obligation
- **enlightenment** — the 18th-century ideal that Hugo inherited and democratized
- **sin** — what ignorance makes possible; the shadow's fruit
