# The Catholic Church in Les Misérables

The Catholic Church in *Les Misérables* is treated with Hugo's characteristic ambivalence. He reveres its saints — Bishop Myriel is the novel's moral foundation — while criticizing the institution's complicity in social injustice and its tendency to withdraw from the world when the world most needs engagement.

## Organization
- **Type**: Religious institution — the established Catholic Church of post-Revolutionary France
- **Period**: 1806–1833 (the novel's action)
- **Key Institutions**: The Diocese of Digne, the convent of Petit-Picpus, various parish churches

## In the Narrative
The Church appears in two registers. In Digne, through Bishop Myriel, it is the agent of mercy and social conscience. In Paris, through the convent of Petit-Picpus, it is the site of withdrawal — sincere but, in Hugo's judgment, insufficient. The novel's treatment of the Church culminates in the Bishop's confession that "I have sometimes resisted progress without pity" (010-106).

## Hugo's Commentary
Hugo's position is that of a liberal Catholic who values individual sanctity (Myriel) while criticizing institutional rigidity (the convent, the curé who refuses to attend the condemned man). The Church's true function, in his view, is to practice caritas — the love that sees Christ in every sufferer — and its failure to do so consistently is a betrayal of its founding purpose.
