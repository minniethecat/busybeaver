# The Gorbeau Tenement

The Gorbeau tenement is one of Hugo's most vivid architectural portraits — a building so dilapidated, so expressive of its inhabitants' misery, that it becomes a character in its own right.

## Geography
- **Location**: No. 50-52 Boulevard de l'Hôpital, Paris (near the Salpêtrière hospital)
- **Type**: Tenement building — multi-story habitation for the urban poor
- **Period**: 1824–1832
- **Associated Characters**: Jean Valjean and Cosette (residents, Book V), the Thénardiers as the Jondrettes (residents, Book XIII), Marius Pontmercy (neighbor)

## In the Narrative
The Gorbeau house is the scene of one of the novel's most dramatic sequences: the ambush of Valjean by Thénardier and the Patron-Minette criminal gang. Marius, observing through a crack in the wall, witnesses the crime and alerts the police — bringing Javert to the scene in a confrontation that nearly captures both Valjean and the criminals.

## Hugo's Description
Hugo describes the Gorbeau tenement with the precision of a social documentarian: the peeling wallpaper, the broken windows, the staircase that groans under the weight of poverty. Architecture, for Hugo, is never neutral — buildings embody the social conditions that produce them.

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance
The Gorbeau house embodies **urban poverty** — the condition of the Parisian poor, crowded into decaying buildings, invisible to the respectable classes who pass on the boulevard below.
