# Faith

Faith in *Les Misérables* is not a settled possession but a field of struggle. The novel's two greatest theological voices — Bishop Myriel and the dying Conventionist G—— — represent opposed forms of faith: the Bishop's orthodox Catholicism (informed by charity and personal sanctity) and the Conventionist's secular faith in progress and human dignity. Hugo refuses to declare one the winner. Both men, by the end of their encounter, have been changed — the Bishop kneels before the Conventionist to ask his blessing.

## Hugo's Exposition

The chapter "The Bishop in the Presence of an Unknown Light" (Book I, Chapter X) is the novel's theological center. The Bishop, who has come to condemn the dying regicide, finds himself instead in dialogue with a man whose faith — though utterly different from his own — he is forced to respect:

> (010-050) "And conscience," added the Bishop.

> (010-064) "The judge speaks in the name of justice; the priest speaks in the name of pity, which is nothing but a more lofty justice."

The Conventionist's response is equally powerful. He does not deny the Bishop's faith; he relativizes it. The Church, he argues, has been an obstacle to the very justice and mercy it preaches. The Bishop, after initial fury, is forced to concede: "I have sometimes resisted progress without pity" (010-106). This admission — that faith can serve reaction — is the novel's most honest theological moment.

## Development Through the Narrative

Jean Valjean's faith is the novel's most dynamic and least institutional. He receives no catechism; his conversion comes through an act of mercy, not a sermon. His faith thereafter is expressed entirely through action — caring for Fantine, rescuing Cosette, sparing Javert, withdrawing from Cosette's marriage. Hugo presents this lived faith as more authentic than the institutional variety.

The convent of Petit-Picpus (Book II, Books VI–VII) represents faith in its institutional and problematic form. Hugo's extended description is ambivalent: he respects the nuns' devotion while deploring what he sees as the waste of human life in cloistered withdrawal from the world. The convent is faith as retreat; Valjean's life is faith as engagement.

Javert is the novel's negative theologian — a man of absolute faith in the law, which functions as his religion. His suicide, when this faith becomes untenable, is the novel's most radical statement about what happens when faith is placed in the wrong object.

## Historical and Social Context

Hugo wrote during a period of intense church-state conflict in France. The Revolution had dispossessed the Church; the Restoration had re-empowered it; the 1848 Revolution had renewed anti-clerical sentiment. Hugo's own position evolved from royalist Catholicism to a kind of liberal theism that was deeply respectful of religious faith while critical of institutional Church power.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **atheism** — not, for Hugo, a position of moral seriousness; the Conventionist is not an atheist but a believer in different gods (progress, humanity)
- **fanaticism** — faith without doubt; what the Bishop learns to transcend
- **superstition** — faith without understanding; the popular religion Hugo gently satirizes
- **the-law** — Javert's false god; faith in institutions rather than in love

> (010-106) "And I have sometimes resisted progress without pity" — the Bishop's confession of institutional sin
