# Progress

Hugo was a convinced progressive — he believed that history moved, however painfully, toward greater justice and enlightenment. But he was not a naive optimist. The cost of progress, as *Les Misérables* makes clear, is measured in human suffering. The novel's philosophy of history, articulated most fully in the Conventionist debate and the Waterloo digression, holds that progress is real but never clean.

## Hugo's Exposition

The dying Conventionist G——, a former revolutionary who voted for the death of Louis XVI, delivers the novel's definitive statement on progress. Challenged by the Bishop — who initially regards him as a regicide and a monster — the Conventionist defends revolutionary violence in terms that the Bishop is forced to respect:

> (010-094) "Yes, the brutalities of progress are called revolutions. When they are over, this fact is recognized — that the human race has been treated harshly, but that it has progressed."

The key word is "brutalities." Hugo does not sanitize revolution. He acknowledges that it is harsh, even cruel. But he insists that this harshness must be weighed against what it achieves. The Conventionist's argument — which the Bishop, after initial resistance, comes to accept — is that the French Revolution, for all its Terror, advanced human dignity in ways that the ancien régime never could.

The Bishop's response is equally important. He does not recant his faith in mercy, but he acknowledges that there are forms of progress — political, structural — that mercy alone cannot achieve:

> (010-106) "And I have sometimes resisted progress without pity."

This confession — that the Church, and the Bishop personally, have been forces of reaction as well as of compassion — is the novel's most honest moment about the complicity of religion in the suffering progress seeks to overcome.

## Development Through the Narrative

The novel's structure — moving from the individual drama of Valjean to the collective drama of the 1832 barricades — mirrors Hugo's philosophy of progress. Individual transformation (Valjean's redemption) is necessary but insufficient; structural change requires collective action. The students at the barricade, whatever their tactical folly, represent the forward momentum of history. Their sacrifice, Hugo implies, is not wasted — every advance in human freedom has been paid for by those willing to die for it.

Waterloo, another extended digression, complicates this optimism. The battle represents progress in reverse — a triumph of reactionary forces over Napoleonic reform. But Hugo insists that even Waterloo could not reverse the deeper historical current: "Often a battle is lost and progress is conquered" (086-003). Defeat does not refute the progressive thesis; it only delays its realization.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **reaction** — the forces, embodied in the restored monarchy, that seek to reverse historical gains
- **stagnation** — what progress combats; the inertia of institutions and habits
- **revolution** — the mechanism of progress; Hugo's term for the painful but necessary birth of a better order
- **the-convent** — Hugo's extended metaphor for the retreat from history, the attempt to opt out of progress

## Key Passages

> (010-094) "The brutalities of progress are called revolutions. When they are over, this fact is recognized — that the human race has been treated harshly, but that it has progressed." — the Conventionist G——

> (010-106) "And I have sometimes resisted progress without pity." — Bishop Myriel, confessing his complicity in reaction

> (011-011) The narrator's description of "the grand aspiration for progress, with the sublime faith, patriotic, democratic, and human" — at the novel's philosophical peak

> (086-003) "Often a battle is lost and progress is conquered. There is less glory and more liberty." — Hugo, on Waterloo

> (010-058) "The death of right is an element of progress." — the Conventionist, on the necessity of structural transformation
