# The Barricade of the Rue de la Chanvrerie

The barricade is the novel's most concentrated stage — the space where all of Hugo's themes converge in a single physical structure: [[justice]], [[mercy]], [[revolution]], sacrifice, and [[love]]. Built of paving stones, furniture, and hope during the June Rebellion of 1832, the barricade is simultaneously a tactical fortification and a symbol of the people's refusal to accept injustice. It is where [[enjolras|Enjolras]] preaches the gospel of the future, where [[gavroche]] sings while collecting cartridges, where [[eponine|Éponine]] takes a bullet meant for [[marius-pontmercy|Marius]], and where [[jean-valjean]] repays the [[mercy]] he once received by sparing [[javert]].

## Geography

| Attribute | Detail |

| **Type** | Revolutionary barricade — a street fortification built by insurgents from paving stones, overturned vehicles, and household debris |
| **Period** | June 5–6, 1832 (the historical June Rebellion against the July Monarchy) |
| **Historical Context** | The June Rebellion was sparked by the funeral of General Lamarque, a popular liberal figure; the uprising lasted two days and was crushed by the National Guard |

## In the Narrative

The barricade occupies the entirety of Volume IV's second half and the beginning of Volume V. Hugo assembles his characters at this single point and orchestrates a symphony of sacrifice. The barricade functions as:

- **A church**: for Enjolras, the barricade is a sacred space where the religion of the future is preached and died for
- **A theater**: each character plays their essential role — the leader, the lover, the child, the martyr, the skeptic, the savior
- **A crucible**: the barricade tests every character; only those capable of self-sacrifice pass through it morally intact
- **A judgment**: the meeting of Valjean and Javert at the barricade is the novel's moral climax — the convict spares the policeman, breaking the logic of retribution that Javert has lived by

The barricade's fall is inevitable; Hugo never pretends otherwise. The 1832 uprising was real and was crushed. But Hugo insists that the barricade's meaning exceeds its tactical outcome: every barricade, however quickly destroyed, advances the arc of [[progress]] by demonstrating that the people are not passive.

## Hugo's Description

Hugo describes the barricade with the precision of a military historian and the imagination of a poet. It is simultaneously a physical object — paving stones, an overturned omnibus, a red flag — and a metaphysical one: the "rampart of the future," the "wall of the ideal." The barricade exists in two registers at once:

> The barricade is the novel's most concentrated image of collective meaning: human beings literally tearing up the street to build something higher, sacrificing their individual lives for a collective future they will not live to see.

The construction of the barricade is described as a creative act — the people as artists of revolution. Its destruction is a crucifixion — the state as executioner of hope.

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance

The barricade embodies **collective action** — the people organized, armed, and willing to die for the future. It is the novel's most sustained argument that progress requires sacrifice, and that sacrifice, however tragic, is not meaningless.

Three symbolic registers:

- **The barricade as sacrament**: In Enjolras's rhetoric, the barricade transforms death from ending to offering. The revolutionaries do not merely die — they testify.
- **The barricade as democracy**: The barricade is built by and of the people. It does not discriminate by class: the worker, the student, the old man, and the child all contribute their stones.
- **The barricade as mercy's theater**: The most politically charged space in the novel is also the site of its most personal moral act — Valjean's release of Javert. The barricade proves that mercy is not weakness; it is a different kind of power.

## Notable Moments

1. **Construction**: The people tearing up the street to build a fortress of freedom — destruction as creation
2. **Gavroche's death**: The gamin singing as he collects cartridges, shot mid-verse — childhood sacrificed to adult causes
3. **Éponine's sacrifice**: Shielding Marius with her body, her love letter to him written in blood
4. **Enjolras's execution**: The revolutionary leader facing the firing squad with serene conviction — the martyr as aesthetic ideal
5. **Grantaire's redemption**: The skeptic asking permission to die beside the believer — "Long live the Republic! I am one of them."
6. **Valjean's mercy**: The convict cuts the policeman's bonds and says "You are free" — mercy's full circuit
