# The Theft from Petit Gervais

After the Bishop's pardon, Valjean wanders in a state of moral disorientation. He encounters a young chimney-sweep, Petit Gervais, who drops a forty-sous coin. Almost automatically, out of the hardened habit of nineteen years in the galleys, Valjean places his foot on the coin and refuses to return it. The child runs away in terror. And Valjean, alone on the road, breaks.

## In the Narrative
This brief scene (Book II, Chapter XIII) is the novel's most concentrated image of conversion. Valjean has already received the Bishop's mercy intellectually; the theft from Petit Gervais is the moment when that intellectual acceptance becomes visceral. He cannot return the coin — the child is gone — and the impossibility of making restitution shatters him.

## Hugo's Commentary
Hugo presents the theft as the death throes of Valjean's old self — the last automatic act of the convict before the Bishop's grace takes hold. "Then his heart burst, and he began to weep. It was the first time he had wept in nineteen years."
