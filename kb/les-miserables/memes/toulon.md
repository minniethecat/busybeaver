# Toulon

Toulon is the geographical correlative of Jean Valjean's suffering. The great Mediterranean naval port was also the site of France's most notorious bagne — the prison-hulks where convicts served sentences under conditions designed to destroy body and soul.

## In the Narrative
Toulon appears only in memory and backstory, but its shadow is long. Valjean's nineteen years there — five for the theft of bread, fourteen more for escape attempts — are the wound that the novel's entire redemptive arc must heal. Javert served there as a guard, and his recognition of Valjean as Madeleine is the plot's first great crisis.

## Hugo's Description
Hugo's Toulon is not described in detail — unlike Paris, it is a place of absence, a black hole in Valjean's past. The novel's silence about the specific horrors of the bagne is itself eloquent: some suffering resists description.
