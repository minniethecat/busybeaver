# Les Misérables

*Les Misérables* is Victor Hugo's magnum opus — a novel that is simultaneously a thriller, a philosophical treatise, a historical panorama, and a sermon. Published in 1862 after nearly two decades of composition, it spans the period from 1815 to 1833 and ranges across France from the provincial worlds of Digne and Montreuil-sur-Mer to the revolutionary streets of Paris. Its protagonist, [[jean-valjean|Jean Valjean]], carries the novel's central argument — that [[mercy]] can transform what [[justice|the law]] can only punish — through a plot of relentless pursuit, moral crisis, and self-sacrificing love.

## Hugo's Purpose

Hugo stated his purpose in a letter to his Italian translator: "I don't know whether it will be read by everyone, but it is meant for everyone. It addresses England as well as Spain, Italy as well as France, Germany as well as Ireland, the republics that have slaves as well as the empires that have serfs. Social problems overstep frontiers."

The novel's preface, dated January 1, 1862, distills this ambition into a single paragraph:

> (P01-003) So long as there shall exist, by virtue of law and custom, a social condemnation which, in the midst of civilization, artificially creates hells on earth, and complicates with a human fatality the destiny which is divine; so long as the three problems of the age — the degradation of man by poverty, the ruin of woman by hunger, and the dwarfing of childhood by physical and spiritual night — are not solved; so long as, in certain regions, social asphyxia shall be possible; in other words, and from a still more extended point of view, so long as ignorance and misery remain on earth, books like this cannot be useless.

The novel is thus a deliberately political act — an argument, conducted through fiction, against poverty, the penal system, and the social structures that Hugo believed were manufacturing suffering on an industrial scale.

## Summary of Content

The novel interweaves the story of Jean Valjean's redemption with a panoramic portrait of French society:

- **Volume I (Fantine)**: Valjean's release from the galleys, his transformation by [[bishop-myriel|Bishop Myriel's]] mercy, his rise as the industrialist Monsieur Madeleine, and the tragedy of [[fantine|Fantine]], whose death leaves her daughter [[cosette]] in Valjean's care
- **Volume II (Cosette)**: Valjean's rescue of Cosette from the [[thenardier|Thénardiers]], their flight through Paris, and their sanctuary in the Petit-Picpus convent; the historical digression on [[waterloo|Waterloo]]
- **Volume III (Marius)**: [[marius-pontmercy|Marius Pontmercy's]] estrangement from his royalist grandfather, his discovery of his Bonapartist father's sacrifice, and his entry into the circle of revolutionary students
- **Volume IV (Saint-Denis)**: Marius and Cosette's silent courtship in the Luxembourg Garden; the [[the-barricade-battle|June Rebellion of 1832]]; the barricade where the novel's personal and political threads converge in an explosion of sacrifice
- **Volume V (Jean Valjean)**: Valjean's flight through [[the-sewers-of-paris|the sewers of Paris]] carrying the wounded Marius; [[javert|Javert's]] suicide; Marius and Cosette's marriage; Valjean's self-imposed exile and death

## Literary Significance

*Les Misérables* is notable for its range of registers — from the psychological intensity of "A Tempest in a Skull" to the documentary precision of the sewer chapters to the epic sweep of Waterloo. It pioneered what Hugo called "the novel of the people": a form that takes the poor, the criminal, and the revolutionary seriously as subjects worthy of the highest literary art.

The novel's digressions — on Waterloo, on argot, on the Paris sewers, on the convent — were controversial at publication and remain a subject of critical debate. Hugo's defenders argue that these digressions are not interruptions but expansions: the novel's argument about justice cannot be confined to its plot because justice, for Hugo, is a problem that involves every dimension of society.
