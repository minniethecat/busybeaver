# Champmathieu

Champmathieu appears only to be nearly destroyed — a poor peasant arrested because he looks like Jean Valjean, about to be condemned to the galleys for crimes he did not commit. His trial at Arras is the novel's most compressed demonstration of everything Hugo argues about law, justice, and moral responsibility.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Champmathieu (first name unknown)
- **Role**: Catalyst — the innocent whose near-destruction forces Valjean's moral choice
- **First Appearance**: Volume I, Book VII, Chapter IX (063-001)
- **Social Position**: Poor peasant, arrested for stealing apples, mistaken for the convict Jean Valjean
- **Key Relationships**: Jean Valjean (the man he is mistaken for, and the man who saves him)

## Character Arc

Champmathieu has no arc — he is a structural device, the innocent man whose function is to be threatened so that Valjean can choose. But Hugo invests him with genuine pathos: a poor man who cannot even understand the legal proceedings that are about to destroy him, whose very ordinariness ("he looked like any other peasant") makes his plight universal.

## Key Actions

- **The trial** (Book VII, Chapter XI) — Champmathieu is identified by multiple witnesses as "Jean Valjean"; only Valjean's self-revelation saves him

## Hugo's Commentary

The trial is Hugo's most concentrated critique of legal epistemology. The court is certain; the witnesses are certain; the evidence fits. And everything is wrong. The law, Hugo argues, is not merely occasionally mistaken — its very method (categories, precedents, "facts") is structurally incapable of recognizing truth when truth takes an unexpected form.

> (063-003) "That divine thing which is called justice" — the narrator's irony at the moment of maximum injustice
