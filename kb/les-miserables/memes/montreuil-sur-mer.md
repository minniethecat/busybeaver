# Montreuil-sur-Mer

Montreuil-sur-Mer is the stage for Valjean's public life as Monsieur Madeleine — the prosperous mayor and factory owner who, unknown to all, is an escaped convict. The town represents the possibility of social transformation through ethical industry.

## Geography
- **Location**: Montreuil-sur-Mer, Pas-de-Calais, northern France
- **Type**: Provincial manufacturing town
- **Period**: 1815–1823
- **Associated Characters**: Jean Valjean (as Monsieur Madeleine, mayor), Fantine (factory worker), Javert (police inspector)

## In the Narrative
Valjean arrives as a stranger, saves the town's declining jet-bead industry through a technical innovation, and within years is the town's most respected citizen — and its mayor. His administration is a model of enlightened governance: he gives his wealth to the poor, lives in austerity, and refuses the Legion of Honor.

But Montreuil is also the site of Fantine's destruction. The same factory that Madeleine built dismisses her when her illegitimate child is discovered. Madeleine, absorbed in his own affairs, does not intervene — a failure of attention that Hugo presents as the limitation of purely economic reform without personal engagement.

## Hugo's Description
Hugo's description of Montreuil is economic rather than picturesque — the town matters as a site of production, where Valjean's technical genius and moral seriousness produce prosperity that contrasts with the squalor of Paris.

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance
Montreuil embodies **the possibility of reform** — and its limits. Madeleine's factory brings prosperity, but prosperity alone cannot protect Fantine. The town is the novel's argument that economic justice, without personal mercy, is insufficient.
