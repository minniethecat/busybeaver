# Waterloo

Waterloo is not in *Les Misérables* in the ordinary sense — the battle occurs before the novel's main action and involves almost none of its characters. Yet Hugo devotes an entire book (Volume II, Book I) to its description. The digression is one of the novel's most controversial and ambitious passages: military history transformed into philosophical meditation.

## Geography

- **Location**: Waterloo, Belgium, approximately 15 km south of Brussels
- **Type**: Battlefield — the site of Napoleon's final defeat, June 18, 1815
- **Period**: June 18, 1815 (the battle itself); the novel's characters pass through in its aftermath
- **Associated Characters**: Georges Pontmercy (Marius's father, a Napoleonic colonel who survives the battle), Thénardier (who loots corpses on the battlefield and, by chance, "saves" Pontmercy)

## In the Narrative

Hugo's Waterloo digression occupies Book I of Volume II — chapters of military history before the narrative proper resumes. The battle serves two narrative functions: it establishes the world-historical context (Waterloo ends the Napoleonic era and inaugurates the Restoration that the novel's characters must navigate), and it introduces the novel's philosophy of history.

The most famous passage is the sunken road of Ohain — a natural feature that Hugo describes in almost geological detail, arguing that this accidental depression in the landscape determined the battle's outcome as decisively as any general's strategy. Napoleon was defeated not by Wellington but by a ditch.

> (086-003) "Often a battle is lost and progress is conquered. There is less glory and more liberty."

## Hugo's Description

Hugo's Waterloo is both precise and visionary. He reconstructs the battle's movements with military exactitude while interpreting its meaning with prophetic sweep. The sunken road, the rain that delayed Napoleon's artillery, the "moral majesty" of the English squares — all are evidence for Hugo's thesis that history is governed by forces larger than individual will.

## Symbolic / Thematic Significance

Waterloo embodies **the irony of history** — the gap between human intention and historical outcome. Napoleon intended to conquer Europe; Waterloo made him a prisoner. The Restoration intended to reverse the Revolution; it accelerated the forces that would overthrow it. Hugo's Waterloo is an argument for humility before the mystery of historical causation.
