# Suffering

The French title *Les Misérables* means "The Wretched Ones" — not "the criminals" or "the poor" but those whose condition is one of comprehensive misery. Hugo's novel is an anatomy of suffering: its causes (social, economic, legal), its forms (physical hunger, moral degradation, spiritual despair), and its one possible redemption (love). The novel's moral gravity comes from its refusal to aestheticize suffering — Fantine's degradation, Valjean's nineteen years in the galleys, the barricade deaths — while simultaneously insisting that suffering can be transfigured.

## Hugo's Exposition

The Bishop's response to the guillotine crystallizes Hugo's position on suffering inflicted by the state. After witnessing the execution, Myriel is shattered not by pity alone but by the discovery that the law — which he had assumed was, at worst, a rough approximation of justice — actively manufactures suffering:

> (004-032) "I did not think that it was so monstrous. It is wrong to become absorbed in the divine law to such a degree as not to perceive human law. Death belongs to God alone. By what right do men touch that unknown thing?"

The guillotine is suffering made legal, suffering dressed in ceremony, suffering that the respectable classes can watch and then go home to dinner. Hugo's critique is not that suffering exists — it is that society *creates* it and then punishes those who bear it.

The novel's preface extends this analysis to all forms of social suffering: "So long as there shall exist, by virtue of law and custom, a social condemnation which creates hells in the midst of civilization..." (P07-001). The key phrase is "by virtue of law and custom" — suffering is not natural; it is produced by specific, identifiable human arrangements.

## Development Through the Narrative

Fantine is the novel's purest case study in socially-produced suffering. She is not guilty of anything but poverty and love. Her descent — from working woman to prostitute to corpse — traces a path that Hugo insists is not exceptional but typical. Each institution that should have helped her (the factory, the police, the hospital) instead accelerates her destruction.

Jean Valjean's suffering takes a different form. His nineteen years in the galleys are not, in Hugo's treatment, merely physical torment. The deeper suffering is moral: the bagne attempts to destroy his capacity for goodness, to convince him that he is irredeemable. The Bishop's pardon breaks this cycle precisely because it treats Valjean as capable of transformation — it refuses to let his suffering define him.

The barricade scenes in Volume IV present suffering as collective rather than individual. The students who die on the barricade suffer and die for a cause; their suffering has meaning. Hugo's ambivalence about revolutionary violence — he honors the sacrifice while mourning the cost — is the novel's most complex treatment of the relationship between suffering and justice.

## Historical and Social Context

Nineteenth-century France was marked by extremes of wealth and poverty that would have been familiar to any reader of Dickens or Zola. The Industrial Revolution had created new forms of urban misery; the penal system was brutal and public; the 1832 uprising depicted in the novel was a real event. Hugo knew these conditions firsthand — his own political activism and exile gave him a personal stake in the suffering he described.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **comfort** — the condition of those who benefit from the suffering they ignore
- **indifference** — the social sin of not seeing suffering; the Bürgermeister who refuses to house Valjean
- **prosperity** — in Hugo's treatment, often built on the suffering of the poor; wealth as moral danger
