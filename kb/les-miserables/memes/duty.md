# Duty

Duty in *Les Misérables* is a contested concept. Different characters invoke it to justify radically opposed actions: Javert performs his "duty" by hunting an escaped convict; Valjean performs his "duty" by revealing his identity to save an innocent man; the Bishop performs his "duty" by lying to the police to protect a thief. Hugo's question is not whether we have duties — all his characters agree on that — but to *whom* or to *what* our deepest duties are owed.

## Hugo's Exposition

The Bishop's life is presented as a model of duty properly understood. His duties are to God, to the poor, to the condemned — not to social convention or institutional preservation. When the prison chaplain falls ill before an execution, the curé refuses to attend: "That is no affair of mine. I have nothing to do with that unpleasant task." The Bishop's response is definitive: "Monsieur le Curé is right: it is not his place; it is mine" (004-023). Duty, for Myriel, cannot be delegated or avoided; it has the character of a personal summons.

The Conventionist G—— articulates a competing conception of duty — one owed to the future, to progress, to humanity as a whole. His vote for the death of Louis XVI was, in his telling, not murder but duty performed at terrible personal cost. The Bishop, who initially condemns this, is forced to recognize that there are duties that transcend the conventional moral order.

## Development Through the Narrative

Jean Valjean's moral life is structured by a series of duty-crises. After the Bishop's pardon, his duty is to become the man the Bishop declared him to be — a duty of gratitude transmuted into a duty of self-transformation. The Champmathieu trial presents the novel's most acute duty-conflict: Valjean's duty to the workers of Montreuil-sur-Mer (whom his factory supports) versus his duty to an innocent man about to be condemned in his place. His choice — to sacrifice himself for the truth — establishes the novel's hierarchy of duties: truth to oneself ranks above utility to others.

Javert's tragedy is that he has a correct but incomplete understanding of duty. He is not a hypocrite or a villain; he is a man whose moral universe contains only one pole — duty to the law. When Valjean spares his life at the barricade, Javert confronts a duty he cannot categorize: gratitude to an enemy. Unable to reconcile law-duty with love-duty, he chooses death.

Marius Pontmercy presents a more modest but equally instructive case. His duties to his father's memory, to Cosette, to the revolutionary cause, and to Valjean (once he learns the truth) pull him in different directions. His maturation consists in learning to rank these duties correctly — a process that culminates in his reconciliation with Valjean.

## Historical and Social Context

Duty as a philosophical concept had a rich tradition in Hugo's France. Kant's categorical imperative had placed duty at the center of ethics; the Napoleonic code had made duty to the state paramount; Catholic moral theology emphasized duties to God and neighbor. Hugo synthesizes and critiques all three, arguing finally that duty is personal before it is procedural, and that the highest duty is always to the concrete person before you.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **self-interest** — what duty overrides; the natural default that must be transcended
- **legalism** — duty perverted into rule-following without moral perception
- **cowardice** — the failure of duty; the curé who will not attend the condemned man
- **love** — in Hugo's scheme, love is duty's proper object, not its competitor
