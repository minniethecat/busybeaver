# Liberty

Liberty in *Les Misérables* is never merely political. Hugo portrays it as a condition of the soul as much as a condition of the state — the state in which a human being can make genuinely free moral choices, uncoerced by hunger, ignorance, or fear. The novel's revolutionary strand (the barricade, the Conventionist, the student societies) represents the political pursuit of liberty; its redemptive strand (Valjean's transformation, Fantine's love, the Bishop's mercy) represents liberty as a personal reality achieved through grace.

## Hugo's Exposition

The Conventionist G——, in his deathbed debate with the Bishop, articulates liberty as the core value of the French Revolution — and acknowledges its cost. His vote for the death of Louis XVI was, in his telling, not an act of vengeance but an act of liberation: the removal of an institution (monarchy) that made genuine liberty impossible. The Bishop, who initially condemns this as murder, is forced to recognize the moral seriousness of the Conventionist's position:

> (010-094) "Yes, the brutalities of progress are called revolutions. When they are over, this fact is recognized — that the human race has been treated harshly, but that it has progressed."

Liberty here is not a natural condition but an achievement — something that must be won, at terrible cost, from the forces that oppose it. Hugo's ambivalence is clear: he does not celebrate the Terror, but he insists that the liberty it bought was real and that the Bishop's moral vocabulary (pity, mercy, individual charity) is insufficient to account for its achievement.

The novel's political philosophy is most concentrated in the digression on argot (slang), which Hugo treats as the language of liberty — the speech of those excluded from official culture, creating their own linguistic world as an act of refusal and self-definition.

## Development Through the Narrative

Jean Valjean's personal liberation is the novel's central freedom-narrative. He enters the novel in chains, both literal (the galleys) and spiritual (hatred, despair). The Bishop's mercy is his first experience of freedom — the discovery that his future is not determined by his past. Every subsequent act — becoming Madeleine, rescuing Cosette, sparing Javert — is an exercise of the freedom the Bishop's pardon created.

The barricade scenes present liberty as collective action. The students and workers who build and defend the barricade are not deluded romantics (though Hugo acknowledges their naivety) but agents of history, asserting the right to freedom against a regime that denies it. Their deaths are tragic but not, in Hugo's terms, meaningless: liberty's advance has always been paid for by those willing to die for it.

Javert's unfreedom is the novel's most sophisticated treatment of the theme. Javert believes himself to be a free agent, enforcing the law by his own choice. In fact, he is the most enslaved character in the novel — enslaved to a code that permits no exceptions, no mercy, no growth. His suicide, when he glimpses the possibility of freedom (through Valjean's act of mercy), is the tragedy of a man who cannot bear to be free.

## Historical and Social Context

The novel is set against the backdrop of post-Revolutionary France: the Restoration, the July Monarchy, the 1832 uprising, the Second Empire. Each regime claimed to represent liberty while restricting it. Hugo, in exile for his opposition to Napoleon III, wrote from the perspective of one who had seen liberty betrayed repeatedly — yet who continued to believe in its ultimate triumph.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **tyranny** — liberty's political opposite; embodied in the novel by the restored monarchy and the Second Empire
- **oppression** — the social mechanism that denies liberty; poverty, the bagne, the factory
- **slavery** — the condition of absolute unfreedom; the novel's recurring metaphor for social degradation
- **the-law** — in Hugo's treatment, often liberty's enemy, enforcing the conditions that make freedom impossible

## Key Passages

> (010-094) "The brutalities of progress are called revolutions." — the Conventionist, on liberty's cost

> The argot digression: language as an act of freedom by those excluded from official culture — the novel's most extended political argument

> (004-032) "Death belongs to God alone" — the guillotine as the state's ultimate denial of liberty
