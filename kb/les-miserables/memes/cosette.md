# Cosette

Cosette is *Les Misérables*'s symbol of love reborn from suffering. Born Euphrasie to the abandoned working woman Fantine, she endures a childhood of brutal exploitation at the hands of the [[thenardier]]s before being rescued by [[jean-valjean]] — an act that transforms both their lives. Through her, Hugo argues that the love a child receives determines the person she becomes, and that the cycle of suffering can be broken by deliberate, protective love.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Euphrasie "Cosette" (surname: Fauchelevent by adoption; Pontmercy by marriage)
- **Role**: Co-protagonist — the beloved daughter of Jean Valjean, the romantic interest of Marius Pontmercy
- **First Appearance**: Volume I, Book IV, Chapter I — "One Mother Meets Another Mother" (037-045)
- **Social Position**: Abandoned child → exploited servant at the Thénardier inn → convent student at Petit-Picpus → fashionable young woman of independent means → wife of Marius Pontmercy
- **Key Relationships**: Fantine (mother who sacrificed everything for her), Jean Valjean (adoptive father and protector), the Thénardiers (childhood exploiters), Marius Pontmercy (husband and beloved)

## Character Arc

Cosette's trajectory is the novel's arc of hope: from the darkest deprivation to a life of love and security. Hugo introduces her at the Thénardier inn in Montfermeil, where she has been left by her desperate mother. The name "Cosette," Hugo tells us, means "little thing" — and she is treated as exactly that: a creature of no consequence, an object to be used. (037-046) For Cosette, read Euphrasie. The child's true name was overwritten by the casual diminishment of her exploiters.

Her childhood is a sustained exercise in cruelty. Cosette is made to run errands, sweep, wash, and carry burdens far beyond her strength. The Thénardiers consider themselves "all the more authorized to treat her with so much severity" because her mother sends less and less money. (039-016) She is the scapegoat for every household frustration, beaten and starved while the Thénardier daughters, [[eponine]] and Azelma, are dressed in finery.

The turning point arrives on Christmas Eve 1823, when Jean Valjean — now a fugitive after the Champmathieu trial — appears at Montfermeil to fulfill his promise to the dying [[fantine]]. He finds Cosette sent alone into the dark forest to fetch water, a child so terrified and exhausted that she cannot lift her bucket. (095-040) The encounter is framed by Hugo as a nativity scene rewritten: the rescuer arrives not as a king but as a convict, and the child he saves is not destined for divinity but for ordinary human happiness.

Valjean takes Cosette to Paris, then to the shelter of the Petit-Picpus convent, where they live in hiding for years. In the convent, Cosette receives an education, grows into health and beauty, and develops a bond with Valjean so deep that Hugo describes it as the love of a father who has never been a father, for a daughter who was never his daughter — a love constructed entirely by choice.

Her adolescence at the Rue Plumet brings her into contact with [[marius-pontmercy]], and the novel's great secondary love story begins. Cosette's love for Marius is pure, romantic, and transformative — it gives Valjean's last years their bittersweet shape. When Marius learns the truth of Valjean's past and distances them, Cosette's happiness is shadowed, but the novel ends with her married and secure, the beneficiary of every sacrifice made on her behalf.

## Key Actions

- **Sent alone to fetch water** (Volume I, Book IV) — the definitive image of her childhood suffering: a terrified child sent into the darkness, where her rescuer awaits (095-040)
- **Departure from the Thénardiers** (Volume I, Book IV) — Valjean buys her freedom with a costly settlement; Cosette leaves Montfermeil holding the hand of a stranger she will learn to call father
- **Life at the Petit-Picpus convent** — years of hidden but protected childhood, the necessary shelter for a girl whose very existence is a danger to her protector
- **Falling in love with Marius** (Volume III–IV) — Cosette's romantic awakening, which sets in motion the novel's resolution and Valjean's final sacrifice

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo's treatment of Cosette is distinctive: she is less analyzed than witnessed, less an argument than a presence. The narrative voice, so prosecutorial with [[fantine]] and so theological with [[jean-valjean]], becomes tender with Cosette. He describes her at the Thénardiers' inn:

> (094-021) Cosette was between them, subjected to their double pressure, like a creature who is ground by a millstone and torn by pincers.

And yet Cosette's essential quality is resilience. Hugo does not present her as permanently damaged by her suffering but as capable of receiving love — the novel's ultimate claim that love, given early and consistently enough, can undo the work of cruelty.

## Symbolic / Thematic Role

Cosette embodies **love as redemption's fulfillment**. If Jean Valjean represents the transformative power of receiving mercy, Cosette represents the transformative power of receiving love. She is the living proof that Valjean's conversion was genuine — not a private spiritual experience but a sustained, costly commitment that produced a flourishing human being. Her happiness at the novel's end is the vindication of every sacrifice: Fantine's, Valjean's, even [[eponine]]'s. She represents the future that suffering purchases.
