# Nature

In *Les Misérables*, nature is never mere scenery. Hugo uses the natural world — forests, rivers, the sea, the night sky — as a moral theater, a symbolic register through which the novel's deeper meanings are expressed. Nature can be sublime (the Alps that Valjean imagines as freedom), terrifying (the sea into which Javert plunges), or indifferent (the night through which Valjean flees). In every case, it speaks — not in human language but in a grammar of light, darkness, vastness, and power that Hugo's characters must learn to read.

## Hugo's Exposition

Hugo's Romantic aesthetics treat nature as a source of moral knowledge unavailable to institutions. The chapter "The Interior of Despair" (Book II, Chapter VII) describes Valjean's flight through the night, pursued by Javert. The darkness is not merely atmospheric; it is the visual correlative of Valjean's inner state — confusion, fear, the absence of moral clarity. When dawn breaks (in a later chapter), it is also a theological event: light as grace, visibility as moral reckoning.

The novel's most famous nature passage is the description of Waterloo, where the landscape itself becomes a participant in the historical drama. The sunken road of Ohain — a natural feature that Hugo describes in almost geological detail — determines the battle's outcome as decisively as any general's decision. Nature here is indifferent to human purposes; it does not take sides, but its blind operation shapes history.

Javert's suicide in the Seine is nature as judgment. The river that receives him is not a passive receptacle; Hugo describes it as a "gulf," an "abyss," a darkness that both mirrors and completes Javert's spiritual darkness. Nature does not punish — but it does not save, either. It is the final absence of the mercy that Javert could not accept.

## Development Through the Narrative

The convent garden at Petit-Picpus represents nature domesticated by religious discipline — ordered, enclosed, denied its wildness. Hugo's treatment is characteristically ambivalent: he respects the nuns' attempt to create a space of peace while deploring the withdrawal from the world that the walled garden symbolizes.

Water, the novel's most recurrent natural symbol, carries multiple meanings. The sea is freedom (Jean Valjean's dream of escape), danger (the bagne at Toulon), and judgment (Javert's death). The sewers of Paris — the novel's most extended "nature" digression — are nature inverted: the river Bièvre, channeled underground and turned into a cloaca, becomes a metaphor for society's hidden filth.

The stars appear at moments of maximum moral intensity. When Valjean, contemplating his decision at Arras, looks at the night sky, Hugo treats the stars as witnesses — silent, distant, but not indifferent. They are the visible sign of an order beyond the human.

## Historical and Social Context

Hugo was a leading figure in French Romanticism, a movement that elevated nature as a source of truth and beauty against the artificiality of urban, industrial civilization. His poem sequences (especially *Les Contemplations*) developed a nature theology that informs the novel's treatment of landscape as moral testimony. The Paris sewers digression also reflects Hugo's practical interest in urban infrastructure — he researched the sewers extensively and advocated for their modernization.

## Contrasting Concepts

- **society** — nature's opposite; the artificial order that nature judges and transcends
- **civilization** — what builds walls (convents, prisons, cities) against nature's wildness
- **the-city** — Paris as anti-nature; the human construction that nature, in the form of the river and the sewer, both sustains and undermines

## Key Passages

> The Waterloo digression: the sunken road as nature's decisive role in history — Hugo's most extended meditation on nature's indifference to human purposes

> Javert at the Seine: "He took a few steps forward and looked down into the gulf" — nature as the final judge

> The night sky over Arras: Valjean looks at the stars, and they bear witness to his moral struggle
