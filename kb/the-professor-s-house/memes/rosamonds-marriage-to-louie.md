Rosamond's [[love-and-desire|marriage]] to [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] is the event around which the novel's [[family-and-domesticity|family]] drama pivots.
[[rosamond-marsellus|Rosamond St. Peter]] — elder daughter of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter]] and [[lillian-st-peter]], former fiancee of [[tom-outland]] — marries [[louie-marsellus]], an energetic Jewish engineer who has moved to [[hamilton]].

For [[kathleen-mcgregor|Kathleen]], the [[love-and-desire|marriage]] represents a betrayal of Tom's [[memory-and-the-past|memory]].
For [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]], it marks the moment when the values of [[solitude-and-creativity|intellectual life]] are "translated into the vulgar tongue" of [[money-and-materialism|money]].
For Rosamond, it inaugurates a [[loss-and-change|transformation]] — a hardening into something worldly, estranged from the girl who grew up in the shabby [[the-old-house|old house]].

## 背景

Before [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] appeared, Rosamond was engaged to [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]].
Tom was the brilliant young physicist whose [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] of the [[cliff-dweller-civilization|Blue Mesa ruins]] and [[science-and-discovery|invention]] of a revolutionary engine form the novel's Book II.
He was killed in the [[world-war-i|Great War]] before they could marry.

His will named Rosamond sole beneficiary.
The formula lay dormant in Professor Crane's physics [[work-and-vocation|labor]]atory.
At the time, this seemed a gesture with little practical consequence.

[[louie-marsellus|Louie]] came to [[hamilton|Hamilton]] "to put in the Edison power plant, just at the time the city was stirred up about [[outland|Outland]]'s being killed at the front" (012-014).
The Professor himself introduced [[louie-marsellus|Marsellus]] to the Cranes.
Through them, [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] learned of [[outland|Outland]]'s [[work-and-vocation|work]], saw its commercial value, and invested his capital to bring it to market.

His engagement to Rosamond followed — along with the transfer of [[outland|Outland]]'s papers from the [[work-and-vocation|labor]]atory to [[louie-marsellus|Marsellus]]'s control.
The precise chronology of the romance is never detailed.
What matters is the reaction: a cascade of loyalty, [[death-and-mortality|grief]], and resentment that reshapes every relationship in the [[family-and-domesticity|household]].

## 经过

The engagement is announced offstage — the novel's present-time narrative begins after the [[love-and-desire|marriage]] is established — but its consequences unfold across Book I in increasingly painful scenes.

The most devastating is Kathleen's confession to her father in Chapter VII:

> When they were little girls, Kathleen adored her older sister and liked to wait on her, was always more excited about Rosie's new dresses and winter coat than about her own.
> This attachment had lasted even after they were grown.
> St. Peter had never seen any change in it until Rosamond announced her engagement to Louie Marsellus.
> Then, all at once, Kathleen seemed to be done with her sister.
> Her father believed she couldn't forgive Rosie's forgetting Tom so quickly. (007-033)

The [[love-and-desire|marriage]] ceremony is never described — a conspicuous absence characteristic of Cather's technique.
The aftermath unfolds instead: the [[louie-marsellus|Marsellus]] estate named "[[outland|Outland]]" (provoking Scott's "[[outland|Outland]], outlandish!" (002-034)), lavish spending on emeralds and furs, and Rosamond's steady [[loss-and-change|transformation]] into a creature of "magnificence."

## 后果

Financially, the [[outland|Outland]] [[money-and-materialism|wealth]] transforms the [[family-and-domesticity|family]]: the new [[home-and-belonging|house]], [[chicago|Chicago]] shopping trips, a planned summer in [[france|France]].
Socially, it elevates [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] and Rosamond into fashionable consumption.
But morally, it does damage that no [[money-and-materialism|money]] can repair.

The sisters' estrangement is the most visible wound.
Rosamond's furs, French dresses, and emeralds become weapons in a silent war that turns Kathleen's face "green with envy" (007-025).
The Professor, watching from his study window, feels "a sharp pain clutched his he[[art-and-religion|art]]. Was it for this the light in [[outland|Outland]]'s [[work-and-vocation|labor]]atory used to burn so far into the night!" (007-034).

The [[love-and-desire|marriage]] also [[loss-and-change|change]]s [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]]:

> Since Rosamond's marriage to Marsellus, both she and her mother had changed bewilderingly in some respects — changed and hardened.
> But Louie, who had done the damage, had not damaged himself.
> It was to him that one appealed — for Augusta, for Professor Crane, for the bruised feelings of people less fortunate. (015-016)

The paradox is central: [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] is kind and generous, yet the world he brings into being corrodes the very relationships he wishes to nurture.
The Professor's refusal of [[outland|Outland]] [[money-and-materialism|money]] — "my friendship with [[outland|Outland]] is the one thing I will not have translated into the vulgar tongue" (004-023) — becomes a line in the sand that the [[love-and-desire|marriage]] forces into the open.

## 史料与考辨

Cather's treatment raises several intersecting themes.
The casual antisemitism of the period registers when Kathleen asks if nobody else calls [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] a Jew (007-020).
Yet [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] is consistently more generous than the Gentile characters who resent him.

The [[love-and-desire|marriage]] embodies the commodification of [[art-and-religion|art]] and intellect.
Tom's genius becomes the engine of a fortune his friends cannot share.

The absent wedding scene is a signature Cather technique.
She wrote that novelists should present "the emotional aura of the fact or the thing or the deed" rather than the thing itself.
The meaning emerges entirely through consequences.
