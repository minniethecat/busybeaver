## Definition

Love in *The Professor's House* appears in multiple forms, and the novel is unusually frank in distinguishing among them — and in ranking them. Romantic love, p[[art-and-religion|art]]icularly in marriage, is portrayed as fragile and often compromised by [[money-and-materialism|money]], habit, and divergent [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]s. Filial love between parents and children is real but insufficient to bridge generational differences. The novel's highest form of love is the disinterested, [[friendship|intellectual companionship]] represented by [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s relationship with [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] — a love that Cather deliberately [[home-and-belonging|place]]s beyond easy categorization as either romantic or platonic.

## In the Novel

The Professor's marriage to [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian St. Peter]] is the novel's primary case study in marital love. They have shared decades, raised daughters, and built a life together, yet the novel opens with them living ap[[art-and-religion|art]] — [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] staying in the [[the-old-house|old house]] while his [[family-and-domesticity|family]] moves to the new one. Cather does not depict the marriage as a failure, but as something that has run its course into a kind of affectionate estrangement. The Professor's observation that

> "People who are intensely in love when they marry, and who go on being so, usually face the worst kind of reaction" (003-024). suggests his philosophical detachment from romantic ideals.

Kathleen and Rosamond, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s daughters, embody different aspects of love's fate. Kathleen's [[art-and-religion|art]]istic sensibility aligns her with her father, while Rosamond's embrace of [[money-and-materialism|wealth]] and social life — facilitated by her marriage to [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] — represents a form of love that [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] cannot recognize as his own. The novel never denies that [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] genuinely loves Rosamond, but it asks whether love expressed through consumption is really love at all.

The novel's most intense expressions of love are reserved for non-marital relationships. The Professor "loved [[childhood|youth]] — he was weak to it, it kindled him" (001-041) — a statement that encompasses his feeling for Tom, for his students, and for his own lost [[childhood|youth]]. Tom's friend [[rodney-blake|Roddy Blake]] speaks of love between men in the language of tragedy and devotion. And [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s relationship with [[augusta|Augusta]], the sewing-woman, is described in terms that blend affection, routine, and a kind of sacramental ordinariness.

The word "love" itself is used sparingly and precisely. When Kathleen cries out "oh, Scott, I do love you very much!" (009-054) to her husband, it is one of the novel's only direct declarations — and its [[home-and-belonging|place]]ment, late in the narrative, suggests Cather's deliberate restraint.

## Character Connections

- **[[Professor Godfrey St. Peter]]**: His capacity for love is deep but unconventional. His greatest love is for [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] — a relationship that defies easy labels.
- **[[Lillian St. Peter]]**: The Professor's wife, whose love has curdled into social [[ambition-and-success|ambition]] and quiet resentment. She represents what marital love becomes when the intellectual and emotional connection fades.
- **[[Kathleen St. Peter]]**: The younger daughter, whose marriage to [[scott-mcgregor|Scott McGregor]] seems to represent a healthier, less materialistic form of love than her sister's.
- **[[Rosamond St. Peter]]**: Her marriage to [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] is loving in its way, but her father sees it as a sellout — love purchased with Tom's [[money-and-materialism|money]].
- **[[Louie Marsellus]]**: The husband who loves Rosamond genuinely, generously, and with an enthusiasm [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] finds suspect precisely because it is expressed through spending.
- **[[Tom Outland]]**: The object of the novel's purest love — [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s love for him, Roddy's, and perhaps Cather's own.

## Critical Context

Cather's treatment of love has been a central focus of queer readings of her [[work-and-vocation|work]]. The intensity of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s feeling for Tom, the novel's evident preference for same-sex [[friendship|intellectual companionship]] over heterosexual marriage, and Cather's own biography (she lived for nearly forty years with her p[[art-and-religion|art]]ner Edith Lewis) have led many critics to read *The Professor's House* as a coded exploration of homosexual love. Other scholars caution against reducing the novel's complex emotional [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]] to any single category, arguing that Cather deliberately creates relationships that resist labeling — a form of love that is neither romantic friendship nor sublimated desire but something uniquely her own. The novel's ranking of love ([[friendship|intellectual companionship]] > filial affection > romantic love > commercialized romance) is one of its most radical features.

## Related Concepts

- **[[friendship]]**: The novel's highest form of love — disinterested, intellectual, and enduring beyond [[death-and-mortality|death]].
- **[[marriage]]**: The institutional form of love that the novel treats most skeptically, p[[art-and-religion|art]]icularly when it becomes entangled with [[money-and-materialism|money]].
- **[[money-and-materialism]]**: The corruption of love by [[money-and-materialism|wealth]] — Rosamond and [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]'s marriage is the prime example.
- **[[death-and-mortality]]**: Love and [[death-and-mortality|death]] are the novel's twin poles — love is what we lose to [[death-and-mortality|death]], and what, in [[memory-and-the-past|memory]], survives it.
