## Description

Lake Michigan is one of the five Great Lakes of North America, and in the novel it functions as [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s connection to the elemental — water, movement, the body. He was "born on Lake Michigan" (001-003) and is from "mixed stock (Canadian French on one side, and American farmers on the other)." The lake is associated, in the novel's [[nature-and-landscape|geography]] of feeling, with freedom, [[solitude-and-creativity|solitude]], and a physical vitality that contrasts with [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s sedentary scholarly life.

## In the Novel

References to the lake are sparse but significant. The Professor is described throughout as having the body of "a tireless swimmer" (001-003), and his swimming in the lake — [[solitude-and-creativity|alone]], often at dawn or dusk — is one of the few physical activities he continues to enjoy without ambivalence. The lake represents a self that exists before and beyond the complications of [[love-and-desire|marriage]], [[fatherhood-and-legacy|fatherhood]], and [[scholarship-and-knowledge|academic life]] — the boy who swam in Lake Michigan before he became [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]].

The lake also functions as a geographical marker of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s origins, distinguishing him from the prairie-bred citizens of [[hamilton|Hamilton]]. Water, in Cather's symbolic vocabulary, is associated with freedom and [[memory-and-the-past|the past]]; the prairie with [[work-and-vocation|work]] and the present. The Professor's continuing relationship with the lake is a form of fidelity to his own deepest self.

## Significance

Lake Michigan is the third term in the novel's [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]] triad: the garden (cultivated nature, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s creation), the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] (wild nature, sublime and ancient), and the lake (elemental nature, personal and original). Each represents a different relationship between the human and the natural, and together they map [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s inner [[nature-and-landscape|geography]] — the [[home-and-belonging|place]]s where, in different ways, he is most himself.
