Spain is the European country most central to [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Godfrey St. Peter]]'s scholarly identity. While [[france|France]] represents his personal history and cultural taste, Spain is the object of his intellectual [[work-and-vocation|vocation]] — the country whose archives he mined for "two summers" (001-037) and whose [[spanish-adventurers-in-north-america|adventurers]] became the subject of his life's [[work-and-vocation|work]].

## The Scholar's Spain

[[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s connection to Spain is [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al and immersive. The narrator notes that "he had been in Spain a good deal, and was an [[power|authority]] on certain phases of Spanish history" (001-003). This is not the tourist's Spain of [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]]'s [[paris|Paris]] itinerary, but the [[scholarship-and-knowledge|research]]er's Spain of archives, monasteries, and records.

His [[spanish-adventurers-in-north-america|Spanish Adventurers in North America]] — the multi-volume [[work-and-vocation|work]] that won him the Oxford prize — was built from "two summers in Spain studying records" (001-037) and "two summers in the [[blue-mesa|Southwest]] on the trail of his adventurers" (001-037). The [[work-and-vocation|work]] required both hemispheres: Spanish archives and American [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]]s.

During "his second Sabbatical year in Spain, Horace and his uncle together" (003-052) — an arrangement that linked [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]] with [[family-and-domesticity|family]] obligation. The sabbatical year was spent p[[art-and-religion|art]]ly in the archives, p[[art-and-religion|art]]ly with his wife's [[family-and-domesticity|family]], as if Spain allowed both modes of existence.

## The Southern Coast

The novel repeatedly invokes the southern coast of Spain as an image of elemental beauty. [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] recalls "skirting the south coast of Spain; from the rose of dawn to the gold of sunset" (009-032) — a [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]] of pure sensation, distinct from the scholarly interior.

> "The Professor, in his younger days, had sailed along the southern coast of Spain." (008-022)

This remembered coastline becomes p[[art-and-religion|art]] of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s inner [[nature-and-landscape|geography]] — a [[home-and-belonging|place]] of freedom and beauty juxtaposed against [[hamilton|Hamilton]]'s "flat and heavy" lake-shore.

## Objects and Memory

Spain enters the novel through material objects. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] declared that "she wanted nothing from Spain but a cork-cutter" after [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] accidentally bought "all of it from his last trip to Spain. It wasn't foresight" (009-001) — a domestic comedy that reveals how Spanish things accumulated in the [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] [[family-and-domesticity|household]].

The "mouldering monasteries in Spain" (002-030) appear in contrast to the ancient cliff-[[home-and-belonging|dwelling]]s of the American [[blue-mesa|Southwest]], an implicit comparison between [[old-world-vs-new-world|Old World and New World]] ruins that runs through the novel.

## Spain and Tom Outland

[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s story intersects with Spain. The Professor and Tom "went down into the Southwest together" with "the copy of the Fray Marcos manuscript [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] had made from the original in Spain" (025-006).

> "By autumn they had become good friends." (025-006)

The friendship forged in the [[blue-mesa|Southwest]] had Spain as its scholarly backdrop. The "third summer together, in [[paris|Paris]], that never came off" (025-008) had a predecessor: the summer in Spain and the Southwest that did.
