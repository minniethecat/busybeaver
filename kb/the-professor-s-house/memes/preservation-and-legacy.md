## Definition

Preservation in The Professor's House takes many forms: [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] preserves European history in his [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]]; [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] preserves the cliff-dweller [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts on [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]]; the [[the-old-house|old house]] preserves [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s [[marriage|married life]] in its familiar rooms; and the entire novel functions as an act of preservation — of Tom's story, of a moment in American [[scholarship-and-knowledge|academic life]], of a [[home-and-belonging|house]] about to be vacated. But preservation is always contested: the cliff-dweller [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts are sold to a German collector, the [[the-old-house|old house]] will be left behind, and Tom's [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]] is commercialized by the [[louie-marsellus|Marsellus]]es. The novel asks what — and who — decides what deserves to endure.

## In the Novel

The most explicit statement of the preservation theme comes through [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al life:

> "The struggle to preserve the dignity of the university, and their own, against the new commercialism." (012-030)

The cliff-dweller ruins on [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] represent preservation in its purest form — [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts kept intact by dry air and [[solitude-and-creativity|isolation]] for centuries:

> "Preserved in the dry air and almost perfect, as if the inhabitants had just walked away." (020-010)

[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] feels the sacred weight of these objects:

> "They were something that had been preserved through the ages by a miracle." (023-052)

Yet the novel's central tragedy is the failure of preservation: the [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts are sold, Tom dies, the [[the-old-house|old house]] is abandoned, and Tom's [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]] is converted into [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]]'s real estate. The novel itself — Cather's [[work-and-vocation|work]] of preservation — is the one thing that endures.

## Character Connections

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]**: His life's [[work-and-vocation|work]] is preservation — of European history in his multi-volume study, of his intellectual integrity against commercial pressure. The [[the-old-house|old house]] is his preserved world (012-030).

- **[[tom-outland]]**: The novel's archetypal preserver — he recognizes the value of the cliff-dweller [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts and tries to protect them. His failure to preserve them prefigures the failure to preserve his own [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]].

- **[[louie-marsellus]]**: Represents the antipode of preservation — commercialization, [[loss-and-change|transformation]], the conversion of [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]] into real estate. His estate "[[outland|Outland]]" preserves only the name.

- **[[rodney-blake]]**: Tom's companion who sells the cliff-dweller [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts — the novel's cautionary tale about the failure to preserve what matters.

## Critical Context

Cather wrote The Professor's House in the mid-1920s, a period of rapid modernization and cultural anxiety about what was being lost. The novel's preservation theme connects to broader Progressive-era concerns about historical conservation (the Antiquities Act of 1906, the creation of Mesa Verde National Park). Critics note the irony: Cather preserves [[outland|Tom Outland's story]] in fiction, even as the novel documents the failure of preservation within its world. The act of writing itself becomes the only reliable form of preservation — the Spanish Adventurers manuscript, if finished, would preserve history; the novel itself preserves Tom.
