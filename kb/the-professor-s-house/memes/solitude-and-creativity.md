## Definition

Solitude in *The Professor's House* is not loneliness but a condition of creative and intellectual possibility. [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]]'s most productive years are those spent in the cramped attic study, in the [[the-garden|walled garden]], or alone in the [[the-old-house|old house]] during the summers when his [[family-and-domesticity|family]] decamps to Colorado. Cather presents solitude as essential to the life of the mind — and as increasingly threatened by the claims of [[family-and-domesticity|family]], institution, and commerce. The novel asks what it costs to protect the conditions under which serious [[work-and-vocation|work]] can be done, and whether [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s [[escape|withdrawal]] from social life is a strength, a pathology, or both.

## In the Novel

The Professor's need for solitude is established in the opening chapter. The [[family-and-domesticity|family]] has moved to the new [[home-and-belonging|house]], but he has stayed behind in the old one, ostensibly to finish his writing. "I'm going to write on here for a while" (001-020), he tells [[augusta|Augusta]]. The real reason is deeper: the [[the-old-house|old house]], with its cramped spaces and familiar discomforts, is the [[nature-and-landscape|environment]] in which his mind has learned to [[work-and-vocation|work]]. The new [[home-and-belonging|house]], with its modern conveniences and social expectations, threatens the solitude his [[work-and-vocation|work]] requires.

The garden is the novel's supreme image of creative solitude. During the long summers when his wife and daughters escape the prairie heat, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] "stayed at [[home-and-belonging|home]] with his garden, sending his wife and daughters to Colorado" (001-007). Alone, he brings his books and papers into the garden and [[work-and-vocation|work]]s under the linden-trees. These solitary summers are presented not as deprivation but as the novel's image of happiness — a man alone with his [[work-and-vocation|work]], in a space he has made beautiful.

The Professor's relationship with his [[work-and-vocation|work]] itself is solitary. "He had [[work-and-vocation|work]]ed out his career and brought up his two daughters" (001-001) — the parallel construction linking [[work-and-vocation|work]] and [[family-and-domesticity|family]] suggests two separate but equally demanding commitments. The novel is clear that the [[work-and-vocation|work]] matters, and that its demands — for silence, for concentration, for uninterrupted time — are legitimate, not selfish.

> "In those months when he was a bachelor again, he brought down his books and papers and worked in a deck chair under the linden-trees; breakfasted and lunched and had his tea in the garden" (001-007).

[[outland|Tom Outland's story]] offers a parallel. His solitary weeks on the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]], alone with the cliff-dweller ruins and his notebooks, are the creative peak of his life — the [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] that defines him. Solitude on the mesa, like solitude in the garden, is the necessary condition for seeing clearly.

## Character Connections

- **[[Professor Godfrey St. Peter]]**: The novel's solitary creator. His need for isolation is both his greatest strength and, by the novel's end, a near-fatal [[escape|withdrawal]] from life.
- **[[Tom Outland]]**: The solitary discoverer. His time alone on the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] is the novel's other model of creative solitude.
- **[[Lillian St. Peter]]**: Represents the social world that competes with his solitude. Her summers in Colorado, which [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] experiences as blessed isolation, she experiences as exile.
- **[[Augusta]]**: The companion who makes solitude bearable. Her presence in the study is so unobtrusive, so practical, that it does not threaten [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s solitude but somehow completes it.

## Critical Context

The theme of solitude and creativity connects *The Professor's House* to a long tradition of thinking about the relationship between isolation and [[art-and-religion|art]]istic or intellectual production — from Wordsworth's solitary wanderer to Virginia Woolf's "room of one's own." Cather, who never married and maintained a fiercely protected private life with her p[[art-and-religion|art]]ner Edith Lewis, knew firsthand the demands of creative solitude. The Professor's struggle to protect his [[work-and-vocation|work]]ing conditions against the encroachments of [[family-and-domesticity|family]] and society has been read as a coded version of Cather's own experience as a woman [[art-and-religion|art]]ist in a culture that expected women to prioritize [[family-and-domesticity|domestic life]].
