## Role in the Novel

Mr. Wagner appears briefly in Book II ([[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s Story) as a government official in [[washington-dc|Washington]] D.C. whom [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] attempts to interest in the cliff-dweller discoveries at [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]]. He does not appear directly in a scene; rather, he is described by [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian St. Peter]] and encountered by Tom at a lunch meeting at the Shoreham Hotel. His role is to represent the bureaucratic machinery that Tom must navigate — and ultimately fails to move — in his effort to secure government protection for the mesa's archaeological treasures.

## Character Portrait

Mr. Wagner is characterized entirely through second-hand description and Tom's brief encounter. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian St. Peter]] describes him as "bothered by a great many foolish people who waste his time" and notes that "he is suspicious" (023-006). This paints a picture of a harassed bureaucrat, accustomed to deflecting petitioners and skeptical of unsolicited approaches. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]]'s strategy for dealing with him — an expensive lunch at the Shoreham, letting him order — suggests a man accustomed to deference and the rituals of official [[washington-dc|Washington]].

## Key Relationships

- **[[lillian-st-peter]]**: Connection. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]], familiar with Wagner's schedule and habits ("I keep a list of his appointments"), arranges Tom's meeting and coaches him on the proper approach (023-006).
- **[[tom-outland]]**: Petitioner. Tom meets Wagner at the Shoreham for an expensive lunch, hoping to interest him in the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] discoveries. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]]'s arch comment that "if you show him enough of the Shoreham pottery, that will be more effective" (023-013) than Tom's actual [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts wryly foreshadows the meeting's futility.

## Critical Significance

Mr. Wagner functions as a symbol of institutional indifference in the novel. He never speaks a line of dialogue; his importance lies in what he represents — the gap between genuine [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] and the bureaucratic machinery that might (but does not) protect it. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]]'s coaching of Tom, with its emphasis on expense accounts, appearances, and the right hotel, underscores the cultural distance between Tom's world of mesa exploration and [[washington-dc|Washington]]'s world of official luncheons. The joke about "Shoreham pottery" — the hotel's fine china being more persuasive than thousand-year-old cliff-dweller ceramics — encapsulates one of the novel's central ironies about value, [[truth|authenticity]], and the failure of institutions to recognize what truly matters.

## Key Scenes

- **[[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] arranges the meeting**: [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian St. Peter]] describes Wagner's suspicious nature and offers to set up a luncheon appointment, coaching Tom on the protocol (023-006).
- **The Shoreham lunch**: Tom takes his pottery to the Shoreham to show Wagner; [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] quips that the hotel's own china may prove more impressive than the [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts (023-013).
