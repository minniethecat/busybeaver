## Role in the Novel

Professor Godfrey St. Peter is the protagonist and moral center of the novel, appearing in the framing Books I and III. A historian by [[work-and-vocation|vocation]], he has spent his career at [[hamilton|Hamilton University]], a Midwestern institution, writing his magnum opus on Spanish adventurers in North America. The novel traces his psychological journey through a midlife crisis precipitated by his [[family-and-domesticity|family]]'s move to a new [[home-and-belonging|house]], the lingering impact of [[death-of-tom-outland|Tom Outland's death]] in [[world-war-i|the Great War]], and his growing alienation from the commercial values that have reshaped his [[family-and-domesticity|family]]'s world. His decision to remain in the dismantled [[the-old-house|old house]] while his [[family-and-domesticity|family]] moves to the new one defines the novel's opening and its central question: what happens when a man outlives the life he built?

## Character Portrait

Cather draws the Professor with physical precision and psychological depth. He is described as having "a long brown face, with an oval chin over which he wore a close-trimmed Van Dyke, like a tuft of shiny black fur" (001-003). His "wicked-looking eyebrows made his students call him Mephistopheles" (001-003), but the intimidating exterior conceals a man of deep feeling and increasingly fragile resilience.

Of mixed Canadian French and American stock, St. Peter "was commonly said to look like a Spaniard" (001-003) — a detail that connects his physical appearance to his scholarly obsession with [[spain|Spain]]. He has the body of "a tireless swimmer" (001-003), and his relationship to [[lake-michigan|Lake Michigan]], where he was born and where he still swims, represents a connection to the elemental that predates and outlasts civilization.

> "The Professor in pyjamas was not an unpleasant sight; for looks, the fewer clothes he had on, the better. Anything that clung to his body showed it to be built upon extremely good bones, with the slender hips and springy shoulders of a tireless swimmer." (001-003)

The Professor's defining trait is his capacity for disinterested [[love-and-desire|love]] — for his [[work-and-vocation|work]], for Tom, for the garden. He has "a deft hand with tools" (001-001) and a craftsman's relationship to his [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]]. His "misfortune was that he [[love-and-desire|love]]d [[childhood|youth]] — he was weak to it, it kindled him" (001-041), a trait that explains both his bond with Tom and his growing distance from his own [[youth-and-aging|aging]] life.

## Relationships

- **[[Lillian St. Peter]]** — Wife. Decades of shared life have settled into [[love-and-desire|affection]]ate estrangement. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]]'s social [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]s and the Professor's scholarly values are increasingly incompatible.
- **[[Tom Outland]]** — Former student, friend, and the most significant relationship of the Professor's life. Their [[friendship|intellectual companionship]], forged in the garden, survives Tom's [[death-and-mortality|death]] as [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] and ideal.
- **[[Kathleen St. Peter]]** — Younger daughter, an [[art-and-religion|art]]ist. The [[family-and-domesticity|family]] member most sympathetic to her father's values.
- **[[Rosamond St. Peter]]** — Elder daughter, formerly Tom's fiancée, married to [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]]. Her embrace of [[money-and-materialism|wealth]] represents a betrayal of her father's ideals.
- **[[Augusta]]** — The sewing-woman who shares the Professor's study. An unlikely companion whose practical wisdom and Catholic faith provide an alternative model of the good life.
- **[[Louie Marsellus]]** — Son-in-law. The Professor finds him generous, energetic, and fundamentally alien — a man of commerce in a world the Professor thought he had protected from it.

## Significance

The Professor embodies the novel's central tensions: Old World versus New, [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]] versus commerce, [[solitude-and-creativity|solitude]] versus [[family-and-domesticity|family]], [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] versus the present. His near-[[death-and-mortality|death]] in [[tom-outlands-invention|the gas]]-filled study at the novel's end — and his decision, assisted by [[augusta|Augusta]], to live — represents a qualified affirmation: life is worth living, but on terms that the Professor, and perhaps only the Professor, can define.
