## Definition

The garden is [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s walled French garden in [[hamilton|Hamilton]], a half-acre of formal European design — gravel, shrubs, bright flowers, and symmetrical linden-trees — created and tended by [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor Godfrey St. Peter]] over more than twenty years. Within the novel's symbolic [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]], the garden represents an ordered refuge from the pressures of [[family-and-domesticity|family]] life, academic obligation, and the encroaching [[money-and-materialism|materialism]] of the modern world. It is a space where [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] can be [[solitude-and-creativity|alone]] with his thoughts, his memories, and his [[solitude-and-creativity|creative work]].

## In the Novel

The garden is introduced in the opening chapter as the one thing that can draw [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] out of the melancholy of [[the-old-house|the dismantled house]] (001-005). It had been "the comfort of his life" (001-006) — and also "the one thing his neighbours held against him," suggesting its foreign, un-American character. Cather describes it in precise horticultural detail:

> "There was not a blade of grass; it was a tidy half-acre of glistening gravel and glistening shrubs and bright flowers. There were trees, of course; a spreading horse-chestnut, a row of slender Lombardy poplars at the back, along the white wall, and in the middle two symmetrical, round-topped linden-trees." (001-007)

The garden serves a seasonal rhythm in [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s life. In spring, when "[[home-and-belonging|home]]sickness for other lands and the fret of things unaccomplished awoke, he [[work-and-vocation|work]]ed off his discontent here" (001-007). During the long prairie summers, when his wife and daughters escaped to Colorado, he stayed behind with his garden, bringing down his books and papers to [[work-and-vocation|work]] under the linden-trees — "breakfasted and lunched and had his tea in the garden" (001-007).

It was in this garden that [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] and [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] formed their deepest bond, sitting and talking "half through the warm, soft nights" (001-007). The garden thus becomes not only a private sanctuary but the site of the novel's most significant intellectual friendship.

The garden reappears at key moments throughout the narrative. In Book I, Chapter III, visitors admire its apple crop (003-030). By Chapter XVII, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] is spraying his rose-vines, and the garden has "never been so beautiful as it was that summer" (017-001), even as the [[family-and-domesticity|family]] gathers for tea there in the closing scenes (017-005), and [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] watches night fall [[solitude-and-creativity|alone]] in the garden (017-019).

## Character Connections

- **[[Professor Godfrey St. Peter]]**: The garden's creator and keeper. It is his refuge, his creative companion, and the physical expression of his European sensibilities. He built it in response to his wife's complaints about his absences (001-006).
- **[[Tom Outland]]**: Met [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] in the garden and spent long nights talking there. The garden is the birth[[home-and-belonging|place]] of their intellectual and emotional connection (001-007, 010-075).
- **[[Lillian St. Peter]]**: Her dissatisfaction with [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s habits indirectly led to the garden's creation. She prefers the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] and the social world it enables.
- **[[Augusta]]**: The sewing-woman who shares [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s study — the indoor counterp[[art-and-religion|art]] to the garden as a space of quiet, productive [[solitude-and-creativity|solitude]].

## Critical Context

The garden functions as a pastoral counterweight to the novel's other settings. Against the ugly, dismantled [[the-old-house|old house]] and the modern, somewhat sterile new [[home-and-belonging|house]], the garden represents a third way: a space that is cultivated but not commercial, private but not isolated, foreign but rooted. Cather uses the garden to explore a recurring tension in her [[work-and-vocation|work]] between European aesthetic values and American practicality. The fact that the garden is "French" and sits in [[hamilton|Hamilton]], Nebraska-like territory, mirrors [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s own dis[[home-and-belonging|place]]ment — a man of European scholarly sensibilities living in a Midwestern university town. Literary critics have noted the garden as a variation on the *hortus conclusus* (enclosed garden) motif, a medieval and Renaissance symbol of contemplative [[escape|withdrawal]] that Cather adapts to the American [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]].
