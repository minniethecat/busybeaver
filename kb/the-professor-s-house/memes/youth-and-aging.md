## Definition

Youth and aging form one of the central polarities in *The Professor's House*. The novel ch[[art-and-religion|art]]s [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]]'s psychological journey through middle age into a crisis that brings him face to face with [[death-and-mortality|mortality]]. Youth — embodied by [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]], [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s students, and his daughters' generation — exerts a magnetic pull on [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]], who is described as having a "misfortune" in that > > "he [[love-and-desire|love]]d [[childhood|youth]] — he was weak to it, it kindled him" (001-041).

> "he loved youth — he was weak to it, it kindled him" (001-041). The novel asks what it means to grow old in a world that prizes the new, and whether the wisdom of age can hold its own against the vitality of youth.

## In the Novel

The theme is established early. The Professor recalls spending "the happiest years of his [[childhood|youth]] in a [[home-and-belonging|house]] at Versailles" (001-002), linking his own [[childhood|youth]]ful past to European culture and refinement — a stark contrast to his present life in [[hamilton|Hamilton]]. This [[memory-and-the-past|nostalgia]] for lost [[childhood|youth]] is not mere sentimentality; it represents a longing for a self that was more open to beauty, adventure, and possibility.

[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] is the novel's supreme figure of [[childhood|youth]] — brilliant, self-taught, full of [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] and [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]. His early [[death-and-mortality|death]] freezes him in [[childhood|youth]] forever, transforming him from a person into a symbol. The Professor, by contrast, is aging in a way that feels like diminishment. Yet late in the novel, Cather offers a counter-movement: [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] experiences "a kind of second youth" (025-003), a renewal that comes not from external stimulation but from an inner acceptance of what his life has been.

The novel also explores aging through [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s relationship with his wife and daughters. His daughters' material [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]s and his wife's social aspirations represent a generational shift that [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] finds alienating. The [[the-old-house|old house]], with its cramped rooms and creaking stairs, becomes a metaphor for [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s aging body — inconvenient, unfashionable, but irre[[home-and-belonging|place]]ably his own.

In a key passage, Cather reflects on the regret of opportunities missed: "he had not spent his [[childhood|youth]] in the great dazzling Southwest" (025-005), linking the theme of [[childhood|youth]] to the novel's geographical imagination — the Southwest represents the adventure [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] discovered too late, through Tom's stories.

## Character Connections

- **[[Professor Godfrey St. Peter]]**: Caught between [[memory-and-the-past|nostalgia]] for his own [[childhood|youth]] and the gravitational pull of age. His "second youth" (025-003) is one of the novel's most hopeful notes.
- **[[Tom Outland]]**: Youth frozen in amber — dead before thirty, his brilliance and promise never dimmed by age or compromise.
- **[[Kathleen St. Peter]]**: The Professor's younger daughter, an [[art-and-religion|art]]ist whose sensitive studies of her father bridge the generational divide.
- **[[Rosamond St. Peter]]**: Represents [[childhood|youth]]'s turn toward [[money-and-materialism|materialism]], her inheritance from [[tom-outlands-invention|Tom's invention]] funding a lifestyle that her father finds vulgar.

## Critical Context

Cather wrote *The Professor's House* in her early fifties, at a point in her own life when questions of [[childhood|youth]], creativity, and aging were intensely personal. The novel's trip[[art-and-religion|art]]ite structure — with Tom's [[childhood|youth]]ful adventure at its center, framed by [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s midlife crisis — enacts the relationship between past vitality and present depletion. Critics have noted the [[power|influence]] of Henry James's late novels, p[[art-and-religion|art]]icularly *The Ambassadors*, in Cather's treatment of the aging protagonist who discovers too late what he has missed. The "second youth" theme connects to a broader modernist preoccupation with time, [[memory-and-the-past|memory]], and the fragmented self.
