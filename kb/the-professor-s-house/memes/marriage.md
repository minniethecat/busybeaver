## Definition

Marriage in The Professor's House is not a romantic ideal but a social fact — a structure that shapes lives, constrains choices, and reveals character. Cather examines [[love-and-desire|marriage]] not through the lens of passion but through its practical consequences: [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s long [[love-and-desire|marriage]] to [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] has become a habit of mutual accommodation; Kathleen's [[love-and-desire|marriage]] to McGregor brings financial strain; Rosamond's [[love-and-desire|marriage]] to [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] transforms her into a [[money-and-materialism|wealth]]y stranger. Marriage is the crucible in which the novel's deeper tensions — between [[love-and-desire|love]] and [[money-and-materialism|money]], [[childhood|youth]] and age, past and present — are heated to visibility.

## In the Novel

The Professor's own [[love-and-desire|marriage]] serves as the novel's baseline. He has lived in the [[the-old-house|old house]] since his wedding:

> "He had lived ever since his marriage, where he had worked out his career." (001-001)

The [[love-and-desire|marriage]] has produced three daughters and decades of domestic routine, but Cather suggests that its early promise has faded into something functional rather than vital. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]]'s social [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]s and [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s intellectual life have diverged:

> "Before his marriage, and for years afterward, Lillian had been the most stimulating companion." (003-025)

The daughters' [[love-and-desire|marriage]]s mark the novel's generational shift. Kathleen's [[love-and-desire|marriage]] to [[scott-mcgregor|Scott McGregor]] brings her closer to [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] in sympathy, while Rosamond's [[love-and-desire|marriage]] to [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] — and the [[outland|Outland]] fortune it brings — creates distance and estrangement:

> "Since Rosamond's marriage to Marsellus, both she and her mother had changed." (015-016)

The Professor reflects late in the novel on [[love-and-desire|marriage]] as one of life's binding structures, comparing it to other forms of necessity:

> "There was Lillian, there must be marriage and a salary." (026-006)

## Character Connections

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]** and **[[lillian-st-peter]]**: Their long [[love-and-desire|marriage]] is the novel's central domestic fact — stable, accommodating, but emotionally thinned by time and divergent values. The Professor built his career within it; [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] has outgrown it (001-001, 003-025).

- **[[rosamond-marsellus]]** and **[[louie-marsellus]]**: The [[love-and-desire|marriage]] that channels the [[outland|Outland]] fortune. Rosamond's [[loss-and-change|transformation]] through this [[love-and-desire|marriage]] is the novel's most visible example of how [[love-and-desire|marriage]] can alter identity (015-016).

- **[[kathleen-mcgregor]]** and [[scott-mcgregor|Scott McGregor]]: The "other" daughter's [[love-and-desire|marriage]], marked by financial modesty and maintained connection with [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] (004-036).

- **[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]** and Rosamond: Their implied engagement represents the [[love-and-desire|marriage]] that never happened — the alternative path, cut short by Tom's [[death-and-mortality|death]] in the war.

## Critical Context

Critics have noted that Cather's treatment of [[love-and-desire|marriage]] in The Professor's House reflects her broader skepticism about the institution. Cather herself never married, and her fiction often portrays [[love-and-desire|marriage]] as a site of compromise rather than fulfillment. The novel's [[love-and-desire|marriage]]s are transactional in various ways: [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s [[love-and-desire|marriage]] enabled his career; Rosamond's [[love-and-desire|marriage]] converts Tom's [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]] into social status; even the implied [[love-and-desire|marriage]] of Tom and Rosamond would have been shaped by the [[science-and-discovery|invention]]'s fortune. This treatment aligns with Cather's larger theme of commercialization, where even the most intimate relationships are reshaped by [[money-and-materialism|money]].
