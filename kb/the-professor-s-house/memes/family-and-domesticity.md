## Definition

Family in *The Professor's House* is a complex institution — neither demonized nor idealized. The [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] family — Professor Godfrey, his wife [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]], and their daughters Kathleen and Rosamond — is portrayed with Cather's characteristic unsentimentality. They [[love-and-desire|love]] each other, but the [[love-and-desire|love]] is complicated by divergent [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]s, unspoken resentments, and the gravitational pull of [[money-and-materialism|money]] after [[death-of-tom-outland|Tom Outland's death]]. The family is the novel's primary social unit, and Cather uses it to explore larger questions: What do we owe our families? What happens when family members become strangers to each other? Can the bonds of blood survive differences in values?

## In the Novel

The novel opens with the family in transition — literally moving from the [[the-old-house|old house]] to the new one. This physical relocation is also a symbolic one: the family is moving from the world [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] built into a world shaped by [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s [[money-and-materialism|money]]. The Professor's decision to stay behind in the [[the-old-house|old house]] is the first sign of his estrangement from the family's trajectory.

Cather renders the family dynamics through accumulated detail rather than dramatic confrontation. We learn that [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] has grown weary of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s [[scholarship-and-knowledge|academic life]] (006-015) and embraces the social opportunities the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] provides. Kathleen, the younger daughter, is the family member most sympathetic to her father — an [[art-and-religion|art]]ist who understands creative [[solitude-and-creativity|solitude]]. Rosamond, the elder, has married [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] and embraced a life of [[money-and-materialism|wealth]] and display that her father finds baffling and distasteful.

Money is the solvent that dissolves the family's old equilibrium. [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]'s commercial [[ambition-and-success|success]] with [[tom-outlands-invention|Tom's invention]] creates new tensions — between the daughters, between [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] and his wife, between the old life and the new. The family dinner scenes in the novel are masterpieces of suppressed conflict, where what is not said matters more than what is.

> "It had been a mistake, our having a family and writing histories and getting on in the world" (008-017).

The Professor's [[love-and-desire|love]] for his daughters is real (001-040) but insufficient to bridge the gap between his values and theirs. His relationship with [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] has settled into [[love-and-desire|affection]]ate distance. The novel suggests that family, for all its claims, may not be the deepest form of human connection — that friendship, [[friendship|intellectual companionship]], and solitary [[work-and-vocation|work]] may matter more.

## Character Connections

- **[[Professor Godfrey St. Peter]]**: The family's centrifugal force — he [[love-and-desire|love]]s them but is moving away from them, toward [[solitude-and-creativity|solitude]] and [[memory-and-the-past|memory]].
- **[[Lillian St. Peter]]**: The Professor's wife, whose social [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]s and cultural aspirations pull her away from her husband's world.
- **[[Kathleen St. Peter]]**: The younger daughter, an [[art-and-religion|art]]ist who understands her father better than anyone else in the family — but even she cannot bridge the growing distance.
- **[[Rosamond St. Peter]]**: The elder daughter, whose inheritance from Tom and [[love-and-desire|marriage]] to [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] represent everything her father distrusts about the new commercial world.
- **[[Louie Marsellus]]**: The son-in-law who [[love-and-desire|love]]s Rosamond genuinely and tries to win [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s [[love-and-desire|affection]], but whose values are fundamentally alien to the older man.

## Critical Context

Cather's treatment of family has been read biographically — she was close to her own family but never married or had children — and in the context of modernist fiction's broader critique of domesticity. Unlike the Victorian novel, which often culminated in [[love-and-desire|marriage]] and family formation, modernist fiction frequently portrayed [[love-and-desire|marriage]] and family as sites of constraint and disappointment. Cather's innovation is to treat this material without bitterness: the [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] family is not a failed family but a real one, whose members [[love-and-desire|love]] each other imperfectly and incompletely. The novel's refusal to resolve family tensions — [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] does not reconcile with his wife, the daughters do not come to understand their father — is one of its most honest features.
