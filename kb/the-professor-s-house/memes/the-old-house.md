## Description

The old house is the novel's opening image and its most potent symbol. It is "almost as ugly as it is possible for a [[home-and-belonging|house]] to be; square, three stories in height, painted the colour of ashes — the front porch just too narrow for comfort, with a slanting floor and sagging steps" (001-001). Despite — or because of — its ugliness, the [[home-and-belonging|house]] contains [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s entire adult life: his [[love-and-desire|marriage]], his daughters' childhoods, his decades of [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]]. Its dismantling at the novel's opening is the physical enactment of the dissolution [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] feels within.

## In the Novel

The [[home-and-belonging|house]] appears primarily in Book I, where its emptying is the novel's central action, and briefly in Book III, where [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s continued presence in the nearly empty structure becomes the setting for his near-[[death-and-mortality|death]]. The [[home-and-belonging|house]]'s [[nature-and-landscape|geography]] is carefully mapped: the garden at the back, the sewing-room on the third floor where [[augusta|Augusta]] [[work-and-vocation|work]]s, and the attic study under the mansard roof — "the sole opening for light and air" (001-009) — where [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] has done his real [[work-and-vocation|work]] for decades.

The [[home-and-belonging|house]] is contrasted throughout with the new [[home-and-belonging|house]], which is modern, comfortable, and built with [[money-and-materialism|money]] from [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s [[science-and-discovery|invention]]. The Professor's refusal to leave the old house — his insistence on "staying on until I finish a piece of writing" (001-020) — is the novel's first declaration of his resistance to the world the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] represents.

> "The moving was over and done. Professor St. Peter was alone in the dismantled house where he had lived ever since his marriage, where he had worked out his career and brought up his two daughters." (001-001)

## Significance

The old house is more than a setting — it is a character in the novel's moral drama. Its ugliness, its inconvenience, its creaking stairs and dripping taps are also its [[truth|authenticity]]. The new [[home-and-belonging|house]], by contrast, is beautiful and alien — a [[home-and-belonging|house]] that has never been lived in and may never truly be. The old house's fate — to be emptied, and eventually, we assume, demolished — is the fate of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s world.
