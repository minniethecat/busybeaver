# Spanish Adventurers in North America

**_Spanish Adventurers in North America_** is the life's [[work-and-vocation|work]] of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter]] — an eight-volume narrative history of early Spanish exploration in the American Southwest, fifteen years in the writing, which earned its author the Oxford prize for history and the five thousand pounds that built the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] into which he refused to move. More than a scholarly [[ambition-and-success|achievement]], the _Spanish Adventurers_ is the novel's central symbol of the intellectual life: its lonely rigours, its transformative friendships, and its uneasy relationship with [[family-and-domesticity|domesticity]], [[money-and-materialism|money]], and institutional recognition. The [[work-and-vocation|work]] is inextricably bound up with [[tom-outland]], whose southwestern upbringing and intimate [[scholarship-and-knowledge|knowledge]] of the [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]] transformed the final four volumes and gave [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] what he called his "[[youth-and-aging|second youth]]" (025-003).

## Composition in the Sewing-Room

The _Spanish Adventurers_ was produced under conditions of deliberate austerity. [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s study was an attic sewing-room on the third floor of [[the-old-house|the old house]] — cramped, poorly heated, and shared for six weeks each year with [[augusta]], the [[family-and-domesticity|family]]'s sewing-woman. The room had no filing cabinets, no adequate stove, and a single bulb that tormented the eyes. Yet it was here that the entire eight-volume [[work-and-vocation|work]] was conceived, [[scholarship-and-knowledge|research]]ed, and written:

> During the fifteen years he had been working on his _Spanish Adventurers in North America_, this room had been his centre of operations. There had been delightful excursions and digressions; the two Sabbatical years when he was in Spain studying records, two summers in the Southwest on the trail of his adventurers, another in Old Mexico, dashes to France to see his foster-brothers. But the notes and the records and the ideas always came back to this room. It was here they were digested and sorted, and woven into their proper place in his history. (001-037)

The room was also the [[home-and-belonging|place]] of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s [[solitude-and-creativity|isolation]] from the "eng[[youth-and-aging|aging]] drama of [[family-and-domesticity|domestic life]]" (001-038). By [[work-and-vocation|work]]ing there — rather than in the proper downstairs study with its roomy shelves — [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] created a [[solitude-and-creativity|boundary between his intellectual and domestic selves]], one he maintained for a decade and a half. The conditions were a deliberate trade-off: discomfort in ex[[loss-and-change|change]] for freedom from interruption.

## Critical Reception and the Arc of Recognition

The critical trajectory of the _Spanish Adventurers_ mirrors [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s own relationship to worldly validation — a progression from indifference through obscure recognition to belated fame, all of it received with the same ironic detachment:

| Volumes | Period | Critical Response |
|---------|--------|-------------------|
| Volumes 1–3 | Early years | "Timidly reviewed by other professors of history, in technical and educational journals. Nobody saw that he was trying to do something quite different — they merely thought he was trying to do the usual thing, and had not succeeded very well. They recommended to him the more even and genial style of John Fiske." (001-047) |
| Volume 4 | Transition | A few young men in the United States and England "were intensely interested in his [[science-and-discovery|experiment]]" (001-048) |
| Volumes 5–6 | Breakthrough | Interest began to be expressed "in lectures and in print" (001-048) |
| Volumes 7–8 | Triumph | Brought "a certain international reputation and what were called rewards — among them, the Oxford prize for history, with its five thousand pounds" (001-048) |

[[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s own response to this arc is telling. During the early years of neglect, he "hadn't, he could honestly say, cared a whoop" — because "when the whole plan of his narrative was coming clearer and clearer all the time, when he could feel his hand growing easier with his material, when all the foolish conventions about that kind of writing were falling away and his relation with his [[work-and-vocation|work]] was becoming every day more simple, natural, and happy — he cared as little as the Spanish Adventurers themselves what Professor So-and-So thought about them" (001-048). The [[work-and-vocation|work]] was its own reward — a point he underscored years later when his wife asked if there was something he would rather have done with the Oxford prize [[money-and-materialism|money]] than build the new [[home-and-belonging|house]]: "If with that cheque I could have bought back the fun I had writing my history, you'd never have got your [[home-and-belonging|house]]. But one couldn't get that for twenty thousand dollars. The great pleasures don't come so cheap" (001-050).

## Tom Outland's Transformative Influence

The most significant event in the composition of the _Spanish Adventurers_ was not a [[scholarship-and-knowledge|research]] trip or an archival [[science-and-discovery|discovery]], but the arrival of [[tom-outland]] in [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s life. Tom came to [[hamilton|Hamilton]] because he had read an [[art-and-religion|art]]icle [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] wrote for a popular magazine about Fray Marcos — "the only thing with any truth in it he'd read about our country down there" (010-029), according to [[father-duchene|Father Duchene]]. What began as a pupil-master relationship deepened into an intellectual p[[art-and-religion|art]]nership that fundamentally reshaped the history:

> If the last four volumes of _The Spanish Adventurers_ were more simple and inevitable than those that went before, it was largely because of Outland. When St. Peter first began his work, he realized that his great drawback was the lack of early association, the fact that he had not spent his youth in the great dazzling Southwest country which was the scene of his explorers' adventures. By the time he had got as far as the third volume, into his house walked a boy who had grown up there, a boy with imagination, with the training and insight resulting from a very curious experience; who had in his pocket the secrets which old trails and stones and watercourses tell only to adolescence. (025-005)

Tom's [[scholarship-and-knowledge|knowledge]] of the terrain was so intimate that he could read the [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]] against the archival record with uncanny precision:

> Two years after Tom's graduation they took the copy of Fray Garces' manuscript that the Professor had made from the original in Spain, and went down into the Southwest together. By autumn they had been over every mile of his trail on horseback. Tom could take a sentence from Garces' diary and find the exact spot at which the missionary crossed the Rio Colorado on a certain Sunday in 1775. Given one pueblo, he could always find the route by which the priest had reached the next. (025-006)

This col[[work-and-vocation|labor]]ation — the fusion of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s European archival [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]] with Tom's embodied [[scholarship-and-knowledge|knowledge]] of the [[nature-and-landscape|southwestern landscape]] — produced what [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] regarded as the finest volumes of his career. Through Tom, he "had been able to experience afresh things that had grown dull with use" (025-004). The relationship was, in [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s reckoning, one of "two romances: one of the he[[art-and-religion|art]], which had filled his life for many years, and a second of the mind — of the imagination" (025-003).

## The Work and Its Costs

The _Spanish Adventurers_ was produced at a cost — not to the author [[solitude-and-creativity|alone]], but to those around him. [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s wife [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] grew jealous of Tom precisely because the younger man could p[[art-and-religion|art]]icipate in the intellectual life of the history in a way she could not (017-004). The Professor's own daughters, [[rosamond-marsellus]] and [[kathleen-mcgregor]], inhabited a world of furs, [[home-and-belonging|house]]s, and social rivalries that seemed, from the perspective of the sewing-room study, remote and faintly absurd.

The [[work-and-vocation|work]]'s completion left [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] curiously emptied. With the horse stolen and the stable door unlocked — the filing cabinets he never bought that now "he really didn't need" (001-047) — he faced the strange aftermath of a great endeavour finished. His refusal to move into the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] built with the Oxford prize [[money-and-materialism|money]] is the novel's most pointed symbol of the disjunction between [[scholarship-and-knowledge|intellectual creation]] and [[money-and-materialism|material reward]]. The [[home-and-belonging|house]], like the prize itself, was something the world gave him for [[work-and-vocation|work]] whose real value was entirely private.

## Historical Scope and Sources

The _Spanish Adventurers_ draws on archival [[scholarship-and-knowledge|research]] conducted in [[spain|Spain]] during [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s two sabbatical years, supplemented by field[[work-and-vocation|work]] in the American Southwest and Old Mexico. Its central figures include:

- **Fray Marcos de Niza** — the Franciscan missionary whose expedition into the Southwest in 1539 first brought European notice to the region. [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s magazine [[art-and-religion|art]]icle about Fray Marcos was what drew [[tom-outland]] to him (010-029)
- **Fray Francisco Garces** — whose manuscript diary [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] copied from the original in [[spain|Spain]], and whose route across the Rio Colorado Tom was able to reconstruct on the ground with st[[art-and-religion|art]]ling accuracy (025-006)
- The broader narrative of Spanish exploration from the sixteenth through eighteenth centuries, which [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] sought to render not as conventional academic history but as something "quite different" — a more "simple, natural, and happy" mode of historical narrative (001-048)

The [[work-and-vocation|work]]'s originality lay not in its archival discoveries but in its narrative method — an approach to historical writing that [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] felt had been misunderstood by his early reviewers, who "recommended to him the more even and genial style of John Fiske" (001-047). What [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] was attempting was something closer to the lived texture of the explorers' own experience, a quality he could only fully achieve once he had [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s firsthand [[scholarship-and-knowledge|knowledge]] of the southwestern terrain to draw upon.
