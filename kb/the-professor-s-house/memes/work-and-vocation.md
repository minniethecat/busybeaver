## Definition

Work, in *The Professor's House*, is a moral category. The novel distinguishes sharply between work done as a vocation — for [[love-and-desire|love]], for truth, for the satisfaction of doing something well — and work done for [[money-and-materialism|money]] or status. [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]]'s [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]] on the Spanish adventurers is the novel's supreme example of vocational work: slow, demanding, largely unrewarded by the world, and utterly absorbing. [[augusta|Augusta]]'s sewing is another form of vocational labor — careful, skilled, faithful. Against these models, the novel sets [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]]'s commercial activity, which is energetic and [[ambition-and-success|success]]ful but lacks the [[pride|dignity]] of work done for its own sake.

## In the Novel

The Professor's work defines him. "He had worked out his career" (001-001) in the [[the-old-house|old house]] — the verb "worked out" suggesting both effort and completion, as if his career were a problem to be solved or a sculpture to be shaped. His study, the cramped attic room, is a workshop, not an office. The distinction matters: [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] is a craftsman of [[scholarship-and-knowledge|knowledge]], not a functionary.

Cather is attentive to the material conditions of work. The Professor writes by a kerosene lamp because he prefers its light. He sits in a "cane-backed office chair that turned on a screw" (001-010). These details are not incidental — they establish that serious work requires specific conditions, and that those conditions are fragile, easily disrupted by a move, a renovation, or a [[family-and-domesticity|family]]'s changing needs.

[[augusta|Augusta]]'s work offers a counterpoint. Her sewing — the "forms," the patterns, the careful fitting — is presented with respect and even tenderness. She is a craftswoman, and her presence in the study is appropriate because her work, like [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s, requires concentration and skill. Their quiet coexistence in the attic room is one of the novel's most beautiful images of shared vocation.

[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s work — his archaeological documentation on the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]], his laboratory [[science-and-discovery|experiment]]s — represents a third model: the work of [[science-and-discovery|discovery]], driven by curiosity rather than utility. Tom "had never had any mathematics or [[science-and-discovery|science]]" (010-027) before his formal education, but his natural gift for observation and his patience make him, in [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s eyes, a truer scholar than many professionals.

> "He had a deft hand with tools, he could easily have fixed them, but there were always so many things to fix, and there was not time enough to go round" (001-001).

## Character Connections

- **[[Professor Godfrey St. Peter]]**: The scholar-as-craftsman, for whom work is a calling that defines his identity and organizes his life.
- **[[Augusta]]**: The seamstress whose skilled labor is presented as a form of devotion — faithful, careful, irreducible to its economic value.
- **[[Tom Outland]]**: The natural-born [[scholarship-and-knowledge|research]]er, whose work — both archaeological and scientific — is driven by pure curiosity.
- **[[Louie Marsellus]]**: The businessman whose work is energetic and [[ambition-and-success|success]]ful, but whose values the novel treats as fundamentally incompatible with vocational labor.

## Critical Context

Cather's treatment of work reflects both her own experience as a professional writer and broader cultural debates about vocation in the early twentieth century. The concept of "vocation" — work as a calling, with spiritual dimensions — was under pressure from industrialization, professionalization, and the growth of corporate capitalism. The Professor's resistance to these forces, his insistence on the value of work done slowly and for its own sake, aligns him with a tradition of craft resistance to industrial modernity that runs from John Ruskin through William Morris to the Arts and Crafts movement. Critics have also noted the gendered dimension of work in the novel: [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s scholarly labor, [[augusta|Augusta]]'s domestic craft, and [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]'s commercial activity map onto different configurations of gender and value.
