## Definition

Home in *The Professor's House* is a deeply contested concept. The novel opens with a [[family-and-domesticity|family]] moving out of one house and into another, and [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s refusal to join them — his insistence on remaining in the dismantled [[the-old-house|old house]] — establishes the central problem: what does it mean to be at home, and what happens when the physical spaces that have contained a life are taken away? Cather treats home not as a given but as something achieved through [[work-and-vocation|labor]], [[memory-and-the-past|memory]], and [[love-and-desire|love]] — and therefore as something that can be lost. The Professor's homelessness at the novel's end, adrift in a world he no longer feels p[[art-and-religion|art]] of, is the culmination of the novel's exploration of belonging.

## In the Novel

The novel's opening pages are an inventory of a house being emptied. The Professor walks through rooms "where he had lived ever since his [[love-and-desire|marriage]], where he had [[work-and-vocation|work]]ed out his career and brought up his two daughters" (001-001). The house is ugly — "almost as ugly as it is possible for a house to be" — but it is saturated with meaning. Every creaking stair, every dripping tap, is a [[memory-and-the-past|memory]]. The new house, by contrast, is modern, convenient, and emotionally blank — a house that has never been lived in, built with [[money-and-materialism|money]] [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] distrusts.

The garden is [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s true home — more than the house itself. He built it, shaped it, and tended it for twenty years. Unlike the house, which belongs to his landlord, the garden is his creation. The "homesickness for other lands" (001-007) that drives him to [[work-and-vocation|work]] in the garden each spring suggests that home, for [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]], is always elsewhere — in [[france|France]], in [[memory-and-the-past|the past]], in the lost world of his [[childhood|youth]].

The novel also explores home through [[outland|Tom Outland's story]]. Tom's [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] of the cliff-dweller ruins — someone else's ancient home — is also a [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] of his own rootlessness. He has no [[family-and-domesticity|family]], no place, no history. The [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] becomes his home, but only temporarily; like [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s [[the-old-house|old house]], it is a home that cannot be kept.

The Professor's final crisis — his near-[[death-and-mortality|death]] in [[tom-outlands-invention|the gas]]-filled study — can be read as the ultimate expression of his homelessness. He has no place in the new house, his [[the-old-house|old house]] is being dismantled, his [[family-and-domesticity|family]] has moved on, and even his body seems to have lost its will to occupy space in the world.

> "He had spent the happiest years of his youth in a house at Versailles where it distinctly was not [the most attractive room]" (001-002).

## Character Connections

- **[[Professor Godfrey St. Peter]]**: A man without a home, despite having two houses. His true home is intellectual — his [[work-and-vocation|work]], his garden, his [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] of Tom.
- **[[Lillian St. Peter]]**: Embraces the new house as a fresh st[[art-and-religion|art]], a declaration of social arrival. Her relationship to home is aspirational; [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s is memorial.
- **[[Tom Outland]]**: The novel's other homeless figure — an orphan, a wanderer, who briefly finds a home on the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] and in [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s friendship.
- **[[Augusta]]**: The figure who, paradoxically, makes the [[the-old-house|old house]] feel like home. Her practical presence, her sewing forms, and her quiet competence anchor the study as a lived space.

## Critical Context

Cather's treatment of home draws on a rich American literary tradition — from Thoreau's cabin to the houses of Hawthorne and James — in which domestic spaces are never merely domestic. The dismantled house is a figure for the dissolution of the nineteenth-century [[family-and-domesticity|family]] and the values it sheltered. The Professor's preference for the ugly [[the-old-house|old house]] over the beautiful new one inverts the expected American narrative of progress and improvement. Critics have also noted the [[power|influence]] of Cather's own displacement — she left Nebraska for the East Coast as a young woman and never returned to live — on the novel's pervasive sense of homesickness.
