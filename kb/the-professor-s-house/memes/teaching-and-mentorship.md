## Definition

Teaching in The Professor's House is a double-edged [[work-and-vocation|calling]]. For [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor Godfrey St. Peter]], it is both a burden — the drudgery of lectures and faculty meetings — and the rare channel through which genuine intellectual transmission occurs. The novel draws a sharp distinction between the institutional apparatus of education (the expanding university, the indifferent student body, the administrative fatigue) and the singular electric relationship of true mentorship: [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s bond with [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]. In this one student, teaching justifies an entire career.

## In the Novel

The Professor's [[academic-life|classroom]] persona is formidable but distant — his students nickname him "Mephistopheles" for his sardonic eyebrows:

> "His students call him Mephistopheles — and there was no evading the searching eyes underneath." (001-003)

Yet the routine of teaching wears on him. He pours himself out to hundreds while receiving little in return:

> "He was wearing himself out to hundreds of students in lectures and consultations." (001-040)

Against this backdrop of institutional fatigue, [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] arrives as the exception that redeems the rule:

> "In a lifetime of teaching, I've encountered just one student — Tom Outland — and he had foretold." (001-042)

The mentorship extends beyond the [[academic-life|classroom]]. The Professor's daughters teach Tom things he needs, and Tom in turn teaches [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] — about the Southwest, about [[science-and-discovery|discovery]], about living with intensity:

> "His daughters were teaching Tom things that he needed to know." (010-077)

Tom's [[death-and-mortality|death]] leaves [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] without his one true student, making the remaining decades of teaching feel like aftermath.

## Character Connections

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]**: Teaching is his life's [[work-and-vocation|work]], but only one student — [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] — made the [[work-and-vocation|work]] worth doing. His pride as a teacher is fulfilled and then extinguished in that single relationship (001-042, 013-031).

- **[[tom-outland]]**: The ideal student — brilliant, self-directed, capable of teaching his teacher in return. His [[death-and-mortality|death]] in [[world-war-i|the Great War]] severs the mentorship at its peak.

- **[[lillian-st-peter]]**: Resents the financial constraints that teaching imposes. "There was [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]], there must be [[love-and-desire|marriage]] and a salary" — teaching as economic necessity rather than [[work-and-vocation|vocation]].

- **[[kathleen-mcgregor]]**: The daughter who most identifies with her father's values, and who herself teaches — a continuation of the [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] pedagogical line.

## Critical Context

Cather's portrayal of [[scholarship-and-knowledge|academic life]] draws on her own experience. The novel was written in 1925, during a period of rapid expansion in American higher education. The Professor's complaint about the declining quality of students — "We have hosts of students, but they're a common sort" (003-047) — reflects contemporaneous anxieties about the democratization of university education. Critics read the [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] relationship as Cather's elegy for a pre-modern model of mentorship: intimate, transformative, impossible to replicate in the mass university.
