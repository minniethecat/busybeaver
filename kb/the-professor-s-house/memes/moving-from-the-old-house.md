The move from the [[the-old-house|old house]] is the frame event of the entire novel.
[[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]] and [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] have spent more than two decades raising [[rosamond-marsellus|Rosamond]] and [[kathleen-mcgregor|Kathleen]] in a rented [[home-and-belonging|house]] that was, by any objective standard, an awkward and inconvenient [[home-and-belonging|dwelling]].

The new [[home-and-belonging|house]] is modern, handsome, and built with [[money-and-materialism|money]] from [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s aviation engine.
Yet [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] cannot bring himself to complete the transition, refusing to leave his attic study in the emptied [[the-old-house|old house]].
This refusal forms the novel's central dramatic question: what does it mean to outlive the life one has built?

## 背景

The [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] [[family-and-domesticity|family]] inhabited the [[the-old-house|old house]] for over twenty years.
It was rented from [[appelhoff|Appelhoff]], a retired German farmer, and [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] never bought a [[home-and-belonging|house]] of his own.
The [[home-and-belonging|house]] was "almost as ugly as it is possible for a [[home-and-belonging|house]] to be; square, three stories in height, painted the colour of ashes" (001-001).

Its every flaw had become intimate: "Certain wobbly stair treads, certain creaky boards in the upstairs hall, had made him wince many times a day for twenty-odd years — and they still creaked and wobbled" (001-002).
Yet this was the [[home-and-belonging|place]] where [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] wrote his magnum opus, *[[spanish-adventurers-in-north-america|Spanish Adventurers in North America]]*, and raised two daughters.

The catalyst was [[money-and-materialism|money]] from [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s [[science-and-discovery|invention]].
After Tom's [[death-and-mortality|death]] in [[world-war-i]], [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] commercialized the [[outland|Outland]] engine and generated substantial [[money-and-materialism|wealth]].
The Professor wryly observed that [[money-and-materialism|money]] could not buy what the [[the-old-house|old house]] had given him:

> If with that cheque I could have bought back the fun I had writing my history, you'd never have got your house.
> But one couldn't get that for twenty thousand dollars.
> The great pleasures don't come so cheap. (001-040)

## 经过

The novel opens with the move nearly complete.
On a September morning, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] walks through empty echoing rooms: "The moving was over and done. [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]] was [[solitude-and-creativity|alone]] in [[the-old-house|the dismantled house]] where he had lived ever since his [[love-and-desire|marriage]], where he had [[work-and-vocation|work]]ed out his career and brought up his two daughters" (001-001).

Yet the move is not finished.
When [[augusta|Augusta]], the sewing-woman, arrives to collect her dress forms, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] blocks the door:

> "No, I won't! Not at all.
> You don't need her to make curtains.
> I can't have this room changed if I'm going to work here.
> He can take the sewing-machine — yes.
> But put her back on the chest where she belongs, please.
> She does very well there." (001-030)

He negotiates with [[appelhoff|Appelhoff]] to retain the empty [[home-and-belonging|house]] for a full year's rent.
He tells his [[family-and-domesticity|family]] he needs the study to finish his writing — but the deeper truth is that he cannot leave.
Every morning he wakes wishing "that he could be transported on his mattress from the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] to the old" (003-001).

The move stretches across the entire span of Book I and into Book III.
Even as the [[family-and-domesticity|family]] settles into new routines, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] remains anchored in [[the-old-house|the dismantled house]], his true [[home-and-belonging|home]] reduced to a single attic room, a gas stove, and a desk he cannot abandon.

## 后果

The move's deepest consequence is [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s estrangement — from [[family-and-domesticity|family]], from [[work-and-vocation|work]], and ultimately from life itself.
The [[the-old-house|old house]] had been the vessel of his creative identity, providing "[[solitude-and-creativity|isolation]], insulation from the eng[[youth-and-aging|aging]] drama of [[family-and-domesticity|domestic life]]" (001-038).
The new [[home-and-belonging|house]] offers no substitute for the worn matting and yellowed wallpaper of the attic study.

Rosamond's offer to build him a study at the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] is gently refused:

> Much easier than to break in a new room, Rosie.
> A workroom should be like an old shoe; no matter how shabby, it's better than a new one. (004-007)

For [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]], [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s attachment to the [[the-old-house|old house]] becomes a source of growing exasperation.
By the novel's end, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s near-[[death-and-mortality|death]] in [[tom-outlands-invention|the gas]]-filled study — saved by [[augusta|Augusta]] — expresses the move's spiritual cost.
A man who cannot live in the present, who can only dwell in [[memory-and-the-past|the past]], risks ceasing to live at all.

## 史料与考辨

Critics read the [[the-old-house|old house]] as a figure for [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s psyche — its dismantling a physical enactment of his internal dissolution.
Cather's own dis[[home-and-belonging|place]]ment from Nebraska and her ambivalence about modern commerce inform the novel's anxiety about what is lost when old things are abandoned for new.

The [[home-and-belonging|house]]'s ugliness speaks to a central Cather conviction.
Authenticity is rarely beautiful by conventional standards.
What is genuinely lived-in acquires value that [[money-and-materialism|money]] and taste cannot replicate.

A parallel exists with [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s attachment to the cliff-dweller ruins on the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]].
Both men find meaning by [[home-and-belonging|dwelling]] with the remnants of a lost world.
