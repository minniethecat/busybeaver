## Definition

Friendship in *The Professor's House* is explored primarily through the relationship between [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor Godfrey St. Peter]] and his former student [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]. This friendship transcends the ordinary — it is an intellectual companionship forged in shared curiosity, long conversations in the garden, and mutual respect. Cather contrasts this deep, formative bond with the more transactional and socially conventional relationships that surround [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] in his [[family-and-domesticity|family]] and [[academic-life|university life]]. Friendship, in the novel's moral economy, ranks above [[love-and-desire|romantic love]], financial obligation, and even familial duty.

## In the Novel

The Professor's friendship with [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] is introduced retrospectively; by the time the novel begins, Tom is already dead, killed in [[world-war-i|the Great War]]. Yet his presence pervades the narrative. The Professor speaks of it in absolute terms:

> "my friendship with Outland is the one thing I put any value on" (004-023). This declaration, made in the context of refusing a financial offer from Tom's estate, establishes friendship as the novel's highest relational value.

The friendship between [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] and Tom was unusual in its origins and nature. Tom, a self-taught young man from the Southwest, walked into [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s garden one day and introduced himself (010-075). Their bond was immediate and deep — they would talk "half through the warm, soft nights" in the garden (001-007). Unlike [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s relationships with colleagues or even [[family-and-domesticity|family]], this friendship was free of calculation or performance.

Cather also explores friendship's fragility and its demands. [[dr-crane|Dr. Crane]]'s "long friendship and helpfulness to Tom" (012-025) represents a lesser, more complicated form of companionship, one that becomes entangled with financial claims after Tom's [[death-and-mortality|death]]. The Professor reflects that his "friendship with Crane had been a strange one" (012-029) — a relationship that, in the end, cannot withstand the pressure of [[money-and-materialism|money]].

In Book III, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]], isolated and depressed, recalls his "dream of self-sacrificing friendship and disinterested [[love-and-desire|love]]" (017-003) as something that has been betrayed by the commercialization of Tom's [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]]. Yet the [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] of the friendship itself remains his sustaining ideal. [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s own words echo across the volumes: "Anyone who requites faith and friendship as I did, will have to pay for it" (024-009).

## Character Connections

- **[[Professor Godfrey St. Peter]]**: For whom friendship with Tom is the "one thing" of value. His capacity for deep friendship is his greatest strength and, after Tom's [[death-and-mortality|death]], his deepest wound.
- **[[Tom Outland]]**: The young inventor and explorer whose friendship transforms [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s life. His posthumous [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]] — both financial and emotional — drives much of the novel's conflict.
- **[[Dr. Crane]]**: A colleague whose friendship with Tom becomes entangled with financial claims, illustrating how [[money-and-materialism|money]] corrupts even the appearance of disinterested companionship.
- **[[Rosamond St. Peter]]**: Tom's former fiancée and [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s daughter. Her [[love-and-desire|marriage]] to [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] and her embrace of the [[money-and-materialism|wealth]] from [[tom-outlands-invention|Tom's invention]] represent a betrayal of the friendship's purity in her father's eyes.

## Critical Context

Cather's treatment of friendship belongs to a long literary tradition of idealized male friendship, from classical models (Achilles and Patroclus) through the nineteenth-century romantic friendship. The Professor-Tom relationship has been read by some critics as homoerotic, while others argue Cather deliberately [[home-and-belonging|place]]s it above sexual categorization — it is an intellectual and spiritual bond that transcends the physical. The novel's structure reinforces this: Tom's story occupies the central Book II, literally framed by [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s life in Books I and III, so that friendship becomes the structural as well as thematic he[[art-and-religion|art]] of the [[work-and-vocation|work]].
