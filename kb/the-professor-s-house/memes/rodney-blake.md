## Role in the Novel

Rodney Blake appears exclusively in Book II ([[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s Story), where he is [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s p[[art-and-religion|art]]ner, friend, and co-discoverer of the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] cliff-[[home-and-belonging|dwelling]]s. His narrative trajectory is one of the novel's most poignant arcs: from loyal companion and skilled engineer to a figure of moral complexity whose decision to sell the Native American [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts creates an irreparable rift with Tom. Blake's subsequent disappearance — he goes to England to enlist in the Foreign Legion — leaves his fate unresolved, and the disputed bank account he left behind becomes a plot thread that connects Book II to the main narrative.

[[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]] "used to say that Rodney Blake might turn up in the Foreign Legion" (011-022), suggesting that even years later, the mystery of Blake's whereabouts lingers in the minds of those who knew his story.

## Character Portrait

Blake is introduced as a self-taught engineer with practical skills that complement Tom's more scholarly bent. He "had come in from his engineering [[work-and-vocation|work]] on a branch railroad" (018-003) and brought with him the technical [[scholarship-and-knowledge|knowledge]] needed to excavate and document the cliff-[[home-and-belonging|dwelling]]s. Unlike Tom, whose instinct is to preserve and study, Blake sees the [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts as a means to a practical end — a tension that drives the central conflict of Book II.

Cather portrays Blake not as a villain but as a product of his circumstances. He is a [[work-and-vocation|work]]ing man with no formal education, and his decision to sell the [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts is framed as pragmatic rather than mercenary:

> "...but none toward me. There is Rodney Blake, of course, if he should ever..." (004-026)

## Key Relationships

- **[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]** — Best friend and p[[art-and-religion|art]]ner in the [[discovery-of-cliff-dweller-ruins|Blue Mesa discovery]]. Their friendship is forged in shared [[work-and-vocation|labor]] and [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] but tested by fundamentally different visions of what the [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts represent. Blake sees a path to a new life; Tom sees a sacred trust.
- **[[father-duchene|Father Duchene]]** — The missionary priest who "warmly agreed with Blake" (022-012) on certain archaeological interpretations. Their relationship suggests Blake's receptiveness to ecclesiastical [[power|authority]] in ways Tom is not.

## Critical Significance

Blake embodies the tension between material necessity and cultural [[preservation-and-legacy|preservation]] that runs through the novel. His decision to sell the [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts — and Tom's refusal to forgive him for it — dramatizes a question that has no easy answer: what obligations do discoverers have toward the objects they find? Blake's perspective is never entirely dismissed by Cather, making him more than a simple foil to Tom's idealism.

The mystery of Blake's disappearance and the "bank account in New Mexico" (017-004) extends his significance beyond Book II. The disputed [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]] that [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] later commercializes originates with Blake — making him an absent presence whose actions ripple through the novel's present-day narrative.

## Key Scenes

- **[[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] excavation**: Blake and Tom [[work-and-vocation|work]] side by side documenting the cliff-[[home-and-belonging|dwelling]] [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts (Book II)
- **The sale decision**: Blake sells the [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts to a German dealer, precipitating the break with Tom
- **Disappearance**: Blake vanishes, eventually reported to have enlisted in the Foreign Legion (011-022)
- **The bank account**: The "mysterious Rodney Blake" account in New Mexico becomes a legal and emotional tangle (017-004)
