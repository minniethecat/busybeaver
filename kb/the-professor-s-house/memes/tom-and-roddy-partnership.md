## Context

[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]], a young call boy for the Santa Fe railroad in Pardee, New Mexico, meets [[rodney-blake|Rodney Blake]] on a cold autumn night after payday. Tom is a "stray" without [[family-and-domesticity|family]], recently recovered from pneumonia. Blake is a new fireman on the division — a big, heavy-built man with a reputation for drinking and unfriendliness, but with a hidden capacity for loyalty (018-001—018-024).

The encounter transforms both their lives, setting in motion the chain of events that leads to the [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] of the [[discovery-of-cliff-dweller-ruins|cliff-dweller ruins]] on the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]].

## The Poker Game

On a cold, clear fall night, Tom goes to find his freight crew at a poker game in the cardroom behind the Ruby Light saloon. [[rodney-blake|Rodney Blake]], still in his greasy [[work-and-vocation|work]] clothes and smelling of whisky, is cleaning out every player at the table (018-002—018-005).

> "The crowd was fussing about one fellow, Rodney Blake, who had come in from his engine without cleaning up. That wasn't customary; the minute a man got in from his run, he took a bath, put on citizen's clothes, and went to a barber." (018-003)

Blake wins over sixteen hundred dollars. The other men insult him, trying to provoke a fight. He ignores them and walks out wordlessly (018-006—018-008).

## Tom's Loyalty

Tom follows Blake to his room in the Mexican qu[[art-and-religion|art]]er. He finds Blake passed out on the bed, gold coins scattered on the floor. Tom collects the [[money-and-materialism|money]], puts it in Blake's grip, and sits watch all night to protect him from potential robbery (018-009—018-012).

> "I trusted all the boys who were at the Ruby Light that night, except Barney Shea. He might try to pull something off on a stranger, down in Mexican town." (018-012)

The next morning, Tom persuades Blake to put every cent into a savings account that can't be touched for a year.

> "From that night Blake and I were fast friends. He was the sort of fellow who can do anything for somebody else, and nothing for himself." (018-024)

## The Summer on the Range

When Tom is still recovering from pneumonia in the spring, Blake without hesitation quits his railroad job and secures [[work-and-vocation|work]] for both of them with the Sitwell Cattle Company, riding the range south of Pardee toward the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] (018-025).

> "Before I knew anything about it, Blake had thrown up his job on the Santa Fe, and got a berth for him and me with the Sitwell Cattle Company." (018-025)

All summer, they drift with the cattle from water-hole to water-hole, reading *Robinson Crusoe* and *Gulliver's Travels*, discussing the Dreyfus case and the hanging of the Anarchists. Blake makes Tom translate his hundred lines of Caesar daily — "He said if I once knew Latin, I wouldn't have to [[work-and-vocation|work]] with my back all my life like a burro" (018-028).

> "After supper, when we lit our pipes and watched the sunset, climbing the mesa was our staple topic of conversation." (018-027)

## Key Participants

- **[[tom-outland]]**: At the st[[art-and-religion|art]] of the friendship, Tom is a teenage call boy, orphaned and [[solitude-and-creativity|alone]]. His act of guarding Blake's [[money-and-materialism|money]] and persuading him to save it demonstrates the integrity that will later define his character.
- **[[rodney-blake]]**: A [[work-and-vocation|work]]ingman marked by repeated betrayals — his mother's re[[love-and-desire|marriage]], a fiancée's infidelity, friends who cheated him out of savings. In Tom he finds "a pal, a straight fellow to give an account to" (018-024). Blake's loyalty and selflessness — "the sort of fellow who can do anything for somebody else, and nothing for himself" — makes the [[discovery-of-cliff-dweller-ruins|Blue Mesa discovery]] possible.

## Aftermath and Significance

The p[[art-and-religion|art]]nership between Tom and Roddy is the emotional core of Book II. Their shared summer below the mesa makes possible the [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] of the cliff-dweller ruins (018-029). It is also the friendship whose eventual rupture — when Roddy sells the [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts while Tom is in [[washington-dc|Washington]] — becomes one of the novel's most painful moments (024-010).

The Professor sees in this [[work-and-vocation|work]]ing-class friendship something noble: "There is, he knew, this dream of self-sacrificing friendship and disinterested [[love-and-desire|love]] down among the day-labourers, the men who run the railroad trains and boats and reapers and thrashers and mine-drills of the world" (017-003). Tom and Roddy's bond represents a purity of human connection that the novel sets against the compromises and [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]s of academic and [[family-and-domesticity|domestic life]].
