## Definition

The tension between American and European culture runs through The Professor's House as a quiet but persistent current. [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]] embodies the transatlantic intellectual: of mixed Canadian French and American ancestry, trained in [[paris|Paris]], holding the chair of European history at an American university. His [[the-garden|French garden]] in [[hamilton|Hamilton]] is a literal transplant of European sensibility onto Midwestern soil. The contrast is not presented as a simple opposition — Cather values both American energy and European cultivation — but as a productive friction that shapes [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s identity and his alienation from the [[money-and-materialism|commercialism]] of 1920s America.

## In the Novel

The Professor's European formation is central to his character. He met [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] in [[paris|Paris]] as a young scholar, a period that remains the emotional touchstone of his life:

> "St. Peter had met his wife in Paris, when he was but twenty-four." (003-025)

His [[the-garden|French garden]] — walled, ordered, hidden from the street — is the physical manifestation of his European sensibility transplanted to [[hamilton|Hamilton]]:

> "The Professor had succeeded in making a French garden in Hamilton." (001-007)

The garden becomes a site of [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] and refuge, where [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] can recall his [[paris|Paris]] years:

> "How it does make one think of Paris, and of so many half-forgotten things." (008-015)

[[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]], by contrast, represents a purely American energy — enthusiastic about [[paris|Paris]]ian restaurants but ultimately a commercial operator who plans to use European antiquities for profit:

> "All the antiquaries are centralized in Paris. I never saw anything very interesting in America." (015-009)

The Professor's reluctance to join [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] and Rosamond in [[paris|Paris]] — "he liked [[paris|Paris]], and he liked [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]. But one cannot do everything" — marks his [[escape|withdrawal]] from both European [[memory-and-the-past|nostalgia]] and American [[ambition-and-success|ambition]].

## Character Connections

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]**: The transatlantic figure — French-Canadian ancestry, [[paris|Paris]]-trained, [[teaching-and-mentorship|teaching]] European history in the American Midwest. His identity is built on the bridge between continents (001-003, 003-025).

- **[[lillian-st-peter]]**: Met [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] in [[paris|Paris]]; her sophistication and social [[ambition-and-success|ambition]] are coded as European-[[power|influence]]d, contrasting with Midwestern [[hamilton|Hamilton]].

- **[[louie-marsellus]]**: Embodies the American commercial approach to Europe — [[paris|Paris]] as a market[[home-and-belonging|place]] for antiques and restaurants, not a site of intellectual formation (015-001, 015-005).

- **[[tom-outland]]**: Represents a purely American [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] — the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] cliff [[home-and-belonging|dwelling]]s, untouched by European influence. His story offers an alternative to the transatlantic axis.

## Critical Context

Cather's own biography mirrors the transatlantic theme: born in Virginia, raised in Nebraska, she traveled extensively in Europe and drew on European literary traditions. Critics note that The Professor's House registers a specifically post-[[world-war-i|World War I]] moment: the war that killed [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] also shattered the cultural confidence of the Old World. The Professor, caught between American vitality and European tradition, belongs fully to neither. His scholarly [[work-and-vocation|work]] — a multi-volume history of Spanish exploration in North America — itself bridges the Atlantic, studying the moment of European contact with the Americas.
