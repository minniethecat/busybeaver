## Role in the Novel

Professor Horace Langtry appears in Book I as the long-standing [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al rival of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]] at [[hamilton|Hamilton University]]. For twenty years the two men have "scarcely had speech with each other beyond a stiff good morning" (003-041), their mutual antagonism having become "a p[[art-and-religion|art]] of their [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al duties to outwit and cramp each other" (003-052). Langtry represents the political, careerist side of [[scholarship-and-knowledge|academic life]] that [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s more scholarly, disinterested approach defines itself against.

Langtry's presence in the novel illuminates the institutional pressures [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] has navigated throughout his career. The rivalry between them — over students, courses, and dep[[art-and-religion|art]]ment chairs — is the novel's most sustained depiction of academic politics.

## Character Portrait

When Langtry first arrived at the university, he "looked hardly more than a boy, with curly brown hair and such a fresh complexion that the students called him Lily Langtry" (003-041). His "round pink cheeks and round eyes and round chin made him look rather like a baby grown big" (003-041). Over the years his curls turned grey but his rosy complexion remained, and his s[[art-and-religion|art]]orial choices reflected his annual summer sojourns in London, from which he returned "very well got up in English clothes" with "a bowler hat of unusual block and a horn-handled walking-stick" (003-041).

Langtry is a Tory in his views, "considered very English in his tone and manner" (003-052). His lectures were dull and the students did not like him, yet over time he became, "in a curious way, an instructor in manners — what is called an '[[power|influence]]'" (003-054) to the waves of rural and small-town students flooding the university.

## Key Relationships

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]]** — Langtry's [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al rival and enemy. Their twenty-year feud encompasses university policy, dep[[art-and-religion|art]]ment chairs, and student favor, yet by the novel's present [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] wonders "what Langtry still had to be sore about" (003-054), reflecting that it was a draw and they had "both been beaten" (003-054).
- **Langtry's uncle** — President of the board of regents and influential in state politics, he secured Langtry's position and attempted to take [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s dep[[art-and-religion|art]]ment from him during [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s sabbatical in [[spain|Spain]] (003-053).
- **"Madame Langtry"** — Langtry's wife, who "paid him out" by [[work-and-vocation|work]]ing against [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] with the regents (003-053).

## Key Scenes

- **Sunday morning encounter**: [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] overtakes Langtry in the park and attempts conversation, but Langtry responds stiffly and excuses himself to church service. The Professor mutters "*Quelle folie!*" at the absurdity of their grudge (003-041–003-050).
- **The rivalry's history**: A narrative retrospective traces Langtry's career from young instructor to established figure, including the near-theft of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s dep[[art-and-religion|art]]ment (003-052–003-054).
