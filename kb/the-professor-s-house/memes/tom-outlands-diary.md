# Tom Outland's Diary

**Tom Outland's Diary** (also referred to as his daybook or ledger) is the archaeological field journal kept by [[tom-outland]] during the summer he and [[rodney-blake]] spent excavating the [[cliff-dweller-civilization|Cliff City]] on [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]]. Along with [[tom-outlands-invention|Tom's scientific invention]], it is one of the two principal legacies he leaves to [[professor-godfrey-st-peter]] — a document so carefully preserved that it survives sealed in a stone cupboard for years before being retrieved. The diary is not merely a prop in the novel; it is the textual [[art-and-religion|art]]ifact that both generates and frames the narrative of **Book II: [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s Story**, which constitutes Tom's first-person account of the [[discovery-of-cliff-dweller-ruins|discovery and excavation of the ruins]].

## Nature of the Document

The diary is described as a plain account — an archaeologist's field record rather than a personal memoir. Tom wrote it in a merchant's ledger purchased in the railroad town of Tarpin, and every evening after supper he sat at the kitchen table in the cabin on the mesa top and wrote up the day's [[work-and-vocation|work]] while his p[[art-and-religion|art]]ner [[rodney-blake|Roddy Blake]] read newspapers. The entries contain what [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] later characterizes as prose of deliberate austerity:

> To St. Peter this plain account was almost beautiful, because of the stupidities it avoided and the things it did not say. If words had cost money, Tom couldn't have used them more sparingly. The adjectives were purely descriptive, relating to form and colour, and were used to present the objects under consideration, not the young explorer's emotions. Yet through this austerity one felt the kindling imagination, the ardour and excitement of the boy, like the vibration in a voice when the speaker strives to conceal his emotion by using only conventional phrases. (026-001)

The diary documents approximately six months of [[work-and-vocation|work]], covering the period from the first [[ambition-and-success|success]]ful entry into the Cliff City in early May through the end of the excavation season after Christmas (017-002). It includes minute descriptions of every tool, cloth fragment, and pottery specimen recovered, often accompanied by pencil sketches and surmises about the object's use within the [[cliff-dweller-civilization|cliff-dweller]] society (026-001).

## Creation and Daily Practice

The rhythm of the diary's creation was inseparable from the physical labour of excavation. Tom describes the ritual plainly:

> We numbered each specimen, and in my daybook I wrote down just where and in what condition we had found it, and what we thought it had been used for. I'd got a merchant's ledger in Tarpin, and every night after supper, while Roddy read the newspapers, I sat down at the kitchen table and wrote up an account of the day's work. (021-012)

This nightly discipline — cataloguing, sketching, speculating — produced a record that went beyond mere inventory. The diary also incorporated the deductions of [[father-duchene]], the Belgian missionary priest who visited the mesa after the [[death-and-mortality|death]] of old [[henry-atkins|Henry Atkins]] (022-015). [[father-duchene|Father Duchene]]'s analysis of the [[cliff-dweller-civilization|cliff-dwellers]] as a "provident, rather thoughtful people" who "lived for something more than food and shelter" (022-007) was transcribed by Tom into the daybook, giving the field journal the scope of a proto-archaeological report.

## Concealment and Recovery

After the [[washington-dc|Washington]] debacle — Tom's failed attempt to interest the Smithsonian Institution in the [[discovery-of-cliff-dweller-ruins|Blue Mesa discoveries]] — and his devastating quarrel with [[rodney-blake|Roddy Blake]], the diary took on a different significance. Before leaving the mesa for the winter, Tom had sealed his daybook in a hidden niche:

> I had written up my daybook carefully to the very end, had even written out some of Father Duchene's deductions. This book I left in concealment on the mesa. I climbed up to the Eagle's Nest in which we had found the mummy of the murdered woman we called Mother Eve, where I had noticed a particularly neat little cupboard in the wall. I put my book in this niche and sealed it up with cement. (022-015)

The diary remained entombed there for several years — through Tom's university years at [[hamilton|Hamilton]], his scientific breakthrough with [[tom-outlands-invention|the Outland vacuum]], and his [[death-and-mortality|death]] in [[world-war-i]]. Remarkably, during the transcendent summer Tom spent [[solitude-and-creativity|alone]] on the mesa after Blake's dep[[art-and-religion|art]]ure — the summer he describes as "my high tide" (024-005) — he deliberately refrained from retrieving it:

> All that summer, I never went up to the Eagle's Nest to get my diary — indeed, it's probably there yet. I didn't feel the need of that record. It would have been going backward. I didn't want to go back and unravel things step by step. Perhaps I was afraid that I would lose the whole in the parts. (024-006)

Years later, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] and Tom went to the mesa together, "climbed the ladder of spliced pine-trees to the Cliff City, and up to the Eagle's Nest," and retrieved the diary from its stone cupboard (025-007). It was one of the last shared acts of their friendship before the war.

## St. Peter's Editorial Project

The summer following [[lillian-st-peter|his wife]] and daughters' dep[[art-and-religion|art]]ure for [[france|France]], [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] planned to edit and annotate the diary for publication — a project that, like so much else in that strange summer of reversion, he barely began:

> It was his plan to give part of this summer to Tom Outland's diary — to edit and annotate it for publication. The bother was that he must write an introduction. The diary covered only about six months of the boy's life, a summer he spent on the Blue Mesa, and in it there was almost nothing about Tom himself. To mean anything, it must be prefaced by a sketch of Outland, and some account of his later life and achievements. (017-002)

The Professor's desultory annotation of the diary becomes the vehicle for his larger meditation on the return of his "original" self — the Kansas boy he had long ago left behind. The diary, like the [[tom-outlands-invention|invention]], becomes a relic of a lost world, and [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s failure to complete the editorial project mirrors his larger [[escape|withdrawal]] from the claims of [[family-and-domesticity|family]] and [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al life.

## The Diary as Narrative Artifact

The diary occupies a unique position in the novel's architecture. Book II is titled "[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s Story," and the entire section — chapters 18 through 24 — reads as Tom's spoken or remembered account of his [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] experience, told to [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] on rainy nights in the [[the-old-house|old house]] (017-020). The diary is thus both source and symbol: the written record from which the oral narrative is drawn, and the physical [[art-and-religion|art]]ifact that anchors the [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] of Tom long after his [[death-and-mortality|death]].

The diary's formal qualities — its spareness, its resistance to self-display, its fidelity to the object rather than the observer — embody the intellectual virtues that [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] most valued in Tom: the capacity to see clearly and record honestly, without the "foolish conventions" (001-048) that encumbered even scholarly prose. In a novel deeply concerned with the fate of [[scholarship-and-knowledge|knowledge]] in a commercial age, Tom's diary represents a form of writing that is pure of mercenary motive — a record made for the sake of understanding [[solitude-and-creativity|alone]].
