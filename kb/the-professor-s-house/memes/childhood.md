## Definition

Childhood in The Professor's House is idealized through [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] but inaccessible in the present. The Professor's recurring image of [[lake-michigan|Lake Michigan]] as "the inland sea of his childhood" (001-043) encapsulates Cather's treatment: childhood is a blue, boundless space that adulthood can only recall, never re-enter.

The novel traces childhood from two angles — [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s memories of his own early years and the more ambiguous treatment of his daughters' childhoods, which have hardened into adult concerns with [[money-and-materialism|money]], status, and property. Childhood innocence is not a state Cather treats as recoverable, but as something that exists only in retrospect.

## In the Novel

The Professor's most persistent childhood [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] is of [[lake-michigan|Lake Michigan]], which recurs as a private talisman throughout the novel:

> "Whenever he was tired and dull, dull and tired of everything, he used to think of the lake." (001-043)

This image — blue water, limitless horizon — functions as [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s escape from the dusty, cluttered present of his [[the-old-house|old house]]. The lake is not merely a geographical location but a [[home-and-belonging|place]] of primal freedom:

> "When he remembered his childhood, he remembered blue water." (001-044)

The connection between childhood and [[lake-michigan|the lake]] is explicitly framed in terms of possibility: "the always possible escape from dullness, was [[lake-michigan|the lake]]" (001-044). Childhood, in the novel's symbolic economy, is not a specific period but the condition of having open possibility ahead of one.

The novel extends this theme of lost youth beyond [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] to the broader cast. The Professor "had spent the happiest years of his youth in a [[home-and-belonging|house]] at Versailles" (001-002) — a detail that links his own youthful happiness to the Old World.

His "misfortune was that he [[love-and-desire|love]]d youth — he was weak to it, it kindled him" (001-041), a self-diagnosis that explains his attraction to [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] and his difficulty with the adult world his daughters now inhabit.

Rosamond's childhood is described by her father from a different emotional register. The Professor recalls her as a child who "regarded his freelance childhood as a gay adventure" (010-072), a characterization that distances the adult Rosamond — calculating, property-minded, married to [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] — from the spontaneous girl she once was.

The [[family-and-domesticity|family]]'s summer exodus "to Colorado with the children" (007-032) belongs to a lost domestic rhythm, a pattern of life that the novel's present has disrupted.

In Book III, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s crisis brings childhood memories to the surface with new intensity. He recalls "important memories of his early childhood, of his mother, his father, his [[home-and-belonging|home]]" (026-008), suggesting that the approach of [[death-and-mortality|death]] — or the temptation of it — draws one back toward one's earliest self.

## Character Connections

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]**: Childhood for [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] is synonymous with [[lake-michigan|Lake Michigan]] and freedom. His "[[love-and-desire|love]] of youth" (001-041) shapes his relationships and his [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]]. His adult life is measured against the blue-water [[memory-and-the-past|memory]].

- **[[rosamond-marsellus]]**: Rosamond's childhood is described as a "gay adventure" — the contrast with her adult self, absorbed in property and social position, is one of the novel's quiet tragedies.

- **[[kathleen-mcgregor]]**: Kathleen is less directly associated with childhood imagery, but her position as the less-favored daughter connects her to a different kind of arrested development.

- **[[tom-outland]]**: Tom's youth on the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] carries its own childhood resonance — the mesa is his [[lake-michigan|Lake Michigan]], a [[home-and-belonging|place]] of original freedom before adult betrayal and [[death-and-mortality|loss]]. His [[death-and-mortality|death]] preserves him in a kind of perpetual youth.

## Critical Context

The elegiac treatment of childhood is characteristic of Cather's late [[work-and-vocation|work]]. Critics have noted that in The Professor's House, as in My Ántonia and Death Comes for the Archbishop, childhood is inseparable from [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]] — the open water, the open mesa, the open prairie.

This is a recognizably Romantic structure (Wordsworth's "shades of the prison-[[home-and-belonging|house]] begin to close / upon the growing boy"), but Cather's version is notably unsentimental: childhood is lost irrecoverably.

The Professor's return to childhood memories in Book III — on what might be his [[death-and-mortality|death]]bed — suggests that the first self is also the last: in extremis, we revert to what we were before the complications of adult life closed in.

## Related Concepts

- **[[youth-and-aging]]**: Childhood is the earliest phase of the youth-to-age arc the novel traces. The Professor's childhood memories anchor one end of the temporal spectrum that the novel surveys.

- **[[memory-and-the-past]]**: Childhood exists in the novel only through [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] — it has no present-tense reality. The reliability and function of [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] are thus central to how the novel constructs childhood.

- **[[home-and-belonging]]**: The childhood [[home-and-belonging|home]] — [[lake-michigan|the lake]] [[home-and-belonging|house]], [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s early [[family-and-domesticity|family]] life — is one version of "[[home-and-belonging|home]]," contrasted with the [[the-old-house|old house]] of the novel's present.

- **[[loss-and-change]]**: The [[death-and-mortality|loss]] of childhood is the primal [[death-and-mortality|loss]] from which all other [[death-and-mortality|loss]]es in the novel derive. Growing up is the first and most definitive [[loss-and-change|change]] the novel registers.

- **[[family-and-domesticity]]**: The [[family-and-domesticity|family]] structure is the container of childhood, and the novel's portrayal of the [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] [[family-and-domesticity|family]] is inseparable from its treatment of what childhood means and how it ends.
