## Role in the Novel

Henry Atkins appears only in [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s narrative (Book II), but his presence — and his [[death-and-mortality|death]] — are essential to the emotional texture of the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] chapters. He was "a castaway Englishman" who "had been a valet" (020-001) before finding his way to the mesa, where he became the camp cook. His [[death-and-mortality|death]], which occurs between chapters, prompts [[father-duchene|Father Duchene]] to visit the mesa — the priest "buried Henry on the mesa" (022-002) and stayed for a week to comfort Tom and Blake.

Atkins's [[death-and-mortality|death]] establishes the mesa not only as a site of [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] and wonder but also as a [[home-and-belonging|place]] where people live, [[work-and-vocation|work]], and die — a fully human [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]]. His burial there, conducted by a Belgian priest beside ancient cliff-[[home-and-belonging|dwelling]] remains, collapses the distance between past and present, making the mesa a continuum of human habitation rather than a mere archaeological site.

## Character Portrait

Cather gives Atkins only a single paragraph of introduction, but the details are vivid and economical. He is a figure of pathos — a man dis[[home-and-belonging|place]]d by circumstance, who found [[work-and-vocation|work]] and purpose at the mesa before his life ended there. The phrase "castaway Englishman" suggests both his rootlessness and his vulnerability, while the detail of his former [[work-and-vocation|profession]] (valet) hints at a story of downward mobility that the novel does not e[[work-and-vocation|labor]]ate.

> "He had been a valet" (020-001)

His role as cook [[home-and-belonging|place]]s him in a position of humble service — feeding the men who are making great discoveries — and his [[death-and-mortality|death]] underscores the physical hardship and [[solitude-and-creativity|isolation]] of life on the mesa.

## Key Relationships

- **[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]** — Atkins [[work-and-vocation|work]]ed for Tom and Blake as the camp cook. Tom's matter-of-fact telling of Atkins's story reflects both [[love-and-desire|affection]] and the stoicism of life on the frontier.
- **[[rodney-blake|Rodney Blake]]** — Blake shared the camp with Atkins and, with Tom, witnessed his [[death-and-mortality|death]].
- **[[father-duchene|Father Duchene]]** — The missionary priest who "buried Henry on the mesa" (022-002) and stayed "on with us a week to keep us from feeling too much down about it" (022-002). This act of pastoral care is one of the novel's quiet moments of grace.

## Critical Significance

Atkins matters precisely because he is minor. Cather's inclusion of a character whose [[death-and-mortality|death]] is neither heroic nor narratively climactic — simply sad and human — is an [[art-and-religion|art]]istic choice that deepens the novel's realism. The mesa is not romanticized; people die there of ordinary causes, and the living must carry on.

His burial by [[father-duchene|Father Duchene]] on the mesa also creates a symbolic layering: the ancient [[cliff-dweller-civilization|cliff-dwellers]] buried their dead in this [[home-and-belonging|place]], and now a European priest buries an English valet here. The continuity of burial practice across cultures and centuries is one of the novel's quiet insistences — that certain human gestures transcend the p[[art-and-religion|art]]icularities of time and belief.
