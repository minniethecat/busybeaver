## Definition

Jealousy and envy permeate the intimate world of *The Professor's House* as persistent, low-grade emotional forces. Cather treats these emotions not as melodramatic crises but as ambient conditions — the steady erosion of goodwill within a family reshaped by money. Jealousy in the novel is characteristically the jealousy of those who have less toward those who have more: Scott's resentment of Louie, Kathleen's envy of Rosamond, the Professor's submerged jealousy of the younger, vital Louie who has usurped his place in his daughter's life. Envy is the structural emotion of a family in which one daughter has married into spectacular wealth and the other has not.

## In the Novel

The novel's most explicit acknowledgment of jealousy comes early, in its account of [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian St. Peter]]'s response to [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]: "Lillian had been fiercely jealous of Tom Outland" (003-024). The narrator elaborates: "St. Peter had had no friend in Hamilton of whom Lillian could possibly be jealous until Tom Outland came along, so well did he understand his wife's nature" (003-025). This is a marital jealousy, the wife's resentment of the younger man who claimed her husband's deepest intellectual and emotional attention.

The more pervasive jealousy in the novel, however, is financial and generational. [[scott-mcgregor|Scott McGregor]] is "touchy and jealous" (006-014) of [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]], whose wealth and expansive style dominate the family's social landscape. Scott's jealousy is complicated by principle — he believes Louie has profited from a discovery that morally belongs to the St. Peter family and to Tom Outland's memory — but it is also the simpler resentment of a man who feels diminished by another man's success.

> "If you permit yourself to be envious of Rosie, you'll be very foolish, and very unhappy." (007-031)

The Professor's warning to [[kathleen-mcgregor|Kathleen]] is the novel's clearest moral statement on envy, and it is characteristically unsentimental. He does not tell her that envy is wrong; he tells her it is self-destructive. Kathleen's envy of [[rosamond-marsellus|Rosamond]] is the most painful emotional thread in the book — a sister's love corroded by the daily visibility of inequality.

## Character Connections

- **[[lillian-st-peter]]**: Her jealousy of Tom Outland reveals the possessive dimension of her marriage and the threat that intellectual intimacy posed to domestic order.
- **[[kathleen-mcgregor]]**: The novel's primary figure of envy — her artistic sensibility, sharp wit, and moral clarity are increasingly poisoned by her resentment of Rosamond's wealth and beauty.
- **[[scott-mcgregor]]**: His jealousy of Louie is professional and financial, but it is also a displaced form of grief for the lost Outland legacy.
- **[[rosamond-marsellus]]**: The object of envy, increasingly oblivious to the pain her wealth causes — or perhaps aware but unwilling to diminish her own enjoyment.
- **[[louie-marsellus]]**: Provokes jealousy effortlessly, through his energy, his money, his strategic generosity. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] is "very tactful and patient" (006-014) in managing the jealousies he stirs.
- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]**: His own jealousy is submerged but present — of Louie's youth, vitality, and claim on Rosamond; of the life he himself might have lived.

## The Structure of Envy

What makes envy so potent in the novel is its structural, not personal, origin. Kathleen is not greedier or smaller-souled than Rosamond; she is the victim of an accident — that Tom Outland left his discovery to Rosamond rather than to the family as a whole. The Outland fortune is windfall wealth, distributed arbitrarily. The novel does not judge Kathleen for envying her sister; it observes, with clinical precision, what envy does to a person over time: "He had never happened to see that change occur in a face before, and he had never realized to what an ugly, painful transformation it could give to a comely face" (007-025).

## Related Concepts

- **[[love-and-desire]]**: Envy and [[love-and-desire|love]] coexist in the novel's families; Kathleen loves Rosamond and envies her in the same breath.
- **[[pride]]**: Scott's jealousy is partly [[pride]] — the wounded self-regard of a man who feels he has been outflanked.
- **[[shame]]**: Kathleen's envy carries the sting of [[shame]] — she knows envy is beneath her, and this knowledge compounds the pain.
- **[[money-and-materialism]]**: Money is the solvent that activates latent envy; before the Outland fortune, the sisters lived in rough equality.
- **[[family-and-domesticity]]**: Envy is the shadow emotion of the domestic sphere — the feeling that cannot be spoken at the dinner table.
- **[[secrecy-and-revelation]]**: Much of the novel's jealousy is sustained by things half-known, half-concealed — secrets kept for the sake of family peace.
