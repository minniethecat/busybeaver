## Role in the Novel

Augusta is the sewing-woman who, for decades, has shared [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s attic study in the [[the-old-house|old house]]. She appears at key moments in Books I and III, and her presence — practical, faithful, and utterly without pretense — provides a crucial counterpoint to [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s intellectual world. In the novel's climax, it is Augusta who finds [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] overcome by gas in the study and, through her practical intervention, saves his life. She is, in Cather's design, the figure who keeps [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] tethered to life when his own will to live has faltered.

## Character Portrait

Augusta is introduced as "a reliable, methodical spinster, a German Catholic and very devout" (001-010). Her physical description — tall, deliberate, carrying the dressmaker's forms — emphasizes her solidity and substance. She is the opposite of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] in almost every way: practical where he is intellectual, devout where he is secular, rooted where he is restless. Yet their coexistence in the attic study is the novel's most durable domestic arrangement — more stable, more respectful, and in some ways more intimate than [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s [[love-and-desire|marriage]].

The dressmaker's forms — "the bust" and "the wire lady" — become a shared language between Augusta and [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]], a running joke that Cather renders with [[love-and-desire|affection]] and subtlety. The Professor's playful refusal to let Augusta remove the forms from the study ("I'm damned if he will! They shan't be wheeled. They stay right there in their own [[home-and-belonging|place]]. You shan't take away my ladies." in Book I) reveals, beneath the humor, a genuine attachment to the shared space they have created.

> "Augusta enjoyed the Professor when he was risqué, since she was sure of his ultimate delicacy." (001-013)

## Relationships

- **[[Professor Godfrey St. Peter]]** — The unlikely companion of his creative [[solitude-and-creativity|solitude]]. Their relationship transcends its apparent hierarchy — she is not merely an employee but a presence that makes his intellectual life possible.
- **[[Appelhoff]]** — Uncle and former landlord of the [[the-old-house|old house]]. The [[family-and-domesticity|family]] connection that brought Augusta into [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s life.

## Significance

Augusta represents an alternative model of the good life — one grounded in [[work-and-vocation|work]], faith, and quiet devotion rather than intellectual [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]. In the novel's final scene, her rescue of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] is not just physical but spiritual. She is the novel's answer to the question of what sustains a life when the grand passions — [[work-and-vocation|work]], friendship, [[love-and-desire|love]] — have faded. Her Catholicism, treated by Cather with respect if not with belief, offers a frame[[work-and-vocation|work]] of meaning that [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s secular humanism cannot provide.
