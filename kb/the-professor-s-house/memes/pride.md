## Definition

Pride in The Professor's House is a double-edged quality: it sustains the self but also walls it off. The Professor takes quiet pride in his scholarly [[work-and-vocation|work]] and in his [[the-old-house|old house]] — a pride that is almost indistinguishable from his sense of identity. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian St. Peter]]'s pride is social and aesthetic, invested in the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] and her daughters' [[love-and-desire|marriage]]s.

[[rodney-blake|Roddy Blake]]'s pride, revealed indirectly through [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s narrative, is personal and wounded — the pride of a man who will not stay where he is not wanted. Cather treats pride not as a sin to be condemned but as a fact of character to be observed: something that holds people together even as it keeps them ap[[art-and-religion|art]].

## In the Novel

The novel's explicit references to pride occur in contexts that illuminate its double function. The first is domestic: [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] "took pride in keeping her p[[art-and-religion|art]] of the compact" (007-032) — the compact being her marital arrangement with [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]. [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]]'s pride here is a form of integrity, a commitment to doing her p[[art-and-religion|art]].

But this same pride also fuels her dissatisfaction: the [[the-old-house|old house]] offends her sense of what she and her husband should have achieved. Kathleen's observation about her mother is revealing:

> "I was always proud of her good looks and good taste." (007-018)

This pride in a parent's qualities, spoken by Kathleen, simultaneously honors [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] and exposes the emotional distance in the [[family-and-domesticity|family]]: pride in one another is what the [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]s offer instead of intimacy.

The second direct reference concerns [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] as a teacher and mentor. His relationship with [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s [[ambition-and-success|success]]or, the young physicist Crane, is described as "beyond gratifying his pride as a teacher and friend" (013-031).

Here pride is [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al — the teacher's pride in the student who surpasses him. But the qualifying phrase "beyond gratifying" suggests that pride, even at its most legitimate, is still a form of self-regard that the novel treats with caution.

The Professor's relationship with his [[the-old-house|old house]] is the novel's most sustained meditation on pride. He refuses to move to the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] has furnished with [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]'s [[money-and-materialism|money]]. This refusal looks like obstinacy but is actually pride in its most irreducible form.

The [[the-old-house|old house]], with its cramped garden and its sewing-room study, is [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s self made spatial. To abandon it would be to abandon something essential. The garden itself gives the [[family-and-domesticity|family]] "a feeling of dignity and purpose" (005-007).

[[rodney-blake|Roddy Blake]]'s pride is the novel's most consequential instance of the emotion. [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] recalls: "Roddy was proud. He didn't like taking orders" (010-073).

This pride — personal, unyielding — is what leads Roddy to sell the [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts without consulting Tom and, later, to walk away entirely after Tom's accusation. Roddy's pride makes reconciliation impossible; it is the engine of the novel's central tragedy.

The theme of honour, closely related to pride, surfaces in the university setting. The Professor's struggles "to preserve the dignity of the university" (012-030) connect personal pride to institutional identity.

When academic honours pass [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] by — "The honour went, instead, to a much younger man" (012-029) — the novel registers the quiet wound of professional pride denied. And yet Professor Crane's assessment is unequivocal: "I think he'd honour it" (013-025), speaking of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s integrity.

## Character Connections

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]**: The Professor's pride is quiet, intellectual, and territorial — it inheres in his [[work-and-vocation|work]], his study, and his refusal to be absorbed into the social world of the new [[home-and-belonging|house]]. His pride is what keeps him alive psychologically, even when he is tempted toward [[death-and-mortality|death]].

- **[[lillian-st-peter]]**: [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]]'s pride is social — she cares about appearances, about having a [[home-and-belonging|house]] that reflects her husband's standing and her own taste. Her pride is not vanity but a form of self-respect that the novel neither mocks nor fully endorses.

- **[[roddy-blake]]**: Roddy's pride is the novel's most dramatic: proud and unwilling to take orders, he acts decisively but also destructively. His pride prevents him from staying after Tom's accusation, making the rupture permanent and Tom's guilt unresolvable.

- **[[rosamond-marsellus]]**: Rosamond's pride is inherited from both parents — she has her mother's social awareness and her father's stubbornness. The Professor "was very proud of her" (002-013), but Rosamond's adult pride is of a different order: the pride of position and property.

- **[[tom-outland]]**: Tom's pride is the pride of the self-made — he came from nothing, and his intellectual [[ambition-and-success|achievement]]s are entirely his own. But his pride also leads to the catastrophe with Roddy: his inability to see that Roddy acted out of practical necessity, not disloyalty.

## Critical Context

Pride in American literature is often inflected by the Protestant suspicion of vanity and the democratic suspicion of distinction. Cather's treatment is more nuanced: pride, in her novel, is almost never simply a vice.

The Professor's pride connects him to his European intellectual [[preservation-and-legacy|heritage]]; [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]]'s pride connects her to a tradition of domestic cultivation. Roddy's pride echoes an older, more elemental code of personal honour — the pride that will not compromise.

What the novel does question is whether pride can survive the modern world's indifference to the values it enshrines. The Professor's pride is predicated on a world that respects [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]]; when that world is dis[[home-and-belonging|place]]d by [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]'s world of commerce and publicity, his pride becomes [[solitude-and-creativity|isolation]] rather than connection.

## Related Concepts

- **[[shame]]**: Pride and shame are the two poles of self-regard in the novel. The Professor's pride protects him from shame, but it also keeps him from the intimacy that might relieve his [[solitude-and-creativity|isolation]].

- **[[guilt]]**: Pride and guilt interact in [[outland|Tom Outland's story]] — his pride prevented him from understanding Roddy's actions, and the guilt that followed reshaped his life.

- **[[ambition-and-success]]**: Pride is the emotional underside of [[ambition-and-success|ambition]] — what [[ambition-and-success|ambition]] seeks in the world, pride seeks within the self.

- **[[teaching-and-mentorship]]**: The Professor's pride in his students — [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]], Crane — is one of the novel's most affirmative emotions. Teaching gives [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] a pride that requires no public recognition.

- **[[friendship]]**: The friendship between Tom and Roddy is destroyed by pride on both sides — Tom's moral pride and Roddy's personal pride. The novel suggests that friendship cannot survive when neither will bend.

- **[[loss-and-change]]**: Pride is what characters use to manage [[death-and-mortality|loss]], sometimes [[ambition-and-success|success]]fully, sometimes not. The [[death-and-mortality|loss]] of dignity — in the university, in the [[family-and-domesticity|family]], in the self — is one of the quiet [[death-and-mortality|loss]]es the novel catalogues.
