# Rosamond Marsellus

**Rosamond Marsellus** (née [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]) is the elder daughter of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter]] and [[lillian-st-peter]], sister of [[kathleen-mcgregor]], and wife of [[louie-marsellus]]. She was formerly engaged to [[tom-outland]] and, after his [[death-and-mortality|death]] in [[world-war-i]], inherited the fortune generated by his revolutionary [[science-and-discovery|invention]] — an outcome that shapes much of the novel's dramatic tension around [[money-and-materialism|money]], loyalty, and the meaning of intellectual [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]].

## Appearance and Character

Rosamond is introduced as a strikingly beautiful young woman, though her father views her with a critical, anatomist's eye:

> Nearly everyone considered Rosamond brilliantly beautiful. Her father, though he was very proud of her, demurred from the general opinion. He thought her too tall, with a rather awkward carriage. She stooped a trifle, and was wide in the hips and shoulders. She had, he sometimes remarked to her mother, exactly the wide femur and flat shoulder-blade of his old slab-sided Kanuck grandfather. (002-013)

Willa Cather consistently describes Rosamond in terms of colour and richness — her "dusky black hair," "deep dark eyes," and the "rich brunette red in her cheeks and lips" that was "like the duskiness of dark, heavy-scented roses" (002-013). She shares her mother's fastidious taste in [[scholarship-and-knowledge|literature]] and aesthetic sensibility, using her beauty and refinement as social currency in the world of [[hamilton]] and beyond.

## Relationship with Her Father

The emotional core of Rosamond's story lies in her relationship with [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]]. She is at once his favourite and a source of profound estrangement. The professor's refusal to accept any financial benefit from [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s estate — expressed in one of the novel's key speeches — drives a wedge between them:

> "Once and for all, Rosamond, understand that he owed me no more than I owed him. Nothing hurts me so much as to have any member of my family talk as if we had done something fine for that young man, brought him out, produced him. In a lifetime of teaching, I've encountered just one remarkable mind; but for that, I'd consider my good years largely wasted. And there can be no question of money between me and Tom Outland. I can't explain just how I feel about it, but it would somehow damage my recollections of him, would make that episode in my life commonplace like everything else. And that would be a great loss to me. I'm purely selfish in refusing your offer; my friendship with Outland is the one thing I will not have translated into the vulgar tongue." (004-023)

Rosamond's reaction is telling — her "face grew hard" (004-029), and she leaves with the cold p[[art-and-religion|art]]ing words: "I shan't forget" (004-032). This scene crystallizes the central conflict between [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s [[solitude-and-creativity|intellectual idealism]] and Rosamond's worldliness, a tension that persists throughout the novel.

## Marriage and Wealth

After [[tom-outland]]'s [[death-and-mortality|death]] in [[world-war-i|the Great War]], Rosamond marries [[louie-marsellus]], an engineer who [[ambition-and-success|success]]fully commercializes [[outland|Outland]]'s [[science-and-discovery|invention]]. The couple names their estate "[[outland|Outland]]", a gesture that infuriates those who knew Tom personally:

> "Outland, outlandish!" he muttered, while he fumbled about for an ashtray. Rosamond pretended not to hear him, but the dusky red on her cheeks crept a little farther toward her ears. (002-034)

[[louie-marsellus|Louie]] lavishes Rosamond with luxury — emeralds, furs, and a chauffeur-driven car — and she embraces the role of the [[money-and-materialism|wealth]]y hostess. Yet her prosperity is shadowed by the question of whether she and [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] have properly ac[[scholarship-and-knowledge|knowledge]]d the contributions of others, p[[art-and-religion|art]]icularly [[augusta|Dr. Crane]], who helped fund Tom's education.

## Role in the Novel

Rosamond functions as a foil to both her father and the [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] of [[tom-outland]]. Where [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] values the intellectual and the intangible, Rosamond moves confidently in the world of [[home-and-belonging|house]]s, emeralds, and social standing. She detests any mention of small sums of [[money-and-materialism|money]] — her father notes that "now that she was [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s heir, [she] detested to hear sums of [[money-and-materialism|money]] mentioned, especially small sums" (002-039). This fastidiousness about [[money-and-materialism|money]], paradoxically, marks her as deeply entangled with it.

Rosamond's journey from the shabby [[the-old-house|old house]] on [[lake-michigan]]'s shore to the grand estate of "[[outland|Outland]]" mirrors the novel's larger meditation on how [[memory-and-the-past|the past]] is preserved, commodified, or lost. Her story asks whether inheriting genius means honouring it or exploiting it — and whether the distinction can ever be clear.
