## Definition

Class in *The Professor's House* is not foregrounded as a political theme; it operates instead as a quiet, structural presence — the invisible architecture within which characters make their choices and measure their lives. Cather traces class through three distinct registers: the rural and immigrant origins of the new university students, the genteel academic poverty of the St. Peter household, and the aggressive social ascent of [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]], whose wealth propels him into a world of country clubs, Chicago hotels, and European travel that the Professor can only observe from the outside.

## In the Novel

The university is the novel's primary site of class observation. [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] and his colleague [[horace-langtry|Horace Langtry]] reflect on the changing character of the student body:

> "We have hosts of students, but they're a common sort." (003-047)

This is Langtry speaking, but the Professor does not disagree with the assessment, only with Langtry's cynical adaptation to it. "To the new generations of country and village boys now pouring into the university in such large numbers, Langtry had become an instructor in manners" (003-053). The "country and village boys" represent a demographic transformation of higher education — the post-war influx of students from farming communities and small towns, often the first in their families to attend university. The Professor honors their ambition; Langtry exploits their social insecurity.

Class awareness also shapes the novel's geography. The old house in Hamilton represents a world of modest, earned comfort; the new house, built with [[rosamond-marsellus|Rosamond]]'s money, represents an aspirational style the Professor finds alien. The contrasts multiply: [[chicago|Chicago]] vs. Hamilton, the Shoreham Hotel vs. the Professor's attic study, [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]'s "smart clothes" vs. the Professor's indifference to dress.

## The Outsider Figure

[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] is the novel's most radical class figure — an orphan who began as a railway call boy in Pardee, [[new-mexico|New Mexico]], was taken in by an engineer's family, taught himself Latin with a Belgian missionary, and arrived at Hamilton University with "a canvas telescope and a handful of uncut turquoises" (010-043). His trajectory — from manual labor to scientific breakthrough to posthumous wealth — crosses every class boundary the novel's society recognizes.

What distinguishes Tom is his indifference to class. He moves through the St. Peter household without self-consciousness; he forges an intellectual partnership with the Professor that transcends the teacher-student hierarchy; he discovers an ancient civilization on [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] and recognizes its value without seeking to own or exploit it. His classlessness is a form of freedom that none of the novel's other characters possess.

## Character Connections

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]**: Occupies the ambiguous class position of the academic — educated, respected, but financially modest. His refusal to enjoy the new house is partly a class refusal: he will not be displaced into a style of living that feels borrowed.
- **[[louie-marsellus]]**: The novel's great social climber — Jewish, self-made, exuberantly generous, and perpetually anxious about his standing. His gift-giving is a form of class negotiation.
- **[[tom-outland]]**: Transcends class through genius and indifference — the orphan who becomes a scientist, the prospector who becomes a scholar, the boy from the Southwest who haunts a midwestern university.
- **[[scott-mcgregor]]**: The "country boy" made good through education and journalism, resentful of Louie's easier path to influence and visibility.
- **[[lillian-st-peter]]**: Her social ambition — her delight in Louie's gifts, her enthusiasm for the new house — marks her as more class-conscious than her husband, more attuned to the social rewards of wealth.

## Critical Context

Cather's treatment of class has been read as characteristically oblique — she registers class almost entirely through style and sensibility rather than through economic analysis. The novel's class awareness is mediated through taste: what people wear, how they furnish their houses, what they notice and what they overlook. This aligns with Cather's broader aesthetic of indirection, but it also reflects the period's anxieties about social mobility in the aftermath of the Great War and the rapid expansion of American higher education.

## Related Concepts

- **[[money-and-materialism]]**: Class and money are related but distinct — the novel is interested in how wealth changes social perception, not just purchasing power.
- **[[academic-life]]**: The university is a class microcosm, with its own hierarchies of status, ancestry, and scholarly reputation.
- **[[ambition-and-success]]**: Class shapes the forms that [[ambition-and-success|ambition]] can take — Louie's commercial drive is coded as lower-class energy, the Professor's scholarly ambition as genteel.
- **[[pride]]**: [[pride|Pride]] is the emotion that mediates class — the Professor's pride in his work, Scott's wounded pride in his modest career, Louie's pride in his ability to give.
- **[[intellectual-vs-commercial]]**: The novel's central value conflict maps onto class — intellectual life is coded as old, rooted, disinterested; commercial life as new, mobile, acquisitive.
