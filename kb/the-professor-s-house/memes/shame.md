## Definition

Shame in The Professor's House is an undertow emotion — rarely named directly, but present in moments of exposure, inadequacy, and social comparison. The novel's characters are constantly measuring themselves against others: [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] against his [[ambition-and-success|success]]ful son-in-law [[louie-marsellus|Louie]], his daughters against each other, [[augusta|Augusta]] against the [[family-and-domesticity|family]] she serves. Shame is the feeling of falling short of some implicit standard — whether financial, [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al, or personal. Cather treats it not as a melodramatic confession but as a quiet tightening, a [[escape|withdrawal]], a moment when a character would rather not be seen.

## In the Novel

The Professor feels a flicker of embarrassment about his [[the-old-house|old house]] and modest circumstances, p[[art-and-religion|art]]icularly when confronted with his daughter's new [[money-and-materialism|wealth]]. As his wife notes:

> "It makes me a little ashamed." (005-011)

[[augusta|Augusta]], the sewing-woman, conceals a moment of vulnerability from the [[family-and-domesticity|family]]:

> "She was ashamed to tell any of us about it." (011-006)

The Professor's own response to his [[death-and-mortality|mortality]] in Book III carries a note of shame — the shame of being rescued, of being found in a state of collapse:

> "I was amazed and ashamed that a man of fifty, a man of his position, should come to such a pass." (023-015)

And at Tom's story's climax, the betrayal of trust produces the deepest shame:

> "I was utterly ashamed to go home to Roddy, dead broke." (023-024)

Even a minor character admits poverty with embarrassment:

> "as if he were ashamed to admit it." (014-008)

Throughout the novel, shame is the emotion that attends exposure — whether of poverty, failure, dependence, or moral inadequacy. The [[the-old-house|old house]] itself, with its shabby furniture and "sham upholstery" (028-007), is a site of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s quiet shame before the world's judgment.

## Character Connections

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]**: The Professor's shame is intellectual and social — shame about his modest [[home-and-belonging|house]], his thinning career, his [[family-and-domesticity|family]]'s slide into commercial values. His [[escape|withdrawal]] in Book III can be read as a [[escape|retreat]] from the shame of being seen in decline.

- **[[augusta]]**: The sewing-woman whose hidden poverty occasioned shame — her injury and its concealment suggest the [[pride|dignity]] of those who bear shame quietly.

- **[[lillian-st-peter]]**: Her remark about being ashamed of the [[the-old-house|old house]] reveals the social pressure [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] resists — she is the voice of the world's judgment.

- **[[tom-outland]]**: [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s diary records the deepest shame in the novel — the shame of having betrayed his friend Roddy, a shame so profound it drives the final action of his narrative.

## Critical Context

Shame has been a central category in literary criticism since the rise of affect theory in the 1990s. Unlike guilt, which involves a judgment about an action ("I did something wrong"), shame involves a judgment about the self ("I am wrong"). Cather was writing decades before affect theory, but her treatment of shame anticipates its insights: her characters experience shame not as a moral problem to be solved but as a condition to be endured. The novel's interest in surfaces — [[home-and-belonging|house]]s, clothing, social performances — is fundamentally an interest in shame's management, the [[art-and-religion|art]] of not being seen as one fears one is.

## Related Concepts

- **[[pride]]**: Shame's opposite and intimate companion — pride is what shame fears losing, and the novel's proudest characters are also its most shame-vulnerable.
- **[[money-and-materialism]]**: The novel's financial drama is also a shame drama — who can afford to hold their head up?
- **[[home-and-belonging]]**: The [[the-old-house|old house]] is the stage on which [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s class shame is performed before his wife's critical eye.
- **[[family-and-domesticity]]**: Family is where shame is often born — the gap between what a [[family-and-domesticity|family]] is and what it wishes to appear is shame's nursery.
