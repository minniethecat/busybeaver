# Kathleen McGregor

**Kathleen McGregor** (née [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]), called "Kitty" by her [[family-and-domesticity|family]], is the younger daughter of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter]] and [[lillian-st-peter]], and the sister of [[rosamond-marsellus]]. A gifted though self-limiting watercolour portraitist, she marries [[louie-marsellus|Scott McGregor]], a journalist and daily prose-poet. Kathleen's arc traces the dissolution of sisterly devotion into [[money-and-materialism|envy]], and her fierce independence — captured in her father's image of her as "an interrogation point" — makes her one of the novel's most sharply drawn characters.

## Appearance and Temperament

Cather gives Kathleen a distinctly modern look, in contrast to Rosamond's rich, operatic beauty:

> Kathleen, the younger daughter, looked even younger than she was — had the slender, undeveloped figure then very much in vogue. She was pale, with light hazel eyes, and her hair was hazel-coloured with distinctly green glints in it. To her father there was something very charming in the curious shadows her wide cheekbones cast over her cheeks, and in the spirited tilt of her head. Her figure in profile, he used to tell her, looked just like an interrogation point. (002-014)

The "interrogation point" metaphor captures Kathleen's essence: quizzical, questioning, precariously balanced. Where [[rosamond-marsellus]] is statuesque and self-assured, Kathleen is quick, angular, and vulnerable — a quality that evokes her father's deepest protective instinct.

## Artistic Gift

Kathleen possesses a genuine talent for portraiture, but one narrowly circumscribed by its subject:

> She had done several really good likenesses of her father — one, at least, was the man himself. With her mother she had no luck. She tried again and again, but the face was always hard, the upper lip longer than it seemed in life, the nose long and severe, and she made something cold and plaster-like of Lillian's beautiful complexion. "No, I don't see Mamma like that," she used to say, throwing out her chin. "Of course I don't! It just *comes* like that."

Her [[art-and-religion|art]] teacher urged her to study at the Art Institute in [[chicago|Chicago]], but Kathleen refused with characteristic bluntness: "No, I can't really do anybody but Papa, and I can't make a living painting him" (004-036). This self-[[scholarship-and-knowledge|knowledge]] — both clear-eyed and self-limiting — distinguishes her from [[rosamond-marsellus]], who rarely examines her own motives.

## Relationship with Her Father

The Professor's bond with Kathleen is the novel's most tender [[family-and-domesticity|family]] connection. When Kathleen was six, a bout of whooping-cough kept her [[home-and-belonging|home]] from a [[family-and-domesticity|family]] trip to Colorado, and she and her father spent a summer together that [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] recalls as a touchstone:

> She was only six, but he found her a square-dealing, dependable little creature. They worked out a satisfactory plan of life together. She was to play in the garden all morning, and was not on any account to disturb him in his study. After lunch he would take her to the lake or the woods, or he would read to her at home. She took pride in keeping her part of the contract.

This story of the bee sting — Kathleen waiting silently on the stairs, her fingers "puffed up like wee pink sausages," refusing to knock on her father's study door — exemplifies her fierce self-reliance. Years later, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] still walks across the [[hamilton|university]] campus to intercept her: "There was something too plucky, too 'I can-go-it-[[solitude-and-creativity|alone]],' about her quick step and jaunty little head; he didn't like it, it gave him a sudden pang" (004-033).

## The Broken Sisterhood

The novel's most painful emotional fracture is the estrangement between Kathleen and [[rosamond-marsellus]]. As children, Kathleen "adored her older sister and liked to wait on her, was always more excited about Rosie's new dresses and winter coat than about her own" (007-033). But [[rosamonds-marriage-to-louie|Rosamond's engagement]] to [[louie-marsellus]] — so soon after [[tom-outland]]'s [[death-and-mortality|death]] — shatters that devotion:

> St. Peter had never seen any change in it until Rosamond announced her engagement to Louie Marsellus. Then, all at once, Kathleen seemed to be done with her sister. Her father believed she couldn't forgive Rosie's forgetting Tom so quickly. (007-033)

The rivalry crystallizes around material things. In a devastating scene, Kathleen's envy of Rosamond's new furs turns her face literally green — "He had never happened to see that [[loss-and-change|change]] occur in a face before, and he had never realized to what an ugly, painful [[loss-and-change|transformation]] the common phrase 'green with envy' referred" (007-019). The scene ends with [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] at his study window, a "sharp pain" clutching his he[[art-and-religion|art]]: "Was it for this the light in [[outland|Outland]]'s [[work-and-vocation|labor]]atory used to burn so far into the night!" (007-027)

## Significance in the Novel

Kathleen represents a middle ground between [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s [[solitude-and-creativity|ascetic idealism]] and Rosamond's worldly [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]. She has enough [[art-and-religion|art]]istic sensibility to recognize quality — her father's portraits prove that — but not enough to sustain a creative life. She has enough moral feeling to resent Rosamond's forgetting of [[tom-outland]], but not enough to transcend the petty agonies of fur-coat envy.

In the novel's moral economy, Kathleen stands for loyalty without power: she remembers Tom, but she cannot protect his [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]]. She sees through [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]'s pretensions, but she can only mutter under her breath. Her green-tinged face at the window is one of Cather's most memorable images — a portrait of the decent, the disempowered, and the quietly he[[art-and-religion|art]]broken.
