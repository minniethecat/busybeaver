Paris is the European city most intimately woven into [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Godfrey St. Peter]]'s personal history. Unlike [[chicago|Chicago]] — a [[home-and-belonging|place]] of commerce and obligation — Paris represents [[childhood|youth]], romance, [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]], and what might have been. It is the city where [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] met [[lillian-st-peter|his wife]], where he spent solitary holidays as a young scholar, and where he planned but never took the defining vacation with [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]].

## A City of Origins

The Professor's life was decisively shaped in Paris. "[[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] had met his wife in Paris, when he was but twenty-four" (003-025). The encounter bound the city inseparably to [[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] and to the beginning of his adult life.

As a young man, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] spent "holidays he had spent [[solitude-and-creativity|alone]] in Paris, when he was living at Versailles" (009-025). These were the formative years of solitary [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]], before [[family-and-domesticity|marriage and children]] reshaped his existence.

The city also surfaces through objects and aesthetic [[memory-and-the-past|memory]]. The old Norwegian sewing woman hired for the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] was "a young Norwegian, trained in Paris" (002-017), lending Parisian [[power|authority]] to domestic crafts. And the sight of a p[[art-and-religion|art]]icular face makes others think of "your student days in Paris" (008-008) — the city as shorthand for [[childhood|youth]] and possibility.

## The Unrealized Summer

Paris becomes the novel's emblem of an unrealized future. [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] and [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] planned "a third summer together, in Paris, but it never came off. [[outland|Outland]] was dead before the next summer began" (025-008).

> "He had never got that vacation in Paris with Tom Outland. He had wanted to take Tom to the Luxembourg gardens, to the Louvre, to Notre Dame." (025-009)

The specific itinerary — the Luxembourg gardens, the Louvre, Notre Dame — renders the [[death-and-mortality|loss]] concrete. These are not abstract landmarks but [[home-and-belonging|place]]s where [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] wanted to watch Tom's face as he experienced them.

At the end of the novel, the aged Professor imagines standing "up in front of Notre Dame, in Paris, again, and see it standing there, more alive than he is" (027-017). The cathedral's permanence mocks his own [[death-and-mortality|mortality]], but the vision also holds Paris as the repository of everything that mattered: beauty, permanence, the ghost of Tom.

## Louie's Paris

[[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] offers his own Paris — a city of restaurants, shops, and antiquaries. He proposes a summer tour: "We'll lead a scholarly life in Paris. I have my own reasons for wishing to go there" (015-003).

[[lillian-st-peter|Lillian]] responds with enthusiasm: "I adore the shops in Paris" (015-005). [[louie-marsellus|Louie]] notes that "antiquaries are centralized in Paris" (015-009). [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|The Professor]] privately recoils.

> "He liked Paris, and he liked Louie. But one couldn't do one's own Paris with Louie — anymore than one could do one's own wife with Louie." (015-015)

The distinction between "Paris" and "one's own Paris" is essential to the novel's understanding of [[home-and-belonging|place]]. A city can be materially the same but experientially different depending on who one shares it with. [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]'s Paris — commercial, social, encyclopedic — is not the Paris of solitary [[scholarship-and-knowledge|scholarship]] or the Paris of [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]].
