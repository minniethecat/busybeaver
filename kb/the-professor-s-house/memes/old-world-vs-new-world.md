## Definition

The Professor's House is a novel poised on a threshold. The [[the-old-house|old house]] — repository of a lifetime's [[work-and-vocation|work]] and [[love-and-desire|marriage]] — will be abandoned for a new one. The old university — intimate, scholarly, European-inflected — is expanding into a modern institution. The old values — intellectual integrity, craftsmanship, quiet devotion — are being dis[[home-and-belonging|place]]d by commercial energy and social [[ambition-and-success|ambition]]. The Professor himself is caught between worlds: old enough to remember what is being lost, young enough to see what is arriving. This tension between the old world and the new is the novel's structural principle, organizing its three Books as a movement from present (Book I) to past (Book II) to aftermath (Book III).

## In the Novel

The novel's very first pages establish the old-[[home-and-belonging|house]]/new-[[home-and-belonging|house]] contrast that embodies the old/new tension. The Professor's study — with its worn furniture, its [[the-garden|French garden]], its accumulated papers — is the old world in miniature. The new [[home-and-belonging|house]], built with [[outland|Outland]] [[money-and-materialism|money]], represents everything [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] resists.

The university's [[loss-and-change|transformation]] mirrors the domestic [[loss-and-change|change]]. Where once there was a community of scholars, now there is an institution:

> "We have hosts of students, but they're a common sort." (003-047)

[[outland|Tom Outland's story]] in Book II is a sustained immersion in the old — the ancient cliff-dweller civilization, the pre-modern Southwest — [[home-and-belonging|place]]d against the novel's modern framing. The contrast is jarring and deliberate: Tom's world of [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] and friendship belongs to a different moral universe than [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]'s world of commerce and estates.

## Character Connections

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]**: The figure on the threshold — formed by the old world, living in the new, [[home-and-belonging|belonging]] fully to neither.

- **[[tom-outland]]**: Represents the old world at its most vital — [[science-and-discovery|discovery]], friendship, craftsmanship — frozen in [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] before it can be corrupted by the new.

- **[[louie-marsellus]]**: The new world personified — energetic, commercial, generous, but fundamentally incapable of understanding what the old world valued.

- **[[lillian-st-peter]]**: Chooses the new world — the new [[home-and-belonging|house]], social [[ambition-and-success|ambition]], [[chicago|Chicago]] and [[paris|Paris]] — while [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] remains loyal to the old.

- **[[augusta]]**: A figure from the old world who adapts to the new without losing her essence — the novel's quiet model of [[ambition-and-success|success]]ful transition.

## Critical Context

Published in 1925, The Professor's House belongs to a moment of acute American anxiety about modernization. The 1920s saw rapid urbanization, the expansion of universities, the rise of consumer culture — all registered in the novel's unease. Cather's own literary career spanned this transition: she began as a chronicler of the frontier (O Pioneers!, My Ántonia) and ended as an elegist of lost worlds. The Professor's House is arguably her most concentrated meditation on the cost of modernization, measured not in economic but in spiritual terms.
