## Definition

Money in *The Professor's House* is not merely a background condition but a dynamic force that reshapes relationships, reveals character, and drives the novel's central conflicts. [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s [[science-and-discovery|invention]] — a revolutionary vacuum device — generates a fortune after his [[death-and-mortality|death]], and the disposition of that wealth becomes the moral test that every major character must face. Cather presents money as simultaneously necessary, dangerous, and, in the wrong hands, spiritually corrosive. The novel draws a sharp contrast between the world of intellectual and creative values ([[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s study, the garden, the cliff-dweller ruins) and the world of commerce and consumption (the new [[home-and-belonging|house]], [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]]'s business dealings, [[chicago|Chicago]] society).

## In the Novel

Money enters the novel's plot through a single transformative event: [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s will, which bequeaths his [[science-and-discovery|invention]] to his fiancée [[rosamond-marsellus|Rosamond St. Peter]]. The [[science-and-discovery|invention]] succeeds commercially under the management of [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]], Rosamond's husband, generating wealth that reshapes the [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] [[family-and-domesticity|family]]'s circumstances. The Professor's new [[home-and-belonging|house]], built with money from Tom's estate, is a visible symbol of this [[loss-and-change|transformation]] — and [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s refusal to feel at [[home-and-belonging|home]] in it is his quiet rebellion against the encroachment of money into the realm of values he holds sacred.

Cather uses [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s old landlord [[appelhoff|Appelhoff]] to establish the theme early: the German farmer is "good-natured and lenient about everything but spending money" (001-006). This careful, Old World attitude toward money sets the baseline against which the novel's more aggressive commercial characters are measured. When [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] arrives, he represents a new, confident American commercialism that [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] finds both impressive and distasteful.

The moral question of the money — who deserves it, what Tom would have wanted — generates the novel's sharpest conflicts. [[dr-crane|Dr. Crane]], who once helped Tom, believes he has a claim on the fortune. The Professor, by contrast, wants nothing to do with it: he would

> "I would rather have done with that money than to have built a house with it" (001-049). His daughter Rosamond's embrace of wealth, and her husband Louie's comfortable navigation of the commercial world, alienate the Professor from his own family.

[[tom-outlands-invention|Tom's invention]] itself is kept deliberately vague — Cather is not interested in the technology but in its human consequences. The wealth it generates becomes a kind of moral reagent, exposing the values of everyone it touches.

## Character Connections

- **[[Professor Godfrey St. Peter]]**: His distaste for the money from [[tom-outlands-invention|Tom's invention]] is almost physical. He refuses any claim on it and finds the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] built with it oppressive.
- **[[Tom Outland]]**: The unwitting source of the fortune. His intentions for the money — "he wanted you to have it" (004-019) — become a matter of dispute and interpretation.
- **[[Louie Marsellus]]**: The commercializer of Tom's [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]]. His business acumen and social polish represent everything [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] distrusts, yet he is not a villain — Cather makes him complex, even sympathetic.
- **[[Rosamond St. Peter]]**: Tom's former fiancée and the legal beneficiary of his estate. Her [[loss-and-change|transformation]] from Tom's sweethe[[art-and-religion|art]] to a wealthy [[chicago|Chicago]] socialite embodies the novel's fears about money's power to [[loss-and-change|change]] character.
- **[[Dr. Crane]]**: His claim on Tom's money — based on "long friendship and helpfulness" (012-025) — raises uncomfortable questions about whether friendship can coexist with financial expectation.

## Critical Context

Cather's treatment of money reflects both her own background (she grew up in a [[family-and-domesticity|family]] of modest means and understood financial precarity) and the broader cultural anxieties of 1920s America. The novel was published in 1925, at the height of the postwar economic boom, when questions about the compatibility of commercial values with [[art-and-religion|art]]istic and intellectual life were urgent. The figure of [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] — the pure-he[[art-and-religion|art]]ed inventor whose creation is exploited by others — has been read as an allegory of the [[art-and-religion|art]]ist in a commercial society. Critics have also noted the [[power|influence]] of Henry Adams's critique of modern capitalism and Thorstein Veblen's theory of conspicuous consumption on Cather's portrayal of the [[louie-marsellus|Marsellus]] lifestyle.

- **[[the-old-house]]**: The Professor's resistance to the new [[home-and-belonging|house]] is a resistance to the money that built it — the [[the-old-house|old house]] represents a world before commercialization.
- **[[american-vs-european]]**: Old World attitudes toward money ([[appelhoff|Appelhoff]]'s thrift, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s European education) clash with New World commercial energy.
- **[[love-and-desire]]**: The novel distinguishes between disinterested [[love-and-desire|love]] and the [[love-and-desire|desire]]s that money enables and corrupts.
