Washington, D.C. appears in the novel exclusively through [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s first-person narrative in Book II, where it serves as the theater of his disillusionment with institutional [[power|authority]].

Sent by [[father-duchene|Father Duchene]] to report the [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] of the [[cliff-dweller-civilization|cliff-dweller ruins]] on [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] to the Smithsonian, Tom arrives with high hopes and a telescope bag full of pottery.

Instead, he spends months navigating a city governed by indifference, self-interest, and e[[work-and-vocation|labor]]ate rituals of bureaucratic [[escape|evasion]].

The Washington episode is the pivot on which Tom's story turns. His failure leads directly to the [[death-and-mortality|loss]] of the [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts, his rupture with [[rodney-blake|Roddy Blake]], and his [[escape|flight]] to [[hamilton|Hamilton]] and friendship with [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|Professor St. Peter]].

## Arrival and First Impressions

Tom arrives in Washington "one cold bright January morning," stepping off the train "just behind the Capitol building" (023-001).

His first response is awed optimism: "I stood for a long while watching the white dome against a flashing blue sky, with a very religious feeling."

After a week of sightseeing — "the most sensible thing I did while I was there. For that week I was wonderfully happy" (023-001) — Tom begins the [[work-and-vocation|work]] of securing government interest.

His [[childhood|innocence]] is methodically dismantled. He is sent from the Indian Commission to the Smithsonian, through a labyrinth of appointments never kept and officials never available.

A clerk tells him that Washington is "full of such things; there were cases of them in the cellar at the Smithsonian that they'd never taken the trouble to unpack" (023-003).

## The Bureaucratic Labyrinth

Tom's experience of Washington is structured around waiting. He spends days "studying the patterns of the rugs, and the shoes of the patient waiters who came as regularly as I" (023-005).

The rhythm of official Washington is perversely inverted: "Nearly everyone else had gone out to lunch — that seemed to be the one thing they did regularly in Washington" (023-005).

His sole ally is [[virginia-ward|Virginia Ward]], a stenographer with "kind eyes and soft Southern voice." She teaches him the city's unwritten rules:

> "If you want to get attention from anybody in Washin'ton," she said, "ask them to lunch. People here will do almost anything for a good lunch." (023-007)

Following her advice, Tom takes the Smithsonian secretary to lunch at the Shoreham after twenty-two days. The lunch costs him a five-dollar bottle of Chateau d'Yquem and yields nothing but the secretary's [[pride|vanity]].

"I was amazed and ashamed that a man of fifty, a man of the world, a scholar with ever so many degrees, should find it worth his while to show off before a boy" (023-015).

## The Smithsonian and the Exposition

The Director of the Smithsonian and his staff "had one interest which dwarfed every other" (023-021). There was an International Exposition in Europe, and everyone was angling for juries and congresses.

[[virginia-ward|Virginia Ward]] confirms the truth: "They don't care much about dead and gone Indians. What they do care about is going to [[paris|Paris]], and getting another ribbon on their coats" (023-021).

This is the novel's sharpest critique of institutional [[scholarship-and-knowledge|knowledge]]. The people charged with preserving American antiquities are indifferent to them, preoccupied with European honors.

## Life with the Bixbys

Tom boards with a young couple whose lives reveal the spiritual poverty of the capital's clerical class. He watches each evening as "all the hundreds of clerks come pouring out of that big building at sunset" (023-020).

Tom's judgment is compassionate but damning: "He wasn't lazy, he wasn't a fool, and he meant to be honest; but he was intimidated by that miserable sort of dep[[art-and-religion|art]]mental life" (023-020).

## The City at Sunset

Tom's most lyrical memories of Washington are solitary ones, taken between hours of fruitless waiting:

> "During my days of waiting for appointments, I used to walk for hours around the fence that shuts in the White House grounds, and watch the Washington monument colour with those beautiful sunsets, until the time when all the clerks streamed out of the Treasury building and the War and Navy. Thousands of them, all more or less like the couple I lived with. They seemed to me like people in slavery, who ought to be free." (023-020)

He remembers "those beautiful, hazy, sad sunsets, white columns and green shrubbery, and the monument shaft still pink while the stars were coming out" (023-020).

The juxtaposition defines Tom's vision of Washington: physical beauty and human diminishment. His final word is a comparison: "Queer, how much more depressing they are than [[work-and-vocation|work]]men coming out of a factory" (023-024).

## The Aftermath

Tom leaves Washington "wiser than I came." He wants "nothing but to get back to the mesa and live a free life and breathe free air, and never, never again to see hundreds of little black-coated men pouring out of white buildings" (023-024).

During his absence, [[rodney-blake|Roddy Blake]] sold the entire collection of [[art-and-religion|art]]ifacts to a German dealer. When Tom confronts him, Roddy's defense is unanswerable: "If there was anybody in Washington that cared a damn, I wouldn't have sold 'em. But you pretty well found out there ain't" (023-047).

> "All I'd been through in Washington was nothing to what I went through that night. I thought Blake must have lost his mind. I didn't for a minute believe he'd meant to sell me out, but I cursed his stupidity and presumption." (023-034)

Washington, meant to be the solution, becomes the cause of catastrophe. The city that was supposed to preserve the mesa's treasures becomes the instrument of their dispersal.

Tom returns west with nothing — except a deepened understanding of what he values, and a wound in his friendship with Roddy that will never fully heal. Years later, [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] calls it Tom's "fruitless errand" to Washington (025-007).
