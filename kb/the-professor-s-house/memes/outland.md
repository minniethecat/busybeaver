## Definition

"Outland" in The Professor's House is more than a surname — it is the novel's central myth. [[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] functions as an absent-presence: dead before the novel's present action begins, yet shaping every major character's life through his [[science-and-discovery|invention]], his [[memory-and-the-past|memory]], and the meanings others project onto him. The name itself becomes a symbol, with [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]] naming his estate "Outland" and [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] [[escape|retreat]]ing into [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] of his lost student. Cather uses Outland's story — told in full only in Book II, "[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]]'s Story" — as the moral and emotional fulcrum of the entire novel.

## In the Novel

[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] appears directly only in Book II, where his first-person narrative recounts his [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] of the [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]] cliff-dweller ruins and his friendship with [[rodney-blake|Roddy Blake]]. But his presence pervades the entire novel:

> "And it was there he and Tom Outland used to sit and talk half through the night." (001-007)

The Professor met Tom as a student, and their friendship became the defining relationship of [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]]'s later life:

> "Just once, in his student Tom Outland — and he had foretold." (001-042)

After Tom's [[death-and-mortality|death]] in [[world-war-i|the Great War]], his vacuum/gas [[science-and-discovery|invention]] becomes the source of the [[louie-marsellus|Marsellus]] fortune. [[louie-marsellus|Louie Marsellus]], who never knew Tom, names his estate "Outland" and marries Rosamond, who was once Tom's fiancée. This commercialization of Tom's [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]] creates deep tensions within the [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|St. Peter]] [[family-and-domesticity|family]].

> "We have named our place for Tom Outland, a brilliant young American scientist and explorer." (002-024)

The Professor feels increasingly alienated from the financial exploitation of his protégé's [[work-and-vocation|work]]:

> "He had been fiercely jealous of Tom Outland. As he left the house, he was wondering why." (003-024)

## Character Connections

- **[[professor-godfrey-st-peter]]**: Tom was [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]]'s most brilliant student, the one relationship that justified his entire [[scholarship-and-knowledge|academic life]]. The Professor's [[death-and-mortality|grief]] and [[memory-and-the-past|memory]] of Tom form the emotional core of Book I and III (001-007, 001-042).

- **[[louie-marsellus]]**: Married Rosamond and commercialized [[tom-outlands-invention|Tom's invention]], naming his estate "Outland" — yet never met Tom. His enthusiasm for the Outland [[fatherhood-and-legacy|legacy]] irritates [[professor-godfrey-st-peter|the Professor]] (002-024, 002-028).

- **[[rosamond-marsellus]]**: Tom's former fiancée, now heir to his [[science-and-discovery|invention]] through her [[love-and-desire|marriage]] to [[louie-marsellus|Louie]]. Her [[loss-and-change|transformation]] from Tom's modest sweethe[[art-and-religion|art]] to [[money-and-materialism|wealth]]y socialite is a key subplot (002-039).

- **[[kathleen-mcgregor]]**: Tom's student and friend; she too benefits from the Outland fortune, though with more ambivalence than Rosamond (002-029).

- **[[rodney-blake|Roddy Blake]]**: Tom's companion at [[blue-mesa|Blue Mesa]], featured in Book II. Their friendship and its painful rupture reveals Tom's idealism.

## Critical Context

[[tom-outland|Tom Outland]] functions as what critics have called a "vanished center" — a character whose absence organizes the narrative more powerfully than presence could. His story in Book II is set in the American Southwest, drawing on Cather's own visits to Mesa Verde. Scholars read the Outland narrative as Cather's critique of commercialization: Tom's pure [[science-and-discovery|discovery]] and friendship are converted, after his [[death-and-mortality|death]], into [[money-and-materialism|wealth]], competition, and [[family-and-domesticity|family]] discord. The name "Outland" itself suggests the outsider, the unexplored territory — both geographic and spiritual — that the novel's other characters can only approach but never inhabit.
