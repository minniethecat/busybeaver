## Overview

On December 1, 1933, the National Social Equality League — under the presidency of Dr. Beard — convened a conference of the country's outstanding Negro leaders at its New York headquarters. The gathering was prompted by the Black-No-More sanitariums' mass transformation of Black Americans into whiteness, which threatened to eliminate the very constituency that these leaders represented. The conference formalized a resolution demanding federal intervention, but the resolution was coldly dismissed, revealing the political impotence of traditional civil-rights organizations in the novel's satirical universe.

## Context

By late 1933, Black-No-More, Incorporated had processed hundreds of thousands of Black Americans, operating sanitariums in twenty cities (004-037). The organization's success created an existential crisis for the established Negro leadership class. Organizations like the National Social Equality League, the Back-to-Africa Society, and similar groups derived their funding and influence from representing Black constituencies — constituencies that were now vanishing into whiteness at an accelerating rate.

The conference was called under the shadow of this crisis. The Black press had been warning of the threat for months, but the leadership class had failed to mount any effective opposition to Black-No-More, Incorporated (005-047). The conference represented a last effort to mobilize collective action before the traditional Negro community dissolved entirely.

## Narrative Account

The conference assembled at the N.S.E.L. headquarters. Dr. Beard, presiding, called the meeting to order and introduced the agenda: a unified response to the Black-No-More threat (005-048). The assembled leaders included representatives from civil-rights organizations, the Negro press, and the clergy.

### The Investigation Deepens

The atmosphere was tense with mutual suspicion. Like most Negro leaders of the era, many of the attendees "deified the black woman but had a white wife," as the narrator sardonically observes (005-048). Each leader suspected the others of insufficient education or questionable motives. Dr. Beard himself had reportedly taken the Black-No-More treatment — a secret whispered among the attendees (005-037).

Despite these tensions, the conference produced a unanimous resolution demanding that Attorney General Brybe investigate and close the Black-No-More sanitariums. The resolution argued that the sanitariums constituted a public menace, disrupting the social order and endangering the existing economic and political structures of the Negro community (005-078).

The resolution was forwarded to the Attorney General, who received it with "much malicious satisfaction" and dismissed it without action (005-099). The traditional Negro leadership, the novel implies, was as irrelevant in the face of technological transformation as the white supremacists they opposed.

## Aftermath

The conference's failure marked a turning point in the novel's political satire. Traditional civil-rights organizations — the N.S.E.L., the Back-to-Africa Society, the Negro Data League — proved incapable of responding to the Black-No-More phenomenon through conventional political channels. Their constituency, offered a technological escape from racial oppression, voted with their feet.

In subsequent chapters, these organizations continue to decline. Charles Spelling and other leaders attempt to negotiate with Matthew Fisher and the Knights of Nordica, hoping to leverage the Knights' political influence against the sanitariums (006-071)(006-098). These efforts also fail, underscoring the novel's satirical thesis: racial politics, built on the mutual dependence of white supremacists and Black leaders, collapses when the biological marker of race becomes mutable.

## Key Passages

> Dr. Beard called a conference of all of the outstanding Negro leaders of the country to assemble at the New York headquarters of the National Social Equality League. (005-047)

> Like most Negro leaders, he deified the black woman but had a white wife. (005-048)

> Not one of it believed that the other Negro leaders possessed sufficient education, intelligence, or integrity to lead the race. (005-049)

> The Attorney General received the resolution from the conference of Negro leaders. He pursed his lips and reached for the next letter. (005-078)

> The Attorney General forwarded the resolution to the Negro leaders with much malicious satisfaction and the curt advice that they take the matter to the state authorities. (005-099)


This episode illustrates the novel's broader satirical method: historical events are not merely referenced but structurally inverted, with the machinery of racial power consistently producing outcomes opposite to those intended by its architects.

This conference represents the novel's most concentrated satire of Black respectability politics — the belief, shared by leaders across the ideological spectrum, that racial progress requires the endorsement of white power rather than its dismantling.
