## Overview

The Angelic Orders are the women's auxiliary of the Knights of Nordica — a detail that parallels the real Ku Klux Klan's "Women of the Ku Klux Klan" organization. Their name, like "Nordica" itself, participates in the novel's satire of grandiose racial nomenclature: white supremacist women as "angels" defending a "Nordic" racial order.

## Role in the Narrative

The Angelic Orders appear in the background of the Knights' activities, representing the gendered dimension of white supremacist organizing — women as the symbolic justification for racial violence ("protecting white womanhood") and as active participants in the enforcement of the color line.

## Satirical Function

Exposes the role of white women in sustaining white supremacy, challenging the sentimental narrative that positioned white women as passive objects of protection rather than active agents of racial enforcement.

## Historical Parallel: The Women of the Ku Klux Klan

The Women of the Ku Klux Klan (WKKK) was a real organization founded in 1923, operating as the women's auxiliary of the second Klan. At its peak, the WKKK claimed over 250,000 members and was deeply involved in Klan electoral politics, education campaigns, and social welfare programs that served only white Protestants. The WKKK's public rhetoric emphasized "patriotism," "morality," and the protection of the home — language that masked its core function of consolidating white racial solidarity through women's social networks. Schuyler's "Angelic Orders" is a thinly veiled satire of this organization, compressing its grandiose self-presentation into a name that suggests heavenly purity while serving hellish ends.

## Gendered Dimensions of the Color Line

The inclusion of the Angelic Orders in the novel's architecture serves an important analytic function: it insists that white supremacy is not solely a masculine project. White women in the novel — from the Angelic Orders' rank-and-file to figures like [[matthew-fisher|Helen Fisher]] — are shown as active participants in and beneficiaries of the racial order. The "angels" metaphor is doubly satirical: it invokes both the traditional association of white women with purity and the reality that this purity was enforced through terror.

## Narrative Economy

Like many of the novel's satirical institutions, the Angelic Orders receive only a few mentions in the text. Schuyler's method is to name an institution with enough precision to evoke the real-world referent, then allow the reader's knowledge to fill in the details. The "Angelic Orders" is thus a form of shorthand: the name itself contains the critique.
