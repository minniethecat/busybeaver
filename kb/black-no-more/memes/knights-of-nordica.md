## Overview

The Knights of Nordica are [[george-schuyler]]'s transparent parody of the 1920s Ku Klux Klan — an organization whose very name invokes an imaginary Nordic past ("Nordica") to justify [[white-supremacy]]. Founded by the cynical [[rev-givens|Reverend Henry Givens]] with "only a hundred members" (004-032), the Knights grow rapidly as the [[black-no-more-treatment]] spreads white panic.

Unlike the real Klan's claims to moral crusade, the Knights are portrayed as an entirely commercial enterprise. Membership costs "five dollars per head" (004-075), and the organization's leader privately "despised" his followers (004-041). The Knights' trajectory from marginal Atlanta fraternal order to national political machine to presidential vehicle mirrors the novel's central satirical thesis: American racism is, at bottom, a confidence game.

## History and Growth

### Founding

The Knights were born from Reverend Givens's entrepreneurial recognition of a market opportunity. When [[max-disher|Max Disher]] first encounters the organization in Atlanta, he reads a newspaper advertisement: "The Knights of Nordica Want 10,000 Atlanta White Men" (004-010). The ad's directness — summoning ten thousand white men to one hall — captures the organization's method: mass mobilization through fear, priced for mass consumption.

Matthew Fisher (Max Disher's post-treatment identity) quickly assesses the opportunity: "Givens must have some money, he thought" (004-021). His calculation proves correct. By presenting himself as an anthropologist who can provide "scientific" racial statistics, Fisher becomes the Knights' Grand Exalted Giraw (008-005), and the organization's growth accelerates exponentially.

### The Economics of Hate

The Knights' business model is brilliantly simple. The Black-No-More treatment makes it impossible to visually identify Black people — and therefore impossible for white people to know whom to discriminate against. The Knights sell certainty: for five dollars, a white man can join an organization that promises to identify, track, and suppress the hidden Negro population.

> "Unlike Givens, he had no belief in the racial integrity nonsense nor any confidence in the racial purity nonsense. But Givens had the reputation of having made over fifty thousand dollars in one year from the sale of memberships in his organization and Matthew felt that he could swallow his scruples for that kind of money." (004-041) (004-041)

This passage is the novel's economic key. The Knights succeed not because anyone believes in their ideology but because the ideology generates revenue. Matthew "swallows his scruples" for the same reason Givens founded the organization: money.

### From Fraternal Order to Political Machine

The Knights' evolution from hate group to political party follows the novel's satirical logic. As the organization's membership swells — "the real white people were pouring into the Knights" (006-092) — its ambitions expand. Givens begins broadcasting on national radio, delivering addresses on "The Menace of Negro Blood" (009-028) that draw enormous audiences.

By the Democratic National Convention, the Knights have become the party's dominant faction. "The sodden and nodding members of the Committee on Permanent Organization insisted on the selection of Rev. Givens" for the presidential nomination (010-046). The Imperial Grand Wizard of a hate group becomes the nominee of a major political party — and Schuyler barely pauses to comment, because the continuity is simply taken as obvious.

## Organizational Structure

| Title | Holder | Function |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Imperial Grand Wizard | [[rev-givens|Rev. Henry Givens]] | Supreme leader and public face |
| Grand Exalted Giraw | [[max-disher|Matthew Fisher]] | Chief statistician and propaganda director |
| Grand Treasurer | B. Gump | Financial operations |
| State Organizers | Various | Local recruitment and dues collection |

The Knights' hierarchy parodies both fraternal orders and corporate structure. Each title is a transparent Klan reference — "Imperial Grand Wizard," "Grand Exalted Giraw" — but the actual work is indistinguishable from any sales organization: recruit members, collect dues, grow the territory.

## Satirical Function

### The Name: "Nordica"

The name "Nordica" mocks the pseudoscientific Nordic racial theories popular in 1920s eugenics discourse. By adopting this invented classical name, the Knights expose the fundamental absurdity of white supremacist mythology — there never was a "Nordica," just as there never was a pure white race awaiting rediscovery.

### The Inability to Identify Members

The novel's funniest and most politically pointed sequence involves the Knights' existential crisis: they cannot tell whether their own members are "really" white or are Black-No-More patients who have infiltrated the organization. Matthew Fisher, their own Grand Exalted Giraw, is a former Black man — and the Knights never discover this. The joke is devastating: an organization dedicated to racial purity cannot identify race, rendering its entire mission circular and absurd.

### White Supremacy as Business

The Knights represent Schuyler's most complete satirical dissection of organized racism. By presenting the Klan-analogue as a purely commercial enterprise — run by a man who doesn't believe in white supremacy, staffed by people who joined for social status, funded by dues from men who need an enemy — Schuyler reduces the most violent ideology in American history to a balance sheet.
