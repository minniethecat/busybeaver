## Overview

Cincinnati occupies a unique position in the geography of *Black No More*: it is both a site of hope — one of the border cities where [[crookman-sanitarium|Black-No-More sanitarium]]s offer transformation to Southern Black migrants — and the site of the novel's most horrific act of violence. The mob attack on a Crookman hospital in Cincinnati, in which "a dozen babies were burned to death" [010-064], marks the point at which the racial conflict the novel has been satirizing erupts into unmistakable tragedy.

## In the Narrative

### Chuck Foster's Migration (Ch. 3)

Cincinnati first appears as a waypoint in [[chuck-foster|Chuck Foster]]'s personal migration narrative. The son of a Birmingham barber, [[chuck-foster|Foster]] "had drifted first to Cincinnati, then to Pittsburgh and finally to New York" [003-017]. In this brief reference, Cincinnati is established as a stepping-stone city — a place Black Southerners pass through on their way to the greater opportunities of the North. It is part of the Great Migration route that the novel both documents and satirizes.

### Border Sanitarium (Ch. 5)

As [[black-no-more-inc|Black-No-More, Incorporated]], scales its operations, Cincinnati becomes one of the strategic border locations for sanitariums. "There were none of these havens in [[the-southland|the South]] because of the hostility of the bulk of white people but there were many all along the border between the two sections, at such places as [[washington-dc|Washington, DC]], Baltimore, Cincinnati, Louisville, Evansville, Cairo, St. Louis and Denver" [005-044].

The border strategy is deliberate: close enough to [[the-southland|the South]] to serve the masses of Black migrants but on the Northern side of the line, beyond the reach of Southern legal hostility. Cincinnati, sitting on the Ohio River — the historical boundary between slave and free states — resumes its nineteenth-century role as a gateway between racial regimes.

### The Mob Attack (Ch. 10)

The novel's most unsparingly violent passage occurs in Cincinnati. After months of mounting anti-Black-No-More agitation — "fiery editorials, radio addresses, pamphlets, posters and platform speeches" [010-064] — the rhetoric becomes action:

> "Finally, emboldened and inflamed by fiery editorials, radio addresses, pamphlets, posters and platform speeches, a mob seeking to protect white womanhood in Cincinnati attacked a Crookman hospital, drove several women into the streets and set fire to the building. A dozen babies were burned to death and others, hastily removed by their mothers, were recognized as mulattoes. The newspapers published names and addresses. Many of the women were very prominent socially either in their own right or because of their husbands." [010-064]

This passage is the novel's pivot point — the moment satire gives way to something darker. The phrase "seeking to protect white womanhood" is [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]]'s bitterest irony: the mob's stated motive (protection) produces its opposite (the destruction of white women's babies, the exposure of white women's racial secrets). Every element of the passage is structured as a reversal: the mob protects by destroying, the women are rescued into public humiliation, the "prominent" are exposed as "[[mulatto|mulattoes]]."

The detail that "the newspapers published names and addresses" [010-064] brings the violence into the realm of the specific and the permanent. The women are not merely attacked — they are identified, cataloged, destroyed socially as well as physically. The mob's fire kills the babies; the newspapers' publication kills the mothers' social existence.

## Significance

Cincinnati's function in the novel is to show the human cost of the racial hysteria [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]] has been satirizing. The earlier chapters treat racial ideology as absurd — [[knights-of-nordica|the Knights]] of [[knights-of-nordica|Nordica]] as buffoons, the [[anglo-saxon-association|Anglo-Saxon Association]] as pretentious frauds, the Negro leaders as self-interested hypocrites. But the Cincinnati attack reminds the reader that absurd ideologies produce real violence. The babies burned in the Crookman hospital are not symbols; they are casualties.

Cincinnati also serves as a narrative demonstration of how racial passing threatens the social order not because it is wrong but because it exposes what has always been true: that many "white" Americans have Black ancestry. The mothers whose babies are "recognized as [[mulatto|mulattoes]]" were passing as white — successfully, perhaps for years — until the mob's fire stripped away their secret. The violence of the attack mirrors the violence of the exposure: both destroy lives, one through fire, the other through truth.
