## Overview

Helen Givens is the novel's most concentrated satire of Southern white womanhood. She appears first as a voice — "I never dance with niggers" — and then as a figure of desire who haunts Max's imagination. Her eventual marriage to "Matthew Fisher" (Max in white form) is the novel's cruelest and funniest irony: the white woman who would not dance with a Black man marries that same man, unaware of his identity.

## Biography

Helen is introduced as "some rich broad from Atlanta up here for the holidays" (001-057). She is "twenty and quite confident" (004-056), a product of the white Southern elite whose racial prejudices are presented as reflexive rather than considered — the unthinking entitlement of her class and region. Her marriage to Matthew Fisher is arranged by her father, Reverend Givens, who sees in "Fisher" a useful ally for the Knights of Nordica.

## Character Analysis

Helen is less a character than a satirical function: she represents the white Southern woman whose "protection" justified the entire apparatus of Jim Crow violence. That she marries a whitened Negro without knowing it is the novel's revenge fantasy — but Schuyler denies the reader the satisfaction of seeing her discover the truth. The secret is kept, and Helen remains happily married to a man whose identity she would find monstrous if she knew it.
