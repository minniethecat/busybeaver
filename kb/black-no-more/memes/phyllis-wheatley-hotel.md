## Overview

The Phyllis Wheatley Hotel is where [[dr-crookman|Dr. Junius Crookman]] stays while announcing his discovery to [[negro-weeklies|the Black press]], and where [[max-disher|Max]] Disher first encounters him in person. Named after Phillis Wheatley (1753–1784), the first published African American poet, the hotel is one of Harlem's establishments named for Black historical figures — a practice that signaled racial pride and institutional autonomy. The irony, of course, is that the announcement made here will eventually destroy the very Black institutional world that named a hotel after Wheatley.

## In the Narrative

[[max-disher|Max]] Disher, having read about Crookman's discovery in the newspaper, rushes to the Phyllis Wheatley Hotel: "Crookman was staying at the Phyllis Wheatley Hotel. Why not go and see him?" (001-072). There he finds "a large crowd" of reporters "from the Negro weeklies" (001-074) and encounters Crookman himself — "tall, wiry, ebony black" (001-074). It is in this hotel that [[max-disher|Max]] volunteers to be Crookman's first patient, setting the entire plot in motion.

## Significance

The hotel's name — honoring a Black poet who was enslaved as a child and achieved literary fame in the 18th century — creates a layered irony. Wheatley represents Black achievement against impossible odds; the treatment announced in her namesake hotel aims to eliminate the very Blackness that made her achievement remarkable. [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]] does not comment on this irony explicitly, but it pervades the scene: Black institutional pride is about to be rendered obsolete by Black scientific achievement.
