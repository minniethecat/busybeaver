## Overview

The Black-No-More treatment is the science-fictional MacGuffin at the heart of Schuyler's novel: a medical procedure that transforms any Black person into a white person in three days, for fifty dollars. Dr. Junius Crookman's invention uses "glandular control and electrical nutrition" (P04-003) to alter skin color, hair texture, and facial features. The treatment is permanent but not hereditary — "the offspring of these whitened Negroes will be Negroes" (003-010) — which becomes both a source of terror for white supremacists and the engine of the novel's comedic escalation.

## Historical Context

Schuyler's treatment fictionalizes real anxieties of the 1920s–1930s: skin-lightening creams, hair straighteners, and other cosmetic products marketed to Black consumers promised "improvement" toward white standards of beauty. The Preface explicitly connects the fictional treatment to "Kink-No-More," a real product advertised for "the immediate and unfailing straightening of the most stubborn Negro hair" (P04-001). Schuyler's genius is to literalize the logic of these products: if straight hair and lighter skin are desirable, why not skip the maintenance and become fully white?

## In the Narrative

The treatment is introduced in the first chapter when Max Disher reads a newspaper account of Crookman's discovery while recuperating from a nightclub humiliation (001-072). Max volunteers as the first patient: "In three days the Negro becomes to all appearances a Caucasian" (001-080). The treatment's commercial success is immediate and overwhelming — "one hundred a day for fourteen days" at "fifty bucks a throw" generates "seventy thousand dollars" (003-012, 003-013). By the novel's middle chapters, hundreds of thousands have undergone the procedure, causing cascading economic, political, and social crises as the color line dissolves.

## Satirical Treatment

The treatment's satirical power lies in its simplicity. By reducing America's most intractable social problem to a commodity, Schuyler exposes the economic basis of racism. The treatment costs fifty dollars — accessible to the masses but not free — and generates fortunes for Crookman and his investors. The real satire is that white America's terror of "racial contamination" is revealed as a fear not of Blackness itself but of the impossibility of identifying it: once anyone can be white, whiteness loses its meaning. The non-hereditary nature of the treatment ensures the anxiety is permanent.

## Key Passages
