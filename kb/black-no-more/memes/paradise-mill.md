## Overview

The Blickdoff-Hortzenboff cotton mill in Paradise, South Carolina, is the novel's most concentrated representation of the Southern industrial economy. Owned by "two Germans who came to this country after the war," the mill employs "one thousand hands," owns "all of the houses in Paradise," and operates "all of the stores" — a company town where economic power is total. Workers earn "around fifteen dollars a week" for an "eleven hours" day, and when the company "speeded up the work so much that the help say they cannot stand the pace," the workers turn to the Knights of Nordica — the organization they belong to — for help unionizing. Matthew Fisher's response reveals the class logic beneath the racial ideology: the Knights exist to protect capital, not labor, regardless of race. (007-007)

## Narrative Significance

The Paradise Mill sequence is the novel's sharpest depiction of the economic infrastructure underlying racial politics. The workers are Knights of Nordica members; they assume the organization will support them against the German owners. Fisher's role is to manage this contradiction — to ensure that white racial solidarity does not evolve into class solidarity. The mill owners, Blickdoff and Hortzenboff, are rendered as comic stereotypes ("a beer barrel on stilts"), but the labor conditions they preside over are described with documentary precision.
