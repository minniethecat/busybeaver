## Overview

Mrs. Crookman appears in a single scene in Chapter 3, entering her husband's office as the Black-No-More business is being formalized. She is described as "petite, stylishly-dressed" and "a beautiful little octoroon," and Crookman's partners Hank Johnson and Chuck Foster immediately note "how easily she could 'pass for white,' which would have been something akin to a piece of anthracite coal passing for black" (003-???). The observation is thick with irony: Crookman, who has invented the technology to make racial passing universally available, is married to a woman who can already pass without it. Her presence in the scene domesticates Crookman — the mad scientist has a wife, a home, a departure schedule — and her octoroon beauty serves as a living counterpoint to the treatment's more radical transformation.

## Biography

Mrs. Crookman enters the office while Crookman, Johnson, and Foster are discussing Max Disher's impending treatment. A "black man in white uniform" announces her: "Mrs. Crookman!" The three men rise; Johnson and Foster "eyed the beautiful little octoroon appreciatively as they bowed" (003-???).

Her function in the scene is practical: she announces that dinner is ready and that Crookman's bags are packed for his departure. "Jus' whut Ah bin tellin' him, Mrs. Crookman," Johnson hastens to say, "We got eve'ythin' fixed tuh send 'im off" (003-???). Foster, separately, thinks: "Mighty nice looking girl, Mrs. Crookman" (003-???).

She does not reappear in the novel, but she is present at the novel's very end: the epilogue's final image, in the Cannes photograph, includes "former Imperial Grand Wizard and Mrs. Givens and Matthew and Helen Fisher. All of them, he noticed, were quite as dusky as little Matthew Crookman Fisher who played in a sandpile at their feet" — and Dr. Crookman, by extension, is accompanied by the wife whose beauty once prompted thoughts of passing.

## Satirical Function

Mrs. Crookman's brief appearance serves a specific satirical purpose: she is the domestic face of the racial-industrial complex her husband has created. The observation that she "could pass for white" without the treatment — followed by the narrator's comment comparing it to "anthracite coal passing for black" — destabilizes the treatment's premise. If Crookman's own wife can already pass, what exactly is the treatment solving that lighter-skinned Black people haven't already achieved through generations of racial mixing? The answer, the novel suggests, is scale: the treatment democratizes passing, making it available to "sooty black" subjects like Napoleon Wellington Jackson, not just octoroons like Mrs. Crookman.
