# Matthew Fisher

Matthew Fisher is the post-treatment identity assumed by [[Max Disher]] after undergoing the [[Black-No-More Treatment|Black-No-More]] procedure. As Matthew Fisher, the protagonist rises from anonymity to the second-highest position in the [[Knights of Nordica]], marries [[Helen Givens]], and becomes deeply entangled in the racial politics he once satirized from the margins.

## Identity and Transformation

"Matthew Fisher, alias Max Disher, joined the Easter Sunday crowds, twirling his malacca stick and ogling the pretty flappers" (004-001). Having transformed from Black to white through [[Black-No-More, Inc.]]'s treatment, Max adopted the name Matthew Fisher and began passing as a white man — initially a spectator, then an active participant in white supremacist politics.

## Rise in the Knights of Nordica

### Infiltrating the Organization

Fisher's rise within the Knights of Nordica is meteoric. He ingratiates himself with [[Reverend Givens|Reverend Henry Givens]], the Imperial Grand Wizard, who "had never regretted that he had taken Fisher into the order and made him his right-hand man" (006-002). Fisher is appointed Grand Exalted Giraw, the order's second-in-command, and presides over mass rallies. At one such gathering, "they waited now expectantly for the words of wisdom and encouragement which they expected to hear fall from the lips of their beloved Matthew Fisher, who now looked down upon them from the platform with cynical humor mingled with disgust" (007-047).

## Political Machinations

As Grand Exalted Giraw, Fisher exploits labor unrest for profit. In [[Paradise, South Carolina]], he orchestrates a protection racket, extracting money from mill owners in exchange for suppressing strikes (007-025). He deploys "secret operatives" (007-057) to monitor labor activity, operating the Knights as an extortion enterprise as much as a racial organization.

Fisher also participates in the political alliance between the Knights and the [[Anglo-Saxon Association]], telling Givens: "With their money and ours, we can certainly get together a larger campaign fund than the Republicans" (009-060). He understands that the Black-No-More treatment threatens "white business" more than "white labor" (004-008), recognizing the economic foundation of racial politics.

## Marriage to Helen Givens

"The second event was the wedding of Helen Givens and Matthew Fisher, Grand Exalted Giraw of the Knights of Nordica" (006-001). Fisher marries [[Helen Givens]], the daughter of Reverend Givens — the same woman who, when he was Max Disher and Black, had rejected him on the dance floor in [[Chapter 1|ch01]]. The marriage consummates both his personal revenge and his assimilation into the white power structure.

## Exposure and Escape

Fisher's racial origins are eventually exposed. Buggerie's investigation finds "no record whatever of Fisher" and concludes "I have reason to suspect that he is one of those Negroes who have been whitened" (011-063). When the ancestry revelations trigger nationwide panic, "through the crisp, autumn night air sped Fisher's big tri-motored plane, headed southwest to the safety of Mexico" (012-059), with Helen at his side.


### The Transformation Experience

> Max Disher, arrayed only in a hospital bathrobe and a pair of slippers, was escorted to the elevator by two white-coated attendants. (001-118)

> Slowly, haltingly, Max Disher dragged his way down the hall to the elevator, supported on either side by an attendant. He felt terribly weak, emptied. (002-001)

> "It's me, Max Disher," replied the ex-Negro. (002-037)

> "Hello Bunny," Max greeted him. "Don't know me do you? It's me, Max Disher. You recognize my voice, don't you?" (002-042)

The physical transformation is not merely cosmetic — it is a rebirth that erases Max Disher and creates Matthew Fisher, a man whose entire existence depends on the successful performance of whiteness. The weakness and disorientation after the treatment suggest that racial passing, even when technologically guaranteed, extracts a profound psychological cost.

> "I know just what that joker's gonna do," said Bunny. "He's goin' back to Atlanta. Am I right, Big Boy?" (002-047)

Matthew Fisher's decision to return to Atlanta as a white man is the novel's definitive statement on the relationship between race and place: the same city that excluded Max Disher now awaits Matthew Fisher with open arms, proving that the color line was never about geography, culture, or personal merit — only about the arbitrary surface of the skin. In becoming white, Max does not escape Atlanta; he conquers it, and the conquest is indistinguishable from surrender.

## Narrative Function

Matthew Fisher embodies the novel's central satirical thesis: that racial identity is a social performance, and that the most successful practitioners of white supremacy may themselves be the very "Negroes" that the system seeks to exclude. His trajectory — from victim of racism to perpetrator of racist exploitation — dramatizes the fungibility of racial identity and the primacy of economic self-interest over ideological commitment.
