## Overview

African repatriation — the proposal that Black Americans should "return" to Africa rather than seek equality in America — appears in the novel as a competing solution to the "race problem." Marcus Garvey's Universal Negro Improvement Association had promoted this vision in the 1920s, and [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]] treats it with the same satirical skepticism he applies to every other racial program. When a character asks "Why should anybody in the Negro race want to go back to Africa?" (005-101), the question is rhetorical: Africa is a fantasy, not a destination.

## In the Narrative

The repatriation theme surfaces in Chapter 5, during the Black leadership conference, where various strategies for preserving the Negro race are debated. The Back-to-Africa option is dismissed as impractical and self-interested — another scheme by racial entrepreneurs to profit from Black desperation. The novel's treatment of Africa as an imagined rather than real destination underscores its broader argument that racial solutions always serve someone's economic interest.

## Significance

African repatriation is the geographic mirror of racial passing: both propose to solve the race problem by removing Black people from white America. The treatment succeeds where repatriation failed only because it is more convenient and more profitable — not because it is more ethical. [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]]'s equal-opportunity skepticism toward all racial solutions is consistent: the problem is not the solution but the premise that Black people need to be "solved."
