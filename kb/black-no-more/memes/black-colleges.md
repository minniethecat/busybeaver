## Overview

Black colleges and universities constituted one of the most significant institutional achievements of post-Reconstruction Black America. These institutions — Howard, Tuskegee, Fisk, Hampton, and dozens more — trained the Black professional class and served as centers of racial uplift ideology. The novel gestures toward this institutional world through characters who are explicitly educated (Crookman, with his German research) and those whose education is implied ([[chuck-foster|Foster]], the former schoolteacher).

## Role in the Narrative

Black colleges appear in the novel's background as part of the institutional infrastructure that the treatment dissolves. If there are no Negroes, there is no need for Negro colleges — and the professional class they produced (teachers, ministers, doctors, lawyers) loses both its client base and its raison d'être.

## Satirical Function

Questions whether Black educational institutions served genuine educational purposes or primarily functioned to reproduce the Black professional class that administered racial segregation on behalf of both white and Black elites.
