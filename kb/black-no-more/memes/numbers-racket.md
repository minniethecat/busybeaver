## Overview

The Numbers racket — Harlem's underground lottery — was a massive illegal economy in 1920s–1930s Black neighborhoods. Players bet pennies on three-digit numbers drawn from financial indices, and "bankers" like Hank Johnson collected the bets and paid out winners. Johnson's career as a Numbers "runner" turned "banker" (003-016) establishes him as a man who understands that Black economic life operates outside white institutions — and that the same entrepreneurial skills transfer to legitimate business when the opportunity arises.

## Role in the Narrative

The Numbers racket is the economic foundation of Hank Johnson's career and, indirectly, of Black-No-More, Incorporated itself. Johnson's Numbers fortune provides the capital for Crookman's venture. The racket's customer base — ordinary Black Harlemites betting pennies — is the same population that will soon spend fifty dollars on the treatment, making explicit the connection between Black economic behavior and the destruction of Blackness.

## Satirical Function

Treats crime and legitimate business as fundamentally the same activity, distinguished only by legal status. Johnson's transition from Numbers banker to corporate financier is seamless — "the best and safest graft I've ever been in" (003-006) applies equally to both.
