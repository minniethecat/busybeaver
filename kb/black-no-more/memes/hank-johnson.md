## Overview

Hank Johnson is the promoter who transforms Dr. Crookman's scientific discovery into a commercial empire. A figure familiar to any student of the Harlem Renaissance — the fast-talking Black businessman who knows everyone and can sell anything — Johnson's gift for publicity and organization turns Black-No-More, Incorporated into a national phenomenon. His partnership with Crookman and Chuck Foster forms the novel's economic engine, and his relentless optimism provides the energy that drives the plot forward.

## Biography

Johnson is introduced in Chapter 3 as the man who recognizes the commercial potential of Crookman's treatment. While Crookman thinks in scientific terms and Foster in cautious financial ones, Johnson thinks in scale — he envisions clinics across the country, stock offerings, national advertising. His background as a promoter in Harlem's Black business community has prepared him perfectly for the role: he knows how to package a product and how to sell it to people who have been taught to distrust everything.

## Character Analysis

Johnson is Schuyler's portrait of Black capitalism — energetic, creative, and ultimately amoral. He does not invent the treatment, but he invents its market. His genius is for turning Black suffering into white profit — a conversion that mirrors the novel's larger argument about how American racial ideology functions economically. Johnson's tragedy (if it can be called that) is that his success destroys the very community that made it possible — as millions of African Americans undergo the treatment, Johnson's market literally disappears.

## The Logic of Scale

Johnson's most significant contribution to the novel's argument is his demonstration of the logic of capital: once a profitable opportunity is identified, it must be pursued to its maximum extent, regardless of social consequences. Johnson does not hate Black people — he is one — but his business model requires their erasure. He personifies what Schuyler presents as capitalism's inherent tendency to dissolve the very identities it exploits. The irony is that Johnson himself does not undergo the treatment — he remains Black while profiting from the disappearance of Blackness.[PN:001]
