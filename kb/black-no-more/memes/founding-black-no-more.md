## Overview

The founding of Black-No-More, Incorporated marks the moment when Dr. Junius Crookman's skin-whitening invention transitions from a scientific curiosity to a commercial enterprise. Following Max Disher's proof-of-concept treatment in Chapter 2, Crookman and his partners Hank Johnson and Chuck Foster formalize their business arrangement in Chapter 3, opening the Harlem sanitarium to paying customers at fifty dollars per treatment. The incorporation catalyzes the novel's central social transformation: the mass conversion of Black Americans into whiteness.

## Context

Dr. Crookman had spent years developing his treatment in Germany, combining endocrinology with electrical therapy to alter melanin production (001-075). Upon returning to America, he recruited two associates: Hank Johnson, an old acquaintance who provided capital, and Chuck Foster, who brought business acumen (001-100)(003-016). The three men had observed the overwhelming public response to Crookman's press interviews — particularly the reaction of Max Disher, who became the first patient — and recognized the commercial potential.

The incorporation occurred at a moment of acute demand. Huge advertisements in Negro weeklies generated a flood of inquiries, and the sanitarium was besieged by applicants before it officially opened (001-100)(002-061). The business was structured to process patients efficiently: Crookman handled the medical procedures, Johnson managed operations, and Foster supervised the clerical staff and finances (001-115).

## Narrative Account

In Chapter 3, Black-No-More, Incorporated is in full operation. Crookman, looking tired and worn, pores over records (003-001). The partners discuss the overwhelming volume of business. "We can't handle all of the business as it is," one partner observes (003-003). The treatment fee — fifty dollars per patient — has generated enormous revenue (003-009).

### The Investigation Deepens

Crookman reports that the first sanitarium has turned out one hundred and fifty patients in the first week alone (003-012). Foster, meanwhile, confirms that they are "taking in thirty-five thousand dollars a week" (003-014). The scale of operations demands disciplined management: Crookman's secretary fields ceaseless inquiries, and the partners strategize about competition and expansion (003-015).

The incorporation also attracts political attention. Newspaper accounts detail the activities of Black-No-More, Incorporated, and the public's reaction ranges from enthusiasm among Black Americans desperate for the treatment to alarm among white supremacists who see it as a threat (003-040)(004-008).

## Aftermath

The business's rapid growth is extraordinary. Within its first year, Black-No-More opens sanitariums in twenty cities (004-037). By Chapter 5, Crookman announces the opening of the fiftieth sanitarium at a business meeting, describing "an average of one new sanitarium every week" (005-023)(005-024). By Chapter 6, the company operates one hundred sanitariums "from Coast to Coast" (006-094).

The commercial success also generates political backlash. White supremacist organizations, led by the Knights of Nordica, organize against the sanitariums, characterizing them as a menace to white society (004-008)(004-037). The tension between the sanitariums' popularity and the political opposition they provoke forms the central conflict of the novel.

This episode illustrates the novel's broader satirical method: historical events are not merely referenced but structurally inverted, with the machinery of racial power consistently producing outcomes opposite to those intended by its architects.
