## Overview

George Samuel Schuyler (1895–1977) was one of the most provocative figures of the Harlem Renaissance — a journalist and satirist whose contrarian skepticism toward racial categories put him at odds with most of his contemporaries. His 1926 essay "The Negro-Art Hokum" had already declared that "the Aframerican is merely a lampblacked Anglo-Saxon," and *Black No More* (1931) extended this argument into fiction. Schuyler's later career — including his turn toward extreme conservatism — has complicated his legacy, but the novel remains a singular achievement of American satire.[PN:001]

## Character Analysis

Schuyler's presence in this wiki as a "character" page is meta-textual: the novel's satirical voice is so distinctive that the author functions as an implicit character within the narrative. His journalism background shaped the novel's documentary texture — the newspaper clippings, the statistical language, the commercial detail — and his ideological iconoclasm shapes its refusal of easy moral conclusions.

## The Negro-Art Hokum and Its Legacy

Schuyler's 1926 essay for *The Nation*, "The Negro-Art Hokum," argued that there was no such thing as a distinctively "Negro" aesthetic — that African American artists were shaped by the same American cultural forces as their white counterparts. This essay provoked a famous response from Langston Hughes, "The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain," which became a manifesto of the Harlem Renaissance. The essay's core argument — that racial categories are social fictions — prefigures the central premise of *Black No More*.[PN:001]

## Schuyler's Satirical Vision

Unlike many satirists of race who spoke from a position of moral outrage, Schuyler's satire operates from a position of almost clinical detachment. The novel does not ask the reader to feel sympathy for its characters; it asks them to recognize the absurd machinery of racial classification. This distinguishes *Black No More* from works like George S. Schuyler's journalistic writing, where his own voice is more directly polemical.[PN:001]
