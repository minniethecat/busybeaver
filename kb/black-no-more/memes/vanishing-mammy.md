## Overview

"Vanishing Mammy" is a popular minstrel song featured prominently at the [[easter-sunday-rally|Knights of Nordica Easter Sunday rally]], performed by the white Broadway singer and comedian Jack Albert in blackface. The song laments the disappearance of the faithful, waddling "Mammy" figure of plantation nostalgia — a lament that takes on savage irony in the context of the [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More treatment]], which is literally making Black people "vanish" into whiteness. Schuyler deploys the song as a devastating satirical set-piece, layering racial performance, commercial entertainment, and political propaganda into a single scene.

## Historical Context

"Vanishing Mammy" reflects the real-world popularity of "Mammy songs" in American popular music of the early 20th century, from Al Jolson's blackface performances to the "Mammy" figure in advertising (Aunt Jemima) and film. The song title also invokes the discourse of the "vanishing Negro" — the demographic anxiety, shared by both white supremacists and Black leaders, that the Black population might be declining or disappearing. Schuyler's satire weaponizes this anxiety by literalizing it through the Black-No-More treatment.

## In the Narrative

"Vanishing Mammy" appears during the Easter Sunday rally and wedding sequence, introduced by the announcer Mortimer Shanker as "a little treat" before the main political speeches (PN 009-020). Jack Albert, described as "America's premier black-faced troubadour," performs the song accompanied by Sammy Snort's Bogalusa Babies jazz orchestra (PN 009-022). The song's lyrics — calling for Mammy to "come waddling back home again" — are rendered in a "plaintive" voice over the radio broadcast, reaching a national audience.

The song's placement is carefully calculated satire: it immediately precedes [[rev-givens|Rev. Givens']] political speech, softening the audience with sentimentality before the racial demagoguery begins. The irony is multilayered — the "vanishing" the song mourns is, in the novel's reality, the result of a medical treatment that white people are clamoring to condemn, creating a comic disconnect between the performed nostalgia and the actual political agenda.

## Satirical Treatment

Schuyler's use of "Vanishing Mammy" operates on several satirical levels simultaneously. First, it parodies the minstrel tradition itself — the blackface performer, the Tin Pan Alley sentimentality, the absurd lyrics about a "waddling" Mammy. Second, it exposes the hypocrisy of white racial nostalgia: white audiences can weep for the vanishing "Mammy" while simultaneously supporting organizations dedicated to white supremacy. Third, the song's title takes on literal meaning in the novel's world — Black people really are "vanishing" through the Black-No-More treatment, and the very people mourning the loss are those driving it through their patronage of the treatment.

The radio broadcast framing adds another dimension: the song, the political rally, and the wedding are all packaged as entertainment, blurring the line between political mobilization and commercial spectacle.

The "vanishing mammy" is Schuyler's sharpest critique of racial nostalgia: the yearning for a lost Black presence is not solidarity but a market signal, a recognition that the disappearance of Blackness threatens the economic and psychological infrastructure of white identity. The mammy figure — nurturing, loyal, asexual — was always a white fantasy; her "vanishing" merely makes the fantasy explicit by transforming it into a commodity.

The "vanishing mammy" phenomenon captured by Schuyler reflects a real anxiety of the Jim Crow era: the fear among white Southerners that Black domestic workers — upon whom white households depended for childcare, cooking, and cleaning — might abandon service for factory work or migrate North. The Great Migration (1916–1970) saw over six million Black Americans leave the South, transforming both the Southern labor market and the racial anxieties Schuyler satirizes. (003-043) (005-040)
