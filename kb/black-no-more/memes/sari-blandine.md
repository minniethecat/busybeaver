## Overview

Mrs. Sari Blandine is a minor but structurally pivotal character in the epilogue of [[george-schuyler|George Schuyler]]'s *Black No More*. Formerly Madame Sisseretta Blandish of Harlem — a beauty culturist whose skin-lightening business was destroyed by the [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More treatment]] — she reappears in the novel's closing pages as the inventor of a skin-*darkening* stain that helps reverse America's racial hierarchy.

## From Blandish to Blandine

As Madame Sisseretta Blandish, she ran "the swellest hair-straightening parlor in Harlem" [002-078]. Her business — like the entire Black beauty industry — was rendered obsolete when [[dr-crookman|Dr. Crookman]]'s treatment enabled Black Americans to become white, eliminating the market for skin-lightening and hair-straightening products. By the novel's epilogue, she has lost her business and is "working on a steam table in a Broadway Automat" [013-127].

## The Egyptienne Stain

After [[crookman-monograph|Crookman's monograph]] reveals that new Caucasians are whiter than old Caucasians — triggering a social panic in which extreme paleness becomes stigmatized as evidence of Negro ancestry — Blandine sees her opportunity. She spends a week at the Public Library researching skin stains, losing her Automat job to "a recent arrival from Czechoslovakia" in the process [013-127].

Within three to four weeks, she develops "a skin stain that would impart a long-wearing light-brown tinge to the pigment" [013-128]. She tests it successfully on her young daughter, who receives a marriage proposal from a young millionaire within a month. After giving free applications to neighborhood women, demand explodes:

> Mrs. Blandine's stain became most popular and her fame grew in her locality. She opened a shop in her front room and soon had it crowded from morning till night. The concoction was patented as Blandine's Egyptienne Stain. [013-129]

## Narrative Function

By the time [[president-hornbill|President-Elect Hornbill]] is inaugurated, "her Egyptienne Stain Shoppes dotted the country and she had won three suits for infringement of patent" [013-130]. The stain triggers a wholesale reversal of the racial economy: where Black Americans once paid to become white, now white Americans pay to become dark. "Everybody that was anybody had a stained skin. A girl without one was avoided by the young men" [013-130].

Blandine thus completes the novel's satirical circle. In the early chapters, the Black beauty industry profited from the desire to approximate whiteness; in the epilogue, Blandine's stain enables and profits from the flight *from* whiteness. The symmetry is exact: the same entrepreneurial logic that sustained the color line now demolishes it from the opposite direction.
