## Overview

Rev. Herbert Gronne represents the accommodationist wing of Schuyler's Negro-leadership satire — the figure who survives by making "statements that sounded radical to Negroes but sufficiently conservative to satisfy the white trustees" (005-060). As president of Dunbar University, Gronne's institution has seen its student body collapse to sixty-five after Black-No-More, and his participation in the Committee for the Preservation of Negro Racial Integrity is motivated as much by institutional self-preservation as by racial principle. He possesses "the asset of looking perpetually earnest and sincere" (005-060) — a quality more valuable than conviction in the economy of Negro leadership.

## Biography

Gronne is introduced in Chapter 5 as one of the Negro leaders gathered at the N.S.E.L. offices. He is described as "another white man of remote Negro ancestry" — a phrase that underscores the arbitrariness of racial classification in the novel's world — and follows a scientific expert with "a long discourse in which he expressed fear for the future of his institution whose student body had been reduced to sixty-five persons" (005-059).

His career trajectory embodies institutional accommodation: "He had been in turn a college professor, a social worker and a minister, had received the approval of the white folks and was thus doubly acceptable to the Negroes" (005-060). His rhetorical skill lies in calibration, not conviction — statements pitched to satisfy two audiences with contradictory expectations. "Much of his popularity was due to the fact that he very cleverly knew how to make statements that sounded radical to Negroes but sufficiently conservative to satisfy the white trustees of his school" (005-060).

> They all listened with respect to Dr. Gronne. He had been in turn a college professor, a social worker and a minister, had received the approval of the white folks and was thus doubly acceptable to the Negroes. Much of his popularity was due to the fact that he very cleverly knew how to make statements that sounded radical to Negroes but sufficiently conservative to satisfy the white trustees of his school. In addition he possessed the asset of looking perpetually earnest and sincere. (005-060)

When Dr. Beard proposes the cynical plan to accept Knights of Nordica money, Gronne is the only leader to raise a procedural objection: "But Doctor Beard, isn't this a rather unusual procedure?" (005-074). Beard's dismissive reply — "Rev. Gronne, it's not near as unusual as Black-No-More. I have probably ruffled your dignity but that's nothing to what Dr. Crookman will do" (005-075) — reveals the hierarchy within the Negro leadership class: Gronne's institutional caution is no match for Beard's ruthless pragmatism. Gronne's objection is procedural, not principled — he questions the method, not the ethics of colluding with white supremacists.

During the revival-like outbreak at the meeting, Gronne joins the emotional display: "face flushed and shouting amens, marched from one end of the room to the other" (005-069), demonstrating that his scholarly persona is as performative as the spirituals Jackson sings. He is later among the leaders approached by Bunny Brown to lecture for the Knights of Nordica (010-100).

## Satirical Function

Gronne satirizes the Negro educator as institutional survivor — the leader whose first loyalty is to his institution's funding, not to racial advancement. His perpetual look of "earnest and sincere" expression is a professional asset, as functional and transferable as Beard's haughty bearing or Jackson's scholarly pretensions. The detail that Gronne is "another white man of remote Negro ancestry" further destabilizes the racial categories the Committee claims to defend: the man chairing the Committee for the Preservation of Negro Racial Integrity does not appear to be Negro at all.

Gronne's Dunbar University — with its sixty-five remaining students — stands as a synecdoche for the entire Negro leadership apparatus: an institution whose existence depends on the racial segregation it claims to oppose, withering the moment that segregation is challenged from within.
