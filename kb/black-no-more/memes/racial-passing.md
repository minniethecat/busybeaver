## Overview

Racial passing is the central satirical conceit of George Schuyler's 1931 novel *Black No More*. In the Jim Crow America of the 1930s, "passing for white" was a known but dangerous social practice among light-skinned African Americans. Schuyler radicalizes this premise through science fiction: Dr. Junius Crookman's Black-No-More treatment transforms any Black person into a white person in three days (001-080). What had been a precarious individual deception becomes an industrial-scale mechanism, allowing the entire Black population to cross the color line — and in doing so, lays bare the absurdity of American racial categories (003-010).

## Historical Context

In the 1920s–1930s, thousands of light-skinned African Americans did "pass" into white society, often cutting off all contact with their Black families. The practice was simultaneously condemned as race betrayal and envied as escape from Jim Crow. The Harlem Renaissance, of which Schuyler was a provocative participant, debated the politics of passing extensively — Nella Larsen's *Passing* (1929) being the most famous literary treatment. Schuyler's contribution is distinctive for its caustic satire: rather than a tragic individual dilemma, passing becomes a mass-market commodity, and racial identity is reduced to glandular manipulation.

## In the Narrative

The Black-No-More treatment functions as the novel's plot engine. Max Disher, humiliated at a Harlem nightclub when a white woman from Atlanta calls him a "nigger," becomes the first customer at Dr. Crookman's sanitarium (001-072). Emerging as "Matthew Fisher," he enters white society, joins the white supremacist Knights of Nordica, and marries the same woman who had rejected him (004-001). Across the novel, hundreds of thousands of Black Americans undergo the treatment, causing economic panic among businesses that sold skin-whitening and hair-straightening products (005-041), existential terror among Southern whites who can no longer identify who is "really" Black (005-062), and the collapse of the color line "which we have so laboriously established" (003-010).

## Key Passages

> "Day by day we see the color line which we have so laboriously established being rapidly destroyed. There would not be so much cause for alarm in this, were it not for the fact that this vitiligo is not hereditary. In other words, the offspring of these whitened Negroes will be Negroes!" — Tallahassee *Announcer* editorial (003-010)

> Dr. Crookman "was wedded to everything black except the black woman — his wife was a white girl with remote Negro ancestry, of the type that Negroes were wont to describe as being 'able to pass for white.'" (003-020)

> "In three days the Negro becomes to all appearances a Caucasian" (001-080)

## Satirical Treatment

Schuyler deploys passing as a double-edged satirical blade. On one edge, the treatment exposes the material basis of white supremacy: when Black people can become white en masse, the Klan-like Knights of Nordica scramble to hire white-looking ex-Negroes as race statisticians to "prove" who is really white (004-010). On the other edge, it mocks the Black bourgeoisie's race-pride rhetoric, personified in Dr. Crookman himself — a "Race Man" who "was wedded to everything black except the black woman" and whose treatment aims to solve the race problem by "depriving [Negroes] of their racial characteristics" (003-020). The satire ultimately suggests that the color line is an economic and political contrivance maintained by elites on both sides.
