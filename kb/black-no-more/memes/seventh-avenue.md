## Overview

Seventh Avenue is the novel's synecdoche for Harlem itself — the street where Max Disher and Bunny Brown move through the Black social world as "gay blades" before Max's transformation, and the street from which Max disappears when he becomes Matthew Fisher. In Schuyler's geographic imagination, Seventh Avenue is to Black Harlem what Times Square is to white commercial New York: the public stage where identity is performed, observed, and judged. When Max leaves it, he leaves Blackness itself.

## Narrative Significance

Seventh Avenue functions as a threshold: to be seen on Seventh Avenue is to participate in Harlem's social economy; to leave it is to exit Black identity. Schuyler's intimate knowledge of Harlem — he lived and worked there as a journalist — gives the street's few mentions a documentary weight that grounds the novel's speculative premise in recognizable urban geography. The avenue's significance is not in what happens there but in what ceases to happen there: after the treatment's mass adoption, the "gay blades" vanish, and the street's social ecology collapses.

## Key References

Max Disher begins the novel as one of Harlem's noted figures on Seventh Avenue, part of the "gay blades" who populate the neighborhood's public spaces (001-012). His departure from this world — and from Blackness — is the novel's originating act.
