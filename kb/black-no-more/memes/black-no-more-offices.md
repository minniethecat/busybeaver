## Overview

The Black-No-More offices on [[edgecombe-avenue|Edgecombe Avenue]] represent the corporate face of racial transformation. As [[crookman-sanitarium|the sanitarium]]'s capacity is overwhelmed by demand, [[chuck-foster|Chuck Foster]] purchases "the big private house on [[edgecombe-avenue|Edgecombe Avenue]] for fifteen thousand" (003-005) to serve as an administrative headquarters. The offices are where the business of eliminating Blackness is managed — a bitter irony, given that [[edgecombe-avenue|Edgecombe Avenue]] was one of Harlem's most prestigious addresses, home to the Black elite whose world the treatment is destroying.

## In the Narrative

Chapter 3 is set primarily in the offices, where Crookman, [[hank-johnson|Hank Johnson]], and [[chuck-foster|Chuck Foster]] discuss business strategy while the switchboard operator handles a flood of calls. The scene juxtaposes the mundane atmosphere of a successful startup with the epochal nature of the business: "No, [[dr-crookman|Dr. Crookman]] cannot see anyone. …[[dr-crookman|Dr. Crookman]] will issue a statement shortly. …Fifty Dollars" (003-015). The switchboard operator's litany reduces racial transformation to a customer service script.

## Significance

The offices on [[edgecombe-avenue|Edgecombe Avenue]] embody [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]]'s materialist critique: racial transformation is not a spiritual or political event but a commercial transaction. The "sweet music of that mob out there" means "fifty bucks" per scream (003-011). By locating the corporate headquarters in a prestigious Black neighborhood, [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]] emphasizes that the destruction of the color line is being administered by the Black elite itself.
